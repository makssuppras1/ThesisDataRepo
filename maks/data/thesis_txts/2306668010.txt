Abstract
Wind power producers trading their production in day-ahead market face significant
uncertainty due to high price volatility and the unpredictable nature of wind power.
Therefore, producer can hedge its position by selling its production in a futures market
at fixed prices spanning a predefined time period, for instance a month, a quarter or
a year before their actual participation in day-ahead market. However, at high spot
price periods producer may face loss of profits. The main goal of wind producers is
to maximize their profits from trading their production over a planning horizon. Thus,
they should formulate an optimal trading strategy by defining their involvement in each
trading floor.
The aim of this master thesis was to formulate a suitable trading strategy for a wind
power producer located in Western Denmark, that participates in both day-ahead and
futures markets. Initially, a way to decide whether electricity options will be purchased
was proposed and six quarterly put options were chosen for the formulation of the hedg-
ing strategies. A stochastic optimization model was built aiming at deciding when and
at which price options will be purchased. Afterwards, the optimal level of involvement
was defined by addressing two different trading strategies, based on the input data used.
The formulated optimization models were further optimized by considering a rolling
horizon, where the position in futures market was constantly redefined by taken into
account the updated wind and price data.
The two strategies were formulated based on the different types of wind power forecasts,
namely a determinist where wind power point forecast was used, and a stochastic, where
wind production was described by a set of scenarios. The outcomes of those strategies
were evaluated using real wind power measurements, spot prices and balancing prices
from NordPool and were compared with each other. In addition, they were further as-
sessed against a benchmark where producer trades its output only in day-ahed market.
The results obtained highlighted the benefits of using electricity options to increase the
profits of a producer and to hedge against financial risks. Finally, stochastic strategy
was proven to be superior in terms of total profits compared to the deterministic one.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Over the last decades, wind power is one of the fastest growing renewable energy
sources, which is characterized by high level of unpredictability and a fluctuating nature
[1]. In addition, electricity cannot be stored, at least not in large scale, but supply has to
always be in balance with the consumption and at the same time unexpected generator
outages or high demand level variations may occur anytime. Those are some of the main
sources of the significantly high level of electricity pool price volatility.
Consequently, wind power producers that trade their production mainly in day-ahead
market face a significant uncertainty due to this price volatility and wind unpredictability.
Thus, they need to hedge against financial risks, since in principle the motive of a firm
should be to maximize its profits. In order to reduce their risk exposure, they can hedge
their position by participating in futures markets, which are becoming more and more
popular for trading electricity. In futures markets, producers can sell their production
at predefined prices and, thus, secure their profits in case spot prices decrease. However,
during high spot price periods they may face profit losses.
1.1
Contribution
It has been observed that when electricity derivatives are utilized properly in hedging
trading strategies, they can be proven beneficial to control financial risks [2]. Therefore,
the goal of a power producer is to conclude to a trading strategy, which will maximize its
profits over a given planning horizon by defining its optimal involvement in each trading
floor, namely day-ahead and futures markets.
Initially, a literature survey was performed to gain knowledge on the general topic
and to examine what has already been done and what should be further studied. Futures
markets and electricity derivatives are analyzed in [2]. In [3] it is proven that forward
contracts can enable hedging the risk of profit variability of a power producer, which
is mainly caused due to price volatility. Moreover, the advantages of option contracts
over forward ones to hedge against financial risks due to to pool price volatility and
unit failures are discussed through illustrative examples in [4] and [5]. However, in the
examples the electricity producer is assumed to own only one production unit, i.e. a
dispatchable thermal unit with predictable output and linear piecewise generation cost.
In addition, just one option is assumed to be signed and the producer is allowed to trade
it only on the first day of the decision period. In reality, options can be exercised any
time during their decision period and up to their expiration date.
Reference [6] demonstrates the benefits of signing an insurance contract against unit
failure of a power producer, as well as its impact on forward contracting decisions. [7]

concludes that diversification, namely trading among multiple markets, could reduce
trading risk. The formulation of an equilibrium model that involves futures market and a
number of day-ahead markets is analyzed in [8] and the obtained results are implemented
to market with various competition levels, namely monopoly, cartel, Cournot and perfect

In [9] it is
demonstrated how the prices of the electricity derivatives can be derived by formulating

In some works it was claimed that the introduction of futures markets may affect
the strategic behaviour of market agents. For example, options make markets more
competitive and the exact effect depends on the strike price of the options, as shown
in [10]. Furthermore, large consumers often purchase call options to protect themselves
against potential price increases, as it was explored in [11]. In addition, the usage of
electricity options for demand-side management is proposed in [12].
The contribution of this master thesis consists in formulating a multi-stage stochastic
programming model, which will decide the optimal trading strategy of a risk-neutral and
a risk-averse wind power producer. The first stage represents the participation in the
futures market, the second corresponds to the bids of the wind producer in day-ahead
market and the third the balancing market. Based on the literature review, electricity
options were chosen over forward contracts as the tool to be used in this project to hedge
against financial risks.
A realistic study case was built by using historical data from Nord Pool’s day-ahead
market (Elspot), financial power market contracts from NASDAQ and wind power fore-
casts and measurements. Moreover, an operation with "rolling horizon" is considered,
where the position in the futures market will be continuously reassessed based on up-
dated data.
Furthermore, purchased options can be exercised anytime during their
settlement period to maximize the total profits of the wind power producer.
The aim of this master thesis project was initially to explore the interconnection
between the futures and day-ahead market. In addition, an illustrative example (toy-
model) was formulated to understand the basic processes. Afterwards, an optimization
model was designed and implemented in GAMS to identify the optimal trading strategy
for a wind power producer participating in both markets. Last but not least, the solution
of the optimization model was evaluated against a benchmark where a wind power
producer trades his output only in the day-ahead market.
1.2
Outline
The master thesis is organised as follows: Chapter 2 provides a detailed description
of the Nordic Electricity market, the different trading floors, the day-ahead and the
futures market are presented. Chapter 3 illustrates all the information related to market
clearing under uncertainty, like scenario generation, stochastic programming, evaluation
of trading strategies and risk management. In chapter 4, an illustrative example allows
a further insight of the interconnection between futures and day-ahead markets and

CHAPTER 2
The Nordic Electricity Market
2.1
Electricity as a "Special" Commodity
Electricity is a "special" commodity for many reasons. Mainly due to the fact that
there must always be a balance between generation and consumption. Moreover, trans-
portation and distribution is performed on a power network, with its specific physical
rules and at the same time storage is uneconomical as of today. In addition, a large
part of the electric demand is of a must-serve nature, like residential consumers and,
especially, hospitals. Last but not least, the origin of the product, as well as its quality
and nature cannot be differentiated by consumers.
2.2
Historical Overview
Initially, the historical evolution of the electricity markets is briefly reviewed. In 1980s
the first ideas for liberalization of the electricity sector were born and electricity market
concepts were introduced in Chile. The deregulation of the european power industry
started in 1990 when the market in England and Wales opened.
By then, the first
market to open in Europe was the english one and it was a model for lot of countries all
around the world. [13] After that, the Nordic countries deregulated their markets and
brought them together into a common Nordic market in the early 1990s. The basis for
deregulation in the remaining Nordic countries was formulated in 1990 by Energy Act.
The first Nordic country to deregulate its power markets was Norway. In 1993 Statnett
Marked AS was established as an independent company and the total volume the first
year of operation was 18.4 TWh, at a value of NOK 1.55 billion.
Norwegian Parliament made in 1995 the framework for integrated Nordic power
market contracts. One year later, a joint Norwegian-Swedish power exchange was created
and named Nord Pool ASA. Finland joined that power exchange in 1998 and one year
later Elbas was operated as a different market for the balance adjustment in Finland
and Sweden. Denmark joined the exchange in 2000 and the Nordic market became fully
integrated.
In 2002 Nord Pool organized its spot market activities in a separate company, called
Nord Pool Spot AS. In 2004 Eastern Denmark joined the Elbas market. In 2005 Nord
Pool Spot opened the Kontek bidding area in Germany, which geographically gave access
to the Vattenfall Europe Transmission control area. Nord Pool Spot launched Elbas in
Germany in 2006, in Western Denmark in 2007 and Norway joined the Elbas market in
2009. The same year Nord Pool Spot implemented a negative price floor in Elspot.

2.3.2
Balancing Market
The balancing power market is a tool for the Nordic Transmission System Operators
(TSO) to keep balance between total generation and consumption of power in real time.
This balancing stage can be seen as two markets, the regulation and the balancing
market.
In the regulation market, TSO buys or sells regulating power prior to the delivery
hour. A participant in that market is offering to buy or sell regulating power prior to
the hour of operations. Some actors provide voluntarily regulating power, while others
are committed to provide regulating power through reserve provision mechanism.
Balancing market is linked to the real-time operations. A participant in the balancing
market is responsible to cover the costs of its part to putting the system off-balance.

2.3.3
Futures Market
Futures market is an auction market in which participants buy and sell physical or
financial products, which are called derivatives and are due for delivery on a specified
date in the future. Financial contracts are used for price hedging and risk management.
In the Nordic region financial contracts are traded in Nasdaq OMX Commodities, which
is the brand name for the worldwide suite of commodity related products and services
offered by Nasdaq. Some of those offerings are power, natural gas and carbon emission
markets, fuel oil, tanker and dry cargo freight, seafood derivatives, electricity certificates,
and clearing services. Nasdaq Commodities offer a full suite of Nordic power products
to ensure that customers’ trading needs are met. [16]
Financial contracts have a time horizon spanning from the following day up to six
years from today, offering daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly or annual contracts. Nord
Pool calculates a system price which is used as the reference price for the futures market.
It is an artificial price that represents a common Nordic price, which would have been
achieved if there was only one bidding area for the whole Nordic market. Financial power
market contracts have no physical delivery. Cash settlement takes place through trading
and delivery period starting the due date of each contract. Technical conditions, like for
instance grid congestion, are not accounted when signing financial contracts. However,
buyers and sellers can manage the risks associated to the physical market prices by
signing financial contracts. [14]
Electricity derivatives can offer to the seller a higher average price than the pool and,
thus, it may be quite useful in case of high price (day-ahead, adjustment, and balancing)
volatility and, therefore, high profit uncertainty.
Electricity spot prices are volatile
due to the characteristics of electricity, such as non-storability, uncertain and inelastic
demand and a steep supply function [2]. Consequently, electricity futures markets are
considered as a mean to hedge against financial risks of power producers. The goal of a
producer is to define the level of its optimal involvement in electricity futures markets
to maximize its profit, while minimizing the risk of profit variability. Once a futures
contract is signed, the revenue from selling the electricity associated with that contract
is risk free. [17]
A categorisation of futures contracts can be done between peak and base contracts.
Peak contracts are only used during peak hours of weekdays, for example between 8am
and 18pm.
On the contrary, base contracts can be signed for all hours in the day.
Typically, peak contracts have a higher price compared to the base contracts.

2.4
Electricity Derivatives
Electricity derivatives aim at reducing, through appropriate hedging strategies, the
risk that market participants face when participating in the electricity market. Different
types of derivatives that are available within the electricity market are among others
forward contracts, insurance contracts and electricity options.
2.4.1
Forward Contract
A common derivative within the electricity market is a forward contract. This contract
is an agreement to buy or sell energy at predefined prices at a specific date in the future.
The date specified on the forward contract is called expiration or maturity date. One
of the parties of a forward contract assumes a long position and so agrees to buy the
predefined amount of energy in the future at the fixed price. The other party assumes
a short position and, thus, agrees to sell the energy the same moment in the future for
the same price [18].
A disadvantage of forward contracts is that their delivery is mandatory. Therefore,
if the power producer is unable to produce all the agreed amount, for instance due to
an unexpected failure, the missing energy must be bought from the day-ahead market
to comply with the forward contract.
Moreover, if the clearing price is higher than
the predetermined contract price, then the power producer will face significant financial
losses.

2.4.3
Electricity Options
An alternative electricity derivative available in the futures market is electricity op-
tions. An option is an agreement that gives the power producer the right, but not the
obligation, to buy or sell a specific amount of energy at a fixed price in a certain date
in the future. Therefore, an option is the right to buy or sell an underlying contract, i.e.
Future, DS Future or Average Rate Future, at a predetermined price at a specific date
in the future. Option contracts in combination with other Nasdaq derivatives are useful
tools to manage financial risks. [16]
This fixed price is known as exercise or strike price. An option provides a higher
degree of flexibility to the producer than a forward contract, since it can decide when the
option will be exercised based on the availability and the production of the generating
unit and the clearing price. However, compared to forward contracts, which entail no
cost, there is a mandatory expense to acquire an option.
There are two main types of electricity options, namely calls and puts options. A call
option gives the holder the right to buy a predefined amount of electricity at the strike
price. On the contrary, a put option gives the right to the holder to sell a predefined
amount of electricity at the strike price. Moreover, for those two types of options there
are also two sides. On the one side is the agent that takes a long position, which means
that a call or a put option is purchased. On the other side is the agent that takes a
short position, i.e. an option (call or put) is sold and, therefore, the obligation to buy
or sell the amount at the strike price is undertaken. [5] Therefore, there are in total four
types of option positions, namely a long position in a call and in a put option and a
short position in a call and in a put option.
Additionally, there are two more types of options, European and American style
options, a distinction that has nothing to do with geographical location. A European
option can only be exercised on its expiration date. On the other hand, an American
option may be exercised at any time before and up to its expiration date. This payoff
depends on the average price of the underlying asset on a specific date and not over a
period. [19]

initial cost of the option is not included in the calculation of the payoff. [18] Figure

In this project, a wind power producer was considered, whose main goal was to de-

only through put option contracts. The producer decides its participation in the futures
market at the beginning of the planning horizon, whereas the decisions related to trading
in day-ahed market are made continuously during the trading period.
Consequently, futures market decisions are made before knowing the realization of

can be deferred in time and they are considered to be made with perfect foresight and

Electricity option decisions are made in different stages during the trading period.
Initially, in stage 1 the power producer needs to decide if an option will be purchased
(decision period) and in stage 2, if option has been bought, whether or not to exercise
it (settlement or delivery period).

3.1
Introduction
Decision-making problems with lack of perfect information in electricity markets are
common. For example, a power producer has to submit its offer in day-ahead market
without knowing the actual electricity prices the following day. Similarly, renewable
power producers do not know precisely their power production at the time of placing
their bids in day-ahead market. This is what motivates the formulation of stochastic
programming models for decision-making under uncertainty.
Most decision-making problems can be adequately formulated as optimization prob-
lems. If the inputs of the problems are well defined, then the problem can be solved
to calculate the optimal decision. Uncertain data are substituted by the corresponding
expected values that will result to a deterministic optimization problem, the solution
of which provides the best result. Nevertheless, most of the times this is not the case.
Input data are not certain, but they are described through probabilistic functions. The
probability distribution of the input data can be presented as a collection of plausible
sets of data with associated probabilities of occurrence adding to 1. In this way, a single
solution that is the best for all sets of input data will be achieved. This solution is not
the best for each individual set of inputs, but it is the best if all of them, weighted by
their probabilities of occurrence are simultaneously taken into account.
3.2

describe adequately a stochastic process, it is necessary to generate a sufficient number
of scenarios, so that they would cover the most plausible realizations of the considered

stage decision variable is defined for each single scenario considered. The aforementioned
decision framework can be visualised by a scenario tree, as the one illustrated in figure

Graphically, a scenario tree is made by branches and nodes. Each branch represents
a realization of the stochastic variable.

decisions are made, has only one predecessor and can have multiple successors. The

3.3
Two-Stage Stochastic Programming
In decision-making under uncertainty, some decisions have to be taken with incom-
plete information. Over the decision horizon, decisions related to options are made in
different stages. Each stage represents a time point where decisions are made or where
uncertainty partially or totally vanishes.
Based on the number of stages taken into
account, stochastic problems can be divided into two-stage and multi-stage.
In a two-stage decision-making process, two different kinds of decisions are observed.
First stage decisions are made prior to the realization of the stochastic process and,
thus, they do not depend on each realization. They are called here-and-now decisions.
In the second stage, decisions are made after knowing the realization of the stochastic
process and, thus, decisions depend on each realization. Those decisions are denoted as
wait-and-see.

3.5.1

In stochastic programming models, uncertain data are modelled as stochastic processes
and, therefore, profit of power producer is a random variable that can be characterized
by a probability distribution. During the formulation of stochastic models, in order to
reduce the risk of experiencing profit variability, risk control is an important factor that
should be taken into account.
The most common way to control risk is by including a term in the model formula-
tion. This term is usually called risk functional or risk measure and considers the risk
associated with profit distribution. Risk measures can be incorporated either into the
objective function of the problem or as an additional set of constraints in the problem
formulation. Different types of risk measures could be for example the variance of the
profit or the value of the profit to exceed a specific value. Some of the most usual risk
measures regarding two-stage stochastic programming problems in electricity markets

4.1
Decision Framework and Assumptions
Electricity derivatives are useful tools that could be used to reduce, through appro-
priate hedging strategies, the risk that market participants face when participating in
the electricity industry. In order to gain a further insight of the connection between
electricity and futures markets, a simplified two-stage stochastic optimization model
is formulated. This model is described in order to evaluate the benefits of electricity
options by comparing the outcome with selling only in the pool or through forward con-
tracts. The study horizon considered covers two consecutive hours in all cases, for the
sake of simplicity. Moreover, the model is solved for three different cases.
In the first case, the power producer sells all its power production in the pool at
variable prices based on the expected pool price and its generation costs. Specifically,
the producer will generate and sell in the pool only if its operation costs are lower than
the hourly pool prices. Otherwise, the producer will prefer not to generate at all, since
losses will occur. In the second case, the power producer signs a forward contract at the
beginning of the first hour. In addition, during the first hour the producer sells energy
only in the pool and in the second hour through a forward contract.
In the third case, power producer purchases a put option at the beginning of the
first hour and, thus, in the second hour either sells its production in the pool at variable
prices or through a put option at fixed prices based on the price realization of the first
hour. If the strike price is higher than the pool price of the first hour, then the power
producer will exercise the option in the second hour. Otherwise, producer can sell all
its production in the pool, since the foreseen prices of the second hour will probably
be lower than the strike price, too. The decision framework of an electricity option

delivery is not mandatory and it is more profitable to sell all the production in the pool.
In general, it can be concluded that the introduction of options improves the expected
profits of the power producer, while forward contract leads to lower average expected
profits. This is mainly due to the fact that the information related to the pool price
during period 1 enables the producer to decide whether it is worth exercising the option
in the second hour to increase its expected profits. On the contrary, when a forward
contract is signed, its delivery during hour 2 is mandatory. Thus, when pool price is
higher than the predefined forward price the producer faces financial losses, which are
avoided with options.
In addition, the revenues in the second case, where a forward contract is signed, are
the lowest. First of all, this could be explained by the fact that the optimization model
is solved only for two hours. In case of a longer time horizon, the expected profits should
be at least higher than the ones of case 1, where the power producer sells only in the
pool at variable prices. Moreover, the price of the forward contract was assumed to be
the same as the strike price of the option to obtain unbiased results. In reality, forward
prices are generally higher than strike prices of electricity options. Thus, if a higher
forward price was considered, then the revenues would have been higher and probably
above the ones of case 1. In the optimization models formulated in the following sections,
only electricity put options were chosen as the tool to hedge against financial risk.

5.1
Decision Framework and Assumptions
For the purpose of this project, it was considered a wind power generator that trades
its production in day-ahead market and participates in the futures market in order to
sell part of its production in predefined prices and to hedge against financial risks. At
the same time, it pays the imbalance costs that may occur due to the difference between
the amount that the producer scheduled to offer in day-ahead and futures market and
its actual production. The electricity derivative used in this model is electricity options,
i.e. the right to buy or sell a DS Futures contract.
The optimization model is solved over a time period between 1st of April 2006 and 30th
of September 2007. Therefore, six different quarterly option contracts can be signed, one
for each quarter of the period under examination. For every quarter, a specific number
of units of each option contract could be purchased, but it is not mandatory for the
producer to exercise all of them.
During the decision period of each option, the decision for the purchase of the option
contracts has to be made. During the settlement period, the optimal number of units
that should be exercised during each quarter will be defined by solving the optimization
problem described in chapter 6, aiming at maximizing the total profits of the wind
producer.
An optimization problem was formulated to determine the optimal trading strategy
of a wind producer participating both in day-ahead and futures market.
The main
assumptions taken into consideration are the following. The wind producer is assumed
to behave as price taker and, thus, option and pool prices are not affected by its decisions.
In addition, the producer does not make any bids for regulation or reserve power supply,
or corrective actions in intraday markets. In addition, the wind producer is assumed not
to apply any control strategies on its production.
Two correlated sources of uncertainty are considered, the pool and balancing prices,
and one uncorrelated, the wind power production. Prices are described by sets of scenar-
ios, while wind production is either a point forecast, namely a single-valued renewable
energy forecast, or is described by scenarios. Furthermore, only the possibility of pur-
chasing put options (long position) is considered.

6.1
Introduction
Wind power producers participate in the various trading floors that are available in
electricity market. In particular, in futures market they sign financial contracts selling
part of their production at predefined prices in order to hedge against financial risks.
Moreover, in day-ahead market they place their bids based on wind power forecasts
issued for the following day. Finally, in balancing market they get to pay the cost of
potential energy deviations.
The objective of the wind power producer is to maximize its expected profits from
trading energy simultaneously in all trading floors. Therefore, a three-stage stochastic
optimization problem, as illustrated in figure 6.1, should be formulated to decide its
level of participation in each floor. The first stage decisions are the ones related to the
purchase of option contracts, the second stage decisions are those regarding the bids in
day-ahead market and the amount that should be sold through options and the third
stage is the balancing market.
For example, from the 2nd of January 2006 until the 31st of March 2006, which is the
decision period for option Q2-06, the decision regarding the purchase or not of that option
should be made. Therefore, the strike price the day the decision is to be made, along with
the forecasted strike prices for the rest of the decision period should be compared with
the expected pool prices between the 1st of April 2006 and the 30th of June 2006, which
is the settlement period of that option. Moreover, the power production of the producer
during the settlement period should also be taken into account for the calculation of the
exact amount of options to be purchased. At the same time, the power producer every
day takes into account the forecasted power production for the following day, as well as
the expected spot prices to decide how much energy will be sold in day-ahead market
and, afterwards, the imbalance costs are calculated.
In general, during the decision period of each option, the power producer needs to
combine all the necessary information to decide whether the corresponding option con-
tract will be signed and at which strike price. At the same time the producer should
decide how much energy to bid in day-ahead market and how much to sell through the
already purchased options. In practice, this problem is intractable, due to its compu-

day-ahead and regulating price forecast and wind power production forecasts 3 to 6

7.3
Risk
Pool and regulating prices, as well as the wind production of a power producer are
considered as uncertain parameters in the stochastic optimization model. Consequently,
the producer could be interested into modelling the risk of profit variability. Therefore,
the risk-averse optimization model presented in subsection 6.4.4 is solved considering a
confidence level a equal to 0.95 and various values of the weighting factor β.
Figure 7.4 demonstrates the total revenues of the power producer versus the CVaR
for various values of the weighting factor β. As can be observed, by increasing the value
of β, the total profits are reduced. Thus, the more risk-averse the producer behaves,
the lower its total revenues are. The optimal solution of the stochastic optimization
problem for β = 0 leads to the highest profits, but at the same time leads to the highest
risk, as defined in terms of the CVaR of the profits. In particular, when β is increased
from zero (risk-neutral producer) to 20 (risk-averse producer), then the total revenues
are decreased by approximately 5.7%, which is translated to 1.57 mAC less profits. On
the contrary, the CVaR increases by 120% when risk is accounted for. The large value
of CVaR indicates risk-averse solution. The consideration of risk control reduces slightly
the total revenues but at the same time the risk of profit variability is efficiently reduced.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions
8.1
Overall Discussion
Wind power producers trading their production in day-ahead market are exposed to
high financial risks, mainly due to price volatility and wind power unpredictability. Con-
sequently, the aim of this thesis was to formulate a trading strategy for a wind producer
participating simultaneously in both day-ahead and futures markets to maximize its
profits and hedge against the risk of financial losses. Therefore, two strategies were for-
mulated based on the wind input data used and their outcomes were compared against
a benchmark, where the wind producer sells its production only in the pool, to further
assess the benefits of electricity options.
The trading strategies aiming at maximizing the total profits of the producer were
applied over a time period between 1st of April 2006 and 30th of September 2007. The
first step was to define a way to decide whether electricity options will be purchased. Six
quarterly put options were chosen for the formulation of the hedging strategies. When
the purchase decisions were made, the closing prices of the options during their decision
period, as well as the spot prices and the wind production during their settlement period
were not available. Consequently, a stochastic optimization model was built aiming at
deciding when and at which price options will be purchased.
Based on the average
capacity factor of onshore wind farms during 2006 and 2007, the nominal capacity of the
farm chosen and the contract size of each option, in total 60 units of each quarterly option
were purchased during the decision period. The option purchase optimization model was
solved separately for each quarterly option and their strike prices were computed.
Under the assumption of a risk-neutral wind power producer, two strategies were
formulated, a deterministic, where wind power point forecast was used, and a stochastic,
where wind production was described by a set of scenarios. Initially, it was concluded
that the participation of a wind producer in both day-ahead and futures markets could
increase its total profits over a period, compared to selling only in the pool, regardless
of which strategy was applied. In particular, in the deterministic strategy electricity
options increased the total profits by 32.5% and in the stochastic strategy by 25.1%.
Moreover, the results obtained from solving the formulated optimization models in-
dicated that the stochastic approach led to higher total scheduled amounts and more
options exercised in total, compared to the deterministic one. The amounts in deficit
and surplus were higher in the stochastic strategy and, thus, the wind power producer
was more exposed to penalties due to imbalances. Nevertheless, 5% higher profits were
achieved in the end of the time period. Both strategies were also evaluated against the
outcome of the optimization model in case of perfect bid. The performance ratio of the
==PAGE:76==
56

deterministic approach was 93.21% and of the stochastic one 97.86%.
Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis was conducted to assess how much the total num-
ber of options and the purchase step affect the total revenues of the producer. It was
proven that by increasing the maximum options purchased in total, the revenues were
higher. In addition, the revenues increased further by decreasing the maximum units
that could be purchased daily.
Due to the uncertain parameters included in the optimization models, risk control
was an important factor accounted in decision-making. The risk measure considered
in this project was Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). In conclusion, risk management
influenced the total revenues of the wind power producer. In particular, the more risk
averse the wind producer behaved, the lower its total profits in the end of the opti-
mization period were. By increasing the weighting factor β from 0 (risk-neutral case)
to 20 (risk-averse case), its total profits decreased by approximately 5.7%. In addition,
although the total scheduled amounts decreased by around 33%, the share of energy
sold through options increased, since the risk-averse producer prefers to sell in futures
market that entails no price uncertainty. Last but not least, by considering risk control
some of the negative peaks in the hourly revenues that occurred in the risk-neutral case
were avoided.
8.2
Future Work
In this project, the decision regarding the purchase of options was made by a stochastic
optimization problem formulated and solved in GAMS. Scenarios for the daily closing
prices of the options for every day of the decision were generated and the problem
was solved with a daily rolling horizon.
The total amount of options that could be
purchased each quarter and the purchase steps were the result of a heuristic approach,
based on physical and economic intuition of the option purchase problem. Therefore, a
more systematic and advanced approach should be considered in the future, in order to
further improve the quality of the option purchase optimization problem. For instance,
instead of buying options with a fixed daily step until the maximum number of options is
purchased, if a daily closing price reaches a high value all the available options should be
bought. One way to define this high value could be for example based on the maximum
strike prices that were observed in the available historical data for quarterly options.
Furthermore, in both trading strategies proposed, the decision regarding the exercise
or not of an option was made by comparing the hourly spot price with the strike price
of the option. The difference between the two prices was not taken into account in the
decision. Imagine a producer that generates 10 MWh and can sell its production either

contract could be a possible scenario to hedge against financial risk.

APPENDIX A
Balancing Market
When the system is in deficit of power production, due to deviations from producers
and consumers with respect to their day-ahead positions then there is need for upward
regulation. On the contrary, when the system has a surplus of generation there is need
for downward regulation. The balancing price at which energy is traded is defined by
the price settlement followed in each area.
A.1
One-Price Market
In a one-price balancing market, deviations from day-ahead hourly bids are settled in
a unique balancing price, regardless of the sign of producer and system imbalances. In
general, up-regulation balancing price is higher than day-ahead price. On the contrary,
down-regulation balancing price is lower than day-ahead price. Figure A.1 illustrates
the producer imbalance and arbitrage opportunity between the two markets in one-price
system.
Figure A.1: Producer imbalance and arbitrage opportunity between balancing and day-
ahead markets in the one-price system (source: [28])
When the imbalance of a power producer is in the same direction as the imbalance of
the overall system, then the balancing price is less favorable than the spot price. On the
other hand, when the producer and the system deviations are in the opposite direction,
the balancing price is more favorable.
As it can be observed from figure A.1, when the overall system needs up-regulation
and the producer imbalance (difference between scheduled and actual generation) is neg-
ative or when the system needs down-regulation and the producer imbalance is positive,
==PAGE:80==
60
A Balancing Market
then the balancing price is less favorable. On the contrary, when the overall system needs
up-regulation and the producer imbalance is positive then the balancing price is more
favorable that the spot price. Likewise, when the overall system needs down-regulation
and the producer imbalance is negative then the balancing price is more favorable that
the spot price. Therefore, it can be concluded that the participation at both day-ahead
and balancing markets opens opportunities for arbitrage for power producers when one-
price settlement is implemented.
A.2
Two-Price Market
In a two-price market, power production deviations are priced differently depending
on the imbalance sign. Deviations that are in the opposite direction compared to the
imbalance of the system, which means that producers unintentionally help TSO to regain
balance between supply and demand, are priced at the day-ahead market price. On the
other hand, imbalances that have the same sign as the one of the overall system are
settled at the clearing price of the balancing market.
Figure A.2 indicates that there is no arbitrage opportunity in case deviation is in the
opposite direction to the system imbalance, since the price obtained at the balancing
market is equal to the day-ahead market price. However, an opportunity loss still exists
when producer and system deviations have the same sign.
