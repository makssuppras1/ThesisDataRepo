Abstract
This thesis investigates the Site Selection Visit (SSV) process within Novo Nordisk’s
clinical trials, focusing on identifying current challenges and proposing strategies for
optimization. The SSV is a critical phase in the initiation of clinical trials, ensuring
that selected sites possess the necessary infrastructure, personnel, and capabilities to
conduct clinical trials in compliance with regulatory standards and protocols.
The primary objectives of this study are to evaluate the effectiveness of existing SSV
processes, identify common challenges, and propose actionable recommendations for
enhancing trial eﬀiciency and quality. The research utilizes a mixed-method approach,
including qualitative interviews with key stakeholders such as Feasibility Managers,
Trial Managers, and Clinical Research Associates, and quantitative analysis of trial
data.
Key findings from the interviews highlight several challenges, including inconsis-
tent information flow, inadequate training and preparedness, and stringent timelines
that impact trial setup and execution. To address these issues, the study proposes
three core solutions: the development of a standardized SSV Master Template to
improve consistency and reduce variability in site evaluations, the introduction of
structured pre-meetings between headquarters and CRAs to enhance communication
and preparedness, and the implementation of a timeline roadmap to define clear
milestones and responsibilities. Recognizing the need for flexibility, the study also
explores the use of recorded presentations as an alternative to live pre-meetings, en-
suring that critical information is accessible even under resource constraints.
Early feedback on these solutions has demonstrated their potential to streamline
trial processes, enhance communication, and improve site readiness. The adoption of
these tools, represents a tangible step toward more eﬀicient and effective clinical trial
management. This research contributes to the broader field of clinical trial optimiza-
tion by promoting the standardization of processes, encouraging the adoption of best
practices, and fostering innovation in pharmaceutical research.
These proposed solutions are informed by the insights gathered during interviews.
Additionally, they reflect my personal analysis and synthesis of the challenges iden-
tified during the research process. By combining stakeholder input with practical
problem-solving approaches, the solutions aim to address real-world ineﬀiciencies in
clinical trial management

Introduction
1.1
Novo Nordisk’s History and Evolution
Novo Nordisk, founded in 1923 in Denmark, was born out of a significant break-
through in diabetes treatment following the discovery of insulin by Dr. Frederick
Banting and Charles Best in 1921 at the University of Toronto.
Their work in
isolating insulin and demonstrating its ability to regulate blood glucose levels
in diabetic animals laid the foundation for the development of insulin-based
therapies, which would transform the treatment of diabetes globally [1].
In 1922,
August Krogh, a Danish professor, and his wife Marie Krogh, who had diabetes, rec-
ognized the potential of insulin and obtained permission to produce it in Scandinavia.
Over the decades, Novo Nordisk has introduced several advancements in diabetes
treatment. These include the production of human insulin in 1982, the NovoPen®
insulin delivery system in 1985, and, more recently, Rybelsus®, the first oral GLP-1
receptor agonist. Semaglutide-based therapies like Ozempic® have further expanded
treatment options for both diabetes and obesity [2] [3] [4].
The table below 1.1 show Novo Nordisk’s key innovations steps in diabetes care
over the years.
As Novo Nordisk diversified its focus to include obesity, cardiovascular diseases,
and rare disorders, managing clinical trials became increasingly complex. The com-
pany’s ability to coordinate trials eﬀiciently is essential for maintaining timelines,
ensuring regulatory compliance, and gathering reliable data. This thesis focuses on
the Site Selection Visit (SSV), an important step in clinical trial setup. The SSV
ensures that trial sites have the infrastructure, resources, and capabilities to meet the
demands of the study.
1.2
Diversification and Research Focus
As Novo Nordisk expanded its research focus, the complexity of its clinical trials
increased. Each therapeutic area presents unique challenges that demand innovative
approaches to clinical trial design and management.

1.2.1
Diabetes
Diabetes has been the cornerstone of Novo Nordisk’s focus since its inception. The
company’s innovations, such as GLP-1 receptor agonists and modern insulins, have
revolutionized diabetes care. Clinical trials in this area demand large, diverse patient
populations and long-term follow-ups to assess eﬀicacy and safety. For example, trials
for oral semaglutide required complex designs to evaluate its effectiveness compared
to injectables and standard treatments, emphasizing the need for robust site selection
processes to ensure geographic and demographic diversity [5] [6] [7].
1.2.2
Obesity
Obesity is a growing global health concern and a strategic focus for Novo Nordisk.
Semaglutide has been a game-changer in obesity management, demonstrating sig-
nificant weight loss in clinical trials.
However, trials in this area face challenges
such as securing sites with the infrastructure to support long-term patient engage-
ment and specialized metabolic assessments.
The integration of multidisciplinary
site capabilities—including dietetics and endocrinology—is importsnt for trial suc-
cess [8] [9] [10].

1.2.3
Cardiovascular Diseases
Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are among the leading causes of death globally, and
Novo Nordisk integrates cardiovascular outcomes in its diabetes and obesity trials.
Landmark trials, such as the SUSTAIN-6 study, showcased the cardiovascular benefits
of semaglutide. Site selection for CVD trials demands careful evaluation of hospitals’
infrastructure to support complex procedures like echocardiograms and stress tests
while ensuring access to patients with diverse comorbidities [11] [12].
1.2.4
Rare Diseases
Rare diseases, such as hemophilia and growth hormone disorders, require a highly
targeted approach to clinical trials due to small patient populations. Novo Nordisk’s
innovations, like glycopegylated rFVIII, demonstrate its commitment to addressing
these challenges. Site selection involves identifying centers of excellence with special-
ized expertise and the ability to engage small, dispersed patient populations effec-
tively [13].
1.2.5
Emerging Therapy Areas
Emerging areas like metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH) and
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) represent Novo Nordisk’s forward-looking research strategy.
Trials in these areas require pioneering site capabilities to conduct novel diagnostic
and therapeutic evaluations. For example, MASH trials demand liver imaging facil-
ities, while AD trials require access to cognitive testing and biomarkers for disease
progression. Additionally, the company’s cardio-renal research has been strengthened
with the development of assets such as ziltivekimab and ocedurenone, which target
inflammation and advanced kidney disease, respectively [14] [15].
1.3
Global Impact and Financial Success
Novo Nordisk operates in over 80 countries and supplies its products to more than
170 countries, serving millions of patients annually.
The company employs over
72,000 people worldwide and is headquartered in Denmark [5].
In 2022, Novo Nordisk achieved net sales of 176.95 billion DKK, with 52% of
revenue coming from North America [16] [17]. Diabetes products accounted for 79%
of total sales, with GLP-1-based therapies like Ozempic® and Rybelsus® driving
significant growth. Obesity care products, including Wegovy® and Saxenda®, saw a
147% increase in sales. These figures highlight the growing global demand for Novo
Nordisk’s treatments and its role in addressing chronic health challenges.

1.4
Clinical Trials and Research
Novo Nordisk conducts clinical trials in over 60 countries, involving more than
6,100 sites and 48,000 participants annually [18].
These trials span all phases,
from early-stage studies assessing safety to large-scale Phase III trials evaluating
long-term treatment outcomes.
The results from these trials are essential for
regulatory approvals and understanding their impact in real-world settings.
The figure 1.2 below illustrates the distribution of active Novo Nordisk clinical
trials across Phases 1 to 4, highlighting the company’s focus on advanced-stage trials,
particularly for diabetes and obesity therapies. Trials in areas such as cardiovascular
and rare diseases require tailored site selection processes to address their unique
challenges. Data on active Novo Nordisk clinical trials across Phases 1 to 4 is based
on a private company presentation.
Figure 1.2. Active Novo Nordisk’s clinical trials phase 1-4. [19]
Ensuring site preparation is a key component of trial success The SSV, conducted
before the trial begins, evaluates whether a site meets operational, regulatory, and
recruitment standards. Sites are assessed for their infrastructure, such as diagnostic
and monitoring capabilities, as well as their ability to adhere to trial protocols.
Proper site selection reduces delays and operational issues, ensuring trials produce
high-quality, reliable data. By selecting well-prepared sites, Novo Nordisk ensures
trials meet their objectives and comply with regulatory standards. The SSV process
also plays a role in meeting patient recruitment goals, contributing to the overall
success of clinical trials [20].

Novo Nordisk regularly reviews its clinical trial processes to find areas needing im-
provement. As part of the Project Trial Excellence, an initiative aimed at enhancing
clinical trial optimization, NN interviewed stakeholders and held workshops to iden-
tify ineﬀiciencies. This effort revealed several small but significant issues, including
gaps in the Site Selection Visit (SSV) process, which informed the development of
new tools to improve SSV eﬀiciency.
One major issue was the lack of a standard procedure for transferring trial in-
formation from global teams to Clinical Development Centers (CDCs). This often
led to miscommunication, delays, increased workload for local teams, and poor site
performance. In this thesis, I aim to understand the main challenges related to clini-
cal trials and support Project Trial Excellence by providing inputs, insights, and my
point of view. By contributing my ideas, I hope to offer valuable perspectives and
solutions to address these issues effectively.
1.5
Sustainable Practices
Novo Nordisk is committed to minimizing its environmental impact while ensuring
that people worldwide have access to essential medicines.
A large proportion of
people living with diabetes reside in low- and middle-income countries, and this
population is expected to grow as diabetes prevalence increases.
In response,
Novo Nordisk has undertaken initiatives aimed at improving access to insulin for
vulnerable populations. The company has reached over 6.7 million people in these
regions through its insulin access programs.
The company is also focused on reducing its carbon footprint. All Novo Nordisk
production sites now source 100% of their power from renewable energy. In addition,
the company is working with its suppliers to transition to renewable energy, with the
goal of achieving net-zero emissions across its entire value chain by 2045 [21].
1.6
Future Directions
Looking ahead, Novo Nordisk plans to continue its efforts to address unmet medical
needs by expanding into emerging therapy areas. The company aims to establish
a stronger presence in the field of cardiovascular disease and further its research
into conditions such as metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH),
chronic kidney disease (CKD), and Alzheimer’s disease (AD). The focus will be on
developing therapies that can modify the progression of these diseases and, in some
cases, offer curative treatments.
Novo Nordisk also places emphasis on maintaining a productive organizational
culture. The company is dedicated to employee engagement and fostering a work en-

2.1
What is a clinical trial?
A clinical trial is a structured research study designed to evaluate the safety and
effectiveness of medical treatments, drugs, or procedures.
These trials provide
essential data that informs regulatory decisions and clinical practices. For chronic
conditions like diabetes and obesity, demonstrating long-term eﬀicacy and safety is
==PAGE:24==
particularly critical [28].
Clinical trials are conducted in four phases:
• Phase I: Focuses on assessing the safety, tolerability, and pharmacokinetics
of a new intervention in a small group of healthy volunteers. For example, a
Phase I trial might test a novel diabetes drug to ensure it does not cause adverse
reactions in participants before advancing to broader trials [29].
• Phase II: Evaluates the eﬀicacy of the treatment in a larger group of partic-
ipants who have the condition being targeted. For instance, this phase could
involve testing the drug’s ability to improve blood glucose levels in patients
with type 2 diabetes.
• Phase III: Compares the new treatment to standard therapies in a large pop-
ulation, providing comprehensive data on its effectiveness and side effects [30].
A diabetes drug in Phase III might be compared against the current standard-
of-care treatment to demonstrate its superiority or equivalence.
• Phase IV: Conducted after regulatory approval, this phase monitors long-term
effects and effectiveness in diverse populations [31].
These phases build on one another, ensuring treatments are safe and effective
before they reach the market. To further illustrate the structured approach to clinical
trials and their critical role in the drug development process, the figure 2.1 summarizes

2.2
Considerations Across Clinical Trial Phases: A Trial
Management Perspective
Clinical trial management involves activities like study design, site selection, patient
recruitment, data collection, and compliance with regulatory standards [29] [32]. Ef-
fective systems streamline these tasks, ensuring:
• Participant safety.
• Reliable data collection.
• Ethical and eﬀicient trial operations [33].
Clinical trial management encompasses a spectrum of activities, each tailored to the
unique demands of different trial phases. From the initial exploration of safety and
tolerability in Phase I to the large-scale assessment of effectiveness and side effects
in Phase III, each phase presents distinct challenges and opportunities for effective
management. Understanding the specific considerations, challenges, and country se-
lection for each phase is pivotal to ensuring the successful execution of clinical trials
and the generation of robust, reliable data.
2.2.1
Phase I: Safety and Tolerability Assessment
In Phase I trials, the primary focus is on evaluating the safety, tolerability, and
pharmacokinetics of a new intervention in a small group of healthy volunteers. From
a trial management perspective, one of the key challenges lies in country selection.
Identifying countries with experienced investigators and facilities equipped to handle
early-stage trials is crucial. Moreover, meticulous planning and execution are essential
to ensure the safety and well-being of participants.
The establishment of robust
data collection and monitoring processes is also crucial for capturing real-time safety
information and pharmacokinetic profiles.
Effective trial management in Phase I
necessitates close collaboration with regulatory authorities to ensure compliance with
safety protocols and ethical standards, laying the foundation for subsequent trial
phases.
2.2.2
Phase II: Eﬀicacy Evaluation in a Larger Population
As trials progress to Phase II, the management focus shifts towards evaluating the
intervention’s eﬀicacy in a larger group of participants who have the condition that
the treatment aims to address. This phase presents challenges in country selection.
Effective trial management requires the identification of countries with access to the
target patient population, experienced investigators, and suitable healthcare infras-
tructure. The challenges also include the development of targeted patient recruit-
ment strategies and the implementation of retention initiatives to address potential
Master Thesis: Bianca Maria Signorini 9
==PAGE:26==
dropout rates. Additionally, the establishment of standardized data collection and
reporting procedures is critical for generating reliable evidence of the intervention’s
effectiveness.
2.2.3
Phase III: Large-Scale Comparative Testing
Phase III trials involve large-scale testing that compares the new treatment to stan-
dard therapies, providing comprehensive data on its effectiveness and side effects.
Effective trial management in this phase demands meticulous planning to navigate
the complexities of multi-country trials, ensuring standardized data collection and ro-
bust monitoring across diverse patient populations and healthcare systems. Eﬀicient
patient recruitment and retention strategies are crucial to meet enrollment targets and
uphold the statistical power of the trial. Challenges in Phase III also include man-
aging and coordinating activities across multiple countries, each with its own unique
patient demographics, healthcare infrastructure, and regulatory requirements.
2.2.4
Phase IV: Long-Term Effects Monitoring
Conducted after a treatment has received regulatory approval, Phase IV trials focus
on monitoring long-term effects and effectiveness in diverse populations. Effective
trial management in this phase involves the establishment of comprehensive post-
marketing surveillance mechanisms to capture real-world data on the intervention’s
safety and eﬀicacy. Challenges in Phase IV include the ongoing engagement of health-
care providers and patients to ensure continuous data collection and the proactive
monitoring of long-term safety and effectiveness.
Country selection for Phase IV
trials is critical, as it requires identifying countries with the capability to conduct
long-term follow-up, real-world evidence generation, and diverse patient populations.

Clinical Trial Feasibility
Clinical trial feasibility refers to evaluating whether a trial can be successfully
conducted based on factors such as site capabilities, patient availability, regulatory
requirements, and logistical considerations. Feasibility assessments are essential for
identifying potential challenges early and ensuring trials run smoothly and eﬀiciently.
This step optimizes the use of resources and improves the likelihood of success while
maintaining ethical and regulatory compliance.
For a company like Novo Nordisk, feasibility assessments are particularly signifi-

These visits allow trial teams to inspect facilities, verify the availability of required
equipment, and interact directly with site staff to understand their readiness. Ad-

10.3.2
Alternative Solution: Slide Recording
Recognizing that pre-meetings may not always be feasible—due to time constraints,
unavailability of CRAs, or CRAs not yet being assigned to the trial—an alternative
solution could involve recording the slide deck presentation. This recording would
provide a detailed explanation of the slides, ensuring that essential information is con-
veyed to CRAs. Although it lacks the interactive Q&A element, the recording would
still be highly beneficial in equipping CRAs with trial-specific knowledge and aligning
expectations. This approach offers flexibility and ensures that critical information is
accessible, even when pre-meetings cannot be conducted.
10.3.3
Benefits of Pre-Meetings (and Slide Recordings)
The introduction of pre-meetings, supported by the option of slide recordings, offers
several advantages. First, it enhances the preparedness of CRAs, equipping them with
the necessary information to conduct comprehensive site evaluations. This reduces
the likelihood of delays caused by incomplete or unclear trial information. Second,
it ensures consistency in how trial information is communicated to sites, presenting
Novo Nordisk as a well-organized and professional organization. Finally, pre-meetings
and recordings foster better collaboration between headquarters and CRAs, creating
a streamlined and eﬀicient process for site selection.

10.4
Implementation 3: Timeline Roadmap
The timeline roadmap is a tool designed to organize and track the major milestones
involved in the clinical trial process.
It provides a clear, step-by-step structure
that helps ensure everyone involved—from global headquarters to local teams—
understands their roles, responsibilities, and deadlines.
This roadmap is essential
for maintaining accountability, avoiding delays, and making the entire trial process
more eﬀicient.
A clinical trial involves many complex steps, and each one can be influenced
by multiple factors, such as the availability of resources, regulatory approvals, and
unforeseen issues at trial sites. The timeline roadmap helps manage these variables
by clearly defining key milestones and the tasks associated with each. One crucial
aspect is ensuring the First Patient First Visit (FPFV) happens only after receiving
approval through the Clinical Trial Information System (CTIS), a regulatory
requirement in Europe. However, other activities can still proceed in parallel while
waiting for this approval, keeping the process eﬀicient.
The key milestones outlined in the timeline roadmap include:
• SSV Preparation and Execution: Scheduling and completing Site Selection
Visits (SSVs) by a specified date (e.g., XX-MMM-YYYY). This first milestone
involves selecting potential trial sites. Tasks include initiating contracts, ensur-
ing the calibration of necessary equipment, and setting up the local lab. This
ensures the site is evaluated properly and ready for subsequent steps.
• Document Submission and Review: All essential documents must be pre-
pared and submitted, such as Good Clinical Practice (GCP) certificates, inves-
tigator CVs, and site-specific documents like Confidentiality Agreements. Sites
must also be registered in systems like the Organization Management System
(OMS).
• Training and Equipment Setup: Teams ensure that all staff complete re-
quired training, like those on CONNECT, and that essential equipment, such
as temperature loggers, is installed and calibrated.
• Investigator Meeting (IM): This is a collaborative step where investigators
and site staff are trained and aligned with the trial objectives, protocol, and
other requirements. .
• Site Initiation Visit (SIV): During the SIV, the site’s readiness is verified,
and any last-minute preparations are completed before site activation .
• Regulatory Approval via CTIS: CTIS approval ensures that all regulatory
requirements have been met for the trial to proceed. This is a mandatory step
before activating a site.

Secondly, the roadmap improves operational eﬀiciency by serving as a centralized

Lastly, the timeline roadmap reinforces professionalism and reliability in Novo

CHAPTER 11
Results and Discussion
This chapter presents the outcomes and implications of the solutions proposed during
this thesis. The focus is on the SSV Master Template, which is still under develop-
ment and expected to be adopted soon, alongside two additional ideas based on
stakeholder interviews and analysis: pre-meetings between headquarters and Clinical
Research Associates (CRAs), and a timeline roadmap. Together, these solutions aim
to streamline clinical trial processes by addressing ineﬀiciencies identified earlier. The
following sections analyze the results, feedback, and broader impact of these solutions.
11.1
Effects of the Challenges in SSVs
The company’s rapid growth and the lack of standardized communication between
global and local teams have led to significant challenges in site selection. Misaligned
trial teams, inconsistent information sharing, and case-by-case adaptations have re-
sulted in delays in site activation and hindered trial readiness. Feedback gathered
through the Trial Excellence Project highlighted these issues, leading to the develop-
ment of targeted solutions, including the Master Template, pre-meetings, and time-
line roadmap. These tools aim to improve site readiness and create a more scalable
framework for future trials.
11.2
Results and Feedback from Implemented
Solutions
11.2.1
SSV Master Template
The SSV Master Template addresses inconsistencies in site evaluations by providing
a standardized framework for feasibility assessments, infrastructure evaluations, and
stakeholder engagement. Early use of the template improved clarity during site visits,
reduced ambiguity, and ensured that all critical aspects were consistently evaluated.
Stakeholders noted that this approach minimized miscommunication and enhanced
site readiness for trials. The Clinical Learning Unit (SLU) is responsible for main-
taining and updating the template to ensure its long-term success.
==PAGE:72==
11.2.2
Pre-Meetings Between Headquarters and CRAs
Pre-meetings were introduced to close communication gaps between headquarters
and CRAs. These meetings provided CRAs with essential trial-specific information,
enhancing their preparedness for site evaluations. Stakeholders reported that pre-
meetings boosted CRA confidence and reduced follow-up clarifications, improving
the eﬀiciency of the site selection process. Recorded presentations were also explored
as an alternative to live meetings, ensuring consistent preparation even under time or
resource constraints. Both methods have been recognized as effective for improving
alignment and eﬀiciency.
11.2.3
Timeline Roadmap
The timeline roadmap introduces clear milestones and assigned responsibilities to trial
processes, addressing delays caused by unclear deadlines. Early feedback highlighted
improved coordination between global and local teams, with stakeholders appreci-
ating the structured approach to tasks like document submissions, site activations,
and training schedules. The roadmap’s visual representation made it easier to track
progress and identify potential bottlenecks. While its full implementation is still un-
der review, early results suggest it has strong potential to improve trial eﬀiciency and
accountability.
11.3
Discussion of Findings
The implemented solutions address significant ineﬀiciencies in the SSV process. The
Master Template standardizes critical trial information transfer, reducing variability
and ensuring that site evaluations are thorough and consistent. Pre-meetings and
recordings help bridge communication gaps, allowing CRAs to conduct more effective
evaluations. The timeline roadmap further strengthens trial processes by clarifying
roles and deadlines, fostering accountability across teams.
These tools align with NN’s broader goals of scalability and operational eﬀiciency.
While the SSV Master Template is still being finalized, it is expected to soon be
adopted as part of the company’s standard processes. The pre-meetings and time-
line roadmap, which were conceptualized through stakeholder insights and personal
analysis, are practical recommendations designed to address the observed challenges.
Together, these solutions aim to create lasting improvements in clinical trial manage-
ment.
