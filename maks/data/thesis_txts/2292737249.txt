Abstract
The matter of this thesis is on physics-based modelling of electric
guitar sound and vibrations with emphasis on studying the dynamic
behaviour of an electric guitar using a Laser Scanning Doppler Vi-
brometer and replicating the experimental ﬁndings numerically.
The main parts of the guitar are modelled numerically, and are
subdued to a modal analysis in ANSYS where natural frequencies
and mode shapes are found.
The electric guitar is disassembled into its main components and
each part is measured individually, as well as measurements of the
entire guitar, with and with out string are carried out. The measure-
ments are used to determine natural frequencies and mode shapes.
The measurements from the main parts of the guitar are used to
validate the results of the numerical modal analysis. The measure-
ments of the entire guitar show how the guitar’s mode shapes are
results of combining the shapes of the individual parts. The inﬂu-
ence of the string tension is found to have little or no eﬀect on the
natural frequencies or mode shapes of the guitar.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In this report a numerical model of the main parts of a musical instrument
are produced, for studying its dynamic properties, and the ﬁndings will
be validated experimentally. When basing a synthetically produced sound
on a real physical model, the highest quality is provided when it comes to
imitating real instruments. This also allows for the artiﬁcial instrument to
have the same control parameters as the real one, therefore expressivity of
control is unbounded [9].
Electric guitars are among the most popular instruments of choice by
musicians world wide. The sound produced by the electric guitar is wel-
comed by almost all musical genre, and its players often take a prominent
role during musical performances. The special characteristics of the electric
guitar sound is caused by a number of factors [8], of which, it would be
of interest to point out the most prominent ones, i.e. how the geometrical
structure of the guitar as a whole, inﬂuences the vibrations of the string,
which in turn inﬂuences magnetic ﬁeld of the pick-up’s, and ultimately the
sound produced.
Multiple studies have been made on the electric guitar, however, mostly
emphasising the output signal to produce sound synthesis. Fleischer and
Zwicker [7] have found that the vibration of the guitar structure causes
the supports of the string to be non-rigid and ultimately causing the string
signal to decay faster than in the case of rigid supports, and when studying
the vibrations of a bass guitar it was found, that the movement of the bridge
was signiﬁcantly less than the movement of the neck [6].
A model of the acoustic guitar has been made from four coupled masses,
where the calculated and measured resonances and phases agreed reason-
ably well [11].
Numerical models have also been made, of the acoustic
guitar. Modelling the top plate of the guitar and its interaction with the
surrounding air [2]. However, the acoustic guitar is not suitable to com-

The sound produced by the guitar is initiated by the excitation of the
string, the so called pluck. The result is a free transient decay of the string,
which represents the coupled body/string vibration of the instrument[14].
When in motion two perpendicular polarisations of transverse oscillation
arise, and are shown to be parametrically coupled such that spontaneous

are exited as well [14]. In [13] a study has been made on how axially mov-
ing supports inﬂuence the transverse vibrations on a string, and using the
Laplace transforms, an exact solution was constructed. Including bending
stiﬀness in the string equation is shown to have a noticeable eﬀect on the
natural frequencies distribution in a way that as the mode number increase,
the ratio between the natural frequencies increases also [5].
The aim of this project is twofold. Firstly, to develop and experiment-
ally validate a numerical model including the main parts of a solid body
guitar thought to contribute most to sound production. Secondly, to gather
and report experience from using the departments newly acquired Scanning
Laser Doppler Vibrometer for contactless full ﬁeld measurements.
A 3D model of the main parts of an electric guitar will be modelled with
commercial CAD software. And they will be imported to a FEM program
for a modal analysis. The inﬂuence of the guitars main structures namely,
the neck and the body will be investigated, to see whether they have a
notorious impact on the dynamic behaviour of the guitar. The dynamics of
the guitar will be veriﬁed experimentally with a full-ﬁeld measurement by
the Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometer.
The structure of the report is the following. Chapter 2 is dedicated to in-
troduce the reader to various topics, where a basic knowledge is considered
helpful, to read the following chapters. In chapter 3 a numerical analysis is
performed and the inﬂuence of the various sub structures of the instrument
are investigated. The results of the numerical analysis is validated experi-
mentally with the SLDV in chapter 4. Chapter 4 contains the experimental
analysis which documents the experience acquired with the Scanning Laser
Doppler Vibrometer used for this report. Chapter 5 is dedicated to the
conclusions.

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter will describe some basic information about the electric guitar
and the Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometer considered necessary when
reading the report. A basic knowledge in structural and vibrational theory
is expected from the reader. The department has procured an electric guitar
(Fender Squier Telecaster (FST)), whereon the physical experiments are
conducted.
2.1
The electric guitar
The Telecaster (1951) was the ﬁrst commercially successful solid body elec-
tric guitar. The solid body guitar solved a feedback problem manufacturers
had been dealing with. Feedback occurs in hollow body guitars, because
the sound produced from the guitar is ampliﬁed by the loudspeakers which
excite the guitars resonance frequency, which increases its amplitude, thus
feeding the loudspeaker with an even louder sound and the loop is repeated
[8]. The solid body guitar automatically solved this problem, because its
amplitude is not a dominant factor in the loudness of the sound. Since the
body of the solid body guitar has only a secondary acoustical function, they
may vary signiﬁcantly in shape and material. Still, accomplished players
have very speciﬁc material preferences, i.e. alder and swamp ash. Diﬀerent
materials are known to have diﬀerent tonal qualities, but the diﬀerence is
subtle. When the string in a solid body guitar is plucked, the kinetic energy
is not radiated away as sound, resulting in a long decay time (good sustain).
Softer materials (the body of the FST is alder and the neck is maple) are
known to have a warm or rich sound, where harder materials (swamp ash)
are known to have a bright tone and good sustain. Sustain is in essence a
measure of internal structural damping.

Chapter 3
Numerical Analysis
For the modelling process, FENDER was contacted by mail and asked
whether detailed drawings of the guitar could be produced for this project,
but the request was denied. The guitar parts will therefore be geometric-
ally modelled in CREO, a commercial 3D-modelling software package. The
models created in CREO will be exported to ANSYS, an advanced simu-

and mode shapes from the modal analysis are compared to the experimental
data from chapter 4.
3.1
Hinged Plate
In order to verify the numerical procedures, the hinged plate from section
4.3.1 is modelled in CREO and analysed in ANSYS. The results of the
analysis are then compared to the measurements from the experimental
analysis.
The plate is only approximately hinged, but in reality it is a
clamped plate. To simulate the hinges, the actual plate is cut with an extra
60 mm on each side. Then a rectangular groove is milled in to the plate,
from both sides and with equal depth. These grooves are placed where the
hinges are supposed to be, thus weakening the restoring moment in the
plate such that it is approximately hinged. The plate is 1 mm in thickness
and 0.4 mm in the grooves. Figure 3.1 shows two ﬁgures of the plate. Figure
3.1(a) shows the plate and ﬁgure 3.1(b) shows the plate and highlights in
green where the plate is clamped.

Guitar body

tedious and cumbersome task to model it on the basis on manual measure-

spline function then connects these points with a smooth curve. This is a
convenient method of modelling a geometry with complex curvatures with
reasonable accuracy.
The various cavities in the guitar body for the electronics and the neck,

4.2
Inquiry on the production process of the guitar body
Observing ﬁgure 4.10(b) through 4.10(e) in section 4.4.1, one might suspect
that the body is made, not from one solid piece of wood, but from three
pieces and then glued together, where the joints are circa where the lines are
seen in the ﬁgures. Speculating that the area around the joints might have
either a stiﬀening or softening eﬀect, resulting in four similar consecutive
shapes.
To test whether this might be the case, two diﬀerent guitar body designs
are analysed. One where material was added around the area of the suspec-
ted joints, and one where material was moved. The diﬀerent guitar body
designs are seen in ﬁgure 3.6.
Both bodies were subjected to a modal analysis, anticipating mode
shapes that might resemble those found in 4.10(b) through 4.10(e) in sec-
tion 4.4.1, thereby explaining the four similar consecutive shapes. How-
ever, there where no diﬀerences to be seen in the mode shapes, so they
were identical to the ones from 3.4. The only diﬀerence between this ana-
lysis and the one from section 3.2.2, are minor diﬀerences in the natural
frequencies.
To force some sort of change in the mode shapes, the test where material
was moved from the body was done iteratively, by removing more and more
material, to see if the pattern with similar mode shapes would occur, but
the pattern did not show.

4.2

In this section some of the experimental procedures are explained to intro-

4.2.1
Excitation
There are several ways to excite a test object and for this project, two
diﬀerent methods were used, which are elaborated here.
Shaker (Mini Shaker Type 4810)
The shaker excites the test object by applying a vibrating force which is
controlled by a generator. The shaker has to be in rigid contact with the
test object. In this project, the shaker is either mechanically screwed on or
"glued" on with beeswax.
Piezo element
A material responding to an alternating current such, that it will contract
and expand in phase with the current imposed upon it.
4.2.2
Excitation Signals
The SLDV is equipped with a built in generator where various types of
signals can be sent to the exciter, e.g. Harmonic Sine and Sweep which are
self-explanatory and, Periodic Chirp and Pseudo Random. Both Periodic
Chirp and Pseudo Random are designed to excite all FFT (Fast Fourier
Transformation) lines of the measured spectrum and an excellent choice for
fast measurements. One method of measuring non-linear systems and ex-
posing non-linearities is to excite the test object with both Pseudo Random
and Periodic Chirp and comparing the frequency response functions (FRF).

lines specify the sample-time at each scan point and the resolution, i.e. the
more FFT-lines the higher the sample-time and a better the resolution is
acquired. For example, in section 4.3 the bandwidth is set to 0-10kHz with
400 FFT-lines. With these settings the sample time for each scan point is
40 ms and resolution is 25Hz.

Investigating the frequency spectra
To see in which frequency spectra the guitar body is responding, a wide
spectrum measurement is made. This will show were the guitar body re-

interest for identifying natural frequencies and mode shapes.
Frequency spectra with SLDV
Figure 4.7 shows the FRF and coherence for the guitar body from 0 to 10
kHz with 800 FFT lines, excited with the pseudo random function. It is
quite clear that the most interesting range is within 0 to 2000 kHz, where
several distinct peaks are detected.
The convergence is quite good up to around 1500 kHz, where it is around
0.9, which indicates a reliable measurement. From there it decreases down
to around 0.4. The convergence measures how much of the input is causing
the output, and a value this low, concludes the measurement in the upper
range is not valid.
Frequency spectra, impulse test
Another wide spectrum analysis was also made, namely an impulse test.
The guitar body is suspended from rubber bands and ﬁtted with an ac-
celerometer and an impulse is applied to the structure. The accelerometer
measures the response of the applied impulse which is caused with the im-
pulse hammer. The hammer is ﬁtted with a steel tip to excite as many
frequencies as possible.
Figure 4.8 shows the FRF and the coherence from the impulse test.
The FRF shows quite distinct peaks up to 5kHz with good convergence,
where-after the response seems to decrease along with the coherence.

Guitar Neck
When building a numerical model of the guitar, it is of interest to know the
actual behaviour of the guitar neck, the same way that it would help know-
ing the behaviour of the guitar body. Namely, by dividing the guitar into
subsections, and then comparing the numerical models of these subsections
to the actual measurements. Therefore the guitar neck is also measured
individually.
The procedures and the setup are similar to that of the guitar body in
section 4.4.1. The supports are the same foam mats, see ﬁgure 4.15 and the
scan mesh is 50x200 points. The connection between the shaker and the
neck is made from bees wax.
The truss rod has not been dismantled from the guitar neck. This is
because the fret board would have to be dismantled also, to get to the
truss rod, and it is permanently glued in place. Removing and reﬁtting the
fret board would be a diﬃcult and cumbersome task, and would put the
structural integrity of the guitar neck at risk.
The extra weight of the tuners is considered negligible, so they have not
been dismantled either.
FRF for the guitar neck
Initially a wide spectrum analysis is made to determine in which area of
the frequency spectra, the guitar neck responds.
The guitar neck is excited with the pseudo random function from 0 to 10
kHz with 3200 FFT lines. The FRF and the coherence are shown in ﬁgure
4.13. It is seen that the neck responds interestingly up to around 6 kHz,
but the coherence begins to decline drastically around 2.5 kHz. Previously,
when studying the guitar body, it was quite clear when the coherence fell to
a non-acceptable level (around 4), but in this case, it is not so clear were to
make this distinction. In [1], a coherence value less than 7.5 is considered
not acceptable, so, when narrowing the frequency spectra, the interval 0-2.5
kHz would make a reasonable measurement.

Conclusions
In this report, ﬁrstly, gathering and reporting experience of using the de-
partments newly acquired Scanning Laser Doppler Vibrometer has been a
major contribution. Secondly, numerical models of the main parts of an
electric guitar are produced and subdued to modal analysis. The full ﬁeld
measurements from the SLDV were used to validate the numerical models.
One contribution of this report was to test and document experience
with the SLDV, since this equipment is new in the laboratory. This was
done by making a full ﬁeld measurement of a hinged plate, of which the
analytical solution was well known, both for the natural frequencies and the
mode shapes. The measurements were compared to the analytical solutions
and were found to be in excellent agreement with each other. Subsequently,
a numerical model of the hinged plate was produced and subdued to a
modal analysis. The mode shapes and the natural frequencies from the
measurements were used to validate the results from the modal analysis.
The main contribution was to carry out and report a full ﬁeld meas-
urement of a solid body guitar with the SLDV. Initially, the guitar was
dismantled into to two main parts, body and neck, to measure the indi-
vidual parts separately.
With the measurements from the guitar body it was possible to identify
the ﬁrst three natural frequencies and the corresponding mode shapes and
these results were also reproduced numerically by a modal analysis. The
guitar neck was also measured and several natural frequencies and mode
shapes were identiﬁed and these results were also reproduced numerically.
The agreement between the measurements and the numerical models where
in good agreement with each other.
The entire guitar was also measured on, and the measurement was com-
pared to the measurements from the separate body and neck. Some of the
mode shapes from the guitar were recognised as a combination of the mode
39

shapes from the body and the neck. The guitar was measured both with
and with out strings and the eﬀect of the tension of the strings was seen to
be negligible, hardly inﬂuencing the natural frequencies or the mode shapes.
As mentioned previously, one objective of this report was use and doc-
ument the experience acquired with the SLDV. Working with the SLDV,
many diﬀerent geometries and many diﬀerent frequency spectra have been
measured.
The accuracy for detecting frequency peaks in the FRF was
very impressive and the visual representation of the operational deﬂection
shapes exceeded all expectations. So the equipment itself, when used for
the objective of this report, hardly had any noticeable drawbacks. It could
be pointed out, that high resolution measurements could be rather time-
consuming, of the order of several hours. The SLDV seemed to be able to
measure almost on any surface. Even, the shiny surface of the guitar body
was not a problem. The back-side of the guitar body was sanded to check
for diﬀerences in the measurements, but no diﬀerence was seen.
Disassembling the guitar proved to be advantageous for the numerical
analysis. Rather than modelling the entire guitar all at once and comparing
it to the measurements, it was possible to model a guitar part individually
and compare the results from the modal analysis to the measurements for
that guitar part speciﬁcally.
It was not possible to measure the guitar neck stripped from the truss-
rod inside, or the fret board, which has to be kept in mind, when comparing
the behaviour of the numerical model to the actual measurements.
The results presented in this report could be used as a basis for future
work on a physics based model of electric guitar sound and vibration. A
more complete numerical model of the guitar, including body, neck and
truss-rod, could be produced and compared to the full ﬁeld measurements
of the guitar. There are full ﬁeld measurements of the guitar with and with
out strings which could also be taken into consideration, when making a
complete numerical model of the guitar.
When the dynamic behaviour of the electric guitar is mapped and sup-
ported by measurements from the SLDV, the results could be used for re-
ﬁning the boundary conditions for the string equation and ultimately sound
production.
