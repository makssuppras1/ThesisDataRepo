Abstract
As Artificial Intelligence (AI) is being adopted in more and more societal settings and everyday
products, the dangers and flaws of AI is seeping in to our everyday lives. As the European Union (EU)
is regularising and proposing how and when to use AI, what must be achieved to ensure safety, and
an emphasis on who is responsible when AI fails, has put a greater focus on explaining the models
and ensuring fairness from the people producing AI products.
This thesis investigates the potential hidden biases within demographics in freely available state-
of-the-art diffusion models, focusing on gender and ethnicity. It further seeks out whether bias can
be quantified and interpreted from cross-attention values between image and text embeddings. The
cross-attention is segmented into masks of interest and evaluated on the effect sizes between the
masks, either within or between protected groups. Segmented masks are typically people or objects
that could divert attention from the protected group. The research also aims to see how bias mitigation
techniques can be used to ensure fairness in biased prompt embeddings, and how these techniques
might alter cross-attention. To asses the problems of historical figures being debiased, the thesis
explores and presents an approach that specifically targets bias mitigation towards only appropriate
groups.
Key findings reveal significant differences in cross-attention between generated images of people
with white skin and those with black skin for certain keywords. Effect sizes comparing hundreds of
generated images shows a trend of cross-attention being more attributed in pictures depicting White
people than Black people. The cross-attention between genders seemed to be more equal in more
cases, showing that gender bias may not be as easily quantified. Bias mitigation was effective for eth-
nicity in a variety of occupations, but less so for gender. It was found that debiasing was more likely
to convert a person with feminine characteristics into a person with masculine ones, than the other
way around. Using a survey, it also became clear that there is a clear trade-off between effectiveness
of bias mitigation and quality. Balancing bias mitigation using binary classification was in most cases
successful and could be applied for numerous historical figures.
These insights contribute to the research of enhancing explainability and fairness in generated im-
ages within state-of-the-art diffusion models. Additional work is required to determine whether cross-
attention can be consistently linked to bias and how exactly this bias originates. However, experiments
indicate that cross-attention is promising for detecting and interpreting bias in Text-to-Image diffusion
models.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
At the intersection of technology and social justice, AI has emerged as a double-edged sword, em-
powering users in both formal and creative settings, but at the risk of perpetuating and even amplifying
social stereotypes and bias [1] [2] [3].
Following advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) and the introduction of ChatGPT in
2022, Generative AI (GAI) is increasingly being integrated, researched, and, discussed in various sec-
tors including tech, business, education, and medicine [4] [5]. One study found that 97% of business
leaders who use ChatGPT or plan to do so think it will be useful to companies [6]. This integration could
provide multiple channels through which GAI could inadvertently propagate social biases and stereo-
types, such as marketing, products and internal business systems. Meanwhile, Western companies
striving to implement principles of fairness, mitigate bias, and increase diversity in GAI are caught in a
polarized political landscape [7] [8], perhaps most strikingly in the US, housing major industry players
like OpenAI, Google, Apple and Meta [9]. This tension has been particularly pronounced for LLMs [3],
but has also recently become evident for Text To Image (TTI) models as well. Notably, Google paused
part of its Gemini model in February, 2024. According to Prabhakar Raghavan (Senior Vice President
at Google): ”our tuning to ensure that Gemini showed a range of people failed to account for cases
that should clearly not show a range” [9], cementing the issue of fairness in AI development. In June,
only a few months later, Apple introduced Apple Intelligence, aiming to add LLMs and TTI models to
devices, with functionality like automatic scheduling and custom emojis, potentially placing racial and
gender stereotypes in the hands of hundreds of millions of users.
Bias in TTI diffusion models has been identified by empirically comparing the distributions of pro-
tected groups, such as ethnicity and gender, in generated images to those in real populations, but more
complex types have been identified as well [1] [10]. Meanwhile, progress has been made in both ex-
plainability and fairness methods for open-source TTI diffusion models, such as Stable Diffusion (SD).
In 2022 Tang et al. [11] an explainability method Diffusion Attentive Attribution Maps (DAAM), which
uses cross-attention to visualise the influence of specific words in the input prompt on the generated
image. Another approach, in the realm of fairness, proposed by Chuang et al. (2023) [12], involves
manipulating Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training (CLIP) text embeddings, which connect tex-
tual and visual information in SD models, to reduce bias between pairs such as female-male and
Black-Caucasian. Since the publication of these papers [12] [11] [1], the new and improved Stable
Diffusion XL (SDXL) has been introduced, raising the question of whether the model’s biases have
changed and if cross-attention could be beneficial in examining potential bias. This thesis aims to ex-
tend bias mitigation to SDXL and combine it with DAAM to detect and interpret statistical and complex
bias. This is done in an exploratory way, probing for bias with a range of experiments, while keeping
pitfalls like the ones encountered by Gemini in mind. Concretely, this thesis aims to add to the dis-
cussions of explainability, fairness, and bias mitigation within TTI diffusion models by addressing the
following research questions:
1. Can bias found in literature be reproduced in the Stable Diffusion XL?
2. How can DAAM be utilised to both detect and interpret bias that could otherwise be hidden or
difficult to explain?
3. How effective is bias mitigation for CLIP text embeddings, and does it affect quality and DAAM?
4. Can debiasing be balanced to diversify appropriate groups, while ignoring others?

CHAPTER 2
Background
This chapter introduces necessary background information to get a better understanding of the under-
lying theory for the models and methods used in this thesis. State-of-the-art literature related to the
topics of this thesis will also be highlighted and summarised, to give a broader idea of what has been
done in the field before and how it fits into the broader aspect on topics such as deep learning, dif-
fusion models, explainability, image segmentation, bias mitigation and fairness in AI. Multiple papers
referenced and discussed in this chapter were published right before or during the time of writing this
thesis, and therefore the literature is state-of-the-art to the best of our knowledge.
2.1
Deep Learning
2.1.1
U-Net
The U-net is a popular deep learning architecture introduced by Ronneberger et al. [13]. The
U-net architecture consists of encoder layers to capture contextual information and lower the spatial
resolution of the input and decoder layers to decode the encoded data together with skip connections
which pass information directly from the encoding layers. In diffusion models, the U-Net serves as a
backbone for denoising the diffusion process.
Figure 2.1: The U-net architecture for 32x32 pixel resolution. Source [13].
==PAGE:12==
2.1 Deep Learning
3
During the contracting path, the U-net performs a series of convolutions and max-pooling oper-
ations until a 32x32 feature image is obtained. The expanding path is a series of up-convolutions,
known as transpose convolutions, and performs the opposite operation of a convolution. The out-
put of each up-convolution is concatenated with the feature map from the contracting path using the
skip-connections. The final output of the U-net is slightly smaller than the original image because the
convolutions do not add padding to the feature images.
2.1.2
Autoencoders
Auto encoders are a specific type of feed-forward neural network where the input is the same as the
output. An input is compressed into a lower dimensional representation, and then reconstructed using
a decoder back to its original state using the lower-dimensional representation. The loss function is
then defined by how close the output is to the input. Autoencoders are unsupervised, as they do not
require any explicit labels for training.
Figure 2.2: The vanilla autoencoder. The encoder takes an input, x, and creates the latent represen-
tation, z, where the decoder does reconstruction of the original image and outputs ˜x.
The training of an autoencoder is defined by two components, the decoded messages (χ) and the
encoded messages (ζ). The encoder (Eθ) will process data from a higher-dimensional space to a
lower-dimensional encoded space χ →ζ, and the decoder (Dϕ) will process data vice versa ζ →χ,
where the encoder and decoder are parameterised by θ and ϕ respectively [14]. The encoded space
is known as the latent space, which captures most of the information from the higher-dimensional
space. A function that can measure how much an output of the decoder differs from the input of the
encoder defines the loss function for classic autoencoders as being:
Lrec(x, ˜x) = ||x −˜x||p
(2.1)
where p indicates the choice of the norm, commonly the L1 norm (mean absolute error) or the
L2 norm (mean square error). The idea is to minimise the difference from the original input to the
generated one.
Variational Autoencoder
A Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE) is an extension of the vanilla autoencoder that regularises the
latent space representation [15]. Instead of outputting the vectors in the latent space, the encoder of a
VAE outputs parameters of a variational family (usually the multivariate Gaussian) in the latent space
for every input. The VAE then imposes a constraint on this latent distribution, forcing it to be a normal
distribution. This constraint makes sure that the latent space is regularised. Typically, the latent space
is regularised by introducing a Kullback-Leibler (KL) term in the loss function.

This thesis focuses mostly on cross-attention, which allows for considering the relationship be-

2.1.4
Transformers
A transformer model is a neural network that learns the context of sequential data and generates
new data from it. The first transformer model was proposed by Vaswani et al. [17]. The transformer

CLIP
CLIP [18] by OpenAI is a deep learning model that learns to understand and represent visual and
textual information in the same latent space. It utilises two encoders, one for processing and encoding
textual input and the other for visual/image input. The model is trained to create similar embeddings for
text and image pairs so that corresponding pairs have similar representations, and non-corresponding
very dissimilar ones, in a process called contrastive learning (see Figure 2.5). This is done by train-
ing the two encoders jointly to maximise the cosine similarity between embeddings of correct image
and text pairs, while minimising the similarity to the incorrect pairs. This can be done in a variety of
ways for instance using a triplet loss (CLIP expands this to a multi-class N-pair loss). Here a correct
pair, like a photo of a monkey eating, and a textual description like ”a monkey devouring a banana” is
combined with an unrelated text description or image.
The contrastive approach allows CLIP to effectively generate associations between images and tex-
tual descriptions, making it applicable for tasks like zero-shot classification and labelling, as well as
conditioning TTI models on text description. For these reasons, CLIP is widely used and serves as
an important step for popular TTI models such as Stable Diffusion and DALL-E 2. CLIP has, crucially
for this thesis, been found to contain harmful biases, and it has been shown that gender and racial

2.2
Diffusion Models
Diffusion models are probabilistic models designed to learn a certain data distribution by gradually
denoising a normally distributed variable. The forward diffusion process is the process of adding noise
to an image until a white-noise image is obtained and by learning the reverse process, images can be
generated from white noise.
Figure 2.6: An example of the diffusion process. Image Source [23]
The concept of deep learning diffusion models was created to learn and sample probability dis-
tributions to generate synthetic images and has been undergoing improvements over the years to
generate state-of-the-art synthetic images. Diffusion models succeed in solving problems of their pre-
decessors with stable learning unlike in Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) [24] models and can
generate high quality images which VAE models struggle with [25].
The extension of diffusion models into Diffusion Transformers (DiT) has also seen great results,
replacing the U-net backbone of a diffusion model with a transformer [26].

Latent Diffusion Model
One of the biggest weaknesses with diffusion models is its long training and inference times. This
was improved with the introduction of the Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) proposed by Rombach et al.
in 2021 [30] which uses a latent space instead of a pixel space and significantly improves the training
and sampling time of the diffusion model. This is done with a slight trade-off in degradation of image
quality. The LDMs first stage compresses perceptual details and remove pixel redundancy of an im-
age through an autoencoder, usually a VAE. Next, the semantic and conceptual features of an image
are learned from a diffusion model on the compression.
The LDM also allows the integration of conditional generation using text prompts. This is done
through an encoder, τθ which takes some condition(s), c and outputs an embedding combined with

2.3
Image Segmentation
Image Segmentation is a widely used technique for partitioning parts of an image into multiple seg-
ments using traditional machine learning methods or deep learning methods. Traditional methods rely
more heavily on methods such as pixel value thresholds, edge detection, and clustering algorithms to
segment an image [31]. Deep learning methods use models such as U-net [13] or Vision transformers
[32] to learn how to segment an image. To analyse certain areas of our generated images, object
detection segmentation is used to create masks of areas of interest, which will be discussed in the
following chapter.
2.3.1
Segment Anything Model (SAM)
The Segment Anything Model (SAM) is Meta’s powerful image segmentation model using encoder
and decoders to segment an image [33]. It has been trained on over 1 billion masks on 11M licensed
and privacy respecting images and is promptable, so it can learn new image distributions and tasks.
The model consists of 3 parts, an image and prompt encoder and a mask decoder [33]. The image
encoder consists of a pre-trained Masked Auto-encoder (MAE), which learns image representation
from images corrupted by masking [34]. The prompt encoder is a version of CLIP using its pre-learned
embeddings together with positional embeddings, and the mask decoder efficiently maps the image
and prompt embeddings and outputs the mask using self-attention and cross-attention. The overall
architecture of the SAM can be found in Figure 2.8.

2.3.2
GroundingDINO
GroundingDINO [35] is a state-of-the-art Detection Transformer (DETR) model that uses the transformer-
based end-to-end detector DINO [36] together with grounded pre-training, which allows for human in-
puts for more general text inputs to detect certain objects/aspects of an image. The GroundingDINO
architecture can be seen in Figure 2.9. GroundingDINO is a dual-encoder-single-decoder architec-
ture. Given an image and text pair, two separate backbones extract the image and text features, which
are then fed into a feature enhancer to fuse together the features using cross-modality feature fusion.
Then a language guided query selection module select queries from image features and are then fed
into the decoder. The output queries of the last decoder layer predicts bounding boxes and extract
the corresponding phrases.
2.4
Explainability
Explainability in AI involves describing and understanding the outputs of models, shedding light on
the inner workings of these systems, which might otherwise remain black boxes. Explainable Artificial
Intelligence (XAI) can be applied in many areas such as data explainability, model explainability and
post-hoc explainability [37]. This thesis focuses on post-hoc explainability by trying to understand the
method from a human understanding view of the models decision by looking at images. XAI is espe-
cially relevant for deep learning models, where inputs propagate through many layers, often with an
enormous amount of tunable parameters. As the size and popularity of AI models increase, so too
does the importance and difficulty of explainability. This is reflected in the EU-act, which categorises
some systems as ’high-risk AI systems’, for instance those with potential for discriminatory behaviour,
and these will face stricter regulations to be deployed within the EU [38].
Other methods of XAI also delves into visualising areas of interest from images. Many Class
Activation Map (CAM) models explain what area has been of interest in a certain classification task
[39][40][41][42]. These methods output a gradient saliency map visualising areas of interest that con-
tribute most significantly to the output of the model. This can indicate whether a classification works as
intended, or uses different aspects of an image to find its prediction. The outputs of a handful of CAM

2.4.1
Diffusion Attentive Attributions Maps
Explainability for TTI diffusion models has been investigated by visualising cross-attention, inter-
polated and averaged across multiple layers, to create saliency maps for LDMs, DAAM for short
proposed by Tang et al. [11]. DAAM explains which pixels of an generated images were attributed
the most attention when generating for a certain token. A visualisation of the cross-attention can be
obtained to get an idea of what the model believes is related to certain tokens calculated by what it
has modified during inference.
The word attention computes the word to word attention of prompts [44]. This method can be used
to make token-image pairs and find the pixels of interest for multiple layers using bi-cubic interpolation,
lastly giving a binary value ([0, 1]) for each pixel of an image. These pixel values can then be aggre-
gated over the spatio-temporal dimensions using the method of Hertz et al. [49] and interpolated to
create a cohesive heatmap known as DAAM. Combining these heatmaps for each token-image pair
will create a global attention heatmap.
From here, a threshold can be made to output a final heatmap, showing us areas of the generated
image that was attributed a lot of attention given the prompt. Heatmaps can be generated for certain
tokens, to see where each individual word has affected the final output. In short, the DAAM method
uses word cross-attention and spatial layout to compute attention heatmaps of an generated image for
each word of interest in a prompt. A visualisation of a DAAM heatmap given a prompt and generated
image can be seen in Figure 2.11.

2.5
Fairness and Bias
Bias within the field of fairness is often discussed in context of protected groups, referring to cat-
egories of people who are legally protected from discrimination under various laws. These groups
include age, gender, and ethnicity/race, the latter two of which are the focus in this thesis. A distinc-
tion is not made between ethnicity and race, and ethnicity will be used in context with the physical
appearance of generated subjects.
Obtaining and ensuring fair models can be tricky, and researchers are proposing many different
ways [50] [12] [51]. Measuring fairness is not exactly run of the mill either, and it is necessary to
define what is fair before continuing on. One way to determine Fairness in a Text-to-Image (TTI)
model is to ensure that for a large number of generated images, each group is represented equally.
For example the probability of generating a female looking person given a profession should be the
same as generating a male looking person for the same profession. This relates to the equality of
outcomes also known as independence in fairness metrics for machine learning stating that:

Research has investigated visual reasoning capabilities and social biases in the different open
source diffusion models and transformer language models [10] [52] [53] [54]. They look into espe-
cially the gender and skin tone distribution for generated images to check for social biases in popular
TTI systems such as Dall-E 2 and Stable Diffusion v 1.4 & 2. One concern is that the underlying
data structure may be biased, leading models to associate certain jobs with specific genders or racial
groups. For multiple models occupations like engineer are perceived as male-dominated, while library
assistant are viewed as female-oriented [10]. These papers investigate mostly whether or not there is
bias to be found within these models and how to find them in other models. But whether or not these
biases are damaging or should be mitigated for is not necessarily discussed or explored.
Apart from distributional bises, complex types of bias have also been documented using SD (Bianchi

by historical inequalities. Bianchi et al. [1] chose the United States for their study because it is ”the
country where the foundational training dataset was constructed.”. Rather than using real-life statistics
as target benchmark, they focus on measuring the amplification of biases. For example, they highlight
that the under representation of non-Caucasian software developers in real life is further exacerbated
in generated images.

3.1
Pipeline
For this project, a pipeline has been set up to easily create images from a prompt, analysing
the images using DAAM heatmaps, and finding relevant parts of the images using Language Seg-
ment Anything Model (LangSAM) has been set up.
The pipeline runs on Technical University of

the computation time. A prompt is fed into the pipeline, extending to the Huggingface StableDiffu-
sionXLPipeline, which generates an image given a prompt and a number of inference steps n = 50.
To generate DAAM heatmaps, the DAAM module needs to be hooked onto our image generation pro-
cess, the attention process can be followed during every step of the generation process. The DAAM
heatmap is then generated first globally for the entire prompt, and then a heatmap is generated for
every word of interest in the prompt. For every word of interest, a separate mask is computed using
LangSAM, an extension of Meta’s SegmentAnything in combination with the GroudingDINO Object-
detection model, to find masks of interest that can be explored. The complete pipeline can be seen in

3.3
Language Segmentation
In order to segment a large variety of photos, LangSAM [57] has been implemented in the pipeline.
The method is a combination of Meta’s Segment Anything [58] model with the GroundingDINO object
detection model [35] to create a powerful tool for segmenting an image based on a token. Another
work by Ren et al. also describes the combination of Segment Anything and GroundingDINO under
the name Grounded SAM [59], but this thesis will use the functionalities of LangSAM.
Figure 3.3: An example of how LangSAM works. Given a text prompt and an image, the object is
found and segmented from the rest of the image. Image source [57].
The idea of combining open-set detector models (GroundingDINO) and prompt-able segmenta-
tion models (SAM) effectively tackles the open-set segmentation challenge, which is inherently more
difficult than classification or recognition due to the dense nature of labelling and requires pixel-wise
classification of unseen labels while still identifying pixels belonging to known classes [60].
This will be utilised to quantify the precision of DAAM with the found segmentation of LangSAM,
and give an idea if the DAAM is generating other features in a picture based on a word or only what
would be expected. LangSAM is very good at finding certain objects, but can lack performance when
segmenting certain attributes of an object. For skin segmentation the github repository of Semantic-
Segmentation [61] has been utilised, which manages to segment the skin of generated people very
well, and allows looking into certain biases related to skin colour for certain words.
3.4
Bias mitigation
Similar to the work of Chuang et al. [12], a set of positive pairs S is generated, where pairs
are based on the protected group, using the following structure: ”a photo of a/an [protected group]
[noun/occupation]”. Experiments are extended to various prompt structures. The positive pairs male/fe-
male and caucasian/afro-american are used in combination to debias both gender and ethnicity with
a single calibration. The use of ”afro-american” is primarily chosen to reduce what is known as con-
cept bleeding, where concepts meant to describe a set of objects, like colour, also end up influencing

using the StableDiffusionPipeline (Base) and StableDiffusionXLPipeline (XL) libraries. To accommo-

and the positive pair prompts are tokenised and embedded, allowing the calibration matrix to be gen-

accurate segmentation using LangSAM.
For the base pipeline, the CLIPTextEncoders ”forward” method is subclassed, and the output is
multiplied with the calibration matrix before being returned. For the XL pipeline the entire pipeline is
subclassed and the ”encode_text” method overridden, where a similar matrix multiplication is done.
The XL model concatenates the embeddings of two text encoders. This creates four possible ways of
generating and applying the calibration matrix for:
1. Encoder embedding 1.
2. Encoder embedding 2.
3. Each encoder embedding separately.
4. The concatenated embedding of both encoders.
While option 3. and 4. will likely be similar; the projection ”budget”, that is, how much the embedding
is allowed to change, given by λ, is forced to be equal among both encoders in option 3, while 4 allows
the regularisation to focus on one encoder more than the other. In order to optimally estimate which
method is most effective at increasing diversity, all four are run 50 times using the prompt ”a photo of
a nurse” in a preliminary experiment. The results are shown in Table 3.2, with random examples of
images in Figure 3.5 (see Appendix A.2 for more examples). The resulting demography indicates that
either projecting on encoders 1 and 2 separately or after concatenation yields the biggest increase in
diversity, where 7 males are generated compared to 0 and 2, for encoders 1 and 2, respectively. All
encoder setups generate 2 additional black individuals. Since the concatenation method only requires
one projection, this setup is used for all experiments. Note that images with multiple people or where
gender or ethnicity was unintelligible have been left out, so rows do not necessarily have equal sums.

3.5
Prompting Strategy and Experiment Design
When generating images using generative AI, it was chosen to ensure certain properties in the out-
put, without biasing the output too much in certain directions. For the experiments in this thesis, the
focus was on generating images that attempt to depict reality, so that potential bias can be interpreted
straightforwardly. For that reason, prompts are designed to ensure realistic features in humans and
surroundings, in a style that resembles a photograph, and avoid art styles, like drawings, 3D model,
pixels, etc. This is done using the prompt prefix: ”A photo of ”, which is also consistent with the litera-
ture discussed in the thesis [1] [12] [51]. Unless otherwise stated, this prefix is applied to all prompts.
All prompts are also made in lowercase. For certain experiments, it will also be essential to keep
protected group identifiers, like ”his”, ”female”, ”Caucasian”, etc., out of the prompt. If the experiment
is based around looking into bias or debias related to gender for instance, then gender neutral words
are essential or bias would be induced into the experiments. In these cases, neutral words such as
”person” together with pronouns such as ”their” are used to specify something belonging to a person
of any gender or ethnicity.
Prompts and experiments are designed in a variety of ways to test different mechanisms of bias in
demography, attention, and quality.
==PAGE:33==
24
3 Methodology
One group of experiments have no protected group identifiers, meaning gender and ethnicity are
not specified in the prompt. The images produced are labelled into separate gender and ethnicity
categories, and the resulting population and attention distribution is analysed. In other experiments
with protected group identifiers, pairs like ”afro-american”/”caucasian”, ”female”/”male”, ”his”/”her” are
used to generate images with known group distributions, and the influence of the words is analysed by
attention scores of different words. Some prompts incorporate objects meant to ”divert” the model’s
attention, typically structured as ”a/an [adjective] [protected group] and [object]”. It is hypothesised
that this encourages the model to attend more strongly to the object rather than the protected group,
depending on how well it associates the adjective and the group.
Since the debiasing method does not distinguish between prompts with and without identifiers, i.e.
”female nurse” will produce male nurses as well (see example in Appendix A.13), debiasing is only
tested on the unspecified group. For many experiments, segmentation masks are used to test how
attention is distributed in various segments of the images.
3.6
Survey to Analyse Bias
A survey is created using Python and Flask with two main purposes. One is to test the imple-
mentation of the debiasing method introduced by Chuang et al. [12] on the new and improved SDXL,
while measuring potential impairments to quality. Another purpose is to see whether the bias found
by Bianchi et al. [1] using SD-v1_4 can be reproduced using SDXL, and assess the effectiveness of
bias mitigation in this context.
For the survey, an introduction screen is first shown, with three examples of images with varying
quality, gender, and ethnicity. This is followed by 66 images that are shown to each participant, the
first six of which are used to show participants the expected range of images and are not used in
the final analysis. The gender appearance categories range from 1 (feminine) to 5 (masculine), and
unintelligible, with the motivation of allowing subtle changes in gender to be recorded. ’Quality’ is
rated as ”poor”, ”bad”, ”fair”, ”good”, ”excellent”. The intro screen for the survey stated: ”Rate ’Quality’
according to the prompt ”a photo of a person” (i.e. photo-real)”. However, the option during labelling
was called ”fit of the ’photo of a person’”. The option was explained verbally to participants but should
be taken into consideration when interpreting the meaning of ”Quality” in the experiments section of
the thesis.
Ethnicity has 4 options: ”Caucasian”, ”Black”, ”Other”, and ”Unintelligible”, however, the debias-
ing method uses the positive pairs ”caucasian”/”afro-american”, creating a discrepancy between the
questionnaire and the debiasing categories (”black” vs. ”afro-american”). The discrepancy is unlikely
to be an issue, since individuals generated with ”afro-american” in the prompt turn out to be black.
”Afro-american” was decided as not suitable for a questionnaire as participants might wonder why
”black” is not being used and if they should distinguish between more than one ethnic group of black
people.

4.4
Experiment 2 - Emotion & Race
Is an emotion more notably related to one race than another? Is it more likely to create a person
of one colour more than another by injecting a certain emotion into a prompt? Perhaps there is an
underlying bias in mentioning that a person is happy than not. To test this, generated images with and
without certain emotions such as Happy or Angry is generated and the distribution of cross-attention
values within generations is explored.
An object that could divert attention is also injected to see if the model thought it was more relevant
than the person of the picture for that given descriptive token. The tokens flower and axe were chosen
as objects since they are some of the few objects that can be described by emotions (happy can

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
Bias related to both gender and ethnicity is evident in the outputs of the SDXL model, demonstrated by
the representation the protected groups in the generated images. This includes both complex biases,
from prompts like ”wealthy African man and their house” and ”a poor white person” in 4.37 observable
in individual images, and statistical biases that become apparent through group distribution. Exam-
ination of DAAM also reveals bias within cross-attention. In many cases it could be seen that the
difference between an object and skin were greater in the classically underprivileged ethnic groups
(”afro-american”) when injecting an object to divert attention (experiment 1 and 2). In experiment 1,
comparing light skin mask to a house mask, when examining wealthy, resulted in a lower cohen’s d
than comparing dark skin mask with the house mask. Even using the word ”black”, which could also
cause concept bleeding, still had a greater cohen’s d to house than ”caucasian” did. Compared directly
between the groups, a higher skin mask mean was found in ”white” and ”caucasian” than ”black” and
”afro-american”. It is worth noting, that all the means for this experiment is below 0. Visual inspections
of the heatmaps for the word ”wealthy” showed that most standardised attention values above 0 were
surrounding both people and the house in multiple cases (See Appendix A.4). Standardised heatmap
values above 0 would also more likely be reflected in light-skinned people than dark-skinned, which
is backed up by the results of the statistical test. Sub-experiment 1.1 shows the same pattern in the
heatmap values, but even though the houses are under the most attention between the three masks
given the person being African, clay huts still occur in the images. This could be due to the textual
embedding of the word ”African” and ”wealth” being far apart in the CLIP embeddings, which is then
directly reflected in the cross-attention. The overall metrics show that when looking at cross-attention
values within ”wealthy”, people with light skin are under more attention than people with dark skin.
Experiment 2 explores how emotions might affect the image when looking at ethnicity. For both
happy and angry, light skin sees more attention than dark skin. However, the effect size between
skin masks for the word angry showed a small effect size, suggesting that there is no great difference
in cross-attention between skin when generating for the token angry, while happy resulted in a huge
effect size. The results insinuate that there is preconceived knowledge in the model that white people
are generally happier than black people but both ethnicities are somewhat just as angry. Experiment
1 and 2 showed that the bias that is generally showed in the prompt also occurs in the cross-attention
values.
In experiments 3-9, multiple different results are found, with standardised attention. Most signifi-
cant effects are found for the ethnicity groups, where adjectives in general (”accomplished”, ”beautiful”,
and ”tired”) attended more to ”caucasian” than ”afro-american”. One of the few experiments with a
significant effect on gender had ”son” attend more to ”physicist” mask compared to ”daughter” and
”physicist”.
Experiment 10 looked into if attention values change when applying debiasing methods to the
prompts and how an adjective might change the distribution of groups. A keyword such as ”beautiful”
flips the gender distribution on its head, making a heavily male-dominated profession into containing
only women. It was also seen within this prompt that it was easier to convert women to men than
vice versa. Adjectives seem to have a huge effect on the outcome of gender given this experiment.
It is worth noting that the addition of ”confident” to the prompt ”a teacher and their coffee” generated
a less divided distribution of gender (34 males and 67 females). But debiasing this prompt made the
distribution more divided (18 males and 82 females), which was the opposite of what was expected.
An explanation could be that adjectives are very related to gender and the debiasing of this prompt

debiased the adjective more than the profession.
The standardised cross-attention values of the images before and after debiasing showed small
to no difference. No strong effect sizes were found between the images and their debiased versions,
suggesting that the distribution within heatmaps stay more or less the same. For experiment 11, using
”a successful doctor” it is clear that debiasing non-standardised attention. However, the values seem
to change in the same direction between groups, and the relations between masks remain the same.
This experiment also has unequal amounts of images in each group, as the groups were created by
labelling 100 images.
To summarise, bias is sometimes found directly in single images or for demographic distributions
across adjectives and occupations. At other times, the bias is more subtle, manifesting itself in how
attention is distributed at pixel level in different masks. This shows that even when an image gener-
ated by stable diffusion does not overtly display bias, the bias could still play a role in the underlying
attention mechanisms. This could potentially be harmful if attention is used to interpret images or for
zero-shot image segmentation as proposed by Tang et al. [11], even if it only occurs sporadically for
certain prompts. Given the widespread and increasing adoption of GAI, any bias is sure to have an
impact on numerous sectors, and the risk of bias spilling into everyday life increases. For future work,
more prompts should be tested to be able to definitely say that bias is reflected in cross-attention.
Having more prompts, apart from ”physicist and [child]”, with both protected groups included, such as
”a poor white person and a wealthy black person” could perhaps show a switch in adjective cross-
attention values when generating both people within the same image.
The pipeline setup turned out to be very useful in automating image generation, segmentation,
producing attention heatmaps, and applying bias mitigation for multiple prompts at a time. However,
even when running on DTUs HPC the process is relatively slow, exacerbated by both DAAM and
debiasing hooking to the Stable Diffusion XL pipeline. GroundingDINO ran on CPU due to missing
administrative rights on the HPC cluster, which also took up a lot of time as the GPU would be much
faster. For future work, optimising the pipeline is a priority. This will include employing code coverage
methods and optimising the process to only find appropriate masks, speeding up the pipeline. Addi-
tionally, object detection should run on the GPU.
The debias method is imprecise and cannot be tuned to specific distributions. It also hits too wide, so
that prompts like ”a wealthy caucasian man” can generate a black woman. Some groups are not debi-
ased at all for gender, and tuning lambda to specific groups and distributions would severely reduces
the generality of the method. Occupations can be separated from traditional figures, but requires an-
other step to the debiasing process. It can not separate hybrid groups with both groups present and
is likely easily over-fitted to the occupations and figures in use.
LangSAM was implemented to find masks based on words in the prompt, disregarding the prompt
prefix and stop-words. It could have been useful to use custom words depending on the prompt.
For instance for the prompt: ”a physicist and her daughter”, ”physicist” and ”daughter” was used to
find masks but the children in the photo were occasionally included in the ”physicist” mask. In this
case, ”adult” and ”child” might have given more accurate masks. The same could be true for prompts
using ”caucasian” and ”afro-american” as well, where LangSAM could be confused or exhibit bias.
Segmentation could also be split from image generation so that multiple words might be tested after-
wards. Using deep learning models in general for segmentation when analysing bias in deep learning,
requires some thought. In practice segmentation was generally found to be accurate, and using words
like person and teacher resulted in highly similar masks. SemanticSegmentation was very reliable in
segmenting skin. However, every generated image is different and the amount of visible skin differs
a lot from prompt to prompt. In some cases, skin only constituted a few pixels or was not visible at all,
for instance if facing backwards or wearing a uniform. A threshold for minimum pixel count or similar
could have been useful to avoid outliers when computing mean attention. An extension of the prefix
”a photo of ” with ”a face of ” was used in the survey to ease labelling and would have likely increased

Outlier detection was not implemented for either original images, debiased versions or attention
maps, as exploration of different prompts and high sample size was prioritised. Having generated
thousands of samples, with even more heatmaps and masks, detecting outliers would have required
a lot of manual labour. Adding additional code or programs to automate this process could have al-
leviated the problem, but this would require more time spent coding. Some manually labelled data
would likely be necessary for testing as well. If done incorrectly removing outliers could also introduce
new bias.
One simple way of detecting if an image has changed too much after debiasing (see Figure 4.38),
would be to use thresholds for metrics like correlation or Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM).
This would also require some tuning and testing.
There were no non-Caucasian to Caucasian transitions for debiasing, but a high rate of non-Black
to Black transitions. Since transitions were shown to degrade quality, the fact that Black and Other
were on average rated higher than Caucasian both before and after debiasing is puzzling. One ex-
planation is that this is bias from the model itself, in terms of ”objective” quality of people generated,
however that might be defined. Another could be bias in, for example, age, emotion, etc., already
discovered within Stable Diffusion by Zhou et al. [52], which could influence perceived quality. Yet
another could be own/other-race effects, one of which is that ”People often have unusual difficulties
accurately identifying previously encountered members of ethnic out-groups, relative to members of
their own ethnic group” [63]. If the in-group effect of identification also plays a role in quality, perceived
beauty or similar, this could play a role, since all participants were Caucasian.
It can be difficult to interpret what attention bias means or why it is necessary when bias does
not show up directly in the image, but in general the bias direction is as expected, meaning there
is likely relevant links between attention values and bias and the method can be used to quantify
bias or identify underlying bias. The debiasing method is difficult to interpret for many prompts, but
attention values gives some indication of bias, which is another argument for why attention values
might be a useful metric. For further research, it could be useful to analyse models that still generate
some form of biased pictures by using attention values as a metric for quantifying how biased a model
is. A proper method would be needed to setup using certain standard examples for what usually is a
biased prompt and then find the numeric bias as a function of the cross-attention values given prompts.
A problem for this thesis was to label generated images. DeepFace, a python library to identify
attributes of faces such as gender, race and age, was implemented for getting labels but turned out
to be imprecise, especially with race, for generated images. The model is trained on real-life images,
which is likely a factor that results in the wrong prediction for many predictions, as a generated image
almost always will look a little off to reality, and more closely in the pixels values of the generated
image. Because of this, the use of automatic labelling from Python libraries was scrapped as an idea,
and a more manual labelling method was used. Ideally, surveys would have been used for a greater
portion of the data, however, with the exploratory nature of the thesis, developing prompts continu-
ously to probe for new bias did not allow for this.
During the later months of this thesis, SD3 (Stable Diffusion 3) was released to the public. It would
be interesting to compare the methods of this paper between SDXL and newer models, to see if bias
still persists and the cross-attention values show the same pattern for the newer models. However,
the model is even larger than SDXL, and having already had memory issues with SDXL on the HPC,
setting up SD3 would likely require a lot of work. Another constraint is that, at the time of writing,
DAAM is no supported for SD3.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis set out to investigate if bias detected in SD [1], can be reproduced in SDXL. Furthermore,
our objective was to analyse the effectiveness of bias mitigation and if DAAM could be used for inter-
pretation and detection of bias.
This is achieved by building a pipeline for generating images, computing DAAM, and performing
image segmentation. We find that complex and statistical bias found in literature with earlier versions
of SD is also present in SDXL. In some cases reduced, in others exacerbated.
DAAM is computed for words to investigate whether bias also occurs in cross-attention values of
generated images that may not appear visually biased. Combining DAAM with language segmenta-
tion, shows multiple cases where cross-attention is not distributed equally on people, skin and/or ob-
jects within the protected group (gender and ethnicity). The most prevalent observation is that White
people in prompt pairs ”caucasian”/”afro-american” were favoured in attention mechanisms when look-
ing a positive words such as wealthy and happy, but also negative like tired. DAAM shows that even
when a word attends more strongly to one mask than another, this is not always reflected in the fi-
nal image. For instance wealthy attends more strongly to house than man, in a wealthy african man
and his house, despite the house not reflecting wealth. Interpreting cross-attention directly remains
a difficult task however, and directions of bias can be difficult to predict. It turns out that otherwise
unambiguous words like happy while on average being predictable can also pay a high attention to
seemingly random objects.
An analysis of CLIP embeddings to balance bias mitigation is done to look at current methods of
achieving fairness. The effectiveness of debiasing is thoroughly analysed and extended to SDXL.
The survey results indicate that the impact of debiasing varies significantly between different occupa-
tions and protected groups, showing no effect in some cases, reducing demographic bias in others
and occasionally increasing it. On average, quality is degraded, although increased in a single case.
Degradation is more severe for images where the method is successful in changing gender and/or
ethnicity, which could have ethical implications.
Using bias mitigation in combination with DAAM shows a clear influence on the scale of attention
values. However, the distribution of attention within different masks remains similar with and with-
out debiasing. Doing binary classification on CLIP embeddings, modern occupations, and historical
figures are divided with high accuracy, allowing balanced bias mitigation. The binary nature of the
classifier does not allow for hybrid groups, but is robust against various alterations of the prompt.
In conclusion, this exploratory thesis provides a unique look into bias and bias mitigation in TTI Dif-
fusion models from multiple perspectives, and provides a stepping stone in employing cross-attention
within fairness. Future work could look at different settings and newer models and propose more
robust methods for probing, analysing, and quantifying cross-attention bias.

A.12
Statistical test combined within-between-group
The purpose of these tests is to compare the difference in mean attention distribution for a word
between a pair of Masks for two protected groups (female/male, caucasian/afro-american). Boxplots
inside the red squares have a clear bias interpretation although not all are statistically tested. Note that
for the physicist Mask, the daughter/son Mask was subtracted to isolate the physicist, and the ”back-
ground” Mask is made by subtracting the lawyer. An illustrative example of the analysis is visualised
in Table A.6 and Figure A.16. The mean attention for [Word] within [Mask 2] is subtracted from mean
attention for [Word] within [ask 1] for each group. To statistically test if there are significant differences
between groups, a two-sided t-test is done. The null hypothesis is that the mean difference in attention
between the two groups is zero, implying that there is no systematic variation in how words attend to
different segments/Masks of images across the groups. This is visually represented in Figure A.16,
which loosely translates to assessing whether Slope A and B are equivalent. The motivation for this
kind of test is to see if words attend to different parts of images for different protected groups. Results
can be found in Table A.7 ’
