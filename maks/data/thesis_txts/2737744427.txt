Spatial Rain Rate Estimation from Radar Images
Using Deep Learning
Master Thesis
Emilie Sofie Lissner (s194341), Mathematical Modelling and Computation,
DTU
Supervisor: Roland L¨owe, DTU
Co-Supervisor: Thomas Bøvith, DMI
Abstract
Accurate rainfall estimates are important for numerous applications. This thesis explores the use of
deep learning to estimate spatial rain rates from radar images. While rain gauges provide accurate
but spatially limited data, radar imagery offers broad spatial coverage but suffers from accuracy issues
due to the indirect nature of precipitation measurement. The study aims to integrate these two data
sources to enhance rain rate estimations without direct dependence on rain gauges as inputs. The
geographical focus is Denmark, utilizing data from 5 radars and approximately 200 gauges. Radar data
is sourced from the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI), and gauge data from Spildevandskomiteen (SVK).
Multiple deep learning models, including Feed-Forward Neural Networks (FFNNs), Physics-Informed
Neural Networks (PINNs) incorporating the Marshall-Palmer relation, and Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs), have been developed and evaluated. These models are designed to predict rain rates from radar
reflectivity and other temporal and spatial features. Various model structures and loss functions have been
explored to optimize performance and minimize errors across all intensities and aggregation levels.
This study introduces a multi-stage PINN model using a weighted loss approach.
This model
first predicts the probability of rain and then the rain rate.
The model outperforms the traditional
Marshall-Palmer parameters with A = 200 and B = 1.6, improving average errors, aggregated error
measures, and errors across most intensities, except in rare cloudburst events. This model has also proven
more effective than the Marshall-Palmer relation on an external test dataset, underscoring its robustness
and applicability in a practical setting.

Part I
Introduction
Accurate precipitation estimates are crucial for various environmental and societal needs, including
severe weather prediction, urban and flood management, agriculture, and risk management. The
need for precise rainwater assessment is becoming more urgent as climate change intensifies the
frequency and severity of extreme weather events.
Traditional rain gauges provide accurate local precipitation measurements but are limited by their
sparse geographical distribution. Conversely, weather radars cover extensive areas but often suffer
from lower accuracy and inherent systematic errors. Integrating these data sources is essential to
produce accurate and high-resolution precipitation maps.
Current modeling approaches often rely on rain gauges to generate predictions. A standard method
is bias-advection interpolation, which involves adjusting radar estimates based on rain gauge data to
correct systematic biases. For example, the Mean Field Bias (MFB) adjustment technique applies
a multiplicative factor to radar intensities based on the ratio of rain gauge to radar measurements
[Thorndahl et al., 2014]. An alternative deep learning-driven approach is Google’s MetNet, which
uses rain gauge data as input in the model using a technique called densification, which merges data
assimilation and simulation into a single neural network pass [Merchant and Kalchbrenner, 2023].
Project Aim
This project seeks to bridge the gap between the high spatial resolution but variable accuracy radar
image data and the high precision but limited spatial coverage of rain gauge measurements. The
goal of integrating these two data sources is to develop a model that can generate spatial rain maps.
This strategy is intended not only to enhance the accuracy and utility of rain data but also to lessen
the dependency on traditional ground-based measurement stations by creating a model that can
operate independently of rain gauge inputs, unlike current modeling approaches.
The challenge lies in accurately estimating rain rates, a complex spatial problem that requires
integrating diverse data sets with varying resolutions, accuracies, coverages, and limitations. By
employing machine learning techniques, this project attempts to develop a model capable of trans-
lating radar images into accurate rainfall rate maps without relying on rain gauge data.
The key questions guiding this study are:
• Do machine learning models based on the Marshall-Palmer relation produce more accurate
rain rate estimates than models that do not incorporate this physical relation?
• How do different model structures and loss functions affect the accuracy of estimating rain
rates across various intensities and reducing aggregated errors?

• What additional features, beyond radar imagery, enhance the accuracy of machine-learning-
based models for rain rate estimation?
• Can convolutional neural networks (CNNs) improve rain rate estimation by explicitly inte-

Part II
Theory
1 Precipitation Characteristics
Understanding the different characteristics of precipitation is crucial for developing a model that
accurately estimates rainfall rates by integrating radar imagery and gauge data. This section explores
the various forms of rainfall and precipitation phases alongside their representation in radar imagery.
1.1 Rainfall Types and Radar Signatures
Rainfall can be classified into three main types: convective, stratiform, and orographic. Each type
exhibits distinct formation mechanisms, characteristics, and signatures on radar imagery.
Convective Rainfall
Convective rainfall is primarily associated with thunderstorms, characterized by intense, localized
precipitation. On radar, it can be identified as cellular or circular patterns with high reflectivity
values, typically exceeding 45 decibels, indicating the presence of strong, concentrated rainfalls
(Figure 1). These patterns are often short-lived, with thunderstorm cells developing rapidly into
small cores no larger than about 20 km in diameter, surrounded by areas of lower reflectivity [The
University Corporation for Atmospheric Research, 2012].
Cloudbursts are an extreme form of convective rainfall. The Danish Meteorological Institute
(DMI) defines them as precipitation exceeding 15 millimeters within 30 minutes or less [DMI
Kommunikation, 2017]. Cloudbursts are exceptionally intense and localized. They are typically
short-lived and can lead to sudden and severe flooding and significant damage.
Stratiform Rainfall
In contrast, stratiform rainfall is indicative of large, uniform areas of moderate to light rain stemming
from stable atmospheric conditions (Figure 2). This precipitation type is critical for water resource
management, given its widespread coverage and long duration, but at a generally lower intensity
than convective rainfall. Stratiform rain’s uniformity and persistence are its defining features,
reflecting a less turbulent atmospheric setting than the conditions driving convective precipitation
[The University Corporation for Atmospheric Research, 2012].
Orographic Rainfall
Orographic rainfall occurs when moist air ascends mountainous terrain, causing condensation
and precipitation [National Weather Service, n.d.]. Orographic precipitation may be detected on
radar images as patterns that align with topographical features. This phenomenon is significant
in mountain regions but less in flatter areas like Denmark. However, data from DMI [Danish
Meteorological Institute, 2010] indicates increased annual rainfall in southwestern Jutland. This
area’s distinct topography, formed during the last Ice Age and featuring the ”Jyske Højderyg,” a
topographical ridge [Houmark-Nielsen, 2023], facilitates this. When westerly winds interact with
this ridge, the uplifted air cools, leading to precipitation.

1.2 Phases of Precipitation
Precipitation can occur in various phases, including liquid, solid, or a mixture of both. The
precipitation phase significantly influences radar reflectivity and the accuracy of precipitation
estimates.
Liquid Phase
The liquid phase, rain, is the most common form of precipitation and has a direct and predictable
impact on radar reflectivity. Raindrops vary in size from tiny drizzle droplets to larger raindrops,
with larger droplets reflecting more radar energy to the sensor. This relationship allows for a relative
estimation of rain intensity based on the strength of the radar return signal. However, variations in
droplet size distribution within a rain event can introduce complexities in accurately quantifying the
amount of rainfall [The University Corporation for Atmospheric Research, 2012].
Solid Phase
Solid precipitation, such as snow or hail, interacts with radar more complexly than liquid precipita-
tion. Snowflakes, with their crystal structures, scatter radar signals differently compared to the more
uniform scattering by raindrops and fall much slower than raindrops. This results in generally lower
reflectivity values for snow, making it challenging to estimate snowfall rates from radar data alone
accurately. Hail, conversely, can produce very high reflectivity values due to its dense composition
and larger size, potentially leading to an overestimation of precipitation intensity [The University
Corporation for Atmospheric Research, 2012].
8/76
==PAGE:9==
Mixed Phase
Mixed-phase precipitation, involving both liquid and solid components (e.g., rain and snow or rain
and hail), presents a challenge in radar interpretation. The radar signature of mixed-phase events
is complex due to the particles’ varying sizes, shapes, and densities. This complexity can lead to
difficulty in distinguishing between precipitation types and accurately estimating their rates. On
radar, mixed-phase events may display as mottled or patchy reflectivity areas, combining liquid and
solid precipitation characteristics. The presence of both low and high reflectivity within a small area
often indicates mixed-phase conditions [The University Corporation for Atmospheric Research,
2012].
2 Precipitation Measurement Methods
This section introduces the two methods used to measure precipitation data in this project: tipping
bucket rain gauges and weather radars. Each method has strengths and challenges that may affect
the accuracy of the data collected.
2.1 Tipping Bucket Rain Gauges
The tipping bucket rain gauge consists of a funnel that collects and directs rainwater into a small
tipping bucket (Figure 3). As water fills one side of the bucket, it eventually reaches a predetermined
weight, causing it to tip over and empty. Each tip is recorded as a specific amount of rainfall.
This mechanism allows continuous precipitation measurement over time [Sarup, 2023]. While
tipping bucket rain gauges are widely used due to their simplicity, several factors can influence
their accuracy. High winds can cause rain to miss the funnel entirely or affect the distribution of
rain within the gauge, leading to inaccurate measurements. Another source of error is that the
bucket needs to be filled before the rain event is detected. This means that tipping buckets are
not useful for detecting drizzle and can not be used for estimating annual precipitation [Sarup, 2023].
One of the primary limitations of rain gauges, including tipping bucket models, is that they provide
local measurements. This means they can only measure precipitation at their specific location,
which may not represent larger areas. Despite the limitations, rain gauges are often considered a
ground truth since they are the most direct way to measure precipitation.

2.2 Weather Radars
Weather radars emit a pulse of microwave energy at a slight upward angle from the horizontal into
the atmosphere. When the pulse encounters precipitation particles such as raindrops or snowflakes,
a portion of the energy is reflected to the radar. This return signal, or echo, provides information
on the location and intensity of the precipitation. As the radar rotates, it completes a 360-degree
scan within minutes, mapping the precipitation around its area. The radar beam’s geometry means
that the radar imagery’s resolution varies with distance from the radar. As radar pulses move away
from their source, they expand, creating larger areas over which reflectivity is averaged. As the
radar beam travels away from the radar source, it widens and increases in elevation. This increase in
elevation with distance means that the radar is measuring precipitation at higher altitudes, which can
differ significantly in terms of precipitation type and intensity. This geometric aspect of radar beams
can introduce errors in estimating the height and intensity of precipitation, especially at longer
distances from the radar. Radar systems can be categorized into two types: those that emit and
receive pulses horizontally and dual-polarization radars, which can also emit and receive vertically.
Dual-polarization technology allows for the distinction between different types of precipitation [The
University Corporation for Atmospheric Research, 2012].
Ground-based weather radar systems have high temporal and spatial resolution in their mea-
surements. Despite their significance, these systems encounter challenges and limitations that can
influence the precision of rainfall intensity estimates. Some of the key limitations include [The
University Corporation for Atmospheric Research, 2012]:
• Cone of silence: This phenomenon is displayed as a donut-shaped gap in radar returns,
appearing because radar pulses do not encounter precipitation particles until they reach the
cloud base and pass through the precipitation layer. This effect results from the radar’s
inability to detect precipitation directly overhead due to the geometry of the radar beam’s
elevation angle.
• Radar Clutter: Radar images can be contaminated by returns from non-meteorological
objects, such as birds, insects, buildings, and airplanes. These unwanted echoes, known as
clutter, can obscure or distort the true precipitation signal.
• Indirect Measurement: Radar systems measure precipitation in the atmosphere, which may
not accurately reflect conditions at the ground level. This discrepancy can lead to differences
between the estimated precipitation from radar and the actual rainfall experienced on the
surface.
• Attenuation: The radar signal can weaken as it passes through intense precipitation. This
signal attenuation can result in underestimating the rainfall intensity, particularly for heavy
rain events.
• Phases: The radar’s ability to accurately estimate precipitation is also affected by the phase
of the particles. Different precipitation types, such as snow, rain, or hail, reflect radar pulses
differently, complicating the conversion of radar reflectivity into accurate rainfall rates.

3 The Marshall-Palmer Relation
Converting radar reflectivity (measured in mm6m−3) to actual rainfall rates (mm h−1) presents
challenges in utilizing radar data for precipitation analysis. This conversion relies on empirical
formulas, such as the Marshall-Palmer relation. Introduced in 1948 by J.S. Marshall and W. McK.
Palmer, this relation is still widely used in meteorology for estimating rainfall rates from radar data.

factor Z and the rainfall rate R through the power law relationship:

3.1 Interpretation and Values of Parameters
The combination of parameters A and B indicates different climatic conditions and precipitation
types, but they are also affected by factors such as instrumental limitations, variability in rainfall
scale, and the methodology used for parameter derivation [Kirsch et al., 2019].
The parameter A scales the relationship to match empirical radar observations with measured
rainfall rates. Higher values of A indicate a denser raindrop concentration, often observed in heavy,
convective rainfall events. Lower values might be more representative of lighter, stratiform rain. In
the study by Kirsch et al. [2019], a threshold of A = 150 was proposed to separate stratiform and
convective rain in Germany.
The parameter B reflects the relationship between radar reflectivity and rainfall rate, indicating how
the reflectivity translates into actual rain intensity. A lower B value suggests a more significant
increase in rainfall rate with an increase in reflectivity. Conversely, a higher B value indicates a less
steep relationship. The most widely used values for the Marshall-Palmer relation are A = 200 and
B = 1.6, as initially proposed by Marshall and Palmer. These values are typically used for moder-
ate rainfall intensities and provide a general baseline for radar rainfall estimation. [Uijlenhoet, 2001].
The variability of A and B can mainly be classified into three groups Uijlenhoet [2001]:
• Geographical Variability: Different regions may exhibit individual distributions of raindrop
sizes due to variations in atmospheric conditions, affecting the appropriate values of A and B
for those regions.
• Seasonal Variability: Seasonal changes influence the type and characteristics of precipitation,
necessitating adjustments to A and B to maintain accuracy in rainfall estimation.

==PAGE:13==
• Rainfall Type Variability: Convective and stratiform precipitation types have different raindrop
size distributions. Convective rainfall requires different A and B values compared to stratiform
rain.
4 Neural Networks for Rain Estimation
Neural networks are inspired by the way the human brain processes information. An artificial neural
network processes data in neurons connected by weights. A neural network consists of an input
layer x, multiple hidden layers, and an output layer. Essentially, a neural network can be viewed as
a function, denoted y = fw(x), that maps the input layer to the output layer:
fw : RM →RD,
(6)
where M is the number of neurons in the input layer, and D is the number of neurons in the output
layer [Herlau et al., 2021]. The complexity of neural networks makes them suitable for tackling
challenging problems with complex relationships between input features and outputs, such as
estimating rainfall rates from radar images.
The network is trained on data to determine the optimal weights using a suitable loss function. Due
to their complexity, neural networks often tend to overfit. Various methods can mitigate this issue.
The following sections explain the neural network framework in detail.
4.1 Neural Network Architectures
The architecture of a neural network, which defines how neurons are organized and connected, can
vary depending on the task. This section describes two common architectures: Feed Forward Neural
Networks (FFNNs) and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).
Feed-Forward Neural Networks
Feed-forward neural Networks (FFNNs), or fully connected networks, are the simplest artificial
neural networks where information flows in one direction only - from the input nodes, through
the hidden nodes, to the output nodes. Each neuron in any given layer is directly connected to
every neuron in the previous layer, emphasizing the network’s fully connected nature. The general
structure of an FFNN with L layers can be described as follows:
z(1) = h(0)(W(0)x+b(0))
z(2) = h(1)(W(1)z(1) +b(1))
...
z(L) = h(L−1)(W(L−1)z(L−1) +b(L−1))
y = f(W(L)z(L) +b(L))
(7)
where
• x is the input vector,
• W(l) are the weight matrices for the l-th layer,

==PAGE:14==
• b(l) are the bias vectors for the l-th layer,
• h(l) are the activation functions for the l-th layer, typically non-linear functions like ReLU,
sigmoid, or tanh,
• z(l) are the activations of the l-th layer,
• f is the final activation function (e.g., softmax for classification tasks or linear for regression
tasks).
This structure describes how each layer in a fully connected network computes a weighted sum
(inner product) of the inputs from the previous layer, adds a bias, and then applies a non-linear
transformation. The final output y is obtained after the data has passed through all layers sequentially
[Herlau et al., 2021].
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for Spatial Data Analysis
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are fundamental building blocks of deep learning methods
for tasks involving images and spatial data. Their design can manage the complexities of spatial
relationships within data. An important ability of CNNs is their use of parameter sharing, which
reduces the number of parameters within the network. This is achieved by applying filters or kernels
that move across the input image, identifying patterns such as edges or textures by applying the
same weights across the entire image. This approach prevents the explosion of the number of
parameters and ensures that the spatial relationships between pixels are maintained.
Convolutional Neural Networks can be divided into three main steps:
• Convolutional Step: The core of the CNN, this step involves sliding a kernel over the input
data (e.g., an image) and computing the dot product at each position. This operation captures
the presence of specific features within the data. Through weight sharing, the convolutional
step reduces the number of parameters, focusing on learning feature representations that are
invariant to the position within the image.
• Activation Functions: After the convolutional layer, the network applies an activation function
to each feature map. This step introduces non-linearity into the model, enabling it to capture
complex patterns and relationships within the data. Functions such as the Rectified Linear
Unit (ReLU) are commonly used for their computational efficiency.
• Pooling Layers: The inclusion of pooling layers aims to reduce the dimensionality of the
data further, summarizing the features detected in each convolutional layer. By taking a
single value to represent all pixels within a specific window (usually through max pooling or
average pooling), this step reduces the computational load and helps to prevent overfitting by
abstracting the higher-level features [Theodoridis, 2015].
Convolutional layers are used in various network architectures, ranging from simple tasks like
regression or classification to more complex models like the U-Net.

critical data points in specific applications, such as studying cloudbursts. To effectively model
such phenomena, it can be beneficial to modify the loss function to place greater emphasis on
extreme events. This project has explored various approaches, including conventional loss functions,
specialized weighted loss functions designed to address data imbalance and physics-informed loss
functions.

4.4 Handling Overfitting
Overfitting is a common challenge in machine learning. It is characterized by models that perform
well on training data but poorly when generalized to unseen data. Several strategies can mitigate
this issue.
Regularization
Regularization techniques such as L1 and L2 control model complexity and prevent overfitting.
These methods modify the loss function by adding a penalty term proportional to the magnitude of
the model coefficients.
L2 regularization (also known as weight decay) adds a penalty to the loss function proportional to
the square of the magnitude of the coefficients. The mathematical representation is:
J′(w) = J(w)+λL2∥w∥2
(17)
Here, λ is the regularization parameter controlling the penalty’s strength, and ∥w∥2 represents the
sum of the squares of the model parameters. This regularization type distributes the penalty across
all coefficients equally, which helps reduce the coefficients’ magnitude but does not necessarily
produce sparse models [Theodoridis, 2015].
In contrast, L1 regularization adds a penalty equal to the absolute value of the coefficients’ magni-
tude, encouraging sparsity within the model parameters:
J′(w) = J(w)+λL1∥w∥1
(18)
where ∥w∥1 is the sum of the absolute values of the coefficients. This sparsity can be particularly
useful for feature selection in high-dimensional data, effectively reducing the number of features
upon which the model relies [Theodoridis, 2015].
Dropout
Dropout is a technique used to improve generalization by randomly deactivating a subset of neurons
during each training epoch. This random deactivation forces the network to not rely on any
single neuron, thus preventing co-adaptations on training data. It simulates robustness by training
numerous thinned networks with shared weights, enhancing the model’s ability to perform well
across unseen datasets [Theodoridis, 2015].
Early Stopping
Early Stopping is a straightforward yet effective method to prevent overfitting by monitoring the
model’s performance on a validation set during training. Training is halted if performance on the
validation set declines or fails to improve further. This technique relies on the idea that continued
training after a model starts to overfit leads to a decrease in generalization ability. By stopping the
training at the right time, Early Stopping ensures that the model retains its generalization capabilities
without becoming overly fitted to the training data [Theodoridis, 2015].
4.5 Neural Networks in Practice
Designing an effective neural network requires a careful balance in the size and complexity of
the model to prevent overfitting with overly large networks and underfitting with small ones.

==PAGE:19==
This process often involves dividing the available data into training, validation, and testing sets,
each serving a distinct purpose in developing and evaluating the model. The initial phase in
designing a neural network typically involves experimenting with different architectures to identify
a suitable starting point. This experimental process is crucial as it helps understand how different
configurations affect the network’s performance. The training set is used to teach the model. During
this phase, the network learns to recognize patterns and features that correlate with the outcomes
based on the provided data. However, solely relying on the training data can lead to a model that
performs well on this data but fails to generalize to new data. To mitigate this, a validation set is
employed. The validation set plays an important role during the training process, allowing for the
fine-tuning of the network’s architecture and hyperparameters. This set acts as a proxy for new,
unseen data. By evaluating the model’s performance on the validation set, developers can make
informed decisions about adjustments to the model’s complexity, such as the depth and width of the
network, and tuning hyperparameters like learning rate and batch size. Finally, the test set is used to
assess the model’s performance. It is crucially not used during the training phase but is reserved
to simulate an independent assessment of the model under realistic, unseen conditions. This helps
to provide an unbiased evaluation of how well the neural network is likely to perform in practice,
reflecting its ability to generalize to new data [Theodoridis, 2015].

5.1 Radar Images
The radar dataset utilized in this project is obtained from the DMI Open Data platform, which

The raw data is stored in the HDF5 file format, with each radar image comprising 1728 by 1984
pixels. The pixel values range from 0 to 255, with 255 indicating pixels outside the radar’s range

6 Feature Description
The relationship between radar reflectivity and rain intensity is complex, necessitating the integration
of various features into our precipitation estimation models. While the Marshall-Palmer equation
provides a simple relationship between Z and R, its parameters are also variable due to geographical
location, seasonal changes, different types of rainfall, and phase changes. Additionally, radar
measurements are prone to various sources of error, which can further complicate the estimation
process. By incorporating a range of features into the model, we can more accurately represent
the complex interplay between radar reflectivity and rain intensity. This section details the spatial
and temporal features utilized, each selected for its influence on the precipitation dynamics and the
precision of its measurement.
6.1 Spatial Features
Spatial features capture a region’s geographical and physical characteristics. These features can be
integrated as channels in the input images, enabling the CNN to process a comprehensive set of
spatial information within a single image framework. This approach ensures that the model can
access the context that influences precipitation, enhancing its ability to make accurate estimations.
21/76
==PAGE:22==
Distance to Nearest Radar
The distance of a given point from the nearest radar station is included as a feature since this
distance influences the precision of radar measurements. As the distance from the radar site
increases, the height of the radar beam above the ground also increases primarily due to the radar
beam’s elevation angle. This means that the further the radar beam travels, the less precise the
ground-level precipitation measurement becomes. Incorporating this spatial feature allows the
model to adjust the radar data interpretation.
Coastal-inland Climate Classification
DMI has developed a Coastal-Inland Climate Classification to account for the varying climatic
conditions across the country. This classification assigns a unique value to each 1 km2 grid cell,
ranging from 0 (indicative of coastal areas) to 100 (representative of inland regions) [Henriksen
et al., 2024].
Topography
Topography influences local weather and precipitation patterns, even in relatively flat regions like
Denmark. The variations in elevation can affect wind patterns, temperature distribution, and how
moisture is gathered and distributed across different areas. We gain a more refined understanding of
these interactions by integrating topographical data into the models.
The topographical data for this project is sourced from the Danish Geological Portal, maintained
by GEUS [GEUS, 2019]. This dataset originates from 1997, with updates made in 2005-2006.
Elevations within the dataset are delineated by contour at 5-meter intervals, providing lower and
upper elevation limits. For this project, the maximum elevation value is utilized. This topographical
information is transformed into a raster format to ensure compatibility with the radar images. This
conversion process adjusts the topography data to match the spatial extent and resolution of the
radar imagery.

7.5 Data Splitting
The primary objective of this study is to model the Z-R relationship without focusing on temporal
dependencies and forecasting. Our dataset spans almost two years and includes measurements from
191 rain gauges. However, 19 of these gauges are located within the radar’s cone of silence and
were therefore excluded from the analysis, leaving 172 usable gauges.
Spatial Split
A spatial split strategy was applied to divide these stations for our machine learning tasks using
an allocation ratio of 80/10/10 for training, validation, and testing, respectively. This approach
resulted in 140 stations designated for the training set, with 16 stations allocated to the validation
and testing sets. We utilized K-means clustering to ensure a geographically diverse distribution
of stations across these splits. This helped distribute the stations in a manner that supports model
generalization by covering a broad range of geographical conditions.
Temporal Split
For the temporal split of the data, a strategy that placed the testing and validation data later in
the timeline than the training data was used. Specifically, the first full year of collected data was
used exclusively for training. The subsequent year was reserved for validation and testing. This
temporal split ensures the model is trained on a comprehensive representation of a full year’s data,
maximizing the diversity of conditions under which the Z-R relationship is learned. The validation
and testing phases, using data from the following year, are positioned to evaluate the model’s
performance on unseen data and to assess its temporal generalizability.

inside the pixel are assumed to average out the variations, making this the most feasible approach
to modeling. The models differ primarily in how they consider the inputs, ranging from basic

10.1 Model Type 1: Parameter Optimization based on the Marshall Palmer Equation
Model Type 1 employs a parametric approach to optimize global parameters A and B within the
Marshall-Palmer equation Z = ARB equation. The objective is to determine whether the data-derived
parameters align with the recommended Marshall-Palmer values of A = 200 and B = 1.6
Model Formulation
The model is formulated based on the logarithmic form of the Marshall-Palmer equation:
logR = logZ −logA
B
(21)
where Z and R are the radar reflectivity and rain rate, respectively, and cannot be zero. This
relationship can be rewritten as a linear model:
Y = aX +b
(22)
where Y = logR, X = logZ, b = −logA/B and a = 1/B.
Stability
Even though the model can be written as a linear model, estimating it using regression techniques
might be problematic. The equation has three logarithmic terms: logR, logA, and logZ. Taking
logarithms of numbers close to zero can lead to instability due to the function approaching negative
infinity. Therefore, R, A, and Z should be sufficiently far from zero. Robust optimization methods,
such as gradient-based methods, should be used to estimate A and B.
Data
The model is trained on a subset of the train data where both R and Z are positive to ensure the
logarithmic transformations are valid. The subset contains 337,733 observations. Any missing
values in the dataset are excluded from the analysis. The model’s performance is validated and
tested on the entire test dataset where R and Z are non-zero, with 33,451 and 33,847 observations,
respectively. This implies that only actual rain events are included in the evaluation.
Implementation
The model can be implemented in PyTorch using various loss functions, including MAE, MSE,
and Huber loss. These different loss functions provide flexibility in model training, allowing for
robustness to outliers and error sensitivities.
Limitations and Assumptions
The model assumes that the parameters are constant across all data points. The model is limited
because it cannot handle cases where R = 0 but Z ̸= 0, as the logarithmic transformation would be
invalid.

10.3 Model Type 3: Image-to-point Neural Network Model
The motivation for Model Type 3 is to incorporate spatial features explicitly, allowing the model to
utilize the full spatial context of the radar images. By doing so, we aim for the model to learn to
distinguish between different weather types, such as convective and stratiform rain.
Model Formulation
This model architecture builds upon the PINN-RAB model and is detailed in Table 8. This neural
network architecture combines convolutional and fully connected layers to process the input data.
Initially, the network uses several convolutional layers with increasing filter sizes, ranging from 16
to 64, to extract hierarchical features from the input data. Each convolutional layer is followed by a
ReLU activation to introduce non-linearity, a max pooling layer to reduce the spatial dimensions,
and a dropout layer to prevent overfitting. The final output of the convolutional layers is flattened.
Following the convolutional layers, the network concatenates the image features with the image
features. The network then branches into three parallel sub-networks designed for estimating R,
A, and B. The A and B branches are two linear layers and are reduced to a single output using
the Softplus activation function. The R branch is more complex, featuring multiple linear layers
interspersed with ReLU activations and dropout layers, ultimately reducing the dimensions to a
single output, which is log-transformed. The model uses the same loss function as the PINN-RAB
model.

10.4 Final Model: Two-stage-model
This model synthesizes insights from all previous experiments and enhances its applicability by
addressing all observations where reflectivity is detected.
Models based on the Marshall-Palmer equation are effective only when reflectivity and rain rate are
positive. Due to radars’ higher sensitivity than gauges, radars often detect precipitation that gauges
miss or detect insects or birds. This discrepancy underscores the necessity for a two-stage modeling
approach, which separates the processes generating zero and non-zero values, allowing for more
precise modeling of each. Initially, the model determines the presence of rain. If rain is detected, a
regression model estimates the rain rate. This method ensures the Marshall-Palmer relationship is
applied only under suitable conditions, enhancing the model’s utility and enabling it to function
effectively whenever the radar detects any precipitation.
Model Formulation
The model architecture is detailed in Table 9. This neural network model is designed to perform
classification and regression tasks using a shared set of input features. The architecture comprises
several branches, each dedicated to a specific task, and incorporates techniques like dropout and
ReLU activation to improve performance and prevent overfitting. The first part of the model is a
classifier that predicts whether it will rain. This classifier takes the input features and passes them
through a linear layer that expands the feature space to 512 units. A ReLU activation function is
then applied to introduce non-linearity, followed by a dropout layer to mitigate overfitting. The
processed features are then passed through another linear layer that reduces the output to two units,
representing the probabilities of rain and no rain. A softmax function is applied to these two outputs
to convert them into probabilities. In addition to the rain classifier, the model has three separate
branches for estimating the parameters A and B and the rain intensity R. Each branch follows
a similar structure. The input features are passed through a linear layer that outputs 512 units,
followed by a ReLU activation and a dropout layer. Finally, each branch has a linear layer that
outputs a single value corresponding to the parameter it is designed to estimate. For parameter B,
the branch output is processed through a Softplus function to ensure it remains non-negative. For R-
and A-branches, the output is directly taken from the final linear layer.

11 Feature Importance
Two methods of feature importance are used: Leave-one-out and permutation-based. These methods
are described in this section.
11.1 Leave-One-Out Method for Feature Importance
The Leave-One-Out approach is a straightforward and intuitive method for assessing feature impor-
tance within a predictive model. This technique involves systematically training the model multiple
times, each iteration with one specific feature omitted from the dataset. After training each model,
its performance is evaluated on a validation set. The validation losses from these iterations are then
compared to assess the impact of each feature on the model’s predictive accuracy.
This method allows us to identify which features influence the model’s predictions and which
do not. By observing the variation in validation loss, we can determine the relative importance
of each feature: a substantial increase in loss upon removing a feature indicates a high degree of
importance. Conversely, a minimal change or decrease in loss suggests the feature may be redundant
or irrelevant.

11.2 Permutation-Based Feature Importance
Permutation-based feature importance is a model inspection technique that measures the importance
of individual features by evaluating the change in a model’s performance when the values of that
feature are randomly shuffled. This method helps to identify which features contribute the most to
the predictive power of a model. The steps involved in this method are as follows:
1. Calculate Baseline Performance: The initial performance of the model is evaluated using a
specific metric.
2. Permute Feature Values: For each feature, the values in the dataset are randomly shuffled,
breaking the association between the feature and the target variable.
3. Re-evaluate Model Performance: The model’s performance is re-evaluated on the validation
dataset with the shuffled feature.
4. Compute Importance Score: The importance of the feature is determined by calculating
the difference between the baseline performance and the performance after shuffling. This
process is repeated multiple times, and the average performance difference is taken as the
feature importance score.

The impacts of different rain intensities on various applications can vary significantly. Some
applications, such as nutrient leaching assessments, require accurate cumulative rainfall estimates;
the total yearly rainfall is crucial here. In contrast, for applications like hydrology, urban planning,
and drainage systems, the more intense rainfall events are of greater importance due to their potential
to cause severe damage.
Impact on Modelling and Evaluation
Handling imbalanced data is crucial in model design and evaluation to ensure accurate and reli-
able predictions. Neural networks trained on imbalanced datasets can become biased towards the
majority class. This bias arises because the loss function tends to minimize errors in the more
frequent classes, leading to poor performance in the minority class, which includes extreme values.
Consequently, the network might overfit the majority class, resulting in a model that performs
well on common values but poorly on rare, extreme values. In rain intensity prediction, minority
classes, such as heavy rain events, are particularly important. To mitigate this issue, techniques
like oversampling the minority class, undersampling the majority class, and using weighted loss
functions can be employed.
Different applications may prioritize specific rain intensities. However, achieving good performance
across all classes is ideal for comprehensive and robust predictions. For the evaluation of models,
it is essential to assess performance separately for different rain intensity classes. This approach
ensures that the model’s performance on the majority class does not solely drive its accuracy. It also
considers its effectiveness in predicting minority classes critical for various practical applications.

16.2 Feature Importance
Feature importance analysis has been conducted on the Feed-Forward Neural Network model
that estimates R using weighted-mean squared error (FFNN-R/W-MSE). This model was selected
because it does not incorporate a physics-based loss component, which could potentially interfere
with the interpretation of the results. The outcomes derived from two distinct methodologies applied
to assess feature importance are detailed in the subsequent sections.
Permutation-based
The permutation-feature importance results indicate that all the features used in the model are
important for predicting the target variable. The feature importance values range from 3.9089 to
4.7812, a relatively narrow range. This suggests that all features contribute to the model’s predictive
performance. Higher values indicate greater importance, meaning that perturbing these features
leads to a more significant increase in the mean squared error (MSE). This implies a well-balanced
model where each feature contributes to the overall accuracy and reliability of the predictions. The
minor differences in importance scores suggest that the model does not heavily rely on any single
feature, which is often desirable as it indicates robustness and reduces the risk of overfitting to
particular aspects of the data.
Leave-one-feature-out Training
The leave-one-feature-out approach to training yields results markedly different from those obtained
through permutation-based feature importance. In this method, radar pixel values emerge as the
most significant feature. Temporal features also demonstrate importance, as evidenced by similar
improvements in the validation MSE. However, among the spatial features, only the distance to

radar and climate shows a noticeable enhancement in validation MSE. It is important to note that a
weighted loss function was employed in these analyses, resulting in the validation loss being on a
scale different from the performance metrics.

impact its performance, as the remaining features can still provide similar information. This sit-

individually. For instance, climate and topography might be correlated, with coastal regions typically
flatter than inland areas. Such geographical correlations could conceal the distinct contributions of
these features to the model’s performance, potentially leading to a misleading evaluation of their

18 Final Model - Two-stage model
Radars detect mist or other objects, such as birds and insects, leading to false rain detections. In
10.8% of cases, the radar detects something while the rain gauge remains dry. The two-stage model
addresses this by first implementing a classification step (stage 1) to distinguish between rain and
non-rain events, followed by a regression step (stage 2) to estimate rain rates. All model types
discussed could be used as second-stage models. The PINN with varying parameters is used as the
second-stage model.
The models struggled with accuracy on cloudbursts, characterized by their sudden onset. To
enhance model performance, we are introducing a new variable: the change in reflectivity Z, defined
as the difference between two consecutive radar reflectivity observations. This variable has been
standardized.

20.1 Standard Marshall Palmer Parameters Overestimates in General
The standard Marshall-Palmer parameters have been widely used across various locations. However,
these parameters may not always be suitable for general application. Relying on a single set of
parameters often short of providing accurate rain estimates. In our observations, the standard
parameters overestimated light to heavy rain intensity. Notably, these parameters performed best
during cloudburst events, except the sampling model.
In this study, most of the estimated B values were significantly higher than the standard 1.6,
suggesting that a less steep relationship between reflectivity and rain intensity provides a better
fit for the data. The estimated A parameter values also displayed two distinct clusters, likely
corresponding to stratiform and convective rain events. This differentiation underscores the need
for separate parameter sets for different types of rainfall. These findings are consistent with other
studies that have analyzed drop size distributions.
20.2 Modelling Extreme Rain Events is Challenging
Handling imbalanced data is more common in classification tasks than in regression. Many statisti-
cal methods do not specifically target extreme events but consider them outliers. In meteorological
studies, extreme rain events, although infrequent, are crucial to understanding due to their potential
for substantial damage. This aspect posed the greatest challenge in this project, prompting the
exploration of various loss functions, sampling methods, and model structures.
Extreme events are rare within the dataset and exhibit a dramatic increase in variation of rain
intensities for higher dBZ values, adding to the complexity. This study defined the evaluation
class for cloudbursts as events exceeding 30 mm/h. However, this class included a wide range of
observations from 30 mm/h to 120 mm/h, further complicating the evaluation.
Achieving accurate predictions for common and extreme rain intensities within a single model
presents significant challenges. A model optimized for predicting standard intensities often fails to
perform well with extreme events. For instance, in our experiments, while Model M1 effectively
captured the majority intensity groups of the data, it struggled to predict heavy rain events accurately.
Conversely, Model M2, adapted using different loss functions and sampling techniques, showed
improved performance under specific conditions. This illustrates a fundamental trade-off between
excelling at general rainfall predictions and accurately handling extreme events.
20.3 Combining Physics and Neural Networks
Machine learning is often criticized for its lack of transparency, interpretability, and tendency to
overfit data. This study explored using a physics-informed neural network (PINN) incorporating
the Marshall-Palmer equation. The PINN model demonstrated more stable convergence and less
overfitting than models that did not incorporate this equation.

==PAGE:68==
The study compared two approaches: one model that only estimated A and B in the Marshall-
Palmer relationship and the PINN approach that combined estimating both the rain rate and the
Marshall-Palmer parameters simultaneously using both a data loss and a physics loss. The PINN
model performed best. The PINN approach gives the model a structural backbone based on well-
understood physics. It allows flexibility to deviate from these equations when the data trends
strongly indicate a need for adjustment.
21 Limitations
There is a notable disagreement between rain gauges and radar data at lower intensities. Radars,
being highly sensitive, can erroneously detect non-rain objects and mist. On the other hand, rain
gauges have a lower measurement threshold and cannot register precipitation below 0.2 mm. This
discrepancy is significant as low-intensity rainfall events are frequent within the dataset, impacting
the accuracy of our modeling efforts. The multistage model employs a classification model to
determine whether radar detects rain, which poses training challenges due to quality issues in input
and output data. Furthermore, assuming that a rain gauge’s measurement gauge is representative of
a radar pixel, it is reasonable in uniform weather conditions, like stratiform rain, but often fails under
localized, intense phenomena such as cloudbursts. This mismatch can lead to underestimating peak
intensities, compromising data reliability for analyzing extreme rain events. Additionally, this study
excludes areas within the radar’s cone of silence, where radar data are sometimes completely absent.
This absence makes the conversion to rainfall rates unfeasible, limiting the model’s application
when missing radar data.
22 Future Work
Several potential methodologies were not explored throughout this project. Ensemble methods
could be used to combine models that are suited to different conditions. Additionally, techniques
such as quantile regression, which may enhance model performance across a broader spectrum of
data, were not implemented. Future research could greatly benefit from integrating these approaches
to improve the robustness and accuracy of models, especially in predicting extreme rain events.
Future research could examine whether there is a time lag between the radar and the rain gauge
data. This time lag could be influenced by distance to radar and precipitation type. In cases where
the radar detects extreme events either one time step before or after the gauge, the current model
configuration heavily penalizes these discrepancies, significantly impacting its performance on two
specific events. Investigating a model structure less sensitive to time lags between radar and gauge
measurements could reduce such penalties and improve accuracy in forecasting these events.
Incorporating hyperparameter tuning could further optimize model performance. By systematically
adjusting and selecting the best model parameters, achieving more accurate predictions is possible.
Further, improvements could involve incorporating additional meteorological variables, such as
temperature, to make the model able to distinguish different types of precipitation. These factors
could provide deeper insights into the dynamics of rainfall and improve the predictive capability
of the models under various weather conditions. Additionally, incorporating cloudburst data from

earlier years into the training dataset could enhance the model’s performance in extreme weather
events. Cloudbursts, both rare and highly variable, pose significant prediction challenges. Enriching
the dataset with historical occurrences of these events may improve the model’s ability to forecast
their intensity and frequency.
Finally, comparing these methods with the bias advection interpolation method, which uses rain
gauge data as inputs, would be beneficial. This comparison could offer insights into different
approaches’ relative strengths and limitations.

Part VII
Conclusion
This section summarizes our findings and implications from the analysis, addressing key questions
about the impact of using deep learning models for rain rate estimation and exploring the effects of
different model structures and loss functions.
Do machine learning models based on the Marshall-Palmer relation produce more accurate rain
rate estimates than models that do not incorporate this physical relation?
The physics-informed model, which incorporated varying parameters A and B, matched the average
performance of the direct rainfall estimation model but was better at predicting intensities above
6 mm/h, which are more critical for most applications. Moreover, the integration of the Marshall-
Palmer relation into the model also stabilized the convergence process.
Incorporating the Marshall-Palmer equation into the model structure has the added benefit of
increasing the interpretability of the outputs, addressing a frequent criticism of machine learning
models - that they act as ”black boxes.” This physics-informed approach effectively merges the
reliability of physical scientific principles with the adaptability and power of machine learning,
resulting in a robust model that reliably performs even on challenging external datasets provided by
Cordulus.
How do different model structures and loss functions affect the accuracy of estimating rain
rates across various intensities and in reducing aggregated errors?
Applications vary in their requirements for rain rate models. The proposed model performed well
in aggregated rainfall and across most intensities but struggled with cloudburst scenarios, which
were better handled by models using oversampling strategies. However, the sampling model un-
derperformed at lower intensities and aggregated rainfall. Addressing the rarity and variability of
cloudbursts in the dataset by weighting the data loss to emphasize high intensities proved crucial for
enhancing performance at higher intensities. Future work could explore different weighting and
sampling strategies or even ensemble methods to combine the strengths of various models. Also,
adding more extreme event data from earlier years could make the model good at these rare but
critical extreme events.
Introducing a multi-stage model enhances the translation of reflectivity into actual rainfall rates,
effectively addressing false radar detections. Unlike the standard Marshall-Palmer equation, which
directly converts all detected signals into rain rates, the multi-stage model processes detections
nuancedly, distinguishing genuine precipitation from erroneous signals. This approach results in a
more accurate and reliable model.
What additional features, beyond radar imagery, enhance the accuracy of machine-learning-
based models for rain rate estimation?
Theoretical studies have shown that seasonal variations, geographic location, and climatic conditions
significantly influence the Z-R relationship for estimating rainfall rates. Feature importance analysis
70/76
==PAGE:71==
confirmed the critical roles of temporal aspects (time of year and day) and spatial features (climate
and proximity to radar), with radar imagery being the most important. Topography emerged as the
least impactful feature, potentially due to its correlation with the climate feature.
For the refined model, we introduced a new dynamic feature: the change in reflectivity from
the previous to the current observation. Incorporating this feature significantly improved the model’s
ability to predict rapid increases in rainfall, which is characteristic of cloudburst events. This
enhancement highlights the importance of temporal dynamics in rainfall prediction
Future work could explore new methods for assessing feature importance and include additional
features such as temperature, which affects the precipitation phase, and wind characteristics, which
can influence the accuracy of rain gauge measurements. These additions could refine the model’s
accuracy and applicability across different environmental conditions.
Can convolutional neural networks (CNNs) enhance rain rate estimation by explicitly inte-
grating radar image data and spatial features?
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) were hypothesized to improve rain rate estimation by lever-
aging their ability to analyze spatial patterns in radar imagery, potentially distinguishing between
stratiform and convective rainfall types. However, training CNNs proved to be over 30 times more
time-consuming than the simpler pointwise model. Additionally, achieving convergence with CNNs
was challenging, limiting the scope for extensive experimental iterations.
Despite the theoretical advantages of CNNs in identifying distinct rain patterns - such as larger,
more uniform areas of stratiform rain versus small, intense convective cells - these models did not
outperform the pointwise model. The pointwise model’s fast training times and robust performance
made it more suitable for extensive testing and optimization.
While there might be potential in exploring different approaches, such as adjusting the crop sizes as
architectures to enhance performance, the pointwise model was favored in this project due to its
efficiency and the spatial coherence of its predictions. This coherence is attributed to the spatial
nature of most included features, which allowed the pointwise model to generate reliable and
accurate rainfall estimates across different scenarios.

22.2 Analysis of Temporal Alignment of Datasets
All events with rain intensity at the gauge exceeding 6 mm/h were selected to investigate the
temporal alignment. Time series data from the hour surrounding each event were extracted. The
mean values of rain intensity (R) and reflectivity (Z) were computed across all selected events.
If there were no temporal misalignment and the rain intensity was symmetric around the peak,
we would also expect the reflectivity to be symmetric. Since rain intensity and reflectivity have a
non-linear relationship, the shapes of the plots are not expected to be similar. Figure 48 displays
these mean values within the time window around the event. The plot shows a slight asymmetry in
the reflectivity plot, with a heavier left side, indicating that the radar detects some rain before the
gauge measures it. However, this temporal misalignment is minor. Ideally, the gauge data should be
aggregated to match the actual times of radar measurements more closely, but this optimization was
not prioritized in this project.
