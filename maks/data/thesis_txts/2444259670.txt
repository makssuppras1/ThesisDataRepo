Abstract
In this thesis report the intersection of additive manufacturing (AM) and injection moulding (IM)
was investigated. The resolution of mask-projection vat photopolymerisation (MPVP) was pushed
to the limit by manufacturing a functional surface topography into a complex, freeform surface. A
custom-build MPVP machine was used for this purpose. The primary motivation was the potential
of reducing lead time and cost of tooling, as well as achieving a more ﬂexible pilot production
method for high precision applications. The end goal was to incorporate Data Matrix codes into
IM consumer products by the use of AM inserts in a conventional IM setup.
In this research, arrays of prisms with di↵erent orientation were used to generate contrasting re-
ﬂectance directly in the surface. The prisms had a width of 98.02 µm and a height of 17.28 µm
and the total insert surface was 10.5 x 10.5 mm. A material selection process focusing on manufac-
turability, resolution and performance resulted in two resins: FunToDo Industrial Blend Red (IBR)
and Formlabs High Temp V1 (HT1). Thorough analysis of output power characteristics provided
a model for detailed control of resin exposure (E). On this basis, the curing behaviours of both
resins were determined. Methods from Design of Experiments (DoE) were used for further param-
eter optimisation. High values of E resulted in dimensions closest to nominal and best perceived
reﬂectance, but also lowest surface coverage. The produced parts were analysed by the use of a
variety of light optical microscopes (LOM). The best combination of coverage and reﬂectance was
found with low values of E. It was not possible to manufacture prism structures in HT1.
Flat top inserts produced by both materials were able to withstand 200+ shots with normal IM
parameters without failure. It was found, that post curing parameters had a paramount inﬂuence
on the IM performance. Cracking of surfaces occurred after insuﬃcient post-curing. For ﬂat top
inserts, both prolonged high and low power post-curing were able to prevent cracking. However,
for surfaces with prism array reﬂectance patterns, only prolonged low power UV post curing could
prevent cracking. High power post curing resulted in immediate cracking prior to IM. Analysis of
the surface cracks showed that they originate from surface defects. They occurred due to a state
of residual stress in the manufactured part which is due to a combination of a unique interlayer
bonding mechanism and di↵erential curing and subsequent shrinkage during post curing.
Keywords: Additive Manufacturing, Mask-Projection Vat Photopolymerisation, Injection Mould-
ing, Process Chain, Direct Rapid Tooling, Soft Tooling

Introduction
1 | Introduction
Additive manufacturing (AM) is one of the most rapidly growing manufacturing technologies and
is expected to grow at a yearly rate of 15 % to 35 % in the next decade [1]. Until recent years,
it has primarily been used for prototyping and non-critical applications due to its limitations in
both performance and production capabilities [2]. However, a combination of recent research break-
throughs and a dramatic reduction in machinery costs have sparked interest for industrial adoption
of AM and in particular gave rise to a whole new generation of business models based on mass-
customisation and on-demand production [3]. Recently, there has been a strong interest in using
AM as an addition to the established production chain in so-called direct rapid soft tooling (DRST)
[4, 5, 6, 7, 8]. The concept seeks to ﬁll the gap between low cost single part prototyping using AM
and full scale production typically by injection moulding (IM). This gap represents a large con-
cern for many companies because signiﬁcant investments are needed for tooling and setting up
production. This potentially leads to a lack of design iterations and innovative business models [9].
Incorporation of DRST can dramatically reduce tooling lead times and costs while subsequently
bringing increased ﬂexibility and on-demand production [8]. However, AM is commonly regarded
as a slow and relatively imprecise manufacturing method and is therefore often not considered as
a method to achieve tight tolerances needed for industrial grade tooling [10].
Recent work in the area of mask projection vat polymerisation (MPVP) has proved that the method
is capable of achieving µm-scale resolution [11]. MPVP manufactures entire layers at a time and
therefore has a relatively fast build time. However, consistent manufacture of functional surfaces
such as optical functionality requires high resolution in conjunction with stability e.g. high repeata-
bility and accuracy. Furthermore, for the process to work in IM setups involving high pressures
and temperatures, a suitable material must be found. The present project seeks to investigate the
boundaries for high precision manufacturing by AM and test the feasibility of using photopolymer
tools directly in a classical IM production chain.
1.1 | Motivation
The motivation for this project arises from multiple areas. MPVP technology provides a method
for AM of high resolution surface features. In the present project, the capabilities of the MPVP
technology are pushed to the limit by pursuing successful manufacturing of µm and sub-µm sized
features. Furthermore, the technology is implemented in a soft tooling process chain in conjunction
with conventional IM to determine if repeatable and accurate replication of aforementioned features
is achievable.
Earlier research has successfully made ﬂat surfaces containing data matrix codes by AM as well
as concave surfaces containing data matrix codes by subtractive manufacturing [12]. The methods
have hitherto not been performed on any freeform surface, which is still of interest as to emulate
the transfer of the data matrix code onto any type product surface. This project aims to inves-
tigate the feasibility of incorporating information Data Matrix codes into consumer products by
means of MPVP soft tooling and injection moulding. Speciﬁcally it is intended to generate injection
moulding inserts containing a data matrix code on a complex, double-curved freeform surface. The
intention is depicted in Figure 1.1 where the surface is converted into a ”minecraft” structure (as

2 | State of the Art
This section presents the state of the art in academic literature regarding the main areas of interest
during the presented work. First the functional surface related to this work is presented, followed
by rapid tooling with a focus on direct rapid soft tooling, and lastly AM with a focus on MPVP
and the achievable resolution.
2.1 | Optical Functional Surfaces
The present work is part of a project related to creating micro-structural optical functional surface
features to create the function of a data matrix code on the surface of a product. Micro-structured
functional surfaces have gained interest due to increasing use in products ranging from consumer
goods to life sciences. Functional surface topography can be linked to friction coeﬃcient, wear resis-
tance, self-cleaning ability, and optical functionality [13]. The latter being of signiﬁcant importance
in industry with the development of bio-mimetic colour generation due to surface structures rather
than using pigments, opening up the possibility of long term resistance to discoloration while being
pigment-free in nature [14, 15].
The manufacture of custom reﬂectance surfaces using microgeometry was explored by Weyrich et.
al., in 2009, where the bidirectional reﬂectance distribution function known from optical modelling
from both real world application and computer animation of light, were used to design a custom
reﬂectant feature on a spherical surface [16]. Using these ideas to create anisotropic optical func-
tional surfaces, a novelty method of creating arrays of prisms as the individual reﬂectance element,
and utilising their orientation to create anisotropy was developed by D. Li et. al. in 2017 [17] and
further tested and ﬁne tuned by F. Regi in 2018 [15]. In 2018 a ﬂat part with the data matrix code
was manufactured using AM, as well as a concave part with the data matrix code using milling,
created by E. Meinild during his MSc thesis [12]. The prism structure with dimensions as it was
created for AM in E. Meinilds work is presented in Figure 2.1, the dimensions of an array was
increased for manufacturability. The methods have hitherto not been performed on any freeform
surface, which is still of interest to emulate the transfer of the data matrix code onto any type of
product surface.
Figure 2.1: A 2D side view drawing of a single prism used in arrays to create the optical surface
topography onto an AM part with the dimensions used during E. Meinilds work.

2.2 | Rapid Tooling
The primary method of mass production of plastic components is through injection moulding. This
type of manufacturing process allows for a wide variety of plastic types to be mass produced [18],
e.g. advanced engineering materials, cheaper materials for disposable consumer goods and bio-
compatible materials. Conventional moulds used during injection moulding are hard metal tools
from tool-steels, that can endure millions of cycles with a low cycle time and low cost pr. part. One
of the main problems using metal tools however is the long production lead time at around 8 weeks
[19], and high initial cost of the tool [20]. This becomes problematic for companies, with a niche
type of product (100-10,000 parts) where the long lead time creates a break in pilot production,
slowing development and the high initial cost lowers the margin of proﬁt. Integrating rapid tooling
and prototyping into manufacturing has been estimated to lower the cost by 50-90% depending on
the size of production [5].
An increasing trend in the subject of rapid tooling is evident from search results on the subject on
”Science Direct” [21], see Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Trend of publications related to the search ”Rapid Tooling” on Science Direct. The
earliest responses should be considered with skepticism as some search results were not directly
related.
The possibility of utilising rapid tooling was already known in 1995 [22], and has since been used
to classify rapid production of tools for IM, as opposed to manufacturing prototypes or functional
parts that are commonly deemed rapid prototyping or rapid manufacturing [19]. The term rapid
tooling covers a wide variety of manufacturing methods, however in the presented work the focus
will be on soft tooling. Because the term ’soft tooling’ commonly refers to cast urethanes or epoxy
composites used as indirect tooling for manufacturing single units or low batches [19], the focus
will be on what is referred to as direct soft tooling in Figure 2.3 where the classiﬁcation of the term
rapid tooling is visualised.

2.2.1 | Direct Rapid Soft Tooling
Direct rapid soft tooling is tool manufacturing for direct use as a mould, rather than being part
of a longer process chain. Moulds can be manufactured using a variety of AM technologies for
a variety of injection moulded plastics. AM polymer moulds are less durable, but cheaper and
faster to make than AM metal moulds [23], AM polymer moulds lack the advantage of being able
to utilise conformal cooling due to low thermal conductivity. The primary concerns when using
polymer AM for injection moulding, are accuracy, wear and heat deﬂection with a trade-o↵in resin
constituents, generally showing less accuracy with increasing wear resistance and heat deﬂection
temperature and higher accuracy with worse mechanical properties [23]. Resin polymer moulds
were estimated to manufacture a 100 to 1,000 cycles [19] before breaking, but with an advance in
materials development for AM resins they were proved to be able to manufacture more than 10,000
[8].
2.3 | Polymer Additive Manufacturing
Additive manufacturing as deﬁned by ISO/ASTM 52900:2015:
”Process of joining materials to make parts from 3D model data, usually layer upon layer, as op-
posed to subtractive manufacturing and formative manufacturing methodologies.” [24].
AM is also referred to as 3D printing, primarily by hobbyists or prosumers, about low-end machines
marketed towards consumers, while AM is related to research and industrial manufacturing, while
predominantly concerning high end machines [8]. There is a multitude of manufacturing methods
utilising AM as single-step processes (multi-step would be processes followed by e.g. sintering).
These can be seen in Figure 2.4 for polymer materials. As thermal reaction bond dependent man-
ufacturing methods would often not be suitable for injection moulding due to issues concerning
thermal stability, this section will primarily concern chemical reaction bonding type processes,
speciﬁcally vat photopolymerisation.
In the ISO/ASTM 52900:2015, vat photopolymerisation is deﬁned as:
”Additive manufacturing process in which liquid photopolymer in a vat is selectively cured by light-
activated polymerisation” [24].

2.3.1 | Vat Photopolymerisation
This section will be a brief description on the state of vat photopolymerisation in general, followed

The ﬁrst technological advancement for AM began with the SLA in 1984 simultaneously in the
United States of America [25] and in France [26]. In 1986, Chuck Hull the American patent author
founded 3D Systems to make the device commercially available [27]. In 1988, the commercially

was granted to Nanoscribe GmbH [32] with the commercial Nanoscribe Photonic GT1 available in
2015 [33]. In 2015 the patent for the continuous liquid interface method of AM was created [34]
with the commercial Carbon3D M1 available in 2016.

achievable laser diameter at 140 µm with a minimum achievable layer thickness of 25 µm [35], while
the Peopoly Moai displays a laser diameter of 70 µm and an achievable layer thickness of 5 µm
dependent on the resin [36].

3.1 | Working Principles of vat photopolymerisation
This section will cover the working principles of vat photopolymerisation. Stereolithography (SLA)
will brieﬂy be introduced as it provided the foundation for MPVP. Lastly, the advantages and
disadvantages of the MPVP will be compared with common AM technologies. One thing to note
during this section is that MPVP is sometimes referred to as DLP, due to the projection device
used. The custom machine used during the presented work is referred to as the DTU DLP.
3.1.1 | Stereolithography
Stereolithography (SLA) is a method were a laser in the visible UV spectrum (380-405 nm) is used
for point-wise solidiﬁcation of photosensitive monomers. The diameter of the laser beam deﬁnes
the minimum resolution of the manufacturing process as this becomes the method of “writing” a
deﬁned geometry onto the surface of a container of liquid photosensitive monomers curing every-
thing in its path. Through computer aided design software, a desired part is 3D modelled. This
part is then sliced using speciﬁc software.
For many ﬁlament deposition modelling (FDM) printers and for SLA, slicing results in a batch of
G-code, which are machine position coordinates for either the feed as in the case of FDM or for
the laser movement in SLA. These coordinates determines the position of the writing head through
each layer (x & y-axis). When a layer has been solidiﬁed completely due to the laser writing, it is
subsequently lowered (z-axis) to allow for liquid resin to reach the surface which is then ready for
the following layer [43]. This layer by layer fashion of manufacturing is common for AM. When a
part has ﬁnished, it is in a soft green state, ready for post processing where it gains its mechanical
properties. The manufacturing principles of SLA compared to MPVP/DLP is illustrated in Figure
3.1.

A single constituent of a part manufactured by SLA is deﬁned as the voxel, which is the x by y
dimension of the beam (pixel), together with the z height deﬁning the volumetric element (voxel is
a portmanteau of volumetric and pixel) [45]. The z dimension of the voxel is closely related to size

depth of the light source which can limit the growth of a voxel. By deﬁning the parameters of
the machines inﬂuence on voxel growth, it is possible to tune the resolution of the manufacturing
method.
Part quality is determined by a number of factors: Control of laser radiant ﬂux, which together
with the photosensitive monomer deﬁnes the penetration depth. The resolution of the z-movement
which determines the layer thickness of the part. The shrinkage of the material is an issue con-
cerning part quality, shrinkage is more pronounced in acrylic based resins, and less in epoxy based
resins, which as a trade o↵cures at much larger exposure energies [43]. Shrinkage also occurs in
the post process UV curing, as the green part is subject to volumetric shrinkage while being fully
cured as the part is exposed to large amounts of UV light.
For quite a number of AM methods some geometries must be further considered when designing,
such as overhangs and unconnected parts, the failure of this type of features can be mitigated by
creating support features in the design. For FDM this is often enough for successful manufacturing,
but for SLA and MPVP another consideration is important, which is to avoid entrapment of liquid
resin inside the design due to closed cavities.

3.1.2 | Mask Projection Vat Photopolymerisation
MPVP, also referred to as DLP, consists of a light source and a digital micromirror device (DMD)
which creates an image by utilising an array of micromirrors. Each mirror in the device is driven
mechanically by changing the angle, which either reﬂects light towards the focal point, known as
the on or 1 state, or onto a light sink, known as the o↵or 0 state[46]. The amount and size of the
mirrors determine the resolution of the image in the focal point by acting as the individual pixels.
The actual printing resolution of the MPVP system is also determined by the lens magniﬁcation.
Low or no magniﬁcation (=1x or <1x) yields higher resolution but also smaller build area and
higher magniﬁcation (>1x) yields lower resolution but increased build area.
Colour image is achieved by utilising a colour wheel in red, green and blue, which spins with an
immense speed, blurring the colours together for the human eye, such that any perceivable colour
can be achieved for each individual pixel [47]. To achieve grey scale, the mirror switches between 0
and 1 states with a speed between 5 µs and 10 µs, if a larger amount of 0 than 1 states per second
is showed, the human eye will perceive the colour as dark grey, and vice versa. Even in what is
perceived as a still image, there will be a lot of mechanical movement in the DMD.
By using the mask based manufacturing method, the layer by layer manufacturing speed increases
compared to SLA, as an entire layer can be cured simultaneously instead of having to write the
image with a scanning laser [43]. This is done by slicing a designed CAD model into a stack of
images rather than G-Code, and projecting one image (referred to as a mask) at a time, curing
the entirety of the layer in one step, in Figure 3.3 a simpliﬁed progression of manufacturing with
sliced images during manufacturing is illustrated. The principle of MPVP/DLP compared to SLA
is illustrated in Figure 3.2.

3.2 | Photopolymers
This section will describe the mechanism of photo-polymerisation during AM and the materials
used throughout the presented work. When describing polymers it is common to di↵er between two
groups, thermoplastics and thermosetting plastics. Thermosetting plastics are a type of polymer
which solidify through crosslinking in a process called curing, primarily due to heat or radiation.
The present work utilises a speciﬁc type of thermosetting polymer called a photo-curing polymer
or simply put, photopolymer [50].

white areas being illuminated to cure the layer. The research team from Autodesk, investigated
both the e↵ect of using grey scale rather than solid black or white, as well as the growth of voxels

3.2.2 | Photochemistry
This subsection will brieﬂy introduce the working mechanism of the photopolymerisation process,
ending with its inﬂuence on the manufactured part. Followed by a section on oxygen inhibition and
photodegradation both of which add negatively to the mechanical properties of the manufactured
part.
The Gibb’s free energy of photopolymerisation is typically of signiﬁcant positive value [53]. This
means, that a signiﬁcant amount of energy from an external light source is required to initialise the
process. Because of this, so-called “photoinitiators” are added to the resin formulation to reduce
the required photonic energy input.
In photochemistry di↵erent photopolymerisation reactions
using photoinitiators occur, one of which is free-radical reactions. In free-radical reactions, the
reaction is initiated by excitation of molecules through free radicals generated by decomposition
of the photoinitiator. Dependent on the reaction types di↵erent photoinitiators are used. In this

3.2.3 | Commercial Resins for High Temperature Applications
There is a general consensus that the selection of high performance materials suitable for AM is still
limited and poses a barrier for further growth within the sector [9, 64, 65]. Simultaneously, however,
the general market for AM materials is expanding rapidly and manufacturers are launching many
new materials claiming better properties than the previous generation [9]. As a consequence, the
market for photopolymer resins is also expanding rapidly and in the recent decades there has been
a strong interest in developing materials for high temperature applications.
In 2005, 3D Systems Inc.
(the company that invented the SLA in 1986 [25]) patented a resin
formulation which was the ﬁrst successful attempt at implementing particles into a photopolymer
suspension [65]. Implementation of particles in the resin was sought-after due to the tremendous ef-
fects noticed in other thermosetting composites e.g. dramatically improved mechanical performance
(in particular the very high sti↵ness-to-weight ratio of carbon ﬁbre reinforced epoxies) or increased
thermal performance (as seen with ceramic reinforced plastics) [66]. Prior attempts had been un-
successful chieﬂy due to undesirable laser di↵raction o↵the particles, highly increased viscosity of
the resin, and a frequent need for stirring due to separation of components [67, 68, 65]. 3D Systems
Inc. used silica nanoparticles which to some degree circumvented the above mentioned problems.
Pure silica has very low thermal conductivity and thermal expansion (approx. 1.4 W/m·K and 0.5
µm/m/°C). Borosilicate (SiO2 with the addition of B2O3 and other oxides) has similar thermal
properties but its thermal expansion is higher (approx. 4 µm/m/°C) and the service temperature
is lower [66]. However, the maximum service temperatures of both materials lie 2-3 times higher
than normal IM melt temperatures. The general e↵ect of implementing ceramic particles into a
polymeric matrix is a dramatically increased thermal performance and stability.
Other commercially available high temperature materials for SLA/MPVP processes also use the
composite approach by adding particles. EnvisionTec E-Tool 2.0 contains barium borosilicate
glass ﬁllers [69]. It is speciﬁcally intended for tooling requiring high pressure and temperature
resistance. Liqcreate Strong-X is stated suited for IM and also use a non-speciﬁed ﬁller. This
resin is speciﬁcally marketed for its strength which is comparable to the performance of industry’s
leading dual-cure cyanate ester resins [70]. Royal DSM Somos PerFORM claims to produce
strong, sti↵, and high temperature resistant composite parts which again indicates the use of par-
ticles. Formlabs High Temp V1 & V2 are both speciﬁed as resins with high performance ﬁllers.

During the IM process high pressures and temperatures are obtained to ensure low viscosity and

to machine and post-process [88]. A number of commercially available aluminium alloys are made
speciﬁcally for injection moulding. They include QC-10, Hokotol, Alumec 89, and Alumold 500
which are all modiﬁed versions of AA7075 [73, 74, 75, 76]. In general, the combination of properties
for both aluminium and steel moulds render the injection moulding process very stable and makes
it possible to minimise or even neglect certain aspects such as elastic and thermal deformation
[89]. However, in the present case a photopolymer insert is incorporated into a tool steel mould.

When inspecting Table 3.2 several mechanical properties of the photopolymers di↵er largely from
the traditional P-20 tool steel. In particular, there is more than a factor 50 di↵erence in elastic
moduli for Formlabs High Temp V1 which implies dramatically higher compliance in the polymer
compared to the tool steel. There is also a factor 20 di↵erence in respective tensile strengths. It is
diﬃcult to compare the reported hardness values between metals and polymers due to unit/test dis-
crepancy. Metal and polymer hardness measurements are not easily converted so they can mostly
be used for comparison between metal-metal or polymer-polymer. However, polymers generally
exhibit much lower hardness than engineering metals [66]. Unfortunately, compressive strength
values are not given for the photopolymers. Compressive strength for metals are often similar to
their tensile strength due to highly isotropic behaviour. Polymers, on the other hand do not ex-
hibit this tendency to the same extent. As a general rule of thumb, polymers are 20 % stronger
in compression than in tension [66]. Also, it can be inferred that the majority of photopolymers
are quite brittle with an elongation at break of 1-2.4 % compared to the 20 % of the tool steel and
5-10 % of AA7075. Therefore, brittle failure could very well be expected.
Unfortunately, very little information is given about the thermal properties of the polymers. Of-
ten, manufacturers seem to compete on heat deﬂection temperature (HDT) as the main indication
of the thermal performance for polymers [69, 70, 80, 81, 83]. Formlabs High Temp V1 denotes
more than double value of HDT compared to E-Tool 2.0 and in fact the highest reported HDT
value. FunToDo does not provide any HDT but instead claim that the resin is tested to keep its
“dimensional properties” in the range of −45°C to +225°C. This will indeed be tested since that
upper bound is right in the range of melting for many consumer grade plastics. Therefore better
performance related to thermal strains are expected from Formlabs High Temp resins. Table 3.2
also shows more than a factor 5 di↵erence in coeﬃcient of thermal expansion between P-20 and
Formlabs High Temp resins. Therefore, the mould insert will expand and contract by a relatively
large amount during a heat cycle. Although no information is provided, it is safe to assume that
the thermal conductivity of the photopolymers are 2-3 orders of magnitude lower than that of the
P-20 tool steel [66]. Therefore, we can expect the insert to have diﬃculties with dissipating the
heat between injection cycles. Also, no information is provided on compressibility (Poisson’s ra-
tio) of the photopolymers. However, when comparing to other well-known thermosets like epoxies,
polyurethanes, or vinyl esters it could be assumed to be 0.35-0.4 [66]. In fact, such a high Poisson’s
ratio would contribute to increase its sti↵ness because of the lower compressibility compared to
both P20 & AA7075. However, because of the large di↵erences in elastic moduli the polymer is
overall expected to exhibit much larger elastic deformation.
In conclusion there are large di↵erences in properties between metal moulds and photopolymers.
Relatively large elastic and thermal deformation should be expected. High pressures could result in
failure due to relatively low yield and ultimate strength. Brittle failure mode should be expected.
However, because of high hardness values long service life should be obtainable. Better thermal
performance should be expected from Formlabs High Temp compared to the other photopolymers.
Ultimately, FunToDo Industrial Blend Red and Formlabs High Temp V1 were chosen for the present
project. FunToDo Industrial Blend Red was chosen due its low viscosity and previously obtained
results with micro features at DTU, and Formlabs High Temp V1 due to its very high thermal
performance and good balance of mechanical properties. These resins will be further described in
the following sections.

3.4 | Data Matrix Code
A Data Matrix is a 2-Dimensional bar code used to store and quickly read encoded information. It
consists of a rectangular or square array of black and white nominally square modules with width
and height X (Further described below). The Data Matrix code is read as a logic array with data
in both horizontal and vertical patterns.
Invented in 1987 by International Data Matrix, Inc.,
Data Matrix codes quickly became popular due to their fast reading times and greater information
storage capacity compared to standard 1-Dimensional bar codes [93]. Applications of Data Matrix
codes range from process chain information like product tracking, time stamp, batch number etc.,
to end-user features like entertainment or quick access to web-content.

3.5 | Polymer Injection Moulding
Injection moulding is a formative manufacturing process characterised by very high stability and
large production volumes. Injection moulding is a dominant polymer production method and ac-
counted for 130 million tons polymer production in 2016 [97]. Injection moulding has several key
advantages and disadvantages when compared to other polymer processing methods [98, 99, 100]:
Advantages
• Very high production rate (down to 3-4 seconds cycle time)
• Can produce a large variety of components from micro features on precision parts to meter
sized parts for automotive industry
• Components with complex shapes can be produced without need for post-processing
• Can achieve tight tolerances and grade A surface ﬁnishes
• Wall thicknesses of sub-millimeter dimensions are successfully fabricated
• The high pressures used in IM ensures a very high degree of repeatability
Disadvantages
• Expensive machines require large investments for setting up production
• Tooling costs are high and increase with the number of cavities and the complexity of design.
Therefore, IM is traditionally only suitable for high volume production runs
3.5.1 | Structure of the IM machine
An injection moulding machine consists of 2 main units: the injection unit and the clamping unit.
There are a number of principal parts critical to the process as depicted in Figure 3.14. Polymer
granulate is inserted into the hopper and continuously dispersed into the barrel by gravity. Here, a
reciprocating screw continuously rotates and moves the plastic granulates forward. A combination
of heating of the barrel and large shearing forces acting on the granules causes them to melt and
ﬁlls up the front of the barrel. The movable mould half is clamped to the stationary mould half by
the clamping cylinder.
Page 28
==PAGE:36==
3
Theoretical Background
Figure 3.14: Structure of the IM machine [101].
The screw is then rapidly pushed forward without rotation by a tightly controlled distance to inject
the melt into the cavity. The screw subsequently continues forward with a lower velocity to further
compress and pack the polymer. The cooling system is then enabled causing the polymer to rapidly
cool down and solidify. When solid, the movable mould half contracts and the ejector pins push
the part out of the cavity. The cycle is then repeated.
3.5.2 | Mould Design

dimensions of the cavity and therefore the ﬁnal dimensions on the part. Since IM is a replicating
process, the ﬁnal dimensions and tolerances of the polymer part are to a large extent dictated by
the mould dimensions and tolerances. Simple mould designs only use one mould half (typically the
stationary one) to decrease tooling costs. More advanced designs feature cavities in both mould
halves and possibly cores to achieve undercuts etc. Figure 3.15 is an example of a typical mould

3.5.3 | The phases of IM
The injection moulding process consists of a number of main phases as depicted in Figure 3.17. The
injection, packing, cooling and ejection phases are most critical for obtaining successful parts. Note
that a distinctive metering phase is absent in conventional IM machines and present in micro IM
machines because of tighter tolerances in dosage [103]. For conventional IM machines the metering
phase is embedded in the remaining phases.
Figure 3.17: The main phases of the IM process [103].
In the injection phase the screw moves with high velocity causing secondary heating and ﬁlls the
cavity rapidly to prevent unwanted freezing in the runner system or the cavity walls. Typically,
injection speeds for normal consumer grade plastics are in the range of 40-100 mm/s [100, 103].
As can be seen in Figure 3.18a there is a balance for injection rate and shrinkage. The reason why
larger injection rates result in larger shrinkage is due to the severe shear heating generated, which
dramatically increases the melt temperature during injection. This way, the plastic has to cool by
a larger ∆T, and therefore larger shrinkage occurs [104].

5.1.2 | Projector
The projector is a Luxbeam RS WQ and is positioned on the X stage as shown on Figure 5.1.
It contains a Texas Instruments™Digital Mirror Device (DMD), which is an array of mirrors with
individual mirror size of 7.54 µm2. One mirror is e↵ectively the size of a pixel when an image is
projected. With a resolution of 2560 x 1600 pixels, this means the array consists of 4096000 mirrors.
The size of the DMD is 19.3 mm x 12 mm not including the distance between each individual mirror.
The lens for the projector is interchangeable. The highest resolution is achieved with a 1x lens which
results in pixels of 7.54 µm2. Higher magniﬁcation lenses (2x or 4x) decreasing the resolution by a
factor 2 or 4, respectively. However, the build area increases by the same factor allowing for larger
parts to be manufactured. Light intensity (or irradiance) also changes with magniﬁcation - higher
magniﬁcation results in lower irradiance because the e↵ective build area increases (light and power
characteristics are further explained in Section 6.1.2). Natural density ﬁlters (ND ﬁlters) are used
to decrease the irradiance when using low magniﬁcation lenses without changing the overall image
characteristics (such as colour hue etc.). Two Thorlabs NE2R10B ND ﬁlters were used in serial
conﬁguration. The transmittance of ND ﬁlters is depending on the wavelength of incoming light.
In the present 405nm case, the transmittance is 7.779%. Therefore, the incoming irradiance is ef-
fectively reduced to approximately 0.6% which brings the overall intensity from W/cm2 to mW/cm2.
Calibration of the projector is done using FLIR FLEA FL3-U3-120S3C-C by GreyPoint™and the
software SPINNAKER SPINVIEW to adjust the focus by ﬁne-tuning the position of the projector
in relation to the borosilicate glass with accompanying membrane on top. The FLEA is held still
while the projector is moved with smallest possible increments until at ﬁnely focused image is seen
in SPINVIEW.

6.1.3 | Software Alteration
In Section 6.1.2, measurements showed a visible power decay.
Each time the light source was
turned on, a spike appeared and then decayed over time. Since LED’s typically exhibit non-linear
power characteristics it was expected that a power surge could explain the behaviour. Therefore,
another method was investigated by a change in the software control. Instead of turning LED
power on/o↵, the DMD-mirrors were turned on/o↵. Speciﬁcally, the function in the software loop
called “project images” was changed to test whether the power surge had an inﬂuence on the power
spikes.
6.1.3.1
|
Software Loop
After pressing start in the GUI, initiating the software loop, the software would ﬁrst call the build
plate to move to a position of origin (the focal point of the projector). This origin is determined
by the user before the process initialises in the GUI. After moving the build plate to the origin, the
repositioning loop starts. For measurement purposes, the build plate was not moved to the origin
as this would block the light measurement sensor access to the focal point.
The repositioning loop starts by moving the build plate away from the origin by a distance set as
the value called reposition o↵set. Afterwards, the build plate moves toward the origin by the same
distance minus the LT set in the GUI. In a manufacturing process, the repositioning loop would
allow the resin to ﬂow to the focal point and set up the following layer projection step.
Each “project images” loop would ﬁrst load an image onto the secondary monitor (the projector).
Then, it sets xset as chosen in the GUI for the layer type, after which it counts time until reaching
the amount of milliseconds chosen in the GUI for te. Lastly, xset is set to 0 (turning the LED o↵),
and then start the repositioning loop again. In brief: A new image was chosen, shown for a set
amount of time at a ﬁxed xset, then the light was turned o↵again before entering the repositioning
loop once again. A layer count process runs simultaneously to determine which layer the machine
has reached. If e.g. the layer count is at 0, the software progresses to build layers loaded as support
layers. When the software determines that it has reached the total amount of support layer images,
it starts the next loop by casting feature layer images, continuing the rest of the manufacturing
process casting remaining feature layers.
The hypothesis was to change the software, such that it would be possible to ﬁx the xset at the level
input in the GUI, and instead switch the entire DMD to a 0 state (the DMD working principle is
further explained in Section 5.1.2). 0 state is achieved by projecting a black image, which uses the
light sink in the projector to dim light.

Jacobs initially described an experimental procedure to generate a working curve for SLA setups.
Since the working principle of MPVP is fundamentally di↵erent from SLA a di↵erent approach is
developed. In this project a working curve is generated by controlled variation of E to known areas
and then measuring the thicknesses of these areas, respectively. A rather large number of E values
should be tested in order to get the best ﬁt possible. A way of varying E is to run a series of images
with a grid of e.g. 32 rectangles. In the ﬁrst image, one of the 32 rectangles is logically white
(= 100 % radiant ﬂux). Second image, two of the 32 rectangles are logically white (including the
rectangle from the earlier image). The sequence then proceeds until the last rectangle is logically
white. This results in a grid of rectangles that each received n
32E depending on the amount of
images n exposing it. The rectangle that received the most images (e.g. highest total exposure) will
be thickest and vice versa the for the image that received the least exposure. Contrary to normal
printing procedure, the working curve experiment should be carried out without build plate but
instead directly on the vat membrane.

6.2.4 | Key Findings
Several trends were noticed during investigation of the results from AM Design of Experiments.
1. FL covering power varies as a function of E. Highest coverage was achieved at values below
1.18 mJ/cm2.
2. The speciﬁc areas of resolved features vary as a function of E. Low E resolve mid & peaks,
medium amounts of E resolve valleys & peaks and high amounts of E resolve only valleys.
3. Defect surfaces carry signs of delamination indicating that the interlayer bonding is inad-
equate.
This can either be due to insuﬃcient curing and loss of mechanical strength or
excessive curing which renders layer attachment diﬃcult.
4. Increasing values of E resulted in better reﬂectance and more vibrant contrast.

Multiple inﬂuential factors in the manufacturing process must be considered when doing an overall
evaluation of the accuracy of the DTU DLP. First of all is the volumetric shrinkage during manufac-
turing, which generally would yield feature dimensions smaller than nominal. Volumetric shrinkage
for IBR is however advertised as being remarkably low for a photopolymer (As described in Section
3.2.5.1), and because of this, it was not expected to be the main inﬂuential factor for the presented
results. Second, is the e↵ect of voxel growth during manufacturing which has a large e↵ect on the
dimensions that were manufactured as has been thoroughly discussed above. The voxel rounding
could be solved by increasing the exposure but this led to other issues such as overexposure as dis-
cussed in Section 6.2.2. Third is the e↵ects of the mechanical system consisting of motor and linear
stage (as discussed in Section 6.1.1) which likely has a large e↵ect in the di↵erence between the 5
inserts. Other mechanical inﬂuences could be due to movement in the ball joint in the DTU DLP
(see the Figure 5.1). Ball joint movement was not observed during the project but the adhesive
force between the insert and the membrane could cause slight rotations, especially considering the
desired level of precision. The fourth key inﬂuential factor on the accuracy of the manufacturing
method was the projector. Between days of manufacturing the projector could become unfocused
either due to slight downward movement on the lead screw or due to movement of the setup around
the vat. This was especially true change of membrane where the projector would have to be recal-
ibrated to ensure proper focus. Even a slightly unfocused projector had an impact on the insert
features, even though this impact was not necessarily visible to the naked eye. The ﬁfth and last
inﬂuential factor for low repeatability of inserts would be due to the software. If some slight lag or
error was present during manufacturing due to communication between software and hardware it
would not be noticeable, however in Section 6.1.2 in Figure 6.3 there is evidence of this behaviour
that could signiﬁcantly impact accuracy during the manufacturing process.

6.5 | Sub-conclusion
1. The theoretical worst-case repeatability and accuracy of the DTU DLP was investigated by
assuming a simpliﬁed system consisting of a motor and a linear stage. The repeatability of
the system depended on the motion direction; 1 µm for uni-directional and ⇠15 µm for bi-
directional. The accuracy of the system depended on the distance the build plate travelled.
For a worst-case cycle, it was found to be ± 60 µm.
2. The light characteristics of the DTU DLP was investigated by a series of experiments.
It was found to follow quadratic power characteristics with a maximum radiant ﬂux of
Φmax = 4.096mW. Exposure was found to be a function of two variables, namely xset and
te. High values of xset resulted in unstable power with an initial spike and subsequent decay
by a quadratic trend. The decay could marginally be reduced by changing the switch-o↵
mechanism of the projector.
3. The curing characteristics of IBR and HT1 were investigated by means of working curve
experiments. A simple ratchet micrometer screw provided the best method of measurement.
Both resins were found to follow a Beer-Lambert relationship with a very high goodness-of-
ﬁt. Resin parameters, Ec and Dp, were found by logarithmic regression of the measurements.
There was a signiﬁcant di↵erence between the resins’ parameters.
4. The accuracy of the DTU DLP during actual printing was evaluated by a series of experiments
with either 1 or 10 layers of low LT. The accuracy was found to depend on the inputted layer
thickness. For the 10 layer experiments, deviations from target of more than 80 % was found
for low LT (0.625 µm) and generally less than 20 % deviation for 1 & 2 µm LT. However, for
the 1 layer experiment, no clear trends could be observed. Generally, the results from the 1
layer experiment exhibited very high deviations from nominal thicknesses.
5. The parts printed generally exhibited a structure clearly divided in 3 zones: (A) elongated
bottom layer, (B) support layers, and (C) feature layers. The support layers showed smoother
surfaces than the feature layers with a mean roughness (Ra) of approximately 7.5 nm and
standard deviation of 1-2 nm. Feature layers were generally rougher, with mean Ra values
between 21.5 nm and 26 nm (standard deviation between 8-20 nm).
6. A design of experiments was carried out to determine the right input parameters for suc-
cessfully manufacturing parts with prisms. It was found that FL coverage (% and location)
depended on E, and that defect surfaces carry signs of delamination which indicated that
the interlayer bonding was inadequate. Low values of E resulted in approx. 70 % coverage
(mids & peaks). Higher exposure resulted in lower coverage and only in valleys, but also more
vibrant reﬂectance. However, the most true to nominal dimensions were obtained by parts
receiving intermediate exposure. Furthermore, it was found that a threshold exists around
approx. E = 2mJ/cm2 where parts suddenly increase in weight. It was concluded that it
was due to diminishing irradiance and therefore insuﬃcient curing throughout the layer for
low E.
7. Four types of defects were observed during investigation of the manufactured parts. Resin
residuals and surface dissolution due to insuﬃcient or excessive cleaning. Membrane scratches
and indentations were replicated directly into the surfaces of the manufactured parts poten-
tially ruining the micro-sized prisms. Lastly, ringing around protrusions or cavities thought
to occur due to ripples created during manufacturing.

7.3 | Sub-conclusion
1. IM-test showed that ﬂat top inserts (both FunToDo Industrial Blend Red and Formlabs High
Temp V1) were able to withstand 200+ shots with both low and high IM parameters without
cracking. Flat top inserts experiencing 200+ shots did not show any signs of failure.
2. Post curing parameters were crucial to prevent surface cracking in the AM insert. High power
(9999 ﬂashes) could prevent surface cracking in ﬂat top inserts, however, prism inserts cracked
immediately after post curing due to excessive and rapid shrinkage. Low power post curing
for 60 minutes was able to prevent cracking in both ﬂat top and prism inserts after post
curing.
3. Analysis of the surface cracks showed that they originate from surface defects (protrusions
or cavities) in the AM insert and propagate hereafter. The cracks are of micro-scale and
show both acute and blunt crack tips. It was concluded that they occur due to a residual
stress state in the manufactured part that originate partly to the unique interlayer bonding
mechanism and di↵erential curing and subsequent shrinkage during post curing.
4. The width and height of prism structures were replicated with a low spread and deviations
from nominal below 30 %.
Corner radii were poorly replicated with deviations up to 73
%. Generally angles were replicated with good accuracy, except for insert 4 which deviated
signiﬁcantly.
5. Cracking in prism inserts could not be prevented in FunToDo Industrial Blend Red. Generally,
some surface cracking was found at less than 5 shots. Continuous replication resulted in clear
wear of the prism surface and reduction of reﬂectance.

Conclusion
8 | Conclusion
The following conclusion is divided into the respective hypotheses. Answers to each of the research
questions are ordered accordingly.
The overall response to the respective hypothesis is present
below the numbering.
Hypothesis 1: Prism array reﬂective patterns in µm scale can be manufactured on a
freeform surface by MPVP.
1. Formlabs High Temp V1 was found to be unsuitable for high resolution additive manufactur-
ing. Neither prisms nor minecraft structure were obtainable in the present project. Further-
more, the XY-indicators on the backside of the CAD-model (of mm dimensions) were faulty
as well. This study therefore supports the recent claims that there is an inverse proportion-
ality between high temperature performance and high resolution for photopolymers. Also,
the viscosity of the resin brought multiple production problems and signiﬁcantly reduced
manufacturing speed.
2. FunToDo Industrial Blend Red (IBR) performed signiﬁcantly better in high precision additive
manufacturing. Prism array reﬂective patterns were obtainable in ﬁne detail and with correct
reﬂectance. IBR was found to be a fast curing resin, and therefore high manufacturing speeds
were obtained.
3. It was not possible to manufacture inserts with surfaces fully covered by prism patterns. It
was found that the coverage percentage depended on exposure. Low exposures resulted in
approx. 70 % coverage but increasing exposures resulted in much lower coverage. However,
higher exposures also resulted in better reﬂectance.
4. Features manufactured on DTU DLP had a coeﬃcient of variation between 4-60 % (and
therefore low repeatability) depending on the measurement area. The primary reason for
this variation was a feature rounding phenomenon, which likely occurred due to low energy
development in single pixel rows and hence insuﬃcient crosslinking. Accuracy of DTU DLP
was also generally low.
Prism angles and heights deviated with up to 60 % and 80 %,
respectively. Signiﬁcantly better accuracy was noted for manufacturing prism widths, with
deviation predominantly below 10 % from nominal.
In conclusion, Hypothesis 1 can be accepted. It is possible to manufacture functional prism array
reﬂective patterns in µm scale on a free form surface. However, it was found that there currently is
a balance between accuracy of prism dimensions and surface coverage percentage. Accurate prism
dimensions were obtainable with very high amounts of exposure, however, this also results in a
signiﬁcant loss of coverage. The reason why low accuracies were reported during this project, was
because the authors were aiming to fully cover the surface topography in order to make the Data
Matrix code scannable.

Hypothesis 2: Prism array reﬂective patterns in µm scale can be replicated using IM.
1. Flat top inserts of both FunToDo Industrial Blend Red and Formlabs High Temp V1 were
able to withstand 200+ shots with both low and high IM parameters. From a qualitative
evaluation, the surfaces showed no signs of deformation or indications of cracks.
2. Post curing parameters were crucial for IM performance of the parts. The failure mode during
IM was surface cracking, and it could be prevented in ﬂat top surfaces with a suﬃcient post
curing (9999 ﬂashes high power or 60 min low power). However, the high power method
resulted in premature surface cracking immediately after post curing for inserts with prism
array reﬂectance patterns. This could be prevented with 60 min low power post curing.
3. Analysis of the surface cracks showed that they originate from surface defects (protrusions
or cavities) in the AM insert and propagate hereafter. The cracks are of micro-scale and
show both acute and blunt crack tips. It was concluded that they occur due to a residual
stress state in the manufactured part that originate partly to the unique interlayer bonding
mechanism and di↵erential curing and subsequent shrinkage during post curing.
4. During IM with high parameters, replication of the width and height of prism structures
yielded a relatively low spread with deviations from nominal below 30 %. Corner radii of the
prisms were generally replicated with deviations of up to 73 % from nominal. Angles were
predominantly replicated with lower deviations.
5. Cracking in prism inserts during IM could not be prevented in IBR. Generally, some surface
cracking was found at less than 5 shots. Continuous replication resulted in clear wear of the
prism surface and reduction of reﬂectance.
Hypothesis 2 had to be rejected. It was only possible to achieve the prism array reﬂectance patterns
in IBR, which failed during the initial 5 shots due to surface cracking.
The prism arrays can
be replicated using polymer AM mould inserts, but because of its very limited durability it was
determined insuﬃcient to accept the hypothesis.

Future Works
9 | Future Works
1. The exposure model from Section 6.1.2 could be coupled with generalised models for resin
curing behaviour. This way, the optimal parameters for any resin within the same primary
chemical composition can be modelled without the need for carrying out extensive experi-
mental work.
2. The e↵ects of kinetics on the curing behaviour of fast-curing resins should be investigated. In
the present project, the inﬂuence of variation in radiant ﬂux compared to curing degree was
not thoroughly investigated.
3. Experiments with other high temperature materials should be carried out to ﬁnd a good
balance between achievable resolution and high temperature performance.
4. A full design of experiments should be carried out to compare the inﬂuence of post curing and
IM parameters on IM performance such as tool life, degree of replicability etc. Furthermore,
the impact of elevated temperatures during printing and post-curing on mechanical properties
and suitability for injection moulding should be considered.
5. The threshold value that results in signiﬁcant weight gain as described in Section 6.2.2 should
be further studied.
6. Various cleaning methods and/or agents could be investigated to decrease the amount of
residuals and defects present on the AM parts.
7. Methods of grey scale explored in Section 3.2.1 should be further studied to determine whether
lowering exposure in the bulk of the structure and increasing exposure for single- and multi-
pixel lines could reduce the rounding experienced in the repeatability experiments in Section
6.4.
