Summary
The objective of this project is to design, implement and test a microwave radiometer
for C-band which will be used to test advanced multiple-beam radiometer antennas
made by ESA providing high resolution for ocean observation. The radiometer will
be designed as a single-sideband Superheterodyne receiver and must be run in total
power mode. Two different designs approaches are made, where design 1 involves
using transistor and Schottky diode to design low noise amplifier (RF-, and IF-gain)
and mixer and design 2 involves using ready-made low noise amplifier (RF-, and IF-
gain) and mixer. In other words, design 2 includes as many off-the-shelf components as
possible. All the passive blocks in the two designs are to be designed and implemented
using microwave strip-line. To begin with, design 1 is attempted, but because it takes
longer time than expected, the design 2 approach is made. The implementation of
the final design is comprised of three different modules as: Front-end module, RF-
module and IF-module. However due to time constrains and unavailability of a proper
isolator, the final radiometer design has not yet been completed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The oceanographic community has a strong interest in high spatial resolution. Tra-
ditional radiometers can only measure approximately until around 100 km from the
coast whereas it is desired to measure up to 15 km. ESA (European Space Agency)
studied advanced Multiple-Beam Radiometers and designed radiometer antennas
which provide high resolution and high sensitivity for accurate ocean observation
from space. The brightness temperature of land is much higher than the brightness
temperature of ocean due to the black-body radiation. This difference makes it easy
to design a system to detect ocean from land. To demonstrate how well the antennas
are designed, there is a need for a receiver. In my project, I have to design and im-
plement a microwave radiometer for C-band. Since it is only for demonstration, the

2.1
The radiometer receiver
A radiometer is a very sensitive microwave receiver, the objective of which is to
measure power.
According to the Rayleigh-Jeans law, the power is expressed in
terms of an equivalent temperature for many microwave applications, such as remote
sensing of the Earth’s surface. This temperature is due to an object’s black-body
that radiates the same power called ”brightness temperature”, TB.
2.1.1
The brightness temperature
Consider a measurement situation illustrated in figure (2.1) where an antenna is
pointed towards an object having a certain temperature brightness TB. The power
which will be measured is expressed in terms of the antenna’s temperature, TA. The
purpose of the measurement is to relate the antenna temperature to the brightness
temperature. In other words, the main goal of the measurement is to find a suffi-
cient resolution and accuracy between the antenna temperature and the brightness
temperature.

2.1.3
Calibration
The output power equation in (2.1) which links the input and output quantities may
not be stable over a long period of time, due to G, and TN. This is because these
parameters represent specifications for active components like amplifier or mixer, and
they are also dependent on supply voltage and physical temperature. The material
for fabrication, for instance microstrip technology is lossy, and will add some noises
to the system. As mentioned above, the goal is to measure the voltage at the output
which corresponds to the antenna temperature. Therefore, it leads to the necessity
for calibration to minimize the fluctuation from gain and noise temperature.
How to calibrate
As mentioned earlier, the radiometer needs to be calibrated; therefore, there is need
for a accurate noise diode with a variable output where the output range is 0-300
K. The most obvious solution is to use a cooled microwave load where its physical
temperature is equal to the noise temperature and which will be measured.
The
simplest and cheapest setup is shown in figure (2.5) where the microwave load is
placed in a freezer and a motor ensures a uniform temperature and then is measured
by a thermometer.

2.2
Radiometer receiver types
A radiometer can be designed in different schematics. The most common are Direct
conversion or Superheterodyne.
The two types of receiver will be examined and
described below.
2.2.1
Direct conversion receiver
The Direct conversion receiver, shown in figure (2.8), as the name reveals, sets the
oscillator at the same as RF frequency, which is converted directly to baseband. It
means that fIF is set to zero, and therefore the advantage of this type receiver is
that it is simpler and less costly, since there is no IF-gain and bandpass IF-filter.
Another advantage is that, there is no image frequency, since the mixer difference
frequency is zero, but it requires reciprocally a very high stable LO, especially for
high RF frequency. In direct conversion receiver, all amplification takes place at the
input frequency and all the selectivity is determined by filters in the same RF range.
Figure 2.8: Block diagram of a Direct conversion receiver[Poz12].
2.2.2
Superheterydone receiver
A Superheterydone receiver, as is shown in figure (2.9), is the most popular type
of receiver used today for broadcast radio and television, radar systems and data
communication systems. Since the power level from the antenna may be as low as
-100 to -120 dBm, the receiver may be able to provide gain as 100 to 120 dB. In order
to receive the desired signal while rejecting all the other channels, a bandpass filter is
needed. The image response must be prevented from reaching the mixer by using a
bandpass filter around the desired RF frequency in front of the receiver since once the
image response is mixed with the desired RF frequency there is no way to separate
them from each other. To avoid instabilities and possible oscillation the gain must
be spread over the RF, IF and baseband stages. Usually the gain must be kept to 50
- 60 dB at any one frequency band. The received signal is up-, or down-converted to
an IF frequency for processing. As figure (2.10) shows, the mixing product has upper
and lower sideband. When using the lower sideband, the LO frequency is given by:

Double-, and single-sideband
The Superheterodyne can either be designed as single-sideband (SSB), where an RF
preamplifier is used or as a double-sideband (DSB), where no RF preamplifier is
used. The choice of DSB is selected when such amplifiers are unavailable or are too
expensive. For the DSB receiver, the receiver noise figure is determined by the mixer
in combination with the first IF-gain, whereas the receiver noise figure for SSB is
determined by the RF preamplifier. The noise temperature for DSB is 3dB lower
than SSB. For DSB, the input bandwidth is twice as the IF bandwidth, the local
oscillator is equal to the center frequency of input band and IF frequency is from DC
up to bandwidth.

2.3.1
Power Dividers and Directional Couplers
Power dividers and directional couplers are passive microwave components which are
used for power division or power combining. A power divider consists of one input
port and two or more output ports whereas a power combiner has two or more input
signal and combines them at one output port. A directional coupler or hybrid is a
4 port network which can be designed to obtain either 90o or 180o power division
between the output ports with equal power division.
The Quadrature (90o) Hybrid
The Quadrature Hybrid is 3 dB directional couplers with 90o phase difference in the
outputs. It consists of 4 ports where power enters at port 1 and is evenly divided
between port 2 and 3, with a 90o phase shift between these outputs. No power is
coupled to port 4 since the port is an isolated port. The Quadrature (90o) Hybrid
characterizes by 4 quantities: coupling, directivity, isolation and insertion loss. The
basic operation of the directional coupler is illustrated in figure (2.11), which shows
the power flow. The coupling is the fraction of input power that is coupled at port 3
with the coupling factor |S13 |2 = β 2. The directivity is used to indicate the ability to
isolate coupled and uncoupled ports. For an ideal directional coupler the directivity
is infinite. The isolation is used to measure the delivered power to the isolated port
(uncoupled port, port 4) . For an ideal directional coupler the isolation is infinite.
The insertion loss is used to measure the delivered power at port 2 (Through port).

2.3.2
Filters
Filters are two-port network and passive devices used to control the frequency re-
sponse by allowing frequencies within the passband and attenuating within the stop-
band. The typical filter response include lowpass, highpass, bandpass and bandstop.
Typical lowpass filter prototypes are either maximally flat or equal-ripple, which
reveals, as the names imply, that the frequency response is totally flat or rippled,
respectively.
Insertion Loss Method
To design filters the insertion loss method, which is flexible and accurate, is used. It
is based on network synthesis techniques which allow the possibility of designing a
filter with a specific type frequency response. This method starts with the design
of a lowpass filter prototype, which is normalized in terms of impedance and cutoff
frequency. Transformations are then used to convert from the normalized to have the
desired frequency response, cutoff frequency and impedance level. The design process
is illustrated in figure (2.16).
Figure 2.16: Design process by the insertion loss method[Poz12].
The image parameter method
The image parameter method is a method used for designing bandpass- and stopband-
filters and involves the specification of passband and stopband characteristics for a
cascade of simple two-port network.

Amplification is an important function in wireless receivers and transmitters. For

Balanced configuration
To achieve input and output matching, the balanced configuration as shown in figure
(2.20) is used by having two 90o hybrid couplers to cancel input and output reflections
from two identical amplifiers. The first coupler divides the input signal into two signals
which drive the two amplifiers with the same amplitude but a 90o phase difference.
The second coupler recombines the amplifier outputs. Due to the properties of 90o
coupler as described in section (2.3.1), reflection from the amplifier inputs cancel at
the input of the first hybrid and similar reflection from the amplifier outputs cancel
at the output of the second hybrid. The balanced configuration improves impedance
match at the input and output, as well as the stability of the individual amplifier. It
will degrade only 6dB loss in gain if a single amplifier section fails but as disadvantage
it requires more space.

2.3.4
Mixer
A mixer is a three-port device that achieves frequency conversion by a non-linear
element. A single-ended mixer topology shown in figure (2.21) uses a Schottky diode,
which with its non-linear behavior will convert the RF frequency down to IF frequency
by mixing it with LO frequency. This topology includes a diplexer, DC/IF return
path, Schottky diode, LO short and IF filter.
The purpose of the diplexer is to
combine RF and LO signals at the input for mixing. DC/IF return path is an open
circuit at RF and LO frequency and serves as a ground for DC/IF current. The
Schottky diode, at the output generates mixing products of the two input frequencies
ω1 and ω2. At the output, there is a short circuit for LO frequency and an IF filter
to filter out all the unwanted frequencies expect IF frequency. Notice, that no bias
network is required since the Schottky diode is non-linear device and itself generates
DC. Typically the LO signal is very large and is tun-able where the FLO is adjusted
to fit purposes. In contrast with the LO signal, the RF input signal is generally a
low-power, modulated signal, operating at a carrier frequency. Mixers are specified in
terms of their conversion loss which is the inverse of mixer gain. The mixer conversion
loss is the relationship between the power delivered to IF port at IF frequency to the
available RF input power at RF frequency and specified as
Conversion loss = −10log10

Two different types of radiometer receiver, namely Direct conversion and Superhetero-
dyne have been introduced in section (2.2). Even though the direct conversion consists
of fewer components and is therefore less costly, it cannot be used since the selectivity
is determined by microwave filter meaning that many sections must be used. Such
a filter with so many sections is lossy which leads to a deterioration of the noise
figure of the radiometer. It is therefore more convenient to design the receiver as
Superheterodyne where the selectivity is achieved by IF-filter with only few sections.
Moreover, the Superhetrodyne receiver is selected to operate as a SSB.
As it is explained in section (2.1.2), the radiometer can be run in different modes.
Two different radiometer modes have been introduced, namely the total power-, and
Dicke-mode. It is chosen to run the radiometer as the power total-mode, since it
consists of less components and the budget for the project is limited.
Thus, the purpose of this project is to design and implement a microwave radiome-
ter as a Superheterodyne receiver in total power mode with an accepted IF frequency
from which the sampling will take place for signal processing to generate images. Con-

is important to ensure that the selecting ADC can perform without aliasing occur-

be at least twice as high as the highest frequency of interest in analog signal to avoid
aliasing not occurring. Consequently, the selected ADC must be able to sample rate

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In this chapter, the results of different blocks and modules are discussed. The reason
why the desired results were not achieved are described as follows:
• Low noise amplifier (using transistor): The result from simulation indi-
cates that the low noise amplifier design is made correctly, which includes a
proper matching network.
The only thing that could cause to issue, is the
bias network. According to [NP06], in active circuit design, whenever isolation
between the RF port and DC-bias port is needed, a bias-T could be used. Usu-
ally, radial stubs are used in bias-T because the advantages of using the radial
is achieving slightly wider bandwidth and it results also in a slightly smaller
PCB pattern [Beh16]. This type of biasing is achieved by microstrip and an
alternative solution could be done using microstrip basing circuit. However, as
it was first discovered later and went to design 2, this solution has not been
implemented.
• PIN-diode switch: It was not possible to make more prints because the re-
sponsible person was on vacation. However, a better design could have been
made, with better isolation between RF path and DC path. As the datasheet of
”SMP1340” claims the PIN-diode works from 10 MHz - 10 GHz, it assumes that
the PIN-diode can be used. Otherwise, another PIN-diode for higher frequency
(but still in the range of interested frequency) could be purchased, since the
price of PIN-diode is not that expensive. The decoupling capacitors have been
changed many times but not much change in result has been observed.
• Isolator: The company from which the isolator is bought, provides tuning to
the desired band. As there was no information on its package about what fre-
quency was is tuned to, it was necessary to measure it. When measured for the
first time, it turned out that it can be used for 6.9 GHz with a good attenuation
and low insertion loss. However, when it was measured the second time, the re-
sult deviated a lot from the first measurement. This may be due to the fact that
the Network Analyzer could have been incorrectly calibrated. Unfortunately, it
was first discovered when all the modules were being implemented.

• Front-end module, RF module and IF module: The results from EM-

results are different. To examine this issue, the voltage on each pin on a single

out that in front-end module, only one of the gain-blocks is functional, which
has the same voltage on every pin as the single gain-block. The same issue ap-
peared also in the RF-module. Since the same gain-blocks in front-end module
are used in RF-module, it is an opportunity to investigate whether they differ
from each other, especially the one gain-block which is not working. If they
differ, it indicates that the problem comes from poor soldering or different com-
ponent values for passive components such as DC-coupling, DC-feed or resistor.
Finally, the measured result turns out to be different voltage at the gain-block
which is not working. Thus, the issue comes either from poor soldering since
the gain-block is very small and is difficult to be soldered by hand, or from
different component values. The same method is used to investigate the issue
in the IF-module, and it turns out to be that only one of the gain-blocks are
functional. The issue could also maybe be because of broken gain blocks due
to ESD (Electrostatic discharge).

Conclusion
For both designs, the simulations were made as expected, but due to lack of time
only the low noise amplifier (using transistor) in design 1 is being implemented and
tested. In design 2, the active blocks in the Superheterodyne receiver are implemented
separately and tested successfully. Thus, the passive blocks such as directional coupler,
RF-filter and IF-filter have been designed, simulated and implemented as expected.
The receiver prototype is made by three different modules and PIN-diode switch. The
EM-simulation result for PIN-diode is achieved but the measured result deviates a
little. The EM-simulation results of all three modules have been achieved, but due to
some of the gain-blocks not beign functional, the receiver prototype is not obtained.
For that reason, it is not possible to conduct some tests with the receiver prototype.
Personal conclusion
By completing this project, I have experienced what is to say about system design
and how important it is to consider all specification which depends on my decision.
First of all, I spent too much time on design 1 designing LNA, which would not have
been necessary, if I had decided from the beginning to purchase all the ready-made
components. Secondly, due to finding the right components, I ordered many times
as it was not easy to find components at the cheapest price. This resulted in using
more money for shipping. If I have been aware of the mistake I made, I could have
purchased better components with higher prices in once go, so I needed only to pay
for shipping once. Indeed, higher price components provide less complexity. Thirdly,
I had a perception of how it will be once I get a job where I can seek less help from my
colleagues. This is due to my supervisor pushing me to think by myself and solve the
problem. Lastly, the most important thing I experienced in this project was becoming
independent. I believe in the job industry my boss will tell only what he wants to be
designed, and all the specifications and purchasing decisions and many other thing
will depend on me.

Scope of Improvement
In addition, to ensure that all modules work either by making a new layout or making
a better soldering, the following can be done as future work:
Voltage divider
All the three modules in the final prototype require the same voltage (3V), but the
PIN-diode print requires less voltage and the current must be controlled. Therefore
future work will be to make a voltage divider which supplies all them. In that way,
only one power supply is required for the final prototype.
Balanced configuration
As introduced in section (2.3.3), it is possible to design a LNA by having input and
output matching using balanced configuration.
The Isolator turns out having an
insertion loss of 6 dB and since there have been three isolators used, it will decrease
the total gain by 18dB. The Input and output matching were also not achieved as
expected. For this reason a simulation of the balanced configuration shown in figure
(7.1) can be used for further work. The result from figure (7.2) indicates if by having
a balanced configuration the input and output reflection becomes really low at -40 dB
at 6.9 GHz whereas if having a single LNA amplifier the input and output reflection
are -6 dB and -8.3 dB, respectively.
