Abstract
Wind energy remains a critical renewable resource [1], with offshore wind farms playing
a vital role in sustainable energy production. This thesis along with my old analysis [2]
explores the capabilities of Lidar remote sensing to assess spatial and temporal wind
patterns and wake effects within a wind farm. By utilizing hourly averaged Lidar data
spanning elevation angles from -1° to 10° and horizontal distances up to 2400 meters,
the study introduces advanced methodologies for data filtering, quantitative analysis, and
wake modeling to improve the accuracy and reliability of wind measurements. [3]
This research employs filtering techniques, including median filtering, interquartile range
filtering, to eliminate noise and identify high-quality data across elevation angles. The
filtered data is then compared with ERA5 and Lolland Airport datasets to validate consis-
tency and assess data quality. PyWake [4] simulations are used to model line-of-sight
(LOS) wake effects, [5] which are superimposed onto real-world Lidar observations to
investigate wake propagation and turbine-induced flow disruptions. [6]
The integration of Lidar data with PyWake simulations reveals valuable insights into wake
depth, intensity, and temporal variability, demonstrating the potential of combining ob-
servational and modeled data for a comprehensive analysis. Quantitative comparisons
further establish the reliability of the datasets and highlight the critical role of effective
filtering in ensuring accurate wind farm analysis.
In conclusion, this study underscores the importance of advanced filtering and model-
ing techniques in enhancing wind farm performance assessment, providing actionable
insights for turbine placement optimization, and contributing to the advancement of off-
shore wind energy solutions.

Introduction to Lidar
Wind lidar (Light Detection and Ranging) technology has become a powerful tool for re-
mote sensing of wind patterns, particularly in offshore wind farms where accurate wind
data is crucial for assessing turbine performance, wake effects, and boundary layer dy-
namics. Unlike traditional anemometers, lidar systems use laser pulses to detect wind
velocities by measuring the frequency shifts in light scattered by particles in the air, a
principle known as Doppler shift. This chapter provides an introduction to the fundamen-
tals of wind lidar technology, with a particular emphasis on the concepts of line-of-sight
(LOS) measurements and the limitations associated with different wind directions relative
to the lidar beam.
1.0.1
Principles of Wind Lidar and LOS Doppler Measurements
Wind lidar systems emit laser pulses toward a target area in the atmosphere. When these
pulses interact with aerosols (small particles or dust) moving with the wind, they scatter
back toward the lidar. The lidar system then detects the change in frequency between
the emitted and returning signal, called the Doppler shift. This frequency shift (∆f) relates
directly to the wind speed component along the lidar’s LOS:
∆f = 2VLOS
λ
(1.1)
where:
VLOS is the wind speed along the LOS, λ is the wavelength of the emitted light.
When the wind moves directly toward or away from the lidar, the Doppler shift is maxi-
mized, enabling a more precise measurement of wind speed along the LOS. However, the
lidar can only measure the component of the wind that lies along its beam path because:
LOS = windspeed · cos θ
When wind direction is perpendicular to the lidar beam (90° or 270° relative to the LOS),
no Doppler shift occurs along that axis, rendering the measurement ineffective for deter-
mining wind speed in that direction.
1.0.2
Limitations and Challenges in LOS Measurements
The directionality of wind relative to the lidar’s beam significantly influences the quality
and interpretation of the data. A few key challenges are:
Perpendicular Winds (90° or 270°):
When the wind direction is perpendicular to the lidar’s LOS, the lidar cannot detect any
wind speed component because there is no Doppler shift along the measurement axis.
For instance, in offshore environments, wind directions around 90° or 270° may produce
very weak or negligible LOS data, close to 0 leading to areas of low data reliability.
Directional Sensitivity:
Winds aligned at 0° or 180° (directly toward or away from the lidar) offer the most ac-
curate velocity measurements because the wind vector aligns fully with the lidar beam,
creating the maximal Doppler shift. This capability is especially useful when assessing

wake effects in wind farms, as wakes often propagate directly behind turbines. Therefore,
analyzing wind patterns at 0° or 180° offers clear insights into turbine wake dynamics and
the broader boundary layer profile.
Partial Directional Winds (e.g., 93°):
For wind directions that are only slightly off from perpendicular (e.g., 93°), the lidar still
detects a small component of the wind speed, but it’s diminished. In practical applica-
tions, these small angles are often considered too weak for accurate wind profiling, which
requires aligning measurements more directly with the wind vector.
1.1
Importance of LOS Measurements for Wake Analysis
In offshore wind farms, understanding wake formation is essential for optimizing turbine
performance and reducing fatigue on downstream turbines. RHI (Range Height Indicator)
scans, commonly used in wind lidar systems, are particularly valuable in capturing vertical
profiles of the wind across the boundary layer. These profiles allow researchers to study:
1.1.1
Wind Intensity and Velocity Profiles:
By aligning the lidar with the prevailing wind direction (typically 0° or 180°), it’s possible
to capture clear intensity and velocity profiles, which reveal variations in wind speed and
turbulence within the boundary layer.
1.1.2
Wake Effects:
Wakes generated by turbines reduce wind speeds and increase turbulence for down-
stream turbines. By positioning the lidar at 0° or 180° relative to the wake, RHI scans can
effectively capture the spatial distribution and intensity of wakes, facilitating a detailed
analysis of wake propagation and its impact on turbine efficiency.
1.2
Filtering Considerations and Data Quality
The accuracy of lidar data relies not only on directional alignment but also on filtering tech-
niques that remove noise and outliers. For instance, Carrier-to-Noise Ratio (CNR) values
can indicate data reliability. A low CNR typically corresponds to poor data quality, often
due to low aerosol content in clear air conditions, which results in weak backscattered
signals.
Additionally, filtering methods, such as median filtering can help eliminate outliers caused
by blade interference or high-wind events. These techniques improve the interpretation of
wind velocity profiles and ensure that data used in wake modeling reflect true atmospheric
conditions.

Filtering Techniques for Data Validation
Accurate data validation is a critical step in wind lidar data analysis, particularly when
working with measurements acquired in challenging environments such as offshore lo-
cations like Lolland. Lidar data is often susceptible to noise and outliers due to factors
such as atmospheric turbulence, system limitations, and the inherent variability of wind
patterns. To address these challenges, advanced filtering techniques are employed to
enhance data quality and ensure reliable results. Among these techniques, Interquar-
tile Range (IQR) Filtering and Median Filtering are particularly effective for refining wind
speed and intensity measurements.
3.1
Median Filtering
Median filtering is a non-linear, technique used to remove noise and outliers in datasets.
Unlike mean filtering, which is sensitive to large deviations, median filtering is more effec-
tive at preserving the integrity of the data while smoothing out noise.

Application in Wind Lidar Data:
Median filtering is applied primarily to temporal or spatial wind speed profiles to eliminate
sporadic data points caused by brief disturbances (such as rain, birds, or turbulence) that

It is particularly useful in removing high-frequency noise, which is often caused by rapid
fluctuations in wind speed or incorrect lidar returns, and preserves edge features in the
data, which is crucial for analyzing wind patterns.

identify and remove outliers from the dataset. The process begins by calculating the
first quartile (Q1) and the third quartile (Q3) of the wind speed data. These quartiles
represent the 25th and 75th percentiles of the data distribution, respectively. The IQR is

3.3
Data Averaging for Enhanced Analysis
The collection of instantaneous lidar measurements over extended periods results in an
extensive dataset that requires careful processing to extract meaningful insights. Without
averaging, the sheer volume of data would produce an overwhelming number of graphs,
making it challenging to identify trends or patterns. Therefore, averaging the lidar data
is a critical step in simplifying the dataset and enabling a clearer interpretation of wind
behavior.
Structure of the Dataset
The dataset comprises measurements taken at 11 distinct elevation angles, which repeat
cyclically after every set of 11 scans. For example, the first elevation angle is 0 degrees,
followed by angles such as -1, -0.99, 0.72, 2.38, 4.05, 5.71, 7.38, 9.06, 10.0, and 9.97
degrees. However, these angles are not fixed and can vary slightly between files, intro-
ducing additional complexity into the averaging process.
Each file begins with a header line specifying the elevation angle, followed by 250 lines of
data containing measurements for velocity, signal intensity, and range gates. To average
the data correctly, the values from the first data line (spanning all 250 range gates) must
be averaged element-wise with the corresponding values from the 13th data line, the
second line with the 14th, and so on. This process results in 11 averaged data lines, each
corresponding to one of the elevation angles.
Challenges in the Averaging Process
Initially, this averaging task was computationally intensive and prone to errors. Verify-
ing the accuracy of the averaging process was particularly challenging due to the large
number of files and the volume of data within each file. The traditional approach involved
creating new files for each set of averaged values and then aligning and averaging them
line by line, which was both time-consuming and inefficient.
Optimizing the Averaging Process
To address these challenges, a more efficient method was developed. Instead of gen-
erating intermediate files, a custom function was implemented to perform the averaging
directly and store the results in a variable. This function incorporated a tolerance pa-
rameter to group elevation angles that were close to each other, ensuring consistency
across files. This approach significantly reduced the computational load and simplified
the verification process.
By adopting this optimized method, it became feasible to compute 10-minute averages
and, subsequently, day-long averages. This streamlined process not only improved com-
putational efficiency but also facilitated the identification of long-term trends and patterns
in the data, enhancing the overall quality of the analysis.

Challenges and Limitations
Non-Dynamic Height Representation
• The height representation in the LiDAR data analysis was simplified, resulting in
horizontal lines in the wake map instead of the intended elliptical or circular patterns.
This limitation arose from the assumption of constant elevation angles for each beam
path, which does not account for dynamic terrain or atmospheric variations.
Incomplete Analysis of Backscatter Intensities
The relationship between LiDAR backscatter intensities and meteorological parameters
(e.g., boundary layer height, visibility) was not explored due to data limitations. This anal-
ysis could have provided additional insights into data accuracy and filtering methods.
Limited Superposition Modeling
• While wake simulations were conducted using PyWake, the study did not fully im-
plement superposition models to simulate cumulative wake effects under varying
conditions. This limited the ability to analyze complex interactions between multiple
turbines.
Short Timeframe for Analysis
• The quantitative analysis was restricted to 3 months (June, July and August), which
may not fully capture seasonal variations in wake behavior. A longer timeframe
would provide more robust conclusions and insights into offshore wake dynamics.
Visibility vs. Intensity Analysis
• The planned analysis of visibility versus backscatter intensity to assess LiDAR data
quality could not be completed due to insufficient meteorological data. This remains
an area for future exploration.
7.0.2
Future Work
Dynamic Height Modeling
• Incorporate dynamic height calculations that account for terrain variations and at-
mospheric conditions to improve the accuracy of wake maps.
Advanced Superposition Modeling
• Implement superposition models in PyWake to simulate cumulative wake effects
under varying wind conditions, enabling a more comprehensive analysis of turbine
interactions.
Extended Temporal Analysis
• Expand the analysis to include multi-month or multi-year LiDAR data to capture
seasonal and long-term trends in wake behavior.
Backscatter Intensity and Meteorological Correlation
• Investigate the relationship between LiDAR backscatter intensities and meteorolog-
ical parameters (e.g., boundary layer height, visibility) to enhance data filtering and
accuracy.
Comparative Analysis with Additional Datasets
• Include more meteorological datasets, such as NORA3, for a broader comparison
with LiDAR observations and wake simulations.

Conclusion
This study successfully demonstrated the potential of LiDAR data for analyzing wind pat-
terns and wake effects in offshore wind farms. By combining LiDAR measurements with
PyWake simulations and meteorological datasets, we achieved a quantitative understand-
ing of wake behavior and its impact on wind farm performance. However, limitations in
height representation, superposition modeling, and temporal analysis highlight the need
for further research. Addressing these challenges in future work will enhance the accuracy
and applicability of wake models, ultimately contributing to optimized turbine placement
and improved energy output in offshore wind farms.
The findings of this study provide a foundation for advancing wind farm optimization and
underscore the importance of integrating remote sensing, advanced modeling, and me-
teorological data in wind energy research.
