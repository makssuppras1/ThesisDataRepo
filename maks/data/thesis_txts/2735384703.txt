Abstract
This thesis presents the development of a mobile electrocardiogram (ECG) monitoring
system to address specific needs in cardiac care research. The system integrates a cus­
tom ECG analysis algorithm and interactive data visualization using Dash to improve us­
ability and efficiency in home heart health monitoring. Through comprehensive require­
ments gathering, the platform is tailored to the research needs by facilitating wearable
data acquisition, real­time data annotation, and data analytics. The current user of this
platform is Ying Gu from DTU Health Tech. The development process follows software
engineering principles within the Scrum framework, focusing on the modularity and re­
liability of the platform. Testing and evaluation strategies ensure accuracy in handling
sensitive medical data. The results demonstrate the system’s ability to enable real­time
and historical ECG waveform visualization along with annotation functionalities to mark
segments efficiently. While limited in the scope of user testing and security features, the
platform’s strengths lie in its comprehensive functionalities and adaptable modular de­
sign. This research provides a foundation for future advancements at the intersection of
healthcare and technology to improve heart health monitoring.

Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
The world we live in today places immense importance on health, particularly heart health.
This thesis explores the intersection of healthcare and technology. It focuses on the de­
velopment of mobile heart health monitoring systems. This area is significant as heart
diseases are a leading cause of death globally. The rise in heart­related health issues
necessitates more accessible monitoring methods.
The drive to study this problem comes from the increasing prevalence of cardiovascular
diseases (CVDs). CVDs are diseases that affect the heart and blood vessels. They are
a major health concern worldwide. At the same time, advancements in technology have
made mobile health (mHealth) solutions more feasible. mHealth refers to the use of mobile
devices to support medical and public health practices. This thesis argues that mobile
technology can offer an effective way to monitor heart health. It can provide real­time
data and early warnings for heart conditions.
This problem is real and affects everyday life. It involves individuals at risk of or currently
dealing with heart diseases. It also presents a theoretical challenge. The challenge lies
in creating a new and tailored system that is scientifically accurate and user­friendly. The
goal is to blend healthcare, technology, and design in a meaningful way.
The effort to develop a mobile heart health monitoring system is justified by several fac­
tors. These include the potential for widespread access to healthcare and the ability to
empower individuals. Users can actively manage their health with real­time data of their
monitoring targets. This approach can prevent heart diseases and facilitate early inter­
vention. It promises to make healthcare more accessible and efficient. Furthermore, it is
worth addressing the importance of developing a new, tailored mobile heart health mon­
itoring system for our targeted user, Ying Gu, who is a researcher in the heart health
field, currently developing a home­monitoring system of health management for patients.
Therefore, at this stage, our system is designed for collecting continuous wearable data
and annotating the recorded ECG data, facilitating machine­learning algorithm develop­
ment. Later on, it can be integrated and introduced for more practical usage. This en­
deavor tackles numerous unaddressed requirements. Existing solutions often do not offer
the flexibility required to meet specific user requirements.
Addressing this issue has significant implications. It stands to markedly improve the well­
being of many by enabling the early detection of heart conditions. A key aspect of this
project is the development of a customized framework. This framework is specifically de­
signed for monitoring heart health. It lays a foundational stone for future technological
advancements in healthcare. In essence, the project does more than solve an existing
problem. It establishes a new precedent for the integration of technology in health moni­
toring. This approach is innovative and tailored to meet specific health needs. It ensures
that future developments can build on a solid, well­defined base.

1.3
Problem statement
The primary objective of this thesis is to address a particular gap within healthcare tech­
nology, especially concerning mobile heart health monitoring and then to solve it by the
development of a web application product. Although mobile systems have made notable
advancements in ECG monitoring, there remains a challenge in the absence of a novel,
customized web application tailored for a certain user. This limitation inhibits the com­
plete utilization of ECG data for clinical insights. This gap not only impacts individual
health management but also hampers the effectiveness of healthcare providers in deliv­
ering timely care.
To fulfill the thesis objective, several specific problems must be resolved:
• Need for a tailored heart health monitoring web application system.
• Lack of flexibility to substitute algorithms, so that researchers can not integrate and
further adjust their own desired algorithm module.
• The Absence of interactive data visualization capabilities, which is crucial for re­
searchers to efficiently analyze large volumes of ECG data.
• A deficiency in real­time annotation tools, necessary for researchers to monitor and
mark relevant segments and patterns in ECG waveform in time.
Addressing these problems is crucial for developing a system that effectively supports
heart health monitoring and management. The importance of this problem is underscored
by the potential to significantly improve health outcomes for individuals at risk of or living
with heart disease. By providing a solution that is both accessible and tailored to indi­
vidual needs, this thesis aims to contribute a valuable tool to the mHealth landscape,
underscoring its worthiness as a thesis topic.

Related Work
2.1
Overview
In the realm of ECG visualization and annotation, there are several tools and platforms
designed to assist in the analysis and interpretation of ECG data, a crucial aspect of
diagnosing and monitoring cardiac health. Many academic papers, open­source projects,
and commercial products leverage advanced technologies to enhance the accuracy and
efficiency of ECG analysis.
2.1.1
Academic Papers
The paper by Moody (2013)[1] presents LightWAVE, an open­source software for viewing,
annotating, and editing ECGs and other physiological waveforms. It facilitates interactive
annotation creation and modification, essential for developing new physiological signal
collections for research. Its C­based CGI backend efficiently retrieves data from open­
access archives or structured datasets and delivers it to the front­end. The JavaScript­
based front­end operates within web browsers without installation. LightWAVE also in­
cludes a Perl­based scribe for recording user annotation edits. Its design allows dis­
tributing the data repository, backend, and front­end across different computers, with the
front­end and scribe required to be on the same domain for browser security compliance.
The paper by Winslow, Granite, and Jurado (2016)[2] introduces WaveformECG, a web­
based platform for managing and analyzing ECG data, as part of the CardioVascular
Research Grid project. It offers functionalities for browsing, uploading, visualizing, anno­
tating, and analyzing ECG data across various vendor formats. The platform allows for
the extraction and storage of information from file headers, including analysis results, and
facilitates annotation using ontology from the Bioportal ontology server. Users can select
groups of ECGs for analysis, which can be distributed across multiple CPUs to decrease
processing time. Analysis results can be viewed and downloaded in CSV format.
The paper ”LabelECG: A Web­based Tool for Distributed Electrocardiogram Annotation”
[3] proposes a web­based tool for ECG viewing and annotating called LabelECG. This
tool facilitates the distribution of large cohorts of ECGs to technicians and physicians who
can simultaneously make annotations through web browsers on various devices such
as PCs, tablets, and cell phones. The authors highlight the potential for deep neural
networks to improve the accuracy of arrhythmia detection based on ECGs. LabelECG
has been successfully applied to annotate about 15,000 12­lead resting ECG records in
three months, supporting the First China ECG Intelligent Competition.
The paper ”PrepAnnECG: A user­friendly MATLAB ECG preprocessing and annotation
GUI for health professionals”[8] introduces an open­source, user­friendly MATLAB pre­
processing and annotation interface. This interface, named preprocessECG, provides
a GUI for tasks such as cropping, manual artifact removal, filtering, and segmentation.
Additionally, custom preprocessing software can be invoked from the interface. The tool
aims to facilitate the preprocessing and annotation of long ECG records, making it suitable
for health professionals preparing files for use with AI tools.
2.1.2
Comercial Products
The Encord ECG Annotation Tool [4] is a collaborative platform designed for AI and Ma­
chine Learning, aimed at enhancing ECG data annotation with a PACS­style interface

for better model development. It offers configurable annotation ontology for complex la­
beling, tools to identify dataset errors, biases, and imbalances, and supports regulatory
compliance including FDA submissions and CE approval, being HIPAA and SOC2 com­
pliant. This tool is geared towards improving the speed and accuracy of training data

NOTOcord [5] is a versatile software designed for both real­time and offline ECG analysis,

and API support for further customization and annotation, ViewECG Web caters to the
needs of the healthcare and pharmaceutical industries by enhancing ECG data analysis
and integration capabilities.

Open­source projects
EcgEditor [9] is a Java­based desktop application developed for the examination, anno­
tation, and analysis of ECG data within the Unisens format. It facilitates the visualization
of multiple ECG channels, and manual marking of artifacts, and includes algorithms for
QRS detection through plugin support.
ECG_Viewer [10], constructed as a Matlab GUI, offers capabilities for the assessment,
processing, and annotation of ECG recordings. It features ECG signal filtering, identifica­
tion of heartbeats via template matching, and refinement of intervals between beats.
2.2
Potential Improvements
2.2.1
Real­Time Data Visualization and Annotation
Our project introduces improvements in real­time data visualization and annotation of
ECG data, tailored specifically for healthcare researchers. By mitigating the limitations
of fixed annotations and static data refresh rates in existing solutions, our innovative ap­
proach offers customizable real­time annotations and dynamic refresh rates.
• Customizable Real­Time Annotation: By enabling healthcare professionals to an­
notate ECG data in real­time with customizable labels, our project could allow for
more precise and patient­specific analysis. This flexibility is especially crucial dur­
ing live monitoring sessions, where immediate feedback on abnormal signals can
significantly impact patient care. Imagine a scenario where a doctor, alerted by a
notification from a patient’s mobile monitoring device, can instantly review and an­
notate the ECG waveform to indicate the nature of the anomaly. This immediate
interaction enhances personalized patient care from home monitoring.
• Dynamic Refresh Rates: Our system’s ability to adjust the refresh rate of ECG
data in real­time can be a game­changing feature. By allowing users to tailor the
monitoring intensity based on the scenario’s criticality, our platform can optimize
resource usage and ensure that data is refreshed at a pace that matches clinical
needs. In high­acuity situations, a faster refresh rate can facilitate timely interven­

tions, whereas, in stable monitoring environments, a slower rate conserves compu­
tational resources without compromising patient care.
2.2.2
Modular Design for Visualization and Analysis Services
Our project’s architectural decision to separate visualization and analysis services intro­
duces a modular and scalable approach to ECG data handling. This separation offers
several improvements over more monolithic designs found in existing tools:
• Distributed Architecture: By deploying on Azure and using InfluxDB, our project in­
herently supports a distributed architecture. This modularity and flexibility in deploy­
ment allow for components of our system (e.g., data storage, analysis, visualization)
to be independently scaled and updated without downtime, a feature that enhances
the modular design principles seen in LightWAVE but with the added benefits of
cloud infrastructure.
• Flexibility in Algorithm Selection: By decoupling the analysis from visualization, our
platform allows for the easy replacement or upgrading of the heart rate calculation
algorithms. This means that as new, more accurate, or efficient algorithms are de­
veloped, they can be integrated into the system without disrupting the visualization
service.
• Independent Scaling: The ability to deploy the algorithm service on a separate Azure
server means that each component can be scaled based on demand independently.
For instance, if the analysis demands more computational power, especially with
complex algorithms, it can be scaled without affecting the visualization component’s
performance. This separation ensures that high data processing loads do not com­
promise the user interface’s responsiveness.
• Enhanced Maintainability: Modular designs facilitate easier maintenance and up­
dates since changes to one module can be made without impacting others. This
leads to a more robust and reliable system where improvements, bug fixes, and
updates are simpler to implement.
2.2.3
Scalability and Performance
Our project will exploit the power of Azure for deployment alongside the specialized ca­
pabilities of InfluxDB for managing time­series data. This innovative combination fills sig­
nificant gaps and offers improvements over existing solutions in several key areas:
• Azure Deployment: Azure provides a global network of managed data centers, of­
fering high availability, redundancy, and scalability. [11] Unlike some systems that
may rely on localized or less scalable infrastructure, deploying on Azure allows our
project to dynamically scale resources based on real­time demand, ensuring that
performance remains consistent even as the number of users or data volume grows.
This capability surpasses the scalability of solutions like LightWAVE, which, while
modular, may not inherently provide the same level of cloud scalability and perfor­
mance optimization.
• InfluxDB for Time­Series Data: InfluxDB is specifically designed for time­series data,
offering high write and query performance, which is ideal for ECG data manage­
ment.[12] This ensures that our system can handle large volumes of data efficiently,
a critical requirement that may not be optimally addressed by systems using general­
purpose databases or file­based storage solutions like those mentioned in academic
papers and commercial products. The use of InfluxDB can significantly improve the
speed and efficiency of data retrieval and analysis compared to other solutions.

Flexibility and Availability

is expected to achieve, from the standpoint of both our end user and the technical re­

requirements, offering insights into how the system should interact with its users.
Finally, we articulate the system requirements, divided into functional and non­functional
categories. Functional requirements specify the actions the system must be able to per­
form, while non­functional requirements address the system’s operation, including perfor­
mance, usability, and compatibility.
3.1.1

3.1.1.1
Project Glossary
• ECG (Electrocardiogram): An electrocardiogram records the electrical signals in the
heart. It’s a common and painless test used to quickly detect heart problems and
monitor the heart’s health[13].
• ECG graph: A curve consisted of ECG data points, with the voltage as ECG value
to be y­axis, and timestamp as ECG timestamp to be x­axis.
• Patient: Person with wearable device whose heart health is recorded and monitored.
• Patient ID: A unique identifier for patients, for them to be recognized.
• Heart Rate: The number of times the heart beats per minute. It’s a critical indicator
of heart health.
• Heart Rate Algorithm: The open source algorithm package we use for calculating
r_peaks and heart rates[7].
• InfluxDB: An open­source time series database designed to handle high write and
query loads, useful for storing and analyzing ECG data over time.
• Azure: A comprehensive cloud computing platform offered by Microsoft, providing
a wide range of cloud services.
• ECG Simulator: A simulator to simulate the real­time data from the source file.
• Annotation: Marks on the ECG curve to highlight some abnormal heart activities
that are saved in database, which can also be presented on our user interface.

Compatibility

3.2
System Design
3.2.1
Component Design
The Heart Health Monitoring System is composed of several key components that work
in conjunction to provide robust functionality for monitoring and analyzing ECG data. The
system’s architecture is designed to ensure scalability, modularity, and maintainability.
Figure 3.2: Component Design Diagram
Below we describe the main components as illustrated in the component diagram:
ECG Simulator (S)
The ECG Simulator is a module designed to generate synthetic
ECG signal data that mimics the electrical activity of the human heart. This simulated
data is used for testing and validation purposes, ensuring that the system can accurately
process and analyze ECG signals under various conditions.
Web Application (W)
The Web Application serves as the user interface of the Heart
Health Monitoring System. It provides users with access to real­time data visualization,
system controls, and analytics results. The application is built to be user­friendly and
accessible, allowing both patients and healthcare providers to interact with the system
effectively.
ECG Detector (D)
The ECG Detector is responsible for the real­time detection and anal­
ysis of ECG signals. It processes the incoming ECG data, identifies any anomalies or
significant events applying the algorithm, and provides immediate feedback or alerts as
necessary. This component is crucial for the early detection of potential health issues.
InfluxDB Connector (C)
The InfluxDB Connector acts as the middleware between the
Web Application and the InfluxDB database. It manages the data flow, ensuring that
16
Development of mobile heart health monitoring system
==PAGE:25==
ECG data is reliably transmitted to the database for storage and later retrieval. This com­
ponent abstracts the database operations from the application logic, facilitating easier
maintenance and updates.
InfluxDB (I)
The cloud­hosted InfluxDB serves as a critical data storage component.
As a time­series database, it is specifically optimized for storing and retrieving sequential
data points, such as ECG readings, which are inherently time­stamped. It is important to
note, however, that the configuration and maintenance of the cloud­based InfluxDB are
not within the purview of our project’s design considerations. The database is treated as
an external service that the system interacts with, rather than an internal component to
be engineered.
Furthermore, according to and together with the components diagram above, the data
flow in our system expands as below: The data flow in the system begins with the ECG
Simulator generating data, which is then sent to the ECG Detector for analysis. The re­
sults, along with the raw data, are transmitted through the InfluxDB Connector to be stored
in InfluxDB. The Web Application fetches this data as needed for display and interaction
with users.

OCL Constraints
When creating Object Constraint Language (OCL) constraints, we
are setting formal conditions that must be met by the instances of a class. These con­
ditions are usually invariants that must always be true, preconditions that must be true
before an operation is executed, and postconditions that must be true after an operation
is executed. The OCL provides a precise and unambiguous language to specify these
constraints, ensuring the model’s integrity and guiding the implementation.
Below we will enumerate OCL constraints for each class. These constraints aim to ensure
the correctness of real­time updates, heart rate calculations, and the integrity of annota­
tions within the system, to name a few. However for simplification we only put the most
important and representative ones in text. More constraints could be found in section A.5
of appendix.

sampling frequency of 125 Hz.

time transmission scenarios. This lets us assess the platform’s ability to handle complex
data, verify database storage, and identify areas for potential future development in real­
time ECG transmission.
