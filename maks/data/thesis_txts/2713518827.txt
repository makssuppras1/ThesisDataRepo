Abstract
Neoepitopes are important for eradication of cancer cells, as they are tumor-specific peptides
emerged from somatic mutations in the deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) of cancer cells, which
causes cancer. When neoepitopes are processed inside the cell, they can be presented on major
histocompatibility complexes (MHCs) and they can thereby be recognized as foreign by the
immune system, specifically by T-cells. There are two types of MHC molecules, namely MHC
class I and II (MHC-I and MHC-II).
Immunotherapies such as immune checkpoint inhibition (ICB) have improved the probability
of patients survival, but the results have not been satisfactory. Due to this, patient-specific
neoepitope prediction has emerged to be one of the biggest topics in cancer research. This
study enlightens the importance of both including MHC-I and MHC-II neoepitopes responses
in neoepitope prediction, to ensure activation of both cytotoxic T-cells (CD8+ T-cells) and
helper T-cells (CD4+ T-cells).
The mutant peptide extractor and informer (MuPeXI) version 1.2 is a state-of-the-art tool
in predicting MHC-I neoepitopes, it predicts the peptide-MHC (pMHC) binding affinities
by use of netMHCpan. In this study, MuPeXI version 1.2 was updated to version 2.0, by
expanding it to also predict MHC-II neoepitopes with incorporation of netMHCIIpan, which
predicts pMHC-II binding affinities. An efficient peptide-MHC binding affinity is crucial for
neoepitope presentation to the immune system, in addition with an expression level above
zero to ensure presence of the neoepitope.
The new version of MuPeXI was used to predict MHC-I and MHC-II neoepitopes based on
next generation sequencing (NGS) data from 26 patients with metastatic urothelial carcinoma
(mUC). In addition a filtering function was added to MuPeXI for MHC-II prediction to nar-
row the amount of prediction data for MHC-II neoepitopes. Experimentally verified MHC-I
neoepitopes selected by MuPeXI were used to benchmark MHC-II predictions. Additionally,
MHC-I results were benchmarked with Antigen Garnish (another neoepitope prediction tool).
Results from this study suggests selection of neoepitope candidates with use of the %rank
< 0.5 for MHC-I and %rank < 2.0 for MHC-II, with an expression level > 0.1 transcripts
per million (TPM). Investigation of additional selection features such as hydrophobicity, for-
eignness score, dissimilarity and differential agretopicity index did not show any addition to
candidate neoepitope selection in this study.

Resume
Neoepitoper er vigtige for udryddelse af kræftceller, da de er tumor-specifikke peptider op-
stået fra somatiske mutationer i deoxyribonukleinsyre (DNA) af kræftceller, som forårsager
kræft. Når neoepitoper behandles inde i cellen, kan de præsenteres på major histocompability
complexes (MHC’er), hvorved de kan genkendes som fremmede af immunsystemet, specifikt
af T-celler. Der findes to typer af MHC-molekyler, MHC klasse I og II (MHC-I og MHC-II).
Til dags dato har immunterapier som immune checkpoint inhibition (ICB) forbedret sandsyn-
ligheden for patienters overlevelse, men resultaterne har ikke været tilfredsstillende. På grund
af dette har patientspecifik neoepitop forudsigelse vist sig at være et af de største emner inden
for kræftforskning. Denne undersøgelse oplyser vigtigheden af både at inkludere MHC-I og
MHC-II neoepitopresponser i neoepitopforudsigelse for at sikre aktivering af både cytotoksiske
T-celler (CD8+ T-celler) og hjælper T-celler (CD4+ T-celler).
Mutant peptide extractor and informer (MuPeXI) version 1.2 er et avanceret værktøj til at
forudsige MHC-I neoepitoper, det forudsiger peptid-MHC (pMHC) bindingsaffiniteter ved
brug af netMHCpan. MuPeXI version 1.2 blev opdateret til 2.0, ved at udvide det til også at
forudsige MHC-II neoepitoper ved implementering af netMHCIIpan, der forudsiger pMHC-
II bindingsaffiniteter.
En effektiv peptid-MHC (pMHC) bindingsaffinitet er afgørende for
neoepitopekspressionen, ved tilstedeværelse af et ekspressionsniveau over nul for at sikre tilst-
edeværelsen af neoepitopen.
Denne undersøgelse er baseret på next generation sequencing (NGS) data fra 26 patienter med
metastatic urothelial carcinoma (mUC), som blev brugt sammen med MuPeXI til at forudsige
MHC-I og MHC-II neoepitoper. Derudover blev der i MuPeXI tilføjet en filtreringsfunktion
til MHC-II forudsigelserne for at indsnævre mængden af forudsigelsesdata for MHC-II neoepi-
toper. Eksperimentelt verificerede MHC-I neoepitoper udvalgt af MuPeXI blev brugt til at
sammenligne med MHC-II forudsigelser. Derudover blev MHC-I resultater sammenlignet med
Antigen Garnish (et andet neoepitop forudsigelsesværktøj).
Resultater fra denne undersøgelse foreslår udvælgelse af neoepitop-kandidater ved brug af
%rank < 0,5 for MHC-I og %rank < 2,0 for MHC-II med et ekspressionsniveau > 0,1 tran-
skriptioner pr. million (TPM). Undersøgelse af udvælgelses parametre såsom hydrofobicitet,
fremmed score, ulighed og differentielt agretopitetsindeks viste ikke nogen tilføjelse til neoepitop-
kandidat udvælgelsen i denne undersøgelse.

1
Motivation
Cancer has always been a serious public health problem worldwide. The International Agency
for Research on Cancer documented that the global cancer burden in 2020 has increased to a
estimation of 19.3 million new cases and 10 million deaths. By 2040 the cancer incidence is
estimated to increase with 47%, which equals 28.4 million cases worldwide[85]. Due to this, it
is evident that there is a crucial need for new methods and tools to help in the fight against
cancer.
Cancer is caused by mutations in the human deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) of a single cell,
which will be processed and presented as peptides on molecules that can be recognized by
the immune system, specifically T-cells whose job is to kill off the cancer cells[54]. There
exist two types of these molecules, namely major histocompatibility complex I and II (MHC-I
and MHC-II)[49]. The main problem is that the T-cells do not recognize these peptides as
foreign, due to cancer being highly complex and in many cases it is necessary to develop
personalized treatment to obtain complete remission for patients. An approach to solve this
problem is to predict neoepitopes, which are MHC bound tumor-specific peptides that have the
potential to be recognized by T-cells[13]. These neoepitopes can then be used in personalized
immunotherapies such as cancer vaccines and adoptive T-cell therapies, to help the patients
own immune system in the differentiation of T-cells that can eliminate cancer cells[15].
2
Aim
The mutant peptide extractor and informer (MuPeXI) version 1.2[11] is a state-of-the-art tool
developed to identify potential neoepitopes bound to MHC-I molecules with use of sequencing
data from cancer patients. MuPeXI predicts neoepitopes with use of netMHCpan[57], a tool
of artificial neural networks developed to predict binding of peptides to MHC-I. As the current
version of MuPeXI only allows for prediction of neoepitopes presented by MHC-I molecules
the aim of this study is to implement the prediction of neoepitopes presented by MHC-II
molecules. This will be done by incorporating netMHCIIpan[56] into MuPeXI. netMHCIIpan
does the same as netMHCpan but for MHC-II molecules.
The new version of MuPeXI will be run with next generation sequencing (NGS) data from 26
metastatic urothelial carcinoma (mUC) patients which will be benchmarked against experi-
mentally validated CD8+ T-cell neoepitopes from the same patients. Additionally neoepitopes
will be predicted with the same data by Antigen Garnish[58] which is a similar neoepitope
prediction tool and benchmarked against the MuPeXI predictions.

Introduction
3.1
The immune system
The immune system is the body’s defence against pathogens and components that do not
look like self, as e.g. cancer. This defence includes molecules, a variety of effector cells and
destructive enzymes capable of raising a specific immune response[30]. Most immune cells are
developed in the bone marrow where they mature before they can circulate in the lymphatic
system or in the bloodstream. The immune system can be divided into the innate and the
adaptive immune system[30]. The innate immune system is the first and non-specific defense
including macrophages, natural killer cells, and granulocytes. The adaptive immune system
is highly specific, selective, and holds a long lasting memory utilising a defense of T- and
B-cells[30][26]. As the focus in this study is to create new methods for defeating cancer cells,
the adaptive immune system will be the center in this introduction.
Humoral immunity describes the B-cell response that includes plasma and memory B cells.
Plasma cells secrete specific antibodies and can also function as antigen-presenting cells (APCs).
Cellular immunity describes the T-cell response with cytotoxic T-cells (CD8+ T-cells) holding
a CD8 co-receptor, helper T-cells (CD4+ T-cells) holding a CD4 co-receptor, and regulatory
T-cells[26][47]. When the T-cells have matured in the bone marrow they migrate to the thymus
were they mature further and develop their T-cell receptors (TCRs) and co-receptors. By use
of their TCRs and co-receptors the T-cells can recognize major histocompatibility complexes
(MHC) presenting peptides on APCs making them capable of discriminating between self and
non-self[26][47]. In fact the most selective step in the pathway through the cell leading to
peptide presentation is the binding to MHC. Due to this fact MHC has a crucial role in T-cell
activation. The MHC molecule consists of two types MHC class I (MHC-I) and MHC class
II (MHC-II). MHC-I and MHC-II function similar as they both present peptides on the cell
surface which can be recognized by CD8+ T-cells or CD4+ T-cells. Peptides that originate
intracellular are presented on MHC-I forming a peptide-MHC(pMHC)-I complex that is rec-
ognized by TCRs on CD8+ T-cells[49]. If CD8+ T-cells recognize the cell as foreign they can
kill it off by release of cytotoxic molecules such as granzymes and perforin. In addition they
also release IFNγ which increases the expression of MHC-I on cancer cells[73]. In contrast
peptides that originate exogenous are proteolytically digested in endocytic compartments and
presented on MHC-II forming a pMHC-II complex where they can be recognized by TCRs on
CD4+ T-cells[49]. CD4+ T-cells are responsible for coordination and regulation of effector
cells[82]. They enhance antibody production and B cell differentiation as well as CD8+ T-cell
responses by interactions and secretion of cytokines and chemokines depending on the CD4+
T-cell type. The MHC-II pathway including CD4+ T-cells plays a significant role in the onset
and control of cellular immunity and is the main focus in this study[56][74].
3.2
TCR specificity and activation
Naïve T-cells recognize immunogenic pMHC complexes on APCs with their TCRs, initiating
a signaling cascade activating specific T-cell maturation. The TCR on the T-cell consists
of two chains, namely an α and a β chain. The TCR α chain has variable (V), joining (J)
and constant (C) regions. The TCR β chain has variable (V), joining (J), constant (C) and
diversity (D) regions[40][30]. The T-cell maturation starts when TCR genes undergo gene
recombination and rearrangements of the V(D)J gene segments generating the V region for
antigen binding. The full α and β chains are formed as V regions are combined with C regions.
This small set of genes can create combinations of 1015 −1020 TCR clonotypes (T-cells with
the same TCR), allowing T-cells to recognize a wide range of antigens[65][40].

To get a functional and efficient T-cell activation the T-cells need at least three signals when
interacting with the APCs (see figure 1).
• Interaction between pMHC-I or pMHC-II complex on APCs with TCR and co-receptor
(CD4 or CD8) on T-cells.
• CD28 antigen non-specific co-stimulatory signal on T-cells that interacts with B7 on
APCs.
• Secretion of different cytokines such as interleukin from T-cells and APCs.
These signals will induce an antigen-specific immune response by starting specific T-cell
proliferation[65].

3.3
MHC molecules
In humans, MHC molecules are encoded in the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) gene loci
which is located on chromosome 6 (see figure 2)[74].
The knowledge about MHC antigen
presentation may be crucial for the development of tools to modulate strong responses against
cancer. MHC-I molecules are expressed by all nucleated cells where they present peptides of
nuclear and cytosolic origin. MHC-II molecules are restricted to be expressed on a selection of
immune cells and on vascular, epithelial or connective tissue cells in regards to inflammatory
signals[74][30][49].

The MHC-I molecule is a composed of a polymorphic heavy α-chain and a β-microglobulin light
chain (see figure 3). HLA-A, HLA-B and HLA-C are the three existing MHC-I α-chain genes
that the human MHC locus encodes (see figure 2)[32]. The MHC-I genes can be homozygous
or heterozygous inherited, giving a potential set of 3-6 different MHC-I molecules depending
on the degree of heterozygosity. The α-chain has three domains namely α1, α2, and α3. It is
the α1 and α2 domains that span the membrane forming a peptide binding groove allowing
for peptides to bind to the MHC-I molecules (see figure 3)[30]. The peptide binding groove
for MHC-I is closed at both ends by conserved tyrosine residues, restricting the size of the
binding peptide to a length of 8-10 amino acids, with 9 amino acids being the most abundant
length[82]. Within the binding groove reside binding pockets, which is where specific peptide
residues bind to MHC-I, these residues are called anchor residues. MHC-I binding pockets P2
and P9 are the most abundant anchor positions, therefore peptides being presented on MHC-I
are selected on their ability to bind to these pockets (see figure 3)[83].

The MHC-II molecule is a heterodimer with a binding platform composed of two domains that
originate from two chains, namely the α-chain and the β-chain. These two chains span the
membrane and form the peptide-binding groove. The chains each contain two domains, α1
and α2 for the α-chain and β1 and β2 for the β-chain (see figure 3)[82][74]. One of the major
differences between MHC-I and MHC-II is in the peptide-binding groove. In contrast to MHC-
I the MHC-II binding groove is open and is capable of binding peptides of different length,
but mainly of 13-25 amino acids, with 15 amino acids being the most abundant length[56].
The interacting peptide lies in an extended conformation along the peptide-binding groove
and interacts with MHC-II primarily by its peptide binding core, spanning 9 amino acids
(see figure 3). The placement of the binding core varies between peptides and has a differ
in amino acids protruding out from the binding groove. These protruding amino acids are
called the peptide flanking regions (PFRs). The 9-mer peptide binding core interacts with
four binding pockets P1, P4, P6 and P9 within the binding groove (see figure 3). The open
binding groove found in MHC-II allows for several peptide-binding possibilities, and finding
the correct placement of the peptide is therefore one of the main challenges when predicting
peptide-binding for MHC-II molecules[30][56].

The α-chain and β-chain pairs for MHC-II molecules are encoded by adjacent loci. HLA-

which combinations are the best.
At present more than 25000 human MHC-I and MHC-II HLA alleles for 45 genes have been
identified and annotated by the IPD-IMGT/HLA database of reference sequences[61], making
the HLA system the most polymorphic region in the human genome. This genetic variation

a wide range of pathogens. The HLA polymorphism is not uniformly spread throughout the
entire sequence of the HLA gene, but is more eminent in the peptide-binding groove[68]. For
MHC-I molecules the majority of polymorphisms take place in exon 2 and 3 that encode the
α1 and α2 domains. For MHC-II the majority of polymorphisms take place in exon 2 that
encodes the α1 and β1 domains. In HLA-DR the α-chain is invariant, and is therefore usually

3.4
MHC antigen processing and presentation
As mentioned in section 3.1 antigenic peptides that are presented on MHC-I originate in-
tracellular. This happens due to all proteins being turned over by proteasomes and other
proteases at the end of their lifetime. Besides old proteins 30-70% of all proteins arrive from
newly synthesized proteins that will fail to become accurately folded and thereby become
non-functional proteins, these are called defective ribosomal products. Hence such nuclear,
cytosolic, mitochondrial and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) proteins will be degraded by protea-
somes to fragments that are approximately 3-18 amino acids long. These fragments are next
trimmed by cytosolic peptidases leaving only a few of them to reach the peptide transporter
(TAP) on the ER membranes. In fact TAP most efficiently transports peptides that are 8-12
amino acids long[48][49]. MHC-I molecules are assembled in the ER and are first released
from the ER membrane when bound to a peptide. Proteins that fail to bind to MHC-I are
removed by the ER-associated degradation system to the cytosol where they go through a
new trimming and degradation cycle for new protein synthesis. If needed the peptides can
be trimmed further by the aminopeptidases ERAP1 and ERAP2 in the ER before they are
loaded on MHC-I that generally loads peptides of 9 amino acids length. After pMHC-I load-
ing chaperones are released in the ER allowing completely assembled pMHC-I complexes to
be released from the ER and exported through the Golgi apparatus before reaching the cell
membrane for presentation to CD8+ T-cells[48][30].
In APCs the antigen processing and presentation of peptides on MHC-II can differ and is
more complex than the MHC-I pathway. In the case of MHC-II, antigens enter the APCs
from the extracellular space into intracellular vesicles (early endosomes) called the endocytic
pathway that can happen through three different mechanisms; clathrin-mediated endocytosis,
macropinocytosis or phagocytosis. These different early endosomes all fuse with lysosomes
forming a phagolysosome that decreases its pH to activate proteases that can degrade the
antigens into peptide fragments. It is also in this fusion that pMHC-II complex formation
takes place. Additionally a number of self-peptides arise from common proteins located in
the cytosol by autophagy, where they are enveloped and form a autophagosome allowing for
cross presentation of endogenously derived peptides[3][30][62]. The autophagosome fuses with
lysosomal compartments forming a autophagolysosome that generates pMHC-II complexes.
The MHC-II molecule is assembled in the ER and transported through Golgi where it will have
a peptide bound in the peptide-binding groove that will later be exchanged for the antigenic
peptide in the phagolysosome or autophagolysosome. Afterwards the pMHC-II complex is
transported to the cell surface where it can be recognized by CD4+ T-cells[30][62].
3.5
Mutation types
Gene mutations can take place in somatic cells or germline cells and give rise to genetic
variations in all kinds of cancer. Somatic mutations occur in a single cell and are the main
cause of cancer development, they occur in the deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) of effected cells
and give rise to tumor-specific peptides called neoepitopes (described in section3.6)[54][14].
In a proto-oncogene of a single cell mutations occur changing it to a oncongene that can
form a tumor in its active form by going through multiple divisions and thereby express

amino acid in the normal DNA sequence non-synonymous mutations can cause a change in
the normal protein sequence. In contrary synonymous mutations are silent and leave the amino
acid in the protein unchanged[14]. Non-synonymous mutations include missense mutations,
frameshift mutations, in-frame insertions and in-frame deletions. Missense mutations are point
mutations changing a single nucleotide in the amino acid codon and frameshift mutations are
caused by insertions or deletions of one or more nucleotides changing the reading frame[14][28].
On the other hand in-frame insertions and deletions cause insertion or deletion of one or more
amino acids in the affected exon, without changing the reading frame[66].
3.6
Neoepitopes
Neoepitopes are tumor-specific peptides that arise from somatic mutations in the DNA of
cancer cells, they are important for cancer elimination as they can be recognized as foreign
by the immune system[13]. When the mutated DNA is transcribed and translated the tumor-
specific peptides can go through both antigen-processing pathways, and be presented on MHC-
I and/or MHC-II molecules on the cell surface for recognition by T-cells. However, only a minor
part of mutations will generate new peptides (neoepitopes) as not all DNA mutations will lead
to altered amino acids, even less will go through the antigen presentation process which loads
the peptide to the MHC molecule, and from these even fewer are immunogenic. A neoepitope
that is considered immunogenic can give rise to an immune response, meaning that it can be
recognized by T-cells[14].

In this context an important measurement is the tumor mutational burden (TMB), which
estimates the tumor neoepitope load. TMB is the approximate amount of gene mutations that

to immunotherapy correlates strongly with the TMB and number of predicted neoepitopes
(neoepitope load)[46]. A downside of this is that neoepitopes have to be identified for each
individual patient as the neoepitope landscape in general is heterogeneous across different
patients[11].

3.7
Tumor microenvironment
Tumor development and formation is not only influenced by genetic changes in cancer cells,
but also the changes in the environment surrounding the tumor. This environment is called the
tumor microenvironment (TME), and is created by crosstalk between malignant tumor cells
and T-cells that communicate through a complex signaling network of cytokines, chemokines,
growth factors, enzymes and other mechanisms of interaction (see figure 5). It is also these
factors that maintain the tumor and make it resistant to treatment[6][4]. In addition tumor
cells are capable of hiding tumor antigens from T-cells to avoid their detection. This can be due
to them having an impaired or non-functional antigen processing pathway or ability to down-
regulate MHC-I on their surface, making it impossible to have proper antigen presentation[15].
This reflects the importance of incorporating the TME in the design of new cancer treatments
and how important it is to disable its tumor-promoting and suppression of the immune system
when eradicating cancer cells.

3.8
Cancer Genomics and Next Generation Sequencing
Eighteen years ago next generation sequencing (NGS) technologies appeared on the market,
making it possible to sequence genomes providing us with information to understand cancer
and how cancer cells are created. Illumina sequencing is the most used sequencing platform
with the lowest cost and more than 90% of the world’s sequencing data is generated with it.
It delivers high accuracy and high amounts of error-free reads. Because of this the Illumina
sequencing platform was used to generate the data for this study[72][29].
The Illumina sequencing platform makes it possible to develop whole genome sequencing
(WGS), whole exome sequencing (WXS or WES) and it can also be used to determine gene
expression at transcript level with mRNA sequencing (RNA-Seq). WGS can sequence the
complete human genome and identify genetic mutations, making it possible to analyse and
identify diseases across the whole genome. WES targets only the genomic sequences of exomes
(coding fragments) that constitute less than 2% of the human genome, but contain 85% of
disease-related mutations. In fact generating high accuracy exome sequences has decreased
the cost and time making it favourable to use in understanding genetic diseases compared to
WGS[34][29]. In regards to this WES is the sequencing method used in this study.
3.8.1
Transcriptome analysis
Transcriptome profiling can be done by either microarray or RNA-Seq analyses and are both
widely used tools for analysing gene expression levels. A study performed a transcriptome
profiling on RNA samples from a human T-cell activation experiment to compare the two
above mentioned methods. Results from the microarray and RNA-Seq analysis showed high
correlation between their gene expression profiles, but RNA-Seq was better in detecting low
abundance transcripts, finding low expressed isoforms, and in the identification of genetic
variants. RNA-Seq has many advantages such as allowing for detection of novel transcripts
and allele-specific expressions[84].
RNA-Seq also termed transcriptome sequencing, allows
a snapshot of the whole transcriptome, that expresses all types of RNA’s in the cell. This
provides a cellular transcriptional profile at a given biological moment, which can be used to
measure expression transcriptomes in an organism[78][29]. Due to the advantages of RNA-Seq,
this study uses RNA-Seq to generate gene expressions.

3.9.1
Somatic variant calling
Many bioinformatic tools have been developed to identify somatic mutations from NGS data,
it is however difficult to detect somatic mutations with confidence due to the low frequency
of these mutations in the genome and due to sequencing errors in the data[17]. To perform
discovery of both germline and somatic mutations in NGS data, the genome analysis toolkit
(GATK) has established a robust pipeline that consists of three main steps; pre-processing of
data, somatic variant calling, and filtering detected mutations. The data for this study was
processed with GATK 3.8, which is described in the following and since this study is focused
around neoepitopes, the data is composed by discovery of somatic variants in WES data (see
figure 6).
1. Pre-processing: According to the GATK guidelines this step includes trimming, qual-
ity check and removal of adaptors from reads in a NGS data sample by a tool named
TrimGalore[37] and alignment of the reads to a reference genome with use of Burrows-
Wheeler Aligner[41].
2. Somatic variant calling: GATK recommends the use of Mutect2[17] for somatic vari-
ant calling. Mutect2 is a state-of-the-art tool in detection of somatic mutations and is
particularly useful for studying cancer subclones, as it has high specificity and sensi-
tivity, especially for mutations with very low allele-fractions. After the pre-processing
Mutect2 takes the NGS data from matched normal and tumor DNA as input. Firstly
it removes low quality reads and keeps candidate variants. Secondly, the variants are
validated through six filters to remove artifacts. Finally the variants are filtrated against
a COSMIC reference list of known germline variants[17]. This is the identification of
somatic mutations illustrated on figure 6, which creates the VCF files.

3.9.2
HLA Typing
Traditionally experimental methods were used to carry out HLA typing through identifica-
tion of HLA variants on the cell surface using antigen-specific antibodies or nucleic acid-based
screening[68]. NGS data has allowed for the development of improved computational tools to
predict HLA genotypes on a larger scale using either WGS, WES or RNA-seq as input[8]. This
approach offers rapid, economical and high-throughput analysis and has become the new and
more practical gold standard approach for HLA typing[68][8]. A study compared different com-
putational methods for HLA typing including OptiType[69], PHLAT[5] and HLAminer[79].
One of the differences between the three tools is that PHLAT and HLAminer can handle both
MHC-I and MHC-II genes, whereas OptiType can only handle MHC-I genes. From this study
Optitype had the most accurate MHC-I predictions with 98% accuracy on WES data, while
the most accurate prediction when comparing both MHC-I and MHC-II was PHLAT with
81% accuracy on RNA-Seq data[8]. This is the identification of HLA’s illustrated on figure 6,
which annotates patient(pan)-specific HLA’s.

MuPeXI is a tool written in python developed to identify potential neoepitopes. The tool
looks for tumor-specific peptides which have a high binding affinity to MHC-I molecules. The
current version (1.2) of MuPeXI uses netMHCpan version 4.1 to predict peptide binding to
MHC-I molecules and interprets variants with the Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor (VEP)
version 95.1[43]. NetMHCpan is a pan-specific prediction approach, which allows for predicting
pMHC binding to any MHC-I molecule with a known sequence. The input for MuPeXI is a
VCF file with somatic mutation calls for a specific cancer patient, a list of that patients HLA’s,
user defined peptide lengths and optionally a RNA gene expression file[11] (see figure 6 (input
for MuPeXI) and figure 7 (pre-processing)). Figure 7 illustrates the MuPeXI workflow and in
the following each step in the workflow will be described.
1. VEP version 95.1: VEP is a tool for analysis, annotation and prioritization of ge-
nomic variants from any species with an assembled genome sequence, and an annotated
gene set. Therefore it is used in MuPeXI to determine the effect of the mutation on the
protein sequence (see figure 7 A). The default annotation is to use Ensembl transcripts,
which is the most rich annotation as it removes the potential for errors in annotation,
providing the largest set of transcript isoforms for the input species. The output from
VEP includes information of genes, and transcript related information as gene ID, gene
symbol, transcript ID, RefSeq ID, amino acid change, mutation consequence (e.g. in-
dels, frameshifts and missense variant mutations), information of affected exons and
introns[43]. When getting output from one gene with same genomic position from VEP
it can include multiple transcripts with distinct amino acid changes, hence different mu-
tations at the same position. This is due to VEP providing information on every genomic
feature that each input variant overlaps, meaning that if a variant overlaps a gene with
multiple transcripts (alternate splicing variants) then the information for each of these
transcripts is annotated. This is not accounted for in MuPeXI, meaning that MuPeXI
predicts neoepitopes for all possible mutations even though they have the same genomic
position. This could easily be discarded from the VEP output by several options that
VEP provides. These options are not chosen due to the possibility of losing biologically
relevant information as for example the "real" functional effect of the variant[43].
2. Neoepitope extraction: Neoepitopes are predicted with information from the somatic
mutation positions in the VEP file. For in-frame mutations (missense, insertions and
deletions) the mutated protein sequence is taken directly from the Ensembl proteome
reference and changed with the mutated nucleotide(s). For frameshift mutations the
mutated protein sequence is annotated by applying the nucleotide changes to the cDNA
reference and translating the mutant cDNA sequence to a protein sequence. Finally
MuPeXI chops the normal and mutated sequences into the input peptide lengths, where
all peptides generated from the mutated sequences are potential neoepitopes[11] (see
figure 7 B).
3. Similarity to normal peptides: In this step peptides that are similar to the human
proteome are rated low as these are most likely non-immunogenic due to central toler-
ance. The chopped mutated peptides are compared with the chopped normal peptides
to search for identical matches, and if they match they are removed from the potential
neoepitopes. In addition the most similar normal peptide is identified for each neoepi-
tope deriving from indels and frameshift mutations, by searching the reference proteome
for the most identical peptide with maximum four mismatches (see figure 7 C).

4. Predicting peptide-MHC binding: NetMHCpan is trained on artificial neural net-
works (ANNs), and is a state-of-the-art method for predicting pMHC binding. In the
early versions of netMHCpan the machine learning prediction models were trained on
data from in vitro pMHC binding affinity (BA) assays. This created a relatively high
rate of false positive peptide predictions, which was a consequence from only modelling
the single event of peptide-MHC-binding[32]. The new version (4.1) of netMHCpan has
incorporated eluted ligand (EL) assays obtained by liquid chromatography coupled with
mass spectrometry, to boost peptide prediction. Training the prediction tool on EL data
included indirect information about signals from peptide processing, such as antigen di-
gestion, MHC loading of peptides and pMHC complex cell surface transport[57][32]. As
pMHC binding is a prerequisite for T-cell immunogenicity, multiple studies have shown
that there is a correlation between pMHC binding strength and peptide immunogenic-
ity. Predictions made with BA data alone, have demonstrated to have a weak correlate
of pMHC presentation and peptide immunogenecity, which is why current models are
trained on both BA and EL data showing a decrease in prediction of false positive
peptides[56].
A problem with EL data is that the data from one patient will be collected from mixed
peptides that correspond to different MHC molecules expressed by the given APC,
termed multi-allele peptides. Tools such as GibbsCluster[2] and MixMHCp[7] can be
applied to deconvolute multi-allele EL data into separate motifs, though the motifs still
need to be assigned to their respective MHC alleles. The newest version (4.1) of netMHC-
pan, has solved this by extending the ANN with an algorithm termed NNAlign_MA.
NNAlign_MA has showed to have a high potential in deconvoluting multi-allele EL data
and constructing accurate pan-specific predictors for pMHC presentation[56][57].
The input for netMHCpan is a single sequence or several sequences in FASTA format,
a selection of the desired MHC-I molecule(s) and a peptide size of any given length (see
figure 7 D). The output from the model is a prediction score, that indicates the likelihood
of the peptide being presented by the chosen MHC-I molecule. It also includes a %rank
score, that is a normalized binding affinity calculated by comparing it to predictions
of a set of random natural peptides across different MHC molecules, which enables
interspecific MHC binding prediction comparisons. Here the developers advise to select
candidate binders based on the %rank score rather than the prediction score[57][56].
To predict MHC-I neoepitopes both the mutated and normal peptides in all the user
defined peptide lengths are provided to netMHCpan together with the user defined
MHC-I molecules.
As the aim of this study is to predict neoepitopes presented by MHC-II molecules the
current version of MuPeXI can be improved to meet this challenge by incorporating
netMHCIIpan version 4.0, a tool capable of predicting pan-specific binding of peptides to
MHC-II alleles of know sequence[56]. NetMHCIIpan is developed exactly like netMHC-
pan, for that reason all aforementioned information about netMHCpan also applies to
netMHCIIpan. The output from netMHCIIpan will then be used in the new version of
MuPeXI to be able to predict potential neoepitopes for MHC-II molecules.

1. Gene expression: Kallisto[12] which is used for the transcriptome analysis has a boot-

3. Hydrophobicity: Hydrophobicity has shown to be an important biochemical criteria

immunogenic neoepitopes in several studies ([71],[16]). Gabriel N. and Mauno V. did
a detailed study[71] of predicted MHC-I neoepitopes that were 8-11 amino acids in

position contain more hydrophobic amino acids than wild-type sequences, indicating
that the hydrophobicity of neoepitopes is an important feature when selecting neoepitope
candidates[71]. Chowell, D. et al. did a study [16] based on an interrogation of 9888
MHC-I peptides, where they found a strong correlation in TCR contact amino acids
being hydrophobic and immunogenic MHC-I neoepitopes. Due to this they trained and
validated a hydrophobicity-based ANN, which enhanced existing prediction algorithms
by decreasing the number of neoepitope candidates, improving their likelihood of being
immunogenic and thereby different from self-peptides[16].
3.10
Antigen Garnish
Antigen Garnish is a neoepitope prediction tool implemented in a R library called antigen.garnish[59].
Garnish_affinity is the main function in the library, which performs ensemble neoepitope pre-
diction and neoepitope quality analysis. The garnish_affinity function predicts all possible
neoepitope sequences with use of an input VCF file with somatic mutation calls for a specific
cancer patient, an optional RNA gene expression file, and an optional peptide length with a
default length on 8-15[59]. The MHC affinity of these neoepitopes is predicted in an ensem-
ble of multiple tools, namely netMHC[1], netMHCII[31], netMHCpan[32], netMHCIIpan[56],
MHCnuggets[67], and MHCflurry[51] (see orange boxes in figure 8). This ensemble MHC affin-
ity prediction was tested with public available databases. Here they found that the ensemble
prediction had the lowest prediction error, maintains accuracy and increases the precision
of affinity predictions compared with the single prediction algorithms[58]. To predict pep-
tide immunogenecity and do quality analysis of the predicted neoepitopes Antigen Garnish
implemented the features illustrated as blue boxes in figure 8, and are described in following:
• Filters the neoepitopes against wild-type peptide sequences to remove non-immunogenic
peptides[58].
• Analyses quality based on a quality metric, differential agretopicity index (DAI) which
is the ratio of MHC affinity of the mutant peptide to MHC affinity of the non-mutated
match. This ratio is especially interesting if the mutation falls within an anchor position.

4
Cancer immunotherapy and neoepitope vaccines
Cancer immunotherapy has shown benefit in many cancer types over the last decade. It can
take many forms such as T-cell therapies[33], immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICBs)[24], and
vaccine-based therapies[52]. They are distinct strategies for immunotherapy but they all have
a common goal, namely to allow the T-cells to infiltrate and recognize the tumor and have
an effective function within the TME. Several immunotherapies are already approved by the
U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), among these are ICBs, witch are by now standard
treatment for specific tumor types such as metastatic melanoma, increasing the overall survival
rate for many patients[15].
4.1
Immune checkpoint inhibitors
The intention of ICBs is to overcome tumor-mediated T-cell dysfunction allowing for proper T-
cell activation and effector function[24]. The co-inhibitory immune checkpoints are molecules
located on the T-cell surface that function as regulatory molecules when interacting with a
co-stimulatory molecule such as CD28. This interaction balances the immune system when
e.g. infected by a pathogen. In contrast APCs and tumor cells within the TME generally
express co-inhibitory ligands.
These co-inhibitory ligands interact with co-inhibatory im-
mune checkpoints on T-cells such as programmed cell death protein (PD-1) and cytotoxic
T-lymphocyte-associated antigen 4 (CTLA-4), which both are negative regulators of the T-
cell function. PD-1 interacts with co-inhibitory ligands PD-L1 or PD-L2 on tumor cells and
APCs and CTLA-4 interacts with B7 on APCs[15]. ICBs will block these interactions di-
minishing the inhibition of T-cell function. FDA-approved ICBs are ipilimumab targeting
CTLA-4, nivolumab and pembrolizumab targeting PD-1 and atezolizumab the anti-PDL1
therapy targeting PD-L1[15][24].
Even though these drugs have shown marked success, ICBs currently only benefit a subset of
patients whom generally have a 10 to 40% response rate. This is due to ICBs having a strong
dependency on "hot" tumors with pre-existing anti-tumor T-cells. If the patient has a "cold"
tumor with a low number of tumor antigen-specific T-cells then they will respond poorly to
ICBs as they will not have the T-cells to kill of the cancer cells[38]. Due to this the new
breaking point for cancer research is to find a strong and specific activation of the immune
system against cancer cells, which is what therapeutic cancer vaccines are all about[45]. These
approaches can then be used in combination with ICBs, where this combination therapy
hopefully can elicit a strong and potent anti-tumor immunity against cancer cells[38].
4.2
Therapeutic cancer vaccines
The essence of therapeutic cancer vaccines is to stimulate the immune system to elicit an
adaptive anti-tumor response by using tumor antigens. This anti-tumor response is aimed to
kill of cancer cells and prevent tumor re-growth and metastasis by continuous surveillance[45].
Though in comparison to other immunotherapies such as T-cell therapies and ICBs, thera-
peutic cancer vaccines have failed to show clinical benefit up to date. This is due to many
factors, as the complicated TME and unsatisfactory designs. Besides the important factors
as an optimal innate immune response initiation, adjuvants, vaccine delivery selection and re-
programming of the TME, one of the crucial steps in development of an effective therapeutic
cancer vaccine is the identification of appropriate tumor antigens[77]. Tumor antigens can be
of two types namely, tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) or tumor-specific antigens (neoepi-
topes). TAAs are not only expressed by cancer cells but also by normal cells which increases
the chances of self-tolerance and autoimmunity. On the other hand neoepitopes can elicit a

strong immune response with specific anti-tumor immunity and high immunogenecity without
damaging the host cell. Despite neoepitope based vaccines requiring long production periods
with high cost caused by personalized identification, the strategies have been tested in early
human clinical trials showing very promising results by numerous pre-clinical models[77].
4.3
Combination therapy
A pre-clinical study done by Liu, L. et al.[42] tested a combination immunotherapy in triple
negative breast cancer murine models with "cold" tumors. The therapeutic cancer vaccine was
constructed by nanoparticles delivering mRNA encoding tumor antigens to dendritic cells in
lymph nodes in combination with an anti-CTLA-4 ICB. This therapy inhibited tumor growth
and elicited a tumor-specific T-cell response.
The study supports the use of combination
immunotherapy, but has yet not been tested in human clinical trials[42].
4.4
MHC-II specific poly-neoepitope mRNA vaccine
A study done by Kreiter, S. et al.[36] on three independent murine tumour models with a
significant fraction of non-synonymous mutations demonstrated high immunogenicity with a
high CD4+ T-cell recognition.
They propose a poly-neoepitope mRNA vaccine, in which
neoepitopes are selected entirely with a bioinformatic approach based on their pMHC-II bind-
ing and expression level, using WES data to identify mutations. In mice trials their findings
induced strong tumor control, diminishing growth of aggressively growing tumors. Not only
did the vaccine reshape the TME, but it also induced cross-priming of tumor-specific CD8+
T-cell responses against the tumor antigen, indicating an orchestration of an effective immune
response. This allowed for successfully targeting of poorly immunogenic tumors by induction
of tumor-specific CD4+ T-cell responses. An explanation for high CD4+ T-cell recognition
of mutations can be that the pMHC-II binding is less strict in length and sequence require-
ments for peptides than the pMHC-I binding. This bioinformatic approach allows targeting of
multiple mutations at once which is a promising strategy to solve problems in current cancer
treatments, such as antigen escape and clonal heterogeneity, which is distinct generic alter-
ations among cancer cells within the same tumor. Finally they were capable of applying the
same predictive algorithm on human cancer data and based on their findings they initiated a
first-in-concept trial in melanoma patients confirming that it is possible to produce a ’just in
time’ poly-neoepitope mRNA cancer vaccine[36].
4.5
Neoepitope specific CD8 T-cell responses after PD-L1 inhibition
A study done by Holm, J. et al.[25] tested the number of neoepitope-reactive CD8+ T-cells
(NARTs) in peripheral blood of 24 patients with metastatic urothelial carcinoma (mUC) fol-
lowing treatment with atezolizumab. With use of pMHC multimer libraries peripheral blood
samples were screened pre-, during-, and post-treatment, showing a increase in the number of
NARTs for patients with controlled disease compared to progressive disease. Approximately
20% of the patients with prior fatal disease experienced long-term survival after treatment
with atezolizumab. This study used MuPeXI to predict MHC-I neoepitopes and saw that an
EL %rank score < 0.5 and expression level > 0.1 TPM correlated with neoepitope recognition
by CD8+ T-cells. In addition they did not see any improved T-cell recognition of neoepitopes
for any specific mutation type, though the majority of predicted MHC-I neoepitopes were
from missense mutations. Based on mRNA expression levels they also observed that a higher
T-cell infiltration is crucial for a broader NART expansion. Though it still needs further in-
vestigation, the study suggests that patients with mUC have a higher chance of responding to
ICBs, if they have early-stage NART differentiation and activation[25].

5.1
Incorporation of MHC-II predictions to MuPeXI version 1.2
Recapping that the aim of this study is to predict MHC-II neoepitopes, the first step was to
expand MuPeXI version 1.2 to not only predict MHC-I neoepitopes, but also MHC-II neoepi-
topes. As stated in section 3.3 HLA-DR, HLA-DP and HLA-DQ are the three existing MHC-II
molecules in human. To predict binding of peptides to these MHC-II molecules, netMHCI-
Ipan was incorporated into the already existing MuPeXI version 1.2 tool. The MHC alleles
are given to MuPeXI as an input string, an example for netMHCpan could be; ′HLA −A01 :
01, HLA −A11 : 01′ and an example for netMHCIIpan could be;
′DRB1_0101, HLA −
DQA10101−DQB10201′. To incorporate netMHCIIpan a function with a path to netMHCI-
Ipan and a function to run netMHCIIpan was added to the program. The new version of
MuPeXI can therefore run both netMHCpan and netMHCIIpan to predict peptide binding by
the function stated in supplementary code 1. This function checks if the alleles given as input
are MHC-II or MHC-I and returns a string that is marked with either ”2_MHC or ”1_MHC
in the beginning. This specific MHC marking makes MuPeXI capable of distinguishing be-
tween MHC-I or MHC-II alleles and thereby run either netMHCpan or netMHCIIpan for
neoepitope prediction.
As the output format from netMHCpan and netMHCIIpan is structured differently a new
function was needed to transform the output file from netMHCIIpan into the same format
as the output from netMHCpan. This function is stated in supplementary code 2, and takes
either a BA file or a combined BA and EL file. It accounts for the different columns in the
two types of output files and builds a dictionary with relevant neoepitope info as affinity, rank
and score. This dictionary is then used in a function to write all relevant peptide info to the
output file from MuPeXI version 2.0.

The performance of MuPeXI was benchmarked against Antigen Garnish another neoepitope
prediction tool (described in section 3.10).
Anitgen Garnish predicts the pMHC binding
according to an ensemble prediction from several peptide binding affinity prediction tools.
This benchmark was used to investigate if the two tools predicted the same neoepitopes and
ranked them similarly. In addition the immunogenic MHC-I neoepitopes derived from the 24
mUC patients could be used to investigate how Antigen Garnish would rank these neoepitopes
in comparison to MuPeXI. Due to Antigen Garnish crashing when running several of the HLA-
II alleles MuPeXI was benchmarked with use of the HLA-I predictions. Even though this is
not optimal as the study is about predicting MHC-II neoepitopes it is useful to investigate
how MuPeXI predicts MHC-I neoepitopes compared to Antigen Garnish as this results will
indicate if it also should be done for MHC-II neoepitopes.
As mentioned in section 3.10 Antigen Garnish is a R library with several dependencies, and
it relies on having an ANN field in the input VCF file with the genomic variants. The input
VCF file for each of the 26 mUC patients was created with snpEff by use of a bash script
(see supplementary code 8). A bash script was made to run Antigen Garnish for all 26 mUC
patients simultaneously (see supplementary code 10), as previously done to run MuPeXI (see
section 5.4.2). In the Antigen Garnish bash script the python script to select HLA’s was edited
to only select the HLA-I alleles in the pan-HLA file, and convert them to fit the Antigen
Garnish input. Antigen Garnish can only use an expression file if all the transcripts in the
input VCF file contain an expression in the expression file. Therefore it was chosen not to add
the expression file to the prediction, they can be added afterwards if necessary. The input to

After the first run of Antigen Garnish the output files were enormous and a single patient file

only keep the rows with mutated peptides. This was done as each row in the MuPeXI output

mutated peptide and wild type had its own row in the Antigen Garnish output.
Compared to MuPeXI each transcript had it’s own row in the Antigen Garnish output file. To
make the Antigen Garnish output comparable with the MuPeXI output, the final processing
of the Antigen Garnish output file, was to merge all transcripts together with the same infor-
mation in the remaining columns. This was done in python by use of a dictionary containing
all columns as keys besides transcript ID, and the transcript ID as value. By use of a loop
the transcripts were added on a string if the key had been seen before, and was finally printed
in a new output file. This merge of transcripts that represent the same information reduced
the Antigen Garnish output file from 7,025,803 predicted neoepitopes, to 1,173,782 predicted
neoepitopes.
5.7.1
Output features
The output from Antigen Garnish contained predictions from netMHC[1], netMHCpan[32]
and MHCflurry[51], wheras it did not contain predictions from MHCnuggets even though the
tool was mentioned in the Antigen Garnish article[58]. The ensemble score is an arithmetic
mean of the BA MHC affinity (nM) from all prediction tools run on the peptides by Antigen
Garnish. An affinity < 500 nM indicates weak binders where a affinity < 50 nM indicates
strong binders. If both netMHC and netMHCpan or both MHCflurry and netMHCpan were
run the superior prediction from netMHC or MHCflurry was used to calculate the ensemble
score. If both netMHC and MHCflurry were run the mean prediction score from these was
used to calculate the ensemble score.
If the allele was not present in neither netMHC or
MHCflurry the ensemble score was empty, and then the only prediction parameter present
was the EL %rank from netMHCpan. This is due to netMHCpan allowing for a much broader
HLA identification[32].
Antigen Garnish outputs a dai_uuid and blast_uuid column. The dai_uuid column represents
mutated peptides with a known wild type peptide for comparison. Whereas the blast_uuid
column is present when the mutated peptide was found to match a reference proteome with
blast[70]. If a mutated peptide had no value in the dai_uuid column then it was not input as a

were present, they were used to pair peptides to their matches for differential agretopicity index
(DAI) calculation, which as mentioned in section 3.10 is the ratio between the affinity of the
mutated peptide and the affinity of the wild type peptide. Due to large affinity predictions
being rather skewed, the developers of Antigen Garnish implemented a correction of the ratio
with following formula:
DAI = wt_affinity
mut_affinity ∗
1
(1 + (0.0003 ∗wt_affinity))
(2)
where wt_affinity is the affinity of the wild type and mut_affinity is the affinity of the mutated
peptide.
This correction was done to prevent very high DAI values occurring from cases
where both the mutated peptide and the wild type bind weakly, but the mutant still having
a lower affinity than the wild type. The blast calculation was done by using the NCBI C
Toolkit BLAST command line application where the BLOSUM62 matrix was used to calculate
sequence similarity between the mutated peptide and the normal peptide found by the blast
search[70]. Finally the min_DAI column is based on the most conservative (lowest) value
from either DAI or blast.
As mentioned in section 3.10 Antigen Garnish outputs the foreignness score and dissimilarity
of the predicted neoepitopes. For foreignness score a value of 0 to 1 indicates the probability
of TCR recognition, where 1 indicates the highest homology to immunogenic peptides. It is
calculated by summing the alignments in IEDB immunogenic peptides. For dissimilarity a
value of 0 to 1 indicates alignment of the neoepitope to the non-mutated reference proteome,
where 1 is the greatest dissimilarity.
5.7.2
Immunogenicity filtering
The Antigen Garnish library has a function called garnish_antigens which takes the antigen
output file, an affinity threshold, a DAI threshold, a dissimilarity threshold, and a foreignness
threshold as input. In the paper describing Antigen Garnish[58] they classify predicted neoepi-
topes by setting the affinity threshold to 50 (classically defined neoepitopes), DAI threshold
to 10 (alternatively defined neoepitopes), dissimilarity threshold to 0.75 (high dissimilarity
neoepitopes) and foreignness threshold to 0.9 (IEDB high neoepitopes) for the immunogenecity
filtering[58], based on data from one patient. The function selects a neoepitope if it fulfills one
of the threshold criteria and outputs a column defining which recognition features the neoepi-
tope was selected by. The Antigen Garnish predicted MHC-I neoepitopes for the 26 mUC
patients were run through the garnish_antigens function with use of the above thresholds.
As the thresholds for DAI, dissimilarity and foreignness score do not have a fixed value this
study used an alternative method to define the threshold. By use of the raw Antigen Garnish
predictions the mean value across all patients was found for dissimilarity, foreignness score, and
DAI. The mean for dissimilarity was 0.02, for foreignness score it was 0.2 and for DAI it was
0.1. As the values for dissimilarity and foreignness score were both above 0 they were accepted
even though they were very low. For DAI the value was not above 1, which implies that the
affinity for the wild type is better than for the mutated peptide. Therefore the method used
for selecting the DAI threshold was to choose all neoepitopes with a threshold above 1 and
from these find the one with the lowest threshold, which gives a DAI threshold of 1.000048.
By using these new thresholds the run of the garnish_antigens function was repeated.

Tools for plot visualisation
Most plots were performed using R Statistical Software (v4.1.2; R Core Team 2021[55]) in
R-studio[63]. Data processing for plots was made with the tidyverse library[81]. Barplots, pie
charts and box plots were made with the ggplot function from the ggplot2 library[80].
The ggplot function allows for group-wise comparisons to indicate if two groups are signifi-
cantly different. Here different test arguments can be used (t-test or wilcox-test) and due to
the data type in this study the non-parametric two-sided wilcox-test was selected. The reason
for using the non-parametric two-sided wilcox-test instead of the two-sided t-test, is that the
t-test hypothesises that the mean of both groups is the same, and thereby assumes that the
data is normally distributed in both groups. The non-parametric two-sided wilcox-test is a
distribution-free test, hence it does not require a distribution of either data sets.
The venn diagrams were made using Python version 3[75] with use of the venn2 function from
the matplotlib library[27] .
Performance measurements were done in R with use of the pROC library[60]. This function
can calculate the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) probabilities and the area under the
ROC curve (AUC) values. The ROC probabilities can be used to make a probability curve,
that is commonly used to evaluate the performance of binary classifications by representing
the classifier’s performance with a graphical representation[23].
A ROC curve shows the trade-off between the true positive rate (sensitivity) and false positive
rate (specificity) at a variety of thresholds for a single classifier. The true positive rate (TPR) is
the number of true positives, which illustrates the probability of an actual positive occurrence
will be classified as positive. The false positive rate (FPR) is the number of false positives,
which illustrates how often a negative occurrence actually will be classified as positive. The
TPR can be calculated as following:
TPR = Sensitivity =
TP
TP + FN
(3)
Here TP is the number of true positives and FN is the number of false negatives. The false
positive rate (FPR) can then be calculated as following:
FPR = 1 −Specificity =
FP
FP + TN
(4)
Here FP is the number of false positives and TN is the number of true negatives[23]. The
TPR and FPR are calculated by use of the roc function from the pROC library[60].
The AUC score is calculated by taking the area under the ROC curve, which the auc function
from the pROC library[60] calcualtes using the TPRs and FPRs.
In this study the ROC curve and AUC score are used to compare the performance of different
features for selection of neoepitope candidates.

As mentioned in section 5.8 performance of each feature can be summarized into a single
measure which is the AUC score.
The AUC score can be used to evaluate the quality of
the features to distinguish between immunogenic and non-immunogenic neoepitopes.
The
higher the AUC score is, the better the feature is at predicting immunogenic neoepitopes.
Normally the AUC score ranges between 0.5 and 1, where 1 means that the feature is perfect
in classifying immunogenic from non-immunogenic neoepitopes. It is said that an AUC score
on 0.5 suggest no discrimination between classes (i.e.
random), hence the feature has no
predictive power. AUC values are equivalent to the wilcox-test statistics from figure 20 and
21, but gives a better visualisation of the results. Accordingly figure 22 B shows that the BA
%rank from netMHC is the best performing feature with an AUC score on 0.63. Additionally

BA affinity from MHCflurry have AUC score on 0.59, 0.56, 0.56, 0.56, and 0.53. Dissimilarity
has a AUC score on 0.52 and foreignness score is the worst performing feature with an AUC
score under 0.5 i.e worse than random, meaning that it cannot be used to discriminate between

7
Discussion
The worldwide exponential increase in the number of cancer incidences has forced researchers
in many new directions in relation to cancer treatment. In regard to immunotherapies ICBs are
among the ones with best clinical outcomes but still with low specificity and varying outcomes,
which is why the interest in therapeutic cancer vaccines has increased drastically[15][25][42].
Besides safety and efficiency, the desire when developing vaccines is to create an of-the-shelf
product that can treat most patients with a specific cancer type or preferable a broad range
of cancer types. Though this scenario is not realistic, if the desire is to eradicate cancer cells
and prevent tumor re-growth without chances of selecting antigens causing autoimmunity or
self-tolerance. As seen on figure 10 the variation in number of predicted neoepitopes between
patients is enormous.
This could be due to everyone having distinct genomes and cancer
being unpredictable, causing distinct amounts and types of mutations. In addition figure 25
and 27 in appendix E shows the spread of different HLA’s between patients which illuminates
the fact of MHC polymorphism being crucial in vaccine development. Thereby the produc-
tion of efficient therapeutic cancer vaccines depends on a proper selection of tumor-specific
antigens (neoepitopes). Moreover, it has been shown that a high neoepitope load equals more
somatic mutations, which increases the chances of TCRs recognizing pMHC complexes[77][14].
Currently most clinical studies and prediction tools have been focusing on the pMHC-I with
CD8+ T-cell response as this is the protagonist in killing off cancer cells. As these investiga-
tions have shown mixed results, recently the focus has been on pMHC-II with CD4+ T-cells
which plays a key role in the onset of cellular immunity[73][36]. Because of this recent interest
in activating CD4+ T cells there will be a need for predicting MHC-II neoepitopes. Therefore,
this study has incorporated MHC-II neoepitope predictions into the already existing MuPeXI
version 1.2, thereby developing MuPeXI version 2.0, which predicts both MHC-I and MHC-II
neoepitopes.
NGS data from 26 patients with mUC was used to evaluate MuPeXI version 2.0. Here all
possible neoepitopes from the given input lengths (9:11 for MHC-I and 14:16 for MHC-II) were
predicted, resulting in 1,287,923 neoepitopes for MHC-I and 1,830,166 neoepitopes for MHC-II
(see section 6.1). Different strategies have been investigated and can be used to decrease this
number and select neoepitope candidates for immunogenicity verification.
7.1
Neoepitope filtering
Most neoepitope prediction algorithms (including MuPeXI) do not include T-cell recognition
predictions due to lack of data. Therefore there is a crucial need of defining additional fea-
tures that can identify the best neoepitopes predicted to mediate T-cell responses (candidate
neoepitopes)[10]. This study has looked into different ways of identifying the best candidate
neoepitopes, using different filtering methods which will be discussed in the following sections.
7.1.1
Binding affinity
Since the peptide needs to be presented on a MHC molecule before it can be recognized by
T-cells, the binding affinity of the peptide is key in the neoepitope selection. Therefore it is
crucial to use tools that are superior in predicting the neoepitope binding affinity. In a study
done by Reynisson, B et al.[57] they benchmarked netMHCpan version 4.1 and netMHCIIpan
version 4.0 with other broadly used prediction tools as MixMHCpred and MHCflurry for
netMHCpan and MiXMHC2pred, MHCnuggets and DeepSeqPanII for netMHCIIpan. These
different benchmarks showed a significantly higher performance of netMHCpan version 4.1
and netMHCIIpan version 4.0, confirming that MuPeXI is using the best tools for predicting

binding affinity of neoepitopes[57]. Both netMHCpan and netMHCIIpan includes predictions
based on BA and EL data. Because EL data contains signals from peptide processing, MHC
loading of peptides and pMHC complex surface transport, the EL %rank score was chosen
to predict the neoepitope binding affinity. To ensure that candidate neoepitopes are strong
binders they were exclusively selected if they had a EL %rank < 2.0 for MHC-II and EL %rank
< 0.5 for MHC-I[56].
7.1.2
Expression level
It has been shown that gene expression level is important when identifying neoepitopes, but
specific thresholds remain undefined[10]. As the threshold for gene expression is arbitrary, one
should be sceptical if neoepitope selection is based only on this parameter. If the RNA file for
a given patient is available, it can be used to filter away low expressed neoepitopes, as they
will not be found as frequently and will therefore most likely not be presented by the MHC
on the surface of APC as frequently as highly expressed neoepitopes. Based on the threshold
uncertainty and only knowledge about the peptide being present in the patient if there is an
expression over zero TPM, the expression threshold for neoepitope candidates was set to 0.1
TPM. This is an commonly used threshold, e.g. in the study done by Holm, J et al.[25]. This
value did filter the predicted MHC-I and MHC-II neoepitopes for the 26 mUC patients thus
retaining a sufficient amount.
7.1.3
MHC-II neoepitope filtering
As stated in section 3.3 a peptide interacts with the open binding groove of MHC-II by its
9-mer core. Because MHC-II neoepitopes are predicted with different lengths (here 14:16),
multiple peptides with same mutation will bind to the same HLA-allele and have the same
core. They will only be distinguished from each other based on the PFRs, binding affinity, core
reliability and expression level. As these peptides are almost identical and express the same
gene and mutation, an assumption was made that if the predicted neoepitope had the same
9-mer core, HLA-allele, mutation consequence and genomic position, then only the neoepitope
with best binding affinity, best core reliability and highest expression was selected (see section
5.2). The pipeline for the filtering is shown on figure 9 and was implemented into MuPeXI
2.0 to supply an output file with neoepitope candidates. Using this filter the total number
of predicted MHC-II neoepitopes for the 26 patients with mUC decreased from 1,830,166 to
5132. This decision to filter the predicted MHC-II neoepitopes is based on assumptions, but
it was considered reasonable as the number of predicted MHC-II neoepitope candidates was
close to the number of predicted MHC-I neoepitope candidates when filtering on a EL %rank
< 0.5 and expression level > 0.1 TPM.
7.1.4
Length preference for MHC-II neoepitopes
In this study neoepitopes were predicted for three different lengths to investigate MHC-II
length preference, though knowing from theory (see section 3.3) that there is an abundance of
15-mer neoepitopes for MHC-II. This was confirmed on figure 12 showing that summing the
percentage for neoepitope length for all mUC patients, 54% of predicted MHC-II neoepitope
candidates were predicted to be length 15 vs.
31% for length 14 and 15% for length 16.
As stated in section 3.9.3 models trained on EL data (e.g. netMHCpan and netMHCIIpan)
can learn the length preference as it contains signals from processing and transport of the
peptides[56]. It could be a reduction in the amount of data processing to exclusively predict
15-mer MHC-II candidate neoepitopes, as it is only a small selection of neoepitope candidates
that will end up in production and used in a vaccine.

7.2.1
MHC-II neoepitopes containing MHC-I neoepitopes
Based on the available data it was interesting to investigate if there would be a abundance of
predicted MHC-II neoepitope candidates containing immunogenic MHC-I neoepitopes com-

figure 13 B does not imply that the number of predicted MHC-II neoepitopes containing im-

distribution (see figure 14) and the fact that only 147 MHC-I neoepitopes were immunogenic
it seems more reasonable. As stated in section 6.2 immunogenic MHC-I neoepitopes appeared
for ten of the 24 patients with mUC. To verify more of the MHC-II sequence, MHC-II neoepi-
topes containing more than one MHC-I neoepitope were further investigated. From these ten
patients two of them had MHC-II neoepitopes containing more than one MHC-I neoepitope
with promising values for binding affinity, expression and core reliability. In regards to this
method being very abstract, if the case was to select MHC-II neoepitopes for a vaccine for
these patients, the predicted MHC-II neoepitopes containing immunogenic MHC-I neoepitopes
would be the best choice. This is due to these parts of the sequence have previously shown
to be immunogenic, increasing the possibility of immunogenecity of the predicted MHC-II
neoepitopes.
Keeping in mind that MHC-I and MHC-II have distinct binding preferences
which will change the pMHC interface.

look into the mutation types giving rise to predicted MHC-II neoepitopes. Although the study
done by Holm, J et al.[25] did not see improved T-cell recognition of MHC-I neoepitopes for
any specific mutation type, they did see an abundance of missense mutations which was also
seen for the predicted MHC-II neoepitopes (see figure 15 A). On the other hand it was predom-

vs. 937 neoepitopes with no CD8+ T-cell recognition (see figure 14). Therefore this can only
support the fact that missense mutations are the most abundant accruing mutation type.

7.2.3
Pre-treatment neoepitope load
It is important to find approaches to evaluate the efficacy of immunotherapy treatments for
individual patients, as it will vary whether patients will benefit from the treatment. Therefore
it is crucial to find an approach that can be used as standard practice to decrease production
time and cost, and most importantly to identify if the immunotherapy treatment is expected
to have an effect on the patient, as it would otherwise be preferable to use other forms of
treatment. A widely used approach is to use a high neoepitope load, which has showed to
correlate with successful outcome for patients treated with ICBs[64]. A study done by Saini, K.
et al.[64] illuminated the effectiveness of neoepitope load in patients treated with ICBs, based
on several studies that supported the correlation between beneficial clinical outcome after ICB
treatment and neoepitope load [64]. The approach used in this study was to investigate if the
pre-treatment neoepitope load of the 24 patients with mUC could predict patients outcome
after ICB treatment.
From figure 16 A and B this approach did unfortunately not show
any significance for predicted MHC-I and MHC-II neoepitope load and clinical outcome after
treatment, which contradicts the theory.
7.2.4
Hydrophobicity based neoepitope filtering
As this analysis is solely based on predicted neoepitopes being selected due to their EL %rank,
expression and core reliability (for MHC-II), an addition of more filtering features could in-
crease the likelihood of the candidate neoepitopes actually being immunogenic. One of the
features that can distinguish non-immunogenic self-peptides from immunogenic neoepitopes is
the sequence hydrophobicity as stated in section 3.9.4 and 5.6, where a positive hydrophobicity
index has shown increase in T-cell recognition. With the available data from 24 mUC patients
figure 17 A and B were created, to investigate if a positive hydrophobicity index could indicate
that the neoepitope is immunogenic. Both figures show that hydrophobicity has no significance
in distinguishing between immunogenic and non immunogenic neoepitopes. These results do
not agree with the theory, as hydrophobicity shows no tendency in immunogenic neoepitopes
having a higher hydrophobicity than non-immunogenic neoepitopes based on clinical results
from 24 mUC patients.
7.3
Antigen Garnish predictions
To examine other features used to discriminate neoepitopes, Antigen Garnish was used to
predict MHC-I neoepitopes for the 26 mUC patients. In addition to the pMHC affinity from
MuPeXI it uses features such as dissimilarity, foreignness score, differential agretopicity (min
DAI) and an ensemble score from netMHC and MHCflurry. Antigen Garnish defines netMHC
and MHCflurry as superior prediction tools especially for the reason that these tools have been
trained on quantitative pMHC-I BA measurements from IEDB[76]. Though as mentioned in
section 3.9.3 BA data has showed weak correlate of pMHC presentation and peptide immuno-
genecity. Looking at figure 20 A, and D, and 21 B, C and D the ensemble score, min DAI,
BA affinity from netMHC, BA %rank from netMHC, and EL %rank from netMHCpan show
significance in discriminating immunogenic neoepitopes from non-immunogenic neoepitopes.
Here the BA %rank from netMHC and EL %rank from netMHCpan are superior in signif-
icance, however this analysis has some bias as the neoepitopes were selected from the best
EL %ranks before clinical verification, and the EL %rank from netMHCpan contains values
for all neoepitopes, whereas the ensemble score and BA %rank from netMHC have multiple
empty values for alleles they are not trained on. These results are supported by the AUC
ROC analysis on figure 22 A and B.

7.3.1
Binding affinity in nM compared with %rank
On account of the ensemble score (nM) being the feature used for predicting binding affinity by
Antigen garnish, the individual impact of netMHC and MHCflurry binding affinity predictions
on the ensemble score was investigated. Figure 21 A shows that MHCflurry has no significance
in discriminating immunogenic and non-immunogenic neoepitopes, on the other hand figure 21
B shows that netMHC has significance in discriminating immunogenic and non-immunogenic
neoepitopes. Though the mean value for immunogenic neoepitopes predicted by netMHC is
500 nM which indicates weak binders and only a small amount of neoepitopes are predicted as
strong binders (nM < 50). For MHCflurry the BA values are much lower than for netMHC, this
states that the combination of netMHC and MHCflurry BA predictions is highly influenced
by netMHC BA predictions.
The ensemble score shows lower significance in discriminating immunogenic neoepitopes from
non-immunogenic neoepitopes compared to the BA %rank from netMHC and EL %rank from
netMHCpan. Additionally the mean affinity for immunogenic neoepitopes is around 400 nM
which defines them as weak binders (see figure 20 A). When predicting on affinity the peptides
that have lowest MHC affinity are chosen solely on this parameter, which increases the risk of
excluding alleles that have an allover higher affinity. In contrast the %rank chooses the best
performing peptides from each MHC allele to ensure a broad allele coverage. For both the
BA %rank from netMHC and EL %rank from netMHCpan the mean %rank for immunogenic
neoepitopes is below 0.5 which defines them as strong binders (see figure 20 B and C). To
ensure selection of the best neoepitope candidates for a vaccine that can reach coverage of all
alleles, this study suggests the %rank as the best solution.
7.3.2
Training on BA data versus EL data
Antigen Garnish does not include the EL %rank netMHCpan prediction in the ensemble
score. netMHC is an allele-specific method that contains specific predictors for each MHC
molecule[31]. It is trained only on BA data and it can only predict binding affinities for MHC
molecules with BA data. netMHCpan is a pan-specific method that contains a predictor that
can make predictions for any MHC molecule with a known protein sequence.
To achieve
this pan-specificity, netMHCpan incorporates information about the MHC molecule, using
a pseudo sequence consisting of MHC residues that are considered important for peptide
binding. If the input MHC molecule is unkown then netMHCpan predictions are based on
a similarity calculation to other MHC complexes which increases the bias. netMCHpan is
furthermore trained on both BA and EL data. Including EL data when training netMHCpan,
allows this predictor to include factors such as antigen processing and presentation that could
influence the probability of actual recognition of the pMHC by T-cells.[32]. The study done by
Jurtz, V. et al.[32] showed that training netMHCpan with a combination of BA and EL data
had a significant improvement in the predictive performance of both BA and EL predictions
compared with training on solely BA or EL data[32]. netMHCII and netMHCIIpan are trained
similarly as netMHC and netMHCpan.
7.3.3
Comparing netMHCII and netMHCIIpan performances
Based on the data used for ANN training of netMHCII and netMHCIIpan, the expectation
would be that netMHCII would perform better for known alleles with lots of available data
and netMHCIIpan would be superior for alleles with less data, and for alleles with no available
training data. The study done by Jensen, K. et al.[31] showed exactly this when comparing
netMHCII and netMHCIIpan predictions. Recalling the fact that therapeutic cancer vaccines
are patient specific and that MHC alleles are highly polymorphic within a population, there

7.3.4
Additional Antigen Garnish features
Foreignness score and dissimilarity on figure 20 B and C do not show significance in discrim-
inating between immunogenic and non-immunogenic neoepitopes, and have the lowest AUC
values illustrated on figure 22 B. Additionally figure 23 showed that when filtering for candi-
date neoepitopes neither foreignness score nor dissimilarity had an influence in the selection
of neoepitopes. Looking at the data only a small amount of the neoepitopes were found in

future where there is more data available it will probably have a much stronger influence on
neoepitope prediction models. Dissimilarity is the comparison of the mutated peptide to the
non-mutated reference proteome. A majority of these values are either NA or zero, as such it
was hard to determine from this data if it is a useful feature. Though it would be expected to
have an importance, as more dissimilarity to normal decreases the risk of self-tolerance, hence
increases the chances of T-cell recognition and lowers the risk of autoimmunity.
min DAI on figure 20 D shows significance in discriminating between immunogenic and non-
immunogenic neoepitopes and have the third highest AUC value illustrated on figure 22 B.
Though the mean value for immunogenic neoepitopes is barely above 1, which is a very low
value, as values above 1 indicate a better binding affinity for the mutated peptide versus the
wild type. Filtering for predicted immunogenic neoepitopes with a min DAI threshold on 10
(see figure 23 A) there were not many neoepitopes fulfilling this criteria. When doing the
same filtering with a min DAI threshold on 1.000048 (see figure 23 B) it did not add any new
neoepitopes to the predicted immunogenic neoepitopes from figure 23 A. Based on the results
from figure 23 A and B all predicted immunogenic neoepitopes could be selected solely by use
of the binding affinity threshold on 50 nM.
7.3.5
Immunogenecity filtering
Out of the 4423 experimentally verified MHC-I neoepitopes selected by MuPeXI (EL %rank <

neoepitopes found in the prediction pool from both tools, though Antigen Garnish leaves out

7.4
Suggestion for vaccine development
In section 4 some types of immunotherapies are mentioned, where several ICBs have been
FDA approved (see section 4.1). The 24 patients with mUC were treated with atezolizumab,
and only 20% of these patients experienced long-term survival (see section 4.5). This might
be due to the fact that ICBs helps to disable the TME, but the problem is especially for
"cold" tumors, that do not have many neoepitopes. With no or not sufficient neoepitopes to
be recognized by T-cells, the patients are not capable of raising a strong immune response
to eradicate the tumor. To raise a strong tumor-specific T-cell response and eradicate cancer
cells therapeutic cancer vaccines have been suggested (see section 4.2), but without promising
results. Due to this the pre-clinical study done by Liu, L. et al.[42] tested a combination
therapy of anti-CTLA-4 ICB, and a therapeutic cancer vaccine delivering mRNA encoding
tumor antigens on murine models with "cold" tumors. Results showed a tumor-specific T-cell
response and inhibited tumor growth (see section 4.3).
Up to date the focus in development of therapeutic cancer vaccines has been on CD8+ T-
cells, as they are the main factor in killing off foreign cells (see section 3.1).
Though as
stated in section 3.6, CD4+ T-cells can recognize a broader range of antigens and are required
to generate an effective long lasting T-cell memory. For this the pre-clinical study done by
Kreiter, S. et al.[36] on murine tumor models, proposed a poly-neoepitope mRNA vaccine
selecting solely MHC-II neoepitopes. These results showed strong tumor control, reshaping of
the TME and cross-priming of tumor-specific CD8+ T-cells (see section 4.4).
Based on these results a treatment suggestion for the remaining patients would be to take a
tumor sample post-ICB treatment, make NGS on the samples, and predict both MHC-I and
MHC-II candidate neoepitopes for immunogenicity testing. Due to MHC-I neoepitopes being
shorter than MHC-II neoepitopes, neoepitope candidates could be selected with the intention
of both being presented on MHC-I and MHC-II, if possible based on their results from binding
affinity, expression and other features. These neoepitopes can then be used to produce patient
specific therapeutic cancer vaccines.

The greatest limitation for this study is that pMHC-II complexes have not been stabilized
properly yet, preventing the experimental verification of predicted MHC-II neoepitopes. Due
to this limitation all validation of MuPeXI version 2.0 was done by use of experimentally
verified MHC-I neoepitopes which is a significant bias as MHC-I and MHC-II are very distinct

both in peptide processing, presentation, and are recognized by distinct T-cells. In addition
to this the probability test done in section 6.2 showed that there was a higher probability
(15%) for a predicted MHC-II containing a non-immunogenic neoepitope compared with the
13% probability for a predicted MHC-II neoepitope containing an immunogenic neoepitope,
and there was no significance in finding an immunogenic/non-immunogenic MHC-I neoepitope

In this study the EL %rank < (0.5,2.0) from hence netMHCpan and netMHCIIpan was used

BA %rank from netMHC was the best feature in predicting immunogenic neoepitopes. This
was also shown in the study done by Holm, J. et al.[25], where the BA %rank showed highest
significance in discriminating between immunogenic and non-immunogenic neoepitopes, but
with use of netMHCpan. Though the EL %rank did also show significance in discriminating
between immunogenic and non-immunogenic neoepitopes. Based on this it could be of interest
to investigate a filter on BA %rank < (0.5,2.0) and a combination of EL %rank < (0.5,2.0)
and BA %rank < (0.5,2.0) to see which feature or combination performs best for MHC-I and
MHC-II neoepitope prediction. Recapping that there is no data on experimentally verified
MHC-II neoepitopes, this investigation was not done in this study.
A great insufficiency for pMHC prediction tool is that there is still a lack of data for many
MHC alleles. This hinders the pMHC prediction tools in generating accurate predictions for
MHC alleles which are very different from the alleles used when training the tools. To decrease
this bias an increase in EL and BA data would make these prediction tools much more accurate
when predicting pMHC binding affinities.
For neoepitope prediction tools the need of more NGS data and experimental verified neoepi-
topes is crucial. For this the study done by Bentzen, A. et al.[9] invented a technique using
pMHC multimers labeled with individual DNA barcodes, allowing them to screen more than
1000 pMHC specificities in a single sample.
Their results also made it possible to detect
low-frequency CD8+ T-cells for cancer-restricted antigens. This calls for more NGS data,
covering more HLAs, experimental verifying more neoepitopes, and then when more verified
data is available machine learning can be used to train new ANN models specific for neoepitope
prediction.
An important advance would be addition of more features for candidate neoepitope selection
and improvement of the already existing features; hydrophobicity, dissimilarity, foreignness
score and differential agretopicity index. As feature performance is only tested on 26 mUC
patients in this study, more experimentally verified neoepitope data could help in the validation
of these features.

Conclusion
The aim of this study was to predict MHC-II neoepitopes. This was done by expanding the
already existing MHC-I neoepitope prediction tool, namely MuPeXI version 1.2. A successful
implementation of netMHCIIpan into MuPeXI version 1.2, created MuPeXI version 2.0. By
this new version of MuPeXI it is now possible to predict pan-specific MHC-I and MHC-II
neoepitopes. From this MHC-II neoepitopes were predicted for 26 patients with metastatic
urothelial carcinoma (mUC).
The prediction of MHC-II neoepitopes resulted in substantially more neoepitopes pr. mutation
than when predicting for MHC-I neoepitopes, and due to the fact that peptides bind to the
MHC-II molecule by their 9-mer core a filtering function to select candidate neoepitopes was
implemented in MuPeXI version 2.0. By this, one candidate neoepitope was selected from
predicted neoepitopes with same 9-mer core, HLA-allele, mutation consequence and genomic
position based on best binding affinity, best core reliability and highest expression, though
requiring a binding affinity with a EL %rank < 2.0 and expression level > 0.1 TPM to increase
the chance of actual immunogenecity prior to selection. The filtering resulted in 5131 candidate
MHC-II neoepitopes. This appeared to be reasonable if comparing to the amount of candidate
MHC-I neoepitopes after filtering for EL %rank < 0.5 and expression level > 0.1 TPM (6069
neoepitopes), and the fact that 54% of candidate MHC-II neoepitopes had a length of 15
amino acids which is the most abundant length for pMHC-II binding.
It was not possible to conclude if MuPeXI version 2.0 is actually capable of predicting im-
munogenic MHC-II neoepitopes, as this study has no data available on experimentally verified
MHC-II neoepitopes. Though it was possible to find immunogenic MHC-I neoepitopes inside
the sequence of predicted MHC-II neoepitopes, which were considered as possible candidates
as the immunogenecity of the sequence was verified by CD8+ T-cell responses.
The MHC-I neoepitope predictions from MuPeXI were benchmarked with Antigen Garnish,
which is another neoepitope prediction tool. This was done to see if both tools would predict
neoepitopes similarly and because Antigen Garnish includes additional features in the predic-
tion. It showed that MuPeXI and Antigen Garnish predicted close to the same amount of
predicted MHC-I neoepitopes (which is the same as unique mutations) and had around the
same kind of mutation types for the 26 mUC patients.
From the 6237 experimentally verified MHC-I neoepitopes selected from MuPeXI by an EL
%rank < 0.5 and expression level > 0.1 TPM (scaled up to 200 pr. patient regardless of
criteria), 87% were found in the Antigen Garnish predicted MHC-I neoepitopes. Like the EL
%rank and expression level is commonly used for filtering candidate neoepitopes in MuPeXI
results, Antigen Garnish uses a immunogenecity filtering function to select candidate neoepi-
topes. This filtering function includes an ensemble score, foreignness score, dissimilarity and
differential agretopicity index. By comparing these two filtering methods, Antigen Garnish
only selects 12% of the experimentally verified MHC-I neoepitopes filtered by MuPeXI. In-
vestigating which features had an influence on the Antigen Garnish candidate neoepitopes it
showed that they could be selected solely by their ensemble score (binding affinity), indicat-
ing that the additional features need further adjustments to have an influence in neoepitope
candidate selection.
One of the most important factors when predicting neoepitopes is the pMHC binding, if the
peptide cannot bind to MHC, it will not be a neoepitope and never be capable of orchestrating
an immune response. To measure binding affinity, EL and BA scores can be used. Even though
Antigen Garnish includes netMHCpan in predictions, it only uses the netMHC, netMHCII
and MHCflurry predictions to determine neoepitope binding affinity. In contrary MuPeXI
