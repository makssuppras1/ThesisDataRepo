Abstract
During this project a 3-pump system for a highly viscous carbon fibre infused 2-part epoxy and
a catalyst has been developed. The purpose of this is to allow experiments, in regards to Big
Area Additive Manufacturing using an industrial 6-axis robot arm, to be carried out. It has been
executed through a series of iterations starting with an evaluation of a pre-existing design. In
addition to this, the development of a computer vision feedback system has been initiated to
actively regulate the flow of the three liquids, thereby avoiding over- and under extrusion and in
the future allow printing overhang and bridges.
The 3-pump system consists of three pump modules each containing an external gear pump,
stepper motor and and an electric 3-way valve. Hoses are dimensioned to reduce pressure loss
and transport the three liquids through a custom hose hub and an off-the-shelf static mixer tube.
The hose hub has not only been designed to attach the hoses, via hose tail adapters, along
with the mixer tube, via a custom made lock nut, but also act as a base to stiffen the mixer
tube and mounting 3D-printed nozzles. The 3-pump system is barely capable of pumping an
80Pa · s epoxy while a thinner liquid containing carbon fibres has presented no issues. Mixing
a 30Pa · s epoxy and hardener has proved troublesome as the pump transporting the hardener
stopped working mid-test, leaving any results thereof inconclusive. It is suspected that the
harsh chemicals in the hardener has interacted with the NBR seals in the pump. But symptoms
of other issues, such as leakage and mechanical faults within the pump, have also been shown.
Due to a short time frame this issue has not been further investigated.
The computer vision make use of two cameras, mounted on either side of the mixer tube
to account for any blind spots. The cameras take numerous pictures per second after which,
through post-processing, shine is removed while the images are simplified. Utilizing various
algorithms the software is capable of using these images to detect the direction of the flow
along with the edges of the epoxy lines, used to determine over- and under extrusion. Currently
the post-processing and control of cameras are executed using separate programs, as such, the
link between these remain unsolved and must be finalized in future iterations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Moulding in concrete requires large complex shapes to be created
one way or another. Oftentimes wooden moulds are created by
carpenters, which is an expensive an labour intensive method,
but with the growing industry of 3D printing, research institutes
have begun experimenting with 3D printing these moulds.
To
achieve this. a positive shape is created in expanded polystyrene,
commonly known as Styrofoam, after which a negative mould is
created by 3D printing layers of epoxy tracing the surface of the
positive shape. However, in order to achieve the strength required
multiple layers are required.
To avoid multiple layers, thereby
reducing material usage and production time, introducing carbon
fibres have been considered and experiments thereof are requested.
This chapter presents the project and the the background thereof.
Status quo is declared along with its limitations and which require-
ments are necessary to surpass the current solution. Lastly, the
scope of the project is defined.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.1
Project description
This project is based on a 6-axis robot arm, and how one can utilize it in order to 3D print using
an exotic material; 2-part epoxy infused with carbon fibres, in a manner similar to a common
extrusion based 3D printer. This task requires a specialized pump system to transport the thick
fluid along a set of hoses and automatically mix them before ejecting them onto a surface. The
2-part epoxy consists of a matrix which makes up the bulk of the material and a hardener which
starts a curing process to fully harden the epoxy. Furthermore, a catalyst will be implemented
to control the curing speed, optimizing the printing sequences for certain structures such as
overhangs. The carbon fibres will be pre-mixed into one of the three fluids determined by the
user.
Currently a 2-pump system is in use and act as a stand-in for the pump system to be devel-
oped. The reason behind this upgrade is the considerable limitations in the current system, the
most obvious of which is the number of pumps, which is to be increased to allow for catalyst.
Furthermore, the current system is only capable of delivering a flow of < 1ml/s making print-
ing a slow process. Lastly, the system is not able to handle carbon fibres as they clog up the
pumps.
The pump system to be developed will consist of three pump modules, one for each of the
three liquids, all connected to an off-the-shelf mixer tube. Additionally, the pump system will
include a cleaning mechanism which allows switching between the epoxy and a solvent when-
ever a total flush of the system is required. Furthermore, a control system will be constructed
consisting of drivers for all motors connected to and controlled by a micro-controller. A pre-
liminary design of a single pump module has already been developed during a special course
independent from this project. Therefore, this project will kick off with extensive test and eval-
uation of that module prior to any systematic development.
To better create constant, high quality prints a video feedback system will be implemented.
Cameras will be attached to the end-effector of the robot arm and take photos of the printed
lines. Based off of these photos a computer program can determine faults in the printed epoxy
and regulate the flow of the three liquids accordingly.

Scope of project
The time frame for this project is relatively short compared to the possible outlook for such
an interdisciplinary subject. Hence, a scope has been defined to maintain a clear vision of the
direction which the project is taking. The project can be broken down into two sub-projects;
a 3-pump system and a computer vision feedback system. Thus, a scope is provided for both
sub-projects.
The pump system seeks to create a proof of concept mainly focusing on the total system in
a broad sense, rather than diving deep into and optimizing specific parts of the system. Heavy
analysis will not be carried out, as these are generally time consuming. Likewise, in order to
save time 3D printers have been heavily utilized rather than the slow processes of the metal
workshop. This in turn allows for more flexible solutions rather than tolerance-dependant solu-
tions.
Due to the time frame all preliminary tests of the vision feedback system has been conducted
using the current pumping system. This allows the two sub-projects to run partially parallel
rather than in series, making simultaneous progress of both projects a possibility.
1.4
Problem statement
The current pumping system is limited in various ways and can not reach the desired level of
flow and media handling. This limitation raises the question:
How can a pumping system with computer vision be
developed to better comply with the desired goals of flow,
media handling and print quality?
This project aims to answer the following research questions:
• Which sub-systems and related disciplines are required to achieve the specified re-
quirements?
• How can one couple readily available components to create a good pumping system?
• How can a pumping system be controlled with computer vision?
• What impact does computer vision have when 3D printing epoxy?

Chapter 2
Pumping system for Big Area
Additive Manufacturing
Big Area Additive Manufacturing (BAAM) refers to large-scale
3D printing where both model and oftentimes material flow is
enormous, compared to more traditional 3D printing methods,
ejecting several kilos of material per hour [1]. This type of 3D
printing differs in more than just size as both material usage and
applications greatly change as seen in large industrial concrete
printers.
The system behind the extrusion is often completely
different to account for various materials as, for example, seen in
this project where a specific pump system is required to transport
the viscous epoxy.
This chapter aims to describe the development and thoughts be-
hind the entire design process of the 3-pump system. To get a good
understanding of how different pumps and motors work, a short
theoretic section initiates this chapter after which the design deci-
sions and calculations are presented. The chapter terminates with
design iterations and tests thereof followed by a presentation of the
final 3-pump system.

CHAPTER 2. PUMPING SYSTEM FOR BIG AREA ADDITIVE MANUFACTURING
2.1
Comparison of pump types
Different pumps exploit different concepts, and the vast amount of different types allow for
many different applications. Therefore, choosing the right type of pump is crucial for a pump
system with such a demanding task of pumping a high viscosity epoxy. Due to the requirements
listed in table 1.2 certain types of pumps can be eliminated immediately.
The flow must be constant and non-pulsating, therefore both reciprocating pumps, such as
piston pumps, and pumps with a variable displacement, such as vane pumps, are eliminated.
Due to the highly viscous liquid, clogging is a risk which must be taken into account. This
risk is further elevated if particles, such as carbon fibres, are present in the liquid. Thus, pumps
containing numerous smaller moving parts, such as diaphragm pumps, are therefore eliminated.
The highly viscous liquid also eliminates centrifugal pumps as the efficiency of these are greatly
reduced with increasing viscosity. Five types of pumps, not eliminated by this reasoning, are
further examined, although the number of useful pumps and variations thereof greatly exceed
the ones presented in this section.
In order to fully understand the various pumps the two sources of power loss, and how they
distinguish from one another, should be known. This differentiation also becomes relevant later
in the report when dimensioning the chosen pump and motor. Pumps are built with clearances
allowing tight fits between the moving parts, but as the pressure builds at the outlet, some fluid
leaks back to the inlet. This leakage is commonly referred to as "slip". This reduces the output
flow of the pump and leads to a waste in power and thereby a decrease in volumetric efficiency.
The other power loss comes from both friction between moving parts and friction due to viscous
shearing of the fluid, this is referred to as hydro-mechanical efficiency.
External gear pump

External gear pumps fall under the category; positive displacement
pump, as a fixed volume is repeatedly closed off and the fluid is me-
chanically moved through the system to create a constant flow. The
external gear pump achieves this by utilizing a set of meshing gears,
moving the fluid around the perimeter of the gears, as seen in figure
2.1. The tight clearance between the meshing gears and between the
gears and the casing prevents any fluid flowing backwards, achieving
a high volumetric efficiency. Because the two shafts are supported on
strong bearings in both ends, the pump is able to achieve high pres-
sures, up to 500 bar [2], which, in conjunction with a compact design,
makes them ideal in many hydraulic applications. However, due to
the tight clearances in the pump, dry runs can wear them down rather
quickly and a high density of hard particles, such as carbon fibres,
is likely to cause abrasive damage over time. The advantages and
disadvantages are presented below:

Similar to the external gear pump, the internal gear pump utilizes a
set of meshing gears. But as the name implies an internal gear is
used instead. The large, outer gear is driven by a motor and meshes
with a smaller eccentric gear separated by a stationary crescent seal.
Internal gear pumps generally have small displacements which con-
tributes to a very accurate flow. These pumps are therefore often used
in operations where fluids must be discharged and/or mixed in pre-
cise amounts. The viscosity range of the internal gear pump greatly
exceeds that of the external gear pump, reaching viscosities up to
1,000,000 cP [3], however, they are only supported by one or two
bearings and the pressure generated from these can rarely reach that
of the external gear pump.
Advantages:
• Suited for high viscosity fluids
• Compact design
• Self priming
• Generally high volumetric efficiency
• Easy disassembling
Disadvantages:
• Susceptible to abrasive damage
• Dry running can damage the pump
For both external and internal gear pumps it is preferred to pump at lower speeds when deal-
ing with highly viscous or abrasive liquids to reduce wear on the gears. However, the volumetric
efficiency is reduced at lower speeds and flow rates creating a dilemma. When pumping with
these liquids it is therefore recommended to choose a pump with a large displacement, to allow
operating at lower speeds, but one should not steer too far from the recommended speed.

Just like in the external gear pumps the lobe pumps transfer the fluid
around the perimeter of the lobes. However, unlike the external gear
pumps the lobes to not make contact with each other or the casing.
Lobe pumps generally have much larger volumes and pump at lower
speeds, allowing them to pump solids and slurries. They are com-
monly used in food and cosmetic product pumping fluids such as
ketchup, yoghurt and honey, and can even pump whole berries due
to the gentle pumping action. The large lobes are often rounded of-
fering high resistance towards abrasive damage. They are are driven
by external gears and are often more complicated to maintain due to
the increased number of parts.
Advantages:
• Suited for high viscosity fluids
• Suited for liquids with large particles
• Self priming (if wetted only)
• Can operate dry
• Capable of pumping solids
Disadvantages:
• Available only in relatively large vari-
ants
• Low suction capability

Peristaltic pumps, or hose pumps, work by squeezing a flexible tube

As the flexible tube is

Comparison of motor type
Most hydraulic systems are commonly powered by a 3-phase electric motor or an internal com-
bustion engine. For this project, however, those motors would likely provide much more torque
to the system than is required and be more troublesome to control. The overall price and safety
measurements required for these are also unsuitable for a small scale project like this. Hence, a
comparison of small scale usable motors are carried out.

Brushless DC motors employ permanent magnets on the
rotor surrounded by electromagnets on the stator. Hall ef-

Stepper motor
Figure 2.7: Simplified illustration
of a stepper motor [12]
The stator of a stepper motor typically consists of toothed
electromagnets while the rotor consists of a toothed disc of
iron. They work by supplying electricity to a set of elec-
tromagnets attracting the nearest teeth on the rotor. Once
the rotor has aligned with the electromagnets another set
is turned on while the first electromagnets are turned off.
This generates a small, but precise rotational motion called
a "step". To turn one full rotation numerous steps are re-
quired, most common is 200 steps/rev. This resolution can
be further increased by allowing the rotor to move between
the steps toggling certain patterns of the electromagnets on
and off. This is called microstepping and is used to fur-
ther increase the precision of the motor at the cost of speed
and torque along with an increase in signals that need to be
passed to the motor. [12]
Stepper motors are generally used for slow applications, but can often reach speeds up to
1000 rpm. The torque output of a stepper motor is relatively high but decreases tremendously
at higher speeds. They can be controlled directly via a controllers but are most often used
with an external driver. The high precision and high torque makes them ideal in open-loop
CNC-machines, 3D printers etc. [11]

One common configuration consists of an encoder mounted to a stepper motor, all together
acting as a high-performance stepper motor. A stepper motor without any feedback mechanism
is limited in its performance, as a missed step can de-synchronize the signals to the electro-
magnets and the teeth on the rotor, thereby leading to positioning errors and even stalling of

forth are required. These motors are often used in applications where precision is tremendously

2.2.1
Chosen motor type
The pump system relies more on high torque than high speed and the DC motor is immediately
eliminated. Comparing the two remaining motors with the requirements makes it clear both
are suitable. The added precision of a servo motor is likely to cancel out with flexing of the
tubes and attrition of the pumps. The increased torque of a servo motor is likely not worth the
additional time required to develop a good system for the servo feedback. Lastly, the stepper
motor is much more simple than the servo motor and therefore contains fewer points of fail-
ure. Additionally they are available in many more configurations and sizes than servo motors.
Therefore, the stepper motor has been chosen.

2.3
Development of a pump system
In many hydraulic system there is a more prominent linear dependency between the compo-
nents, compared to many engineering projects where circular dependency is more dominant.
Hence, there is a well-defined 16 point approach, developed by Jacek S. Stecki et.al [14], which
is used when synthesizing such a system. This project shares similarities to a hydraulic sys-
tem and an approach inspired by that has been utilized. The approach used in this project is
described below:
1. Decide on system concept
2. Setup a hydraulic diagram
3. Determine pressure losses and operating pressure
4. Select and size the pump
5. Select and size the motor
This approach differs from Stecki’s as most of his points are irrelevant for this project, such as
choice of hydraulic fluid, tank and cooling, hence the short list. Furthermore, Stecki’s approach
is developed specifically for hydraulic systems, where the overall pressure in the system is
much higher and pressure losses only effect the the overall efficiency, rather than greatly inhibit
the system, as is the case in this project. Therefore, his approach allow for a well-defined
operating pressure from which everything else is determined, as opposed to this project where
the operating pressure the depending variable, determined by the pressure losses in the system.

2.3.1
System concept and layout
The final system concept is based on a few initial ideas and layouts, although the overall idea
largely remained the same. The final idea was determined with a priority for simplicity, time-
consumption and low cost. The ideas were;
• A 3-pump system with a gravity-fed input
This idea was considered in an attempt to improve priming using gravity to force the
fluids through the bottom of the tank to the inlets ports of the pumps. This introduced
a large opening where a motor agitating the thixotropic epoxy could be mounted if
deemed necessary. However, this required specific tanks with large bottom valves
to be available for purchase or custom made. A custom made tank would likely be
expensive and very time-consuming, while an off-the-shelf tank was available for
purchase for around DKK 2000,001 at the time of investigation. One major flaw of
this idea was reloading the tanks, as the user would manually have to lift the heavy
tanks above the pumps, likely above ones head, and fasten it.
• A 3-pump system with dosing pumps the end effector
This idea came as a solution to the high pressure losses in the system, as discussed
later in this chapter. 3 primary pumps would move the three liquids though the hoses
and into smaller dosing pumps.
This would split the pressure losses over several
pumps reducing the torque required to drive each pumps Having different pumps in the
system could potentially increase the accuracy of flow and likelihood of success, but
in turn would create a tremendously complex system which would be both expensive
and time-consuming to develop.
• A 3-pump system with suction-fed input
This idea is similar to the first idea, but instead of gravity feeding the liquids, a suction
hose was simply inserted into the original barrel, thereby eliminating the need for
special tanks and reducing the risk of injury relating to heavy lifting. The disadvantage
of this is the increased pressure loss occurring at the inlet side, both from the hose it self
and having to work against gravity. The pressure loss from the hose could be alleviated
using a larger suction hose and the pressure loss from the gravity is comparably small
as will be discussed later.
The third idea was chosen as it contains fewer components making it much simpler than the
alternatives. Furthermore, this simplicity leads to less time consumed, both during development
and during construction. The price is also significantly lowered due to the lower number of
component. However, one should not disregard the alternatives as they are both good ideas and
could be implemented in future iterations of this pump system if necessary.

It is common to introduce control elements for various reason; to maintain one directional
flow, routing flow in different directions, reducing pressure or flow etc. However, due to the
highly viscous fluid it was decided to avoid these as much as possible since the likelihood
of these failing are high. Most control elements are designed for hydraulic fluids or various
gasses and the viscosity of the epoxy greatly surpass these. Therefore, only one crucial control
elements is present in the pump system; the 3-way valve which is simply used to swap between
epoxy and a cleaning solvent.

The three motors and pumps all have to be dimensioned based on the highest of the pressure

pump and motor does not stall, the flow will remain constant and positive and the pressure in
the subsystems will therefore increase, resulting in an automatic compensation. The trade-off of
this feature, however, is an increase in power consumption. As seen in table 2.3 the maximum
pressure is 26.56 bar and to account for fittings and bends an operating pressure of 30 bar is
chosen.

case. In reality the pressure loss will be lower as the mixed epoxy have a lower viscosity than the
matrix. However, the viscosity will increase as the epoxy cures. Whether it cures fast enough
for the increase in viscosity to be a problem, is unknown as no data of the change in viscosity
is present. Extensive testing will have to be carried out to determine this.

Dimensioning of the pump
The size of a pump is largely governed by the amount of fluid which can be moved for each
revolution, also called displacement. The actual flow of a pump (including slip) is given by the
equation:
QP = ηvP · QtP = ηvP · Dis · n
(2.3.7)
Searching for a pump revealed that external gear pumps usually have a minimum rotational
speed of 700 rpm depending on the size and displacement. The usual volumetric efficiency
for a gear pump is around 85%[15]. Inserting these assumptions along with the required flows
into equation (2.3.7) and isolating for the displacement, D, one can narrow down the required
displacement for each of the three modules:
Dis1 =
6.27ml/s
0.85 · 700rpm = 0.63ml/rev
Dis2 =
2.82ml/s
0.85 · 700rpm = 0.28ml/rev
Dis3 =
0.91ml/s
0.85 · 700rpm = 0.09ml/rev
These are the displacements if pumping at the speed required to achieve minimum rated prop-
erties, 700rpm. Increasing the rotational speed will lead to a lower required displacement, and
these values can therefore be considered as maximum allowable displacements for each of the
pumps. Pumps typically are designed with specific displacements and finding ones which sat-
isfy the required displacement is trivial. Companies develop gear pumps of various sizes in
steps of 0.25 ml/rev, 0.50 ml/rev, 0.75ml/rev etc. Displacements of 0.50 ml/rev and 0.25 ml/rev
would fit nicely for the matrix and hardener, respectively.
Finding an external gear pump with a displacement less than 0.09 ml/rev is more difficult.
But since the catalyst is unknown and the viscosity and mixing ratio are assumed values, it is
likely that a completely different type of pump is required. For the sake of demonstration it was

Sub-conclusion
The module and setup of the motor and pump works nicely. Having a direct drive provides a
very simple design with few components as opposed to gearing the motor or introducing other
power transmitting elements. However, calculations determined the pump was too large which
fits well with the findings of the tests. It was concluded that the pump is too large for such
small flows and should be exchanged. The pump requires higher speeds to properly generate a
negative pressure high enough to prime the epoxy. Furthermore, the it seems that high pressure
loss is present in the system, since the motor would stall by simply blocking the output of the
pump with a finger. The pressure loss is likely located in the hoses, due to the extraordinarily
thick epoxy. The actual pressure losses in the system will be fully explored and discussed later
in the report as the design task progresses.

To further develop the pumping system and printing, computer vision can be implemented to

Although most of the work begin computer vision lies in software, it is vital to choose the cor-
rect camera and lens setup to generate proper data/pictures for the software to analyse. Hence, it
is important to have understanding about the specifications when choosing the optimal camera
for an application. The sensor type, shutter method and exposure time all have an impact on
how the object and features are perceived by the computer.
Sensor type
The defining component in a camera is its sensor. The two most common sensor types found
in modern cameras are the charged-coupled device (CCD) and the complementary metal oxide
semiconductor (CMOS).
The CCD is an analog device converting light into analog signals using an array of photo-
sensitive sites [20]. The charge from the sites are then converted to voltage using capacitors
before being converted to a digital signal. This process gives the sensor a high sensitivity and
pixel-to-pixel consistency, producing very accurate and detailed pictures. The sensor access all
the photosensitive sites at the same time removing the risk of motion distortion. Due to the
process of charging and discharging the capacitors between each picture, this sensor is limited
in speed.
The CMOS sensor is completely digital, using photodiodes and transistors at each pixel site
to activate and reset each individual pixel. Having a digital signal from the pixel site heavily
increases speed compared to the CCD sensor, and the continually reduction in transistor size
allows for a high pixel density allowing for cameras with small form factors and very high
resolution. With resolution being the number of photosites located on the camera sensor. Due
to the grid structure of the pixels on the CMOS sensor, the rows and columns are often accessed
sequentially, creating a rolling shutter. This means the pixel in one column is not taken at the
same time as in another column, this can create distorted images when objects or the camera are
moving at high speed [21]. By adding additional transistors to the sensor a global shutter can be
created where all pixels are active simultaneously and then read sequentially by the chip. This
is more complex and slightly slower than the rolling shutter, with the advantage of removing
the motion distortion. [20]

Monochrome or color
Monochrome sensors captures all light at each photosite no matter the color, and the values
recorded at each site can directly be put together to create the final image. Whereas color
sensors use color-filtered photosites so each site only receive light of a certain color, often
red, blue and green. The three colors are then combined to create the image. By filtering out
light with certain colors, less light reaches each sensor, as shown in figure 3.1, and results in
the sensors being less sensitive to light, compared with a monochrome sensor where each site
receives all light. [22]
Figure 3.1: Illustration of how a color-filtered photosite only receives some of the light, where the
monochrome photosite receives all of it. [22]
Color sensors use color-filtered photosites to filter out all color except one of the three colors:
Red, Green, Blue. The different color-filtered photosites are placed in a fixed pattern alternating
between the colors on the sensor. Each color is then stored in an array, three in total, with only
the known data added, as shown in figure 3.2. From these three arrays a demosaicing algorithm
is applied. The algorithm calculates the missing values for each array, by looking at the intensity
of the neighbouring values. From this three arrays are made describing the captured color image
[23]. Having to describe three colors on every pixel makes a colored images three time larger
in data size, than a monochrome image with one value for each pixel.

pattern) and how demosaicing (in this example de-bayering) are used to create the resulting color image.

Depending on the application having a single camera creating 2D photos is not sufficient to

3.2
Development of vision system
A vision system can take form in many ways depending on desired performances. To produce
high quality prints, key features must be determined and corrected. The key features relevant
to this project are sagging and line width. Sagging becomes an issue when printing overhangs
and bridges whereas line width is directly related to the flow of the epoxy as over- and under
extrusion will be visible.
3.2.1
System Concept
• Optimizing flow of catalyst to prevent sagging by looking from the side
In order to produce overhang and bridges the epoxy must be partially cured to
prevent sagging. This can be achieved by increasing the amount of catalyst in the
mixture during these print sequences. To help estimate the amount of catalyst needed,
computer vision must be able to identify when the layers are sagging and increase the
amount of catalyst accordingly. With a single camera this can only be achieved if the
camera can look at the printed models from the side or at an angle. This solution,
however, requires in most cases a rotating end effector to look from the side and
follow the shape of the printed model.
• Optimizing total flow by analyzing line width
When ejecting epoxy it is likely that the flow is either under- or over extruding. This
often occurs near corners where the robot arm slows down, while the pumps maintain
the same flow thereby creating undesired blobs of epoxy. Then tracing a complex
surface it is also likely that the flow should be regulated, as the speed of the robot arm
is likely to change when moving around especially in the z-directions. To determine
how a flow should be regulated it is simplest to look at the line width from above. If
the lines bulges out and become wider than the nozzle it is likely that over extrusion
occurs. Likewise, if the lines are narrower than the nozzle, under extrusion is likely to
be present. However, this solution requires multiple cameras to account for the blind
spots produced by the mixer tube or a rotating end effector to move the camera around
the printed models.
• Optimizing total flow and catalyst using stereo
As epoxy sags it moves further away from the cameras, albeit only a small distance.
Using a stereo setup will allow two cameras working together to calculate the distance
to a given point using triangulation while still looking straight down. A setup such as
this is similar to that presented in the previous concept, and can just as easily determine
the width of the lines. However, a stereo setup can only be fully advantageous in the
areas with no blind spots, and due to the mixer tube there is a massive blind spot in the

3.4.5
Determine print direction
Due to the physical setup of the cameras and hose hub, each camera will experience a blind
spot caused by the mixer tube. This is the main reason for the two cameras, but to know
which camera to use at any given time the print direction must be determined. Furthermore
determining the direction will also help in finding which line of epoxy is most recent when
multiple lines have already been printed.
Determining the print direction requires a process called optical flow. The theory behind this
is reasonably simple and can be summarized in two steps; Firstly recognizable features, such as
corners or high-contrast spots, are determined using an algorithm and the location of these are

Page 59 of 72
==PAGE:65==

saved. Secondly, after a short movement, the same features are detected and the new positions
are compared to the previous ones, thereby determining the direction.
To accomplish this two functions from OpenCV are used. On the initial image the function
goodFeaturesToTrack() is used. This function utilizes either the algorithm Harris Corner detec-
tion or the function cornerMinEigenVal(), to detect corner-like features. Both Harris Corners
and cornerMinEigenVal() creates a map by taking a pixel and applying an n × n grid around it
to calculate a covariance matrix. Harris corners then finds the local maxima from the response
map, while cornerMinEigenVal() finds the minimal eigenvalues. The grid size, the number of
features found, and the quality of the found features, can all be defined. The function goodFea-
turesToTrack() then returns a the location of these features. [29]
During testing Harris Corners proved more reliable than cornerMinEigenVal(), but is still not
perfect as the uniform background and straight line does not provide many features to detect.
To find the features in the second image the OpenCV function calcOpticalFlowPyrLK() has
been utilized. The function takes the previous image and the location of its key features, and
tries to locate them in the new image. The function uses the Lucas-Kanade method to determine
if the new feature positions fits with the optical flow from the other features. Since Lucas-
Kanade describes small movements a pyramid approach is used, meaning small movements
are removed and big movements are converted to small movements [30]. The severity of the
pyramid approach depends on the defined level. calcOpticalFlowPyrLK() returns the points for
the features in the new image and, by standard vector calculation, an average between all the
vectors are found and used as the print direction [31]. Examples of this are presented in figure

detecting all line segments in an image, and if the lines follows the cir-

with the location of the center-point, as illustrated in figure 3.8. [32]

created from the light reflected in the wet epoxy. To remove the shine a custom filter is made.
The filter is based on a mean filter, but instead of effecting every pixel, it looks only for pixels
with a value higher than a given threshold, and only the surrounding pixels which have a lower
value are used to calculate the new pixel value. So the new pixel value is the mean of the
neighbouring pixels with a value lower than the threshold.

3.4.7
Find lines & calculate width
To find the edges of the epoxy, the Canny edge detector algorithm has been used. This algorithm
works by calculating the gradient to find potential edges. To remove all fake edges, thresholding
is used to remove those with a gradient lower than what a true, well-defined edge would have,
and marking the remaining edges as either strong or weak edges. Using hysteresis the weak
edges can be determined to either be a false or true edges. It is deemed a true edge if it is
connected to a strong edge. [33]
In OpenCV Canny edge detection can be used with the function Canny(), where the thresh-
olds to determine strong and weak edges can be user defined. The color of everything not
considered an edge is set to 0 (black) and the edges are set to 255 (white), so the resulting
image is all black with only the true edges marked in white.
The shine on the printed epoxy, having a high contrast to the surrounding epoxy, was initially
detected as edges as well, which is the reason why the custom filter was designed. On figure
3.10 the line detection algorithm has been run on both a filtered and unfiltered image to illustrate
the necessity.

To determine the width of the epoxy, the pixels in a row between the white lines are counted.
To prevent interference from nearby epoxy lines, the pixels are counted from the center and to-
wards either edge, stopping when intersecting a white line. To improve accuracy this is repeated
with 20 different rows and an average width is calculated.
The final width can be affected by miss-detected or non-detected edges. To prevent sending
incorrect information to the pump controller, the final width is compared to the desired width.
If the final width is twice the desired, an error in the edge detection is assumed and the result
is discarded. Likewise, if the width is less than one-third of the desired width the result is also
discarded. Both of these thresholds are custom and can be defined as needed based tests and
experience. Depending on the result a signal to increase or decrease the flow can be sent to the
pump controller if the extruded epoxy does not conform to the desired width.

The program communicates with the controller by serial communication trough USB. To

3.5
Sub-conclusion
The computer vision system consists of two cameras mounted on either side of the hose hub
looking straight down on the print surface. This layout has been chosen to avoid blind spots
when determining the width of the epoxy. Regulating catalyst and thereby reduce sagging, has
been disregarded as a catalyst has not yet been chosen and no tests or evaluation could therefore
be carried out.
The cameras are setup to take 3 images per second limited by the post processing. The soft-
ware is able to determine the print direction by utilizing optical flow. The images are processed
to remove shine using a custom filter, after which the image is cropped around the nozzle to
reduce processing time. The Hough Circle transform is utilized to locate nozzle and thereby
determine the location of the cropping. Detecting the edges of the epoxy is then carried out
using the Canny edge detector which returns simple black and white images where edges are
presented as white. This allow a simple counting algorithm to determine the actual width of the
epoxy, after which the epoxy can be regulated based on the results.
Due to time constraints the software taking the photos has not been fully linked with the
remaining software. As such, the implementation of the software and robot computer, has not
been carried out. Likewise, tests concerning active regulation of the three pumps has not been
carried out, but remain open for future iterations of the project.

4.2
Conclusion & Discussion
Throughout the development of the 3-pump system and computer vision system certain design
choices have been made which is represented in the outcome and performance of the products.
The final solution adheres to most of the "Need to have" requirements stated in the beginning
of the project. However, it is clear that future development is required for this solution to work
great as some problems are still embedded in the system.
The final product incorporates of numerous disciplines pertained to the development of the
various sub-systems, as both solid mechanics, fluid mechanics, electrics, automation and pro-
gramming have been integral.
The pump system operates at a pressure of around 30bar produced by external gear pumps
with displacements of 0.50 and 0.25ml/rev for the matrix, hardener and catalyst, respectively.
The operating pressure is likely a bit lower as a small safety margin was added to account for
the fittings. These pumps are driven by Nema 34 motors coupled with a 1:3 custom made gear
box. The motor and gearbox provide a maximum torque of 1.67Nm at a rotational speed of
700 −800rpm which corresponds to a total theoretical flow of 10ml/s. The flow range should
in theory extend beyond this, but have not been tested due to the low reliability of the system.
A method of converging the three liquids have been designed and sits firmly on the end
effector plate while providing a seat for and off-the-shelf mixer tube. The mixer tube has been
made more rigid in a manner which also allow easy exchange of custom made 3D printed
nozzles.
The computer vision system incorporate two cameras to account for blind spots as a conse-
quence of the long mixer tube. The cameras take numerous pictures during a print, after which
the images are processed to determine both print direction and width of the extruded epoxy
lines, thereby ascertaining over- or under extrusion. This concludes the first iteration for a vi-
sual feedback system leaving only the feedback communication with the pump system to be
developed.
The system showed no issues when introduced to a carbon fibre infused media as shown
during tests using cooking oil. Any abrasive damage will likely not appear until the pump has
run for a longer time. However, a thicker media is likely to increase the speed at which abrasive
damage appears, but this has not been tested as it requires a more robust and consistent system
than what the project produced.
The system is unable to consistently provide the desired flow when pumping the thick 80Pa · s
epoxy. This was proven during tests as the flow halted halfway through the mixer tube after air
had been sucked into the system which could not escape again. When properly primed however,
the system does show some promise as the flow moved in a constant, even flow before suddenly
halting. It is likely that the gears in the pump have been worn down so much, that the air leaked
into the system was able to simply move back and forth, rather than being pushed forward. In
a hydraulic system a gear pump should be able to last a lot longer than the tests in this project,
but normal hydraulic fluid acts lubricating to the gears and it is likely that the epoxy does not.

Pumping the 30Pa · s matrix and the pertaining hardener could easily be pumped through the
hoses, but mixing them through the hose hub and mixer tube proved troublesome. The thin
hardener would quickly ooze out of the primed hose creating a large gap of air in the hose
allowing the matrix to be pumped up the hardener’s delivery hose rather than down the mixer
tube. It is likely that this is merely because the pump was not given enough time to build up the
required pressure. Unfortunately, this issue were not further explored as the pump connected
to the hardener suddenly stopped working and was not even able to pump cooking oil. Due

harsh chemistry in the hardener has interacted with the seals in the pump. Alternatively a small
leakage could be present sucking in air, but as it happened somewhat sudden this is unlikely.
4.2.1
Safety
During cleaning of the various parts it was discovered that the supplied solvent EpoxyClean
6002 dissolves the supplied NBR gloves much faster than stated in the data sheet. Despite
toxicological information showing no health risk, it is still recommended to avoid contact with
skin, eyes and open wounds. Using gloves of a different material should be considered.
The pump system utilize gear pumps with no feedback or pressure relief valves, due to the
likelihood of these failing with a fluid this thick. Because of this blocking the flow will allow
the pressure to continuously build, up to 167.6, 356.0 and 640.9 bar for the three modules, until
the motor stalls. However, it is more likely that a component breaks or bursts. Therefore, one
should implement a safety system to make sure pressure never exceeds a a certain threshold.
An old-ham coupling is already implemented and experimenting with 3D printed torque discs
could be a solution.
4.3
Future work
This first iteration of a pump system has taken a great step towards a working system. There
are however, some issues which must be resolved before consistent and reliant 3D printing with
epoxy and carbon fibre can be achieved.
In order to overcome the pumping issues it should be attempted to find a pump better suited
for such a thick fluid. The external gear pump was an experimental approach and exchanging
that for an internal gear pump will likely improve the performance of the entire system. Fur-
thermore, it might be beneficial to attempt raising the tanks above the pumps using gravity to
help feeding the pumps.
The reason for the hardener pump suddenly failing is currently unknown and should be un-
veiled one way or another. Depending on the findings the pump should be changed accordingly,
to avoid this issue happening again in the future.

exothermic reaction can reduce the pot-life 
and open time significantly. 
For automated applications, contact the Sys-
tem Engineering Department of Sika Industry.
Pressing
An adequate bonding pressure is necessary to 
obtain a voidless contact between the sub-
strates and the adhesive. The specific pres-
sure is, however, dependent on the core ma-
terial and must be determined by tests. The 
pressure must always be below the maximum 
compressive strength of the core. After start-
ing the press process, do not release the pres-
sure until the press time has elapsed.
Removal
Uncured SikaForce®-422 L45 may be removed 
from tools and equipment with SikaForce®-
096 Cleaner (formerly SikaForce®-7260 Clean-
er). Once cured, the material can only be re-
moved mechanically. 
Hands and exposed skin have to be washed 
immediately using hand wipes such as Sika® 
Cleaner-350H or a suitable industrial hand 
cleaner and water.
Do not use solvents on skin.
STORAGE CONDITIONS
SikaForce®-422 L45 has to be kept between 
10 °C and 30 °C in a dry place. Do not expose 
it to direct sunlight or frost. After opening of 
the packaging, the content has to be protec-
ted against humidity. 
Lowest temperature during transportation is 
 -20 °C for max. 7 days.
