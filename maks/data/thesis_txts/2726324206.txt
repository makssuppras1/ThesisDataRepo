Abstract
The increasing demand for efficient microchannel fabrication, as compared to the existing
CNC machining solution at DTU, has driven the exploration of alternative methods. While
Laser Powder Bed Fusion was initially considered, it also posed limitations, leading to the
development of a process flow for microchannel fabrication in the DTU Nanolab clean­
room. Additionally, the creation of sidewall nanofeatures to optimize bubble nucleation
motivated the investigation of a plasma trimming process on a Deep Reactive Ion Etching
(DRIE) etcher.
The thesis work encompassed two main phases. The first phase focused on optimizing
the Bosch process within the DRIE system for deep etching of microchannels. The FADE
(FAst, Deep Etching) process was successfully established, enabling the production of
microfluidic chips with channel depths of 500 μm.
The second phase involved transferring the plasma trimming process to the DRIE etcher,
which was effectively achieved. Plasma trimming tests conducted on the DRIE etcher
successfully reduced structures from 2 μm to less than 0.5 μm using AZ 5214E resist.
In summary, by utilizing the FADE process on the DRIE etcher, the thesis project suc­
cessfully fabricated 21 microchips with microfluidic channels exceeding a depth of 500
μm. Furthermore, the plasma trimming technique was successfully transferred to the
DRIE etcher.

Introduction
The growing demand for electronics has created a need for more efficient cooling meth­
ods and thermal management. This thesis aims to address this need by developing and
manufacturing a microchip with silicon (Si) sinusoidal microchannel structures at DTU
Nanolab. The proposed channels, intended to be 200 µm wide and have a depth rang­
ing between 300 µm and 500 µm, are to be covered by a borosilicate (B2O3) glass wafer.
The objective of this project is to facilitate heat transfer experiments conducted by Dr. Mo­
hamad Bayat and his students, focusing on the investigation of thermal behavior in a flow
boiling (two­phase) cooling setup, which is a promising research direction at the Section
of Manufacturing Engineering, Department of Civil and Mechanical Engineering at DTU.
The primary goal is to enhance the critical heat flux (CHF) and heat transfer coefficient
(HTC) through topological optimization and advanced Si manufacturing techniques.
Boiling heat transfer operates through the combined mechanisms of transporting latent
heat of evaporation and the movement of nucleated hot bubbles. This is different from
heat sinks and heat exchangers, which rely on single­phase convection heat transfer.
These distinct characteristics contribute to the significantly higher HTC observed when
boiling water on an unprocessed surface. When comparing this to the HTC of single­
phase forced and natural convection, the HTC for boiling water on an unprocessed surface
is significantly larger, typically ranging from 10 to 1000 times that of the single­phase
convection. These numbers are, however, for pool boiling not flow boiling. There is a
fundamental distinction to be made between pool boiling and flow boiling. In the case of
pool boiling, the surface is heated in a chamber of water without bulk fluid flow, while in flow
boiling, water is actively transported through a channel. Nevertheless, the aforementioned
numbers can provide a glimpse into the potential of flow boiling.
The design of these channels could provide improved heat transfer capabilities for en­
hanced cooling performance. This chapter provides an overview of the current solution
and highlights the limitations that necessitate the development of a new approach. It is
ended by describing the project design and the organization of the thesis.
The design of these channels holds the potential for enhancing heat transfer capabili­
ties and improving cooling performance. This chapter offers an overview of the existing
solution while emphasizing its limitations, which drive the need for a novel approach. Ad­
ditionally, it concludes by outlining the project design and providing an overview of the
thesis.
1.1
Current Solution
The current solution, illustrated in Figure 1.1, involves a heatsink with inlet, outlet, and
microchannels that are manufactured using computerized numerically controlled (CNC)
machining of copper (Cu). The full drawing can be seen in Appendix A, Figure A.1. How­
ever, for micro­ and nanofabrication purposes, CNC machining is no longer a viable op­
tion. Therefore, an alternative method called Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF) was con­
sidered. LPBF is a laser­based 3D printing technique for copper (Cu) that was anticipated
to address the limitations of the current solution.
However, this approach comes with several drawbacks. Firstly, the manufacturing pro­
cess using LPBF is highly time­consuming. Additionally, utilizing 3D­printed Cu restricts
the production of very small channels (< 10 μm thickness) and leads to reduced thermal

1.2
Cleanroom Fabrication of Chip with Microchannels
To overcome the limitations of the current solution, a new approach is proposed. The
desired solution involves a holder with an insert, allowing for easy replacement of the
microchip. By separating the chip from the holder, the design and manufacturing of the
channels can be easily modified without the need to reproduce the entire holder. This ap­
proach offers a more efficient and cost­effective solution to accommodate different chan­
nel designs, thereby facilitating the iterative research process.
The primary goal of this thesis is to manufacture a microchip that meets the specified
requirements. This involves the design of the fabrication process and the optimization
of deep reactive ion etching (DRIE), which serves as the primary method for etching the
deep microchannels required for the chip. The focus will be on developing a precise
and efficient process for achieving the desired channel dimensions and structure. The
objective of refining the DRIE process is to enhance the effectiveness and reliability of
DRIE for producing the deep microchannels essential for the microchip [2].
1.3
Adding Nanofeatures to Microchannels
The incorporation of nanofeatures on the sidewalls of microchannels has emerged as
a promising strategy to improve heat transfer capabilities. This approach is based on

the hypothesis that sidewall nanostructures can serve as nucleation sites for bubbles,
potentially enhancing bubble formation and heat transfer [2]. However, the fabrication of
nanostructures is typically a challenging and costly process compared to microstructure
patterning methods.
UV lithography, a widely used technique for microstructure patterning, currently faces
limitations in achieving the required resolution for precise nanostructure fabrication. Nev­
ertheless, there is potential for overcoming this limitation through the implementation of a
post­lithographic method known as plasma trimming. By leveraging plasma trimming, it is
theorized that UV lithography can be extended to enable the fabrication of nanostructures,
thus offering a viable solution to the challenges associated with patterning nanoscale fea­
tures on microchannels.
This section aims to provide an overview of various fabrication techniques employed in
micro­ and nanoscale production to introduce nanofeatures onto microchannels. Addi­
tionally, it highlights the advantages and limitations of UV lithography in this context. The
importance of achieving higher resolution in UV lithography is emphasized, and the po­
tential role of plasma trimming as a viable solution is acknowledged.
There are several ways to define structures for micro­ and nanoscale production. UV
lithography, DUV lithography and EBL are among these. A comprehensive comparison
of the three methods is presented in Figure 1.2, focusing on the capital cost, resolution,
and process time. Figure 1.2a illustrates the trade­offs between these factors, highlighting
the relatively low cost of UV lithography, the higher resolution of DUV lithography and EBL,
and the faster process time of UV lithography. The exclusion of EBL from Figure 1.2b is
justified based on its significantly longer processing time compared to other methods.

In order to enhance both time and cost efficiency, an analysis has been conducted to
explore options for improving the resolution of UV lithography. Among the potential so­
lutions, plasma trimming emerges as a promising candidate [5], [6]. Currently, plasma
trimming is performed in DTU Nanolab using a plasma ashing machine. However, the
process exhibits limited repeatability when conducted on this particular equipment. In
pursuit of a more reliable, cost­effective, and time­efficient approach, this thesis aims to
assess the feasibility of utilizing a DRIE machine as an alternative to plasma ashing for
achieving plasma trimming.

1.4
Experimental Setup
As seen in Figure 1.3 and Figure 1.4, the experimental setup comprises a steel chassis
that is attached to the tubing of the flow loop through soldering, which was designed by
Martin Ryhl Kærn, a previous senior researcher at Section of Thermal Engineering, De­
partment of Civil and Mechanical Engineering. Within the steel chassis, the Cu heatsink
is positioned, which includes the inlet, outlet, and microchannel structures. To ensure
proper thermal conductivity, the heating element is soldered to the bottom of the heatsink,
and polyetheretherketone (PEEK) inserts are placed to minimize heat loss. A B2O3 glass
cover, transparent in nature, is secured onto the heatsink using screws within the steel
chassis. Positioned above the microchannels is a high­speed camera, oriented downward
to capture bubble formation and observe the flow behavior. Additionally, an infrared cam­
era is placed beneath the setup, facing upward to continuously monitor the temperature
of the heater [1].
Figure 1.3: Schematic of the Test Setup [1].

Project Design
In essence, the primary objective of this thesis is to produce microchannels in Si with a
width of 200 μm and a depth ranging from 300 μm to 500 μm. To accomplish this, the first
step is to develop a process flow specifically tailored for implementation in DTU Nanolab.
Subsequently, an optimized recipe must be established for the deep etching process,
ensuring a rapid and deep etch while minimizing sidewall corrosion. Additionally, the

Coefficient (HTC). However, achieving such structures poses challenges given the current
plasma trimming process. Therefore, an examination will be conducted to determine the
feasibility of transitioning the plasma trimming process from the plasma ashing equipment
to the plasma etching equipment.
The project is divided into two distinct parts: the fabrication of the microchannels on one
hand, and the simulations, design, and testing of the fabricated microchannels on the
other. This thesis focuses specifically on the fabrication aspect, while separate theses
will be necessary to carry out the remaining parts of the project.

2.1
Process Flow
The process flow for fabricating a chip with microchannels has been designed as shown in
Figure 2.1. Initially, a 1 µm layer of SiO2 is deposited onto the Si wafer. This layer will later
act as a hard mask for the DRIE process. Next, a 1.5 µm layer of the positive photoresist
AZ 5214E is applied onto the SiO2 layer, followed by maskless exposure. The photoresist
is then developed to remove the soluble regions, revealing the desired pattern.
The pattern from the photoresist is transferred into the underlying SiO2 layer through oxide
etching. This process selectively removes the SiO2 in the exposed areas, leaving behind
the desired pattern in the oxide layer. To etch deep channels into the Si substrate, DRIE is
performed as the next step. Once the deep etching process is complete, the photoresist is
stripped, and the oxide is removed using buffered hydrofluoric acid (BHF) wet etching. At
this stage, the wafer, which now contains microchannel structures, is bonded with a boron
glass wafer to seal off the channels. Finally, the wafer is diced into 1 cm x 1 cm chips.
For a comprehensive overview of the entire process flow, please refer to Appendix C.

Plasma­Enhanced Chemical Vapor Deposition (PECVD)
Plasma Enhanced Chemical Vapor Deposition is a thin film deposition method that works
by using plasma to enhance the chemical reactions of reactant gasses, also called pre­
cursor gasses, on the substrate surface. It is a low­temperature process compared to
Optimizing Plasma Etching for Deep Etched Microchannels
6
==PAGE:18==
some other thin film deposition methods, and is therefore favorable in cases where high
temperatures cannot be used, e.g. when the substrate is temperature sensitive, like for
some metals and III­V materials.
The PECVD system mainly consists of a vacuum chamber (also referred to as the process
chamber), a gas handling system, and an electrode system. The substrate is inserted
into the vacuum chamber using a loadlock and is then placed on a heated electrode (the
platen), as seen in Figure 2.2. The precursors are introduced into the chamber and an
electric field is applied to the gasses, causing them to ionize and thereby, the plasma is
generated. The plasma provides energy to break the molecular bonds of the precursor
gasses, which allow the reactive species to react with the substrate surface and form the
solid film, layer by layer [7], [8].
Figure 2.2: The PECVD System [9].
The machine used is a SPTS Multiplex PECVD system with a 600W high frequency (HF)
generator of 13.56 MHz. To achieve a SiO2 deposition, the gasses SiH4 and N2O are in­
troduced into the process chamber. The plasma is created by applying the high­frequency
power supply to the mixed gasses, which contribute enough energy to break the molec­
ular bonds of SiH4. This then decomposes into SiH3 radicals and H atoms. Meanwhile,
N2O dissociates into O atoms that are then free to react with the SiH3 radicals forming
SiO2 molecules [8].
An important parameter for thin film deposition is the mean free path (MFP). This describes
the average distance a particle can travel between two consecutive collisions with other
particles or obstacles. The higher the MFP, the higher the ion energy of the process
can become, which in turn results in a higher quality film with higher density and fewer
defects. Assuming that a process is carried out in a relatively clean environment, this is
mainly controlled by the pressure level [10].
The amount of power used also influences the film quality. A higher power gives a higher
deposition rate, which in turn gives a higher ion energy on impact, which, as mentioned
before, improves the film quality.
In summary, PECVD is a valuable method for depositing thin films in a controlled and
efficient manner. With the ability to enhance chemical reactions using plasma, it enables

substrates. By adjusting parameters such as pressure and power, the film quality, density,

2.3
Optical Lithography
Optical lithography is a technique used to transfer patterns from a mask, which can be ei­
ther physical or digital, to a surface, in this case a Si wafer. The method consists of three
fundamental steps: coating, exposure, and development. Initially, a thin layer of photore­
sist, a light­sensitive polymer, is uniformly applied onto the wafer. It is worth noting that
there are two types of photoresists: positive and negative. As seen in Figure 2.3, the pos­
itive photoresist contains photosensitive compounds that decompose upon exposure to
specific wavelengths of light, while the negative photoresist contains additives that induce
crosslinking upon exposure, rendering it strong and durable. The spin coater is a Gamma
2M cluster manufactured by SÜSS MicroTec that is able to coat with several photoresists,
among these AZ 5214E and AZ nLOF 2020. This equipment serves multiple purposes,
including HMDS priming, spin coating of resists, and soft baking. HMDS priming plays
an important role in fostering good adhesion between the wafer and the applied resists,
and it consists of vapor priming a thin layer of hexamethyldisilazane onto the wafer shortly
before applying the resist [11].
Figure 2.3: The Photolithographic Process ­ Positive vs. Negative Photoresist [12].
After the coating process, the subsequent step involves the transfer of the design onto
the resist­coated wafer. This is achieved by moving the wafer to a so called maskless
aligner, where it is exposed to light of a specific wavelength in the desired pattern. Mask­
less aligners does not require physical masks. Instead, they project the design directly

struments, were utilized. The first is the MLA100 Tabletop Maskless Aligner, which emits

Maskless Aligner that emits UV­A light from a 405 nm laser source. These aligners offer

Following exposure in the aligner, the wafer proceeds to the development stage. In the

employed in this process is the Gamma 2M developer, manufactured by SÜSS MicroTec.
In conclusion, optical lithography is a sophisticated technique that enables the precise
transfer of patterns from masks to a Si wafer. The three fundamental steps of coating,
exposure, and development are crucial in achieving accurate pattern formation.

Bonding
In this study, anodic bonding is utilized to establish a permanent bond between a Si and a
B2O3 glass wafer. Anodic bonding is a direct bonding technique that initiates by applying
pressure at the center of the wafers, causing them to come into contact. Subsequently,
an electric field is applied at elevated temperatures, leading to the migration of alkali ions
from the B2O3 glass wafer to the Si wafer. Simultaneously, oxygen ions migrate from the
Si wafer to the B2O3 glass wafer [15].
The anodic bonder used in the DTU Nanolab Cleanroom is an SB6 wafer bonder from
SÜSS MicroTec.

3.1
What is Plasma?
Plasma, often referred to as the fourth state of matter, is essentially an ionized gas. It
is formed when a gas is heated to such a high temperature that its atoms lose electrons
and become positively charged ions, along with the presence of free electrons and neutral
atoms. In the sequence of states of matter, when a solid is heated, it transitions into a
liquid. Further heating converts the liquid into a gas, and with increased temperature, the
gas eventually becomes ionized, giving rise to a plasma state. However, in the context of
plasma etching and ashing processes, the high temperature and highly charged nature
of a fully ionized gas make it unsuitable for direct use. Therefore, a different approach is
taken to generate the plasma specifically for these processes [18].
3.2
Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP)
To generate the desired plasma for etching, a technique known as inductively coupled
plasma (ICP) is commonly employed. In an ICP system, a low­pressure gas is introduced
into a chamber, and an electromagnetic field is applied to induce the formation of the
plasma. This is achieved using a radiofrequency (RF) power source that generates a
current in a coil surrounding the chamber. The resulting plasma consists of a mixture of
ions, electrons, and neutral species, but it is not fully ionized like a traditional plasma. This
means that the plasma has a much lower temperature plasma and allows for DRIE. The
DRIE etcher utilized in DTU Nanolab is an Advanced Deep Reactive Ion Etching (DRIE)
Pegasus system from SPTS equipped with a 5 kW 13.56 MHz radiofrequency (RF) power
supply. As seen in Figure 3.1, The coil and platen powers in an ICP system are decou­
pled, which means that separate generators are used for the coil and the platen. This
decoupling is done to enable independent control of the power supplies, allowing pre­
cise regulation of their respective parameters. The coil power supply focuses on creating
and sustaining the plasma, while the platen power supply allows for controlling the bias
voltage applied to the substrate. This independent control allows for optimized plasma
characteristics such as density, ion energy, and electron temperature, and it offers pre­

accurate etching or film growth [7], [19].
First, the wafer is securely clamped to a specialized electrostatic chuck known as a thick
dielectric electrostatic chuck (TDESC). To maintain the desired temperature, a cooling oil
is circulated within the chuck, enabling precise temperature control. Additionally, helium
(He) gas is introduced beneath the wafer to facilitate heat transfer from the wafer to the
chuck.
Different gas species can then be introduced through mass flow controller and nozzles,
and the coil is used to generate a cylindrically symmetric, oscillating magnetic field, which
is then coupled with the gas. The magnetic field induces an electric field that ionizes the
gas, creating the plasma by stripping electrons from the atoms. At this point, the electrons
oscillate back and forth due to the opposing electrical current induced by the magnetic
field, while the nuclei remain relatively immobile due to their mass. The plasma, at this
stage, does not have any preferred direction. To bring the plasma towards the substrate
and initiate the etching process, a biased DC voltage is applied to the platen. This creates
a negatively charged electric field around the platen, which attracts the positive ions of
the gas. The ions gain kinetic energy due to their motion and acceleration, and when
they collide with the surface, they transfer their energy, resulting in chemical reactions
which leads to removal of material. The physical etching occurs through bombardment by
energetic particles that have sufficient energy to physically dislodge or sputter away atoms
or molecules from the material. Compared with electrically charged ions, radicals are
electrically neutral but are eager to combine with atoms on the material surface, forming
either volatile or nonvolatile products, which facilitates the chemical etching processes,
where kinetic energies of etching species play a less important role [19].
The strength of the magnetic field is controlled by the coil power.
Higher coil power
strengthens the magnetic field, which, in turn, increases the plasma density. This is be­
cause the magnetic field induces an electric field onto the plasma, according to Faraday’s
law. By sustaining the magnetic field, the plasma density can be effectively controlled
independently of the plasma energy. The energy, on the other hand, is controlled by the
platen power, which manages the electric field that attracts ions to the substrate surface
[7], [19].
In this thesis, the focus is primarily on the plasma etching process for Si using the Bosch
process. Additionally, a secondary emphasis is placed on the use of plasma etching for
photoresist trimming, utilizing a custom recipe developed specifically for this purpose.

Other Plasma Processing Systems
3.3.1
Barrel Etcher ­ Plasma Asher
The plasma asher employed in the DTU Nanolab Cleanroom is a TePla model 300 Semi
Auto Plasma Processor.
This particular device utilizes microwave­generated oxygen
plasma. The plasma generation process relies on microwaves with a higher frequency of
2.45 GHz, resulting in a significantly higher plasma density compared to other methods
such as RF generated plasma in ICPs.
The process chamber of the plasma asher is enclosed within a Faraday cage and there is
no applied bias, which allows for isotropic etching. In this configuration, oxygen ions are
able to approach the wafer surface from all directions, ensuring a uniform etching process.
This isotropic etch capability is advantageous for certain applications where precise and
uniform material removal is required [20].
The primary application of plasma ashing is the removal of resist materials from wafers
through a process known as ashing. During ashing, the resist is oxidized and transformed
into volatile compounds, which are then rapidly evacuated from the chamber due to the
presence of a vacuum. This efficient removal of the resulting ashes from the process
chamber is the reason why this technique is referred to as plasma ashing.
In addition to its application in plasma ashing, the plasma ashing technique can also be
utilized for a process known as plasma trimming. Plasma trimming involves the horizon­
tal reduction of the size of the photoresist pattern, enabling the creation of features in
the nano­scale range. This technique becomes particularly valuable when attempting to
achieve fine feature sizes that are beyond the resolution capabilities of conventional UV
lithography.
3.4
Why Plasma Processing?
As manufacturing technologies advance, conventional methods for patterning structures
with structures above the µrange are no longer feasible, due to the increased requirements
for precision and control of structures in the µand nm range. Consequently, alternative
material removal processes need to be employed to address these challenges effectively.
A crucial factor of material removal processes is their directionality, which can be char­
acterized as either isotropic or anisotropic. Isotropy refers to uniformity in all directions,
while anisotropy indicates a preferred direction of material removal.
Plasma processing is commonly used for material removal and can involve both physical
and chemical mechanisms, often combining the two. As seen in Figure 3.2 (2), purely
chemical etches tend to be isotropic, meaning that material removal occurs uniformly in
all directions. On the other hand, physical etching tends to be more anisotropic, shown in
Figure 3.2 (1), resulting in material removal primarily in one direction. In addition, physical
etching processes can also lead to tapered sidewalls in the etched features [19], [21].
To address the challenges posed by purely isotropic or anisotropic etching, the technique
called DRIE is employed. DRIE combines both chemical and physical etching processes,
allowing for better control over the etch profile. Combining the two mechanisms allows
DRIE to achieve a more anisotropic etch with vertical sidewalls, thereby overcoming the
limitations of purely chemical or physical etching techniques, illustrated in Figure 3.2.3.
In contrast, in plasma ashing, the reactive species responsible for material removal (such
as oxygen radicals) are typically evenly distributed throughout the plasma chamber. These

In summary, as structures become smaller, alternative manufacturing methods become
necessary due to the increased demand for precision. Plasma processing techniques,
involving both physical and chemical mechanisms, offer viable possibilities. By combining
chemical and physical etching in processes like DRIE, it becomes possible to achieve the
desired etch profile with improved sidewall control and overall precision.

3.5.1
The Bosch Process
The Bosch process is a highly effective anisotropic etching technique well­suited for achiev­
ing deep trenches in Si. It is divided into two distinct steps. The first involves an isotropic
SF6 plasma etching step in conjunction with an applied bias, which removes material uni­
formly in all directions.
Following the etching step, a protective polymer layer is deposited using C4F8 plasma.
This passivation layer safeguards the sidewalls of the trench, preparing them for subse­
quent etching.
This constitutes the first Bosch cycle. During the etching step of the second cycle, the
passivation layer in the bottom is removed, and the isotropic etch step is repeated, as

To ensure the effectiveness of each step and prevent undesirable reactions, the process

3.5.2
DREM Process
The DREM process is specifically designed for achieving high aspect ratio etches. It offers
an advancement over the Bosch process by incorporating an additional step known as
bottom removal.
The process initiates with the main etching step: an isotropic SF6 plasma etching, which
is performed without applying any bias. Following this, a sidewall protection step takes
place, involving the deposition of a passivation layer using C4F8 plasma.
Subsequently, an Ar)etching step is implemented along with an applied bias that specif­
ically targets and eliminates the bottom film of the trench. This step effectively clears
the path for the primary etching step, the isotropic SF6 etch. Together, these three steps
constitute one complete cycle of the DREM process, as illustrated in Figure 3.4 [21], [23].
The specific process parameters can be found in Table 3.2.
As for the Bosch process, the DREM process is split into steps to separate the gasses
from each other. Compared to the Bosch process, the DREM process has much smaller
scallops.

3.5.4
Process Limitations
The plasma etching processes are subject to certain limitations, including the loading
effect. This effect can be classified into two types: micro and macro loading. Micro loading
occurs when there are multiple structures in close proximity or trenches with a high aspect
ratio (HAR). In plasma etching, the availability of free radicals for chemical etching is
crucial. With high opening ratios, fewer radicals are accessible near the bottom of the
trench, leading to slower etch rates. This phenomenon is illustrated in Figure 3.5 [25].
Macro loading, on the other hand, refers to variations in etch rates across the wafer due
to factors such as gas distribution, plasma uniformity, and edge effects. One example is,
when comparing structures near the wafer’s edges to those in the center, more radicals
are available near the edges, resulting in higher etch rates at the periphery compared to
the center [25].

4.3.3
Fast, Deep Etch (FADE) Process
Based on the tests conducted in subsection 4.3.2, aimed at identifying a variation of the
Bosch process specifically optimized for deep etching, a new recipe called Fast, Deep
Etch (FADE) was developed. This variant proved to be more suitable for achieving sub­
stantial etch depths. The analysis of the obtained results enabled the selection of the final
parameters that ensure efficient deep etching. The parameters of the FADE process have
been modified as follows compared to the original Bosch process:
• Etch time: Extended to 9 s to allow for the desired depth to be reached.
• Platen power: Adjusted to 80 W to enhance the etching process while maintaining
the stability of the plasma.

4.8.3
Wafer Bonding
The bonding process was carried out on an SB6 wafer bonder from SÜSS MicroTec. The
Si wafer used in the process had a thickness of 1000 µm, while the B2O3 glass wafer had
a thickness of 500 µm ± 20 µm. The wafers were assembled manually by following the
following steps:
1. The Si wafer was held with the desired bonding surface facing upward.
2. The B2O3 glass wafer was positioned on top of the fingers, ensuring that the wafers
were in close proximity without touching. A small gap of a few millimeters was left
between the wafers.
3. The other hand was used to place the thumb underneath the center of the Si wafer,
while the index finger was simultaneously placed on top of the B2O3 glass wafer,
also at its center.
4. Carefully, the wafers were pushed together, creating a slight vacuum that was suf­
ficient to keep them securely joined during handling.
5. The wafers could then be placed on a chuck, which was inserted into the bonding
chamber.
To ensure proper anodic bonding, the thickness of the two wafers was set to 1300 µm,
which is lower than their actual combined thickness. This reduction in thickness ensured
sufficient pressure for the bonding process to occur successfully. The bonding process
followed a specific anodic bonding recipe, with the following parameters:

Dicing
As mentioned in section 4.7, the etched microchannels have an approximate depth of 500
µm. The bonded wafer has a total thickness of 1500 µm ± 20 µm, consisting of a 1000
µm Si wafer and a B2O3 glass wafer with a thickness of 500 µm ± 20 µm. The dicing

Figure 4.12: Dicing Pattern; Vertical Dicing Lines (a) and Horizontal Dicing Lines (b).
Firstly, 450 µm vertical lines were diced into the Si wafer from the backside, followed by
the frontside dicing of 400 µm vertical lines into the B2O3 wafer (a). Finally, horizontal
lines were diced from the frontside, extending through the entire wafer (b). This dicing

The decision to cut the chips in this manner was influenced by the fact that wafer was

the entire wafer would introduce particles into the channels that could not be easily flushed
out, if at all, after the dicing process. To overcome this challenge, the aforementioned
dicing process was successfully utilized, addressing the particle contamination concern
and enabling the fabrication of individual chips with intact microchannel structures.

4.9
Final Remarks on Microchannel Fabrication
In comparing the work conducted in this thesis with the research by Dr. Chang [23],
a similar approach to process optimization has been followed, inspired by the ideology
employed in developing the DREM process. Specifically, the reduction of platen power
has been implemented to enhance selectivity, and the etch step has been prolonged to
achieve faster etch times. These modifications have yielded satisfactory results; how­
ever, further improvements are still possible in terms of sidewall corrosion. The research
team associated with Dr. Bayat’s work needs to establish the significance of sidewall cor­
rosion and determine acceptable levels, potentially even considering its positive impact
on bubble formation.
The observed sidewall corrosion can be attributed to the absence of sufficient sidewall pro­
tection, particularly in the form of fluorocarbon deposition, which is a known consequence
of deep etching processes. As the etch depth increases, it becomes more challenging to
maintain effective sidewall protection, leading to increased sidewall corrosion.
Based on the results obtained, it is evident that the final set of parameters used in the etch­
ing process provided the most effective sidewall protection against corrosion, as demon­
strated in Figures 4.5.a.1 and 4.5.a.2. These images showcase less signs of sidewall
corrosion compared to the other settings, indicating that the selected parameters offered
better protection to the sidewalls during the etching process.
If the influence of sidewall corrosion is found to have a positive impact on flow boiling
behavior, additional tests can be conducted to better control and optimize the sidewall
corrosion. Conversely, if the impact is deemed insignificant, it may not even be practical
to pursue the fabrication of nanostructures using plasma trimming.
On the other hand, if the sidewall roughness is deemed excessive, further optimization of
the Bosch process becomes necessary, particularly for the fabrication of nanostructures
using plasma trimming. In such cases, the focus should be on maintaining or slightly re­
ducing process time while ensuring that the sidewall roughness remains below a specified
threshold. Nevertheless, it is important to acknowledge that there will likely be a trade­off
between achieving desired depth and minimizing sidewall corrosion.
Comparing the channels fabricated in this thesis with those discussed in the flow boiling
microchannels review by Deng et al. [2], it should be noted that the channels produced
using DRIE are fabricated on 500 µm thick wafers. Therefore, it is logical that none of
these channels reach the depth of 500 µm. Thus, the channels fabricated in this thesis
exhibit greater depth when compared to similar microchannels manufactured using DRIE,
as observed in the work by Deng et al. [2]. This indicates that the DRIE process employed
in this thesis has been successful in achieving relatively deeper microchannels.

Study of Plasma Trimming and Process
Analysis
The ability to incorporate nanofeatures on the sidewalls of microchannels might hold great
potential for enhancing the formation of bubbles by creating additional nucleation sites.
As previously mentioned, trimming is a cost­effective and high­throughput technique uti­
lized for defining nanostructures through UV lithography and plasma post­processing.
However, in the DTU Nanolab cleanroom, plasma trimming is currently carried out using
a plasma asher, leading to inconsistent outcomes due to the machine’s lack of precise
process control.
The main objective of plasma trimming is to reduce the width of structures while avoiding
excessive thinning. Thinning refers to size reduction in the vertical direction, whereas
trimming involves reducing the size in the horizontal direction.
To assess the variations in the plasma ashing machine, a study was conducted. First, two
test processes were performed to compare plasma trimming in cold and hot chambers.
The intention was to investigate the impact of the chamber’s thermal state on the results
of plasma trimming.
Subsequently, several tests were conducted on the DRIE system, focusing on parameters
such as process chamber pressure, platen power, chiller temperature, and process time.
The aim was to establish a center recipe for plasma trimming.
The findings of the study are discussed throughout the chapter, and the chapter concludes
with a summary and discussion of the results.

carried out in the hot chamber produced significantly different results. In this case, no
structures were left after the etching process, indicating an inconsistency in the fabrication
process when using this specific machine.
This outcome was not entirely unexpected, as it aligns with the well­established princi­
ples outlined by the Arrhenius equation [24], which states that higher temperatures can
accelerate chemical reactions. The increased temperature in the hot chamber likely led
to enhanced chemical reactions during the plasma trimming process, causing excessive
material removal and resulting in the complete disappearance of the microstructures.
The lack of successful trimming in the hot chamber experiment highlights the need for fur­
ther investigation and optimization of the plasma trimming process on the DRIE plasma
etcher. The inconsistencies observed in the fabrication process with the hot chamber
necessitate a more thorough examination of the parameters, such as temperature and
duration, to ensure more reliable and reproducible results. The findings emphasized the
importance of carefully controlling the process parameters and optimizing the experimen­
tal conditions to achieve consistent and desirable trimming results.
5.3
DRIE Plasma Trimming
Based on previous literature and the exploration of various plasma processing techniques,
it was observed that plasma trimming processes have been implemented using different
approaches [5], [6]. These approaches included both high and low­pressure environ­
ments, moderate generator power settings, and the utilization of gas mixtures containing
oxygen. Furthermore, plasma trimming had been carried out using both barrel etchers
and ICPs. Given the diverse range of possibilities, the decision was made to first, get an
idea of the process chamber pressure level. Afterward, plasma trimming recipes could
be tried out, and finally, a center recipe for plasma trimming was discovered.

5.3.3
Influence of Process Parameters
During the experiments conducted for this study, several important process parameters
were identified and tested, and their significance is outlined below. These findings con­
tribute to a better understanding of the underlying dynamics and optimization of the pro­
cess.
Platen Power
As depicted in Figure 5.6, an interesting finding emerges regarding the impact of platen
power on thinning and trimming effects. It is observed that beyond a certain threshold,
the platen power becomes more influential in facilitating thinning rather than trimming.
More specifically, at a platen power setting of 40 W, the reduction in horizontal size is
comparable to that achieved at both 60 W and 80 W settings. However, a notable disparity
arises in the vertical size reduction, where the 60 W and 80 W settings exhibit a significant
increase compared to the 40 W setting.
This finding suggests that increasing the platen power beyond a certain point leads to
enhanced thinning of the structures while maintaining a relatively similar level of trimming.
The higher platen power levels seem to exert a greater influence on the vertical dimension
of the structures, resulting in a more pronounced reduction in height compared to the width
reduction.
The observed differences in the effects of platen power on thinning and trimming highlight
the importance of optimizing this parameter in the plasma trimming process. By carefully
selecting the appropriate platen power, it is possible to achieve the desired balance be­
tween thinning and trimming, ensuring more precise control over the final dimensions of
the fabricated structures.

As illustrated in Figure 5.7a, an interesting observation is lower temperatures contribute
to a greater reduction in the vertical direction compared to the higher temperature of 20°C.
When the temperature is set at ­20°C, there is also a noticeable amount of trimming oc­
curring, although the difference compared to structures trimmed at the higher temperature
is not substantial.
The results suggest that decreasing the chiller temperature significantly affects the reduc­
tion in structure height, resulting in enhanced thinning. However, in terms of trimming,
which relates to the reduction in structure width, there is no significant difference between

These findings emphasize the role of chiller temperature in the plasma trimming process.
By keeping a temperature level around room temperature, one can achieve a more pro­

Experimental investigations were carried out to examine the impact of process time on
the plasma trimming process. The results, as seen in Figure 5.7b, highlight interesting
findings regarding the relationship between process time, trimming, and thinning effects.

It can be observed that a process time of 135 seconds yields a comparable amount of
trimming as a 60­second process. This suggests that beyond a certain duration, the
additional process time does not significantly contribute to further reduction in the width of
the structures. However, there is a notable distinction in terms of the vertical size reduction
between these two process times. The 135­second process exhibits a more significant
reduction in height compared to the 60­second process.
Furthermore, when comparing the 90­second process to the 60­second process, there
is a slight increase in thinning but a similar level of trimming. This implies that extend­

maintaining a similar level of width reduction.
These findings indicate that the process time plays a crucial role in controlling the thin­
ning and trimming effects during plasma trimming. Optimizing the process time allows
for precise control over the final dimensions of the structures. By carefully selecting the
appropriate duration, it is possible to achieve the desired balance between thinning and
trimming to meet specific fabrication requirements.
Argon
The inclusion of Ar in an etching process introduces a more physically­driven etching
mechanism, akin to ion bombardment, due to Ar’s chemical inertness. This can result in
a higher surface roughness compared to etching processes with a stronger chemical com­
ponent. Additionally, the presence of Ar can lead to the formation of grass­like structures,
which are actually instances of photoresist corrosion.
Based on the practical experience gained in this study, it is observed that the addition
of Ar helps reduce the reflected coil power, thereby contributing to plasma stabilization.
However, this stabilization comes at the cost of surface quality in the resulting structures.
Therefore, a trade­off must be considered between achieving plasma stability and ob­
taining the desired surface quality of the etched structures. In this study, it was selected
to omit Ar from the process, due to the physical nature of the etch, when the gas was
included in the plasma.
5.3.4
Final Remarks on Plasma Trimming
A center recipe for plasma trimming on DRIE was successfully developed, showcasing
notable differences compared to previous studies [5], [6]. The parameters selected for
this recipe exhibited significant deviations from the parameters utilized in prior investiga­
tions. Specifically, the coil power employed in the developed recipe was set at 3000 W,
representing a substantial increase of six times compared to the highest power used in
previous studies. The pressure was maintained at 45 mTorr, residing on the lower end
of the pressure range, while the platen power utilized was eight times higher than that
reported in comparable studies.
Notably, the study yielded optimal results when utilizing a pure O2 plasma, in contrast to
other studies that incorporated O2 mixed with other gasses. These variations highlight the
importance of machine­specific parameters, as each machine is different, and equipment
may necessitate unique settings. Furthermore, the parameters derived from previous
studies were in systems with dissimilar specifications to the SPTS DRIE Pegasus machine
employed in this study, which further emphasizes the need for tailored optimization.
Moving forward, future investigations should prioritize the optimization of plasma trimming
parameters to establish a robust and consistent fabrication process.

6
Conclusion
The background of this project stemmed from the need for a more efficient and effective
method of manufacturing smaller microchannels for heat transfer experiments. The ex­
isting method of CNC machining was found to have limitations in terms of feature size,
while the alternative, Laser Powder Bed Fusion was limited in processing time and ther­
mal conductivity. Therefore, the objective was to develop a process flow for microchannel
fabrication in the DTU Nanolab cleanroom. Additionally, there was an interest in explor­
ing the possibility of transitioning from a plasma asher with limited parameter control to a
DRIE etcher with greater control over various parameters.
The thesis work was divided into two main phases. The first phase focused on DRIE
and the associated processes for microchannel fabrication, while the second phase con­
centrated on the investigation of transferring the plasma trimming process and testing
potential plasma trimming recipes on the DRIE etcher.
In the first phase, lithography was performed on microchannel designs with varying thick­
nesses ranging from 10 μm to 300 μm. Both the Bosch process and the DREM process
were tested, with the Bosch process being selected due to its higher etch rate. The pat­
terned chips were then subjected to DRIE experiments to optimize the Bosch process
for deep etches with an improved etch rate and reduced sidewall corrosion. This led to
the establishment of the Fast, Deep Etch (FADE) process, enabling the production of
microchannels at a wafer scale.
The final wafer fabrication process involved PECVD deposition of a 1 μm SiO2 layer on
a 1mm thick SSP Si wafer, followed by lithography using a 1.5 μm layer of positive resist
AZ 5214E. Advanced oxide etching transferred the resist pattern into the oxide, and deep
etching was carried out using the FADE process to create channels exceeding 500 μm in
depth. Subsequently, the photoresist and oxide layers were removed via plasma ashing
and wet etching, respectively. The wafer was then successfully bonded to a 500 μm B2O3
glass wafer and diced into 21 chips measuring approximately 1 cm x 1 cm. While the
dicing process faced challenges due to the unavailability of DSP wafers, the successful
manufacturing of the microfluidic chips was achieved.
The second phase focused on the migration of the plasma trimming process from a
plasma asher to the DRIE etcher. The need for this migration was established by con­
ducting experiments using identical recipes but varying temperatures, which resulted in
non­repeatable outcomes. Plasma trimming tests were subsequently conducted on the
DRIE etcher using chips coated with both negative (AZ nLOF) and positive resist (AZ
5214E) of 1.5 μm thickness on 500 μm Si chips with structures of different sizes. Plasma
trimming was successfully performed on AZ 5214E resist using the DRIE etcher, reduc­
ing linear structures from 2 μm to less than 0.5 μm. However, further optimization of
the recipe was not possible within the project’s scope, highlighting the potential for future
investigations to refine the process.
In conclusion, this thesis addressed the need for an improved microchannel fabrication
method. The use of DRIE and the development of the FADE process enabled wafer­scale
production with enhanced efficiency. Additionally, the investigation into transferring the
plasma trimming process to a DRIE etcher showed promising results, with a successful
reduction of structures using the optimized recipe.
