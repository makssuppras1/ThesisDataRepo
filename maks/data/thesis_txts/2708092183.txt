Abstract 
 
Continuous manufacturing has historically led to significant improvements in production 
processes, including reductions in equipment size, better equipment utilisation due to 
continuous production, and higher product quality due to steady-state process operation. 
Motivated by these potential gains, the pharmaceutical sector has in recent years shown an 
increased interest in moving from batch to continuous processing. 
Chromatography is one of the central downstream units to isolate and purify a product from 
impurities and offers unparalleled separation power. While traditional chromatography units 
are inherently operated in batch mode, several continuous counterparts have been developed 
over the years. Since these alternatives are based on the interconnection and periodic 
switching of inlet and outlet ports between multiple columns, they are inherently more 
complex. 
The objective of this thesis is to enhance the understanding of the behaviour of these 
processes. Eventually, this will enable the identification of potential application areas as well 
as the successful design and operation of continuous chromatography units. To achieve this, 
the topologies and working principles of existing batch and chromatographic processes are 
first compared to each other to gain a qualitative understanding. Secondly, a case study based 
on numerical simulations with a mechanistic model is conducted to gain quantitative insights 
into the performance and dynamics of the processes. 
In the case study, a batch chromatography and a Simulated Moving Bed (SMB) process are 
first designed to match the separation specifications of a given Multicolumn Countercurrent 
Solvent Gradient Purification (MCSGP) process. Secondly, a control system is designed for each 
of the three processes to investigate how they perform in open- and closed-loop under 
disturbances. The behaviour is compared by mapping the operable disturbance space for two 
representative disturbances that can be expected in a production process.  
The results show that for the case study system, the base case design of the batch process 
outperforms the MCSGP and SMB processes in terms of volumetric productivity and solvent 
consumption. However, the SMB process shows the most satisfactory closed-loop 
performance and can be operated under the widest range of disturbances.

Introduction 
A production process for biopharmaceuticals can be divided into an upstream fermentation 
and recovery section and a downstream purification section. Besides filtration and 
crystallisation, chromatography is the central downstream unit operation to isolate and purify 
the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) [1]. 
Chromatography may be defined as a process that employs a fixed bed of solute-interacting 
material (stationary phase) to separate a mixture of components that are carried through the 
bed by a fluid phase (mobile phase) [2]. This definition includes classical adsorption processes, 
which have been used extensively in many parts of the chemical industry. Important large-
scale processes include water treatment, exhaust gas cleaning, sweetening of sour gas, gas 
drying, and air separation [3]. To distinguish these processes from chromatography in a 
narrower sense, the latter is sometimes defined as processes where a pulse of feed is injected 
into a fixed bed, and the components separate based on their different migration velocities 
along the flow path [4, 5]. 
Chromatographic processes are characterised by an extreme separation power and a 
versatility that allows high-purity separations from the milligram scale in life science 
applications to the ton scale of synthetic industrial products [6, 7]. The applications of 
chromatography can be classified as preparative and analytical applications. They can be 
distinguished based on the primary goal of the separation process: in preparative separations, 
the goal is to collect fractions of product while in analytical separations the goal is to collect 
information about the mixture [8]. 
Today, both analytical and preparative chromatography are widely applied techniques. In 
analytical applications, chromatography offers almost universal applicability with respect to 
the range of samples that can be analysed. Furthermore, it offers remarkable precision in the 
identification and concentration measurement of products and impurities. Due to these 
features, most laboratories that analyse chemical mixtures are today equipped with a liquid 
chromatography apparatus and it is frequently the first choice of technique [9]. 
Besides analytical applications, chromatography exists today as an established industrial unit 
operation for the separation of complex mixtures [6, 7]. Especially in the pharmaceutical 
industry, chromatography is established as an important general-purpose separation process. 
In the production of biopharmaceuticals, production processes commonly employ at least 
three chromatographic steps [2]. This can be attributed to the fact that many established 
separation techniques in the petroleum and commodity chemicals industry, such as distillation 
and extraction, do not fulfil the high-purity requirements of the pharmaceutical industry [6, 9].

1.1 Development of Adsorption and Chromatographic Processes 
 
 
Figure 1.1 Timeline of adsorption and chromatography process development 
The history of adsorption processes reaches back to the earliest written records of humankind, 
where sand and coal were used to clean water from impurities [3]. Industrial processes were 
developed in the 19th and 20th century and motivated important theoretical works by Ostwald, 
Gibbs, and Langmuir. The invention of chromatographic processes in the narrower sense is 
attributed to the Russian botanist Tswett, who demonstrated the separation of plant pigments 
in a chromatography column and subsequently proposed the term “chromatography” in 1903 
[6].  
A breakthrough for chromatographic processes in the chemical industry was enabled through 
the introduction of the Simulated Moving Bed technology, a multicolumn chromatographic 
process mimicking a moving bed of stationary phase. The process was first developed and 
patented by Union Oil in 1961 [7] and was commercialised as the SorbexTM technology. Finally, 
since the 1980s the applications of chromatography have seen a considerable expansion due 
to the rapid development of the fine chemical, biotechnological, and pharmaceutical sectors 
and their need for high-purity products [6].  
1.2 Continuous Chromatography 
Batch elution chromatography is currently the dominating technology for pharmaceutical 
separations [6]. In batch elution chromatography, feed material is loaded onto the column and 
subsequently eluted with a solvent mixture – making it an inherently instationary process. In 
continuous chromatography, multiple columns are interconnected and operated in a way to 
allow for continuous feeding and product withdrawal. 
The motivation for moving from batch to continuous processes in the pharmaceutical industry 
is at least partly driven by the historical success of continuous manufacturing in other 
industries. Many companies employing batch processes in the steel and chemical industries 
==PAGE:15==
3 
could not compete with upcoming continuous alternatives [10, 11]. The economic advantages 
of continuous processes across industries include reductions in equipment size, better 
equipment utilisation due to continuous production, and better product quality due to steady-
state process operation [10, 12, 13].  
Today, the only well-established continuous chromatographic process in the pharmaceutical 
industry is the SMB process for separating racemic mixtures [13, 14]. In recent years, a variety 
of continuous chromatography processes have been proposed, aimed at filling the gap 
between SMB-suitable binary enantiomer separations and standard capture and polishing 
steps in the industry [15]. Continuous chromatographic processes promise significant 
increases in productivity [16, 17], reductions in solvent [17, 18] and resin consumption [19, 20], 
and an overall decrease in cost [16]. The latter is a strong driver considering that about 70 – 
80 % of biologics manufacturing costs are associated with downstream processing [21].  
These benefits can be realised when the increased complexity of the equipment and operation 
are accepted. Multiple columns with interconnections, valve switching, and synchronisation 
constraints pose challenges to the design and implementation of continuous chromatographic 
processes [22]. 
1.3 Novo Nordisk 
Novo Nordisk A/S is an international pharmaceutical company with headquarters in Denmark. 
The company discovers, develops, produces, and sells biological medicines within the areas of 
Diabetes, Obesity, and rare diseases, such as Haemophilia and Growth Disorder [23]. 
Additionally, Novo Nordisk has several drugs in the pipeline addressing other serious chronic 
diseases, such as Alzheimer’s and Cardiovascular Disease – the world’s leading cause of death 
[24].  
Novo Nordisk was founded through the merger of Novo Industri A/S and Nordisk Gentofte A/S 
in 1989 – two companies each of which had an over 60-year long history in Diabetes care 
research and insulin production [25]. Today, Novo Nordisk is the largest provider of insulin by 
volume and holds a global Diabetes care market share of 29.6 % by value (Q2 2021, [26]), 
providing products for 32.8 million patients (2020).  
1.4 Motivation 
This thesis has two main objectives. Firstly, a holistic understanding of the batch and 
continuous chromatography process landscape is built. Secondly, different chromatographic 
processes are quantitatively compared with respect to their performance at the design point 
and their operable disturbance space in open- and closed-loop control. 
To achieve the first objective, the literature is reviewed to map, classify, and characterise 
different batch and continuous chromatographic processes. A special emphasis is placed in 
this thesis on understanding how the different processes conceptually relate to each other. 
==PAGE:16==
4 
 
Figure 1.2 Operable disturbance space in open- and closed-loop, the shaded regions indicate under which disturbances 
the process performs according to a given performance criterion with and without a control system 
The second objective is achieved by the means of conducting a case study. Batch and 
continuous chromatographic processes and their associated control systems are designed to 
purify an industrial Calcitonin mixture from impurities. While this separation task may not 
directly correspond to one of Novo Nordisk’s downstream unit operations, it is representative 
of a standard polishing step in a biopharmaceutical production facility, and the learnings from 
the case study will likely be transferable and generalisable to similar separation problems. The 
method used for the analysis are numerical simulations of the different processes in open- 
and closed-loop. While no experimental validation is performed, the process model has 
previously been experimentally validated and can be expected to represent reality accurately 
enough to provide insight into the behaviour of the studied systems.  
The concept of the operable disturbance space is illustrated in Figure 1.2. Disturbances are 
inputs that cannot be manipulated while still affecting the process performance. By keeping 
all manipulable inputs at their nominal design values (open-loop), the process performance 
may quickly deteriorate when disturbances deviate from their nominal values. A control 
system is designed to reject disturbances acting on the process (closed-loop), i.e. to restore 
the process performance in the event of disturbances. Consequently, the region of 
disturbances under which the process can be operated with satisfactory performance is 
expected to be larger in closed- than in open-loop. 
1.5 Outline of Work and Contributions to the State-of-the-art 
The thesis is structured as follows: in Chapter 2 the available literature on batch and 
continuous processing in general and specific to chromatography is reviewed. This includes a 
short introduction to classical batch chromatography and a review of the topology of 
continuous chromatographic processes as well as their associated design and control 
strategies.

Based on the findings in Chapter 2, in Chapter 3 a Value Stream Mapping is performed to 
identify potential application areas for continuous chromatography in a representative 
production process from Novo Nordisk and to assess the benefits from a systems-perspective. 
In Chapter 4 the methods used for the case study analysis are presented. Different types of 
chromatography models are reviewed including information on the computational 
implementation and numerical methods. Furthermore, shortcut design methods used for 
designing batch, SMB, and MCSGP processes are presented as well as the primary methods 
for system identification and control design. 
In Chapter 5 the case study results are presented. First, the implementation of a mechanistic 
model for an MCSGP system from the literature is validated by comparison to reference 
simulation data. Second, a batch and an SMB process for a multicomponent, centre-cut

2.2 Batch and Continuous Manufacturing 
The main trade-off for the choice between batch and continuous processes is between 
productivity and flexibility [28, 31, 32]. Continuous processes are generally favoured for high-
throughput applications because the low idle time enables a high volumetric productivity. 
Apart from the reduced idle time, however, continuous processes are not inherently more 
productive than batch processes. For instance, it can be shown that for a chemical reaction 
with positive reaction order, a batch reactor requires a smaller volume than a continuously 
stirred tank reactor to achieve the same conversion [33]. A thorough comparison between 
batch and continuous processes in general is shown in Table A.1 in Appendix A. 
Batch processes are often favoured in multipurpose plants because of the simple nature of 
the units that can flexibly perform several different tasks [34-36]. Continuous processes tend 
to employ specialised equipment, which is potentially harder to adapt to a new task.  
Pseudo-continuous processes require a synchronisation of several batch units, which puts 
additional constraints on the processing times. This can have a negative impact on the 
volumetric productivity and make it difficult to adapt the unit to new tasks because the 
scheduling of the units to achieve pseudo-continuity is highly problem specific. The choice for 
semi-continuous processes is usually made when control over the feed addition rate is 
==PAGE:21==
9 
required instead of adding all feed materials at once (e.g. nutrient addition in fed-batch 
fermentation). 
The comparison shows additional reasons why continuous processes are favoured in large-
scale plants with low profit margins: continuous processes enable a high degree of automation 
due to simpler process control in steady-state [30, 32, 37, 38], simpler material and energy 
integration and reduced labour requirements [12]. On the other hand, the comparison also 
indicates why batch processes are often favoured for small-scale multi-product plants. The 
latter require frequent cleaning of equipment, which is more easily incorporated into a batch 
processing sequence.  Furthermore, for processes with slow kinetics that require very long 
residence times, batch processes may be the only feasible alternative [28, 39]. 
The pharmaceutical industry has special requirements compared to other manufacturing 
industries. Product quality, cleaning requirements, speed-to-market and material tracking are 
particularly important factors. A detailed comparison between batch and continuous 
processes with respect to these metrics is shown in Table A.2 in Appendix A. This comparison 
shows the clear potential continuous processing offers from a quality, product safety and 
process development perspective [12, 30, 40]. However, it also gives some justification for the 
traditional use of batch processes in the pharmaceutical industry: especially material tracking 
is much simpler due to clearly defined residence times compared to the wide distributions 
encountered in continuous processes [37]. 
However, the comparison also shows that other factors become more important nowadays. 
The residence time problem can be solved by a careful characterisation of its distribution via 
tracer experiments [37]. The more online measurements (Process Analytical Technology), 
automation, and process control strategies are in place, the more continuous processes profit. 
Finally, the possibilities for modern, model-based process design methods are significantly 
improved when continuous processes are utilised due to their often simpler mathematical 
description. 
Table A.3 in Appendix A highlights the different requirements of the pharmaceutical and bulk 
chemical industry. The comparison shows that continuous processes are the only sensible 
choice for bulk chemical production and with few exceptions (e.g. gas adsorption), this is the 
dominant mode of operation today. For the pharmaceutical industry, the choice is not as 
obvious. The productivity-flexibility trade-off tends to favour batch processes, but this situation 
may be different for high-volume oral drugs and dedicated facilities. From a product quality 
perspective, a clear trade-off exists between easy batch tracking and cleaning routines versus 
improved process control and reduced contamination in closed, continuous processes.

2.4 Batch and Continuous Chromatography 
Batch and continuous chromatography are based on the same physical phenomena and 
separation principles. This is also expressed by the fact that the same models and governing 
equations are used to describe the processes. Therefore, the transport phenomena are first 
reviewed from a general perspective before describing the specific process topologies. 
2.4.1 Separation Principle 
Chromatography is an adsorptive separation process based on the deposition of molecules on 
a surface [8]. The separation effect is caused by differences in the affinities of the species in 
the mixture to the surface. As illustrated in Figure 2.4, this leads to differences in the partition 
coefficient of solutes in the mobile and the stationary phase. In both batch and continuous 
chromatography this effect is exploited by passing a liquid containing the mixture over a bed 
of solids.  
 
 
Figure 2.4 Two species with different adsorption affinities, left: weakly adsorbed component, right: strongly adsorbed 
component 
Species molecules only move in the flow direction while being in the liquid phase and are 
retained while being in the solid phase. The more strongly adsorbed a component, the higher 
the fraction of molecules that are in the stationary phase at a time, and the slower the net 
migration velocity of the component [42]. The resulting differences in the migration velocities 
are the immediate effect that is exploited in all chromatographic processes. 
2.4.2 Transport Phenomena 
The main transport phenomena in chromatographic columns are shown in Figure 2.5 for a 
single solute [6]. Convection refers to the transport of solutes through the interparticle space 
with the interstitial velocity of the mobile phase.  
Axial mixing in the mobile phase is caused by two effects. The first are radial or circumferential 
gradients in the axial velocity caused by irregularities in the packing. The resulting uneven 
velocity over the cross-section causes packets of fluid to move ahead or behind the mean flow 
[9], causing a dispersive effect (eddy diffusion, [7]). The second effect is molecular diffusion in 
axial direction, which can often be neglected in preparative applications due the high flow 
velocity [7]. 
==PAGE:24==
12 
 
Figure 2.5 Main transport phenomena in chromatographic systems happening at different length scales 
The adsorption process consists of a series of finite-rate processes: solutes are transferred 
from the bulk mobile phase to the particle surface (external mass transfer), diffuse into the 
pores and adsorb onto the surface. Details on the mechanisms of these processes are 
described in [6]. 
The adsorption process itself can be driven by various types of attachment forces that define 
the type of solid resin. Common attachment forces that are exploited in protein 
chromatography are hydrophobic interaction (HIC, RPLC), ionic binding (IEX) and biospecific 
interaction (BIC) [2]. The adsorption equilibrium depends significantly on the composition of 
the mobile phase. Certain additives can alter polarity, pH or ionic strength of the liquid and 
thereby alter the relative affinity of the solutes to the liquid and solid phase. This effect is 
exploited in gradient elution chromatography. 
The adsorption equilibrium mostly determines the migration velocity and retention time of the 
solutes, as well as the asymmetry of the elution peaks and peak interaction. Dispersion, 
adsorption kinetics and mass transfer effects are, in the first approximation additive, 
contributions to concentration profile broadening [6]. 
2.4.3 Batch Chromatography 
Batch chromatography generally follows a two-step procedure. First, a certain amount of the 
feed mixture is introduced into the column (loading). Second, this material is eluted from the 
column by introducing solvents at the inlet (elution). However, depending on the binding 
strength of the solutes, part of the feed material can already elute during the loading phase.  
This operating mode is referred to as elution chromatography and is the dominating 
technique in preparative applications today. Other modes of operation are used for certain

Elution chromatography can further be classified into isocratic and gradient elution 
chromatography [8]. In isocratic elution, the solvent composition stays constant during elution, 
while in gradient elution its composition is altered in a step-wise or linear manner. 
Another useful way to classify batch chromatography processes in an industrial context is 
based on the purpose they serve in the production process. Two major types of 
chromatographic steps can be distinguished: capture and polish.  
In capture steps, the product is separated from unrelated impurities with very different 
physical properties [2]. This allows loading a large amount of feed mixture onto the column, 
eluting loosely bound impurities in a wash step, and eluting the product at once with a step 
change in the solvent strength. 
In purification or polishing steps, the product is separated from similar impurities. This task is 
more difficult because these impurities tend to have similar affinities to the solid phase as the 
product [2]. Therefore, a smaller amount of feed is introduced into the column, which 
separates in time and space on its path to the outlet of the column, resulting in a series of 
concentration peaks at the outlet. Fractions of the outlet concentration profile are collected at 
specified cut times to capture the product peak and to discard the impurity peaks. Capture and 
polishing steps are illustrated in Figure 2.6. 
2.4.4 Recycling Chromatography 
Polishing chromatography can be improved by using the concept of recycling unresolved 
fractions during the next run as illustrated in Figure 2.7. In situations where complete 
resolution of the peaks is difficult to achieve, it can be advantageous to accept an incomplete 
resolution and only collect the pure part of the unresolved peaks while recycling the mixed 
zone. This method is already established in the industry [43]. An optimisation study with a 
ternary mixture showed that this method can improve both yield and productivity compared 
to a conventional polishing step [43].

2.5 Continuous Chromatography 
As defined in Section 2.1, different degrees of continuity can be distinguished. Here, true 
continuous processes are first reviewed, followed by semi-batch and pseudo-continuous 
processes.  
==PAGE:27==
15 
 
Figure 2.9 Spatial separation in moving bed chromatography illustrating that liquid and solid phases moving in different 
directions are required to achieve continuous separation 
The first observation that can be made about batch chromatographic processes is their 
inherently discontinuous nature. The different fractions are collected at the same point in 
space (column outlet) but at different points in time. For any steady-state process, the fractions 
would need to be collected at different points in space and at the same time, however.  
This can only be achieved, when the apparatus provides the possibility that different solutes 
move away from the feed location in different directions. Since in a batch process, the solid 
phase is stationary and the liquid phase moves in one direction only, continuous separation is 
not possible. The latter can only be achieved if the solid and liquid phases move in different 
directions away from the feed location. 
This circumstance is illustrated in Figure 2.9, where the red arrows describe the possible 
directions of solute movement. The figure also shows two truly continuous processes, the True 
Moving Bed (TMB) and Annular Chromatography (AC). Due to restrictions on the movement of 
the solid (e.g. particle breakage, inhomogeneous flow, implementation difficulties [6]), none 
of these are used in practice. 
In a True Moving Bed, where liquid and solid move in countercurrent, two possible directions 
of movement are possible and therefore, at most two components can be separated 
completely. The True Moving Bed is therefore an inherently binary separation technique. In 
Annular Chromatography, the two phases move in crosscurrent. This allows for infinitely many 
possible directions of solute movement and the process is therefore a true, continuous, 
multicomponent separation technique. 
2.5.1 Simulated Moving Bed 
The SMB principle can be derived from the TMB principle by recognising that the solid phase 
needs to move relative to the ports only. Instead of moving the solid, the feed, extract, raffinate, 
and desorbent ports are moved in the direction of the liquid flow instead, causing an 
equivalent relative motion. Since continuous valve movement along the bed is impractical, the 
==PAGE:28==
16 
bed is divided into at least four columns. The valves then move in discrete steps or switches to 
the next location. The transition from the TMB to the SMB is illustrated in Figure 2.10. 
In a full, closed-loop TMB or SMB, four zones with specific tasks can be identified. These tasks 
are achieved by choosing different ratios of liquid to solid flow rate in each zone. The higher 
this ratio, the stronger the driving force for solutes to move in the liquid flow direction.  
In zone I the strongly retained component must move in the liquid flow direction towards the 
extract port. In zones II and III the strongly retained component must move in the solid flow 
direction towards the extract port while the weakly retained component must move in the 
liquid flow direction towards the raffinate port. In zone IV the weakly retained component 
must move in the solid flow direction to be transported towards the raffinate port and to 
regenerate the desorbent for recycle. These conditions require that for a given solid flow rate, 
the liquid flow rate is highest in zone I, followed by zone III, II, and IV. A schematic of the 
internal concentration profile of an SMB process is shown in Figure 2.11.

2.5.2 Multicolumn Countercurrent Solvent Gradient Purification 
The concept of recycling chromatography is extended from a single column to a pseudo-
continuous multicolumn setup in the MCSGP process. Instead of recycling the mixed fractions 
during the next run, it is recycled to a different column. Pseudo-continuity can be achieved by 
employing sufficiently many gradient elution batch processes that are scheduled in a 
staggered mode, such that when one column elutes a mixed fraction, a different column is 
ready to receive it. 
The original MCSGP process proposed by Aumann et al. (2007) consists of six columns and is 
fully pseudo-continuous, i.e. one column is in the feeding and one is in the product elution 
phase at every point in time [48]. The process is shown in Figure 2.14, where each column 
elutes a batch chromatogram shown in Figure 2.15. Each column alternately undergoes a 
batch and interconnected mode. In batch mode, the pure fractions are eluted while in the 
interconnected mode, the mixed fractions are eluted and received to and from a different 
column. 
Going from right to left in Figure 2.14, first the mixed fraction of weak impurities and product 
is fed onto the regenerated column, followed by the feed and the mixed fraction of strong 
impurities and product. During the feeding phase, the weak impurities are eluted and after 
the feed, the solvent gradient is applied to the column to elute the remaining fractions. Eluent 
is added to the recycle fractions to adjust the solution to the desired solvent gradient. 
The flow rates in each column are adjusted to synchronise the duration of each of the substeps 
(see design methodology in Section 4.3.1). Note that always the same three columns interact 
with each other during the interconnected steps. Thus, an equivalent process can be 
constructed with three columns only. The three-column process has the additional advantage

2.5.3 Periodic Counter Current Chromatography (PCC) 
While SMB and MCSGP are alternatives to batch polishing steps, PCC-type of processes were 
developed as continuous alternatives to the capture step. A variety of different variants of the 
process have been proposed under names such as SMCC, CaptureSMB, StepSMB or BioSMB 
[20, 51]. All these variants work according to the same core principle of sequential loading. 
The problem with batch capture processes is that, due to concentration front spreading effects, 
not the whole binding capacity of the resin can be utilised if yield loss needs to be avoided. 
This situation is illustrated in Figure 2.17, where the curve describes the internal concentration 
profile of product in the unit. If the column is loaded further, and the concentration front 
advances to the right, product will start breaking through and yield loss occurs. Therefore, 
loading needs to be stopped and the column is washed and eluted. Note that in batch capture 
chromatography only part of the resin is saturated. It is estimated that in batch capture steps 
the resin capacity utilisation is often as low as 50 – 60 % [51]. The drawback of this is that the 
ratio of feeding to elution time is low because an only partially loaded column is sent to elution. 
This problem can be overcome by recycling the breakthrough of one column to a second 
column (sequential loading). The idea is illustrated in Figure 2.18 for a semi-batch example 
process proposed in [52].

2.5.4 Comparison between Batch and Continuous Chromatography 
It is of interest to evaluate to which extend the advantages and disadvantages of continuous 
processes discussed in Section 2.2 and shown in Table A.1 and Table A.2 in Appendix A apply 
to the case of batch and continuous chromatography.  
First, batch chromatography is not a typical batch process where material is filled into a vessel, 
processed, and emptied. Instead, it is a flow-through process, where a series of steps (loading 
– elution) is executed in a serial but potentially uninterrupted manner. This means that batch 
chromatography can in principle be operated without significant capacity losses due to idle 
time. From a flexibility perspective, neither batch nor continuous chromatography can be 
preferred, since chromatography columns are usually specialised equipment that cannot carry 
out other process steps.  
From a controllability perspective, continuous chromatography does not provide continuous, 
steady-state process outputs except for True Moving Bed processes, which are not realizable 
in practice. Only in the limit of infinitely many columns per zone does the SMB process fully 
realise this advantage.  
As shown in Chapter 3, the reduced need for intermediate storage provides a real advantage 
of continuous chromatography processes, leading to potential reductions in the facility 
footprint. The labour requirements for batch chromatography are not expected to be higher 
than for continuous setups, since both processes can be fully automated. 
The start-up behaviour of continuous chromatography can indeed be problematic when 
frequent interruptions, e.g. due to cleaning procedures are required because continuous 
chromatography setups generally require a significant number of cycles to reach their steady-
state [6]. Since chromatography does in general not require manual handling of materials and 
is a closed system, contamination concerns are not expected to be different for batch and 
continuous setups.

and

development 
perspective,

chromatography may in this case even be preferable due to the higher complexity of the 
continuous processes, which require significant design efforts. From a quality control 
standpoint, batch chromatography can realise the advantage of simpler material tracking 
because material is not recycled within the unit, and thereby its residence time can be well 
defined. 
In summary, the reduced need for intermediate storage appears as the main potential 
benefit of continuous chromatographic processes judging from these general viewpoints. 
This means that the increased complexity, design efforts, start-up duration, and material 
tracking difficulties need to be justified by a significantly increased performance of these 
processes in terms of e.g. productivity, eluent, and resin consumption.

2.5.5 Comparison of Working Principles  
Above, three major types of continuous chromatographic processes are described: SMB, 
MCSGP, and PCC. All three of them can be reduced to a single feature that distinguishes them 
from their batch counterpart, which is the concept of recycling to a different column. 
In the MCSGP process, only the mixed fractions are recycled to improve the yield. In the SMB 
process, the entire concentration profile is recycled, and the products are drawn from the pure 
parts of the concentration profile at the extract and raffinate ports. Not collecting the entire 
pure part of the concentration profile as in steady-state recycling chromatography has the 
advantage of reducing the eluent consumption because the same amount of product is 
collected at a lower flow rate and thus, higher concentration. Finally, in the PCC process the 
dilute part of the outlet concentration profile is recycled to increase the degree of loading, 
resin utilisation, and the associated productivity. 
The MCSGP process is clearly equivalent to the batch recycle process shown in Figure 2.7 with 
the only difference that the recycle fraction is not stored intermediately but is recycled directly 
to another column. 
Upon closer inspection, the SMB process is very similar to the steady-state recycling process 
with a single column shown in Figure 2.8. Consider the one-column analogue of the SMB 
process where one column sequentially performs the tasks of all four zones and its liquid 
outlet is stored in a plug flow device as described in [53]. In that case, the entire concentration 
profile of the SMB is stored externally except for one of the zones. The single-column analogue 
is shown in Figure 2.19 and a comparison with Figure 2.8 shows that the only difference to

2.6 Process Design 
Three types of process design methods have been used in the literature for the design of batch 
and continuous chromatography systems. 
1. Design of experiments 
2. Rigorous, mechanistic model-based methods 
3. Shortcut design methods 
Chromatography systems present challenging design and control problems due to features 
such as nonlinearity, time- and space-dependence, cyclic steady-states, mixed-discrete 
variables (pooling, switchings), delays, and various performance trade-offs [54, 55]. For this 
reason, chromatography design and control have been treated extensively in theoretical 
studies in the literature [56]. In practice, however, the design is still largely empirical and the 
scale-up is based on trial-and-error [2, 57, 58]. Process control is currently implemented on a 
rudimentary level only or even based on manual feedback from operators [8, 59]. 
2.6.1 Design of Experiment 
The first class of design methods, Design of Experiments (DoE), is based on carrying out a pre-
defined number of experiments on lab-scale, where different combinations of design variables 
are tested with respect to the response variable (objective function). Subsequently, a purely 
empirical model is fitted to the multivariate data, which is then used to carry out optimisation 
studies [60]. Numerous experiment-based design studies are available in the literature, mainly 
for batch chromatography design [61-63]. They will not be reviewed in detail here because 
design methods two and three are more comprehensive and require fewer experiments due 
to the inclusion of knowledge on the mechanisms of the systems. 
2.6.2 Rigorous Methods 
The second class of design methods is dominating modern design studies of both batch and 
continuous chromatography in the literature (batch: [64-68], SMB: [69-73], PCC: [19, 74, 75]). 
First, a mechanistic model is selected and its parameters are estimated based on a small 
number of experiments. The design problem for chromatography is usually treated as an 
optimisation problem to determine appropriate values of the design variables. A thorough 
review of optimisation studies both offline for design and online for control is given in [56].  
Common objective functions are combinations of specific productivity, yield, and eluent 
consumption, while purity is usually treated as a lower bound constraint. Most optimisation 
studies for both batch and continuous chromatography focus on optimising a subset of the 
manipulated variables only, taking design parameters, such as column dimensions, as fixed.  
Mathematically speaking, the optimal design problem for chromatography is an optimal 
control problem due to the dynamic nature of the process. There are three methods available 
for solving optimal control problems: dynamic programming, indirect methods, and direct 
methods [76]. Only the latter is used in practice for chromatography optimisation. Direct 
methods include full discretisation of the governing PDEs (collocation), as well as single and 
==PAGE:36==
24 
multiple shooting methods. While all three methods have been used for chromatography 
design and control (collocation: [68], multiple shooting: [77]), single shooting methods are by 
far the most popular. In single shooting methods, the only decision variables are the 
discretised manipulated variables and the objective function is calculated by solving the model 
equations for every combination of manipulated variables that the optimisation algorithm 
chooses [78].  
2.6.3 Shortcut Methods 
The third class of design methods are shortcut methods. Shortcut methods are based on 
simple models of the process and provide simple design guidelines without the need for 
solving a mechanistic model.  
Few shortcut methods for the design of batch processes are available in the literature, as 
experiment-based and rigorous optimisation-based methods dominate. Recently, a shortcut 
method for binary systems with Langmuir isotherms has been proposed that provides the cut 
time and injection volume for a given yield and purity [79]. The method can, however, not 
easily be extended to ternary systems and neglects any mass transfer effects. In Section 4.3.2 
a design method for batch chromatography is proposed that does use numerical simulations 
of the model equations but does not require scanning the entire space of decision variables. 
For continuous chromatography systems, shortcut methods are much more popular. For SMB 
systems, the triangle theory provides useful starting estimates for the main operating 
variables [6, 8]. A variant of the triangle theory that is applicable to nonlinear systems is 
described in Section 4.3.3. 
For the MCSGP process, a powerful design method based on a given batch chromatogram has 
been developed in [48]. The method is described in Section 4.3.1. For PCC-type processes, 
shortcut methods are also available based on a single, measured breakthrough curve of a 
batch column [80, 81] but will not be reviewed further here. 
2.7 Process Control 
Feedback or feedforward control of chromatography columns based on online measurements 
are today absent or limited to rudimentary forms in industrial applications [55, 59]. In the 
literature different control strategies have been proposed. Figure 2.20 provides an overview 
over the most common technologies.  
The most common controller applied in the literature is Model Predictive Control (MPC). An 
MPC controller solves the dynamic optimisation problem of finding the best inputs to minimise 
an objective function over a finite horizon. The objective function can be the deviation from a 
given setpoint or the same objective functions that have been used during the design stage. 
==PAGE:37==
25 
 
Figure 2.20 Control strategies proposed for chromatography column control 
The MPC controller can act as purely feedforward, calculating the optimal manipulated 
variables as a reaction to online measured disturbances. In the presence of plant-model 
mismatch and unmeasured disturbances, however, this will lead to inaccurate predictions and 
control action [38]. 
Therefore, all MPC implementations for chromatography systems in the literature contain a 
form of output feedback from online measurements to improve the predictions. Output 
feedback can be incorporated in several ways. In [55, 82, 83] this is achieved by periodically re-
estimating the model parameters to align model predictions and measurements. The most 
common method, however, is to use a state estimator in form of a Kalman filter [84]. In most 
cases, the state estimation procedure is augmented with a disturbance estimator to achieve 
offset-free predictions [85-89]. A model prediction bias correction can also be addressed in an 
easier manner by adding the latest prediction error for key variables, such as yield and purity, 
to future inequality constraints or setpoints [90, 91]. 
Some authors [73, 77, 90] have succeeded in performing the online optimisation with the full, 
nonlinear model (Nonlinear MPC), but for most applications this strategy is computationally 
too expensive [88]. Therefore, in most MPC control studies, a linear dynamic surrogate model 
is trained first. The linear models can either be black-box models that are trained with 
simulation data from the nonlinear model [85, 92, 93] or linear state-space models that are 
obtained from the linearisation of the nonlinear mechanistic model around the cyclic steady-
state [86, 88, 89, 94]. 
However, MPC is not the only control strategy that has been proposed for the control of 
chromatography systems. Instead of calculating optimal future inputs online at every time 
step, a fixed control law can be designed offline. In [95], a PID controller is used to shift the 
assembled UV-profile of a three-column MCSGP process by manipulating the duration of the 
interconnected mode. In [96], a PID controller is used to control the product stream impurity 
content by manipulating the duration of two steps of the twin-column MCSGP process. The 
==PAGE:38==
26 
SMB process has also been controlled via static control laws: in [93, 97] the IMC control design 
method is used to control the position of the concentration fronts of the elution profile of an 
SMB unit. The setpoints for these controllers are determined via offline optimisation. 
Fixed control laws can also be used for batch processes, even though they do not possess 
cycle-to-cycle dynamics. In [98] the cutting points and feeding time are used to control yield, 
purity, and productivity of a batch elution process. Like the online parameter estimation 
procedure in MPC applications, the process model that provides the basis for the control law 
design can also be updated online [81].

Methods 
To analyse the case study in Chapter 5, methods are used from four scientific fields within 
Process Systems Engineering: modelling, process design, process control, and numerical 
solution methods. The focus of this thesis lies on the design and control as well as on the 
fundamental understanding of continuous chromatography systems. Even though 
mechanistic modelling builds the basis for the quantitative analysis and is heavily relied upon 
in the case study, the topic is therefore only briefly be discussed here. Numerical solution 
methods and control design methods are also treated only briefly, since they represent 
standard practices in the field. Emphasis is put on the design and control methods developed 
in this work. 
4.1 Modelling 
Two major types of models are used in chromatography modelling and modelling in general: 
mechanistic models and empirical models. Mechanistic models are mathematical descriptions 
of physical phenomena, such as conservation laws and phase equilibria. Empirical models have 
a generic mathematical structure with parameters that are estimated purely based on 
experimental data. 
4.1.1 Mechanistic Modelling 
The mechanisms and physical phenomena in chromatography columns are well-known, and a 
wide range of mechanistic models with different levels of fidelity are available. Figure 4.1 
shows how, starting from all relevant physical phenomena, different mechanistic models can 
be obtained by employing different combinations of assumptions on the system [6, 7]. As an 
example, in protein adsorption chromatography, pore diffusion is usually the dominating 
kinetic resistance compared to external mass transfer and adsorption kinetics [107]. Therefore, 
models lumping all kinetic resistances into a single parameter are well-suited to describe these 
processes [7].  
The General Rate Model is the most comprehensive model and describes all relevant transport 
phenomena. However, it also contains the highest number of parameters that need to be 
estimated from experiments. The transport-dispersive model is often used to describe protein 
chromatography, especially in multi-column setups, because it offers a compromise between 
model resolution and computational time [108].  
It can be shown that in linear chromatography with sufficiently fast kinetics, the effects of axial 
dispersion, external mass transfer, pore diffusion, and adsorption kinetics are additive with 
respect to the band broadening [6]. However, the superficial velocity affects the band 
broadening due to axial dispersion and kinetic effects in different ways. Therefore, a model 
including at least one kinetic parameter and a dispersion term should be used when the effect 
of different flow rates in the column is of interest.

Several commercial and open-source chromatography model solvers are available, e.g. Aspen 
Chromatography™ [111], CADET [112], and GoSilico™ [113]. However, to achieve maximum 
control over model selection and numerical solution methods, a custom implementation 
relying only on MATLABs [114] built-in ordinary differential equation (ODE) solvers is used.

4.2.1 Partial Differential Equations 
The governing equations in Table 4.1 are a set of coupled, nonlinear partial differential 
equations (PDEs). MATLAB features a finite element based PDE-solver pdepe [115]. It is, 
however, not suited for chromatography simulation because it only features symmetric 
function approximations, which become unstable for highly asymmetric problems, such as 
convection-dominated species transport in packed bed columns [116, 117]. 
Therefore, the governing equations are first manually discretised in space, leading to a system 
of ODEs that can be efficiently integrated with standard ODE solvers. This strategy known as 
the method of lines (MOL) [118] is commonly used for chromatography simulation in the 
literature [58, 66, 89, 119].

coupled local or global polynomial shape functions, which are inserted into the PDEs to 
transform the differential terms into algebraic ones. A weak solution requirement, Equation 
(4.12), is then imposed that demands a set of the integrated, weighted errors over the domain 
to be zero.

4.2.3 Strong and Weak Coupling 
There are two basic strategies to solve multicolumn setups in which one columns outlet forms 
the inlet to a second column. In the weak coupling approach column one is simulated for a 
given time and its outlet concentration profile is stored. Subsequently, column two is simulated 
by feeding it the stored concentration profile. In open-loop setups this strategy is valid, but in 
closed-loop setups (see Figure 4.3) weak coupling introduces an error, since it does not 
consider how the outlet of column two might affect column one. Weak coupling approaches 
are reviewed in detail in [124]. 
 
Figure 4.3 Open- and closed-loop setups, in closed-loop setups the behaviour of the systems is strongly (two-way) coupled, 
which has important implications for the simulation strategy 
In this work, the strong coupling approach is used instead. This means that all columns are 
simulated simultaneously and the outlet of column one is immediately fed to column two and 
vice versa. This strategy involves solving a larger system of coupled ODEs and avoids any error 
in closed-loop setups. To reduce the computational time, the Jacobian sparsity pattern is 
calculated in advance and is passed to MATLABs ODE solver ode15s.

Elution, regeneration, and equilibration volumes 
The regeneration volume is chosen as small as possible while ensuring that the most strongly 
retained component is entirely removed from the column. This can be checked by inspecting 
the predicted internal concentration profiles in the liquid and solid phase after the 
regeneration solvent has passed through the column. 
The equilibration volume is chosen as small as possible while ensuring that the regeneration 
solvent in the entry zone of the column is entirely replaced by equilibration solvent. Again, this 
can be checked by inspecting the internal solvent concentration profile of the column after the 
equilibration solvent has been admitted. 
The elution volume, which needs to be fed before the regeneration solvent is admitted, can be 
determined by timing the start of the regeneration phase such that the regeneration solvent 
front starts reaching the column outlet right after the second cut has been performed. This 
way, it is avoided that the high regeneration solvent strength affects the concentration profile 
during elution and the elution volume is kept to a minimum.

4.3.3 SMB 
Shortcut methods available in the literature for the design of SMB units are mostly based on 
the analytical solution of the ideal model. The latter predicts the migration velocity of 
concentration fronts within the unit depending on the liquid flow rate in each section of the 
SMB. Constraints on the allowed migration distance within the unit during one switching 
interval are then used to derive bounds on the operating variables. The region of complete 
separation forms a triangle in the (𝑚2, 𝑚3) space, giving the method the name triangle theory 
[6]. 𝑚𝑖 is the ratio of net liquid to the apparent solid flow rate in zone 𝑖 of the SMB. 
For linear isotherms, the bounds on the flow rate ratios for complete separation can directly 
be given as the Henry constants of the relevant solutes [6]. For nonlinear isotherms, the 
bounds are significantly more difficult to derive. Explicit solutions have been developed for 
several isotherms including binary [126] and multicomponent Langmuir isotherms [127]. 
Using numerical solutions of a set of equations derived from the ideal model, the method has 
been extended to a wide class of binary isotherms [128]. The method has been successfully 
applied to binary Bi-Langmuir and implicit isotherms for ion-exchange equilibria [129, 130]. 
Except for the linear and binary Langmuir isotherm, all the above methods require significant 
calculation efforts to determine the region of complete separation. The methods are based on 
the ideal model of a True Moving Bed, which makes them approximate for SMB columns with 
few columns per zone [131] and significant mass transfer limitations. Furthermore, the 
methods assume complete separation, which is frequently not required, and many use the 
approximation of the multicomponent separation via a binary system. 
Based on these limitations, a significant amount of fine tuning in numerical simulations with 
the mechanistic model is expected after the initial design parameters are obtained from any 
given shortcut method. Therefore, it is decided to use a simpler strategy to generate the initial 
design.

Solvent strength 
The influence of the solvent strength on the performance of the SMB is seldomly studied [133], 
and no shortcut design methods for choosing the solvent strength are available in the 
literature.  
Here, a driving force based approach is proposed. The design method above shows that the 
driving velocity difference for the separation is the velocity difference between the rear of the 
weak component and the front of the strong component. A sensible way to select the solvent 
strength is therefore to evaluate this velocity difference over a range of solvent concentrations 
and to select the solvent strength at which the maximum difference is achieved.

transfer limitations.

Fine tuning via numerical simulations 
The design method described above can provide rough but useful starting estimates for the 
design of a binary SMB. Especially due to the significant assumptions on the internal 
concentration profile for the determination of 𝑚1 −𝑚4, fine tuning of these parameters with 
the nonlinear model is necessary. The fine tuning procedure is described in [8]. To summarise 
it, if the extract purity is too low, increase both 𝑚2 and 𝑚3, by the same amount to shift the 
concentration profile to the right. If the raffinate purity is too low, decrease both 𝑚2 and 𝑚3 to 
shift the profile to the left. If both purities are too low, decrease the difference 𝑚3 −𝑚2 and 
vice versa. An optimal operating point is found when all four critical points in Figure 4.9 
migrate exactly as far as permitted [8]. Additionally, the column length can be increased if the 
mass transfer limitations make the separation difficult even at low 𝑚3 −𝑚2.

Extension to pseudo-binary separation & SMB cascades 
The method described above is applicable to binary mixtures. However, it can easily be 
extended to pseudo-binary mixtures and SMB cascades (Figure 2.13). It is important to decide 
which components to declare as weak and strong for each criterion in Equations (4.32) - (4.35). 
Consider a centre-cut separation where a target component C is to be extracted from a mixture 
of product and non-valuable impurities A, B, D, and E. As described in Section 2.5.1, the first 
decision is which split to perform first. Different measures of the ease of separation are 
available based on the isotherm. Here it is proposed to use the same metric as is used to select 
the solvent strength, i.e. the maximum driving velocity difference 𝑣𝑤𝑟−𝑣𝑠𝑓. 
Without restriction of generality, assume that the first SMB removes A and B while the second 
SMB removes D and E. In the first SMB, C can be declared as the strong component because D 
and E do not need to be desorbed in zone I but are permitted to break through and partly 
leave through the raffinate port [135]. Components D and E can be assumed to be present at 
their feed composition in each section and this should be accounted for when calculating all 
isotherm slopes and secants. In zone III, components A and B are assumed to be at 75 % of 
their feed concentration for the calculation of 𝑚3. Attention must be paid in zones II and IV. In 
zone II, it is the rear of component B that plays the role of the weak component, but in zone 
IV it is the weakest component A that plays the role of the weak component, since A migrates 
faster than B [136]. 
In the second SMB, the feed consists of C, D, and E. Now, C plays the role of the weak 
component and attention must be paid in zones I and III. In zone I, E plays the role of the 
strong component while in zone III, D plays the role of the strong component because D 
migrates faster than E. 
SMB cascade coupling 
When two SMBs are connected in series, the second SMB will receive a non-constant feed from 
the first one because the SMB is only truly continuous in the limit of infinitely many columns 
in each zone. This can lead to highly irregular behaviour in the case of non-congruent 
switching times. The first option to address this problem is to place a large enough 
intermediate storage tank in between the SMBs that dampens the oscillations of the feed to 
the second SMB. 
The second option is to use congruent switching times for the two units [135, 137], i.e. coupling 
the switching times to be integer multiples of each other. This results in regular oscillations 
that are significantly more favourable for the monitoring and control of the unit. Since the 
system state repeats in a predictable manner, this method allows for averaging procedures 
with a constant averaging period. Considering that part of the motivation for using continuous 
chromatography is to avoid intermediate storage, the latter option is preferred here. 
By coupling the two switching times, one degree of freedom gets lost during design and 
control of the cascade system. The values 𝑚1 −𝑚4 are crucial for the separation performance 
==PAGE:63==

of the unit while the feed flow is imposed as a constraint, meaning that they cannot be given 
up as a design degree of freedom. Since the apparent solid flow rate is fixed via Equation (4.36), 
one degree of freedom with respect to the column dimensions length and diameter needs to 
be given up. Here, it is decided to keep the maximum superficial velocity constant during the 
design and to adjust the column length to achieve the required apparent solid flow rate instead

4.4 Process Control 
In this work, control systems for batch, SMB and MCSGP processes are designed. Fixed control 
laws in the form of discrete transfer functions are designed offline instead of using online 
optimisation as discussed in Section 2.7.  
For the batch chromatography system, a combination of a simple feedback controller and an 
MPC is proposed. The reason the batch column is treated differently, is that the use of simple 
feedback control laws would lead to significant interactions, since the cutting times affect both 
yield and purity. The proposed MPC-part of the controller is the solution of the optimal pooling 
problem and does not require any numerical solution of a process model. The latter is enabled 
by the fact that the pooling decision does not affect the state of the column itself, and a similar 
treatment is not possible for the SMB and MCSGP processes. 
For the design of the feedback control laws, the workflow shown in Figure 4.10 is used, which 
solely relies on established control theory tools. The latter will only briefly be reviewed, since 
they are described extensively in the literature [38]. The steady-state analysis and controller 
pairing is carried out using the Relative Gain Array (RGA), discrete transfer functions as 
described in Section 4.1.2 are estimated based on step tests with the nonlinear model, and the 
controller synthesis is carried out using the Direct Synthesis (DS) method.

5.4 MCSGP Control 
The MCSGP process is characterised by a very high number of potential manipulated variables. 
All flow rates (except for the feed flow), solvent concentrations, and the switching time in Table 
C.1 can be used as manipulated variables. The primary control objectives for all processes are 
defined as keeping the switch averaged yield and purity close to their specified setpoints.  
To reduce the problem size, the potential manipulated variables are first narrowed down by 
physical arguments. The regeneration step only removes all adsorbed solutes from the column, 
so if its flow rate and solvent strength are sufficiently large, 𝑄1, 𝑐𝑚𝑜𝑑,1(𝑡= 0) and 𝑐𝑚𝑜𝑑,1(𝑡=
𝑡𝑠𝑤𝑖𝑡𝑐ℎ) are not expected to have a significant effect on the yield and purity. The switching time 
is undesirable as a manipulated variable, since it affects all process steps and is expected to 
lead to significant interactions between control loops. 
The flow rate 𝑄6,𝑖𝑛 and the solvent concentrations during step five and six are more difficult to 
exclude as manipulated variables. Given that in this specific case (see Figure 5.1), the yield loss 
occurs predominantly during the elution of the weak fraction, it appears to be possible to affect 
this yield loss by reducing the migration velocity during steps five and six, e.g. by reducing the 
solvent concentration in these sections. Unexpectedly, using numerical simulations with the 
mechanistic model, this can be shown not to be the case. A possible explanation is provided in 
Appendix D. 
This leaves the flow rates and solvent concentrations in steps two (S+P elution), three (P 
elution), and four (W+P elution) as candidate manipulated variables. The solvent gradient is 
here reduced to a single degree of freedom by varying the start and end points together, i.e. 
keeping the gradient slope constant.  
Step tests are performed with the six remaining possible manipulated variables. Subsequently, 
the best pairing between the manipulated variables and the controlled variables yield and 
purity is determined via the RGA. Since there is no KW1 impurity in the product outlet, the 
purity can be replaced by the content of strong impurities in the product stream. KW1 is 
implicitly prevented from polluting the product stream because the yield controller stabilises 
the advance of the product concentration front that in turn displaces the KW1 peak.

5.6 SMB Control 
For SMB process control via fixed feedback control laws, using yield and purity as controlled 
variables is usually not the preferred choice. Instead, the adsorption and desorption fronts of 
the concentration profiles inside the unit are used as controlled variables [93, 97]. The reason 
is that, at least in the linear isotherm case, the positions of the adsorption and desorption 
fronts depend linearly on the flow rate ratios 𝑚𝑖, which is a desirable property when linear 
control design methods are used. Due to the nonlinear shape of the concentration profiles, 
yield and purity do not depend linearly on the position of the adsorption fronts and the flow 
rate ratios. 
Different measures of the position of the adsorption fronts have been investigated in the 
literature, including inflection points [97], foot points (bottom part of the concentration fronts) 
[143], and the integrated areas between a setpoint and a measured concentration profile [144]. 
The latter has been reported to be sensitive to shifts in the front position while being less 
sensitive to deformations of the profile shape than the other methods. While in [144] it is 
recommended to use the area between 5 and 60 % of the maximum peak height of the 
nominal profile, this proves to be a poor indicator of yield and purity for the system in this case 
study. Instead, it is rather the foot points that leak into the respective sections and determine 
yield and purity of the process. For the present system, the area between the setpoint profile 
and the measured one around the foot points proves to be a good choice. The controlled 
variables are illustrated schematically as the shaded regions in Figure 5.11. The exact 
integration bounds are given in Table E.2 in Appendix E. 
The internal concentration profiles of the unit cannot be measured. Therefore, the assembled 
elution profile (AEP) [97] is used as an approximation. The assembled elution profile is obtained 
by combining the measured outlet concentrations of each column during a switch. The 
measurement frequency is selected as one switch of SMB1. Since one switch of SMB2 takes 
half the time of one SMB1 switch, the AEP for SMB2 is averaged over the last two SMB2 
switches.

5.7 Disturbance Space Quantification 
Two disturbances are chosen for the disturbance quantification: the feed flow rate and the 
total feed concentration. A disturbance in the feed flow rate can be caused by two reasons: 
either the feed flow rate is intentionally increased to increase the production rate, or the feed 
pump delivers a different flow rate than intended. Changes to the feed concentration can 
occur due to variation in the upstream fermentation or purification processes.  
Another reason for choosing these disturbances is that they can accurately be quantified with 
the given process model. In contrast, to properly quantify the effect of gel changes or 
temperature variations, the dependence of the model parameters on these factors needs to 
be known. Even though only two disturbances have been evaluated in this work, the concept 
is easily extended to other disturbances that can be quantified with the given model, such as 
buffer composition or the fraction of certain impurities in the feed. Similarly, the effect of 
model uncertainty can be quantified and visualised using the same concept applied to model 
parameters, such as the packing porosity or the mass transfer coefficient. 
To quantify the operable disturbance space, the term “operable” needs to be carefully defined 
and depends highly on the specific application and performance specifications. Here, the 
criterion is that the control system must return both yield and purity to within ±1 % of their 
setpoints within ten cycles of the SMB (200 min at the design point), ten batch chromatography 
runs (200 min at the design point) and seven MCSGP cycles (210 min at the design point). A 
constant lower bound criterion for the yield and purity is not sensible for the present case 
study system. Due to the delay in the measurement, in some cases the performance has 
already deteriorated before the controller can react (see for example Figure 5.8, left). 
To determine the operable disturbance space, the feed flow and total concentration are varied 
within ±50 % of their nominal values via a step change at 𝑡= 0, and open- and closed-loop 
simulations are carried out with the three systems. The range of the disturbances is chosen 
here to test the boundaries of the control systems. The actual disturbances that can be 
expected in a specific production facility are highly dependent on the remaining process. Still, 
the operable disturbance space is a useful indicator to compare the performance of the control 
systems. 
The results are shown in Figure 5.13, which corresponds to the schematic shown in Figure 1.2. 
The first observation is that the operable disturbance space in open-loop is significantly 
smaller than in closed-loop for all three systems. This indicates that the designed control 
systems reject the chosen disturbances very effectively and restore the system performance 
in the specified time under a wide range of feed flows and concentrations. 
The second observation is that the batch process has the largest operable open-loop 
disturbance space while the MCSGP process has the smallest. This may be related to the fact 
that, since the continuous processes recycle product within the unit, their steady-state can be

69

very sensitive to changes in the input variables due to accumulation effects (see e.g. Appendix 
D). This high sensitivity can also be observed in other processes that employ recycle of species 
within a system, such as reaction-separation-recycle systems, and has been termed the 
snowball effect [38]. 
The third observation is that for all three processes, the operable feed flow and concentration 
are inversely related both in open- and closed-loop. This indicates that it is mainly the total 
mass flow of species that determines the behaviour of the system. For batch chromatography, 
it is known that the dimensionless concentration profiles and thus, yield and purity, depend 
on the product of feed concentration and sample volume instead of on their individual values 
[145]. The disturbance spaces show that a similar relationship exists for SMB and MCSGP 
processes where the sample size is replaced by the feed flow. 
The fourth observation concerns the ratio of the operable to the total examined disturbance 
space. This ratio is larger for the SMB process (64 %) than for the MCSGP process (52 %) and 
the batch process (45 %). The result may be interpreted as the consequence of a combination 
of two factors: firstly, the SMB process has a higher measurement frequency (5 min) compared 
to the MCSGP and the batch processes (20 min). This means that the control system receives 
more frequent information that it can react to. Secondly, all control systems were initially 
tuned to achieve a similar performance in the test disturbance cases (±10 % feed flow and 
concentration). The fact that their performance differs for part of the disturbance space may 
indicate that the dynamics deviate more strongly for the MCSGP and batch processes than for 
the SMB process in this part of the disturbance space. 
Due to the increased computation time of the MCSGP process, these disturbance space 
simulations are performed in parallel on DTUs HPC cluster [146].

Discussion and Recommendation 
The methods for the design of the batch and the SMB cascade processes described in Sections 
4.3.2 and 4.3.3 have successfully been applied to the case study separation system, resulting 
in three process alternatives achieving the same production rate, yield, and purity in cyclic 
steady-state. Subsequently, control systems based mainly on simple feedback control laws and 
an optimal pooling routine have been designed for each system to meet a given dynamic 
closed-loop performance. Finally, the performance of the closed-loop systems has been 
compared under a wide range of disturbances to test the boundaries of the control systems 
and to create a useful metric for their comparison.  
In this chapter, the findings are discussed, compared, and translated into recommendations 
for Novo Nordisk. The design and control methods are reflected upon, and it is considered 
how they can be improved and extended based on the experience with the case study system. 
Finally, an outlook on possible future work is given from a wider perspective.

demonstrate that for this case study, the batch process outperforms the continuous processes 
in terms of productivity and eluent consumption. The biggest difference can be observed 
between the SMB process and the two other processes. A combination of two reasons is 
responsible for this: firstly, the SMB suffers from its isocratic operation, i.e. the most strongly 
retained impurity KS2 needs to be fully desorbed in zone I by employing a high desorbent flow 
==PAGE:84==
72 
rate instead of the using a short solvent regeneration step as in the batch and MCSGP 
processes. Secondly, the SMB is a binary separation process at its core, which is why two SMBs 
in series are required to perform a centre-cut separation. Thereby, the mixture gets 
significantly diluted in the first SMB and the second SMB needs to process a high feed flow 
rate, resulting in large column volumes and a high desorbent consumption. 
The reason the batch process performs better than the MCSGP process may be related to the 
nature of pseudo-continuous processes: except for the recycle, the MCSGP process undergoes 
the same process steps as the batch process. However, all phases need to be synchronised, 
which constrains the flow rates in each step of the MCSGP. This synchronisation constraint is 
not present in the batch process, which is why it can perform every step in series at the 
maximum allowable flow rate, leading to shorter cycle times. 
It should be mentioned that despite this shortcoming of the MCSGP and other pseudo-
continuous processes, the recycle concept should in principle enable operation at close to    
100 % yield, which is not as easily possible in a classical batch process with finite column 
efficiency due to the overlapping concentration profiles. Indeed, a slight change to the base 
case operating variables of the MCSGP can avoid the unintended product elution through the 
weak outlet and raise the yield to almost unity [109]. Under these specifications, the 
performance metrics of the batch process may look quite different, meaning that the MCSGP 
process should not be dismissed from a design perspective based on the results of this case 
study. On the other hand, the recycle concept can also be applied to batch processes (see 
Section 2.4.4), which avoids the synchronisation constraint while retaining the MCSGP feature 
of mixed fraction recycling. These processes are therefore important candidates to be 
considered from a productivity perspective. 
The comparison of the processes from a dynamic perspective (see Section 5.7) has shown that 
in the presence of disturbances, the continuous processes experience a more significant 
deterioration of performance in open-loop than the batch process due to the snowball effect 
in mass recycle systems. This highlights the clear need for a process control system for the 
operation of these systems in the presence of disturbances. While there are nuances between 
the closed-loop performance of the three systems as discussed in Section 5.7, all systems can 
reject a relatively wide range of disturbances. The SMB process stands out with the widest 
operable disturbance space, possibly due to its higher measurement and control frequency.  
However, none of the processes can be dismissed from a control perspective and with a well-
designed process control system in place, the choice for a process alternative should be made 
primarily based on the process performance at the design point. 
6.2 Recommendations 
Conceptually, continuous processes are very attractive due to their low idle times and steady-
state operation. However, as shown in Section 2.5.4, many of the classical qualitative 
advantages of continuous processes do not apply to chromatography, while some of the 
disadvantages including the increased start-up time and more difficult material tracking 
==PAGE:85==
73 
remain. Therefore, a quantitative analysis needs to show that continuous chromatography 
provides the economic advantages to justify its increased complexity. 
The quantitative analysis has been carried out for a representative polishing step in this thesis. 
The results indicate that an SMB cascade is generally likely to be less efficient for centre-cut 
separations and cannot be recommended for similar separation tasks. The steady-state 
performance of the MCSGP process also proves to be inferior to the batch process. This result 
may, however, not be generalisable to other sets of process specifications and requires further 
analysis. While the continuous processes show slightly improved closed-loop behaviour, the 
difference does not justify the lower steady-state performance, since all processes are able to 
reject a wide range of disturbances with their respective control systems. 
A very interesting option that is recommended to be explored are batch recycle processes, 
since they combine the advantages of the MCSGP and batch processes from a productivity 
standpoint. A clear recommendation can also be given for the implementation of online 
concentration measurement systems, such as online-HPLC. This will enable improved process 
monitoring and control for all types of chromatography systems and is a prerequisite for all 
control strategies developed in this thesis.  
The thesis also showed the potential stemming from a mechanistic model based analysis of 
chromatographic processes: in a few months, three entirely different systems could be 
designed, controlled, and tested under a wide range of conditions – an impossible task when 
relying only on experimental analysis. A clear recommendation can be given to expand the 
application areas of model-based analysis. 
Given the state-of-the-art and the analysis in this thesis, where do we stand within the topic of 
continuous chromatography in the industry and what needs to be done? First, the thesis has 
shown that continuous chromatographic processes are generally well-understood. The 
knowledge of batch chromatography can directly be applied to continuous chromatography, 
and the same mechanistic models are able to explain the behaviour and dynamics of the 
systems. Furthermore, all chromatographic processes are at their core based on the same 
separation principle, meaning that the ease or difficulty of separation depends on the mixture 
properties in the same way for batch and continuous chromatography.  
Differences in the design-point performance between batch and continuous chromatography 
therefore stem from the different process topologies. While in this work a thorough analysis 
between selected key process alternatives has been performed, the landscape that needs to 
be quantitatively explored and added to the comparison is larger, with batch recycle, open-
loop SMB and PCC-type of processes to mention. Nevertheless, the experience gained in this 
work provides an indication which designs are preferable in which situation and that 
continuous processes are not by default superior to their batch alternatives from a design 
perspective. 
==PAGE:86==
74 
Thinking about continuous chromatography as part of a bigger production process shows that 
it is important to consider its behaviour under disturbances. To address this, the results in this 
thesis have shown that we are able to apply well-known, simple and effective control methods 
to these highly complex processes. The decision for or against continuous chromatography is 
therefore not restricted by their controllability and disturbance rejection ability. 
6.3 Potential Improvements and Extensions 
While the process designs and control systems developed in this thesis all fulfil their required 
specifications, some improvements can be suggested in hindsight. First, the control systems 
exclusively rely on feedback mechanisms that are well suited to reject slowly varying and 
unmeasured disturbances like the step changes assumed in this work. In a batch production 
facility, however, batch-to-batch variation translates into rapidly varying disturbances. To 
reject these efficiently, a feedforward control system is required that adjusts the manipulated 
variables to cancel the effect that the disturbance is going to have on the system. 
From a design method perspective, a more rigorous procedure for deriving the bounds in the 
(𝑚2, 𝑚3) plane could provide better starting estimates for the design under highly nonlinear 
conditions (see discussion in Section 4.3.3). Additionally, it would be interesting to compare 
the applied shortcut design methods to a rigorous, optimisation-based design approach. 
From a model analysis point of view, the concept of the disturbance space proved to be very 
helpful for the comparison and could be extended to more disturbance dimensions as well as 
the analysis of the effect of model uncertainty on the open- and closed-loop performance. 
6.4 Future Work and Outlook 
The analysis in this thesis has shown that the choice for or against a batch or continuous 
process is in the special case of chromatography not as obvious as the historical success of 
continuous processing suggests and requires a careful quantitative analysis. However, 
chromatography is not the only unit operation in a biopharmaceutical production facility: 
centrifugation, crystallisation, reaction and filtration are processes that are often performed 
in batch mode and require a similarly rigorous analysis to give a useful recommendation.  
A logical next step to build upon this work would be to extend the analysis to capture steps, 
i.e. to compare the PCC-type of processes to batch capture. Motivated by the performance of 
the batch process in this work, the analysis should also be extended to advanced batch 
chromatography concepts incorporating mixed fraction recycling. 
After all individual continuous counterparts to existing batch unit operations have been 
characterised, the production process needs to be studied from a systems perspective. This 
involves comparing an end-to-end continuous production process consisting of the best 
identified continuous unit operations to a current batch-based facility. Analogous to this work, 
this can be done based on both steady-state and dynamic considerations.

Figure D.1 Weak outlet concentration (left) and accumulation of KW1 (centre) and product (right) upon a decrease in 
solvent strength in sections five and six of the MCSGP process 
Figure D.1 (left) shows the weak outlet concentration after a step decrease of the solvent 
concentration in steps 5 and 6. Initially, the decrease in the solvent strength indeed stops 
product and KW1 from being eluted in steps 5 and 6 (𝑡< 500 min ). However, instead of being 
transferred upstream towards the product elution, KW1 ( 𝑡< 500 min )  and product ( 𝑡<
3000 min ) accumulate in sections 5 and 6, leading to increasing concentration peaks during 
the W+P elution phase and thereby increased migration speeds of the concentration fronts. 
As the product concentration increases, it first starts to increasingly displace accumulated KW1, 
which then starts to break though at the weak outlet (𝑡= 500 min ). Eventually, the product 
accumulates to such a high concentration that it also starts breaking through (𝑡= 3000 min ). 
Thereby, it stops displacing previously accumulated KW1 and a new steady-state is reached, 
where the outlet concentrations of product and KW1 return exactly to their values before the 
decrease in the solvent concentration (𝑡= 4000 min ). The reason, the solutes accumulate, is 
that the final state of the W+P elution column does not depend on its initial state if sufficiently 
much product is present on the column in the initial state. A similar effect can be observed in 
elution chromatography, where for Langmuirian isotherms, the rear of the concentration 
profile does not depend on the injection width [79]. It is also important to consider the 
extremely long delay of around 70 cycles of the MCSGP until the final steady-state is reached. 
A second phenomenon that can be observed is that an increase in the product flow rate 𝑄3 
results in a decrease of the strong impurity content in the product outlet stream. While it is 
true that the increased flow rate should push more of the mixed S+P fraction profile out of the 
column, a secondary effect of increasing 𝑄3 dominates here.  
==PAGE:100==
88 
 
Figure D.2 Left: product outlet upon an increase in the product flow rate, centre/right: Internal liquid concentration profile 
evolution during W+P elution before (centre) and after (right) the product flow rate increase, KS1 scaled by a factor of four 
The tag-along effect occurs for Langmuirian isotherms when a high concentration of a weakly 
retained solute is present and a low concentration of a more strongly retained component. In 
that case, the weaker component becomes a significant competitor for adsorption sites and 
displaces the stronger component from the resin. Thereby, it tends to drag along the stronger 
component, leading to long-lasting interactions and a long plateau of the strong component 
under the peak of the weaker one [7]. 
An increased product flow rate leads to an increased amount of product being eluted during 
the product elution phase and a decreased amount of product being recycled in the 
subsequent S+P elution phase. This leads to a decrease in the amount of product that 
accumulates in sections five and six and in a decrease of the product elution peak during the 
W+P elution phase. The lower product elution peak, the less pronounced the abovementioned 
tag-along effect and the less far the strong impurities migrate during the W+P elution phase. 
Finally, this leads to the product elution phase receiving a column where the strong impurity 
peak is further away from the outlet and less of it elutes despite the initially increase flow rate. 
This hypothesis can be supported by inspecting the evolution of the internal liquid 
concentration profile in a column during the W+P elution phase before and after the product 
flow rate is increased from 0.3 to 0.4 ml/min as shown in Figure D.2. At the same position in 
the column and at the end of the W+P elution phase, the KS1 front has migrated farther than 
after the product flow rate increase. 
Finally, note that around 𝑡= 1000 min, the weak impurity KW1 starts to appear at the product 
outlet. This can also be explained by the decreased peak height of the product during W+P 
elution. As the latter migrates decreasingly far, the KW1 front is displaced decreasingly far until 
eventually not all of it is displaced from the column during W+P elution and it starts to elute 
during the subsequent product elution phase.
