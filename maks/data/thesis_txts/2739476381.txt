Summary
The goal of the current thesis is the visualization and analysis of the sound field in a
large national theater in Europe using microphone arrays. Already existing datasets
measured by the DTU’s Acoustic Technology group are used and the implementation
takes place in a MATLAB environment.
The starting point of the analysis is the investigation of the main acoustic parameters
relevant for concert halls as specified in ISO 3382-1, which are also visualized across
different areas in the room.
As complementary information, the 3D analysis of the sound field is also presented, fo-
cusing on the spherical harmonics transformation of the signals recorded from a spherical
microphone array (Eigenmike 32). This transformation allows for the investigation of the
incoming signals’ directivity which is achieved using the Pseudointensity Vector method.
Integrating both types of analysis provides important information for the listening expe-
rience in the current concert hall. The importance of this work reflects to the fact that by
just using one configuration setup, a holistic analysis of the sound field can be achieved.
This type of information is challenging to obtain, rarely available and expensive, yet
highly valuable for audience, sound or set engineers, and professional musicians.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Room acoustics play an important role in the listening experience in small rooms, large
auditoriums, and concert halls, and the perception of each person can lead to a sub-
jective characterization of it. The opinions of people who work in concert halls or are
directly connected to them, including conductors, music critics, and well-traveled music
aficionados, are highly valued. The attempt to connect the opinions and the subjective
perception of established people in the world of music revealed a set of parameters [1]
and later on, a common terminology [2] which is crucial for the characterization of con-
cert halls. The groundbreaking and extensive research of Leo L. Beranek revealed a
subjective rank order of fifty-eight concert halls around the world [3].
Taking a step beyond in [4], the author suggests that for the precise characterization of
concert halls, it is necessary to expand the list of standard parameters that are used.
After examining 10 different concert halls around the world, the study highlights the im-
portance of a more detailed analysis in high-quality performing arts spaces. It is also of
great importance to highlight the impact of the acoustics of various types of concert halls
in the emotional state of listeners, as reviewed in [5]. After many years of continuous
research in the acoustics of concert halls and the need to directly connect the estab-
lished terminology with subjective parameters, Kuusinen, A. and Lokki, T. introduced
”The wheel of concert hall acoustics” in which the main subjective aspects are gathered
and matched to the respective established parameters extracted mainly extracted from
ISO-3382 [6]. Τhe importance of this publication lies in the fact that it serves as a
bridge connecting all prominent features of room acoustics to perceptual ones in a more
structured and intuitive way.
Extensive research has been made around the objective parameters of room acous-
tics [7] [8]. In several publications authors aimed to understand the relation between
some of them [9] or the effect that different equipment [10] or method [11] has on them.
One of the most important parameters for room acoustics is the Reverberation Time
(RT), which represents the time of a 60 dB linear decay of sound energy [12]. In [13], the
authors tested several methods for the calculation of the reverberation time, aiming to
compare them with measured values, proving that there is always a deviation between
measured and simulated results, due to background noise.
Apart from the prominent features that are widely used to characterize a sound field, in

recent years, the attention has turned towards the 3D analysis of sound in rooms that
helps exploring the directionality and many other factors. The use of spherical micro-
phone arrays has a great impact in the analysis of the 3D sound field since they offer
great advantages [14]. Conventional methods suggest the use of specific microphones
(omnidirectional and figure-of-eight) for the calculation of the ISO-3382 parameters.
Overcoming this limitation, in several publications, the authors attempted to replace
these microphones with one spherical microphone array, utilizing its advantages, by
calculating both its omnidirectional and the first-order components. In [15] [16] [17]
the authors aimed to validate this suggestion. More specifically, in [15], the authors
compare measurements taken from an omnidirectional and a figure-of-eight microphone
pair versus the calculated omnidirectional and dipole components from a spherical array,
proving that for mid and high frequencies the results are satisfying. Later, by expanding
their research and focusing on more parameters, they proved that spherical microphone
arrays can be used to obtain room impulse response measurements [16]. Measuring in a
different large room, researchers in [17] validated once more this proposal, pointing out
a few deviations in one specific parameter (early lateral fraction).
Lastly, for the proper analysis of the sound field, source localization or direction of ar-
rival of a sound is essential, and for this purpose many algorithms have been developed
throughout the years. Recently, source localization using 3D microphone arrays is quite
common. In [18] the authors focused on the evaluation of MUSIC (multi-signal clas-
sification) using a high-order microphone array in noisy environments.
Additionally,
in [19] authors addressed the source localization problem, suggesting a solution utilizing
arbitrary microphone arrays. Finally, a low-complexity algorithm was suggested, where
both the energy and directional information are provided [20], while using a spherical
microphone array.
Inspired by the existing state-of-the-art in literature, the aim of the current thesis is the
characterization of the sound field in a national theater in Europe. The analysis starts
with the fundamental concepts of the analysis of the objective parameters as described
above and moves on to 3D analysis techniques trying to extract a combined outcome
suitable for the holistic sound field characterization. The thesis will be structured as
follows. In chapter 2, the theory behind the practices that are analyzed in the document
will be reported, starting from section 2.1 which analyzes the established room acoustic
parameters, and moving on to section 2.2 where the spherical harmonics subject is
explained. In chapter 3 the methods that were used during the execution of the thesis
are analyzed and their results are provided. Moving on to chapter 4, a discussion of the
results is included, followed by final comments and future work.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In this chapter the theory of the analysis methods of the sound field in closed spaces are
described. First, the basic relevant ISO-33821 parameters that provide the prominent
features of the sound field will be described. Then, taking a step beyond the analysis of
the fundamental parameters, we will focus on more sophisticated methods that provide
advanced information, related to sound directivity and 3D sound field information.
2.1
ISO 3382-1 room acoustic parameters
As extensively described in [6], ISO 3382-1 provides a set of fundamental parameters,
crucial for the key acoustical analysis of a room. For a holistic preliminary understanding
of the acoustics of a room, an analysis of most of the parameters is needed. Due to some
limitations during and after the measurement procedure, a specific set of the parameters
will be analyzed in this thesis.
Those are: Reverberation time, Early Decay Time,
Clarity, Lateral Fraction. In the following subsections, descriptions and mathematical
expressions are given.
2.1.1

calculation the Energy Decay Curve is used which is given by the Schroeder’s backwards

An important phenomenon that can be observed in complex rooms, such as large au-

two fitting lines. The combination of those two fitting curves is called multi-exponential
decay curve [22]. This phenomenon occurs because the late energy is returned to the
listening area from a significantly more reverberant auxiliary space.
Similar to the T60, Early Decay Time (EDT), is another important parameter as it is
related to the perceived reverberance. In the case of EDT, the first 10 dB decay time
is measured. Unlike T60, EDT is more location dependent, which means that it can
change from seat to seat. A fast decay of EDT expresses better clarity.

CHAPTER 3
Methods and results
In this section, the simulation results are presented. The aim is to characterize the sound
field in a national theater in Europe. The theater has a classic horseshoe shape which
is a traditional design for opera houses. It was built in the 19th century, with 12 666 m3
volume, including approximately 1400 seats. The floor plan (Figure 3.3a) and the cross
section (Figure 3.3b) are depicted below. The initial analysis considers the fundamental
ISO-33821 parameters and moves on to more complex directional properties.
The evaluation is based on datasets measured by the DTU’s Acoustic Technology group
in summer 2023 using distributed microphone arrays and is implemented in a MATLAB
environment [23].
3.1
Measurement setup
The measured dataset consists of room impulse responses (RIRs) measured with a spher-
ical microphone array and an omnidirectional sound source. All RIRs were obtained us-
ing the exponential sweep for frequencies ranging from 20 Hz to 20 kHz, for a sampling
frequency of 48 kHz. In terms of equipment, the omnidirectional sound source is a do-
decahedron provided by HBK - Hottinger Brüel & Kjær, which uses 12 loudspeakers in
a dodecahedral configuration to radiate sound evenly with a spherical distribution [24]
and the microphone array is an mh Acoustics em32 Eigenmike® [25], which consists of 32
14 mm electret microphones, placed in uniformly distributed capsules on a rigid sphere
of diameter 8.4 cm. Regarding hardware limitations, according to Figure 3.2, the sound
source can only be considered as an omnidirectional up to 1000 Hz, so for the upcoming
analysis we will focus only to the three lower frequency bands, 200 Hz, 500 Hz, 1000 Hz.
To accurately record the measured data, the source and receivers were strategically
placed on the stage and auditorium as seen in Figure 3.3. More specifically, the RIRs
were measured in 17 different receiver locations. For the current thesis, only one source
location was taken into account, located on stage at the coordinates (0.007, -1.55, 1.03)m.
The origin of the axes is located at the (0,0,0)m point of the x, y, and z axes, according
to Figure 3.3.

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
During the course of the thesis the sound field of large rooms was analyzed and visualized.
Starting from the fundamental room acoustic parameters, we moved on to more refined
techniques, utilizing the functionalities of all the available equipment. Throughout the
thesis, proper processing of the measured impulse responses occurred, with the aim of
characterization of the current concert hall sound field in terms of acoustic behavior.
More specifically, measurements were taken with Eigenmikes in 17 receiver position, in
a 1400-seat national theater in Europe. The analysis was performed using MATLAB [23].
The current chapter will focus on providing an important commentary on the analysis
described in the previous sections, as well as the attempt to interconnect the basis of
the room acoustic analysis to subjective aspects. Suggestions for future work will also
be included.
First, the analysis of the parameters that are included in ISO-33821 show a frequency
and location dependency, as well as an interconnection between them. Since perfor-
mance spaces are large and complicated, the sound field can vary from seat to seat.
More specifically, for higher frequencies, the reverberation time falls naturally, due to
air and material absorption, whereas in lower frequencies the decay is larger since the
sound propagates easily. Regarding the EDT, we observe that the frequency affects less
the results, but still the effect of higher frequencies is more or less visible. By comparing
the extracted values to some typical ones [26], it is clear that the extracted ones are in
the expected range for opera houses.
Secondly, the clarity metric C80 follows T30 trend in terms of frequency dependence. This
metric appears to improve for higher frequencies, indicating a better speech intelligibility
and music detail, which are important for the current theater, since most performances
in this place are operas and ballet performances. In this case, the audience requires a
clear understanding of the lyrics, followed by an adequate music experience. Further-
more, we also observe a conflict between C80 and JLF. Especially in the seats that are
under the balcony, even though the clarity values belong to the desirable range, the
lateral energy appears to have quite large values. This could lead to excessive lateral
reflections that could potentially blur the clarity and remove precision in both music
and speech.
Moving of to the more advanced analysis methods, the spherical harmonic analysis was
==PAGE:42==
30

used. The flexibility, the simplicity, and the wide application fields helped us analyze
the sound field in a more holistic way, considering also its 3D features. The PIV method
was extensively used, both for identifying the source localization, also for determining
the direction of arrival of signal segments throughout its duration. Its effectiveness was
demonstrated in the source localization part, where minor deviations from the real val-
ues were observed. These deviations could be due to anomalies during the measurement
procedure or the analysis. Regarding the measurement procedure, a few reasons that
could affect the validity of the results are the equipment malfunctions, the deviation
from the ideal measurement position or the existence of background noise. In regards to
the deviations due to the analysis, the elaboration of this thesis showed that the results
can be affected by either the chosen window size or for the truncation of the sound.
Diving into the sound propagation across time, the PIV method analysis helped us ex-
tract hedgehog plots, which ended up being quite informative for both the direction and
the strength of the sound. It was decided to analyze those two aspects for time windows
of 1 ms but depending on the application, different time windows can be considered.
Each of the positions that were chosen, reveals different insights for the room. The plots
for each receiver validate the aforementioned results. For central receiver locations, the
early reflections do not affect the listening experience that much, so the spatial impres-
sion and the intimacy feeling can be shrunken. The contribution of the direct sound
and the early reflections from the ceiling are noticeable but compared to the other two
locations, we could say that the sound can be more poor but with better speech intelli-
gibility. As we move away from the center, the first early reflections start to be more
amplified, enriching the sound, adding the spatial impression and warmth to the music
experience. For receiver positions that are quite close to the side walls though, attention
has to be drawn since at these positions, there is a risk of music and speech blurriness
due to the big amount of reflections appeared.
In summary, several conclusions are drawn from the current analysis. First, we realize
that in such large spaces the listening experience can vary from seat to seat, providing
different attributes. In addition, the acoustic design of the theater appears to provide a
nice balance of the parameters that were analyzed, providing a satisfactory environment
for the audience. Furthermore, it is important to mention that the whole analysis was
based on measurements extracted by only one type of microphone array. Even though it
is suggested to use various types of microphones for certain parameters, it was managed
to convert the responses as if they were obtained from different microphones. This gives
us the power to simplify the equipment of future measurements to only one spherical
sound source and a spherical microphone array (Eigenmike). Lastly, the importance of
this thesis lies in its potential to analyze the acoustics of the theater, without knowing
the exact layout of it.
In conclusion, as for future work, the calculation of the whole list of the acoustic pa-
rameters can be considered so the analysis is more complete. Furthermore, it would be
interesting to make it easy to adapt in any room just by providing the measurements, the
