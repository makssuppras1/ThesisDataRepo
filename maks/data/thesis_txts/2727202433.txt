Abstract
This thesis investigates the application of phase-type distributions in regression and mixed effects models.
It begins by introducing the phase-type distribution and its properties. Then it proceeds to the focus of the
thesis, which is the analysis of regression models with phase-type distributions. Firstly two estimation
methods are presented: the already established maximum likelihood estimation and a new approach using
quasi-likelihood and techniques from generalized linear models. The robustness of the quasi-maximum
likelihood estimation method is assessed by comparing it to maximum likelihood estimation in various
scenarios. Furthermore, the thesis introduces mixed effects models with phase-type distributions, including
an extended quasi-likelihood approach for parameter estimation. A concrete modeling example is provided,
illustrating the application of mixed model phase type regression and exploiting the unique properties of
phase type distributions. Overall, this thesis contributes to the understanding and practical implementation
of phase-type distributions in statistical modeling, utilizing their properties and providing insights into
estimation methods for regression and mixed effects models.

1 Introduction
This thesis focuses on integrating covariates into phase-type distributions, making a phase-type regression
model. This is achieved through an approach similar to a generalized linear model and is later extended
to a mixed regression model, where the regression coefficients are allowed to be random. The primary
objective is to derive methods of estimating parameters of the phase-type regression model and to show
how phase-type regression models can be applied.
Regression analysis is a branch of statistics that aims to uncover the relationship between multiple
predictor variables (covariates) and an outcome variable. This thesis delves into regression models
that treat the outcome variable as a random variable that has a distribution with parameters influenced
by the covariates. A prominent model in this context is the generalized linear model (GLM), proposed
by [Nelder and Wedderburn, 1972]. They assume that the outcome variable, labeled as Y , follows a
distribution within the exponential family, and they model the expected value of Y as a function of the
covariates, formulated as E[Y ] = m(x‚ä§ő≤). Here, x‚ä§is a vector of covariates, while ő≤ is the vector of
parameters we aim to estimate. To estimate ő≤, they propose the iterative weighted least squares method,
which is an iterative algorithm.
In this thesis, we relax the exponential family assumption. Following the research of [Wedderburn, 1974],
we examine estimation methods for scenarios where we possess information about only the mean and
variance of Y rather than its entire distribution. These methods are especially relevant for regression
models with phase-type distributions.
Phase-type distributions, initially conceptualized by [Neuts, 1975], are rooted in continuous-time Markov
chains and Markov jump processes. Their flexibility and compelling properties, such as closure under finite
mixtures and order statistics, have made them popular in applied probability. Parameter estimation for
phase-type distributions is typically done with the EM algorithm, as outlined by [Asmussen et al., 1996],

The bulk of this thesis is about integrating covariates into the phase-type distribution, thereby making
a phase-type regression model.
To integrate covariates, we follow the methodology proposed by
[Albrecher et al., 2022], who also constructed an estimation technique based on maximum likelihood
and the EM algorithm for these models. We propose a novel estimation technique for the phase-type
regression based on quasi-likelihood, which offers some computational advantages. To demonstrate these
advantages, we benchmark the performance of the two estimation methods. We also extend the phase-type
regression model by incorporating random regression coefficients into the regression model. This extension
allows for a broader application of regression with phase-type distributions in various statistical modeling
problems. We finalize the thesis by exemplifying a real-world scenario that takes advantage of some of the
special properties of phase-type distributions.
This thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 delves into Markov jump processes and phase-type
distributions, going through their fundamental properties and characteristics. Chapter 3 presents the
phase type regression model, and the estimation methodology suggested by [Albrecher et al., 2022]. The
chapter then discusses the theory of GLMs and quasi-likelihood. Lastly, quasi-likelihood is applied
for parameter estimation in the phase-type regression model. Chapter 4 is dedicated to evaluating the
performance of quasi-likelihood estimation and comparing it to the maximum likelihood estimation method.
Chapter 5 introduces a mixed model incorporating phase-type distributions, allowing regression coefficients
to be random. And finally, in Chapter 6, we showcase an illustrative example that uses the mixed model.
In that chapter, we also showcase some of the properties of phase-type distributions and how they can be
helpful in an application scenario.

phase-type distributions. Consequently, the parameters of the phase-type distribution also describe an
underlying Markov jump process. After this, we introduce the phase-type distribution and derive its density
and distribution function, as well as its n‚Äôth order moments. Lastly, we describe some of the special
properties of phase-type distribution. Namely: closeness under convolution, finite mixtures, and order
statistics.

iterative weighted least squares estimation algorithm. Here we also touch upon inference in GLMs and
introduce the concept of deviance. Lastly, we propose an alternative way to estimate the parameters in the
phase-type regression using quasi-likelihood and iterative weighted least squares.

Limitations of the QMLE
The quasi-likelihood method also has some drawbacks. Notably, it is less efficient than the maximum
likelihood method, resulting in larger standard errors for ő≤, especially in small sample sizes. However, this
drawback is mitigated by the fact that computing the (MLE) in small sample settings is usually not that
computationally expensive to calculate.
Another limitation of quasi-likelihood is its inability to handle censored data. As demonstrated by
[Olsson, 1996], censored data can be incorporated into the EM algorithm and likelihood function. The
parameterization of quasi-likelihood using mean and variance functions is not well-suited for handling
censored observations. Therefore, the maximum likelihood method should be used when dealing with
censored data like in [Albrecher et al., 2022].

One scenario where this type of mixed model is applicable is when the number of covariates is large
compared to the number of observations, raising concerns about overfitting. A Bayesian statistician may
counteract overfitting by assigning a prior distribution to the coefficients b. If the prior has a mean of zero, it
effectively shrinks the posterior estimates toward zero. This can be seen as a form of soft covariate selection,
where the covariates are not removed from the model, but their coefficient estimates are pulled toward zero,
reducing the risk of overfitting [Hoerl and Kennard, 1970]. However, this approach requires specifying the
prior distribution for b. In our approach, we treat b as a random variable and use the estimated realization
ňÜb when making predictions with the model. Instead of specifying a prior distribution, we estimate őł using
restricted maximum likelihood. Various techniques exist for estimating őł, with cross-validation being a
popular choice. However, [Reiss and Todd Ogden, 2009] demonstrated that cross-validation has worse
finite sample properties than restricted maximum likelihood. By including both ő≤ and b in the model, we
allow for including both penalized and unpenalized regression coefficients.

8 Conclusions
In conclusion, this thesis has thoroughly explored various aspects of phase-type distributions and their
applications in regression modeling.
Chapter Three demonstrated the methodology of generalized linear models for estimating parameters
in phase-type regression. It introduced the quasi-likelihood function, approximating the real likelihood
function and making parameter estimation more manageable by breaking it into two parts. First, estimating ő≤
using quasi-likelihood, and second, estimating T and ŌÄ using the EM algorithm. Although quasi-likelihood
is an approximation, it retains the asymptotic properties of a likelihood function. This property enables
inference on the QMLE of ő≤.
The fourth chapter discussed the robustness of quasi-maximum likelihood estimation. It investigated
the behavior of QMLE under various circumstances, such as different sample sizes, different transition
matrices T, different functions m(¬∑), and varying the number of covariates. The chapter also compared
quasi-maximum and maximum likelihood estimations, including a comparison of their computational
efficiency. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of QMLE, showing that it, in most cases, provides
similar results to MLE for large enough samples while being computationally faster to estimate. We also
saw that the QML method had a disadvantage to maximum likelihood when dealing with extreme data.
Additionally, the QMLE method allows for inference on the model without the need to estimate ŌÄ and T.
The fifth chapter extended the methodology to mixed effects models with phase-type distributions. It
introduced the concept of mixed effects models, where regression coefficients are split into fixed effects
and random effects. The chapter derived the restricted quasi-likelihood of the mixed model using an
extended quasi-likelihood and a Laplace approximation. In Chapter Six, we showed a concrete example of
the application of the phase-type mixed model and demonstrated some useful properties of phase-type
distributions in real-world scenarios.
