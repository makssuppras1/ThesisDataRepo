Abstract 
   This study provides a methodology considering a two-dimensional approach of each mode 
(i.e., travel time and travel budget) utilized to calculate the probability of each mode alternative 
based on multinomial logit and simulated through system dynamics. By changing the 
parameters of the modes, their utility is expected to change, leading to the generation of modal 
shift. This will allow the formulation of several scenarios, which in turn are going to help 
approach the target set by Denmark to become fossil-free by year 2050. The model is capable 
of considering spatial disaggregation and scenario analysis, both being studied within 
Denmark.  
   The scenarios that have been analyzed in this study include the development of a new metro 
line operating within the city of Copenhagen, expected to reduce travel times and improve the 
offered level of service, thereby leading to reduced intangible costs for the metro and an 
increased utility compared to other alternative modes. Decreasing the ticket price of public 
transit leads to a decrease of their associated tangible costs and an increase in the utility of 
public modes compared to other modes. Increasing the taxation scheme of fuel prices affects 
the tangible cost of private mode and decreases the utility of private cars. The investment on 
public transit infrastructure and level of service of the existing public fleet may lead to reduced 
travel times, which affect the intangible cost of public modes. In addition, increasing the 
registration and ownership tax of private cars affects the tangible cost of private modes and 
decreases the utility of private cars.

1. Introduction 
   As the trend for urbanization grows and people all around the globe show the tendency to 
move into urban centers, the need for improving public transportation is constantly arising. 
This is no less due to the fact that the transportation sector contributes significantly to the 
worldwide energy consumption, at a level of 25.7% of global energy use (IEA, 2014). In 
addition to that, 92% of the energy used in the transportation sector comes from oil, despite the 
steadily increasing use of electricity during recent years. Therefore, there is a justified cause 
for concern for improving the sustainability in transportation. The European Union, for 
example, is considering transport one of its major strategic policies at the moment (Barisa, 
2016) and aims at directing all member states towards an increased use of renewable energy 
sources. In particular, all member states are required to increase the renewable share of energy 
in the transport sector to 10% by 2020 (European Parliament, 2009). In the case of Denmark, 
the travel demand is increasing steadily and private modes have a large share, accounting for 
over 76% of total inland demand, according to the latest statistics regarding the year of 2015 
(Statistics Denmark, 2015). Additionally, transportation is responsible for 25% of CO2 
emissions in Denmark, 60% of which attributed to passenger vehicles (Winther, 2015). 
Therefore, in order to achieve the required level, the use of private cars needs to be reduced 
and new policies that support and improve the level of service of public transportation services 
would have to be implemented. Hammadou and Papaix, (2015) also mention this link between 
urban transport and climate policies. 
   Simulating the preferences of commuters is a complex task. This complexity arises due to 
the heterogeneity of travelers, their decision-making regarding the modes of transport and the 
characteristics of the trips. A different number of parameters, including behavioral traits, their 
personal financial situation, their perception and expectations on each mode and many other 
unobserved factors are able to affect their choice. In order to properly monitor those choices 
and their preferred mode of commuting during their activities, a deeper study in modal shift is 
required. Obviously, modeling this complex task requires a sophisticated tool and an extensive 
methodology. The tool should be capable of identifying which factors affect the travelers’ 
modal choices and analyze the sensitivity of these factors on the available modes of transport. 
Moreover, developing alternative policy scenarios and relevant analyses will assist on how to 
incentivize people to shift from private cars to more sustainable modes of transport. 
==PAGE:8==
2 
 
   The purpose of this study is to develop a model that is able to analyze the modal choice of 
inland transportation within the country of Denmark. In this model, the System Dynamics 
approach is combined with the widely known and preferred discrete choice model, the 
Multinomial Logit function. The model is capable of forecasting the modal share that is taking 
place within this specific transportation system for the modeling horizon of 2010 to 2050. 
Moreover, a set of policy scenarios have been developed and analyzed. Using this model, it is 
possible to analyze the impact of future car technologies, whether they create new opportunities 
or not and if so, how can those opportunities be exploited. In addition to that, the model aims 
in facilitating the integration of traffic modeling in existing energy modeling frameworks, 
which in turn have the goal of assisting Denmark in achieving its target of becoming free of 
fossil-fuels by the year of 2050. By providing an optimized method for analyzing the existing 
and future traffic conditions, the results of this model can be inserted into those energy 
modeling frameworks, through which, an energy-wise optimal share of vehicle fleets is 
produced. In addition, the reasons why people appear to be using their private cars more often 
are identified and as a result, the possible incentives that could be provided in order to shift 
commuters towards public modes are explored. 
   The main driver for developing this model are tangible and intangible costs included in the 
transportation sector, since nowadays, they appear to be significantly influential in the 
decision-making process of commuters on how they are conducting their trips. For instance, 
the competition between faster and more expensive modes (e.g. car) with slower but cheaper 
modes (e.g. bus or rail) is ensured by evaluating the utilities of each mode based on the income 
category and expectations of the traveler. Additionally, by introducing costs that operate on 
multiple layers, policy analyses can be conducted for both taxation schemes, as well as 
regulations regarding the existing infrastructure and public fare pricing. This study is also 
accounting for emerging technologies within the private car sector and is also able to consider 
the potential development for future infrastructure on a detailed disaggregated geographical 
level. Finally, in order to test the sensitivity of this model on these changes regarding costs, 
additional scenarios have been conducted and their results have been carefully scrutinized to 
analyze the effects of those new policies and their influence on modal share. 
   The remainder of this thesis is arranged as follows: Section 2 introduces the materials and 
methodology. Section 3 presents the results while Section 4 discusses the results and 
concluding remarks are provided in Section 5.

1.1  Literature review 
1.1.1 Modal Share 
   By definition, a modal share is the percentage of passengers that use a particular type of 
transportation or the number of trips that are conducted through the use of that particular mode 
of transport. While it is mainly defined as a concept of passenger transportation, modal share 
is also measured in freight transportation, calculated through the mass of products that are 
transported. It constitutes an important concept for the development of sustainable transport, 
since it is the main way to set targets that correspond to this development. In order to achieve 
those goals, a necessary modal shift needs to take place, where the change between alternative 
modes of transport can be either reinforced or discouraged, depending on the desired end goal. 
However, these changes have to operate on multiple layers within a specific transportation 
system. 
   A transportation system is considered to be comprised of three elements: Vehicles or 
equipment that move people or goods (objects), guideways or what the vehicles move along 
and an operation plan or set of procedures by which objects and vehicles are moved over 
guideways (Boyce, 2005). However, as Barisa, (2016) mentions, users were so far excluded 
from this system. Taking into accounting the complexity of their decision-making and the 
different factors that drive them in choosing their preferred mode of transportation, they should 
be also represented in this whole system as an integral part of it. It is worth mentioning that the 
main focus in modal shift is aimed towards users, since freight transportation is more 
constrained, depending on the market trends and policies that are in effect (Baindur and Viegas, 
2011). 
   The transportation sector contributes significantly in the energy consumption nowadays. 
According to Novosel et al. (2015), it contributed 32.8 % of the total energy consumption in 
Croatia for the year of 2011 and due to the recent economic growth and investments in 
infrastructure, the energy demand for transportation has increased by 70% in the last decade 
(Novosel et al., 2015). Another example is the case of France, where 40% of the total CO2 
emissions in 2009 were produced from the transportation sector, 80% of those coming from 
road transport (Hammadou and Papaix, 2015). Therefore, the call for energy savings is urgent. 
However, in order to implement new policies and plan for future scenarios, where the increased 
use of renewable energy sources is suggested, the behavioral aspects and modal choices of 
users need to be forecasted. This can be achieved through proper modeling in modal shift, 
==PAGE:10==
4 
 
especially in road transport, since the majority of distances that are traveled are short distances 
(usually trips from home to work and then back to home). 
   As Hammadou and Papaix (2015) suggested in their study, the factors that affect modal 
choice are the existing infrastructure available to the public, socio-demographic factors and the 
use of policy tools. As far infrastructure is concerned, users are more willing to use public 
means of transportation for their daily commuting, as long as they find the level of service 
acceptable. This includes clean buses and/or trains, feelings of safety and comfort and constant 
departure and arrival times that are consistently specific in time, being neither early nor late. 
Additionally, Hensher (1998) suggests that there is a common misconception concerning 
infrastructure, where people believe that better roads means more cars. This is not entirely true 
however, since an improved traffic network has the possibility to enhance public means as well. 
   Socio-demographic factors are more complex and various in their nature, therefore there 
needs to be extra care when taking them into account and attempting to model them. According 
to Schafer and Victor (2000), there is a global trend concerning the travel time that users wish 
to spend, as well as the budget they are willing to commit for transportation. These two 
concepts are presented as Travel-Time Budget (TTB) and Travel-Money Budget (TMB) 
respectively. Their study also states that every person is willing to spend 1.1 hour/day on 
average for commuting, a percentage that is larger in larger, dense urban areas, where the 
number of small trips to various destinations is increased. The same study also suggests that 
transportation costs constitute a small fraction of a household’s total budget, approximately 3-
5% for households that do not own a personal car. However, as the level of income rises, users 
shift to faster modes of transportation, aiming to increase their mobility and that leads to the 
fact that wealthier societies have been linked to increased mobility levels as well (Schafer and 
Victor, 2000). The panel study that was conducted by Thøgersen (2005) suggests that according 
to the theory of planned behavior, users usually make their choices according to past habits, 
opportunities and car ownership, a fact which enhances the use of private cars. The study also 
suggests that people who do not own cars demonstrate a stronger relationship between their 
attitude and behavior, mostly because they have fewer alternatives to choose from. On the 
contrary, the owner of a car is always able to choose whether to commute with it or not.  
Another study, conducted by Guiver (2006) demonstrates the different opinions expressed for 
public buses and cars. In general, public buses received a fair amount of negative comments, 
most of them referring to episodes of poor performance. Some users were feeling vulnerable 
to other users and not comfortable enough, while also feeling that traveling by bus resulted in 
==PAGE:11==
5 
 
longer trips than what they were wishing for. However, many users also appeared to prefer this 
mode and this choice was attributed to reduced anxiety levels compared to driving their own 
cars and the ability to enjoy the view out of the windows. Public buses were also considered as 
the most accessible way of commuting, providing their services to persons of low income as 
well. Thus, those advantages provided by public buses should be prioritized, in order to create 
a shift from private cars. On the other hand, the comments concerning private cars were 
referring to a safer and more comfortable and private way of commuting but those that did not 
own one were also considering them a major source of pollution and the main contributors to 
traffic congestion. At this point, it is also worth mentioning that a significant number of car 
owners enjoy driving as an activity on its own and consider their cars to be status elevators that 
improve their societal image (Mehbub Anwar, 2009). This might also be the reason why the 
majority of the population appeared more eager to accept congestion charging rather than an 
increase in parking fees (Hammadou and Papaix, 2015). This conclusion hints at the fact that 
aggressive policies against private cars might prove insufficient, compared to schemes that 
elevate the service provided by public means. Hammadou and Papaix (2015) who pointed out 
a number of various interesting facts also highlight the impact of social factors that affect modal 
choice: Students were strongly favoring public means of transportation, while walking 
activities were largely influenced by age, taking individual physical conditions into account. 
Blue-collar workers were more likely to own and use a car, mostly because they had to resort 
in living in suburban areas, located far from their work spots and families without children 
tended to also favor public means. An interesting fact pointed out by Beirão and Sarsfield 
Cabral (2007) is that many users in their study in Portugal appeared to have positive feelings 
towards the light rail, seeing it as a very convenient and “fun” means of daily commuting. 
   Policies also appear to have a significant impact in modal shift. However, the users’ reactions 
vary per each case specifically and therefore extra attention should be paid when planning and 
implementing new measures that affect public transportation. Kafashi et al. (2016) found out 
that increased price policies in Malaysia shifted users away from private cars and the only 
barrier that prevents more people from using public means was the level of service, which 
resulted in longer trip durations. Paulley at al. (2006) conducted a survey concerning fares in 
public buses and trains for the case of England, seeing as this is a major source of income for 
improving the existing infrastructure and level of service. The results showed that users were 
less eager to accept increased fares for non-work trips and in lines that were serving rural areas, 
where the use of private car is more convenient. Therefore, the use of public means could be 
==PAGE:12==
6 
 
possibly reinforced by increasing fares only in busy, urban and suburban areas. Beirão and 
Sarsfield Cabral (2007) suggested that new policies in Portugal should be more competitive 
and market oriented. In addition, the public should be informed about the benefits and 
opportunities bound to appear by using less private cars and the need to concern more for the 
environment should be stressed to incentivize the users. Martin and Shaheen (2014) 
investigated an innovative bike-sharing method in the United States. The results indicated that 
this enhanced use of public bikes reduced the use of private cars in both Washington DC and 
Minneapolis. This shift was enhanced in the case of dense, core urban areas, while also serving 
as a connection to the public network in rural areas, where it was also embraced positively. 
1.1.2 Modeling Tools 
   The first tools used to model modal shift in transportation were discrete choice models, 
providing the statistical background on how users were selecting their mode for commuting. 
Koppelman and Bhat (2006) provided a thorough study on the various discrete choice models, 
as well as their evolved forms. The first type of model was the Multinomial Logit model 
(MNL), were the errors are extreme-value distributed, identically and independently distributed 
across alternatives and across observations and individuals. This model suffered from the 
limitations imposed by the Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives (IIA) property, which 
prevented the prediction of behavioral relationships among all the alternatives that were 
considered, leading to errors in the predictions. In order to combat this challenge, the Nested 
logit models (NLMs) were developed, where group subsets (or nests) of alternatives that are 
more similar to each other were assumed to share common components in their random error 
terms (Koppelman and Bhat, 2006). 
   Recent advanced discrete choice models are the General Extreme Value (GEV) models, 
derived by McFadden (1978) and generalized by Ben-Akiva and Francois (1983), which relax 
the independence assumption between the error terms of alternatives. Another alternative is the 
mixed multinomial logit models (MMNL) class of models, first applied by Revelt and Train 
(1980) and Cardell and Dunbar (1980). This class includes an analytically intractable 
integration of the multinomial logit formula. The last class mentioned are the mixed GEV class 
of models, which involve multidimensional integration of the order of random coefficients, 
bringing it to the order of 1000 (Koppelman and Bhat, 2006). Zou et al. (2015) used discrete 
choice models, namely MNL and nested logit models, in order to approach travel behavior 
issues in the case of Beijing, China. Their aim was to focus on the behavior of users in 
developing countries, rather than developed ones, for which there is already a vast amount of 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
literature. After reviewing the available methods and taking into account the timeframe 
required for this study as well as the complexity of calculations necessary to produce a discreet 
choice model, the multinomial Logit function was deemed appropriate as the basis of the 
model. 
   Not all the aforementioned models however were sufficient to model the complex behavior 
that users demonstrate, when selecting their preferred mode of transportation. As stated by Zou 
et al. (2015) travelers try to search for alternative departure times and travel modes before 
deciding to make a trip. Their decisions are based on the expected gain and cost included. In 
addition to that, users are able to sense their surrounding environment and interact with it, 
exhibiting proactive reactions and social behavior. They set goals, interact with other users and 
are able to respond to changes imposed. In order to properly conduct simulations, the users 
need to be identified as “agents” and microscopic, per-entity modeling is required (Novosel et 
al., 2015). These models are called Agent Based Models (ABM). 
   According to Abbas (2016), ABM is a bottom-up approach and is characterized by the 
agents’ ability to adapt and anticipate the future. ABM simulation is best applied when 
interactions between agents are complex, nonlinear, discontinuous or discrete, while also 
exhibiting learning and adaptation traits on these agents. One of the most widely used tools for 
ABM is MATSim (Multi-Agent Transport Simulation). This framework was developed jointly 
at TU Berlin, ETH Zurich and Senozon Company. It is capable of simulating millions of agents 
or huge, complex networks. In MATSim, each agent is assigned to at least one plan, which is 
a series of activities and connected trips, which act as the “mental layer” of the simulation. 
These in turn are submitted to the traffic flow simulation, which tries the optimal assignment 
under the physical constraints caused by the agents, creating the “physical layer”. The utility 
value of every plan generated is the score and in this simulation, every agent attempt to 
optimize its plan to get a larger score. Therefore, the traffic network and facilities act as the 
supply, while the population and the available daily travel plans act as the demand (Abbas, 
2016). Novosel et al. (2015) state that the input inserted in MATSim should rely on a conducted 
survey among a limited number of people. In their study, they represented agents as the product 
of the total number of employees to the percentage of employees having personal vehicles as 
their mode of transport. Origin-destination (O-D) matrices were also used, where rows 
represented probabilities of work in municipalities and the sum of columns represented the 
total number of registered businesses for each municipality. The results obtained from 
MATSim are time series of kilometers that vehicles travel each day of the week. Hager et al. 
==PAGE:14==
8 
 
(2015) applied simulations utilizing MATSim, in order to come up with different scenarios for 
the Stuttgart metropolitan area. 
   Another known tool for conducting ABM modeling is TRANSMIMS (Transportation 
Analysis Simulation System). It is a set of new transportation and air quality analysis and 
forecasting procedures and consists of mutually supporting simulations, models and databases 
that create integrated regional transportation systems analysis through advanced and analytical 
techniques. The input data consists of land-use data, census data, vehicle population, traveler 
activity survey, transportation network and itinerant travel-trip table. The first module is the 
population synthesizer module, which then feeds the processed data into the activity generator 
module. The generated activities are then inserted into the route planner module and then the 
traffic micro-simulator module which leads into the final stage, being the emissions estimator 
module (Lu, 2002). Suzumura and Kanezashi (2004) also suggested a highly scalable, multi-
model traffic simulation platform (M3) on parallel and distributed environments for the case 
of Dublin, Ireland. The available platforms considered were MATSim, VISSIM and SUMO. 
The planned input was a series of trips or individual per travel trajectories through a journey 
planner external component. The expected results are average travel time and CO2 emissions. 
   Lastly, the energy contribution aspect of the transportation sector has been taken into account 
in a number of studies, mainly due to the sector’s large contribution to the global energy 
demand. Novosel et al. (2015) modeled the hourly distribution of the transport energy demand, 
utilizing that data to analyze the impact that personal electric vehicles (EVs) can have in a 
future, more sustainable energy system. The energy system was modeled through 
EnergyPLAN and the transportation system through MATSim. The EnergyPLAN software is 
a deterministic input output computer modeling tool that produces energy balances, annual and 
hourly energy production by source and the CEEP (Critical Excess of Electricity Production). 
Daly et al. (2012) also stated the lack of proper transportation modeling in 3E 
(energy/economy/environment) models. For that reason, they employed the TIMES model, 
where the motorized travel demand is expressed in PMT (person miles traveled), constituting 
of PTML (long) and PTMS (short). It is also worth mentioning that the transport sector in 
TIMES comprises a stock of technologies that serve motorized travel demands. These two 
studies have shown an effort to integrate agent-based modeling in the energy sector (Novosel 
et al.) and an attempt to model travel behavior and travel time in TIMES (Daly et al.). However, 
an extensive approach is required to properly model a complex system such as the 
transportation sector.

1.1.3 System Dynamics 
   Problems in the transportation sector are holistic. This means that feedback loops are 
involved, as well as counterintuitive behavior (Abbas and Bell, 1991). This holistic approach 
is something that cannot be easily simulated using the tools and methodologies that were 
mentioned in the previous sections. This is why System Dynamics (SD), need to be introduced, 
when dealing with issues and alternative scenarios in transportation. According to Abbas and 
Bell (1991), system dynamics were originally called industrial dynamics. Developed by the 
Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), it is a methodology rooted in system theory, 
cybernetics, information science, organizational theory, feedback control theory, military 
games and tactical decision-making. System dynamics is a qualitative description, exploration 
and analysis of complex systems and is able to simulate behavior through computer simulation. 
It is comprised of three elements. The first are rates, which are essentially streams of actions, 
decisions or activities. The second are levels, which describe the current state of the system 
and the last element are auxiliaries. These are algebraic or integral calculations that aid in 
capturing necessary information for rates. The sources that provide information for a system 
dynamics simulation are personal observations, literature search, theoretical knowledge, other 
existing models, facts, assumptions, data, heuristics and deterministic specifications (Abbas 
and Bell, 1991). Flynn at al. 2014 employed system dynamics, along with agent-based 
modeling and discrete choice modeling in order to investigate a set of problems in a university 
that trains optologists. Since these problems were grounded in the whole professional career 
system, a holistic approach was required, simulating the ever-changing environment that 
surrounds the system that was focused.  
   In a SD simulation, the system is represented by stocks, flows and endogenous feedback 
loops that indicate changes taking place in it (Flynn et al., 2014). The need to incorporate SD 
in transportation sector studies comes from a number of different factors (Abbas and Bell, 
1991): 
 Human error in data collection 
 Human behavior which is hard to model 
 Uncertainty for the future 
 Provided feedback for the interaction between the transportation sector and other sectors 
 Alternative policies or structured assumptions incorporated 
==PAGE:16==
10 
 
   MacMillan et al. (2014) characterized the interaction between variables by reinforcing 
(positive) loops, which amplify patterns of behavior and balancing (negative) loops, which are 
used to dampen these patterns.  Wang et al. (2007) employed SD in the case of Dalian central 
city in China while studying a flexible restriction policy on vehicle developing, which will 
allow the reasonable increase in GDP and population. Azatuliffah and Riza (2013) pointed out 
that SD provide a better understanding for future analysis, due to its iterative nature. It also 
aims to a better understanding of the structure of a system’s behavior. Doll et al. (2010) applied 
a SD model to study alternative levels of road user charges for long and heavy vehicles (LHVs). 
This application was able to ease the negative impacts of the LHVs’ introduction on the railway 
market share. Bernardino and van der Hoofd (2013) formed a SD framework for improved 
parking policies. Liu et al. (2014) analyzed the motorization process in China and employed 
SD to simulate CO2 emissions for the next 15 years. Vrat (2010) used SD to discuss about the 
containment of greenhouse gases (GHG) in the transportation sector. Finally, Aschauer (2013) 
employed SD to examine the interdependencies between logistics strategies and freight 
transport. These models served as a preliminary inspiration on how the system dynamics 
framework for the national transportation system of Denmark should be developed. 
   However, Abbas and Bell (1991) mention the limitations that system dynamics has as a 
methodology. First of all, only the time dimension is taken into account, meaning that spatial 
aspects are not easily modeled. Additionally, SD models are aggregated models that show the 
impact of different policies, meaning that additional detail is needed in order to be more 
accurate numerically. Since SD incorporates randomness and stochasticity, uncertainties are 
also possible to arise. Concluding, SD models are hard to be checked for their validity. This is 
due to the fact that their results are validated according to how useful their impact was and not 
based on numerical exactness. Therefore, there is no sufficient literature to account for their 
validity and each case has to be checked on its own, in order to obtain feedback concerning the 
SD model that was employed. 
   There are various tools that are used in order to employ system dynamics. Wang et al. (2007) 
used the Vensim PLE software for their study in Dalian central city, China. MacMIllan et al. 
(2014) employed the STELLA software to analyze the societal benefits of commuter bicycling. 
This study suggests the use of the AnyLogic software (Anylogic n. d.) for use in the analysis 
of the Danish transportation system. AnyLogic has a user-friendly platform that is able to 
operate on different levels of feedback loops, including various statistical distributions in the 
analysis of interactions. Using AnyLogic makes possible to combine discrete choice models 
==PAGE:17==
11 
 
with system dynamics, formulating a tool which is capable of conceptualizing the commuters’ 
decisions and the creation of different scenarios. Various companies are already employing the 
AnyLogic software for a number of transportation issues, such as comparing future railway 
models with existing ones, maximizing boat voyage revenues, developing solutions for railway 
infrastructures, implementing bus itinerary applications for commuters, traffic network 
planning and monitoring traffic flow and safety. 
   Fifty-one papers, articles, websites and surveys were selected and examined, concerning 
modal shift in transportation and the various tools and methods employed to analyze it. A 
significant number of them are describing case studies were various simulations were 
conducted, concerning either existing or future networks or policies that are examined for 
implementation. The goal is to provide a framework on how system dynamics can be combined 
with discrete choice modeling in the transport sector in order to analyze the commuters’ mode 
selection criteria. 
   Literature on system dynamics in the transport sector is rare and that is due to the difficulty 
in obtaining a quantitative evaluation of its results. Being an aggregate methodology, its 
validity lies on the impact of usefulness in each study where it was employed. Therefore, there 
are no sufficient criteria for validating its exact numerical results. Concerning its application, 
there are some limitations that need to be taken into account: 
 Only the time dimension is considered 
 Uncertainty is still existent 
 Additional care in evaluating numerical results 
 No sufficient literature to evaluate the method 
   Having the above in mind, it is possible to proceed with the application of system dynamics 
in mode choice probability in the transportation sector, forming a framework that will aid in 
modeling the existing network. It will also allow to examine future policies, taking into account 
two dimensions (i.e., tangible and intangible costs) of each mode. This way, future scenarios 
can be created, aiming in raising stakeholder awareness.

2. Methodology 
   System Dynamics were primarily employed, since the transportation sector can be regarded 
as an ever-evolving system, with increasing complexities, as more technologies are bound to 
be introduced. In addition to that, many existing technologies are going to be withdrawn in the 
future as redundant. This is certainly going to create new opportunities for the various modal 
choices that are being examined and alter the decision-making process of users in the market. 
In order to properly model the effect of the aforementioned shifts, as well as account for the 
increasing demand in the market, created by the ever-growing population, System Dynamics 
are chosen as the main focus of this study.  
   Due to the active and evolving entrepreneurship in the transportation sector, the emerging 
opportunities from new technologies and the global financial crisis that is becoming a concern 
for the users, the most influential factors in modal choice are costs. From the user’s point of 
view, these costs may represent the capital required to buy and register a new private car, 
maintain an existing car that is owned or simply the cost to buy a ticket for a public mode. For 
the public sector that operates the relevant legislation and policies, the costs of interest may be 
the fuel prices that may or may not incentivize the increased use of public modes, public fares 
and the taxes that are associated with every mode. In this study, all these costs were named 
tangible costs and they are related to direct pricing and payment. 
   In addition to the tangible costs, there is also the concept of value of time, as it has been 
already mentioned by Schäfer and Victor (2000). For example, walking or biking to the chosen 
destination may appear very attractive to a user, since it is almost completely free (including 
only minor costs of buying and maintaining a bicycle). However, travel speeds associated with 
those choices are considerably slower. Thus, it is more likely to lead to an increased travel 
time. Depending on the purpose of the trip, time gains a different value and this value is also 
affected by the user’s income. Being a significantly influential factor in the choice of mode, 
the value of time needs to be examined as well and it is incorporated in this study in the form 
of intangible costs, which are assigned to every available mode. 
   Despite their importance, costs alone are not enough to account for the choice of mode. Users 
are regarded as rational agents and their choice of how to conduct each specific trip is mainly 
independent from choices made by other users. However, acting as agents in a dynamic system, 
they are able to exchange information and feedback, which can alter their decision-making 
process in several occasions. Therefore, their behavioral aspects and traits needed to be taken 
==PAGE:21==
15 
 
into consideration when modeling the transportation system’s dynamics. The tool that was 
employed in this study, in order to properly account for those behavioral traits was the 
Multinomial Logit Function. 
2.1 Logit 
   The Logit function was incorporated into System Dynamics, in order to simulate the users’ 
behavior that is demonstrated in modal choice. As Train (2003) suggests, Logit is the most 
widely used discrete choice model and the main reasons for its popularity are its closed form 
over the examined alternatives, as well as its easy interpretation. During this study, the decision 
makers are considered all persons that comprise the population of Denmark (from here on 
called users) over the period between 2010 and 2050 and the available alternatives are all 
available modes that can be selected when inland passenger transportation is conducted. As a 
discrete choice model, there are some restrictions that need to be taken into account when 
employing it. First and foremost, the alternatives that are studied have to be mutually exclusive, 
meaning that the choice of one alternative implies that no other alternative was chosen. 
Furthermore, they need to be exhaustive, accounting for every option available to the user. 
Finally, they need to be finite, meaning that can they should be able to be counted. Being a 
behavioral model, Logit is based on the hypothesis that the decision maker is examining the 
available options from this finite set of alternatives. When considering the use of Logit, traits 
need to be assigned to both the attributes and decision makers and these traits are essentially 
used in order to produce the distribution of modal choice. 
   Briefly mentioning the main characteristics employed in the multinomial Logit function, the 
traits of particular interest to the researcher are identified by the representative utility Vni and 
the unobserved factors εni. Later on in this chapter, these concepts are introduced in the 
particular model and the method on how they are describing the transportation system in 
Denmark is presented and analyzed. 
   The popularity of Logit stems from the assumption that εni is independently and identically 
distributed extreme value for all i. Essentially, this means that the unobserved factors 
considered in the study are considered uncorrelated and as having the same variance for all 
alternatives. While this assumption can prove significantly restrictive for many realistic cases, 
it also leads to much more convenient and faster calculations, where the modelling of optimal 
choice selection is not hampered and can still represent real behavior. However, this restriction 
is also the reason why this model is unable to capture some preferences that are expressed by 
==PAGE:22==
16 
 
the examined decision makers, such as the desire to use walking/running as much as possible 
in daily or weekend trips, for fitness reasons, over specific time intervals of the year. 
2.2 Material 
2.2.1 LTM 
   The data input that was employed in the model was obtained by the Landstrafikmodellen 
(LTM) study, the Transportvaneundersøgelsen (TU) survey and the TIMES modeling 
framework. LTM (Rich, 2015) short for Landstrafikmodellen, is the new national Danish 
transport model. It is a 4-step transportation simulation model, which covers all trips produced 
in Denmark, in addition to foreign trips that use Denmark as a transit country. Following the 
political agreement “A Green Transport policy” from 2009, the model was developed in order 
to strengthen the basis for planning in the transportation sector. Thereby, following in the 
footsteps of Holland, Great Britain, Sweden and Norway, Denmark now has a national traffic 
model system, covering the needs for improvement in the decision-making basis. The 
interpretation of LTM’s results and the understanding of its limitations requires professional 
knowledge and experience; hence, proper use of the model is achieved after participation in 
both theoretical and practical courses. 
2.2.2 TU Survey 
   TU is short for Transportvaneundersøgelsen and has the basic aim of surveying the transport 
behavior of the population that is residing in Denmark. DTU Transport, on behalf of a number 
of Danish authorities and organizations, which are funding the program, manages it. The third 
generation of TU provides data for the years 2006-2015 and the objective of the survey is to 
answer the question of how much, how, where, when and why do people travel in Denmark. 
The uniqueness of the TU survey in both a national and an international perspective relies on 
its reasonably consistent results from 1992 to the present day, the large quantity of data 
included, its role as the primary source of knowledge regarding the way Danish residents travel 
and the definition of how individuals and transport behavior are related. 
2.2.3 TIMES-DK 
   The TIMES (The Integrated MARKAL-EFOM System) model generator was developed as 
part of the IEA-ETSAP’s methodology for energy scenarios to conduct in depth energy and 
environmental analyses (Loulou et al., 2005). All TIMES models are constructed from the three 
basic commodities of Technologies (representations of physical devices), Commodities (such 
as fuels, monetary flows and emissions), Commodity Flows and Scenarios. Its counterpart used 
==PAGE:23==
17 
 
in the Danish energy sector is TIMES-DK, which acts as the first system model that is able to 
cover the whole national energy system of Denmark, as well as investments in the period that 
is being modeled and simulated. TIMES-DK was developed in cooperation with the Danish 
Energy Agency (DEA), the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and Energy Engineering 
Economic Environment Systems Modeling and Analysis (E4SMA). As a tool, it is suitable for 
conducting medium- and long-term analysis and the modeling horizon that it covers are the 
years 2010-2050. 
   The reason why the period of years 2010-2050 was chosen was to properly align the results 
of the model in accordance with results produced by other models that were employed in 
cooperation with the TIMES modeling framework. In that way, a comparison and exchange of 
feedback between the different approaches is able to take place, resulting in the more efficient 
integration of travel behavior in energy models regarding transportation. This study breaks 
down that period into 5-year intervals and milestones. Therefore, there is the milestone year of 
2010, 2012, 2015, 2020 and so on until 2050. All technologies that are considered to enter or 
exit the market are thus introduced in these milestone years and the same process applies to 
any considered changes in the availability of modes or development of new infrastructure. 
2.3 AnyLogic 
   The model is constructed for the Danish transport system in AnyLogic [version 8.1.0, 
University] software (http://www.anylogic.com). AnyLogic is a user-friendly platform and its 
SD module is able to operate on different levels of feedback loops, including various statistical 
distributions in the analysis of interactions (Grigoryev I., 2014). It is a modeling and simulation 
tool based on a Java platform and includes a code generator and a graphical editor. The system 
is then depicted as a combination of stocks and flows. AnyLogic was used to model variety of 
problems such as pedestrian modeling (Liu et al, 2014) reliability evaluation of Integrated 
Energy Systems (Li et al., 2016) development of a Smart Grid (Delamare et al., 2015) 
congestion detection of pedestrians in crowd disasters (Huang et al. 2015) modeling of 
pedestrian behavior (Shaaban and Abdel-Warith, 2017). In particular it is used for a number of 
issues such as transportation, comparing future railway models with existing ones, maximizing 
boat voyage revenues, developing solutions for railway infrastructures, implementing bus 
itinerary applications for commuters, traffic network planning and monitoring traffic flow and 
safety (AnyLogic, n.d.).

2.4 Model Description 
   Six regions are represented in the model: based on the Great Belt Corridor, the country is 
divided to Denmark-East (DKE) and Denmark-West (DKW). Each one of those two regions is 
subsequently divided to urban (U), suburban (S) and rural (R) urbanization types. As the final 
layer of disaggregation, those 6 regions are divided into 4 areas each, corresponding to the 
various levels of income. These levels of income are low (L), medium (M), high (H) and very 
high (VH). Thus, the national annual demand for Denmark in every year is represented by 24 
groups, which are aggregated when calculating the total demand in this model. The base year 
of the model is 2010 and the simulation horizon is 2050. The Danish National Travel 
Survey (TU), which is an interview survey that documented the travel behavior of the Danish 
population, is used to capture the socio-economic characteristics of commuters by urbanization 
type. The annual demand is taken from Danish national transport model, LTM, which is a four-
stage simulation transport model of Denmark (Rich, 2015). The demand is disaggregated on 
urbanization type and aggregated on all modes of transport. The annual Danish population 
projections are taken from Statistics Denmark. The travelers are grouped based on income 
category corresponding to low, medium, high and very high income.  
   Modal choice in the model is driven by costs, both tangible and intangible. Tangible costs 
include the cost to buy and register a new private car, maintain an existing car, fuel prices, 
public transport ticket prices and any mode-specific taxes or subsidies. Intangible costs include 
in-vehicle time, waiting time, public transport access and egress time and the level of 
congestion.  The concept of value of time is a significant factor for calculating the intangible 
cost (Schäfer and Victor, 2000). For example, walking or biking have low tangible costs, but 
higher intangible costs due to time. The value of time is dependent on the purpose of the trip 
and the user’s income level. In this study, value of time is taken from TU survey and grouped 
in four categories based on households’ income. Table 2-1 presents the seven modes of 
transport examined in this study, their geographic availability, their tangible and intangible

2.4.2 Logit formulation 
   As it has been already mentioned, Logit is the most widely used discrete choice model and 
the main reasons for its popularity are its closed form over the examined alternatives, as well 
as its easy interpretation (Train, 2003). Being a behavioral model, it is based on the hypothesis 
that a modeled user choses the options (from a finite number of alternatives that maximizes the 
total utility. For the decision maker n, the utility of alternative i, belonging in a set of j 
alternatives, is symbolized by Uni. Thereby, the decision maker will choose the alternative i, 
only if Uni > Unj, for every j≠i. 
   The utility Uni is mainly composed of the representative utility Vni. The representative utility 
contains all the attributes of interest to the researcher. For this study, different modes 
(alternatives) and attributes of the users (e.g., income) were examined. However, the users’ 
choices are not perfectly defined by the selected variables due to unobserved factors, resulting 
in a difference between the representative utility Vni and the actual utility Uni, symbolized as 
εni, εni = Uni-Vni. This quantity represents the uncertainty associated with the relationship 
between the behavioral traits displayed by the users and their actual choices. The total cost

2.4.3 System Dynamics 
   Change in SD can take a vast number of forms, thereby leading to complex and enormous 
variety of structures that include feedback exchange. However, there are some fundamental 
modes of behavior that can facilitate the study of dynamic systems (Sterman, 2000). These are 
the exponential growth, goal-seeking and oscillation forms. 
   Exponential growth is the result of self-reinforcing (positive) feedbacks. A large quantity that 
leads to a greater net gain, resulting in an even larger quantity that subsequently creates a 
constantly strengthened growth. An example of this behavior can be the population growth. A 
larger population leads to an increased birth rate, resulting in an even larger population. The 
exponential growth is not always related to growth and can also be applied to systems that may 
be constantly degenerating (such as an ever-increasing economic decline). While this 
==PAGE:29==
23 
 
phenomenon is present in many cases, the same is not true for linear growth. A linear growth 
represents the state of a system where the rate of change is constantly the same, despite the 
received feedback from those system changes. Therefore, it implies no feedback exchange, 
which is something very rare. As an example, suppose that the price of public tickets changes 
but the rate of commuters using the bus remains the same. This is not generally expected since 
those users will communicate the change and sooner or later, a number of them will opt to 
select another way to commute, if possible. 
   Goal-seeking is created by negative loops that aim to bring balance in the system, trying to 
maintain a certain equilibrium or a desired end result. They act when there are any 
discrepancies in the system and aim in counteracting these effects. An example could be the 
rest a person receives. During the day, exhaustion may accumulate and so at the end of the day, 
the person is going to sleep, restoring energy before the next day. 
   Oscillation is similar to goal-seeking modes of behavior, however the difference is that in 
this case, there are constant periods when the system overshoots itself, reversing its equilibrium 
state back and forth in an unstable way. This overshooting is the result of delayed feedback 
exchange, which results in delayed decision-making. In reality, most systems are belonging to 
this category, since there are bound to be delays of various magnitude during the feedback 
exchange between elements of a system. As an example, think of a shop owner that tries to 
serve the local demand for a product. If the owner is not properly informed about the demand 
and has considered it to be lower than it is, the expected orders that are arranged will not satisfy 
this demand. As soon as the owner realizes that, corrective action is taken and more orders are 
asked to be dispatched. However, until those new orders arrive, the consumers might seek the 
product elsewhere. Now that the owner has realized that, there might be some leftover stock 
that needs to be handled. The resulting overshooting of orders would not happen if the owner 
was informed for the correct amount of demand.  
   The most common way to communicate SD in a complex system is through the causal loop 
diagram. In this representation, variables that corresponds to elements within the system are 
connected by links, which denote the causal influences between those variables. These causal 
links are then in turn assigned a polarity, which can either be positive or negative (using the 
“+” and “-“ signs respectively). The importance of these links is to show how the effect 
changes, once a change in the cause takes place. Therefore, a positive link would imply that if 
the cause increases, then effect will increase compared to what its state would have been, if no 
==PAGE:30==
24 
 
changes were made. It is important to underline that any correlations among the available 
variables are not simulated in SD. Correlations reflect past behavior of the system, therefore, 
they are going to emerge once the system that is studied is properly simulated. 
   However, despite the fact that causal loop diagrams are very good for communicating the 
structure of a system, they are not able to capture the stock and flow nature that is present in 
SD. For this reason, stocks and flows are also introduced in the model. Stocks represent 
accumulations and quantities that are distributed into the system. Based on information from 
stocks, decision are made and they also act as the driving force for inertia in the system, as well 
as providing it with memory. Finally, stocks are also responsible for delays in the system and 
constitute an essential part of it. Flows are the drivers for exchanging quantities between stocks. 
In their general form, flows are functions of the stock, as well as of other variables and 
parameters present in the system. 
   Stocks, flows and endogenous feedback loops that indicate changes taking place in SD 
represent the system. In this study, they aim in representing the overall transportation system 
of Denmark. Stocks represent the number of Mpkm that is assigned to every modal choice that 
was considered. The Logit function (i.e., Eq. 12) was incorporated into SD, in order to simulate 
the users’ behavior that is demonstrated in modal choice. The annual historical and projected 
demand, taken from LTM, are used as the stock demand. The probability of each mode is 
calculated endogenously in the model and Eq. (14) is used as a flow for each mode.

2.5 Scenarios 
   The model is designed based on the two dimensions of trips: travel time and travel budget. 
To understand how different factors influence modal shift, a set of scenarios are developed 
(Table 2.4).  
2.5.1 Developing Copenhagen Metro (DCM) 
   In the first scenario, the new metro line for Copenhagen is considered. According to 
Copenhagen Metro (intl.m.dk n. d.), Cityringen is a completely new metro line that is 
constructed on the same principles as the two existing lines that form the metro network. It is 
going to be a circle line of a length of 15.5 km, located directly below downtown Copenhagen, 
the “bridge quarters” and Frederiksberg. Cityringen is designed to include 17 underground 
stations. Planned to open in July 2019, the two new lines, called M3 and M4 are going to serve 
a number of central spots and hubs situated in the Danish capital, not only significantly 
reducing travel times but also providing direct transfer to regional rail and commuter train 
networks. This will be done by providing access to the Copenhagen Central Station 
(Københavns Hovedbanegården), Nørrebro Station and Østerport Station. Finally, Cityringen 
will operate the same way as the existing metro lines do. 
2.5.2 Public Ticket price (PTP) 
   In the second scenario, the pricing of public fares is considered and the sensitivity of the 
model is first tested by applying a change in one of the included tangible costs, namely the 
public tangible cost. In this case, public tickets are considered dropped by 10% in 2018, in 
order to check whether or not this solution provides an impactful incentive for users to shift to 
public transportation. 
2.5.3 Increase of Fuel Price (IFP)    
   The third scenario tests the model’s sensitivity on a different approach, this time regarding 
the total vehicle costs for private cars. A new taxation scheme is introduced, where the price 
of fuel is considered to be 20% increased. As was the case with the PTP scenario, this change 
is introduced in 2018. 
2.5.4 Additional Public Infrastructure (INF)     
   The next scenario brings changes that operate on multiple levels. To begin with, additional 
infrastructure of Metro and S-train lines is introduced in DKW Urban and Suburban areas, to 
simulate a possible availability of those modes in city centers like Aarhus or Aalborg and their 
metropolitan areas. For this hypothesis, similar characteristics to those existing in existing lines 
==PAGE:33==
27 
 
are used. In addition to that, the overall improvement of public infrastructure is considered, 
resulting in increased vehicle speed, shorter travel times and reduced access and egress times. 
In order to properly simulate this, the public intangible casts for all modes are considered to be 
reduced by 10%. 
2.5.5 Raise of Taxes (ROT)   
   The fifth alternative scenario is once again a policy regarding private cars. This time, 
additional taxation is once explored, this time by suggesting an increase of registration and 
ownership taxes of fossil-fuel dependent vehicles by 20%. This means that in this case, some 
available car technologies are not affected by this change, in order to reflect various policies 
that may be considered as incentives for shifting to those fossil-free solutions. 
2.5.6 Combination (COM)    
   The final scenario aims to test the overall sensitivity of the model by introducing the 
combined operation of all the aforementioned measures that were analyzed in this section. 
Therefore, due to the multiple changes that take place, it is expected to be the scenario with the 
more drastic changes considering modal shift.

during 2010 and almost the same share is achieved in 2050 (83.7% and 83.3% respectively). 
At the same time, those milestone years are associated with the minimum shares of public 
modes (2010 for the train and S-train, 2050 for the bus and metro). This essentially means that 
the same shift that is taking place in 2010, also appears in 2050. The same reasons that were 
previously mentioned are responsible for these shifts from public modes to private cars. To 
begin with, the lowest total vehicle cost associated with private car technologies appears in 
2010, at 1.64 DKK/pkm. While the available technologies at that time were also limited, their 
respective costs were significantly lower compared to those that correspond to years 2020 and 
beyond. In 2050, this average cost is 1.89 DKK/pkm. However, ticket prices also reach their 
maximum during that time, while this average vehicle cost is slightly lower than its maximum, 
which is observed in 2030. In addition to that, the intangible costs are reaching their maximum 
values in 2030 and from then on they are considered steady until 2050. As it has been 
previously mentioned, all costs associated with NMT are maintaining a steady level. Thereby, 
this explains why the shift that takes place from 2035 until 2050 is mainly from public modes 
to private cars.

The main reason for this phenomenon is that the total vehicle cost associated with private cars

existing ones. This refers to the transition from 2nd to 3rd generation of diesel, gasoline, natural 
gas and electric cars. Therefore, due to the limited availability of technologies to choose from, 
the average cost associated with private cars is increased. In 2035, the 4th generation of the 
aforementioned technologies is introduced, thereby providing more options in the market, thus 
reducing the average cost. 
   Regarding the ticket prices, they are maintaining a steady degree of increase, providing the 
public modes with the necessary momentum to challenge the market during that time. In 
addition to that, the rate at which the intangible costs are increasing is also steady. This explains 
the brief shift from private cars to public modes between 2025 and 2030. It is worth mentioning 
at this point that both the average cost associated with bicycles as well as the intangible costs 
of walking are roughly steady throughout the period of 2010-2050. This is why their shares are 
also steady, showing only minor fluctuations.

   Overall, the results from the cases that were conducted showed that policies aimed at 
discouraging the use of private cars have significant less effects than those that incentivize the 
use of public modes. Since many years now, the ownership of a car is considered a quality that 
elevates personal status and more often than not it is considered a very reliable way to 
commute. Therefore, it is not easy to convince car owners to abandon their vehicles, which are 
often considered as investments that need to pay off their initially high capital costs. As 
mentioned during the literature review section, drivers will often accept congestion times and 
decide to endure them, as long as they can continue using their cars and quite often, they are 
ready to accept new policy schemes that might be disadvantageous, such as increased parking 
fares or toll fees. 
   However, the improvement of existing infrastructure for public modes appears to be more 
successful in attracting commuters and shifting them from using their private vehicles. Often 
associated with long waiting times and inconsistent itineraries, public modes can significantly 
benefit from the improvement of their level of service and also from increased availability, 
since there are areas where there is no access to a number of them. As an example, it is easily 
observed from this study that introducing S-train lines in Western Denmark or new lines for 
the metro in Copenhagen, the effects are drastic and the use of private cars is considerably 
limited.  
4.3 Modeling Benefits and Limitations 
   The resulting model of this study was one that managed to combine the complex interactions 
involved in System Dynamics with discrete choice modeling used to simulate consumer 
behavior, through the use of the Multinomial Logit function. This work can facilitate the 
integration of transport models into energy simulation frameworks and contribute to the end 
goal set by Denmark to become fossil-free by 2050. By identifying the connections between 
the various modes that operate on a national level and the magnitude of their influence, 
measures that will support scheduling in that direction can be formulated and this model can 
also serve as a basis for future analyses and further scenario formulations. 
   In addition, various policy schemes can be reviewed and evaluated using the principles that 
were introduced on this study. As an example, there were scenarios where the sensitivity of the 
model was tested, based upon possible taxation schemes. The analyses could be expanded on 
other layers as well or on various modeling horizons, since the structure of this study was based 
on milestone annual intervals. The fact that the last case was a combination of all previous 
==PAGE:52==
46 
 
alternative scenarios proves that there is also the possibility to combine analyses and check 
their impact. 
   Finally, there is the possibility of adding spatial elements in the study as well. For the purpose 
of structuring this model, the country of Denmark was divided in 24 sections, based on the 
location and the level of income of residents. While this is far from discussing the possibility 
of including GIS (Geographic Information Systems) data, it is still possible to capture the effect 
of investments and improvements in various urban and suburban centers. The inclusion of 
income levels, allows for modeling expected shifts in the financial conditions on a national 
level and their effects. This distribution also allows the examination of urbanization patterns 
and the expected increase of urban population, in the form of increased demand for transport 
in urban areas.  
   However, there are some limitations. To begin with, there is no data available that allows for 
a detailed geographical analysis of the transportation system, such as map coordinates for bus 
stops. Therefore, the model is not able to produce a scrutinized analysis of mode selection 
based on spatial information and has to rely on existing surveys and questionnaires. 
   As described in the Methodology chapter, the Multinomial Logit function is a discrete choice 
model, which assigns the probability of every alternative among a set of alternatives to have 
the maximum utility for one or more users and therefore be selected by those users. The traits 
and attributes of the examined alternatives and users are mainly describing the effects and 
parameters that are being studied and thus, in the case of this study, the main drivers were the 
various costs that are associated with the transportation sector. This essentially means that the 
nature of the model is cost-driven and therefore, the mode of choice for each user is selected 
based on the financial efficiency it provides. However, it is safe to assume that there are other 
factors affecting this decision. While the constants of this model have been introduced to act 
as unobserved factors that have not been accounted for, but still affect the modal choice, there 
are some limitations that due to their nature, they are hard to be modelled. For instance, there 
might be a number of users who will opt to bike or walk more for exercise and fitness reasons 
or simply because the weather at that day might be very pleasant in order to do so. In this 
model, these variables could not have been introduced since not only would they require 
significant weather data, but also surveys as to what current conditions might be regarded as 
pleasant for doing so. 
==PAGE:53==
47 
 
   In addition to that, the model is able to calculate the optimal choice for every case but this 
choice might not always correspond to a real situation. For example, suppose that this model 
calculated that one user, commuting in an urban area, should take the bus. There is a chance 
that the bus arrived late due to a sudden delay or it arrived already congested with passengers, 
not being able to receive more. Our user might realize that it takes too long to take the next 
bus, so maybe she/he takes a taxi. The model would be unable to account for those instances. 
However, it can show the overall underlying trends connecting the modes and influencing their 
relations.  
   Furthermore, the nature of the model includes the quality of independent alternatives with 
uncorrelated ties between them. This essentially means that a drop of one alternative’s utility 
by a certain quantity leads to an equal increase in the utility of all other alternatives by said 
quantity. This cannot be assumed when examining transportation options, because not all 
competing modes can be considered equally attractive. Thus,  need for a nested logit model 
arises, where similar alternatives are grouped together in separate nests. However, this 
limitation is confronted by grouping the different modes of transport in larger groups, which 
act as nests (private, public and NMT) and then by imposing limitations on their availability, 
as per the area where they operate or are planned to be operated. 
   To conclude the imposed limitations, this study forecasts the emergence of new technologies 
in the private car market, but does not account for the number of vehicles per technology 
expected to contribute to the total of that market on the annual milestone levels. Therefore, the 
utility of private cars relies on average costs based on the respective existing technologies and 
not on a specific share of the fleet of available vehicles. The reason for that is mainly because 
this model acts as a way to forecast and analyze patterns in the transportation sector. The 
distribution of the available car stock is optimized through the use of TIMES-DK on an energy 
simulation level. 
 
 
==PAGE:54==
48 
 
5. Conclusion 
   According to the results of this study, it is indeed observed that the travel demand is 
increasing steadily and private cars have a large share, accounting for almost 83% of the total 
inland demand in Denmark. The upward trend in transportation demand is expected to reinforce 
the dominance of private cars and as the results in the BAU scenario have shown, that is indeed 
the case for the biggest part of the modeling horizon. The main reason regarding this effect is 
the availability of private cars, which are able to serve all kinds of trips, irrespective of their 
geographical location or length. In addition to that, it appears that the costs that are associated 
with cars are able to offset the limitations imposed by public policies or infrastructure. This 
was especially underlined in the case of the ITF and ROT scenarios, when new taxation 
schemes were examined. Even when the fuel, capital registration and taxation costs were 
raised, the resulting modal share was the least impactful, creating only marginal shifts away 
from private modes. The results also show that private car usage is essentially not easy to be 
discouraged by aggressive policies against it. On the contrary, additional improvements 
regarding the public traffic network can have a significantly more positive effect and are 
therefore able to translate it into increased public pkm. 
   Indeed, it appears that by improving the overall level of service provided by public modes, 
new opportunities are emerging and effects that are more drastic take place. Lowering the ticket 
prices was more impactful than raising private vehicle taxation, thereby gaining more value as 
a possible incentive. However, providing public modes with additional infrastructure to support 
them gave them an even greater advantage, especially when their availability was increased. 
As an example, when additional S-train lines were considered for DKW, the S-train was able 
to compete with the bus, despite the fact that it is still absent from rural areas. This means that 
public modes are an attractive alternative when it comes to urban and suburban trips, an 
advantage that can certainly be exploited, taking into account the increasing urbanization rate. 
   The emerging private car technologies appear to be reinforcing the dominance of private cars 
in most cases. This is to be expected after all, especially taking into account the fierce 
competition contained within car industry. Technological breakthroughs are immediately 
exploited and there is a constant improvement in the level of service that is provided, translated 
as increased vehicle speed, optimized fuel consumption and cheaper maintenance. Therefore, 
the need for a parallel improvement of the public sector is accentuated, in order to keep up in 
the competition.  
==PAGE:55==
49 
 
   The results suggest that public transportation infrastructure investments do have a role in 
increasing ridership, and they also appear more successful in slightly reducing private cars in 
urban areas. On the other hand, increasing the fuel tax does not motivate many users to switch 
away from private cars. A possible reason for this is the fact that there is a plethora of other 
costs associated with owning and maintaining a car, so that an increase in fuel price does not 
heavily affect users' decisions. However, increasing the vehicle registration tax does promote 
slightly more NMT, particularly cycling. A reason for that is that there are no additional 
incentives for public modes; thereby, the bicycle is able to absorb part of the share that shifts 
away from cars.  
   This study mainly focuses on developing a methodology for analyzing modal shift; as such, 
the scenarios and results presented above are intended as a proof-of-concept for how a SD 
model can used to analyze the effect of different transport policy decisions. There are many 
possibilities, e.g., different policies regarding taxation can be examined and at multiple time 
intervals. They can also be variated by providing different taxation levels, depending on 
whether a specific technology can be incentivized, in order to attract more consumers from the 
available market demand. The same principle applies to fuel prices and a more complex system 
of pricing can be scrutinized, in order to support Denmark’s goal of becoming fossil-free by 
the year 2050. 
   The public sectors can benefit from the analysis by studying the evolution of their market 
shares through multiple ticket price levels, shifting the market in their favor when the 
opportunity presents itself. The SD framework can form the basis of a cost-benefit analysis for 
increasing public transportation infrastructure. The optimization or improvement of the 
existing network may lead to reduced travel times and more optimal itineraries, thus leading to 
reduced intangible costs for the respective modes. Their complex relationships can be 
examined in the various areas of interest, thereby supporting new investments or acting as risk 
contingency plans for those that are still under consideration. The SD facilitates the study of 
the complex and ever-evolving transportation sector. 
   In addition, schemes regarding the incentives to be provided for NMT modes (cycling in 
particular) can be explored. In this study, it has been shown that NMT can profit more from 
the imposition of taxation policies regarding private cars, compared to incentives for the public 
modes, which are proved to be harder to compete against. However, a combination of those 
measures along with an improvement of NMT infrastructure could very well work in their 
==PAGE:56==
50 
 
favor. An example would be bicycle lanes in more urban areas, accompanied by shorter waiting 
times on traffic lights. The result would be reduced intangible costs for NMT, something that 
is bound to increase their utility and create a shift towards them as well. 
   While the basis of the model is comprised mainly of costs, the SD framework combined with 
multinomial logit can identify the trends in the transportations sector and underline the effect 
of changes operating within it. This study can be later on expanded by adding GIS data into it, 
reinforcing the spatial elements that are included and thereby produce a highly detailed 
mapping of trips generated on a national level. In addition to that, the inclusion of cost factors 
facilitates the integration of transport models in energy simulation frameworks and is able to 
provide a basis for analysis when different energy policies are examined.
