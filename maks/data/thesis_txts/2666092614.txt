Ethereum Blockchain
Alexandros Spyropoulos (s182346)
February 14, 2021
Abstract: Over the past few years Ethereum blockchain has been seen an ever increasing popularity mainly due
to it’s ability to support decentralized applications (dApps) by the use of smart contracts. Decentralized exchanges
(DEXs) is an area that got developed and currently ﬂourishing on top of that technology. However, and due to the
transparency and latency of the blockchain, transactions on DEXs are susceptible to various attacks. Front-running
attack is one of the most important where basically an adversarial trader is exploiting information in purpose of
market manipulation. In this work I present a model of a variant of front running attack: sandwich attack. Based
on this model I performed three experiments to measure a trader’s proﬁt in relation to the attack. Lastly, I provide
all the technical details and speciﬁcations of the execution environment.
Keywords: Blockchain, Ethereum, Smart contracts, Front running attacks, Sandwich attacks, Solidity, Python

Introduction
It’s only a few years since blockchain emerged into a disruptive technology. That among other things brought
to light the decentralized exchanges (DEXs) that are using cryptocurrencies for trading other assets. That type
of cryptocurrency exchange allows for secure peer-to-peer (P2P) cryptocurrency transactions without the need of
a trusted authority. Blockchain transactions are gathered into a public memory pool for a short time before a
miner fetches them and mine them. This time window constitutes an opportunity for various exploitations such as
the front-running attack which is basically when an adversary is exploiting information to change the price of an
asset for ﬁnancial gain. For the rest of the paper the reader should become himself familiar with the following terms:
A. Blockchain
Blockchain technology initially has been invented to address the need to perform an economical transaction between
two parties without an intermediary centralized authority. Blockchain in a nutshell allows disparate users to make
transactions by creating an immutable record of those transactions. Essentially it is an electronic ledger based on
the P2P architecture that can be shared among users to creates an immutable record of transactions. The ﬁrst
blockchain created for that reason was Bitcoin in 2009. Only a few years later the Ethereum blockchain came out.
The big advantage of Ethereum is the ability to run decentralized code (smart contracts) that eventually created a
big potential to many diﬀerent applications such as DEXs.
B. Smart Contracts
Smart contracts are lines of code that are stored on a blockchain and automatically executed when predetermined
terms and conditions are met. There are many diﬀerent smart contracts categories.
D. Decentralized exchanges (DEXs)
DEXs are cryptocurrency exchanges that operate without a central authority, allowing users to transact peer-to-
peer and maintain control of their funds. DEXs reduce the risk of price manipulation, as well as hacking and theft,
because crypto assets are never in the custody of the exchange itself.
E. Automated market makers (AMMs)
AMMs are a subclass of decentralized exchanges that rely on mathematical formulas to set the price of a token.
There is no centralized party to make the trades. That means that anyone can trade all the time with an AMM
without any intermediaries. There are many diﬀerent types of AMMs such as constant product, constant sum,
constant mean.
F. Uniswap
Uniswap is a decentralized exchange protocol built on Ethereum blockchain.
More speciﬁcally it is a constant
product AMM that relies on the mathematical formula A · B = k, for some constant k, to perform the exchanges.
G. Front running attack
Front running attack is known from traditional ﬁnancial markets since 1970 [8]. But recently (around 2017) front

running attacks appeared in blockchain ecosystem with the rise of smart contracts and DeXs on Ethereum. The
attack is basically when an adversary beneﬁts from prior access to privileged market information about upcoming
trades. A more detailed overview will be presented in section 2.
G. Sandwiching attack
A variant of front-running attack which has a steps, a front-running attack and a back-running attack.
H. Arbitrage
Arbitrage is the process of simultaneous buying and selling of an asset from diﬀerent sources to make a proﬁt on
the price diﬀerence. An example in the cryptocurrency world would be the following: suppose that we have two
markets. Market A has an exchange rate 1 BTC for 2 ETH and market B 1 BTC for 3 ETH. Now someone can go
and buy 3 ETH (by providing 1 BTC) from market B and sell them to A so to receive 1.5 BTC. Thus making a
proﬁt of 0.5 BTC (1.5 - 1).
Why is this important?
Front-running attack is been a major issue on the decentralized ﬁnancial markets. At the same time it makes it
diﬃcult to estimate an amount of money that is exploited in a period of time. This is because is hard to identify
the attack plus the amount of money that is gained per transaction is usually small. An estimation showed that
an attacker could achieve a max of 440,000 USD with in a period of 158 days[7]. Qin et al [6] after scanning and
analyzing two years of results they found that the proﬁt for sandwich attacks was 1.51M USD. Researchers along
with Banchor team also implemented a python bot that performs front-running attack in Bancor exchange network
just for educational purposes [4]. There are many papers related to front-running attack presenting the various
cases of the attack [5]. One of them is the sandwiching attack has been described for the ﬁrst time in Zhou et al
[7]. However and to the best of my knowledge, I found no paper that provides implementation details on how to
build an environment that uses a test blockchain for emulating the various types of front running attack. This can
be useful for conducting various experiments to study and understand better the behaviour of the various entities
in this type of attack.

Front Running Attack
2.1
Attack strategies
Front running attack is a course of action where someone beneﬁts from early access to market information about up-
coming transactions and trades, typically because of a privileged position along the transmission of this information
and is applicable to both ﬁnancial and non-ﬁnancial systems[5].
Front-running attacks can be distinguished into three cases: displacement, insertion, and suppression attacks. In
all these cases, a victim user, let’s call it Victim, is trying to invoke a function on a contract that is in a particular
state, and a malicious user, let’s call it Attacker, will try to invoke her own function call on the same contract in the
same state before victim. In this paper I am going to emulate the insertion attack which is usually a transaction
that changes the state of the smart contract it calls. That means that the Attacker can insert transactions before
and after Victim’s transaction to change the state of the smart contract and make proﬁt.
2.2
Possible attackers
The possible adversaries that can perform a front running attack are:
• Miners: The miners on the Ethereum blockchain are in the best position to perform front running attacks.
This is because they are the ones that mine a block and so they can insert their own transaction in the order
of their favor.
• Users: Users that monitors the network can view the transactions and perform a front running attack. This
is because each transaction is paid by using gas price. The higher the gas price the more likely a miner is to
prioritize the transaction.
• Bot-nets: Programmable bots that act as users but they can be more eﬃcient in their attacks.
For the rest of the paper has to do with sandwiching attack which is a type of front-running attack. This type will
be explained also in section 4.

Technological Stack and Design Decisions
In order to conduct our experiments we needed to use the below components.
3.1
Ganache
Ganache is a lightweight test blockchain implementation for Ethereum. It is used for deploying and testing decen-
tralized apps in a safe and deterministic environment. The beneﬁt of using this tool is the smooth learning curve
and the ease of use for developing a set up for a blockchain app/experiment. The downside of it is that it does not
have a transaction mempool that’s why we had to implement our own in python.
3.2
Truﬄe
A development environment, testing framework and asset pipeline for blockchains using the Ethereum Virtual
Machine (EVM). We used truﬄe for writing the Solidity contracts and compile them to bytecode and ABI. The
compilation is essential for the deployment of our contracts to ganache and the communication with them. The
deployment happens using the web3.py library.
3.3
Web3.py
Web3.py is a Python library for facilitating the sending of transactions, the interaction with smart contracts, the
reading block data and a variety of other use cases. Our implementation has been developed on top of that library.

Threat Model
In simple words the model consist of one agent victim (V) that tries to arbitrage between a pool (P) and a market
(M) and an agent attacker (A1) that tries to perform a sandwich attack to victim’s transactions. In our second
experiment I add an extra agent attacker (A2) to our model. All the transactions from V, A1 and A2 are gathered to
a mempool (MP). From there miner (MN) will fetch them to mine them. For simplicity the P and the M maintain
reserves for only one pair of tokens WBTC/ETH. In addition and for the sake of clarity I didn’t use real gas values
to our transactions but rather small numbers such as gas = 2 wei although I could use the web3.eth.getGasPrice()
that the web3 library provided.
Our behaviour of our entities is as follows:
• P maintains the reserves of two crypto-currencies and interact with both V, A1 and A2. Is also responsible
for the exchange of tokens. The exchange is based on the general formula:

outcome is an amount y of token A. In our implementation we used Uniswap’s exchange formula [9] which
also includes 0.3% fees. The formula is:
Given that ResWBTC: WBTC reserve, ResETH: ETH reserve and amountWithFee = AmountIn * 997:

• M behaves as the P
• V tries to perform arbitrage. He constantly asks and compares prices from P and M in order to ﬁnd a chance
to perform arbitrage. He achieve this by making a call to M and P that asks how many WBTC can he get for
1000 ETH. The source that will provide him with the most currencies will be the one to start the arbitrage
from. Right after he places two transactions one to buy WBTC(for getting ETH) from P/M and one to
buy ETH(for getting WBTC) from M/P. Both of them are set with a ﬁxed gas price of 2 Wei. Once his
transactions are placed he performs no other action until both are mined. For the sake of the experiments I
provided the pool and the market with diﬀerent token reserves making enough price diﬀerence so that V can
always ﬁnd an opportunity to arbitrage. The exact numbers will be mentioned in the experiment section.
• MP is a mempool that accumulates all the transactions from V, A1 and A2 before MN fetches them for
mining.
• MN every 2 seconds fetches all the transactions from the MP starting with the one with the highest gas price
and mines it. The miner terminates this process either when he reaches the gas limit of 12 Wei or when there
is no other transaction in the mempool to mine.
• A1 has an objective to maximize his proﬁt. To achieve this he constantly scans the mempool trying to ﬁnd a
transaction to attack. Once he ﬁnds that transaction he checks to see if that transaction will be proﬁtable to

attack. He does this calculation oﬀ-chain, by emulating the mining process of the following three transactions
a) A1 front-run transaction b) V transaction, c) A1 back-run. If by the end of his emulation he is proﬁtable
then he places his two transactions to the mempool. The ﬁrst transaction that he places is one with the exact
same details (market/pool, currency, amount) with the transaction that wants to attack but with an higher
gas price (3 Wei) than V, and then he places the opposite transaction of that. Meaning if the ﬁrst was a sell
ETH to acquire WBTC then the second is a sell WBTC to acquire ETH and vice versa. Also he can promote
reordering of a transaction by adjusting the gas price. Once his transactions are placed he performs no other
action until both are mined. This process of deciding whether the attack is proﬁtable and execute it, it has
been described also by Qin et al [6] and deﬁned as transaction replay algorithm.
• A2 behaves as A1 but set his transactions with gas price 4 Wei.

Experiments
Every experiment will run for a max 10 blocks. Meaning V will have max 10 tries to perform arbitrage. I could
allow more tries but I am checking the short-term behaviour of these entities. V always tries to perform an arbitrage
with a value of 100000 ETH. A1 and A2 they don’t have a certain value for interacting with M and P. Instead they
will copy the amount from V’s transaction. Before we dive into the experiments’ details it’s important to deﬁne the
following terms:
Successful attack:
A successful sandwich attack is one that front-run’s (placing a copy of V’s transaction to be mined prior to V’s
transaction) and back-run’s (placing a transaction right after V’s transaction) a V’s transaction within the same
mining block. We assume that both of the transactions should be at the same block so that an attacker can take
advantage of the ﬁnancial terms that exist in a speciﬁc block state. This is because the attack initiates at a moment
where there are certain prices available in block Bi where there is no guarantee that these will be valid for the next
block Bi+1 also.
Failed attack:
In a failed attack one or both of the attacker’s transactions fail to be mined together with V’s transaction with in
the same block.
Slippage:
Slippage refers to the diﬀerence between the expected price of a trade (which is a transaction here) and the price
at which the trade is executed.

5.3.4
Discussion
Our results do not to agree with the original hypothesis. That happened due to the gas limit of the miner that
made all the attacks to fail to be mined with in the same block. However and since there is no other interaction
by any other actor to our environment the prices of M and P remain the same for next blocks. That is why A2
continued to increase his proﬁt in steady pace. On the other hand A1 was also getting a proﬁt but less than A2
and only in half of his attacks. Lastly, we observe that the sandwich attack of A1 and A2 incur a 1.72% proﬁt loss
to V in comparison with the base experiment and 0.05% proﬁt gain in comparison with the experiment 1.
5.4
Discussion of all experiments
What is evident from the experiments is that the success of the sandwich attack is correlated with the ability of
the attacker to place his transactions directly before and after V’s transaction. Additionally, in a situations where
two or more attackers are competing for a transaction that creates race conditions and the attack is more diﬃcult
to succeed. The fastest attacker will have his attack succeed.

6.2
Python classes
In order to interact with the deployed Solidity contracts in ganache I built the implementation on top of web.py
library. Each of the below classes are keeping an address of their corresponding deployed contract so that they can
interact with them.
• Pool This class is used only for interacting with the pool Solidity contract and it has no other logic in it.
• Market Same as above, market class it’s used only for communication with the market Solidity contract .
• Victim The victim class contains the logic for the arbitrage and the transaction placing. The logic of the
victim is as follows. First ask the pool and the market how much WBTC he can take for a certain amount of
ETH. Then he compares the two amounts and places an transaction to the market or the pool that returned
the biggest amount of WBTC. Right after he places the opposite transaction i.e. if the ﬁrst was buy WBTC
for ETH the second is buy ETH for WBTC. This time we don’t need to specify the amount. This is because
the Solidity contract which will be executed upon the ﬁrst transaction with return a WBTC/ETH amount
that is stored to the contract so that it can be used for the second immediate transaction. Once the victim
place both of the transactions it waits until both of them are mined and then he can start all over again.
• Attacker 1 This class contains all the methods used for the sandwich attack that has been explained in
section 4.
• Attacker 2 This class contains the same functionality as Attacker 1 class.
• Miner The most common miner transaction order techniques are Parity and Geth. Geth ﬁrst sorts and
separates the list of transactions into lists of individual sender accounts and sorts them by nonce. Right after,
they are merged back together and sorted by gas price, always comparing only the ﬁrst transaction from
each account. Parity prioritizes local and retracted transactions ﬁrst and polishes transactions with heavy
computation and the sorts by gas price[7]. Considering that we hadn’t a Ethereum client node with this
behaviour integrated we implemented a custom class that sorts by gas price and can be seen in Mempool
class. The block time in Ethereum varies from 13s to 19s but the average is 15s[1]. In order to have a faster
execution environment I set the block time to 2s
• Mempool Is a max priority queue meaning having always ﬁrst the element with the highest value. The
priority criterion is the gas price. The transaction with the highest gas price comes ﬁrst. Although this
should be a simple queue and model the transaction ordering to the Miner class, for saving time and since we
don’t evaluate the miner we implemented it that task here.
