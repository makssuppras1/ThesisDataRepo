Abstract
This thesis investigates the principle of using microwave imaging for breast cancer detection.
The technology is based on acquiring complex transmission coeﬃcients for a conﬁned area,
using several antennas placed around the breast.
An eight-channel Active Switching Network has been designed, manufactured and its
characteristics have been documented. Eight antennas can be connected to the ASN and
share two ports connected to a Vector Network Analyzer. It has an operational frequency
range of 0.5 - 6 GHz. A Low Noise Ampliﬁer has been implemented in the Rx circuit to
improve sensitivity of the system.
Speciﬁcations required an isolation of ≥120 dB. Measurements showed that an isolation
of ≥100 dB were obtained for most channels. Unwanted resonances and crosstalk decreased
isolation in some conﬁgurations, and suggestions for improvement have been presented.
The ASN is a 6-layer PCB with GCPW transmission lines and various RF switches.
The mechanical structure is a three part construction designed using a 3D CAD software.
The mechanical structure not only holds the PCB and its connectors, it also improves the
performance regarding gain and isolation signiﬁcantly.
The entire system can be operated from a PC using an USB - I/O unit and an imple-
mented control circuit.
It has been concluded that the ASN is competitive to existing products and that it can
be used in a microwave imaging system.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Recent studies have shown that microwave imaging might be a promising method for de-
tecting breast cancer. The method is based on placing several antennas around the breast.
Each antenna transmits an electromagnetic signal while the remaining antennas receive the
signal after having propagated through the breast. This makes it possible to obtain sev-
eral complex transmission coeﬃcients for all directions. Using an algorithm which solves
Maxwells equations, it is possible to obtain the complex permittivity and permeability for a
deﬁned area, thus creating a 3D image of the breast. The data are acquired using a Vector
Network Analyzer (VNA) and an Active Switching Network (ASN).
This thesis will focus on the Active Switching Network with the following project struc-
ture:
• Research of existing switching network technologies
• Develop speciﬁcation of an 8-channel ASN
• Design an architecture for both the high frequency switching part as well as the control
electronics
• Simulations to ensure that speciﬁcation is met with the developed architecture
• Manufacturing, measurement and documentation of the ASN

1.1
Motivation
Mainly three diﬀerent ways of measuring breast cancer are used today[2].
• Mammography
• CT scanning
• MRI scanning.
CT and MRI scannings are quite complex and requires huge equipment and a special de-
signed room for operating.
They are accurate but the cost is very high and requires a
trained operator. The MRI rely on magnetic resonance imaging whereas the CT scanner
uses x-ray.
Mammography is a more simple test method and often used for screening sessions.
Similar to the CT scan it relies on x-ray imaging. Two images are taken from diﬀerent angles,
and is done by pressing the breast between two plates. This is a very uncomfortable method
and is said to be unpleasant for the patient [2]. In Denmark every women between 50 and
69 years of age are oﬀered a free mammography screening every second year, which means
many examinations are made every year. Figure 1.1 shows an example of a mammography
screening.
Figure 1.1: Example of a mammography screening [2]
Microwave imaging has great potential since it has several advantages over Mammogra-
phy. The physical measurement setup does not provide any discomfort towards the patient

and images can be obtained without squeezing or moving the breast in unpleasant ways.
It is possible to obtain a 3D image of the breast, much more informative than the two
angle images from Mammography. Also it removes use of x-ray and thereby eliminates any
radioactive radiation towards patients and operators.
The aim is that a microwave image system will be fairly portable and easily handled.
This is its great advantage versus existing technologies. Also the price of the microwave
imaging system is much lower.

The breast is surrounded by 32 antennas, each connected to a measurement unit. Using
this measurement unit each antenna is capable of both transmitting and receiving, a so-
called multistatic system.
The received signal is down-converted to 1 kHz to be A/D
converted into a PC for data processing. The hardware has an operating frequency range
of 0.3 to 3 GHz.
2.1.1
Antenna System
The Antenna System is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.2. It consist of 32 antennas placed in the
horizontal direction around the breast. The antennas are made from 3.58 mm diameter
coaxial cable (Micro Coax UT141-SS) where 3.5 cm of the outer conductor has been stripped
at the tip.
Figure 2.2: Antenna System, 32 horizontal antennas surrounds the target [3]
The entire antenna structure is placed in a bowl that can be ﬁlled with glycerin wa-
ter, which electromagnetic parameters are similar to the inside of the breast. This is done
because the diﬀerence from free air to the breast are immense, thus resulting in huge reﬂec-
tions.
Alternatively the antennas should be placed right up to the breast without any air gap.
But a ﬁxed sized antenna would not make due. Small breasts would leave a large air gap
to the antennas whereas large breasts might not be able to ﬁt in a ﬁxed system. Using this
coupling liquid the issue of the many diﬀerent breast sizes are eliminated.
Furthermore, because the liquid is quite lossy reﬂections from outside the measurement
tank are attenuated which is a nice feature for avoiding clutter.

2.2
Concept Architecture
The concept of the architecture is shown in ﬁgure 2.3. The same antenna system used in
previous research will also be implemented in this project.
The antennas will be directly connected to an eight channel Active Switching Network
through 50 Ω coaxial cables. The switching network enables each antenna to either transmit,
receive or be passive (terminated). This will be controlled from a computer through a USB
to I/O Unit which makes it possible to change state of each channel and make the data
acquisition from the VNA.
Figure 2.3: Architecture of the system
The ASN will be connected to a VNA. Making a 2-port measurement with the VNA
makes it possible to aquire the scattering parameter, S21, which holds information of the
transmission through the breast. The algorithm used for image reconstruction derived from
previous research depends on this complex scatter parameter, thus the VNA is a perfect
instrument to implement in the system.
The S-parameters will be collected in a dataﬁle on the computer and can be processed
for the 3D image reconstruction.

LNA (Low Noise Ampliﬁer)
The LNA is added to the system to provide a high sensitivity. From previous presented
requirements it is stated that the LNA should have a wide bandwidth of 0.5 to 6 GHz which
is quite demanding for a well performing LNA. Also it must be an oﬀ-the-self component

Through a research of LNA’s available on the market the choice has fallen on VMMK-

been internally matched to 50 Ωon both input and output, which means that no external
matching circuits are needed.
For best performance both input and output must be biased with 1.8 V. The LNA has
a shut down feature, which means that when the input is 0 V it will be turned oﬀ. This is
a great feature because this increases the isolation.
According to the datasheet [8] the noise ﬁgure is typically 2 dB which is acceptable.
Actually the NF of the LNA does not inﬂuence the system much, because two blocks are
placed in cascade before this stage (will be described in details in section 3.5 “System Noise
Figure”). The price per unit is 40 DKK.

very diﬃcult component to solder as it is a small 0402 package with three legs. This makes
little room for an oﬀset or a skew in the placement. This is quite demanding since it has to
be placed with hands and must be done with careful precision.

• Antennas should be designed as a 50 Ω termination with a maximum deviation of
±40 %

4.1.1.2
Grounded Coplanar Waveguide & Spurious Modes
This section is dedicated to the analysis of grounded coplanar waveguide (GCPW) behavior
and spurious modes because it has signiﬁcant impact on the performance of the circuit. The
GCPW is the primary type of transmission line for all RF-lines in the circuit because it
provides ﬂexibility in choosing conductor width for ﬁxed substrate height.
An illustration of a grounded coplanar waveguide is seen in ﬁgure 4.5. As can be seen
it consist of a dielectric material with the height, H, and then the conductor on top with
a width, W, and two ground planes on either side separated by a gap, G. A ground plane
on the bottom side of the dielectric is also added, thus creating a Grounded Coplanar
Waveguide.
Figure 4.5: Grounded Coplanar Waveguide
The characteristic impedance is depended on dielectric material and its height, the width
of the center conductor, together with the gap, G. Because of this it is easy to alter the
impedance of the GCPW thus making it one of the preferred PCB transmission lines in
high frequency design. Being able to change the width, but keeping the correct impedance
by altering the gap is a very desirable feature because IC packages often have very diﬀerent
pin sizes.
Spurious modes contains unwanted signals that can cause degradation to the actual
intended signal. The GCWG is able to transmit two fundamental modes, even and odd
[15][16][17]. The odd mode is a quasi-TEM mode and is useful in circuit application and
thus the one that is desired (also called CPW mode). The even mode, also called the slotline
mode is to be suppressed. The slotline mode will exist if the ground planes do not have the
same potential. The diﬀerence in potentials are often due to propagation delay along the
==PAGE:53==
CHAPTER 4. TEST SETUPS
42
ground plane. The propagation delay can arise due to asymmetry either due to fabrication
errors or simply speciﬁc layout requirements.
A way to suppress unwanted modes is the use of a via fence. The pitch of these via
holes should be 1/8 of the wavelength of the highest operating frequency, according to [18].

5.2
Mechanical Structure
The mechanical structure has been designed to ensure that the isolation does not decrease
when the PCB is enclosed. In a square normal box, reﬂection would arise on the lids. This
would create unwanted resonances in the frequency response. This must be avoided at all
costs when dealing with a PCB where isolation is of very high priority.
Optimally each transmission path should be enclosed in a cavity, thus isolated completely
from the rest of the PCB. This would decrease the crosstalk to a minimum and thus be
comparable to simulation results.

to the VNA only comes from an active antenna (Rx mode) and not from any of the other

traces which will introduce crosstalk.
• The PCB will be placed in an enclosure. This will cause in reﬂections which will
introduce in unwanted resonances in frequency response.
• Power and control lines might pick up RF signal and distribute throughout the system.
This is especially critical at the biasing of the LNA as well as the small packaged
switches where trace distance is very little.

6.2.1
Improve Isolation
Some channels does not live up to speciﬁcations and it is thus necessary to analyze how
the isolation on these channels can be improved. Various methods have been considered
and some have been tested by experimental lab measurements. The following points could
improve isolation.
• If each channel was completely isolated with a grounded wall the isolation could be
increased signiﬁcantly. This has been tested partially by soldering some cobber walls
on the PCB wherever it was possible. At locations where two channel traces where
close to each other the crosstalk was greatly reduced with this wall.
Also many
resonances where removed, because the signal in a cavity could not as easy reﬂect on
the aluminum structure to another cavity.
• EMI absorber could be taped on the inside of the box. This would reduce reﬂections
and thus remove resonances. Its aﬀect has not been tested on the ASN, but it is a
very widely used technique in both laptops and mobile phones. The recommended
EMI absorber with the frequency range matching the ASN is AB7020 by 3M [21]. It
has a pricing of 500 DKK for an A4 sheet, and for this reason it has not been tested,
but is kept in mind as a viable solution.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Previous work at DTU had proven that microwave imaging for breast cancer is indeed
possible, but with much room for improvement. Being able to acquire the forward elec-
tromagnetic scatter vector in a more suitable way was necessary for further development.
Not only should the system be automated and be able to switch antenna automatically, it
should do so with wider bandwidth, higher isolation and better sensitivity.
A concept architecture for this project was developed and consists of eight antennas con-
nected to an Active Switching Network, connected to a VNA that can acquire S-parameters
of the breast. The entire system is controlled with a USB to I/O control unit from the PC.
Speciﬁcations were developed based on research of similar solutions. Requirements, such as
isolation of 120 dB and bandwidth between 0.5 - 6 GHz had high priority.
An eight-channel Active Switching Network was designed using ADS simulation that
concluded that the system would meet requirements. A layout was made using ADS, where
a 6-layer PCB was designed and manufactured. The PCB had a high complexity with RF
signal on both top and bottom layer, a power plane and a signal plane for control lines.
A mechanical structure was designed using Solidworks.
The structure had isolation
walls that isolate one channel from another channel, thus avoiding reﬂection that would
arise using a ﬂat lid. These reﬂections would make resonances on transmission lines, hence
reduce the isolation. The isolation walls were not suﬃcient to suppress resonances, there-
fore extra isolation walls were soldered on the PCB. Extra walls did increase the isolation
signiﬁcantly, but with room for improvement. Three suggestions to improving the isolation
were presented; fully enclosed channels (would need a new PCB layout), EMI absorber on
the inside of the aluminum box, and using striplines rather than GCPW transmission lines.
It was possible to make a fully operating Active Switching Network which could be
controlled from a PC. It was controlled by writing to three diﬀerent ports, and table 3.7
showed which binary numbers to write to each port for any state of the eight antennas.

tested for Rx gain, Nx isolation and Tx insertion loss. The measured data were compared
to simulations for analysis and to determine if full potential has been reached. The system
has a good gain for all channels with a gain between 8 and 10 dB for frequency range 0.5 - 4
GHz. From 4 - 6 GHz the gain drops signiﬁcantly, thus the sensitivity at the high frequency
is low, but is still usable due to calibrations before operation.
A noise ﬂoor of -100 dB was obtainable with the VNA used for measurement. Thus it
was not possible to measure any isolation below this dB level. Isolation varied a lot for each
channel depending on which antenna was in Rx mode and Tx mode. For most antennas an
isolation of at least 100 dB were obtained and could be approved. Some channels picked
up resonances due to the mechanical structure, others picked up direct crosstalk from a
neighbor channel. As previously mentioned, this could be suppressed by three diﬀerent
suggestions.
The insertion loss for Tx mode was measured and found to be close to simulation from
0.5 - 4 GHz and thus as expected. In the frequency range 4 - 6 GHz the insertion loss
becomes very high and is suspected to be due to a higher loss in the DC blocking capacitors
and the switches than expected.
Chapter 7 describes suggestions for future work. The 8-channel ASN can be expanded
to 32-channels by connecting ﬁve ASN together as showed in ﬁgure 7.1.
It can be concluded that the ASN is competitive to existing products and can be imple-
mented in a medical microwave imaging system.
