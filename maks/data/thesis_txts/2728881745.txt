Abstract
This Master Thesis presents various path loss prediction model for rural mobile macro­
cell for communications between Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) and a ground station
at low altitudes within the 5G network. The models are formulated using empiric data
captured from test flights conducted at Odense Airport. A comparative analysis focused
on the accuracy and applicability of the models was conducted. Two probabilistic mod­
els, the Floating Intercept (FI) and the Close­in (CI) model, derived form the log­distance
path loss models, were investigated. These models include empirically derived fit curves,
where path loss parameters are calculated to achieve optimal fitting.
Additionally, a model based on Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) is explored to capture
signal behaviour and propagation effects like shadowing and fading effects. The dataset
obtained from the test flights undergoes preprocessing and filtering prior to model appli­
cation. Among the evaluated models, the FI model show better performance, followed by
the CI model. This results aligns with the expectations, given that the FI refines the CI for
enhance fitting accuracy. The ANN model displays higher error rates than previous two
models, contrary to expectations.
As a conclusion, the ANN model has the potential for a wider application and general­
ization but for further development, more test flights in diverse scenarios are needed,
including rural, urban and suburban environments.

Introduction
In recent years, technological advancement in various fields had led to improvements in
many everyday applications. Drones are among the examples of this technological ad­
vance.
Drones or unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), were initially conceived for military purposes
at the end of last century. They were designed to fulfill the mission without putting at
risk soldiers in dangerous situations. Nowadays, drone production has shifted towards
a bigger audience, reducing the weight and cost while maintaining safety and enhancing
communication reliability. The fact that they can fly beyond visual line of sight (BVLOS)
has increased the number and variety of applications beyond military domain. In agri­
culture, Puri et al., (2017) [1] conducted a review of different drones in the market and
explored their potential applications. Su et al., 2022 [2] analysed the impact of artificial
intelligence (AI) algorithms and UAV sensing systems on precision agriculture (PA). They
discussed how these technologies would increase crop production while reducing costs
and environmental footprints. UAVs had been gradually introduced in civil engineering.
Seo et al., (2018) in [3] explored the use of drones as a cost­effective alternative for bridge
inspection. Rakha., (2018) [4] reviewed various solutions for analysing and inspecting
building infrastructures, combining UAVs with 3D photogrammetry, Infrared (IR) cameras
and 3D modeling. Furthermore, UAVs have gained importance in package transportation
applications. The introduction of UAVs in package delivery would reduce costs in a hihg
demanded service due to e­commerce. Grzybowski et al., (2020) [5], proposed a solution
to optimize logistic costs. The aim is not only to reduce shipping time, but to perform
multitask, as described by Khosravi and Pishro­Nik., (2020) [6].
These applications are becoming greatly dependant on BLOS scenarios, thus wireless
communications, such as satellite and cellular links, are the pillars of UAV technologies.
The 3rd Generation Partnership (3GPP) is dedicated to creating Mobile Broadband stan­
dards, among other objectives. It has addressed connectivity concerns regarding UAV
applications. Starting from release 15 TR 36.777 [7], 3GPP has tackle the challenge of
establishing a standard for UAV applications over cellular technology. Specifically, Re­
lease 15 focus on LTE technology. In release 17 TR38.901 [8], the study of channel
modeling at higher frequencies has been incorporated.
As previously mentioned, the use of UAVs is expected to rise significantly, increasing
their necessity and applications. Achieving this requires a robust cellular network. The
5G network will fulfill the requirements of modern UAV applications. Compared to previ­
ous generations, it offers higher data transfer rates and lower latency, enabling real­time
transmission of high quality videos, essential aspect for its control. It also introduces Ultra­
Reliable Low Latency (URLL) and high­bandwidth, which enhance drone control.
This led to develop the the concept of U­Space. The U­Space emerged to support com­
mercial operations with drones, entailing greater complexity and automation [9]. Is a set
of services that ensure access to airspace for a large number of drones. The mandatory
services are the following:
• Network Identification Service: Provides the identification of the UAVs operators
and the position of the drone during the flight.
• Geo­Awareness Service: Provides the information of the environment, limitations,
conditions ...etc.
• Flight Clearance Service: Provides an airspace without conflicts between UAVs.

• Traffic Information Service: Provide information to the operators about possible
traffic near the UAV.
In the context of this scenario, the objective of the master thesis is to develop three link­
budget estimation models in the U­Space based on empiric data capture from test flights.
Two of these models are grounded on the log­distance path loss model, the Flotaing
Intercept (FI) and Close­in (CI) models, while the other one utilizes Machine Learning
algorithms, specifically an Artificial Neural Network (ANN). Additionally, a comparative
analysis of between the models will be conducted, considering both the state of art and
the model proposed by 3GPP.
The structure of the Master Thesis is as follows: it starts with an introductory chapter
regarding the state of the Art, Section 2. Following this, the Measurement Setup and
Campaign will be explain in Section 3.2. This section is crucial for result analysis. In
Section 4, the processing of the empiric data will be explained. There is a transformation
of the data from the raw dataset to the filtered dataset used to obtain the path loss models.
Following to that, in Section 5, Section 6 and Section 7 the models are explained. Lastly, in
Section 8 results from the simulation will be presented. In Appendix I there is an overview
of the expected project plan and how it was in the end.

2
State of the Art
In this section, the objective is to explore recent literature on channel modeling for UAV­to­
ground­station links. There are numerous papers that have addressed channel modeling
using same methodologies as in this experiment.
Theodore S. Rappaport and George R. MacCartney are the two main researchers in wire­
less communications and channel modeling. There are several instances of channel mod­
elling using probabilistic models presented in Section 6.1 and 6.2. For instance, in [10],
the close­in and floating­intercept model were used to fit empiric data for Line­of­Sight
(LoS) and Non­Line­of­Sight (NLoS) scenarios acroos frequencies of 28 GHz, 38 GHz,
60 GHz and 73 GHz. This study was conducted in a dense urban location. A similar work
was conducted in [11], operating at 28 GHz and 73 GHz, an outdoor path loss model was
obtained using the models previously mentioned. The results indicate that the floating
intercept and the close­in at a reference distance of 1 meter perform almost identically.
Similar examples can be found in suburban and urban scenarios: [12], [13], [14], [15] or
[16]. All this examples served as methodological guide for the probabilistic models in Sec­
tion 6. These models are based on the 3GPP [7] [8] and WINNER II [17] specifications.
As mentioned, this models will be used in the project but with an aerial­to­ground connec­
tion. There are several papers focused on indoor channel model, using the probabilistic
models mentioned. This papers were not in the interest of the Master Thesis due to the
nature of the scenario and data.
Therefore, given the amount of papers based on empiric path loss models, the literature
review was confined to air­to­ground (AG) or ground­to­air (GA) empiric models based on
the log­distance equation.
In the realm of millimeter waves (mmWave), several studies have been conducted. In
[18] a review was done for different scenarios between up­link (UL) and down­link (DL)
as well as various frequency bands. The review also proposes a model for mmWave in a
dense urban scenario. Measurements were carried out at 28 GHz and 73 GHz in Victoria,
Australia; Paris, France; Mumbai, India and New York, USA. The chosen model was the
floating­intercept, outlined in Section 6.2. The corresponding path loss parameters were
obtained for the different cities.
Various studies regarding channel modelling have been conducted for lower frequency
bands. [19] proposes a dual­model for 970 MHz and 5 GHz for over­water (OW) sce­
nario. In this case, channel stationary distance (SD) was obtained for both bands as well
as the log distance model. As an extension of this work, [20] measurements were con­

4.2.1
Relative Angles
The final goal is to calculate the Path Loss of the UAV­gNB link. Therefore, both anten­
nas gains from the UAV and the gNB are essential. The antenna radiation pattern will
tell us how the antenna radiates and its properties as a function of space. This means,
the gain of the link is going to depend on the UAV position, reason why the link was in­
tended to be maintained in the main beam of the antenna. The radiation is going to be
three­dimensional, and it is common to describe this radiation through two planar patters,
called principal plane patterns, also referred as the antenna patters. This patters are ob­
tained by slicing the 3D radiation in the horizontal and vertical plane. These two planes are
also commonly called azimuth plane pattern and elevation plane pattern[38]. The term
azimuth would refer to the horizon and the angle φ would represent the azimuth angle
from the north position equal to 0º up to the position of the object. Therefore, the range of
the azimuth angle φ would be 0 < φ < 360. On the other hand, the term elevation refers
to the vertical plane and the angle θ represents the angle between the horizontal plane
and the vertical position of the object. Therefore, the range of θ would be −90 < θ < 90,
being 90º pointing to the sky and ­90º to the ground.
Hence, the antenna will point depending on an azimuth angle and elevation angle, or what
is more commonly known, depending on the tilt. Normally, the antennas are down­tilted
to make more efficient the link between an mobile terrestrial User Equipment (UE) and
the gNB. In our case, the antenna was tilted by 4º as mentioned in Table 3.1. For the
aerial UE, the coordinates would be similar to the spherical coordinate system. The data
captured by the scan gives geographical coordinates, i.e., the positioning of the drone
would be described by the latitude, longitude and the altitude. As seen in Figure 4.2, the

The data recorded from the scanner have to be translated into a path loss sample PLi
using Equation 4.4 [24][39]. The PTx would be the transmitted power per reference symbol
in the network. Gtot(θ, φ) is the total gain from the gNB and the antenna gain located in the
drone as a function of the relative elevation angle and the relative azimuth angle of the link.
Note that the drone antenna is omnidirectional and with 0dBi gain. Therefore, the total
antenna gain would be the gNB antenna gain. Lastly, Ri is the Reference Signal Received
Power (RSRP) recorded by the scanner. From the data recorded, it can be seen the
Primary Synchronization Signal RSRP (PSS­RSRP) and the Secondary Synchronization
Signal RSRP (SSS­RSRP). For calculating the path loss, the SSS­RSRP was chosen for
two main reasons. The SSS­RSRP it is helpful to estimate the accuracy signal strength
and gives a more accurate measure of the signal strength on ongoing communication,
which is the scenario under study. The Primary Synchronization Signal (PSS) are used
in initial cell connection, which would not be the focus of the experiment.

5.1
Release 15 TR 36.777
3GPP has published under Release 15 TR 36.777 [7] a study on Enhanced LTE support
for Aerial Vehicles for frequencies from 700 MHz to 800 MHz. In this document, there are
several problems described also experienced in the project, specially in the process of
the data after the test flights. In the Down­link (DL), the number of aerial UEs, compared
to terrestrial UEs, experiencing cell­edge­like radio conditions is higher. This cell­edge­
radio conditions are characterized by poor DL SNIR. Due to the elevated position of the
aerial UEs and Line­of­Sight propagation, the interference in the DL experienced is higher
than in terrestrial UEs. The number of cells detected increases with the height, causing
a more likelihood of interference. Normally eNB antennas are down tilted, a notorious
disadvantage for UAV links. This could cause that depending on the location, the UAV

Probabilistic Path Loss Model
An appropriate path loss model would be needed to described how the signal propagates
and attenuates trough the channel [41]. It is important to consider the main characteristics
of the scenario, rural macrocell and LoS communication. Once the Path Loss model
is chosen, empiric data from the field would be fit into it. The objective is to obtain a
generic Path Loss model, based on already published and utilized models, height and
distance dependent. It was decided to use the Floating Intercept and Close­in models
as probabilistic path loss model due to their popularity and use in scenarios with same
characteristics as the one of this project. This models are based on the log­distance
model, where the receive power attenuates logarithmically with the distance. Following,
both models will be explain in detail.

Another alternative to the close­in path loss model is the floating Intercept (FI) path loss

tively. The channel model is described in Equation 6.3, where β is the intercept in dB and

PLFI(d)[dB] = β + α∙10log10(d) + Xσ
(6.3)
This model is a best fit for empiric data than the close­in path loss model. With the inclu­

at a reference distance that is not necessarily 1m. This makes the fitting as accurate as
possible since it varies with the necessities of the model caused by the empiric data. This
model is more flexible than the Close­In.

Data Reduction
A problem phased at the very beginning was having different RSRP values for a location.
This was not too frequent but there was a concern about the training in the neural network,
hence a reduction was implemented.
The reduction could be made using the mean or the median of the RSRP values in a
same location. The mean is the average value of a group of inputs, it is greatly affected
by great values or values that are far away from that average. The median is the central
value of the input data, thus it is less affected by outliers. So the selection has to be made
by the error and the nature of the input data, i.e., if it contains many atypical inputs. The
error was evaluated by the root mean square error (RMSE), plotted in Figure 7.2b. The
error difference it is not very high, it increases when the amount of points per location
increases too. As said before, the median will evaluate the most common value, so if in a
same location the RSRP follows a tendency but there is also an outlier, the resulting RSRP
will not be highly affected. In Figure 7.2a the mean and the median reduction method is

Over­fitting is the biggest challenge faced in this stage. The goal behind the neural net­

behaviour since it would be ”too” accurate [52], see Figure 7.6a. With the introduction of
new data, the neural network won’t be able to perform and estimate the correct values.
On the other hand, trying to prevent this under performance could be a problem too, what
is called under­fitting. There are several mechanisms to cope with this problem.

Architecture
One of the most important decisions is the architecture of the ANN. It is understand by

complexity of the network increases with the number of connections per neural network,
i.e., depends on the number of layers and neurons per layer. Since it is a process with a
large amount of options, it was restricted to a number of hidden layers and neurons per
layer. At the beginning of the network design, a first training was focused just to see the
range of neurons in one layer. The optimum number was between 10­30 neurons in a

combination of 40 possible architectures of maximum 3 hidden layers and 10,15,20,25 or

include in the experiment, as a consequence it was restricted this way. The problem faced
and data characteristics did not require a high level of complexity but the ambition was
also not to study very basic architectures. Therefore, the final combination of 40 possible
architectures was decided this way.

8.2
3GPP simulation
The 3GPP path loss model showed in Equation 5.5 it was implemented with a Monte Carlo
method [58]. The idea behind is to build a probabilistic model to estimate the average of
the path loss parameters explained in Section 6.2. Through the 3GPP model the path
loss of an specific positioning of the drone will be calculated. We need random variables,
in this case, the noise addition introduced in the Gaussian variable Xσ and the position of
the UAV. Therefore 10 identical simulations were implemented to obtained the path loss
parameters α, β and σ.
The height taken into account goes from 1.5m to 300m, the range permitted by the model.
An altitude vector will be declared with 100 points. In each simulation, the drone will be
located 1000 times randomly inside a box of 800mX800mX300m for each height, making
100,000 random positions for simulation, i.e., after all the simulations, the path loss and
the parameters would be estimated a million times.
For each point, the simulation will output the 3D distance and the path loss. For each
height, the best α and β will be obtained by a non­linear fitting model, following the pro­
cedure explained in Section 6.2. The result of every simulation is an specific value of α, β
and σ for each height. After 10 simulations there would be 10 values of these parameters
for each height. Last step would be to generate a linear fitting model that best fits the
behaviour of the parameters as a function of the height.

The Nelder­Mead method was chosen for several reasons. First, it addresses the problem
to face, find the parameters that minimizes the non.linear fitting. It is applied for non­
linear optimization and after evaluate alternative methods with very similar results, it was
selected because of the short time computation.
The Nelder­Mead algorithm uses a simplex of n dimensions. A simplex is a geometric

form that represents the simplest possible polytope, a geometric object with flat sizes.

dimension simplex is a triangle... etc. The method consists on generating a random­size
simplex and reshape it through a series of iterations one vertex at a time. It shifts towards
the position that minimizes the objective function.
A n+1 point simplex is used for a vector x of n dimensions. In the case of the experiment,
the dimension n of the parameter vector x is 1 and 2, for the close­in and floating­intercept
respectively. First, a random simplex it is defined around the initial guess x0. After, the
simplex it is modified as:

2. Sort the simplex points from the lowest value f(x(1)) to the highest f(x(n + 1)). In
every iteration, the method discards the worst point x(n+1) and accepts other point.

Conclusion
The Master Thesis under the title Link Budget Estimation for 5G communications in U­
Space aims to generate three path loss prediction models. This models will make possible
to estimate the path loss in a communication link between an UAV and a ground station.
The communication is done through the 5G network. The models will be develop from
empiric data capture from test flights conducted in the Hans Christian Andersen Airport
next to Odense. The flights were conducted at a low altitude, from 22­34m and in the
frequency band of 3.5 GHz. One model was generated form the 3GPP indications in Re­
lease 17 TR 38.901 [8] and used as a base model for building MATLAB functions used
later in the project. This model helped to build the scripts while the test flights were con­
ducted. Secondly, two probabilistic models will be generated based on the log­distance
model [41], where the path loss increases logarithmically with the distance. There are two
interpretations, the close­in (CI) model [10][42] and the Floating­Intercept (FI) model[47]
[17]. The CI model depends on the Path Loss Exponent (PLE) a parameter that gives
information about the channel and the attenuation it has depending on the scenario. The
CI is supported by the path loss at a reference distance d0m calculated empirically or if it is
not possible, by the Free Space Path Loss (SPL) derived form Friis Equation, see Equa­
tion 6.2. The FI on the other hand, is the best possible fit for the empiric data, having two
parameters: the slope (α) and the intercept (β). Both models include the shadow effect
in a Gaussian variable with zero mean and standard deviation σ. Last model was based
on Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs). A neural network was design to predict the RSRP
in order to calculate the path loss of the link. Therefore, the project would conclude with
three empiric models and a base model able to predict the path loss of an Air­to­Ground
(A2G) link depending on the 3D distance from the gNB and the UAV height from the sur­
face. The accuracy of the models will be calculate and compare between them. Lastly, a
coverage map was obtained from one of the models.
There is a wide variety of literature about log­distance model and its applicability and
ANNs. Papers related with path loss prediction in an A2G or (G2A) link using the 5G
network in a rural environment were more limited. There were more literature under this
conditions about the log­distance model than about ANNs. A first review of the litera­
ture was done, see Section 2. It was surprising the amount of papers related with the
log­distance model focused on urban and suburban scenarios. Nevertheless, there are
two papers to highlight: [23] and [24]. These experiments were conducted in the same
location, which give a good insight on which steps to follow. However, the design of the
flights was different so the frequency band used. Lastly, there were some literature about
machine learning applicability but not the method chosen in this paper, ANNs. The flights
were conducted in rural macrocells and with Line­of­Sight (LoS) with the gNB.
The first problem arrived with the time spend waiting for the empiric data. In the end two
dataset were obtained from different test flights but only the last one was used for the
models. The flights were conducted in the same way but the raw data was completely
different between both datasets. The second one output more reasonable and physically
possible results so the first dataset was discharged. This give more time than expected
without working with the empiric data, which allowed to focus more on the baseline model
and develop the functions from synthetic data generated with that model.
Once the data was obtained, preprocessing and filtering was done for the three models.
Lastly, a non­linear fit was conducted to the FI and CI in order to obtained the path loss
parameters and the ANN designed was fed with the filtered data to calculate the path loss.

It is worth mentioning the results were not as expected. At the beginning this fact it was

the code implied more time spent on this search than in sophisticating the models. After
reviewing several times, it was concluded that the first dataset had non reliable data and
the 25m flight data from the second dataset was acting as an outlier.
After obtaining the models, the results can be compared. AS it was expected, the FI per­
forms better than the CI model. The floating­intercept it is conceived as a better fit in the
log­distance way. The close­in model is usually used in closer distances, urban, surbur­
ban and indoor locations. As mentioned before, the near­field effects and behaviour of
waves close to the antenna is included in the path loss at a reference distance param­
eter, therefore, the behaviour at larger distances could differ greatly from the free­space
path loss characteristics. However, the results did not differ from the ones obtained from
the floating­intercept, which is more adaptable. This means the communications is under
close to free­space propagation conditions. It can be assumed from the values of the
path loss exponent, very close to 2, value of free­space propagation. The differences in
prediction were never greater than 0.5 dB, therefore, unless the calculation needs to be
very precise it is indifferent to use the FI and CI. This statement can be extended to every
rural macrocell UAV­gNB connection with more test flights in different locations. The small
difference between these two models could be explain because the communication was
kept under the main lobe and the filtering. This reduce the shadowing and fading effects,
lowering the standard deviation compare to other papers.
The ANN was expected to be more accurate in the predictions since it is train to learn
about the shadowing and fading effects from the propagation. The RMSE oscillated be­
tween 1.5­2dBs. This is not a huge error rate but compare to the FI or CI where never was
higher than 1dB, it is far less accurate. This could be caused by the different preprocess­
ing done for the model and the inclusion of data from 800­1500m which was excluded in
the CI and FI models. In the end, the ANN would be able to learn from complex relations
between the attributes and target, to predict a complex behaviour, such as the shadowing
or fading. The fact of performed test flights in one scenario had limited the training. In
the end, the feature selection affected the NN design and training. With a wide variety
of scenarios, the features could have been different, like antenna characteristics (gain,
MIMO dimension, tilt, frequency band... etc) or the topography (buildings, hills... etc).
Feeding the ANN with this kind of data could make the model more accurate but since
it was information that did not vary in our scenario it was not included since it would not
have contribute to the training of the network.
This feature in ANN design and the kind of information it can be included that is not quan­
tize, like the topography, would generate a very accurate model and very versatile. It
would no be needed to change from urban,suburban or rural or depending on the fre­
quency. For this a big amount of data in different environments and different configura­
tions it is needed. Also note that the NN design process it took more than what it was
expected since there are lot of parameters to modify and the is no step­by­step process
but finding the best combinations of them.
Lastly, generating a coverage map from the models would be the final goal. This would
allow to estimate the link budget for U­Space communications, nevertheless, it was im­

Future Steps
The future steps and future work can be explain regarding the final goal. The Master The­
sis it stands as a starting point of a possible bigger project. Ig the final goal is to generate
a coverage map for the entire country of Denmark in order to design a UAV network able
to navigate in the country without connection problems.
To generate a coverage model with this characteristics, coverage model for different sce­
narios need to be develop. Therefore, more test flights need to be conducted, not only
in urban or suburban scenarios but also in rural scenarios. A large dataset with differ­
ent characteristics will be needed to develop a better and more generalized model. For
example, to develop a more generalized rural path loss model, more flights in different
scenarios need to be perform in order to see how the dissimilar antenna characteristics
affect the path loss. The dimension of the MIMO, the type of antenna, the pointing (az­
imuth and elevation), frequency band, radiation patters... etc. Moreover, the flight design
could explore more the edge of the main lobe in order to calculate the path loss of this
type of communications.
Regarding the test flights design, apart from exploring the edges of the main lobe of the
antennas, the secondary loves can be also explore. This way, it can concluded if the
noise is too high to hinder a link between the UAV and the gNB or not. This means that
the relative elevation angle and relative azimuth angle need to have a wider range. As
could be seen in the test flights design, the relative azimuth was varying around 0º but
never getting greater than 3º or ­3º. The distance and specially the altitude have to have
wider range too. The UAV are considered to flight up to 300m and 2000m distance. This
would give a proper overview of all the possible connections and how the signal behaves.
More specific further steps related with the code would be to develop a more accuracy
filter function. The upload of the data from the Excel sheet to MATLAB and all the filtering
done in the program, was rather rudimentary. If develop a MATLAB app or a live function
that could filter big datasets only tweaking few parameters would reduce the implemen­
tation and simulation time. There is also about filtering by height discharging data that
deviates a certain quantity from a mean, i.e., implement a Gaussian filter.
Another feature that can be improved and it was a handicap for this project was the an­
tenna gain. Not having the radiation patterns and fitting a curve for the radiation patter
give a certain amount of inaccuracy. If the goal is to generate a model as accurate as
possible then knowing the radiation is crucial, specially because is angle dependant. If
the project follow to explore the main lobe edges or the secondary lobes then having an
accurate radiation pattern is essential.
Lastly, every step should be included in a executable software. This program will be able
to calculate the path loss of any geographical point at any allowed altitude in Denmark or
even more, every country with antenna database.

SNIR
The signal­to­noise­plus­interference is a metric that indicates the quality of the signal and
is calcualted as:
SNIR =
Psignal
Pnoise + Pinterference
A higher SNIR indicates a good quality of the signal and lower SNIR indicates a higher
noise and/or interference power. As can be seen for example in Figure A.1, there is a
low point around 1200m for the PCI 532 as well as a high point in PCI 531. This would
mean interference between these two cells. Also note that the SNIR is decreasing with
the distance, which is expected as the power of the signal will decrease also with the
signal.

Project Plan
In this section the initial project plan and how it ended up being would be comment on.
The learning related with project planning would be included too.
First of all, the initial project plan is shown in Table I.1. As can be see compared to
the actual planning, it was well design. In the end, it was estimated to have 2 datasets
from two different days of test flights. Until the arrival of the data, a basemodel would be
implemented after reading the appropriate literature. In the end, only the 3GPP model was
implemented but at the beginning a MATLAB toolbox from the Vienna University, called
Vienna Simulation, was considerate. In the end it was discharged due to its complexity
and the amount of parameters there would be needed to approximate.
From the actual planning, see Table I.2, the same line was followed with small changes.
At the beginning, a basemodel would be implemented starting from a MATLAB script
from a DTU course taking in June 2022. After the arrival of the data, some functions
would be already done, in the basemodel stage, so the implementation of the models,
both the probabilistic and Machine Learning would be faster. In the end, there was a
problem, were the results from the first data set were not expected, but not only that,
some were not even physically possible. After the arrival of the 2nd dataset and some
changes, reasonable results were obtained. Finally, 3 weeks were dedicated only to write
and small modifications to the script. As can be seen in the Table I.1, the occurrence of
errors was taking into account.
What it was learn from the planning process, first of all, it is very important to have a prior
knowledge on the topic and knowing how much is going to take to implement things. For
example, if I need to elaborate path loss models from empiric data, I would make a better
plan. Secondly, it is important to give time to solve errors because errors will happen. If
in the end there are less errors as expected then the project can go further than expected
which is always good. Lastly, writing the report have to always go through the project.
Due to the nature of this one, at the beginning there was no much to write about and after
the arrival of the data I was sumerge into the MATLAB implementation that I did not had
time to write. Nevertheless, it is important to write alongside the project so the final report
is as refine as possible.
