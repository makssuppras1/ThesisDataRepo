Abstract
Wind energy has become one of the fastest growing renewable energy sources as the de-
mand for carbon-neutral energy continues to grow. Modern wind turbines are highly de-
pendent on pitch control systems for power regulation and structural protection, but the
systems, especially electro-hydraulic pitch configurations, have a high probability of fail-
ure during operation. Therefore, the realization of reliable and fault-tolerant pitch con-
trol method has become an important research direction to improve the performance of
wind turbines and reduce operation and maintenance costs. Integral sliding mode con-
trol(ISMC), a robust control method that can effectively enhance fault tolerance. By using
deep neural networks to estimate the transient function in ISMC, the robustness of the con-
troller can be further improved, while maintaining Lyapunov-based stability guarantees.
Therefore, this project adopts a DNN-enhanced ISMC approach to address the aforemen-
tioned challenges in wind turbine system. The study investigates methods to improve the
accuracy of DNN-based transient function estimation and compares the proposed control
strategy with baseline methods.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
With the global emphasis on carbon neutrality and the rapid development of renewable
energy, wind power has become one of the fastest-growing energy sources. Modern wind
turbines are increasing in both size and capacity, presenting greater challenges for control
system design. Among the critical subsystems, the pitch control system is essential for
adjusting the blade angle to regulate aerodynamic torque and power output. It enables op-
timal power capture under partial load conditions and ensures structural protection during
high wind speeds by reducing excessive mechanical loads [1].
However, wind turbines operate in dynamic environments characterized by system nonlin-
earity, external disturbances, and parameter uncertainties, making pitch control a complex
engineering problem. Moreover, offshore wind installations introduce additional difficul-
ties due to the remote and harsh environment, which increases the time and cost associated
with maintenance. In addition to the direct maintenance cost, unplanned downtime results
in lost energy production and missed revenue, further emphasizing the need for reliable
control solutions.
Studies like [2, 3, 4], have shown that the pitch regulation system is one of the most failure-
prone components in wind turbines, and faults in this system can lead to severe operational
consequences [5, 6, 7]. High-power wind turbines often employ electro-hydraulic pitch
systems, which provide superior power density, robustness, and smoother dynamic perfor-
mance compared to their purely electric counterparts [8, 9, 10]. To enhance system avail-
ability and reduce maintenance requirements, advanced fault detection and fault-tolerant
control strategies for the electro-hydraulic pitch subsystem are being actively investigated.
These methods have the potential to improve turbine availability and extend turbine up-
time by enabling early failure diagnosis and corrective action without requiring immediate
shutdown.
1.2
Related Works
In many industrial process control applications, the Proportional–Integral (PI) controller
remains one of the most widely used control strategies in modern automatic control sys-
tems. Originally proposed by Ziegler and Nichols [11], the PI controller features a simple
algorithmic structure that is easy to implement, while offering a good degree of robustness
suitable for most linear systems. The inclusion of the integral term effectively eliminates

steady-state errors, enabling the system output to accurately track the reference signal.
Even in more complex or hierarchical control architectures, the PI controller is frequently
employed as a foundational or inner-loop controller due to its reliability and ease of tun-
ing. Consequently, in wind turbine systems, PI controllers are commonly adopted for
regulating the pitch control system [12, 13]. While these studies demonstrate the capa-
bility of pitch angle control and hydraulic actuators to track reference signals effectively,
they generally overlook the influence of practical nonlinearities such as friction and actua-
tor resistance. Moreover, potential issues such as fluid leakage during extended operation
of hydraulic systems are not taken into account. To address these limitations, further op-
timization of the control strategy is required to enhance system robustness and long-term
reliability.
In recent years, deep learning (DL), in particular reinforcement learning (RL) has gained
considerable attention as a promising approach for enhancing the robustness of wind tur-
bine systems. Unlike traditional model-based control methods, RL does not rely on precise
mathematical models, and it demonstrates strong adaptability and fault-tolerance. More-
over, as more data becomes available, RL exhibits significant advantages in online learn-
ing and long-term performance optimization. For instance, Hongyang Dong and Shuyue
Lin [14] incorporates an incremental model into the RL framework as a surrogate to cap-
ture real-time system variations. By employing an actor–critic structure, it achieves opti-
mal control performance and enables the system to adapt to complex disturbances and fault
scenarios. Sihame Chouiekh et al. [15] designs an online-updated cost function and inte-
grates it with a critic neural network and an adaptive dynamic programming framework,
allowing the controller to effectively respond to varying disturbances. Martín de Frutos
et al. [16] employs double deep Q-learning to achieve multi-objective optimization, suc-
cessfully maximizing energy production while minimizing noise emissions. Jingjie Xie et
al. [17] proposes an incremental model-based RL method, where a recursive least squares
algorithm is used to dynamically estimate the input matrix, thereby enhancing real-time
adaptability and control efficiency. While these studies demonstrate that reinforcement
learning can significantly improve system robustness, a critical limitation remains: due to
its model-free nature, RL generally lacks rigorous Lyapunov-based stability analysis. The
absence of such mathematical guarantees implies that, under certain disturbances, the con-
trol system may exhibit divergent behavior in practice. Therefore, the ability to establish
system stability through Lyapunov methods remains a crucial aspect in the deployment of
control strategies for wind turbine systems.
To mitigate the influence of nonlinear uncertainties on the system, while enabling rigorous
stability guarantees via Lyapunov-based methods, sliding mode control, a robust control
method that introduces a sliding surface from the initial moment to reject matched un-

certainties, has attracted attention due to its inherent robustness and ability to render the
closed-loop system insensitive to disturbances. The discontinuous nature of SMC ensures
finite-time convergence of system states to a pre-defined sliding surface, upon which the
system exhibits the desired dynamic behavior. In general, SMC is regarded as a model-free
control strategy, as it does not require an accurate mathematical model of the controlled
system [18, 19, 20, 21], but instead depends only on the bounds on the uncertainty terms.
However, an important extension known as integral sliding mode (ISM) control represents
a notable exception [22]. ISM control incorporates a more robust structure by designing a
sliding manifold composed of two components: one corresponding to the classical SMC
design, and the other known as the transient function, which ensures that the system states
lie on the sliding surface from the initial time instant. The design of this transient function
relies on the nominal dynamics of the system under ideal, disturbance-free conditions. For
this reason, ISMC is often considered a model-based approach. By introducing an integral
term into the sliding surface, ISM control offers superior disturbance rejection capabilities
compared to conventional SMC, particularly in the presence of persistent disturbances or
modeling uncertainties—such as fluid leakage and friction effects commonly observed in
electro-hydraulic systems.
In recent years, several studies have been devoted to improving the ISM framework. For
example, various extensions have been proposed in the literature: in [23], both matched
and unmatched uncertainties were explicitly addressed to enhance robustness; a second-
order integral sliding mode control strategy was introduced in [24] to improve control
precision under complex dynamics; and more recently, a discrete-time formulation was
developed in [25] to facilitate implementation in digital control systems. In addition, var-
ious studies have investigated the application of ISM control in electro-hydraulic wind
turbine systems. For instance, in [26], an adaptive robust ISM controller was developed
to address system uncertainties and external disturbances by constructing a nonlinear Lya-
punov function and incorporating a parameter estimation mechanism. However, the tran-
sient function was not constructed based on the nominal dynamics of the system. In [27],
an advanced state improved exponential reaching law-based integral sliding mode was
proposed, where control parameters were optimized using the Harris Hawks Optimization
algorithm. This study accounted for nonlinear disturbances such as friction and pressure
variations. Nevertheless, it did not provide a stability analysis of the transient function
behavior. These studies indicate that, in the implementation of ISM controllers, assump-
tions such as reliance on the nominal system dynamics are inherently restrictive and often
difficult to satisfy in practical applications. As a result, the estimation and construction of
the transient function present a significant and ongoing challenge.

tegration of deep neural networks (DNN) with integral sliding mode (ISM) control of-
fers a promising solution. When sufficient system operation data are available, DNNs
are well-suited to compensate for the lack of accurate model information. Furthermore,
the stability of such a DNN-augmented ISM control framework can often be established
through Lyapunov-based analysis [28, 29]. There has been substantial research on the
integration of DNNs with sliding mode control (SMC) [30, 31, 32], where DNNs are typi-
cally employed to adaptively tune key control parameters via their learning capabilities. In
particular, within the context of DNN-assisted ISM control, a notable approach involves
using a pair of shallow neural networks to estimate the system’s drift dynamics and con-
trol effectiveness gain, respectively [33]. This method has been analytically shown—via
Lyapunov stability theory—to ensure that the system states remain ultimately bounded,
while maintaining the robustness and performance benefits of ISM. Furthermore, Study
[34] extends the aforementioned approach to accommodate nonlinear systems with par-
tially unknown dynamics and time-varying state constraints. In this work, a time-varying
control principle is introduced, which is designed based on the minimum distance between
the current system state and the nearest constraint boundary. This mechanism enables the
system to converge to the desired target state while ensuring that it avoids infeasible or
unsafe regions in the state space.
Building upon prior research, the study investigates the possibility of applying DNN-ISM
to pitch system in wind turbine to exploit its robustness towards uncertainty. Furthermore,
the capability of adapting this online is tested under fault for an improved fault rejection
capability. This choice is motivated by several advantages of the DNN-ISM approach:
it allows for Lyapunov-based stability analysis, enables effective estimation of both sys-
tem drift term and control effectiveness term, and supports real-time learning and adapta-
tion. The proposed control strategy will be applied to an electro-hydraulic actuated wind
turbine system, aiming to ensure accurate blade pitch angle tracking in the presence of
nonlinear uncertainties such as friction and aerodynamic disturbances. The effectiveness
of the DNN-ISM method will be evaluated and compared against conventional control
methods under various conditions, including external disturbances and hydraulic leakage
scenarios.

Theoretical Foundations of Control
Strategies
This chapter presents the theoretical foundations of two conventional control strategies,
along with their integration with neural networks. The traditional methods include the PID
controller, which serves as a baseline, and the ISM controller, which is designed to enhance
robustness against system uncertainties. A comparative analysis of these controllers in the
context of a wind turbine system is conducted to justify their selection and to motivate the
incorporation of a DNN into the ISM framework.
3.1
Baseline Control Strategy Design
The PID controller is a widely used and well-established control strategy. Due to its sim-
ple structure, ease of implementation, and minimal parameter tuning requirements, it is
frequently applied in various industrial scenarios, either as a standalone solution or as the
foundational component of more complex control schemes. The proportional, integral,
and derivative terms are used to adjust the controller’s response by influencing present
error, accumulated past error, and predicted future error, respectively. In this project, the
PID controller has been selected as the baseline for evaluating and comparing the perfor-
mance of alternative control methods.

3.2
Classical ISM Control Strategy Design
Due to the limited robustness of the PI controller against uncertainties and external distur-
bances, as well as its relatively slow transient response, this project extends the baseline
PID controller by incorporating an ISM controller [22]. The ISM control strategy offers
advantages, including strong robustness, fast dynamic response, and suitability for nonlin-
ear systems. Sliding mode control enables the system to rapidly converge to a predefined
sliding surface and subsequently reach the target state. As a result, this method exhibits
adaptability to system uncertainties and nonlinear disturbances, while maintaining a fast

3.3
Overview of DNN and Long-short Term Memory
According to Study [40], the DNN employed in this project is a type of feedforward neural
network (FNN). A FNN is a basic type of artificial neural network in which information
flows in one direction—from input to output—without any feedback connections. Its ar-
chitecture typically consists of an input layer, one or more hidden layers with nonlinear
activation functions, and an output layer. FNNs are trained using the backpropagation
algorithm, which adjusts the network weights by minimizing the prediction error through
gradient descent. They are well-suited for static function approximation and regression
tasks, offering simplicity, efficient training, and ease of implementation.
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) is a specialized type of recurrent neural network (RNN)
designed to model temporal sequences and long-term dependencies. Its architecture in-
cludes memory cells and gating mechanisms (input, forget, and output gates) that regulate
information flow over time. LSTM networks are particularly effective in handling time-
series data, as they mitigate the vanishing gradient problem and retain relevant historical
information, making them suitable for dynamic system modeling and sequence prediction.

4.3.3
Offline Training for DNN
The weights of the neural networks ˆΦ and ˆΨ are initialized through pre-training. In this
project, two neural networks with architectures identical to ˆΦ and ˆΨ were constructed in
Python. Each network consists of two hidden layers and uses the tanh as the activation
function. The input and output dimensions are set to match those of ˆΦ and ˆΨ, forming
two FNN structures. Using the simulation results of the ISM controller under fault-free
conditions, as described in the previous section, the data of ˙x are collected as the ground
truth of ˆf(x)+ˆg(x)u0, while the corresponding state x is used as input. Both networks are
trained simultaneously for 1000 epochs using the mean squared error (MSE) as the loss
function. After training, the learned weights are exported and used as the initial weights
for ˆΦ and ˆΨ in the MATLAB implementation. It should be noted that weight initialization
through pre-training does not guarantee immediate stability once the neural networks are
incorporated into the control loop. Since the training process in Python aims to minimize
the global MSE, some transient behavior may still occur during simulation before the
controller output reaches a stable state. Further details of the offline training process will
be discussed in the next chapter.

included in the system state x used as the input. Thus, the neural network can simply pass
through the value of vβ from the input to the output without the need for actual prediction.
To investigate the causes of the poor prediction performance for the other two components
and explore possible solutions, two hypotheses are proposed:
1. The architecture of DNN 1 may be too small. The two hidden layers with 16 neurons
each might not be sufficient for modeling the system, and a larger or deeper network
could be necessary.
2. The input information provided to DNN 1 may be insufficient. Predicting the output
from a single time-step state x could be too limiting, and it might be necessary to
use the system states over a time window as input for better prediction.
It is worth noting that both of these approaches can still ensure Lyapunov stability when
applied to ISM, since the first approach only increases the network’s capacity, and the
second treats the extended input as part of the system state.
Additionally, to further improve the prediction accuracy during offline training, the output
of the neural network will be reduced from three to two dimensions, predicting only ˙vβ

and ˙Fh. As previously discussed, vβ does not actually require prediction. This adjustment
allows the network to focus its full capacity on learning the remaining two components

Conclusion
Based on the investigations presented in the previous chapters, this project explored the
application of DNN-based ISM control in enhancing the fault tolerance of pitch systems.
Through offline training of neural networks, it is observed that smaller networks, due to
their limited number of neurons, may not be sufficiently capable of accurately estimat-
ing the transient function. This limitation can be addressed by increasing the amount of
input information or by enlarging the network size. Under nominal conditions, the term
f(x)+g(x)u0 in the transient function resembles the time derivative of the system state x.
Therefore, providing a time window of state inputs improves the performance of offline
training. It is also observed that increasing the window length alone does not significantly
enhance prediction accuracy; substantial improvement is only achieved when both the net-
work capacity and the window length are increased. However, larger networks result in
longer inference times, which limits their feasibility for real-time applications.
In real-time simulations conducted in MATLAB, the PI, PIISMC, ISMDNN1, and IS-
MDNN3 controllers all perform comparably under nominal conditions. However, when
an internal leakage fault is introduced, none of the controllers are able to maintain satis-
factory performance, and the differences between them are minimal. The addition of the
ISM module and the DNN does not lead to significant improvements. Furthermore, the
DNN fails to accurately estimate the transient function in the faulty scenario. Two pos-
sible explanations are proposed: first, the parameters of the PI controller may have been
tuned so effectively that a relatively large fault is required to degrade its performance, in
which case additional modules provide limited benefit. Second, the weight update strat-
egy used for online adaptation differs from the offline training procedure, which can lead
to discrepancies between offline training results and online prediction performance.
