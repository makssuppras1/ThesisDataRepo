Abstract
Facial recognition systems have become pivotal in various security and identification
applications due to their reliability and non-intrusiveness. However, the performance
of these systems heavily relies on the quality of the captured biometric sample. A
critical concern is the potential for bias when dealing with different demographic
groups such as ethnicity, gender, and age.
This thesis aims to evaluate the performance of several Quality Components of the
Open Source Face Image Quality (OFIQ) framework across demographic groups
to identify any potential biases. OFIQ is a reference implementation for the in-
ternational standard ISO/IEC DIS 29794-5[1], developed by the Federal Office for
Information Security (BSI)[2]. This standard aims to provide a consistent method
for assessing facial image quality, which is crucial for various applications. OFIQ
evaluates facial images using 34 different quality assessment components. The out-
put of OFIQ’s quality measures is a quality score in the range of 0-100, with a higher
score indicating better quality.
The thesis examined the consistency and fairness of these quality components across
demographic variables. For this purpose, the two datasets VGG-Face2 and Balanced
Faces in the Wild (BFW) were selected for their comprehensive demographic rep-
resentations. This work is limited to a subset of OFIQ quality components, namely:
luminance mean, luminance variance, under-exposure prevention, over-exposure pre-
vention, natural color, and unified quality score.
Key findings revealed significant performance differences of aforementioned quality
components across demographic groups. For ethnicity, the African American group
exhibited very high discard rates in luminance mean and luminance variance, with
discard gaps of up to 54 % and 17 %, respectively, indicating clear biases. The unified
quality score, which is based on a model from MagFace, showed the worst perform-
ance for the East Asian group, with a mean discard gap of 10.03 % in the critical
0-50 scalar range. This suggests that potential biases in image quality assessment
are not exclusively limited to OFIQ but also reflect biases inherent in the underlying
MagFace model.
In terms of gender, males were found to be assigned higher quality scores than fe-
males, especially considering luminance mean, with discard percentage gaps reaching
up to 25 %. Looking at the unified quality score, males were scored marginally bet-
ter than females with a max discard percentage difference of 11.6 %, indicating a
moderate bias against females in these assessments.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The introduction will set the stage by outlining the background and motivation
behind this thesis and defining its scope and objectives.
Following this a brief
overview of works related to this field will be presented.
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, biometric system have become an integral part of various security
and identification processes, helping to assist and even replace manual tasks. This
shift is greatly influenced by their ability to provide reliable and fast identification.
Among these systems, facial recognition has gained widespread adoption across mul-
tiple sectors, notably in law enforcement [3], border control [4] and personal device
security.
Biometrics refers to the measurement and statistical analysis of people’s unique
physical characteristics. The main premise of biometric authentication is that every
individual can be accurately identified due to their intrinsic physical traits.
Biometrics includes a wide range identification methodologies, using characteristics
such as fingerprints, iris, face and voice. Each method has their own strengths and
weaknesses, however face recognition stands out as being one of the least intrus-
ive methods, since it does not require direct eye contact, and can be done from a
distance.
Due to this widespread adoption and the critical roles that facial recognition systems
play in these areas, it is imperative to ensure that these systems treat all individuals
equally, regardless of their demographic characteristics such as ethnicity, gender, and
age. The potential for bias in facial recognition technology can lead to significant
consequences, including unjust treatment, discrimination, and a lack of trust in
these systems. Ensuring fairness and accuracy in facial recognition not only upholds
ethical standards but also enhances the reliability and efficacy of these technologies.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.1.1
Current Challenges Related to Demographic Variables
Despite the advancements in facial recognition technology, significant challenges
remain, particularly concerning the accurate and fair identification of individuals
across diverse demographic groups.
Variations in physical properties related to
ethnicity, gender, and age can impact the facial recognition task, leading to potential
biases and inconsistencies.
Ethnicity
Ethnic diversity introduces a wide range of facial features and skin tones that can
affect the performance of facial recognition systems. Individuals from different ethnic
backgrounds may have distinct facial structures, such as variations in eye shape, nose
width, and cheekbone prominence.
Moreover, skin tone and lighting conditions are interrelated factors that can impact
the system’s ability to accurately detect and analyze facial features. Since both skin
tone and lighting conditions can individually pose challenges, their combination
can further complicate the system’s ability to accurately detect and analyze facial
features, potentially affecting the performance and accuracy of facial recognition
systems.
Gender
Gender-related physical differences, such as variations in facial hair, makeup usage,
and overall facial structure, present significant challenges for facial recognition sys-
tems. These differences can lead to variability in key facial landmarks, which are
critical for accurate identification.
For example, facial hair can obscure certain features, while makeup can alter the per-
ceived shape and color of the face. Such alterations may cause the system to struggle
with consistent recognition, potentially leading to mismatches or false rejections, es-
pecially when the same individual appears with or without these modifications.
Age
Aging introduces dynamic changes in facial appearance, including wrinkles, sagging
skin, and changes in facial contours. These age-related transformations can signific-
antly impact the accuracy of facial recognition systems, as the system must adapt
to the evolving features of an individual’s face over time.
1.1.2
Face Image Quality Assessment as a Tool
The efficacy of a facial recognition system depends heavily on the quality of the
captured biometric sample. High-quality images ensure that the facial features are
captured with sufficient detail, enabling the algorithms to perform accurate matches.

Poor-quality images can lead to higher error rates in facial recognition, including
false matches and false rejections.
To prevent low-quality samples from being stored in a reference database, Face
Image Quality Assessment (FIQA) is used to ensure sufficient image quality. FIQA
aims to provide a comparable metric that reflects the quality of a facial image, in
terms of its suitability for facial recognition purposes.
1.1.3
OFIQ
Open Source Facial Image Quality (OFIQ) [5] is one such FIQA tool designed to
evaluate and ensure the quality of facial images used in recognition systems. De-
veloped by the Federal Office for Information Security (BSI) [2], OFIQ serves as a
reference implementation compliant with the international standard ISO/IEC DIS
29794-5 [1]. This standard aims to provide a consistent and reliable method for
assessing facial image quality, which is crucial for various biometric applications.
OFIQ provides comprehensive feedback about the quality of facial images based on
34 distinct metrics, including an overall quality score. These metrics are categor-
ized into two main types: capture-related and subject-related quality components.
Capture-related components refer to factors that influence the quality of the im-
age during the moment of capture, such as lighting, focus, and exposure. These
components are largely dependent on the environment and the technical aspects of
the imaging process. On the other hand, subject-related components focus on the
characteristics of the face itself, such as facial expression, pose, and the presence of
occlusions (like glasses or facial hair). These are intrinsic to the subject and can
vary significantly from one individual to another.
By assessing both capture-related and subject-related factors, OFIQ ensures a de-
tailed and holistic evaluation of image quality. A table of all 34 quality components
can be seen in appendix figure 6.1.
All quality components return a native value and a scalar value. The scalar val-
ues are mapped to a 0 to 100 scale and most follow a higher-is-better convention,
meaning that a scalar value of 100 is the optimal score for the respective quality
component.

Examined Quality Components
Six out of the 34 total quality components from OFIQ will be analyzed in this thesis,
all of which will be the scalar representations. The following capture-related quality
components from the OFIQ tool will be evaluated, as they were specifically chosen
for their high likelihood of being affected by ethnicity, which is the main focus of
this thesis:
Luminance Mean Scalar
The luminance mean quality component measures the average brightness of an im-
age. It is calculated as the absolute value of the deviation of the mean luminance
from the median of possible values. This measure is crucial for ensuring that images
are neither too dark nor too bright, which can reduce the accuracy of face recognition
algorithms.
A luminance mean scalar value of zero indicates an image where the average lumin-
ance is either extremely low or extremely high. In other words, it could be very
dark or very bright, both of which can adversely affect the visibility of facial fea-
tures. Conversely, a luminance mean scalar value of 100 represents an image that is
perfectly balanced in terms of brightness. This ideal level of brightness ensures that
facial features are clearly visible without being under-exposed or over-exposed [1].
Luminance Variance Scalar
The luminance variance quality component assesses the contrast in an image by
measuring the variance in luminance values. The goal is to have a balanced contrast
that enhances the visibility of facial features without making the image too uniform
or too varied in brightness. High-quality images should ideally have a moderate
level of luminance variance.
A luminance variance scalar value of zero indicates an image with very low contrast,
where luminance values are nearly uniform across the entire image. This lack of
contrast can make it difficult to distinguish facial features, leading to reduced ac-
curacy in face recognition. Such images may appear flat and lack detail, hindering
the algorithm’s ability to identify unique characteristics of the face.
Conversely, a luminance variance scalar value of 100 represents an image with op-
timal contrast, where there are significant but balanced variations in brightness

Under-exposure Prevention Scalar
The under-exposure prevention quality component checks for under-exposure by
determining the proportion of pixels with low luminance values.
Specifically, it
calculates the proportion of pixels for which luminance values fall within the range [0,
25]. An image is considered under-exposed if it has a high proportion of dark pixels,
which can obscure facial details. Ensuring under-exposure prevention is critical for
fairness, as darker-skinned individuals might be more prone to under-exposure in
certain lighting conditions.
An under-exposure prevention scalar value of zero indicates that the image has
a high proportion of dark pixels within the specified luminance range, making it
severely under-exposed. This level of under-exposure can obscure facial features
and significantly reduce the accuracy of face recognition algorithms. Such images
are problematic because critical details needed for accurate recognition are lost in
the shadows.
Conversely, an under-exposure prevention scalar value of 100 represents an image
that is perfectly exposed with respect to under-exposure. This means the image has
a very low proportion of dark pixels within the range [0, 25], ensuring that facial
features are clearly visible and not obscured by shadows [1].
Over-exposure Prevention Scalar
The over-exposure prevention quality component evaluates over-exposure by calcu-
lating the proportion of pixels with high luminance values. Specifically, it measures
the proportion of pixels for which luminance values fall within the range [247, 255],
indicating hot spots or overly bright areas.
Preventing over-exposure is import-
ant for maintaining the visibility of facial features, particularly for individuals with
lighter skin tones who may be more susceptible to over-exposure.
An over-exposure prevention scalar value of zero indicates that the image has a high
proportion of pixels within the specified luminance range, making it severely over-
exposed. This level of over-exposure can wash out facial features and significantly
reduce the accuracy of face recognition algorithms. Such images are problematic
because critical details needed for accurate recognition are lost in the bright areas.
Conversely, an over-exposure prevention scalar value of 100 represents an image that
is perfectly exposed with respect to over-exposure. This means the image has a very
low proportion of pixels within the range [247, 255], ensuring that facial features are
not washed out by excessive brightness [1].

Natural Colour Scalar
The natural colour quality component measures the plausibility of the skin color
by checking if the RGB values fall within a natural range when converted to the
CIELAB color space. This measure ensures that the colors in the image appear
realistic, which is vital for recognizing individuals accurately regardless of their
ethnicity.
A natural colour scalar value of zero indicates that the skin tones in the image fall
outside the natural range, resulting in unrealistic or unnatural skin colors. This can
occur due to improper lighting, color balance issues, or other factors that distort
the true skin tone. Such inaccuracies in skin color can hinder the performance of
face recognition algorithms, as the algorithm may struggle to correctly identify and
differentiate between individuals.
Conversely, a natural colour scalar value of 100 represents an image where the skin
tones are perfectly within the natural range, resulting in realistic and plausible skin
colors. This ideal level of color accuracy ensures that facial features are represented
as true to life as possible, which is essential for the reliability and accuracy of face
recognition algorithms [1].
Unified Quality Score
The unified quality score is a metric that evaluates the overall quality of facial images
for biometric applications. It is derived through a systematic process that includes
resizing, cropping, and normalizing the image, followed by passing it through a pre-
trained MagFace model specialized in facial recognition[6]. The model generates
a feature vector, from which the Euclidean norm is calculated and then mapped
to a quality component value using a sigmoid function. The unified quality score
ensures that images are suitable for accurate biometric comparisons, maintaining
consistency and reliability across various datasets and conditions, and is essential
for effective and fair biometric systems.
A unified quality score of zero indicates that the image is of very poor quality for
biometric applications. This could be due to a variety of factors such as improper
lighting, blurriness, occlusions, or any other distortions that significantly hinder the
ability of the recognition algorithm to extract useful features.
Conversely, a unified quality score of 100 represents an image that is of excellent
quality for biometric applications. This indicates that the image has been captured
under optimal conditions, with proper lighting, clear visibility of facial features, and
minimal distortions [1].

1.5
Literature Review
1.5.1
Assessing Bias in Face Image Quality Assessment
Babnik and Štruc (2022) [11] analyzed demographic biases in various Face Image
Quality Assessment (FIQA) methods using the Balanced Faces in the Wild (BFW)
dataset. The study evaluated general-purpose IQA, supervised FIQA (sFIQA), and
unsupervised FIQA (uFIQA) methods across three state-of-the-art Face Recognition
(FR) models: ArcFace, VGGFace2, and FaceNet.
Their findings indicated that
all FIQA methods were more affected by racial variations than by gender, with
supervised and unsupervised FIQA methods showing a strong bias favoring white
individuals. Furthermore, they found that methods less biased racially performed
worse overall.

1.5.2
Evaluating Impact of Race in Facial Recognition across
Machine Learning and Deep Learning Algorithms
Coe and Atay (2021) [12] investigated the impact of race on facial recognition per-
formance across machine learning and deep learning algorithms. They evaluated
racial bias across five machine learning algorithms (SVC, LDA, KNN, DT, and LR)
and three deep learning algorithms (AlexNet, VGG16, and ResNet50) using ra-
cially imbalanced and balanced datasets. Their results showed that deep learning
algorithms outperformed machine learning algorithms in mitigating racial bias, with
VGG16 being the most effective in reducing bias.
1.5.3
On the Potential of Algorithm Fusion for Demographic
Bias Mitigation in Face Recognition
Kolberg et al. (2024) [13] explored the use of algorithm fusion to mitigate demo-
graphic bias in face recognition systems. The study benchmarked 12 face recognition
systems and applied multiple fusion techniques to improve fairness across gender,
skin color, and combined subgroups. Their experimental results demonstrated that
while it was possible to improve fairness for individual demographics, achieving
fairness for demographic subgroups was more challenging.
1.5.4
Bias, Awareness, and Ignorance in Deep-Learning-Based
Face Recognition
Wehrli et al. (2022) [14] examined the relationship between bias and the aware-
ness of sensitive features in deep-learning-based face recognition models. The study
tested the hypothesis that reducing a model’s awareness of sensitive attributes like
gender and race would decrease bias. Their findings revealed that blinding models
to sensitive features did not significantly impact accuracy or bias, indicating that
reducing awareness is not an effective method for mitigating bias in face recognition
systems.
1.5.5
Evaluation of Gender Bias in Facial Recognition with
Traditional Machine Learning Algorithms
Atay et al. (2024) [15] assessed gender bias in facial recognition using traditional
machine learning algorithms. They found that some algorithms, such as LR and
LDA, were more biased towards female subjects, while others, like DT, SVC, and
KNN, mitigated gender bias effectively. The study highlighted the need for balanced
training datasets to reduce bias in facial recognition systems.

Reversed Gini Coefficient
In the context of the OFIQ tool, quality components generally follow a higher-is-
better convention, meaning that higher values indicate better quality. To align the
Gini coefficient with this convention, we reverse it by subtracting the Gini coefficient
from 1. This transformation ensures that higher reversed Gini values correspond to
better (more equal) quality distributions.
The reversed Gini coefficient RG is calculated as:
RG = 1 −G
(2.3)
This reversal allows us to interpret the Gini coefficient in a way that is consistent
with the higher-is-better convention of the quality components, making it more
intuitive to interpret the results.
Modified Gini Coefficient
To further enhance our analysis and better highlight outliers in the data, we modify
the reverse Gini coefficient by cubing the results. This transformation emphasizes
the differences in distribution, making it easier to identify extreme values and out-
liers.
When cubing the reversed Gini coefficient, larger discrepancies between values are
magnified. This means that outliers or extreme values will have a more pronounced
effect on the final metric, making it easier to identify where significant inequality
or bias exists. An example of this can be seen in figure 2.1, where the standard
reversed gini coefficient is plotted against the cubed reversed gini coefficient for an

Using Mean Instead of Median for RMG
When calculating the RMG values for the different groups, the means of the quality
components will be chosen over the medians. Typically, the median is preferred as
a measure of central tendency when distributions are not normal, because it is less
affected by outliers. However, in this case, using the median could pose significant
issues.
Imagine the resulting quality scores for a quality component measured across ethni-
city groups resulted in a distribution of the means being [98.05, 99.01, 98.4, and 98.9]
and the medians being [100, 100, 100, and 100]. Using the medians when calculating
the RMG coefficient will result in an RMG value of 1, indicating perfect fairness,
since all values are equal. This, however, does not accurately reflect the minor differ-
ences present in the distribution. Using the means instead provides a clearer insight
into fairness. The mean values captures the minor differences between groups, of-
fering a more precise measurement of bias and avoiding the median’s tendency to
mask these differences.
2.3.2
Discard Percentage
While the RMG coefficient is a valuable measure for assessing the overall fairness of a
distribution, it has limitations when it comes to identifying biases towards a specific
group within the data. To address this, the discard percentage will be introduced
as an additional metric for analyzing the data.
The discard percentage helps in evaluating how many data points fall below a certain
quality threshold. This is particularly useful in biometric evaluations, where we want
to ensure that facial recognition systems do not disproportionately discard images
from certain demographic groups due to poor quality scores.

To calculate the discard percentage, quality scores for images from different demo-
graphic groups are collected. Next, a range of thresholds (e.g., 0 to 100) is chosen
to evaluate the quality scores. For each threshold, the number of images from each
demographic group that fall below this threshold is counted. The discard percent-
age is then calculated by dividing the count by the total number of images in that
demographic group and multiplying by 100, giving the percentage of images below
the threshold.
The discard percentage assesses fairness by comparing the discard percentages for
each demographic group at various thresholds. This comparison reveals how different
groups are affected by the quality assessment process. If a significant percentage of
images from one ethnicity are discarded at a lower quality threshold compared to
others, it indicates a potential bias.
For instance, if 30 % of images from one ethnic group are discarded at a quality
threshold of 50, while only 10 % from another group are discarded at the same
threshold, this suggests that the quality assessment process is biased against the
first group.
To quantify the disparity, the gap between the highest and lowest discard percentages
across demographic groups is calculated for each threshold. This discard percentage
gap helps in identifying the thresholds where the disparity is most pronounced.

Discussion
The discussion section provides an in-depth analysis and interpretation of the res-
ults presented in the previous chapter. This includes examining the potential biases
identified in the quality components across different demographic groups and evalu-
ating the implications of these findings. The goal is to understand the significance of
the observed disparities and assess the fairness and accuracy of the facial recognition
systems in various contexts.
4.1
Interpretation of Results
This section delves into the results to identify and understand biases in facial image
quality assessments across different demographic groups. By analyzing key metrics
like the RMG coefficient and discard percentage gaps, the discussion highlights dis-
parities based on ethnicity, gender, and age, evaluating their implications for the
fairness and accuracy of the system.
4.1.1
Ethnicity
Of the six total quality components that were analyzed, four of them exhibited
sufficiently low RMG values to suggest potential biases, as seen in figure 3.4. These
four quality components were, in descending order, the under-exposure prevention,
luminance variance, unified quality score and luminance mean.
Discussion of Under-exposure Prevention Across Ethnicity
The under-exposure prevention quality component had a RMG value of 0.953 for
the VGG dataset and 0.899 for the BFW dataset, which are not very indicative of
any biases since it is still in the higher range. However when inspecting the histo-
gram, the African American group can clearly be seen having a distinctly separate
distribution from the rest of the ethnicities.
39
==PAGE:53==
CHAPTER 4. DISCUSSION
This is then further cemented when also taking the discard percentage plot into con-
sideration, where the maximum discard gap is 21.05 % (VGG) and 24.79 % (BFW),
indicating a large disparity between the African American group and the rest of the
groups.
However when comparing the mean discard gap in the lower range (2.57 % (VGG)
& 3.58 % (BFW)) versus the higher range (9.21 % (VGG) & 11.52 % (BFW)), it be-
comes clear that the vast majority of the discard gap is found in the less practically
important high end.
Therefore, the relatively low RMG value combined with the location of the found
discard gap, suggests that the under-exposure prevention quality component is not
biased to a concerning extend.
Discussion of Luminance Variance Across Ethnicity
The RMG values of the luminance variance quality component closely resembles
those of the under-exposure prevention seeing as they are in the high end close to
1, with a score of 0.939 for the VGG dataset and 0.865 for the BFW dataset.
However when inspecting the discard percentage plot for the luminance variance,
it again shows a clear tendency of the African American group being the most
discarded with a maximum separation between the African American and Caucasian
Latin groups of 11.28 % (VGG) and 17.08 % (BFW), and a mean of 6.36 % (VGG)
and 9.05 % (BFW). This suggests a clear bias, however like with the under-exposure
prevention, most of the discard gap disparity is located in the upper range (50 to
100) of the scalar value, as evident by the mean discard gap increase of 236.9 %
(VGG) and 215.53% (BFW). This effectively means that if a threshold value of
50 is used, the mean discard gap between best and worst performing group would
only be 2.93 % (VGG) or 4.38% (BFW), and with a lower threshold value, these
differences would become less significant.
Discussion of Luminance Mean Across Ethnicity
The results of the luminance mean quality component indicated significant disparit-
ies, particularly for the African American group, which consistently showed a higher

4.2
Re-evaluated Fairness Metrics
During the course of this thesis, other statistical metrics were explored to assess
fairness and bias. While these metrics provided initial insights, certain limitations
became apparent, leading to a reevaluation of their suitability for the analysis. This
section outlines the methodologies and challenges associated with two such metrics,
KL Divergence and ANOVA, that were initially considered, but ultimately aban-
doned in favor of more robust approaches.
4.2.1
KL Divergence with intra group mean baseline
This subsection explores the use of Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence as a metric
to measure bias between different groups based on ethnicity, age, and gender. The
KL divergence quantifies how one probability distribution diverges from a second,
expected probability distribution. An "intra-group average baseline" measurement
is employed to establish a benchmark for comparing distributions within and across
groups.
The methodology involved dividing each group into two halves. The KL divergence
was calculated between these two halves to derive a baseline value representing "no
inherent bias." This process is repeated for all groups, and the mean value of these
baseline KL divergences is computed to obtain a single reference value for inherent
bias.
This intra-group average baseline is used to compare against the KL divergence
values calculated between different groups. A KL divergence value exceeding the
intra-group average baseline by 1.5 times the standard deviation would be considered
statistically significant, indicating a meaningful difference between the groups being
compared.
It is important to note that KL divergence is an asymmetric measure, meaning
the divergence from, for example, East Asian to African American is not necessar-
ily equal to the divergence from African American to East Asian. This asymmetry
poses a challenge when comparing biases between groups, as it complicates the inter-
pretation of the results. Due to this inherent asymmetry, the initial approach using
KL divergence was reevaluated and ultimately scrapped in favor of more symmetric
measures to ensure consistency and reliability in assessing group biases.
An example of a table contained KL-Divergence calculations across the four ex-
amined ethnicities can be seen in figure 6.22 in the appendix.

4.2.2
ANOVA
This subsection explores the use of Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) as a metric to
measure bias between different groups based on ethnicity, age, and gender. AN-
OVA is a statistical method used to determine whether there are any statistically
significant differences between the means of three or more independent groups.
The methodology involved applying ANOVA to the quality components across the
different groups to evaluate whether the observed differences were statistically sig-
nificant.
By comparing the variance within each group to the variance between
groups, ANOVA tests the null hypothesis that the group means are equal.
However, the results indicated that every single quality component yielded a p-value
of 0, signifying that all differences between the groups were considered statistically
significant by ANOVA. This outcome suggested that even minor variations were
flagged as significant, which did not align with the practical significance of these
differences.
Due to this limitation, where ANOVA identified all differences as statistically sig-
nificant, it was concluded that ANOVA might not be the most suitable metric for
assessing practical significance in the context of bias measurement.

4.3.2
Impact of Convergence at Threshold Extremes on Mean
Discard Gap Analysis
The discard percentage plots used in the results section illustrate how different
groups are affected by varying quality component thresholds. These plots show the
percentage of images discarded as the quality components threshold increases from
0 to 100.
A key characteristic of these plots is the convergence of lines at the extremes of the
threshold values. At a threshold value of 0, no images are discarded in any group,
resulting in a discard percentage of 0 for all groups. Consequently, the difference
between the best and worst performing groups is also 0 at this point. Similarly, at
the maximum threshold value of 100, all images are discarded, causing the discard
percentages for all groups to converge to 100 %. Again, the difference between the
best and worst performing groups is 0 at this point.
This convergence at both ends of the threshold spectrum impacts the calculation of
the mean discard gap percentage. The mean discard gap percentage is an average
measure of the difference in discard rates between the best and worst performing
groups across the range of threshold values. However, since the discard percentages
must converge at the threshold values of 0 and 100, the mean discard gap is influ-
enced by these points of convergence, potentially masking the differences that occur
in the mid-range thresholds where biases might be more pronounced.
Therefore, while the mean discard gap percentage provides a useful overall meas-
ure of performance disparities, it is essential to consider the convergence effect at
the extremes.
This effect can dampen the apparent differences between groups,
highlighting the need to closely examine the discard rates at mid-range threshold
values to fully understand the biases and performance disparities among different
demographic groups.
4.3.3
Impracticality of Low RMG Values
The RMG coefficient is used to measure fairness in the distribution of quality com-
ponents across different groups. However, achieving extremely low RMG values,
such as 0 or close to 0, is not practically possible for the data used in this study.

This is due to the inherent properties of the Gini coefficient and the nature of the
image datasets.
To achieve an RMG value of 0, the distribution would need to be highly imbalanced,
with one group possessing all the "value" while the others have none. Mathematic-
ally, this would look like [0, 0, 0, x], where only one group has any value at all. This
scenario represents the maximum "unfairness" according to the Gini coefficient, as
it indicates that one group is entirely dominant.
However, when dealing with image datasets, such as the BFW dataset, it is highly
improbable that all images in a group would receive a scalar value of 0 for any
quality component. For example, with the BFW ethnicity groupings, each group
contains 2500 images. It is practically impossible for all 2500 images in three out of
four groups to all receive a scalar value of 0 due to the natural variation in image
quality and content.
As a result, it is also improbable to achieve very low RMG values such as 0.1. In
practical terms, the lowest RMG value observed in this study was 0.374 (calculated
for the BFW ethnicity groupings for luminance mean). This value serves as a more
realistic lower bound for the RMG coefficient in this context.
Therefore, it can be concluded that there is a practical cutoff for RMG values, below
which it is not feasible to achieve due to the distribution characteristics of the image
datasets. This cutoff lies somewhere between 0 and 0.374, indicating that values
within this range are not practically possible for the given data.

5.2.1
Investigate Recognition Performance Differences
One of the key findings in this study was the significant difference in unified quality
scores between the Indian Asian and East Asian ethnicities within both the VGG

Future work should investigate whether this significant difference in unified quality
scores translates into worse recognition performance in facial recognition (FR) mod-
els. This can be done by running a facial recognition model, such as MagFace, across

performance compared to the East Asian group.

quality score threshold value that correlates with the maximum discard percentage
gap. In the VGG dataset, the maximum discard percentage gap of 12.4 % occurs at
a threshold value of 33, and in the BFW dataset, the maximum discard percentage
gap of 16.99 % occurs at a threshold value of 29. By applying these threshold values,
the dataset will be reduced to the point where there is the largest difference in unified
quality scores between the two ethnicities.

Comprehensive OFIQ Analysis on Entire VGG Dataset
For future work, it would be beneficial to rerun the OFIQ calculations on the entire
VGG dataset. Currently, the analysis has been conducted on approximately two-
thirds of the dataset due to errors generated by OFIQ. In the current setup, OFIQ
calculates all 31 quality components for each image. However, for the purposes of
this thesis, only six of these components are analyzed.
The issue arises when an error occurs while calculating some of the quality compon-
ents for an image, causing the process to crash and discard all the calculations for
that image. This problem significantly reduces the dataset size and completeness of
the analysis.
A viable solution to this problem is to edit the "ofiq_config.jaxn" file to exclude
the unnecessary quality components, leaving only the six components of interest.
This modification would greatly reduce the likelihood of encountering errors, as the
system would only process the relevant components, minimizing potential points of
failure.
Despite this straightforward solution, it has not been implemented within the time-
frame of this thesis due to the substantial computational time required. Running
OFIQ calculations on the VGG dataset, even when limited to the six quality compon-
ents of interest, still demands more than a week of continuous computing. However,
completing this step would significantly enhance the analysis by providing quality
component results for the entire VGG dataset. This comprehensive data set would
improve the reproducibility and replicability of the tests, ensuring more robust and
reliable findings.

Develop Mitigation Strategies
One of the primary goals of this thesis was to identify biases in the quality com-
ponents and develop strategies to mitigate these biases. However, this part of the
work was not fully accomplished due to a combination of time constraints and the
complexity of the initial data analysis phase.
After identifying the biases in the quality components, the next logical step would
have been to investigate the underlying causes of these biases by examining how each
quality component is calculated. Understanding the specific algorithms and factors
contributing to the bias is crucial for developing effective mitigation strategies. This
process requires detailed knowledge of the computation methods for each quality
component, which involves significant time and effort to acquire.
The time spent on determining the best way to analyze and present the data was
substantial. A considerable portion of the thesis timeline was dedicated to refining
the analysis methods and ensuring accurate bias identification. This phase involved
multiple iterations and adjustments, making it challenging to allocate sufficient time
for developing mitigation strategies.
Developing mitigation strategies would require a thorough examination of the al-
gorithms and their impact on different demographic groups. Potential strategies
might include adjusting the weighting of certain quality components, redesigning al-
gorithms to be more inclusive, or implementing post-processing techniques to correct
identified biases.
