Abstract
This thesis investigates the metabolic and mitochondrial adaptations of previously developed Warburg-null mammalian cell
lines - chinese hamster ovary (CHO) and human embryonic kidney (HEK293) cells - engineered through combined knockout of
lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) and pyruvate dehydrogenase kinases (PDKs) to abolish lactate production. These knockouts are
relevant both for improving biopharmaceutical cell line productivity and to explore how metabolic networks adjust when the
Warburg eﬀect - a hallmark of proliferative cells that ferment glucose to lactate under aerobic conditions - is suppressed.
We characterized a panel of Warburg-null clones by assessing growth proﬁles, central carbon metabolism via LC-MS en-
dometabolomics, redox balance, reactive oxygen species (ROS), mitochondrial mass, and sensitivity to a set of diverse inhibitors
targeting key cellular processes. We found that clones grew largely similar to their wild-types, although HEK293 clones dis-
played more heterogeneous proliferation. Endometabolomics revealed consistently elevated NADH and reduced NAD+/NADH
ratios, but the accumulation of TCA intermediates and glycolytic byproducts varied across clones. ROS levels and mitochondrial
responses were also clone-speciﬁc: HEK B1 exhibited pronounced mitochondrial mass expansion with moderate superoxide
production, whereas CHO-S 4B6 showed lower total ROS and a distinctive metabolic proﬁle. Toxicity assays demonstrated that
all knockouts were sensitive to mitochondrial uncouplers, conﬁrming reliance on oxidative phosphorylation, but exhibited varied
responses to proteasome and glutamine pathway inhibition.
These results highlight the remarkable metabolic ﬂexibility of engineered mammalian cells and demonstrate that clones with
similar or identical genotypes can adopt distinct metabolic states. This underscores the importance of multi-clone characterization
in metabolic engineering and provides insights for both bioprocess optimization and the study of the Warburg eﬀect.

Introduction
Mammalian cell lines such as chinese hamster ovary (CHO)
and human embryonic kidney (HEK293) cells are the work-
horses of modern biopharmaceutical manufacturing, used for
the production of complex recombinant proteins
1, 2. The global
biopharmaceutical market is projected to reach USD 1,183.9
billion by 2032, with a compound annual growth rate (CAGR)
of 8.6%
3, driving an urgent need to increase either produc-
tion capacity or the productivity of existing cell lines. One
major limitation to productivity is the accumulation of lactate,
a byproduct of aerobic glycolysis, which impairs cell growth,
viability, protein yield, and product quality through medium
acidiﬁcation and osmotic stress caused by base addition to con-
trol pH
4, 5. Consequently, strategies to reduce lactate produc-
tion have been suggested, including the knockdown or knock-
out of lactate dehydrogenase A (LDHA)
6, 7, which converts
pyruvate to lactate while regenerating nicotinamide adenine
dinucleotide (NAD+).
Understanding the eﬀects of eliminating lactate production re-
quires a closer look at mitochondrial metabolism. Mitochon-
dria are central hubs of cellular energy metabolism, producing
ATP via oxidative phosphorylation while maintaining an elec-
trochemical membrane potential
8. This potential arises from
the proton gradient generated by electron transfer from re-
ducing agents such as NADH and ﬂavin adenine dinucleotide
(FADH2) through the electron transport chain (ETC). A frac-
tion of these electrons inevitably leaks to oxygen, forming
reactive oxygen species (ROS), primarily the superoxide anion
(O·−
2 )
9. At moderate levels, ROS participate in signaling and
redox homeostasis; at excessive levels, they can damage lipids,
proteins, and deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA)
10. While oxidative
phosphorylation is highly eﬃcient - yielding roughly ten times
more adenosine triphosphate (ATP) per glucose molecule than
glycolysis - many highly proliferative cells, including stem cells
and cancer cells, preferentially ferment glucose to lactate even
in the presence of oxygen. This phenomenon, called the War-
burg eﬀect, was ﬁrst described by Otto Warburg in 1923
11 and
remains a hallmark of rapidly growing mammalian cells.
The Warburg eﬀect is metabolically paradoxical: it is energy-
ineﬃcient yet widely observed in proliferative mammalian
cells. Warburg originally hypothesized that aerobic glycolysis
came from defective mitochondrial respiration
12, but modern
evidence demonstrates that most Warburg-positive cells re-
tain functional mitochondria
13. Several hypotheses have been
proposed to explain the Warburg eﬀect, including the rapid
provision of biosynthetic precursors and maintenance of redox
balance via NAD+ regeneration
14. Altogether, characterizing
the consequences of abolishing lactate production is not only
relevant for developing improved bioprocesses but also for ad-
vancing our understanding of the Warburg eﬀect in proliferative
mammalian cells, motivating the present study.
Previous attempts to fully eliminate lactate production in mam-
malian cells had reported that "loss of LDH activity is lethal in
CHO cells"
15. However, Hefzi et al.
16 demonstrated that com-
plete removal of LDH activity is possible in both CHO and
HEK293 cells by simultaneously knocking out pyruvate dehy-
drogenase kinases (PDKs) - either all or a subset of isoforms
- , generating cell lines termed Warburg-null. This strategy
exploits the regulatory role of PDKs: when activated by high
NADH, acetyl-CoA, or ATP levels, PDKs phosphorylate and
inhibit the pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) complex, limiting
pyruvate entry into the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle
17. In
LDH-deﬁcient cells, NADH accumulation would normally in-
duce PDK activity, leading to PDH inhibition, resulting in the
inability to process the pyruvate. By removing both LDH and
PDK activity, cells can bypass this feedback regulation, thus
allowing the ﬂux of pyruvate through PDH
16.
Hefzi et al.
16 validated several Warburg-null clones by con-
ﬁrming gene knockouts, loss of LDH activity and lactate se-
cretion. They found stable growth rates throughout and showed
increased TCA metabolite levels, decreased NAD+/NADH ra-
tio and increased oxygen consumption in one tested clone.
Furthermore, three clones were treated with BAM15, a mito-
chondrial uncoupler, and showed heightened sensitivity, thus
demonstratingtheirrelianceonmitochondrialrespiration. How-
ever, this initial study examined a limited number of clones and
left some critical questions unanswered: (i) Are the observed
metabolic adaptations - such as elevated TCA metabolites, al-
tered redox balance, and respiratory dependency - consistent
across multiple CHO and HEK293 clones with varying PDK
knockout combinations? (ii) Do these cells exhibit enhanced
mitochondrial eﬃciency, increased mitochondrial biogenesis,
or diﬀerences in ROS because of potential dependency on res-
piration? (iii) Warburg-null clones showed sensitivity towards
BAM15, but what - if any - other sensitivities or resistances
are exhibited?
These questions are also relevant to oncology, and the Warburg-
null model is of interest, as many cancers exhibit high lactate
secretion. Previous work suggested that LDH inhibition alone
may not be suﬃcient as a therapeutic strategy
18, and synergis-
tic eﬀects were observed in taxol-resistant breast cancer cells
when combining LDH knockdown with taxol treatment
19. Yet,
studies exploring the broader metabolic consequences of com-
pletely abolishing the Warburg eﬀect across multiple mam-
malian cell lines with normal growth do not exist.
To address these gaps, this thesis systematically investigates the
metabolic and mitochondrial adaptations in a broad panel of
Warburg-null cell lines, including multiple CHO and HEK293
LDH/PDK knockouts. We ﬁrst extend the characterization of
these lines beyond the limited clones studied previously by pro-
ﬁling growth behavior and central carbon metabolism through
targeted LC-MS endometabolomics, with a particular focus
on glycolysis and TCA intermediates and NAD+/NADH ratios
to capture redox shifts. Next, we examine mitochondrial re-
sponses by quantifying total ROS, mitochondrial superoxide
(SOX), and mitochondrial mass via ﬂow cytometry, provid-
ing insights into how LDH/PDK knockouts impact organelle
function and redox homeostasis. Finally, we assess the func-

Results
Very little is known about the cellular consequences of com-
pletely abolishing the Warburg eﬀect.
While Hefzi et al.
16
demonstrated that CHO and HEK293 cells can tolerate com-
plete loss of LDH activity through concurrent PDK knockouts,
their study was limited to a small number of clones and left open
key questions regarding mitochondrial and metabolic adapta-
tion, and phenotypic vulnerabilities. To further reveal the role
of mitochondria and redox homeostasis in Warburg-null cells,
we extended the work of Hefzi et al.
16 to a broader panel of
CHO and HEK293 LDH/PDK knockout lines.
Growth proﬁling and targeted metabolomics of TCA cycle in-
termediates were conducted for CHO-S WT, 4B6, F5 and HEK
293 WT, B1, B10, A8, F10, CO2 B2, D07 B2, and D07 D3
(Table 1 for full overview). Data for HEK A8, F10, CO2 B2,
D07 B2, and D07 D3 were generated in parallel by a colleague
and are included here with permission to enable broader trend
analysis.
We also quantiﬁed NAD+/NADH ratios to assess
redox balance and metabolic shifts. To probe mitochondrial
responses, we performed ﬂow cytometry-based measurements
of total ROS, mitochondrial SOX, and mitochondrial mass.
Finally, we expanded toxicity testing beyond BAM15 to in-
clude seven compounds targeting distinct cellular processes,
enabling the identiﬁcation of additional phenotypic sensitivi-
ties or resistances.
By integrating growth, metabolomic, mitochondrial, and toxi-
city analyses, these experiments provide a comprehensive view
of how Warburg-null mammalian cells adapt to the complete
loss of lactate production.
Warburg-null CHO lines maintain fast wild-type (WT)
growth, while HEK knockouts show more variable
proliferation
The Warburg eﬀect has been proposed to support rapid prolif-
eration by channeling glucose to lactate at rates 10-100 times
faster than complete mitochondrial oxidation, and by providing
the biosynthetic requirements of uncontrolled cell division
14.
Conversely, other studies suggest that elevated glycolysis may
simply reﬂect mitochondrial overload, with excess glucose ex-
creted as lactate
20.
Previous work by Hefzi et al.
16 demonstrated that one Warburg-
null clone, CHO-S F5, showed no reduction in growth rate,
indicating that high glycolytic ATP production is not essential
for normal proliferation in that clone.
We wanted to determine whether this observation extends to
additional Warburg-null clones. To address this, we performed
batch culture experiments (n = 3) in 125 mL non-baﬄed shake
ﬂasks, monitoring viable cell density (VCD) and viability over
seven days. This allowed us to directly compare the growth
dynamics of wild-type and Warburg-null cell lines and evalu-
ate whether loss of the Warburg phenotype consistently aﬀects
proliferation. Figure 1A shows the growth proﬁles of CHO
cell lines over seven days. All three lines - CHO-S WT, 4B6,
and F5-initially grew at similar rates, reaching
4-5 × 106
cells mL−1 by day 4. By day 7, however, CHO-S F5 exhibited
a sharp decline in both VCD and viability, dropping to 2 × 106
cells mL−1 and near-complete loss of viable cells (24%). In
contrast, CHO-S WT and 4B6 continued to proliferate, reach-
ing 6.8-7.7 × 106 cells mL−1 with viabilities above 75%. While
this late-stage collapse in F5 viability aligns with shake ﬂask
batch culture growth proﬁles by Hefzi et al.
16, the high viability
of CHO-S WT does not.
HEK 293 growth behavior (Figure 1B) was more heteroge-
neous. By day 4, HEK B1 had the lowest VCD (2.3 × 106
cells mL−1), while HEK WT, B10, and F10 reached 4 × 106
cells mL−1. By day 7, B1 and CO2 B2 had not yet plateaued,
whereas HEK WT and D07 D3 showed declining VCDs. No-
tably, all HEK lines maintained 67-90% viability at day 7, in
contrast to the steep F5 decline observed in CHO.
Growth rate analysis (Figure 1C) further highlights the dif-
ference between species. CHO clones grew at similar rates
(0.85-0.9 d−1), whereas HEK clones were slower and some
showed signiﬁcant diﬀerences among themselves. HEK B1
was the slowest (0.39 d−1), roughly 30% lower than the fastest-
growing HEK B10 (0.56 d−1). HEK B10, F10, A8 and D07 B2
showed similar growth rates to HEK WT, while the rest was sig-
niﬁcantly lower. These results show that, while Warburg-null
CHO clones largely sustain wild-type growth, HEK knockouts
exhibit reduced and more variable proliferation.
Diﬀerences in compound sensitivity highlight clone- and
species-speciﬁc dependencies
To investigate the functional dependencies of the Warburg-null
knockout cell lines, we conducted a series of toxicity assays.
By selectively inhibiting key metabolic and cellular pathways,
we aimed to identify which functions are most critical for the
survival and proliferation of each clone. Cultures were treated
in six-well plates in triplicate (n = 3), and VCD and viability
were monitored daily over ﬁve days.
The compounds and their expected cellular eﬀects are brieﬂy
summarized below:
Aminooxyacetic acid (AOA) is a broad inhibitor of pyridoxal
phosphate (PLP)-dependent enzymes. This includes but is not
limited to: (i) aspartate aminotransferase, a key component
of the malate-aspartate shuttle; and (ii) aspartate transaminase
and alanine transaminase, allowing glutamate and pyruvate to
be used for TCA intermediates production. Inhibition disrupts
cytosolic NAD+ regeneration and mitochondrial utilization of
glycolytic substrates
21, and also decreases glutathione (GTH𝑟𝑑)
synthesis
22. Cells that rely heavily on mitochondrial NADH
production or ROS detoxiﬁcation are likely to be more sensi-
tive. However, because the inhibitory eﬀects are so broad, it is
diﬃcult to draw clear causal conclusions.
BAM15 is a mitochondrial uncoupler that dissipates the inner
membrane proton gradient, increasing respiration and energy
expenditure
23. Cells with alternative ATP-generating pathways
may tolerate BAM15 longer, whereas lines relying on oxidative
phosphorylation are expected to be more vulnerable.
Bortezomib inhibits the proteasome, causing proteotoxic stress
through misfolded protein accumulation and triggering mito-
chondrial depolarization and ROS production

and organelle membranes

swelling of cells

Clones reliant on oxidative metabolism
may be more susceptible.

Discussion
In this thesis, we investigated the metabolic responses - focus-
ing on mitochondrial metabolism - of LDH and a subset of
PDK knockout clones in CHO and HEK cell lines. Our objec-
tive was to determine whether the metabolic changes observed
in a single clone by Hefzi et al.
16 would extend to a broader
panel of similar knockouts. Based on prior work, we hypoth-
esized that loss of LDH activity would lead to pyruvate and
NADH accumulation, potentially impacting redox balance and
mitochondrial function.
To address this, we characterized each clone through batch cul-
ture growth proﬁling, toxicity assays, endometabolomic analy-
sis, total ROS measurement, and assessment of mitochondrial
SOX and mass.
Below, we summarize our main ﬁndings:
(i) Growth Proﬁles: Batch cultures revealed that Warburg-
null clones exhibited growth rates comparable to their respec-
tive wild-types in 8 out of 11 cases (Figure 1C), indicating
that the Warburg eﬀect is not essential for proliferation in
most clones, consistent with Hefzi et al.
16. (ii) Toxicity as-
says: In a six-well plate assay run in triplicates (Figure 2),
HEK knockouts were generally more vulnerable to mitochon-
drial respiratory inhibition than CHO knockouts. Additionally,
CHO knockouts and HEK B1 showed greater sensitivity to
proteasome inhibition and glutamate metabolism disruption.
(iii) Endometabolomics: Triplicate endometabolomic proﬁl-
ing (Figure 3) showed elevated NADH levels in all knockouts,
but pyruvate accumulation was not always observed. Notably,
CHO 4B6 displayed only a halved NAD+/NADH ratio and no
increase in log2 fold-change of pyruvate, glyc3p or malate, in
contrast to other clones.
(iv) ROS Levels: Total ROS was
elevated in all clones except CHO 4B6 (Figure 4). (v) Mito-
chondrial SOX and Mass: CHO 4B6 and HEK B1 exhibited
higher mitochondrial SOX levels, while CHO F5 and HEK
B10 remained similar to wild-type (Figure 5). Mitochondrial
mass was also increased in 4B6 and especially in B1, whereas
F5 and B10 were largely unchanged.
Despite Warburg-null cell engineering leading to increased
reliance on mitochondrial oxidative metabolism and elevated
NADH levels, these results show that this metabolic rewiring
does not inevitably lead to total redox collapse or uniform
accumulation of pyruvate and TCA cycle intermediates as pre-
viously shown
16.
While all knockout cell lines showed increased sensitivity to
BAM15, an uncoupler of mitochondrial oxidative phospho-
rylation, thus demonstrating its reliance on it (see Figure 2),
as well as elevated NADH levels compared to their WT (see
Figure 3A), the CHO-S 4B6 cell line emerged as a notable
exception. In 4B6, the NAD+/NADH ratio was only halved
compared to wild-type, whereas other knockout lines experi-
enced more dramatic reductions (see Figure 3B). Additionally,
while most knockouts showed elevated levels of TCA interme-
diates and glyc3p, 4B6 did not display these increases.
Higher levelsof glyc3panddihydroxyacetonephosphate(DHAP)
in most knockout cell lines suggest that this pathway is used as
an alternative to LDH for the regeneration of NAD+. This is
supported by the ﬁndings from Liu et al.
30 that found elevated
DHAP and glyc3p levels in yeast with decreased NAD+/NADH
ratios. This is because the production of glyc3p from DHAP
by glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase 1 (GPD1) can regen-
erate NAD+ from NADH in the cytosol, which in turn enables
pyruvate to enter the TCA cycle via pyruvate dehydrogenase
(PDH). However, glyc3p can also serve a more direct role in
mitochondrial electron transfer. In 𝛽-cells - which naturally
exhibit low LDH activity
31 - glyc3p has been shown to be ox-
idized back to DHAP by mitochondrial glycerol-3-phosphate
dehydrogenase (ﬂavoprotein), simultaneously reducing FAD
to FADH2, a process called glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle. This
FADH2 then donates electrons to coenzyme Q via complex
II
32, thereby contributing to the mitochondrial proton gradi-
ent. Through this mechanism, cells could potentially bypass
the need for NAD+-dependent PDH activity altogether, while
still fueling oxidative phosphorylation. This could explain the
elevated pyruvate levels observed in most clones. The dual
function of glyc3p - both as a means to regenerate NAD+
and as a substrate for mitochondrial electron shuttling - could
partially relieve the pressure on cytosolic NAD+ regeneration
under conditions where lactate production is suppressed.
An alternative route for mitochondrial NADH and cytosolic
NAD+ recycling - that was also observed in 𝛽-cells - is the
malate-aspartate shuttle, which indirectly transfers NADH into
mitochondria and NAD+ into the cytosol by cycling malate and
aspartate. This shuttle is reversible but requires high cytosolic
NADH levels for activation and mainly supports electron trans-
fer to complex I. In this context, the unchanged glyc3p levels in
4B6 compared to other Warburg-null cell lines suggest a dis-
tinct metabolic strategy. One possibility is that 4B6 channels
glyc3p toward glycerophospholipid synthesis more eﬃciently
than other clones - thereby replenishing NAD+ through an-
abolic pathways - or by converting glyc3p into glycerol and
secreting it. Alternatively, 4B6 may achieve greater cytosolic
NAD+ regeneration through enhanced activity of the malate-
aspartate shuttle. However, this explanation is challenged by
the observation that neither malate nor aspartate levels are el-
evated in 4B6, whereas malate is consistently more abundant
in all other knockout lines.
Further evidence that 4B6 is not more reliant on the malate-
aspartate shuttle than the other CHO clones comes from glu-
taminase inhibition experiments. Cells highly dependent on the
malate-aspartate shuttle are expected to be more sensitive to
BPTES, a glutaminase inhibitor. This is because glutamate is
required in the malate-aspartate shuttle for transporting aspar-
tate back into the mitochondrial intermembrane space via the
glutamate-aspartate antiporter. This expectation aligns with
the behavior of HEK B1, the only cell line showing elevated
levels of malate, aspartate, and oxaloacetate (OAA, another
metabolite needed in the malate-aspartate shuttle) (see Figure
3), therefore hinting towards a high use of the malate-aspartate
shuttle. In turn, B1 also exhibits the most pronounced drop in
viability and VCD among HEK lines after four days of BPTES
treatment. In contrast, 4B6 showed a slower drop in viability
and VCD compared to other CHO cell lines, supporting it may
actually be less reliant on the malate-aspartate shuttle (Figure
2). A third mechanism for replenishing NAD+ was described

in detail by Kim et al.
33. In HEK 293 cells, they observed
that when NADH levels rise due to LDH inhibition, the de-
saturation of polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) into highly
unsaturated fatty acids (HUFAs) via the delta-5 and delta-6
desaturases (D5D and D6D) can serve as a route for glycolytic
NAD+ recycling. This mechanism could explain why the 4B6
clone does not exhibit increases in glyc3p or pyruvate. Instead,
4B6 may direct most of its pyruvate toward acetyl-CoA pro-
duction - which is indeed elevated in 4B6 - supporting fatty
acid biosynthesis. NAD+ could then be regenerated during
PUFA desaturation.
Nonetheless, drawing deﬁnitive conclusions from these obser-
vations is challenging. Glutamate, for instance, is not only a
key player in the malate-aspartate shuttle but also serves as a
precursor for lipid biosynthesis, amino acid and nucleotide syn-
thesis, and the de novo synthesis of GTH𝑟𝑑
25. This underscores
the interconnectedness of metabolic pathways and the diﬃculty
of attributing observed phenotypes to any one mechanism in
isolation. To further explore the distinct compensatory mech-
anisms at play in Warburg-null cells, transcriptomic proﬁling
could help identify which pathways are up- and downregulated
in an attempt to restore NAD+/NADH balance.
Overall, these ﬁndings challenge the assumption that disrupting
the lactate pathway necessarily diminishes redox homeostasis,
and instead highlight the remarkable metabolic plasticity of
engineered cells. While most cell lines likely maintain redox
balance through the glycerol-3-phosphate or aspartate-malate
shuttles, the 4B6 clone may achieve balance through alterna-
tive routes. One possibility is that 4B6 continuously converts
glyc3p into glycerol for secretion or into glycerophospholipids,
thereby allowing more ﬂux into GPD1-mediated NAD+ regen-
eration. Another possibility is that 4B6 bypasses the GPD1
pathway altogether, instead relying on NAD+ recovery through
PUFA desaturation.
Supporting this hypothesis, transcrip-
tomic data from Hefzi et al.
16 show that the Gpd1 gene can
be found downregulated in Warburg-null cell lines.
Another notable observation supporting the metabolic plas-
ticity of Warburg-null cell lines is the absence of a uniform
metabolic phenotype across all knockouts. While a reduced
NAD+/NADH ratio was a consistent feature, other metabolic
responses varied considerably. This variability is evident from
the diﬀerent growth rates observed among HEK lines, as well
as from the varied metabolite levels in the central carbon
metabolism. Further diﬀerences appear in the oxidative stress
response. Despite all knockout lines exhibiting increased sen-
sitivity to BAM15 - consistent with enhanced reliance on ox-
idative phosphorylation - and prior evidence of increased oxy-
gen consumption in F5 cells compared to CHO-S WT
16, total
cellular ROS levels were not uniformly elevated. Given that
increased oxidative phosphorylation is often linked to elevated
mitochondrial ROS, particularly through electron leakage at
Complex I
34, 35, this inconsistency was unexpected.
Interestingly, Murphy
34 reports that a low NAD+/NADH ra-
tio (i.e., high NADH/NAD+, as noted by the author) can drive
ROS generation at Complex I. This pattern aligns with our data
for CHO-S F5 and HEK lines B1 and B10, which all show both
drastically reduced NAD+/NADH ratios and elevated ROS lev-
els (Figure 4). However, CHO-S 4B6 again presents an excep-
tion. Although its NAD+/NADH ratio is halved compared to
wild-type, total ROS levels are lower than WT. When looking
at mitochondrial ROS speciﬁcally, as measured by MitoSOX,
4B6 shows elevated levels relative to its WT control. This may
suggest that 4B6 produces more mitochondrial ROS but has an
enhanced capacity for cytosolic ROS clearance. If true, this
would be expected to coincide with increased levels of GTH𝑟𝑑,
yet we do not observe this in 4B6, making the explanation less
straightforward.
Another example of diﬀerences in phenotypes lies in mitochon-
drial mass. HEK B1 shows a large increase in mitochondrial
mass compared to all other cell lines. Interestingly, Lee et
al.
36 showed that ROS stress induced by H2O2 treatment can
increase mitochondrial mass in human lung ﬁbroblasts, possi-
bly explaining the mass in B1, which also exhibits high ROS
levels throughout. Since ROS levels in B1 were not increased
exogenously, it is unclear whether higher ROS levels from in-
tracellular processes induced mitochondrial mass expansion,
or whether increased mitochondrial mass - and likely higher
respiration - led to elevated ROS levels.
Taken together, this variability further highlights the capacity
for metabolic plasticity in the face of glycolytic disruption.
The concept of a "screening window" may help to explain this:
prior to knockout, cells occupy a large plane of viable metabolic
states. The knockout-induced metabolic shock constrains this
plane, but crucially still allows for some variance in metabolic
states.
In plain terms, the knockout constraints represent a
window, not a keyhole. The surviving cells, therefore, portray
a variety of adaptive metabolic states rather than a singular re-
sponse. However, this introduces a form of survivorship bias:
we only observe cells that managed to re-establish metabolic
balance after the "screening window". As such, the phenotypes
we detect reﬂect viable outcomes of the imposed constraints.
Consequently, focusing only on diﬀerences between knock-
out and wild-type lines may obscure the more fundamental
insights. Instead, identifying traits that are conserved across
all surviving lines as well as wild-types could oﬀer deeper
understanding into the essential metabolic processes that are
indispensable for cell viability and thus intolerant to changes.
Several limitations should be noted when interpreting these
results.
First, the endometabolomics data were reported as
relative rather than absolute concentrations, making any direct
comparisons with values from literature challenging. However,
relative comparisons within our dataset remain valid, particu-
larly since we included wild-type controls, enabling internally
consistent analysis.
Second, in some cases, only one biological replicate was avail-
able, requiring an assumed standard deviation of zero for error
propagation.
This led to an underestimation of propagated
error and is marked in the ﬁgures accordingly.
Moreover,
these endometabolomics data represent only a single timepoint
during mid-exponential growth, limiting interpretation about
metabolic ﬂuxes or dynamic changes.
The CellROX staining also present limitations. Firstly, CHO
and HEK runs were seperated and two diﬀerent ﬂow cytome-
ters, using diﬀerent excitation lasers for SYTOX Red, were

A major strength of this study is its broad scope, encompass-
ing multiple CHO and HEK knockout lines with varying ge-
netic deletions in glycolytic regulators.
This allowed us to
assess commonalities and diﬀerences across a wider spectrum
of metabolic states. Importantly, our ﬁndings for CHO-S F5
were largely consistent with those of Hefzi et al.
16, particularly
with regard to relative levels of glyc3p, NADH, and pyruvate
to CHO-S WT. Observed diﬀerences in acetyl-CoA levels may
come from the use of diﬀerent media and culture vessels: Hefzi
et al.

batch cultures in CD OptiCHO media with 1-4 mM glutamine,
whereas our study used 125 mL non-baﬄed shake ﬂasks batch
cultures in CD CHO media with 8mM glutamine. In addi-
tion, our work expands on the existing dataset by incorporating
further layers of phenotypic data, including ROS levels, mito-
chondrial mass, mitochondrial SOX, and compound sensitivity
across a broader panel of cell lines.
Further research should aim to directly measure the activity
of the glyc3p shuttle, the malate-aspartate shuttle, as well
as the glycerol, glycophospholipid and HUFA biosynthesis
in Warburg-null cells to test the hypothesis that these path-
ways contribute to NAD+ replenishment.
Techniques such
as stable isotope tracing, enzymatic activity assays, or West-
ern blots targeting key enzymes would be well suited for this
purpose. In parallel, quantifying absolute metabolite concen-
trations and performing time-course metabolomics would en-
able more deﬁnitive insights into metabolic ﬂux dynamics.
Complementary measurements of glucose consumption using
FLEX2 assays and oxygen consumption via Seahorse analysis
could further support the hypothesis that Warburg-null cells
rely primarily on oxidative phosphorylation for ATP genera-
tion. Repeating the ROS, MitoSOX, and mitochondrial mass
assays using correct dye concentrations and true biological
replicates will enhance the robustness and interpretability of
redox-related ﬁndings. In addition, applying alternative meth-
ods for assessing mitochondrial membrane potential - such as
TMRE staining under optimized ﬂow cytometry conditions -
could overcome the limitations encountered with JC-1. Lastly,
integrating transcriptomic or proteomic proﬁling would pro-
videvaluableinsights into the broader regulatory andmetabolic
rewiring that occurs in response to Warburg-null engineering.
Altogether, this work serves as a starting point for further in-
vestigations and underscores the importance of characterizing
multiple clones of a given genotype, as their metabolic be-
havior can vary substantially. It also underscores the value
of generating additional Warburg-null cell lines - whether in
CHO, HEK, or other mammalian cell lines - not only to iden-
tify clones with improved growth and productivity proﬁles, but
also to deepen our understanding of how cells adapt to forced
reliance on oxidative metabolism. Insights gained from such
models could eventually be translated to other contexts where
metabolic plasticity is critical, including cancer biology. Such
eﬀorts could oﬀer new perspectives on tumor metabolism and
potentially uncover novel therapeutic targets.

Conclusion and Outlook
This study set out to investigate how Warburg-null cell engi-
neering aﬀects cellular metabolism, with a particular focus on
redox balance, TCA cycle activity, and mitochondrial func-
tion.
Our ﬁndings demonstrate that although all knockout
cell lines exhibited a reduction in the NAD+/NADH ratio, the
magnitude and nature of these eﬀects varied across clones.
Notably, the CHO-S 4B6 clone showed the smallest reduction
in NAD+/NADH ratio, did not accumulate TCA intermedi-
ates, and exhibited lower overall ROS levels despite elevated
mitochondrial ROS. In contrast, HEK B1 displayed clear evi-
dence of mitochondrial biogenesis and elevated ROS levels.
Moreover, the uniform sensitivity of all clones to BAM15
and other compounds with similar mechanisms further con-
ﬁrmed the heightened mitochondrial respiratory dependence
of Warburg-null cells, while also revealing variations in their
responses to proteasome inhibition and disruptions in gluta-
mate metabolism.
Overall, these results highlight the metabolic plasticity of en-
gineered cell lines. There is no uniform metabolic outcome
following the suppression of lactate production, and the diver-
sity of responses emphasizes the importance of characterizing
multiple independent clones when evaluating the functional
consequences of a knockout. This variability also reﬂects the
existence of a "screening window" that allows for diﬀerences
in metabolic states upon knockout, resulting in multiple viable
phenotypes.
Given the experimental limitations highlighted in the discus-
sion, follow-up experiments are required to conﬁrm the ob-
served trends. Future research should focus on (i) validating
ROS and mitochondrial measurements using biological repli-
cates, (ii) performing transcriptomic, proteomic, Seahorse-
based metabolic analyses, and ﬂux-based approaches to di-
rectly assess the roles of the glycerol-3-phosphate and malate-
aspartate shuttles, as well as anabolic fatty acid pathways in
maintaining NAD+ regeneration.
By revealing the non-uniform metabolic responses to Warburg-
null engineering, this work contributes to the ﬁeld of mam-
malian cell metabolism by emphasizing the need for multi-
clone, multi-layered characterization. Whether due to unde-
tected unintended genetic edits or the eﬀects of the "screening
window", this work demonstrates that metabolic rewiring can
take divergent paths and that single-clone analyses risk over-
looking critical aspects of cellular adaptation. These insights
will inform both bioprocessing strategies and the rational de-
sign of future metabolic engineering eﬀorts.
