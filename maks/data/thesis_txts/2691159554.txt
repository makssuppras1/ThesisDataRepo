Abstract 
The design requirements for new fishing vessels tend to be mostly based on existing 
fishing vessel models. The design stage normally does not include an initial study of 
the seakeeping and dynamic stability of the vessels, especially due to the lack of 
documented criteria to do so. This study focuses on the compliance of a selected stern 
trawler fishing vessel with existing seakeeping and parametric rolling criteria to 
develop customized criteria suitable for this type of ship. To develop the study, a 
methodology based on a computational analysis was carried out. Such methodology 
investigates and sets the common conditions that the selected fishing vessel works 
under, computes them in the commercial software SHIPFLOW and postprocesses the 
results through a code developed in MATLAB (RBSMC). The 3D model required for 
the computation was created in Rhino 3D from the lines plan provided by the Ship 
designer. On this matter, both seakeeping and the second-generation IMO stability 
criteria (specifically parametric roll) are assessed through a systematic study of the 
influence of the centre of gravity over the ship responses under different working 
conditions and contrasted with the customized seakeeping criteria and the vulnerability 
criteria for parametric roll. This approach permitted to understand the behaviour of this 
vessel, where its responses show to be highly dependent on their variable working 
conditions and wave characteristics. It was seen that the resonance of the studied 
motions (heave, pitch, and roll) take place at longer wave lengths than a tanker or a 
container would. The centre of gravity location highly influences the responses. It was 
found that the trawling condition was one of the most affected because of the external 
force represented as a shifted centre of gravity. Under this working condition the vessel 
developed high pitch and roll angles of low acceleration that surpassed the limiting 
values. Meanwhile, in other conditions the responses fitted the criterion. On the 
parametric roll side, it was seen that the most prone working condition to develop the 
phenomena was the light ship condition, where the variation of GM is suitable, and the 
appropriate waves could trigger it. In general, the damping coefficient is high enough 
to prevent the phenomena to take place. SHIPFLOW presents some drawbacks that can 
be improved so more conditions can be studied, and more accurate results can be 
obtained. The developed criteria mostly present limiting values above the responses of 
the vessel except for the trawling condition for which the criteria can be refined. With 
the methodology presented and the customized criteria, it is possible to evaluate more 
fishing vessel models in the design stage. 
 
 
 
Keywords: Seakeeping; Regular Waves; Fishing Trawlers; Parametric Rolling; 
SHIPFLOW; IMO.

Introduction 
 
1.1 
Background 
 
Modern vessel procurement is often specified in terms of the vessel's performance and 
speed in a certain sea area and state. This is unclear in terms of measurable seakeeping 
criteria/properties e.g., rolling angles, acceleration, frequencies of the movements, 
dimensioning of structure with regards to slamming, risk of broaching, water on deck, 
perceived comfort, possibility to perform given tasks in intended operating 
environment, physiological load, and the ship's course-keeping. Establishing these 
seakeeping properties is difficult since ships have different purposes and dimensions. 
There are already some established properties, which are considered in this study (ISO, 
1997) (Lewis, 1989) (Nordforsk, 1987), but they apply mainly to large ships. 
Displacement and semi-displacement work boats (<75 meters in length), e.g., fishing 
vessels, are traditionally built based on earlier models, since there is often lack of time 
and budget to develop them. So well-established calculation methods for these vessels 
are lacking, and so is the categorization of how different work operations are influenced 
by the ship’s motions. Hence, it becomes difficult to assess in the design stage whether 
a vessel has sufficiently good seaworthiness compared to others of its kind or if the 
design needs to be iterated. There are some studies focused on the evaluation of 
different hull shapes of fishing vessels (Sayli, Alkan, Nabergoj, & Uysal, 2006) and 
others that assess performance in relation to sea conditions, also for fishing vessels 
(Soares, Tello, & Silva, 2010), however these are limited to certain regions and for a 
limited range of vessels and conditions. 
 
Fishing vessels responses are highly dependent of their varying loading conditions 
when operating. These loading conditions open another important variable, which is the 
changing centre of gravity during the operation due to the increase of the cargo (fish). 
Such changing centre of gravity, along with the fishing procedure and the sea state, 
impact the ship with accelerations, forces, and moments of different magnitude all over 
the structure. In turn, the motions affect the work environment, the crew members, and 
even the installed equipment on decks. (Riola & Arboleya, 2006). 
 
1.2 
Study objectives 
 
This report is part of the research project “Arbetsbåtars Sjöegenskapskriterier (ASK)” 
financed by Trafikverket (The Swedish Transport Administration) and coordinated by 
SSPA Sweden AB. 
 
The present thesis focuses on the compliance of a selected, well-documented, stern 
trawler fishing vessel (Stella Nova IX) with existing seakeeping and parametric rolling 
criteria with the purpose of (a) developing customized criteria suitable for this type of 
ship and (b) proposing an assessment methodology to optimize such criteria, using the 
software SHIPFLOW. 
 
To achieve these objectives, the following steps were followed:

1.2.1 Scope and delimitations 
 
Due to time constraints, the study was based on one stern trawler fishing vessel model, 
that operates in the Baltic Sea and in the North Sea. The selection of the fishing vessel 
was made by SSPA Sweden AB given that this ship is a well-documented representative 
case of the region due to its characteristics and fishing procedure.  
 
In order to frame the study, the wave characteristics were based on the most frequent 
sea states in the zones of study (see section 4.1.3) and the working conditions on the 
stability booklet provided by the ship designer (section 4.1.1). The sea states were 
statistically chosen from the data source ERA5, from the record of 2019 and 
2020.(Copernicus) 
 
The seakeeping criteria were selected based on the studied literature in relation with 
their relevance and applicability to fishing vessels. (Papanikolaou, 2001) (Sayli, Alkan, 
Nabergoj, & Uysal, 2006) (Soares, Tello, & Silva, 2010)

2.1 
Seakeeping 
Seakeeping ability or seaworthiness refers to the capability of a vessel to perform its 
mission at sea. Seakeeping takes into consideration different aspects, like waves 
characteristics and the ship particulars. From these it is possible to predict the different 
types of phenomena the vessel experiences at sea i.e., motions, wetness, slamming, etc. 
(Lewis, 1989), (Graham, 1990), (O'Hanlon & McCauley, 1974). 
 
These phenomena are studied according to the degrees of freedom a vessel presents 
when floating freely, see Figure 2-1.

2.1.1 Waves 
Wave’s characteristics are important inputs to consider when analysing a vessel at sea. 
It depends on the taken approach and wave theory adopted for the analysis of the vessel 
on how the results may vary.  
 
Since there are several wave theories to consider, this section presents a theoretical 
background on the wave’s physical models and its properties. Linear wave theory and 
Stokes waves are of interest due to their modelling possibility on many commercial 
software. 
 
Surface gravity waves can be classified in short-term waves and long-term waves, see 
Figure 2-2. 
 
 
Figure 2-2: Surface gravity wave types (Britannica, 2012) 
Regular and irregular waves are the ones of interest for the purpose of the thesis. These 
are described in the following subsections. 
 
2.1.1.1 Regular waves 
Regular waves are normally created by wavemakers in basins for study purpose and 
cannot be found in oceans due to the controlled environment needed. These waves are 
characterized by its amplitude and frequency.  
 
The governing theories behind these types of waves can be linear or non-linear. Each 
of those model waves, consider the orbital motion of the water particles, see Figure 2-3. 
 
One of the most well-known linear approaches is the Airy wave’s theory, which is also 
used for modelling random sea states (Goda, 2000) under this approach the modelled 
fluid flow is inviscid, incompressible and irrotational.

conditions (Molland, 2008).

- 
Stationary conditions, i.e., probability distribution of the wave elevation does 
not change in a given period of time.

The wave elevation is Gaussian distributed. 
- 
The wave heights are Rayleigh distributed.

2.1.1.3 Seakeeping criteria 
These limiting criteria are quantitative and qualitative measurements that show whether 
the vessel withstand the environment where is carrying out its mission while performing 
it successfully. These criteria consider crew habitability, safety, and operability in 
correlation with the motions the vessel experiences. (Riola & Arboleya, 2006) (Sayli, 
Alkan, Nabergoj, & Uysal, 2006). 
 
Given the importance of these criteria, it is necessary to implement standardized 
acceptance limits that guarantee a safe operability and overall mission. However, this 
is not an easy task as every ship possess its own behaviour according to its mission, 
zone of operation and operability conditions. Hence this study analyses a sole vessel 
(fishing vessel) under a range of conditions that are frequently encountered and produce 
critical ship responses.

standards asses the motions and accelerations impact from the ship responses over the

2.1.2 Numerical predictions for seakeeping 
 
Given the non-linearity of the natural environment in which a vessel sails, it is necessary 
to make use of numerical methods to facilitate the result generation with sufficient 
accuracy. 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
14 
 
There are many approaches to carry these numerical estimations. In general, the Navier-
Stokes’s equations and continuity equations comply with the requirements for solving 
the problems considered in seakeeping, however due to the time and capability for 
solving every aspect of the phenomena, this does not seem feasible. Similar problems 
are perceived when applying the RANS model for simplified models or the Euler 
solvers, which do not solve the boundary layer due to the no consideration of the 
viscosity. (Molland, 2008) 
 
From the previously stated reasons, Potential flow is the approach mostly used for 
solving the seakeeping phenomena. 
 
2.1.2.1 Potential flow analysis and panels method 
The potential flow approach has the following characteristics: 
 
- 
Assumes an irrotational flow. 
- 
Much faster than Euler and RANS solver because the mathematical problem is 
smaller (less equations). 
- 
Based on BEM (Boundary element method), this means that it only discretizes 
the boundary of the domain and not the whole volume. 
- 
Hard to simulate breaking waves and splashes. 
 
The governing equation of the potential flow is as follows: 
 
 
𝜙𝑡= (−𝑉𝑥+ 𝜙𝑆) + (𝜙𝑤+ 𝜙𝐼) 
(2.10) 
 
Where the first term describes the steady flow, and the second term describes the 
periodic flow induced by the waves: 
 
- 
𝑉𝑥 is the potential of (downstream) uniform flow with ship speed 𝑉. 
- 
ϕS is the potential of the steady flow disturbance. 
- 
ϕw is the potential of the undisturbed wave. 
- 
ϕI is the remaining unsteady potential. 
 
The following are some methods used for solving ϕI and ϕS: 
 
- 
Strip Theory: Which approach reduces the 3D problem to a 2D one by dividing 
the ship in cross-sections of given thickness and a simplification of the free 
surface condition. This method carries some inaccuracies because of the 
simplification, i.e., not accurate for solving bulbous bows, usable for slender 
ships (L/B > 5 and B/T>4), suitable for ships moving at low speeds (Fn < 0.4) 
and long waves with small motions, added resistance needs to be corrected 
empirically. 
- 
Unified theory: It also relays on the slenderness of the ship to justify a 2D 
approximation for the analysis in the near-field but coupled with a 3D approach 
for the far-field, however, is not as accurate as Strip theory method. 
- 
Green Function Method: Depending on the approach of the equation solving, 
the implementation of this method varies significantly, and it could derive to 
large errors due to the omission of ϕS. 
 
==PAGE:25==
 
15 
 
Even though linear methods are quite useful from time-computation perspective, the 
inaccuracies carry large errors, and the application is highly limited. In the cases of 
large and slender ships, these methods tend to be somehow accurate for linear regular 
waves. However, for the case of smaller and not so slender vessels these errors tend to 
be significant (Molland, 2008). From this, that a non-linear approach is more suitable 
for fishing vessels, where the discretization of the hull and free surface is done by panels 
like in the Rankine Singularity Method (Molland, 2008). 
 
One of the commercial software that implements a numerical code with a nonlinear 
approach that is still faster than a RANS model is SHIPFLOW. This software presents 
a package dedicated to seakeeping named SHIPFLOW Motions, which performs the 
added resistance, resistance, sinkage, and motions in both regular and irregular waves 
calculations. 
 
The code in charge of the calculations of the physics is called XPTD which is a time 
accurate potential flow panels method solver, for fully non-linear free surface 
conditions. (SHIPFLOW, 2021). 
 
The approach used by SHIPFLOW 6 not only simplifies the mathematical solution but 
saves computational time. However, this comes to at a cost. As explained paragraphs 
above, the viscosity is not considered under this model, hence the damping coefficients 
corresponding to the viscous effects need to be input in the software for obtaining an 
accurate result. 
 
There are different theories and approaches to calculate the damping coefficients, 
through linearization, non-linear and approaches, these methods are deeply explained 
on Himeno, 1981. Other methods are throught the use of CFD analysis by simmulating 
a roll decay motion or controlled rolling motion (ITTC, 2011), these can be also carried 
with experimental procedures in towing tanks. 
 
Particullary, Watanabe and Inoue present an interesting approach that is simple to 
implement, though the accuracy can be discussed. This method seems to be the most 
suitable for the implementation procedure and due to that this study does not focus on 
the calculation of the most accurate parameter, but on the influence of the motions on 
the seakeeping performance. For a more accurate damping coefficient calculation, a 
experimental test or a CFD Rans simulation can be implemented. 
 
2.2 
Stability 
 
The stability assessment of ships has been extensively studied through time in a vast 
literature (Molland, 2008). In the last 20 years a topic of interest that compromises 
ship’s stability from a different approach is being assessed by IMO and soon to realise 
new regulations about. This is the dynamic stability of ships, related to parametric 
rolling, surfing and more. On this matter, it is of interest to analyse and understand how 
certain working conditions cand lead to the occurrence of a phenomena as parametric 
roll on small ships as fishing vessels (understand small as length below 75 m) which 
can lead to capsize of the vessel or loss of control (Second generation intact stability 
criteria). 
 
==PAGE:26==
 
16 
 
To understand this phenomenon properly, a brief introduction to the intact stability of 
a vessel is explained and from them the theory behind the Second-generation intact 
stability criteria. 
 
2.2.1 Intact stability 
 
This concept refers to the stability of a vessel or naval structure in no damage 
conditions. The study of the stability of a ship is of great importance in order to 
guarantee the integrity of it on seas, even though some considerations and 
approximations are taken into account when performing this study i.e., no changing 
hydrostatic curves for small angles, small angles of motion, stability at larges angles, 
calm water, and geometrical considerations. (Molland, 2008) (Lewis, 1989). 
 
Is under this approach that certain regulations and criteria need to be fulfilled. IMO 
plays an important role on this matter by presenting guidance and concepts backed up 
by hundreds of researches related to ship’s stability. (Belenky, Bassler, & Spyrou, 
2011). 
 
Through time, this approach has proven being accurate enough. However certain 
conditions scape from the scope of these assumptions, and it is logical to happen due to 
that ships usually do not navigate under these ideal conditions, but under rough and 
extreme conditions depending on the route. (Molland, 2008). 
 
The mathematical values that characterize the stability of a ship are the Metacentric 
radius (GM) and the righting lever or curve of statical stability (GZ) 
 
2.2.2 Second generation intact stability criteria 
 
The second generations intact stability criteria assess phenomena as breaching, 
parametric roll, and surfing, trying to comprehend, explain and predict its occurrence 
to prevent accidents, damages, and the loss of the ship. 
 
For the sake of this thesis, only the parametric rolling phenomena is studied to 
determine design criteria related to seakeeping performance. (Belenky, Bassler, & 
Spyrou, 2011) 
 
2.2.2.1 Parametric rolling 
 
Parametric rolling is a special condition of resonance that triggers large rolling angles 
without infinite increase (resonance). This happens due to the change on the stability 
(GM) caused by the incident waves, that alters the submerged geometry, and the initial 
rolling the ship experiences, amplifying the rolling response. This behaviour is strictly 
conditioned by the frequency range and wavelength and as these conditions do not 
happen constantly, the amplified response decays in time. (Belenky, Bassler, & Spyrou, 
2011). 
 
Figure 2-7 shows an example of how the waterplane, which is linked to the moment of 
inertia of the ship, changes depending on the relative location of the vessel with respect

to the wave. This figure explains the development of the parametric roll phenomena 
when the vessel encounters the triggering conditions and the effects on the GZ curves.

4.1.3 Considerations 
 
4.1.3.1 Centre of gravity 
When using SHIPFLOW, both the longitudinal centre of gravity (LCG) and the vertical 
centre of gravity (VCG) can be specified, and this will set a specific trimming condition 
as the software calculates the centre of buoyancy by the given draft. 
 
If the longitudinal centre of gravity is not specified, them the software locates this 
coordinate right below the longitudinal centre of buoyancy (LCB) to main the trimming 
and heeling condition of the imported hull. 
 
Given these features and that the buoyancy centre can only change to compensate the 
weight by altering the trimming conditions, the draft is set free to change according to 
the variation of the centre of gravity. 
 
A particular case of study is the trawling condition. In this condition the vessel is under 
an external load (purse drag) that affect the trimming of the ship. As SHIPFLOW 
Motions does not have a feature to account for external loads, it was seen necessary to 
recreate the condition by shifting the centre of gravity by virtually adding a weight at 
the appropriate location that will produce the same sink, trim and heel.

4.2 
Methods and tools 
 
As an initial step for the study of the behaviour of the Stella Nova IX (Ex-Carmona), it 
was seen proper to develop a study of the responses of the vessel under a range of 
conditions. The selected condition is the first one (departure condition) at 12 knots with 
a wave height of 0.85 m (0.0184 Lpp) and a range of wavelengths from 0.25Lpp to 
7Lpp. The study is carried out under head waves to study heave and pitch motions and 
under beam-waves to study rolling motions. Along with this, a study of the heave, pitch 
and droll decay motion is performed in order to obtain the natural period for each 
motion (a general damping coefficient was assumed in this procedure). Under this 
initial study it is expected to understand the resonance frequencies of the vessel. Results 
of this first study are presented in section 5.1. 
 
Once the initial study is finished, the working conditions and parametric rolling are 
evaluated as shown in figures Figure 4-5 and Figure 4-6. 
 
The 3D model of the fishing vessel is created using Rhino 3D student license ver.6. To 
avoid any problem with how SHIPFLOW generates the panelization from the 
geometry, it is necessary to simplify some “complicated shapes”. The modifications are 
expected to do not alter the results and to accelerate the convergence of the simulation. 
In this way the aft part of the keel was softened, and the transom was made completely 
vertical. The complications take place due to how SHIPFLOW’s Potential code 
approaches the geometry and how the code solves the flow behind the transom of the 
vessel. This is further explained in section 6.2. 
 
The wave characteristics were obtained as explained in section 4.1.2.1 through the data 
provided by ERA5 and with help from SSPA by gathering the data referred. 
 
Once the information from the vessel and the wave characteristics are obtained, it is 
possible to proceed with the computational analysis in SHIPFLOW for the seakeeping 
assessment. With help of the developed code RBSMC the final responses in different 
zones of the ship are obtained and possible to be evaluated through the customized 
criteria. 
 
On the side of the parametric rolling assessment, the evaluation is done mathematically 
through the vulnerability criteria specified in sub-section 2.2.2.2 (Belenky, Bassler, & 
Spyrou, 2011).

5.1 
Ship responses 
 
Under the conditions specified in sub-section 4.2, a systematic study of the vessel was 
carried out in order to understand the heave, pitch and roll motions of the vessel. 
 
It is important to mention that SHIPFLOW had certain limitations when trying to 
compute large waves. To put it simple, The code was not able to “sustain” larger than 
the domain waves, this caused the vessel to encounter smaller wave heights than the 
input ones when the large wavelength see. The response of the vessel then, is smaller 
than what it should be. This behaviour is noted in Figure 5-1, where the asymptotic 
value of the RAO’s for Heave and Pitch are smaller than the 1. Such responses ar not 
physically possible as in reality a vessel’s response follows the wave motion at long

6 
Discussion 
 
6.1 
Results discussion 
 
6.1.1 Ship responses 
Fishing vessels seem to differ in behaviour from tankers and containers. The studied 
vessel presents a response to waves quite different of what could be expected from 
larger ships. As Figure 5-4 suggests, the resonance occurs at large wavelengths, 
especially for the roll motion, with a minor increase in the motions at high frequency 
waves. This unexpected behaviour opens more questions regarding the criteria for 
designing a fishing vessel. 
 
Its constantly changing displacement and speed increase the range of possibilities for a 
“proper” design. These different and variable working conditions, along with the 
environmental characteristics, create a series of different possible scenarios to study 
and to optimize. 
 
A good way to approach the large number of possibilities would be the analysis under 
irregular waves. However, there is a limited number of computational tools that can be 
used to simulate this type of vessels without incurring in high uncertainties. 
 
6.1.2 Seakeeping results 
In general, the vessel studied presents results under the customized criteria, except for 
the trawling conditions, which seem to be highly affected by the shifted centre of 
gravity, producing roll motion of higher magnitude in comparison to the other 
conditions. This is supported by the comment of the skipper and from the designer. 
They both stated that the highest responses take place when the vessel is trawling.  
 
The vessel present is comfortable in most of the scenarios studied, again the trawling 
condition seems to escape from the trend of the other results. (See Table 5-2). 
 
The external force when trawling sets the initial condition for rolling motion. This 
behaviour was only present during the trawling condition and the rolling effects were 
not in the same magnitude when the vessel was simulated in lightship condition or fully 
loaded, even under beam waves. 
 
Not only rolling motions were higher when simulating the trawling condition. Pitch 
motion presented as well high values (See Figure D-19 and Figure D-23); this is also 
related to the position of the centre of gravity that was shifted in order to simulate the 
external force from the purse. In addition to this, conditions 7, 9 and 10 present larger 
pitch angles in comparison to other simulations. However, the vertical accelerations are 
low, which reflects slow pitch potions with mid-high amplitude. On this matter, a more 
forward centre of gravity seems to produce higher pitch motions. Another condition 
with mid-high pitch angles is condition 02 which was analysed under the same waves 
as condition 09 and close speeds (12 and 11.75 knots), in this case it is possible to see 
that condition 09 presents higher responses of this motion, where the vessel condition 
differs in the displacement with a further LCG from the transom. The wave direction is 
==PAGE:76==
 
66 
 
suspected to be the most important factor that differentiates the responses in these two 
simulations. 
 
Other remarking responses, that were observed in every analysis, were the pitch angle 
and pitch acceleration in the engine room. It seems that because of location of the 
floatation centre, centre of gravity and the vertical location of the engine room deck, 
the responses in this zone are quite like the ones at the bow (close value responses). 
This is also supported by the skipper comments, who stated that there are rather strong 
motions in the engine room and in the bow. 
 
On another note, the MSI and MII present small values as can be expected by the 
magnitude of the accelerations and only surpass the limiting criteria in conditions 5 and 
6. The MII is estimated for both roll and pitch as both motions are large in these two 
conditions. Meanwhile the MSI reaches its maximum value in condition 5, where the 
pitch and roll motions are the highest.  
 
Regarding the frequency of deck wetness, it is shown that the higher values take place 
when the vessel is trawling (see Table 5-7 and Table 5-8). This is due to the high vertical 
accelerations and motions. On this matter a match with the answers from the 
questionnaire was found, where the skipper stated that the trawling condition presented 
strong motions. It is also important to point that fishing vessels present a feature called 
camber, which is a curvature in the deck that allows to drain the water thought scuppers 
by the sides of the freeboard. This design is part of the concept of fishing vessels due 
to the high frequency of water on deck when operating. On this note the deck wetness 
probability are 1.5% and 27.6%. Condition 7 shows that at fully loaded condition, when 
the freeboard is small, large waves are critical for the criteria. 
 
From the questionnaire it is seen that the vessel presents low responses to large 
wavelengths and high roll responses in short waves, there was no comment related to 
medium wavelengths. This is in correlation with Figure 5-5, where a similar behaviour 
is observed. At the same time, the responses of the vessel in fully loaded, when the 
condition is characterised by a negative trim (forward draft deeper than aft draft), 
difficult the manoeuvring according to the skipper. 
 
6.1.3 Parametric rolling 
 
No parametric rolling phenomena seems to be followed by any of the conditions 
studied, according to the vulnerability criteria. However, after estimating the required 
parameters that could trigger parametric rolling, it is observed that the event is likely to 
happen under certain circumstances. For example, a more appropriate wavelength 
under the trawling condition could have triggered the phenomenon, as the velocity 
requirement was fulfilled, and the GM presented a significant variation of almost 0.49 
m. Or longer waves under the light-ship weight condition could have developed the 
event as these produce a higher variation of GM. 
 
As Table 5-17 shows, the dependency of the event regarding the parameters p and q is 
also linked to the damping coefficient of the vessel. The estimated damping coefficient 
may influence greatly depending on its value and condition. For instance, at lower 
values of p (0.15), but inside the initial instability zone range, the damping coefficients 
are high enough to prevent the phenomena from taking place. The requirement for the 
==PAGE:77==
 
67 
 
parametric rolling to take place for lower values of p, is an even higher value of q, 
which represents the variation frequency GMa (GM variation amplitude) to such extent 
that the ship loses its stability (negative GM). 
 
The behaviour of the required changing GM is close to the values of the actual estimated 
change when the vessel is sailing at 12 knots. As this condition is reached, the remaining 
ones would be dependent of the wave characteristics (wavelength) which is also in the 
range of the studied conditions. 
 
By studying the parameters presented in Table 5-17, the phenomena is likely to happen 
at light-ship condition, where the damping coefficient is smaller due to a shallow draft 
and at smaller wavelengths when the speed is lower if the variation of GM is large 
enough, which could happen in high-stepped waves. However, at fully loaded 
condition, parametric rolling is less likely to take place as the damping coefficient is 
too large.  
 
6.2 
Methods discussion 
 
Due to the conditions and characteristics that fishing vessels present, the results 
obtained do not apply to other fishing vessels. However, the methodology can be 
replicated not only for similar vessels but for different fishing vessels within a range of 
characteristics that the computational tools allow. As Figure 4-5and Figure 4-6 show, 
the workflow is characterised by a succession of steps that can be followed for different 
hull shapes, working conditions and environmental characteristics. 
 
Some of the tools used are straight forward, as the CAD software Rhino 3D, which 
allowed an easy and fast modelling of the hull and its corresponding adjustments for 
the import to SHIPFLOW and for the use of RBSMC. 
 
The extrapolation of the results obtained by SHIPFLOW, and the evaluation of the 
Parametric rolling conditions were possible to be done in MATLAB. 
 
SHIPFLOW is an useful and versatile software that allows for the computation of a 
potential solver that can compute the responses of the ship in calm water and in waves. 
However, some limitations were identified when setting the different conditions to be 
analysed. The first limitation falls in the approach the software computes the flow 
around the vessel’s transom. This large transom is a challenging geometry that 
continuously sinks and emerges from the water. As potential flow does not consider the 
viscosity of the fluid (irrotational flow), the physical behaviour of the flow at the 
transom level is not computed properly. More specifically, the constant change between 
wet and dry transom creates a flow recirculation at this region, which is not possible to 
be directly computed, unless certain considerations and adjustments are taken. Another 
challenge was found during the panelisation of the geometry. This feature faces 
problems trying to panelise the quite complex aft end of the keel, compressing the 
element excessively and even overlapping them. For this reason, the geometry of this 
part was simplified as shown in Figure 4-8. 
 
As explained in sub-section 4.1.3.1, there is no implemented feature that can apply an 
external load to the vessel for which it was necessary to recreate the same effects in a 
approximated way. The external load was simulated as an added weight (11.3 tons) on 
==PAGE:78==
 
68 
 
the main deck and located at the same longitudinal and horizontal position of the acting 
force. The extra weight produced the same trim and heel angles that the load will cause 
in static conditions. At the same time, the added weight was simulated but shifting the 
centre of gravity of the vessel according to the required trim and heel angle in static 
conditions. 
 
Regarding the time computation, this was dependent of the condition simulated. Some 
of these lasted for 12 h and others surpassed the 24 hours. The dependency was due to 
the convergence criteria specified in sub-section Methods and tools4.2. In some 
conditions the convergence was reached when the error in the resistance had a value of 
1%. In other cases, the solution converged at 100 encountered waves and the longest 
simulations lasted for a total of 10000 iterations (time steps). In order to set an 
appropriate amount of data, a number of encountered waves was set depending of the 
wavelength and angle of incidence. This was also done for reaching enough repetitions 
in the responses. 
 
Finally, there was an interesting observation when studying the RAO’s under different 
wavelengths. It was seen thought PARAVIEW that SHIPFLOW was non-
dimensionalising the results with a undermeasured wave height when simulating long 
wavelengths. The presumption is that this underestimation happens because the domain 
is too small to capture the correct wave profile. This observation was communicated to 
the developer who agreed with the statement. This issue was solved by calculating the 
RAO’s by the corresponding wave height that was observed by the ship instead of the 
wave height defined at the inlet boundary.

Conclusion 
 
The purpose of this thesis was to (a) define and test relevant seakeeping criteria for a 
stern trawler fishing vessel (Stella Nova IX) from a seakeeping and stability 
perspective, and to (b) propose an assessment methodology to optimize such criteria, 
using the software SHIPFLOW. To achieve these objectives, a systematic analysis was 
performed on how different parameters (common wave characteristics) influence the 
seakeeping characteristics of the fishing vessel, specifically the changing centre of 
gravity (see section 4.1.3) to develop suitable seakeeping criteria for fishing vessels. 
 
From this study, it seems that fishing vessels behave quite different from large ships. 
The behaviour can be attributed to the high damping coefficients in relation to the 
weight displacement of the vessel and other proportion ratios. The estimation of the 
damping coefficient is done under assumptions and accepted uncertainties, given that 
the study of this parameter is a whole topic itself and it escapes from the scope and 
intention of the thesis. 
 
The customized criteria present high limiting values for most of the studied conditions 
with exceptions of the trawling condition. It is recommended to evaluate these 
particular conditions with a tool that can simulate an external force, so the shifting of 
the CG is not necessary and less assumptions are taken. 
 
These criteria can be used for the study of more fishing vessels to assess the responses 
under different working conditions. To refine the mentioned criteria, more studies of 
similar fishing vessels can be carried out. If the ship’s responses are in the same 
magnitude as the ones presented in this report, then the limiting values can be adjusted 
depending on the working condition. The trawling condition needs to be specified as a 
particular scenario, where the limits of the criteria might change due to the nature of 
the operation. 
 
The fishing vessel model was also tested for compliance with the second-generation 
IMO intact stability criteria, regarding the parametric roll phenomenon, followed by an 
evaluation of the triggering conditions of the phenomenon. 
 
Parametric rolling is a phenomenon that can be triggered by a series of different 
scenarios and fishing vessels are not exempt from it. However, they are less likely to 
experience it at fully loaded conditions, where the damping coefficients are high enough 
to prevent the phenomena. 
 
From the performed analysis, it is seen that the studied vessel is more vulnerable to 
experience parametric rolling when sailing in light-ship condition, if the wave 
conditions are met and if the damping coefficient of the vessel is low enough to not 
mitigate the phenomenon. 
 
Lastly, in this study, the feasibility and efficiency of the SHIPFLOW Motions Potential 
Flow code for determining the motion of a fishing vessel in moderate seas and under 
different wave patterns, were tested. 
 
==PAGE:80==
 
70 
 
SHIPFLOW Motions 6 has room to improve. As stated before, the panelisation of the 
hull can be improved to be able to capture more “complicated” geometries and a feature 
of a resizable domain could be of help when simulating long wavelengths. 
 
Given the geometry of these type of vessels (large keels and large wet transoms in 
proportion to hull), potential flow codes seem challenged to solve and reach results as 
straightforward as with larger ships. 
 
7.1 
Suggestions for further research 
 
More scenarios dependent of the wave characteristics should be studied to have a deeper 
understanding of the fishing vessel’s responses. As an extension of the study, not only 
common condition but also all possible scenarios should be analysed. 
 
Another extension of this study could be done by analysing more types of hulls of 
different proportions (such as Lpp/B, B/T and Cb) to test the robustness of the 
methodology and to identify if the customized criteria suit more fishing vessels of 
similar and different characteristics. On this matter, the customized criteria can be 
refined by setting more accurate limiting values, ranges and making it dependent of the 
working condition. 
 
A deeper investigation of the damping coefficient and its influence on the ship 
responses, specially roll motion can be carried out to understand its influence on the 
parametric rolling phenomenon. 
 
As only the Parametric rolling phenomena has been studied, a next step can be the 
assessment of the remaining phenomena specified in the Second-generation IMO 
stability criteria. 
 
The code RBSMC was developed to extrapolate the responses from the centre of gravity 
of the vessel, obtained by SHIPFLOW. This code can be applied to different hull 
shapes. It will be interesting to develop an interphase with SHIPFLOW that allows a 
direct import of the results to the code. 
 
With the mapped responses on the “vertical levels”, obtained from RBSMC, it is 
possible to carry a structural analysis of the foundations of equipment on the different 
decks to investigate if these are well designed. 
 
Regarding the methodology, it would be interesting to use the new developed code by 
Flowtech, SHIPFLOW Motions 7. Which seems to have solved many of the 
shortcomings in SHIPFLOW Motions 6 and re-asses the motions in a more robust way.
