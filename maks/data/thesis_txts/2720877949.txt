Abstract
AlphaFold2 (AF2) is a deep learning model developed by Google DeepMind, that accu-
rately predicts the three-dimensional structure of proteins with atomic precision. This
master’s thesis evaluates AF2’s capability to predict variant protein structures. AF2
was compared to a dataset of 116 variant crystal structures and four wild-type (WT)
crystal structures from four different proteins. Furthermore, for each of the four WT
structures, similar WT crystal structures were obtained through the Protein Data Bank
(PDB). This was done to estimate the experimental inaccuracies in protein structure
determination and was used as a control dataset. For every crystal variant structure,
an AF2 structure was generated. The Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) was calcu-
lated for each structure pair and for each of the four protein variant datasets for both
the crystal structures and the AF2 generated structures. The RMSD was calculated
for each structure pair and for each of the four protein WT control datasets as well.
The AF2 predicted χ1 angles were also evaluated by comparing to both variant and
WT crystal structure χ1 angles. The results showed that AF2 predicts protein variant
structures of high quality, but it was not possible to assess if AF2 was able to clearly
and reliably distinguish between variant and WT structures as the calculated differences
mainly were within the range of experimental inaccuracy. The results from AF2 were
compared to ESMFold, another protein structure prediction model. Furthermore, abla-
tion of the input Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) to AF2 was shown to have little
effect on producing AF2 structures that more closely resemble crystal variant structures
compared to using the entire MSA.

Resumé
AlphaFold2 (AF2) er en deep learning model, udviklet af Google DeepMind, der nø-
jagtigt forudsiger den tredimensionelle struktur af proteiner med atomisk præcision.
Dette kandidatspeciale evaluerer AF2’s evne til at forudsige proteinvarianters tredimen-
sionelle struktur. AF2 blev sammenlignet med et datasæt af 116 krystalstrukturer af
varianter og fire krystalstrukturer af vildtype proteiner fra fire forskellige proteiner. Yder-
mere, blev et kontroldatasæt konstrueret med krystalstrukturer fra the Protein Data
Bank (PDB) for hver vildtype krystalstruktur. Kontroldatasættet blev benyttet til at
estimere måleusikkerhederne i krystalstrukturerne. For hver variant krystalstruktur blev
en AF2 struktur genereret. Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) blev beregnet for
hvert strukturpar og for hvert proteinvariant datasæt (krystal og AF2). Ligeledes, blev
RMSD beregnet for hvert vildtype kontroldatasæt. De forudsagte AF2 variantstruk-
turers χ1 torsionsvinkler blev sammenlignet med variantstrukturernes og WT- struktur-
ernes χ1 torsionsvinkler. Resultaterne viser, at AF2 forudsiger variantstrukturer af høj
kvalitet, men det var ikke muligt at vurdere hvorvidt AF2 kan skelne klart og pålideligt
mellem variant- og vildtype-strukturer, da de beregnede forskelle for det meste, var in-
denfor den eksperimentelle usikkerhed. Resultaterne fra AF2 valideres yderligere ved
at sammenligne med ESMFold, en anden model der kan forudsige proteinstrukturer.
Derudover vises det at en reduktion af antallet af sekvenser i input Multiple Sequence
Alignment (MSA)’et til AF2 havde en lille indflydelse på at producere proteinstrukturer,
der lignede variantkrystalstrukturerne mere i forhold til at bruge hele MSA’et.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The evolutionary paths of proteins follow certain steps that include amino acid substi-
tutions, insertions, and deletions. Protein evolution is constrained by thermodynamic
stability as well as evolutionary conservation. Thus, protein sequence modifications that
result in an unstable or nonfunctional protein will generally not be conserved. The most
frequent mutations are amino acid substitutions, as this type of mutation normally has
a higher propensity to preserve thermodynamic stability and evolutionary conservation
as the resulting structural change is small (Arodź and Płonka, 2012).
A significant
minority of amino acid substitutions leads to disease, and it is therefore of great value
if the effect can be predicted and understood (Cagiada et al., 2021). The prediction
of variant effects is an ongoing challenge but recently, protein language models such as
ESM1b have been utilized to predict variant effects and have achieved impressive results
(Brandes et al., 2022; Rives et al., 2021). Yet, if it is possible to include structure in-
formation it could lead to even better results as well as aid our understanding of how
disease-causing variants affect protein stability and function from a structural and mech-
anistic perspective. Furthermore, accurate structure predictions of protein variants can
aid in the interpretation of results from high-throughput experiments (Mcbride et al.,
2023).
In November 2020, Google DeepMind announced the deep learning model, AlphaFold2
(AF2), capable of producing structure predictions nearing experimental accuracy (Jumper
et al., 2021). Thus, with access to computational resources, it is possible to do large-
scale structure predictions with AF2 in many domains within structural biology and
potentially increase the data foundation for assessing variant effects.
The scope of this thesis is to evaluate AF2’s capabilities to predict protein variant
structures. A variant structure is defined as a variant crystal structure that differs by
a single amino acid compared to the WT crystal structure. Several studies have been
carried out that assess AF2’s capabilities to predict variant structures and variant effects
and it continues to be a challenge to validate the results and define how to utilize AF2
for variant structure and effect predictions (Mcbride et al., 2023).
In this master’s thesis the assessment of AF2’s capabilities to predict protein variant
structures focuses on comparing AF2 variant structures to variant crystal structures
and WT crystal structures. The RMSD and χ1 angles are used to evaluate how closely a
AF2 structure is to a given crystal structure. The approach for comparing variant struc-
tures (both AF2 and crystal) is inspired by the paper from Arodź and Płonka, 2012. A
subset of the dataset from Arodź and Płonka, 2012 is also used in the evaluation of AF2.
Concretely, the assessment of AF2 seeks to determine whether a given predicted AF2

Theory
2.1
Protein Structure
A protein is composed of a linear sequence of amino acids and each amino acid (20 in
total) has distinct physicochemical properties. The conformational space of proteins
is large with many degrees of freedom, and it would be too laborious for a protein to
seek out every possible conformation as described in Levinthal’s paradox. Therefore,
(many) proteins fold into a well-defined three-dimensional structure that is defined by
the sequence of amino acids (Anfinsen’s dogma) (Kuriyan et al., 2013).
The amino acids in a protein are connected by peptide bonds (-CO-NH-) and the
conformation of each residue is described by the three torsion angles of the backbone
denoted ω, ϕ, and ψ and the side chain torsion angles denoted by χ1, ..., χ5 (Figure 2.1).
The torsion angle of the peptide bond, ω is planar (≈180◦) with a double bond character
as the π electrons of -NH- bond are in the same plane as the π-electrons of the -CO-
bond. The two other torsion angles of the backbone, ϕ and ψ describe the conformation
of the -N-Cα- and -Cα-C- bonds, respectively. The torsion angles of the backbone and
the side chains describe the conformational properties of the protein (Williamson, 2012).
Figure 2.1: Torsion angles of the protein backbone and sidechain. Source: (Bhuyan and
Gao, 2011).
The description of a protein structure using torsion angles is very specific and it is
useful to describe protein structures in broader terms as well. Briefly, a protein structure

2.1 Protein Structure
4
can be divided into four levels: primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary (Kuriyan
et al., 2013).
The primary structure is the amino acid sequence. The secondary structure describes
the backbone conformations in a region of the protein that leads to a distinct substruc-
ture pattern, denoted a secondary structure element. The main occurrences of secondary
structure elements are α-helices and β-strands. The α-helix is formed when the consec-
utive backbone torsion angles ϕ and ψ have angles ≈−60◦and ≈−40◦, respectively
(Kuriyan et al., 2013). This leads to the hydrogen bond pattern [i to (i + 4)] between
the -CO- and the -NH- bond of the protein backbone (Zvelebil and Baum, 2007). The
β-strand is formed when consecutive amino acid residues adopt the angles: ϕ = −120◦
and ψ = 120◦. This results in the formation of hydrogen bonds that make the secondary
structure favorable as this conformation gives a lower energy state. The tertiary struc-
ture is the organization of secondary structure elements into a protein domain. Protein
domains can exist and function independently. Lastly, the quaternary structure is the
assembly of several subunits (polypeptide chains) into a multimeric complex (Kuriyan
et al., 2013).

2.2
Missense Variants and Assessment of
Variant Effects
Amino acid substitutions, insertions, or deletions are the three main methods in which
protein evolution happens (Williamson, 2012). Point mutations occur when a nucleotide
is substituted in the coding DNA so the codon may prescribe a different amino acid. Con-
sequently, the sequence of amino acids that a protein consists of is not static across time
and therefore changes. The resulting protein after an amino acid substitution is a mis-
sense variant. Amino acid substitutions are generally tolerated compared to insertions or
deletions (indels) but can lead to loss of function due to loss of protein stability or due to
perturbation of the active site (Cagiada et al., 2021). Nonetheless, a fair share of amino
acid substitutions is tolerated and might even improve the protein function and thus
amino acid substitutions also facilitate the exploration of both improved functionality,
new functionalities, and stability improvements (Arodź and Płonka, 2012). Typically, a
fraction of amino acid substitutions will be neutral and have similar properties as the
WT (Cagiada et al., 2021; Jepsen et al., 2020).
Assessing the effect of a missense variant can be achieved using experimental and
computer-based methods. Experimental high-throughput methods where the effects of
thousands of missense variants are probed have increasingly contributed to the knowl-
edge landscape of protein variants. Examples of such experimental methods are mul-
tiplexed assays of variant effect (MAVE) and variant abundance by massively parallel
sequencing (VAMP-seq) (Jepsen et al., 2020). Additionally, the convergence of ever-
increasing sequence data and computational resources and improvements in deep learn-
ing methods have resulted in protein language models that can predict variant effects
comparable to experimental methods (Brandes et al., 2022).
2.3
Protein Evolution and Multiple Sequence
Alignments
Proteins with common evolutionary origin and function are called orthologs and proteins
with a common ancestor are called homologs (Zvelebil and Baum, 2007). Using this in-
formation it is possible to compare sequences and gain information about conservation,
function, stability, and structure. Homologous sequences are compared in a Multiple
Sequence Alignment (MSA) as it provides insights into the evolutionary relationships
between sequences (Lund et al., 2005). In a MSA, the rows represent the sequences and
the columns the amino acid residues at each position in the sequences. By utilizing the
patterns in the MSA it is possible to infer contacts and important residues for function-
ality (Rives et al., 2021).

There exist many equally accepted algorithms for constructing MSAs. Traditionally,
a MSA is built by pairwise alignments that are scored using a similarity matrix, for
instance, BLOSUM-62. The pairwise alignments are built using dynamic programming
algorithms. The most well-known algorithms are the Needleman-Wunsch and Smith-
Waterman algorithms. The simplest approach to creating a MSA is aligning all found
sequences to the query one by one. More advanced methods exist such as calculating
all the pairwise distances between all sequences and then building a phylogenetic tree
using a clustering method, for instance, UPGMA (Lund et al., 2005). The MSA is then
built guided by the tree.
It is increasingly important to have fast search methods when building MSAs. This is
due to the rapid increase of sequences in databases. If it is possible to find sequences that
effectively match the query fast, it is possible to build deep and high-quality MSAs. Many
search methods are built utilizing profile Hidden Markov models. The search methods
HMMer and HMM-HMM–based lightning-fast iterative sequence search (HHblits) are
based on profile Hidden Markov Models. These two search methods are used in AF2
(Jumper et al., 2021). The ColabFold implementation of AF2 uses the MMseqs2 search
method that uses some of the principles of sequence alignments and MSA generation
mentioned above (Steinegger and Söding, 2017).

2.4
Transformers
The advent of protein language models has paved the way for powerful predictive models
in biology. The underlying architecture in protein language models is the transformer.
The transformer architecture was first described in the paper Attention is all you need
(Vaswani et al., 2017).
==PAGE:15==
2.4 Transformers
8
The transformer is a deep neural network designed initially for translation tasks
but is now used more broadly. The transformer consists of two main components: an
encoder and a decoder that both utilize an attention mechanism (Figure 2.4).
The
attention mechanism enables the model to learn contextual relations between tokens. In
protein language models it is the encoder that is useful because the learned contextual
relations can be used as input to downstream models (Rives et al., 2021). The input to
a protein language model is for instance a MSA or a protein sequence and the protein
language model then learns the contextual relations in the MSA or protein sequence. The
Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT) model is particularly
powerful and uses the encoder from the transformer.
The concepts from BERT are
used in both AF2 and ESMFold. In the following two sections, the Feed forward neural
network (FFNN) is described briefly as it is used in the encoder and then the encoder
architecture is described in the section after (Section 2.4.2).
2.4.1
Feed Forward Neural Networks
The Feed forward neural network (FFNN) is central in deep learning and transformers
and a brief description is provided.
A FFNN is the simplest artificial neural network and is used for classification or
regression tasks. The FFNN resembles the biological neural networks in the brain which
gave the algorithm its name.
Let M be the number of neurons in the input layer (number of inputs) and D the
number of neurons in the output layer (the predictions) then the FFNN can be thought

3.1
AlphaFold2
AF2 is developed by Google Deepmind and consists of several tools but the core part
of AF2 is the deep learning model that predicts the three-dimensional structure of a
protein from its sequence (Jumper et al., 2021). The performance is attributed to the
novel neural network architectures, specifically the two main modules, the Evoformer
module and the Structure module (Figure 3.1).
Figure 3.1: The high level AF2 architecture. Source: (Jumper et al., 2021).
==PAGE:19==
3.1 AlphaFold2
12
The AF2 architecture consists of 3 main parts: the feature embedding, the Evoformer
Module, and the Structure module. During inference, the Evoformer module and the
Structure module are executed three times which is termed recycling. This iterative
approach improves accuracy as the final loss is applied repeatedly across all layers. In this
thesis, the ColabFold implementation of AF2 is used as ColabFold is readily available.
3.1.1
Feature Embedding
The main inputs to the Evoformer module are a MSA and a pair representation. The two
main inputs are created from several derived input features such as template features,
amino-acid sequence features, and MSA features.
3.1.2
Evoformer
The Evoformer consists of Nblock = 48 identical Evoformer blocks. The output of the
last Evoformer block is a highly processed MSA representation where the first sequence
embedding (the query), and the pair representation are fed to the Structure module.
Figure 3.2: The Evoformer module. Source: (Jumper et al., 2021).
The Evoformer module uses a transformer-based architecture that iteratively updates
and improves the information in the MSA and pair representations. Within each Evo-
former block, the updates can be divided into MSA operations and pair representation
operations. The MSA operations use self-attention to capture evolutionary relationships
among the sequences in the MSA. The pair representation operations use self-attention
as well to capture the interactions between residues. Additionally, in each Evoformer,
there are non-attention-based operations, such as FFNNs and gating mechanisms.

3.1.3
Structure Module
The Structure module consists of Nblock = 8 blocks and uses the outputs from the
Evoformer module to predict the three-dimensional atom coordinates. The final output
is the three-dimensional structure.
Figure 3.3: The Structure module. Source: (Jumper et al., 2021).
In the Structure module, there is a sub-module called the Invariant Point Attention
(IPA) module that updates the target sequence representation (single representation).
The output of the IPA is then passed through a transition block that updates the transla-
tion and rotation matrix for each backbone frame. The final structure is predicted using
small per-residue networks. Lastly, the structure is relaxed using gradient descent in the
Amber32 force field. The quality of the final structure is described with the Predicted
Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT) score which is a per residue confidence score.
3.2
ColabFold
ColabFold is an implementation of AF2 hosted on Google Colaboratory, a proprietary
version of Jupyter Notebook (Jumper et al., 2021; Mirdita et al., 2022; Mirdita et al.,
2019; Mirdita et al., 2017; Mitchell et al., 2019).
ColabFold differs in several ways
from AF2. Firstly, the construction of MSAs is different where ColabFold uses Many-
against-Many sequence searching (MMseqs2) instead of HHblits and HMMer. Secondly,
ColabFold compiles only one model and reuse weights, whereas AF2 compiles five dif-
ferent models (Jumper et al., 2021; Mirdita et al., 2022). Lastly, Colabfold uses an
early stop criterion to avoid additional recycling if a suﬀiciently accurate structure was
already predicted.

Figure 3.4: The workflow in ColabFold. Source: (Mirdita et al., 2022).
When using ColabFold in Google Colaboratory, the input sequence is sent to an
MMseqs2 server searching the UniRef 100 database and a database with environmental
sequences. Two MSAs are obtained and then decomposed to a single MSA that is used as
an input feature to AF2. MMseqs2 is also used to find structure templates. The output
is five models ranked in descending order from highest pLDDT score to lowest (Figure
3.4). In this thesis, the highest-ranked structure is used unless otherwise specified.
Initially, default settings are used for all structure predictions (Table 3.2). Later AF2
structures are generated where the number of sequences in the input MSA is reduced.
The number of sequences is reduced by subsampling 128 and 64 sequences randomly from
the MMseqs2 generated MSA from the default setting run. The custom MSAs contains
several thousand sequences and are ordered by Hamming distance. Additionally, two
AF2 structures are generated for the two variants in the dataset with a large RMSD
between the variant crystal structure and WT crystal structure using MSAs with 32
sequences. The sequences are sampled by dividing the custom MSA into 32 chunks and
then randomly sampling one sequence within each chunk.

The matchmaker command from ChimeraX is used to obtain the consecutive protein

The input to the matchmaker is the .pdb files of two pro-

tein structures. By default, ChimeraX does the following before calculating the RMSD

• Secondary structure assignments are recalculated using the DSSP algorithm. This

• Generating sequence alignments using the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm with the

be more similar to the crystal variant structures.
The pre-processing of the MSA in AF2 reduces the stochastic behavior in the network.
We therefore randomly subsample the custom MSA to increase the stochastic behavior
(del Alamo et al., 2022; Jumper et al., 2021). We hypothesized that decreasing the depth
of the MSA might push AF2 to predict different structures because AF2 exploits the
information in the MSA as the raw input features for end-to-end training. The MSAs
were reduced to 128 sequences 64 sequences for all variant structures in the P00720
and P61626 datasets. For two crystal variant structures that showed the highest RMSD
compared to the WT crystal structure in the P61626 variant dataset, 10 AF2 predictions
were made for each with 10 different modified MSAs containing 32 sequences.
Furthermore, we analyzed how close the five different AF2 structures compare to
the crystal variant structure and the crystal WT structure across the different MSA
inputs. It is not clear that a specific model has the propensity to resemble either the
crystal variant structure or the crystal WT structure more compared to the other models
(Appendix A.4, A.5). On the contrary, it seems that the less information that there is
in the MSA the larger the increase in RMSD to both the crystal variant structures and
the crystal WT structures.
4.5.1
ESMFold Structures Are Close to AF2 Models
We compare the ESMFold protein variant structures to the AF2 structures, crystal
variant structures, and WT crystal reference structure.
The initial analysis shows that ESMFold with one recycle is identical to the ESMFold
structures with recycling three times. The ESMFold structures resemble the generated
AF2 models where the entire custom MSA is used. As the MSA depth is reduced the
AF2 structures quality decrease compared to the ESMFold structures, meaning that the
RMSD increases for the AF2 structures (Figure 4.8).
When comparing the ESMFold structures to both variant crystal structures and WT
structures the results are similar to that of AF2 where the entire MSA is used.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
Increasing our understanding of variant effects is achieved with experimental methods
in tandem with computational methods that elucidate aspects of biology such as protein
stability, protein evolution, and protein function. Moreover, a better understanding of
variant effects is important for understanding how single amino acid mutations result in
disease. Using structural information from AF2 can contribute to the knowledge land-
scape of missense variants by integrating predicted variant structures into variant effect
prediction methods, and by adding interpretability to the results of high-throughput
experiments such as MAVEs and VAMP-seqs. However, AF2 was only trained on stable
structures so it is not known if AF2 can predict accurate variant structures and clearly
discern between variant structures and WT structures.
The scope of this thesis was to assess AF2 capabilities to predict variant structures.
The assessment was done by analyzing the structural changes between variant crystal
structures and AF2 structures. It was observed that the regions that diverged the most
and therefore resulted in larger structural changes were prevalent in loop regions, the
C-terminus and the N-terminus. Furthermore, the loop variability in AF2 generated
structures was lower compared to crystal structures and it was observed that regions of
lower-than-average pLDDT scores in AF2 structures resulted in the main discrepancy be-
tween predicted and experimental structures. It is important to note that the calculated
RMSDs between WT crystal structures and crystal variant structures are < 1.4 Å (≈
width of a carbon atom) for 114/116 variants. It is therefore subtle structural changes
that AF2 needed to capture and often within the range of experimental uncertainty.
It was diﬀicult to discern whether the AF2 predicted variant structures were distin-
guishable from the crystal WT structures and the crystal variant structures as many of
the RMSDs were within the range of experimental uncertainty and the range of confor-
mational flexibility in proteins. This made it diﬀicult to conclude if AF2 actually had
predicted the ’correct’ variant structure. It was therefore considered to modify the MSA
input as it might leave out evolutionary information and activate the stochastic behav-
ior in the AF2 network. It was possible to generate diverse structure ensembles but of
poorer quality compared to using all the evolutionary information (the entire MSA).
The interpretation of the results is limited to single-domain proteins as those were
the only proteins represented in the dataset. Hence, increasing the dataset would allow
for broader and more representative results. It was considered to add additional variant
crystal structures to the dataset for comparison but the noise in the initial structures
that were obtained was high and would require further filtering to obtain an informative
dataset. Moreover, few proteins are as well-studied as the ones used in this thesis so the
dataset that one could possibly create, will likely be of inferior quality.
==PAGE:37==
5.1 Future Directions
30
5.1
Future Directions
Naturally, it is interesting to uncover the MSA modifications in more depth and how
the different modifications affect the structures. One could speculate that specific vari-
ant structures can be modeled better by subsampling the custom MSA in a specific
way, by only introducing a subset of evolutionary information or introducing mutations
throughout the MSA.
It would also be interesting to look into how the five different structures for each
prediction differ and if it is always the case that the highest confident structure can be
interpreted as an average of the five predicted structures. Lastly, it will be interesting to
assess AF2’s variant prediction capabilities of crystal variants that are more dissimilar
compared to the WT crystal structure as in this thesis, AF2 was only tested on crystal
variant structures that structurally are very similar to the WT crystal structure.
==PAGE:38==
CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to assess AF2’s capability to predict variant structures. A
dataset of 113 crystal variant structures and four WT crystal structures were used in
the assessment. The crystal structures in the dataset were from four different proteins.
Furthermore, for each of the four WT structures a control dataset of similar WT crys-
tal structures was obtained and used to assess the background noise in the structures.
AF2 was assessed by calculating the RMSD between variant AF2 structures/crystal vari-
ant structures and between variant AF2 structures/WT crystal structures. Similarly, a
comparison between the predicted χ1 angles from the AF2 variant structures was com-
pared to the χ1 angles of the variant crystal structures and the WT crystal structures.
The RMSDs were calculated using ChimeraX and the χ1 angles were calculated using
MDTraj.
The AF2 generated variant structures were of high quality and accuracy but the
structural changes were too subtle to clearly assess whether the AF2 variant structures
were structurally closer to the crystal variant structures or to the WT crystal structures.
Most of the single mutation structural effects on the crystal structure were small and
within the range of the inherent inaccuracies in structure determination and the con-
formational dynamics of proteins. Thus, AF2 can predict crystal variant structures of
high accuracy but it was not possible to assess if AF2 is sensitive enough to discern the
structural changes between variant structures and WT structures.
Additional AF2 structures were generated using reduced MSAs with 128, 64, and 32
sequences. This was done to increase the stochastic behavior in the AF2 network and
test if it was possible to obtain diverse variant structure ensembles. The AF2 variant
structures diverged in equal amounts to both the crystal variant structures and crystal
WT structures and it was not possible to obtain AF2 variant structures that were more
similar to the crystal variant structures.
ESMFold, another structure prediction model was evaluated as well, and it achieved
similar results to AF2. ESMFold was not evaluated as thoroughly as AF2.
Importantly, the dataset contained only single-domain proteins which limits the con-
clusion to this class of proteins. Moreover, the small structural changes between crystal
WT and crystal variant structures decrease the discriminatory power that AF2 might
have in predicting variant structures.
Consequently, it would be interesting in the future to investigate variant crystal
structure pairs with a larger RMSD and preferably on a high-quality data set.
