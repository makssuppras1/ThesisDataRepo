Abstract  
 
This study aims to explore the project performance of IT Projects in DONG energy by conducting a 
case study of recent projects and a Quantitative Comparative Analysis, from the perspective of system 
oriented problem solving. This has been supported by conducting interviews with a broad range of 
roles in the company and by analyzing internal data. The combination of Qualitative and Quantitative 
research gave the author the opportunity to explore different views of project success within the 
company. 
By the combination of factors defined by the literature with factors derived and developed in 
collaboration with different roles in the Organization, it has been possible to define project success 
in the context of the organization. In addition, variables that are considered significant for project 
success have been determined. 
It has been learned that the approach of combining quantitative and qualitative research was useful 
in the analysis. The approach has been highly iterative and uncovered consistent misalignments which 
could be derived and grouped in order to suggests improvements. 
Projects nowadays are widely different when looking at the context and the environment they are 
undertaken in and the initial idea of the project was to investigate if the widespread perceptions of 
cost overruns in IT projects are true. It was found that it is not as simple as just to compare basic 
measurements, but is depended on the context and the perception of project success. Also it was 
shown that uncertainty in many cases decrease through the projects lifecycle.  
As a result of the investigation the overlying complexity of the organization, due to certain symptoms 
have been described and have been aligned with the misalignments as symptoms, using a combination 
of proposed approaches in order to define an overall solution.

Abstrakt 
 
Formålet med denne undersøgelse er at undersøge projekter s performance i henhold til DONG 
Energy IT. Dette er opnået ved at gennemføre en case-studie af aflsuttede projekter  igennem en 
“Quantitative Comperative Analysis”   fra et systemorienteret problemløsnings perspektiv.  Det har 
været understøttet ved en række interviews med mange forskellige slags roller i virksomheden og ved 
analysen af interne data. Kombinationen af kvalitativ og kvantitativ forskning gav forfatteren 
muligheden for at udforske forskellige synspunkter på projektsuccess i virksomheden. Ved 
kombinationen af faktorer, der er defineret af literaturen, sammen med faktorer der er afledt og 
udviklet i samarbjede med forskellige roller i virksomheden, har det været muligt at definer project 
success i rammerne af organisationen. Der er blevet defineret variabler der anses for at være 
væsentlige for project success. Det er blevet opdaget at at tilgangen til problemet, i og med at 
kombinere kvantitativ og kvalitativ analyse, var meget nyttig i analysen. Tilgangen har været yderst 
iterativ og har således opdaget en del komplikationer, som kunne udledes og grupperes med henblik 
på at foreslå forbedringer.  
Projekter er idag meget forskellige. Den oprindelige idee´ har været at undersøge om “rygteerne” 
omkring vilde budget overskridelser i IT projekter er sande. Det har vist sig at projekter i høj grad 
afhænger af konteksten og omgivelserne de bliver sat i værk i og at project success i høj grad afhænger 
af opfattelsen af personen i denne kontekst der ser på projektet. Det er også blevet vist at usikkerheden 
falder i løbet af projekternes livscyklus. Som et resultat af undersøgelsen    er der blevet defineret 
symptomer for overliggende kompleksitet i virksomheden og der er blevet lavet konkrete 
løsningsforslag baseret på literaturen.

1. INTRODUCTION  
This Chapter introduces the topic and scope of the project, the company which it is related to, the 
problem statement and research questions which seeks to be answered throughout the different 
sections of the report.  
1.1 INTRODUCTION TO THE PROJECT  
Energy in modern society is vital. Almost everything we do when we navigate through modern life 
is dependent on electricity – from making coffee to being able to see at night. The government, 
national security, healthcare sector and other vital institutions are heavily dependent on a stable 
energy supply and the electricity sector is therefore classified as critical infrastructure.  
The security of energy supply has become more important over past decades. The focus on security 
is based on the risk associated with the dependence on external sources. Because of these concerns, 
the EU has worked on establishing an internal energy market to ensure the security of energy supply 
and also to be more independent of external sources. (Court and Auditors 2015)  
The EU Commission Vice-President for Energy Union Maros Sefcovic said: “In the Energy Union 
strategy, we are committed to empowering European consumers, creating a single well-functioning 
energy market, putting energy efficiency first and becoming the number one in renewables” (EC 
2016). 
The liberalization of the Danish energy market was enabled through EU policy. In 2004 all EU 
member states had to open their markets to business customers and in 2005 to private customers. 
Danish energy prices however were, compared to other EU member states, relatively high in the years 
of the liberalization of the markets. The DK government saw the liberalization as an opportunity to 
make Danish energy prices fit for competition and the market better for consumers due to lower prices 
caused by competition, and providing freedom to choose a supplier among the energy companies 
operating in the European market.   
In general, because of the competition -the different energy companies on the market now had to 
compete with foreign energy companies on energy products. The competition however had an impact 
on the organizations themselves, adopting the focus on investing in renewable energy and efficiency 
to meet the needs of a rapid changing energy market.

1.2 DONG ENERGY 
DONG was founded in 1972 by the Danish government, namely as Dansk Naturgas A/S, the name 
was changed to Dansk olie og naturgas A/S in 1973 and to DONG in 2002.  
Because of the above described liberalization and the upcoming competition in regards to the Energy 
market - 6 Danish energy companies (DONG, Elsam, Energi E2, Nesa, Københavns energi and 
Frederiksberg forsyning merged in 2002, and became DONG Energy (DE). Today Dong Energy has 
approximately 5500 employees (including oil and gas sections) and DKK 61 billion in revenue. The 
company is active across the whole energy lifecycle. 
As energy provider Dong Energy is heavily reliant on their IT systems and if they fail it can have 
severe consequences and high costs. However, due to digitalization and the rapid evolution of 
technology, it is also crucial to stay competitive and up to date regarding computer systems with focus 
on the areas of reliability, safety and time to market (The time between development and production). 
Therefore, DE has to be up to date within the domain of portfolio, program and project management, 
in order to deliver projects which, satisfy the customer and are aligned with the overall business 
strategy. 
In recent years – complexity and project success have become more of a focus, as IT projects both in 
general and in DONG Energy become more important due to digitalization and the evolving domains 
in technology. Therefore, the company is facing several challenges to stay competitive.

1.3 PROBLEM STATEMENT AND RESEARCH QUESTION  
The research questions are defined in order to keep the author on track of the main question which 
seeks to be answered by this project. Recent studies showed massive time and cost overruns in IT 
Projects. In addition to poor customer satisfaction in IT projects in DONG Energy based on internal 
surveys, the project is based on the assumption that project performance in DE IT is not monitored 
sufficiently and that there is no measurement of project forecast quality. These assumptions result in 
the following research questions:  
• What is the lifecycle of projects in Dong Energy IT projects?  
• How can project performance be measured?  
• Which methods are currently used for project forecasts in DE IT? 
• How is the forecasting embedded in the projects lifecycle? 
• How efficient is the actual project forecast in DE? 
• How can project forecasting in DE IT be improved? 
• What are the key operational challenges that should be changed to increase the forecast 
efficiency? 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The main research questions can be summarized as:  
“How is it possible to determine and improve project performance in DE IT Projects”

2. RESEARCH METHOD 
The research method and its scope is outlined in this chapter. The research method describes the way 
or techniques by which data is collected, how respondents are approached, how questions are asked 
and how data is analysed. In order to be able to answer the questions described in the former section, 
the overall approach is systems oriented problem solving.  
The research philosophy of this thesis is mainly in favor of critical realism, “we will only be able to 
understand what is going on in the social world if we understand the social structures that have 
given rise to the phenomena that we are trying to understand” (Saunders, Lewis, and Thornhill 
2008)  
For the data collection and research process a case study is used. The case study is supplemented with 
interviews and a Quantitative Comparative Analysis (QCA). 
2.1 SYSTEM ORIENTED PROBLEM SOLVING  
System oriented problem solving is an iterative process, which takes root in Systems Engineering 
(SE). Systems engineering is a perspective, a process, and a profession (INCOSE and Wiley 2015). 
System oriented problem solving in the matter of the thesis, has to be understood as approach and 
perspective to formulate the problem statement and solve it through an iterative problem solving 
process.  
2.2 CASE STUDY 
Much of what we know about the empirical world is drawn from case studies (Gerring 2004). The 
technical definition of a Case study of this thesis is adopted from (Yin) and is described as follows:  
1. “A case study is an empirical inquiry that: 
- 
Investigates a contemporary phenomenon in depth and within its real life context, especially 
when  
- 
The boundaries between phenomenon and context are not clearly evident  
 
2. The Case study inquiry  
- 
Copes with the technically distinctive situation in which there will be many more variables of 
interest than data points, and as one result  
- 
Relies on multiple sources of evidence, with data needing to converge in a triangulating 
fashion, and as another result  
- 
Benefits from the prior development of theoretical propositions to guide data collection and 
analysis “ (Yin 2009)

“In general, case studies are the preferred method when (a) “how” or “why” questions are being 
posed, (b) the investigator has little control over events, (c) the focus is on some contemporary 
phenomena within a real life context. “  (Yin 2009) 
Project performance can be defined as a contemporary phenomenon within real life context and where 
the boundaries between phenomenon and context are not clearly evident. In order to be able to analyse

2.3  QUALITATIVE COMPERATIVE ANALYSIS (QCA)

researchers to draw different combinations of conditions (independent variables) leading to create an 
outcome (dependent variable) (Ragin, 1987).

are: “The truth table Algorithm” and “Quine (QCA 3.0)” both methods make use of the Quine-Mc 
Cluskey algorithm and can produce the same results”. For the analysis in this project “The truth table 
algorithm” is chosen. The software can be downloaded without any charge at: 
 (http://www.u.arizona.edu/~cragin/fsQCA/software.shtml)

2.8 SUMMARY  
The thesis investigates the current project performance in Dong Energy IT Projects. A case study and 
data analysis will be the bases of a giving the company a holistic view of the actual situation in regards 
to project success/performance and will also serve as a basis for Quantitative Comparative analysis 
in order to investigate possible pathways to improve or determine important factors for project 
performance. Implications which may be discovered throughout the analysis will be addressed in the 
solution space.  
The data analysis which serves as input for the QCA analysis is partly based on theoretical 
foundations discovered in the literature review, interviews with different roles in the company and 
the data available from different sources and systems in the company. To ensure comparability, the 
data mining follows the case study framework described in the research method and is also addressed 
in the relevant sections.  
The goal is to: 
(1) Investigate the widespread claim of extensive cost and time overruns in IT projects 
(2) Define what project success is and how it is determined 
(3) Provide solutions through analysis, within real life context

3.1 PROJECT MANAGEMENT AND PROJECT LIFECYCLE DEFINITIONS 
The history of project Management can be tracked as far as back to 2570 BC to the completion of 
the great Pyramid of Giza, were some records remain of how the work was managed. However, 
modern project management ,emerged in the 1950´s (Stretton 2007). The first real papers have been 
published in 1958 and Project Management has since then emerged to the “profession” it is today – 
“where the professional project manager is a major part of the management in not only the 
construction industry, but a variety of other businesses”. (Snyder 1987) 
 
Project Management has emerged from sub-disciplines of engineering – this includes programme 
management and portfolio management – to the dominant model in many organisations for strategy 
implementation, business transformation, continuous improvement and new product development.  
(Winter et al. 2006) 
 
The literature about project management is massive and yet the area is still emerging as an 
independent discipline with its own theoretical function. (Koskela & Howell, 2002; Morris, 2002; 
Sauer & Reich, 2007; Söderlund, 2004) 
 
The general definition of a project is as follows “A project is a temporary endeavour undertaken to 
create a unique product, service or result. A project is temporary in that is has a defined beginning 
and end in time, and therefor defined scope and resources. (Barron et al. 2009)  
Best practices almost in common, define the baseline of a Projects lifecycle as follows:  
- 
a series of phases that represent the evolution of a product, from concept through delivery, 
maturity, and to retirement.

3.2 PROJECT SUCCESS   
Traditionally the Project Constraint model recognizes three key constraints; “Cost”, “Time” and 
“scope”, also referred to as Iron triangle or triple constraint. (Bannerman 2008) 
The traditional measure of the immediate performance of a project is:  schedule (time), budget (cost), 
scope, and/or quality – which then often is called project management success. The “Iron Triangle” 
exists as various formats, other variants include all four elements, referred to as project diamond or 
four-legged school (Caccamese and Bagantini 2012).  In the literature has been much discussion on 
the topic and despite that, no consensus has emerged on a general definition of project success. Like 
the determination of quality, success in that context is multidimensional and therefore perceptual -
depending upon the view of the stakeholder (Bannerman 2008). In the Multilevel framework of 
success, described by Bannerman (2008), the empirical indicators of project Management Success 
are also described as, schedule met, Budget not Exceeded and project scope achieved.

3.3 PROJECT ACTUALITY  
Project actuality reflects a shift in the thinking in regard to “mainstream” project management. The 
shift focuses on thinking and research in order to tackle the “so far neglected themes from 
practitioner’s experiences within project working and management novelties, thus creating 
knowledge which is relevant to practice and reflects the interests of both academic and practitioner’s 
communities”(Cicmil et al. 2006). 
The perspective of project actuality is contrasting the traditional approaches, which are based on 
rational, objective and universal representations. It is argued that certain elements of importance, 
like political reality, reflection based knowledge, intuitive, holistic and synchronous management 
practices are “removed far from the vision of Project Managers as rational technicians”. “Dealing 
with technical issues that are resolvable through the application of superior knowledge of the 
planning and control techniques, while marginalising political and social aspects of project 
reality”(Cicmil et al. 2006).  
 
3.4 FORECAST  
A forecast in general is a prediction or estimate of the future. According to the business dictionary it 
is “a management tool that helps management in its attempts to cope with the uncertainty of the 
future, relying mainly on data from the past and present and analysis of trends” (Business Dictionary, 
n.d.). However, large organizations are exposed to hundreds of project proposals each year and 
governors need to rely on the accuracy of project forecasts to be able to make the right decisions. 
There are numerous forecast methods in the literature but due to the scope of the thesis, the focus is 
mainly on the theoretical foundation for project forecast and IT project forecast.  
Boehm’s cone of uncertainty Figure 4, has been the foundation for certain IT project forecast metrics 
(Eveleens and Verhoef 2009). It also describes the evolution of the amount of uncertainty during a 
project. According to Todd Little (2006) the term has been popularized by Steve McConnell and the 
idea is, that uncertainty decreases significantly  as one obtains new knowledge. Eveleens and Verhoef 
(2009) Found that organizations usually have no idea about their actual forecast quality and they often 
are assumed to be accurate. It is also commonly assumed that if single projects show deviations 
between forecast and actuals, which are expected due to unforeseen circumstances, that these effects 
will cancel each other out at the portfolio level.

Eveleens and Verhoef (2009) Did a case study of various industries regarding the quality of forecast 
in IT projects compared to industry benchmarks provided by Mc Connell and concluded that these 
are only useful under certain circumstances; This implies – no bias, forecasting quality coincides, and 
perfect project phase fit.  Additionally, they found that previous studies on both cost over and 
underrun in literature often only report on overrun or underrun. They argue that it would be incorrect 
to compare figures, with those of other authors.   
Flyvbjerg (2006) Describes inaccurate forecasts in project management as a major source of risk, and 
concludes that in the 70 year period for which cost data is available, accuracy in cost forecast has not 
improved. He also points out that psychological and political explanations better account for 
inaccuracy in forecast and is arguing to use Reference Class forecasting, based on the findings from 
(Kahneman and Tversky 1979). The method relies on data from previous projects, defined as 
reference classes, and is meant to avoid human bias in relation to project forecast.

3.5 COMPLEXITY 
Complex or “complicated” can have different meanings for different people, depending on the 
context. However, according to the oxford dictionary complexity is defined as: Consisting of many 
different and connected parts “a complex network of water channels” and not easy to analyze or 
understand (Business Dictionary, n.d.)  
The main idea behind a complex system, according to Nassim Taleb, is that the ensemble in that 
system behaves in ways not predicted by its components. The interaction matters more than the nature 
of the units, as an “emergent” property of the whole.  (Talib, n.d.). 
Many systems increase in complexity, primarily for two reasons: increase of interconnections and 
increase of customisation, (Fixson 2006). 
The systems-oriented perspective defines systems where people and technology interact as socio-
technical systems (STS). They are not just technical systems, but are dependent on human interaction 
(B. J. Oehmen and Thuesen, n.d.). The interactions of people with the system is, by adopting that 
perspective, subsequently increasing the complexity within the system. 
According to Geraldi, Maylor, and Williams (2011) has the academy research focused on two stream 
of work in regards to project complexity, the complexity in projects and the complexity of projects. 
Also the work of Wilson and Perumal (2009), is to mention, as they define complexity in the 
dimensions of product complexity, process complexity and organizational complexity and how these 
dimensions are related. They distinguish between value add and non–value add complexity, which 
copes with lean thinking.

cost dimension and that the complexity of the projects in reality is unknown. Another example could 
be that higher prioritization of other projects results in implications or bottlenecks for resources 
allocated to these projects.

strategic reference to the overall picture and there is no clear prioritization of these project evident.

the budgets in a matter of fact are the same as forecasts when looking at the literature and the 
definitions by the organization(Eveleens and Verhoef 2009).

The first step in a QCA analysis is to select the outcome. That was done through the cost analysis and 
is defined as the dependent variable. For doing so, a case analysis has been conducted and a cost 
analysis on the background of qualitative and quantitative data.  
The second step in the analysis is to select cases and is identical with sampling in other statistical 
methods. The diversity in the selected cases is ensured by having defined the cases beforehand by the 
data constraints for the cost analysis. The case reduction to only contain B projects, are based on the 
constraints for comparability for case studies and the implications due to data insufficiency. QCA 
require some diversity in case in order to ensure the explanatory strength of the QCA minimization. 
(Jordan et al) 
Yamasaki and Rihoux (2009) have provided 6 strategies for selecting causal conditions (independent 
variables): 
1. The comprehensive approach – where all factors from existing theory are considered, in an 
iterative process 
2. The perspective approach, where a mixed set of conditions representing two or three theories 
from empirical literature and tested  
3. The significance approach, where the conditions are selected on the basis of statistical 
significance criteria  
4. The second look approach, where the conditions that are considered as important although 
dismissed in a previous analysis 
5. The conjuncture approach, where the conditions are selected based on joint interactions 
among theories, which predict multiple causal combinations for a certain outcome 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 - 50 - 
6. The inductive approach, where conditions are mostly selected on the basis of case knowledge 
and not on existing theories  
In the context of this thesis the conditions have been selected upon the comprehensive and inductive 
approach. The inductive approach has been used in interviews and are based specifically on the 
context of DE project environment and accessible data.

7. GOAL DEFINITION 
By conducting the cost analysis and further the investigation of project characteristics, many 
misalignments between the literature and the actual situation has become obvious. The cost analysis 
showed a general improvement of the forecasts during the projects lifecycle. The interviews and the 
QCA analyses indicated misalignments between the in house governance and the actual practice. This 
section seeks to summarize the findings and to define a strategy to deal with the misalignments. In 
addition, the QCA analysis gave some insights of the influence of certain characteristics on the project 
outcome on the bases of cost estimation and uncertainty in projects. Also the QCA research method 
have been very useful as bottom up investigation approach – by the combination of qualitative and 
quantitative research. The results of the QCA gave some good indications for the importance of 
governance to project success, which is seen as good argument for looking at the project initiation 
phase.   
During the interviews the author has become aware of the dominating answer that complexity in 
regards to the system landscape, architecture and project model was a common perception of all 
internal project members. There have been some variations in the views on the “how” and “why´s”, 
but in general there was consensus about it. Also resource allocation has been mentioned quite often 
and the reason for that was blamed on the “silos” in the organization. When looking at the literature, 
this is explained by different approaches, from the lack of use of best practices, to the result of 
overlying organizational and process related complexity.

8.1 DISCUSSION AND CONCEPT DEVELOPMENT 
This section seeks to outline the overall concept of improvements, based on the analysis. The author 
is using the definitions and best practices from PMI, articles published through the PMI network, 
MIT-PMI-INCOSE Lean enablers and the system oriented problem solving approach as motivation.

During the interviews it was often discussed and/or mentioned that a project portfolio management 
department would be a good start to align the project initiatives with the overall strategy. However, 
newly started initiatives within DE are actually planning for this to happen. At the time the thesis was 
written, the organization was still organized as described in the analysis section and nothing has yet

respective business units delivering projects. It was for example often mentioned, that interfaces,

get lost in “black boxes” or are dependent on specific developers with domain knowledge.

(1) The Project idea phase and the actual project phase are separated (Governance)

of achieving the objectives in the project- based organization, is to ensure the organizational 
infrastructure exists to undertake projects effectively”(Garland 2009).

(2) Lack of clear requirements in all projects (Requirements) 
Key words adapted from top ten challenges: insufficient planning, lack of or insufficient dependencies, double work, competing 
resource requirements, Incomplete understanding of stakeholder requirements, lack of appreciation for the complexity of the 
requirements, unstable project priorities, stakeholders are unable to clearly articulate their requirements, Insufficient propagation of 
changes to cost, schedule, and performance baselines, unclear understanding of stakeholders perception of value, no learning from 
previous need definitions. 
It has become a dominating factor throughout the interviews, observations and also by the study of 
evaluation documents, that IT Projects are struggling with a consistent way of defining requirements 
in regards to the Business Units and to translate them to functional requirements. Some projects used 
user stories as requirements (user stories is an adapted term from agile, and describes the requirement 
from the perspective of the user), some projects defined requirements first in the execution phase and 
so on. Also there have been examples of projects using agile terms, such as user stories, but have 
been developed with a waterfall approach (V-model, linear not iterative). Which certainly have 
created confusion between project members, as these words become confusing for them. The general 
expectation in the interviews has been that requirements defined by projects in the initiation or 
analysis phase is a fairy tale, and again exceptions are the rule. Often Test Managers first had to 
define requirements by themselves in collaboration with the project team, in order to be able to define 
a test strategy. Lack of requirements also challenges resource allocation with specific domain 
knowledge if the translation of business requirements to functional requirements reveal specific 
competences needed for systems integration, development or other skills. Also value creation based 
on stakeholder perception is challenged if it is not clear which requirements are linked to business 
priorities in different parts of the organization. Standardized metrics for business requirements could 
be a good start and standardized knowledge sharing between the business partner and project 
manager. The project Manager should be on boarded earlier, in order to align Business requirements 
and functional requirements. That could be achieved by the implementation of a portfolio unit which 
describes that process or by changes to the commitment of and requirements of the project model in 
regards to the initiation phase, and/or analysis phase of the project.

(3) Data inconsistency – Lack of standardization of reporting 
Key words adapted from top ten challenges: Metrics are “rear-view-mirror” oriented, Metrics do not consider human behavior or cross 
functional processes, diverse and data repositories do not allow efficient acquisition and aggregation of data for metric, insufficient 
oversight to baselines, Metrics have short term focus 
The case study, observations and data analysis showed that each project contain a certain WBS 
number, which indicates the different phases a project is going through. It is the Project Manager who 
can decide how much detail the WBS numbers are representing. For Example, the WBS number for 
the analysis phase have a - 01 at the end, maturation -02, execution -03 and evaluation -04. It is in 
general possible and easy to break down the numbers further, and to for example align them with the 
overall deliverables in the project plan. In the actual way of reporting projects, there is in the financial 
dimension only reporting on project phases in regards to budgets (Start, FID) and not deliverables 
according to the project plan, they are separated by systems. It was learned that one could easy break 
down the WBS numbers to represent deliverables of the project plan. In that way valuable information 
about the products, interfaces and actual work would be captured. The way the reporting is done 
today, separates the project plan and cost reporting in the long run leaving valuable information about 
systems and among other metrics -amount of work needed (lead times). When reporting to the 
steering committee, the project manager uses Microsoft project as baseline. However, the first step 
should be to align cost reporting with the project governance, so it reflects all phases in all projects 
and ideally the project plan. When looking at reference class forecasting developed by Kahneman 
and Tversky Flyvbjerg (2006), project information about work break down structures and in general 
reference classes about earlier work are crucial information, which can be very favorable for 
conducting forecasts, based on prior experience.  
Another important point is the different view of the different roles in the project department about 
the overall picture. Depending on persona and role in the company, there is a general lack of 
consistency towards the overall picture of project success, which certainly would be in favor of 
holistic End-To-End metrics regarding the projects lifecycle.

8.2 GENERAL SOLUTION  
The solution provided in this section, aim to cover all defined objectives in the long run, but requires 
the commitment of a large number of stakeholders. Through the analysis and the linkage of objectives 
to the overall picture, it seems necessary to change fundamental elements of the context and the 
conditions under which IT projects are executed in DE. Also the perception of project success should 
be evaluated, with a holistic view on project performance – not only with one dimension of the iron 
triangle and in short term. The Lean enablers provide guidance according to the importance of certain 
aspects, while promoting lean thinking.  
From a holistic perspective it would make sense to categorize the projects of Business Units into 
programs, the programs could maintain discrete benefits from related systems, subsystems, human 
resources, planning and alignment with the overall governance and the governance of the Business 
Unit. Lean enablers could be used as implementation guide for the respective programs, as they cope

with the methodology of Project/Program Management. It would be less complex to capture metrics, 
benefits and repetitions of systems and platforms when the projects are organized within programs. 
Another advantage is, that the solution will not centralize specific Business Needs of the respective 
Business Unit to the staff functions. Instead the Project Management office can act as centralized 
unit, providing high level metrics and ensure alignment with the governance on that level.  According 
to PMI “A Programme contains a group of related projects which are managed in a coordinated way 
to obtain benefits and control over the overall outcome” and is shown in Figure 25.

8.4 DISCUSSION 
This section begins with the discussion of problem statement, described in section 1.3 and how it has 
been demonstrated through the research and analysis of the thesis. The deviation between the real 
result and the initial thoughts is outlined, in order to validate if the questions have been answered.  
By outlining the project model of DE, the lifecycle for projects in the context of the DE have been 
described. Also the forecasting methods have been showed through internal definitions, which in that 
context is the method of DE. Further it was shown, that the budgets actually are forecasts, when 
compared with the definition of IT project forecast methods based on Boehm’s cone of uncertainty. 
The efficiency of the actual forecast in DE has been shown through the cost analysis, as the variations 
of the Start/FID plot compared to actuals. The author could have provided numbers for showing the 
variations, but it has been seen as sufficient to show the variations in percentages. It has been 
described how certain misalignments became evident through the cost analysis and through the case 
research process of project characteristics for the QCA. A number of assumptions have been made 
through indicators in the data, on observations or based upon the interviews. As a result of the 
analysis, different approaches have been chosen which provided solutions based upon the discovered 
misalignments/objectives. At the end a number of suggestions have been provided with the overall 
goal of improving project performance.  
8.5 LIMITATIONS OF THE STUDY 
The amount of data derived for the case study have been quite extensive, and based on defined 
constraints through interviews and the perception of the interviewed roles. Most certainly, bias could 
not be fully avoided in terms of the qualitative data.  
Case studies have several implications described in the literature, for example reliability, validity, 
and generalizability. However, the author was aware of data limitations and followed the proposed 
constraints for comparability – but the limitations are still valid. The limitations of the QCA analysis 
are shown in table 10.  
Due to a number of assumptions and generalizations according to the project characteristics, the 
variables which have been defined are seen as indicators and should not be interpreted fully as 
quantitative data, as they include assumptions based on interviews and observations, combined with 
quantitative data. Also the amount of projects derived from the portfolio, did not provide a full picture 
of the portfolio, as stated in the discussion of the cost analysis.

8.7 CONCLUSION  
This master thesis presents the investigation of context related project success, in relation to the 
environment of Dong Energy. The purpose of the thesis has been to investigate the common 
perception of cost overruns in IT projects and was able to define project success in relation to the cost 
dimension within the company. That was possible by conducting a cost analysis, with the knowledge 
of prior theoretical propositions in relation to IT project forecast quality and definitions.  
The Quantitative Comparative Analysis, have been applied in order to investigate project 
characteristics/variables which has been considered influential for projects in order to be successful. 
The investigation has been done by using qualitative and quantitative data, and uncovered several 
misalignments within the company. These insights combined with the results of the Quantitative 
Comparative Analysis implied, certain pathways of improvements, which combined could be used to 
suggests major improvements.
