Abstract
Functional near-infrared spectroscopy has been established as a reliable
and easy-to-use, non-invasive way to measure functional brain activation.
The primary goal of this study was to implement a model to identify
high consciousness levels in patients with acute brain injury from data
collected using CW-fNIRS. To achieve this, we investigated an active
and an imagery tongue paradigm on healthy participants by training one
group classifier for each task. The classifier we build is equivalent to a
paired t-test; the higher the accuracy, the greater the difference between
a stimulus and a resting period. The analysis of the healthy participants
was used to examine the feasibility of the tongue paradigm. Then, for
each patient diagnosed with a disorder of consciousness, one individual-
level classifier was trained. The accuracy of the classifier was used as an
awareness metric for the patient. For the physical task, we achieved a
cross-validation accuracy of 0.833 when we trained the data using the ini-
tial session of the healthy participants and 0.875 when we validated this
model using their follow-up session. For the imagery task, we achieved
a cross-validation accuracy of 0.766 when we trained the data using
the initial session of the healthy participants and 0.653 when we vali-
dated this model with their follow-up session. For the DOC, 8 patients
achieved a statistically high classification accuracy and were considered
as "responders". Overall, we showed that our analysis can reliably de-
tect a physical tongue movement in healthy participants. Even though
we didn’t manage to generalize our results for the tongue motor imagery
task, we demonstrated the potential of using an individual-level classifier
to detect "responders" among patients with a disorder of consciousness
using CW-fNIRS.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Scope of Thesis
Functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) utilizes near-infrared light
to penetrate the biological tissue while it captures the diffused light sev-
eral centimeters away from the source. Through this process, the relative
oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin is estimated, and the capabil-
ity to monitor functional activation over certain areas and to create to-
pographic maps is enabled. Over the last decades, FNIRS devices have
been equipped with more advanced optical technologies, offering that
way a higher degree of mobility with lower sensitivity to motion arti-
facts compared to other similar techniques such as fMRI. Consequently,
the interest of the research community is increasing more and more, es-
tablishing the fNIRS as an affordable, portable, and reliable method to
measure functional brain activation.
The godfather of functional near-infrared spectroscopy is considered to
be Frans F. Jöbsis, who, in 1977, demonstrated how to use near-infrared
==PAGE:18==
2
Introduction
light as a non-invasive method to detect changes at the oxyhemoglobin-
deoxyhemoglobin equilibrium [18]. In the following years, three different
fNIRS techniques were developed: the continuous wave (CW), the fre-
quency domain, and the time domain (Figure 1.1). The continuous wave
approach is based on continuous light illumination and can measure the
attenuation of the light through the brain, while the frequency and the
time domain approaches can also measure the time that light needs to
travel through the tissue [41]. In the frequency domain technique, light
with modulated intensity is transmitted through the brain tissue, while
in the time domain approach, the head is illuminated with short pulses
of light.
The CW-fNIRS devices are less expensive and more widely
available than TR-fNIRS or FD-fNIRS. However, they are less accurate
since they have lower depth sensitivity and can measure only the relative
hemoglobin concentration [10].
In most preliminary studies, fNIRS was developed and used to monitor
the oxygenation level in newborn infants [5]. In 1993, a new study for
adults was published indicating a significant activation in the prefrontal
cortex during cognitive stimulation and occipital activation during vi-
sual stimulation [47]. A year later, the same research group performed
an interesting experiment demonstrating an extremely large increase in
the oxygenated hemoglobin concentration during spontaneous epileptic
seizures [48].
All the above approaches were based on a single chan-
nel fNIRS, but at the same time, Hoshi Y. and Tamura M. succeeded
in measuring multiple cortical regions simultaneously [15]. In the fol-
lowing years, new studies were published exploring the motor cortex
activation during a muscle movement [4, 43, 37] using 10Hz temporal
resolution, slowly setting the fundamental challenges of handling non-
neuronal activation observed by systematic or physiological confounds
(e.g. heartbeat) [16]. After the 2000s, more portable fNIRS devices with
multichannel capabilities became widely available (Cubeddu, ETG-100,
ETG-7000), leading to an explosive interest in fNIRS studies [10] for
standalone fNIRS brain-computer interfaces [7, 28, 44], or paired with
other modalities such as EEG or fMRI [50, 2].
At 2004, Coyle et al. [25] set the initial challenges for an fNIRS-BCI

exploring the physiological confounds in fNIRS data and the ways to
treat them. Based on this research, three independent fNIRS-BCI stud-
ies were released. The first one was published from the same research
group [7] and achieved a classification accuracy of 80% using the "ball
squeezing" paradigm to encode yes and no responses. In the second re-
search, Sitaram et al. [44] used the "hand" paradigm and managed to
distinguish between motor imagery and resting period with an 80% ac-
curacy using both Support Vector Machine and Hidden Markov models.
The third study came out by Naito et al. [28], and it was the first fNIRS
study on patients with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis.
Their study re-
vealed very promising results on patients who were not totally locked-in.
Following the publication of Owen et al. [33], where they used fMRI and
detected motor imagery activity in a patient diagnosed with unrespon-
sive wakefulness syndrome, similar publications came out using fNIRS
[27, 19, 23, 24] for patients with disorder of consciousness (DOC).
A disorder of consciousness is defined as a condition where the normal
consciousness has been impaired by brain damage. These disorders are
categorized into groups based on the level of consciousness as chronic
coma, unresponsive wakefulness syndrome (UWS), minimally conscious
state (MCS), and locked-in syndrome. The first study on patients with
prolonged DOC using fNIRS was conducted in 2013 by Molteni et al. [27]
investigating the hemodynamic response of two MCS patients using a so-
matosensory, a passive hand movement, and an active hand movement
paradigm without showing significant results but they pointed out the
significance of the correct interpretation of the results. The next study
came a few years later from Kempny et al. [19], showing significant re-
sults for the healthy controls during an active and an imagery kinaesthetic
motor paradigm in the M1 and S1 areas, but it couldn’t show significant
activation in the DOC patients. In recent years, Li Meng et al. [23]
used a tennis motor imagery paradigm and achieved very good classi-
fication results for 5 UWS patients, using only the relative oxygenated
hemoglobin from M1 and S1 areas.
Despite the promising results, the volume of research in this field remains
very low. There is a great need not only to reproduce the reported results

of the current research but also to investigate more novel paradigms to
detect awareness in patients with DOC more reliably. This study will
try to discover residual awareness in patients with DOC using the tongue
motor imagery paradigm. This analysis aims to initially find the areas of
interest for the corresponding task on healthy controls and then use those
characteristics to assess the level of consciousness in patients with acute
brain injury. This analysis will not only help us evaluate the probability
of the patient’s recovery, but it can also be used as a brain-computer
interface in cases of patients with high consciousness levels but complete
paralysis, like the Locked-in syndrome.

1.2
Research Questions
Classifying a patient’s consciousness levels remains still a challenging
clinical process, with around 40% of non-communicating DOC patients
misclassified as being in the unresponsive wakefulness state [21]. Even
though supplementary methods such as functional magnetic resonance
imaging (fMRI) or EEG, have demonstrated remarkable results, the ef-
fort you need to set up the experiment and the cost-efficiency ratio ren-
der them as unsuitable for a bed-side tool. Therefore, there is an im-
perative need to explore novel approaches that enable quick, and less
resource-intensive bedside evaluations. This project will investigate the
feasibility of the tongue motor imagery (MI), measured through contin-
uous wave functional near-infrared spectroscopy (CW-fNIRS), to detect
residual awareness in patients with DOC.
==PAGE:21==
1.3 Thesis structure
5
Analytically, this study will focus on investigating the following:
• Develop a pre-processing pipeline to obtain the clean oxy/deoxy-
genated hemoglobin concentration from the raw light intensities.
• Evaluate the reliability of fNIRS detecting active physical move-
ments.
• Examine the feasibility of the tongue motor imagery paradigm in
healthy participants.

3.1
Pre-processing
In the pre-processing step, we need to convert the raw measurements to
relative oxygenated and de-oxygenated hemoglobin. To understand all
the steps taken, first, we need to define the main sources of noise. First
of all, fNIRS data could be affected by instrumental noise, which might
be caused by equipment deterioration or by environmental parameters
like lighting conditions. Another type of noise that we meet on fNIRS
data is the experimental noise, such as motion artifacts related to head
movements. Finally, the most difficult kind of interference we encounter
in fNIRS data is the physiological noise. This type of noise is not related
to neuronal activity, but it is caused by a systemic function of the body.
It is considered to be the most challenging since it appears on the same
frequencies as the frequencies that we are interested in. Some examples
are the heartbeat (1∼2 Hz), the respiration (0.2∼0.4 Hz), and the Mayer
waves (∼0.1 Hz).
Our pre-processing pipeline will use different methods to tackle each type
of noise separately, but before that, it needs to convert the light inten-
sities to optical densities. Then, it addresses the instrumental noise by
rejecting noisy channels using the scalp coupling index. Next, it removes
part of the physiological noise using the short channel regression. Af-

3.1.2
Scalp Coupling Index and Interpolation
The next step in the pre-processing pipeline is to identify channels sus-
ceptible to noise, then remove them, and finally interpolate them using
their closest channel. In order to achieve that, we used the scalp coupling
index, which was coined as a term by Luca Pollonini in 2014 in a research
about the auditory cortex activation in response to natural speech and to
simulated cochlear implant speech [36]. The scalp coupling index is used
as a measure to quantify the quality of the contact between the optical
probes and the scalp, and despite its simplicity, it still remains one of the
most novel approaches to reject noisy channels. This method utilizes the
two signals with different wavelengths, 760nm and 850nm (more details
in chapter 2.3.1), to find areas with strong cardiac oscillations. Even
though cardiac oscillations are considered as noise and are unwanted on
the signal with the hemodynamic response, they can indicate the exis-
tence of intracranial physiological activity. Therefore, they can indicate
a good coupling between the scalp and the optodes. More specifically,
this method starts by filtering both signals (760nm and 850nm) of each
area to keep the frequencies between 0.5 and 2.5 Hz. Then it calculates
the zero lag cross-correlation between them and uses it as a quantitative
metric of the signal-to-noise ratio of the area/channel.
After we rejected all the noisy channels, the next step was to interpolate
each one of them using the information from its closest channel. The
reason we did this interpolation was, firstly, that we were already lim-
ited on the amount of data, and secondly, that we wanted to retain the
structure of the data for the further analysis.
In our case, we rejected all channels with a scalp coupling index less than
0.8. In the table 3.1, we can see how many channels were rejected for all
our different datasets.

3.1.6
Band-Pass Filtering
The final step of our pre-processing pipeline was to apply a band-pass
filter to the oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin concentration in
order to remove any residual physiological noise. We chose a second-order
Butterworth filter with cutoff frequencies [0.01, 0.2]. When working with
fNIRS data, we generally expect slow responses. Therefore, we set the
high cutoff frequency as low as 0.2Hz.
3.2
Feature Extraction
At this point, we assume that every recording contains the "clean" oxy-
genated and deoxygenated hemoglobin concentration for 8 different ar-
eas/channels of the brain (Figure 3.2).
Since we have a limited amount of participants (table 2.1), we needed
to reduce the dimensions of the feature space as much as possible to
avoid over-fitting. First, we kept only the oxygenated hemoglobin and
omitted the deoxygenated, reducing the channels from 16 to 8. In addi-

each symmetrical left-right channel, reducing the channels even further
to 4. Before extracting the features of interest, we normalized each of
the principal components to make sure that all the data is on the same

(physical or imagery tongue movement) and every rest event within a

3.2.1
Principal Component Analysis
Principal Component analysis is a linear dimensionality reduction tech-
nique widely used in pre-processing, feature extraction, visualization, and
==PAGE:40==

lossy compression [3]. PCA applies a linear transformation on the data,
projecting it into a new coordinate system while trying to retain the
maximum initial variance. The unit vectors of the new coordinate sys-
tem are called principal components and are ordered by the amount of
the variance they capture. Thus, the first principal component captures
the most variance, the second principal component captures the next
greatest variance and is orthogonal to the first, and so on. According to
[3], we get the maximum variance when we set the principal components
to be equal with the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix ordered by
their eigenvalues.

3.3.1.1
Paired stimulus-Rest periods
The first step we took in all the pipelines/models that we developed
was to pair all the stimuli events, which could be a physical tongue
movement or an imagery tongue movement event, with their following
rest event for each participant. One trial consists of both the stimulus
and the rest period, with the rest period being significantly influenced
by the stimulus period. For example, the rest period of someone who
responded to the initial stimulus would look different from the rest period
of someone who did not. Therefore, we paired all the stimulus periods
with their corresponding rest periods for every participant by subtracting
the features of the rest period from the features of the stimulus period
(stimuli-rest). The problem with this approach is that then we have only
one class. To overcome this problem, we multiplied the features of half
of our data-points with -1 and we labeled those data-points as class 1,
while we kept the other half as they were and we labeled them as class
0.
This approach is quite similar to a paired t-test or a dependent samples
t-test, where the measurements you compare should come from the same
participant. Our assumptions are the following:
• H0: If the participants were not able to follow the paradigm, there
wouldn’t be any kind of pattern between the stimulus and the rest
period, and their differences would be randomly distributed around
==PAGE:46==
30
Methodology
zero.
Thus, our classifier wouldn’t be able to separate the two
classes.
• H1: If the participants were able to follow the paradigm, the stim-
ulus and the rest periods should have very different features, and
their differences should have a pattern. More specifically, classes 0
and 1 should show the opposite pattern, and our classifier should
be able to separate them.
3.3.1.2
Feature Selection
At this point, regardless of the dataset and the task, the data-points
contain the paired features, which are the slope and the mean of four
different brain areas (8 features per data-point).
In order to further
reduce the number of features, we selected which areas to keep and which
to discard with respect to the type of task (physical/imagery).
In a
physical task, we expect a higher activation on the Motor area followed,
while in an imagery task, we expect an activation primarily in the SMA.
Thus we could choose the features only of those two areas, but instead,
we evaluated the performance of all areas, and we tried to prove our
hypothesis.

3.3.1.4
Cross Validation
Cross-validation is a method used widely in machine learning to evaluate
the robustness of a model or, in other words, to evaluate how much a
model can be generalized to an independent dataset. The process through
which cross-validation achieves this involves splitting the dataset into
multiple combinations of training and validation sets and averaging the
performance metric to obtain a more generalized result. Cross-validation
can also be used to find the optimal parameters of your model. To achieve
this, you need to use nested cross-validation, which involves multiple
layers of cross-validation, but this process is more data-intensive and
demands a larger dataset. In our analysis, we used two types of cross-
validation: the stratified k-fold and the leave-one-out.
The stratified k-fold cross-validation splits the dataset into k subsets
(folds), ensuring that the same proportion of classes as the original
dataset was preserved at each subset.
Then, it uses one of the sub-
sets as the validation set and the rest as the training set. This process is
repeated until each of the subsets has been used once as a validation set.
Figure 3.6: Stratified K-Fold Cross Validation [34].
==PAGE:49==
3.3 Classification

Leave one out cross validation is a special case of k-fold where each fold
contains only one data-point.Thus in this approach, each data-point is
used once as a validation set while the remaining N −1 data-points are
used for training.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Discussion
First, we used the physical trial to validate our pre-processing pipeline,
where we were expecting to see a very high activation in the Motor area.
Also, we were expecting that the patients hospitalized in the intensive
care unit without DOC, would not perform well during the initial session,
but they would improve their performance in the follow-up session. This
behavior was highlighted in the figures with the average features and
time-series, but we also proved it by building a classification model that
was able to perform with very accuracy using the features from the Motor
area. This proves that our pipeline can reliably detect a physical tongue
movement using fNIRS.
Despite those facts, we still believe that there were cases where our
pipeline didn’t handle them correctly.
As we already mentioned (ta-
ble 3.2), there were several cases where we had pairs of channels with
==PAGE:80==
64
Conclusion
very high power differences, indicating the potential existence of residual
noise. To tackle this issue, we normalized every channel separately. How-
ever, this is not considered a very good practice since it might amplify or
"hide" the noise and create more false positives. Rejecting high-power
channels in the pre-processing step would be considered more appropri-
ate.
Then, we continued the group-level analysis with the tongue imagery
task. From the average time-series, we observed a good response only
at the initial session of the healthy controls, while in the rest of the ses-
sions, it seems that the participants were not following the paradigm.
From that, we concluded that the paradigm might be difficult to follow.
From the average time-series of the healthy controls, we observed a very
good pattern at the SMA, which aligns with what we were expecting. On
the other hand, we also observed a good pattern in the Frontal and Pari-
etal areas, which wasn’t expected and might indicate the existence of a
periodic component. The classification results verified what we described
above. For the healthy controls, we obtained very high accuracy using
the SMA area, but it didn’t perform well on the other datasets. There-
fore, we can not conclude if the paradigm is appropriate for detecting
consciousness, at least with the analysis that we conducted.
For the individual approach, we assumed that if the participants were
following the paradigm, we would be able to train a classifier with a high
accuracy. Even though we didn’t manage to generalize our results of the
tongue motor imagery, we still believe that the SMA is the most appro-
priate area to detect someone responding to the paradigm. Therefore,
we trust the results from this area more. The results from the other
areas could still indicate "responders", but this would not be strictly re-
lated to this specific paradigm and might also occur with a different one
(e.g., mental arithmetic, somatosensory). Even now, we remain blinded,
and we don’t know the clinical evaluation of DOC patients, but accord-
ing to the researchers/doctors who performed that evaluation, several of
the "responders" that we identified had a good clinical image, showing
very promising results for a CW-fNIRS quick evaluation tool or BCI. By
knowing the clinical evaluation and which patients were identified with
==PAGE:81==
5.2 Conclusion
65
a higher level of consciousness, sensitivity and specificity can be defined,
which can help us choose the features or where to set the threshold. For
example, with a high threshold or very few features, it is more likely to
have a higher specificity, while with a lower threshold or more features,
we will probably identify more patients as "responders," and we will have
higher sensitivity.
Even though we showed that our approach managed to identify some
patients with higher consciousness levels, the classifier was more "loose"
than the paired t-test that we performed.
This was probably caused
by several reasons. First, for the t-test, we examined 16 features, and
by using the Bonferroni correction, we made our statistical test very
strict. In addition, the classifier was validated using leave-one-out cross-
validation, which tends to overfit and report better results.
5.2
Conclusion
In conclusion, in this master thesis, we managed to show that our pipeline
to process fNIRS data can detect physical tongue movements reliably us-
ing the Motor and the SMA areas. For the imagery task, even though
we achieved high classification accuracy in the healthy controls using the
SMA and the Parietal areas, we didn’t manage to generalize our results.
Therefore, with the analysis that we performed, we cannot determine
whether tongue motor imagery movement can be detected using CW-
fNIRS. However, we demonstrated the potential of an individual-level
classifier, without the need for prior training, to detect residuals of con-
sciousness in DOC.
==PAGE:82==
66
Conclusion
5.3
Future Work
This project provided useful insights into the opportunities and limita-
tions of our current approach and several ideas for future work. Even
though functional near-infrared spectroscopy has the potential to pro-
vide a higher cost-efficiency ratio to measure brain activation than other
approaches, such as EEG and fMRI, more research needs to be conducted.
There is an imperative need, for standardized protocols in order to en-
sure the reliability and reproducibility of fNIRS results across different
research groups and applications. Common benchmarks need to be de-
fined to provide researchers with an easier examination of all the differ-
ent paradigms proposed so far. In addition, more advanced and reliable
pre-processing techniques need to be established to ensure proper noise
removal, which will eliminate false positive errors and enhance the overall
accuracy and validity of the findings.
Despite the technological advancements, the fNIRS devices have not
reached the level of precision and usability required for everyday appli-
cations. The spatial resolution remains still one of the biggest drawbacks
of this approach. In addition, even though most of the vendors claim
that the devices are not affected by other supportive machines, such as
respiratory devices, in the DOC patients, we observed much more noisy
data. Therefore, this issue must be addressed before fNIRS can be used
to monitor patients in critical condition.
Finally, the application of fNIRS should not be restricted only to appli-
cations related to patients with disorders of consciousness, but it should
be expanded to other domains like psychology, sports science, pediatric
research, or neurovascular coupling.
