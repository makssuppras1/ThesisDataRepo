Abstract
5G is the state-of-the-art technology in mobile network communications. Thus, all
the operators want to be able to provide new 5G services to their customers. In order
to reach the most complex use case scenarios, an evolved backhaul and packet core are
required. In 5G, all is about the synchronization precision. Providing synchronization
in Ethernet networks is a challenge since they are asynchronous. In this project, an
investigation of the optimal implementation of PTP over Ethernet networks is done.
The goal is to provide recommendations for network planners that need to make
decisions when planning and implementing the PTP protocol in their mobile networks.
The OMNET++ simulation environment was chosen to perform the study of the PTP
protocol. Five sets of experiments were carried to determine optimal implementations
of the PTP protocol. After obtaining the results from the simulation experiments, a
theoretical analysis is made to determine the interference produced in a UE between
two neighboring cells that are not synchronized. Finally, recommendations based on
the results obtained from the simulation experiments are given at the end.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Mobile traffic has been growing since the first commercial cellular network was de-
ployed in Japan by Nippon Telegraph and Telephone (NTT) in 1979.
NTT was
the first company to provide 1G communications nationwide.
Since then, mobile
communications have spread around the world due to constant technological devel-
opment. The parallel development of applications for fixed global networks and the
development of new communication protocols caused the operators to adapt the mo-
bile networks from voice dominant 2G networks to packet data Long-Term Evolu-
tion (4G LTE) mobile networks.
Nowadays the mobile network industry is experiencing a revolutionary technolog-
ical development to deliver new applications for the users. At the beginning of 2019,
network operators and smartphone manufacturers started a race when the new term
”5G NR (New Radio)” defined by the 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP)
came into general use [39]. 4G LTE networks have shown excellent performance for
most of the applications. However, 4G has limits in the amount of data that can be
transferred through an air interface (500 Mbps average throughput per user) and the
time required to get data or establish a call (50 ms average latency). 5G promises
to increase the throughput to 20 Gbps and reduce latency to values lower than 4ms.
5G, however, can do a lot more because the way it is defined: increased frequency,
responsiveness, bandwidth and wider provisioning.
Between some of the most promising applications we can find remote operations for
patients that can not travel to meet the healthcare specialist able to do the procedure,
or, cars connected to the network providing information about the state of the road.
However, 5G is at an early stage and the fastest commercial available 5G networks
are giving around 1-2Gbps throughput and 10ms latency, which is far from what 5G
standard is promising.
As in all the systems with a shared spectrum, 5G can be implemented as Time
Division Duplex (TDD) or Frequency Division Duplex (FDD). Indeed, some opera-
tors are planning to implement a FDD scheme for the low frequency spectrum and
a TDD for the rest of the spectrum resulting in an 80% of the throughput of a site
being transmitted on the TDD side [15]. TDD technology requieres redundant syn-
chronization with 1.5µs absolute Time Error (TE) to release 5G full potential. Thus,
Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS) is not enough and synchronization needs
to be provided also from the network to the RAN.

1 Introduction
1.1
Scope of the thesis
In Denmark all the operators are competing to be the first one to deploy a 5G network
commercially available.
Thus, all the companies have to prepare the network to
accommodate the 5G RAN when the new hardware is ready to deploy.
This thesis is focused on how to provide necessary synchronization in the backhaul
network for 5G TDD systems with PTP. The main goal is to reproduce PTP, analyze
the optimal configuration in a certain topology with PTP elements and test some
simulation scenarios in the network simulator OMNET++. Also, the impacts, that

Basics of LTE
5G has been developed based on LTE to ensure backwards compatibility. In order to
understand 5G, it is useful to have knowledge of LTE.
2.1.1
Basics of the air interface
LTE was developed with a completely new approach for the air interface compared
to older generation systems. The air interface was completely revisited to design a
new air interface capable of support mobile internet services, which required higher
bit rates and low latency.
The air interface is the wireless connection for radio-communications between a
base station, in this case called E-UTRAN Node B (eNodeB), and the User Equipment
(UE). The air interface involves the both the physical layer, where electromagnetic
signals are transmitted between transceivers and receivers, and the data link layer,
whose main purpose is to ensure that all the information is transmitted correctly and
that all the resources are used optimally so all the users connected to an eNodeB can
use their services. In contrast to previous generation systems, all these functionalities
are mainly controlled by the eNodeBs.
The air interface of any mobile system is shared between multiple UEs. There
are two main ways of sharing the interface. The first one is to divide the frequency
for several users, i.e. Frequency-Division Multiplexing (FDM), which allows several
independent signals to be transmitted at the same time. This technique splits the
frequency spectrum in channels. Each channel is centered around a carrier frequency
and has a certain bandwidth, which defines the width of the channel. The carrier is
modulated with a passband signal generating several sub-frequencies that encode the
information of all the independent signals from the UEs. The other alternative for
sharing the spectrum, is to divide the time into slots, which is also known as Time-
Division Multiplexing (TDM). Every UE gets assigned a time slot, i.e. only one

FDM system in case A and a TDM system in case B.

Modulation is the process where the data is conveyed by changing the carrier signal.
In particular, it uses a finite number of signals to modify the carrier wave so each
modification represents a different sequence of binary numbers. Modulation can be
done modifying the amplitude of the carrier wave, the frequency or the phase. Several
modulation techniques are used for transmitting data in LTE. In the next subsection
it is shown that depending on the technique, it is possible to transmit more bits or
less. Apart from modulation, the eNodeB applies a Forward Error Connection (FEC)
code, which adds redundant bits wit a code rate in order to protect the data so the
receiver can correct the data if the signal experienced bit errors.
In order to avoid simultaneous transmissions in the same frequency between the
Downlink (DL), which is the transmission from the eNodeB to the UEs, and the
Uplink (UL), which is the opposite, are split. If the FDD scheme is adopted, up-
link and downlink transmissions are simultaneous but at different carrier frequencies,
which are separated by a gap of frequencies which are not used. This gap of frequen-
cies is called guard band. In the case that TDD scheme is adopted, the DL and UL
will get assigned time slots to transmit at a certain time. Also, for TDD it is necessary
a guard period between downlink and uplink transmissions to let the eNodeB switch
the operation mode. Depending on the configuration, it can be assigned the structure
of uplink and downlink time slots.

Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (OFDM), and uplink, Single Carrier
Frequency Division Multiple Access (SC-FDMA).

2.1.2
OFDM
OFDM splits the data into several streams that are transmitted through different
sub-carriers simultaneously instead of trying to send data at very high speed. The
slower transmission of the data streams minimize the negative effect of transmissions
over multiple paths in previous technologies [36]. The sub-carriers are placed one next
to each other with 15 kHz spacing in a way that they are orthogonal in frequency and
do not interfere with neighboring sub-carriers. Thus, the more bandwidth available
the bigger the number of sub-carriers that will fit on it.
Each sub-carrier is able to use its own modulation, which gives a lot of flexibility for
optimal performance in very different scenarios with different levels of interference.
Several modulations are available in LTE such as Quadrature Phase-Shift Keying
(QPSK), or, 16 Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (16 QAM). QPSK is only capable
of transmitting 2 bits per symbol, while 16 QAM can transmit 4 bits per symbol. A
symbol is the time where single modulation is applied to a sub-carrier. Indeed, a
standardized cyclic extension is added after a symbol to avoid overlap between the
neighboring symbols. It is important to notice that external noise will degrade the
quality of a sub-carrier depending on its modulation. To understand this concept,
Figure 2.2 shows the constellation diagrams for QPSK and 16 QAM respectively.

two points are very near from each other, it could happen that data would be the

OFDM schedules not only in frequency with sub-carriers but also in time with
symbols. Indeed, the scheduler of an eNodeB allocates frequency every 1 millisecond
[2].
The minimum amount of resources that can be assigned to a single user is
called Physical Resource Block (PRB). A PRB contains 7 symbols and 12 carriers.
7 symbols are equal to a time slot in LTE and 1 millisecond contains 14 symbols.
Figure 2.3 shows how the spectrum is divided into symbols and sub-carriers. This
allows the eNodeB to use effectively the resources and assign the best carriers for users
depending on their quality. The quality of the carrier is evaluated by the eNodeB with
a Channel Quality Indicator (CQI) feedback from the UEs. CQI will be explained in
more details in the next chapter 3.
In summary, OFDM divides the spectrum with orthogonal sub-carriers that carry
different information in parallel. Also, the symbols are interspersed with guard in-
tervals. This makes OFDM a technique with high spectral efficiency, and robustness
against Inter Symbol Interference (ISI) and co-channel interference. The guard peri-
ods make this technique also very robust against timing errors.

SC-FDMA
SC-FDMA is the technique used for transmission in the uplink. It is a similar tech-

devices need to consume the lowest amount of power possible. In OFDM there is high
Peak to Average Power Ratio so we will require expensive and efficient RF power am-
plifiers that transmits the radio frequency signal via the antenna to the base station
being the highest power item within the mobile. This increases the cost of the mobile
terminal and drains the battery faster [20]. Figure 2.4 shows how the neighbouring
sub-carrier are grouped together into single carriers to ease the power amplification.

2.1.6
Mobility in LTE
UEs are constantly moving while they are connected to an eNodeB. The fact that the
users have mobility creates complex scenarios that the eNodeB has to handle. The
network has to track all the UEs for two reasons. First, UEs need to know when
to transfer data in the uplink so the data will arrive in the correct time slot. The
main technique used to synchronized all the UEs is the Timing Advance (TA), which
allows the user to how advance in time has to transmit. Second, it is important to
know when the user is about to leave the covered area by the eNodeB. In this case,
the eNodeB has to coordinate with a neighbouring base station a handover of the
UE. Depending on the architecture the handover can be handle directly by the X2
interface. Otherwise, it has to follow the S1 interface. In any case, it is ensured that
the traffic from the user will be forwarded to the target eNodeB. The source eNodeB
will only stop the connection with the UE once it receives the confirmation that EU

The handovers are controlled by the network but the network has to base its
decision on several reports made by the target UE, which is measuring the signals’
strength that receives. The UE measures three different parameters: Received Signal
Strength Indicator (RSSI), Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP) and Received
Signal Radio Quality (RSRQ). RSSI represents the entire received power including
power received from the serving eNodeB co-channel power interference power from

2.1.7
Interference coordination in LTE
When UE is close to the eNodeB interference is negligible since the eNodeBs are
placed far away to cover the maximum area possible. Thus, the power of the signals
arriving from neighbouring eNodeBs is low compared to the power of the received from
the serving cell. This makes negligible the interference created by the neighbouring
eNodeBs. However, as mentioned in the previous subsection (2.1.6), UEs on the cell
edges can experience intercell interference when a neighbouring cell uses the same
frequency than the serving cell. In this case the bit rate of the users is limited since
the modulation has to be very robust and higher coding rate is needed to correct
errors. X2 interface can be used to exchange interference‐related information between
neighboring eNodeBs and coordinate transmission power to avoid interference [16].

5G use cases
The main use cases that 5G NR will support are:
• enhanced Mobile Broadband (eMBB): This use case provides access to

• Ultra-Reliable and Low Latency Communications (URLLC): This use

• It can be that between different factories there is a need of absolute synchro-

• LTE techniques to coordinate eNodeBs over the air interface only achieve less

• Granularity of the TA limits the synchronization that a UE can receive.

5G New Radio (NR)
5G is developed by redesigning the LTE air interface. 5G air interface is called NR
and its base station is called Generation Node B (gNB). It uses OFDM in the DL
and SC-FDMA in the UL as LTE. However, LTE only has frequency bands that
go from 400 MHz to 6 GHz.
5G divides the spectrum in two ranges, Frequency
Range (FR)1 which corresponds from 450 MHz to 6GHz, and FR2 which corresponds
from 24250 MHz to 52600 MHz [3]. Thus, giving backward compatibility to LTE with
the FR1 and expanding the spectrum FR2. Notice that FR2 is on the millimeter wave
frequencies.
Apart from the new frequency range, 5G is different from 4G in the amount
of bandwidth that can give to the single UE. LTE was limited to up to 20 MHz
bandwidth. 5G can give up to 100 MHz bandwidth per channel. Moreover, the key
feature about 5G compared to LTE it is the flexible numerology. LTE only allows
sub-carrier spacing of 15 kHz, but in 5G, sub-carrier spacing of 30, 60, 120 and 240
kHz are also available [28].

3.1
Interference in mobile networks
One of the main limitations in the radio access network of a mobile system is inter-
ference. It is indeed a bottle neck in the mobile network. The modifications that any
signal will experience in air interference in the cellular network can be distinguished
as [34]:
• Path-loss: A signal generated in a transceiver loses power as the area is occu-
pied by the signal increases with the distance.
• Shadowing: Obstacles in the direction where the signal is transmitted absorb
a big quantity of the power from the signal which attenuates the power that
can be transmitted.
• Fading: Signals not only lose energy because of the absorption of the obstacles
but some of the signal is reflected by the discontinuity of the medium between
the object and the air.
It is important to notice that this phenomena lower the power of the signal, which
makes more difficult for the receiver to detect but they do not create interference as
itself. Thus, there is a limit where the signal can be transmitted but also the signal
does not stop at the receiver but continues until the power of the signal is completely
dispersed in the air. The main types of interference are [34]:
==PAGE:32==
18
3 Interference Management: Challenges and techniques
• Inter Symbol Interference (ISI): ISI is the interference created by neigh-
bouring symbols that can reach the receiver at the same time because of multi-
path delays. This type of interference is considered as intra-cell interference
and the transmission technique OFDM is designed to be robust about this type
of interference.
• Inter-user Interference (IUI): The IUI occurs when there is Multiple Input
and Multiple Output (MIMO) transmissions. With this technique users can
increase its bit rate by transmitting several data streams in parallel. However,
they can interfere between them creating IUI. Another case, where IUI can hap-
pen, is when two users have allocated the same resources in time and frequency
while using multi-user MIMO, which allows transmissions between users under
the same base station.
• Inter-cell Interference (ICI): ICI refers to the interference created by neigh-
bouring cells that are using same resources than the serving base station. This
type of interference can not be mitigated unless there is coordination between
the neighbouring cells. However, OFDM is indirectly cancelling this type of in-
terference by assigning the best sub-carriers based on the CQI feedback received
from the users.

However, there are coordination techniques that show great potentials to
overcome these challenging problems [26].
• Beamforming: This technique refers to transmit in a very directional way, so
it is easier for the receiver to detect the signal while it is not transmitted in any
other direction, avoiding possible interference for other users. This technique
is a key point on 5G millimeter-wave transmission. Coordinated beamforming
between neighbouring base station is possible if they transfer the necessary
information to align precoding of the signals.
• Small cells: Small cells are micro-base stations (can be also pico or femto

populated areas. They create Heterogeneous Networks (HetNets) in cooperation
with macro-base stations. Small cells can be connected directly to the macro-

Small cells add the external interference since macro-base stations have more
neighbouring cells. However, users can be closer to the eNodeB and reduce the
path-loss. Also, they offload macro-base stations from users.

4.1
Synchronization
Synchronization consists on providing a reference signal to a several devices so they
can coordinate functions.
Synchronization has been always a key feature in mobile networks to maintain and
control, the timing between time slots, the carrier frequency as well as to coordinate
intercell interference. The importance of synchronization in the radio interface of a
mobile network is shown with a theoretical analysis in chapter 7.
Depending on the application and the technology, different levels of synchroniza-
tion with different positions are required. There are three types of synchronization
that can be provided to a device:
• Frequency synchronization: In the network, each device has a clock to deter-
mine the rate at which the data can be transmitted. Frequency synchronization
is the transmission of the rate at which one device operates so other devices that
receive this information can operate at the same rate.
• Phase synchronization: In phase synchronization the devices can also get a
coordination in time, meaning that they can perform operations not only at the
same rate but also at the same moment.
• Time synchronization: Is the same as phase synchronization with the only
difference that apart from operating at the same moment all the devices have
assign the same time for that moment. Thus, all the devices have the same
Time of Day (ToD).

4.3
Synchronization technologies
There are three main technologies that are used to synchronize base stations in a
mobile network:
• Synchronous Ethernet (SyncE): Conventionally mobile communications
using TDM had implicit synchronization technologies to match clock frequen-
cies. Due to the later standardization of packet switch mobile networks based
on IP over Ethernet, synchronization has to be provided since Ethernet is
asynchronous.
Ethernet transmitters synchronize adjacent nodes by sending
pulse every 16 ms [37] but the this reference is not forwarded to other ports
in the node. SyncE uses the physical layer to provide continously a synchro-
nization signal that can be traceable to a common clock by also transmitting
the Synchronization Status Message (SSM) every second. In particular, SyncE
must provide to all the slave nodes/clocks in a network a precision of at least
±4.6 ppm (parts per million) of the reference source rate [35].
Apart from
sending the synchronization signal, in SyncE the signal has to be extracted
from the slave port, delay variations (jitter) are removed with a Phase-locked
loop (PLL) before forwarding the signal to the output port or output circuit.
Also, the PLL maintains the synchronization for longer when the reference sig-
nal is lost by generating the signal based on average values recorded. Figure 4.2
shows a simplified scheme of the transmission of the reference signal through
the network. Ports labeled as master only send the reference signal while ports
labeled as slave only receive the reference signal.
• GNSS: Several GNSS systems are available around the world that can provide
very accurate frequency and time synchronization accuracy. Indeed, it provides
a time reference with an accuracy of 100 ns. GNSS sends a very accurate signal
known as 1PPS, or pulse per second, used to calculate ToD, reference time and
phase, and to synthesize the fundamental source radio frequencies [43]. However,
it can not be implemented everywhere such as indoor locations where there is
a poor coverage of the satellites system. Also, it as an expensive solution since
it has to be implemented in all the base stations. Apart from being expensive,
there is the threat that the satellite signal can be jammed.
Indeed, Global
Positioning System (GPS) outages have been experienced and an intentional
jamming can be produced by radio-frequency interference.
• PTP: IEEE 1588 Precision Time Protocol is the main technology to provide a
time, phase and frequency reference through the network. It is an upper layer
protocol than can be contained on a IP packet or Ethernet frame. It calculates
the difference between nodes and then updates the time at the slave node to

4.4.1
Overview
Every device on a network requires at least to have one oscillator so it can coordinate
tasks and work at a certain rate. In the context of PTP networks a clock is referred
to the device using that oscillator [32]. Each of these devices will have several ports
to connect between other nodes with optical or electrical connections.
Each port
will have a PTP port state which is determined by the master-slave hierarchy that is
created with the PTP protocol.
The clock with the best time reference is called Grand Master (GM) or Primary
Reference Time Clock (PRTC) whereas the clocks that only received reference time
for themselves are called Slave Clock (SC). Also, the devices are classified depending
on their capabilities:
• Ordinary Clock (OC): Ordinary clocks are devices that have a single port
in the Ethernet network. Thus, they can only be grand masters or slave clocks.
Grand master clocks have a very precise oscillator or/and they base its time on
a GNSS system.
==PAGE:44==
30
4 Synchronized packet switched networks
• Boundary Clock (BC): Boundary clocks have multiple ports, each of them
having a defined state. In a device/clock, PTP frames will suffer from delays
which will make the precision worse. Boundary clocks solve the problem of the
loosing precision when a PTP message is forwarded from an input port to an
output port by generating a new frame at the output to avoid introducing errors
for the residence time that the packet remains on the device.
• Transparent Clock (TC): Transparent clocks are devices with several ports
that forward PTP messages. To avoid delays and inaccuracies while forwarding
the message, the TC corrects the time stamps before forwarding it to the next
clock.

PTP provides precise transfer of time, frequency and phase by timestamps carried
in PTP messages transported over a Ethernet packet-based network. The precision
of PTP depends on the precision of the timestamps. PTP provides synchronization
in two phases: first establishing a master-slave hierarchy and after measuring delays
and forwarding synchronization information [45].
PTP algorithm implements the following operation:
1. The GM clock obtains the reference time from GNSS system.
2. To send the reference information of time phase and frequency synchronization
it exchanges packets that are timestamped over the Ethernet network.
3. The slave clock has to calculate the difference in time and the difference that
its own clock has in frequency.
4. The slave clock adjusts its time and frequency to the same as reference GM.

5. This is repeated periodically to continuously correct the possible drift that the
slave clock can suffer.
There are three main different implementations of PTP that can be done to a
device figure 4.4 shows them. Each device will receive the signal in a electrical or
optical connector and forwarded to the physical Ethernet. After the signal is pro-
cessed, it is forwarded to a hardware where the Ethernet frame is processed by Field
Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) or micro-controller. Finally, the information ar-
rives to the operating system of the device where the applications can perform their
functions [30]. Figure 4.4 case a) shows the implementation in software. It does not
have high cost since it implemented on software on top of the operating system as
an application. Therefore, the timestamping as well as the clock are implemented on
software. However, the timestamping performance is very low since the packet has
to be processed in all the lower layers until it is evaluated by the PTP application.
Thus, it can only achieve millisecond precision. Case b) shows an implementation on
hardware there the FPGA or micro-processor contains an oscillator and the times-
tamping is done in the Media Access Control (MAC) layer after processing the frame.
The hardware implementation can have precision around 30 ns. In this implementa-
tion PLLs are required for generating modulation signals and other operations in the
device. Case c) shows a hardware implementation done just in the Ethernet PHY
which gives the highest precision possible to PTP since it is processed in the physical
layer and delays of other layers are avoided. Both hardware implementations have to
communicate to PTP stack in order to coordinate functions.

PTP Protocol details
4.4.4.1
Message types
The PTP stack can be over UDPoIP as well as Ethernet. Its messages are classified
in event messages and general messages. Event messages are timestamped at the
transmission and reception while general message do not carry timestamps [23].
• Announce message: Each device has a data set describing the characteristics
of the clocks as well as configuration parameters. Announce messages are used
to share information with other devices about the quality of the clock.
• Synchronization and Follow_Up messages: This messages carry the times-
tamp of the egress time sent by the master. Ideally, only the Sync message is
used. However, if the master is a two-step clock, the Sync timestamp is set to
zero and the timestamps is included in the Follow_Up message.
• Delay_Req message: It carries a timestamp with the egress time of the slave
clock. This message is only defined for the E2E mechanism.
• Delay_Resp message: Contains the timestamp of the ingress time that the
Delay_Req message arrived to the master clock. This message is only defined
for the E2E mechanism.
• PDelay_Req message: This message contains the timestamp of the egress
time of the sender node. It is only used by the P2P mechanism.
• PDelay_Resp and PDelay_Resp_Follow_Up messages: This messages
contains the timestamp of the ingress time of the previously received PDe-
lay_Req message. It is only used by the P2P mechanism. It is important that
PDelay_Req and PDelay_Resp messages have the same lengths to avoid possi-
ble asymmetries. If the clock sending the PDelay_Resp message is a two-step
clock, it sends the egress time of the PDelay_Resp in the PDelay_Resp_Follow_Up
message.
• Management and signalling messages: Are used by management proposes
in the PTP protocol.
4.4.4.2
Port States
Each port in a node has a stated defined in which is used by the PTP protocol to
decide the actions to be taken on that port. The state machine of a node depends on
whether the node is configured to be always a slave or not. If it is configured to be
only a slave the state machine is reduced, i.e. the node can never reach the master
state. Otherwise, the node can acquire the following states:
• Master: The port forwards reference time information so the clock in the other
end of connection can correct its time reference.
==PAGE:51==
4.4 IEEE 15588v2 PTP
37
• Slave: The port receives reference time information so its clock can be adjusted
to the common reference time.
• Passive: The port is excluded from the PTP network so it will not forward
any time reference or start a synchronization mechanism. A port can become
passive if there are several paths to the same master to avoid loops. It can also
become passive in the case the node is also a grand-master, i.e. it is connected
to a GNSS system.
• Pre_Master: This state is used when a port decides that it has the best time
reference in the network. Eventually, it will become a master unless it receives
an announce message from a different clock with better accuracy.
• Listening: The port is just waiting to receive announce messages. After he
receives a message or the time limit exceeds, it will change its state.
• Uncalibrated: After a port receives and announce message with higher accu-
racy than the one it has, it moves to uncalibrated state and starts the synchro-
nization mechanism.
• Faulty: if there is a fault, the port will move to faulty state until the fault is
solved, then it will move to the initializing state.
• Initializing: All the ports of a node are in this state at the same time until
the PTP stack is ready to start the synchronization process. After, they will
move to the listening state.
• Disabled: Ports in this state are not participating in the PTP network. The
difference with passive state is that they completely ignore PTP messages while
port in passive state can process information of received PTP messages.
4.4.5
Topology definition: Best Master Clock Algorithm (BMCA)
Before nodes can start performing synchronization mechanisms they need to establish
PTP network with master-slave hierarchy. In order to do this, the nodes execute an
algorithm called BMCA:
• Each node that considers itself the best clock will start forwarding Announce
messages.
• Then, other nodes in the listening state will receive the announce messages,
compare the data that they have about the best master clock and decide whether
they keep its reference time or they synchronize to the new GM.
• The nodes that decide to update their reference will start the synchronization
mechanism with the node that previously sent the Announce message. After
they are synchronized, they will sent Announce messages though their master
ports so the slave clocks can update their reference time also.
==PAGE:52==
38
4 Synchronized packet switched networks
• The nodes that have better clock accuracy, comparing to the Announce mes-
sage, will send an Announce message in reply. Then, the other node will receive
the announce message with better time reference and will update its state ac-
cordingly.
• Eventually, all the master-slave topology is defined and the time reference of the
slave clocks can be traced to the PRTC. The topology will not contain timing
loops.
It is important to notice that the transparent clock are not included in the topology.
Instead, they will only forward the PTP messages and add correction values for the
residence time that PTP messages last inside the transparent device.
The BCMA specifies how the different parameters of the data set are compared to
decide which clock is the best one. The algorithm compares the following parameters
in the following order:
• Priority 1: It is an 8 bit configurable value (1-255).
• Clock Class: Defines the traceability of the reference time from a defined list.
For example a clock connected to a GNSS system will have better class that a
clock without direct GNSS reference.
• Clock Accuracy: This value is defined depending on the type of source where
the time reference is obtained, e.g. Atomic clock or GNSS signal.
• Clock Variance: Defines the stability of the clock, i.e. how much the clock
can drift from the reference value between Sync messages.
• Priority 2: This is also a configurable value to difference clocks. It can be
used to difference primary GM clocks from backup GM clocks.
• Source Port ID: Ussualy configured as the MAC address of the PTP node. It
is used to distinguish messages from different clocks and to avoid timing loops.

5.1
OMNET++
Objective Modular Network Testbed in C++ (OMNeT++) is the simulation envi-
ronment chosen for the simulations in this project. It has been chosen since it has a
good graphical interface, results are easy to extract from simulations and it has been
used for developing PTP simulations in the past [14, 24].
OMNET++ is an environment which gives a lot of flexibility when developing
simulations. In particular, once the components are build they can be interrelated in
a wide variety of combinations to form complex components or to adjust the set up for
a concrete simulation. It also allows to run a simulation with different configuration
parameters, which is very helpful when making studies about networks and protocols.
It has a debug mode to remove errors and help the user. Modules are developed in
C++ while simulations with existing modules use a different programming language
called Network Description (NED).
Furthermore, OMNET++ has been used for
different projects and contains several libraries like INET which give the required
network components and stacks to build simulations with a lot of flexibility and
configurable parameters.
OMNET++ is an Dicrete Event Simulator (DES). Simulations rely on a model
to imitate time real behavior. In particular, a DES simulates a process as a series of
instantaneous events and between events the system is unchanged. Thus, anything
between events is omitted and times without events are skipped.
Events happen
instantaneously and future events are queued until the scheduled time arrives. Figure
5.1 shows the simulation of several PTP nodes. In the lower-left-hand corner the
status of the elements of the ”Slave” node are show, whereas in the lower-rigth-hand
corner all the network events are shown.
The interface of the libPTP provides a icon based nomenclature that shows the
configuration of the simulated nodes as well as message types. Figure 5.2 shows the
possible node icons that can be visualized depending on the configuration as well as
the type of the messages.

5.2
Implementation of PTP stack
LibPTP provides the network node model that is used in all the simulations. The
PTP node defined in LibPTP can be used to simulate a TC, BC or OC apart from
being highly configurable.
It is formed by software and firmware components as
well as hardware, which is simulated as a Network Interface Card (NIC). The NIC
is simulated as a complex component where each functionality is assign to a basic
module.
(a) PTP node
(b) NIC hardware
Figure 5.3: Models of the node architecture and NIC architecture from libPTP.
Figure 6.17 shows the architecture of a PTP node (A) and the detail of the NIC
implementation (B). Next sections, briefly explain the implementation of each module
and its functionalities. It is important to notice that the clock as well as the PTP
controle module are defined inside the NIC as a hardware components. Thus, meaning
that the PTP stack is implemented at layer 2 in hardware and not only as software,
which would give bad timestamp resolution.
Clock
The clock simulates the oscillator that a Ethernet switch with PTP support would
have. The main purpose of this module is to count time so all the other elements
of the network can have a discrete time reference to place events. The frequency of
the oscillator is determined by a parameter called ticklength which is the interval in
==PAGE:56==
42
5 Simulation tools and implementation
nanoseconds between two ticks of the clock, i.e. the period of the frequency. The
imperfections of the frequency, i.e. stochastic noise is implemented with a parameter
called TdGen which add/substracts value to the real time accounted by the clock
each oscillation. TdGen can be implemented with different model or excluded from
the implementation.
On top of the pure hardware clock and API is implemented so the time and the
scale of the clock can be modified by other elements, so the clock can be synchronized
to the reference time of the GM clock. Also, another API is configured to schedule
events for the clock considering the error generated by the noise instead of following
the perfect scheduling of the simulation.
Ethernet PHY
Simulates transmission and reception delays on experience on a real PHY. The
delay is configurable so it can be adapted to the specification of a real PHY compo-
nent.
MAC
The MAC module forwards already encapsulated from the upper layers to the
PHY and adds the destination address if not filled already. The frames received from
the network are directly passed to the upper layers. It also contains the MAC address
of the node, the duplex mode and a queue size for ethernet packets.
On top, libPTP adds functionalities of timestamping for PTP frames, modification
of PTP frames to add the residence time and asymmetry corrections. Moreover, the
mean path delay is added if the delay mechanism is P2P.
Delayer
This component adds residence time to frames based on a model. It can be used to
simulated higher loads in the network as if the network would have a bigger amount
of traffic.
MAC Relay Unit
The relay unit decides the output ports where the node needs to forward the
frames based on the MAC address table. On top, libPTP adds functionality based
on the clock type(OC, TC or BC) and the type of PTP frame.
Encapsulation
It encapsulates frames before they are forwarded to the MAC relay unit. On top,
libPTP adds routing information to the Ethernet frames so they will be forwarded to
the correct ports according to the master-slave hierarchy PTP topology.
PTP NIC Control
PTP NIC Control forwards the port configuration from the PTP stack to the
MAC layer and the ports. Moreover, it can handle resquest as creation of Follow_Up
messages and notify the PTP stack on events.
Logical Link Control (LLC)
LLC handles the forwarding of packets from the higher layer to the MAC layer
based on the type of service.
PTP Ethernet Mapper This module adds the specified Ethernet type defined
on the IEEE 1588v2 standard [22]. It also assigns the Ethernet multicast adresses
depending on the type of the message as specified on the standard.

PTP stack
Implements the IEEE 1588v2 standard. Each PTP message is implemented as
services. However, some of the functionalities are implemented on the MAC layer with
the configuration forwarded by the PTP NIC control module, e.g. the timestamping
is perform in MAC to avoid higher residence times.
The tasks handled in the PTP stack are communication with the clock servo,
datasets, creation of PTP messages, communication and forwarding of information
with the MAC layer. State and port data are handled directly in the MAC layer.
Moreover, the PTP stack hosts the PTP services: the BMCA implementation and
the message creation and forwarding.
Clock servo
Implements a Proportional-Integral servo to correct the offset from the clock. It
adjusts the local time of the clock to the reference time and scales this value to
minimize the error.
5.3
Goals
This thesis tries to solve a real-world problem, which is helping network planners to
decide how to design PTP synchronization architectures optimally for the backhaul.
Thus, reducing the interference in the radio interface. In particular, this thesis is fo-
cused in the behaviour of PTP over Ethernet, which is defined in the ITU-T G.8275.1
[41]. Moreover, this thesis develops simulations in OMNET++ based on the library
libPTP as a framework for synchronization system analysis. libPTP provides the
basic modules explained in this section that where used to build the simulations [14].
Thus, several experiments are carried in chapter 6 to evaluate the best configurations
for PTP nodes, as well as, best positioning of the nodes in faulty scenarios.
5.4
Validation of the model
libPTP is checked and validated to ensure that the model is correct. In order to
validate a model, it is important to ensure that individual components are correct,
i.e. the precision of the results is accurate enough. The simulator with libPTP has
been previously tested with basic tests [23]. In particular, all the components of the
PTP node were tested.
In this project, the general functioning of he clock component is checked with
an external element measuring the offset between clocks. BMCA tests from [23] are
repeated to compare results in this project showing the correct functioning of the
simulation. Moreover, the delay mechanisms are also analysed for a simple network
with two nodes.
Section 5.5 explains all the assumptions that are considered in the model. Section
5.6 show the results obtained to validate the model. An analysis of the parameters
of the model for a optimal set up is also performed.

5.5
Assumptions of the model
libPTP provides a model with many components forming a device. This, gives a
very detailed model close to reality.
However, each of the components has many
parameters that define its behaviour. Thus, the simulations require a high number of
parameters to be determined so it can be defined a proper environment to test other
parameters of the protocol:
• The clock frequency is set to 20 MHz which corresponds to one tick of the clock
every 50 ns. Higher values would give more realistic oscillation times. However,
every every tick of the clock is an event to be process by the simulation and to
considering higher values would slow down the simulation speed.
• In the simulations carried, it is considered that clocks are perfect oscillators.
The only noise considered is stochastic noise, i.e. aging and temperature are
not considered.
• The PI-based servo parameters define how the time offset is reduced to zero.
This parameters are left as default as they where optimized for the node archi-
tecture.
• The clock scale factor is another limitation of how much the time offset can be
reduced. The maximum value used is taken from Intel i210 data sheet.
• The granularity of the simulation is 1 picosencond. This defines the precision
of the time measurements.
• The Ethernet PHY chip has intrinsic asymmetry conditions(between transmis-
sion and reception) which are considered to be around 65 ns.
• At the start of the simulation the nodes power up with a random uniform
distributed time in the first milliseconds.
• Clocks start with a random time offset between them from 0 to 5 ms.
• The packets rates of the PTP messages are configured to be the ones specified in
the G.8275.1 standard [41]: 16 packets per second (pps) for all the messages and
8 pps for Announce messages. Also, the PTP stack of a device is configured to
change state of the port to holdover after 3 Announce messages are not received.

6.1
Experiment 1: Traffic load and PDV performance
impact
The PTP protocol sends its messages over a busy network full of user data, control
and management traffic. This background traffic affects the delays experienced by
the PTP frames inside the devices (queuing and processing delays). Thus, it is impor-
tant to simulate this effects an analyse how the synchronization accuracy is affected.
Moreover, the validation test were simulated with connections of 2 meters. In the
experiments we will consider 1 km connections for two reasons: it matches a real
scenario and macro base stations with GNSS will not experience jamming simultane-
ously.
The implementation of the background traffic in the simulation is not trivial and
can affect the results of the PTP depending a lot on the traffic type.
Thus, the
approach chosen in this project is to directly implement the delays on the MAC layer.
This way the simulation is kept as simple as possible and all the computation is on
processing the PTP protocol.
Simulations have been studied and used to estimate queuing and processing delays
on internet IP routers with different load conditions [6, 10]. Also, test bets have
been performed by making use hooks (low layer events that make an output on the
application layer) which give information about where the message is at a certain
time [7]. Moreover, QoS analysis for delay sensitive applications have been studied
to enhance their performance [5]. With these output delay distributions, the delays
experimented by the PTP protocol are implemented. In particular, the PTP protocol
is considered a Class 1 application with the highest priority in the QoS scheme while
user data is considered to be best effort traffic with higher delays [29].
Three main different delays scenarios are considered and simulated: low (10 µs ±
5 µs), moderate (66 µs ± 22 µs) and high (280 µs ± 126 µs). Each scenario intends

takes more time for a 2 step device to update its delay and local time after the path
delay changes. Therefore, it increases the duration of synchronization intervals.
It is important to notice that in both experiments, 3 and 4, the P2P delay mecha-
nism achieves lower TE and jitter than the E2E mechanism at higher loads/asymme-
tries when the nodes are configured as BCs. This is due to the fact that P2P delay
mechanism measures delays only between peering nodes. Also, in the P2P mecha-
nism, each active port of a device has to measure the peering delay always even if
they are in master state or the device is configured as a TC. In this way, the link
delay is corrected at each hop updating the PTP frames and the path delay is explicit
in the correction field. This allows to reduce the error in the estimation of the path
delay and makes P2P the first option in networks with full support in all the nodes.

Summary
In this chapter, 6 different experiments have been carried to analyze the performance
of the PTP protocol in different conditions and topologies.
The first set of experiments evaluated the effect of path delay variation by setting
queuing delays at certain traffic loads. This experiment concluded that the delay
experienced by the PTP frames degrades the accuracy of the synchronization.
The second set of experiments conducted evaluated asymmetries created different
traffic loads in the UL and DL directions. It was concluded that asymmetries in the
paths are one of the main factors degrading the performance of the PTP protocol.
==PAGE:82==
68
6 Analysis of the simulations and results
The third set of experiments evaluated the performance of BC and TC node. TC
resulted to improve performance and reduce complexity. It was concluded that BC
nodes should be used only in key points.
The fourth set of experiments evaluated the performance between 2 step and 1
step nodes. The performance of both configuration gave similar results.
The fifth set of experiments evaluated how clock noise can affect TC and BC
nodes. Results shown that TC nodes are less affected by the clock noise while BC
nodes degrade the synchronization performance considerably. P2P delay mechanism
showed to have better accuracy than the E2E mechanism with BCs.
The sixth set of experiments evaluated the performance of the protocol in faulty
situations. It was concluded that error in the convergence time to the back up GM
depends on the traffic load. Moreover, the relative TE between SC depends on the
distance from the aggregation node. A BC as aggregation node showed to be the best
option to keep low the relative TE between SCs.

7.2
Recommendations for planning an Ethernet
backhaul with PTP full path support
This section tries to give the best recommendations based on the carried experiments
and analysis of the effects of lack of synchronization in the RAN.
• It is a requirement to classify and forward PTP frames with the highest priority
of the QoS system.
Having low delay makes the PTP protocol very stable
at higher traffic loads. However, if it is not prioritized it could happen that
the internal clock of a device would not be able to synchronize at microsecond
precision.
• Asymmetry is the main factor that increases the TE. Thus, must be ensured
that both UL and DL follow the same path in case there is serveral possible
routes to from the RAN to the core. Radio-links and other static asymmetries
have to be compensated by configuring their value in the PTP stack.
• For nodes that are in the middle of a chain, i.e.
they have two ports, it is
recommended to configure them as TC. TCs are more robust to lack of syn-
chronization than BCs in the sense that they only need to correct the residence
time will reduce the amount of control loops required by the protocol and sim-
plify the architecture of the topology. Moreover, it will reduce the convergence
time required to synchronize when there is a change in the PTP topology.
• The P2P delay mechanism is the recommended mechanism to use since it re-
duces the complexity of the protocol by measuring only peer delays and trans-
porting the primary time reference as well as the accumulated network delay in
a Sync message.
• When choosing between 1 step or 2 step node configuration, 1 step configuration
should be chosen if the timestamp precision is in the nanosecond order. However,
==PAGE:92==
78 7 PTP synchronization: Impact on RAN and recommendations for optimal performance
this feature is highly dependent on the hardware capabilities and it has to be
tested before deployment.
• A direct connection to the a first aggregation point from all the slave base
stations in the RAN gives the opportunity to configure the aggregation point
as a BC. The advantage of configuring a boundary clock is that it provides the
same exact reference time for all the nodes connected to the aggregation to it.
Thus, the relative TE between the nodes will always be lower even if the BC
node is running in holdover mode.
• Positioning of the GM clocks has to be done the closest possible to base station
since each hop will degrade the performance of the PTP protocol in a certain
degree.
• It is not recommended to connect the back up GM to the same node as the pri-
mary GM since a clock errors can degrade the performance in backup scenarios.
Following this recommendations together with testing of hardware and evaluation
of the topology, the backhaul should be able to provide the required time synchro-
nization for supporting LTE advance services, as well as, LTE and 5G time domain
systems.

given by the PTP protocol working optimally. It is shown how lack of accuracy will
degrade the user experience loosing up to 70% of the bandwidth in not coordinated
interference scenarios.
The second part of the chapter proposes several recommendations for backhaul
network planners based on the results obtained by the simulations to ensure that the
synchronization accuracy is good enough to provide 5G services.
==PAGE:93==
CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
5G is the state-of-the-art technology in mobile network communications. Thus, all the
operators want to implement 5G to be able to provide new services to their customers.
In 5G, is all about the synchronization precision. The precision of the synchronization
of base stations will determine how efficient is the usage of the spectrum, i.e. how
much difference will be with the 4G LTE systems.
Nowadays, there is not a big
difference between LTE and 5G and thus the synchronization implementation will
make the difference. The global synchronization of radio base stations not only will
allow the implementation of new services but will avoid the need of guard bands
between operators.
5G RAN technology gives much more flexibility and adapts the base stations to the
new usage cases that LTE was not capable to handle. However, 5G RAN technology
is not enough to give the required performance to reach the most complex cases such
as URLLC applications. In order to reach the most complex use case scenarios, an
evolved backhaul and packet core are required. Capabilities such as network slicing,
separation of control and user plane, automation and ensured ultra low latency will
be crucial.
Air interface limitations such as path-loss, shadowing and fading will always limit
the performance of the RAN and interference will never completely disappear. How-
ever, interference management techniques will be a keystone in 5G. Beamforming will
reduce massively the interference in 5G networks by directing the propagation of the
signals and reducing the overlapping of carriers between neighbouring base station.
Device level synchronization will allow IoT devices to communicate directly and of-
fload the base station. ICIC will be also a technique that will cancel interference in
the cases that previous techniques do not reach.
Synchronization requirements established by the ITU-T standards are very tight.
Relative time offset between neighboring base stations require ± 260 ns for CoMP
domains or even ± 65 ns for MIMO transmissions. The absolute TE for TDD deploy-
ments requires at least ± 1.5 µs for basic services. To provide this synchronization
in Ethernet networks is a challenge since they are asynchronous. Technologies such
as SyncE are not anymore sufficient to provide the accuracy requirements. GNSS
is capable of given the required precision but it vulnerable against jamming attacks.
Therefore, PTP hardware support in the backhaul network is needed.
In this project, an investigation of the optimal implementation of PTP over Eth-
ernet networks is done. The goal is to provide recommendations for network planners
==PAGE:94==
80
8 Conclusion
in decision making when planning and implementing the PTP protocol in their mobile
networks. The OMNET++ simulation environment was chosen to perform the study
of the PTP protocol. OMNET++ gives a very flexible environment for simulations as
well as providing a library, called libPTP, as a framework to develop the simulations.
The models of libPTP were validated with a set of experiments that concluded its
suitability for the further study of the PTP protocol. In particular, the tests to vali-
date the model include: the correct functioning of the clock modules in a device, the
correct generation of the master-slave topology with the BMCA algorithm and the
correct synchronization of a full time implementation between a master and a slave.
A description of all the models is given, and the assumption of constant parameters
for the PTP experiments are stated in the report as well.
Five sets of experiments were carried to determine optimal implementations of the
PTP protocol. In the first experiment, the effect of PDV in the performance of the
protocol is analysed. It is concluded that the PTP messages need to be configured as
the highest class priority traffic since the delays affect considerably the performance
of the protocol. In the second set of experiments, the effect of asymmetries between
the UL and DL is studied. It is determined that uncompensated asymmetry is one
of the main parameters that degrade the performance of the PTP protocol. In the
third set of experiments, BC and TC nodes are compared. It is concluded that TC
nodes perform optimally in positions where the synchronization reference is only for-
warded to from one ingress port to one egress port. In the fourth set of experiments,
the performance of 2 step and 1 step nodes is compared. The results are similar for
both configurations. Thus, this feature needs testing on real hardware and depends
completely in the precision of the timestamping provided by the hardware implemen-
tation. The fifth set of experiments, analyses drift in some devices to see how the
PTP protocol can handle situations with faulty nodes in the network. Faults in BC
nodes show to have a bigger impact in the TE. Moreover, the P2P delay mechanism
shows a better performance than the E2E delay mechanism in this case. Finally, the
sixth set of experiments evaluate PTP protocol in faulty scenarios where the GM fails
and a new topology needs to established to synchronize to the a new GM. The PTP
protocol showed to perform the best when the distance between the GM and backup
GM is minimized. Moreover, the TE is reduced when the GM is closed to the SC
nodes.
After obtaining the results from the experiments a theoretical analysis is made.
The interference produced in a UE between two neighboring cells in a real deployment
scenario is considered. The analysis is carried for 5G and LTE technologies. It shown
how severe is the impact of the lack of synchronization in the RAN with and without
interference management (ICIC).
Finally, recommendations based on the results obtained from the simulation ex-
periments are given at the end.
