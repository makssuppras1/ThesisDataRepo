Sammenfatning
Denne rapport kombinerer risikoanalyse (RA) og sygdomsbyrde (BoD) for at vurdere
sundhedsrisici ved uorganisk arsen, et naturligt forekommende kræftfremkaldende stof.
Kronisk eksponering for iAs tyder på, er forbundet med kræft, type 2-diabetes, hjertekar-
sygdomme, og neurologiske forstyrrelser. Ved hjælp af litteratur blev RA anvendt til
benchmarkdoser, mens BoD estimerede sygdomsbyrde via incidens og ydermere, DALY’er.
RA giver konservative tærskler, mens BoD viser befolkningspåvirkning, og en kombina-
tion af tilgange kan styrke fremtidig kemisk risikovurdering, når et kemikalie forårsager
mere end en sygdom. Uorganisk arsen og den danske befolkningsgruppe er anvendt som
eksempel, for at kombinere de to metoder.

Abstract
Exposure to chemicals through food and drinking water can contribute to various dis-
eases. Traditional risk assessment (RA) and burden of disease (BoD) approaches are rarely
combined, which can leave an incomplete picture of the health impacts of inorganic ar-
senic (iAs), as it contributes to multiple different health outcomes. This thesis will provide
a comprehensive review of existing literature to fill out this gap.
Inorganic arsenic (iAs), a known Group 1 carcinogen, is a naturally occurring contami-
nant frequently found in our diet. Chronic exposure to iAs has been linked to various
adverse health outcomes, including several cancer types, type 2 diabetes (T2D), cardio-
vascular diseases (CVD), and neurodevelopmental disorders. This thesis compares two
approaches for evaluating the health risks of dietary iAs exposure: the traditional RA and
the BoD framework.
Using a shared dataset derived from literature and regulatory reports (EFSA 2009, 2024
and US EPA 2025), both RA and BoD methods were applied to the same health endpoints:
bladder,- lung,- and skin cancer, T2D, CVD, and neurodevelopmental effects. While RA
focused on calculating benchmark dose levels (BMD/BMDL) and margin of exposure
(MOE), the BoD approach quantified disease burden using incidence numbers attributed
to exposure of iAs and disability-adjusted life years (DALYs). This dual analysis allowed
for a direct comparison of methodologies and outcomes.
Results indicate that although both frameworks identify similar critical health endpoints,
the BoD approach offers a more population-centered estimate of health impact, while RA
provides conservative safety thresholds. The integration of both approaches enhances
the understanding of iAs-related risks and may support improved decision-making for
food safety authorities. This project suggests that a combined RA-BoD approach can be a
valuable tool for future chemical risk evaluation, especially within the context of public
health.

Introduction
1.1
Introduction of Inorganic Arsenic
Arsenic is a naturally occurring element, and can also be introduced through industrial
activities. Chemically, it is classified as a metalloid due to its metallic and non-metallic
properties. When arsenic combines with elements such as chlorine, sulfur, or oxygen, it
forms inorganic arsenic (iAs). When it bonds with hydrogen and carbon, it is classified
as organic arsenic, (Chou & Harper, 2007). Arsenic can exist in four oxidation states: -
3, 0, +3, and +5. In its elemental form, it is not soluble in water and is predominantly
present in inorganic forms as oxy-anions of trivalent arsenite (As3+)or pentavalent arse-
nate (As5+). It enters water sources through the natural dissolution of rocks and indus-
trial pollution. iAs primarily exists in two oxidation states, (As3+) and (As5+), (Chou &
Harper, 2007). iAs leaches into groundwater, ultimately contaminating drinking water.
From there, it can also spread to oceans, lakes, rivers, and other aquatic environments,
(Nielsen & Larsen, 2014), (Chou & Harper, 2007)
This project focuses on dietary exposure to iAs. iAs is common in the environment, and
found in staples such as grains, e.g. rice or drinking water, which can pose health risks
(EFSA Panel on Contaminants in the Food Chain (CONTAM) et al., 2024), (U.S. Envi-
ronmental Protection Agency, 2025). Rice and drinking water easily absorbs iAs from its
surroundings, making it a source of exposure, (Mawia et al., 2021).
1.2
Introduction of Risk Analysis and Burden of Disease
IARC is the specialized cancer agency of the World Health Organization (WHO) pub-
lished a monograph identifying carcinogenic hazards to which humans may be exposed,
including iAs. It is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen, indicating that there is sufficient
epidemiological evidence to confirm its carcinogenicity in humans (International Agency
for Research on Cancer, 2025). iAs poses a potential risk of cancer development, includ-
ing skin, bladder, lung, prostate, liver and kidney cancer. Additionally, it has been linked
to non-cancerous health effects such as diabetes type 2 (T2D), cardiovascular diseases

(CVD´s), reproductive toxicity, neurodevelopmental and neurotoxic disorders,(U.S. En-

Risk assessment (RA) and burden of disease (BoD) both contribute to food safety in their
own way. RA involves around scientific data and studies to evaluate risks associated
with certain hazards, (“risk assessment | EFSA — efsa.europa.eu”, n.d.). BoD estimates
allow researchers to compare foodborne diseases and chemicals by assessing their public
health impact based on severity and duration, (“Calculating the real burden of disease —
food.dtu.dk”, n.d.). RA is a tool to estimate the risk of certain food contaminants. RA´s
address both microbiological and chemical hazards. The RA process consists of four key
steps: hazard identification, hazard characterization, exposure assessment, and risk char-
acterization. The first step, hazard identification, involves recognizing the potential haz-
ard, and examining the toxicokinetics and toxicodynamics of the chemical. Toxicokinetics
focuses on the absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) of a chemical
contaminant, examining how the body processes the chemical hazard. Toxicodynamics
explores the effects of the chemical on the body, and what the chemical does to the body,
(Johanson, 2018). The hazard characterization assesses the dose-response of health ef-
fects, which can range from mild symptoms, such as stomach pain to severe outcomes -
cancer or even death. At this stage, dose-response calculations are performed to establish
a tolerable level of exposure to the chemical hazard. The third step, exposure assess-
ment, determines the amount of the hazard present in the food, and how much food is
consumed. This involves analyzing occurrence and consumption data. Finally, risk char-
acterization integrates the previous steps to evaluate the overall level of risk, (Committee
et al., 2019). Evaluating the overall risk combines hazard, dose-response, and exposure
to express health impact as risk levels or population burden. Appendix A provides infor-
mation on what each of the subcategories mean.
The BoD concept was developed to compare the overall impact of diseases and health
risks on populations. Its key metric, the Disability-Adjusted Life Year (DALY), created
by the World Bank and WHO in the 1990s, combines Years of Life Lost (YLL) from pre-

When assessing the BoD caused by food-borne chemicals, the focus is often on identify-

many people will fall ill due to a chemical.
There are three metrics that are considered, when interpreting BoD. Incidence, prevalence
and cause-specific mortality. The first presents how many new cases can be attributed to
iAs. The second, how widespread a disease is in a population; in other words, how many
are already affected by a certain disease. The last illustrating the impact of a disease on
mortality rates. How many cases are caused by a chemical (Hernandez & Kim, 2019),
(Dicker et al., 2006). Furthermore, DALY can be calculated to see, how many years are
lost due to a chemical, (Jakobsen et al., 2024).
In 2010, BoD estimates were used to calculate global DALYs lost from exposure to food-
borne chemicals such as dioxins, lead, and aflatoxins, (Gibb et al., 2015). Additionally,
BoD can be quantified in financial terms, including healthcare costs, lost jobs, or prema-
ture deaths, (Udompap et al., 2015). BoD essentially has the same structure as RA, but
risk characterization differs, quantifying the risk in number of cases, (Devleesschauwer
et al., 2015). BoD plays a crucial role in achieving food safety. Decision-makers therefore
need evidence on which hazards causes the highest BoD in a population, and which foods
bear the highest responsibility for it, (Jakobsen et al., 2024).

1.3.1
Aim of this Project
Chronic diseases such as T2D, CVD, neurological disorders, and various types of can-
cer are commonly attributed to genetics, diet, or lifestyle choices. What if disease isn’t
just about genes or lifestyle, but also hidden toxins? This report integrates two key ap-
proaches: traditional risk assessment and conventional burden of disease analysis. These
fields complement each other, with risk assessment gaining greater clarity as the bur-
den of disease provides a quantifiable measure of the associated risk. BoD can serve as
a valuable complement to risk assessment by providing additional details. Since iAs is
associated with multiple health endpoints, relying on a single method may result in the

2
Method
The method to conduct this project is divided into four major categories: hazard identi-
fication, hazard characterization, exposure assessment and risk characterization. Hazard
identification in both RA and BoD involves understanding how the body processes the
chemical (toxicokinetics), and how the chemical affects the body (toxicodynamics). Haz-
ard characterization assesses the link between iAs and health effects using dose–response
analysis, with both approaches ultimately relying on benchmark dose modeling. The
third category, the exposure assessment, will be the same for both approaches, relying on
occurence and concentration data. The risk characterization will differ, where RA gives
a semi-quantitative result, and BoD gives a quantitative result. The same studies will be
used for both approaches, so the results are comparable. Essentially, the method is beings
in chapter 2 and ends with chapter 8.
2.1
Literature Search
The literature search aimed to identify studies linking iAs exposure to selected health out-
comes, providing a foundation for the project’s analysis. Before conducting a systematic
literature search for this project, reports from the US EPA (U.S. Environmental Protection
Agency, 2025) and EFSA (EFSA Panel on Contaminants in the Food Chain (CONTAM) et
al., 2024) were reviewed to gain insight into both cancer and non-cancer endpoints. Based
on this background, three specific non-cancer, and four cancer endpoints were selected:
T2D, CVD, neurodevelopment, prostate,- bladder,- lung,- and skin cancer. These end-
points, along with iAs, were then used as keywords in the literature search in PubMed.
The initial screening was conducted at the abstract level. If an abstract appeared rele-
vant, the full text of the article was then reviewed. Additionally, references cited within
these articles were also examined, even though they were not part of the original PubMed
search. Additional sources were reviewed and included if they appeared relevant to the
project. The following sections present the results of the literature search, including the
identification of relevant health endpoints and supporting evidence for the analysis.

3.6
Cardiovascular Diseases
CVD is often linked to lifestyle, but studies suggest iAs in our diet and drinking water
could also be a risk. It may cause oxidative stress, leading to circulatory issues (Dubois-
Deruy et al., 2020; EFSA Panel on Contaminants in the Food Chain (CONTAM) et al.,

When iAs enters the bloodstream, it can interfere with cellular systems, disrupting func-
tions, such as mitochondrial activity and other pathways, which promote excessive ROS
production and leads to oxidative stress, (Dubois-Deruy et al., 2020). This causes dam-
ages to membranes, DNA damage and damage in the endothelial cells (Sidhu et al., 2015).
Excessive ROS disrupt biological processes, with early cardiovascular effects including
impaired endothelial function and reduced nitric oxide (NO) levels. Endothelial cells line
blood vessels, forming a protective barrier and regulating blood flow via NO-mediated
vasodilation. When this barrier weakens, it can allow iAs to enter the bloodstream, (Al-
berts et al., 2002). Without enough NO, arteries can narrow, reducing blood flow to the
heart (Hadi et al., 2005).
Excessive ROS cause endothelial cells to express cell-adhesion molecules, and oxidative
stress heightens this effect. The endothelial layer shifts from a protective to a vulnerable
barrier, promoting inflammation through immune cell infiltration. The compromised en-
dothelial barrier allows low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol to seep into the blood
vessel walls, where it can accumulate. As this build-up grows, macrophages (a type of im-
mune cell) engulf the lipids, and transform into “foam cells” due to the high fat content.
These foam cells settle in the inner layer of the artery, forming fatty streaks, an early sign
of plaque development. Plaques are fatty deposits that can clog arteries and restrict blood
flow. Foam cells also release inflammatory cytokines, small proteins that signals between
cells during immune responses (Zhang & An, 2007). These cytokines attract more inflam-
matory cells, damaging surrounding tissues. This can result in blood clots enlarging, and
block an artery, potentially leading to a heart attack or stroke, (Sidhu et al., 2015). The

Hazard Characterization
The purpose of this section evaluates the link between iAs intake from food and water
and its health effects through dose–response analysis. This section will solely rely on
epidemiological studies (Huang et al., 2024). New findings on non-cancer outcomes will
be explored. Even low iAs levels may contribute to chronic disease, (U.S. Environmental
Protection Agency, 2025).
Hazard characterization uses dose–response data to show how exposure is related to
health risks. Appendix B provides additional information on benchmark dose models
(BMD).
The Panels; EFSA and US EPA made a comprehensive dose-response analysis for several
of the endpoints. The BMD method uses all dose–response data to estimate a dose (and
its lower confidence limit, BMDL). The study selection differ between the two Panels.
EFSA reviewed meta-analyses, original cross-sectional, case–control and cohort studies,
characterized by estimates of the individual or group level of exposure to iAs were con-
sidered. US EPA used single-studies and meta-analyses. They were used to utilize wider
array of studies more fully in the derivation of toxicity values.
Both EFSA and EPA made dose-response models using different tools. EFSA used their
own webtool, which can be found on their homepage. Data from epidemiological stud-
ies (relative risks (RR) or odds ratios OR)) are converted into quantal data, numbers of
cases, to facilitate benchmark dose modeling. The focus is on quantifying low-dose health
risks while accounting for confounding and population heterogeneity. US EPA uses their
own tool for Bayesian benchmark dose models, where probability, variation and uncer-
tainty are integrated in their models. This model is used across several endpoints (e.g.,
bladder/lung cancer, IHD, diabetes). Rather than transforming data into quantal form,
EPA calculates “effective counts” to incorporate adjusted estimates directly into meta-
analyses, preserving epidemiological integrity.

4.1.1
Uncertainties
Taken together from both approaches, it is important to recognize that every human is
different—particularly in biological processes such as methylation capacity. This means
that some individuals metabolize iAs more efficiently than others. These differences in-
fluence how iAs and its metabolites appear in the body, which is why measuring urinary
arsenic species is often used to estimate exposure. However, the reliability of such mea-
surements can vary depending on study design. Some studies rely on a single spot urine
sample per participant, where it would be more informative to collect multiple samples
over time to better reflect ongoing exposure. Furthermore, there is uncertainty regarding
the total iAs exposure in some studies, as not all potential exposure routes are fully ac-
counted for. Due to the time limitation of this project, only dietary exposure—through
food and beverages—has been considered. Other important exposure pathways, such as
inhalation and dermal absorption, are not included in the scope of this thesis, although
they may contribute to the overall exposure profile.

7.3
Disability-Adjusted-Life-Years (DALY)
The purpose of DALYs is to allow comparison of public health impacts across multiple
diseases, including those caused by foodborne chemicals, by integrating information on
their occurrence, severity, and mortality, (Jakobsen et al., 2024).
DALY is used as a population health metric, where a calculation of healthy life years lost,
by adding the adjusted number of years lived with a disability (YLD), and the number of
years lost due to premature death (YLL), (Devleesschauwer et al., 2014). The equations to
calculate them are:
YLD = N * D * DW
(11)
YLL = M * RLE
(12)
DALY = YLD + YLL
(13)
YLD is defined by the number of incident cases (N), and the average duration until re-
covered or death (D), and the disability weight (DW), reflecting the reduction life quality.
YLL is defined by the number of deaths (M), and the remaining life expectancy at the time
of death (RLE). DALYs are determined by combining the years of life lost (YLL) due to
premature death with the years lived with disability (YLD), weighted by the severity of
the condition, (Jakobsen et al., 2024).
In this project two approaches were used to calculate DALY for each endpoint.

Discussion and Conclusion
The aim of this project was: "What are the differences and similarities between traditional risk
assessment and the disease burden of iAs in food, and can the results from these be combined into
an overall estimate of the impact and refine the assessment of iAs?". The project assesses the
risk of adverse health effects from iAs using two approaches. Both share three core steps:
hazard identification, hazard characterization, and exposure assessment. They diverge at
the risk characterization stage:
• RA produces a MOE, indicating an tolerable exposure level for each endpoint.
• BoD estimates the number of cases per endpoint. On top of that the DALYs are
calculated for a population-based impact.
Although, both approaches follow the same framework, their outcomes differ.
Toxicokinetics,- and dynamics data link dietary iAs exposure to all endpoints, ultimately
causing oxidative stress and reduced DNA repair, increasing vulnerability to adverse ef-
fects.
For bladder cancer, the study from (C. M. Steinmaus et al., 2013), was selected due to a
lack of alternatives providing dose-response data. Its use is debatable, as the dose levels
are high compared to the exposures in this project. The resulting MOE of 2.70 indicates a
potential concern, as exposure is close to the effect level. The BoD when using data from
EFSA is estimated to be approximately 190 new cases attributed to iAs per year, while
the BoD using US EPA´s approach approximately 25 new cases attributed to iAs over a
lifetime.
For the lung cancer endpoint, two studies were used (C. Steinmaus et al., 2014; C. M.
Steinmaus et al., 2013), both showing doses close to those causing adverse effects. The
2013 study had exposures up to 860 µg/L, while the 2014 follow-up included doses >100
µg/L. MOEs of 9.81 and 0.81, both below 10, suggest potential health risks. Despite

To evaluate oxidative stress, which is likely linked to the endpoints, it raises the question
of whether a threshold for iAs exposure should be established. Oxidative stress is a mech-
anism that may occur at varying exposure levels. Epigenetic changes or impaired DNA
repair are taken into account, it suggests that even very low exposures could contribute
to adverse effects. In that case, a health-based threshold may not be appropriate, as these
mechanisms suggest there could be no exposure level without risk.
Observing the UF = 3 set by the US EPA. This factor is applied for potential inter-individual
differences in toxicokinetics- and dynamics. US EPA made a multi-study analysis with
many studies and heterogeneous populations, where each study adjusted for many sen-
sitive subgroups and confounders (smoking, gender, nutritional status, life-stage, genetic

variability and methylation capacity). This resulted in a leave-one-out approach, where
one study is left out of the Bayesian dose-response meta-analysis of IHD or T2D. The
pooled slope never increased more than 2.5-fold, being the argument why a 10-fold UF
is not needed. The use of UF = 3, according to US EPA, accounts for inter-individual
differences within the sensitive population. Age, genetics and health status uniformaly
make them more "vulnerable" to exposure of iAs. This is because there is only a limited
number of sensitive populations studied, and may not represent all "at risk group". More-
over, when the entire US population is considered (sensitive and not-sensitive groups),
using data from the sensitive individuals already captures much of the variation that is
expected. In other words; a higher UF is not needed, due to it considering the sensitive
subgroups, according to the panel. It can be discussed if the UF of 3 is reasonable, because
usually a UF of 10 is used, to account for uncertainties and variation between the studies
and the studies´ concerned population.
Essentially, RA results in one critical effect, where BoD considers all health effects. In
EFSA’s assessment, the critical effect identified is skin cancer, because the BMDL is 0.06
µg/kg bw/day, whereas the US EPA identifies IHD, resulting in RfD of 0.06 µg/kg-day.
These results are also observed, when interpreting the incidence numbers and DALYs.
The differences between the two approaches are mainly two different results in the risk
characterization; MOE and incidence numbers. On top of that, the DALYs are calculated
to get the population-level impact, by determining the which endpoint burdens the most.
A key aspect of this project is the comparison between the number of iAs-related cases
and the calculated MOEs. By using the BMDL, a more conservative result is derived. The
BMD reflects the most likely dose associated with a specific effect. Relying on BMD is
therefore a more balanced approach.
Using only one method to assess all health endpoints from iAs exposure risks losing im-
portant information, especially given the wide range of effects. Integrating BoD into RA
is particularly relevant when multiple adverse outcomes occur. In such cases, BoD offers

B
Benchmark Dose Models
The benchmark dose (BMD) is a dose level derived from a fitted dose–response curve
that corresponds to a specified change in response—known as the benchmark response
(BMR)—relative to the control or background level. The BMDL (benchmark dose lower
confidence limit), represents the lower bound of the BMD’s credible interval and is typ-
ically used as the reference point (RP) in risk assessment. This approach is applicable
to all types of toxicological effects and utilizes the full range of dose–response data to
estimate the overall shape of the response curve for a given health endpoint. The goal
of applying dose–response modeling is to determine a dose that can serve as a reference
point for establishing safe levels of human exposure. This reference point is selected from
animal or human toxicity studies and should indicate a level below which no significant
adverse health effects are expected. To ensure reliability, the modeling must account for
uncertainties and biological variability, including differences between and within species
(inter- and intraspecies variation). Conducting robust toxicity studies requires testing a
Page 65 of 89
==PAGE:79==
B
Benchmark Dose Models
range of dose levels to generate data across the full spectrum of potential effects. These
dose groups help define both the lower and upper bounds of the dose–response rela-
tionship, enhancing the model’s accuracy. By incorporating all available data, the BMD
method provides a statistically sound and biologically relevant estimate of risk.
When applying the BMD approach, credible intervals should be calculated for all rele-
vant datasets, as each corresponding BMDL may serve as a potential RP. These intervals
indicate the range of uncertainty around the estimated “true BMD” for each endpoint. A
common approach is to select the lowest BMDL across endpoints as the RP. However, this
method may not always be optimal, as it doesn’t account for the biological significance
of endpoints or the variability in confidence intervals. In practice, a more holistic eval-
uation is often necessary—one that considers not only the magnitude of the BMDLs but
also the precision of the estimates (e.g., the width of the credible intervals). This process
requires expert judgment and is the responsibility of the risk assessor, who must make a
well-supported decision on which BMDL to adopt as the final RP. A key advantage of the
BMD approach is that it incorporates all available data and explicitly includes uncertainty
in the selection of the RP. The BMDL continues to represent a dose at which the likelihood
of observing an effect larger than the benchmark response (BMR) is low. However, if the
data quality is poor—indicated by a broad credible interval—this implies a high degree of
uncertainty in the BMD estimate. In such cases, relying solely on the lowest BMDL might
misrepresent the actual risk. Therefore, a more nuanced approach that integrates statis-
tical uncertainty, biological relevance, and expert judgment helps ensure a more reliable
and transparent risk assessment. (Committee et al., 2022)
