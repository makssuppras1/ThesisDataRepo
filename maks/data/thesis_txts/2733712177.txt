The fate of barbiturates in the hyporheic 
zone examined through field investigations 
at Grindsted stream 
 
 
 
 
Master thesis by Anna Charlotte Priess Sørensen (s184095) 
DTU Sustain, the Technical University of Denmark 
==PAGE:2==
Abstract
Soil contamination poses a risk not solely to groundwater but also to surface water. Cur-
rently, contaminants from the soil contamination at the Grindsted factory site situated in
Jutland, Denmark, are continuing to discharge into the Grindsted stream. Barbiturates are
a group of pharmaceuticals, which are included in the plume along with other contaminants.
To potentially remediate the contamination and protect the stream water, it is important
to understand the physical and chemical conditions of the flow system and how it affects
the fate of barbiturates, which is the topic of this thesis. The work is conducted through
a combination of literature studies on theory and previous field investigations from the
Grindsted stream and entirely new field investigations made specifically in connection with
this thesis. A North bend meander with a high inflow of barbiturates to the Grindsted
stream, which have not been investigated previously, is subject to investigation during two
field campaigns. The campaign first examines the wider area, and the second campaign
examines a transect placed across the stream applying the same methods. Hydraulic
potentials and field parameters are measured, and water samples are collected and analyzed
for tracers, redox parameters, and barbiturates. This is done to assess the flow system
and fate of barbiturates.
The findings of the field investigations are generally in line with previous investigations.
They reveal that the hydraulic potential decreases across the stream with variations along
either side of the stream because of varying groundwater flow and hydraulic conduct-
ivities. The equipotential lines found under the transect indicates how and where the
flow discharges into the stream. The extent of the hyporheic zone is not identified by
the measured redox parameters, but it does not extent as deep as 30 to 40 cm beneath
the streambed. The highest concentrations of barbiturates are discharging closest to the
South bank where the sampling depths cross flow lines. Dilution is found throughout the
transect, but potential for degradation is only found in the middle of the stream where the
residence time is the longest. The most reduced conditions (manganese and iron), which
are the most prevalent in the area, are also found in the middle of the stream.
A conceptual model is created, which suggest that natural attenuation is not an effective
mean of remediation. Instead, active intervention is suggested to protect the stream and
remove the contamination, if the concentrations of barbiturates exceed a given threshold.
Finally, further monitoring and investigations of the contamination of the stream water
and hyporheic zone are suggested.

1
Introduction and problem statement
Fresh clean water is a scare resource only growing more rare with the effects of climate
change, while also being at risk from other human related activities (Biksey and Brown,
2001). The Water Framework Directive of the EU was created to prevent further damage
an to protect and improve water resources and the ecosystems that they are a part of
(Miljøministeriet, 2023a). Soil contamination poses a risk not only to the groundwater,
which is the main sources of Danish drinking water, but also to surface water as the
contamination can seep into the groundwater and be transported to the surface water,
thus impacting the surface water ecosystem as well. Groundwater is protected by law and
in 2014 surface water affected by leaching from soil contamination was added as an area
of intervention under the soil contamination law (Jordforureningsloven) (Miljøministeriet,
2023c).
The task of handling the contaminated sites depend on size and scale of the contamination,
and in 2020 the 5 regions of Denmark identified the 10 largest soil contamination sites in
the country, the so-called legacy sites (Generationsforureninger). These legacy sites are
defined by expected clean-up costs exceeding 50 million Danish kroner per site, and that
if they are left untreated, they will create significant problems for many future generations
to come (Miljøministeriet, 2023b).
The previous factory site in the city of Grindsted, situated in Jutland, holds one of these
legacy sites, and the activities from the factory have resulted in four sites of contamination,
the factory site being one of them (Danske Regioner, 2022). The contaminants have seeped
into the ground and contaminated the groundwater, which flows towards and is discharging
into the nearby stream, the Grindsted stream. The contamination poses a risk to the to
both human health and aquatic life. Back in 1946 the local anglers complained about the
contamination because the fish they caught had a distinct smell and distaste (Billund
Kommune, 2023). The contamination entering the stream will also alter the chemical
composition of the stream water and sediments thus affecting the whole ecosystem from
primary producers to fish - impacting the distribution and abundance of aquatic and
sedimentary life, (Dent et al., 2000).
Despite the many years of investigation of the Grindsted factory plume and the Grindsted
stream there are still contaminants for which aspects of their fate in this environment are
unknown, the largest group amongst these contaminants are the barbiturates. Barbiturates
were introduced in 1904 as a mean to deal with psychiatric and neurological disorders
but were also found to be effective when addressing sleeping disorders (López-Muñoz
et al., 2005). From the 1920s to the mid-1950s barbiturates were favored as hypnotics
and sedatives, and their peak usage was in the 1930’s and the 1940’s. However, the
problems of addiction and death when overdosed lead to reduced usage compared to today
(López-Muñoz et al., 2005). The barbiturates in the factory plume have, through previous
site investigations, been found to enter the Grindsted stream in groundwater discharge
areas, however not all of these areas have been examined in detail (Sonne et al., 2012;
1 of 116
==PAGE:9==
Rambøll, 2023). The groundwater discharges into the stream through the hyporheic zone,
which is the first 0 to 1 meter of sediments under the streambed. Here the nutrient rich
groundwater mixes together with the aerobic stream water in sediments that contain
higher levels of microbes, thus creating some unique chemical and biological conditions,
which varies from both the groundwater and stream water (Woessner, 2000; Winter et al.,
1998; Biksey and Brown, 2001).
Little is know about the degradation of barbiturates, however degradation is assumed to
occur under aerobic conditions as these have been the focus of multiple studies, including
Peschka et al., 2006; Yu et al., 2006, and Bilić, 2023. Bilić, 2023, found degradation of
isopropylbarbituric acid and degradation potential for secobarbital during aerobic batch
experiments. The degradation was attributed to a combination of hydrolysis and biotic
degradation. Hydrolysis is the main form of degradation examined when it comes to
barbiturates, yet the experimental conditions often differs from the natural environment
in terms of temperature, as seen in studies by Maulding et al., 1972; Garrett et al., 1971,
and Hasegawa et al., 1958. The fate of barbiturates in the hyporheic zone is not expected
to solely rest upon the possibly more oxic conditions in the hyporheic zone, factors such as
the residence time, the microbes present, and the redox parameters at the site of discharge
will most likely also have an affect, since they affect the fate of other chemicals. The
residence time and the microbes present at the discharge site have been shown to affect the
fate of chlorinated ethenes by Ottosen et al., 2020, and Weatherill et al., 2018, respectively.
While Morsing and Petersen, 2022, found that sulfonamides can be transformed under
iron/manganese reducing conditions. The importance of redox parameters are further
supported by Ingerslev, 2023, who found possible, but not finally confirmed, degradation of
pentobarbital in the hyporheic zone where conditions were primarily iron and manganese
reducing.
The Danish authorities want to remediate the contamination near the Grindsted stream
in order to reduce the contamination inflow to below a desired threshold. A suggested
threshold for the concentration sum of barbiturates is 7 μg/L (Boyd et al., 2023).
This thesis aims to gain a better understanding of barbiturates’ fate as they enter and flow
through the near stream subsurface and the hyporheic zone by examining the combined
influence of the chemical and physical characteristics of the near stream subsurface and the
hyporheic zone and how it relates to barbiturates and their degradation, distribution, and
residence time. This thesis has the following four objectives to structure the investigation.
1. Understand the physical and chemical properties of barbiturates in Grindsted along
with the interaction between the groundwater, the hyporheic zone, and the surface
water.
2. Combine the knowledge of the flow system, the geology, and previous barbiturate
detection to investigate the contaminant inflow to the stream and the residence time
in the hyporheic zone.
2 of 116
==PAGE:10==
3. Interpret results from field data and compare them with previous measurement cam-
paigns of the Grindsted area to enlighten the spread and degradation of barbiturates
as well as gaining a better understanding of the near stream subsurface and the
hyporheic zone at the part of the stream subject to investigation in this thesis.
4. Evaluate the fate of barbiturates in terms of potential degradation and the need for
active intervention to remove the contamination.

2
Assessment of barbiturates
This section introduces barbiturates, the chemicals investigated in this thesis, which are
found in Grindsted. It provides general information regarding the structure and history of
barbiturates. Moreover, it focuses on the specific barbiturates examined, their physical
and chemical properties, and degradation.
2.1
An overview of the structure and history
Barbiturates are a group of more than 1,000 pharmaceutical compounds. All barbiturates
are derived from barbituric acid (CAS no. 67-52-7), which is synthesized from malonic
acid and urea, see Figure 1a (National Center for Biotechnology Information, 2023; López-
Muñoz et al., 2005; Peschka et al., 2006). The fundamental structure of all barbiturates is a
2,4,6-pyrimidine ring with up to three side-chains, see Figure 1b. The different barbiturates
are then created by substituting various groups on the 1. and 5. positions in the ring.
One side-chain attach to the 1. position and two attach to the 5. position, see Figure 1b,
where the changeable side-chains are noted with R1, R2, and R3. Examples of barbiturates
can be seen in Figure 2 and in Appendix A.

2.1.1
The history of barbiturate usage
The clinical introduction to barbiturates started in the early 19 hundreds, despite more
than 2,500 barbiturates being created eventually only 50 to 60 were marketed and employed
(López-Muñoz et al., 2005; Peschka et al., 2006).
They were originally used as the active component in medicine to treat sleep disorders and
were also effective in managing epileptic seizures, while in the 1920s to 1950s they were
some of the only drugs applied as sedatives and hypnotics (López-Muñoz et al., 2005).
Their peek popularity and use were in the 1930s, 1940s and 1960s, despite the fact that no
barbiturate managed to eliminate the problems, which caused addiction and death when
overdosed (López-Muñoz et al., 2005; Peschka et al., 2006). In the 1960s and 1970s they
also became popular for recreational use, which have decreased since then (Davis, 2018).
4 of 116
==PAGE:12==
Barbiturates were still the most common active component in sleep medication until the
1970s (Holm et al., 1995). Barbiturates have since then mainly been used in veterinary
drugs (Peschka et al., 2006; Davis, 2018). Today there are only a few barbiturates still in
use in human medicine (López-Muñoz et al., 2005). Generally, they are infrequently used
and today they are primarily used as treatment for epilepsy, whereas they are hardly ever
used in medicine to treat sleep disorders, which were their initial usage (Davis, 2018).

2.2
The fate of barbiturates in the environment
The physical and chemical properties of chemical compounds can be used as indicators
for where they expectedly will end up in the environment. These properties include Kow,
Henry’s constant (KH), and solubility. Table 1 contains some of the physical and chemical
properties of the barbiturates in focus. Table A2 in Appendix A contains the properties of
all the barbiturates found in Grindsted.
The barbiturates are not expected to volatilise because their Henry’s constants are all
in the range of 10–14 to 10–13, see Table 1, and based on the definition by Baun et al.,
2017, compounds with a KH < 3 · 10–7 are less volatile than water. Applying the formula;
Koc = 0.41·Kow, by Karickhoff, 1981, Koc values are estimated to be between 0.4 and 51.6.
Given these values, the barbiturates are estimated to have large mobility in soil, according
to McCall et al., 1980. Additionally, the barbiturates are not expected to bioaccumulate
because of their low LogKow values. Chemicals with a LogKow larger than 4 are labelled
potentially bioaccumulative in accordance with REACH (Baun et al., 2017). As the
barbiturates do not volatilise, sorp, or are anticipated to bioaccumulate, the majority of
the barbiturates will move with the water, which is also evident from the large range of
solubility, 550 to 7460 mg/L, see Table 1. When pH is equal to a compounds pKa, the ra-
tio of dissociated to the non-dissociated form of that compound is 50:50 (Baun et al., 2017).

In Grindsted the pH ranges from 5.02 to 7.29 (Rambøll, 2020), and the pKa range of the
barbiturates in focus is from 7.3 to 8.14 with isopropylbarbituric acid being the exception,
see Table 1. Isopropylbarbituric acid has a pKa of 4.6 and the dissociated form is therefore
expected to dominate. The reason isopropylbarbituric acid has a much lower pKa than
the rest of the barbiturates may be because it only has one side-chain while all the others
have two, see Figure 2. The concentrations of barbiturates found in Grindsted could be
a little affected by their pKa but any decrease in concentration is expected to happen
mainly via dilution or degradation.
The environmental toxicity of barbiturates is not well known, as none of the barbiturates
in focus have an environmental hazard classification. Standardized tests examining the
ecotoxicity of barbiturates in fresh water have only been found for four out of the eight
barbiturates in focus, these being barbital, amobarbital, pentobarbital, and secobarbital
(Boyd et al., 2023). The test which produced the lowest 50% response for lethal con-
centration (LC50) was a 96 hours test with fish using secobarbital, the LC50 was 21.6
mg/L (Baun et al., 2017; Boyd et al., 2023). Nothing have been found regarding the
chronic affects of barbiturate exposure making it impossible to postulate about affects the
barbiturates already have and will continue to have on the biodiversity in the Grindsted
stream.
2.3
Degradation of the Barbiturates in focus
Degradation can be divided into abiotic and biotic degradation. Abiotic degradation
includes photolysis and hydrolysis. Photolysis will only briefly be mentioned, as this thesis
examines barbiturates underneath the streambed and barbiturate samples are expected
to have minimal light exposure during handling. Biotic degradation or biodegradation is
when microorganisms act on a given substance, thus degrading it.
2.3.1
Abiotic degradation
Photolysis:
Pentobarbital is not considered susceptible to sunlight induced photolysis
according to Nobay and Acquisto, 2014, because it does not have chromophores that
absorb the wavelengths required. However, this is contradicted by Hofmann et al., 2020,
who lists pentobarbital along with butalbital, butobarbital, and phenobarbital as having
photosensitizing potential because they become active under light exposure. In respect to
phenobarbital Nobay and Acquisto, 2014, agree with Hofmann et al., 2020, stating that it
is susceptible to photolysis as it has chromophores that absorb wavelengths higher than
290 nm. According to Barton et al., 1982, and Otsuji et al., 1968, barbital can degrade
through photolysis. The photolysis of barbital is found in alkaline aqueous medium with
UV light at 254nm (Otsuji et al., 1968). Barton et al., 1982, further claims that photolysis
of barbital yields derivatives of urea and biuret. So, according to the literature four out of
the eight barbiturates in focus do either have photosensitizing potential or are proven to
undergo photolysis, as such the minimization of sunlight exposure during the handling of
samples should be insured.

Hydrolysis:
Literature agrees that barbital can undergo abiotic hydrolysis, and that
diethyl malonuric acid is a metabolit (Garrett et al., 1971; Jackson et al., 1954). Fretwurst
(1954), reported a finding of 50% of degraded barbital as diethyl malonuric acid (Gardner
and Goyan, 1973). However, according to Garrett et al., 1971, and later verified by
Gardner and Goyan, 1973, diethyl malonuric acid can cyclize to barbital, the parent
compound. Dieethylacetylurea and α-ethylbutyl urea are two additional metabolites of
barbital (Garrett et al., 1971; Jackson et al., 1954). Garrett et al., 1971, suggest that the
cyclization of diethyl malonuric acid to barbital occurs simultaneously as it degrades to
diethylacetylurea. According to Wen and Fulco, 1985, the lack of degradation potential,
which is seen for barbital among other barbiturates, can be a result of the compounds low
lipophilicity.
Not much is known regarding the hydrolysis of allobarbital at pH below 10, however the
compound is susceptible to hydrolysis, with one or two degradation pathways (Hermann,
1976). Opening of the ring structure are the suggested degradation pathway for barbit-
urates in general. Whether the ring opens in the 1,2 and/or 1,6 positions in the case of
allobarbital is yet to be determined (Hermann, 1976), see Figure 1b for the ring structure.
Garrett et al., 1971, examines the hydrolysis of a number of barbiturates under neutral
to alkaline conditions at 80◦C, these include among others barbital, amobarbital, pento-
barbital, and secobarbital. During the experiments Garrett et al., 1971, found increasing
first order rate constants with increasing pH. In a study by Hasegawa et al., 1958, the
effect of pH on the hydrolysis of phenobarbital was investigated under three temperature
conditions (40, 50, and 60◦C). It is in line with Garrett et al., 1971, that the degradation
rate is accelerated under alkaline conditions, while also arguing that degradation depends
on the temperature (Hasegawa et al., 1958).
Phenobarbital are not expected to hydrolyse in the environment, given the fact that
the chemical lacks functional groups which easily hydrolyse (Nobay and Acquisto, 2014).
Similarly, pentobarbital is also not expected to hydrolyse in the environment because of
the lack of functional groups (Nobay and Acquisto, 2014), but this contradicts the work
by Garrett et al., 1971. The hydrolysis of secobarbital and butalbital was investigated by
Peschka et al., 2006, who reported no hydrolysis of either compound in the pH range 2 to
12. Contradictory, Maulding et al., 1972, did report hydrolysis of butalbital in neutral to
alkaline conditions within the temperature range from 60 to 80◦C. Butalbital was found
to have ring openings in the 1,6 and 1,2 position with respectively neutral and alkaline
pH conditions (Maulding et al., 1972). The metabolites found by Maulding et al., 1972,
were diamide and malonuic acid. Nothing have been found regarding the hydrolysis of
isopropylbarbituric acid and butobarbital. As for the other barbiturates, the results can
be questioned given the high temperature conditions of some of the experiments. Also the
literature are not all in agreement regarding the abiotic hydrolysis of the remaining focus
barbiturates. Conflicting findings have been found for pentobarbital, secobarbital, and
butalbital reporting; both hydrolysis and no hydrolysis. Additionally, phenobarbital is not
found to undergo hydrolysis while barbital, allobarbital, and amobarbital do. Thus, the
process of abiotic hydrolysis cannot be excluded as a possibility if degradation is observed.

2.3.2
Biotic degradation
Microbial degradation can play an important role in the removal of chemicals from the
environment. Complete mineralization is when a chemical is converted into water, carbon
dioxide, and other simple products (Winter et al., 1998). Complete mineralization is not
always achieved during the microbial degradation processes, and it is slower in anaerobic
conditions (Sijm et al., 2007). Primary degradation or biodegradation is when the structure
of the parent compound is transformed leaving intermediate products, some of which can
be of additional concern for the environment (Sijm et al., 2007; Winter et al., 1998). The
rate of degradation depends on the chemical structure, the environmental conditions, and
the microorganisms present medium (Winter et al., 1998). Caracciolo et al., 2013, describes
the importance of the microbes present at the contamination site, showing the relationship
between degradation and microbes. The literature review preformed by Draborg, 2021,
found the information pertaining to the biodegradation of barbiturates to be contradicting,
In the review only a few metabolites were identified along with one possible degrader. The
identified degrader was Bacillus megaterium ATCC 14581 (Kim and Fulco, 1983).
Peschka et al., 2006, conducted no-light experiments investigating the aerobic biode-
gradation of a number of barbiturates, including butalbital and secobarbital, finding no
transformation resulting from microbial processes during an incubation period of 42 days.
Thus, Peschka et al., 2006, reports barbiturates as hardly degradable in the environment
and persistent. Nobay and Acquisto, 2014, states that biodegradation of phenobarbital
is expected to be an unimportant process in the environment, contradicting the results
reported by Yu et al., 2006. Yu et al., 2006, conducted aerobic batch studies using
activated sludge and found phenobarbital to have ready biodegradability potential with
80% degraded after 50 days of incubation. The studies also found 70% degradation of
secobarbital after the 50 days, which differs from the findings of Peschka et al., 2006, but
it is not sufficient to view secobarbital as potentially ready biodegradable (Yu et al., 2006).
Ingerslev, 2023, investigated the degradation of six barbiturates in the hyporheic zone
at a transect of the Grindsted stream, but could not definitively confirm the occurrence
of degradation. However, a correlation between the tracer bromide and pentobarbital
was found and it indicates possible degradation in the hyporheic zone (Ingerslev, 2023).
Additional work into the degradation of barbiturates at the Grindsted stream was conducted
by Bilić, 2023, who examined the aerobic degradation of barbiturates in batch experiments
using sediments from the Grindsted stream. Seven barbiturates were investigated over a
period of 60 days, which found full degradation of isopropylbarbituric acid (Bilić, 2023).
Degradation above 20% was also seen for secobarbital, which supports the findings of
Yu et al., 2006, however no degradation was observed for the remaining five barbiturates
(Bilić, 2023). Information in the literature on biodegradation only sheds light on four of
the barbiturates in focus and all in respect to aerobic biodegradation. Nothing is found
about anaerobic degradation. According to the literature butalbital does not biodegrade,
while isopropylbarbituric acid do and for pentobarbital, secobarbital, and phenobarbital it
is either conflicting or suggested the hypotheses need testing.

3
Interactions of the hyporheic zone
The hydrological cycle describes how all water are connected and move between the
different compartments (Winter et al., 1998). This study focus on a small part of the
cycle, namely the interactions of groundwater, surface water - in this case stream water -
and the hyporheic zone. The hyporheic zone is located just beneath the streambed and it
is where the two waters mix.
3.1
Groundwater
The term groundwater describes the water found in the subsurface underneath the water
table in the saturated zone. The subsurface is divided into the unsaturated zone and
the saturated zone, which are separated by the water table. Groundwater is separated
into aquifers by low permeability materials and layers creating three types of aquifer -
confined, semi-confined, and unconfined. Confined and semi-confined aquifers are respect-
ively covered and semi-covered by a confining low permeability layer at the top. These
covers can protect the deeper confined aquifers, while the unconfined aquifers closer to the
surface are more susceptible to contamination (Woessner and Poeter, 2020).
Groundwater moves in a three dimensional flow field from high to low pressure. This pres-
sure is often referred to as the hydraulic head and is depicted on maps with equipotential
lines which the groundwater flow lines are perpendicular to (Woessner and Poeter, 2020).
If the hydraulic head of an aquifer is above the surface terrain, then this groundwater is
artesian. The hydraulic head of an unconfined aquifer is equal to the water table, which
can be located in varying depths. However, near permanent bodies of water like lakes,
rivers, and streams the water table is usually found nearer to the surface (Woessner, 2020;
Woessner and Poeter, 2020).
Groundwater and stream water are interconnected to the point where impacts on one
of these will results in consequences for the other either in terms of quantity or quality
(Abdelhalim et al., 2020). The interaction of groundwater and stream water is based on
the vertical movement of the flow systems (Woessner and Poeter, 2020), see Figure 3.
But a number of other factors also influence the interactions of groundwater and stream
water, these being hydraulic conductivity distribution, stream channel geometry, and the
hydraulic gradient (Balbarini et al., 2017; Woessner, 2000).
Groundwater does not always interact with surface water, in order for groundwater to
enter into a stream, or another surface water-body; the water table near the stream must
be greater than the stream water level (Woessner, 2020). When groundwater and stream
water do interact, the age of the groundwater will vary. Water from the deeper part of the
aquifer will have travelled longer and be older, while water from the top of the aquifer is
younger (Modica et al., 1998; Woessner, 2020).

The flow processes of groundwater and stream water interactions depend on the scale,
which are sorted into three; sediment/hyporheic scale (0 to 1m), reach/local scale (1 to
1000m), and catchment/regional scale (+1000m) (Balbarini et al., 2017; Banerjee and
Ganguly, 2023). The most dynamic flow systems are the local systems, which are also the
shallowest and the ones that interact with surface water the most, but systems can be
overlain to obtain more details (Woessner, 2020).
3.2
Stream water
The interconnection of groundwater and stream water is important because they can and do
influence one and another, for instance they both function as sources and sinks (Banerjee
and Ganguly, 2023). However, some streams are disconnected from the groundwater
because of an unsaturated zone lying beneath the streambed. Hydrologically connected
streams can interact with groundwater in three ways, they either gain water, lose water or
both (Banerjee and Ganguly, 2023; Woessner, 2020). Gaining streams primarily receive
groundwater discharge, which sustain a baseflow; in fact groundwater contributes to about
35 to 55% of stream water in gaining streams (Banerjee and Ganguly, 2023; Biksey and
Brown, 2001; Winter et al., 1998). A stream’s baseflow is defined as all the water, which
enters into the stream between water input events like rain, persistent sources such as
melt water etc. that only experience slow variations (Sophocleous, 2002).

The surface-subsurface exchange of water is controlled by hydraulic and morphological
features, with the stream curvatures resulting in a characteristic spatial pattern of gaining
and losing zones, mainly located near the banks, along a stream (Boano et al., 2010;
Woessner, 2000; Wroblicky et al., 1998). Flow direction through the streambed is connected
to changes in the streambed or the meanders (Winter et al., 1998). Balbarini et al., 2017,
created a flow model to investigate the groundwater flow to stream meanders at reach
scale (1 to 1000m), and found that peak groundwater discharge occurs at the outside
extremes of the bends, see Figure 4, while also confirming the 3-dimensional flow of water
discharging to a stream. Spacial discharge variability is caused by meander bends, with the
greatest discharge volumes at the most outward bending points of meanders and decreasing
discharge amounts moving towards the center of the stream (Balbarini et al., 2017; Boano
et al., 2010).

A major pathway for the transfer of chemicals between the terrestrial and aquatic sys-

groundwater/surface water interaction are; acid/base reactions, precipitation/dissolution
of minerals, sorption and ion-exchange, oxidation-reduction reactions (redox), dissolution
and exsolution of gases, and biodegradation (Winter et al., 1998), all of which are mostly
affected in and around the hyporheic zone.
3.2.1
The effect of climate change on water interactions
Some of the effects of climate change can already be observed with these affecting the
hydrological cycle. Guevara-Ochoa et al., 2020, used a simulation model to investigate the
effect of two climate change scenarios (RCP4.5 and RCP8.5) on the hydrological cycle and
specifically on the interactions between groundwater and surface water. Both scenarios will
cause significant alterations to the spacio-temporal patterns of the groundwater/surface
water interactions along with the water balance. Variations were observed on monthly,
seasonal, and annual scales. The average annual discharge to streams is expected to
increase for both scenarios. While recharge of the aquifer increases for RCP4.5, it decreases
for RCP8.5. According to the simulations climate change could modify groundwater levels
and reverse groundwater/surface water interactions (Guevara-Ochoa et al., 2020).
3.3
Hyporheic zone
The finest scale for viewing the interactions between the groundwater and the surface
water is the hyporheic scale, which focuses on the hyporheic zone (Harvey and Wagner,
2000). The hyporheic zone is where the groundwater mixes with the stream water before
entering the stream. The hyporheic zone is located along the entirety of the wetted
channel, but there is great variation in the characteristics of the hyporheic zone (Harvey
and Wagner, 2000). The hyporheic zone’s physical and biogeochemical extent depends on
many factors, some of these being; the head distribution, flow direction of groundwater,
stream hydraulics and channel bed conditions, and the magnitudes and distributions of
hydrogeologic parameters (Woessner, 2000).
14 of 116
==PAGE:22==
Time and space greatly varies the size and geometry of the hyporheic zone (Winter et al.,
1998). Although the hyporheic scale is considered to be from 0 to 1m below the channel
bed, Woessner, 2000, found using simulated flow dynamics that the mixing zone of stream
water and groundwater could exist in depths as deep as 1.7m below the channel. However,
during periods of high flows the size of the hyporheic zone lateral to a stream decreases
with approximately 50% (Wroblicky et al., 1998). Banerjee and Ganguly, 2023, defines
the hyporheic zone as water where minimum 10% of the total water volume is stream
water. Water in the hyporheic zone moves back and forth between the active stream and
the subsurface via hyporheic flow paths, with lengths varying from centimeters to meters,
which keep the surface water in contact with the chemically reactive coatings and microbial
colonies in the subsurface (Harvey and Wagner, 2000). This differs from groundwater flow
paths, which only either enters or leaves the channel once (Harvey and Wagner, 2000).
Flow paths:
The hyporheic flow also varies between streams and with the discharge
amounts entering the system as shown by Wroblicky et al., 1998, utilizing groundwater
flow models. Applying a 3-D model Trauth et al., 2013, researched the hyporheic flow and
found it to increase for larger Reynolds number while the residence time decreased. The
average lateral distance of hyporheic flow paths are 20% of the longitudinal length (Trauth
et al., 2013). This means that the water in the hyporheic zone moves more along the
stream than across the stream, maybe because of the current in the stream, despite the
hyporheic zone only containing a fraction of stream water. In fact the circulation of stream
water in the hyporheic zone only make up a fraction ranging between 1·10–5 and 1·10–6/m
of stream length, and this decreases with increasing groundwater discharge (Trauth et al.,
2013). A field experiment by Triska et al., 1989, found that the flow paths varied based
on observed differences in travel time. These differences were also in part attributed to
assumed permeability variations in the subsurface (Triska et al., 1989). Applying models
to predict the horizontal fluvial flux in the hyporheic zone, Cardenas, 2009, found that
across the point bar of meanders is where lateral hyporheic flow primarily take place,
see Figure 6 (B). Field investigations of a stream by Zhang et al., 2017, found that
hyporheic flow about 10 cm from the bank was affected by the infiltration and evaporation
of precipitation, while 30 cm from the bank groundwater was the main influence. Also,
mixing of precipitation with groundwater and stream water intensified around 10 to 30 cm
from the bank (Zhang et al., 2017), showing that precipitation can be of importance when
examining the hyporheic zone.

Tonina et al., 2016, examined the impact of the composition of sediments on flow paths in
the hyporheic zone using a flow model. They found that the heterogeneity or homogeneity
of sediments making up the hyporheic zone affects both the flow paths and the zones of
upwelling and downwelling along a stream. The tortuosity of flow lines and the residence
time of short flow paths increases when comparing heterogeneous sediments to homogeneous
sediments (Tonina et al., 2016). It was also found by Tonina et al., 2016, that some zones of
discharge from the groundwater and recharge to the groundwater switched when changing
the composition of the subsurface with heterogeneous composition, reducing the extent
of zones where water is lost. The permeability of sediment significantly influences the
movement of the hyporheic flow (Zhang et al., 2017). Low permeability can extent the
residence time in the sediment but also alter the flow path so that the water move around
the low permeability section.
Chemical composition:
When the groundwater and stream water are mixed the chem-
ical composition is changed, making the hyporheic zone important for stream processes as
well as potential contaminant transport across the zone (Banerjee and Ganguly, 2023).
The mixing makes the hyporheic zone notably different in terms of both the chemical
and biological characteristics when comparing it to both groundwater and stream water
(Woessner, 2020; Dent et al., 2000). The zone generally contains higher levels of organic
carbon and microbes, which can promote the breakdown of pollutants (Banerjee and Gan-
guly, 2023; Biksey and Brown, 2001). That is why the hyporheic zone can be important
when it comes the the protection of both groundwater and stream water (Zhang et al.,
2017). The hyporheic zone also affects the biology in the stream spatially in terms of the
distribution and abundance of everything from microorganisms to fish (Dent et al., 2000;
Woessner, 2020).
Through the hyporheic zone, groundwater contributes to the concentrations of both
dissolved solids and nutrients in the stream water, which contains less of both (Banerjee
and Ganguly, 2023; Biksey and Brown, 2001; Dent et al., 2000). It is the water fluxes
and their residence times, as they moves through the hyporheic zone, which controls the
delivery of solutes as well as the spatial extent and rate of biogeochemical changes in the
stream (Cardenas, 2009). Kaufman et al., 2017, investigated how the dissolved oxygen
concentration in the hyporheic zone reacts to changes in surface flow and found that
the response time ranged from hours to days. Dissolved oxygen in the hyporheic zone
reacts quicker to an acceleration of surface flow than a deceleration, thus showing that the
dynamic redox conditions in the hyporheic zone are equally driven by the very dynamic
nature of streams (Kaufman et al., 2017). There is generally enhanced biogeochemical
activity in the hyporheic zone, because of the oxygen-rich surface water stimulating aerobic
microorganisms, resulting in biodegradation rates greater than in both groundwater and
stream water (Winter et al., 1998). Also, the retention of solutes in the hyporheic zone is
greater than in the stream during low-flow conditions (Triska et al., 1989).

3.4
Redox conditions
Chemical reactions in which one chemical is oxidized while another is reduced are called
redox reactions. The different reactions that is occurring in a media determines the redox
conditions. Dissolved ions and gasses make up the primary redox species, which are;
iron, manganese, ammonium, nitrite, nitrate, sulfate, hydrogen sulfide, nitrous oxide,
methane, and oxygen (Christensen et al., 2000). Redox reactions generally take place in
accordance with the energy hierarchy, with high energy yield reactions i.e. oxygen and
nitrate reduction, happening first. The hierarchy makes it possible to identify redox zones
in different environments where reactions have been induced, like in contaminated aquifers.
A redox zone may be identified from the dominant redox process occurring, however several
other processes may also be present (Christensen et al., 2000).
Redox conditions in an undisturbed aquifer will normally be different from one with a
contaminant plume, and within that plume multiple redox zones can exist with conditions
being more reduced close to the source of contamination (Christensen et al., 2000). The
presence of redox sensitive compounds can sometimes support the degradation of organic
pollutants and pharmaceuticals (Christensen et al., 2000), the latter was examined by
Burke et al., 2014, in tank experiments under both oxic and anoxic conditions. Out of
27 pharmaceuticals - 16 were found to degrade under both oxic and anoxic conditions, 3
degraded specifically under anoxic conditions, 6 degraded under oxic conditions, while
2 persisted no matter what (Burke et al., 2014). One of the 27 pharmaceuticals was
phenobarbital, which degraded under both oxic and anoxic conditions with the degradation
rate constant being slightly higher under anoxic conditions.
3.4.1
Natural attenuation
Groundwater entering the hyporheic zone helps regulate the temperatures in the zone,
which along with the high nutrient and microbe levels creates a unique habitat, which
can facilitate biodegradation and transformation of organic and inorganic contaminants
(Banerjee and Ganguly, 2023; Biksey and Brown, 2001). Additionally, the change from
anaerobic to aerobic conditions through the mixing of groundwater and stream water is
important for natural attenuation (Biksey and Brown, 2001).
Natural attenuation is the concept of nature breaking down contamination caused by
human activity. Electron donor availability, i.e. the right redox conditions, is not the only
requirement for natural attenuation. The presence and abundance of specific bacteria
are also important, with the specific bacteria required depending on the contamination
in question (Ottosen et al., 2020). The microorganisms and microbial processes in the
hyporheic zone including those required for natural attenuation can be significantly affected
by groundwater/surface water interactions. Changes to these interactions can 1. alter the
availability of resources thus changing the activity, density or composition of the microbial
communities, and 2. change the transport direction of the microorganisms (Dent et al.,
2000).

==PAGE:25==
The hydrogeology was found to have the greatest impact on the near stream attenuation of
chlorinated ethenes when examined by Ottosen et al., 2020, as it affects the residence times.
So, if the residence time is not long enough, natural attenuation may not be observed or
observed at minimal scale despite all other requirements being fulfilled. Additionally, field
experiments by Weatherill et al., 2018, showed that in cases where the correct bacteria is
present, the biotransformation will tend to be spotty. Weatherill et al., 2018, also found
that mass transformation, whether it is occurring via an oxic or an anoxic process, is
controlled by the sediment surface area available for biofilm colonisation, delivery rates of
critical reactants, reaction timescales, and exposure time in the reactive zones.

4.1
The Grindsted contamination
Grindsted is a town located in southern Jutland, Denmark. The town has about 10.000
citizens and takes up an area of 8.5 km2 (Eggert et al., 2020). The city is crossed by
a stream moving westward called the Grindsted stream. Chemical production already
began in Grindsted in 1914 and it is still ongoing today. In 1924 the chemical production
site Grindstedværket was founded, see Figure 7, e.g. producing pharmaceuticals. Today
Grindstedværket and the resulting contamination is one of the main reasons why the
city is known (Danske Regioner, 2022). The owners of the factory, its name, and focus
have changed over the years - today it is owned by US company International Flavors &
Fragances, it is named IFF Grindsted, and its focus is on producing ingredients for the
food production industry (Billund Kommune, 2023).

sites, two of which have been labeled as legacy sites, Grindstedværket and Kærgård
klitplantage (Danske Regioner, 2022). Legacy sites are defined by their expectancy to
cause trouble for generations to come if left untreated and their expected clean-up costs.
The expected clean-up cost for the contaminated areas associated with the factory site
itself summarizes to a three digit million amount in Danish kroner (Danske Regioner, 2022).
The contamination on the factory site was examined in the period from 1984 to 2012
after which the focus shifted to the plume and the stream (Frederiksen et al., 2019). The
stream is located approximately 1.5 km South of the factory (Balbarini et al., 2017).
The groundwater plume consists of multiple compounds such as chlorinated ethenes and
degradation products (TCE, cisDCE, and VC), BTEX (Benzene, Toluene, Ethylbenzene,
and Xylenes.), and a number pharmaceuticals also referred to as the Grindsted phar-
maceuticals (Maurya et al., 2018; Sonne et al., 2012). The Grindsted pharmaceuticals
include ethyl carbamate, sulphonamides, and barbiturates (COWI, 2019). In terms of the
pharmaceutical contamination the source area is largely out-washed, meaning that the
pharmaceuticals are in the plume (Frederiksen et al., 2019). The part of the plume with
the greatest amount of contamination is around the waste water facility called Renseanlæg
Vest, and from there the plume is flowing to the stream (COWI, 2019). The contaminants
seeped deep into the aquifer at the factory and as the plume is flowing towards the stream,
the plume is also flowing upward moving closer to the terrain (COWI, 2019).
4.2
Geology
Grindsted is located on the outwash plain of the last ice-age, which left behind 5 to 15 m
of Quaternary deposits in the form of meltwater sand (Heron et al., 1998; Ottosen et al.,
2020; COWI, 2019). These deposits consists of medium grained sand with beds of both
silty fine grained sand and gravelly coarse grained sand (Maurya et al., 2018; Sonne et al.,
2012). Underneath the Quaternary deposits lies Tertiary Miocene deposits. The Miocene
sediments are 60 to 80 m thick and consist primarily of fine grained sand with intermediate
parts of coarser grained sand (Maurya et al., 2018; Ottosen et al., 2020). Pockets of clay,
silt, and brown coal are also found in the Miocene sediments, which are called the Odderup
formation (Heron et al., 1998; COWI, 2019). About 70 to 80 m below the surface lies a
thick layer of Miocene clay (Sonne et al., 2012). Erosion into the outwash plain during
the postglacial age is what created the stream valley, which today is Grindsted stream
(Maurya et al., 2018). In the channels and on the banks of the stream sandy sediments
are deposited while peat layers are formed on the flood plains (Maurya et al., 2018).
4.3
Hydrology
There are two aquifers underneath the Grindsted area. The upper aquifer in the Odderup
formation, which is contaminated and the lower aquifer from which the region gets its
water supply (Sonne et al., 2012). The two aquifers are separated by the thick Miocene
clay layer found in 70 to 80 m depth. The upper aquifer is unconfined and in hydraulic
contact with the stream (Balbarini et al., 2017). Half-way between the factory site and
20 of 116
==PAGE:28==
the stream a layer of clay and lignite begins to form, which increases from 0.5 to 5 m
when moving towards the stream (Balbarini et al., 2020). This layer is embedded into
the Miocene sand and separates the upper aquifer into a shallow unconfined aquifer and
a deep semi-confined aquifer at the stream (Balbarini et al., 2020). The hydraulic head
measured in wells at the factory site and in wells located between the factory and the
stream are similar to those measured in well screens at the stream beneath the clay and
lignite layer, thus indicating a connection between the aquifer at the factory and the
deeper aquifer at the stream, while hydraulic head in wells screens above the clay and
lignite layer at the stream are different from those measured further upstream indicating a
disconnect and supporting that the aquifer does separate into two at the stream (Balbarini
et al., 2020). The subsurface at the stream are nearly completely saturated, because the
groundwater level is so close to the surface in the shallow unconfined aquifer, while the
deep semi-confined aquifer is artesian (Maurya et al., 2018; COWI, 2019), see Figure 7.
4.4
Contaminant inflow to the stream
The Grindsted stream is a meandering gaining stream, receiving water from both sides as
it travels from East to West. The stream has a catchment area of 200 km2 and is 1 to 2.5
m deep and 8 to 12 m wide (Balbarini et al., 2017). The stream’s water discharge is 2000
L/s (Ottosen et al., 2020; Maurya et al., 2018). The plume has reached and is leaking into
the stream. The amount of chemicals in the groundwater indicates that chemicals will
continue to leak into the stream for many years to come (Frederiksen et al., 2019). Eleven
groundwater discharge areas (GWDs) have been identified along the stream (Draborg
et al., 2021). The part of the plume which is in the shallow aquifer discharges to the
stream along a 2 km stretch from Vestre Boulevard to Morsbøl Skolevej (Frederiksen et al.,
2019; Balbarini et al., 2020; Sonne et al., 2012).
Sonne et al., 2012, identified 6 groundwater discharge zones along a 5 km stretch of the
Grindsted stream using temperature measurements and water samples from both the
stream and the groundwater, while testing for a wide range of known contaminates in
the plume. Discharge zones 1 and 3, which are not the same as the GWDs, was found
to contain the highest overall concentrations of contaminates (Sonne et al., 2012). In
work by Andreasen and Lade, 2021, the discharge of sulfonamides to the stream was
investigated and it was found that the discharge occurred along the North bank, as the
largest concentrations were observed there, in a North bend meander in GWD5, see
Figure 8. Applying passive flux meters, slug tests, and water sampling methods to one
section of the stream, see GWD5 in Figure 8, Ottosen et al., 2020, was able to determine
seepage velocity and residence time for chlorinated ethenes in the banks of the stream.
The residence time is 0.5 to 37 days in the banks of the stream (Ottosen et al., 2020).

4.4.2
Area of investigation
Despite, many years of investigation at the stream only a few have studied barbiturates
and these investigations have not taken place in groundwater discharge area 3, GWD3. In
fact very little is known about GWD3 because the majority of studies, such as the work by
Ottosen et al., 2020, and Morsing and Petersen, 2022, have focused on chlorinated ethenes
or sulfonamides, which discharge in much larger concentrations in some of the other GWDs
(Rambøll, 2023). While Sonne et al., 2012, did test for barbiturates in transect B, see
Figure 8, which was located just downstream of GWD3, none were detected. However,
results from Rambøll, 2023, does indicate that barbiturates discharge at GWD3 as large
concentrations were found in well B616, see Figure 9.
Well B616 is located in the center of GWD3 on the North bank of the stream, see Figure 8,
and it can serve as a guiding point for the investigations to be preformed in the stream.
The general geology near the stream contains a layer of clay and lignite, which divides
the aquifer into two, resulting in different hydraulic heads found in the screens located
above and below this layer. Although, the hydraulic head does increase with depth in
B616, this separating layer is not there. A geological profile along the stream shows
the presence of either a thick lignite or clay layer throughout the profile, however the
surrounding subsurface of B616 and B617 is an exception (Rambøll, 2023). B617 is located
between GWD3 and GWD4, which is why B616 is the better choice. B616 also has more

and screen location depths in the near stream wells are why GWD3 was chosen as the

Fieldwork and methods
Descriptions of the accomplished fieldwork carried out during October and November 2023

5.1
Fieldwork strategy
The purpose of the fieldwork was to examine the inflow of barbiturates to the stream along
with the residence time, distribution, and degradation in the hyporheic zone. Furthermore,
the purpose was also to gain information regarding a previously less-explored part of the
Grindsted stream.
The fieldwork was divided into two campaigns investigating the barbiturate contamination
in two ways. The first campaign explored the spatial inflow and distribution of barbiturates
along both banks of the stream in one depth beneath the streambed, while the second
campaign explored the inflow, residence time, and degradation of barbiturates in a transect
across the stream in multiple depths. The location of the transect was somewhat determined
prior to the first campaign, but the sampling locations and depths of the second campaign
were adjusted based on the results of the first campaign. Figure 10 depicts the locations
for all samplings done in both campaigns. A list of the different methods applied can be
found in appendix B.1.

5.1.1
The first campaign
During the first campaign water sampling points in the streambed were installed along
both banks of the stream in the chosen section of the stream, GWD3. 6 points were placed
on either side with approximately 4-5 meters distance. Placement was done pairwise so
that points on the North and South side were across from each other. Well B616 was used

Fewer points were installed downstream than upstream from B616 as Sonne et al., 2012,

point was installed in the middle of the stream out from B616, which was to help adjusting
the final location of the transect to be installed in the second campaign.
5.1.2
The second campaign
In this campaign all work was conducted in a transect across the stream. The original
location of the transect was adjusted a little based on results from the first campaign, see
Figure 10. The transect consisted of 5 sampling locations one of which was on the North
bank, while the rest were in the streambed. Samples were collected in varying depth at
the different locations, see Figures B4 and B5 in appendix B.3.
Sampling locations were named; GWT0(2), GWT1(2), GWT3(2), GWT4(2), and GWT5(2),
going from North to South. Sampling in GWT0(2) was done in the initial depth of 3 m

location, which was sampled below 1 m in hope of getting indications on were the deeper
parts of the plume were flowing.
5.2
Methods applied in the field
5.2.1
Piezometers and the hydraulic potential
Piezometers were used to collect the samples. Piezometers are clear polyvinyl chloride
(PVC) with an outer diameter of 2 cm (Cremeans et al., 2020). Other types of thin pipes
can be used as well, like metal pipes.
The piezometers were installed in the streambed using a metal spear with a stopper on
top and a hammer. Piezometers were installed in all the planned locations during both
campaigns along with some additional locations in the second campaign, see Figure 10. All
piezometers installed in the first campaign were left in the stream to allow for additional
sampling from the piezometers during the second campaign, but only the ones on the
South side were still in place during the second campaign.
Piezometers were installed in a single depth during the first campaign (40 cm) and vary-
ing depths in the transect during the second campaign. Two metal piezometers where
26 of 116
==PAGE:34==
installed in 1 m beneath the streambed, these deep piezometers were not used as piezo-
meters but also as anchors for the piezometers installed in shallower depths, see Figure 11a.
The piezometers were purged right after installation using a thin plastic tube and a 140 mL
syringe. The tube was placed all the way to the bottom of the piezometer and then pulled
up a few centimeters to avoid getting too much sediment into the tube when extracting
the water. The piezometers were purged until the water became clear or the piezometer
ran dry. Piezometers which gave enough water were attached to a flow cell to measure
pH, dissolved oxygen content, electric conductivity, and temperature using a small pump
powered by a 12V battery, see where in Table B3 in appendix B.5. After purging, the
piezometer was corked and the majority of the piezometers along the North bank was tied
to pole with a string to prevent the stream from pulling them up and carrying them away
with the strong water current. The piezometers were then left for a minimum of 10 to
12 h so that the equilibrium between the groundwater and the surface water could instill.
Once the equilibrium had instilled the piezometers were used to determine the hydraulic
gradient by measuring the hydraulic potential (ΔH) and dividing it with the length of the
piezometer inserted into the stream bed (ΔL), see Figure 11b for an illustration of the
setup.

Temperature spear:
In the first campaign a temperature spear was brought along
as a mean of detecting the groundwater flow to the stream and identifying where the
optimal locations for installing piezometers might be. The spear was inserted 20 cm into
the streambed and the temperature was measured. The measured temperature in the
streambed was then compared to the temperature of the stream water. This method can
be used to determine the groundwater flow as describe by Cremeans et al., 2018. However,
the method with the temperature spear is not applicable all year around in the Grindsted
stream as observed by Ingerslev, 2023. The difference in temperature need to be at least
2 to 3 degrees in order for this to guide the placement of the piezometers during the
campaign. Yet, this was not the case during the first campaign, so the piezometers were
placed with a predetermined distance of about 4 to 5 meters and the temperature spear
was not brought and used during the second campaign.

Drive point well
A drive point well consists of a cone tip, a filter of a desired high, and iron pipes that are
about 2 cm in diameter.
The drive point well can be extended by adding pipes. Two drive point wells were installed
during the second campaign, one on the North bank (GWT0(2)) and one in the center of

long enough, the well was hammered down to the next depth, otherwise an additional pipe
piece was attached before hammering. The filter was hammered into the ground so that
the middle of the filter was at the desired depth. The filter was 10 cm high and the cone
tip was about 5 cm high. The depths can be seen in Figure B5 in appendix B.3.

Results and discussion
6.1
Hydraulic conditions
The measured hydraulic potentials from the two campaigns show the same overall tend-
encies. The hydraulic potential is higher along the North bank and it decreases towards
the South bank. Also, the hydraulic potential increases with depth, see Figure 14 and
Figure 15. Figure 14 and Figure 15 show the groundwater flowing mostly horizontal from
the North and then starting to turn upward, thus flowing vertically into the stream, with
the deeper water discharging closer to the South bank. Additionally, the equipotential lines
are closer together near the streambed indicating, that the groundwater flow is speeding
up.
Figure 14: The estimated equipotential lines under the streambed at transect 1 based on hydraulic
potential measurements indicated in black. From left to right - GWT5(1), GWT3(1), GWT1(1), and
B616.
The groundwater flow, see Figure 15, is similar to the findings of Balbarini et al., 2017,
see Figure 5. Balbarini et al., 2017, found that in a North bend meander the deeper
groundwater will discharge along the streambed from the middle to the South of the
stream, and the sharper the meander bend, the closer to the South bank the deep ground-
water will discharge. However, transect 2 was located in a less sharp meander bend than
the one depicted in Figure 5, so the flow lines in transect 2 may not turn as sharply
towards the stream, and neither discharge to the stream from the South bank of the stream.

which were found by Baggers, 2024, and variations in the groundwater flow. This is

6.2
Field-parameters
The measured field-parameters show the expected patterns, with pH and temperature
being similar to previous investigations at the Grindsted stream. Low oxygen concen-
trations (below 1 mg/L) were measured where the electric conductivity was above the
conditions in both the pristine aquifer and the stream water, see Table 4. This indicated
the presence of contaminants in both the high electrical conductivity areas in B616 and
the streambed. Screen B616-4, at the North bank, represents a pristine aquifer given its
high concentration of dissolved oxygen, its low electric conductivity, and the fact that
no barbiturates were detected in the screen. The temperature varies slightly between
the different compartments, however this is not expected to have a noticeable impact
on the system. The pH ranges from slightly acidic to neutral, see Table 4, which fits
reasonably well with the range determined by Rambøll, 2020, for the Grindsted area,
which is from 5.02 to 7.29. This means that the dissociated form of the barbiturates will
only be dominant for isopropylbarbituric acid as expected, see pKa values in Table 1.
Oxygen levels (above 1 mg/L) indicating aerobic conditions were only measured at two
locations in the subsurface, which were in screen B616-4 and GWT0a(2) i.e. at the top
screens at the North bank. Oxygen may be present in other locations despite not being

The temperature decreased a little between the two campaigns, likely because the second

6.4
Redox conditions
Nitrate reducing, manganese reducing, and iron reducing conditions have been identified
both in the plan view of the area and in transect 2. Conditions were more reduced
along the South bank than the North bank. Also, conditions became more reduced
with the depth. While aerobic conditions were found in the top screens at GWT0(2)
and B616 at the North bank, they could only be suggested in some locations along the
North bank based on high concentrations of nitrate in the plan view. Sulfate reducing
conditions could not be identified but have been suggested. Redox conditions have gen-
erally been found to be overlapping one and another in accordance with the redox hierarchy.
The concentration criteria for the different redox conditions are based on the redox hierarchy
and the criteria set by Christensen et al., 2000, which were created for a landfill plume.
The criteria set for this thesis are as follows.
• Aerobic conditions: O2 > 1 mg/L
• Aerobic conditions suggested: NO–
3 > 9 mg/L (this is concentration found in the
stream water).
• Nitrate reducing conditions: O2 < 1 mg/L, NO–
3 > 0.5 mg/L, Mn2+ < 2 mg/L, and
Fe2+ < 2 mg/L.
• Manganese reducing conditions: NO–
3 < 0.5 mg/L and Mn2+ > 2 mg/L.
• Iron reducing conditions: NO–
3 < 0.5 mg/L and Fe2+ > 2 mg/L.
• Sulfate reducing conditions suggested: O2 < 1 mg/L, NO–
3 < 0.5 mg/L, Mn2+ < 5
mg/L, Fe2+ < 150 mg/L, and CH4 < 1 mg/L (methane was not measured).
The plan view of the redox conditions is illustrated in Figure 19. Figures E8 to E11 in
subsection E.3 show the measured concentrations for the individual redox parameters. P3
was most reduced location along the North bank with manganese reducing conditions.
Similar to P3, GWT1(2) was showing more reduced conditions, however not nearly as
reduced as P3, since the nitrate concentration was above 0.5 mg/L. This could indicate
that the barbiturate plume is closer to the surface at P3 and that the top edge discharges
around P3 and GWT1(2). Measured oxygen was below the criteria for aerobic conditions
at all the locations in the streambed where it was possible to measure. However, aerobic
conditions were suggested at P1, P2, and P4 because of the high nitrate concentrations.

from the North bank to the South bank. The concentrations also increase with depth

locations on the South side contained barbiturates.

Residence time
Both the vertical flow velocity and the residence time varied across transect 2, with the
longest residence times found at GWT3(2), in the middle of the stream, and at GWT5(2),
along the South bank. The long residence time at GWT3(2) was the result of low hydraulic
conductivity, whereas it was the result of the small hydraulic gradient at GWT5(2).
The flow velocities and residence times in Figure 27 were only calculated for the vertical
movement of the groundwater, see further details in appendix E.5. The residence times
and velocities are therefore only accurate for the first meter or less into the streambed.
The hydraulic gradient was decreasing from the North bank towards the South bank, while
the hydraulic conductivities varied from location to location as measured by Baggers, 2024.
This has resulted in variations in the residence time across the stream, thus indicating the
effects of the hydraulic conductivity.

(0.41 to 3.05 d/m) found in this thesis may be reliable despite all the assumptions made,
as they fit with previous findings. The results, mainly the velocities, are however generally
lower than what was determined by Ottosen et al., 2020, which is probably because the
hydraulic conductivity they found was higher.
To summarize, the residence time fluctuates across the stream as a result of the decreasing
hydraulic gradient and the variation in hydraulic conductivity. The velocities and residence
times determined fit within previously found ranges from further downstream.
The
residence time is an important factor when it comes to natural attenuation, because even
if all other conditions are met for degradation, it only take place if the residence time is
long enough for the reactions to occur.
6.8
The fate of barbiturates
Possible degradation of barbiturates were determined at GWT3(2), in the middle of the
stream. The degradation aligns with the longest residence time. Manganese reducing
conditions may be the result of isopropylbarbituric acid degradation occurring.
The most reduced conditions were identified along the South bank, which coincided with
the highest concentrations of barbiturates. The reduced conditions could indicate that
either degradation of barbiturates were taking place at the South bank or the plume
contained compounds that caused the conditions to become more reduced. However, along
the North bank the most reduced conditions (manganese reducing conditions) did not
align with the highest concentrations of barbiturates, nevertheless they did align with the
locations where isopropylbarbituric acid were measured. This could indicate that isopro-
pylbarbituric acid degrades causing the conditions to become manganese reducing. But
while Bilić, 2023, did confirm degradation of isopropylbarbitric acid, she kept conditions
aerobic, which would have caused any manganese to precipitate, so whether or not the
degradation of isopropylbarbituric acid creates manganese reducing conditions cannot be
determined. So the possibility of the plume containing other compounds that could cause
manganese reducing conditions cannot be eliminated.
After adjusting for dilution, it appeared that some degree of degradation was taking
place in transect 2, however only at GWT3(2), see Figure 28. Degradation at GWT5(2)
can neither be suggested nor rejected, as the samples crossed flow lines, see Figure E29
in appendix E.6. Concentrations were very low and only two depths were sampled at
GWT1(2) making it difficult to assess whether or not the decrease in the ratio between
barbiturates and chloride was the result of degradation, see Figure E28 in appendix E.6.

6.8.1
Conceptual model
A conceptual model was created for transect 2 (in two figures). It suggests that the plume
discharges 2.5 m from the North bank and towards the South bank, with the highest
concentrations of barbiturates discharging near the South bank. The longest residence
time and the possible degradation is found at GWT3(2), in the middle of the stream,
where the conditions are manganese to iron reducing.
The assessed flow lines indicate vertical flow into the streambed, however their accuracy
deeper in the streambed decreases as there is a lack of data in depth at the South side of
the stream, see Figure 29. The hyporheic zone must be located in the first 30 to 40 cm
beneath the streambed, as it could not be identified by the sample depths applied in this
thesis.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the fate of barbiturates in a small section of the Grindsted stream,
aiming to understand the groundwater flow system from the North bank and into the
stream and how it influenced the distribution, residence time, and degradation of barbitur-
ates.
Literature studies revealed that all the barbiturates would stay in the water phase since
they are all very mobile and do not tend to volatilize or sorp, thus a decrease in concen-
trations would be the result of dilution and degradation. The literature was found to
be inconclusive about the extent of degradation, containing with many knowledge gaps,
contradicting findings, and experimental conditions that does not exist in the environment
at the Grindsted stream.
Groundwater and stream water are interconnected with water flow between them and
mixing together in the hyporheic zone. This connection interferes with the near stream
equipotential lines making it difficult to determine flow lines into the area of investigation.
However, it was assessed that the flow line crossing the stream at well B616, would most
likely cross the stream from the North/North-East going towards the South-West. Yet,
transect 2 was not installed in accordance with the assessed flow line, but instead crossing
the stream towards the area where high concentrations of barbiturates were measured
during the first campaign.
Spatial variations in hydraulic potential was found along either side of the stream and
across the stream, as a result of varying hydraulic conductivities and flow. The groundwater
flow increased as it neared the streambed where it discharged to the stream vertically. The
residence times found were in line within previous field studies at GWD5, which is located
further downstream from the area of investigation in this thesis. The longest residence
time was found in the middle of the stream where the hydraulic conductivity was the lowest.
Concentrations of barbiturates varied along either side of the stream, but increased from
the North bank towards the South bank. There were two patterns of contamination levels
identified in transect 2, with the highest levels of contamination located a little deeper in
pattern A than in pattern B. Very uniform ratios of barbiturates were found in the middle
of the stream and towards the South bank, i.e. at GWT3(2), GWT4(2), and GWT5c-d(2).
Primarily manganese and iron reducing conditions were found and they were located from
the middle of the stream and towards the South bank. The hyporheic zone would be located
from 30 to 40 cm beneath the streambed in transect 2, since no aerobic conditions were
identified in any of the sampled depths in the streambed. However, potential degradation
was found for isopropylbarbituric acid in the middle of the stream, i.e. at GWT3(2),
where the conditions were manganese and iron reducing and the residence time was the
longest. In a previous investigation conducted upstream of the area, at GWD2, potential
degradation of pentobarbital was also found under manganese and iron reducing conditions.
55 of 116
==PAGE:63==
A conceptual model was made for transect 2 in order to summarize all the findings. Based
on the collective findings, natural attenuation does neither seem a reliable nor effective
solution to reduce the contamination. Therefore, active intervention and remediation is
suggested in case the barbiturate concentration in the stream increases above the threshold
when the stream level eventually drops.
In conclusion, the plume discharges from the middle to the South of the stream under
manganese to iron reducing conditions, and potential degradation have been identified
under these conditions were the residence time is the longest. However, since the potential
for degradation was minor where found, and that was not where the most significant
concentrations of barbiturates were located, active intervention could therefore be needed.

Future investigations
Based on the work presented in this thesis, this section presents topics and suggestions for
possible future investigations in order to further clarify some of the findings and add new
information.
• Remeasuring the concentrations of barbiturates in the stream water at a time when
the water level is lower and collecting the samples along the South bank. This is
suggested because the stream’s water level changed a lot between the two campaigns,
and the highest concentrations of barbiturates were found along the South bank.
• Measuring the methane concentrations along with the other redox parameters. This
could make it easier to identify sulfate reducing conditions.
• Conducting degradation experiments under more naturally occurring conditions, like
Bilić, 2023 did. This would make it easier to compare the experimental results to
what may be happening in the environment.
• Investigating the flow lines across the stream and and flow paths under the South
side of the stream. This is suggested because of the uncertainties that the lack of
data point introduced.
• Identifying the exact extent of the hyporheic zone and examining its effect on the fate
of barbiturates. This would make it clearer whether or not remediation is necessary.
• Identifying where the bottom edge of the plume discharges to the stream. This could
be useful information for planing potential remediation efforts.
• Investigating how the different groups of chemicals impact the fate of one and another,
because the plume consists of multiple groups of chemicals.
• Examining the presence of phenobarbital further, as it is not known to be one of the
Grindsted barbiturates, and it was unexpectedly detected in a few samples at low
concentrations.
• Including more barbiturates in the investigations, since only half of the Grindsted
barbiturate were included in this study.

B.7
Conditions at the Grindsted stream
The fieldwork conducted at the Grindsted stream was influenced by two factors; the amount
of precipitation that fell up to, between, and during the field campaigns and the decreasing
number of daylight hours. The number of daylight hours restricted the daily time available
to preform the work required for the data collection. The precipitation influenced the
work in multiple ways, see the daily rainfall amounts in Figure B6. Obviously, it was a
bit inconvenient to work while it rained, but it also meant constantly having to cover
the equipment to avoid it not getting too wet. The rain combined with our walking back
and forth on the North bank created a lot of mud, which made moving around more of
a task and the equipment had to be cleaned when packing up to reduce the amount of
mud. Finally, the added rain impacted the water level in the stream, resulting in two
fairly different levels for the two campaigns, see the water level on Figure B6. Essentially,
it made it impossible to walk across the stream during the first campaign, while possible
for some during the second campaign.

Analysis of anions by ion chromatography – suppressed conductivity detection 
 
Mikael E. Olsson 
Satomi Matsuura 
DTU Environment 
April 2016 
Revised April 2018 
 
 
Principle 
 
Anions in aqueous samples are analyzed by ion-chromatography (IC) – suppressed conductivity detection using 
and anion-exchange column under isocratic conditions with a carbonate/bicarbonate eluents system. No pre- 
treatment of samples other than filtration are needed. Anions included in the analysis are 
 
Chloride (Cl-) 
Bromide (Br-) 
Fluoride (F-) 
Nitrate (NO3 ) 
Nitrite (NO2 ) 
Phosphate (PO4   ) 
3- 
 
Sulfate (SO4   ) 
2- 
 
 
And the quantification limit is 0.5mg/l. 
 
 
Chromatography 
 
The anions are separated on an anion-exchange column  equipped with a guard column (IonPac AS22, 4 x 250 
mm, Ionpac AS22 Guard column, 4 x 50 mm, Thermo Scientific). Separation is achieved at isocratic conditions 
with 4.5 mM bicarbonate/ 1.4 mM carbonate as eluant. The flow is kept constant at 1,2 ml/min, the column 
compartment at 20 degrees and the total analysis time is 13 min per sample (Appendix A). A 200 µl sample loop 
is used, enabling an injection volume up to 100 µl. 
 
Suppressed conductivity detection 
 
In order to remove background conductivity from eluent and sample counterions the eluent is converted tio a 
weakly dissociated form by the suppressor. The Dionex AERS 500 is an electrolytically regenerated suppressor 
enabeling it to operated continuously with a water source as a regenarent. In the present method the suppressed 
eluent is directed back into the suppressor and used as the regenerant water source making the operation of the 
suppressor effortless (auto suppression recycle mode). It should be noted however that the recycle mode is 
recommended for matrices containing little or no precipitating ions such as transition ions, calcium or magnesium. 
If any of these contaminants are present an external water mode should be used where the regenerant water source 
is added externally using a peristaltic pump. See the Dionex ERS 500 Suppressor manual. 
 
The suppressor (Dionex AERS 500 for Carbonate, 4 mm, ThermoScientific) is operated at 41 mA when using the 
chromatographic conditions above with the compartment held at 20 °C. Detection is achieved by a conductivity 
detector (ICS-5000 Conductivity detector, ThermoScientific) held at 20 °C.

E.5.1
Required residence time
Isopropylbarbituric acid and secobarbital are the only two Grindsted barbiturates for
which biodegradation have been confirmed in literature, see Table 2. A first-order rate
constant has been determined for Isopropylbarbituric acid by Bilić, 2023, ranging from
0.06 to 0.08d–1. No rate constant was found for secobarbital.
However, using the rate constant for isopropylbarbiturate and assuming first-order degrad-
ation, the required residence time can be calculated by isolating it (Th) in the formula.
C
C0
= e–k1·Th
(2)
Where C is the concentration, C0 is the original concentration, k1 is the rate constant, and
Th is the residence time. The ratio C/C0 should be at least 0.8 to show that degradation
is occurring. Using the rate constant for isopropylbarbituric acid and C/C0 = 0.8 the
required residence time has been found to range from 3.72 to 2.79 days.
