Abstract 
γδ T cells are, unlike αβ T cells, activated by mechanisms which are independent of major histocompatibility 
complex (MHC). With involvement of butyrophilin subfamily 3, member A1 (BTN3A1) human Vγ9Vδ2 T cells 
becomes activated when there is an elevated intracellular level of the phosphorylated antigens (pAgs) such 
as IPP and HMBPP. IPP occurs at a low amount in normal functioning cells, but the intracellular levels of IPP 
can be raised if a cell becomes a tumor cell. Activated Vγ9Vδ2 T cells can secrete various cytokines, which 
act on the tumor cells, and thereby take part in the immune defense against tumors. This has increased the 
interest of γδ T cells in the field of immunotherapy. Mouse γδ T cells do not display reactivity against pAgs 
and neither do they express a BTN3A1 homologue. BTN3A1 have not been predicted in pigs, but 
experiments on γδ T cells purified from tissue, showed that tissue γδ T cells produced IFN-γ, among other 
cytokines, when cultured with IPP+IL-2. In this project, I have explored the activation of porcine γδ T cells 
with different pAgs and IL-2, in order to determine pigs’ potential as a model animal for human γδ T cells. I 
found that when peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were incubated with IPP ± IL-2 or HMBPP ± 
IL-2, the γδ T cells inconsistently produced IFN-γ. In stimulations without IL-2, the IFN-γ+ γδ T cells had a 
mixed CD2-/+ expression. When IL-2 was added, the phenotype was pushed towards CD2+high IFN-γ+ γδ T 
cells. I concluded that the inconsistent production of IFN-γ by porcine γδ T cells is not enough evidence to 
confirm or deny the possibility of pigs being used as a model for human γδ T cells.

1. Introduction 
In this section the reader will be introduced to the relevant theory concerning human and porcine γδ T 
cells, before being introduced to the hypothesis. Hereafter the reader will be introduced to additional 
theory concerning methods used in this project.  
1.1. Human γδ T cells 
T lymphocytes (T cells) are subdivided into two major groups, αβ – and γδ T cells, distinguished by their T 
cell receptor (TCR) (Cheon et al., 2014)(Wu et al., 2014)(Woodland, 2015).  αβ T cells generally express 
either CD4 or CD8 linage markers and falls into the subset of helper or effector/cytotoxic T cells (Wu et al., 
2014)(Alberts et al., 2014)(Murphy, Travers, Walport, & Janeway, 2012). CD4+ T cells and CD8+ T cells 
represent the vast majority of T cells in mice and humans, and are regarded as conventional T cells by many 
(Mangan et al., 2013)(Adams, Gu, & Luoma, 2015). αβ T cells recognize antigens presented by the major 
histocompatibility complex (MHC). 
γδ T cells are a minor T cell population in peripheral blood, as they only account for 2-10%  of the total T 
cell population, in normal human peripheral blood (Map, 2004) (Devilder et al., 2006)(Sedlak, Patzl, 
Saalmüller, & Gerner, 2014), but are the major T cell population in epithelial tissues, such as the gut, lungs, 
and reproductive tract (Kazen & Adams, 2011). γδ T cells are activated by other mechanisms than αβ T 
cells, as their activation is not restricted to antigen presentation by MHC molecules. γδ T cells recognize 
unconventional antigens such as stress molecules, like MICA and MICB, and phosphorylated antigens. 
The γδ TCR is, as the αβ TCR, generated by VDJ (variable, diversity, and joining) recombination. Humans 
have three true Vδ genes, Vδ1, Vδ2, and Vδ3 (Kazen & Adams, 2011). The γδ T cells are specific located in 
the tissue based on their TCR δ chain(Lalor & Mcloughlin, 2016)(Wu et al., 2014)(Kazen & Adams, 2011).  
Vδ1 is commonly found in the dermis, mucosal epithelium, spleen, and liver. Vδ2 is associated with 
peripheral blood. Vδ2 almost exclusively pair with Vγ9 (also termed Vγ2). In peripheral blood Vγ9Vδ2 T cells 
constitute 65%–90% of all γδ T cells (Chen, 2013)(Wu et al., 2014). Vδ3 is associated with the liver and 
intestinal epithelium (Lalor & Mcloughlin, 2016).  
1.1.1. 
Functions of γδ T cells  
The role, γδ T cells play in host defense and what antigens, they respond to are less understood compared 
to αβ T cells (Adams et al., 2015)(Vantourout & Hayday, 2013).  γδ T cells display characteristics of the 
innate and the adaptive immune system (Wesch, Peters, Oberg, Pietschmann, & Kabelitz, 2011). 
γδ T cells’ main function is to take part in stress surveillance. They can respond to stress signals that 
dendritic cell and monocytes do not respond to, such as MHC class I-related proteins A and B (MICA and 
MICB). MICA and MICB are upregulated due to infections, tissue inflammations (Lalor & Mcloughlin, 2016) 
(Hayday, 2009). MICA and MICB are recognized by C-type lectin-like receptor NKG2D (Rincon-Orozco et al., 
2005) (Witherden, Ramirez, & Havran, 2014), which are associated with natural killer (NK) cell, but NKG2D 
is also found on activated CD8+ T cells, macrophages and γδ T cells (Jamieson et al., 2002). NKG2D enables a 
high frequency of γδ T cells, to without involvement of MHC, to rapidly initiate an immune respond that is 
not delayed by clonal expansion (Hayday, 2009). Activated Vγ9Vδ2 T cells can function as antigen 
presenting cells. They can take up and process antigens and present them on MHC I and MHC II(Brandes, 
Willimann, & Moser, 2005). This function links γδ T cells’ stress surveillance to adaptive immunity.

1.1.2. 
Activation of Vγ9Vδ2 T cells 
In consistency with stress surveillance, Vγ9Vδ2 T cells are activated by both exogenous and endogenous 
phosphorylated antigens (pAgs) such as produced HMBPP and IPP. This activation is important for the 
control of infections and tumors (Wang et al., 2011). Upon activation, the Vγ9Vδ2 T cells can secrete 
various cytokines and chemokines such as interferon-γ (IFN-γ) and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) which 
act on tumor cells or their microenvironment and eventually kill the tumor cells (Wu et al., 2014). The 
mechanism by which Vγ9Vδ2 T cells are activated by pAgs has been, and to some extent still is, an enigma. 
Different models for the activation have been proposed and are still subject to debate. There is, 
independent of the model, a common consensus about butyrophilin subfamily 3 member A1 (BTN3A1) 
playing an important role in the activation. 
1.1.3. 
Synthesis of IPP and HMBPP  
Isopentenyl pyrophosphate (IPP) and its isomer, dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP), are the precursor to 
all isoprenoids. Isoprenoids (also known as terpenoids) are present in all living organisms and consist of 
more than 35000 different structures (Banerjee & Sharkey, 2014), which make isoprenoids the largest class 
of natural products (Schrader & Bohlmann, 2015)(Banerjee & Sharkey, 2014). The structure and function of 
isoprenoids are highly diverse. Some isoprenoids form biological important compounds such as steroid 
hormones, cholesterol, and ubiquinones (used in e.g. respiration) (Phillips, León, Boronat, & Rodríguez-
Concepción, 2008). Other isoprenoids have high commercial interest as they can be used as flavors, 
pigments, or polymers.  
IPP is also termed isopentenyl diphosphate (IDP), which is the IUPAC-preferred term (Banerjee & Sharkey, 
2014). Even though, IPP will be used, since it is the most common used term. IPP is in nature produced by 
two distinct pathways; the mevalonate (MVA) pathway and the 2-C-methyl-D-erythritol 4-phosphate (MEP) 
pathway (also termed the non-mevalonate pathway and 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate (DXP) pathway). 
An important difference between the two pathways is the formation of intermediate (E)-4-hydroxy-3-
methyl-but-2-enyl pyrophosphate (HMBPP) in the MEP pathway.  
The MVA pathway is carried out by eukaryotes, fungi, and by the two major groups of pathogenic bacteria, 
staphylococci and streptococci (Morita, Jin, Sarikonda, & Wang, 2007) (David A Rhodes, Reith, & Trowsdale, 
2016). In the MVA pathway, IPP is synthesized from coenzyme A (CoA) with involvement of six different 
enzymes (Heuston, Begley, Gahan, & Hill, 2012). Acetyl coenzyme A (CoA) and acetoacetyl CoA form 3-
hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl CoA (HMG-CoA). HMG-CoA is reduced to mevalonic acid (mevalonate). This 
reduction is catalyzed by the highly regulated enzyme 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA reductase (HMGR). 
Mevalonate is then, in three ATP requiring reactions, converted into IPP and DMAPP (Berg, Tymoczko, 
Stryer, & Stryer, 2007). For detailed reaction and enzymes, see Figure 1.1.  
The MEP pathway is carried out by some parasites, most eubacteria - including mycobacteria and gram-
negative rods such as pseudomonas and salmonella (David A Rhodes et al., 2016)(Morita et al., 2007). 
Bacteria, such as Listeria monocytogenes and Streptomyces, use both pathways (Morita et al., 2007). The 
same goes for higher plants where the MEP pathway is localized in the chloroplast and MVA pathway in the 
cytoplasm (Banerjee & Sharkey, 2014). The MEP pathway synthesize IPP from pyruvate and glyceraldehyde 
3-phosphate in eight enzymatic steps. For detailed reaction and enzymes, see Figure 1.1.

Squalene is synthesized from IPP. Squalene is a 30 carbon atom (C30) long, organic compound that, among 
others, is used in the biosynthesis of cholesterol and steroids.  In the synthesis, two C5 molecules, one IPP, 
and one DMAPP are condensed, which forms the C10 compound, geranyl pyrophosphate. Geranyl 
pyrophosphate is then condensed with one IPP molecule to form farnesyl pyrophosphate (C15). In the last 
step squalene is formed by tail-to-tail condensation of two farnesyl pyrophosphate molecules (Berg et al., 
2007). The last step is mediated by farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase (FPPS).

1.1.4. Butyrophilins 
The butyrophilin family consists of butyrophilin (BTN) and butyrophilin-like (BTNL) proteins. BTN and BTNL 
are immunoglobulin (Ig) receptor proteins, belonging to the immunoglobulin superfamily. Their structure 
indicate a close phylogenetically relation to B7 (among others containing the costimulatory receptor B7-1 
(CD80) and B7-2 (CD86)) and the murine Skint proteins(Abeler-Dörner, Swamy, Williams, Hayday, & Bas, 
2012). Like B7 and Skint proteins, BTN and BTNL proteins (with exception of BTNL2) consist of two 
extracellular Ig domains (IgV and IgC), a transmembrane region, and a cytoplasmic tail (Arnett & Viney, 
2014). Butyrophilins (again with exception of BTNL2) differ from B7 and Skint proteins by having a cytosolic 
B30.2 (also termed PRYSPRY) domain(David A Rhodes et al., 2016), see Figure 1.2.  
The human BTN gene cluster consist of seven genes, which have been mapped to the MHC class I region of 
chromosome 6 (D. A. Rhodes, Stammers, Malcherek, Beck, & Trowsdale, 2001). BTN are divided into three 
subfamilies: BTN1A1, BTN2 and BTN3 (also termed CD277). BTN1A1 is a single gene, whereas the BTN2 and 
BTN3 subfamilies both consist of three; BTN2A1, BTN2A2, and BTN2A3; BTN3A1, BTN3A2, and 
BTN3A3(David A Rhodes et al., 2016). The human BTN family consist of 13 members: BTN1A1, BTN2A1, 
BTN2A2, BTN2A3, BTN3A1, BTN3A2, BTN3A3, BTNL2, BTNL3, BTNL8, BTNL9, BTNL10 and SKINT-like 
(SKINTL).(Arnett & Viney, 2014) The murine orthologous genomic region to the human BTN cluster is 
located on chromosome 13(David A Rhodes et al., 2016). This region contains only two genes, BTN1A1 and 
BTN2A2 orthologs, Btn1a1 and Btn2a2(David A Rhodes et al., 2016). The BTN1A1 and BTN2A2 genes are 
conserved across species. Functional orthologs of BTN1A1 and BTN2A2 have been found in all studied 
vertebrates, whereas the BTN3A gene lineage is absent from rodent species (David A Rhodes et al., 2016). 
Mice, in contrast to humans, have a large number of Skint proteins. The Skint locus on chromosome 4 
consists of 11 genes(Boyden et al., 2008). Members of the BNT family display diverse functions: BTN1A1 is 
found in mammalian milk - it is essential for the regulation of milk–lipid droplets secretion(Ogg, Weldon, 
Dobbie, Smith, & Mather, 2004); BTNL2 can induce the development of forkhead box P3 (FOXP3)-
expressing T cells with a regulatory cell phenotype and function (Arnett & Viney, 2014); expression of 
Skint1 by thymic epithelial and stromal cells drives the differentiation of Vγ5Vδ1 fetal thymocytes towards 
an IFN-γ producing phenotype(Arnett & Viney, 2014)(Vantourout & Hayday, 2013), and BTN3A1 is involved 
in activation of Vγ9Vδ2 T cells (discussed in further detail below). 
1.1.5. 
BTN3 
As mentioned, the pAg mediated activation of Vγ9Vδ2 T cells have been an enigma for a long time.  A major 
breakthrough came around 2012, when it was shown that BTN3 expressing cells (most human immune 
cells, including T cells, B cells, monocytes, dendritic cells, and natural killer (NK) cells(Arnett & Viney, 2014)) 
treated with anti-CD277 mAb, 20.1mAb, exclusively had a stimulatory effect on Vγ9Vδ2 T cells (Harly et al., 
2012). After this discovery, each of the BTN3 isoform’s ability to induce Vγ9Vδ2 T cell responds was 
examined in a knockdown study. The knockdown study was necessary because the BTN3A isoforms cannot 
be extracellular discriminated from one another by the available antibodies (Karunakaran & Herrmann, 
2014). This is due to their high similarity with only a minor angular difference in the hinge between the IgV 
and the IgC domain which separates them from one another(Gu, Nawrocka, & Adams, 2014).It was shown 
that re-expression of each BTN3 protein restored the ability to induce Vγ9Vδ2 T cell responds when 
activated with 20.1mAb. Re-expression of BTN3A1, and to less extend BTN3A3, restored the activation 
ability when knockdown cells were treated with NBP. It was only re-expression of BTN3A1 that restored the 
ability to activate Vγ9Vδ2 T cell when knockdown cells were treated with soluble pAgs (Harly et al., 2012). 
==PAGE:10==

Vavassori and colleagues (2013) have presented results showing that the extracellular domain of BTN3A1 
binds pAgs in a shallow pocket. They also report that BTN3A1 interact directly with the Vγ9Vδ2 TCR, and 
the IPP facilitates this interaction. Therefore, they suggest an activation model where BTN3A1 and pAgs 
form a complex that directly stimulates the γδ TCR (Vavassori et al., 2013). Other groups have focused on 
the intracellular B30.2 domain, which BTN3A2 lacks. They have published results ((Harly et al., 2012) 
(Sandstrom et al., 2014) (Wang & Morita, 2015)) showing that they have been unable to observe the same 
extracellular bindings as Vavassori et al. and propose an inside-out signaling model, suggesting that the 
B30.2 domain functions as an intercellular pAg sensor, sensing changes in the pAg metabolism.

1.1.6. 
The B30.2 domain 
The B30.2 domain is formed by two anti-parallel β sheets (Sheets A and B) which forms a β-sandwich. Sheet 
A forms a positively charged surface pocket that was shown to bind pAgs. With isothermal titration 
calorimetry (ITC), the interaction between the B30.2 domain and HMBPP and IPP was measured. Results 
showed clear binding of HMBPP with an affinity of 0,5µM, whereas IPP bound with 0,5mM (Sandstrom et 
al., 2014). This correlates with the 103 fold difference in potency as previously reported (Altincicek et al., 
2001). Comparison of BTN3A1 and BTN3A3’ B30.2 domain has revealed that BTN3A3’s inability to mediate 
a Vγ9Vδ2 T cell response is caused by a one amino acid difference (BTN3A1 has a histidine, and BTN3A3 an 
arginine) at position 351, which is located in the binding pocket. When switching the amino acids, BTN3A3 
acquires the ability to bind pAgs and activate Vγ9Vδ2 T cells, while BTN3A1 loses the ability(Sandstrom et 
al., 2014). It has been demonstrated that when pAgs binds to the B30.2 domain, the binding causes a 
decrease in extracellular BTN3A1 membrane mobility. This conformational change somehow leads to a 
Vγ9Vδ2 T cell response, but how this is mediated still remains to be determined.

This inside-out signaling model mediated by the B30.2 domain is supported by the fact that most pAgs are 
produced intracellular in the MVA partway or by intracellular pathogens. An intracellular way of detecting 
disruption in the MVA (disruption occurs e.g. in tumor cells) and raised levels of pAgs, caused by 
intracellular pathogens, seems more efficient than extracellular detecting. Extracellular detection would, 
due to diffusion in extracellular milieu, require higher levels of the pAgs to leave the cell before it could be 
detected. How the Vγ9Vδ2 T cells respond to the extracellular change of BTN3A1 is still unknown. The 
significant question then is whether the BTN3A complex is the direct ligand of the γδ TCR(David A Rhodes 
et al., 2016) 
1.1.7. 
Mevalonate pathway as a therapeutic target 
The MVA pathway and its downstream biosynthesis of isoprenoids are a target for therapeutic 
manipulation (Buhaescu & Izzedine, 2007).  Statins are amongst the most used pharmaceutical agents in 
the world, as they are used to prevent / treat cardiovascular disease. Statins interrupt cholesterol synthesis 
in the liver, which lowers the level of LDL cholesterol in blood, by competitively inhibition of HMGR.  In 
recent years have inhibition of FPPS with nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates (NBPs) been used in cancer 
therapy. NBPs blocks FPPS which leads to accumulation of IPP and DMAPP (see Figure 1.1). One of the most 
used NBPs are zoledronic acid which is in clinical use for osteoporosis and for tumor induced 
hypercalcaemia (Wu et al., 2014) (Fowler, Copier, Dalgleish, & Bodman-Smith, 2014). 
1.2. Porcine γδ T cells 
Pigs have a high frequency of γδ T cell compared to human and mice, even though the porcine γδ T cells 
have not been studied as extensive as human or mice γδ T cells (Sedlak et al., 2014). One problem when 
studying porcine γδ T cells is that there are, in contrast to human and mice, no monoclonal antibodies 
available to specific distinguished porcine γδ T cells based on their TCRs. This section addresses what is 
known about maturation, distribution, and characterization of porcine γδ T cells. 
1.2.1. 
Maturation 
γδ T cells originate from pluripotent precursors from the bone marrow or fetal liver, which migrate to the 
thymus where they differentiate and maturate. γδ T cells are the earliest detectable T cell population in 
pigs (Balam-May, Ramírez-Estudillo, Lazo-Vázquez, & Vega-López, 2014)(Sinkora et al., 1998). It takes less 
than 3 days from the cell enters the thymus (this happens around 40 days of gestation) until it expresses 
CD3 and γδ TCR. Unlike αβ thymocytes, the transition from CD3- γδ TCR- to CD3+ γδ TCR+ thymocytes 
happens without any transitional states of CD3low or γδ TCRlow (Sinkora, Sinkora, Reháková, & Butler, 2000). 
This lack of transitional states has made it impossible to describe the early stages of γδ thymocytes because 
there are no other specific linage markers. After the TCR expression occurs, γδ thymocytes require around 
15 days to fully differentiate and maturate (Sinkora et al., 2000).  
In the thymus, 12 different γδ thymocyte subsets have been identified. The subsets are differentiated upon 
their expressions of CD1, CD2, CD4, CD8 isomer, and CD45RC. These 12 subsets are divided into two major 
groups 1) CD4+ γδ thymocytes, which are always CD1+ CD2+CD8αβ
+ -  these cells have no counterpart in the 
periphery; and 2) CD4- γδ thymocytes which are further categorized upon their CD2 and CD8αα expression 
(Šinkora, Šinkorová, & Holtmeier, 2005). It is suggested that CD2+CD4- CD8-CD1+CD45RC- γδ thymocytes 
function as precursor to all γδ T cells found in periphery. This is based on results showing that CD2+CD4- 
CD8-CD1+CD45RC- γδ thymocytes are the only subpopulations that proliferate within the CD4- group 
(Šinkora et al., 2005). The many different γδ thymocytes subsets are therefore an expression of the

increasing CD45RC expression, while differentiating into the three major γδ T cell subpopulations found in

1.2.2. 
Distribution  
γδ T cells are found at many different anatomical sites. A comparison of their frequencies in liver, bone 
marrow, lymph nodes, spleen, thymus, tonsils, and blood in six month old pigs revealed that γδ T cells are 
most abundant in peripheral blood, spleen, and liver. At these sites, γδ T cells on average pose respectively 
31%, 31%, and 26% of the total lymphocyte population (Sedlak et al., 2014) 
The frequency of γδ T cells in pigs’ blood and spleen are age dependent. In newborn piglets γδ T cells 
account for around 20% of all peripheral blood lymphocytes (Sinkora et al., 1998). The amount of γδ T cells 
in peripheral blood increases and reaches its maximum when the pigs are 19-25 weeks old. At this point, γδ 
T cells can account for up to 60% of all peripheral blood lymphocytes (Talker et al., 2013). When pigs are 
approximately six months, the amount of peripheral γδ T cells starts to decrease until it stabilizes around 
30% of total peripheral blood lymphocytes(Yang & Parkhouse, 1996) (Sinkora et al., 1998) (Sedlak et al., 
2014)(Tang et al., 2005). The high proportion of γδ T cells in porcine peripheral blood is in line with other γδ 
T cells high species such as ruminants and birds, but in sharp contrast to γδ T cells low species as humans 
and mice. Γδ T cells only account for 2-10% of the total lymphocyte population in human and mouse blood 
(Wu et al., 2014). In humans and mice γδ T cells are mostly found in the dermis (humans), the epidermis 
(mice), the intestine, the lungs, and the uterus (Vantourout & Hayday, 2013). 
1.2.3. 
Phenotypic characterization 
Porcine γδ T cells, unlike human and murine γδ T cells, cannot be characterized and differentiated by their 
different TCR chains. As it is now, the work with porcine γδ T cells is limited by the lack of available specific 
antibodies. Phenotypic γδ T cells can be identified by different monoclonal antibodies TCR1-N4 (clone 
PGBL22A). TCR1-N4 recognizes a conserved determinant on the δ chain of the γδ TCR(Davis et al., 1998). 
Characterization of porcine γδ T cells is traditionally, based on CD2 and CD8αα expression (Sedlak et al., 
2014)(Štěpánová & Šinkora, 2012)(Yang & Parkhouse, 1996) (Sinkora et al., 1998). As previously mentioned, 
there are three γδ T cell subpopulations found in the periphery, CD2- CD8-, CD2+ CD8-, and CD2+ CD8+
α. CD2- 
CD8- γδ T cells are prevalent in peripheral blood, but also found in lower frequencies at other anatomical 
sites (Sedlak et al., 2014; Sinkora et al., 1998; Yang & Parkhouse, 1996) (Wen et al., 2012). In peripheral 
blood, CD2- CD8- γδ T cells account for almost 90% of all γδ T cells (Štěpánová & Šinkora, 2012) – this is in 
line with own results. CD2+CD8– and CD2+CD8α
+ γδ T cells preferentially home to the lymphoid tissues. In 
the spleen CD2+CD8– and CD2+CD8α
+ are the dominating γδ T cell subpopulation (Yang & Parkhouse, 1996) 
(Sinkora et al., 1998)(Sedlak et al., 2014). The CD2 CD8α distribution in the spleen is age dependent. In 
young pigs the dominating phenotype is CD2+ CD8-. As the pig gets older, the amount of CD2+ CD8-

BTN3A1 in pigs

searching for the human BTN3A1 in the pig genome, predicted proteins described as “butyrophilin

Even though BTN3A1 has not been described/predicted, porcine γδ T cells have showed IPP reactivity. In a 
study from 2011 by Wen et al., the cytokine production of in vitro cultured sort-purified γδ T cells was

1.3. Hypothesis

model for γδ T cell immune therapy.  
This study was of interest as mice cannot be used as human models for pAgs activation of γδ T cells, as mice 
lack BTN3A1 and have not shown reactivity against pAgs, therefor is another model needed. Pigs are 
interesting as model because they are a γδ T cell high species and their immune system resembles the

1.4. Flow cytometry 
Flow cytometry is a technology that, by the use of laser, allows for a multi-parameter analysis of a single 
cell in a heterogeneous cell mixture. When a cell passes through the laser beam, light is scattered and 
fluorescence are emitted. This scattered light and fluorescence are measured by the flow cytometer. Light 
scattering and fluorescence will be explained before moving on to how a flow cytometer works.  A flow 
cytometer can be divided in three systems; fluidics, optics, and electronics. The purpose of the fluidic 
system is to transport cells from a solution to a laser beam for data acquisition. The optic and the electronic 
system are responsible for collecting and quantifying data, as cells pass through the laser beam. 
It is worth mentioning that a flow cytometer is not only used to analyze cells (including bacteria, plankton 
and sperm), but also particles, such as DNA fragments, viruses, latex beads and more (Givan, 2011). In this 
section “cells” will be used as a term of any particle flowing through a flow cytometer. 
1.4.1. 
Light scattering 
Light becomes deflected and scattered in all directions, as it strikes cells passing through the laser beam.  
Light scattering is affected by the cell’s surface, membrane, nucleus, and any granular material inside the 
cell (Quirke, 1992). Light scattered at low angles (0,5-10°) in the forward direction is called forward 
scattered light (FSC). FSC is proportional to the size of the cell (Macey, 2007). Some light enters the cell, 
where it gets deflected to larger angles by the nucleus and other contents of the cell. Light scattered at 90° 
to the right angled side is called side scattered light (SSC). SSC is considered to be proportional to the 
granularity or internal complexity of the cell (Macey, 2007)(Quirke, 1992). Correlation between FSC and SSC 
enables one to differentiate between different cell types in a heterogeneous cell population.  
1.4.2. 
Fluorescence  
In addition to FSC and SSC that provides information about cells physical characteristics, some cells can also 
emit fluorescence. Fluorescence occurs when a fluorescent compound (a fluorochrome) absorbs energy, 
which causes an electron in the compound to rise from its ground state to a higher energy level (the 
electron becomes excited)(Quirke, 1992), whereupon the excited electron quickly falls back to its ground 
state by emitting the excess energy as a proton of light. Fluorochromes absorb light energy over a range of 
wavelengths, but only a specific spectrum of wavelengths will cause the fluorochrome to become excited, 
this is termed excitation spectrum. The emitted light will be of longer wavelengths than those absorbed 
(McCarthy, 2007).  Fluorochromes can be conjugated to an antibody which specific binds to an antigen. This 
allows for detection of a cells expression of antigens by detection of fluorescent. 
1.4.3. 
Fluidics 
In the fluidic system, the sample is injected into the core of a stream of sheath fluid (often PBS) (Quirke, 
1992). The sample and sheath fluid run through the flow chamber by laminar flow (Jaroszeski & Radcliff, 
1999). The laminar flow ensures the sample and the sheath fluid, in adjacent layers, to slide smoothly past 
each other instead of mixing (Young, Freedman, Ford, & Sears, 2012). There is a pressure difference 
between the sample and the sheath fluid. The sample pressure is always higher than the sheath fluid 
pressure. This pressure difference causes cells in the sample core to align in a single file fashion. This 
process is also known as hydrodynamic focusing (Jaroszeski & Radcliff, 1999)(Quirke, 1992). Hydrodynamic 
focusing ensures a uniform illumination of the cells at the laser beam. Increasing the sample pressure will 
increase the flow rate and the width of the sample core (Quirke, 1992). High flow rate allows more cells to 
pass through the laser beam at a given time - data will therefore be acquired faster,  but the uniformity of 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
the sample core will decrease (Adan, Alizada, Kiraz, Baran, & Nalbant, 2017). This can lead to cells passing 
through the laser beam at a less optimal angle (Quirke, 1992). A low flow rate ensures a largely uniform 
sample core which generally is preferred when working with samples requiring a higher resolution, such as 
DNA content analysis (Adan et al., 2017)(Quirke, 1992), whereas a high flow rate can be appropriate for 
quantitative measurements such as immunophenotyping. 
1.4.4. 
Optic 
The optic system in a flow cytometer consists of an optical bench which, in a fixed position, holds the 
illumination source and excitation and collection optics. Excitation optics consist of one or more lasers and 
lenses that shape and focus the laser beam (Adan et al., 2017) (Quirke, 1992). Collection optics consist of a 
collection lens and a system of dichroic mirrors and optical filters. Lasers are preferred as illumination 
source in flow cytometers as they provide a stable and narrow light beam of a single wavelength (Jaroszeski 
& Radcliff, 1999) (Macey, 2007). A flow cytometer can, as mentioned earlier, consist of one or more lasers 
with different wavelengths. More lasers allow for a broader use of fluorochrome which enables one to 
study even more parameters at once. In flow cytometers with more than one laser, each laser will be 
focused at its own spot at along the fluidic stream. There will therefore be more than one interrogation 
point (the interrogation point is the place where a cell crosses the laser beam, it can also be called the 
analysis- or observation point), as the cells pass through one laser beam at the time (Givan, 2011). 
When a light signal is generated by a cell passing through the interrogation point, the system of dichroic 
mirrors and optical filters directs the light to its appropriate optical detector. There are three types of 
optical filters; longpass, shortpass, and bandpass. Longpass filters transmit light longer than or equal to a 
specific wavelength, whereas shortpass filters transmit light shorter than or equal to a specific wavelength. 
Bandpass filters transmit wavelengths within a narrow range. Bandpass filters are placed in front of the 
detectors, as they ensure that only wavelengths close to the emission peak of the fluorochrome that has 
been assigned to the detector, are transmitted.  Filters have a number indicating the value of the 
wavelength, they transmit. The value assigned to shortpass filters indicates the maximum wavelength they 
transmit, whereas the value assigned to longpass filters indicated the minimum wavelength transmitted. 
Bandpass filters’ value indicate the center of the wavelength and bandwidth(Macey, 2007). For example, if 
a bandpass filter is labeled 450/50, the filter will transmit wavelengths between 425nm and 475nm(Quirke, 
1992). Longpass and shortpass filters are usually used in combination with a dichroic mirror (Macey, 2007). 
Dichroic mirrors can direct different wavelengths in different directions. For example, a 620 shortpass 
dichroic mirror will transmit wavelengths equal to or shorter than 620nm, while longer wavelengths will be 
reflected. 
1.4.5. 
Electronics 
The generated light signal is converted to an electronic signal by photodetectors. The photodetector can 
either be photodiodes (PDs) or photomultiplier tubes (PMTs)(Snow, 2004). The strong light signals 
generated by FSC are detected by PDs while the weaker light signals generated by SSC and fluorescence are 
detected by PMTs. PMTs do not convert the light signal into an electronic signal as efficient as PDs, but 
PMTs are more sensitive and therefore preferred for detecting SSC and fluorescence(Jaroszeski & Radcliff, 
1999). At the photodetector, the light is converted into a proportional number of electrons that create an 
electrical current. The electrical current is, in an amplifier, converted to a voltage pulse. The maximum 
amount of scatter and fluorescence is obtained when a cell is in the center of the laser beam.  The voltage 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
pulse is converted to a digital value by an analog to digital converter for computer processing. The digital 
signal can then be displayed as dot plots or histograms(Adan et al., 2017).   
1.4.6. 
Compensation 
Fluorochromes has a wide emission spectrum which results in spillover into other detectors than it had 
been assigned to, therefor is compensation is essential in a multicolor staining panel (Biosciences, 2009) 
(Adan et al., 2017). Compensation correctly aligns populations affected by spillover (Biosciences, 2009). By 
the use of single stained controls (this can be cells or beads)(Maecker & Trotter, 2006), can the spectral 
overlap each fluorochrome has into each detector be calculated – this is called the spectral overlap value.  
From the spectral overlap value software compensation algorithms can calculate the compensation value, 
which is applied to the data correct spillover problems post acquisition (McLaughlin et al., 2008) 
(Biosciences, 2009).

2. Materials and Methods 
2.1. Blood Samples 
Blood samples were, to the extent it was possible, collected from pigs at the National Veterinary Institute’s 
(VET) own facilities at Frederiksberg, Denmark. Blood samples were otherwise collected at the Danish 
production farm Askelygård. In the study, mini-pigs from VET’s own facilities (obtained from Ellegaard 
Göttingen Minipigs (Dalmose, Denmark)) and outbred Danish Landrace/Yorkshire/Duroc /Hampshire pigs 
from VET’s own facilities and from Askelygård, have been used. The Danish Landrace/Yorkshire/Duroc 
/Hampshire pigs were 12-13 and 22-25 weeks old, and the mini-pigs were around a year old.  
2.2. General methods 
In all washing steps cells were centrifuged for five minutes at 300g, in a SL 40R centrifuge from 
ThermoFisher. Cells were counted using NucleoCounter NC-200, a high precision automated cell counter 
from Chemometec. Pictures of cell cultures were taken with a Samsung Galaxy s6 edge phone through the 
lens of a Leica DMIL microscope. Fetal bovine serum (FBS) had been heat inactivated at 56°C for 30 mins. 
before use. In the cell culturing experiments, all work with cell, prior to harvest of the cell, were performed 
sterile. This includes the use of sterile tubes and solutions.  
2.3. Culture medium 
A complete culture medium were mixed; it consisted of RPMI-1640, 8% FBS, 20 mM N-2-hydroxyethyl-
piperazine-Nk-2-ethanesulphonic acid (HEPES), 2 mM GlutaMAX™, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 1x non-essential 
amino acids (NEAA), 100 µg/ml gentamicin, 10 µg/ml ampicillin, and 50 µM 2-mercaptoethanol (all from 
Gibco/Life Technologies). Glutamax™ was chosen instead of L-glutamine, as Glutamax™ is more stable and 
does not spontaneously degrade in aqueous solutions (Thermofisher, n.d.-b). 2-mercaptoethanol prevents 
toxic levels of oxygen radicals in the culture medium (Thermofisher, n.d.-a) and utilize cells uptake of 
cysteine (Bannai, 1992). 
2.4. Stimuli 
IPP and HMBPP (Sigma-Aldrich) were delivered as trilithium salt without data on how to dissolve the salts. 
IPP was dissolved according to data from Echelon Biosciences in H2O, 1mg/ml (Echelon Biosciences, n.d.) 
and  HMBPP was dissolved in PBS, 10mg/ml, according to data from Cayman Chemical (Cayman Chemical, 
2015). IL-2 (Novus Biologicals) was reconstituted in sterilized distilled H2O according to manufacturer’s data 
(Novus Biologicals, 2016). Just before addition to the cell cultures all stimuli were diluted in complete 
medium. 
2.5. PBMC purification 
Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated using Lymphoprep, a density gradient medium, 
in SepMate™ tubes (both from Stemcell Technologies).  SepMate™ is a tube for density gradient 
centrifugation that facilitates isolation of PBMCs. Briefly, heparinized blood was diluted 1:1 in PBS/2% FBS 
and carefully added to SepMate™ tubes containing 15m Lymphoprep and centrifuged for 20 mins at 1200g. 
The supernatants were transferred to 50 ml falcon tubes and washed twice in PBS/2% FBS. If the pellet was 
very red, the pellet was resuspended in 5ml red blood cell lysis buffer for 3 mins. and washed twice in 
PBS/2% FBS. 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
 
2.6. Harvest of cells 
Cells were harvested by pipetting the liquid up and down three times before transferring the suspension to 
labeled FACS tubes. To ensure that no cells stuck to the bottom, the wells were washed with one ml cold 
PBS/0.5%FBS, which further were transferred to the FACS tubes.   
2.7. Cell staining 
All samples were washed twice in PBS/0.5%FBS prior to antibody staining. After second wash, the 
supernatants were discarded and pellet resuspended in the remaining liquid, approximately 100µl. 
Antibodies used in the same staining step were mixed together in a master mix, together with 
PBS/0.5%FBS.  20µl master mix was added to the samples and incubated in the dark at 5°C for 30 minutes. 
The samples were hereafter washed twice in PBS/0.5%FBS. This procedure was used for both primary and 
secondary antibody staining. After extracellular staining, the cells were fixated by resuspending the pellet in 
250µl fixation/permeabilization solution (BD Biosciences) and incubated for 20 minutes in the dark at 5°C. 
Samples that were not going to be intracellular stained were washed twice in PBS/0.5%FBS, whereas 
samples that were going to be intracellular stained were washed twice in 1xPerm buffer (BD Biosciences).  
The samples were intracellular stained by resuspending the pellet, in the remaining liquid (approximately 
100µl) after last wash, adding 30µl intercellular antibody mix (intercellular antibodies were mixed with 
1xPerm buffer) and incubated in the dark at 5°C for 30 minutes. After incubation the samples were washed 
twice in 1ml 1xPerm buffer and fixated by incubating them, covered in tinfoil, for 10 minutes at room 
temperature with 250µl 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA). After incubation the cells were washed twice in 2 ml 
PBS/0.5%FBS. All fixations and intercellular stainings were performed in a fume hood. All stained samples 
were kept in the dark at 5°C for up to two days. Prior to acquisition on the flow cytometer the cells were 
resuspended in 200µl PBS/0.5%.  
2.8. Proliferation staining 
Cells were washed twice in PBS and resuspend in 2x the desired final concentration in PBS that had been 
pre-warmed to room temperature (RT).  Cell Proliferation Dye eFluor450® (eBioscience) was reconstituted 
in DMSO to stock concentration 10 mM. A 20 μM dye solution was prepared by mixing Cell Proliferation 
Dye eFluor450® 10 mM with PBS (RT). While flicking the sample tube, an equal volume of the 20 μM dye 
solution was added. The samples were incubated for 10 minutes at 37°C in the dark. The labelling was 
stopped by adding 4-5 volumes of cold RPMI-1640 with 10%FBS and incubated on ice for 5 minutes. The 
samples were washed three times in RPMI-1640 10%FBS before they were resuspended in complete media. 
2.9. Flow cytometry  
Flow cytometry data was acquired with a BD LSRFortessa ™. The data was analyzed with use of BD 
FACSDiva™ software, version 8.0.2. The flow cytometer was setup using BD Cytometer Setup and Tracking 
(CS&T) beads. The PMT baseline voltage, automatically set by CS&T, was optimized to fit the cells used in 
the setup using single stained cells. The voltage was adjusted so the unstained cells population was placed 
below 103 on the scale and stained population were placed between 103 and 105 on the scale. 5000 cells 
were acquired for compensation.

3. Results 
Two different ex vivo experiments aiming to target γδ T cells were designed. The experiments were inspired 
by the protocol “Expansion of Human Peripheral Blood γδ T Cells using Zoledronate” by Kondo et al.  (2011) 
and the ex vivo culturing of purified porcine γδ T cells with IPP and IL-2 by Wen et al. (2012). Experiments 
with human PBMC’s have shown that monocytes, which have a high endocytic activity, almost exclusively

sorted γδ T cells are clearly less sensitive to activation with PMA and Con A than unsorted. Therefore, to 
give the γδ T cell optimal growth conditions, the ex vivo experiments presented here, were performed on 
PBMC’s and not purified γδ T cell’s.

3.3. Gating strategy 
A hierarchical gating strategy was used to analyze the γδ T cells. Hierarchical gating uses parent and child 
gates, where the criteria from the parent gate is passed on to the child gate (Walker, 2017). Four gates 
were used to identify γδ T cells and two gates were used to further characterize them, see Figure 3.2. The 
first gate has been named “Live cells”. The gate discriminates between cells that were alive during the last 
extracellular staining and cells that were not. Dead cells are not of interest as they have increased non-
specific antibody binding and greater autofluorescence than live cells, which will lead to false positive 
results(Bio-Rad, n.d.). The second gate, gates on “Single cells” in a FSC-H/FSC-A dot plot. This gate is 
important because cells that pass through the integration point in clumps (or doublets) may not be 
intercepted as individual cells and can therefore impair the analysis. Single cells will typically fall along the 
diagonal when plotting voltage area versus voltage height (this can either be FSC or SSC). Clumps will fall off 
the diagonal, because the clumps have approximately the same voltage height as the single cells, but an 
increased area (Bushnell, 2016)(Bio-Rad, n.d.). The third gate is gating on lymphocytes. In the density plot 
the correlation between FSC and SSC is used to differentiate between cell populations. γδ T cells were in a 
PE-CF594/ FSC-H density plot gated as the positive population. The γδ T cells were then characterized based 
on their expression of CD2 and INF-γ production. 
 
Figure 3.2 Hierarchical gating strategy used for identification and characterization of γδ TCR cells. 
The showed figures are representative for the hierarchical gating strategy used on all samples. A) Live 
PBMCs were gated as the population negative for the LIVE/DEAD Near-IR stain (APC-Cy7). B) Single cells 
were gated in a FSC-A / FSC-H dot plot. C) Lymphocytes were gated in FSC-A / SSC-A density plot. D) γδ T 
cells were gated as the positive population in a PE-CF594/ FSC-H density plot. E) The expression of CD2

Discarded stainings 
The staining of CD3 – FITC, CD8 – PerCP-Cy5-5 and MHC II were discarded and therefore not used in the 
data handling - they are shown in Figure 3.4.  Both the staining of CD3 and CD8 looked abnormal, whereas 
the MHC II Population looked like it had been spread due to compensation. CD3 was included as a 
lymphocyte marker and can therefore be discarded without many concerns about the quality of the γδ T 
cells gating. Discarding the CD8 and MHC II stainings do not impact the results presented here, but, to a 
larger extent, it limits the ability to describe homing characteristic, activation level, and cytokine profile of 
the γδ T cells.

3.5. Expansion of porcine peripheral blood γδ T cells using zoledronic acid 
Since the results from the IPP and HMBPP stimulations were inconsistent, and some of the stainings were 
unsuccessful, it was decided to try to activate the γδ T cells and get them to proliferate by using NBP. NBPs 
were shown by Harly et al. to activate cells containing both BTNA3A1 and BTNA3A3 instead of, as with 
PAgs, only BTNA3A1 (Harly et al., 2012). Zoledronic acid was chosen because it is the active component in 
Zometa® by Novartis which is FDA (U.S. Food and Drug Administration) approved and commercially 
available (Novartis, 2016).

4. Discussion 
The goal for this present study was to investigate pigs’ potential as an animal model for γδ T cell immune 
therapy, by experimentally activating and characterizing porcine γδ T cells with pAgs. 
I found that CD2+Dim/High and CD2- porcine γδ T cells from porcine peripheral blood, to some extent, produce 
IFN-γ in response to stimulation with IPP/HMBPP plus IL-2. This finding is consistent with the findings from 
Wen et al., (Wen et al., 2012), who showed that porcine γδ T cells, purified from the spleen and 
intraepithelial lymphocytes (IEL) produced IFN-γ when stimulated with IPP plus IL-2. In addition to Wen et 
al., I showed that γδ T cells, also without addition of IL-2, produced IFN-γ in response to the stimulation 
with IPP/HMBPP. This led to the findings that IL-2 increased the percentage of γδ T cells that produce IFN-γ, 
while pushing their phenotype towards CD2+High. IL-2’s ability to increase γδ T cells’ response to pAg 
stimulation is consistent with findings from human studies (Li & Pauza, 2011). In this study IL-2 were able to 
induce IFN-γ production on its own. This is inconsistent with human findings (Li & Pauza, 2011).  
In the preformed culturing experiments, cell survival was a problem, especially in the setup where PBMCs 
were cultured with zoledronic acid. In the setup, all lymphocytes were dead after 10 days. Lack of survival 
and proliferation of ex vivo cultured porcine PBMCs have earlier been reported as a problem by our group 
(Andersen, 2016). There are many factors which can influence cell survival. One is the culture media. There 
is no commercial media, specialized for porcine cells, available. The porcine PBMCs were therefore cultured 
in a medium much inspired by culture medium optimized for murine T cell culture. In the last experiment, I 
tried to optimize the culture medium by adding autologous plasma instead of FBS. This did not have an 
obvious effect. It seems that we have not yet identified the compounds essential for porcine PBMCs growth 
and survival. After the last experiment, the possibility of optimizing the culture medium, by harvesting 
medium from a mixed lymphocyte culture and add this to the cultures, was discussed. Another factor, 
when it comes to growth and survival, is cell density. The difference in cell survival between the two 
culturing experiments could be due to difference in cell density - 1.33*106/ml vs. 1*106/ml. 
I have also demonstrated another challenge of working with pigs - the lack of directly conjugated 
antibodies. This resulted in an antibody panel with seven different staining steps. Despite an attempt to 
test the impact multiple stainings have on the quality of a staining, it is not clear what effect multiple 
staining steps have on the quality of a staining. There is a risk that the antibodies stop binding to their 
antigen. If this happens to a directly conjugated antibody, it will result in a false negative event, but if a 
secondary antibody stop binding to its primary antibody and instead binds to another primary antibody, it 
will result in false positive events. False positive events might not be as big a threat to the results presented 
here, compared to if I had tried to identify a rare cell population. 
It was tempting to conclude that the live/dead staining at day 10 in the final experiment did not work, but 
in all other setups, the staining has worked without problems, and the low number of cells also supports 
the accuracy of the staining. In relation to the missing compensation, AF647 normally has very little 
spillover into the APC-Cy7 channel, therefore the positive staining should not originate from AF647 
spillover.

5. Conclusion 
I hypothesized that porcine cells have a protein, with a mechanism similar to human BTN3A1, which makes 
it possible for γδ T cells to recognize cells with elevated intracellular level of IPP/HMBPP. I find no evidence 
to truly confirm or deny that porcine γδ T cells can be used as model for human γδ T cells, as the results 
from this are very inconsistent. 
6. Future Studies 
A lot of work lies ahead before it becomes possible to include pigs as a model organism for human γδ T 
cells. If the experiments in this project are going to be performed again, it should be considered to start out 
by clarifying whether the porcine cells take up pAgs or NBP. To do this, I would suggest incubating purified 
monocytes and purified γδ T cells with pAgs or NBP, and hereafter analyze the cells’ contents of IPP. It is 
also important to clarify the needs of porcine cells in order for them to survive and proliferate in an ex vivo 
culture.
