II 
 
Summary 
Corynebacterium glutamicum (C. glutamicum) as an industrial workhorse plays an 
important role in the field of industrial amino acids production. Nowadays, it has also 
been demonstrated to serve as a versatile and robust microbial cell factory for producing 
numerous building block chemicals and biofuels. 
 
In this thesis we reviewed the characters and applications of C. glutamicum in terms of 
its taxonomy, genetic manipulation approaches as well as various biosynthetic products.  
 
Driving by the large commercial potential of C. glutamicum, numerous studies were 
carried out to develop genetic tools for engineering C. glutamicum as an industrial cell 
factory. C. glutamicum has been used for large scale manufacture of amino acids as well 
as numerous important chemicals. Furthermore, C. glutamicum is generally regarded 
as safe (GRAS), therefore, it can be widely used into food industry. For instance, acetoin 
and diacetyl, two flavor compounds that are commonly used as food ingredients to 
enhance flavor of butter or cheese, can be produced by reconstructed C. glutamicum. 
Compared with the boosting studies of acetoin-producing strain which has achieved the 
titer of 102.45 g/L [1], few studies were carried out to reconstruct C. glutamicum as a 
diacetyl-producing factory.      
 
The α-acetolactate, the intermediate of acetoin and diacetyl, is synthesized by the 
pyruvate condensation that is catalyzed by the α-acetolactate synthase (ALS), encoded 
by gene als. In this project, we aim to construct a diacetyl-producing C. glutamicum. 
First, based on the previously constructed strain C. glutamicum JS112, which is an 
efficient platform for converting lactose in whey permeate (DWP), and other dairy 
waste such as mother liquor, into value-added products, we overexpressed the als gene 
from Enterococcus faecalis to redirect the metabolic flux for diacetyl production. 
Subsequently, we demonstrated the versatility of this platform for producing diacetyl 
from DWP. 
 
More specifically, a new metabolic pathway towards diacetyl was reconstructed by 
genetic modifications and medium optimization. The genetic modification 
manipulations included (i) redirecting carbon flux into the diacetyl biosynthetic 
pathway by overexpression of the als, (ii) breaking of the branched pathway of pyruvate 
by deleting the ppc and ldhA gene. The resultant strain was able to produce diacetyl 
with a titer of 2.57 g/L. Furthermore, to improve the titer of diacetyl, the harvested cells 
were treated with 1% (w/v) Triton X-100 for permeabilization. As a result, the 
permeabilized C. glutamicum (PCG) cells were able to achieve the titer of 7.54 g/L, 
which was 3.2-fold of that produced by unprocessed cells. Thus, this study 
demonstrates that C. glutamicum could be developed as an efficient platform for 
biosynthesis of diacetyl from DWP through metabolic engineering strategies.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
It is expected that the global market of metabolites will grow from 9.8 billion USD to 
17.1 billion USD during the period 2018-2023 at a compound annual growth rate 
(CAGR) of 11.9%.[2] Since Kinoshita et al reported that the oil-isolated bacterium 
Corynebacterium glutamicum could secret the amino acid L-glutamate in the culture 
medium of the soil-isolated bacterium(1957)[3], the popularity of exploring C. 
glutamicum as an industrial amino acid producer has been constantly growing 
(Leuchtenberger, 1996).[4] It turned out that C. glutamicum is a very versatile 
microorganism, which can be employed as a workhorse to produce several 
economically valuable amino acids such as L-glutamate, L-lysine, L-tryptophan, L-
phenylalanine, L-serine[5], L-valine, and L-isoleucine.[6] C. glutamicum is important 
for industrial biotechnology, which is widely used as microbial platform for metabolic 
engineering and as a model organism for systems biology[7]. Nowadays, it is frequently 
applied as a production host for amino acids and nucleotides [8] [9]. Moreover, 
numerous research efforts were carried out to investigate its potential as a commodity 
chemicals producer [10] such as ethanol (Hideo.K, 2006)[11] , lactic acid, succinate, 
and pantothenate[12], materials , and fuels [13].  
 
1 Taxonomy 
The glutamate-producing C. glutamicum initially was named as M. glutamicus, 
(Kinoshita et al., 1957)[16] and then with the isolation of other various microorganisms, 
they were named differently. Barksdale initially categorized the genera 
Corynebacterium, Mycobacterium, Rhodococcus and Nocardia into one group the 
‘CMN (Corynebacterium–Mycobacterium–Nocardia) group’ due to their similar 
characters based on studies of content of cell wall and lipid profile analysis[14]. In one 
classification, these genera were classified under the family of Mycobacteriaceae [15]. 
Subsequently, Liebl et al. (1991[17]) showed that these species are all included in the 
species C. glutamicum (ATCC13032, DSM 20300, and NCIB 10025) according 
modern taxonomic classification methods.  
When classifying the genera of Corynebacteria and qualification of microorganism, the 
compounds of cell wall and lipid composition are the mainly important evidence. On 
the other hand, it may be worth noting that compared with the wild-type 
Corynebacteriales genera, engineering C. glutamicum cells envelope have eveloped 
according to its properties and permeability was also increased according to its high 
effectivity of secreting all categories of metabolites and proteins. (Lee et al. 2016[18]). 
The peptidoglycan, one of the essential compounds for constituting envelope, plays an 
important role in keeping normal cell morphology and protecting cells against osmotic 
stress. Hirasawa [19] and Levefaudes et al. [20] reported that lack of m-DAP amidation 
could lead to a changed PG therefore, the lysozyme resistance dramatically decreased 
both intercellular and extracellular. These studies provided evidence that there was an 
inseparable relationship between DAP amidation and C. glutamicum tolerance to lytic 
enzymes. Besides, it was proved that the DAP amidation could significantly increase

2.1 Genome library 
In the beginning of the 1990s, many studies were carried out to sequence the individual 
genes of C. glutamicum, and several genes from amino acid biosynthetic pathway in C. 
glutamicum were cloned and analyzed (Thierbach et al.,1990; Vrljic et al., 1996[30]) 
simultaneously. These studies have provided a general understanding of metabolism of 
C. glutamicum, but the complete pathway could not be described because of the 
incomplete gene information. This bottleneck was rapidly broken when the 
determination of the size of the C. glutamicum ATCC13032 chromosome was first 
reported by using rare-cutter restriction endonucleases and PFGE (Bathe et al. 1996) 
[31]. Based on this initial analysis, the physical genome map of C. glutamicum 
ATCC13032 was constructed. Later, the complete genome sequence of C. glutamicum 
ATCC 13032 was reported by J.Ohnishi and co-workers (Ohnishi et al., 2002; Tauch et 
al., 2002a[32]), poninting the beginning of a new era for C. glutamicum in the field of 
industrial microbiology and biotechnology. Subsequently, a fully annotated C. 
glutamicum genome sequence was established, which was a big leap forward in terms 
of understanding the biology of this organism and this would boost metabolic 
engineering efforts, leading to overproduction of compounds of biotechnological 
relevance (Kalinowski J, 2003). C. glutamicum R is another widely used strain, was 
initially cultivated under condition of oxygen deprivation (Inui, 2004) [33]. The R strain 
was then engineered as a cell factory to produce ethanol and organic acids such as 
succinate, lactate, and acetate, relying on its anaerobic metabolism under oxygen-
deprived conditions[34][35]. In the last decades, the genome information as well as 
bioinformatics tools have been developed at a tremendous speed. To date, 450 type 
strains, including ATCC13032, have been deposited at American Type Culture 
Collection (ATCC; http://www.actt.org). In addition, about 54 C. glutamicum strains 
have been sequenced, including 27 complete sequences and 27 draft genomes, such as 
C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 (Kalinowski et al. 2003 [36];Tauch et al. 2002 [37]), C. 
glutamicum R (Yukawa et al. 2007 [38]), C. glutamicum MB001 (Baumgart et al. 2013 
[39]), C. glutamicum S9114 (Lv et al. 2011 [40]), and C. glutamicum ATCC14067 (Lvet

al. 2012 [41][42]). The diverse genome data provide a rich source of information, which 
in combination with expression analyses by proteome and transcriptome technologies, 
allow for a comprehensive systemic understanding of gene expression and regulatory 
networks in this organism. This information will also facilitate reconstruction of 
metabolic pathways leading to industrially important products.  
 
2.2 Substrate spectrum 
C. glutamicum can naturally utilize a wide range of carbon sources. Carbohydrates 
exclusively serve as feedstock when producing amino acid production in large scale. 
Molasses or starch hydrolysates, containing high concentrations of various sugars, are 
widely utilized as feedstocks in industrial amino acids fermentation 2003 [43] [44]. The 
main substrate of fermentation are sugars such as the monosaccharides glucose, 
fructose, mannose, arabinose and ribose or disaccharides such as sucrose and 
maltose( Alexander et al. 2013) [45].In addition, organic acid such as L-lactic acid, D-
lactic acid, acetic acid, propionic acid, pyruvic acid and gluconic acid, as well as 
alcohols such as ethanol or inositol can be metabolized by C. glutamicum. Furthermore, 
some amino acids can be utilized by C. glutamicum. e.g. L-glutamate. The costs of the 
feedstock accounts for a large proportion of the cost of the final fermentation product, 
therefore, apart from improving product yields and titers the utilization of feedstock is 
another important issue[46]. The selection of feedstock tends to be renewable, cheap 
and no food applications [47] (Buschke et al. 2013). Recently, many efforts have been 
carried out to domesticate C. glutamicum toward the ability to efficiently utilize novel 
feedstocks. A novel approach to increase productivity of already existing amino acid 
producing strains is to feed simultaneously different carbon sources. In this way, the 
metabolic limitations due to transcriptional regulation of metabolic pathways can be 
circumvented (Blombach et al. 2009 [48]; Krause et al. 2010 [49]).In contrast to most 
other microorganisms, C. glutamicum has the ability to co-metabolize different carbon 
sources efficiently (Arndt and Eikmanns 2008 [50]). 
Lately, several studies have focused on engineering C. glutamicum into being able to 
efficiently utilize sugars such as arabinose and xylose, which are derived from 
lignocellulose [51] (Dhar et al. 2016). Other feedstocks that that have been touched 
upon are lactose and galactose from delactosed whey permeate, methanol (Witthoff et 
al. 2015), glycerol (Meiswinkel et al. 2013b), myoinositol, N-acetyl-glucosamine 
(Matano et al. 2014), starch, cellulose, hemicellulose and cellobiose (Kim et al. 2014). 
 
2.3 Transport engineering of C. glutamicum: substrate uptake  
Compared with enzyme catalysis, a different mechanism was shown in microbial 
fermentation (and whole cell bio-transformations) which requires import of small 
molecular weight substrates and products across the catalytically active cells. Recently, 
there are few studies about the characterization and engineering of these transport 
processes. Transport engineering, an integral strategy for strain development, involves 
import of substrate, export of intermediates, product re-uptake and product excretion. 
Apart from direct overexpression or deletion of the target transporter genes, the 
respective transcriptional repressors or activators governing their expressions may be

4 Recent Technologies and Methods for Strain Development  
4.1 Individual Strategies of Metabolic Engineering 
 
4.1.1 Enzyme Engineering 
Enzyme engineering, an important toolbox of metabolic engineering, involves 
alleviating enzyme inhibition, changing catalytic efficiency or cofactor availability [92]. 
Almost all cellular metabolisms are controlled by allosteric regulation. Based on this 
basic mechanism, production of amnio acids, carboxylic acids, amines, and alcohols in 
C. glutamicum has been achieved by deregulation of allosteric inhibition [93]. Protein 
engineering has been applied to enhanced catalytic efficiency for γ-aminobutyrate 
(GABA) production in C. glutamicum. To broaden the activity [94] of glutamate 
decarboxylases (GAD) derived from E. coli or lactic acid bacteria, mutant GADs from 
E. coli and Lactobacillus brevis were obtained by protein engineering, and that could 
lead to the increased accumulation of GABA in C. glutamicum[95]. Protein engineering 
has also been applied in modifying the preferences of cofactors, which are involved in 
various intracellular reactions such as biosynthesis of amino acids, alcohols, etc. [96]. 
Bommareddy et al. [97] successfully generated a de novo NADPH generation pathway 
by altering the coenzyme specificity of a native NAD-dependent glyceraldehyde 3-
phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) from C. glutamicum to NADP-dependent strain 
by rational protein engineering, resulting in 60% growth of L-lysine yield compared 
with control group. As a reverse situation, the preference for NADPH over NADH of 
the Corynebacterium 2,5-diketo-D-gluconic acid (2,5-DKG) reductase was engineered, 
resulting in construction of a 2,5-DKG mutant that showed a sevenfold increase in 
activity with NADH compared to that of the wild-type enzyme [98]. This mutant 2,5-
DKG reductase may be a valuable new catalyst for use in industrial production of 
vitamin C and other NADH required metabolites. 
 
4.1.2 Genetic Engineering: Overexpression of Genes 
There are some methods to overexpress endogenous genes such as increasing the copy 
numbers, stronger and/or inducible promoters [99] and stronger ribosome binding sites 
[100] respectively. For instance, to overexpress L-lysine relative genes, the genome-
reduced L-lysine producer strain GRLys1 has two copies of genes relevant for L-lysine 
(2 copies each of lysCT311I, asd, dapA, dapB, ddh, lysA, and lysE).[101] In addition, high 
productivity of L-glutamate production was achieved by improving the promoter 
activity of glutamate dehydrogenase gene gdh.[102] Furthermore, when introducing 
four different -10 and/or -35 sequences of the gdh promoter to the upstream of the 
chromosomal gdh, glutamate dehydrogenase activity increased 4.5-fold, resulting in an 
about twofold increased L-ornithine production [103].  
 
4.1.3 Gene Regulatory Engineering 
Gene regulatory engineering is generally used to derepress a biosynthetic pathway 
[104]. For instance, to derepress L-arginine biosynthesis genes, the arg operon 
==PAGE:17==
 
11 
 
repressor gene argR was deleted [105]. In addition, to derepress L-leucine biosynthesis 
(and tryptophan biosynthesis) genes for overproduction of L-leucine (Vogt et al. 2014 
[106]) or 2-ketoisocaproate (Vogt et al.2015 [107]; Bückle-Vallant et al. 2014 [108-
109]), the ltbR gene which encodes IclR-type repressor of L-leucine and L-tryptophan 
biosynthesis genes was deleted [110]. Besides, dimethylitaconate productivity [111] 
was improved by overexpression of activator protein RanA [112]. Overexpression of 
sigma factor genes altered the transcription globally and in the case of sigA 
overexpression carotenoid was improved production (Taniguchi et al. 2017 [113]),

and Wendisch 2015[114]; Toyoda et al. 2015 [115]). On-demand control of biosynthetic 
genes was achieved using riboswitch-mediated regulation of translational initiation of 
L-lysine export gene lysE (ON mode; Zhou and Zeng 2015a [116]) and citrate synthase 
gene gltA (OFF mode; Zhou and Zeng 2015b [117]). 
 
4.2 Cloning tools for C. glutamicum 
The application of E. coli-C. glutamicum shuttle cloning vector was promoted by the 
discovery of various silent native plasmids in different C. glutamicum strains and the 
development of highly efficient DNA transfer technology. Subsequently, the genetic 
manipulation of C. glutamicum has been dramatically developed (Ozaki A et al, 1984; 
Santamaria R et al,1984 [118]). After that, more genetic tools were discovered and 
employed into the genetic studies and the strain evolution. 
Endogenous plasmids have been found in many C. glutamicum strains, which has 
significantly promoted the development of molecular modification tools in the field of 
molecular biology. These endogenous plasmids could be divided as pBL1, pCG1, 
pXZ10142, pCRY4 according to the relativity between the amino acid sequence of 
initiation factors and DNA replication machinery. The construction of various plasmid 
vectors is based on the small-size silent plasmids in C. glutamicum such as pBL1, pCG1, 
pGA1. These plasmids can be divided into the basic cloning vector, the promoter-probe 
vector for analysis of promoter strength and the expression vector for gene expression. 
 
4.2.1 Vector  
 
Cloning vector 
Cloning vector is usually constructed by introducing C. glutamicum silent plasmid 
replicon into E. coli plasmid. Cloning vector is usually selected by antibiotic resistance 
as a selective marker such as kanamycin resistance, chloramphenicol resistance and 
streptomycin and tetracycline resistance derived from the endogenous plasmid of C. 
glutamicum. However, the addition of antibiotic in industry does not only increase cost, 
but also increase the horizontal transfer possibility of antibiotic resistant genes causing 
strain pollution. To reduce this potential risk, alternative selection markers have been 
developed and widely applied in vaccine and medicine industries in the past years.[119] 
How to avoid addition of antibiotic during fermentation process is also a hot topic in 
genetic engineering. 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
12 
 
 
Promoter-probe vector 
Promoter-probe vector is mainly used to find endogenous promoter, probe exogenous 
promoter and adjust intercellular condition. Promoter-probe vector harbors various 
reporter genes encoding expression products, which are easy to be detected and 
quantified. Recently, antibiotic resistant gene is the most common reporter gene in C. 
glutamicum promoter-probe vector.[120] Antibiotic resistant gene is easy to detect, thus 
it is widely applied. However, antibiotic resistant gene is unable to probe weak promoter. 
To overcome this shortage, some reporter genes were enveloped to meet some specific 
needs, e.g. α-amylase gene Streptomyces griseus for probing of late promoter [121].  
β-galactosidase, chloramphenicol acetylamino transferase and β-glucuronidase are 
commonly used as reporter proteins in C. glutamicum. Gene gfp can also be used as a 
reporter gene to detect the strength of promoter in active cells. In addition, there are 
also some studies about other reporter genes that include: pigment melanine, 
fluorescent protein et al. [122]..    
 
Expression vector 
Metabolic engineering always requires the overexpression of key enzyme genes. 
Therefore, the expression vector plays an important role in research of gene function 
and construction of industrial strain. The promoter of E. coli-C. glutamicum shutter 
expression vector is usually from E. coli such as Plae, Ptrc, Ptac et. al. [123] These 
promoters are under the strict control of the lacIq gene resulting in strict expression of 
endogenous genes, therefore expression shutter vector is widely used in research. 
However, high cost of inducer is a large challenge for industry. Based on the studies of 
promoter PRPL and the mechanism of operon in γ bacteriophage, thermal-sensitive 
inducible vector was explored.[124] For instance, the pEC901[125] induced expression 
of target gene relying on promoter PRPL and thermal-sensitive suppressor gene cI857 
under high temperatures (above 40℃). Furthermore, pCeHEMG857 [126] was used to 
purify target proteins relying on operon OL1 bacteriophage and thermal-sensitive 
suppressor gene cI857. 
In addition to the inducible vectors, a series of constitutive expression vectors were 
constructed and applied to the biosynthesis of enzyme and biomass such as 
pTRCmob[127], pCH[128] et. al. Compared with inducible vector, constitutive 
expression vector is suitable for industrial manufacture. 
 
4.2.2 The development of replacing antibiotic selection system 
The diffusion of antibiotic resistant genes could cause numerous bacteria that are 
antibiotic resistant. Therefore, the horizontal transfer of antibiotic resistant gene 
between different strain is a large challenge in vaccine, medicine and fermentation 
industry. Besides, the increased cost and metabolic burden for the growth of bacteria is 
also a disadvantage caused by addition of antibiotic. To overcome these problems, 
alternative strategies were carried out to replace antibiotic selection system. There are 
some mature systems including post segregational killing (PSK), operator-titration 
(ORT) and auxotrophy complementation (AC). The selection of transformant and 
==PAGE:19==
 
13 
 
stability of intercellular plasmid rely on the host-plasmid balanced lethal system. 
Post Segregational Killing System  
Host bacteria harbors synthetic genes for biosynthesis of active toxin, however, plasmid 
carries gene encoding anti-toxin, therefore, host cells can survive relying on the 
expression of anti-toxin gene from plasmid and plasmid will be selected by the toxin 
synthesized by the host.  
One of the applications of PSK system is hok-sok, a natural binary system. Among this, 
gene hok encodes a lethal protein Hok, however, the mRNAs encoded by gene sok and 
hok are antisense nucleotide chains, therefore, the hybridization of these two kinds of 
antisense nucleotide chains can repress the transcription and expression of gene hok. 
As a result, the cell without plasmid will die due to the expression of Hok. However, 
the PSK system is not suitable for long-time fermentation. 
 
Operator-Repressor Titration System 
ORT system is similar with AC system to some extent. Firstly, ORT system needs to 
repress the expression of genes related to growth in host bacteria, which is achieved by 
inserting the lacIq operon in front of target gene to repress the origin promoter of target 
gene. However, it is necessary to maintain the existence of the plasmid while ensuring 
the survive of the host bacteria. Therefore, in order to enhance the expression of target 
gene, plasmid harboring unexpressed promoter lac is used to neutralize the expression 
of lacI repressor inserted in the target gene promoter. (Fig. 4.1 ) Among this system, 
unexpressed lac promoter is served as the selective marker. 
ORT system was initially used in E. coli and then employed in the fields of medicine 
and therapeutic drugs.

The AC system is based on the genetic complementation relying vector to express the

it is widely used.

et. al.. For instance, to increase the productivity of alanine in C. glutamicum, AC system

4.3 Cellular biosensors  
Cellular biosensors is a kind of sensors made by host cells that produce signals and the 
host cell can receive the signals to mediate themselves behavior or properties of 
heterologous pathways [129]. For instance, riboswitches and transcriptional regulators 
are genetically encoded biosensors which can monitor the metabolite concentration in 
host cell and mediate the expression of relative genes. Fluorescence-activated cell 
sorting (FACS) technique is developed to detect fluorescence emitted from individual 
cells and applied for high throughput screening of mutant strains. The combination of 
FACS technique with systems metabolic engineering could be a effective strategy for 
strain development [130]. 
Transcriptional regulators can be used as genetically encoded biosensor to screen 
metabolic-accumulating mutants or to direct gene expression which regulates the 
intracellular metabolic concentration [131]. Lrp[132] and LysG[133] are two amino 
acid-responsive transcriptional regulators that have been used to develop genetically-
encoded biosensors in C. glutamicum. Lrp-biosensor[134], a transcriptional activator 
which was constructed by a transcriptional fusion of the brnFE promoter with a 
fluorescence reporter gene eYFP was used for the development of strains overproducing 
branch-chain amino acids or L-methionine. In addition, the Lrp-biosensor could sense 
increased intracellular concentration of branches-chain amino acids or L-methionine 
and translate into fluorescence signal outputs, therefore, mutants could be sorted and 
screened by FACS [135]. This system has not only been applied for monitoring of live 
cell imaging and L-valine titer in engineered L-valine producing C.glutamicm [136] but 
also performed an adaptive laboratory evolution of L-valine producer [137]. LysG-
biosensor was constructed by fusion of the lysE promoter with a promoterless 
fluorescence reporter gene, thus mutant libraries could be screened wither expression

5.2.1 Glutamate  
Since the discovery of the food flavoring monosodium glutamate produced by C. 
glutamicum, this microbe has been widely used as glutamate producer, exclusively. The 
industrial glutamate producer was constructed with a high titer of more than 150g/L, 
meanwhile the yield was almost 70%.[147] It is widely known that the core technology 
of industrial glutamate production is induction treatment, including induced by biotin 
limitation or treatment with certain fatty acid ester surfactants or with β-lactam 
antibiotics such as penicillin. Recently, it is reported that the gene NCg11221 [148] is 
relative with the expression of glutamate exporter. Therefore, the secretion mechanism 
==PAGE:25==
 
19 
 
of C. glutamicum was more clear that before. Later electrophysiological studies 
indicated that the NVg11221 gene product actually possesses the activity of a 
mechanosensitive channel [149], through which glutamate is secreted under the 
mediation of passive diffusion. [150] Based on the proposal mechanism, various 
mechanism has been proposed. For instance, to alter membrane tension by inhibiting 
lipid or peptidoglycan synthesis under induction conditions, such as biotin limitation 
and penicillin treatment. This strategy enables the protein to export glutamate by 
changing the NCg11221 gene product. More recently, another mechanosensitive 
channel MscCG2 [151] was identified as the second glutamate exporter, which has 
common feature with NCg11221: both of them need to be activated by altering of 
membrane tension in order to produce glutamate. And then, Schultz et al. suggested that 
there is a close relationship between the regulator protein encoded by odhI and the 
reduction of 2-oxoglutarate dehydrogenase (ODHC) activity when producing 
glutamate.[152] Based on this study, a conventional industrial method of glutamate 
production was triggered.[153] 
Recently, Liu et al.[154] has improved the productivity of glutamate under both shake-
flask and fermenter cultivations by expressing the Vitreoscilla hemoglobin gene vgb 
under a tac promoter in wild-type C. glutamicum. Furthermore, an industrial glutamate-
producing strain XW6 was constructed which was able to efficiently produce glutamate 
with a titer of 65.2 g/L (yield of 63%) in the biotin-excessive corn stover hydrolysate 
(lignocellulose biomass) through two genetic modifications, that is, (1) engineering 
odhA ribosome-binding site to inhibit ODHC activity, and (2) truncating C-terminal 
amino acid residue of the NCg11221 to active glutamate secretion except of induction 
treatment.[155] 
 
5.2.2 Lysine 
Lysine, an essential amino acid with significant commercial value, is widely used as 
feed additive to promote the growth of animals such as swine and poultry as well as 
fish feeds [156]. Derived by the growing market for lysine, exhaustive studies were 
carried out to manipulate C. glutamicum as lysine-producer in terms of metabolism 
engineering. [157] 
Recently, to construct a lysine hyper-producer, Xu et al. [158]. reconstructed wild-type 
C. glutamicum with seventeen stages of modification to increase the carbon flux into 
the lysine-biosynthetic pathway, resulting in strain Lys5-8 with a titer of 130.82 g/L 
(163.52g/L as lysine hydrochloride). Furthermore, a low NADPH-dependent strain 
[159] was constructed by replacing NADPH-dependent dapB gene with an E. coli gene 
to switch its nucleotide-cofactor specificities from NADPH to NADH. As a result, both 
the lysine titer and conversion yield were increased from 82.6 g/L to 117.3g/L and 35% 
to 44%, respectively. 
On the other hand, derivative of wild-type C. glutamicum ZL-92 was constructed by 
adequately expressing of the bypasses for glucose uptake (lolT1, lolT2, and PpgK) and 
the native phosphoenolpyruvate-dependent sugar phosphotransferase system (PTSGlc). 
Because the improvement of glucose uptake system, ZL-92 [160] achieved a final 
lysine titer of 201.6 g/L with a conversion yield of 65% on glucose.

5.2.3 Branched-chain amino acids 
The branch-chain amino acids such as leucine, isoleucine and valine are widely used in 
various fields including cosmetics, feed additives as essential nutrition, agriculture 
chemicals, pharmaceuticals and dietary supplements. For instance, most athletes use 
them as supplements to promote the strengthen of skeletal muscles based on the 
nutraceutical effect of these essential amino acids. [166] Besides, the intermediates of 
these amino acids can also be used to produce biofuels.[167]   
The branched-chain amino acids are mainly used in pharmaceutical industry, therefore 
the highest degree of purity is necessary. From this perspective, many manipulations 
were carried out to minimize the by-production of other amino acids to a level, at which 
the final production is not necessary to purify. For instance, to decrease the cost of 
additional purification when producing valine, the alanine aminotransferase gene alaT 
[168] was deleted in valine-producing C. glutamicum. To avoid the co-production of 
valine when producing leucine, the new isoleucine-auxotrophic leucine producer was 
constructed by replacing native transaminase IlvE with endogenous AspB or 
heterologous E. coli TyrB.[169]

5.2.4 Aromatic acid production pathway: Shikimate pathway 
Sustainability, the common goal of human, not only includes the goal to replace fossil 
fuel dependency by renewable feedstocks but also aims to maintain and improve quality 
of life by developing the access to pharmaceuticals and availability of foodstuff. 
Biotechnology plays an important role in fulfilling these objectives, as the biological 
metabolism is very versatile to manipulate, giving rise to a wealth of various organic 
compounds.[180] Among of these compounds, the aromatic amino acids produced 
during the shikimate pathway has the valuable potential to replace commonly fossil 
fuel-derived aromatics as bio-derived aromatic building block. The productivity of 
certain intermediates may be increased by metabolic engineering, when redirecting 
metabolic flux from other competitive branches pathway to target products. Microbial 
bio-synthesis system has been drastically developed by this strategy for a variety of 
compounds valuable in chemical industry including aromatic and non-aromatic acids 
such as para-hydroxybenzoic acid, muconic acid and para-coumaric acid as well as 
aminobenzoic acids and aromatic α-amino acids. Furthermore, shikimate pathway is 
the exclusive pathway to produce natural products and secondary metabolites that are 
valuable in food and pharma industry.(Knaggs, 2003[181]; Kayser and Averesch, 
2015;[182] Lai et al., 2017[183]) Reconstruction of such routes has been applied in the 
manufacture of resveratrol, opioids, vanillin and reticuline.

5.2.4.1 Peculiarities of the shikimate pathway in model organisms 
The shikimate pathway, an exclusive pathway in plants and microorganisms (Kayser 
and Averesch[185],2015), is the central metabolic route leading to formation of 
tryptophan (TRP), tyrosine (TYR), phenylalanine (PHE). It starts with the condensation 
==PAGE:28==
 
22 
 
of phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) and erythrose-4-phosphate (E4P), intermediates of 
glycolysis and pentophosphate pathway, respectively, which are metabolized by a series 
of condensation and redox reactions via 3-deoxy-d-arabino-heptulosonate-7-phosphate 
(DAHP), 3-dehydroquinate (DHQ), 3-dehydroshikimate (DHS) to shikimate. From 
there the central branch point metabolite chorismite is obtained via shikimate-3-
phosphate under ATP hydrolysis and introduction of a second PEP [186]. Recently, 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae is widely used as host to reconstruct complex biosynthetic 
pathway.[187] Therefore, the shikimate pathway of yeast is paid more attention. 
Besides, C. glutamicum is also an excellent host for bioproduction of aromatic 
compounds because of its high productivity, and highly resistant properties against 
compounds. Recently, there are two major bottlenecks for synthesis of aromatic amino 
acids. The generation of DAHP is the first bottleneck, which is a stereo-specific 
condensation reaction of E4P and PEP catalysed by DAHP synthase.

5.2.4.2 Gene engineering for shikimate pathway 
The utilization of shikimate is regulated by the qsu gene cluster. Kogure et al. (2016) 
constructed a shikimate-overproducing strain by knockout the qsuB and qsuD, 
encoding 3-dehydroshikimate (DHS) dehydratase and shikimate dehydrogenase 
respectively. DHS dehydratase catalyzes the conversion of DHS into PCA, and 
shikimate dehydrogenase mainly catalyzes the conversion of shikimate into DHS as 
well as the interconversion of QA and DHQ. This strategy was also used to construct 
4-hydroxybenzoate (4-HBA)-producing strains because both 4-HBA and shikimate are 
produced via the shikimate pathway. In addition, it is also necessary to delete gene pobA, 
encoding the degradation of 4-HBA in C. glutamicum, when constructing 4-HBA-
producing strains. 
The conversion of CA into β-ketoadipate is regulated by cat gene cluster. To construct 
cis, cis-muconic acid (MA) producing strain from catechol, Becker et al. [188] 
knocked-out catB, encoding muconate cycloisomerase, to inhibit MA degradation, 
meanwhile, catA was overexpressed to increase the CatA level. As a result, the MA 
producing strain was constructed. Subsequently, Lee et al. [189] knocked-out not only 
catB but also the pcaG/H, encoding PCA dioxygenase alpha/beta subunits, to block 
PCA degradation and then introduced a codon-optimized heterologous PCA 
decarboxylase gene from Klebsiella pneumoniae to accomplish the conversion of PCA 
to CA. To construct a producing platform of hydroxybenzoic acids, Kallscheuer and 
Marienhagen [190] overexpressed qsuB and deleted the pcaFDO-pcaCBGH genes 
which are involved in PCA degradation. Furthermore, Okai et al. optimized a PCA-
producing strain C. glutamicum ATCC21420 to enable it utilize the lignin-derived 
phenolic compound ferulic acid as substrate by overexpressing vanAB which encodes 
the vanillate O-demethylase subunits A and B. 
It is important to block the upstream degradation of 3-HBA when constructing a 3-HBA 
producing strain. Based on this strategy, 3-HBA producing strain was constructed by 
deleting  genDFM - genR - genKH, belonging gen gene cluster which is involved in 
3-HBA and gentisate catabolism.[191]  
The phd gene cluster is involved in utilization of phenylpropanoids such as cinnamic 
acid, p-coumaric acid and caffeic acid produced by the extending synthetic pathway of 
aromatic amino acids phenylalanine or tyrosine. Besides, phenylpropanoids are also 
used as intermediates of polyphenol production e.g., (2S)-flavanones and stilbenes. 
Kallscheuer et al. reconstructed a strain for the production of stilbene resveratrol by 
deletion of the phd cluster to block phenylpropanoid degradation, at the same time, 
qsuB was also deleted to reduce PCA accumulation.

5.3.1 Heme 
Heme is a biological molecule containing a porphyrin structure and a ferrous ion (Fe2+) 
chelated by coordinate bonds (Kwon SJ et al.,2004)[198]. Because heme has a 
hydrophobic ring structure on the outside and a high-density electron cloud in the center, 
==PAGE:31==
 
25 
 
heme is involved in many key biological processes, such as energy generation by 
electron transfer in prokaryotic cells and mitochondria, molecular oxygen circulation 
by red blood cells in animals, detoxification of reactive oxygen species, and several 
other redox transfer reactions in cells.  
There are two nature synthesis pathways of heme. The C4 pathway begins with the 
condensation of succinyl-CoA and glycine substrates into amino levulinic acid (ALA). 
The C5 pathway begins with a glutamate substrate along with ATP and NADPH co-
substrates. The C4 pathway was found in phototrophic bacteria, yeast and mammals, 
and C5 pathway was found in most chemotrophic bacteria, cyanobacteria and algae. 
The ALA synthesizing step is the critical step for regulating the concentration of heme 
(Zhang et al.,2015) [204]. Kwon et al. [205] have reported that heme synthesized from 
recombinant E. coli was a potential iron source for mice. However, there are endotoxin 
lipopolysaccharides (LPS) in the outer membrane of E. coli, which is pathogenic. 
Therefore, the separation of LPS from heme iron should be included into the 
manufacture process, which is a cost hurdle for commercial applications.  
Alternatively, C. glutamicum could be a versatile candidate, which is nonpathogenic 
and generally recognized as safe. The potential of synthesis heme iron by C. 
glutamicum was investigated by Su-In and co-workers. They indicated the potential that 
C. glutamicum-synthesized heme iron could be supplied directly to animal feed without 
purification, which is a cost hurdle for E. coli-synthesized heme iron. To prove their 
hypothesis, the gene hemA encoding ALA synthase was introduced into C. glutamicum 
HA (KCTC 12280BP), an adaptively evolved strain tolerant to oxidative stress. To 
investigate the potential of the strain as an iron additive for swine, the strain was 
cultivated in a 5-ton fermenter followed by spray-drying the biomass with flour as an 
excipient (biomass: flour = 1:10 (w/w)). Surprisingly, the pig which was fed with the 
10% this additive along with regular feed, had 1.1kg greater increase in weight without 
diarrhea in 3 weeks as compared with that in a control pig which was fed an additive 
containing only flour. Furthermore, to verify if C. glutamicum-synthesized heme is a 
potential electron carrier, lactic acid bacteria were cultured under aerobic conditions 
with the extracted heme. The biomass of the aerobically grow Lactoccus lactis was 97% 
greater than that under fermentative growth conditions. In addition, heme was also 
proved the potential as preservative for L. lactis and L. plantarum. [206]

Chapter 2 Biosynthesis of Diacetyl and Acetoin 
1 Acetoin 
Acetoin (3-hydroxy-2-butanone) is a popular food additive with a pleasant butter-like 
flavor (Xiao et al.,2014) [207]. Since it can be obtained from biomass and possesses 
reactive carbonyl and hydroxyl moieties, the United States Department of Energy 
designed acetoin as one of 30 promising platform chemicals that were given priority 
for development and utilization in 2004 (Werpy and Petersen,2004[208]). Acetoin are 
usually added into manufacture of artificial butter to make the final product butter-
flavored, because it would otherwise be relatively tasteless. Besides, acetoin is chiral, 
and its enantiomers (3S)- and (3R)-acetoin are more valuable than the racemate as they 
are potential pharmaceutical intermediates (Xiao,2007[209]). Moreover, they can be 
used to synthesize liquid crystal materials and novel optically active α-hydroxyketone 
derivatives (Saito,1992[210]). However, the commercial production o acetoin is mostly 
based on chemical methods that is high cost, low yield and environmentally unfriendly 
(Gu et al,2014[211]). In addition, there is a safety concern about the use of 
chemosynthetic, non-natural acetoin in food and cosmetics (Xu et al,2018,[212] Li et 
al,2017). On the contrary, the biotechnological production of safe and natural acetoin 
could be more ecological and sustainable than their chemical counterparts [213]. 
Biotechnological methods, including microbial fermentation [214], whole-cell bio-
catalysis [215] and enzymatic bio-catalysis[216], have consequently gained great 
achievement over the past decades. It is reported that the Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
JHY617-SDN was able to efficiently accumulate 100.2 g/L acetoin from glucose during 
fed-batch fermentation [217]. Gluconobacter oxydans NL71 could produce 165.9 g/L 
acetoin from 2,3-butanediol in a whole-cell catalysis process [218]. This number is the 
highest acetoin titer ever reported. However, there are rare investigation about the 
production of optically pure (3R)-acetoin. Nevertheless, there are also some 
representative publications with good results. Gao et al. [219] obtained a (3R)-acetoin 
titer of 12.2 g/L by using a two-enzyme coupling system. By constructing an efficient 
E. coli whole-cell biocatalyst, Guo et al. [220][221]. obtained a high (3R)-acetoin titer 
of 86.7 g/L from optically pure (R,R)-2,3-butanediol. Subsequently, Lu et al. [222] 
obtained a (3R)-acetoin titer of 102.45 g/L, by reconstructing C. glutamicum 
ATCC13032 to produce (3R)-acetoin.

2 Diacetyl 
Diacetyl has a strong, buttery flavor and is essential at low concentrations in many dairy 
products, such as butter, butter-milk and fresh cheeses. It is also considered to be the 
most important off-flavor in the brewing process and in the wine industry. Same as 
acetoin, diacetyl is also a α-acetolactate-derived by-product of fermentation by many 
microorganisms. Diacetyl is formed from pyruvate in two steps, where the first step 
involves condensation of two molecules of pyruvate to α-acetolactate by α-acetolactate 
synthase (ALS). α-acetolactate is chemically unstable and spontaneously decomposes 
into diacetyl through non-enzymatic oxidative decarboxylation (ALOX) [223].  
Compared with boosting studies of acetoin-producing microorganism, biosynthesis of 
diacetyl has still not been fully explored. Jeroen, H, et al. reconstructed the engineering 
of Lactococcus lactis for the efficient conversion of sugar into diacetyl by combining 
NADX-oxidase overproduction and α-acetolactate decarboxylase inactivation. It is 
reported that 8% carbon flux was found to be rerouted via α-acetolactate to the 
production of diacetyl by preloading the cells with NADH-oxidase before their use as 
a cell factory [224]. Furthermore, Xiang, G, et al. investigated the potential of Candida 
glabrata, an efficient pyruvate-producing strain, as an diacetyl-producer. A diacetyl-
producing strain was constructed based on genetic modification and medium 
optimization, including (i) amplification of acetolactate synthase (ALS) to channel 
carbon flux into the diacetyl biosynthesis pathway, (ii) deletion of the ILV5 gene to 
eliminate the branched pathway of α-acetolactate and (iii) deletion of the BDH gene to 
restrict diacetyl degeneration. As a result, the reconstructed strain showed an almost 1:1 
coproduction of α-acetolactate and diacetyl (0.95 g/L). To optimize the cultivation 
medium, Fe3+ was added to enhance the conversion of α-acetolactate and resulted in a 
two-fold increase in diacetyl production (2.1 g/L). In addition, they altered the vitamin 
level in the flask culture to increase carbon flux towards diacetyl biosynthetic pathway 
and a titer of 4.7 g/L of diacetyl was achieved [225].However, in all cases the outcome 
has been either a low titer or a low yield ( Table 1.1). 
In our group, to compare diacetyl production strategies in Lactococcus lactis and design 
an efficient respiration-dependent cell factory, a genome-scale metabolic model was 
used, in which all alternative product pathways have been inactivated. As a result, a 
homo-diacetyl-producing L. lactis was constructed with high titer (95 mM or 8.2 g/L) 
by comprehensive rerouting of metabolism, activation of respiration, and finally metal 
ion catalysis [226].

Chapter 3 Metabolic engineering C. glutamicum for producing diacetyl  
Abstract  
 
Delactosed whey permeate (DWP) is a side production of whey processing, which is 
always discarded as waste, despite of its high concentration of lactose and metal irons. 
We have domesticated the industrail workhorse Corynebacterium glutamicum (C. 
glutamicum) as an efficient platform for converting DWP, and other dairy waste into 
value-added chemicals, for diacetyl production. We introduced a plasmid expressing 
acetolactate synthase, and managed to channel the metabolic flux towards diacetyl. 7.54 
g/L diacetyl was achieved using our constructed C. glutamicum. 
 
1 Introduction 
 
Diacetyl is known to be the major component in butter [227], and is widely used in 
artificial butter flavoring, margarines, or similar oil-based products to impart butter 
flavor to the final product. Diacetyl can be made by chemical or biological means. The 
chemical approach involves high temperature treatment of sugarcane bagasse under 
oxygen limiting conditions, and through a series of radical reactions diacetyl is formed. 
[228] However, there are numerous disadvantages of chemical synthesis e.g., high cost, 
pollution and low yield. On the contrary, microbial production of diacetyl is safe and 
environmental friendly.  
Trace amounts of diacetyl can also be formed in C. glutamicum. It has been found that 
the presence of metal ions can accelerate the chemical conversion of α-acetolactate into 
diacetyl, which can be used for industrial diacetyl production. [229] There are lots of 
studies to investigate potential of various prokaryotic organisms such as lactic acid 
bacteria and Enterobacter aerogens as diacetyl producers. In these bacteria, pyruvate is 
condensed to α-acetolactate under the catalyzation of acetolactate synthase. 
Subsequently, α-acetolactate is converted to diacetyl through non-enzyme 
decarboxylation (NOD) in the presence of oxygen. The intermediate α-acetolactate can 
be channeled to acetoin through acetolactate decarboxylase (ALDB). In addition, 
diacetyl can be converted to acetoin and 2,3-butanediol in a two-step reduction process 
by diacetyl reductase (DR) and butanediol dehydrogenase (BDH [230]).

Converting sugar into diacetyl is not novel, and can be done using Enterobacter 
aerogenes and lactic acid bacteria such as Lactobacillus casi (L. casi) and Lactococcus 
lactis (L. lactis), which have the ability to catalyze the condensation reaction of 
pyruvate to α-acetolactate [231]. Furthermore, yeast Candida glabrata ( C. glabrata), 
the well-established microorganism for the industrial production of pyruvate[232], has 
similar biosynthetic pathway of diacetyl, and does not produce the enzyme ALDB [233], 
which competes with diacetyl for α-acetolactate. ALS was overexpressed and pyruvate 
alternative pathway were blocked to [234] increase the carbon flux toward the 
intermediate α-acetolactate for diacetyl production. In addition, a combined 
inactivation of ALDB was found to be necessary to reroute the metabolic flux from 
acetoin to diacetyl synthesis. It is reported that L. lactis mutants with low ALDB and 
lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) activities were able to overproduce diacetyl and α-
acetolactate [235]. In another study, a UV mutant strain of E. aerogenes with decreased 
==PAGE:37==
 
31 
 
ALDB, LDH and DR activities was developed, and a maximum diacetyl titer of 1.35 
gL-1 was achieved [236]. In addition, NADH oxidase (NOX) overexpression in L.lactis 
was reported to be an efficient strategy to reroute the metabolic flux away from lactate 
production toward oxidized products [237]. Furthermore, a combination of NOX 
overexpression with ALDB inactivation in L.lactis was observed to reroute 80% of 
pyruvate produced from glucose breakdown to diacetyl synthesis via α-acetolactate 
[238]. Moreover, Gao X et.al [239]. reconstructed a strain DA-3 of yeast Candida 
glabrata (C. glabrata) by overexpressing of ALS and deletion of acetohydroxy acid 
reductoisomerase (AHAIR) and diacetyl reductase (DR), and an almost 1:1 co-
production of α-acetolactate and diacetyl (0.95 g L-1). It is proved that the deletion of 
gene ILV5 and BDH did increase diacetyl production by lowering acetoin formation 
from 0.23 to 0.11 g L-1 and 2,3-butanediol formation from 0.28 to 0.08 g L-1. Meanwhile, 
thiamine (VB1 ) and nicotinic acid (NA) were added into medium as supplement to 
achieve the diacetyl titer of 4.7 g L-1. 
Compared with blooming studies of acetoin-production using C. glutamicum, there are 
rare studies for the production of diacetyl. In order to investigate the potential of C. 
glutamicum as diacetyl synthesis platform, we reconstructed a strain JS112ALS, which 
produce α-acetolactate for diacetyl. At the same time, JS112ALS can utilize lactose as 
carbon source in DWP to produce value-added production. 
Whey and its processed varieties are overlooked feedstocks with a great potential. In 
dairy industry, large amounts of whey are produced, e.g. as a by-product of cheese 
manufacturing. There are 4-5 % lactose, 1 % protein, 0.4 % lipids and small amounts 
of minerals and vitamins in whey, thus whey is rich in nutrients. Most of the protein 
and lactose can be separated by membrane filtration, and then concentrated for 
functional food ingredients. Although DWP, a concentrated residual of the process, 
contains approximately 10% lactose and minerals, it is commonly used as feedstuff to 
livestock with little economic value. There are several efforts that have been put to 
make nutritious DWP more valuable. For instance, the lactose in DWP is converted into 
valuable chemicals through microbial fermentation. 
Lactose can be metabolized by various bacteria such as Kluyveromyces lactis, 
Kluyveromyces marxianus and Candida pseudotropocalis, which are initially able to 
metabolize lactose. It is reported that Saccharomyces cerevisiae (S. cerevisiae) can 
utilize lactose if lactose first is hydrolyzed to glucose and galactose even though it lacks 
a functional lactose metabolism. However, the fermentation process is prolonged due 
to the catabolic repression caused by concurrent release of glucose and galactose. After 
that, the reconstructed S. cerevisiae strain that can utilize lactose has been generated, 
but they still grow slowly on whey. By the comparation, Escherichia coli (E. coli) is an 
important model organism, which is able to utilize lactose naturally. Numerous efforts 
have been carried out to build recombinant E. coli for chemical production from lactose.  
C. glutamicum is a robust, generally recognized as safe (GRAS) Gram-positive 
bacterium. It has been employed as platform for amino acids and nucleic acids product 
for several decades. Recently, it also has been employed as cell factory to produce bulk 
chemicals such as succinic acid, acetoin and ethanol. For diacetyl, this was 
accomplished by overexpressing the native α-acetolactate synthase gene (als) in C. 
==PAGE:38==
 
32 
 
glabrata, and 4.7 g L-1 titer of diacetyl was achieved. C. glutamicum lacks a β-
galactosidase naturally and is unable to metabolize galactose, therefore there are few 
studies about converting lactose containing waste into value added chemicals. When 
introduced the lacY and lacZ genes from E. coli, C. glutamicum R163 could grow on 
the glucose moiety of lactose, but galactose remained unused and accumulated in the 
medium. Barrett et al. successfully employed the engineering C. glutamicum to produce 
L-lysine on a whey-based medium through heterologously expressing both the lactose 
and galactose metabolism from lactic acid bacteria. However, the plasmid-based 
expression vector was instable, and the reconstructed strain grew slowly on lactose. 
Furthermore, a derivative of C. glutamicum which is able to metabolize lactose with 
normal growth rate have done by introducing genetic elements from two lactic acid 
bacteria, namely S. thermophilus and L. lactis by using a recently developed 
chromosomal integration tool. Subsequently the growth and lactose metabolization rate 
was speed up by using adaptive laboratory evolution. 
On the basis of lactose-utilizing C. glutamicum, we have reconstructed a derivative of 
C. glutamicum which has been introduced gene als to produce α-acetolactate.

treated cells were used as PCG cells for the production of diacetyl. And then PCG cells 
were used for pyruvate catalysis.

was approved that the permeabilization could enhance the productivity of diacetyl, 
which also provide a strategy for enhancing the productivity of diacetyl and metabolic 
rate. As expected, JS112ALS is able to produce diacetyl, however, it is still unable to 
meet the industrial scale manufacture.
