Summary
With the gradual rise in world population, the standard linear production approach rely-
ing on the principle of take-make-use-dispose causes limitation of supply and environmen-
tal issues. A circular plan of the production procedure aims at reducing unrecoverable
waste and to maintain the material quality. A central philosophy with opposing ideas
to a traditional linear economy is a ce, which supports the prolonged stay of materials
in re-circulation. The planned actions of European Commission in terms of CE in every
step of the value chain identify four key areas: production, consumption, waste manage-
ment as well as the market for secondary raw materials. Thereby, the methodological
procedure construction of how companies and dedicated industries should prioritise their
waste streams in terms of producing secondary materials, is of paramount importance.
The purpose of this study was to provide an environmental assessment method for
identifying and ranking waste materials by examining their prioritisation based on their
appropriateness as secondary materials and their circularity potential. The assessment
methodology of these resource flows combines the life cycle thinking and Circular Econ-
omy (CE) concepts and covers related requirements to availability of waste materials,
their quality and their potential environmental impact.
The main objectives of the
study are to: 1) to perceive the relations which may exist between CE and alternative
existing sustainability concepts, 2) to determine the tools required to assess the rele-
vance and environmental performance of waste materials within the scope of CE, 3) to
propose relevant indicators to assess the environmental performance of waste materi-
als in recycling loops and validate through a case study, 4) to perform an Life Cycle
Assessment (LCA) aiming at evaluating the environmental load of waste materials in
recycling loops, the potential substitution of primary materials from secondary materials
by using relevant impact category indicators, 5) to measure and validate the ”circular-
ity” potential of waste materials using relevant indicators through a case study, 6) to
compare the overall ranking performance of waste materials provided by the different
tools used to access the recycling scenarios and identify reasons for differences in results.
The findings of this study will contribute to a larger, ongoing project titled ’Waste
to Value’, conducted by COWI A/S in collaboration with the Technical University of
Denmark, the Norwegian University of Science and Technology, and the IT Company
Oracle. The model has been constructed in Excel, and assesses the waste stream based
on seven parameters comprising four indicators to support the advancement of envi-
ronmental sustainability. A case study on four recyclable waste fractions mis-placed

in theSmall Combustible Waste (SCW) at eight Danish Recycling Centres (RCs) was
chosen. The test of the RP model resulted in scores of 33.27, 40.49, 71.18, and 25.45
for textiles, plastics, waste electrical and electronic equipment (WEEE) and wood waste
respectively. Thus, three waste fractions were assessed as being of moderate priority,
while the WEEE stream had significant priority according to the model. The WEEE
fraction scored the highest RP in all of the four indicators. A comparison with one of the
first models quantitatively assessing waste in terms of its recyclability was done. The
comparative assessment focused on the prioritisation of the waste fractions at Roskilde
Festival (Sund and Pedersen, 2019). Although the method used in the bachelor thesis
project focused on different materials, the waste fraction of car batteries was compared
with that of WEEE and both had a significant priority of 62 and 71.18 respectively,
while the PVC-made air beds fraction resulted in moderate priority with 37, while the
plastic waste comprising also of the same polymer accounted for 48.56. Additionally,
for the individual parameters, there was a strong correlation in three out of five indica-
tors for plastic and air beds and two out of five results for WEEE. Thereby, an overall
agreement in the results between the two methods given the relative uncertainty of the
methods.
Moreover, the life cycle assessment of the four aforementioned waste fractions has been
conducted, in order to test the robustness of the model and to validate the results of the
prioritization provided by the methodology. The results indicated that SCW retains the
considerable potential for enhanced management. With the exception of plastic waste,
all waste fractions in SCW provided CC savings in recycling scenarios. In particular,
textile waste and WEEE provided the largest environmental savings (-4480 and -231 kg
CO2-eq/tonne of recyclable waste). This outcome highlights the important contribution
that effective source-segregation of household waste materials may offer for recycling and
the associated climate change impacts of waste management.
A third approach measuring circularity based on existing formulas proposed by Ellen
MacArthur Foundation has been tested to examine the circularity potential of the waste
products formed by the four waste streams in the context of a circular economy.The de-
rived circularity scores were 0.456, 0.452, 0.451, and 0.359 for textiles, WEEE, plastics
and wood waste respectively, with 0.1 being the completely linear and 1 being the fully
circular option. The results were uniform for the first three fractions, thus no waste
fractions were assessed as being of distinctive priority and more efforts could be done
for the waste products to be regarded as circular.
The overall performance ranking recommendation by combing the three methods with
an equal and subjective weighting principle showed that textiles, WEEE, plastics and
wood waste should be priotised in a descending order.The final recommendations for
on-going and future ”Waste-to-Value” studies are 1) to use the ILCD method’s environ-
mental impact results as data inputs for the RP methodology and also 2) to improve
and validate an indicator capable to address the short supply horizon of minerals, fossil
carriers and the depletion of biotic natural resources with the use of relevant case studies.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Nowadays, Sustainable Development (SD) is a crucial subject in politics, society and
corporations. To accomplish the transition towards SD, the involvement of those three
parties is needed (UN, 2015).
However, the transition towards SD is difficult for a
minimum of two reasons. First, the global population is increasing: in 2030 the world
population is expected to be 8.55 billion individuals(UN, 2017). This suggests an increase
of over one billion people in eleven years (Statista, 2017). Second, overall, maintaining
economic development has priority across all countries (UN, 2015). Economic develop-
ment is measured in terms of change in Gross Domestic Product (GDP), that does not
take the non-financial negative impacts on the surroundings or society under considera-
tion (Velenturf and Jopson, 2019). Combining these two reasons makes one expect that
the demand for products will increase, whereas negative social group and environmental
impacts stay unaccounted for. Therefore, it is questionable whether or not these chal-
lenges can or will not oppose the transition towards SD (Kopnina, 2015). Nonetheless,
the involvement of corporations towards environmental SD is critical for a minimum of
three reasons. First, the standard linear production method depends on the principle of
take-make-use-dispose and results in waste, pollution and depletion of natural resources
(Saidani et al., 2019). Second, natural resources are scarce which suggests that their
accessibility is restricted. In 2030, it is expected that the demand for natural resources
to be larger than the quantity of natural resources two earths would be able to give
(OECD, 2012). Corporations generally face the limitation of quantity as their inputs
consist foremost of natural resources. Third, the utilization of natural resources in pro-
duction harms the surroundings in two ways: the reduction of quality of the resources
results in pollution and therefore the resources are taken off the environment (Murray
et al., 2017).
Waste and the depletion of natural resources cause numerous threats for the environ-
ment such as the Climate Change. The Climate Change causes global warming, leading
to a rising water level, a threat for the biodiversity and an increasing risk of natural
disasters (UN, 1987). Thus, corporations are forced by the limitation of availability
and the restricted access of natural resources and environmental concerns to alter their
linear production approach. A circular plan of the production procedure operates from
the intention to reduce waste and to preserve the material quality. Therefore, a philos-
ophy that can act as an alternative to a traditional linear economy in which we keep
resources in circuit for a prolonged period of time, extracting their maximum value from
their use and then recover products and materials at their end of life, is the Circular

Economy (CE). The EU action strategy for the CE (EU, 2015) emphasizes that the
value of commodities, materials and resources ought to be maintained within the econ-
omy for as long as attainable, and also the generation of waste minimised. The planned
actions support the CE in every step of the value chain and four key areas are identified:
production, consumption, waste management as well as the market for secondary raw
materials (EU, 2015). The inclusion of a life cycle approach is deemed necessary to as-
sess the potential of CE strategies, accounting for both the product and the system level.
1.1
Literature Review
Different tools exist for mapping resources and material flows at the regional level (Mate-
rial Flow Analysis (MFA). In the literature, a wide range of mapping of regional resource
flows for European cities and at national and European level is elaborated. Similarly,
established methods exist for assessing environmental aspects of resource management
and for handling data uncertainties, i.e MFA, Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). Despite the
fact that, conducted analyses of specific resource flows exist, for instance Phosphorus in
Denmark (Klinglmair et al., 2017), the methodological basis for assessing resource flows,
as well as environmental effects are to a certain extent already present. Nonetheless,
it is lacking a unification of the individual methods into an overall analysis model that
enables a concise and systematic prioritization based on available data.
Taking environmental assessment models into consideration, there the focus is mostly
on materials that have special environmental related issues. For instance, some waste
flows have better quality for recycling than others. Hence, in order to provide a full
understanding and a full evaluation of these recycling solutions, DTU Environment uses
life cycle assessment for the last 15 years and has developed life cycle assessment model,
EASETECH (Clavreul et al., 2014), which is a dedicated waste-LCA software. It is
appropriate for designing waste management processes, handling various technologies
coupled with the associated sensitivity of their inputs and outputs. Additionally, it com-
putes the environmental load of the constructed system and aid decision-making. By
setting up the exact same real-life process it is possible to access information about the
waste qualities,follow the substances throughout the system (Astrup, 2016).
At the same time, there are few models that have focus on the material flows that
are particularly valuable or have critical supply sources horizons (Graedel et al., 2012).
However, as supported by (Walker et al., 2018), when addressing the challenges of reduc-

that all of these will lead to a net environmental benefit. Therefore, it was proposed
that an environmentally-based assessment of strategies should be conducted alongside
with the tools and methods that the LCA community has developed to validate the
environmental certifications of new products and business models. On the basis of their

In the ”Circularity Indicators” project, the Ellen MacArthur Foundation and Granta
design have developed a technique for capturing the deviation from linear form of prod-
ucts and firms in a quantitative way facts and numbers (Ellen-MacArthur-Foundation,
2015). Material Circularity Indicator (MCI) represents the degree of the material flow’s
circulatory on a scale of zero to one. The higher the score, the more recyclable or circular
the product.
The MCI can act as a decision-making tool for product designers and consumers to
decide materials, use it for communication of information or to measure firms. The hy-
pothesis here is that the circularity of a company is formed from the individual material
circularities of its products. For MCI to induce a result equal to 1, all the raw materials
used would need to originate from reused parts or recycled materials, with none loss in
utilisation (100% recycling efficiency). Any waste generated throughout the assembly
and end-of-life of the product would also have to be reused or recycled with none loss
(”zero waste”). A product with fully linear material flows, where all raw materials are
derived from virgin material and no waste is reused or recycled in any respect, is valued
at 0.1. This methodology received growing interest recently from the scientific commu-
nity. For instance, (Niero and Kalbar, 2019) proposed that coupling material circularity
indicators with LCA indicators via MCDA can advance the assessment of CE strategies
at the product level and validated this proposal by testing alternatives cases for beer
packaging sector.
A group of researchers (Lonca et al., 2018) compared the MCI methodology to LCA
and mentioned that LCA provides a broader scope than the MCI, it still reflects a
relative contribution to environmental sustainability while the MCI focuses on micro-
scale improvements in resource use efficiency. The comparison of CE indicators with
LCA results may indeed reveals potential trade-offs e.g. between the goals of resources
circularity and reducing environmental burden.
Finally, (Figge et al., 2018) encour-

2.2.1
From waste to resources: boosting the market for
secondary raw materials
Recycling is an essential requirement for a CE as the sources and the materials can be
recirculated, returned to the markets and reused. Recycling assures that a disposable
waste does not lose its value by becoming a beneficial source of other products. To
understand the abilities of these subsidiary or secondary raw materials, it is important
to eliminate the current obstacles of their trading, enhance the waste treatment proce-
dures and ensure great quality requirements. All these will allow the industrial sector
to take advantage of the secondary raw materials and assure their safe provision. Dis-
mantlement and recycling are the options of a material at the end of it’s life for CE,
as through these processes, the re-usability is ensured. Re-inserting these materials at
the starting point of production, then the environmental impact and the manufacture
cost is minimized. Waste control methods affect the properties and the quantities of the
materials and so measures to strengthen these operations are important. Nonetheless,
other separate impediments restrain the enhancement of this crucial sector of economy
and market and the distribution of the materials, so the Commission is investigating the
possible barriers more thoroughly (EU, 2015).
In the case secondary materials are utilized in an industrial process, the production
faces a vital problem related to their quality. Considering the lack of EU-wide stan-
dards (EU, 2015), it may be hard to determine the levels of down-cycling or eligibility
for high-grade recycling such as for plastics. By developing these quality standard, not
only confidence in secondary raw materials and recirculated materials can be built, but
also a broad market will be supported. Therefore, the Commission along with the inter-
ested industries will initiate the work for the EU-wide quality standards for secondary
raw materials where it is required. Furthermore, through these legislative proposals, is
easier to ascertain whether or not a secondary raw material is an actual waste by law or
it can be re-used, by elucidating current regulations on ’end-of-waste’ (EU, 2015).
Additionally, a vital matter for the advancement of a secondary raw material based
market is the connection with the regulations on chemicals. A great amount of chemical
compounds are classified as threats for the health and the environment, because of their
toxicity and become topic to restrictions or prohibitions. The life span of these harmful
substances can be wide and therefore it is possible to trace them is recycling streams of

2.2.2.1
Plastics
In terms of CE, the recycling of plastic is vital. Approximately, 25% of plastic heads
to recycling and about 50% reaches landfills (EU, 2015), while vast amounts of plastic
reaches in the ocean as microplastics. The importance of preventing plastic accumula-
tion in the ocean is depicted at the 2030 Sustainable Development Goals where the aim
of preventing marine pollution is included. Wiser collection and affirmation plans for
collectors are essential to avert recyclable plastic from landfills and incineration. In plas-
tics though many hazardous auxiliaries create many challenges as they demand special
and innovative equipment for the recycling process. These innovations that may lead to
the enhancement of recyclability of plastics will advance CE.
2.2.2.2
Critical raw materials
European industry is highly dependant on raw materials for the production of commodi-
ties as their provision affects the European economy (Graedel et al., 2012). In many
situations their disposal influences several environmental impacts. These critical raw
materials are usually found on the production of electronic devices, such as cobalt, pal-
ladium, gold silver. The recycling of these materials is limited and so their economic
possibilities are minimised. The recovery of crude materials is essential but poses many
difficulties that must be discussed for the transition into CE. Currently, European leg-
islation promotes the recycling of electronic devices but solely high-quality recycling is
able to assure the recovery of critical crude materials. Sorting, disassembling and finally
recycling though these materials is a major challenge.
2.2.2.3
Biomass and biomaterial-based products
Biomaterials such as wood, crops or fibres can be utilised for a variety of commodities
for the construction sector, furniture, paper, food, textiles and chemical production or
generation of energy from biofuels. Bioeconomy supports CE by granting an alternative
to fossil-based products and energy. Other benefits of bio-materials are connected to
their renewability and biodegradability. The attention though on sustainable sourcing
though is important as their depletion must be avoided. Biomaterials like wood are
versatile and their reusability and recycling is highly possible. The Commission will
encourage adequate utilisation of bio-based resources through various steps along with
direction and allocation of best procedures on the successive utilization of biomass and
assistance for innovation in the bioeconomy. The reviewed legislative framework on waste
has come into effect in July 2018 (EU, 2018b). It establishes clear goals for minimization
and prevention of waste and sets an serious plausible long-term path for waste treatment
and recycling.

2.2.3
Circular Economy strategies
• Cradle-to-cradle thinking (Cradle2Cradle) is the reverse of use-and-use thinking. A
vital basis of CE is the notion of searching at the life cycle of an entire product. The
idea is to optimise a product in order maximise its performance and value-chains
across its durability. Some industries are currently oriented towards this thinking,
avoiding the cradle-to-grave mentality which supports the discarding mentality.
• Industrial symbiosis is the foundation for building a network of collaboration be-
tween companies. In this network waste products of a company may be used as
crude materials for another company. Kalundborg Symbiose in Denmark is a case
of such innovative collaboration, where nine pubic and private companies partner
in order to trade and exchange resources striving towards CE.
2.2.4
Sustainable Development (SD) goals can be
achieved through Circular Economy
The SD goals outline the objectives in order to achieve a sustainable society.
In a
society based on CE, industries and companies are able to operate with those SD goals.
For instance, by utilizing and recycling the waste water of other companies then the
Objective 6 - clean water and sanitation of the SD goals is achieved.
Solar cells or
the generation of excess heat from industry can be used to self-supply energy thus
achieving Objective 7 - sustainable energy. Furthermore the Objective 8- decent jobs and
economic growth is accomplished by employment generation in a service-based economy.

2.4 Life cycle thinking and Circular Economy

2.4
Life cycle thinking and Circular Economy
In order to comprehend the life cycle methods, it is important to consider that all
commodities and services are linked with a value chain. This value chain consists of
activities and decisions that aim to improve the competitive advantage of a product by
taking into account that every action has consequences. Through life cycle reasoning
reckons the materials, fuels, energy and water used and the possible derivatives such as
emissions and waste generated by manufacturing, utilizing and/or discarding a certain
product(Pajula et al., 2017). The starting point of life cycle is the extraction of crude
material and its conversion and is followed by the production and transportation to the
consumer for the utility phase. The end-of-life stage is the last part of the loop and
involves the possible recirculation of the product via recycling of the material, the gen-
eration of energy from incineration or other processes or the disposal. In all the stages,
every action brings consequences. For instance the provision of material is linked to the
energy demand and the emitted gases. The base of the life cycle mentality is prevent
the change of environmental load (Hauschild et al., 2018). That is translated into the
fact if by reducing the environmental impact potential other impacts will remain the
same levels. Usually the value chains are universal and every impact is affected on an
international level. For instance, energy conservation may lead in an elevated amount of
material input at the production stage. Life cycle mentality focuses on preventing these
kind of impacts (Pajula et al., 2017).
The CE depends on the sustainable utilisation of resources. The “3R” principles—
reduce, reuse, and recycle materials and energy express the alternative options to perform
CE ((Yuan et al., 2006)). Common economic systems though depend on the concept
“take-make-dispose” production model (Sitra, 2015). Commodities and production de-
pend solely on the preliminary utility of the product and recycling is isolated from
production. The reduction of waste is achieved in CE by either implementing an effi-
cient utility of consumed materials or develop products that are recyclable and reusable.
The aim is to retain the optimum promising value, connected to generation and the
utilised materials, in the concept of CE (Sitra, 2015).
The schematic illustration bellow (Figure 2.2) depicts in a simple way the basic
phases of a CE model. The phases are interconnected, as materials can be utilised in
a succession way, for instance when companies trade secondary products, products are
redeveloped, or customers prefer product-service systems. The ideal operation of the
system is achieved by decreasing the amount of virgin resources (EU, 2014). Reusability
and remanufacturing are the best cases of the CE as they preserve much of the energy
utilised in the initial production, like in extraction of resources and further handling.

The five steps of life cycle impact assessment (stage 3) are listed in Figure 2.5 and
are described bellow (ISO, 2018a):
• Classification allocates the LCI findings to one or more impact categories, for
example CO2 affects global warming while SO2 affects population’s health and
acidification.
• Characterisation alters the LCI findings into common units thus accumulates the
outcome into the same impact category. For instance, CH4 and N2O are reformed
into CO2 equivalents with emission-specific factors (a global warming potential of
1 kg CH4 is even to 25 kg CO2 eq.) and announced as CO2 equivalents.
• Normalisation estimates the magnitude of category indicator outcomes compared
to reference data. The aim is to comprehend the relative magnitude for every
indicator outcome of the researched system. Normalisation is not obligatory.
• Grouping states that the impact categories are gathered and rated. The rating
(high, medium and low priority) of the impact categories in a provided order
depends on value-choices and the outcomes may differentiate depending on the
researcher. Grouping is also an elective step.
• Weighting alters and probably accumulates indicator findings throughout impact
categories by utilizing numerical factors depended on value-choices. Sensitivity
analysis is applied to examine the aftermath of value-choices. Weighting is also an
elective step.
The term “the midpoint methods” is used to describe the methods in which characteri-
sation factors are only examined while normalisation or weighting factors are excluded
from the research (Pajula et al., 2017). For instance, the ReCiPe method contains mid-
point indicators along with endpoint-indicators (Huijbregts et al., 2017). In (Huijbregts
et al., 2017) it is stated that the midpoint indicators in which weighting is excluded, are
described to be more robust but less subjective in comparison to the endpoint indicators.

3.1.4
Abiotic Resource Renewability Potential Impact
Ratio (ARR)
Apart from the exposure of toxic hazards to human health, the knowledge of renewable
and non-renewable fractions in the waste is critical so that is possible not to risk the
depletion of resources. Resources that can be replenished biologically or naturally in
limited time are characterised as renewable. The benefit of such resources is that they
cannot deplete by excessive utilisation and consumption in comparison to non renewable
resources which are not able to reproduce naturally after excessive consumption. There-
fore, in the process of prioritisation in recycling, commodities that are produced from
renewable resources are in lower scale. Materials with high priority in recycling are the
these that are produced with non renewable resources. The earth contains a restricted
quantity of non-renewable resources, such as metals, minerals and fossil fuels like coal,
oil and gas. The fundamental notion behind this indicator is that extracting a large
concentration of resources at the present will push future generations to extract reduced
concentration or lower value resources. For instance, the depletion of fossil fuels may
result in the reduced supply of fossil fuels for the forthcoming generations. A common
unit of measurement is the kilogram of Iron (Fe) equivalent (kg Fe eq). The quantity of
materials contributing to resource depletion is transformed into equivalents of kilograms
of Iron. The rationale that supports this claim has been documented by an infographic
from the BBC showing some of the non-renewable resources that are commonly used
and how much of them it is left.

3.3.1
Circularity indicator (life cycle based)
This section presents an adaptation of an existing indicator to measure the Circularity
of waste streams. The purpose of this indicator is to quantify the recuperative character
of the material stream of a waste product within the guidelines of Circular Economy,
providing information about the recirculating route of materials. For this study, the
employed method reflects the approach published by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation
(Ellen-MacArthur-Foundation, 2015) with Granta Design and co-funded by LIFE, Eu-
ropean Union’s financial instrument.
This method targets to specify the possibility to utilise materials in relation to waste
material streams into the waste product, of either virgin, recycled or reused content,
the waste product lifespan and, ultimately, the usability of materials at their end-of-life
phase, with respect to unrecovered, recycled or reused outlets. The indicator included
numerous drivers and is based upon the subsequent principles:
• using feedstock from reused or recycled sources
• reusing components or recycling materials after the first cycle of the waste product
• keeping waste products in prolonged use (e.g., by cascading use)
==PAGE:57==
3.3 Circularity Potential
35
• making more intense use of waste products (e.g. via performance ranking models)
Indicators covering these driving principles are aggregated into a single score, which is
not to be interpreted directly. Given this extent of the circularity assessment method-
ology, it is evident that enhancing the Circularity Indicator of a waste product will not
inevitably translate as an improvement of the circularity of the whole waste management
system. It should be also stated, that the indicator is not covering the full life cycle of
a waste product, hence it is likely that a waste product with a better circularity score
might be worse in terms of environmental impact. This statement justifies the coupling
of the MCI with the LCA methodology and the RP methodology in order to constitute
an environmental assessment toolbox towards CE.
The circularity indicator is derived from the main four flows that are presented below:
1. Material intake from literature studies with real case scenarios (Faraca, 2019).
2. Waste product lifespan: the ambition is to boost the endurance of the material
versus the average equivalent waste product.
3. Material Production: the ambition is to magnify the recovery and reuse of material
at a disposal level.
4. Disposal efficiency: the ambition is to disregard lower amount of materials to
disposal in dedicated areas such as landfills or energy recovery plants. Furthermore,
it is essential to decrease the amount of recyclable materials that may end up at
disposal areas and are not able to be recovered again.

4.5
Life Cycle Assessment modelling
approach
4.5.1
Scenario definition
The goal of the assessment was to estimate the potential environmental load derived
by enhanced sorting of SCW. This was done by making a comparison of impacts from
recycling the recyclable fractions in SCW between impacts from the current inciner-
ation of the same fractions. The FU was the management of the annual amount of
recyclable waste wrongly-placed as SCW consisting of the four recyclable fractions un-
der study: textiles, plastic, WEEE, wood at eight Danish RCs (i.e. 8.944 tonnes of
recyclable waste). The goal was accomplished by subdivision of the overall assessment
into four reference flows, with each stream accounting for one tonne of each of the four
recyclable fractions under study: textiles, plastic, WEEE, wood. The thesis complied
with ISO framework (ISO, 2008) and retained a consequential approach to address the
==PAGE:73==
4.5 Life Cycle Assessment modelling approach
51
consequences of a possible alteration in waste management practices.
The time related representativeness was 2018-2050 and the geographical related rep-
resentativeness was Denmark. Nonetheless, Denmark exports some of the recyclable
fractions to be recycled abroad (textiles are transported to Lithuania) and so due to the
lack of information, the “regular” European solutions and efficiencies for recycling were
adopted.
The system boundaries begin after the collection of the disposable material fractions
at RCs, but it is hypothesized that the wastes have been already gathered there, and
finish with the replacing of the subsidiary products or energy or the decisive handling
of any remaining fraction (see the case of plastic in Figure 4.6. The case of wood in
Figure A.3, the case of WEEE in Figure A.4 and the case of textiles in Figure A.5 are
situated in Appendix A.4. Moreover, all scenarios include the transportation phase of
waste from the RCs to the treatment facilities.
As for the BAU scenario it was presumed that the four waste fractions were pro-
cessed in a generic Danish incineration plant. The flue-gas cleaning system is based on
Vestforbrænding. The technology involves grate incineration with wet flue gas cleaning.
Emissions from landfill leaching from bottom ash when utilized in road construction
were also included. The inputs were the corresponding waste material fractions and the
outputs were fly ash, iron scraps, aluminium, bottom ash and cleaned flue gas. The
incineration plant is characterized by a separation efficiency of 50% for Aluminum and
80% for iron. The electrical efficiency is set equal to 22% and the thermal efficiency
equal to 73% (Møller et al., 2013). The efficiency reaches approximately 100% with
bottom ashes efficiency of 4% Heat and electricity production by incinerating the recy-
clable materials at a municipal solid waste incinerator (MSWI) was considered to be a
counter-factual claim for a baseline scenario. In this study, the alternative treatment by
recycling is promoted in order to align with Denmark’s vision towards sustainability and
circular economy (Technical and Environmental Administration, 2016). In the recycling
scenarios, it was speculated that the gathering of recyclable materials was an upstream
process where citizens participated actively before the collection. The recycling scenarios
elaborated in this study are:
1. Recycling of plastic, where plastic materials are gathered in a plastic sorting fa-
cility. A ballistic separation process was used to gather plastic films in order to
recycle them into a polyolefin mix (assumed as HDPE for modelling purposes) for
the production of plastic recycled materials. Hard plastic, which contains major
polymers (PP, HDPE, PVC, PET and PS) endures a homo-polymer reprocessing.
The products of this process are granules that are destined for non-food packaging
materials (Faraca, 2019).
2. Recycling of wood. Wood was torn into shreds and was dried. The dried pieces of
wood are then compressed with organic resins to form a spruce wood. A secondary

4.5.2
Life Cycle Inventory (LCI)
Data used for the modelling were compiled from (Faraca, 2019) Ph.D. thesis, who did
an thorough review of scientific articles, reports and databases (see the compiled list
of references in Appendix A.4.8). Such data were inserted into the original model as
parameters defined by a probability distribution, in order to include related uncertainty.
However, in this study, the focus of LCA modelling approach was to test the robustness
of the RP method in order for the latter to be able to operate as a stand-alone method.
Therefore, in order not to enlarge the scope with an extensive LCA study, the parameters
were inserted as average and where necessary as median values, not with a probability
distribution. The original data on the foreground system (including waste management
activities) were sourced and reproduced mainly from (Andreasi Bassi et al., 2017), (Bi-
ganzoli et al., 2015), (Wernet et al., 2016), (Rigamonti et al., 2014) and (Turner et al.,
2015)(all references are listed in Appendix). Data on the background system (interact-
ing with the foreground system by supplying energy and material inputs) were screened
mainly from (Wernet et al., 2016). However, ecoinvent datasets were edited to adapt
the electricity sources to Danish situation, when the process was assumed to occur in
Denmark.
When the ecoinvent datasets described processes assumed to occur outside Denmark
(i.e. for steel, aluminium, copper and plastic primary production, according to (Wer-
net et al., 2016)), the database was not revised, as it represents an average European
production. Baseline values for pre-treatment losses and recycling losses reflect those
used in (Faraca, 2019). Capital goods were excluded from the assessment and assumed
that the marginal infrastracture works will adjust in capacity according to changes in
demand (in line with a long-term temporal scope; (Weidema et al., 2009)).
The LCIA results are presented in the Section 5.2.1 of the next chapter, in order to
have their own visibility.

6.6
Limitations of the RP methodology
6.6.0.1
Limitations of the RP model
In order for the model to be able to calculate the priority scores, the contents of the
waste stream in question must be known. This is not always the case with some waste
streams, as they can either vary from time to time or be unregulated, in which case
approximations must be made - leading to uncertainties. With some of the parameters
in the test scoring over 100, and even below 0, they were given default scores of 100
or 0. This may in some situations downplay certain priority scores for a parameter, as
they invalidate potential extremes. For example, as mentioned previously, WEEE and
plastic are providing orders of magnitude of difference in impacts when recycled, but
the model normalised the scores to 100.

6 Discussion
6.6.0.2
Limitations of the LCA method
The LCA method is based on characterized potential environmental impacts, which
provide quantification of contributions to the different impact categories. This LCIA
step was chosen because the focus was to provide simplicity for the RP methodology,
which has indicators to examine the potential impacts and then normalise them with
a reference scenario in common units (Climate change in kg-CO2eq, Human toxicity in
kg 1,4 DCB, Metal depletion kg Fe). If the scope was wider, a normalisation LCIA step
would allow to evaluate the magnitude by putting results in perspective. In addition,
it could aid identify the background load of society as reference system. For instance,
the regional impact of recycling versus incineration in Denmark or Europe could be cal-
culated by dividing by population in that region or he environmental space currently
occupied per person in 2050. Instead of the aforementioned external normalisation, an
internal normalisation was used and can be seen in the supplementary information (Ex-
cel file with the title “Appendix B - Testing the RP methodology - PROCESSES ReCipe
Renewable, PROCESSES ReCipe Fossil, PROCESSES ILCD Renewable, PROCESSES
ReCipe Fossil ”).
Another limitation is the absence of uncertainty and sensitivity analysis, or combined
constituting for the global sensitivity analysis (Bisinella, 2017). The fluctuation of the
results attributed to uncertainty can be significant. This claim is supported by (Faraca,
2019), who reported that losses from sorting and reprocessing can be sensitive to the data
range when examined the WEEE and textile fraction. In contrast, the efficiencies for
heat and electricity in the MSWI can influence greatly the outcome of the incineration
scenario.
6.6.0.3
Limitations of the MCI method
The Ellen MacArthur Foundation who developed the circularity methodology used in
this study supported that financial worth can often be seized when the end-of-life waste
management is operated by an industrial branch with short processing time (Ellen-
MacArthur-Foundation, 2015). As a matter of fact, reusing materials of a waste product
conserves more of its virtue, embedded energy, and complexity than recycling it, which
consists in only recovering its basic materials. This supports the claim that, reuse is
above recycling and incineration according to the waste hierarchy. The fact that reuse
was not included in the examined case study, indicated that there was some circularity
potential of the waste streams that was not obtained. For instance, the textile waste
would have been prioritised higher as secondary material and would be more circular if
reuse was quantified.
Another aspect that is evident is that improving the MCI of a product or a company will
not inevitably translate as an advancement of the circularity of the whole system. Nev-
ertheless, an extensive use of this methodology could shape such a systems improvement.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis, a model has been tested, improved and validated with the purpose of evalu-
ating in a quantitative way waste fractions. The fractions belonging to a specified waste
stream generated by industries and municipal sources within the EU-28, were assessed
in terms of their competence as a recyclable material, in contrast to being disposed of.
The model has been constructed in Excel, and assesses the waste stream based on seven
parameters comprising four indicators to support the advancement of environmental sus-
tainability. Consequently, the parameters have been weighted according to the indicator
they belong to. Three indicators were found relevant in assessing the potential impacts
of environmental sustainability, whereas only one was found relevant in assessing the
ability of waste to act as fuel. These components include the non toxic potential impacts
ratio between two scenarios (recycling against disposal), the hazardous potential impacts
ratio that arises from the recycling scenario versus the counter-factual scenario of other
recovery of the waste stream, the abiotic resource depleting potential impacts of the
two opposing scenarios in the waste hierarchy and finally its flammability classification.
Each indicator is weighted equally, however, the combustibility of a waste stream has
only one indicator, thus it is evident that it affects the weight of the parameters and can
not be compared straightforward with another parameter.
A case study was chosen to test the model performance. Recycling centers in Den-
mark was considered a suitable case based on the potentials that were identified re-
garding the waste management of small combustible waste. The leaking of important
resources to MSWI appears to be a vital issue, involving increasing environmental aware-
ness. Thorough information on the composition of SCW is predominant to improve
current waste management practices. Already some municipalities and waste compa-
nies have expressed their willingness to look into more sustainable waste management
practices.
They have therefore in recent years increased their sorting efficiency and
this potential can be quantified. The composition of SCW indicated that on average,
56% of SCW was identified as recyclable waste, while 32% was combustibles.
Four
different waste fractions were selected among nine waste fractions from the recyclable
waste fraction of eight recycling centers. These included textiles, plastics, WEEE and
wood waste. One fundamental assumption is that waste management occurred when
the waste streams arrived presorted by citizens at the recycling collection center. It
was also assumed that textile waste comprises of clothing and household curtains and
sheets and that they were fully separated from their accessories such as shoes. The dif-
ferent values needed to calculate the different parameters were extracted from a detailed
review and inventory of a Ph.D. thesis conducted at DTU environment (Faraca, 2019),

who focused on life cycle assessment of the nine waste fractions small combustible waste.
The test of the RP model resulted in scores of 33.27, 40.49, 71.18, and 25.45 for
textiles, plastics, WEEE and wood waste respectively. Thus three waste fractions were
assessed as being of moderate priority, while the WEEE stream had significant prior-
ity according to the model. The WEEE fraction scored the highest RP in all of the
four indicators. Although some assumptions were made, the results of the test were
regarded as plausible. However, more tests are required to make a reliable assessment
of the performance ranking of the model. Moreover, the life cycle assessment of the four
aforementioned waste fractions has been conducted, in order to test the robustness of
the model and to validate the results of the prioritization provided by the methodology.
A third approach measuring circularity based on existing formulas has been tested
to examine the circularity potential of the waste products formed by the four waste
streams in the context of a circular economy. The values are dimensionless with the
highest value being 1 and indicating completely circular flow the disposal stage to the
stage of manufacturing a primary material. A score of 0.1 is the lowest score that can
be found, referring to a linear flow. In order for the three methodologies to be aligned
an overall performance ranking recommendation has been provided.
It was assessed that especially the indicator and parameters evaluating the priority
of the abiotic resource depletion should be altered such that it considers the depletion
not only of metals, but also fossil fuels and minerals. Thus, it can be concluded that
a satisfactory model has been constructed in terms of evaluating the ability of a given
waste fraction to act as a recyclable material.
As far the LCA considerations are concerned, the results indicated that SCW retains
the considerable potential for enhanced management. The consequences of correctly sort-
ing recyclable waste were examined by addressing the contribution to climate change
(CC), human toxic potential (HTP) and metal depletion potential (MDP) savings, in
order to be in line with the RP methodology.
With the exception of plastic waste,
all waste fractions in SCW provided CC savings in recycling scenarios. In particular,
textile waste and WEEE provided the largest environmental savings (-4480 and -231
kg CO2-eq/tonne of recyclable waste). This highlights the important contribution that
effective source-segregation of household waste materials may offer for recycling and the
associated climate impacts of waste management.
The Material Circularity Indicator resulted in scores of 0.456, 0.451, 0.452, and 0.359
for textiles, plastics, WEEE and wood waste respectively. The highest value that could
be reached was 0.78 for glass waste in WEEE fraction. The results were uniform for the
first three fractions, thus no waste fractions were assessed as being of distinctive priority
and more efforts could be done for the waste products to be regarded as circular and
restorative.

priority given in textiles, plastics, WEEE and wood waste. The approach involved nor-

depletion of biotic natural resources through relevant case studies.
