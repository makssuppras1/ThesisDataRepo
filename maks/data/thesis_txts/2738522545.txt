Abstract
Efficient management of bike­sharing systems in urban environments is crucial to ensur­
ing the optimal availability of bicycles across various stations. This study addresses the
challenge of predicting future demand for a station­based bike­sharing system to improve
fleet distribution by predicting spatio­temporal activity using historical data and external
variables such as weather conditions and temporal factors.
The model generates predictive images indicating areas with anticipated rental and drop­
off imbalances, thereby identifying regions that require bike redistribution. These predic­
tions assist the operational team in decision­making, providing insights into how the fleet
should be distributed to maximize usage. This approach enhances the precision of de­
mand forecasting and improves the efficiency of resource allocation, contributing to the
overall effectiveness of urban bike­sharing systems.

Introduction
Urban mobility planning is a complex and dynamic challenge, particularly in cities full of
lively activity where the demand for shared vehicle rentals fluctuates based on numerous
factors such as weather conditions, day of the week, and seasonal variations. Under­
standing and predicting vehicle rental activities can significantly enhance transportation
planning, resource allocation, and the overall efficiency of vehicle­sharing systems. This
kind of mobility services, e.g., car­sharing, bike­sharing, scooter­sharing, taxis, offer sev­
eral benefits like reduced traffic congestion, reduced parking space need and reduced
pollution [1], [2]. Shared mobility is complementary to public transport which operates in
a fixed schedule meanwhile vehicle­sharing is an on­demand service, meaning that users
can access these services as needed, usually by using an app in their smartphones, rather
than following a fixed schedule or route. The on­demand nature of these services provides
flexibility and convenience, allowing users to request transportation when and where they
need it.
This project aims to utilize advanced neural network techniques to predict bike rental ac­
tivities across an entire city. By leveraging a ConvLSTM model, which combines a spatial
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with a time­series Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM)
network, this approach captures spatio­temporal patterns effectively. The model outputs
a predictive image that provides detailed information about future bike rental activity city­
wide.

Related work
There is a lot of related literature about mobility demand forecasting that focus mainly in
the prediction of demand by using past historical data such as [3] where they use Ran­
dom Forests and focusing mainly in predicting the whole city activity and not having into
account spatial needs. [4] also uses Random Forests and considers spatial patterns by
creating a grid over the city and grouping rentals within it. In [5] they use a Generalized
Linear Model to forecast demand by having different models for each of the stations.
In [6] they use Long­short term memory neural network (LSTM) model to predict bike­
sharing demand at a city­wide scale by aggregating the activity into smaller regions,
focusing in predicting the gap between inflow and outflow of bikes. In this work they
compare several different machine learning models including autoregressive integrated
moving average (ARIMA), extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost) and artificial neural net­
works (ANNs) like recurrent neural networks (RNN) and LSTM, with LSTM showing better
performance.
There exist other works in which ANNs are used to capture spatial patterns using Con­
volutional Neural Networks (CNNs), first introduced in [7]. CNNs are particularly effective
for tasks involving spatial hierarchies in data, such as image and video recognition. This
architecture revolutionized image processing tasks due to its ability to learn and identify
spatial patterns through convolutional layers.
Recent advancements have significantly expanded the applications and capabilities of
CNNs. For instance, in [8], the authors use a CNN model to predict events in a city by
generating an image­like prediction that indicates the number of events in each part of the
city. Similarly, CNNs have been utilized in urban mobility and traffic prediction to model
spatial dependencies and forecast traffic patterns. In [9], the authors employ several CNN
blocks, named ST­ResNet, to predict the inflow and outflow of traffic.
Combining CNN and LSTM networks has proven effective for capturing both spatial and
temporal dependencies in data. This hybrid approach uses the strengths of CNNs in iden­
tifying spatial patterns and the capabilities of LSTMs in modeling temporal sequences.
One notable architecture in this domain is the ConvLSTM, which integrates convolutional
operations within the LSTM structure to simultaneously process spatial and temporal in­
formation.
In the work done in [10] the authors predict the evolution of typhoons by using image
data, they use a combination of CNN and LSTM where they use the CNN as a encoder
that captures the spatial patterns, this is then input the LSTM network (plus other vari­
ables) which processes the temporal sequences and outputs a predicted feature vector.
Finally, a deconvolution network (decoder) reconstructs this feature vector into an image,
preserving the spatial structure and any specific markers from the input images.
An important aspect that significantly impacts the accuracy and reliability of mobility de­
mand observations is censoring. Censoring occurs when the observed data is not fully
representative of the true underlying activity. Specifically, the data from any mobility ser­
vice often does not account for the full demand in a given region and time. This dis­
crepancy arises because the observed demand—such as the number of bike rentals—is
constrained by the number of available vehicles. Consequently, the actual demand is
likely higher than what is recorded. For instance, if all available bikes are rented out, any

There are several works that estimate the latent demand in shared mobility services. For

Dataset
The data for this project is obtained from the bike­sharing mobility service Donkey Re­
public. This service fetches and stores relevant data for each rental a customer makes.
A rental consists of a pickup location and a drop­off location: a pickup is when the user
starts a rental, and a drop­off is when the user finishes it.
Donkey Republic is a virtual station bike­sharing service, meaning that while there are
no physical docking stations to park the bikes, users are encouraged to end their rentals
within specific areas of the city. This implies that the majority of the activity occurs in these
stations.
Although Donkey Republic operates in several cities across Europe, this project focuses
solely on Copenhagen. In this region, there are over 5000 bikes and 2162 stations, an
overview of the station’s location is shown in Figure 3.1.

4.2.1
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are used to capture spatial patterns and relation­
ships within data. They are particularly effective in image recognition and processing
tasks due to their ability to learn hierarchical features through convolutional layers. These
layers apply convolutional filters to input data, which help in detecting features such as
edges, textures, and shapes.
Figure 4.3 shows an example of a convolution using a kernel of size 2x2 over an image of
size 4x4. In this example, the kernel slides over the original image to systematically apply
the convolution operation at each position where it can fully fit within the image bounds.
The output of each operation is computed by taking the sum of element­wise products of
the kernel and the image segment it covers, producing a new matrix element each time.
This results in a matrix that represents a transformed version of the input, reducing its
spatial dimensions and emphasizing specific features.
Some important parameters of convolution operations are:
• Kernel Size: refers to the dimensions of the filter used in the convolution process.
In the example image, the kernel size is 2x2. This means the kernel covers a 2x2
area of the input image to compute each value in the output feature map.
• Padding: involves adding an appropriate number of rows and columns to the input
image. This addition is typically of zeros (zero­padding), which allows the convolu­
12

tion operation to be applied to border elements of the input image.
• Strides: The stride defines the step size that the convolution kernel moves each
time it slides to a new position over the input image. A stride of 1, as shown in the
example image, means the filter moves one pixel at a time. Using larger strides
reduces the spatial dimensions of the output feature map because the kernel skips
over pixels of the input image at each step.
CNNs use this operation and the values of each one of the elements of the kernel are

4.2.2
Long Short­Term Memory Neural Networks
Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM) networks are a type of recurrent neural network (RNN)
that are designed to capture temporal dependencies in sequential data. LSTMs address
the vanishing gradient problem commonly associated with traditional RNNs by incorporat­
ing memory cells and gating mechanisms. These gates—input, forget, and output gates—
control the flow of information, allowing the network to maintain long­term dependencies
and selectively update its memory state.
Figure 4.4 shows a the strucutre of a single LSTM cell. The key components of the cell
are the long term memory, which is the top horizontal line that spans from Ct−1 to Ct and
the short term memory which is the bottom horizontal line ht−1 to ht and the three forget,
input and output gates.
• Forget Gate determines which parts of the long­term memory (Ct−1) should be dis­
carded. It takes the previous hidden state (ht−1) and the current input (xt), applies
a sigmoid function (σ), and multiplies the result by the cell state.
• Input Gate controls the update of the cell state with new information. It also takes
ht−1 and xt, passes them through a sigmoid function to determine which values to
update, and through a tanh function to create new candidate values. These are then
multiplied and added to the cell state.

and xt to calculate the output through a sigmoid function and then applies a tanh
function to the cell state before multiplying the two to produce the new hidden state
(ht).
Eventually, several of these cells can be stacked one after the other to form a deep LSTM

4.2.3
Convolutional Long Short­Term Memory Networks
Convolutional Long Short­Term Memory (ConvLSTM) networks combine the strengths of
CNNs and LSTMs to model spatiotemporal data. In ConvLSTM, the convolution opera­
tions replace the fully connected layers in the LSTM units, enabling the network to learn
spatial features and temporal dynamics simultaneously. This makes ConvLSTM particu­
larly suitable for tasks involving sequential image data, where both spatial and temporal
correlations are important.
In a ConvLSTM cell, the input, hidden state, and cell state are all 3D tensors, allowing
the model to capture spatial dependencies across different time steps. The convolutional
operations within the LSTM gates allow for localized feature extraction, which is essential
for accurately modeling the spatial structure of the data over time.
There is another kind of model that also uses a combination of CNN and LSTM, called
CNN­LSTM. Unlike ConvLSTM, CNN­LSTM uses separate CNN and LSTM layers. The
CNN part extracts spatial features from the data, and then these features are fed into the
LSTM to capture the temporal dynamics. While both architectures aim to handle spa­
tiotemporal data, ConvLSTM integrates these operations within a single layer, whereas
CNN­LSTM combines them in a sequence of layers.
ConvLSTM models takes a 5D tensor as a input with the following dimensions: (samples,
timesteps, rows, cols, channels), those dimensions are:
• Samples: Number of samples in the batch.
14
Analyzing Station­Based Bike­Sharing Systems through a Data­Driven Rebalancing Strategy
==PAGE:23==
• Timesteps: Number of timesteps in each sequence. This is the dimension that cap­
tures the temporal dimension, with this, the model process that number of samples
consecutively.
• Rows: Height (or number of pixels) for each frame.
• Cols: Width (or number of pixels) for each frame.
• Channels: Number of channels for the frames.
For this work it is used ConvLSTM model over CNN­LSTM because the former outper­
forms CNN­LSTM on tasks that involve predicting future states of spatial data (like weather
forecasting, video prediction, and bike rental activity) because of their ability to simulta­
neously capture spatial and temporal patterns [15] [16] [17] [18].

5.1
Data Processing and Cross­validation
Initially, the dataset consisted of 2555 frames, representing daily bike rental data. How­
ever, the weather data associated with these frames had several missing values (NaNs),
particularly in the earlier days. To ensure that the model had complete information for both
bike rentals and weather conditions, a data cleaning was done by removing any frames
with missing weather data. This step was important because the presence of NaNs in
the weather variables would negatively impact the model’s ability to learn from the data.
After applying this data cleaning process, the dataset was reduced to 1240 frames. This
subset of the data had complete weather information for each day and enables to train
the model more effectively.
To evaluate the predictive performance of the model over different time periods and sea­
sons, a time series­cross validation technique was employed. This approach is partic­
ularly suitable for time­dependent data, where the order of observations is crucial. Un­
like traditional cross­validation methods that randomly split the data, time series cross­
validation respects the temporal order of the data, making sure that training data always
precedes testing data. A example of this process is shown in Figure 5.1.

6.2
Operational usage
A key operational focus should be on zones (cells or groups of stations) where the number
of pickups significantly exceeds the number of drop­offs, that means that the need for
bikes in those places is higher than the availability. Similarly, those zones with a predicted
drop­offs greater than pickups means an imbalance where there will be more available
bikes than the true demand. Based on this difference, and on the current number of
available bikes, it’s possible to asses if it’s needed more or less bikes in each of them.
This process is shown in the next equation:
mi,g = p∗

Where:
• mi,g: is the number of bikes to move to cell g on day i.

• ci,g: is the current number of available bikes in cell g on day i.
If mi,g is a positive number, it means that the outflow of bikes is predicted to be more than
the inflow, thus, more bikes are needed, on the other hand, if mi,g is negative, it means
that the inflow of bikes is predicted to be greater than the outflow thus, there is a surplus
if bikes that might need to be allocated somewhere else.

Once the number of bikes needed in each cell is computed, the final step is to disaggregate

first the most performant stations, if a cell has a mi,g > 0 the bikes will be allocated in the

be recommended to deallocate bikes first from the stations with least number of rentals
and until it’s empty or the number of bikes reached the recommended amount.
6.2.1
Deployment
To execute the model daily and automatically, it is deployed in a Linux virtual machine
(VM) that is hosted in Google Cloud Platform.
The code is transferred from the local machine to the VM using GitHub. To execute the
code in it, a Python virtual environment is created in the VM and a crontab is set up
to execute it every day. A crontab is a time­based job scheduler in Unix­like operating
systems, used to schedule jobs to run periodically at fixed times, dates, or intervals. This
ensures that the code runs automatically at the specified time each day without manual
intervention.
A summary of the pipeline of this process that is executed every day is the following:
1. Preprocess data to transform previous n days (which was selected to be 2) into a
140x121 of 2 channels array.
2. Get weather forecast for the next day to predict.
3. Generate prediction, which is the array­like values with predicted pickups and drop­
offs for each cell.
4. Retrieve number of current available bikes in each cell.
5. Compute bike movement recommendation based on mi,g for each station.
6. Store the results into BigQuery database.

6.3
Future work
Future work aims to enhance the accuracy of the bike rental prediction model by incorpo­
rating a censoring model that estimates the actual demand for bike rentals. The current
model primarily focuses on predicting observed rentals, which may not fully capture the
true underlying demand due to limitations such as bike availability and station capacity. By
addressing these limitations, a better understanding and prediction of the latent demand
for bike rentals can be achieved. This proposed approach involves developing a censor­
ing model that accounts for unobserved demand. This model will estimate the number
of potential rentals that were not realized due to constraints such as bike shortages or
full stations. Integrating this model with the one developed in this work would be able to
predict true values of the latent demand.
Another task for future work would be to improve the model’s predictive power during
high­activity seasons. This could be achieved by incorporating additional variables such
as holidays, events, and other factors that might significantly affect rental activity. By
including these variables, the model can provide more accurate predictions during periods
of higher demand, ensuring better resource allocation and planning.

Conclusions
In this work, it is proposed a model that can predict both pickups and drop­offs for a
bike rental mobility service. The goal is to provide insights and improve the allocation and
distribution of the fleet, thereby reducing unmet demand and ensuring that bikes are avail­
able when and where customers need them. It is based on a ConvLSTM neural network
that capture the spatio­temporal dependencies of the data. The model was trained using
image­like data that contains both pickups and drop­offs for each smaller division (cell) of
the city , additionally, temporal and weather variables were used to improve the model’s
performance. Lastly, the model’s predictions are transformed into actionable recommen­
dations for the operational team, specifying which stations to move bikes to and from in
order to optimize availability and meet customer demand efficiently.
