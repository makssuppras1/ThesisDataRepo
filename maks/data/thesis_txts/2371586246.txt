II. 
Abstract 
There is an increased interest by the baking industry to improve the understanding of the 
baking process. For the production of baked products traditional techniques and conditions 
are still applied during the baking process, despite its being performed in large scale globaly. 
A significant problem for the baking industry is to produce baked foodstuff consistently with 
the same quality characteristics, such as crispiness. In order to achieve that a deeper 
understanding of the baking process and its parameters is needed. The aim of this project is 
to evaluate the effect of the baking conditions on the crispiness of bread rolls. The factors 
controlled and investigated are: temperature, relative humidity and air flow speed in the 
oven. The evaluation of the effect of these factors is achieved by measuring the moisture 
loss, the water content in the crust and the crumb, the colour and the reflectance in a range 
of wavelengths and the crispiness with mechanical and acoustic measurements. 
Temperature and relative humidity were the factors that affected the quality characteristics 
of the baked bread rolls the most.

1. Introduction 
A great concern for the baking industry is to consistently deliver products of high standard. 
The taste, the appearance as well as the texture and the nutritional value, are important 
quality characteristics. One of the most important textural characteristic concerning the 
baking industry is crispiness or crispness. There have been many approaches to determine 
the crispiness of food products but an objective method for the instrumental determination 
of bread crispiness still has not been reported.  
A significant problem for the baking industry and for foodstuff like bread rolls is the 
retention of crispiness. Usually, the retention of crispiness can be achieved by preventing 
moisture from the environment to migrate to the product with the use of packaging 
==PAGE:7==
materials. In the case of bread rolls, this is hard to achieve as moisture migrates from the 
wet crumb core to the crispy crust. (Luyten, Plijter, & Vliet, 2004). Crispiness of food 
products has been determined and evaluated with different approaches such as sensory 
evaluation with trained panels, instrumental measurements of force curves, acoustic 
analysis of the sound emitted from fracturing during compression tests and structure 
characterisation. (Luyten et al., 2004; Primo-Martín et al., 2010; Van Nieuwenhuijzen et al., 
2008; Van Vliet, Castro-Prada, Luyten, Lichtendonk, & Hamer, 2007; Vincent, 1998). 
1.1. Bread roll and baking technology 
Bakery products and bread have been known to people for 10.000-12.000 years and has 
been improved through the ages. Egyptians are considered the pioneers of the art of bread 
making throughout the world (Mondal & Datta, 2008). Baking technology spread rapidly 
wherever wheat and cereal could be cultivated and resulted in a wide variety of bread 
products, differing in size, shape, colour, flavour, texture and nutritional value.(Hussain & 
Demont, 2010). The common factor connecting all these products is that they are produced 
by mixing wheat flour with water and depending on the type of the bread, some leavening 
agents (e.g. yeast, baking powder) and then undergo a heat processing step.(Cauvain, 2012). 
The product of interest in this project is the bread roll, characterized by having a dry, thin 
outer layer called “crust”, enclosing a higher moisture, sponge-like cellular structure, called 
“crumb”. The moisture content of the crust usually lies in the range of 12-17%, while that of 
the crumb reaches that of 35-42%. The crust colour, depending on the type of flour used, 
commonly has a light golden brown colour and depending on the thickness, it has a hard 
and brittle character that gives the desired crispiness. The crumb, on the other hand is 
characterized by the white colour (for plain wheat flour) with a cellular structure that is 
formed to a great extent, by the production of carbon dioxide gas from the yeast 
fermentation and by the incorporation of air bubbles in the dough during kneading dough. It 
is soft with a degree of resilience and a degree of “chewiness”.(Cauvain, 2012) 
 
 
 
 
1.1.1. Bread roll ingredients and their role 
1.1.1.1. 
Wheat flour 
Wheat flour when mixed with water forms an elastic dough, by forming a strong network 
that traps the carbon dioxide gas produced during the fermentation from the yeast or by 
the addition of baking powder. These unique properties are attributed to the proteins 
contained in the wheat flour, gliadins and glutenins that contribute to the flour quality and 
the dough rheological properties. Glutenin is responsible for the elasticity of the gluten and 
affects the time that the dough needs to be developed, while gliadin is responsible for the 
volume of the loaf (Hussain & Demont, 2010) . Another constituent of the flour is the starch, 
consisting 75-80 % of the wheat flour weight. During the grinding process of the wheat 
==PAGE:8==
kernels, starch granules are damaged, affecting the quality of the flour. The level of 
damaged starch affects the colour and the size of the gas cells of the final product as the 
enzymes α and β amylases, break down the starch in fermentable sugars that are 
subsequentlyafterwards are fermented by the yeast, leading to higher CO2 and ethanol 
production. The colour is affected by the caramelization and Maillard reactions during 
baking. Starch granules with excess of water and with supplied energy in the form of heat, 
absorb water, swell and increase in size and viscosity. This process is called starch 
gelatinisation and plays an important role on the taste and the texture of the final product. 
(Mposkou,2004) 
1.1.1.2. 
Water 
Water represents around 40 % of the dough weigh and around 35% of baked bread 
products (BROWN, LANGLEY, & BRAXTON, 1998). It plays an important role as it acts as a 
solvent during the mixing of the ingredients, hydrates the starch granules and the flour 
proteins and acts as a mean for the dispersion of the yeast in the dough. (Gebruers, Courtin, 
& Delcour, 2009). It also acts as a dissolvent for the CO2 produced by the yeast.(Brown 1993) 
1.1.1.3. 
Yeast 
Yeast (Saccharomyces Cerevisiae) is used to produce carbon dioxide and ethanol 
(evaporated during the baking process) that contributes to the expansion of the baked 
products. In the early stages of baking, yeast activity is at maximum and produces large 
quantities of carbon dioxide by fermenting the sugars available from the breaking of the 
starch into fermentable sugars from the enzymes. These gas bubbles are released in the 
aqueous phase of the dough, retained by the gluten network formed during kneading and 
give the necessary volume in the bread roll during baking. Yeast also contributes to the 
flavour of the final product (Cauvain, 2012) 
1.1.1.4. 
Salt 
Salt contributes to the flavour development and controls the water activity, retarding the 
growth of molds(Cauvain, 2012).It can usually be found in concentration of 1-2 % of the 
flour weight. Salt also has another function in dough making, shielding the charges of the 
gluten proteins, allowing them to interact better and produce a stronger network, thus 
controlling the rheological properties of the dough and as a result the texture of the baked 
products(Hoseney, 1994) 
 
1.1.1.5. 
Other ingredients 
Other ingredients used in the production of bread and bread rolls are usually fats, sugars, 
emulsifiers, oxidants, enzymes and conservatives. Fat gives bread products increased 
volume and increases the shelf-life of the product. Sugars are used as a source of 
fermentable carbohydrate for the yeast and also affect the flavour of the product.(Hoseney, 
1994).Emulsifiers are used as dough strengtheners and as crumb softeners. (Cauvain, 
2012)They improve gas retention, give better oven spring and increased volume, they 
increase the handling properties of the dough and the crumb structure and they also 
==PAGE:9==
improve the rate of hydration and the water absorption (Stampfli & Nersten, 1995). 
Enzymes used for the production of bread rolls are usually amylases, proteases and 
oxidoreductases. Amylases are used to improve the gas-retention of the dough, leading in 
better volume and crust colour, increase the fermentable sugars by breaking down starch 
and enhance flavour. Proteases are used to reduce the gluten elasticity in order to produce 
softer dough and for the improvement of crispiness (Hussain & Demont, 2010; Primo-
Mart??n et al., 2006). Furthermore, oxidants like ascorbic acid or azodicarbonamide used in 
concentrations of parts per million, improve the strength of the dough and improve the 
volume and the texture of the products. Finally, concervatives such as calcium or sodium 
propionate are used to increase the shelf life of the product by preventing the growth of 
molds (Hoseney, 1994) 
1.1.2. Bread roll making technology 
The bread roll making process consists of different stages: mixing of the ingredients, 
fermentation, molding and proofing, baking. 
1.1.2.1. 
Mixing of the ingredients 
Mixing is the homogenization of all the ingredients together. In this stage, energy is supplied 
in the dough by kneading in order to develop the gluten network. As the proteins and the 
damaged starch in the flour are getting hydrated, the resistance of the system increases 
progressively. Another important process taking place during mixing is the incorporation of 
air bubbles in the system. Those air bubbles produce the initial cells where the CO2, 
produced during the fermentation, diffuses. Summarizing the mixing process, the 
ingredients are uniformly dispersed and dissolved in the water, starch and gluten proteins 
are hydrated and develop the gluten network with the input of mechanical energy 
(kneading) and air bubbles are incorporated in this gluten structure. (Cauvain, 2012; 
Hoseney, 1994) 
1.1.2.2. 
Fermentation 
Fermentation is the process where the mixed dough is kept under optimum conditions for 
the yeast to act. Temperature and humidity are kept constant at this stage for an 
appropriate time, to soften and expand the dough. Yeast converts the fermentable sugars 
into carbon dioxide that expands the volume of the mixture. The ingredients undergo 
chemical and physical changes as the pH drops, flavour and aroma compounds are formed, 
the temperature rises and the consistency of the dough changes. The fermentation process 
is influenced by many factors such as the quality and the type of the ingredients used, the 
fermentation time, the temperature and the humidity of the environment. (Hoseney, 1994; 
Hussain & Demont, 2010) 
1.1.2.3. 
Molding and proofing 
In this step after the fermentation, the dough is divided in small pieces of proper weight, 
shape and size. Afterwards, the dough is left to rest, under controlled conditions, with an 
environment temperature of 30-35 oC and a relative humidity ranging from 75 to 90%. This 
stage is called proofing and it takes 55-65 minutes. In this stage the dough increases greatly 
==PAGE:10==
in volume and it is essential to have the appropriate conditions in order to achieve the 
desired texture, volume, colour and crust formation in the following baking step.(Cauvain, 
2012; Hoseney, 1994; Hussain & Demont, 2010) 
1.1.2.4. 
Baking 
Baking is the last and the most important step of the production of bread rolls. Baking is a 
heating process with temperatures usually ranging from 200 to 250 oC. The time needed 
depends on the oven temperature and the final point is usually when the bread rolls reach a 
core temperature of 92-96 oC. During this stage there are different physical and chemical 
reactions occurring on the dough. In the early stages of baking, a crust is almost 
immediately formed in the outer layer of the dough, as the relatively low temperature 
dough is introduced in the hot oven. On the surface of the dough, evaporation of the water 
takes place, leading to cooling down of the surface, thus starch in the crust retains its 
structure. During this early stage, the dough expands rapidly as the yeast activity reaches its 
maximum, ethanol produced from yeast vaporizes, the gases incorporated in the dough 
heat and expand and carbon dioxide becomes less soluble (escaping from the aquatic 
phase). As the temperature rises, inactivation of the yeast and enzyme activity takes place, 
starch gelatinizes and the proteins coagulate. At 65 oC the starch gelatinizes and the dough 
starts becoming more and more elastic up to 95 oC. At approximately the same 
temperatures proteins begin to undergo denaturation (60-70 oC) and lose their water 
binding ability, releasing water to the starch, thus promoting its gelatinization. (Cauvain, 
2012; Hoseney, 1994; Hussain & Demont, 2010).During baking the surface of the bread roll 
is heated by convection from the hot air of the oven and forms a crust on the outer surface 
and an evaporation front is observed. The temperature of the surface increases and heat is 
removed by conduction towards the inside of the product. When the water on the surface 
has fully evaporated, the front moves towards the inside. Different structures are formed: a 
dried layer, the crust, that increases in thickness with baking; and a wet core, the crumb, 
where the moisture remains constant. The crust temperature tends to the oven 
temperature while the crumb temperature tends to 100 oC(Zanoni, Pierucci, & Peri, 1994). 
As soon as the temperature on the surface reaches 115 oC Maillard reactions occur leading 
to the formation of the characteristic crispiness and colour, flavour and aroma of the baked 
bread products. With the continuous increase of the temperature on the surface of the 
product, the sugars start to caramelize and form coloured substances, imparting a distinct 
flavour to the baked product.(Hussain & Demont, 2010) 
1.1.2.5. 
Post baking – Cooling and bread staling 
During cooling a firming effect can be seen on the bread that comes from the retrogradation 
of the amylose portion of the starch. This firming allows the bread to be sliced and is a 
short-term effect, while the long-term firming of the bread is called staling.Staling is the 
result of the retrogradation of the amylopectin portion of the starch.(Cauvain, 2012) In the 
end of the baking process, the crispiness of the product is difficult to retain due to the 
migration of water from the moist crumb to the dry crust and from the environment when 
the product is kept in high humidity conditions. This moisture transfer results in a soft, 
leathery crust causing the staling of the bread crust.(Hoseney, 1994)

1.2. Crispiness 
Crispiness is one of the most important textural characteristics of baked products and a lot 
of studies have been conducted to define it objectively with instrumental measurements 
and sensory evaluation from trained panels. It is usually associated with the freshness and 
quality of bread products. The importance of crispiness for the consumers was highlighted 
from Szczesniak(SZCZESNIAK, 1963) , while Vickers and Bourne related the perception of 
crispiness with the emission of sounds during fracturing of a foodstuff.(VICKERS & BOURNE, 
1976) 
There has been a particular focus of measuring crispiness using acoustic-mechanical 
measurements. Chaunier et al (Chanvrier, Chaunier, Colonna, Valle, & Lourdin, 2005) 
investigated crispiness in cornflakes, by acquiring acoustic and mechanical data 
simultaneously, while Chen et al. (CHEN, KARLSSON, & POVEY, 2005)investigated the 
crispiness of biscuits, in an attempt to link the mechanical and acoustic properties to 
crispiness. Varela et al (Varela, Chen, Fiszman, & Povey, 2006) demonstrated that sensory 
crispness in almonds is highly correlated with the rate of emission and size of acoustic peaks 
and demonstrated that sensory crispiness can be evaluated effectively by acoustical and 
mechanical measurements. Another detailed study was performed by Castro-Prada et al 
(Van Vliet et al., 2007) to simultaneously measure the mechanical and acoustic properties of 
crispy cellular solid foods.   Primo-Martin et al ((Primo-Martín et al., 2010) conclude that the 
crust of breads prepared with shorter proving volume are crispier and tend to retain the 
crispiness for longer time when stored at low RH%. They hypothesize that this is affected by 
the morphology of the crumb with a more closed structure, smaller gas cells and a thicker 
crust, having a significant and positive effect on the crispiness. In another study, in 2008 
Primo-Martin et al(Primo-Mart??n, de Beukelaer, Hamer, & Van Vliet, 2008) studied the 
effect of air and vacuum cooling on the fracture behaviour, moisture content and crispiness 
of bread crust. Vacuum cooling proved to increase the number of force and sound events, 
decrease the cooling time, gave breads with lower moisture content in the crust and the 
crispness of the product improved. Also, the bread crust crispness retention improved 
significantly.(Primo-Martín, de Beukelaer, Hamer, & van Vliet, 2008) 
Another approach in improving the crispness of crispy breads was performed by Hirte et al 
(Hirte, Hamer, Meinders, van de Hoek, & Primo-Martín, 2012), by controlling the water 
vapour permeability of the crust,. They created a model that suggests possible combinations 
of size and quantity of channels in order to achieve up to eight-fold longer sensory crispness 
retention without decreasing the crumb softness retention. In another approach Hirte et al. 
(Hirte, Hamer, Hoffmann, & Primo-Mart??n, 2013), tried to  control the water vapour 
permeability by changing the composition of the crust. They prepared composite breads 
with the same crumb but different crusts varying in starch/protein ratios and proved that 
cracks were formed that led to an increased water vapour permeability of the crust and an 
eight times longer crispness retention. 
==PAGE:12==
Finally, Schirmer et al. (Schirmer, Hussein, Jekle, Hussein, & Becker, 2011), studied the effect 
of the amount of steam injected during the baking process on the crispness, crust thickness 
and colour of bread rolls. They proved that by increasing the amount of steam supplied in 
the oven increased the volume, the crust thickness, the compression-forces, the maxima 
sound level and the overall crispiness. 
 
 
1.3. Aim of this study 
It can be concluded that the crispiness of bread products is affected by many parameters 
and factors along the production, from the raw materials to the final product. 
The aim of this study is to evaluate the effect of a combination of these factors on the 
crispiness of bread rolls. The effect of temperature, relative humidity and the air flow speed 
in the oven on the textural properties of bread rolls is evaluated.  
This effect is evaluated with the use of mechanical and acoustical measurements 
simultaneously, as well as the moisture content of the upper and lower crust and crumb of 
the bread rolls. The effect on the colour of the bread rolls as well as in a wide range of 
wavelengths is studied.

2.2.3. Water content and moisture loss 
The moisture loss during the baking process was determined by weighting the samples in 
each position before and after the baking process.  
The water content of the bread roll crust (top and bottom part of bread roll) and the crumb 
were determined by placing the samples in an oven at 105 oC for 24 h. A cylinder of 50 mm 
was used for sampling from the centre of each bread roll. The bottom and top crust were 
separated from the crumb with the use of a knife. Each sample was weighted before and 
after the drying process and the water content was calculated from the difference of the 
measured weights.  
The samples used for the determination of the moisture content were placed in the 
positions 5 and 6 on the baking tray.  
2.2.4. Multispectral analysis 
Multispectral images were taken from the baked samples, from position 3 and 4 on the 
baking tray. The L*a*b colour space was used to show the colour change of the bread rolls. 
The three coordinates of the L*a*b scale represent the lightness of the colour for L* from 0 
to 100, the position between red/magenta and green for a* from -120 to 120 and the 
position between yellow and blue for b* from -120 to 120. 
(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lab_color_space) (The mean and standard deviation of the 
reflectance of the samples from a total of 18 spectra, as well as the colour space L*a*b of 
the baked samples, were obtained with the use of VideometerLab (Videometer A/S, Lyngsø 
Allé 3,2970,Hørsholm,Denmark). 
2.2.5. Texture profile analysis and Acoustic measurements 
Texture profile analysis was performed in the samples positioned on the 1st and 2nd place on 
the baking tray. The texture analysis was performed using a texture analyser with a 30kg 
load cell (TA-XT Plus, Stable Micro Systems Ltd, Surrey, UK). The samples were placed on a 
flat table and a double compression test was performed in each sample.  A 100 mm 
compression plate was used (bigger than the diameter of the bread rolls), the pretest speed 
was set to 2mm/sec, the test speed was 10mm/sec and the target mode was set to distance 
==PAGE:15==
(30mm). The trigger type was set to Auto(Force) and the trigger Force at 0.04903 N. The 30 
kg load cell was chosen after a series of pretest experiments. The max Force indicating the 
hardness-fractuability was measured. 
Simultaneously with the texture analysis, the sound emitted during the double compression 
test was recorded using a reference Acoustic Envelope Detector (AED) supplied by Stable 
Micro Systems. A fixed distance of 2cm from the bread roll and 3cm height from the flat 
table to the microphone was used for sound recording. To reduce the noise level of the 
instrument motors, the filter option from the Acoustic envelope detector was turned on and 
a blank sound test sample was taken before each experiment, used for the setting of a 
threshold for determining the sound peaks. The data acquisition rate was set to 500 points 
per second for both force and acoustic signals. The threshold was set to 50 dB and a 
threshold of 5 dB was chosen for the determination of the sound events.  Recording and 
initial signal analysis were performed using the texture analyzer Exponent program v 
6.1.11.0 from Stable microsystems. A macro was written that counted the recorded sound 
events (AUX) above the set thresholds, giving also the sum of the peaks of the sound events. 
For every sound recording the first 6 seconds of the fracture process were analyzed, as the 
fractures after the first compression are not representative. The average sound pressure 
level (SPL) was calculated by the total sum of the sound events divided by the number of the 
sound events. Moreover, the max SPL for each test was noted. 
2.2.6. Statistical analysis 
The design of the experiment and the statistical analysis of the samples was achieved with 
the use of Microsoft Excel and Minitab v17. 
3. Results and Discussion 
Bread roll samples were baked under different conditions (temperature, relative humidity 
and air flow speed) as described on table 2 in the design of the experiment and were cooled 
down for 70 minutes at ambient conditions. Subsequently, a series of tests were performed 
to collect information about the effect of the factors on the crispiness, colour, moisture 
content and uniformity of the baking process taking into account the position in the oven.  
A series of preliminary experiments were performed to decide the baking time that was set 
to 19 minutes. Baking for shorter time led to poor acoustic samples for the lowest values of 
the factors while baking for longer periods led to overburnt samples. 
The number of the samples baked each time was decided after considering the size of the 
bread rolls and the baking tray. For each baking process all the samples were weighted 
before and after the baking process to evaluate the moisture loss during the baking process, 
as well as the homogeneity of the oven. The time intervals between the processes and tests 
was kept to the minimum feasible and the ambient conditions were the conditions at the 
time of the experiments in the laboratory and ranged from 23-25 oC and 25-30 % RH. The 
transportation of the samples between the buildings, from the laboratory to the pilot oven 
and backwards, was done in Styrofoam boxes to prevent the effect of the weather 
conditions on the measurements.

From the table with the p-values and the coefficients we can see that temperature has a 
positive statistical significant effect in all of the responses. This means that the higher value 
of temperature gives higher responses in hardness, AUX, avgSPL and SPL, thus in crispiness 
too. This can also be observed from the normal and half normal plots and the pareto charts. 
A positive significant effect can be observed for the responses of Hardness 1, AUX, avgSPL2 
and the SPL, meaning that with the higher values of the relative humidity the responses get 
higher values, thus increasing the crispiness of the breadrolls. Moreover a significant 
negative effect can be observed for the interaction between the airflow speed and the RH, 
leading to the result that lower values of these factors could lead in higher values of the 
response. This can be due to the position of the airflow nozzles and the pipe from where the 
steam was injected in the oven. The air flow speed has a significant positive effect on AUX1 
and on SPL for both positions, resulting in higher responses with the higher settings. On the 
other hand, a negative significant effect can be observed for the interaction of the 
temperature with the relative humidity for the response of the SPL, resulting in higher 
values of the response with the lower values of the interaction. The residual plots show that 
the residuals are normally distributed and the R squares show that the models for the 
acoustic responses are fitting the data relatively well, giving good predictions with new 
observations. On the other hand, the models for the hardness are not fitted satisfyingly and 
the predicted responses cannot be taken into consideration.  
Overall, the crispiness seems to be affected mostly by temperature and the RH in the oven 
during the baking process. In this study, it was proved that the increased relative humidity in 
the oven increases the maximum sound pressure level, as well as the average SPL and the 
amount of the sound events. Similar conclusions can be seen in the study of Schirmer et all 
(2011). 
Conclusion 
The effect of the different parameters: Temperature, Relative Humidity and air flow speed in 
the oven were evaluated with different methods in this study. The results show that the 
temperature has most significant effect on all the responses measured (moisture loss, 
moisture content in the crust, multispectral analysis and mechanical and acoustical 
measurements of crispiness) , followed by the Relative humidity in the oven. For more 
==PAGE:43==
reliable results, more experiments should be performed in the pilot oven, after performing 
some modifications for the better stability of the baking process, as there was some leakage 
of steam from the different slits and openings for fitting data cables. With improved 
insulation and some modifications the maximum relative humidity during the baking 
process could be increased dramatically, as now it was limited to 60 % maximum RH%), 
leading to lower energy consumption, more stable baking process and more reliable results 
in the performed experiments. 
It was concluded that the moisture loss in the pilot oven was affected mostly by the 
temperature of the baking process. The results of this project show that the baking process 
of the oven is uniform and the positioning of the samples seems to play no role in the result 
of the measured observations, despite some variations in the results of the rest of the 
measurements. Those variations can be due to different factors such as the proofing of the 
dough, some leakage of steam in the baking process etc.  
The water content of the crust and the crumb of the baked bread rolls cannot be concluded, 
as there was an outlier that affected the results importantly. Also, the separation process of 
the crust from the crumb takes time and this can lead to wrong measurements from the 
transportation of humidity from the environment and the wet crumb to the crust. 
The multispectral analysis and the L*a*b* scale measurements seem to be a promising and 
powerful tool for the prediction and evaluation of the baking process, as it gave good 
correlations and good models for the prediction of new responses. Moreover, it could be 
possibly be used for the determination of the water content of the surface of baked 
products, but further experiments with various designs and parameters need to be 
performed in order to make the multispectral analysis a reliable method for the evaluation 
and prediction of the baking process. It is also very promising as it could probably be 
implemented inline on production lines making really fast, accurate and non-destructive 
measurements. 
Finally, the evaluation of the crispiness with simultaneous acoustic and mechanical 
measurements seems to give reliable results, but more studies need to be performed in 
order to have this method as a reliable tool for the evaluation of the baking process. 
Future perspectives 
In order to better understand better the effect of these parameters on the crispiness of 
bread rolls a series of studies could be performed. First of all, a model for the acceptance of 
the crispiness with sensory evaluation of baked bread rolls under different values of the 
factors and with simultaneous measurements with the texture analyser and acoustic 
measurements, could be created. That way a crispiness scale could be created, that could in 
the future be modified for other products. With a series of studies for the effect of these 
factors in steps, the baking process could be understood in depth, leading to the creation of 
simulation models , which when combined with a crispiness or a quality characteristic scale 
(either crispiness or colour by multispectral analysis), would lead to an automation of the 
process according to the desired results. By increasing the controlled factors, such as the 
ingredients quantities and qualities the accuracy of a model of that type could increase 
==PAGE:44==
impressively, reducing the time needed for the production of baked products by 
optimisation of the processes and even lower energy consumption.
