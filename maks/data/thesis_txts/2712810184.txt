Summary
Normalizing flow can transform simple probability distributions into complex proba-
bility distributions, which can be used in generative models, reinforcement learning,
variational inference, etc.
In this thesis, we present a new method based on the current state-of-the-art
method. We replaced the method of constructing the spline of neural spline flows
with Continuous Piecewise-Aﬀine Based transformation, thus we call the new method
as CPAB flow. We implement the CPAB-flow(Coupling) and evaluated it by density
evaluation on different datasets.
The results show our method outperforms the rational-quadratic neural spline
flows in density evaluation in low dimensional datasets.
However, the current implementation is not so stable that it possibly occurs nan
loss values in a long training process, which causes the failure of evaluation on high
dimensional data sets due to the model crash in the long training process. Thus there
are still future works to do to complete the implementation of CPAB flow.
We also investigate the application of neural-spline-flow on generative models for
image data sets, but not applied the CPAB flow on it due to the unstable in training.
But currently, we can conclude the CPAB flow performs the state-of-art method in
low dimensional distributions while with the potential of better performance in high
dimensional distributions.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years, with the high-speed development of artificial intelligence, more and
more data sets are explored by engineers and researchers. Modeling the probability
distribution of the data sets and generating samples is a research direction that draws
attention increasingly.
Some methods based on unsupervised learning are proposed for solving this problem,
the most effective techniques include Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)[11],
Variational Auto Encoders (VAEs)[14], and Normalizing Flows(NF).
GANs and VAEs have great performance in learning complex data distributions like
nature images. However, both GANs and VAEs lack the exact evaluation and infer-
ence of the probability distribution, which often results in low-quality blur results in
VAEs and the training of GANs challenging with model collapse, posterior collapse,
and vanishing gradients that result training instability [1, 24].
Normalizing Flows have powerful expressiveness while many current issues of GANs
and VAEs are avoided by applying reversible functions. Normalizing Flows converge
faster compared to GANs and VAE, and it is more stable than GANs in the training
process[21].
Normalizing flows provide a general mechanism that constructs flexible probability
distributions from a base probability distribution by applying a series of bijective
transformations.
It initially be used for density estimation of datasets and then
widely used in modeling and inference including image generation, audio generation,
and reinforcement learning, etc.
With the maturation of this field in applications, much recent work on Normalizing
Flows contributed to a variety of new methods, which with improved expressiveness
and wider applications.
Currently neural spline flows (NSF)[9] has the best performance overall. By applying
monotonic rational-quadratic spline as the transform function in Normalizing Flows,
NSF obtains inspiring expressiveness. The spine is defined to be monotonic to ensure
its invertibility. Besides, to ensure the model is trainable, the spline is differentiable
and has computable Jacobian determinant.

1.1
Motivation
At the end of the discussion in the neural spline flows paper with the sentence:
"Monotonic splines are one way of constructing invertible elementwise
transformations, but there may be others"[9].
Thus, we thought about if there is anything that could replace the spline, then we no-
ticed that one-dimensional Continuous Piecewise-Aﬀine Based(CPAB) transform[10]
possibly be a good choice.
By setting the right parameters, a one-dimensional CPAB transformation could achieve
a similar effect as an arbitrary spline transformation. Intuitively, we can replace the
splines with 1-D CPAB transforms, letting the neural networks to find the optimal θ
that minimizes the loss.
Besides, the one-dimensional CPAB transform also matches the requirements of the
transform function in Normalizing Flows, it is invertible, differentiable, and we only
need to find a method to compute Jacobian determinants eﬀiciently.
Now we have seen the potential of in-drop replacing spline transform with CPAB
transform in neural spline flow to create a new type of Normalizing Flows that we
call CPAB flows.
1.2
Project objectives
The main goal of this project is to apply one-dimensional CPAB transformation into
Normalizing Flows to create new tools that we call CPAB flows.
As one of the sub-goals, we plan to review the relative theories in both Normalizing
flows and CPAB transformation so as to describe CPAB flows clearly.
Another important sub-goal is, we need setup experiments for exploring the prop-
erties of CPAB flows. Then we can compare CPAB flows with other Normalizing
Flows through the experiment results to evaluate the performance and analysis its
advantages and restrictions.
Finally, make a concrete conclusion that lists our work clearly and proposes the future
work.

CHAPTER 2
Normalizing Flows
The framework of Normalizing Flows is firstly proposed for solving density estima-
tion problems in Density estimation by dual ascent of the log-likelihood[26] and A
Family of Nonparametric Density Estimation Algorithms[25], then be popularized by
Variational Inference with Normalizing Flows[23], which reviewed the related work
exhaustively and broadened the conceptual framing of flows.
The motivation for normalizing flows is to use a simple probability distribution to
build the more complex and expressive probability distribution we want. The flows
are a series of transformations, letting the original probability distribution transform
into what we want - the final probability distribution.
According to the definition from the paper Normalizing Flows: An Introduction
and Review of Current Methods [17], normalizing flows are a family of generative
models with tractable distributions where both sampling and density estimation can
be eﬀicient and exact. It is based on maximum likelihood estimates and invertible
neural networks. This type of model has two advantages: it is flexible and fast to
generate, and it is easy to calculate the likelihood.
To take a view of the normalizing flows, we illustrate its basic supportive theories,
applications, and classic methods.
2.1
Basic Theories
The tools needed to build normalizing flows are Jacobian Determinant, Change of
Variable Theorem, and Chain Rules. Besides, to understand the functionality and
requirements of transform functions is also important.
This section briefly introduces how the theories support normalizing flows.

2.1.4
Transform Function
Each transform in the normalizing flow is expressed by an artificial neural network.
According to the properties of the neural network, to make the normalizing flow
trainable and computable, there are some prerequisites to the transform function.
At first, the transform function must be differentiable. Otherwise, it’s not possible to
compute the gradients.
Secondly, the transform function must be invertible for doing backpropagation.
Finally, the Jacobian determinant must be eﬀiciently computable. Directly computing
the Jacobian determinant through the Jacobian matrix, which generally costs O(D3)
(D is the dimension of hidden layers) that is too expensive. Fortunately, we only
need the determinant of Jacobian rather than the Jacobian itself, thus we could find
a smarter way to eﬀiciently compute the Jacobian determinant without the Jacobian
matrix.

2.3.3
Coupling Flows
The coupling layer was introduced in Nice: Non-linear indepen- dent components
estimation [6], which enables transform to be highly expressive in Normalizing Flows.
As one of the most widely used flow architectures, the Coupling transforms have
tractable Jacobian determinants and the inverses are eﬀiciently computable.
Neural Spline Flows[9] describes the procedures of coupling transform[6]. The cou-
pling transform ϕ maps an input x to an output y in the following way:
1. Split the input x into two parts, x = [x1:d1, xd:D].
2. Compute parameters θ = NN(x1:d1), where NN is an arbitrary neural network.
3. Compute yi = gθi(xi) for i = d, ..., D in parallel, where gθi is an invertible
function parameterized by θi
4. Set y1:d1 = x1:d1, and return y = [y1 : d1, yd : D].
The Jacobian matrix of a coupling transform is lower triangular, so we can get the
determinant easily by computing the product of diagonal elements in the Jacobian
matrix.
The Fig.(2.4) shows the the procedures of coupling transform. Notable that when
building the coupling flows, we normally add permutation layers between each cou-
pling layer, otherwise only the elements in the XB are transformed while the elements
in XA never changed.
There are more advanced methods for partition x have been explored and in common
practice. For example, in place of permutation using 1 × 1 convolution(Glow)[15],
non-volume preserving flows (RealNVP)[7].

2.3.4
Autoregressive Flows
Kingma proposed autoregressive flows in Improved Variational Inference with Inverse
Autoregressive Flow [16].
Normalizing Flows: An Introduction and Review of Current Methods [9] describes the
definition of Autoregressive Transform[6] as following.
yt = h(xt; θt(x1:t−1)),
(2.17)
where x1:t = (x1, ..., xt), θt is an arbitrary function that mapping Rt−1 to the param-
eters, and θ1 is a constant.
The Jacobian matrix of the autoregressive transform is triangular, which means
the determinant is the product of its diagonal elements. The output yt only depends
on x1:t in Eq.(2.17). Kingma also introduced inverse autoregressive flows(IAF)[6],
which output yt depends on current xt and previous outputs of y. The transform is:
yt = h(xt; θt(y1:t−1)),
(2.18)
where y1:t−1 = (y1, ..., yt) and else as same as Eq.(2.17).
Eq.(2.17) and (2.18) with the same form, but the inverse of inverse autoregressive
flows can be computed more eﬀiciently.
As we can see from Fig.(2.5), Eq.(2.17) with the strength in parallel computing,
because yt only depends on x1:t, that can be easily parallelized. On Eq.(2.18), the
output depends on previous outputs, so the computation is inherently sequential that
cannot be parallelized.

CPAB Transformation
Transformations based on continuous piecewise-aﬀine velocity fields[10] proposed a
transformation method which called CPAB transformation.
CPAB transformation applies a tessellation onto the space domain to construct Con-
tinuous Piecewise-Aﬀine (CPA) velocity fields, then take integration on them to gen-
erate different transform functions on the different areas so that to realize the overall
transformation.
The tessellation evenly separates the domain space into pieces. Then CPA velocity
fields are built by applying aﬀine transformations on each piece while restricting the
neighbor pieces to keep continuous after the transformation.
CPAB transformation can be intuitively applied for diffeomorphism of images and
signals, but it also with the potential of being used in neural networks. Nicki applied
it to deep learning networks in Deep Diffeomorphic Transformer Networks[5] that
created inspiring new tools which improved the performance in image verification.
It’s worth saying that the paper on CPAB transformation mainly demonstrated the
2D transformation with the applications on images, but in our case, we mainly focus
on 1D transformation. Thus, the basic theories of CPAB transformation will be illus-
trated with a focus on 1D transformation.

CPAB Flows
We noticed the one-dimensional CPAB transform can reach a similar effect as the
spline transform in neural spine flows(NSF). In intuitive, it’s worth replacing the
spline transform with the one-dimensional CPAB transform in NSF to see what hap-
pens, and this is the original idea of CPAB flows.
In this chapter, we first introduce the CPAB spline which is the core of CPAB flows.
CPAB spline is created based on the restricted one-dimensional CPAB transform.
and keeps the same functionality as other splines.
Then we demonstrate the implemented CPAB Flow(C), which is created by replacing
the RQ spline in RQ-NSF(C) with CPAB spline, thus we obtain CPAB Flow(C).
Finally, we analyze the possible properties of CPAB Flow(C) based on the known
information.
4.1
CPAB Spline
Spline transform only deals with one-dimensional data; thus we take one-dimensional
CPAB transform with restrictions as the spline transform, which is called CPAB
spline.
Similarly with other splines having the number of bins to control the flexibility,
CPAB spline has θ to control the flexibility.
CPAB spline is fully defined by θ, which is generated by a neural network in practice.
The number of elements in θ is related to the number of knots in the CPAB spline,
that normally with more elements in θ, the spline is more flexible, but we found the
problem of gradient explosion with a large number of elements in θ in practice.
In our guess, the gradient explosion is caused by the too much flexibility of the spline.
The spline is defined by θ, and the variety of each element in θ will change the shape
of the whole spline due to the spline being generated related to the dot product of θ.
Therefore, to avoid the gradient explosion from over flexibility, the number of elements
in θ of CPAB spline K are restricted as small, which is set in the range of 3 to 8 in
our experiments.
Furthermore, too big values in θ would cause computation diﬀiculties, thus we
restrict the value in θ in a small range [−R, R]. In practice, based on the test experi-
ments, we set R as 3.
In practice, we noticed the CPAB spline would lose flexibility outside of the range
0 to 1. Fig.(4.1) shows the several cases of CPAB spline loss flexibility outside [0,1].

on θi.

ResNet
Theoretically, any neural network could be used to fit the θ of the CPAB spline, but
we chose ResNet considering it performs better in highly deep networks that match
the situation of CPAB flows. In highly deep networks, both forward and backward,
the gradient will lose information that makes the networks hard to train.

As we can see in Fig.(4.4), by adding identity highways between different network
layers, ResNet decreases the information loss in propagation, which makes the training
easier. As CPAB flows normally with highly deep network layers, ResNet would be
a good choice.
4.3
Analysis
We can review and analyze the potential properties of CPAB flows before getting into
the experiments.
At first, because the restriction on θ, single CPAB spline has limited expressiveness.
Secondly, the numerical differentiation method is not strictly precise and may cause
the model to be diﬀicult to converge. We’d better find mathematical analytic expres-
sions for computing the Jacobi Determinant to improve the accuracy.
Furthermore, considering CPAB spline with so many restrictions in θ, which includes
the value range and the number of elements, this possibly restricts the flexibility of
the transformation.

of training, the loss value turns to be Nan, which causes the training to fail.
We have identified the issue coming from training the neural networks, but the
models are too complex to debug in a short time, thus we still have not solved this
problem.
We have some ideas to solve the problem. At first, we would like to change the
restriction on θ and try more tuning. Furthermore, we have three guesses for the
problem.
One guess is the numeric problem may happen with a particular probability, that
the training in large datasets with a higher possibility of getting this issue, thus it
normally fails in learning large datasets. We consider setting a reload mechanism
that once the Nan loss occurs, we reload the model parameters from a saved model
in the early status, with introducing some random modifications to the parameters
to help the training jump out of this dilemma.
Secondly, we guess the complex structure of ResNet follows diﬀiculties in training,
that switching to another more stable network would make the training easier.
Thirdly, we guess the properties of the CPAB spline make it hard to fit θ with
respect to the logabsdet directly. We consider expressing the effect of θ on the CPAB
spline with a mathematical formula and fitting the parameters in this formula with
respect to the logabsdet.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
6.1
Review
In this thesis, we firstly have reviewed the normalizing flows in both theory and prac-
tice. The supportive theories for normalizing flows were introduced, which contain
Jacobi Determinant, Change of Variable Theorem, Chain Rules, and the transform
functions. To strengthen the understanding, applications of normalizing flows are
also discussed with visualized cases.
The classic types of normalizing flows were introduced with the basic formula
of transform functions.
As the most widely used framework in normalizing flows,
coupling and auto-regressive are illustrated with more details. As the state-of-the-art
method, much ink was poured into neural spline flows for illustrating their properties.
CPAB transformation was introduced with application effects while taking one-
dimensional transformation as the focus. Theoretical supports are listed to explain
why we take 1-D CPAB transformation as the replacement of the transforms in neu-
ral spline flows. The implemented library for CPAB transformation libcpab[4] was
introduced with application cases.
As the core of CPAB flows, the CPAB spline was demonstrated in detail. Its re-
strictions in usage were listed and analyzed with experimental cases. We described the
implementation of the CPAB flows with a coupling layer, and analyzed its potential
problems.
Multiple experiments were carried out to explore the properties of our imple-
mented CPAB flows. The results prove that CPAB flows can do the same work as
other normalizing flows.
Furthermore, CPAB flows with the best performance in
density estimation of low-dimensional data but unknown their performance in higher
dimensional data due to the training problem.
6.2
Performance Analysis
CPAB-Flow(C) outperform Neural Spline Flows in learning low dimensional datasets.
However, due to the numeric problem during the long training, we can’t make the
CPAB flows best trained to see if it is able to outperform others in higher-dimensional
datasets. Thus, to avoid the numeric problem, you have to take care of the hyper-
parameters carefully. Some guesses of the causes are demonstrated in Section.(5.3.2).
==PAGE:50==
40
6 Conclusion
In the aspect of training speed, CPAB-Flow(C) is almost 2× slower than RQ-
NSF(C).
All in all, based on current results, we can state that the CPAB flow we proposed
is the state-of-the-art method for density estimation in low-dimensional datasets.
6.3
Future Work
Due to the time constraints, we have some planned work that hasn’t been finished.
First of all, fix the numeric problem in the long training process, so as to see the
performance of CPAB-Flow(C) in higher-dimensional datasets.
Secondly, to improve the precision of logabsdet in CPAB splines, we’d better find
the mathematical analytical expression of logabsdet. In this way, possibly the model
would converge easier and result in a better performance.
Thirdly, replace libcpab with difw in CPAB spline that to see if there is any improve-
ment.
Then, do more experiments to find the optimal setup of θ.
Finally, build CPAB spline into auto-regressive framework to build another practical
tool.
