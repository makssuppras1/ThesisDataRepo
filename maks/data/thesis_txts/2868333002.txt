Abstract
Denmark is facing an increasing problem with pesticide being detected in an increasing number of water
abstraction wells. Specifically, it remains unclear whether contamination primarily originates from point or
diffuse sources. This is of great concern as the two sources are handled by different authorities. Estimating
the source type is therefore important.
This study aimed to quantify the impact of point and diffuse sources located at the same site. A site with
a point and diffuse sources was identified. The site is located downstream of an abstraction well field
where contamination with pesticide metabolites, including dimethachlor-ethane sulfonic acid (DMC ESA),
dimethachlor-oxalamic acid (DMC OA), alachlor ethane sulfonic acid (ALC ESA), and N,N-dimethylsulfamide
(DMS), has been observed. A site investigation was conducted to assess the contributions of these sources
and to characterize the local geology and hydrogeology.
The results revealed that DMC ESA was present at high concentrations in both sources, aligning with the
findings from the abstraction well field in 2024. Other compounds, such as DMC OA, CGA 369873 and DMS
exhibited significant variation between the point and diffuse sources. Overall, the highest concentrations
were observed at the point source, but the concentrations at the diffuse sources were within a comparable
range. This highlights the substantial contamination potential of the diffuse sources
To further quantify the contributions of the two sources, 2D flow and solute transport models were devel-
oped in COMSOL Multiphysics. It was decided to implement a side view model to simulate the vertical
distribution of contaminants and a top view model to estimate the impact on the abstraction well field.
Simulations for all modelled compounds suggested very low concentrations (Just at or below the detection
limit of 0.01 [µg/L]) at the abstraction well field in 2024, which increased to approximately the measured
2024 concentrations by 2058. The diffuse source impacted multiple abstraction wells, while the point source
affected only one. Notably, the diffuse source produced significantly higher concentrations than the point
source, with the point source concentrations remaining below the detection limit at all times.
Several factors were identified that could explain the inconsistency between the model predictions and the
measured concentrations in 2024. These include a potential underestimation of the hydraulic conductivity
and porosity from the site towards the abstraction well field . Additionally, contributions from surround-
ing fields not included in the model may have elevated the measured concentrations from the abstraction
well in 2024. Further examination showed that changing the diffuse source area significantly influenced ab-
straction well concentrations, while increasing the site concentrations of the point source had lesser impact.
Adjusting pumping rates also affected the plumes of the sources, influencing which wells were contami-
nated by the respective sources.
This thesis has enhanced the understanding of the relationship between point and diffuse sources located

Introduction
In the EU, 65 % of the human population relies on groundwater for their drinking water, making it a
crucial resource to protect (European commission, 2024). However, in 2016, it was found that 24 % of
the total groundwater area in the EU was of poor chemical status, and 9 % was of poor quantitative status
(European Environment Agency, 2022). A significant contributor to poor drinking water quality is pesticide
contamination, primarily originating from agriculture (European Environment Agency, 2022).
Contamination of groundwater is especially concerning in countries like Denmark, where 99 % of drinking
water comes from groundwater resources (Miljø- og ligestillingsministeriet, 2024). In 2023 pesticides were
detected in 51 % of the drinking water wells tested for pesticides in Denmark, compared to only around
29 % in 2019 (Thorling et al., 2024). Additionally, 12.2 % of the tested wells exceeded the limit values for
individual pesticides in drinking water (Thorling et al., 2024). The drinking water quality criteria (DWQC)
for pesticides is 0.1 [µg/L] for individual pesticides or their metabolites, and 0.5 [µg/L] for the total sum of
pesticides (Miljø- og Ligestillingsministeriet, 2011).
Metabolites of chloroacetamide herbicides, such as dimethachlor (DMC) and alachlor (ALC) metabolites,
have been detected in a growing number of abstraction wells in the last couple of years. The main challenge
associated with DMC and ALC is contamination caused by their metabolites. Metabolites of particular
concern include dimethachlor-ethane sulfonic acid (DMC ESA), dimethachlor-oxalamic acid (DMC OA),
CGA 369873, alachlor ethane sulfonic acid (ALC ESA), and alachlor oxalic acid (ALC OA) (Thorling et al.,
2024). These metabolites are more mobile and persistent than their parent compounds, making them a
greater threat to groundwater resources (Lewis et al., 2016).
The most commonly found compound in abstraction wells is N,N-dimethylsulfamide (DMS) detected at
32.2 % of the selected wells in 2023. (Thorling et al., 2024). DMS is a metabolite of which can originate from
several different pesticides (Nyrop Albers et al., 2023). It posses a great risk for groundwater contamination
as it is a very mobile and persistent compound (Frederiksen, 2023).
Pesticide contamination sources can be divided into two types: point and diffuse sources. Point sources
are identified by a smaller area with high concentrations and are typically found in places where pesticides
are handled, such as pesticide storage, filling, and washing areas. Diffuse sources on the other hand, cover
a larger area with lower concentrations, typically agricultural fields where pesticides have been applied
These two types of sources can contribute very differently to the contamination of groundwater both due
to the difference in concentrations and due to the size of the affected area. Management of the two sources
are different; point sources are handled by the regional authorities, while diffuse sources are managed
by the municipalities and the Danish Environmental protection Agency (Roost et al., 2022). Therefore it
is important to differentiate between the two sources to get the right authorities involved in remediation

Distinguishing between point and diffuse pesticide sources in real cases is challenging, as boundaries be-
tween the two often blur due to environmental and management factors. For example, multiple small
point sources can resemble a diffuse source, while intensive pesticide use in a smaller field area may mimic
a point source (Roost et al., 2022). These complexities highlight the need for further research and careful
analysis to accurately identify and manage contamination sources.
In this project, a site with both a point and a diffuse pesticide source will be investigated. The site is
located outside the village of Sengeløse in Central Zealand, Denmark, and has been used for agriculture
since 1877 and continues today. Previous investigations in this area have been conducted, and pesticide
contamination has been detected on site (Weeth et al., 2024). The compounds of interest at the site are
DMC, ALC and their metabolites as well as DMS. The site is located downstream from an abstraction well
field where groundwater is collected for drinking water for the Greater Copenhagen area (HOFOR, 2024).
The compounds of interest have also been detected at the abstraction well field and it is suspected that a
part of the contamination originates from the site (Weeth et al., 2024). However, more information is needed
to determine if the contamination originates from the site, and if so whether the point or diffuse source at
the site is the dominating source.
Aim
The aim of this project is to quantify the contamination from the point and diffuse source located at the
site, providing an enhanced understanding of the impacts on the abstraction well field. To achieve this, the
following four objectives have been established:
1. Examine the transport and degradation of DMC, ALC and their metabolites as well as DMS detected at
the nearby abstraction well field and the diffuse and point sources at the site.
2. Analyse the point and diffuse source located at the site regarding geology, hydrogeology and the con-
tamination distribution of DMC, ALC and their metabolites as well as DMS.
3. Asses the transport of DMC, ALC and their metabolites as well as DMS from the point and diffuse
sources at the site to the abstraction well field by modelling the obtained data.
4. Compare the contribution from the point and diffuse source to the nearby abstraction well field.
Finally the perspective of this study will be discussed on relation to methods applied, handling of pesticides
sources and management of the well field.

2.1
Pesticide contamination
When discussing pesticide contamination, there are two types of contamination sources: point sources
and diffuse sources. A point source is a localized source with a small area but a high concentration at
the source and steep concentration gradients in both horizontal and vertical directions. Point sources are
typically associated with areas where pesticides are handled, such as filling and washing areas, pesticide
storage facilities, and similar locations. A diffuse source, on the other hand, is characterized by a larger area
with lower concentrations at the source and low concentration gradients in both horizontal and vertical
directions. This type of source commonly occurs in agricultural fields where pesticides are applied in
diluted concentrations for crop cultivation. At a diffuse source a smaller range of pesticides is expected
compared to a point source. Only a few selected pesticides are used at diffuse sources depending of the
crops, where several different pesticides can be handle at point sources.
(Roost et al., 2022). The typical distribution of contamination from the two types of sources are shown in

While the conceptual distinction between point and diffuse sources are clear, distinguishing them in real
world scenarios is often challenging. The boundaries between these two types of sources can blur due to
environmental or management factors. For instance, if there are many smaller point sources within the
same area, these sources can resemble a diffuse source. Conversely, if a smaller area has been subjected
to intensive agricultural use, the diffuse source could resemble a point source due to its smaller area and
higher concentrations (Roost et al., 2022).
This complexity underlines the need for further research, as no universally effective method currently exists
to reliably differentiate between point and diffuse sources (Roost et al., 2022). Accurate identification re-
quires careful analysis of spatial patterns, source histories, and environmental conditions to apply effective
management strategies.
From a legislative perspective, the two source types are also treated differently. In Denmark, the manage-
ment of these sources falls under different authorities. Point sources are handled by the regional authorities,
while diffuse sources are managed by the municipalities and the Danish Environmental protection Agency
(EPA) (Roost et al., 2022).
2.2
Dimethachlor
2.2.1
History and usage
DMC belongs to the herbicides group chloroacetanilides and was used as the active compound in the prod-
uct Teridox 500EC. Teridox 500EC was primarily applied to fields sowing oilseed rape plants. DMC was
imported into Denmark from 1980 to 1990 and remained in use until 2011, when it was banned. In 2012,
a ban on possession was also implemented in Denmark (Miljø- og Ligestillingsministeriet, 2012). In total,
approximately 161 tonnes of DMC were sold in Denmark.

have significantly higher water solubility values than DMC. This implies that they are even more prone
to dissolve in water and may have increased mobility in aqueous environments compared to DMC. The
vapour pressure of DMC OA and ESA are low, indicating that these metabolites are likely to remain in the
liquid phase rather than in the gas phase.
2.2.3
Degradation and metabolites
Degradation of DMC have been shown to take place at both aerobic and anaerobic conditions in soil. The
degradation of DMC exhibits variability in half-lives (DT50) under different conditions. Under aerobic
conditions, DT50 ranges from 2.4 to 15.6 days, while under anaerobic conditions, values range from 11.3 to
19.4 days, with some studies reporting much higher values of 50.73–63.99 days (EFSA, 2008; L´opez-Ruiz
et al., 2020). These differences in half-lives can be attributed to variations in soil types, temperature, and
experimental setups. The persistence of DMC in the environment can be categorized as low to medium
under aerobic conditions, but it is generally higher under anaerobic conditions. An overview of the DT50
values from the studies is provided in Appendix A.
Abiotic degradation processes such as hydrolysis and photolysis have not been found to play a significant
role in the degradation of DMC in soil and water.
The metabolites of DMC formed in the largest fractions are DMC OA and DMC ESA (EFSA, 2008). An
aerobic degradation experiment quantified the occurrence of the different metabolites of DMC. Between
12–35 of the parent compound degraded into DMC OA, and 15.8 % degraded into DMC ESA, with all other
metabolites accounting for less than 10 % (EFSA, 2008).

Data regarding pumping rates and hydraulic heads from AWF-K and AWF-N were used in the flow model.
The pumping rate data was provided by HOFOR, which manages both abstraction well fields. Data was
provided for the period 2018-2024 and the data is presented in Appendix H.
Initially, the approach was to adjust the modelled hydraulic heads using yearly average hydraulic head
values for the different wells for 2018, 2021, and 2024. However, inconsistencies were observed in the ob-
tained data, where increased pumping rates resulted in higher hydraulic heads. This contradicts theoretical
expectations, and as a result, the flow model did not align with the average hydraulic head values provided
by HOFOR.
It was decided to fit the top view flow model to align with the hydraulic head gradient from the site to AWF-
K. The average hydraulic gradient from the site to AWF-K was determined from two sets of hydraulic head
measurements. The measurements from the site were conducted on January 31st 2024, and on August 2nd
2024, these can be seen in Appendix H. The hydraulic head gradinet was deducted to be between 1.92 and
2.15 meters for 2024.
A seasonal variation over the year was observed. This variation can be seen in Appendix H. However it
was determined that the seasonal variation have been relatively steady from 2021 to 2024. The boundary
conditions were based on data from October 2018, the pumping rates used in the top view flow model were
set to an average monthly value for the wells from October 2024, as to represent the same season.

to significantly influence the abstraction well. In comparison the highest accumulated concentration of all
pesticide found at the point source (at B11) is 22 [µg/L]. It is therefore not likely that the point source will
achieve concentrations high enough to impact the abstraction well. This underlines the relative insignifi-
cance of the current point source contribution.

7.5
Summery of model results
The key findings from the flow and solute transport models are listed below.
• Larger variation in transport time was observed for the diffuse sources compared to the point sources.
This is mainly due to the large difference in surface area between the two sources.
• Pumping rate have a big impact on both the concentrations at the wells, and which wells are affected.
Both sources were observed to be affected when different pumping scenarios were tested.
• The assessment of the diffuse source indicate that a clear impact can be expected to AWF-k due to
only this source. Concentrations at above the DWQC were observed in multiple scenarios including
the diffuse source.
• The effect of the point source at AWF-K was found to be negligible. A large increase in site concentra-
tions are needed for the point source to have an impact on AWF-K.
Overall, under the assumed conditions at the site, the diffuse source poses the greatest impact on AWF-K,
with the point source being negligible. Several parameters influence both the transport and impact from
both sources.

Further perspectives
8
Further perspectives
When assessing the contamination from point and a diffuse sources, several parameters are essential to in-
clude in the investigation. Unexpectedly, concentration between point and diffuse sources can be in similar
magnitudes at long-term agricultural practices. To obtain a reliable representation of a diffuse sources, mea-
surements across the entire area of the sources are necessary as variations along the source can occur. It is
therefore recommended to collect samples throughout the diffuse source area to achieve the most accurate
representation.
Determining potential sources outside the chosen area of interest are important. Consideration of both
upstream and downstream sources in regards to a site is necessary. Most diffuse pesticide sources are
agricultural fields, which are often located in close proximity. These fields may contribute contamination
levels comparable to those of the diffuse sources within the investigated area. Without accounting for the
influence of surrounding areas, there is a higher likelihood of misinterpreting the contamination sources
within the investigated area.
The impact of the point source compared to the diffuse source was negligible. Further investigated across
multiple sites with different with other conditions and the presence of both sources is needed to evaluate
whether this trend is consistent. This will help determine how to handle locations where both point and
diffuse source are present.
An increased focus on fields as diffuse pesticide sources is needed. Especially, the management of water
abstraction well fields, should consider the impact which can be contributed to diffuse sources. Continuos
monitoring of the abstraction wells is suggestion, as pesticide contamination can occur several years after
application.
Model simulations provide valuable insights into the quantification of various pesticide sources. The con-
tributions from diffuse and point sources are heavily influenced by the flow conditions in the study area,
which must be determined with precision. As most pesticide contamination have been on going for several
decades, changes to the flow pattern might have occurred.
The selection of pumping rates in a steady-state setup therefore requires careful consideration due to their
substantial impact on the behaviour and extent of the contamination plume. To achieve more accurate rep-
resentations of the dynamic regarding groundwater flow, future studies should consider applying a time-
dependent flow model. However, such an approach is contingent on the availability of a comprehensive
and suitable dataset.
2D models with different perspectives can be applied to simulate the transport of contaminants from sites
with both point and diffuse sources. Contaminant transport is inherently a three-dimensional process, ne-
cessitating several assumptions and adjustments for each 2D model to achieve realistic results. For future

studies, it is recommended to construct a 3D model to achieve a more accurate representation of the trans-
port process, as it would provide a more comprehensive depiction of the dynamics involved.

Conclusion
9
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the contribution from a point and diffuse source regarding DMC and ALC
metabolites as well as DMS. The aim of this project was to quantify the contamination from the point and
diffuse source located at the site, which has provided an enhanced understanding of the impacts on the
abstraction well field.
DMC and ALC, as well as DMS are more mobile and persistent than their parent compounds. At the site,
DMC and ALC compounds were observed in various stages along the degradation pathway, indicating
differences in degradation conditions between the two sources. DMS was found in different phases but
with no other metabolites present.
The geology of the site showed clay till being the dominant feature, followed by a thinner sand layer, and
limestone appearing close to the surface. Overall, the point and diffuse source had similar geologies with
the main difference being the thickness of the clay till and sand layer. Flow conditions at both sources were
aligned with an overall flow direction.
DMC ESA was seen to be the most dominant metabolite at both sources. The concentration of contaminants
at the diffuse source were observed to be higher than expected with values close to the measured concen-
trations at the point sources. The investigation of the sources showed that metabolites were very location
specific with large difference between the sources. The vertical distribution was greater at the point source
indicating that leaching will continue in the coming years.
By applying numerical modeling, the transport from the different sources towards AWF-K was assessed.
DMC and ALC metabolites showed similar transport tendencies, with DMS being more mobile. Variation
in arrival time was observed for the diffuse source, which affected multiple wells while the points sources
had a more steady transport time, and only affected one well. Overall the point sources showed a slower
transport than the diffuse sources.
Altering the pumping rates highly impacts the contamination plumes for both sources, changing which
wells were mostly exposed to contamination from the sources. Surface area and site concentrations were
also shown to impact the affects on AWF-K.
The contribution from the point sources were determined to be negligible as all concentrations at AWF-K
were below the detection limit.
The diffuse source was determined to affect AWF-K at multiple wells, with the maximum concentration
exceeding the DWQC. There was however wells which at all times were below the detection limit, even
with various pumping rates applied. Applying both sources at the same time further showed that the point
source is insignificant compared to the diffuse sources.

Conclusion
This project has contributed to extending the understanding of point an diffuse sources and their impact to
nearby abstraction wells. Valuable insights regarding future field investigation of point and diffuse sources
have been obtained. However further research across multiple sites is necessary to evaluate whether the
observations are present at other locations.

Redox at site
As part of the investigations carried out by WSP, oxygen levels and measurements to determine redox
conditions were taken along the transect. A cross-section showing the measured oxygen concentrations in
[mg/L] at various depths of the wells along the transect has been created and is presented in Figure B.3.
Figure B.3: Cross section showing the oxygen level in the wells in the transect. The x-axes is the concentration of
oxygen in [mg/L] and the y-axes is the depth in m DVR90.
From the figure, it can be seen that overall, the soil at the transect exhibits oxygen-poor to anaerobic condi-
tions in the deeper parts. However, oxygen levels of 2.8 [mg/L] were found in well F2 at a depth of 11.19
m DVR90. Oxygen levels above 0.1 [mg/L] were also detected in wells F3, F1, and F5, indicating that some
aerobic degradation could occur in this area.
Comparing the oxygen levels found in well F2 to the redox measurements conducted by WSP reveals sim-
ilar degradation conditions. At 11–10 m DVR90, NO3- levels of 41–32 [mg/L] were detected, indicating
oxygen to nitrate-reducing conditions. At deeper levels, between 8–6.5 m DVR90, oxygen levels were
below the detection limit of 0.1 [mg/L], and NO3- concentrations decreased, suggesting nitrate-reducing
conditions. Additionally, SO42- concentrations decreased between 11–10 m DVR90, indicating a transition
from nitrate- to sulfate-reducing conditions (Alvarez & Illman, 2005).
Redox measurements were also conducted in well F7, where oxygen levels were below the detection limit.
In well F7, a decrease in Fe concentrations was observed at depths of 6–4 m DVR90, indicating iron-reducing
conditions.
Overall, at the site, a pocket of oxygen presence is observed on the eastern side of the transect, from well
F3 to well F5, at approximately 10 m DVR90. This suggests that pesticides flowing through this area may
have better conditions for degradation.

Hand augering
Hand augerings are made with a hand-auger drilling down into the ground collecting soil at the wanted
depths. The soil samples are collected in a rilsan bags for analysis.
HPT-Sondering
The Hydraulic profiling tool (HPT) is a tool that can be used to make a profile of soil and its hydraulic
properties over depth. The HPT works by pushing a probe down into the ground. On the probe there is a
membrane where a constant water flow is send out. On the backside of the probe the backpressure from
the soil is measured. The HPT also measures the permeability and hydraulic conductivity (Hedegaard &
Christensen, 2018). A sketch of the HPT can be seen in Figure E.1
Figure E.1: A depiction of a HPT. The HPT has a membrane where water is pushed out into the soil and a meter that
can measure the backpreasure. The HPT also has a conductivity meter which measures the conductivity.

tTEM
The Towed Transient ElectroMagnetic method (tTEM) is a tool that maps the shallow subsurface (0-80 m). It
works by having a vehicle driving the the TEM tool around scanning the subsurface. TEM is a geophysical
technique used to investigate subsurface resistivity by inducing and analysing electromagnetic fields. A
steady current is transmitted through a transmitter loop, creating a primary magnetic field. When the
current is shut off, it generates inductive currents in the subsurface, which in turn produce a secondary
magnetic field. The properties of this secondary field, measured by a receiver coil, are influenced by the
resistivity of the subsurface materials (McLachlan, 2024).
Slug test
A slug test is a test done to determine the water flow. The slug test can be done in two ways with either a
falling-head slug test where a rise in the hydraulic head is done by quickly adding a known amount of water,
or a raising-head slug test where a fall in the hydraulic head is done by removing a known amount of water.
Both tests test the rate of change in the hydraulic head by monitoring. The test is done when the hydraulic
head is back to its original static level. In this project the falling-head slug test is used. The test is done by
using the HPT-probe which also monitors the hydraulic head afterwards.
Soil core collection
To collect soil cores an assembly of tubes are needed. Firstly a plastic tube is secured inside a metal tube -
from now on referenced to as the inner tube. The plastic tube is used for collecting the soil, and the inner
tube is used both for protecting the plastic tube from breaking and for pushing soil into the plastic tube.
The plastic and the inner tube is then places inside a larger metal tube - from now on referenced to as the
outer tube - which both helps with pushing the soil core into the inner tube and keeping the surrounding
soil in place. A sketch of the setup can be seen i Figure E.2.

Suction cell
A suction cell is a tool used to sample pore water from the unsaturated zone (Fisker & Larsen, 2023).
Placement of the suction cells happens by pushing the cell into undisturbed soil. To do so, firstly a outer
tube as the one described in section E is pushed into the soil to push away the surrounding soil. The outer
tube is placed 20 cm above the desired depth for the suction cell. Then a inner tube whit a suction cell
placed at the end connected to a pipe is placed in the outer tube- The suction cell is thereby pushed into
undisturbed soil. The pipe connected to the suction cell is necessary for transport of the pore water to the
surface. After placement both the outer and the inner tube is removed form the hole and the suction cell
and the pipe is left in the ground. The suction cell is then secured with quartz flour and water and the hole
is then closed of. A sketch of the placement of a suction cell is shown in Figure E.3
