Abstract
Three diÔ¨Äerent estimators for Markov jump processes (MJPs) observed as a mixture of
continuous and discrete observations are developed. The interpretation of this problem
as that of a partially observed Markovian arrival process is emphasised. This creates
a connection between continuous-time observations and phase-type distributions. This
link is used to combine generalised results from the phase-type literature with methods
for estimation in pure discretely observed MJPs to form hybrids of various estimators.
The Markov assumption is furthermore relaxed by allowing noisy observations and
phase-type distributed sojourn times. These relaxations result in a generalisation of
the continuous-time hidden Markov model; this is dubbed the hidden Markovian arrival
process. The likelihood function is derived and an EM-algorithm is devised for parame-
ter estimation.
It is concluded that the EM-algorithm vastly outperforms direct optimization of the
likelihood function, though with the caveat that this may be due to some implementa-
tional aspects. An MCMC approach is also developed, and of the three estimators, this
appears the most promising.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Markov processes constitute a class of stochastic processes that has seen wide use in
almost all Ô¨Åelds of engineering, biostatistics, computer science and other areas. Their
simple, and intuitive yet powerful construction makes them applicable to describe the
behaviour of many types of systems. From the evolution of crediting ratings, to model-
ing the progression of various stages of cancer, there are many tasks for which they are
suited [LS02][AK02]. An attractive feature is their analytical tractability, which makes
them suitable for use in statistical models. And indeed, when they have been fully ob-
served, maximum likelihood estimation is easy [Alb62]. However, only in rare cases is
the situation so simple, and often one most make due with incomplete observations. In
this thesis, estimators are developed for one such diÔ¨Écult observations.
The focus is on developing estimators for the parameters in a Markov process, both
from a frequents and Bayesian perspective. The eventual use in statistical context is the
objective, and throughout, statistical applications of the methods are hinted to. The
problem is described in more detail below.
1.1
Problem Description
Observe a Ô¨Ånite state Markov jump process, X(t), deÔ¨Åned on the state space (1, . . . , m),
with initial distribution œÄ and inÔ¨Ånitesimal generator Q. The process is initialised at
t = 0 according to œÄ. The state of the MJP is sampled at some time points t1, . . . , tn.
These time points can either be discrete observations or events. Events are transitions
which are observed immediately when they occur, i.e. in continuous time. Thus, the
measurements (1, . . . , n) are for estimation purposes labelled as discrete observations
or events. The state measurements to a time ti, i = 1, . . . , n, are denoted xti. These
observations are denoted by X(ti) = xti, after the jump has taken place. This sampling
scheme is illustrated in Figure 1.1.
It is convenient to separate the generator as Q = Q0 + Q1, where Q0 gives the in-
tensities of transitions not associated with an event and Q1 speciÔ¨Åes the intensities of
transitions that are associated with an event. This model is called a Markovian arrival
process (MAP) and is explained in more detail in Section 2.3. The task is to estimate
œÄ, Q0, and Q1.
When Q1 = 0, two methods for solving this problem were given in [BS05] for a sin-
gle realization and were extended to multiple realizations of the same MJP in [BS09].

1.1.1
The Markov Embedding Problem
It is possible that, given a Markov jump process observed under the described scheme,
a maximum likelihood estimator for the parameters of interest does not exist. It is also
possible that several equally good solutions (in terms of their likelihood) exists. This is
the problem of existence and uniqueness for the maximum likelihood estimator, and this
problem is closely related to a classic problem from probability theory: The so-called
Markov embedding problem. This is the question, Ô¨Årst posed in [Kol36], of whether a
discrete-time Markov chain can be estimated from discrete samples of a continuous time
Markov jump process. This problem, as it concerns maximum likelihood estimation,
was reviewed in [BS05] and some suÔ¨Écient conditions for existence and uniqueness were
given. This thesis is primarily concerned with developing estimators for the parameters
of interest when they exist, leaving the problem of existence and uniqueness for further
work.

1.2
Markov jump processes in survival analysis
An important application area is event history analysis using multi-state models, where
events are modeled as transitions between states in a Markov jump process [JK09]. Event
history data is usually incomplete, for example when applied to survival data. In survival
analysis, the states correspond to diÔ¨Äerent stages of a disease, and the progression or
digression from one state to another is usually only observed at doctor follow-ups. The
event is therefore only known to have taken place between two discrete observations.
These kinds of observations are called interval censored, and present a major problem
when applying multi-state models in survival analysis [AK02]. A crude but common ‚ÄôÔ¨Åx‚Äô
is assuming the transitions occurred right in the doctor‚Äôs oÔ¨Éce [PK91, p. 161].
Another problem with survival data is that the number of events is not known pre-
cisely either; several transitions may have occurred in-between follow-ups. The refer-
enced articles assume all transitions have been observed. This assumption is common
and usually left unexamined [AK02, p. 110]. It is reasonable, given a suÔ¨Éciently high
follow-up frequency, and given that the transitions are non-reversible. In models with
reversible transitions, for example models with recovery, the assumption is questionable.
In event history analysis, any transition is called an event; the time some event(s) oc-
curs is merely known more precisely than others. In this thesis, an event will only be a
transition whose time is known exactly. This convention is inherited from the literature
on Markovian arrival processes.
Explicit solutions to these problems exist [Jac+03], and they involve some ad-hoc mod-
iÔ¨Åcation of the likelihood. This thesis presents a framework based on the Markovian
arrival process and is an elegant and general way to model this type of incomplete data,
that requires no modiÔ¨Åcations or questionable assumptions to account for censored ob-
servations. Example 1.1 below describes how this framework can be used on a survival
data example.
Example 1.1 (The CSL1 study). The CSL1 study [PK91] was a randomized clinical
trial designed to evaluate the eÔ¨Äect of the drug Prednisone on patients diagnosed with
liver cirrhosis. 512 patients were randomized into either a treatment or control group.
Patients in the treatment group were given daily doses of Prednisone, those in the con-
trol group were given a placebo.
Of particular interest in this study was the prothrombin index, a measure of how well
the liver is functioning. Lower values than normal indicates a damaged liver. In doctor
follow-ups, which occurred 3, 6 and 12 months after treatment-start, then once a year,
the prothrombin index was recorded as a percentage of the normal value. The patients
were followed until death or right-censoring occured (caused by either leaving the study-
prematurely, or being alive at the end of the study).
This study can be modelled with the framework as follows: The prothrombin index

1.3
Thesis objective and overview
There are four main objectives in this thesis:
Firstly, to situate the approach from survival analysis to these types of mixed observa-
tions of a Markov jump process within the framework of the Markovian arrival process.
Secondly, it is to extend the methods for handling discrete observations from [BS05] to
also incorporate continuous data (i.e. events). Thirdly, it is to look at some ways to
relax the Markov assumption, allowing for more Ô¨Çexible models, and develop estimators
for these. And fourthly, it is to compare the three methods in terms of their eÔ¨Éciency.
==PAGE:15==
1.3 Thesis objective and overview
5
The thesis is organised as follows: Chapter 2 summarises the necessary general the-
ory from likelihood theory and stochastic processes, including the Markovian arrival
process. This chapter does not include all the relevant general theory. Some of the more
advanced theory is postponed until the relevant chapters, in order to not overwhelm the
reader. In Chapter 3 it is argued that the examined model can be seen as a Markovian
arrival process with a special observation scheme. Some analytical results are also pre-
sented.
Chapter 4 presents an EM-algorithm for the problem at hand. An alternative approach
to the EM-algorithm is examined in Chapter 5, which is direct optimization of the
incomplete-data likelihood function. A third approach to estimation in the model is
described in Chapter 6, which involves simulation using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo
method in a Bayesian paradigm. Chapter 7 and 8 examines ways of relaxing the Markov
assumption. In particular, Chapter 7 develops estimators for the case where there is
uncertainty involved in the observation of the state of the MJP. Chapter 8 examines one
method to circumvent the constant transition rates that are otherwise a requirement of
homogeneous MJPs.
The three methods of estimation are compared in Chapter 9 on simulated data. Chap-
ter 10 summarise the main results of the thesis and conclusions are presented. Ideas for
further work are also discussed.

2.1.2
ClassiÔ¨Åcation of states
A state i in a Markov process is called transient if, given that the process starts in state
i and then leaves it at some point, there is a positive probability that it will never return
to it. On the contrary, if it returns for certain then the state is called recurrent. An
absorbing state is state where, if the Markov process enters it, it will never leave again.

A Phase-type renewal process is a renewal process where the inter-event times are de-

exponential distributions is a great advantage, the phase-type renewal process is still

2.4
Likelihood
Likelihood is one of the most important concepts in modern statistics. It is an entire
framework for modeling and making inference in problems involving uncertainty. Due
to the wide use of likelihood, a vast amount of literature exist on the subject, and it
can only be given a brief treatment here. This section reviews the concepts of likelihood
theory needed for the problems dealt with in this thesis. For a more thorough review,
see for example [Paw01].
The key quantity in likelihood theory is the likelihood function. This function describes
the available information about some unknown parameter Œ∏ in some data set Y = y. For

2.4.2
The Bootstrap Method
The bootstrap method is a family of computational approaches for obtaining measures
of uncertainty about sample estimates. Largely, the bootstrap methods fall into para-
metric and non-parametric methods.
In non-parametric bootstrapping, one has a sample of size N. From this sample the
parameter(s) of interest is estimated. A new sample is then drawn, with replacement,
from the original sample. If N is suÔ¨Éciently large, this new sample, called a bootstrap
sample, will almost certainly be diÔ¨Äerent from the original. From the bootstrap sample,
the parameters of interest are re-estimated.
This process is repeated a large number of times, yielding a new estimate for the param-
eter every time. This collection of estimates is then an empirical representation of the
distribution of the parameter estimate, from which things such as the standard error
can be found.
In parametric boot strapping, the key diÔ¨Äerence is that the bootstrap samples are not
obtained by re-sampling from the original sample. Instead, they are simulated from
the estimated model. This approach relies more on the model; if the model is oÔ¨Äthe
uncertainty estimates will probably be nonsensical.
In this report, as all data used is simulated, the chosen models are accurate. Therefore
only the parametric approach will be employed.
The bootstrap method is very easy to implement, even for the most complex estima-
tors. Additionally, it makes no assumptions about the distribution of the estimator,
unlike when using Fisher information. Its main downside is the computational aspect; if
one wishes K samples of the estimator, it will take K times as long to Ô¨Ånd the variance
of parameter(s) as its actual estimate.

3.1
Another look at the problem
The Markovian arrival process, as it has been used in the literature, is not fully observed.
Most commonly, only the time-intervals between events in a MAP are directly observed.
This is due to the MAP‚Äôs origin in queueing theory, where it has been used as an ar-

phase-type renewals. It is also this correlation structure that, among other things, makes
estimation in a MAP especially diÔ¨Écult [BKF14]. Estimation methods for even more
diÔ¨Écult observation schemes of a map have also been treated in the literature [BK10].

direct observation of the underlying Markov jump process allows the use of the Markov
property, which simpliÔ¨Åes the correlation structure in the data signiÔ¨Åcantly. Apart from
the events, the Markov process is also sampled at discrete time points, providing even
further information. The estimation problem at hand is therefore easier than usual for

3.3
Informative sampling times
When Ô¨Åtting a model to longitudinal data with random observation times, it is impor-
tant to consider the reasons why an observation was made at a certain time-point. In
many applications, the sample times are not Ô¨Åxed before-hand; they may be chosen de-
pending on the state of the process, or they may slightly deviate from a Ô¨Åxed observation
scheme due to certain factors. From a theoretic point-of-view, the sample times t1, . . . , tn
are actually random variables and should be modeled along with the state-observations.
The simplest case is when the joint likelihood of the observation times and the states,
L(x1, . . . , xn, t1, . . . , tn), is proportional to the likelihood of only the state observations,
where the observation times are seen as Ô¨Åxed. When this is the case, the sampling times
are said to be non-informative and they can thus be ignored in the modeling.
This is basically the same consideration that needs to be made when dealing with miss-
ing data; the fact that an observation (or a covariate) is missing may actually give some
information about the value of the missing observation. In practice when dealing missing
data, the data is assumed to be missing at random (MAR), i.e. the mechanism whereby
data goes missing is independent of the value of the missing observations.
In the same way, sampling times could be informative of the trajectory taken between
two samples. Whether or not a sample time is informative needs to be considered on an
application-by-application basis.
In survival analysis, four diÔ¨Äerent types of observations were identiÔ¨Åed in [GKS91].
These are
‚Ä¢ Ô¨Åxed: Each process (patient) is observed at times Ô¨Åxed before-hand.
‚Ä¢ random: The observation times are random, but independent of the MJP X(t).
‚Ä¢ doctor‚Äôs care: The doctor chooses the sampling time dependent on the current
state; severely ill patients are more frequently examined.
‚Ä¢ patient self-selection: Patients choose to visit the doctor, dependent on their cur-
rent disease state.
The conclusion of the article was that the sampling times for Ô¨Åxed, random and doctor‚Äôs
care type of observations are non-informative, whereas patient self-selection is informa-
tive. Certain multi-state models in survival analsis where the sample times cannot be
assumed non-informative have been analysed, see for example [CYC10].

The EM-algorithm for
Discretely Observed MAPs
Due to the ease of estimating the intensity matrices for a fully observed MAP, the EM-
algorithm is a natural method to estimate the parameters of one observed in discrete
time. The discretely observed data is seen as a partial observation of the entire Markov
process. The task is then to estimate the complete data from the incomplete. An EM-
algorithm for estimation in discretely observed Markov jump processes was developed
in [BS05]. The model examined there can be considered a special case of the model
examined here, where Q1 = 0 and only one process was observed. Several variants of
the EM-algorithm for the generic MAP can be found in the literature [BKF14, Ch. 5],
[BK10], [Bre02], [Ryd96].
The EM-algorithm presented here is a hybrid between that presented in [BS05] and
an EM-algorithm for a MAP, modiÔ¨Åed to account for the additional information.
The EM-algorithm requires a speciÔ¨Åcation of the complete-data likelihood function.

6.1
BrieÔ¨Çy on Bayesian statistics
Within statistics, one talks of a dichotomy between the frequentist and the Bayesian
paradigm. These paradigms are based on the same underlying mathematics, but they
rely on diÔ¨Äerent philosophical assumptions that signiÔ¨Åcantly alters the way statistical
inference is done in practice. In short, frequentists consider a probability a long-run
frequency in a given experiment, whereas Bayesians interpret probability as a subjective
belief that a variable will take on a particular value.
The Bayesian interpretation is less restrictive in that it allows probability distributions
to be given to a larger number of quantities. In particular, unknown but Ô¨Åxed quantities,
such as parameters in statistical models, are in the Bayesian paradigm seen as random
variables in their own right that can be given a probability distribution. [Gel+14]

6.2
Markov chain Monte Carlo methods
Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is the most popular collection of methods for solving
the Bayesian inference problem. They work by sampling from the posterior distribution,

6.3.4
Connections to the EM algorithm
Parallels can be drawn between the Data Augmentation algorithm and the EM algorithm.
The E-step of the EM-algorithm and the I-step of the Data Augmentation algorithm are
clearly related: In the I-step, the suÔ¨Écient statistics are simulated given the observed
data. In the E-step, the expectation of the suÔ¨Écient statistics are calculated. The M-
step and P-step both provide a current estimate of the parameters of interest.
These steps can be mixed to create diÔ¨Äerent variants. The E-step of the EM-algorithm
could be replaced by the I-step. This would produce a so-called Monte Carlo EM al-
gorithm. The most time-consuming part of the EM-algorithm is indeed the calculation
of the expectation of the suÔ¨Écient statistics. Simulation may be faster, although many
simulations should be performed, then averaged, to obtain decent accuracy.
Another possible variant is the so-called Bayesian EM algorithm. In this, the E-step
is unchanged, but in the M-step, the mode of the posterior distribution is found. Under
suitable conditions, this algorithm will give a point-estimate of the Bayesian Maximum
aposteri (MAP) estimates. Bayesian methods are usually focused on estimating the en-
tire posterior distribution, but when point-estimates are available, they can be used as
diagnostic tools for checking whether an algorithm has converged. [MK07]

Up until this point, all models examined have been governed by an underlying Markov
jump process. For continuous processes, the Markov assumption implies exponential
sojourn times in the states. While the exponential distribution is very widely used, it
has one major weakness. Let T be exponentially distributed, then the following applies
P(T > t + s | T > s) = P(T > t).
(8.1)
This is called the memoryless property, and this property is what makes the exponential
distribution especially analytically tractable. In the context of MJPs, this means that
the probability of exiting a state in some time-interval is independent of how long the pro-
cess already spent in the state. This assumption is unrealistic in many applications: For
the Markov-illness-death model with recovery, it means that the probability of dying or
recovering from the diseased state within a given time-period is independent of how long
an individual has been sick. For many diseases, this is not an appropriate assumption
[TS10]. This chapter introduces the semi-Markovian arrival process (SMAP): A generali-
sation of the MAP that allow any sojourn time distribution. A particular class of SMAPs
are focused on, namely those with phase-type sojourn times, because estimation in this
class is manageable and can be done with the computational methods already developed.
The key idea is that the observed process X(t) is governed by a latent MAP X‚àó(t)
with an expanded state-space. The MAP with an expanded state space can be seen as
the latent process in an HMAP, where the SMAP is the observed process. This formu-
lation allows the use of the computational methods from Chapter 7. This approach is
described in more detail in Section 8.3. First, the proper framework is introduced.

butions encompass a large class of phase-type distributions. The method for converting
a phase-type distribution into its canonical form is given in [Cum82].
The implications of these two results are very important. The problems of over-parametrisation
and lack of uniqueness can be overcome by only considering Coxian distributions with
ordered states. Doing this does not severely limit the Ô¨Çexibility of the phase-type dis-
tribution. Phase-type distributions are in fact highly Ô¨Çexible and can approximate any
positive distribution arbitrarily well [Cox55].

Results on simulated data
Most of the described algorithms have been implemented in R. In this chapter, they are
used on simulated data. The purpose is to retrieve the parameters used in the simulation,
if this is possible. How the algorithms perform compared to each other is of particular
interest, both in terms of speed and accuracy. Initially, the methods from Chapter 4-6
are considered. An example with misclassiÔ¨Åcation and Coxian distributed sojourn times
follow later in Section 9.3
9.1
Performance issues
For the EM algorithm, the calculation of the integrals involved in the E-step is by far
the most computationally demanding task. They are calculated using the uniformization
approach described. The matrices of suÔ¨Écient statistics should be calculated and stored.
For equi-distant data, the matrices can be reused. For fully equidistant data, the suÔ¨É-
cient statistics need only be calculated once per iteration. The inclusion of events slows
down one iteration signiÔ¨Åcantly, because these cannot be equi-distant. Thus an entire
set of expectations of suÔ¨Écient statistics has to be calculated for every event observed.
In this way, the EM-algorithm scales with the number of time-steps in the data. The
number of observations is nearly irrelevant to the speed, so long as these are equi-distant.
The recursive formulas for both the simple matrix exponential and the integrals should
be used. This will ease the burden of non-equidistant data.
Direct optimisation is by far the easiest of the methods to implement. The same con-
siderations mentioned for the EM-algorithm apply to the likelihood evaluation. The
gradient has not been implemented here. The evaluation of the gradient is quite time-
consuming, but its implementation may lead to fewer steps being needed to converge.
The most time-consuming part of the Gibbs sampler is simulating the Markov pro-
cess through the time steps. In this manner, the speed with which one iteration can be
performed depends on the length of the time-interval over which the process is observed.
The inclusion of non equi-distant data is irrelevant. Markov processes with long sojourn
times are faster to simulate, because in these, less jumps need to be simulated.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
This thesis had the goal of developing estimators for the parameters in a Markov jump
process, when it had been observed both discrete and continuous-time. Methods for
handling this are known from survival analysis. It was described in some detail how
a suitable framework for this problem is the Markovian arrival process. A serious of
analytical considerations were made that resulted in a more general likelihood function
than found in the survival analysis literature. An important result was the connection
between event-type observations and phase-type distributions. Using this result, the
EM-algorithm from [BS05] could be mixed with generalisations of procedures known
from the estimation of phase-type distributions, creating a hybrid algorithm capable of
handling this mixture of data.
This was also a theme when applying MCMC techniques. Event-type observations could
be accounted for by generalising a sampling algorithm from the phase-type literature.
This again allowed for extending the approach of [BS05] to also deal with continuous-
time data.
Methods were looked at to relax the Markov assumption. If the state of the under-
lying MJP is only observed indirectly, the Markov property vanishes. The resulting
model is a continuous-time hidden Markovian Arrival Processes (HMAP). The link to
phase-type distributions was not quite as clear, but nevertheless, results from the liter-
ature were extended to account for event-type observations for the likelihood function
and the E-step in the EM-algorithm. Finally, it was shown how the class of semi-Markov
process with phase-type sojourn times can be seen as a special type of HMAP, allowing
the same estimation algorithms to be used.
With regards to maximum likelihood estimation and the implementations provided here,
the EM-algorithm vastly outperformed direct optimisation, both in convergence speed
and numerical stability. It is however stressed that the inclusion of the gradient in the
direct optimization as described in Chapter 5 could change the picture. With regards
to phase-type estimation, it was found in [BEN11] that there was no reason to prefer
one of the algorithms over the others, and this could also be the conclusion reached here
upon inclusion of the gradient.
The EM algorithm was also faster than the MCMC approach. This is not a fair compari-
son however, because the Gibbs sampler gives a representation of the entire distribution
of the parameters, whereas the EM algorithm only gives a point estimate. A more fair
comparison may be MCMC vs. EM + Bootstrap. Here, the MCMC is much faster, and
gives comparable standard deviation estimates. Thus, the MCMC approach looks quite
==PAGE:78==
68
10 Conclusion
promising. In [Ryd+08], EM + Bootstrap was compared to MCMC, and the conclusion
was also that MCMC showed the most potential.
In the standard software used in survival analysis, such as the MSM package for R
[Jac+11], only direct optimization of the likelihood function is employed. Perhaps the
described alternatives should be explored.

Further topics
This section describes some further topics within the Ô¨Åeld that have not been adressed

10.1.1
MCMC for HMAPs
The chapter on hidden Markovian arrival processes only developed the likelihood func-
tion and the EM algorithm. There is no apparent reason why the MCMC approach could
not be extended to HMAPs as well. Indeed, there are several examples of MCMC being
applied successfully to Hidden Markov models in the literature [Ryd+08]. The most cru-
cial part of a Gibbs sampler would be to develop an algorithm that could simulate the
underlying Markov Jump process given the indirect data. This has been done in [Chi96]
for a discrete-time hidden Markov model, and the generalisation to continuous-time hid-
den Markov models with discrete observations is most likely straightforward. However,
to incorporate event-type observations would probably require a diÔ¨Äerent approach. A
Metropolis-Hastings type algorithm may be suitable.
10.1.2
Goodness-of-Ô¨Åt tests
Methods to assess the goodness of Ô¨Åt of Markov models to data are naturally very use-
ful. One method to check the Markov assumption is to Ô¨Årst a MAP to the data, then
an HMAP or an SMAP. These can then be compared using a log-likelihood ratio test.
In [BS09], a hypothesis test was developed to determine whether or not two generator
matrices were identical. This can be used to check the homogeneity assumption.
A more formal approach to checking the goodness of Ô¨Åt, in the form of a Pearson-
type goodness-of-Ô¨Åt test, was given [AF02], but only for discretely observed data. The
method was extended to Hidden Markov Models with one absorbing state, where the
absorption time is known exactly in [TS08]. Extending this approach to MAPS could
be interesting.

10.1.3
The inclusion of covariates
When Ô¨Åtting a Markov model across many subject, such as done in a clinical study, it is
probably unreasonable to assume the transition intensities are constant across patients.
This eÔ¨Äect can be controlled for by describing the transition intensities in terms of

Throughout this thesis, the homogeneity of the Markov process has been implied. This
can be a limiting assumption. A very popular model in survival analysis for modelling
rates (particularly hazard rates) is the semi-parametric cox proportional hazards model

for the lack of time-dependency. However, in Semi-Markov processes, the hazard is only
