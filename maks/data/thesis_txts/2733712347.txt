Abstract 
Renewable energies are gaining more and more attention in the energy paradigm, in 
order to alleviate the effects of climate change. Nevertheless, one of the main barriers is 
their intermittency, which difficults coupling the energy production with the demand. This 
problem does not only happen at a utility scale but also at a residential level. That is why 
households may decide to install PV panels with batteries to mitigate the mismatch 
between the PV production and their consumption. 
This Master Thesis aims to investigate the current market situation of grid-connected 
residential photovoltaic (PV) installations with battery storage in Spain, encompassing 
the available commercial models of batteries and inverters for this type of hybrid 
installations. As accurate system performance estimates are crucial before consumers 
invest in these technologies, the accuracy of three distinct modeling software tools is 
evaluated by comparing their results against real-world data. Additionally, the economic 
viability of installing photovoltaic systems with batteries in Spain is assessed under 
different scenarios, including location, consumption, subsidies, battery technology and 
electricity prices. 
Spain is an advantageous country for the installation of photovoltaic systems, not only 
because of the high radiation it receives compared to the rest of Europe but also the 
government is promoting support schemes in order to encourage people to install PV 
systems with batteries.  
The literature research has revealed that the most common battery chemistries 
implemented with PV systems are lithium-ion batteries (lithium iron phosphate or lithium 
nickel manganese cobalt oxide batteries) and lead acid. Although the market research 
and the feasibility analysis indicate that lithium-ion batteries are more suitable/profitable 
than lead acid. The most common energy management strategy is maximizing the self-
consumption (by minimizing the energy taken from the grid) of the consumer as this will 
increase his independency from the grid, resulting in an electricity bill reduction. 
Regarding the modelling tools, among the three software evaluated (PVSyst, SAM and 
PV Sol premium), it has been concluded that the least accurate when estimating the 
state of charge (SOC) of the battery and the power at which it is charged and discharged 
has been PVSyst, SAM and PV Sol Premium gave better and more similar estimates. 
No major differences have been noted in the results of the software for the rest of the 
variables analyzed (irradiation in the plane of array (GPOA), module temperature, PV 
production or inverter output). 
Finally, through the different feasibility studies performed in Spain, it has been 
determined that it is more profitable to include batteries along with photovoltaic panels in 
locations with high radiation, when the home's consumption is higher outside of daylight 
hours and even more if in during these periods the electricity rates are higher. Lead acid 
batteries have not been so interesting from an economic point of view since despite they 
are cheaper, they have to be replaced every year and a half, while although lithium-ion 
batteries are more expensive they only have to be replaced every 10 years. It has also 
been proven that government incentives that reduce battery investment result in better 
economic metrics, allowing to install batteries with higher capacities, since with current 
prices, at the moment it is only profitable to install small batteries (1 kWh).

1 Introduction 
1.1 Current global energy panorama 
The world's population is growing rapidly, and this is putting a strain on energy resources. 
In addition, advances in civilization, such as the development of new technologies and 
the growth of the middle class, are also increasing global energy needs. 
Fossil fuels are not a sustainable source of energy. They are finite resources that are 
being depleted at an alarming rate. In addition, the combustion of fossil fuels releases 
greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, which contribute to climate change. Climate 
change consequences include among others changes in weather patterns, spread of 
diseases, sea-level rise, changes in the ecosystem… [1]. Despite these problems, fossil 
fuels remain the main source of energy in the world [2]. 
Additionally, the world is facing a major energy crisis, triggered by Russia's invasion of 
Ukraine. The crisis is causing energy prices to soar and is disrupting supply chains, which 
is having a significant impact on businesses and consumers around the world [3]. 
To address the climate crisis, most countries have committed to reducing their 
greenhouse gas emissions and increasing the share of renewable energy in their energy 
mix. This commitment is reflected in international agreements such as the Paris Climate 
Agreement, COP21, the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, and Europe's 
2030 energy plan [4]. The efforts to promote green energy solutions as well as the 
expected depletion of fossil fuel reserves in the future has placed renewable energy and, 
in particular, solar and wind energy in the forefront of technological and societal interest 
[5]. 
Despite the progress made in renewable energy research, there are still many 
challenges to overcome before we can achieve a fully renewable energy future. Three 
key areas for improvement are increasing conversion efficiency, reducing material costs, 
and improving dispatchability [6]. 
 
1.2 Residential PV in Spain 
Given the worldwide trend of high electricity prices, new regulations and government 
subsidy programs, consumers are gradually betting on including renewable energy 
installations in their homes, mainly solar thermal energy and/or photovoltaic energy, for 
self-consumption purposes. Hence, reducing their dependence on the grid. 
Particularly, the promotion of photovoltaic solar energy for self-consumption in Spain is 
due to a series of factors: 
• 
Spain is a country with a warm and sunny climate. Its location in southern Europe 
gives it a high number of hours of sunshine, with an average of 300 days of 
sunshine per year. In Madrid, the average radiation is 3.39kWh/day/m2 [7].This 
climate makes it an ideal place for solar energy generation [8]. 
• 
In 2018, the Spanish government repealed the solar tax, which taxed the 
production and consumption of energy generated through photovoltaic

installations. This measure was an incentive for solar self-consumption, as 
consumers no longer had to pay for the energy generated by their own 
installations [8]. 
• 
In 2019, the government published Royal Decree 244/2019, which regulates the 
administrative, technical, and economic conditions for solar self-consumption 
installations [9].  
• 
Excess energy generated by solar self-consumption systems can be 
compensated in a simplified way, based on the difference between the energy 
consumed and the energy generated in the billing period [10]. 
• 
Administrative permission for grid connection is no longer required for

• 
From a financial perspective, the government has allocated 660 million euros to 
aid for the execution of various incentive programs linked to self-consumption 
and storage. The subsidy varies from 15% to 45% depending on specific case 
[10].  
• 
The cost of solar PV panels has fallen significantly in recent years, making them 
more affordable for homeowners. 
In fact, the highest energy consumption of a home in Spain is electricity, with 43% of the 
total energy consumption of the home, which is also why it is one of the first expenses 
that families seek to cut [11]. 
According to the annual report of APPA1 there was in 2022 installed , 2,649 MW of PV 
capacity designed for local consumption in Spain, of which 39% of the power 
corresponded to residential installations (1,024 MW) and 61% to industrial projects 
(1,625 MW). Since 2018, the annual installed power for self-consumption in Spain has 
grown at an average rate of more than 90% each year. In total, the installed power of 
self-consumption in Spain at the end of 2022 amounts to 5,211 MW [12].

Further to this, the Government published in 2021 a self-consumption roadmap which

higher, reaching up to 14 GW [9]. 
 
1.3 Self-consumption limit in residential PV systems 
As it was briefly mentioned earlier, one of the main drawbacks of the PV technology is 
its unmanageable intermittency. Generally, solar energy is produced during sunny hours. 
The more perpendicular the sun's rays hit the solar panels, the greater the energy 
production is. This is why solar panels produce the most energy around midday, when 
the sun is at its highest point in the sky. At dawn and dusk, the sun's rays hit the solar 
panels more obliquely, so energy production is lower. During the night, in the darkness, 
there is no solar production. Likewise, if a cloud covers the sun, the incident radiation 
decreases, thus also reducing the energy output. 
What happens on a practical level is that the solar production curve does not usually 
coincide with the home consumption curve, as can be seen on Figure 2.

The surplus solar energy would be injected into the grid at a lower price than the 
electricity imported from the grid when the solar production is not sufficient to cover the 
power demand. Therefore, to maximize the financial benefits of distributed grid 
connected photovoltaic  systems with rate structures that discourage the export of 
excess PV generation, it is important to minimize the export of electricity generated by 
these systems [14]. 
Therefore, the consumer strives for maximizing self-consumption, which is understood 
as the ratio between the solar energy consumed over the total solar energy produced. 
This would reduce the energy imported from the grid, reducing the amount of your 
electricity bill. 
One option for consumers is to try to align their energy consumption as much as possible 
with the solar production curve. However, this is not always possible. Another more 
innovative alternative would be to provide the system with some type of energy storage.  
 
1.4 Increasing self-consumption in residential PV systems with 
energy storage  
As introduced in the previous section, coupling the residential PV installation with a 
storage system would allow the surplus energy to be stored when solar production is 
greater than demand, to be used later when solar production does not cover demand. 
Thus, increasing the self-consumption ratio. 
==PAGE:18==

Figure 3: Typical solar PV and storage charging/discharging Schedule [14]. 
According to Figure 3, the peak electricity generation from a solar PV system occurs 
during midday. Nonetheless, households typically experience their highest electricity 
consumption during the morning and early evening. Electricity usage within households 
tends to be lower in the midday period, especially for families that are away during the 
day. As a result, a substantial portion of the electricity generated by the solar panels is 
often surplus and is sent back to the electricity grid. Therefore, adding batteries to the 
residential PV systems increases the self-consumption of the household. 
Although there are other methods to maximize solar self-consumption, such as smart 
controls, thermal storage using electric hot water heaters, and dynamic electric vehicle 
charging; batteries are the primary method being used by developers today [14].  
This type of installations, customer-sited stationary storage systems that are connected 
to the distribution system on the customer’s side of the utility’s service meter, are 
denominated “behind the meter” (BTM) [15]. 
Grid-connected residential photovoltaic systems with battery energy storage consist of 
different elements, PV modules, batteries, and inverter. Two different connection 
schemes can be distinguished [16] [17] [18]: 
• 
DC coupled: The PV modules and batteries are connected to an inverter with 
two DC inputs and one AC output. This type of inverter is known as hybrid 
inverter, and it is bi-directional. The AC output of the inverter can be used to 
supply the home or to inject electricity into the grid. 
• 
AC coupled: The battery is connected to behind the meter as an independent 
AC unit. On the solar panel side, there is a one-way solar inverter. On the battery 
side, there is a bi-directional battery inverter. This connection scheme is more 
complex than the DC coupled and leads to higher losses, although the main 
advantage is that it allows to add batteries to an already existing PV system.

In this case, the thesis will be focused on DC coupled systems since the idea is to directly 
design a hybrid system with PV modules and batteries, whereas AC battery systems are 
more geared towards retrofitting an existing PV system, and are less common on the 
market, as will be shown in chapter 3. 
 
1.5 Modelling of PV systems with energy storage  
Nowadays the importance of using simulation tools for the study and dimension of PV 
system with or without battery storage is undeniable, since system performance is a 
direct driver of project cash flows, which heavily influence the system's overall worth. 
Therefore, to guarantee the success of photovoltaic systems, it is essential to accurately 
model their operation before installation, especially if they include batteries which are an 
expensive and complex device. In the end, modelling software are crucial to mitigate the 
financial risk associated with investing in these type of systems [19]. Some of the benefits 
of using simulation tools include: 
- 
Determine the solar energy available at each moment according to the radiation 
of the place. 
- 
Allow to deal with the complexity of the chemical reactions of batteries and their 
==PAGE:20==
Performance modelling and feasibility analysis of grid-connected residential 
photovoltaic systems with battery energy storage in Spain 
7 
translation into electrical terms. 
- 
They allow the system to be optimally sized taking into account the user's 
consumption profiles. It also allows determining the energy flows between the 
different elements of the system (modules, batteries, grid, loads) in each period 
of time. 
- 
The user can use a simulation program without needing to know the complex 
physical calculations behind the model, they are user-friendly. 
- 
High repeatability allows all calculations to be carried out again after having made 
some modification to the input parameters. 
- 
Long-term calculations taking into account system degradation. 
- 
Fast calculation speed with high precision. 
Overall, modelling software allow to first design the system and then sized it, once the 
simulation is run the output normally includes a performance analysis and economic 
viability of the project [20]. 
Over the past two decades, the solar energy industry has developed and refined a suite 
of modeling tools to support the work of practitioners, among the more established 
products are PVSyst, HelioScope, SAM, PlantPredict, Aurora, PV*SOL, and CASSYS 
[19]. However, not all these PV modelling tools allow to include batteries within the

1.6 Problem formulation 
One of the primary challenges associated with residential PV systems is the temporal 
mismatch between solar production and household energy consumption. This mismatch 
can lead to excess energy production during the day when solar radiation is abundant 
and a deficit during the evening and nighttime hours when demand is higher. This 
problem may be reduced by including batteries to store the excess energy and save it 
for later when the solar production is not enough to meet the user’s demand. However, 
batteries are an expensive and relative new component, with complex operating 
principles, and it is crucial to accurately estimate the benefit gain in energy terms to 
consider this in an economic study, to finally determine whether the decision would be 
profitable or not. Therefore, the tools selected for assessing the system energy 
production and power flows are a key step in this process. Finally, from a feasibility point 
of view it is also interesting to identify which factors may affect the profitability of the

system, such as energy consumption profile, location of the system, electricity prices, 
…This will be useful to determine under which circumstances it would be more 
interesting to invest in batteries.

1.8 Project limitations 
The main limitations faced in this project have been encountered when simulating a real 
PV installation with batteries located in Denmark and evaluating the results obtained by 
each software in comparison with the real values measured in the installation. On the 
one hand, it was necessary to define the actual installation from a technical point of view. 
Here the first assumptions have been made, since the real degradation of the 
photovoltaic panels was not quantified, nor the soiling factor, nor the exact percentage 
of electrical losses or mismatch losses.

estimation. While in this project the comparison could only be made for three days, of 
which the battery was only connected for two days, and they were also November days

As explained previously, a PV system with batteries usually consists of the PV modules, 
the batteries, the inverter, and an energy management system. This section will explain 
each of these components in more detail, except for the photovoltaic modules, which are

3.1 Battery fundamentals 
A battery is an electrochemical device that can store energy in the form of chemical 
energy, which, in the case of rechargeable batteries also known as secondary batteries, 
means that it can convert chemical energy in electric energy and vice versa by means of 
a redox reaction. Batteries consist of different components [41]:  
• 
Positive electrode (cathode during the discharge): It is the electrode where 
electrons flow into the battery during discharge. It is the oxidizing element of the 
cell, which allows the absorption of electrons from an external electrical circuit, 
reducing itself while the electrochemical reaction is taking place. 
• 
Negative electrode (anode during the charge): It is the electrode where 
electrons flow out of the battery during discharge. It is the reducing element, 
which allows electrons to be delivered to an external electrical circuit, oxidizing 
while the electrochemical reaction is taking place. 
• 
Electrolyte: It is the conductor that provides a means of transferring ions 
between the electrodes within the cell, this element may be found in liquid and 
solid form. 
• 
Separator: It is an ion-permeable, electronically non-conductive material or 
spacer that prevents the short-circuiting of the positive and negative electrodes 
of the cell. 
• 
Terminals: Elements that allow the connection of the internal electrical circuit of 
a cell, with an external electrical circuit. They are current collectors. 
• 
Container: Element that allows to efficiently and safely group all the components 
mentioned above, it is important because it protects the other elements from 
possible deterioration due to environmental conditions.

Battery technologies vary significantly in terms of their energy and power densities, 
energy efficiencies, cycle-life, accessibility, and operational requirements. Several

3.2 Batteries technologies 
The most used battery technologies in combination with residential PV systems are lead-
acid batteries and lithium-ion batteries [44]–[46]. Both types of batteries are suitable 
since they can be operated under the so called “cycle operation”, which means that the 
battery normally goes through complete charge and discharge cycles. 
 
3.2.1 Lead-acid batteries 
The battery is filled with a dilute solution of sulfuric acid (H2SO4). The negative electrode 
is made of lead, and the positive electrode is made of lead oxide (PbO2) [44]. The 
charging and discharging phases of a lead-acid battery work as follows: 
Charging phase: When a lead-acid battery is charging, the lead electrode is oxidized, 
and the lead oxide electrode is reduced. This reaction produces sulfuric acid and water, 
which replenishes the electrolyte in the battery. This reaction is nonspontaneous and 
requires an input of energy to proceed. The charging process is typically done first at 
constant current until the charging end voltage is reached. Then, the battery is charged 
by applying a constant voltage, until it is fully charged [44]. 
Discharging phase: When a lead-acid battery is discharging, the lead electrode is 
reduced, and the lead oxide electrode is oxidized. This reaction consumes sulfuric acid 
and produces water, which dilutes the electrolyte in the battery. The reaction proceeds 
spontaneously and releases energy in the form of electricity [44]. The discharging 
==PAGE:32==

photovoltaic systems with battery energy storage in Spain 
19 
process is typically done at a constant current, and the voltage will decrease as the 
battery becomes more discharged. Continued discharge of the battery can lead to a deep 
discharge, which can damage the battery's internal components. 
There are two main types of lead-acid batteries: open (vented) and valve-regulated. 
Valve-regulated batteries include absorbed glass mat (AGM) and gel batteries. The most 
commonly type used in grid-connected PV systems are the valve-regulated because of 
their lower maintenance [47].

3.2.2 Lithium-ion batteries 
In this case, the positive electrode is made of a lithium-containing compound, such as 
lithium cobalt oxide (LiCoO2), and the negative electrode is made of carbon, such as 
graphite. The electrolyte is a solution of lithium salts in an organic solvent [44]. 
Charging phase: When a lithium-ion battery is charging, lithium ions move from the 
negative electrode to the positive electrode through the electrolyte. This reaction requires 
an input of electric energy. Same as before, the charging process follows the CCCV-
procedure (Constant current / constant voltage) [44]. 
Discharging phase: When a lithium-ion battery is discharging, lithium ions move from 
the positive electrode to the negative electrode through the electrolyte. This reaction 
releases energy, which is used to power the device that is connected to the battery [44]. 
The discharging process, same as for the lead acid battery, is typically done at a constant 
current, and the voltage will decrease as the battery becomes more discharged. Deep 
discharge can shorten the lifespan of a battery and make it more likely to fail. 
There also different types of lithium-ion batteries depending on the material used in the 
anode: Lithium cobalt oxide (LiCoO4), Lithium manganese oxide (LiMn4O4) , Lithium 
nickel manganese cobalt oxide (LiNiMnCoO4 or NMC), Lithium iron phosphate

3.3 Hybrid inverters 
A hybrid solar inverter is a device that combines the functions of a solar inverter and a 
battery inverter. It can convert the DC electricity from solar panels into alternating current 
AC that can be used by your home, and it can also store excess electricity in batteries 
for later use [28]. 
A traditional solar grid-tie inverter only converts DC electricity from solar panels into AC 
electricity. A battery inverter only converts DC electricity from batteries into AC electricity 
(or converts AC into DC ) . However it is not designed to extract maximum DC electricity 
from solar panels as PV inverters are [28]. 
The main features of hybrid inverters include bi-directional power conversion, power 
regulation, power monitoring and power maximization [28]. 
There are also different types of hybrid inverters [18], [57]: 
• 
Off-grid hybrid solar inverter: This is the most basic type of hybrid solar 
inverter. It can store solar energy in a battery and is normally used in standalone 
or off-grid systems.  
• 
Multimode hybrid solar inverter: it offers different operation modes, such us 
==PAGE:35==
Performance modelling and feasibility analysis of grid-connected residential 
photovoltaic systems with battery energy storage in Spain 
22 
grid-tie mode, which is like a normal solar inverter without a battery; hybrid mode, 
which allows to charge the battery with surplus energy and discharge it when 
necessary; back up mode, under this the inverter operates as a normal inverter 
connected to the grid, but in case of grid outage it can still provide backup power 
and the system can continue operating, provided it has metering info at the point 
of grid connection. Additionally, some inverters may also offer an off-grid mode 
for installations not connected to the grid. 
• 
All-in-one Battery Energy Storage System (BESS): is a new type of hybrid 
solar inverter that includes batteries and an inverter in a single unit. It can be 
easily adapted to any existing solar system. 
• 
Advanced AC coupled system: This is a type of hybrid solar inverter that is 
used with AC batteries (consist of lithium battery modules, a battery management 
system (BMS) and an inverter in one compact unit). AC batteries are easier to 
use than DC batteries, but they are less efficient. Advanced AC coupled systems 
can improve efficiency by using multiple hybrid solar inverters.

3.4 Energy management systems 
An energy management system (EMS) is a system that manages the flow of energy in a 
power system. In residential applications, an EMS usually has four main functions: 
forecasting, data acquisition/analysis, human-machine interface and optimization and 
control [67]. 
The EMS will manage the system, especially the charging and charging of the batteries, 
based on the established objectives and defined constraints. Generally, The EMS can 
schedule and optimize the operation of the system to meet technical constraints, based 
on the forecasts of PV generation, load consumption, and energy market prices [67]. 
In this section the main operating modes offered by some of the previously mentioned 
hybrid inverters’ manufactures are reviewed. 
For example, the hybrid inverters SUN2000-L1 (single-phase) and SUN2000-M1 (three-
phase) manufactured by Huawei offer three different working modes for the battery 
control, which are maximum self-consumption, time of use and fully fed to grid [68]. 
Growatt offers different modes of operation, within the normal mode of operation it offers 
three different possibilities [69]:  
• 
Load first: it always seeks to cover the demand first with PV generation, then 
with stored energy from the batteries and if this is not enough with energy from 
the grid. Batteries are charged when PV production exceeds consumption. This 
mode maximizes the self-consumption rate. 
• 
Battery first: the system will prioritize charging the battery, from the  PV panels 
and grid (if enabled). This mode is suitable for periods when electricity prices are 
low, so that users can save money by charging their battery during these times 
and using the stored energy when electricity prices are high. It is convenient to 
define operating hours for this mode. 
• 
Grid first: the system will prioritize feeding PV energy into the grid. This mode is 
suitable for periods when electricity prices are high, so that users can earn money 
by selling their excess PV energy to the grid. The user must configure the mode's 
schedule. 
Finally, it has been seen that the PV Master app of GoodWe, also offer several operation 
modes for hybrid inverters [70]: 
• 
General mode: similar to the load first strategy of Growatt. 
• 
Forced off-grid mode: the system automatically disconnects from the grid, even 
if the grid is available. 
• 
Back up mode: The battery is only used to power critical loads during a grid 
outage. 
• 
Eco mode: similar to the general mode. 
• 
Peak shaving mode: to avoid getting power from the grid above the grid power 
limit, the battery will discharge. If the household consumes less electricity than 
the import power limit during the set time period, the battery will be charged from 
the grid to reach lower power import imit.

System Advisor Model, widely known as SAM, is a free and open-source software tool

According to this algorithm every time step the system evaluates the PV production and 
the load demand and compares them. If the PV output is greater than the load, the 
battery can get charged with the PV output, if allowed, and if more energy is still required 
to charge the battery and this option is enabled, it will get energy from the grid to get

to supply the deficit, if allowed, and if grid charging is allowed the battery will get power 
from the grid once it reaches the minimum SOC. If the battery cannot get discharged to 
cover the load it will get charged with the grid in the case this option is enabled.

not violate.

demand and charging the battery, the extra surplus will be injected into the grid.

with the input grid power targets discharge option should be selected, enabling that the 
battery charges from the system and that the battery discharges only when the load 
exceeds the system output and disenabling that the battery can charge from the grid. 
The input grid power target should be 0 W, which will minimize the power taken from the 
grid by discharging the battery, when possible, every time step that the load is greater 
than the input grid power target, which at the same time maximizes the self-consumption.

4.3.1 Modelling flow and EMS strategies 
The dispatch priority which regulates the power flows is the following [75]: 
1. Direct consumption: Electricity generated by the PV system is used 
immediately to cover the household's consumption. 
2. Battery discharge: If the PV system is not generating enough electricity to cover 
the household's consumption, the batteries will be used to provide the remaining 
electricity. The batteries will continue to discharge until they reach their minimum 
state of charge (SOC). 
3. Grid cover: If the batteries are empty, the household will draw electricity from 
the grid. 
4. Battery charge: If the PV system is generating more electricity than the 
household is consuming, the excess electricity will be used to charge the 
batteries. The batteries will continue to charge until they reach their maximum 
SOC. 
5. Grid feed-in: If the batteries are fully charged and the PV system is still 
generating excess electricity, the excess electricity will be fed into the grid. 
6. Maintenance charge: If there is not enough PV energy available to charge the 
batteries, the batteries can be charged from the grid using boost, full, or 
equalization charging methods. These charging methods are used to maintain 
the batteries in good condition.

5.2 Sensing and data acquisition of the study case system 
The different measurement systems and sensors that monitor the installation can be 
divided in two groups: the DTU measurements and the Fronius measurements.  
On the one hand, the DTU measurements are measured with a 10 second time period 
by a NI CRIO datalogger and logged in a DTU SQL Server database. These 
measurements include: 
• 
In plane irradiance (EKO MS-40S pyranometer). 
• 
PV module backside temperatures of the array (Tm-RS485-MB). 
• 
PV input voltage and current (using voltage dividers and shunt resistors). 
• 
Battery voltages and current of the system (using voltage dividers and shunt 
resistors) 
• 
AC output of the systems using Janitza UMG 604-PRO power analyser. 
A voltage divider consists of two resistors connected in series, and the output voltage is 
taken across the lower resistor. This arrangement allows the circuit to 'divide' the input 
voltage into a smaller fraction that is within the range of the datalogger [80]. 
A shunt is a low-ohm resistor that can be used to measure current by connecting it in 
series within the current line and a datalogger is measuring the voltage across the 
resistor. The entire current flows through the shunt and generates a voltage drop, which 
is then measured. Based on Ohm's law and the known resistance, the measured voltage 
drop can be used to determine the current [81]. 
On the other hand, the Fronius measurements are performed by the Fronius monitoring 
system with a sampling period of 5 minutes and uploaded to the Fronius Solar Web 
portal, this includes: 
• 
Inverter own measurements, such us the PV production, battery power, load 
consumption, DC and AC currents and voltages of the 2 DC inputs and the three-

Finally, the maximum resolution attainable in the results generated by the three software 
programs is hourly. Consequently, both the meteorological and consumption data utilized 
as input parameters for the simulations and the variables employed to evaluate the 
simulation outcomes will be represented as hourly averages. Each hourly average would 
be calculated for the time interval extending from xx:00 to (xx+1):00, resulting in an 
average value corresponding to the time xx:30. The software use the beginning of the 
interval (xx:00) both when entering data and displaying results, but the average value 
entered/calculated is for the entire interval (xx:00 - (xx+1):00). This means that the 
average value represents the value at the midpoint of the interval, which is xx:30.

5.3.8 Overall software modelling accuracy conclusions 
In general, the three tools provide accurate estimates of the system's variables, with 
minor discrepancies as noted earlier, except for the battery power (and consequently the 
SOC and AC power output) in the case of PVSyst. This is likely due to the fact that 
PVSyst's battery model has not yet been internally assessed/validated. In contrast, SAM 
and PV Sol Premium produce results that closely align with the measured data. However, 
to definitively determine which software is more precise between SAM and PV Sol 
Premium, a more comprehensive study would be necessary, involving a longer period of 
analysis and detailed information about module degradation and other losses. Hence, 
the subsequent chapter, feasibility analysis, could be conducted using either of the two 
software. Nevertheless, given that SAM offers a parametric analysis tool for optimizing 
system configuration, SAM has been selected as the preferred option.

investment has generated losses, whereas a positive ROI is representative of a profitable 
project. The higher the ROI the higher the profitability. 
 
6.3 Financial parameters 
For the economic evaluation is necessary to define several economic/financial 
parameters such capital expenditures (CAPEX), operational expenditures (OPEX),

Regarding the CAPEX, the PV modules average prices has been estimated on 0.40 
€/Wp based on the web page of Leroy Merlin, a bricolage store [82]. The average price 
for inverters is 0.345 €/W according to the data gathered in Table 3. The cost of LPF and 
NMC batteries has been estimated on 692 €/kWh and 643 €/kWh, respectively, 
according to Table 2, whereas the lead acid battery price is estimated in 211 €/kWh 
based on different sources [23], [55], [63]. Furthermore, it is crucial to consider the design 
and installation costs in conjunction with the commercial pre-offer received from Naturgy, 
which can be found in Annex 9.12, Therefore, according to the pre-offer, these costs, 
excluding the inverter and module expenses, result in 1.095 €/Wp.  
The cost of residential PV installations not including batteries is around 1.4-2 €/Wp [83]. 
Hence, the cost considered for this study (1.84 €/Wp) is in the medium-high range of the 
current market values. 
As per the OPEX, according to Naturgy’s pre-offer the maintenance would be around 
11.5 €/month resulting in a 138 € annual fee. It is also necessary to account for the 
inverter cost replacement, the offered warranty for the chosen model is 10 years [84], 
therefore, in the economic analysis an inverter replacement will be considered every 10 
years. Additionally, the life span of the batteries should also be considered. Lithium ion 
batteries have a useful life of 3500 cycles whereas lead acid batteries have a useful life 
of 500 cycles [23], which will result in a replacement every 10 years approximately in the 
case of lithium ion batteries and a replacement every year and 5 months in the case of 
lead acid batteries, considering that, in general terms, batteries run a full cycle every day.

7 Conclusions 
In this project, first an analysis of the Spanish market has been carried out regarding the 
installation of BTM batteries together with photovoltaic installations. Next, the precision 
of SAM, PVSyst and PV Sol Premium has been evaluated by simulating a real 
photovoltaic system with battery storage located in Denmark and comparing the results 
obtained with the real data collected from the installation. Upon determining that SAM 
and PV Sol Premium have resulted to be the most accurate software, SAM was finally 
used to conduct the feasibility analysis. In this analysis, several simulations have been 
carried out to determine how different factors may influence the economy viability of 
including batteries with the photovoltaic installation. The main conclusions that can be 
drawn from this master thesis are: 
• 
In general terms, the estimates of the three software in terms of GPOA, 
temperature and photovoltaic production are similar. However, the most precise 
software when simulating photovoltaic installations with batteries have been SAM 
and PV Sol Premium. PVSyst has proven to be the most inaccurate when 
estimating the power used to charge or discharge the battery or the SOC, which 
may be because the battery model has not yet been validated internally by 
PVSyst.  
• 
Batteries with small capacities (about 1 kWh) are profitable in cases where the 
main electricity consumption occurs at night, outside of PV production hours. In 
the event that the consumption profile coincides more with the production hours, 
installing batteries together with the PV system is not that interesting. 
• 
Regarding the location of the project, batteries are a better choice in the case of 
central or southern Spain where the yield of the PV installations is higher than in 
the north. 
• 
Although the price per kWh of lead acid batteries is much lower than that of 
lithium-ion batteries, their useful life is much shorter, having to be replaced every 
one and a half year, which results in a considerable high OPEX that makes its 
installation unprofitable in the long term. 
• 
If the consumer tariff has higher prices during the night, which also coincides with 
consumption peaks, it makes the installation of batteries more necessary. 
• 
Finally, it has been proven that with incentives, the price of batteries is 
significantly reduced, thus increasing their profitability. In fact, with a reduction of 
€490/kWh the appropriate battery size turns out to be a considerable size. Battery 
installation is therefore expected to increase in the future as battery prices 
continue to decline. 
• 
Higher ROI results in lower payback periods and lower LCOE. 
• 
A higher self-consumption ratio directly translates into greater net savings and a 
more profitable investment.
