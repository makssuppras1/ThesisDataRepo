Abstract
The increased competitiveness within the manufacturing industry is pushing towards
fast, ﬂexible and economic solutions.
Moreover, the market demands lightweight
components with superior mechanical properties, such as high performance parts made
of hardened steels usually surface-ﬁnished by grinding. Within this context, hard
turning arises as an attracting alternative, owing to the important development of
the machine tools and cutting tools. Its lower cost, higher productivity and more
favourable ecological aspects are pointed out as the main advantages over grinding.
Nevertheless, there are still important issues, namely severe tool wear and surface
quality of the workpiece, limiting its introduction in the industry.
In the present project, both experimental and modelling works are combined. On
the one hand, hard turning process set-up was deﬁned enabling cutting forces, tool
wear and surface quality monitoring. Experimental hard turning tests, needle bearing
rings’ hard machining at diﬀerent cutting conditions, were carried out. On the other
hand, cutting force and two dimensional tool wear models were developed. Oblique
cutting mechanics and tool geometry were introduced in the cutting force model. In
regard to the wear model, required mechanical and thermal models were developed
in order to calculate stress and temperatures at tool-chip/workpiece interfaces.
Modelling results were validated with experimental data. The cutting force model is
able to predict turning forces with a maximum relative error of 20%. In regard to
the tool wear model, the maximum deviations from experimental data lie within a
maximum relative error of 25%. These two models laid the foundations for future
optimization tool providing Ideko and Danobat with an important process knowledge
in order to develop a more robust and optimized hard turning process.

2.1 Metal Cutting
Material removal processes are one of the most applied material shaping methods.
These are usually referred as machining processes. This processes have undergone
through an important development over many centuries. For instance, the huge in-
crease of the automotive industry and start of the mass-production during the 19th
century produced a great advance of the ﬁeld. Later during the 1970s and 1980s, with
the introduction of the Computer Numerical Control (CNC) current concept of ma-
chine tools were developed. Subsequently, huge development of machining processes
came. Currently these processes can be divided in three main groups [6]:
• Geometrically Deﬁned Cutting Edge processes, usually referred as conventional
machining or metal cutting including milling, turning or drilling.
• Geometrically Non-Deﬁned Cutting Edge processes, known also as abrasive
processes such as grinding and polishing.
• Non-Conventional Machining processes, which might use various energy forms
such as thermal, electrical or chemical. Some examples of these processes can be
Laser Assisted Machining (LAM), Electro Discharge Machining (EDM), Elec-
trical Beam Machining (EBM) or Electro-Chemical Machining (ECM).
In regard to metal cutting, highly applied due to high productivity and attainable
accuracy levels, Ivester et al. [7] (data from 2000s) reported that these processes
contribute nearly to the 5% of the GDP in developed countries, e.g. in U.S. equals to
$250B per year. Indeed, other shaping processes such as casting, forging or additive
manufacturing are commonly followed by metal cutting operations in order to get the
ﬁnal shape of the component.
Many diﬀerent machining operations can be distinguished. Starting from the most
common turning, milling and drilling to some more speciﬁc such as broaching, hobbing
or gear skiving. All of them use a cutting tool to remove material from the workpiece
and form chips. Therefore, relative motion between the tool and the workpiece is
required. For instance, during turning operation the workpiece is rotated while the
tool ﬁxed. Nevertheless, in other operations such as milling the tool is motioned by
the spindle. Other operations carried at modern machining centers can combine both,
tool and workpiece rotation.
Although geometry and kinematics may diﬀer, the underlying principle of any metal
machining operation remains the same. All processes are performed with a wedge

has increased signiﬁcantly over decades as ﬁrst illustrated by the Taniguchi curve

by hydrostatic guideways and spindles, in order to avoid "stick-slip" phenomena and
provide good damping properties. For instance, composite-ﬁlled polymer concrete

be found providing stiﬀstructure, high mass inertia and ensuring low thermal dis-

positioning accuracy of 1 µm and slide repeatibility of 0.1 µm [13].
The other essential factor for reliable and highly-productive machining operations is
the cutting tool, in terms of both material and geometry. The progress in material
science has led to high-performance cutting tool materials with increased hardness
and toughness at elevated temperatures. There has been a huge development over
decades since the introduction of High Speed Steels (HSS) at the start of 20th cen-
tury as shown in Figure 2.2. Later, during the ﬁrst mid of the 20th century diﬀerent

2.2.1 Hard Turning vs Grinding
Grinding process is common in high performance parts, however, industry seeks for
increased process ﬂexibility, higher productivity rates and more eco-friendly processes.
Within this context, hard turning arised as an attractive alternative. The possibility
of machining geometrically complex components in a single set-up can be regarded
as the main advantage over grinding [16]. In terms of productivity, higher Material
Removal Rates (MRR) (up to 4-6 times) are obtained. Finally, dry cutting can be
carried out in hard turning with the respective advantage from the ecological point
of view. Nevertheless, as reported by König et al. [17], industry remained skeptical
about hard turning for many years. This could have been attributed to the lack of
knowledge and uncertainty of the process.
Thereby, diﬀerent review works about
hard turning were provided by the academic society [18, 17, 16, 19, 8, 15].

the choice between hard turning and grinding needs to be determined for each single
application based on the ﬁnal workpiece requirements and speciﬁc context. Within
this context, Klocke et al. [15] tried to evaluate qualitatively the hard turning and
grinding process capabilities using diﬀerent case studies and providing a capability
map shown in Figure 2.3.
In terms of surface roughness, comparable and in some cases even better results have
been obtained by hard turning. Average surface roughness (Ra) below 0.5 µm and Rz
less than 2 µm have been reported [15]. Moreover, another important surface charac-
terization parameter is Rsk indicating surface skewness. Surfaces having more valleys
than peaks result in negative Rsk value with better ﬂuid retention and higher bear-

than ground ones as indicated by [15]. Regarding the fatigue properties, M’Saoubi et
al. [20] reported higher fatigue resistance of hard turned parts using unworn tools in
comparison with ground components.
Despite the important advances in the ﬁeld of hard turning, there are still steps to
be carried out for an increased reliability of the process since scrap parts in hard
turning cause important economical loss.

2.2.2 Tool Wear & Surface Integrity in Hard Turning
Due to the high hardness of the workpiece material, tool wear is one major problem
during turning hardened steels [21]. As CBN tools provide acceptable performance in
hard turning due to their high hot-hardness, they are the preferred choice for hardened
steel machining. High speed hard machining takes advantage of CBN high hardness
at elevated temperature. Owing to the thermal load, nowadays CBN tools are usually
coated with PVD ceramic layers in order to reduce diﬀusion wear and friction force
[22]. Due to the almost complete transformation of the mechanical energy into the
thermal at the shear plane, thermal softening of the workpiece occurs. This phe-
nomena is so-called "self-induced hot machining" [16]. Depending on the application,
high (up to 90%) or low content (around 50%) CBN tools are recommended [23].
High cost of these tools in comparison to ceramic or carbide cutting tools requires
optimized process plans with high productivity rates and reliability [21, 24]. Thus,
wear mechanisms present in CBN tools have been widely studied [24, 25, 21, 26, 27].
The presence of diﬀerent wear mechanisms in metal cutting is widely known, due to
extreme thermomechanical conditions at the tool-chip/workpiece interfaces. Gener-
ally, wear is described by ﬁve diﬀerent wear mechanisms: abrasion, adhesion, fatigue,
diﬀusion and tribo-chemical reactions [24]. Wearing of cutting tools depends on many
diﬀerent aspects, such as, tool and workpiece material, cutting geometry, cutting con-
ditions or the working environment. Despite diﬀerent wear patterns can arise, crater
and ﬂank wear are the most controllable during high speed hard turning as shown in
Figure 2.4. Thereby, tool life during these operations is determined by either ﬂank
wear land width (VB) or crater wear depth (KT).

Typically initial breakdown with a rapid ﬂank wear increase is observed during hard
turning as indicated by Poulachon et al. [22]. Afterwards, ﬂank wear becomes con-
stant while crater wear appears due to abrasion mechanism.
As the crater wear
progresses, rake angle of the cutting tool increases weakening the cutting edge. This
might lead to chipping of the cutting edge or even to catastrophic failure. Therefore,
hard turning requires strong cutting edge with chipping resistance, provided by cham-
fered tools with negative rake angle and edge honing. Apart from the direct tooling
cost, tool wear can directly aﬀect the ﬁnal workpiece quality. Despite usual tool wear
criterion for conventional turning is deﬁned as 0.3 mm ﬂank wear [28], a general tool
life criterion for hard turning should also consider surface quality requirements [26].
Indeed, surface integrity of hard turned components has attracted the interest of nu-
merous authors. It is widely reported the fact that both grinding and hard turning
can induce microstructural, hardness and residual stress changes in workpiece sur-
faces [29, 17, 15]. This is produced due to the thermomechanical loads exerted at the
surface. In regard to the microstructural changes, White Layer (WL) and Dark Layer
(DL) are found in many material removal processes other than hard machining, such
as, grinding [30] or EDM [31, 32]. These layers are result of short-time metallurgical
transformations.
WL, few microns thick, results from the microstructural modiﬁ-
cation of the martensite, and it appears white after etching when looking through
the Light Optical Microscope (LOM) as shown in the Figure 2.5. This ﬁrst layer is
usually followed by a thicker (around 20-40 µm) DL which consists on overtempered
martensite. The appearance of WL-s are reported under diﬀerent cutting conditions
and tool wear levels.
In terms of mechanical properties, WL-s produced at diﬀerent conditions diﬀer. Even
though all show higher hardness than the bulk material (from 8 to 10 GPa [33]), WL-s
produced due to high thermal input, with increased retained austenite content, show
high brittleness and susceptibility to cracking [15]. Hence, they are unwanted and
considered as a damage for the component. On the contrary, martensitic ﬁne grain
white layers due to the mechanical eﬀect show still high hardness, lower brittleness and
excellent corrosion resistance. Thereby, this WL type can be considered advantageous

Other essential characteristic for the evaluation of surface integrity of the workpiece
is the distribution of residual stresses due to the machining process. This aspect have
attracted the interest of multiple studies for many decades as reviewed by Jawahir et

hard turned parts are high performance components, thus, surface residual stresses
are highly important. In case high thermal input to the workpiece, occurring at high
cutting speeds and/or when using worn tools, tensile stresses arise at the surface.
This could lead to detrimental fatigue performance or even cracking which is highly
unwanted. However, when the predominant eﬀect of WL formation is heavy plastic
deformation at low cutting speeds and unworn tools, compressive stresses to a depth
around 200 µm are produced. In this case, fatigue life of hard turned components is
higher than for ground ones according to Matsumoto [36] and M’Saoubi [20]. These

2.3 Modelling in Metal Cutting
Diﬀerent advances in machine tool, cutting tool technology or process set-up require
predictive modelling tools to integrate them within virtual process planning systems
and deﬁne optimal cutting conditions, lubricant, etc; and avoid laborious and time-
consuming experimental testing procedures. Within this context, modelling shows a
tremendous potential. However, industry is interested in readily applicable process
performance measures, such as, tool-life, surface ﬁnish, subsurface integrity, chip-
form/chip breakability, burr formation, part accuracy, etc. as outlined by Arrazola
et al.
[45].
Nevertheless, machining processes are rather complex phenomena to
model and simulate.
The main challenges are generally referred as severe plastic
deformation of the metal and the extreme tribological conditions present at the tool-
chip/workpiece interfaces [1, 46]. First, basic models, where cutting mechanics and
thermal behaviour of the metal cutting operation are described, become essential.

Secondly, industry-relevant outcomes are the main objective that need to be out-

diﬀerent modelling approaches can be adopted such as analytical, numerical, empir-
ical and hybrid (see advantages/disadvantages in Table A.1 in Appendix A.1). Due
to the higher computational capability and diﬀerent advances in computational tech-

mesh with the resulting higher computational times are required for accurate results.
Moreover, FEM modelling remains still as a "black-box". Final results are highly
inﬂuenced by among others, friction law, material constitutive law or Taylor-Quinney
coeﬃcient being these still challenging to assess. Regarding the empirical approaches,
they can be considered as the simplest methods, yet only applicable to the tested
range. Due to their negligible computational time and strong physical foundation,
analytical modelling has attracted high interest during decades.
2.3.1 Shear Plane
Orthogonal cutting has dominated the research in modelling of machining operation
owing to its relative simplicity. Extensive amount of works are found on chip formation
models (see some in Figure 2.9) in the literature. In order to carry out predictions
of cutting forces and temperature in the cutting zone, chip formation models are
essential.

model does not consider the eﬀect of strain, strain-rate and temperature on the mate-
rial property. Later, Lee and Shaﬀer [49] presented the ﬁrst slip-line model applied to
metal cutting, which served to lay the foundation for future works. One of the most
important contribution among this ﬁeld was developed by Palmer and Oxley [50] since
they introduced the parallel-sided primary shear zone (see Figure 2.9b) introducing

2.3 Modelling in Metal Cutting
2.3.2 Cutting Forces
Regarding the cutting forces, diﬀerent approaches may be found. Mechanistic em-
pirical modelling has been widely adopted. This is based on a concept of chip load.
Cutting forces are measured and speciﬁc cutting forces are obtained in each direc-
tion, dividing the total forces by the chip load. The main drawback of these models
is the need for developing an extensive database of speciﬁc cutting force values ob-
tained through experimental tests for each cutting tool geometry, diﬀerent cutting
conditions and workpiece material combination. Moreover, extrapolation of speciﬁc
cutting forces to diﬀerent cutting conditions or tool can lead to inaccurate results due
to the lack of physical meaning. However, rapid implementation and avoiding complex
chip formation models makes empirical modelling attractive to industry people.
Aiming at overcoming these limitations, uniﬁed cutting mechanics was ﬁrst addressed
by Armarego [55], known also as classical oblique model integrating both main cutting
forces (shear and friction) according to the shear plane model and edge forces. Main
advantage over the mechanistic method was the possibility of implementing into a
broad range of cutting tool geometries and machining operations requiring only few
experimental tests. Basic variables (shear & friction angle and shear ﬂow stress of the
work material) to deﬁne oblique speciﬁc cutting forces are obtained from orthogonal
cutting test following the method presented by Budak et al. [56]. In order to calculate
the cutting forces, cutting edge is discretized and local forces are calculated. Total
cutting forces are computed summing the contribution of each element.
Nevertheless, reliability of oblique cutting models are heavily inﬂuenced by the chip
formation model, hence, shear and chip ﬂow angles. Armarego’s empirical approach
assummes Stabler’s rule [57] of equivalent chip ﬂow and inclination angles. This sim-
pliﬁcation might introduce error in force predictions, thus, diﬀerent analytical models
arised. Usui [58] developed a cutting force model in which chip ﬂow angle was pre-
dicted based on minimum energy piling up orthogonal cuttings with same shear angle
and rake angle. Shamoto and Altintas [59] proposed a novel approach to compute
analytically both shear and chip ﬂow angles combining maximum shear stress and
minimum energy principles from Krystof and Merchant respectively. However, nei-
ther of these cutting force models could handle thermal softening and strain-hardening
eﬀects on the workpiece material. Thereby, based on Oxley’s early work diﬀerent ther-
momechanical models were developed using work material constitutive model. Budak
and Ozlu [60] implemented these models using Johnson-Cook constitutive model for
oblique cutting to predict cutting forces in milling operations.
Moufki and Moli-
nari [61, 62] proposed a novel thermomechanical model applied to turning forces
considering tool geometry and global chip ﬂow. Nevertheless, accuracy of this ther-
momechanical modelling techniques rely on the material constitutive law and friction
models. Currently, as reviewed by Melkote et al. [46], developing these models for
metal cutting is still a major challenge due to the diﬃculty acquiring stress–strain
and friction data that can represent precisely the cutting process without the need of
data extrapolation.

2.3.3 Temperature Fields
Temperature modelling is also essential since surface integrity and tool wear are di-
rectly governed by the thermal loads. In terms of analytical thermal modelling, Hahn’s
work [63] introduced the moving band heat sources to predict temperature ﬁeld in
metal cutting, a general approach that has been adopted by all subsequent analytical
solutions. Later Komanduri and Hou [64, 65, 66] published an extensive review work
and proposed an enhanced analytical model considering heat generations at both pri-
mary and secondary shear zones in addition of integrating the heat partition ratio
at the tool-chip interface. This analytical approach was employed and improved by
diﬀerent authors [67, 68, 69] including the rubbing heat source due to the ﬂank wear.
On the other hand, diﬀerent numerical methods were implemented.
Lazoglu and
Altintas [70] and Ulutan et al. [71] have modelled successfully machining tempera-
tures in three-dimensional operations using Finite Diﬀerence Method (FDM). Several
eﬀorts were made using FEM simulations for temperature modelling, though large
discrepancies are found among each others, concluding that output results are highly
inﬂuenced by ﬂow stress model of the work material, friction model at the tool-chip
interface and/or applied computational techniques [72].
2.3.4 Tool Wear
Finally, tool wear modelling is becoming a relevant topic for the industry in regard to
minimizing machining costs and process optimization [45]. Tool wear has been a topic
of interest for the academic society from the early days due to its multi-phenomena
nature. Taylor’s publication [73] was the pioneering work on the ﬁeld, more than
a century ago, presenting a semi-empirical tool life equation. Even though most of
literature concerning tool wear in machining was based on empirical analysis, some
analytical tool wear models were developed during the last decades. Usui et al. [74]
proposed an analytical ﬂank and crater wear models using normal stress and tem-
peratures as model inputs and considering abrasive and adhesive wear mechanisms.
Following the same approach, Huang and Liang [75, 76] proposed analytical equations
for crater wear depth and ﬂank land width computation in orthogonal hard turning.
In this later model, diﬀusion was introduced as third wear mechanisms due to the
presence of high temperatures during machining of hardened AISI 52100 and aﬃnity
of the CBN tool binder with the steel. The increased activity in FEM modelling has
enabled simulation of tool wear for coated complex shaped tools [77, 78] as shown in
the Figure 2.10. Nevertheless, the required long computational time is still a huge
obstacle for the implementation of these numerical methods.

2.4 Critical review of the SOTA
To conclude, increased interest and activity is found in regard to hard turning dur-
ing the last decades. Important developments in terms of machine tool performance
and cutting tool material and geometries have enabled machining hardened steels at
competitive productivity rates. Nevertheless, methodologies for characterizing hard
turning processes and more eﬃcient optimization tools are required by the industry.
It is necessary to identify process windows in order to deﬁne optimum cutting con-
ditions and have an adequate tool wear control ensuring a desired ﬁnal workpiece
quality. Based on the literature review, generally hard turning has been addressed
independently by either experimental or modelling point of view with the respective
limitations above-mentioned. Thereby, the present project intends to combine exper-
imental and predictive modelling approaches in order to develop a complete process
characterization tool adressing cutting conditions, tool wear and work part quality.

3.1.3 Thermal Model of Orthogonal Cutting
Temperature at tool-chip and tool-workpiece interfaces is a dominating factor on the
tool wear evolution since all wear mechanisms are highly aﬀected by the thermal
input. Thereby, it is necessary to introduce a thermal model as indicated in Figure
3.1. Diﬀerent attempts on temperature ﬁeld modelling are found among literature.
In this case analytical approach is adopted from [67, 68], based on Komanduri’s [64]
modiﬁed moving heat band source and considering the following assumptions:
• Cutting mechanics are described by orthogonal model using Merchant’s single
shear angle model shown in Figure 3.2.
• Thermal solutions are steady-state assumming stable chip formation process.
• All deformation energy is converted into heat, not considering Taylor-Quinney
coeﬁcient. Heat loss along the interfaces and tool, chip and workpiece surfaces
are considered negligible.
• The tool is assummed a thermally semi-inﬁnite medium.
• Primary, secondary and tertiary heat sources are plane heat sources. Secondary
heat source as well as chip formation variables (e.g. shear angle or chip contact
length) are not aﬀected by crater wear.
• A cutting wedge angle of 90◦is considered to simplify the problem. Moreover,
sharp cutting edge is assummed without the presence of neither built-up-edge,
cutting edge radius or chamfered edge.

3.2 Cutting Force Model
The second part of the modelling work of the present project concerns the cutting

this is usually limited to research and academia environments. Thereby, cutting force

3.2.1 Oblique Cutting Mechanics
Due to the complex geometry of the current cutting tools, which are optimized for
an improved chip evacuation and longer tool life among other purposes, accurate
cutting force model is a challenge.
Generally, turning tools are given the normal
rake angle (αn) and edge inclination angle (λs) (see Figure 3.21). Moreover, these
tools are comprised with the main and side cutting edges linked by the nose radius,

which inﬂuences cutting forces and improve surface roughness [1]. Owing to this,

Hence, it is essential to understand cutting mechanics of oblique machining operation.
Unlike in orthogonal cutting shown in Figure 3.2 and described by Merchant’s single

plane but perpendicular to the cutting edge. Moreover, the shear plane is deﬁned by
the normal shear angle (φn) and oblique shear angle (φi) as indicated in Figure 3.22.

Experimental Work 4
In order to characterize the hard turning process and validate the tool wear model,
experimental work was carried out. Prior to the cutting tests, the process set-up and
procedure were deﬁned and work material characterization was done.
4.1 Workpiece, Turning Process and Tooling
Aiming at hard machining a workpiece with industrial relevance, needle bearings’s
inner ring was chosen; standardized IR 50×58×40 ring with the outer diameter of 58
mm, inner diameter of 50 mm and 40 mm length. These are high performance parts
for the automotive industry with demanding quality requirements. Currently, these
rings are ground after the hardening treatment, hence, hard turning of these parts is
attracting high interest [15]. This experimental hard turning tests were carried out
in the precision lathe Danobat LT-400 with hydrostatic spindle and guideways and
granite bed. Regarding the machining operation, OD turning along the whole part
was carried out. In each test the initial outer diameter of 58 mm was reduced down
to 53.9 mm with diﬀerent amount of passes depending on the cutting conditions.
The clamping of the inner ring was done from the inside, using a mandrel collet
chuck - Röhm Cartridge Mandrel MFR-04 - shown in Figure 4.1a (see more details in
Appendix C.1).

Conclusions 6
In this project valuable characterization work of hard turning process was done. First,
a suitable process set-up in terms work parts, tooling, ﬁxturing and measuring equip-
ment was deﬁned. Hard turning process set-up with high degree of process monitoring
was developed, providing the opportunity to analyze all tool wear, surface quality and
cutting forces evolution.
It was demonstrated that conventional approach of determining tool-life criteria by
speciﬁc ﬂank wear land width (VB) of 300 µm and/or average surface roughness value
is not suitable for hard turning. Due to the demanding quality requirements of the
ﬁnal workpiece, all tool wear, surface quality or cutting forces ﬂuctuations need to be
considered for an optimum tool life criteria in hard turning.
Regarding the experimental results, surface roughness values lower than 0.5 µm and
2 µm were obtained in terms of Ra and Rz. These values are similar to those reported
in literature [15], and in some applications can replace grinding operations. It was
observed that the most important aspect aﬀecting the surface topography of the ﬁnal
workpiece is the tool wear. Hence, highly controlled tool wear is essential. In respect
to the cutting conditions, the feed rate would have to be determined based on the
desired surface requirements.
For instance, in order to obtain the surface quality
required by the presented needle bearing inner ring, listed in Table 5.2, ﬁnal pass is
recommended at 0.05 mm/rev feed. In addition, the cutting speed is found to be
the predominant cutting condition concerning the tool wear evolution, due to its high
inﬂuence on the ﬂank and rake face temperatures. Thus, correct choice of cutting
conditions is relevant for a successful hard turning operation.
Thereby, the modelling work carried out during this project is a valuable tool for
cutting conditions optimization. On the one hand, cutting force models estimates
forces within 20% relative error, while in principal and feed directions the error is
reduced down to 6%.
Although during this project the use of the force model is
limited to check the reliability of literature data, many options arise. Indeed, cutting
force modelling is still a hot topic in the research ﬁeld since machine tool behaviour
and the ﬁnal workpiece quality are highly inﬂuenced by them. On the other hand,
tool wear model is able to predict ﬂank wear with a maximum relative error of 25%.
This makes possible the quantiﬁcation of diﬀerent cutting tool conditions on the tool
wear without the need of extensive and time-consuming experimental tests. Moreover,
once the tool wear eﬀect on the surface quality of the workiece is characterized, as
in Figure 5.6, it is possible to quantify the maximum allowable tool wear in order to
identify the preferred cutting conditions.

Future Lines 7
Being hard turning a topic of interest for Ideko and Danobat Group, there is still great
room for improvement with numerous opportunities for research and experimentation,
based on the generated knowledge.
First, the tool wear model, which is limited to two dimensions, is reccommended to be
revised. One of the shortcomings of wear model is the thermal aspect, since lower ﬂank
temperatures than those reported in literature were observed. In addition, the thermal
model is highly simpliﬁed neglecting the tool geometry and oblique cutting mechanics.
Hence, it is suggested considering the tool nose geometry and oblique nature of the
cutting process for the computation of heat sources at the three deformation zones.
In addition, the presented mechanical and thermal models can be used to compute
the thermomechanical input into the workpiece. It is widely known that these ther-
momechanical loads aﬀect the surface integrity of the of the workpiece in terms of
hardness, microstructure and distribution of surface residual stresses. These aspects
are highly important for the functional behaviour of the ﬁnal workpiece, hence, it is at-
tracting high interest at the present times. Therefore, cutting conditions optimization
process should consider these later aspects. Hence, it is suggested the development
of a surface integrity model to assess the ﬁnal workpiece quality prior the deﬁnition
of the cutting conditions using as groundwork the cutting force and thermal models
developed during this project. The suggested complete optimization tool concept is
illustrated in Figure 7.1.
