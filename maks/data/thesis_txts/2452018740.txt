Abstract
The eT program is a joint effort between NTNU, Norwegian University of Science and Tech-
nology, and DTU to develop a coupled cluster code which is 100% molecular orbital-based.
The current version of the program serves as start platform for the development of an ef-
ficient multi-level coupled cluster program. In addition to total energies, the program will
offer calculation of molecular properties via response functions theory. This project concerns
the development of the linear response framework, which comprises the implementation of
both an iterative subspace algorithm to solve frequency-dependent coupled cluster linear
response equations, and a general driver for coupled cluster linear response and coupled
cluster equation-of-motion second-order properties. Both transition strengths and frequency-
dependent electric dipole polarizabilities were implemented for equation-of-motion coupled
cluster singles and doubles.
Within coupled cluster linear response, transition strengths
and frequency-dependent electric dipole polarizabilities have been so far implemented at the
coupled cluster singles level of theory. Illustrative results are provided to demonstrate the
applicability of the coupled cluster approaches for calculating molecular properties.

Introduction
Theoretical models for simulation of molecular properties can aid the interpretation of experi-
mental results. The combination of the experimental and theoretical disciplines provides fun-
damental understanding of the experimental observations and facilitates the extraction of the
desired structural information [1]. The interpretation of X-ray absorption spectra is an example
of an area where the interplay can be beneficial, and with advances on the experimental side,
several models and methods for computations of X-ray absorption spectra have been developed,
see e.g. [2–14]. Among the many correlated electronic structure models, the coupled cluster
(CC) ansatz [15–18] offers accurate ab initio calculations through its hierarchies of CC models,
which systematically improve the inclusion of dynamic electron correlation [1, 19, 20]. Popular
hierarchies include coupled cluster singles (CCS), coupled cluster singles and approximate dou-
bles CC2, coupled cluster singles and doubles (CCSD), coupled cluster singles and approximate
triples CC3, coupled cluster with singles, doubles, and triples excitations (CCSDT), and, for
static properties, the coupled cluster singles and doubles scheme augmented by a perturbative
treatment of triple excitations (CCSD(T)) [21–27].
Computationally advantageous expressions for molecular properties for exact theory and approx-
imate methods can be obtained from response theory [28–31]. Response functions have been
derived and implemented for many different wave function (and density functional) methods,
including several CC models [1], in which the linear response function allows the calculation of
second-order properties [32–35]. CC linear response has produced accurate results for excita-
tion energies and polarizabilities [34, 36–38]. Another quite successful approach for calculating
molecular properties based on a CC parametrization is the equation-of-motion coupled cluster
(EOM-CC) method [39–44]. Even though EOM-CC property expressions have historically been
derived following a different strategy than in CC response theory [39], it has been shown that
EOM-CC can be recast as a modified form of CC response [45, 46]. This is the strategy used to
implement EOM-CC second-order properties in the present work.
As key element of this thesis, a CC linear response and EOM-CC framework has been imple-
mented in the coupled cluster program eT [47]. An iterative subspace algorithm was implemented
for solving frequency-dependent linear response equations, whose solutions are needed for the
calculation of second-order properties (including their residues). Some improvements in the pre-
conditioner of the eigenvalue solver for excitation energies and excited state vectors were also
implemented. Illustrative results were produced from both EOM-CC and CC linear response
calculations.
The studied properties are one-photon excitation transition strengths and static and frequency-
dependent electric dipole polarizabilities. The performance of EOM-CC in comparison to CC
linear response is (re-)examined for the dynamic polarizability of N2, the geometry-dependent
static polarizability of a water molecule, and the production of size-intensive transition moments
for systems of non-interacting LiH molecules. Additionally, the EOM-CC approach is applied
to simulate core excitation spectra at different K-edges of a few small to medium size organic

This thesis is organised as follows. Key equations from CC theory are provided in Chapter 2, with
Section 2.2 describing the CC linear response and EOM-CC approaches for molecular properties
within the CC framework. Chapter 3 describes the CC code eT, the program building blocks
and code structure. In Chapter 4, the subspace algorithm and its driver implemented as part
of this project is described in detail. Results from the illustrative studies are presented and
discussed in Chapter 5. Section 5.1 is a study of the frequency-dependent polarizability of the
N2 molecule, in which the results obtained from EOM-CC calculations with eT are compared

polarizability of a water molecule is examined in Section 5.2, again with a comparison between
EOM-CC and CC linear response at the coupled cluster singles and doubles level of theory. A
re-investigation of the size-intensivity of the dipole transition strengths of non-interacting LiH
molecules is described in Section 5.3. The two remaining studies are combined in Section 5.4 and
address the simulation (and interpretation) of near edge X-ray absorption fine structure spectra
for the cis and trans isomers of azomethane and for a set of organic molecules containing amide
moieties, for which experimental results were available. Final remarks and conclusions are made
in Chapter 6.

2.2
Molecular (response) properties within coupled cluster theory
Molecular properties may be evaluated using the response function approach. Response theory
as developed in the 1980s [51] – where response functions are subjected to a pole and residue
analysis – has been applied successfully to calculate a large variety of molecular properties. A
formulation of coupled cluster response theory along the lines of Ref. [51] was presented by
Koch and Jørgensen [52]. In the late 1990s, the quasienergy approach [31, 35] tied response
function theory closely to the energy derivative techniques in time–independent theory [53]. Us-
ing the quasienergy approach, response functions could be straightforwardly derived for both
variational and non-variational wave functions. The quasienergy approach proved particularly
advantageous for the re-derivation of coupled cluster response theory [35]. An extensive review
of the advances up to 2012 in the calculation of molecular properties using wave function meth-
ods was presented in Ref. [1].
Without aiming at being exhaustive, the next paragraphs summarise the essential definitions
and working equations to obtain second-order properties and one-photon spectra within coupled
cluster linear response theory and equation-of-motion coupled cluster at the CCSD level.

3
eT – a Coupled Cluster Program
The eT program is a fully independent molecular orbital-based coupled cluster program with
expected release primo 2020. An interface to E. Valeev’s Libint C++ integral package version
2.4.2 [55] is applied for solving the atomic orbital (AO) integrals. At present, the major goal is
to present a quantum chemistry program that is computationally competitive with respect to
other available software offering the same features. The current form of the program will later
serve as a platform for further development into an efficient multi-level coupled cluster [56–58]
program. eT is written in modern Fortran consistent with the 2008 standard and exploits object-
oriented programming. Object-oriented programming allows for polymorphism and inheritance,
resulting in a code with maximized re-usability and flexibility as well as a better organisation
of the code, if done correctly.
3.1
Code structure
The eT code consists of three main classes (objects): wave functions, solvers, and engines that
collaborate within the framework of a main program. The layers of the program are illustrated
in Figure 3.1 The main program is parted in two: one reference calculation and one CC calcu-
lation. In its two sections, the main program initiates two objects: a wave function object and
a calculation engine object. The identity of both objects are given on input.
The engine is responsible for directing the solver object(s) and facilitates the collaboration be-
tween the wave function and solver object(s) to produce the desired results. The solvers are
objects driving an algorithm to solve the problem at hand. The solver is driving either a Pulay
direct inversion of the iterative subspace (DIIS) [59, 60] algorithm or a Davidson subspace algo-
rithm for solving eigenvalue problems [61]. A solver object contains solver-specific routines and
calls solver-general routines via objects called solver tools. Solver tools offer problem-specific
and task-independent functions. These tools are only applied locally in the individual solvers
and are therefore not considered as main class objects.
Wave function-specific routines and variables are contained in the wave function object. This
object is better interpreted as a wave function model and not the wave function alone. The wave
function objects exploit inheritance. CCSD has features and functions in common with CCS
and can therefore inherit these by being defined as an extension to CCS. The same applies to
CC2. Functions that have the same functionality, but where the explicit content may change,
can simply be overwritten in the CCSD and CC2 objects by a modified function with the same
name as in the CCS class.
eT offers Hartree-Fock (HF) or unrestricted Hartree-Fock (UHF) as reference state for the CC
calculations. In the reference calculation, the reference ground state solver communicates with
an AO manager object. This object is the only part of eT which makes use of the external Libint
package for integral calculations. When a reference calculation is completed, the AO integrals

The interplay between the objects is best described through an example.
Example: CCSD excited state with a HF reference state
The main program allocates a reference calculation engine and a HF wave function object which
is passed to the engine. A subroutine reads the input to determine which algorithm is requested
for solving the ground state, say, SCF-DIIS. A solver object of type SCF-DIIS is allocated. Now,
the solver object applies the identity of the reference wave function and its associated functions
to solve the ground state equations. Once the reference calculations are completed and the
reference calculation engine has cleaned up redundant files and variables, the main program
continues to the second block, specifically the CC calculation.
The requested feature is read from input. In this example, the task is to solve for excited states.
Thus, an excited state engine is allocated along with a CCSD wave function object. The CCSD
wave function object is initiated. In this process, the object extracts information from files writ-
ten by the reference ground state solver and engine. The CCSD wave function is then passed
to the excited state engine. First, a CC ground state solver is allocated and run, the algorithm
type determined on input or default. Then, a CC excited state solver is run. The excited state
solver has access to the parameters calculated by the CC ground state solver, since these are
saved in the wave function object. The excited state results are printed to output within the
solver, and the program has ended.

response solver implemented as part of the present work.

Implementation Work
The code written in this project involves the implementation of a linear response solver, the
calculation of electronic transition strengths and electric dipole polarizabilities, and construction
of the overall property engine. eT can currently offer the calculation of EOM-CCS and EOM-
CCSD transition moments and frequency-dependent polarizabilities. The framework to compute
the same properties with CC linear response is built but is not fully implemented due to an
incomplete implementation of the F-transformation at the CCSD level. The present chapter
provides a brief description of the property engine and a thorough description of the implemented
linear response solver algorithm in terms of the steps of an iterative subspace algorithm. We
refer to Chapter 3 for a description of the overall program structure of eT.
4.1

The property engine implemented in eT directs all solvers necessary for performing the calcula-
tion of the desired property. CC wave function parameters from ground state, excited states, and
multipliers calculations are involved in property calculations. The engine directs the following
solvers to obtain the necessary parameters:
- CC ground state: amplitudes, tµ

of properties, since the calculation of neither transition moments nor polarizabilities are an

increasing αyy at R = 1.10 Å and 1.25 Å, respectively, and decreases from 0.23 a.u. to 0.13 a.u.

surfaces indicate that the two methods, although not numerically identical, capture the same

5.3
The curious case of the size-intensivity of the transition strengths
in LiH clusters
Molecular properties are either size-extensive or size-intensive. Extensive properties scale with
the size of the molecular system, while intensive properties are independent of size [70]. Within
25
==PAGE:36==
molecular properties, excitation energies and transition moments are examples of size-intensive
properties. Koch et al. calculated transition moments for systems of one to five non-interacting
LiH molecules from the CCSD linear response function and compared them to results obtained
from EOM-CCSD calculations [54].
Their results showed that the linear response approach
produces size-intensive dipole transition moments while EOM-CCSD does not. In fact, their
results showed a very poor performance of the EOM-CCSD approach, where an extrapolation
of the transition strengths revealed that an unphysical negative transition strength would be
obtained at 19 molecules. We re-investigated the behaviour of the EOM-CCSD dipole transition
strengths when the system is expanded. Systems of one to five non-interacting LiH molecules
were built for the assessment, with an internuclear distance of 4 a.u. within each molecule as in
Ref. [54].
Regrettably, in the study of Ref. [54], it was not specified how the LiH molecules were arranged
with respect to each other, nor the distance between them. However, from the way the results
are presented (cf. ’a sequence of LiH molecules’), it would appear that the z-axis is the leading
molecular axis. Moreover, the authors write that they used the DZ basis set, but this turned
out not to be available for Li. We used therefore Dunning’s DZ for H and Dunning’s TZ for
Li. With this choice, we could reproduce both the ground state energy −7.987 a.u. and the first
excitation energy of the lowest total-symmetric state 4.355 eV as reported in Ref. [54].
In Table 5.4, the results we obtained for the excitation energies, right and left transition moments,
and total dipole strengths are summarised at high precision. Due to numerical instabilities, we
could not selectively choose one given excitation on a given LiH molecule, nor converge the
energy any tighter than shown in Table 5.4. However, for each group of molecules we obtained
the expected number of (almost) degenerate excited states, two for the dimer, three for the
trimer, and so on. The EOM-CCSD and CCSD linear response left transition moments are, for
each specific cluster, identical, whereas the right ones are different. For the monomer, EOM-
CCSD and CCSD linear response yield, nonetheless, almost identical results. When more than
one LiH is considered, the individual moments and strengths of the degenerate excited states are
different from each other, and different from the values of the monomers. However, summing
the individual strengths of the degenerate states for each cluster, and dividing by the number
of LiH molecules in the given cluster, we obtain exactly the same strengths obtained by Koch
et al. in Ref. [54].
These “averaged” dipole strengths are plotted in Figure 5.11.
The plot
exactly reproduces the trend reported in Ref. [54] to illustrate the lack of size-intensivity of the
transition strengths of the EOM-CCSD approach.

5.4
Core excitations and NEXAFS spectra
Near-edge absorption fine structure (NEXAFS) spectroscopy is an element-specific technique
which can be applied for studies of molecular electronic structure [71, 72]. In a NEXAFS exper-
iment, a core excited state is formed when an inner-shell electron is excited to an unoccupied
orbital by an X-ray photon. The X-ray photon energy can be set to target specific elements
within the compound and specific orbital excitations within the element [71]. If the X-ray photon
energy targets a 1s excitation, the K-edge spectrum of an element is measured [71].
Calculation of core excitations
A typical excited state solver solves the eigenvalue of a matrix from the bottom-up, an approach
that obtains low-energy (valence) excitations. Thus, an alternative approach must be taken to
target core excitations. A simple scheme to calculate core excited states and X-ray absorption
spectra has been presented by Coriani and Koch [12]. It is based on the core-valence separation
(CVS) approximation, in which the excitation manifold is cleared of all excitations that do
not include at least one core orbital [73]. The implementation of the CVS approximation in a
generalized Davidson algorithm is performed in eT by applying a projector Pν
I which removes
all elements not involving at least one core orbital I from the trial vector(s) in each iteration [12].

NEXAFS spectra of azomethane
Azomethane (dimethyldiazene), CH3N=NCH3, is a colorless to slightly yellowish explosive gas
and the simplest organic azo compound. It can exist in two isomeric forms, cis, (Z)-dimethyl-
diazene, and trans, (E)-Dimethyldiazene, where trans is expected to be the most stable one [74,
75]. It is an intermediate product in the degradation of unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine, one of
the main fuels of rocket engines. It is also produced by oxidation in the liver as an intermediate
in the metabolism of the highly carcinogenic 1,2-dimethylhydrazine [76]. The photodissociation
of azomethane to produce methyl radicals was investigated already in 1929 by Ramsperger [77,
78], and many experimental and theoretical studies of the photodissociation of azomethane have
been performed since then [79]. The photo-decay of trans-azomethane from the nπ∗excited
29
==PAGE:40==
state has been used as a benchmark and demonstration of ab initio multiple spawning (AIMS)
procedures [80].
Despite the difficulties related to the safe handling of the sample, the NEXAFS and XPS (X-ray
photoelectron) spectra of azomethane (and azoethane) at the C and N K-edges have recently
been measured for the first time by Stefano Stranges and his team at the Elettra Sincrotrone
facility in Trieste [81]. Our calculations are carried out in collaboration with Prof. Stranges to
assign the spectral features observed and to determine whether the sample contains both trans
and cis isomers or not.
Experimental and calculated spectra are plotted together in Figure 5.12. Based on the calculated
spectra, two conclusions can be drawn: the spectral feature at 288 eV in the experimental
spectrum at the C K-edge, which is completely missing in the computed spectra, is due to
vibrations (as it has been subsequently verified [81]); the computed spectral profiles support the
hypothesis that the sample only contains the trans isomer.

Conclusion
An algorithm solving the frequency-dependent linear response equations has been implemented
in the coupled cluster program eT. This algorithm is a key element in the calculation of response
functions and their residues. Since the eT program is still under development, a substantial
amount of time was spent on modifying and adjusting the code to continuous structural updates.
Equation-of-motion coupled cluster transition moments and frequency-dependent polarizabilities
have been fully implemented for the CCS and CCSD approximations, while the coupled cluster
linear response properties are still unfinished due to an incomplete implementation of the F-
transformation to date. Illustrative examples of the application of equation-of-motion coupled
cluster and coupled cluster linear response have described the applicability of these approaches
for calculating molecular properties.
