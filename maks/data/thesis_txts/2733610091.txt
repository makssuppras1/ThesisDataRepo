Abstract 
Green hydrogen production has gained significant traction in recent years following the focus on 
development of sustainable energy supply from renewable energy sources. Production of hydrogen 
by electrolysis can be integrated with surplus electric power from renewable energy sources in the 
concept of Power to X (PtX), which is considered the most suitable path for green hydrogen 
production. Hydrogen is produced in electrolysis by dissociation of water, where 9 kg water is needed 
to produce 1 kg hydrogen and 8 kg oxygen. The rate of hydrogen production and water consumption 
is determined by the electrolyzer power rating. Most electrolyzers consume 45-55 kWh per kg 
hydrogen, meaning ~0.2 m3/h water is required per MW electrolyzer. The water must be of ultrapure 
quality, why the amount of raw water effectively consumed per kg hydrogen produced is 1.4 to 3.3 
times higher due to water loss in pretreatment and purification. This means that large quantities of 
water are needed for large scale applications of hydrogen production by electrolysis, which has given 
rise to concerns regarding potential water resources and their availability. The future water demand 
for PtX in Denmark towards 2040 is estimated to 20-25 mil m3 ultrapure water per year. In this project 
challenges and opportunities in terms of quality, quantity, and availability of potential raw water 
resources were evaluated for PtX in Denmark and applied on local scale for a case specific 150 MW 
electrolysis plant in Vinkel, Skive by BioCirc. The raw water resources were examined using 
literature study and publicly available data from national- reports, databases, and models. 
The findings indicate the extent of raw water resources in Denmark and Skive show good possibility 
for PtX supply. Rainwater, fresh groundwater, brackish water, and seawater showed the largest 
resource quantity, but treated wastewater from municipal wastewater treatment plants also showed 
promising quantity for large scale PtX application. The availability of these resources depends on the 
location and further influenced by national regulations. Fresh groundwater is available in regions 
where the sustainably available groundwater resource is not fully exploited. Using rainwater for PtX 
requires collection and storage system construction, and the supply is unstable because of high 
seasonal variation. Treated wastewater is available in fluctuating, but guaranteed outlet volumes from 
large capacity treatment plants. Brackish water is abundant along the coastal line of Denmark, 
available in large and stable volumes. The open sea provides effectively unlimited volumes of 
seawater, but its availability is highest for offshore PtX plants.  
All the evaluated resources showed different challenges for ultrapure water production in terms of 
quality, treatment, energy consumption and wastewater generation. The use of hard waters such as 
groundwater, brackish water and seawater can cause for scaling, precipitation, and corrosion, where 
fresh surface water and treated wastewater with high concentration of suspended solids, biological 
and nutrient loads can cause problems with fouling, clogging, biocorrosion and contamination. Fresh 
groundwater was found to require the lowest treatment and energy, followed by rainwater, fresh 
surface water and treated wastewater. Brackish water and seawater require more treatment and higher 
energy consumption to obtain ultrapure water quality. The preferable raw water resource for PtX 
application is a stable and easily accessible resource, that is simple and inexpensive to treat for 
ultrapure water production. However, the possible raw water resource for any PtX application is case 
specific, and depends on facility- location, requirements and goals, national and municipal regulation, 
and the perspectives included in the suitability evaluation.

1 Introduction 
Climate change due to increased greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions is a global challenge. Energy 
production by fossil fuel combustion is the biggest contributor to GHG emissions, and reducing the 
dependency on fossil fuels for energy supply is imperative to meet emissions reduction goals and 
mitigate climate change (Chauhan & Ahn, 2023; Uusitalo et al., 2017). Using hydrogen as an 
alternative energy source has gained significant traction because of its high energy density and storage 
capacity (Chauhan & Ahn, 2023). Electrolysis based hydrogen production can be integrated with 
surplus electric power from renewable energy sources in the concept of Power to X (PtX), which is 
considered the most suitable path for future green hydrogen production (Squadrito et al., 2023; Woods 
et al., 2022). PtX and hydrogen production enables storage of energy and indirect electrification of 
sectors difficult to electrify. The concept is a rapidly developing industry in global competition, as 
many countries worldwide recognize the possibilities and commercial gains in these new green 
technologies (Brintbranchen, 2023b; Woods et al., 2022). However, large quantities of water are 
needed for large scale applications of hydrogen production by electrolysis, which has given rise to 
concerns regarding water resources and their availability (Squadrito et al., 2023).  
Generally, the demand for water is rising with a growing global population and industrialization, 
while anthropogenic pollution and the effects of climate change strain water resources and their 
quality (European Environment Agency, 2021; Hoekstra et al., 2012). This has led to many parts of 
the world facing a mounting freshwater scarcity, where rivers run dry for substantial periods of the 
year and groundwater is being pumped at a rate beyond replenishment (Hoekstra et al., 2012; Postel, 
2000). Regions with high renewable energy potential and the space to establish PtX facilities can be 
areas facing growing concerns on insufficient water availability for human and ecological demand 
(i.e., water stress) (European Environment Agency, 2021; Woods et al., 2022). Water resource 
availability varies considerably locally and regionally depending on geological and climatic factors 
as well as seasonal variability. Large changes in the use of internal water resources impacts the water 
balance and affects local water ecosystems resulting in potential water stress (United Nations, 2023a).  
Denmark wants to be internationally competitive with a leading role in the PtX industry and hydrogen 
production with many projects in the pipeline on Danish soil (Brintbranchen, 2023b). The Danish 
government sealed an agreement on the development and promotion of hydrogen and green fuels in 
2022 effectively making PtX a part of the Danish climate strategy on green transition and vision of 
CO2 neutrality in 2045 (Danish Ministry of Climate, Energy and Utilities, 2022). PtX is defined, in 
Denmark, as technologies that uses power from renewable resources to produce fuels, chemicals and 
materials based on green hydrogen produced by electrolysis (Danish Energy Agency, 2021). With the 
launch of the Climate Program 2022, it was stated that the domestic electrolysis capacity for 
production of green hydrogen should be at least 4-6 GW by 2030. The government have allocated 
funds for national investment support and set up a PtX secretary (task force) in the Danish Energy 
Agency to improve the framework conditions for PtX in Denmark (Danish Ministry of Climate, 
Energy and Utilities, 2022; Ebbehøj, 2023).

The PtX sector is already moving quickly, and more than 34 PtX projects have been announced on 
Danish ground (Figure 1) with a total electrolysis capacity >9 GW until 2030 (Ebbehøj, 2023). 
Numbers from Brintbranchen in Denmark even states that 46 PtX projects is already announced and 
plenty is actively proceeding (Brintbranchen, 2023a). With a big vision for green hydrogen in 
Denmark, the primary resource for the vision to succeed – the water supply for electrolysis – 
constitutes a minor part of the agenda. The total theoretical water demand for the >34 announced 
projects is estimated to 13 mil m3 per year, which corresponds to about 1% of the total drinking water 
consumption in Denmark. However, the required water demand for electrolysis may constitute a 
much larger part of the drinking water consumption at local scales. For instance, proceeding PtX 
projects in Esbjerg, Denmark, requires 5 mil m3 per year for electrolysis, which compared to the local 
drinking water consumption of 8 mil m3 per year represents a completely different scale (Ebbehøj, 
2023). 
 
Figure 1: Planned PtX projects in Denmark towards 2030 according to the Danish Energy Agency. Capacity and 
estimated water use is illustrated for selected projects (Ebbehøj, 2023).  
The future water demand for PtX in Denmark towards 2040 is predicted to be 20-25 mil m3 per year 
of ultrapure quality (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a). The raw water demand for 
production of ultrapure water will show higher numbers, why understanding the effect on local water 
resources and the balance of supply and demand is important when considering PtX. To enable 
expansion of PtX technologies both locally and globally a sustainable approach to water management 
must be included (Woods et al., 2022). It is crucial to frame a responsible energy production that does 
not lead to increased water stress or competition for a vital resource in compliance with UN 
Sustainability Development Goal 6: “ensure availability and sustainable management of water and 
sanitation for all” (United Nations, 2023b)

1.1 Aim and Objectives 
The aim of this master’s thesis project is to analyze the water resource requirements concerning 
quality and quantity in relation to Power to X based hydrogen production by electrolysis. Challenges 
and opportunities in water supply management in terms of quality, quantity, and availability of raw 
water resources for Power to X in Denmark will be analyzed and applied on local scale for a Power 
to X plant initiative in Vinkel, Skive, Denmark, by BioCirc.  
The objectives of this thesis project are to:  
1) Identify water quantity and quality requirements for electrolysis-based Power to Hydrogen 
production.  
2) Map the potential raw water resources for Power to X electrolysis-based hydrogen production 
quantitatively and qualitatively including temporal and geographic variations and analyze the 
relation between resource and demand.  
3) Apply the knowledge of water requirements in the case study to assess and evaluate potential 
water supply options for BioCirc’s Power to X plant in Vinkel, Skive, Denmark, with the 
relevant questions of: What is the water demand? What and where are the resources? And how 
to ensure sufficient resource capacity?  
The objectives are addressed from a literature study on water for Power to X (section 2), a water 
resource quantity assessment (section 4), a water resource quality assessment (section 5) and a final 
comparison of different water resources for Power to X (section 6).  
1.2 Case Study: BioCirc Power to X Plant in Vinkel, Skive, Denmark 
BioCirc is a circular bioeconomic company founded in Denmark in 2021. BioCirc has an ambition 
of establishing land-based circular energy clusters to abate CO2 emissions by producing green energy 
(BioCirc, n.d.-a). In this project, a BioCirc energy cluster in Vinkel (Vasehøjvej 16, 7840 Højslev), 
Skive, Denmark, is used as a case study. The energy cluster in Vinkel will include generation of 
renewable electricity, biogas production and digestate upgrade, production of green hydrogen and e-
methanol from electrolysis and Power to X (Figure 2). The cluster will be energy integrated, but any 
excess energy in terms of heat from the processes will be allocated to district heating (Figure 2) 
(BioCirc, n.d.-b). The emphasis for this project is on the segment of the energy cluster that involves 
electrolysis and Power to X (Figure 2) with a focus on the requirements and use of water resources 
for this purpose. 
The energy cluster in Vinkel is still in the development phase, but as a fundamental basis for this 
project, the following prerequisites are stated: The electrolysis capacity will be of approximately 150 
MW with ultrapure water production running continuously with the utilization rate in the electrolyzer. 
The type of electrolyzer is not yet determined, and both alkaline and proton exchange membrane 
electrolysis are considered. The electrolysis process will be running with dry cooling allocating 
excess heat to internal processes and/or district heating (E. D. Rothuizen, personal communication, 
October 26, 2023).

2 Background  
The following sections present an overview of Power to X and hydrogen production using electrolysis 
technology and covers the water requirements for Power to X and electrolysis in terms of both 
quantity and quality. Additionally, the aspect of water resource management is introduced.  
2.1 Power to X and Hydrogen Production 
Focus on development of renewable energy sources has increased in recent years (Squadrito et al., 
2023). These energy sources can be converted into heat and electricity, and especially wind and solar 
energy play an important role, as they are considered cost-effective technologies with high power 
generation potential (Squadrito et al., 2023). This has led to a continuous growth within the energy 
system and a compromised share of the electricity supply from wind and solar energy (Götz et al., 
2016). However, both solar and wind energy are highly dependent on location and weather conditions 
and thereby the power generation becomes irregular. This leads to situation where the energy supply 
and demand do not coincide causing both fluctuating energy prices and unreliable energy supply 
(Squadrito et al., 2023; Uusitalo et al., 2017). Fossil fuel-based energy systems provides the flexibility 
to meet the energy demand via storage of the resources, which renewable energy sources cannot 
provide. For this reason, a transition towards a 100% renewable energy system must be supported by 
other measures such as energy storage technologies, dispatchable generation methods, and sector 
coupling (Skov et al., 2021; Uusitalo et al., 2017). 
PtX plays a key role in sector coupling, as the technology enables the conversion of energy (electricity 
P) to different types of end products (X) and thereby moving the energy from the electricity market 
to other sectors (Skov et al., 2021). Hydrogen is a promising end-product candidate, as it is a clean 
energy carrier with a high energy density (2.7 times higher than fossil fuels) and strong energy storage

high applicability, as it e.g., can be converted back to electric energy in fuel cells, used for mechanical 
energy, burned to generate heat, and used in production of other chemicals such as methane and 
methanol (often termed E-fuels) (Squadrito et al., 2023; Uusitalo et al., 2017). Hydrogen sources are 
defined as green and sustainable when there are no CO2 emissions related to the production path. The 
production of green hydrogen by electrolysis is of increasing interest, because large amounts of 
hydrogen can be obtained from what is considered a sustainable and environmentally friendly 
production (Ursua et al., 2012; Woods et al., 2022). Electrolysis, where raw water (H2O) is dissociated 
into hydrogen (H2) and oxygen (O2) by an electric current can be integrated with the electric power 
from renewable energy sources (Woods et al., 2022). The surplus electricity from renewable energy 
sources is then converted into chemical energy and stored as hydrogen.  
2.1.1 Electrolysis Technology  
The electrolysis reaction is endothermic and nonspontaneous meaning it requires external energy to 
be produced. Following the laws of thermodynamics, the amount of total energy required is 
determined by the process enthalpy change (ΔH), and for the reaction to take place, a part of the 
energy must be supplied by electricity corresponding to Gibb’s free energy of the reaction (ΔG). The 
rest is thermal energy equal to the product of process temperature (T) and the entropy change (ΔS) 
(Amores et al., 2021; Millet & Grigoriev, 2013; Ursua et al., 2012). The electrolysis process can occur 
when a minimum voltage equal to the reversible cell voltage (Vrev) is applied to the process. The 
voltage is applied by circulating a direct current through water to separate its molecules into hydrogen 
and oxygen. The current flows between a positive electrode (anode) and a negative electrode 
(cathode) enabling the transfer between electrons and ions. Anions release electrons to the anode, 
where oxygen is generated and cations receive electrons from the cathode, where hydrogen is 
generated. This is described by two half-cell reactions, oxidation and reduction respectively, with the 
global electrolysis reaction defined in equation 1 (Amores et al., 2021; Ursua et al., 2012).

The thermodynamics of the reaction is temperature dependent, and the electricity demand described 
by Gibbs free energy (ΔG) of the reaction is reduced when the process temperature (T) is increased. 
With temperature increase, the thermal demand (T•ΔS) increases and more energy is supplied to the 
reaction from heat (see Appendix A for thermodynamic relation) (Amores et al., 2021). Power to 
hydrogen production by electrolysis is often operated at lower temperatures around at 60-80 °C, as 
the electric energy requirements are highest at <100°C (see Appendix A for thermodynamic 
relation).This is done to facilitate that greatest amount of energy to the process is supplied by the 
surplus electricity from renewable energy sources (Amores et al., 2021).  
Water electrolysis is also pH dependent. When the electrolysis takes place under neutral conditions 
(pH=7) the anode tends to acidify due to the generation of proton (H+) and the cathode alkalizes as 
hydroxide (OH-) is released and changes the pH surrounding the electrodes (Amores et al., 2021). 
Protons and hydroxide are the most mobile ions, and will be responsible for the ionic transport, why 
a concentrated solution (electrolyte) of either one is required to avoid concentration overpotentials 
(overvoltage) (Millet & Grigoriev, 2013). The electrolyte raises the ionic conductivity, and at acidic

(PEM) electrolysis are currently the main commercially available technologies for green hydrogen 
production from renewable energy sources (Amores et al., 2021; Millet & Grigoriev, 2013; Ursua et 
al., 2012).  
2.1.1.1 Alkaline Water Electrolysis 
The operating principle of an alkaline water electrolysis cell contains two electrodes that are separated 
by a porous gas-tight diaphragm and immersed in a liquid electrolyte (Figure 3 A). The liquid 
electrolyte is a highly concentrated alkaline aqueous solution usually based on 32% w/w KOH, 15% 
w/w NaOH, which enhances the ionic conductivity and allows for ionic transport of OH- (Amores et 
al., 2021; Ursua et al., 2012).  Water is reduced at the cathode yielding OH- and hydrogen gas. OH- 
circulates within the electric field across the diaphragm recombining on the surface of the anode 
producing oxygen (see equation 2 and 3 in Appendix B) (Ursua et al., 2012). The electrolyte 
concentration increases as electrolysis of water takes place, thus maintaining the water supply to the 
electrolyzer is crucial for maintaining an optimal electrolyte concentration and the efficiency (Amores 
et al., 2021). Alkaline water electrolysis typically operates at a temperature range from 65°C to 100°C 
and at a pressure below 30 bar, producing hydrogen with a purity level up to 99.9 vol%. The energy 
consumption is dependent on the type of electrolyzer and the exact construction, but usually ranges 
between 48-56 kWh/kg H2 (Amores et al., 2021; Ursua et al., 2012). The main advantages of alkaline 
electrolysis are that the technology is mature and commercially developed with great durability and 
relatively low costs. The main drawbacks are the corrosive character of the alkaline electrolyte and 
the need for a purification step to reach purity levels of hydrogen above 99.9 vol% (Amores et al., 
2021).  
2.1.1.2 Proton Exchange Membrane electrolysis 
The operating principle in PEM electrolysis is similar to alkaline electrolysis, but the electrodes are 
separated by a polymeric membrane, which functions as the electrolyte (Figure 3 B). The membrane 
is constructed with a thin crossed linked structure and gas-tight properties commonly made of 
perfluorosulfonic acid (PFSA). The functional sulfonic groups have strongly acid character resulting 
in a proton conducting ability (Amores et al., 2021; Ursua et al., 2012). Water is oxidized at the anode 
producing oxygen, electrons, and protons that will circulate through the membrane to the cathode. At 
the cathode protons are reduced yielding hydrogen (see equation 4 and 5 in Appendix C). The 
polymeric membrane limits the operating temperature to 80°C, and PEM electrolysis is usually 
operated at a temperature range from 50°C to 80°C and a pressure below 35 bar. The energy 
consumption is likewise dependent on type of electrolyzer, but usually ranges between 47-52 kWh/kg 
H2. The hydrogen purity can reach levels of 99.99%, and in some cases even 99.999%, but PEM 
electrolysis is also associated with high investment costs and shorter lifetimes than alkaline 
electrolyzers (Amores et al., 2021; Ursua et al., 2012).

2.2 Water for Electrolysis based Power to X  
Based on the atomic composition of water, 9 kg water is needed to produce 1 kg hydrogen and 8 kg 
oxygen by electrolysis (Tækker Madsen, 2022). The water for electrolysis-based hydrogen production 
has to be of ultrapure quality to ensure efficient operation and production  (Woods et al., 2022). Thus, 
a raw water source needs purification to meet the ultrapure quality, leaving the requirements for raw 
water volumes higher than 9 kg (Woods et al., 2022). Furthermore, the electrolyzer usually requires 
cooling water to run the process and additional water for cleaning, which adds to the total water 
consumption (Tækker Madsen, 2022). In practice the amount of water effectively consumed is 
estimated closer to 15 kg or more per kg of hydrogen (Woods et al., 2022). The rate of which hydrogen 
is produced is determined by the electrolyzer power rating, implying that the rate of water 
consumption also depends on the type of electrolyzer. Most electrolyzers consume 45-55 kWh per kg 
hydrogen, which means that 0.16-0.2 L of ultrapure water is required per kWh or 0.2 m3/h of ultrapure 
water required per MW electrolyzer (Tækker Madsen, 2022). By this means, the larger electrolysis 
and PtX hydrogen production, the greater water consumption. When the requirements for larger water 
volumes rise along with the size of the PtX facility, the water resources of sufficient volume become 
fewer.  
Considering water quantity is interconnected to water quality. The quality of different waters 
determines their usability, and the demand for water of good quality is steadily increasing (Boyd, 
2019). Hence, advancing alternative water resources such as rainwater, reclaimed water, wastewater, 
and seawater desalination for PtX application has found its way to the agenda (Zhang et al., 2021). 
The relevance of different raw water resources for PtX depends on the demand and the size of the 
facility, but also on the purpose of the PtX production and the location of the facility (Bak, 2023). 
Location of a PtX plant near alternative water sources can be of critical importance to ensure 
accessibility to sufficient water supply. Otherwise, the availability of a groundwater resource becomes 
crucial in cases where other sources are unavailable (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 
2023a). Notably, reclaimed water and municipal wastewater are available in large quantities and are

located near larger wastewater treatment plants (Zhang et al., 2021).  Seawater is also abundant, with

plants (Newborough & Cooley, 2021). PtX facilities running smaller electrolyzers (0-50 MW) may

A keynote is, that the demand and applicability of raw water sources is not directly scalable with the 
size of electrolyzer. It is also very location dependent, why potential raw water sources that are 
applicable for a given PtX facility is case specific (Bak, 2023).   
2.2.1 Ultrapure Water Quality 
Ultrapure water is defined as high purity water with almost no other electrolytes than hydrogen and 
oxygen ions (Zhang et al., 2021). In an ideal state, ultrapure water must be entirely free of 
contaminants, including pathogens, suspended solids, radioactive compounds, inorganic and organic 
compounds, thereby removing all components except water molecules.  However, this is effectively 
impossible, hence the required level of ultrapure water quality is conditional on the purpose of use 
(Zhang et al., 2021). The required water quality for hydrogen production by electrolysis depends on 
the type of electrolyzer, the electrode material, system design and in some cases also the brand of 
electrolyzer, why the specific requirements are commonly from the manufacturer (Tækker Madsen, 
2022; Woods et al., 2022). At present, research on the exact ultrapure water specifications and 
requirements for hydrogen production by electrolysis is in its early stages of development. With many 
variables and effects, there is no unified water quality standard for all electrolyzers (Tækker Madsen, 
2022).  
According to Silhorko-Eurowater (Tækker Madsen, 2022), a suggested starting point for the required 
water quality can be measured in ionic conductivity for standard alkaline electrolyzer as <1 μS/cm 
and <0.1 μS/cm for PEM electrolyzers and advanced alkaline electrolyzers. For comparison Ursua et 
al., (2012) presents <5 µS/cm for alkaline electrolysis and <1 µS/cm for PEM electrolysis. Referring 
to the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM),  Silhorko-Eurowater (Tækker Madsen, 
2023) additionally presents ultrapure water Type I and Type II as water quality guidelines for PEM 
and AWE, respectively (see Appendix D for specifications). In agreement, Winter et al., (2022) 
presents that the water purity for electrolysis should as a minimum be ASTM Type II water (see 
Appendix D for specifications). There is yet not much known on the long-term effects of impurities 
on the electrolyzer performance and production efficiency, but some important parameters and 
impurities influencing and challenging the water electrolysis process have been identified (Table 1). 
For several impurity parameters there is limited quantitative information available, and challenges 
described remains largely qualitative. Such challenges are associated with contamination of catalyst, 
membrane and hydrogen product, conductivity disturbance, ionic poisoning and lowering of proton 
transport, corrosion and deposition, membrane fouling, reduction of production efficiency and 
increased need for cleaning (Amores et al., 2021; Tækker Madsen, 2023; Winter et al., 2022; Woods 
et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021).

2.2.2 Ultrapure Water Production 
The treatment steps to reach ultrapure water quality depends on the initial quality of the raw water 
source. The higher quality of raw water source, the less requirements for treatment and vice versa 
(Tækker Madsen, 2022). The production of ultrapure water can be divided into  main steps: 1) 
Pretreatment, where the raw water source is brought to a stable water quality resembling drinking 
water quality, and 2) Polishing, where impurities are removed to obtain ultrapure quality (Figure 4) 
(Tækker Madsen, 2022; Winter et al., 2022). For each necessary pretreatment step the volume of raw 
water will be reduced. Thus the quantity of raw water needed for ultrapure water production depends 
on the quality of the raw water source and the recovery rates in treatment (Table 2) (Tækker Madsen, 
2022).

In the polishing process (Figure 4), RO is used to remove ions, molecules, and particles in a 
demineralization step. RO reduces the conductivity and removes organics and silica, but it is often 
necessary to filter the permeate in a secondary RO system (double pass) to ensure sufficiently low

be removed before RO with e.g. active carbon (Tækker Madsen, 2022). Dissolved gasses are not 
removed in the RO process, why installation of a degassing system after RO treatment is necessary. 
To reach the very low conductivity essential for the electrolysis process, a final deionization step is 
required. A mixed bed filter or an electrodeionization (EDI) unit can be applied in this step (Tækker 
Madsen, 2022).

2.2.3 Water Consumption for Electrolyzer Cooling and Cleaning  
During the electrolysis process a part of the electric energy is transformed into heat, why the system 
must be cooled to maintain the operation temperature (Zhang et al., 2021). Depending on the 
construction of the system, temperature control can be performed in different ways. Examples of 
cooling solutions are to continuously cool the water or the electrolyte and recirculate it to the cells,  
to cool the system with an evaporative cooling tower or a dry cooling system (Tækker Madsen, 2022; 
Zhang et al., 2021). An evaporative cooling tower requires twice the amount of water required for 
electrolysis, being approximately 0.4 m3/h per MW. The quality requirements for cooling water are 
however lower than for ultrapure water. It is also known that waste heat from larger electrolyzer 
systems can be integrated into other infrastructure systems such as district heating systems or 
wastewater treatment plants (Tækker Madsen, 2022).  
When the ultrapure water has entered the electrolyzer system, a continuous influx of metal ions from 
piping and process equipment, as well as ions and organic compounds from the electrolyzer stack 
influence the water quality during the process. To secure the lifetime of the electrolyzer, a continually 
polishing loop of the water in the electrolysis cell is necessary. This is particularly pronounced in 
PEM electrolysis to ensure efficiency of the membrane electrolyte.  However, the exact requirements 
and water use for cleaning depends on the specific electrolyzer and manufacturer recommendations 
(Tækker Madsen, 2022). 
2.2.4 Reject Water from Electrolysis and Ultrapure Water Production  
During the electrolysis process pollutants can be released from the electrolyzer stack, and 
electrolyzers can promote chemical reactions of the existing pollutants, which can modify them to 
more problematic substances (Tækker Madsen, 2024). The impact from electrolysis on the water 
quality and water lifetime is yet an upcoming researched topic but shows relevant considerations for 
both ensuring ultrapure water quality in the electrolysis process and for the composition of wastewater 
from the process. Ultrapure water is continuously fed into the electrolyzer, which dilutes the 
concentration of pollutants from the electrolysis process, but eventually the process water or the side

stream polishing loop will be concentrated in a degree, where it must be discharged. Specifically for 
AWE electrolysis, the concentration of alkaline electrolyte must be maintained, and it can be 
considered that wastewater from AWE electrolysis will show alkaline properties.   
Production of ultrapure water from different raw water sources also generates wastewater, as a 
proportion of water and concentrated pollutants is rejected in each treatment step. The composition 
of the reject stream from pretreatment is reflected by the quality of the raw water source and the 
treatment operation. Additionally, the reject water from ultrapure water production can contain 
substances from the treatment process, such as anti-scalants, cleaning and precipitation agents (APV 
systems, 2000; Morales et al., 2023; Newborough & Cooley, 2021).  Chemical cleaning of membranes 
is largely performed with caustics, acids, detergents, or complexing agents often in combination with 
disinfectants, which will be present in the reject water as well (APV systems, 2000).  The volume of 
reject water from each treatment step is reflected in the recovery rates. Generally membrane processes 
produce more wastewater because of physical cleaning techniques such as backwashing and water 
flush (APV systems, 2000). Particularly for RO desalination of high salinity water, around half the 
initial volume is rejected as brine, which contains twice the initial salt concentration (given 50% 
recovery) (Gray et al., 2011). A common brine disposal method is dilution by transfer into the deep 
sea, but this method is limited for inland locations increasing the complication and costs of brine 
handling (Gray et al., 2011). Additionally, large volumes of saltwater are needed for production of 
ultrapure water due to the lower recovery, which equals larger reject water volumes. In contrast, the 
brine from brackish water desalination has lower salt content, but can include suspended solids, 
nutrients and organic compounds from its origin, and can require additional treatment before 
discharge to aquatic environments can be a disposal option (Gray et al., 2011). The presence of solids, 
nutrients and both synthetic and natural organic compounds in reject from especially treated 
wastewater complicates its disposal and can lead to challenges in handling and disposal of large 
volumes of sludge  (Gray et al., 2011; Vishali & Kavitha, 2021).  
Roughly said, the substances that must be removed from the water stream to obtain ultrapure water 
are rejected in treatment and must be handled as a more or less complicated waste stream. Handling 
and discharge of reject water from treatment of different raw water sources show different challenges 
and potential environmental impacts. Discharge of wastewater and connection of industrial 
wastewater to public wastewater facilities in Denmark requires permission under the Environmental 
Protection Act (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2006, 2018). Thus, the wastewater 
generation associated to a raw water source is an important consideration in the PtX planning.

2.3 Water Resources and Management 
Earth’s hydrosphere comprises the sum of water in the oceans, in the ground, on the surface, and in 
the air in both liquid, solid, and gaseous phases. Approximately 97.5% of the hydrosphere is saline 
water, whereof the oceans account for ~96.5%,  and the remaining ~2.5% is fresh water (Figure 5) 
(Shiklomanov, 2000; Shiklomanov & Rodda, 2004). The majority of freshwater is present in icecaps, 
glaciers and permanent snow cover, approximately one third is fresh groundwater, and less than 1% 
of the fresh water is concentrated in river systems and lakes (Figure 5) (Dutta & Sharma, 2019; 
Shiklomanov, 2000; Shiklomanov & Rodda, 2004). Although, most of Earth’s surface is covered by 
water, the freshwater available to humans and ecosystems comprise a very small part (Figure 5). 
 
Figure 5: Distribution of water on Earth in approximate numbers (Dutta & Sharma, 2019) 
2.3.1 Water Balance and Availability 
The quantity of freshwater in a specific compartment of the hydrosphere that is available for 
ecological or human needs is dependent on the rate at which new water enters the compartment and 
replaces the existing supply (Boyd, 2019). Water reservoirs are continuously recharged, but the rate 
of movement and residence time are different for water in it’s different phases (Lal, 2015; 
Shiklomanov & Rodda, 2004). The period of full water renewal (recharge) in different freshwater 
compartments varies from ≈300-1500 years for deeper groundwater reservoirs, to ≈17 years for lakes, 
and ≈16 days in rivers (Boyd, 2019; Shiklomanov, 2000). Freshwater with a renewal period of several 
years or decades are conventionally considered static storage, and intensive use of these will result in 
storage depletion. Freshwater resources that are yearly replenished in the hydrological cycle are 
considered renewable. These mainly cover regional runoff, groundwater inflow to river systems, and 
upper aquifer groundwater systems (Shiklomanov, 2000). The volume of renewable freshwater 
resources varies considerably according to geographic, seasonal, and climatic factors, and is not 
uniformly distributed with population. Facilitating a sustainable water withdrawal is limited to the 
renewal rate of the resource (United Nations, 2022, 2023a).

A part of the precipitation will return to the atmosphere from surface water evaporation and 
transpiration in plants (combined: evapotranspiration (ET)). The amount of the precipitation left after 
evaporation is termed the net precipitation and is available for water systems on the landmass. The 
net precipitation will run off in the watercourse (Q) and/or infiltrate the soil surface to be stored (ΔS) 
in upper soil layers, surface water bodies or seep further down to the groundwater (Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency, 2000a; Mohajerani et al., 2021). The magnitude of water, that 
infiltrates to the groundwater aquifers is termed the groundwater recharge and is complicated to 
measure exactly. The groundwater recharge depends not only on the net precipitation, but also on the 
geological conditions in the soil layers between the terrain and the groundwater aquifer, and 
connection to surface waters (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2000a; Hartmann, 2022). 
The recharge can be down to ~0% of the net infiltration dependent on climate, season and vegetational 
conditions, but also reach >90% of the net filtration in areas with limited soil and low temperatures 
(Hartmann, 2022). In general, the groundwater recharge is limited in regions with annual rainfall less 
than 250 mm unless the soils are very permeable (Santos Pereira et al., 2009a).  
The water balance equation can be altered to accurately reflect any water system by specifying all 
inflow and outflow streams. Hence, it is possible to quantify the rate of change in water storage for 
any water system (Mohajerani et al., 2021). Importantly, any additional water abstraction from a water 
system will cause changes to the water balance. The primary consequences of excessive water 
abstraction from groundwater sources include disturbances and decline in water table levels, risk of 
saline intrusion, reduction of water flow in streams, and aquifer depletion (European Environment 
Agency, 2021). In the case of excessive water abstraction from surface water bodies, the main effects 
involve alterations in hydrology and morphology. In both situations, it can result in introduction of 
pollutants, such as chemicals, nutrients, and organic matter through return flows, affecting both 
groundwater and surface water systems as well as habitats and ecosystems (European Environment 
Agency, 2021). Thus, the importance of efficient water use, and sustainable water management 
applies both in preservation of the hydrological balance, the water quality, and the water availability. 
2.3.2 Managing Sustainable Water Use in Denmark 
In Denmark, all drinking water supply is obtained from abstracting groundwater (with exception of 
Christiansø), and considerable amounts are also used for field irrigation, water supply for industry, 
horticulture, and fish farming (Thorling et al., 2023). The total annual groundwater abstraction has 
been between 600 mil to 800 mil m3/year in the past 20 years apart from 2018, where it reached ~900 
mil m3 because of drought and excessive field irrigation (Thorling et al., 2023). The groundwater 
resource is monitored by Danish authorities in the Danish Groundwater Monitoring Program 
(GRUMO) to assess the overall water balance and facilitate a sustainable use of the available resource 
(Thorling et al., 2023). Groundwater and surface water may not be abstracted without permission 
(Vandforsyningsloven, 2022), and all water abstraction facilities must report their annual groundwater 
withdrawal volume to the municipalities (GEUS, n.d.-b; Thorling et al., 2023). This information is 
indispensable for assessment of the quantitative state of the groundwater resources and to enable an 
==PAGE:27==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384

optimal allocation of the available water resource (Thorling et al., 2023).  However, the extent of the 
available water resource is a difficult measure.  
As a part of the implementation of the EU's water framework directive (2000/60/EF) the Danish 
Ministry of Environment have ruled out national water area plans (Vandområdeplanerne 2021-2027) 
with informative documents and an action program to achieve the desired environmental state of 
Danish water bodies in terms of quantitative and chemical condition (Danish Environmental 
Protection Agency, 2023b). With contribution from explanatory reports made by Geological Survey 
of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS), a common basis on groundwater exploitation has been described 
for Danish aquifers: It is evaluated that up to 30% of the total deep groundwater recharge can be 
exploited. In other terms, 70% is reserved to ensure: 1) sufficient water availability for associated 
groundwater-dependent terrestrial ecosystems, 2) the groundwater body continues to contribute to 
supporting ecological flow i.e., ecological quality for fish, small animals, and plants, and 3) that 
lowering of the pressure level does not accelerate e.g., pyrite oxidation, saltwater intrusion or 
thrusting into the deposit (Henriksen et al., 2019, 2023).  
In a recently published rapport from the Danish Ministry of Environment (Danish Environmental 
Protection Agency, 2023a), an assessment of the quantitative state of Danish groundwater bodies 
showed that several groundwater bodies, especially around the big cities in Denmark, are already, or 
are in danger of becoming in a quantitatively poor state due to overexploitation (Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a). Additionally, increasing findings of pollutants in the 
groundwater means that the water quality is no longer fit for drinking water purposes using the 
existing water treatment. Consequently, an increasing number of drinking water supplies must search 
for new sources, mix water of different qualities or introduce more advanced water treatment (Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a). Areas of Particular Drinking Water Interest (OSD) and 
areas of Drinking Water Interest (OD) have a key role at national and local scale. The designation 
links quantitative assessments of drinking water demand with protection of the water quality for 
drinking water purposes. The structure of groundwater abstraction and drinking water catchment 
areas (IOL) adapts to the findings of contamination, and OSD and OD areas are revised for the 
purpose of securing drinking water supply in Denmark (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 
2023a). Abstracting groundwater for industrial and agricultural purposes outside of OSD and OD can 
facilitate a more sustainable water abstraction (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a).  
The challenges in quantity and quality of the groundwater resource impose, that in some areas of 
Denmark securing all needs and interests for water is becoming difficult. This applies not only for 
drinking water supply, both also industry, agriculture, and the water needs of nature (Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a). Increasing focus on use of alternative water sources for 
non-drinking water purposes and measures to beneficially use “problem water” are evolving in 
Denmark to facilitate sustainable water use and secure future drinking water interests.

4.2 Groundwater Quantity 
Using groundwater as raw water resource for ultrapure water production requires the lowest water 
volume (Table 7). Groundwater is a long-term renewable resource so it important to facilitate a 
sustainable use of the resource. It is undesirable to face situations where the water abstraction is higher 
than the groundwater recharge, as this would result in depletion of the aquifers and challenge the 
security of water for drinking water supply, streams, ecosystems etc. Geological Survey of Denmark 
and Greenland (GEUS) has estimated the sustainably available groundwater resource at national and 
regional level, which states the volume that can be abstracted in relation to acceptable impacts on a 
30-year period average (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a; Henriksen et al., 2023). 
The total sustainably available groundwater resource in Denmark amounts to a total of ~1.1 billion 
m3/year, of which approx. 0.5 billion m3/year within OSD areas and IOL for water supply facilities 
(Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a). However, the sustainably available groundwater 
resource is only derived from a water balance between aquifers and recipients, and does not include 
the quality of the resource (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2023a).  
Based on the sustainable resource and national water abstraction, GEUS has composed a view of the 
degree of exploitation of the groundwater resource on a national and regional scale (Figure 7).

4.2.1.2 Comparison with groundwater models 
The national water resource model (DKmodel2019) (GEUS, 2019) describes the natural hydrological 
cycle and human impacts by integrated groundwater-surface water modelling (GEUS, n.d.-c). In 
DKmodel2019, the degree of exploitation is calculated for individual groundwater bodies as the total 
abstraction from the groundwater body divided by total recharge/inflow to the groundwater body. A 
groundwater body is defined from the physical boundaries of the water-bearing layers (three-
dimensional) and by mutual contact of several aquifers, thus a groundwater body can consist of one 
or more aquifers from different water bearing layers (Henriksen et al., 2019). The total abstraction is 
found as the sum of average abstraction volumes from all intakes coupled to the individual 
groundwater body in a period from 2011 to 2017. They assume, that the total abstraction volume from 
a facility is equally distributed on all connected intakes (GEUS, 2019; Henriksen et al., 2023).  
To evaluate the degree of groundwater exploitation in Skive municipality, the results from the 
DKmodel2019 (GEUS, 2019) were isolated to groundwater bodies in Skive with coupled intakes (i.e., 
at least one water abstraction facility is coupled to the groundwater body) by applying and filtering 
the DKmodel2019 data in QGIS. The degree of exploitation for each groundwater body with coupled 
intakes was defined using the respective data layer from the DKmodel2019 (Figure 10). A 
visualization of the degree of exploitation is also available from the online accessible model, but since 
the model results are given for each individual groundwater body, it results in overlapping layers and 
masking of potentially important information.

4.3 Surface Water Quantity 
In this assessment surface water covers both fresh surface water and saline surface water including 
oceans. The extent of each in Denmark will be shortly described in the following sections.  
4.3.1 Surface Freshwater 
Approximately 1.5 % of the total area of Denmark is covered by freshwater lakes, with the majority 
being in Central and Eastern Jutland and North Zealand. However, many lakes are small of size and  
only a minor part is larger than 1 ha (10.000 m2) (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.-b). 
Danish lakes and ponds are protected by the Nature Protection Act (Naturbeskyttelsesloven, 2022) 
when the area is 100 m2 or more, and applies that their condition must not be altered. The protection 
includes both the open water surface and the adjoining wetland, and applies for both natural and 
artificial lakes, where plant and animal life has developed. Lakes smaller than 100 m2 are protected 
if they are located in a protected nature zone, or form part of or interfere with a protected watercourse 
(Danish Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.-f). The total length of streams and watercourses in 
Denmark accumulates to 64.000 km and include both public and private watercourses. All streams 
and watercourses are covered by the Watercourses Act (Vandløbsloven, 2019), and additionally 
28,000 km are also protected under the Nature Protection Act (Natura 2000) (Danish Environmental 
Protection Agency, n.d.-f). The greatest amount is found in Region Southern Denmark, where 32% 
of the total protected watercourses are located, followed by Region Central Jutland and Region North 
Jutland (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.-f).  It is not allowed to abstract or divert water 
from streams, change the water level or obstruct the free flow of water without permission from 
authorities (Vandløbsloven, 2019). Thus, the use of surface freshwater as resource for PtX application 
will be strictly limited by laws, ownership, and regulation by authorities. Therefore, it is chosen to 
prioritize evaluation of other relevant resources for PtX application, and surface freshwater is not 
further evaluated in this assessment in terms of quantity.  
4.3.2 Saline Surface Water  
Denmark is surrounded by ~8500 km coastline, 10.6·106 ha marine waters, and the approximate 
maximum distance to the coast is 50 km, thus saline surface water is abundant in Denmark (J. W. 
Hansen & Høgslund, 2023; Statistics Denmark, 2017). The marine area of Denmark can be divided 
into coastal waters including fjords, and sea areas which are open territorial waters. Defined in the 
water area plans 2021-2027 (VP3) (Danish Environmental Protection Agency, 2023b), coastal waters 
are inner territorial waters within 1 nautical mile (~1.85km) from the coast, while sea waters extend 
from the coastal waters outer limit to 12 nautical miles from the coastline corresponding to the outer 
territorial waters. Coastal waters are covered by the EU's water framework directive (2000/60/EF), 
but outer territorial waters are not (K. Nielsen et al., 2001).  
The salinity level is used to differentiate the marine waters into sea water and brackish water. 
Seawater is characterized by a salinity between 30‰ and 40‰, whereas the salinity in brackish water 
is <0.5‰ up to 30‰ (K. Nielsen et al., 2001).  In the Northern Sea area at the west coast of Jutland 
the water salinity is >33‰ and the highest in Denmark. In the Kattegat area the salinity is 18-33‰, 
and in the inner Danish waters the salinity ranges from 15-20‰ in the northern most part and 7-15‰ 
in the southern most part. In the Baltic Sea, the salinity is only ~8‰ (K. Nielsen et al., 2001). The 
==PAGE:41==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384

salinity levels vary in the inner waters as low salinity water flows from The Baltic Sea into the inner 
waters, thus a large part of the marine area is characterized as brackish water (Danish Environmental 
Protection Agency, n.d.-h). The extent of brackish water also governs the Danish fjords, which also 
have variable salinity dependent on their connection to the sea and the freshwater outlets into the 
fjord (K. Nielsen et al., 2001). In this assessment, saline surface water is termed as brackish water 
when governing fjords and coastal waters, and seawater when governing outer territorial waters and 
open sea. Quantifying the volume of saline surface water (both brackish water and seawater) in 
Denmark is beyond the scope of this project. However, this resource is plentiful (effectively 
unlimited), and it is fair to assume that it can sufficiently supply raw water for a PtX plant.

4.5 Rainwater Quantity 
Water resources are not only found on land, and especially in Denmark great amounts of water 
precipitates each year. Precipitation occurs approximately every other day with an average of 171 
days per year (Statistics Denmark, 2017). The national average precipitation from 2011 to 2020 is 
782 mm/year, but on average it rains the most in Central Jutland and the least in the Kattegat region 
(DMI, n.d.-b). The annual precipitation on national scale have increased about 100 mm since the 
1970’s, and further increase in precipitation is expected, particularly during the winter, where 
precipitation is expected to increase by around 25% by the end of this century. Summers are more 
challenging to predict, but they will likely be characterized by longer dry periods and an increase in 
frequency and intensity of cloudburst (Danish Ministry of Environment, n.d.; DMI, n.d.-b). With an 
average precipitation of 782 mm/year, the volume of rain on one square meter in Denmark 
corresponds to 0.78m3/year. Assuming 50% evaporation the annual rainwater volume on Denmark’s 
surface amounts to more than 16 billion m3. In principle, it is not possible to exploit the full volume 
of rainwater, but it provides an overview of the extent of rain in Denmark. Eventually, rainwater needs 
to be collected before it can be used as a resource.  
4.5.1 Rainwater Collection  
Precipitation can be collected for raw water supply from catchment areas of more or less impervious 
surfaces such as roofs and paved roads.  The collected water can be used directly on site, but storing 
capacity is essential for periods without precipitation (Lee et al., 2023). The collection capacity of a

the intensity of precipitation. The catchment area of a rainwater harvesting system comprises the 
surface that directly receives precipitation, from where it is collected in pipes for the water supply 
system. The surface texture and permeability affects the quantity of collectable rainwater and the 
material of the catchment surface also affects the quality of the collected rainwater (Lee et al., 2023). 
In this section water supply from rainwater collection is quantified, and the quality of collected 
rainwater is reviewed later.

runoff is especially the surface material, the surface construction, and the amount of rainfall. The

4.5.2.1 Modelling of Rainwater Collection for BioCirc PtX 
BioCirc has provided an on-site precipitation and surface rainwater utilization model to this project. 
The model includes hourly water balances based on the hourly water demand for PtX and the hourly 
precipitation for the period 2018 to 2022 obtained from DMI Open API (given by BioCirc).  The 
model also includes the volume and water balance of the storage tank for collected rainwater. 
Ultrapure water will be produced continuously at the same rate it is used in PtX production, hence 
water in the storage tank will be used continuously with the PtX production. Upon initiating ultrapure 
water production, the storage tank should have an initial filling level with collected rainwater, which 
is also a determining factor for the subsequent storage capacity and the ability to keep the water 
production running continuously. When the amount of collected rainwater is higher than the PtX 
demand, the surplus will be stored in the tank to supply when the amount of collected rainwater is 
lower than demand. When the storage tank is full, the excess amount of collected rainwater will be 
lost. If the water level in the storage tank is lower than the demand, water is supplied from a secondary 
source.  
The model given by BioCirc contains precipitation data from 2018-2022, but many datapoints are 
missing from 2020 to 2022 making it unrepresentative, why it was chosen to only evaluate on the 
years 2018 and 2019. The supply-demand balance for BioCirc Ptx is thus evaluated on the driest year, 
2018, and the wettest year, 2019. Initially, the model is run with all operational parameter values 
given by BioCirc (termed case 0) (Table 15) except from the hourly raw water demand, which is 
estimated as a factor 1.5 of the ultrapure water demand. Subsequently, the model is run with variable 
values of storage tank volume, initial filling level of tank and with a runoff factor corrected for sloped 
roof and paved roads (termed case 1-4) (Table 15). The water demand and size of catchment area 
remain constant in all cases.

The mapping of water resource quantity show that fresh groundwater is a large resource in Denmark,

quantification method. In Skive the sustainable groundwater resource is not fully exploited, and the 
demand for BioCirc’s PtX plant would correspond to less than 1% of the unexploited volume. Further

limitedly available despite the large extent in Denmark. Rainwater must be collected and stored to

5 Mapping of Water Resources for Power to X: Water Quality 
Water quality is commonly defined by to the physical, chemical, and biological characteristics of the 
water (see Appendix K). The water quality determines in a high manner, the suitability of water for 
various uses. Assessing water quality of raw water resource for PtX application governs multiple 
aspects. Importantly, the ultrapure water quality is necessary in the electrolysis process to support 
electrolyzer performance and ensure the production efficiency and purity of hydrogen. The initial 
quality of a raw water sources determines the requirements for treatment to obtain ultrapure water. 
Thus, in the process of abstracting a raw water resource to supply ultrapure water for electrolysis in 
PtX, three main aspects regarding the impact of water quality can be emphasized:  
1) Complications in the electrolysis process and reduced efficiency of hydrogen production from 
impurities in the electrolysis water 
2) Complexity in pretreatment and purification of the raw water to ultrapure water quality  
3) Composition of the reject water stream from ultrapure water production and complications in the 
discharge of it.  
5.1 Water Quality Challenges of Raw Water Resources for Power to X 
A way to assess water quality for PtX application could be to work backwards from ultrapure water 
quality to raw water quality to identify impacting parameters on one or more of aspect 1-3, and 
address parameters that may otherwise potentially complicate the process. The main challenges in the 
electrolysis process from contaminants in the water are corrosion, chemical precipitation and 
deposition (scaling), disturbance of electric conductivity and membrane pore blocking if operating 
PEM electrolyzers. The main parameters affecting both the electrolysis process (from section 2.3.1) 
and the treatment process to obtain ultrapure water (from section 2.3.2) were ions (conductivity), 
organics, sulphates, silica, particulates, and micro-organisms. The following sections present different 
quality parameters in water sources and complications connected to their presence that may impact 
the applicability of the water source for PtX.  
5.1.1 Precipitation and Scaling 
Deposition/scaling in water installations, water equipment, and on membranes most often arise from 
precipitation, where dissolved solids form solid particles and settle out of the solution. Scaling on 
membranes blocks the surfaces and degrades membrane performance (Gray et al., 2011).  The process 
of precipitation is affected by both temperature and pH off the solution, and different ions and 
minerals can precipitate under different conditions (Vishali & Kavitha, 2021). 
Divalent cations in water, especially calcium (Ca2+) and magnesium (Mg2+), are known to precipitate 
from water as hard particles causing deposits and scale formation (Appelo & Postma, 2005; Boyd, 
2019). In most natural waters, both calcium and magnesium are found in substantial levels (Table 
17), but also bound to bicarbonate, sulphate, or chloride. The sum of divalent ions that can precipitate 
gives water it’s hardness character. The level of hardness is often expressed in the unit of mg/l CaCO3 
equivalent, that would corresponding to similar effect as Ca2+ and Mg2+ concentrations (Appelo & 
Postma, 2005; Diggs & Parker, 2009). The hardness degree varies in different raw water sources 
applicable to PtX (Table 17). Groundwater is the freshwater type that can possess the highest hardness 
==PAGE:53==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384 
22-01-2024 
43 
 
(Table 17) due to the dissolution of minerals in groundwater aquifers. Treated wastewater can show 
around the same hardness as groundwater (Table 17), which will often be the case when groundwater 
is the primary source for household water supply. The hardness level of seawater is very because of 
the high salt content, and the hardness of brackish water will increase with salinity (Table 17).  
Silicon dioxide (SiO2), or silica, is known to cause scaling and is a challenge in desalination treatment. 
Silica scaling on membranes and heat exchangers are common and reduces the process efficiency. 
Precipitation of silica causes hard scaling, which is very difficult to remove without damage to the 
system (Bush et al., 2018).  Thus, the concentration of silica must be very low in ultrapure water for 
electrolysis. Silica can be present in various forms, and most waters will contain silica as minerals 
containing silicon are common in earth’s crust (Boyd, 2019; Fitts, 2024). The concentration of silica 
in different water types vary dependent on the origin of the water but is highest in freshwaters because 
of contact with minerals (Table 17).  
Cation ions from metals such as cobber (Cu), cadmium (Cd), zinc (Zn) and iron (Fe), which is present 
in trace concentrations in most water types, can undergo metal ion hydrolysis in water and form 
precipitating metal hydroxides (Vishali & Kavitha, 2021). The release of protons during metal 
hydrolysis reaction will decrease the pH and enhance the corrosive potential of the water (Boyd, 
2019). Metals can also react with e.g sulfide and precipitate in a broad range of pH as metal oxides 
(Lewis, 2017). This can cause for scaling and damage the electrolyzers, but their presence can also 
cause disturbance of the electrode potential. Many metals exist in complexes in natural waters but 
can also exist as free ions dependent on the pH (Fitts, 2024). At neutral pH most metals are sorbed to 
particles, but at lower pH the metals are mobilized, and the concentration of metals increases (Appelo 
& Postma, 2005). Common metals found in both fresh groundwater and fresh surface water (besides 
Ca and Mg) are Cu, Zn, manganese (Mn) and aluminum (Al) (Table 17). They often range in µg/l 
concentrations, but can also be found in the low range of mg/l concentrations (Zhou et al., 2020). 
Particularly for seawater, mineral scaling from strontium and barium can by problematic as these can 
be found in notable concentrations (Table 17). The content and concentration of metals in brackish 
water will be dependent on the freshwater and seawater input and governs the same metals as those 
present in these water types (Table 17). In treated wastewater the metals found are commonly Cu, Zn, 
and nickel (Ni) (Table 17). Dependent on the collection surface, metals can also be present in collected 
rainwater, which is particularly pronounced if collected from metal roofs.  
5.1.2 Dissolved Solids and Conductivity  
The concentration of dissolved solids in water impacts the corrosive potential of water, causes of 
precipitation, and the conductivity. The concentration of total dissolved solids (TDS) is determined 
as the fraction of solids passing through a filtration of water, where those retained are the suspended 
solids. Mostly, a filter with a pore size of 0.45-1 µm is used to separate dissolved and suspended 
particles, but also larger filters (2 µm) are applied in practice and the exact division is not well defined 
(Jansen et al., 2021). Particles with a diameter ≤0.1 µm can be precipitated, meaning that small and 
finely dispersed particles may still appear in the filtrate (Appelo & Postma, 2005; Jansen et al., 2021). 
Most of the TDS are constituted by inorganic particles, and waters with high TDS concentration are 
often said to be highly mineralized and possess higher ability to cause for deposition and corrosion 
(Boyd, 2019). TDS in water is approximately related to the conductivity, where waters with higher 
==PAGE:54==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384 
22-01-2024 
44 
 
TDS show higher conductivity (Boyd, 2019; Walton, 1989). The conductivity is influenced by 
temperature, as the ionic mobility increases and decreases at higher and lower temperatures, 
respectively. The contribution from the ionic product of water can also influence the conductivity at 
high (>9) or low pH (<5) (Walton, 1989).  
The concentration of TDS in different raw water types vary from being lowest in rainwater to highest 
in seawater (Table 17). TDS of groundwater usually varies in ranges below 500 mg/L dependent on 
aquifer composition, but can be higher in aquifers influenced by subsurface deposits (Boyd, 2019). 
Very large variations in TDS of different groundwaters are mostly observed with variations in the 
concentrations of sodium (Na+) and chloride (Cl-), which can be contributed to groundwater from 
marine origin (Appelo & Postma, 2005). The composition of seawater is rather similar worldwide, 
and the main contributors to the high TDS are the high concentration of Na+ and Cl- (Table 17). TDS 
in surface freshwater varies dependent on geological and climatic factors but is most often below 
1000 mg/L (Table 17). However, TDS in streams and lakes vary more than TDS in larger rivers (Boyd, 
2019). Brackish waters show high variation in TDS because of the mix of freshwater and saline water 
(Table 17). TDS in rainwater can vary geographically but is generally low (Table 17). However, the 
TDS will increase when the rainwater is collected from a surface (Table 17).  TDS does not reflect 
the specific solids but is, however, characterized by the presence of small ions able to pass through 
filtration, which can be difficult to remove completely in water treatment. Namely halogen anions 
such as Cl-, fluoride (F-) and bromide (Br-), which are particularly present in saline waters (Table 17) 
are often not fully retained in the RO process and can cause for gas formation and electrode damage 
in the electrolysis (Becker et al., 2023; Gray et al., 2011). Waters with high concentration of TDS 
requires RO treatment, as UF is not effective to remove ions. Roughly said, the higher TDS, the higher 
requirement for treatment to reach the low conductivity of <1 µS/cm required for electrolysis.   
5.1.3 Corrosion and Fouling 
The corrosive potential of water increases with decreased pH along with elevated levels of dissolved 
oxygen and carbon dioxide. Further, corrosion in pipes and installations can be accelerated by 
bacterial activity and the presence of oxidants such as nitrate, sulphate, or chlorine (Albrechtsen & 
Tang, 2016; Boyd, 2019). Nitrate, sulphate, and chlorine are present in all raw water types, but in 
highest concentration in seawater, fresh surface water and brackish water (Table 17). Several types 
of bacteria and microorganisms may be involved in so called biocorrosion, which can occur in pipes 
and installations from development of biofilm layers. Additionally, they can give rise to corrosion, 
deposits, and potential clogging of pipes, but also cause for biological growth, contamination, and 
reduced efficiency of the system (Fink, 2016). Notably, treated wastewater and fresh surface water 
can contain bacteria and microorganisms in pronounced concentrations (Table 17). Inorganic 
materials such as acids and bases can also be present in treated wastewater, which can cause for both 
corrosion and toxic effects, but also odor from e.g., hydrogen sulfide (Jansen et al., 2021). 
Fouling on membranes is caused by deposition of solutes and particles on membrane surfaces or in 
membrane pores because the concentration of impurities become highest near the membrane surface. 
Fouling degrades the membrane performance and affect the quality of the water produced (Ismail et 
al., 2019). Over time all membranes will foul, but fouling can be enhanced by the presence particles 
and colloids, scaling agents e.g. calcium carbonate, calcium sulphate or silica, metal oxides, oil, 
==PAGE:55==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384 
22-01-2024 
45 
 
organic matter, and microorganisms (Ismail et al., 2019). The content of suspended solids (SS) 
includes e.g. soil particles such as silt and clay, organic particles, and microorganisms, which are 
larger in size than dissolved particles. A high concentration of SS can beside causing fouling and 
particle deposition on membranes, also serve as carriers for other substances like nutrients, organics 
and heavy metals that tend to adsorb to particles (Boyd, 2019; Gong et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2021). 
The concentration of SS in different water types is very dependent on the origin of the specific waters 
and will often be highest in waters that have been in contact with soils, organic matter etc., such as 
surface water and wastewater (Table 17) (Boyd, 2019). The concentration of SS is linked to the 
turbidity of water, as more turbid water tends to have higher SS, which can be a useful indication 
when evaluating water quality.  
Organic matter covers carbon-containing compounds, which are derived from both living organism 
and industrial sources e.g., organic chemicals (C. Shah, 2017).  The organic content of water can be 
present in a dissolved state or as particulates, and the sum is the total organic carbon (TOC) (Tundisi 
& Tundisi, 2011b). TOC should be low in ultrapure water, as high content of organic material is linked 
to oxidation to carbon monoxide (CO) and CO2, which reduces the pH and increases corrosion and 
can potentially contaminate the hydrogen gas. High TOC is also linked to increased risk of biofouling 
on membranes and irreversible pore blogging (Gray et al., 2011; Yang et al., 2021). TOC is highest 
in waters that have been in contact with organic matter such as surface freshwater and treated 
wastewater (Table 17). TOC in brackish water is dependent on the balance between freshwater and 
saltwater input, and ranges in the same concentrations as surface freshwater (Table 17). In general 
TOC decreases with higher salinity, thus TOC in seawater is low (Table 17) (Thurman, 1985). 
However, seasonal algal blooms can cause temporary but considerable increases in TOC (Gray et al., 
2011). The concentration of TOC in groundwater and rainwater is also low as both water types are in 
limited contact with organic material (Table 17). TOC in collected rainwater will be dependent on the 
collection surface and can be as low as in rainwater or higher than surface freshwater and treated 
wastewater (Table 17).

5.2 Variability of Raw Water Resources for Power to X 
Treatment plant designs and operations such as RO and membrane filtration can be very source 
dependent and high variability in the quality of the feedwater can cause implications. In general, 
plants operate optimally if the feedwater quality is consistent  (Gray et al., 2011). The concentration 
range of the descriptive quality parameters for the different raw water resources insinuates the 
variability of the water source (Table 17). Overall, surface waters and collected rainwater can be 
found in a broad range of qualities, whereas groundwater quality varies in lower ranges and rainwater 
even lower (Table 17).  Different factors impact the composition and quality of the different water, 
and the essential for each water type are highlighted in the following sections.  
5.2.1 Rainwater and Collected Rainwater 
The composition of rainwater is influenced by the source of water vapor and ions in the atmosphere. 
The atmosphere contains e.g., marine salts, dust, combustion products, and nitrate from electrical 
activity in clouds (Boyd, 2019). Inland rainwater near oceans will show higher concentrations Na+ 
and Cl- due to oceanic vapor influence (Appelo & Postma, 2005).  In highly populated areas and 
industrialized regions, rain can have elevated sulfate concentrations due to atmospheric 
contamination with sulfur dioxide from fossil fuel combustion. Rain composition also varies during 
a rainfall event duration, and initial rainfall is more concentrated than later rainfall  (Boyd, 2019; Lee 
et al., 2023).  
The composition of collected rainwater depends on the collection surface, but will in general be of 
higher quality if collected from roofs compared to fortified areas (Ledin et al., 2004). If rainwater is 
collected from fortified areas the water may contain e.g., oils, pesticide residues, salt, PAH and PCB 
(Albrechtsen & Tang, 2016; Danish Nature Agency, 2014). If rainwater is collected from roofs, the 
roof material can release substances to the rainwater. Building materials can contain biocides, metal 
roofs can release metals such as zinc, iron, lead etc., and roofing felt can release PAHs, softeners, 
waterproofing agents, etc. (Albrechtsen & Tang, 2016; Danish Nature Agency, 2014). The collection 
surface has high impact on the water quality but is also a controllable factor in rainwater collection. 
A more dominant factor and challenge in the variability of collected rainwater quality is the “first 
flush” effect. The first flush effect is observed when the initial runoff during a rain event shows higher 
concentration of substances, because the first rain that falls after a dry period will wash of deposited 
particles, dust, leaves, animal feces etc. from the surface. The concentrations decrease as the sources 
depletes and subsequent runoff will show lower concentration levels (Albrechtsen & Tang, 2016; 
Ledin et al., 2004; Lee et al., 2023). An improved water quality can then be achieved if the first flush 
==PAGE:59==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384 
22-01-2024

of water during a rain event is not collected for reuse, but with the drawback of lower effective water 
volume. The opposite effect with higher concentrations after a long rain event has also been observed, 
which may be due to slow release of substances from e.g. roofing material (Ledin et al., 2004). 
Additionally, collected rainwater must be stored for use, which can deteriorate its quality over time. 
Rainwater collected from fortified areas can be stored in few days before considerable deterioration, 
where rainwater collected from roofs can be stored up to weeks before considerable deterioration 
(Danish Nature Agency, 2014).  
5.2.2 Groundwater 
The composition of groundwater is influenced by biogeochemical reactions in the geological layers, 
and groundwater-sediment interactions, which will vary among aquifers dependent on the geology in 
the area (B. Hansen & Thorling, 2018). The main drivers for variable groundwater composition is the 
interaction between the carbonic acid system and carbonates in aquifer systems and redox processes 
in the groundwater layers (Appelo & Postma, 2005; Thorling et al., 2023). Carbonic acid formed from 
aqueous CO2 associates with carbonate ions from dissolution of geological layers consisting of 
carbonate rocks (e.g. limestone) or carbonate minerals to form bicarbonate (HCO3-) (Appelo & 
Postma, 2005). The reaction between carbonate minerals and carbonic acid influences the dissolution 
and precipitation of carbonate minerals (such as Ca2+ and Mg2+) in groundwater, as increase of CO2 
results in dissolution, and removal of CO2 causes the carbonate minerals to precipitate (Appelo & 
Postma, 2005). Danish groundwater is highly characterized by carbonate minerals as aquifers in 
Denmark consist primarily of limestone and other calcareous rocks (Danish Environmental Protection 
Agency, n.d.-a; Thorling et al., 2023). The concentration of Ca2+ and Mg2+ in Danish groundwater is 
highest in the eastern part of Denmark, particularly in the capital area, and decreases in the western 
part of Denmark (see Appendix L for map) (GEUS, n.d.-e). In very deep or coastal near aquifers, the 
groundwater can be affected by saltwater intrusion resulting in salinization of the water. When 
pumping water from an aquifer interfering with ocean water, the saltwater intrusion can extend further 
into the aquifer and its characteristics becomes brackish (Boyd, 2019; Thorling et al., 2023).   
The geochemical environment in the groundwater zone is influenced by redox processes, and the 
ability of a soil layer to oxidize or reduce decreases with depth in groundwater reservoirs (Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.-a). In oxidized environment, groundwater is characterized by 
containing dissolved oxygen, sulphate and nitrate, ferric ion and manganese(III) ion. Moving towards 
a reduced environment, groundwater will not contain dissolved oxygen and nitrogen will appear in a 
reduced state as gaseous nitrogen or as ammonium/ammonia. In reduced groundwater, sulphate is no 
longer present, and iron and manganese will be found in reduced form as ferrous ion (Fe(II) ion) and 
manganese(II) ion, which precipitates if water becomes oxidized (Appelo & Postma, 2005; Boyd, 
2019; Danish Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.-a) (see Appendix M for reduction sequence). 
The redox state of groundwater in Denmark is categorized into water types A to D, ranging from 
oxidized (A) to heavily reduced (D), based on content of redox-sensitive substances in the water 
sample (Thorling et al., 2023) (see Appendix N for characterization).  
5.2.3 Surface Water  
The composition and quality of surface waters is affected by several factors such as size of water 
body, location and geology, climatic conditions, inflow and outflow, water retention time, point source 
==PAGE:60==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384

contribution, and the biological environment in the water (Boyd, 2019; Danish Nature Agency, 2014; 
Tundisi & Tundisi, 2011a). By other means, the quality of surface waters shows high variability.  
Surface waters are characterized by continuous inflow and outflow of water, and the higher flow the 
lower hydraulic retention time of the water (Boyd, 2019). The retention time impacts the water 
quality, the impact from physical, chemical, and biological processes becomes bigger with the longer 
retention time (Boyd, 2019; J. W. Hansen & Høgslund, 2023). With a longer retention time, the water 
quality is highly impacted by local inputs of nutrients from land runoff and inputs from point sources 
such as treated wastewater outlets, rainwater-sewer outlets, industry etc. A short retention time means, 
that the water is quickly renewed, and the local input of substances is distributed over a larger volume 
and has less impact on the water quality (J. W. Hansen & Høgslund, 2023). The quality of brackish 
coastal waters is more impacted by point source contribution than for seawater, and the quality also 
depends on the ocean connection, freshwater inputs, and tidal actions. Saltwater is heavier than 
freshwater causing a saline wedge beneath the freshwater flow resulting in stratification (Boyd, 2019). 
The layering limits the mixing between surface and bottom water, and since different biological 
processes take place in the two water layers, differences in water chemistry arise. The higher degree 
and period of stratification the greater differences in the water chemistry (J. W. Hansen & Høgslund, 
2023).  
5.2.4 Treated Wastewater  
The composition of treated wastewater varies in time and place due to variations in amounts of 
discharged substances, variations in water consumption, and infiltration from precipitation. 
Fluctuations in the loads of water and pollutants can appear daily, weakly, or monthly, with up to a 
factor 10 between the highest and lowest hourly load (Jansen et al., 2021). Periods with higher flow 
rates results in higher load at the treatment plant, which can decrease the treatment efficiency. 
Additionally, increased flowrates can shorten the sedimentation time reducing the particle removal 
rate and result in higher SS concentration in the effluent. Thus, the raw wastewater flowrate is 
correlated to effluent quality (Yang et al., 2021). The composition and quality of the treated 
wastewater also varies dependent on the type of inlet wastewater e.g., domestic, municipal, or 
industrial wastewater, as well as treatment technology and efficiency at the treatment plant.  
In Denmark, the ionic composition of treated wastewater will resemble that of groundwater, as the 
water supply is obtained from groundwater abstraction. Treated wastewater effluent must obey with 
discharge criteria to avert pollution of the aquatic environment provided by national laws and decrees 
(Danish Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.-g). In general, the quality of wastewater effluent 
should comply with discharge criteria, but the variations in the composition and volume of the influent 
can result in the discharge criteria being exceeded. Additionally, the standard discharge criteria are 
only set for chemical- and biological oxygen demand (COD and BOD), total phosphorous (TP) and 
total nitrogen (TN), and do not include criteria on particles (SS, TDS), microorganisms, metals, and 
other inorganic materials. Therefore, the quality of treated wastewater should be assessed for the 
specific WWTP when considering water supply for PtX.

5.3.1 Groundwater Quality in Skive  
A reported groundwater quality will depend on the specific conditions at each monitoring well, as 
well as the geological formation of the aquifer and the depth of sampling. The geology in Skive is 
characterized by multiple layers of Quaternary sand and Quaternary clay layers (ice age deposits), 
deeper clay layers that from Pre-Quaternary deposits (before Ice Age), and deep limestone (see 
Appendix O for hydrostratigraphic model of Jutland) (Thorling et al., 2023). Groundwater in Skive 
is primarily abstracted from aquifers in the Quaternary layers or aquifers in buried valleys in the Pre-
Quaternary clay, which have been filled up with Quaternary deposits, but also from aquifers in Pre-
Quaternary sand/gravel (GEUS, 2019; Thorling et al., 2023). Because of the geological formation in 
Skive, the average water hardness is 10.7 °dH (190.5 mg/l CaCO3) characterized as medium hard, 
which is the second softest water category to be found in Denmark (see Appendix L for hardness in 
Denmark) (GEUS, n.d.-e).

5.3.3 Quality of Skive Fjord  
Skive Fjord belongs to the least salty part of the Limfjord, because of lower seawater exchange and 
input of large volumes of fresh water from several large river systems in Skive area (Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.-c; SEGES, 2018). The water mixing affects the hydraulic 
retention time in the fjord, which is known to have a significant impact on the water quality (J. W. 
Hansen & Høgslund, 2023). The freshwater input to estuaries varies seasonally and can decrease by 
a factor 4 to 10 during summer. The annual variability in retention time is expected to vary by a factor 
2 (Conley et al., 2000). The retention time in Skive Fjord is affected by tidal exchange and wind 
circulation. Skive Fjord is a partially mixed estuary characterized by micro-tidal conditions, and the 
retention time can vary from 2-3 months in summer to 1 month in winter (Møhlenberg et al., 2007).

(Danmarks Miljøportal, n.d.). Water quality in Skive Fjord is monitored in several different

opening where Skive Fjord separates from Lovns Bredning (Location shown by Id: 93740007 in 
Appendix Q). To represent the quality of the water closer to the bay, where BioCirc could abstract 
water, older data from two monitoring stations closer to the bay (Location shown by Id: 93740009 
and Id: 93740008 in Appendix Q) were included as well. Thus, data from the three stations was 
collected in the period 1980 to 2023. Selected parameters were analyzed with respect to seasonal, 
location specific, and depth related variation using mean values and standard deviation. Based on all 
analyzed data (given in Appendix R) the main quality parameters of Skive Fjord were presented along

The pH in Skive Fjord is slightly alkaline and quite stable during seasonal and depth changes (Table

The conductivity of Skive Fjord increases during summer and spring and increases with depth as 
result of increased salinity (Table 20 and Appendix R).

nutrients and produce organic material and oxygen in the illuminated surface layer.  TOC in Skive

oxygen concentration in the surface layer, and high concentration of nutrients in deeper water, where 
oxygen depletion also can occur, and the breakdown of organic matter then becomes limited (J. W. 
Hansen & Høgslund, 2023). The oxygen concentration in Skive Fjord shows the same pattern (Table 
20 and Appendix R). The water chemistry changes dependent on monitoring location and varies more 
closer to the bay (Table 20). Additionally, the quality in the bay is also highly impacted by input from 
point source contribution from e.g., treated wastewater outlets, rainwater-sewer overflows and 
outlets, pond farming and industry, which supplies both nutrients and organic content to the fjord.  
Abstracting water from Skive Fjord to supply PtX would require pipelaying at the bed of the fjord, 
and the quality of the feed water to the treatment facility will be reflected by the water chemistry of 
deeper fjord water with higher salinity, conductivity, and nutrient levels. The exact water quality will 
also depend on freshwater outlets and point sources near the pipe laying. Additionally, the pipe flow 
can resuspend sediments from the fjord bed resulting in higher SS concentration than measured in the 
fjord, but also of organic matter and nutrients which are stored in the sediment (J. W. Hansen & 
Høgslund, 2023). The feed water quality can also be affected by macroalgae and benthic animals 
from the fjord bed environment. If BioCirc were to use water from Skive Fjord as supply for ultrapure 
water production, the quality of the feedwater will vary dependent on season, biological processes, 
and distance to the bay, as well as be influenced by land runoff and point sources near the pipelaying 
location. The main challenges in terms of the water quality in Skive Fjord is the salinity, the high 
biological and nutrient load, and the suspended solids.

Quantity 
The quantity and extent of water resources in Denmark is large for rainwater, fresh groundwater, 
brackish water, and seawater (Table 22). The quantified extent of rainwater is estimated to ~16 billion 
m3/year reflecting the annual average precipitation on the surface of Denmark. The quantity of fresh 
groundwater is limited to 1.1 billion m3/year equal to the sustainably available groundwater resources 
on national scale. The extent of surface freshwater is not quantified but is limited to large streams and 
lakes in Denmark (Table 22). Treated wastewater is quantified to 683 mil m3 based on the total 
effluent from municipal wastewater treatment plants in Denmark in 2021. Treated wastewater and 
fresh surface water are marked as medium, as the extent of these resources in volumes, that can meet 
the demand for PtX, are lower than the remaining four (Table 22). Brackish water and seawater are 
effectively unlimited resources. The extent of brackish water governs fjords and coastal waters, while 
sea water governs the open oceans.  
Availability 
Even though all the raw water resources can be found in medium or large quantities in Denmark, their 
availability can be limited by other factors. Using rainwater for PtX is contingent upon collection and 
storage system construction, and the supply is unstable because of high seasonal variation (Table 22). 
However, excessive rainwater volumes are becoming a larger problem in Denmark, which suggest 
for increasing possibilities of exploiting this resource. The availability of fresh groundwater is 
location specific, as it depends on how much of the sustainable groundwater resource is already 
exploited at the location (Table 22). Groundwater abstraction is limited by the Water Supply Act and 
municipal regulation and can only be assessed with an active abstraction permission. Additionally, 
the sustainably available groundwater volume determined by GEUS does not account for the quality 
of the resource, and groundwater of good quality is of high demand for drinking water supply. Hence, 
the volume of the sustainable resource might not correctly reflect the amount of groundwater 
available for all uses. With raising challenges in groundwater pollution from e.g., nitrate and 
pesticides the volume of good quality groundwater for drinking water supply decreases, and the 
resource might be even more strictly reserved to this purpose. The availability of groundwater for 
industrial use could be narrowed down to resources outside OSD, OD and IOL.  
The availability of fresh surface water is also location specific, as it depends on the quantity of this 
resource at the location, national regulation, and ownership of the resource (Table 22). Surface 
freshwater resources are a necessity aquatic life and ecosystems, why frameworks on conservation of 
the ecological status of surface water bodies limits its availability. In contrast, treated wastewater is 
available in large and continuously produced quantities in Denmark without any other demand for 
the resource (Table 22). However, due to an increased centralization of treatment plants in Denmark, 
the availability of treated wastewater in sufficient quantities for PtX application can be limited to 
high-capacity treatment plants.  
The extent of coastal line in Denmark makes brackish water and seawater are readily available (Table 
22). Yet, brackish water in both fjords and at the coast can be limited by conservation regulation 
similar to surface freshwaters. Brackish water is also available from coastal or deep salt water intruded 
==PAGE:71==
Julie Elise Christoffersen

aquifers. Coastal near aquifers are mostly outside of OSD, OD and IOL in Denmark, but the 
availability depends on whether water abstraction from coastal aquifers will cause any effect on near 
aquifers used for drinking water supply. Similarly, the availability of brackish water from deep 
aquifers can also be limited from the importance of drinking water supply from above laying aquifers. 
Seawater is abundant in the open ocean, but the availability of this resources is highest for offshore 
placement, where else it is limited to long pipe laying.  
The availability of various raw water resources for PtX varies dependent on the location, further 
influenced by national regulation. Despite this, the prospects for sufficient water resource capacity 
for PtX in Denmark seems promising.  
Quality 
Water quality with respect to ultrapure water production is another parameter, that varies between the 
raw water resources. Rainwater contains low concentrations of ions resulting in low hardness, TDS, 
and conductivity, which is a good basis for ultrapure water production (Table 22). The concentration 
of salts in rainwater will however be higher in coastal areas and can further increase with high wind. 
The quality of rainwater will deteriorate during collection, which can partly be controlled by choice 
of collection material (Table 22). The impact from first flush effect is less controllable, as the extent 
of deposition can vary seasonally and spatially, which makes the quality of the collected rainwater 
less predictable. Because of the continuous water demand for PtX, the stored rainwater is 
continuously used, and the effect of storage should not have a high influence on the quality compared 
to the first flush effect. In terms of quality, fresh groundwater has medium TDS, low TOC and SS, 
and stable temperature and pH (Table 22). The main problem causing substances for PtX application 
is the hardness causing ions, and the hardness of fresh groundwater vary considerably in Denmark. 
The hardness problem can be averted by natural softening from ion exchange if groundwater is 
abstracted from coastal near aquifers. Further, the ionic composition and concentrations in 
groundwater vary both spatially and with depth but does often not show temporal variation at a 
specific location. Though, the water quality can vary more in terrain aquifers compared to deep 
aquifers, as these aquifers are more vulnerable to impacts from activities on the surface (e.g. farming). 
Deep aquifers are by this reason often used for drinking water supply, which can imply higher 
competition for deep groundwater.  
In contrast to fresh groundwater the overall quality of fresh surface water can vary considerably both 
spatially and seasonally (Table 22). Both TDS, TOC, and SS can vary in a large range up to quite high 
values (Table 22). Additionally, fresh surface water is highly impacted by point source contribution 
of particularly nutrient loads. The quality of treated wastewater can vary between treatment plants 
and with fluctuations in water loads (Table 22). In general wastewater effluent in Denmark should 
reflect the discharge criteria, but as these are only set for biological and nutrient load, additional 
preservations to substances such as metals and pollutants in the effluent should be taken. Brackish 
water also shows variable quality dependent on location, season, distance to bay, and the mix of saline 
and freshwater inputs (Table 22). The quality of brackish water is also affected by stratification, and 
the quality in upper and lower water layers can be quite different. If brackish water is abstracted from 
coastal wells, the SS concentration can be decreased from a natural filtration throughout the well, 
==PAGE:72==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384

which can enhance its applicability for PtX. Contrary, the composition of seawater is similar at 
different location, with subject to spatial and seasonal variations in salinity. However, the salinity in 
seawater results in high hardness, TDS, and conductivity (Table 22).   
In general, the hard waters such as fresh groundwater, brackish water and seawater can be problematic 
due to or scaling and corrosion, where high concentration of suspended solids, biological and nutrient 
loads such as in fresh surface water and treated wastewater can cause for problems with fouling, 
clogging, biocorrosion and contamination. However, how suitable a raw water resource if for 
ultrapure water production depends not only on the quality but also on the variability of the quality, 
as variable water sources can complicate the treatment.  
Treatment Requirements and Energy Consumption 
Overall, each of the resources show different challenges regarding their quality, and the treatments 
requirements correlated to the quality. The energy consumption in treatment thus reflects the 
treatment requirements. Treatment requirements for collected rainwater is low to medium (Table 22), 
as the quality is generally good provided that the collection material is chosen hereafter. However, 
the first flush effect enhances the variability and requirements for treatment. Based on the potential 
content of organic matter, suspended solids, and microorganisms in collected rainwater the treatment 
requirements are estimated from that of surface water (Table 22). The energy consumption in 
treatment and the required volume to produce ultrapure water from rainwater is also estimated from 
that of surface water and may prove to be lower (Table 22).  
Fresh groundwater shows the lowest requirements for treatment, why this source requires the lowest 
quantity to produce ultrapure water (Table 22). However, it might be worth considering a softening 
process, if using hard og very hard groundwater to avert scaling problems in ultrapure water 
production, as hardness is not removed in filtration and aeration. This would add to both required 
quantity and energy consumption thus equate (or even heighten) groundwater with the other raw 
water resources. The treatment requirements and energy consumption for fresh surface water and 
treated wastewater are the same as both raw water resources require removal of particles, organics, 
and microorganisms (Table 22). This also means that the same amount of fresh surface water and 
treated wastewater is needed to produce ultrapure water (Table 22). The quality of brackish water lies 
between that of fresh surface water and sea water in a variable range, which will be reflected in the 
treatment requirements. To remove the content of salt, brackish water requires desalination (RO), but 
it also contains organics and suspended solids from freshwater inputs, which can require filtration or 
sedimentation before RO to avoid fouling (Table 22). Thus, the exact energy consumption and 
quantity for ultrapure water production will depend on the exact water quality and will increase with 
salinity. Seawater requires high treatment before ultrapure water production for full retention of ions, 
which requires the most energy and raw water volume to produced ultrapure water (Table 22).  
The treatment options for different raw water resource for ultrapure water production suggested by 
Silhorko-Eurowater serve as guidance. The energy consumption, treatment recovery and associated 
reject stream of the different raw water sources will in all cases be dependent on the applied treatment 
technology, equipment, and process construction.

Reject Water 
The level of wastewater generation and wastewater content from ultrapure water production is only 
lightly touched upon in this assessment. The suggested treatment processes by Silhorko-Eurowater  
(Tækker Madsen, 2022) presented in this assessment do not contain specifications on wastewater 
generation, why the reject stream from pretreatment and ultrapure water production from different 
raw water resources are estimated as inversely correlated to the quality of the raw water (Table 22). 
By other means, the more requirements for removing substances from the raw water, the more 
substances will be present in the reject stream. The reject stream will be composed of residual 
concentrate waste from the treatment and can require additional treatment before disposal or 
discharge. Reject water from rainwater treatment will contain retained particles etc., from the 
collection surface, where groundwater reject will show high hardness from removal of cations and 
carbonates and potential concentration of nitrate (Table 22). Reject water from surface freshwater and 
treated wastewater will show high organic and nutrient load, but especially for treated wastewater the 
nutrient and both synthetic and natural organic compound load complicates its disposal (Table 22). 
The concentrated wastewater no longer obeys with discharge criteria and will require additional 
sludge handling and treatment. The presence of bacteria, infectants and pollutants in wastewater may 
also require disinfection of the reject stream, also from a health perspective in work environment 
(Table 22). Desalination of saline waters will result in a reject stream of concentrated brine, which is 
pronouncedly highest from seawater desalination (Table 22). Brine requires dilution before discharge, 
increasing the water demand, unless the brine is exploited otherwise e.g., for salt mining. The brine 
from brackish water treatment will be less concentrated than saltwater brine but will contain higher 
organic and nutrient low from the freshwater input. Thus, wastewater from treatment of each of the 
presented raw water resources will pose different challenges and post treatment requirements, which 
is a substantial consideration in the use of a raw water resource.  
6.1 Evaluation of Water Resources for Power to X 
All the evaluated raw water resources show different opportunities and challenges in quantity, 
availability, variability, treatment- and energy requirements, and reject stream. So how to determine 
the optimal resource?  
Considering the full process from raw water resource to ultrapure water quality and electrolysis 
supply, the preferred attributes of a raw water resource would be a stable resource in both quantity 
and quality. A stable water quality provides better conditions for establishment of an efficient 
treatment system to produce ultrapure water. A stable quantity means, that the resource can be 
supplied to the PtX plant in both continuous and sufficient volumes. Although stability may provide 
the best prerequisites for running a hydrogen production, it is not necessarily that straightforward. 
Each of the assessed raw water resources show different requirements, challenges, and different 
reservations to be addressed before application. Additionally, there will always be different local 
conditions and regulations that apply affecting the options of raw water sources.  
First and foremost, a water resource needs to be of significant quantity, but it also needs to be available 
for use. The relationship between quantity and availability of the resources in Denmark shows that 
seawater and brackish water are the resources in Denmark of largest quantity and highest availability

6.1.2 Considerations in Selection of Raw Water Resources 
Rainwater and seawater both show four advantages and two disadvantages (Table 23). Comparing 
the two, rainwater would be the preferred choice if aiming for lower treatment requirements, energy 
consumption and authority regulation, but seawater would be the preferred choice if aiming for a 
secure and stable water supply. Similarly, fresh groundwater shows four advantages and two medium 
which suggests the highest suitability seen from a PtX perspective (Table 23). In contrary, if seen 
from a resource sustainability perspective or the perspective of the authorities, groundwater may not 
be a suitable raw water resource for PtX. The renewal rate of groundwater is very long while the 
competition for the resource is high, and it serves as the foundation for all drinking water supply. 
Hence, the interest in further exploiting the resource may be very limited, especially if other resources 
are available.  
A further consideration is the importance of low variability in quality compared to the quality in terms 
of treatment requirements and energy consumption. Treatment systems operate most efficiently if the 
feedwater quality is consistent, as high variability in feedwater quality can cause implications. The 
presented treatment requirements and energy consumption is somewhat based on an assumed average 
water quality of the respective water sources. If the water quality is very variable, the system must 
however be designed to handle the highest load and not an average load. Therefore, the treatment 
requirements and energy consumption may appear to be higher for a variable resource, than what is 
at first presented. Opposed to this, a resource with low quality requires more treatment reflecting 
higher energy consumption, but if the quality is not variable and the predictability of the feedstock is 
high, the system should be able to run with high efficiency. For instance, a resource such as seawater 
might have high energy consumption in treatment, but the composition of seawater is stable, 
compared to e.g., fresh surface water, which require lower treatment and energy consumption, but 
has a variable water quality. High predictability in feedwater quality can provide higher system 
efficiency in treatment, but a stable resource of low quality regarding ultrapure water production can 
be attached to another challenge in handling of a more complicated reject stream.

This also leads to considerations in evaluating resources with similar treatment requirements and 
energy consumption, such as fresh surface water and treated wastewater. The advantage of using 
wastewater compared to fresh surface water is the higher availability, but treated wastewater can 
entail a more complex reject stream. It is also worth noting, that the energy consumption for treatment 
of groundwater is presented to be 2 kWh, while that of collected rainwater, surface freshwater and 
treated wastewater is 2.2 kWh. In the grand scheme of things, a difference of 0.2 kWh might not be 
as influential as e.g. a stable supply.  
Additionally, if the location of a PtX plant is fixed, as in the case of BioCirc, the need and possibilities 
to transport the resource to its point of use is worth including. In all cases some transport of the 
resource will be necessary, but if a plant is located far from e.g. the largest and most stable resource, 
the transportation adds as potential drawback and additional costs. For BioCirc, rainwater collection 
as raw water resource requires less transportation than treated wastewater from Skive WWTP and 
brackish water from Skive Fjord. On the other hand, treated wastewater and water from Skive Fjord 
is available in large and continuous volumes compared to rainwater. Fresh groundwater can also be 
accessed close to BioCirc’s site, but this resource is in contrary more restrained by authority 
processing and regulatory permits. To assure adequate water supply, BioCirc can be compelled to 
transport raw water from a further distance to their site, than what could have been the case if the 
focus was to determine the optimal location of their facility.  
Thus, it is emphasized that evaluating the suitability of a raw water resource for a PtX application 
depends on the specific optimization goal and whether the focus is on considerations such as resource 
sustainability, energy savings, financial aspects, or facility location. The expected goals for a PtX 
company would be a stable and easily accessible resource, that is simple and inexpensive to treat for 
ultrapure water production. The apparent choice for a stable resource with inexpensive treatment 
would be the use of fresh groundwater. Rainwater can also be simple and inexpensive to treat but is 
less stable and less accessible. Treated wastewater, brackish water and seawater are apparent options 
for stable and available resources but are less simple and more expensive in treatment.  
7 Learnings and Deliberations   
In the development of this thesis some learnings, considerations, and attention points have emerged 
in connection with the mapping of resources for PtX. Overall, this subject is yet uncharted ground, 
and numerous components in this project have not been combined in this way before. The 
understanding of water resources as a determining parameter for PtX application is still evolving, 
which was particularly reflected by the limited experience within the field. This assessment is 
conducted solely from existing information and data, why the results and outcomes should be viewed 
in the light of this approach and the fact that research in this area is in the early stages of development. 
Further, it should be noted that the analysis in Skive has focused exclusively on BioCirc, but other 
interests in using the water resources for PtX or other industrial purposes should be considered in 
their further assessment.  
7.1.1 Water Quality in Relation to PtX 
To examine water quality of different raw water resource for PtX application is also a new perspective 
in a recently developing state. Water quality is often considered in relation to the health of humans 
==PAGE:80==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384 
22-01-2024 
70 
 
and ecosystems, why descriptive and measuring parameters are defined mainly for securing drinking 
water supply and maintaining ecosystems. Water quality monitoring in Denmark is performed to 
secure the quality of waters for their use and preservation. Therefore, the assessment of water quality 
of resources for PtX purpose is described in the light of the existing water quality data framework. 
Even though, much can be described from existing monitoring, all components in a water source are 
not covered in monitoring, and different parameters are monitored for different water types. 
Particularly for treated wastewater effluent, it was observed that limited publicly available quality 
data, could be found for treatment plants in Denmark other than for parameters required for discharge 
compliance. Neither was it possible to obtain additional data on e.g., TDS, conductivity, metals, and 
organic pollutants etc. from Skive Vand.  To adequately describe the quality of treated wastewater in 
relation to PtX requires receiving additional data from the specific treatment plant or conducting 
additional measurement on the effluent in terms of conductivity, specific cation and anion 
concentrations, metals and other oxidants, and additional contaminants e.g., pharmaceuticals or other 
organic substances that can complicate the reject stream and is disposal. The latter also applies for 
water quality in Skive Fjord, as it is affected by point source contribution from Skive WWTP. With 
respect to Skive Fjord, recent measurements were only available from monitoring far from the bay. 
As the quality can vary notably closer to the bay, additional measurements in the bay would be 
preferable to evaluate the water quality for BioCirc’s PtX in Vinkel. Overall, to better assess, evaluate 
and compare water quality of different raw water resources for PtX suggests for introduction of 
focused analyses on the (until now) known water quality parameters for PtX. 
7.1.2 Groundwater Quantification and Regulation 
In general, it applies to the whole of Denmark, that permission for water abstraction is granted by the 
individual municipalities based on provisions in the Water Supply Act. Thus, the municipal 
regulations frame the use of water resources within municipal borders. Because regulations and 
frameworks can be different between municipalities, it restrains the use of resources across municipal 
borders. For example, groundwater aquifers are not confined by municipal borders, why multiple 
municipalities manage the same water resources with potentially different frameworks. By this 
means, that permission to abstract groundwater from the same aquifer is managed by different 
authorities with different approaches to sustainable groundwater abstraction. Skive Municipality 
operates with the rule of thumb that 1/3 or the net precipitation can be given in abstraction permits, 
which might be different in the neighboring Viborg municipality. In the process of mapping 
groundwater quantity in Skive municipality, it was observed that some aquifers in Skive showed high 
degree of exploitation primarily because of many abstraction permits granted in Viborg municipality. 
So even though the mapping showed that the sustainable groundwater resource is not fully exploited 
in Skive, it will be relevant to evaluate the water balance of groundwater on resource level in terms 
of aquifers and catchment areas rather than within municipal borders.  
Additionally, determination of the groundwater recharge is a difficult measure, and will vary 
depending on season due to seasonal variation in net precipitation and infiltration. Likewise, the 
groundwater demand can vary with season particularly regarding water demand for irrigation, which 
increases in dry periods. A future approach to assessing groundwater quantity could include mapping 
of a seasonal groundwater balance additional to an annual/total balance. A further point of attention

is the fact, that Jupiter is not always updated on abstraction permits, which connects an assessment 
based on publicly available data to some uncertainty. This can potentially have significant impact 
when considering the water balance on local scale, but less on regional and national scale.  
7.1.3 Water Balance and Reuse at BioCirc 
The focus in the project was on the water demand for PtX, and the full water balance at BioCirc have 
not been mapped. The volume of wastewater generation from PtX depends on the choice of raw water 
resource and is important to address in their future assessment. The use of e.g. treated wastewater or 
polluted water as raw water source for ultrapure water production generates a wastewater stream with 
unwanted substances. However, these substances would, in principle, eventually enter the water 
system and aquatic environment, and the use of these resources enhances the possibility of addressing 
these substances by e.g. evaporation and incineration. Additionally, the BioCirc cluster concept 
prompts a symbiotic approach to water supply, with the possibility to include internal water reuse to 
increase the self-sufficiency. When the hydrogen is oxidized e.g, by composition, H2O is regenerated 
as vapor. If the hydrogen is used internally, BioCirc can condensate the vapor and recover it as liquid 
water for reuse. If the use is externally, the water vapor may enter the atmosphere or be recovered at 
the point of use entering another water balance.   
8 Conclusion  
In this project water requirements concerning quality and quantity in relation to PtX hydrogen 
production by electrolysis have been examined as well as the challenges and opportunities in water 
supply from different raw water resources. The findings showed that the extent of raw water resources 
in Denmark show good possibility for PtX supply, but local conditions apply to their availability. 
BioCirc’s PtX initiative in Skive was used as a case study to evaluate raw water resources for PtX on 
a local scale. With subject to unknown water loss and the analysis focusing exclusively on BioCirc, 
the water resource capacity in Skive is sufficient for BioCirc’s PtX demand.  
The water demand for electrolysis-based hydrogen depends on the size of electrolyzer, but on average 
~0.2 m3/h is required per MW electrolyzer. BioCirc’s demand for running a 150 MW electrolysis was 
identified to 26.7 m3/h corresponding to an annual demand of ~0.23 mil m3 when the electrolysis 
operates at 100% capacity. Electrolysis requires water of ultrapure quality, where the applied 
technology defines the exact quality requirements with no unified water quality standard for all 
electrolyzers. Guidelines present that the conductivity should at least be <5 µS/cm, the water should 
not contain more than 50 µg/l total organic carbon and 3 µg/l silica, and preferably, be low in metal 
cations, halogen anions, oxidants, suspended solids, and microorganisms. Little is still known on the 
long-time effects of impurities, but the main challenges govern conductivity disturbance and damage 
to electrodes, scaling, deposition, membrane fouling and corrosion. Ultrapure water is produced from 
a pretreated raw water source in a four-step purification process. The quantity of raw water for 
production of ultrapure water depends on the initial water quality and the treatment efficiencies. To 
account for water loss in pretreatment and purification, 1.4 times the ultrapure water demand is 
needed if using groundwater, 1.5 times when using treated wastewater and surface freshwater, and 
3.3 times when using seawater.

Fresh groundwater, surface freshwater, rainwater, treated wastewater, brackish water, and seawater 
were mapped as potential raw water resources for PtX in Denmark and for BioCirc’s 150 MW 
electrolysis. The fixed location in Vinkel, Skive, limits BioCirc’s options of raw water resources to 
fresh groundwater, rainwater collection, treated wastewater from Skive WWTP and brackish water 
from Skive fjord. The main findings in the evaluation of quantity and quality, as well as challenges 
and opportunities of different raw water resources include:  
 The national extent of rainwater as resource approximates to 16 billion m3/year, though the 
availability is limited because of requirements for collection and storage systems. By rainwater 
collection on 50% of the production site, BioCirc can cover around 50% of their water demand for 
PtX. The quality of rainwater shows good basis for ultrapure water production, but the water 
supply is unstable due to high temporal variation.  
 The sustainably available fresh groundwater resource amounts to 1.1 billion m3/year on national 
scale. Overexploitation is prevalent in many regions, why the availability depends on the local 
exploitation degree and regulatory approval. The resource is not fully exploited in Skive, and the 
unexploited volume was found to 72.6 mil m3/year and 54.4 mil m3/year, using two different 
quantification methods. In both cases the annual demand for BioCirc’s PtX plant correspond to 
less than 1% of the unexploited volume. The quality of fresh groundwater varies geographically 
and with depth but show low variability at a specific location. The treatment requirements are low, 
but high hardness can pose a challenge.  
 The use of surface freshwater for PtX does not appear to be an evident option. Sufficient quantity 
is limited to larger lakes and streams and the availability is further limited by national regulation 
and preservation. The water quality in surface freshwater bodies varies both geographically and 
temporally, which can complicate the treatment.  
 The national discharge of treated wastewater amounted to 683 mil m3 in 2021 making this source 
an available option. The dry weather outlet volume from Skive WWTP in 2022 was around 5 times 
the demand for BioCirc’s PtX. The outlet volumes depend on the plant capacity and fluctuate over 
time, but a continuous water supply is guaranteed from the continuous wastewater generation. The 
quality of treated wastewater imposes higher treatment requirements and complications in reject 
water handling and discharge.  
 Brackish water is abundant along the coastal line of Denmark, available in large, stable volumes. 
The volume of Skive Fjord was estimated to 525 mil m3, whereof BioCirc’s PtX demand 
corresponds to 0.15%. The combined freshwater inputs and seawater mixing, makes the water 
quality variable both geographically and temporarily. The treatment requirements highly depend 
on the salinity, but also varies with the freshwater influence. A reject stream of brine combined 
with biological and nutrient load is generated in treatment complicating its discharge. 
 The open sea provides effectively unlimited volumes of seawater, but its availability as resource 
for PtX is limited to coastal near or offshore plants. The composition of seawater is stable with 
some variations in salinity. The high salinity entails high requirements for treatment and results in 
a reject stream of concentrated brine, which is especially difficult to discharge inland.  
A stable and easily accessible resource, that is simple and inexpensive to treat for ultrapure water 
production is preferable for PtX production, and the apparent choice for such resource in Denmark 
==PAGE:83==
Julie Elise Christoffersen  
s160384 
22-01-2024 
73 
 
will be the use of fresh groundwater. This resource is, however, subject to high demand and national 
regulations limiting its availability. Treated wastewater, brackish water and seawater are apparent 
options for stable and available resources, but these are less simple and more expensive in treatment. 
Thus, there is no unequivocal answer to the choice of raw water resource for PtX application, as it 
depends on facility location, requirements and goals, national and municipal regulation and the 
perspectives included, why it must be evaluated case specifically. In the case of BioCirc, fresh 
groundwater is an option and an evident choice if permission is granted by the municipality. The use 
of wastewater from Skive WWTP is a notable option to support and enhance sustainable water use in 
Denmark. 
The insights from this study contribute the evaluation of water resources for BioCirc’s PtX facility in 
Skive, to the understanding of a balanced use of local water resources, and to valuable considerations 
for the future establishment of PtX in Denmark.
