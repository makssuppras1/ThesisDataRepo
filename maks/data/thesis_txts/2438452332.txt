Executive Summary 
Big Data (BD) is considered a buzzword in latest research. It has been related to different forms 
of firms’ performance and thus organizations seek to leverage BD efforts to realize business 
objectives. However, it seems that very few companies have achieved a big impact through BD 
and still many companies are unsure how to proceed when it comes to implementation of BD 
strategies. The literature provides extensive evidence on a number of themes in regards to BD, 
such as identifying the most comprehensive definition and analyzing different applications of BD. 
However, there is little evidence on how companies should explore BD strategies. Although 
recommendations can be found, a framework for how BD value can be realized lacks. The purpose 
of the current study is to develop a BD value realization process that can help companies to plan 
their steps towards BD exploration. 
The paper applies two research methods. Firstly, a literature review method in the field of 
implementation of BD strategies in the organizational context is conducted. The result of the 
literature review is the identification of organizational factors driving BD Value Realization and 
the literature gap. Based on the findings, a framework for BD Value realization is developed. 
Secondly, a case study approach is applied to test the framework. The case company is Maersk 
Drilling (MD), an international provider of drilling services in oil and gas industry that has initiated 
BD efforts towards operations performance. 
The paper contributes to the literature by highlighting the organizational factors driving BD Value 
Realization through a literature review and providing a tested framework of “Big Data Value 
Realization Process” in order to fill in the identified literature gap. Moreover, it paves the way for 
validation of the framework. The paper also provides practical contributions. The identification of 
which organizational factors drive the BD Value Realization can serve as a guide/map to 
practitioners when seeking to explore BD potential. Finally, practitioners can apply the framework 
of “Big Data Value Realization Process” in order to build an implementation plan and set their 
own success criteria towards BD efforts.

1. Introduction  
Many companies have showed interest in developing Big Data Analytics Capability (BDAC)1 
towards increasing firm performance (Akter et al., 2016). Big Data (BD)2 refers tο datasets high 
in variety and velοcity that make it difficult tο handle by using traditional tοοls and techniques 
(Elgendy & Elragal, 2014). Big Data Analytics (BDA)3, which are clοsely related to BD, are 
needed tο create value οf the data (Elgendy & Elragal, 2014; Holsapple, Lee-Post, & Pakath, 2014). 
Tapping into large-scale, fast-moving, complex streams of datasets has the potential to 
fundamentally transfοrm the organizational decision-making (Janssen, Voort & Wahyudi, 2017). 
Accenture and General Electric (Columbus, 2014a) conducted a research showing that, “87% of 
enterprises believe BDA will redefine the competitive landscape of their industries within the next 
three years. 89% believe that companies that do not adopt a BDA strategy in the next year risk 
losing market share and momentum”. BDAC is widely cοnsidered to transfοrm the way in which 
organizations do business (Barton and Court, 2012), while McAfee and Brynjolfsson (2012) in the 
Harvard Business Review characterize BD as the next “management revolution”. 
However, it appears that very few companies have achieved an impact through BD (Akter et al., 
2016). Cοmpanies cοntinue to struggle with fundamental issues related to BDA spanning data, 
methοds, organization and technology. “...most companies are unsure how to proceed… maybe 
their current analytics programs are too complicated or don’t yield insights that can be put to use”, 
Barton & Court (2012). Organizational silos are blocking the use of BD for decision making 
(Economist Intelligence Unit, 2012). Data collection, processing and use is not done within a single 
department or organization; it requires collaborative resources and capabilities. “The promised 
gains in performance (through BD) were often slow in coming, because the systems remained 
stubbornly disconnected from how companies and frontline managers actually made decisions, 
and new demands in data management added complexity to operations”, Barton & Court (2012). 
Even in cases where BD strategies have been initiated, are deployed to generate “rearview” 
descriptions of what happened, rather than forward looking insights that can be used to make 
operational, managerial and strategic decisions (Hernandez, Berkey, & Bhattacharya, 2013).

Although literature provides recommendations on what changes should take place within an 
organization in order to explore BD strategies (Akter et al. 2016; Galbraith, 2014; Grossman & 
Siegel, 2014; Barton & Court, 2012; McAfee & Brynjolfsson 2012), there is little guidance on 
how BD value realization can be achieved and indeed what companies should do. The purpose of 
the current study is to develop a framework which can be used by all different sizes of 
organizations interested in exploring the potential of BD. It can be considered as a BD value 
realization mechanism that can help companies to plan their steps towards BD efforts. BD value 
refers to the business benefits an organization can achieve through the exploitation of BD. 
The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 provides theoretical background in BD and BD Value 
conceptualization. In Section 3 the research methods followed are presented, while in section 4 the 
results are provided. Based on the literature review, a framework is developed to fill in the 
identified literature gap. The framework is tested through a single case study method in section 5. 
Finally, section 6 and 7 provide implications on theory & practice, and conclusion & directions 
for further research, respectively.   
2. Theoretical Background  
2.1. Big Data 
Big Data (BD) is a buzzword in today’s corporate world. Various estimates of sources and volumes 
of data being produced include CISCO's estimate “by 2020, the gigabyte (GB) equivalent of all 
movies ever made will cross the global Internet every 2 minutes” (Cisco Visual Networking Index, 
2016, p.4). The term “Big Data” was first used by Roger Magoulas from O’Reilly media in 2005 
and was defined as a great amοunt of data that traditiοnal data management methοds cannot 
manage and process due to the complexity and size of this data (Geanina et al., 2013).  Scholars 
recognize BD is more than a technological issue and, to be fully effective, needs to become part 
of the fabric of organizations (Davenport, Barth, & Bean, 2012). BD should be incorporated into 
strategic activities such as marketing and new product development (Xu, Frankwick, & Ramirez, 
2016). An assumption underpinning BD is that data is an asset. As with other assets, it can be used 
to improve competitiveness, innovation and efficiencies in organizations (Braganza, Brooks, 
Nepelski, Ali, & Moro, 2016).  
McAfee & Brynjolfsson highlighted the characteristics of BD, the so-called three Vs: Volume, 
Variety, Velocity (Harvard Business Review, 2012). Volume refers to the unprecedented amount 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
of data created and collected, often in real-time, which is difficult to store using traditional 
methods. Velocity in this context is the frequency of data generation and delivery (Russom 2011). 
The Variety highlights that BD is generated from a broad range of sources, across multiple formats 
and is contained in multidimensional data fields, including both structured and unstructured data 
(Russom 2011). Gandomi and Haider (2015) added three other Vs; Value, Variability and 
Veracity. Value emphasizes the need to turn BD into something useful, which helps make a 
business case for collecting and leveraging BD with a clear business understanding. Variability 
refers to data whοse meaning is constantly changing which happens when data collection relies οn 
language processing. Veracity refers to that data may be incomplete, out-of date and contains noise 
(Gandomi & Haider, 2015). These Vs make BD difficult to handle in traditional ways (Elgendy & 
Elragal, 2014).  
2.2. Conceptualization of Big Data Value 
Business prοcesses are among the last remaining points of differentiation for organizations that 
provide similar prοducts and use comparable technologies, (Davenport, 2006). The literature 
provides evidence of BD value in several areas; relates BD with inventory and supply chain 
management, demand planning, resources management, customer segmentation, cost 
minimization, price optimization, profit maximization, productivity, sales, profitability, market 
share and return on investment (Barton and Court, 2012; McAfee and Brynjolfsson, 2012; 
Manyika et al., 2011; Davenport, 2006). “..and analytics competitors do all those things in a 
coordinated way, as part of an overarching strategy championed by top leadership and pushed 
down to decision makers at every level” (Davenport, 2006). A research at the MIT Center for 
Digital Business in partnership with McKinsey’s business technology office showed that 
companies in the top third of their industry in the use of data-driven decision making were, on 
average, 5% more productive and 6% more profitable than their competitors (McAfee & 
Brynjolfsson, 2012). 
When data is analyzed and used properly, it is transformed into knowledge that can support 
decision-making process within organizations. BD can be used to answer four types of questions 
(Hayashi 2014). The first type of question is descriptive, referring to BD use in order to describe 
occurrences. The second is diagnostic, referring to BD use in order to offer insight/explanation 
why something happened. The third is predictive and refers to the use of BD towards extracting 
information from existing data-sets and determining patterns as well as predicting future outcomes

and trends. The fourth is prescriptive, concerning both structured and unstructured data, and 
cοmbines advanced analytic techniques and disciplines in οrder to predict, prescribe and adapt.

3. Research Method 
The paper applies two research methods. First, a literature review method (Jones & Gatrell, 2014; 
Webster & Watson, 2002) in the field of implementation of BD strategies in the organizational 
context. Based on the literature review method, a framework for BD Value realization is 
developed. Second, a case study approach (Yin, 1994) is applied to test the framework.  
3.1. Literature Review Method 
The review consists of search, selection, analysis, and synthesis processes. The aim was to provide 
an analysis of the ﬁeld rather than providing a descriptive overview (Jones & Gatrell, 2014). The 
literature review aimed at identifying organizational factors that drive BD value realization and 
the literature gap in implementation of BD strategies in the organizational context. 
3.1.1. Literature Search and Selection 
The aim of the search and selection was to arrive at a number of literature materials that (1) have 
specifically mentioned the term “Big Data”, “Big Data Analytics” or, “Big Data Value” in the title, 
abstract, or keywords, and (2) focus on the exploration, or implementation of BD strategies in an 
organization context. The main search tools employed were IEEE Xplore, Scopus, SpringerLink, 
Science Direct and Google Scholar academic databases and search engines. Journal articles, 
conference papers, consultancy papers, reports, books and other written material were reviewed. 
The search and selection focused on the latest publications, mainly within the last 1-5 years. 
The selection of the literature was based on a screening process. The main selection process 
involved two rounds.  In the first round, the identified literature was primarily judged based on 
abstract, title, and keywords. Literature that had one or more of the search keywords as focal 
concepts in an organizational context were included. In the second round, -because the search and 
selection up until then had had limited focus-the full texts were inspected in order to check whether 
the selected literature provided connections between exploration/implementation of BD, BDA or 
realization of BD Value with, organization design/transformation/models/capabilities/resources 
etc. The search and selection processes are illustrated in the figure below:

3.1.2. Literature Analysis and Synthesis 
The analysis focused on analyzing and summarizing existing theories on BD value realization in 
organizational context, focusing on what organizational factors impact the BD value realization, 
and identifying supporting evidence and gaps in the literature (Jones & Gatrell, 2014). Thus, an 
iterative process of open and selective coding, and analyzing insights from the selected literature 
was conducted. Based on the literature analysis, organizational factors driving BD value 
realization and the literature gap are identified. Then, the findings from the literature analysis are 
synthesized in order to develop the “BD Value Realization Process”, a framework of phases and

framework was synthesized based on: (1) the organizational factors as identified in the literature

3.2. Case study Method 
The framework is tested though the case study of Maersk Drilling (MD) that currently seeks to 
leverage BD efforts. MD provides drilling services to oil and gas companies and was found suitable 
for the scope of this study for a number of reasons. First, MD was willing to share internal data 
and spend time on interviews. Second, it is an international that is currently seeking to leverage 
BD strategies towards operations performance. Third, MD has complex organizational structure 
and dynamic work environment that was considered interesting to further investigate. Finally, the 
drilling industry is responding to a competitive market since it is based on the oil prices that 
fluctuate continuously, which means that drilling companies have to always find ways to 
differentiate.  
3.2.1. Data Collection 
The data collection follows a qualitative research approach (Morgan & Drury, 2003). The data 
collection in this paper results from two different methods of collection. First, data was generated 
through action research (Eady, Drew, & Smith, 2015). Action research should focus on real-life 
issues relevant for both theory and practice through collaboration that includes systematic research, 
reflection and scientific knowledge (Coughlan, Draaijer, Godsell, & Boer, 2016). Second, semi-
structure interviews were conducted with different stakeholders involved in the implementation of 
BD strategies at MD. 
3.2.1.1. Qualitative Data Collection through Action Research 
The author spent 16 weeks at the MD’s headquarters, acting as an insider action researcher (Roth, 
Sandberg, & Svensson, 2011), organizational member and researcher at the same time. The 
advantage of insider action research is the potential for thorough organizational embeddedness that 
can result in findings not available from just an observer’s perspective. Thus, it enables the insider 
action researcher to acquire a different and perhaps deeper understanding (Eady, Drew, & Smith, 
2015). Its limitation is that the high degree of involvement can have a blinding effect on the 
researcher who can become subsumed by his/her participation in the organizational practice.

Therefore, the position as an insider action researcher requires honest and transparent reporting of 
the empirical context and data collection (Huang, 2010).  The action research included 
participation in the roll-out BD project with main purpose to gain: a) a thorough understanding of

current BD efforts and challenges, and c) insights in MD’s current operations performance

3.2.2. Data Validation 
Sources of evidence were used to ensure that the described situations and measures are correctly 
defined. Notes were taken from the action research. Interviews and informal conversations were 
conducted with different stakeholders and the data was cross-checked in order to be validated. 
Additionally, in order to overcome the subjectivity factor and bias in the respondents’ answers 
interpretation, the key points of the interview were summarized together with the respondents at 
the end of the meetings. If any misunderstanding existed, this was corrected and clarified. After 
ensuring validation of sources and data, objectivity and transparency, the data collection was 
assumed to be cοmpleted and a state of saturatiοn was reached (DiCicco-Bloom & Crabtree, 2006). 
4. Results 
In this section, the literature review is presented, followed by the identification of a research gap. 
Based on the latter and the results of the literature analysis and synthesis, a Big Data Realization 
Framework is developed. 
4.1. Organizational Factors Driving Big Data Value Realization 
4.1.1. Theory 
Akter et al. (2016), in the International Journal Production Economics, developed a hierarchical 
multi-dimensional model of Big Data Analytics Capability (BDAC) and its alignment with 
business strategies for enhancing firm performance. The model includes three primary dimensions 
(BDA management capability, BDA technology capability and BDA talent capability) and 11 sub-
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
dimensions. Overall, they answered the question of “What capabilities (technical and non-
technical) should an organization acquire to succeed in big data efforts?” and they highlighted the 
moderating effect of Business Strategy Alignment. 
Grossman and Siegel (2014), in the Journal of Organization Design, introduced a framework for 
determining how BDA capability should be distributed within an organization. They called their 
framework CSPG framework, having its basis on the Star Model (Kates & Galbraith, 2010) and 
was composed of four dimensions: Analytics Culture, Analytics Staffing, Analytics Processes, and 
Analytics Governance.   
Galbraith (2014), in the Journal of Organization Design, applied the Star Model (Kates & 
Galbraith, 2010) to describe how BD should impact each major element of an organization. He 
focused on various organizational changes that a company will have to make when it pursues a big 
data strategy. 
McAfee and Brynjolfsson (2012), in the Harvard Business Review, claim that “companies won’t 
reap the full benefits of a transition to using big data unless they’re able to manage change 
effectively” and identified five organizational challenges as the drivers to fully realize big data 
strategies (leadership, talent management, technology, decision-making, and company culture). 
They also provided an approach of four key points to build a capability from the ground up.  
Banton & Court (2012), in the Harvard Business Review, provided three areas in which companies 
have to develop their strengths in order to benefit from big data; obtain multiple data sources, build 
prediction and optimization models, and transform their organizations. They focused mainly on 
how an organization should embrace and adjust big data opportunities on their capabilities and 
provided a practical guide for companies seeking to leverage BD efforts.

Internal Environment, Business Processes, and Resources.

4.1.2.1. Business Initiatives 
Akter et al. (2016) highlight the importance of aligning Big Data Analytics Capabilities (BDAC) 
with Business Strategy. In fact, they consider that ACBSA (Analytics Capability Business Strategy 
Alignment) is a distinctive capability, playing a moderating role, which allows firms to link overall 
capability with firm performance. Only by aligning BD efforts with the overall Business Strategy, 
managers can make a successful decision-making that can lead to higher firm performance. The 
lack of business strategy alignment with BD mechanisms can lead to every possibility of the firm’s 
performance declining (Akter et al., 2016). Galbraith (2014) states that the BD strategy should 
focus on establishing a Digital Business by using data and analytics to create insights and custom 
reports that can be sold to customers and lead to a new profit center. In the same direction, 
Grossman and Siegel (2014) mention that BDA need to be integrated into corporate strategy. 
Barton & Court (2012) highlight the importance of developing analytics models that allow 
managers to predict and optimize outcomes. “The most effective approach to building a model 
rarely starts with the data; instead it originates with identifying the business opportunity and 
determine how the model can improve performance”, (Barton & Court, 2012). Moreover, they 
mention the importance of developing business relevant BDA that can be put in use and align with 
how companies arrive at decisions. Finally, McAfee and Brynjolfsson (2012) highlight the 
importance of spotting opportunities, articulating a compelling vision, setting clear goals and 
asking the right questions. 
4.1.2.2. Internal Environment 
As internal environment are considered elements regarding organizational structure and culture. 
McAffee & Brynjolfsson (2012), Galbraith (2014), and Grossman & Siegel (2014) highlight the 
importance of a leader advocating for BD and Analytics. BD leaders develop the vision of what 
BDA can do for the business and encourage people to realize this vision. Whether it is a Chief 
Digital Officer (CDO), the CIO, or a digital division head, the digital leader needs to promote data 
as a strategic asset that can help grow the company and make it more successful (Galbraith, 2014).  
Both Galbraith (2014), and Grossman & Siegel (2014) recommend the development of BD Units 
or Departments. Such structural changes will constitute a shift in power to the digital units, and 
the company will need to rebalance its power structure and prepare for faster decision making 
(Galbraith, 2014). Moreover, many references in the organizational culture were identified. 
Grossman & Siegel (2014), in their “Analytics Culture”, state that BDA should viewed as 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
organizational function, while based on McAfee & Brynjolfsson (2012) the first question a data-
driven organization asks itself is not “What do we think?” but “What do we know?”. “This requires 
a move away from acting solely on hunches. It also requires breaking a bad habit in many 
organizations: pretending to be more data-driven than they actually are…Too often, we saw 
executives who spiced up their reports with lots of data that supported decisions they had already 
made using traditional approaches”, (McAfee & Brynjolfsson, 2012). Based on Barton & Court 
(2012), managers must view analytics as central to solving problems and identifying opportunities- 
to make it part of the fabric of the daily operations. Thus, an adjustment in culture and mind-set is 
necessary. 
4.1.2.3. Business Processes 
 
Both Galbraith (2014) and McAffee & Brynjolfsson (2012) point out the importance of cross-
functional teams. The BD capability is only possible if an organization is designed to operate in 
real time. “We need to design a decision process that uses real-time data, analyzes it to produce 
instant insights, and processes those insights to arrive at real-time decisions. Using real-time 
decisions, organizations can take quick action. We need much faster-acting companies in order to 
profit from BD” (Galbraith, 2014).  Processes incorporate all the elements of the BDA technology 
capability (BDATEC) dimension of Akter et al. (2016); BDA connectivity, compatibility and 
modularity. BDA connectivity refers to the connectivity among different business units in sourcing 
and analyzing a variety of data from different functions (e.g., supply chain management, customer 
relationship management). BDA compatibility enables continuous flows of information for real-
time decisions. Moreover, it contributes to clean-up operations by synchronizing, merging 
overlapping data and fixing missing information. Finally, BDA Modularity embodies flexible 
platform development which allows the addition, modification or removal of features to, or from, 
the model as needed in order to tap business opportunities and improving firm performance (Akter 
et al., 2016). The Analytics Processes of Grossman & Siegel (2014) include the development and 
deployment of analytics models as well as the measurement of the business value earned through 
BD efforts.  Barton & Court (2012) recommend that in order companies to leverage data, they 
need to source it creatively from multiple sources, build models that balance complexity and easy 
usage, and visualize in a simple way the analytics.  
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
4.1.2.4. Resources 
All five authors highlight the importance of talent management. In order to execute real-time 
decision processes, the organization needs the people who are skilled in digital tools and work 
effectively in teams (Galbraith, 2014). Akter et al. (2016) points out the four components of BDA 
talent capability with which data scientists should be equipped: BDA technology management 
knowledge, technical knowledge, business knowledge, relational knowledge. BDA technical 
knowledge refers to knowledge about technical elements, such as operational systems, statistics, 
programming languages, and database management systems. BDA technology management 
knowledge refers to the big data resource management knowledge in order to support business 
goals. BDA business knowledge involves the understanding of various business functions and the 
business environment. Finally, BDA relational knowledge refers to the ability of analytics 
professionals to communicate and work with stakeholders from other business functions. 
Moreover, Akter et al. (2016) highlight the importance of BDA management capability with four 
dimensions of planning, investment, coordination and control. Barton & Court (2012) suggest that 
in order to exploit BD, companies need to upgrade their analytics capabilities by a multifaceted 
approach that includes training, role modelling by leaders, and incentives and metrics to reinforce 
behavior. Except for human resources/capabilities, authors mention the importance of IT 
infrastructure (Galbraith, 2014; Barton & Court, 2012; McAffee & Brynjolfsson, 2012). Barton & 
Court (2012) state that many legacy IT structures deliver data in batches, so they cannot provide 
continuous flows of information for real-time decisions and thus may hinder new types of data 
sourcing, storage and analysis. Therefore, they propose that companies turn to vendors who can 
provide analytics services, emerging software or new cloud-based technologies. Moreover, they 
highlight the importance of developing simple tools that are easy for managers to use. 
4.1.3. Summary of Organizational Factors 
The literature review concluded in four organizational factors; Business Initiatives, Business 
Processes, Resources, and Internal Environment. It is important to be clarified that the scope of 
influencing factors is within organization and thus the external environment has not been 
considered as a factor. The organizational factors driving BD Value Realization are summarized 
below: 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
I) Business Initiatives: the direction the organization seeks to take in regards to 
exploration/implementation of BD efforts; a vision/business strategy of what exactly they want to 
accomplish 
II) Business Processes: includes all the day to day activities that will link BD efforts with the 
organizational decision making norms 
III) Resources: includes all the tangible and intangible assets used by an organization to conceive 
and implement its BD efforts 
a. BD Strategic Capability: includes the dimensions of BD planning, investment, 
coordination and control (see section 4.1.2.4.) 
b. BD Talent Capability: includes the dimensions of BD technology management 
knowledge, technical knowledge, business knowledge, and relational knowledge 
(see section 4.1.2.4.) 
c. BD Infrastructure: includes all the technical infrastructure required to support the 
implementation on BD efforts (hardware and software infrastructure for 
development of the BDA and visualization) 
IV) Internal Environment: includes the organizational culture and structure. 
4.2. Literature Gap  
Although the authors provide organizational factors driving BD Value Realization and 
directions/recommendations on what changes should take place within an organization in order to 
explore/implement BD strategies, their approaches lack operability; no or little evidence on how 
BD value realization can be achieved is provided. The aforementioned factors are moderators and 
thus cannot explain how the input, e.g. business targets/objectives/goals, transforms into output, 
e.g. business outcomes/impact, (Figure 3). Barton & Court (2012) offer some evidence –a practical 
guide- of how organizations to realize BD value but no clear framework is provided.  
Searching in the literature for a BD Value Realization Mechanism or similar, the frameworks 
provided concern the processing of BD itself and focus on it from a technical perspective (Bizer, 
Boncz, Brodie, and Erling, 2012; Marx, 2013; Chen and Liu, 2014; Zhou, Chawla, Jin, and 
Williams, 2014; Domingue, Lasierra, Fensel, and Kasteren, 2016). The latest BD process provided 
in the literature is the BD chain as defined by Janssen, Voort & Wahyudi (2017) and consisted of 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
four main activities: Data collecting, Data preparing, Data analyzing and Decision-making. Data 
preparation includes activities related to improving the data quality, data enrichment and creating 
a dataset that is suitable for using analytics. It is important to clarify that the aforementioned BD 
Chain is a process that focuses on the BD Management activities themselves and does not consider 
any other factors/activities, e.g. organizational factors, capabilities or business initiatives. 
The only evidence on BD Value Realization process is provided by Braganza, Brooks, Nepelski, 
Ali, & Moro (2016) who, based on a single case study, developed a linear archetype BD business 
process for resource management without though considering what organizational capabilities 
required or organizational factors driving the value realization through BD. Based on the 
archetype, they recommended a three-phase approach towards BD initiatives. The three-step 
approach can be considered as a practical implication towards exploration of BD efforts. However, 
still no clear framework is provided and the implications are resulting from a single case and not 
tested. Therefore, a framework for BD value realization process that could operate as a guide for 
building an action will be developed in the next section.

5.1. Maersk Drilling 
As aforementioned, MD supports global oil and gas production by providing drilling services to 
oil and gas companies. The company employs overall 4000 onshore and offshore employees. The 
business model of MD includes three main parties; drilling assets, customers, and external 
suppliers/vendors/3rd parties since its drilling assets operate based on “heavy” equipment that 
require continuous maintenance: 
1. Drilling Assets 
Maersk Drilling owns 24 drilling units; 4 drill-ships, 4 semi-submersible rigs and 16 jack-up rigs 
(Appendix A). 
2. Customers 
The clients of MD are oil companies such as Total, Shell, Petrogas, Statoil, BP, etc., by providing 
them with drilling services based on contracts signed between the contractor (MD) and operator 
(oil companies). In order the contract to be fulfilled, the operator will complete a specific quantity 
of wells. Maersk Drilling, as the contractor, is in charge of drilling and operating the well. The rig 
and equipment are owned, maintained and managed by Maersk Drilling. The drilling crew is also 
employed by Maersk Drilling while employees by the operator or other external suppliers can be 
also included in the crew. The contracts are on a fixed daily rate. 
3. External Suppliers/Vendors/3rd Parties 
The 24 assets of the company have to be preserved and maintained based on specific regulations. 
Several specialized, external suppliers are controlling specific equipment on the rig.  
During the previous years with high oil prices which led to high-profit margins for the drilling 
companies, there was no specific focus on innovation in the industry. However, nowadays the 
market situation has changed dramatically and shaped a new demand for more productivity and 
innovative business models. The combination of contract roll-offs, a high portion of old rigs still 
in operation, high-and-increasing leverage and no incremental demand from oil companies is a 
lethal mix (Pedersen & Kvernland, 2015). Thus, MD is currently seeking to change its business 
model; an illustrative example is that Maersk Drilling has entered into a jack-up alliance agreement 
with Aker BP ASA (Aker BP). The alliance is founded on a unique tripartite collaboration model 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
that also includes service provider Halliburton. The alliance aims at lowering the cost per barrel 
for Aker BP and increasing profitability for the alliance partners. The framework agreement is five 
years firm with the option to extend for a further five years and will be based on an integrated well 
delivery model with aligned incentives. The new alliance approach focuses on increasing 
collaboration efficiency and enabling standardization and simplification of processes, ultimately 
shortening the lead time from discovery to first oil.

5.2. Current Operations Performance System of Maersk Drilling 
The pain points of the current Operations Performance System of MD that led to the decision to 
leverage Big Data efforts are: 
I. 
Insufficient/Biased Operations Performance Reporting Process: Since today, the decision-
making regarding the operations performance is based on the input by the crew onboard. 
The crew fills in a daily report for the operations performance via MD’s ERP system (IFS) 
in order to be accessible to employees onshore and at the headquarters. Additionally, they 
are the ones who are offering input in regards to potential maintenance work. This means 
that the monitoring of the operations performance and potential maintenance projects are 
based more on intuition and human experience rather than on data. Moreover, since the 
people who perform the operations and the people who report the daily operations 
performance are the same, it can lead to biased reported information.  
II. 
Insufficient Operations Performance Metrics: Current performance metrics are considered 
functionally siloed and do not provide always necessary insight. They use four operations 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
performance metrics (Appendix B) that are considered to not be sufficient for measuring 
operations performance. 
III. 
Decision-Making Culture: the focus is more on gathering/manipulation rather than insight 
generation. Managers use data to support decisions they had already made. 
IV. 
Reactive Decision-Making Processes and Actions: All the decision-making processes and 
actions regarding operations performance improvements are reactive and not predictive 
which has caused to MD additional costs on breakdowns and inconsistent operations.  
For the aforementioned reasons, MD decided to initiate BD efforts towards Operations 
Performance. In the above reasons, should be also added-that implies increase in market 
competition and thus need for differentiation-, and 2) the benefits of a good reputation in the 
market as a pioneer in operations efficiency and digitalization.  
5.3. BD Efforts at Maersk Drilling  
As already mentioned, MD has initiated BD efforts, in order to differentiate and increase 
operations performance. Thus, MD is currently in cooperation with General Electrics, a global 
supplier of industrial equipment and software analytic tools. Their cooperation is based on sensors 
established in specific types of equipment in MD’s assets. These sensors provide data for the 
performance and condition of the equipment. MD seeks to translate this data into Drilling 
Productivity and Predictive Maintenance.  
The Drilling Productivity and Predictive Maintenance solutions aim at reducing the maintenance 
level on equipment by at least 20 percent. The solutions are supposed to enable detection of 
anomalies and potential equipment breakdowns at an earlier stage than traditional methods used 
to. Thus, they will provide the foundation for data-based decision making that will significantly 
lower the risk and impact of breakdowns. It is expected that they will reduce both Operational 
expenditure (OPEX) and Capital expenditure (CAPEX) as well as downtime on rigs. However, 
these digital innovations will not only reduce the total well cost for MD and its clients, but will 
also be an additional profit for the company since MD seeks to sell them as additional services on 
a daily rate to its customers. 
MD seeks to collect data from the sensors established at the assets’ equipment, develop advanced 
analytics, translate them at performance metrics and display the latter in performance dashboards 
==PAGE:30==

towards transparency. These dashboards and reporting will allow comparisons between the assets, 
identification of best practices and actions based on data-driven decision making.

6.3. Recommendations  
Based on the developed framework, it is essential that the BD efforts that MD initiated towards 
operations performance will be tied to the business opportunities/issues wants to target. As 
mentioned in previous section, BD and BDA will take root faster if related to business objectives 
and there is a clear business case that can quantify the benefits of BD. MD should move towards 
an outcomes-based mindset and minimize needless or unproductive requests for BDA support. 
Thus, considering what steps considered missed or incomplete in the BD Value Realization 
Process of MD towards Operations Performance, a number of recommendations are presented 
below:  
i. 
Definition of questions to be answered: 
As already mentioned, the overall business outcome from the exploitation of BD in 
Operations Performance is the reduction of the total well cost for MD and its clients. The 
business outcome has been broken down into three smaller business outcomes: 1) Increased 
Operational Efficiency, 2) Reduced Maintenance, and 3) Breakdown Avoidance. Based on 
the desired business outcomes, as focus areas were considered Predictive Maintenance and 
Drilling Productivity.

b. Rig Teams (Operations Department), who are in the front-line and represent the

Team as they are the ones that define the metrics.  
iii. 
Build a BDA Task Force: The project owner should synthesize a team, consisted of 
representatives of Operations Business Intelligence Team who will be enhanced by the 
addition of BD Analysts, dedicated to translate the analytics of the tool to insights and 
metrics that will reflect the questions to be answered.  
iv. 
Develop Business-relevant Metrics/Insights: Having as starting point the ideas collected 
from the workshop, the BDA Task Force should set a series of meetings with managers to 
better understand the type of decisions they make towards operations performance on a 
daily/monthly/yearly basis and how these decisions affect revenue and customer retention. 
These meetings will ensure that the metrics/insights developed will be aligned with MD’s 
decision norms and processes. Based on the ideas collected from the workshop and the 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
outcomes of the series of meetings, the BDA task force will dedicate sufficient time to 
develop the metrics/insights.  
v. 
Present the metrics to the people interested: It is essential that the metrics/insights will be 
presented to the people that will actually utilize them on an operational or strategic level 
and on a daily, monthly or yearly basis. A creative discussion on the metrics developed for 
potential adjustments/improvements will help to develop the metrics at the best possible 
way. The metrics developed should be SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, 
Relevant, Time Phased (Doran, 1981). 
It is considered that by following the above recommendations, the step of “Synthesization of 
Analytics into Insights” will be implemented and then the next steps and phases of the framework 
can take place. 
7. Implications on Theory and Practice  
This section highlights the areas of contribution to theory and practice. The main theoretical 
contributions of this paper are: a) Identification of the organizational factors driving Big Data 
Value Realization through a literature review, b) development of the “BD Value Realization 
Process” framework in order to fill in the literature gap, identified through a literature review and 
tested through a single case study method, and c) theoretical ground for scholars to validate the 
framework through cases studies in different organizations/industries/markets. Moreover, this 
research has implications for practitioners who are either planning to, or already implementing, 
BD efforts. This paper contributes to practice in two ways. Firstly, it identifies which 
organizational factors drive Value Realization through BD strategies. These factors can serve as a 
direction for practitioners when seeking to initiate BD efforts. Secondly, it outlines a framework 
of “Big Data Value Realization Process” tested through a single case study. The latter is a-five-
phase systematic approach towards BD value realization which can be a guide for practitioners to 
build an implementation plan and develop their success criteria.  
8. Conclusion and directions for further research 
Nowadays, organizations are seeking to leverage BD strategies in order to realize strategic or 
operational business goals. However, it appears that very few companies have achieved a big 
impact through BD and still many companies are unsure how to proceed towards realizing business 
value through BD. The literature provides little evidence on how companies to initiate, plan or 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
implement BD strategies. Although recommendations can be found, a framework for how BD 
Value Realization can be achieved lacks. Thus, the purpose of the current paper is to develop a 
framework which can be used by all different sizes of organizations interested in exploring the 
potential of BD. It is a BD Value Realization mechanism that can help companies to plan their 
steps towards BD efforts. The framework developed was tested through a single case study. The 
case company operates in the oil and gas sector as a drilling services provider and is currently 
seeking to leverage BD opportunities towards increasing its operations performance. The paper 
contributes to the literature by identifying the organizational factors driving BD Value Realization 
through a literature review and providing a tested framework of “BD Value Realization Process” 
in order to fill in the literature gap identified. Moreover, it paves the way for further research and 
validation of the framework. The paper also contributes to practitioners who seek to leverage BD 
efforts by identifying which organizational factors drive BD Value Realization that can serve as a 
guide in regards to where to focus on when seeking to initiate BD efforts. Finally, practitioners 
can apply the framework of “Big Data Value Realization Process” in order to build an 
implementation plan and set their own success criteria towards BD strategies. 
The BD Value Realization factors identified and the framework developed are based on a literature 
review and not on a single case study. However, the current paper is not without limitations. First 
of all, the factors identified focus within organizational borders and do not expand to external 
factors such as industry/market factors (e.g. competitiveness). Secondly, the framework is tested 
using a single exemplar case in the oil and gas industry. Although the case company is an 
international company operating in many geographical areas, it is recommended that the 
framework is tested in dissimilar contexts such as public sector and validated. Finally, the case 
company operates in a high velocity market characterized by non-linear business processes, based 
on real-time information, cross-functional relationships, which are prerequisites for BD efforts to 
succeed within an organization. Therefore, it would be interesting to also test the framework in a 
more stable market where business processes are more analytical and linear.

A strategic framework for realizing value through Big Data 
Vasilopoulou Ioanna Eleni, Edwards Kasper 
Abstract  
Organizations are seeking to leverage Big Data (BD) efforts to realize business objectives. It 
appears that very few companies have achieved substantial impact through BD and still many 
companies are unsure how to proceed in order to capture BD value.  Scant attention in the literature 
has been given on how BD value can be realized. This paper uses a literature review to identify 
organizational factors driving BD Value Realization. Based on the factors, a strategic framework 
aiding BD Value Realization is developed and tested through a single case study method. The case 
company provides drilling services in the oil and gas industry. 
Keywords: Big Data, Big Data Analytics, Big Data Value Realization, Organizational Design, 
Literature Review 
1. Introduction  
Many companies have showed interest in developing Big Data Analytics Capability (BDAC)4 
towards increasing firm performance (Akter et al., 2016). Big Data (BD)5 refers tο datasets high 
in variety and velοcity that make it difficult tο handle by using traditional tοοls and techniques 
(Elgendy & Elragal, 2014). Big Data Analytics (BDA)6, which are clοsely related to BD, are 
needed tο create value οf the data (Elgendy & Elragal, 2014; Holsapple, Lee-Post, & Pakath, 2014). 
Tapping into large-scale, fast-moving, complex streams of datasets has the potential to 
fundamentally transfοrm the organizational decision-making (Janssen, Voort & Wahyudi, 2017). 
Accenture and General Electric (Columbus, 2014a) conducted a research showing that, “87% of 
enterprises believe BDA will redefine the competitive landscape of their industries within the next 
three years. 89% believe that companies that do not adopt a BDA strategy in the next year risk 
losing market share and momentum”. BDAC is widely cοnsidered to transfοrm the way in which 
organizations do business (Barton and Court, 2012), while McAfee and Brynjolfsson (2012) in the 
Harvard Business Review characterize BD as the next “management revolution”. 
                                                             
4 BDAC: Big Data Analytics Capability 
5 BD: Big Data 
6 BDA: Big Data Analytics 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
However, it appears that very few companies have achieved an impact through BD (Akter et al., 
2016). Cοmpanies cοntinue to struggle with fundamental issues related to BDA spanning data, 
methοds, organization and technology. “...most companies are unsure how to proceed… maybe 
their current analytics programs are too complicated or don’t yield insights that can be put to use”, 
Barton & Court (2012). Organizational silos are blocking the use of BD for decision making 
(Economist Intelligence Unit, 2012). Data collection, processing and use is not done within a single 
department or organization; it requires collaborative resources and capabilities. “The promised 
gains in performance (through BD) were often slow in coming, because the systems remained 
stubbornly disconnected from how companies and frontline managers actually made decisions, 
and new demands in data management added complexity to operations”, Barton & Court (2012). 
Even in cases where BD strategies have been initiated, are deployed to generate “rearview” 
descriptions of what happened, rather than forward-looking insights that can be used to make 
operational, managerial and strategic decisions (Hernandez, Berkey, & Bhattacharya, 2013). 
Although literature provides recommendations on what changes should take place within an 
organization in order to explore BD strategies (Akter et al. 2016; Galbraith, 2014; Grossman & 
Siegel, 2014; Barton & Court, 2012; McAfee & Brynjolfsson 2012), there is little guidance on 
how BD value realization can be achieved and indeed what companies should do. The purpose of 
the current study is to develop a framework which can be used by all different sizes of 
organizations interested in exploring the potential of BD. It can be considered as a BD value 
realization mechanism that can help companies to plan their steps towards BD efforts. BD value 
refers to the business benefits an organization can achieve through the exploitation of BD. 
The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 provides theoretical background in BD and BD Value 
Realization. In Section 3 the research methods followed are presented, while in section 4 the results 
are provided. Based on the literature review, a framework is developed to fill in the identified 
literature gap. The framework is tested through a single case study method in section 5. Finally, 
section 6 and 7 provide implications on theory & practice, and conclusion & directions for further 
research, respectively.

2. Theoretical Background 
2.1. Big Data 
Big Data (BD) is a buzzword in today’s corporate world. Various estimates of sources and volumes 
of data being produced include CISCO's estimate “by 2020, the gigabyte (GB) equivalent of all 
movies ever made will cross the global Internet every 2 minutes” (Cisco Visual Networking Index, 
2016, p.4). The term “Big Data” was first used by Roger Magoulas from O’Reilly media in 2005 
and was defined as a great amοunt of data that traditiοnal data management methοds cannot 
manage and process due to the complexity and size of this data (Geanina et al., 2013).  Scholars 
recognize BD is more than a technological issue and, to be fully effective, needs to become part 
of the fabric of organizations (Davenport, Barth, & Bean, 2012). BD should be incorporated into 
strategic activities such as marketing and new product development (Xu, Frankwick, & Ramirez, 
2016). An assumption underpinning BD is that data is an asset. As with other assets, it can be used 
to improve competitiveness, innovation and efficiencies in organizations (Braganza, Brooks, 
Nepelski, Ali & Moro, 2016).  
McAfee & Brynjolfsson highlighted the characteristics of BD, the so-called three Vs: Volume, 
Variety, Velocity (Harvard Business Review, 2012). Volume refers to the unprecedented amount 
of data created and collected, often in real-time, which is difficult to store using traditional 
methods. Velocity in this context is the frequency of data generation and delivery (Russom 2011). 
The Variety highlights that BD is generated from a broad range of sources, across multiple formats 
and is contained in multidimensional data fields, including both structured and unstructured data 
(Russom 2011). Gandomi and Haider (2015) added three other Vs; Value, Variability and 
Veracity. Value emphasizes the need to turn BD into something useful, which helps make a 
business case for collecting and leveraging BD with a clear business understanding. Variability 
refers to data whοse meaning is constantly changing which happens when data collection relies οn 
language processing. Veracity refers to that data may be incomplete, out-of date and contains noise 
(Gandomi & Haider, 2015). These Vs make BD difficult to handle in traditional ways (Elgendy & 
Elragal, 2014).  
2.2. Conceptualization of Big Data Value 
Business prοcesses are among the last remaining points of differentiation for organizations that 
provide similar prοducts and use comparable technologies, (Davenport, 2006). The literature 
provides evidence of BD value in several areas; relates BD with inventory and supply chain 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
management, demand planning, resources management, customer segmentation, cost 
minimization, price optimization, profit maximization, productivity, sales, profitability, market 
share and return on investment (Barton and Court, 2012; McAfee and Brynjolfsson, 2012; 
Manyika et al., 2011; Davenport, 2006). “..and analytics competitors do all those things in a 
coordinated way, as part of an overarching strategy championed by top leadership and pushed 
down to decision makers at every level” (Davenport, 2006). A research at the MIT Center for 
Digital Business in partnership with McKinsey’s business technology office showed that 
companies in the top third of their industry in the use of data-driven decision making were, on 
average, 5% more productive and 6% more profitable than their competitors (McAfee & 
Brynjolfsson, 2012). 
When data is analyzed and used properly, it is transformed into knowledge that can support 
decision-making process within organizations. BD can be used to answer four types of questions 
(Hayashi 2014). The first type of question is descriptive, referring to BD use in order to describe 
occurrences. The second is diagnostic, referring to BD use in order to offer insight/explanation 
why something happened. The third is predictive and refers to the use of BD towards extracting 
information from existing data-sets and determining patterns as well as predicting future outcomes 
and trends. The fourth is prescriptive, concerning both structured and unstructured data, and 
cοmbines advanced analytic techniques and disciplines in οrder to predict, prescribe and adapt. 
As Roden, Nucciarelli, Graham, & Li (2017) state: “BD provides the raw materials for an 
organization to extract new knowledge, not as a one-off exercise, but through multiple iterations 
of analysis, to support descriptive, diagnostic, predictive and prescriptive decisions at operational 
and strategic level”. It is in fact οnly by extracting meaningful value (through BD) that 
organizations can recοgnize the impact BD can have on their operations model. Only when this 
data is analyzed and used, it is transformed into knοwledge that can support decision-making. 
Thus, BD can be cοnsidered as a strategic capability helping decision-makers recοgnize value and 
apply new intelligence either within their organization or beyοnd (Roden, Nucciarelli, Graham, & 
Li, 2017). McAfee & Brynjolfsson (2012) state that “Data-driven decisions are better decisions. 
For that reason it has the potential to revolutionize management.” They consider that due to use of 
BD, organizations can measure and therefore manage more precisely, can make better predictions 
and smarter decisions, and target more-effective interventions.

3. Research Method  
The paper applies two research methods. First, a literature review method (Jones & Gatrell, 2014; 
Webster & Watson, 2002) in the field of implementation of BD strategies in the organizational 
context. Based on the literature review method, a framework for BD Value realization is 
developed. Second, a case study approach (Yin, 1994) is applied to test the framework.  
3.1. Literature Review Method 
The review consists of search, selection, analysis, and synthesis processes. The aim was to provide 
an analysis of the ﬁeld rather than providing a descriptive overview (Jones & Gatrell, 2014). The 
literature review aimed at identifying organizational factors that drive BD value realization and 
the literature gap in implementation of BD strategies in the organizational context. 
3.1.1. Literature Search and Selection 
The aim of the search and selection was to arrive at a number of literature materials that (1) have 
specifically mentioned the term “Big Data”, “Big Data Analytics” or, “Big Data Value” in the title, 
abstract, or keywords, and (2) focus on the exploration, or implementation of BD strategies in an 
organization context. The main search tools employed were IEEE Xplore, Scopus, SpringerLink, 
Science Direct and Google Scholar academic databases and search engines. Journal articles, 
conference papers, consultancy papers, reports, books and other written material were reviewed. 
The search and selection focused on the latest publications, mainly within the last 1-5 years.

3.2. Case study Method 
The framework is tested though the case study of an international Offshore Drilling Company 
(ODC) that currently seeks to leverage BD efforts. ODC provides drilling services to oil and gas 
companies and was found suitable for the scope of this study for a number of reasons. First, ODC 
was willing to share internal data and spend time on interviews. Second, it is an international that 
is currently seeking to leverage BD strategies towards operations performance. Third, ODC has 
complex organizational structure and dynamic work environment that was considered interesting 
to further investigate. Finally, the drilling industry is responding to a competitive market since it 
is based on the oil prices that fluctuate continuously, which means that drilling companies have to 
always find ways to differentiate.  
3.2.1. Data Collection & Validation 
The data collection follows a qualitative research approach (Morgan & Drury, 2003). The data 
collection in this paper results from two different methods of collection. First, data was generated 
through action research (Eady, Drew, & Smith, 2015). Action research should focus on real-life 
issues relevant for both theory and practice through collaboration that integrates systematic 
research, reflection and scientific knowledge (Coughlan, Draaijer, Godsell, & Boer, 2016). The 
author spent 16 weeks at the ODC’s headquarters, acting as an insider action researcher (Roth, 
Sandberg, & Svensson, 2011), who is organizational member and researcher at the same time. The 
advantage of insider action research is the potential for thorough organizational embeddedness and 
contextual understanding that can result in findings not available from just an observer’s 
perspective. Thus, it enables the insider action researcher to acquire a different and perhaps deeper 
understanding (Eady, Drew, & Smith, 2015). Its limitation is that the high degree of involvement 
can have a blinding effect on the researcher who can become subsumed by his/her participation in 
the organizational practice. Therefore, the position as an insider action researcher requires honest 
and transparent reporting of the empirical context and data collection (Huang, 2010).  Second, 
semi-structure interviews were conducted with different stakeholders involved in the 
==PAGE:59==

implementation of BD strategies at ODC. Multiple sources of evidence were used to ensure that 
the described situations and measures are correctly defined. Notes were taken from the action 
research. Interviews and informal conversations were conducted with different stakeholders and 
the data was cross-checked in order to be validated. Additionally, in order to overcome the 
subjectivity factor and bias in the respondents’ answers interpretation, the key points of the 
interview were summarized together with the respondents at the end of the meetings. If any 
misunderstanding existed, this was corrected and clarified. After ensuring validation of sources 
and data, objectivity and transparency, the data collection was assumed to be cοmpleted and a state

4. Results 
4.1. Organizational Factors Driving Big Data Value Realization 
4.1.1. Theory  
Akter et al. (2016), in the International Journal Production Economics, developed a hierarchical 
multi-dimensional model of Big Data Analytics Capability (BDAC) and its alignment with 
business strategies for enhancing firm performance. The model includes three primary dimensions 
(BDA management capability, BDA technology capability and BDA talent capability) and 11 sub-
dimensions. Overall, they answered the question of “What capabilities (technical and non-
technical) should an organization acquire to succeed in BD efforts?” and they highlighted the 
moderating effect of Business Strategy Alignment. Grossman & Siegel (2014), in the Journal of 
Organization Design, introduced a framework for determining how BDA capability should be 
distributed within an organization. They called their framework CSPG framework, having its basis 
on the Star Model (Kates & Galbraith, 2010) and was composed of four dimensions: Analytics 
Culture, Analytics Staffing, Analytics Processes, and Analytics Governance. Galbraith (2014), in 
the Journal of Organization Design, applied the Star Model (Kates & Galbraith, 2010) to describe 
how BD should impact each major element of an organization. He focused on various 
organizational changes that a company will have to make when it pursues a BD strategy. McAfee 
and Brynjolfsson (2012), in the Harvard Business Review, claim that “companies won’t reap the 
full benefits of a transition to using big data unless they’re able to manage change effectively” and 
identified five organizational challenges as the drivers to fully realize BD strategies (leadership, 
talent management, technology, decision-making, and company culture). They also provided an 
approach of four key points to build a capability from the ground up. Banton & Court (2012), in 
==PAGE:60==
59 
 
the Harvard Business Review, provided three areas in which companies have to develop their 
strengths in order to benefit from BD; obtain multiple data sources, build prediction and 
optimization models, and transform their organizations. They focused mainly on how an 
organization should embrace and adjust BD opportunities on their capabilities and provided a 
practical guide for companies seeking to leverage BD efforts.

4.1.1.1. Business Initiatives 
Akter et al. (2016) highlight the importance of aligning BDAC with Business Strategy. In fact, 
they consider that ACBSA (Analytics Capability Business Strategy Alignment) is a distinctive 
capability, playing a moderating rοle, which allοws firms to link overall capability with firm 
performance. Galbraith (2014) states that the BD strategy should fοcus on establishing a Digital 
Business by using BDA to create insights and custom repοrts that can be sold to customers and 
lead to a new profit center. In the same direction, Grossman & Siegel (2014) mention that BD 
needs to be integrated into corporate strategy. Barton & Court (2012) highlight that the most 
effective apprοach in order to develop a mοdel rarely starts with the data; instead it οriginates with 
identifying the business oppοrtunity and determine how the model can imprοve perfοrmance. 
Moreover, they mention the importance of developing business relevant BDA that can be put in 
use and align with hοw companies arrive at decisions. Finally, McAfee and Brynjolfsson (2012) 
highlight the importance of spοtting opportunities, articulating a vision, setting clear gοals and 
asking the right questiοns. 
4.1.1.2. Internal Environment 
As internal environment are considered elements regarding organizatiοnal structure and culture. 
McAffee & Brynjolfsson (2012), Galbraith (2014), and Grossman & Siegel (2014) highlight the 
importance of a leader advοcating for BDA. BD leaders cascade the visiοn of what BD can dο for 
the business, encourage people to realize this potential and hοld them accountable for the results. 
Both Galbraith (2014), and Grossman & Siegel (2014) recommend the development of BD Units 
or Departments. Moreover, many references in the organizational culture were identified. 
Grossman & Siegel (2014), in their “Analytics Culture”, state that BDA should be viewed as 
organizational function. McAfee & Brynjolfsson (2012) recommend a shift from acting sοlely on 
hunches and instinct while arguing that the first question a data-driven cοmpany asks itself is not 
==PAGE:62==
61 
 
“What do we think?” but “What do we know?”. They also highlight that a data-driven company 
culture requires breaking a common habit in many organizations: pretending to be more data-
driven than they actually are. ”Too often, we saw executives who spiced up their reports with lots 
of data that supported decisions they had already made using traditional approaches”, (McAfee & 
Brynjolfsson, 2012). Barton & Court (2012) recommend that managers make analytics part of the 
fabric of the daily operations and, thus, an adjustment in culture and mind-set is necessary. 
4.1.1.3. Business Processes  
Both Galbraith (2014) and McAffee & Brynjolfsson (2012) point out the importance of cross-
functional teams. Processes incorporate all the elements of the BDA technology capability 
(BDATEC) dimension of Akter et al. (2016); BDA connectivity, compatibility and modularity. 
The Analytics Processes of Grossman & Siegel (2014) include the development and deployment 
of analytics models as well as the measurement of the business value earned through BD efforts.  
Barton & Court (2012) recommends that in order companies to leverage data, they need to source 
it creatively from multiple sources, build models that balance complexity and easy usage, and 
visualize in a simple way the BDA.  
4.1.1.4. Resources 
All five authors highlight the importance of talent management. In order to execute real-time 
decision processes, the organization needs the people who are skilled in digital tools and work 
effectively in teams (Galbraith, 2014). Akter et al. (2016) points out the four components of BDA 
talent capability with which data scientists should be equipped: BDA technology management 
knowledge, technical knowledge, business knowledge, relational knowledge. Moreover, Akter et 
al. (2016) highlight the importance of BDA management capability with four dimensions of 
planning, investment, coordination and control. Except for human resources/capabilities, authors 
mention the importance of IT infrastructure (Galbraith, 2014; Barton & Court, 2012; McAffee & 
Brynjolfsson, 2012). Barton & Court (2012) state that many legacy IT structures are built to deliver 
data in batches, so they cannot provide continuous flows of information for real-time decision-
making. Therefore, they propose that companies turn to vendors who can provide analytics 
services, emerging software or new cloud-based technologies. Moreover, they highlight the 
importance of developing simple tools that are easy for managers to use.

4.2. Literature Gap  
Although the authors provide organizational factors driving BD Value Realization and 
directions/recommendations on what changes should take place within an organization in order to 
explore/implement BD strategies, however their approaches lack operability; no or little evidence 
on how BD value realization can be achieved is provided. The aforementioned factors are 
moderators and thus cannot explain how the input (e.g. business targets/objectives/ goals) 
transforms into output (e.g. business outcomes/impact). Barton & Court (2012) offer some 
evidence –a practical guide- of how organizations to realize BD value but no clear framework is 
provided.  
Searching in the literature for a BD Value Realization Mechanism or similar, the frameworks 
provided concern the processing of BD itself and focus on it from a technical perspective (Bizer, 
Boncz, Brodie, and Erling, 2012; Marx, 2013; Chen and Liu, 2014; Zhou, Chawla, Jin, and 
Williams, 2014; Domingue, Lasierra, Fensel, and Kasteren, 2016). The latest BD process provided 
in the literature is the BD chain as defined by Janssen, Voort & Wahyudi (2017) and consisted of 
four main activities: Data collecting, Data preparing, Data analyzing and Decision-making. Data 
preparation includes activities related to improving the data quality, data enrichment and creating 
a dataset that is suitable for using analytics. It is important to clarify that the aforementioned BD 
Chain is a process that focuses on the BD Management activities themselves and does not consider 
any other factors/activities, e.g. organizational factors, capabilities or business initiatives. 
The only evidence on BD Value Realization process is provided by Braganza, Brooks, Nepelski, 
Ali, & Moro (2016) who, based on a single case study, developed a linear archetype BD business 
process for resource management without though considering what organizational capabilities 
required or organizational factors driving the value realization through BD. Based on the 
archetype, they recommended a three-phase approach towards BD initiatives. The three-step 
approach can be considered as a practical implication towards exploration of BD efforts. However, 
still no clear framework is provided and the implications are resulting from a single case and not 
tested. Therefore, a framework for BD value realization process that could operate as a guide for 
building an action will be developed in the next section.

4.3. Big Data Value Realization Process  
The above findings are synthesized and the framework of “BD Value Realization Process” is 
developed below. The design of the framework has its basis on the Project Life Cycle theory 
(Barron M. & Barron A., 2011) with the phases of initiation, planning, implementation/execution 
and closing. Five phases are proposed; Business Strategy Alignment, Planning, Experimentation, 
Evaluation, and Replication/Expansion/Termination. Under each of the phases, one or more 
success criteria have been identified. Each of the phases includes one or more milestones; in the 
phase of Business Strategy Alignment the desired business outcomes will be translated into 
questions to be answered, while in the phase of Planning the questions will be translated into data 
to be acquired. The phase of Experimentation includes three milestones; the data are transformed 
into analytics, analytics into insights, and insights into decisions. In the phase of Evaluation, the 
decisions are translated into actions. The final phase will be either Replication, Expansion or 
Termination depending on the actions performed, the strategy the company decides to follow 
towards digitalization and the investments is willing to make. Finally, there are recommended 
steps under every milestone. 
The Business Strategy Alignment includes all the processes for defining the business outcomes that 
a company seeks to achieve through leveraging BD; solve business problems (e.g. shrinking 
market share, inefficient operations) or explore new business opportunities (e.g. sell new services 
to customers). It is essential that from the very beginning there is a causal link between what a 
company will measure through BD and what wants to accomplish in order to not result in a solution 
that is not tied to a business problem/opportunity. The recommended steps in this phase are to 
create a task force from strategic and operational level that together will decide the desired business 
outcomes and the business unit that will be the testing ground based on what they want to achieve. 
It is essential that employees from both strategic and operational level will work together on the 
aforementioned decisions in order that the BDA developed: (1) will be aligned with the company’s 
business processes and decision norms, and (2) will be useful for the employees in the front line, 
serving actual business goals. Moreover, they will breakdown the desired business outcomes into 
questions. Based on what needs to be answered and the impact of the desired outcomes, they need 
to ensure sponsorship from the senior management team. Finally, at this phase the BD strategic 
capabilities of planning, coordinating and controlling have to be ensured before the next phase of 
planning starts. 
==PAGE:65==
64 
 
Planning includes all the processes for defining the BD talent capabilities needed as defined in 
section 4.1.2.4. as well as the team that will work on the following milestones/ steps, the data to 
be acquired in order to answer the business questions as well as the BD infrastructure and BDA 
Models that should be in place in order to extract the data. After ensuring the right BD talent 
capabilities, a task force from operational level together with data scientists will be needed in order 
to break down the questions into variables, the variables into metrics and metrics into data. 
Considering what data needs to be acquired, the decisions on what BD infrastructure and BDA 
Models can support properly the data extraction will be made.  
Experimentation includes all the processes for the first translation of BD into decisions. As already 
mentioned, this phase includes three milestones; the first data acquired will turn into first analytics, 
the first analytics into first insights and first insights into first decisions. In the first milestone, all 
the data management activities will take place; BD collection, preparation and analysis.  Before 
the BD analysis, the BD Analytics Model will be deployed.  It is essential that the deployed BDA 
Models will be the less complex possible in order to be practical for the people in the front-line. 
In the second milestone, the BDA will be synthesized and translated into insights. The latter should 
be visualized through a simple tool in order to be accessible from the managers that will use them 
for decision making. When the insights are accessible, decisions will be made, completing the third 
milestone of the experimentation phase. 
Evaluation includes the actions taken based on the first data-driven decision making as well as the 
evaluation of these actions. If the actions are considered to bring about the expected outcomes, 
then Replication or Expansion will follow depending on the investment and the degree of BD 
involvement in the daily processes the company seeks. If this is not the case and the company 
decides to not continue with leveraging BD opportunities, Termination will take place.

5. Testing the Big Data Value Realization Framework at ODC  
5.1. BD Efforts at ODC 
ODC is currently in cooperation with a global supplier of industrial equipment and software 
analytics tools. Their cooperation is based on sensors established in specific types of equipment in 
ODC’s assets. These sensors provide data for the performance and condition of the equipment that 
ODC seeks to translate into Drilling Productivity and Predictive Maintenance. The latter solutions 
aim at reducing the maintenance level on equipment significantly. They are supposed to enable 
detection of anomalies and potential equipment breakdowns at an earlier stage than traditional 
methods used to. Thus, they will provide the foundation for data-based decision making that will 
significantly lower the risk and impact of breakdowns. It is expected that they will reduce both 
Operational expenditure (OPEX) and Capital expenditure (CAPEX) as well as downtime on rigs. 
However, these digital innovations will not only reduce the total well cost for ODC and its clients, 
but will also be an additional profit for the company since ODC seeks to sell them as additional 
services to its customers. ODC seeks to collect data from the sensors established at the assets’ 
equipment, develop advanced analytics, translate them into performance metrics and display the 
==PAGE:67==
66 
 
latter in performance dashboards. These dashboards and reporting will allow identification of best 
practices and actions based on data-driven decision making.  
5.2. Big Data Value Realization Process at ODC 
In the Business Strategy Alignment Phase, a task force from strategic and operational level has 
been developed. It was decided that the testing ground would be Operations Performance. 
Moreover, decisions on which assets and what types of equipment will be included in the roll-out 
of the BD efforts took place. The overall business outcome from the use of BD in Operations 
Performance is a reduction of the total well cost for ODC and its clients. The business outcome 
has been broken down into three smaller business outcomes: 1) Increased Operational Efficiency, 
2) Reduced Maintenance, and 3) Breakdown Avoidance. Operational efficiency, maintenance 
reduction and breakdown avoidance will be a source of savings for ODC and its clients, while 
there are opportunities for additional revenue for ODC, in case the BD solution is sold to its 
customers as an additional service. In this way, ODC would balance its profit and the customer 
satisfaction through consistent operations. Business Questions has not been raised but two 
directions were identified and all further phases focused on: Predictive Maintenance and Drilling 
Productivity. As for the sponsorship, it has been ensured as ODC sees significant business 
potential from leveraging BD efforts. Finally, ODC acquired BD Strategic Capabilities by defining 
the “owner” of the roll-out project which was decided to be the team responsible for Operations 
Performance Improvement (OPI). More specifically, the full responsibility in terms of 
planning/coordinating/controlling was on the Team Leader of the PI team, reporting to the head of 
the team and the Chief Operating Officer (COO). 
In the Planning Phase, no BD Talent Capabilities have been involved. The company has no BD 
Analysts in its workforce and did not proceed into any hiring. A team was synthesized by the team 
leader of the OPI Team with representatives from all departments with interest in this project. 
However, BD Analysts or members of the Operations Business Intelligence (OBI) team that define 
the scorecard, performance metrics are not involved at this stage of the process. Moreover, no steps 
for definition of variables, metrics and data needed have taken place. In regards to the BD means, 
the BD Infrastructure has been deployed. A data logger system offshore including hardware and 
software systems, was established on the rigs in order to provide streaming data access for the 
performance of the equipment onboard. Finally, Decisions on BD Analytics Methods/Tools took 
==PAGE:68==
67 
 
place. ODC evaluated the possible vendors and decided to purchase one of the possible BDA 
solutions. The latter enables data-driven decisions in regards to operational efficiencies and 
maintenance. 
In the Experimentation Phase and milestone of Data to Analytics, the data has been collected via 
the data loggers. For the data preparation, external software developers were hired. The BDA tool 
was deployed in order to meet the requirements of ODC. This tool requires streaming data access 
from drilling system in order to provide near real-time insights into drilling process. The data is 
streamed from equipment data logger to the tool’s “performance analytics” engine to generate 
insights and present them via stakeholder-specific dashboards. The solution takes into account 
various operational methods and uses an innovative method to infer the drilling process in near 
real-time. The solution detects deviations and facilitates drill-down to the sources of deviation. 
Currently, ODC is in the milestone of Analytics to Insights. At this stage, except from OPI team, 
OBI team has gotten involved. It is notable that, although the BDA tool provides some Key 
Performance Indicators (KPIs) based on the processes monitored but they are not necessarily 
aligned with what the OPI and OBI team seek to measure. Thus, the two teams are working on 
using the Analytics from the BDA tool to evaluate the current ODC’s KPIs towards Operations 
Performance. 
6. Discussion and improvement suggestions  
6.1. Observations/ Evaluation of the BD Value Realization Process at ODC 
It was observed that currently ODC faces significant challenges towards synthesizing BDA into 
Findings. Based on what was described above and the framework, what steps of the “BD Value 
Realization Process” are fulfilled, skipped, incomplete or pending in ODC’s current BD efforts are 
summarized below.  It was observed that in the Business Strategy Alignment phase the desired 
Business Outcomes were not broken down into questions to be answered. There was a direction 
of where to focus but what exactly OPI team, the “project owner”, was seeking to answer was 
unknown. In the Planning phase, although ODC ensured the means for generating Analytics, no 
definition of variables/metrics/data to be acquired was noticed which means that the BDA 
developed are not necessarily tied to the business outcomes ODC seeks to accomplish. Based on 
the framework, it is considered that the root cause of the latter was that 1) no questions of what 
they want to accomplish have been raised in the previous phase, 2) no BD Talent Capabilities were 
==PAGE:69==
68 
 
acquired and 3) the team responsible for Operations Performance Metrics (OBI) was not involved 
early in the process. Although ODC purchased a “ready solution” that provides basic insights, this 
does not mean that the tool has not to be adjusted in ODC’s needs. ODC needs BDA Professionals 
that will monitor the Analytics generated by the tool and translate them into valuable insights. It 
is also important to note that the external software developers, hired in the next phase of 
Experimentation for preparing the data, do not have the capabilities of a BD Analyst. A BD 
Analyst, not only deals with large volume of data, but also is aware of the business (Akter et al., 
2016). That is why also, in the next phase of Experimentation, the Analysis of Data is considered 
incomplete.  
6.2. Test of the Framework  
By the case study method and based on the framework, it can be considered that currently ODC 
faces significant challenges towards synthesizing the BDA into metrics/insights because has 
skipped or left incomplete some crucial steps in the previous phases. The fact that the step of 
translating the business objectives into questions in the Business Strategy Alignment phase was 
skipped, and subsequently the questions into variables and metrics in the Planning phase, enhanced 
by the lack of BD Talent capabilities, provide a logical explanation of why the Synthesization of 
BDA into metrics/insights is hard to be implemented in the Experimentation phase-the phase in 
which ODC currently is. The causal link between the skipped/incomplete steps and the challenges 
ODC currently faces can be considered as evidence of the developed framework’s functionality. 
6.3. Recommendations 
It is essential that the BD efforts ODC initiated towards operations performance will be tied to the 
business objectives seeks to target. As mentioned in previous section, BD and BDA will take root 
faster if related to business objectives and there is a clear business case that can quantify the 
benefits of BD. Considering what steps considered missed or incomplete in the BD Value 
Realization Process of ODC, a number of recommendations are presented below:  
i. 
Definition of questions to be answered: 
In order to synthesize the Analytics into Metrics, the objectives of Predictive Maintenance and 
Drilling Productivity have to be translated into questions. However, the translation cannot succeed 
if it is undertaken exclusively by the OPI Team. It is essential that all stakeholders, that have power 
and interest in it, to be involved; from stakeholders that are in the front-line and execute operations 
==PAGE:70==
69 
 
on a daily basis until stakeholders that focus on operations performance at strategic level and work 
closely to the customers. Thus, it is recommended that a workshop is organized by the project 
owner with representatives from all teams interested in and influenced by BD activities. The 
purpose of the workshop will be to prompt broader and creative thinking about potential questions 
to be answered in regards to Operations Performance. A possible question raised could be: “What 
decisions we could make if we had access to any possible data?” or “What would we like to know 
if we had access to any possible data?” The participants of the workshop could be separated into 
mixed teams, brainstorm and collaborate in order to develop ideas. 
ii. 
BD Analysts embedded in the Operations Department:  
In order to synthesize the insights generated from the tool and develop new metrics, ODC needs 
BD Analysts embed in its workforce, able to link the analytics with the business objectives. It is 
considered that the BD Analysts should be part of the OBI team as they are the ones that define 
the KPIs.  
iii. 
Build a BD Task Force:  
The project owner should synthesize a team, consisted of representatives of OBI team enhanced 
by the addition of BD Analysts, dedicated to translate the analytics of the tool to insights and 
metrics that will reflect the questions to be answered.  
iv. 
Develop Business-relevant Metrics/Insights:  
Having as starting point the ideas collected from the workshop, the BD Task Force should set a 
series of meetings with managers to better understand the type of decisions they make towards 
operations performance on a daily/monthly/yearly basis and how these decisions affect revenue 
and customer retention. These meetings will ensure that the metrics/insights developed will be 
aligned with ODC’s decision norms and processes. Based on ideas collected from the workshop 
and the outcomes of the series of meetings the BD task force will dedicate sufficient time to 
develop the metrics/insights.  
v. 
Present the metrics to the people interested: 
 It is essential that the metrics/insights will be presented to the people that will actually utilize them 
on an operational or strategic level and on a daily, monthly or yearly basis. A creative discussion 
on the metrics developed for potential adjustments/improvements will help to develop the metrics 
at the best possible way.  
==PAGE:71==
70 
 
It is considered that by following the above recommendations, the step of “Synthesization of 
Analytics into Insights” will be implemented and then the next steps and phases of the framework 
can take place. 
7. Implications on Theory and Practice  
This section highlights the areas of contribution to theory and practice. The main theoretical 
contributions of this paper are: a) Identification of the organizational factors driving Big Data 
Value Realization through a literature review, b) development of the “BD Value Realization 
Process” framework in order to fill in the literature gap, identified through a literature review and 
tested through a single case study method, and c) theoretical ground for scholars to validate the 
framework through cases studies in different organizations/industries/markets. Moreover, this 
research has implications for practitioners who are either planning to, or already implementing, 
BD efforts. This paper contributes to practice in two ways. Firstly, it identifies which 
organizational factors drive Value Realization through BD strategies. These factors can serve as a 
direction for practitioners when seeking to initiate BD efforts. Secondly, it outlines a framework 
of “Big Data Value Realization Process” tested through a single case study. The latter is a-five-
phase systematic approach towards BD value realization which can be a guide for practitioners to 
build an implementation plan and develop their success criteria.  
8. Conclusion and directions for further research 
Nowadays, organizations are seeking to leverage BD strategies in order to realize strategic or 
operational business goals. However, it appears that very few companies have achieved a big 
impact through BD and still many companies are unsure how to proceed towards realizing business 
value through BD. The literature provides little evidence on how companies to initiate, plan or 
implement BD strategies. Although recommendations can be found, a framework for how BD 
Value Realization can be achieved lacks. Thus, the purpose of the current paper is to develop a 
framework which can be used by all different sizes of organizations interested in exploring the 
potential of BD. It is a BD Value Realization mechanism that can help companies to plan their 
steps towards BD efforts. The framework developed was tested through a single case study. The 
case company operates in the oil and gas sector as a drilling services provider and is currently 
seeking to leverage BD opportunities towards increasing its operations performance. The paper 
contributes to the literature by identifying the organizational factors driving BD Value Realization 
==PAGE:72==
71 
 
through a literature review and providing a tested framework of “BD Value Realization Process” 
in order to fill in the literature gap identified. Moreover, it paves the way for further research and 
validation of the framework. The paper also contributes to practitioners who seek to leverage BD 
efforts by identifying which organizational factors drive BD Value Realization that can serve as a 
guide in regards to where to focus on when seeking to initiate BD efforts. Finally, practitioners 
can apply the framework of “Big Data Value Realization Process” in order to build an 
implementation plan and set their own success criteria towards BD strategies. 
The BD Value Realization factors identified and the framework developed are based on a literature 
review and not on a single case study. However, the current paper is not without limitations. First 
of all, the factors identified focus within organizational borders and do not expand to external 
factors such as industry/market factors (e.g. competitiveness). Secondly, the framework is tested 
using a single exemplar case in the oil and gas industry. Although the case company is an 
international company operating in many geographical areas, it is recommended that the 
framework is tested in dissimilar contexts such as public sector and validated. Finally, the case 
company operates in a high velocity market characterized by non-linear business processes, based 
on real-time information, cross-functional relationships, which are prerequisites for BD efforts to 
succeed within an organization. Therefore, it would be interesting to also test the framework in a 
more stable market where business processes are more analytical and linear.

Article structure
Review policy
As this journal has adopted a blind reviewing policy, please remove all identifying features from the
paper by ensuring that it would not be possible to identify the authors or their affiliation from names
appearing in the main text, in-text citations, reference list, acknowledgements, or any file name.
Authors are requested to include their name, the title of their article and their full contact details on
a separate title page as detailed below.
Length of article
Articles should be as concise as possible. Regular articles should not exceed 25 standard manuscript
pages and short communications should not exceed 10 standard manuscript pages, including tables
and figures. A standard manuscript page is A4 or letter size, text with 1.5 line spacing, 12 pt font
and ample margins. In exceptional cases the Editors may waive this requirement. Supplementary
material is allowed, which will be available in e-version only.
Subdivision - numbered sections
Divide your article into clearly defined and numbered sections. Subsections should be numbered
1.1 (then 1.1.1, 1.1.2, ...), 1.2, etc. (the abstract is not included in section numbering). Use this
numbering also for internal cross-referencing: do not just refer to 'the text'. Any subsection may be
given a brief heading. Each heading should appear on its own separate line.
Introduction
State the objectives of the work and provide an adequate background, avoiding a detailed literature
survey or a summary of the results.
Material and methods
Provide sufficient details to allow the work to be reproduced by an independent researcher. Methods
that are already published should be summarized, and indicated by a reference. If quoting directly
from a previously published method, use quotation marks and also cite the source. Any modifications
to existing methods should also be described.
Theory/calculation
A Theory section should extend, not repeat, the background to the article already dealt with in the
Introduction and lay the foundation for further work. In contrast, a Calculation section represents a
practical development from a theoretical basis.
Results
Results should be clear and concise.

additional item directly alongside the revised version of your manuscript. If your research article is

Online proof correction
Corresponding authors will receive an e-mail with a link to our online proofing system, allowing
annotation and correction of proofs online. The environment is similar to MS Word: in addition to
editing text, you can also comment on figures/tables and answer questions from the Copy Editor.
Web-based proofing provides a faster and less error-prone process by allowing you to directly type
your corrections, eliminating the potential introduction of errors.
If preferred, you can still choose to annotate and upload your edits on the PDF version. All instructions
for proofing will be given in the e-mail we send to authors, including alternative methods to the online
version and PDF.
We will do everything possible to get your article published quickly and accurately. Please use this
proof only for checking the typesetting, editing, completeness and correctness of the text, tables and
figures. Significant changes to the article as accepted for publication will only be considered at this
stage with permission from the Editor. It is important to ensure that all corrections are sent back
to us in one communication. Please check carefully before replying, as inclusion of any subsequent
corrections cannot be guaranteed. Proofreading is solely your responsibility.
Offprints
The corresponding author will, at no cost, receive a customized Share Link providing 50 days free
access to the final published version of the article on ScienceDirect. The Share Link can be used for
sharing the article via any communication channel, including email and social media. For an extra
charge, paper offprints can be ordered via the offprint order form which is sent once the article is
accepted for publication. Both corresponding and co-authors may order offprints at any time via
Elsevier's Webshop. Corresponding authors who have published their article gold open access do
not receive a Share Link as their final published version of the article is available open access on
ScienceDirect and can be shared through the article DOI link.
