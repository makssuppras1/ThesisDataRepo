Abstract
Background: Previous research investigating the inﬂuence of weight loss in obese individuals with knee OA have focused their
investigations on knee mechanics only. In additions, the studies also reported increases in walking speed following weight loss
which additionally has been associated with alteration in gait mechanics. As such, the isolated inﬂuence of body weight (BW)
on lower limb gait biomechanics in obese individuals with knee OA is not entirely clear. Therefore, the objective of the present
thesis was: (1) To describe the role of BW and BW changes on gait biomehcanics of both the ankle, knee and hip over time while
walking at standardized walking speed. (2) To investigate whether alterations in gait biomechanics following weight loss were
related to mere BW. As the ﬁrst peak knee adduction moment (KAM) has been a popular target measure when investigating
gait biomechanics in individuals with knee OA, ﬁrst peak KAM was chosen as the primary outcome variable.
Materials and Methods: Data from the CAROT trial (ClinicalTrials.gov identiﬁer: NCT00655941) were used to conduct
the present investigation.
The trial consisted of a 16 week weight loss intervention (week 0-16) and a 52 week weight loss
maintenance intervention (week 16-68). Three-dimensional gait analysis was performed at week 0, 16 and 68. Additionally,
experimental weight gain measurements were also performed at week 16 (week 16+) wearing a loaded weight vest. Relative
alterations in frontal and sagittal ankle, knee and hip moments and angles were calculated over time following the two phases
of the trial. In addition, linear regression models were generated following the 16 week weight loss intervention and following
experimental weight gain.
Results: 81 participants completed both phases of the trial. Following weight loss (week 0-16) the participants lost on average
13.36 kg (P<0.0001). Following weight loss maintenance (week 16-68) the participants gained on average 5.08 kg (P<0.0001).
This resulted in a statistical signiﬁcant weight loss from baseline to week 68 of 8.28 kg (P<0.0001). Results revealed that ﬁrst
peak KAM did not alter systematically with BW following weight loss and weight loss maintenance. However, throughout the
trial the participants increased their peak knee extensor moment and peak hip ﬂexor moment and decreased their peak plantar
ﬂexor moment. In addition, linear regression models showed no statistical signiﬁcant association between alteration in ﬁrst
peak KAM and alterations in BW neither following weight loss nor following experimental weight gain. The only signiﬁcant
associations found were the peak hip extensor moment and the peak plantar ﬂexor moment following experimental weight gain.
Conclusion: Due to the presented results, it was deduced that alterations in gait biomehcanics following weight loss was not
related to mere loss in BW. Instead, it was suggested that weight loss induced alterations in the coordination strategy during
gait. The adaptive gait pattern following the duration of the study was suggested to include increased loading of thee knee
due to increased peak knee extensor moment during weight acceptance and unloading of the knee during pre-swing due to the
participants using the hip ﬂexors to pull the limb o↵the ground rather than using the ankle and knee to push o↵during forward
propulsion.

1.
Background
Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common joint disease worldwide and serves as a leading cause of disability
[1, 2]. Obesity is a known predictor for onset and progression of knee OA [3–5]. Accordingly, reductions in
body weight (BW) have been proven e↵ective in relation to knee joint pain reduction, improved function and
delayed disease progression [6–9]. Traditionally, the link between knee OA and obesity was considered to be
due to excessive joint loading [10]. From this perspective, the aim of weight loss in patients with knee OA
was to reduce joint loads and thereby delay knee OA onset and progression [8, 11, 12].
Gait biomechanics have been used widely to investigate the inﬂuence of both knee OA and BW on gait
function [13–21]. Based on the conceptual understanding of the causal link between knee OA onset and
progression and knee joint loading, knee joint biomechanics have been widely investigated in both individuals
with knee OA and obese individuals without knee OA. The knee adduction moment (KAM) has been a
popular target measure as KAM has been found to be indicative of joint loading [22]. At the same time,
research has shown that KAM has also been associated with both knee OA progression [23] and knee OA
severity [24, 25].
Literature comparing knee OA patients with healthy individuals show that increased ﬁrst peak KAM has
been observed repeatedly in individuals with knee OA [26–30]. However, when looking at obese individuals in
comparison with healthy weight individuals without knee OA, it has been observed that the obese individuals
walk with lower ﬁrst peak KAM compared to healthy weight (HW) individuals. As such, despite the fact
that obesity is a strong predictor for knee OA, the gait pattern of obese individuals does not necessarily
reﬂect the same as that observed in obese and overweight individuals with knee OA [3–5]. From a clinical
point of view, the inﬂuence of excessive body weight and the inﬂuence of weight loss in individuals with knee
OA is clear [6–9]. However, from a biomechanical point of view, it could be interesting to learn how BW and
weight loss treatment inﬂuence gait biomechanics in obese individuals with knee OA.
Harding et al. (2012)[18] investigated the interaction between BMI and knee OA presence. The cohort of
Harding’s study included a subject group with moderate knee OA and an asymptomatic subject group,
both divided into body mass index (BMI) subgroups; healthy weight (BMI < 25kg/m2), overweight
1
==PAGE:10==
(25kg/m2 < BMI < 30kg/m2) and obese (BMI > 30kg/m2).
The study associated being overweight
and obese with a less ﬂuctuating KAM (decreased ﬁrst peak to mid stance KAM range) during stance com-
pared to healthy weight subjects (BMI < 25kg/m2). On the contrary, knee OA presence was associated
with an overall higher KAM magnitude during stance compared to asymptomatic. However, there was not
discovered any interacting inﬂuence between BMI and knee OA on KAM. The study by Harding et al. (2012)
[18] provides interesting insight into the interplay between BMI and knee OA but does not provide informa-
tion related to the inﬂuence of weight loss treatment on gait biomechanics in knee OA patients.
However, this was investigated by Messier et al. (2005)[12] and Aaboe et al. (2011) [8]. Messier et al.
(2005)[12] and Aaboe et al. (2011) [8] investigated the inﬂuence of weight loss on knee mechanics in knee
OA patients following moderate (< 5%BW) and substantial (> 10%BW) weight loss, respectively. Results
of their respective studies showed a positive relation between weight loss and ﬁrst peak internal abduction
moment as well as a positive relation between weight loss and knee joint loads. Aside from this, Aaboe et al.
(2011) [8] also reported an increase in self-selected walking speed following weight loss. Messier et al. (2005)
[12] did not report walking speed in the study from 2005. However, in the follow up study from 2011 [31]
including the same cohort, a signiﬁcant increase in walking speed was reported following weight loss [31].
Messier et al. (2005) concluded that moderate weight loss (< 5%) reduced knee joint loads to a higher
extent than the weight loss could explain alone, thus indicating that other factors such as changes in the
neuromuscular coordination might also inﬂuence gait biomechanics following weight loss. When comparing
the ﬁndings of Messier et al. (2005) [12] and Aaboe et al. (2011) [8], they both reported increased walking
speed following loss of body weight and associations between ﬁrst peak KAM and weight loss. However, as
walking speed has been additionally related to alterations in gait kinetics and kinematics [15, 32, 33], it is
unknown whether the previously observed adaptions reported in these studies were related to the BW solely,
whether neuromuscular adaptations had occurred or if other factors also play a role.
Therefore, the objective of the following thesis was to investigate the role of BW and BW changes on gait
biomechanics in obese patients with knee OA while eliminating the potential inﬂuence of alterations in walking
speed. The investigation was carried out by using data from the CAROT trial (Inﬂuence of weight loss or ex-
ercise on cartilage in obese knee osteoarthritis patients: an RCT, ClinicalTrials.gov identiﬁer: NCT00655941).
The study was a longitudinal study including a 16 weeks weight loss intervention period followed by a 52
weeks weight loss maintenance period. At weight loss follow up at week 16 there was additionally conducted
an experimental weight gain experiment (denoted week 16+).
2
==PAGE:11==
It was decided to carry out the investigation in two steps; Firstly, the purpose was to describe the inﬂuence
of weight loss and subsequent weight loss maintenance on gait biomechanics in knee OA patients. Secondly,
the aim was to test if changes in the gait biomechanics were merely related to changes in BW in knee OA
patients walking at a ﬁxed walking speed. The ﬁrst part of the investigation was referred to as the weight loss
experiment (observing data from week 0-16-68) whereas the second part of the investigation was referred to
as the weight gain experiment (observing data from week 0-16-16+). The purpose of the weight loss experi-
ment was to observe how gait biomehcanics were inﬂuenced by weight loss and subsequently observe whether
the gait biomehcanics remained constant following a longer period of weight loss maintenance. Previous
weight loss studies in individuals with knee OA have focused their investigation on knee joint biomehcanics.
As gait is complex, it was decided for the present investigation to explore the inﬂuence of weight loss and
weight loss maintenance on both ankle, knee and hip joints. The purpose of the weight gain experiment was
to investigate whether the mechanical changes observed following weight loss, were related to mere loss in
body weight by investigating associations between gait biomechanics and alterations in BW following weight
loss and experimental weight gain. To support this, it was also decided to investigate whether the observed
alterations following weight loss were directly reversible following experimental weight gain.
Due to the previously mentioned ﬁndings relating decreases in body weight with decreases in ﬁrst peak KAM
in individuals with knee OA, ﬁrst peak KAM was chosen as the primary outcome variable. For the weight
loss experiment, it was hypothesized that ﬁrst peak KAM (KAM1) would signiﬁcantly decrease over time
following the weight loss period (week 0-16) and subsequently remain unchanged over time following a weight
loss maintenance period (week 16-68). For the weight gain experiment (week 16-16+), assuming that alter-
ations in gait biomechanics following alterations in BW were related to mere body weight, it was hypothesized
that the relationship between changes in BW and changes in ﬁrst peak KAM would be signiﬁcant and with
identical slope magnitudes when comparing weight loss (week 0-16) with experimental weight gain (week
16-16+). The described hypotheses can be seen below.

2.
Materials and Methods
The present thesis used data from the CAROT trial (ClinicalTrials.gov identiﬁer: NCT00655941) [34], which
was an interventional clinical trial conducted in 2010 by Henning Bliddal, Professor, MD at The Parker Insti-
tute, Frederiksberg Hospital, Denmark. The following passage initially contains information on patients and
study design of the CAROT trial (Section 2.1) and data acquisition processes of the CAROT trial (Section
2.2). Subsequently, it contains information on variable selection and how data was processed for the present
thesis (Section 2.3).
2.1
CAROT trial: Study design and patients
The CAROT trial [34] was a longitudinal trial including obese patients diagnosed with knee OA. The trial
consisted of two phases; one phase including a 16 week weight loss intervention period (week 0-16) and a
second phase including a weight loss maintenance period of 52 weeks (week 16-68). Thus, the study had a
total duration of 68 weeks. Gait analysis measurements were conducted at weeks 0, 16 and 68. Addition-
ally, experimental weight gain measurements were performed at week 16 (week 16+), where the participants
wore a weight vest loaded with the number of kilos that each participant had lost during the previous 16 weeks.
The overall inclusion criteria to enter the CAROT trial [34] was; Age above 50 years, diagnosed knee OA
based on clinical symptoms and radiographs, and BMI > 30kg/m2. Exclusion criteria were; Lack of moti-
vation for losing weight, inability to speak Danish, planned bariatric surgery, total knee alloplasty (TKA),
or receiving medication therapy for obesity. Additionally, participating in the gait analysis required that the
participant could walk without walking aid. Lastly, it was only patients who lost more than 5 kg from week 0
to 16, who were accepted to participate in the experimental weight gain experiment. Based on the mentioned
criteria, a total of 192 participants were recruited and entered phase one of the study.
4
==PAGE:13==
2.1.1
Phase 1 - Weight loss program; week 0-16
The weight loss phase was carried out using a supervised dietary program. The initial 8 weeks of phase 1, the
participants received formula diet only. The additional 8 weeks, the participants were enrolled in a dietary
program consisting of a hypo-energetic diet of normal food, meal replacements (The Cambridge Diet, the
Cambridge Health and Weight plan UK) and nutritional education.
Throughout the entire weight loss phase, each participant was o↵ered weekly sessions (1.5 h/week) with a
dietitian where they received nutritional instructions and behavioural therapy. The weekly sessions at the
dietitian were additionally used to reinforce and encourage the patients in the weight loss phase and thereby
obtain a high degree of compliance. The aim of the weight loss phase was for each participant to loose more
than 10% of baseline BW.
2.1.2
Experimental weight gain; week 16+
The experimental weight gain experiment was conducted using an adjustable weighted vest from Reebok®.
The weight of the vest was adjusted for each patient to equal their individual weight loss (±0.1kg). The
vest was worn and attached to the upper body and fastened with Velcro® fasteners. The participants were
allowed to get comfortable with walking with the vest before preceding to the gait analysis. Throughout this
assignment, data from the experimental weight gain experiment will be denoted as data from week 16+.
2.1.3
Phase 2 - Weight loss maintenance program; week 16-68
In the weight maintenance phase, the participants were assigned to either dietary instruction group (D),
exercise group (E), or no attention group/control group (C). This group division was used in previous studies
to test whether if structural progression of knee OA and symptoms di↵ered between the three intervention
groups following 52 weeks [34, 35]. Note that the group division was not used in the present thesis.
2.2
CAROT trial: Data acquisition
The subjects conducted gait analysis at standardized walking speed at week 0, 16, 16+ and 68.
2.2.1
Gait analysis system
Gait analysis data were acquired in a gait lab, using a three dimensional (3D) motion analysis system (Vicon,
MX, Vicon Motion Systems, Oxford UK) including six cameras (MX-F20, Vicon Motion Systems, Oxford
5
==PAGE:14==
UK) sampling with a frequency of 100 Hz. The laboratory was equipped with a gait bridge of 10 m, con-
taining two force platforms (AMTI OR 6-5-1000, Watertown, MA, USA) which sampled ground reaction
forces at a frequency of 1500 Hz. Lastly, the gait lab was equipped with a photocell system which registered
the walking speed of the participant and displaying it on a digital display. The analysis system recorded
positional data based on reﬂective markers placed bilaterally on the anterior and posterior iliac spines, lateral
aspect of the thighs, lateral femoral epicondyles, lateral aspects of the shanks, lateral malleoli, calcanea, and
second metatarsal heads. Vicon’s Plug-In-Gait (Plug-In-Gait, Vicon Peak, Oxford, UK) was used to estimate
the position and orientation of the 7 segments of interest (Pelvis, both thighs, shanks and feet) [8, 36]. When
data were collected, the data from the force platforms were synchronized with kinematic data recorded by
the cameras. Subsequently 3D kinetics were calculated using inverse dynamics via the Plug-In-Gait model
(Vicon Motion Systems, Oxford, UK) [8].
2.2.2
Gait analysis procedure
The gait analysis procedure was initiated by obtaining anthropometric measures and placing reﬂective mark-
ers at the anatomical landmarks mentioned in the above section.
Subsequently, a static calibration was
performed.
Prior to initiating the measurements, each participant was allowed to practice walking across the gait bridge
to adapt to the predeﬁned walking speed of 4.5 km/h. Additionally, the practice trials were used to determine
the starting point for each subject to ensure a clean foot strike on either of the two force platforms without
targeting them. The goal of the gait analysis was to obtain 6 successful trials, whereto they all had to be
within a ±0.1km/h range of the predetermined walking speed and with successful force platform hits. All
participants conducted the gait analysis bare footed.
The participants only had their a↵ected knee analyzed. If both knees appeared to be a↵ected, the participant
was asked to pick which knee they experienced as the most symptomatic one. Then the most symptomatic
knee was chosen for analysis, based on the participants report.
2.3
Data Processing and variable selection
The outcome variables included were frontal knee and hip joint moments, sagittal ankle, knee and hip joint
moments, sagittal ankle, knee and hip joint angles and foot progression angle.
For all outcome variables included, the desired outcome measures were extracted during stance unless oth-
6
==PAGE:15==
erwise stated. Data was processed as follows: For all successful gait trials for each participant, stance was
deﬁned from heel strike (HS) to toe o↵(TO) for the a↵ected leg. Following localization of stance, all tri-
als were time normalized. Subsequently, within subject averaging was conducted for all successful trials,
thus resulting in one single averaged time-course pattern during stance phase for the a↵ected leg for each
participant. Subsequently, the desired outcome measure was extracted from the within subject averaged
curve. Descriptions of the variable selection for each outcome variable are found in the following sections.
All data processing was conducted using MATLAB®( R2018b Update 4 (9.5.0.1067069)) unless otherwise
stated. More speciﬁc information on the data processing and excerpts of the Matlab code can be found in
the Appendix A.1.

2.3.1
Primary outcome variable
The primary outcome variable was ﬁrst peak frontal knee joint moment. The Plug-In-Gait model (Vicon
Motion Systems, Oxford, UK) calculated the joint moments as internal joint moments. However, the liter-
ature reporting ﬁndings of the frontal knee joint moment most frequently report their ﬁndings in terms of
external joint moments [18, 38]. Based on the rationale that the internal joint moment is a product of the
external forces acting on the joint, it was decided to interpret the frontal knee joint moment as an external
joint moment and denote it as the knee adduction moment(KAM).
7
==PAGE:16==
Therefore, positive values of KAM indicate that the external frontal knee joint moment drives the knee to-
wards adduction (varus) whereas negative values indicate the external frontal knee joint moment drives the
knee towards abduction (valgus). The time-course pattern of KAM during stance is typically a biphasic re-
sponse with local maxima during both early stance (ES) and late stance (LS) and a local minima during mid
stance (MS), see Figure 2.2. In the current investigation, peak KAM during ES was of primary focus, also
denoted ﬁrst peak KAM. First peak KAM was deﬁned as the maximum value during ES (11%−40% of stance).

2.3.2
Secondary outcome variables
The following contains descriptions of the secondary outcome variables included in this study and how they
were deﬁned. All joint moments of the secondary outcome variables were contrary to ﬁrst peak KAM re-
ported in internal joint moments:
Frontal hip joint moments: The frontal hip joint moment during stance is exempliﬁed in Figure 2.3. Positive
values of the frontal hip moment indicate hip abductor dominance whereas negative values indicate hip ad-
ductor dominance. Similar to KAM, the frontal hip joint moment time-course pattern is typically biphasic
with a peak during ES and LS. For the present purpose, peak values during ES (11% −40% of stance) were
extracted and deﬁned as ﬁrst peak hip abduction moment.

Positive values of the hip angle denote hip ﬂexion whereas negative values denote hip extension. The desired
measure of interest was the hip ﬂexion/extension excursion which was deﬁned as the range from maximum
peak hip ﬂexion angle from HS through ES (0% −40% of stance) and the minimum peak hip extension angle
from LS to TO (61% −100% of stance).

assignment, it was desired to look at the plantar ﬂexion/dorsal ﬂexion excursion which was deﬁned as the
range between the maximum peak dorsal ﬂexion angle during LS (61% −90% of stance) and the TO plantar

2.4
Statistical analysis
The results were presented as mean (SD) unless otherwise stated. As mentioned previously, within subject
averaging was used from which the desired parameter of measure was extracted.
For the weight loss experiment (week 0-16-68), two tailed paired t-test was applied to test if any outcome
variables altered signiﬁcantly over time. For the weight gain experiment (week 0-16-16+), linear regression
analysis was conducted. From the linear regression analysis, slopes and p-values were extracted. In cases
where slopes were signiﬁcant at both weight loss (week 0-16) and experimental weight gain (week 16-16+),
a t-test was applied to compare slopes of the regression models. The linear regression models presented in
plots included 95% conﬁdence bounds. In addition, for the weight gain experiment, two tailed paired t-test
was also applied to test for any signiﬁcant changes following changes in BW during the experiment. For all
t-tests, Microsoft® Excel for Mac (Version 16.16.27 (201012)) was used. Statistical signiﬁcance was accepted
at P<0.05.

4.
Discussion
4.1
Weight loss experiment
The purpose of the weight loss experiment was to observe how gait biomehcanics were inﬂuenced by weight
loss and subsequently investigate whether the observed mechanical alterations remained constant following
a longer period of weight loss maintenance. It was hypothesized that ﬁrst peak KAM would signiﬁcantly
decrease over time following the 16 weeks of weight loss and subsequently remain unchanged over time fol-
lowing the 52 weeks of weight loss maintenance.
The above presented results revealed, that ﬁrst peak KAM did not change following a statistical signiﬁcant
weight loss of 13.36 kg over the period of 16 weeks. However, following the period of additional 52 weeks
of weight loss maintenance, where the participants gained on average 5.08 kg, ﬁrst peak KAM decreased
signiﬁcantly. Based on the presented ﬁndings, both hypotheses related to the weight loss experiment were
rejected. The following sections discuss the cause for our unexpected ﬁndings from the weight loss experi-
ment and look further into the mechanical alterations observed in the remaining joints during the weight loss
experiment. Initially results from the weight loss period (week 0-16) are discussed. Following that, results
from the weight loss maintenance period (week 16-68) are discussed and lastly results over the entire study
period (week 0-68) are discussed.
4.1.1
Alterations in gait biomechanics during the weight loss experiment
Week 0-16
The stated hypothesis on ﬁrst peak KAM decreasing following weight loss was outlined due to the intuitive
expectation that loss of BW would lead to decreases in the vertical ground reaction force (vGRF) which takes
part predicting the variance in ﬁrst peak KAM [40]. Additionally, previous studies investigating weight loss
in both obese individuals with and without knee OA observed decreases in ﬁrst peak KAM following weight
loss with a varying choice of walking speed being either predetermined or self selected and including weight
22
==PAGE:31==
losses which were both smaller and larger then that of the present study [8, 12, 41, 42]. Despite the above,
the present study could not demonstrate the same. Based on this, it was proposed that the participants
must have altered their coordination strategy during gait following weight loss resulting in ﬁrst peak KAM
increasing to an extent that counteracted the expected decrease in ﬁrst peak KAM related to the loss of BW.
When considering obese individuals while walking, they appear to have a characteristic waddling gait pattern
including wide foot stance and trunk sway [43–46]. This gait strategy appears to be more dynamic medial to
lateral and less dynamic vertically, thus causing a reduction in the the vGRF during loading response. Li et
al. (2019) [6] reported that obese individuals with knee pain increased knee excursion in the sagittal plane
and decreased knee excursion in the frontal plane following weight loss. Based on this, it could be expected
that the participants adapted into a gait pattern more dynamic vertically, which might be responsible for
counteracting the expected decrease in ﬁrst peak KAM following weight loss.
From the results, it was observed that knee ﬂexion excursion did not change following weight loss. Despite
that, the peak knee extensor moment was observed to increase. Due to the impact of reductions in BW on
vGRF, it was deduced that the increased peak knee extensor moment following weight loss was related to
altered motion within the knee joint. McMillan et al. (2010)[13] both reported knee ﬂexion excursion, peak
knee ﬂexion angle and knee angle at IC and found both smaller peak knee ﬂexion angle and knee ﬂexion
angle at IC when comparing obese individuals to HW individuals. Based on this, they did not ﬁnd statis-
tically signiﬁcant di↵erences in the knee ﬂexion excursion between obese individuals and HW individuals.
In addition, Harding et al. (2012) [18] associated being overweight and obese with knee OA with a smaller
saggital knee moment range (peak knee extension moment to peak knee ﬂexion moment range). As such,
the ﬁndings of unchanged knee ﬂexion excursion despite an increased knee extensor moment could be due to
the participants increasing their peak knee ﬂexion angle and knee angle at IC simultaneously following the
weight loss.
However, in relation to the above Schipplein et al. (1991) [47] discovered that walking with a gait pattern
including higher knee extension moment (demanding a higher quadriceps muscle force production), would
serve as a way to improve joint stability of the knee in the frontal plane and thereby decrease the adducting
knee joint moment. In addition, Shull et al. (2013) [48] described that the variance in KAM was primarily
described by the knee adduction angle which inﬂuences the length of the lever arm of KAM and second to
most described by vGRF. Therefore, decreased excursion in the frontal plane and increased dynamics in the
sagittal plane following weight loss might also contribute to decreases in ﬁrst peak KAM.
From this, it was deduced that increased knee extensor moment and knee ﬂexion angles could potentially
inﬂuence ﬁrst peak KAM in both directions. The overall outcome of these mechanisms would depend on
23
==PAGE:32==
which mechanism is the most dominant and which mechanisms have the highest impact on ﬁrst peak KAM.
However, based on the the results of the present investigation, it was not possible to conclude further on the
inﬂuence of these mechanisms.
For the hip, there was observed an increase in hip excursion and an increase in the hip ﬂexor moment. As
such, it was reasonable to deduce that the weight loss caused the participants to walk with a more extended
hip during late stance. Opposite, the gait dynamics of the hip during early stance did not seem to be inﬂu-
enced by the weight loss. As for the ﬁrst peak hip abduction moment, McMillan et al. (2010) [13] observed
that obese individuals had a smaller ﬁrst peak frontal hip joint moment than that of the HW individuals.
In addition, M¨undermann et al. (2005) [24] reported the same for individuals with knee OA. Accordingly,
the present investigation found an increase in ﬁrst peak hip abduction moment following weight loss which
seemed expected due to the ﬁndings of McMillan et al. (2010) [13] and M¨undermann et al. (2005) [24].
For the ankle, it was observed that the ankle excursion was uninﬂuenced by the weight loss. Contrary, the
peak plantar ﬂexor moment increased signiﬁcantly. As discussed in relation to the knee joint, the increased
peak plantar ﬂexor moment was proposed to be caused by altered dynamics of the ankle, as the isolated im-
pact of loss in BW would cause the peak planar ﬂexor moment to decrease. The ankle excursion was deﬁned
as the range between peak dorsal ﬂexion angle and the plantar ﬂexion angle a TO. Therefore, in order to
explain the increased peak plantar ﬂexor moment despite unchanged ankle excursion, it was assumed that
the peak dorsal ﬂexion angle increased in magnitude while the plantar ﬂexion angle TO decreased with the
same magnitude following weight loss.
In terms of the foot progression angle, Runharr et al. (2011) [19] associated obesity with a greater toe-out
angle. Accordingly, present results revealed that following weight loss, the foot progression angle increased
causing the measured foot to become more internally rotated. However, Shull et al. (2013) [48] related a
more internally rotated foot with reduced ﬁrst peak KAM in individuals with medial compartment knee OA.
In addition, Koblauch et al. (2013) [49] investigated the inﬂuence on internally and externally rotated feet
on the frontal knee joint moment and knee joint compressive forces during gait in healthy individuals and
found that walking with externally rotated feet resulted in signiﬁcantly higher ﬁrst peak frontal knee joint
moment and medial compartment compressive forces. Opposite, walking with internally rotated feet was
associated with lower ﬁrst peak knee adduction moment and medial compartment compressive forces. Due
to the ﬁnding of a more internally rotated foot and no alteration in ﬁrst peak KAM following weight loss
in the present study, the toe-out angle did not assist in explaining the cause for our ﬁndings of ﬁrst peak KAM.

Returning to the waddling gait pattern of obese individuals, a number of studies have found that both
increased trunk sway and increased step width was associated with reduced magnitudes ﬁrst peak KAM
[50, 51]. Accordingly, Ponta et al. (2014) [44] observed that lateral trunk sway decreased following weight
loss in obese individuals and Vartianien et al. (2012)[42] observed that the step width decreased following
weight loss obese individuals. As such, decreased trunk sway and step width following weight loss might
have caused an increase in ﬁrst peak KAM. Motion capture data of the upper body were not obtained in the
CAROT study [34]. Additionally heel marker data which are used to calculate step width was not available
for all trials. Thus, it was not possible to investigate neither trunk sway nor step width for the included
participants. In addition, M¨undermann et al. (2008) [50] associated increased lateral trunk sway with re-
duced ﬁrst peak frontal hip moment. As the present investigation observed increased ﬁrst peak hip abduction
moment following weight loss, it was suspected, that step width and trunk sway might have inﬂuenced the
ﬁndings of ﬁrst peak KAM.
Summarizing, following weight loss it was observed that participants walked with increased peak plantar ﬂexor
moment, peak hip ﬂexor moment and peak knee extensor moment whereas the peak knee ﬂexor moment and
peak hip extensor moment did not alter. In addition, it was observed that the ankle ﬂexion excursion and
the knee ﬂexion excursion did not change whereas the hip ﬂexion/extension excursion increased. However,
in relation to the ankle ﬂexion excursion and the knee ﬂexion excursion it was proposed that unchanged
excursion were resulting from increased peak dorsal ﬂexion angle and decreased plantar ﬂexion at TO and
increased peak knee ﬂexion angle and knee angle at HS. As such, following a 16 week weight loss intervention,
it was proposed that the participants adapted to a gait strategy including increased knee extensor moment
explained by increased knee ﬂexion during early stance. Whereas during late stance, they applied a gait
strategy which was increasingly driven by the ankle and hip due to increased hip ﬂexor moment and plantar
ﬂexor moment. The observed increases in the hip ﬂexor moment and plantar ﬂexor moment was considered
due to a more extended hip and a more dorsal ﬂexed foot.
Week 16-68
Following weight loss maintenance it was observed that the participants on average gained 5.08 kg. Ad-
ditionally, during this period, ﬁrst peak KAM decreased signiﬁcantly by 4.50 Nm. The presented ﬁndings
following weight loss maintenance were also unlike the hypothesis. As explained previously, a decrease in ﬁrst
peak KAM following weight loss was expected due to the previous studies reporting decreases in ﬁrst peak
KAM following weight loss in knee OA patients [8, 12]. Accordingly, it was expected ﬁrst peak KAM would
alter systemically with the BW. Therefore, following the weight loss maintenance, it was expected that ﬁrst
25
==PAGE:34==
peak KAM remained unchanged. However, as it was observed that ﬁrst peak KAM decreased signiﬁcantly
following this period which in addition included a slight but signiﬁcant weight gain, it must concluded that
the participants must have altered their gait strategy following weight loss maintenance to an extent that
caused a decrease in ﬁrst peak KAM despite a slight weight gain.
The only variables which appeared to alter systematically with the BW were the foot progression angle and
the peak plantar ﬂexor moment. They both increased following weight loss and decreased following weight
loss maintenance where the BW slightly increased. It was previously mentioned, that a more internally ro-
tated foot was related to reduced ﬁrst peak KAM [48]. However, as the present investigation observed a more
internally rotated foot with with no alteration in ﬁrst peak KAM following weight loss and a more external
rotation with a reduction in ﬁrst peak KAM following a slight weight gain, it was evident that the foot progres-
sion angle in this particular case did not play an essential role en explaining the magnitude of ﬁrst peak KAM.
As for the peak plantar ﬂexor moment, it decreased following weight loss maintenance. Hortobagyi et al.
(2011) [41] found that the peak plantar ﬂexor moment decreased signiﬁcantly following weight loss in obese
individuals without knee OA, thus suggesting that gait is driven by the ankle function in obese individuals.
Opposite, in the present study, there was observed an increase following weight loss at week 16 and a de-
crease following weight loss maintenance at week 68 including a slight increase in BW. As such, obese knee
OA patients might not have the same ankle driven gait as obese but otherwise healthy individuals. Ro et
al. (2019) [28] described that subjects with knee OA had a signiﬁcantly lower peak plantar ﬂexor moment
relative to healthy controls. Accordingly, they found that the ROM of the ankle was smaller for individuals
with knee OA. Based on the present ﬁndings of decreased peak plantar ﬂexor moment following weight loss
maintenance, the dynamics of the ankle might be more determined by the symptomatic condition related to
knee OA rather than the BW. For this particular cohort, knee joint pain altered systematically with BW
both following weight loss and weight loss maintenance [8, 34, 52]. Riecke et al. (2010) [52] reported an
average pain reduction on the visual analogue scale (VAS) of 11.1 mm following weight loss (week 0-16)
whereas Christensen et al. (2015) [34] reported a signiﬁcant decrease of 6.1 mm, 5.6 mm and 5.5 mm for
the diet, exercise and control groups respectively from baseline to week 68. As such, due to the peak plantar
moment altering systematically with the BW, it seemed probable that the peak plantar ﬂexor moment was
inﬂuenced by the symptomatic condition of the knee rather than the BW. However, observing the ankle
excursion, it did not alter accordingly. Following weight loss maintenance, the ankle excursion increased
signiﬁcantly whereas the peak plantar ﬂexor moment decreased signiﬁcantly. One explanation to this might
be that the participants during the weight loss maintenance period altered their ankle dynamics in late and
terminal stance towards a less dorsal ﬂexed foot during late stance and a much more plantar ﬂexed foot at
26
==PAGE:35==
TO causing an increase ankle excursion yet a decrease in the peak plantar ﬂexor moment. In relation to
the decreased peak plantar ﬂexor moment, Fisher et al. (1997)[53] found that individuals with knee pain
ambulated with increased hip ﬂexor moments and decreased peak plantar ﬂexor moment during terminal
stance. They suggested that this was a strategy used to unload the knee in the pre-swing phase by lifting the
limb o↵the ground using the hip ﬂexors rather than pushing o↵with the ankle. In the present investigation,
there was observed an increased magnitude of the peak hip ﬂexor moment and decreased peak plantar ﬂexor
moment following weight loss maintenance. As such, the proposed gait strategy by Fisher et al. (1997)[53]
might be reﬂected in the participants of the CAROT trial [34].
Aside from the above, following weight loss maintenance, there was observed an increase in magnitude of the
peak knee extensor moment and a decrease in magnitude of the peak knee ﬂexor moment, causing a shift
towards a gait pattern including more knee extensor moment and less knee ﬂexor moment. Despite this shift,
there was not observed any alterations in the knee ﬂexion excursion which expressed the ROM of the knee
during early stance. As such, the increase in the peak knee extensor moment might either be caused by the
slight weight gain following this period, however more likely was it that the knee ﬂexion excursion was shifted
towards a more ﬂexed knee both during IC and during the time of peak knee ﬂexion angle. Opposite to the
present ﬁndings, Henriksen et al. (2006)[54] observed a shift towards more knee ﬂexor moment during LS
and less knee extensor moment during ES following pain relief in patients with knee OA. As such, the shift
towards more knee extensor moment and less knee ﬂexor moment could be in response to the slight increase
in BW and joint pain following the weight loss maintenance period [8, 34, 52].
However, when observing the alterations in the hip ﬂexor moment and knee extensor moment following both
weight loss and weight loss maintenance, it was observed that the alteration for each respective period was
progressive and not alternating like the BW which was reported to inﬂuence the knee joint pain in knee OA
patients [8, 34, 52]. Based in this, the shift in sagittal knee joint moment towards more knee extensor moment
and less knee ﬂexor moment was not considered related to the slight increase in BW following weight loss
maintenance. Collectively, this progressive pattern indicated that alterations in gait biomechanics following
weight loss and weight loss maintenance were not related to BW only. In addition, the observed response
pattern could question whether biomechanical adaptations following short term weight loss are useful in terms
of understanding how weight loss treatment inﬂuence gait biomechanics.
Week 0-68
When observing the ﬁndings from week 0 to week 68 there was observed an average weight loss of 8.28 kg.
Accordingly, there was observed a signiﬁcant decrease in ﬁrst peak KAM of 4.26 Nm following this period.

Observing this ﬁnding isolated, it appeared to comply with the expectation related to ﬁrst peak KAM fol-

was evident that alterations in ﬁrst peak KAM could not be explained by the BW alone. Based on the
ﬁndings of ﬁrst peak KAM following the weight loss experiment, it seemed appropriate to conclude that for
this particular study, the participants seemed to reorganize their gait strategy over time following signiﬁ-
cant decreases in BW and that the reorganization resulted in a decrease in ﬁrst peak KAM following 68 weeks.
Looking into how the gait strategy altered throughout the time period of the entire weight loss study for
the explanatory variables, there was observed a shift towards a gait pattern including more knee extensor
moment and less knee ﬂexor moment. Assuming a concomitant shift in the peak knee ﬂexion angle and
the knee ﬂexion angle during IC, the increased knee extensor moment was a result of a more ﬂexed knee
during HS and early stance. Kaufmann et al (2001) [27] found that knee OA patients walk with reduced knee
extensor moments to minimize pain. Accordingly, M¨undermann et al. (2005) [24] reported that individuals
with knee OA made initial contact with the ground with the knee in a more extended position than that
exhibited by control subjects. Additionally, Aaboe et al. (2011) [8] reported decreases in knee joint pain in
the participants following weight loss. As such an increase in the peak knee extensor moment seemed to be
expected following weight loss in knee OA patients.
As for the hip dynamics, the hip ﬂexion/extension excursion increased following week 68. This complied
with the ﬁndings presented by Hortobagyi [41], who reported an increased hip ROM following weight loss in
obese but otherwise healthy subjects. As for the hip moment, there was observed no alteration in the peak
hip extensor moment and an a signiﬁcantly larger peak hip ﬂexor moment following the study period. Based
on this, it was reasonable to assume that the alteration in the hip ﬂexion/extension excursion primarily was
due to increased hip extension angle during late stance explaining the larger peak hip ﬂexor moment. How-
ever, Astephen et al. (2007) [29] and M¨undermann et al. (2005) [24] both reported that knee OA patients
exhibited a larger hip ﬂexion moment compared to asymptomatic controls. As such, it was interesting that
the participants additionally increased the peak hip ﬂexor moment following symptomatic relief caused by
the weight loss [8, 34].
In terms of the ankle dynamics, a decreased peak plantar ﬂexor moment was observed following the study
period, yet the ankle ﬂexion excursion was observed to increase. Remembering that the peak plantar ﬂexor
moment was one out of two variables that systematically altered as the BW changed throughout the weight
loss experiment. The peak plantar ﬂexor moment altered opposite to the BW throughout the study period,
meaning that when the BW decreased the peak plantar ﬂexor moment increased and vice versa. Based on

==PAGE:37==
this, it was suggested that the decreased peak plantar ﬂexor moment was explained by the ankle being signif-
icantly more plantar ﬂexed at TO and less dorsi ﬂexed during late stance when the BW decreased. This shift
towards a more plantar ﬂexed foot may have reduced the ability for ankle moment production for forward
progression due to the ankle plantar ﬂexors being in a shortened position which changed the muscle force
production due to the force-length relationship and thereby also the moment production. As mentioned pre-
viously, Hortobagyi et al. (2011) [41] found that peak plantar ﬂexor moment decreased signiﬁcantly following
weight loss in obese individuals without knee OA, thus suggesting that obese ambulated with a gait pattern
more ankle driven compared to healthy individuals. The present ﬁndings of the peak plantar ﬂexor moment,
showed that following weight loss maintenance, the peak plantar ﬂexor moment decreased to a higher extent
than it increased following weight loss, thus resulting in a peak plantar ﬂexor moment lower at week 68
relative to baseline. As such, the participants appeared to adapt to a gait pattern less driven by the ankle
throughut the trial.
In summary, at week 68 following a weight loss of 8.28 kg compared to baseline, it was observed that the
participants altered their gait strategy in a way that increased their peak hip ﬂexor moment, increased their
peak knee extensor moment and decreased their peak plantar ﬂexor moment. In addition, it was observed
that the knee ﬂexion excursion did not change whereas the hip ﬂexion/extension excursion and the ankle

unchanged ﬂexion excursion resulted from increased peak knee ﬂexion angle and knee angle at HS. Fisher
et al. (1997) [53] found that individuals with knee pain increased their hip ﬂexor moments and decreased
their peak plantar ﬂexor moment during terminal stance. They suggested that this was a strategy used to
unload the knee in late and terminal stance by lifting the limb o↵the ground using the hip ﬂexors rather
than pushing o↵with the ankle. Baulinas et al. (2002) [26] mentioned, that walking without knee ﬂex-
ion moment in late mid stance is recognized as a quadriceps avoidance patter. Contrary increased knee
extensor moment during early stance has been associated with increased quadriceps engagement which in
turn has been associated with decreased ﬁrst peak KAM yet increased knee joint loading [47]. Supporting
this, Koblauch et al. (2013) [49] suggested that increased knee ﬂexion angles might contribute to increased
joint loading. Thus, based on the presented ﬁndings, it was proposed that following weight loss and weight
loss maintenance while walking at standardized walking speeds the included participants adapted to a gait
pattern including decreased quadriceps engagement causing less loading of the knee joint during late stance
and increased quadriceps engagement during early stance causing increased loading of the knee and that the
decreased quadriceps engagement during late stance was a way to unload the knee during forward propulsion.
The gait strategy of unloading the knee during forward progression was also reﬂected in the hip and ankle
through increased hip ﬂexor moment and decreased peak plantar ﬂexor moment.

4.2
Weight gain experiment
The purpose of the weight gain experiment was to investigate whether the mechanical changes observed
following weight loss were related to mere loss in body weight by investigating associations between gait
biomechanics and alterations in BW following weight loss and experimental weight gain. In addition to this,
it was also decided to investigate whether the observed alterations following weight loss were directly re-
versible following experimental weight gain by investigating the alterations in gait biomehcanics throughout
the weight gain experiment. Section 4.2.1 discuss the associations between gait biomechanics and alterations
in BW following weight loss and experimental weight gain whereas Section 4.2.2 discusses the alterations in
gait biomehcanics throughout the weight gain experiment.
For the weight gain experiment, it was hypothesized that the relationship between alterations in ﬁrst peak
KAM and alteration in BW would be signiﬁcant and with identical slope magnitudes when comparing weight
loss (week 0-16) with experimental weight gain (week 16-16+). This hypothesis was outlined based on the
assumption that alterations in ﬁrst peak KAM following alterations in BW was related to mere BW. Based
on the presented results, a signiﬁcant association was not found between changes in ﬁrst peak KAM and
changes in BW neither following weight loss or experimental weight gain. As such, the hypothesis related to
the weight gain experiment was also rejected.
4.2.1
Associating gait biomechanics with BW
Recalling the ﬁndings from the weight loss experiment, it was observed that ﬁrst peak KAM did not alter
systemically in time with BW following weight loss, thus indicating that alterations in ﬁrst peak KAM was
not solely related to the loss of BW. This proposition was conﬁrmed by the results from the weight gain
experiment, where P-values of the linear regression models related to ﬁrst peak KAM were insigniﬁcant both
following weight loss (week 0-16) and experimental weight gain (week 16-16+). As such, alterations in ﬁrst
peak KAM following alterations in BW were not related to mere BW. Instead it was proposed that the
response was a combination of changes in the neuromuscular coordination and loss in BW.
As for the secondary outcome variables, there was accordingly observed no signiﬁcant association between
alterations in any of the variables and alterations in BW following weight loss (week 0-16). Thus emphasising
the previous proposition, that when obese individuals with knee OA loose BW, it causes adaptations of their
neuromuscular coordination during gait. However, following experimental weight gain, there was observed
30
==PAGE:39==
a positive association between alterations in the hip extensor moment and alterations in BW and between
alterations in the peak plantar ﬂexor moment and alterations in BW. Thus indicating, that when applying
an acute load in obese individuals with knee OA following a weight loss, the participants increased their peak
plantar ﬂexor moment and peak hip extensor moment in accordance with the magnitude of the load applied.
4.2.2
Alterations in gait biomechanics during the weight gain experiment
Based on the weight loss experimental and the initial part of the weight gain experiment, it was proposed
that alterations in gait biomechanics following alterations in BW in obese individuals with knee OA, was
due to a combined response from loss in BW and adaptations in the neuromuscular coordination strategy.
As such, investigating the alterations in gait biomehcanics throughout the weight gain experiment does not
provide additional useful information on whether biomechanical alterations following weight loss is related to
mere BW. However, it can provide information on how acute loading of the body inﬂuence gait biomechanics
in individuals with knee OA following a substantial weight loss. The following paragraphs looked into this
topic by comparing the mechanical alterations following weight loss (week 0-16) with mechanical alterations
following experimental weight gain(16-16+).
Week 0-16 vs. Week 16-16+
From the previous evaluation of the alterations in gait biomechanics following weight loss (week 0-16), it was
suggested that the participants adapted to a gait pattern including more loading of the knee during early
stance by increasing the knee extensor moment whereas during late stance, they applied a gait strategy where
forward propulsion in the pre-swing phase was primarily driven by the ankle and hip.
Following the experimental weight gain (week 16-16+) there was observed increased magnitudes in all frontal
and sagittal kinetic outcome variables except for the peak knee ﬂexor moment. In addition, both the knee
ﬂexion excursion and the hip excursion also increased signiﬁcantly, whereas the ankle excursion and the foot
progression angle was not a↵ected by the experimental weight gain.
Comparing the results from the weight loss (week 0-16) with the experimental weight gain (week 16-16+),
it was observed that none of the variables which altered following weight loss were reversed following the
experimental weight gain. Opposite, all the variables altered progressively following the experimental weight
gain. From the investigation of the associations between alterations in gait mechanics and alterations in
BW following experimental weight gain, it was recalled, that the peak hip extensor moment and the peak
plantar ﬂexor moment was positively associated with the BW, whereas none of the other variables showed a
31
==PAGE:40==
signiﬁcant association. As such, it was assumed that the increased ﬁrst peak KAM, ﬁrst peak hip abduction
moment, peak knee extensor moment and peak hip ﬂexor moment to some extend was described by increased
dynamics of the respective joints. Accordingly, when observing the included kinematic measures following
experimental weight gain, there was observed increases in both the knee ﬂexion excursion and hip excur-
sion, thus indicating that the participants increased their ROM in respective the joint following experimental
weight gain. As the study did not include measures of frontal knee and hip angles, it was not possible to
conﬁrm our assumption related to these parameters. In addition, it was assumed that knee dynamics did not
increase during late stance, as the peak knee ﬂexion moment did not change following experimental weight
gain.
Nevertheless, the increased hip and knee dynamics following acute loading of the participants was proposed
to be an expression of improved functionality following the weight loss seen from the perspective that the par-
ticipants were able to ambulate less restricted using more degrees of freedom following experimental weight
gain compared to baseline measurements, where they ambulated at the same load. However despite this, the
participants still avoided using the knee for pushing o↵the ground during pre-swing phase.
Previous longitudinal studies reported that weight gain has been associated with worsening in function re-
lated pain in individuals with knee OA [55, 56]. As such, it was intuitively expected that the experimental
weight gain would limit participants mechanically and that the experimental weight gain would reverse the
biomechanical adaptations which were observed following the weight loss. However, as this was not the case,
the results from the experimental weight gain may suggest that the weight loss signiﬁcantly improved the
functionality of the participants to an extend that enabled them to use a gait strategy including more degrees
of freedom when handling external loads on the body. However, it should be noted, that the proposition of
improved functionality following weight loss can not be veriﬁed based on gait mechanics only. To support
this proposition, functional assessment should have been conducted using specialized functional assessment
tools.

Conclusion
From the present investigation, it was discovered that following the weight loss experiment, which initially
included a substantial weight loss over 16 weeks and subsequently a slight but signiﬁcant weight gain over a
weight loss maintenance period of 52 weeks, and while ambulating at standardized walking speed, ﬁrst peak
knee adduction moment did not alter systemically with the body weight throughout the experiment. There-
fore, it was suggested that the participants must have altered their gait strategy in response to the weight loss
causing the unexpected ﬁnding reported of ﬁrst peak KAM. Despite ﬁrst peak knee adduction moment not
altering systemically in magnitude with the body weight throughout the study, it was observed at the end of
the study that ﬁrst peak knee adduction was signiﬁcantly reduced when compared to baseline. Analysis of
data from an experimental weight gain experiment, showed that there were no signiﬁcant association between
alterations in ﬁrst peak knee adduction moment and alterations in body weight. As such, the results from
the weight gain experiment conﬁrmed the proposition from the weight loss experiment. Therefore, it was
concluded that alteration in ﬁrst peak knee adduction moment following alteration in BW was not related
to mere BW and that alteration in ﬁrst peak knee adduction moment following alterations in BW caused
changes in the neuromuscular coordination strategy during gait.
Investigating the secondary outcome variables brought insight into how the participants reorganized their gait
pattern in response to alterations in body weight. At the end of the weight loss experiment, the participants
altered their gait strategy during forward propulsion in late stance by increasing their dynamics of the hip
and decreasing their knee ﬂexor moment and their ankle drive. A pattern which has been associated with
unloading of the knee during forward propulsion by using the hip ﬂexors to pull the limb o↵the ground
rather than using the ankle and knee to push for forward progression. Contrary, during weight acceptance
in early stance, the participants altered their gait strategy by increasing their peak knee extensor moment.
A strategy which in turn has been associated with increased load on the knee.
For the weight gain experiment, it was additionally observed that following weight loss, none of the explana-
tory variables were associated with alteration in BW. However following experimental weight gain, it was
33
==PAGE:42==
observed that the peak hip extensor moment and the peak plantar ﬂexor moment were positively associated
with alterations in body weight.
Finally, it was investigated whether the mechanical alteration observed following weight loss were reversed
when applying external load on the body following a substantial weight loss. Contrary to expectations, the
participants increased their gait dynamics following experimental gain by increasing all kinetic variables ex-
cept for the peak knee ﬂexor moment which did not alter signiﬁcantly following experimental weight gain.
Recalling, that there was found positive associations between alteration in the peak hip extensor moment
and peak plantar ﬂexor moment and alterations in body weight, the dynamics might not have increased as
much for these parameters. Nevertheless, due to the increase in the remaining gait variables it was suggested
that a probable explanation to these ﬁndings was that the weight loss might have improved the functionality
of the participants to an extent that enabled them to use a gait strategy including more degrees of freedom
when handling external loads on the body.
5.1
Limitations of the study
The present investigation entailed a far-reaching investigation of the inﬂuence of BW and BW changes on gait
biomehcanics in obese individuals with knee OA. Despite this, the study was subject to several limitations:
The present investigation was conducted at standardized walking speed. This decision was based on the fact
that previous weight loss studies in individuals with knee OA reported increases in walking speed following
weight loss, which in turn has been associated with alteration in gait biomehcanics [15, 32, 33]. As such,
the ﬁxed walking speed was chosen in order to identify alteration in the gait strategy which was related
to the BW and not to the walking speed. However, ﬁxing the walking speed might also induce bias. For
the present investigation, the participants ambulated at 4.5 km/h. In the study by Aaboe et al. (2011) [8]
including the same participants, the participants ambulated at self selected walking speeds. At baseline, the
average walking speed was 4 km/h whereas following weight loss at week 16, the average walking speed was
4.2 km/h. As such, the predetermined walking speed of the present thesis was higher than the participants’
preferred walking speed both at baseline and following weight loss at week 16. Thus the participants were
forced to walk faster than their preferred walking speed which might induce compensatory gait strategies to
meet the demands of the walking speed. However, as the present thesis investigated relative alterations in
gait variables over time any potential inﬂuence of compensatory gait strategy caused by the walking speed
was assumed not to be of signiﬁcant character.
34
==PAGE:43==
Considering the choice of outcome variables; The present thesis sought to describe the inﬂuence BW on gait
biomechanics of both the ankle, knee and hip in individuals with knee OA as previous weight loss studies
only described the inﬂuence of weight loss on knee mechanics in individuals with knee OA. It was desired to
get exhaustive insight into the alterations in gait biomechanics following alterations in BW while keeping the
variable selection as simple as possible. Choosing the ankle and knee ﬂexion excursion and the hip ﬂexion/ex-
tension excursion to describe the motion for each of the three joints in the sagittal plane was an attempt to
reduce the amount of variables included. However for the purpose of contributing to the interpretation of
the sagittal joint moments, these particular values seemed a bit too simple as it entailed several assumption
on how the the joint angles were inﬂuenced by BW alterations.
Further, the present thesis investigated gait alteration over various time spans and following several di↵erent
interventions. As such, the proportions of the study are quite large which made it diﬃcult to investigate all
aspects of the topic in depth. However, this was also considered as a strength as the proportions of the study
also brought up nuances of the inﬂuence of BW and alterations in BW on gait biomechanics which had not
been possible to discover in other ways.
Lastly, the present thesis applied within subject averaging in the data processing. The application of within
subject averaging might also induce bias as the averaged time course patterns resulting from within subject
averaging does not represent an actual physiological response for the participants. However, the potential
deviation was assumed not to a↵ect the main conclusions. Using a statistically more sophisticated method
for analysis such as the mixed linear model would have provided a more precise result. The mixed linear
model includes all observations in the statistical model and has the ability to include covariates. However,
for the purpose of the present thesis it was decided that the application of simple statistical methods and
within subject averaging were suﬃcient.
5.2
Future studies
The present investigation sought to investigate the role of BW and BW changes on ankle, knee and hip
biomechanics in obese patients with knee OA while walking at standardized walking speed. Due to the limi-
tations presented in the previous section (Section 5.1), suggestions for future research features an exhaustive
investigation of lower limb peak joint angles and joint moments both during late stance and early stance and
both in the frontal and sagittal plane. In addition, inclusion of measures of step width and medial to lateral
trunk sway could also be desired for such an investigation.
35
==PAGE:44==
From a clinical point of view, suggestions for future research includes investigations of functional assess-
ment and evaluation of knee joint pain following the weight loss maintenance period. Ideally, as weight loss
maintenance how proven very diﬃcult [57, 58], functional and symptomatic assessment could be desired fol-
lowing even longer periods of weight loss maintenance to evaluate the clinical impact of weight loss treatment.

A.1
Matlab Data Processing
The study included 81 subjects in the analysis. Each subject conducted gait analysis at week 0, 16, 16+ and
68. At each visit, the participants had 6 successful trials. Based on this data structure, the present data
processing was divided as following. For each combinations of plane (sagittal, frontal), joint (ankle, knee,
hip) and variable type (angle, moment) included in the analysis (E.g. frontal knee joint moment, sagittal
hip angle etc.) there was generated a script for each visit (denoted visit script). The processing of the data
within each visit script was as follows: Initially, the code ran a for loop going through the ID numbers of
the participants and loading each participant’s data ﬁles for that visit. Based on the name of the data ﬁle,
the code localized whether to extract data from the left or right leg and subsequently extracted the data
of interest and trimmed the data into ranging during stance of the diseased leg only. Subsequently, the
extracted data were time normalized and within subject averaging was conducted by calculating the average
of the available successful trials. Note that the participants were not excluded if they had less than 6 suc-
cessful trials. Based on the within subject averaged curve, the desired peak value or parameter(s) was/were
extracted and inserted into a Mx81 matrix, where M denoted the number of parameters extracted. E.g. for
the frontal knee joint moment, the desired variable of interest was ﬁrst peak KAM (maximum peak value
during early stance). Thus for ﬁrst peak KAM, the data processing resulted in a 1x81 vector including a ﬁrst
peak KAM value for each participant. In each script, data quality was subsequently checked by plotting the
within subject averaged curve along with the extracted parameter(s). After the data were visually inspected,
the vector/matrix including the parameter(s) of interest for each participant was/were saved in a mat ﬁle. A
similar script was conducted for the remaining visits. Thus for the frontal knee joint moment, this resulted
in 4 visit scripts from week 0, 16, 16+ and 68 respectively. Subsequently, a main script was constructed.
The main script initially loaded the mat ﬁles which was saved in the end of each visit script. From these
data, delta values were calculated: ’week0-week16’, ’week16-68’ etc. Subsequently, linear regression analysis
was conducted and plotted. The main script then included some plotting for data quality inspection and
calculation of descriptive statistics. Lastly, the data was saved in an Excel sheet where the last statistics

were calculated.
