Abstract
This thesis investigates the use of Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) to determine
the modal parameters of an offshore wind turbine (OWT). Using comprehensive
datasets of acceleration measurements collected from an operational wind tur­
bine, the study identifies critical modal parameters such as natural frequencies,
damping ratios, and mode shapes.
Automated techniques have been developed and applied to manage and analyze
large datasets. The study found that, while OMA is highly effective in identifying
lower modes, it faces challenges with higher­order modes because of their lower
energy levels, complex mode shapes, or lack of excitation. Correlations between
modal parameters and environmental and operational conditions, such as wind
speed and rotor speed, have been explored, providing insights into the turbine’s
behavior under various operating conditions.
The discussion throughout this report highlights the automation process, which
facilitates efficient data handling and analysis, enabling continuous monitoring and
assessment of the wind turbine’s dynamic behavior. This also includes the use of
a predictive model to better understand the response of the turbine under various
conditions.
The findings emphasize the potential of OMA in the offshore wind energy sec­
tor, despite the limitations encountered with higher­order modes. Overall, this
project contributes to future efforts in the estimation, identification, and predic­
tion of modal parameters for OWTs, emphasizing the importance and benefit of
automated data analysis methods and continuous monitoring systems within this
field. These advancements contribute to enhanced structural health monitoring,
potentially extending the operational lifetime of wind turbines, and ensuring their
reliable and efficient performance under varying environmental conditions.

Introduction
Offshore wind energy has emerged as a significant contributor in the transition
toward renewable energy sources and providing sustainable energy in the future.
Offshore conditions, such as strong and predictable wind patterns, offer substan­
tial advantages and potential in this transition. However, the complex dynamic
behavior of offshore wind turbines (OWTs) poses significant challenges for modal
analysis and structural health monitoring. In addition, the unique environmental
and operational challenges faced by these turbines introduce the need for ad­
vanced methods to monitor and analyze their structural dynamics to ensure relia­
bility and longevity throughout their lifetime.
During this thesis, different Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) algorithms are ap­
plied to determine the modal parameters of a monopile­supported OWT under real
environmental and operational conditions. These parameters, including natural
frequencies, damping ratios, and mode shapes, are critical for understanding the
dynamic behavior of OWT structures and their response to ambient forces. Accu­
rate identification of modal parameters enables better prediction of the turbine’s
performance under varying conditions, essential for optimizing its performance
and maintenance strategies.
The OWT assessed in this study is similar to the one shown in Figure 1.1 and is
part of a Vattenfall Test Center. The data used for the OMA comes from acceler­
ation measurements recorded throughout the entire year of 2022 and is gathered
from accelerometers at three different sensor locations on the OWT. These mea­
surements encompass all operational phases of the turbine, including periods of
operation, standstill, and idle conditions.
The data is segmented into 10­minute timeframes, which corresponds to industry
standards and allows for detailed analysis and efficient data management. Ad­
ditionally, Vattenfall provides information about the environmental conditions and
operational parameters at the start of each timeframe to complement the acceler­
ation data.

1.1
Main focus of the thesis
At the start of the project, different research questions and approaches to the
thesis were considered, after which it was decided to mainly focus on
How to accurately, and with high certainty, cluster identified poles and categorize
clusters as modes when modal parameters change as a function of operational
and environmental conditions.
This focus includes long­term tracking of modal parameters through an automated
analysis and investigation of their correlations with environmental and operational
conditions, such as wind and rotor speed. This topic also introduces the need
for a predictive model, aiming to forecast how the system’s response is affected
by and varies with these conditions over time. Other approaches, such as virtual
sensing and using modal parameters for change detection, were also discussed,
but it was decided to leave these for future projects and studies.

1.2
Literature review
In line with the increasing demand for renewable energy sources and the develop­
ment of offshore wind energy, research within this field is rapidly expanding. Nu­
merous of these studies focus on the application of OMA techniques to accurately
identify modal parameters for OWTs. Additionally, there is significant interest in
virtual sensing and structural health monitoring, aiming to enhance the reliability
and longevity of OWTs.
In [1], Toftekær et al. address the growing need for innovative methods to ex­
tend the lifetime of OWTs. They introduce a novel virtual sensing method for
indirectly measuring stress states in monopile­supported OWTs and use different
OMA techniques to identify the first two natural frequencies of the turbine. Their
work is applied to an OWT similar to the one in this study and has therefore been
a source of inspiration for the research in this report.
The challenges posed by environmental and operational conditions on OWTs are
presented by Weijtjens et al. in [2]. They describe a methodology that includes
data collection using accelerometers, automated OMA to identify resonance fre­
quencies, and a case­by­case tracking strategy to monitor structural changes.
Their paper was important in the implementation of the predictive analysis con­
ducted in this report.
A review of different OMA techniques for in­service modal identification is con­
ducted by Zahid et al. [3], which offers valuable insights into the pros and cons
of these methods and how they compare to the more traditional experimental
modal analysis. This paper is a recommended read for those who wish to gain an
overview of methods that can be used to address vibrational problems by under­
standing the dynamic characteristics of structures.
Aside from the many interesting research articles and papers on OMA and off­
shore wind turbines, the book Introduction To Operational Modal Analysis by Rune
Brincker and Carlos E. Ventura [4] provides the essential theoretical background
about OMA and presents practical examples of its application. It has been used
extensively throughout this report. The same can be said for the book Operational
Modal Analysis of Civil Engineering Structures by Carlo Rainieri and Giovanni
Fabbrocino [5]. Lastly, the master thesis Data­driven model updating for OWTs
by Panagiotis Vlachopoulos [6] presents a comprehensive approach to identifying
the modal parameters of an OWT using different OMA techniques.

1.3
Outline of the report
This thesis is structured into seven chapters, intended to provide the reader with
the necessary theory, information, discussions, and assessments that have led to

Operational Modal Analysis
Operational modal analysis is an emerging tool in the field of structural dynam­
ics, particularly for operational structures such as wind turbines, bridges, high rise
buildings, and towers. Unlike traditional modal analysis methods such as exper­
imental modal analysis, which require controlled excitation, OMA relies on the
response of the structure under ambient (operational) excitation to identify its dy­
namic properties. The method is non­intrusive and ideal for structures that are
difficult to excite artificially, or where operational conditions are of primary interest
[4, p.1­15].
The fundamental principle of OMA is the examination of output­only data from a
structure to determine its modal parameters, including natural frequencies, damp­
ing ratios, and mode shapes. This analytical method treats ambient excitation as
a stochastic process, which can be approximated as white noise [4, p.1­3]. The
assumption is that the excitation spans a wide frequency spectrum, thereby ef­
fectively engaging all the structural modes of interest. Although exact white noise
characteristics are not strictly required, it is essential to ensure a comprehensive
excitation of relevant modes to facilitate their detection within the measured data.
After collecting raw data, this is subjected to signal processing, where it is pre­
pared for further analysis.
Identification techniques in both the time and the frequency domain can be applied
to the response data in order to obtain the system’s modal parameters from the
output­only measurements. The modal parameters are identified even when the
excitation deviates from the ideal white noise, which is a common scenario for
real structures in operational environments. The final phase in the OMA process
is the validation of the modal parameters. This is typically done by comparing the
identified parameters to the expected behavior of the structure, obtained from e.g.
a FEM­model or design values.
2.1
Signal Processing
Signal processing is integral to the comprehensive analysis and interpretation of
the dynamic behavior of structures, such as OWTs, through OMA. Initially, modal
information is hidden in the chaos of randomness of measured data. Through
signal processing, the modal parameters of importance become visible [4, p.201].

2.1.1
Random Vibrations and Signals
Random vibrations are non­deterministic signals whose future behaviour can not
be precisely predicted. In OWTs, these vibrations result from a variety of natu­
ral sources such as wind, waves, and rotor rotation. The random vibrations are
characterized in terms of statistical properties such as mean, variance, autocor­
relation, and power spectral densities (PSDs).
2.1.2
Transforms
Signals are typically recorded as accelerations or strains in the time domain, pri­
marily due to the temporal nature of the natural and operational responses of struc­
tures, such as vibrations and structural movements. These responses evolve as a
structure interacts with environmental forces such as wind and waves. Capturing
these signals in the time domain enables direct observation and analysis of their
dynamics and variations, providing an intuitive and straightforward representation
of the turbine’s behavior over specified periods.
To perform a more in­depth analysis, time domain signals are transformed into the
frequency domain using a mathematical technique known as the Fourier Trans­
form. This process decomposes a time domain signal into its constituent fre­
quency components. The primary purpose of this transformation is to reveal the
spectral characteristics of the signals, which are not readily apparent in the time
domain representation. Analyzing the frequency domain allows for the identifica­
tion of specific frequencies at which the turbine exhibits resonant behavior. It also
enables the assessment of the energy distribution in different frequency bands
and the detection of periodic or harmonic components that may indicate regular
operational patterns or potential structural issues. The theoretical framework for
this analysis is primarily based on [4, p.53­63].

2.1.3
Free Decays
In OMA, free decays are essential for extracting modal parameters. This sec­
tion provides a comprehensive overview of the principles and applications of free
decays.
Definition and Basic Principles
Free decay refers to the natural response of a system to an initial disturbance in
the absence of external forces. Mathematically, it represents the solution to the
homogeneous form of the system’s differential equation. For a single­degree­of­
freedom (SDOF) system, the equation of motion (EoM) can be expressed as
m¨y + c ˙y + ky = 0
(2.22)
where m is the mass, c is the damping coefficient, k is the stiffness, y is the dis­
placement, ˙y is the velocity, and ¨y is the acceleration. Solving this equation yields
the free decay response, characterized by exponentially decaying sinusoidal func­
tions for underdamped systems [4, p.83­87].
State Space Representation
Free decay responses can be effectively analyzed using state­space methods.
The state space formulation represents the system dynamics through a set of
first­order differential equations. For a discrete time system, the free response
solution y(k) is given by
y(k) = CAku0
(2.23)
where C is the observation matrix, u0 are the initial state conditions [4, p.118], and
A is the discrete time system matrix expressed as
A = exp(D∆t)
(2.24)
where D is the continuous time system matrix [4, p.251].
Operational Modal Analysis Using Free Decays
In OMA, free decays are used to identify the modal parameters of the system.
The response data from free decays can be organized into matrices that facilitate
modal parameter estimation. For instance, Hankel matrices can be constructed
13

June 28, 2024
from free decay measurements to derive the observability and controllability matri­
ces. These are crucial for identifying the modes and damping ratios of the system
[4, p.251­258].

2.1.7
Link to OMA
The signal processing techniques discussed in Section 2.1 are fundamental in
OMA, enabling the identification and analysis of dynamic properties of structures
from ambient excitations. Key parameters such as mean, variance, and auto­
correlation functions facilitate the extraction of modal parameters such as natural
frequencies, damping ratios, and mode shapes.
Transforms, particularly the Fourier Transform, convert time domain signals into
the frequency domain, revealing natural vibration frequencies. This is essential for
analyzing ambient vibrations and extracting modal parameters such as identifying
dominant frequencies corresponding to the structure’s modes.
CFs are essential for estimating autocorrelation and cross correlation of measured
signals. These functions enable the separation of signal components correspond­
ing to different modes, facilitating accurate identification of dynamic characteris­
tics.
SD functions, including auto spectral and cross spectral densities, represent the
distribution of signal power over frequency. This analysis helps identify natural
frequencies and associated mode shapes, with integration over specific frequency
bands isolating individual mode contributions.

2.2
Time Domain Methods
In the time domain, the modal parameters are estimated by analyzing the time
series of response measurements directly. It involves handling the free decays of
the system, closely related to the correlation functions described in Section 2.1.4.
This implies that all modes present in the signal can be observed at any time
throughout the observed free decay [4, p.239].
2.2.1
Identification of the state space model
In this study’s time domain analysis of the OWT, the Stochastic Subspace Iden­
tification (SSI) method is employed as the principal analytical tool, which uses
measured data to directly identify an effective state space model for complex dy­
namic systems [3].
In the context of structural dynamics, the EoM for a multi­degree­of­freedom (MDOF)
system describes the behavior of a linear dynamic system as
M¨q(t) + C ˙q(t) + Kq(t) = f(t)
(2.42)
with M, C, K representing the mass, damping, and stiffness matrices, and ¨q(t),
˙q(t) and q(t) as the acceleration, velocity, and displacement vectors at continuous
time t. The external force vector is given as f(t) [7][5, p.105].
Although (2.42) gives a solid representation of a vibrating structure, [7] presents
certain drawbacks relating to its application in experimental modeling. Measuring
all the degrees of freedom (DOFs) in a finite element model is not possible and is
also, in most cases, unnecessary. Furthermore, the EoM is formulated in contin­
uous time, while measurements are typically collected in discrete time samples.
Additionally, real­life data collection scenarios frequently involve measurement
noise and unknown excitation sources beyond the external force vector f(t).
The EoM is therefore converted to a discrete­time stochastic state space model
through model reduction, sampling, and modeling of noise
xk+1 = Axk + wk
yk = Cxk + vk
(2.43)
where yk is the measurements, xk is the discrete state vector, wk is the process
noise, vk is the measurement noise and k describes the time constant. The state
transition matrix A describes the dynamic behavior of the system through its eigen­
values, and C is an output matrix defining the transformation of internal states to
19
==PAGE:36==
June 28, 2024
the outside world [7]. The modal parameters are obtained from these two matri­
ces.

2.3
Frequency Domain Methods
Frequency domain methods are another way of estimating modal parameters in
OMA. Frequency domain methods are widely based on the relation between input
and output PSDs [3] and the physical information is obtained from the SD func­
tions, which are previously described in Section 2.1.6, as opposed to the time
domain which is based on CFs [4, p.262].
There are several different identification techniques in the frequency domain, and
in this section, the focus will be on the Basic Frequency Domain (BFD) Method
and Frequency Domain Decomposition (FDD).
An important distinction between the time and frequency domain is that free re­
sponses are present over the entire considered period in the time domain, whilst
in the frequency domain each mode has specific frequency bands where modes
are dominating. This gives the advantage of a so­called “natural modal decompo­
sition” where it is easy to consider the frequency bands in which different modes
dominate. The FDD is not directly based on natural modal decomposition, but the
technique utilizes the fact that the modes outside of the smaller frequency bands
will have less influence on the results. A downside of working in the frequency do­
main is that spectral density estimates may be affected by bias, making it harder
to justify the modal parameter estimations [4, p.261].
2.3.1
Basic Frequency Domain Method
The BFD method, commonly known as the peak­picking method, is one of OMA’s
simplest and most widely used techniques for modal identification. This method
involves identifying the natural frequencies of a structure by observing the peaks
in the PSD plots of the response measurements. When a structure is subjected to
ambient excitations, it exhibits strong responses at its natural frequencies, which
appear as prominent peaks in the PSD plots [5, p.127­130].
Principle and Application
The BFD method assumes that around each resonance, only one mode is domi­
nant, making it essentially an SDOF approach. This assumption allows the output
PSD matrix to be approximated by the contribution of the dominant mode alone.
Practically, the process begins by computing the auto spectra and cross spectra
of the response signals. The trace of the PSD matrix, representing the sum of
the auto spectra at each frequency is used to identify the frequency peaks that
correspond to the natural frequencies of the structure [5, p.127].
23
==PAGE:40==
June 28, 2024
Practical Implementation
One notable implementation of the BFD method involves a procedure for the effi­
cient identification of natural frequencies using averaged normalized PSDs. This
approach involves calculating the coherence function between response signals,
which helps to confirm the identified natural frequencies. The mode shapes are
then estimated using the values of the transfer functions at these natural frequen­
cies, treating the ratio of responses at different measurement points relative to a
reference sensor as the mode shape components [5, p.127­130].
Strengths and Limitations
The BFD method is particularly advantageous due to its simplicity and computa­
tional efficiency, making it suitable for quick preliminary analysis. However, it has
limitations, especially when dealing with closely spaced modes or modes with sig­
nificant damping. In such cases, the method might identify operational deflection
shapes instead of true mode shapes, since the peaks in the PSD may represent
a combination of multiple modes rather than a single mode’s response [5, p.129].

Modal Assurance Criterion
The Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC) was first introduced by Randall J. Allemang
and is defined as ”a scalar constant relating the degree of consistency (linearity)
between one modal and another reference modal vector” [11]. It is thereby a
way to compare two mode shape vectors to assess their resemblance. The MAC
is applicable for comparing identified mode shapes with reference mode shapes
and assessing the similarity between mode shape vectors obtained from different
OMA identification techniques. To see the correlation between two mode shape

noted in [11] there could be several reasons behind these results. Therefore, it
is important to understand that the MAC value should be used solely to indicate
consistency, and not to validate the mode shapes or determine their orthogonality.
2.5
Limitations of OMA
OMA is an effective method for analyzing a structure’s response to ambient ex­
citations and estimating its modal parameters. However, the methodology has
certain constraints and limitations in its applications which may, if not handled
correctly, affect the precision of the modal identification process. The two main
limitations of OMA are its inability to mass­scale mode shapes and the potential
of under­excitation at certain modes, as presented in [4, p.10].

2.5.1
Scaling of Mode Shapes
For modal parameters, such as eigenfrequencies and mode shapes, to accurately
contribute to structural response simulation, mass­normalized mode shapes are
necessary, as they allow for direct interpretation of the modes. Obtaining these
normalized mode shapes requires knowledge of the scaling factors for the modes
[12, 13]. OMA is particularly relevant for structures exposed to ambient excita­

forces. This means that the initial mode shapes identified from the response data
are un­scaled. It is therefore necessary to find the scaling factor so that the modes
can be mass­normalized.

2.5.2
Lack of Excitation
The nature of ambient excitations may cause under­excitation for certain modes,
signifying the second significant limitation of OMA. Since the excitation forces
27
==PAGE:44==
June 28, 2024
used in OMA arise from ambient sources such as wind, waves, traffic, and seis­
mic activity, they are not measured, making it hard to control their spectra and
spatial locations. This can cause situations where not all the vibration modes of
the structure are sufficiently excited by the ambient conditions, thus making them
hard to identify from the response data [4, p.10]. This affects the accuracy of the
modal parameter identification and may lead to certain modes being undetected
or wrongfully characterized.
2.5.3
Additional uncertainties
In addition, other factors may also influence the accuracy and validity of the re­
sults. The ambient vibrations that OMA relies on are sensitive to unwanted sig­
nals, or random errors, that interfere with the measurement and analysis of the
structural response. These errors may result from environmental (e.g. wind and
temperature fluctuations) and operational (e.g. machinery or human and traffic
activity) conditions, or simply arise from the measurement systems themselves
as electronic noise [4, p.10].
2.6
Bayesian Predictive Analysis
Bayesian predictive analysis is based on Bayesian probability theory, which pro­
vides a mathematical framework for updating the probability of a hypothesis as
more evidence becomes available. Bayesian theory incorporates prior beliefs and
updates these beliefs with new data to form a posterior distribution.
Bayesian inference involves three main components [17, p.96­105]: the prior dis­
tribution, which represents the initial beliefs about a parameter before observing
any data; the likelihood function, which represents the probability of the observed
data given a specific value of the parameter; and the posterior distribution, which
represents the updated beliefs about the parameter after considering the new
data. This is calculated using Bayes’ theorem, which can be mathematically ex­
pressed as:
P(θ|D) = P(D|θ) · P(θ)
P(D)
(2.65)
where P(θ|D) is the posterior distribution, P(D|θ) is the likelihood, P(θ) is the prior
distribution, and P(D) is the marginal likelihood or evidence [17, p.14­16].
In machine learning, Bayesian predictive analysis is used to forecast future out­
comes based on probabilistic models. This approach is particularly useful in situ­
ations where uncertainty and variability are significant. Bayesian methods
28
==PAGE:45==
June 28, 2024
provide a natural way to quantify uncertainty and make predictions that incorporate
all available information. The key steps in Bayesian predictive analysis include
model specification, posterior inference, and prediction. Model specification in­
volves defining a probabilistic model that describes how the data are generated,
including specifying prior distributions for all model parameters. Posterior infer­
ence uses observed data to update the prior beliefs and compute the posterior
distribution of the model parameters. Prediction uses the posterior distribution to
make predictions about future observations, involving integrating over the poste­
rior distribution to account for uncertainty in the parameter estimates.
In the context of this thesis, Bayesian predictive analysis is used to understand
the behavior of the OWT under various environmental and operational conditions.
The model is trained using the available data, where the natural frequencies are
regressed against defined predictor variables. Once trained, the model can pre­
dict natural frequencies for new sets of predictor variables, providing probabilistic
predictions that offer a measure of uncertainty.

4.1.4
Labeling of modes
Normally, the damping ratios of the modes can be used to distinguish between
the FA and SS directions, as the damping ratio in the FA direction is typically
significantly larger than in the SS direction. The difference in damping ratios is
expected to be considerably lower in standstill conditions compared to operational
conditions, as the contribution of aerodynamic damping, which normally comes
from the rotating blades, is greatly reduced [21, p.6]. As a consequence damping
in the FA direction becomes very small. The minimal differences in damping ratios
make it difficult to label the modes as FA and SS based on this criterion, and this
approach is therefore avoided.
An attempt is made to separate and label the modes based on the mode shape
vector of each cluster and their corresponding MAC values relative to the ref­
erence mode shapes, as presented in Section 3.3. High MAC values, typically

MAC values can only be used to prove consistency between two sets of mode

5
Long­term tracking and
estimation of modal parameters
In Chapter 4 it was demonstrated that the OMA toolbox captures the first two
bending modes for single time frames with high certainty. However, the labeling
and separation of these two modes into the FA and SS directions, along with their
corresponding modal parameters, require further investigation.
In this chapter, modal parameter estimation will be conducted using larger datasets
as input. Acceleration data spanning entire months will be analyzed. This ap­
proach facilitates not only the estimation and long­term monitoring of the system’s
modal parameters but also the observation of variations in these parameters in re­
sponse to environmental and operational conditions over long periods.
To achieve this, the OMA toolbox has been automated to process exstended pe­
riods of data, without any human interaction. Accelerations recorded at each 10­
minute interval within a specified period are used as input. A clustering analysis
is then performed, where frequency bands in which the first and second bending
modes are expected to appear, are defined. The clustering analysis identifies two
clusters in each band, corresponding to the FA and SS directions, respectively.
Modal parameters such as eigenfrequencies, damping ratios, and mode shape
vectors for each cluster are then extracted and saved for further post­processing
and analysis.
To accurately observe trends and variations in the modal parameters over time
the clusters is assigned to a mode and labeled. Similarly to the single time frame
analysis, this is done based on MAC values compared to the reference mode
shapes. The cluster is automatically assigned to the mode and the direction of the
reference mode shape that produces the number closest to 1.0. For the cluster to
be considered as a real mode, the highest calculated MAC value must be greater
than 0.9. Any clusters with values lower than this are disregarded.
A challenge with the automated toolbox occasionally occurs during the clustering
of poles, where the analysis is sensitive to outliers that fall outside the vertically
aligned clusters. In cases where two distinct vertical clusters cannot be detected,
the automated analysis may erroneously select a small number of inconsistent
poles as an independent cluster and extract their associated modal parameters.

Correlation of Modal Parameters
with Environmental and
Operational Conditions
Modal parameters in OWTs vary with environmental and operational conditions.
These factors include wind speed, wave heights, tidal levels, and temperatures,
as well as rotor speed and pitching of blades. This chapter analyzes some of
these factors in relation to the estimated modal parameters over time, aiming to
identify correlations and provide valuable insights into the turbine’s behavior.
This analysis prioritizes the potential effects that wind speed, rotor speed, and
blade pitch angle have on modal parameters. Based on available SCADA data,
it is known that the rotor speed typically increases with higher wind speeds, while
blade pitch adjustments control the rotor speed, preventing it from exceeding the
turbine’s capacity at the highest wind speeds.
This report does not consider the effects of wave heights, as this data is not avail­
able. Additionally, tidal variations are minimal in the area of the OWT and will
not affect the modal parameters significantly. Temperature effects are also disre­
garded, as the OWT is primarily constructed of steel, which is assumed to not be
influenced significantly by temperature changes within such small ranges.

6.3
Predictive analysis of response
A predictive model, based on Bayesian Ridge Regression has been employed to
describe the response of the OWT, using the python module scikit­learn. The ob­
jective of this model is to enable predictive analysis of the natural frequencies and
understand their dependence on and development with various environmental
and operational parameters such as wind speed and rotor speed. By using mea­
sured responses from a range of specific conditions, the model aims to project
how the turbine will perform under future conditions. It can also be used to detect
anomalies and deviations due to extreme conditions. For more information on the
methodology and theory used in the model, the reader is referred to [22].
The model uses the wind speed and the rotor speed as predictor variables X1
and X2 to provide probabilistic estimates Y of the natural frequency. Including
blade pitch angles as an additional predictor variable was also tested, but this
did not significantly enhance the results. The model is trained using previously
estimated natural frequencies as training data. For each measured frequency the
model predicts a frequency based on the underlying information provided by the
predictor variables. These predictions are then validated against a distinct set of
test data to evaluate the model’s performance and reliability.
The model has been trained using two distinct approaches. First, data from a sin­
gle month were utilized. This method captures variations and patterns specific to
that month, facilitating the detection of anomalies and the analysis of short­term
behavior. Secondly, the model has been trained with natural frequencies obtained
from data covering a year’s environmental parameter variations and operational
conditions. This approach captures a wider range of conditions, including sea­
sonal trends and long­term patterns, ensuring that the model is generalized across
different scenarios.
In this report, the predictive analysis is performed for the FA1 frequency, but can
equally be employed for the remaining identified modes.

Table 6.1 shows the summary of the posterior distributions, which describes the
updated beliefs about the frequency based on the measured data. The intercept
of the model is represented by α, which is the expected value of the frequency
when the predictor variables are zero, i.e no contribution from the wind speed or
rotor speed. The β parameters are related to the predictor variables X1 and X2
and describe the expected change in the frequency per one unit change in these.
Finally, σ describes the standard deviation of the residuals from the regression
model, which is the difference between the measured and predicted results. In
this way, the variability in the data which is not explained by the model itself is
captured.

their standard deviation, and HDI is the highest density interval, with HDI 3% and
HDI 97% marking the bounds within which the most probable values occur. In
other words, 94 % of the posterior distribution results appear in this interval.

7.1
Discussion
In the research conducted within this project, OMA is applied to determine the
modal parameters of an OWT during standstill and under various operational con­
ditions, utilizing a full year of acceleration data provided by Vattenfall. To facilitate
computations on large datasets, automated techniques are employed, enabling
the analysis to run without human interaction.
Both frequency domain and time domain techniques are used to analyze the data
and provide modal parameter estimates for the OWT. These include the Basic Fre­
quency Domain Method and Frequency Domain Decomposition in the frequency
domain, as well as DATA­SSI in the time domain.
In the initial phase of the project, data from individual 10­minute time frames is an­
alyzed to gain information about the system’s response and to identify its modes.
Firstly, the turbine is assessed during standstill conditions to isolate the modal pa­
rameters without the influence of the advanced dynamics normally present during
operational conditions. Here, the first tower bending modes are found at 0.218 Hz
and 0.220 Hz in the FA and SS directions. Additionally, the second bending modes
are identified at 1.25 Hz and 1.32 Hz in the FA and SS directions respectively. While
other frequencies are also detected, both in the stabilization diagrams and in the
power spectra, they are attributed to blade interactions, and therefore considered
blade modes, rather than true bending modes of the system. Consequently, they
are not the focus of further analysis.
For the individual analysis of the turbine during operational conditions, a larger dif­
ference between frequencies of the first FA and SS bending modes is observed,
corresponding to 0.231 and 0.223 Hz, respectively. For the second bending mode,
the frequency at 1.32 Hz is linked to the SS direction, and 1.34 Hz is first assumed
to represent the FA direction. Notably, the frequencies identified for the first and

To analyze larger amounts of data and train models based on the results, the OMA
process is automated. This is achieved by introducing a clustering analysis and
tuning it to capture clusters appearing within specific frequency bands. During the
post­processing phase, each cluster is automatically assigned a direction based
on their MAC values relative to predefined reference mode shapes.
The long­term tracking for the standstill period identifies two clusters within the
frequency bands of the first two modes. The automatic labeling proves success­
ful for the first mode, with results consistent with the frequencies found in the
individual analysis. For the second mode, however, the automated process is not
able to provide reliable labeling results. Although the frequencies from the cluster­
ing analysis mostly correspond to the mode 2 frequencies found in the individual
analysis, the labeling into the FA and SS directions appears more arbitrary than
consistent.
The long­term tracking under operational conditions results in two clusters around
the mode 1 frequency, while only one stable and vertically aligned cluster can
be found around the mode 2 frequency. The two clusters assumed to represent
mode 1 are successfully labeled into the FA and SS directions, with the FA mode
generally exhibiting a higher damping ratio. The higher damping in the FA direction
is attributed to aerodynamic damping and is an indication of correct labeling.
In the mode 2 frequency band, the stable clusters are regarded as real modes
and assigned to the SS direction. The clustering analysis also assigns modal
parameters to the FA2 mode, but these are considered the results of scattered
and unstable poles, and therefore disregarded as spurious modes. Although there
could be several reasons for the absence of the FA2 mode, it is assumed that
under­excitation is the primary factor preventing this vibration mode from being
identified in the response data.

A significant part of the long­term analysis involves investigating possible cor­
relations between the modal parameters and the corresponding operational and
environmental conditions at the site of the OWT. This includes a predictive anal­
ysis using a linear regression model, focusing primarily on modal parameters of
the FA1 bending mode.
During the transient state, the influence of rotor speed on the modal parameters
is notable, with an increase in the first natural frequency of the tower around the
3P harmonics of the blades. Aside from this, the correlation between the natural
frequency with the wind and rotor speed is rather constant in the transient state.
However, at higher wind speeds, a negative correlation is observed, with natural
frequencies decreasing as wind speed increases. In these conditions, the ro­
tor reaches a steady state, and the decrease in frequency exhibits a more linear
behavior with rising wind speeds. This is likely attributed to a combination of in­
creased aerodynamic loading and blade pitching. As wind speed increases, so
does the load on the turbine blades. Blade pitching is used to prevent turbine
overcapacity, and likely contributes to the observed negative frequency change.
Conversely, the damping ratio increases during the steady state, before it experi­
ences a notable decrease at the very highest wind and rotor speeds. This effect
is also attributed to the pitching of the turbine blades.
7.2
Conclusion
This project shows the great potential of OMA in the field of modal identification
and structural health monitoring for offshore wind turbines. Using a full year of ac­
celeration data from an operational wind turbine, this research has demonstrated
that OMA techniques are effective in capturing the essential dynamic character­
istics of such structures. The study highlights the ability of OMA to accurately
identify the lower, more energized modes of vibration, which are critical for as­
sessing the structural integrity and operational performance of wind turbines. The
project also underscores the limitations of OMA. While lower modes are captured
with high accuracy, higher­order modes, which are less energized, present more
challenges, and are hard to identify accurately.
At the start of the project, different research questions and approaches to the
thesis were considered, after which it was decided to mainly focus on how to ac­
curately, and with high certainty, cluster identified poles and categorize clusters
as modes when modal parameters change as a function of operational and envi­
ronmental conditions. This focus included long­term tracking of modal parameters
through automated analysis and investigation of their correlations with
91
==PAGE:108==

environmental and operational conditions, such as wind and rotor speed. This
topic also introduced the need for a predictive model, aiming to forecast how the
system’s response is affected by and varies with these conditions over time.
The use of automated techniques in this study proved useful for managing and
analyzing large datasets, enabling efficient and effective extraction of modal pa­
rameters from the clusters of poles. This automation is crucial for real­time struc­
tural health monitoring and predictive maintenance, offering a scalable solution
for the wind energy industry.
Overall, the insights gained from this research contribute to the advancement
of structural health monitoring systems for offshore wind turbines. By improv­
ing the understanding of wind turbine dynamics and providing robust methods for
modal identification, especially automated techniques, this project supports ongo­
ing efforts to optimize the operation and maintenance of OWTs. The findings also
directly address the primary focus of the research by providing comprehensive
methods for clustering and categorizing modal parameters under varying opera­
tional and environmental conditions.
In conclusion, OMA has proven to be a powerful tool for the modal analysis of
offshore wind turbines, particularly for identifying lower modes. Although there
are challenges to be addressed, especially with higher­order modes and complex
operational conditions, the advancements made in this study lay the foundation
for future work regarding the estimation, identification and prediction of modal
parameters for offshore wind turbines.
7.3
Future work
Looking ahead, several key areas deserve further exploration to build on the work
presented in this thesis. Firstly, there is significant potential in expanding the
range of regression models used for predicting modal parameters. Future projects
should introduce additional regression models, rigorously testing and comparing
their performance in various operational conditions. The incorporation of non­
linear models could also improve the model’s performance, potentially facilitating
more efficient capture of harmonic effects.
In addition, it could be useful to extend the analysis to include data from subse­
quent years. By incorporating future data, it can be investigated how predictive
models evolve and whether the initial correlations and patterns observed hold true
over time. This could be used to, e.g. detect damages on the structure.
