Abstract
The aim of this master thesis is the investigation of the fault response of wind turbines in an oﬀshore wind
power plant which is connected to an HVDC link. In this way, severe fault is considered in two diﬀerent
cases. In the ﬁrst case, a severe symmetrical fault is applied at the middle of the cable which connects
the point of common coupling with the oﬀshore substation (step-up transformer of substation and HVDC
converter). In the second case, a severe symmetrical fault is applied inside the wind farm. The main
focus of this master thesis is to secure the stability of the system after fault and the compliance of the
wind power plant to the grid code during fault. After reviewing the literature, the best proposed solution
has been implemented in order to fulﬁll the requirements of this master thesis.
In this regard, WT
and HVDC models have been developed and adapted through this work in Matlab/Simulink. Using the
developed models, simulations have been done in order to observe the impact of the proposed controller
on the system. Finally, the impact of the HVDC converter during severe symmetrical fault has been
investigated.

Chapter 1
Introduction
It has been experienced that during severe fault the wind turbines are not capable of maintaining
the synchronization with the grid or other AC sources which provide voltage-frequency. Those
instabilities occur when the wind turbine is incapable to track the angle of the grid voltage during
severe faults. In this thesis those instabilities during severe fault have been examined and also an
eﬃcient solution for the problem is implemented.
1.1
Literature Review
The massive increase of the installation of wind turbines (WT) in Europe has led to the extension
and to the adaption of the grid codes in a new standard. That happened in order to consider the
large capacity of wind power plants (WPP) [13]. The adaption of the grid codes is needed in order
to avoid unnecessary disconnection of the wind farms (WF) during fault. Disconnection of a WF
can lead to load shedding and even black out of an area if the WF is responsible for supplying high
portion of the loads.
The grid codes have some diﬀerences from country to country. For example, the Nordic grid code
from Nordel has been applied to all WF which are connected to the Nordic system [9] [8]. However,
Denmark is an exception. The WPP are divided in two categories with respect to their voltage
level. Diﬀerent grid codes are applied in WPP which have a voltage level higher than 100 kV
than those which voltage level is lower than that.
If the voltage is below 100 kV the applied
grid code is provided by Eltra and Elkraft [3] while for a voltage level higher than 100 kV the
Danish grid code complies with the requirements which have been analysed in [9]. The common
European goal is to have a common grid code among the European nations which will help the
interconnection between the countries of the continent. The diﬀerent grid codes have also aﬀected
the WT manufacturers who are forced to adjust each project according to the grid code of the
associated country. The main idea of a common grid is the better and more eﬃcient behaviour of
the WT across the countries. [9]
This project focuses on investigating the eﬀects of the severe faults on the system. The voltage
drop dipping around zero is deﬁned as severe fault. Such a voltage dip happens merely during
three phase symmetrical faults in which three phases are shorted with the ground equally. Even
the worst scenario of asymmetrical fault will not dip the voltage more than 0.33 pu and it cannot
cause loss of synchronization in the system [5]. Hence, in the current project only symmetrical
faults have been considered as severe fault. This kind of fault happens rarely during operation
of WT, but precaution has to be taken as long as they are the most severe ones and can cause
instability in the system. The WT has to withstand symmetrical faults and to control the output
during and after the fault and has to secure the stability in the system. Severe fault can lead to a
loss of synchronization (LOS) on the system. The LOS is going to be investigated later on in the
project. [5][4][2]
The need of the WT to be connected in case of severe fault and the requirement from the grid
code, has made the study of the response of the WT to this type of faults necessary. The low
1
==PAGE:10==
voltage ride-through (LVRT) or fault ride-through (FRT) for WF is one of the requirements from
the grid codes and the main investigation in the current thesis project. During fault, the voltage
in the faulted point dips according to the origins of the fault, aﬀecting a wide area of the system.
Therefore, the WT have to be capable to stand in those changes of voltage otherwise, they will
start to trip. Tripping of a WF resulting in imbalance between the generation and the consumption
which, depending on the size of the WF, can lead to load shedding and black out in a wide area. In
order to secure power stability in the system the grid code requires from the WF to stay connected
for some time during fault. The period of time that the WT has to be connected during the fault
depends on the grid code requirements of the area. The characteristic of the FRT is described as
the voltage against time and it shows for how much time the WT has to be connected for diﬀerent
values of voltage in case of fault in the system [1]. As long as the WT is connected during fault,
the grid code demands from the WT to supply a certain amount of reactive current to the system
in order to keep the stability in it. The amount of the injected reactive current depends on the dip
of the voltage as well as the regulation which has been applied from the grid code[13][9][1][6].
According to [2], there are multiple control solutions which have been proposed in order to maintain
the stability in the system during and after severe faults. The main focus of the proposed solutions
is to maintain the synchronization of the WT with the grid during severe fault. Moreover, the
proposed solutions has to comply also with the grid code requirements during severe fault. That
means that they have to maintain the synchronization of the system but in the same time fulﬁll
the requirements of the grid code. After fault clearance, the system is passing through the recovery
period in which the system is trying to reach a new operation point (equilibrium point) in which
the system will be in balance between production and consumption with the disconnected faulted
part. A controller is needed in order to keep the stability of voltage and frequency, by controlling
the active and the reactive power in the system [2][4] [10]. In [2] has been proposed diﬀerent kind
of PLL controller, but the most reliable is the one which has as a feedback from the frequency of
the system.
As seen by the aforementioned studies, the main point of reference is that WF are connected via
AC transmission to the grid and all the controllers has been tuned according to that. The current
project is going to investigate the impact of severe faults in the oﬀshore wind power plants which
are connected to HVDC converter. In this work, the response of WTs and HVDC converter are
investigated in two diﬀerent cases. The ﬁrst case is a fault in the middle of the export cable.
It is expected that the response of the WT will be the same as if the WF is connected via AC.
Furthermore, the response of the HVDC converter will be investigated. In the second case the fault
is occurred inside the WF. No studies have been found in literature regarding this fault, which
makes it more interesting and worth studying.
1.2
Objectives
The main objective of this study is the investigation of a wind farm which is connected to the
HVDC converter during severe faults.
The thesis focuses in ways to maintain the stability of
the system during fault and post-fault. This has been succeeded by implementing a state of the
art controller which can keep the synchronization of the frequency of the wind turbines with the
fundamental frequency during and after a severe fault.
All the simulations and the fault case
studies has been done by using MATLAB’s Simulink software. It is also worth mentioning that
substantial time in this master thesis has been spent on developing the model. The steps followed
in order to achieve the results that are shown in this master thesis are:
• Reading and understanding the literature.
• Model development of WT.
• Applying severe fault at the PCC in the WT model which is connected in AC grid and
observe the impact on it.
• Implementing the proposed solution in the WT and observe the response of the system.
• Model development of HVDC.
• Applying severe fault at the PCC in the WT model which is connected in HVDC converter
and observe the impact with and without the proposed solutions.

• Implementing a detailed model of WF which is connected to HVDC converter.
• Observe the impact of severe fault in the middle of the export cable with and without the
proposed solution.
• Observe the impact of severe fault inside the WF with and without the proposed solution.
• Extracting the results and observing the impact of the controller on the system.
1.3
Variable naming convention
In this thesis the following rules have been applied when naming the variables. Values in per
unit (pu) and physical values has been deﬁned by lower and upper case respectively. That rule
does not apply for values which are directly relative to time such as frequency (f), rotational
speed (ω). Also, the bold letters are representing vector. The letters which have been deﬁned
by Italic font are are representing variables and the letter which has been deﬁned with no italic
are not variables (e.g.: indicators). Further, according to mathematical rules the imaginary unit
(j) denotes a mathematical constant, hence it has to be written in normal font. However, for
visualization purposes in the equations below, it has been written in italic font. Also, reference
signals are indicated by a star superscript.

able to operate, it needs an external ac grid which is providing voltage and frequency. The HVDC

2.2
HVDC converter
The WF connection via HVDC converters is a massively developing concept for industry as well
as researchers. There are some diﬀerences between AC and DC connection of WFs. In reality, DC
connection has two main advantages with respect to power transmission. First of all, the losses on
the transmission cable are lower than in AC grid. Moreover, in DC the power can be transmitted
in bigger distances than the AC connection. Although, apart from the advantages, the HVDC
converter has some downsides as well. The main downside of an HVDC transmission is the current
limitation of its HVDC converter. This current limitation comes from its structures which cannot
exceed a certain value of current. Usually the current of an HVDC converter cannot exceed the
1.5 pu. During a severe fault an AC grid can inject more than 5 or 6 times of its nominal current
(depending on the short circuit ratio of the grid). That is the major disadvantage of an HVDC
converter that needs to be taken into account in this project in which, the WF is connected to
a HVDC converter. It is worth mentioning that apart from the sizing and the construction, the
operation of HVDC converter is the same as it is in the converter of the WT.
In this section controllers used in order to design the HVDC converter are outlined. In this project
the HVDC converter is operating in ﬁxed frequency grid forming mode. The HVDC converter has
to provide voltage and frequency for the WT since the WTs are equipped with a grid following
control scheme. In order to generate the fundamental frequency on the HVDC converter a PLL
block has been used. PLL block has been used also in the WT for the tracking of the voltage’s
angle. The PLL of the HVDC converter will be introduced later in this section. Afterwards, apart
from the frequency, the HVDC converter has to generate the voltage of the grid. That has been

3.1
Grid code requirements
To comply with grid codes, WTs have to inject a speciﬁc amount of reactive current as it has
been described in the literature review and is shown in Figure 3.1-1. Accordingly, the grid requires
diﬀerent injections of reactive current depending on the severity of the fault. The severity of the
fault is determined by the voltage drop at the faulted point. In this way, during a severe fault, the
WT has to inject the reactive current which has been set by the grid code. The numbers that are
shown in Figure 3.1-1 may vary for diﬀerent WTs. These values depends on the current capabilities
of the WT’s converter. In this project the limitations on the current is 1.2 pu. That means that
during the fault when the voltage drop is, for example, lower than 0.4 pu, the grid code demands
from the WT to inject 1pu of reactive current. Regarding the maximum current capabilities of the
WT, the maximum active current that can be injected would be:

precisely. The proposed controller which has been implemented in this thesis is the most feasible

will not be satisﬁed. It can be seen that in case when the HVDC converter remains connected and
contributes during fault the WTs succeed to comply with the grid code requirements, by keeping
the reactive current close to 1 pu.

Overall, the support from the HVDC converter during fault is crucial for stable operation of WTs.
It has been illustrated by simulation result that the WF is not capable to comply with the grid

Chapter 5
Conclusions and
Recommendations
A severe fault has been applied in a WF which is connected on HVDC converter. The aim of this
master thesis is to secure the stability of the system during the fault, while the WPP is complying
to the grid code.
5.1
Conclusions
In this master thesis two major factors have been addressed. The ﬁrst, was that during a severe
fault the WF has to inject reactive current according to the grid code, as it has been discussed in
the literature review and in chapter 3. The second factor was to secure the stability of the system
while it is complying with the grid code. A description about the LOS has been given following
with the impact of it in the stability of the system.
In Chapter 3, it has been shown that in case of severe fault in the system the injected active current
of the WT has to be limited in order to secure the stability on the system. Furthermore, in the
same chapter it has been shown what is the relation of the ratio between the active and reactive
current with the X/R ratio and the necessity to keep the ratio of the current as close as possible
to the X/R ratio. Consequently, it is shown that in order to keep the stability of the system the
requirements of grid code has to be modiﬁed for voltage dips close to 0.
Also, a controller has been implemented in Chapter 3 in order to satisfy that the ratio of active
and reactive current will remain close to the X/R ratio and within the limits.
In the results at Chapter 4, the impact of the controller which has been implemented in the WT
model on the system response to severe faults has been presented. For a better understanding on
the fault each case has been run with and without the implemented controller. It has been shown
that the controller is capable of maintaining the stability of the system during and after fault for
both cases.
Moreover, in the last section of the Results it was shown that the contribution of the HVDC
converter during the fault is necessary. If the HVDC converter is blocked, it was shown that the
system does not lose its synchronization, but it failed to comply to the grid code. In case that the
HVDC converter does not provide any support during the fault the reactive current on the wind
turbines is oscillating between 1 and 0.5 pu in the whole period of the fault. This reaction is not
acceptable as long as the grid code demands from the WPP to inject a speciﬁc amount of reactive
current during the fault.
Regarding that the renewable sources are increasing nowadays and especially the wind energy
sector, the need for a sustained system is rising. This project is just the start of a new spectrum
of investigations which will come to surface in the upcoming years.

5.2
Future work
• In this master thesis severe faults inside the WF and in the middle of the export cable have
been investigated.
There is still room for further analytical investigations of the system
response. Comparing response of a WF when connected to HVDC transmission with that of
connected to an AC grid would be valuable.
• As it can be seen in the master thesis it was investigated a WF which consist only of 6
WT (due to hardware limitations for simulating big WFs). That means that the current
project can be extended beyond, by adding more WTs in the model. That will trigger new
problems to face on. The main problem is that the cabling size is increasing and as a result
the capacitance on the system will increase. The need of compensator is needed in that
situation in order to compensate for the extended capacitance on the system.
