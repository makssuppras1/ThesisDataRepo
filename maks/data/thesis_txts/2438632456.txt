Abstract
Demand response has captured the attention of governments, policy-makers, resear-
chers and different stakeholders in the past years. It constitutes a powerful solution
to the challenges posed by the high penetration of renewable energies in power sys-
tems, assuring security of supply and market efficiency. Thus far, the design of these
programs has relied on micro economic theories of utility maximization and consumer
rationality. However, the results of different experiments have noted a gap between
the technological potentials of demand response and the actual results.
New economic disciplines have demonstrated that individuals are not rational
decision-makers. Instead, they are characterised by different psychological properties
that make them bounded rational. The existing experimental background and pro-
posed models of bounded rationality settle a new framework to understand human
decision-making.
Several experts have emphasised the importance of introducing
insights of human behaviour in the design of residential energy use programs.
This master thesis investigates the impact of bounded rationality on a demand
response program. It evaluates its effects on price signals, load shifting and social
welfare distribution. In addition, this work proposes new designs of demand response
models that incorporate different aspects of bounded rationality.
The base model used in this work is the coupon incentive-based demand response
(CIDR) program. This thesis proposes a new game theoretical approach to the pro-
gram based on the Stackelberg game. The game models the interaction between the
retailer and the consumers and finds the optimal equilibrium point. It is solved using
two methodologies: bilevel optimization and iterative algorithm.
The results show that bounded rationality has a great impact on the outcomes
of the CIDR program. The obtained profit increase of the retailer ranges from 0%
to 118% of the expected profit under rational assumptions. The increase of social
welfare is not equally distributed, being the retailer the most benefited in most of the
cases.
The obtained results can be used as guidelines in the design of future demand
response programs. The findings show how different aspects of bounded rationality
are translated into consumers’ price elasticities and give some hints on how to exploit
this flexibility to boost demand response.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background of demand response programs
Demand side management (DSM) have proven to be a powerful solution for the chal-
lenges encountered in the current and future operation of power systems [1]. Among
the different DSM solutions, demand response (DR), aims to increase the flexibility
on the demand side through time-based tariffs and other forms of financial incentives
[2]. Fostered by the development of smart grid technologies and distributed energy
resources (DERs), demand response benefits are two-fold.
In the first place, it increases the system’s security.
This is essential to meet
the international agreements that strive for higher penetration of renewable energy
sources in the years to come. Denmark for example, has set a target of 100% renewable
energy on the total energy consumption by 2050 [3]. However, renewable energies
pose several challenges in the security and operation of power systems due to their
discontinuity and stochastic nature. Demand response can offer a solution to some of
these challenges by changing the current operation of power systems, where supply
follows demand. Instead, demand could be adjusted to the intermittent nature of
supply and increase the system’s security [4].
In the second place, Daniel S. Kirschen argue in [5] that demand response can
lead to a more mature electricity market. So far, the price elasticity of the demand
for electricity in the short run has been considerably small. He explains that this is
due to two economic and social factors: on one side, the cost of electricity accounts
for a small proportion of the households’ and industries’ budgets, yet it represents
an essential good for both of them in most of the industrialised countries. Therefore,
consumers will probably not reduce their energy consumption in order to avoid a
small increase of the electricity costs since the savings could be offset by the loss of
profit or comfort. On the other side, the electricity has been historically considered a
commodity that is always ready and easy to use, making that people do not balance
the costs and benefits every time they use it. The low demand elasticity leads to
price spikes in the electricity market and the exercise of market power by generating
enterprises [5]. The increase of demand elasticity through DR programs, can help to
reduce the price spikes in the short run and the exercise of market power, leading to
efficient electricity markets [5].
The high potentials of demand response have captured the attention of govern-
ments, policy-makers and researchers, as well as the different stakeholders involved,
namely transmission system operators (TSO), distributing companies (DISCOs) and

retailers [4]. As a result, different DR programs have been proposed and tested. One
can categorise them into price-based DR and incentive-based DR programs [6]. In
the first category customers are exposed to time-varying prices that reflects the real
costs of the electricity production, examples of these programs are critical-peak pric-
ing (CPP), peak load pricing (PLP), time-of-use (TOU) and real-time pricing (RTP)
[6].
In CPP schemes, consumers are charged high prices during peak periods, which
are notified in advance via email or phone notifications, on a limited number of critical
event days. The results from a CPP program tested in California with non-residential
customers showed relatively stable demand response [7].
PLP programs have a similar modus operandi, where high prices are charged to
customers exceeding certain load volumes during peak periods notified in advance.
The difficulty of these programs remains in establishing a fair load limit, which might
differ depending on the customer class, leading to potential manipulations [2].
TOU programs propose a tariff with two to three different unit prices depending
on the time period of the day. These rates reflect the average cost of electricity during
those time periods. TOU tariffs have been extensively tested worldwide; during 1970
and 1980 the U.S Federal Energy Administration initiated 14 experiments with TOU
tariffs, Germany also conducted experiments in Freiburg and Saarland [8]. Different
studies from the experiments conducted [9], [10], [11], [12], have shown very different
estimates of price elasticities. This might be due to differences in the study method-
ology and the type of consumer faced [8]. Although TOU programs have achieved
a successful shift of peak load, they have not fully exploited the existing DR poten-
tials and are not capable of meeting the needs of power systems with high share of
renewable energies [2].
Finally RTP propose an hourly fluctuating tariff that reflects the wholesale price
of the electricity. Consumers are normally notified about the hourly prices on a day-
ahead or hourly ahead basis [6]. One of biggest demonstration project was GridWise
initiative where 112 households equipped with smart technologies responded to TOU,
CPP, and RTP prices changing every five minutes [13]. Another experiment with
RTP tariffs was conducted in Chicago in 2003, showing that consumers responded
by conserving energy during peak hours, not leading to a consumption increase in off
peak hour [14].
One questionable implication of price-based programs is equity issues, since they
could have a negative effect on low socio-economic households, whose ability to avoid
high prices is smaller compared to other social groups [8]. Overall, price-based pro-
grams cannot be implemented with conventional metering and billing systems, requir-
ing specific technologies to kick off. On top of that, consumers are not used to take
hourly decisions related to their energy consumption. Therefore the conversion to
time-varying tariffs is expected to be slow.
Incentive-based demand response programs appear as an efficient and reliable
alternative while price-based demand response develops [6]. In these kind of programs
the participants receive financial incentives to modify their consumption for certain
time periods, independent from their retail electricity rate [6]. Examples of these
==PAGE:23==
1.2 Bridging the gap: understanding bounded rationality
3
programs are interruptible load contract (ILC) and peak time rebate (PTR). ILC
provides rebates to customers agreeing to decrease the load during system emergencies
[6]. These type of programs can apply penalties in case of failure of curtailment and
have been traditionally offered to large industrial customers [15].
PTR programs
offer rebates in the electricity tariff to customers reducing the load in peak hours.
Consumers who do not accept the monetary rewards will be charged their normal
retail tariff.
Two experiments were conducted in Anaheim and Ontario [16],[17],
respectively were the rebates had a predetermined constant value not reflecting the
actual cost of the operating conditions [18].
As it can be seen, a wide variety of demand response programs have been pro-
posed and tested in the past years. Their evolution has gone hand in hand with
the development of smart communications and technologies that enable their success.
However, the technological and economical potential of these programs differ from the
actual market behaviour [19]. Explanations for this gap are: limited access to capital,
lack of relevant information on available technologies, imperfect markets, misaligned
incentives and organisational barriers [20]. Many of these failures are closely related
to how individuals make decisions [21]. As a result the ability to overcome them rely
on a better understanding of human behaviour [20].
1.2
Bridging the gap: understanding bounded
rationality
Up until now, demand response programs have striven to change consumers’ demand
elasticity through price signals, assuming that prices are the best way to achieve
“socially optimum behaviour” [8].
The idea of influencing demand with prices is
based on the micro economic theories of consumer rationality and utility maximiza-
tion [22]. These theories present individuals as rational actors, “having preferences
that are ordered, known, invariant, and consistent” [20]. Rational agents make de-
cisions by maximizing an expected utility function; when doing so they are aware
of the different alternatives of choice and their future consequences [23]. However,
new economic disciplines such as behavioural economics, have proposed a drift from
the homo economicus perspective. Behavioural economics is mainly focused on the
bounds of rationality of economic agents, incorporating insights from psychology into
microeconomic theory [20]. The term bounded rationality was first coined by Herbert
A. Simon in 1957:
“The alternative approach employed in these papers is based on what I shall call the
principle of bounded rationality: The capacity of the human mind for formulating
and solving complex problems is very small compared with the size of the problems
whose solution is required for objectively rational behaviour in the real world — or
even for a reasonable approximation to such objective rationality.” [24]
==PAGE:24==
4
1 Introduction
Simon negates that individuals are rational decision-makers and proposes that
people have “psychological properties”, like limited knowledge and cognitive limits,
that make them unable to solve a utility maximization problem. Instead, individuals
will use simplifications and shortcuts that lead to a satisfactory solution which might
not be the optimal solution [23]. He explains bounded rationality with an example of a
chess player: “as soon as the player discovers a strategy that guarantees a checkmate,
he adopts it. He does not look for all possible check-mating strategies and adopt the
best” [25].
Ever since Simon proposed the theory of bounded rationality several psychol-
ogists and behavioural-economists have investigated the psychological properties of
individuals and proposed new models of human choice behaviour. Time-inconsistency,
anchoring and adjustment, framing and reference dependence and limited attention
are some of the psychological facts that influence individuals’ choices [26].
Geroge Ainslie in [27] highlighted that individuals show a preference towards more
immediate outcomes and apply more discounting between outcomes in the present and
near future than between periods in the far future. He showed that the discounting
applied by humans dealing with intertemporal decision-making follows an hyperbolic
curve.
Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky performed an extensive set of experiments
in order to obtain a map of bounded rationality [28]. They developed a model of
choice under risk called prospect theory, “in which value is assigned to gains and
losses rather than to final assets and in which probabilities are replaced by decision
weights” [29]. They showed that people value differently losses and gains, generally
losses harm more than gains do good, and that they tend to overweight low probability
events. As a result the way a choice is presented to the decision-maker will influence
her preferences and the final outcome [20]. They also showed that when people make
decisions they rely on previous information, “anchor on defaults”, and will generally
do insufficient adjustment towards the truth, that implies searching and processing
new information [30].
Xavier Gabaix in [26] proposed a sparsity based model of bounded rationality,
which is an alternative to the traditional “max” operator. In his model, decision-
makers make simplifications, by paying attention to some variables and neglecting
others, resulting in a sparse representation of the problems they face.
Once the
selection is done, individuals will find the optimal choice, given the restricted set of
variables they decided to pay attention to.
All these different models and findings create a new framework to better under-
stand human choice behaviour. Several studies [31], [8], [20], [22] have emphasised the
importance of introducing the insights of human behaviour in the design of residential
energy use programs. In the same vein, the European Union has committed to fund
several projects in order to bridge the gap between the technological potentials of
energy end-users programs and the actual market behaviour. Some examples of these
projects are BEHAVE, Penny Project and PEAKapp among others.
In this context, this master thesis investigates the impact of bounded rationality
on a demand response program. This thesis, therefore, builds and tests a DR model

under the assumption of utility maximization and consumer rationality. The model
is based on the coupon incentive-based demand response (CIDR) program proposed
by Zhong et Al. in [18]. In addition, this piece of work includes a new approach
to represent the interaction between the retailer and the consumer based on the

In these innovations consumers are no longer modelled as rational agents but as

The outcomes of the four models are compared to the base model to analyse the

Different demand response programs have been proposed and put in practice in several
real-life setups. Among them, this master thesis focuses on the Coupon Incentive-
based Demand response (CIDR) [18]. This program is used as the basis for innovations
on consumer behaviour due to its simplicity and flexibility. The fact that this program
is incentive-based, allows to study how consumers choose their degree of participation
compared to other programs where the participation is mandatory. On the other hand,
it offers a simpler setup than real-time pricing programs, since the optimal price
signals are only calculated for certain time-steps and not on an ongoing basis. As a
result, a model based on the CIDR program is built and tested, assuming perfectly
rational consumers, to be later used as a reference case.
This chapter focuses on how this demand response program works, describing
who are the main actors involved and their interactions. Different approaches to the
implementation of this program are also discussed as well as the solution methods
used.
2.1
Definition of CIDR
Coupon Incentive-based Demand Response program was first proposed by H. Zhong et
Al. in [18]. This program focuses on retailers or load serving entities (LSE), the terms
are used interchangeably throughout the text, that offer flat rates to their customers,
yet still need to participate in a wholesale real-time electricity market (RT), also
referred to as balancing market. They assumed that the LSE estimates the electricity
consumed by their customers during the twenty-four hours of the next day and buy
that energy in a day-ahead market (DA) the day prior to the actual delivery. Due
==PAGE:28==
8
2 Coupon Incentive-based Demand Response (CIDR)
to the time lag between the purchase and delivery of the energy, the forecast load
can differ from the predicted one and the retailer will have to participate in the real-
time wholesale electricity market. If for a certain hour the energy purchased in the
day-ahead market is higher than the actual energy consumed, the surplus of energy
will be sold at the real-time price. On the contrary, if the energy purchased in the
day-ahead is not enough to cover the demand, the LSE will have to purchase the
lack of energy at the real-time price.
While the retailer’s profit is subject to the
fluctuations of the wholesale market, the consumers pay a risk-averse flat tariff that
decouple their expenses from the volatile market prices. With the proposed CIDR
program the retailer can motivate consumers’ flexibility through voluntary coupon
incentives when the upcoming real-time price is causing potential losses [18]. In this
way, if a price spike is expected, it will be beneficial for the retailer to pay a coupon
(e ) to the consumers for every MWh that they reduce their consumption.
The
other way around, expected low RT prices motivates LSE to pay a coupon (e ) for
every MWh that the consumers increase their consumption. Coupons are therefore
working as a negative premium (e /MWh) that reduces the flat tariff for every MWh
of demand response provided. The coupon is sent through communication networks
to the consumers, who can either ignore this price signal and consume the same
electricity at the retail flat rate, or modify their consumption and get a rebate thanks
to the demand respond provided. Note that the targeted consumers must have smart
meters and communication capabilities installed at home to be able to participate
in the program. Finally the LSE gathers all the responses from the consumers and
submits a final bid to the TSO before the gate closure of the balancing market. It
should be noticed that the foreseen real-time price could be modified by the bids
from the different participants in the market, influencing the awaited profit of the
LSE. The coupons could be sent in an hourly basis, however it is the LSE’s choice to
determine when it is more beneficial to induce demand response.
The CIDR program was compared to the flat rate and real-time pricing programs
(RTP) in terms of social welfare, consumer surplus and LSE profit in [18].
They
qualitatively showed that the social welfare increases as compared to the flat rate.
This increase is shared among the LSE and consumers. Consumers’ surplus is higher
than in the flat rate, since they still pay a flat rate but get some rebates, and it is also
higher than in the RTP, since they are not exposed to the fluctuations of the market.
On the other hand, the LSE profit is increased compared to the flat rate, still lower
than in a RTP program where the financial risks of the market are transferred to the
consumers.
Zhong et Al. consider that the CIDR program is readily implementable because
it maintains the existing structure of flat rates while inducing voluntary demand
response thanks to smart grid communications. They pointed out that the fact that
it is a voluntary scheme brings in more uncertainty to the LSE compared to other
programs where the participation is mandatory. However, this uncertainty can be
tractable if there exists an effort from the LSE in understanding consumers’ behaviour
[18].
Therefore, the main challenge faced by a retailer willing to put into practice the

CIDR program is the calculation of the optimal coupon price.
The coupon price
needs to benefit both sides, it has to maximize the retailer’s profit while maximizing
the consumers’ utility.
2.2
Approaches to CIDR
As it has been mentioned before, CIDR program is readily implementable, nonetheless
it is still challenging to find the optimal coupon price that should be sent to the
consumers. Two different approaches to this problem are discussed in this section.
2.2.1
Multi-round iteration
Zhong et Al.
proposed in [18] a multi-round iteration between the LSE and the
consumers to obtain an optimal coupon price. This iterative process is depicted in
Figure 2.1 for a single-hour where a price spike is expected .
The LSE, in view of the expected price spike, sends a coupon price to consumers in
order to induce demand response. The first coupon price is an initial guess based on
similar situations in the past. The consumers receive the broadcast coupon through
communication networks and submit their offers of demand response to the LSE.
Afterwards, the LSE anticipates the impact of the bid with the updated demand
response in the real time market. The LSE estimates her profit and evaluates if it
has increased compared to the previous situation. If it has increased, she will start
another iteration round and broadcast a new coupon. Else, the LSE has found an
optimal coupon price and stops the iteration round.
One of the main drawbacks
pointed out by the authors in [18] is that the multi-round iteration request several
interactions with consumers, which could result in a reduction of the participation
rate. On top of that, the multi-round iteration could potentially lead to the Cobweb
effect, where due to the time-lag between the retailer and consumers’ decisions, the
equilibrium point is never reached. This effect is illustrated in Figure 2.2 where two
characteristic supply and demand curves are depicted.
The retailer could expect
a certain response from consumers Q1 based on past experiences and send coupon
price P1 accordingly. The actual response to the coupon price P1 might differ and
end up in a different response Q2. LSE then plans new coupon price P2 under the
expectation that consumers will respond with Q2. The repetition of this process in
each iteration round creates undesired oscillations. This effect has been investigated
in [2] where they propose a new balancing market design including demand response.
They provided evidence that the activation of demand response leads to the Cobweb
effect, resulting in unwanted oscillations on the supply side.

2.2.2
Stackelberg game
This master thesis proposes a different approach to the implementation of the CIDR
program that allows to study the effects of bounded rational consumers.
In this
approach, the exchange of information between the LSE and the consumers occurs
only once.
The optimal price signal is obtained prior to the interaction with the
consumers, by anticipating their response. This is done following a game theoretical
approach of the CIDR problem.
The interaction between the LSE and the consumers can be formulated as a Stack-
elberg game. Stackelberg games are non-cooperative sequential games where there
is a hierarchical structure between the players [32]. One generalisation of the single-
leader-single-follower Stackelberg game is the so called single-leader-multiple-follower
game, where there is one leader and multiple followers, all of them acting for their
own benefits, see Figure 2.3 for a schema of the game. The sequence of the game
gives the advantage to the leader to make decisions first, continued by the followers’
decision. In the CIDR program one could consider the LSE as the leader and the con-
sumers as the followers. The sequence of the game will be as follows: first, the leader
plays an action following her own strategy; in this case she chooses a coupon price
that maximizes her profit in the real-time market. After the leader has made her de-
cision the followers choose an action according to their own strategy; in this case the
followers are choosing the electricity consumption that maximizes their utility from
participating in the demand response program. The retailer is playing this game with
several consumers at the same time. However, the consumers are not competing be-
tween themselves; each consumer does not consider the strategy of another consumer
when choosing how much electricity she will consume. Therefore, the game consists
of a single-leader-multiple-follower game with no competition among the followers. It
is assumed that there is perfect information in the game, if no communication error
happens the consumers are perfectly informed about the coupon price sent by the
retailer. Similarly, the retailer knows the action played by the consumers, which is
the demand response offer. In this kind of game the leader’s action is influenced by
the follower’s action and vice versa.
Stackelberg games can be formulated as bilevel optimization problems [33], where
the leader solves her optimization problem, called the upper-level problem, taking into
account the follower’s optimization problem, called the lower-level problem, which
at the same time is parametrized by the upper-level decision variables [34].

An extension of the mathematical formulation in [18] has been proposed for this

of the CIDR problem. Two add-ons have been proposed and formulated:
• First the consumers’ utility function is no longer modelled with a piece-wise
linear function.
Instead of using the auxiliary variable yi and the equation
(2.3b) to simulate the concave shape of the utility function, one could use a
quadratic function. This innovation reduces the number of variables, since the
dual variables associated to each of the pieces k of the piece-wise utility function

predicted price spike or a low real-time market price. With this generalization

real-time price is lower than the flat rate, the coupon will be negative inducing
an increase in the load.

CHAPTER 3
Modelling bounded
rationality
So far, the vast majority of demand response programs have been designed adopting
micro-economic theories of consumers’ rationality and utility maximization [8]. How-
ever, it has been recently shown that these theories fail to describe human behaviour,
especially when applied to energy behaviour at residential scale [8]. Several stud-
ies [8], [20], [22] have pointed out the urgency of combining social psychology and
behavioural economics in the design of demand response programs as well as other
energy efficiency programs.
This chapter includes a new approach to the design of the CIDR program described
in Chapter 2. It incorporates four different models of behaviour in the consumers’
optimization problem. The innovation on the consumers’ model are: limited attention,
framing and valuation, intertemporal decision-making and hyperbolic discounting.
Each of them is addressed individually in a separate model. The Stackelberg game
described in Section 2.2.2 is reformulated to include the interaction between the LSE
and the bounded rational consumers. The solution methods used to solve the new
formulations are also explained.
3.1
Limited attention
The responsiveness of consumers to price signals in demand response programs can be
affected by multiple factors. An extensive analysis conducted by Peter C. Reiss and
Matthew W. White in [40] identified that the households using electricity for space
heating or air conditioning were the most responsive group, yet a significant percent-
age (44%) of the studied sample showed no price-responsiveness. This reluctance to
respond may be due to different reasons. For example, high-income households will
normally have a larger budget and will not pay attention to energy prices. Mean-
while, other households might not have the capabilities to respond, either due to lack
of information or understanding [8]. All in all, people do not pay enough attention to
the price signals leading to lower participation rates. In order to better understand
how people make decisions one could refer to the sparsity-based model of bounded
rationality proposed by Xavier Gabaix in [26].

In this model, Gabaix introduces the “sparse max” operator, that is a “behavioural
version of the traditional max operator” [26].
Based on the recent psychological
findings, an agent that faces a decision, pays less or no attention to some aspects of
the problem and neglect them [26]. On the basis of that evidence, the sparse max
operator works as follows: “first, the agent builds a simplified model of the world and
thinks about the world through this simplified model. Second, this representation is
sparse, that is, uses few parameters that are nonzero or differ from the usual state of
affairs. These choices are controlled by an optimization of her representation of the
world that depends on the problem at hand”.
This model of human choice could be applied to the case of a consumer choosing
whether to participate or not in the CIDR program.
If the consumer was purely
rational, she should take into account several factors: the coupon price, the level of
comfort at home, but also the electricity price in the market, the cost of transmission
lines, the weather, the needs of the children, etc. However, the consumer will not
think about all of them. Instead, she will be more concern about factors that appear
more important to her and disregard others. This perceived representation of the
factors can be modelled with a sparse vector, where the factors that the consumer is
not paying attention to are assigned a zero, and the ones that she is paying attention
to are assigned a one. In Gabaix’s formulation x represents the vector with the real
values of these factors, xs is the perceived representation of x and m is the attention

3.2
Framing and valuation
Other key factors that influence consumers’ decisions in their residential energy use are
framing and reference dependence [20]. Both aspects have been extensively studied by
the psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky throughout their work. They
showed that the way a choice is given to a decision maker can have an influence over
the decision outcome. If for example the same choice is framed as a gain or as a loss
by changing the reference point, the individual’s preference might change even though
the outcome of the choice was the same in both contexts [41]. This can be explained
by what the authors called the “certainty effect”, which is a “tendency towards risk
aversion in choices involving sure gains and to risk seeking in choices involving sure
losses” [29]. An example of the certainty effect can be found in Appendix B. All
these findings have been encompassed in the theory proposed by Daniel Kahneman
and Amos Tversky called Prospect theory. This theory presents an alternative model
of decision-making under risk, in opposition to the expected utility theory, where
individuals making decisions will follow two phases:
• An editing phase, in which the prospects are simplified using different operations
that transforms the outcomes and probabilities to facilitate the task of decision
[29].
• An evaluation phase, in which the edited prospects are evaluated, with their
probabilities and values, and finally the prospect with higher value is chosen
[29].
This evaluation phase is formulated by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky as
follows:

3.3
Intertemporal decision-making
When the consumers make decisions about their energy consumption, they might not
only think about the electricity they consume now, but plan their future consumption
in advance. Therefore, the scheduling of the household tasks is crucial in the energy
consumption pattern of each consumer. As an example, one can imagine a household
in which they normally cook at 18:00 and then use the dishwasher at 20:00. At 18:00
they receive a message from the LSE offering a coupon to reduce the load. Then, they
have to consider the convenience of accepting the coupon. If they accept the offer,
they might have to postpone the dinner for later, however at 20:00 they normally
play board games. Another option could be to order a pizza instead and be able to
accept the coupon while not sacrificing their board-games night.
The ability to shift the load in time or its substitution with other energy carriers or
goods are key factors in the consumers’ elasticity. The aforementioned example shows
how consumers normally plan their consumption in advance to consider the different
options available. The decision-making process the consumer is facing when choosing
her participation in the CIDR program is no longer a one time step decision. She
will on the contrary, face several decisions along the time frame considered, denoted
by T. As a result, the decision made at time t1 is influenced by the decisions made
at t2, t3, ...T. Similarly, the decision made at t2 is influenced by the decisions made
at t3, t4, ...T, and so on. As it can be seen, the problem complexity increases for
the decision-maker who should take into account all possible outputs in future time
steps to determine her decision at time t1.
As mentioned by Cigno A. and Hool
Bin in [42], “in any real-life situation the accomplishment of such a task would be
far beyond anyone’s analytical and computational capacity”. They propose therefore
that individuals, due to their bounded rationality, will simplify the decision choice
problem resulting in a shorter foresight than the one they are concerned about.
This section includes the intertemporal decision-making process just described in
the consumer’s optimization problem.
The simpler approach is a multi-time step
problem, in which the consumer optimizes her utility for several time steps. In this
case, the consumer decides on her energy consumption at time t1, when the coupon
has been broadcast, assuming no more coupons are broadcast for the future time
steps in the time horizon considered. In order to model the shift of the load in time
and the substitution effect one could think of an energy budget. Each consumer has a
virtual energy budget, which determines how much electricity she can consume in the
time frame considered. Initially the consumer has planned an amount of electricity for
each time step, whose summation over the time window constitutes its energy budget.
Due to the interaction with the LSE, the load can be reallocated in time. However,
the total consumption in time needs to remain within the limits of the energy budget.

3.4
Hyperbolic discounting
People value differently the goods in time when dealing with intertemporal choices.
As an example, one would prefer to receive 100 e now than in one year. So far the
valuation in time has been modelled in traditional economic theory with an exponen-
tial discounting function. Using this discounting function the evolution in time of the
utility curve of two different amounts of the same good should never cross each other
[44]. To better understand it two situations A and B are presented. In situation A
one has to choose between receiving 50 e right away or receiving 100 e in two years.
In situation B one has to choose between receiving 50 e in four years or 100 e in 6
years [44]. The option chosen in both situations should be the same, since it is the
same choice given at 4 years’ greater distance. However, behavioural psychologists
have shown in various experiments that more discounting occurs between the present
and the close future than between periods in the faraway future [27]. This explains
why people will normally choose 50 e in case A and 100 e in case B [44]. The
discounting curve followed by people when devaluing expected rewards will have an
hyperbolic shape instead of an exponential one [27]. Hyperbolic discounting leads
to problems of time-inconsistency and conflicts between different selves, the “current
self” versus the “future self” [45]. Among the different versions of the discounting
curve, this master thesis follows the version used by George Ainslie in [44] :

CHAPTER 4
Results and discussion
Results from different demand response experiments have highlighted the gap between
the potential DR and the actual response obtained [8]. Moreover, some studies have
showed inconclusive results in the assessment and categorisation of the participants
price responsiveness [46]. Based on that, this master thesis proposed four models of
demand response where consumers are characterised by different aspects of bounded
rationality. These models aim to analyse the impact of the consumers’ choices in the
expected outcome of the DR program.
This chapter includes the results from the different models described in Chapter 2
and Chapter 3. It starts with the case description, that explains the assumptions
behind the data used to run the models. It continues with the analysis of the results
from the CIDR model under the assumption of rationality. It finishes with a discussion
of the results obtained with the different models of bounded rationality.
4.1
Case description
This piece of work uses synthetic data as an input for the different models. The use
of synthetic data is justified by the complexity of modelling human behaviour and
the absence of accurate data that describes human choices in demand response pro-
grams. As a result, different assumptions have been made to obtain the required data.
4.1.1
Consumers
The consumers’ utility of electricity consumption is modelled with a quadratic func-
tion. The concave shape of the utility function is based on the staircase shape of the
demand curve in the electricity market. The utility function is described by the co-
efficients a2, a1, a0, where a2 is the quadratic coefficient, a1 the linear coefficient and
a0 the constant term. These parameters also define the group the consumers belong
to. The aim of including different groups is to have a more representative sample of
consumers. A group of consumers is defined by similar a2, a1, a0 coefficients, obtained
from a normal distribution. These coefficients are chosen according to the baseline
consumption which is the amount of electricity the consumer is expected to consume
when there is no demand response. According to Dansk Energy and Energinet an

the optimal load level when no coupon is issued in the consumer optimization prob-
lem.
For simplicity, all the cases consider that 10 consumers participate in the demand
response program and that they belong to the same group. However, the effects of
the number of consumers and groups have been investigated in Appendix C showing
that the profit of the LSE increases linearly with the number of consumers and that
the slope changes depending on the groups considered. As a result, increasing the

Section 3.1 explained that people are not able to pay full attention to all the variables
affecting their decisions and will simplify their decision-making process resulting in
a sparse representation of the problem. This is reflected in lower participation rates,

simulations have been run with different participation rates, ranging from 10% to
100%. The sample of consumers considered in all the simulations are 10 consumers
belonging to 3 different groups, as explained in Section 4.1.1. The program outcomes
depend on the group of attentive consumers. Therefore, for each participation rate
the models are run 3000 times to cover all the different combinations of attentive and

and that her profit will increase by 3.03% compared to baseline program conditions,
as it has been previously mentioned in the second case of Section 4.2. However, if
only 10% of the consumers respond, the increase in profit will be 0.304% which is
89.96% lower than the one obtained in the rational case. Facing inattentive consumers,
results in a lower profit increase than the one obtained in the rational case, the
difference between both values is the loss faced by the retailer. This loss has been
normalized with the rational case and plotted in blue bars in Figure 4.6.
It can
be observed that the loss decreases linearly with the increase in the participation
rate, where 100% participation rate represents the rational case.

previously mentioned, the profit obtained by the LSE is proportional to the number
of consumers participating in the program. Therefore, lower participation rates result
in lower profits, which compared to the rational case result in higher losses.
However, if the LSE has perfect information about the participation rates, she can
adjust the coupon prices and reduce the loss, by solving optimization problem (3.5).

case of 10% participation rate, the loss could be reduced by 1.36% percentage points.
Even if the retailer can anticipate that only 10% of the consumers will participate, the
demand response they are able to provide is considerably small compared to the case
of 100% participation, resulting in lower profits. On the other hand if one compares
the profit increase of PI referenced to NPI, it can be seen that it is 1.14 times bigger
for 10% participation. This means, that the retailer can benefit from anticipating
how many consumers participate and to which group they belong to. This gain is
decreasing with the participation rate because the sample of consumers gets closer
to the one the retailer is optimizing for. This can also be seen in the values of the
coupon prices presented in Figure 4.7.
On average, PI coupon prices are smaller than NPI coupons for low participation

faces a sample of consumers from group 1, the coupon will be smaller than the one
sent to a sample of consumers from group 2 or 3, more details about this can be
found in Appendix C.2. As a result the value of the coupon on average is smaller
for low participation rates, but it presents a higher standard deviation. For higher
participation rates, the composition of the sample of consumers does not change
significantly and the coupon price converges towards the value of rational consumers.

4.3.4
Hyperbolic discounting
Section 3.4 introduced the concept of hyperbolic discounting. When dealing with in-
tertemporal decision-making, people apply more discounting between the present and
the close future than between periods in the far future. This means that individuals
prefer more immediate outcomes, resulting in inconsistent decisions with their future
preferences. The effect of hyperbolic discounting is studied in the CIDR program un-
der different values of the discounting factor, κ, ranging from 0 to 2 in steps of 0.25.
According to George Ainslie in [44], the discounting factor should be close to one.
The setup is the same as the one used in Section 4.3.3 with three hours as the time
frame considered and 625 model runs to account for variability in the consumption
patterns. The lower bound of the budget constraint has been fixed to 0.98 times the
total baseline consumption. In this way, the consumer has some flexibility since the
final energy consumed can be 2% lower than the program baseline.
The reference rational case used in this study is case 1 of Section 4.2, with 10 con-
sumers of the same group. The LSE, assuming that consumers are rational, expects
a profit increase of 3.58% compared to the baseline program conditions. On the other
hand, the consumers receive the price signal and optimize their utility over the time
frame considered applying an hyperbolic discounting. The load reduction offers are
sent to the LSE, who updates her profit and obtains a lower profit increase than the

CHAPTER 5
Conclusions and future
work
This master thesis uses the coupon incentive-based demand response (CIDR) program
as a framework to understand the impact of bounded rationality in demand response
programs. It proposes a Stackelberg game to model the interaction between the load
serving entity (LSE) and the consumers, that is able to find the Nash equilibrium
in one shot, avoiding the use of iterative algorithms that can lead to sub-optimal
solutions.
The results of the different models have shown that bounded rational consumers
have a big impact on the performance of the CIDR program. Even-though the CIDR
with bounded rational consumers still increases the overall social welfare, this im-
provement is generally smaller compared to the reference case with fully rational
consumers. In particular, the loss of profit faced by the LSE ranges from -100% to
+18% of the expected profit under rationality assumptions. On the other hand, antic-
ipating bounded rationality does not generally lead to significant improvements. The
losses can be reduced by 0-2 percentage points in most of the cases, except for fram-
ing and valuation where the reduction ranges from 11-141 percentage points. Both,
the LSE and consumer, benefit from the CIDR program, however the shares of the
social welfare increase shows that the program is more advantageous for the LSE
than for the consumer. In addition, if the LSE is able to identify consumers’ bounded
rationality this inequality grows.
A series of case studies identified various impacts of bounded rational consumers
that can be summarised as follows. Participation rates have a high impact on the
program outcomes and the value of perfect information (VOPI) is considerably small.
When designing large scale demand response programs it is necessary to identify the
responsive participants and find similarities in their responses. The adjustment of the
price signals according to the group of consumers boosts the profits, although this
increase looks small when the participation rate expected is much higher.
Having perfect information about how people value the coupon prices leads to
significant improvements in the profits obtained by the LSE. The valuation process
carried out by the consumer has been mimic with a value function that tries to
capture the reluctance of the consumers to participate and their flexibility; which can
be interpreted as the usable appliances to provide demand response. It is important to
find the minimum price signals that fulfils consumers’ requirements in order to exploit
==PAGE:84==
64
5 Conclusions and future work
their available flexibility. Otherwise, the price signal can result in an opportunity cost
for the load serving entity.
When consumers optimize their energy consumption over some period of time
and are characterised by a low flexibility, the losses of profit faced by the LSE are
considerably high. Having perfect information about the consumers’ response in this
case does not lead to improvements in the obtained profits. On the contrary, when
consumers are able to substitute the electricity with other commodities, the losses are
reduced and VOPI values are higher. Nevertheless, the gains obtained do not reach
the values of the case in which consumers optimize for one single time step. Therefore,
assessing the consumers’ flexibility beforehand is beneficial, yet the program outcomes
are limited by the fact that people have a longer foresight than the program time-line.
Finally, when consumers discount the benefits of participating in the DR program
following an hyperbolic discounting curve, the loss of profit faced by the LSE shows
an hyperbolic decrease with the increase of the discounting factor. Therefore, the fact
that consumers discount their future outcomes, results in higher profits for the LSE.
Hyperbolic discounting can be interpreted as an occasional augmented price elasticity
of the consumers. The preference for the immediate reward spurs consumers’ demand
response and results in more beneficial outcomes.
The results show that in order to obtain the desired demand response, the price
signals should be tailored to the consumers’ characteristics. Bilevel optimization is
a powerful tool to find those price signals and delineate the potentials of demand
response.
However, its complexity might undermine its application in large scale
interventions.
As a part of the future work, the following extensions could be considered. First,
it would be interesting to include new models of bounded rationality. The existing
literature and experimental results related to bounded rationality are very broad.
Anchoring and adjustment is one of the findings that can fit into demand response
programs. The intertemporal decision-making model could be extended to incorpo-
rate how people estimate future coupon prices. In this way, consumers can have an
estimation of the future coupon values based on defaults (anchoring), e.g. there is
no coupon; or information received during the program (adjustment), e.g. if at 18:00
there is a coupon at 21:00 there is a higher coupon. The estimation of future coupon
prices affects the here and now decisions turning into a different demand response
offer. Another extension could be focus on the laziness effect. Experimental results
of demand response programs have pointed out that people get tired of responding
to price signals after consecutive calls. This can diminish the occasional augmented
price elasticity showed in the hyperbolic discounting model.
Second, different approaches to the CIDR problem could provide additional in-
sights. Stochastic programming can be used to deal with uncertainty in the LSE
optimization problem. This thesis has worked with deterministic models, however
there are numerous sources of uncertainty: real-time market prices and consumers’
response are the main ones. Therefore the retailer could follow different strategies
depending on the risk she is willing to take, from stochastic optimization to robust
optimization. Another approach to the intertemporal decision-making problem of the
==PAGE:85==
5 Conclusions and future work
65
consumer could be to use dynamic programming. With this approach the multi-time
stage optimization problem of the consumers dealing with intertemporal decision-
making can be transformed into a sequence of simpler problems.
Finally, the impact of bounded rationality could be also studied in different de-
mand response programs or market setups. New balancing market designs including
demand response or peer-to-peer electricity markets are interesting frameworks to
study the effect of bounded rationality.

In this appendix an experiment carried out by psychologists and behavioural economists
is included to give a better grasp of how people make decisions.
Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky have worked with framing effects, showing
that the preference of a decision outcome can change depending on how the decision
choice was formulated. They showed that effect with the formulation of the following
problem:
(Note: Both the formulation of the problem and the explanation has been directly
copied from the article [28])
The Asian disease
Imagine that the United States is pre- paring for the outbreak of an unusual Asian
disease, which is expected to kill 600 people. Two alternative programs to combat
the disease have been proposed. Assume that the exact scientific estimates of the
consequences of the programs are as follows:
If Program A is adopted, 200 people will be saved
If Program B is adopted, there is a one-third probability that 600 people will be saved
and a two-thirds probability that no people will be saved
“In this version of the problem, a substantial majority of respondents favor Pro-
gram A, indicating risk aversion. Other respondents, selected at random, receive a
question in which the same cover story is followed by a different description of the
options:”
If Program A’ is adopted, 400 people will die
If Program B’ is adopted, there is a one- third probability that nobody will die and
a two-thirds probability that 600 people will die
==PAGE:90==
70
B Insights on bounded rationality
“A substantial majority of respondents now favor Program B’, the risk-seeking
option. Although there is no substantive difference between the versions, they evoke
different associations and evaluations. This is easiest to see in the certain option,
because outcomes that are certain are overweighted relative to outcomes of high or
intermediate probability (Kahneman and Tversky, 1979). Thus, the certainty of saving
people is disproportionately attractive, while accepting the certain death of people is
disproportionately aversive. These immediate affective responses respectively favor A
over B and B’ over A’.”

C.2
Performance of bilevel optimization for different
number of consumers and groups
This section analyses the effect of number of consumers and group category in the
results of the bilevel optimization problem (2.12). The number of consumers is varied
from 10 to 50 to observe its impact on the LSE profit, demand response and coupon
price. This is done for three cases, in each case the consumers belong to a different
group with utility functions presented in Figure 4.1. Figure C.4a shows the profit
increase experienced by the LSE. Figure C.4b shows the total load reduction offered
by the consumers. Finally, Figure C.4c shows the optimal price signals for the dif-
ferent cases. It can be observed that the profit increases linearly with the number
of consumer. The slope changes depending on the consumers’ characteristics. The
same tendency is observed in Figure C.4b. Consumers belonging to group 1 have low
utility and baseline consumption which limits the demand response they are able to
provide. As a result, the optimal coupon price found is smaller than for the other
two groups. Since the sample of consumers considered are characterised by similar
parameters of the utility function but not exactly the same there are some differences
in the value of the coupon for the case of group 1. In this case, (2.12d) is binding
and therefore the value of the dual variable will have an impact in the stationarity
condition (2.12c) and in the value of the optimal coupon found. The complementar-
ity conditions are checked and they hold for all the cases. Consumers of group 2 are
willing to provide more demand response than group 1 but less than 3. The optimal
coupon price found for group 2 is the same as in the case of group 3. Looking at
the utility function of group 2 one can observe that the baseline consumption is at
a steeper part of the curve, therefore every unit of demand response provided has a
higher decrease in utility than for the consumers of group 3. This explains why both,
the profit increase and demand response, are smaller for group 2 than for group 3.
