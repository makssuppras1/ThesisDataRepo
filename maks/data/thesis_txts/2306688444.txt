Abstract
In this thesis we present an investigation of the ﬂuid dynamics design criteria for biological
micropores. More speciﬁcally we will look at the geometrical structure of some speciﬁc
bordered pits and plasmodesmatas. The biological microporer play an important role in
the transportation of water vascular plants. Although believed to have high permeability
they still account for an signiﬁcant part of the viscous dissipation in the plants.
The analysis will build on analytical lubrication theory, numerical simulations in Comsol
and experimental work. We will show that the classical lubrication theory, formulated by
Reynolds[1] in 1886, is insuﬃcient under certain assumption. This is proven by experiments
and conﬁrmed by numerical simulations. We extend the theory to obtain better predictions
of the resistance in biological micropores. We will deﬁne a critical angle as an qualitatively
understanding of the design criteria of biological micropores and ﬁnd the optimised solution
from the theory of calculus of variations.

Introduction
Micropores play an important role in ﬂuid and solute transport between living cells. Ex-
amples are plasmodesmatas and pit membranes, found in plants, see Fig. 1.1. These pores
are remarkably permeable, and it is believed that their geometric shape is optimized to
reduce viscous dissipation. The physical design principles governing their shape are, how-
ever, not yet fully understood. Solutions to pressure driven ﬂows for simple geometries are
known and some analytical exact solutions exist. However, when the geometry becomes
more complex one must rely on approximations. One method often used is the theory of
lubrication, ﬁrst introduced by Osborne Reynolds in 1886. The theory is based on the as-
sumption that the channel height is slowly varying. With this assumption Reynolds states
"we may without error, disregard any curvature there may be in the surfaces" [1, pp. 187
]. With the assumption just slightly changed it is, however, not completely certain that
we can disregard any curvature. We will address this issue in the project.

Vascular transport in plants occurs in the xylem and phloem. The xylem is respon-
sible for the transport of water whereas phloem conducts the products of photosynthesis.
Xylem transport is due to evaporation of water at the top of the tree, see Fig. 1.3. The
higher concentration of e.g sugar in the top of the plant due to photosynthesis creates a
concentration gradient causing a hydrostatic pressure which drives the ﬂow in the phloem.
This is called the Pressure Flow Hypothesis, ﬁrst proposed by [7] and recently conﬁrmed by
[8]. The rate of transport of water in the xylem is controlled by several physical character-
istics, one being the hydraulic resistance of the xylem. Modelling the xylem as a perfectly
cylindrical and straight pipe the Hagen-Poiseuille ﬂow can be used to describe the ﬂow
and resistance in the xylem. The hydraulic resistance in a cylindrical pipe is proportional
with the inverse radius to the power four. This is however in practice not the case. The
xylem pipe consists of many adjacent cells and the water has to ﬂow laterally between the
cells through pits [9].
The study of the important characteristics for resistance will be build from a ﬂuid
dynamic point of view. In 1886 Reynolds developed what is known as Lubrication Theory.
The theory is based on the assumption that the channel height is slowly varying compared

Recently in 2014 Tavakol et al.[11] extended the lubrication theory to also work for faster

on the trinity of analytics, numerics, and experiments. Analytical solutions are obtained
from lubrication theory. Lubrication theory formulated by Reynolds is the zeroth order
perturbation of the ﬁelds in the perturbation parameter given by the channel height and
length ratio. We will denote higher order perturbations as extended lubrication theory
(ELT) [11] and the lubrication theory formulated by Reynolds as classical lubrication
theory (CLT). Numerical results are primarily calculated using the software MatLab and
Comsol. Experiments will validate our predictions from extended lubrication theory.
Through speciﬁc examples we will show that Reynolds’ statement may not be valid in the
faster height variation limit and that the extended lubrication in some cases gives a better
prediction of the ﬂow rate and also a deeper understanding of the dynamics. From this we
will improve our understanding of the important characteristics in biological micropores.
The model will be based on simple 2D geometries, due to its simplicity. We will show that
the more realistic 3D cylinder description shows the same behaviour as the 2D geometry.

Governing equations
We will in this chapter introduce the continuum approximation and basic hydrodynamic
equations. We will brieﬂy introduce the continuity equation and the Navier-Stokes equa-
tions which we will repeatedly use. We will also derive some simple ﬂow solutions and
how the solutions are normalised, which is particularly important in this thesis. First we
introduce the concept of ﬂuids.
The word ﬂuids originates from the Latin word ﬂuidus, which roughly means "to ﬂow".
Fluid dynamics is the description of kinematic eﬀects of for instances gases, liquids, and
plasmas. These are phases of matter and an important diﬀerence from the characteristics
of solids consist in the deformation under external forces. A ﬂuid will deform and not
return under small external forces, whereas a solid will deform but return to its initial
form. The form of a ﬂuid is also described by the container surrounding it whereas solid is
not. Some ﬂuids are said to have free surfaces such as liquid, where the container does not
specify its form. Gasses do not usually have free surfaces. Liquids and gasses have some
important diﬀerent characteristics: they diﬀer in density and the in molecule interaction.
The molecules in liquids are closely packed with distance of the order of an atom radius,
comparable to solids. The molecules in gasses do on the other hand interact much less
and the distance between them is much larger making the density approximately one per
mille of the liquid. Due to the highly dense packing of molecules liquids are to a good
approximation incompressible and are often for practical reasons treated that way.
2.1
The continuum approximation
A ﬂuid consists of many small molecules and the continuum description, which will be
introduced in the following, only holds down to a certain limit.
At very small length
scales one can measure the impact of every single molecule moving around. Increasing
the length scale of interest will decrease the impact of every singe molecule. At a given
length scale there will be so many molecules moving randomly that an average description
is appropriated and hence we have reach the continuum description.
We consider the
molecule distance in a liquid to be λ = 0.3 nm and hence its volume is V = λ3. We describe
the total number of particles with N, the standard deviation is described by

printer works the following way; a platform was lowered down into a bath of a ﬂuid called

where the laser hits the resin. The platform is then lifted up again pilling oﬀthe layer

ers can be several thousands, depending on the size of the structure and resolution. The
Form 1+ can print features down to 300 microns and a layer thickness between 25 and
200 microns [16]. The software OpenSCAD was used to make the 3D CAD models of the
chip. OpenSCAD does not have a graphical user interface editor like the CAD software
AutoCAD. Instead it is based on a script ﬁle which makes it possible to deﬁne structures
by mathematical expressions.

Extended Lubrication Theory
In microﬂuidic systems where viscous forces dominate most ﬂows analytical or approxi-
mated solutions are often already known. We have already met some cases; the parallel
plates and the axis-symmetric cylinder. However, small perturbations in the cross-section
of the channel can lead to complex ﬂow patterns and exact analytical solutions are no
longer available. One way to deal with this problem is the application of lubrication the-
ory. Lubrication theory is build on the assumption that the change of the height is much
smaller than the length of the channel. We will in this chapter ﬁrst give a more general
introduction to lubrication theory.
We will then derive equations from the lubrication
theory in some speciﬁc cases, namely the parallel plates and the cylinder.

5.1
The Finite Element Method
The Finite Element Method is a comprehensive tool for solving both ordinary and partial
diﬀerential equations. It is build on the idea of making a discretization of many coupled
diﬀerential equations. The idea is to split the domain into many small sub-domains, also
called elements. The grid can have many diﬀerent geometries depending on the problem of
interest. Each corner of a grid is call a node. A basic function (sometimes also called a test
function) is associated with each of these node. The basic function takes the value 1 on
the node and 0 on the neighbouring node. The basis function is a local approximation to
the real solution and can in principle have any form but usually polynomials are preferred
since they are easy for the computer to both diﬀerentiate and integrate. The accuracy of
the solution depend on the order of the polynomial.

The Finite Element Method is in many ways similar to another numerical method: the
Finite Diﬀerence Method (FDM). In many cases the problem can solve using both methods.

solve using FDM. Here FEM has its great advantages; the problem can be solved for
arbitrary geometry of interest. The already mentioned test functions takes the value one
on the nodes and zero on the neighbouring nodes. These nodes form what we call a mesh.
Probably the most used mesh takes the form of triangles. This is seen in Fig. 5.1. The
geometry of the mesh can be optimised to the speciﬁc problem but usually triangles will
do the job. Using FEM software like COMSOL the triangles automatically adopt to the
geometry of the problem.

6.2.1
Comparison with anti-symmetric channel
We have compared the symmetric and anti-symmetric channels with the same zeroth order
pressure drop in Chapter 3, see Fig. 3.9. The zeroth order pressure drop does not depend on
any derivatives of the shape function but only the functions itself. Derivatives of the shape
function are represented in both second order and fourth order pressure drop contributions.
This means that viscosity to zeroth order is only due to the height of the channel whereas
viscosity to higher orders is also due to the shape of the perturbation of the channel.
In most cases the pressure drop will be dominated by viscosity due to the height of the
channel. Comparing two channels with same zeroth order pressure drop enables to get a
more isolated impression of the impact of the shape.

7.2
Compare optimisation with earlier obtained results
The idea behind an optimised channel is not to ﬁnd a geometry of a channel with the
less resistance. Such a channel does not physically exist because it would have inﬁnite
width. However, what we have learned and seen repeatedly is that the hydraulic resistance
approach a saturation point. What we have just seen from the optimisation is that at a
certain point there is not much more to win in resistance decrease.
We have also associated this opening with a critical angle. For the opening cosine func-
tion we deﬁned the critical angle as θ = 50◦which corresponds to the amplitude parameter
λ = 1.35. The volume of the channel with λ for the opening cosine function, Eq. (6.19), is
V/V0 = 1.675. The optimum found from calculus of variations was V/V0 = 1.67. The an-
alytical ﬂow rate from the cosine opening angle is signiﬁcantly smaller than the optimised
channel but the numerical simulations do not diﬀer much from each other. As expected
the ﬂow rate is lower for the cosine channel than the optimised channel. For the tangent
hyperbolic function, with the constant zeroth order ﬂow rate, Fig. 6.14 suggest that for an
opening angle we could expect the ﬂow rate to have reached at least 95% of the physical
possible maximum ﬂow rate.
Many optimisation parameters are in play in the design of the biological micropores. It
is therefore not possible to understand the structure of biological micropores only from the
analysis in the thesis. We may, however, conclude some qualitatively behaviour of biological
micropores from the opening angle analysis. The opening angle and the optimised channel
structure we have found is in the order of magnitude in agreement with the structure of the
bordered pit and the plasmodesmata presented in Fig. 1.1. The two channels are expanding
their radii with about three times, which was also roughly the behaviour of the optimised
channel structure in Fig. 7.2. As mentioned many other parameters are important for
the micropore. We have for instant not taking the porous membrane in bordered pit into
account.

Conclusion
We have build and developed an experimental device, a channel chip, that could simulate
the ﬂuid ﬂow in micro channel systems. The structure and shape of the channel in the chip
could be changed so that in principle many diﬀerent shapes could be measured. The chip
consisted of three pieces printed by a 3D printer. The pieces were printed individually and
assembled afterwards with screws. The primary challenge was to keep the chip leak-prove.
The hydraulic resistance in the device was found by measuring the ﬂow rate and the pres-
sure at the inlet and outlet. The channel had an characteristic length about 1 mm which
is three order of magnitude higher than in some biological micropores, e.g. some bordered
pit. Therefore a silicone oil with viscosity η = 1 Pa s was used instead of water to decrease
the Reynolds number. Experiments on channels with ﬁnite width parallel plates were used
to validate the setup. It was found that the results were consistent with theory for channel
with height up to 1 mm.
The theory of classical lubrication theory, formulated by Reynolds, was derived and
expressions for the pressure drop, ﬂow and resistance for some speciﬁc channel structures
were found.
We have numerical validated the classical lubrication theory in situations
of low ratio between height and length. It was experimentally proven that the classical
lubrication theory is no longer valid for this ratio approaching the value one. This mo-
tivated us to further develop the classical lubrication theory with the so-called extended
lubrication theory. The lubrication theory was expanded to second and fourth order in δ
for a channel with a inﬁnite parallel plate geometry and axis-symmetric cylinder geometry.
Comparison with numerical simulation for the examples of a cosine structure showed that
the extended lubrication theory worked better for the parallel plates geometry than the
axis-symmetric cylinder. The resistance for cylinder geometry depends on the inverse ra-
dius to the fourth order whereas the parallel plates only depend in the inverse height cubed.
It was argued that this was the reason why lubrication theory worked best for the parallel
plates. Comparison of the extended lubrication theory and experimental results showed
that the extended lubrication theory gave a much better prediction of the resistance than
the classical lubrication theory.
We studied lubrication theory for a channel with a cosine and a tangent hyperbolic
structure in order to understand the importance of the geometrical angle for the resis-
tance. This was compared with an optimisation analysis made from theory of calculus of
variations. From this analysis a critical opening angle for the channel was deﬁned, θ = 50◦.
67
==PAGE:82==
An optimised volume was also deﬁned as 1.67 times the volume of a straight channel. The
angle can help in understanding the physical design criteria for biological micropores. We
have compared the solution to the optimised channel with real-world example. Qualitative
analysis shows that the angle might very well give part of the explanation of the character-
istic of some biological micropore geometries, e.g. the bordered pit and the plasmodesmata.
