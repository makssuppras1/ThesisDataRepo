Abstract
Two-dimensional (2D) transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) have attracted sig-
nificant attention in recent years due to their unique electronic and optical properties,
stemming from their atomically thin nature and strong light-matter interactions [1, 2].
Monolayer tungsten diselenide (WSe2), a member of the TMDC family, has emerged
as a promising material for various optoelectronic applications, such as photodetec-
tors, light-emitting diodes, and solar cells. One of the key features of monolayer WSe2
is the presence of tightly bound excitons, which are electron-hole pairs that play a
crucial role in determining the optical and electronic properties of the material. In
this study we investigate the electrical tuning of excitons in planar monolayer WSe2
by applying an external bias at room temperature with atmospheric pressure and
at 4K with vacuum, with the aim of achieving control over their optical properties.
Using a combination of micro-photoluminescence spectroscopy (PL) and differential
reflectivity we demonstrate the ability to control exciton and charged excitons.
This work contributes to the fundamental understanding of exciton dynamics in
two-dimensional materials and has significant implications for the design of tunable
optoelectronic devices, sensors and quantum technologies [3–5].

Motivation
The emerging field of two-dimensional (2D) materials, particularly transition metal
dichalcogenides (TMDCs) such as monolayer WSe2, has attracted significant atten-
tion due to their remarkable optical and electronic properties. These materials exhibit
strong excitonic effects, which are crucial for the performance of various optoelectronic
devices. However, the ability to manipulate exciton properties in a controllable man-
ner remains a challenge that needs to be addressed to unlock the full potential of
these materials.
The motivation behind this study is to explore the possibility of electrical tuning
of excitons in monolayer WSe2, which could pave the way for a new class of tunable
optoelectronic devices with improved performance and versatility. As mentioned, we
worked with simple planar WSe2; moreover, quantum emitters can be produced if
strain is induced to the monolayer and eventually electrically tuned as the simple
planar WSe2. Luckily, one of our samples got involuntarily strained and a shallow
optical study was performed.
Our question was how the photoluminescence (PL) behaves under different excita-
tion power of the LED and by applying certain voltages. By examining the electrical
tuning of excitons in monolayer WSe2, this research aims to contribute to the grow-
ing body of knowledge in the field and inspire further investigation into the unique
properties and potential applications of 2D materials.

1.3
Transition Metal Dicalchogenides (TMDCs)
The first question I got was: why TMDC? In recent years, transition metal dichalco-
genides (TMDs or TMDCs) have emerged as a highly promising and versatile class
of materials with exciting applications in numerous fields, ranging from electronics to
energy storage and catalysis. These materials possess unique electronic, optical, me-
chanical, and chemical properties, which are closely linked to their atomic structures
and composition. This introduction aims to provide a comprehensive understanding
of TMDs, their structure, properties, and potential applications.
1.3.1
Composition and Structure
TMDs are a class of layered materials consisting of a transition metal atom (such as
Mo, W, or Re) sandwiched between two chalcogen atoms (typically S, Se, or Te) (see
Figure 1.1: Periodic table showing the various possibilities of transition metals, M,
and calchogenides, X. Bottom part of the figure shows the structure of WSe2 in 3D
==PAGE:14==
4
1 Introduction
Figure 1.1). The general chemical formula for TMDs is MX2, where M represents the
transition metal and X represents the chalcogen [10]. Among all the choices of transi-
tion metals group VI are of particular interest, specifically those containing tungsten
(W) and molybdenum (Mo), because they exhibit remarkable stability and they have
a tunable electronic and optical property which will be discussed in Chapter 4. The
stability of these TMDs can be attributed to the following factors:
• Strong Covalent Bonding: Group 6 TMDs, such as MoS2, WS2, MoSe2, and
WSe2, are characterized by strong covalent bonding between the transition
metal (Mo or W) and the chalcogen atoms (S, Se, or Te) within the layers
(see Figure 1.1). This strong in-plane bonding results in robust and stable lay-
ered structures that can withstand harsh environments and resist degradation
[11].
• Electronic Configuration: The electronic configuration of group 6 transition
metals, including Mo and W, results in a filled d-orbital, which contributes to
their chemical stability. This full occupancy of d-orbitals reduces their propen-
sity to participate in chemical reactions, enhancing their overall stability [10,
12].
• Thermodynamic Stability: Group 6 TMDs exhibit high thermodynamic sta-
bility, which is a crucial factor determining the performance of materials in
various applications. For instance, MoS2 and WS2 have been found to be sta-
ble over a wide temperature range, making them suitable for applications in
high-temperature environments [7].
The crystal structure of TMDs is characterized by strong in-plane covalent bond-
ing between the metal and the calchogen atoms and weaker out-of-plane van der
Waals interactions between the layers (see Figure 1.1) [11]. Depending on the stack-
ing order we can divide them into different structures or phases: 2H, 3R and 1T (see
Figure 1.2). WSe2 is typically found in the 2H phase, which is a hexagonal (trigonal
prismatic) structure. In this 2H phase the layers are stacked in an ABAB pattern
with weak van der Waals interactions between the layers. The 2H phase is the most
thermodynamically stable and common structure for WSe2. In the ABAB stacking
sequence, the layers are arranged in such a way that atoms in the second layer (B) are
positioned over the centers of the hexagons formed by the atoms in the first layer (A).
When the third layer (A) is added, its atoms are aligned directly above the atoms in
the first layer (A), and so on. The pattern continues as the layers stack, creating an
ABAB sequence [13, 14].

they were at different k-points.

in the overlap of electronic states from adjacent layers. However, when the dimension
of a TMD material, such as WSe2, is reduced, it can lead to several important effects
on the bandgap energy, including changes in the density of states, the effective mass
of charge carriers, and the quantum confinement effect. First, the density of states
near the band edges can be affected by the reduction in dimension. In a bulk material,

The quantization of energy levels in confined materials arises from the wave-like be-
havior of electrons and the resulting standing wave patterns that are formed in a
confined region. When a material is confined in one or more dimensions, such as
in a thin-film or a nanoparticle, the electronic wavefunctions are forced to adopt a
specific set of boundary conditions at the boundaries of the confined region. These
boundary conditions lead to the formation of standing wave patterns, which have
discrete energy levels. This is because the electrons in the standing wave must have
a specific wavelength that fits within the confined region and satisfies the boundary
conditions. This confinement leads to a quantization of the electronic energy levels,
which reduces the number of available energy states for the charge carriers to occupy.
As a result, the density of states near the band edges can be significantly reduced,
which can increase the bandgap energy of the material. Second, the reduction in di-
mension can lead to an increase in the effective mass of charge carriers. This increase
in effective mass arises because the wavefunction of the electron or hole is confined
to a smaller volume, and the probability of the electron or hole being located in a
specific region of space is increased. This confinement makes it more diﬀicult for the
electron or hole to move, and it responds less strongly to external fields, resulting in
a larger effective mass. The confinement-induced increase in effective mass can lead
to an increase in the band gap. Moreover, the heavier effective mass can also lead to
reduced mobility and slower carrier transport in the material. And third, regarding
the reduction in the density of states, this is a consequence of the confinement of the
electronic wavefunctions in the vertical direction. When the dimensions of a material

are reduced, the electronic wavefunctions become confined in the vertical direction,
which reduces the volume of the material and the number of electrons that can occupy
it. The reduction in the volume of the material and the number of electrons that can
occupy it leads to a reduction in the density of states, which is a measure of the
number of available electronic states per unit energy. In other words, the density of
states represents the probability of finding an electron at a particular energy level in
the material. When the dimensions of a material are reduced, the confinement of the
electronic wavefunctions leads to a quantization of the energy levels, which results in
a reduction in the number of available electronic states per unit energy. This reduc-
tion in the number of available electronic states per unit energy leads to a reduction
in the density of states and an increase band gap. For example, in thin-films of WSe2,
the energy levels can become discrete due to the quantum confinement effect, which
leads to a reduction in the density of states near the band edges and an increase in the
bandgap energy. From literature, bulk WSe2 is typically reported to be around 1.4 -
1.6 eV [7] , depending on the exact experimental conditions and the specific sample
being measured. However, for a monolayer of WSe2, the bandgap energy has been
reported to be around 1.6 - 1.7 eV, which is slightly higher than the bulk value. This
increase in bandgap energy for monolayer WSe2 is due to the quantum confinement
effect mentioned before, which leads to an increase in the energy spacing between the
electronic energy levels [7] [17].
We have discussed that decreasing the layers of WSe2 will increase the effective mass
of the charge carriers which can lead to a weaker PL response. This is because an
increase in effective mass can make it more diﬀicult for the charge carriers to move
through the material and recombine to emit light. As a result, the radiative recom-
bination rate (which is responsible for the emission of light) decreases, leading to a
weaker PL response. In WSe2 monolayers, the effective mass of both electrons and
holes increases due to quantum confinement effects. However, despite this increase in
effective mass, WSe2 monolayers have been observed to exhibit a strong PL response.
The reason for this behavior is not fully understood, but it may be related to several
factors that can affect the PL properties of monolayer WSe2, including:
• Material quality: The quality of the monolayer WSe2 can significantly impact
its PL properties. High-quality materials with fewer defects and impurities can
exhibit stronger PL signals despite the increased effective mass of the charge
carriers [19].
• Strain engineering: By applying strain to the monolayer WSe2, it is possible to
modulate its electronic and optical properties. This can potentially improve the
PL signal by affecting the charge carrier dynamics and recombination processes
[20]
• Doping and external gating: Doping the monolayer WSe2 or applying external
gate voltages can help in tuning its Fermi level, which in turn can influence the
charge carrier density and mobility, and consequently the PL signal strength
[21, 22]

• Exciton effects: Monolayer WSe2 is known for its strong excitonic effects due

• Environmental factors: The surrounding environment, such as the choice of sub-

1.4
Excitons
Excitons are bound electron-hole pairs that arise in semiconductors, insulators, and
some organic materials due to the Coulomb interaction between an electron and a
hole. They were first introduced by Russian physicist Yakov Frenkel in 1931 [24]. In
a simplified way, an exciton can be visualized as a hydrogen-like system, where an
electron is attracted to a hole, similar to an electron bound to a proton in a hydrogen
atom. Excitons can be classified into two main types: Wannier-Mott excitons and
Frenkel excitons. Wannier-Mott excitons are typically found in inorganic semiconduc-
tors and have a larger spatial extent (tens to hundreds of nanometers), lower binding
energy (a few meV to tens of meV), and are more sensitive to the dielectric properties
of the material [25]. Frenkel excitons, on the other hand, are more commonly found
in organic materials and some inorganic insulators, have a smaller spatial extent (less
than 1 nm), and higher binding energy (hundreds of meV to a few eV) [26].
The formation of excitons is closely related to the absorption and emission of light
in semiconducting materials. When a material absorbs a photon with energy equal
to or greater than the bandgap, an electron can be promoted from the valence band
to the conduction band, creating an electron-hole pair. The electron and hole can
then interact through the Coulomb force and form an exciton. The recombination
of an exciton can lead to the emission of a photon with energy slightly lower than
the bandgap energy due to the exciton’s binding energy (Eexciton = Egap−Ebinding
Figure 1.4). However, the exciton does not necessarily form at the very same instant
the electron is excited. Let’s break down the process step by step:
1. When a photon with energy equal to or greater than the bandgap is absorbed by
the material, it promotes an electron from the valence band to the conduction

band, creating an electron-hole pair. At this point, the energy of the electron-
hole pair is equal to the bandgap energy (Eg) and they are stil not bounded.
2. The electron wants to return to a lower energy state (ground state), so it will
tend to move closer to the hole, attracted by the Coulomb force. The electron
and hole are not bound together as an exciton from the very beginning. They
are initially independent quasi-particles that interact with each other due to
their opposite charges.
3. As the electron and hole move closer, their attractive interaction gets stronger,
and at some point, they can form a bound state, which is the exciton. The
exciton is characterized by a lower total energy state compared to the initial,
separate electron-hole pair.
The reason we don’t say Eexciton = Egap is that the exciton represents a bound
state of the electron-hole pair with lower energy compared to the initial, separate
electron-hole pair. When the exciton forms, energy is released in the form of the
exciton binding energy (Eb).
Therefore, we express the energy of the exciton as:
Eexciton = Egap−Ebinding
The key point is that the exciton is a bound state with lower energy than the
initial electron-hole pair created by the absorption of a photon with energy equal to
the bandgap. The formation of the exciton is a dynamic process where the electron
and hole move closer together, attracted by the Coulomb force, and eventually form
a bound state with lower total energy.
Spin-orbit coupling also plays an important role in the optical properties of excitons
in materials with heavy atoms, such as WSe2. The physical process arises due to the
interaction between the spin and orbital motion of electrons in a material, and can
lead to a splitting of energy levels and the mixing of spin and orbital states. The spin-
splitting in TMDs is particularly significant because it leads to the formation of two
types of excitons (A-excitons and B-excitons) which arise from different spin configu-
rations of the valence and conduction bands [28]. In TMDCs, at the band edges, there
are multiple local extrema (i.e., valleys) in the Brillouin zone, typically labeled as K

excitons in TMDs. For example, spin-orbit coupling can affect the selection rules for
the radiative recombination of excitons and the orientation of the emitted photons.
The study of excitons has led to the development of various optoelectronic devices
and technologies. For example, the discovery of exciton-polaritons in microcavities
has opened up new avenues for research in quantum information processing.
Ad-
ditionally, the investigation of excitons in two-dimensional (2D) materials, such as
TMDs, has revealed unique optical properties, strong light-matter interactions, and
potential applications in valleytronics [18, 32].

1.5
Trions
Trions are charged quasi-particles that consist of two electrons and one hole or two
holes and one electron (see Figure 1.6).
They arise in semiconductors, especially
in two-dimensional (2D) materials, including monolayer WSe2 [32, 33]. Trions are
formed due to the Coulomb interaction between the charged particles, and their
properties are significantly influenced by factors such as the dielectric environment,
temperature, and carrier density [33, 34].
Figure 1.6: Process of trion formation where an electron is captured by a bounded
electron-hole pair, an exciton. In this case, the trion is charged negatively which leads
to a negatively charged exciton (A−)
In monolayer TMDs like WSe2, the strong quantum confinement and reduced
dielectric screening lead to enhanced many-body interactions, resulting in the forma-
tion of trions with relatively large binding energies. These trions can be observed in
the photoluminescence (PL) spectra of the material, where they appear as a distinct
peak at a lower energy compared to the neutral exciton peak [28].
Reduced dielectric screening is a phenomenon that occurs in low-dimensional ma-
terials, such as two-dimensional (2D) materials and nanostructures, where the ability
of the material to screen or attenuate electrostatic interactions is weakened. In a
bulk material there are plenty of polarizable charges surrounding the charge carriers
or an electron-hole pair, which eﬀiciently screens the Couloumb interaction between
them.
In contrast, in low-dimensional materials, the number of those polarizable
charges is significantly reduced due to the quantum confinement and consequently
the dielectric screening is weaker compared to bulk. This has several consequences
on the electronic properties of low-dimensional materials such as the effect of large
binding energies because the weaker screening allows for stronger attractive interac-
tions between electrons and holes, leading to more tightly bound quasi-particles [34,
35].

2.2
Mechanical exfoliation and transfer
WSe2 monolayers can be created or obtained using a variety of techniques, I will briefly
introduce a few however, the one I used in this thesis is mechanical exfoliation.
• Chemical vapor deposition (CVD): In this method, WSe2 monolayers are grown
on a substrate by exposing it to a mixture of precursors in a chemical vapor
deposition reactor. The precursors react to form a thin film of WSe2 on the sub-
strate. The quality and thickness of the WSe2 monolayer can be controlled by
adjusting the growth conditions (such as temperature, pressure, and precursor
flow rate) [36]
• Molecular beam epitaxy (MBE): This method is similar to CVD, but it al-
lows for more precise control over the growth conditions. In MBE, individual
molecules of the precursors are deposited onto the substrate, allowing for precise
control over the thickness and quality of the WSe2 monolayer [37]
• Mechanical exfoliation: This is the simplest method for creating WSe2 mono-
layers. In this process, a bulk crystal of WSe2 is exfoliate it with scotch tape
(blue tape) to peel off single layers and transfer it to a target substrate which
in our case is PDMS. The number of layers can be controlled by repeating the
exfoliation process multiple times [38, 39].
The advantages in using mechanical exfoliation are:
• The simplicity of the process, which does not require a specialized reactor or
deposition system.
• The ability to obtain high-quality monolayers with minimal defects.
• The ability to obtain small amounts of material for characterization and testing.
It is probably the first method used to prepare graphene [39] and it is widely accepted
that this method yields a fair size (a few micrometers to several tens of micrometers
[38]). Nevertheless, the problem of this method is its randomness the diﬀiculty to
obtain high-quality monolayers with minimal defects, such as wrinkles, folds, and
cracks.
These defects can be introduced during the exfoliation process, and they
can reduce the yield of high-quality monolayers. After exfoliating lots of flakes and
taking into account different variables such as speed of the peeling and the direction
of the mechanical force (Figure 2.2), to increase the yield of high-quality monolayers
using mechanical exfoliation, it is important to carefully select the bulk crystal and

resulting in the emission of a photon. The energy of the emitted photon is the differ-
ence between the energies of the electron and hole states. This type of recombination
can be either radiative (emits light) or non-radiative (dissipates energy as heat) and
it is quite fast. When an exciton recombines, it can emit a photon with an energy
that corresponds to the exciton’s binding energy plus the energy difference between
the electron and hole states. This process is also known as excitonic emission. To
determine the origin of the peaks in a PL spectrum, you can consider the following
strategies [43, 44]:
• Temperature dependence: Excitonic emission generally has a stronger tempera-
ture dependence compared to electron-hole recombination. As the temperature
increases, the exciton binding energy decreases, leading to a redshift of the ex-
citon emission peak. Comparing the PL spectra at different temperatures can
help you identify exciton-related peaks.
• Time-resolved PL measurements: Time-resolved PL can provide information
on the lifetimes of the emission processes. Typically, excitonic emission has a
shorter lifetime compared to free electron-hole recombination, which can help
differentiate the two processes.
• External perturbations: Applying external perturbations such as voltage can
affect the exciton binding energy or electron-hole recombination rates. By an-
alyzing how the PL peaks change under these conditions, you can gain insight
into the underlying processes.

the energies required to create excitons in the material. When photons with energies
matching the excitonic resonances are absorbed by the WSe2 monolayer, the exci-
tons can be excited and their subsequent recombination can lead to the emission of
photons with energies equal to the excitonic resonance energies. In a reflectivity mea-
surement, the incident light is typically composed of photons with a range of energies
or wavelengths. When the incident light interacts with the WSe2 monolayer, some of
the photons with energies matching the excitonic resonances can be absorbed by the
excitons, leading to a reduction in the reflected light intensity at the corresponding
energies. This reduction in reflectivity at the excitonic resonance energies can be used
to identify the energies of the excitonic resonances and to obtain information on the
excitonic properties of the material. In the case of WSe2 monolayers, the A-exciton
resonance energy is approximately 1.66 eV or 747 nm in wavelength [42]. Therefore,
in a reflectivity spectrum of a WSe2 monolayer, one would expect to see a reduction
in reflectivity at energies corresponding to 1.66 eV or 747 nm. The depth of the reduc-
tion in reflectivity at the A-exciton resonance energy can provide information on the
strength of the excitonic resonance and the exciton binding energy in the material.
To perform the reflectivity measurement, we collect incident light which is directed
onto the sample at a specific angle of incidence, in our case is 0º. The reflected light
is then collected and directed into the spectrometer for measurement. To accurately
measure the reflectivity of the sample, it is important to have a reference reflectiv-
ity measurement. This is typically done by measuring the reflectivity of a highly
reflective surface, such as a silver mirror or a reference material with known optical
properties, at the same angle of incidence and under the same experimental conditions
as the sample. The choice of the excitation light for differential reflectivity is another
parameter to be aware of. In general, the excitation light should have energies close
to or above the bandgap energy of the sample, which for WSe2 is approximately 1.66
eV or 747 nm in wavelength. In our case, we choose red-green (500 - 800 nm) If the
goal of the differential reflectivity measurement is to study the excitonic properties of
the WSe2 monolayer, such as the energies and strengths of the excitonic resonances,
then it may be beneficial to use a light source with energies close to the excitonic
resonance energies. However, it is important to note that using light sources with
higher energies can also lead to interesting effects, such as increased carrier densities.
Therefore, the choice of the excitation light should take into account the specific goals
of the experiment and the desired outcomes. It is also important to avoid saturation,
that is why it is a well practice to start by taking measurements knowing saturation
do not occur. In reflectivity, it might happen with our reference target, silver mirror.
Thus, first measure the reflectivity of our reference.

It can exhibit this behaviour due
several reasons [53]:
• Reduced thermal broadening: At lower temperatures, the thermal broadening
of the PL peaks is reduced, allowing for better resolution of the individual peaks.
This can make it easier to distinguish the A0 and A+ peaks in the spectrum.
• Enhanced exciton binding energy: At low temperatures, the exciton binding en-
ergy is typically enhanced due to reduced dielectric screening. This can increase
the stability of excitons and trions, resulting in more pronounced PL peaks for
these species.
• Reduced non-radiative recombination: Non-radiative recombination processes,
such as phonon-assisted recombination, are suppressed at low temperatures.
This can lead to an increase in the radiative recombination eﬀiciency of excitons
and trions, resulting in stronger PL peaks.
• Increased carrier localization: At low temperatures, carriers can become more
localized due to the reduction of thermal energy. This can increase the proba-
bility of trion formation, leading to an enhanced A+ peak compared to RT.
At the same time, the PL is blue-shifted compared to the one at room temperature.
This can be caused by several factors [3]:
• Exciton-exciton interactions: At low temperatures, exciton-exciton interactions
become more significant. These interactions can cause repulsive forces between
excitons, resulting in a blue-shift of the PL peak position.
• Phonon interactions: At low temperatures, the phonon population is reduced,
leading to less phonon-assisted recombination. This can cause a blue-shift in
the PL peak position as non-phonon-assisted recombination becomes more dom-
inant.
• Localized states: At low temperatures, localized states such as defects or impu-
rities can become more important in the PL process. These localized states can
have different binding energies and contribute to blue-shifted PL peaks.
• Burstein-Moss effect: At low temperatures, the Burstein-Moss effect can be
more pronounced. This effect occurs when the Fermi level moves into the con-
duction band due to increased carrier concentration, resulting in a blue-shift of
the PL emission.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusions
In conclusion, this research focused on the electrical tuning of WSe2 monolayers and
bilayers at room temperature and 4K. We found that the photoluminescence (PL)
properties of WSe2 are significantly influenced by varying temperatures and electrical
environments, which have important implications for its potential applications in
optoelectronics and quantum technologies.
At room temperature, injecting holes and electrons into WSe2 monolayers resulted
in changes in PL intensity, peak position, and linewidth. The observed PL modulation
is attributed to a combination of factors, including the formation of trions, exciton-
exciton interactions, and screening effects. The injection of carriers also resulted in a
logarithmic growth of the PL intensity, which can be attributed to local heating effects
and the dynamic equilibrium between carrier injection and recombination processes.
At low temperatures (4K), the WSe2 monolayer exhibited multiple PL peaks that
can be attributed to various excitonic species, defect-related emissions, and localized
excitons. Additionally, the PL spectra showed a blueshift at 4K, which can be at-
tributed to factors such as exciton-exciton interactions, reduced phonon population,
and the Burstein-Moss effect. Injecting carriers at 4K further modified the PL proper-
ties of WSe2, with changes in peak positions and intensities related to the formation
of new excitonic complexes, localized states, and potential quantum emitters.
The bilayer WSe2 exhibited distinct PL behavior compared to the monolayer,
with the presence of interlayer excitons and modified band structure contributing to
the observed differences. Injecting holes and electrons into the bilayer resulted in
different PL responses, highlighting the unique carrier dynamics in this system.
This research has provided valuable insights into the electrical tuning of WSe2
and its potential applications in optoelectronics and quantum technologies. Future
work could focus on elucidating the role of defects, strain, and local environments on
the PL properties of WSe2, as well as investigating the potential of WSe2 for use in
novel devices, such as single-photon emitters and tunable light sources.
The findings of our study have significant implications for the understanding and
application of WSe2 in optoelectronic devices, quantum information processing, and
other emerging technologies. Our work highlights the importance of temperature and
defect engineering in modulating the properties of WSe2 and its optical response .
Future research directions could include time-resolved PL measurements and varying
the applied voltage to verify the mechanisms behind the observed PL changes. Addi-
tionally, exploring other 2D materials, heterostructures, and experimental conditions
