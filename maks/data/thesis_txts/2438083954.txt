Abstract 
 
The aim of this master’s thesis was to investigate the indoor environment and energy consumption of 63 
apartments in Block 3 located in Korngården, Ballerup. The purpose was to investigate if its occupants 
changed behavior after renovation, and a comparison between the results of the model before and after 
renovation. The method was to determine a relation between the indoor and outdoor temperature in each 
apartment for the period from May 2016 to May 2017. The measured indoor temperature from Keep 
Focus was used for the correlation. The determined correlations were defined in the IDA ICE simulations 
program. in the controller for the “ideal heater” for the model before and after renovation. The model 
before renovation was simulated for the period from January 1st, 2014 to Dec 31st, 2014. The model after 
renovation was simulated for the period May 2016 to May 2017. 
The results for the model before renovation showed that the occupants had changed behavior after 
renovation. The deviation between the simulated and measured energy consumption was 49%. The 
correlation between the simulated and measured energy consumption showed that the simulated 
consumption level was twice as big as the measured energy consumption. The deviation for the model 
after renovation was 31%. The deviation should be much smaller because the simulated period was the 
same, like the period for the defined linear equations in the controller for the ideal heater. The deviation in 
the model after renovation cause the defined linear equations for the relations between the indoor and 
outdoor temperature. 
The relation between the measured and simulated operative temperature in the model after renovation 
wasn’t linear for all apartments. The highest correlation was for Apartment 26 which had a number of 
determination at R2= 0.7662, and the slope was 0.9522. The correlation was different between the 
apartments. That caused the difference in the measured indoor temperature and the difference in the 
defined correlation between the indoor and outdoor temperature for each apartment.  
The conclusion was that the occupants changed behavior after renovation based on the method for using 
the correlation between indoor and outdoor temperature for the period between May 2016 and May 2017.

1. Introduction  
An individual’s indoor environment is a very important factor in our lives. Most people spend much 
of their time indoors. The energy consumption was another important factor, which had big influence 
on the climate, environment and the occupants. When the apartments had low energy consumption. 
then the occupants will save money, doesn’t exacerbate the level of CO2 emissions nor damage the 
environment. In this project the focus was on a simulation of 63 apartments in Korngården in 
Ballerup before and after renovation. Korngården consist of 4 blocks, where here the focus was only 
on block 3; apartment from building 84 to 98 on all levels. These apartments had been renovated in 
2015. The apartments would not be identified by their real number in this report, due to new occupant 
security rules. They would be given a number from 1-63.  
 
Figure 1.Site plan for blocks in Korngården  [11]. 
The buildings in Korngården had many technical problems and structural damage, therefore, the decision 
was taken for it to undergo a huge renovation. Keep Focus had placed one instrument in each apartment 
to measure the indoor temperature on an hourly basis. The instruments measured data from the period 
between May 2016 and May 2017. This indoor temperature of each apartment was taken after renovation 
of Block 3. The only data before renovation was the energy consumption of all apartments.  
The primary purpose of this project was to investigate if the occupants of Block 3 changed behavior 
before and after renovation, based on the measured indoor temperature from the period between May 
2016 and May 2017. The data was used to determine a relation between indoor and outdoor temperature, 
modeling that relation, and then simulating the model before and after renovation. The second purpose 
was to investigate whether occupants have an influence on energy consumption and indoor climate, as 
well as the factors that may influence the difference between the measured and simulated values. Finally, 
a comparison of the energy consumption of the simulated model before and after renovation was 
conducted. 
All apartments were simulated with the correct conditions: heating, ventilation, occupant number, light 
and building construction, based on the material provided by Keep Focus and Dominia A/S. The climate 
fil was also made for the simulation period, the year 2014 and between May 2016 and May 2017, based 
on the data from DMI and DTU Climate Station. 
In this report the method was first presented, then a chapter about Korngården. followed by a literature 
review. The next chapter was about the measurements from Keep Focus, which were analyzed. Then the 
second part of the method was presented, which was a description of the IDA ICE simulation. Finally, the 
results were presented followed by the discussion and conclusion.

2. Description of Korngården 
There are a total of 248 apartments in all four blocks, 63 of which are in Block 3. In 2006 energy labeling 
was prepared for all apartments in Korngården, by “Dansk alemennyttigt boligselskab”. The energy 
labeling includes an energy plan with proposals for making the apartments more energy efficient, as their 
levels of energy consumption were very high.  
 
Figure 2. Energy labeling for the energy consumption in all 4 blocks in Korngården [12]. 
Figure 2 shows illustration from the energy labeling for the heating, electricity and domestic hot water use 
in a scale form A-M, where A corresponds to the least consumption and M to the highest consumption. 
The predictions from the calculations showed a heating consumption at 88.8 kWh per m2 [12] giving it a 
B-rating on the scale, which, compared to the electricity consumption;7.9 kWh per m2 and a G-rating on 
the scale – is not bad.The block’s water consumption was also very high, at 0.90 m3 per m2 which results 
in a rating of F [12].  
The energy labeling also includes proposals for improving energy consumption. One of the proposals was 
a circulation pump, used to reduce the electricity and water consumption, which closes during some hours 
of the night to reduce usage, estimated to be around 15MWh of energy. There was also a proposal for 
changing incandescent bulbs to more energy efficient lighting in all staircases [12]. This will reduce 
around consumption by approximately 1.500 kWh of electricity, and with light sensors installed in the 
staircases, about 10.000 kWh of electricity would be saved [12].

3. A review of energy, indoor climate and occupant behavior 
 
Buildings within the European Union are responsible for 40 % of global energy consumption and 36 % of 
CO2 emissions [7]. This energy was most likely being used for heating, ventilation and lighting. The EU 
building directive aims to increase energy efficiency [1]. In Denmark there was tightened energy 
requirement for new construction and renovation. Buildings are required to have low energy consumption 
and make use of renewable energy. The EU directive has required that occupants need to receive 
information about their heating usage four times a year, to make them aware of their consumption and 
encourage them to save more energy [6]. 
3.1. 
Energy consumption 
Before starting the renovation of the buildings, is it a requirement for the Energy Agency to make energy 
labeling for the building. [4]. The Energy labeling contains suggestions for improving the energy of the 
building. After the renovation it can be used as a comparison, showing the energy consumption before 
and after renovation. The energy consumption was, theoretically, going to be less after the renovation was 
completed. However, the exact reduction in practice will not be the same as the calculations made on 
paper; there will be a deviation between the calculated and the eventual measured energy consumption [4]. 
Typically, this deviation was caused by the occupant’s individual energy consumption, and the level of 
deviation depends on each apartment/building[3]. 
 
Figur 6. The calculated and measured energy consumption pr. m2 residential, split in energy labeling [4].   
In the book, “Forskellen mellem målt og beregnet energiforbrug til opvarmning af parcelhuse,” Kirsten 
Gram Hansen [4] ,describes the difference between the calculated and measured energy consumption. In 
Figure 1, the diagram shows the deviation between the calculated and measured energy consumption, 
based on around 230.000 of energy labels. The low energy labeling has a higher spread/deviation than a 
high energy labeling. The second crucial factor the diagram shows, was the deviation in each result. This 
deviation was due to the different energy consumption in each apartment. In the book ( [4]) Hansen 
describes that it was possible to have different heating use in two apartments who have the same number

3.2. 
Occupant behavior 
An investigation of indoor environment was very important for human health, as most people spend more 
than 90 % of their time indoors [1]. The level of energy consumption was a significant factor in the indoor 
environment investigation [1]. The energy consumption was a value that indicates how much energy the 
building uses. The energy use in buildings is linked to the behavior of the occupants [1]. The occupant has 
the opportunity to open and close windows and turn lightning on and off, which has a direct influence on 
the energy consumption and the indoor environment[1]. The various occupants’ behavior was different 
from each other, depending on their social, cultural, physical and psychological background [1]. The 
temperature, humidity, air velocity, noise and illumination levels are factors in the physical environment 
that could lead the occupant to make the choices that they do [10]. Factors that have an indirect influence 
on occupant behavior defines the context of the investigation, while the insulation of the buildings, 
orientation of facades, heating systems and thermostat types are fixed variants [10].  
 
Figure 7. Flux diagram: from Drivers to Energy consumption and indoor environment [2]. 
The Flux diagram above was from a review by V. Fabi et al. (2012) [10] which describes the most 
important points that has an influence on indoor environment and energy consumption. The first point 
was ‘Drivers’, the factors leading the occupant to take action - like outdoor temperature, indoor 
temperature, rain or wind, etc. The second ‘Occupant stimoulus’ and the third ’Actions Scenarios’ are 
when the occupant’s behavior was observed, when they can control the indoor environment and energy

3.2.1. Simulations of occupant behavior 
 Ali Malkawi et al. [2] describes how to simulate occupant behavior in building energy programs. They 
propose a “Dynamic Schedule “where the human behaviors can be linked to building simulation 
programs. The user can link each behavior and its energy use. The Dynamic Schedule was a methodology 
guided by operation schedules, which ultimately reflect the load changes like occupant behavior [2]. 
 
Figure 8. A detailed process of the "Dynamic Schedule"[2]. 
The “Dynamic Schedule” consist of 3 processes: ‘Schedule Generation’, ‘Schedule Population’, and 
‘Simulation Program’ - and finally, the internal loads. The Schedule Generation consists of various 
occupant schedules like, lighting, HVAC, occupancy and so on. The schedule could be made for one 
week, day, hour, or month depending on the situation. The Schedule Population was input information for 
the building simulation program, and could include, for example, weather (climate) files for the whole 
year [2]. The “Real time” in this process describes a third type of input, which could be necessary for the 
simulations. Finally, the simulations will be run where the output was “internal load” its results of the 
energy consumption, where the occupant behavior was taken to consideration [2].

measurements was for hourly basis for one year.

The climate fil  
The weather values for the year 2014 was not available in DTU Climate Station. The values were 
therefore taken from DMI. The required values for the climate fil to IDA ICE were: air temperature, 
relative humidity, wind direction, sky cover and direct and diffuse solar radiation. DMI delivered all the 
data aside from the direct and diffuse solar radiation. Instead of that, they delivered the mean level of 
global solar radiation, which was the sum of direct and diffuse solar radiation. The method used to 
estimate an approximation of values for diffuse solar radiation was to use the sky cover for the 
calculation. The diffuse solar radiation was deemed to be the sky cover multiplied by the mean global 
solar radiation. The direct solar radiation was the mean global solar radiation minus the diffuse solar 
radiation. The climate file can be seen in Appendix B.

7. Discussion  
The discussion part consisted of discussion of the method and results of the model before and after 
renovation. In addition, the assumptions and considerations that had be taken were discussed. 
Method  
During the method it was crucial to find a correlation between indoor and outdoor temperature for the 
period between May 2016 and May 2017. Due to the large amount of data, it would be easy to shift the 
results relative to each other. Thus, the indoor temperature and outdoor temperature needed to be 
equivalent to the same time and date. The use of Vlookup in Excel was very crucial and resulted in one's 
time of indoor and outdoor temperature. Then the data could be illustrated in a graph to determine the 
crack in the scatterplot according to [4].  
To determine the crack point, no tools had been used, only a visual note of where the crack point was read 
out from the graph. Therefore, the method to find the crack point was not exact. This would affect the 
linear equations, which were found for the relation between the indoor and outdoor temperature for both 
heating and cooling season, figure 14. Therefore, it would affect the final result of the energy 
consumption. The data based on the linear equations was from May 2016 -May 2017, because there 
weren’t indoor temperatures measured for 2014. The assumption was that the occupants that were living 
in Korngården were still the same people, and they lived in the same apartment after renovation. 
Therefore, the relation found between indoor temperature for May 2016 and May 2017 was also used for 
the model before renovation (2014). Then, it was possible to investigate if the occupants changed 
behavior after renovation based on the result of energy consumption. 
IDA ICE simulation  
The electricity data in the model before renovation was taken from the measured data from May 2016 to 
May 2017. Electricity consumption was not necessarily the same before and after renovation. After 
renovation the new windows and the establishment of balconies, would result in more natural light 
reaching the apartments. This will, among other things, have resulted in less electricity consumption. But 
the assumption was that the occupants were using the same amount of electricity before and after 
renovation.  
The weather data from April 13th to April 17th 2017 was missing and the assumption was to insert the data 
weather from the April 8th to April 12th 2017 in its place. This wasn’t exact, but this was the only solution 
to run the simulation and get data from the month of April. The weather file for 2014 didn’t have the 
exact number for direct and diffuse solar radiation, so the assumption was to calculate the values based on 
the sky cover and the given average solar radiation. This would affect the outdoor temperature in the 
simulations, which will affect the operative temperature in the apartments, because the operative 
temperature in the apartments was a function of the outdoor temperature.  
Energy consumption  
The simulated energy consumption for the model before renovation (Block 3) was 426 MWh without 
domestic hot water. The measured energy consumption for all four blocks was 1733 MWh, which means 
that the energy consumption for Block 3 was 433 MWh and 217 MWh without domestic hot water. The 
usage level for domestic hot water was assumed to be 20 MWh per month. The deviation between the 
measured and the simulated was 49%. This means that the occupants changed behavior after renovation,

because the deviation was high. There were also some uncertainties in determination of the crack point, 
that had influence on the indoor temperature of the apartments. The total energy consumption included 
the apartments in Buildings 94, 96 and 98 - the total number of apartments was 12. This could also affect 
the deviation between the measured and the simulated energy consumption for Block 3.  
In the model after renovation the simulated energy consumption was 314 MWh. The deviation was 3 % 
between the measured and simulated energy consumption. This value was high, especially because the 
relation between indoor and outdoor temperature was defined based on the measured indoor temperature. 
This deviation caused the defined linear equation in the controller for ideal heater.  
In Table 2 and Table 3 all linear equations for the relation between indoor and outdoor temperature were 
shown. The coefficient of determination (R2) varied between apartments. This means that some 
apartments had a correlation between indoor temperature, while others did not have an equally linear 
relationship.  
Apartment 21 had this linear equation: y=0.167x+19.285 , R2=0.613 
Apartment 17 had this linear equation: y=-0.0092x+22.866, R2=0.006 
Apartment 21 had a better relationship between indoor and outdoor temperature compared to Apartment 
17. The choice of crack point also affected the linear equations.  
Graph 1 shows a linear variation between the measured and simulated energy consumption for the model 
before renovation; y= 1.923 x+0.6448, which means that the simulated values was double that of the 
measured value. This also resulted in altered occupant behavior before and after renovation. 
Other aspects could influence energy consumption. The most crucial was window control. The windows 
were defined in both simulations as “Never open”. In reality the windows were opened, depending on the 
activity of each person. To get a more exact value nearer to reality, an investigation should be made into 
the window opening activity in each apartment, and a controller “Macro” made for the window opening 
for each apartment. 
Operative temperature  
When the measured indoor temperature was analyzed the quartile sets were found for some apartments 
with same number of occupants: two occupants and two children. The quartile sets were seen in Table 3, 
which shows big difference between the apartments. No two apartments had the same quartile set. 
Apartment 18 had the lowest minimum indoor temperature of 19.1°C, whereas Apartment 38 had the 
highest indoor temperature value at 21.1°C, the difference was 2°C. Apartment 12 has the highest 
maximum indoor temperature 30.1°C, while Apartment 9 had the lowest maximum indoor temperature 
26.2°C, the difference was 3.3 °C. Based on this small analysis of the measured values, it was seen that 
each indoor temperature in the apartments could depend on the occupant’s behavior. They have same 
number of occupants, same outdoor temperature and all couples were employed.  
The relation between simulated (before renovation 2014) and measured operative temperature was shown 
in Tables; 5 and 6. The average measured minimum operative temperature was 18.8°C, whereas the 
average simulated minimum operative temperature was 19.5°C. The difference was 0.7°C, which was 
very small. The average measured maximum operative temperature was 27.69 °C, while the average

8. Conclusion  
 
The measured indoor temperature for the period of May 2016 to May 2017 was used to determine a 
relation with the outdoor temperature. The linear equations were modelled in a controller for ideal heater 
in IDA ICE in both models, before and after renovation. After simulating the model before renovation, 
the energy consumption was compared with the measured energy consumption for the period between 
May 2016 and May 2017. For Block 3, the deviation was 4 % between the simulated and measured 
energy consumption for the whole year.  
The conclusion was that the occupants had changed behavior after renovation., based on the deviation and 
other uncertainties like the linear equation for the correlation between the indoor and outdoor 
temperature. The determined linear equations were defined in the controller for ideal heater. Some of the 
correlation wasn’t linear and that resulted in a deviation in the model before and after renovation.  
In the model after renovation the simulated yearly energy consumption was 31% higher than the 
measured energy consumption. The deviation was very high, especially as the defined linear equations 
were based on the measured indoor temperature for the same period, like the simulated period. The 
conclusion was that the deviation only caused the defined linear equations. The uncertainty occurred 
when selecting the crack point, which resulted in non-linear equations, which were defined in IDA ICE. 
The measured indoor temperature varied between the apartments. The analysis of the quartile sets of the 
apartments with same number and type of occupant, showed that no two apartments had the same 
temperature. Here, the conclusion was that each person adjusted the indoor temperature based on their 
own behavior. If the indoor temperature was low, then some occupants would use their heater, while 
others would not. This would have a direct influence on energy consumption.  
The relation between measured and simulated operative temperature was different between the 
apartments. This was dependent on the defined linear equation for the relation between indoor and 
outdoor temperature. The highest correlation between the measured and simulated operative temperature 
was y=0.9544x+1.33 (R2= 0.7662). The method for modelling the relation between indoor and outdoor 
temperature was useful but the determination of the crackpoint was not precise; a factor made very clear 
when the correlation between the measured and simulated operative temperature was found
