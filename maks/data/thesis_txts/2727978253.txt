Preface 
 
It is with great pleasure that I present to you the culmination of my scientific research on the 
grounds of my master’s thesis, entitled “Evaluating the biocompatibility of materials using 
differentiation of stem cells and metabolic profiling”. 
In the beginning of this thesis, the goals were to investigate stem cells co-cultured with 3D printing 
materials and how differentiation, metabolic profiling and media consumption is affected. The 
first step of this research was to establish a postprocessing protocol that would optimize the 
material’s biocompatibility. During this investigation, we tested numerous postprocessing 
protocols in which the leaching of uncured resin residues was assessed. Having discovered the 
remarkable benefits of wet autoclavation in leaching, we pivoted our research towards enhancing 
its benefits. Different wet autoclavation protocols were investigated; one round, three rounds and 
one long round of wet autoclavation. Throughout this report, three rounds and one long round 
are used interchangeably since the duration of the autoclavation process is practically the same 
in both cases. Following the leaching experiments, we started the cell experiments with fewer 
different post-processing 3D prints. In the interim, cell media from those experiments was 
collected for NMR analysis to later investigate the cells’ media consumption and metabolic 
profiling. There were a lot of back and forth in the cell lab and 3D printing lab in an attempt to 
optimize the cell culture experiment. Advanced 3D designs were required that would interfere 
less with the stem cells and would make the staining procedures of higher quality. The workload 
in the cell lab and having a substantial volume of NMR data occupied most of our time during this 
thesis. In the end, a limited metabolomic study was conducted due to lack of time to extensively 
study the metabolomics. To conclude, this shift in our research led us to promising results for 3D 
printing applications in biomedical devices.

Abstract 
 
Additive manufacturing is a cutting-edge technology that has recently contributed greatly to the 
advancements of biomedical devices, such as dental devices, lab-on-a-chip, 3D cell culture etc. 
However, material biocompatibility issues have impeded the rapid growth of this field. Especially, 
stereolithography (SLA) resins consist of toxic chemical compounds. In some cases, not fully 
polymerised resin residues leach out from the 3D print to the living tissue, causing harmful effects 
that range from low to high level of severity. Postprocessing of 3D printing materials removes 
those toxicants and subsequently improves their biocompatibility. In this study, we investigated 
the effect that different postprocessing protocols had in the leaching of uncured compounds and 
the material biocompatibility. Formlabs Biomed Clear (BIOMED), Clear and Dental LT and 
3DResyns Biotough D90-MF (D90) SLA resins were tested. Leaching experiments were conducted 
and analysed with nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR). Adipose-derived stem cells (ADSCs) were 
cultured along with the 3D prints and cell viability, morphology, differentiation capability and 
metabolic profiling were assessed. Among the post-processes, wet autoclavation was the most 
effective resulting in 3D prints that leach least resin residues in the aqueous media. Prolonged 
versions of wet autoclavation (60 min) improved those results. Potential toxic compounds were 
identified among the leachates of the 3D prints. BIOMED dry and wet autoclaved 3D prints 
exhibited similar cytocompatibility; with wet autoclaved showing a better behavior in 
differentiated cells. D90 wet autoclaved 3D prints showed good cell viability in both non-
differentiated and differentiated ADSCs whereas the dry autoclaved prints caused cell death in 
the short-term. Cell metabolomics showed that 3D printed materials affected the metabolic 
profiling of the cells; with prolonged wet autoclaved parts having a similar profile with the cells 
without material.

Introduction 
 
Recent advances in biomedical engineering 
Tissue engineering has emerged as a promising field in biomedical research with innovative 
approaches constantly being developed in research areas such as tissue regeneration, organ 
transplantation, disease modelling, and drug development. Narrowing down this broad field, 
microfluidic devices, like Lab-on-a-Chip and Organ-on-a-chip, are miniaturised platforms that 
hold great potential in mimicking the architecture and functionality of human biological tissues1.  
A good comprehension of the tissue structure and the biological interactions that take place in 
the cellular environment is of great importance for tissue engineering. For this purpose, cell 
culture models were developed and constantly improved. Cell cultures have contributed to 
innovations in research areas, such as drug discovery and development, toxicity, cancer research, 
biomedical research 2. Accordingly, cell cultures are inextricably linked with tissue engineering. In 
the last years, the realisation of 3D cell cultures models tends to replace 2D cell culture models. 
3D cell cultures resemble in a greater level the in-vivo conditions of the tissue; the complex 
architecture, the cell-cell interactions and cell-extracellular matrix communications. Thus, 3D cell 
culture models provide more reliable results and are more relevant for studies like tissue 
modelling and novel drug responses 3.  
 
Additive Manufacturing  
 
An emerging technology that contributes greatly to the acceleration of tissue engineering is 
additive manufacturing, also known as 3D printing, which in the last decades became widely 
available4. 3D printing has great potential in replacing the previous golden standard, soft 
lithography5. Fabrication of complex designs is made possible while the production process is fast 
and simplified. 3D printing cut down additional steps in the fabrication process, such as assembly 
or need of specific molds, and clean room facilities 6. Among the great advantages of 3D printing 
is also the design freedom and personalization. To this day, customised medical tools and devices 
have been manufactured for dental, prosthetics and surgical applications. Examples of which 
include bone and craniofacial implants7, dental crowns and bridges8 and stents9. Other 
applications include 3D printed cerebral organoids modelling Zika virus exposure10, hydrogels for 
soft tissue regeneration11, microfluidic systems simulating the blood-brain barrier (BBB)-on-a-
chip12. Taking into consideration the benefits of 3D printing, it is no wonder it became one of the 
most cutting-of-edge technologies with promising applications in many fields.  
Different 3D printing techniques have been used for the fabrication of in-vitro biological models, 
such as fused deposition modelling (FDA), stereolithography (SLA), selective laser sintering (SLS), 
inkjet printing (IJ). Among those techniques, SLA will be discussed further since it is in the scope 
==PAGE:19==
2 
 
of this project13. After the SLA patent expired, SLA printers gained popularity and became widely 
available at a reasonable price14.  In SLA, a liquid photopolymer resin is cured layer-by-layer by a 
laser beam. The 3D print is fabricated on a build platform, which is submerged in a vat filled with 
resin, gradually elevating as the printing process goes on. SLA produces high resolution end-
products with smooth surfaces. A similar technique to SLA is digital light processing (DLP) and 
liquid-crystal display (LCD), where instead of a laser, a digital light projector or an LCD screen 
respectively is used, that can cure entire layers simultaneously, resulting in faster printing process 
while keeping the resolution high 15. An advancement of SLA is low force stereolithography (LFS). 
In this technique, a flexible tank is used which reduces forces and leads to higher success rates, 
smoother surfaces and easier support removal. Also, an advanced light projector unit is employed 
resulting in improved accuracy and faster printing speed 16.  
 
Biocompatibility of medical devices   
 
The introduction of 3D printing in the biomedical field created a great demand for new materials, 
with physical and chemical properties corresponding to each application’s needs. An essential 
prerequisite of those materials is to be biocompatible. Biocompatibility is an umbrella term that 
refers to the characteristic of a material to not induce an immunological response to the human 
tissue that will be applied to17. Biocompatibility could refer to mild immunological or toxicological 
reactions, such as skin irritation and allergies, but also to chronic irreversible conditions, such as 
carcinogenesis18. A material is certified as biocompatible depending on its intended use. For that 
reason, materials should comply with the relevant standards or specific certifications at least from 
one regulatory authority, such as ISO standards in Europe (ISO 1099319) or the equivalent US 
authorities (USP VI20). For example, ISO 10993 consists of 22 sections that include quality and risk 
assessment management and specific reactivity tests to assess different aspects of 
biocompatibility. Reactivity tests vary from cytotoxicity, skin irritation to chronic toxicity, 
genotoxicity, and carcinogenicity etc19.  Regarding the intended use of the material, certifications 
should be acquired only for the relevant sections.  
 
New materials suitable for 3D printing, that are claimed to be biocompatible, are constantly being 
developed and commercialized in response to the growing demand of additive manufacturing of 
medical devices. Guttridge et al. conducted an extensive study on 3D printing materials, 
identifying more than 130 materials intended for different medical application, with the majority 
of those to be designated for dental applications21. However, in terms of certifications little 
information was found. In principle, the granted certifications should be explicitly stated in the 
official documents of the material. In most cases, superficial and vague statements are claimed. 
Among the tested materials, only 7 had correctly demonstrated their granted certification to the 
specific section of the standard. The majority of materials claim compliance with ISO 10993, 
without mentioning which of the 22 subsections they comply to. However, this is not sufficient to 
declare the biocompatibility of a material. In few cases, manufacturers refer to biocompatibility 
with general terminology to their certified biocompatibility while others do not even have a 
certification in place 21.   
==PAGE:20==
3 
 
Usually, 3D printing materials are rendered biocompatible after a series of postprocessing and 
sterilization steps. In general, postprocessing removes toxic residues from the 3D print’s surface 
and/or increases the cross-linking of the material, resulting in less harmful compounds22. The 
manufacturer should technically provide the user with a detailed guide on how to establish 
biocompatibility. However, comprehensive information about post-processing is given only in a 
few cases, which is only applicable for specific geometries21. To this end, the user is solely 
responsible for ascertaining biocompatibility for the desired application and for establishing an 
optimised protocol of postprocessing for the 3D print21. 
It is common that 3D printed materials have been successfully granted biocompatibility for acute 
toxicity that refers to short-term biocompatibility (24-72 hours). In real life applications, a 
biomaterial such as an implant, needs to stay for longer in the human body. Unfortunately, studies 
showed that 3D printing materials, certified for cell culturing, when tested longer for cytotoxicity 
induced decreased cell viability and adverse effects23. A noticeable exemption was the successful 
cell culturing and cell proliferation on 3D printing culture dishes for 56 days24. It is evident, that 
the benefits of a long-term biocompatible 3D printing material would be plentiful and would 
improve the current state of those medical devices. Nevertheless, biocompatibility is an obstacle 
that could impede the development of tissue engineering. 
 
Toxicity of 3D printing materials 
 
Several studies about 3D printing materials have reported toxicity and adverse effects in living 
tissues, varying from acute toxicity to reproductive toxicity and carcinogenesis. In SLA, uncured 
compounds of the resin are proved to be highly toxic for living organisms. Uncured compounds 
result from incomplete photopolymerization. In SLA, photopolymerization has an upper limit of 
conversion around 60%25. Non-polymerised resin residues are proven to induce cytotoxicity, 
genetic and reproductive mutations, and developmental toxicity26–30.  
The chemical composition of SLA resins is proprietary, but the core composition is known. These 
resins consist of liquid monomers and oligomers, in most cases of acrylates, methacrylates and 
urethanes, epoxies and photoinitiators, that induce the polymerization process, and often various 
additives, such as photoabsorbers and plasticizers, that enhance printability and other properties 
of the final product31. Uncured oligomers and monomers, that did not manage to bond to the 
main polymeric macromolecular core leach out from the finished product. These compounds are 
proven to be highly toxic and to induce harmful effects in living tissues31. Studies have shown that 
acrylates, methacrylates and photoinitiators cause toxicity both in vitro and in vivo. In dental 
implants, traces of uncured 2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate (HEMA) and triethylene glycol 
dimethacrylate (TEGMA), were detected in the human circulation system32. To be more specific, 
methacrylate leachates, such as HEMA, have been reported to be affiliated with oral mucosal 
irritation33.  
==PAGE:21==
4 
 
Photoinitiator residues, for example from camphorquinone, have also been associated with 
inducing oxidative stress to cells and eventually causing cell death31. Rogers et al. found a 
commercial light stabilizer, Tinuvin 292, as a main leachate of biocompatible resins. This leachate 
is found to be highly cytotoxic and causes chromosomal defects in reproductive cells27. 
 
Leaching of toxic compounds 
 
There are two main mechanisms that are responsible for the migration of these toxic compounds: 
degradation of the 3D printed product or leaching. In the first mechanism, surface erosion 
happens due to chemical, mechanical, or thermal factors 31. Erosion is more common in the case 
that 3D printing resins are comprised of compounds with low solubility in water, such as HEMA 
and TEGMA34. Leaching of uncured residues takes place when the material is submerged in 
aqueous solvents that lead to the extraction of unbound compounds. In the research field of 
biotechnology, the leaching mechanism is prevalent since bioassays, that include living cells or 
simulate living tissues, take place in aqueous media31. The effect of leaching depends on the 
properties of the 3D print (geometry and composition), polymerization ratio and time interval in 
solvent35. Studies have shown that uncured resin compounds can be extracted with both 
inorganic and organic solvents 31. Considering the first statement, researchers venture to diminish 
the toxic leachates prior to the actual experiment and have successfully increased 
cytocompatibility. A common approach is to add a postprocessing leaching step where the 3D 
prints are placed in aqueous media for intervals varying from 1-10 days, allowing them to leach 
out uncured toxic substances prior to their placement in the actual experiment 36–38. 
 
Postprocessing  
 
An approach to remove uncured toxic compounds from 3D prints is to optimize the cross-linking 
of the resin with different postprocessing strategies prior to the implementation to the material 
in real life conditions. Postprocessing consists of two main steps: washing and UV light curing 22. 
In some cases, additional steps such as coating with known biocompatible materials, 
polyethylene glycol diacrylate (PEG-DA) and polydimethylsiloxane(PDMS), was shown to be 
beneficial in terms of biocompatibility31. Washing removes resin residues from the 3D print’s 
surface. Common solvents used are water and isopropyl alcohol. UV curing polymerizes uncured 
resin compounds resulting in less toxic oligomers and/or monomers in the 3D print 22. In some 
cases, sterilization postprocesses are implemented such as steam, gamma and ethylene oxide 
sterilization39. Detailed postprocessing instructions are rarely provided by the manufacturer. 
Even in cases where a postprocessing guide is in place, the geometry of the 3D print could modify 
the biocompatible result that the company claimed 21. To this end, studies are being conducted 
with a generic outline of postprocessing in mind, which is washing and UV curing, while 
researchers are trying to create an optimised detailed protocol that could yield biocompatibility. 
==PAGE:22==
5 
 
 
In studies where ethanol wash and UV curing were prolonged, commercial resins NextDent C&B 
and Formlabs Denture teeth resin A2 became more biocompatible40. However, 3D systems VisiJet 
Crystal showed that 72 hours of ethanol wash did not yield any positive effect in terms of 
biocompatibility41. In another study, researchers showed that extending the UV curing time 
interval did not have any significant effect on biocompatibility of Formlabs Denture teeth resin 
A2 whereas increasing the UV curing temperature showed increased cell viability42.  Although the 
favorable results of postprocessing often come at the expense of other physical or mechanical 
properties of the 3D print 40. Inoue and Ikuta increased the biocompatibility of 3D prints with a 
high temperature heat process in nitrogen but at the same time the materials’ optical properties 
were compromised43.  
 
An efficient post-process that leads to a couple of benefits is autoclavation, also called water 
steam sterilization. It was shown that not only does it sterilize the 3D print but also contributes 
to its biocompatibility. Piironen et al. demonstrated the effects of dry autoclavation in long-term 
cell viability and proliferation on Formlabs resins (Clear, Dental LT Clear, Dental SG, High Temp). 
Among their tested materials, only the ones eligible for autoclavation (Dental SG and High Temp) 
managed to support cell culture for 56 days. Speculations have been made that during the 
autoclavation process uncured toxic residues are extracted from the 3D print. A limitation of this 
approach is that not all materials can withstand autoclavation24.    
 
Overall, a universal postprocessing protocol is missing; different processes and solvents are used 
for random intervals. Thus, it is difficult to interlink results of different studies and evaluate 
whether methods are robust, and results are reproducible.  
 
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance 
 
Identification of leachates is usually achieved with mass spectrometry (MS) or Fourier transform 
infrared spectroscopy (FTIR). Rogers et al. identified residues of light stabilizer, Tinuvin 292, 
leaching from Formlabs biocompatible resins, Dental LT and Dental SG using liquid 
chromatography-mass spectroscopy (LC-MS)27. Lately, another analytical technique, nuclear 
magnetic resonance (NMR), has been found useful in the identification and quantification of 
leaching compounds of 3D printing structures. Nejedla et al. identified surfactant residues in 
leachates of E-Shell 300 photopolymers (EnvisionTEC Inc.) using both NMR and MS44. Rimington 
et al. also identified acrylate compounds in leachates of Formlabs Clear, VeroClear RGD810 and 
EOS PA2200 using FTIR and NMR 37. 
NMR is a powerful and non-destructive analytical technique that has been widely used in various 
research areas. NMR takes advantages of the magnetic properties of atomic nuclei, such as 
hydrogen (1H) and carbon-13 (13C), when placed in a magnetic field and hit with radiofrequency 
radiation. Electromagnetic energy is absorbed and emitted by these nuclei. This signal can be 
detected from NMR and once analysed gives valuable information about molecular structures 
and interactions. NMR is a technique widely used for identification of chemical compounds in 
==PAGE:23==
6 
 
various settings, understanding the structure and interaction of biomolecules and drug 
compounds with other molecules, quantifying metabolites in biological systems etc45.  
 
Cytocompatibility  
 
As previously discussed, in vitro cell models are of great importance for tissue modelling and drug 
screening and thus it is crucial that 3D printing materials are compatible with cell culturing. 
ISO10993 section 5 describes ways to test cytotoxicity of medical devices. The results are 
interpreted by evaluating the cell growth, metabolism, and morphology. Cytocompatibility is cell 
dependent and different cell types have different reactions with 3D printing materials19. Some 
cell types are more robust and others are more sensitive to toxic compounds. For example, 
immortalised cells appear to be more robust than stem cells and the latter are thus a better 
candidate for such studies24.  Thus, the intended use of the device dictates which cells are relevant 
to be evaluated.   
 
 
After reviewing several studies about biocompatibility of 3D printing materials it is evident that 
those materials are inadequately studied and with no robust and easy to implement method to 
render a material biocompatible and therefore their safe application in biomedical devices. 
Stricter regulations should be applied in the market of 3D printing materials and any 
biocompatible material should be marketed only when it is accompanied by the relevant certified 
documentation. In such a broad research field, there is still need for additional studies, thoroughly 
designed, with the intended use of each medical device in mind.  
 
The aim of this study is to establish an efficient postprocessing protocol that could minimize the 
toxic leachates of 3D printing materials and increase biocompatibility. We aim to show that this 
protocol is robust and yields the same results for different materials. The study mainly focused 
on the effects of autoclavation and ways to maximize those, by using different types of 
autoclavation and extending the overall duration. NMR was employed as a method to evaluate 
the leaching effect and intensity. Our goal is to interlink the reduction in leaching compounds with 
an increase in biocompatibility. For that reason, stem cells were used in order to evaluate the 
effect the test materials had on the cell growth, proliferation, differentiation and metabolism.

Postprocessing  
 
The post-processing protocols are summarised in Table 2.  
Short protocol. 3D printed structures were washed for 20 minutes in isopropanol (IPA) in a 
sonication bath (D90) or a Formlabs FormWash station (Biomed, Dental LT and Clear). Prints were 
subsequently cured by UV light for 30 minutes at 60οC in a UV light polymerization chamber, in 
Formlabs FormCure   
Long protocol. 3D printed structures were first washed in IPA as described in the short protocol. 
The prints transferred to bags with IPA and incubated for 1h. Another round of this washing step 
was repeated. Prints were subsequently cured by UV light for 60 minutes at 60οC in a Formlabs 
FormCure. Finally, the prints were incubated in Milli-Q water overnight.  
Cleaning + purification solution or only a cleaning solution. D90 was also processed according to 
the company instructions. This involved a 3X20 min sonication in 3Dresyns cleaning solution 
(Cleaning Fluid Bio for washing and cleaning 3D prints, 3Dresyns) followed by a 30 min UV curing 
at 60οC. Optionally, the prints were treated with the tester and purification solutions (Cure Tester 
& Purification kit for full cleansing of 3D prints, 3Dresyns) that reacts with unreacted resin.  
 
Autoclavation of the 3D printed structures (Table 3): 
Dry autoclavation: 3D prints were autoclaved with a 60L Top-Loading & Vertical autoclave 
(Priorclave Ltd., UK) at 121οC for 20 minutes in sterilization pouches. 
1X wet autoclavation: 3D prints were placed in 50 mL MilliQ water and autoclaved for 20 min at 
121οC using the same autoclave as above. 
3X wet autoclavation: 3D structures were autoclaved three times according to the protocol 1X 
autoclavation above. Milli-Q water was replaced at the end of each round. 
Long wet autoclavation: 3D structures were placed in 500 mL water and autoclaved for 60 minutes 
at 121οC.

Leaching  
 
Sterilised 3D printed structures were placed in 48-well plates where wells were filled with 1 ml 
sterile Milli-Q water. The plates were incubated for 48 hours at 37οC in 5% CO2, 95% air 
atmosphere, and 100% relative humidity, simulating the conditions of cell culture. Milli-Q water 
samples, without any material, were also added in the well plate, as a control sample. Triplicates 
of each sample were used. After incubation, the leaching samples were collected and analysed 
with nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR).  
1D 1H NMR spectra were acquired with a NOESY pre-saturation pulse sequence using a Bruker 
NMR spectrometer at 500 MHz, equipped with a sample changer (SampleJet). A total of 128 scans 
were collected using a spectral width of 12019 Hz, giving a 2.7 s acquisition time and analysis time 
per sample was 24 minutes (d1=4s, aq=2.7s). NMR samples were prepared with 300 μl of the test 
sample and 250μl DSS phosphate buffer solution at pH 7.5. Spectra were manually corrected for 
phase and baseline distortions and the chemical shift was normalised to the buffer’s reference at 
0.00 ppm using MestReNova software46. 
Ethanol (1.17-1.20 ppm and 3.8-3.6 ppm) contaminations were excluded from the analysis. Peaks 
that were observed in the test but not in the control samples were recorded as leachates. The 
peaks integrals were calculated as a measurement of the leachates. Background from the control 
sample was subtracted from each peak respectively.  
 
Visual observation of the 3D printed structures 
 
The printability of the 3D printed structures and test structures from Ameralabs (AmeraLabs 
Town, AmeraLabs) was assessed with 5MP Edge Dino-Lite digital microscope (AM7915MZTL, 
AnMo Electronics Corporation).  
Autofluorescence of the 3D prints was assessed with the ZeissTM AxioObserver Z1 
epifluorescence microscope (Carl Zeiss MicroImaging GmbH), using a channel with excitation at 
450/40 nm and emission at 500/50 nm. Images were analysed with ImageJ v1.53. For each image, 
the same regions of interest (ROI) were set and analysed for mean gray value. Background 
fluorescence from each sample was subtracted from the measured values. The higher the mean 
gray value the higher the autofluorescence of the material.

In vitro cell culture  
 
Adipose-Derived Stem Cells (ADSCs) (StemPro™, R7788115, Gibco™, Thermo Fisher) were 
cultured in T-75 cell culture flasks (NuncTM, Thermo ScientificTM, Thermo Fisher) in commercially 
available reduced serum culture medium (MSC Growth Medium 2 and Supplement mix, C-28009, 
PromoCell GmbH) with 2 mM L-glutamine(25030081, GibcoTM, Thermo Fisher)  at 37οC in 5% CO2, 
95% air atmosphere, and 100% relative humidity. The cell culture medium was changed every 3-
4 days. Cell culture passage used varied from p2-p4. When the cells reached 75-80% confluency, 
they were detached with TrypLE Express (TrypLE™ Express without phenol red, 12604013, 
GibcoTM, Thermo Fisher) and seeded into 48-well plates at densities in a range of 2–2.5 x 104 
cells/well for each 3D printed structure. 0.4 ml of suspension was added to each well. Wells 
without any material included were used as controls. Cells were allowed to attach for 72 hours at 
37οC in 5% CO2 prior to cell viability and staining assays. Preliminary observation of the cells’ 
growth was conducted using a Zeiss Primovert microscope (Carl Zeiss MicroImaging GmbH, 
Germany). Triplicates of each sample were used in the experiment, if not mentioned otherwise. 
Cells were differentiated into adipocytes using a designated differentiation kit for adipogenesis 
(StemProTM Adipogenesis Differentiation Kit, A1007001, GibcoTM, Thermo Fisher). In a similar way, 
once cells reached the appropriate confluency, they were seeded into 48-well plates at density of 
2 x 104 cells/well with the same controls in place. After 72 hours, the growth medium was 
replaced with the differentiation medium included in the kit with 10 mg/ml penicillin (100 U/mL)-
streptomycin (100 μg/mL) (15140122, GibcoTM, Thermo Fisher) and maintained in culture. 
Triplicates of each sample were used in the experiment.

Multiple rounds and longer wet autoclavation  
 
The type of autoclavation appeared to be the driving force for enhancing the leaching of the 3D 
printed structures, with wet autoclavation resulting in end-products that leaches less in aqueous 
media. Based on this, multiple rounds of wet autoclavation were implemented to investigate 
whether a further reduction on the leachates could be obtained. Hence, 3 consecutive rounds of 
wet autoclavation and one more efficient protocol, a long autoclavation round (60 min) simulating 
those 3 rounds, were conducted, followed by incubation in water and NMR analysis, as previously 
described. The leachates of the three autoclavation rounds showed a 2-fold decrease in leachates 
in both BIOMED and D90, when compared to the one round wet autoclavation (Figure 4). In the 
case of wet long autoclavation, leachates were reduced even further; with a 10-fold and 3-fold 
reduction for BIOMED and D90 respectively when compared to the three rounds of autoclavation. 
Last, comparing dry to wet long autoclavation, the amount of leachates was significantly reduced; 
especially for BIOMED where leachates were 100 times lower while for D90 were 36 times lower. 
Overall, the effect of wet autoclavation is enhanced by increasing the duration of the process.

Additional 3D printing resins on leaching experiments

 Discussion 
 
3D printing is a groundbreaking technology that can contribute highly to the advancements of 
biomedical research with applications ranging from medical and dental devices, surgical tools, 
lab-on-a chip devices and advanced cell culture models.  Implementation of 3D printing in these 
fields is mainly hindered by the lack of biocompatible materials. Here, our attempt is to investigate 
if there is a connection between postprocessing, leaching and cellular viability and behavior.  
Leaching experiments showed that the main driving force that extracts more uncured resin 
residues is wet autoclavation. Post-processes, such as washing and curing steps, appear to have 
minor impact on the final product, irrespective of the duration of those steps or the solvents used. 
While there are studies supporting the claim that dry autoclaved 3D prints leach fewer toxic 
residues, there are none investigating the effects of wet autoclavation 24.  A similar approach to 
wet autoclavation is incubation of 3D prints in cell growth medium or water for long time intervals 
prior to cell experiment36–38. Cell viability was improved but the results of wet autoclavation are 
more substantial and faster. This difference in those two approaches is possibly attributed to the 
pressure and temperature conditions that are present during autoclavation. Wet autoclavation 
appears to be a powerful technique that in a fast and convenient way delivers both sterilised and 
more biocompatible 3D printing structures in one process step.   
It is likely that the driving mechanism of leaching is diffusion. Uncured semi-liquid molecules are 
in contact with the solvent and diffuse out of the print to equalize the concentration gradient 
inside the print and in the solvent. Based on this assumption, the high temperature conditions in 
autoclavation promote the diffusion and eventually the leaching of those uncured compounds. In 
dry autoclavation, uncured residues are potentially vaporized, but the absence of a solvent is 
slowing down the leaching process. The effect of wet autoclavation was proven to be time 
dependent. By increasing the duration of wet autoclavation the final leachates dropped even 
lower (Figure 4, Figure 5). Also, surface-to-volume ratio is affecting the leaching; cylinders, that 
higher SA/V, resulting in smaller amount of leachates (Figure 6). However, BIOMED wet long 
cylinders surprisingly leached more than the respective rings. This inconsistent result could be 
attributed to the fact that a manual autoclave was used for the cylinders, due to defects of the 
automatic autoclave that was used in all the other experiments. 
Furthermore, the results of wet autoclavation were reproduced in four different materials, from 
different brands and printed in different 3D printers. Some materials showed more resistance in 
the leachates quantities when moving from dry to wet and wet long autoclavation, such as Clear. 
Surprisingly, Dental LT which is a certified biocompatible material (EN-ISO10993-
1:2009/AC:2010,10993-3:2009,10993-5:200949) leaches highly when dry autoclaved whereas 
once it has been wet autoclaved the leachates drop significantly. Kreb et al. showed that 16.5 mm 
thick culture devices fabricated with dry autoclaved Dental LT reduced the viability of 
mesenchymal stem cells23. It would be interesting to investigate whether a wet long autoclavation 
of such thick structure would be powerful enough to render the device biocompatible. 
==PAGE:48==
31 
 
Unfortunately, not all materials are suitable for autoclavation and thus our application will be 
limited to materials with high heat deflection temperature. Regardless of the non-autoclavable 
materials, wet autoclavation did not cause any significant deformities in the 3D printed structures. 
Printability of D90 was exceptionally high prior and post autoclavation; making D90 a good 
candidate for fine miniaturized devices. Although, mechanical properties after wet autoclavation 
and degradation of the parts should be investigated. 
Overall, an autoclave is an easy-to-use instrument commonly found in a research facility that does 
not require specialized personnel to operate it. It is widely used for the sterilization of the lab 
tools and materials. In a future study, wet autoclavation should be implemented in perfusion cell 
culture 3D printing systems. It is highly likely that the accumulation of leachates in batch cultures 
is a worse case scenario compared to perfusion, where the continuous supply of fresh media will 
discard any leachates. 
The origin of NMR peaks is not fully determined, but some peaks are attributed to compounds 
including vinyl and aromatic protons, which are known to be toxic for cellular environments.  
Previous studies have reported that oligomers and/or monomers of methacrylates and uncured 
photoinitiator elements are found among the leachates and based on this we speculate that this 
is the case in our experiment as well. Regardless of the composition of the leachates, we support 
that a 3D printed material that leaches less uncured residues is less likely to cause adverse effects. 
In the case of wet long autoclaved materials, previously reported peaks disappear completely 
from the NMR spectra. Among those peaks were peaks from vinyl and aromatic protons, leading 
us to the assumption that toxic oligomers have been released in the autoclavation water and the 
end-product is clearer of toxicants. However, it would be more beneficial for our analysis to 
calculate the concentration of those leachates from the peaks integrals to assess which are found 
in higher quantities. Unfortunately, the composition of these commercial resins is proprietary and 
that would be a challenging and laborious task. Finally, NMR appeared to be an efficient technique 
that can detect leachates in aqueous media in a fast and time-effective manner. In the future, a 
2D NMR analysis could be conducted to identify each compound of the leachates and determine 
the cause of toxicity and work towards a mitigation strategy.  
When testing the materials with the ADSCs, our assumptions around linkage with leaching and 
biocompatibility were strengthened. Cells showed higher viability when growing with the wet 
autoclaved 3D printed materials. Cells were growing both closer to and underneath the material 
as indicated by covering the whole area of the well. By contrast, D90 dry autoclaved prints caused 
cell death usually after 4-5 days of culture. Despite the fact that BIOMED dry autoclaved prints 
extract more leachates in the media, the cell viability was just slightly lower than in BIOMED wet 
autoclaved prints. This result led us to assume quantity and the composition of the leachates that 
influence biocompatibility. 
In most of the cases, cells are successfully differentiated to adipocytes, where we observed that 
the proliferation is paused, cells are becoming rounder and some characteristics of adipocytes 
are formed, such as lipid droplets. However, it was in the case of BIOMED dry autoclaved rings 
==PAGE:49==
32 
 
that the cells had the largest diameter, but no lipid droplets could be observed. This suggests that 
something could still leach from this material, hindering the differentiation of the cells but not 
necessarily the proliferation. In a future study, adipocytes cultured for longer and assessment of 
specific biomarkers, such as fatty acids, in the NMR would give better insights into how 
differentiation is affected by the 3D prints. 
Additionally, the 3D printed materials appear to have an impact on the amino acids 
metabolomics, with the D90 once wet autoclaved structures to have the biggest differences when 
compared to the cells in the control well.  In general, a similar metabolomics pattern was followed 
but whether the difference in the metabolism of nutrients is causing an adverse effect to the cells 
is still under investigation. However, the metabolomics results normalised to the differentiation 
media were not coherent with the metabolic pathways that were expected. A very high 
production of glucose and essential and non-essential amino acids cannot be explained. It is 
possible that a more relevant normalization should be conducted. In a future study, metabolomics 
should be normalised to media incubated in the same conditions as the cells to investigate the 
media degradation. Overall, our metabolomics study is preliminary and a more thorough analysis 
on the metabolomics would be interesting to better understand the interactions between the 3D 
prints and the cells from a metabolomics perspective.  
A major limitation to our study is the lack of different cell types. Biocompatibility is cell type- 
specific, with different cell types to be more sensitive or more robust in experimental settings. In 
order to establish wet autoclavation as an effective postprocessing technique more cell types 
should validate the previously discussed results regarding biocompatibility. 
In the end, autoclaved BIOMED and D90 were studied in terms of physical appearance and 
biocompatibility and their results are summarised in the following table (Table 5). The wet 
autoclaved materials had the best features in regards to biocompatibility, with D90 having the 
extra benefit of higher resolution. Those materials are great candidates for biomedical 
applications and should be studied extensively to successfully implement them.

Conclusion 
 
To conclude, we evaluated the connection between leaching and biocompatibility of 3D printing 
materials. We proved that wet autoclavation successfully extracts toxicants during that process, 
leading to a purer end-product with increased biocompatibility. We successfully validated that 
leaching uncured compounds out of a 3D print has a beneficial effect in terms of biocompatibility. 
We established a protocol that successfully delivers short-term biocompatible 3D prints, 
fabricated with 2 different materials from different manufacturers in different printers. Our 
research could have a great impact on the biomedical field, if wet autoclavation is confirmed to 
be a universal postprocessing technique that can render various 3D printing materials 
biocompatible. For that reason, in vitro experiments with additional materials and cell types as 
well as in vivo experiments should be conducted.
