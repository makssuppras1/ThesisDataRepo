Abstract 
Research has shown that the ions present in pore water can weaken the mechanical properties 
of chalk reservoirs. This phenomenon is perceived as water weakening. Water weakening is a 
controversial issue with many proposed mechanisms explaining it. One promising theory 
assigns the water weakening in a disjoining pressure arising from repulsive forces developed 
in the electrical double layers between neighboring grains. 
In this study, the behavior of the electrostatic double layer related forces as a function of the 
concentration of specific ions in the pore fluid it was evaluated. To accomplish this, single 
salt brines from the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2, 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2, 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4, and 𝐾𝐶𝑙 salts were formulated for a great ionic 
strength spectrum. The pH and electrical conductivity of the brines were determined. 
Afterward, 14𝑔 of brine were utilized to saturate 10𝑔 of powder into vials. The grain 
particles of the system brought in suspensions with a vibration pad and then settled naturally. 
Vials’ sediment column porosity was measured with nuclear magnetic resonance 
spectrometry. A centrifugation force was applied to the vials to simulate roughly 280𝑘𝑃𝑎 
overburden stress and column porosity was measured anew. Finally, fluid from the vials’ 
aqueous phase was collected and analyzed for pH and electrical conductivity. The results 
compared with the ones of the original brines. 
The sediment column porosity results were linked with the magnitude of the repulsive forces 
acting among the chalk grains. The natural settlement porosity results suggested that 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 
and 𝐾𝐶𝑙 brines can develop the greatest repulsive forces while after the simulation of the 
overburden stress, the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 brines. A correlation was identified between the porosity trends 
observed in the vials saturated with 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 and 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 brines and the zeta potential 
measurements available in the literature. The electrical conductivity results suggested that 
any potential precipitation has been eclipsed by chalk dissolution.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
The water weakening effect 
Petroleum reservoir exploitation may include primary, secondary, and tertiary recovery. In 
primary recovery, fluids are driven from the reservoir to surface owing to an existing pressure 
potential. The mechanism dictating how reservoir pore pressure declines during primary 
recovery production may is solution gas drive, gas-cap drive, natural water drive, or 
compaction drive. At compaction drive, reservoir compaction contributes significantly in the 
oil and gas extraction.  
Valhall, Ekofisk and Tyra offshore fields in North Sea Central Graben, discovered around 
half a century ago, are some of the many fields that compaction drive is known to play a 
fundamental role. In Valhall field it has been reported that the compaction induced 
contributes over 70% on hydrocarbon recovery (Cook et al., 1996). The aforementioned 
fields have in common the high-porous chalk reservoir rock formation with reporting porosity 
values exceeding 40% in many cases. 
Effective stress is one of the parameters imposing the reservoir compaction. Effective stress 
is a measure of the pressure difference between overburden pressure and pore pressure. If 
during primary recovery, the pore pressure is reduced adequately, the highly applied effective 
stress may evoke pore collapse. During an event of pore collapse, studies have shown that 
there is porosity and permeability declination, fractures closure, and subsidence (Sulak, 
1991). Subsidence in offshore hydrocarbon fields may be defined as the progressive 
downward settling of the seabed surface induced by reservoir compaction. Subsidence has a 
strong impact on highly porous un-cemented or poorly cemented reservoirs (Hettema, 
Papamichos, & Schutjens, 2002). 
In Ekofisk field, subsidence discovered in November 1984 (Sulak, 1991). The profile of 
subsidence versus reservoir depletion pressure can be regarded in Figure 1.1. The solution to

platform legs at a cost of $1 billion.  
Water injection in reservoirs is a secondary recovery mechanism. Water injection is 
administered for reservoir pressure support and oil displacement towards production wells. In 
the Tor formation of the Ekofisk field at 1987 and at 1994 in the entire Ekofisk field, water 
injected to maintain the reservoir pressure and avert further subsidence. Observations showed 
that subsidence accelerated after water injection. 
Since 1987, roughly $3 billion was spent on the Ekofisk field to counter subsidence effects 
(Nagel & Pattillo, 2006). In Tyra field, an estimated $3.34 billion redevelopment plan has 
been approved to counter subsidence effect with expected completion at 2022 (Total, 2018). 
By October 2013, subsidence treatment in Valhall required the expenditure of $4.2 billion

2. Theoretical Background 
2.1 
Chalk 
Carbonate reservoirs contribute to a substantial fraction of the world’s oil reserves. 
Limestone and dolomite rocks are the two primary subcategories of carbonate rocks. Chalk 
rock is a sedimentary rock, a variation of limestone rocks, with great homogeneity at the 
physical property measuring scale (Fabricius, 2007),  chemically made up by calcite mineral 
(𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑂3). 
Chalk is composed mainly by marine calcareous nannofossils. The nannofossils incorporate 
clasts of the sizes of clay to slit (Fabricius, 2007) which fluctuates from 0.032mm to 0.63mm 
(Wentwurth, 1922). Frequently, admixtures of sand-size clasts (>0.63mm) can be 
distinguished in chalk leading to a mudstone or wackestone microtexture chalk (Fabricius, 
2007) (Figure 2.1). Packstone microtexture is less frequently faced while grainstone 
microtexture is scarce.  
 
 
Figure 2.1: Lithology classification of carbonates (edited from Kendall (2005), after Dunham (1962)). 
 
Chalk form by the sedimentation of calcareous ooze on the ocean’s seabed. Porosity on the 
newly deposited particles is about 70% (Fabricius, 2007). As burial depth increases, porosity 
decreases. That’s owing to the diagenetic process. Mechanical compaction and pore-filling 
cementation are the two primary diagenetic processes which decrease the porosity. 
Mechanical compaction independently may bring the porosity to values between 40% and 
50% (Lind, 2006). Mechanical compaction is the process at which clasts forced closer 
together due to overburden stress. Pore-filling cementation occurs when groundwater moves 
between the grains and leaves behind mineral deposits, bonding the grains to each other.

2.2.2 
Specific surface area  
Specific surface area (SSA) of a rock material may be defined as its total surface area per unit 
of mass or its bulk volume. Commonly, the units are in 𝑚2/𝑔 or in 𝑚2/𝑚3 . The SSA 
provides knowledge for the size of the particles of the rock material since bigger particles 
have a lower SSA and smaller particles a higher. 
The basis of measuring the SSA lies in the nitrogen adsorption on a powder specimen after 
Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) stated in 1938 (Brunauer et al., 1938).  
Originally, the sample can be cleaned from contaminants by the application of an inert gas 
such as helium. Later, it is heated and degassed to discharge the moisture from the material’s 
surface. The specimen is then cooled into cryogenic conditions routinely with the utilization 
of liquid nitrogen (77K).  
The measurement begins by adding gradually a controlled amount of nitrogen gas to the 
system. The nitrogen partial pressure builds up, so does the adsorption. Adsorption is 
measured as a function of the volume of nitrogen employed with respect to its partial 
pressure. 
After the adsorption reaches a maximum, the desorption step emerges which is a procedure of 
evacuating the nitrogen from the pore space recording its partial pressure in the system.  
SSA corresponds to the adsorption of the monolayer with nitrogen. Monolayer is the single 
layer of nitrogen molecules that contacts the entire chalk surface while multilayer refers when 
nitrogen molecules are packed in layers in the pore space (Figure 2.4).

2.2.5 
Low – field Nuclear Magnetic Resonance spectroscopy 
Low-field nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is a technique accepted in 
mixtures of grain material with a fluid to present knowledge such as fluid type, fluid quantity 
(porosity), and pore size distribution (Coates et al., 1991).  
The low-field NMR spectroscopy picks up a sharp signal from hydrogen and since hydrogen 
is abundant in water, hydrogen is the primary source of detection of a system containing 
water. 
Due to spins of the protons and neutrons, the atomic nucleus possesses a nuclear magnetic 
moment. When the number of protons and/or neutrons are even, the atomic nucleus is in the 
ground state and their magnetic moment is constantly zero. The NMR spectroscopy is 
relevant to any nucleus of an odd number of protons or neutrons, or both.  
The first step in the NMR spectroscopy applied on a sample, is the polarization of the nucleus 
possesses a magnetic moment towards the longitudinal direction with an external static 
magnetic field, 𝐵0. The application of the external magnetic field exerts a torque inducing the 
alignment of the nuclear spin axis of the nucleus possesses the magnetic moment. The 
nuclear spin axis proceeds around the applied magnetic field in motion pattern referred to as 
precession. The angular/precessional frequency at which the nucleus precesses is called 
Larmor frequency named after the Irish scientist Joseph Larmor.  
 
 
Figure 2.10: The nuclei precesses towards the static magnetic field (Soderberg, 2019). 
 
The second step involves the magnetization of the nuclei from the longitudinal direction 
towards a transverse plane with the use of an oscillating magnetic field (𝐵1) perpendicular to 
the static magnetic field. When a proton is at the low-energy state, it may pass to high-energy 
state by absorbing energy provided by 𝐵1. In an extension of that, the protons interactions 
develop a coherent phase that precess concurrently. Nuclear magnetic resonance is the term 
appropriated to describe those two phenomena.

has decreased, net magnetization does. When a 180° pulse is applied, the previously slower

Electrical double layer 
When an aqueous ionic solution is in contact with a solid surface, some ions may adsorb to 
counterbalance the solid surface charge. Electrical double layer (EDL) is the term utilized to 
characterize the structure that is set up by the ions on the surface of the solid. 
For a definite distance from a charged solid surface, there is an electrical potential induced 
between the excess of ions located close to the surface trying to neutralize its charge and the 
bulk solution. The electrical potential has been described to decrease exponentially away 
from the solid surface (Ehrenstein, 2001).  
The electrical double layer is split into two layers. 
• The first layer is claimed as the Stern layer. The Stern layer consists of ions 
oppositely charged from the solid’s surface charge and it possesses the highest 
electrical potential towards it. 
• The second layer is the diffuse layer. In the diffuse layer, the capacitance of ions 
interacting with the solid’s surface depends on factors such as thermal motion and 
electrical attraction (Israelachvili, 2011).

2.5 
The disjoining pressure hypothesis  
The disjoining pressure hypothesis points out that the cohesive Van der Waals attraction 
forces that act in contact cementation between two grains are counteracted by electrostatic 
repulsive forces induced in the overlapped electrical double layers (EDLs) of the grains 
(Figure 2.14)(Madland, 2013; Nermoen et al., 2018). Those forces may apprehended as 
independent variables and their net effect manifests the mechanical behavior of the material 
(Nermoen et al., 2018). While the cohesive forces depend on rock frame criteria, the 
electrostatic repulsive forces fluctuate conforming to the ions incorporated in the EDL. Yet, 
the surface potential extracted from the ionic composition in the EDL is pivotal for weighing 
the impact between grains (Madland, 2013).

2.6 
The electrostatic repulsive forces assessment 
A novel method suggests that the impact of the electrostatic repulsive forces can be studied 
when a system possesses powder particles and an aqueous ionic solution. In such a system, 
the powder particles will settle, and they will develop a sediment porosity conforming to the 
electrostatic repulsive forces magnitude.  
Any overlapping of the EDLs is prevented by electrostatic repulsive forces (Figure 2.15). 
Thus, any aqueous ionic solution that extends the thickness of the EDL will simultaneously 
strengthen the porosity in the powder sediment.  
When overburden stress acts on the powder sediment, the grains come closer to each other up 
to the point that the EDL related forces allow. The new equilibrium porosity is a consequence 
of the magnitude of the overburden stress and the EDL related forces.  
The DLVO theory can be adopted for the approximation of the electrostatic repulsive forces 
(Derjaguin & Landau, 1941; Verwey & Overbeek, 1948). The DLVO theory combines the 
effect of the EDL realated forces and the short-range Van der Waals attractive forces 
functioning between the powder particles.

2.7 
The calcite – brine system for the CaCl2, MgCl2, Na2SO4, and KCl brines 
While the monovalent ions (Cl-, Na+, and K+) has presented the weakest interaction towards 
calcite surface (Nystr et al., 2001), the divalent ions (Ca2+, Mg2+, and SO42-) are regarded as 
potential determining ions (PDI) in a sense that they have an affinity with the calcite surface 
(Nystrom et al., 2001; Pourchet et al., 2013; Song et al., 2017). 
Experimental evidence establishes that an excess of molar Ca-ions is encountered in the EDL 
of calcite compared with the aqueous solution in equilibrium (Huang et al., 1991; Pourchet et 
al., 2013). Pourchet et al. discovered experimentally a sharp adsorption tendency of Ca-ions 
into the calcite surface in low molar concentrations (Figure 2.16). They also identified a 
molar adsorption plateau of 6mM (𝐼≈1.8 ∙10−2𝑚𝑜𝑙/𝐿), and higher concentration of Ca-
ions in the aqueous phase would contribute to EDL compression, a phenomenon recognized 
as screening. Further, they indicated that the adsorption profile of SO4-ions in the calcite 
surface is rather analogous to Ca-ions (Figure 2.17). However, the capacity of SO4-ions in the 
EDL is seven-fold poorer than the Ca-ions (Pourchet et al., 2013). 
Song et al., (2017) proposed that Mg-ions behave almost similarly with Ca-ions towards the 
calcite surface, but Mg-ions input to surface charge is somewhat smaller than Ca-ions.  That 
can be originated from the larger hydrated size of Mg-ion compared to Ca-ion (Tansel et al., 
2006).

3.4.7 
pH and electrical conductivity measurements 
A magnetic stirrer bar was placed in the plastic container of the brine which was placed at the 
top of a magnetic stirrer plane (Figure 3.16). The pH probe was rinsed with DIW, dried softly 
with a napkin, and then immersed into the brine; hence, the measurement commenced. The 
brine was stirred at 300𝑟𝑝𝑚 for 10 seconds since more time in the stirring process could 
have induced an increase in fluid’s temperature which affects pH and electrical conductivity 
values. The pH recorded value was the one after the equilibrium of brine with the probe had 
been achieved. The equilibrium was verified by observing no difference in the pH value after 
a time period. The same principle applies to electrical conductivity measurements. After the 
measurements, the containers were sealed and set aside. 
The uncertainty evaluated from the analytical balance procedure accepted uncertainty and the 
corresponding probe uncertainty. It should be noted, that the uncertainty is sensitive for the 
brines of lower salinity and it minimizes when the high salinity brines are used. That is 
because the relatively lower mass of salt measured in the analytical balance has greater 
uncertainty because the instrument’s accepted uncertainty has a defined value.  
Since higher brine volume corresponds to a higher amount of salt utilized, it was selected to 
work with 100𝑚𝑙 or 50𝑚𝑙 brines when desired electrical conductivity was below 0.5𝑚𝑆/𝑐𝑚 
and not with the 30𝑚𝑙 brines.

3.4.8 
Vial synthesis 
The empty vials were placed in the analytical balance where their weight was recorded. 
Fourteen grams of brine fluid was added to the vials through a plastic pipette and that was 
accompanied by the addition of 10𝑔 of powder through a spoon and funnel. The 
measurement of the weight of the vial before and after the addition of the powder was the 
concept behind the identification of the fluid and powder mass used. The choice of the 
addition of the fluid prior to the powder lies in the idea that while powder is settling is 
becoming wet. Any potential gas bubble trapped may be dissolved in the previously degassed 
brine. The details of the vial synthesis can be observed in Tables D.1-D.4 in Appendix D. 
 
3.4.9 
Vial agitation 
The vials were agitated so the grain particles are brought into suspensions and they later settle 
naturally. The agitation procedure begins by placing the vials in the ultrasonic bath and 
operate it for two cycles of 15𝑚𝑖𝑛. The vials were rested for a single day and then they were 
brought into the vibration pad. The vials were placed over the vibration pad and while it was 
operating, they were tilted and twisted until they had completed two full cycles. The visual 
inspection of the vials was suggesting that the grain particles have been detached from the 
wall and the particles at the bottom of the vial have been brought to suspensions.

3.4.10 
Sediment column porosity determination preparation 
Prior to analyzing all the vials for their sediment column porosity by the NMR analyzer, the

The uncertainty of this experiment was solely from the NMR analyzer measurements 
uncertainty. The method for determining the uncertainty of NMR analyzer measurements has 
been described in a later experiment of this chapter. 
 
3.4.11 
Sediment column porosity determination 
In total, 92 vials were measured in NMR analyzer. Among them, two vials were synthesized 
with equilibrated water and one with ethylene glycol. 
The vials were placed into the socket of the NMR analyzer. The parameters related to the 
quality of the acquisition were set and the measurement began. The parameters can be 
identified in Table A.3 in Appendix A. Attention was paid that the decays of the spin echo 
train are fully developed. The obtained decays were handled in software WinDXP where the 
𝑇2 distribution profiles were generated. 
The sum of the yielded left and right area value of the 𝑇2 distribution profile conforms to the 
initial mass of water utilized in the vial synthesis. The pore fluid was determined as a 
percentage of the left area value relative to the sum and converted to mass by using the brine 
mass value from the vial synthesis procedure. The pore fluid mass was converted to volume 
by the application of the corresponding brine density. The grain volume was determined by 
dividing the grain mass with its density value. Thus, porosity is determined as the ratio of the 
pore volume to bulk volume. 
The uncertainty of this experiment is a consequence of the analytical balance measurements 
and the NMR analyzer’s uncertainty.

sediment column during the centrifugation process. The density measurements data from 
Sohnel were accepted (Sohnel et al., 1984). An exponential trendline was added as the best fit 
(Figure 3.17). The exponential function describing the behavior of density versus the mass 
content of the salt in the brine was employed for the calculations.

5.1 
Stevns Klint chalk powder characterization 
The carbonate content was acknowledged as the arithmetic mean of the two measurements as 
98%. This result fits well with the associated values for SK chalk (Hjuler & Fabricius, 2009; 
Katika et al., 2015). By this experiment, it was confirmed that the relatively big grain 
particles in the powder sample had a carbonate origin. 
The SSA was determined as 1.55𝑚2/𝑔. This result is in harmony with literature’s proposed 
values (Hjuler & Fabricius, 2009). 
By usage of the arithmetic mean the grain density was determined as 2.71𝑔/𝑐𝑚3. That value 
is precisely similar to the accepted value for chalk (Hjuler & Fabricius, 2009). 
Since chalk has high homogeneity and the specimens for the carbonate content, specific 
surface area, and grain density experiments were received from the plastic box where all the 
powder was stored, it was expected that all the powder spent in this study had identical 
properties. 
 
5.2 
Equilibrated water experiments 
According to Figure 4.2, a threshold of 40𝑝𝑝𝑚 of 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑂3 should be included in the DIW to 
produce equilibrated water with chalk. In Figure 4.2, it was seen that pH builds up as a 
function of concentration up to the 40𝑝𝑝𝑚 of 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑂3. At this value, the pH reaches a plateau 
and the water carries a pH value of around 9.8 at 40𝑝𝑝𝑚. That is a consequence of an 
equilibrium among the reactions 2.7-2.14. The value of pH equal to 9.8 is identical to the 
discoveries of Madsen for a closed system with components DIW and calcite (Madsen, 
2001). It should be expressed that if a significant amount of 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑂3 added to the system of 
DIW-chalk, more dissociated ions would end up in the aqueous phase and that would disturb 
the pH value. 
The results of Table 4.3 demonstrated that the pH value of the equilibrated water was 
untouched after the degassing procedure. The results are in accordance with the literature 
(Fricke et al., 2005). Since the equilibrated water system before degassing was a closed 
system, the amount of 𝐶𝑂2 was little. Thus, the amount of 𝐶𝑂2 lost during the degassing 
period was minuscule. It should be reported that the equilibrated water restored atmospheric 
air since it was exposed for hours in the atmosphere during the brine formulation procedure. 
The result of Figure 4.3 indicates that after 120ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟𝑠 the fluids of DIW, equilibrated water, 
and equilibrated water with chalk powder have been equilibrated with atmosphere. The 
equilibrated water with chalk powder balanced at the value of pH equal to 8.4 which is in 
excellent agreement with the measured value by Madsen (Madsen, 2001).

5.4 
 Natural settlement porosity  
According to Figure 4.6, the natural settlement porosity becomes well defined by the first 
24ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟𝑠. However, it was considered that 70ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟𝑠 is the minimum amount of time required 
to consume prior to the porosity measurements of the vials’ sediment column with the NMR 
analyzer. It was discovered that after 144ℎ𝑜𝑢𝑟𝑠 the porosity of the vial 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 (1) started to 
build up. It is presumed that this effect was induced by shifts in the ambient temperature. The 
experiment was conducted during the spring season at which there is a progressive advance in 
temperature towards the summer season. Experiments with 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 have confirmed that 
temperature is a pivotal factor in the ζ-potential and the weakening of calcite (Ahmadi et al., 
2019; Nermoen et al., 2018). 
The trends in Figure 4.7 demonstrate that for all vials, sediment column porosity increases 
until ionic strength is around 1.0 ∙10−2𝑚𝑜𝑙/𝐿. After this plateau, the porosity drops as ionic 
strength increases (screening). The equilibrated water vial had a sediment column porosity 
value slightly above 60%. 𝐾𝐶𝑙 and 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 vials achieved the highest sediment column 
porosity values at about 66% while the peak values for the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 and 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 vials were 
about 64%. At the very last value of ionic strength, the sediment column porosity of the 𝐾𝐶𝑙 
vial was about 62% while for the rest vials, it was in the range of 58% to 60%. The ethylene 
glycol vial yielded the lowest value at about 55%. 
It is expected that porosity increases because of ions are getting in the EDL of chalk 
developing greater electrostatic repulsive forces among grain particles. The particles push the 
other particles further apart. At higher ionic strength, porosity is reduced because of the 
excess of ions in the EDL does not change the charge in the mineral surface thus, shortening 
the length the electrostatic repulsive forces act. 
The value of the ionic strength with the peak porosity in the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 and 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 brines is in 
agreement with the ζ-potential measurements of Song et al. (2017) and the adsorption profile 
of Ca-ions presented by Pourchet et al. (2013). However, the trends of the 𝐾𝐶𝑙 and 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 
brines cannot be explained satisfactorily by the ζ-potential measurements.

The ethylene glycol and the equilibrated water are acknowledged to promote no electrostatic 
forces with the chalk surface. However, when the equilibrated water is in contact with chalk, 
it will evoke dissolution of chalk. The dissolved ions are those who can thus be adsorbed on 
the chalk’s surface and activate repulsive forces between neighboring grains.  
Figure 4.8 demonstrates that for raising the concentration of salt in the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 and 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 
brines used for the saturation of the chalk, the 𝑇2 distribution of the vials’ bulk fluid moves 
towards faster relaxation rates while for the 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 and 𝐾𝐶𝑙 brines it was not manifested 
such a profound tendency. That arose due to dispersed particles existed in the bulk fluid of 
the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 and 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 vials. The dispersed particles increased the surface area of the pore 
space in the bulk fluid shifting the 𝑇2 distribution towards faster relaxation rates. The y-axis 
magnitude of the bulk fluid in the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 and 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 vials was strengthened at high ionic 
strength by the downturn in the porosity of the sediment column. 
It is believed that the dispersed particles in the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 and 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 vials’ bulk fluid are coming 
from the dissolution products of chalk. As the concentration of salt in the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 and 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 
vials aqueous phase increases, the chalk dissolution does accordingly. That phenomenon is in 
accordance with studies in the literature (Gutjahr et al., 1996; Ruiz-Agudo et al., 2011). 
Alternatively, the formation of the precipitants 𝐶𝑎(𝑂𝐻)2 and 𝑀𝑔(𝑂𝐻)2 is not expected 
because those substances are developed at greater pH values (Um & Hirato, 2014). 
According to Figures 4.9 and 4.10, for all the vials’ sediment column pore fluid it was 
observed a double peak. That suggests the existence of two types of pores, the comparatively 
big and small pores. It is believed that during the application of the vibration pad in the 
agitation process, owing to inertia, the bigger particles of the powder does not extend to 
suspensions with the same ease as the smaller particles. Thus, the bigger particles will form a 
bottom layer in the vial and the smaller particles the upper. That generated the double peak 
since big particles will develop bigger pores than the small particles. 
When the electrostatic forces evolved in the powder are strong, bigger pores are grown. The 
bigger pores have a lower specific surface area and consequently the more to the right-hand 
side the 𝑇2 distribution profile is shifted. The most pronounced results of this conception 
were recognized in Figure 4.10.  
By studying the left-hand side plots of Figure 4.10, when porosity increased (𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 (0.4) 
and 𝐾𝐶𝑙 (2) vials), the left peak of the double peak decreased in y-axis magnitude whereas 
the right peak increased. Exactly opposite is the tendency in the right-hand side plots of 
Figure 4.10 where the higher ionic strength, weakened the y-axis amplitude in the right peak 
of the double peak and raised the amplitude of the left peak.

Since the lower examined porosity percentages were about 58%, the percentage of 58% was

5.6 
Centrifuged sediment column porosity 
The results of Figure 4.13 demonstrated no effect of the centrifugation process on the pH and 
electrical conductivity of the aqueous phase in contact with chalk since the variations 
appeared are in the accepted probes’ uncertainty. Based on the results of Figure 4.14, it was 
concluded that the centrifuged sediment column porosity has arrived at equilibrium after 
15𝑚𝑖𝑛 of centrifugation.  Based on Figure 4.15, there was no detectable change in porosity 
value with respect to the amount of time the vial was exposed to relax. That was particularly 
compelling since it allows the simulation of numerous overburden stress configurations. 
In Figure 4.16, the sediment column porosity of the vials varies from 40% to 46%. For ionic 
strength up to 1.0 ∙10−2 all the porosity values fluctuate in the range 43% to 45% without 
any perceptible trend. For higher salinities, the samples saturated with 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 manifested the 
highest porosity values, slightly above 45%. The 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 and 𝐾𝐶𝑙 samples recorded porosity 
values particularly in the range 43% to 44%. The lowest porosity values were detected in the 
𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 samples where the high ionic strength samples had porosity values between 41% 
and 42%.  
The range of porosities recorded includes values lower than what one can find in the Valhall, 
Tyra, and Ekofisk fields. The low porosities can be explained by a better packing between the 
grains since as it was demonstrated previously there were two different sizes or rock grains 
and a great grain size distribution can contribute to lower porosity values. It should be 
mentioned that after touching the chalk powder of the broken centrifuged vials, the texture 
was resembling a typical texture of a hard-chewing gum. 
It should be stated that in the high values of ionic strength of Figure 4.16, the applied 
overburden stress on the vials’ sediment column was lower even though a constant speed was 
employed in the centrifugation process. Since the centrifugation force depends on the density 
of the bulk fluid, and the increase of ionic strength implies a build up in density, the 
centrifugation force for relatively high ionic strength is lower. For instance, the overburden 
stress acting on the sediment column saturated with equilibrated water was calculated as

brine with the highest density (1074𝑘𝑔/𝑚3), it was 263𝑘𝑃𝑎. The same followed for the

5.8 
The weakening potential of the brines 
For weighing the weakening potential of the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2, 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2, 𝐾𝐶𝑙, and 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 brines on 
chalk saturated with, it should be acknowledged how the developed electrostatic forces can 
disturb the contact cementation of chalk. In this study, it was presented that in the natural 
settlement state, the 𝐾𝐶𝑙 and 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 brines can yield greater repulsive forces. However, 
when the overburden stressed simulated by centrifugation, which was more relevant to 
reservoir conditions, the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 brines provided the higher porosity values and consequently 
the strongest repulsive forces. As Nermoen et al. (2018) described the weakening of chalk 
depends on the distance between the grain particles of chalk’s solid frame. For a given chalk 
rock material there would be a distribution of distances among the chalk grains that the 
electrostatic forces govern. Thus, when assessing the weakening potential of the brines it 
should bear in mind that for some part of the rock 𝐾𝐶𝑙 and 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 brines can be more 
efficient while in some other part of the rock, the 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 brines. The latter brine’s weakening 
potential appears to be more pragmatic to reservoirs because of the distances among the 
grains in the centrifugation state of this study can better resemble with the distances among 
grains in the reservoirs due to similar porosity values.

6. Conclusions & Future Research 
6.1 
Conclusions 
In this study, the behavior of several ions upon altering the repulsive forces functioning 
among chalk particle surfaces was evaluated experimentally. To accomplish this, NMR 
spectrometry was utilized to project accurately variations in the porosity of sediment columns 
saturated with numerous ionic aqueous solutions. Two overburden stress configurations were 
considered. The grain natural settlement state and the centrifuged sediment column state. The 
pH and electrical conductivity of the original ionic aqueous solutions compared with that 
after equilibrating with chalk surface. Based on the findings of this study the subsequent 
conclusions can be summed up: 
• The NMR spectrometry can be particularly effective for the assessment of the 
electrostatic repulsive forces generated between neighboring grain particles. The 𝑇2 
distribution can be utilized for the assessment of the interaction of the system chalk-
water based on the appearance of dispersed particles/precipitants in the aqueous 
phase. 
• In the natural settlement porosity state, the recorded maximum porosity value for all 
the samples was manifested at ionic strength approximately 1.0 ∙10−2𝑚𝑜𝑙/𝐿. That 
was in accordance with the available 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 and 𝑀𝑔𝐶𝑙2 ζ-potential measurements in 
the literature. 
• In the natural settlement porosity state, 𝐾𝐶𝑙 and 𝑁𝑎2𝑆𝑂4 samples marked the highest 
porosity values while in the centrifuged state it was 𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙2 samples. Higher porosity 
values were translated as greater repulsive forces while lower values as screening. 
• The porosity of the sediment column from the vials did not build up after the 
centrifugation process with time. That allows the simulation of many possible 
overburden stress configurations acting on the vials’ sediment column. 
• The electrical conductivity results from the bulk fluid suggests that any precipitation 
has been overshadowed by chalk dissolution. 
 
6.2 
Future study suggestions 
The following topics were recommended for further study: 
• Firstly, it was suggested the experimental investigation of the connection of the 
electrostatic repulsive forces to the weakening effect. This is conducted by saturating 
core plugs with brines presented in this study and testing their mechanical behavior. 
• Secondly, it was proposed the assessment of the electrostatic repulsive forces acting 
among chalk powder grains at various overburden stress configurations thus, a sketch 
of the surface potential distribution to be drawn and modeled.
