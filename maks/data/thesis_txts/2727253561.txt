Abstract
In this thesis, I delve into the details of three distinct, but related, distance oracles. I
analyse their theoretical bounds and go through the proofs of why they work. Finally, I
implement the oracles and perform experiments to compare them with each other and
their theoretical bounds. I perform a variety of experiments to understand how these
oracles perform on different types of graphs and with slight modifications.

Introduction
In graph theory, the shortest­path problem is a much­studied subject. Here, the goal,
given a graph, is to obtain the shortest path between two given vertices. It is a prob­
lem with many real­world applications such as networking, traffic routing and handling of
autonomous vehicles. It is also a problem that has been studied heavily and has many
solutions with unique advantages and drawbacks.
To answer shortest distance queries, one might employ a Distance Oracle. A Distance
Oracle is a data structure that can quickly return an estimate of the distance between two
vertices in a graph. In order for this to add value, it should be faster than simply utilising
a classic single source shortest path algorithm. However, some time is needed to pre­
possess the graph and obtain the oracle. Given that we wish to return an estimate of the
graph distance between two vertices, we not only analyse our oracles on the traditional
time and space, but also on stretch, which gives an upper bound on the approximations
provided.
In this thesis, I will look at a series of distance oracles, starting with the one described
in the seminal paper by Thorup and Zwick [1]. By implementing this and performing ex­
periments, I will be able to compare the theoretical bounds with actual results. Using that
as a starting point, I will look at related oracles and perform a comparison of the their
performances with respect to several analysis parameters.
1.1
Related Works
This paper is in no means intended to be a complete overview of the world of Distance
Oracles. Besides the oracles related to the Thorup­Zwick model, several other families of
solutions to the approximate All­Pair Shortest­Path algorithm exists.
In 1993, more than a decade before the work of Thorup and Zwick, Althöfer Et al [2]
published a paper on construction of Sparse Spanners in Weighted graphs. In this, they
demonstrated a method for construction a subgraph of a graph, such that for a vertex pair
(u, v), their distance in the subgraph is at most a (2k −1) factor bigger than their distance
in the graph. They showed how this could be constructed for k > 0. This has the same
stretch as the construct that Thorup and Zwick would go on to describe, but has a query
time of O(n1+1/k), which is still dependant on the size of the graph.
In 2001, Cohen and Zwick published the paper All­pairs Small­Stretch Paths [3]. In this,
they introduce three related algorithms that all produce constant query time algorithms of
stretch 2, 7/3 and 3. The basic ideas of these algorithms all have to do with partitioning
the graph and running Dijkstra’s algorithm on these partitions. The downside of these
algorithms are that O(n2) space is needed as all estimations are stored in a table. Uri
Zwick would go on to coauthor the paper Approximate Distance Oracles [1], which we will
become better acquainted with in section 2.2.
Somewhat more mathematical in nature is Mendel and Naors paper on Ramsay parti­
tions and proximity data structures [4]. They achieve a constant query time with O(n1+1/k)

oracle over a sampled subgraph. In another [6], he showed how to reduce the query time
from linear to logarithmic time. We shall learn more about this in section 2.3.
In newer developments, Shiri Chechik have defined two algorithms with constant query

has 2k −1 stretch and O(n1+1/k) space. She obtains this result by computing two con­

distances between vertices in some selected subsets are calculated. Then, a series of
cluster­covers are computed, with the property that if two vertices are in the same cluster­
cover they are close in the graph. Using the cluster­covers first, we can get either an
upper or a lower bound on a graph distance, which we can later refine with the other
precomputed distances. The main drawback of this oracle, is the fact that it cannot be
used to estimate distances between every pair of vertices, as part of the preprocessing
is obtaining a subset of vertices called vhappy. Distance queries must then include one of
these vertices.

2
Distance Oracles
Let G = (V, E) be a weighted undirected graph consisting of n vertices and m edges with
positive weights. We wish to be able to quickly estimate the distance between any two
vertices (u, v) ∈V . We wish that the time required to do so should not depend on the
size of the graph, and that we have some control of the strengths of approximations. It is
assumed that graph distances are metrics. This means that for a distance between two
vertices u and v, δ(u, v), we have δ(u, u) = 0, δ(u, v) = δ(v, u), and δ(u, v) ≤δ(u, w) +
δ(w, v).
2.1
Naive Solutions
Before delving into the world of distance oracles, I will quickly motivate why these con­
structs are useful by summarising the naive solutions to the problem of giving the distance
between two vertices.
Perhaps the most obvious way of solving this problem is by simply running any well­known
graph distance algorithm and returning the result obtained from this. Using Dijkstra’s algo­
rithm, this would take O(m + n log n) time. While this does provide perfect measurement
of the distance between u and v, the dependence of the size of the graph on the query
time does mean that it will perform slowly on very big graphs.
By running Dijkstra’s algorithm for every vertex in the graph, we can precompute the dis­
tances between every vertex pair and store them in some table. In this way, we can ob­
tain O(1) time per query, while still returning exact distances. However, we need to store
O(n2) numbers, which again scales very badly with big graphs. Additionally, running Di­
jkstra’s algorithm for every vertex would result in a preprocessing time of O(nm+n2 log n).
Both of these naive solutions have their own unique advantages, but they suffer with
graphs over a certain size. Either query time or space consumption and prepossessing
time will explode. The interesting solutions to the problem lie somewhere between these
two. We want to obtain a data structure that has a lower space consumption than the sec­
ond solution, while having faster queries than the first solution. In order to obtain this, we
will have to sacrifice our perfect distances and instead return an estimation of the distance
between vertex pairs.
2.2
The Thorup­Zwick Oracle
The following solution, presented by Thorup & Zwick, is the Approximate Distance Or­
acle [1]. Here, we perform some preprocessing in order to gain the ability to answer
distance queries with some given stretch. After preprocessing, we obtain an oracle with
space usage O(kn1+1/k) and query time O(k), where k is a preprocessing parameter. We
can query this oracles with vertex pairs and obtain distances that are at most overestima­
tions by a factor of 2k −1.
2.2.1
Preprocessing
In the original paper, the preprocessing method is initially described for metric spaces,
and later adapted for weighted undirected graphs. Here, I will present the method for pre­
processing graphs directly. The preprocessing algorithm takes a graph, G, and produces
4

a Distance Oracle data structure. This can then be used to answer distance queries. Be­
ing a layered structure, we can choose a number of levels, k, which has an impact on the
accuracy of the oracle.
The preprocessing algorithm starts by performing a layered sampling of vertices with k
levels. The resulting sets are given the names A0, A1 ,..., Ak, with A0 being the set of all
vertices in G. Each element of Ai is sampled independently from A(i−1) with probability
n−1/k meaning that A0 ⊇A1, ... ⊇Ak = ∅. An element of Ai can be referred to as an
i­center.
Then, k iterations are performed, starting at i = k −1 and ending at i = 0. In each
iteration we do the following. We first wish to compute δ(Ai, v) for every v. This value
corresponds to the shortest distance from v to any i­center. During this process, we also
identify a witness pi(v). The witness should satisfy that δ(Pi(v), v) = δ(Ai, v). In other
words, the witness is the vertex in Ai with the minimum distance to v.
To accomplish the above, we run a slightly modified version of Dijkstra algorithm. Here,
we start by adding every element w ∈Ai to our priority queue. We further set pi(w) = w
and δ(Ai, w) = 0. Then, we run the algorithm as normal, saving the shortest distances in δ.
When performing any update of the form δ(Ai, v) = δ(u, v), we similarly set pi(v) = pi(u).
Once this has run through, we have obtained witnesses and distance to i­centers for every
v in O(m) time.
Now, we construct a cluster C(w) for each i­center that is not also a center of the suc­
ceeding level. In other words, we compute C(w) for every vertex in Ai −Ai+1. The cluster
consists of every vertex around the center, where δ(w, v) < δ(Ai+1, v). This is achieved
by growing shortest­path trees T(w) around each of the i­centers. During this process, we
also calculate and save δ(w, u) for u ∈T(w). It is worth noting that we have δ(Ak, v) = ∞
for every v, meaning that the clusters of elements in Ak−1 consist of the entire graph. From
these clusters, we can construct a bunch B(v) for every v. If v ∈C(u), then u ∈B(v).
Like the computation of δ(Ai, v), the above is also computed using a modified version
of Dijkstras algorithm. However, we stop the algorithm once a computed distance to a
vertex v is above that of δ(Ai+1, v). That way, we only visit vertices that are within the
relevant cluster. Because of this, we do not spend time according to the size of the entire
graph to accomplish this. Utilizing a Fibonacci heap, we have O(log n) time to pop the
minimum value in the heap. Given that ϵ(C(w)) is the set of edges that touch a vertex in
C(w), we spend a total of O(|ϵ(C(w)) log n) time to construct clusters. The conversion of
clusters to bunches can then easily be done in O(∑|B(v)|) time.
Most of the actions taken above take O(km) time. The outlier here is construction of
the clusters which takes time O(|ϵ(C(w))| log n) time. As the size of the clusters are the
same as the bunches, and since the expected size of the clusters are kn1/k, we can use
linearity of expectation to achieve ∑(n1/k|ϵ(v)|) = 2kmn1/k. This dominates the other
terms of preprocessing and thus the final time complexity is O(kmn1/k).
2.2.2
Space complexity
After preprocessing, we store Pi(v) and B(v) for i < k and v ∈V . Additionally, for a sub­
set of vertex pairs, we store the distance δ(u, v). The size of p, along with the computed
distances for the k layers, use O(kn) space. The bunches, along with the other computed
distances use space according to the total size of the bunches for every vertex. In other
words, O(∑|B(v)|). This gives us a total of O(kn + ∑|B(v)|). To analyse this further,

In order to return our estimation, we introduce a third temporary variable, w, that we ini­

query algorithm obviously runs in O(k) time.
2.2.4
Stretch
We wish to show that the above algorithm returns an answer that is at most a factor
2k −1 bigger than the minimum distance between any two vertices u and v. Formally,
we wish to show that δ(u, v) ≤dist(u, v) ≤(2k −1)δ(u, v). As the initial distance be­
tween u and w is zero, we simply return δ(u, v) if u is in the bunch of v. Otherwise, we
return two sum of two distances, which are guaranteed to be equal to or greater than
δ(u, v). This means that δ(u, v) ≤dist(u, v) is clearly satisfied. Now, we wish to show
that dist(u, v) ≤(2k −1)δ(u, v). As we are returning the sum of two distances, it makes

2.3
The Wulff­Nilsen Distance Oracle [6]
So far, when querying our oracle, we have effectively searched through at most k sets to
find a vertex to route through and estimate a distance. While it is not immediately clear
how to do it, Christian Wulff­Nielsen has shown how one can perform a binary search over
the our range from 0 to k −1, reducing the query time to O(log k). In this chapter, I will go
through the theory that enables this, with the intention of later implementing the oracle.

2.3.2
Additional Preprocessing
To have every iteration of the Wulff­Nilsen bdist­algorithm run in constant time, we need
to have the values of j maximising dj(u) precomputed. Wulff­Nilsen mentions that this
can be done naively in O(k log k) time per vertex, but since we wish to not break the pre­
processing time usage, he presents the following way to get this down to O(k) time per
vertex, resulting in O(kn) time for the entire graph.
For each vertex, we start by building a binary tree of sequences in the following way:
Build a binary tree where the sequence 0, 2, ..., k −4, k −2 is a the root. Identify an i,
that splits the sequence in two parts of similar size. Let the left child of the root be the
sequence 0, .., i and the right be i, ..k −2. Recurse until leaves of size 2 are found. We
can now, from the bottom up, identify a j maximizing dj(u) for each sequence in the tree
in constant time per sequence and O(k) time overall. Given any other sequence S, we
can obtain a subset of O(log k) elements of the tree, whose union is exactly S. Iterating
though these elements, we can thereby find the j maximizing dj(u) for that sequence.
We now wish to show that we only need to find the value for
k
log k sequences per vertex.
For each vertex, we can use the above method to obtain j for the range 0..i −2, where
i is the midpoints between 0 and k −1. When this is done, recurse on the ranges 0..j
and i..k −1. We stop when we reach a range of size at most log k. Doing this, we can
effectively sketch how our binary search is going to look and in doing so, only calculate the
value maximising dj(u) for ranges that there exists a possibility that we need. Since this
is at most
k
log k ranges, we have an processing time of O(k) per vertex and (nk) overall.
This is dominated by the time from Thorup­Zwick, meaning that our total preprocessing
bound stays unchanged.

2.4
The Chechik Distance Oracle [12]
Building upon the results shown in the previous chapter, we can improve the query time
even more while keeping the same memory bound. Shiri Chechik has accomplished this
task twice, using two different methods. In this chapter I will explain one of these, which
results in an oracle with the usual space bound, similar stretch, and a O(1) query time.
Before I get into the algorithm, a few preliminaries.
We introduce the symbol ⌈x⌉2∗, which for any number x equals 2⌈log2 x⌉. In other words, it
is x rounded up to the nearest power of 2. We can use the same notations for sets, where
⌈S⌉2∗= {⌈x⌉2∗|x ∈S}.
So far, I have only in passing mentioned oracles other than Thorup­Zwick and Wulff­
Nilsen. These are far from the only existing oracle. Another oracle is the Mendel­Naor
oracle [4], which gives O(n1+1/k) size, 128k stretch, O(n2+1/k log n) construction time and
constant query time. A complete walkthrough of this subject is outside the scope of this
thesis, and I will not go into details on how this works, but I will provide the following brief
idea behind the data structure.
From the set of vertices V , create sets X0, X1, ..., Xs, such that V = X0 ⊊X1 ⊊... ⊊
Xs = ∅. Now, we construct trees T1, T2, ..., Ts+1 from each of these sets. We assign labels
∆to our tree­nodes such that if v is the child of u, we have that ∆(v) ≤∆(u)/k for k ≥1.
Store the trees in an array. Mendel and Naor show that we can assign labels in such a
way that the least common ancestor between two nodes in such a tree has a label that is
an 128­approximation of the distance between two vertices v and u. They also show that
this can be calculated in constant time.

2.4.3
Time Analysis
I now show that the algorithm presented above indeed runs in constant time. We start
the algorithm by running the Mendel­Naor Oracle, which they have proven runs in con­
stant time. Then, we obtain the variable d. Here, the fact that there is a constant number
of powers of 2 between our lower and upper bounds lets us check this in constant time,
assuming we store even and even in dictionaries, meaning that this part runs in constant
time as well. By the same logic, dmin and dmax can be obtained in constant time as well.
When it comes to checking whether an index is terminal, we can first check whether, the
given index equals k −1. Otherwise we perform two set membership operations. This
also takes constant time.
We now want to demonstrate that all three branches of the algorithm also runs in con­
stant time. It is easy to see that all return values are either a call to dist with a terminal
index (meaning that dist runs in constant time), a direct approximation, or a legitimate

2.4.4
Space analysis
Having talked about time usage, I now present the following analysis of the space usage.
Following a previous claim that the asymptotic space usage is the same as the Thorup­
Zwick and Wullf­Nilsen data structures, we show that the newly precomputed constructs
can be stored without affecting the established O(kn1+1/k) bound.
We store I(v, j) and Dj(v) for every v ∈V and every even j between 2 and k −1.
This clearly gives a space consumption of O(n · k). We store even(v, d) and even(v, d) for
every v, and d. Using the notation ⌈B(v)⌉2∗to denote the set of distances between v and
vertices in the bunch of v, we easily see that these are no bigger than the already stored
bunches.
We also store the Mendel­Naor oracle, which per their analysis is O(nn+1/k), still fitting
within our bounds. Finally, the x1, x2, and x3 variables, which all obviously take up O(nk)
space each. Thus, we have shown that none of the additional constructs added to our
data structure have an effect on our space bound.

3.2.7
Sampling Strategies
In their paper, Thorup and Zwick present a method for derandomizing the set sampling
when creating the oracle over a metric space. They then go on to mention that more work
is needed in order to create a derandomized solution to create the sets A0, A1, ..., Ak ef­
ficiently. We can investigate some simple known methods to do so. While the problem
of choosing the optimal samples is provably NP­complete [11], we may be able to uti­
lize some heuristics in order to obtain samples that are better than the random ones. It
stands to reason that nodes of high degree exist on more shortest paths than nodes of low
degree. Therefore, it seems reasonable that using these as samples would yield better
results than using random nodes. One notable issue with the approach is that we may
run into situations, where the memory usage is greater than the theoretical value. In the
randomised approach, we can run the preprocessing algorithm again and hope for lower
memory usage. This is not possible with this approach, as the resulting oracle will use
the same memory every time. We can construct an example where the oracle simply
computes all distances.
Figure 3.16: Visual intuition behind the pitfall of sampling by degree
In the above graph, we have a completely connected subgraph to the left. This is con­
nected to a more sparse subgraph to the right by an edge with weight 1000. All other
edges can be considered to have an edge of 1. Consider the case where we sample
every vertex in the left subgraph and none in the right. We will, of course, calculate the
distances between the sampled vertices and all other. But, because of the distance be­
tween the two subgraphs, we will also calculate the distance from every non­sampled
vertex to every other. This means that we have calculated the distance between every
pair of vertices in the graph.
To find the vertices of highest degree, we can sort the list of vertices in the graph ac­
cording to their degree. This can be done in O(n log n) time which breaks our previous
preprocessing time bound.
Another reasonable assumption is that vertices that are more central, exist on more short­
est paths, than those which exist on the edge of the graph. Several definitions of graph
centrality exist, and one of the most common definitions is the Closeness Centrality [17].
Computing this value entails computing the sum of the distances from a vertex to every
other node. Of course, performing this calculation for every node in order to sample the
nodes with highest centrality would defeat the entire purpose of the creating the distance
oracle. However, it might still be interesting to see if using these vertices improve the
performance.
Both of the aforementioned strategies are subject to at least one notable shortcoming;
the strategy only takes into account values that are computed for each vertex individu­
ally. This could lead to cases where we only sample vertices in some small subgraph

the graph. For this we can use a k−center algorithm. Of course, solving the k−center
problem exactly is NP­hard [18], but we can employ a greedy 2­approximation and see if
this yields any interesting results.

One important point about the implementation is the fact that the Mendel­Naor oracle
were not implemented. As we still need to obtain the initial 128k estimation needed for
the query algorithm, a Thorup­Zwick oracle with that stretch was used instead. When
timing the query, we exclude the runtime of this from the result. This also means that the
preprocessing time and space usage will differ from the theoretical results.
Besides this, the newly added constructs all take the form of two­level dictionaries. We
introduce dictionaries for D, I, even, even, x1, x2 and x3. As en example, D[v][i] in the im­
plementation corresponds to Di(v). The preprocessing of B, p and δ has been left exactly
as before.

5
Conclusion
I have provided definitions and analysis of the Thorup­Zwick, Wulff­Nilsen and Chechik
distance oracles, of which the two latter build on the first. In each case, the oracle was
implemented, and experiments were run to test the oracles real­world performance in
comparison to the theoretical bounds. The results of these have been discussed with
consideration for the practical application of these data structures.
I have investigated the usability of the distance oracle on various archetypes of graphs,
and noted the performances on each of these. This information is relevant in case one
would want to obtain an expectation of an oracles performance that is not a theoretical
upper bound.
I have looked into various ways of replacing the random selection that is present in every
oracle with a systematic and algorithmic approach, and shown why the obvious candi­
dates for such a process are not viable.
Perhaps the most interesting result of thesis has been the shown divide between the
theoretical and practical results of the oracles. As theoretical computer scientists we of­
ten, at least implicitly, consider O(1) to be the best possible result. When presented with
a problem, we often strive to come up with such a solution. However, as experiments in
this thesis has shown, in doing so, we might end up with a solution that is so involved that
anything gained theoretically is impaired by the complicated nature of the constant­time
solution.
A similar point arises when looking at the four parameters on which we judge our ora­
cles. If we mainly look at an increase in k as a trade­off between memory usage and
stretch, we might fear that increasing k to the point where memory usage is minimized
will cause our estimations to become so big that they are barely useful. Yet, looking at
the results, we see that our estimations are still pretty good when doing so. The point of
these last two paragraphs is to underline how the theory and practise of computer science
plays together. It shows the synergy we get, when we use theory to understand, analyze,
and prove, while validating this using practical implementation.
