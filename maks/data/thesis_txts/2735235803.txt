Abstract
The SWOT mission, employing interferometric wide swath altimetry, significantly advances
satellite altimetry by providing high spatial resolution data, particularly beneficial for coastal
area analysis—a key focus due to their importance in climate change impact and effect
on coastal communities. We uncover that SWOT’s advanced spatial resolution and com-
prehensive two-dimensional coverage provide an unprecedented level of detail in studying
tides within complex coastal environments, significantly enhancing what traditional nadir al-
timetry could achieve. Yet, this improvement in data quality introduces fresh challenges for
tidal research, notably in the management of intertidal regions to avoid data pollution and
secure precise tidal measurements. Notwithstanding these obstacles, SWOT’s introduction
offers finer details in coastal modeling, particularly valuable in locations where integrating
tide gauge data is impractical. Moreover, the detection of the tidal bore in the Bristol Chan-
nel as a novel observation highlights SWOT’s capacity to redefine coastal surveillance and
aid in adaptation and mitigation strategies for future sea level change.
Resumé
SWOT-missionen, der anvender interferometrisk altimetri, fremmer betydeligt satellitaltimetri
ved at levere data med høj rumlig opløsning, hvilket er særligt gavnligt i analyser a fkystnære
områder - et nøglefokus på grund af deres betydning i forhold til klimaændringers indvirkn-
ing og effekt på kystsamfund. Vi viser, at SWOT’s avancerede rumlige opløsning og om-
fattende todimensionale dækning giver et hidtil uset detaljeniveau i undersøgelsen af tide-
vand inden for komplekse kystmiljøer, hvilket betydeligt forbedrer det, som traditionel nadir-
altimetri kunne opnå. Dog introducerer denne forbedring i datakvalitet nye udfordringer for
tidevandsforskning, især i håndteringen af intertidale regioner for at undgå degradering af
data og sikre præcise tidevandsmålinger. På trods af disse udfordringer giver SWOT mu-
ligheden for at opnå en højere detaljegrad i de kystnære områder for tidevandsmodeller.
Desuden fremhæver detektionen af tidevandsbølgen i Severn River SWOT’s kapacitet til at
forbedre kystovervågning og bistå i klimatilpasnings strategier, med særlig fokus på frem-
tidige havniveaus ændringer.

Introduction
Satellite altimetry plays a crucial role in contemporary oceanography by providing vital mea-
surements of Sea Surface Height (SSH)). These measurements are foundational for a
broad spectrum of applications, including examining climate change, understanding ocean
currents, determining the Earth’s geoid, enhancing weather forecasting, and more (Ablain et
al., 2015; Andersen & Knudsen, 1998; Dohan & Maximenko, 2010). Starting with the launch
of the TOPEX/Poseidon (T/P) mission in 1992, the endeavor to measure SSH in open ocean
areas has been notably successful, achieving high levels of orbital precision and accuracy
(Tapley, 1994). Subsequent missions, such as Jason-1, Jason-2, and Jason-3, have con-
tinued this trend, providing globally averaged Root Mean Square (RMS) accuracy of less
than 3.4 centimeters (Le Traon, 2013). This precision has been instrumental in mapping
Mean Sea Level (MSL) changes over extended periods.
Following the success in open-ocean settings, there has been a natural progression to
extend altimetry techniques to coastal regions. Approximately 40% of the world’s popula-
tion resides within 100 kilometers of coastal shores, making these areas crucial for eco-
nomic, social, and environmental dynamics, but also vulnerable to sea level rise (Nicholls &
Cazenave, 2010; Reimann et al., 2023). Tides stand as a cornerstone in coastal oceanogra-
phy, exerting a profound influence across numerous domains including navigation, coastal
management, environmental conservation, and climate science (Haigh et al., 2020; Pethick,
2001). Understanding the dynamic ocean is crucial in accurately assessing changes in the
static ocean. Thus, extending satellite altimetry to the coastal domain becomes indispens-
able, enabling us to capture the full extent and consequences of changing sea levels (Srini-
vasan & Tsontos, 2023). However, utilizing altimetry near the coast introduces numerous
challenges, due to the complexity of tides in these regions, limited spatial coverage, and
land contamination of the radar returns (Vignudelli et al., 2019).
Building upon this foundational understanding, this thesis focuses on the innovative altime-
try satellite mission, Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT). SWOT represents a
paradigm shift in our ability to monitor and understand Earth’s oceans, particularly coastal
areas, by providing a detailed, high-resolution view of SSH. The significance of this mission

==PAGE:12==
is anchored in its revolutionary ability to observe coastal oceanographic phenomena with
unparalleled detail. It represents the first satellite mission to utilize Interferometric Wide
Swath (IWS) altimetry. This innovative approach not only harnesses signal power, akin
to conventional altimetry, but also leverages the phase difference between two onboard
antennas. This technique facilitates observations in a two-dimensional grid with an approx-
imate resolution of 20 meters over a 120-kilometer-wide swath, while maintaining a 20-
kilometer nadir gap (Altenau et al., 2021). This represents a substantial enhancement over
traditional altimetry methods, which typically only provide SSH observations approximately
every seven kilometers along a one-dimensional track, thereby dramatically expanding our
ability to study and understand the complexities of coastal and oceanic dynamics (Vignudelli
et al., 2019).
The study presented here was initiated to explore the novel capabilities offered by the SWOT
mission. Initially, the research direction was ambiguous due to the absence of data at the
outset in late summer of 2023, compounded by the pioneering nature of the SWOT mis-
sion. The availability of KaRIn synthetic data in late August and the subsequent release of
the first beta pre-validated data in September marked the commencement of data-driven
insights. Throughout autumn 2023, successive data updates culminated with the release of
high-resolution pixel cloud data in November. These developments enabled detailed anal-
yses across various regions, including Denmark, Norway, and Greenland, among others,
serving diverse objectives. The project’s final direction crystallized in November, driven by
the initial success in identifying tidal constituents and observing the Tidal Bore in the Bristol
Channel and Severn Estuary, something which has not been achievable with conventional
altimetry. The region stands out as an exceptional case study for evaluating the SWOT
mission’s capabilities in coastal areas, primarily due to the high tidal range, the intricate
complex patterns of wetting and drying, and the availability of data from tide gauges. These
characteristics offer a unique opportunity to explore the precision and efficacy of SWOT in
mapping and understanding coastal dynamics in difficult environments, further highlighting
the mission’s potential to revolutionize coastal monitoring and management. We compare
SWOT’s performance in SSH measurement against local tide gauges and the global Finite
Element Solution (FES) 2014 model. Additionally, we discuss observations of the tidal bore
in the Bristol Channel.

Research Background
The emergence of SWOT presents unprecedented opportunities to integrate traditional al-
timetry with new methodologies, facilitating the monitoring of SSH and offering enhanced
insights into global tide modeling. However, before delving into the deeper theory and anal-
ysis of SWOT data, it is imperative to grasp the utility and constraints of conventional al-
timetry, tide gauges, and tide models.
2.1
Satellite Altimetry: Monitoring Sea Level
The advent of nadir satellite altimetry with missions like T/P in the early 1990s marked the
beginning of an era where measuring SSH from space became possible (Schrama & Ray,
1994). These missions were designed to observe large-scale oceanographic phenomena
such as currents and tides and to monitor global sea-level rise with high accuracy and spatial
coverage (Cazenave et al., 2001).
However, the transition from open ocean to coastal regions introduced new challenges.
Coastal zones are complex, with various land-sea interactions, smaller-scale oceanographic
processes, and diverse topographies, all of which can reduce the accuracy of altimetry data
(Vignudelli et al., 2011). Yet, the most significant issue faced by conventional altimetry is the
contamination of the altimeter’s footprint by land surfaces. As the satellite approaches the
coast, the returned signal increasingly includes components bounced off the land, distort-
ing the waveform used to calculate sea level. Moreover, standard geophysical corrections
applied to the altimeter’s data, such as ionospheric, tropospheric, and sea state bias cor-
rections, are less effective near coastlines due to land interference and the dynamic nature
of coastal environments (Fernandes et al., 2021).
Recent years have seen substantial improvements in coastal altimetry, thanks to new satel-
lite missions and advancements in technology. The Satellite with ARGOS and ALTIKA
(SARAL) mission, operating at Ka-band frequencies, showed that a smaller radar foot-
print could reduce land contamination, providing more reliable data closer to shorelines
(Valladeau et al., 2015; Verron et al., 2015). Similarly, the advent of Synthetic Aperture
Radar (SAR) in missions like CryoSat-2 and Sentinel-3 has led to higher resolution and more

5
==PAGE:15==
accurate sea level data (Boy et al., 2016). These advancements are complemented by so-
phisticated retracking algorithms and improved correction techniques, designed specifically
for the complexities of the coastal zone. For instance, new methods like the Adaptive Lead-
ing Edge Subwaveform (ALES) retracker and the GNSS derived Path Delay Plus (GPD+)
wet tropospheric correction have significantly improved the quality and quantity of valid mea-
surements near the coast (Fernandes & Lázaro, 2016; Passaro et al., 2014).
Despite these advancements, challenges persist in coastal altimetry. The proximity to land
continues to impact the quality of the altimeter’s waveform, and the performance of correc-
tions like the sea state bias correction degrades near coastlines. Furthermore, the rapid
spatial and temporal changes typical of coastal zones demand even higher resolution and
more sophisticated models to capture the dynamics accurately. Each coastal region has its
unique geophysical processes, making a one-size-fits-all approach ineffective and necessi-
tating region-specific solutions and continuous innovation in altimetry techniques (Vignudelli
et al., 2019).
In conclusion, advancements in conventional satellite altimetry, driven by new technologies
and processing tools, have expanded its reach from the vast open oceans to also enter the
domain of dynamic and complex coastal zones. While these enhancements in conventional
altimetry have facilitated improved surveillance of coastal regions, significant limitations re-
main, notably in spatial resolution and land contamination issues (Vignudelli et al., 2019).
The introduction of the SWOT mission heralds a new era of coastal observations, equipped
with unparalleled spatial resolution and the ability to provide SSH observations across a
two-dimensional grid, offering fresh perspectives and capabilities previously unattainable in
the study of coastal environments.
2.2
Tide Gauges
Tide gauges, with their historical roots stretching back to ancient times, are indispensable
for chronicling sea level changes along coastlines. Notable records from Amsterdam dating
back to 1700 (Veen, 1945), Liverpool since 1768 (Woodworth, 1999), and Stockholm be-
ginning in 1774 (Ekman, 1988), highlight their long-term value in tracking sea level trends
and variability. The evolution of tide gauges significantly advanced in the 1830s with the
advent of automatic versions, first deployed in the Thames Estuary, England (Matthäus,
1972). By the late nineteenth century, these instruments had spread globally, forming an
Chapter 2: Research Background
6
==PAGE:16==
early but expansive observation network (Douglas, 1998) (Jevrejeva et al., 2014).
Tide gauges can capture the tidal dynamics of coastal areas, often overlooked by conven-
tional satellite altimetry (Saraceno et al., 2008). Limitations of satellite altimetry become
particularly pertinent given the substantial variations between sea level signals in the deep
ocean and those along the coast. These discrepancies are influenced by factors like ocean
stratification and the shape of the continental slope (Hughes & Meredith, 2006), as well as
friction that may confine open-ocean Rossby waves to coastal regions (Huthnance, 2004).
Additionally, the movement of coastally trapped waves, a source of differences between
coastal and deep-ocean sea level changes (Holgate & Woodworth, 2004), is something
satellite altimetry cannot adequately capture. Therefore, tide gauges are indispensable in
providing detailed data on shallow-water tidal effects, including compound and over-tide am-
plitudes (Cipollini et al., 2017), and demonstrate how local geography, especially in fjords,
can amplify tidal waves.
Despite their widespread deployment, primarily across the Northern Hemisphere’s coasts
and selected oceanic islands, the distribution of tide gauges is uneven, with a marked de-
crease in records before 1960. Moreover, these devices, fixed to the land, provide sea level
measurements relative to their immediate geological setting. This anchorage introduces
complexities due to potential land movement, whether from natural processes like glacial
isostatic adjustment or human-induced factors like groundwater extraction (Wöppelmann &
Marcos, 2016). Additionally, a unified reference level for individual tide gauge records is ab-
sent, despite explicit suggestions for establishing one (e.g., (Woodworth et al., 2012)). This
lack of standardization poses challenges in aggregating and comparing records accurately.
Moreover, given that tide gauges are typically positioned along coastlines, offer coastal sea
level measurements, and inherent challenges that altimeters face in coastal zones, studies
that have attempted to correlate or contrast altimeter-derived with tide gauge-derived sea
level rise have essentially been limited to comparing rates across disparate locations. This
underscores a profound knowledge gap, particularly in parts of the world’s coastline—often
in vulnerable areas of developing countries—that lack direct sea level measurement infras-
tructure. In these regions, satellite altimetry emerges as the exclusive source for tracking
sea level variations.

A critical aspect of this knowledge gap pertains to the understanding of coastal tides, which
play a significant role in various oceanic and geodetic applications, including sea level mea-
surements, gravity field determination, and the production of renewable energy. The quest
for precise tidal signal extraction has spurred extensive efforts to develop accurate tide
models at both regional and global scales (Andersen et al., 2023a; Hart-Davis et al., 2023;
Lyard et al., 2021).
2.3
Global Tide Models
One of the primary motivations behind early research into ocean tides was the essential
requirement to account for their effects as a geophysical correction to accurately derive
estimates of Sea Surface Height Anomaly (SSHA) from satellite altimetry. By incorporat-
ing these corrections effectively, researchers could delve deeper into understanding various
sea surface processes, including SSHA and surface currents. These models can be catego-
rized as either satellite-derived (empirical), data-constrained, or data unconstrained. Histor-
ically, models relying on satellite altimetry observations have demonstrated superior perfor-
mance in accurately estimating tides, largely owing to the long-term observations provided
by satellite altimeters like T/P, the Jason missions, and the Sentinel missions, which have
operated on a tide-favorable orbit for over three decades (Stammer et al., 2014). Recent
empirical models have adopted a similar approach, utilizing SSHA data corrected for ocean
tides from altimetry missions to derive residual tidal signals, thus generating semi-empirical
tide models. This methodology, successfully employed in various models, involves applying
residual tidal signals back to the original tidal corrections (Cheng & Andersen, 2017; Ray,
2013; Savcenko & Bosch, 2007).
Data-constrained or data-assimilating numerical models, such as FES2014 (Lyard et al.,
2021), offer high-resolution estimates of tidal constituents, which is particularly valuable in
coastal regions. Typically, resultant tide models provide gridded maps of tidal constituents,
which can be converted into tidal heights, and subsequently applied to satellite altimetry
data.
FES2014 and other global models typically demonstrate robust performance in open ocean
regions; however, their accuracy tends to decrease near coastal areas where local dynam-
ics exert greater influence (Lyard et al., 2021). Tide gauges play a crucial role in elucidating
the intricate interactions influencing sea level variations along coastlines, thereby aiding in

8
==PAGE:18==
the refinement of these models. Nonetheless, their spatial coverage is inherently limited,
primarily confined to locations along the shore. Consequently, while tide gauges offer valu-
able insights, they cannot fully capture the complexity of coastal dynamics, which merits
complementary monitoring from the SWOT mission.
2.4
Objectives
Recent global assessments of tide model accuracy have pinpointed coastal areas as ex-
hibiting the most considerable discrepancies when benchmarked against in-situ measure-
ments (Stammer et al., 2014). This disparity underscores the imperative need to refine tide
modeling and understanding of tides in coastal regions to boost the precision of sea level
measurements and enhance the effectiveness of applications dependent on accurate tidal
data. The urgency of addressing these modeling challenges extends beyond the scientific
pursuit of understanding coastal dynamics; it becomes vitally important in the context of sea
level rise’s impact on human societies and natural ecosystems, which will predominantly oc-
cur along coastlines. Research indicates that with a sea level rise of merely 90 cm by the
year 2100, the potential number of global individuals facing the threat of flooding, assuming
no proactive relocation, is projected to soar to 100 million or more (Hauer et al., 2016; Hinkel
et al., 2014).
This study explores SWOT’s capability to deepen our understanding of tidal dynamics and
sea level variations in coastal zones, particularly through the analysis of coastal SSHA and
tidal phenomena in the Bristol Channel and Severn River, utilizing tide gauge comparisons.
The objectives of this study are threefold.
1. First, we will compare SSHA from SWOT with local gauge stations, to assess the
accuracy of various SWOT products in the CAL/VAL orbit.
2. Second, we will investigate how SWOT products can be utilized to determine tidal
constituents, with a focus on the dominant semi-diurnal constituent M2.
3. Third, we will explore novel utilization possibilities of SWOT to investigate geophysical
phenomenons, by focusing on the tidal bore up the Severn River as a case study.
These three objectives are discussed separately in Chapter 7, 8, 9 respectively.

Coastal Tide Dynamics
Tidal phenomena on Earth primarily stem from the gravitational pull of the moon and the
sun. Despite the sun’s larger size, the gravitational pull of the moon on Earth is about
twice as strong as that of the sun, as a consequence of the moon’s proximity to Earth and
gravitational inverse square law. Tides arise from the differential in gravitational pull on
different parts of the Earth and the centrifugal force due to the rotation of the Earth-moon
and Earth-sun systems (see Figure 3.1). The centrifugal force is uniform throughout the
Earth (orange arrows), whereas the gravitational pull from the moon (green arrows) and
sun decreases with distance from the influencing celestial body.
At Earth’s center of mass, gravitational forces from the moon and centrifugal forces can-
cel each other out, but elsewhere, their discrepancy generates tide-generating forces. The
vertical components of the resulting force effect are marginal compared to the Earth’s own
gravitational pull, and it is therefore, the horizontal components, tractive forces, that are re-
sponsible for mobilizing oceanic waters, generating extensive long wavelength tidal waves
that materialize in the ebb and flow of sea levels. Earth’s rotation leads to two high and two
low tides each day, known as semi-diurnal tides, due to the changing gravitational pull of
the moon as the Earth spins. The semi-diurnal lunar tidal constituent, called M2, represents
the lunar semi-diurnal tide, occurring every 12.42 hours rather than 12 due to the moon’s
orbit around Earth. This extra time accounts for the change in the moon’s position relative
to Earth as Earth rotates.
The concept of an ”equilibrium tide” refers to the theoretical sea surface level in a scenario
where Earth is entirely water-covered, with the water reacting instantly to tidal forces. This
idealized model differs from the actual ocean tides, affected by the continents’ obstruction
and the water’s inertia. The actual tide’s complexity arises from water’s delayed reaction to
tidal forces, the continents obstructing tidal wave flow, and the varying positions and motions
of the Earth, moon, and sun across different planes, as well as their shifting distances.
Nonetheless, the equilibrium tide acts as a vital reference point in the computation of the
observed tide.
The cyclical nature of sea levels has been the subject of extensive research for centuries.

Notably, in the 17th century, Isaac Newton significantly advanced tidal research by expli-
cating gravitational forces. The terminology and algebraic framework widely used in tidal
studies today were substantially developed by A. Doodson. His work laid the foundation for
further in-depth analyses of tidal potentials, notably advanced through Fourier analyses by
Cartwright and Tayler, 2007, as well as Cartwright and Edden, 1973. These contributions
have been fundamental in shaping our current understanding of tides.
The disparity between the equilibrium tide and the observed tide is significantly influenced
by the geographical location, bathymetric characteristics, and topographical features of the
specific area.
A perfect example of this is the Bristol Channel, with one of the largest

amplifying tidal forces, leading to remarkable tidal behaviors. As the water is pushed and
pulled by these forces, the interaction between the ocean’s vast energy and the channel’s

3.3.2
Non-linear tidal dynamics
Nonlinear tidal dynamics in shallow waters emerge from the interaction of oceanic motion
equations with tidal potentials. If we take an instance of a tidal oscillation characterized by
a singular frequency, denoted as ω1, an additional harmonic component of frequency 2ω1
may manifest in stationary water bodies. When two frequencies, ω1 and ω2, interact due to
nonlinear terms like advection V · ∇V, the resultant tidal response will include harmonics of
2ω1, 2ω2, ω1 −2ω2, and 2ω1 + ω2 (Godin, 1972). Concurrently, the friction term would yield
ω1, ω2, 3ω1, 3ω2, 2ω1−ω2, 2ω2−ω1, 2ω1+ω2 and 2ω2+ω1. For example, for a combination of
the principal tidal constituents M2 and S2, the advection term would produce M4, S4, MS4,
and MSf.
When a tidal wave comprises several harmonic frequencies, the spectrum of shallow water
tidal frequencies can be expansive. These additional frequencies are generally split into
two primary groups. The first consists of shallow water constituents with distinct astronom-
ical arguments, enabling their theoretical separation from other constituents. The second
group includes shallow water constituents whose astronomical arguments align with those
of existing constituents, making them indistinct (Amin, 1976).

3.3.3
Superposition of tidal constituents
The generation of higher harmonic waves in tidal systems is primarily due to non-linear
effects. Yet, once formed, these harmonics engage in linear interactions with other tidal
constituents, adding complexity to the overall tidal behavior. The superimposed effects of
a primary harmonic wave and its higher harmonics can introduce variations to the primary
wave’s typical symmetrical pattern. Such variations can manifest as irregular occurrences
of high or low tides, contingent upon the phase differences between the interacting waves.
However, these interactions do not give rise to new frequencies (D. Pugh & Woodworth,
2012).

3.4
Tidal Aliasing
The utility of satellite altimetry for ocean tide measurements is inherently constrained by
the temporal resolution dictated by the satellite’s orbital design. All altimetry satellites have
a set repeat cycle ranging from a few days to about a month. This leads to diurnal and
semi-diurnal tides manifesting as signals with extended periods when observed from a fixed
location, a phenomenon known as tidal aliasing (Andersen, 1995).
According to the Shannon sampling theorem, to accurately sample a signal, the sampling
frequency, fs, should be at least twice the highest frequency present in the signal, fmax;
that is fs ≥2fmax (Dodson, 1992). This sampling frequency fs is referred to as the Nyquist
frequency (Landau, 1967). In practical terms, a signal with a period Tk can be adequately
reconstructed if sampled at intervals shorter than Tk/2. If not, a signal of period Tk will be
aliased, meaning it appears with a longer, aliased period τp.
The aliased period τp is the interval required to sample the aliased signal and properly
reconstruct the original frequency fk. Due to factors like data noise, oceanic circulation
dynamics, and data gaps, satellite altimetry observations often span a duration greater than
τp.

3.6
Tidal Bore
A tidal bore is a fascinating hydraulic event where a strong tide results in a swift rise in
river water levels, forming a moving wavefront or a series of waves known as a surge. The
formation of a bore is influenced by specific estuarine conditions such as a flat riverbed, a
converging shape of the estuary funneling the tide into a narrow region, and a substantial
tidal range, usually above 6 meters (Bartsch-Winkler & Lynch, 1988).
In the Bristol Channel, the tidal bore typically occurs shortly after low water when the tide
is rising rapidly. This is because the bore is a result of a strong incoming tide pushing up
against the outflowing river water. So, on a graph of tidal heights, you would expect to see
the tidal bore occur as an abrupt rise in the water level on the leading edge of the incoming
tide, after the low tide and before the peak of high tide is reached (Gao & Adcock, 2017).
Despite their regularity, the formation and dynamics of tidal bores in estuaries are highly
complex phenomena, influenced by factors like channel bathymetry and river flow, mak-
ing them a persistent subject of study. Their environmental impact is significant, affecting
ecosystems in numerous estuaries worldwide (Bonneton et al., 2015).

Coastal Zone Radar Altimetry
Satellite radar altimetry has been a transformative tool in oceanography, delivering nearly
three decades of global ocean surface topography data. This wealth of information has en-
abled significant advancements in our understanding of large-scale ocean circulation and
heat storage, crucially influencing our insights into climate phenomena such as El Niño and
La Niña, and sea level change. Although these observations hold for open-ocean altime-
try, a significant area still sees minimal utilization of this technology. This area is critically
important, aligning with the region where variations in circulation, sea level, and sea state
exert the most profound effects on human society: the coastal zone.

samples contaminated by non-ocean-like reflection. The power received at a given gate is
correlated with the relative fraction of sea and land areas in the corresponding footprint and
with the reflective properties of each type of surface (Gommenginger et al., 2011).

4.2
SWOT Altimetry
The SWOT satellite was launched into orbit on December 16, 2022, following an extensive
period of technological development and mission planning (Fu et al., 2009; Rodriguez et
al., 2017). Developed as a collaborative project between National Aeronautics and Space
Administration (NASA) and CNES, with contributions from The Canadian Standards Asso-
ciation (CSA), and The United Kingdom Space Agency (UKSA), the mission’s objective was
to acquire global, high-resolution oceanographic and hydrological observations.
The mission’s main instrument is a Ka-band Radar Interferometer (KaRIn) instrument which
employs IWS technology to capture 2D images of SSH and surface roughness across two
50-km wide swaths, separated by a 20 km nadir gap (Fjørtoft et al., 2014) (see Figure
4.2). A conventional Jason-class dual-frequency (Ku/C) nadir altimeter is utilized to provide
measurements within the central gap.
The nadir altimeter, leveraging the legacy and successful cross-calibration of numerous
preceding altimetry missions, boasts a well-documented instrument performance and can
act as the reference for long wavelengths. Cross-calibration with other nadir missions with
the same instrument, such as Sentinel-3 and Jason-CS, will also be conducted to ensure
consistency and accuracy across datasets. (Morrow et al., 2019).
The KaRIn instrument provides a basic measurement resolution of 2.5 m along-track, with
a cross-track resolution ranging from 70 m in the near-nadir swath to 10 m in the far swath.
The objective of IWS altimetry is the same as conventional altimetry and can be divided
into three main components. Firstly, it involves determining the satellite’s exact position, or

4.2.1
Precise Orbit Determinaton
To yield precise altimetry measurements it is essential to know the exact location of the
satellite in space and time. To estimate the position of the satellite center of mass and
velocity in orbit as accurately as possible, several instrument measurements are used. To
meet the 1.5 cm radial accuracy required for the operational orbit, SWOT, like the Jason
series of altimeter satellites, is equipped with state-of-the-art Doppler Orbitography and
Radiopositioning Integrated by Satellite (DORIS), Laser Reflector Assembly (LRA), and
Global Positioning System Payload (GPSP) tracking systems. In concert, these systems
deliver Precise Orbit Determination (POD) (Fjørtoft et al., 2014).

DORIS
SWOT is equipped with the same DORIS receiver as Sentinel-3. It affords the ability to

The operation of DORIS relies on a dense global network of ground stations transmitting

DORIS orbitography beacons transmit signals at two separate frequencies (2036.25 MHz

radial component.

Centre National d’Études Spatiales (CNES), and for the first time, DIODE also provides a

acquisition in areas like coastal zones, inland waters and ice (Dettmering et al., 2014).

4.2.3
Corrections
The ability of altimetry to precisely measure sea surface height hinges on the accurate de-
termination of the range from satellite to sea surface. This range is estimated as half the
round-trip time of the radar signal divided by the speed of light in a vacuum. However,
to transform this raw measurement into an accurate sea surface elevation, various instru-
mental and atmospheric corrections must be applied. A number of these corrections need
special attention close to the coast and in shallow water regions (Andersen & Scharroo,
Chapter 4: Coastal Zone Radar Altimetry
34
==PAGE:44==
2011). These corrections are briefly discussed below.
Range Corrections
Dry Tropospheric Correction (DTC) corrects for the fact that the velocity of an electromag-
netic signal is reduced by the presence of ”dry” gases and water vapor in the Earth’s tropo-
sphere. These gases affect the refraction index, with their impact varying based on density
and temperature. Under conditions of hydrostatic equilibrium and the ideal gas law, the
integrated range delay from the troposphere is functionally dependent on surface pressure.
Direct measurement of nadir surface pressure from a satellite is not feasible, so surface
pressure is inferred from model-assimilated meteorological data from European Center for
Medium-Range Weather Forecasting (ECMWF). The error spectrum for the dry troposphere
is encapsulated using results from the Chelton model (D. Chelton, 1994).
The Wet Tropospheric Correction (WTC) accounts for the delay caused by water vapor
and cloud liquid water in the Earth’s troposphere. Water vapor is the primary factor in this
correction, with liquid water contributions from clouds being typically less than one cen-
timeter. This correction is complex due to its significant temporal and spatial variability,
particularly in coastal areas where moisture content can rapidly change, resulting in cor-
rections ranging from a few millimeters in arid regions to over 30 centimeters in humid
areas. A three-frequency microwave radiometer, similar to the Advanced Microwave Ra-
diometer (AMR) flown on the Jason series, estimates columnar water vapor by measuring
the brightness temperature at three specific microwave frequencies. However, the footprint
of the radiometers is approximately 20km, and thus an ECMWF model is used for WTC
instead (∆Rwet).
The ionosphere’s impact on electromagnetic wave propagation arises from its abundance
of free electrons and ions, starting at altitudes above approximately 100 kilometers. Solar
radiation at these heights has sufficient energy to ionize atmospheric gases, stripping away
electrons. Between 100 to 300 kilometers, ions such as NO+ and O+
2 predominate, while
higher layers, extending well above the operational altitude of radar altimeters, are charac-
terized by the presence of ions like H+, O+, N +, and He+ (Bilitza, 2001). SWOT uses the
Ionex model (Schaer et al., 1998) to derive an envelope for the ionospheric error spectrum
over the global oceans (∆Riono).
The Electromagnetic (EM) or Sea-State Bias (SSB) arises from the differential reflection
Chapter 4: Coastal Zone Radar Altimetry
35
==PAGE:45==
of radar signals by ocean wave crests and troughs. Wave troughs reflect radar signals
more effectively than wave crests, leading to a bias where the calculated mean sea level
is shifted towards the troughs, causing an overestimation of the actual distance between
the satellite and the sea surface. Additionally, the natural asymmetry in ocean wave height
distribution, with a median inclined towards troughs, contributes to what is known as the
skewness bias. The correction (∆RSSB) is calculated using a parametric model given by
(Gaspar et al., 1994) (D. Chelton, 1994) utilizing the Significant Wave Height estimated
from the volumetric coherence of the Karin interferograms.
Geophysical Corrections
Since satellites purely measure ranges, observations are susceptible to tidal dynamics that
the satellite does not experience but that tide gauges do. Among these, pole-tide, earth-tide,
and loading tide corrections are crucial for isolating the ocean’s dynamic height contributors
to sea surface height variations.
The pole-tide correction accounts for the ocean’s response to variations in the Earth’s rota-
tion axis relative to its crust. These variations, known as polar motion, cause redistribution
of mass and, consequently, changes in sea level. The correction (∆Rpole tide) is computed
based on models that predict the ocean’s response to observed polar motion, ensuring that
altimetry measurements reflect sea levels independent of these geophysical signals (S. D.
Desai, 2002) (Wahr, 1985).
Solid Earth tides, caused by the gravitational pull of the moon and the sun, lead to deforma-
tions of the Earth’s crust and variations in the gravitational field, affecting sea levels. The
earth-tide correction (∆Rsolid earth tide) removes this effect from altimetry measurements. It
is computed using models of the Earth’s tide-generating potential and accounts for both
solid Earth and ocean tides, allowing for the accurate determination of sea surface height
changes due to ocean dynamics alone. The corrections are made based on a model de-
veloped by Cartwright and Edden, 1973.
Loading tides refer to the change in sea level due to the weight of the ocean tides themselves
on the Earth’s crust. This effect causes the crust to deform under the load of the water
mass, affecting sea levels and, thus, altimetry measurements. The loading tide correction
∆Rload tide is calculated using a model that estimates the deformation of the Earth’s crust
under tidal loads and the consequent sea-level changes, ensuring that the measured SSH
Chapter 4: Coastal Zone Radar Altimetry
36
==PAGE:46==
reflects true ocean surface variations without the influence of these loading effects (Ray &
Erofeeva, 2014) (Lyard et al., 2021).
Geophysical corrections are integral to processing satellite altimetry data, enabling the ac-
curate measurement of sea surface heights by accounting for geophysical phenomena that
influence sea levels.
Instrument Corrections
The last type of correction is an instrumental correction, which is either corrected by in-
ternal instrumental calibration loops (∆Rinternal), or cross-over calibration (∆RXover). The
instrumental error includes instrumental drift, system bias, thermal expansion of the beam,
Ultra-Stable Oscillator (USO) drift correction, etc. However, the most important errors for
SWOT are roll-errors and phase-errors which conventional altimetry does not experience
(Rodriguez et al., 2017). As we can see in Figure 4.3 the look angle (θ) significantly impacts
the horizontal position (x) and the SSH. The satellite attitude systems alone are not capable
of achieving satisfactory roll accuracy. Nonetheless, it is feasible to monitor and correct for
long-term drifts in attitude at this scale over time by utilizing crossover points, thanks to the
highly correlated nature of the error of the look angle. To address this issue, the approach
taken involves modeling the correction using crossover points in four crossover regions.
Assuming that these points change gradually, it’s feasible to interpolate the correction be-
tween them, ensuring accurate adjustments are made across the measurement span. This
technique is depicted in Figure 4.4.
The nadir altimeter on SWOT plays a crucial role in facilitating this correction. It serves as a
secondary, independent source of height observations, which are instrumental in assessing
the range observations at the crossover points. This enables the application of corrections
through a least squares inversion method, enhancing the accuracy of the measurements
(see Rodriguez et al., 2017 for further detail.). The accuracy of these corrections is ex-
pected to be superior in the 21-day repeat period product compared to the 1-day repeat
product. This improvement in accuracy is attributed to the increased number of crossover
points available in the longer repeat cycle, which results in a reduced interpolation distance,
enhancing the reliability of the correction process.

6.1.4
Outlier Removal
With the creation of the products that will be used in the thesis, the next step was to filter
clear outliers from the three products. This was especially important for the 50m grid product
which only has 19 days of observations. A single bad observation could have a big impact
on the data quality and the estimation of tidal parameters. Filtering outliers with a Standard
Deviation (STD) threshold involves defining boundaries around the mean (µ) of a dataset
based on its STD (σ). The boundaries are calculated as µ±kσ, where k is the chosen STD
threshold, here we choose k = 2. Data points outside these boundaries, given by µ ± kσ,
are considered outliers and are removed. This method, leveraging the principles of normal
distribution, ensures the exclusion of extreme values that could distort the data analysis.
This ensures that all unrealistic values are filtered. However, since the SSH exhibits a
periodic signal, this method is not the best filtration of outliers, especially not in the Bristol
Channel where the tidal range approaches 12 meters. This implies that an observation 6m
above the real value at low tide would not be filtered away. Therefore, another approach
was applied subsequently.
Periodic Outlier Removal
We here describe a systematic approach to identifying outliers within the dataset by fitting a
periodic function, a technique particularly useful for analyzing time-series data that exhibit
periodic behavior. The approach has been applied to each grid point in the three SWOT
products.
Initially, the time series for each grid point was assessed to ensure that a sufficient number
of data points was available for robust model fitting. Missing data points would either be due
to missing data in the area or due to outliers removed by the first outlier removal described
in the previous section. This was true for many grid points located in the intertidal mudflats,
where the alternating wetting and drying resulted in a chaotic time series. Should the dataset
be deemed insufficient, all observations were marked as outliers due to the lack of adequate
data for model fitting and the grid point removed from the dataset.
An initial guess of the model parameters was formulated based on the dataset’s statistical
characteristics, such as its STD and median. This guess aids in fitting a periodic function to
the SSH data, optimizing parameters to align the model closely with observed values. The

6.1.5
Filtration of Intertidal Mudflats
There are several approaches to detect grid points that run dry during low water. Here we
will explain two methods used in this analysis which both have their pros and cons.
Lowest Low Water Filtration
The simplest approach is to identify the day in the observational record with the lowest wa-
ter level and then filter out values based on a specified threshold. This method is straight-
forward to implement and offers reasonable precision since dry areas would exhibit higher
height values than the subtidal gridpoints in the proximity. However, it has limitations. It can
only be applied locally, and when shifting to a different region of interest, a new analysis must
be conducted. Moreover, tides propagate as long-wavelength waves, and within extensive
regions such as the Bristol Channel, various areas experience different tidal phases. Con-
sequently, relying on a single threshold value is not ideal, and more nuanced approaches
are needed. In this study, grid points have been assessed using two distinct threshold cri-
teria to account for varying tidal phases within the region, with longitude 2.9° W being the
region divider, as depicted in Figure 6.7. grid points with an SSHA value above -2.8 meters
and a longitude value west of 2.9° W are considered ’dry,’ and similarly, grid points east of
2.9° W with SSHA values above -3.2 m are also identified as running dry indicated in white
in Figure 6.7.

Distribution-based Filtration
An alternative approach to filtration involves examining the distribution of SSHA values
within the time series for each grid point. The ”Rule of Twelfths” states that maximum
tidal flow occurs precisely between low and high tide (Bencker, 1937), while slack water—
representing the lowest tidal speed—is observed at the times of high and low tide, as illus-
trated in Figure 6.8. Consequently, in regions primarily influenced by M2 tides, one would
expect a greater concentration of observations around the high and low tide moments com-
pared to the periods in between. This expectation theoretically results in a bimodal distribu-
tion, characterized by prominent peaks corresponding to the high and low tide instances.
We employ this knowledge to categorize all grid points based on their bimodal distribution
into three different categories. The categorization is determined by both the relative height
between the two peaks in the distribution and the separation between them, specifically,
whether there is a defined valley between the two peaks. This valley’s measurement is
based on the height of the intersection between the two Gaussian curves that constitute the
bimodal distribution, compared to the total height of the smaller peak.

Category 1 - Subtidal Grid Point
In this category, the two peaks are approximately of the same height, and there is a clearly
defined valley between them. The valley height is at most 3/5 the height of the smaller peak.
Grid points falling into this category are consistently submerged and generally exhibit good
data quality.
Category 2 - Intertidal Mudflat
This category includes grid points where the low-water peak is significantly higher than
the high-water peak, exceeding 25 % in height. However, there remains a distinct valley
between the peaks. These grid points are typically associated with intertidal mudflats, where
the grid point alternates between being submerged and exposed.
Category 3 - Bad Grid Point
Grid points in this category exhibit two peaks of roughly the same height, but there is no
well-defined valley between them. In these cases, the valley height is more than 3/5 the
height of the smaller peak. Grid points in this category are labeled as ”Bad grid points”
due to their noisy and unreliable observational records, which closely resemble a normal
distribution.

6.2.1
Radar Tide Gauges
All tide gauges utilized in this thesis, besides the tide gauge at Nell’s Point, are radar tide
gauges. Radar tide gauges are normally positioned several meters above the surface of
bodies of water and send pulses vertically down and receive the echo (see Figure 6.11).
These gauges employ various techniques for measuring changes in sea level. Some uti-
lize a time-of-flight approach, where the gauge monitors the time it takes for a radar pulse
to travel from a transmitter/receiver unit to the water surface and back. Others employ a
Frequency Modulated Continuous Wave (FMCW) system, which involves mixing transmit-
ted radar waves with signals reflected from the surface to determine the phase shift between
the two waves and thereby calculate the range. Manufacturers typically claim a range accu-
racy of approximately ± 1 cm over a measuring range spanning 1.5 to 30 m. (Woodworth &
Aarup, 2002). These tide gauges operate continuously, emitting pulses at regular intervals
(e.g. every few seconds) to track changes in height over time. However, the measurements
are often averaged over larger time intervals, typically around 10 minutes.
Figure 6.11: A radar tide gauge installed at Gladstone Dock, Liverpool. Figure from Wood-
worth and Smith, 2003.

Radar tide gauges have several advantages over traditional tide gauges, such as those that
use a float in a stilling well. They are non-contact, meaning they don’t physically interact with
the water, reducing maintenance and avoiding biofouling problems. Additionally, they can
perform well in a wide range of environmental conditions, including icy waters and during
extreme weather events, making them versatile tools for monitoring sea level (Woodworth
& Smith, 2003).
6.2.2
GNSS-IR Tide Gagues
The tide gauge at Nell’s Point employs a technique that differs from the other tide gauges.
Here a Global Navigation Satellite System-Interferometric Reflectometry (GNSS-IR) type
tide gauge has been installed, which is a technique that is emerging as a promising method
for in situ sea-level monitoring. The receiver at Nell’s point is located 20m above sea level
and utilizes data from high-precision GNSS instruments to sense the near-field environment,
offering the advantage of continuous, weather-independent sea-level information and ver-
tical land motion monitoring (Larson et al., 2013a).
Unlike traditional GNSS applications focused on positioning, timing, and atmospheric sound-
ing, which rely on direct satellite signals, GNSS-IR for water-level monitoring utilizes signals
reflected off the nearby water surface as indicated in Figure 6.12. These reflected signals,
delayed compared to direct signals, induce measurable interference manifested as oscilla-
tions in the recorded GNSS signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) data. Analysis of the frequency of
these oscillations enables estimation of the vertical distance from the water surface to the
GNSS antenna phase center (Peng et al., 2019).
Studies have shown that water levels retrieved from a single geodetic GNSS receiver us-
ing the GNSS-IR technique exhibit comparable accuracy to traditional tide gauges (Larson
et al., 2013b; Löfgren et al., 2014). Moreover, GNSS-IR can sense larger areas (tens to
hundreds of meters from the station) compared to traditional tide gauges, allowing for the
capture of sea-level extremes occurring at some distance along the coast. Additionally,
GNSS stations are often installed at relatively elevated locations, such as on top of build-
ings, reducing the risk of damage or destruction during intense storms. Contrarily, the tide
gauge effectively measures the SSH at a point up to several hundred meters away from the
tide gauge itself, exposing it to signal contamination in complex coastal areas.

Tidal Constituents in Bristol Channel
Tide models, such as FES2104 (Lyard et al., 2021) and DTU22 (Andersen et al., 2023b),
play a crucial role in the study of sea level rise and the dynamics of our oceans. These
models allow us to separate the dynamic ocean components from altimetry data, enabling
us to isolate the static signal related to phenomena like sea level rise. The implications
of this research extend beyond the realm of scientific inquiry. Accurate tidal models are
crucial for various societal applications. Improved tide models not only benefit scientific
studies but also have practical applications, such as enhancing tide maps, aiding in coastal
infrastructure planning, and mitigating the impact of sea level rise on vulnerable coastal
communities (Haigh et al., 2020).
Despite their significance, these models face significant challenges when it comes to coastal
regions. However, a significant breakthrough is on the horizon with the imminent availabil-
ity of SWOT data. Consequently, this chapter will concentrate on the determination of tidal
phase and amplitude, specifically focusing on the lunar-induced semi-diurnal parameter,
M2. These findings will be compared with a state-of-the-art global tide model FES2014,
illustrating the potential for SWOT data to advance our understanding of coastal tidal dy-
namics.
As elaborated in Section 3.2 , tides are typically represented as the sum of numerous har-
monic functions, each characterized by its phase and amplitude. Ideally, one would aim
to determine as many tidal constituents as possible, but this must be balanced with the
law of diminishing returns and the computational resources available. Any unresolved con-
stituents can influence or ’spill over’ into the ones that have been successfully determined.
However, the capacity to identify these constituents is significantly influenced by the orbital
configuration of the satellite and the temporal sampling rate of the satellite mission. This
sampling rate is crucial as no satellite can continuously observe tidal variations, but their
discrete sampling frequency affects the aliasing of the signal.

Tidal Bore in Severn River
The significant improvement in spatial resolution offered by SWOT opens up new avenues
for observing various geophysical phenomena. Additionally, the CAL/VAL phase, with its
1-day repeat period, provides opportunities to capture phenomena that unfold over several
days. During this thesis, several novel possibilities were explored, including lead detection
in sea ice, tidal landscapes within coral lagoons, and the study of tidal bores.
Figure 9.1: The Earth (blue) follows the celestial equator (black), which crosses the ecliptic

This section will delve into the tidal bore phenomenon, a fascinating occurrence frequently
observed up the Severn River. The tidal bore shares characteristics with a standing wave
and a hydraulic jump, resulting in a noticeable drop in water height.
The potency of tides is intricately tied to the interplay among the Earth, the Moon, and
the Sun in the celestial realm. High tides manifest during full and new moons when the
gravitational forces of the Sun and Moon synchronize. Yet, this alignment isn’t confined
to a mere flat plane; it extends into the third dimension. Twice a year, around March 20th
and September 22nd, the equinoxes denote moments when the Sun traverses the celestial
equator (see Figure 9.1). During these periods, the gravitational influences of the Sun and

Moon synergize more profoundly with Earth’s water bodies due to the Sun aligning with the
equatorial plane. This alignment coincides with new and full moons, resulting in spring tides
characterized by higher highs and lower lows. It’s also when tidal bores in the Severn River
reach their pinnacle.

The creation of the tidal bores is influenced by the gradual non-linear distortion of the tidal

gauge stations. This tidal influence is especially evident at Sandhurst and Epney Rivers,
where the tides solely affect the measurements during the tidal bore’s return.
When there is a significant imbalance in the ebb-flood cycle, characterized by a longer du-
ration of the falling tide compared to the rising tide and stronger flood currents than ebb
currents, a tidal bore can develop in the upper reaches of the estuary. Tidal bores occur
under specific conditions: notably, a large tidal range, along with a lengthy, shallow, and
converging channel. These prerequisites are met by the Bristol Channel and Severn River
system, leading to the formation of tidal bores on roughly 130 days each year in the Severn,

Discussion of Results
Utilizing IWS altimetry, SWOT offers an unprecedented spatial resolution of altimetry data,
revolutionizing the field by introducing a wide range of new applications for altimetry data.
To fully explore the potential of this new data source, the focus was placed on studying
tides in the Bristol Channel. This choice was made firstly to assess the usability of SWOT
in a complex coastal environment characterized by one of the world’s highest tidal ampli-
tudes and a complex pattern of exposed mudflats, which presents a significant challenge
to the applicability of SWOT. Secondly, tides represent the largest source of variability in
SSH globally, and accurately quantifying them is crucial for several scientific endeavors,
including geoid determination and understanding sea-level rise. The latter is particularly
important from a societal and environmental perspective for comprehending the impacts of
the climatological changes on tides, impacting all humans on Earth.
In the preceding three chapters, we have investigated the accuracy of three SWOT prod-
ucts by benchmarking them against six local tide gauge readings in the Bristol Channel.
We explored the capability of SWOT to provide a comprehensive view of the channel’s tidal
dynamics, with a particular emphasis on the M2 tidal constituent. Additionally, we demon-
strated the novel potential of SWOT to observe geophysical phenomena that are inacces-
sible to conventional altimetry, through the observation of the tidal bore along the Severn
River. In the following sections, we will discuss the most significant findings.
10.1
SSHA from SWOT and Tide Gauges
We found that the 50m product delivered SSHA determinations with an RMSE between 2.5
cm and 10.1 cm concerning gauge stations using radar technology, with an STD between
the four points used for the analysis ranging from 2.8 cm to 11.5 cm. This is an incredibly
high accuracy in complex coastal areas with a tidal range of up to 12 m. Comparingly, a
study conducted in the Gulf of Finland by Mostafavi et al., 2021 with a tidal range of 15-20
cm investigated the Ocean retracker for Sentinel-3a and ALES+ for Jason-3. They found
RMSE of 4-5 cm 2-3km from the coast by SA3 and 6-7 7-10km from the coast by Jason-
3. Previous research has also evaluated and compared the accuracy of the Sentinel-3A
Chapter 10: Discussion of Results
98
==PAGE:108==
satellite in the same area ((Birgiel et al., 2018, 2020; Liibusk et al., 2020). In the study by
Birgiel et al., 2018, the Sentinel-3A SSH data achieved an accuracy with RMSE of 11.5
and 9.9 cm using the SAMOSA2 and SAMOSA+ trackers for SSH retrieval. The RMSE
recorded from the 50m product at Nells Point, where a GNSS-IR tide gauge is installed,
was significantly higher at 41.9 cm. This elevated RMSE was observed not only for the
50m product but also for the 170m and 2000m products, suggesting potential inaccuracies
with the tide gauge at Nells Point. This finding is particularly intriguing since tide gauges
are typically regarded as the standard for accuracy, serving as benchmarks for satellite
altimetry. The selection of GNSS-IR technology at Nells Point is attributed to its location
on a steep hill, where the installation of traditional tide gauges is not possible, indicating
a challenging environment for measurements. This scenario opens up the possibility that
in difficult environmental settings, SWOT measurements could indeed be equally reliable
as those from a tide gauge, potentially offering not just a spatial resolution advantage over
traditional tide gauges but also similar accuracy.
The 170m product achieved low RMSE values at Avonmouth Portbury and Hinkley Point,
registering 5.5 cm and 7.3 cm, respectively, with a correspondingly low STD, which is partly
due to enhanced smoothing. Conversely, at Newport, both the RMSE and STD were sig-
nificantly higher, likely as a result of intertidal mudflat contamination during low tide.
In comparison, the 2000m product exhibited generally higher RMSE values, ranging from
9.7 cm at Hinkley Point to 22.9 cm at Avonmouth Portbury. The lower STD observed with
this product can be attributed to a greater degree of smoothing and the use of half the
number of grid points (i = 2) compared to the 50m and 170m products (i = 4).
Overall the accuracy of the three SWOT products is very high compared to the tidal range.
Merely investigating the RMSE merits the use of the HR SWOT products in addition to its
superior spatial resolution. However, it has to be remembered that the observational pe-
riod was shorter, increasing the chance of low RMSE, and additionally, the low degree of
smoothing allows increases in the effect of short-scale variations in SSH related to wave dy-
namics, which is unwanted in an analysis of tides. Merely examining the RMSE underscores
the value of using the HR SWOT products, not only for their superior spatial resolution but
also for their precision. However, it’s important to note that the observational period was
relatively short, which could potentially lead to lower RMSE values by chance. Furthermore,
Chapter 10: Discussion of Results
99
==PAGE:109==
the minimal degree of smoothing applied increases the sensitivity to short-scale variations
in SSH associated with wave dynamics, an element that is generally undesirable in tidal
analysis.
The dichotomy between the increased sensitivity and noise levels in the 50m product, while
delivering superior spatial resolution, underscores the necessity for further research into
advanced filtration algorithms. In our study, we employed lowest-low-water filtration and
distribution-based filtration techniques, which proved effective yet have their limitations.
Consequently, future research could benefit significantly from exploring more sophisticated
and accurate filtration methods, such as LSTM-based filtration or other machine learning
strategies, which could be applied more universally. Implementing such advanced tech-
niques would facilitate a greater degree of smoothing while minimizing the risk of contami-
nation from terrestrial features, intertidal mudflats, exposed coral reefs, and similar entities.

We analyzed the tidal amplitude and phase lag of the M2 constituent across three SWOT

Notably, the amplitude difference between the 50m and 2000m products consistently mea-

period and the degree of smoothing on the results. Furthermore, we observed that the
amplitude of all products increases with distance from the ocean. This phenomenon sug-
gests that the funneling shape and gradual increase in bathymetry throughout the channel
contribute to amplifying the tidal amplitude.
Our findings further indicate that the more smoothed products at 170m and 2000m resolu-
tions achieved amplitude determinations and phase lag estimations within 19 centimeters
and 1.97 degrees, respectively, though values were notably lower at most test sites. The
observational period for these products spanned between 76 and 85 days out of a possi-
ble 86, depending on the product and tide gauge comparison. Missing data resulted from
either outlier removal or gaps in the observational records of SWOT or the tide gauges.
Despite this, the duration was sufficient to provide reliable estimates of the M2 parameters,

with performance deemed satisfactory given the tidal range, where the maximal difference
amounted to 19cm, approximately 2% of the signal.
The performance of the 50m product demonstrated exceptional accuracy at Avonmouth
Portbury and Hinkley Point, achieving tidal amplitude accuracies within 0-7 cm and phase
lag differences of 0.08-0.13 degrees. In contrast, accuracy was notably lower at Newport
and Nells Point. It’s important to note that, while the M2 signal theoretically has an alias
period of 12.4 days, an observational period of only 19 days does not provide sufficient time
for a robust resolution of the amplitude. Nonetheless, it’s noteworthy that even within this
short observation window, the maximum vector difference in the M2 parameter reached 40
cm at Newport, while remaining as low as 1 cm at Avonmouth Portbury.
Of particular interest is the comparable performance of the 170m and 2000m products to
the FES2014 model at Avonmouth Portbury, since tide from the station was used to con-
strain the FES2014 model. Notably, the SWOT products outperform FES2014 closer to
the sea, at Nells Point and Hinkley Point, where the vectorial difference between FES2014
and tide gauges measures 32 cm and 40 cm, respectively. In the FES2014 model, tidal
phase lag and amplitude are primarily interpolated from the open ocean into the Bristol
Channel, utilizing Avonmouth Portbury as an assimilation point. Consequently, FES2014’s
error increases with distance from this station, both towards the ocean and up the Severn
River. The superior performance of SWOT suggests its potential inclusion in such data-
constrained tide models in the future, offering significant enhancements to tide models in
these critical coastal regions.
10.3
Tidal Bore in Severn River
The tidal bore represents a fascinating phenomenon as it transitions from an oceanic fea-
ture into inland waters, a domain traditionally challenging to monitor via satellite, particularly
with altimetry technologies. Our exploratory research on a minor tidal bore within the Severn
River has yielded novel insights. Tide gauges have distinctly captured a non-linear distortion
of the tidal wave, observable through SSHA measurements from the SWOT, highlighting
a hydraulic jump approximately 0.5 to 1.0 meters in height leading the tidal wave. This
discovery yet again underscores the significant potential of utilizing SWOT data for the de-
tailed monitoring of hydraulics within river estuaries and deltas globally, marking a pivotal
advancement in the field.

Conclusions
The advent of SWOT introduces an unparalleled spatial resolution in altimetry data, herald-
ing a revolution in the field and opening up many new applications. To thoroughly investigate
the possibilities presented by this innovative data source, the research focused on studying
tides in the Bristol Channel.
Initially, the accuracy of SWOT products at resolutions of 50m, 170m, and 2000m was as-
sessed through comparison with local tide gauges, revealing RMSEs generally ranging from
approximately 3cm to 11cm across different products and stations. This finding holds par-
ticular significance in the context of a complex inlet with substantial tidal amplitudes. Some
stations exhibited lower accuracies, likely attributed to boundary errors or contamination
from intertidal mudflats during low tide, or due to variations in tide gauge types.
The HR 50m product effectively distinguished submerged channels from tidal mudflats, out-
performing the 170m and 2000m resolutions in this regard. However, the higher resolu-
tions exhibited lower STDs between grid points and were less influenced by short-distance
changes in SSH induced by waves. Hence, for tidal analysis, utilizing a robust filtration al-
gorithm to smooth the higher-resolution 50m product is recommended. This preserves its
precision while capitalizing on the smoothing benefits of higher-resolution products.
A tidal analysis revealed the effectiveness of all three SWOT products in resolving the semi-
diurnal lunar constituent M2. These products surpassed the current state-of-the-art global
tide model FES2014, underscoring the significance of incorporating SWOT data into future
iterations of such models. This integration holds promise for enhancing the accuracy of
coastal tide predictions, which are pivotal in various scientific endeavors such as geoid
determination and estimations of sea level changes. The latter is particularly crucial for
adaptation and mitigation strategies to the impacts of future sea level rise and for effective
flood management.
Finally, the high spatial resolution and two-dimensional coverage of SWOT observations
unlock the observation of geophysical features previously inaccessible with conventional
altimetry, such as the tidal bore in the Severn River, moving the field into uncharted land.
