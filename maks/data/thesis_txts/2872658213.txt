Abstract
Privacy is fundamental in modern security protocols, as it allows users to control their
sensitive information. However, formal privacy verification remains challenging, and
the traditional approach of trace equivalence can be difficult to formalize correctly. In
contrast, (α, β)-privacy provides a declarative approach to privacy verification, which
is decidable under certain restrictions. The tool support for automatic verification,
called noname, uses a low-level language that is less intuitive compared to other
security languages like Alice and Bob style languages and its extensions, such as
choreographies.
This thesis addresses the challenges of formalizing privacy in security protocols
by introducing an approach for extending choreographies with privacy features. We
formalize a translation from the extended choreographies into the noname tool for
privacy verification. Our aim is to allow protocol modelers to design privacy-sensitive
protocols more intuitively while benefiting from automated verification.
This work achieves our primary goal of integrating privacy guarantees into chore-
ographies and their translation to the noname tool. Moreover, it highlights areas for
future improvement, especially in generating minimal protocols that can be verified
automatically. Our main contributions are as follows:
• An extension of choreographies with privacy features from (α, β)-privacy.
• The design of a new intermediate language, noname+, which bridges the struc-
tural gap between choreographies and noname, including a simplification pro-
cedure.
• A partial translation strategy from extended choreographies to noname, includ-
ing role choices and state management techniques.
• An implementation of the translation procedure in a prototype tool.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Privacy is about the individual’s right to keep their personal information secret.
Everyone should have the right to choose what information to share and with whom,
even in the digital world. We continue to share large amounts of information online
since we digitalize more aspects of our lives. Thus, our need to protect sensitive
information grows, and software must be secure by design.
However, while we design security protocols to guarantee both confidentiality and
integrity, they often lack privacy guarantees. One hurdle is that privacy is difficult
to formalize and verify.
The traditional approach to privacy verification is trace
equivalence. This approach is powerful, but complex scenarios are difficult to define
correctly. In contrast, the approach of (α, β)-privacy [1] offers a more declarative
approach for privacy verification in security protocols. In addition, [2] shows that
(α, β)-privacy is decidable for a bounded number of sessions and a particular alge-
braic theory. The tool called noname [2] implements the decision procedure for this
subset of (α, β)-privacy. Nevertheless, the language for protocols in noname is less
intuitive than other popular security protocol languages, like Alice and Bob-style lan-
guages. These languages give a comprehensive view of all roles and their interactions.
Choreographies are an extension of Alice-Bob languages that can model the behavior
of multiple parties in a concurrent, distributed system. Compared to noname, we can
more effectively model protocols as choreographies. However, traditional choreogra-
phies do not inherently address privacy.
This thesis addresses the challenges of formalizing security protocols with privacy.
We aim to extend choreographies with privacy features from (α, β)-privacy.
The
goal is to create a new tool to translate the extended choreographies into a verifiable
protocol in noname. Thus, we need to update the translation from choreographies
to local behavior. In addition, we want to propose a translation procedure from the
extended choreographies to noname.
1.1
Contributions
The contributions of this thesis are as follows:
1. We propose extending choreographies with privacy features from (α, β)-privacy.
Then, we enable protocol modelers to design privacy protocols as choreogra-
phies. In addition, we introduce a construction for specifying a default behavior
in interactions. If participants receive ill-formed messages, they react with the
default behavior. This construction improves the robustness of choreographies
1
==PAGE:11==
by making error handling explicit.
2. We update the translation from choreographies to local behavior to support
the new privacy features and default behavior. In addition, we also extend
the local behavior to reflect the extensions to choreographies. As part of this
work, we discuss the approach for generating checks to distinguish scenarios of
received messages in the translation. We propose a practical procedure for our
tool based on [3].
3. As choreographies and noname require different structures, we propose a noname+
language which extends noname with sequences. We create a translation pro-
cedure from the extended local behavior to noname+ transactions in two steps:
(1) remove branching sends, and (2) add sequences when operations do not
follow the left-to-right order. Then, we have a noname+ transaction for each
role in the protocol.
4. As noname does not support the sequence operator, we try to eliminate the
sequences in noname+ transactions with a simplification procedure. We use two
simplifications: (1) removing non-effectful operations and (2) reordering non-
dependent operations. However, we do not guarantee eliminating all sequences
with the simplification procedure.
5. We propose an attempt at solving the last challenges of translating choreogra-
phies to noname. We want to allow the modeler to specify each role in the
choreography as a privacy choice. Here, we propose an initial transaction to
generate a session identifier and role choices. In addition, we want to eliminate
the last sequences in noname+ transactions.
Here, we split a transaction at
each sequence to obtain a set of transactions and add state hints to derive state
information automatically. However, we get an additional transaction for each
sequence, and the total number of transactions becomes too large to verify in
noname automatically. Thus, we suggest that future work should improve this
solution.
We achieve the primary goal of extending choreographies with privacy features and
defining a translation to noname. In addition, we have established a practical pro-
cedure and tool support. However, some challenges remain in obtaining a protocol
we can automatically verify in noname. Our tool can successfully translate extended
choreographies to noname protocols, but the result contains too many transactions for
noname to handle automatically. We identify the robustness of the output protocol
as a challenge for future work.

2.2
Benefits of Privacy-Extended Choreographies
The noname tool provides a robust implementation of the decision procedure for
verifying (α, β)-privacy in protocols [2]. However, the protocol language can seem
6
==PAGE:16==
unintuitive to the modeler, which motivates the design of a higher-level language.
Thus, we want to create a new tool that allows modelers to write protocols as chore-
ographies but still utilize noname to verify privacy.
In this section, we illustrate
two benefits of using choreographies for protocol design: (1) automatic derivation of
checks, and (2) focus on message flow rather than internal logic. We use the BAC
protocol as an example to illustrate these benefits by comparing the noname and
choreography versions of the protocol.
Automatic derivation of checks.
In noname, the protocol modeler must write
checks manually using nested try-catch blocks and if-statements. For example, the
BAC protocol (fig. 2.2) includes three try-catch blocks and two if-statements, even
though the protocol logic is relatively simple. This example shows that the modeler
must analyze which checks are sufficient to ensure the protocol follows the intended
behavior. The manual approach becomes increasingly error-prone in more complex
protocols as the modeler may forget checks.
In contrast, the benefit of choreographies is that checks are generated automat-
ically in the translation procedure [6]. The BAC protocol as a choreography (fig.
2.3) has no direct checks as we generate these automatically. We can automatically
derive that the tag must try to decrypt the received message with its key sk(x) if it is
known. In addition, the tag must check the nonce NT in the encrypted message (line
5) to see if it matches its nonce. Thus, we can apply the checks needed to distinguish
expected honest behavior from unexpected or malicious behavior.
We only allow the protocol modeler to specify a common default behavior for
all error cases in our extended choreography. This approach may be too simplistic
for protocols where the continuation behavior should depend on the nature of the
error. However, we showed in figure 2.4 that we can simulate an error case by letting
the reader send the wrong nonce. This approach can be misleading as it suggests
the reader follows the intended behavior when it sends the wrong nonce. The only
successful case from the tag’s perspective is when it can respond with ok (line 5).
Concluding, we should be careful when simulating error cases, and more fine-grained
error handling would require extending the choreography further.
Writing protocols as choreographies gives us the advantage of automatic checks,
which give a more reliable result than the manual checks in noname. Any modeler can
forget to add a manual check that can be made, especially in more complex protocols.
In addition, whenever checks fail, we can specify a default behavior in our extended
choreography to allow the protocol to continue.
However, we do not distinguish
between error types, and the modeler may specify additional cases manually for
more fine-grained error handling.
Focus on message flow rather than internal logic.
Writing protocols as chore-
ographies benefits us because it allows us to focus on the overall message flow and
provides a comprehensive view of all roles and their interactions.
In contrast to
noname, choreographies abstract away low-level details like state management and
transaction structure.
In the BAC example (fig. 2.3), we saw that the modeler must be aware of the
performance limitations of protocols in noname. Here, we combined the tag and reader
actions to reduce the total number of transactions. The resulting transactions may
seem unintuitive as there is no explicit mapping from participants to transactions.
In addition, the modeler must ensure that the state information is passed correctly
7
==PAGE:17==
between transactions, like the session message in the BAC protocol (line 3, fig. 2.2).
We also need additional checks for the session message to ensure the tag and reader
are in the same session.
We want to shift the responsibility for low-level details from the modeler to our
translation tool.
Thus, we separate the overall protocol design from the noname
implementation concerns by writing protocols as choreographies. The goal is to im-
plement a tool that generates correct transactions we can verify in noname. Then, the
modeler can concentrate on the participants’ interactions at a higher level. Moreover,
this approach leads to more intuitive and maintainable specifications that are easier
to understand, implement, and review from a development perspective. All while we
preserve the ability to verify privacy guarantees with noname.
In the following chapters, we present the translation procedure and discuss the
challenges of many transactions and the need for state information.

Chapter 3
Theory
We need to understand the theory behind choreographies and (α, β)-privacy before
we present our extended choreography and translation to noname. Since both con-
cepts are well-described in the literature, we provide a brief overview and focus on
illustrative examples. We start with some common concepts for both choreographies
and (α, β)-privacy. Then, we outline choreographies where we describe the syntax
and the translation to local behavior based on the work of [6]. Lastly, we describe
(α, β)-privacy to understand how noname works based on the work of [1, 2]
3.1
Preliminaries
We will start with some common concepts for both choreographies and (α, β)-privacy.
In particular, both theories rely on frames and recipes, which we define first. Then,
we present the supported algebraic theory and the cryptographic operations.
3.1.1
Frames and Recipes
The first concepts are frames and recipe.
We use frames to structure an agent’s
knowledge by labeling all known terms. Then, we do not reference a term directly
but rather by its label. We use recipes to construct expressions based on the labels
in the frame.
Definition 3.1.1 (Frame). A frame F is a mapping from labels to choreography
terms. We define:
F = [X1 7→t1, . . . , Xn 7→tn]
where Xi are labels, and ti are ground terms, meaning they do not contain any labels.
We define the domain of a frame as dom and the image of a frame as img, such that:
dom(F) = {X1, . . . , Xn}
img(F) = {t1, . . . , tn}
As an example, an agent knows a key k, a message msg, and a random value rand.
We capture this knowledge in a frame as follows:
F = [X1 7→k, X2 7→msg, X3 7→rand]
Definition 3.1.2 (Recipe). A recipe r only contains labels and function symbols.
9
==PAGE:19==
Given the frame F from before, we can construct the recipe:
r = scrypt(X1, X2, X3)
Given a frame and a recipe, we can construct the corresponding term by applying
the frame.
Definition 3.1.3 (Frame application). We can apply a frame F to a recipe r by
substituting all labels with a term in the frame, written F[r].
For example, if we have F and r from before, then we can apply F to r to obtain:
F[r] = scrypt(k, msg, rand)
Lastly, we define the static equivalence of two frames, which means that the two
frames are indistinguishable.
Definition 3.1.4 (Static Equivalence of Frames, from [1]). Let F1 and F2 be two
frames. We say that F1 and F2 are statically equivalent, denoted by F1 ∼F2, iff for
all recipes r1 and r2, the following holds:
F1[r1] = F1[r2] ⇐⇒F2[r1] = F2[r2]
Intuitively, the frames are static equivalent if all pairs of recipes r1 and r2 always
give the same result in both frames or different results in both frames.
3.1.2
Constructor/Destructor Theory
The tool noname restricts the algebraic theory to constructor/destructor theories [2].
Thus, we also restrict our thesis to this theory.
Definition 3.1.5 (Algebraic Theory, from [2]). A rewrite rule in constructor/de-
structor theory is one of the following forms:
• Decryption:
d(k, c(k′, X1, . . . , Xn)) →Xi
where i ∈{1, . . . , n}, d is a destructor, c is a constructor, fv(k) = fv(k′), and
all Xi are variables. Here, fv defines the free variables.
• Projection:
di(c(X1, . . . , Xn)) →Xi
where i ∈{1, . . . , n}, di is a public destructor (called projector), and c is a
constructor of arity n, and all Xj are variables. We require a rule for every
i ∈{1, . . . , n}.
• Private extractor:
d(c(t1, . . . , tn)) →t0
where d is a private destructor (called extractor), c is a constructor, and t0 is
a subterm of one of the ti.
Then let E be a set of such rules, and =E be the reflexive, symmetric, and transitive
closure of the rewrite relation →E from E. Then we require:

2. No constructor can occur in both decryption and projection rules.
3. For every decryption rule d(k, c(k′, X1, . . . , Xn)) →Xi, we require
k = k′ ∨k ≈f(k′) ∨f(k) ≈k′
for a public function f.

3.3
(α, β)-privacy
As stated, we need a better approach to define privacy because the traditional ap-
proach based on trace equivalence is challenging and impractical. For this reason, we
explore (α, β)-privacy, which provides a declarative way of specifying privacy proper-
ties. Several articles already describe the technical details of (α, β)-privacy [1, 2, 7, 8].
Thus, we will focus on giving the reader an overview of the main ideas in this section.
We will introduce (α, β)-privacy for a fixed state and illustrate the concept with an
example. After, we look at (α, β)-privacy for a transition system. Lastly, we explain
how to write protocols in the noname tool. However, we refer the reader to [2] for the
detailed theory description for the decision procedure behind noname.
3.3.1
Basic Concepts
We start by introducing the basic concepts used in (α, β)-privacy.
As the name
implies, (α, β)-privacy uses two formulae, α and β in Herbrand Logic. Specifically,
α specifies all the information we release explicitly, and β is all the information the
intruder has found. The idea is that an intruder should not be able to derive anything
from β that is not already entailed by α. [1]
Herbrand Logic is a First-Order Logic (FOL) variant that allows us to define
cryptographic operations [2]. This logic uses the Herbrand Universe modulo a con-
gruence relation ≈. We will use the congruence relation defined in table 3.1. We
have an alphabet Σ and an interpretation I, which maps every uninterpreted symbol
to the Herbrand Universe. Then we have the model relation ⊨Σ such that
I ⊨Σ s ˙= t ⇐⇒I(s) ≈I(t)
Then let Σ be the alphabet of constants, function symbols and relation symbols,
and let V be the set of variables. We define Σ0 ⊂Σ as the high-level vocabulary
(called payload). Then Σ \ Σ0 is the technical vocabulary. [1] The function fv returns
the set of free variables in a formula.
Definition 3.3.1 ((α, β)-privacy, from [2]). Given formula α over Σ0 and β over
Σ with fv (α) ⊆fv (β). We say that (α, β)-privacy holds iff every model I of α is
16
==PAGE:26==
consistent with β. Specifically, every Σ0-interpretation I ⊨Σ0 α has a corresponding
Σ-interpretation I′ |=Σ β such that I and I′ agree on the variables in fv (α) and on
the relation symbols in Σ0.
3.3.2
Fixed state
We want to reason about (α, β)-privacy for a fixed state, a single (α, β)-pair. De-
ciding whether (α, β)-privacy holds for a single (α, β)-pair is undecidable because of
Herbrand Logic. Thus, we restrict the problem to specific (α, β)-pairs given by the
Message-Analysis Problem. [9]
In the message analysis problem, we use the frames concr and struct. The frame
concr consists of concrete knowledge, whereas struct consists of the structural infor-
mation. We require the frames to have the same domain (def. 3.1.1), but we only
allow terms in struct to contain free variables from V. Then, we formalize the two
frames with the formulae ϕconcr and ϕstruct.
Definition 3.3.2 (Message-Analysis Problem, from [1]). Given a formula α, an
interpretation I of α, and a frame struct. We require that all terms in struct are
free in α. Moreover, we define concr = I(struct) such that concr is the concrete
frame of struct. Then we have
MsgAna(α, struct, I) ≡α ∧ϕconcr ∧ϕstruct ∧concr ∼struct
Then, we let β ≡MsgAna(α, struct, I), and the Message Analysis Problem satisfy
privacy, iff (α, β)-privacy holds.

reason about it in a transition system. We want to express (α, β)-privacy for all the

and semantics to express the reachable states. In this section, we first define the
Multi Message-Analysis Problem [2] to reason about (α, β)-privacy in states. Then,
we present the protocol specification and an example to show the intuition of the
semantics.

updates. [2]

Chapter 4
Extending Choreographies with
Privacy
We aim to extend choreographies with privacy because they provide a more intuitive
approach to formalizing protocols.
In section 3.2, we saw that the original cryp-
tographic choreographies from [6] support basic operations like communication and
memory operations. Protocol specifications for (α, β)-privacy also include these basic
operations and privacy-related operations like non-deterministic choices and releases.
To support privacy, we must ensure that choreographies have the same expressive
power as protocols for (α, β)-privacy. Consequently, we must extend choreographies
to support these privacy-related operations. We must also change the local behavior
syntax to include these operations and update the translation from choreographies to
local behavior. In addition to these core extensions, we also improve the choreogra-

Chapter 5
Derivation of Recipes and
Checks
Translating from choreographies to local behavior requires recipe compositions and
derivation of destructions and checks, as we saw in section 4.3.
We need recipe
compositions in steps like send and destructions and checks to distinguish different
scenarios of received messages. The original translation from [6] does not specify any
concrete methods for the composition of recipes nor the derivation of destructions
and checks.
Thus, we consider the work of [3] as the starting point to obtain a
concrete method. The authors present two functions, compose and analyze. We can
use the compose function to compose recipes and the analyze function to derive the
destructions and checks.
However, implementing the analyze function as outlined in [3] presents several
challenges we must address. In [3], they use a table-based approach with a fixed set
of constructors and destructors. However, the noname tool presents a more flexible
approach where users can define their own rewrite rules for constructors and destruc-
tors. We want to capture the flexibility of noname, where the pure approach from
[3] is insufficient. In addition to this challenge, the analyze function needs to track
previous destructions and checks to avoid redundant checks, but the implementation
is not straightforward. Lastly, we distinguish between destructions and checks in the
receive translation. Consequently, we must ensure that the analyze function respects
this distinction.
We address the lack of concrete methods using the approach presented in [3]. The
compose function can be used directly as described in [3] to derive recipes. However,
we must adapt the approach for the analyze function as the predefined version presents
several challenges. In this chapter, we present our refined approach and discuss the
challenges.
5.1
Recipe Composition
We need to derive recipes when translating choreographies to local behavior. This sec-
tion will define the derivation with the function compose, as in [3]. The composeF (t)
function takes as input a frame F and a term t, and then it returns a set of recipes
{r1, . . . , rn} such that F[ri] = t hold for each ri. The function recursively composes
all the possible recipes (def. 5.1.1).

An important result of the compose function is that it terminates which is crucial
for the implementation. The tree of recursive calls to compose has finite branching
and finite depth to guarantee termination. The branching of compose is finite because
the set of constructors Σc is finite, and each constructor has a fixed finite arity. The
depth of the recursion tree is finite if all recursive calls are on strictly smaller terms.
If the subterms t1, . . . , tn are not strictly smaller than t, we cannot reach the base
case (eq. (5.1)). Again, the restriction to constructors ensures that all subterms are
strictly smaller than t.
We want to show why we restrict the function to constructors for termination.
Consider the generic call composeF (t), and suppose that we choose the destructor
f = proj1. We must ensure t ≈proj1(t′) by the definition, and we get the recursive call
r ∈composeF (t′). We only succeed when t′ ≈pair(t, t0) for any t0. As a consequence,
we only consider recipes r = pair(r′, r0) for r′ ∈composeF (t) and r0 ∈composeF (t0).
The computation of r′ results in a loop, as the recursive call is on the same term
t as the original. Thus, we get infinite depth when destructors are allowed. As a
result, we conclude that the restriction to constructors is necessary to ensure the
termination of the compose function.
We refer to [3] for the formal proof and the argumentation on soundness and
completeness of the compose function.
5.2
Decomposition and Checks
Our extended translation procedure from choreographies to local behavior (sec. 4.3)
distinguishes received messages by computing destructions and checks. This section
builds on the analyze approach from [3] with some adaptions to fit the needs of our
translation procedure.
The original analyze function consists of two phases with a fixed set of rules. The
first phase destructs terms using the predefined rewrite rules and adds the new sub-
terms to the frame. The second phase finds checks to compare terms in the updated
frames. We preserve the two-phase structure, where the first phase is responsible for
the destructions δ and the second phase is responsible for the checks ϕ (see translation
in section 4.3).
We describe the analyze function based closely on [3] with some adaptions. Then,
we present an improvement to the table approach because we want to allow user-
defined rewrite rules as in noname.
Lastly, we discuss the challenges of tracking
previous destructions and checks and how to avoid redundancy of the analyze function.

5.2.3
Tracking Previous Checks and Destructions
The analyze function as described in [3] assumes that we never add redundant checks.
Hence, we omit the checks that our previous checks imply. However, as described
in the previous sections, our updated procedure ignores this assumption. We must
address this issue because we want minimal local behaviors in our translation from
choreographies instead of many redundant checks.
Example.
We start by illustrating the problem with a simple example.
Let F
be the starting frame F = [X1 7→pair(a, b)]. The first phase analyze(1) returns the
updated frame F ′ and destruction recipes δ:
F ′ = [ X1 7→pair(a, b), X2 7→a, X3 7→b ]
δ = { X2 := proj1(X1), X3 := proj2(X1) }
Without any tracking of destructions, then analyze(1)(F ′) would give:
F ′′ = [ X1 7→pair(a, b), X2 7→a, X3 7→b, X4 7→a, X5 7→b ]
δ′ = { X4 := proj1(X1), X5 := proj2(X1) }
We already destructed X1 in the first computation, so we should not do it again in
the second.
Similarly, the second phase analyze(2) returns the same checks whenever we call
it with the same frame. In addition, it also creates checks that we entail by the
destructions from the first phase. For example, if we compute analyze(2)(F ′), we get:
ϕ = {X1 ˙= pair(X2, X3)}
However, given that the destruction recipes from δ succeeded, we can entail the equal-
ity in ϕ from these destructions. Thus, our second phase also needs to be aware of
previous destructions.
Our example illustrates several redundancies in the computation of destructions and
checks. We need to solve the following problems:
1. Redundant destruction: entailed by previous destructions.
2. Redundant checks: either entailed by
(a) previous checks, or
(b) previous destructions.
Eliminating Redundant Destructions.
We want to avoid destructing the same
term multiple times in the first phase of analyze. Our solution is to track all suc-
cessfully destructed labels in a set prevDestructed. In the original procedure, we set
analyzeState(new) = dom(F) which we replace by
analyzeState(new) = dom(F) \ prevDestructed
Then, we never queue any successfully destructed labels in another call to analyze(1).
34
==PAGE:44==
However, we must ensure that any term cannot have different successful destruc-
tions in different calls. We consider the following result from analyzing F:
analyze(1)(F) = (F ′, δ, prevDestructed)
If Xi ∈prevDestructed and F[Xi] = ti, then we must ensure that we cannot destruct
ti to a new term tj ̸∈img(F ′) (def. 3.1.1) after analyze(1) is done. In other words, we
must ensure that we have exhausted all destructions of terms corresponding to labels
in prevDestructed.
The procedure analyze(1) will always successfully destruct all terms ti that match
a projection rule or a privacy extractor rule because they have no condition (column
2, table 5.2). Moreover, for any term ti, we try all rules in case the constructor for
ti occurs in multiple rules. Thus, we only have a problem when we can destruct a
term ti by multiple rules, where one of the rules fails due to a missing key. Then,
we add Xi to prevDestructed, even though the decryption could potentially succeed
in the next call if we obtain the key. However, we do not queue Xi in the next call,
as we already destructed it.
We acknowledge that our algebraic theory (def. 3.1.5) only prevents a constructor
from occurring in both a decryption and a projection rule.
However, we do not
have any restrictions on the duplicate constructor in two different decryption rules
or the same constructor in both a decryption and a privacy extractor rule.
Our
standard rewrite rules (fig. 5.1) avoid the problem as all constructors only occur
in one rule (except for the projection of pairs). However, we must ensure that all
user-defined rules also avoid this problem. The current implementation does not have
any guarantees for the user-defined rules, but we could consider one of the following
solutions:
1. Require that any constructor c in a decryption rule cannot occur in any other
rewrite rules.
2. Extend the tracking to record which labels failed in a decryption rule due to a
missing key.
The first solution is too restrictive as we want a flexible tool supporting many pro-
tocols. The second solution is more flexible, but it requires more bookkeeping. We
leave this for future work on the tool.
If we return to the example at the beginning of this section 5.2.3, we will add
X1 to prevDestructed after the first call to analyze(1). Then, in the second call to
analyze(1), we will not queue X1 as we already destructed it. Thus, the result of the
second call analyze(1)(F ′) will be (F ′, δ) instead of (F ′′, δ′) as intended. Tracking
previous destructions solves the problem of redundant destructions in simple cases,
which we want for now.
Eliminating Redundant Checks.
We want to avoid checks that we can entail
from previous destructions. In the second phase analyze(2), we iterate over all the
entries in the input frame F. We avoid checks that we can entail from destructions by
only considering all the labels in F that are not in prevDestructed (from analyze(1)).
Then, we only generate checks for the new subterms extracted from the first phase.
If we consider the example, we do not get the check X1 ˙= pair(X2, X3) as X1 ∈
prevDestructed. We only generate the checks related to the new labels X2 and X3
because subterms’ equivalence implies composed terms’ equivalence.

We also want to avoid checks that we can entail from previous checks. Specifically,
if we already have the check r1 ˙= r2, then we do not want to generate this check
again or the check r2 ˙= r1. Given a set of previous checks ϕ and the new checks ϕ′,
we make a check difference, such that we only return ϕ′ \ ϕ. The check difference is
defined as:
ϕ′ \ ϕ = {r1 ˙= r2 | ∀(r1 ˙= r2) ∈ϕ′. (r1 ˙= r2) ̸∈ϕ ∧(r2 ˙= r1) ̸∈ϕ}
Then, we remove all new checks that we can entail from previous checks. In addition,
we can remove duplicate checks in a similar manner, where we only keep one of the
checks r1 ˙= r2 and r2 ˙= r1.

to noname+
We have extended the syntax of choreographies with privacy features and updated
the translation to local behavior. We want to present the first step in translating
local behavior to a protocol specification in (α, β)-privacy as supported by noname [5].
Firstly, we define local contexts to simplify the first translation steps. Then, we define
the translation in two steps: (1) removing branching sends, and (2) sequentialization.
6.1
Local Contexts
Each local behavior is structured as a chain, requiring traversal at each step of the
translation. To simplify the translation process, we aim to avoid distinguishing in-
dividual cases when they are treated equivalently. Consequently, we introduce local
contexts, which abstract over cases, allowing us to focus on the key aspects of the
translation.
Definition 6.1.1 (Local Context). A local context, denoted as L[◦], is a function-like
structure to abstract local behaviors. It satisfies the following:
• Substituting ◦with a local behavior L1 results in a local behavior L[L1].
• Substituting ◦with a local context L2[◦] results in a new local context L[L2[◦]].
We define a local context as trivial if it is the identity function, i.e. L[◦] = ◦.
And we define a local context L[◦] as minimal if it cannot be decomposed into smaller
contexts (and is not trivial).
The concept of a local context allows us to generalize and abstract over equivalent
cases in the translation steps. We can illustrate the definition by providing some
examples.
Example 6.1.1 (Local Contexts). We give two examples of local contexts.
L1[◦] = receive(X). ◦
L2[◦] = receive(X1). lock(). X2 := cell[X1]. unlock. ◦
Here, L1[◦] is a minimal local context, while L2[◦] is not.
Both local contexts have a hole at the end, which can be substituted. For example,

the result of applying L1 to L2 is:
L1[L2[◦]] = receive(X). receive(X1). lock(). X2 := cell[X1]. unlock. ◦

i.e. 0:

L2[0] = receive(X1). lock(). X2 := cell[X1]. unlock. 0

of local behaviors.

1. L = 0
2. L = Pn

The decomposition lemma shows that minimal local contexts serve as building
blocks. Most local behaviors are chains of such contexts, except end, branching sends,
and conditionals due to multiple or no continuations.
This abstraction simplifies

translation.
6.2
noname+
Transactions in noname are structured differently from choreographies. In noname,
we need transactions with operations in a left-to-right order (fig. 3.3). For example,
receive operations should occur before send operations. The local behavior structures
operations into a primary and atomic part, so we are not guaranteed the left-to-
right order. Thus, we make an updated syntax using sequences, i.e. processes are
sequentialized using a semicolon. We call the updated syntax for noname+ (see figure
6.1). The only difference from the current syntax of noname is the sequence marked
in red.

Implementation.
The auxiliary functions recursively call each other in a loop. If
we forget to implement a case where an operation never matches any cases in our
auxiliary functions, the translation will never terminate. Thus, we add robustness to
the implementation by adding a fuel counter to each auxiliary function. Each time
we create a sequence, 0 ; toLeft(·) we deduct one such that we get: fuel −1. We
set the initial value of the fuel to a large number, such as 100 000. If we forget to
implement a case, the translation will terminate after 100 000 steps. However, if it
succeeds, the fuel must be large enough to allow the translation to finish. Thus, we
stop any local behavior that needs more than 100 000 sequences, which exceeds the
fuel counter. However, such a local behavior is not practical anyway.

Simplification: eliminate
sequences
We want to experiment with verifying the translation of choreographies using the
noname tool. However, the current version of the tool, noname v0.3, does not support
sequences. As a result, we cannot directly verify a noname+ protocol in the noname
tool. We must eliminate all sequences by splitting transactions and then add state
information to ensure consistency across them. This process results in complex trans-
actions with many messages and additional checks to maintain correctness. We want
to bring the noname+ protocols a step closer to noname by simplifying protocols. We
simplify each transaction by removing operations that do not affect the privacy anal-
ysis. This simplification will reduce the complexity of the needed state information.
We observe two simplifications: (1) operations with no effect are redundant, and (2)
reordering operations can eliminate sequences.
7.1
Transactions with no effect
We want to simplify each transaction in noname+ by removing operations that are
redundant. Operations are redundant to the privacy analysis if they do not affect
the system, whereas operations are effectful if they change the system’s state. In
noname+, we have three effectful operations: send, cell write, and release. If it does
not contain these operations, we consider a transaction redundant to the privacy
analysis.

Chapter 8
From Choreography to noname:
A Translation Attempt
This chapter presents the final step in translating choreographies to noname by ad-
dressing three key challenges: (1) generating role choices, (2) eliminating sequences,
and (3) ensuring session consistency. We address these challenges by extending our
tool to protocols that include role choices and state hints. Then, we perform two
transformations on the choreography to integrate these features. Without modifica-
tion, these transformations allow us to maintain the existing translation procedure
from choreographies to noname+ transactions.
We have not yet discussed the support for role choices in choreographies.
In
chapter 2, we saw that the BAC protocol in noname (fig. 2.2) represents the tag x as
a privacy choice, x ∈{t1, t2}. We extend our tool to support role choices by allowing
the modeler to define a role instantiation. Then, we transform our choreography to
simulate and broadcast the choice of roles by a dedicated initialization role.
Next, we eliminate sequences in noname+ by splitting transactions at each se-
quence to obtain a set of transactions. However, we must ensure state information
across the transactions, as we saw with the session message in the BAC example
(chap. 2). We have not found an automatic solution to determine the relevant state
information. Furthermore, we want to avoid relying on a naive approach that con-
siders everything as relevant state information. Thus, we propose state hints where
the modeler specifies the relevant information.
We automatically generate session messages between transactions with the infor-
mation from the state hints. In addition, the resulting messages can be distributed to
other agents to enforce session consistency across all transactions. Then, we ensure
that every participant operates within the same session context.
Lastly, we discuss why this is only a translation attempt, as the output contains
many transactions. Thus, the protocols become too large to verify automatically in
noname because there are an infeasible number of states to explore.

8.4
State Hints
After splitting transactions, some checks may refer to variables defined earlier in the
sequence, making them out of scope. We encountered this issue in figure 8.4, where
Transaction x1 creates the nonce X5, and Transaction x2 contains a conditional
check that depends on X5. However, since we create X5 in the first transaction, it is
not in scope during the execution of the second. As a result, any checks involving
X5 in Transaction x2 become invalid. We address this issue by introducing session
messages, as illustrated in the noname example (fig. 2.2).
We have not found an automatic solution to determine the necessary state in-
formation. Alternatively, we let the modeler specify the necessary information with
state hints. When we run the resulting BAC protocol (appendix A.1.4) after remov-
ing the last sequences, we get the error in noname: ”X5 must be declared before use”.
From this error, the modeler can easily identify the missing state information, and
they should add this in a state hint. In the BAC protocol, X5 refers to the nonce Nt
from the choreography, which gives us the state hint in figure 8.1.
While the state hint ensures consistency across the two transactions for the tag x,
we also bind the nonce Nt to our session identifier. Then, we allow other transactions
to ensure all receives messages have the same session context. Specifically, our reader
r in the BAC example checks if the nonce Nt is bound to the same session identifier
as the received role choice of x.

modeler specifies the state hint at the point in the choreography where the resulting
protocol needs the information. Then, our parser can accurately define the state hint

transactions in the resulting protocol. In the introduction of noname, we specified a

increase this bound for the noname tool to find a privacy violation.

future work.

experiments primarily focused on the BAC protocol to illustrate the translation to
noname. Other protocols may involve more complex interactions, leading to larger
resulting protocols. As a result, our translation and applicability to various protocols
remain uncertain in the current implementation. We leave this as future work to
explore the translation of other protocols to noname and assess their feasibility for
verification.

9.1
Input Protocol
Figure 9.1 shows the input of the BAC protocol to our tool. Input protocols for our
tool must adhere to the syntax and structure required by our extended choreography
syntax (fig. 4.1). In addition, we need initial declarations as required by noname
(sec.
3.4).
To enable seamless translation, we allow the user to define Sigma0,
Sigma, Algebra, and Cells declarations exactly as in noname [5]. These declarations
form the header of the resulting noname protocol.
After this header, the user provides the actual protocol definition, which consists
of a role instantiation block and a choreography describing interactions between roles.
We have already introduced an example of such a specification for the BAC protocol
in figure 8.1.
This approach allows the user to work in the high-level choreography language
while our tool generates the low-level noname protocols that are verifiable.

9.3
Robustness
Robustness is a central design goal for our tool, as we want to ensure that we can
reliably handle a wide range of input protocols. In addition, we want to provide
meaningful feedback to the user whenever the input is ill-formed. Currently, the tool
includes the following quality-assurance measures:
• A comprehensive test suite of unit and integration tests that validate each
translation step for various inputs (implemented using the Tasty2 testing frame-
work).
• Property-based testing for the parser, allowing us to trust the parsing process
of standard input choreographies (powered by QuickCheck3).
Testing has been crucial to the development of our tool, as we initially followed the
principles of test-driven development (TDD). This approach has helped us obtain a
high level of confidence in the correctness of the foundational components. Then, we
also ensure that changes to the code do not break existing functionality.
Although these measures already provide a strong baseline for correctness and
reliability, we suggest further improvements in the next section to make the tool
more robust and user-friendly. Complex and large-scale protocols, especially, require
a strong guarantee from our tool.
9.3.1
Potential Improvements
The tool can benefit from improvements to the current version. Firstly, we recom-
mend including a static analysis phase to check the input protocol for common errors
before the translation. This phase would include the following checks:
• Function declaration verification: We want to ensure that every function in the
protocol is properly declared before we use it in the choreography.
• Keyword and identifier verification: We want to maintain a list of reserved
keywords to ensure that role names, messages, etc., do not clash with the tool’s
internal keywords.
• Labels for branching sends choices (see appendix A.1.5): noname does not sup-
port numbers, so we use number constants N0, N1, N2 . . . in the choice of
branching sends.
The current version always instantiates {N0, . . . , N9} in
Sigma. However, we must validate that no branching send needs more than
10 choices (or less for redundant number constants).
In addition to the static analysis, we suggest improving error messages and diag-
nostics. Then, we would enhance the user experience by providing more actionable
error messages. Here, we could draw inspiration from the Elm programming lan-
guage, which is known for its clear and helpful error messages [13].
In section 8.4, we saw that when we get the resulting transactions for noname, the
variables’ names are different from those in the input choreography. Here, we saw the
nonce Nt (see figure 8.1) is called X5 in the result (see figure 8.4). In the translation
to local behavior, we rename all variables to use as labels (sec. 4.3). We suggest that

These improvements will make the tool more robust when dealing with large,

Chapter 10
Conclusion
This thesis addresses the challenge of modeling security protocol with privacy. We
extended the choreography language with privacy-related features from (α, β)-privacy
and default behavior. We have formalized and implemented a translation from chore-
ographies to the noname tool to support automatic privacy verification. Then, we
allow protocol modelers to design privacy-sensitive protocols in the high-level chore-
ography language, which is more intuitive than the language for the noname tool.
While we extended the choreography language, we also extended its local behav-
ior and translation. Our work contributes with a two-step translation from the local
behavior to the extended noname language, noname+. First, we simulate branching
sends with non-deterministic choices, and then we sequentialize the local behavior to
follow the left-to-right order of operations as required by noname. Furthermore, we
explored simplification techniques to reduce the complexity of the resulting transac-
tions.
We propose an attempt at the last translation step from noname+ to noname,
where we use dummy roles to support broadcasting and self-messaging and state
hints for session management across transactions.
While we achieve the primary
goal of implementing a translation procedure from choreographies to noname, the
resulting protocol is too large for automatic verification. We view this thesis as the
first step towards a more practical approach to privacy-aware choreographies, but
the scalability and feasibility of automatic verification remain a challenge.
10.1
Related Work
The work presented in this thesis builds upon a foundation of research in formalizing
privacy guarantees in security protocols and methods for automated verification.
The theory of (α, β)-privacy introduced in [7] provides the foundational formal-
ization for privacy guarantees in a declarative way. In [8], the authors build on this
theory by defining (α, β)-privacy as a reachability problem. The work of [2] further
develops a decision procedure for verifying (α, β)-privacy in security protocols for
a bounded number of sessions and a specific algebraic theory. Here, noname [5] is
the tool that implements this decision procedure, enabling automatic verification of
privacy guarantees in a class of protocols.
Cryptographic choreographies are an ongoing area of research, which [6] formal-
izes. Their work defines a precise language for describing protocols as choreographies
and provides precise translation semantics for obtaining a local behavior. Further-
more, the translation automatically generates the local behavior for each participant,
62
==PAGE:72==
given the global choreography specification. This approach allows protocol model-
ers to reason about the intended communication flow of distributed systems without
delving into the complexities of low-level protocol details.
As this work assumes
derivation of knowledge and checks, we base the practical analysis of terms on the
work from [3].
Beyond these works, there are other approaches to symbolic protocol verification,
such as ProVerif [14]. In contrast to noname, ProVerif supports an unbounded number
of sessions.
However, it relies on diff-equivalence, making modeling and proving
privacy properties more restrictive and challenging in practice.
An interesting consideration of implementing the BAC protocol in noname [5] is
their use of memory to prevent replay attacks. We have not explored replay attacks
in this thesis, but automatically including memory operations for replay prevention
could be a valuable addition to our tool. In addition, in [2], they explore protocols
with an active, participating intruder. We do not explicitly support the intruder role
in our work, but it would be an interesting next step for the tool. Including such
a feature would expand the possibilities for modeling and verifying protocols with
more complex interactions involving active adversaries.
