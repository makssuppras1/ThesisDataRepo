Abstract 
The municipality of Copenhagen in its 'Climate Change Adaptation Plan' (Copenhagen City, 2011) 
promotes the separation of combined sewer in Low Impact Development (LIDs) solutions and the 
integration of LID into future urban planning. In this context the Greater Copenhagen Utility HOFOR 
commissioned to the consultancy company SWECO the design and a 3-year long monitoring plan of 
LIDs solutions in the residential area of Hornemanns Vænge (Valby), which is used as case study for 
this Master Thesis.  
 
The thesis work is divided into field work, modelling and monitoring parts. The objectives of the field 
work are to assess whether or not the saturated hydraulic conductivity measured at the top soil is 
representative of the saturated hydraulic conductivity measured at a depth, and to assess the saturated 
hydraulic conductivity degree of variability. In addition, a model in MIKE SHE is proposed to model 
the hydrological performance of one of the soakaways constructed in Hornemanns Vænge. The 
soakaway design is innovative because the soakaway is equipped with infiltration wells that connect 
the bottom of the soakaway (in the clay layer) to the sand layer. The baseline scenario models the 
soakaway only and the model outputs are the yearly maximum water level in the soakaway, and the 
soakaway degree of filling at the start of rain events (to reflect the initial conditions of the soakaway 
at the rain event start). The baseline scenario is compared with a scenario performed with different 
values of clay saturated hydraulic conductivity and a scenario where the soakaway is equipped with 
infiltration wells. In addition, the master thesis proposes a monitoring plan of the LIDs solution in 
Hornemanns Vænge, to investigate the hydrological performance change over time, to compare the 
performance among the different LIDs solutions and to investigate how the uncertainties of the design 
variables affect the performance.  
 
The field work shows that the average saturated hydraulic conductivity on the top soil is 2.4∙10-5 m/s 
and 1.8∙10-5 m/s, measured by double ring and infiltration hole testing method. The spatial degree of 
variability on the top soil is low probably because the top soil in Hornemanns Vænge consists of 
imported soil with uniform soil properties. The average saturated hydraulic conductivity in the clay 
layer is 8.7∙10-7 m/s with a standard deviation of 1.1∙10-6 m/s. The spatial degree of variability in the 
clay layer is lower than the one reported by other studies. The field work concludes that the saturated 
hydraulic conductivity at the top soil is not representative of the conductivity at a depth. 
 
The field work data was used as an input in the modelling; the baseline scenario has a clay saturated 
conductivity of 5.0∙10-7 m/s. The degree of filling at the start of rain events ranged between 0-45% in 
the baseline scenario. The modelling results suggest that the higher the saturated hydraulic 
conductivity, the lower the degree of filling at the beginning of rain events (e.g. the maximum degree 
of filling at the start of rain events is only 15% with a clay conductivity of 3.0∙10-6 m/s). The soakaway 
was not functional at clay saturated conductivities below 3.0∙10-7 m/s, as the water level in the 
soakaway keeps increasing, without facing any significant emptying phase. Nevertheless, it is 
relevant to point out that the results might be overestimated (i.e. the water level in the soakaway is 
higher than the real one) because the modelling included only 1D vertical infiltration. With the 
introduction of the infiltration wells 99% of the infiltration occurs through the infiltration wells, rather 
than at the soakaway bottom and because of this the water level in the soakway is very low compared

1 Introduction 
Urban drainage is a very old field, which traditionally focused on conveyance of water away from 
the urban area (Fletcher et al., 2014). In the past years, the increase of population living in urban areas 
together with the land use change (i.e. the increase of impervious surfaces), increased the hydraulic 
load on the urban drainage network. In addition to this, climate change is expected to modify the 
rainfall patter in Scandinavia, increasing the occurrence of extreme rain events (Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 
2011). As a consequence to the above, an increase in Combined Sewer Overflows (CSO) and flooding 
episodes was observed, and they are expected to be even more frequent in the future, due to climate 
change. As a solution to solve the urban water cycles issues mentioned earlier, the use of sustainable 
stormwater technologies has been attempted in many cities (Madsen, nd). Sustainable stormwater 
technologies aim at restoring the hydrological cycle prior the urban development by enhancing 
infiltration, evapotranspiration onsite storage and detention, evaporation, transpiration.  The so-called 
Low Impact Developments (LID) solutions treat the stormwater on site, hence allowing the 
disconnection of a certain area from the drainage network. Examples of LIDs solutions are 
bioretention basins, green roofs, permeable pavement, swales, etc. These infrastructures have the 
potential to alleviate the hydraulic load to the drainage network as well as to make the cities more 
livable by serving as recreational area, increasing the ecosystem biodiversity ect. The municipality of 
Copenhagen elaborated a 'Climate Change Adaptation Plan' in 2011 (Copenhagen City, 2011) in 
which among other things promotes the separation of combined sewer in LID solutions and the 
integration of LID into future urban planning. In this context the Greater Copenhagen Utility HOFOR 
commissioned to the consultancy company SWECO the design and a 3-year long monitoring plan of 
LID solutions in the residential area of Hornemanns Vænge (Valby), which is used as case study for 
this Master Thesis. The LIDs solutions designed by SWECO consists of three raingardens, four 
soakways, five infiltration wells, a detention storage with infiltration wells. Different LIDs 
technologies were designed with the purpose of comparing their functionalities and performances. 
The LIDs solutions designed by SWECO are innovative because they propose a non-conventional 
approach according to the following: i) the soakways are equipped with infiltration wells that connect 
the soakaway bottom in the clay layer to the sand layer; ii) the stormwater runoff is conveyed to two 
soakaways by surface runoff against the conventional approach which is via underground pipe. With 
this Master Thesis work the hydraulic modelling of one of these innovative solutions is investigated. 
To support the modelling, a field work campaign to measure the soil infiltration rate was set up.

1.1 Aims and Objectives 
The master thesis work is organized into three different parts (field work, monitoring and modelling) 
having the following objectives.  
 
Field Work  
 
 Is the saturated hydraulic conductivity measured at the top soil representative of the 
infiltration rate at a different depth (for instance the bottom of an infiltration unit)? In order 
to answer this questions infiltration tests at different depths will be performed (e.g. top soil, 
bottom of the soakaway); 
 What is the spatial variability of the parameter saturated hydraulic conductivity? 
 
Monitoring Program  
 
 Propose an investment priority list for the equipment needed for the 3-year long monitoring 
program; the objectives of the monitoring plan are to investigate the LIDs solutions 
performance over time, to compare the performance among the different LIDs solutions and 
to investigate how the uncertainties of the design variables affect the performance.  
 
Modelling 
 
 Model the hydrological performance of a soakaway with the software Mike SHE: 
o The simulation outputs were the water budget, the yearly maximum water levels in the 
soakaway and degree of filling at the start of a rain event; 
o The outputs were analyzed for (1) a baseline scenario with a single soakaway; (2) a 
scenario with increased precipitation due to climate change, (3) a scenario with 
different values of saturated hydraulic conductivity based on the field work results, (4) 
a scenario with soakaway and infiltration wells.

Site Location 
The case study of this project is located in the residential area of Hornemanns Vænge, Valby. The 
plan view of the site can be seen in Figure 1. The residential area consists of six apartment blocks

Existing stormwater runoff conditions in Hornemanns Vænge 
 
Currently the runoff from Hornemanns Væange is accommodated by the sewerage pipe in Kulbanevej 
which discharges the flow into the creek Harrestrup Å.  Model simulation performed by SWECO 
(2015) with Chicago Design Storm (CDS) rain having 10 years return period showed that the diameter 
of sewer pipe from Honrnemanns Vænge to Harrestrup Å would need to be increased from 600 to 
700 mm to avoid the build-up of water above ground level. In addition to the current situation, the 
stormwater runoff from the new buildings of Hornemanns Vænge would likely increase the frequency 
of combined sewer overflows at the creek Harrestrup Å.  
 
To alleviate the hydraulic conditions of the pipe in Kulbanevej and the recipient Harrestrup Å, the 
water utility HOFOR commissioned the project ‘LIDs in Hornemanns Vænge’ to the consultancy 
company SWECO. With the implementation of the project, the runoff from the westernmost part of 
the roof will be disconnected from the sewer network and instead be accommodated LIDs solutions 
constructed in the gardens. In Figure 2 the LIDs catchment is highlighted in green. All the gardens, 
with the exception of Garden 1 (due to the polluted soil) will host a LIDs solution. The chosen LIDs 
solution are different from garden to garden and they include a raingarden, four soakaways equipped 
with infiltration wells, five infiltration wells only and a detention storage equipped with infiltration 
wells.

equivalent to the bottom of the soakaway. In particular, the priority is to determine the infiltration 
rate at near-saturation conditions2 because under this circumstance its value is approximately the 
saturated hydraulic conductivity.

The clay till often contains earthworm holes, fractures and sand lenses. In particular, the hydraulic 
conductivity appears to be strongly dependent on the small scale physical features at the site, where 
the upper layers normally have a higher conductivity due to the presence of fractures (Bockhorn et 
al, 2017). The saturated hydraulic conductivity in clayey till shows a high degree of variability, in the 
range of 10-10-10-4 m/s. Bockhorn et al. (2014) measured a variation of more than 2 orders of 
magnitude between double ring infiltration tests in a small scale site (100 m X 100 m). Another factor 
affecting the hydraulic conductivity is temperature. Braga et al.  (2007) observed a strong correlation 
between temperature and hydraulic conductivity, with a cyclical pattern of higher and lower 
infiltration rates occurring across the year. However, the uncertainty due to spatial variability is much 
higher (e.g. 10 or 100 times) than the uncertainty/variations due to temperature (e.g. 2 times).  
 
The double ring infiltrometer is a widely used tool to measure the infiltration rate. In Denmark, private 
citizens are allowed to install a soakaway in their garden based on infiltration rate results from the 
testing procedure described by Gabriel et al. (2012), hereby referred as to ‘Infiltration Hole’ test. Both 
methods were used during the field work, hence a brief introduction to the testing procedure is 
introduced below.  
 
The double ring test is performed with the use of a pair of stainless steel rings having the following 
diameters: 28/53 cm, 30/55 cm and 32/57 cm. The size of the rings does not affect the measurement 
result. The outer ring is installed to prevent horizontal infiltration. The rings are installed 
approximately 5 cm into the ground surface. Water is poured at the same level in both rings, as close 
to the surface as possible (max 10 cm). The water level measurements are taken from the inner ring 
as long as the infiltration rate reached a constant value (e.g. two consequent measurements show a 
change <10% (Eijkelkamp, 2016).  
 
The ‘Infiltration Hole’ test is performed inside a hole of approximately 0.25m X 0.25m inner surface 
area, and 30 cm depth from the grass roots. Water is poured inside the hole and when the drop in 
water level has reached a steady state, the infiltration rate is measured.  
 
The testing methods reported above are practical to use on the top soil, rather than at a depth. In fact, 
performing for instance a double ring measurement at a depth makes it necessary to excavate a pit to 
install the double rings and thus, this procedure tends to be quite invasive. It is not recommended to 
assume that the infiltration rate at the top soil is representative of the infiltration rate at lower layers, 
especially when the geological layer of the top soil is different from the geological layer at the depth 
of interest. This is the case in Hornemanns Vænge, where the upper layers are imported soil, followed 
by the clay layer. Even within the same geological layer (e.g. clay layer) it is critical to perform the 
infiltration test at the elevation of interest, because the hydraulic conductivity tends to decrease with 
depth. It appears clear that when the soakaway is envisioned to be installed a few meters below the 
ground level it is crucial to perform infiltration tests at the relevant depth, ideally by means of non-
invasive infiltration tests methods. According to this, double-ring tests were performed during the 
construction phase of the project at the bottom of the soakaways in Garden 3 and Garden 4. In 
addition, the saturated hydraulic conductivity was measured inside a caged borehole and compared 
to the results from the double ring test. The aim was to assess the applicability of this non-invasive 
testing method, in alternative of the double ring at the bottom of the soakaway.

A Mike SHE model was set up to continuously simulate the performance of one of the soakaways 
constructed (the southernmost soakaway in Garden 4).  The Mike SHE tool simulates the processes

An important factor governing the infiltration process in the unsaturated zone is the soil water content. 
The maximum water content in the soil is the water content at saturation (e.g. water content equal to 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
21 
 
the porosity). Nevertheless, under real conditions the soil matrix only reaches near saturated water 
contents. The water content resulting after 24 days of draining without the contribution of 
evapotranspiration is referred to as water content at field capacity (Dingam, 2002). In other words, 
the water content at field capacity measures the ability of the soil to hold water against gravity. Water 
is available for gravity drainage between saturated water content and water content at field capacity. 
Below field capacity the water can only be removed from the soil pores by root uptake or surface 
evapotranspiration. Beyond the wilting point the water is held too tight between the pores and plants 
are no longer able to extract it. The infiltration process is driven by gravity (e.g. hydraulic 
conductivity) at saturation water content, while at field capacity the capillary forces are predominant 
(Dingam, 2002).  
 
The water retention capacity of a soil is graphically represented by the soil moisture retention curve, 
where the water content is plotted in the x axis and the pressure head (cm) in the y axis. Often, the 
curve is plotted in semi-logarithmic scale with the log10(-pressure head in cm) also called pF in the y 
axis. The shape of the water retention curve is characteristic of the soil type and varies greatly among 
different soil texture classes. Clay shows high water retention capacities at any water content. Sandy 
soil have large particles pores and little water remains after they have been emptied. As a result, the 
retention curve of sand is flatter compared to the one of clay. This can be observed in Figure 20. 
 
 
Figure 20: Typical retention curve for sand, loam and clay soil (Hartmann, 2003) 
The Van Genuchten function is one of the most widely tool used to describe the water retention curve 
and hydraulic conductivity as a function of soil moisture content. The shape of the curve can be 
manipulated with the empirical constants ‘alpha’ and ‘n’. The former is equal to 1/pressure head at 
air entry value and the latter is related to the pore-size distribution. Increasing ‘n’ the retention curve 
gets flatter, while increasing ‘alpha’ lowers the pressure head at air entry value (see Figure 21).

3.3 Hydraulic Monitoring  
The first step of a monitoring plan is to define the study objectives. In particular, the objectives set 
for the monitoring plan of the LIDs solutions in Hornemanns Vænge are: 
 
 Does the performance improve, decay or remain constant over time? Previous studies found 
a performance decrease over time due to clogging. In the study of Warnaars et al. (1999) the 
hydraulic performance of two infiltration trenches in central Copenhagen was monitored for 
almost three years after the installation. A small reduction of the performance (i.e. the 
saturated hydraulic conductivity decreased in the range of 30 to 70%) was observed during 
that time, presumably due to clogging (Warnaars et al, 1999).  
 
 How does the performance compare among the different LIDs solutions in Hornemanns 
Vænge? The LIDs solutions in Hornemanns Vænge are all different from one another, thus it 
is relevant to rank them according to the hydraulic performance criteria. In addition, the design 
proposed an innovative approach in terms of water conveyance to the LIDs solutions. In 
several of the LIDs the stormwater is delivered by surface runoff and this should be compared 
to the conventional approach (i.e. water conveyed by underground pipe).  
 
 How do design variables uncertainties affect the performance? The design variable with the 
highest degree of uncertainty is found to be the runoff coefficient of the green area. In the 
design performed by SWECO its value was assumed to be 0.5, which is significantly higher 
than the yearly average runoff coefficients normally used for the design of sewer networks 
(i.e. 0.10-0.30). The monitoring shall investigate the runoff contribution from green area to 
be compared with the chosen design parameter. 
 
The hydraulic performance can be quantified in terms of yearly maximum water level and initial 
water level conditions. The same criteria will be used to evaluate the hydraulic performance of the 
southernmost soakaway in Garden 4, modeled with MIKE SHE. 
 
A conceptual model of the LIDs solutions to be investigated (i.e. the mass balance) will be developed 
in order to identify the type of data that needs to be collected. Based on this the monitoring plan and 
associated equipment will be defined. This Paragraph will guide the reader through the steps 
mentioned earlier, while the overview of the monitoring plan including details about the equipment 
installation, minimum number of measuring devices to be used, comparison with others monitoring 
studies etc., will be presented in Paragraph 4.3  Results and Discussion. 
 
The general formulation of the water balance for a single LIDs solution is presented below

Roof Runoff  
The roof runoff depends upon the rainfall intensity, the size of the roof and the runoff coefficient. 
Typically, the runoff coefficient ranges from 0.9 to 1.0. The runoff from the roof is conveyed to the 
LIDs solutions either by underground pipe or by surface runoff. The roof runoff can be measured by 
the use of flowmeters when the runoff is delivered to the LIDs by underground pipe. Many 
measurement devices have a limited range of flow conditions for which they are applicable. Large 
errors in measurement can occur when the flow is outside this range (Geosyntec Consultants and 
Wright Water Engineers, Inc., 2009). It is therefore critical to estimate the range of water flow 
expected to measure and make sure that the measuring device fits within this range. 
 
Green Area Runoff 
The green area runoff is observed after the interception loss and soil wetting have occurred. 
 
The interception denotes the process by which precipitation falls on vegetative surfaces, where it is 
subjected to evaporation. The interception is a function of the land cover (e.g vegetation type, density, 
and stage of development characterized by the leaf area index) and the rainfall patter (e.g intensity, 
duration, frequency). Literature values are available for several land cover types. According to the 
landscape design proposed in Hornemanns Vænge the vegetation cover will vary among the gardens, 
hence the interception loss cannot be assumed the same.  
 
The green area runoff coefficient depends upon the rainfall intensity and duration, the size of the 
catchment area, the soil type, and the saturation conditions. The runoff coefficient takes into account 
==PAGE:40==
 
 
26 
 
the water lost due to water ponding in depression storages and soil wetting, which is a function of the 
initial soil moisture content, the soil infiltration capacity and the return period of the rain event (Figure 
48, Annex 5). Quantification of the runoff from green area is challenging. Indirect estimation is 
possible by measuring the water level in the garden, together with data on rainfall event (e.g. duration, 
return period), initial soil moisture content (with the use of soil moisture devices) and infiltration 
capacity of the soil (measured through double-ring infiltrometer).  
 
Traditionally the stormwater is delivered to the LIDs by an underground piped, such as in Garden 3, 
Garden 4 south and Garden 5. In the rest of the implemented LIDs the stormwater arrives by surface 
runoff. This is a novel approach which deserves further investigation, and its functionality should be 
compared with the traditional approach.  
 
Output 
Infiltration  
This term denotes the infiltration from the LIDs solution to the surrounding soil. Its value is a function 
of the soil water content and the infiltration capacity of the soil. The latter can be measured for 
instance through double-ring infiltrometer tests, which however is not suitable to represent the 
geological heterogeneity. Nevertheless, the higher the number locations tested, the better the 
infiltration rate spatial variability is described and quantified. The overall infiltration rate of a LIDs 
solution can be calculated by looking at the water table drop over time.  
 
Delta Storage 
Water level in the LIDs 
The delta storage terms denotes the water level difference between the start and the end of the rain 
event. The water level in the LIDs is a function of its emptying time, which depends on the infiltration 
rate and the saturation conditions of the soil. The water level can be measured by the use of pressure 
transducers.

4.1 Field Work Results  
4.1.1 Testing on the top soil 
The results of the testing campaign on the top soil are presented in Table 7 and Figure 25. The mean 
saturated hydraulic conductivity on the top soil (n=7) with the double ring test was 2.4∙10-5 m/s. The 
testing locations were spread among different gardens (with at least one testing location per garden 
with the exception of Garden 3) and between the northernmost (n=5) and southernmost (n=2) parts 
of the garden. In general, the saturated hydraulic conductivity did not show significant differences as 
a function of the garden or side (north/south) location. The saturated hydraulic conductivity is within 
the same order of magnitude (between 1.4∙10-5 and 3.5∙10-5 m/s) among the different gardens, with 
the exception of one location in Garden 4 where its value was lower (2∙10-6 m/s). The limited spatial 
variability is presumably connected to the fact that the top layer in Hornemanns Vænge consist of 
imported soil and it exhibits more uniform geological properties than intact soil.  
 
The comparison between the saturated hydraulic conductivity with double ring and infiltration hole 
tests shows good accordance (Table 7, Figure 25), with the saturated hydraulic conductivity measured 
by the infiltration hole test being always slightly lower than the one with the double ring. The 
saturated hydraulic conductivity measured by the infiltration hole test was expected to be higher than 
the one with the double ring. The main reason to believe this is that the infiltration hole test accounts 
both horizontal and vertical infiltration. The surface area available for vertical infiltration is 
approximately 0.06 m2  in the infiltration hole test vs 0.07 m2  in the double ring. In addition, the 
infiltration hole test includes the horizontal infiltration contribution with an area available for the 
infiltration which ranges from approximately 0.30 m2 (assuming a water height inside the hole equal 
to the hole depth) to 0.01 m2 (i.e. when the water height above the hole bottom is 1 cm). Nevertheless, 
the infiltration test with the hole was performed at a depth deeper than the depth used for the double 
ring test. The double ring test was performed on the top soil and is representative of the first 20 cm 
below ground level, while the infiltration hole test was performed up to 40 cm below ground level. 
The lower saturated hydraulic conductivity with the infiltration hole testing method compared to 
double ring can be explained assuming that the soil properties 30-40 cm below ground level were not 
the same as the ones immediately below ground level (<20 cm).

4.1.3 Comparison with the results from GEO 
The double ring results at a depth are compared with the saturated hydraulic conductivity measured 
by GEO during the geological survey in 2015. The testing procedure adopted by GEO was different 
from the one employed in this study, hence a brief introduction is here provided. The testing was 
carried out inside a 5 m deep borehole, which extended from the ground level. The geological 
horizons crossed by the borehole were the upper fill layer, the moraine clay layer and the sand (in the 
last 30-40 cm). Nevertheless, the filter screen covered the interval between 2 and 5 m below ground 
level and hence included the moraine clay and the sand layers only (i.e. the filter screen length 
extended in the clay layer for c. 80-85% of its length, and c. 15-20% in the sand layer). The saturated 
hydraulic conductivity measured by GEO was fairly good, between 1.6∙10-5-3.5∙10-5 m/s. Given the 
fact that most of the filter screen laid in the clay horizon, which according to this study has a 
conductivity in the range of 2.8∙10-6-1.2∙10-7 m/s, it can be concluded the fairly good conductivity 
measured by GEO is not representative of the clay layer and is rather due to the sand layer. This 
comparison shows that when performing an infiltration tests it is important to clearly define which 
geological horizon is the target of the investigation. For instance, if the target is the moraine clay 
layer, the testing needs to be performed in the clay layer only, because including even a small portion 
of another geological horizon such as sand, risks to yield values of infiltration rate which are not 
actually representative. In general, the field work results together with the literature review showed 
that it is crucial to perform the testing at the relevant depth (e.g. where the soakaway will be 
constructed) because the hydraulic conductivity even within the same geological horizon is a function 
of the depth below ground level.

4.1.4 Measurement error and uncertainty evaluation  
The main measurement error in the double ring infiltrometer (and infiltration hole) experiment comes 
from reading error, especially in low permeability soil (Zekele et al., 2005). In particular, the 
identified sources of random errors in the double ring testing are: i) the water level was measured on 
a measurement stick floating on the top of the water. On few occasions the wind caused the measuring 
stick to move, increasing the probability of observational errors; ii) the measuring stick resolution 
was 1 mm, hence any changes in water level lower than 1 mm were estimated; iv) parallax; v) poor 
connection between the ring wall and the ground, which leads to leakage of water and overestimation 
of the infiltration rate. As of systematic errors, the main source of error is connected to the measuring 
stick sensitivity (defined as the smallest absolute amount of change that can be detected by a 
measurement) that was limited to 0.0005 m (this value is assumed by the author based on the 
experience on the field). 
 
Zekele et al. (2005) reports a 10% measurement error (1 mm reading error per 1 cm depth) as a 
practical scenario for field infiltration measurements with double ring infiltrometers. Nevertheless, 
the main source of uncertainty related to the infiltration rate measurements is the spatial variability, 
rather than the measurement error.

The circumstances that led to 2001 maximum water level in the soakaway are described as an 
illustrative example of the reasons that lead to the water level peaks in the soakaway . The 7th of 
August a rain event with a return period of 11 years (accumulated volume equal to 53 mm in 8 hours) 
provokes the water level in the soakaway to increase from 11 cm to 33 cm. The yearly maximum of 
63 cm is registered the 22th of September, 45 rain events (with a small return period, the average 
being 0.05 years) after the extreme rain event of the 7th August. The minimum water level between 
the 7th of August and the 22th of September was 30 cm. The time required to infiltrate 30 cm of water 
given a conductivity of 5∙10-7 m/s is 7 days, assuming no additional rain input. If additional rain 
events happen within this time scale the soakaway capacity might not be recovered and the water 
level overall keeps increasing.  
 
Based on the above it can be argued that the maximum water level is caused by a combination of 
factors such as the occurrence of a fairly extreme rainfall event followed by smaller but frequent rain 
events that contribute to the water height build up in the soakaway. The infiltration rate of the soil is 
also a key parameter because it dictates the magnitude of the water table build up.

Degree of filling at the start of rain events 
The degree of filling at the start of rain events ranged between 0-45% in the baseline scenario. 
Locatelli et al. (2015b) modeled the degree of filling at the start of rain events as a function of the 
soakaway design return period. The study found that the higher the soakaway design return period, 
the lower the soakaway degree of filling at the beginning of rain events. In particular, the degree of 
filling at the start of rain events ranged between 5-45% for a soakaway designed with a return period 
of 5 years and a saturated hydraulic conductivity of 8.2∙10-7 m/s (Locatelli et al, 2015b). In this Master 
Thesis study the degree of filling at the start of rain events ranged between 0-45%, given a soakaway 
designed with return period of 10 years and a saturated hydraulic conductivity of 1.0∙10-8 m/s. The 
results in this study are not in agreement with the findings of Locatelli et al. (2015b), because the 
same degree of filling is found, given a soakaway designed with a return period which is twice the 
return period reported by Locatelli et al. (2015b) (i.e. in the study of Locatelli et al. (2015b) the 
soakaway was designed with a return period of 5 years, while in this study the return period is 10 
years). This discrepancy can be explained by the fact that the model of Locatelli et al. (2015b) 
included 2D infiltration, while the one of this study only 1D vertical infiltration. The water levels

4.2.2 Model Limitations 
A limitation of the current model step up is that the water movement in the unsaturated zone is limited 
to the vertical direction. Including the horizontal infiltration process (e.g. with the coupling of the 
unsaturated and saturated zone) is expected to reduce the water level in the soakaway and reduce and 
emptying time of the soakaway.  
 
The groundwater table was assumed to be fixed at 7 m below the ground level and 4.8 m below the 
soakaway bottom. The interaction between unsaturated and saturated was not investigated. 
Nevertheless, unless the water table is deep the interaction between infiltration rate and water table 
should be considered. In general, the extensive infiltration can lead to the increase of the average 
water table level and lead to groundwater mounds. In addition, the infiltration rate decreases as the 
water table approaches the soakaway. Locatelli et al. (2015) showed that infiltration is affected by 
the groundwater table when the unsaturated depth is lower than 11-12 m for silty clay loam. 
 
The dependency of the hydraulic conductivity on temperature is not included. Braga et al. (2007) 
observed a strong correlation between temperature and hydraulic conductivity, with a cyclical pattern 
of higher and lower infiltration rates occurring across the year. According to this it can be argued that 
the water level modeled is overestimated during the summer months and underestimated during the 
winter months.  
 
The effect of clogging on the infiltration capacity of the soakaway is not modeled. Clogging reduces 
the effective area through which infiltration occurs, hence leading to a lower infiltration capacity and 
a water level increase in the soakaway. The bottom of the soakaway is more prone to clogging, rather 
than the sides. The sediment thickness at the bottom is normally within 0.1-1 cm, which is enough to 
cause an impermeable bottom (Bouwer, 2000). Warnaars et al. (1999) monitored the performance of 
two infiltration trenches located in central Copenhagen and observed a 30-70% reduction on the initial 
infiltration capacity of the system already after 2 years and ¾ of operation, due to clogging.  
 
The software MIKE SHE is normally used to simulate hydrological processes in large catchments 
and is less suitable to model small soakaways. It is time consuming and tedious to set up a model in 
MIKE SHE. In future studies other approaches are recommended (e.g. coupling MIKE URBAN and 
MIKE SHE).   
 
Due to the lack of data the model cannot be calibrated and validated.

Comparison with other monitoring studies 
Warnaars et al. (1999) monitored for nearly three years two infiltration trenches installed in central 
Copenhagen. The infiltration trenches were located in a garden surrounded by apartment blocks. The 
two trenches were constructed 16 m in length, 0.8 m in height and width. 600 m2 of roof and paved 
areas are connected to the infiltration trenches, which has an active volume of 8m2. Warnaars et al. 
(1999) measured continuously rain, runoff flow and water levels in the trenches. Rain was measured 
by a tipping-bucket device, the inflow to the trenches was measured by an electromagnetic flow 
device located in the flow-division manhole. The water levels in the trenches were monitored with 
the use of pressure transducers placed at either ends of the trenches.  
 
In the study of Dietz and Clausen (2005) two rain gardens with dimensions 2.74 m by 3.35 m and 
storage depth of 15.24 cm were monitored to investigate the stormwater runoff reduction. The roof 
runoff connected to the raingardens was measured by using tipping buckets. The precipitation was 
measured using a heated tipping-bucket rain gauge.  
 
The comparison with the studies above shows similarities in terms of monitoring equipment used in 
the monitoring and the proposed one by this study.

Comments about the equipment installation and sensitivity 
Rain Gauge 
Critical factors regarding a rain gauge installation are the following (Geosyntec Consultants and 
Wright Water Engineers, Inc., 2009): 
 
 Vegetation (trees) or structures should not obstruct precipitation. A general rule of thumb is 
to place the device twice as far from the obstruction as the obstruction is tall; The same 
indication is given by the supplier Eijkelkamp (Eijkelkamp, 2016). 
 Provide enough height above the ground to prevent vandalism; 
 Being aware of possible influence of wind patterns around buildings in urban areas (e.g. make 
sure gauge location conditions are comparable to the area tributary to LIDs solution). 
 
The possible locations selected to host the rain gauge are (Figure 43): 1) on the top of a bike shed; 2) 
on the top of one of the residential buildings; 3) on the top of maintenance service building.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The prons and cons of every option are evaluated according the criteria of: 
 Location accessibility, which is related to the site height. This factor is particularly important 
since the rain gauge will have to be accessed approximately once per month to retrieve data 
and on a longer time scale for instrument calibration; 
 Obstruction both in terms of vegetation and building; 
 Location Representativeness: the rainfall pattern captured by the rain gauge needs to reflect 
the rainfall characteristic one of the site.   
 
Ideally, the rain gauge would be located at the top of one of the residential buildings (Table 17). 
However, in practice this will not be feasible because the roofs are difficult to access. To compromise 
between optimal location and instrument accessibility, it is suggested to locate the rain gauge at the 
top of the maintenance service building. It is recommended to register rain data with a minute 
frequency like DMI rain gauges, to allow data comparison.

Flowmeters 
It is envisioned to install a flowmeter in Garden 5, Garden 3(south) and Garden4(south). A critical 
challenge of the use of flowmeters is to make sure they capture completely the expected flow range, 
from low intensity rainfalls to high intensity rainfall. Butler and Davies (2011) reports that gutters are 
often designed to accommodate a fixed rainfall intensity of 50 mm/h. The catchment area of a single 
gutter is approximately equal to 108 m2, hence the maximum flow is expected to be 5400 L/hr. It is 
suggested to couple an electromagnetic flow meter to measure high flows (e.g. more than 75 L/hr) 
and a tipping bucket for low flows (e.g. <100 L/hr) as implemented in the study of Garnier et al. 
(2016). In the study of Dietz and Clausen (2005) the roof runoff was also measured by the use of a 
tipping bucket. 
 
Green Area Runoff 
The green area runoff shall be investigate at least in Garden 2 and 3 (or 4). The investigation aims at 
assessing the magnitude of the green area runoff to compare it with the design criteria of the LIDs 
solution (runoff coefficient equal to 0.5). In addition it would be interesting to compare the 
performance of the soakaways were the runoff is conveyed by underground pipe to ones where the 
water is conveyed by surface runoff. 
  
Water Level Measurements  
The water level measurements shall rely on the use of pressure transducers. The time step chosen to 
store the water level data is a trade-off between space availability in the datalogger memory and the 
minimum water level that wants to be detected.  All the LIDs (with the exception of the rain garden 
in Garden 2) will have a connection to the sand layer through infiltration wells. With this set up the 
sand infiltration rate will be the governing one. Assuming its value to be 5∙10-4 m/s a water height of 
10 cm would infiltrate in 3 minutes. It is recommended to program the data logger of the pressure 
transducers to register the water level continuously and store the average water levels every 3 minutes 
as a start. The time step can always be changed/adjusted after the first data is retrieved and analyzed.   
Storing the data with a time step lower than three minutes is not considered practical in terms of 
memory card availability. In the study of Dietz and Clausen (2005) the water level inside a rain garden 
with saturated hydraulic conductivity of 3.8 cm/hr (1.0∙10-5 m/s) was measured every minute and a 
30-minute average was recorded. In the study of Braga et al. (2007) the water level measurements 
==PAGE:65==
 
 
51 
 
inside an infiltration basin with a saturated hydraulic conductivity of 0.61 cm/hr (or 1.7∙10-6 m/s) 
were taken and recorded in a 5 minute time interval.

6 Conclusion 
Field Work 
The average saturated hydraulic conductivity on the top soil with the double ring infiltrometer was 
2.4∙10-5 m/s with a standard deviation of 1.2∙10-5 m/s. While the testing locations were spread among 
the five gardens the saturated hydraulic conductivity spatial variability was limited, probably because 
the top layer in Hornemanns Vænge consist of imported soil and exhibits a more uniform geological 
properties than intact soil. The average saturated hydraulic conductivity on the top soil with the 
infiltration hole test was 1.8∙10-5 m/s with a standard deviation of 6.1∙10-6 m/s. The double ring test 
showed slightly lower values compared to the infiltration hole test. The average saturated hydraulic 
conductivity at the bottom of the southernmost soakaways in Garden 3 and Garden 4 with the double 
ring infiltrometer was 8.7∙10-7 m/s with a standard deviation of 1.1∙10-6 m/s. The investigation 
concludes that the saturated hydraulic conductivity at the top soil is not representative of the saturated 
hydraulic conductivity at a depth. In particular, the saturated hydraulic conductivity decreases with 
depth with the upper layers characterized by higher saturated hydraulic conductivity due to the 
presence of earthworms, roots etc. According to this, it appears important to perform the infiltration 
tests at the relevant depth and to include in the testing only the geological horizon which is the target 
of the testing. The saturated hydraulic conductivity spatial variability in the clay layer was higher 
than the spatial variability observed on the top soil, but still lower compared to the findings of other 
studies. Bockhorn et al. (2014) measured a change of saturated hydraulic conductivity with a variable 
of more than two orders of magnitude. According to the above it is not excluded that increasing the 
number of testing in Hornemanns Vænge would result in a higher spatial variability. 
 
Modelling  
The degree of filling of the soakaway in the baseline scenario did not exceed 50%, and the maximum 
water level in the 10 years simulation was 0.66 m. The modeling results suggest that yearly maximum 
water levels in the soakaway are caused by a combination of factors such as rainfall volume and the 
water level at the beginning of the rain event. The scenarios performed with saturated hydraulic 
conductivities of the clay layer equal to 1.0∙10-7, 3.0∙10-7, 3.0∙10-6 m/s (in agreement with the field 
work results) allowed to conclude that: i) the soakaway does not work with conductivities lower than 
3.0∙10-7 m/s because the water level keeps building up and the soakaway never empties; ii) the 
modelling output (e.g. yearly maximum and the degree of filling) significantly varies provided small 
changes of the saturated hydraulic conductivity (1 order of magnitude). Although the soakaway was 
designed with an infiltration rate of 1.0∙10-8 m/s the modelling shows that it gets completely full after 
1 year simulation with a clay saturated hydraulic conductivity equal to 1.0∙10-7 m/s. This is 
presumably caused by the fact that the modelling included only 1D vertical infiltration and the water 
levels are expected to be lower with 2D infiltration. The results from the infiltration wells scenario 
show that with the introduction of the infiltration wells screened in the sand layer really improved the 
performance. The maximum water level during the 10 years of simulation was 10 cm for the 
infiltration wells scenario. The soakaway degree of filling at the start of rain events is <1% for a 
return period of 10 years. With this set up the yearly maximum water levels are caused by rain events 
with high rainfall intensities (i.e. < 30 minutes). Given the modelling results above it is possible to 
argue that with the use of the infiltration wells either the soakaway volume or the number of 
infiltration wells could be reduced. All the simulations were performed with runoff coefficients of 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
54 
 
0.1 and 0.9 for green area and roof runoff, respectively. In contrast, the LIDs design assumed 0.5 
runoff coefficient for the green area.  
 
Monitoring Plan 
In order to monitor the hydrological performance of the LIDs over time the data that needs to be 
collected includes precipitation, flow measurements and water levels in the LIDs solutions. Based on 
this the priority equipment for the monitoring plan includes (as a minimum) a rain gauge, nine soil 
moisture devices and at least 36 pressure transducers. A critical point of the monitoring was found to 
be the flow measurement because the measuring device needs to capture a large range of flows. 
According to this it has been suggested to couple an electromagnetic flow meter to measure high 
flows (e.g. more than 75 L/hr) and a tipping bucket for low flows (e.g. <100 L/hr). The flow 
monitoring is envisioned to be performed in 3 different locations in Garden 5, Garden 3 (south) and 
Garden 4 (south). The design variable with the highest degree of uncertainty is found to be the runoff 
coefficient of the green area. According to this, the use of soil moisture devices is envisioned to be 
used to investigate the green area runoff contribution to the LIDs.
