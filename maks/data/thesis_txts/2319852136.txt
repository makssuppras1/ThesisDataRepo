Abstract
In biped robot locomotion, mostly a serial type legs have been used so far. This type of leg shows many
disadvantages, like signiﬁcant weight, low stiﬀness and poor disturbance rejection. All of the above result
in high energy consumption and low stability. In this paper, an analysis of a new type of leg, i.e. three
degrees of freedom delta type leg is investigated. First, a kinematic analysis of both delta and serial
robot is performed, including forward and inverse kinematics calculation, visualisation of the robots
and workspace analysis. Further, equations of motions for both robots are implemented and tested in
Simmechanics environment. Finally, both models are compared in terms of performance in diﬀerent
walking scenarios. In this paper, it is shown that in speciﬁc scenarios, delta leg is superior to the serial
type in terms of maximum torque and energy consumption.
Keywords
Delta robot, biped robot, kinematics, dynamics, equations of motion, bipedal walking,
periodic gait, dynamic stability

Chapter 1.
Introduction
1.1
Walking robots
The interest in walking machines dates way back to the ancient times, since mankind has always
seemed to be curious about researching and development of machines and mechanisms that resemble
animal behaviour. Existence of various walking mechanisms and animal-like machines is known to have
existed in ancient Greek and China times, and numerous sketches and design plans of artiﬁcial systems
mimicking humans or animals emerged during the renaissance period in Europe [1].
In modern times, walking robots research has gained momentum after the end of Second World War
because of sudden development in many branches of technology. From 1968, when ﬁrst walking robot,
Phony Pony, controlled by computer, was introduced by McGee and Frank at the University of South
Carolina [2], numerous legged machines have been proposed and investigated. Noteworthy research has
been done at Massachusetts Institute of Technology in the topic of running and jumping legged robots,
started by M. Raibert in 1980. He presented various types of running machines using a model of inverted
pendulum for the movement synthesis. The posture while moving was stable and methods of stability
were not complicated [3]. Since then, MIT has presented a few walking machines moving with dynamical
stability, like Troody, Spring Flamingo or M2 [4].
ﬁgure 1.1: Asimo, Petman and Nao robots
Other important works have also been done in the topic, showing multiple advantages of legged
robots in the rough environment. RISE and Stickybot from Cutosky showed the ability to use biolog-
ically inspired limbs to climb surfaces perpendicular to the ground [5]. In Boston Dynamics company,
BigDog and SmallDog robots have been created, showing high capabilities in terms of moving through
rough and unsteady terrain [6].
Since in this paper main focus is on biped robotics, signiﬁcant achievements for these types of
machines should be mentioned. First noticeable works on two-legged machines has been presented by
1
==PAGE:13==
professor Kato in 1960s, introducing WL and WAP robotic series [7].
Later, more advanced robots
started to appear. Honda has developed E and P bipedal robots, which lead to development of well
know ASIMO robot [8]. Diﬀerent kinds of humanoid robots were introduced, like Kawada’s HRP-2 or
QRIO from Sony [9, 10]. One of the most popular biped robots have been created by Boston Dynamics,
including PETMAN and Atlas robots [11, 12]. Finally, a worthy of note is developed and investigated
at KAIST, HUBO robot, which has won the Darpa challenge in 2015 [13].
1.2
New type of leg
Main problem with the recent biped designs resides in the application of a serial kinematic chain
as leg of the robot. The main advantage of serial robots is big workspace and ease in kinematic and
dynamic analysis. On the other hand, there are some serious disadvantages to the serial type leg, like
low stiﬀness, accumulation and propagation of errors through the links, low eﬀective load and need for
high torque. This is why several new ideas for a leg type has been discussed and proposed.
ﬁgure 1.2: Atrias and raptor robots
One of the new concepts was proposed by KAIST university in their fast running Raptor robot,
where a composite, elastic leg was used [14]. Another example can be a Atrias robot from Oregon state
university, where a four-bar closed kinematic chain was implemented as a biped leg [15]. Some other
works on substitution of serial leg were also proposed, like parallel type legs from O. Kwon et. al. or Z.
Qi et. al. [16, 17]. However, the amount of research on the subject seems to be limited.
1.3
Parallel robots
In this thesis, a new approach for a biped leg is taken, with a use of delta type parallel robot. There-
fore, some historical overview of these type of machines is advisable. Works on parallel mechanisms can
be traced back to 1928 [18], when James Gwinnett patented a three degrees of freedom moving platform
for application in movie theaters. Around ten years later, a new type of parallel mechanics was developed
by Willard Pollard [19], which was a 5-DOF parallel robot with three arms. Further noticeable works in
the ﬁeld were performed by Eric Gough, inventor of ﬁrst octahedral hexapod parallel robot and Klaus
Cappel, who introduced a motion simulator of a helicopter based on a parallel mechanism idea [20, 21].
Noteworthy work has been presented by D. Steward, whose reasearch on parallel machines has had great
impact on further development in this ﬁeld [22]. One of the most important inventions in the area was
2
==PAGE:14==
done be Raymond Clavel in 1985, who came up with an idea of four degree of freedom Delta manipulator,
which was based on the concept of parallelograms [23]. Since then, thousands of delta robots has been
produced and many diﬀerent variations and models were used in industry.
1.4
Scope
This thesis considers various aspects of the delta robot and it’s superiority as a biped robot’s leg over
the serial type leg. First, a kinematic analysis of delta and serial mechanisms are presented. Further,
equations of motion for both serial and delta type biped robots are calculated and veriﬁed. In the next
chapter, a simulator for walking of the robots is described and visualisations for robots are presented.
Further step is analysing both structures in terms of maximum torque and general energy consumption
and comparing them together. Finally, conclusions from the project are given and future work plans are
discussed.

Chapter 2.
Background theory
In this chapter, some basic theory on serial and delta manipulators as well as bipedal walking is
presented. Also, a few general concepts from the ﬁeld of robotics are introduced and explained.
2.1
Robotics fundamentals
In this section, some fundamental knowledge from the robotics area ais discussed, in order to ensure
full clearness of the content of this thesis.
• Kinematic chain - part of a mechanism consisting of several rigid bodies connected together by
joints,
• simple kinematic chain - a kind of kinematic chain in which each link is connected to two or less
other links,
• closed-loop kinematic chain - a kind of kinematic chain in which one of the links in connected to
three or more other links,
• manipulator - a mechanical device that is used to manipulate objects in its surroundings. It is
often characterised as a robot with a function of human upper limb,
• kinematics - area of physics that describes the geometric properties of body motion without in-
cluding it’s physical attributes, like masses or forces,
• dynamics - area of physics that describes motion of bodies under the inﬂuence of forces,
• end-eﬀector - part of the manipulator located at it’s end and interacting with the environment,
• degree of freedom - a parameter describing the state of a physical system. Number of degrees of
freedom, or mobility, is the lowest number of independent variables needed for deﬁning mechanical
system’s conﬁguration,
• reference frame - point or set of points in space with respect to which position or change in position
of a body is considered,
• actuator - a mechanical device in the control system that produces an input signal to the system
depending on provided control signal.
2.2
Serial robots
Since this thesis is using serial leg biped as a comparison material, some basic knowledge of this
concept will be included in this section. Serial manipulator or robot is a series of rigid links connected
together by actuators in a serial fashion, creating an open kinematics chain. Each actuated joint adds a
degree of freedom to the end eﬀector.
4
==PAGE:16==
Serial robots are very popular, being the most common robot type in industry and robotics area in
general. This is due to ease in design and analysis, and large workspace which enables industrial manip-
ulators to reach further. However, as brieﬂy mentioned in the introduction chapter, serial manipulators
have also numerous disadvantages, namely:
• poor stiﬀness due to the nature of open kinematic chains,
• necessity of carrying the weight of a number of actuators for each consecutive actuated joint,
• accumulation and ampliﬁcation of position errors for each consecutive link resulting in low precision
and dexterity,
• poor relative payload ability.
2.3
Parallel robots
2.3.1
General overview
A parallel robot or manipulator is, as opposed to serial manipulator, a group of rigid links that
create a closed kinematic chain after being connected together with joints, i.e., end eﬀector is connected
to the base of the manipulator by more than one kinematic chain. Also, unlike serial robots, not all
joints in this mechanics have to be actuated.
The biggest disadvantage of parallel manipulators in comparison with serial robots is it’s limited
workspace. However, there are many important advantages that are worth mentioning, such as:
• fast dynamic response,
• better payload ability,
• actuation distribution,
• high stiﬀness and precision.
2.3.2
Delta robot
Since in this project a delta parallel robot was chosen for a biped leg, a brief description of the
concept will be presented. Delta type robot is a fully parallel robot, which means that the number of
degrees of freedom of the end eﬀector equals the number of kinematic chains connecting end-eﬀector to
the base platform, which in this case equals three. It consists of upper base platform, three two-link
kinematic chains and lower end-eﬀector platform. Delta robot is a RUU type manipulator, i.e., for each
kinematic chain it has one actuated revolute joint, one universal joint in the middle of the kinematic
chain (at the elbow), and one universal joint at the end eﬀector platform [24].

ﬁgure 2.1: Conceptual ﬁgure of delta type leg
The most important property of delta robot is the use of parallelograms in each kinematic chain,
which enables the mobile platform to maintain ﬁxed orientation with respect to the base platform, so
that the platforms are always parallel to each other. Therefore, the three degrees of freedom of the delta
robot are purely translational.
2.4
Biped walking fundamentals
2.4.1
Gait type
Gait is a method of relocating (changing the position) using legs and it is one of the methods of
locomotion of walking robots. There are many diﬀerent types of gaits. Classiﬁcations and descriptions of
those gaits usually come from observing the animal world. The type of a gait might be running, jumping
or walking.
Basic terms found in the literature and used in describing the gait can be as follows:
• gait period - time of realisation of one sequence of legs’ rearrangement,
• periodic gait - type of gait in which each step of the gait is repeated in the same way ,
• double support - situation in which the robot has two diﬀerent contact points (surfaces) with the
ground (two feet on the ground),
• single support - situation in which the robot has only one contact point of surface with the ground
(one foot on the ground),
• support polygon - the smallest convex set that contains the area of both feet of the robot.
6
==PAGE:18==
2.4.2
Stability
There are three distinct types of stability of walking robots’ movement:
• statically stable - this type of robot has a lot of active degrees of freedom, it’s conﬁguration (posture)
is constant. For this type of stability, movement if usually described only with the use of rules of
kinematics. The trajectories of movement of the body and limbs are set precisely and are expected
to be executed accurately.
• quasi-statically stable - compering to the former group, the number of degrees of freedom is lower.
Between the static fazes of the movement there exist fazes of lack of static stability. However, the
machine doesn’t fall due to conservation of dynamic stability for short periods of time. An example
of this kind of machine might be a biped robot with large endings of the legs (feet).
• dynamically stable - can have from a few to numerous degrees of freedom. Such machines can be
characterized by constant change in conﬁguration and the results of this change is dynamically
stable movement of the device. They keep the stability during the movement, due to completion
of conditions of dynamical balance of moments and forces. A classical example of this kind of
movement might be one or two-legged robot moving with jumping sequences.
2.4.3
Miscellaneous
• planar robot - a type of robot with movement constrained to a 2D surface,
• femur - upper part of a leg,
• tibia - lower part of a leg,
• ground projection of center of mass - projection of the center of mass of the robot on the supporting
plane. Robot is statically stable if it is acted on only by gravitational forces and his GPCoM is
located inside the support polygon,
• virtual leg - line segment between the base of robots leg and the end point of it’s foot.

Chapter 4.
Dynamics and equations of motion
In this chapter dynamical analysis of both serial and delta bipeds is discussed. The main focus is
on the equations of motion, which includes methods of obtaining the systems, variables, parameters and
constraints. Further, a veriﬁcation of the systems’ correctness is conﬁrmed using the Simulink’s library
Simmechanics.
4.1
Overview
It should be pointed out that this thesis focuses on the planar robot analysis, which in case of serial
robot means that the whole robot is constrained on the sagittal XZ plane, and in case of delta biped,
due to three dimensional nature of the delta robot itself, the trunk was constrained on the sagittal plane
and feet of the robot are at all times on the XZ plane as well.
In this chapter not only the manipulators themselves are considered, but whole biped mechanisms,
i.e. two legs and trunk in each case. There were four systems calculated in this project - a single support
system and double support system for both robots. For each of those systems a fully parametrized, easy
to use and modify Matlab function was implemented.

4.3

In order to simulate realistic walk of the robots, a proper approach for contact and impact forces
had to be taken. There are many diﬀerent ideas on how to approach that topic. In this paper a method
of switching between stationary and moving object was chosen, with exchange of the initial conditions.
First, to each supporting leg, a two degrees of freedom foot with springs and dampers was attached.

The way as the contact simulation works can be described in following steps:
• free leg is moving towards the ground,

• when free leg touches the ground constrain it to the ground (set velocities of the end of the foot to

• set initial velocities for the system with constrained leg as ﬁnal velocities of the previous system.
The approach described in this chapter has many advantages like ease in implementation and use,
adding more reality to the model and enabling of estimation of both normal and impact forces. As for
the disadvantages, the main problem is the need of change in the actual EOMs (adding passive elements)
in order for the system to work.
Moreover, the approach might be expanded, adding avoidance of
discontinuity (velocities changing from zero to large values in one instant) and presence of the sliding on
the surface.

System constraints
There are 16 constraints in the system:

Chapter 5.
Walking
This chapter focuses on the idea of walking for both serial and delta robots. First, a general idea
for walking taken in this project is discussed. Further, a walking simulator implemented in this project
is described and the control laws for both serial and delta robots used in the project are introduced.
Finally, a demonstration of the algorithm is provided for the delta robot and analysis of the walking is
performed.
5.1
Approach
In this thesis, an inspiration for the walking robot idea was taken from the segway personal trans-
porter robot. Segway is a self-balancing, wheeled robot that uses the concept of inverted pendulum
on cart for locomotion and maintaining stable position. This idea was taken and re-introduced for the
walking biped robot.
ﬁgure 5.1: Segway personal transporter inspiration
The idea for the walking segway taken for this project is fairly straight-forward and bases on a
several basic principles:
• In the stand-by position keep the center of mass close to the front, so it is easy to start the
movement at any time (ﬁgure 5.1),
• initiate the movement by tilting the trunk angle forward,
• as the center of mass is moving forward and the robot is starting to collapse, initiate symmetrical,
periodic walking pattern that maintains the trunk angle.
The main diﬀerence between the regular segway and the walking segway is turning the continuous
rolling motion into discrete walking pattern (ﬁgure 5.1) and the general complexity of the system and
control variables.
From the controller point of view (described in the section further in this thesis), the most important
variables are:

5.2
Walking simulator
In this section implementation of the simulator for walking is described. In the approach for walk-
ing taken in this project only single support phase is taken into consideration. When one of the legs is
grounded, the other is moving towards the next position, and vice versa. After the free leg reaches the
ﬁnal position and the switching condition is fulﬁlled, the two legs exchange the ﬁnal and initial states
between each other and the process is repeated.

==PAGE:40==
ﬁgure 5.3: Walking simulator schematic
For each simulation, following initial parameters can be chosen:
• trunk angle,
• length of the grounded virtual leg,
• step length.
The three values above deﬁne the whole initial conﬁguration of the robot. Additionally, initial ve-

As for the switching criterion, when the free legs foot end crosses the grounded leg foot point on the
x axis and simultaneously crosses the ground position on the z axis, the system has ﬁnished the whole
step trajectory and the switching between systems is performed.
5.3
Control algorithm
It has been proven [28] that for a ﬁve link serial robot with four control torques, that such a robot
is possible to be controlled and that the system’s dynamics are stable. It has also been shown that the
zero-dynamics of the ﬁve-link biped can be stabilized with the proportional and derivative tye controller
[29]. Bipedal robots [30] suggests a certain method of stabilizing the robot using this law, however in this
paper, in order to continue with the idea of the walking segway robot, a modiﬁed version of the algorithm
is proposed. Also, additional modiﬁcations are added in case of delta, which is a more complex system
to control.
In general, the idea for the proposed controller is to maintain the angle of the trunk and symmet-
rically change the leg positions, i.e. initial position of the grounded leg will be ﬁnal position of free leg
and vice verca. This should ensure general stability and symmetric, periodic gait.

5.4
Leg shortening compensation
In some cases, the above control laws were resulting in shortening of the subsequent steps after a

raise the free leg’s motor by a certain angle, depending on the previous step shortage magnitude. The
resulting value would be then added in the process of calculating the trajectory for the free leg.

Chapter 7.
Conclusions and future work
7.1
Summary
In this thesis, analysis and feasibility of delta type leg for a biped robot was investigated. First, a
general introduction into the topic of robotics and bipedal locomotion is provided. Further, a kinematic
overview of the delta and serial manipulators is done, including such topic like inverse kinematics, forward
kinematics and workspace analysis. In the next chapter, dynamical analysis of both robots is provided,
namely equations of motion are described, with the existing state variables, parameters and constraints.
Next, the approach for walking taken in this project is discussed and the control algorithms for the
robots are introduced. The walking simulation environment implementation is also described. In the
ﬁnal chapter, an extensive analysis of delta leg with comparison to the serial leg is provided. First, a
standing analysis is done, where performance in terms of maximum torque is checked. Further, a one
step simulation is performed, where both systems are tested in terms of maximum torque and general
energy consumption.
7.2
Delta leg
As seen in the analysis chapter, in the case of standing in stable position, delta leg seems to be
better than serial leg in terms of maximum torque (lower maximum torques at all times) in each case,
despite having lower steady-state error. Therefore it is concluded that in stand-by positions for the taken
approach for walking, where the center of mass is close to the front of the robot, delta legs are more
energy eﬃcient and require actuators with less capability than the serial leg.
When it comes to one step simulation, for the larger virtual leg lengths serial leg tends to perform
slightly better then delta leg in terms of the maximum torque used in the system. However, such a long
virtual leg lengths are usually not wanted, since it constricts robot’s ability to make bigger steps. On the
other hand, for lower virtual leg lengths, where the possibility of making bigger steps is present, delta leg
tends to perform much better than serial leg. It is therefore inferred that delta leg requires less capable
motor in order to perform stable walking.
In case of general energy consumption, where the torques during the simulations were integrated,
it can be seen that when one takes the sum of all torques in the systems, serial robot seems to be more
energy eﬃcient than the delta robot. However, it should be pointed out that the delta robot in this
project has three motors per leg and the serial robot, being a planar machine, has only two motors per
leg. When the energy consumption is distributed among the number of motors, obtain ratio turns in
favour of the delta leg, where the general consumption seems to be equal or better. This might suggest
that in full 3D motion, in which case serial robot required two additional motors, delta leg might perform
comparably or better then the serial leg, despite lower maximum torque requirements.
The biggest downside of the delta leg biped system found in this project was the computation time
needed for compilation of the programs and running the simulations.
In many instances, especially
52
==PAGE:64==
obtaining the equations of motion and gathering data for one step simulations, the delta leg system
required very eﬃcient hardware and many hours of code running.
7.3
Controller
The controller implemented for this project is considered a prototype and it’s initial function was
to be able to perform sensible analysis and comparisons between delta and serial robots. However, the
chosen approach and control laws seem to have signiﬁcant advantages that should be noticed.
Taken approach and resulting control laws are fairly simple in design and implementation but still
have good complexity-to-performance ratio. This results in fast computation time of the control signal
with the desired robot performance and is possibly ﬁt for on-line control in the real product. The step
shortening compensation part of the controller adds some additional ﬂexibility and with proper tuning,
should provide proper control signal for any delta robot’s conﬁguration and parameters. It should also
be emphasised that implemented control laws are versatile and can possibly be used in various types of
biped robots, since almost identical controllers were used in both serial and delta leg with a successful
results.
Current version of the controller has some disadvantages that should be taken under consideration.
Firstly, with the step shortening compensation term, the resulting step lengths are slightly unreliable
and might vary from step to step. Also, there is no direct velocity control of the robot at this point of
the project. The above downsides should be addressed in the future works on the controller. Some ideas
for improving the controller are presented in the next section.
7.4
Future work
Since the ﬁnal goal is to develop a real-life walking biped robot, there are several steps that should
be taken in the future in order to obtain a high quality, reliable product.
Firstly, an optimisation of the delta leg parameters should be performed with a chosen cost func-
tion, weather it is a minimal maximum torque, minimal energy consumption or any other criteria. This
optimisation should take into consideration the proportions of the base platform radius to the femur,
tibia and mobile platform lengths. Environment and code created for this project enables the user to
easily create the function for this optimisation process. For this part, it might also be sensible to use a
diﬀerent type of regulator that provides a optimal control signal.
Second step should be to enhance the controller performance and improve it’s reliability. This should
include optimal tuning of the proportional and derivative gains, and the step shortening compensation
part. Also the velocity control should be taken into consideration, in order to obtain a reliable and stable
system. Both of the above might be done with a use of speciﬁc mapping. In the ﬁrst case the value of
the step compensation could possibly be decided depending on the velocity of the robot or as in this
project, on the length of previous step or steps. In the second case, the velocity might be controller by
the knowledge of the trunk angle. The mapping mentioned above might be performed using the genetic
algorithm method or a neural network and should be investigated in order to obtain a high performance,
53
==PAGE:65==
reliable controller.
A very important step in obtaining a real-life product is expanding the control into the 3D motion.
Since at this point only planar movement was considered and the trunk was always constrained on the
sagittal plane, there is no lateral stabilisation present in the control algorithm. While the planar motion
is suﬃcient for the analysis purposes and a feasibility check, for the ﬁnal product full three dimensional
control should be implemented.
After completing the above, next step would be to prepare a realistic CAD model of the delta leg
and the whole delta robot system using the optimised parameters obtained in the part described earlier.
After designing the mechanism in environment like Solidworks or Autodesk Inventor, the model could be
exported to Simmechanics for ﬁnal tests. Especially the controller should be tested and possibly re-tuned
for this realistic model.
Final step of the process would be manufacturing of the mechanical parts and preparing the electronic
circuit for the robot. This should include preparing the energy supply, choosing the motors and the
central processing unit and introducing tilt measurement sensors, like gyroscope and accelerometer.
After assembling the full robot, real-life tests should be performed.
