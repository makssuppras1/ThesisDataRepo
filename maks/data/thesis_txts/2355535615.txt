Abstract
This master thesis aims to ﬁnd optimized movement proﬁles for a wedge-
shaped plunger-type wave generator, in order to produce stable nonlinear, deep
water waves. Both numerical and experimental methods are utilized. A fully non-
linear potential ﬂow solver developed at DTU is used for the numerical work, fol-
lowing improvements that reduce reﬂection to 1%. Numerically optimized move-
ment proﬁles are found for waves of length 0.5-2 m and steepness 3-7%, satisfying
a convergence criteria of 1% error in generated waves with respect to stream func-
tion theory solutions. Results indicate a reduced transfer function compared to that
presented by Wu [25], dependent on wave steepness. Experimental work is carried
out at DTU in Flume 1 of Building 114, measuring 20.2 m long by 0.6 m wide. Sig-
niﬁcant energy losses reported in previous work are reduced, albeit marginally, by
installing rubber seals on the wedge sides. Subsequent experimental work yields
movement proﬁles with errors of 4-7% in generated waves with respect to stream
function theory solutions. Numerically optimized movement proﬁles are scaled to
account for the known energy losses, but still yield waves with errors of 4-7%.
This matches but does not improve on work by Yang et al. [26] using a piston-
type wavemaker. Uncertainty analysis of parallel wire conductivity wave gauges
indicates a need for improved precision in measurements.

Introduction
Wave-structure interaction is a vital part in the design of marine structures, which may
be subject to steep nonlinear free surface waves. Marine structures of particular interest
are ships, offshore platforms and windmills and, more recently, wave energy convert-
ers. As these operate in deeper waters, accurate and reliable modelling of deep water
waves becomes a critical factor in understanding the wave loads such structures must
withstand. In the case of ships, one may be interested in evaluating the seakeeping and
added resistance in a given sea state; for oil platforms and offshore windmills, the de-
sign of foundations and substructures are critical. Wave energy converters are a special
subset of marine structures as they aim to extract, or absorb, energy from incoming
waves. However, theory suggests that to absorb waves efﬁciently, the device must also
be able to generate waves.
The ability to produce nonlinear deep water waves numerically and experimentally
is of interest for several reasons. Despite currently being computationally expensive,
numerical time domain methods have the potential to model a large variety of marine
structure designs in different wave conditions at relatively low cost, thereby reducing
the need for expensive model testing. This could result in better ship designs and more
efﬁcient wave energy converters. However, in order to ensure that numerical results are
reliable they must ﬁrst be validated by experiment, hence the need for producing accu-
rate nonlinear waves experimentally. In the case of wave energy converters, ﬁnding the
movement proﬁle required to produce speciﬁc waves is equal to ﬁnding the movement
proﬁle required to absorb the same waves. This work could therefore be used as part of
the design of new wave energy converters.
In numerical wave tanks it is possible to specify desired waves using high-order
Stokes, cnoidal, or stream function theories, and time step the solution. In physical
wave tanks, however, waves are generated by a moving boundary. The majority of
current experimental methods for generating nonlinear waves are based on piston or
ﬂap-type wave generators. While these have been shown to provide satisfactory results
for shallow water waves, there is still some difﬁculty in generating stable nonlinear,
deep water waves. It is thought that a plunger-type wave generator will be better suited
for this task, as its shape more closely matches the velocity proﬁle of deep water waves.
This is therefore studied both numerically and experimentally, with the aim of achieving

Recent work by Yang et al. [26], improving on the second-order wavemaker theory
presented by Zhang and Sch¨affer [27], has focused on the generation of intermediate
and deep water waves using a piston-type wavemaker. They were able to generate waves
with a root-mean-square (RMS) error of at most 9% (generally below 5%), compared
to expected (stream function theory) values.
This thesis investigates the control signals required to move a wedge-shaped plunger-
type wave generator in order to produce stable nonlinear, deep water waves. An opti-
mization algorithm is implemented in both a numerical and experimental wave ﬂume.
Here, the objective is produce waves with an error of less than 1% with respect to a
stream function theory solution [7]. The numerical work proceeds using a fully nonlin-
ear MATLAB code developed at DTU by Kontos [15]. The experimental work is divided
into three parts. First, the experimental setup is improved to reduce energy loss in the
ﬂume, as documented in recent work by Jean [12] and Hillaire [10]. Control signal op-
timization is then carried out using experimental data. Finally, numerically optimized
control signals are validated experimentally.
The thesis is structured as follows. Chapter 2 provides a background on the steady
wave problem and its extension to nonlinearity, introducing the potential ﬂow formula-
tions. Wavemaker theory for plunger-type devices is also introduced. Chapter 3 intro-
duces the numerical method, some improvements to the initial code, and validation of
same. Chapter 4 details the experimental setup and method. Chapter 5 presents the re-
sults of the numerical and experimental work. Chapter 6 provides an overall summary,
with discussion and conclusions.

2.2
The steady wave problem
Analytic solutions corresponding to a single periodic wave train propagating with con-
stant form can only be found by applying several assumptions and approximations to
the general case of water wave motion. These assumptions are as follows [8]:
• The bottom of the domain is impermeable and ﬂat
• The waves are collinear and of inﬁnite length transverse to the direction of prop-
agation, i.e. the ﬂow is two dimensional
• The ﬂuid is incompressible
• The boundary layer is small, such that inviscid ﬂow theory can be used
• The ﬂuid is irrotational
With these assumptions in place, the ﬁrst approximation that must be made is that of
the wave speed, or celerity. To do this, two frames of reference are employed: one
stationary, and one moving with the waves. Figure 2.2 shows the frames of reference
and various parameters that characterize a wave.
The two frames of reference in Figure 2.2 are used to describe the wave speed c
relative to a ﬁnite current. The (x,y) frame is stationary, while the (X,Y) frame moves
with the waves at velocity c, such that x = X +ct, and y = Y. The horizontal velocities
are therefore given as U(X,y) = u(x,y,t)−c and u(x,y,t), respectively. The free surface
elevation is deﬁned as η(X). In the (X,Y) frame all ﬂuid motion is steady, and consists
of a ﬂow in the negative X direction, roughly of the magnitude of the wave speed,
underneath the stationary wave proﬁle. The mean horizontal ﬂuid velocity in this frame,
for a constant value of Y over one wavelength L, is denoted by −¯U. In the stationary

Numerical Methods
This chapter introduces the numerical method, brieﬂy outlining relevant aspects of the
fully nonlinear MATLAB code developed by Kontos and used in this thesis [15]. The im-
provements that have been made are described, and are validated by comparison to the
original code. Finally, the practical aspects of the optimization procedure are outlined.
The essential idea with the numerical simulations is as follows:
1. Choose desired wave, specifying wavelength, steepness, and number of stream
function modes
2. Calculate stream function theory wave amplitudes
3. Use Wu transfer function to generate initial guess of stroke amplitudes
4. Run simulation and compare results to desired amplitudes
5. Update guess and repeat until convergence criteria is met
3.1
OceanWave3D
The code, hereafter referred to as OceanWave3D (OW3D) is based on a ﬂexible-order,
ﬁnite difference based, fully nonlinear potential ﬂow solver that has been developed at
DTU [2, 6], itself based on work by Li and Fleming [16]. The current version has been
modiﬁed to allow for nonlinear wave-structure interactions in a ﬁxed or moving frame of
reference, using a novel immersed boundary technique [15]. OW3D solves the Laplace
equation within the physical domain, subject to boundary conditions at the sides and
body surface. The fully nonlinear free surface boundary conditions are approximated
by a high-order Weighted Essentially Non-Oscillatory (WENO) scheme [22], and are
time-stepped by a standard fourth-order, four-stage Runge-Kutta method. The WENO
scheme is essential in modelling nonlinear wave-structure interactions, as it provides a
means for dealing with discontinuities (i.e. structures) in the numerical scheme.

3.1.3
The internal free surface
As mentioned in Section 3.1.2, the transformation from the physical domain to a time-
invariant computational domain requires that the free surface is smooth and continuous.
This condition is fulﬁlled by creating an artiﬁcal free surface in the interior of the wedge
that is C2 continuous at the intersection with the exterior free surface. There are several
steps in this process: ﬁrst, the intersection points between the body and free surface are
found using the bisection iteration method between the ﬁrst interior and exterior free
surface points. Values for η, ηx, and ηxx are then extrapolated on each intersection
point, serving as boundary conditions for the creation of the internal free surface. The
essential algorithm for this procedure, executed at each Runge-Kutta stage, is as follows
[15]:
1. Compute the position and velocity of the body
2. Construct the new interior free surface
3. If a new exterior free surface point is added, its η and ˜φ values are extrapolated
4. The Laplace problem is solved using the IBM-WLS method to satisfy the body
boundary conditions
5. The FSBCs are stepped forward in time using WENO for the spatial discretization
This procedure proves to be the critical part of the code, limiting its use for short, steep
waves. Attempts to improve upon this will be discussed later.

3.2.1
Improving speed
In an attempt to reduce the computational time, the Generalized Minimal Residual (GM-
RES) algorithm is implemented with a preconditioning matrix. GMRES is an iterative
method developed by Saad and Schultz [19], and is available as a built-in function in
MATLAB. Combining this with a preconditioning matrix can speed up convergence, as
shown in recent work by Bingham and Zhang [2]. The condition number of a function
indicates how much an output value changes for a small change in the input argument;
the aim of preconditioning is to reduce the condition number of a matrix. In this case,
lower-upper factorisation is used to express the second-order, linear equivalent of the
sparse system matrix A (from Equation (3.7)) as a product of a lower and upper trian-
gular matrix; namely M = LU. This system can then be solved by
(LU)−1Aφ = (LU)−1b.
(3.20)
As the computational cost grows with each iteration of GMRES the method is
restarted after 20 iterations, with the most recent solution as the initial guess for the
next set of iterations. Unfortunately this implementation does not have the desired ef-
fect; the convergence of the method is very slow, as seen in Figure 3.4, so the GMRES
solver is no faster than solving directly by back-substitution.
3.2.2
Reducing reﬂection
The domain is set up such that the generated waves are convected out of the domain at
the right boundary; this is done by imposing Neumann conditions on the free surface
elevation and velocity potential, using one ghost point to build the derivatives. As there
is no forward speed (i.e. the wedge moves vertically), a homogeneous Neumann condi-
tion is imposed on the free surface elevation and an inhomogeneous Neumann condition
is imposed on the velocity potential. For this latter, the x-derivative of the velocity po-
tential (i.e. the u-velocity) is set equal to the velocity of the outgoing wave, which is
updated every half and whole time step by extrapolation from the previous time step.

3.3
Validation of OW3D
3.2.3
Increasing the steepness limit
In its current state OW3D is limited in the waves it can produce, both in terms of steep-
ness and period; it becomes unstable for short, steep waves. Investigation of the error
suggests that the problem arises at the intersection of the wedge and free surface, pri-
marily between the third and fourth Runge-Kutta steps. Two possible sources of error
are considered: that the calculation of the intersection points leads to problems with
the subsequent deﬁnition of internal, external, and ghost points close to the wedge sur-
face; and that the ﬁtting of the internal free surface breaks down due to extremely high
gradients.
The internal free surface is currently ﬁtted with a 7th-order polynomial, in order
to ensure C2-continuity with the external free surface. Errors are observed in this pro-
cess when the steepness is increased beyond 6%; the ﬁtted free surface extends out of
the wedge, leading to errors in ﬁnding a subsequent intersection point. An alternative
method is considered in an attempt to remedy this situation. The derivatives of the free
surface are calculated at the ﬁrst internal point using a one-sided stencil. The rest of
the internal free surface points are then forced to take the value of the zeroth derivative,
i.e. forcing the internal free surface to be a ﬂat line. This implementation allows the
steepness to be increased to 8%. Due to time constraints further improvements are not
attempted. Instead the current limits are investigated both in terms of wave period and
steepness.

3.3.4

Initially the MATLAB nonlinear optimization function fmincon is used to generate new
guesses based on the relative least-squares error. The default fmincon approach is an

lower and upper bound for subsequent guesses. The initial guess consists of the desired
stream function amplitudes modiﬁed by Wu’s transfer function. The lower and upper
bounds are 10% and 200% of the initial guess, respectively. Unfortunately fmincon
shows very slow convergence; Figure 3.9 illustrates this for a 1 m wave of 6% steepness.

4.3
Semi-analogue optimization
Due to the slow speed of the numerical simulations, the ﬂume is used as part of a “semi-
analogue” optimization loop, where measured data is analyzed and used to calculate a
relative least-squares error. The procedure mirrors that of the numerical optimization,
and is as follows:
1. Choose desired wave, specifying wavelength, steepness, and number of stream
function modes
2. Generate initial guess for movement proﬁle amplitudes based on experimental
transfer function
3. Download movement proﬁle to LinMotTalk and run wedge
4. Gather data from wave gauges
5. Extract sample and perform harmonic analysis
6. Calculate error with respect to desired stream function amplitudes
7. Generate new guess and repeat until convergence
Initially fmincon is used to generate new guesses; this is subsequently changed to the
defect correct scheme presented in Section 3.3.4.

Discussion and conclusion
6.1
Numerical work
The OW3D code received from Kontos [15] has been improved by the addition of an
exponential sponge layer that gradually relaxes the propagating nonlinear waves to a
linear replacement, resulting in approximately 1% reﬂection when running simulations
for many periods. This has allowed a defect correction optimization scheme to be ap-
plied to waves of various lengths and steepnesses, with a convergence criteria of 1%
error relative to a stream function theory solution. Optimized harmonic stroke ampli-
tudes meeting this criteria have been achieved for waves of length 0.5-2m and steepness
3-7%. Comparing the initial guesses to their optimized counterparts indicates that the
higher harmonic terms are increased by up to 300%, resulting in movement proﬁles that
have sharper peaks and lower, broader troughs. New transfer functions based on the op-
timized fundamental amplitudes show a departure from that proposed by Wu [25]; for
a given wave number, the numerical transfer functions decrease with increasing steep-
ness. One reason for this could be that Wu’s work does not take higher harmonics into
account in the free surface elevation and velocity potential.
Despite the improvements made in terms of reducing reﬂection, there is still much
work to be done with more fundamental aspects of the numerical solver. As described
in Kontos [15], the current limiting factor is the construction of the internal free surface.
This limits the steepness to approximately 7% for shorter waves, beyond which steep
gradients at the free surface-wedge intersection lead to the simulation breaking down.
Initial attempts to ﬁx this with a new internal free surface method were not entirely
effective (i.e. the steepness limit was increased to 8% for some cases). This should
therefore be the focus of further work. Increasing the speed of the simulations is also
desirable; other iterative methods could be investigated to achieve this.
6.2
Experimental work
Replacing the original porous foam layer at the sides of the wedge with rubber seals was
shown to reduce the energy loss reported in earlier work [12], and improve the smooth-
ness of the wedge movement. However, an analysis of several repeated experiments
55
==PAGE:66==
Chapter 6
Discussion and conclusion
indicates a signiﬁcant degree of uncertainty in the measurements, and highlights the
need for high precision in both the laser sensor and wave gauges. The updated transfer
function still falls signiﬁcantly short of the expected values, indicating the continuing
presence of energy loss in the ﬂume. This may be due to the generation of vortical struc-
tures around the wedge sides and bottom/back. While the generation of vortices around
the wedge sides was not noticeable during the experiments, it is possible that adding dye
to the water could reveal the presence of vortical structures during the wedge movement.
The semi-analogue optimization failed to produce waves of under 4% error with
respect to the desired stream function solution. This may be due to measurement uncer-
tainties giving inconsistent data to the defect correction scheme. Thorough testing of the
wave gauges is therefore recommended, in order to establish such things as resolution,
possible hysteresis effects associated with wave number, and the effect of the meniscus.
The linearity of the gauges has been conﬁrmed by Jean [12], so measurement nonlin-
earity is not signiﬁcantly inﬂuencing the results. Analysis of the KC number indicated
that inertia effects may also play a role for waves of length L < 0.75m, hence a redesign
of the wave gauges with probes of smaller diameter would also be desirable. Another
possibility is to implement a new means of measuring the free surface elevation, for
example a servo-type or mechanical wave gauge, or optical measurements using a small
ﬂoat.
The 2-norm errors associated with the numerical optimized movement proﬁles (<
1%) were not validated experimentally. In general the experimental errors were ap-
proximately 2-7%. While this may indicate problems with the numerical method, the
experimental limitations outlined above are clearly signiﬁcant. Furthermore, the move-
ment proﬁles were scaled to make up for energy losses in the tank. The increased initial
energy may have an effect on the shape of the waves in the far ﬁeld.
Finally, the RMS error between the attained and desired surface elevation was com-
pared to results presented in Yang et al. [26]. By implementing a second-order dis-
persion correction Yang was able to produce deep water waves with an RMS error of
approximately 2-5% (rising to 5-10% for a wave with period T = 0.6s), using a piston-
type wavemaker. This thesis sought to improve on these results by using a plunger-type
wavemaker. However, the results presented in Table 5.5 show RMS errors in the range
3-5%, dropping to 2-4% for T = 0.6 s. Clearly the plunger-type wavemaker is not
performing as well as hoped but, as this is merely the ﬁrst attempt, compared to the
extensive revisions and improvements that have been made to piston-type wavemaker
theory, improved results may prove possible. With regards to this, the following recom-
mendations are made for improving the experimental setup:
• Implement a new guide structure for the wedge, ensuring vertical and horizontal
alignment with the ﬂume and a reduced clearance between the wedge and back
plate.

• Realign the ﬂume walls to ensure a consistent seal across the full wedge stroke.
• Redesign or replace the wave gauges, as outlined above.
• Investigate different plunger shapes, such as those presented in Arcari [1].

Setting up the wave gauges
The ﬂume should be ﬁlled to the desired water level before carrying out these instruc-
tions.
1. Set up the wave gauges in the desired locations, and put each wave gauge into its
“mean” position, i.e. halfway between the stops. The distance between the stops
on the wave gauges should be a known quantity, as mentioned above.
2. Attach each gauge to a “probe” port on the mermaid box
3. For each attached wave gauge, run a cable from the “outlet” port of the mermaid
box to a free port on the NI USB-6218 box
4. Attach the NI USB-6218 box to the PC
5. Open the Data Acquisition 2016 software
6. Select the NI USB-6218 (Dev-4) box (making sure to select “Differential” mea-
surements) and the channels in use
7. Zero each wave gauge using the “Zero adjust” screws on the mermaid box
8. Lower each wave gauge to the bottom (most submerged) position
9. Adjust the gain of each wave gauge using the “Gain adjust” screws on the mer-
maid box until saturation is just achieved, then come back one turn
10. Check the range of the wave gauges by raising them to their top position and
conﬁrming there is no saturation.
