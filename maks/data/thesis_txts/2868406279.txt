Abstract
This thesis focuses on predicting river cross section properties trough a machine learning
framework, with the intention of supporting hydraulic modelling. A simplified representa­
tion of cross­section geometry is derived from measured profiles using a power­law curve,
characterized by three key parameters: bankfull width (wfull), bankfull depth (hfull), and
shape factor (s). An additional parameter, ∆H, representing the vertical offset relative to
surrounding terrain, is included to position the cross section within the terrain.
The cross­section parameters are estimated through an optimization process that mini­
mizes the error between modeled and observed cross sections, ensuring both shape and
hydraulic characteristics (e.g., flow area and hydraulic radius) are preserved. Notably,
the simulation of water depths using these estimated cross­sections achieved an RMSE
of 0.253 meters and an average relative error of 12.63%, indicating a moderate level of
accuracy in hydraulic predictions.
To predict these cross­section parameters, a neural network­based model integrates geospa­
tial features such as Digital Terrain Model (DTM) statistics, sinuosity index, catchment
area, and sediment type. A two­step model architecture is proposed:
• An autoencoder is pre­trained to compress high­dimensional geospatial inputs into
a lower­dimensional latent space.
• A Feedforward Neural Network (FFNN) maps the compressed features to the target
parameters.
The feature sensitivity analysis highlights the importance of sinuosity and bankfull width
as the most influential predictors. The residual analysis comparing model predictions to
key features, reveals systematic errors for lower sinuosity index, indicating channelized
rivers, and higher residuals in anthropogenic areas when comparing to land use.
Results demonstrate that the model effectively predicts shape (s) with a test R2 of 0.91,
and width (wfull) with a test R2 of 0.71. However, depth (hfull) and vertical offset (∆h)
remain challenging to predict accurately with R2 of 0.34 and 0.69 respectively, despite
observed correlations with DTM­derived terrain features.
While the proposed approach enables large­scale estimation of river cross­sections where
no field measurements exist, current limitations in predicting depth and ∆h introduce un­
certainties for hydraulic modeling applications.

Terminology
Measured CRS (Cross Section) refers to river cross sections obtained from field mea­
surements or survey data. These cross sections represent the actual bathymetry
and hydraulic conveying properties of the river at specific locations.
Estimated CRS (Cross Section) refers to simplified or parameterized cross sections de­
rived from measured data. These are fitted using statistical models, to standardize
representation while retaining key hydraulic characteristics.
Predicted CRS (Cross section) refers to cross sections generated through machine learn­
ing models based on geospatial and terrain features. These predictions aim to ap­
proximate the parameters and hydraulic properties of river cross sections.

Introduction
Flood risks are increasing due to climate change, emphasizing the urgent need for effec­
tive flood warning systems to mitigate impacts on communities, economies, and ecosys­
tems. These systems function by integrating real­time meteorological data with hydro­
logical and hydraulic models to predict flood events, providing early warnings that help
authorities and residents take precautionary measures. Models such as LISFLOOD and
the European Flood Awareness System (EFAS) play a critical role in enabling timely and
accurate flood forecasting, which is essential for informing management strategies (Wet­
terhall et al. 2018).
Hydraulic modeling of rivers is essential for understanding river dynamics and develop­
ing accurate flood forecasting models. These models simulate water flow, surface levels,
and discharge rates, providing critical insights for flood prediction and water resource
management. However, the reliability of hydraulic models depends heavily on the qual­
ity and availability of input data, particularly geometric and bathymetric measurements of
river cross­sections Dey et al. 2019. Bathymetric data is especially challenging to obtain
because it requires in­situ measurements, which can be costly, time­consuming, and dif­
ficult to conduct in remote or inaccessible river sections. At national or global scales, the
lack of detailed cross­sectional data presents significant challenges, especially for smaller
streams (Townsend et al. 2004). Collecting this data is often challenging due to several
factors:
• Accessibility: Some river sections are difficult to access due to remote locations
or hazardous conditions.
• Resource Constraints: Field surveys are time­consuming and require specialized
equipment and trained personnel, which can be costly.
• Temporal Variability: River morphology can change over time due to sediment
transport and erosion, necessitating frequent updates to cross­sectional data.
For example, in Denmark, with approximately 64,000 km of rivers and streams, less than
0.5 % of the river network has been measured and collected by The Agency of Climate
Data, representing just 294.95 km. The article by Dey et al. 2019 estimates that the cost
of measuring cross­sections with a detailed bathymetric survey is approximately €300 /
2250 DKK per hectare or $35,000 / 238000 DKK per km. Given Denmark’s extensive
river network, this translates into a total cost exceeding $2.24 billion USD or 15.23 billion
DKK, for comprehensive measurement, rendering such efforts economically infeasible.
This data gap underscores the need for alternative methods to estimate cross­sectional
parameters effectively. Given the high costs and logistical constraints of direct measure­
ments of river cross sections, researchers have explored alternatives such as modeling
cross sections with 1) stretch specific approaches and 2) spatial variation approaches.
Both of these rely on standardized cross section shapes.
The study by (Dey et al. 2019) evaluates different bathymetric modeling approaches, from
simplified geometric cross­sections to interpolation­based methods, and their impact on
hydraulic simulations.
They standardize river cross­sections in three different setups
being a simplified triangular channel shape, a functional bathymetric surface based on
four parameters, and interpolation between sparsely distributed surveyed cross sections.

While simplified geometric shapes are sufficient for low­sinuosity rivers, more complex
reaches require detailed field surveys to capture local variability in hydraulic simulations.
Similarly, (Fleischmann et al. 2019) analyze cross­section generalization across regional
and continental hydrodynamic models, using bankfull width, bankfull depth, and rectan­
gular approximations to standardize cross­sections. Their results indicate that locally sur­
veyed cross­sections significantly reduce RMSE in water level predictions, reinforcing the
limitations of large­scale empirical estimations.
The Danish national model, DK Modellen (Stisen et al. 2019), estimates river widths and
depths using empirically derived power­law relationships, and assumes square cross sec­
tion shapes. Power­law in this context is relating to the modelling of spatial variation of
river cross sections, being approach 2.
W = a · Ab,
D = c · Ad
(1.1)
where W and D represent bankfull width and depth, A is the catchment area, and a, b, c, d
are empirically calibrated coefficients that vary by geographical region. The model divides
Denmark into seven hydrological subregions with distinct parameter sets, reflecting re­
gional differences in geology and hydrology. For rivers without measured cross­sections,
the model applies typetværsnit (standardized cross­sections) based on regional empirical
scaling laws. While this method provides a structured framework for large­scale model­
ing in data­scarce regions, Dey et al. 2019 note that such simplified, power­law­based
approaches may fail to capture the full spatial variability and geomorphic complexity of
smaller streams.
Additionally, (Neal et al. 2015) suggest a scalable geometric representation of river cross
section geometry using a power function that relates the flow of width for a given depth
of flow to the bankfull width and bankfull depth using the shape parameter s. This imple­
mentation was shown to estimate the wetted perimeter within 2% of channels tested.
To improve bathymetric estimates, (Musaeus et al. 2024) introduced a methodology using
ICESat­2 altimetry data to infer submerged cross­sections by calibrating a steady­state
hydraulic model. Their approach integrates observed water surface elevation and slope
to estimate cross­section depths, demonstrating promising accuracy for large rivers but
struggling with smaller streams due to limited spatial resolution and parameter estimation
challenges. Further advancements in cross­section parameterization come from remote
sensing techniques. (Bauer­Gottwein et al. 2024) and (Zhou et al. 2024) leveraged satel­
lite altimetry and drone­based Doppler radar, respectively, to infer hydraulic properties.
These methods primarily estimate water surface elevation and velocity to indirectly derive
cross­sectional dimensions, reducing reliance on in­situ measurements. Which means
that we would first need to perform e.g. a drone survey before we can derive cross sec­
tions. Even though these approaches are more efficient than existing manual methods, it
is still a lot of work if you want to do it for an entire country. Also, both of these methods
remain constrained by the availability of consistent water level observations, particularly
in smaller, ungauged streams.
Collectively, these studies illustrate that current methods for standardizing river cross­
sections whether empirical, geometric, interpolated or estimated and derived from water
level observations, still fall short of providing a scalable solution for large­scale hydraulic
modeling. This underscores the need for more detailed and spatially adaptive standard­
ization approaches that can both capture river variability, accurate bottom elevation and
remain scalable.

1.1
Machine Learning Approaches in River Cross Section
Predictions
Recent advancements in machine learning (ML) may enable improved river cross­section
predictions beyond traditional power­law approaches. (Chang et al. 2023) introduced
a multi­modal ML framework that significantly outperforms empirical power­law relation­
ships in predicting river width and depth (defined as the distance from bankfull water sur­
face to the channel bed) across the United States. Their model incorporated 17 predictors,
including catchment area, discharge, stream order, slope, elevation, and land use, with
discharge emerging as the dominant predictor, explaining nearly 70% of the variance in
width and depth. This marks a substantial improvement over conventional power­law
models, which typically rely on a single predictor, catchment area. In contrast, (Chang
et al. 2023) ML approach reached R2 values of 0.75 for width and 0.67 for depth. The
bottom elevation is implicitly inferred based on flow characteristics but is not directly pre­
dicted. This is a crucial limitation since accurate bed elevation placement is necessary for
positioning standardized cross­sections within a digital terrain model (DTM), especially in
ungauged streams where no water level measurements are available.
Despite the improvements ML methods bring to river cross­section predictions, a critical
gap remains: Current studies examine larger catchment sizes, which might not be ap­
plicable to small, lowland streams relevant to Denmark. Furthermore, there are little to
no studies validating these models against measured cross­section profiles rather than
just hydraulic model performance metrics such as water level RMSE. And limited studies
investigate a scalable solution to modeling bed elevation. This is particularly relevant for
smaller, ungauged streams where direct measurements of water level and bed elevation
are scarce.
1.2
Objectives and Research Questions
In summary, standardized cross section profiles are commonly used for hydraulic mod­
elling on large scales. Their parameters are either derived from remote sensing data,
which is a catchment­specific approach that requires both data collection and fine­tuning,
or by developing models for investigating their variation in space. The existing models are

In Denmark, the modelling of river cross sections is challenging, because the streams are
relatively small. On the other hand, terrain information are available in high resolution on
a national scale and frequently updated. In addition, The Danish Agency of Climate Data

small­stream cross sections in Denmark

Methodology
The objective of this thesis is to model and predict the spatial variation of river cross­
section properties. To achieve this, we begin by characterizing cross­section shapes using
power relationships, proceed to develop predictive models for the cross section geometry
and bed elevation, and finally evaluate the impact of prediction uncertainties on residual
analysis and hydaulic impacts.
The methodology outlined in this chapter presents a structured approach to meeting these
objectives, as illustrated in Figure 3.1. The process follows a stepwise workflow:
Figure 3.1: Methodology overview illustrating the steps involved in modeling and predict­
ing river cross­section properties and sections.
1. Standardization of Cross­Section Properties: To capture spatial variation, we de­
fine a standardized set of parameters: width, depth, shape, and vertical offset, that
can effectively describe the full flow capacity of smaller streams. These parameters
are derived by fitting power­law curves to measured cross sections using only these
four key values. We then evaluate how well these simplified estimations describe
the measured cross sections.
2. Derivation and Engineering of Predictor Variables: Geospatial and hydrological
features relevant to cross­section variability are derived and engineered from high­
resolution and open datasets described in section 2.2.
3. Development of Predictive Models: Machine learning models are developed and
trained to predict cross section geometry and bed elevation based on the engineered
features.
4. Performance Assessment: The impact of standardization and prediction uncer­
tainties is evaluated through:
• Quantitative accuracy assessments of hydraulic properties (e.g., hydraulic ra­
dius and flow area).
• Analysis of how these uncertainties influence the simulated water levels in hy­
draulic models using the steady­state solver from (Kittel et al. 2021).
This workflow ensures a comprehensive approach to characterizing, predicting, and as­
sessing cross­section properties, while addressing the challenges and implications of pre­
diction variability.

3.1
Standardized Characterization of Cross Section
Properties
A simplified cross section is estimated from measured cross sections to streamline a set
of properties that describe the conveyance properties of a river cross section for hydraulic
modeling. The approach is based on the method proposed by (Neal et al. 2015) which
describes river cross sections using a power­law curve. This standardized shape is char­
acterized by the properties:
• s: Shape factor, describing the curvature of the cross section.
• wfull: Bankfull width of the river cross section.
• hfull: Bankfull depth, being the maximum depth of the river cross section.
In addition, ∆h has been incorporated because it is essential for determining the location
within terrain data.
• ∆h: Vertical offset of the predicted cross section relative to the reference terrain
(Z­kote).
These parameters are fitted to the measured cross sections and are considered the
ground truth for training the predictive model. By defining standardized cross­section
properties, they provide a reliable reference against which model predictions can be eval­
uated. Together, these properties enable precise placement and representation of a cross
section within its surrounding terrain. The reference depth, ∆h, plays a crucial role in defin­
ing the vertical offset between the flow area and the terrain elevation, ensuring that the
cross­section geometry aligns correctly with the landscape. The placement of the cross
section using ∆h is illustrated in Figure 3.3.

3.1.3
Estimations of Cross Section Properties from Measured Cross
Sections
As part of standardizing the cross­section properties, the next step involves fitting these
properties to a large dataset of measured cross­sections.
The goal of this step is to estimate the parameters hfull, wfull, s, which describe the ge­
ometry of the cross section. While the ∆h is derived directly from the estimated cross
section. These parameters are determined for all ≈4750 observed cross sections using
a numerical optimization procedure. The process aims to derive simplified yet accurate
representations of cross­section geometries for hydraulic modeling purposes and serve

as a simplified approximation of cross section varitions for the machine learning setup.
To estimate the parameters an optimization process is employed that minimizes the error
between the estimated cross section and the measured/ observed cross section.
The parameters are estimated by minimizing an objective function that combines two cri­
teria:
1. Shape Fitting: Minimize the mean squared error (MSE) between the observed and
modeled elevation points along the cross section.
2. Area Fitting: Minimize the difference between the observed and modeled water­filled
areas to ensure the flow conveyance is accurately represented.
The total objective function is expressed as:
Total Error(θ) = MSEz(θ) + λ · MSEA(θ)
(3.2)
Here:
• MSEz(θ): Measures the squared differences between observed and modeled ele­
vation points, ensuring the predicted cross­section profile aligns with the true bed
elevation.
• MSEA(θ): Quantifies the discrepancy in water­filled area between the observed and
modeled cross section.
• λ: A weighting factor that balances the importance of the two criteria. The chosen
value is λ = 0.1.
Since the area error (MSEA) is naturally larger in scale than the elevation error (MSEz), as­
signing equal weight to both would cause the optimization to disproportionately minimize
the area difference while sacrificing bed elevation accuracy. To emphasize the correct
placement of the bed elevation profile, a lower weighting factor of λ = 0.1 is chosen. This
ensures that elevation accuracy remains the dominant optimization objective while still
accounting for area consistency.
Fitting solely to MSE or area error independently does not yield satisfactory results as:
• MSEz alone: May produce shapes that fit elevation points well but do not accurately
represent the flow area.
• MSEA alone: May yield unrealistic shapes that distort the observed cross­section
geometry.
The weighting factor λ = 0.1 was determined through trial and error. Different values
were tested, and the resulting cross sections were visually and quantitatively assessed to
ensure a good balance between elevation accuracy and area representation.
The optimization is performed using the L­BFGS­B algorithm, which allows for bound
constraints on the parameters. These constraints ensure physical plausibility, such as
hfull, wfull > 0 and s > 1.4. To achieve this, we first identify hfull (bankfull depth) and
wfull (bankfull width) from the observed data. These values are based on the vertical and
horizontal extents of the observed cross section relative to the surrounding terrain. Once
these initial parameters are defined, we optimize them, along with the shape factor s, by
fitting a power­law curve to the observed cross sections. The optimization minimizes the

and the error in the water­filled area.

3.3.2
Feature Sensitivity Analysis
To assess the impact of different feature groups on model performance, a feature sensi­
tivity analysis was conducted by systematically excluding predefined groups and mea­
suring the change in predictive accuracy. The feature groups were structured based on
their statistical and physical relevance to cross­section properties:
• DTM min, max, mean: Represents overall terrain elevation properties.
• DTM range, std: Captures the spread of elevation values, differentiating uniform
and varied terrain.
• DTM 25, 75 percentiles: Provides a non­parametric measure of terrain distribu­
tion.
• DTM left, right slope: Describes bank slopes of nearby terrain.
• Kurtosis, Skewness: Characterizes the shape of the elevation distribution be­
yond mean and variance.
• Sinuosity, Bankfull Width: Describes channel morphology, influencing cross­
section structure.
• Catchment Area: Accounts for upstream drainage influences on channel form.
• Sediment: Represents sediment properties affecting hydraulic conditions.
Each group was removed individually while keeping all other features unchanged, en­

3.4
Predicting Cross Section Properties using Neural
Networks
Building upon the statistical estimation of cross­section properties, this section explores
the use of neural networks to predict these properties directly from geospatial features.
By leveraging the relationships between terrain characteristics and cross­section param­
eters, the machine learning approach aims to provide an efficient and scalable method
for estimating key parameters: bankfull width (wfull), bankfull depth (hfull), the shape
factor (s), and the vertical offset (∆h).
As shown in the schematic (Figure 3.7), the model integrates geospatial features as
inputs, such as the Digital Terrain Model (DTM) and other relevant terrain descriptors,
and generates predictions for cross­section parameters. The midpoints of the cross sec­
tions, used for extracting these geospatial features, are defined based on the national
stream centerline dataset, ensuring spatial alignment.
At the core of the framework is the cross­section model, a neural network trained to
predict the synthetic cross­section properties. The model outputs the four properties as
derived for each cross section, as described in section 3.
3.4.1
Model setup
The model architecture as illustrated in Figure 3.7 combines an autoencoder for dimen­
sionality reduction and a feed forward neural network (FFNN) for predicting the cross
section parameters. In this project, the autoencoder is employed as a pre­processing
step to capture key patterns in the cross­sectional predictor variables. By reducing the
dimensionality of the input features, the autoencoder provides a meaningful latent repre­
sentation in the form of a ”latent space.” This representation is then fed into a predictive
Feedforward Neural Network (FFNN), which utilizes the compressed features to pre­
dict key cross­sectional parameters. This setup enhances the model’s ability to handle
high­dimensional data efficiently, reducing redundancy while preserving the most rele­
vant information for predictive tasks. We have opted for this framework, because the
geospatial input features are highly correlated. A comparison of predictive performance
of our setup against a simple neural network without dimensionality reduction is included
in Appendix A.3.
The predictive cross section model architecture consists of two main components:
• Autoencoder: The autoencoder is an unsupervised neural network trained to
compress the input geospatial features into a lower­dimensional latent space. It
consists of three main parts: an encoder, a bottleneck layer, and a decoder. The
input layer contains 18 neurons, corresponding to the dataset’s features. The
encoder progressively reduces dimensionality through one hidden layer with 32
neurons, followed by a bottleneck layer of 16 neurons, which captures the most
important patterns in the data. The decoder mirrors the encoder, reconstructing
the input through one hidden layer with 32 neurons and an output layer of 18 neu­
rons. This architecture enables the model to efficiently compress and reconstruct
26
Predicting Standardized River Cross Sections in Denmark Using Machine Learning
==PAGE:37==
the data by minimizing reconstruction loss. The autoencoder is pre­trained sepa­
rately, and its weights are frozen, meaning they are not updated when training the
FFNN.
• Feedforward Neural Network (FFNN): The FFNN takes the latent representa­
tions generated by the pre­trained encoder (from the autoencoder) as its input.
The autoencoder’s bottleneck layer outputs a 16­dimensional latent representa­
tion, which is passed to the FFNN for prediction. The FFNN consists of a fully con­
nected hidden layer with 32 neurons and ReLU activation, followed by a dropout
layer (rate = 0.3) to reduce overfitting. The output layer consists of four neurons,
corresponding to the target cross­section parameters: bankfull width (wfull), bank­
full depth (hfull), the shape factor (s), and ∆h. To ensure non­negative outputs
for physical constraints, the first three outputs are transformed using the SoftPlus
activation function. The FFNN is trained in a supervised manner, minimizing the
prediction error for these parameters in a multi­output setup.
This combined model integrates the autoencoder with a fully connected FFNN for pre­
dictions, as illustrated in Figure 3.7.

harder for the FFNN to capture meaningful spatial patterns.

5
Discussion
5.1
Summary of Key Findings
This study investigated river cross­section properties through a three­part analysis: first,
by examining whether cross sections could be effectively described using a standard­
ized power­law­based shape definition; second, by investigating their variation in space;
and third, by testing the predictive modeling of these parameters to assess the influence
of different topographical variables on river cross sections. Key findings are as follows:
• Standardization of cross­section properties: Standardizing cross­sections us­
ing power­law fitting provided a simplified yet effective parameterization of river
bathymetry. This approach incorporated a vertical offset to establish a reference
elevation depth, achieving a generally reliable approximation across most cross­
sections. However, slight deviations were observed, particularly for hydraulic ra­
dius and flow area. The RMSE for the hydraulic radius was found to be 0.1359
m with a relative error of 14.23%, indicating moderate discrepancies that could
impact hydraulic calculations. Similarly, the flow area had an RMSE of 0.8168 m2
and a relative error of 10.39%. Residual analysis of the hydraulic radius revealed
a consistent pattern of underfitting; natural riverbed variations led to smaller wet­
ted perimeters in measured cross­sections, while the estimated cross­sections,
following a smoother, more uniform shape, tended to overestimate these dimen­
sions. Consequently, this led to a systematic overestimation of water depths in
hydraulic simulations using the estimated cross­sections. The water depth simu­
lations, as shown in the RMSE of 0.253m and an average relative error of 12.63%,
illustrate that the estimated cross sections struggled to accurately replicate the ac­
tual water surface elevations, as shown in the results section and highlighted in
Figures 4.2a and 4.5.
• Model complexity: A trade­off exists between model complexity and predictive
accuracy.
While the standardized parameterization approach and multi­output
modeling provides structure, certain parameters, especially vertical positioning
(∆h) and depth (hfull), remain difficult to generalize, particularly in leave­one­area­
out (LOAO) experiments as demonstrated in Table 4.2 and Figure 4.21 and 4.20.
Furthermore, feature comparisons revealed distinct spatial patterns in cross­section
properties. ∆h exhibited strong linear correlations with DTM range and standard
deviation, suggesting that a dedicated model focusing on these relationships could
improve prediction accuracy. Bankfull width extracted from the DTM showed high
correlation with measured width, indicating that this parameter might be directly
derived from geospatial data rather than predicted. In contrast, shape (s) demon­
strated the highest predictive ability but lacked a clear linear correlation with in­
dividual features, implying that its predictability is more dependent on the overall

feature set. Predictive modeling results showed that shape (s) and width (wfull)
were the most accurately predicted parameters.
• Spatial variability in prediction errors: Residual analysis showed that land­use

were more pronounced in anthropogenic and urbanized areas. Furthermore resid­
ual analysis highlighted higher variance in regions with low sinuosity (Figure 4.16),

• Key features influencing prediction accuracy: Features such as Sinuosity and

river systems.
5.2
Limitations
While this study demonstrates the feasibility of predictive cross­section modeling, sev­
eral factors limit the generalization of its conclusions.
A primary limitation is the dataset size. The dataset covers only 0.5% of Danish streams
and rivers, limiting its representativeness for nationwide generalization. Additionally,
the dataset is skewed toward small and medium­sized streams, meaning that large
rivers, which may exhibit different morphological and hydraulic behaviors, are under­
represented. Expanding the dataset with additional field measurements and including
a wider variety of stream types would improve model robustness.
Furthermore, the study does not explicitly filter artificial or non­natural rivers from the
dataset. The presence of channelized sections introduce noice and may prevent the
model training from converging to the right solution. Higher residuals in low­sinuosity
areas suggest that filtering such sections from training data could improve model per­
formance.
Another aspect to consider is whether the parameterization approach itself influences
the model’s performance. While standardizing cross sections using a fixed set of param­
eters provides a structured representation, an alternative approach would be to train the
predictive model directly against flow area or hydraulic radius, rather than solely mini­
mizing parameter­specific losses. This could help enforce physical plausibility, ensuring
that predicted parameters align with hydraulic constraints and improve the overall con­
sistency of cross­section estimations, leading to a better combined prediction of cross
section geometry.

Finally, the model’s learning dynamics may be affected by the dominance of the shape
factor (s) in training. Since s showed high predictive ability and weak correlation with in­
dividual geospatial features, it may disproportionately influence optimization, potentially
overshadowing other parameters such as depth (hfull) and vertical positioning (∆h). In­
vestigating whether this dominance skews model performance could be beneficial for
refining the predictive framework.
Despite these limitations, this study establishes a foundation for cross­section prediction,
and future work could address these challenges by expanding datasets, refining feature
selection, and incorporating additional physical constraints into the modeling process.
5.3
Outlook
Building on these findings, several refinements could improve predictive modeling of
cross­section properties. A key improvement would be dividing the modeling process
into specialized models for shape, ∆h, and depth + width, since these parameters have
distinct relationships with terrain and hydraulic variables, and show distinct differences
in model predictive performance.
Expanding data coverage is another critical step. Incorporating more extensive datasets,
particularly for large rivers, and filtering artificial channels could enhance model gener­
alization across diverse hydrological settings.
Additionally, integrating real­world calibration data, such as in­situ water level sensor
measurements, could help validate and refine predictions. Comparing modeled cross
sections to observed flow conditions would provide insights into how errors shows in
hydraulic simulations.
By addressing these refinements, future work can bridge the gap between conceptual
cross­section modeling and real­world hydraulic applications, contributing to more re­
liable large­scale hydrodynamic assessments, especially in areas with limited survey
data.

Conclusion
This study explored the feasibility of predicting standardized cross­section properties for
hydraulic modeling using machine learning frameworks. The findings highlight both the
advantages and challenges of this approach, emphasizing the role of feature extraction,
model generalizability, and the influence of data characteristics. The objectives this
study aimed to address were the following:
1. Can we describe cross sections with standardized parametric formulations
and how accurate is this approach for small streams ?
Standardizing cross­sections using power­law relationships for width, depth, shape
and vertical offset to terrain provided a structured approach to parameterization.
The vertical offset (∆h) worked as a standardized metric for correctly getting a ref­
erence elevation depth based on the DTM, for correctly placing the cross section
in the terrain. However, while these methods captured general trends, their ac­
curacy for small streams was limited, as relative errors were higher despite a low
RMSE given the stream size. The method showed a consistent pattern of over­
fitting water depth with a mean relative error of 14.23 % due to estimated cross
sections underfitting flow area with a mean relative error of 10.39 % as showed in
Figure 4.5.
2. How do the cross section parameters vary spatially, and which factors influ­
ence these variations?
Spatial variability in cross­section properties was influenced by topographic and
hydrological conditions. Features such as bankfull width derived from the DTM,
terrain slope, and elevation range were strongly correlated with variations in width
(wfull) and vertical offset (∆h) as seen in Figures 4.10 and 4.9.
3. To what extent can we predict the variation of cross­section parameters,
how accurate are these predictions, and how does accuracy differ across
regions?
Readily available geospatial datasets, including DTMs, river network data, and
sediment classifications, enabled the extraction of meaningful features for predict­
ing cross­section properties. Predictions for width and shape were relatively ac­
curate, while depth and vertical positioning (∆h) were more difficult to estimate
reliably. Regional differences in accuracy were evident, with the model perform­
ing well for natural, high­sinuosity rivers but exhibiting greater errors in artificial
channels.
Leave­one­area­out (LOAO) experiments highlighted generalization
challenges, particularly for depth predictions, where localized hydraulic conditions
were difficult to capture.
4. Which features affect prediction accuracy, and therefore which factors influ­
ence the shape of cross sections in small Danish streams?

as local slope, bankfull width, and terrain variation, which shaped cross­sectional

These findings suggest that while the model can effectively predict certain cross­

Overall, this study demonstrates that while machine learning provides a viable frame­

The sinuosity index is a measure that quantifies the degree of meandering in a river
channel, with higher values indicating more pronounced bends and curves. (cite).
For each cross section, the sinuosity index is calculated by analyzing a 200­meter river
segment centered around the cross section point. This local analysis involves the fol­
lowing steps:
1. Stream Length: The actual length of the river, measured along the centerline be­
tween two points within the 200­meter segment.
2. Straight­Line Distance: The direct distance between the same two points, calcu­
lated as the shortest possible path.
The Sinuosity Index (SI) is then computed as the ratio of the channel length to the
straight­line distance, defined as:
