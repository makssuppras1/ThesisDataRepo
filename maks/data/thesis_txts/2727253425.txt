Abstract
This thesis presents the design of an optimization framework to minimize the manufac­
turing costs of a semi­submersible floater and linear catenary mooring system coupled
with the IEA 15 MW reference turbine. The framework includes both static and dynamic
structural response, natural frequency, and geometric constraints used to optimize the
parameterized geometric design variables. The Quick Load Analysis of a Floating off­
shore wind turbine model is used to calculate the static and dynamic responses of the
floater­turbine system, while the hydrodynamics are solved by used of an in­house ana­
lytical model. The effect of water depth on the cost of the floater is also explored through
the optimization of the system at different sites with similar sea states.
This work shows that the use of simplified models allows for faster optimization than is pos­
sible with state­of­the­art software, though there are some sacrifices to accuracy. Namely,
with heave natural frequency and the dynamic response in pitch. Additionally, optimization
at varying water depths presents an optimal depth for the lowest cost of the floater­turbine
system between 100 m and 200 m.

Introduction
Following growing concern with the ongoing global climate crisis, the European Union
(EU) has increased investment in renewables and, specifically, the installation of wind
energy, over the past decade. In 2022, Europe installed 19 GW of wind energy capacity,
4% more than in 2021, with 129 GW more expected to be installed between 2023 and
2027. This is, however, not on pace with the EU’s 2030 targets, which is in large part due
to permitting bottlenecks, though governments are working actively to lessen this barrier
(Wind Europe, 2023).
Figure 1.1: Wind energy installations in Europe as predicted by WindEurope (Wind Eu­
rope, 2023)
Within the two types of wind energy, onshore and offshore, the latter is predicted to see
a drastic rise in installation by 2050, growing to nearly 1,000 GW globally (IRENA, 2019).
The European Commission has set a goal of installing 230­450 GW of offshore wind by
2050, the upper limit of which would cover 30% of the EU’s projected energy usage at
that time (Ghigo et al. 2020). Further specified within offshore wind, is floating offshore
wind, which can take advantage of previously inaccessible wind resources, the specifics
of which are covered in the next section.
1.1
Floating offshore wind
Floating offshore wind can offer numerous benefits to the offshore wind sector. Namely,
the accessibility to large stretches of deep water seas. Within Europe, about 80% of the
offshore wind resources are located at water depths greater than 60 m, at which point
bottom­fixed turbines become financially unappealing (Wind Europe, 2018). Additionally,
the economic capacity to operate floating offshore wind turbines (FOWTs) in deeper wa­
ters ensures access to areas of greater wind speed and consistency. This access affects
the levelized cost of energy (LCOE), which is projected to decrease drastically over the
next two decades, thus increasing the viability of FOWTs even further.

The environmental impacts must be considered for the implementation of FOWTs in deep­
water conditions. An extensive review, the first of its kind for this topic, conducted by
a team from the California Polytechnic State University determined that the effects of
FOWTs can be considered, for the most part, ”minor” (Farr et al. 2021). It is noted, how­
ever, that the size and configuration of the FOWT farm likely scales the effects, either
linearly or nonlinearly, in currently unknown ways. FOWTs, however, are projected to
have a much lesser effect than bottom­fixed turbines due to less disruption to the seabed
during installation (IRENA, 2019).
FOWTs are installed with one of four main classes of substructures, with the main differ­
ence between them being how they are stabilized in water (Scheu at al. 2018).
Figure 1.3: FOWT substructure classes (Scheu et al. 2018)
The spar, or spar buoy, class is stabilized with ballast, which means that the structure is
filled with ballast in order to lower the center of gravity to below the center of buoyancy.
This induces a counter moment to restore pitch and maintain stability. Barge substruc­
tures are stabilized primarily by their water­plane area. They may also have additions
such as heave plates or moon pools attached to increase damping. The tension leg plat­
form (TLP) is characterized by its use of taut mooring line. The tensioned wire is attached
vertically to anchors directly below the turbine and TLP hull. Given its rigidity, the dy­
namics of the system are similar to bottom­fixed structures. Lastly, the semi­submersible
structure is stabilized by both ballast and water­plane area. Its motion is heavily impacted

behavior (Scheu et al. 2018).
1.2
Motivation and scope of project
In this thesis, a semi­submersible floating platform and accompanying catenary mooring
system are designed. Select design parameters are then used as the inputs for the opti­
mization framework. The Quick Load Analysis of Floating wind turbines (QuLAF) model
is used to analyze a simplified combination of the floater­mooring system and the IEA 15
MW turbine in the frequency domain. The hydrodynamic properties are calculated with
an in­house model used to analyze a given cylinders with known orientation and position
called the slender model.
Fast optimization of semi­submersible floating platforms is difficult to achieve, given the
complexity of their hydrodynamics and geometry. In the industry, state­of­the­art models
such as WAMIT or Ansys Aqwa are used for the hydrodynamics and hydrostatics, though
they are both time­consuming and require significant computational resources.
The motivation behind this work is to develop a quicker and lighter optimization framework
for the design of a semi­submersible floating platform and catenary mooring system with
the IEA 15 MW turbine by using simplified models. The implementation of which builds
upon the work in Phan (2021) and seeks to surpass its limitations. This is done chiefly
with the inclusion of the dynamic response of the floater, the effect of which on the floater
design is then explored.
Additionally, the effect of water depth on the design and manufacturing cost of semi­
submersible floaters is examined. The contribution of the water depth to the design and
cost of the floater is not beholden to a specific site and can be applied to various locations
worldwide. As such, four sites of similar sea state but different water depths are optimized
with the same constraints and subsequently analyzed.

Theoretical background
This chapter discusses the models and theory behind this thesis. The framework of com­
putation utilizes the Quick Load Analysis of Floating wind turbines model, which is detailed
in Section 2.1. Following, a brief description of the floater and the slender model, used to
calculate the hydrodynamic terms, are outlined, as is relevant to this thesis, in Sections
2.2 and 2.3, respectively.
2.1
Quick Load Analysis of Floating wind turbines
The system is analyzed by using the Quick Load Analysis of Floating wind turbines (QuLAF)
model, which was developed at DTU by Pegalajar­Jurado, Borg, and Bredmose (2018).
Although state­of­the­art (SoA) models like HAWC2 and FAST are commonly used, QuLAF
was chosen to lower computational cost.
A study performed by Madsen, Pegalajar­
Jurado, and Bredmose (2019) comparing QuLAF and FAST displayed that QuLAF re­
turned accurate results in a shorter computational time. This vast reduction in CPU draw
can be attributed to the choice to analyze the dynamics terms in the frequency domain
rather than the time domain. This is achieved by rewriting the equation of motion in fre­
quency terms.

2.3.8
Ballast system
The ballast system of a floating support substructure serves to maintain stability of the
structure at equilibrium, which is done by balancing the gravitational and buoyancy forces
on the structure as a whole. The ballast material being either solid or liquid has an effect
on its physics and must be considered. Namely, liquid ballast systems introduce a new
added mass component and are therefore more complex.
This thesis uses a solid ballast system with iron concrete to form cylinders, which there­
fore allows it to be considered within the slender model. This distinction must be made
because the ballast not only contributes mass to the mass matrix, but also inertia by way of
its location and orientation within the floater. This model sets the ballast as a single point
load at the bottom of the floater, thus neglecting the inertial contribution (Phan, 2021).
The gravitational forces affecting the floater are the wind turbine mass, sum of the vertical
mooring forces, and the floater steel mass. The buoyancy force consists of the density
and submerged volume of the displaced water, which is calculated by the slender model.
The ballast equilibrium equation can then be written:
mballast = mbuoy −(mwt + mmoor + mfloater,s)
(2.69)
The ballast is filled into all parts of the floater and initially has equivalent height in each
cylinder. The ballast in the pontoon is constrained by vertical height, while in the cen­
ter column and buoyancy tanks the vertical height is variable and they are instead con­
strained by the diameter. A problem arises, as, within the model, the height parameter,
y1, determines the length of the pontoons and the vertical height of the center column and

buoyancy tanks. Consequently, two situations occur that must be accounted for: first,
y1 ≤Dpn,i, where Dpn,i is the inner diameter of the pontoon and second, y1 > Dpn,i.
In the first case, y1 can be used directly in the slender model without issue, though in the
second case a new parameter, y2, must be introduced. This parameter represents the
additional height of ballast in the center column and buoyancy tanks necessary beyond
the diameter of the pontoon. From here, the height of the ballast in each floater component
can be calculated and implemented into the slender model.
This thesis does not focus on the ballast heavily and all calculations are based on the
work in Phan (2021). Thus, this section is a cursory overview of the system being used
with this floater, but for a more detailed look, one is referred to that work.

3.2.2
Methodology
In this section, the process of design evaluation is outlined. Each site was evaluated
with all conditions apart from water depth and sea states remaining the same. For each
location, the slender model was implemented in the following steps:
1. Defines dimensions of floater components and evaluate in the slender model to
determine submerged volume, center of mass and buoyancy of the floater
2. Calculate mooring mass and stiffness
3. Calculate mass of needed ballast and buoyancy force
4. Run slender model with ballast and save; hydrostatic stiffness, added mass, and
mass moment of inertia matrices, wave excitation forces, inertial moments, mass,
and center of gravity of the floater.
5. Load in environmental data and conditions; calculate aero­ and hydrodynamic forces
6. Calculate mass, damping, and restoring matrices for the whole system; pre­calculate
transfer function
7. Calculate dynamic response vector
8. Calculate natural frequencies of each DOF

the reduction in mass of steel over having a lower center of buoyancy.
The chain diameter was not optimized on Phan (2021) and thus is not considered here
for comparison. It can be seen, however, that in this work, Dchn is reduced by about half.
A smaller chain diameter directly leads to smaller linear density. This, in turn, greatly
reduces the mooring stiffness, which, in turn, allows for larger displacement in surge.

Conclusion
This work has shown that a fast optimization framework for the cost of a semi­submersible
floater and catenary mooring system is possible to develop. The simplified models used
allowed for this speed, with some sacrifices to accuracy. Additionally, the effect of water
depth on design variables and therefore the cost of the floater­mooring system has been
explored, providing some insight on optimal water depths for cost­efficient designs.
The comparison of the dynamic responses to those in Allen et al. (2020), illustrated some
of the drawbacks of both QuLAF and the slender model. These include most notably,
the drastic under­prediction of the nacelle acceleration and the over­prediction of both the
dynamic pitch response and heave natural frequency.
The optimization results showed that the framework was able to recreate a similar floater
design to that of the UMaine VolturnUS­S. The inclusion of the dynamic response and
additional constraints proved to make the difference when compared to the floater design
in Phan (2021). Ultimately, however, the main changes between the floater in this work
and the reference are with the draft length and mooring chain diameter, which were both
reduced by nearly half.
The analysis of the effect of water depth showed that the cost of the steel contributes
more to the overall cost than anything else, and therefore, the optimizer prioritizes re­
ducing floater mass wherever possible, though this influence wanes in deeper waters.
Additionally, the pitch natural frequency is a driving factor only at the shallowest water
depth, while the displacement of both surge and pitch have large effects on the design
at all sites. Given this, the mooring chain diameter steadily rises with the water depth to
increase the mooring stiffness and reduce surge motion. The trends seen with the overall
floater costs and input variables suggest that there is an optimal water depth between 100
and 200 m.

Future Works
In this chapter, future work on this project is briefly suggested.
While the analysis of the dynamic response provides understanding of the floater motion,
more can be included into the optimization framework to improve the final design. Firstly,
as mentioned in Section 3.3, the excitation of the pitch displacement occurs at lower
frequencies than expected, which can be explored more to better the accuracy of the
model. Additionally, fatigue analysis can be performed to account for effects of operation
on the structure over time.
For a deeper understanding of the optimized floater itself, a performance analysis could
be undertaken in a SoA software. The objective function could also be updated to include
the logistical costs included with installation to provide a more accurate cost estimate.
Lastly, the contribution of the water depth to the floater design and cost can be expanded
upon with, for example, more sites of varying depths and sea states, individual analysis of
design variables and how they change with the increase of depth, and focused analysis
of sites between 100­200 m depth to determine the optimal point for low cost.
