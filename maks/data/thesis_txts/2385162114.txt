Abstract 
From several nationwide studies the indoor air quality in many Danish schools has proven to be 
very poor due to lack of ventilation. This can have a negative impact on the pupils’ power of 
concentration and learning abilities. In this study two classrooms at DTU and a public school have 
been investigated from CO2 concentrations during different natural ventilation scenarios. The CO2 
concentration was measured using a large number of CO2 loggers arranged in a grid of 
measurement points in desk height. The resulting data were used in order to generate surface plots 
that dynamically visualised the spatial variation of the CO2 concentration. Data from a Pilot 
experiment at DTU were used in the development of a visualisation program. The program uses 
Excel macros for creating the surface plots. Drawings were made using Sketchup and Photoshop. 
The resulting video presentation was created in iMovie. Measurements carried out in the classrooms 
showed alarming concentrations for several periods. The visualisation showed seemingly effective 
ventilation resulting from five minutes ventilation sessions, but the effect was short term due to the 
rapid, subsequent build-ups. Ventilation sessions of 10 min duration without occupants managed to 
reach background level, which resulted in less rapid build-ups afterwards and a more long-term 
effect.  
The developed program proved to be a very powerful tool to illustrate and clarify the ventilation 
effect on the air quality in an educationally and easy understanding way to occupants of the 
building.

Resumé 
Flere landsdækkende undersøgelser af indeklimaet på danske skoler har vist at mangelfuld 
ventilation forårsager nedsat luftkvalitet. Dette kan have stor indflydelse på elevers koncentration 
og dermed deres indlæringsevne. I dette kandidat projekt er der blevet udført undersøgelser af CO2 
koncentrationen i to klasseværelser på henholdsvis DTU og på en folkeskole. Begge steder blev der 
udført manual naturlig ventilation med varierende frekvens og varighed. CO2 koncentrationerne 
blev målt med et stort antal CO2 loggere anbragt i et net af målepunkter i bordhøjde. Resultaterne er 
blevet brugt til at generere dynamiske farve ”surface-plots” som viser den rummelige variation af 
CO2 koncentrationen som funktion af tiden. Data fra de indledende målinger fra et 
undervisningslokale på DTU blev brugt til udviklingen af et visualiseringsprogram. Programmet er 
udviklet under anvendelse af Macro programmering i Excel og tegninger er udført i Sketchup og 
Photoshop. Til den endelige video-præsentation er programmet iMovie benyttet.   
Målingerne i klasseværelset på folkeskolen viste periodevist meget høje koncentrationer af CO2. 
Visualiseringerne viste at ventilationer af 5 minutters varighed tilsyneladende var effektive. Men 
effekten var kortvarig og CO2 koncentrationen steg efterfølgende hurtigt til et højt niveau igen. 
Ventilationer af 10 minutters varighed samtidigt med at eleverne midlertidigt forlod rummet, 
nedbragte derimod CO2 koncentrationen til baggrundsniveauet. Dette resulterede i en mere 
langvarig lav CO2 koncentration før niveauet steg igen. 
Det udviklede program viste sig at være et effektivt redskab til at illustrere og klarlægge effekten af 
ventilationen på en pædagogisk og let forståelig måde.

1 Introduction 
The indoor air quality is very poor in many schools all over the country and studies show that poor 
air quality affects children’s health and the ability to learn (Wargocki et al., 2006). This is critical, 
as children spent 20% of their awake time per year in schools, where they must be receptive to 
learning and feel comfortable (OECD, 2016).  
A large mount of older school buildings use manual natural ventilation as the primary ventilation 
method, and these types of schools have reportedly larger indoor air quality problems, than schools 
with balanced mechanical ventilation (Clausen et al., 2016a). The problems of bad air quality may 
lie in the method of manual natural ventilation. The effectiveness of the method is difficult to 
predict and to control, it depends on many parameters and an acceptable air quality is challenging to 
maintain with large occupancy densities with this method (Stabile et al., 2017).  
As no budget supports renovation of the entire school building sector with mechanical ventilation 
systems (Clausen et al., 2016a), Danish schools must get the best out of their current facilities, 
therefore it is important that the effectiveness of manual natural ventilation is studied and presented 
in a helpful and easily understandable manner towards occupants of these buildings. This could be 
done by visualising different ventilation techniques and their effect on the indoor air quality, based 
on measurements from real classrooms with these challenges. If teachers and pupils could see what 
is happening to the indoor climate, when different initiatives are introduced, they might get 
motivated to prioritise the ventilation.  
 
The objective of this project is to investigate the effect of manual, natural ventilation in two 
classrooms, by developing dynamic visualisations that show the development of the CO2 
concentration. The measurements are carried out by 28 pcs. CO2 sensors evenly distributed in a grid 
pattern in desk height in the following experiments: 
Classroom at DTU: Pilot experiment to provide data for development of the visualisation 
programme. 
Classroom in Public School: Three different scenarios for manual natural ventilation with different 
frequency and duration of the ventilation sessions.  
The aim is to: 
- 
Visualise the behaviour of CO2 concentrations through space and time. To this end a 
programme should be developed to handle the data and create a video presentation.  
- 
Use the developed presentation programme as a tool to evaluate the indoor air quality and 
the effect of different ventilation scenarios.

2 Theory 
2.1 Indoor Environment 
If the indoor environment is to be assessed, several parameters should be evaluated, in order to get 
an overall and detailed picture of the quality. In Denmark, the most common parameters to evaluate 
are thermal environment, CO2-concentration, ventilation rate, daylight conditions, acoustics, 
formaldehyde, radon, humidity/mould, and particles, (DS 3033, 2011).  
In this study focus will lie mainly on the CO2 concentration, in less degree thermal environment. 
Temperature and CO2 concentration can indicate whether or not a facility has adequate or 
inadequate ventilation. Often, heat and CO2 are the largest climatic loads in a building, and by 
dimensioning the ventilation rates according to these loads, other contaminations may be kept under 
acceptable levels as well. For indoor air quality investigations of school buildings, CO2 is an 
especially important parameter, as classrooms have high occupancy densities. 
2.1.1 Carbon Dioxide – CO2  
Human beings contribute to the reduction of the air quality by different bio effluents, and one of 
them is exhaling CO2 as a part of the human metabolism. The exhalation of CO2 is in itself not a 
problem, and the World Health Organisation does not classify the compound as pollutant (Stabile et 
al, 2017). Nonetheless, if the compound accumulates in an enclosure, the air is perceived as stuffy, 
and this could cause negative effects on the occupants. The perception of poor air quality due to 
CO2 accumulation occurs, when the concentration exceeds certain concentrations and the 
ventilation rate is too low. In certain concentrations, CO2 is not noticeable, as it is always present in 
the air. The background concentration of CO2 lies in the range of 350-400 ppm (Rammer Nielsen, 
2012), and should be taken into account when calculating CO2 concentrations from occupancy load 
and ventilation rate (DS 3033, 2011). Table 1 demonstrates recommended thresholds for CO2 
concentrations for schools and children’s institutions, according to voluntary classification of 
indoor climate parameters (DS 3033, 2011, p. 30).

As class A++ suggests, CO2 contents in air less than 800 ppm are rated as the best classification, and 
exceeding 1500 ppm classifies as the worst. According to Danish building regulations 2015 § 
6.3.1.3(2), the CO2 content must not exceed 0.1% for indoor air in Danish schools for future school

school buildings should accommodate classification A+ in table 1. The Danish Working 
Environment Authority (“Arbejdstilsynet”) considers the air exchange inadequate if the CO2-
concentration exceeds 2000 ppm in longer periods during a working day and triggers an immediate 
injunction, if the content is above 2000 ppm in a period of 20 min or more (Clausen et al., 2016a).  
The perception of indoor air is closely related to the timespan, for which occupants are exposed. 
The concept of adaptation is important, when it comes to evaluating the quality of the air we 
breathe. Upon entering a room with poor air quality, it is initially perceived as stuffy and stale.

2.1.2 Thermal environment  
The thermal environment in buildings results from different heat contributions: Internal and 
external loads (equipment, occupants, solar), the technical installations and outdoor climate (DS/EN 
ISO 7730, 2006).  
Dissatisfaction due to thermal conditions can be caused by general too high or too low 
temperatures, which affect the whole body. It can also be caused by heating or cooling of individual 
body parts, such as cold feet, warm head or draft at neck (DS 3033, 2011). The thermal 
environment is composed of different physical factors: air velocity and turbulence intensity, the 
humidity level and the surface and air temperatures. The combination of the latter results in the 
concept of “operative temperature”, which determines the occupant’s heat loss by radiation and 
convection (DS/EN ISO 7730, 2006).

values in table 2 are used as guidelines for evaluating temperatures in this study.

The Danish Building Regulation states in § 6.2.1, that the individual builder should specify the 
maximum number of hours per year, in which the operative temperature may exceed 26 or 27 °C 
(Danish Building Regulations, 2015). The standard DS 474 (DS/EN 474, 1993) recommends no 
more than 100 hours above 26°C and 25 hours above 27°C during the time of occupancy in a 
typical year (Toftum, 2016). 
The activity level of the occupant also plays an essential role in the perception of thermal comfort. 
Occupants are less vulnerable to higher air velocities if their activity level is high, whereas low air 
velocities can be annoying for sedentary occupants, as it is perceived as draft (DS/EN ISO 7730, 
2006). A comfortable thermal environment is important at facilities intended for sedentary work, for 
instance classrooms.

2.2 The Effect on Health and Learning among pupils 
2.2.1 Health 
Poor indoor air quality has different impacts on the body. Symptoms of poor air quality can be 
headache, nausea, lost focus, unrest, tiredness, and seediness. The effects caused by poor air quality 
are not directly hazardous, but lead to discomfort and lowering of physical and psychological state. 
These effects can make pupils feel ill and motivate them to stay home. As poor air quality is a 
symptom of insufficient ventilation rates in a given facility, other contaminations besides CO2 could 
be accumulating as well, such as Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) (Stabile, 2017). Too high 
concentrations of CO2 and temperature are not a direct threat to health, but are clear indicators of 
poor indoor air quality and ventilation design, which in turn can be indicator of other health 
threatening circumstances.  
2.2.2 Learning 
The association between poor indoor environment and learning has been investigated in many 
countries and the conclusions vary from study to study due to different experiment conditions. The 
studies were conducted in different countries, which means that different environments, weather 
conditions, legislations and ventilation methods must have affected the objectives and conclusions 
(Clausen et al., 2016a). Despite the differences, these studies tend to result in the same important 
conclusion – that poor indoor environment has negative effects on the ability to learn.  
A study executed by Scottish researchers describes correlations between CO2 concentration, 
absenteeism and learning. The measurements were performed in 60 different, natural ventilated 
classrooms in middle school level. The study showed that for every 100 ppm increase in the mean 
CO2 concentration, the school attendance dropped with 0.2 % per year (Gaihre et al., 2014). 
Another study performed in the United States found an association between educational 
achievement and CO2 concentration. The study showed association between increased ventilation 
rate and increased proportion of pupils passing math- and reading tests. Standardised test scores and 
background data of the pupils constituted the necessary knowledge in terms of educational 
performance among the pupils. 87 % of the schools had ventilation rates below American Society 
of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE) recommendations (ASHRAE 
Standard 62, 2001), (Haverinen-Shaughnessy, 2011). In Denmark, association between insufficient 
classroom ventilation and effects on learning abilities has been investigated as well. The test scores

for pupils in mechanically ventilated schools were proven to be higher, than for pupils in naturally 
ventilated schools (Toftum et al., 2015). 
 
2.3 Indoor air quality in Danish Schools 
 
The past years, several nation wide investigations conclude that Danish schools suffer from 
inadequate ventilation and poor indoor air quality. The increased focus on the indoor environment 
in schools emerges from general attention towards the school system, aiming at improved 
performance and wellbeing among pupils. Unfortunately, the poor indoor air quality in Danish 
schools has negative consequences and do not support the aim to improve the educational quality in 
Danish learning environment (Clausen et al., 2016a). Danish pupils spend 20% of their awake time 
pr. year in school, and this is the highest rate in Europe – in the whole world only beaten by 
Australia (OECD, 2016). At the same time, the number of pupils per class in increasing: in 2009 an 
number of 20.1 pupils/class was registered; in 2015 this number increased to 20.5 pupils/class 
(Statistikbanken, 2017). The number of pupils pr. class increases, even though the total number of 
pupils in Denmark had decreased over the past years – this means that a higher number of small 
schools have been merged to fewer, larger schools, with larger occupancy load pr. classroom 
(Clausen et al., 2016a).  
A study has been performed in the region of Copenhagen including 9 schools (85 classrooms in the 
heating season, 83 in the non-heating season)(Clausen et al., 2017). During the heating season, the 
threshold of 1000 ppm CO2 concentration was exceeded in 59% of the usage time, the threshold of 
2000 ppm was exceeded in 13% of the time. In the non-heating season, the threshold of 1000 ppm 
was exceeded 12% of the usage time.  
The highest measured concentration of CO2 was a mean value of 2345 ppm in a manual natural 
ventilated classroom during heating season. At the same school, another classroom had a mean 
value of 680 ppm, still in the heating season, but with mechanical ventilation. The natural ventilated 
classroom also varied the most in CO2 concentration from season to season, compared to the 
classrooms with mechanical ventilation.  
Another Danish investigation has measured the indoor climate parameters at 50 different schools 
nation wide in 195 classrooms (Clausen et al., 2016b). This investigation showed that the 
recommended threshold of 1000 ppm was exceeded 47% of the usage time. 91% of the time above

2.4 Ventilation 
 
The main purpose of ventilation is to provide sufficient amounts of fresh air to a building, in order 
to dilute the concentration of pollution generated by people, equipment and materials inside the 
building. Ventilation must be applied for all kinds of buildings, regardless of other air conditioning 
approaches in terms of cooling, heating or moisture control. Looking at the systems capability to 
dilute indoor contaminants can assess the effect of ventilation. This capability is in turn affected by 
the quantity and quality of the fresh air supply and how effective the air is distributed into the 
building (Awbi, 2003).  
 
In this particular case, the emphasized type of ventilation system is the method of natural 
ventilation, as the critical indoor environment evaluations of Danish schools seem to be strongly 
correlated to inadequate natural ventilation of schools (Clausen et al., 2016b).  
2.4.1 Method of Natural Ventilation  
Natural ventilation is supply and removal of air, driven by non-mechanical methods. The driving 
force is the natural movement of air, initiated by differences in air densities. Air densities vary due 
to different moisture contents and temperatures. Air movement due to density differences is called 
buoyancy driven flow, this phenomena imitates displacement ventilation, as colder, dryer air let in 
from the opening of the window, will displace warm and moist air from below and upwards 
(DesigningBuildings, 2017a). Besides from buoyancy driven flow, wind driven flow can contribute 
to the air exchange rate caused by buoyancy, or replace it after the effect of buoyancy has 
decreased. Wind driven flow is powered by pressure differences outside the building, as air tends to 
move from wind direction towards the sheltered façade of the building. Wind driven flow is highly 
effective, when cross ventilation scenarios can be established inside the building. The risk of draft 
and uncontrolled effects may be high. The method is often needed on a warm, stagnant day, when 
buoyancy cannot function (Heiselberg and Perino, 2010). The contribution from wind driven 
ventilation is often expected to increase the air exchange rate (DesigningBuildings, 2017b). 
==PAGE:18==
 
10
The concept of natural ventilation can be applied in many different ways, all exploiting the driving 
force of density- and pressure differences of air. For instance automatic ventilation (automatic 
opening of windows driven by CO2 or temperature sensors), trickle ventilation (small slots in 
windows, that allow small, controlled amounts of air into the room, providing background 
ventilation to larger facilities), and manual natural ventilation (controlled entirely by occupants, by 
opening windows themselves when required), (DesigningBuildings, 2017c). It is possible to install 
natural ventilation systems that will provide sufficient amount of fresh air supply and removal. 
However, these systems are fragile, as they are influenced by many variables. The Danish Building 
regulation § 6.3.1.3 (3) states that natural ventilation systems can be used, if they are capable of 
meeting the 0.1% CO2 threshold, but rooms with high occupancy densities should combine with 
mechanical solutions (hybrid ventilation), in order to maintain acceptable indoor air quality. Many 
school buildings are not provided with mechanical ventilation solutions and the fresh air supply 
relies on the manual natural ventilation or infiltration through leakages of the building (Stabile, 
2017).   
The effect of manual natural ventilation is difficult to predict, as it relies on micro-climatic 
conditions and the individual occupant’s motivation for opening or closing a window. The most 
common way of using manual natural ventilation among occupants is to open a window at small 
frequency, for a longer period of time (Fuoco et al., 2015). The ventilation is most effective initially 
and after a while an inefficient mixed ventilation state occurs. Longer periods of open windows will 
result in less air exchange after a while and cooling of the building mass itself, instead of the air 
only. The most advantageous natural ventilation strategy is reportedly to open a window for a rather 
short period of time with a high frequency. The effective, initial airflow rate is achieved, fresh air is 
supplied, and a comfortable thermal environment will be maintained, or will be restored quickly 
afterwards (Heiselberg and Perino, 2010).  
An Italian study investigated the effect of manual natural ventilation in school buildings as function 
of prescribed airing procedures .The effectiveness of different airing durations was evaluated from 
the level of pollutants and CO2 concentration. The study found that the average indoor CO2 
concentration decreased as function of airing duration, the study tested 5-20 min durations. The 
study emphasize how fresh air supply affected the indoor CO2 concentration with large 
effectiveness, compared to only having internal doors open towards hallway areas. Nevertheless, 
the occupants utilized this as an attempt to ventilate. Even though the average indoor CO2 
==PAGE:19==
 
11
concentration decreased as function of airing duration, levels of ultrafine particles increased as 
function of airing duration (Stabile et al, 2017).  
 
2.5 The school building  
 
The building sector has mostly been preoccupied with reducing the energy consumption of 
buildings the past decades, as legislations towards ventilation and indoor air quality was introduced 
later, in 1995. Solutions for this challenge have been the top priority, due to continuous tightening 
of regulatory requirements in this field. In the mean time, the quality of the indoor environment has 
been less prioritised and the importance of it underestimated. As the building design measures 
aimed at energy reduction has become a more integrated part of the building sector, the attention is 
now increasingly turned towards the challenges of indoor climate. The idea of a sustainable and 
modern building now includes a comfortable and healthy indoor climate as well, besides low energy 
consumption (Heiselberg and Perino, 2010). Indoor climate and energy consumption are closely 
related and newer building design aims to balance these fields. The existing building stock has 
difficulty in meeting these expectations, as the older architecture obviously cannot meet 
contemporarily criteria for energy use and indoor climate. Additionally, envelope renovations and 
different use of the buildings compared to original design intentions challenge older buildings to 
meet acceptable indoor environment. Also, energy reducing actions such as sealed cracks and 
disconnected ventilation systems lead to reduce air changes and poor air quality (Fuoco et al., 
2015).  
A Danish study of the 9 schools in region Copenhagen outlined some significant building 
characteristics that influenced the CO2 concentration (Clausen et al., 2016a). Especially four 
properties of the buildings influenced the CO2 concentration: ventilation type, temperature (external 
or internal temperature not specified), volume pr. pupil and width/height ratio of the classroom. The 
most significant and determining building property was the type of ventilation. Correlations 
between too high CO2 concentrations and classrooms facing architectural enclosures, such as 
schoolyards compared to classrooms facing a free area, were found as well. This could be explained 
by noisy environment in schoolyards, leading to closing the windows, or reduced air change rates 
caused by less wind in sheltered areas. The study also concludes, that problematic CO2

concentrations decreased with newer architecture, as newer buildings have balanced mechanical 
ventilation to a higher extent than older buildings.

3 Method  
 
In the following section, the methods of the surveys are described. This includes the four 
measurements of CO2 at desk height, measured for the surface plot visualisations. The first 
measurement was conducted at DTU Lyngby Campus, in building 116, room 018. The three 
subsequent measurements were conducted in a classroom in the public school in Greve, 
Holmeagerskolen. Both classrooms had manual, natural ventilation.  
 
3.1 Hypothesis 
 
By distributing a large number of loggers in an even pattern in desk height, the spatial and dynamic 
development of CO2 concentration can be tracked in the zone of the occupants.  
The different measurement points will form the basis for different surface plots over time reflecting 
the development.

5 Procedure for measurements 
The plan for the measurements appears in appendix A.  
5.1 Pilot experiment – DTU Lyngby Campus, building 116, room 018  
 
The measurement at DTU was carried out in order to test the intended method and to assess whether 
the dynamic, spatial variation in CO2 concentration was possible to track and visualise afterwards. 
The generated data should help prepare a setup in Excel that could visualise the spatial deviations 
dynamically.  
 
Settings 
Finding a natural ventilated classroom at DTU campus in Lyngby was challenging, as almost every 
teaching facility was provided with mechanical ventilation. With help from several Campus Service 
technicians, appropriate facilities were found. A plan of campus marked with potential non-
mechanical ventilated facilities was received, see appendix B.  
 
The lecture rooms in building 116, indicated with the blue circle, resembled primary school 
classrooms the most, as they were not auditoriums. The lecture rooms had mechanical ventilation,

5.1.1 The developed programme for the visualisations  
The programme HOBOware Pro processed the 28 data sets initially. Temperature, CO2 
concentration and time step data were exported as Excel files. This first actual dataset with 28 
measurements made it possible to assess the optimal data output. On this basis the development of 
the excel spread sheet, needed to generate the dynamic surface plots, could proceed. 
 
In order to process the 28 data sets and generate the dynamic surface plots, an Excel programme has 
been developed. As each of the 28 loggers measured several parameters every 10 second, the data 
amount would be difficult to manage afterwards without a controlled, digitalized process. 
 
The Excel programme has two main functions:  
1. To transform the 28 data set into a specific format used for generating surface plots.  
2. To run a dynamic surface plot by substituting the transformed data set from one time step 
with the data set from the next timestep and so on, via Macros. 
 
The Excel programme is available at the attached USB key and a detailed description of the 
function and systematics of the programme are described in appendix E.  
 
When the data sets are transformed and processed by the programme, the dynamic surface plot is 
generated and recorded in Quick Time Player. The recordings have subsequently been edited in 
iMovie. To make it possible to watch dynamic data sets from several hours recording, the speed of 
the data presentations has been increased 20 times. Additionally, the recordings have been cropped 
and processed with service messages from the observation.  
The Excel programme provided the coloured plots and the running time. The classroom drawings, 
legend, and measurements were initially made in Sketch Up, imported in Adobe Photoshop and 
eventually with transparent background, superposed on the movie.

7 Data interpretation 
 
The interpretation is based on an overall assessment of the visualisation and the data statistics. The 
interpretation involves references to video clips in slow-motion, which aim to support the different 
points.  
7.1 Pilot Experiment at DTU, building 116, H018 
7.1.1 The indoor air quality 
Looking at the statistic figures presented in table 3, it is seen that the mean value and median 
correspond to category A++ for indoor air quality in schools and institutions (DS3033). The 
maximum mean CO2 concentration is 745 ppm, which means that the air quality was within the best 
category for indoor air quality described in DS3033, 100% of the time that day. The mean 
temperature spans from 21°C to 23°C during the school day (figure 18). This temperature span 
corresponds to category A for recommended operative temperatures. The both temperature and CO2 
concentration development in figure 18 is characterized mainly by one large build-up, that drops in 
the end of the day.   
7.1.2 The ventilation effect  
During the pilot experiment, the occupants made the following ventilation initiatives: two windows 
were opened and closed within an hour in the beginning of the day. Door 2 was closed when the 
lecture started at 8:30, and door 1 was opened and closed many times during the day. At 11.36 AM 
three windows were opened at full frequency for 10 minutes.  
 
The students arrived 8.00 and opened window 1 and 2. At 8.25 AM window 1 was closed again.  
In this time period the occupancy increased from 0-18 students. In the visualisation the increasing 
occupancy load in the beginning of the day is seen, starting from 8 O’clock. The concentration in 
the right lower corner increases slightly and few peaks appear. The two open windows and the open 
hallway door have limited effects, as the total mean CO2 concentration temperature and increased 
with 100 ppm and 1°C respectively in this first half hour, according to figure 18. The ventilation is 
not reflected clearly in the visualisation at this point.  
==PAGE:50==
 
42
A small drop in mean concentration occurs at 8.55 AM, which is seen in figure 18, corresponding to 
50 ppm. In the visualisation, the drop in concentration is reflected as well, but not very clearly. The 
yellow areas turns a bit greener in the right corner from 8.55 AM. At that time, door 1 was opened 
and cross-flow was created with window 2. The window was closed at 8.58 AM, and at that time, 
the concentration starts to increase immediately again. The opening of the hallway door seems to 
have a small, increasing effect of the ventilation effect, if it is combined with open windows.  
 
From 8.58 AM-9.51 AM the class did not have any window open. 23 people were present and the 
mean CO2 concentration increased with 530-710 ppm in 55 min. Again, the increase of CO2 
concentration appears mainly in the right corner in the visualisation. The local increase could 
indicate, that the air is stagnant and the air is not mixed evenly across the room.  
The build-up is slightly interrupted in the time period 9.51 AM-10.07 AM and 10.41 AM-10.47 AM 
due to decreased occupancy and opening of door 1 during the breaks. When the hallway door is 
open, the build up decreases slightly or reflects approximated steady state. The latter appears 
between 10.41 AM-10.47 AM in figure 18.  
 
At 11.26 AM, door 1 was opened and people started to leave. This time period is available in video 
clip 1.01. The opening of door 1 and the decreased number of occupants have a minor effect of the 
CO2 concentration. At 11.36 AM-11.45 AM four windows were opened (door 1 still open) and 
three occupants were present. This intervention is very clear in both the visualisation and in figure 
18. The visualisation reflects an instant dilution of the air and in figure 18, the mean CO2 
concentration drops with 200 ppm and the temperature 0.5°C in 10 minutes. This ventilation session 
is the only coincidence of temperature drop during the day as well, indicating ventilation 
effectiveness in terms of thermal loads as well. After the closing of the windows, the graph in figure 
18 phase out around 500 ppm and do not increase again.  
The 10 min ventilation was able to change the air effectively in the rather large classroom, but it did 
not have to compensate for large occupancy densities either.

7.2 Holmeagerskolen: Scenario 1  
7.2.1 The indoor air quality 
In table 5, the mean CO2 concentration and median are 1544 ppm and 1600 ppm respectively. Both 
values correspond to the worst category of recommended concentrations CO2 in schools (DS3033, 
table 1). The cumulative graph in figure 24 shows that for 2/3 of the usage time, the CO2 
concentration exceeded 1500 ppm, and 27% of the time, the air quality corresponded to category A 
(<1200ppm).  
The school day had a mean CO2 concentration above 2000 ppm between 10.42 AM-11.02 AM (20 
min) and between 11.09 AM-11.57 AM (48 min), which are two coincidences that are so severe 
that they would trigger an immediate injunction by Danish Working Environment Authority 
(Arbejdstilsynet). The statistical figures already indicates, without further interpretation, that 5 min 
ventilation per hour is not enough to maintain an acceptable indoor air quality for this particular 
classroom.  
7.2.2 The effect of “5 min ventilation pr. hour” 
It is seen from table 4 that the build-ups had large climaxes, ending in mean concentrations that 
exceeded 2000 ppm. The subsequent 5 min ventilation sessions were quite effective, in terms of 
concentration drops, spanning from 1000-2000 ppm. Even though the drops are very large, the

8 Discussion 
The ventilation 
The results from the measurements at Holmeagerskolen indicate clearly, that the ventilation 
conditions are inadequate. Scenario 1 got the worst evaluations with two coincidences of mean CO2 
concentrations above 2000 ppm for 20 min and 48 min respectively. In scenario 2, the mean CO2 
concentration exceeded 2000 ppm in five minutes and 8 min during the day. Scenario 3 had better 
indoor air quality, but 13 min with concentrations above 2000 still occurred. 
 
Comparing the two controlled scenarios, scenario 1 had the worst results in terms of indoor air 
quality with mean CO2 concentrations within category C 60% of the usage time, compared to 
scenario 2, with mean CO2 concentrations within category C 17% of the time. With this 
comparison, scenario 2 must have been more effective in terms of air change than scenario 1. The 
indoor air quality in terms of CO2 seems to be improved as function of ventilation duration, just like 
the study made by Stabile et al, 2017.  
The ventilation sessions in both scenario 1 and 2 resulted in large concentration drops that were 
seemingly effective while they lasted, but the effects were brief. Especially the 5 min ventilation 
session had very short-term effect. During the 5 minutes, the CO2 concentration did not reach 
background concentration, which meant that the subsequent build-up started at a higher 
concentration, compared to the longer sessions in scenario 2. The ventilation effects between the 
two scenarios are compared in figure 45:  
 
In figure 45, the air is changed quicker in scenario 2 scenario than for scenario 1. Additionally, the 
concentration ends at background concentration in scenario 2 before the session is over.

The natural ventilation conditions are quite similar for the classroom at DTU and C4 at 
Holmeagerskolen. They both have façade glazing with windows towards an enclosed backyard, and 
they both have entrances from a large hallway area. Both classrooms seem to have improved effect 
of ventilation, when the hallway door and windows were open at the same time, this is seen 8.55 
AM in the classroom in DTU (figure 18) and during the passive observation in Holmeagerskolen, 
8.50 AM (figure 28). The hallways did not supply the classrooms with fresh air, but the pressure 
difference between the hallway and the exterior might have caused cross flow through the 
classrooms, when a window and the hallway door were open at the same time. With cross flow the 
air change rates increases (Designingbuilding.co.uk – Cross ventilation, 2017b).  
When the hallway door was open exclusively, the classrooms were not supplied with fresh air, but 
the CO2 concentration decreased a bit, for instance a 50 ppm drop at 9.51 AM in the classroom at 
DTU, when door 1 was opened. The pressure difference between the hallway and the classroom 
might cause a small dilution of the CO2 concentration in the classroom, due to air movement from 
pressure equalising. For the classroom in Holmeagerskolen, an open hallway door had very small 
effect. For instance, the hallway door was open during build up from 12.35 AM in scenario 1. The 
concentration increased rapidly to unacceptable concentrations anyway.  
 
Seasonal aspects 
In scenario 3 the classroom was ventilated almost the entire day with frequent opening of window 
and the exterior door. This behaviour was possibly due to the maintenance of an acceptable thermal 
environment at the same time. In figure 28 the mean temperature was in the range of 22.5 °C-26 °C 
the entire day. In the heating season, it would not be possible to have full ventilation almost the 
entire day without damaging the thermal environment. Even at the specific spring day, May 22, the 
window and the door were closed at 10.30 AM, even though the internal temperature was measured 
to 23 °C at this point. The closing of the windows and the exterior door could have been due to 
draft problems despite the rather high internal temperature. The pupils were sedentary and therefore 
==PAGE:69==
 
61
more vulnerable to draft nuisances (DS/EN ISO 7730, 2006). These actions lead to the large build 
up prior to the lunch break with concentrations exceeding 2000 ppm. For the classroom at 
Holmeagerskolen, scenario 1 and 2 might show the real ventilation challenges in the classroom to a 
higher extent than scenario 3, as the ventilation patterns could imitate winter conditions. At 
wintertime, the window and the exterior door might be open for short periods of time, due to 
thermal discomfort with cold air supply. Additionally, the pupils might be less motivated to go 
outside in the breaks.  
 
Architectural aspects 
The size of the classrooms differed from each other and this may affect the indoor air quality. The 
pilot experiment was run in a larger, more rectangular room, with occupants located in the right end 
only. With 23 occupants, this resulted in 15,6 m3 /occupant, from calculation (1). The classroom in 
Holmeagerskolen is smaller, more squared, with occupants located in the centre. With 25 
occupants, this results in 7,8 m3/occupant, from calculation (2). The occupants at DTU had twice 
the air volume available, as the pupils in Holmeagerskolen, which might have caused the better air 
quality. Investigations regarding indoor climate in Danish schools (Clausen et al., 2016a) also 
proved correlation between higher room volume per pupils and lower CO2 concentrations. The 
same influence is seen in this comparison.   
 
The visualisations 
The purpose of the visualisations was to show the effect of ventilating. The four visualisations 
manage to do so, as they react instantly to the opening of windows and reflect the changes in the 
spatial distribution of CO2. The dynamic surface plots show one surface plot for every 10 second in 
order to capture the dynamic in the CO2 concentration and how it develops through time and space. 
This dynamic is interesting, because it shows the consequences of controlling the air quality in 
different ways, and how different actions affect the CO2 concentration over time and in what way.   
The visualisation managed to show that the ventilation sessions gave instant results and was 
seemingly effective, but the CO2 concentration increases very fast as soon as the openings are 
closed again. They showed how the spatial variations are huge during the five min ventilation and 
the subsequent build up, and how the mixing of the air is reflected by even CO2 distributions. The 
visualisations showed exciting differences in spatial CO2 behaviour, as the build-up during scenario 
1 tended to increase from the outer edges towards the centre, whereas the build-up during scenario 2 
==PAGE:70==
 
62
started from centre and distributed towards the outer edges. The build-up of CO2 distribution from 
the edges towards the centre could be due to remaining CO2 in the upper parts of the classroom that 
sinks into the measurement zone along the walls. The buoyancy driven flow might have difficulties 
in removing the CO2 from the upper corners in the room with only 5 minutes of ventilation. This 
problem is properly accentuated with a sloped ceiling. The measurements are limited to one layer of 
the room volume and do not show the behaviour of the air flows in the other parts of the room. 
When the build-up of the CO2 distributes from centre towards the edges, this might indicate that the 
room is effectively free from CO2, also in the parts of the room, which are not in the measurement 
zone.  
The visualisation of the pilot experiment also showed the rather local increase of CO2 in the right 
end of the classroom. The heat plumes generated by occupants might only have initiated air 
movement in this end of the room (Skistad et al., 2003). This could explain the local increase and 
slow distribution to the rest of the room, as the air in this part of the room was more stagnant. The 
CO2 concentration was within category A++ the whole usage time, based on measured 
concentrations in the whole classroom. Because of the large spatial variations, one could argue that 
the air quality could be evaluated from the local measurements instead of the mean CO2 
concentration, as the occupants were surrounded by poor air quality more locally. Such 
considerations were made possible because of the dynamic, spatial visualisations of the CO2 
concentrations.  
 
The large peaks occurring once in while is reflected in the videos as blushing points, that disappears 
again rather quickly. The sight of the many peaks could make the air look even worse at some 
points, as they presumably results from pupils breathing directly into the HOBO loggers. This could 
be interpreted as sources of error. In order to smooth out the peaks, the method of running mean 
could have been applied to the data, but by doing so, the 10 seconds dynamic could be lost, which 
was a bigger priority. The peaks did contribute to exciting data interpretation, as the peaking 
loggers symptomatic showed correlation to their own location in the room. Also, downwards peaks 
occurred once in while, which means that not all peaks could be due to direct breathing into the 
meters. Downward peaks might reflect local air movements or draft at the current logger.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:71==
 
63
The method 
The loggers were placed at desk height in order to capture the concentrations in the occupancy 
zone. If the loggers should be distributed evenly in the classroom at desk height, it was almost 
unavoidable to have some of them located near the breathing zone of a pupil. The locations of the 
loggers were a compromise between several considerations: aiming to capture the air quality at the 
occupancy zone, avoid logger location too close to breathing zone, and make the setup as invisible 
as possible, so that the pupils were not distracted unnecessarily. This compromise also lead to 
several displaced loggers compared to the theoretical locations of the loggers. Some of the locations 
were not possible, due to flow areas or lack of furniture in desk height at the specific locations. 
These uncertainties might have influenced the measurement precision of the spatial variation.  
 
More controlled scenarios would ease the interpretation of the results and strengthen the arguments 
assessed from the visualisations. In order to evaluate the effect of natural ventilation systems 
thoroughly, parameters such expected airflow rate, ventilation efficiency, thermal comfort, and 
cooling of the construction, could be assessed as well (Heiselberg and Perino, 2010). The 
theoretical planning of the measurements deviated from the practical, as the pupils and teachers 
contributed to the planning and execution of the measurements, more or less intentionally. The 
pupils’ behaviour seemed to be depending on the specific teacher, and they were too small for 
taking responsibility for the measurements. The lunch break had full occupancy in scenario 2, even 
though they were supposed to leave the room. The lack of corporation with a substitute teacher at 
this point affected the measurements and challenged the strictness of the scenario control. After 
lunch, a permanent teacher took over and supported the scenario firmly, leading to a more 
successful control of the scenario. The important cooperation with the teachers also let to inputs 
regarding the ventilation control. Even though attempts were made to control the durations of the 
build-ups and ventilation sessions, these varied very much, as both teachers and pupils wanted to 
contribute to the experiment.  It is recommended to match expectations with the class prior to such 
measurements, in order to avoid unwanted, varying parameters and have too many contributors 
affecting the setup. A clear division of roles would have made the control stricter and thereby easier 
to process and more useful for conclusions. Better control of time durations of the build-ups and the 
presence of occupancy during ventilation sessions could have eliminated some of the variations that 
challenged the data processing. Still, the variation in ventilation duration let to exciting results in 
terms of interpreting the ventilation effect.  
==PAGE:72==
 
64
 
Even though the pupils and teachers were very interested in participating in the ventilation 
initiatives, the observational study made it clear, that the routines quickly are interrupted or 
forgotten, due to too many other agendas during a busy school day. This is problematic in a natural 
ventilated classroom with heavy occupancy like the one in Holmeagerskolen. In order to maintain 
an acceptable air quality in this room, the occupants need to stick to some ventilation routines, that 
are systematic and timed. Lack of time from the teachers counteracts the ability to ventilate 
properly. Natural ventilation systems are capable of providing fresh air if they are dimensioned 
correctly. The method is less costly in terms of energy use, compared to mechanical systems. It is 
more appreciated by the occupants in terms of user control (Heiselberg and Perino, 2010). At the 
same time, some facilities are too large and overloaded to be ventilated properly by non-mechanical 
solutions, and a proper indoor environment must be prioritised as well (Heiselberg and Perino, 
2010). Natural ventilation should be utilized, when the climatic conditions allow it, but the 
occupancy density in the classroom at Holmeagerskolen seems to be too large for the system to 
work. It is simply not capable of maintaining an acceptable indoor air quality, unless the classroom 
is manually ventilated several times per hour. This is hardly realistic, especially not in the 
wintertime.  
Hopefully, visualisations of the ventilation effects could motivate and inspire the users to establish 
routines and make more ventilation initiatives.  
Visualising the ventilation effect and CO2 contamination might motivate and concretize the 
importance of ventilate properly.    
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:73==
 
65
9 Conclusions 
 
Two classrooms have been investigated from CO2 concentrations during different natural 
ventilation scenarios. The CO2 concentration was measured in 28 measurement points in desk 
height, in order to generate surface plots that dynamically visualised the spatial variation.  
Traditional data statistics helped analysing the quality of the indoor air and supported the 
interpretation of the visualisations.  
Data from the pilot experiment at DTU were used in the development of the programme. The 
measurements in the classroom at the public school showed that the three ventilation scenarios had 
alarming concentrations for long periods of time. Especially in Scenario 1(5 min ventilation per 
hour) the visualisation showed seemingly effective ventilation during the five minutes, but the 
effect was short-term due to the rapid, subsequent build-ups. Scenario 2 (10 min ventilation 
durations without occupants) managed to reach background concentration, which resulted in less 
rapid build-ups afterwards and thereby a more long-term effect. The most effective method is 
concluded to be an intense 10 minute long occupancy free session, as the manages to change the air 
entirely and start from lowest concentration possible and thereby increase the effect slightly in the 
long term. The visualisations showed even CO2 distribution during the rest of the build ups, 
indicating rather mixed air. Even though the 10 minute, empty session was the most effective one, 
this approach is still inadequate in the long run, as they have to be introduced more often than once 
per hour in order to meet acceptable concentrations. This is probably not sustainable during a busy 
school day with a tight schedule.  
Data from the three scenarios in the Public School showed that this programme is a very powerful 
tool to illustrate and clarify the ventilation effect on the air quality in an educationally and easy 
understanding way to occupants of the building.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:74==
 
66
10 Perspectivation  
 
The dynamic visualisations could be applied to other kinds of rooms with other kinds of ventilation 
methods, in order to see the differences in dynamic, spatial variation, within different building 
typologies and ventilation methods. Further the dynamic visualisations could also be applied to 
measurements in vertical planes in order to provide better understanding of the ventilation of the 
room. 
The program could be further developed to handle a higher number of data loggers. An user 
friendly interface is needed as well as the ability of the program to accommodate different shapes of 
the measurement area. 
The videos could be tested on teachers and pupils, to evaluate the effect of visualisations of the 
indoor air quality: do the visualisations improve the understanding of ventilation effects? Do the 
videos make an impression on the teachers, the school administration, the municipality, so that the 
ventilation are prioritised higher? 
Future scenarios could be controlled to a higher extent and the logger locations could be more 
precise, in order to optimise the results. One of the purposes of the investigation was to evaluate the 
ventilation, based on the visualisations but it is not a traditional evaluation method though and it 
could be beneficial to compare the arguments with more traditional ventilation evaluations, such as 
ventilation rate calculations, ventilation efficiency, CFD investigations, or zonal modelling. These 
evaluation methods could support the result processing, in order to improve the critical approach 
towards the method of the logger grid.  
Further investigations could test how many ventilation sessions are actually needed in order to 
maintain an acceptable air quality in the classroom at Holmeagerskolen. Could combinations of 
scenario 1 and 2 maintain an acceptable air quality, with increased amount of sessions?  
The seasonal influence of the effectiveness could be tested as well, by conducting the same 
experiments during heating season, in order to compare the visualisations and results. 
Other possibilities of natural ventilation for Holmeagerskolen could be examined as well, such as 
automatic natural ventilation or trickle ventilation. Is it possible to maintain an acceptable indoor air 
quality, for instance with background ventilation from trickle ventilation, combined with automatic 
openings of the windows, when the loads exceeds certain threshold?

Appendix D – Calibration of loggers 
 
Calibrating each logger takes about 20 min, so initially 25 loggers were calibrated. This process 
were distributed into two days 
 
The HOBO loggers are calibrated using the Calibrator programme installed in the lab at 412.  
The loggers are purged with different gases.  
 
The HOBO logger is initially connected to power, in order to warm up the machine, this will 
improve its ability to calibrate more correctly.  
In the meantime the programme is opened, and the max concentration is set to 5000, as this is the 
limit for the measurer. The pressure and temperature in the room are assessed or measured, and the 
values are set in the programme. For the loggers, the room temperature were set to 23,4oC and the 
pressure to 1013,25 hPa.  
Each logger is connected to the programme and it appears as connected. The current measured 
concentration appears in the display. The cover, protecting the CO2 measurer, is taken off. The 
yellow cover protecting the gas inlet is taken off as well and the first gas tank is connected with a 
hose. The gas tank should have a pressure at ~3 ATM. Now, the current measured CO2 
concentration should drop to zero. When zero is reached (or 10 ppm or 20 ppm), “Zero”-button is 
hit, and the programme will run through 100 channels securing the zero value. When this is done, 
the outlet of the zero-gas tank is closed and the other gas tank is connected to logger with a new 
hose. The pressure level of the gas outlet is adjusted similar to the zero-gas, and the tap is opened. 
The zero concentration should increase to 5000 ppm, and after this level is reached, the averaging 
of the channels is repeated once more. The logger is freed from the top-gas hose, and the yellow 
cover is installed again. The cover of the CO2 measurer is installed again, and the HOBO logger has 
been calibrated and is ready for use again.

Appendix E – About the Excel Programme  
 
 
Initial settings 
- 
Offset button default set to “0”.  
- 
The cell “C2” is called “time code”. Time code 1 corresponds to first time step in 
measurement, and it is integrated in the time display in cell “C4”.  
- 
In cell N1 the time step is shown, which is “00.00.10” in this coincidence, as the loggers 
measured every 10th second.  
 
Importing the CO2 and time data into the programme:  
Each hobo file should be converted to excel files. Afterwards the measured values are separated 
from one column into several columns. This is done by selecting the column and press “from text to 
columns” in “Data”. Select comma as separator and make CO2 into the type “text”, as it otherwise 
will interpret it as time. Select the CO2 data columns and the time column and transpose it by 
selecting it all and then paste it “special” in the hobos sheet. In the insert special select transpose. 
 
What to be changed before running first macro: 
- 
Change the matrix to the right, in order to make it reflect the setup in the classroom. The 
number of rows and columns should correspond to number of loggers in each direction. 
Write the logger number in the sketch matrix to the right. This is step is only important in 
order to keep track with the matrix to the left and the logger numbers/locations. 
- 
Change the code in each cell in the matrix the left, so that each cell corresponds to the 
logger “code” selected in first column. The code in the matrix should be updated with the 
number of time codes you want the visualize as well, for the range that VLOOKUP (in 
Danish LOPSLAG) should be looking at and replace with the right CO2 value in the current 
cell at a specific time. For instance, if there is 3243 timesteps (from ~6AM to 3PM), this is 
multiplied with 28 (number of hobos) and row nr. 15 is added additionally. The range 
should then be changed to $F$90819.The F44:F71 selection should be changed to the range 
that the CO2 values are to be found in, in the “hobos” sheet. For instance $A$3;$DTU$30.  
- 
The macro itself called “KopierDataNed”, should be changed according to the description in 
the macro itself. 
- 
Make sure that the macro running the dynamic correspond to the timesteps you want to 
visualise.
