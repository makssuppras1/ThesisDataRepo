Abstract
A two-dimensional high-order free surface Navier-Stokes solver based on the spec-
tral method is developed with the geometric flexibility to simulate wave flow with
arbitrary seabed geometries given the first and second derivative of still water
depth.
This solver supports explicit low storage Runge-Kutta fourth-order five-
stage method and implicit first-order backward Euler method as a time integration
method. The test case ’Luth experiment (1994) [8]’ validates the accuracy of the im-
plemented model. The comparison with wave boundary theory and the computed
wave boundary indicates its accuracy in simulating the boundary layer, which is
the significance of using more expansive Navier-Stokes solvers instead of potential
solvers. The linear dispersion analysis shows that by the high-order spectral method,
the required number of grids per wavelength is much less than in the traditional
finite difference model. The pressure Poisson equation derived from the pressure-
velocity coupling process is the most expensive step in each time step. Therefore,
a p-multigrid method is developed to accelerate the computation of the dense, non-
symmetric, non-positive definite matrix that is derived from the pressure Poisson
equation. The matrix-free Gauss-Seidel method is adopted as smoother during each
relaxation sweep to significantly save the storage.
While the point Gauss-Seidel
method converges slowly because of the bad eigenspectra property of iterative ma-
trix discretized by the spectral method so that its smoothing property is not sat-
isfactory, an efficient block Gauss-Seidel smoother is developed that has a superior
smoothing effect than point Gauss-Seidel smoother and speedup the convergence
remarkably. Among the multigrid structure, F-cycle and W-cycle archive optimal
scalability O(N 2) in both vertical and horizontal directions. V-cycle multigrid has
worse scaling except for grid refinement in the horizontal direction by using point
Gauss-Seidel as smoother which has much better O(Nlog(N)) scalability. The full
multigrid method is implemented to speed up the convergence by generating an
accurate initial guess efficiently. Since the matrix operator in each grid level is dif-
ferent, pure multigrid might not be robust in some cases. Therefore, the developed
p-multigrid is combined with the Krylov subspace method to make it more robust.
Results show that full multigrid preconditioned GMRES is the most robust solver
among preconditioned conjugate gradient, bi-conjugate gradient stabilized method,
and GMRES.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Free surface wave dynamics is the field of study that describes the behavior of
the free surface flow. The study field can be applied to earth science, naval archi-
tecture, ocean engineering, and even the movie industry, where the special effect
of water waves is the most challenging thing to overcome. It is indispensable for
the marine industry to perform numerical modeling of free surface flow during the
research and development process of a new marine architecture since the numeri-
cal experiment is the cheapest and safest way to yield data with acceptable accuracy.
The difficulty in modeling the wave flow in marine phenomena is its large scale
of the physical domain and the need for long time integration, especially for the
study of coastal processes that govern the long-term period formation mechanisms
of coastal dunes, which is important in constructing naval and offshore structures.
There are an abundant amount of numerical models that describe the free surface
wave dynamics. The shallow water wave model models the three-dimensional flow
under the two-dimensional computational domain by integrating the variables along
the vertical direction, and it is a hydrostatic model. Although it has good simplic-
ity and computational efficiency, its application and robustness are limited within
shallow water wave cases and the flow, where details and other physical phenom-
ena such as reflection and diffraction are not important. Another alternative to the
hydrostatic wave model is the Boussinesq equation, which keeps the computational
efficiency of the shallow water wave model at the cost of implementation complexity.
The reasons that make it a popular water wave model compared with shallow-water
equation are its capability of capturing most of the important flow field detail like
interaction with structure, reflection, and diffraction, which shallow-water equation
fails to describe and model a wide range of wave scenarios. While the essence of the
hydro-static of the Boussinesq model still limits its application for the case where the
change of vertical direction needs to be investigated. The fully nonlinear potential
flow model is a non-hydrostatic model that is aimed at describing the wave model
where the study is interested in the change in flow field characteristics along the ver-
tical direction. It captures the vertical change by the cost of doing three-dimensional
computation, which is suitable for the widespread scientific and engineering appli-

However, none of the models introduced above can describe the boundary layer.
The boundary layer plays an important role in investigating phenomena such as
sediment transport, friction force, drag force, and wave load. What is more, when
the turbulence effect needs to be considered, only the mathematical model that
incorporates the boundary layer effect is capable of simulating turbulence.
The
Navier-Stokes equation model is one popular solution to modeling boundary layers.
For the free surface flow, there are three approaches to modeling the surface effect:
• Interface fitting;
• Interface tracking; and
• Interface Capturing
All of these methods are solving another transport equation to describe the position
of the free surface. While the latter two method methods are more robust by simu-
lating the fluid flow on both sides of the interface, it introduces extra computational
cost. Since in most of the application applications in the ocean and offshore engi-
neering, the benefit of simulating the breaking wave effect does not outweigh the
computational performance, the current study only focuses on the interface fitting
method.
Li et. al. [3] implemented the three-dimensional free surface wave model by us-
ing the projection method and the solver was accelerated by the multigrid method.
However, this model is based on a low-order finite difference method, the truncation
error of which could have non-negligible order of magnitude that could result in
large dissipation and dispersion error when long-time integration is required. This
is why the high-order method shows its advantage. Max et. al. [1] developed a
two-dimensional spectral method version of [3]. However, in the real Navier-Stokes
scheme, because the viscosity term is getting prohibitively stiff if the requirement
of the grid resolution in the boundary layer is high, explicit time integration of [3]
and [1] will be impractical. Besides, even for the non-stiff whereas the simulation
case is a large scale which is the often case in marine engineering, classical high-
order Runge-Kutta time integration of [1] would be memory-hungry. Therefore, in
the current study, low-storage Runge-Kutta fourth-order five-stage time integration
[19] is implemented for explicit time integration, and the backward-Euler method is
implemented for implicit time integration.
So far, although abundant models have been developed to accelerate the simu-
lation of potential wave models such as Allen et. al. [9], no existing literature men-
tioned the multigrid method for the spectral Navier-Stokes solver in hydrodynamics
application, which has the matrix of dense non-symmetric, non-positive definite
equations. Therefore, in addition to the spectral Navier-Stokes model, to speed up
the computation performance of the dense, non-symmetric, and non-positive definite

matrics, which is resulted from the discretization pressure Poisson equation of im-
plemented spectral free surface Navier-Stokes solver, an efficient p-multigrid method
based on matrix-free strategy is developed and is combined with Krylov subspace
method to accelerate the computation significantly and lower the required storage
from O((NxNz)2) to O(NxNz) and it achieves the optimal scalability of O(N 2) in
both horizontal and vertical direction. The smoother of the p-multigrid is based on
Gauss-Seidel iteration. A block Gauss-Seidel method is developed to improve the
smoothing efficiency.

Chapter 2
Mathematical Model of Free
surface Flow
The most ubiquitous phenomenon in fluid flow that occurs in the natural en-
vironment is the free surface wave and turbulent flow.
The investigation of the
boundary layer is important in the sediment transport process at the river and sea
bed and especially near the surf zone where the water wave is significantly influ-
enced by the rapidly changing seabed geometry. Although the bulk region beneath
the nonbreaking waves could be justifiably described by the potential flow model, it
fails to resolve the characteristics of the wave boundary layer. Therefore, in appli-
cation where the boundary layer detail is of interesting to study, the Navier-Stokes
model should be applied.
2.1
Free surface Navier-Stokes equation
For almost all free surface flow, the flow can be considered as incompressible
flow. The free surface treatment of the current study is the surface fitting, in which
the surface is explicitly tracked and the position of the free surface is governed by
a transport equation. The limitation of this surface treatment is that it fails to
describe the wave overturning. However, the assumption of assuming wave over-
turning free is reasonable for describing the flow which is not in the sloshing region.
Besides, the free surface model that is capable of describing the wave overtuning
such as volume of fluid and level-set method will either increases the computational
cost of the model since the fluid in the two sides of the free surface interface should
be computed and the mesh should be in high resolution at the interface region to
avoid numerical dissipation, or makes the implementation much more difficult. Be-
sides, the discontinuity at the interface poses another difficulty under the framework
of spectral method. Therefore, the surface fitting method is the best choice in the
current study for treating the free surface.
In the surface fitting method, the divison of the pressure term in the momentum
equation could make the modeling and computation more convenient. The total

3.2

method of lines, where the model differential equation is semi-discretized in which
only the spacial term is discretized, the partial differential equations are therefore
transferred to systems of ordinary differential equations:

Solution procedure
Therefore, the explicit Navier-Stokes equations can be solved by the following
summarized procedure
1. Obtained the initial value for velocity field u, w, and free surface position η;
2. Update the free surface position by Equation (3.1.4a);
3. Solve for the pressure field at the current time step implicitly by Equation (3.2.8);
4. Update the velocity field for the next time step by Equation (3.1.4c) and
Equation (3.1.4d).

High-Order Spectral Method for
Solving Differential Equation
In this chapter, the single domain spectral method is present where the Fourier
basis and polynomial basis that serves as the fundamental numerical scheme for the
incompressible Navier-Stokes equation are derived and how the model and the nodal
expansion could be used for the construction of differential matrix and mass matrix
which is then the fundamental of pseudo-spectral collocation method. In the hori-
zontal direction, the Fourier-type operator is adopted to exploit the effectiveness of
the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) because the problem can be defined as periodic
problems by tricks; In the vertical direction, the Jacobi-type operator is adopted.
The spectral method shares high accuracy and computational efficiency because
it takes advantage of orthogonality. Therefore, contrary to the traditional finite
difference method, finite volume method, and finite element method which has an
algebraic rate of convergence as the spatial resolution is refined, the spectral method
has an exponential rate of convergence until machine error.
Furthermore, the filter is presented to mitigate the instability due to the aliasing
error resulting from nonlinear convolution.

If the direct solver is applied to

solver. In addition, the difference in hydrodynamic pressure between the last time

from the previous two time step can be used as the initial guess of the pressure field
of the current time step, so the convergence is faster.

Krylov subspace iterative method
The Krylov subspace methods are robust methods for solving the system of
equations
Ax = b,

Assuming A is invertible. The Krylov subspace of rank m of the problem is spanned
by the set of column vectors which is written as
Km(A, b) = span{b, Ab, A2b ..., Am−1b}
(5.2.2)
The idea of the Krylov subspace method is to transform an approximately equiva-
lent system of equations of size m with m ≪n, in which the column spaces of the
new system of equations are orthonormal to each other. Therefore, the new sys-
tem can be solved much more efficiently by exploiting the orthogonality property.
The Krylov subspace method searches for an approximate solution ˆx within this
Krylov subspace, which is based on the fact that the solution of the linear system of
equations a is represented naturally by the Krylov subspace so that the linear com-
bination coefficient of each orthonormal vector decreases relatively rapidly. On the
other hand, the Krylov subspace method does not require the A matrix to be explic-
itly known, but the matrix-vector product Ab. In the current study, this property

of the direct method to O(M × N), and the computational complexity of it is re-

method to O(Nlog(N) × Mlog(M)) since the fast Fourier method (FFT) and the
fast Chebyshev method (FCT) are applied in the horizontal and vertical direction,
respectively.

Biconjugate gradient stabilized method
A variant of the conjugate gradient method that could be generalized to nonsym-
metric systems of equations is the biconjugate gradient stabilized method [12]. In
this method, the residual of each iteration step rj is required to be orthogonal to a
different space ATKj(AT) instead of Kj(A) in the conjugate gradient method. This
method has faster and smoother convergence than both the conjugate gradient and
the original biconjugate gradient method. The idea behind the biconjugate gradient
method is to use two different sequences of residuals, in which they are conjugate to
each other. The stabilized version of the biconjugate gradient method is to include
an additional stabilization process in each iteration. The biconjugate gradient sta-
bilized method has better performance for solving systems with ill-conditioned and
non-symmetric metrics, which means this method is more suitable for linear systems
of equations studied in the current study.

5.3
Discussion of Krylov subspace method
The conjugate gradient method for non-symmetric, non-positive definite met-
rics, which are the cases of the current study, diverges most of the time. In the
numerical experiment, the conjugate gradient method generally converges until
Nx × Nz = 2 × 2. The biconjugate gradient stabilized method behaves much better
than the conjugate gradient method and converges until the simulation case reaches
about Nx × Nz = 16 × 16 with a much better rate of convergence than the con-
jugate gradient method. Both the conjugate gradient method and the biconjugate
gradient staggered method do not guarantee convergence and diverge for slightly
large problems, which makes the pure conjugate gradient type method useless. The
GMRES method does guarantee that the divergence could be avoided, and it natu-
rally has the maximum iteration step which is the size of the matrix, while GMRES
suffers from slow convergence when the system is ill-conditioned. As a consequence,
the method requires a stringent storage requirement. Although there is a restart
version of GMRES where the number of orthonormal vectors of Krylov subspace is
restricted, and the algorithm restarts the Krylov subspace after a certain number of
iterations, selecting the latest approximation as the initial guess and constructing
a new orthonormal basis. But treatment might not only increase the iteration to
convergence.
The convergence history of three Krylov subspace methods for the test case
Nx × Nz = 2 × 2 is shown in Figure 5.12. It is observed that the conjugate gradient
method has the worst performance compared to GMRES and the biconjugate gradi-
ent stabilized method. Besides, the conjugate gradient method shows a highly high
oscillation, which indicates that it is highly unstable and prompts us to mention
being diverged. It is worth mentioning that even for such a small case, the GMRES
and biconjugate gradient stabilized method still takes around ten iterations to reach
a reasonable tolerance. Such a slow convergence is unacceptable in real applications.
To solve this problem, one can resort to preconditioning.

Spectral Multigrid Method
As mentioned in the preceding section, the error component with the highest
wave number is damped out the fastest in the Gauss-Seidel method. For the error
component with low wave number numbers, which is called the smooth error, the
Gauss-Seidel method slowly removes them. The main task of accelerating the con-
vergence of the iterative solver could be to eliminate the error component of a low
wave number. The largest wave number on the computational grid is described by
its minimum grid size. Therefore, the error component of low wave numbers in fine
grids can be represented by the error component of high wave numbers in coarse
grids, which could be eliminated efficiently given that the coarse problem can be
solved much faster. The multigrid algorithm exploits this property which solves the
system of equations by keeping transferring the residual from fine grids to coarse
grids and efficiently eliminating the error of the error equations by a few smoothing
of the Gauss-Seidel method at each grid level until reaching the coarsest grid level,
and consequently, interpolating the error from the from coarse grids to fine grid and
followed by post smoothing of a few iterations of Gauss-Seidel method. Above pro-
cedure completes one iteration of the multigrid cycle. [16] generally introduces the
idea of spectral multigrid without specific detail. To make the idea of the spectral
multigrid method suitable for the free surface Navier-Stokes wave model, in this
chapter, an efficient spectral multigrid is implemented.
6.1
Spectral multigrid vs classic multigrid
The basic idea behind the spectral multigrid and classic multigrid is the same,
which efficiently removes the smooth error by transferring residuals to different grid
levels. The transferring process for the spectral discretization scheme, which is a
global scheme, is much different from the low-order discretization schemes, which are
local schemes. Because the interpolation should preserve the high-order accuracy of
the spectral method (otherwise the spectral method will lose its significance), the
spectral interpolation has to be used for the interpolation. Besides, unlike the classic
low-order method the operator of the spectral method is not fixed, the generation of
which is by a black-box process. To illustrate this difference, here the discretization

6.2
Multigrid structure
The general structure of the multigrid method is presented as follows, taking
four levels of grid hierarchy as an example
• Smoothing: a few iterations of the Gauss-Seidel method, which takes advan-
tage of the property that the Gauss-Seidel method eliminates error components
with high wave numbers rapidly in the first few iterations.
• Residual Computation: computing residual after smoothing R = f −AU
• Residual Transferring (Restriction): transfer the residual down to the coarser
grid.
• Error Interpolation (Prolongation): Interpolating the error computed on coarser
grids into finer grids.
• Correction: subtract the error from the approximate solution.
• Post-smoothing: A few iterations of the Gauss-Seidel method.
==PAGE:55==
6.2. MULTIGRID STRUCTURE
50
After the smoothing process, the residual of the smoothed procedure is interpolated
to the next finer grid, and the error equation Ae = R is smoothed. This restriction
process is carried out until the coarsest level of the grid is reached. The solution
of the error e at the coarsest grid level is solved exactly by either direct solver or
iterative solver. Consequently, the error e is interpolated back to the next finer grid
until the upper bound of the fine grid is reached. During the prolongation, the error
is subtracted from the intermediate solution to correct the solution closer to the true
solution. The above steps complete one multigrid iteration.
It is noticeable that the interpolation method applied in p-multigrid is the spec-
tral interpolation method described in Section 4.4 instead of doing a linear averaging
as is adopted in the multigrid method of the low-order method since the linear in-
terpolation will destroy the fast convergence of the spectral accuracy. Taking the
grid levels of Ω4p = 32, Ω2p = 16, Ωp = 8 for the horizontal and Fourier grids and the
vertical Chebyshev grid as an example, which is shown in Figure 6.1. It is depicted
that, unlike the classical geometrical multigrid, the coarser grid is not the subset of
the fine grid, but the Chebyshev and Fourier grid with a different order for the sake
of stability and the avoiding ill-conditioned resulted matrix.

guess.

be explained by calculating the cost of the full multigrid method. The cost of a

2D wave over a submerged bar
In real engineering applications, the bottom of the water floor is commonly not
flat. The test case is the wave over a submerged bar which was originally proposed by
Luth et. al (1993) [8], and it is widely used as the benchmark test of an implemented
wave model. The schematics are as Figure 7.8 shows, the still water height is 0.4
m, the length of the experiment is 25 m, and the four vertexes of the trapezoid
submerged bar are located at (6, −0.4) m, (12, −0.1) m, (14, −0.1) m and (17, −0.4)
m. The dimensionless depth of the generated single waveform is kh = 0.67, the
wave height is H = 0.02 m, and the wave period is T = 2.02 s. Therefore, the
resulting wavelength is L = 3.75 m. The linearized small amplitude potential wave
theory is applied as an exact generation function, but as the wave propagates, it
will automatically become the nonlinear Stokes wave.
Figure 7.8: Schematics of the wave over a submerged bar
The resolution has to be carefully chosen to obtain a reasonable simulation re-
sult. Besides, filtering is indispensable for the stability of the numerical scheme,
since the wavelength becomes smaller in the region of the plateau of the submerged
bar, and physical quantity fluctuation in the wake region after the flow passes the
submerged bar becomes more intensive, resulting in the erroneous numerical fluctu-
ation. Therefore, the numerical scheme is vulnerable to instability. The numerical
experiment indicates that simulation with an order of resolution Nx = 500 in the
horizontal direction and Nz = 15 in the vertical direction gives an accurate predic-
tion, as Figure 7.9 shows.
The Navier-Stokes solver and Euler solver are tested against the experimental
results. After the effect of the initially generated waves disappears, the simulation
results of the two solvers show good agreement with the experiment. It could be
concluded that for a low Re laminar wave flow over a submerged bar, the viscous
effect is negligible and the viscosity dissipation term can be eliminated to reduce
computational cost and improve numerical stability.

Chapter 8
Conclusions and Future Works
This chapter lists the achievement of the current study and the future study that
is based on the current study.
8.1
Conclusion
In the current study, a two-dimensional free surface Navier-Stokes solver based
on spectral method with explicit fourth-order low storage Runge-Kutta and implicit
first-order backward Euler as time integration method is developed. Results show
high accuracy in simulating complex seabed geometry (without submerged objects)
by the validation of Luth’s experiment [8]. It has high performance in terms of
keeping low dispersion and dissipation error. To accelerate the computation, an effi-
cient p-multigrid is developed, which achieves the optimal scalability of O(N 2) and
is accelerated by full multigrid. It is worth mentioning that when the ratio Nx/Nz
is large, V-cycle multigrid structure is the best choice since it has the scalability
O(Nlog(N)). The smoother of the relaxation step is improved by using the block
Gauss-Seidel method, which has a much better smoothing ability. However, in the
numerical experiment, it is discovered that the pure multigrid method sometimes
fails to converge to the desired accuracy, with a relative residual of less than 1e −8.
Therefore, the p-multigrid method is combined with the Krylov subspace method
to keep the robustness. It is concluded that the full multigrid preconditioned GM-
RES is the most robust method, while the full multigrid preconditioned biconjugate
gradient stabilized method can be the fastest method for certain cases.
8.2
Future work
This subsection lists some valuable studies that are based on the current study.
• Implementation of the high-order time integration method of the
implicit free surface Navier-Stokes model:
The model will be unstable when the linear multistep method (Adam-Moulton)
is used as implicit time integration.
The instability is from the Neumann

boundary condition of pressure Poisson equation [5]. This instability can be
circumvented by writing the Navier-Stokes equation in the skew-symmetric
form [6]. However, the σ coordinates pose difficulty in doing so because the
vertical velocity of the convection term has a different physical meaning and
dimension.
• Fix the SIMPLE and PISO algorithm for Navier-Stokes:
The SIMPLE and PISO method is the most popular velocity-pressure cou-
pling method because of their relatively cheap computational cost and is more
suitable for RANS turbulence modeling. However, in the current study, the
implemented SIMPLE and PISO solver fail to converge.
• RANS turbulence modeling:
RANS turbulence modeling is important for sediment transport study in in-
dustry and is relatively computationally expansive. The spectral method has
the potential to improve the efficiency of the RANS model.
• Extension to three dimensional:
Most of the flow phenomenon is three-dimensional, and the currently developed
two-dimensional has very limited application in real engineering problems.
• Spectral element solver:
Although the current Navier-Stokes solver is geometric flexible, it is not ca-
pable to solve the flow with objects submerged in the water.
Because the
single-domain spectral method will experience a strong Gibbs phenomenon if
there is a submerged object in the middle of the water, the multi-domain spec-
tral method or discontinuity spectral element method can solve this problem.
• Improvement of the multigrid:
According to Heinrichs [18], the algebraic multigrid method has theoretically
much better performance than geometric multigrid.
However, the classical
algebraic multigrid method [17] which is implemented by the author and is
validated to be correct by a matrix of the system resulting from a low-order
discretized elliptical PDE problem, fails to converge. Future work could de-
velop an algebraic multigrid method that could solve the problem of the current
study.
• Parallelization of the Navier-Stokes solver:
Parallelization of the solver is indispensable for large problems since the de-
gree of freedom could be the magnitude of at least a million, which means
serial computing will be super slow. The currently developed model can be
translated to high-performance code such as C++, Fortran, Java, and Julia
and then parallelize the code to speed up the computation.
