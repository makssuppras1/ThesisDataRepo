1 Introduction 
1.1 
Rain Erosion Issues 
During their operational lifespan, wind turbine blades are subjected to a range of atmospheric and 
environmental conditions. Inspection reports from wind farms with blades in operation for several years reveal 
varying degrees of leading-edge erosion. Erosion typically initiates as formation of small pits near the leading 
edge, which progressively enlarge and coalesce and leading into further damages such as cracking, 
delamination in the regions near the leading edge. 
The results of change of aerodynamic profile of the blade can lead to AEP loss. According to the literature and 
several experiments conducted in the industry, the range of this loss is estimated to be between 1 to 6% as

2 Erosion Mechanisms and Theory 
The rain erosion theoretical and experimental development is stemmed from Springer - 1976 - Erosion by 
Liquid Impact. [1]  
The main idea in Springer theory of liquid erosion defined as “weight loss of a material subjected to repeated 
impingements of liquid droplets varies with time”. 
According to Springer, the liquid erosion process is influenced by 4 major groups of parameters 
a) 
liquid related parameters (density, velocity of a compressive wave, viscosity, surface tension) 
b) 
rain parameters (shape, size of the droplet and their distribution, concentration) 
c) 
impact parameters (velocity and angle of incidence) 
d) 
solid parameters (density, velocities of the compression and shear waves, modulus of elasticity, 
Poisson's ratio, endurance limit, ultimate tensile strength, compressive and shear strength, fracture 
toughness, hardness, grain size, surface roughness, curvature of surface, thickness 
It is apparent already here that these parameters require a significant amount of testing on various materials. 
The simplest way is experiment and Springer charted out the 3 categories of stages as illustrated in Figure 
2. 
Initially for some period of time (referred to as the incubation period), the weight loss is insignificant. For some 
time after the incubation period the rate of weight loss is nearly constant and the weight loss varies almost 
linearly with time, designated as the "steady rate erosion" region. Past this region the relationship between 
the weight loss and the exposure time is known to be more complex. 
But driven by accelerated experimental test methods, Springer’s approach is to translate exposure time into 
total volume (or mass) of liquid impinged per unit area, or with the number of impacts per unit area “n”, which 
can be controlled in a test environment.

2.2 
Theory of Erosion by Impact 
The theories and ideas of present-day testing stemmed from the idea of Springer model. Therefore, it is good 
to make a brief explanation of the model so that it can be related to current-day testing. For clarity and not 
mixing with current day terminology, the exact symbol or figure reference is made from Springer’s book. 
2.2.1 Springer Model 
The core of the model presented by Springer for erosion of homogeneous material is presented here. It 
explains a liquid drop impinges on a solid substrate and the assumptions made to simplify the problem. This 
process is explained briefly in this picture. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 3: Impact of a liquid drop on an elastic surface (ref. 
Illustration Fig 1-13 from Springer book page 21/264) 
==PAGE:12==

When a liquid droplet impinges upon a solid surface, the droplet comes to a sudden stop (Figure 3) 
The sudden deceleration causes a pressure build-up at the liquid-solid interface. The high pressure inside the 
droplet then forces the liquid to escape laterally. Damage is produced by both high pressure at the liquid-solid 
interface and by the lateral outflow of the liquid.  
This normal force by the high-pressure impingement generates stress waves traveling along the surface and 
inside the material. The changes in the stresses across these waves and the corresponding changes in the 
normal and shear forces are responsible for some of the material damage. As the precise mode of material 
failure is unknown, it considers the normal force as the major contributor of the damage. The jets of liquid 
squirting out laterally from the droplet further may also impose high shear stresses along the surface thereby 
creating further damage. But that part is not easy to quantify and thus Springer proposed to assume that to 
consider the problem 
- one dimensional 
- later outflow to be negligible 
- normal impact pressure is uniform and constant across the area of impact

The approach of the area growth analysis is based on the following ground rules 
 
the defect must be singular and count only until it interacts with adjacent defect 
 
the defect must be “Breakthrough to substrate – cohesive failure” as defined in ref. [12] 
 
avoid the point if the failure is driven by peeling effect than the impact by droplets (i.e., either 
“Breakthrough to substrate – adhesive failure” or “Failure before incubation - failure at n to n-1 layer 
interface” as defined in ref. [12]) 
 
the defect center is taken as the representative point for velocity of impact related to that defect 
 
area growth rate is taken as straight-line slope between the two points in steady loss region on Area 
Vs Total Impingement graph.(i.e., after “End of Incubation - Initial material removal” and before 
“ Breakthrough of substrate” as defined in ref. [12])

6 Learning Points from this Thesis 
This section is prepared to note down the experiences and ideas gathered during the annotation process. 
6.1 
Singular approach – Cluster approach 
This was an idea that due to rapid growth of damage in high speed tests, quickly merging, resulting few 
datapoints to analyse the growth rate. In addition, as the incubation occurs simultaneously and closely to each 
other, i.e., the impact velocities on these cluster of spots do not vary much. 
Then the idea of annotating them together as a single cluster so that the growth spots are followed as 
combined area and thus more growth steps can be annotated rather than singular approach where the 
annotation is stopped whenever a singular spot connected with nearby ones. It is illustrated as in the figure 
below.  
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 41: Cluster annotation approach idea 
 
However, the plotted data shows many anomalies when singular and clusters area growth are put together. It 
might be due to that the area growth rate for multiple incubation turns out to be double, trippled or quadruple 
in the area growth compared to singular spot. 
6.2 
Early or false incubation 
This is another pitfall when annotating with a fixed approach of annotating every single dot. It turns out that 
those appear early incubation process are more related to coating application defects. It is sometimes not that 
obvious but they turn out to be outliers in dataset and always lead to wrong fitting curves. But they do have a 
particular characteristic such as they appear earlier but grow very slow or almost not growing at all until some 
points in the test. This is understood simply as like inclusion or holiday in a coating layer, which is exposed 
quickly in the early stage but stop growing. Yet after a certain amount of impingement, they grow again possible 
due to more cracking inwards the pits.’ 
==PAGE:47==
Quantification of erosion in whirling arm rain erosion test                                                        s214028
 
 
 
45 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The same exclusion should be considered for those spots lying outside the leading edge of the blade, i.e., 
outside the two white lines. It is also due to the reason that those areas are not with the ultimate impact 
scenario and the failure or incubation should not start from those areas. If they appear in the early stage of 
the test, it is very likely that it is due to inherent coating defect. Pre-rejection of those outlier points will also 
save time in annotation process. 
6.3 
Area Growth Rate per Impingement H 
In calculating the area growth rate per impingement as demonstrated in section 5.1.2, 5.2.2, 5.3.2 and also in 
5.3.3 for estimation for coating repair life, it was discussed that due to the early false incubation phenomenon 
explained in section 6.2, the area growth rate can be varied depending on the data points chosen.  
For this thesis work, the criteria is to calculate the growth rate in this steady loss zone, as illustrated again 
here below (Figure 43). This choice is because of the interest of the paint repair campaign planning and to 
predict average performance of the coating after incubation.  
Considering the performance of a coating, every coating will have imperfections, so incubation will also occur 
on those local sites, relatively quickly at low total impingement H values due to breakup of pinholes, voids or 
holiday in coating. These were proposed to be named as “False incubation in section 6.2. If the incubations 
for those false ones are taken for the estimation repair time frame, it would also be over conservative. On the 
bright side, most of these imperfections, despite breaking up quickly at low total impingement, the growth of 
damage is usually stagnated like normal good coating spots and thus doesn’t impact much about area growth 
calculation. Yet they will dominate in estimating time to incubate, may still impact the coating repair estimation.  
Likewise, if the incubation region data points are used for area growth rate calculation, it might end up with 
very optimistic rates as area growth in that early stage can be very insignificant and might end up 
overestimating the coating durability as these rates will not be reflecting steady loss region.
