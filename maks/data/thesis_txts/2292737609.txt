II
Summary (English)
This study aimed at implementing a picoinjection module as a link in an existing microdroplets
system. This involved chip design, in which several parameters had to be optimized in order
to have a stable system, and it involved exploration of a large parameter space regarding
stable operation of the chip. This was done in order to extend the current toolbox with a way
of adding reagents to preformed droplets at high speed (>100 Hz). The addition of reagents
is very important in order to perform multistep reactions that requires incubation between
reaction steps.
A successful injection chip has been shown, which consists of a reinjection module, optical
ﬁbers for droplet detection, on chip micro-electrodes, a pressure regulated injection channel,
a mixing channel and outlet. Electrodes are used to temporarily destabilize the otherwise
surfactant stabilized droplets in order for them to take up liquid from the injection channel.
The electrodes are triggered by a signal produced by refracted and scattered laser light,
which is transported to and from the chip by optical ﬁbers, which is inserted into designated
channels on the chip - a feature not yet seen in the literature. This is a large step towards
miniaturization, as it relieves the use of heavy, expensive microscopes.
It has been shown that injection can be successfully triggered by the optical signal, and that
injections are highly controllable. This is done by showing injection of every other drop in
a pattern rather than injection of every drop. It was also shown that triggering could be
based on cells encapsulated in the droplets. It was seen that the chip was very sensitive to
pressure ﬂuctuations, but this could be partly suppressed by pressurizing the chip. Without
pressurizing the chip, the injected volumes varied with 17-38%, which was reduced to 9-18%
when pressurizing the chip. Even though injected volumes varied, it was seen that the average
volume injected was independent on how many droplets were skipped between injections.
In order for the picoinjector to perform stably over extended periods of time, it is suggested
that a pressure ventilation channel is implemented in the chip. This channel consists of a
forked oriﬁce, where thin channel slits prevent droplets from entering the channel, but allows
oil to pass, balancing ﬂuctuations in pressure, for example caused by pulsation of the ﬂow
from syringe pumps.
Once stability is achieved, the injection module can easily be incorporated in screens, for
example as a method of passive sorting, reagent addition or dilution of reactions.

III
Summary (Danish)
Dette studie søgte at implementere et pico-injektionsmodul som en del af et eksisterende
mikro-dråbe system. Dette involverede chip design, hvor ﬂere parametre måtte optimeres for
at have et stabilt system og derefter undersøgelse af et stort parameter-rum for at opnå stabil
betjening af chippen. Dette er med til at udvide den nuværende dråbe-værktøjskasse med
en måde at tilføje nye reagenser til allerede genererede dråber med høj hastighed (>100 Hz).
Tilføjelse af nye reagenter er essentiel for at udføre ﬂertrins reaktioner, hvor inkubation er
nødvendig imellem reaktionstrinnene.
En successfuld injektions-chip er blevet implementeret, hvilken består af et modul der kan
genindføre dråber, optiske ﬁbre til at detektere dråberne, chip implementerede mikroelek-
troder, en trykreguleret injektions kanal, en zig-zag kanal til at fremme blanding af reagenserne
og et outlet til genopsamling af dråberne. Elektroderne er nødvendige for midlertidigt at
destabilisere dråberne, der ellers er stabiliserede ved hjælp af et overﬂadebehandlingsmiddel,
så de kan forenes med væsken i injektionskanalen. Elektroderne bliver aktiveret af et signal
produceret af brudt og spredt laser lys, der bliver transporteret til og fra chippen af optiske
ﬁbre, der indsættes manuelt i chippen gennem dertil indrettede kanaler - en funktion endnu
ikke set i litteraturen. Dette er det store skridt imod miniaturisering, da det erstatter den
funktion der normalt er båret af tunge, dyre mikroskoper.
Det er blevet vist, at injektion kan blive successfuldt aktiveret af et optisk signal, og at
injetionerne kan kontrolleres. Dette er vist ved at injicere hveranden dråde i et møsnter i
stedet for injicering af hver eneste dråbe. Det er også blevet vist at injektion kan aktiveres
baseret på det optiske signal fra en celle der er enkapsuleret i dråberne. Chippen var dog følsom
overfor ﬂuktuationer i trykket i chippen, hvilken kun kunne delvist afhælpes ved at tryksætte
hele chippen ved hjælp af en tryk pumpe på udgangen. Uden tryksætningen varierede den
injicerede volumen med 17-38%, hvilket blev reduceret til 9-18% når chippen blev sat under
tryk. På trods af at det injicerede volumen varierede, kunne der ikke ses betydelig forskel på
det gennemsnitlige volumen injiceret om hver dråbe blev injiceret eller kun nogle dråber blev.
For at picoinjektionen kan udføres stabilt over længere tid, bliver der foreslået at implementere
en tryk ventilerings-kanal på chippen. Sådan en kanal består af en forgrenet munding, hvor
tynde kanaler ikke tillader dråber at komme igennem, men som tillader den omkringliggende
oile at passere, hvilket vil balancere ﬂuktuationer i trykket, der for eksempel kan fremkomme
af pulserende ﬂow genereret af sprøjtepumperne.
Når stabilitet er opnået, kan injektionsmodulet nemt indgå i screens, for eksempel som en
passiv sorterings metode, tilføjelse af reagenser eller som fortynding af reaktioner.

IV
Preface
This thesis was made at the department of Micro- and Nanotechnology at the Technical
University of Denmark in the Fluidic Array Systems and Technology group, in collaboration
with Symphogen A/S in fulﬁlment of the requirements for acquiring a M.Sc. in Physics and
Nanotechnology. The work presented here is carried out over 6 months (corresponding to
35 ECTS points) in the autumn 2014 and winter 2015. Associate professor Martin Dufva
supervised the project together with Thomas Glasdam Jensen and Jenifer Clausell-Tormos
(Symphogen A/S).
The study was somewhat a part of the Immune antibody repertoires in microdroplets (ARiM)
project, which is funded by Innovationsfonden.
The thesis aims to implement a picoinjection module which will increase the current mi-
crodroplets tool box currently available in the project, which implies designing a chip from
scratch and exploring a large parameter space regarding the operation of the device.
The thesis contains four chapters, in which the subject is introduced, experimental methods
described and results are presented and discussed in relation to current literature on the
subject. The thesis is rounded oﬀby putting the injection module into broader perspectives
and suggesting further work on the device.
Lyngby, 30.01.2015
Marie Louise Laub Busk

Introduction
1.1
High Throughput Screening and Droplet Microfluidics
1.1.1
High Throughput Screening
High Throughput screening (HTS) is deﬁned as the process of running multiple (thousands of)
reactions in a short period of time. Currently multiwell plates, in addition with automated
pipetting by robots, are considered the golden standard in commercial HTS, and screen-
ing rates can reach approximately 1 compound per second (100.000 compounds/day)[1, 2, 3].
Multiwell plates are small plastic trays with small wells in which multiple reactions can be
perfomed in parallel. The ﬁrst use of multiwell plates dates back to the 1950’s, where Hun-
garian Doctor Gyola Takatsy machined 100-well plates in order to overcome the cumbersome
work with common test tubes during a severe inﬂuenza outbreak[4]. Already in the late ’50’s
and early ’60’s, the ﬁrst 96 multiwell plates became commercially available as John Liner
and Nelson Cooke put the plates on the market, and in 1968 Cooke Labs, USA issued the
patent and trademark for the Microtiter® plate[4]. During the 1990’s plates with up to 9600
wells were produced, and in 2000 approximately 125 million multiwell plates were used world
wide[4]. One ﬁeld where HTS is a necessity, is in lead drug discovery[2]. The current trend in
drug discovery is that only 1 in every 10 drugs that enter clinical trials are approved. The clin-
ical trials are the most expensive parts of drug discovery, and thus improving the proportion
of drugs that become approved after entering clinical trials are much sought after[5]. One way
of improving the rate, is to improve the quality of the primary screens. There are two main
targets of screening; Binding and Function, and absorption, distribution, metabolism and
excretion/Toxicity (ADME/Tox) screening[5]. Improving screening for function will provide
more potential drug candidates, whereas ADME/Tox, screens will remove toxic (or candidates
that do not absorb/distribute/metabolize or excrete well), that would have been otherwise
rejected during pre-clinical or clinical trials, which are the most expensive parts of drug devel-
opment. The ADME/Tox screens are typically diﬃcult and time consuming, and require high
content screening rather than high throughput screening[3]. Improving screening for function
is already well underway, as HTS has already gone from 96-well plates to 1536-well plates,
an evolution that reduced the cost of a typical screen from an estimated $35 million to $1.1
million[3]. The savings come from a 16-fold increase in well density, a 100-fold decrease in
working volumes and a 50-fold decrease in storage space requirement[2]. Some of the issues
that has been experienced going even smaller than the 1536-well plate includes; Evaporation,
mixing, dispensing very small volumes accurately and cross contamination as capillary eﬀects
dominate at small length scales[1]. One technology that shows promising features to overcome
these limitations, and take the multiwell plates to the next level, is droplet microﬂuidics.
1.1.2
Droplet Microﬂuidics
The basic formulation of the concept of microﬂuidics, is the ﬂow of ﬂuids in systems on the
submillimeter scale that are inﬂuenced by external forces, for example pressure diﬀerences or
pumping[6], and is a vast, multidisciplinary ﬁeld that stretches from physics into chemistry
and biology. Maybe the greatest achievement in the ﬁeld of microﬂuidics is the appearance
==PAGE:20==
2
Introduction
of lab-on-a-chip systems that aim to develop systems of entire bio-/chemical laboratories on
a single silicon or polymer chip[6].
The branch of microﬂuidics called droplet-based- or two-phase microﬂuidics, involves the
encapsulation of aqueous solutions in an innert, immiscible carrier ﬂuid, typically an oil. These
drops, (also called droplets or microdroplets) that range from femto- to nanoliters in volume,
each constitute a reaction chamber separated by the immiscible carrier ﬂuid[7].
Droplets
are generated at kHz rates[8] and can be made with a very narrow range of volumes (1-3%
polydispersity)[7], hence very monodisperse emulsions can be generated quickly. Typically the
droplets are stabilized by addition of a surfactant to the carrier ﬂuid, which allows the droplets
to be stored and incubated oﬀchip without coalescing, even at elevated temperatures. The
strength of droplet microﬂuidics is the ability to manipulate and detect single droplets on chip
at high speed. Some of the functions that have already been applied on chip includes: rapid
mixing of reagents (ms time scales[9, 10]), splitting and fusion of droplets[11, 12, 13], sorting
based on size or ﬂuorescence signals[14, 15], as well as injection of additional reagents without
breaking the emulsion[16, 17], all at kilohertz rates. This is depicted in Figure 1.1.

Another advantage of droplet microﬂuidics is that single cells can be encapsulated and survive

cells in the aqueous solution. More on this topic follows in chapter 2. This is rather ineﬃ-

to bias the stochastic nature of the encapsulation, for example by hydrodynamic ordering
of the cells[8, 19]. Many biological assays have already been shown in droplets, for example:
directed evolution for enzyme improvement[20], enzyme kinetic assays[21], reverse transcrip-

screening of Ab producing primary cells[23], protein expression detection[24], immunoassays[25],
and much more.
Some of the positive eﬀects that might be connected with miniaturization includes: better
signal to noise ratio, less reagent consumption (thus reduced price), shorter reaction times due
to shorter diﬀusion distances, lower detection limits, etc. This all originates from the small
reaction volumes, which increases concentrations of reagents, reduces diﬀusion distances and
so forth [26]. One of the current limitations to droplet microﬂuidics is the inability to handle
heterogeneous assays, such as cell secretion assays that require multiple washing steps. There
is currently no existing solution to removing buﬀer from compartmentalized cells and adding
new buﬀer [26], though several methods are currently being explored.
1.1.3
Adding Reagents in Multistep Reactions in Droplets
Many reactions require the addition of reagents at diﬀerent time points. In droplet microﬂu-
idics, there are two ways of adding reagents without breaking the emulsion: fusion of droplets
and picoinjection[16]. Fusion of droplets can be both passively or actively induced, where pas-
sive fusion relies on channel geometry to slow down drops momentarily to make them bump
into each other, and active fusion destabilizes the droplets using an electric ﬁeld[13]. Since
the generation of droplets is very controlled, the volume of added reagent is also very well
deﬁned, and can be as small as picoliter or femtoliter volumes, a feature that is impossible
to achieve with conventional pipetting[1]. The disadvantage of droplet fusion is that the two
streams of droplets to be fused must be synchronized in order to correctly and reproducibly
fuse the droplets, and if more that one reagent is to be added, synchronizing multiple streams
of droplets becomes increasingly challenging[16]. This can be done, but is cumbersome work.
The picoinjector overcomes this disadvantage by adding the reagents from a reservoir channel
perpendicular to the channel in which the droplets travel (the main channel). Due to the
surfactant, droplets will not take up any reagents from the picoinjection channel, unless the
interface is disrupted. This is done by applying a very localized electric ﬁeld just before the
injection nozzle. By controlling the injection pressure and drop velocity, the injected volume
can be varied with sub-picoliter precision[16]. A comprehensive description of the picoinjector
is given in chapter 2. The electric ﬁeld can be triggered by optical- or ﬂuorescent detection
of droplets, or maybe even on signal from contents of the droplets (for example encapsulated
cells). In this way the drops that are injected can be selectively chosen, thus minimizing
reagent consumption. Picoinjection has been achieved at kilohertz rates[16], and is thus 1000
times faster than pipetting microtiter plates, making this technology ideal for screening large
libraries of biological or chemical compounds. The picoinjector can be used to great advan-
tages, particularly in cell-based assays, where reagents toxic to the cell needs to be added
after an incubation step. This could for example be the addition of a marker or a stain that
would disrupt the cell integrity if incubated with the cell for extended periods of time, or
stains that will bleach if incubated for too long.
1.1.4
Antibodies as Cancer Therapy and Combinatorial Drug Therapy
One very interesting cell-based assay that monitors the viability of the cells, is the screening
of Abs for cancer therapy. An ideal screen will encapsulate cancer cells along with a library
of Abs or Ab producing B-cells. It is then possible to stain for cell death and sort all the
droplets that contain a dead cancer cell - these droplets have Abs with possible anti-cancer
eﬀects. Human Abs are perfect for drug discovery as they are abundant, highly speciﬁc and
==PAGE:22==
4
Introduction
self-tolerant, meaning that the Ab itself does not provoke an immune response[27]. Abs are
the secreted form of the B-cell antigen receptor, and the number of possible diﬀerent Abs
produced by a single person, the Ab repertoire, may contain 1016 diﬀerent Abs. In reality
this number is limited to the number of B-cells in the human body, and is closer to 109[28]. Abs
are produced to recognize all biological macromolecules, and the binding of an Ab can disable
cells, bacteria and viruses as well as render it susceptible to destruction by other components
of the immune system[28]. The speciﬁcity of the Ab arise from the variable regions on the
heavy and light chains, which constitute the antigen binding site. The amino acid sequence
in the variable regions diﬀer greatly from Ab to Ab, and only binds to one antigen, or a very
small portion of antigens with similar binding sites (epitopes)[28].
Surface antigens are expressed in non-malignant cells as well as in cancers[29], and when
known, can serve as a speciﬁc target for Ab therapy. These possible targets could be ligand-
receptor growth pathways and survival pathways, which when blocked will induce tumor cell
death, but not induce cell death in healthy cells[29]. It is also believed that Abs can stimulate
the adaptive immune system, and thus have an anti-cancer vaccine eﬀect[29]. The Abs used in
Ab therapy are monoclonal Abs (mAbs), meaning that they are speciﬁc to one target. A way
of producing many mAbs is by hybridoma cells secretion (fusion of an Ab producing B-cell
(parent cell) and a myeloma cell) that are "clones" of one unique parent B-cell, and thus
creates mAbs of the speciﬁcity of the parent cell. The challenge in mAb production is the
isolation of this one parent B-cell that secrets Abs with the desired properties[27]. The classical
way of doing so is through the panning of phage display libraries or by analyzing immortalized
B-cells in microtiter plates. Phage display libraries contain only fragments of the Ab, and
tests only for speciﬁcity, not for function. Since the library contains only fragments, the heavy
and light chain are cloned separately, meaning that when combined, it is not certain to be the
same Ab secreted by a human B-cell, and may lose it’s self-tolerance[27]. Using microdroplets,
B-cells can be co-encapsulated with a target cancer cell, and be screened for binding as well
as function. As microdroplet technologies are run at kilohertz rates, an entire Ab repertoire
(109 Ab producing cells) from a single person can be screened in 11.5 days using microdroplet
technology. If the same was to be done using multiwell plates, running at 1 Hz, it would take
almost 32 years.
Currently the most common form of cancer treatment is surgery, radiation therapy, and
chemotherapy, which is very unhealthy for the patient, as the chemotherapy kills all rapidly
growing cells, also non-malignant cells[30]. Very often, diﬀerent drugs are administered dur-
ing chemotherapy, which gives synergistic eﬀects, as the drugs are designed to target dif-
ferent molecular mechanisms, which increases tumor killing and decreases the risk of drug
resistance[30]. It can easily be imagined that Ab therapy will beneﬁt from the same eﬀects if
administered in combination, as also Abs can target diﬀerent receptors on the cancer cell.
A very big problem in combinatorial drug screening is that the number of combinations
explode as the number of possible leads increase, and some knowledge about the drugs is
needed in order to reduce the numbers of possible combinations. This is for example done using
computer simulations[30] and primary screens for binding aﬃnity or function. Combinatorial
drug screens can be done using HTS/HCS in microtiter plates, but it can be imagined that
combinatorial drug screens can be performed using the picoinjector, as many injectors can be
placed in series, and switched on an oﬀindependently[16].

2.1
Droplet Microfluidics
Droplet microﬂuidics are, as all microﬂuidic problems, governed by conservation of mass
and momentum. In ﬂudic problems, conservation of mass and momentum translates into
two governing equations: the Navier-Stokes equation from conservation of momentum, and
the continuity equation from mass conservation.
In the following sections, the governing
equations, and equations derived from the governing equations, are discussed in the context
of droplet microﬂuidics. It should be noted that the governing equations cannot be solved
analytically, except in few, simple cases, and that more qualitative assessments are applied in
this ﬁeld.

which is ever present, and protects the contents of the droplets from contamination[34].
Droplet generation is essentially a battle between forces, where local ﬂuid stresses tend to
promote droplet break oﬀ, while surface tension tends to resist it[7]. In diﬀerent droplet gen-
eration geometries, slightly diﬀerent eﬀect dominate the droplet break oﬀ. Some of the most
common methods will be described in the following.

The T-junction
The T-junction consists of a channel with the carrier ﬂuid (main channel), where the dispersed
phase enters from a perpendicular side channel (side channel). In the T-junction, three diﬀer-
ent ﬂow regimes are observed, as well as two modes depending on side channel geometry. The
two modes are conﬁned- and unconﬁned droplet generation, and the three ﬂow regimes are
squeezing, dripping and jetting[37]. Which regime is observed is dependent on the capillary
number. When shear forces are unimportant (Ca<10−2), for example at low ﬂow rates, the
squeezing regime is seen[38]. This is only observed in the conﬁned droplet generation mode,
which happens when the side channel has approximately the same width as the main channel.
==PAGE:29==

The dispersed phase emerging from the side channel eventually blocks oﬀthe entire main
channel, and a droplet is broken oﬀdue to the build up of pressure in the carrier ﬂuid behind
the droplet rather than by shear forces[7, 38]. The smallest drop size observed in the squeezing
regime has a droplet length similar to the width of the main channel[38]. As ﬂow rate is
increased, a critical capillary number Cacr ≈10−2 is reached, and the ﬂow regime changes to
the dripping regime[38], where the dispersed phase enters the main channel, and droplets are
broken oﬀfrom the tip of the "ﬁnger" by shear forces[38, 39]. When the ﬂow rate is increased
even further, the capillary number increases, and the ﬁnger stretches further down the main
channel, and jetting is observed. Jetting is caused by instabilities in the ﬁnger due to shear
forces[34]. The two latter regimes are observed in both conﬁned and unconﬁned geometries.
The upper left panel in Figure 2.2 depicts squeezing in a conﬁned droplet generation geom-

Co-ﬂowing streams
This technique generates droplets when the dispersed phase is introduced through a capillary
or a needle into the continuous phase, and the continuous phase completely surrounds the
dispersed phase. Two ﬂow regimes are observed for this technique: dripping and jetting[34, 40].
They are very similar to the regimes in the T-junction, where dripping is observed when the
droplet breaks oﬀclose to the needle or capillary, and jetting is observed when the droplet
breaks oﬀfurther down stream at the tip of a longer ﬂuid "ﬁnger"[34, 40]. Typically dripping
occurs at lower ﬂow rates (low Ca), while the jetting regime occurs at higher ﬂow rates, though
the transition is not yet fully explored[34, 40]. As this technique requires a more complex three
dimensional structure, the device cannot be easily fabricated using soft lithography as is the
case with both the T-junction and the ﬂow-focusing device, which is probably why the latter
are preferred in many cases. Dripping in a co-ﬂowing streams setup can be seen in the bottom
left panel in Figure 2.2.
Flow-focusing
In the ﬂow-focusing conﬁguration (Figure 2.2, bottom right panel), the continuous phase,
ﬂowing through two perpendicular side channels, meets the dispersed phase at a nozzle where
droplets are formed[34, 41, 42]. Very similar to the co-ﬂowing streams conﬁguration, both a
dripping regime and a jetting regime can be observed[34]. Jetting occurs at high ﬂow rates,
and break-oﬀis mediated by the shear force on the dispersed phase, while dripping occurs at
lower ﬂow rates, and droplet break-oﬀcan be pressure driven or shear force driven, depending
on the geometry[34]. This junction design is chosen in this work, because it produces droplets
more stably at moderate to high capillary numbers and consume less oil than using the T-
junction[18].

2.2
Addition of Reagents in Droplets
One of the most important features of chemical and biological reactions is the ability to add
new reagents to the reaction at diﬀerent times. In the microscopic world, which is dominated
by capillary- and surface forces, addition of reagents can be particularly diﬃcult. Tools have
been developed for the addition of reagents in droplet microﬂuidics, and the principles behind
these methods are discussed in the following sections. A brief discussion of mixing of reagents
in droplets is also presented, as fast mixing at low Reynolds number requires speciﬁc action.
2.2.1
Fusion of Droplets and Picoinjection
In the category of adding reagents to previously formed droplets, two methods exist: droplet
fusion and picoinjection[49]. In droplet fusion two droplets are merged to form one bigger
==PAGE:32==

droplet. As droplet size is very well deﬁned during droplet generation, this is a good way to
introduce the same amount of reagent in every drop containing the analyte[13]. This could for
example be useful when adding PCR reagents to a population of analytes. Fusion of droplets
can be done either passively, using the geometry of the channels to facilitate fusion, or actively,
by destabilizing the droplets with electric ﬁelds[13, 49]. Droplet fusion is a useful technology,
but in order for it to work nicely, synchronization of the droplet streams is necessary[13, 16, 49].
This can be overcome by using the picoinjection method. Here the reagent is stored in a chan-

detecting droplet size or a ﬂuorescent signal in the drop, allowing selective injection of every
single drop.

Passive Fusion of Droplets
Passive fusion fuses droplets without aﬀecting them by external forces such as electric ﬁelds
or heat, but rather utilizes diﬀerences in ﬂow velocity (typically induced by changes in the
channel geometry) to bring the droplets close and fuse them.
The most common way of
passive fusion relies on channel geometry to slow the droplets down (expanding the channel),
draining the oil between the drops and then form a liquid bridge between the droplets[50]. An
example of passive fusion can be seen in Figure 2.5 a). This way of passive fusion requires
drops that have not been stabilized by surfactant, or is only partly stabilized[50, 51]. One
example of passive fusion with surfactant stabilized drops were found[52], though it is not
one-to-one fusion and fusion is only successful in 60% of the cases[52]. Mazutis et al. report

one-to-one fusion of fully stabilized (small) droplets with partly stabilized (bigger) droplets,
that is then fully stabilized further down stream before collection[51]. It is believed that the
mechanism that causes fusion is diﬀusion of surfactant from the small droplet, that is satu-
rated with surfactant, to the larger drop when the two droplets are brought in close vicinity of
each other by passing through a winding channel. When the droplets are both unsaturated,
the interface is weakened and the increased shear forces in the winding channel causes fusion.
It is imperative that the concentration of surfactant is low enough, otherwise fusion of the
droplets is ineﬃcient[51]. In the case where droplets do not need surfactant stabilization prior
to fusion, a two sided T-junction device can be used to alternately generate droplets[50], which
can then be fused at a trifurcation in the channel. The trifurcation works by draining the
carrier phase between the droplet. The rate of drainage is determined by the resistance in the
two symmetric side channels, and due to the symmetry, the drops continue straight across the
trifurcation. By matching the draining time, dependent on resistance in the side channels, to
the travel time, two or more drops can be fused before exiting the trifurcation chamber[50]. If
the droplets needs to be collected and incubated, it might be possible to add oil with a high
concentration of surfactant from side channels after fusion.
One method has been shown that can reliably fuse surfactant stabilized droplets. This method
utilizes selective grafting of hydrophilic patches to catch and fuse droplets[53].
When the
droplet passes the patch, it becomes stuck on the surface, and when the next droplet passes
the patch, it too fuses to the patch[53]. When the drag on the dispersed phase from the carrier
phase ﬂow exceeds the surface energy gained by fusing with the hydrophilic surface, the now
fused droplets detaches from the patch and continues as one droplet. The drag on the stuck
droplets is proportional to the velocity of the oil phase, and hence proportional to the ﬂow
rate. By controlling the ﬂow rate of the carrier phase, the number of droplets fused before
they detach can be controlled[53]. The down side to this method is that it works only for ﬂow
rates smaller than the critical ﬂow rate that detaches a single drop from the hydrophilic patch
before if is allowed to fuse, hence limiting the throughput. It is also not investigated whether
material is carried over between fusion events leading to cross contamination.
Electrocoalescence
In order to fuse surfactant stabilized droplets, electrocoalescence is often applied, as it is
faster than methods that apply heating or optical methods[13, 49]. Though electrocoalescence
is more diﬃcult to implement than passive fusion methods, as it requires the fabrication of
electrodes on the microscale, it is often more robust than the passive methods, and works for
a broader range of reagents[49]. One of the most important requirements for fusion used in
droplet-based microﬂuidics is to avoid cross contamination between droplets[54]. To prevent
cross contamination, chips where the electrodes are separated from the ﬂuidic channels by the
substrate are preferred. This can for example be done by patterning indium tin oxide (ITO) or
titanium gold onto glass slides or by injecting low temperature solder into channels designated
for the electrodes[11, 12, 45, 55]. Both droplets in close proximity and droplets spaced by oil has
been fused by electrocoalescence, though most reports say that synchronization is a necessity
[11, 12, 13, 49, 55]. An example of electrocoalescence can be seen in Figure 2.5 b). Regarding the
theory about coalescence, some diﬀerences appear. In less thorough descriptions, coalescence
is explained as induced dipoles in the water droplets, which in turn induces a coulombic
attraction force between the droplets, which, to a ﬁrst order approximation, scales with the
electric ﬁeld squared[54, 56, 57, 58]. It must be noted though, that the ﬁrst order approximation

Picoinjection
Picoinjection adds reagent to drops from a reservoir connected to the droplet channel by a
perpendicular side channel, which is held back by the oil phase and Laplace pressure at the
interface between the phases. Droplets are destabilized momentarily by an electric ﬁeld, which
allows them to take up an amount of reagent from the injection channel, which is determined
by the velocity of the drop and the pressure in the injection channel[16, 17, 22]. It is believed
that the mechanisms that lead to ﬁlm rupture and coalescence in the picoinjector can be
described by the same theory as electrocoalescence, as it is basically the same phenomenon.
The only diﬀerence being that the coalescence occurs between the droplet and the injection
reservoir. The newly formed drop then pinches oﬀthe perpendicular channel just as if it was
a droplet generating T-junction. An example of picoinjection can be seen in Figure 2.5 c).
Two factors are important for picoinjection to perform stably: the interface at the nozzle must
be stable, and the electric ﬁeld in the channel must be suﬃcient to destabilize the droplets
without damaging the contents of the droplets[16]. The interface stability is determined by
the stability of the pressure in the picoinjection channel, and by increasing or decreasing the
pressure, the injected volume can be controlled[16].
Picoinjection has the advantage that drops can be controllably injected by switching the
electrodes on and oﬀ. In order to do this, some kind of synchronization is necessary. This
has been done by focusing a laser through a microscope objective onto the chip down stream
of the picoinjector and then activating the electrodes based on a ﬂuorescent signal captured
from the droplets[16]. This has the limitation that a microscope is needed for the triggering,
and cannot be used for inspecting other parts of the chip while injection is occurring.

channel, the Laplace pressure across the interface must be big enough to counter the forward

2.2.2
Mixing of reagents in Droplets
An important feature of droplet microﬂuidics is the ability to mix the reagents inside the
drops in milliseconds. This is very important in many chemical and enzymatic reactions,
for example in enzymatic activity assays, where a deﬁnite onset of the reaction is needed in
order to measure the reaction time. In a straight channel, the reagents in the drops will not
be mixed due to the laminar ﬂow conditions inside the droplet[13]. In the straight channel,
symmetric recirculating ﬂows are generated in the top and the bottom halves of the drop, see
Figure 2.6, because the areas touching the sides of the channel are equal. When the droplet
enters a winding channel, one side of the droplet touches the inner arc, which is a shorter
path, in turn leading to a smaller recirculating ﬂow, and the other side touches the outer
arc, which is a longer path, thus generating more recirculation, and thereby breaking the
symmetry of the ﬂow, see Figure 2.6[13]. The sharper the turn is, the bigger is the diﬀerence
in path length, and thus the more eﬃcient the mixing[13].

2.3
Detection of Droplets or Droplet Content
The detection of droplets or contents of the droplets is essential in order to synchronize the
application of electric ﬁelds in sorting and fusion/picoinjection, as well as for detection of
reactions occurring inside the droplets. Optical detection oﬀers many diﬀerent opportunities
to detect droplets and their contents, for example by ﬂuorescence, scattering, absorption etc.
In the following section, ﬂuorescence- and scattering based detection will be discussed to an
extent that will allow the reader to understand why exactly these methods are applied.
2.3.1
Fluorescence
Quantum mechanics has shown that molecules exists in discrete energy levels, also called
electronic states, with the lowest energy level called the ground state[63]. For each energy level
several vibrational states exist as well, which relates to the energy of molecular vibrations.
==PAGE:37==

The energy spacing between vibrational levels (wave numbers from 300 to 3000 cm−1) is
much smaller than the spacing between the discrete energy levels (wave numbers between
12000 and 25000 cm−1)[64]. Fluorescence can occur, if a molecule is excited into a state above
the ground state, and then relaxes back under the emission of a photon. The wavelength of the
photon is determined by the energy gap between the states, which can diﬀer from molecule
to molecule[65], but it is more complicated than so.
As transitions between energy levels
happens fast compared to the vibrational motions of the molecule, transitions between the
energy states occur at a ﬁxed vibrational coordinate, which determines the vibrational state a
molecule arrives in during excitation. This is called the Franc-Condon principle, and is more
easily understood when looking at the energy bonds in a Jablonski diagram[64]. An example
of a realistic Jablonski diagram can be seen in Figure 2.7[64]. Often Jablonski diagrams are
simpliﬁed with energy levels appearing straight, which is incorrect according to the quantum
mechanic theory, and which fails to explain the Franc-Condon principle.

Fluorophores
Molecules that ﬂuoresce often have similarities in their structure. Very typical for a ﬂuo-
rophore is an aromatic ring or several π-bonds[65]. A ﬂuorophore is characterized either as
being intrinsic or extrinsic. Intrinsic ﬂuorophores are naturally occurring molecules with the
ability to ﬂuoresce and can for example be aromatic amino acids[65]. Extrinsic ﬂuorophores
are molecules that can be added to a sample, and for example bind to a speciﬁc target or
just exist in solution. Very common examples include ﬂuorescein and rhodamine and deriva-
tives there-oﬀ. Extrinsic ﬂuorophores can for example bind to DNA or cellular compounds of
interest, which are not intrinsically ﬂuorescent[65].
Fluorophores can be characterized by the following properties: excitation wavelength, emis-
sion wavelength, absorption coeﬃcient, quantum yield and photostability. Excitation- and
emission wavelength is important when using several ﬂuorophores in the same sample with
diﬀerent targets, as overlapping signals would make it impossible to distinguish the signals.
Luckily ﬂuorophores exist in vast numbers with a broad range of exitation and emission wave-
lengths, so ﬁnding multiple ﬂuorophores that have no overlaps is possible. The absorption
coeﬃcient describes the probability of a ﬂuorophore to become excited by a photon and Quan-
tum yield describes the emission rate of the ﬂuorophore, as non-radiative relaxation to the
ground state can happen as well, and aﬀects signal strength. Photostability describes the rate
of bleaching of the ﬂuorophore[65].
In droplet microﬂuidics, ﬂuorescence can be used for identiﬁcation of droplets, or as identi-
ﬁcation of droplet contents. In one example PCR ampliﬁcation of a wild type allele and a
mutated allele produces ﬂuorescence with diﬀerent wavelengths, allowing for the quantiﬁca-
tion of mutated to wild type allele ratio[52]. Also enzymatic activity can be assessed using
ﬂuorescence as it is done by Agresti et al. where horseradish peroxidase is improved by di-

as ﬂuorescent staining allows the distinction between live and dead cells - a very crucial op-
portunity in drug discovery. By excitation with a laser line or focused laser spot, ﬂuorescent
signals from single droplets can be read and analysed in real time and used for example in
sorting or picoinjection[20, 52]. However, detection of ﬂuorescence requires the use of a micro-
scope to collect the light, as it has no inherent direction. This makes it impossible to catch
the ﬂuorescence using optical ﬁbers, the signal would simply be too weak. For this reason,
another way of detecting the droplets was explored, which could work through optical ﬁbers.

PDMS - The Wonder Material?
In many ways PDMS is an ideal material for microﬂuidic systems.
It is transparent to
visible, UV and infra red (IR) light of wavelengths 240 to 1100 nm[70, 71], it is easy and cheap

to fabricate, it is chemically inert, it is elastic and hence does not break so easily and it
makes it easier to make connections to the device, and lastly it has a low polarity and a low
conductivity[71]. But as with any material, nothing is perfect. PDMS has some downsides,
which is important to consider before choosing it as material for an application. PDMS is
hydrophobic, which makes it diﬃcult to ﬁll channels of PDMS with aqueous solutions, though
exposure to oxygen plasma makes it hydrophilic for a couple of days[71]. Another problem
with PDMS is that is swells in contact with non-polar solvents, it can absorb solutes and
PDMS ogliomers that has not been cross-linked can diﬀuse into the solution in the channel
network, possibly causing harm to the reactions[71]. Swelling of the PDMS can cause leakage
if the PDMS is bonded to a glass slide or it can render critical features, such as membranes,
mixers or electrodes useless[71]. Whether a solvent will swell PDMS can in many cases be
predicted by the Hildebrand solubility parameter. If the parameter of the two substances,
PDMS and solvent, are close to equal, the solvent will, in most cases, cause swelling. It is
though not always the case, as the Hildebrand solubility factor consists of contributions from
dispersion-, polar- and hydrogen binding forces, and only if the contributions are similar, the
PDMS will swell. An example of solvents that have a solubility parameter close to that of
PDMS, but does not cause swelling is ﬂuorocarbons[71], which is why ﬂuorocarbon oils are very
often used as the carrier phase in droplet microﬂuidics. As molecules can be absorbed into
PDMS as well, it is important to investigate the uptake of the speciﬁc molecule. For example
is the ﬂuorescent compound, ﬂuorescein not absorbed in the PDMS, where as rhodamine
is[71]. The absorption of a ﬂuorescent compound into the PDMS can hinder the detection of
ﬂuorescence in the droplets. Absorption of molecules is especially a problem in experiments
that requires precise control and knowledge of solute concentration, for example in drug dose
response experiments[70].
PDMS is a two component material consisting of a liquid polymer base and a liquid curing
agent. Upon mixing, the curing agent initiates cross-linking of the PDMS polymer chains,
a process that can take several hours depending on temperature[72]. Since the polymer is
liquid, it is easy to pattern using casting. Casting is a simple process where the liquid PDMS
is poured over a mold, which has a protruding pattern of the channel network that is desired
to replicate in the PDMS. The smallest feature sizes depends solely on the fabrication of the
mold and can be down to nanoscale[72]. Once the PDMS is cured, holes can be punched
manually in the soft polymer to act as connections to the channel network. The device is
typically sealed by bonding the patterned PDMS to a glass or polymer substrate. In order to
bond the PDMS, it is exposed to oxygen plasma, which creates silanol groups on the surface
of the PDMS, which can then react in a condensation reaction with silanol groups present
on the surface of glass substrates or other plasma treated PDMS surfaces[73], see Figure 2.11.
The exposure to plasma also allows chemical treatment of the channels.

2.5.1
The Cell Cycle and Cancer
Cell division and programmed cell death (apoptosis) is the key to tissue homeostasis (keeping
the tissue stable, homos: same, stasis: position)[75]. Cell division is controlled by the cell
cycle, and occurs if there is a reproductive signal, which can originate from inside, or outside
the cell. This begins the cell cycle[75]. In the human body, only a few cell types frequently
divide once the cell has become specialized, and the signal to begin cell division is not related
to the environment of a single cell, but rather the environment of the tissue in which the cell
belongs[75]. The most common reason for initiation of cell division is due to tissue injury,
which causes the release of growth factors, for example platelet derived growth factor, which

The cell cycle can be divided into two phases: interphase and mitosis/cytokinesis (M-phase).
Interphase is the phase where the cell commits to, and prepares for cell division, and it then
divides during the M-phase[75, 76]. Figure 2.14 shows the animal cell cycle, restriction points
and sub-phases. In the following, the sub-phases and the transition between the phases will
be discussed in greater detail.
The progression through the cell cycle is controlled by diﬀerent cyclin dependent kinases
(cdk’s). Kinases are enzymes that catalyses the phosphorylation of speciﬁc proteins. Phos-
phorylation changes the shape and function of proteins, because the charge of the protein
is altered[75]. As the name suggests, cdk’s depends on cyclin activity. Cdk’s are present in
the cell throughout the cell cycle, but cyclins are only synthesized when the cell commits to
progression in the cell cycle. The binding of cyclin to a cdk exposes to active site on the cdk,
which allows the enzyme to phosphorylate proteins[75, 77].
The ﬁrst phase of the cell cycle, just after division, is called the Gap 1 phase, or just G1-phase.
In this phase the cell commits to cell division if a growth signal is received, and prepares for
DNA synthesis[75]. Cells that do not receive a growth signal is arrested in G1-phase and
enters a resting phase termed G0. The inhibition of progression from G1-phase is mediated
by retinoblastoma protein (RB), which acts as an inhibitor at the restriction point. G1-S
cyclin-cdk phosphorylates RB, which becomes inactive and the cell can progress to the S-
phase[75]. Another checkpoint in the G1-phase checks the genomic DNA for damage. If any

DNA may lead to mutations of the cell, which can lead to the formation of cancer, which is
why cells with DNA damage is destroyed (if possible)[75].

the G2-phase[75]. Activation of the DNA damage checkpoint in the S phase is ﬁrstly due to ac-

DNA (ds-DNA), and can be stabilized by proteins. Replication protein A (RPA) binds to

happens that a mutation in ATR or Chk1 leaves it activated, the ds-DNA is cleaved causing

phosphorylates checkpoint protein 2 (Chk2), which in turn triggers a cascade of events that
pauses the replication and repairs the break. Sometimes these repairs go wrong, which results
in alterations in the chromosome. If these alterations accumulate, it can lead to mutations in
the cell division control, and a tumor might begin to form[78].
When the chromosomes have been replicated successfully, the cell enters the Gap 2 phase,
or just G2.
In G2 phase the cell prepares for cell division by synthesizing the necessary
components, for example the microtubules that will move the chromatids during mitosis[75].
In the end of G2 is a checkpoint leading to the M phase. Maturation-promotion factor (MPF)
is a kinase that plays the major role in the transition from G2 to M. MPF forms a complex
with p34cdc2 and cyclin B, after which it becomes active and phosphorylates many proteins
related to mitosis and cytokinesis[76]. Some of the proteins that is phosphorylated are nuclear
lamins, that aid in the disassembly of the nucleus, while vimentin and caldesmon aid in
the rearrangement of the cytoskeleton upon phosphorylation. MPF and M-phase histone H1
kinase induces changes in microtubule dynamics and begin to form mitotic spindles, and cause
chromosome condensation[76]. The condensed sister chromatids are aligned at the center of
the cell by microtubules, then they are disconnected from each other, and one chromosome
from each pair is transported to either side of the cell and division of the cytoplasm begins
to take place in the process of cytokinesis[75].
If a cell becomes apoptotic during the cell cycle, the cell is detached from the neighbouring
cells, and the cell begins to break into cell fragments. The DNA is also broken into smaller
pieces in the process. The small cell fragments is then phagocytosed (engulfed) by the neigh-
bouring cells, that can recycle some of the cellular components, for example amino acids and
nucleotides[75].
Cancer cells diﬀer from healthy cells in the sense that cancer cells have lost control over
cell division, for example by mutation of inhibition sites on cdk’s[75, 77]. The mutations that
lead to cancer cells changes the cells to the point where it no longer looks anything like the
tissue it came from[75]. Two classes of proteins regulate the cell cycle: oncogene proteins are
positive regulators in cancer cells, and are typically over expressed or over active, leading to
rapid progression through the cell cycle. An example of an oncogene protein is the HER2
membrane protein, which is over expressed in breast cancer. HER2 is a growth factor receptor,
and over expression lead to over stimulation of cell division[75]. In some cancers, the interphase
cdk’s are hyper active, as inhibition sites have been blocked by amino acid replacement[77].
The other class is called tumor suppressors, and are negative regulators. One example of
a tumor suppressor is the RB protein. In cervical cancer, a protein that inactivates RB is
expressed, and thus there is no restriction point in the G1 phase. It often happens that tumor
suppressors are inactivated in cancer cells, thus preventing the inhibition of cell division. This
can for example be caused by viruses such as the Human papillomavirus (HPV)[75]. Many
cancer therapeutics targets factors that will cause cell cycle arrest in rapidly dividing cells[75].

2.5.2
Antibodies and Their Potential in Cancer Therapy
Monoclonal antibodies are fast becoming targets for pharmaceutical companies[27], as diﬀerent
antibodies are capable of binding to a very broad variety of antigens and they are self tolerant,
meaning that they do not aﬀect the healthy human cells[28]. One human have millions of
diﬀerent antibodies, thus screening of antibody libraries have the potential to provide many
new drug leads. The ﬁeld where therapeutic Abs have the potential to produce the largest
impact, is in cancer therapy.
Antibody Structure and Function
Antibodies are the secreted form of the B-cell antigen receptor, which targets molecules,
macromolecules, virus particles or cells that have a binding site for the antibody receptor[28].
Antibodies consists of two polypeptides called the heavy and the light chain. Each antibody
has two identical heavy chains and two identical light chains. The heavy and light chains
are connected in a Y like structure, presenting the antigen binding site at the tips of the
fork. The heavy and the light chain consists of a variable region and a constant region. The
constant region can for example bind to receptors on phagocytes or other inﬂammatory cells
or to complements. The variable region is the region presented at the tips of the Y, and make
up the antigen binding site[28]. As the name suggests, the variable region diﬀers in amino
acid sequence from antibody to antibody, and can thus target a wide variety of antigens. The
variability arises through a process called gene rearrangement. The genes encoding the heavy
and light chains are separated by intermediate genes, and only when they are brought into
proximity by an enzymatically mediated process can they be transcribed. Many combinations
of these gene segments create the variability, but variability can also arise from errors in the
recombination process[28]. The structure of the antibody can be seen in Figure 2.15. Before
a B-cell is released into circulation, it undergoes tests to ensure that it will not be speciﬁc
for healthy human cells or molecules. Cells that have receptors for such targets are given a
signal to undergo apoptosis. This check makes sure that the immune system does not attack
the healthy cells[28].

Antibodies as Drugs
Naturally produced antibodies have been used as passive immunotherapy since the late nine-
teenth century, but not all therapies have been eﬃcient due to the variable nature of anti-
bodies. This kind of treatment also bears the risk of transmitting blood-borne diseases and it
is very costly[27]. In the mid-1970’s, hybridoma technology was developed. This technology
fuses antibody-producing B-cells with immortalized cells, which results in a cell line with
a long life time, which is capable of producing antibodies with the same speciﬁcity as the
primary cell it was derived from[27]. This allows the production of monoclonal antibodies,
which all target the same pathogen/target, and is thus more eﬃcient as a therapeutic agent.
The ﬁrst antibodies produced in this way came from mice, which were often immunogenic to
humans, and only recently have antibodies derived directly from humans been produced[27].
Antibodies can induce tumor cell death and anti-tumor activity in several ways, some which
include direct cytotoxicity, Fc-mediated immune eﬀector engagement etc.[29]. Direct cytotox-
icity induces tumor cell death by the binding of antibodies directly on cancer targets, which
perturbs signalling pathways or can carry conjugated cytotoxic targets to the tumor[29]. Fc-
mediated immune eﬀector engagement utilizes the Fc portion of the antibody, which is the
part of the heavy chain, which can be recognized by the inﬂammatory cells, to activate NK
cells, macrophages or dendritic cells through Fc receptors on the cell surface. These immune
cells will then be able to phagocytose the tagged tumor cell.
Producing mAbs is not a challenge. By fusing an Ab-producing B-cell with a myeloma cell
(resulting in what is called a hybridoma cell), a long living cell line capable of expressing
antibodies of a single speciﬁcity can be produced[27]. The challenge is ﬁnding the right Ab-
producing B-cell. Antibody binding is classically done by panning phage display libraries or
by distributing immortalized B-cells into microtiter plates with one cell per well, and then
assessing the binding properties of the antibodies produced in each well[27].
Both phage
panning and analysis in plates are cumbersome, and can be eased with the use of droplet
microﬂuidics.

2.6.1
Clean room Fabrication of PDMS Molds
The molds used to cast the PDMS chip is made of a silicon wafer patterned with SU-8
photoresist (SU-8 2075/2035, Micro resist technology, Berlin, Germany). The surface of the
wafer is not very important, thus the cheapest available 4" silicon wafer was used. In the
process, typically 6 wafers were produced at a time. The process began by dehydrating the
wafers in a 250◦C oven over night. On the same day, SU-8 2075 (or 2035 for thin layers) was
poured into a syringe and closed with a plug that ﬁts the dispenser on the spin coater, and
left in an upright position to allow air bubbles to rise to the top of the syringe. On the next
day the wafers were spin coated using a Karl Süss RC8 photoresist spinner for single wafers,
with a manual dispensing system. The dispensing system used pressurized air to dispense
the resist from the syringe. The amount dispensed was controlled by the number of seconds
the pressurized air was on. For the SU-8 2075 resist, 8 seconds was used, whereas 4 seconds
was suﬃcient for the less viscous SU-8 2035. To spin coat the wafer, the recipe was entered
into the computer controlling the spinner, and the recipe was run. The resist was spread by
pre-spinning the wafer at 500 rpm for 5 seconds. Directly after the spread cycle, the main
cycle began automatically. The angular velocity and the spin time determined the thickness
of the resist. The typical parameters used is given in Table 2.1. After spin coating, the wafers
were baked on a hotplate. The plate was ramped up to 65◦C over 10 minutes and then baked
for 3 minutes, before ramping up to 95◦C over 10 minutes and then baked for 9 minutes. The
wafers were then allowed to cool completely to room temperature, which took approximately
3 hours.

for 13 minutes (6 + 7 minutes). The baths allowed the submersion of one wafer at a time
using a plastic wafer holder. After submersion in the developer, the wafers were rinsed with
Acetone and left to dry in a dedicated drying box.
After the wafers were dry, the thickness of the SU-8 features were measured either by an optical
proﬁler or by a stylus proﬁler, if the optical proﬁler was unavailable. In the optical proﬁler,
the interferometric scan was used, as the confocal scan only measured the half thickness.
When the thickness was conﬁrmed, the wafers were ready to be taken out of the cleanroom.
2.6.2
Picoinjection Chip Fabrication
Once the mold was ﬁnished in the clean room, fabrication of the chip could begin. First,
curing agent and PDMS elastomer (184 Sylgard, Diatom A/S, Hvidovre, DK) were mixed
thoroughly in 1:10 weight ratio and then left under vacuum for approximately one hour in
order to remove any trapped air in the viscous elastomer. Next the mold was placed in a
plastic petri dish, and the PDMS mixture was poured into the dish until it was ﬁlled up
approximately 0.5 cm. The dish was then baked in the oven at 70◦C for 4 hours, or over night
to cure the PDMS. The petri dishes were removed from the oven and the PDMS excised with
a scalpel, and then peeled oﬀthe mold. The chips were cut into microscope slide sizes using
a scalpel, then using a 0.75 mm biopsy punch, holes were punched at the inlets and outlets
of both ﬂuidic channels and electrode channels. The PDMS was then bonded to a glass slide
(75 mm x 26 mm) after both bonding surfaces were exposed to an oxygen/nitrogen (30/70%)
plasma at approximately 0.7 mbar and 200 Watt for 3 minutes (Plasma cleaner Atto with
manual control, Diener electronic). To improve the bonding, the chip was heated in an oven
for 10 minutes at 70◦C. Directly after bonding, the ﬂuidic channels were silanized by ﬂusing a
2% (v/v) solution of 1H,1H,2H,2H-Perﬂuorododecyltrichlorosilane (ABCR GmbH, Karlsruhe,
Germany) in ﬂuorocarbon oil (NOVEC HFE-7500, Walbom A/S, Kastrup, DK) through the
ﬂuidic channels. This was done in order to increase the hydrophobicity, and increase the
==PAGE:50==

wettablility by the ﬂuorocarbon, of the ﬂuidic channels in order to reduce the risk of droplets
wetting the channel wall, possibly causing cross contamination between droplets.
The channels were dried using vacuum, then rinsed in ﬂuorocarbon oil and dried again. When
the chip was dry, the electrodes were fabricated using a low temperature solder (Indalloy ♯19
wire Ø 0.5mm, Indium corporation, Torino) with a melting temperature of 60◦C. The chip
was placed on a hotplate at 80 −90◦C, and a small (∼5 cm) piece of electrical wiring (Ø
0.6 mm) was inserted in the outlet of the electrode channel. The thin solder wire was then
pushed into the inlet of the electrode channel, thus melting and ﬁlling the entire channel.
When the channel was ﬁlled, another piece of electrical wiring was pushed into the inlet of
the electrode channel, completing the circuit, or the inlet was left with a small piece of solder
sticking out, as only one wire is actually needed. This is repeated for all electrodes on the
chip. When the chip is removed from the hotplate, the solder solidiﬁes and the conductivity
of the circuit is tested using a multimeter. This ﬁnishes the chip, and the ﬂuidic inlets and
outlets are covered with scotch tape to prevent dust particles from entering the device, which
can cause blocking of the channels.

2.6.4
LabVIEW™Script
The entire script running the data acquisition and voltage generator is far too vast to be
described in detail here. The mechanisms involved with the pattern recognition of the optical
ﬁber signal however is fairly simple and quite important, and will be described below. The
relevant parts of the script can be seen in Appendix A, Figures A.2 and A.3. The user interface
can be seen in Figure A.1.
Picoinjection mode, triggering on empty drops (Figure A.2):
The amplitude of the signal from the photodetector is stored as a variable in the script. This
variable is compared to the noise level, which is calculated elsewhere in the script (not shown),
and signal processing depends on whether it is above (TRUE) or below (FALSE) the noise
level, shown in the two separate frames in Figure A.2.
When there is no drop, the signal lies below the noise level, and when a drop passes between
the ﬁbers, it creates a peak from the front of the drop, and a peak from the backside of the
drop. When a peak is observed, the outer frame changes from FALSE to TRUE as soon as
the signal rise above the noise level (the second frame, Figure A.2). In this frame, the signal
is compared to the variable, PicoPeakLocalMax, which is zero before a peak is detected. If
the signal is larger than PicoPeakLocalMax, the value in PicoPeakLocalMax is replaced by
the signal value, and the time stamp is stored in LastPeakTime, which is used to judge the
==PAGE:55==
Methods
37
distance between the front end peak and the back end peak. If the signal is smaller than
PicoPeakLocalMax, nothing happens.
This frame loops until the signal again falls beneath the noise level, and the frame changes
back to FALSE (the ﬁrst frame, Figure A.2). Now the programme has detected a peak, stored
the maximum amplitude and the time stamp of the peak. If a peak has occurred before, the
time stamp from that peak is stored as OldTime. If the time since the last peak lies between
the parameters TimeMin and TimeMax (in µs, set by the user in the user interface, see Figure
A.1), and the peak amplitude lies between Threshold min 1 and Threshold max 1 (set in the
user interface), it triggers the electrodes (→DIGI_SORT becomes TRUE). The variables,
OldTime and PicoPeakLocalMax, are updated.
It is also possible to tell the programme to skip X number of peaks before triggering the
electrode. This proved that injection could be selectively chosen based on the signal from the
optical ﬁber.
CellSpotter mode, triggering on the cells inside drops (Figure A.3):
This script begins in the same way as the Picoinjection script. When a peak has been detected,
and the outer frame is set back to FALSE (ﬁrst frame in Figure A.3), the ﬁrst deviation from
picoinjection mode happens.
When the ﬁrst peak is detected, it triggers a loop, which looks for changes to the signal
between the two peaks corresponding to the front and the back of the drop. When a cell is
present, it scatters light, and the signal drops below the level of the base line of the signal. If
the signal after the ﬁrst peak drops below a set BaseLine, before a certain time, SniﬀerTime
(corresponding to the size of the drop), the variable CellFound is set to TRUE. When the
second peak occurs, the same check of the time between the peaks and the amplitude of the
peak is made as in Picoinjection mode, in addition, CellFound must be TRUE before the
electrode triggers. When the electrode has been triggered, the variables CellFound, OldTime
and PicoPeakLocalMax are updated.
2.6.5
Analysis of the Data Files Generated By LabVIEW™
The data collected by the LabVIEW™programme, that is the signal from the PMT, the
photodetector and the high-voltage generator, as well as information about the peaks recorded
by the PMT, were stored in .txt ﬁles, in order to further process the data. This processing
was done using MATLAB ®. Processing involved plotting the data, counting events and
performing statistics. One example of a script plotted data from the PMT as the ﬂuorescence
intensity as a function of peak width in a scatter plot. The populations in the scatter plot could
then be gated (typically two gates were set, one for empty drops and one for injected drops),
and the number of events in each gate was counted, as well as the mean and standard deviation
of the ﬂuorescence and width of each gate. As each event was labelled with a time stamp,
they could be traced back to the signal stream, and the shape of the peaks corresponding to
events in each gate could be investigated. This allowed explanation of outliers (events that
were out side populations), and deviations from expected counts. Another script was made
as well, which could count the number of peaks above a certain level from the data stream
ﬁles.

Cell Culture
SEMK2 cells were kept in culture to be available for encapsulation into droplets. The cells
were stored in a cryogenic freezer at -150◦C in 10% Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) and 90% Fetal
Bovine Serum (FBS). Before the cells were thawed, cell culture medium was prepared. The
SEMK2 cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 Medium, GlutaMAX™Supplement (Gibco, Thermo
Fisher Scientiﬁc Inc.) with the addition of 10% (v/v) FBS and 1% (v/v) Penicillin/Strepto-
mycin (P/S) mixture to reduce the risk of infection in the cell culture. The culture medium
was warmed to 37◦C in an incubator, and the cells were removed from the freezer and thawed
quickly (60-120 s) in a water bath at 37◦C. 15 mL warm medium was moved to a falcon tube,
and the cells were carefully pipetted into the warm medium. The cells were then spun down
in a centrifuge and resuspended in warm medium. Cells were counted and viability was as-
sessed (Vi-Cell cell counter, Beckman-Coulter), and the cells were resuspended to the desired
cell culture concentration (0.3×106 cells/mL, typically) and were then transferred to culture
ﬂasks. The culture was kept healthy by changing medium two times a week. The cells were
quickly growing, so the culture was diluted 1:10 with fresh medium at each passage. Once
in a while the cells were inspected under a microscope to conﬁrm stable culturing, and a cell
count was done.

Results
It is the aim of this work to design and fabricate a PDMS chip that can perform picoinjection
in a droplet microﬂuidics pipeline in order to add new reagents to the pre-generated droplets.
The picoinjector must incorporate droplet reinjection, droplet detection, droplet injection,
mixing of the droplet content and droplet recollection. In order for this to be successful,
several parameters must be investigated and optimized, and the operation of the chip must
be characterized. In the following sections, the results of the design process, optimizations,
and characterizations are presented, and the results are discussed in Chapter 4.
3.1
Design and Development of the picoinjection chip
The ﬁrst goal of this work was to implement a single picoinjector. The design underwent
three iterations, and the most critical features can be seen in Figure 3.1. The ﬁnal design
(Iteration ♯3) can be seen in detail in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.1: Nozzle design during the three design iterations. Changes from iteration to iteration is
marked with a red square.
Between iteration ♯1 and ♯2, the shape of the nozzle was changed from a straight nozzle to
a v-shaped nozzle, and channels for inserting optical ﬁbers in the chip was added. Between
iteration ♯2 and ♯3, a short piece of narrow channel was added at the nozzle.
Another parameter which need to be explored in the depth of the channels on the chip. When
detecting droplets with optical ﬁbers, the diameter of the ﬁbers determine the depth of the
channels, as they have to ﬁt into the channels designated for guiding the ﬁbers and holding
them in place. Preliminary experiments showed that the chips with deep, approximately 70-
80 µm, channels, performed injections more stably than chips with a channel depth of only
20-25 µm (data not shown), and hence the deep channels were used throughout the rest of
the work.
In the ﬁnal design, droplet reinjection, detection, injection, mixing and recollection was suc-
cessfully implemented. Chip function could now be evaluated. As theory predicts that the
electric ﬁeld is important for the destabilization of the droplets, simulations of the electric
ﬁeld generated by the electrodes in the picoinjection chip was performed.

1080 ± 90 µs, while the injected drops had a width of 123 ± 9.7 data points (2460 ± 194 µs).

estimated as follows:

4.1
Discussion
4.1.1
Picoinjection Chip Design
As previously described, the picoinjection chip design underwent three design iterations. In
this section, the failing points of the two ﬁrst iterations will be discussed, and connections to
current literature on the subject will be drawn.
The initial design was based on the work of Eastburn and colleagues[22], with a straight
injection channel. It was found that it was impossible to achieve a stable meniscus at the
nozzle of a straight channel, which is required, if droplets are going to be selectively injected.
Looking at the equation for the Laplace pressure (eq. 2.2.13), it is clear to see why a stable
meniscus is impossible. Since the channel is straight, the radius of curvature is the same along
the channel, and hence the pressure required to move the ﬂuid into the injection channel has
to be bigger than the Laplace pressure, which is supposed to hold the meniscus back at
the nozzle. This was conﬁrmed by inspection under a microscope: when the injection ﬂuid
arrived at the nozzle and started forming a meniscus in the main channel, the radius of
curvature simply kept growing, hence the Laplace pressure decreased and thus it was even
more favourable for the injection ﬂuid to continue into the main channel and break oﬀin
droplets (as squeezing in a T-junction). This can be seen in Figure 4.1, where a time series
of 5 microscopy images are shown. The white arrows in the ﬁrst and last frame indicate the
direction of ﬂuid motion. It is clear to see that the radius of curvature grows from the ﬁrst
to the second frame and as well from the second to the third frame. In the last two frames,
a droplet breaks oﬀand the liquid retreats back into the injection channel. The chip used
in Figure 4.1 is actually a droplet maker, where the channel on the right is blocked oﬀby
injecting UV curable glue into the channel. The 1st design iteration was never fabricated due
to the results obtained with the droplet maker.
The reason why the straight channel injector works from some, for example Eastburn[22],
is because every single droplet in the assay is injected. In such an assay, the injection ﬂuid
could as well have been controlled by a syringe pump[17], which would supply a steady amount
of injection ﬂuid to the nozzle. In order for such systems to work stably, droplets must be
injected very periodically, as uneven spacing between the droplets will result in more or less
ﬂuid volume injected into each drop, as pressure in the injection channel have been shown to
correlate (linearly) to the injected ﬂuid volume[16]. The pump, in this case, is simply used to
replenish the liquid drawn by the droplet before the next droplet arrives.
As the injections performed in this work aimed at having combinatorial injections, it was
important that droplets could be chosen not to be injected without the injection ﬂuid produced
more droplets, which are useless to the assay. In order for this to succeed you have to stop the
ﬂow generated by the pressure in the injection channel by applying a counter pressure at the
nozzle. The counter pressure arise from the Laplace pressure at the interface. By having a
wide injection channel, which narrows down at the oriﬁce, the pressure required to move the
ﬂuid into the injection channel is determined by the Laplace pressure of the meniscus formed
in the wide part of the channel, where the radius of curvature is large. When the channel then

4.1.3
How The Amplitude of the Electric Pulse Aﬀects Injection
It was shown that in order to successfully inject droplets, a voltage above 40 V was required.
In the literature it is very common to write the voltage which is applied on the electrodes,
which is typically between 20V and 600 V[9, 16, 17, 22, 25, 45], even though it is actually the
electric ﬁeld strength (voltage per length), which is important for injection, but also which
can have a negative eﬀect on encapsulated bio-molecules. For example are electric ﬁelds used
to rupture cell membranes in a process called electroporation[80], something which is very
unwanted in most cell-based assays, which requires cells to be alive to perform a function, or
it can create a bias in viability assays.
4.1.4
Triggering the Picoinjector Using Optical Fibers
The most successful and innovative part of the project, was the addition of droplet detection by
optical ﬁbers. The classical way to trigger electrodes on chip is by measuring ﬂuorescence just
upstream of the picoinjector or sorter using a laser focused through the microscope objective.
Microscopes are expensive and thus ﬁt badly within the cheap, versatile set-up that is a
commercial system. In spite of this, detection with a microscope is extensively used in the
==PAGE:80==
62
Discussion and Conclusions
current literature[9, 14, 16, 20, 23, 26].
One article have been found, which uses optical ﬁbers
to characterize micro droplets based on reﬂection, where the ﬁbers are used to measure the
length of each drop[81], but no papers have been found, which uses refraction and scattering
to detect droplets. No literature have been found that utilizes optical ﬁbers for triggering
neither sorting nor picoinjection.
Replacing the detection mechanism with optical ﬁbers is useful in two ways. In a research set-
up, not having to use the microscope for detection, allows in to be used for more demanding
tasks, such as analysis of the droplets down stream from the picoinjector, which is beneﬁcial
for understanding and characterizing the injections. In a commercial purpose, replacing the
microscope with optical ﬁbers and possibly small laser diodes and detectors, greatly reduces
the space the set-up requires and reduces the price, as the expensive microscope set-up is
unnecessary.
One thing that limits the throughput unnecessarily in the way the triggering is run in this
thesis, is that droplets have a large volume of oil in between them.
A way of increasing
throughput could be to decrease the spacing between each drop (simply done by increasing
the ﬂow rate of the droplets or decreasing the ﬂow rate of the oil). The reason why the
drops were kept well spaced in this work, was to ensure that the detected drop was also the
injected drop, a problem which you do not have when triggering by ﬂuorescence, as this can
be measured immediately before the injection channel. It might be possible to determine
the relation between ﬂow rates and required delay, using ﬂow rates that results in diﬀerent
spacing between the drops, but still fulﬁlling the requirement that the spacing is larger than
the distance between the optical ﬁbers and the injector. It is expected that delay and ﬂow
rate is linearly connected, as ﬂow rate is linearly connected to velocity of the liquid for deﬁned
channel dimensions, and droplets that ﬁll the entire channel (plug ﬂow). In this way it might
be possible to interpolate the required delay, when the spacing is shorter than the distance
between the ﬁbers and the injector. Ultimately limiting the throughput, is the speed with
which electrodes can be activated and inactivated, as well as how fast the laser signal can be
processed.
4.1.5
Selective Injection of Droplets
Precise control over when injections happened, was shown in section 3.8. Best control was
shown when pressurizing the outlet. Without pressure on the outlet, the pressure had to be
adjusted accordingly if not all droplets were injected, which was not necessary when pres-
surizing the outlet.
The reason for raising the pressure in the entire chip, was to reduce
the sensitivity to small pressure ﬂuctuations, and looking at the scatter plots in Figure 3.18
and 3.19, it seems to work, as dripping from the injector is greatly reduced, and populations
become much more distinct - The injected volume changes less.
Not only was it shown that droplets can be selectively injected, showing as accurate distribu-
tions in the two populations (Table 3.1), it was also shown that even if droplets were skipped
between injections, the volume of the injected ﬂuid did not change, which can be seen in
Figure 3.20. The ﬂuorescence intensity of the droplets injected with pressure on the outlet,
have approximately the same average, and the same can be said about the droplets injected
without pressure on the outlet. What was diﬀerent in the two cases, was the variation in the
intensity, which was decreased substantially by applying a pressure on the outlet. As ﬂuores-
cence intensity is believed to be directly proportional to the concentration of the ﬂuorescein,
and hence the volume of the injection, it can be concluded that the volume is independent of
==PAGE:81==
Discussion
63
how many droplets are skipped between injections. This is the ﬁrst step towards combinato-
rial injections, which may be useful in applications such as drug discovery for cancer therapy.
It is believed that the implementation of several picoinjectors is well on it’s way when this
level of control can be shown.
One problem was to correctly asses the volume of the injected droplets. The ﬂuorescence
intensity could not be directly translated, as the intensity measured varied from day to day
(which is also seen in Figure 3.20, as the intensity of the droplets injected without pressure
is higher than with pressure, though the width of the signal is smaller). Several factors could
inﬂuence the intensity: for example will changes in the gain on the PMT aﬀect the intensity,
bleaching of the ﬂuorescein can also aﬀect the intensity. Even though all ﬂuorescent droplets
and substrates were stored, wrapped in aluminium foil, it was still exposed to some light during
experiments. Many droplets were typically generated at a time and used in experiments for
several days to save time. It was also seen that intensity depended on how the focus was in
the microscope. The only real issue is the focus in the microscope. Changes in PMT gain can
be prevented by making sure the gain is set to the same value every time, and bleaching of
ﬂuorescent compounds can be partly circumvented by generating new droplets and preparing
new solutions for every experiment, though it is a little time consuming. It is much more
diﬃcult to ensure that the microscope is focused at the exact same depth every time, as this
might also diﬀer from chip to chip, and how it is sitting in the holder.
Because the ﬂuorescence intensity could not be translated to volume due to unknown param-
eters causing variations, the width of the ﬂuorescence signal was used to estimate the injected
volume instead. As the volume of the empty droplets was known, the width of the empty
droplets was used to normalize the width of the injected droplets. Even though this gave a
nice result from the data shown in Figure 3.19, the credibility of the estimate is questioned.
Between experiments, the width of the ﬂuorescence peaks recorded varied quite substantially.
Some times they were recorded to have an average width of 90 data points, and other times
123 data points, even though the parameters set on the pumps etc. were the same. One ex-
planation that can be given to this, is that the width of the peak is measured as the number
of data points which lie above the noise level for each peak, hence if the noise level is diﬀerent
from experiment to experiment, very likely, diﬀerent widths are recorded. It is believed that
a better way to asses droplet width is required, for example by ﬁtting a gauss curve and mea-
suring full width at half maximum (FWHM). This computation, however, requires that more
data is stored and processed in real time, which may slow down the acquisition speed. The
most common way of measuring drop size, is by measuring on microscope images[16, 79, 82].
The details in the literature is quite sparse, and it is unclear whether it is done using a high
speed camera or an ordinary microscope camera. It was the observation in this study that
a normal microscope camera was unable to capture precise images of injected drops which
could allow for droplet volume estimation, as the shutter speed was simply too slow compared
to droplet velocity.
4.1.6
Stability of the Picoinjection System
Even though it was possible to successfully inject droplets, some concerns about the stability
of the system still remain. First of all, when droplets injected with ﬂuorescein were analysed
based on their ﬂuorescence, the intensity of the signal decreased over time with a time scale
of approximately 20 seconds.
As it is believed that the intensity of the signal is directly
related to the injected volume, the decrease is believed to be caused by depletion of liquid
==PAGE:82==
64
Discussion and Conclusions
in the injection channel. The pressure in the injection channel simply results in a too slow
in-ﬂow of new liquid to replenish the liquid which is injected into droplets, and it cannot be
increased as this will cause the injector to drip. In some cases, it was seen that the liquid
almost disappeared completely, as the intensity of the injected droplets almost reached the
background intensity of the droplets, see for example Figure 3.18. To prevent this, pressure
in the injection channel had to be adjusted manually very often in order to keep a relatively
stable intensity, which is very inconvenient if long screens are performed. When adjusting
the pressure, it was also very diﬃcult to adjust the pressure correctly, as too much pressure
caused injection of a larger volume and thus an increase in intensity.
Only very few papers on this subject speciﬁes for how long their system can run stably. Only
one in ﬁve analyzed articles reported a coeﬃcient of variation for a 30 minute long experiment,
which was just 4.4% while running at 400 Hz[17]. Others report a CV below 3%, but this
is only measured from 6 drops[82], which is not statistically representative compared to how
many drops are typically passed through the system during a screen. The lowest CV achieved
in this work is 9% (skipping two droplets with pressure on the outlet), measured over 8084
drops. It is believed that imaging the injected droplets with a high speed camera is necessary
to fully understand what is truly going on. Collecting data in this work was a compromise
between recording the ﬂuorescence or imaging with the microscope, as both at the same time
was impossible on the set-up. The quality of the images taken with the microscope camera
was quite poor, and hence focus remained on the ﬂuorescence, though in retrospect a mix
between the two might have shed a little more light on the determination of injected volume.
It was also seen that the ﬂuorescence intensity of the injected droplets had a slight periodic
variation. It is believed that periodic variations in the pressure can be due to ﬂuctuations
in the ﬂow generated by the syringe pumps. It can be the motors of the pump which causes
this, or it can be the piston in the syringe, which does not move smoothly. All reports on
picoinjection in literature uses syringe pumps to transport the droplets, and this problem has
not been taken up to discussion in any of them. Only one recent article discusses the pressure
sensitivity of the system[83]. It is possible that improving the equipment might reduce this
problem. In the work by Sjostrom et al.[17], a very low CV is achieved over an extended period
of time, using the NEMESYS syringe pump system (Cetoni GmbH), which is supposed to
be pulsation free, and is supposed to move so smoothly that the piston the the syringe is
prevented from sticking to the syringe.
As mentioned earlier, other, more random variations in intensity was seen as well, and is
believed to be caused by unexplained pressure ﬂuctuations. It is postulated that the stability
of the chip can be increased by adding ﬂuid resistors to the chip. The over all chip pressure can
be increased by adding a resistor before the outlet, and back ﬂow in the injection channel can
be limited by a resistor in the injection channel. Even though this might solve one problem -
stability when the liquid is ﬁlling the entire channel - it might cause other problems, such as
slowing down transport of ﬂuid to the meniscus as it is depleted during injection, which was
seen to be a problem already. Adding a ﬂuid resistor to the oil spacing channel might also
prove beneﬁcial, as this will reduce the eﬀect of pressure changes when droplets pass by the
oil spacing channel.
The oil spacing channel also limited the size of the droplets, which could be used in the chip.
By widening the oriﬁce where the oil spacing channel meets the main channel, larger drops
can be used without braking them up and causing large pressure ﬂuctuations, as the oil will
have more room to move around the drop. A suggestion to how this can be done is seen in
==PAGE:83==

well as widening the oil spacing channel, it is believed that widening the mixing channel is

Figure 4.4: A suggestion to how the oil spacing channel can be changed to facilitate larger droplets.
Only recently was a new article on stability of the picoinjection module discovered[83]. They
conﬁrm that the picoinjection module is very sensitive to pressure ﬂuctuations in the chip,
less in passive systems than in active systems, but enough to make stable injection very
diﬃcult. They also mention that pressure ﬂuctuations due to ﬂow resistance created by the
droplets becomes signiﬁcant, when droplet size is much bigger than the (limiting) channel
dimension[83], which is believed to explain some of the instabilities seen in this work to some
extent . As a solution, Rhee et al. suggest a pressure ventilation system implemented in the
picoinjection module, consisting of a forked channel structure immediately upstream of the
picoinjector, which vents pressure to an open outlet. The forks in the channel are too small
for droplets to pass through, but excess oil can pass through unhindered. The venting channel
consists of multiple smaller channels in the fork to minimize the ﬂow resistance imposed by the
structure[83]. The venting channel works, as increases in pressure, increases the ﬂow out of the
venting channel, and as the outlet is open, there is little resistance, which equilibrates pressure
on the other side of the venting channel, whereas ﬂuctuations resulting in low pressure, results
in a decrease in ﬂow out of the venting channel, keeping the pressure in equilibrium.
4.2
Conclusion
This study successfully implemented the picoinjection module in an existing series of micro
droplet systems present in the research group at DTU Nanotech and at Symphogen A/S.
The electric pulse, required for destabilizing the surfactant stabilized droplets, was triggered
by pattern recognition in a signal produced by refracted and scattered laser light from the
micro droplets. Light was transported to and from the chip by optical ﬁbers, which was easily
inserted into the PDMS chip, and the detection method required minimal eﬀort in regards
to fabrication. The optical signal was able to detect and trigger on droplets themselves or
on encapsulated cells in the droplets, which gave a distinct pattern to the droplet signal.
The signal and the electrodes were so well synchronized, that droplets could be selectively
injected, ensuring that the detected droplet chosen for injection, was indeed also the injected
droplet. This was shown by injecting only every second or third drop. It was seen that by
pressurizing the outlet, the injections became very stable, and the ratios of empty to injected
droplets only varied with <1 % compared to the expected values. It was also seen that the
==PAGE:84==
66
Discussion and Conclusions
ﬂuorescence intensity (and hence the volume) of the injected droplets was independent on
how many droplets were skipped between injections. Because such good control over selective
injection was possible, the system was past the ﬁrst step towards combinatorial injections.
Parameters were explored which aﬀected the injections.
These included the geometry of
the channel, for example the shape of the injection nozzle, the electrode placement, and
the reinjection- and spacing module.
In the operation of the device, droplet size, speed
and spacing were explored, as well as required voltage, delay between droplet detection and
electrode triggering, and how pressure aﬀected the stability and volume of the injections.
The picoinjection system was sensitive to pressure ﬂuctuations in the system, periodic ﬂuc-
tuations in injected volume, based on the ﬂuorescence intensity of the droplets injected with
ﬂuorescein, was suspected to originate from pulsating ﬂows generated by the syringe pumps.
Other pressure ﬂuctuations in the system or the environment made it impossible to precisely
control the injected volume between experiments, and it was postulated that the pressure,
which resulted in a stable meniscus at the oriﬁce where the injection channel met the main
channel, was insuﬃcient to replenish the injected ﬂuid, which was transported away at the
nozzle with each injection. This caused a depletion of liquid from the injection channel, in
turn resulting in a reduction in injected volume over a time course of 20 - 30 seconds. Stability
could be restored by adjusting the pressure manually, but manually adjusting pressure every
minute is quite incompatible with running long screens on the system.
Ways to overcome pressure sensitivity was suggested, and involved pressurizing the outlet of
the chip to increase the over all pressure in the chip. This reduced the CV from a minimum of
17% (max. 38%) without pressurizing the outlet to a minimum 9% (max. 18%) pressurizing
the outlet. Other suggestions involves adding a pressure ventilation channel before the pi-
coinjector as described by Rhee et al.[83], adding ﬂow resistors to the oil spacing and injection
channel, changing the shape of the oil spacing oriﬁce, and adding a larger ﬂuid reservoir closer
to the picoinjection channel.
The shape and position of the electric ﬁeld generated by the on chip electrodes was investigated
by simulations using the COMSOL Multiphysics® software. It was found that the electrode
design of the picoinjector chip, resulted in a localized ﬁeld with the maximum ﬁeld strength
positioned just before the injection nozzle, which is believed to be the optimal placement, as
the localized ﬁeld prevents destabilization of droplets further upstream, and destabilizes the
injected droplet just as it enters the injection nozzle. It was also seen that the position of
the ﬁeld could be improved by adding another ground electrode on the opposite side of the
working electrode, as this increased the spot size where a high ﬁeld strength was seen, but
did not change the position relative to the injector nor did the ﬁeld stretch further upstream
of the injector. This conﬁguration was not tested, as the initial design worked ﬁne, and the
process from a new design to a ﬁnished chip takes a long time.
Viability of SEMK2 cells, a leukaemia derived suspension cell line, was investigated in droplets
containing diﬀerent concentrations of surfactant, and in diﬀerent sized droplets, by analysis
in a ﬂow cytometer. This was done to establish a base line for a viability assay, in which the
cancer cells should be encapsulated with an antibody known to cause apoptosis. Unfortunately
the data was very inconclusive, and no conclusion could be reached based on the experiments,
and it was decided not to continue with the viability assay, instead focusing on optimizing
the injector.

4.2.1
Future Aspects
The picoinjector presented here, has the potential to become a very powerful tool in a droplet
systems pipeline with only a moderate eﬀort. To continue the work described in this thesis,
it is the author’s recommendation to begin with the implementation of a pressure stabilizer,
as ﬂuctuations in pressure was the major limitation to the current system. It is believed
that this simple addition to the chip design will provide stable injection, allowing for further
characterization of the chip.
In order for the injector to be useful, the parameter space
involving the volume of the injected ﬂuid must be explored. These parameters are: pressure
in the injection channel, reinjection ﬂow rate and onset/delay of the electric pulse. In order
to do this, one must also determine the relation between ﬂow rate and the required delay of
the electric pulse.
A suggestion to the next design iteration is shown in Figure 4.5. As the ﬂow in the ventilation
channel changes with ﬂuctuations in the pressure, it is important that the optical ﬁbers are
placed between the ventilation channel and the injector, otherwise the delay would be diﬀerent
from drop to drop. It is recommended to install a high speed camera in order to conﬁrm the
results obtained using ﬂuorescein and the current set-up, as catching the droplets passing by
the injection channel was near impossible with an ordinary microscope camera, using the ﬂow
rates reported is this work.
Figure 4.5: Suggestion for future picoinjection design. A pressure ventilation channel is suggested
just upstream of the picoinjector. It is imperative that the optical ﬁbers detecting the
passing droplets are placed between the ventilation channel and the injector to ensure an
equal delay for all drops. In this way it does not matter if droplets are equally spaced.
Once the picoinjector is well established and able to run stably with a low CV for extended
periods of time, it can be useful for many applications, and can even be used for multiple
purposes in the same assay. Here is an example: cell encapsulation is a stochastic process,
which results in a large portion of empty droplets which is of no value to the experiment.
If each droplet containing a cell requires the addition of a reagent, the picoinjector will be
able to selectively inject droplets only if it contains a cell. This injection increases the droplet
volume substantially, which means that the droplets can be passively sorted based on size after
injection, for example by a bumper array. This means that not only is the reagent added only
to the droplets which is of interest, but empty droplets are also discarded in the process.
==PAGE:86==
68
Discussion and Conclusions
Typically only 30 % of the droplets contain a cell, which means that by this enrichment will
greatly reduce the number of droplets which needs further processing, for example sorting.
It might also be interesting to investigate, if droplets can be injected with single cells, in
order to overcome the stochastic encapsulation process. If the injected volume can become
well deﬁned by stabilizing the chip, one can imagine having a high concentration of cells in
the picoinjector, which allows single cells to be added to each droplet.
Another application of the picoinjector is for dilution of reactions, for example PCR reactions
in droplets.
One step in the Ab screens performed in the ARiM project, is an RT-PCR
reaction.
The RT-PCR reaction is inhibited in small volumes (below 3-5 nL), the exact
molecule resposible is not yet known, but is part of the cell lysate. The picoinjector can thus
be useful for increasing the droplet volume after cell lysis in order for the RT-PCR to work.
It is also necessary to pair the genetic information about the heavy- and light chain of the
antibody produced by one cell, which requires two PCR steps (Nested PCR). The picoinjector
could be very useful for adding the reagents needed in the second PCR into each individual
droplet, which carries the DNA from the previous PCR. This step is currently carried out in
bulk with puriﬁed DNA from the ﬁrst PCR step in droplets, but the bulk reaction have many
risks of getting contaminated from other DNA, which will ruin the entire reaction. the risk
of cross contamination is also very high, and the puriﬁcation process laborious.
Serial combinations of picoinjectors on the same chip can allow large combinations of reagents,
for example diﬀerent antibodies and cell lines. In cancer research, it is often interesting to test
diﬀerent combinations of antibodies (in diﬀerent concentrations) on diﬀerent cell lines. This
increases the chance of ﬁnding which cell lines are sensitive to which antibodies, and some
antibodies experience synergistic eﬀects when paired with other antibodies[30]. By adding 5
picoinjectors on a single chip, 32 combinations can be made, by increasing this number to
10 picoinjectors, 1000 combinations can be made, and hence the possibility for combinations
increase dramatically with the number of injectors on a chip.
It is thus clear to see that the picoinjector has the possibility to become a powerful tool, which
can be used in many aspects of droplet microﬂuidics for several distinct purposes.
