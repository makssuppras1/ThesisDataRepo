2. Abstract 
 
The purpose of this thesis was to characterize the ability to process brewers spent grain (BSG) of several 
machines: A rotary drum press (RDP), a screw press and two homogenizers. The BSG was generated by 
brewing a 550 L wort with 100 kg Weyermann pilsner malt. The pressed BSG fractions produced (solid frac-
tions & liquid fractions) was analyzed by the following methods: Water content, ash content, total protein 
content, soluble protein content, dry particle size distribution, wet particle size distribution, total phenolic 
content (TPC), sugar content and antioxidative abilities.  
The RDP and the screw press proved that they could press out >50 % of the water content.  The type 1 ho-
mogenizer did not have an effect on the BSG while the type 2 homogenizer made the PGW fractions into 
sludges.  
The maximum dry matter % reached was 39 % with the average of the solid fractions being around 31 % 
and the liquid fractions being around 10 %. The ash content was consistent for all experiments with the av-
erage for solid fractions being 1 – 1.5 % and 0.1 – 0.5 for the liquid fractions. The average total protein was 
around the same concentration as the BSG for the solid fractions at 20-23 DM%. The protein content w/w 
total % of the solid fracted increased by 20-40 % after being pressed. The homogenized liquid fractions 
showed an increased total protein content and increased viscosity with the highest measured protein % at 
39 w/w DM%.  
Soluble protein was found to make up for ~1 % of the total protein content with the samples having a con-
centration of 100-500 mg/L. Maltose was found to be the predominant sugar present and homogenized frac-
tions showed reduced glucose content. The maltose concentration averaged 10-30 mg/mL for the pressed 
fractions and 60-80 mg/mL for the worts. The glucose concentrations were on average 7-9 mg/mL for the 
worts, 1-5 mg/mL for the liquid fractions and 0.1-0.5 mg/mL for the homogenized fractions. 
The dry particle size distributions were disrupted by BSG aggregation, but the homogenized fractions had a 
significantly lower average particle size. The wet particle size distributions were divided into worts and the 
PGW and sludges. The worts had a similar distribution while the rest had identical distributions. The worts 
had most of its particle surface area made up of particles with a diameter of 0.3-1.5 µm while for the rest 
the diameter was 4-16 µm. 
The TPC value of the worts were 500-600 mg GAE/L and the average PGW fraction had 200-300 GAE/L with 
the sludges being at 200-400 GAE/L. The antioxidant data were inconclusive because of protein precipita-
tion.  
The screw press and the RDP have produced varying results depending on the settings and parameters, but 
overall, this thesis could not place one above the other for processing BSG.

4. Introduction (Motivation) 
 
In a brewery a lot of malt is used in the brewing process in order to make beer. After the malt has been 
used it becomes brewers spent grain (BSG). BSG is the largest by-product in the beer brewing process. For a 
regular lager around 21-22 kgs of BSG is created for each hectoliter of beer produced (Kunze, 2019). BSG has 
been found to contain a high protein share of the dry weight (up to 28% (Kunze, 2019)).  
Usually the BSG is either sent off to be used as animal fodder, or it is burned at the brewery.  
In 2019 annual global production of beer was 1.91 billion hectoliters (Statista, 2021) and in turn the annual 
BSG production can be estimated at ~40 million metric tonnes. 
There is thus much potential for BSG to become a main source of protein in the food industry. The big prob-
lem with BSG, however, is its high water content when exiting the beer production. With its water content 
being around 70-80 % w/w (Kunze, 2019), plus residual sugars and protein content the risk of fungal or bac-
terial growth taking place is inevitable. 
A simple way to combat decay of the BSG is to reduce the water content, but this is also a very costly pro-
cess as it takes a lot of energy to dry the BSG by heat.  
The energy needed to dry the BSG can be reduced by processing the BSG mechanically. The idea is that the 
water content can be sufficiently reduced so that it may become economically viable to dry the BSG to a 
level, where it can be used in food products. A way to mechanically reduce the water content is to use a 
food grade press. An example of a press is the screw press which have been used in the food industry the 
last decades. An alternative press to use is the Dacofi© filter press. This is a custom-made press designed at 
DTU brewery. This press can process around 1 ton of BSG an hour. If BSG are to be used as a material in the 
food industry, the brewery needs to be able to process the BSG fast and at low costs. The food grade 
presses might be a solution solve this problem and are therefore interesting to investigate. 
On top of the processed BSG, the watery fractions, which are pressed out, can maybe also find a use in the 
food industry.  
In the food industry homogenizers are used to reduce materials to pastes. This ability could be utilized 
when processing BSG with the presses.  
The BSG are to be analyzed nutritionally, physically and chemically. Knowing the physical parameters helps 
in designing the processing of the BSG. Knowing the nutritional values identifies whether the product can 
be of economically interesting, and the chemical parameters will help determine which additives are 
needed for the processed product.  
No study has been made which compares the ability of different presses to process BSG. Nor one which ho-
mogenizes BSG before pressing it. This thesis will investigate these topics.

5. Goal 
 
The goal of this thesis is to characterize the custom-made Dacofi© filter press’ (Rotary Drum Press) ability 
to process BSG. The Rotary Drum Press will be compared with a commercially available screw press.  
A custom-made homogenizer will also have its ability to process BSG evaluated. There is also a commercial 
homogenizer alternative to compare the custom-made against. 
The categorization of the machines ability to process the BSG will be done by analyzing the processed frac-
tions produced. The physical and chemical properties, which are investigated, are dry matter %, ash con-
tent, total protein content, soluble protein content, wet particle size distribution, dry particle size distribu-
tion, total phenolic content and antioxidant ability. The parameters were chosen because they describe the 
BSG nutritionally, physically or chemically. Besides this, the machines utilized will also be evaluated by mass 
balancing.

6.1 Brewing Equipment and Brewing Recipe 
 
For this master thesis all the brewers spent grain (BSG) was made on site with the brewing equipment of 
DTU Brewery. DTU Brewery has a fully functional small-scale brewery in which the BSG can be made. As 
only the BSG and wort is of interest for the thesis, I will not explain the brewing process beyond the making 
of the wort. The wort sample is taken just before the boiling of the wort has begun.  
 
6.1.1 Recipe & Brewing Profile 
For all the brews regular pilsner malt was being used and all the malt used throughout the experiments was 
from the same batch. The reason only one type of malt is used is because the different malt types differ in 
chemical composition and this type of BSG is the most common kind. This way the results are much easier 
to compare if the BSG is uniform. All the ingredients will not differ from brew to brew. For the sake of con-
sistency and comparability of results all the brews have been done in the same way using the same recipe 
every time.

6.1.2 Mash Tun  
The mash tun is custom made with a workable volume of ca. 350 L. The mash tun can rotate the water con-
tents through a pipe system and in this way the water can flow out from both atop the malt cake and below 
the malt cake. The pipe system is also able to move up and down in the mash tun, this is done by manually 
using a fixed drill which lowers or raises the pipes depending of the screwing direction.  
The mash tun has an open top and is equipped with a glass window at the bottom. The mash tun can be

While the mash tun has many up sides and can do a lot of stuff, it has some short comings when doing cir-
culation of the malt cake. While the rotation of the pipes should loosen the cake, it often just rotates the 
entire cake, which doesn’t improve the extraction of sugars from the malt. This can be solved by manually 
loosening the malt cake frequently. This flaw of the system can be the reason for differences in the out-
come of the brewing process.  
Having much of the brewing process being done manually can lead to a lot of small differences in the brew-
ing process. These small differences can add up and make different worts and in turn different BSG. To 
avoid this problem, it would be beneficial to have the BSG be made off campus and order a large enough 
amount to last for the many experiments which needs to be done. This will make the starting product uni-
form and one more variable can be removed.  
 
6.1.3 Sparging Vessel 
While the wort is being made in the mash tun, water is getting heated in a heating vessel besides it. The 
vessel is ca. 250 L and is used for heating most of the water for the mashing process.  
The sparging vessel is connected to the mash tun so it can be pumped into the mash tun. The issue is that 
the water is being pumped in from the bottom. When the sparging phase is started, the water needs to 
come from the top to extract as much as possible of sugars left in the malt cake. The sparging vessel is con-
nected to a pump which is how it pumps its water. This pump can be rerouted to a flexible pipe which can 
then be put at the top of the mash tun. This way the sparging water can come down unto the BSG cake and

6.2 Rotary Drum Press 
 
The Rotary Drum Press (RDP) also called the Dacofi© Filter Press is a custom-made press with four rollers. 
The RDP can be used for many types of materials, but this thesis will only discuss the application of the RDP 
with BSG. In the middle of the four rollers there is a pressure chamber. Two of the rollers have a channel 
system installed along with a filter for collection of filtrate from the pressed BSG. The two rollers without 
filters press against the ones with the filters creating the pressure in the chamber. Both sets of rollers ro-
tate, but the sets rotate in opposite directions so the BSG is pressed outwards and inwards. The two filters 
are a 100-micron filter along with a 300-micron filter. Each of these filters create pressed BSG and pressed 
BSG water. For the rest of the thesis the pressed BSG will be referred to as a solid fraction and the pressed 
BSG water will be termed the Pressed Grain Water (PGW) fraction. This way the RDP produces four frac-
tions for each run: A 100-micron solid fraction, a 100-micron PGW, a 300-micron solid fraction and a 300-
micron PGW.  
The two rollers with a filter are supposed to be connected to a pump which then will pump the PGW out of 
the RDP and into a container of choice. The BSG is fed to the RDP from the top on which a funnel can be 
mounted. The schematic of the RDP can be seen in figure 3 (Frødig, 2017). The RDP has a capacity of 
around 1 ton per hour. 
 
The problem with the RDP in its current state is that the solid fractions are a hassle to collect as they need 
to be manually rather than automatically collected. Around the RDP a metal casing has been put in place. 
This casing makes sure that the BSG does not fall off at the sides, but it greatly lowers the pathway to

6.3 Screw Press 
 
A screw press is a press that works by propelling material forwards by the use of a screw. At the end of the 
screw is a piston which is pressing against the opening of the screw chamber. The screw presses material 
against the piston and in so doing the water is pressed out of the material. This water will pass through the 
filter which surrounds the screw. The material will slowly pass by the piston in the small opening between 
the piston and the screw.  
As only BSG will be pressed during this thesis, the screw press will be evaluated from a standpoint of press-
ing BSG.  
The screw press used for the thesis is a Vincent CP-4 of which the first model was introduced in 1994 
(VincentCorporation, 2021). The manufacturer says the screw can go up to 30 rpm, but this is limited by the 
motor used for the screw. The screw press used has a limit of 60hz. The speeds used for this thesis were 
15hz, 30hz and 50hz. 15hz is equal to ≈5 rpm, 30hz ≈ 10 rpm and 50hz is equal to ≈17 rpm. The screw press 
is shown in figure 4. Two filters were available for the screw press: A 0.2 mm filter and a 0.35 mm filter. For 
this thesis the 0.35 mm was used for almost all experiments, and the 0.2 mm one was tested once.

The Vincent CP-4 is equipped with a wide funnel for the screw press in which the BSG is put. As the funnel 
is quite wide and is made for a lot of material, your arm can easily get in there. The problem for the screw 
press is that BSG cakes can form if a lot of BSG is put into the funnel at the same time. These cakes will have

wooden stomper was used to disperse the cakes. The screw could still catch the stomper, so when working 
with the screw press it is important to keep these dangers in mind.   
The screw press makes two fractions when pressing BSG: a solid fraction and PGW fraction. The solid frac-

filter and ends in the tray. The tray has a hole through which the liquid run but this hole is slightly elevated, 
so the water doesn’t run out right at the start. A tube can be secured to the hole and the liquid can then be 
redirected to a container.

6.4 Homogenizers 
 
A homogenizer is a machine that lowers the average particle size of a material. This can be achieved by 
crushing or chopping up the material. This thesis will discuss the two types of homogenizers used and as 
BSG was the only material used, the homogenizers effects will be based solely on BSG.  With the homoge-
nizers homogenizing the BSG, it should be easier to have more of the BSG exposed to water and conse-
quently more compounds should be extractable.  
 
6.4.1 Type 1 Homogenizer 
 
The first homogenizer (type 1) used was a custom-made homogenizer which was codesign by a technologist 
at the DTU Brewery. This machine will first chop the BSG by the use of rotating choppers. The chopped ma-
terial is then transported to a crusher by a screw conveyer. could easily cause a lot of damage if a hand 
should get caught in them, so to avoid this, the choppers are covered by a shield which doubles as a funnel. 
For this thesis the crusher was not operational so the BSG was only chopped by this homogenizer. The type 
1 homogenizer is shown in figure 6.

6.4.3 Homogenizer Shortcomings 
 
The type 1 homogenizer are designed to be easily moveable which is definitely an advantage of the ma-
chine, but the machine parts are not meant to be separated. The shield which covers the choppers were 
secondarily designed as a funnel. The shield is very simple in its design but its use as a funnel isn’t practical. 
The funnel needs to be wider to avoid spillage of BSG during processing.  
As the homogenizer is still in early design it was prone to break downs and would do so, the times it was 
used. The reason for the break down was not disclosed, so I do not know the specific problem which caused 
the break down, but this flaw must be identified and fixed before the homogenizer can go commercial.  
The choppers might prove too rough to reduce the BSG in size and that might render this homogenizer use-
less for BSG purposes until the second part of the homogenizer is operational, but that remains to be seen.

7. Batches and Experiments 
 
This section will explain what experiments where done and how they were done. The experimental samples 
gathered will also be explained in this section. 
 
7.1 Experiment Premise 
 
Five different brews were made during this thesis. Each brew produced an amount of BSG for the pro-
cessing. Each batch of BSG had different processing done to gather as much data as possible. As the 
amount of BSG made during each brew were around 120 kg, the number of experiments which could be 
undertaken, was limited, and even more so, because I decided that the BSG should be processed the same 
day or the next day at the latest. In the cases where the BSG was to be processed the day after brewing, the 
unprocessed BSG was stored in a cold room (~4-5 ˚C) to postpone rotting. 
The experiments conducted were numbered to reflect the BSG batch, from which they originated (1 for 
first batch and so on). The data in this thesis is therefore divided into five subsections based on which batch 
of BSG was used for it. Naming of the samples is done based on batch number and experiment type.  
In the rest of this chapter, I will give an introduction to which experiments were conducted, what sample 
types were gathered for each batch, and the reason why the specific experiments were conducted. Each 
cycle of experiments started with brewing a wort, which would be made into beer. This way we made sure 
that the BSG produced was as close to what BSG from a larger brewery would look like as possible. 
All experimental samples gathered was stored in a freezer to avoid spoilage.

8.1.2 Part Discussion 
 
The type 2 homogenizer had a huge impact on the PGW fractions of the two runs done with it. The thicker 
PGW fractions suggest a high protein content and/or fiber/lignin content. These sludges will be analyzed 
and if the properties of the sludges are desirable, then it would be interesting to work more with the ho-
mogenizer type 2. The type 1 homogenizer have not impacted the mass balance considerably. 
It should not be forgotten that the two presses both have manual gathering. The RDP must have its solid 
fractions gathered manually while the screw press has much liquid fraction which must be gathered manu-
ally as it is collected in a tray. There is definitely some waste coming from just having to handle it manually 
and not automatically.  
Which one of the presses that can press out most water from the BSG, are still to be concluded. Looking 
just at the solid fractions compared to the liquid fractions, the 15hz screw are very close to the RDP. The 
composition of the fractions will have to be looked at to conclude, which press produces the driest pressed 
BSG, and which gives largest mass of dried BSG.  
The BSG lodged into the dead space of the RDP is a flat amount which seems to change depending on the 
water amount in the BSG being run. It should also not be forgotten that the process it far from automated, 
which means that the RDP will run until a decision is made that no more BSG are going to come out. This 
does give a source of error, which can affect the results a bit.  
 
8.2 Dry Matter %, Water Content and Ash Content 
 
The dry matter % of the unpressed and pressed BSG is very important as it determines how much energy 
must be used in order to dry the BSG. Along with knowing the BSG dry matter %, the wort and the pressed 
BSG water is also tested. While testing the dry matter % the ash content % was also tested. The methods 
for analyzing the dry matter content (w/w %) and the ash content (w/w %) is explained firstly, and then the 
data for each analysis is presented simultaneously.

8.2.1 Drying Method 
 
The sample is placed in a porcelain crucible and then the crucible is placed in an oven at 104 ˚C overnight. 
The weight of the crucible and the sample mass is noted. The next day the crucible is put to cool off in an 
exicator, and afterwards the crucible is weighed again. The exicator is used to avoid that the sample ab-
sorbs water from the air whilst cooling With these 3 measurements the dry matter % can be calculated. The 
amount of dry matter in the crucible is calculated by subtracting the mass of the crucible pre oven to the 
post oven mass. The dry matter is then divided by the sample mass to find the dry matter %. The water % is 
just the dry matter % subtracted from 100 %.  
For accurate measurements each sample was tested three times (triplicate).  
 
8.2.2 Ash Content Method 
 
The ash content is done by continuing the drying process. After the crucible is weighed after the oven, it is 
put into the furnace at 600 ˚C overnight. This process will burn everything in the sample and leave only ash 
to be weighed. The crucibles are also put into an exicator to cool off and then they are weighed. The ash 
content is calculated the same way the dry matter is calculated.

8.3 Total Protein 
 
The total protein content is very interesting to know as the demand for green proteins becomes higher and 
higher. All samples had their total protein amount analyzed by the Dumas analysis. 
 
8.3.1 Method 
 
The Dumas analysis is method to determine the content of nitrogen in a sample. By knowing the mass of 
the analyzed sample along with protein to nitrogen ratio of the sample, the total amount of protein in the 
sample can be determined. The sample is heated to 900 °C in the presence of oxygen. This will lead to the 
release of CO2, H2O and N2. The CO2 and H2O are removed by the use of two special columns which ab-
sorb the gasses. The remaining 𝑁2 passed through the columns and a signal is measured by a thermal con-
ductivity detector. By measuring the signal of standard samples which’ nitrogen concentration is known, 
the nitrogen in the food sample can be determined. When the nitrogen % in the sample is known, it is mul-
tiplied by a specific nitrogen to protein factor. Afterwards the protein % of the sample is then known 
(McClements, 2021).    
The method gives the result in nitrogen (w/w %) of dry matter. A factor is used to convert the nitrogen into 
protein. This factor differs depending on the food material. For this thesis the factor 6.25 is used. All sam-
ples were tested 3 times.  
 
Samples for the Dumas assay has to be almost completely devoid of water. While a small amount of water 
can be okay, it is easier to have completely dry samples. Samples for the Dumas analysis were therefore 
dried. Often the drying of the samples was done at the same time as analyzing the dry matter % and ash 
content %.

8.4 Soluble Protein 
 
The soluble protein content is quantifiable by the use of the Bradford assay. This is a quite simple assay. 
The assay consists of mixing the Bradford reagent with the samples and letting the solution change color. 
After the incubation time the samples are run through a spectrophotometer and the light is measured at a 
specific wavelength. Besides the samples this is also done to some standards. When the intensities of the 
samples have been measured, the standards are used to make a standard curve from which the concentra-
tion of the samples can be calculated. For this thesis the worts and sludges were diluted 20 times before 
testing and the rest was diluted 10 times.  
The standard concentrations used to make the standard curve were: 25 µg/mL, 20 µg/mL, 15 µg/mL, 10 
µg/mL, 5 µg/mL, 2.5 µg/mL and 0 µg/mL. 
To make sure nothing beside the proteins reacts with the reagent, the samples are centrifuged beforehand. 
The centrifuge used ran at 4200 rpm with a run time of 10 min which included the acceleration time. The 
supernatant is then used for the Bradford Assay. Each sample was tested twice (duplicates). Duplicates 
should be good enough for this analysis. 
The manual for the Bradford assay is shown in appendix 3 (see attached appendix).

8.5 Sugar Content 
 
High Pressure Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is a method used to analyze liquid samples of its dissolved 
contents. For this thesis HPLC was used to quantify the amount of maltose and glucose in the samples. 
These two molecules are the predominant sugars in beer wort which in turn makes them present in the 
BSG and its processed fractions.  
The liquids to be analyzed by HPLC cannot contain any solids and must therefore be filtered. The liquid is 
filtered into HPLC vials which are made specially for the HPLC machine. The vials are 2 mL and needs to be 
filled with at least 1 mL to ensure accurate readings. Besides the worts all liquid fractions were opaque and 
even the worts contained a lot of smaller particles in suspension. To avoid clogging the filters all the liquid 
fractions were centrifuged before the supernatant were to be filtered. The centrifuge used were running at 
4200 rpm. The time was 10 min which included the acceleration time.  
Along with the samples, maltose and glucose standards also had to be run along them. The standards make 
it possible to accurately calculate the concentration in the samples. Four standards of both maltose and 
glucose were made. The standard concentrations were: 10 mg/mL, 5 mg/mL, 2.5 mg/mL, 1.25 mg/mL.  
For this thesis every sample was tested twice (duplicates). Only the liquid fractions were tested by HPLC. 
The solid fractions could be measured but a lengthy extraction would have to be carried out first, which 
might not yield that precise data anyway. Because of this, the solid fractions were omitted.

8.6 Dry Particle Size Distribution 
 
To get an estimate of the particle size in the solid fractions, the samples were sieved. Due to the fact that 
initial sampling of <1 kg was first started to be done from batch 3, there is no data for the particle size of 
the solid fraction from batch 1 and 2. The sieving is done to get a size estimate of the different fractions. 
The results are used to compare between fractions to spot differences and identify the causes. 
 
8.6.1 Method  
 
The sieving is done in a special shaking sieve. The sieve could be programed for 3 different rotation speeds 
and for different time lengths. The speed chosen was 300 rpm for 10 min. This made sure that the particles 
had been distributed properly. The sieve had 5 different filter sizes (see table 34). This gave 6 filtered frac-
tions. 
Table 34 – Particle size intervals of the sieve 
Intervals (X = particle size) 
 X >1.25 mm 
1.25 mm > X > 1 mm 
1 mm > X > 0.5 mm 
0.5 mm > X > 0.25 mm 
0.25 mm > X > 0.125 mm 
0.125 mm > X 
  
Before the sieving can commence the sample needs to be dry, because else the wet BSG would stick to the 
filters. This were done by drying the BSG sample beforehand. As the BSG samples were stored at -30 ˚C the 
BSG was very hard to take sieving samples of. To circumvent this problem, the sample containers were left 
out for at least an hour before a sample could be obtained. The BSG cake would thaw mostly at the sides of

the container but that was enough to obtain enough sample material for sieving. Depending of the water 
content in the sample, the sample was either easy or very hard to remove from the container. Higher water 
content meant a more solid clump of BSG.  
The drying was done in aluminum trays in an oven at 104 ˚C for 2 nights. There wasn’t a specific amount 
dried each time as the sieving results is given in percentages, but the aim was to have above 100 g of dry 
sample matter. This was achieved by drying 350-450 g wet sample matter, depending on the samples dry 
matter % (See dry matter section). The weight of the tray was noted along with the wet matter and then 
the tray was weighted after drying. 
After the 10 min of shaking, the particles had been divided into the 6 intervals. These 6 fractions were 
weighted and then afterwards the ratio between the fractions were calculated. The results are given in % of 
total mass (w/w %).

8.8 Total Phenolic Content 
 
The Total Phenolic Content (TPC) can give indications to the antioxidant activity of the sample as most phe-
nols can act as antioxidants. Besides this, phenols can also be aroma compounds or harmful (usually the 
natural occurring phenols are not harmful). If the BSG are to be treated with chemicals down the line, some 
could react with the phenolic content in which case it would be valuable to know the TPC of the fraction. 
 Only the liquid fractions have been tested, as the solid fractions would contain only small amounts of phe-
nolic content, and they were also more time consuming to conduct as the phenolic contents would have to 
be extracted before the assay could be done.  
The way the TPC is measured is by use of the Folin-Ciocalteu (FC) method. The principle of the FC method is 
that the phenolic compounds react with a redox reagent, the Folin-Ciocalteu reagent (FC reagent), which 
creates a blue complex. A higher TPC means a more intense blue color. The color intensity is measured us-
ing a spectrophotometer. The FC reagent quickly decomposes in an alkaline solution, and therefore acid is 
added to the reagent. The reagent is yellow and to makes sure that the excess amount of reagent is not in-
terfering with the color, the extra reagent is precipitated using sodium carbonate. This leaves the solution 
with a high turbidity making it unreadable by the spectrophotometer. This is counteracted by adding lith-
ium salts to the FC reagent which removes the turbidity. The lithium salts bind the precipitated FC reagent 
which then dissolves again. 
For how the analysis was done, see appendix 5 (see attached appendix).  
All the data is stated in µg gallic acid equivalents (GAE) / mL. The measured absorbance of each sample is 
calculated into the µg GAE / mL by the use of a standard curve, which is made from 7 different concentra-
tions of gallic acid (166.67 µg gallic acid / mL was not used, as I was instructed that it could be omitted). The 
samples were all centrifuged at 4200 rpm for 10 minutes including the acceleration time before the super-
natant was tested. Each sample was tested three times (triplicates).

reliable result. This is, however, only half true as some reducing sugars do and some don’t. In the article 
mentioned in the TPC assay manual (Prasad Bastola, 2017), xylose and glucose where both tested. It was 
concluded that xylose does impact the results, but glucose do not. The two reducing sugars present in the 
samples are glucose and maltose. As glucose showed to not interfere with the results, I am going to assume 
that neither does maltose.

8.9 DPPH Radical Collection Ability 
 
The DPPH Radical Collection Ability Assay (DPPH Assay) is an antioxidant assay, in which a solutions ability 
to inhibit oxidation is tested using the reagent 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH). DPPH is a stable radical 
which can be used to evaluate antioxidants’ ability to scavenge free radicals or hydrogen atom donors.  
==PAGE:64==
S154042 
DTU 
Master Thesis 
Rasmus Frederik Helsted Kristensen 
 
5-Aug-21 
Page 63 
 
The assay measures the inhibition% of a solution. The EC50 is calculated from the assay data. EC50 is the 
concentration of antioxidant which is needed to achieve 50 % inhibition. This means that a solution with a 
low EC50 is better at scavenging radicals than ones with a high EC50 value. The EC50 value is used to com-
pare compounds/solutions. The antioxidative ability is hard to quantify numerical but EC50 makes it possi-
ble to pinpoint, which antioxidant is stronger. 
In this assay butylated hydroxy toluene (BHT) is used as positive control for the assay. BHT should have an 
inhibition% of 70-80 %.   
A solution of DPPH is made using ethanol (the solution has a purple color almost like a potassium perman-
ganate solution). Because DPPH is dissolved in ethanol, the rest of the solutions will also be made using eth-
anol. The assay is done by adding DPPH to the samples and letting them incubate in darkness for 30 
minutes, and after the incubation the samples have their intensity read by a spectrophotometer. Along 
with the samples, there are antioxidant blind samples (BHT and ethanol), there are positive controls (BHT 
and DPPH) and then there are blanks with DPPH (DPPH & ethanol and DPPH & water). All this was carried 
out on microtiter plates.  
The method used to generate the data is described by the manual which is in appendix 6 (see attached ap-
pendix). The samples need to be centrifuged before being tested (as the supernatant is tested). The centrif-
ugation was done at 4200 rpm for 10 minutes including the acceleration time.  
 
8.9.1 Results 
 
For this thesis there was a big problem when doing this assay. The sample values were very unreliable and 
not consistent at all. It was at specific concentrations this occurred. It was discovered that the flawed read-
ings were due to precipitated proteins. Later in the process it was discovered that this could have been 
eliminated by adding an extra assay step. This step would involve incubation in Eppendorf tubes, centrifug-
ing the tubes, and then transfer the supernatant to the microtiter plate for reading.  
I found out too late that this step was recommended and as such I did all the analysis without this step. This 
proved to have detrimental consequences as the data was very inconsistent.  
Besides the problem with the protein, the positive controls had been too long in the freezer as their value 
had dropped below the expected amount.  
For this assay I diluted my samples 1:5. This proved to only be useful for the wort samples but for other 
samples, this dilution made the data values too small.  
As the results of the DPPH assay was unusable the results have been moved to appendix 2. Some of the re-
sults might prove to be accurate but without redoing the assay there is no way of knowing it.

As the data is unusable there is very little to conclude from it. The samples do show some trends such as 
the wort samples have about the same value. This was to be expected and the values are wrong so very lit-
tle can be taken from this attempt at a DPPH analysis. The assay must be redone to generate usable data.

9. Discussion 
 
Both of the commercially available machines (screw press and type 2 homogenizer) were both designed 
before the year 2000. It is to be assumed that better models have been designed since then and that the 
price of the older models have fallen significantly.  
There wasn’t any data which indicated that either press was superior, but the type 2 homogenizer from 
Seepex were superior to the type 1 homogenizer. When the smashing part of the type 1 homogenizer be-
comes operational, this might change. This does not bode well that significantly older models can do just 
about the same job as the other and newer models.  
As the screw press produced similar results with some difference depending on screw speed, it is hard to 
determine which speed is best. The 15hz pressed out the most amount of water from the BSG but was also 
considerably slower than the two other speeds tested. Because of the difference in BSG it proved hard to 
compare the different speeds across experiments. If the objective is to reduce the water content the most 
without thinking of time, then the 15hz speed is the best.  
As the 30hz screw press runs had conflicting data surrounding its mass balance percentages, it was not 
clear if the 0.2 mm screw press filter significantly impacted the process, compared to the 0.35 mm filter. 
With the sludges pressed from the homogenized BSG having a very high protein content (33-39 w/w DM%) 
and a relatively high dry matter % at 11-13 w/w %, these fractions could be utilized as additives in food pro-
duction. The fiber/lignin content would still have to be tested but this could conceivably be used as a natu-
ral thickening agent with a high protein content.  
The effects of heat and cooling on the BSG was not tested directly. The microbiological activity and rotting 
of the fractions would be very interesting to examine, because it would provide knowledge on how to treat 
and store the fractions, so that they have a longer duration before turning bad.  
Whether the cooling of the BSG would have an impact on the pressing process for the RDP or the screw 
press, was not tested, and it cannot be ruled out that it could have a large impact on the process though no 
effect were directly observed.  
The samples were heated up each time they were to be analyzed and then put back in the freezer when the 
sample was transferred from the container. Because of this the samples would have been thawed and fro-
zen several times. Sometimes some samples had to be thawed for an extended period of time (>15 min), 
before they could be returned to the freezer. In this thesis it is assumed that the sample composition does 
not change while the sample is thawed. This is, however, a flawed assumption as some of the samples con-
tain enzymes and microbiological activity. For some chemical compounds this assumption is true but for 
some it is wrong. The degradation of the samples would be something which would be interesting to test if 
the project is continued.

For the RDP the 300-micron fractions had a larger mass% share than the 100-micron fractions. Over 50 % of

could press out additional water, if the BSG had already been pressed in the screw press at 50hz.  
Most of the pressed solid fractions had a dry mater % of 30-33 %. The liquid fractions would have an aver-
age of 7-9 dry matter %, while the wort was at 12-14 %. The sludges had a dry matter % of 12-19 %. Ho-
mogenization would increase the dry matter % of the liquid fractions. For the solid fractions the ash con-
tent were 1-1.5 %, compared to 0.1-0.5 % for the liquid fractions.  
The type 1 homogenizer did not provide any measurable impact on the BSG. Using the type 2 homogenizer, 
homogenizing the BSG before pressing it, sludges were produced instead of PGW fractions. For the RDP 
pressing the homogenized BSG yielded less liquid fraction than when pressing the non-homogenized BSG. 
This was the opposite for the screw press.  
The total protein content for the worts were just below 5 %, while most of the solid fractions were either 
just below 20 w/w DM% or above it. While the solid fractions had around the same protein content w/w 
DM%, the protein content w/w total % increased by 20-40 % after being pressed, because of the increased 
DM% of the fractions. The sludges had a total protein content of >25 w/w DM% and some being above 30 
w/w DM%. The homogenized samples showed an increased protein content with the highest total protein 
content measured at 39 w/w DM%.   
The worts showed to have the highest soluble protein concentration of around 500 mg/L. The other liquid 
fractions averaged 100-300 mg/L with the sludges being in the top end of the range. The soluble protein 
was calculated to be around 1 % of total protein.  
Maltose was confirmed to be the most predominant sugar in the liquid fractions with an average of 10-30 
mg/mL for the pressed fractions and 60-80 mg/mL for the worts. The glucose concentrations were on aver-
age 7-9 mg/mL for the worts and 1-5 mg/mL for the liquid fractions. The glucose concentration would drop 
a lot when the BSG had been homogenized before being pressed. The glucose concentration for these sam-
ples were 0.1-0.5 mg/mL. 
The dry particle size distributions had their data disrupted by BSG aggregation. Despite this, the homoge-
nized fractions showed to have a significantly lower average particle size. The results showed similar results 
for the screw press and the RDP. The wet particle size distributions were divided into two groups. The 
worts had one particle size distribution and the rest of the samples had another. A slight protein precipita-
tion was visible in two wort samples. The sludges, PGWs or homogenized PGWs size distributions almost 
weren’t distinguishable from each other. The worts had most of its particle surface area made up of parti-
cles with a diameter of 0.3-1.5 µm while for the rest the diameter was 4-16 µm. 
For TPC the wort fractions were significantly higher than for the rest at 500-600 mg GAE/L and the screw 
sludges showed to have higher TPC than the screw press liquid fraction counterpart. The RDP pressed PGW 
fractions had an average of ca. 200 mg GAE/L. The DPPH data collected are unusable because of protein 
precipitation.  
The screw press and the RDP have produced varying results depending on the settings and parameters, but 
overall, this thesis could not place one above the other for processing BSG.

11. Future Work 
 
For comparing the screw press and the RDP more experiments will have to be done. Only the 50hz screw 
press speed was directly compared with the RDP.  
The whole thesis suffered from not having uniform BSG for each experiment. This has made it hard to com-
pare data across the five batches of experiments.  
The entire DPPH antioxidant assay would have to be redone as the results were unusable.  
A fiber/lignin analysis was not done for this thesis. It would be helpful if the fiber/lignin content of the liq-
uid fractions was analyzed. This would help identify if the high viscosity in the sludges are caused by fibers 
or proteins.  
The soluble protein assay showed inaccuracy. For this it would be beneficial to rerun the assay to confirm 
the values of the thesis.   
For the TCP analysis it will have to be checked whether maltose affects the assay or whether it doesn’t re-
act with the reagent as assumed in this thesis.  
Further testing surrounding the 0.2 mm filter should be done as only one experiment was done with the 0.2 
mm filter, and the data of that experiment didn’t vary considerable from the 0.35 mm filter experiments.  
The degradation of the processed BSG fractions should be examined to better understand how long the 
fractions can be out of the freezer, before they start to deteriorate.  
One detail, which wasn’t put much thought into, is that some breweries (mostly the larger ones) have im-
plemented the use of mash filters. Mash filters replaces the lauter tun and presses as much wort out the 
BSG as possible to obtain the most amount of extract. By the use of a mash filter the BSG is obviously going 
to be much drier than if a lauter tun was used. If a brewery is using a mash filter, the presses might thus not 
be able to reduce the water content of the BSG much further  
The mashing profile of a brew impacts how much extract is extracted from the malt. This thesis did not look 
at how different mashing profiles impact the nutritional values and chemical composition of the BSG. This 
could be interesting to look at, as every brewery uses their own mashing profile when making a wort. 
Other parameters which could be investigated to get a more complete profile of the pressed BSG fractions, 
would be protein distribution, viscosity and digestibility. Digestibility testing is quite expensive to do, so this 
should only be done, when the results are for sure going to be used.
