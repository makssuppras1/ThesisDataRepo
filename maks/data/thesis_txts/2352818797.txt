Abstract
The growing demand for electrical energy, and the desire to produce it via intermittent renewable sources,
calls for a way to e’¼āciently store large amounts of electrical energy. Electrochemical storage systems are
good candidates for energy storage in general; however, for most conventional systems, such as the Li-ion
batteries, there is no economic gain from upscaling the batteries. For this purpose, the redox ’¼éow batteries
have proven to be a more suited technology. To fully understand these systems, fundamental studies of
the electrochemical characteristics of the redox-active materials are necessary.
The aim of the present work was to investigate the heterogeneous kinetics of the quinone anthraquinone-
2-sulfonic acid (AQS), using glassy carbon (GC) as a platform for the experiments. Aqueous solutions of
the quinone were studied using cyclic voltammetry (CV), both with stationary and rotating electrodes,
and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). The in’¼éuence of surface oxidation of GC on the AQS
kinetics was investigated by subjecting the GC electrodes to various degrees of electrochemical oxidation.
The di’¼Ćusion coe’¼ācients of the AQS species were determined from CV experiments, whereas the kinetic
parameters were determined by ’¼ütting equivalent circuits to the recorded Nyquist plots, and through use
of a rotating disk electrode (RDE). It was furthermore attempted to study the cycling stability of AQS
and 1,2-dihydroxybenzene-3,5-disulfonic acid (BQDS) by using aqueous solutions of the two quinones as
anolyte and catholyte, respectively, in a stationary H-cell.
The experimental results indicate that oxidation of GC has a positive e’¼Ćect on the kinetics of AQS,
as was made clear by an increase of the standard heterogeneous rate constant upon oxidation of the used
GC electrodes. The results also indicate that AQS adsorbs to GC, and that the relative contribution
from surface-bound molecules to the observed total current in voltammetric experiments increases with
the oxidation potential of GC. This re’¼éects an increase in the surface area of the oxidised GC surfaces as
the oxidation pre-treatment potential increases.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Humankind depends heavily on the generation of electrical energy, and with an ever-growing global
population, the demand increases each year. In 2012, where the worldwide consumption of electricity
was around 20,000 TWh, it was predicted that the demand will have doubled by 2050 [1, 2].
This
demand is primarily met by burning of fossil fuels, which is not in concord with the need to halt global
warming. Renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar energy, are a sensible solution to the problem
of increasing the energy production without doing irreversible damage to the ecosystem. However, the
intermittent nature of these sources makes it impossible to signi’¼ücantly increase their contribution to the
production of electrical energy without a reconstruction of the current electrical grid. For example, the
production from wind turbines is completely dependent on the weather conditions and typically peaks at
night when the demand is lowest. In order to smooth out the intermittency of renewable energy sources,
large-scale electrical energy storage (EES) facilities should be implemented in the near future. These
facilities will serve several purposes, the most important being to store electrical energy when there is an
excess in the production, and release it to the electrical grid again when the production is insu’¼ācient.
There are several ways to store electrical energy, most of which entails the conversion of electrical
energy to another form of energy. These include the conversion to kinetic energy in ’¼éywheels, to potential
energy in pumped hydroelectricity and compressed air energy storage, and to chemical energy in electro-
chemical systems [3]. All three types of conversions have been successfully tested as EES plants around
the world [4, 5]. However, when looking at both the performance requirements and cost, electrochemical
energy storage seems to be the superior choice for large-scale EES [6].
Among the di’¼Ćerent types of electrochemical systems, redox ’¼éow batteries (RFB) have drawn much
attention in the last four decades, as is evident from the thousands of articles that have been released on
the subject since the 1970s1. RFBs operate in much the same way as conventional secondary batteries,
with two redox pairs shuttling between the oxidised and reduced forms to release energy to a load, or to
receive energy from a power source. The major di’¼Ćerence is that the redox-active species are dissolved
in two electrolytes (the anolyte and catholyte, respectively), which are stored in tanks external to the
cell stack and then pumped through the stack during operation. RFBs as a technology for EES was ’¼ürst
explored by Lawrence Thaller at NASA in the 1970s, who worked with the iron-chromium system [2]. The
’¼ürst operational RFB, which was based on the all-vanadium system, was demonstrated by Skyllas-Kazacos
and co-workers at the University of New South Wales in the late 1980s [5]. RFBs utilising metal ions,
especially the all-vanadium system, have since been widely studied and systems are now available that
can store up to 6 MWh [5]. RFBs can discharge energy over relatively long time periods (up to 10 hours,
compared to e.g. 1ŌĆō5 hours for Li-ion batteries [2]), and they become the best solution economically when
discharge times are longer than 3 hours [2]. This makes them well-suited for large-scale EES. The main
reason why RFBs have not been widely implemented into the electrical grids of the world is cost; the
price is still in the area of $500ŌĆō2500 kWh-1 [5, 7, 2]. The redox-active species typically contribute to
a large part of the system price. For the all-vanadium redox ’¼éow battery (VRB) system, the vanadium

One way to reduce the price is to use other, less expensive types of redox-active materials. Several

organic molecules that have only recently been studied as redox-active materials for RFBs [10]. Quinones
have reversible and fast proton-coupled electron transfer processes [10], which makes them ideal for use
in electrochemical systems.
The work presented in this thesis centres on the investigation of the heterogeneous kinetics of primarily
anthraquinone-2-sulfonic acid (AQS), a quinone that may be used as the redox-active material in the
anolyte of RFBs. The in’¼éuence on the kinetics of AQS of varying degrees of electrochemical oxidation
of glassy carbon (GC), the material used as platform for the experiments, was the focus point of the
investigation. It has previously been shown for VRBs that oxidation of the carbon materials used as
electrodes improves the heterogeneous kinetics of the negative cell reaction [11]. The e’¼Ćect has been
explained as a catalytic e’¼Ćect from the oxygen functional groups introduced on the carbon surface by the
pre-treatment [12]. This has a direct in’¼éuence on the performance of the batteries, as the improved kinetics
will allow the cells to operate at lower overpotentials. It is therefore interesting to investigate whether a

2.1
Working principles and general trends of redox ’¼éow batteries
An RFB roughly consists of three parts: A cell stack, reservoirs for the electrolytes, and a ’¼éow system
for the electrolytes (pipes, pumps, control system, etc.) [10]. The two redox couples are dissolved in two
separate electrolytes, forming the anolyte and catholyte. The electrolytes are pumped through the cell
stack during operation, the anolyte to the negative electrode and the catholyte to the positive electrode.
The electrodes are separated by an ion-selective membrane, which ideally retains the redox-active species,
but allows non-active ions to pass. During discharge, the active species in the anolyte are oxidised. The
generated electrons ’¼éow through the external circuit to the positive side while supplying a load, where
they take part in the reduction of the catholyte active species. The working principle of an RFB is thus
very similar to that of a fuel cell, and they are often called regenerative fuel cells [6]. They can also be
classi’¼üed as a secondary battery system, since the redox reactions are reversible [3]. Figure 1 shows a
simpli’¼üed schematic of an RFB, whereas Figure 2 shows an exploded view drawing of an RFB.

2.1.1
Cell stack
In order to get a su’¼āciently high voltage, several ’¼éow cells are arranged in a stack.
The stacks are
generally arranged in a bipolar fashion in order to make the current ’¼éow in series from one cell to the
next [9]. The current is set by the electrode area, since the available area dictates how many electrons are
released per time unit [13]. The bipolar plates support the porous electrodes and direct the ’¼éow of the
electrolytes, and are therefore often constructed with channels on both sides [4]. The plates must have a
low bulk contact resistance, be structurally and mechanically stable, and be chemically compatible with
the electrolytes during operation [4].
2.1.2
Flow system
Once the electrolyte has been pumped from the reservoir through the cell stack, it is typically returned to
the same reservoir. A disadvantage of doing so is that the relative concentration of oxidised and reduced
species in each electrolyte changes progressively during operation [9], which means that the voltage will
change (according to the Nernst equation). The fact that external reservoirs are required adds to the size
and cost of RFBs. It also puts demand on the delivery system, as a uniform delivery of the dissolved
species to the entire surface area of the electrodes is required [9].
2.1.3
Power and energy density
Due to the separation of cell stack and redox-active materials, power and energy density can be scaled
independently, which is not possible in conventional batteries. The power is determined by the electrode
area and number of cells, but also on how fast the cell reactions are. The power (or power density) can
be improved by using faster redox couples, using electrocatalysts, making a better ’¼éow cell design, or
carrying out electrode surface modi’¼ücations that improve the redox kinetics [2]. Most RFBs have power

2.1.5
Electrolytes
Much of the research that has been carried out has focused on acidic aqueous electrolytes. These types
of electrolytes are cheap and have a low viscosity, which makes it easy to pump around in a ’¼éow system.
However, in aqueous RFB systems the voltage is limited by the potential window of water electrolysis.
A high voltage will cause problems due to the evolution of oxygen and hydrogen gas, which limits the
attainable voltage in aqueous RFBs [10]. Switching to an organic electrolyte would increase the useable
potential window.
However, this would introduce new problems, such as risk of ’¼éammability of the
electrolytes and possibly higher viscosity, which would increase the pumping costs. Regardless of the
choice of electrolyte, it has to be an adequate ionic conductor to provide su’¼ācient rate capability and
minimise resistance and power loss [3]. It is also important that the electrolytes have roughly the same
ionic strength, since a large di’¼Ćerence will drive the transfer of active ions across the membrane due to
the e’¼Ćect of osmotic pressure [5].

Membranes
The ion-selective membrane used to separate the two electrolytes is crucial for the performance of a RFB.

2.1.7
Temperature
The solubility of the redox-active species depends on temperature. This means that once a concentration
of active species has been chosen, the temperature has to be controlled carefully to avoid precipitation
[13]. For organic species, the temperature stability of the structures can further limit the operational
temperature range. Here the working principle of RFBs turns into another advantage, as the electrolytes
serve as coolant for the cell stack; heat produced during the cell reactions is carried away by the continuous
’¼éow of liquid through the stack [13].
2.1.8
Morphology changes and depth of discharge
Due to the fact that the redox-active species are not part of the electrodes, RFBs do not have problems
with morphology changes of the electrode surface, which can be a severe problem for some conventional
batteries due to the formation of dendrites [3, 5]. This gives a more stable and durable performance [8],
and long service lifetimes can be expected from RFB systems (assuming that the remaining components
can also tolerate the oxidising and often acidic environment) [3]. This also means that RFBs can tolerate
deep discharges, making it possible to utilise a much wider range of the energy storage capacity [5]. Cells
can also be left completely discharged for long periods of time without negative e’¼Ćects [5].

GC polishing series
During the course of the experimental work, it was observed (through CV scans in blank 1.0 M H2SO4)
that residues of AQS were adsorbed to the used GC electrodes, even after polishing. A polishing series of
a single GC electrode (GC1I) was therefore conducted to determine how long it would take to completely
clean the GC surface again. This was done by performing many rounds of polishing with the aqueous
1.0 ┬Ąm Al2O3 suspension, and carrying out a CV scan in between each round. A reduction at -1.34 V
for 1 minute and an oxidation at 2.1 V for 5 minutes of the GC electrode were lastly carried out. The
resulting voltammograms are shown in Figure 44 and 45. All measurements were carried out with a CH
Instruments 760E potentiostat.
Figure 44 shows that a noticeable change of the surface characteristic becomes evident after 40 minutes
of polishing, and that each 1 to 1.5 hour round of polishing both decreases the double layer capacitance
and the size of the peaks attributed to the AQS redox reaction.
The inset in Figure 45 once again shows that the reduction treatment of GC has little in’¼éuence on
the double layer capacitance, whereas the oxidation treatment (as seen from the main ’¼ügure) has a huge
impact, in the area of two orders of magnitude. This large increase in double layer capacitance could very
well explain why it is so di’¼ācult to remove the response from AQS molecules: If micro’¼üssures have been
formed on the GC surface, AQS molecules will adsorb to the exposed surface area in the ’¼üssures, and a
very thorough polish, probably with a coarser-grained Al2O3 suspension, would be necessary to remove
the AQS response completely.

6.1.8
Summary
The kinetic parameters of AQS on GC have been determined through a series of experiments. Comparison
of the standard heterogeneous rate constant determined through the di’¼Ćerent types of experiments (see
Section 6.1.1, 6.1.3, and 6.1.7) reveals that the values di’¼Ćer by up to two orders of magnitude, making
the absolute values unreliable. However, the general trend is the same throughout the experiments: Upon
oxidation of GC, the double layer capacitance increases, the charge-transfer resistance decreases, and
consequently the heterogeneous standard rate constant increases. Furthermore, the changes become more
pronounced at high GC oxidation potentials, especially at 1.9 V and above a signi’¼ücant change occurs.
The increase in double layer capacitance is most likely caused by an increase in microscopic area, which is
again caused by the formation of micro’¼üssures in the GC surface upon oxidation. An increase in surface
coverage upon oxidation of the GC electrodes also suggests an increased microscopic surface area.
The impedance of the electrodes was modelled by various equivalent circuits, which all assume a
smooth electrode surface. If the increase in double layer capacitance is indeed caused by the formation of
micro’¼üssures, the use of these models becomes questionable. A porous electrode model would have to be
used instead, which takes into account the contributions from the electrolyte-’¼ülled pores in the surface.
A detailed discussion of these models is beyond the scope of this thesis. However, the R-Q(R-Q) circuit
seemed to model the impedance of the electrodes oxidised at high potentials quite well, which supports
results from cyclic voltammetry that indicate that AQS adsorbs to GC surface.
It was noted that the electrodes oxidised at 1.1 V displayed a more capacitive behaviour than elec-
trodes oxidised at higher potentials. This was caused by a previous oxidation of the electrodes at 2.1 V
and insu’¼ācient polishing before using the electrodes again. This shows that thorough polishing of the
electrodes is important in order to return the surface to a mirror-like state after each experiment. A
coarser-grained Al2O3 powder and higher solid loading in the slurry should therefore have been used
initially to remove the porous layer of oxidised GC. The insu’¼ācient polishing will be a major source of
error of the conducted work.

Future experiments
Achieving an even stirring proved to be a bigger challenge than anticipated, due to the geometry of one

felt electrodes instead of the GC rods is probably a good starting point. Once the performance has been
improved, i.e. lower overpotentials and the possibility to draw higher currents have been achieved, the
H-cell will be cycled between 20 and 80% SOC to follow the stability of the AQS-BQDS system.

7. CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
The aim of the present work was to investigate the heterogeneous kinetics of the quinone AQS on un-
oxidised and oxidised GC surfaces. This was done through a series of experiments involving the char-
acterisation techniques cyclic voltammetry, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, and hydrodynamic
measurements using a rotating disk electrode.
The di’¼Ćusion coe’¼ācients of AQS, in an aqueous solution of sulfuric acid, were determined from a series
of CV scans with increasing scan rates and application of the Randles-Sevcik equation. This yielded values
of 1.94 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ6 cm2 sŌłÆ1 and 1.74 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ6 cm2 sŌłÆ1 for the oxidised and reduced species, respectively, which
compares relatively well with a combined di’¼Ćusion coe’¼ācient of 3.71 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ6 cm2 sŌłÆ1 found in previous
work [17]. The same series of experiments were carried out for the quinone BQDS, where it was however
not possible to determine the di’¼Ćusion coe’¼ācients due to the irreversible kinetics at the applied scan
rates.
EIS was applied to determine the standard heterogeneous rate constant of the two quinones.
Values of 9.77 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ4 cm sŌłÆ1 and 1.22 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ3 sŌłÆ1 were determined for AQS, depending on which equivalent
circuit (Randles or R-Q(R-Q)) was ’¼ütted to the experimental data. A value of 2.55 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ4 cm sŌłÆ1 has
been reported in the literature [17], which is somewhat smaller than what was found here. For BQDS,
consecutive EIS scans yielded considerably di’¼Ćerent charge-transfer resistances, which resulted in the
standard heterogeneous rate constants 3.71 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ5 cm sŌłÆ1 and 5.07 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ5 cm sŌłÆ1 obtained through ’¼ütting
with an R-QR circuit. A value of 1.55 ┬Ę 10ŌłÆ4 cm sŌłÆ1 has previously been reported [17].
A series of GC oxidation experiments were carried out in order to study the in’¼éuence on the AQS
kinetics. The electrodes were oxidised at potentials ranging from 0.9 to 2.1 V vs. Ag/AgCl for 5 minutes
each in a solution of 1.0 M H2SO4. The cyclic voltammograms recorded in a blank solution of 1.0 M H2SO4
showed an increase in charging current, and consequently of the double layer capacitance, with increasing
GC oxidation potential. A decrease in impedance with GC oxidation potential, and therefore an increase
in double layer capacitance, was observed on Nyquist plots recorded in the blank. The capacitance was
quanti’¼üed by ’¼ütting an R-Q circuit to the experimental data, which resulted in an e’¼Ćective capacitance for
each electrode. CV scans in several AQS solutions of approximately 2 mM showed decreasing peak split
and increasing peak currents with increasing GC oxidation potential. This indicated that the total current
is increasingly dominated by contributions from surface-bound molecules, and therefore that AQS adsorbs
extensively to oxidised GC. EIS spectra were recorded in the AQS solutions at the formal potential of
the quinone. A Randles circuit was initially ’¼ütted to the spectra, which resulted in unsatisfactory ’¼üts for
a majority of the GC electrodes. An R-Q(R-Q) circuit was additionally ’¼ütted to the spectra. These ’¼üts
were much more consistent with the experimental Nyquist plots, which agrees well with the voltammetric
data that indicates that AQS adsorbs to GC. The parameters obtained from the ’¼üts showed that as a
general trend, the double layer capacitance (determined as an e’¼Ćective capacitance) increased, the charge-
transfer resistance decreased, and the heterogeneous standard rate constant increased with increasing GC
oxidation potential. The increase in double layer capacitance, which was very pronounced at 1.9 and
2.1 V oxidation of the GC electrodes, is presumed to result from the formation of micro’¼üssures in the
GC surface, and thereby an increase in the microscopic surface area. As a consequence of this, it became
77
==PAGE:92==
7. CONCLUSION
increasingly di’¼ācult to polish the electrodes su’¼āciently, which was made clear by CV scans in blank
solutions of 1.0 M H2SO4 where the peaks attributed to the AQS redox reaction could be observed. This
complicated the experiments, as it was not possible to return to the same starting point for each new GC
oxidation potential. The oxidation of GC could furthermore have formed new functional groups on the
surface, which could enhance the adsorption of AQS through chemisorption. This was supported by the
appearance of a new peak on the cyclic voltammograms of GC electrodes oxidised at 1.5 V and above,
which however disappeared on subsequent scans. It is therefore unknown whether the enhancement of
the AQS kinetics is purely a surface area e’¼Ćect, a consequence of the interaction of AQS with the surface
functional groups, or a combination of both.
The kinetic parameters of AQS were also studied through hydrodynamic measurements, i.e. by using
a GC rotating disk electrode. Here the standard heterogeneous rate constant and transfer coe’¼ācient were
determined for an unoxidised GC RDE, and one oxidised at 1.7 V. This was accomplished by recorded
CV scans at several rotation rates for both the unoxidised and oxidised GC RDE, and then applying the
Koutecky-Levich equation to determine the two parameters. Standard heterogeneous rate constants of
5.12┬Ę10ŌłÆ4 cm sŌłÆ1 and 8.65┬Ę10ŌłÆ4 cm sŌłÆ1 were found for the unoxidised and oxidised electrode, respectively,
and an ╬▒z value of 0.670 was found for both cases. An ╬▒z value of 0.677 has previously been reported
for AQS [17], which is consistent with the value determined in this work. The k0 values again show
that oxidation of GC has a positive e’¼Ćect on the kinetics of AQS. The rate constant of the unoxidised
electrode determined from the RDE experiments compares relatively well with the values determined
from the initial measurements of AQS. However, when comparing to the values determined throughout
the GC oxidation experiments, the values di’¼Ćer from each other with around two orders of magnitude,
making the absolute values dubious.
Measurements conducted with gold electrodes showed that the charge-transfer process of AQS has a
more ideal response on gold, closer to that predicted by a Randles circuit, than observed on GC. However,
determination of the surface coverage and observations made in blank solutions of 1.0 M H2SO4 suggested
that AQS most likely also adsorbs to gold to some degree.
In an attempt to study the cycling stability of an all-quinone system consisting of AQS as the anolyte
and BQDS as the catholyte, a static H-cell was constructed, using Na’¼üon 212 as the membrane material.
It was shown that it is indeed possible to cycle the two quinones. However, high overpotentials resulting
from insu’¼ācient stirring and a low electrode surface area limited the attainable number of cycles.

8. OUTLOOK
8
Outlook
The work conducted during this project was mostly limited to the investigation of AQS, and even there
much more work could be done. Many aspects of the electrochemistry of AQS still have to be investigated,
including its behaviour when carbon paper or felt (the materials used in RFBs) is used as electrode material
instead of GC, which was used as a model system in this study. Glassy carbon also proved to be di’¼ācult
to work with, due to the persistent porous layer formed during the GC oxidation experiments. If GC is
to be used again in future studies, an improved polishing procedure needs to be established, in order to
fully regenerate the GC surface after oxidation, and thus making sure that all experiments to have the
same point of reference. This could be achieved by using coarser-grained Al2O3 particles, and using a
paste (or slurry) instead of a suspension to polish the electrodes. Higher concentrations of AQS could
also be used to suppress the contributions from surface processes.
Future work should also include a study of the electrochemistry of BQDS, which was only investigated
super’¼ücially here.
Setting up an actual all-quinone redox ’¼éow cell (e.g.
a set-up similar to the all-
vanadium redox ’¼éow cell shown in Figure 65) would also be valuable, both to study the inherent ability of
the investigated quinones to act as energy carriers, and to investigate how di’¼Ćerent degrees of oxidation
of the carbon electrodes a’¼Ćects the overall performance of the cell.
Figure 65: Photograph of an all-vanadium redox ’¼éow cell at Aarhus University.
In a broader perspective, many other ’¼üelds must be investigated further for redox ’¼éow batteries to
become a commercial success as an EES technology. The used carbon electrodes are already relatively
inexpensive, but they must also be produced to be e’¼ācient and durable if they are to last the projected
service lifetime of up to 20 years [50, 51]. In the same way, the membrane material must be durable and
as cheap as possible, and additionally highly permselective [8]. In the present work, only one combination
of quinones has been studied, but with the many structural alterations that are possible with the quinone
family, the number of combinations are essentially endless. It is however required that the redox couples
are chemically stable in the relevant potential ranges, have large potential di’¼Ćerences, have high solubilities
of both oxidised and reduced species, and possess fast redox kinetics [8]. Cell design must be optimised to
minimise transport losses, pumping costs, and shunt currents [8]. Last but not least, the batteries must
79

be scaled up and integrated into the energy grid [8], which will be of increasing importance as the amount
of intermittent renewable energy increases in the energy mix. Despite the many challenges that still lie
ahead, redox ’¼éow batteries seem to have a promising future in a world that depends more and more on
the accessibility of electrical energy.
