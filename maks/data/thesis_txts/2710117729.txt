Abstract
Nonlinearity in mechanical systems is a widely reasearched topic, but nonlinearity de-
tection often relies on techniques where the excitation and response is known. Random
decrement signatures or free decays of a structure have been suggested to be able to detect
nonlinearities using response only measurements. This could make nonlinearity detection
possible where other known methods fall short.
The aim of the thesis is to investigate the random decrement technique for nonlinearity
detection. The method is implemented and used to investigate single degree-of-freedom
systems with different nonlinearities. Additional parameters are presented to increase the
reliability of the analysis.
Using a new convergence measure, the method is found to distinguish between linear
and nonlinear systems within some threshold. Increasing nonlinearity is observed in the
outcome of the analysis for damping and cubic nonlinearities.
Investigating an experimental setup with known cubic nonlinearity is found to indicate
both cubic and bilinear behavior through random decrement analysis.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Linear models are widely used by engineers to describe vibrations in mechanical systems
or structures.
The behavior of these systems can be assumed linear within a limited
dynamic range when nonlinear effects and forces are negligible. The assumption of non-
linear behavior can be tested when both the excitation and response are known, but for
many structures subjected to ambient excitation only the response of the structure can be
measured. When the excitation forces are unknown there is no general way to determine
whether the system exhibits approximately linear behavior.
Operational modal analysis (OMA) aims at estimating modal properties of structures
from their operating response, where the excitation force is not measured. One fundamen-
tal assumption is that the system behaves approximately linear, but this assumption is
diﬀicult to validate as there is no general method to address this issue or to estimate the
degree of nonlinearity of the system in an output only setting.
In OMA both time- and frequency methods are widely used, but recently a method
based on the random decrement technique has been able to distinguish between linear and
nonlinear behavior of a single degree-of-freedom (SDOF) mechanical system [1]. Knowl-
edge of the systems behavior is important when performing OMA as the methods should
be applied with caution, or methods for nonlinear systems should be applied instead.
Some of the most well known methods for nonlinearity detection utilize homogenity
check, the coherence function and Hilbert transforms, [2, 3]. It is necessary to measure
the excitation to use these analysis methods and measuring the excitation is not always
possible for dynamical structures under ambient excitation.
Recently Vesterholm et al. [1] have suggested that the random decrement (RD) tech-
nique can be used to detect nonlinearities using response signals. The RD technique was
first introduced in the 1960’s by Cole [4, 5, 6] as a way to analyze the vibration of struc-
tures subjected to ambient excitation. The auto correlation was used for modal parameter
estimation, but later the RD technique was introduced as a way to transform the measured
time series into a free decay. It was shown in [4], that the RD technique was promising
for detecting systems with amplitude dependent nonlinearities.
Ibrahim [7, 8] introduced the auto and cross RD functions which made it possible
to estimate mode shapes using the technique. Later Vandiver et al. [9] gave a thorough
mathematical basis and intuitive background for the RD technique used on a single-degree-
of-freedom system excited by Gaussian noise.
The RD technique has been used by Haddara [10] to estimate nonlinear damping
parameters for rolling motion, but later it has also been used by Shiryayev and Slater [11]
to conclude that the RD signatures of bilinear, trilinear and cubic nonlinearities systems
are different from the free responses. In [1], it was shown that the RD technique could
detect nonlinearities using trigger points in a measured signal and this was tested for a
stick slip friction nonlinearity on a single-degree-of-freedom system.
The aim of this work in this thesis is to investigate the RD technique for nonlinearity
1
==PAGE:10==
detection using measurements from both experiments and numerical simulations. Even
though the RD technique was invented in the 1960’s, the method has only recently been
used for nonlinearity detection [1] and only few nonlinear systems have been tested. Some
aspects of the RD technique for nonlinearity detection e.g. measurement length, number of
averages and RD signature length are not well defined despite affecting the outcome of the
analysis. These aspects will be investigated using numerical simulations and standardized
methods for estimating the length of RD signatures across multiple types of mechanical
systems will be suggested. Using these methods, several simulated responses of linear and
nonlinear systems will be analyzed with the RD technique to evaluate sensitivity of the
technique to different nonlinearities.
The analysis of simulated responses from single degree-of-freedom systems using the
RD technique shows sensitivity towards all tested nonlinearities. Measurements from an
experimental test setup with a known cubic nonlinearity [12] indicate nonlinearity as the
magnitude of the response increases. Indications of both a cubic and bilinear nonlinearity
are presented and compared to the numerical simulations. As a impact is introduced in
the approximately linear range of the test setup, the RD technique shows indications of
nonlinearity at the affected response levels.
Chapter 2 of the thesis provides a background to the RD technique, where important
terms are introduced with examples. Chapter 3 introduces the use of the RD technique in
relation to nonlinearity detection. The end of the chapter describes a method to remove
the noise tail of the RD signature to make analysis of different mechanical systems more
comparable. Chapter 4 describes the implementation and simulation of SDOF systems
with different nonlinearities. Chapter 5 describes the experimental test setup and pro-
cedures. The results are presented in chapter 6, where both the convergence and effect
of the noise tail cutoff is presented. The results of both the numerical simulations and
experimental measurements are presented case by case and compared.

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter will present the RD technique from the intuitive theory to trigger points and
the relationship between RD signatures and correlation functions.
2.1
The Initial Development of the Random Decre-
ment Technique
The RD technique was developed in the 1960’s by H. A. Cole [4, 5, 6] when he was
working with the analysis of structural responses to random vibrations. The responses
were diﬀicult to interpret directly, and it was hard to detect any significant changes in
the dynamic responses of the structures. Some information could be found from extensive
analysis of the time histories, but this process could be very time consuming, and it would
be done after an experiment had taken place. Spectral densities were considered with
damping ratios estimated from the bandwidth of the half-power point, but this technique
had large variances on the estimates. No available method could use spectral densities
to detect non-linearities from the response to ambient loads.
The RD technique was
developed as a way to transform a random time series into a free decay (or decrement)
that would contain information about the structure without introducing uncertainties from
transformation into the frequency domain or effects of the random loads.
The random response of a structure at some point in time t0 + t due to a random
load is the result of the initial displacement and velocity at time t0 as well as the random
load applied in time interval with length t. The concept of the RD technique is to pick
a time segment every time the response is at a certain displacement level, x(t) = a, and
average these segments. When the number of segments increase the random part due to
the random load will become negligible. The velocity at the displacement level a will vary
randomly, but there will be an equal amount of positive and negative slopes, so the average
velocity will be zero. Only the effect of the initial displacement is left in the signal which
is the free decay of the structure.

2.4
Defining Trigger Conditions
The trigger condition TX(t) is used to identify the times ti, where the stochastic process
X(t) satisfies the TC. These points in time are called trigger points (TP). A segment of
the observed time history of the stochastic process X(t) is then selected for each time the
trigger condition is satisfied, and these segments are averaged to form the RD signature.
The trigger conditions are usually defined as conditions for amplitude and slope, which
is equivalent to initial conditions for position and velocity of a mechanical system. Each
segment used to calculate the RD signature will then share the same initial conditions.

the correlation function, for a linear system excited by a stationary, zero mean, Gaussian
distributed input. This relationship between RD signatures and correlation functions are
presented along with the different trigger conditions presented in this section.

could be identified as trigger points because they are all within [a1, a2].

Chapter 3
Nonlinearity Detection using Random
Decrements
This chapter will present the analysis methods used to detect nonlinearity in single degree-
of-freedom (SDOF) systems using the RD technique. Newly developed methods in [1] for
detecting frequency and damping related nonlinearities are presented along with examples
to their applicability for other types of nonlinearities at the end of this chapter.
3.1
Properties of the Linear System
Additivity and homogeneity are the two properties that defines linearity
Additivity:
g(y1 + y2) = g(y1) + g(y2)
(3.1)
Homogeneity:
g(c · y) = c · g(y),
∀c ∈C
(3.2)
These two properties are together known as the superposition principle.
The equation of motion for the harmonic oscillator in Eq.
(2.2) is an example of
a linear ordinary differential equation, which satisfies the principle of superposition. For
linear mechanical systems this means that if the response x1(t) to an input f1(t) with initial
conditions T1 = {x1(0), ˙x1(0)} is x1(t), and if the response x2(t) to an input f2(t) with
initial conditions T2 = {x2(0), ˙x2(0)} is x2(t) then the response to c1f1(t) + c2f2(t) with
initial conditions T1+2 = {c1x1(0) + c2x2(0), c1 ˙x1(0) + c2 ˙x2(0)} must be c1x1(t) + c2x2(t)
for c1, c2 ∈C.
This principle of superposition does not apply to nonlinear systems. Providing experi-
mental proof of linearity of a system by simply testing all combinations of constants c1, c2
and input f1(t) and f2(t) is impossible. Providing a single set of constants and inputs
that violate the principle of superposition is evidence that the system is nonlinear.
The solution to the harmonic oscillator Eq. (2.2) with f(t) = 0 (homogeneous solution)
can be written as [13]
x(t) = Ce−ξt cos (ωdt + ϕ)
(3.3)
where c and ϕ are constants determined from the initial conditions. The solution to the
harmonic oscillator has an oscillating cosine with an exponentially decaying envelope and
both frequency and damping are constant regardless of the initial conditions of the system.
For the unforced SDOF oscillator with nonlinear damping and stiffness terms fnl( ˙x, x),
the general equation of motion is
¨x + 2ξωn ˙x + ω2
nx + fnl( ˙x, x) = 0
(3.4)
and no general solution exists for all nonlinear equations of the same type as the one
above. Because of the nonlinearities, the shape of the free decay for a nonlinear system
13
==PAGE:22==
will change depending on the initial conditions [19]. This property can be exploited to
detect nonlinearities in mechanical systems.
For a linear system excited by a white noise, the RD signature will be proportional
to the free decay of the system. The proportionality between the free decay and the RD
signature for linear systems as well as the influence of initial conditions on the free decay
of nonlinear systems makes the RD signatures a possible way to detect nonlinearities.
Triggering points can identify the points in time where the signal crosses a certain level
or are within a level range for both amplitude and slope of the signal. The RD signatures
for each triggering condition can then be compared for differences that are a result of
nonlinearities.
3.2
Nonlinear System Identification with the RD
Technique
Several different methods exists for Nonlinear System Identification (NSI) when both the
input and response of the system is known [2]. It was suggested by Vesterholm et al. [1],
that the RD method can be used for nonlinearity detection, in an output only setting.
Detection of nonlinearities is the first part of NSI process that has the following 3 steps
[2]
1. Detection of nonlinearity
2. Characterization of nonlinearity
• Determine the location of the nonlinearity
• Determine the type of nonlinearity
• Determine the functional form of the nonlinearity
3. Estimating parameters of the nonlinearity
The NSI process was an inspiration in the development of [18], but there were some
limitations to the initial study as only cubic, Coulomb and quadratic nonlinearities were
investigated. The object of the following sections is to present the theory of the method
for both stiffness and damping related nonlinearities as well as presenting the possibilities
and limitations of using the same method for other types of nonlinearities e.g. piecewise
linear stiffness or impact nonlinearities.
3.3
A relevant Trigger Condition for Nonlinearity
Detection
It is mentioned in 3.1 that a linear system satisfies the principle of superposition with
appropriate initial conditions. Providing just one example that a system does not adhere
to the principle of superposition provides proof of nonlinearity. The RD technique for
nonlinearity detection compares the RD signatures from different initial conditions. If the
RD signatures are equal for all initial conditions then the system is assumed to be linear,
but if the RD signatures are different then it is an indication of nonlinear behavior.
While several trigger conditions were presented in Chapter 2, they were not well suited
to investigate and differentiate RD signatures in both amplitude and slope. A TC capable
of investigating several different initial conditions is required and this TC is designed to
have the properties stated in [18]

• Investigate a set of initial conditions in the form of amplitudes and slopes
• Be adjustable to achieve a high number of triggering points for each RD signature
• All members in the resulting set of RD signatures must be equal in case of a linear
system
The trigger condition that fulfills the requirements is presented in [1] and can be written

A comparison between the LC, PP, LE and ENV triggering condition was made in [20],
where it was shown that parameter estimation using both LE and ENV trigger condition
were one-to-one between vibration response and modal parameter while that was not the
case for the LC and PP triggering condition. The same investigation showed that the
resulting RD signatures for similar amplitudes for PP and LC triggering conditions were
not independent of forcing as originally suggested by Cole [4]. Other properties related to
the distribution of the time segments used to calculate the RD signature, showed that the
LE triggering condition is able to produce more stable Gaussian signal segments compared
to the PP triggering condition within the early lags (within one period of the natural
frequency).

different slopes, as the slope of the LE TC is defined to be 0. The ENV triggering condition

One of the general assumptions for the RD technique is that the input has zero mean, and
then the response will also have zero mean if the system is linear. For some asymmetric
nonlinear systems similar to the single stop, the mean of the response could be non zero

even though the mean of the excitation is 0 (not taking the forces of the single stop into

Measurements of linear systems where the mean of the response has some offset from
0 can be treated by simply subtracting the mean of the measurement. Nonlinear systems
with nonzero mean are treated the same way, since it can be diﬀicult to distinguish between
linear and nonlinear systems before performing any analysis.
This does have one implication for asymmetric nonlinear systems, when analysing re-
sponse signals. The amplitude levels in table 3.1 used to identify TPs, will be displaced by

the outcome of the analysis would be affected by this effect.

Defining Condition Crossings
Condition crossings (CC) is a new term which describes the number of times non-adjacent
trigger point has been identified within a trigger condition. An example could be a signal
that crosses the amplitude interval defined by (2.25). Here two neighbouring points could
be identifies as trigger points as they are both within the interval, but this only constitutes
one condition crossing. This is important as the number of CCs are less affected by the
sampling frequency compared to TPs.

4.4
Simulation of Discontinuous ODEs
A few of the nonlinear systems presented in table 4.1 required special consideration when
used together with Matlab’s ODE solver.
The impact nonlinearity (stop or double stop) was the first case that was treated and
it could be considered a special case of the bi- or trilinear system where the stiffness at
the boundaries are rapidly changing over a small distance and very large compared to
the stiffness of the underlying linear system. An event function was used in combination
with Matlabs ODE solvers to detect impact. An event is then defined by a value and
a direction, equivalent to the response value and direction of motion. When the system
moves towards the stop then an impact is detected, when the system passes the stop. The
solution is refined and velocity of the system is then multiplied by −1 and the iteration
continues.
The collision is modelled as an elastic collision as the kinetic energy is the same before
and after the collision. Furthermore the collision is instantaneous and both these proper-
ties are idealizations of most collisions that occur over finite time with deformations and
energy dissipation. A coeﬀicient of restitution was not included as this could affect the
identification of TPs. An example of simulations using this method is seen in figure 3.7.
This method was also used for the bilinear and trilinear systems along with ODE45.

6.1
Stable Measure of Convergence for RD Sig-
natures
The length of the RD signatures are an important factor that influences the nonlinearity
indicator NORI along with the length of the recorded measurement. However no standard
method to determine the lengths of the RD signatures in relation to nonlinearity detection
has been proposed. This poses a problem for the outcome of any analysis made with this
technique, as the inclusion of the noise tail in the RD signatures could result in a linear
system interpreted as nonlinear or vice versa.
The number of TPs has previously been used to confirm convergence of the RD sig-
natures [1], but the conclusion of convergence was made with the LC triggering condition
for damping ratio estimates [22]. The PP triggering condition is fundamentally different
from the LC triggering condition, because the LC TC only has one TP (or two for the
symmetric window) for each level crossing. The PP trigger condition can have several
neighbouring points identified as TPs, which is likely when using wide amplitude and
slope intervals and high sampling frequency.
The problem arises when comparing the two TCs across several different sampling
frequencies. Increasing the sampling frequency for the LC trigger condition, the number
of TPs does not change. Increasing the sampling frequency for the PP trigger condition,
also increases the number of identified TPs for any fixed signal length. Simply stating the
number of TPs as a measure of convergence for the RD signatures could be a meaningless
number, when using the PP trigger condition.
This is why the condition crossing (CC) was previously introduced. This parameter is
not constant when changing the sampling frequency, but it converges towards a maximum
number given a fixed signal length.

illustrated in Fig. 6.1. The PCA is performed on the simulated response of a linear system

6.2
Noise Tail Cutoff and Convergence of NORI
The aim of this analysis is to find parameters that could be used with the RD technique
to reliably conclude whether a system is linear or nonlinear. Responses of linear systems
are used to study the effect of noise tail cutoffs and convergence. A combination of noise
tail cutoff and convergence parameters are sought where linear systems are interpreted as
linear.
The noise tail of RD signatures affects the outcome of the analysis by introducing
noise. This noise increases the NORI parameter whether the system is linear or not, so
removing this noise tail before the RD analysis is performed increases the reliability of
the results, especially when a fixed NORIthresh is considered. The noise tail of correlation
function estimates was investigated in [31]. The noise tail was estimated to start when the
amplitude had decayed to 5 −20% of the amplitude at zero lag given a fixed noise ratio.
A study of the amplitude cutoff at a fraction of the initial amplitude R can be seen in Fig.
6.2 for different linear systems with 9000 CCs and all TCs from Table 3.1 and 3.2.
In this study, the Hilbert transform was used to estimate the envelope amplitude of the
RD signatures. The figure shows that an increase in RD signature length also increases
NORI. This was expected, but the rate of increase is different for R = 0.3 and R = 0.05.
At R = 0.05 the more of the noise tail is included in the analysis of the system, and as
R is decreased all linear systems start to indicate nonlinear behavior when NORI> 6.
Additionally, the effect of the noise tail could be seen as it was increasingly diﬀicult to
find any point where the amplitude envelope was below R = 0.05. For these reasons the
amplitude cutoff was set to R = 0.1 to avoid including the noise tail in the analysis.

6.4.2
Bilinear Nonlinearity
The bilinear system is characterized by a discontinuous change in stiffness.
The RD
technique is applied to a bilinear system with increasing α in Figures 6.10 to 6.12, where
the change in stiffness is at the origin. For the case with the lowest change in stiffness
α = 1.21, the system shows indications of nonlinearity as NORI = 6.8. The SIC parameter
is similar to the linear case, but the RAIC parameter shows clear signs of nonlinearity.
While this parameter was usually designed to detect damping related nonliearities, it is
the parameter that most clearly deviates from the linear case. The pattern in the RAIC
parameter comes from the design of the A matrix in (3.11), where Di,j
+ and Di,j
+ are
neighbouring column vectors.
As the system is asymmetric the unit normalization at
τ = 0 scales the up and down RD signatures in different ways. This effect can be seen in
the RAIC pattern where there is an observable color change between neighbouring points
on the axis.
As α increases the pattern in the RAIC parameter becomes more pronounced as
RAICmin decreases. A similar pattern becomes visible in the SIC parameter. One in-
teresting property is the unit normalized RD signatures where the minimum value is less
than −1. This has not occurred for any other system encountered so far, and it is also
one thing that would be impossible to see for a linear system.
For this case, RAIC is the parameter that most clearly indicates nonlinear behavior
even though the nonlinearity is related to the stiffness of the system.

6.5
Dampening Related Nonlinearities
The simulated response of models for nonlinear damping forces found in Table 4.1 are an-
alyzed using the RD technique. The relevant plots are showed for both Coulomb damping
as well as quadratic damping. Figures for structural and displacement squared damping
are shown in the appendix B and A as they show behavior similar to quadratic damping.
The effects of quadratic damping are seen in Figures 6.13 to 6.15 for increasing strength
of the nonlinearity. The RD signatures in the figures show increasing differences as χ
increases. The number of periods are also greatly reduced as the RD signature reduces to
R = 0.1 faster due with increased damping. The RAIC parameter increases from top right
to bottom left, but this effect decreases as χ increases. The RAIC parameter becomes less
responsive with increased strength of the damping nonlinearity, while NORI still increases.
As the damping is increased an asymmetry becomes clear in the RD signatures. A bias of
the RD signature was also mentioned by Asmussen [15], that is caused by an asymmetry
in the up- and down crossings of the TC. This asymmetry causes changes in the SIC
parameter even though there are no stiffness related nonlinearities in the equation of
motion.

6.7
Experimental results
This section contains the results of the experiment made using setup described in 5.1
and 5.3. Before each recording the natural frequency was adjusted to ≈9.6 Hz. Only
measurements without drifts are included in the results. The velocity and position signals
are obtained by integrating the acceleration measurements in the frequency domain and
setting the energy of all frequencies below 1 Hz to 0, to avoid any low frequency drift.
The experimental setup behaves approximately as a SDOF at low frequencies where
a cubic nonlinearity was known to be more prominent as the magnitude of the response
increases [12]. This behavior was tested using 4 different excitation levels and a description
of the experimental test cases are in Table 6.2 and the result of the analysis is given in
Fig 6.22 and 6.23.
Case 1 has the lowest response standard deviation (or RMS) and is just below the in-
dicator threshold as NORI = 6.4 and the RD signatures do not show any visual deviations
from linear behavior. The other indicator parameters SICmin = 0.996 and RAICmin = 0.93
show indications of nonlinear behavior, as the RAIC value deviates from 1. However at
this level of response the SIC parameter does not indicate a frequency related nonlinearity,
instead the RAIC parameter indicated nonlinearities in the system.
This trend is also seen in case 2 and 3 with NORI = 9.8 and NORI = 14.9 respectively
indicate nonlinear behavior. For case 2 and 3, SICmin = 0.97 and SICmin = 0.88 while
RAICmin = 0.88 and RAICmin = 0.78 show increasing nonlinearity for all 3 parameters.
The RD signatures show a small frequency difference between TCs going from blue to red.
Case 4 shows the system with the largest excitation and response measurement. For
this case NORI =, SICmin = 0.81 and RAICmin = 0.74. This case is similar to case 3,
but all patterns in the parameter plots are more prominent as the deviations from 1 have
increased, but the RD signatures are comparable to case 3.
The increased response showed increasing nonlinearity indicators for all cases. However
a comparison between the patterns in cases 1-4 and the cases using simulated responses of
different nonlinearities indicates that two different nonlinearities are present. One of these
nonlinearities is the cubic nonlinearity, where phase differences between the RD signatures
can be observed. The RAIC parameter plot show a pattern that has been observed in the
analysis of the bilinear system in Figures 6.10 to 6.12. Among the cases simulated, this was
the only system with RAIC patterns similar to the one observed in experimental cases 1-4.
The pattern in the SIC plot has similarities between both the bilinear and cubic system.
From the patterns in the parameter plots as well as the RD signatures, the results indicate
that the system show both cubic and bilinear behavior.

RD Analysis of Impact Nonlinearity
A impact nonlinearity is added to the system to introduce a strong nonlinearity. The
measurements are performed in the linear response region for each measurement, so any
deviations from linearity is due to the impact nonlinearity. Before the impact nonlinearity
is introduced, a measurement of 10 minutes in length is performed to measure the standard
deviation of the underlying linear system.
Cases 5-8 are impact related and, where case 5 has no introduced nonlinearity. These
cases can be seen in Fig. 6.22 and 6.25. The linear system in case 5 has NORI = 5.0
which is below the threshold, SICmin = 0.998 and RAICmin = 0.97 so it is interpreted as
linear. Cases 6-8 have introduced impact nonlinearities and the degree of nonlinearity is
measured by the standard deviation of the reference measurement at the location of the
impact nonlinearity and the distance between the impact nonlinearity and the beam, see
Table 6.3.
The impact nonlinearity is located outside the amplitude intervals from Table 3.1 for
case 6. However the RD signatures are different from the linear case for negative lags.
This is reflected in the parameters NORI = 9.5, SICmin = 0.97 and RAICmin = 0.86
which indicate a nonlinear system. The SIC parameter shows two distinct lines at the
amplitude indices 5 and 6 with the largest slopes. As the nonlinearity is located within
the 6’th amplitude interval, a deviation from normality is expected.
The impact nonlinearity is located within the amplitude band with index 4. For case
8 NORI = 18.4, SICmin = 0.74 and RAICmin = 0.70 which indicate a nonlinear system.
The patterns in the SIC parameter show clear deviations from 1 within amplitude bands
with indices 5 and 6 specifically, but other indices with large slopes j = 4 also deviate from
1. One thing to note here is that the impact nonlinearity is located within the amplitude
band i = 4, and the system has the highest degree of asymmetry of cases 5-8. Because
the mean was originally subtracted from the signal, the equilibrium point of the system
and the signal mean are different. Comparing the amplitude indices with the position
of the impact nonlinearity is increasingly biased as the asymmetry is increased. As the
difference between the signal mean and equilibrium of the system increases, so does the
displacement of the amplitude intervals i.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The aim of this project presented in this thesis was to determine if random decrement
(RD) signatures could be used for nonlinear system detection.
The nonlinearity optimized random decrement method has been implemented accord-
ing to [1], while some additions to the method have been proposed and investigated to
increase the comparability between analysis of different single degree-of-freedom systems.
These additions involve the measure of convergence and methods to estimate a standard
length of the RD signature. Using these added methods it is possible for the RD tech-
nique to detect nonlinear behavior and distinguish between linear and nonlinear behavior
with the original threshold set by Vesterholm et al. [1]. However investigations show that
another threshold could be appropriate for different signal lengths.
Using the methods tested on linear systems, different nonlinear systems are analyzed.
The systems with a damping or cubic nonlinearity showed significant changes in the indica-
tor parameters for nonlinearity. Increasing the strength of the nonlinearity also increased
the indications of nonlinearity. One indicator parameter designed to detect damping non-
linearities showed significant sensitivity to systems with discontinuous changes in stiffness.
Data obtained from an experimental setup with known cubic nonlinearity showed no
indications of nonlinearity at low excitation. As the excitation was increased so did the
nonlinearity indicators. The results from the experimental data show indications of both
a bilinear and cubic nonlinearity.
The applicability of this method is dependent on the system under investigation. Some
indicator parameters appear more linear as the strength of damping nonlinearities is in-
creased. The suggested methods to estimate convergence and a standard length of RD
signatures are less consistent, when a strong asymmetry is present. Without similar or
more reliable methods, the outcome of analysis using the RD technique is more diﬀicult
to interpret directly without taking several factors into consideration.
Measurement time is another thing that could affect the utility of the method. Roughly
110.000 periods are needed to conclude that a system is linear with the threshold given by
[1] which corresponds to more than 30 hours of measuring time with a period of 1 s. During
this time the characteristics of the applied force could change e.g. wind or excitation due
to traﬀic. This could mean that the deflection of the system under test changes similarly
and distinguishing between these periods of the response could be diﬀicult.
Using the RD method, all investigated types of nonlinear behavior was detected as the
effect of the nonlinearity was increased. This does show potential for the method to be
used as a indicator of nonlinearity of different kinds.
The future of the RD technique for nonlinearity detection could continue along the path
of system identification, where not only frequency and damping related nonlinearities can
be detected, but other types as well. This could include positions of the nonlinearities in
59

continuous or multi degree-of-freedom structures. Another possibility is the use of rapid
RD measurements where adaptive measures could be used to determine the required signal
length for conclusions of linearity.
