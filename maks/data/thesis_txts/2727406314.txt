Abstract 
This current study investigates the potential of an Anion Exchange Membrane water 
electrolysis cell to be used as a means to capture CO2 and impede global warming. 
Firstly, the currently applicable technologies are presented and their pros and cons are 
analyzed. In the second part, the experimental methodology that was followed is 
described in detail. As a part of our investigation, the in-plane and through-plane 
direction conductivities of the fumatech FAA-3-50 membrane have been examined. In 
the in-plane measurement, different ionic forms of the membrane were tested and the 
effect of temperature on the conductivity has been determined. Additionally, a current 
was applied to analyze the time required for a bicarbonate form membrane to be fully 
converted to a hydroxide form. In though-plane conductivity measurements, a cell with 
an FAA-3-50 HCO3- was assembled. The cell was tested and its performance was 
monitored under various cathode and anode feeds. From the first test’s acquired data, 
the I-V curves were constructed and compared with existing bibliographical research. 
Then, a steady current experiment was conducted also under different feeds and the 
voltage and CO2 crossover as a function of time were assessed. In the last experiment, 
the effect of current densities on the CO2 permeate concentration was investigated. Last 
but not least, some conclusions were made about the potential of the presented method, 
including some possible improvements and aspects that need to be further explored.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
1.1 Carbon dioxide as a greenhouse gas 
Carbon dioxide (CO2) has always been present in the Earth’s atmosphere since it is a 
part of the carbon cycle, one of the three ecosystem cycles. However, CO2 
concentration in the atmosphere varies with time and has steeply risen, reaching 415 
ppm in 2019 (Rahimi et al., 2020). This is a main result of the industrial revolution as 
it can be seen in Figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1: CO2 concentration from 1751 to 2022 (NOAA Climate.gov, May 2023) 
 
In the above Figure 1, the blue line represents the total CO2 concentration in the 
atmosphere, while the grey is the anthropogenic CO2. The main (anthropogenic) source 
of CO2 leading to this significant increase is the extraction and burning of fossil fuels 
(NASA, 2023). A consequence of this upward trend in CO2 concentration is the 
temperature rise on the Earth’s surface, since CO2 works as a radiation trap. This 
happens primarily because CO2 traps re-emitted solar radiation from the surface of the 
Earth, a phenomenon known as the Greenhouse effect. Additionally, Cheng et al. 
(2022) have created maps that include the average regional CO2 concentrations in two 
different time periods (Figure 2), one spanning from the end of the 19th century until 
late 20th century and the other being a ten-year period in the 21st century (2004-2013). 
From the maps it is evident that the parts of the world that exhibit industrial activity 
have accumulated higher concentrations of CO2 emissions over time. 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
 
 
Figure 2: Global average CO2 concentrations in two different time periods (Cheng et al., 2022) 
This greenhouse effect has led the United Nations Intergovernmental Panel for Climate 
Change (IPCC) to set a goal for greenhouse gas emissions decrease by 2060 in order to 
limit the observed trend. The developed scenarios target a more likely temperature 
increase containment to 2 °C and a more optimistic 1.5 °C containment as presented in 
Figure 3 below.

Convention on Climate Change, 2022) 
Based on the need for CO2 emissions mitigation, the implementation of Carbon Capture 
and Storage techniques is considered imminent.

1.2 Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) techniques 
Carbon Capture and Storage methods can be divided into three different categories: 
• Pre-combustion 
• Post-combustion 
• Oxy-combustion 
The pre-combustion techniques take place prior to the combustion process. Post-
combustion techniques are implemented after the combustion process (Madejski et al., 
2022). The third method, oxy-combustion, occurs during combustion under an oxygen 
rich atmosphere (Vaz Jr. et al., 2022). An oxygen-enriched environment is achieved by 
separating the oxygen contained in air from nitrogen before combustion (Madejski et 
al., 2022). 
Pre-combustion is a fuel gasification leading to the formation of a synthesis gas, with 
main components hydrogen and carbon monoxide (Vaz Jr. et al., 2022). At the first 
step, coal reacts with oxygen to produce a gas (Mitra et al., 2022). Then, this gas reacts 
with water to form hydrogen and carbon monoxide (Eq. 1). This syngas is then 
introduced to steam and via the water gas shift reaction (Eq. 2) carbon dioxide and H2 
are produced (Mitra et al., 2022). 
𝐶+ 𝐻2𝑂→𝐶𝑂+ 𝐻2  (𝐸𝑞. 1) 
𝐶𝑂+ 𝐻2𝑂→𝐶𝑂2 + 𝐻2 (𝐸𝑞. 2) 
A schematic of a precombustion technology is depicted in Figure 4 below. 
 
Figure 4: Schematic of precombustion technology (Mitra et al., 2022)

On the contrary, post-combustion is an ensemble of techniques for capturing CO2 from 
a flue gas. Some examples of post-combustion processes are chemical adsorption and 
physical absorption (Madejski et al., 2022). In the adsorption process, CO2 contacts 
porous particles that can adsorb the gas due to its surface affinity (Vaz Jr. et al, 2022). 
A schematic of post combustion technologies can be seen in Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5: Post combustion schematic (Mitra et al., 2022) 
 
In the case of absorption CO2 is absorbed and transported from one phase of the mixture 
to another. More specifically, according to Vaz Jr. et al. (2022) “carbon dioxide is 
solubilized by passing to the liquid phase of the mixture”. 
However, adsorption processes require high energy input that limits their 
implementation. On the other hand, absorption methods showcase higher technological 
maturity and economic viability, with their main drawback being high-energy 
expenditure (Vaz et al., 2022). Post combustion technologies are the most promising 
technologies for CO2 separation and capture, since they can be coupled with coal fired 
plants, which are responsible for a significant amount of greenhouse emissions from 
the power sector (Mitra et al., 2022). 
 
1.2.1 Amine Scrubbing 
One of the most utilized carbon dioxide capture technologies from conventional coal-
fired plants is amine scrubbing. According to Rochelle (2009) amine scrubbing will 
continue to be the main technique utilized for CO2 removal from coal-fired plants until 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
 
2030. This is mainly due to the technology of absorption with aqueous amine being 
widely implemented and well-described (Rochelle, 2009).  
In this process, carbon dioxide is absorbed by an aqueous amine solution of low 
volatility at ambient temperatures. After the absorption, the CO2-rich amine solution is 
treated with water vapor at temperatures ranging from 100° to 120 °C in order to 
regenerate the amine. Following amine regeneration, the water vapor is condensed 
leaving pure CO2 for storage (Rochelle, 2009). 
The most popular amines in use for carbon capture are monoethanolamine (MEA), 
methyldiethanolamine (MDEA), diethanolamine (DEA), aminomethyl propanol 
(AMP) and piperazine (PZ). MEA and DEA exhibit high absorption rates but are 
limited by high energy consumption requirements for regeneration (Wu et al., 2020). 
Additionally, monoethanolamine and diethanoamine are highly prone to oxidative 
degradation (Wu et al., 2020). MDEA is an amine that has a high absorption capacity 
combined with low energy requirements for recycling but is hindered by the slow rate 
of absorption (Wu et al., 2020). Furthermore, AMP also possesses high absorption 
capacities and the same trend applies to the absorption rate. However, the technology 
is under development and the cost of utilization is high (Wu et al., 2020). Lastly, 
although piperazine displays high absorption rates like AMP, it is an amine solution 
that is restricted by its high cost, toxicity and corrosive nature (Wu et al., 2020). 
One of the most well-known mechanisms for amine CO2 capture is that of MEA. 
Various mechanisms have been proposed for the reaction between MEA and CO2 (Lv 
et al., 2015). The first is the zwitterion mechanism, which is a two-step mechanism, 
suggesting that MEA reacts with carbon dioxide to form a zwitterion (Eq. 3), that is 
then neutralized by a base to form a carbamate as shown in Equation 4 (Lv et al., 2015).

easily undergo oxidative degradation. Chi and Rochelle (2002) have proposed a

1.2.2 Chemical capture (KOH/K2CO3 - CaO) 
Another utilized carbon capture approach based on adsorption and absorption is related 
to the use of chemicals. Three of these technologies include CO2 absorption in an 
aqueous solution of potassium hydroxide (KOH) or potassium carbonate (K2CO3) and 
the adsorption of CO2 on calcium oxide (CaO). Chemical absorption methods are 
preferred since they are low cost, highly efficient and technologically mature (Borhani 
et al., 2015). 
The potassium hydroxide method is based on the full dissociation of KOH in water 
providing hydroxide ions that can react with CO2 towards the formation of bicarbonate 
(HCO3-) and carbonate ions (CO32-).

It has been demonstrated that increasing the temperature decreases CO2 loading due to 
the decrease in CO2 solubility at higher temperatures (Rastegar & Ghaemi, 2022). A 
similar downward trend in CO2 loading was observed for the increase in KOH 
concentration. This is a result of the increase of absorbent molecules compared to the 
significantly lower increase in CO2 molecules absorbed (Rastegar & Ghaemi, 2022). A 
completely reverse trend to the ones described for the temperature and KOH 
concentration was observed for the pressure. Researchers have found that increasing 
the pressure leads to an increase in CO2 loading. This was mainly attributed to 
increasing CO2 partial pressure that promotes the mass transfer driving force (Rastegar 
& Ghaemi, 2022).

However, this method has a significant drawback connected to the utilized solution. 
Potassium hydroxide is a strongly alkaline solution and as a result it is highly corrosive. 
This means that a possible application of this post-combustion method will increase the 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
 
operating costs due to frequent metal-part-equipment maintenance or even replacement. 
Furthermore, the fact that temperature does not enhance CO2 solubility (and as a result 
absorption) makes it a less efficient solution, since the flue gases will have to be cooled 
down before treatment in case of high temperature processes. Therefore, an alternative 
solution has to be found to cope with the corrosive nature of KOH solutions. 
A viable solution to limit corrosion problems is to replace KOH with potassium 
carbonate. This results in a new mixture that has several advantages such as high CO2 
solubility and easy sorbent regeneration (Borhani et al., 2015). Moreover, K2CO3 has 
low toxicity and does not degrade as easily as amines (Borhani et al., 2015). Even 
though K2CO3 has certain advantages compared to the previous sorbents it also has 
some disadvantages. The most important one is the slow reaction rate between CO2 and 
the K2CO3 solution. As a result, CO2 dissolution is limited (Borhani et al., 2015). 
Additionally, carbon steel corrosion issues may arise using potassium bicarbonate 
solutions (Borhani et al., 2015). Lastly, absorbent precipitation complications may 
result in solvent losses (Borhani et al., 2015). 
In this method a carbon dioxide rich stream is introduced to a potassium bicarbonate 
solution in an absorption column. The two fluids are added in the column in a 
countercurrent flow way. The gas enters the column at the bottom, while the solution 
at the top. Post fluid interaction, the CO2-rich solution exits the column at the bottom 
and the clean gas at the top. After the carbon capture process, the CO2-rich solution is 
treated in the stripper to regenerate the initial K2CO3 solution (Borhani et al., 2015). A 
schematic of the process is depicted in Figure 8 below. 
 
Figure 8: Schematic of the K2CO3 solution CO2 capture process (Borhani et al.,2015)

The described process is carried out at high pressures, around 30 bar. High pressure is 
required to work as a driving force to ensure CO2 transfer from the gas to the solution 
(Borhani et al., 2015). In addition, high pressure ensures that the operating temperature 
is close to the atmospheric boiling point of the solution, prohibiting excessive 
evaporation losses (Borhani et al., 2015). 
According to Borhani et al. (2015) potassium carbonate reacts with water to produce 
potassium bicarbonate (Eq. 13). This reaction, more specifically the transformation of 
carbonate to bicarbonate ions is pH dependent (Borhani et al., 2015). At pH values 
greater than 8, the mechanism proceeds through the reaction of carbonate with 
hydroxide ions (Eq. 14 & 15). While, at a pH lower than 8 bicarbonate ions are 
produced by the reaction of carbonate ions with water molecules (Eq. 16 & 17). 
Furthermore, the pH threshold indicates if Eq. 14 or Eq. 16 is the rate determining step 
(Borhani et al., 2015).

It can be noted that increasing the temperature favors the kinetics of the reaction leading 
to higher CO2 absorption (Rouxhet et al., 2022). Although the corrosion issue has been 
mitigated by the utilization of potassium carbonate instead of potassium hydroxide, the 
main issue remaining is that the new absorbent has very slow kinetics. Thus, mass 
transfer limitations are present leading to increased absorption column volumes 
required and consequently higher capital investments (Rouxhet et al., 2022). 
Since the capture stage constitutes 70% of the operating costs for carbon capture and 
storage (Sun et al., 2018), care should be taken into the materials used for carbon 
dioxide capture. This is the main reasoning of the third method for CO2 capture that 
utilizes solid adsorbents. In addition, solid adsorbents can be used in a wide range of 
temperatures that also makes them favorable (Sun et al., 2018). Furthermore, they can 
==PAGE:20==
 
19 
 
be more easily disposed, posing smaller threat to the environment (Sun et al., 2018) 
compared to liquid absorbents. 
Carbon dioxide adsorbents can be categorized in three different groups based on the 
operating temperature. The first category is low-temperature sorbents (T < 200 °C), the 
second category is intermediate-temperature (200 °C < T < 400 °C) and the last 
category is high-temperature (T > 400 °C) (Sun et al., 2018). One of the most used solid 
high-temperature adsorbents for CO2 capture is CaO. According to Sun et al. (2018) 
this is mainly attributed to “the high reactivity with CO2 (theoretical capture capacity 
of CaO is 17.8 mmol g-1) and the availability of low cost natural CaO precursors.”. 
Calcium oxide is significantly cheaper than the amine absorbents used for amine 
scrubbing with prices per ton of CO2 ranging from $16-44 and from $31-80 for CaO 
and amines respectively (Sun et al., 2018). 
Carbon capture with CaO is a two-step mechanism. The first step is the carbonation 
of the CaO (Eq. 18), resulting in the production of calcium carbonate (CaCO3). The 
second step is the calcination (Eq. 19) during which CaCO3 is decomposed into CaO 
releasing CO2 and regenerating the sorbent. Carbonation occurs at temperatures ranging 
from 600 °C to 800 °C and the used sorbent is calcined above 800 °C (Sun et al., 2018).

The interesting thing about the carbonation reaction though is that it consists of two 
stages a fast initial stage and a significantly slower second stage (Sun et al., 2018). The 
second stage has a slower rate due to the diffusion of CO2 through the formed CaCO3 
layer (Barker, 1973; Sun et al, 2018). The diffusion coefficient of carbon dioxide 
through CaO is 0.003 cm2 s-1, while for CaCO3 the coefficient is equal to 0.3 cm2 s-1 
(Sun et al., 2018). Therefore, research has shown that the first fast step is slowed down 
after a critical thickness of a CaCO3 layer was formed (Barker, 1973; Sun et al, 2018). 
After that the rate determining factor is the diffusion of CO2 through the CaCO3 layer. 
Based on results from Alvarez and Abanades (2005), the critical layer thickness is 
approximately 49 nm.

• Carbonation temperature

carbonation reaction. Increased CO2 partial pressure results in easier diffusion of carbon 
==PAGE:22==

carbonation (Sun et al., 2018). 
In a similar way an increase in carbonation temperature promotes the carbonation 
reaction rate. According to Li et al. (2012), just increasing the carbonation temperature 
from 500 to 600 °C reduces the thickness of the CaCO3 layer formed. Therefore, carbon 
capture of the adsorbent was improved (Sun et al, 2018). 
Carbonation time also positively affects carbonation reaction. Extending the time 
required for carbonation has as a result an increased capacity of CO2 capture by the 
CaO adsorbent (Sun et al, 2013; Sun et al, 2018). 
Contaminants have different effects on carbonation rate based on the contaminant. 
Steam promoted the carbonation rate by promoting the diffusion of CO2 through the 
adsorbent (Sun et al., 2018). On the other hand, SO2 limits the carbonation rate since it 
reacts with CaO towards CaSO4 (Sun et al., 2018). 
Overall, post-combustion methods taking advantage of CO2 absorption (Amine 
Scrubbing, KOH/K2CO3) and CO2 adsorption (CaO) are reliable technologies for the 
abatement of CO2 emissions. However, their main disadvantage is the high thermal 
energy requirements to regenerate the sorbents. Additionally, the corrosive nature of 
some of them dictates that they should be handled carefully to avoid extra operational 
costs for equipment replacement. Last but not least, it should be taken into account that 
in all the above-mentioned methods chemicals are a prerequisite for the process to take 
place.

1.3.1 Electrochemically Mediated Amine Regeneration (EMAR) 
This method mainly targets to reduce the high energy requirement for amine 
regeneration. It should be noted that for thermal amine absorbent regeneration in the 
case of direct air capture (DAC) the energy requirement is approximately 200-300 kJ 
per mol CO2 and for point source cases (e.g. flue gases) the requirement is still as high 
as 100-150 kJ per mol CO2 (Renfrew et al., 2020). On the contrary, EMAR combines 
the advantage of traditional amine scrubbing, being highly efficient for CO2 removal 
with the ability of electrochemical processes to target specific molecules rather than the 
medium (Stern et el., 2013).  
According to Stern and his co-workers (2013), the EMAR process can significantly 
improve the currently applied thermal amine scrubbing technology. Firstly, the novel 
method has the capability to desorb 80 to 90 percent of the absorbed CO2, something 
which was not possible due to amine thermal degradation in the traditional process. 
This results in a decrease up to 40 percent in the circulation rate of the amine absorbent 
and consequently in the capital expenditures by reducing the plant size (Stern et al., 
2013). Furthermore, EMAR increases the energy efficiency of the amine scrubbing 
stage (Stern et al., 2013). Another advantage of EMAR is that the absorbent and the 
compound that participates in the redox reaction are distinct (Stern et al., 2013). More 
specifically the absorbent is the amine and the redox species is a transition metal. The 
transition metal is initially at an inert metallic condition and then is oxidized to form its 
cation, competing with carbon dioxide for bonding with the amines (Stern et al., 2013). 
In the EMAR process, the scrubbing step is substituted by a two-compartment 
electrochemical cell that can operate at the absorber temperature (Rahimi et al., 2022). 
Stern et al. (2013) have developed an EMAR system based on amines and copper. They 
have chosen this combination because amines have high CO2 absorption abilities and 
the electrochemistry of copper is well-known (Stern et al., 2013). From the different 
amines, polyamines were selected due to the stability of polyamine-copper compounds 
that cause better CO2 desorption (Stern et al., 2013). The EMAR method relies on three 
reactions, each one of them taking place at the absorber (Eq. 23), the anode (Eq. 24) 
and the cathode (Eq. 25). In the absorber reaction carbon dioxide is absorbed by the 
amine. Then, the amine enriched with CO2 is dispensed to the cell anode, where copper 
(Cu) oxidation takes place and copper cations (Cu2+) are produced. In the anode

to the absorber tank (Stern et al., 2013; Rahimi et al., 2022).

there is a finite number of amines that possess the ability to form chemical compounds

1.3.2 Membrane Capacitive Deionization (MCDI) 
Another method utilized for electrochemical CO2 capture is Membrane Capacitive 
Deionization. MCDI is a CO2 capture technology based on adsorption. The principle of 
MCDI is that upon current application, bicarbonate and carbonate ions can be separated 
from the electrolyte and stored inside the pores of an electrical double layer (EDL). At 
the same time hydrogen cations are stored in the pores of the EDL of the cathode 
electrode (Rahimi et al., 2022). For desorption to occur, the electrode polarity should 
be reversed, which is accomplished by reversing the current applied to the cell (Legrand 
et al., 2018; Renfrew et al., 2020; Rahimi et al., 2022). The EDL is formed on the 
electrodes of the cell by covering one of them with an anion exchange membrane 
(AEM) and the other with a cation exchange membrane (CEM) (Legrand et al., 2018;

Renfrew et al., 2020; Rahimi et al., 2022). An AEM involves a hydrophobic polymer 
such as poly-sulfone, polyphenylene or polystyrene that form the backbone and also 
incorporates hydrophilic anionic functionalities like quaternary ammonium, 
alkylophosphonium, pyridium or imidazolium. These functionalities can either be 
integrated to the main polymeric chain or can be attached as side chains to the polymer 
(Garg et al., 2022). Figure 11 shows a typical graphical representation of an MCDI cell.

specifically, research should focus on losses and cost reduction. However, it can be 
utilized for flue gas separation since it is still a competitive technology for point source 
emission mitigation. 
 
1.3.3 Bipolar Membrane Electrodialysis (BPMED) 
The third method presented is Bipolar Membrane Electrodialysis. This method relies 
on CO2 capture in a stack of alternating anion exchange and bipolar (BPMs) membranes 
(Eisaman et al., 2011; Rahimi et al., 2022). The BPMED system consists of two 
different types of membranes and two different types of solutions, one acidic and one 
basic (Eisaman et al., 2011). Bipolar membranes are a significant part of the setup, since 
they are responsible for water separation, under current application, to produce OH- and 
H+ ions (Eisaman et al., 2011; Renfrew et al., 2020). The current also promotes the flow 
of OH- ions to the anode and H+ ions to the cathode side of the unit. This process is a 
crucial step due to the formation of a pH gradient though the BPMED unit, that also 
leads to the transport of bicarbonate and carbonate ions inside the setup (Renfrew et al., 
2020). 
When the CO2 anions reach an acidic solution because they exhibit low solubility, they 
form gaseous CO2 (Eisaman et al., 2011). A schematic of a BPMED unit can be seen 
in Figure 12.

A typical BPMED setup requires an energy input of 100 kJ per mol of CO2 for the 
regeneration step and 200 kJ for the absorption step in the case of a KOH absorbent 
solution. This makes the total energy toll for the process to be approximately 300 kJ 
per mol CO2 (Eisaman et al., 2011). According to Renfrew et al. (2020) the energy cost 
is also dependent on the anionic form of CO2 given that for the formation of HCO3- one 
H+ atom is required, while in the case of CO32- two protons are required. This makes 
the process energy cost for the carbonate scenario double compared to the bicarbonate 
equivalent (240 kJ/mol CO2 and 120 kJ/mol CO2 respectively). 
Overall, BPMED is a technology quite different from the previously mentioned 
electrochemical processes. BPMED is a method that can be carried out in ambient 
temperature and therefore decreases the energy requirements for heating the system. 
Additionally, it decreases water deficit (Renfrew et al., 2020). On the other hand, the 
energy penalty related to water dissociation being the driving force of the system 
increases energy consumption significantly. Furthermore, the cost of the utilized 
membranes makes the BPMED process financially inefficient (Renfrew et al., 2020; 
Rahimi et al., 2022). As a result, the method should be amended in order to be 
competitive with other CO2 capture methods. This can be achieved by enhancing the 
==PAGE:29==

cell performance (higher current density operation or more effective water splitting) or 
by utilizing membranes that exhibit better long-term stability (Renfrew et al., 2020). 
 
1.4 Anion Exchange Membrane Electrochemical Cell for CO2 capture 
As evident from previous chapters there is a plethora of methods, either thermal or 
electrochemical, which can be implemented for CO2 capture. The main issues for the 
former are the high energy penalty in order to regenerate the sorbents and the slow 
reaction kinetics in the case of K2CO3. This means that either considerable amounts of 
heat have to be supplied to the system or significant alterations in pressure have to be 
applied for sorbent regeneration (Muroyama et al., 2020). In the case of electrochemical 
methods, although they seem to be superior to the thermal ones, they lack when it comes 
to scalability. This is possibly a direct result of the technological immaturity of these 
technologies. For example, EMAR has the long-term operation limitation and MCDI 
and BPMED are utilizing membranes that increase the capital expenses substantially 
and that prohibits their implementation in large scale.  
A technology that may overcome high energy as well as scalability limitations is 
Electrochemical swing with an Ion Exchange Membrane. This method combines 
the lower energy requirements of the electrochemical technologies with a less 
demanding configuration compared to EMAR, MCDI and BPMED. Moreover, this 
method is based on the principle that an anion exchange membrane acts as a blockade 
that only allows anions to go through. In the case of a water feed that contains CO2 the 
anions that are transported through the membrane are carbonate and bicarbonate ions 
(Muroyama et al., 2020).  
Muroyama and Gubler (2022) have proposed an electrodialytical sorbent regeneration 
method. The goal of this method is to enrich a hydrogen gas stream with CO2 that has 
been previously absorbed by a KOH solution resulting in the formation of K2CO3. This 
method takes advantage of hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) that takes place at the 
electrolysis cell cathode and combines it with an anion exchange membrane. Then, the 
bicarbonate and carbonate ions are transported through the membrane to the anode side 
of the cell. On the anode hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR) takes place and gaseous 
CO2 is formed (Muroyama & Gubler., 2022). The main advantage of this method is that 
no chemicals are utilized and the only thing required is to provide the cell with 
electrons, through current application, for the reactions to occur. 
==PAGE:30==
 
29 
 
In the Anion Exchange Membrane Electrolysis method, the gap between the electrodes 
and the membrane is of crucial importance. In the zero-gap configuration the electrodes 
are firmly touching the anion exchange membrane. This ensures better contact between 
the electrodes and the membrane. In addition, this also has positive and negative effects 
in the cell’s overall performance. The compact design of the membrane electrode 
assembly (MEA) has a substantial effect on the catalytic activity and the performance 
of the cell (Garg et al., 2022). In more detail, the close contact between the membrane 
and the electrodes significantly reduces the ohmic losses but at the same time favors 
hydroxide ion transport over (bi)carbonate ions (Muroyama & Gubler, 2022). 
According to Muroyama and Gubler (2022), “zero gap leads to a drop in the faradaic 
efficiency to ~35% at current densities > 20 mA/cm2”, which according to the 
researchers is an indication of OH- being the main charge carrier. This is a direct result 
of the higher mobility of OH- ions compared to bicarbonate and carbonate ones (Garg 
et al., 2022). To increase the system’s Faradaic efficiency, the charge carrier should be 
either CO32- (50% efficiency) or HCO3- (>50% efficiency). This method requires at 
least 240 kJ per mol of CO2. 
However, in the case of bicarbonate and carbonate being the charge carriers, the overall 
conversion of the cell is lowered (Garg et al., 2022). This drawback is countered by the 
water oxidation at the anode side. This reaction results in the formation of oxygen and 
protons (H+). Especially, for every O2 produced 4 H+ are also produced. Given that the 
protons react with the HCO3- and CO32- ions to the formation of CO2, for every O2 
produced two CO2 can be formed from carbonate ions and four if the carriers are 
bicarbonate. This 2:1 ratio between CO2 and O2 though results in a lower conversion 
efficiency (Garg et al., 2022). A schematic of the process inside the AEM cell is 
depicted in Figure 13.

In a previous study, fundamentally similar to the one presented above, Muroyama and 
his co-workers (2021) have proposed the exploitation of the HER and HOR for CO2 
separation from a gas-stream. In this case, the main goal is to separate CO2 based on 
the carbonation of the AEM (Muroyama et al., 2021). Although it can be included in 
the category of Electrochemical swing with an Ion Exchange Membrane, the main 
difference is that no capture solution is used. Therefore, there is no absorbent 
regeneration at the cathode side. 
In this method, HER produces OH- ions that react with the CO2 contained in the gas 
feed to produce HCO3- and CO32- ions. Then, the two carbon anion species are 
transported though the anion exchange membrane to the anode compartment of the cell. 
There, HOR takes place and leads to the formation of gaseous CO2 (Muroyama et al., 
2021). 
It can be noted that the AEM plays a crucial role in the process. However, AEMs exhibit 
poor chemical stability in alkaline cell conditions (Garg et al., 2022). According to Garg 
et al. (2022) the main mechanism that leads to membrane degradation is attack by 
nucleophilic hydroxide ions. In this mechanism, OH- negatively impacts both the cation 
functional group as well as the backbone of the polymer chain (Garg et al., 2022). As a 
result, positive charges are removed and consequently the membrane conductivity is 
lowered. The OH- attack can occur either through nucleophilic substitution to the α-
carbon or through Hofmann elimination to the β-hydrogen (Garg et al., 2022). 
The presented CO2 capture methods have their own advantages and disadvantages. 
These methods’ main advantage is the low energy penalty compared to the currently 
utilized capture methodologies. Additionally, they are technologies that offer high 
customizability and can be operated without interruptions (Muroyama et al., 2020). On 
the other hand, the methods face some limitations like the low stability of the AEM. 
Furthermore, to be able to compete with other technologies, selectivity as well as their 
performance under low CO2 concentrations have to be enhanced (Muroyama et al., 
2020). 
In this Master Thesis, the proposed idea is based on the research from Muroyama et al. 
(2021) with a slight modification that instead of a direct gas feed to the cathode side, 
the carbon dioxide feed is diluted in a water stream. The main goals are to evaluate the 
cell performance as well as the cell’s ability to separate CO2 from a water stream. If the 
hypothesis is true, then the method may prove very useful for CO2 abatement.

2.2 Through-plane conductivity measurement 
The through-plane conductivity measurement comprises of the following steps: 
1. Electrode preparation 
2. Membrane treatment 
3. Membrane Electrode Assembly 
4. Cell assembly 
5. Cell testing 
The first step includes the preparation of the catalyst spray and the spraying of the 
electrode. The second step as explained in detail in Chapter 2.1 results in the 
acquisition of a membrane with the desired counterion. In the MEA formation, the 
AEM is “sandwiched” between the electrodes and this new structure is a prerequisite 
for the cell assembly step. During cell assembly the MEA is combined with two bipolar 
==PAGE:38==
 
37 
 
plates (one on each side of the MEA). Lastly, in the testing, the assembled cell’s 
performance under the various test conditions is evaluated. 
 
2.2.1 Electrode preparation 
Electrode preparation is a multistep process that involves the synthesis of the basic 
components (the catalyst spray and the support) and the spraying. For the synthesis of 
the electrodes, three in-house developed recipes were used.  
The catalyst spray comprises of three components, the ionomer dispersion, the catalyst 
nanoparticles and ethanol. The first step is the creation of the ionomer dispersion. The 
ionomer dispersion contains 1% Fumasep FAA-3-film (FUMATECH BWT) polymer 
dissolved into 99% Ethanol. In more detail, to prepare the ionomer dispersion, 1g of 
the FAA-3 film is dissolved in 99g of ethanol and the resulting mixture is stirred for 15 
minutes until we get a two-phase dispersion (Figure 19). The nanoparticles are already 
available from a commercial supplier and the same applies to Ethanol. In all the 
following recipes the ionomer dispersion used is the same, however, the catalyst or the 
catalyst loading may differ. 
 
Figure 19: Ionomer dispersion  
The first catalyst synthesized contained 10% Pt/C, the second 20% Pt/C (both 
supplied by Sigma-Aldrich) and the third 100% IrO2 (provided by Alfa Aesar). In 
order to prepare the first catalyst solution, 0.486 g of 10% Pt/C powder were weighted 
using an analytical scale. After, approximately 17 g of Ethanol and 32.4 g of ionomer 
dispersion were weighted. The three components were mixed together in a borosilicate 
bottle and the bottleneck was tightly sealed using parafilm to prevent ethanol 
==PAGE:39==
 
38 
 
evaporation. In the next step, the bottle was placed into a beaker that was half filled 
with water and introduced to the Branson 5510 Ultrasonic cleaner water bath for 
sonication. The sonication lasted at least 2 hours. 
In the case of the other two catalyst solutions, the followed procedure was the same as 
above, but the weights of the components were modified accordingly. In the second 
catalyst case, the 20% Pt/C nanoparticles weighted 0.243 g and were dissolved in 5.4 g 
of ionomer dispersion and 8.5 g of Ethanol. In the last catalyst, 0.384 g of 100% Ir (IV) 
oxide were weighted and the liquid phase of the mixture consisted of 9.6 g of ionomer 
dispersion and 13.4 g of Ethanol. 
During the sonication the catalyst support was prepared. In the conducted experiments, 
two different types of support were utilized. These supports were an 8 by 8 cm Nickel 
foam mesh and an 8 by 8 cm Carbon cloth. In the case of the nickel mesh, the initial 
material thickness had to be reduced to 300 μm using a hydraulic press. The thickness 
of the carbon cloth was approximately 400 μm. The nickel mesh support was used for 
the 10% Pt/C and 100% IrO2 electrodes. On the contrary, the carbon cloth support was 
used for the 20 % Pt/C electrode. After reaching the desired support thickness, the 
weight of it was determined. Then, the support material was mounted on a metal plate 
covered with aluminum foil with the aid of tape. The final step for the support 
preparation was heating it at 150 °C for 1-hour prior to spraying. 
When the catalyst solution was ready, the solution was sprayed on the support under 
constant heating at 150 °C. The spraying was carried out with a spray gun utilizing 
pressurized air. This ensures a uniform catalyst loading on the support. Furthermore, 
the application of heating was important for the evaporation of the liquid phase of the 
catalyst solution. After the conclusion of the spraying, the weight of the electrode was 
measured to determine the catalyst loading. The desired catalyst loading for the Ir (IV) 
oxide electrode was 3 mg/cm2, while for both Pt/C electrodes it was 0.38 mg/cm2. The 
formula used for the loading calculation is given below (Eq. 38).

2.2.5 Cell testing 
After completion of the MEA, the cell was transferred to the rig for testing. The cell

taking impedance measurements in a frequency range from 1 Hz to 1 MHz. In addition 
the cell was connected with two heating elements and a temperature control 
thermocouple to maintain a constant temperature. Moreover, the inlets and the outlets 
of the anode and the cathode were both connected with polytetrafluoroethelyne (PTFE) 
tubing for the circulation of the various electrolytes used in the experiment. 
The rig (Figure 24) apart from accommodating the cell has multiple instruments which 
include: 
• Gas outlets connected with Brooks Instrument SLA5850 Elastomer Sealed 
Thermal Mass Flow Controllers and Meters for various gas circulation in the

were operated by an independent interface that was constructed using 
Laboratory Virtual Instrument Workbench (LabVIEW). This enables

• A CAL 9300 Temperature controller for cell temperature regulation.

feasible to capture CO2 from air taking advantage of electrochemical diffusion of CO2 
through the AEM cell. To be able to showcase that, a program should be constructed 
that will finally support the hypothesis by two interconnected sets of results: 
1. An I-V curve that shows that it is power wise favorable to use this technique. 
2. A significant concentration reading of CO2 in the sensor should be acquired 
indicating efficient separation of carbon dioxide from air. 
The following steps of the CO2 gas analysis: 
• H2O-H2O electrolysis 
• Ar-H2O electrolysis 
• Ar-H2O containing CO2 
• H2-H2O electrolysis 
• H2-H2O containing CO2 
In each of the above steps the impedance using a frequency range from 10 Hz to 100 
kHz was measured and the I-V curve was constructed. Additionally, in the steps that 
CO2 was included in the cathode feed, the CO2 concentration was monitored.

In the case of the FAA-3-50 membrane, the theoretical range for a 0.1 mA current is

mere 2 to 3 hours. This indicates that the experimental trend is also supported by 
mathematical calculations. However, in both FAA-3-50 current experiments the time 
was slightly greater than the calculated one. At 0.1 mA the required time was 11.5 hours 
and in the 0.3 mA case 3.5 h. This deviation from theory can be attributed to a major, 
in our opinion, modification to Ziv and Dekel’s (2018) protocol, which suggested that 
that produced CO2 has to be purged of by N2. In our sets of experiments, that was not 
the case. This may have resulted in back diffusion of CO2 causing re-saturation of the 
membrane with HCO3 to some extent. Consequently, the decarbonation time has also 
risen.  
An additional reason for the longer decarbonation time is that we assume 100% current 
efficiency. In practice, this is not the case and a portion of the produced OH- ions may 
for example diffuse into the water solution causing a pH increase, without any 
==PAGE:55==
 
54 
 
contribution to the membrane conversion. Moreover, the above calculations are done 
with a basis that only bicarbonate ions are involved in the conversion, which is not 
100% accurate, since there might be a percentage of carbonate ions present. As we 
know, 2 electrons are required for the carbonate conversion and thus the total 
conversion time required is doubled. All the presented arguments support the deviation 
from the mathematically calculated conversion time values. 
Another interesting observation is that independently from the applied current, both 
membranes exhibit the same re-saturation time trend. In the case of the reference 
membrane, the CO2 pumping started at t1= 9078.52s and at t2= 9088.24s for 0.7 and 0.9 
mA respectively. Taking into account the data from Tables A.1 and A.2, the total time 
for re-saturation has been calculated and is equal to 28 minutes for 0.7 mA and 21.5 
minutes for 0.9 mA. For the FAA-3-50 membrane in the 0.1 mA and 0.3 mA 
experiments CO2 was pumped at 57692s. Considering the data presented in Tables A.3 
& A.4 the total times for re-saturation are 20 and 25 minutes for the 0.1 and 0.3 mA 
respectively. This gives us the time required to reach the pure HCO3- form of the 
membrane that ranges from 20 to 28 minutes. 
This quick re-saturation time is a main outcome of instant reaction between the 
produced OH ions, as described in equation 32, with CO2 molecules to form bicarbonate 
and carbonate ions (Ziv & Dekel, 2018; Zhengur – Khais et al., 2020). The formed 
species tend to convert the membrane back to the pure HCO3- form and for the full 
conversion some hours are required. However, the biggest part of the carbonation 
process is already done in a matter of minutes (Yanagi et al., 2008; Suzuki et al., 2013; 
Divekar et al., 2017; Zheng et al., 2019; Zhengur - Khais et al., 2020).

3.2.3 Full experiment I-V curves 
As explained in Paragraph 2.2.5 the final cell (100% IrO2 anode – 10% Pt/C cathode) 
was tested under different anode and cathode side feeds. For all the steps, the I-V curve 
profile was created and is depicted in Figures 41 & 42. In the first step after water 
electrolysis instead of water the feed changed to a carrier gas and more specifically 
argon. In both water electrolysis and Ar-H2O steps the electrochemical reactions that 
took place are HER on the cathode (Eq. 40) and OER on the anode (Eq. 41) and the 
membrane was in the OH- form. Therefore, there should not be observed a significant 
change in the voltage values between the two steps. This is supported by the 
polarization curves that showcase that at small currents (below 62.5 mA/cm2) the cell 
behaves in the same way as in water electrolysis. However, at currents larger or equal 
to 62.5 mA/cm2, the I-V curve corresponding to Ar is above the equivalent for water. 
More specifically at 62.5 mA/cm2 the voltage is equal to 2.2 V, while in the water case 
the voltage was 2V. This is probably due to a decrease in membrane conductivity due 
to the lower membrane hydration on the permeate side. This loss of hydration results in 
higher membrane resistances and an increase in ohmic losses. Therefore, the 
overpotential slightly rose and subsequently the voltage. 
In the next step CO2 was introduced on the feed side and the cell’s reactions are 
modified as shown in equations 42 & 43. Based on the new cathode side reaction, 
instead of OH- formation, HCO3- and CO32- ions are formed. The addition of carbon 
dioxide on the cathode has led to a further increase in the voltage values measured. As 
a consequence, the voltage value at 62.5 mA/cm2 rose to 2.28 V. An explanation for 
==PAGE:63==
 
62 
 
this new increase is that instead of OH-, HCO3- and CO32- ions formed on the cathode 
side are the charge carriers. Given that the membrane is in OH form from the 
electrolysis step, through the CO2 migration from the cathode to the anode, the 
membrane’s high OH conductivity is reduced to the lower (bi)carbonate ion 
conductivity value. So, the cell voltage increase is primarily related to the membrane 
conductivity change. Another possibility that may contribute to a smaller extent may 
be that of the modification of the electrode reaction. 
In the last step, H2 was chosen as a carrier gas replacing Ar. In addition, CO2 pumping 
on the feed side continued. The shift from Ar to H2 was accompanied by a decrease in 
voltage values. More precisely, from the value of 2.28 V in the case of Ar as a carrier 
gas, the new voltage is equal to 2.18 V at 62.5 mA/cm2. This decrease in voltage is a 
direct result of the removal of the OER on the cell’s permeate side. When the carrier 
gas is shifted, water is produced on the anode side (Eq.44) instead of oxygen (Eq.43) 
and this leads to a notable decrease in the anode potential and consequently in the 
overall cell potential. However, in the presented I-V curves this decrease cannot be 
seen. This is because the voltage reduction, post hydrogen application on the anode, 
requires a substantial amount of time but the measurements were acquired shortly after. 
Therefore the below polarization curves are not steady state and the decreasing voltage 
trend differs from the one presented in Figure 44.

concentration decreased slightly and was equal to 332 ppm. 
To sum up, current density has a positive influence in the amount of CO2 transported 
through the membrane. However, at higher current density values a decline is observed. 
This is in accordance with the findings of Muroyama and his co-authors (2021) that 
increasing concentrations of CO2 on the anode side may hinder CO2 transport due to 
back-diffusion through the membrane. Also, as expected a higher CO2 content in the 
cathode side leads to a higher concentration in the permeate side for the same current 
density values. It should be noted that the higher CO2 feed concentration may be also a 
contributing factor to the more rapidly declining trend in the case of pure CO2.

Chapter 4: Conclusion 
To sum up, CO2 capture plays a critical role in limiting the concentration of Greenhouse 
gases in the atmosphere and will constitute the driving force to combat climate change. 
Currently available technologies either rely on chemicals that require high thermal 
energy penalties to regenerate them, such as amines or CaO, or have corrosive 
properties, like KOH, or slow CO2 capture kinetics, for example K2CO3.  
On the other hand, novel electrochemical technologies are becoming increasingly 
popular since they can reduce energy requirements. However, these technologies are 
immature compared to their already well-established counterparts. EMAR, a very 
promising process, is facing scalability issues mainly due to long-term operation 
instabilities. In addition, BPMED and MCDI have limited implementation due to the 
high cost that is associated with the membrane materials utilized. 
In the current Thesis a carbon capture technology is proposed based on an AEM 
electrolysis cell. In this technique CO2 is separated from a water stream with the aid of 
an anion exchange membrane. CO2 is transported from the cell’s cathode compartment 
to the permeate side in the form of HCO3- and CO32- ions that have been formed in the 
feed side by reacting CO2 with OH- produced via water electrolysis.  
Based on our investigation from the in-plane experiments, it is evident that higher 
temperatures lead to higher membrane conductivities. In addition, it has been 
demonstrated that anionic conductivity in the FAA-3-50 membrane decreased in the 
following order OH- > Cl- > CO32- > HCO3-. Another observation from the in-plane 
measurement is that increasing the applied current results in a significant decrease in 
the membrane decarbonization time. Lastly, the re-saturation of the AEM is completed 
very fast independently of the applied current. 
In the through-plane direction conductivity measurements, the polarization curves were 
constructed based on the acquired data and they seem well in fit with currently available 
data from literature. Another interesting remark is that higher voltage values lead to 
higher CO2 crossover. In a similar way, low CO2 content in the cathode compartment 
of the cell results in a lower voltage value. Finally, the role of current density was 
investigated and according to the experimental data, higher current density values 
promote CO2 transfer through the AEM until a threshold. For values above this limit, 
the accumulation of CO2 in the permeate side of the cell causes back diffusion of CO2 
through the membrane.  
==PAGE:73==
 
72 
 
The method’s main advantage is that no chemicals, only electrons, are required to 
capture the CO2 and H2O can be regenerated on the permeate side by reaction between 
the bicarbonate and carbonate ions with hydrogen. Another advantage is that the 
technology enables near room temperature operation at 40 °C. However, to be 
competitive with other methods, the energy losses have to be mitigated. That can be 
done either by investigating the available AEM membranes and selecting one that has 
higher conductivity and longer stability, or by testing other electrode materials. The 
former reduces ohmic losses and the latter activation losses. Other operational aspects 
that may have to be taken into account are a possible temperature increase or an alkaline 
solution instead of water that both may lead to higher membrane conductivities. 
In our opinion this is a very promising method for CO2 capture, even though some 
aspects require further investigation and improvement.
