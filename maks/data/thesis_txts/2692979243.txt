ABSTRACT
Sustainability concerns are leading the energy sector towards a more resilient and carbon
neutral system, reshaping the energy mix with a growing share of renewable energy-based
production. In this context, the progress in the technologies’ eﬃciency and economic
competitiveness are participating to the increase of the penetration of wind energy in the
power system. This brings new challenges to maintain the secured and balanced operation
of the grid. Indeed, wind plants are less ﬂexible energy source and do not provide inertia to
the power system. Consequently, the actors of the wind energy sector need tools to measure
and analyse the impact of wind turbines on the grid, so as to adapt the regulation or
conduct new research to improve the integration of renewable energy sources in the power
system. To do so, dynamic models are used to perform transient stability analysis. In the
past 20 years, generic public models have been developed and can be used to represent
hypothetical or real machines.
This thesis aims at scaling a dynamic model to characterize the behaviour and power
capping capability of real 2 MW wind turbines installed in Aakirkeby. The modelling
process is supported by an existing academic model of a Type 3 machine, technical data
and a set of 10-minutes measurements from the real 2 MW technology. The information
gathered are analysed and confronted on speciﬁc time frames to understand the steady
state of the machine in terms of active and reactive power, rotational speed and pitch
angle. From the available data, the modiﬁable elements of the model are identiﬁed, being
parameters or diagrams, to reproduce the observed behaviour. The design of the new
parameters is supported by related theories on the MPT curve, the pre-pitching control
logic and the implementation of power capping capability. The ﬁnal model is also used to
conduct a comparative analysis between power capping and delta power control from the
machine and the grid perspective. Thus, a new version of the model is extended with delta
power control. The systems responses are investigated on 6 scenarios of wind climates at
diﬀerent speed amplitude and turbulence intensity.
The ﬁnal model is equipped with a scaled generator, an adapted MPT curve, a scaled
CP -Table and an additional pre-pitching control logic. The parameters are set to provide
a compromise between the accuracy on the simulated power, pitch angle and rotational
speed. The model is estimated to oﬀer an average error below 6% on the power, 0.5% on
the speed and 0.5◦on the pitch angle on the compared datasets. Besides, it is found that
the strategy implemented to enable power capping capability maximizes the rotational
speed of the turbine. This strategy is reproduced in the simulation without compromising
the operation in a normal conﬁguration. Eventually, the power capping capability of the
turbine proves to be interesting to limit and stabilize the power production of a wind
turbine with a simple control logic. Delta power control appears more interesting to reduce
the oscillations on the machine under turbulent wind climates, and to reserve a part of the
production.

INTRODUCTION
In a context of growing concerns to reduce the global greenhouse gas emissions and take
actions against climate change, the European Commission proposed the 2030 Climate
Target Plan to cut Europe emissions by at least 55% by 2030 [1]. The generation of
electricity and heat only is estimated to account for 30% of these emissions [2]. Thus, there
is a growing need to oﬀer more sustainable electric energy sources. In this context, the
wind energy sector keeps on developing to propose more eﬃcient and reliable technologies.
1.1
Status of the Wind Energy Sector
Despite the pandemic, 2020 has seen a new record regarding the installation of new capacity
of wind plants worldwide with 93 GW installed during the year, bringing the global wind
capacity to 743 GW. This capacity is estimated to avoid the emissions of 1.1 billion tonnes
of CO2 per year (compare to the average level of conventional units). Recently, the
innovations in the technology’s eﬃciency and ﬂexibility and the economy of scales have
established wind power as one of the most cost-competitive power sources. Besides, the
pandemic context has proved it to also be resilient [3].
In Europe, 16.4% of the electricity production has been covered by wind sources in 2020,
against 15% in 2019. This increasing trend still requires to be conﬁrmed because of the
unusual electricity demand proﬁle experienced in Europe during the lockdown. Still, over
the next ﬁve years, a new capacity of 80 to 105 GW is expected to be installed in the
EU, while 27% of the decommissioned farms are expected to be repowered, as shown in
Figure 1.1. As the global wind capacity rises, the size of the wind turbines also increases.
Indeed, the average rating of a new machine is 3.3 MW for onshore facilities and 8.2 MW
for oﬀshore facilities in 2020, against 2.7 MW and 5.6 MW in 2017, respectively [4][5].

1.2
Integration Challenges & Grid Code Requirements
Conventional generation units provide a controllable power production and are required
to support the safe, stable, and reliable operation of the grid through diﬀerent ancillary
services. Being wind a vol energy source by nature, a wind-based power production is less
controllable and stable. Thus, the integration of wind energy in the power system leads to
new challenges to secure the electricity provision on the grid [6].
To tackle the issue of the controllability of the power production, modern wind turbines
(WTs) are variable-speed generators connected to the grid through power electronic devices
(partially or completely) which allow to control and ﬁlter the active and reactive power
output of the machine. However, the rotational mass of the WT becomes isolated from
the rest of the electrical network. Therefore, the rotating turbine does not contribute to
the global inertia of the power system, as usual units do. The inertia of the power system
is decisive because it determines its ability to contain the propagation of a disturbance in
the grid frequency. So, it inﬂuences the stability of the electricity distribution. Indeed, the
rate of change of the grid frequency translates the (un)balance between power production
and consumption. Thus, the growing penetration of WTs in the power system leads to a
reduction of the global inertia and compromises the stability of the grid [7].
As a result, grid operators tend to require the new wind power plants to provide the
same ancillary services as the conventional units [8]. In Denmark, wind farms with a
capacity larger than 11 kW must withstand frequency and voltage deviations under normal
and abnormal operations without compromising their power output. They also must
remain connected and provide reactive power capability under fault events. Depending
on the size of the generation, diﬀerent requirements might be applied [9]. Currently, the
Transmission System Operators (TSO) should be able to activate some functionalities
for all new installed turbine, like an external control of the active power production, an
external absolute limitation of this power and a control of the power factor at the point of
connection with grid [9]. Moreover, the improved controllability of modern WTs allows
them to propose services to regulate the voltage, to provide inertial response in the case of
frequency disturbances and to participate to the regulation of the grid frequency. This last
service particularly requires to reserve a part of the wind-based production to re-balance
the power generated with the power consumed.
1.3
Status of Wind Turbine Models for Power System
Analysis
As explained in the previous part, the increase of the wind power penetration in power
systems implies many challenges in terms of grid integration, for reasons of ﬂexibility
and stability, among others. As a result, the actors of the wind industry must be able
to measure the impact of wind generators on the grid, in order to properly integrate the
renewable energy sources. This type of study is usually done through transient stability
analysis, and then requires dynamic models to simulate the behaviour of wind power
generation units connected to a grid [10]. These types of model can be used by both the
Transmission System Operators and Distribution System Operators (DSO) in charge of the
2
==PAGE:23==
1. Introduction
power grid, but also by wind turbine manufacturers, technical consultants, or academics
for research or education purposes.
Depending on the scope of the study, two types of wind turbine models can be found in
the literature [10]. The Electromagnetic Transients (EMT) type refers to a simulation
which includes electromagnetic transients. Also described as the detailed ones, the EMT
models are very accurate but require a lot of technical data along with high computational
costs. On the other side, the Root Mean Square (RMS) type refers to a positive sequence
model, based on electromechanical average over one line and period. This simpliﬁed model
is more commonly requested by grid operators, considering the level of precision needed
for large-scale grid studies [10].
Initially, dynamic models for stability analysis were vendor-speciﬁc, and thus of high
technical complexity. In contrast with other conventional generators, most of the wind
turbine models were not widely standardized, and the information on validation procedure
were limited [10][11]. Then, over the past twenty years, several generic models have been
deﬁned for speciﬁc software by R&D entities or wind turbine manufacturers [10]. In 2007,
a research group of the Risø National Laboratory published a collection of models for
three diﬀerent wind turbine concepts to be implemented in the power simulation tool
PowerFactory (DIgSILENT) [12]. Besides, between 2005 and 2015, the Western Electricity
Coordinating Council (WECC) and the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC)
jointly developed standardized generic models applicable for stability analysis, based on
the existing models of the literature [10][13]. The IEC standard 61400-27-01 released in
2015 classiﬁes the four main wind turbine typologies available on the market, introduces
a generic simpliﬁed model for each technology, and describes the validation procedure
associated [13]. Nowadays, most of the modelling solutions presented in the literature refer
to the IEC standard.

2.1
Wind Turbine Typologies
To begin with, this section points out the generic working principle of a wind turbine.
Then, it deﬁnes the four technology typologies found in the market nowadays.
2.1.1
Working principle
A wind turbine is a generator which converts the kinetic energy of the wind into electrical
energy. The main steps and components of the energy conversion process are described in
the simpliﬁed diagram of Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1. Simpliﬁed concept of a wind turbine (Adapted from [17])
First, a turbine composed of several blades, usually three, is driven by the wind. The
extracted kinetic energy is transmitted to the shaft of an electromechanical conversion
device: the generator. This transmission often includes a gearbox system. Then, the
produced electrical power is fed into the grid through a power electronic interface and
a transformer. The power electronic interface, usually implemented in modern turbines,
allows to control the generator voltage and frequency. In addition, the conﬁguration of the
aerodynamic rotor can also be adjusted. These controllers enhance the ﬂexibility of the
power generation, and thus the capability of wind turbines to behave as active generation
units [17].

2.1.2
Standard typologies
Wind turbine technologies are usually classiﬁed in four main typologies, widely described
in the literature [18][19][20]. The deﬁnitions given here are based on the description of the
IEC standard 64100, and presented in the Figure 2.2 [13].
Figure 2.2. Wind turbine typologies (Adapted from [10])
Type 1 (or A) WTs are directly connected to the grid through an asynchronous generator,
without power converter. The rotor is connected to the generator via a gearbox. Since this
technology uses a squirrel-cage induction machine, the turbine consumes reactive power,
which is compensated by switched capacitor banks. Besides, this type of wind turbine often
works at ﬁxed rotational speed since the operational speed range given by the asynchronous
generator is limited. As a result, the rotational speed of the turbine is directly coupled to
the grid frequency, which leads to a direct transmission of the power ﬂuctuation to the grid.
It can work with ﬁxed (Type 1A) or variable blade pitch angle (Type 1B). This concept
used to be the most popular technology before speciﬁc requirements from the grid code
were applied to wind turbines [21].
Type 2 (or B) WTs are similar to Type 1, but include a variable resistance at the terminals
of the wound rotor of the asynchronous generator. This resistance makes the machine
capable of working in variable speed operation, and thus limits the power ﬂuctuation
injected to the grid. This type is associated with variable pitch operation since it is
normally equipped with a blade pitch angle control.
Type 3 (or C) WTs are equipped with a wound rotor induction machine. The most
common generator used is a Doubly Fed Induction Generator (DFIG). The stator is
directly connected to the grid, while the rotor is indirectly coupled through back-to-back
power converters. The rotor-side of the power converters controls independently the active
and reactive power output of the machine, while the grid-side of the power converters
controls the DC-link voltage and power factor. Type 3 WTs oﬀer both variable pitch and
8
==PAGE:29==
2. V80-2MW Technology in Kalby
variable speed controls, with an operational speed range usually between ± 30% of the
synchronous speed. These technologies can be equipped with a crowbar device on the rotor
or a chopper device in the DC-link for fault protection. The presence of power converters
allows Type 3 turbines to have reactive power control capability, and to remain connected
during and after faults. Components and working principles of Type 3 wind turbines will
be further discussed in Chapter 3.
Type 4 (or D) WTs are directly connected to the grid through a full-scale power converter.
Thus, the stator is decoupled from the grid, and is therefore not sensitive to grid short-
circuits. The generator can either be an asynchronous or synchronous machine. Some
technologies use a direct drive synchronous generator, and therefore do not include a
gearbox. The DC-link likely includes choppers for fault protection. The Type 4 concept
became a competitive technology of Type 3 due to the developments in power electronics
[21]. Nowadays, new wind turbine models are Type 3 or Type 4, where the last one is
preferred for oﬀshore installations.

In this project, a reference model of wind turbine is modiﬁed to represent the real machines

This chapter gives an overview of the existing approaches used to model the dynamic
behavior of a Type 3 wind turbine for power transient analysis.
The 6.3 MW reference model used in this project to represent a 2 MW wind turbine has
been built according to the variable speed DFIG modelling solution introduced by research

Eventually, Section 3.4

6.3 MW reference on which this model will be based on.
3.1
Model from Risø DTU National Laboratory
In 2007, research groups of the Risø DTU National Laboratory and the Aalborg University
published a report presenting a model database for wind turbines. The database has
been developed between 2001 and 2007 as part of a Danish research project. The report
introduces diﬀerent models and control strategies for three concepts of wind turbine: a
ﬁxed-speed turbine equipped with active stall control, a variable-speed turbine equipped
with a doubly-fed induction generator and a variable-speed turbine equipped with a
multi-pole permanent magnet synchronous generator [12].
The goal of each model is to represent the interaction between the wind turbine mechanical
structure and the electrical grid during diﬀerent operation modes (normal operation, fault
events, etc.). All of them had been developed and implemented on the power system
simulation tool DIgSILENT PowerFactory, which can simulate load ﬂow calculation, RMS
ﬂuctuations and transient events. The software PowerFactory (PF) is presented in detail
in Section 3.4.

3.2.4
Control system
The wind turbine control system block represents all the control loops implemented to
guarantee the optimal and/or desired operation of the machine. To deliver the required
control signals, this block uses several measurements inside the machine, at the grid
terminal and on the rotating bodies, along with external power and voltage references.
The control system of a Type 3 WT is organized in a hierarchical way, divided into two
main levels: the fast control level of the DFIG coupled with the slower control level of the
turbine [12]. Both control strategies will be discussed below, with the overall control logic
summarized in Figure 3.5.
DFIG control level
The DFIG fast control level refers to the electrical part of the machine. It is mainly
composed of two controllers applied to each power converter on the rotor branch. The
RSC deals with the independent control of both active and reactive power at the grid
terminal, while the GSC maintains the voltage level in the DC link and guarantees a unity
power factor on the rotor branch. As a consequence, the reactive power fed to the grid
only comes from the stator circuit [12]. To do so, the DFIG control needs three reference

Turbine control level
The turbine slow control level refers to the mechanical part of the machine. It manages
the generator speed and the pitch angle of the blades to optimize and/or limit the energy
extracted from the wind. Several control strategies can be applied, and two of them will
be further discussed here, as introduced by [12]. The method implemented in the reference
model corresponds to the second strategy.
First strategy (I)
The generator speed is controlled through a power controller to guarantee the optimal
operation of the turbine, before the rated power is reached. When the rated power is
achieved, the blade angle controller limits the power production to the rated value by
pitching the blades, in order to limit, among other things, the fatigue on the structure, the
noise and/or the terminal frequency.
The power controller is always active, both in optimization (also referred as partial-load) and
limitation (also referred as full-load) mode. Its main objective is to control the rotational
speed to achieve the optimal power extraction below the rated point, while allowing a
limited speed variation around the nominal value to absorb aerodynamic vibrations. The
power controller is composed of two control loops in cascade. The ﬁrst one determines
the reference speed from the measured power from the ideal P −ω characteristic of the
machine. The reference is then compared to the measured speed in a PI loop to deliver
the reference active power to the rotor-side converter.
On the other side, the pitch controller acts in the power limitation mode, and is not active
until the rated power is reached. It is composed of a PI loop which compares the measured
active power output at the grid with the rated value, thus generating the reference pitch
angle sent to the pitch servomechanism. The PI gain can be adjusted as a function of the
aerodynamic sensitivity of the turbine (dP/dβ) for higher precision.
Second strategy (II)
In this case, the optimal power reference is determined through the generator speed using a
Maximum Power Tracking method (MPT). Then, the rotational speed is controlled through
the pitch controller to limit the over speeding of the machine and the power output.
Here again, the power controller is always active, optimizing the power extraction below
the rated value. For a given generator speed, the optimum power output which can be
extracted from the wind is tracked from the MPT characteristic. This curve translates

the ideal behaviour of the turbine over the allowed rotational speed range. On another
hand, the pitch controller still acts as power limitation and is not active until the rated
rotational speed is reached. Similarly to the ﬁrst strategy, the measured rotational speed
of the generator is compared to the rated reference in a PI loop to generate the reference
pitch angle sent to the servomechanism. The measurement signal is often ﬁltered through
a low-pass block to avoid the ampliﬁcation of noise in the system.

3.2.5
Protection system
The protection system refers to the devices and strategies implemented in the machine to
protect it from any damage caused by over speeding of the rotating bodies, high currents,
or over- under-voltages in the electrical circuits. The most sensitive electrical components
of Type 3 WTs are usually the power converters since the thermal time constant of
semi-conductors is quickly reached in case of high currents [18].
When a large disturbance on the external grid occurs, large fault current might be induced
in the stator, leading to a voltage drop on the stator branch. Because of the voltage
diﬀerence, the power transfer to the grid through the grid-side converter of the rotor
branch is limited. Thus, the DC-link tends to be overloaded and the DC-voltage tends
to increase [12]. Moreover, the coupling of the rotor and stator windings implies that
excessive currents are induced in the rotor circuit. The limited speed range of the generator
limits the control over both voltage and current in the rotor branch, which can damage
the electrical components.
Then, a crowbar device is enabled to short-circuit the rotor winding of the DFIG and
isolate the power converters. Another solution is to add choppers to the DC-link. In
both cases, a speciﬁc control strategy is implemented in case of severe faults. The active
power reference is no longer deﬁned by the measured rotational speed, which is oscillating
because of the unstable electrical torque. Instead, it is deﬁned by a reference rotational
speed determined by the wind speed and sent to the MPT. When the crowbar is enabled,
the independent control of P and Q is lost. Therefore, the control of the reactive power is

==PAGE:43==

transferred to the GSC. Thus, Type 3 wind turbines remain connected to the grid during
and after fault conditions, following requirements from the grid operator.
In the reference model, the crowbar device is implemented in the DFIG block. The
protection is enabled through a binary output signal bypass, based on the measurement of
current, generator speed and voltage in the system. The signal is active for a minimum
period of time.

Modelling Alternatives
Alternatives to the modelling solutions previously oﬀered for power system analysis of
Type 3 WTs are presented in the following part. These alternatives refer both to the
overall structure of the generic model and/or to the speciﬁcation of some blocks. Most of
the solutions implemented in the literature and cited here are based on the IEC standard
released in 2015 [10][11][28].
3.3.1
Generic model in the IEC 64000-27-1
As introduced in Chapter 1, the IEC released the ﬁnal 64000-27-01 standard on wind
generation modelling in 2015. The generic models deﬁned were jointly developed by the
WECC and the IEC in the past decade. The solutions were based on the existing literature
and resulted from the cooperation with research groups and manufacturers [10].
The reference 64000-27-01 speciﬁes standard dynamic electrical simulation models for
wind power generation to be implemented in any simulation tool. It also classiﬁes the
wind turbine technologies in four main types (see Chapter 2). The models are useful
for the study of large disturbance on a grid, and for short-term stability and dynamics
analysis. They are not intended for long-term analysis or for the study of sub-synchronous

phenomena. Moreover, some physical eﬀects like wind turbulence, wind shear, wake or

According to the standard, Type 3 WTs, like Type 4, can be represented by a generic

• The aerodynamic system, which translates the energy extraction from the wind by

• The electrical equipment system, which account for the circuit breaker at the point

DIgSILENT PowerFactory
PowerFactory (PF) is an engineering power system analysis software meant for the analysis
of generation, transmission, distribution and industry in electrical power systems. It is
developed by DIgSILENT (DIgital SImuLation of Electrical NeTworks), a consulting
company which provides services in the ﬁeld of electrical power systems, particularly
regarding the simulation and grid integration of renewable energies.
The software provides a range of tools and functionalities dedicated to the control and
analysis of complex power systems, so as to achieve the users’ objectives of planning and
optimisation. To do so, PF oﬀers a large set of simulation functions like load-ﬂow analysis,
short-circuit calculations, harmonic, stability and model analysis, or protection coordination
[29]. In this project, two of these tools are mainly exploited. Load-ﬂow simulations are
27
==PAGE:48==
3. Wind Turbine Modelling for Power System Integration
used to calculate the steady-states of the wind turbines, while RMS simulations are run to
analyse the response of the system to short- and long-term events. The RMS tool allows to
perform electromechanical simulations of root mean square values where electromagnetic
transients are neglected. Short-term simulations (≤100 seconds) are run with a standard
computational time-step of 0.1 second. Longer simulations are performed with a larger
time-step of 0.5 seconds in order to limit the computational time required while maintaining
the robustness of the calculations.
As a software of power system modelling, PF proposes a large database of pre-deﬁned
(or built-in) models of standard components, like transformers, and templates of complex
units, like DFIG wind turbines. It also gives the possibility to create user-deﬁned models
from generic or empty blocks. The models created are structured in a speciﬁc system
modelling approach, from which the following main deﬁnitions are extracted:
• The Composite Frame is a schematic diagram in which diﬀerent models are
interconnected. Every slot of the frame is referenced in the associated Composite
Model database. The elements of the frame can be built-in models or common
user-deﬁned models.
• The Composite Block Deﬁnition is a schematic diagram of a model interconnecting
diﬀerent blocks. Like the Composite Frame, the blocks of the model can be built-in
or common user-deﬁned functions.
• The Common Model associated with a Composite Block Deﬁnition characterizes the
parameters of the blocks.
In this project, the existing reference model is modiﬁed at diﬀerent levels of the modelling
structure. The adjustment process requires to modify the parameters of the diﬀerent
Composite Blocks, and also to change the Composite Frame by adding, removing or
adapting some models and their interconnection.

The theory previously developed allows to build the hypothetical MPT curve of the V80

power curve of the V80 series. On another hand, it has been shown previously (Figure 4.6)
that the measured characteristics between Pout and ωgen are uncertain, especially because
of the 10 minutes averaging. Thus, the building process of the MPT has two objectives:
• Reproduce the dependency between wind speed and active power production described
by the generic power curve of the 2 MW brochure (Figure 2.4).
• Match, as much as possible, the dependency observed between generator speed and
active power production in the 10-minutes measurements.

Pre-pitching
As mentioned in Chapter 3, the power and speed controllers of a variable speed wind
turbine work in a complementary way to ensure the optimal operation of the system for all
wind speeds. In the modelling solution implemented here, the optimization of the power
in the partial-load operating region is realized by the MPT control. Then, the power
limitation is ensured by the speed controller leading the pitch angle servomechanism. Thus,
the objective of this next section is to accurately model the pitch angle of the 2 MW blades
in steady state, based on the information provided by the 10-minutes measurements.
As mentioned before, these measurements are averages. Since the pitch angle of the blades
is not linearly proportional to the wind speed, the relation between a 10 minutes average
pitch and 10 minutes average wind (or power) can be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from the real
steady state behaviour of the machine. Especially when the wind varies a lot during the 10
minutes period. As a consequence, it is not relevant to exploit the whole dataset displayed
in Figure 4.7 directly.
In order to limit the bias induced by the averaging of the data, it is chosen to study the
behaviour of the turbine only for periods when the average wind speed does not vary a lot.

completely limit the power output which keeps on increasing up to the rated level. In order

similar pre-pitching control loop in the existing model.

The three possible solutions are:
1. In the current modelling solution implemented, the pitch angle is controlled to
match the reference speed command ωref. Thus, the ﬁrst solution simply consists in
decreasing ωref to trigger the pitch controller earlier in the process. In this case, the

Then, the pitch angle is controlled to match both the smaller speed reference and
limit the power at the rated level. Here, the pre-pitching action is determined by
ωref and the parameters of the PI controller of the power loop.
3. The third solution oﬀers to actively vary the initial pitch angle command sent to the
servomechanism depending on the control signal, in this case the measured rotational
speed [31]. The objective is to slowly increase the real angle while the speed and
the power output increase, slightly before the rated limit. ωref is kept at the rated
level. In this case, the pre-pitching action is determined by the characteristic Kβ0
implemented between the input signal ωmeas and the initial pitch angle command β0.
Each solution is implemented in the current model in PF and simulated for the same wind
speed conditions: an linear ramp from the partial to the full-load operating region. Then,
the responses in active power output, generator speed and pitch angle are analysed to
evaluate the viability of each solution, and choose the most relevant one to eventually
implement.

and RMSE is given in Appendix B.4.

eﬃciency of the diﬀerent tested solutions. 13 new periods are chosen with the same criteria
on reliability and distribution of the points cited before to have a representative validation
dataset. The similarity between the tuning and the validation dataset is important for the

4.2.5
Reactive power capability
As stated in Section 4.1, the 10-minutes datasets provide measurements of the output
voltage and reactive power delivered by the three 2 MW WTs too. As a consequence, one
can also investigate the reactive power capability of the V80 machines erected in the Kalby
wind farm.
Deﬁnition & Motivation
A power generator is said to provide Reactive Power Capability (RPC) when it has the
ability to control its reactive power production or consumption. Type 3 and 4 wind turbines
have this ability due to their power electronics, which allow an independent control of
the active and reactive power output. Type 1 and 2 WTs can also provide RPC when
equipped with additional capacitors banks [18].
The amplitude of the reactive power capability of a generator depends on the voltage
and the active power experienced. Moreover, the production (and consumption) of Q in
a DFIG is limited by its technical properties: the rotor voltage for low rotational speed,
the rotor currents at higher speed, and the stator currents in general [32]. The largest
reactive power available is expected when the generator reaches synchronous regime, while
the lowest capability is expected in large sub-synchronous regime [33].
Controlling the reactive power ﬂow transmitted to the grid by a wind turbine is particularly
interesting when it comes to voltage support. Indeed, wind plants equipped with RPC can
help balancing the local bus voltage, and thus provide ancillary services to maintain safe
and suﬃcient electricity distribution throughout the grid [32][33]. The requirements for
RPC for a wind plant are agreed with the grid operators and regulated by the local grid
code. In Denmark, wind farms of category C (with a nominal capacity between 1.5 and
25 MW) are required to provide an independent control of their reactive power production,
with a minimum capability described by two P/Q and V/Q characteristics (see Appendix
B.7) [9].
Methodology
The reactive power output considered for the RPC can be deﬁned as the reactive power
measured directly after the generator (LV-side) or as the reactive power measured at the
turbine output, after the individual transformer (MV-side). That is why both LV-side and
MV-side signals are studied to determine the reactive power behavior of the 2 MW turbines.
The MV-side signals are estimated from the LV-side measurements of the 10-minutes
datasets, based on the simpliﬁed approach introduced in Appendix B.2.
In the current model, the RPC can be represented by a two-dimensional characteristic on
the voltage and the active power to take the double-dependency of the reactive power into
45

4. Methodology to Represent the 2 MW Turbines
account. Then, the characteristic delivers the reactive power set-point sent to the DFIG
controller. To build this Q vs. V/P curve, the relation between the measured voltage and
reactive power is studied for diﬀerent active power range. A resolution of 200 kW is chosen
in order to display enough points in each case, but also to consider the uncertainty of the
active power measurements induced by the averaging of the data. For each P, the speciﬁc
V/Q relation can be ﬁtted upon the selected points.
4.3
Power Capping Capability
Besides the normal operation mode, it has been seen in Chapter 2 and Section 4.1 that
the V80-2MW machines installed in the Kalby wind farm were also equipped with power
capping capability. Similarly to the modelling of the steady state in normal operations, it
is possible to use the 10-minutes datasets to represent the steady state of the turbines in
capping operations in the 2 MW model. In order to reproduce the principles deployed in
the real turbines, one ﬁrst need to understand how the machine behaves in this speciﬁc
conﬁguration, and confront the solution extracted with the theoretical solutions oﬀered in
the literature and in the reference model.
In the following section, the principle of power capping capability is ﬁrst deﬁned. Then,
strategies used to implement this type of control are examined from related works. The
methodology established to analyze the behavior of the actual 2 MW systems is presented
afterwards, along with the strategy implemented in the reference 6.3 MW model.
4.3.1
Deﬁnition & Motivation
In this report, the power capping capability of a wind turbine refers to the ability to limit
the active power production to a level below the nominal capacity. The WT operates as
usual when the power production is below the capping level, extracting the kinetic energy
of the wind in an optimal way.
Power capping capability can be deployed to reduce the loads stressing the WT structure
under speciﬁc climate conditions, such as turbulent winds. It is also usually used to limit
the power production of a wind farm in order to meet the electricity demand on the grid
[34].
Furthermore, power capping capability can be interesting when it comes to primary
frequency reserve. As introduced in Chapter 1, wind power plants have the potential to
participate in the grid regulation, and are sometimes required to by modern grid codes.
One aspect of this regulation is the balance of the frequency measured at diﬀerent points
in the grid. The power electronics implemented in Type 3 and 4 WTs allow to control
the active power production fed to the grid. Thus, these types of turbine are able to
provide frequency support through fast control actions like inertial control, droop control
or frequency primary reserve [7][8].
An electricity generator acting as primary reserve should be able to increase or decrease
its power production within the 30 seconds following a disturbance on the grid. To do
so, the production of a WF can be decreased by occasionally pitching the blades of the
turbines and reducing their aerodynamic eﬃciency. This way, a WT can provide reserve
for down-regulation. Nevertheless, providing up-regulation reserve requires to initially
46
==PAGE:67==
4. Methodology to Represent the 2 MW Turbines
operate in a derated mode, so as to be able to increase the power production when needed.
Power capping capability is then interesting to limit the maximum production of a WT
and provide primary reserve to support the grid frequency regulation [6][7].
The power limitation principle described here can be deﬁned as power capping, curtailment,
down-regulation or derating in the literature.
4.3.2
Power capping capability in the literature
Diﬀerent strategies developed to enable power capping capability in a variable-pitch
variable-speed WT have been detailed in the literature over the past 20 years. Some of
the methodologies examined are inspired by real industrial solutions. Some authors also
oﬀer new theoretical solutions which can improve the traditional ones. In every case, the
strategies aﬀect actively or passively the active power, generator rotational speed, pitch
angle and generator torque set points to cap the power production. The most relevant
propositions are brieﬂy presented thereafter.
According to [35] and [36], the standard strategy implemented in the wind industry consists
in derating the active power set point while increasing the rotational speed of the turbine
to its rated level. In this case, the reference power sent to the DFIG P Conv,ref
grid
is taken as
the minimum value between the capping command Pcap and the optimal power that could
be extracted from the wind in normal operation [37][38]. The choice is made at the MPT
control level using a two-dimensional look-up table. The new power command induces
a drop in the generator torque Tgen, which leads to a passive increase of the rotational
speed ω to operate at a smaller power coeﬃcient and re-balance the system. The speed
variation is limited afterwards by the pitch controller action to the speed reference ωrated.
As a result, this strategy implies a steep transition of the system between normal and
capping operations [35].
Similar strategies, inspired by the industry, control the rotational speed based on a wind
speed measurement so as to operate at a convenient non-optimal tip speed ratio λ and
reduce the power coeﬃcient CP [35]. Another theoretical solution proposes an active
control of ω through two controllers on β and Tgen, completed by an additional pitch
compensation loop. This last control level ensures that the pitch controller is enabled only
when the system is close to the rated regime, or when the variation of ω has no eﬀect
on the power extraction. Increasing the rotational speed of the turbine before pitching
the blades to reduce CP allows to convert a part of the redundant wind energy in kinetic
energy and to improve the inertial response of the machine [36].
A second common solution consists in derating the active power production below the
rated rotational speed. In this case, ω is set to a speciﬁc level related to the power capping
command. Diﬀerent relation between Pcap and ω have been deﬁned in the literature. In
[35], the pitch angle is controlled to maintained ω to the value reached in normal operation
and to reduce the power production at the capping level. This strategy induces larger
torques on the turbine shaft and larger pitch variations to limit the aerodynamic eﬃciency,
which can increase the risk of stall.
Other speciﬁc relationships between torque and rotational speed set-points inspired by
manufacturers are exposed in [34]. Each solution regulates more or less on Tgen and ω
while the reference of the pitch angle controller is adapted in each case. For example, a
47
==PAGE:68==
4. Methodology to Represent the 2 MW Turbines
solution oﬀered by General Electrics consists in increasing the rotational speed to a speciﬁc
level for power command below 75% of the rated power. Then, for larger commands, the
rotational speed is maintained at the rated level and only the pitch angle varies.
Finally, a third theoretical strategy consists in maintaining the tip speed ratio at the
optimal level. To do so, the rotational speed set-point is changed until the rated reference
is reached. At the same time, the pitch angle is increased to achieve the desired reduction
of the power coeﬃcient. In this solution, the wind speed is measured to calculate the ω
ensuring an optimal λ. Then, both rotational speed and pitch angle are controlled to meet
the desired power extraction. This solution is supposed to avoid the steep variation in the
rotational speed from normal to capping operations, and to help a quicker restoration of
the normal operations once the power reserve is launched [35][39].

In this chapter, the methodology developed to model the steady states of the 2 MW wind

sets of parameters are depicted for the ﬁrst and second solutions, in order to give a good

With the ﬁrst solution (in blue), the pitch angle starts to change for slower winds when

turbine. Thus, simply limiting the speed at a smaller value also limits the power output at
a lower value, providing power capping. As a result, the ﬁrst pre-pitching solution is not
viable.
With the same speed reference, the second solution equipped with an additional control
loop on power (in orange) allows a certain over-speeding margin, and then a larger power
output. The pitch controller is enabled for faster winds, once the control action of the
power loop can not compensate for the action of the speed loop. As a consequence, the
two control loops are in conﬂict: the speed PI loop wants to limit the speed (and so the
power through the MPT) while the power PI loop wants to increase the power to the rated
level. Thus, the behaviour of the system is not the one desired, and the second solution is
disregarded too.
The third solution (in green), which varies the initial pitch command, oﬀers a slow increase
of the angle after 10 m.s−1, once the minimum speed of the pre-pitching loop is reached.
In comparison with the previous solutions, the power curve is smoothed by the pitch
angle action in the transition between partial and full-load regime. The rated power is
eventually reached for faster wind speeds than before. Thus, the behaviour induced by the
Kβ0 characteristic implemented is the one desired.
Since only the third strategy appears to be a relevant method to model the pre-pitching
action of the V80 turbines, this solution is chosen and implemented in the ﬁnal 2 MW

Summary
In this chapter, the results of the process developed to model the steady states of the
2 MW turbines have been exposed step by step.
To begin with, the parameters needed to adjust the DFIG block of the existing model have
been calculated.
Secondly, the MPT curve of the model has been built through an iterative process. The ﬁnal
parameters of the curve oﬀer a compromise to accurately represent both the dependency
observed between Uwind and Pout in the reference power curve, and ω and Pout in the
10-minutes measurements. In the end, the performances of the MPT are limited by the
deﬁnition of the power coeﬃcient given in the hypothetical CP -Table.
Afterwards, the modelling of the pre-pitching action observed in the data required to
implement and tune an additional control loop in the pitch controller block to vary the
initial pitch angle of the system. The addition of the pre-pitching loop aﬀected the precision
of the simulated power curve while improving the shape of the pitch angle characteristic in
steady state. Once again, the ﬁnal solution has been chosen as a compromise between the
representation of the pre-pitching behavior and the accuracy of the simulated active power
output.
The next steps consisted in scaling the angular amplitudes of the blades in the characteristics
of the model to reduce the error made on the simulated pitch angle. The scaling process
developed allowed to improve the accuracy on the pitch angle, but at the cost of a worse
precision in the active power output.

6
COMPARISON OF POWER LIMITATION
METHODS
In this chapter, a comparative study is conducted between two methods of active power
limitation in wind turbines: Power Capping Capability (introduced in Chapter 4) and
Delta Power Control. The impact of each method will be analysed with the help of the
2 MW model previously built. In the end, the opportunities and drawbacks associated with
each solution are assessed from both grid and turbine perspective. To do so, Section 6.2
deﬁnes the principle of Delta Power Control. Then, Section 6.3 presents the methodology
of the comparative study, while Section 6.4 analyses the main outcomes. Finally, Section
6.5 discusses the results. But ﬁrst, Section 6.1 introduces the motivation behind this study.
6.1
Motivations
When it comes to the limitation of the active power production of a WT, the principle of
Delta Power Control is mostly cited in the literature. The theory behind this principle and
power capping capability have been examined in many ways. Though both methods oﬀer
to reduce wind-based power production, the two solutions have not often been considered
together. As a consequence, this chapter aims at understanding why each method would
be preferred by a farm or a grid operator to limit the power output of a WT. Besides, this
comparative study provides an example of the type of analysis which can be conducted
with the 2 MW model built during this project.
6.2
Delta Power Control
First of all, the principle of Delta Power Control is introduced and two solutions are
considered to be implemented in the 2 MW model.
6.2.1
Deﬁnition & Motivation
In the wind energy sector, Delta power control (or delta control in this report) refers to
the control of the active power production of a WT Pout at a proportion of the maximum
achievable production Pavail. This way, a share of the available power is always kept as
a reserved production which can be enabled by switching back to the normal operation
control. Two delta control modes can be considered [40][41]. The ﬁrst one, referred to
as absolute or constant delta control reduces the active power production by a constant
margin ∆P (see Equation (6.1)) [38][42]. The second one, referred to as proportional delta
control generates a share δ of the available production, as explained in Equation (6.2).
The latter is the most studied in the literature. A comparison between power capping and

Delta power control is interesting to reduce the loads on the structure in speciﬁc climate
conditions and to protect the WTs. It can also be used to reduce the power production
of a farm to track a speciﬁc power schedule and meet the consumption demand [42].
Nevertheless, delta control is mainly studied for the opportunity of primary or secondary
power reserve it provides, which can serve for frequency support [8], [39]-[43]. Indeed, as
already mentioned in Section 4.3.3 about power capping capability, derating the power
production of a WT allows to provide up-regulation to the grid. More recently, delta power
control has also been of interest for active wake control. In this case, the possibility to
derate the production of individual turbines is studied to optimize the wake eﬀect in a
wind farm and maximize the overall WF power production [44][45].
6.2.2
Solutions provided in the literature
Diﬀerent strategies have been explored in the past years for Type 3 WTs to provide delta
power control, particularly for a proportional control. The main solutions applied to
proportional delta control are presented in the ﬁrst place.
Similarly to power capping, reducing the power production of a turbine requires to reduce
its eﬃciency to extract the energy of the wind by aﬀecting its rotational speed and/or
the pitch angle of its blades. Thus, when the WT operates in the partial-load region, the
objective of the delta control is to work at a power coeﬃcient CP,δ proportional to the one
achieved in normal operation CP,NO (ideally CP,opt), as expressed in Equation (6.3).
Pδ = (1 −δ)Pavail

To do so, three main solutions can be distinguished.
• The ﬁrst solution consists in modifying the tip speed ratio λ of the machine in priority,
so as to operate at a non-optimal CP . In theory, this could be achieved by working
at a λ higher or lower than λopt, as shown in Figure 6.2 [38]. A lower λ implies a
smaller rotational speed ω but also the need to consume kinetic energy when the
turbine returns to normal operation. On the contrary, a higher λ implies a larger ω,
which induces more fatigue on the rotating shafts but allows to release kinetic energy
when the power reserve is released. Hence, increasing ω is always preferred in the
literature since it could provide a faster inertia response for frequency support [40].
The pitch angle might also be changed once the maximum rotational speed is achieved
[42][45]. In this case, the power command Pδ can no longer be deﬁned by the MPT,
and need an additional control loop, for example based on a wind speed measurement
[39]. However, the limitation on ω is not always considered [40][46]. Some solutions
use the same type of control on ω with an additional pitch oﬀset in order to increase
the power reserve, which is limited by the rotational speed range [43].
• The second solution aims at maintaining λ at the optimum level and only acts on
the pitch angle β. ω is controlled to reach λopt while β is controlled to achieve the

Implementation in the 2 MW model
Since proportional delta power control is more examined in the related works, only this
mode will be investigated in the following comparative study. To provide this type of
control, the third solution introduced above exploits the mechanical properties of the
blades and the rotor. Since these information are not available for the 2 MW WTs modelled
during this project, the third solution will not be considered. At the same time, reference
[45] states that both the ﬁrst and the second strategies can reduce the lifetime fatigue on
most of the components of the machine. Thus, the choice of the best strategy depends on
diﬀerent constraints like the model of the turbine, the environment or the speciﬁc need of
the operator. As a consequence, it has been chosen to implement both strategies in the
2 MW model for the comparison with power capping. From now on, the ﬁrst one will be
referred to as ∆Max-Ω, while the second one will be referred to as ∆Opt-Λ.

6.5
Discussion
The comparative study gives some understanding on the common and individual
opportunities and drawbacks oﬀered by power capping capability and delta power control
in terms of mechanical impacts on the turbine and electrical impact on the grid.
Mechanical impact on the structure
Delta power control helps to reduce the variations experimented by the pitch
servomechanism by operating at a larger β on average. It can also diminish the oscillations
in ω under turbulent wind climates, and so the oscillations on the rotating shafts. In
general, it divides the torques on these shafts while derating the active power production.
Nevertheless, the derating action also induces more variations in the turbine torque,
especially under turbulent winds.
Power capping capability can also reduce the variations experimented by the pitch
87
==PAGE:108==
6. Comparison of power limitation methods
servomechanism and the generator’s shaft when the capping command is low enough
(Pcap ≤\
Pavail). It also limits the torques on the rotating shafts in general. Like delta
control, the capping action induces more variations in Ttur. However, the strategy chosen
for power capping also implies more variations in ω at a higher average speed, which could
aﬀect more the rotating structure of the WT. Moreover, the variations in β can become
larger than usual when the capping command is above the mean production, especially
under highly varying wind climates.
Limitation of the active power output
By deﬁnition, delta power control divides the active power production of a WT for
every wind speed.
Thus, the power production still varies with the wind at a lower
amplitude. Nevertheless, the dynamic transients implied by the control action can induce
more oscillations in the power output for large δ command and compromise the stability
and the reliability of the power production at high wind speeds.
On the other side, power capping capability limits the active power production when
necessary. If Pcap is chosen above the mean power production, the capping action helps
to stabilize the power fed to the grid at a constant level.
But this stability might
be compromised by dynamic transient eﬀects at large Pcap, especially under turbulent
conditions. However, these eﬀects can be reduced by acting on the PI loop of the pitch
angle controller.
Creation of active power reserve
By dividing the available power production continuously (for all wind speeds), delta
power control provides active power reserve on the short- and long-term. Nevertheless,
the size of the reserve is bound to the wind variations, especially at low and medium
wind speeds where the production varies quickly. Power capping capability can provide
active power reserve only if Pcap is low enough. Hence, the size of the reserve depends on
the diﬀerence between Pcap and Pavail, which is highly subject to the wind variations. As
a result, enabling power capping capability creates a reliable power reserve on the short-
or long-term only under speciﬁc conﬁgurations.
Implementation
The solution implemented to enable power capping capability demanded only few
modiﬁcations to the existing 2 MW model. On the contrary, the implementation of delta
power control has proved to require additional control loops and adjusted characteristics
in the model. As a consequence, it appears that power capping is easier to implement
in a turbine control. Moreover, it is possible to improve the performances of the system
equipped with power capping by changing the pitch PI controller. On the contrary, the
strategy implemented for delta power control appears more complex to improve for the
studied 2 MW technology since it is not sensitive to the parameters of the turbine controller.

Limitations of the study
The conclusions drawn here are limited. Indeed, the resolution of the analysis conducted
for each scenario is limited because of the computation time of the simulations. Moreover,
only one strategy has been considered for each method, which bias the comparison. That
is why it would be interesting to complete the previous analysis with intermediate reserve
command α or intermediate wind speed scenarios, especially between MW and HW. It
would also be interesting to compare the two methods with other individual strategies,
following the examples found in the literature. This way, it would be possible to identify
the characteristics linked to the strategy implemented or linked to the power limitation
method. This analysis would help to complete or correct the conclusions made above.
Besides, the analysis conducted here only considers the turbine and grid perspectives.
Eventually, it would be interesting to cover other aspects of the implementation of power
limitation methods in WT, like the economic opportunities and impacts of each solution,
or the fatigue induced on the diﬀerent components of the structure.
6.6
Summary
The analysis conducted in this chapter allowed to compare the performances and impacts
of two active power limitation methods in a Type 3 WT for diﬀerent wind climate scenarios.
Power capping capability allows to limit the maximum power production of a WT, while
delta power control allows to divide the production continuously. The ﬁrst method has
been modelled with the solution deﬁned in the V80-2MW WTs of the Kalby WF for power
capping. The second method is based on a strategy which maintains the rotational speed
at the usual level and varies the pitch angle of the blades. Other strategies have been
examined from related works, and the strategy maximizing the rotational speed has been
implemented too. However, it has been disregarded for delta power control since it did not
comply with the requirements of the grid code for response delays.
Eventually, both power limitations methods allow to reduce the power production of a
WT and to reduce the torques on the shaft. On one hand, delta power control appears to
be more interesting to limit the loads on the structure under turbulent wind conditions,
to reduce continuously the active power fed to the grid or to create power reserve for
diﬀerent ancillary services. On the other hand, power capping appears more interesting to
limit the peak production of a WT and to reduce the ﬂuctuation of the power fed to the
grid. For wind speeds above the rated level, both methods can provide a similar response.
Nevertheless, the performances and drawbacks of each method depends on the dynamic
transients induced by the conﬁguration of the machine (PI parameters, reserve command).

CONCLUSION
This thesis focused on the construction of a dynamic model to characterize the 2 MW WTs
installed in Bornholm for power system integration analysis. The main goal was to model
the dynamic behaviour and the capping capability of real 2 MW machines using an existing
support and a set of measurements from the technology in Bornholm. Thus, the study was
based on an academic model of a 6.3 MW WT to represent the 3 V80-2MW turbines of
the Kalby wind farm. The modelling process was supported by four months of 10-minutes
average measurements available for each turbine, and the technical speciﬁcations of the
V80 series and the Aakirkeby substation. The 2 MW model was also used to compare two
active wind power limitations methods.
7.1
Results & Objectives
The project aimed at fulﬁlling the objectives listed in Section 1.5, which conclusions are
presented hereafter.
• To study the modelling of wind turbines for the simulation of dynamic
power systems
The two main modelling approaches used nowadays to represent Type 3 WTs have
been examined with the modelling description provided by the research teams of
Risø and the IEC Standard 64000-27-01. The hypothesis and principle supporting
each block have been studied to be able to identify the role of each part of the model
in the representation of the diﬀerent dynamic aspects of a WT, and to modify them
accordingly. Particularly, the representation of the turbine control developed in
the ﬁrst approach and implemented in the reference 6.3 MW model, consisting of a
speed-based pitch angle controller coupled with a MPT curve, coincides with the
Opti-Speed and Opti-Tip technologies implemented in the V80 series modelled in
this project.
• To adapt the existing 6.3 MW model to represent a real 2 MW wind
turbine
An existing model has been scaled to represent the speciﬁc technology of the three
V80-2MW WTs of the Kalby wind farm. The information gathered on this technology
from technical description and 10-minutes average measurements have been analysed,
confronted, and conﬁrmed or reviewed to support the adaptation process. Thus, the
behaviour of the real machines has been extracted from the data by selecting speciﬁc
time frames of study, or using a statistical approach. Additional assumptions have
been made from related works to ﬁnally modify some characteristics of the model
and some parts of its structure. Therefore, the generator characteristics, the MPT
curve and the amplitude of the pitch angle in the CP -Table have been adapted, while
the pitch controller has been equipped with an extra pre-pitching control logic. The

aim was to reproduce the expected steady states of the WTs in terms of active power,
rotational speed and pitch angle. The ﬁnal parameters have been set to provide a

rotational speed and below 0.5◦for the pitch angle at equivalent wind speeds on the

• To study power capping capability methods and to implement them in

10-minutes data have been analysed to extract the periods of power capping and to
understand the speciﬁc behaviour of the WTs in this conﬁguration. This way, the
strategy eﬀectively implemented have been identiﬁed. Then, the model have been
modiﬁed accordingly to provide the desired capping capability in the case observed,
without compromising the modelling of the normal operations. In the end, the 2 MW
model is equipped with a power capping capability maximising the rotational speed
at 17.5 RPM to limit the power production in less than 1 s.
• To analyse the grid and machine levels implications of an active power
limitation control using Power Capping Capability or Delta Power
Control
The results of two 2 MW models have been compared for the same wind climate
and power limitation scenarios. The delta power control solution has been extracted
from related works and chosen for its compliance with the grid requirements in terms
of response delay. Delta power control appears more interesting to decrease the
oscillations induced by the wind on the rotational speed and the pitch angle under
turbulent wind climates (up to -2% and -33% respectively in the case studied), while
power capping induces more variations in general. Besides, both models allow to limit
the power production of a WT, either by dividing the whole power or by capping the
maximum. However, dynamic transients aﬀect the ﬂuctuations of the output power
already induced by the wind. These ﬂuctuations can be signiﬁcantly decreased under
power capping with an adequate capping command and/or pitch controller (down
to 0.4% against 2.7% in normal operation, under the high wind speeds considered).
Regarding power reserve, delta power control allows to continuously save a part
of the generation for most of the climate conﬁgurations. On the contrary, power
capping can mostly provide short-term reserve under speciﬁc conditions.

C.1
Implementation of the ∆Max-ΩStrategy
This section explains how the characteristics of the control loops implemented in the ∆Max-
Ωstrategy have been built. First, the process developed to build the two-dimensional MPT
table is described step by step. Then the process developed to draw the two-dimensional
Pitch-To-Power table is presented. The two processes are summarized in Figure C.1.
MPT-Table
For each wind speed Uwind and delta command δ:
• (1) The power production PNO, pitch angle βNO and power coeﬃcient CP,NO
experienced in normal operation are extracted from the steady state characteristics.
• (2) The δ command determines the desired power coeﬃcient CP,δ and the new power
setpoint Pδ.
• (3) The CP -Table is interpolated to ﬁnd the rotational speed ωδ needed to achieve
CP,δ at βNO.
• (4.a) If ωδ is below ωrated, the new setpoint of the MPT curve associated with δ is
given by (Pδ, ωδ).
• (4.b) If ωδ is above ωrated, the new setpoint of the MPT curve associated with δ is
given by (Pωrated, ωδ).
Pitch-To-Power
For each wind speed Uwind and delta command δ:
• (1) The power production PNO and power coeﬃcient CP,NO experienced in normal
operation are extracted from the steady state characteristics.
• (2) The δ command determines the desired power coeﬃcient CP,δ and the new power
setpoint Pδ.
• (3) The CP -Table is interpolated to ﬁnd the pitch angle βδ needed to achieve CP,δ at
ωrated.
• (4) The setpoint of the curve associated with δ is given by (Pδ, βδ).

MPT-Table

C.3
Sensitive Parameters of the ∆Opt-Λ Strategy
This section presents a sensitivity analysis of the inertia of the turbine and the pitch angle
maximum rate of change on the response of the system to a δ command.
The inertia of a system translates how much it will react to a variation of one of its
parameter. All other properties equivalent, a system with a smaller inertia will be more
sensitive to the variation of a signal. It will react faster but will also oscillates more. In
the case of the ∆Opt-Λ strategy, a system with a smaller inertia will be longer to stabilize
at small δ because of larger oscillations. But it will be faster to stabilize at large δ because
it reacts more quickly in general.
