Abstract 
This Master thesis aims to explore the use of behavioral design theory in practice by 
comparing and discussing the differences and parallels between a theoretical point of 
view and a practical appliance through a literature study and a case study with the 
multinational company, Philips. 
The literature study covers a range of current, relevant literature regarding behavioral 
change supporting a design team in various ways in a behavioral design development 
process. Through an extensive analysis and discussion of the literature, it is concluded 
that a gap exists in behavioral design, entailing a lack of support of synthesis in the 
process. To explore the practical use of behavioral design theories, an investigation of a 
design team is conducted focusing on the team’s operational execution of product 
development projects involving behavioral design. This case study reveals three core 
challenges: Different mindsets and understandings of behavioral design within a design 
team, lack of setting specific behavioral design goals, and failure to reflect specifically 
on the use behavioral design. In an attempt to improve this situation, a support tool is 
suggested by the authors, the BD Retrospective, which aims to support a design team 
in reflecting on the use of behavioral design in product development projects. Thus, 
team members will have an opportunity to continuously improve their competencies 
within the field by means of this tool. To explore the value of such a reflection tool 
externally from the case study team, an initial qualitative evaluation of BD Retrospective 
is performed using ‘designerly actions’ as the assessment method for the three identified 
challenges. This evaluation shows promising results as regards to the effect of BD 
Retrospective on a design team’s ability to reflect on the use of behavioral design, work 
towards well defined behavioral design goals and continuously improve when it comes 
to using behavioral design in practice.

Learning Objectives 
 
A student successfully completing a Master Thesis project shall be able to: 
 
 
 
1. 
Collect and organize academic literature regarding current, relevant theory and 
methodology on behavioral design and synthesis. 
 
2. Compare and discuss theories in order to obtain greater insights into the field 
of behavioral design. 
 
3. Analyze and critically assess behavioral design theory in order to identify 
strengths and weaknesses within the various theories. 
 
4. Collect relevant information about behavioral design within a multinational 
firm and their current knowledge as well as analyze their needs and require-
ments for a support tool for behavioral change. 
 
5. Develop and propose a support tool incorporating the synthesis process for 
behavioral design projects that can be integrated into the context of a multi-
national firm. 
 
6. Discuss the difference between a theoretical point of view and a practical, hand-
on approach within the field of behavioral design. 
 
7. Evaluate and refine the support tool in collaboration with a multinational firm 
and within a relevant context. 
 
8. Communicate and document the project in a clearly understandable and struc-
tural manner in writing, visually and orally. 
 
9. Plan the overall project and its components using milestones, and modify this 
plan according to current project status.

1.1. Aim of the thesis 
In the last couple of decades there has been an increasing tendency of the appliance of 
psychological and socio-economical principles as well as development of persuasive 
technologies (Cash et al., 2017) (Hermsen et. al., 2015) (Wendel, 2014). This tendency 
can be seen through the widespread use of gamification, personalization of products 
and design for emotions (Fogg, 2009). As part of this tendency behavioral design has 
emerged, and it has since then expanded to various fields of design. Despite the field 
still being relatively new, an increasing amount of theoretical frameworks and models 
for behavioral design has emerged in recent years. These theories are developed by 
experienced designers, wanting to use their widespread knowledge on the topic to de-
scribe how psychological factors can be incorporated into the product design in order 
to influence the behavior of the user (Wendel, 2014) (Fogg, 2009). However, despite the 
increasing amount of academic literature on the topic these theoretical approaches to 
designing for behavior change are not operationalized (Cash et al., 2017), and thus, the 
practical appliance of behavioral design is still a significant challenge.  
Therefore, the aim of this thesis is to explore why applying behavioral design theory in 
practice is a challenge as well as determining the core roots of the challenge through a 
case company study. This is done in order to develop a tool that supports designers in 
the practical application of behavioral design in product development projects. 
1.2. Research aim and objectives 
Based on the aim of the thesis, the overall research aims can be formulated. 
 
Investigate the appliance of behavioral design from a theoretical point of view in order to 
determine the core challenges of a practical use in a case company, and finally to develop 
a tool, supporting a design team to a more coherent behavioral design process. 
 
To support the overall aim, the following research objectives for this thesis are 
identified: 
x 
Obtain a theoretical foundation of academic literature regarding current, rele-
vant theory and methodology on behavioral design. 
x 
Bring together a theoretical understanding and a practical case company 
investigation in order to map out key challenges for practical usage of 
behavioral design. 
x 
Develop a support tool based on the key challenges to assist designers in an 
improved behavioral design process. 
x 
Evaluate this tool in a fitting manner and discuss the implications and limita-
tions of the tool. 
 
For time plan (original as well as updated) see appendix 1. For original project 
description see appendix 2.

1.3. Structure of the thesis 
The thesis is structured into nine chapters presenting and discussing the progression of 
the work in a chronological order, and it is therefore intended to be read in this order. 
The purpose of each chapter is shortly described. 
Chapter 1 presents the aim of the thesis, the research questions and objectives as well as 
the structuring of the thesis. 
 
Chapter 2 explains the methodology used for each step of the process. 
 
Chapter 3 reviews and discusses current, relevant academic literature for behavioral 
design in order to create the theoretical foundation enabling a better understanding of

Chapter 4 investigates and analyzes current practice for a behavioral design process at 
the case company, Philips in Eindhoven, the Netherlands, in order to determine core

2.1. Introduction to Methodology 
The methodology used for the literature study, for collecting and analyzing the case 
company research and for the development and evaluation of a support tool will each 
be presented and explained in this chapter. 
 
For structuring and presenting the methodology used in this thesis the Design Research 
Methodology (DRM) by Blessing and Chakrabarti (2009) will be used. This describes 
the four stages of progress for the project, and the basic means and the main outcomes 
for each of these (figure 2.1).  
 
 
Figure 2.1: The four stages and content of the thesis as well as the basis means and outcomes. 
Adapted from Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2009. 
 
As defined by Blessing and Chakrabarti (2009), this project is a type 3 design research, 
meaning that the first three stages (literature study, case company research and the 
development) have been performed comprehensively, while the fourth stage (the 
evaluation) is performed only on an initial basis. 
 
The four stages are presented in figure 2.1, each stage corresponding to a chapter of the 
thesis. 
2.2. Stage 1 – Research Clarification: Literature 
Study 
In chapter 3 of this thesis, a literature study is conducted and the methodology behind 
this is presented below in the order of execution. 
 
==PAGE:16==
- 7 - 
Firstly, a large number of literature regarding the two overall areas of the study is ex-
plored in order to cover the Research Clarification. In this section the literature is re-
viewed, analyzed and discussed in order to determine the specific focus of the research 
in the following project work. 
 
In order to collect and organize a large amount of academic literature regarding current, 
relevant theory and methodology on behavioral design and synthesis, several 
approaches are used. Firstly, main keywords from the two fields (e.g. persuasive tech-
nology and reframing) are used as a starting point of the literature search as well as a 
way of structuring the collected literature. Additionally, the common method for sys-
tematic literature search, snowballing (Wohlin, 2014), is applied. Finally, the most 
prominent authors within the fields (such as Fogg, Dorst and Schön) as well as key 
design journals are used to guide the literature search. 
 
To review the academic literature the theories are compared and discussed across 
author and through similarities and differences (appendix 3: Structuring literature).  
The theory within the two areas of the literature is first approached on an overall level, 
and then the various theories are analyzed and critically assessed by identifying 
strengths and weaknesses within each field. To better facilitate this discussion the indi-
vidual theories are broken down into more general topics, which enables a comparison. 
Additionally, the academic literature is discussed by structuring it in various ways to 
obtain greater insights and a better overview. It is sorted in various ways, such as by 
level of abstraction and concreteness, by how easily it can be applied in a design process 
and by where in the design process it adds the most value. This approach to the litera-
ture study and these various ways of structuring and discussing the literature make it 
possible to obtain a deeper understanding of the various theories within each of the two 
fields of design as well as across. 
2.3. Stage 2 – Descriptive Study I: Case Study 
The second stage for the project is the first descriptive study, which involves a thorough 
investigation of the case company, as described in detail in chapter 4. This second stage 
of the DRM revolves around obtaining a better understanding of the current situation 
(Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2009). This involves empirical research of the case company, 
here presented as the method of data collection and method of analyzing the data. 
2.3.1. 
Method for collecting case company data 
The empirical data of the case company is collected in three steps; 1) initial research to 
create a foundation and basic understanding of the current situation, 2) a validation and 
discussion of the initial research, and lastly 3) a focused and solution-oriented discus-
sion. These three data collections occur separately, yet they each build onto each other, 
leading to a more and more concrete and in-depth knowledge on the core challenges 
for the case company in regards to a behavioral design process. Thus, the descriptive 
study evolve through the three types, exploratory study, descriptive study and explana-
tory study (Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2009). 
 
==PAGE:17==
- 8 - 
The initial research is done through a qualitative data collection (Blessing and 
Chakrabarti, 2009); nine interviews are conducted and documents supporting this are 
gathered. This qualitative approach is chosen as the aim is to get an in-depth under-
standing of the case company’s current situation including their knowledge and use of 
behavioral design. The interviews are skype-based and semi-structured (Drever, 1995). 
The people interviewed have various competencies and are in different ways related to 
behavioral design processes to get a more holistic perspective on their know-how of the 
topic. All interviews are between 30-60 minutes and follow an interview-guide created 
specifically for each interviewee on the basis of the previous interviews and already-
gathered insights (appendix 4: Case study interviews). 
 
Additional data collection is done through a three-hour review meeting with two central 
team members from the case company in order to get highly qualitative feedback and 
additional insights (section 4.3.3.). The results from the initial case company research, 
information and insights regarding the behavioral design, are visualized and presented 
(section 4.3.1.; appendix 6: Process map A) to facilitate a discussion and convey the 
authors’ understanding of the situation. This is done in order to ease communication 
and thus obtain additional insights into the case company’s needs and requirements for 
a support tool.  
 
Finally, a workshop is conducted (section 4.4.1.). This is structured as to give the best 
possible outcome through a three-stage process: Create comfort, stage a discussion and 
develop together (Onarheim and Cash, 2015). This structure enables the participants to 
get in the right mindset, focus on the core challenges and generate possible solutions 
for these. The participants are five members from the core team at the case company. 
These are chosen because they all have a very deep knowledge about behavioral design 
or have a particularly interesting view on the matter. Different materials to support and 
enable a better discussion are used, such as an (updated) visualization of the current 
situation including various quotes from the present participants as argumentation. This 
enables the participants to more easily understand and accept the presented situation 
based on the appertaining arguments. 
2.3.2. 
Method for analyzing the case company data 
The data gathered in the three data collections are analyzed in various ways and through 
an iterative process. As the case company research is highly qualitative, methods for 
examining these insights on a deeper level are applied in order to determine their cur-
rent knowledge as well as analyze their needs and requirements for a support tool for 
behavior change. 
 
For the initial data collection the method Affinity Diagram (also known as KJ Method) 
(Project-Management, 2017) is used; by structuring and grouping the information and 
quotes from each interview, common themes and relations appear, thus revealing a new 
layer of information. This is done both for each interview, in order to analyze them 
individually, and several times with all the qualitative data after all interviews are con-
ducted (appendix 5: Analysis of case study interviews). 
 
==PAGE:18==
- 9 - 
The visual representation of the case company’s behavioral design process (the process 
map; appendix 6: Process map A), created as part of the additional data collection, has 
several purposes. It is a method for structuring and analyzing the insights from the 
initial case company research, as well as discussing the current process both among the 
authors and with the case company team at the review meeting. Additionally, it is de-
veloped in order to conduct a Gap Analysis (Wirth, 2005) to determine where the cur-
rent behavioral design process is lacking and make suggestions to fill these gaps and 
thus facilitate a discussion on the challenges of the current practice.  
 
The method Thematic Analysis (Braun and Clarke, 2006) (similar to the Affinity 
Diagram) is applied on all the empirical data, across the various data collection methods 
(interviews, review meeting, workshop). As all the case company research is qualitative, 
this method of analysis is used in order to get a deeper understanding and identify the 
core challenges regarding the needs and requirements for a behavioral design support 
tool. Through the thematic analysis, common themes are determined. This is done by 
iteratively structuring, defining and discussing the data in a continuous process 
throughout the project as more insights appear and the themes become more clear. The 
themes and commonalities are reviewed several times by discussing the level of the 
challenges identified in order to correctly define the final themes. The result of this 
method is clearly framed themes, formulating an understanding of the core challenges 
identified throughout the case company study (section 4.5.). 
2.4. Stage 3 – Prescriptive Study: Development 
In the third stage the prescriptive study is executed. This entails the explorative research 
leading to the development of a support tool for reflection on behavioral design, de-
scribed in detail in section 5.2. Firstly, existing methods of reflection are reviewed and 
compared in order to evaluate how they match to the situation and context of the case 
company. 
 
Based on this a support tool is proposed. This tool incorporates elements from the litera-
ture study (both from the field of behavioral design as well as synthesis) and from the 
descriptive study of the case company. Because the tool is developed by synthesizing 
findings from the literature research and the empirical research, the solution space for 
a solution which fits both of these, ends up being very defined and thus quite small. 
This is a result of a very coherent synthesis process of combining an in-depth discussion 
of the literature with a comprehensive research study. From this, the tool is developed 
by incorporating various elements, identified from the explorative research of existing 
reflection tools. 
2.5. Stage 4 – Descriptive Study II: Evaluation  
For stage four, the second descriptive study, an evaluation of the support tool is exe-
cuted, described in chapter 6. This is an evaluation followed by suggestions for refine-
ments for the tool to support the case company and similar contexts. 
 
==PAGE:19==
- 10 - 
As mentioned, this fourth stage is only performed on an initial basis, as the support tool 
is assessed through an initial qualitative evaluation. An additional, more elaborate eval-
uation and quantitative test would thus be recommended for further work (section 8.1). 
 
The evaluation is conducted based on the empirical qualitative data of two workshops 
with design graduate students in a relevant context in order to assess how the tool 
supports a design team (elaborated in section 6.5) (appendix 14: Evaluation plan and 
appendix 15: Evaluation results). For this evaluation the themes extracted from the 
thematic analysis in the case company research are used as assessment method. These 
are concretized by being formulated into specific ‘designerly actions’, as done by Zahedi 
and Heaton (2017). Through the designerly actions it is possible to evaluate the test 
persons’ usage of the support tool in the context of a behavioral design project, and thus 
qualitatively evaluate the tool.

3.1. Introduction to Literature Study 
The first step towards the cause of why behavioral design is difficult to apply in practice 
will be presented in this chapter. The aim of this chapter is to achieve an in-depth 
understanding of the theory within the fields of behavioral design and synthesis. This 
will form a foundation of how the theory of behavioral design facilitates a practical 
approach in order to compare it to how a multinational firm actually incorporates 
behavioral design in practice (chapter 4). 
 
First, in section 3.2., the field of behavioral design will be explored in regards to what 
literature exists to achieve an in-depth understanding of the theory through analysis 
and discussion in order to figure out how the theory supports a design process. Next, in 
section 3.3., literature regarding synthesis will be presented and a range of different 
processes and methods will be discussed in regards to how they facilitate synthesis in 
different parts of a design process. Last, in section 3.4., the theory of the two fields will 
be compared in order to emphasize exactly how synthesis is incorporated into existing 
behavioral design theory to get a better understanding of why behavioral design is 
difficult to apply in practice. 
3.2. Behavioral Design 
This section will revolve around the design field, behavioral design. Firstly, there will be 
an example of how behavioral design has been applied followed by a brief introduction 
of the field. Secondly, a range of current, relevant behavioral design literature will be 
reviewed individually and afterwards analyzed through a comparison of similarities as 
well as differences. Finally, a discussion on how the reviewed literature contributes in a 
design process will be discussed through a comparison with the process of the Double 
Diamond Model (Design Council, 2005). 
The aim of this is to get an in-depth understanding of the field and investigate how the 
theory is covering a design process, thus supporting a designer.

3.2.1. 
Outlining behavioral design 
 
The Piano Staircase – A behavioral design example 
 
The Piano Staircase (The Fun Theory, 2009) is an interactive staircase, where each step 
resembles keys on a piano and plays the tune of the corresponding key when stepped on; in 
other words, it looks and sounds as if you are walking on a giant piano. This Piano Staircase 
was implemented in a subway station in Stockholm, Sweden in 2009 in an effort to see if ‘fun’ 
could improve the frequency of people taking the stairs. The overall purpose of this staircase 
was to improve public health with the idea that “Something as simple as ‘fun’ is the easiest 
way to change people’s behaviour for the better” (Design of the World, 2010).  This overall 
purpose is broken down into a much simpler and concrete goal: Making more people take the 
stairs by motivating them through ‘fun’ and gamification principles. And it worked! The Piano 
Staircase was a great success as 66% more people took the stairs in the observed period. As 
a result of this success the Piano Staircase has afterwards been implemented in many other 
cities all around the world in order to change people’s behavior. 
 
 
Figure 3.1: The Piano Staircase’s resemblance to a piano. Picture from Design of the World, 2010, 
http://www.designoftheworld.com/piano-stairs/ 
 
 
Behavioral design solutions, such as the one presented above (figure 3.1), are present in 
many aspects of our everyday lives. They are developed on the basis of various 
behavioral sciences, incorporating principles from persuasive technologies (Fogg, 
2002a), nudging (Tromp, 2013), behavioral economics (Dolan et al., 2012) (Wendel, 2012) 
and human psychology (Hermsen et al., 2014) (Cash et al., 2017). These design solutions 
can focus on behavior change from a social or governmental point of view (Tromp, 2013) 
(Dolan et al., 2012) or on changing habits for the individual (Fogg, 2007). This can be 
done by influencing the user in various ways, through their emotional responses or 
through their environment (Dolan et al., 2012) (Kahneman, 2011) (Eyal, 2014a), in order 
to guide the user through the stages of change towards developing a new habit 
(Prochaska and Velicer, 1997) (Eyal, 2014a). This thesis defines behavioral design as 
follows (Kristiansen and Angel, 2017) (Nielsen and Aakerlund, 2016):  
==PAGE:23==
- 14 - 
 
“A specific type of design where designers seek to develop products and/or 
systems that influence human behaviour through interventions” 
(Kristiansen and Angel, 2017, p.vi) (Nielsen and Aakerlund, 2016, p.vi)

thesis, and thus the practical appliance of the literature describing the behavioral design 
principles outlined above, will be reviewed and discussed in the following sections 3.2.2

3.2.2. 
Review of behavioral design theories 
The following sections will introduce and review current, relevant behavioral design 
literature in order to subsequently discuss the practical appliance of these in a design 
process. This literature review aims to explore different aspects within the field of 
behavioral design and to present these various perspectives and mindsets for 
approaching designing for change in a structured and understandable manner.  
 
The theories will each be reviewed in order to get a deep understanding of the spectrum 
of behavioral design literature. The theories will be divided into four categories 
according to how they support the designer in a behavioral design process. These 
categories are defined by the authors of the thesis as described in figure 3.2.  
 
 
Figure 3.2: The behavioral design literature is categorized into the four levels, framework, process, method and tool, 
according to how they support the designer. 
Framework 
A framework is defined as the foundation of behavioral design. It gives the designer a 
broad understanding of the underlying principles, which is necessary to execute a 
behavioral design process. 
 
Prochaska and Velicer (1997) introduce the Trans-Theoretical Model (TTM), which is 
a behavior change framework with a psychological point of view. It describes the six 
stages of change that individuals undergo (in several iterations) in the progress of 
establishing or changing a habit: Precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, 
maintenance, and relapse. The framework helps in understanding the similar influences 
(e.g. triggers, correct timing and types of interventions) that affect people in the same 
==PAGE:24==
- 15 - 
stage of change. Critically, it can be said that the framework is quite prescriptive and 
may not reflect the practical reality for all individuals. 
 
Based on TTM, Klein (et al., 2013) introduces the Computerized Behavior 
Intervention (COMBI) which is a holistic model using the framework of TTM as its 
foundation. It integrates many existing behavioral design theories describing the 
internal factors (such as cues, threats and severity) and external factors (e.g. barriers, 
skills and social norms) influencing the user for each step, as well as the relation 
between the factors. A limitation of the model is that it requires the designer to have a 
very in-depth understanding of the user’s internal and external influences. 
 
Wendel (2014) presents the Create Action Funnel as a way of understanding the user 
behavior in the book Designing for Behavior Change: Applying Psychology and 
Behavioural Economics. Developed in co-operation with Eyal, it describes the execution 
of an action through a series of mental ‘events’, or unconscious actions.  If the brain 
responds like intended the target action (or behavior) will occur. 
Firstly, a cue is needed, which will provoke an intuitive, automatic reaction, leading to 
a conscious evaluation of the costs and benefits, of the ability to perform the action, and 
the right timing, which all enable the execution of the action. 
It is interesting how Wendel describes this process as a funnel, which channels the 
action through these mental responses towards the user. The funnel might also leak; 
some users might drop out along the way due to distractions, lack of ability or urgency, 
causing unsuccessful behavior change. 
 
The three frameworks, TTM, COMBI and Create Action Funnel, all describe a process. 
However, this process is the process that the user undergoes when changing behavior, 
and not a design process. Therefore, these theories are sorted under the category 
“framework” as they support the designer in understanding the fundamental principles 
of behavior change. 
 
In Dual Process Theory Kahneman (2011) presents a framework for how the brain 
works and how one makes decisions. He describes two distinct operation systems in the 
brain, system 1 and system 2. The first is controlled by instantaneous emotional 
reactions on a subconscious level. The second system is rational, slow and conscious 
and where the deep analysis and reflections occur. The way in which Kahneman 
differentiates between the conscious and the unconscious mind has influenced many 
newer studies within behavioral design and is for instance also referred to as reflexive 
and reflective respectively (Hermsen et al., 2014) (Dolan et al., 2012). 
 
In The Functional Triad Fogg (2002a) explains how computing technology can be 
persuasive in three different ways: As a tool, a medium and a social actor. The framework 
describes how people respond to any kind of computing technology based on the three 
functions. 
 
The technology as a tool increases the user’s capability of doing certain things by making 
the activity easier or more efficient, like calculations, or doing things that otherwise 
==PAGE:25==
- 16 - 
would be impossible like comparing fingerprints to a database of thousands of 
fingerprints. 
As a medium the technology provides a way of communicating with the user either 
symbolic or sensory. Symbolic could be showing text or symbols to convey information, 
while sensory uses audio and video or even smell and touch sensation. 
The social actor comes into play as users in many ways treat a computer like a human 
being, which can be used persuasively towards the target behavior. This also implies 
that the persuasiveness differs from person to person. The more socializing the 
computer acts the greater it influences the user, which can both be positive and negative 
in regards to the person affected. 
 
A technology can have any or all of the three functions. They can all be persuasive and 
motivate the user towards a certain behavior. However, Fogg does not explicitly state 
how to implement these functions into software requirements and system features 
(Oinas-Kukkonen and Harjumaa, 2008). As this framework revolves around persuasive 
technologies it is therefore also limited to this field of behavioral design. 
 
In A Systematic Framework for Designing and Evaluating Persuasive Systems 
Oinas-Kukkonen and Harjumaa (2008) discuss persuasive design from a system and 
software development point of view. Based on a conceptual and an empirical analysis 
seven postulates are presented and discussed: 
1. 
Information technology is never neutral. 
2. People like their views about the world to be organized and consistent. 
3. Direct and indirect routes are key persuasion strategies. 
4. Persuasion is often incremental. 
5. Persuasion through persuasive systems should always be open. 
6. Persuasive systems should aim at unobtrusiveness. 
7. Persuasive systems should aim at being both useful and easy to use. 
 
These postulates are the underlying assumptions, they form the basis of the framework, 
and they are generically valid. Oinas-Kukkonen and Harjumaa state that all seven 
postulates need to be taken into consideration when either designing or evaluating 
persuasive design systems. 
 
Furthermore, Oinas-Kukkonen and Harjumaa state that in order to increase the 
persuasiveness of a computer-based information system it is essential to understand the 
system features and system requirements. This includes a detailed description of the 
functional requirements, the non-functional requirements and the constraints. The 
system features are categorized into four aspects that the system should provide support 
for: Primary task support is about providing support for the user’s primary task; 
Dialogue support regards the human-computer interaction that helps the user 
continuously moving towards the target behavior (this highly relates to Fogg’s 
definition on a social actor (Fogg, 2002a)); System credibility makes the system seem 
more credible and thus more persuasive; and Social support keeps the user motivated 
through the use of social influence. 
 
==PAGE:26==
- 17 - 
These system qualities or features are to be actively used when designing or evaluating 
the system. Therefore, Oinas-Kukkonen and Harjumaa provide a list of design 
principles including practical examples for each of the four aspects in order to ease the 
implementation of these into a persuasive design to achieve a higher level of 
persuasiveness for the system and thus a higher chance of reaching the target behavior. 
 
As this framework is primarily based on the work of Fogg, the same limitation applies; 
the focus is on software technology and the applicability of the theory is therefore 
limited. 
 
Nodder describes in his book, Evil by Design (2013), how the seven sins (pride, sloth, 
gluttony, anger, envy, lust and greed) can be used for persuasion and deception by 
influencing the user’s behavior possibly without them knowing it. By knowing these 
principles it will give the designer or user a better understanding of how design works 
in regards to persuasiveness. The framework shortly discusses the ethics in deceiving 
users and four ways this theory can be used. It can be used for evil, which only benefits 
the designer, for commercial purpose, which both benefits the designer and the user, 
for motivational purpose, which mainly benefits the user and finally for charitable 
purpose which mainly benefits society. It is also discussed why deception can be positive 
for the deceived. Engaging people emotionally rather than rationally is the most 
efficient way to persuade. 
The framework provides a great understanding of how these principles work with key 
notes on what to be aware of when developing products, but lack a more structured 
approach for how to use it in a design process. 
 
Tromp (2013) describes in the framework Social Design how applying design thinking 
to social issues can focus on two intentions or concerns: Individual concerns or collective 
concerns. Design often prioritizes the individual concerns rather than the societal, like 
a car providing a comfortable mean of travel for the individual, but will have negative 
consequences for the environment on a long term. Tromp argues that the designer 
should focus on how the gap between individual and collective concerns can be closed 
through development of interventions in order to benefit both. This should be done by 
reframing the problem in order to broaden the problem focus to a wider social context. 
As a part of the framework, the process Social Implication Design is developed (Tromp 
and Hekkert, 2016), which will be described further in the following process section. 
Furthermore, Tromp discusses how explicit and implicit design works and how these 
are used most efficiently in regards to what the intention is and how these are presented.  
 
In Influencing Behaviour: The Mindspace Way Dolan and colleagues (2012) discuss 
how to change the behavior of the individual through policies seen from an economical 
perspective. They start by highlighting the differences between contextual influences 
and cognitive influences as well as differentiating between the two operational systems 
in the brain, system 1 (the automatic, unconscious, fast mind) and system 2 (the 
reflective, slow, controlled mind), and then proceed to focusing on the contextual 
influences operating in system 1 (Kahneman, 2011). 
 
==PAGE:27==
- 18 - 
Mindspace is an acronym of the nine biggest effects on behavior; a list of the factors that 
needs to be considered in designing for behavior change (Dolan et al., 2012): 
1. 
Messenger. Depending on the perception of the messenger the information can 
be more or less persuasive. This includes factors such as authority, similarity, 
formality, feelings towards the messenger, consensus, consistency etc. 
2. Incentives. (Economic) incentives can be used as driver for behavioral change 
when you know how people respond, such as the fact that loss is perceived 
greater than the equivalent gain. 
3. Norms. Social norms regulate our behavior on a partly conscious, partly 
automatic level. 
4. Defaults. When a pre-set option is available, users often choose this option. It 
is therefore possible to influence user behavior towards the desired behavior 
without limiting the user’s free will. 
5. Salience. Attention is drawn to what we find novel, accessible, simple, relatable, 
what we can easily ‘encode’. 
6. Priming. Subconscious cues such as words or phrasing, objects, icons and smells 
influence behavior. 
7. Affect. Emotional responses happen instantaneously and can influence behavior 
indeliberately. 
8. Commitment. Setting a goal will influence commitment and increase the 
likelihood of sticking to this commitment, the changed behavior. Reciprocity is 
an important aspect of this. 
9. Ego. People behave in a way that supports a positive self-image. People like to 
be self-consistent, so if faced with a dilemma where their beliefs and behavior 
do not match, they are more likely to adjust their beliefs than their actions. 
 
The nine factors can be seen as a checklist to use when designing for behavior change 
in a social context, as the factors are described in detail and provide a good 
understanding of the concepts. However, how to practically apply this and how it 
integrates in the design process are not mentioned (Cash et al., 2017). 
Process 
A process is defined by a range of steps which the designer can follow during a design 
process. Four processes, based on the underlying principles of behavioral design, will be 
presented. 
 
Wendel (2014) has created the book, Designing for Behavior Change: Applying 
Psychology and Behavioural Economics which is a generic guide for designers to use 
when designing a behavior changing product within software technology. It is a process 
divided into four overall stages: Understand how decisions are made and what makes a 
behavior, discover your goal and the user’s goal, design the product around the behavior, 
and refine the product through the use of tests and measurements.  
 
Overall, the process aims to provide a comprehensive design guide for behavioral 
change based on a standard product development process. However, Wendels’ design 
process does not include the collection of user data and research. Wendel focuses 
==PAGE:28==
- 19 - 
instead on the analysis and understanding of the user behavior as well as the 
development and refinement of the solution, which he divides into the four stages.  
Wendel incorporates psychological and behavioral economical design principles and 
design thinking into product design, creating a wide-breadth introductory guide for 
designers (Eyal, 2014b). Process steps and common issues within the process are also 
described in detail. In particular, Wendel achieves to connect the last two stages of the 
process, Design and Refine, in a way that it becomes one fluent, iterative design 
development.  
The foundation for the book is a comprehensive literature study, which speaks to the 
quality of the guide, however, only a few concrete methods to support the detailed 
process steps are included (Cash et al., 2017). 
 
Tromp and Hekkert (2016) have developed the Social Implication Design Method 
(SID) which is part of Social Design (Tromp, 2013) and based on Vision in Product 
Design (Hekkert and van Dijk, 2011). The Social Implication Design process helps the 
designer understand the social impact of design on three layers (social realm, behavior 
and interaction) through five steps (phenomenon, social statement, behavior, strategy 
and design proposal). The idea behind the method is to focus on the collective concerns 
and through the steps figure out how a solution which focuses on individual concerns 
can benefit both. The final solution should be black boxed until the last step. 
 
Even though it is named as a method, SID is in this case defined as a process as it 
contains steps the designer should go through, but does not have defined specific 
actions on how to perform these steps. However, as these process steps are not 
described in more detail, the practical appliance of the process is limited. 
 
In Creating Persuasive Technologies: An Eight-Step Design Process Fogg (2009) 
presents his eight-step design process with the belief that the best way to change 
behavior with design is to start small and keep it simple. 
 
The first four steps are defining target behavior, audience, obstacles and channel. These 
four factors should be established before moving on. Fogg emphasizes the importance 
of not being too ambitious in regards to the target behavior; if the main goal is for people 
to exercise more it might be better to start with a smaller goal like “make 10 lunches 
while waiting for the bus”. Next is to find the right audience as well as through what 
channel the audience should be contacted. Again it is best to focus on the audience with 
the greatest chance of changing their behavior and preferably through a channel that 
they already are familiar with. Essential for defining the obstacles are the three factors 
motivation, ability and trigger that together form the behavior (Behavior = Motivation x 
Ability x Trigger) (Fogg, 2007). The first four steps can be taken in any order and one or 
more of the steps might be defined beforehand due to a given a certain project scope. 
 
The fifth step is about finding relevant examples where a similar intervention has 
worked before as there is no reason to use time and resources on developing completely 
new interventions. It is suggested to find at least three interventions which overcame 
each of the three obstacles. 
 
==PAGE:29==
- 20 - 
The sixth step is combine and imitate the previous examples to develop a new 
intervention. The seventh step runs on the principle of fail fast, succeed early and is 
about making small iterations by testing interventions until something works in 
practice. When the intervention works it is time to expand in the last step. This is done 
by expanding one of the first four steps, either by setting a new target behavior, focus 
on a more resistant audience or changing the channel. 
 
This process is based on Fogg’s own framework, The Functional Triad (Fogg, 2002a), 
and has the same limitations in regards to the technological computing aspect. The 
process is also limited as it is dependent on earlier examples where behavioral design 
has worked, but as behavioral design is a fairly new topic with few great examples it 
naturally restricts the process. 
 
In Behavioural Design: A Process for Integrating Behaviour Change and Design 
Cash (et. al., 2017) describes a gap between the traditional design process and behavioral 
design and thus creates the Behavioral Design process which enables a comparison and 
translation with other fields of design. The behavioral change model consists of three 
overall stages behaviour mapping, field work and intervention development and 
emphasizes an iterative development and user testing. It is developed on the basis of a 
comprehensive literature study, including behavior change theories such as Michie’s 
(2011) Behavioral Change Wheel and Kahneman’s (2011) Dual Process Theory. In the 
development of the process, Cash and colleagues also discuss several underlying factors 
in behavioral design based on a theoretical analysis, such as the overall process the user 
undergoes, whether the user is consciously or unconsciously affected as well as various 
other strategies. 
Method 
A method describes specific actions to support the designer during a behavioral design 
process. The six methods presented in this section range between having only few action 
steps to being more complex. 
 
The Hook Model created by Eyal (2014a) describes how products create habits by 
making the trigger for using that product an internal emotion, e.g. the reflex of logging 
into Facebook whenever you feel lonely or bored. The model takes the user through four 
steps that loop: Trigger, action, variable reward and investment.  
 
In order to start a behavior the user needs some kind of trigger that tells what to do 
next, which can both be external and internal. For external triggers it might be 
notifications or buttons that tell what the user should do while internal triggers mainly 
are based on emotions such as loneliness or boredom. Both could be triggers to e.g. 
open Facebook. Next comes the action which is defined as the simplest behavior in 
anticipation of a reward, like looking at the notification or simply scrolling on Facebook. 
The action will then lead to some kind of variable reward. It could be clearing the 
notification or finding something entertaining. Finally there is the investment which 
increases the likelihood of the user going through the loop again by storing value to the 
==PAGE:30==
- 21 - 
product. Investing time or money on a product will also increase the value of the 
product and the more times the user goes through the loop, the more value is stored. 
 
Ideally, after going through the loop (a number of times) the user will have internal and 
unconscious triggers which finally result in the desired habit. The model can also be 
used to understand and act upon existing habits. 
 
Developing an Intervention (Community Tool Box, 2017) is a method created for 
communities and organizations. It consists of thirteen concrete actions, each with sub-
questions and information on how to achieve the desired action. Focus is mainly on 
correctly framing the goal, identifying potential obstacles and specify how to test, adapt 
and measure the elements needed to successfully implement the intervention. 
 
This method differs from the remaining theories as it is derived from change 
management. It is interesting to see, despite this fact, how many similarities exist 
between this method and the previously-mentioned methods; the importance of 
understanding the underlying factors that influence how well the intervention or new 
behavior is received. Additionally, it is quite important to include it in the literature 
review as it contributes to a wider perspective on behavioral change. 
 
Behavioral Lenses (Media Lab Amsterdam, n.d.) is one of 57 methods within an overall 
design ‘toolkit’. It aims at achieving a better understanding of the users and the context 
and to get a direct and concrete relationship between the current and the target 
behavior. This is done through six actions, whereas one is to identify the target behavior 
within one (or more) of five lenses: Habits and impulses, Knowling and finding, Seeing 
and realizing, To want and to be able, and To do and to keep doing. The method helps 
focus the design through the use of the lenses, however, additional methods are 
required. 
 
Fogg and Hreha’s (2010) Behavior Wizard is a method for classifying types of behavior 
in a systematic way in order to match this with a relevant solution. The method is 
created on a large theoretical foundation, especially Fogg’s own research (2002b, 2009) 
forms the core basis. The method revolves around the Behavior Grid which is a 5x3 
matrix presenting five various ‘flavours’ of behavior and three durations of the target 
behavior. When the target behavior (and triggers) are identified various relevant 
material for the specific behavior is generated from the website BehaviorWizard.org, 
however, this database is no longer running. 
 
The Behavior Wizard’s best function is thus as a tool for classifying behaviors in order 
to help articulate the otherwise fuzzy terminology that exists within the field of 
behavioral design.  
 
In order to get a more holistic approach to behavioral design Michie (2011) has created 
the Behavioral Change Wheel (BCW) which is based on 19 different frameworks. The 
wheel consists of three sources of behavior, motivation, capability and availability, nine 
intervention functions and seven policy categories. Each of the sources of behavior is 
further divided into two categories. The intervention functions are the activities which 
==PAGE:31==
- 22 - 
change the behavior while the policies are the actions that enable or support the 
intervention activities. Besides from the wheel, the method also consists of a matrix 
which shows what interventions are ideal in regards to what sources of behavior and 
what policy categories are chosen. This in particular can support the designer during an 
ideation process. 
This new framework was also created in order to manage all the limitations from each 
theory that BCW covers. 
 
BCW is a holistic model, incorporating many theories of behavioral design, and as a 
result of this the designer is required to have a good underlying understanding of 
behavioral design in order to use it (Cash et al., 2017). 
 
In Persuasive by Design Hermsen (et al., 2014) introduces a behavior change model 
which is divided into three layers: A blue layer that divides the behavior into reflective 
or reflexive; a red layer which focuses on the threats of behavior change; and green layer 
which focuses on the social impact. Two additional intervention layers exist, the black 
and the purple, which display methods and strategies for reflective and the reflexive 
behavior, respectively. 
 
This method has been tested in design teams to improve it and make it as intuitive as 
possible, but one of the limitations is the complexity. It requires a great understanding 
of behavioral change in order to use it in practice (Hermsen et al., 2015). 
Tool 
A tool is defined by something tangible that can support the designer in performing a 
specific action, such as brainstorming. Three tools will be presented in this section. 
 
Lockton (et al., 2010) has developed Design with Intent which is a tool for two ways to 
go about behavioral design: Inspiration mode and prescriptive mode. In inspiration mode 
the starting point is six overall lenses that describe different ways of designing based on 
shared considerations, behavioral understanding or assumptions on how to best 
influence users. The prescriptive mode takes starting point in a brief/problem and 
consists of different target behaviors which each are linked to some of the lenses. These 
two modes describe two overall ways to approach behavior change, yet more actionable 
steps are absent. It is a tool to use when designing, and the inspiration mode in 
particular is primarily useful as part of an ideation as it serves to inspire new ideas.  
 
Brains, Behavior and Design (Cervantes et al., 2011) is a toolkit consisting of five tools 
for a behavioral design process: Reference cards presenting behavioral economics 
research findings; concept ecosystem poster visualizing the relationship between 
behavioral economics concepts; irrational situations guide with insights into the 
underlying psychological factors; 21 strategy cards for brainstorming; and a loss/gain 
worksheet to understand and design for trade-offs. 
Based on this toolkit Pfarr (2017) has developed the Behavior Change Strategy Cards, 
which are 23 cards for supporting design teams during brainstorming for behavior 
==PAGE:32==
- 23 - 
design solutions. The cards are divided into five different categories, Make it personal, 
Tip the scale, Craft the journey, Set up the options and Keep it simple.  
 
Both of these tools aim to assist the design team in brainstorming. The tools are 
therefore primarily useful as inspirational tools to use in the ideation phase. As a result 
of this, both the Brain, Behavior and Design cards and the Behavior Change Strategy 
Cards are limited to being an assistive tool, and additional tools are therefore required 
in the design process.

Discussion of behavioral design theories 
As the first part of the literature study, 22 theories have been presented and reviewed. 
In this section all of these theories will be discussed and analyzed in order to get a more 
in-depth knowledge and understanding of the field of behavioral design.  
 
This section will go through and compare the various perspectives and discuss them 
through similarities (or differences). The discussion will be across the four defined levels 
all the way from the high level frameworks to the more hands-on tools.

Stages 
By analyzing the behavioral design theories (frameworks, processes, methods and tools) 
some tendencies appear. One of these is the way behavior change is explained through 
the stages the user undergoes. These stages describe the process in which the target 
behavior is build up for the individual user. Four of these stage processes have been 
identified: Cash (et al., 2017) divides the overall process of implementing a behavior into 
three phases; Wendel (2014) introduces the sequential order of mental responses for an 
action in the Create Action Funnel; and both Eyal (2014a) and Prochaska and Velicer 
(1997) present models of the stages in which a habit is created in The Hook and TTM 
respectively. Also the model COMBI (Klein et al., 2013) incorporates this. 
 
These models for stages of change are all generic and can thus be applied in a wide sense 
in behavioral design projects. The relation between them are illustrated below (figure 
3.4). 
 
 
Figure 3.4: The relation between the behavioral design literature relating to stages of change 
Behavioral strategies 
Another tendency within the theories are the way in which many of them describe what 
a behavioral design solution must do and contain in order to increase the chances of 
successfully changing behavior. The authors defined these as behavioral strategies. 
 
Fogg (2002a) describes how persuasive technologies exist in three roles. This is 
important in the design process as the designer must include considerations on how the 
solution will function in all three roles (as a tool, medium and social actor). 
 
Other theories discuss the elements that must be present in the solution to increase 
persuasiveness: Oinas-Kukkonen and Harjumaa (2008) refer to these elements as 
system features and divide them into four categories (primary task support, dialogue 
support, system credibility and social support), and in the Mindspace Way (2012) Dolan 
and colleagues present the nine most influential factors to be incorporated into the 
solution when designing for behavior change (messenger, incentives, norms, defaults, 
salience, priming, affect, commitment and ego). 
The focus (or field of design in which they operate) of each of these theories and 
categorization of these influential design elements are quite different, as one focuses on 
software technology and the other on policy-making, respectively. However, despite 
this difference in focus both Oinas-Kukkonen & Harjumaa and Dolan et al., discuss 
these influential design elements and emphasize the importance of how the designer 
==PAGE:34==
- 25 - 
must utilize them in the design. It is thus interesting to see the similarities across two 
so different theories and fields of design. 
 
Klein and colleagues (2013) as well as Michie (2011) have created holistic models that 
incorporate additional aspects instead of simply these functions of what the design 
should contain. As these models are very comprehensive, their value lies within the way 
they are used throughout the span of the entire project. This, however, also limits the 
models as they thus have to be used throughout the entire project; e.g. you cannot 
simply use a holistic model for brainstorming as it requires that the designer has used 
it to analyze the data as well. As a result, these models require a high level of 
commitment as they cannot be used exclusively in one phase.  
Motivation, ability and trigger 
One of the elements that appear in many of the theories is the trinity of motivation, 
ability and trigger – the basic elements needed in order to change behavior. They are 
the core elements which form the foundation of behavioral analysis and development 
in many of Fogg’s theories (2002b; 2007; 2009), as well as Behavior Change Wheel 
(Michie, 2011), COMBI (Klein et al., 2013) and Wendel’s (2014) Create Action Funnel. 
However, this trinity often appears in various names. Fogg (2007) states that B=MAT, 
meaning that behavior is made up by motivation, ability and trigger; similarly, Michie 
(2011) defines the core of her Behavior Change Wheel as motivation, capability and 
opportunity, where opportunity can be understood as ‘how and when the user is 
reminded to perform an action’, thus a trigger. 
 
Klein and colleagues (2013) describe the core determinants for the behavior as 
awareness, motivation and commitment. These differ from Fogg and Michie but are still 
related as the COMBI model illustrates how the user’s awareness is determined by 
influencers such as cues, threats and severity, which highly relates to triggers. 
Commitment are described to be influenced by the user’s mood and coping strategies, 
which again can be said to relate to ‘ability’. 
 
The six steps in Create Action Funnel also revolve around the trinity, however, divided 
in a slightly different way (Wendel, 2014): The step ‘cue’ is directly linked to triggers. 
The two steps ‘evaluation’, where the user considers if the benefits counterbalance the 
costs, and ‘reaction’, where the user (consciously or unconsciously) thinks “do I want to 
do this?” together make up the user’s motivation. The step ‘ability’ directly relates to the 
user’s ability to perform the intended action. Where Wendel’s model differs the most is 
in regards to the step ‘timing’ as this influences both the motivation, the ability and the 
trigger, as these are not static but vary with time, e.g. the user can be more or less 
motivated to perform an action depending on the time when he/she is triggered to do 
so. 
 
Despite the difference in naming, the notion of a motivational factor, user capability 
and an internal or external timed cue is a recurring element within behavioral design 
theories. 
 
==PAGE:35==
- 26 - 
Several other authors mention the trigger for behavioral change as one of the key 
elements within behavioral design (Fogg, 2009) (Cash et al., 2017) (Eyal, 2014a). If a 
person has both the motivation and the ability to change but simply forgets to do so 
when a chance is provided, a well-timed trigger might be the only thing missing, but 
there is a lot to consider when designing successful triggers. 
 
Dolan (et al., 2012), Eyal (2014a) and Klein (et al., 2013) discuss triggers as being either 
external, meaning from the user’s environment and context e.g. a traffic sign or a 
notification, or internal and linked to emotions such as loneliness or boredom. Other 
authors like Tromp (2013), Cash (et al., 2017) and Wendel (2014) discuss the difference 
between triggers as implicit, where the user is not aware of the influence, and explicit, 
where the user is well aware of what triggers their behavior change. Cash (et al., 2017) 
argues that an implicit trigger is more effective than an explicit trigger and Tromp (2013) 
agrees. However, Tromp also mentions that it depends on the channel that is used, 
something that Fogg (2009) focuses on in the fourth step of his Eight-Step Process.  
 
Triggers can be perceived differently by the brain, and so Kahneman (2011), Hermsen 
(et al., 2014) and Dolan (et al., 2012) discuss how the brain processes the trigger input 
either reflexively, as an immediate reaction, or reflectively, where the response is 
conscious and processed more slowly and thought-through. As people react differently 
to triggers it can be argued that whether a trigger is explicit or implicit does not always 
depend on the trigger itself but on the person affected. 
 
A goal for behavioral design is to create new well-integrated habits but this takes time 
(Eyal, 2014a). First the user needs to be exposed to an external trigger, and when this 
happens the user is typically aware of the trigger and thinks about what action to take, 
thus the trigger is explicit and reflective, respectively. Through several iterations of 
influence (Prochaska and Velicer, 1997), the user might connect emotions to the trigger 
and henceforth the user will not recognize the trigger while acting instinctively making 
the trigger internal, implicit and reflexive. 
Individual or system perspective 
Most of the theories mention behavioral change in regards to individuals which makes 
sense as one design can influence different people in very different ways (Fogg, 2002a). 
Tromp (2013), however, discusses whether behavioral design can be used for system 
design in order to not only focus on what she refers to as individual concerns, but rather 
on collective concerns. Tromp also argues that in order to design towards collective 
concerns, it is necessary to somehow turn it into an individual concern and make the 
solution beneficial for both aspects (Tromp, 2013). 
 
Tromp (2013), Oinas-Kukkonen and Harjumaa (2008) as well as Fogg (2002a) discuss 
how social impact and personalization increase the influence towards the users 
regarding behavioral change. By putting the user into a social context and compare 
them to others as well as making the design more personal, the influence is increased. 
However, this can be positive or negative depending on the individual. This is also 
something to consider when designing for a system as factors such as personalization 
==PAGE:36==
- 27 - 
can make the interventions a lot more efficient towards the target behavior for some 
individuals, but might have the same amount of negative affect on others. 
Supporting the designer 
It is interesting to see how the theories can support the designer in understanding the 
user’s current behavior, how to influence that behavior or both. Overall you can say that 
the theories tend to have an analytical value, as they present a way of viewing and 
approaching the end user data in terms of categorizing it into ‘boxes’ in order to get a 
better understanding of the user’s behavior – or the theories tend to have value in a 
more practical manner by supporting the designer to explore relevant types of 
interventions in regards to what is needed in order to reach the target behavior. 
 
Different ways of supporting the development of interventions have been discussed. 
The fifth step in Fogg’s (2009) Eight-Step Process is about finding examples from 
previous projects where a similar behavior change has worked already, while Lockton 
and colleagues (2010) provide a list of examples in order to ease the design process, as 
designing new interventions from scratch is too extensive (Fogg, 2009). Lockton’s lenses 
are also introduced in order to make it easier for the designer to focus on behavioral 
design while developing. Different tools have been developed such as Behavioral Design 
Strategy Cards (Pfarr, 2017) and Behavioral Lenses (Media Lab Amsterdam, n.d.) in 
order to use behavioral design more directly while brainstorming. 
 
Furthermore, models like BCW strive to make the behavioral design theory more 
applicable in practice (Michie, 2011). Michie tries to inspire the designer when 
developing but in a more holistic way. This is done by linking the Behavior Change 
Wheel’s three layers together; sources of behavior, intervention functions and policy 
categories. Michie did this by developing the Behavioral Change Matrix. This matrix 
gives an overview of what kinds of intervention functions are best suited for curtain 
sources of behavior and what policy categories that can be used to support the chosen 
intervention. 
 
Fogg and Hreha (2010) developed the Behavioral Wizard to categorize different types of 
behavior changes, which is primarily valuable for the designer as a method for 
determining the type of target behavior. Practically, this could also make it easier for 
designers to  communicate and more explicitly discuss behavior change through a 
simple and precise terminology. 
 
Finally, Cash and colleagues (2017) cover many behavioral design theories in order to 
bridge the gap that exists between behavioral design and the traditional design process. 
In doing so he eases the application of the relatively new field of behavioral design for 
traditional designers. 
Contribution in the design process  
After an in-depth analysis of the behavioral design theories it will be discussed where in 
the design process each theory contributes from initial research to final refinement. To 
do so the Double Diamond Model (Design Council, 2005) is used, as this is a widespread 
==PAGE:37==
- 28 - 
and acknowledged representation of a design process with a clear division of stages. 
Firstly, the definition of the four stages (Design Council, n.d.2018) is stated in figure 3.5. 
 
 
Figure 3.5: The definition of the four phases in the Double Diamond Model. Adapted from DesignCouncil.org.uk, 2018 
 
To use the four stages of the Double Diamond specifically for a behavioral design 
process the following definitions are defined by the authors: 
1. 
Discover is solely gathering insights and collecting user data.  
2. Define is using the user data collected in the “discover” stage together with 
behavioral change theory to understand and describe the user’s current 
behavior as well as framing the overall design challenge. 
3. Develop is generating ideas by exploring the target behavior and ways of 
influencing the user through various behavioral design strategies. 
4. Deliver is finalizing the behavioral design solution up until the intervening. 
 
Where “define” ends and “develop” begins is difficult to sharply determine as the phases 
are very closely linked; the output of “define” ought to be the input to “develop”. As a 
result of this – although many of the theories aim to support the “develop” phase in 
development of behavioral strategies and constructing the target behavior – the theories 
can also support the “define” phase in framing the design challenge and defining the 
current behavior. 
 
As all 22 behavioral design-related theories are discussed in regards to their 
contribution and are placed into the Double Diamond model (illustrated in figure 3.6), 
three tendencies appear: 
 
Firstly, no behavioral design theory is explicitly used in the “discover” phase, which 
makes sense as behavioral design is not about data collection but rather about analysis 
and development. With that said, it is still seen as an advantage to have knowledge 
about behavioral design when gathering user insights. 
 
Secondly, several theories are placed in either “define” or “develop”. TTM (Prochaska & 
Velicer, 1997) and Dual Process Theory (Kahneman, 2011) are solely supporting the 
“define” stage as these describe the psychological process which people go through, and 
not how to use these in the context of designing interventions. Oppositely, many 
theories only cover the “develop” stage as these theories focus on inspiring ideas and 
developing behavioral design solutions (see figure 3.6). The authors of these theories 
==PAGE:38==
- 29 - 
therefore anticipate that the designer has the required knowledge about the user and 
the target behavior before commencing the development. 
 
Thirdly, many of the theories are placed in both “define” and “develop” (see figure 3.6). 
Even though most of the theories aim at supporting the designer in the Develop stage, 
in order to do so they need some kind of relation to the “define” stage as well, meaning 
that they contribute as a way of analyzing the behavior change as well as a way of 
developing and inspiring behavior change solutions. 
 
In addition to the three tendencies, some similarities and differences between the 
behavioral design-related theories are noteworthy in regards to their placement in the 
Double Diamond process and will now be discussed. 
 
Community Tool Box (2017) lies outside the Double Diamond model as it has a change 
management perspective on behavior change and interventions and therefore cannot 
be projected onto a design process. 
 
It is worth mentioning that Fogg, Tromp and Wendel all are covering both “define” and 
“develop” with two different theories but as each of their two theories are part of the 
same work they are put together as one theory in the Double Diamond. 
 
Fogg’s Functional Triad (2002a) together with his Eight-Step Process (2009) are 
targeting both phases as the Functional Triad supports defining the current situation 
while the Eight-Step Process focuses on the development of interventions. 
Even though the two theories are closely connected, these theories still lack a clear 
connection between the two phases, which makes it difficult to use in practice 
throughout a whole design process. The Behavioral Wizard, also developed by Fogg in 
collaboration with Hreha (2010), might have minimized the gap between the two 
phases, but as the database is no longer available it only assists in defining the current 
situation. 
 
Wendel’s (2014) Designing for Behavior Change, including the method Create Action 
Funnel, targets the whole design process except “discover”, as research is not specified. 
Wendel discusses how to understand the user and what makes up a behavior, thus 
supporting “define”, and how to develop a good design solution. Additionally, Wendel 
connects the “develop” and “deliver” phases very well as he describes how to iteratively 
and connectedly develop and refine the design solution. Despite the breadth of 
Wendel’s design process, there is a gap between “define” and “develop” as he does not 
specify the transition between the two phases. 
 
Cash’s (2017) work differs from many of the other behavioral design theories as it 
revolves around the gap between behavioral design and traditional product 
development. Cash is well-aware that the process of behavioral design does not cover 
the whole design process, and thus emphasizes that the process he developed ⎼ likewise 
other behavioral design processes ⎼ cannot stand alone. 
==PAGE:39==
- 30 - 
 
Figure 3.6: The distribution of the behavioral design literature in the four phases of the Double Diamond Model 
 
Tromp’s and Hekkert’s (2016) Social Implication Design Method, which is a part of the 
Social Design framework (Tromp, 2013), supports both “define” and “develop” but in 
contrast to the other theories, this theory includes reframing as a part of the process 
steps which makes this framework particularly interesting. The theory explores the 
initial problem by looking at it from different angles and thus uses reframing as a 
synthesis transition to make a connection between the “define” and “develop” phases. 
As a result of this, synthesis becomes a part of the framework which is rarely seen within 
behavioral design theory and in doing so the theory supports a more applicable 
approach through the whole design process without losing the designer along the way. 
 
Michie (2011) also tries to link the two phases together. The Behavior Change Wheel 
uses sources of behavior, intervention functions and policy categories which the 
designer can use to define. These three layers of the BCW can then be linked together 
using the Behavioral Change Matrix which can inspire the designer during ideation and 
development. Although Michie connects the two phases practically, the theory is still 
lacking to support synthesis as part of the method. It can be discussed whether the 
==PAGE:40==
- 31 - 
model makes synthesis easier for the designer as the BCW provides a way of structuring 
the research in a manner that could broaden the designer’s perception of the problem. 
 
Based on these findings it is highlighted that one of the main challenges within the 
process of behavioral design is the gap between the “define” phase and the “develop” 
phase as most of the theories does not include a transition between the two phases. The 
transition is seen not only by practically linking the two phases together but also by 
incorporating synthesis, and thus creating a more connected and holistic design 
process. Of all the theories that incorporates both of the phases only Tromp and 
Hekkert’s (2016) Social Implication Design Method includes synthesis as the bridge 
between “define” and “develop”. 
 
As synthesis is left out of most of the theories it is interesting to look at what exactly 
happens within this part of the process and how synthesis methods can support the 
design process within the field of behavior change (section 3.3.).

Based on the review and discussion of the 22 various theories related to 
the field of behavioral design in the previous sections (3.2.2. and 3.2.3.) 
the following findings can be concluded:

The authors of the theories have different perspectives on and

3.3. Synthesis 
Generically, the term synthesis describes the combination of discrete units into a 
cohesive entity which has greater value than the individual units accumulated (Oxford 
Dictionaries, 2017); thus, a way to combine different things or ideas in order to come up 
with new things and ideas. The word origins from greek, sunthesis (collection) and 
suntithenai (to put together) and is contrasted to analysis; in greek, analusis which 
means to unloose, to dissolve or resolve into elements (Chakrabarti, 2002). 
 
Different definitions and usages of synthesis in a design-related context will now be 
discussed in order to better cover and understand the term in section 3.3.1. before 
exploring various methods of incorporating synthesis into a design process in section 
3.3.2. 
3.3.1. 
Defining synthesis 
Chakrabarti (2002) defines synthesis in a design process as where a product moves from 
function to form. In this synthesis process the designer uses innovative abduction 
(Roozenburg, 1993). This is a creative process with reasoning based on innovative 
abduction. Even though different methodologies can be used during this process the 
result cannot be guaranteed logically. 
 
Chakrabarti discusses synthesis as having two possible uses in a design-related context: 
Synthesis as a phase in the design process, as defined by Archer (1965), or as a function 
for problem solving, defined by Hall (1962). 
 
Archer (1965) defines synthesis as a phase in a six-step design process where analysis 
and synthesis are the center of the process (figure 3.7). Preceding those steps are 
programming and data collection and following are development and communication. 
Even though the process is divided into a linear process consisting of these well-defined 
steps, each phase still overlaps and each step encourages the designer to evaluate on the 
early stages making the process more iterative. Yet, Archer also emphasises the 
importance of thoroughly covering the problem in the analysis before moving forward 
into synthesis. If the problem has been thoroughly explored before synthesis is begun, 
the better and more efficient the synthesis process will be and new innovative ideas can 
be generated more or less automatically. Separating the synthesis from the analysis 
should also reduce the risk of fixation when the designer is generating ideas. 
Chakrabarti (2002), however, concludes that it could be questionable how the different 
phases are identified and followed in a relatively linear process.  
 
==PAGE:43==
- 34 - 
 
Figure 3.7: Synthesis as a phase in a design process. Adapted from Archer, 1965 
 
Hall (1962) has another approach to analysis and synthesis. He describes synthesis as 
a function rather than a part of a linear process of steps. Instead of a phase which fits 
into a specific part of the process, synthesis can be used as a one of six problem-solving 
functions throughout the whole process. The six functions are: Problem definition, 
selecting objectives, system synthesis, system analysis, selecting the best system and 
communicating results. Synthesis, according to Hall, is described as “collecting, 
searching for, or inventing a set of ideas, alternatives, or options”. 
 
Synthesis is defined by Kolko (2010) as an “abductive sensemaking process”, where 
the designer organizes, manipulates, prunes and filters the research data in order to 
reveal a cohesion and make sense of the insights. He states that synthesis is made up by 
two elements: Sensemaking and abduction.  
The first is finding relations within a cluster of data, which will result in a higher value 
than the accumulated units of data. Sensemaking is about discovering relations between 
otherwise unrelated issues through the process of actively filtering the data and visually 
organizing it. Finding these relations should focus on exploring good or even just 
possible relations, in order to open up the data as wide as possible, and not about 
identifying the ‘right’ relations. 
Similar to Chakrabarti (2002), Kolko (2010) describes abductive reasoning as a core 
value of synthesis. It is the other half of synthesis according to Kolko, as this is how the 
designer arrives at a new (and improved) understanding of the research data. Abduction 
is derived from the two traditional ways of reasoning, deduction and induction. 
However, unlike these, abductive reasoning uses prior experiences and ‘common’ 
reasoning as the basis of the hypothesis and the facts are instead used suggestively. 
Abduction is therefore often explained as “inference to best explanation” (Douven, 2011). 
Thus, the designer reaches a conclusion by using previous experiences to formulate the 
most likely explanation about the research data (Kolko, 2010). 
 
Dorst (2011) agrees that abduction is a core element within productive thinking, 
particularly within design, as he differentiates between two types of abductive 
reasoning. He describes the two types through the terms the ‘what’, the ‘how’ and the 
‘value’ (figure 3.8). In the first type of abductive reasoning the ‘what’ is unknown, 
meaning that the product/service/system is still undetermined but the ‘how’, meaning 
==PAGE:44==
- 35 - 
the working principle used to reach the ‘value’, and the ‘value’ that we wish to create are 
known. This type of abduction can be seen in traditional problem solving. In the second 
type only the ‘value' is set. Because only the desired end goal is known, this type of 
abductive reasoning represents the complex and ‘open’ problems met within the field 
of design. 
Exactly because the second  type of abductive reasoning results in so complex problems, 
the development of a strategy to tackle these problems is essential. This strategy is 
known as ‘framing’ (Schön, 1984). Dorst defines framing as “the creation of a (novel) 
standpoint from which a problematic situation can be tackled” (Dorst, 2011) and 
describes framing as the key element in design abduction (Paton and Dorst, 2011) (Dorst, 
2015). The designer therefore needs to create a frame in order to approach and solve the 
design challenge at hand (Kolko, 2010).  
 
 
Figure 3.8: Two types of abductive reasoning. Adapted from Dorst, 2011 
 
Dorst and Cross (2001) discuss the co-evolution model (Maher et. al., 1996) to describe 
how designers increase their understanding of a problem while developing solutions, 
illustrated in figure 3.9. Nine experienced designers were observed in a development 
process to evaluate how they move between the problem space, P(t), and the solution 
space, S(t). Opposite Archer (1965), who argues that ideas generated during the analysis 
phase lead to fixation later in the process, Dorst and Cross encourage the designer to 
explore possible solutions early in the process and move between the two spaces – the 
problem space and the solution space – with constant iterations through analysis, 
synthesis and evaluation. The problem and the solution are thereby developed 
simultaneously. The goal is to end up with a matching problem-solution space to make 
sure that the right problem is solved with the right solution. Cross (1997) suggests that 
the creative process from problem to solution is created by building a ‘bridge’ between 
the two spaces rather than a ‘creative leap’. 
 
 
Figure 3.9: Co-evolution model. Adapted from Dorst and Cross, 2001 
 
Smulder, Dorst and Reymen (2009) build upon the co-evolution model by introducing 
co-evolution in a multi-actor design situation. This is done by exploring the model 
on a inter-subjective level (multiple people) rather than a intra-subjective level 
(individually) as Dorst and Cross (2001) discuss the model. Smulder and colleagues 
==PAGE:45==
- 36 - 
divide the model into two problem spaces and two solution spaces, as shown in figure 
3.10. The client has one perception of both the problem space and the solution space 
while the designer has another perception of the two spaces. By communication the two 
actors influence each other’s two spaces through the principles of co-evolution in order 
to end up with common understanding and perception of both the problem space as 
well as the solution space.  
 
 
Figure 3.10: Co-evolution model in a multi-actor design situation. Adapted from Smulders, Dorst and Reymen, 2009 
 
Summarily, synthesis supports the designer in exploring the problem, and asks ”why” in 
order to discover a broader and deeper understanding of the problem which will inspire 
ideas and open up for new possible solutions. This requires several iterations in order 
to do so but will result in a holistic, cohesive design process with a more thorough and 
complete solution. 
3.3.2. 
Facilitating synthesis 
The aim of this section is to explore various ways of facilitating synthesis in a design 
process. Similar for them all is their way of approaching the problem and the design 
process with a new perspective by relating research to design in order to open up the 
solution space and generate new, innovative, and holistic ideas (Kolko, 2010). 
 
Although synthesis for the designer appears to happen automatically and almost 
instantaneously, Kolko (2010) has formulated an overall synthesis framework that 
describes the process in a stepwise and simplified manner: Prioritizing, judging, and 
forging of connections (figure 3.11). The starting point of this process consists of two 
elements: The data the designer has gathered through design research, and the 
designer’s knowledge created through his personal experiences and perception. Firstly, 
the large quantity of research data collected through e.g. interviews with stakeholders 
and users as well as market trends are prioritized; it must be decided which information 
is more important and which information is less so, and thus hierarchically structure 
the data. Subsequently, all this data is judged and filtered as to whether the information 
is relevant for the project. All the information that the designer finds irrelevant in 
relation to the design problem is put aside. As the third step, the designer defines and 
forges connections between the remaining relevant data. To do so the designers have to 
use their prior experiences. In doing this, the designer uses abduction to explain and 
forge the new relations, and thus actively produces new knowledge. 
 
==PAGE:46==
- 37 - 
 
Figure 3.11: Synthesis framework. Adapted from Kolko, 2010 
 
A method for incorporating synthesis actively into the design process is through the use 
of concept maps. A concept map is a tool to visually organize and represent knowledge 
by linking elements and describing the relation between them (Kolko, 2009; 2010). This 
can be used by the designer to arrange and better understand the attained knowledge 
about the design problem and the user research; a graphic representation of how the 
designer perceives the design problem, which relates to mental models (Cash, 
Dekoninck and Ahmed-Kristensen, 2017) (Kolko, 2010). A mental model is an internal 
representation of how a designer perceives some sort of system, and this representation 
aims to explain and predict how the system behaves (Bossche et al., 2011) (Jonker et al., 
2011) (Liu and Stasko, 2010) (Johnson and O’Connor, 2008). A designer thus has a mental 
model of the design problem; a way he/she understands and approaches the problem, 
mentally arranges the user research and sees possible solutions. In particular, mental 
models are relevant in design teams in regards to having a shared mental model within 
the team, as this increases the overall performance of the team (Cash, Dekoninck and 
Ahmed-Kristensen, 2017) (Johnson and O’Connor, 2008) (Jonker et al., 2011). 
 
As concept maps provide the designer with a way of creating and visualizing his/hers 
perception of the frame, concept maps can thus support in facilitating an active 
reflection of the design problem and stage a discussion internally as well as between 
designers in a team. Through a discussion each person’s mental model can be altered, 
thus enabling synthesis. 
 
“A frame functions as a hypothesis about the connections among data” (Klein, Moon and 
Hoffman, 2006). Thus, it can be argued that a frame is a subjective perception of a 
certain situation, and in order to explore this situation reframing is necessary. The 
definition of reframing is “to change the way something is expressed or considered” 
(Cambridge English Dictionary, 2017). Reframing in a design context thus refers to a ‘re-
thinking’ of the frame of the design problem. Kolko (2010) discusses reframing as an 
activity on an individual level while Paton and Dorst (2011) discuss how it works between 
people. Paton and Dorst discuss how the creation of a brief and negotiation between 
designer and client leads to a common understanding of the frame which implies 
reframing is a part of the negotiation. In order to cover the definition of reframing in a 
design context, different ways of reframing will be discussed. 
 
Methodologically, reframing can be formulated into a prescriptive process for the 
designer to follow in order to actively create a new frame for the design challenge at 
hand. Kolko (2010) describes this in a stepwise reframing method. Firstly, the 
preliminary problem must be identified and formulated as the initial frame. Next, the 
designer should create alternative frames for the design opportunity by shifting 
perspective, e.g. viewing the frame from a new entity (the content of the frame), new 
context (the surroundings of the frame) or new embodiment (the shape of the frame). 
Based on this, the designer should focus on the user’s primary goal by extrapolate likely 
user goals for each new frame. This will make each possible frame seem more holistic 
==PAGE:47==
- 38 - 
and credible. Lastly, the design implications and constraints must be extrapolated, 
fitting the new frames and context. The new frames might create new implications or 
make existing, overlooked implications clear. By following these steps, the designer 
actively and explicitly reflects on the current frame and explores new perspectives and 
how this affects the context in order to achieve reframing. 
 
Another way to practice reframing is with Dorst’s (2015) Frame Creation (illustrated in 
figure 3.12). It is a nine-step process for development of system design which instead of 
focussing on the problem itself incorporates everything revolving around the problem 
by creating a frame around the problem. The broader and more complex the system 
boarder is, the greater the chance is of developing a more complete solution. The first 
steps of the process are about analysing the problem as well as the problem owner and 
from that figure out what makes the problem so difficult to solve. Next, all stakeholders 
which are closely related to the problem are investigated as well as their surroundings. 
Within this broader field underlying ‘themes’ should be identified, which is one of the 
core activities. These themes should then be linked to explore possible solutions which 
would benefit the various stakeholders within the frame. Finally, the implementation is 
planned and executed. 
 
 
Figure 3.12: The steps in the Frame Creation process. Adapted from Dorst, 2015 
 
Frame Creation explores the problem in depth and aims at creating solutions that will 
benefit not only the problem owner but all stakeholders surrounding the problem in 
order to make a more complete solution. This also makes the process very extensive and 
time consuming as everything has to be taken into account. Kolko (2010) describes 
synthesis with the steps ‘prioritize’, ‘judge’, ‘forge connections’ and ‘abduction’. By 
comparing these steps to Dorst’s Frame Creation some similarities are identified: As 
well as Kolko, Dorst describes a forging of connections by linking themes from the 
various stakeholders together, and both theories incorporate abduction by exploring 
solutions that can benefit all the stakeholders. However, Frame Creation differs from 
Kolko’s framework in regards to the two first steps, prioritize and judge. This 
emphasises just how comprehensive Frame Creation is, as the method does not narrow 
==PAGE:48==
- 39 - 
down the content of the problem and research but aim to grasp a solution that involves 
every stakeholder and aspect of the problem space. 
 
Vision in Product Design (ViP) by Hekkert and van Dijk (2011) is a context-driven 
product development approach (figure 3.13). The future vision of the product and the 
values and context of the current product are the starting point of the model. By 
deconstructing the product and envisioning the interaction between the user and the 
product and how the experience could be, ViP revolves around the context of the 
solution rather than the solution itself (Boeijen et al., 2014). 
 
 
Figure 3.13: Vision in Product Design process. Adapted from Boeijen et al., 2014 
 
The Vision in Product Design process focuses on constructing the vision and context of 
the product before developing the actual product. In doing so, it uses reframing, which 
is argued to be a way of incorporating synthesis. 
 
The model is limited by the importance of asking the right questions in order to fully 
deconstruct and envision the product and context. This requires the designer to have 
an extensive knowledge about the current product and tendencies within the market as 
well as being able to think abstractly (Boeijen et al., 2014). As the model does not focus 
on the product itself, it enables the model to be compatible with not only the ‘classic’ 
product development process but also more generally in supporting any kind of 
development process.  
 
A more hands-on synthesis method by Cross (2008) is the Morphological Chart 
method which practices product synthesis in four steps. The designer should first break 
down the product into features or functions and afterwards list various means on how 
to solve these features and functions. The designer can use existing means of achieving 
the function and come up with new ideas. After breaking the product down into these 
sub-solutions and structuring them in a manageable way, the synthesis step can begin 
by developing different solutions by combining one or more sub-solutions from each 
feature or function. Before doing this the designer should create different criteria for 
==PAGE:49==
- 40 - 
how to combine the sub-solutions as the total number of combinations might be too 
comprehensive. 
 
Similarly, Goldenberg and colleagues (2003) have created the method Systematic 
Inventive Thinking. Through a historical analysis of product development trends they 
created the method as a way of categorizing and generating new ideas. Focus is on the 
current product and deconstructing it into attributes and elements, and in doing this 
generating new ideas. Where Cross combines sub-solutions based on functionality, 
Goldenberg and colleagues present five specific methods for generating new possible 
solutions. In breaking down the product you take a more holistic perspective on why 
and how the product is a success. 
 
What Morphological Chart and Systematic Inventive Thinking have in common is the 
way they first deconstruct the solution in different ways in order to combine and 
generate new solutions. In the re-constructing process, synthesis is present (Dorst, 2011). 
Additionally, both methods have a very hands-on approach which makes them easy to 
use, but also requires that the designer knows ‘what’ the solution should do, which is 
not always the case within a design process (Dorst, 2011). They differ from the other 
synthesis methods, as they support synthesis after the problem has been defined and 
does not support reframing of the problem itself. 
 
Through this discussion of the synthesis models in this section it becomes evident that 
synthesis can be incorporated into a design process through a variation of different 
processes and methods. 
The co-evolution process supports synthesis both individually and as a team. Synthesis 
within a team can further be supported by the use of mental models to facilitate a 
discussion towards reframing. According the Hall (1962) synthesis can and should 
happen continuously throughout a design process. Frame Creation and ViP incorporate 
synthesis into the process when defining the problem, while methods like 
Morphological Chart and Systematic Inventive Thinking incorporate synthesis into the 
product development phase. Furthermore, synthesis can be incorporated within a 
broader scope – seen in Frame Creation that includes the surroundings of the problem 
– as well as in a more specific context – as e.g. Morphological Chart where synthesis is 
focused only around the product. 
3.3.3. 
Facilitating synthesis in the industry 
Even though there is a large and diverse amount of design synthesis theory – 
frameworks that describe the process of synthesis step by step, as well as hands-on 
methods, which in a structured way guide the designer through synthesis – some 
difficulties still appear when using it practically in the industry. Kolko discusses how 
design consultancies do not prioritize resources to synthesis activities and thus 
“synthesis happens casually or not at all” (Kolko, 2010). This is due to the fact that the 
process often is reflective, messy and difficult to see and measure and is therefore 
considered as a ‘waste of time’. Kolko also believes that design principles and methods 
can make the process more tangible which can help emphasise the importance and 
relevance of synthesis as it is actually both significantly useful and profitable when 
==PAGE:50==
- 41 - 
developing novel design. Daalhuizen (2014) emphasises the importance of design 
methods and how they support the designer through the process and makes sure the 
work done is value adding. Formulating synthesis into models might also ‘convince’ the 
design consultancies of the importance of facilitating synthesis because methods and 
activities are easier to set aside resources to (Kolko, 2010). 
 
3.3.4. 
Key findings from review and discussion of 
synthesis theories 
Based on a review and discussion of various literature revolving around 
the design term ‘synthesis’ in the previous sections (3.3.1.-3.3.3.) the 
following findigs can be concluded: 
x 
Synthesis is defined by a range of authors. They all agree that 
synthesis is an essential part of the design task, but differ in their 
understanding of how it relates to the design process. Some view 
synthesis as a particular phase in a linear design process preceding 
analysis, and others discuss how synthesis is a process developing 
both the problem and the solution simultaneously. An aspect which 
many of the authors agree on, is that synthesis involves abductive 
reasoning. 
 
x 
Various models have been reviewed, presenting methods for 
incorporating synthesis into the design process. These methods vary 
in how they support the designer in this activity, yet a common 
feature which can be seen across many of the models is the fact that 
they look at the problem in a different way in order to reframe it. 
 
x 
Additionally, it is important to operationalize synthesis into methods 
and models to ensure and support the implementation of synthesis 
activities into an industrial context. 
 
 
3.4. Comparing theories on behavioral design and 
synthesis 
Theories within the field of both behavioral design and synthesis have been reviewed 
and discussed separately. In continuation of the discussion in section 3.2.2., the 
behavioral design theories will now be discussed more in detail in regards to why they 
lack facilitating synthesis and whether some aspects of synthesis are present. 
 
As discussed previously (section 3.2.3.) most of the theories on behavioral design are 
lacking a way to support synthesis in the design process. This is mainly because the 
theories’ initial step is fixating the focus on a certain problem and target behavior as 
==PAGE:51==
- 42 - 
seen in e.g. BCW (Michie, 2011) and the Hook Model (Eyal, 2014a). After that, the 
problem is not considered or reframed further.  
 
Synthesis theories emphasize that iterations should be a part of the design process. This 
is particularly evident in the co-evolution model (Maher et. al., 1996). Even in Archer’s 
(1965) relatively linear process he argues that each step of the process evaluates the 
previous steps. Fogg’s (2009) Eight-Step Process also incorporates iterations, but this 
happens relatively late it the process and these iterations have no connection to the 
early steps in the process. Wendel (2014) incorporates iterations into his process model 
when moving from the development of the product to the refinement of it. Hall (1962) 
prescribes that synthesis should occur in every step of a design process, yet Wendel and 
Fogg only incorporate synthesis through iterations after the problem has been well-
defined, and not in the process of defining the problem itself. 
 
Another principle of synthesis is adding together smaller parts from the analysis in order 
to see the problem in a bigger picture. Klein and colleagues (2013) do this with their 
model, COMBI, by considering every element both internally and externally influencing 
behavior change (e.g. emotions, barriers, skills etc.). In that way the model is very 
comprehensive which also makes it complex. In regards to synthesis the COMBI model 
lacks evaluation and reflection of the initial problem which synthesis theory suggests to 
do through iterations. 
 
Kolko (2010) emphasizes the importance of actively taking a new perspective on the 
frame in order to achieve reframing. Nodder’s (2013) Evil by Design is differentiating 
from other theories by incorporating a more ethical point of view in relation to behavior, 
which could be used as an input to rethink the current frame by asking “what if the 
frame was seen from an ‘evil purpose’ viewpoint? How would this reframe the 
problem?”. Thus, it can be argued that Evil by Design could add value, as a way of 
incorporating synthesis, if it is applied as additional theory within a behavioral design 
process. 
 
The tools Brains, Behavior and Design (Cervantes et al., 2011) and Behavior Change 
Strategy Cards (Pfarr, 2017) can be related to the two synthesis methods, Morphology 
Chart (Cross, 2008) and Systematic Inventive Thinking (Goldenberg et al., 2003). All 
four of these are hands-on ways to actively support the designer (through objects such 
as cards, chart etc.) in an ideation phase. However, the two behavioral design tools differ 
from the synthesis methods as they merely inspire ideas on the basis of the analysis or 
support the refinement of an idea. They do not ‘deconstruct’ the idea/product and 
‘rebuild’ it into something new as the synthesis methods do. This is an important 
distinguishment as this is why these behavioral design tools do not facilitate synthesis; 
They do not include the re-construction process (Dorst, 2011). 
 
The behavioral design theories, Developing an Intervention and Social Implication 
Design both support synthesis as they incorporate the principle of Dorst’s (2015) Frame 
Creation; Developing an Intervention (Community Tool Box, 2017) relates to Frame 
Creation as it focuses on including several stakeholders and aims at exploring the 
‘problem’ (change) in a wide perspective. 
==PAGE:52==
- 43 - 
 
Similarly, Tromp and Hekkert’s (2016) process, SID, aims to solve individual concerns 
as well as collective concerns, and in doing so, it reflects the Frame Creation model by 
broadening the frame and including several stakeholders into the frame. 
The two processes are similar in the way that they both explore the context of the initial 
problem in order to get a broader understanding of how to solve it. This exploration is 
executed through reframing, which makes SID the only theory, covered in the review, 
that explicitly incorporates synthesis into the process.  
However, compared to Kolko’s (2010) four-step synthesis framework, SID – in contrast 
to Frame Creation – incorporates the two first steps of prioritizing and judging. These 
two steps narrow down the solution space, thus making the solution less holistic, but 
the process will also be less time consuming. Furthermore, SID and Frame Creation 
differ in terms of how reframing is incorporated into the process; While Frame Creation 
describes a very structured and linear process to reframing, the process of SID 
encourages the designer to move back- and forwards throughout the process creating a 
more non-linear and iterative process. 
 
 
==PAGE:53==
- 44 - 
3.5. Key findings from Literature Study 
Based on the discussion of the relation between behavioral design theory 
(reviewed and discussed in section 3.2.2.-3.2.3.) and synthesis theory 
(from section 3.3.1.-3.3.3.) the following fings can be concluded: 
x 
Many of the behavioral design theories are lacking the support of 
synthesis in the design process. Behavioral design theory tends to 
fixate the problem early on opposed to what is prescribed in the 
synthesis methods, which often use iterations to reconsider the initial 
problem. In other words, behavioral design theories build upon the 
analysis and add elements together, but lack the iteration of 
deconstructing and reevaluating the problem. When iterations are a 
part of behavioral design theories the iterations are often placed later 
in the process, only rethinking the solution rather than the problem 
itself. 
 
x 
Behavioral design theory does not in itself incorporate synthesis 
activities, and the theory of behavioral design cannot stand alone in 
a design process. As a result of this, it is clear that synthesis must be 
included more actively.

4.2. Introduction to the case company 
Philips is a multinational firm with departments in eight different countries spread 
around the world (Philips research, 2018). Philips is founded in Eindhoven in the 
Netherlands where the headquarter is located. This is also the location that has been 
researched and analyzed in the thesis as regards behavioral design. 
 
Philips has the user in the center of the design and has moved towards system driven 
and innovative design solutions where the products are an integrated part of the 
surroundings. To come up with the best ideas, a broad diversity of expertises are 
incorporated within each project in order to cover three different types of design: 
Design to innovate (focus on value for the user), Design to differentiate (products that 
differentiate compared to competition) and Design to optimize (reduce cost etc.). 
 
The department in Eindhoven includes three stakeholders: Philips Research, Philips 
Design and external business partners (see figure 4.2). The external business partners 
carry out market research and determine where to invest in projects. The funding is 
==PAGE:56==
- 47 - 
allocated to Philips Research and Philips Design to enable execution of projects. Philips 
Research are mainly focused on internal improvements while Philips Design are 
working with people research and product development. If Philips Research have 
discovered a potential beneficial investment they can either attempt to sell the idea to 
the external business partners to get funding, or use the internal budget to fund it. As 
Philips Design is ‘below’ Philips Research in the hierarchy, the only way Philips Design 
can get funding for projects which they find relevant is by selling the idea to either 
Philips Research or directly to the external business partners. This thesis will not further 
concern Philips Research.

Philips Design is divided into several different teams based on product functionality and 
people competencies: People Research, Interaction Design, Strategy Design, Product 
Design, amongst several others.  
 
Within Philips Design, a team named Persuasive Design Lab (PDL) also exists. This team 
consists of associates from different teams within Philips Design. The purpose of PDL is 
to join forces internally with the aim of developing competencies within behavioral 
design to make this discipline more practically applicable within the company. This 
team will be the focus of this thesis and the subject of the case study. 
The five team members of PDL and have been interviewed to get a better understanding 
of their process, communication and current knowledge on behavioral design. 
4.2.1. 
Persuasive Design Lab 
PDL was established around two years ago and is part of an effort to share knowledge 
and build upon each other’s work within behavioral design. 
The overall process of PDL is divided into two parallel tracks, running in yearly cycles. 
The first is the Exploration Track, investigating how to make behavioral design theory 
more practically applicable. This includes tasks such as development of a methodology 
and concrete guidelines based on theoretical studies, knowledge sharing, etc. The other 
is the Evaluation Track, where the members from PDL work as consultants in various 
product development projects within Philips Design involving behavioral change. This 
is done to support the projects, but just as well to gather knowledge about executing 
behavioral design projects and to gain experience for future projects. 
 
The situation at Philips is rather complex in terms of the structure of different 
departments and teams but also in the way they work, especially within PDL. All 
members of PDL are also assigned to other teams within Philips Design (figure 4.3) and 
==PAGE:57==
- 48 - 
work only part time within PDL (around two days per week). Thus, PDL members are 
working on projects within their primary teams but are also assigned to different 
projects related to PDL every month. 
 
 
Figure 4.3: Persuasive Design Lab consists of members from various teams within Philips Design 
 
At Philips Design, and also within PDL, the employees work in a Scrum mode of 
operation, which supports agile development of mainly software products (Scrum.org, 
2018). Each team works with 4-weeks sprints where the project tasks are prioritized and 
the work is focused according to the actual sprint. At the end of each sprint a Scrum 
Retrospective takes place, where learnings are presented and evaluated, and the team 
reflects on their work process and together discuss and plan what to do in the next 
sprint. 
 
Different tools are available during the projects, and dashboards are widely used within 
all projects. These work as an online database where all the knowledge and other 
information gathered during the project are collected. In order to gather information 
from the users, the team uses Communication as a Service (CaaS), which is a network 
and a database of relevant users who test ideas and give feedback as well as participate 
in online forums with the design team. 
 
The projects strive to build prototypes as early in the process as possible in order to start 
testing with users to get early feedback. In other words, they work after the principle of 
‘fail fast, succeed early’. 
 
Despite their common goal of developing competencies within behavioral design, the 
different team members of PDL rarely work together on the same project. 
Oftentimes, one or two members of PDL are drawn into a specific phase of a project as 
an expert on a specific task, such as end user research, ideation, UX design or user 
testing. The lead of the project decides if and when they are needed, often in the middle 
==PAGE:58==

project anywhere during the product development process and then only be a part of 
the project during a single sprint. Ideally, PDL would like to choose the projects in which 
they see potential to develop behavioral design in practise, but this is not a reality for 
the time being. 
4.2.2. 
Knowledge on behavioral design 
PDL has a catalogue of various behavioral design theories, laying the foundation of the 
behavioral change work. These range from psychological models (or psychosocial 
theories) to holistic models (which aim to incorporate and explain the underlying 
behavioral principles) to design models (which focus more on specific elements that 
should be incorporated into the product). This catalogue of behavioral design theory 
includes models such as the Hook model (Eyal, 2014a), TTM (Prochaska and Velicer, 
1997), BCW (Michie, 2011), Dual Process Theory (Kahneman, 2011), COMBI (Klein et al., 
2013) and Fogg’s (2007) Behavior Model (B=MAT), all of which are reviewed and 
discussed in the literature study (section 3.2.). The theories also vary in complexity, and 
the members of the PDL have in the interviews explicitly expressed a difference in 
opinion regarding the direct applicability of the various theories in the projects.  
 
Based on these and additional behavioral design theories, PDL has formulated eleven 
persuasive design principles, (figure 4.4), aiming to be more applicable and operational 
within projects than the theories themselves. The principles can be used as inspiration 
for ideation of new products in order to change the user behavior through the design. 
This, as well as experience within the field, is what makes up the team members’ 
foundation for the work in the Exploration and Evaluation tracks.

4.3.2. 
Analysis of the current situation 
The findings in the process map (figure 4.6 and appendix 6: Process map A) are presented 
and analyzed in this section by means of a gap analysis (section 2.3.2.). From this 
analysis, several insights into the process of behavioral design projects became clear. 
 
Several methods and tools are included in the process map, especially within the phases 
of “discover” and “frame”. However, not all of these relate directly to behavioral design 
theory. Instead they support more generic practices of a development project. 
 
In general, the behavioral design methods dominate the first phases of the process, but 
lack when the ideation process begins. The eleven persuasive strategies (section 4.2.2.) 
are placed in the beginning of the “ideate” phase, which is the last step in the process 
where behavioral design is present. Most of the methods and tools after this point are 
based on suggestions. The fact that PDL found it necessary to create these strategies 
emphasizes that they have difficulties using behavioral design during ideation. 
 
The comment “compare ideas with theory” (in the process map visualized as a thought 
bubble, placed between “make insights actionable” and “translate insights into ideas”) 
is an attempt to facilitate a discussion on whether a behavioral design dimension is 
missing in the remainder of the process. 
 
Seven templates have been developed by a member for the PDL team in an attempt to 
structure the process of how to implement behavioral design into the process. The 
templates themselves are placed in the method layer, while their titles are used as 
process steps. The fact that the title of the seven templates for behavioral design fit into 
the process level implies that they are rather general and not suited for methods – they 
do not specify any actions enough to be described as methods. The templates are also 
less detailed in terms of tasks to carry out within the ideation phase which further 
emphasises how the application of behavioral design during ideation is difficult in 
practice. It should be noted that the PDL member who developed the templates mainly 
works with people research, which might have influenced the content of the templates.  
 
All in all, this indicates that some kind of gap is present between the phases research 
and ideation. This gap is identified as “handover difficulties” and is represented in the 
process map as a red lightning and with a legend explaining the core problem. The 
theory of behavioral design has a different purpose when applied during research 
compared to when it is applied during ideation: Researchers seek a way of structuring 
the user data they collect into “boxes” while designers need the data to inspire ideas to 
design interventions that meet the actual user needs. Thus, different models are 
prefered by researchers compared to designers. These different perceptions of the 
purpose of the theory lead to different mindsets within the team. As a result of this, it 
is difficult for designers to use the data provided, because the researchers do not 
consider how to structure the data in a way that enables the designer to start generating 
ideas.

- 53 - 
The process “tagging and logging the projects” at the very end of the process (under 
“reflect”) speaks to the fact that PDL wants to have some sort of reflection on how the 
process went to enable future improvements based on historic learnings. However, 
since the database is no longer being updated, this has not been a successful way of 
doing it. 
4.3.3. 
Planning a review meeting 
The process map has been developed to get an overview of the case company’s current 
process. The process map is now to be reviewed with two PDL members from the case 
company during a 2,5-hour review meeting. There were several purposes with this 
review, all of which are presented in the process map (figure 4.6) in various ways, as the 
process map is meant to enable a constructive discussion at the review meeting. 
 
The process map is to be reviewed to verify its content. This is important to do because 
the process map reflects the authors’ perception of the process, and thus needs a 
verification by the team members to be accepted as being ‘correct’. 
 
In all the elements derived from the case company, it was specified where in the 
company the information came from (e.g. a specific interview or document) through 
references in the process map. These references were deliberately created quite small in 
size to keep the discussion objective and to keep the actual sources of information out 
of the discussion. 
 
Because the information from the case company was not sufficient to cover the whole 
process in the process map, suggestions have been added by the authors. These 
suggestions are intended to start a discussion on what the design team actually does. 
The comment “bubbles” are mainly added as clarifying components for the authors to 
obtain a better and more coherent understanding of the process. One of these bubbles 
is “handover difficulties”, defined by the authors as a ‘breakdown’, which was added to 
highlight the main challenge, which was found during the development of the process 
map (section 4.3.1.). 
4.3.4. 
Results of the review meeting 
A review meeting with two of the PDL members has been held (appendix 7: Review 
meeting notes). This resulted in several interesting findings which changed some of the 
key elements of how the authors perceived the process. The main takeaways from the 
review are that the process is much more complex and iterative than first expected, that 
the team members’ way of working in terms of their individual competencies are more 
flexible, and that they lack an overview of the process themselves. These will be 
discussed in this section. 
 
The overall process is in many ways different than first perceived. First of all, the process 
is much more complex and iterative. This is partly due to the use of Scrum (Scrum.org, 
2018) which incorporates 4-week sprints with a small review after each sprint, rather 
than overall milestones as depicted in the process map A (appendix 4). The focus of the 
==PAGE:63==
- 54 - 
discussion revolved around the process and method levels, and thus almost every 
element within these levels has been discussed. The other levels either got too abstract 
or too concrete to facilitate a valuable discussion. 
 
It was discovered that even though the various members of the PDL team have different 
competencies within fields like research and design, they are still involved in the process 
outside of their respective field; Researchers are a part of the ideation and development, 
while designers are a part of the research. The statement “compare the ideas with the 
behavioral design theory” turned out instead to be “compare the ideas with the 
research”, as the theory does not currently support this part of the process. Despite the 
fact that researchers and designers work closer together than first perceived, the gap 
between research and ideation was still confirmed as the translation of researched data 
into tangible design ideas is still identified as a great challenge. 
 
A great deal of the discussion in the review concerned the explanation of the various 
elements of the process map, in order for the two participants from the case company 
to get a better understanding of the process map. This was necessary as the team 
members were not aware of what elements were based on the information received from 
them and what was added as recommendations from the authors. Thus, a verification 
of the authors’ perception of the process for behavioral design project within the case 
company was limited. 
4.3.5. 
Discussion of the review meeting results 
Results of the review meeting, presented in the previous section 4.3.4., will now be 
discussed. The aim of this is to achieve a deeper understanding of some of the main 
challenges identified in the meeting by comparing the practical situation of the case 
company in relation to applying behavioral design in practice. 
  
Firstly, the gap between research and ideation was confirmed, despite the fact that it is 
a little different than first anticipated. Employees from research and from design are not 
as divided as first assumed. The transition between the two phases happens within one 
team and the mentioned “handover” is not simply handing over information from one 
person to another. Furthermore, the fact that they compare the ideas with the research 
implies that they move back and forth between research and ideation through 
iterations, meaning that the research data is still difficult to apply for inspiration in a 
design process. 
 
Several observations from the review confirmed that the process is much more complex 
than first assumed. Firstly, the team members explicitly explained that the process is 
much more iterative than depicted in the process map. Secondly, it is noteworthy how 
the team members acted throughout the review; the fact that they needed explanation 
for most of the elements, both the ones added based on the information from them as 
well as the suggested elements confirmed the complexity and lack of simplicity of the

5.1. Introduction to Development 
The literature study in chapter 3 identified a gap in the theoretical approach of 
behavioral design. This gap was confirmed from a practical perspective in the previous 
chapter, 4, through a case study, involving a range of interviews, a review meeting and 
a workshop. All of this resulted in an identification of the need for a way for the design 
team to reflect on the appliance of behavioral design in development projects as well as 
their own competencies within the field. This will be discussed further in chapter 7. 
 
Through a thematic analysis of the investigated design team in the case company, three 
main themes have been identified (in section 4.5.) as the primary challenges towards an 
improved use of behavioral design in practice. The themes are defined as “having 
different mindsets and understanding”, “lack of goal setting” and “lack of reflection” 
regarding behavioral design, and a support tool for reflection should aim to solve these 
challenges. Therefore, this chapter will revolve around the development of a tool for 
reflecting on behavioral design in the context of the case company.  
 
Firstly, in section 5.2., the case company’s current way of reflection (in continuation of 
chapter 4) will be further discussed along with an alternative reflection tool in order to 
achieve a basic understanding of the elements required for a reflection tool in the 
context of the case company. Additionally, it will be discussed, in section 5.3., how such 
a tool can facilitate a reflection specifically on behavioral design. On the basis of this 
the support tool, BD Retrospective, will be developed and described in detail in section 
5.5., in regards to content as well as context. 
5.2. Tools for reflection 
Reflection is found to be the solution for the case company in order to improve their 
ability to apply behavioral design in practice. This section will discuss (in continuation 
of section 4.2.1.) how the design team at the case company is currently reflecting through 
the Scrum Retrospective. Furthermore, the 4Ls Retrospective will be presented as a 
reflection tool with a more personal development focus (see figure 5.1). The aim is to 
achieve a basic understanding of the key elements for reflections, thus the investigation 
of reflection tools will be limited to these two tools. 
 
 
Figure 5.1: Left – the Scrum Retrospective. Right – the 4Ls Retrospective. Adapted from GroupMap.com, 2017 
 
The case company is currently applying Scrum Retrospective as a reflection tool, 
which is one of the most widely used reflection tools (Mahnic & Vrana, 2007). It is 
integrated as the last step in each of the 4-week sprints and is used to reflect back on 
==PAGE:72==
- 63 - 
the last sprint. It is done by discussing the four categories, “What went well?, What 
could’ve gone better?, What do we want to try next? and What puzzles us?”. The 
purpose of the Scrum Retrospective is to reflect back on how the last sprint went both 
in regards to how the team collaborated as well as how the process ran. Based on those 
reflections a plan for the next sprint is created and improvements can be implemented 
for how the Scrum Team can improve the development process (GroupMap, Scrum 
Retrospective, 2017) (Devendra, 2014) (Sutherland and Schwaber, 2017). 
 
4Ls Retrospective has the same goal as the Scrum Retrospective in regards to as a 
group agreeing on how to improve the current situation using four areas. For this tool 
the areas are “Liked, Learned, Lacked and Longed for”. Through more personal 
questions, it aims to improve the team’s attitude and drivers. One of the advantages of 
the 4Ls Retrospective is the simplicity which enables it to be applied in various kinds of 
retrospectives for reviewing personal performance (Retrium, 2017) (Pat Kua, 2015). The 
two methods differ on the type of subject of reflection: Where the Scrum Retrospective 
aims at improving the process on a more general level, the 4Ls Retrospective aims at 
improving the team as a unit and the individuals within the team on a more personal 
level (GroupMap, 4Ls Retrospective, 2017). These two retrospectives are widely used and 
exist in many variations, but the principles are the same. 
 
Many similarities exist between the Scrum Retrospective and the 4Ls Retrospective. 
One of these is the structure of dividing the tool into four areas. This structure is widely 
used and can be seen in various other tools and templates, e.g. the SWOT Analysis (Hill 
& Westbrook, 1997). It is worth noticing that the same structure exists in these quite 
different types of tools. Based on this, it is argued that a development of a new reflection 
tool should be structured in a similar way as these tools. 
 
The tools, which is used during a retrospective, is supported by a Scrum Master who 
functions as a facilitator (Sutherland & Schwaber, 2017). The facilitator is an important 
role and has the responsibility of managing the activities and the team dynamics during 
a retrospective (Derby, Larsen, & Schwaber, 2006).  
The facilitator is making sure that the right questions are answered in the right order. 
First, data should be gathered, both facts and feelings, then insights are generated and 
lastly it is decided on what to do next. 
It is important that everyone is heard during a retrospective, to make sure that a 
dominant person does not control the discussion. It is the facilitator’s responsibility to 
reach out to the introvert people. 
5.2.1. 
Choosing a suitable reflection tool for the case company 
A basic knowledge about reflection using the Scrum Retrospective and the 4Ls 
Retrospective is obtained, and this knowledge is now to be compared to how a reflection 
tool will suit the context of the case company the best. It should be considered that the 
overall goal of PDL is for the team to develop general approaches on how to use 
behavioral design in practice in all of Philips.  
==PAGE:73==
- 64 - 
The aim of this section is to use the obtained knowledge from the two reflection tools 
to identify key elements to implement in the BD Retrospective in order to specifically 
reflect on behavioral design. 
 
As the overall goal for the case company team is to create a behavioral design practice 
which can be used in all of Philips (described in section 4.2.), it will be beneficial if their 
findings from the reflection could be easily shared. As the Scrum Retrospective focuses 
on the process itself rather than personal development, as the 4Ls Retrospective does, 
it will be more suited for the context of the case company. Scrum Retrospective is 
already being used, thus it will also be easier to implement into their current reflection, 
if the new tool is based on the same principles. 
 
The reflection should increase the team members’ personal experience with applying 
behavioral design. It can be argued that the 4Ls Retrospective is more suited when 
working with behavioral design as personal experience might be more valuable for the 
designers, than a generic approach, as they learn how behavioral design works for them, 
individually. Thus, the tool should include some personal reflection. However, this 
should not be the foundation of the reflection as it will make it more difficult to share 
their experience with others, as the knowledge is based on personal preferences. 
Additionally, as the members of PDL work separately, it will be difficult to work towards 
a common process if they mainly focus on their own preferences. 
5.3. Reflection on behavioral design 
The key elements of reflection in regards to the case company have been established in 
the previous section, 5.2.1. Now it will be discussed how behavioral design is to be 
implemented into the BD Retrospective, based on the literature study (chapter 3) and 
the case study (chapter 4). 
The aim of this section is to define the key elements to be implemented in the 
development of the tool in regards to focusing the reflection on specifically behavioral 
design. 
 
A variation of key elements within the tool is needed in order to facilitate a retrospective 
on behavioral design. First and foremost, the reflection tool should facilitate a 
continuous personal development within the field of behavioral design. The main 
challenge for the team is how to apply behavioral design in practice. As the field of 
behavioral design is rather complex and still a relatively new field (Hermsen et al., 2014), 
it will be relevant to continuously explore the theory. Thus, the tool should incorporate 
an element ensuring that the team reflects on what interests them, to make sure they 
explore the field to expand their current knowledge and develop personal competencies. 
 
The reflection tool should increase the team members’ ability to focus the reflection on 
behavioral design specifically while navigating in the relatively complex field of 
behavioral design. It became clear in the workshop (see section 4.4.2.) that it was the 
ability to reflect, which was the main challenge for the team. They have the motivation 
to develop more competencies on behavioral design through reflection and because 
==PAGE:74==
- 65 - 
they already use sprints and retrospective reflection through the Scrum approach, a 
trigger for reflecting is also present. 
The increased ability to reflect should be incorporated into the tool by dividing the field 
of behavioral design into specific topics to reflect on, and by making those topics visible 
in the tool. This will ensure that the reflection revolves around behavioral design as well 
as making it easier to grasp. 
 
The reflection tool should facilitate a discussion towards a shared understanding 
despite different preferences within the team. Currently the team members at the case 
company have different understandings and perceptions of how to use behavioral 
design: The researchers prefer holistic models like BCW (Michie, 2011) and COMBI 
(Klein et al., 2013) while the designers prefer B=MAT (Fogg, 2007) and the Hook Model 
(Eyal, 2014a).  
This should be considered when implementing behavioral design topics into the 
reflection tool. The topics should therefore represent the different aspects of behavioral 
design, without favoring specific theories. 
 
Facilitating goal setting and visibly showing behavioral design improvements should be 
incorporated into the support tool. During the workshop (see section 4.4.2.) it was 
stated by the design team that they had no measurable goal to aim for, which made it 
difficult for them to reflect on whether or not they had actually improved their skills in 
applying behavioral design. Furthermore, it was stated that this was difficult because of 
the complexity of the field of behavioral design. 
To facilitate this, one area of reflection should be goal setting, both in terms of 
specifying when the goal is reached as well as defining the steps towards it. The goals 
should be set within the defined topics to make it more specific. 
 
Lastly, the reflection tool should facilitate a reflection on how to support synthesis of 
the problem in a behavioral design process. In the literature review (section 3.2.3.), it 
was found that behavioral design theory rarely supports synthesis while defining the 
problem. A topic specifically revolving around how behavioral design can support 
reframing of the problem should be incorporated.

5.5. BD Retrospective 
Based on the discussion in the previous sections, 5.2.-5.3., on what key elements should 
be incorporated into the support tool, a tool for reflecting on behavioral design is 
developed: The Behavioral Design Retrospective or the BD Retrospective. 
 
This section will describe each part of the tool in section 5.5.1.-5.5.3. and how it should 
be staged at the case company in section 5.5.4., all in relation to the key elements. 
5.5.1. 
Description of the tool 
 
BD Retrospective (illustrated in figure 5.2, for larger version see appendix 12: BD 
Retrospective template) is divided into four areas, “What went well?”, “What could have 
gone better?”, “What interests us?” and “What to do next?”. Each area is supported by 
sub-questions. Additionally, the four topics “Stages of change”, “Persuasive strategies”, 
“Motivation, ability and trigger” and “Problem understanding” from behavioral design 
and synthesis theory have been added to three of the areas. All these elements will now 
be discussed in relation to the findings in section 5.2.-5.3. 
 
 
Figure 5.2: The template for the BD Retrospective 
5.5.2. 
The four areas of BD Retrospective 
The overall structure and reflection questions within the four areas of the tool are 
mainly based on Scrum Retrospective to fit the current practice of the case company. 
“What went well?” and “What could have gone better?” together with the sub-questions 
make the team reflect on how behavioral design was applied during the last sprint to 
form a foundation for the rest of the retrospective.  
==PAGE:77==
- 68 - 
 
These two areas then lead to “What interests us?”. The four topics, which will be 
presented in section 5.5.3., are left out of this area to encourage the team to create their 
own topics and through those expand their knowledge on behavioral design. In relation 
to this, the last circle in the three other areas is also left empty, to encourage reflection 
on new topics. This area is primary inspired by the 4Ls Retrospective for the team 
members to achieve more personal competencies. 
 
It should be noted that the questions for the four areas all comprise of “what”, and not 
of “why” which is the question word more commonly associated with reflection and 
higher-level taxonomy thinking. This is done in order to avoid the reflections becoming 
focused onto a project level.  
 
The area “What to do next?” is to set specific behavioral design goals for the team to 
work towards. This will make it easier to see the how their development is progressing. 
Secondly, a plan on how to get there and what action steps are necessary should be 
defined, whether it is trying out something new or continuing to do what is already 
working. All of these reflections are supported by the sub-questions within the area. 
5.5.3. 
The four topics of BD Retrospective 
Four topics are added to three of the four areas (figure 5.3). Three of the topics are 
directly related to behavioral design while the fourth topic is related to how to 
behavioral design can support synthesis. The four topics are defined as follows: 
 
Stages of change: The stages the user undergoes when changing or adapting a behavior 
or habit, the user’s current stage and where you aim for them to be. 
 
Persuasive strategies: The behavioral design principles; different strategies to influence 
the user in order to reach the target behavior. Design principles you can incorporate 
into the design in order to achieve a higher level of persuasiveness. 
 
Motivation, ability and trigger: The three elements needed for the user to perform an 
action; what motivates the user, what are the user’s capabilities and what triggers the 
behavior? Reflective or reflexive, internal or external, conscious or unconscious trigger? 
 
Problem understanding: Your perception of the design problem; the design frame. Can 
you take a new perspective on the design challenge? How has behavioral design 
influenced how the problem is perceived? 
 
These topics are added to the template to increase the team members’ ability to focus 
the reflection on behavioral design, to keep the reflection of behavioral design on an 
overall level without favoring specific theories or models and to develop a shared 
understanding of behavioral design. The fourth topic should support a reflection on 
how behavioral design can support reframing of the problem. 
 
==PAGE:78==
- 69 - 
 
Figure 5.3: The four topics in the BD Retrospective used to focus the reflection 
 
The content of the four topics in BD Retrospective is created on the basis of the 
discussion in the literature study in chapter 3. 
The first three topics are found from behavioral design theory (section 3.2.): “Stages of 
change”, “Motivation, ability and trigger” and “Persuasive strategies”. As the literature 
review also covers most of the theories presented in the case company’s own literature 
catalogue, these topics should be familiar in the context of the case company. 
Additionally, “Persuasive strategies” is named to fit the terminology used at the case 
company. 
 
The fourth topic, “Problem understanding”, differs from the others as this revolves 
around a reflection on how the problem is framed and how this is changed and 
developed throughout the project. In particular the design team is to reflect on how 
behavioral design has influenced how the problem is perceived. Thus, this topic 
incorporates the findings from the discussion on synthesis (section 3.3.2.), where it is 
found that it is essential to undergo iterations of synthesis in a design process, along 
with the discussion on how most of the behavioral design theories are lacking synthesis 
(section 3.2.3.). Even though that this topic operates more on a project level, rather than 
a process level like the remaining topics, it is argued that this is not an issue as the 
reflection still revolves around how behavioral design has shaped and supported the 
process of reframing the problem. 
5.5.4. 
Staging the tool 
In addition to a description of the tool itself, it is important to touch on how the BD 
Retrospective is to be staged and used in the context of the case company, as well as 
what this entails. 
 
Staging is described by Andreasen, Hansen and Cash (2015) as the process of 
establishing how a design team works, knows and operates and fitting this to a project, 
to best support the design activity. It is important to discuss how the BD Retrospective 
is to be staged as clearly defined staging can support practice of the tool, the interaction 
of the design team, facilitate the design process and the development of individuals’ 
capabilities (Andreasen et al., 2015).  
 
BD Retrospective is developed primarily based on the Scrum Retrospective, which is 
used in the case company (section 4.2.1.), thus it should be easy for the company to 
implement the tool into their existing setup. The reflection tool is to be implemented 
in continuation of Scrum and thus BD Retrospective is to be staged accordingly. A range 
of the most prominent ‘guidelines’ – developed on the basis of the guidelines for Scrum 
(Sutherland and Schwaber, 2017) – for how the tool is to be staged, will be presented 
below. 
==PAGE:79==
- 70 - 
 
The BD Retrospective is to be used by the PDL team after each Scrum Retrospective 
between the sprints. Thus, the reflection tool will be used frequently throughout the 
projects to ensure the team members will continuously improve their capabilities for 
behavioral design. Like Scrum Retrospective, the BD Retrospective is executed through 
a group discussion for the whole design team. The meeting should at most be three 
hours for one-month sprints (Sutherland and Schwaber, 2017), which is the case at 
Philips. 
 
It is essential that the retrospective is performed with a facilitator, which for the BD 
Retrospective is suggested to be the Scrum Master in order to get a coherency between 
the two retrospectives. As a reflection facilitator, he/she is to participate in the reflection 
as a peer team member. However, the facilitator is in charge of ensuring that the 
discussion is productive and positive, and must encourage the team to improve and 
explore the field of behavioral design. This is especially important when discussing the 
area “What interests us?”, as the team members have to come up with additional topics. 
Likewise the facilitator plays a vital part in the fourth area “What to do next?” by 
encouraging the team members to set ambitious, realistic goals, determine a concrete 
plan towards the goal and set a metric for how to measure the success of the goal.  
In order to ensure that the reflection is productive and positive, the facilitator is 
responsible for all team members to understand the purpose of the meeting. This 
purpose is threefold (adapted from the Scrum Guide by Sutherland and Schwaber, 2017) 
and is defined as the following: 
1. 
Inspect how the last sprint went in regards to behavioral design, in particular 
stages of change, persuasive strategies, motivation, ability, trigger, and problem 
understanding 
2. Identify and order the major items that went well and potential improvements 
3. Create a plan for implementing improvements to the way the Scrum Team does 
its work within a specific topic. 
 
Through this responsibility it is important to define the requirements for a BD 
Retrospective facilitator. It is inevitably important that the facilitator is familiar with 
and demonstrates a comprehensive understanding of BD Retrospective, both 
concerning the design and execution of the tool as well as the purpose. However, it is 
to be noted that the facilitator is not required to be a specialist or more experienced in 
behavioral design than the remaining participants, as the template for the BD 
Retrospective through the topics supports the design team in ‘covering’ (a term to be 
discussed in chapter 7) a reflecting on the various aspects of the field of behavioral 
design. 
 
The guide, accompanying the BD Retrospective template itself, also supports the 
facilitator’s task of focusing and enabling a good reflection, as well as supporting the 
team members through the sub-questions and explanation of the tool. 
 
The BD Retrospective is to be used continuously throughout projects in order for it to 
have an effect. In doing so it should be possible to see continuous improvements by 
looking back on previous reflections to see how the content of “What to do next?” moves 
==PAGE:80==
- 71 - 
to “What went well?”, as illustrated in figure 5.4, and how the challenges in “What could 
have gone better?” hopefully will not reappear. Additionally, the content of “What 
interests us?” should increase in complexity as the team advances and develops more 
competencies in using behavioral design. 
 
 
Figure 5.4: Illustrating how the various elements within the BD Retrospective should move as the

The authors wish to make BD Retrospective applicable in a context outside the one of 
which it was built, i.e. the case company, Philips. Thus, the reflection tool will be 
evaluated externally in order to assess BD Retrospective outside the case company 
context, which will be explicitly described in the following chapter.

6.1. Introduction to Evaluation and Refinement 
The BD Retrospective has been developed and presented. This chapter will present how 
the tool will be initially qualitatively evaluated on the basis of three themes and 
corresponding designerly actions in section 6.2., describe the set-up of the evaluation 
workshops in section 6.3., present the results of the evaluation in section 6.4., and finally 
discuss the results in relation to the three themes, the key elements of the tool and the 
case company in section 6.5, followed by refinements in section 6.6. 
 
The aim of the evaluation workshops is to assess the tool in a context outside of the case 
company to investigate how well the key elements facilitate a retrospective in the way 
they are intended. 
6.2. Qualitative evaluation 
For the qualitative evaluation of the BD Retrospective, the themes identified through 
the thematic analysis of the case company (chapter 4) will be used. The BD Retro-
spective will thus be evaluated through the same themes describing the challenges that 
the tool aims to solve. By incorporating these themes, the reflection tool will support 
the design team in developing competencies within behavioral design. 
 
In order to make the themes more concrete and enable them to be used for evaluation, 
the themes are broken down into ‘designerly actions’ (used by Zahedi and Heaton, 2017), 
which are concrete actions to look for in the qualitative evaluation. The reflection tool 
will thus be evaluated by identifying how the members of a design team perform any of 
these actions as they use the BD Retrospective. The designerly actions are described 
with their respective theme as follows (figure 6.1):

the case company

The first theme states that the design team should develop a common mindset and 
understanding of behavioral design. In order for the team to do this, the BD 
Retrospective must support the team in discussing their different perceptions or 
mindsets and in agreeing upon a common mindset. Additionally, the reflection tool 
should facilitate that the team discusses behavioral design on an overall topic level in 
order to set aside different understandings of the behavioral design theory. 
The second theme is goal setting on behavioral design. The reflection tool should 
support the team in agreeing on a behavioral design-related goal, defining specific 
expectations to reach and discuss concrete steps on exactly how to achieve these goals. 
The third theme is reflecting on the behavioral design process, and thus the BD 
Retrospective should support the team in identifying where and how behavioral design 
principles or theory are applied as well as reflect on what went well, what could have 
gone better, what interests them and what to do next. 
 
Each of these themes and corresponding designerly actions are defined to specify which 
elements of the tool are to be evaluated. The two first themes mainly revolve around 
the four topics in the BD Retrospective and how the topics support the reflection. As 
these topics are something completely new compared to the two retrospective tools on 
which the tool is based (section 5.2.), it is especially important to evaluate the use of 
topics in the reflection tool. 
6.3. Set-up of the evaluation 
The qualitative evaluation of the support tool is set up as two workshop sessions outside 
the context of Philips. In this section the workshops will be presented in relation to 
participants, the fictive behavioral design case, the authors’ role and the plan (appendix 
14: Evaluation plan). 
 
The participants are all design Master-students, who have much experience in working 
on various design projects, but very little specifically in behavioral design as only one of 
the participants has worked with behavioral design previously. Despite having little 
experience within the field, it is argued that these participants will still be able to grasp 
the basic principle of behavioral design because of their broad range of previous design 
experience.   
 
In each workshop a design team works with a fictive behavioral design project in order 
to subsequently fill out the BD Retrospective to reflect on how behavioral design was 
used within the case project. It is an open-ended design project about supporting 
patients in getting a better habit of easily taking their medicine several times a day. This 
case is inspired by a project from the case company, just simplified. This should make 
the case easy to understand, approach and relate to for the participants. 
 
Each workshop is a two-hour session with a design team consisting of three participants. 
The plan for the workshops is as follows:  
 
==PAGE:85==
- 76 - 
After an introduction, a ‘crash course' in behavioral design is presented (appendix 8: 
Plan of the workshop), where the most prominent and easily-understandable models 
and strategies within the field (subjectively determined by the authors on the basis of 
the case company) are introduced. Hereafter, the fictive behavioral design project case 
and corresponding personas are presented (appendix 14: Evaluation plan). The team 
then works on the case for a period of time to gain some experience using the theory 
and behavioral design principles and to have a concrete work process to reflect upon. 
From this point onwards the facilitators are passive, simply observing the participants 
and only intervening if the participants have questions or seem stuck. This was done to 
avoid influencing the participants in order to be able to evaluate how well the BD 
Retrospective can stand alone. 
 
After the participants have worked on the case, the reflection template is explained. The 
team then reflects on how they applied and approached the case project with regards to 
behavioral design, first individually for 15 minutes by each filling out the BD 
Retrospective template with post-it notes, then in plenum for 30 minutes, where 
everybody presents their individual reflections on a large version of the template. 
Together they discuss their reflections in order to find commonalities and additional 
reflections. The individual reflection in the workshop is added to make sure that every 
participant contributes to the shared group reflection, as the team has no facilitator due 
to the fact that the authors are passively observing. The workshop ends with feedback 
from the participants.

6.4. Result of the evaluation 
After having conducted a qualitative evaluation of the BD Retrospective through 
workshops with two design teams, the findings will be presented before they are 
analyzed and discussed in the following section (additional results can be found in 
appendix 15: Evaluation results). 
 
==PAGE:86==
- 77 - 
The main results of the evaluation were that the four areas and sub-questions more or 
less worked as intended and that the topics mostly work as intended if they are the 
center of reflection. All of this will be described further below. 
 
The four areas of the BD Retrospective (for detailed description, see section 5.5.3.) 
contributed to both individual reflection value and joined reflection value in regards to 
behavioral design. The first two areas, “What went well?” and “What could have gone 
better?”, created a foundation for the reflection, as the usage of behavioral design, in 
terms of what went well and where it could have gone better, was identified. In the two 
last areas, the participants showed motivation to explore the field of behavioral design 
(in “What interests us?”) which led to setting specific goals and a way of achieving them 
(in “What to do next?”). Thus, the participants understood and moved through the 
template as intended. 
 
 
Figure 6.3: Left – workshop one, where the participants used post-it notes on the BD Retrospective template.  
Right – workshop two, where the participants wrote directly on the template, thus filling out the topics. 
 
The post-it notes used during the individual reflection led to the fact that the four topics 
were more or less excluded from the reflection as they were physically hidden behind 
the post-it notes (figure 6.3 – left). To avoid this for the second workshop the 
participants were told to write directly on the template and then write their reflections 
again on the shared template (figure 6.3 – right). This way, the participants were forced 
to consider the four topics on the template. As they had to write their reflections on the 
shared template again it invited to a discussion as the team had to agree on what to 
write. The second group had more difficulties reflecting, both individually and 
collectively, and had to look at the different behavioral design models for inspiration 
during the reflection, but the reflection was more related to behavioral design compared 
to the first group. 
 
The four topics supported the evaluation in a variety of different ways. Common for all 
participants were that they quickly identified how they applied “Persuasive strategies” 
and “Motivation, ability and trigger”, while “Stages of change” and “Problem under-
standing” were initially left empty. As a result of the fact that they did not cover these 
two topics, the participants concluded that they lacked using theory within “Stages of 
==PAGE:87==
- 78 - 
change” (despite actually using the Hook Model) and “Problem understanding”. 
Therefore, they concluded that these might be relevant to explore further. 
 
Regarding the theme of developing a shared mindset and understanding of the theory, 
it became a little more difficult to evaluate whether the tool supports this. As the 
participants had the same presentation of the theory and afterwards worked together, 
the team had more or less developed a shared mindset and understanding already 
during the project work.  
Furthermore, the participants of the workshops mainly discussed behavioral design in 
relation to the individual models rather than on a more general level (especially in the 
first workshop), and thus the topics did not achieve their intended purpose within this 
theme.  
 
The reflection in general led to some interesting insights into how reflection can support 
a team in developing behavioral design capabilities. During the reflection the parti-
cipants themselves pointed out that the theory is difficult to use in practice and that 
people quickly can have different ways of using the theory – which are some of the key 
challenges identified from the case company in this thesis (section 4.5.). The evaluation 
team arrived at these challenges through reflection during a mere two-hour workshop. 
 
The participants’ joined discussion of their work process contributed to additional 
reflections. This happened especially for the second group as they had to write the 
reflections again and thus agree on what to add to the shared template. Based on their 
reflections the participants started a discussion on how to overcome the challenges and 
formulated specific goals as well as more concrete actions and ideas on how to move 
towards these goals. What the participants lacked was to set expectations for the goals. 
 
In many ways the BD Retrospective supports reflection on behavioral design as 
intended. The four main questions and the sub-questions inspired the participants 
during the reflection, both in terms of looking back on their own work process as well 
as looking forward and setting specific goals and action steps towards these goals. 
Additionally, the participants moved through the template and the four questions as 
intended by gradually increasing the complexity of the reflection. What they were 
lacking was adding expectations to these goals. 
6.5. Discussion of the evaluation 
Based on the results of the initial qualitative evaluation and the key elements, of which 
the tool consists (defined in section 5.4.), two main subjects will be discussed in this 
section: The importance of a facilitator and how the BD Retrospective topics guide the 
reflection. The topics will be discussed in relation to the theory of behavioral design and 
Scrum as well as the case company. 
 
It became clear in the evaluation that a facilitator, as defined in the Scrum handbook 
(Sutherland and Schwaber, 2017), is important during a reflection. The individual 
reflection was added to the workshop plan to make sure that every participant was heard 
==PAGE:88==
- 79 - 
during the group reflection, but it seemed to reduce the group discussion as it became 
a presentation of reflections rather than a discussion. The participants only described a 
behavioral design goal and a way to get there, but lacked setting specific expectations 
to their goals, and thus some of the key elements (defined in section 5.4.) did not have 
the expected result. Furthermore, the first workshop demonstrated the importance of 
the topics being the center of reflection, as the post-it notes led the reflection away from 
behavioral design. Despite this, the participants performed the reflection mostly as 
intended which emphasizes that the tool itself will be a valuable support for a facilitator 
to manage the team reflection. 
 
The participants reflected on behavioral design through the use of specific theories, like 
B=MAT (Fogg, 2007) and the Hook Model (Eyal, 2014a), rather than on a topic level. It 
is noteworthy that these two models are the same ones the designers in PDL preferred 
(appendix 4: Case study interviews), which emphasizes how these models might be easier 
to apply during ideation. At the case company the team members have knowledge of 
several theories and different preferences individually, and it would be interesting to 
see how the topics would influence a more experienced team. 
The topics “Stages of change” and “Problem understanding” were by the participants 
identified to not be a part of their process (despite their use of the Hook Model), which 
led them to discuss how these two topics could be implemented more into the design 
process in the future. This is especially relevant for “Problem understanding” as this 
topic encourages the designer to reflect on how behavioral design facilitates synthesis, 
which is important due to the fact that the theory of behavioral design itself does not 
facilitate this (as discussed in the literature study in section 3.2.3.). 
6.6. Refinement 
The BD Retrospective has been developed and evaluated on an initial, qualitative basis. 
Based on this as well as feedback from the case company, some refinements are to be 
implemented before any additional evaluation (which will be described in section 8.2.). 
The refinements are mainly directed towards the structure of the template based on 
feedback and observations, which will be presented below. 
 
The placement of the sub-questions in the BD Retrospective Guide (appendix 13: BD 
Retrospective guide) and the four topics’ placement on the template (appendix 12: BD 
Retrospective template) led to some confusion for the participants, as it seemed like each 
of the sub-questions was related to the individual topics. The placement of either the 
questions or the topics should thus be reconsidered. As the topics are the center of the 
template, and each of them is important to reflect upon, they should have a central part 
of the template. The sub-questions’ placement is inspired directly from the other 
retrospective tools (described in section 5.2.), and the questions should therefore 
preferably stay, as this is familiar to the case company. 
 
In addition to the topics, it was pointed out that there should be a clear distinction 
between the topics related directly to behavioral design and the fourth topic which 
==PAGE:89==
- 80 - 
revolves around synthesis, to avoid confusion on whether “Problem definition” is a term 
within behavioral design. 
 
A few suggestions to the area of “What to do next?” were received. First, it was suggested 
that the area was divided into three spaces; Goal, steps/plan and measurement. This 
way it would be clear for the team, alone from the structure of the template, what is to 
be identified and discussed within the fourth area of the template. Furthermore, it was 
suggested to add some kind of scale (e.g. from 1 to 10) on how well behavioral design 
was used during each sprint. This way, it would be easier to trace back and visually see 
a personal development over a period of time. 
As these refinements are all relatively basic and revolve only around the layout of the 
template, they will be implemented in the further work on this thesis and thus not 
illustrated within this report.

7.1. Introduction to Discussion 
The support tool, BD Retrospective, has been developed, evaluated and discussed after 
a comprehensive project work involving a literature study and an extensive case 
company research and analysis. Based on this, the final solution as well as the additional 
insights attained in this thesis will be discussed in the following sections in regards to 
the academical contribution (section 7.2.) and the limitations (section 7.3.). 
7.2. Contribution of the thesis 
This section will discuss the contribution of BD Retrospective in regards to the insights 
found in the literature study and in the case company research as well as the 
contribution of the tool itself. 
7.2.1. 
Contribution of the literature study 
In behavioral design literature, an extensive amount of different theories exist to 
understand a current behavior and to develop interventions to change this behavior. 
When comparing these theories, they have different strengths and weaknesses, as 
discussed in the literature study in section 3.2.3. Some of these theories are ‘small’ in 
term of easily applicable but not so comprehensive like the Hook Model (Eyal, 2014a) 
and B=MAT (Fogg, 2007) while others aim at a more holistic approach such as BCW 
(Michie, 2011) and COMBI (Klein et al., 2013). Despite this, all the behavioral design 
theories covered in the literature study (section 3.2.2.) can be broken down into a few 
topics which more or less cover them all. The authors of the theories might have a 
different perspective on behavior, but on a more general level, it can be argued that they 
are stating more or less the same. 
Michie (2011) created BCW in an attempt to combine a broad variation of theories into 
one in order to overcome each theories’ limitations, but this also added just another 
theory to the field of behavioral design. This attempt to unite different theories into one 
did not solely solve the problem of making behavioral design more practically 
applicable. In other words, the behavioral design theory simply cannot stand alone in a 
complete development process, which the designer should be aware of. When Cash (et 
al., 2017) developed the behavioral design process he was well aware of the fact that this 
process alone was not enough to cover a whole development process. 
Furthermore, many of the authors create these methods based on their extensive 
experience within the field (Fogg, 2009) in an attempt to share the experience. However, 
it can be argued that by looking at a method, the reader might get a better 
understanding of behavioral design theory, but will not gain actual behavioral design 
experience, and thus merely methods are not sufficient in order to better apply it in 
practice. 
7.2.2. 
Contribution of the case study 
To get a better understanding of why behavioral design is difficult to apply in practice 
it is necessary to investigate how the theories are actually integrated into a design 
==PAGE:92==
- 83 - 
process, in this case at Philips Design and in PDL in particular. To do so, their way of 
working has to be explored first. 
Their way of working is structured with Scrum through 4-week sprints with a review 
and retrospective after each sprint (section 4.2.1.). Scrum is defined on the basis of the 
Agile Manifesto (Beck et al., 2001), which consists of a range of values to follow. Two of 
which will be discussed in relation to PDL. The first value is “Responding to change over 
following a plan”; in PDL the process is iterative and flexible which implies that they 
value to quickly respond to change over having a fixed plan to follow. The other value 
is “Individuals and interactions over processes and tools”. This is visible in PDL through 
the fact that the team members are drawing on their experience rather than following 
a specific process. They do not have an established procedure for projects involving 
specific steps and milestones to follow. Both of these agile values implies that following 
a strict method is not how theory is incorporated, thus a new method will not solve the 
challenge of applying behavioral design – experience will. 
 
As identified in the empirical study (chapter 4) the team at the case company ‘mentally’ 
divides the behavioral design theory into being either ‘small’ theories (like the Hook 
Model; Eyal, 2014a) or more holistic theories (as BCW; Michie, 2011). Regardless of their 
preferences for either, they are having difficulties applying them; the small theories are 
not in themselves enough to cover a whole design process and additional supporting 
models are therefore needed; and the comprehensive, holistic theories are difficult to 
apply in such a flexible and iterative design process which is practiced at Philips. Thus, 
more generally speaking, the team at the case company struggles to incorporate the 
behavioral design theory, regardless the type, into their design process, and the 
development of yet another model will therefore not be the solution to their problem. 
7.2.3. 
Contribution of BD Retrospective 
In order to overcome many of the mentioned challenges of incorporating the theory of 
behavioral design into a flexible agile working process, the BD Retrospective is 
developed. Instead of defining a specific process or method to directly apply, the BD 
Retrospective aims at supporting the team’s ability to gain more experience and make 
each individual able to comprehend the theory of behavioral design on a higher level. 
As this is done through overall topics, which are common for most theories, the BD 
Retrospective facilitates the application of behavioral design across a wide range of 
behavioral design theories. Thus, specific terminology and process steps can be avoided, 
supporting agility and complexity which are present at the case company. 
 
Furthermore, the gap between research and ideation, consisting of the lack of synthesis, 
which is discovered in the literature study (chapter 3) is just as well present in practice 
at the case company (chapter 4). To overcome this gap, the BD Retrospective explicitly 
includes reflection on synthesis to make sure a more coherent process is executed. Thus, 
while gaining experience in behavioral design, they also gain experience on how 
synthesis is incorporated into the process.

- 84 - 
7.3. Limitations of the thesis 
Naturally, some limitations will be present both for the solution, BD Retrospective, but 
also in regards to the process leading towards the solution. These limitations will be 
discussed in the following sections. 
7.3.1. 
The tool and its context 
BD Retrospective is not developed for beginners, i.e. people who are novice on behavioral 
design, as it requires at least a basic understanding of the theory. The tool is designed 
to improve the individual’s application of the theory in practice, which implies that some 
knowledge is needed as the tool does not support the application of the theory itself. 
The outcome of the tool thus depends on the people using it; the better understanding 
of the theory the designer has, the better the reflection will be – and therefore the 
development of behavioral design capabilities. 
 
Preferably, the reflection with the tool should be controlled by a facilitator in order to 
keep the reflection and discussion on track. To do so, it requires that the facilitator is 
well aware of the purpose of the tool, to make sure the discussion revolves around the 
topic of behavioral design specifically and does not end up revolving around the design 
process itself. 
 
The BD Retrospective is developed to fit one specific case company, thus some 
requirements to the context exist in terms of how it is staged. The BD Retrospective is 
to be used frequently just like a classical Scrum Retrospective, and without the Scrum 
structure it requires additional effort to apply. As the Scrum Retrospective is still 
important to perform, the BD Retrospective should be performed in continuation of 
this, thus it requires that the people applying it set aside the time to reflect. 
 
The three themes are identified from a single case study. The BD Retrospective is 
developed on the basis of solving three challenges, defined as three themes, which are 
discovered at the case company. As this case study is performed on a single company 
some of the challenge at the case company may only be relevant for this particular 
context, thus other challenges might appear by further investigating other companies. 
 
Further validation of the four topics is necessary. This is where the BD Retrospective 
stands out compared to the Scrum Retrospective and the 4Ls Retrospective as the four 
topics are added to each of the four areas of the tool. The four topics are defined based 
on the literature review (section 3.2.) which inevitably is influenced by the authors’ 
subjective evaluation of the theory. Only an initial qualitative evaluation has been 
conducted to evaluate the four topics in terms of relevance and applicability. 
Furthermore, the participants did not have the sufficient knowledge about behavioral 
design to validate these aspects in depth. 
==PAGE:94==
- 85 - 
7.3.2. 
Communication and expectations 
Several limitations and challenges have occurred during the project in particular in 
regards to the communication with the case company. As it was not possible to travel to 
the case company headquarter in Eindhoven during the project, all communication was 
through either email or Skype. Thus, it was difficult to get a clear picture of how exactly 
the company is structured in terms of teams, work processes etc. It also entailed that all 
the case company data was based on what the team members of PDL more or less 
explicitly stated, as it was not possible to observe how they worked and thus get less 
subjective statements. In order to overcome this challenge it was found necessary to 
repeat some of the basic questions in several interviews to receive different perceptions 
of the situation which were then analyzed in depth. 
 
The first project map and the review meeting could have been more efficient. One of the 
challenges with the first version of the process map (A, appendix 6) was that the six 
layers made it too extensive, yet it did not cover the complexity of the process such as 
the iterations and crossovers between research and ideation. During the meeting it was 
intended to review the authors’ perception of the process, discuss gaps and suggestions 
as well as discuss the main challenges, but all these activities were too extensive. 
Furthermore, the participants of the review meeting seemed to have different 
expectations in regards to the outcome of the meeting; where the authors expected to 
get a review of their perception of the process, the team members saw this as an 
opportunity to get a better understanding of their own process. As a result of this most 
of the dialogue revolved around explaining the process map rather than discussing the 
content. This was also caused by the fact that the participants did not have sufficient 
knowledge about their own process, and thus could not verify or dismiss the perception 
of the process for most of the map.

Based on the discussion of the thesis and BD Retrospective in this

x 
A broad variation of theory within the field of behavioral design exists, 
with different methodology and approaches. Despite of this, all the 22 
reviewed theories are covered by three overall topics. Some of the 
authors of these theories (e.g. Fogg) have over 15 years of experience 
within the field of behavior change, which is difficult to share through 
descriptions. Furthermore, the theory of behavioral design has certain 
limitations, which the designer should be aware of. Cash (et al.,2017) 
developed the behavioral design process well-aware of, that the 
process cannot stand alone within a product development process. 
 
Contributions 
x 
The theory of behavioral design is built on certain assumptions which 
do not always match reality. More specifically, the product design 
process at the case company is highly complex and iterative, which is 
difficult to capture in the theory. Thus, the missing link for applying 
behavioral design in practice is a support tool for developing compe-
tencies and gaining experience rather than a model of how to execute 
a behavioral design development process. 
 
x 
Based on the data gathered from the theory and the case, the 
reflection tool BD Retrospective has been developed. The tool 
supports a team in continuously improving their competencies within 
behavioral design through structured reflection. Unlike theory, the 
BD Retrospective fits into a flexible agile development process. 
 
Limitations 
x 
The BD Retrospective is developed as a support tool to improve the 
designers’ ability to apply behavioral design based on existing 
experience. It does not provide any introduction nor training in 
behavioral design. Thus, a basic knowledge about behavioral design is 
required beforehand. 
 
x 
The tool has been developed based on a single case company and how 
they work. Thus, it will probably work as intended within this specific 
company, but might need some adjustments for it to fit into another 
context.

==PAGE:97==

8.1. Conclusion 
The overall aim of this thesis is to investigate how behavioral design can be applied in 
practice, in this case in relation to a multinational firm. To investigate this an extensive 
literature study of current, relevant behavioral design literature was conducted, which 
led to further investigation of how behavioral design relates to synthesis. It was 
concluded that the theory of behavioral design rarely supports synthesis, especially in 
regards to defining the problem within a design process. This resulted in identification 
of a gap in the design process between defining the problem and generating tangible 
ideas for product design. 
To further investigate this gap, a case study was conducted within the multinational 
company, Philips. The aim of this case study was to determine how behavioral design is 
currently being applied in practice by a design team. Thus, the teams’ process of 
behavioral design projects was mapped out, resulting in the realization that the process 
is much more complex and iterative than first anticipated. In addition, it was found that 
the process was relatively unstructured and to a high degree relied on the personal 
experience of the individual team members. The investigation of the case company team 
led to the identification of the following three main challenges on how to apply 
behavioral design in practice: Firstly, team members have a different understanding of 
the behavioral design theory. Secondly, common goals for the development of 
behavioral design are not defined and shared among the team members. Thirdly, the 
team does not consistently learn from previous projects preventing continuous 
improvement on a process level. 
 
The case study together with the literature study led to the conclusion that gaining and 
sharing experience is the best way to increase the ability to apply behavioral design 
theory in practice. In the light of this, a reflection tool called BD Retrospective has 
been developed as a part of this master thesis project. The main purpose of this tool is 
to support a behavioral design team to enable continuously process improvement by 
benefitting from learnings from previous projects in a systematic way. 
 
BD Retrospective supports a team to reflect specifically on their application of 
behavioral design. It is developed on the basis of widely-used retrospectives and thus 
incorporates many of the same elements. The BD Retrospective consists of four areas of 
reflection, “What went well?”, “What could have gone better?”, “What interests us?” and 
“What to do next?”. The four areas are supported by sub-questions along with four 
topics. The four topics, “Stages of change”, “Persuasive strategies”, “Motivation, ability 
and trigger” and “Problem understanding” provide a structured way of reflecting on the 
application of behavioral design within a product development process, as well as how 
behavioral design supports synthesis. The topics cover a wide range of behavioral design 
principles from the theory, enabling a discussion across theories despite different 
preferences. 
An initial evaluation of the support tool has been conducted with design students 
outside of the case company to validate the tool’s application. This evaluation has given 
promising results both for the structure of the tool in general as well as for the four 
topics, thus moving one step closer to rendering behavioral design applicable in 
practice. 
==PAGE:98==
- 89 - 
8.2. Further work 
As defined by Blessing and Chakrabarti (2009), this thesis has reached a type 3 design 
project, as the evaluation of the support tool has only been performed on an initial basis. 
In this section, a plan for suggested further work will be described based on the current 
state of this project. 
8.2.1. 
Research of theory and practice 
As the development of behavioral design theory is still in a relatively early stage, it is 
relevant to investigate more academic literature on the topic. This needs to be done to 
keep the four topics in BD Retrospective relevant. 
 
The features of BD Retrospective are currently based on a single case company, thus one 
of the next steps should be to investigate additional companies and other contexts 
where BD Retrospective will be relevant. With the knowledge obtained from the first 
case company, a more structured approach to investigate other companies can be 
applied. One way to do this could be through the use of mental models (Cash, 
Dekoninck and Ahmed-Kristensen, 2017) (Johnson and O’Connor, 2008) (Jonker et al., 
2011).  This would enable a more thorough qualitative understanding of the differences 
between each individual in regards to behavioral design. Alternatively, a more 
quantitative research method could be applied such as questionnaires in order to obtain 
more specific empirical data. 
8.2.2. 
Evaluation and quantitative validation 
The initial qualitative evaluation gave some good insights into how the BD 
Retrospective works in practice, and how many of the various elements within the tool 
support reflection on behavioral design. However, as this was merely an initial 
evaluation, several aspects of the tool still need to be evaluated and validated further. 
First step towards this would be to evaluate the BD Retrospective in a context much like 
at the case company. The participants should have an extensive knowledge about 
behavioral design and thus process individual perceptions on how to apply it. This way 
it would be possible to evaluate how well the four topics facilitate a reflective discussion 
across different theories. Furthermore, it should be evaluated how this discussion 
facilitates the ability to share different mindsets and understandings of the theories, and 
whether the tool supports the participants to move towards a shared mindset. These 
evaluation sessions are suggested to be thoroughly analyzed in order to refine the 
different elements of the tool and in particular the four topics as these at the moment 
are based mainly on the authors’ perception of what the theories of behavioral design 
contain. 
 
When the BD Retrospective has been evaluated and refined sufficiently according to 
qualitative evaluation, it should be tested and validated through more structured, 
quantitative methods. Based on this, it will be possible to make adjustments to ensure 
that the tool can be applied in a broader context. 
 
==PAGE:99==
- 90 - 
As the BD Retrospective is developed to continuously improve each individual’s ability 
to apply behavioral design, the tool’s impact cannot be measured in the short term. 
Thus, it is recommended to observe the impact of the tool over a period of time 
spanning several product design projects. In addition, it would be beneficial to establish 
various kinds of key performance indicators to capture potential process improvements. 
 
After having discussed the suggestions for further work, the application of behavioral 
design in practice is one step closer to being realized with BD Retrospective.
