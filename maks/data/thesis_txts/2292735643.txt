Summary (English)
The global energy demand is predicted to rise with over 30% in the next twenty
years. The growing demand for energy is driven by the rise in living standards in
highly populated countries like China and India. At the same time, many of the
known resources are drying out. In 2013, 57% of the global energy consumption
was covered by oil and gas. To meet the rising energy demand it is therefore
of great importance to increase the recovery of hydrocarbons in the existing oil
and gas elds. Reservoir simulation plays a key role in both the implementation
of tertiary EOR methods and in production optimization of secondary recovery
methods like water ooding.
In this thesis, we study reservoir simulation, using both the Black Oil model
and the Compositional model.
The goal of this thesis is to implement and
simulate a series of benchmark test cases, in three dierent reservoir simula-
tors and study/compare the obtained results.
The test cases includes Black
Oil simulation of water ooding, gas injection, horizontal well modeling and a
Compositional model with four components.
We start by considering the mathematical models for multiphase ow in porous
media and their physical background. The rock and uid properties needed in
both models are discussed. The Fully Implicit method to solve the nonlinear
system ow equations is considered. The spatial discretization of the equations
using the method of Finite Volumes and Two Point Flux Approximation is
described. The Implicit Euler method is used for temporal discretization. The
Peacemann well model for multiphase ow used by the reservoir simulators is
described.
==PAGE:4==
ii
We implement three Black Oil benchmark test cases from the SPE Comparative
Solution Projects (CSP) and a four component Compositional test case. The
CSP test cases are the rst CSP (SPE1), the seventh CSP (SPE7) and the ninth
CSP (SPE9). We also consider the Black Oil formulation of the Compositional
test case.
The result from Eclipse is considered as the benchmark solution. The absolute
and relative errors of the production values are calculated against the Eclipse
solution. Finally the simulation time, the number of time steps and Newton
iterations used by the simulators in the dierent test cases are compared.
Overall, the obtained simulation results showed good agreement and matched
the published results from the original papers.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The global energy demand is increasing, driven by the rise in living standards
around the world. In 2013, 57% of the global energy consumption where covered
by oil and gas. Even though the amount of global energy consumption covered
by alternative sources are predicted to rise in the future, the demand for oil
and gas will still increase. As the demand for energy increases, many of the
known hydrocarbon resources are depleted. To meet the increasing demand for
oil and gas it is therefore of great importance to increase the recovery from
existing hydrocarbon elds.
Research into improving the recovery factor of
existing elds through various methods, such as Enhanced Recovery methods,
are conducted around the world. Production optimization of secondary methods
like waterooding is an example of an active eld of research that is ongoing.
In the eort to increase the recovery factor, reservoir simulation plays a crucial
role.
Prediction of the future performance of a reservoir and investigating methods
for optimizing the production is the primary objective of reservoir simulation.
Reservoir simulation is the numerical simulation of uid ow in a porous medium.
The porous medium (the reservoir rock) is typically a sandstone, a limestone or
a chalk, like in the Danish north-sea. The reservoir rock properties, such as the
porosity and the permeability are close to always very heterogeneous.
==PAGE:14==
2
Introduction
The uid usually consists of one or more of the uid phases, water, oil and gas.
The behavior of the reservoir uid strongly depends on its thermodynamically
properties i.e. its composition and the stage of production.
The production of a hydrocarbon reservoir is typically divided in to three stages.
In the primary stage of recovery the hydrocarbons are produced by depletion,
i.e. the reservoir pressure drives the ow. Production by depletion typically has
a recovery factor of 15 to 30%. In the secondary stage of recovery, the reservoir
pressure is maintained through injection of water or gas in to the reservoir.
Ideally, the injection of water will push the oil towards the production wells.
However, in reality this rarely happens, due to the heterogeneous nature of the
reservoir rock and the dierence in uid properties between the phases, like the
viscosity.
Production by pressure maintenance usually has a recovery factor
below 50%. At the tertiary stage of recovery, Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)
methods are implemented.
The EOR methods includes surfactant ooding,
polymer injection and thermal methods.
The main objective of these EOR
methods is to reduce the residual oil saturation, by achieving miscibility.
Mathematically the multiphase ow is modeled by a system of non-linear par-
tial dierential equations (PDEs). The only option for solving the system of
equations is by numerical methods. In this thesis, we consider the Finite Vol-
ume method together with the Two Point Flux Approximation for discretizing
the system of equations in space. For temporal discretization, we consider the
implicit Euler method. These methods are the industry standard. The Fully
Implicit Method for solving the system of equations is considered. To calcu-
late the derivatives of the system equations we consider the use of Automatic
Dierentiation.
The mathematical models that describes the multiphase ow considered in this
thesis are the Black Oil model and the Compositional model. The Black Oil
model assumes three phases and three components is present in the reservoir.
The Black Oil model uses the formation volume factors and the solubility of
the phases to predict the phase split. This model has for many years been the
standard model in reservoir simulation and is still very relevant today. The gen-
eral Compositional model assumes a nite number of phases and components,
although the standard is a three-phase model. The Compositional model uses
an equation of state to predict the phase split (ash calculation). The Compo-
sitional model provides the basis for modeling a number of EOR methods. The
reservoir simulators used in this thesis are:
The Eclipse reservoir simulator (E100 and E300) owned by Schlumberger. Eclipse
is considered the industry standard and provides a basis for simulating a very
large set of dierent ow situations. It has both a Black Oil simulator (E100)
and a Compositional simulator (E300).
==PAGE:15==
3
The COmpositional SImulator (COSI) owned by Lloyds register.
COSI is a
Compositional reservoir simulator in its core formulation. It can simulate ow
problems in both Compositional and Black Oil formulation.
The Matlab Reservoir Simulation Toolbox (MRST), developed by SINTEF Ap-
plied Mathemathics. MRST provides a number of modules for solving dierent
kinds of ow problems, The MRST Black Oil module is used in this thesis.
COSI simulations are performed on a workstation, which has an 8 core Intel
Core i7-4790 3.60 GHz processor with 15.6 GB RAM and 64-bit Ubuntu 14.04
LTS.
Eclipse and MRST simulations are performed on a laptop which has an 4 core
Intel i7-3630QM 2.40GHz processor with 8.0 GB RAM and 64-bit Windows 8.1.
In this thesis, we consider the three Black Oil benchmark test cases SPE1, SPE7
and SPE9, proposed as a part of the SPE Comparative Solution Project CSP
and a four component Compositional test case.
A very short description of the test cases are: SPE1 is a gas injection model,
SPE7 is a horizontal well model, SPE9 is a water injection model and the Com-
positional model is a depletion model.
We give short examples of how to implement the Black Oil test cases SPE1 and
SPE9 in COSI and Eclipse respectively.
The goal of the thesis is to implement and simulate these benchmark test cases in
the three reservoir simulators mentioned above and study/compare the obtained
results.

Chapter 2
Rock and Fluid Properties
In order to perform a simulation of multiphase uid ow through a porous me-
dia, an analysis of the uid and rock properties is required. Properties such as
rock and uid compressibility, capillary pressures, uid density and viscosity,
rock porosity and permeability, relative phase permeabilities, phase formation
volume factors and solubility are needed.
Determination of these properties
are obtained using various techniques, involving dierent types of scientic and
engineering disciplines, such as geology, chemistry and petro-physics. The tech-
niques used includes seismic surveys, various well-logging tools as well as core
and uid samples taken from the reservoir. The results are then used in reser-
voir simulation to predict the behavior in the reservoir under production. Here
we give a short introduction to the rock and uid properties that are used to
simulate the ow in the reservoir.
2.1
Upscaling
For a typical hydrocarbon reservoir, the rock properties are highly heteroge-
neous on all length scales. Depending on the rock type, typical grain and pore
sizes ranges from (0.1-10 µm).
Hence, rock properties such as porosity and
permeability are dened at micro-scale. Seismic surveys have a resolution on
==PAGE:20==

m-scale. Typical well-logs has a resolution on cm-scale and are conned to the
local area surrounding the well. Furthermore both seismic surveys and well-logs
have relatively large error margins. The rock-core samples taken from the reser-
voir are at cm-scale. The samples are analyzed in the laboratory to determine
properties, such as porosity, permeability and rock compressibility. A typical
hydrocarbon reservoir is measured at the m-scale.
The limited information
available to determine rock properties (few core samples to represent the entire
reservoir, uncertainty of well-logs and seismic surveys), mean that geo-statistical
models must be used to determine properties throughout the reservoir. Given
the numerical techniques used in reservoir simulation presently used, and the
computing power currently available, it is not feasible to model a hydrocarbon
reservoir on neither micro nor cm-scale. This limitation is not about to change
in the foreseeable future, and leaves the question of how to meaningfully upscale
properties from micro-scale to macro-scale. Upscaling of rock and rock-uid in-
teraction properties are an ongoing eld of research and a further discussion is
not the scope of this thesis. For a more thorough discussion of the subject the
reader is referred to [1]. The important lesson here is that the values of the
properties are estimates with a considerable uncertainty and not exact values.

2.6.3
Classication
Depending on the pressure, temperature and uid composition a hydrocarbon
reservoir can contain any combination of the three phases, water, oil and gas,
(α = w, o, g).
During production the amount of each phase existing in the
reservoir, may change signicantly. This can be due to decreasing pressure in
the formation during production, and/or injection of water or gas. In a three
phase ow system, the reservoir can be in either an under saturated state or a
saturated state. In the under saturated state all gas components are dissolved
in the oil phase. Hence, only the water phase and the oil phase exist in the
reservoir. In the saturated state, all three phases coexist in the reservoir. For
an isothermal system, the state of the reservoir depends on the pressure. The
pressure where the reservoir goes between saturated and under saturated state
is called the saturation pressure.

Solution Strategy
To solve the multiphase ow equations arising in reservoir simulation, several
dierent solution techniques are used. The most widely used techniques are
the IMPES method, the sequential method, the Fully Implicit method and the
Adaptive Implicit method. For Two-phase, Black Oil and the compositional
model, the most commonly used solution techniques are the IMPES method
and the Fully Implicit method. The expression IMPES stands for implicit pres-
sure explicit saturation. The basic idea of the IMPES method is to split the
coupled ow equations into a pressure equation and a saturation equation. The
pressure equation is then solved using an implicit scheme and the saturation
equation using a explicit scheme. This formulation is relatively easy to imple-
ment and is widely used for two-phase ow systems. The IMPES technique does
however suer from a lack of stability due to the explicit formulation of the sat-
uration. For a more detailed discussion of the IMPES technique, the sequential
technique and the adaptive technique we refer to [8]. The fully implicit method
is unconditionally stable and is often used for Black Oil and compositional type
models. Here we will give a short introduction of the Fully Implicit method
using a single-phase compressible ow situation. We also briey discuss the use
of Automatic Dierentiation to compute the derivatives needed to linearize the
system.
Regardless of the solution technique used, a substantial amount of the run-time
is used on solving the resulting linear system of equations. Thus, a fast linear

5.1
Black Oil Equations
The Black Oil Model is a system of equations that models simultaneous ow
of three phases (water, oil, gas), through a porous medium. The hydrocarbon
components are divided into an oil component and a gas component. The oil
component are assumed to exist only in oil phase, whereas the gas component
are assumed to exist in both the oil phase and the gas phase, depending on
reservoir pressure. The gas component is assumed to consist mainly of methane
and ethane. The water component is assumed to exist only in water phase. The
assumptions mean that there are mass transfer between the oil phase and the
gas phase. There are dierent ways to implement the Black Oil Model, like the
choice of primary variables and the way rock and uid properties are given or
computed.

Compositional Model
It is not always possible to model the compositional eects from enhanced oil
recovery methods such as gas injection, chemical ooding and thermal ooding
using Black Oil type models. To model these type of ow situations the Com-
positional Model is more accurate, since it automatically takes in to account the
vapor-liquid phase split of all components, depending on the thermodynamical
conditions.
Where the Black Oil Model assumes that the reservoir uid consists of three
components only, namely water, oil and gas, the Compositional Model can han-
dle an arbitrary nite number of components. In a general Compositional Model
an arbitrary number of phases and components can be modeled, although the
typically used models only consider three phases namely water, oil and gas.
Here we consider an isothermal, three phase Compositional Model, with no mass
transfer between the water phases and the hydrocarbon phases. We express the
conservation equations in terms of mass fractions, although mole fractions or
concentrations also can be used (see [8] for formulation in terms of mole frac-
tions). The model is described under isothermal conditions. Diusive eects are
included in the model. Except for special cases liked fractured ow, diusive
eects are usually neglected, because of the small contribution to the overall
ow.

Synthetic Reservoir Models
Synthetic Reservoir Models are used to test and compare reservoir simulators.
The models are designed to focus on specic areas of interest, both in terms
of research and software testing. Synthetic models are widely used to compare
dierent methods and formulations, and measure the state of the art simulation
capability. To provide an independent series of datasets for this purpose the
Society of Petroleum Engineers (SPE) have proposed a number reservoir models.
The SPE Comparative Solution Projects (CSP) are a series of reservoir models
proposed as benchmark problems for reservoir simulation. The CSP includes
water ooding, coning, gas injection and gas condensate models (for a full list
and description of Comparative Solution Projects see [16]). Here we will consider
the CSP Black Oil models SPE1, SPE7 and SPE9. The models are implemented
in both MRST, COSI and Eclipse. For a Compositional case a four component
model is considered and implemented in Eclipse and COSI. The Eclipse solutions
will be used as the benchmark solution. We will give a short description of how
to implement these models in both MRST, COSI and Eclipse.

7.3
Seventh Comparative Solution Project, SPE7
The seventh SPE CSP study was posed by Ngheim et.al in 1991 (see [20]). The
project was designed to study simulation of hydrocarbon production through
horizontal wells. It aims to study dierent approaches of modeling horizontal
wells and the eect of the horizontal well length on the oil production. Due to
coning eects in the thin reservoir, the well length has a signicant eect on the
oil production.
The reservoir dimensions are (2700×2700×160)ft3 and is discretized on a (9, 9, 6)
Cartesian grid. It is a Black Oil problem, with a homogeneous permeability
eld and uniform porosity throughout the reservoir.
The reservoir has one
horizontal water injector well completed in the x−direction in the bottom layer,
and one horizontal producer completed in the x−direction in the top layer (see
Figure 7.15). The reservoir data is listed in Table 7.8. The reservoir is simulated
for a period of 1500 days. Initially the reservoir is at bubble point pressure, with
no free gas. The initial state of the reservoir is listed in Table 7.9 and the uid
PVT functions are listed in Table 7.10.
The study is divided into 8 cases.
The rst 6 cases are designed to study
the eect of production rates and well length on the recovery. This is done
by maintaining reservoir pressure through water injection at a constant bottom
hole pressure and keeping the liquid production at a constant rate. The pressure
maintenance means that very small volumes of free gas is released. I the last
two cases water is injected at a constant rate, causing the reservoir pressure to
drop. Hence, substantial volumes of gas are released from the oil phase. The

7.4
Compositional Model
Is this section we consider a Compositional model, with four components plus
the water component. The model is inspired by an example by Kristian Jessen1.
The reservoir shape is irregular, with a dome formed on the north-west side of the
center. The reservoir rock is almost incompressible.The reservoir is discretized
on a (18, 27, 5) corner point grid, with 2470 cells. Figure 7.22 shows the reservoir
grid, with the initial pressure distribution. The oil-water contact goes through
the lower part of the reservoir. The reservoir is produced by depletion, with
a single well located in the south-west part of the reservoir and completed in
the lowest 3 layers, just above the oil-water contact. The producer is bottom
hole pressure controlled. Initially the reservoir is above bubble-point pressure.
The porosity is uniform throughout the reservoir. The permeability is assumed
homogeneous in all directions. The production of the reservoir is simulated for
a total of 10 years. The data is listed in Table 7.15.

7.4.1
Grid
To construct a synthetic grid suitable for the simulations, we consider a method
from the area of computer graphics. We start by a (18, 27, 5) Cartesian grid.
We then perturb the z−coordinates in all layers, by sampling a 2D Perlin noise
function (see [26]). Figure 7.23a shows a 2D Perlin noise function sampled on
a (50 × 50) grid. The Perlin noise function gives a smooth noise, with values
between 0 and 1.
We then consider the noise as a height map and perturb
the z−coordinates by sampling the noise on a (18 × 27) grid.
This gives a
equidistant spacing in the z−direction for all grid points.
To avoid this we
add a high frequency noise to each layer, by sampling the noise function on a
higher zoom level and adding the values to the z−coordinate in each grid point.
Figure 7.23b shows the noise function on two dierent zoom levels. This method
is known as Fractual Brownian Motion (fBm) and is widely used in computer
graphics to create realistic content. For a discussion of this and other methods
used in computer graphics, we refer to [27] and [28].

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The primary goal of this thesis was to implement and simulate a series of bench-
mark test cases in the three reservoir simulators, Eclipse, COSI and MRST, and
compare the results.
We have studied a four component Compositional test case and the three Black
Oil test cases, SPE1, SPE7 and SPE9, proposed in the SPE Comparative Solu-
tion Project. The Compositional test case where also formulated and simulated
as a Black Oil problem.
Overall, the solutions computed by the simulators where in agreement and the
CSP test cases matched the published results in the original papers.
The SPE1 test case showed very high agreement between all simulators. The so-
lutions are also in agreement with the results presented in the original paper[17].
The simulation results from the SPE7 test cases (1-3)ab all showed high agree-
ment. The results for the cumulative oil production and the bottom-hole pres-
sure in the producer showed very small deviations from the mean values ob-
tained by the participants in the original project. MRST and COSI showed
high agreement with the mean value, for the test cases 4ab. The result com-
puted by Eclipse did however show a deviation of 14.33% and 18.56% for the
cumulative oil production, in the cases 4ab respectively. The reason for this
==PAGE:132==
120
Conclusion
discrepancy needs further investigation. The modeling of the friction pressure
drop in the horizontal well bore in the Eclipse simulation showed no signicant
contribution to the production rates. The reason for this could be the lack of a
realistic value of the roughness factor.
Dierences in the handling of the residual oil saturation in the calculation of
the initial state between the simulators showed some disagreement of the initial
uids in place in the SPE9 test case. This dierence resulted in a faster water
breakthrough in the COSI simulation and hence a higher water production. This
underlines the importance of background knowledge on the part of the reservoir
engineer.
The Compositional test case were simulated both as a Compositional model in
E300 and COSI and as a Black Oil model in E100 and MRST. As expected, the
results from the compositional formulation showed a high degree of agreement.
The result from the Black Oil simulation in E100 also showed good agreement,
with the compositional simulation. However, the Black Oil MRST solution did
show some disagreement, with the other solutions. This shows the importance
of choosing the correct formulation for the ow problem, as it will aect the
result.
The Black Oil formulation do however have the advantage of a signicant lower
simulation time compared to the Compositional formulation. For the composi-
tional test case, the dierence in Eclipse was approximately one order of mag-
nitude.
The trade of between precision and simulation time, can for high-resolution
reservoir models be an issue that must be considered. This especially becomes
important, when the simulation model is the basis for optimization, where the
model is simulated a number of times with dierent control settings, until a
local maximum is found.
The use of an input deck (text le), provides a fairly exible way of specifying
dierent types of ow problems, without the need of dening the actual govern-
ing equations. The direct access to the source code and the use of Automatic
Dierentiation in MRST provides the basis of quick prototyping of new ow
models. For large high-resolutions models, MRST is not a suitable choice, due
to the simulation runtime. We note that this was never the intention of the
MRST project.
The use of reservoir simulation as a tool for the decision process in the de-
velopment of a hydrocarbon reservoir requires a high theoretical background
knowledge of the reservoir engineer.
==PAGE:133==
121
Future work
To implement and work with Black Oil and Compositional models as the basis
for reservoir production optimization as a part of Closed-Loop Reservoir Man-
agement is an interesting eld. This could be NPV optimization of a reservoir
produced by water ooding, where an optimal control problem is set up using
the injection/production scheme as the control variables.
Simulation of realistic Black Oil and Compositional models under uncertainty
of physical parameters like the permeability and porosity using the ensemble
Kalman lter is another interesting eld.
Dual porosity/permeability models for ow in fractured porous media is an
obvious extension of the reservoir simulation models considered in this thesis,
since fractures are a natural phenomenon in many reservoirs.
