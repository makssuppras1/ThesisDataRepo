Abstract
The aim of this project is to design and construct a power assisting exoskeleton for the upper body.
The thesis is initiated by a review of the human anatomy and the current technology, which leads
to the design phase. In this phase, various parameters of interest and requirements are stated. This
enables the development of design proposals, from which the best candidate is selected. The selected
proposal is the focus throughout the remaining project.
A preliminary selection of actuators enables a ﬁrst arm edition to be designed and it’s properties are
used to analyse the system’s dynamic behaviour with numerical methods. The arm is simulated in
diﬀerent load scenarios where the arm handles an object with a mass of 12 kg and 17 kg. The reaction
forces and torques from the kinematic analysis are used for validating the gear and motor as well
as dimensioning the structural parts, bolt connections amongst others and reﬁne the design through
iterations. The parts are each described in detail during the dimensioning phase. The dimensioning
have proven that the parts can endure the loads which exist during the investigated arm gesticulations
with the 12 kg load in both a conservative shoulder ﬂexion and shoulder abduction scenario. For pure
ﬂexion of the forearm, the exoskeleton can increase the lifting capabilities of the arm with 17 kg. The
highest joint velocities the investigated load scenarios are 184.2◦/s in the elbow joint and 116.3◦/s for
the shoulder joints.
Lastly, the ﬁnal solution is evaluated with respect to the speciﬁed requirements, which were created
in the design phase of the project. It can be concluded that the ﬁnal weight, exclusive control system
and battery, is 11.78 kg. Furthermore, the exoskeleton can be adjusted to users within the hight range
of 1.65-1.85 m.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
An exoskeleton is an external skeleton, which is the structure of all insects. An exoskeleton in the
technical understanding is a mechanical device to be mounted on a human, in order to amplify the
force of the user or create force where there is none.
Exoskeletons have been developed as early as 1890, where the ﬁrst parallel acting exoskeleton was
developed, [1]. Early solutions focussed on increasing rehabilitation of patients and power assisting
for some users. The ineﬀecient power sources and lack of suﬃcent control systems have been some
of the main limiting factors for designing exoskeletons. The current development of batteries with
high power to weight ratio and computer technology, have provided the basis for more succesful
solutions. Today, there is an increasing focus on exoskeletons for rehabilitation of injured users and
power assisting for fully functional users, with respect to work, [2].
The solutions are in general
either aimed for the lower- or upper-limb, but a few full-body solutions is developed. A rising interest
in protecting people with heavy work and simultaneously increasing their eﬃciency, have lead to
a research in exoskeletons within both the military and industries. Commercial car companies like
Honda and Toyota are currently entering the research ﬁeld, which indicates that the commercialization
of exoskeletons is accelerating, [2].
For people working with medium heavy lifts on a daily basis, like employees in storages, factories
with assembly of machine parts and various installers, often suﬀers from back and joint issues. These
health issues appear after repeating loads in the range of 10-20 kg, which are in a weight class which
many handles without the use of of mechanical aids, as this slows the process down. This problem is
now approached by an increasing number of companies, as there is a vast marked for these solutions,
if they prove eﬃcient, safe and mimics the user’s motion to a comfortable extent.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Project Objective
This thesis focuses on the development of an upper body exoskeleton, which can enhance the lifting
capabilities of a healthy user. The aim is to increase the lifting capability of the user by at least
10 kg per arm, at speeds approximating the natural motion of a human performing the same task
without any power assist. In order to reach this goal, the problem is thoroughly examined to clarify
the extensive aspects of such a device. The overall structure of the thesis is:
 Initial clariﬁcation of the human anatomy, motions and limitations.
 Review of the current technology within the ﬁeld of exoskeletons in order to locate design chal-
lenges preliminary to the construction.
 Speciﬁcation of requirements for the exoskeleton which allows the development of proposals,
which one is selected and remains the focus of the remaining project.
 Initial selections of actuators and gear based and implementation of these in the chosen proposal
together with sensor considerations.
 Kinematic analysis of the exoskeleton with the use of numerical methods, which results in reac-
tion forces and torques during.
 Dimensioning and further construction of the components and connections, based on the loads
determined in the kinematic analysis.
 FEM analysis of a selected, essential component in order to verify it’s construction
 Final evaluation of the exoskeleton
The scope of this thesis thereby range from the initial investigations of the development, to the ﬁnal
solution prepared for the creation of blue prints. The ﬁnal solution can be seen in Figure 1.1 and 1.2.

Chapter 2
The Human Anatomy
The human anatomy is a complex combination of muscles, ligaments and bones. In order to mimic
the natural human motions with an exoskeleton, one must ﬁrst obtain an understanding of the human
anatomy. The following descriptions will be limited to primarily the shoulder complex but also include
the elbow and wrist joint.
2.1
The Bone Structure
The bone structure of the shoulder complex can be seen in Figure 2.1 and it is formed by the bones;
humerus, clavicle and scapula, [2]. The clavicle is the only bone connecting the shoulder complex
and the trunk. A shoulder complex contains the four joints: sternoclavicular (SC), acromioclavicular
(AC), scapulothoracic (ST) and the glenohumeral, joint (GH), [3], – see Figure 2.2.
The glenohumeral head is also referred to as the shoulder joint. Kinematically, the clavicle and scapular
move together with respect to the sternum (front bone in Figure 2.2), and the rib cage, thereby forming
the shoulder girdle, [4]. Hence, the active joints in the shoulder girdle are constituted by the SC, AC
and ST joint.
Figure 2.1: Shoulder complex bone structure, [5].
Figure 2.2: Joints in the shoulder complex, [5].

2.2
Movement of The Upper Limb
This section will investigated the degree of freedom, DOF, which exists in the joints of relevance for
the designing an upper body exoskeleton.
The shoulder joint – 3 DOF
The shoulder joint consists of the glenohumeral head and glenoid cavity. The majority of the bones
in the limbs are not stationary when moving a body part. A coordinate system has to be used as a
reference in order to specify the movement of a joint. Since the glenohumeral head can move when the
shoulder girdle moves, this reference is necessary. With the stationary reference to the scapular, the
glenohumeral head and glenoid cavity is in practice simulated as a ball-and-socket joint with three,
rotational degrees of freedom even though there is a slight oﬀset in the radii of 3 mm. A ball-and-socket
joint is a lower order, kinematic pair due to the surface contact. The glenohumeral joint can perform
the three rotations; ﬂexion/extension, abduction/adduction and internal/external rotation, [4], as seen
in the kinematic diagram Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Kinematic drawing of the shoulder and elbow joint, [4].
The shoulder girdle - 2 DOF
The shoulder girdle can move in three directions called elevation/depression, protraction/retraction
and upward/downward rotation with the sternum (front bone of the thorax) as reference, resulting in a
complex movement pattern. However, the upward/downward rotation is coupled with the movement
of the arm.
When lifting the arm, starting from fully stretched downwards, the motion is both
accomplished by a rotation in the glenohumeral joint and by a rotation of the shoulder girdle as
illustrated in Figure 2.4 (C), [3]. Due to the coupled motion, the humerus joint is elevated when the
arm is lifted, which complicates the situation from a kinematic point of view. The shoulder girdle
is for now deﬁned as having two independent DOF. The scapulothoric joint is a bone-muscle-bone
connection, which in fact can not be categorized as a regular joint, [2]. The scapular glides on the
back of the chest underneath muscles and skin and combined with its coupled and uncoupled motion,
variations in its relative size compared to the clavicle from person to person, makes it very hard to
deﬁne kinematically correct, [3]. Therefore, the literature often deﬁnes the whole shoulder complex as
possessing ﬁve degrees of freedom.

The elbow joint – 2 DOF
The elbow joint possess two degrees of freedom; pronation/supination and ﬂexion/extension, see Fig-
ure 2.3. Flexion and extension in the elbow joint can be modelled with an uniaxial hinge joint and the
pronation and supination can be simulated with a simple uniaxial pivot joint. Both joints are simple,
kinematic, lower order pairs due to their surface contact, [6].
The wrist joint – 2 DOF
The wrist joint contains two degrees of freedom consisting of ﬂexion/extension and radial/ulnar de-
viation. A complex bone structure in the wrist demands the rotation to be carried out around four
diﬀerent axis in an exoskeleton, in order to mimic the natural movements, [2].
Anatomic range of motion
The human joints create natural limitations for the range of motion as seen in Figure 2.5. These lim-
itations can vary from person to person, but are for a healthy, grown-up person as seen in the ﬁgure.
An exoskeleton must cope these limitations to avoid injuring the user. In this project, the primary
limitations that will be considered is ﬂexion/extension, abduction/deduction and internal/external
rotation, corresponding to limitations a, b and d in Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: The range of motion for the upper body, [7].
A fully stretched elbow joint can rotate 145◦by performing a ﬂexion [7], and from a zero-measurement
as thumb is pointing upwards, it can pronate 80◦(twisting wrist inwards) and supinate 85◦, [8].

2.4
Degrees of Freedom Considerations
Shoulder joint (glenohumeral joint)
In order for an exoskeleton to be able to mimic the natural motion of the user, it must possess the
same number of degrees of freedom as the human body. To avoid a highly complex solution, only the
most necessary joints can be actuated and the rest can either be left passive or completely neglected.
Some scenarios will be presented to understand the important degrees of freedom an exoskeleton must
possess:
1) Picking up a heavy mass requires ﬂexion/extension of the shoulder joint, but there is no need for
any motion beside the bending of the hips and knees if the object is placed far below waist level.
2) Reaching to one side with a single hand, supporting oneself when rising from a chair, or carrying
a long object that must be balanced requires a shoulder abduction/adduction.
3) Placing an object to one side relative to the torso, requires a slight internal/external rotation of
the shoulder joint combined with perhaps a ﬂexion/extension. If the user turns his torso or entire
body to face the object, then it can be handled like in 1).

It is clear that the ﬂexion/extension rotation of the shoulder joint must be actuated. A normal human
is strongest in ﬂexion/extension, weaker in abduction/adduction and weakest in internal/external ﬂex-
ion. Based on this and the motion description above, the abduction/adduction can be actuated but
also left as passive, while the internal/external rotation can be left passive with no severe consequence
for a picking phase.
The shoulder girdle (bone complex including scapular)
As previously described, the shoulder girdle can generate three motions. When the arm is raised from
a vertical downward resting position to above the head by an abduction, the motion is consisting
of several rotations. These rotations occur in both the GH, AC, and the SC joint, which results in
a bi-directional motion of the centre of the glenohumeral joint (CGH), [35]. The described motion
occurs in the frontal plane of the human body – see Figure 2.8. Researchers have described the motion
of the CGH in general, based on the following assumptions:
 Persons with equivalent body sizes possess identical CGH-movements
 The CGH-motion is not inﬂuenced by training
 Internal/external rotation has no inﬂuence of the motion of the CGH
 It is of no consequence for the CGH motion, whether the arm is elevated with abduction/ad-
duction or ﬂexion/extension or a combination of the two movements
 The bone lenghts are proportional to the body size, [35]
When motion occurs in the frontal plane around a sagittal axis, confer Figure 2.8, the z-positon of the
CGH is 0, whereas the x- and y-positions can be seen in Figure 2.9. The arm elevation angle measured
on the frontal plane for abduction is denoted θ1.
Figure 2.8: Human anatomic axis and planes, [36].
Figure 2.9: Movement of CGH in the frontal plane,

the amount of pronation/retraction will result in a horizontal distance of 91.4−108.1mm, from simple
trigonometry. However, the pronation/retraction can be neglected as a DOF in an exoskeleton, since
user can perform arm gesticulations while actively compensating for the tendency to pronate/retract.
Furthermore, the lifting capacity fades when the shoulder is pronated/retracted towards the extremi-
ties. In order to mimic the shoulder naturally for the speciﬁed range of motion, the minor degrees of
freedom should be implemented, but they are not critical for the functionality of the exoskeleton.
Elbow
The two rotations of the elbow – ﬂexion/extension and pronation/supination, are both essential to
obtain the ﬂexibilities of a human when handling objects. Flexion/extension must be actuated, while
the rotation of the forearm can be passive without severe consequence of the user’s abilities to handle
a load.

Categorization of Exoskeletons
Investigation of existing solutions are of great importance in any design proces, as this will clarify the
design challenges. The ﬁndings can save time during the design phase. In the process of understanding
the designs of existing exoskeletons, their purposes must ﬁrst be clariﬁed. In the following, current
exoskeletons are categorized with respect to their applicatons, [2].
 Rehabilitation of injured patients with regaining strength and motion, where the motion and
load can be controlled to avoid further injury. These devices are often mounted to a frame,
which simpliﬁes the solution and eliminates the necessity of a compact and light weight design,
since the actuators and energy source can be mounted on the frame.
 Assistive exoskeletons ampliﬁes the force of the user, allowing the user to use less force when
performing a task. These solutions have a wide range of applications - from improving endurance
of e.g. elders to soldiers and regular workers carrying higher loads. This type of exoskeleton
is mainly movable which demands a complete design of either a upper-/lower- or a full-body
solution.
 A haptic device is usually a frame mounted solution enable to capture the user’s motion and
transfer this to a virtual reality and likewise give a force response from the virtual reality to the
user. Instead of using the signals in a virtual world, the haptic device can be connected to a
robot, enabling e.g. a special surgeon to perform a surgery on a patient from a diﬀerent location
through a slave robot.
Exoskeletons can furthermore be categorized as acting either in series or in parallel with the human
body. A walking assistive solution acting parallel to the user will transfer the loads parallel with the
user’s legs and thereby reduce the load in the latter. In a similar solution acting in series with the user,
all the power will be transferred through the user’s legs, [1]. Especially rehabilitation devices contains
passive degrees of freedom, which means they are either free to move in a certain way or perhaps spring
loaded. Passive joints can be seen in other exoskeletons as well, with the purpose of simplifying the
solution whenever the actuation force required, can be supplied by the user. Furthermore, exoskeletons
are in general either constructed as upper-, lower- or full-body solutions.

3.3
Actuators in an Exoskeleton
For fully understanding the advantages and disadvantages of the diﬀerent exoskeletons, diﬀerent ac-
tuators will be presented and discussed in this section.
Pneumatic and Hydraulic Pistons
Pneumatic and hydraulic pistons have a high power-to-weight ratio and can be working in two di-
rections in contrast to a human muscle. Pistons require an external system to pressurize the gas or
ﬂuid, which adds mass and bulkiness to most designs. Furthermore, pressure building systems tend to
develop noise. Pistons can be mounted various positions due to the ﬂexible hoses. Forces can only be
subjected in the axial direction, since all other loadings will cause extensive wear and potential damage.
Electroactive Polymers
Electroactive polymers change their length when subjected to voltage. The polymers can contract
soundlessly in the same way as a human muscle, with a neglectable amount of friction, [31]. This
technology can prove to be the solution of creating artiﬁcial muscles, but the current levels of force,
contraction ratio and durability cannot yet compete with classical actuators, [1]. Since the human
muscles are mounted very close to the joints, a relatively small contraction will result in an extensive
movement of the attached bone. The nature of an exoskeleton moves the actuators further away from
the human joint than the muscle, which is why the electroactive polymers or similar artiﬁcial muscles
must provide a bigger contraction in order to create the same range of motion, [29].
Piezoelectric Materials
Piezoelectric components can generate a voltage when subjected to stress and they are able to create
strain when subjected to a voltage, [31]. This technology is primarily used on a small scale and is not
yet applicable in a scale relevant for exoskeletons.
Pneumatic Muscle Actuator (PMA)
Pneumatic muscle actuators were invented in the 1950’s by McKibben, [29]. In general, the actuator
work like a muscle, just actuated by air. An oriented net of non-extensible threads combined with
a rubber tube, secures a lengthwise contraction of the actuator when pressurized. The actuator is
one-way-acting, similar to a natural muscle. Advantages of the actuator include very low weight, good
compliance around curved surfaces due to high ﬂexibility and high ability to mimic the natural mus-
cles. Disadvantages of the PMA includes extensive nonlinearities in the motion-pressure relation, a
requirement of a compressor and a slightly, limited contraction ratio, [31]. A McKibben actuator with
a lod capacity of around 20 kg, can contract 20 % when pressurized, [29]. However, the requirement
of a compressor makes the combined system often bulky, noisy and relatively heavy.
Electric Motors
The most used actuators in robotics and exoskeletons are electrical motors. Advantages include high
gear ratios, precise motion control, huge availability and potentially low noise development. Disad-
vantages in relation to exoskeletons count their output of pure rotation, when only partial revolutions
are required. The majority of electric motors lack power compared to their weight and they therefore
demand large gear ratios, which introduces friction and inertia in the system, leaving many without
back-drivability. In many cases, back-drivability is an attractive ability if fast shifts in movements
are wanted, [31]. Furthermore, electric motors become bulky when combined with the necessary gear
trains.
AC-motors are relative complex regarding the speed control and are mainly used in industry providing
motions with constant speed. Therefore, these will not be described further and the focus is on the
DC-motors.
14

DC-motors can be grouped as following:
 Brushed DC-motors have the advantages of being inexpensive to purchase, easy to control and
highly reliable. Disadvantages include the requirement for maintenance, development of sparks,
noise and heat and short life time when subjected to high intensity usage, [32].
 Brushless DC-motors can be controlled precisely, are more silent and develop less heat compared
to brushless DC-motors, have low demands for maintenance, no spark development, high eﬃ-
ciencies and long lifetimes. The main disadvantages are the high cost and the demand of a more
complex motor controller, [32].
 DC step-motors can be controlled very precisely regarding both position and speed and have a
long life time. Disadvantages include the requirements of a complex controlling unit and program
and the developement of both noise and heat at high, rotational speeds, [33].
3.4
Regenerative System
In order to maximize the output of the energy source, regenerative systems can advantageously be
included in the exoskeleton. An externally, driven electric motor produces electricity since it works as
a generator. Enabling e.g. a battery to be recharged frequently whenever the arms have a motion in
the same direction as the gravitational force to drive the motor, would extend the eﬃcient working
time. Passive storage of energy in springs could help lifting the arms and in that way compensate for
the gravitational inﬂuence on the gesticulations. The regenerative systems will most likely complicate
the control of the exoskeleton, which is not a part of the project scope. Therefore, a regenerative
system will not be investigated further. However, suggestions for sensor selection and features for
controlling the exoskeleton will be discussed later in the project.

The whole construction is mounted to the back plate with a ﬁxed arm, to obtain a rigid force transfer.
This eliminates the shoulder elevation/depression, but this movement is only required when the upper
arm is reaching higher than around 120◦measured from the legs, which is close to the full range of
motion. It is assessed that an arm elevation which exceeds this range, will tend to lift the entire
exoskeleton as the shoulders rise, so the elevation can be executed if needed.
The shoulder abduction/adduction motor is connecting the rest of the arm to the shoulder. A mount
on the upper arm is ﬁxating the mechanism to the human limb with a brace and straps – the latter
are not drawn. It is possible to elongate the distance from shoulder to elbow joint to various user
sizes. A screw connection to the back plate with slots enable adjustment for a varying shoulder width.
Elbow ﬂexion/extension is actuated, which is a necessity in all proposals, since the function is essential
when lifting a weight. Pronation/supination of the forearm is carried out passively by two arm mounts
inspired from the exoskelton developed by Shaoping Bai and his team, [38], where the padded inner
parts can slide and rotate relative to the outside of the braces, see in Figure 4.7.
Figure 4.7: Proposal 4 with arrows indicating degrees of freedom.
The abduction/adduction motor is mounted directly on back arm and is linked to the arm construction
through a hinge connection with three revolute joints, which is a construction with one remote centre
of rotation.
When the shoulder performs an internal/external rotation, the axis of ﬂexion/extension and abduc-
tion/adduction change their common angle, which can be accomplished by the hinge construction,
shown in Figure 4.8.
A disadvantage of the shoulder hinge, is the loss of statically determination due to the passive degrees
of freedom. It is assessed, that even heavy weights lifted, will not demand a too high load for the
human shoulder to easily withstand the forces that can appear during the shoulder protraction/re-
traction motion. The argument is that the load in this direction will be small, as it is parallel to the
ground and do not work against gravity in normal ﬂexion/extension actions, which are the majority
of the lifting motions.
It should be noted that the exoskeleton will not provide any assistance in a conﬁguration where the
revolute joints of the hinge are orientated vertically and not horizontally as shown in Figure 4.7. This
is however a rare position for a human when handling objects since smaller muscles must perform at
this position. The design is therefore considered acceptable, since no human ordinarily will lift any
signiﬁcant weight in that position.

5.1
Sensor and Control Considerations
A control unit and sensors are necessary, if the exoskeleton should mimic the user’s motion smoothly
and continuously. A complete alignment is hard, as the responsiveness of the system must be fast and
provide the correct activation of the actuators in countless of limb conﬁgurations. The Technology
Review of the Existing Exoskeletons revealed that diﬀerent sensors are used in combinations, for fully
controlling the exoskeletons. Based on the investigations, the following sensor types would be recom-
mended if the control system should be developed.
It is decided that the user must apply a small force on the exoskeleton in order to activate it. This
imply that the user’s limbs will not be weightless, but instead be supported against load above this
activation limit. If the user attempts to lift a object which would overload the actuators, the control
system must not increase the power of the actuators above a certain threshold, which could cause
structural damages.
Exoskeletons, like the Body Extender from the Technology Review, constantly apply 10% of the
load to the user, to maintain the user’s feeling with the objects handled. Applying a constant fraction
of the load is not necessary in this case, as the user will hold the object in his hand and will feel the
extent of support from the exoskeleton based on the pressure on this arm against the brace system.
As the purpose of the exoskeleton is to assist workers, it could have negative consequences for the
users if used on a daily basis. If the user have a physical job and his upper limbs become more or less
weightless when using the exoskeleton, this would, over time, lead to a natural reduction of muscle
mass. This would demand a higher eﬀort if regular work must be carried out without the exoskeleton
in periods.
Contact Forces
A measurement of the interaction force between the user and the exoskeleton, will provide valuable
information about the load situation. If the control unit conﬁrms that the contact force on e.g. the
forearm is rising, it must imply that the user is either subjected to a load or intentionally trying to
force the arm in a certain direction.

the exact location of the arms relative to e.g. the back of the user. If there had been no passive degrees
of freedom, the angle measurements could be used for calculating the exact hand position relative to
the user, but since this is not the case, measurement of the angular position will only have a limited
relevance for the control system. When the actuator reaches it’s end position, it would be wise to slow
down the motion, to prevent the user from harming the equipment and potentially himself. A rotary
encoder could provide the necessary information in this situation.
Finally, the rotary encoders can provide the velocities of the actuators and be used to limit these,
with the aim of increasing the user’s safety. The encoders can often be mounted directly on the motor.

actuators end of motion. These contacts would either cut of the power entirely or be used to reduce
the power if the user tries to exceed the range of motion. If the contacts cuts of the power bypassing
the control unit, this would provide a safety feature towards failure of the control unit and prevent
harm of both equipment and user.
Overload Considerations
Limiting the output torques of the motors, by using the mentioned sensor inputs, provides a good
security against overload. If fast incidents happens - e.g. trying to grasp a heavy object from falling,
the load should be registered as too high. If the responsiveness of the control system is adequately
high, the power should be reduced in the actuators and if these are back-drivable, the system should
not be subjected to any overload.

Gear Selection
Various gears can be used in the exoskeleton. A harmonic drive is one of the most compact gear types
and possesses simultaneously a high gear ratio. These are used in robots and in other applications
where space is limited and zero-backlash is wanted.
A harmonic gear is generally consisting of three elements - a wave generator, a circular spline and
a ﬂex spline, as seen in Figure 5.6.
The wave generator is actually consisting of two parts.
The
inner part has an elliptical shape and is called the wave generator plug. The wave generator plug is
inserted in a bearing, which also deforms elastic in the same shape. The ﬂex spline is a torsionally
stiﬀ, high strength component with the capability of ﬂexing in the radial direction, when the inserted
wave generator rotates. The ﬂex spline has external gear teeth, which interacts with internally cut
gear teeth in the rigid circular spline. As the circular spline is cut with two teeth more than the ﬂex
spline, a relative rotation will occur during a rotation of the wave generator, [42].
Figure 5.6: The harmonic drive model, CSD-32-160-2A, [42].
The company called HarmonicDrive has a selection of extra compact devices, with zero backslash and
high torque-to-weight ratios. By examining the product categories, the relevant models narrows down
to the FB-2 pancake and the CSD-2A component set, which both can deliver a gear ratio of 1:160.
This is acceptably close to the approximate gear determination.
A further comparison shows that the CSD-2A can deliver higher torques with equal component mass,
which makes it the optimal choice. A construction goal is to obtain the highest output torque with
the lowest mass, which seems to be optimal for drives with a mass of 0.51 kg, since the next step is 1 kg.
The harmonic drive must be mounted in a gear house, which also contributes to the total weight.
Therefore, it is decided that the drive mass should not exceed 0.51 kg. The selected gear, CSD-32-
160-2A, comes in two editions - with a standard hollow bore and a big bore, where the ﬁrst is selected

Kinematic Analysis
A kinematic analysis must be carried out for the exoskeleton, in order to determine the forces and
torques that arise during use. As the exoskeleton is consisting of diﬀerent components linked to each
other forming a mechanism, the problem is investigated with the theory multibody dynamics.
The highest loadings will presumably occur when the user lifts objects in front of him with pure
ﬂexion/extension of the shoulder and elbow joints. With this reasoning, the most loaded motion will
take place in a plane parallel to the sagittal plane - see Figure 2.8. It is therefore deemed suﬃcient to
construct the kinematic analysis in 2D for the ﬂexion/extension motion. However, to investigate the
necessary torque output of the abduction/adduction motor, another 2D analysis will be made for this
motion, which occurs in a plane parallel to the frontal plane, Figure 2.8.
When the force and torque results are found, the maximum values from each of the investigated load
scenarios, will be extracted and listed in a table. This table will contain the loads which will be used
for the coming dimensioning calculations. The kinematic analysis will be solved numerically with
Matlab and the code can be found in Appendix E. The methods and notation are based on P. E.
Nikravesh’s ”Computer-Aided Analysis of Mechanical Systems”, [49].
6.0.1
Theory
Inverse Dynamic Analysis
The type of analysis required, depends on the type of initial speciﬁcations applied to the system. If
the forces of the mechanism are known, a forward dynamic analysis will provide the corresponding
motion. The aim of this project is to initially specify a motion, which the preliminary motor and
gear can be expected to deliver. This will be speciﬁed in a kinematic analysis, in which the positions,
velocities and accelerations of the system will be determined in all timesteps. Once these parameters
are known, the force and torques acting on the mechanism, can be determined. The last step is called
an inverse dynamic analysis or a kinetostatic analysis, [49].
Constraints
When diﬀerent components are connected to each other, they form a kinematic pair or a joint, which
must be described with kinematic constraint equations. Furthermore, a number of driving constraint
equations, equal to the number of degrees of freedom of the system, must be speciﬁed, [49]. The
driver constraints describe the independent coordinates, formulated as expressions which often are
time-dependant equations.
A revolute joint in 2D, will demand three constraint equations in total. Two of them must specify the
restrictions of vertical and horizontal movement, while the third is the driving constraint. The driving
constraint will in this example specify the rotational speed of the joint completing the constraints for
the joint. Once the constraint equations are formulated, these must be diﬀerentiated with respect to
time, in order to obtain the Jacobian terms, for assembling the Jacobian matrix.

The Method of Appended Driving Constraints
Generally, planar kinematic constraint equations constitute a non-linear system in terms of the coor-
dinates, q. In order to solve the equations for the unknowns, at any given time, the appended driving
constraint method can be utilized in combination of the Newton Raphson method.
In the appended driver constraint method, a set of kinematic constraints are deﬁned and then the
remaining degrees of freedom, expressed with driver constraints, are appended to the equation set.
If the number of kinematic constraints for the joints are denoted by m and the number of driving
constraints is k, then the appended method yields a total number of n = m + k constraints, which
must be solved as a function of time, [49].
Newton Raphson Method
The Newton Raphson method is an algorithm which ﬁnds approximations to the roots of functions,
with increasing accuracy through iterations. The method is based on an initial guess and can generally
be written as, [49]:
Φq(qj)∆qj = −Φ(qj)
(6.1)
qj+1 = qj + ∆qj
(6.2)
The algorithm is repeated until the error is acceptably small. The Newton Raphson method will be
used for ﬁnding the positions, q, from which the velocities and accelerations can be derived. Once the
Jacobian matrix is assembled with the velocity expressions, the values of the velocities can be found
in every time step, when solving the linear system of equations:
Φq ˙q = k1
(6.3)
The right-hand side of eq. 6.3 is obtained by a single diﬀerentiation of the constraints with respect to
time followed by a transfer of all terms, not relating to velocity, to the vector k1.
The accelerations are determined similarly, with eq. 6.4, where the constraints have been diﬀerentiated
twice with respect to time. The procedure is now repeated, but this time all the terms not related to
acceleration are moved to the new right-hand side, k2:
Φq¨q = k2
(6.4)
Final Determination of Forces and Torques
Once the position, velocity and acceleration are determined for each body in the the mechanism, the
inverse dynamic approach can be utilized. This is executed by solving the following equation system:

(6.5)

It should be noted, that placing the local coordinate systems in the mass midpoints of the bodies,
will cause the mass matrix to be a simple diagonal matrix, which will be the pratice in this project.
As the terms ΦT

Bearing Selection for the Gear Output Shaft
Another set of bearings must be implemented in the gear and motor house, in order to remove all
other forces and torques except for the turning torque of the motor from the harmonic drive. These
bearings will be subjected to radial and axial forces, together with torques perpendicular to their axis
of rotation. These torques result in under-deﬁned loadings, as the torque can either aﬀect the bearing
as a radial force, as an axial force or even as a combination of the two, which require the design to
dictate how the loads are placed.
The load case of the shafts and its bearings, are multi-directional, which increases the demands for
the bearings to a drastic level. Especially the ﬂexion/extension torque, Tz3, which is the highest load,
acts as a bending torque of the axis, as shown in Figure 7.7.

actuator unit and added bulkiness to the solution. Furthermore, the lubrication ﬁlm builds up by
hydrodynamic eﬀects at higher rotational speeds, thereby improving the bearing capacity. At low ro-

The roughness of the shaft is chosen to be 0.4 µm and the recommended tolerance of h9 is applied to
the shaft and a H7 tolerance for the bore in the house.
It should be noted that the coeﬃcient also depends on the rotational speed and the actual contact
pressure. Lubricating with grease can reduce the coeﬃcient of friction with approximately 50 %. In
order to remain conservative, it is assumed that the coeﬃcient of friction in greased condition is 0.1
for further calculations, [48].

7.7.1
Screw Dimensioning
As all the investigated parts consist of aluminium, the material data for the alloy is found in Table
5.1. The screws are made of an alloy steel with the high property class, 10.9, in accordance to DIN EN
ISO 898-1 (1999-11), with a yield strength of σy,b = 900 MPa, [50]. The theory used in this subsec-
tion is based on ”Machine Elements Analysis and Design”, [34], and ”Machine Design An Integrated
Approach”, [41].
In order to determine the number of screws or bolts needed to fasten the diﬀerent parts together,
the failure modes of a screw must be understood. There are primarily three failure modes of a screw,
caused by a tensile, shear or a torsional load. When dimensioning bolts and screws, it is common to
dimension these against failure when they are subjected to tensile load, as the parts clamped together
will not move due to the frictional contact between them. In this situation, the screws will only be
loaded in pure tension, which will be the approach of the following calculations. The failure of tor-
sional stress can also be of concern, but this failure type is connected to the frictional forces acting on
the screw, which can be reduced by lubrication of the screw before assembling the parts. Torsional
failure will therefore not be investigated further.

8.1
Finite Element Analysis
The advantage of the ﬁnite element method with respect to mechanical construction is that even
parts with a complex geometry, load and support condition can be analysed with respect to strains
and stresses. The Hinge connection is a component with an arbitrary shape, which makes it hard to
determine the stress throughout the construction analytically. However, the stress of the holes in the
Hinge and the stress on the rods which assembles the Hinges, have been investigated analytically - see
Appendix C. The FE analysis will be executed in the CAD program Solid Works.
Type of analysis
The use of the exoskeleton constantly changes the load levels and directions together with the bound-
ary conditions. The maximum values in the kinematic analysis have been extracted and multiplied
by safety factors, so these values represent the maximum load subjected to the exoskeleton. The
consequence is that no inertial forces will exceed this level and as the motion is as relatively slow, the
Hinge can be subjected investigated by a static, linear analysis, under the assumptions that:
 The material has a linear elastic behaviour
 Deformations are small compared to the component’s dimensions
 Loads are constant - both regarding magnitude and direction for the current analysis
 Boundary conditions remain unchanged for the current load scenario investigated
Element type
A 3D solid element is utilized in problems where the object of interest has an arbitrary shape, which
is applicable for the Hinge. Furthermore, the thickness of the Hinge is relatively large compared to
it’s overall dimensions, which means that shell or plate elements in this case would lead to inaccurate
results. The 3D element that will be utilized in the simulation is the 4-node tetrahedron element. A
4-node tetrahedron uses linear interpolations to ﬁnd the stress and strain in the other parts of the
element from the Gauss points. FEM-programs usually apply the smallest number of Gauss points,
that will lead to a valid solution without causing instabilities, [52].

The exoskeleton can provide the user with an extra lifting capability of 12 kg per arm, in both a very

lifting capability of 17 kg.

emergency button wired to his gloves, that can be triggered by the thump if the power must be cut.
As the harmonic gear units are back-drivable, the user will be able to move his limbs even when the
power is cut oﬀ.

drawing validations and overall design considerations which all are connected and must be performed
in some degree for every design iteration. The iterations are carried out in the amount possible within
the time frame of present the project.

Conclusion
The aim of this project was to design and dimension a power assisting exoskeleton for the upper body
of healthy users.
In order to accomplish this, the relevant human anatomy was investigated and the limitations were
clariﬁed. With a detailed understanding of the complexity of motions, the investigation of current
exoskeletons clariﬁed how others have approached the design challenges. Based on these informations,
the key factors for the design was selected and used to develop four design proposals and select the
one, which fulﬁlled the requirements best.
The sensor considerations had an inﬂuence on the chosen design, with respect to the brace system and
later on during the acceptance of various limitations for the exoskeleton. The decision of choosing a
strong aluminium alloy, 7050-T6, has made it possible to make a light and strong exoskeleton.
The initial, rough estimate of the required actuator and gear, made it possible to design a ﬁrst
edition of the exoskeleton, from which the parameters like mass moment of inertia and geometrical
distances could be used as inputs for the kinematic analysis. This analysis revealed that for certain
conﬁgurations - like scenario 2, where the hand follows a straight path, parallel and close to the body,
the elbow joint’s velocity increase substantially due to change of rotational directions of the joints.
This resulted in a reselection of the gear for the elbow joint to a smaller gearing of 1:100 instead of
1:160 for the shoulder units. The simulated scenarios were conservative with respect to the motion
and the load and yielded in results of which the maximum values were extracted and multiplied by
safety factors. These loads made it possible to validate the motor and gear, dimension adequate bear-
ings, dimension selected parts with respect to failure against plastic deformation and dimension the
screw connections of special interest. The Hinge had a shape which made it complex to determine
the strength of the component with analytic calculations. Therefore, the part was investigated with a
FEM analysis, which resulted in a design optimization of that part.
The evaluation of the ﬁnal solution showed that the exoskeleton is limiting the user’s motion to
some degree. The shoulder abduction/adduction, ﬂexion/extension and elbow ﬂexion/extension are
the actuated and primary rotations, which all have a satisfying range of motion. The forearm prona-
tion/supination is limited by the brace system. If the user wear clothes and do not tighten the braces
too much, a relative motion between the braces and the user will occur and extend the range presum-
ably equal to the natural range. The limitations of the remaining degrees of freedom in the shoulder
will only have a small eﬀect, as these can be compensated for with small changes of the grasping
procedure and are therefore deemed satisfying.
The rotational speed of the joints have proven to be comparable to the natural human motion, which
was a prioritized design requirement. Furthermore, the exoskeleton has proven capable of increasing
the lifting capabilities of the user with 12 kg with big arm gesticulations in both the frontal and

saggital human plane. For a pure ﬂexion of the elbow, the exoskeleton can provide a lift increase of
17 kg.
The exoskeleton is adjustable to user with height in the span of approximately 1.65-1.85 m. The total
weight of the exoskeleton is 11.78 kg, which is below the requirement stated in the design phase. The
weight is relatively high, but the potential maximum lift increase of 34 kg is approximately three times
higher. Based on this and the fact that the solution satisﬁes the initial speciﬁcation of requirements,
the solution is assessed as an attractive exoskeleton for power assisting purposes.

Chapter 11
Future Work
The exoskeleton must be further prepared before a functional model can be produced and tested. If
the weight of 11.78 kg must be further decreased, the parts with high safety factors can be reduced.
This must however be executed with caution, as not all possible load scenarios are investigated in the
project which can be reﬂected in some safety factors. An expansion of the investigated load scenarios
can improve the certainty for, whether a speciﬁc part dimension can be reduced.
Blue prints must be made in order to manufacture the individual parts.
In order to distribute the forces comfortably to the user, a harness with straps and paddings must be
mounted to the back plate, presumable in combination of an increased back support as described on
a conceptual level in the thesis.
The control system of the exoskeleton must be developed and the appropriate sensors selected. As
the exact amount of sensor signals can be hard to determine before the production, a prototype must
be manufactured where the various sensors can be tested.
