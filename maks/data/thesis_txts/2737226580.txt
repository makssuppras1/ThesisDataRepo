Thesis - MSc Sustainable Energy
Abstract
The contemporary landscape of global energy demands needs a comprehensive understanding
of renewable energy trends, with solar energy emerging as a pivotal player in the global energy
matrix. This thesis investigates the economic and environmental impacts of solar energy policies
in Spain through an empirical analysis of policies implemented to foster solar energy production.
Leveraging freely available data sources, the thesis conducts an in-depth exploration of the solar
technologies promoted by these policies, further enhancing our understanding of the measures
taken to drive sustainable energy practices. The analysis reveals that solar energy policies
have significantly contributed to increase solar energy production, leading to notable economic
benefits such as reduced energy costs. Furthermore, it is studied how these policies impact
environmental sustainability by analysing changes in air pollution levels. The results indicate
that financial incentives and subsidies are crucial in promoting solar energy production, while
regional disparities and regulatory changes play significant roles in influencing the effectiveness
of these policies.

Introduction
In the context of the global shift towards renewable energy, understanding the specific effects
of policy measures on solar energy production is crucial for several reasons. First, solar en-
ergy represents a pivotal element in reducing greenhouse gas emissions and mitigating climate
change. The shift from using fossil fuels to adopting sustainable energy sources is essential
for achieving international climate goals, as outlined by the Paris Agreement [1]. Second, the
economic implications of solar energy policies, including job creation, technological innovation,
and energy security, are profound. These policies can drive significant economic benefits by
fostering new industries and reducing dependency on imported fossil fuels.
This thesis builds upon the existing body of research on solar energy policies by examining their
specific impacts within the Spanish context. Spain has experienced notable fluctuations in its
solar energy sector due to various policy shifts. Previous studies have highlighted the initial
success of the feed-in tariff (FIT) system in boosting solar energy production, followed by a
period of instability due to abrupt policy changes. For instance, [2] in 2012 documented the
rapid expansion and subsequent decline of the Spanish solar PV market, emphasizing the role of
regulatory frameworks and financial incentives. Also in 2012, [3] provided a detailed analysis of
the FIT design elements and their implications, offering valuable lessons for cost-efficient solar
PV deployment. Later, in 2020, [4] further explored the financial effects of these institutional
changes, noting a significant weakening of the sector and a decrease in investor confidence.
Additionally, [5] in 2021 examined the broader impacts of renewable energy policies on energy
markets and the potential for policy-induced market distortions.
This study includes regression analysis to quantify the impact of factors such as electricity
prices, energy policy changes (taxes), financial incentives (subsidies), regional disparities, and
regulatory changes on solar energy production in Spain. By applying econometric methods

Literature Review
In this section, previous studies that are most closely related to this work are presented.
According to [7], government subsidies have played a significant role in increasing the demand
for solar panels. This demand expansion is particularly evident in major markets such as the
United States, Germany, Japan, and China, where substantial subsidies have been provided to
support solar energy production. The subsidies not only increased demand but also encouraged
solar panel manufacturers to invest in innovation, leading to significant improvements in techni-
cal efficiency and reductions in production costs. A noteworthy finding from the research in
[7], which implies that current subsidies can have long-lasting effects, is that the benefits of
innovation, induced by consumer subsidies, persist even after the subsidies are phased out.

In the study [7], it is also emphasised that the effects of national subsidies on innovation can
have international spillovers, with innovations in one country benefiting other countries as well.
To conclude, the analysis suggests that while consumer subsidies have significantly benefited
the solar industry, the optimal strategy to combat climate change may necessitate a blend of
different approaches beyond subsidies, including support for research and development and
potentially a coordinated international approach to subsidy design.
Another work outlined in [8] offers a focused analysis of the California Solar Initiative (CSI) [9],
one of the most significant solar subsidy programs in the United States. The study found that
upfront rebates offered through the CSI significantly boosted the adoption of residential solar
installations. An increase in rebates from $5600 to $6070 was predicted to increase installations
by 10%, with the analysis suggesting that 53% fewer installations would have occurred without
any subsidies.
The study also provides an economic analysis of the CSI, revealing that of the $440 million
in rebates distributed, about $121 million were identified as "rents," meaning they went to
installations that would have been completed even without the financial incentives [8]. This
suggests that while the program was effective in promoting solar energy production, a portion
of the subsidies may have been allocated to projects that did not need them to be financially
viable. Consequently, it points the need for careful consideration of subsidy amounts to ensure
that funds are directed towards projects that really require financial support to be realised.

3.1
Global renewable energy trends
Historically, renewable energy was the cornerstone of energy generation, with biomass, wind,
and hydropower being the primary sources. The discovery of fossil fuels shifted the focus
towards more energy-dense sources, relegating renewables to a secondary status for centuries.
However, the late 20th century marked a turning point, as the finite nature of fossil fuels and
their environmental repercussions became undeniable as the sector accounts for approximately
60% of total emissions [12]. This period witnessed a renewed interest in renewable energy
technologies (RETs), propelled by innovations, policy support, and a growing consciousness of
their environmental benefits [13].
Electricity generation from renewable sources has experienced remarkable growth due to ad-
vancements in technology that reduce costs [12], with solar thermal and photovoltaic technolo-
gies leading the way. These technologies offer a promising alternative for both large-scale power
generation and meeting the demands of domestic and small-scale applications [13].

solar, and other renewables like waste, geothermal, wave, and tidal energy. Particularly in recent
years, Figure 1 shows that while the use of coal has either plateaued or slightly decreased, the
use of natural gas continues to grow. The use of oil remains relatively minor in comparison to
other sources. Nuclear energy shows a stable contribution, with a slight increase in the last few
years.

3.2
Residential solar PV energy in Spain
Residential solar photovoltaic (PV) systems in Spain represent a significant opportunity for the
nation to advance its clean energy goals and reduce its greenhouse gas emissions. The European
Commission’s renewable energy targets for 2030 have catalyzed Spain to assess its potential to
meet these objectives, particularly through the adoption of rooftop PV systems in the residential
sector.
Geographically, Spain’s diverse landscape presents both opportunities and challenges for the
deployment of residential rooftop PV. Rural areas, with their single-family homes, offer greater
potential for PV installation due to the availability of ample rooftop space. Conversely, urban
areas are characterised by multi-story buildings that limit available rooftop area, presenting a
constraint for large-scale PV deployment [17]. See Figure 3.
From an economic perspective, the existing surplus reimbursement system in Spain reduces the
financial returns of PV installations [17]. This indicates the need for stronger regulations to
fully utilize all residential rooftop spaces. Additionally, residential rooftops in Spain may not be
sufficient to meet the existing power requirements. This highlights the necessity for adaptable,
location-specific strategies and the potential contribution of energy communities [17].

Residential solar thermal energy in Spain
Solar thermal energy represents a big impact in reducing reliance on traditional energy sources

3.4
Electricity market in Spain
The electricity market in Spain, particularly for large industrial consumers, is a crucial aspect of
the country’s industrial competitiveness within the European internal energy market. Spain’s
limited electricity interconnection capacity with the rest of Europe has been a significant factor
in keeping its market prices bigger than in many other countries. This situation affects the
competitiveness of energy-intensive industries due to higher increased energy bill expenses [24].
The existing four high-voltage lines lines connecting Spain and France, which are the primary
routes for electricity exchange with the broader European market, have historically been insuffi-
cient to allow for the free flow of electricity [25]. Building new cross-border transmission lines
is not only technically challenging, given the need to traverse the Pyrenees, but also expensive
and subject to lengthy planning, environmental considerations, and political negotiations [24].
The introduction of the European Union’s internal energy market aimed to ensure the free move-
ment of energy across the EU without technical or regulatory obstacles, promoting efficiency
[26]. However, the goal of price convergence has been hampered in countries like Spain, where
geographical and infrastructure limitations have led to less market integration and higher prices
for industrial consumers [24], [27]. Consequently, Spain’s ability to trade electricity with the
European system accounts for just 3% of its total generation capacity, rendering it an "electrical
island" with a pressing need to strengthen connections with neighbouring systems [28].
The support mechanisms for large electricity consumers in Europe, including Spain, are designed
to mitigate the previously mentioned impact of higher energy costs. These mechanisms are
implemented at both the national and European levels, with the aim of levelling the field for
industries that are significant consumers of electricity [24].

Specifically, in Spain, the main support mechanism for large industrial consumers has been
the interruptibility demand management service. This service, managed by the transmission
system operator, is designed to provide an efficient response to potential emergency situations,
thereby minimising the effects on the safe operation of the electricity system. However, recent
years have seen a reevaluation of this mechanism, with a focus on reducing overall costs for
consumers [29]. This has led to changes such as reducing the minimum blocks of supply that
can participate in the service and lowering the starting prices for auctions [24].
Additionally, Spain is planning to implement new support mechanisms for large electricity-
intensive consumers. This includes compensation for indirect CO2 costs, which is a mechanism
already in use across the EU that does not provide a competitive advantage over others. There’s
also proposed compensation for financing renewables, high-efficiency cogeneration, and cov-
ering extra costs in non-mainland territories. A notable initiative is the establishment of a
fund to cover public coverage of forward contracts and guarantees issued by CESCE (Spanish
Export Credit Insurance Company) for power purchase agreements (PPAs) with renewable
electricity generation units. This fund will allow electricity-intensive consumers to secure energy
at predefined prices in the long term, reducing risks associated with fluctuations in wholesale
electricity prices [24].
However, achieving competitive energy prices in the long term will require continued efforts to
increase interconnection capacity, further integrate the European electricity market, and imple-
ment supportive regulatory measures that enhance industrial competitiveness while ensuring
environmental sustainability [24], [30].

3.5
Solar energy policies in Spain
During the 2008–2012 economic crisis on Spain’s photovoltaic sector, the drastic reduction in
feed-in tariffs led to market stagnation. The legislative framework of the Spanish PV sector has
undergone significant transformations, particularly highlighted in the period from the early 2000s
through the subsequent years, which can be broadly categorised into two phases: expansion and
contraction [5]. The timeline of these regulations is summarised in Figure 5.
The expansion phase was notably initiated with the enactment of Royal Decree (RD) 436/2004
[32], which aimed to consolidate various regulations concerning renewable energies. This decree
was pivotal in defining a remuneration framework conducive to renewable energy production,
especially photovoltaic energy, by introducing a dual remuneration system that allowed produc-
ers to either exchange their electricity output with power companies or sell it directly on the
daily market.
The sector received a further boost with RD 661/2007 [33], which continued the dual remunera-
tion model but with significant enhancements. It established a grassroots system that ensured

The decisive blow came with RD-L 1/2012 [38], which indefinitely halted the tariffs, premiums,
and supplements system, significantly impacting the profitability and investment attractiveness of
the sector. This led to a marked decline in the growth and investment in the Spanish photovoltaic
sector.
The so-called "sun tax" in Spain, officially regulated under RD 900/2015 [39], was not a tra-
ditional tax but a series of access tolls and charges. It applied to those generating their own
electricity, particularly via solar installations, requiring them to contribute to the costs of the
general electrical system, including infrastructure and conventional energy generation cost.
This tax made solar less economically attractive, adding significant costs to solar investments.
Charges varied based on the system’s output and consumption balance.
In the wake of these legislative changes, the sector experienced stagnation, RD-L 15/2018 [40]
emerged as a turning point, simplifying processes for self-consumption facilities, allowing finan-
cial reimbursement for excess clean energy, and abolishing the "sun tax." This legislative shift
appears to signal a new phase of growth for the Spanish PV sector, driven by more favourable
conditions for large-scale investments and a renewed focus on renewable energy within Spain’s
broader energy policy framework.
After 2018, the Spanish government has continued to evolve its policy landscape to support
and regulate the growth of renewable energy, particularly photovoltaic energy. RD 244/2019
[41] was a significant legislative step forward, establishing the technical, administrative, and
financial conditions for self-consumption of electricity, as well as to be compensated for the
excess energy produced and fed into the grid. This decree aimed at simplifying the process
for both individual and shared self-consumption installations, promoting the deployment of
solar PV systems among households, businesses, and communities. It represented a move to-

For each kWh drawn from the grid by the self-consumer in 2019, only up to a certain ratio

RD 647/2020 [42] significantly advanced the regulatory structure for the renewable energy

procedures, making the regulatory pathway more straightforward. The decree also offered flexi-
bility by allowing extensions for provisional operational notifications under certain conditions,
facilitating a smoother transition from planning to operational stages for projects. Collectively,
these measures aimed at reducing administrative burdens and clarifying regulatory pathways,

Methods
The method used is similar to the one followed in the work in 2022 [6], which involves a detailed
empirical analysis to understand the impact of solar energy policies on solar PV installations.
This analysis is conducted by considering various factors such as financial incentives, electric-
ity prices, and the distinction between retrofit and new-build installations. The study applies
econometric models to quantify the effects of these factors on the solar energy production.
This approach can be adapted to study the economic impact of energy policies on solar energy
production in Spain by focusing on the corresponding influencing factors and employing similar
econometric techniques.
This study evaluates the impact of solar energy policies, electricity prices, financial incentives,
regional disparities, and regulatory changes on solar energy production in Spain using a compre-
hensive panel dataset covering 17 regions from 2015 to 2020. Recognizing the complexities and
multifaceted influences on solar energy outputs, it employs a methodological approach capable
of discerning the precise effects of energy policies, market prices, and environmental factors on
solar energy production.
To address potential endogeneity issues, such as omitted variable bias or simultaneity between
the predictors and solar energy production, the instrumental variable (IV) method is employed.
Endogenity may particularly arise from the reciprocal relationship between solar energy pro-
duction and energy prices. Increased solar energy output could influence market energy prices,
which in turn might affect further solar energy production decisions.

An instrumental variable must satisfy two key conditions: it must be exogenous, meaning it is
not correlated with the error term of the regression equation, and it must be strongly correlated
with the outcome, in this case, the lagged energy price. The instrumental variable should not
directly affect the main dependent variable, which is solar energy production. In this study,
environmental taxes are used as the instrumental variable. They are considered exogenous since
they are determined by external policy decisions rather than by the state of the solar energy
market. Additionally, they are strongly correlated with energy prices, thereby helping to isolate
the impact of energy prices on solar energy production from other confounding factors [54].

The use of lagged energy prices (LagEnergyPriceit−1) serves multiple purposes in the model.
First, it helps solve the problem where current solar energy production might affect current
energy prices, making it difficult to determine which one is causing changes in the other. By
using the previous year’s energy prices, risk of reverse causality is reduced. This means it can be
better seen how past prices influence current solar energy production. For example, if electricity
prices go up, people might decide to install solar panels to save money, but this takes time. By
looking at last year’s electricity prices, it can be studied how these past prices affect solar energy
production today.

Second, using lagged energy prices takes into account the delayed response of households to
changes in energy prices. Households and producers need time to react to price changes, like
installing solar panels. This approach gives a more accurate picture of how past energy prices
impact current solar energy production, recognizing that decisions are influenced by previous
price signals rather than immediate changes.

To execute a two-stage least squares (2SLS) regression in Python, the statsmodels package
is used, which offers comprehensive support for various econometric techniques, including
instrumental variable (IV) regressions. This approach addresses the endogeneity of the lagged
energy price by using environmental taxes as the instrument in the first stage and then using the
predicted values from this stage in the second stage regression.
Five distinct econometric models have been developed to address various complexities and inter-
actions within the data. These models incrementally integrate more sophisticated econometric
techniques. The goal is to progressively control for endogeneity and omitted variable biases that
might skew the analyses.
The first model serves as the baseline, where only non-instrumented energy prices are considered
in the regression analysis. This model provides initial insights into how solar energy production
is influenced by energy prices, treated as if they were exogenous. In the second model, potential
endogeneity concerns regarding energy prices are addressed through the use of instrumental
variables. This method helps to isolate the causal impact of energy prices on solar energy output
by removing biases associated with simultaneous causality or omitted variables that influence
both prices and production, considering energy prices without subsidies. In the third model,
subsidies are included to test the role of subsidies in influencing solar energy production while
still addressing the endogeneity of energy prices. Finally, model 4 expands upon the previous
models by incorporating fixed effects and additional covariates, addressing not only the endo-
geneity of energy prices but also accounting for unobserved heterogeneity across regions and
time. Additionally, a model 5 is included where subsidies enter the equation as a dummy variable.

The ambiguity in the effects of solar thermal policies in Spain is underscored by the minimal
production of solar thermal energy in many regions. Additionally, the lack of extensive infras-
tructure utilising solar thermal energy further complicates the assessment of policy impact and
effectiveness.

7
Discussion and conclusion
The findings of this thesis highlight the complex interplay between policy measures, economic
outcomes, and environmental impacts in the context of solar energy in Spain.
The Russian-Ukrainian conflict has led to a substantial decrease in Russian gas supplies, which
previously supplied a significant share of the EU’s energy requirements. This reduction has
forced the EU to diversify its energy sources rapidly, expanding the utilization of alternative
energy options and enhancing gas supplies from regions outside of Russia [28]. The conflict’s
impact is particularly evident in the observed energy market dynamics in 2022, see Table 8,
where increased energy prices and supply chain disruptions affected renewable energy projects,
including solar energy production. These factors contributed to the observed decline in solar
energy production during this period. However, the subsequent regulatory changes and financial
54
==PAGE:58==
Thesis - MSc Sustainable Energy
incentives in 2023 significantly boosted solar energy production, reflecting the resilience and
adaptability of the energy sector in response to external shocks.
The regression analysis provided insights into the determinants of solar energy production in
Spain. Model 1, using ordinary least squares (OLS) regression, showed that the lagged energy
price did not significantly impact solar energy production. However, subsidies, although showing
an insignificant effect in this model, were found to be significant in subsequent models. The
low R-squared value in Model 1 indicated that other factors not included in the model might
influence solar energy production.
Model 2 addressed the potential endogeneity of energy prices by using taxes as instrumental
variables. The results showed a significant negative effect of the instrumented lagged energy
price on solar energy production, suggesting that higher energy prices, when adjusted for taxes,
negatively impact solar energy production. However, the model explained a small portion of the
variance in solar energy production.
Model 3 included subsidies in addition to the instrumented energy prices, finding that subsidies
had a statistically significant positive effect on solar energy production. This shows that even
small increments in subsidies can positively influence solar energy outputs, encouraging the
production of solar energy.
Model 4 further included control variables such as year and region to account for other factors
impacting solar energy production. This model emphasized the significant positive impact
of regulatory changes and financial incentives, particularly from 2019 onwards, due to the
implementation of policies that simplified procedures and provided compensation for surplus
energy.

==PAGE:59==

Model 5 introduced subsidies as a dummy variable, indicating the presence or absence of
subsidies. This model found a strong positive effect of the presence of subsidies on solar
energy production, contrasting with the smaller effect observed when subsidies were treated as
continuous variables. The model underscored the importance of subsidies in promoting solar
energy production and the effectiveness of government incentives in driving the growth of the
renewable energy sector.
For solar thermal energy, the study concluded that the policies had minimal impact on production
due to the limited infrastructure and utilization of solar thermal energy in many regions. The
lack of extensive infrastructure and targeted subsidies for solar thermal energy compared to
photovoltaic energy further complicated the assessment of policy effectiveness.
Regarding the environmental impact, the initial regression analysis (Model A) showed that solar
energy production did not have a statistically significant impact on PM2.5 concentrations in
Madrid from 2015 to 2023. This indicated that other factors, such as industrial emissions, traffic
emissions, weather conditions, and other forms of energy production, played a more significant
role in determining air quality.
However, the more comprehensive Model B, which included economic variables (energy prices,
taxes, and subsidies) and fixed effects for region and year, provided deeper insights. The results
revealed that subsidies, aimed at increasing solar energy production, were positively associated
with PM2.5 concentrations, though this result was contradictory and might be influenced by
other confounding factors. The significant negative yearly fixed effects suggested a general
improvement in air quality over time, potentially due to broader environmental regulations and
technological advancements.
This study has several limitations. Firstly, the methodology does not differentiate between retrofit

and new-build installations of solar PV systems as in [6], which could provide more nuanced
insights into the impacts of different policy measures. Secondly, the study focuses primarily
on production data and does not consider the impact of policies on solar energy consumption.
Thirdly, the environmental impact analysis is limited to PM2.5 concentrations in Madrid, which
may not fully capture the broader environmental implications of solar energy production across
different regions. Additionally, the available data limits the temporal scope to the period from
2015 to 2020, potentially overlooking long-term trends and effects.
Future research could address these limitations by examining how solar energy policies affect
both production and consumption of solar energy. Specifically, analyzing consumption data
could provide insights into household behaviour and energy usage patterns. Additionally, studies
could also explore the impact of solar energy policies on different types of households, consider-
ing factors such as income levels, urban versus rural settings, and household size. Expanding the
environmental impact analysis to include other pollutants, regions, and additional variables like
weather conditions and traffic emissions would provide a more comprehensive understanding of
the environmental benefits of solar energy production. Finally, extending the temporal scope of
the study could help capture long-term impacts and trends, providing a more robust evaluation
of solar energy policies.
To sum up, the study concluded that while taxes influence the broader energy market by affecting
prices, subsidies directly promote solar energy production by reducing financial barriers and
incentivizing investment. The dual approach of managing taxes and providing subsidies is
crucial for mitigating the adverse effects of external shocks and ensuring sustained growth in
the renewable energy sector. The discussion emphasized the resilience of the energy sector in
adapting to external shocks like the Russian-Ukrainian conflict and the critical role of supportive
frameworks and financial incentives in fostering renewable energy development.
