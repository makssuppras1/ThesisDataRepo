Abstract
In this work iron electro deposited nickel cathodes are investigated for
hydrogen evolution reaction under commercial industrial alkaline conditions
(30% KOH, 80°C) with an applied current density of -800 mA/cm2. A increase
in cell eciency associated with higher iron-ion concentration in the elec-
trolyte was observed. A rst order rate law was realized for the electro depo-
sition of iron on the nickel substrate and its associated reaction rate constant
and half-life was determined. Iron phases grew into needle-like and hexagonal-
like structures identied by scanning electron microscopy. Both Fe0, Fe2+ and
Fe3+ was identied by x-ray photoemission spectroscopy. Both face-centered
cubic and body-centered cubic lattices was identied by transmission electron
microscopy. The iron-oxide phases are proposed as maghemite (Fe2O3) and
magnetite (Fe3O4).

Introduction
At a certain point in time one particular homo sapiens in the African region decided to
utilize stone tools to harvest food which is where the material exploration began. From
that point in time and forth a rapid exploration of new and improved tools have been re-
lentlessly developing. Today, material science plays a fundamental role in this exploration.
In material science the relationships between properties and structures of materials are
examined and understood for the engineer to further develop functional materials used in
vast amount of technologies. Therefore, material sciences partly lays the foundation for
improvement of life on Earth. The exploration of vital engineered materials have made
advancements in several very dierent elds up until present times like medicine and
health, national security, information technology, aerospace, telecommunications, trans-
portation, agriculture, textiles, plastics, and the environment and will hopefully continue
so. Particularly, a general consensus for environmentally friendly solutions has spread
as the environment is of great concern today in the modern society. Therefore, further
explorations and inventions of material sciences are of great importance in regards to the
future of humanity.
1.1
Electrolysis & the Green Energy Transition
As the global population and the average living standards continues to increase the energy
demand will follow through. Historically, the human specie has overcome this problem
by means of technology. The problem however, has become more complex now that it
is not just a matter of supplying the energy demand. It is now also critical how the
energy demand is supplied. The global energy consumption from 1980 and projected
into 2030 can be seen in g. 1(a). In fact, the energy demand is forecasted to double by
2050 and triple when we exit the 21th century [1]. It is therefore both a political and
public concern to meet both future energy demands and the possible environmental con-
sequences that may be caused as a result of how the global population decides to solve
the rst problem. Scientists and engineers have been and still are searching for green
energy solutions to these problems such as hydrogen, methane or ammonia production
to better accommodate the usage of renewable energies such as solar, wind, geothermal
or hydropower solutions.

1.2
Commercialized Water Electrolysis and the Iron Problem
The amount of publications on electrolysis has increased signicantly during the last
couple of decades g. 2(b). While much of the work has been unspecied in terms of
electrolysis technique, a emerging tendency for more specialized research is growing. Al-
kaline water electrolysis (AWE) is the most established commercialized technology [9]
with the operating temperature of 80-90 °C and pressure of 15 bar (referred to as AEL in
g. 2(b)). A typical commercial alkaline electrolysis system and cell design is shown in
g. 3(a) (the fundamental principles of the cell will be described in section 2.1). The sys-
tem contains of many pipelines which are usually made of stainless steel alloys containing
many dierent elements. One element which is particularly abundant in stainless steel is
iron. With this in mind and the fact that alkaline solutions are very corrosive it is only
expected that iron with time will dissolve from the pipelines into the electrolyte. It has
been shown that iron impurities has a catalytic eect on the anode responsible for the
oxygen evolution reaction (OER) [10] due to decreasing the over-potential of the Nickel
electrodes. However, for the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) on the cathode it is still
discussed whether iron impurities are activating or deactivating the Nickel electrode [11].
The consensus however, is that iron deposits on the cathode surface and forms dendrite
like structures which increases the surface area and hence activating. However, the cat-
alytic activity of iron is slightly lower than Nickel [11] so what the summed up result is
can be hard to predict.

iron deposited Nickel substrates will be analysed through various techniques in order to
determine the identity of each specie in conjunction.

2.3
Resistances in a Electrolysis Cell
In section 2.2 a theoretical ideal system was considered. The cell potential of real com-
mercial cells is always larger than the thermo-neutral potential given in eq. (8). The
additional potentials are typically referred to as overpotentials or overpotential and are
divided into three categories:
η = ηohm + ηact + ηconc = ηohm + ηact,fuel + ηact,O2 + ηconc,fuel + ηconc,O2
(9)
The ohmic overpotential ηohm corresponds to resistances in the cell, the resistance in the
electrodes, the external electric wiring, connections etc. The concentration overpoten-
tial ηconc corresponds to resistances in regards to ionic transfer in the electrolyte. An
electrolyte with high ionic conductivity will yield low concentration overpotentials. This
overpotential also depends on the anode-cathode distance, the conductivity of the di-
aphragm and gas bubbles formations in the electrolyte. Hydrogen and oxygen gas is
generated at the surfaces of the cathode and the anode, respectively as shown in g. 4(a).
Initially, when the gas bubbles are generated the enclosed volume is not large enough for
the buoyant force following Archimedes' Principle, to overcome the high surface tension
of water. Also, the solubility of gases in any liquid tends to decrease with increased tem-
perature and decreased pressure. However, when the gas bubbles start to coalescence, the
buoyant force will allow the bubbles to rise. These bubbles at the electrolyte-electrode
interfaces will inhibit a certain amount of the electrode active areas.
The activation
overpotential ηact corresponds to the electrodes ability to transfer charges across its inter-
faces. This can be tuned by adding catalytic layers on the electrodes and will be covered
in section 2.5.

Electrodeposition in Electrolysis
Approaches for nano-material synthesis can be divided into the "top-down" and "bottom-
up" categories. The "top-down" approach utilises techniques that involve cutting, milling,
etching in order to design material according to desired orders and shapes. Examples
could be such as laser beam-processing, lithography and mechanical techniques. The
"bottom-up" approach utilises molecules chemical properties for self-assembly into, hope-
fully, useful materials. Electrochemical deposition ts into the "bottom-up" category as
material is dissolved into the electrolyte as ions and attracted to a electrode surface to
eventually deposit.

In 1986 Rilay and Moran [22] were able to identify a curious relationship between
iron impurity concentration in 30 wt% KOH electrolyte, 37 °C and Ni electrodes by ap-

Initially, they found a strong potential-time behaviour at 250 mA/cm2 which was not re-
ported from commercial parties. Then they performed experiments at this current with
0.03 ppm and 3 ppm dissolved iron which showed that only the strong potential-time
behaviour will occur at low iron concentrations.
In 1991 Brossard and Huot [23] conducted a in-situ electrochemical experiment on several
cathodes for which Ni was one of them in 30 wt% KOH with added 0.25mM Fe and 0.25
mM Mo. Cathodic polarization was performed for 75000 s at 70 °C and 100 mA/cm2.
They found that Fe was electrocatalytic while Mo was detrimental to this eect. They
performed the experiment several times where they removed the cathode at -900, -850 and
-150 mVHg/HgO and recorded SEM images of each cathode. This showed a clear change
in morphology of deposits and by EDS they were able to quantify that with increasing
potential, the Fe content increased on the surface while Mo and Ni decreased.
In 2006 Mauer et al. [24] showed that Iron prevents deactivation of Nickel electrodes
through a surface interaction eect in alkaline electrolysis in addition to increasing the
surface area.
Bossard et al. [25] performed electrodeposition of Fe on Ni cathodes at 2000 mA/cm2
in a 30 W/O KOH electrolyte at 70 °C. The Fe ion concentration is 14 ppm and by
SEM they nd needle like structures on the cathode surface after 16 hours suggesting the
morphology of deposits is time-dependent.

3.2.4
Scanning Electron Microscopy & Energy Dispersive X-ray Analysis
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a technique that generates images of sample
surfaces by scanning the surface with high energy beam of electrons. When the beam of
electrons hit the sample surface three dierent signals can be collected corresponding to
secondary electrons, backscattered electrons and characteristic X-rays. These signals can
be used to get information about the surface topography, composition etc. SEM is not
exactly a surface technique as the probing depth is approximately two microns.
In a typical SEM, the electron beam passes some scanning-coils in the electron column
to the nal lens. The electron column then deects the beam horizontally and vertically
generating scans in a raster fashion over the sample surface. Electronic devices are used
to detect and amplify the signals and display them as an image on a cathode ray tube
in which the raster scanning is synchronized with that of the microscope. The image
displayed is therefore a distribution map of the intensity of the signal being emitted from
the scanned area of the specimen.
Non-conducting surfaces are generally not able to generate SEM images. However,
with specialized SEM instruments such as eld emission gun (FEG) SEM operated at
low potential, high vacuum or at low vacuum, high potential it is possible to get images.
The characteristic X-ray is used in what is often referred to as Energy Dispersive X-ray
Analysis (EDX/EDS) from which near surface elements can be identied and their mutual
distribution estimated.
In present work images are collected using a Zeiss Sigma 300 eld emission SEM with
a resolution of 1 nm at 15kV and a FEI Quanta FEG 250 SEM with a resolution of 2 nm
at 30 kV. Both setups are equipped with EDS detectors.

Conclusion
In present paper it was found that increasing the iron-ion concentration in the electrolyte
yield a higher cell eciency which is due to a increase in eective surface area. Core-
shell nanoparticle needle- and hexagonal-like structures of Fe0, Fe2+ and Fe3+ species was
identied on the cathode surface which may be associated with metallic iron, Fe2O3 and
Fe3O4, respectively.
Additionally, the electro deposition of iron on the nickel substrate was identied as a
rst order reaction and the associated rate constant and half-life time is determined.
While the iron-oxide phases were inconclusive in terms of their mutual presence under-
neath the initial deposition layers metallic iron had a conclusive increase in abundance
below initial layers of deposits suggesting that the oxidation of deposited iron is a sub-
sequent mechanism. Potassium was found to co-deposit on the nickel substrate which
potentially causes the iron lattice to attempt segregation of potassium identied as streak-
ing in transmission electron microscopy. Ni-oxide phases present on the cathode are NiO
and/or NiOOH while Ni(OH)2 was deemed completely absent.
The ndings of present paper is relevant for further research within the eld of com-
mercial alkaline electrolysis and in turn a step in the direction of a more sustainable
future with solid green energy solutions.

Future Work
For future work the following approaches are suggested:
 Bandwidth calibration of electrochemical cell to minimize output noise.
 For XPS measurements one could increase the binding energy range for Fe to get a
more accurate background model estimation.
 XPS and Raman spectroscopy using standards as reference to accurately conrm
the presence of maghemite (Fe2O3) shell and magnetite (Fe3O4) core.
 Or consider XPS valence band analysis to discriminate between maghemite and
magnetite due to the former being a semiconductor material while the latter is a
semi-metal.
 HRTEM to qualitatively determine that particle is indeed a maghemite shell and
magnetite core with metallic iron nano particles on the surface.
