Abstract
The work of this thesis focuses on investigating when app-fatigue occurs and for whom based on
three different Free-to-Play smartphone games; Candy Crush, Candy Crush Soda, and Pokémon GO.
The dataset used in this study spans over four years and contains approximately 10,000 distinct users
for each game. The users’ time series are divided into streaks, which is an active period of no or short
interruptions in the game. To investigate app-fatigue three different methods are applied; k-means
algorithm to discover game patterns within the streaks, gradient boosting model to predict whether
a streak is the users’ last, and survival analysis to detect the impact on the factors influencing the
survival probability. It is discovered that younger users and male users are the first to experience
app-fatigue. A possible explanation for this is novelty seeking. When a user experience app-fatigue
their last streak tends to be intensive meaning that it is either long or the usage within the game
is high. Furthermore, a long inactive time between streaks encourages the streak to be the last.
Future work should be devoted to exploring the hypothesis that users experiencing app-fatigue first
is explained by novelty seeking tendencies.

Introduction
In recent years the smartphone has become an essential personal device, which supports us in almost
every accepts of our lives (Cao and Lin 2017). By 2022, approximately 6.648 billion people own a
smartphone world-wide. This means, that around 84% of the world’s population owns a smartphone
- a number that is expected to increase even further over the coming years (Turner 2022). As the
number of smartphones increases, so does the number of apps. In 2021 there were 230 billion app
downloads world-wide. We spend on average most time on social media apps and least time on
entertainment apps (Mobile 2022). These different categories engage us in various ways. Some
apps, like social media, we tend to use for a long time, whereas apps in other categories we only
use for a limited period. The phenomenon of getting tired of an app and then dropping it is defined
as app-fatigue. We will investigate this phenomenon in our thesis. One of the categories, where
app-fatigue is likely to occur, is in games. With people spending on average 7.7% of their time on
games, the smartphone game industry has generated a $81.5 billion revenue world-wide in 2021
(Wijman 2021), (Mobile 2022). The group of smartphone games with the highest revenue in 2021 is
Free-to-Play (F2P) games. An advantage of F2P games is that the user is not required to pay in order
to participate (Curry 2022). Thus, we will examine app-fatigue in three different F2P games in this
thesis; Candy Crush, Candy Crush Soda and Pokémon GO.
1.1
Scope
The goal of our thesis is to analyze app-fatigue using a global large-scale dataset. The focus is to
investigate if it is possible to determine when app-fatigue occurs and for whom. Thus, we will
aim to answer the questions below. For each question, example questions are given to provide an
understanding of the meaning of the questions.
• Which game patterns within the game, with regards to the users’ time series, can be dis-
covered?
Do users slowly stop playing? Do they intensify their usage of the app through time? Do they
play continuously or more sporadic?
• How do the users’ demographic features affect the patterns in the time series and can this
have an impact on when app fatigue occurs?
Do older users keep playing longer? Do female users play more than male users? Do users
from Europe play more than users from Asia?
• Does the way the user has acted in the past influence their future gaming pattern?
Can the last part of the time series be distinguished from the first?
• Which factors encourage app-fatigue?
How is the survival length influenced by demographics? Does total smartphone usage have
an impact on app-fatigue?

Literature Review
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate when and for whom app-fatigue occurs within smart-
phone games. This research topic is relatively new and therefore not much research has been done
in this field. This chapter provides a literature review regarding topics that influence app-fatigue.
The data used in this thesis is collected from smartphones, which includes data from F2P smartphone
games. Firstly, The general smartphone usage and how demographics impact the usage are exam-
ined. Throughout the thesis, usage and demographics play a key role in understanding the users
and their game behavior within the app. The thesis focuses on examining F2P games, therefore it is
relevant to gain an insight into the F2P business model. Additionally, the research on the concept
customers lifetime value is examined, to understand which factors influence the likelihood of users
to make an in-game purchase. At last, churn prediction of smartphone games is researched since
app-fatigue can lead to users leaving the app. Here different thresholds of allowed inactivity within
the app are examined, including which features have the potential to impact the prediction of churn
the most.
2.1
Smartphone usage
Smartphones nowadays have become an indispensable personal device that supports our activities
in almost every aspect of our lives. A tremendous number of apps have been created to serve a
wide variety of useful purposes (Cao and Lin 2017). The number of smartphone users have rapidly
increased over the years. In 2017 approximately 4.435 billion people across the globe owned a smart-
phone. This number is in 2022 approximately 6.648 billion people. This means that approximately
84% of the world’s population owns a smartphone - a number that is expected to increase over the
coming years (Turner 2022). As the number of smartphone users increases so does the number of
app downloads. In 2021 there were 230 billion app downloads world-wide. It is reported that 92.5%
of time spent on a smartphone is spent using apps, where the time is distributed as followed; 43% on
social media apps, 25.4% on photo and video apps, 7.7% on gaming apps, 3.1% on entertainment apps,
and 20.8% on other apps. The remaining 6.5% of time spent on a smartphone is on web browsing
(Mobile 2022).
In this thesis, insights on smartphone usage are relevant, since the data used in this thesis is collected
from smartphones. Thus, an overall understanding of smartphone usage is provided through studies.
This will lead to an examination of how demographics and other factors, e.g. how often users return
to an app, can impact smartphone usage.
2.1.1
General smartphone usage
A small-scale study (Falaki et al. 2013) based on usage traces from 255 users shows that the mean
number of interactions with the smartphone per day varies between 10 and 200. Additionally, the
mean interaction length varies from 10 to 250 seconds. Furthermore, the number of applications
used varies from 10 to 90 over the period of gathered data, which varies from 7 to 28 weeks. A
recurring theme in their findings is the diversity across users, hence there are many ways of using a
smartphone. A large-scale study (Böhmer et al. 2011) from 2011 showed that their users on average
spent 59.23 minutes per day on their smartphones with an average session length of 71.56 seconds.

On average each phone in the dataset has 84 applications installed, either by the user or preinstalled,
however only less than 10 applications are used most of the time. This study focuses on the top 10
most common apps for their users, where Youtube is in the first place. The most common game
application downloaded for smartphones is Candy Crush which is in fifth place among the most
common apps.
General smartphone usage provides a good overall understanding of the topic. However, people
have different interests, which can lead to different smartphone usage. Two people can even have
the same app installed but use it differently, e.g. frequency of the app. These differences make
it possible to infer personal demographic attributes from apps, which can impact the marketing,
products, and services. When demographics of the users are known, it enables the business owner
to fit the mold of the ideal customer, e.g. an 18 year old man often has other interests than a 40 year
old man (S. Zhao, Li, et al. 2019).
2.1.2
How demographic factors impact smartphone usage
Even though the number of smartphones increases world-wide, is it not equally distributed between
countries or age groups. People in advanced economies are more likely to own a smartphone than
people in emerging economies. Nine in ten people from South Korea, Israel, and the Netherlands
own a smartphone, whereas only six in ten people in Poland, Russia, and Greece own a smartphone.
In India, it is only 24% of the population who owns a smartphone, which is the lowest amount
among the investigated countries. Within all the countries it is often young people and highly
educated people who own a smartphone, e.g. in Japan 96% of the population under 35 years owns a
smartphone, while only 44% of the population above 50 years owns a smartphone. Also in countries
such as Germany and Australia younger people are more likely to own a smartphone than older
people. When it comes to gender there is no major difference between men and women in relation
to owning a smartphone. However, in India 34% of men owns a smartphone whereas only 14% of
women own a smartphone, which is the largest gap among the investigated countries (Taylor and
Silver 2019).
In (Peltonen et al. 2018), a large-scale study with 3,293 participants from 44 countries. It is shown that
app usage is influenced by geographic differences, whereas the differences reflect cultural boundaries
between countries. It was demonstrated that country information has almost twice the impact on
explaining app usage than any other demographic factor.
Another study (Lim et al. 2015) investigated the country differences in terms of their participants’
app installation behavior. It was found that people from the United Kingdom are more likely to
abandon an app due to forgetfulness. People from the United Kingdom and Canada are more likely
to be influenced by price when choosing an app, whereas people from Mexico are more likely to
pay for apps since they believe that paid apps have more features. Preferences for app downloads
depend on the users’ country. In Italy people are more likely to download sports apps, in India they
are more likely to download education apps, and in Korea they are more likely to download gaming
apps.

2.1.3
Other factors that influence smartphone usage
A large-scale study, (Jones et al. 2015), investigates how users revisit apps on their phones. By
applying clustering, the users are divided into three groups; checkers, waiters, and responsives.
Checkers are those that exhibit brief but quick revisit patterns (less than an hour). Waiters exhibit
short-medium revisit patterns (one minute to four hours) and also exhibit longer revisit patterns (two
hours to three days). The last group, responsives, exhibits both short and long revisit patterns. They
discovered that checkers have a 60% probability of launching a new app 4-8 minutes after launching
the previous one. Waiters and responsives have a pivot point at one hour and eight hours, which
means that between those marks waiters are more likely to launch a new app than responsives.
In (De Nadai et al. 2019), a study based on data collected through an Android application with 415,000
users, explores the strategies and limitations of in-app usage. It shows that people are generally
limited by the time available, which was also expected. In the study, they calculated a measure of
the persistence of the apps. Categories such as social and communication apps have the highest
persistence, which means they are kept for a long time, which supports the view of smartphones
being used mainly for social connection. On the contrary, categories such as events and games
have a low persistence and indicate temporal and trend patterns. A strong negative correlation
between age and app usage is found. The study separates the users into two groups based on their
app behavior - keepers and explorers. Keepers tend to behave according to constant and repetitive
habits, whereas explorers tend to change their behavior.
Another study (S. Zhao, Ramos, et al. 2016) performed in China, uses a combination of k-means and
MeanShift algorithm to cluster data of 106,762 users. By using features such as app usage divided
into different app categories, they discover 382 different user types. This study also investigates
the relation to demographics, which shows that demographics highly influence usage behaviors.
Thereby the assumption that all smartphone users are similar and can be treated as a uniform group
is disproved.
After gathering information about smartphone usage and how it is influenced, it is interesting to
examine how it can be applied in the real-world.

2.2
Game behavior in Free-to-Play
In 2021 there were more than 3 billion players across the world who generated revenue of $180.3
billion from videogames split among various devices: smartphones (45%), consoles (28%), PCs (2%),
and tablets (7%) (Wijman 2021). Furthermore, people spend 7.7% of their time on their smartphones
playing games (Mobile 2022). The group of smartphone games with the highest revenue in 2021 is
F2P games (Curry 2022). In this thesis, only F2P games are considered. Hence an understanding
of the F2P business model is provided, along with studies that explain when players are willing to
make an in-game purchase.
2.2.1
Free-to-Play
F2P is videogames that do not require the player to pay in order to participate, e.g. Hearthstone
and Candy Crush. The F2P business model is to convince players to buy in-game resources. An
importance of the business model for F2P is to monitor user engagement, which is defined as how
much time a player spends in a gaming session. It is shown that the longer a player remains engaged,
the more likely they are to spend money (Seif El-Nasr 2013). In (Lewis 2014) they identify three
methods for F2P games to earn money; Currency Confusion, Pay to Skip and Monetized Rivalries.
Currency Confusion is when the game has an arbitrary currency to buy upgrades and extra features.
Thereby, the game is substituting real money with an arbitrary currency, leading the players to be
unaware of how much money they are spending. In Pay to Skip, the players can pay money to
skip tiresome issues, however sometimes the issues are added arbitrarily by the game developer.
Monetized Rivalries is also known as pay-to-win, where the players’ competitiveness is exploited to
encourage them to spend money, they would not otherwise have spent to achieve status. This is
often done by including an in-game leaderboard.
2.2.2
Customer lifetime value prediction
When using the business model for F2P it is important to identify the most profitable users. A
common concept is to predict the potential value of customers, by identifying the customer lifetime
value (CLV). This concept has been established in marketing research and is based on the total
income a company can expect from an average customer. One important aspect of the CLV is to
model the future purchases of a customer. According to CLV theory, purchase behavior consists of
three steps: 1) Customer acquisition; is the period from installing the game to the first purchase. 2)
Retention; is the phase after the first purchase, where the customer is redefined as a paying customer.
3) Expansion; is the retention of paying customers which leads to an expansion of revenue (Flunger,
Mladenow, and Strauss 2019).

The study in (Hanner and Zarnekow 2015) shows, that players that did not play the installed game
the first day, will have a low probability of becoming paying customers. Furthermore, they show that
the retention rate increases, when the number of purchases increases; also, the amount of money
spend per purchase increases with the number of purchases made. They can conclude that if a player
did not like the game at first, they will never become paying customers.
A study in (Voigt and Hinz 2016), investigates how the time until the players’ first purchase influ-
ences CLV. The longer time it takes a player to make their first purchase, the lower the player’s
CLV is. Furthermore, they discover a positive correlation between the amount of money spent on
the first purchase and the players’ future CLV. In (Seif El-Nasr 2013) a prediction model is created
based on smartphone games, that classifies players into paying and non-paying, as well as social
and non-social players. Players that play consistently in the first week, are more likely to become
paying customers. Additionally, social players are also more likely to become paying customers.
2.3
Churn prediction in games
In section 2.2 it was established that user engagement for F2P games is an important factor for a
successful business model. Thus a feasible business must be able to analyze and predict the behavior
of the players. Predicting churn has therefore become a key challenge in in-game analytics (Hadiji
et al. 2014). Churn prediction is a prediction of when a customer will leave a service or cancel a
subscription. It is a crucial prediction, that is useful in many industries, as it is often more costly to
acquire new customers than to retain the existing ones. It is particularly important in F2P games
that generate revenue via in-game purchases and in-game advertising (Avaus 2022).
2.3.1
Methods to predict player churn
When it comes to churn prediction there exist multiple definitions and methods. One of the chal-
lenges is to determine the allowed amount of inactivity before a user is churned. A study of churn
prediction, based on high-value players in F2P games, defines churn as 14 consecutive days of in-
activity (Runge et al. 2014), whereas another study defines churn as five weeks of inactivity (E. Lee
et al. 2019).
In (Runge et al. 2014), they study churn prediction, based on F2P games with binary classification, to
label whether a customer is churned or not. Both neural networks, logistic regression, decision tree,
and support vector machine are trained on the labeled data of previously observed player behaviors
up to a given date. It is then predicted whether a player will churn in the following week from that
date. In general, the classifiers are not that distinguishable. It is concluded that churn prediction is
more difficult on highly casual games1, than assumed. Additionally, they also show that if churned
players receive a substantial amount of in-game currency; it would not have a significant influence
on getting churned players to return to the game.
Another study which is based on virtual world data proposes to predict churn with a hybrid model
that combines the marketing tool recency, frequency, and monetary (RFM), with a social network
influence approach. The RFM model differentiates important customers from large data by the in-
terval of customer consumption, frequency, and amount of money. The hybrid model is tested in
combination with different classification models and performed the best with a random forest (Liao
et al. 2015).

A study, (Hadiji et al. 2014), based on five months of data gathered in 2013 from F2P games is carried
out. Churn is defined by two definitions. Firstly, churned players are defined as players, that do not
return after a specified cut-off date. This definition of churn is very harsh and not representative of
real-world applications. Trying to relax this definition, churned players are now defined as churners
if they have a low amount of sessions after the cutoff date. This definition separates the players into
engaged and no longer engaged players, as opposed to the harsher separation of churned and non-
churned players, and thereby makes it more applicable to real-world settings. In this study, different
binary classifiers are tested, which included naive bayes, neural network, logistic regression, and
decision trees. Here decision tree is found to perform best overall in terms of weighted F1-score.
The model with the relaxed churn definition performed best. The most important features for both
models are the average time between gaming sessions, number of gaming sessions, and current
absence time.
2.3.2
In-game purchases impact on churn prediction
In (Perianez et al. 2016) they analyzed a major smartphone social game, where high-value players
(Whales) in general can be labeled as churned players if they are inactive for ten or more consecu-
tive days. Furthermore, the most important features when predicting churn, is the cost of the last
purchase, days since the last purchase, and the level in the game. In another study, (S. K. Lee et al.
2016) they predicted player churn in the game Crazy Dragon. Here the most important features are
the number of purchases and the number of times the players attended their guild 2. Additionally,
the amount of virtual currency a player has left is also a relevant factor for predicting churn.

Summary of literature review
From the literature review an understanding of smartphone usage, game behavior in F2P games,
and churn prediction in games is gained. It is shown that clustering techniques can be applied to
discover different types of smartphone behavior, without using demographic features. In this thesis,
a k-means algorithm will be used to discover different game behaviors, also by other features than
demographics.
Furthermore, the literature review shows that gender, age, and country influence smartphone usage.
To see if demographics also impact game behavior, two models are created, a prediction model
to predict the last streak, and a survival model to predict the correct order of users leaving the
game. These two models will initially be created by using only non-demographic features, e.g. time
spend playing the game (baseline models). Later on, new models will be created by introducing
demographic features to the baseline models. Comparing the models with and without demographic
features should exhibit the impact they might have on the model predictions.
In churn prediction, different lengths of inactivity are analyzed, ranging from 14 days to five weeks.
The length of inactivity is important in this thesis, as the users’ streaks within the game have to be
identified. Furthermore, it is shown that churn prediction is difficult to use in casual games, which
Candy Crush and Candy Crush Soda are. At last, the time between sessions has a major influence
on churn prediction, hence an attribute that captures this behavior will be included in the prediction
model.

Streak Behavior
In the previous section, we explored the datasets, where information such as distributions of daily
duration used playing Candy Crush and the users’ demographics were gathered. In this chapter, we
wish to gain more insight into the users’ gaming habits and patterns within Candy Crush. Therefore
the users’ time series are divided into streaks. We define streaks as a series of time where the users
are active in the game with no extended interruptions of inactivity. In order to identify streaks,
we have to determine the allowed amount of inactivity within a streak. This is investigated with a
sensitivity analysis. Subsequently, the streaks are analyzed by different visualizations to discover
the distributions of the number of streaks each user has. Furthermore, it is investigated if there is a
difference in length of streak and time spent playing in the streak for different users and by different
streak numbers. At the end of this chapter, a new dataset is created where each observation is one
streak. The dataset includes features associated with the respective streak. This dataset will be used
in the analyzes of the next chapters to help determine when app-fatigue occurs and for whom.

Discovery of Different Game Patterns
In the previous chapter 4, we created a dataset containing streaks. In this chapter, the purpose is
to discover different behavior within the streaks. Thus, a general understanding of how different
users play Candy Crush is achieved. To reveal hidden patterns within the streaks, the k-means
algorithm is used to cluster the streak data. Demographic features will not be included in the k-
means algorithm, since the purpose of this analysis is to divide clusters based on the users’ time
series, despite the users’ demographic features, to discover different behaviors in the streaks. To
visualize these clusters, a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is performed. Thereby, the data
points can be projected onto the first and second principal components, hence the data points are
visualized in a two-dimensional space.
Two different clustering analyzes are conducted in this chapter. The first one is conducted by using
the features length, non_usage, 50quant, streakNo and med_time. However, using all these
features, patterns for the users’ Candy Crush behavior cannot be revealed. Thus, the second cluster
analysis is conducted without non_usage and med_time. For the second cluster analysis, the users’
movement between the clusters, and the demographic aspect are analyzed to discover if tendencies
occur.
Firstly, we introduce the theory behind the clustering analysis. We provide an understanding of the
k-means algorithm and how to choose the optimal number of clusters and a PCA.

means clustering is a centroid-based clustering algorithm that tries to minimize the sum of squares
between the points and their respective cluster centroid. Considering the euclidean distance the

5.1.1
How to choose the optimal k clusters?
In this section, different methods for choosing the optimal number of clusters will be explained. We
will use two methods to determine the optimal k, which are; the Elbow Method and Silhouette Score.
The Elbow Method
The Elbow Method calculates the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) also known as inertia.
This is the sum of squared distance between point and centroid, for each value of k, usually in the
range from 1 to 10. It measures if the points are close to their respective centroid. When visualized
the graph looks like an elbow. As the number of clusters increases the WCSS will decrease since
the points will be closer to their respective centroid. There is a point where the graph will rapidly
change and form the shape of an elbow, at this point the optimal k can be found (Saji 2021).

The Silhouette Analysis
The Silhouette Analysis measures how well the clusters are separated. The silhouette score ranges
from -1 to 1, where 1 means that the clusters are clearly separated and distinguishable. If the value
is 0 the clusters are indifferent and the distances between the clusters are not significant. If the
silhouette score is -1 the clusters have been assigned incorrectly. The silhouette score is calculated
as:
Silhouette Score =

max(a, b),

where a is the average distance between each point within a cluster and b is the average distance be-
tween all clusters. For each k the silhouette score is calculated. The optimal k is where the silhouette
score is highest (Bhardwaj 2020).
5.1.2
How to handle random initialization of the centroids?
A disadvantage of the k-means algorithm is that its performance is highly dependent on the initial-
ization of the randomly chosen centroids. If a centroid is initialized as an outlier it might end up
with no other points in this cluster. On the other hand, multiple centroids might be initialized in
one cluster resulting in poor clustering. A method for solving this is the k-means++. This algorithm
improves the initialization of the centroids and thereby the results of the clustering. It is only the
initialization of the centroids that differs from the k-means algorithm, step 2, listed in table 5.1. The

5.1.3
Advantages and disadvantages of the k-means algorithm
Some of the advantages of the k-means algorithm are that it is computationally faster compared to
other clustering algorithms e.g. hierarchical. It can handle a large dataset and provide tight clusters.
A disadvantage of the k-means algorithm is that it is difficult to determine the optimal value of k.
Due to stochastically in the algorithm, the results of the clusters will change sizes when it is run
multiple times. At last, the k-means algorithm can only handle numerical data (Gikunda 2018).
5.2
How to visualize high-dimensional data
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a widely applicable technique where the purpose is to find a
lower-dimensional representation of a high-dimensional dataset. The goal of PCA is to identify the
most meaningful basis of independent linear combinations of the original variables to re-express a
dataset. A lower-dimensional representation can be insightful in different cases e.g. a preprocessing
step of high-dimensional data or a visualization technique (Herlau, Schmidt, and Mørup 2018, p. 29).
In this study, we will use a PCA as a visualization technique for our datasets.

The input data is a multivariate data matrix X, with m observations and n variables. A highly

achieved by subtracting the mean for each measurement type. If scaling is not performed the first

CX = 1
nXXT,
(5.5)
where X is the dataset and n is the number of variables. The covariance matrix is a method for
detecting redundancy in the data. This can be detected by the absolute magnitude of the covariance
between the variables since the covariance measures the degree of the linear relationship between
the variables. Redundancy is a significant feature to be aware of in the PCA. This means, that if
one variable can be explained by another variable, then only one variable is sufficient to include. In
step 3, the PC is estimated by calculating the eigenvectors of the CX since the covariance matrix
is always squared. In step 4 the number of eigenvectors used to re-express the dataset is chosen.
The eigenvalues to the eigenvectors are sorted. Thus, the highest eigenvalue explains most of the
variance in the dataset. Thereby, the number of dimensions, k, wanted in the lower-dimensional
space is obtained by choosing the k largest eigenvalues with their corresponding eigenvectors. In
the last step, the chosen k eigenvectors are multiplied with X to recast X along with the PC’s (Shlens
2014).
5.2.1
Advantages and disadvantages of a PCA
Some of the advantages of a PCA is that it removes correlated features, thus the PC’s are not corre-
lated. Furthermore, a PCA also reduces the chance of overfitting since the number of features used
is reduced. Lastly, it is possible to visualize high-dimensional data in lower-dimensional space. The
disadvantages of a PCA are that it can be hard to interpret the PC’s since they are a linear combi-
nation of all the features. Additionally, some information within the data is likely to be lost when
PC’s are created (Tutorials 2019).

How to find the ideal settings for the model
The purpose of tuning hyperparameters for the model is to find the ideal setting. To prevent the
model from overfitting the dataset is split into a training- and test set. Where the test set is saved
for later to evaluate the model. A validation set is introduced, which is a part of the training set.
This is used for an unbiased evaluation of the model fit on the training set. When the results using

the validation set are satisfying, the final evaluation is performed on the test set. It can be costly to
extract a validation set from the training set, as the model performance can be greatly biased by the
size of the supplied training set. To prevent this Cross-Validation (CV) is implemented. In CV the
training set is split into k smaller dataset called k-folds. For each k-fold the model is trained on k−1
parts of the training set and afterward evaluated on the remaining training set. The performance
measure reported by the k-fold CV is the average of the values computed in each k-fold. This process
is illustrated in figure 6.1.
Figure 6.1: Illustration of the k-fold Cross Validation method where k is equal to five. (S.-L. Developers 2022)
The k-fold CV is computationally expensive since the method uses all the data available. Further-
more, using k-fold CV reduce bias in the training model (S.-L. Developers 2022).
Two methods are often considered when tuning the hyperparameters; grid search and random
search. When performing a grid search, a grid of the values of the hyperparameters is given and for
each combination the model trains. Therefore the grid search is time-consuming. To compare with
random search, a grid of hyperparameters values are created as in the grid search. However, the
values within the grid are selected in random combinations. Thus, a random combination of values
for the hyperparameters is used to train the model. Therefore, the number of combinations can be
set based on time and resources. Both of these methods can be combined to k-fold CV (Worcester
2019).
In (Bergstra, Bengio, and Bottou 2013) it is proven that random search was more efficient for hyper-
parameter optimization since not all hyperparameters are equally important to tune.

6.1.2
Advantages and disadvantages for gradient boosting model
An advantage of using the GBM is that the model often has high accuracy. Furthermore, the model
can handle both categorical and numerical input as well as missing values. At last, the model has
a lot of flexibility, which means different loss functions are tried out and various hyperparameters
are tuned. However, some disadvantages are that the large flexibility in the model can lead to a
large grid search when tuning the parameters. Additionally, the model also uses a larger number of
weak learners which is time and memory exhaustive. The model can tend to overfit since it keeps
improving when adding a new weak learner (Kuramar 2020).
6.1.3
How to handle class imbalance
Class imbalance is a situation where the classes in the dataset are not equally distributed. This can
lead to misleading accuracy scores since the model will predict the majority class. This is handled
by resampling the dataset. If the dataset is large down-sampling is applied. Here observations are
removed randomly in the majority classes until the classes are equally distributed. A disadvantage
of this method is that information is lost. Alternatively, up-sampling is applied where copies of
the minority classes are added until the classes have the same size. However, this can also affect
the model since it can be affected by duplicated observations (Herlau, Schmidt, and Mørup 2018,
p.217-218).

Feature Influence on Survival Length
App-fatigue is a phenomenon that occurs for most people during their engagement with an app.
Most users drop Candy Crush at some point during the period. Therefore, we are interested in
investigating the factors behind this phenomenon. In this chapter, the factors behind users dropping
Candy Crush after their first streak are investigated. We assume that factors influencing the first
streak also apply to the remaining streaks. For this purpose, different theories in survival analysis
are used which enable us to discover whether demographic features and smartphone usage have an
impact on which users drop Candy Crush first. Kaplan-Meier estimates are used to create survival
curves divided into different demographic groups, and groups based on smartphone and Candy
Crush usage. The Log-Rank test is used to test whether there is a significant difference between
these groups. Afterward, a Cox Proportional Hazard (CPH) model is fitted to the data. This model
provides insights into how different parameters influence the survival length.
The chapter is structured into three sections. The first section provides an understanding of the
different theories in survival analysis. In the second section, survival curves created by the Kaplan-
Meier estimates are analyzed. In the third and last section, a CPH model is created to examine the
parameters’ influence on the survival length of the first streak. Additional results for this survival
analysis are found in appendix D.
7.1
What is a survival analysis?
The application of the survival analysis was first used in medical studies and is still widely used in
this field e.g. to describe the survival of patients that are exposed to different medicines. However,
it is also applied in different areas e.g. to predict customer churn or predict the next failure in a
machine. Survival analysis is a branch of statistics used to analyze data in which the time until the
event is of interest (Pereira 2022). In this thesis, the time until the event is defined as the time until
the users stop playing Candy Crush. The time is given in weeks.
Given a specific time where subjects’ survival time is observed, it is not guaranteed that the in-
formation gathered is completed. In cases where the subjects’ information is not complete, the
observations can be censored. There are two different types of censoring, right censoring and left
censoring. Right censoring is when an event is not observed at the specific time, where the subject is
observed. Thus, it is known that an event for subjects will occur after the time ends. Left censoring
is where the subject enters the period later than the beginning of the period (Jenkins 2005). We do
not need to censor the data since all users’ presence at the beginning of the period is known and the
event happens within the given period.

How app-fatigue occurs in Candy Crush
To investigate app-fatigue in Candy Crush we used four different methods. Streaks are identified
by determining a threshold of allowed inactivity by a sensitivity analysis. The k-means algorithm
is used to discover three different streak behaviors for playing Candy Crush. GBM is fitted to the
data to discover different characteristics of the last streak for each user vs. the other streaks. The
last method is a survival analysis with the purpose to discover which factors influence the survival
length of the first streak.
From these methods, we discovered that young users and men experience app-fatigue before older
users and women. One of the game patterns found includes streaks of long length and low daily
Candy Crush usage relative to the length. Users with this pattern are mostly women above 48 years.
From the survival analysis, it is proved that women and older adults are more likely to play Candy
Crush for a longer time. Thus, a hypothesis is that men and young users are more likely to show
novelty seeking. Another gamin pattern includes streaks of medium length and high daily Candy
Crush usage. Users with this pattern are mostly from Asia. The third gaming pattern includes
streaks of short length and low daily Candy Crush usage. Many streaks show this pattern, thus the
demographic distributions are similar to the distributions in the entire dataset.
When investigating the features’ influence on the last streak by the GBM, the users’ break between
the current streak and the previous streak is most important. A long break between streaks increases
the risk of the streak being the last one. Users with high and low smartphone usage play Candy
Crush the least and have long breaks between streaks. Thus, users with high or low smartphone
usage tend to experience app-fatigue faster than other users. Furthermore, we discovered that a
longer streak length or high duration of playing Candy Crush in a streak indicates that the streak

Discussion
We have analyzed app-fatigue using different methods. In this chapter, we will summarize and
discuss the findings. During our study we have encountered limitations, which have introduced
uncertainty to the results; thus, it is important to discuss where and how this uncertainty is present.
Retrospective, we could have improved our study by e.g. introducing new games or methods. At
last, we will make recommendations based on the findings to the F2P gaming industry including
how they potentially can increase their revenue.
9.1
Key findings of this study
In this thesis, we have investigated three different F2P games to gain an understanding of app-
fatigue. In this study, we define app-fatigue as when a user gets tired of an app and drops it, which
is at the end of the last streak. The three games included in our thesis are Candy Crush, Candy
Crush Soda and Pokémon GO.
Engaged users have few but long streaks
For all three games, it is possible to identify streaks in the users’ gaming patterns. A streak is defined
as a series of time where the users are active in the game with no more than 14 consecutive days
of inactivity. From the identified streaks, various methods are applied to fulfill the scope of our
thesis (section 1.1); clustering analysis, prediction of the last streak, and survival analysis. In the
analysis of app-fatigue in Candy Crush, Candy Crush Soda and Pokémon GO we discovered similar
results. Engaged users have fewer but longer streaks of high usage, whereas the uncommitted users
have more short streaks of low usage. We can divide the users’ streaks into three groups of different
characteristics. One large group includes streaks of short length and little time spent within the app.
The two other groups include a small number of streaks, however, these groups capture distinctly
gaming patterns in the streaks. The first group includes streaks of medium length and high usage
time within the app. The other group includes long streaks but with low usage time within the app
compared to the length.
High inactive time between streaks increases the risk of app-fatigue
In relation to app-fatigue, we wanted to study if the users’ past gaming pattern within the app,
influence their future gaming patterns. This leads us to investigate the pattern of the last streak, and
whether it is possible to distinguish the last streak from the rest, to detect app-fatigue. For all three
games, the three most important features are; the time of inactivity between streaks, the length
of the streak, and the duration spend within the app in the streak. It applies to all three features
individually, that if the value is high, it is almost certainly the last streak. With the exception of
the inactive time between streaks for Pokémon GO, where the results are ambiguous. This could be
due to the fact, that many Pokémon GO users only have one streak in total, which would make the
inactive time between streaks zero. In Pokémon GO it is easier to distinguish the users’ last streak
compared to Candy Crush and Candy Crush Soda. One explanation could be, that Candy Crush
and Candy Crush Soda are highly casual games and Pokémon GO is not. In (Runge et al. 2014) they
stated that churn prediction in highly casual games is difficult. Another explanation could be, that
the data in Pokémon GO is highly influenced by Japanese users. This means that there is a possibility
of the streak pattern being similar for a large part of the data, which could make it easier for the
prediction to distinguish the last streak.

==PAGE:108==
Older users and females users experience app-fatigue later than others
While investigating the features’ impact on the survival length, we discovered a clear correlation
between age and survival time for all the games. This means that younger users experience app-
fatigue before older users. This result is also reflected in the prediction of the last streak, where older
people tend to have yet another streak. Considering the users’ gender, a tendency for all the games is
discovered. Here it is found that female users have a higher survival probability than men, however
in Pokémon GO this is only the case until half a year. This result is also reflected in the prediction
of the last streak, where females tend to have yet another streak. Furthermore, the game pattern
of having a longer streak with low duration is more present for women than men, which means
that women play the games over a longer period than men. Only considering Asia and Europe,
since most users are from either one of the two continents, we see that Asian users have a higher
survival rate than Europeans for Candy Crush Soda and Pokémon GO. This tells us that Candy Crush
Soda and Pokémon GO are more popular among Asians. In Asia, there is a different gaming culture
than in Europe, which may explain the popularity of the games among Asians (Newzoo 2021). In
Candy Crush, it was not possible to distinguish the continents, which indicates that Candy Crush is
a popular game world-wide. Only for Candy Crush, we investigated smartphone usage in general,
where it was clear that users with either high or low smartphone usage play the least Candy Crush,
hence they are more likely to experience app-fatigue.
9.2
Limitations of this study
Due to different limitations in the study, some uncertainties are present in the results. These uncer-
tainties are explained below.
Uncertainties related to data collection
Overall, the data we have worked with is of good quality. There are approximately 10,000 users
present in each game, where approximately 40% of the users have provided their age. Thus, it is
reasonable to compare the results of the games. However, the limited amount of users that provided
their age causes great uncertainty in the age related results. We believe that the true age distribution
resembles the distribution given in the data, however, the true age distribution remains unknown.
To increase the certainty of the age related results, more data is needed.
Even though the data is of overall good quality, there still exist some uncertainties in data. First of
all, age, gender, etc. can be predefined when installing the app, which means that some users might
skip this step and just accept the predefined information, which can lead to a false narrative. Also,
a smartphone can have multiple users e.g. parent lending their phone to their child. This could
also give a false narrative if, for example, both the parent and child are playing the same game, as
this would mean that one user’s data would be recorded by multiple users. Furthermore, the users’
gaming pattern, in particular when streaks end, can be affected by external factors such as a low
battery, vacation time, and theft. Hence, app-fatigue may not always be the reason for a streak
ending.
Another uncertainty is that the knowledge of the users’ streak behavior beyond the data period is
unknown. This means that we do not know if the users’ first streak is their actual first streak, this
also applies to the users’ last streak.

Omitting users in the middle of a streak
When defining the streak data we choose to omit users that play the first and last day of the period,
thus ensuring that no users are in the middle of a streak. Retrospective, it would have been beneficial
to extend the period e.g. omitting users that play in the first 14 days and last 14 days, since the
threshold of inactivity is 14 days. Thus, the certainty of the users not being in the middle of a streak
increases. However, we could fear that this solution might have omitted too many users.
Daily smartphone usage is only examined in Candy Crush
In the analysis for Candy Crush, the daily smartphone usage is included. Due to the size of the
smartphone usage data, it is challenging to handle. Therefore, this feature is not included for Candy
Crush Soda and Pokémon GO, considering the limited time in our study. We believe that if the daily
smartphone usage was included in the analysis of Candy Crush Soda and Pokémon GO, we would
see similar results.
Users’ in-game patterns and purchases could have provided a more in-depth understanding
Our work is limited by not knowing the users’ in-game patterns and purchases. By adding this fea-
ture to our analysis, we could have examined if there is a correlation, e.g. between streak length and
levels, and between in-game purchases and demographics. Hence, a more in-depth understanding
of the users’ behavior and time series within the app, could have been achieved. In the literature re-
view, it is stated that in-game purchases influence user engagement, which, if it had been included,
perhaps could have improved the prediction of the last streak. This additional dimension of the
characteristics describing the last streak could perhaps have added additional insights.
Non-casual games are not examined
The focus of our thesis is casual games, as they are the most popular type of smartphone games. Thus,
our analysis is based on three casual games, where Candy Crush and Candy Crush Soda resemble
each other and are highly casual. The last game, Pokémon GO, is a low casual game. Even though
we found similar results for all three games, we cannot know for sure if it would look different with
another game, e.g. a non-casual game like The Bard’s Tale.
9.3
Improvements and future work of this study
With additional time we would have implemented improvements into the existing analysis of app-
fatigue. These improvements are described below.
Challenges to the definition of app-fatigue
We chose to define that app-fatigue occurs at the end of the last streak for the users. This is not
necessarily the whole picture of when users churn. If the last streak is close to the cut-off date
of the data, we do not know for sure if the users will play again. As a consequence, we chose to
omit data from the users that played the first and last day of the provided data period. We chose to
define app-fatigue as relatively harsh and binary, this definition, however, has the benefit of being
relatively simple and easy to apply. A more real-world view of app-fatigue would have been to relax
the definition by defining app-fatigue as a low amount of sessions in a limited period of time as they
did in (Hadiji et al. 2014). This however, begs the question of how long that period may be.
Ensure robustness in the choice of threshold
We chose a threshold of 14 inactive days within the streaks. This decision is based on an analysis of
various users in four different groups. These groups are divided according to the users’ total usage
within the app for the entire period. To reach this decision some of our choices qualify as educated
guesses, e.g distribution of groups. To be able to choose a more robust threshold, we could have
added more users to each group or divided the groups according to daily smartphone usage. We
chose to capture weekly patterns in the threshold, if this is disregarded, we would probably have

==PAGE:110==
chosen a threshold of 10 or 12 days. This would lead to more streaks of shorter length. This could
impact the highly engaged users in the way that their longest streak might be split into two or three
smaller streaks. Thus, the gaming pattern discovered in a small cluster with long streaks might not
be found. Furthermore, it could also impact the characteristics of the last streak and influence the
prediction.
Experiment with other clustering techniques
In the clustering, we find one very large cluster and two smaller clusters. We suspect that the
large cluster conceals multiple distinct gaming patterns, which are not discovered by the k-means
algorithm. This is especially a concern for Pokémon GO, where the large cluster contains 93% of the
streaks. If we had more time, we would investigate our suspicion and try out different clustering
techniques, e.g. DBSCAN. When using DBSCAN it is not necessary to predefine the number of
clusters, as it is for k-means. Furthermore, it can detect outliers and not include them in the clusters.
Thus, noise from outliers is not included in the clusters. At last, DBSCAN can create arbitrarily
shaped clusters. However, it does not perform well when clusters are of varying densities (Seif
2018).
The users’ first streak vs. the last streak in survival analysis
When examining the factors’ impact on the survival length, we chose to investigate the users’ first
streak, since we assumed that the factors impacting the first streak also impact the remaining streaks.
In retrospect, we could have used the users’ last streak to analyze the factors. This would be consis-
tent with the definition of app-fatigue, which occurs in the last streak. However, we believe that our
assumption is reasonable, thus we do not expect major differences by using the users’ last streak.
This is supported by the finding that older users tend to have yet another streak.
Include total screen time
In the dataset for the games, there is the feature screen_time, which is the total screen time used
within the same interval as the game is played. This feature is not used in the analysis, however for
future work, it could be interesting to include, as it most likely will provide information regarding
the time users may spend on other apps while playing the game.
Hypotheses regarding novelty seeking should be examined further
During the analysis of the survival curves, we made some hypotheses concerning that novelty seek-
ing could explain why some users experience app-fatigue before others. The results show that
younger users, male users, and users with high daily smartphone usage are more likely to expe-
rience novelty seeking, due to their low survival probabilities. In future work, these hypotheses
regarding novelty seeking, should be explored in combination with the differences in how gaming
apps are adopted by different users.
9.4
Business view of the findings gained in the thesis
One main finding that applies to all three investigated games is, that younger users are more likely
to drop the game than older users. This is relevant and valuable information to the companies that
are developing and marketing/advertising the games, as this information potentially can increase
their revenue if used correctly. We have two different approaches for the companies to manage this
finding and possibly increase their revenue. We distinguish between two user groups; young users
under 36 years and older users above 35 years.

Focus on customer retention for older users
The first suggestion is for the companies to focus on improving their relationship with their older
users. The older users already play the most and thereby experience app-fatigue later than younger
users. From (Seif El-Nasr 2013) it is known that users that play consistently in the first week are
more likely to become paying customers. Our results imply that older users are more likely to play
consistently compared to younger users, hence older users have a higher likelihood of becoming
paying customers. If the companies increase their focus on their relationship with the older users,
their revenue could potentially increase. A way to manage their relationship with the older users
could be to make targeted advertisements e.g. for a small fee extra levels will be available or give
extra lives as a gift.
Focus on customer attraction for young users
Another suggestion for the companies to potentially increase their revenue is to focus on increasing
the number of paying customers. At the moment the young users play the least, which means that
if the relationship with the young users is managed properly there is a potentially large group of
customers in the young users. If it is possible to turn young users into paying customers, the revenue
could potentially increase. However, as younger users are more likely to experience app-fatigue after
a short period, it would require a lot of resources to change this. At first, the companies need to
understand why young users leave the games. There are multiple ways of obtaining that knowledge,
one of them being through focus groups. When the knowledge is obtained, the companies can focus
their resources on managing their relationship with their younger users. However, the strategy
would most likely be to get the younger users to play for a longer time and then later focus on
turning them into paying customers.
Our recommendation
Both suggestions to potentially increase the revenue are based on customer-company relationships.
One is customer retention, where the purpose is to increase the average revenue per customer (Os-
terwalder and Pigneur 2010). This would require some resources; however, it is always easier and
cheaper for the companies to improve an already good relationship than to attract new customers.
Hence, the companies should focus their attention on older users. The second approach is both
customer acquisition and retention, where the revenue is potentially increased by new customers
but also by the existing customers (Osterwalder and Pigneur 2010). In the F2P gaming industry,
this would mean attracting younger users, especially from the age group 18-25, since they are rep-
resented the least in all the games. However, it is shown that younger users tend to experience
app-fatigue before older users. To turn the young users into paying customers there should also be
a focus on customer retention tailored for that group as a paying customer relationship develops
over time. From this, we would recommend the companies to first focus on their relationship with
their older users, as this solution will require fewer resources, and may still increase their revenue.

Conclusion
Smartphones play a major role in today’s society, making our everyday life easier (Cao and Lin 2017).
84% of the world’s population owns a smartphone, and as this number is expected to increase, so
is the number of apps (Turner 2022). In 2021 there were 230 billion app downloads world-wide.
There are different categories of apps where social media apps are the most time-consuming, here
we spend 43% of our smartphone time on average, whereas we on gaming apps spend on average
7.7% of our time (Mobile 2022). The users’ engagement is dependent on the type of apps. Social
media and communication apps are used over a longer period of time, whereas the need and desire
to use gaming apps can stop from one day to another. This phenomenon of getting tired of an app
and dropping it is defined as app-fatigue. The phenomenon of app-fatigue is investigated in this
thesis based on three different F2P games; Candy Crush, Candy Crush Soda, and Pokémon GO. The
dataset used in this study spans over four years and contains approximately 10,000 users for each
game.
Different game patterns, with regard to the users’ time series, are revealed within the games. The
users’ game pattern is divided into streaks, which is a period where the user is actively playing the
game with no or only small interruptions. Engaged users have a few intensive streaks of long length
and high usage; uncommitted users have more streaks of short length and low usage. The inactivity
between streaks has a significant influence on when the game is dropped. When a user experience
app-fatigue their last streak tends to be intensive meaning that it is either long or the usage within
the app is high. Furthermore, a long inactive time between streaks encourages the streak to be the
last.
Users above 35 years tend to experience app-fatigue later than younger users, thus their streaks are
often longer than younger users’. The gender aspect of the investigations revealed that women tend
to experience app-fatigue later than men. This is also reflected in the streaks, where women tend
to have longer streaks than men. A suggestion as to why younger users and/or men experience
app-fatigue first could be due to novelty seeking. For Candy Crush, there is no significant difference
in app-fatigue regarding the users’ continent. For both Candy Crush Soda and Pokémon GO Asian
users tend to experience app-fatigue the latest. A factor that encourages app-fatigue is either high
or low daily smartphone usage. This result needs to be verified by investigation of other apps since
it was only possible to examine this in Candy Crush.
The results of this study can be used in the real world to potentially increase the revenue in F2P
gaming industry. This can be achieved by retaining users over 35 e.g. by offering extra lives as gifts.
In (Seif El-Nasr 2013) it is stated that users that play consistently in the first week are more likely
to become paying customers, which older users above 35 show good potential for since they have
longer streaks and a relative late experience of app-fatigue compared with younger users.
