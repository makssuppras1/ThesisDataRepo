Abstract
Underground structures such as tunnels have become a necessity in today’s age for
the speedy commute of goods and services. Tunneling is an extensive construction
mechanism, and the geotechnical conditions play a signiﬁcant role in determining
the overall cost of the project. The soil conditions in Denmark primarily consist of
Clay Till as a result of the glacial activity. Clay Till may be rich in large boulders
and stones which tend to cause heavy wear of the Tunnel Boring Machine (TBM)
blades.
In this project, the borehole samples obtained from Levantkaj region were stud-
ied in the laboratory. The stones in the Clay Till were separated out according
to the size and a series of tests were performed to determine the strength of the
rocks. The tests performed on the stone samples included Unconﬁned Compression
Strength test (UCS), Point Load Strength test (PLS), and Equotip hardness test.
The distribution of the stones based on its size and strength were analyzed using
a probabilistic approach. The distribution of the gravels, stones, and boulders was
determined analytically, and the results were compared with the study performed
in the Storebælt region in 1988.
The primary rock minerals of the stones and gravels found in Clay Till were
ﬂint, limestone, and granite. The results from the tests were correlated among each
other and also compared with existing literature. The probability of ﬁnding a stone
(D=60-600mm) in 1 m3 Clay Till was approximately 7% and the probability of
ﬁnding gravel (D=16-60mm) in 1 m3 Clay Till was 93%, based on available data. A
high concentration of the stones was of the size 60-70mm in diameter and stones of
size larger than 64mm were mainly found at depths 10m and 18m below the ground
level. Furthermore, the distribution of large stones and boulders were found to be
almost 200% more from the current study when compared with the data from the
Storebælt region.

Chapter 1

1.1
Background
The drilling rate of a Tunnel Boring Machine (TBM) in Clay Till has a signiﬁcant
relationship with the mechanical properties of the rock, and hence, an engineer
must be aware of the mineral, number, and sizes of the stones. Yarali [8] studied
this relationship and determined that there existed a decreasing linear relationship
between the Drilling Rate Index (DRI) and the Unconﬁned Compressive Strength
(UCS), Point Load Strength (PLS), Surface hardness tests such as Schmidt Rebound
Hardness (SRH). When compared with the Brazilian Tensile Strength (BTS) results,
the relationship was poor, but still showed a linear trend to a small degree. Thus
these indices were very eﬃcient to determine the drillability with minimal sample
preparation and simple laboratory tests.
Jian et al.[9] studied the distribution of stress on the surface of a cutter when
acting on a boulder by FEM analysis using ABAQUS. The study concluded that the
wear of the cutter was directly proportional to the contact area of the cutter with the
rock, and the crushability of the stone was higher as the area of contact increases.
The study also suggested that the wear of the drill parts directly depended on the
strength of the stone. Thus these studies give an idea of how the wear of the TBM
blades diﬀers with the size of rocks and type of mineral.
Lin et al.[10] studied the wear of the blades depending on the material properties
of the cutter blades and the rock minerals using an experimental setup. They studied
the mass loss in the cutter blades when subject to TBM cutter performance test
on Guangzhou granite, Yinchang gneiss, and Milou granite using the test setup as
mentioned above. The result was that as the material hardness was higher, the
wear when tested on Guangzhou granite and Yinchang gneiss was lesser, while in
the case of Milou granite, the wear decreased initially with the increase in hardness

1.2
Motivation and Aim of the project
The nature of the Clay Till cannot be assessed only using the borehole description
since the bore-log data does not give information on the distribution of stones and
boulders. Extensive laboratory study of the number, size and strength distribution
and statistical analysis of the data is the solution to obtaining an eﬀective idea of
the wear that could happen on the TBM blades and the number of times the blades
need to be replaced during the course of tunneling.
The aim of this project includes the determination of the occurrence, size, and
strength of the gravel, stones, and boulders by investigating borehole samples in
the laboratory. The samples that were investigated were from the Levantkaj region
in Copenhagen.
The location map of the region along with the location of the
boreholes are represented in Figure 1.1. The stones, gravels, and boulders from
the boresamples were separated out, and a series of tests were performed on them.
The number, and the strength data were further used to determine the statistical
distribution based on the size and strength of the stones, gravels, and boulders. The
detailed objectives of this project include:
1. Separation of gravel, stones, and boulders from the borehole samples.
2. Separation based on their size using ISO standard sieves of sizes 16mm, 32mm,
and 64mm diameter.
3. Geological identiﬁcation of the stones of sizes larger than 32mm.
4. Determining the probability of occurrence of gravel, stone, or boulder in 1m3
of Clay Till.
5. Studying the eﬀect of the sample size of investigation on the probability density
function of the occurrence of a stone (60-600mm diameter).
6. Performing laboratory tests (Equotip hardness, Unconﬁned compression test,
and Point load test).
7. Comparison of the strength from diﬀerent tests.
8. Determining the strength and size histograms using statistical analysis.
9. Comparing the distribution of boulders determined in the Storebælt region
with the current studies.

Chapter 2
Theoretical Background
2.1
Transportation of till by the glaciers
Clay Till is a sedimentary soil that occurs as a result of glacial deposition. The
deposition as a result of glacial activity is termed as a diamiction, which means that
the deposits are a mixture of all grain sizes and are well graded.
In order to be able to understand the nature of the Clay Till at a location, it is
necessary to understand the glacial process that takes place in order to transport
the Till [1]. The various stages in a glacial ﬂow can be explained with the help of
Figure 2.1.

Chapter 2. Theoretical Background
The major part of the glacial dynamics occur at the glacier front. The sediments
are built up at the front of the glaciers based on the melting or freezing of glaciers
during summer or winter respectively. In summers, the glacier melts and it retreats,
and during winter, the glacier moves forward due to accumulation. As the accumu-
lation gets larger at the active glacier region, a pressure is developed at the glacial
front causing the accumulation to move over the existing glacial front. This results
in the occurrence of huge sediment build-up at some locations.
In the glacier front, there is a huge build up of Moraine or Till which consists
of ice. The longer the glacier has stayed in the location, the larger will be the till
build up in the region. In some cases, the glacial ice is covered by a thick layer of
Moraine or Clay and thus acts as an insulation. When the ice melts, the sediments
remaining will be a small percentage of the total volume. Contrary to the belief
that the surrounding soil may create heat and fasten the melting process, these take
thousands of years to melt. As the glacial water melts, it is common to ﬁnd potholes
ﬁlled with water in which sediments also get transported over time due to glacial
transportation.
The velocity of ﬂow of glaciers depends on the type of surface below them. If
the surface below the glacier consists of clay, it acts as a lubricant and hence moves
forward faster. In case of coarse gravel, the glacier tends to ﬂow slowly and in case
of very large boulders, the glacier seizes to ﬂow.
The material transported by the glaciers may be a mixture of large boulders,
stones, sand gravel and clay and it is very hard to predict the distribution of these
materials. When excavated, the top layer of Clay Tills is usually brown due to the
interaction with rain water that reacts with the minerals in the clay and start to
rust. The lower layers are typically greyish in colour.

Granite:
Granites are common intrusive igneous rocks consisting of crystals of diﬀerent min-

probability of occurrence of an event is based on prior information provided. Ac-

event B gives some information about A is given as from Equation 3.9.
P(A | B) = P(B | A) · P(A)
P(B)
(3.9)

is termed as the posterior and is deﬁned as the probability of occurrence of an
event after consideration of the prior belief and the data [21]. P(B) is known as
the evidence and is a normalizing constant which can be neglected. Therefore the

Chapter 5
Conclusion and further research
In this thesis, the occurrence of the large stones and boulders in Copenhagen Clay
Till were studied along with geological identiﬁcation of their mineralogy. A series of
tests were performed in order to obtain the strength associated with their minerals.
The boresamples obtained from Levantkaj were analysed in the laboratory. The
four boresamples analysed consisted of layers of Clay, Sand, Clay Till, Gravel, Gravel
Till and Limestone. Approximately 0.9m3 of Clay Till was investigated. The number
of gravels in 1 m3 of Clay Till was determined to be approximately 3500-4000 and
the number of stones in 1 m3 of Clay Till was determined to be around 200-350.
The Limestone had the highest number of stones in 1m3 of investigated sample.
There were no boulders found in the samples analysed for any of the units studied.
This may be due to the dimensions of the boreholes.
In order to analyse how the sample size investigated could aﬀect the Probabil-
ity Density Function (PDF) of the probability of occurrence of a stone, Bayesian
statistics were used. When a uniform prior distribution of 0,05 was considered and
diﬀerent volumes of boresamples were used as observations, it was found that beyond
0,069m3, the uncertainty (,i.e. standard deviation) was approximately constant.
Flint, limestone and granite were the most common rock minerals found in the
Clay Till. A large proportion of stones larger than 64mm were granite and this could
be because of the glacial transportation of large boulders from Sweden, wherein the
bed rocks are mainly granite. There was a large proportion of limestone which could
have originated from northern parts of Denmark. Flint was found to possess a very
high strength demonstrating an average Unconﬁned Compressive Strength (UCS)
of 195MPa and limestone was found to have the lowest strength of around 30MPa.
The strength of the minerals could be related exponentially with bulk density ρb
and linearly with Young’s modulus E50.
The hardness test gave a measurement of the age and weathering condition of

the rocks. Gneiss was found to possess the highest hardness of about 850HLD and
Limestone was found to possess the lowest average hardness of 400HLD.
Comparing the Point Load Strength (PLS) of the various ﬂint samples, the dark
brown grey ﬂint clearly showed a higher PLS when compared to the grey ﬂint.
Sandstone possessed the highest PLS of 12MPa and limestone possessed the lowest
average PLS of 2MPa.
When comparing the results from the UCS test with the PLS test, The UCS was
found to be approximately 15 times the PLS. The hardness test results showed a
power regressive relationship with the UCS which was comparable with the results
obtained by Aoki and Matsukura [7].
Comparing the current results of distribution of gravels, stones and boulders
based on their maximum diameter with the results obtained by DGI in 1987, the
number of large boulders were found to increase by almost 200%. The data analysed
was available only for stones upto 300mm diameter and were assumed to follow a
certain projected exponential behaviour for stones and boulders of larger diameter
given by the equation N = 0, 0765D−3,995

Analysing the boresamples does not allow to study the extent of the boulders in
the horizontal direction hence the actual number of boulders in Clay Till may be
underestimated. In order to have more assurance of the accuracy of the projected
distribution, geophysical methods could be adopted in order to record the occurrence
and frequency of large boulders.
