Focusing of Synthetic Aperture Radar Data with the Backprojection Algorithm
by Nick PETTHA THANTHRIGE
Synthetic aperture radar (SAR) processing is readily used in radar imaging systems due to
a high obtainable spatial resolution. Frequency domain methods based on the Fast Fourier
Transform are the most widely used methods in SAR image formation algorithms. The meth-
ods may however impose assumptions on the problem in question, consequently leading to
the strategy not being fully applicable for the speciﬁc purpose. The possibilities are however
manifold. To lighten the approximations, extensions of the frequency domain methods, may
be sought or even a solution in the time domain looked for.
This thesis deals with the establishment of a time domain back-projection algorithm (BPA), this
for the focusing of radar ice sounding data. The system dealt with, is a low altitude airborne
radar known under the acronym POLARIS. The system was in 2011 ﬂown above Antarctica
to perform imaging of ice sheets and a subset of the raw data acquired at the time, are in this
thesis found processed and interpretated. Several data scenes are characterized by a highly
varying elevation of the ice surfaces. In the SAR processing, it is desired to take accurate navi-
gational data and topography into account. Time domain methods are slow, but the ability of
the BPA to accomodate topography and sensor motion variations, turns out to be a valuable
asset in the trade-off when compared to its frequency counterparts.
Focusing and spectral analysis of three data scenes have been performed, two of which are
located geographically near each other and they are therefore characterized by similar geolog-
ical conditions. The scenes involve Fimbul ice shelf and the Jutulstraumen Glacier in Queen
Maud Land, Antarctica. Both data scenes are obtained from coastal ﬂight tracks where the ice
is warm. The remaining scene is located far inland, where the ice is colder. Successful focusing
of the data led to a reduction in radar clutter, improvements in SNR and resolution. Frequency
spectra of the processed data were found to be highly governed by the geophysical conditions
at a particular scene. This has led to broad spectra obtained for the coastal ﬂight tracks and
narrow spectra for the track located inland. The BPA is slow when it is desired to focus long
tracks, but processing results have alleviated the downside of time domain back-projection.

1.1
Introduction
The desire for earth observation for the purpose of climate research is of great interest across a
variety of scientiﬁc disciplines. Especially Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) concepts are impor-
tant and SAR systems are readily employed for earth observation missions [25]. Such systems
are capable of carrying out high resolution imaging of features on the ground or in the subsur-
face of the earth. Speciﬁcally, radar ice sounding is the topic of this thesis. A radar ice sounder
is a pulse radar that during its observation window, transmit pulses and records the magni-
tude and time-delays of the received echoes from a given scatterer. Radar imaging is herefore
carried out from the stored data, namely what is known as raw SAR data.
It is customary to transmit a linear frequency modulated (LFM) waveform in order to achieve
a large time-bandwidth product of the transmitted signal, this is the core of high resolution
SAR imaging. The transmission of such a signal means that the signal is transmitted with
well deﬁned modulation parameters and the pulse modulation allows for pulse compression
on the receiving side. The technique leads to considerable improvement of spatial resolution,
when compared to the obtainable resolution when pulse compression is not employed. In ice
sounding, pulse compression is of high value as it enhances the resolution in the dimension
perpendicular to the ﬂight direction and importantly, it improves the signal-to-noise ratio of
the highly attenuated signal.
They key characteristic of a SAR in contrast to conventional radars, is the ability to obtain good
resolution in the ﬂight direction by utilizing the movement of the radar platform. By taking
advantage in the forward motion of the radar, a long antenna can be synthesized. The ﬂight di-
rection is the direction associated with Doppler frequency and the resolution is here obtained
by integration of the measurements in a coherent manner. Coherent imaging thus requires an
image formation algorithm to focus stored raw radar data. Several algorithms exists [2], and
most are computionally efﬁcient algorithms based on frequency domain methods. In this the-
sis a time domain back-projection algorithm (BPA) is implemented to focus ice sounding data.
Time domain algorithms are in general slow compared to frequency algorithms, however sev-
eral advantages in time domain focusing algorithms exists. By use of the BPA, variations in
ﬂight track and ground topography can accurately be accomodated.
The system in question for the work described in this thesis is ESA’s Polarimetric Airborne Ice
Sounder (POLARIS), devloped by the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The system is
a nadir looking fully polarimetric radar that operates in the P-band at 435 MHz. The system
was build to deepen the understanding of P-band wave scattering and propagation through
ice sheets and also to verify already existing surface clutter suppression techniques [11],[26].

1.2
Thesis outline
This thesis contains a total number of six chapters. Without the introduction to the thesis and
the conclusions, the remaining four chapters are outlined in the following description.
Chapter 2. Ice sounding: This chapter aims to present the principles related to radar ice sound-
ing. This involves fundemental principles of a pulse radar and the connection to pulse com-
pression. Furthermore the POLARIS system is introduced and spectral estimation for result
interpretation is touched upon.
Chapter 3. Synthetic aperture radar: The chapter describes the concept of a synthetic aperture
radar. The relevant principles hereof, includes the radar range-variation to a point target and
a description on the relevance of Doppler frequency, it is furthermore seen how a SAR can
obtain high resolution in the azimuth direction. A brief description of a two-dimensional SAR
signal is included.
Chapter 4. Time domain back-projection: The chapter takes subset in the theoretical foun-
dation established prior to chapter 4. SAR focusing in the time domain is introduced and the
input requirements for the back-projection algorithm is deﬁned. Furthermore it is seen how
the BPA deals with range migration and an approximate propagation delay for ice-sounding
with POLARIS is derived. The ﬁnal part of the chapter presents an implementation wise de-
scription of the BPA.
Chapter 5. Focusing & validation: The chapter presents various results for focusing of raw
POLARIS data. This includes three data scenes of different nature in terms of topography
and ice types. Preceding the results for POLARIS, various tests cases are demonstrated for
another airborne SAR system, known as EMISAR. A large part the result section deals with
the interpretation of focusing results obtained with the BPA. The interpreation is done with
post processing that involves spectral analysis. Finally a performance analysis is included and
intermediate results are presented for a created simulation model.

Chapter 2
Ice sounding
Ice sounding radars must operate at low frequencies to facilitate the possibility of reaching
high penetration depths. This is due to a more prominent signal attenuation at higher frequen-
cies. Most ice sounders have typically operated at frequencies below 150 MHz [11]. POLARIS
is an ice-sounding radar developed by the Technical University of Denmark, the radar oper-
ates at a higher frequency than what is typically operated with in ice sounding. The system
will be introduced in section 2.2. The purpose of this section is to initially in a broader sense
introduce radar principles related to ice sounding and then to restrict the descriptions to the
applied principles relevant for focusing of radar ice sounding data.
2.1
Radar principles
In the broadest sense, a radar system operates by transmission of electromagnetic waves that
propagates through space, are reﬂected at a target and then returned to the radar. The backscat-
tered power received at the radar Pr is described by the radar equation [27].
Pr = PtGtGrλ2σ
(4π)3R4

(2.1)
Here Pt is the transmit power, Gt, Gr are the gains of the transmitting and receiving antennas
respectively. In the monostatic case where the transmitter and receiver are colocated, i.e. same
antenna is used for transmission and reception, then Gt = Gr. λ is the signal wavelength in
the propagation medium. σ is the radar cross section (RCS) of a target, which is the quantity
describing a targets ability to reﬂect power. As it describes the reﬂection characteristics of a
target, modelling of the RCS is important when it is desired to describe the scattering from
surfaces, internal layers or the bedrock. An analytical characterization requires the inclusions
of appropriate models such as the Kirchoff or small pertubation model [18].
R is the range to the target. L is a loss factor that constitutes the transmit and receive losses. In
ice sounding the the loss factor must account for the attenuation in the ice, which has a strong
dependency on temperature [22]. To achieve sufﬁcient SNR and resolution, pulse compres-
sion is typically employed. The basic principle is to transmit a modulated pulse and correlate
received echoes with the transmitted signal, in this way the transmitted pulse length is decou-
pled from the obtainable resolution.
In ﬁgure 2.1 the geometry related to ice sounding is sketched in a cartesian xyz coordinate
system. The x-direction represent the platform motion in the along-track direction also known
as azimuth. The y-direction is the across-track direction or range direction, which is perpen-
dicular to the ﬂight direction. In contrast to most imaging radar systems, ice sounders have a
sensor conﬁguration that looks directly down towards nadir, the system is therefore known as
a nadir looking system. The geometetry is here drawn for a look angle θ.

The pulse waveform is represented by the gray curves, illustrating an inherent issue in ice-
sounding, namely surface clutter that masks the nadir signal of interest. As the nadir signal is
received, the recieved signal is a superposition of the nadir signal and surface returns, rather
than only the sub-surface nadir signal. POLARIS is able to suppress the off-nadir surface clut-
ter in the along-track direction by Doppler processing and in the across-track direction using
a wide antenna [11]. Furthermore, as pulse compression is used, strong range sidelobes from
the surface interaction are seen as an indirect type of radar clutter. Pulse compression thus
gives rise to a strong pulse compressed surface response, consequently targets at other ranges
can be masked by the sidelobes of the strong surface response. This may especially be an issue
for weak signals received from larger depths. The sidelobes can nevertheless be signiﬁcantly
reduced in an image formation algorithm by spectral or temporal weighting functions.

Spectral leakage occurs when a time domain signal that has been truncated is represented in
the frequency domain. The reason herefore, is that a frequency transform assumes a periodic
input signal. Frequency transformation of ﬁnite data amounts will therefore lead to spread-
ing of the frequency components. As any relevant data processing method must work with
discrete ﬁnite data, windowing of data is a necessity. For data processing purposes, the fast
Fourier transform (FFT) is typically used since it is a fast implementation of the otherwise slow
discrete Fourier transform (DFT). The use of no truncation window, results in high sinc side-
lobes which are unwanted. Welch’s method of PSD estimation stems from work years earlier
by Bartlett, namely Bartlett’s method of PSD estimation [24], thus the name Bartlett window.
The most commenly used windowing functions in this project are Hamming and Blackman
windows. Depending on the purpose of an analysis, the tradeoffs between the two windows
must be considered.

Synthetic Aperture Radar
A synthetic aperture radar is an imaging radar, that uses the forward motion of the platform it
is mounted on to generate high resolution images of geographically observed scenes. In raw
data, the received energy for a point target is spread in both range and azimuth, therefore SAR
processing is a two-dimensional problem, as it is ideally aimed for collecting the energy into
one pixel in a ﬁnal focused SAR image. For a point target, the energy is spread in the range
direction by the duration of the transmitted LFM pulse and in azimuth, by the time it is ob-
served by the antenna. During the observation time of a point target, the range distance from
the sensor to the point on the ground varies as a function of along-track space. This range vari-
ation consequently means a phase variation of the signal across the synthetic aperture length,
which corresponds to a Doppler bandwidth of the azimuth signal. If the range delay is greater
than the sample spacing in range, the signal energy will migrate over range bins. This must be
accounted for in the SAR processing, the phenonomena is known as range cell migration.
A SAR system is much different from a conventional radar, since it relies on the coherent inte-
gration of echoes along the ﬂight track forming a long synthetic aperture. For the realization of
such a system, requirements on hardware and software must be ﬁrmly set. A SAR requires a
coherent transmitter, this involves waveform generation, pulse modulation and power ampli-
ﬁcation. The requirements can be summed up in a few simpliﬁed statements: In the receiving
part, it is essential that phase information of received signals can be extracted accurately. The
system therefore demands a stable local oscillator (LO), to ensure phase stability in the system.
Secondly, SAR image generation requires a SAR processor that employs a focusing algorithm
to obtain the desired resolution. Finally, information on the ﬂight trajectory and velocity of the
platform is required for the SAR processor. In the following sections the concepts related to
synthetic aperture radar imaging will be deﬁned.
The intial section covers the range variation from sensor to target in a SAR, hereafter the conse-
quence of the range variation in relation to SAR imaging is discussed. Furthermore the concept
of a synthetic aperture radar is presented by introducing the key aspects hereof. This involves
Doppler frequency and how a high theoretical azimuth resolution is obtained in a SAR system.
Then the signiﬁcance of spatial frequency is introduced and it is seen how it is related to the
problem in question. Finally, a brief establishment of a generic two-dimensional SAR signal is
included.

Chapter 4
Time domain back-projection
As previously determined cf. chapter 3, a SAR is a radar that uses the forward motion of the
platform to obtain ﬁne resolution in the along-track dimension. Frequency domain methods
for SAR image formation have readily been demonstrated in literature [1],[30]. Even though
well deﬁned frequency domain methods exists, they are generally limited by various assump-
tions as for instance assumptions on platform motion and range curvature. Early FFT meth-
ods were developed in the mid 1980’s known as Fourier-Hankel and range migration (ω −K)
methods [14],[23]. Despite the advantages of low computional complexity, the methods are
not generalized as they only apply to linear aperture problems. Although extensions of the al-
gorithms to the non-linear case are possible, the fast advantage diminishes and the algorithms
becomes obsolete [28]. On the other hand, the BPA (back-projection algorithm) requires accurate
knowledge on the imaging geometry to focus SAR data. In ice sounding, this involves require-
ments on accurate ﬂight track information and surface topography. The time domain BPA is
invariant to acquisition geometry and is therefore regarded as a generalized method for SAR
image formation. The disadvantage is however high computional complexity, nevertheless the
nature of the BPA allows for parallelization, which makes it suitable for a GPU implementa-
tion [11]. In the scope of this project, the BPA is however implemented without parallelization
as no requirements on computational time has been set.

4.4
Range cell migration correction
In section 3.1.2 the origin of RCM was established, nevertheless further eloboration on RCMC
techniques are required to carry out the task of correcting the range cell migration. Due to the
range variation of a point target seen from the radar, a range delay greater than the range sam-
ple spacing can occur. Range migration correction is readily done with fast frequency domain
methods [21]. In a range Doppler focusing algorithm, the range migration must be done prior
to azimuth compression. However for time-domain backprojection, RCMC is an inherent part
of the algorithm.
Considering an input data sample X(xr, yr) that represents the currently available datum at
a given sensor position and nearest range cell corresponding to the round-trip delay, it could
directly be used in the backprojection summation. This does not neglect range migration, but
rather the nearest sample available is used as the representative value, this will be denoted
nearest neighbour interpolation. However if it is feasible to do an exact interpolation, this
is clearly the desireable thing to do. Generally speaking, it is furthermore desired to carry
out an accurate RCMC, without introducing unnecessary complexity into the system. RCMC
interpolation in the BPA is of very high cost. For each focused output pixel, a number of
input samples corresponding to the synthetic aperture length L are considered for integration,
consequently the number of required interpolations are determined from a given aperture
length and the azimuth sample spacing on the input. L/(v/ fpr f ) interpolation are required
for the reconstruction of a single output pixel.

are shifted such that the time t is exactly at the peak of the sinc function. In this way the other
samples occuring at the neighbouring multiples nT are multiplied by the sinc zero-crossings
and will cancel, yielding an interpolated sample. The frequency response of the sinc function
is a rectangular function, thus the interpolation acts as a low-pass ﬁltration, this is simply the
sampling theorem. The length of the interpolator kernel, will be denoted M and it should be
limited to lower the number of computations.
4.5
The back-projection loops
In section 4.2 a functional description of the BPA was given and a top-level explanation of
the implementation was further included. The purpose of this section is provide a thorough
elaboration on the implementation-wise description of the back-projection loops. The BPA can
be set up in various ways, for instance a column-by-column focusing can be performed and the
algorithm can moreover with little effort be parallelized. The current implementation relies on
a row-based focusing, which means that all pixels at one range, is initially focused and then
the algorithm continues to the following range, i.e. a row based focusing. This, of course on
the assumption that the azimuth of an image represents the horisontal dimension, which in
this project is implicit. The implemented BPA requires three for-loops. Below, the tasks of the
three individual loops are seen.

In section 4.5 the methodology behind the integrator loop was demonstrated. The current im-
plemented version of the BPA, is based on the accumulation of every single pulse contribution,
windowing coefﬁcient and phase correction, which is done within the accumulator loop.
Another strategy for implementation is to preserve the three abovementioned contributions
and letting them be stored in three respective vectors, that are processed outside the integrator
loop. By doing this, the sinc-interpolator can be applied in a single vectorized operation for
the whole aperture and then afterwards accumulation done to the output grid. So rather than
using the loop-implemented kernel (appendix A.3) for every single sample wished acquired,
the call to an optimized kernel should instead be made only once for a given synthetic aperture.
With the changed strategy a much improved performance is to be expected.

Chapter 6
Conclusions and recommendations
The implementation strategy for the BPA, was initially validated by carrying out several tests
on EMISAR data. The tests demonstrated expected behaviour of the algorithm. By analyzing
the degredation of spatial resolution with the use of different ﬁlter lengths, it was found that
an additional increase of the theoretical value of synthetic aperture at a given range had to be
imposed. Therefore a slight increase in SAR length was included in the focusing of ice sound-
ing data. The BPA, was then further validated by comparison with pre-processed data, that
had already been focused with the back-projection algorithm, obtained from [9]. The compar-
isons showed very similar results, it is therefore concluded that both implementation strategies
seem to work and fullﬁll the desired goal of focusing ice sounding data.
Prior to analytical representations of focusing results for POLARIS, several test cases were also
performed with regards to the POLARIS system. This was in order to present the mechanics of
the BPA. Here the range variation of point targets down through Fimbul ice shelf was shown.
By plotting the range-migration curves for many scattereres with an increasing depth, the in-
tegration space was seen to signiﬁcantly change its form at the air-ice interface. The purpose
of the demonstration, was to present a visual interpretation of the inﬂuence caused by appli-
cation of the established small angle round-trip delay approximation. A change of relative
permitivity, resulted in a change of the SAR integration angle at the surface. Several results
in the spectral domain, were furthermore included for the three different scenes. In all of the
three cases, the same refraction index of ηice ≈1.78 was used. As EPICA is of a different ice
type, it can be argued that different refraction indicies for KM-tracks and EPICA should be
used. Given that sloping internal layers within the ice volumes can accurately be detected, the
round-trip delay should be modiﬁed, to take sloping layers into account in the focusing. In
this connection, proper refraction indicies must be found and taken into account as well.
The general PSD results, reﬂected the differences in observation of different ice-types. The
Fimbul ice-shelf, was due to ice-inclusions, found to be characterized by a rather broad scat-
tering pattern in the ice volume. The surface backscattering was diffuse compared to EPICA,
where specular reﬂections were seen. Due to the permanent colder ice at the EPICA way-
points, PSD estimates of the volume, also showed a narrow spectrum. Jutulstraumen Glacier
was after focusing, seen to be characterized by a lot of ice crevices. This is led to a broader scat-
tering pattern of Jutulstraumen data near the surface, than what was seen in a Fimbul scene.
A satisfactory focusing is stated on the basis of the visually apparent differences, when com-
paring different levels of processed data. Here raw range compressed data, were in general
compared to cases that had been processed by unfocused SAR processing, an increase in SNR
was seen. By including the phase correction, i.e. the usual focusing, an increase in SNR and
spatial azimuth resolution was seen obtained.
Surface spectra were sought compensated, therefore a removal of the aperture weighting func-
tions were done. With removal of the windows, the far ends at larger look angles of the spectral

estimates were seen to rise signiﬁcantly. This is a phenomena currently not understood. Due
to the unexpected behaviour, a model simulating the POLARIS system with different point
targets, contributing to the full Doppler bandwidth in different ways, would be of high value.
Efforts were made in the establishment of a point-target simulator. These results were brieﬂy
presented and they demonstrated that simulated point-targets with POLARIS input, could
be focused. The theoretical foundation for the description of a simulation model, was how-
ever largely neglected and a only brief inclusion in the theory section, involved a generic two-

signal may be expressed and the treatment of the problem should be done in potential future
work of this project.
In general, focusing of long-tracks are time consuming, this since the number of interpolations
and phase corrections increase. Focusing by use of nearest neighbour interpolation, was seen
to be satisfactory in terms of computational time. For ofﬂine processing, the computation
times for nearest neighbour interpolations are deemed acceptable. However, for increasing
ranges the computation time, is especially increased for the focusing of longer tracks. With the
current implementation tested on a "intel(R) i5-6600k CPU @ 3.50GHz" with 4 CPU cores, it
took almost 7 seconds longer to focus a 30 km track, when increasing the aperture length from
130 m to 370 m. The performance of the sinc interpolator was found to be poor, the focusing
of a 2.3 km track required more than 60 seconds of computations. In the section dealing with
performance considerations, a suggestion for a future work optimization of the interpolator
was made. This involved the establishment of a vectorized version of the interpolator, which
by the use of Matlab can readily be done.
