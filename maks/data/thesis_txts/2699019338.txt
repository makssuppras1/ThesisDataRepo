1. Introduction 
The potential use of fusion energy would greatly help humanity in being able to move away 
from traditional fossil-based power production by producing clean energy in a steady manner 
that can compete with fossil fuels. Fusion power combines the positive aspects of renewable 
energy by producing clean environmentally friendly energy and the consistent availability of 
power of tradition fuels. The currently investigated concepts work on a similar principle to 
traditional means, with the distinction that the heat source is the fusion of atoms in a plasma, 
where the produced heat is absorbed by a  water cycle that then drives a turbine which 
converts that kinetic energy into usable electricity.  
The main purpose of this project is to investigate the thermal stability of potential materials 
as armour of plasma facing components (PFC). These materials will have to withstand extreme 
heat radiation from the plasma reliably for years to make the reactor economically feasible. 
The heat radiation induces microstructural changes in the material in the form of recovery, 
recrystallization and grain growth. These changes lead to a deterioration in the mechanical 
properties and potentially to failure.  
Over the years the most promising candidate became tungsten and tungsten-based materials, 
due to tungsten having the highest melting point  of any element, high strength and good 
thermal conductivity, while still being highly resistant to sputtering. Recrystallization is 
estimated so that it can be accurately predicted if tungsten were to be used in a fusion 
reactor, since recrystallization of tungsten leads to the mentioned loss of mechanical 
properties and also embrittlement. Embrittlement might happen due to instabilities in the 
plasma, where bursts of energy might lead to recrystallization in the material. In a 
recrystallized situation a brittle material would be damaged more than a ductile one. 
 
This project mainly investigates two types of tungsten: heavily rolled pure tungsten plate and  
heavily rolled potassium doped tungsten. The pure tungsten has been rolled in 3 different 
ways, with two methods utilizing a high draft, where one plate is cross-rolled and one is not, 
and then the last plate has been cross-rolled with a low draft. The other material is rolled 
tungsten doped with 80 parts per million of potassium, where the potassium’s main desired 
purpose is to interfere and slow down the microstructural change in the material when 
annealed. 
This thesis monitors the behaviour of the material using annealing for various periods, where 
the microstructural effects are mainly analysed using hardness measurements, light optical

2. Theory 
This section serves to provide the reader with background knowledge on the fusion process , 
the process of harvesting power from fusion and how tungsten could potentially make the 
harvest possible. The theory section also describes the materials, the material’s 
manufacturing and the reasons on why Tungsten is believed to have the highest potential to 
be used in fusion reactors. Furthermore, the material science and the theory behind the 
annealing process is described, along with the theory behind X-ray diffraction. 
   
 
2.1. 
Fusion reactor  
The main idea behind having a fusion reactor is harvesting the same type of energy production 
that occurs naturally in stars. Successfully capturing and harvesting this energy would result 
in a very strong alternative to both fossil fuels and renewable energies, since the result is 
relatively clean energy in vast amounts. 
 
The main goal of the ITER agreement, signed in 2006 by the EEA nations, Switzerland, the 
United Kingdom, the United States of America, China, Russia, Japan, India and Korea, is to 
create a joint platform where experimental work can be carried out with the end goal being 
creating a working ITER fusion reactor. Currently the European Fusion Roadmap from 
EUROfusion suggest that the fusion reaction should be successfully demonstrated under full 
power operation in 2035 with the ITER experimental reactor currently under construction in 
France. In this reactor hydrogen fuel in a plasma is fused under tremendous heat and 
pressures in the range of 200 kPa to create helium atoms, where the fusion process is kept 
under control using a powerful magnetic field. This fusion of hydrogen atoms into a helium 
atom releases vast amounts of energy and a neutron, which then in turn can be harvested. 
Making the capture and harvest of this energy possible is not straightforward because of the 
release of a high energy neutron (14 MeV) in the process. As a result this limits the options 
available to build such a reactor. 
The reactor is built upon the TOKAMAK concept, the leading candidate principle for practical 
fusion. In this concept a mixture of deuterium and tritium (hydrogen isotopes, 2H and 3H 
respectively) is kept at about 2*108 Kelvin in the form of a low-density plasma. The reaction 
is confined in a toroidal shape, where a current running around in the torus in a helical fashion 
generates an additional electromagnetic field which confines the plasma. There are many 
==PAGE:9==

challenges associated with making fusion reactors possible, such as the production (known as 
breeding) of the naturally sparsely occurring tritium fuel, where neutrons have to be absorbed 
by lithium and the resulting reaction forms tritium. For a worldwide use of fusion reactors, 
the breeding would need to be done at a sufficient scale, using either breeding in the first 
reactors or by the use of existing fission reactors. 
  
The released energy exists in 2 forms, where 80% is the kinetic energy of the neutrons and 
20% as radiated heat to the inner components of the reactor. In order to harvest the energy 
of the neutrons, first they must be slowed down which in turn produces heat. The neutrons 
themselves are very damaging to materials through either atomic displacement or through 
element transmutation where the resulting element is less desired due to its properties that 
are worse for the use in a fusion reactor. 
2.2. Fusion powerplant  
 
In a reactor with magnetic confinement (see figure 1) the fuel reaction of tritium and 
deuterium is surrounded by a breeder blanket, which contains lithium (for a breeding 
reaction) and a material like beryllium or lead that acts as a neutron multiplier (helping release 
additional neutrons). This produces an additional 20% of energy on top of the fusion energy, 
which is stored as heat in the blanket and then extracted using a coolant.   
The coolant and the primary heat exchanger need to be in a biologically insulated system, 
since the coolant will contain activation products (materials made radioactive by the capture 
of neutrons) as well as the radioactive tritium.

2.3. 
Annealing phenomena 
 
Annealing in material science describes a thermal treatment by which thermally activated 
processes affect and change the physical properties of a material due to microstructural 
changes. Atoms can migrate in the crystal lattice of the material and usually the number of 
dislocations decreases. Dislocations refer to imperfections or defects in an otherwise ideally 
uniform lattice. The relevant dislocation for plastic deformation is mobile or otherwise called 
glissile dislocations. The dislocation can be of two main types called edge and screw 
dislocations (see figure 3, with edge dislocations on the left and screw ones on the right).  
 
Generally speaking, when a material is plastically deformed, energy is stored partly in the 
form of these two types of dislocations. Plastic deformation can be desired because of a few 
reasons e.g., an increase in Yield strength, or in the case of tungsten to make it more ductile. 
As a result of this deformation, the material will be thermodynamically unstable, and its 
atoms will require additional kinetic energy transferred to them in the form of heat to 
overcome these dislocations. Like in most aspects of physics, the structure prefers a lower 
state of energy, meaning thanks to annealing the stored energy inside the lattice can be 
reduced and the material will move towards a state closer to equilibrium. There are three 
phenomena related to annealing: recovery, recrystallization and grain growth.

2.3.1. Recovery  
 
Recovery is the earliest process of annealing. It happens first as it requires less thermal 
activation, meaning recovery usually happens at lower temperatures than the other 
phenomena. The main effect behind recovery is the restoration of the original non-deformed 
properties of the microstructure. The stored energy is reduced by rearranging or removing 
the defects in the crystal structure, where changes happen primarily within grains. When heat 
is introduced, the dislocations in the material travel in slip planes and this movement is 
defined together with the slip direction. The more heat is transferred, more dislocation 
movement can be observed, where the movement happens through gliding, climbing (for 
edge dislocations), cross-slip (screw dislocations) or a combination. As the dislocations move, 
they can rearrange themselves to either a lower energy state or annihilate each other 
completely if the two dislocations have an opposite Burgers vector (see red vector in figure 
3). 
   
Since not all dislocations will find a dislocation with an opposite vector locally, there will be a 
dominant presence of dislocations with one type of Burgers vector. The leftover dislocations 
will form dislocation walls, called subgrain boundaries, by rearranging and aligning 
themselves within a grain. Just like grains it can be said that these subgrains have a same 
orientation within a region but are characterised as low angle boundaries (LABs).

2.3.2. Recrystallization  
 
Recrystallization can be viewed as a competing process to recovery, since these two 
phenomena can happen concurrently, but as opposed to recovery which happens 
homogenously through the material, recrystallization requires an initial nucleus to form, 
where growth can happen. The whole process is defined by the replacement of defects in the 
structure by defect-free grains and as opposed to recovery it removes all the dislocations in 
the microstructure by sweeping boundaries. 
The necessary nuclei form at elevated temperatures. They require enough energy in order to 
achieve a radius big enough so that the nuclei are not dissolved. Once a stable nucleus forms 
it allows the growth of strain-free grains until the point where their boundary meets another 
strain-free grain, consuming the deformed grains in the process. As opposed to recovery 
which is a continuous process where its endpoint cannot necessarily be defined, 
recrystallization has a defined starting point and stops once it reaches a fully recrystallized 
state. The amount of recrystallization in the material can be quantified using  ‘’volume 
fraction of material recrystallized’’ and can be typically tracked using the hardness of the 
material. 
This project looks at a material deformed through rolling, where recrystallization can have a 
preferred direction along the rolling and normal direction. This is because in heavily rolled 
material the dislocation structure would have aligned with the rolling direction and thus 
during recrystallization the new grains can consume more dislocations in this direction and 
then normal direction.

2.3.3. Grain growth  
 
Grain growth happens at a slower rate than recrystallization due to grain growth having a 
lower driving force. The result is lower velocities of the traveling boundaries. In some 
materials with well annealed grains (meaning that there is no preferred axis) grain growth can 
happen without previous recovery or recrystallization. This is not the case with this project 
that investigates a highly deformed material, where grain growth has to be preceded by 
recovery and recrystallization. Since recovery and recrystallization happens, the stored 
energy in the form of defects cannot be the driving force of grain growth, but rather it is the 
reduction of the total energy of high-angle boundaries (HABs) that are present by the 
minimization of their number[7]. Due to this reduced driving force grain growth is more 
affected the presence of solutes in the material. 
  
There are two types of grain growth: normal or continuous and abnormal or discontinuous 
(sometimes referred to as secondary recrystallization) grain growth, where continuous grain 
growth happens homogenously throughout, and the resulting grain sizes will be of similar 
size. In anisotropic materials or a material that has particles of two different phases present, 
abnormal grain growth can happen, meaning not all grains have the same growth rate. This 
can lead to a consumption of the smaller grains or to cause a quickly growing grain to grow 
around a slowly growing one. In the end abnormal growth might reach an endpoint where no 
more abnormal growth can happen at which point only normal growth will happen.   
  
In a highly deformed material, such as a rolled one, the presence of low energy LABs can mean 
that the driving force that is needed for grain growth is overall lower. It can be monitored 
with a macroscopic property such as hardness but can also be clearly seen by using light 
optical microscopy after using an etching agent on a prepared sample.  
 
 
2.4. 
Tungsten 
 
Tungsten (from Swedish meaning ‘’heavy stone’’), or alternatively Wolfram, is a rare metal 
found normally only as a compound with other elements. It has been investigated already 
since its discovery as an element in 1781 thanks to having the highest melting point of any 
discovered element.  Over the years it had a variety of applications, where most widely it was 
==PAGE:15==
Thermal stability of tungsten materials for fusion reactors 
15 
 
used as light bulb filaments, but also for X-ray tubes, electrodes in tungsten arc welding, as 
radiation shielding and military applications because of its high density and hardness. 
  
Extraction of tungsten happens in multiple steps, where the ore is eventually purified into a 
tungsten oxide WO3, which is then heated in a pure hydrogen atmosphere to reduce it to its 
pure form. The pure powder is not melted directly into a bar because of its high melting point, 
where instead a two-step sintering process is used[8]. Initially heated during pre-sintering to 
1200 °C and then it is sintered from room temperature to a range of 2700-3100 °C. The 
sintering is also done in a pure hydrogen atmosphere to avoid the presence of oxygen and 
the resulting formation of oxides. The resulting ingot has a relative density of 92-98% 
compared to the maximum density. 
Tungsten is the leading and most promising candidate as a plasma facing material(PFM) to be 
used in fusion reactors, specifically as an armour material for the first wall and the divertor. 
It is required that the first wall material will be able to withstand a temperature of 800 °C for 
a period of 5 years and that the divertor material will last up to 2 years at 1125 °C. Due to 
these requirements a material capable of transferring high heat fluxes and able to withstand 
thermal shock fracture is desired(due to potential instabilities in the plasma), while keeping 
the thermal stresses and surface erosion of the material low[7] Tungsten meets these 
requirements by having a high melting point of 3422 °C[6], high thermal conductivity , 
excellent erosion resistance, low thermal expansion coefficient and high sputtering threshold.
 
  
The potential use of tungsten is not straight-forwards due to its intrinsic brittleness at low 
temperatures, which is undesired since generally failure propagation happens faster in brittle 
materials than in ductile materials, thanks to the fact that ductile materials can undergo 
plastic deformation, meaning they can usually absorb more energy before failure[8]. 
Tungsten has a high ductile-to-brittle transition temperature (DBTT, the temperature where 
tungsten goes from brittle to ductile) because of its body-centered cubic structure, the 
resulting absence of close packed planes (planes with a high density of atoms) and its high 
melting point. This means that the brittleness only disappears at high temperatures. The DBTT 
can be lowered by mechanical work done to the material, but this is undone when the 
material recrystallizes.

2.4.1. A.L.M.T. tungsten, W-ALMT 
 
The main material being investigated in this project are pure rolled tungsten plates. In 
general, rolling is one of the most widely used forming processes. The main idea behind 
mechanically working and plastically deforming the tungsten is to reduce the intrinsic 
brittleness of the sintered material. Rolling is commonly split into cold and hot rolling (rolling 
below and above the recrystallization temperature respectively). The recrystallization 
temperature estimated to be 40% of the melting temperature creates some challenges for 
hot rolling. The rolling process includes a preheating stage and several reheating steps, which 
have to be done because of the heat transfer between the tungsten and the rollers. The 
forming process starts off with the highest temperature as a non-deformed material does not 
recrystallize, but in-between the passes the temperature is lowered. If the temperature were 
to be too low cracks would develop because of the materials inability to plastically deform at 
that temperature. Annealing can be done between the rolling steps to relieve the stresses 
and avoid the cracks, but this has to be done with careful consideration since recrystallization 
should be avoided in order to avoid grain boundary embrittlement. The two applied rolling 
methods for materials in this project are unidirectional rolling and cross rolling. In 
unidirectional rolling each pass is done along the same direction, where as a result this 
direction is referred to as the rolling direction, compared to cross-rolling where the plate is 
rotated by 90° between each pass. This results in a non-permanent rolling or transverse 
direction (defined as the direction perpendicular to the rolling direction), but they are still 
assigned according to the last step in order to ease the communication of the orientation of 
the material.   
W-ALMT has been manufactured and rolled in 3 different ways by A.L.M.T. Corp., Japan, with 
a reported purity of 99.99 wt.% with the main impurities coming from the presence of Fe, P, 
and O. [ref Japan paper]. The three types are ITER, which is unidirectionally rolled; CHW, made 
using high draft cross rolling and finally CLW, manufactured with low draft cross rolling. The 
reported properties of the three plates are summarised in the table 1 below using information 
from [ref Japan paper], where all the differences between the 3 types are only due to the type 
of rolling, not the underlying properties of the tungsten.

2.4.2. Tungsten doped with potassium, WK80 
 
Over the years of investigating tungsten as light bulb filaments, potassium doping was 
demonstrated to have a desired positive effect on delaying the microstructural restoration 
processes. The investigated processes, namely recrystallization, recovery and grain growth, 
where most importantly recrystallization affects the previously gained ductility of the 
material by mechanical work [ref Umberto paper 1]. Since the beginning of the 20th century 
potassium doping was discovered to have a positive effect in delaying recrystallization and 
was subsequently used in tungsten wires for commercial light bulbs to impede grain boundary 
motion, as a result stabilizing the microstructure, which combined with the work done to it 
by drawing of the wires themselves leads to a tungsten filament that has sufficient lifetime at 
high temperatures. This effect is being investigated now in these thin heavily cold-rolled 
tungsten plates which are doped with potassium.  
The investigated tungsten provided by PLANSEE SE has a potassium content of 80 parts per 
million (ppm), which is higher than that of commonly used plate materials. This amount of 
potassium doping is more typical for tungsten wires, where such wires were used for years as 
e.g. light bulb filaments or as Röntgen ray tubes[ref Coolidge 1913]. The potassium doping is 
the reason behind its name with W standing for Wolfram, K for potassium doping and 80 for 
80 ppm. Due to the high melting temperature, as with any tungsten, the sheets have to be 
produced by sintering of tungsten powder. The resulting cold rolled sheets have a dimension 
of 180 x 30 x 0.1 mm3 along the transversal, rolling and normal directions (referred to as TD, 
RD and ND), which are then cut by electro discharge machining into pieces of 5.5 x 4.5 x 0.1 
mm3 [ref Umberto paper 2].

2.6. X-Ray Diffraction 
  
X-Ray diffraction was used in this project in order to analyse the WK80 specimen in-situ while 
it was being annealed. This method is amongst the most effective for determining the 
microstructural development in materials, where all the data comes from only the structure 
and not the chemical composition of the material[]10.  
 
The analysis is done using X-rays which are a form of electromagnetic radiation with short 
wavelengths and high energies. They are produced by accelerating electrons using a high-
voltage field which are then shot into a metal target. By the sudden deceleration of the 
electrons, their energy is converted to the energy of the X-ray radiation, where the 
wavelength of the resulting X-ray is reversely related to the acceleration voltage of the 
electrons[ref ch.2.1.1]. The physical principle behind this generation can be seen in figure 6.  
 
When an accelerated electron has enough energy to release an electron from an inner shell 
(eg.: K-shell), the vacancy will be filled by an electron from an outer shell. When relocating 
the falling electron releases an X-ray with a characteristic wavelength or a photon with 
Figure 6 Characteristic X-ray radiation 
==PAGE:22==
Thermal stability of tungsten materials for fusion reactors 
22 
 
specific energy. If a K-shell were to have a hole, it can be filled by an electron from a higher 
shell. This electron jump produces a characteristic Kα X-ray when coming from the L-shell or 
a Kβ X-ray when the incoming electron originates in the M-shell (see figure 6a). In this 
described case of the K-shell hole, it is much more likely that the electron will come from the 
L-shell and as a result Kα radiation is more intense than Kβ radiation. The Kα radiation has 
two different wavelengths due to the structure of the L-shell where the L-shell consists of 3 
individual orbits. The jumping electron can come either from the L2 or L3 shells, but not from 
the L1 shell. As can be seen in figure 6b the Kα1 radiation comes from the L3 shell and the Kα2 
radiation comes from the L2 shell, where Kα1 has a slightly shorter wavelength and double 
the intensity of the Kα2 radiation. The doubling of the intensity comes from the fact that the 
L3 shell has 4 electrons as opposed to the 2 electrons in the L3 shell.  The Kα1 and Kα2 radiation 
are often referred to as the Kα doublet and are the most widely used monochromatic X-ray 
sources for diffraction work[ref]. 
  
The diffraction of X-rays can either be ‘’inphase’’, where constructive interference will 
happen, or they can be ‘’out of phase’’ and as a result destructive interference will occur. In 
order to be able to obtain information on the lattice spacing  from the diffracted X-rays, the 
diffracted waves cannot interfere destructively with each other. Bragg’s law (equation 9) 
shows which beams will be inphase:  
nλ = 2dsinθ

In Bragg’s law n refers to an integer, λ is the wavelength of the incident beam, d is the lattice 
spacing and θ is the incident angle. An illustration of a diffracted beam can be seen in figure 
7, where the incident angles of two inphase waves and the lattice spacing of the studied 
material can be seen.   
An XRD analysis will result in a diffractogram which shows the counts of intensity at 2θ angles 
for which Bragg’s law is fulfilled. By the exact position of the resulting peaks a specific lattice 
plane can be identified when compared to a database of already examined materials.  
Background noise will always be present in a XRD experiment and needs to be accounted for. 
It can happen due to small variations in the parameters, e.g., the noise from the machine itself 
or an imperfectly planar surface of the investigated sample. The prominence of the 
background noise (or inversely the signal-to-noise ratio) can be decreased by an increase in 
intensity of the incident beam, a longer counting time or by changing the amount of surface 
area scanned. 
The peak position in the diffractogram as mentioned previously correlates to the lattice plane 
spacing. When investigating a sample during annealing the thermal expansion of the material 
will lead to larger spacings in the lattice. This will result in a shift of the peak towards lower

2θ angles, which can be seen in Bragg’s law where the wavelength stays the same but  an 
increase in lattice spacing will lead to a lower 2θ angle.    
A parameter that was monitored in the XRD experiment was the full width at half of the 
maximum intensity (FWHM). This is the width of the measured peak at its half maximum 
intensity when accounting for the background noise intensity as well. Since this parameter is 
sensitive and the area under the peak (known as integrated intensity) remains constant, 
FWHM shows the progression of any structural changes. The width comes from the present 
stresses and defects. The width change can happen because of plastic deformation or in the 
case of the investigated annealing phenomena it can change due to grain growth, recovery or 
recrystallization.  The annealing related processes lead to a narrowing of the peak’s width and 
enables the monitoring of the processes. 
After data has been obtained from the experiment, a fitting process needs to be done in order 
to be able to have a mathematical approximation of the obtained peaks and their shapes. This 
is done using the pseudo-Voigt function, which is a combination of a Gaussian and a 
Lorentzian distribution function. A pseudo-Voigt profile with several ratios of the Lorentz and 
Gauss functions can be seen in figure 8, with the black line showing a fully Gaussian 
distribution and the red line a fully Lorentzian distribution. These ratios are adjusted for each 
obtained peak to have the best approximation possible. Usually the Gaussian distribution 
shows the impact of the strains in the material and the Lorentz distribution represents the 
effect of the grain size.

3.2.1. Hardness measurement 
 
This study tracks the annealing related phenomena and their effect on the strength of the 
specimen. As strength is related to hardness, a Vickers hardness tester Struers Duroscan 70-
G5 (figure 19) was used to track the strength of samples that have undergone various lengths 
of annealing time and have been prepared according to the previously described procedure 
in the form of grinding and polishing. Using the Duroscan a series of 24 indents on the W-
ALMT sample are made, 12 indents each for the core and the edge region of the TD-ND

surface of the sample. These 2 regions are treated as 2 separate measurements of 12 points, 
where from each measurement the highest and lowest values are discarded. This is done in 
order to eliminate any outliers and the resulting 10 data points give a sufficient sampling size 
in order to determine the final surface hardness.  
  
It is important to have a minimum space of 0.6 mm between the sample’s edge and the 
indents as to not be measuring the edge of the sample which could lead to inaccurate results. 
A 0.6 mm gap between each indent has to be ensured in order to avoid the overlap of any 
plastic deformation zone that is always created when the indenter penetrates the sample 
surface.  A sufficient plane parallelity between the TD-ND surface of the sample and the 
microscope objective must be ensured. The planarity is provided by the previously mentioned 
sample holders, where the specimens are placed and initially adjusted by eye before grinding. 
The subsequent preparation steps will eliminate any residual unevenness due to the 
uniformly applied load. If the planarity is off, the created diamond shape indents might have 
a dominant ‘’lengthening’’ in the direction of the slope of the surface. These kinds of faulty 
measurements have to be discarded and redone once they reach a disparity in diagonal length 
of more than 5%, since the resulting data points are inaccurate.

3.2.2. X-ray diffraction  
The WK80 specimen was investigated using X-ray diffraction to monitor the changes in the 
internal strain. The experiment was done ‘’in-situ’’, meaning the sample was being scanned 
while under an elevated temperature. The diffractometer used was a parallel beam 
diffractometer, specifically a BRUKER D8 DISCOVER, with copper radiation. 
  
 
3.2.2.1. 
In-situ XRD 
The material can be investigated after it has undergone annealing for a certain time. This 
provides good information on the material, but the obtained data point provides information 
only at a certain point in time. Assumptions are made  of the behaviour of the material 
between the investigated points and in most cases this leads to a correct characterization of 
the material. The in-situ experiment provides almost continuous data of the development of 
the microstructure during annealing and thus leads to a more accurate and complete picture 
of the material’s behaviour. Tracking the annealing of a single sample over time as opposed 
to annealing multiple samples also helps to minimize the effect of sample variation on the 
results. 
==PAGE:36==

In order to be able to setup the experiment a quick initial scan of the WK80 specimen needs 
to be done in order to be able to identify the 2θ ranges where the {200} plane-family are. 
Since this quick scan data is not used to characterize the material’s behaviour a low step 
number and higher scan speed is used. This measurement revealed the positions of the peaks 
at room temperature, where the 200 peak is at 58.1° and the 400 peak is at 153°. 
Subsequently the scan ranges of 2θ were chosen to be 50°-62° and 142°-160° for the 200 and 
400 peaks respectively. These ranges have to be sufficiently large because it is important to 
be able to scan the peak completely including the very end of their tails, since peak shape and 
area under the peak provide information about the state of the material. It is also important 
to note that the peak positions were first obtained at room temperature and a material will 
expand when heated, increasing its lattice spacing. The expansion will lead to a shift in the 
peak’s position towards a lower 2θ angle , therefore the ranges have to be sufficiently large 
to scan the peaks fully even at elevated temperatures. With the previously mentioned ranges 
this is ensured, since the θ shift was previously calculated to be -0.3° and -2.4° for the 200 and 
400 peaks at 1100°C[ref Kevin Thomas 7.15.4]. 
     
 
3.2.2.2. 
XRD setup

heating band inside a BTS-basic/solid vacuum dome made of beryllium(figure 20). The dome 
with a thickness of 0.5 mm permits X-rays to pass through and scan the sample unobstructed, 
due to beryllium having a low electron density and thus a very small interference. The sample 
inside the BRUKER D8 Discover needs to be non-moving when using the dome and so only the 
source and the detector are moving when changing the 2θ angle. The source needed to be 
switched to a copper source because the machine is usually run with a chromium source 
frequently used to analyse steels. A chromium source would deliver a weaker signal and not 
reveal the 400 peak at all. After the sample has been mounted to the base of the beryllium 
dome and the heating band, the X-rays are aligned to the centre of the sample and the vertical 
position is adjusted.  
The wires for the heating band are connected to the power supply and the water coolant

4.2. 
As received grain size for W-ALMT 
One of the properties that has not been quantified for the as received W-ALMT was the grain 
size. Line intercept method was used to calculate the average grain size throughout the whole 
12 mm thickness of the plates. The method consists of counting the number of interceptions 
for a certain number of horizontal and vertical lines. Analysing the whole 12 mm thickness 
allows the inspection of 2 edge regions and the core region of the plate. The sample was 
prepared, etched and inspected using light optical microscopy. The plate was divided into 12 
parts of 1 mm in length, where the average grain size was calculated for each mm. In order to 
be able to have a meaningful result, there needed to be a sufficient number of counts. This 
was achieved by having 4 vertical lines and 9 horizontal lines. This proved to be enough since 
for every 1 mm long region a vertical count of 110-140 intersections and a horizontal count 
of  140-170 intersections was achieved. After obtaining the interception counts the total 
length of the region’s lines were divided by the number of interceptions. The lines had a total 
length in each region of 4 mm and 6.3 mm for the vertical and horizontal lines respectively. A 
part of the as received CHW sample can be seen in figure 25 (note that the figure is rotated 
90°) 
 In figure 26 the resulting grain sizes can be seen. Overall, not a lot of variation can be seen in

5. Conclusion  
 
The mapped decrease in hardness over annealing time has shown the behaviour of the W-
ALMT plates in detail. The ITER and CHW show similar behaviour over time with relatively 
close times to reach a fully recrystallized state. The main difference between the CHW and 
ITER plates is the difference in the behaviour of the core and edge regions. It is very clear that 
the restoration processes are delayed in the case of ITER edge more than with the CHW edge 
region, but the ITER and CHW core regions behave in a similar fashion to each other. The delay 
in the ITER edge region leads to a more pronounced difference in the behaviour of the two 
regions. The CLW plate clearly has the best performance and resistance to recrystallization, 
but this is at a cost of a very sudden drop in hardness that happens over a shorter time period 
than that of the ITER and CHW plates. Both CHW and ITER plates have a gradual initial 
decrease in hardness values, but the CLW has a miniscule initial reduction in hardness in 
comparison.  
The obtained hardness values were used to map the time to half hardness reduction, using 
data for a range of temperatures from 1125°C to 1250°C. This showed that none of the W-
ALMT materials would be able to withstand operating temperatures of 1125°C for an 
operating time of 2 years, but if the operating temperature were to be successfully lowered 
in the future, these materials could be used as armour for fusion reactor components.  
The as received grain sizes were obtained to see the effect of the different manufacturing

methods on the W-ALMT plates, since the underlying material is the same. This revealed a 
different as received condition of the CLW plate when compared to the ITER&CHW plates. 
The CLW plate has a larger difference in grain size of its edge and core regions. The average 
aspect ratios of the grains were calculated and revealed a dominant elongation of the grains 
in the horizontal or rolling direction. The aspect ratio of CLW was found to be higher than of 
the other 2 plates, with a more prominent elongation in the horizontal direction.
