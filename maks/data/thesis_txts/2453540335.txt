Abstract
Our world is currently under the strong trend of connecting objects to the Internet. At
the same time our current communication technologies started to struggle with handling
mass scale deployments of Internet of Things (IoT) applications. As a consequence the
Low Power WAN (LPWAN) networks were designed. However, despite being energy effi-
cient and long range, high latency and issues with reliability became apparent. Therefore
the LPWAN technologies could not serve safety critical applications. The focus of this
thesis is to identify reasons and solve the issue of LPWAN unsuitability for safety criti-
cal deployments. Network slicing was identified as the most promising technology. The
benefit of network slicing in the LPWAN deployments would be traffic separation and
improvements of Quality of Service (QoS) for safety critical devices. However, during
work serious issue with the backhaul technology was identified. It was observed that
multi-hop radio paths in the backhaul exist, introduce additional delay. Therefore, the
work was directed towards solving that issue. To improve latency the design of Long
Range WAN (LoRaWan) gateway integrated with an eNodeB was suggested. To improve
reliability handover procedure to Narrowband IoT (NB-IoT) was proposed. Modelling
and discrete event simulations were conducted using Network Simulator 3 (NS3) simu-
lator framework. Functionality for the baseline scenario was implemented and validated.
Implementation of proposed modifications as well as network slicing were identified as
possible avenues of future research.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The Internet of Things (IoT) is constantly influencing our daily life. Everyday, peo-
ple around the world use smart devices to track their activities, remotely control their
households or use smart features of their cars[2]. That makes the IoT one of the key
driving forces of our civilization. It is estimated that the total value of smart-device
market currently oscillates around 190 billion US dollars and the forecast is that its
value will reach over 1100 billion USD by the year 2026[16]. That creates huge oppor-
tunity for startups and vendors of devices. As the industry grows we can observe new
IoT applications rise in the safety critical equipment areas. Autonomous cars [37], Un-
manned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) [12] and health monitoring equipment[35] are the perfect
examples of such applications. On the other hand there is number of applications with
much lower failure consequences, such as outdoor temperature sensors. Due to those
requirements, providing communication service for all kinds of IoT applications is a big
challenge. There are many factors that have to be considered, and the scale of deploy-
ment creates huge burden on network designers and operators. Technologies currently
established within the industry are quickly becoming unsuitable for the huge amount
of end devices[25]. Therefore, new family of IoT communication protocols, called Low
Power Wide Area Networks (LPWAN) such as Narrowband IoT (NB-IoT), Sigfox, Long
Range WAN (LoRaWAN) were created to meet the requirements of those new IoT ap-
plications. However, their authors were concerned with providing connectivity, while
the requirements of safety critical applications were of lower priority. Nevertheless, low
deployment cost and good coverage made them the most popular way of communication
for IoT applications[9].
Several factors are mentioned as obstacles preventing LPWAN technologies to be used
in safety critical deployments. The most impacting are high delay and data loss [7]. The
experience shows that application of a network slicing technology brings a significant
improvement [10] of the aforementioned issues in other network technologies.
Therefore, this work investigates the reasons for insufficient Quality of Service (QoS),
occurring in the LPWAN networks. The QoS it is a set of factors with the most impact
on transmission quality. These are end-to-end delay, throughput, packet loss. Since
the time of delivery and completeness of data are especially important in the real-time
communication systems, they are likely to suffer the most from poor QoS.
First of all, the goal of the thesis is to investigate reasons for unsuitability of LPWAN
networks for the real-time communication at the example of remote UAV application
control. Secondly, to answer the question what are the possible solutions for those prob-
lems and if network slicing can be one of them.
The thesis presents complete solution that is verified with modeling and simulation in

Our research shows that there are several reasons for high delay and possible packet loss.
The most critical reason is lack of customizable resource distribution that would depend
on priority of the application. The network slicing then is definitely one of the solutions
that can make a positive impact in that matter. However, the work showed that there
is also space for improvements in the backhaul design of the LPWAN networks. Multi-
hop radio paths introducing additional delay were identified. Therefore this thesis is
focused on the improvements of the backhaul technology. As a part of the work, model
of current network and backhaul was implemented as an NS3 model and solutions for
multi-hop radio path was prepared. The thesis presents combined entity for LoRaWAN
and the Long Term Evolution (LTE) called modified eNodeB, as well dual-connectivity
for end-devices as a solution increasing reliability.
The thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 presents necessary theoretical information
and concepts; Chapter 3 identifies problems and presents solutions; Chapter 4 describes
design, validation criteria and process of the network model; Chapter 5 defines simulation
scenarios; Finally, in Chapter 6 conclusions are drawn.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
LPWAN technologies
LPWAN (Low Power WAN) technologies [38] are defined as a low bit-rate communication
networks, designed for long-range communication (up to 10km) under constrained energy
resources. They are widely applied within the IoT industry especially for deployments
with battery-powered devices. Currently, there are several major players on the market,
described in this chapter.
2.1.1
LoRaWAN
The LoRaWAN is a wireless transmission network designed for use on the distance up to
10km. Among its main characteristics are low data rate up to 50kb/s and high robustness
for interference. The main purpose of the network is to transport user data from the
end device up to the network core, where they can be processed. The technology may
operate at different license-free frequency bands like 169MHz, 433MHz, or 868MHz. As a
highlight, the technology implements modulation technique derived from the chirp spread
spectrum, that offers a great trade-off between data rate and coverage. End devices
operating within the network are extremely efficient in terms of power consumption and
according to the vendor they may operate up to 10 years on single AA battery [13].
Network architecture
The standard LoRaWAN deployment is presented in the figure 2.1.
From the top-down view, there are several main entities within the LoRaWAN network:
end-device, LoRa gateway, and LoRa network server.
The end-device nodes are also often referred to as sensors and its main task is to interact
with the environment and communicate over LoRa radio with the gateway that concen-
trates traffic and forwards it to the Network Server.
The Network Server adds logic to the network in other words, fulfills tasks of message
routing and scheduling downlink messages.
Following the multipath algorithm implemented in the network server, each end de-
vice may simultaneously send messages to multiple gateways. Primary role of gateways
within LoRa is forwarding messages from end-device to the network server without fur-
ther integration into the payload.

Modulation
The LoRaWAN physical interface operates using a proprietary modulation scheme that
was derived from Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS). The CSS implements variable data
rate for different transmission conditions utilizing orthogonal spreading factors. This
way the node which is in poor signal conditions is provided with lower data rates but
higher resiliency for noise.
It is vital to highlight that as in other wireless systems, data transmission over the
LoRa channel is associated with constantly increasing noise liming channel capacity.

be successfully sent can be derived from the formula:

Knowing the fact that channel capacity is inherently dependent on SNR, methods for
reducing the impact of that factor are desired. The Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum
(DSSS) is a traditional method of reducing the signal to noise ratio. Figure 2.2 presents
DSSS modulation scheme. The input data are multiplied by the code sequence with a
number of shifting chips. As an effect, we get modulated signal with higher bandwidth
than the input signal that in turn can be transmitted over the wireless medium. Knowing
the code sequence, the operation is reversible at the receiver side. Hence, according to
formula 2.1 we are able to reduce the impact of SNR on the transmitted signal. However,
the DSSS modulation technique might require a precise clock in the transmitting devices
as well as the significant processing power of those. Considering the cost and limitations
of embedded systems as well as their power efficiency, implementation of DSSS might
be infeasible.

Scheduling
As already mentioned, the LoRaWAN is designed for use in cases where most of the com-
munication happens in the uplink direction. That is directly related to energy efficiency.
The LoRa end-devices are optimized to spend the most of time in the state where the
radio can remain turned off. In order to achieve that, the vendors designed a scheduling
system where end-device’s radio interface is only accepting downlink transmissions for
a short period of time after the uplink transmission. Figure 2.4 presents a scheduling
strategy from the perspective of the end device. The TX message can be transmitted any
time data are available. Immediately after the transmission, the end device schedules
two timers RW1 and RW2. Then receive window remains open for RW1 amount of time.
If data are received both windows are closed and the device is ready to transmit the next
portion of data. However, in case of no data received during RW1 transmission window,
the RW2 timer is still active and the end device waits for downlink data. After the data
are received or RW2 is expired, the transmission is considered as finished. The values
of RW1 and RW2 are propagated to end devices during the initial connection procedure
[13].

2.1.2
NB-IoT
Another technology and at the same time the biggest competitor[27] for LoRaWAN is
Narrowband IoT (NB-IoT). The technology evolved from the LTE network and con-
stantly gains popularity due to new deployments. Same as LoRa the NB-IoT is designed
for battery-operated and power-efficient deployments. However, the biggest difference is
maximum data rate granted for a single end-device. Contradictory to LoRa, the NB-IoT
provides a five times higher data rate reaching up to 250kbps in both uplink and down-
link direction. Even though the technology evolved from previous 3GPP’s standards it
does not provide backward compatibility hence, already deployed devices cannot gain
access to the NB-IoT network. [5]

2.1.3
Sigfox
Sigfox is a closed full stack technology providing LPWAN. It works under the assump-
tion of Connectivity as a Service (CaaS) and uses D-BPSK modulation technique for
radio transmission. The operation band is unlicensed and publicly available, therefore
depending on the geographical region the maximum speed varies between 100bps and
600bps. According to the vendors Sigfox uses lightweight protocol that introduces low
overhead into the messages. Moreover, single device is allowed to transmit only 140
messages per day to the Sigfox cloud. [33]
Network architecture
Figure 2.7 presents the architecture of the Sigfox network. The sensors are connected to
the Sigfox antenna which is inter-connected with Sigfox AP. The Sigfox AP is one of key
entities in the system since it is responsible for the authentication of the user as well as
conversion of Sigfox frames into IP packets that are then sent to the Sigfox cloud. The
AP might be back-hauled with various technologies like LTE for remote deployments
or wired networks like fiber or ADSL if the deployment environment allows. Since the
Sigfox cloud is provided as a service there is no possibility for standalone deployments
by the end user.[33]

Radio technology
Sigfox is operated in 868 MHz spectrum and it uses UNB [3] signals with duty cycle for
data transmission. That limits end-devie possibility to transmit infrequently and for a
very short time, at the same time increasing energy efficiency. Figure 2.8 presents the
way of resource allocation. As already mentioned, 100bps D-BPSK modulation scheme
was used, that causes sub-carrier spectrum of 100Hz. However, allocation of full 192kHz
is required to provide sufficient transmission quality. The modulation is centered with
Fc frequency, the sub-carrier is selected from range of 150kHz and it’s frequency equals
to Fc+Foffset [29].

Sigfox protocol
Following the assumption of energy saving, only minimal amounts of data may be sent
over the Sigfox protocol. The end device is restricted to follow convention of message size
up to 12 bytes. As a result the total frame size is 24 bytes including protocol overhead.
Considering data rate of 100bps, the transmission time of single frame may vary between
1 and 4 seconds where typically it will be around 1-2 seconds [29].

CHAPTER 3
Problem definition
Current deployments of telecommunication solutions for IoT are designed under the
assumption of best effort data transmission especially, when it comes to the LPWAN
networks. Provided resources are not separated between individual costumers hence, re-
source distribution does not match a real demand of the applications. Some applications
may starve others in a non-predictable way and without any control. In technologies
such as public LoRaWAN or Sigfox that problem was solved by limiting traffic for each
user. The Sigfox type networks limit each user with up to 144 messages per day, while
public LoRaWAN deployments introduce 1% duty cycle per each end device. Therefore
resources in transmission are limited equally for any application regardless of current
network load.
Unfortunately because of too high restrictions such as duty cycle the
approach is infeasible for more demanding applications that work in real-time. Together
with the problem of weak resource management, the problem of in-suitable backhaul
arises. Resources in the backhaul can be easily overprovisioned or the backhaul can be
underestimated. That can create further troubles with an efficiency of resource utiliza-
tion in other networks like LTE. In some deployments the resources assigned for the
backhaul are not constant and gateways must compete for bandwidth with end users of
that networks.
To exemplify this, let’s assume minimal load on the LoRaWAN gateway and LTE link
as a backhaul providing up to 300Mbps bandwidth. That is a clear example of resource
overprovisioning.
Returning to the radio resource management the use case of the UAV/drone control can
be brought[1]. Since all three technologies mentioned in the background section are long
range, they might be suitable for that purpose with exception to the Sigfox since there
is no possibility to regulate the daily limit of messages. Though, for clearance of the big
picture and possibility of the private network deployment the LoRaWAN was chosen as
a focus technology. Regardless that choice, other technology might be used as well after
required modifications in the resource provisioning area that suits demand of real-time
traffic application.
The UAV might be controlled over the radio from a distant location using the LoRaWAN
network device by sending GPS location of the drone in LoRaWAN uplink messages and
getting steering commands through the downlink message. In that scenario multiple
short messages are sent in both directions, hence required bandwidth is minimal and fits
well the LoRaWAN specification. However, due to the time critical nature of the mes-
sages, very low latency is required. Practically, that means the network has to be either
serving single application or be designed to prevent potential disturbance from other
coexisting applications. The goal of this work is hence to check whether the network

slicing application can reduce currently seen interference. Hence if a real-time commu-

3.1
Minimal slice creation
As described in Chapter 2, the network slicing is an overall idea for network virtualization
and may have many different forms when it comes to the implementation. Beginning
from the simple connection of the NFV and SDN to the sophisticated frameworks like
FLARE. However, most of the approaches struggle with the same problem which is cre-
ation of the minimal slice by defining accurate granularity of the NFVs.
Each slice usually consists of a set of virtualized resources that are then mapped into the
physical resources. The problem of minimal slice creation is crucial because it should
allocate only necessary amount of resources for given architecture type to make it usable.
That is very important from the perspective of resource utilization to avoid overprovi-
sioning.
When it comes to the network slicing in mobile networks, initial work was done by
5g!Pagoda [24] and the work is further continued towards solution with a small granu-
larity of VNFs that are then composing individual slices. The minimal slice described in
said document provides bare connectivity with no security and no delay or bandwidth
guarantees. On top of that additional features can be added with great flexibility.
That still leaves the issue of network slicing within the LoRaWAN network open. As
a minimal slice only basic features should be considered.
Therefore, provisioning of
resources should not exceed one currently given for the public LoRaWAN networks.
3.2
Slice orchestration
In order to create a fair solution in terms of flexibility, complexity and costs of deploy-
ment, the case evaluation has to be done. Chapter 2 section presents different approaches
to slice orchestration. However, depending on whether the whole network is in one ad-
ministrative domain or multiple, additional orchestration may be needed.
The situation is relatively easy for homogeneous networks that spreads over multiple ad-
ministrative domains. Networks with the same slice orchestration technology does not
require additional orchestrators. However, in some cases where networks are heteroge-
neous and multiple slice orchestration technologies are deployed, additional orchestrator
supporting 3rd party orchestration has to be used [32].

Network model
To evaluate the influence of changes discussed in the previous section, the modeling
and simulation method is used.
The NS3 simulation environment was used.
It is a
discrete event simulator designed to perform simulations on the Internet type networks.
Extended framework and C++ programming language were a determinant choice for the
simulator. Moreover, until now many additional modules were released and are publicly
available. The alternative software OPNET Modeler was considered as well, however
due to the high licence cost and closed source network modules it was not chosen.
The NS3 simulation environment consists of two major parts.
The first is a model
view where network devices, packets and links are defined in the form of C++ classes.
The second is a topology script that defines a simulation scenario. In the topology script
network entities are deployed and network stacks are created. Additionally, the simulator
allows to use wide library of ready-to-use models of most common protocols.
Since the model of the network is not a direct implementation, but rather its abstract
form, used models do not usually have exactly the same features as corresponding devices
in the real world. However, the functionality necessary for evaluation of measured values
always corresponds to the specification of modeled protocol.
4.1
LoRaWAN model design
The model of LoRaWAN used for the simulations was derived from the existing study on
the scalability evaluation of LoRaWAN networks [4]. The model consists of two major
entities that are used as nodes for simulation of LoRaWAN network behaviour. The end
device entity represents an abstraction of the end device class A. The LoRaWAN gate-
way is another entity that’s behaviour can be simulated. Both devices can be connected
with a LoRa radio spectrum channel that models loss of power and adds erros over the
radio link.
The model of all entities was designated upon assumption of testing capabilities of the
LoRaWAN radio interface, especially occurring interference and errors during transmis-
sions. Moreover, the impact of different types of messages on the overall performance can
be tested. Therefore, gateways and network server were implemented as a single entity.
Moreover, the model did not support connectivity through a link between gateways and
a server. Instead, all the operations were handled by function calls on the programming
language level. Figure 4.1 well illustrate the described situation. The network server is
virtual and does not reside on any of the nodes.

scheduled and server returns to the Idle.

However as model validation later showed, the approach was not consistent with the
technology specification.
Referring back to the original design, presented in figure 4.3, there are a couple of inaccu-
racies. First is immediate feedback from the gateway about available resources which is
not available in real world systems. Therefore, after removing software calls between the
server and the gateway, the feedback will be delayed with at least delay occurring on the
link between the gateway and the server. Secondly, the downlink transmission occurs
after RW1 and RW2 timer expires, so always at the end of the transmission window.
That in turn, leads to the situation where even a little delay on the link between the
server and gateway makes a significant impact in the DL message delivery rate.
As a countermeasure, the downstream conditions were changed. The first issue is the
lack of immediate feedback from the gateway. Therefore, the correction is sending the
DS message through the gateway from which last US message was received. Moreover,
two additional conditions apply before the message can be transmitted. First, any of
the RW timers must me active and the second, DL message must exist. Those condi-
tions are assert at the end of each US packet reception and after each generation of the
DL message. The downlink transmission scheduling system was modified as well. After
successful reception of the US packet, the RW1 and RW2 timers are scheduled.

the connections within LTE. It is responsible for connection establishment and release
as well as mobility control.
Secondly, the RRC layer is connected with the number of Packet Data Convergence
Protocol (PDCP) connections. The PDCP connection is created every time new radio
bearer is established by the RRC. The PDCP protocol is responsible for transfer of
user and control plane data as well as serving some security functions. The Radio Link
Control (RLC) layer is responsible for the transport of received PDCP messages to the
other end of the radio interface. The functionality of RLC is among others segmentation
control, error correction and duplication control. The MAC and PHY layers are the
layers directly controlling sending radio messages over the interface.

stratum (NAS) is a bearer between the UE and the EPC. Rest of the stack functions as
a mirror of the eNodeB stack.

4.4
Model validation
4.4.1
Validation criteria
There are several key parameters to check whether the model produces correct results
and therefore if results are valid to interpret. These parameters are:
- Delay between the end device and the gateway
- Packet loss between the end-device and the LoRaGateway
- End-to-end packet loss
- End-to-end delay
The model validation have to be performed using 100 LoRaWAN end devices transmit-
ting 21 bytes of data to the network server every 6 minutes. The network server then
replies with a downlink message to each end device every 30 minutes.
The outcome of experiment should be as follows. The packet loss on the LoRaWAN
radio should not exceed 5% with a delay for single message not exceeding 2 seconds. Ad-
ditionally, the end to end packet loss should not be higher than 6% as the LTE network
while skipping the radio interface should be a reliable transmission medium. The end to
end delay should remain around the same value as for LoRa radio interface. Since the
delay through EPC in real life deployments is around maximum 30ms (value for ideal
full LTE conditions), the additional overhead is negligible.
4.4.2
Validation process
Simulation on the two model versions was performed and packet delivery statistics were
tested. The simulation time for both versions was 1 hour and other parameters as de-
scribed in the validation criteria.
Primarily, the original version of the model without modifications was tested. Figure
4.18 presents statistics of end-to-end downlink messages delivery for unmodified model.
The messages were sent from end-device and captured by the network server being vir-
tual entity as in figure 4.1. We can observe result of 100% messages delivery. Which
definitely meets previously set standard. Therefore, the conclusion can be drown that
this version of model is valid for packet delivery criteria.
Secondly, figure 4.19 presents modified version of the model from figure 4.8 before intro-
duction of changes for RW timers scheduling that were described later. The Ethernet
link between entities did not introduce any packet loss and introduced minimal delay.
Therefore, it should meet validation criteria. However, we can observe significant packet
loss of downlink messages reaching 10%. That does not meet specified validation cri-
teria and therefore modified model cannot be considered as valid. Finally after careful
analyses, the new design of RW timers scheduling policy was designed and presented in
the ”LoRa server modifications” subsection of this chapter.

CHAPTER 5
Simulations scenarios
In the previous chapter, the model design of the network entities was described. The
description was followed by validation criteria necessary to assume that the model can be
used for further research. After ascertainment that the criteria are fulfilled, the simula-
tion can be performed by following simulation scenarios. This chapter describers precise
architecture of simulated networks, measured values as well as predicted outcomes. That
creates a complete simulation scenarios description that can be compared between each
other and therefore will provide the means for the conclusion of the design choices.
5.1
Baseline scenario
The baseline scenario portrays networks widely deployed within the industry. The main
characteristics of the network architecture are multiple radio links and no guarantees
on the latency within the network. Moreover, as already described in the model design
section, certain level of abstraction is taken as an assumption.
Figure 5.1 presents a draft of the network architecture that consists of one LoRaWAN
gateway, one eNodeB a network server and number of end devices referred to sensors.
Since the study is not focused on network scalability, but its performance evaluation
under normal conditions, the number of end devices equals to 50.
Each device transmits data to the network server with message inter-arrival time of
100ms, that follows 3GPP study recommendations on LTE support for aerial devices [31].
However, due to the lower protocol overhead the message size differs from recommended
and equals 200 bytes. Since maximum bitrate that LoRaWAN can provide is 50kbps the
value of 625 bytes is maximum for transmission in 100ms intervals.
In order to minimize the impact of statistical errors and at the same time to create
meaningful sample size, the simulation time should be not shorter than 1 hour.
Moreover, the simulation shall be performed under different LTE network conditions.
So the primary simulation variable is a distance of LoRaWAN gateway from eNodeB.
The measured values in this simulation scenario will be end-to-end delay and end-to-end
packet loss. The forecast outcome of the simulation is observation of large end-to-end
delay exceeding 100ms that grows with the distance of of LoRaWAN gateway from
eNodeB. Moreover, end-to-end packet loss may occur.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
Due to great coverage and low cost, the importance of the LPWAN technologies for
IoT industry cannot be underestimated. However, as the number of connected devices
is growing and more heterogeneous applications are appearing on the market, their co-
existence becomes a significant burden. Therefore, we need to address that issue and
prepare a solution that will combine both low cost and QoS sufficient to meet the needs
of all applications. Since the first requirement is already satisfied by LPWAN networks,
improving upon them seem to be the most efficient way to proceed. The network slic-
ing technology is one of the enablers that can provide a balance between the cost of
deployment and performance. That is possible since the goal of network slicing is to
create an abstract overlays over the physical infrastructure that using virtualization will
separate prioritized traffic from the best-effort traffic. Moreover, the prioritized traffic
in network slicing has QoS guarantees while the best effort traffic is transmitted with
no guarantees.
The primary aim of the thesis was to identify issues making LPWAN protocols unsuitable
for real-time communications. The secondary goal was to investigate possible solutions
for this very problem. The assumption that network slicing might solve the problem
was followed by an empirical study. Additionally, the side goal of the research was to
prepare a solution minimizing end-to-end delay in current LWPAN deployments.
The study has taken a practical approach to the problem and solution was prepared for
the existing use case of remote UAV control. The first identified issue was the existence
of multiple hops connected with radio links in the backhaul. Each of the hops introduces
an additional delay that negatively impacts overall transmission quality. As a counter-
measure, the gateway and eNodeB were combined and therefore the number of hops was
reduced. The second issue was lack of traffic separation, therefore unfair resource distri-
bution. The problem was resolved by proposing a design integrating network slicing into
the LPWAN network. Finally, the last identified issue was possible unreliability of the
network in case of poor radio conditions. It was resolved by a handover to a different
transmission technology.
Despite the fact, that only UAV use case was analyzed, the methodology employed is
sound and can be reused for different scenarios.
Designed solutions were described in the form of a computer model which is a base for
simulations. The model for the baseline simulation scenario was implemented and vali-
dated. Since the validation did not yield positive results, the modelling process has been
reiterated. A corrected solution was prepared and implemented, however, the validation
process was not repeated due to high complexity of the problem and time constrains.
Those missing elements of the implementation can be subject to future work. Finally,
==PAGE:54==
6 Conclusions
45
simulation scenarios for the design evaluation were prepared and forecasts about the
results were stated.
The research presents a complete solution to the problem of real-time communication
over LPWAN networks. Even though the evaluation part is still incompleate, initial
analyses seem very promising. Moreover, the developed model is unique and presents
significant value for further research in the domain of latency in LPWAN networks. The
design of the baseline scenario used for the simulation of current deployments may be
helpful in investigating and evaluating ideas in LPWAN networks.
The study can be extended with implementation of the network slicing model and de-
termine the impact of this technology on latency. Moreover, as stated in the problem
description, the solution has multiple aspects that can be evaluated. The most critical
parts lays behind the creation of minimal slice, intra-slice orchestration and inter-slice
orchestration. Referring back to the research goals, at least two main aims were com-
pletely fulfilled while the side goal was partially fulfilled. This supports a conclusion,
that the framework described in this work has potential and it is worth to continue
research in that direction.
