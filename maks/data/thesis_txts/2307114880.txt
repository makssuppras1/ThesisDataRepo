1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 BACKGROUND: THE DANISH CLIMATIC AND ENERGETIC GOALS 
 
The worldwide problem of climate change and the increase of energy demand in the last 
decades are moving societies toward more sustainable behaviours. Moreover, growing 
awareness about the scarcity of natural non-renewable fossil fuels resources is leading 
the use of new renewable energy sources. Energy systems throughout the world are 
facing the challenge of supplying existing and coming energy needs in a sustainable way 
that does not increase the carbon dioxide level in the atmosphere and thus enhance the 
greenhouse gases (GHG) effects any further. The climate change is real and it is evident 
from scientific observations such as the increase of earth’s surface and ocean average 
temperatures, the increasing rate of the ice/snow melted in the Polar regions and the 
consequential increase of the sea level [1].  The effects of GHGs emitted from human 
activities are proved and, without the implementation of intense climate change 
mitigation policies and related sustainable economic development practises, GHGs 
emissions are predicted to grow over the 21st century and beyond. 
The Danish government wants Denmark to contribute actively to meet the calls from 
scientists that significant reductions in greenhouse gas emissions are necessary, and this 
is why an historical new Energy Agreement was signed in Denmark in 2012 which sets 
the framework for the national green transition [2]. 
The Agreement contains a wide range of ambitious initiatives, bringing Denmark closer 
to the long term target of 100% renewable society in the energy and transport sectors 
by 2050. Moreover, the government has established as intermediate goal that the 
electricity and heating supply must be fully independent of fossil fuels by 2035.  
The energy agreement contributes to create green growth in Denmark. The initiatives 
point towards the long-term goals but at the same time the high level of security of 
supply in Denmark must be maintained. The agreement also contributes to achieve 
Danish climate-policy goals and at international level it brings Denmark to the forefront 
with regard to the green transformation of the energy system. In this context, the Danish 
transformation can be an example to be followed by other countries. Denmark can show 
that full transformation can be realised both technically and economically.  
As it is possible to read in [2]: “If Danish energy policy can create ripples globally, it could 
be perceived as a significant contribution to the fight against climate change. 
Improvements in energy efficiency and renewable energy will make Danish enterprises 
and households less vulnerable to the increasing prices of fossil fuels caused by global 
population growth and diminishing resources”. 
The Danish government’s climate and energy policy goals stated in the Agreement are 
summarized below [3]:

 The overall climate target set a total reduction in Danish greenhouse gas 
emissions of 40% by 2020, compared to the 1990 level.  
 The long-term benchmark is to contribute to the EU target to reduce greenhouse

global mitigation efforts. 
 Within the EU, Denmark has an obligation to reduce non-ETS emissions in the

together with residual resources such as waste and biomass. Many municipal and

technologies are introduced, the core elements of those scenarios include: the

1.2 AIM AND SCOPE 
In this context the district heating (DH) network plays an important role for the future 
sustainable energy systems because it allows implementing large scale renewable 
energy sources in the heating system. Hence the aim of this thesis work is to evaluate a 
possible future solution for the operation of the district heating network. 
The case study of this project is Roskilde district heating system. As most of the Danish 
DH it is a third generation DH. The idea of the thesis is to analyse the possibilities to 
make it work as a fourth generation low temperature district heating with the 
integration of booster electrical heat pumps.  
A technical model is first developed to simulate the state of the district heating network 
in terms of mass flow rates and temperatures.  Then an optimization problem is carried 
out to evaluate the introduction of booster heat pumps in the system, in terms of 
location and amount of supplied heat power, with the goal of minimize heat losses in 
the network, hence to reduce the temperature of the water flowing into the system. 
The results from the district heating simulation and optimization are then presented and 
discussed together with an economical evaluation. 
The project is divided into 8 chapters.  
After a general introduction on the Danish goals concerning climate and energy, chapter 
2 deals with the description of Danish district heating in the past, nowadays and in the 
future.  
In chapter 3 the methodology used for the model simulation and for the formulation of 
the optimization problem is described. 
In chapter 4 the district heating case study is presented and all the assumptions on the 
input parameters are stated. 
In chapter 5 the results from the simulation and optimization problem are presented. 
Chapter 6 deals with the economical evaluation of the optimized configuration of the 
analysed district heating. 
In chapter 7 a general discussion on the obtained results is presented and the last 
chapter contains the conclusion of the thesis.

2 DISTRICT HEATING 
In a temperate climate such as the Danish one, heating plays an important role. Today 
63% of heating in private Danish houses for both space heating (SP) and domestic hot 
water (DHW) is provided by district heating (DH) [9]. 
District heating basically makes use of heat produced in central locations and distribute 
it through pipelines to a large number of end users that can be for example a 
neighbourhood, a town centre or a whole city.  In that way heat, that has no or very low 
value in one place, can be transformed to high value energy source in places where there 
is a high demand for heat, such as small towns or large urban communities. 
This approach allows any available source of heat to be used, making the system very 
flexible and increasing both the security of supply and the production efficiency. 
If one unit breaks down, there are alternatives available and at any given time the 
district energy company may choose the cheapest fuel. Establishing a district heating 
network also allows utilizing low-quality heat in the society. This could be surplus heat 
from industry and waste incineration, and heat from combined heat and power (CHP) 
production. 
Combined heat and power production (CHP) is the currently main technology for 
producing heat in the Danish DH systems [10], and this is one of the most important 
reason why it has been possible to increase energy efficiency and to reduce the carbon 
emissions over several decades. When producing heat and power using CHP, the overall 
energy efficiency is significantly higher than when producing heat and power separately. 
A CHP plant may have a total efficiency (combined heat and power) of 85-90% resulting 
in an overall fuel saving of approximately 30%, compared to separate production of heat 
and electricity. 
2.1 BRIEF HISTORY OF DANISH DISTRICT HEATING 
 
The first CHP in Denmark was built back in 1903 [9]. It was small local waste incineration 
plant which made it possible to handle waste into an environment-friendly way, and to 
provide electricity and heat to a new hospital. From here on, DH from CHP systems 
expanded in the larger Danish cities and in 1970’s around 30% of all dwellings were 
heated with the use of district heating systems. By the time of the energy crisis in 
1973/74, energy consumption per capita had risen considerably. The energy crisis made 
it evident that saving energy was critical both to decrease the dependency of imported 
fuels and to reduce consumer heating costs. Therefore, a decision was made to expand 
the fuel-efficient CHP systems to not only the larger cities, but also to medium and 
smaller size cities in Denmark.  
Up to 1979 there was no law regulating the heat supply in Denmark. Most heat 
consumers had small oil-fired boilers or other forms of individual heating. In order to 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
 
fulfil the policy goals, Denmark passed its first heat supply law in 1979. The law 
contained regulations regarding the form and content of heat planning in Denmark and 
marked the beginning of a new era in public heat planning, which still exists today.

2.2.2 PLANNING AND REGULATION 
The Danish district heating legislation is set out in the Heat Supply Act from 1979 that 
regulates the heating sector and provides local authorities (i.e. municipalities) with the 
power to engage in local heat planning, decision-making on energy infrastructure and 
resource prioritisation. The legislation and accompanying guidelines were developed by 
the Danish Energy Agency (DEA), but the actual implementation of the legislation and 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
 
policies is under the responsibility of the local municipalities. The Danish approach to 
heat regulation provides a clear division of authority where local decision-makers have 
full authority over local heat system designs, but they do so by relying on a centralized 
policy and a technical framework provided by the national level. This ensures that 
district heating projects are in line with the overall national ambitions with regards to 
the development of the heat sector, but at the same time the evaluation and decision 
on a concrete heat project is undertaken by a local authority with detailed knowledge 
of local urban development, heat demand and any other relevant local considerations. 
Main principles of the initial Danish Heat Supply Act from 1979: 
 
• Local authorities/municipalities are responsible for the approval of new heat supply 
projects. 
• Local authorities/municipalities have to make sure that the heating project with the 
highest socioeconomic benefits is chosen. 
• Where possible, heat must be produced as combined heat and power. 
• The heat price cannot be higher or lower than the actual heat production costs. 
 
The Danish Heat Supply Act has also established specific zones of heat networks 
throughout the country. Within each zone a specific heat supply is promoted through 
the Danish heat legislation. The zones are as follows: Natural gas supplied through the 
natural gas grid, decentralised district heating, centralised district heating and individual 
heat supply. 
The price of heat is not the same in all Danish DH areas. The method for setting the heat 
price is regulated by law. The legislation states that the heat price paid by the consumer 
should cover all necessary costs related to supply heat. However, heat supply companies 
must remain not-for-profit, under Danish law. The heat plants cannot charge more for 
the heat than the actual costs of producing and transporting heat to consumers. These 
costs also include depreciation of assets and financing costs, so that the heating 
companies can be financially sustainable both in the short and long term. Cost of heat 
to the consumer is therefore affected by the following parameters: 
 Production facility investment 
 District heating network investment 
 Production facility operation and maintenance 
 District heating network operation and maintenance 
 Fuel prices 
 Efficiency of the production facility 
 Heat loss in the district heating network 
 Taxes and VAT 
 Financial support/grants 
==PAGE:20==
 
19 
 
 Electricity price (for DH production facilities that either use or produce 
electricity) 
2.2.3 THE IMPORTANCE OF HEAT STORAGE 
Today, all centralised CHP plants and most decentralised CHP plants sell electricity at 
the market price. Therefore, they must aspire to optimise their production according to 
the market price of electricity on the spot market, where prices are set for each hour 
(see paragraph 2.3.2). This means that CHP plant operators aim at producing most 
electricity and heat in cogeneration when electricity prices are high. Likewise, they try 
to minimise their production when electricity prices are low. This can be done by active 
use of the heat accumulators in the system.  
A heat storage can provide many advantages: 
 It improves the economy in a district heating system 
 The consumption can be decoupled from production  
 More sustainable heat sources can be implemented in the system 
 
Hot water can be accumulated with two different kinds of storage systems: the day-to-
day and the seasonal storage. 
The main purpose of the day-to-day (short-term) storage is to decouple the power and 
heat productions at the CHP plants so that it is possible to optimise their cogeneration 
according to the fluctuating electricity market price without compromising the heating 
supply. It introduces flexibility to the energy system, which is crucial for optimising the 
total system, both economically and environmentally. This kind of storage is also used 
when external heat production, which cannot be controlled by the district heating 
company, is introduced into the DH system such as the surplus heat from industrial 
plants where production varies throughout the day or week. 
The seasonal storage is a large scale heat storage which allows using heat from warmer 
to cooler seasons. In Denmark it is used mainly for large scale sun collectors that produce 
much more heat during the summer than what is needed immediately. These systems 
are typically a pit, i.e. a very large hole in the ground, with a liner in the bottom, filled 
with water and covered by a floating layer of insulation. Other solutions are ground 
water storage systems, where surplus heat can be stored at lower temperature and then 
utilised later at higher temperatures by using heat pumps. For both systems, the heat is 
stored for weeks or months until the heat demand rises and will be consumed during 
autumn and winter. These kinds of systems will become more and more important for 
the green transition of the DH network. 
 
In Denmark, more than 42% of all electricity comes from the wind turbines. Due to the 
fluctuating production from wind turbines, there is often surplus electricity at very low 
prices. In combination with both short and long term storage, the surplus electricity can 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
 
be used in the district heating system to produce hot water either directly by electric 
boilers or indirectly by heat pumps as it will be explained later in paragraph 2.3.  
2.3 FUTURE SUSTAINABLE DISTRICT HEATING 
District heating has an important role to play for the implementation of future 
sustainable energy systems and for the realization of the national green transition. 
Diminishing fossil fuel reserves and increasing climate change due to greenhouse gas 
emissions, have caused the need for a transition towards a Danish energy system that 
will be supplied by 100% renewable energy sources (RES) in 2050. Such transition 
requires introduction into the energy systems of large amounts of intermittent 
electricity production, from e.g. wind turbines and photovoltaic solar cells (PV), and 
great reductions in energy consumption, which will change the role that DH plays in the 
Danish energy system.  
One of the future challenges will be to integrate district heating with electricity sector 
as well as transport sector to create smart energy systems.  
The general trend in the development of the new generation of district heating systems 
is towards lower forward temperatures.  
In a historical perspective the evolution of DH has gone through three generations which 
are characterized by the type of transport media and by the network temperature levels. 
The new DH generation is the 4th generation of the technology development which 
reduces the network supply temperature down to consumer required level.  It follows 
the steam-based systems (1st generation DH), the high-temperature water systems (2nd 
generation DH with supply temperature above 100°C), and the medium temperature 
water systems (3rd generation with supply temperature < 100°C). The evolution of the 
DH network during the past years is represented in Figure 6. 
The trend throughout these first three generations of DH has been towards lower 
distribution temperatures, material lean components, and prefabrication leading to 
reduced manpower requirements at construction sites. Following these identified 
directions, a future fourth generation of district heating technology should involve lower 
distribution temperatures, assembly-oriented components, and more flexible pipe 
materials [11].

2.3.1 LOW TEMPERATURE DISTRICT HEATING  (LTDH) 
Low temperature district heating system allows to achieve two of the main 
requirements for the future district heating and the whole energy sector: high energy 
efficiency and high share of renewable energy. LTDH systems utilize supply and return 
==PAGE:23==
 
22 
 
temperatures of around 55/25°C which are sensibly lower compared to the current 
standard medium temperature district heating system, 80/40°C. 
A number of demonstration projects have proven that the district heating supply 
temperature at slightly above 50°C can meet the end-user’s Space Heating (SH) and 
Domestic Hot Water (DHW) demands in central-northern European climates, in properly 
designed and operated district heating networks and in-house installations [12]. The 
major advantages due to the reduced network temperature level are summarized below 
[13]: 
- 
Reduced network heat loss: a network with decreased temperature level and 
increased insulation can effectively reduce the heat loss along the pipeline 
which occur due to the temperature difference between the heat vector 
circulating into the pipe and the ground temperature. This increases supply side 
efficiency and competitiveness of district heating systems to supply also low 
energy buildings in low energy density areas. 
- 
Increased utilization of RE: thanks to the low operating temperatures, it is 
possible to exploit more RE sources and make them competitive to individual-
building heat generation technologies. In fact, heat vectors with a temperature 
below 60°C allow geothermal plants to be able to satisfy the base load demand; 
in the same way, the efficiency of solar thermal collector would increase, both 
in case of roof applications and large-scale solar thermal field. Moreover, the 
LTDH allows to integrate the intermittent renewable sources such as wind 
energy into the heating grid thanks to electrical heaters or heat pumps. 
- 
Reduced pipeline thermal stress: as the supply temperature lowers, the not 
uniform distributed temperature gradient along the pipeline decreases. 
Moreover, the thermal stress may lead to the risk of pipe leakage hence, this 
risk will be reduced as well as the maintenance costs. This will also prolong 
lifetime of DH network. 
- 
Reduced heat loss in thermal storage units: thermal storages in a DH system 
range from large seasonal storage tanks to small-scale tanks at the consumers’ 
installation. Lower network temperature will reduce the heat loss in thermal 
storage tanks. 
- 
Improved power to heat ratio in CHP plants: the profit from a CHP plant greatly 
depends on the amount of power being generated. This is particular relevant 
under a liberalized electricity market. Low network supply and return 
temperatures allow to extract more power from the steam expansion process 
as demonstrated in [14]. 
- 
Increased heat pumps efficiency: the efficiency of electric heat pumps is 
function of the sink temperature; hence, without considering the type of heat 
source, if the forward temperature of the DH is lower, the efficiency will be 
higher. LTDH allows the utilization of a broader range of heat pump 
technologies. 
==PAGE:24==
 
23 
 
- 
 Surplus heat utilisation: the low network supply temperature allows further 
exploitation of low-exergy excess heat sources, such as the one coming from 
industrial processes or from heat recovery from cooling facilities. Utilisation of 
this excess heat will be more efficient and cheaper the lower the required 
district heating supply temperature is. 
- 
Flue gas condensation: the low network return temperature in LTDH systems 
increases the possibility for flue gas condensation. This is particularly relevant 
for biomass/waste plants due to the high moisture content in the fuel. 
As it is possible to read in [15]: “LTDH is expected as one of the enablers in the transition 
to eco/low-carbon society. The transition to the new generation system is not merely 
lowering down the network temperature, but a paradigm shift to increase energy 
efficiency, reduce environmental impact, optimize resources and material utilization, 
augment the socio-economic benefit, and improve citizen’s quality of life.” 
The minimum supply temperature level into a DHN is determined by the space heating 
demand requirements and by the hygienic and comfort standards of the domestic hot 
water.  
It can be reduced only to the level that can guarantee: 
- 
The delivery of domestic hot water with required temperature of 45°C for 
kitchen use and 40°C for other tapping use [DS 439,2009]; 
- 
The design indoor comfort temperature in buildings that is usually set equal to 
20°C; 
In Denmark, to prevent the growth of Legionella bacteria the domestic hot water 
temperature is required to be no lower than 50°C if the system has water circulation, 
otherwise for system with storage tank it is suggested to keep the temperature no lower 
than 55°C. Legionella is a gram-negative bacterium that includes many species like L. 
pneumophila and L. longbeachae. It mainly grows and multiplies in the aquatic 
circumstances like cooling towers, humidifiers, evaporative condensers and DHW 
system. The pathogen bacteria can be accumulated in the biofilm located in the 
plumbing system. People with weakened immune systems can get severe lung 
inflammation by inhaling the aerosol from the contaminated water. The ideal 
temperature for Legionella multiplication ranges between 20°C to 45°C. Therefore, it is 
necessary to keep the DHW temperature above those levels. Legionella bacteria can be 
killed rapidly at high temperature. For temperature at 70°C, the bacteria are killed within 
1 min, 60°C within 5 min, and 50°C within 80 min [16]. 
The required temperature for LTDH networks can be supplied with most current heating 
supply technologies such as CHP plants, boilers, waste incineration plants and 
renewable energy sources such as solar heating and deep geothermal. However, in the 
urban area exist many other heat sources which have temperature below the minimum 
required by the DHN. These heat sources can be for example sewage water, the waste 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
 
heat recovered from server centres or electric transformers, shallow geothermal heat 
etc. and due to the temperature level below 50°C they belong to another category of 
district heating named ULTRA LOW TEMPERATURE DISTRICT HEATING. This type of 
district heating would have the great advantage of being able to exploit a wider range 
of available waste heat increasing the flexibility of the decentralized heat supply and 
achieving a more sustainable development of the heating supply system. However, to 
satisfy the DHW requirements the water temperature has to be increased utilising 
thermal boosters close to the end users such as electrical heaters or small scale heat 
pumps. The feasibility of some ULTDH applications with booster heat pumps 
installations for DHW preparation in single family houses has already been studied in 
[17] , [18] and [19] where positive results are registered.  
 
2.3.2 ELECTRICITY MARKET IN DENMARK 
The future LTDH will be greatly involved into the process for the integration of electrical 
power coming from renewable energy source. Therefore, a short description of the 
Danish electricity market is introduced in this paragraph. 
In Denmark the electricity price is settled into the Nordic Power Exchange, Nord Pool, 
that is composed by a series of international electricity trading markets, incorporating 
the Scandinavian and Baltic countries [20]. The Nordic countries deregulated their 
power markets in the early 1990s and brought their individual markets together into a 
common Nordic market. The term ‘deregulation’ means that the state is no longer 
running the power market, and free competition is introduced. Deregulation was 
undertaken to create a more efficient market, with exchange of power between 
countries and increased security of supply. Available power capacity can be used more 
efficiently in a large region compared to a small one, and integrated markets enhance 
productivity and improve efficiency.  
The power price is determined by the balance between supply and demand. Factors 
such as the weather or power plants not producing to their full capacity can impact 
power prices. The day-ahead market at Nord Pool, called ELSPOT, is the main arena for 
trading power. It consists in an auction based exchange for the trading of prompt 
physically delivered electricity.  
Here, contracts are made between seller and buyer for the delivery of power during the 
following day. 12:00 CET is the deadline for submitting bids for power which will be 
delivered the following day. Based on an advanced algorithm, the market price is set at 
the value where the curves for supply price and demand price meet for each hour of the 
following day. 
==PAGE:26==
 
25 
 
 
Figure 7 Electricity market price  
The primary role of a market price is to establish equilibrium between supply and 
demand. This task is especially important in the power markets because of the inability 
to store electricity and the high costs associated with any supply failure. 
A well-functioning and competitive power market produces electricity at the lowest 
possible price for every hour of the day. The balance price represents both: 
- 
 The cost of producing one kWh of power from the most expensive source needed 
to be employed in order to balance the system; 
- 
 The price that the consumer group is willing to pay for the final kWh required to 
satisfy demand; 
The price formation process is therefore economically effective for society. 
The majority of the volume handled by Nord Pool is traded on the day-ahead market. 
For the most part, the balance between supply and demand is secured here. However, 
incidents may take place between the closing of the day-ahead market at noon CET and 
the delivery of the next day. Therefore, the power system could be off-balance because: 
electricity load is greater or smaller than the one foreseen at the time of day-ahead 
market; renewable energy generation is different from the forecasted one; there are 
failures in the transmission or production systems. Hence, in addition to the day-ahead 
market there is the Intra-day market, named ELBAS, where electricity can be traded up 
to one hour before physical delivery based on bilateral contracts.  
The transmission system operators (TSO) have the responsibility to keep the 
transmission system in balance and for this reason other two markets are used to 
balance the supply and demand during the hour before the final deliver: The Regulation 
market and the Balancing market.  
The cost of electricity is variable and usually is between 80-300 DKK (price data from 
2015). However, analyzing the hourly prices settled in the day-ahead market, a strong 
correlation between the electricity price and the forecasted electricity production from

renewable energy sources can be noticed.  In Denmark for example, where the main 
renewable electricity source is represented by the wind turbines, when a lot of wind is 
forecasted the electricity prices are lower. An example is shown in Figure 8.

being able to handle the intermittence of the renewable sources.

3 METHODOLOGY 
After the description of the advantages of using a low temperature district heating 
network, it is easy to understand how important is the necessity to study an optimal 
network configuration to make it real and feasible. In this chapter the methodologies 
followed to create the model of the analysed district heating network and to carry out 
the optimization problem are described. 
3.1 MODEL OF THE DISTRICT HEATING NETWORK 
To simulate the state of the network in terms of mass flow rates and temperatures, a 
sample model has been developed in Matlab. 
The District Heating Network (DHN) can be topologically described using the basic 
concepts of the graph theory.  
The standard network topology allows representing the DHN in terms of nodes and 
branches where every branch connects a pair of nodes and corresponds to a supply and 
a return pipe.  
The nodes can be divided into [21]: 
- 
 Plant nodes which represent the heat plant facilities or the injection points of the 
heat in the network; 
- 
Customer nodes which represent the end users to whom the heat is delivered and 
they are typically a leaf node in the network; 
- 
Intermediate nodes that are neither plant nor customer nodes. They are junctions of 
two or more branches. Some of the intermediate nodes may be branching nodes, 
i.e. nodes where three or more branches meet.  
The pressure change nodes are not considered in the simulation. 
A connected graph which has no cycles is called tree and the DHN without internal loops 
can be represented by a direct tree.  In practice a DHN is mostly tree-like, but it may 
contain a small number of cycles. Cycles make the network more robust, because in case 
of pipe failure an alternative route to distribute heat may be available.  
However, the model developed in this study has a non-cyclic topology.  
An example of a DHN representation is shown in the figure below in which node 1 
represents the heating plant, nodes 2-4-5 can be customer nodes, or represent 
aggregated sub-networks/users, and node 3 is a branching node. The same scheme is 
valid for both supply and return pipes. The only difference between the two are the 
flows directions: for the supply pipes the water flows from the node plant 1 till the leaf 
nodes, for the return ones it flows in the opposite direction from the leaf nodes to the 
plant.

3.2 THE OPTIMIZATION PROBLEM IN GENERAL 
The optimization problem consists in determining the optimal values of the decision 
variables of the system in order to reach a given objective. The objective is stated in the 
objective function that will be minimized under the constraints of the model equations.  
There are different kind of optimization problems and they can be classified based for 
example on the linearity/non-linearity of the model equations, or based on the types of 
variables: integer or continuous.  
- 
The linear programming optimization (LP) is used when the model equations are 
linear and the variables are continuous; 
- 
The mixed integer linear programming optimization (MIPL) is used when there are 
linear model equations but some variables are integers (representing for example 
yes or no decisions); 
- 
The non-linear programming optimization (NLP) is used when the equation of the 
model and/or the objective function are non-linear; 
==PAGE:35==
 
34 
 
- 
The mixed integer non-linear programming optimization (MINLP) is used when there 
are non-linear equations and integer variables involved in the system; 
The optimization problem to be solved in this project is a non-linear programming type 
because it has many non-linear model equations.

4.4 NEW CONFIGURATION OF THE SYSTEM WITH ELECTRICAL BOOSTERS 
In this part of the project, the goal of the optimization problem is to minimize heat losses 
in the district heating grid. For this reason, a new configuration of the system modelled 
in paragraph 4.3 is analysed.  
The new configuration well fits into the context of the Danish green transition towards 
fully renewable energy systems. In fact, the idea is to introduce electrical boosters in 
some specific pipes in order to: 
- 
be able to decrease the forward temperature of the DHN to decrease the grid heat 
losses due to the temperature difference between the heat vector and the 
surrounding ground (4th generation of district heating);  
- 
facilitate the integration of fluctuating and intermittent renewable energy sources 
which produce electricity (such as wind systems) into the heating network. 
In this optimization the forward temperature of the DHN will be decreased till 40°C to 
evaluate the changes in heat losses. However, to meet the heating needs of the 
customers, additional energy has to be provided close to the end users. Hence, the 
electrical boosters introduced in the new configuration are essentials to guarantee a 
minimum water temperature that has to satisfy the domestic hot water requirements. 
In fact, in order to prevent the growth of the Legionella bacteria inside the domestic hot 
water two simple measures can be taken. If DHW is stored in a tank, the temperature 
has to be higher than 50 °C to kill the bacteria, otherwise the tap water cannot be stored 
after being heated. In addition, the Danish building standard [DS 439,2009] requires that 
temperature of hot tap water has two levels: 40°C and 45°C, respectively for bathroom 
or kitchen use.  
The space heating demand, in this case, does not represent an obstacle for the low 
temperature district heating. The annual heat load request of the considered single 
family houses in Marbjerg is very low (12.5 MWh/year/house) hence it is assumed that 
they are equipped with modern low energy heating systems such as floor or wall 
heating. These systems can easily work with water temperatures below 50°C (it is 
possible to lower the temperature even to around 30-40°C), while the older radiator 
systems required temperatures of 50-70°C [27].  
As shown in Figure 29, the new configuration has three electrical boosters located in the 
network branches B-D-E which carry the water directly towards the single family houses. 
==PAGE:49==
 
48 
 
  
Figure 29 Analysed DHN with booster heat pumps 
Those boosters are modelled as heat pumps and they have the function of increasing 
the water temperature if it does not satisfy the domestic hot water requirements or in 
general the customers’ heat request.  In the network scheme each branch represents, 
as usual, both the supply and the return pipe. 
The chosen heat pumps have a configuration as the one showed in Figure 30. The district 
heating hot water system is divided into two flows: one goes into the evaporator of the 
heat pump and the other into the condenser. The two streams are mixed in the return 
flow, combining the residue heat from the evaporator and the cold water coming from 
the heat exchangers with the secondary network. The DH flow which runs through the 
evaporator represents the heat source for the heat pump and it heats up the 
temperature of the rest of the flow that passes in the condenser.  
 
Figure 30 Booster heat pump configuration 
Heat pumps allows to transfer a certain amount of heat from a source with lower 
temperature to a sink that has a higher temperature. This is possible thanks to an 
==PAGE:50==
 
49 
 
external work made by the electrically driven compressor. The ratio between the heat 
delivered and the electricity consumed defines the coefficient of performance (COP), a 
performance indicator that characterizes the heat pump. This type of heat pump has a 
COP equal to approximately 5.5 [18], a value higher than the average COPs because the 
temperature of the heat source is very high (at least equal to 40°C).  
The location of the booster heat pumps in the branches B-D-E is not defined a priori in 
the new configuration, but will be an output of the optimization problem.  
 
4.5 DESCRIPTION OF THE OPTIMIZATION PROBLEM 
4.5.1 OBJECTIVE FUNCTION 
The objective function of the optimization problem is to minimize the total heat losses 
in the system.  Those are calculated as in paragraph 3.1.3 
𝑄𝑙𝑜𝑠𝑠= 𝑈𝐿(𝑇𝑤−𝑇𝑔)     [𝑊] 
The formula is applied for all the branches of the network, A-B-B1-C-D-D1-E-E1, 
considering separately supply and return pipes. The water temperature, 𝑇𝑤, is equal to 
the average between the water temperature at the beginning and at the end of the 
considered branch. The sum of the 18 heat losses contributions will be minimized.  
4.5.2 CONSTRAINTS 
Different types of constraints are involved in the optimization problem.  
Linear Equality Constraints 
First of all, among the linear equality constraints, the mass balance equation has to be 
satisfied at each branching node: the incoming flow must be equal to the outgoing flow. 
This condition has to be verified also when the the mass flow rate is divided before the 
heat pumps.

The mass flow rate in branch B is equal to the sum of the mass flow rate passing through 
the evaporator and the condenser in branch B1.  
The heat loss equations for the branches without heat pump, A-C, have a linear form 
since the length of the pipes are not variables, and can be written in the linear equality 
constraints.

The last type of linear equality constraints is represented by the total lengths of pipes 
containing a heat pump. The total pipes’ lengths are expressed in Table 2 and

𝐿𝐵+ 𝐿𝐵1 = 50 [𝑚]; 𝐿𝐷+ 𝐿𝐷1 = 53 [𝑚]; 𝐿𝐸+ 𝐿𝐸1 = 80 [𝑚] 
Linear Inequality Constraints 
In these kind of constraints all the linear inequalities are expressed. In particular, lot of 
them are represented by temperatures conditions such as: 
- 
The temperatures at the beginning of a branch are always greater than the ones at 
the end of the considered branch (with reference to the water flow direction) due 
to the heat transferred to the surrounding ground; 
- 
The flow temperatures outside the evaporators are lower than the ones going inside; 
- 
The flow temperature outside the condensers are greater than the ones going inside;  
- 
The supply temperature at a customer node is always greater than the return 
temperature at the same node;

4.5.3 BOUNDS 
The bounds are used to limit the range of the variables. All the variables have by default 
the lower bound (LB) equal to zero and the upper bound (UB) equal to infinity. However, 
to characterize the problem some exceptions are made.  
It has been assumed that the temperature at the aggregated customers’ supply nodes 
must be at least 55°C. In this way, considering a pinch temperature of 5°C in the heat 
exchange between primary and secondary network, the temperature requirements for 
domestic hot water preparation and to avoid legionella bacteria growth are satisfied.  
The return temperature at the customer nodes, instead, has to be higher than 25°C. In 
fact, considering that the indoor comfort temperature is between 18°C-20°C and that 
there is a pinch temperature of 5°C in the heat exchanger between primary and 
secondary network, the heat vector must have a temperature greater than  𝑇𝑖𝑛𝑑𝑜𝑜𝑟+
 ∆𝑇𝐻𝐸𝑋 to be able to transfer its heat. 
The supply temperature at the injection point can vary in a range between 40°C and 
80°C. 
The lower bound of the return temperature at the injection point is set equal to 20°C. 
This constraint has been defined because otherwise during the optimization the system 
was trying to lower the return temperature as much as possible toward zero to minimize 
the heat losses, and these result was not reliable. 
The water temperature outside the heat pump evaporator has to be greater than 25°C 
to simulate a reliable operation of the heat pump.  
The mass flow rates are limited by the maximum velocity of the water in the pipes. The 
LOGSTOR A/S company recommend to not exceed the velocity of 1 m/s into the smaller 
branch distribution pipes. Hence, the maximum volume flow [m3/s] has been calculated 
as the product between the maximum velocity and the section area of the considered 
pipe; then the maximum mass flow rate [kg/s] has been evaluated multiplying the 
volume flow rate with the water density. In the same way a minimum water flow rate 
has been calculated considering a minimum water velocity of 0.1 m/s. The mass flow

5.2 OPTIMIZATION RESULTS 
The optimization is performed for different days of the year. Due to computational time

To minimize heat losses, the system locates the heat pumps as close as possible to the

to zero. That is a reasonable result because in this way the high temperature water 
coming out from the booster heat pumps goes directly to the heat exchanger with the 
secondary side of the network without losing other heat, hence the heat losses in 
branches B1, D1 and E1 tend to zero.  
 
 
Figure 34 Heat pump location in the optimized network 
The optimal system configuration has always the forward temperature at the injection 
point equal to its minimum possible value, i.e. 40°C. The water temperature is then 
boosted to 55°C by the heat pump to satisfy the DHW temperature requirements and 
since the heat pump is situated at the end of the branches, this water temperature is 
the same of the one entering the heat exchanger.  
The system tends to have the mass flow rate as low as possible, and its value is always 
close to the lower bound, 0.6 kg/s at the injection point. This minimum value is enough 
to satisfy the heat load request for almost all the hours of the year, except for the very 
cold days when the mass flow is slightly higher and reaches values such as 0.85 kg/s.

The optimized system configuration allows to considerably decrease the heat losses 
thanks to the lower forward temperature. Some typical days, which reflect the seasonal 
operating conditions of the network, are analysed.  
During winter, the forward temperature registers its highest values and referring to 
Figure 33, the first 2000 hours of the year have an average temperature of 68°C with 
peaks that can reach 75°C at the injection node 1. Due to the high temperature values 
the heat losses in those hours are greater than the average, and have peaks of 5.8 kW. 
With the optimized system that considers the forward temperature at the injection node 
always equal to 40°C, the heat losses are considerably lower. Considering one week at 
the beginning of February (day of the year 34-35-36-37-38-39-40), in which the average 
hourly heat loss with the old system configuration is 5 kW, the average hourly heat loss 
with new system configuration becomes equal to 2,36 kW. Hence, a reduction of 53% of 
the heat losses is achieved with this configuration. 
During summer, considering the first week of July, the average hourly heat loss is equal 
to 4,35 kW, instead the average heat loss calculated with the optimized system is equal 
to 2,9 kW that means a reduction of 32,5% of the heat losses.  
As it is possible to notice, with the optimized system, the heat losses are slightly higher 
during summer compared to the winter values. This happens because the return 
temperature during the summer days, in which the heat load request is very low, has a 
value of around 35°C at the injection node and it is higher compared to the winter case 
where the return temperature at the same node is 25°C. The water is not able to transfer 
all its heat contents, hence, it goes back to the injection point with a higher temperature 
and loses more heat due to the higher temperature difference with the ground.  
During mid-seasons such as spring and autumn, the behaviour of the optimized system 
is in between the ones during summer and winter. As soon as the heat demand 
increases, the heat losses decrease because a higher amount of heat is extracted from 
the same mass flow rate and therefore the water temperature in the return pipe is 
lower. For example, considering a week at the end of September, the hourly heat losses 
with the new system configuration are equal to 2.5 kW corresponding to a decrease in 
heat loss of 43%.  Almost the same result is found analysing the first week of April where 
a reduction of 44% in heat loss is registered.

7 DISCUSSION 
In this project the possibility to lower the forward temperature of the district heating 
relying on booster heat pumps has been analysed. From the obtained results some 
considerations can be made.  
First of all, from the simulation of the considered part of Marbjerg network, the strategy 
by which Roskilde Fors A/S operates the plant is clear: to follow the variation of the heat 
load request, the forward temperature is kept as constant as possible and the mass flow 
rate varies according to the heat load. During summer, when the heat load request is 
very low, according to this operational mode, the mass flow rate at the injection point 1 
is decreased and can reach values below 0.2 kg/s. 
 
When the mass flow is so low, according to the temperature drop equation, the water 
temperature will decrease much faster. In  Figure 39 temperature difference between 
the first and the last node of the analysed network, hence the difference between the 
temperature at the injection node 1 and the furthest one that is node 6, is reported. 
Since the ground temperature is considered to be the same throughout the year 
independently of the outside temperature and the specific heat of the water is 
considered constant as well, the only variable parameter in the temperature drop 
equation remains the mass flow rate. As it is possible to see in Figure 39,  during summer 
the temperature difference between the first and last node can arrive at 4°C, and this is 
a big decrease compared with what happens in winter time when the mass flow is 
higher.  This means high relative heat losses compared to those obtainable operating 
the plant with a lower forward temperature and a higher mass flow rate. These 
increased heat losses are also reflected into the relative heat losses graph in Figure 32. 
==PAGE:65==
 
64 
 
 
Figure 39 Temperature gradient between the first network node and the furthest one 
In the optimization part carried out in this project, the goal was to minimize the network 
heat losses of the considered grid, therefore to find an optimal network configuration 
from a technical point of view. 
The stated problem has many constraints to satisfy and in particular lots of them are 
non-linear. This particular circumstance makes the optimization problem heavier. The 
computational time increases in an exponential way and for example the time need to 
perform the optimization of a single hour of the year was around 30 seconds while to 
perform the optimization on an entire day, hence 24 hours, the average time was 6000 
seconds. Therefore, the choice of considering only specific seasonal representative days 
was made due to long computational time.  
The computational time could be a limit for future simulations on larger modelled 
systems with a higher number of non-linear constraints. A solution could be to simplify 
the problem formulation avoiding to insert unnecessary non-linear constraints and 
tightening the range of variation of the different variables involved in the problem. 
  
The optimization goal stated in this project is achieved by lowering as much as possible 
the temperature of the water flowing into the pipes: both the supply and return ones. 
Since there were any constraints regarding electricity or heat prices, the optimized 
configuration always works with the minimum possible supply temperature, equal to 
40°C, and the minimum water flow rate needed to satisfy the required heat load at the 
specific hour. In this way the reduction of heat losses in the system is significant.  
From the results it comes out that the heat losses during summer hours are slightly 
greater than those ones in winter, respectively an average value of 2.9 kW and 2.36 kW, 
and this happens because the amount of heat transported by the minimum amount of 
mass flow rate admissible in the network pipes is not totally transferred to the end users. 
The surplus heat is carried by the return water that has a higher temperature compared 
to the winter case (the average return temperature in summer is 35°C whereas in winter 
is 25°C) and this is translated in higher heat losses into the return pipes.  
==PAGE:66==
 
65 
 
A solution to overcome this problem would be certainly represented by the installation 
of storage tanks. The simulations carried out in this project do not take into account the 
storage, however, it would allow a better performance of the network and to minimize 
heat losses. As stated in the paragraph 2.2.3, heat storage would improve the economy 
of the district heating system by decoupling the consumption request from the 
production, and it would allow a more sustainable heat supply.  
In the specific examined network, thanks to the storage it would be possible to store the 
surplus heat generated by the heat pumps when they boost the water temperature to 
at least 55°C.  In this way the water stream that flows towards the heat exchanger 
between the primary and the secondary side of the network would be equal only to the 
amount that is needed to cover the heat load request and, therefore, the water 
temperature of the return flow is decreased to the lowest admissible level, 25°C.  
The configuration of the system with the heat storage tank is represented in Figure 40. 
 
Figure 40 Booster heat pump configuration with storage tank 
Operating the network with the storage tanks would lead to a decrease of heat losses 
of 50%, hence the simulated system would have yearly heat losses of 7%. 
As stated before, the optimization does not take into account the costs of energy, 
therefore the optimized solution tends to take to supply temperature at the lowest level 
possible and the rest of heat needed to satisfy the heat load request is supplied by the 
heat pump. However, this technical optimization could result unfeasible from an 
economical point of view.  For future developments of this study, to take into account 
also the heat production prices a multi-objective optimization can be performed. In this 
type of optimization more than one objective function can be stated in the problem, for 
example both minimization of heat losses and minimization of costs, and the so called 
Pareto curves will show the area of feasible solutions to satisfy the objective goals. 
The economical evaluation of the optimized configuration has been carried out to see if 
the project is feasible also from an economical point of view other than the technical 
point of view. The average price of electricity from 2015 has been considered. As stated 
in chapter 6, the prices utilized for the simulation do not take into account the general 
taxes applied by the Danish government but only the tariffs which are used to cover the 
grid and system transmission expenses. Therefore, the prices reflect the technical cost 
needed to produce and supply the energy including the cost to maintain the network 
==PAGE:67==
 
66 
 
grids.  The economical evaluation using these technical prices shows that the installation 
of three heat pumps in the selected branches is feasible and can also lead to money 
savings. It has been carried out considering that the heating from the centralised plant 
is produced mainly from a waste incineration heating plant which has not the highest 
fuel price. In some Danish municipalities the price for one MWh heat can reach values 
above 1000 DKK as reported by the Danish Energy Regulation Authority (DERA) in the 
district heating prices report of March 2015 [30]. The optimized configuration could then 
be even more convenient in terms of costs if it is applied to more expensive district 
heating networks. 
Moreover, it should be considered that the LTDH configuration, and even more the 
ULTDH configuration as the one that came out from the network optimization, allows to 
integrate new low temperature heat source that could be for example: industrial waste 
heat, electronic centres, solar panels, shallow geothermal heat sources etc.  
The heat coming from the low temperature sources will have a lower price compared to 
the actual prices of fuels needed to produce the necessary DH heat, hence even more 
savings could be obtained using those heat sources.  
In further developments of the study the economic evaluation should be carried out 
considering the price variation of the electricity on the day-ahead market. The operation 
of the district heating, and the economical savings that can be achieved are tightly 
dependent on the electricity price. The network should be operated in such a way to use 
the boosters heat pumps when the electricity price is low and decrease their use when 
the electricity price is higher.  
Also in this case the storage tank plays a fundamental role. It allows to use electricity in 
a more convenient way: in fact, it should be refilled as soon as the electricity prices are 
lower and use the stored heat whenever the electricity prices get higher. Sometimes it 
also happens that the electricity prices are negative when for example the amount of 
energy from renewable sources is more than the forecasted one. The surplus energy 
could be efficiently stored into the heating network thanks to the storage tanks and this 
would allow a higher penetration of fluctuating renewable energies into the heating 
system decreasing the pressure on biomass and waste incineration plants.  
Economic savings would be much higher than the ones calculated in chapter 6 and the 
payback time for the investment on heat pumps would be lower. 
In general, when the electricity prices are low, the CHP plants, which are the main 
heating source for the Danish district heating networks, tend to minimize their 
production since lot of power if forecasted from other sources. Therefore, they will 
consequentially reduce also their heating production. This condition fits well with the 
heat pump operation schedule that will take advantage of the low cost electricity to 
provide more heat to the district heating grid and to fill up the storages.  
When the electricity prices are high, the CHP plants will increase their production. In this 
case if the hot water storages are full, the heating could be provided firstly by those 
facilities in such a way to maintain the district heating supply temperature at a low level. 
In this situation of electricity need, the low supply temperature of the DH allows to 
==PAGE:68==
 
67 
 
produce more electricity from the CHP power plants since the fluid into the CHP turbines 
can be expanded till a lower temperature level.  
In this way a general optimization of the energy supply systems can be achieved as well 
as a more sustainable operation of heating and power plants.  
Further studies on this project can be made also considering different network 
configurations. At the moment the analysis has been carried out considering the 
installation of small scale heat pumps very close to the end user customers. However, 
looking at the bigger network, one idea to reduce the investment costs on multiple heat 
pumps could be to install less heat pumps with a higher heat capacity at the beginning 
of the branches that supply heat to a high density populated area. The bigger heat 
pumps could in this way have profit from the economy of scale. An example of this 
possible solution is shown in Figure 41.

Another implementation could be to divide the supply water flow into two different 
flows, one for the DHW preparation and the other for the space heating system. In this 
way the electrical boosters can be used to increase only the water flow for DHW 
preparation since it needs to satisfy some specific requirements and less electrical 
power would be utilized. In fact, considering new space heating systems such as wall or 
floor heating, the supply temperature can be also around 30-40°C, hence the boosting 
for that flow is not necessary needed except during specific climatic conditions. 
Among all the possible further studies that could be carried out from this initial project, 
an important one is the analysis of the thermal stress of the pipes. In fact, with the

water temperature inside the pipes. This dynamical load could lead to the damage of 
the network due to thermal stress, hence this aspect has to be taken into account.

8 CONCLUSION 
In this project a case study of district heating system has been presented and analysed.  
The goal was to evaluate the possibilities to lower the forward temperature of the water 
circulating into the network in order to reduce the heat losses of the system due to the 
temperature difference existing between the heat vector and the ground.  The new 4th 
generation DHN tends to lower the supply temperature of the heat vector to 50-40°C 
and it is called Low Temperature District Heating. This kind of heating system has many 
advantages and among all of them it allows to utilize the heat coming from low exergy 
heating sources as well as to sensibly decrease the grid heat losses. 
Electrical energy driven heat sources are also integrated into the future LTDH grid 
because they will have the strategical role of connecting the heating system with the 
electrical energy coming from the intermittent and fluctuating renewable energy 
sources such as wind and solar power.  
To simulate the operating condition of the analysed Roskilde DHN, a Matlab model has 
been developed. It allows to evaluate the state of the system in terms of mass flow rates 
and temperatures in specific points of the grid.  From these data it is possible to calculate 
the heat losses of the network as well.  The simulation results show that the network is 
operated in a specific way: the variation of the heat load request from the end 
customers is satisfied varying the water mass flow rates and keeping the forward 
temperature almost constant at high level (average of 73°C). The model is considered 
validated since the total annual heat loss calculated is equal to 13.8%, a value similar to 
the one declared by Roskilde DH company of 13%. 
After the model simulation, a new configuration of district heating has been evaluated. 
It includes the installation of three electrical booster heat pumps. The new system’s 
operation has been determined based on a non-linear optimization problem in which 
the objective function was to minimize the system heat losses. This goal has been 
achieved by lowering the forward temperature to 40°C and relying on the installed heat 
pumps to boost the water temperature to the admissible value needed for the domestic 
hot water preparation, i.e. 55°C in the network’s primary side. The position of the heat 
pumps was a variable of the problem, and from the optimized system results they are 
allocated as close as possible to the end customers. In this way the water with higher 
temperature has to cover the lowest possible path in the network’s pipes before going 
into the heat exchanger. The optimized configuration allows to decrease the network 
heat losses of 32÷53% depending on the season (higher reductions are obtained during 
colder seasons). 
At the end, an economical evaluation of the new optimized system configuration has 
been carried out to evaluate the feasibility of the project. Considering the average 
technical prices to produce a kWh of heat or electricity, i.e. without considering state 
duties, the heat pumps’ installation result to be economically feasible and has a NPV at 
the end of the heat pumps’ lifetime equal to 56750 DKK. The investment payback time is 
equal to 14 years considering an interest rate of 4%.  
==PAGE:71==
 
70 
 
In this work the hourly variation of the electricity price according to the spot day-ahead 
market has not been taken into account, but only the average yearly value. Therefore, 
further study developments could include the analysis of the DH network based on the 
variation of the hourly electricity prices in such a way to optimized the electrical 
boosters heat pumps also according to the availability of electricity coming from 
renewable energy sources. 
Moreover, the heat storage has to be included in the network configuration because it 
would allow to decouple the heat consumption from the production and implement 
more sustainable heat sources. 
It has to be noticed that the economical evaluation has been carried out considering 
only the technical price of the energy. This decision has been made because it is the 
most efficient way to calculate the real costs to produce 1 kWh of the heat delivered to 
the end customer. However, taxes can completely change the costs of the system based 
on political decisions. The operational costs of the optimized systems could then be 
significantly higher compared to the ones obtained in this economical evaluation.  
 
In my personal opinion, if in the future years these kind of district heating networks will 
allow to obtain a 100% renewable energy systems and a sensible reduction of 
greenhouse gas emissions, it will be completely worth to pay higher energy bills anyway.
