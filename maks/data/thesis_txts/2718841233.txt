Abstract
In order to improve the performance of a trampette jump it is essential to analyze the vari­
ous affecting factors. Presenting different ideas on the topic to gymnastics coaches might
help to improve the overall performance of their athletes. This study aims to determine
whether there is a primary technical­related factor or factors that result in the best tram­
pette jump performance in TeamGym gymnastics. Three different measurement methods
are conducted and compared to obtain optimal results.
Teamgym is a team competition form of gymnastics where eight to ten gymnasts perform
on three different apparatuses: trampette, tumbling, and floor. The performance on each
apparatus, for each gymnast, is judged during a competition. The combined results from
the three apparatuses are used to determine the team’s final score. Thus, it is crucial
to perform well on every apparatus. The trampette exercise includes a mini­trampoline
where the gymnasts perform elements with and without a vaulting table. These elements
include either one or more somersaults (saltos) and/or twists, with different body positions.
Full­in half­out is a jump with a double forward salto and 1½ twist (540◦), performed with a
tucked or straight body shape. Physics can be used to describe the requirements needed
to maximize factors such as acceleration in the trampette, launch velocity, and with that
the height of the jump.
This study focuses on the analysis of a Full­in half­out jump, with a straight body position,
on a trampette. To analyze the physics of the movement, XSens DOT sensors were used
as well as Brower TC­i Gate Timing System. The sensors were then tracked and marked
from a video in the Tracker video analysis and modeling program, and the obtained data
was then compared to sensor and timing system data.
Six athletes participated in the analysis by performing, on average, four to six Full­in half­
out jumps each. Three timing system gates were placed at distinct distances from the
trampette to calculate the running speed. A total of five sensors were placed on the
assumed centers of mass of the athlete’s body to capture the movement. One sensor on
the trunk, one on each shank, and one on each forearm. Videos were recorded of each
jump for the motion tracking in Tracker. A total of sixteen variables were analyzed, i.e.
acceleration in the trampette, launch velocity when the athlete is leaving the trampette,
height of the jump, running speed, length and height of the jump into the trampette, body
angles, grading, and genetic factors.
The result findings presented in this thesis indicated that there is not a specific technical­
related factor or factors that result in the best performance of a trampette jump. The
compared measurement methods showed some similarities but were not identical. The
thesis is completed with future suggestions for improvements for a better understanding
of the results as well as suggestions for future work.
Keywords:
Trampette, Full­in half­out, performance, the center of mass, sensor, Tracker, accelera­
tion, velocity.

1
Introduction
TeamGym is a popular form of gymnastics originating from Scandinavia. It is a team
competition, where eight to ten members perform on three apparatuses: trampette (mini­
trampoline), tumbling, and floor. Each team’s final score is calculated using the results
from each of the three apparatuses. It is therefore important to perform well on all appa­
ratuses.
The performance of a trampette jump is evaluated in accordance with the trampette exe­
cution section from TeamGym Code of Point (CoP) 2022. The execution requirements are
both in the aerial phase and the landing, such as height and an extended body position
prior to landing [9]. The performance will be elaborated further in section 2.4.3 and 3.2.
There are specific factors in the technique for a trampette jump, prior to the take­off from
the trampette, that enhance the performance of the jump. Those factors include the run­
ning speed and technique, the length and height of the jump into the trampette, and the
body position when entering the trampette and leaving the trampette (take­off). It is al­
ways in the coaches’ best interests to optimize these techniques to improve performance
and, as a result, maximize the final score. Since there are many factors to consider when
coaching, the coach needs to know what to prioritize. The focus area can be diverse
for different coaches. However, many of them base their coaching on experience and
tradition­driven ways rather than physics. It is well­recognized that sports physics can
help improve an athlete’s performance.
Very few studies are available on the physics of TeamGym gymnastics, which may explain
why coaches rely more on previous experience and tradition. Reasons that may contribute
to the lack of studies may be the complexity of gymnastics in general and that TeamGym
is a relatively new gymnastics discipline [20].
1.1
Thesis description
This master thesis studies the trampette performance of Icelandic women competing in
TeamGym gymnastics.
The Icelandic national women’s team in TeamGym gymnastics is known for its perfor­
mance in international competitions after multiple victories. The team performs best on
the floor and the tumbling, but usually not on the trampette. To improve the teams’ overall
performance on the trampette, it is necessary to look into the various phases of a jump
and the factors that affect the performance.
Only the athletes fulfilling specific requirements set by coaches have a chance to secure
a spot on the national team. Therefore, the selection of the performed jump was in con­
sultation with the head coach of the Icelandic national women’s team. In accordance with
the head coach, it was estimated that it would be optimal to choose the Full­in half­out
jump, on the trampette, in the study since that is one of the requirements for the athletes.
Many factors affect the performance of the jump. It is interesting to look at these factors
and see if it is possible to conclude if one of them is better than the other ones in giving a
good performance of the jump. In this study, measurements were conducted on Icelandic
female gymnasts in TeamGym to assess these factors. These measurements included
sensor and video tracking of different masses of the body, focusing on the estimated

2.1
Background
2.1.1
Gymnastics
Gymnastics is a popular sport with a long history rooted in the demonstration of athleticism
and entertainment. The sport originates from ancient Greece and has since that time,
developed into the gymnastics that we know today [23]. It is a multifaceted sport that
necessitates a high level of physical fitness and techniques. Physical skills including
strength, power, speed, agility, and flexibility all contribute to a competitive gymnast’s
success [38]. One of the main skills required in gymnastics is the gymnasts’ ability to
rotate about one or more body axis [18].
The International Gymnastics Federation (FIG) governs seven worldwide disciplines of
gymnastics: Women’s and Men’s Artistic Gymnastics, Rhythmic Gymnastics, Aerobic
Gymnastics, Acrobatic Gymnastics, Parkour, Gymnastics for All, and Trampoline Gym­
nastics ­ including Double Mini­trampoline and Tumbling. Each discipline has its own set
of rules about conduct and scoring, as well as a unique focus on physical skills [23]. There
are other gymnastic disciplines currently not recognized by FIG such as Aesthetic Group
Gymnastics and TeamGym.
Gymnastics has been a part of The Olympic Games since the beginning in 1896 and is
today one of the biggest and most popular sports events of the Olympics [33]. Among the
different gymnastics disciplines, there are only three competing at the Olympics: Women’s
and Men’s Artistic Gymnastics, Rhythmic Gymnastics, and Trampoline Gymnastics [8].
2.1.2
TeamGym
TeamGym is a group form of gymnastics recognized by the European Union of Gymnas­
tics (UEG), which has its roots in Scandinavia. It is a perfect combination of Rhythmic
Gymnastics and Trampoline Gymnastics. In 1996, the first European Championship in
TeamGym was held in Finland and since then the popularity has risen within Europe [29]
[24].
In competition, the teams consist of eight to ten gymnasts and are either men’s, women’s,
or mixed categories (half male and half female). Each team performs on three appara­
tuses: trampette, tumbling, and floor [9]. In short, the floor exercise is a choreographed
routine to music involving all gymnasts and includes balance, jumps, acrobatic elements,
a group element, and a flexibility element. The tumbling exercise includes a tumbling track
where the gymnasts perform three rounds of a series consisting of a minimum of three
acrobatic elements, forwards and backward. The trampette exercise includes an inclined
mini­trampoline where the gymnasts perform three rounds of different elements with and
without a vaulting table. In addition to being physically fit, each of the three disciplines
requires high levels of technical skills in acrobatic and gymnastic movements [19].

2.2
Evaluation of TeamGym
In TeamGym, the evaluation in competition is in accordance with rules defined in the Team­
Gym CoP, which is reviewed and updated every four years [19]. A final score for each
team includes three separate scores from the three apparatuses. The separate scores
are the sum of three different scores, the Composition (C) score, the Difficulty (D) score,
and the Execution (E) score. The C Score is based on the composition requirements for
each apparatus and has a maximum of 2.00. The D score is the sum of all difficulty values
of approved elements for each apparatus. This score is open meaning that the team that
performs the most difficult elements gets the highest D score. The E score is based on
the execution faults for the performance, where the deductions for a specific apparatus
are subtracted from 10.00 [42]. For each apparatus, there are different requirements for
the C, D, and E scores explained in the CoP [9].

2.3.1
Technique
As mentioned before, there are specific factors in techniques for a trampette jump, prior to
the take­off from the trampette, that can enhance the performance of the jump. Although,
there are certain techniques that are required for the time in the air and the landing, that
enhance the performance as well. Generally, the trampette can be split up into six phases:
running, jumping into the trampette, in the trampette, take­off, in the air, and the landing.
The techniques will be explained from a coach’s point of view, with regard to each phase
of the trampette.
Running is the first phase and is considered a very important factor that many coaches
put too little focus on. Both speed and acceleration are important. The gymnast should
start slow and accelerate to reach a fast speed after around 10­15 meters. The last three
steps are the most important ones, they should be powerful and there the athlete should
be in a more upright position, with the eyes focused on the trampette. Additionally, the
running technique is important. The positioning of the arms should be with an elbow angle
of approximately 90◦, shoulders down, high knee lift, and strike the ground with a claw­like
motion. Overall, the running should be light, upright, and easy [5].
The jump into the trampette is more likely to be better if the running phase went well.
Generally, the jump­in should be long and low. Though, a little height is essential to reach
up into the trampette. The gymnast should lift the knees up and then kick down into the
trampette to generate more force. When jumping in, it is beneficial to move the hands in
a specific circular motion to get them up in the trampette. The body position entering the
trampette is also a key factor. Many coaches want the gymnast to enter the trampette
with a piked body position, where the hands point forward and the hips are more closed
or more angled. This body position gives the gymnasts the opportunity to go higher if they
can reach an open position before take­off. Typically, women and younger boys cannot
reach a sufficiently open position when entering the trampette in a piked position. When
that occurs, the gymnast has greater difficulties gaining enough rotation. Therefore, it can
work better to teach them to enter the trampette with a more open position, where the hips
are more open and the hands are more upraised.
The in­trampette and take­off phases are explained together. The time of these phases
is around 0.1­0.15 seconds. Up into the jump, there must be a powerful hip and hand
movement. The gymnast should extend the position from the trampette to an almost
straight position with hands raised above the ears. It is important to make sure that the
hands do not go down too early. They should remain raised until the gymnast exits the
trampette. The head should be in an almost straight position, preferably pointing a little

==PAGE:18==
bit up in the direction of the hands. This way the gymnast has the opportunity to go a little
higher in the jump. It is important that the gymnast sees the landing zone when in the
trampette and where he is going to land. Every jump should begin with a straight position,
and once the take­off has occurred, the movement is adjusted to the type of jump that is
to be executed. The initiation of a rotation is different for different types of body positions.
For a tucked body position the gymnast should bend the knees in the direction of the core,
in a piked position the bum is lifted up, and in a straight position the heels are used to kick
in the rotation.
When the gymnasts are in the air they should be able to see the landing zone and have
a feeling about what is happening. According to the type of jump being performed, the
body position must be appropriate. When the jump includes twists the timing of the twisting
is important. As mentioned before, the gymnast has to reach a straight position before
beginning the twisting. In the end, the gymnast must prepare for the landing by opening
the hips and making sure that the hands are not raised too high.
In the landing, it is important to lower the center of mass. This is done by lowering the
hands and then it will be more effortless to land. The hips, knees, and ankles should be
bent to support the weight of the landing and to be able to push the landing mat down.
The feet should be shoulder­width apart and the weight should be transferred a little over
to the cuboid to activate the gluteal muscles and make the landing easier.
Overall the techniques include the running speed and technique, the length and height
into the jump, the body position when entering the trampette and leaving the trampette
(take­off), the initiation of the rotation, the body position in the air, the preparation before
landing, and the landing support [15].

Trampette Evaluation
As mentioned above, the trampette final score includes C, D, and E scores. Each score
includes specific requirements which will be explained below.
2.4.1
Composition requirements
The C score has a maximum of 2.00 and is based on the composition requirements. For
each requirement missing there is a specific deduction.
The team is required to perform at least one round with a vaulting table and one without
a vaulting table. In the first round, all gymnasts need to perform the same element and in
the second and third rounds, the gymnasts need to perform the same element or increase
difficulty. The teams must perform one round with double or triple salto and one round with
at least 540◦. It is not allowed to perform blind landings in triple saltos or repeat elements
from another round [9].
2.4.2
Difficulty requirements
The D score is based on the difficulty of the elements performed. It is an open score,
meaning there are no limits to the highest possible score. Despite that, the selected
element should always fit the technical skill of the gymnasts [9].

2.6
Physics of a trampette jump
When analyzing a trampette jump, there are many distinct physics factors to consider. The
key factors include force, speed, acceleration, and body angle. As previously mentioned,
not many studies have been conducted on TeamGym specifically. Previous studies have
focused on a vertical jump on either a trampette or a large trampoline, which can give
some insight into the physics of the motion studied in this study. Even though, there are
various other affecting factors to be considered when analyzing a Full­in half­out jump.
A trampette jump can be split into five phases requiring different physical strengths and
abilities. These phases are running, jumping into the trampette, in the trampette, in the
air, and landing. The physics of each phase is discussed in detail in sections 2.6.1 ­ 2.6.5
but only certain factors are analyzed in this study.
2.6.1
Running
According to Wiemann et al. [43], the term ”sprinting” is frequently used to refer to running
at your maximum capacity for a brief period of time. There are different apparatuses that
require running or sprinting as that is one of the physical demands of gymnastics. In
TeamGym gymnastics, it is essential for the gymnast to generate a sprint when running
towards the trampette. Since the running distance towards the trampette is generally
rather short, or around 25m [19], the gymnast must accelerate fast to quickly achieve
maximum velocity.
Sprinting is a multidimensional movement technique that has three distinct phases: the
initial starting phase, the acceleration phase, and the peak running speed phase. During
the start and acceleration phases, the athlete uses hip and knee extensors to accelerate
through a concentric force. Therefore, the capacity to generate a significant concentric
force or power and to generate high velocity during acceleration is most important at
the start of the sprint. The hip extensors and ankle extensors are primarily responsible
for propelling the body forward during the maximal running speed phase. To be able to
continue accelerating throughout the sprint distance, the athlete must reach a high speed
and a significant concentric force during the starting phase [26].
Kale et al.[21] state that a successful sprint running performance requires good starting
ability and the highest maximum running speed.
Three forces are acting on the athlete during running. Those are the horizontal Fx and
vertical Fy forces due to the contact between the athlete’s foot and the ground, and the
third is the gravity force Fg that works to pull down on the athlete through the athlete’s
center of mass. In order for the athlete to lift off the ground while running, Fy has to be
larger than Fg. The force that drives the athlete forwards is Fx which is therefore directly
related to the running speed.
The running speed is calculated with equation 2.1 and the acceleration can be calculated
by differentiating the velocity with respect to time, as seen in equation 2.2. Both equations
are seen below.

The running towards the trampette can be analyzed in a similar way as when an athlete

when jumping towards the trampette.
2.6.2
Jumping into the trampette
There have been some studies conducted to examine a run towards a target, but none
were identified in relation to a jump into a trampette. As previously mentioned, looking
up the movement of an athlete running before a long jump can give some insight into the
physics of the jump into the trampette. Here, the air resistance is negligible.
There are two main phases of the jump: the take­off phase and the flight phase. The
take­off phase is when the athlete jumps from the ground, and the flight phase is when
the athlete is in the air until landing on the apparatus. Since the trampette is above ground
level, the gymnasts must jump vertically and horizontally to land on the trampette. In a
long jump, the athletes must push the legs in front of the hips when landing to optimize
the landing distance [39]. Similarly to this, gymnasts need to be able to get the feet in
front of the hips before touching the trampette when jumping into it.
When maximizing the jumping distance in a long jump, the horizontal velocity and vertical
velocity are both of great importance as well as the angle of the body. The horizontal
velocity of the take­off is directly translated from the horizontal velocity (speed) of the run­
up, while the vertical velocity is a combination of the run speed and the height gained from
the push off the ground [36] [17]. The body angle is dependent on the leg stiffness which
gives a certain ankle angle α when jumping from the ground. Figure 2.5 gives a visual
representation of how the angle is established.

2.6.4
In air
When gymnasts move in the air, there are factors to be considered. Angular momentum
is a factor that is conserved in air. This factor can be explained in terms of two other
rotational factors: angular velocity and moment of inertia. The angular velocity is a vector
that describes the body’s rotational speed and direction, and the moment of inertia is a
measure of how resistant a body is to changes in angular velocity about the rotational axis.
The angular momentum simply is the product of the angular velocity and the moment of
inertia [20] [11],
L = Iω,
(2.17)
where L is the angular momentum, I is the moment of inertia, and ω is the angular velocity.
Newton’s second law can be used to describe the movement of a gymnast in the air. Since
air resistance is negligible for all gymnastic movements in the air, the gravitational force
causes a constant acceleration of the center of mass. It is therefore possible to predict the
body’s center of mass’s subsequent motion if its initial position and velocity are known.
While the gymnast is in the air, there is no net momentum or torque because weight
acts through the center of mass. Therefore, the angular momentum remains constant
throughout the flight.
If the body changes shape in the air, the gymnast’s angular velocity is determined by
the conservation of angular momentum. Both the magnitude and direction of the angular
velocity respond to changes in the body’s moment of inertia. The moment of inertia in­
creases with a more extended body position of the gymnast [32]. Meaning that a tucked
body position would have a lower moment of inertia than a straight body position. The
angular velocity also responds to certain limb movements that have angular momentum.
As a result, the angular velocity can change within the limits required by the conservation
of angular momentum, while the linear velocity is determined.
Since the gymnast can rotate about different axes, it is necessary to understand the
physics of each rotation. The jumps with a twisting salto are mathematically more complex
than a salto without twists [6]. In order to perform a jump on a trampette, with both twists
and turns, the gymnast needs to generate angular momentum before dismounting from
the trampette. The initial rotation of the jump is the horizontal rotation of the salto, where

2.6.5
Landing
Landing is the final phase of the jump on the trampette. It is of great importance that the
gymnast has a good and stable landing in order to prevent injuries and get a high score
[25].
Many gymnasts have the extraordinary ability to land after a free fall from large heights.
It requires that the gymnast has the ability to control the forces and their distribution as
well as having a great physical form. Factors such as increased falling height, resulting
in increased impact velocity, and skill complexity can result in an increased magnitude of
impact forces during landing [25].
The ground reaction forces will exert the gymnast from the landing mat at the same angle
as the athlete has when landing on the mat. This is because the landing creates a reaction
force that can cause the gymnast to be pushed forward or backward. Therefore, gymnasts
try to have a straight body shape before landing so that the bounce from the mat will be
upwards [7]. Different force magnitudes are developed depending on the height and the
type of landing. A higher vertical ground reaction force is the result of a greater jump
height. The impact of vertical ground reaction force on the gymnast’s linear and angular
momentum is an example of an external force that the gymnasts must counteract using
muscle power. The duration of the landing is a factor that also impacts linear and angular
momentum [25].
The landing mat compresses as the gymnast presses it. The force generated from the
jump from the trampette will affect the acceleration in the landing. Where the gymnast
uses a trampette to produce a greater acceleration, the acceleration will be the product
of the gymnast’s muscular power and the effect of the trampette [16].

3.1.3
XsensDOT sensors
Xsens DOT is a development platform designed for the analysis and reporting of human
kinematics with precision motion tracking. The platform features high­accuracy wearable
sensors and a software development program (SDK). The Xsens DOT sensors can be
used alone or with multiple sensors connected. Each sensor is small, lightweight, and
water­resistant, which makes it an excellent platform for analyzing motion. The sensor
kit comes with five sensors and non­slippery harnesses with pockets for the sensors.
XSens DOT software is used to organize the sensors, connect them and control the motion
recordings.
The Xsens DOT is an inertial measurement unit (IMU) that includes an accelerometer,
a magnetometer, and a gyroscope. The accelerometer measures the acceleration, the
magnetometer measures the magnetic field like a compass, and the gyroscope measures
the angular velocity or rate of turn. Each of these independent elements gathers data that
can be utilized for analysis, but when combined, they can be used to compute orientation
through a process known as sensor fusion [31]. The coordinate system of the sensors
is a right­handed coordinate Cartesian system that is body­fixed to the sensors where x
is upwards, y is to the left, and z is out. The orientation determined by Xsens DOT is
the orientation of the sensor’s coordinate system in relation to the local earth coordinate
system [40]. A sensor and its corresponding coordinate system can be seen in figure 3.4.
Figure 3.4: The XSens DOT sensor with the coordinate system where x is upwards, y is
backwards, and z is outwards [40].
In the app, there are two ways to record data with the sensors; recording mode and
real­time streaming. Motion data is recorded on the sensor’s internal storage while in
recording mode and can be exported for post­processing. Real­time streaming involves a
continual Bluetooth connection that streams and logs motion data to a central device (like
a mobile phone). For each mode, there are different output rates available. In this study
the recording mode was chosen with an output rate of 60 Hz, giving a time step size of
16.7 ms [40]. Through the app, the recordings are manually started, stopped, and saved
or sent to a chosen device.

Evaluation of the performance
The performance of the jumps is evaluated in accordance with the trampette execution

Conclusion
This chapter covers the conclusion of the overall findings of the study as well as a discus­
sion of improvements and possible future work.
5.1
Conclusion
The goals of the study were to analyze if there is a technical­related primary factor or
factors, that result in the best performance of a trampette jump in TeamGym gymnastics
as well as compare the measurement methods used in the study.
Ideally, the results from Tracker and the sensor could be completely identical since the
sensor is being tracked in Tracker. Looking at the graphs it can be seen that the mea­
surements correspond quite well, but are not identical.
The reason why these two measurement methods are not identical can be due to human
errors when measuring. The human errors that could occur in the sensor measurements
are that the sensor is not placed at the body’s center of mass, that the sensor is loose
inside the pocket and moves which could lead to the sensor mistaking the movement of
the sensor as a sudden movement of the athlete. Human errors for the video analysis in
Tracker are that the tracked points are misplaced, i.e. not on the sensors and not at the
body’s center of mass, and that the angle of the video affects the actual dimensions of the
measurement resulting in imprecise analysis results.
It was somewhat expected for the data from the sensor and Tracker to not be identical.
The sensors are fixed on the athletes and have their own built­in instruments for track­
ing, that track the athlete’s movements automatically, while for Tracker the sensors are
tracked by manually choosing the points and trying to place them on the sensors. With
that being said, there are more errors expected to come from the Tracker results than
from the sensor, and therefore we consider the sensor data to be more reliable.
Overall, there are correlations between different variables from all three measurement
methods. This means that even though there are possible errors in the data, the mea­
surements are still related in some way. Since the measurement methods are not com­
pletely identical it is difficult to estimate which value of the variables is correct and should
be used to determine the results for the technical­related factors. Due to this, it is more
challenging to estimate which technical­related factor gives the best performance.
As the grading is a description of a good performance, a correlation between that and
any technical­related factor would conclude that optimization of that factor would result in
a good performance. Even though the judges focus on factors such as the body shape
in air and in landing, when grading, these execution faults are dependent on everything
that the gymnast does prior to take­off such as the running speed. This is in accordance
with what previous research have suggested, that gymnasts can have very little effect on
their performance after launching into the air. Therefore they must optimize everything
that happens prior to the launch to optimize their performance.
Since there were no visible correlations to the grading it is not easy to make distinct con­
clusions about which technical­related factor has the greatest effect on the overall perfor­
mance of a trampette jump. Although, the factors that are considered important by the
judges are dependent on the technical­related factors.

Correlations between other variables that somehow depict the performance were inspected.
The variables that were estimated to be able to depict the performance were: the height
of the jump, the launch velocity, and the acceleration in the trampette. The technical­
related factors that had some correlations with these variables were: the running speed,
the body angle when jumping into the trampette, and the body angle when leaving the
trampette. The jump into the trampette was not correlated with any variable. Perhaps
it can be concluded that the jump into the trampette has a less significant effect on the
performance than the other technical­related factors. Hence, it is more important to run
fast and position the body correctly into and from the trampette.
Analyzing if there is a primary technical­related factor is a relatively big and open question.
It is difficult to draw conclusions about the performance based on a few technical­related
factors since there are many other factors that can affect the results. That is, even though
the athletes run fast, they can slow down in the trampette by altering their body shape
and therefore get less height in the air or perform worse.
Overall, result findings indicate that there is not a specific technical­related factor or factors
that result in the best performance of a trampette jump. The compared measurement
methods showed some similarities but were not identical. All in all, it might not be ideal to
be comparing measurement methods at the same time as analyzing if there is a primary
factor or factors, since there is no baseline to compare the findings to. It might be better
to choose a single measurement method, that has already been optimized, to be able to
draw better conclusions.
5.2
Improvements
There are different things that can be improved in the study to improve the results. The
improvements should be made before any other aspects are looked into.
The setup of the measurements could have been much more precise. The placement of
timing gates was different between clubs since it was estimated, after the first 6 measure­
ments, that the second gate should be placed closer to the third gate. This was done to
get more accurate results for the running speed into the trampette since the athletes can
change their speed fast in a short amount of time.
The video setup should have been more professional. The same camera should have
been used for all measurements and set at a fixed distance so that the full run, all timing
gates, the jump, and the landing were visible. Another reason why placing the camera at
a fixed distance would improve the measurements is that it could prevent the video from
being angled. This improvement would help make the Tracker data more accurate since
the dimensions would be more precise. It would also improve the data if a high­quality
camera would have been used which would give a higher frame rate and therefore more
data points.
Some improvements regarding the sensors could be made. Placing a colored tag on top
of the sensor, when the sensors are placed on the athlete, would make them more notice­
able in the videos and therefore improve the sensor tracking in Tracker. With improved
tracking, it would be possible to see improved results for the Tracker data which could
then result in a higher correlation between Tracker and the sensor. Another improvement
could be to place the trunk sensor more appropriately. Placing it lower and closer to the
body’s center of mass instead of at the trunk’s center of mass might result in more accu­
rate results. The reason why it was placed higher is that the athletes all wore loose shirts
so placing it higher made the sensor harnesses sit tight and not move with the athlete. To
Physics of a Trampette Jump in TeamGym
59
==PAGE:72==
be able to place it lower the athletes would need to wear only a sports bra so the sensor
harnesses could be placed directly on the skin.
Performing some test runs on the sensors, before starting the measurements, would be
preferred but since the sensors were borrowed, and not owned, that was not possible. If
a test run would be performed then the data could have been loaded into the computer
and analyzed before the measurements, to obtain a better knowledge of the expected
results. It would also give a better overall knowledge of the sensors and their function
which could lead to better results. If test runs were performed before the measuring, it
would be possible to know when to press start for the sensor recording. This could help
solve the issue with the late set time and missing data points for the run.
Choosing a more simple jump to perform, or a jump that does not contain twists along
with the turns might have made the analysis of the measurements easier. Even though,
an easier jump might have given less variety in the data since the Full­in half­out is a
rather difficult jump to perform which requires a strong gymnastics background, good
muscle memory, and knowledge of the body’s ability in the air. Therefore, by choosing
the Full­in half­out it would be certain that the athletes chosen for the measurement would
be familiar with basic trampette techniques and perform in the way that was required to
get concrete results. Another reason why it was chosen as the performed jump was, as
previously mentioned, that the Full­in half­out is considered to be a standard jump that
national TeamGym gymnasts in Iceland must be able to perform.
The larger the sample size, the smaller the margin of error in the measurement. There­
fore, it would have been preferred to have many more athletes participating and more
jumps, or at least an equal amount of jumps, performed by each athlete. The sample
size was not larger due to uncontrollable circumstances such as the Covid­19 pandemic.
An increase in the sample size might result in more accurate results. An equal amount
of jumps performed by each athlete could result in stronger correlations for the genetic
variables.
Additionally, there are some improvements that could be made regarding the markings of
the events. First, analyze the specific events in Tracker and mark them from the visualiza­
tion. Then analyze the data from the graphs, to see where each event should be marked
according to physics. Combining the two could lead to a more precise overall marking.
5.3
Future work
There are other factors that can be taken into account, to enhance the results.
For future work, it would be interesting to look into the forces applied by the athletes
throughout the performance since forces are a key factor in sports. Placing force plates
under the trampette’s legs would be one approach to analyzing the forces in the trampette.
Knowing the forces applied could further improve the velocity and acceleration analysis.
A way to make the results more precise would be to combine the centers of mass of the
body parts into the body’s center of mass. To do this, the mass and length of each body
part to which a sensor is attached must be known.
Other factors to explore would be to include the results from the gyroscope in the sensor to
analyze the motion in air. The results from the gyroscope could also give more information
about the athlete’s body position in the different phases of the study.
