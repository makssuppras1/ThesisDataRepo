Abstract 
The Groundwater Directive has been developed in Water Framework Directive in EU, 
setting objectives of achieving good quantitative and chemical status. Much 
importance is attached to quality of groundwater. Landfill is a common method of 
waste disposal, and leachate from landfill containing abundant amount of pollutants 
poses a risk to groundwater. Hence, risk assessment at landfill site is important for 
regional water source management. Landfill leachate plume in sandy/gravel aquifers 
has been subject to many studies, however landfill on clay till has been rarely worked 
on. In this study, a landfill located on a complex geological setting with clays is 
studied and risks to the connected aquifers were assessed.   
Pillemark landfill is an old landfill located on south of Samsø island. There is a 
shallow aquifer right below the landfill, and a deeper aquifer serving as a water 
supply aquifer. Between these two aquifer there is a clay layer. This site is remediated 
with a pumping system collecting the leachate under the landfill. To assess the risks in 
two aquifers, chemical data has been analyzed, a 3D flow model has been set up, and 
finally chemical transport simulation has been performed.  
By interpreting chemical data, the past and current contamination has been understood. 
In shallow aquifer, the zone below the southern part of the landfill has been 
contaminated severer than the zone below the northern part. The most critical 
contaminants 
are 
xenobiotic 
organic 
compounds 
4-CPP, 
MCPP 
and 
4-chloro-2-methylphenol, as well as inorganic compounds chloride and ammonium. 
There is a degradation of MCPP to 4-chloro-2-methylphenol in shallow aquifer. Deep 
aquifer is clean as no chemicals is higher than criteria and background concentration 
threshold. 
The steady state 3D flow model was built with COMSOL Multiphysics, based on a 
geological model. As a prerequisite, hydraulic conductivities of two aquifers have been 
estimated from slug tests and grain size analyses. The model simulated the flow fields 
in two aquifers. In shallow aquifer, water flows towards the central well in horizontal 
direction, and in vertical direction, water flows down until reaching the remediation 
well or furtherly goes down into the deep aquifer. In deep aquifer, flow direction is 
towards southwest. Well 109.214 captures water from the zone under the landfill. 
However, due to uncertain hydraulic conductivities and recharge, as well as the lack of 
observed data for calibration, the simulation has uncertainty.  
A vertical transport simulation has been performed at the beginning of this project, for 
chloride, ammonium and XOCs. This simulation found that chloride and 4-CPP are 
most critical chemicals and need to be furtherly assessed. Hence, full scale simulations 
from landfill to waterworks were performed, based on the hydrogeological information 
obtained from the 3D flow model and mass discharge assumption for worst case.

According to these simulations, chloride concentration is in a safe level in the 
waterworks, however, 4-CPP concentration is still higher than criterion and poses risk 
to the water abstracted by well 109.214.  
Future works are suggested to focus on data collection, measurement of hydraulic and 
geological properties such as hydraulic head and hydraulic conductivity of clay, and 
estimation of mass discharge and net-infiltration. These will form more solid frame for 
risk assessment.

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Background 
Groundwater contributes to 97% of freshwater resource on earth, excluding glacier 
and ice caps. About 75% of EU residents rely on groundwater for water supply 
(European Commission, 2008), not only for drinking water use but also for industry 
and agriculture. In Denmark, more than 99% of water use depends on groundwater. 
The abstracted water from subsurface will just be modestly treated by aeration and 
filter before sending to users (Jørgensen & Stockmarr, 2009). Therefore much 
importance is attached to inherent quality of groundwater. The Groundwater Directive 
has been developed in Water Framework Directive in EU, clarifying the chemical 
status criteria of groundwater and specification of pollution control, and setting 
objectives of achieving good quantitative and chemical status. 
Groundwater contamination is usually linked to anthropogenic activities, such as 
waste disposal, agriculture, and industrial activities. Landfill is a common method of 
waste disposal. Leachate from landfill contains abundant amount of pollutants such as 
DOC, inorganic compounds, heavy metal and xenobiotic organic compounds 
(Christensen et al., 2001). In many countries, liners and leachate collecting system 
have been built to reduce the leachate from landfill. However, there are also a large 
number of landfills without liners or leachate collecting system, which lead to threat 
to groundwater (Breukelen et al., 2003; Depountis et al., 2009; Hoehn et al., 2000). 
Thus, landfill risk assessment is important for regional water source management and 
has been studied all over the world. Landfill leachate plume migration differs in 
direction and velocity at different sites, depending on hydraulic gradients, 
conductivities and physical boundaries such as geological strata, infiltration, rivers, 
wells etc. (Christensen et al., 2001; Masoner et al., 2015; An et al., 2013). The 
composition and fate of contaminants is also site-specific, relatively to type of waste 
deposits and landfill age, as well as soil property, redox condition and microbial 
community etc. In general, xenobiotic organic compounds (XOCs) are frequently 
found at municipal solid waste (MSW) landfill sites, including aromatic hydrocarbons 
(benzene, toluene and etc.), phenols, and pesticides such as Mecoprop and other 
phenoxyacid herbicides (Baun et al., 2004; Kjeldsen et al., 2002). XOCs always have 
high toxicity already at low concentrations, and most of them are bioaccumulative. 
Attenuation of XOCs in leachate plume always is affected by degradation and 
sorption. Inorganic compounds, such as chloride and ammonia, are frequently found 
at high concentrations at landfill sites. Inorganic compounds rarely contribute to 
severe pollution in groundwater, but if enter into surface water the trophic structure 
may be disturbed due to some nutritive components like ammonium, which may lead 
to eutrophication and mortality of fish (Barkoh et al., 2004). Hence, being able to 
control the discharge of contaminants at landfill leachate plumes is crucial.  
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
Landfill leachate plume in sandy/gravel aquifers has been subject for many studies 
(e.g., Breukelen et al., 2003; Dong et al., 2015), however landfill on clay till has been 
rarely worked on. Previous studies suggested that there is a high spatial variability of 
hydrogeological parameters and chemical concentrations in clay-rich geology, leading 
to high uncertainty on the flow and transport (Milosevic et al., 2012; Thomsen et al., 
2012; McKay et al., 1998; Gerber et al., 2001). 
Groundwater modeling tools are widely used for risk assessment at landfill sites (e.g., 
Munroet al., 1997; Tsanis, 2006). In Denmark, groundwater modeling is highly 
developed with support by policy and legislation, along with geophysical mapping 
and national database (Refsgaard et al., 2010). Previous studies have been using 
groundwater model integrated with field site measurements for risk assessment in 
groundwater (Balbarini, 2017; Chambon et al., 2011; Henriksen et al., 2008). Modeling 
on heterogeneous geological setting with clay is more complicated and with more 
uncertainty, due to spatial variation of hydraulic hydrogeological properties that poses 
challenge on data collection and requires more refinement. 
In this project, a landfill site in a clay till environment is studied. Pillemark landfill is 
located in the southern part of Samsø Island, on top of a shallow aquifer in contact 
with the landfill (Høyer et al., 2017). Hårmark waterworks is near the landfill and 
abstract groundwater from a deeper aquifer. Between the two aquifer there is a clay 
till layer, which should protect the deep aquifer from landfill leachate. A remediation 
pumping system has been established, collecting the leachate under the landfill. 
However, the vertical gradient of head drives a downward flow, making a potential 
risk from landfill to deep aquifer. Besides, the heterogeneous geology makes up a 
complex flow field which will result in complicated chemical transport network. 
Therefore the impact from landfill to two aquifers is concerned and to be identified in 
this study. 
1.2. 
Objectives 
In this thesis, the overall objective is to assess the risk arising from Pillemark landfill 
to: 1) the shallow aquifer; 2) the deep aquifer, especially at the point of Hårmark 
waterworks which abstract water from deep aquifer. The objective is achieved by: 
- 
Reviewing of landfill in clayey till, including source, transport and fate of key 
contaminants, groundwater flow, and modeling tools for risk assessment. 
- 
Analyzing the impact on shallow aquifer by interpreting chemical data, and 
constructing a 3D model with detailed geological setting to identify the 
hydrogeology, flow and contaminant spreading trend in the shallow aquifer. 
- 
Assessing the impact on deep aquifer by evaluating the chemical data, 
interpreting the flow field and velocity by the 3D flow model, and modeling 
solute transport of key contaminants through the overlying clay till.

2. Review of Landfills, Fate of Key Contaminants 
and Modeling of Clayey Tills 
Previous studies on landfills in clayey till provide an overview on possible 
contaminants and their fates, and methods for risk assessment. This chapter introduces 
some landfill sites located in clay till all over the world and fate of key contaminants 
found in landfill sites. Typical models for risk assessment at clayey till sites are also 
introduced. 
2.1. 
Landfills in Clayey Till 
Landfill on sandy geology has been well studied worldwide, but only few studies 
focused on landfill at clayey till. Risby Landfill is an example of Danish old landfill 
located in a clay till setting and has been studied (Milosevic et al., 2012; Milosevic et al., 
2013; Thomsen et al., 2012). It deposited household waste and gardening waste. 
Groundwater paths were identified by using chloride and water isotopes. The redox 
condition in groundwater was characterized as anaerobic and methanogenic in the 
hotspot and less reduced near the stream. Contaminants such as chlorinated solvents, 
phenoxy acid pesticides and BTEX were found in groundwater at Risby Landfill site. 
Concentrations of chlorinated solvents were below the detection limit at 15 m outside to 
the hotspot, suggesting the immobilization in clay till. 4-CPP, which is a dechlorination 
product of dichlorprop, was widely detected at this site. Anaerobic degradation of 
4-CPP was found near the hotspot and aerobic degradation of 4-CPP possibly existed 
near the stream.Estimations also suggested that there is degradation of BTEX. Note that 
significant spatial variation of chemical concentrations was found in leachate plume 
because of the underlying clay till.  
Some landfills located at clay till have been studied in other countries. The Laidlaw 
landfill (McKay et al., 1998) in Sarnia, Ontario, Canada, is located on a 25-40 m thick 
clay-rich glacial deposits, accepting industrial waste. Chemical monitoring was 
focused on chloride which was in a 20 times higher concentration in leachate than in 
background. Lateral transport of chloride was observed, agreed with the lateral 
groundwater flow according to observed hydraulic head gradient. Contaminants 
migration rates in the upper zone were much faster than the lower zone in clay till, 
because of the weathered and fractured geology in upper zone. Another example of 
landfill in clay till is Fredericton landfill in southern New Brunswick (Munro et al., 
1997). It is a municipal waste landfill located at clayey till with a minimum thickness 
of 10m. Groundwater flow most significantly is vertical downward. High 
concentration of chloride and DOC were observed and these components were used 
for vertical transport estimation, results indicating that advection and dispersion are 
more significant than diffusion.

2.2. 
Key Contaminants 
Chemical fate characterization is important in risk assessment in aquifers as processes 
such as sorption, degradation significantly affect the migration and concentrations of 
chemicals. In this section, fate of critical inorganic contaminant (chloride and 
ammonium) and organic contaminants (phenoxy acid pesticides) are discussed 
according to findings from previous studies. Redox condition at landfill sites, as a 
prerequisite for chemical fate evaluation, is also introduced. 
2.2.1. Redox Condition 
Redox condition in groundwater contamination plume has effect on biochemical 
process in groundwater and can provide supports for selecting remediation methods. 
Identification of redox condition is a crucial prerequisite for chemical fate evaluation 
and risk assessment.  
Leachate from landfill entering into pristine aquifer usually change the redox condition 
and develop a reduced plume, due to presence of reduced compounds in leachate and 
depletion of electron acceptors (Christensen et al., 2001). A gradient of redox condition 
is always found in aquifers under landfills (Lyngkilde & Christensen, 1992; Bjerg et al., 
1995; Basberg et al., 1998; Heaton et al., 2005; Milosevic et al., 2013): At the hotspot 
zone, the groundwater is often in strong reducing condition with methanogenic process; 
downgradient from it is sulfate reduction zones, following by less reducing conditions 
from sulfate reducing, iron reducing, manganese reducing to nitrate reducing; beyond 
the reducing zones is aerobic if the pristine aquifer is oxidized. 
Analysis of groundwater composition is always used to determine redox condition. 
Redox sensitive species are mostly of interest. Table 2-2 shows the redox sensitive 
species and criteria used to define redox condition in two Danish sites (Lyngkilde & 
Christensen, 1992; Bjerg et al., 1995). Oxygen is the most common electron acceptor. 
High concentration of oxygen (usually larger than 1mg/L) indicates an aerobic 
condition. Sulfate is another electron acceptor decreasing concentration of sulfate 
indicates sulfate reducing condition, where sulfide will be produced, therefore high 
concentration of sulfide also indicates sulfate reducing condition. In aerobic condition, 
ammonium can be oxidized to nitrate, therefore presence of nitrate may indicate 
aerobic condition or nitrate reducing condition. To distinguish these two conditions 
based on ammonium and nitrate content, the temporal and spatial changes in fractions 
of ammonium and nitrate among total nitrogen are always investigated. Nitrite and 
dinitrogen oxide (N2O) are intermediate products in conversion between ammonium 
and nitrate, they may indicate nitrate reducing condition. High concentration of 
dissolved iron (Fe2+) sometimes indicates an iron reducing condition as it is the end 
product in iron reduction, and it will act as electron donor in aerobic condition. 
Dissolved manganese (Mn2+) is the end product of manganese reduction, indicating a 
manganese reducing condition. Methane is the indicator of methanogenic condition,

and it can be generated by degradation of organic carbon or reduction of CO2 by 
microorganisms (Bordenave et al., 2013). Analyzing groundwater composition is a 
very common approach to evaluate redox condition, however it is quite depended on 
site condition such as groundwater flow and background concentrations. Besides, 
numerous and representative samples and data are needed.

Redox potential is a parameter describing the oxidizing or reducing capacity and can be 
used to evaluate redox condition (Søndergaard, 2009). Electrochemical measurement is 
an approach to measure redox potential between two electrodes. It cannot be simply 
defined as positive redox potential indicating oxidizing environment and negative 
redox potential indicating reducing environment, or a lower redox potential 
representing a more reducing condition. It depends on the methodology of

2.2.2. Fate of Chloride and Ammonium in Aquifers  
Chloride is an inert component in aquifer, which means it doesn’t undergo chemical or 
physic-chemical process in aquifer (Christensen et al., 2001; Fatta et al., 1998); 
therefore chloride is always used as a conservative tracer in groundwater studies. The 
only natural attenuation mechanism of chloride is dilution.  
Ammonium in landfill leachate is usually generated from destruction of organic 
compounds containing amidogen (Kulikowska & Klimiuk, 2008). In aquifers, 
ammonium majorly undergo two attenuation processes except dilution: oxidation and 
sorption by cation exchange.  
In aerobic condition, ammonium can be oxidized into nitrite and nitrate performed by 
microorganism. Anaerobic oxidization of ammonium is likely to take place with 
MnO2 or nitrate. These processes have been widely used in wastewater treatment and 
recently being proofed in groundwater. Aerobic and anaerobic ammonium oxidizing 
bacteria have been found in soil (Schmidt et al., 2002; Miller et al., 2009), and 
investigations on contamination plume also suggest degradation of ammonium may 
have significant contribution in ammonium attenuation (Erskine, 2000; Christensen et 
al., 2001; Atta et al., 2015).  
Sorption of ammonium is mostly due to ion exchange. Ion exchange capacity of 
ammonium is highly depended on the sorbent and ion composition in aqueous phase. 
In neutral condition, cation exchange of ammonium is likely to take place on clay 
minerals (Chang & Donahue, 2007; Zhang et al., 2013). Cations carrying more charge 
number such as Al3+, Ca2+, and Mg2+ are competitors to NH4+ in cation exchange. 
Abundant amount of cations with low charge such as Na+ also forms competition to 
NH4+ (Buss et al., 2004). 
High concentration of ammonium entering surface water will cause problems as it is a 
nutrient and contributes to eutrophication (Huang et al., 2003). In surface water, 
biological degradation and sorption are also two major processes of ammonium 
attenuation (Du et al., 2017). Besides, in wetlands, ammonium can be absorbed by 
plants (Lusby et al., 1998; Gao et al., 2014). 
2.2.3. Fate of Phenoxy Acid Pesticides in Aquifers  
Phenoxy acids are a group of xenobiotic organic compounds and widely used as 
pesticides. Phenoxy acids such as MCPP (2-(4-chloro-2-methylphenoxy)-propanoic

acid), 
dichlorprop 
(2-(2,4-dichlorophenoxy) 
propanoic

and 
4-CPP 
(2-(4-Chlorophenoxy) propionic acid) have been observed at many landfill sites in 
Denmark (Kjeldsen, 1993; Tuxen et al., 2003; Baun et al., 2004) and in other 
countries (Gintautas et al., 1992; Harrison et al., 1998). Phenoxy acids are mostly 
attenuated by sorption and degradation. 
Sorption of phenoxy acid depends on pH, sorbent material and organic carbon content. 
Madsen et al. (2000) found that sorption of phenoxy acids primarily depends on pH, 
and sorption is stronger in low pH (<6.7) condition than in high pH condition. No 
sorption or very low sorption of e.g., MCPP and dichlorprop, have been found in 
sandy aquifers (Rügge et al., 1999; Tuxen et al., 2000). Distribution coefficient (Kd) 
of organic compounds are usually estimated with empirical equations relative to 
organic carbon content in soil and Kow of organic compounds. However, phenoxy 
acids can be charged in neutral pH condition. Schellenberg et al. (1984) has 
established estimation formula for sorption coefficient of ionic compounds, and it is 
suitable for material containing organic carbon content (foc) larger than 0.1%. For 
materials which have foc less than 0.1%, estimation of sorption coefficient or 
retardation factor by experiment combined with transport model is preferred. 
Jørgensen et al. (1998) calculated retardation factor of MCPP in fractured clay by 
fitting modeling concentrations with column experiment concentrations, the values 
ranging from 1 to 6.8.  
Aerobic degradation of phenoxy acids has been numerously reported in field scale 
studies (Harrison et al., 1998; Tuxen et al., 2000; Sørensen et al., 2003), while in most 
cases no degradation were observed under anaerobic condition (Rügge et al., 1999; 
Reitzel et al., 2004). However, there are still a few cases revealing anaerobic 
degradation of phenoxy acids. For instance, Harrison et al. (2003) found degradation 
of MCPP in nitrate reducing condition, and Milosevic et al. (2013) found 
dechlorination of dichlorprop to 4-CPP and further degradation of 4-CPP in anaerobic 
condition. The origin of 4-CPP in landfill leachate could be impurity of herbicides and 
metabolite of 4-CPP (Reitzel et al., 2004).

2.3. 
Modeling tools for Risk Assessment in Clayey Till Settings 
Modeling tools are widely used for risk assessment at sites with clay till settings. 
Usually these models aim to provide hydrogeological information and simulate 
chemical transport in clay till. This chapter presents an overview of some typical 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
modeling tools for risk assessment in clay tills. 
Simplified clay till can be considered as porous media with low permeability. Modeling 
tools for simulating chemical transport in porous media has been plenty studied (e.g. 
Lucille et al., 2000; Yang et al., 2008), usually taking into account chemical, 
physic-chemical and biochemical processes such as advection, dispersion, diffusion 
sorption and degradation. However, clay tills are not simply porous media at many sites, 
which are often found with fractures (Munro et al., 1997; Klint & Gravesen, 1999;). 
Modeling for fractured clay till has been developed. EPM model is commonly used, 
assuming the fractured system as equivalent porous media, in which fitted effective 
porosity and/or hydraulic conductivities were used (e.g. Khaleel, 1989; Chambon et al., 
2011a). Dual porosity models are also used for fractured clay, which assume two porous 
media representing matrix and fracture (e.g. Knorr et al., 2017). Another type of model 
for fractured clay system is discrete fractured-porous media model (e.g. Munro et al., 
1997; Chambon et al., 2009). In these models, chemical transport is simulated both in 
parallel fractures and in matrix, along with the interaction between fractures and 
matrix. 
In many sites, not only clay tills are of interest, but also the connecting aquifers, and 
risk assessment usually takes the whole system into account. Therefore, modeling for 
multi-layer geology is important. One way is to deal with each layer as a single 
component. Lemming et al. (2010, 2012) used a 1D model to calculate mass discharge 
through fractured clay till into an aquifer, and combining with a degradation model for 
chemical fate simulation in aquifer, the concentrations at a supplying well located 
downgradient to the source zone was estimated. Similar to Lemming’s case, Troldborg 
et al. (2008) has built a modeling tool for multi-layer system including a layer of clay 
till, considering both local scale and catchment scale. For local scale, a leaching model 
estimated mass discharge; for catchment scale, a transport model used the mass flux 
from the local scale model, simulated chemical transport along the source zone to the 
supply well, and calculated mass flux and chemical concentration at supply well.  
Transport models for multi-layer systems have been developed, in which the layers will 
no longer be handled individually. Malaguerra et al. (2013) built a transport model for 
three geological layers (sand-clay-chalk) connecting to surface water, using COMSOL 
Multiphysics, and evaluated the potential risk of supply well from surface water.  
Parameswari & Mudgal (2015) built a four-layered 3D model to simulate contaminants 
migration and degree of dilution. 
In the sites with unknown and complex hydrology, where it is difficult to understand the 
flow field and transport with observed data, groundwater flow model is usually used to 
provide support for identifying hydrogeological properties such as gradient, velocity, 
and water balance which are required in chemical transport simulation (Kurniawan et 
al., 2007; Juhler et al., 2010; Raynauld et al., 2016;).  
In general, solute transport models have been developed and widely used in risk 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
assessment at clayey till sites, and sometimes groundwater flow model is also used to 
provide hydrogeological information and contribute for chemical transport simulation. 
Modelling for homogeneous geological and hydrogeological property has been studied 
more than modelling for heterogeneous geology and complex hydrogeology. Risk 
assessment in complex geology with clay till setting is still a challenge and need to be 
intensively studied.

Methods for Determining Hydraulic Conductivity 
Hydraulic conductivity is the key parameter of groundwater flow in subsurface. To 
obtain the values of hydraulic conductivity, two methods are applied: grain size 
analyses and in-situ slug test. 
4.2.1. Grain Size Distribution 
Hydraulic conductivity is related to grain size distribution and many empirical 
solutions have been established to correlate between hydraulic conductivity and grain 
size distribution (Pucko et al., 2015). Estimation of hydraulic conductivity (also 
referred as K value in this report) based on grain-size distribution has been studied and 
widely used.  
The results of grain size analysis of soil samples collected from borehole 109.297 and 
109.299 was provided by Knud Erik Klint. An Excel based tool HydrogeoSieveXL 
(Devlin, 2015) was used to estimated K value from the grain size distribution data. 15 
methods of estimating hydraulic conductivity were applied in HydrogeoSieveXL, each 
requiring a certain applicable condition specified soil properties such as soil type and 
d10 range. The geometric mean of results from all valid methods was calculated as the 
final estimated K value.

4.2.2. Slug Test  
Slug test is an in-situ methods to determine hydraulic properties of aquifer. The basic 
principle is to add or remove water in groundwater well and then monitor the change of 
hydraulic head which can be interpreted with different models to obtain value of 
hydraulic parameters.  
The first step of slug test is to measure the static water level in the well, and then 
establish an initial displacement of water level. Two methods are widely used to 
create the initial displacement. One of which is introducing a vacuum condition in the 
well. After the equilibrium of new water level, the vacuum is released. Another 
method is to add a volume of water into the well. The recovery of water level over 
time is recorded by monitoring the pressure in the well. After the water level reaches 
the initial static level, the test is finished (Hinsby et al, 1992).  
In this project, slug tests were performed to measure hydraulic conductivities in the 
two aquifers. The wells that were planned for slug test and their relating soil types are 
listed in Appendix 6, with some basic information of wells and aquifers that useful for 
data treatment. The field trip for slug test is reported in Appendix 6. Table 4-2 and 
Figure 4-2 show the wells where slug tests were done and K values were estimated 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
from. The method selected for creating initial displacements depends on the well 
property. For instance, wells with too large or too small diameter are not suitable for 
vacuum test, because the bladder used for vacuum test cannot fit these wells, therefore 
slug test were performed by adding water in these wells. 
Slug test data were recorded with program SLUGTESTV30 Geoprobe® on the field 
laptop, which included time series and pressure in the well. After calculating the 
displacement based on pressure, the datasheet consisting of time series and 
displacement was input into AQTESOLV®, with the basic information of wells and 
aquifer. K value was estimated based on input data, with different solution methods. 
Butler and Zhan (2004) method was used for tests with inertial effects in confined 
aquifer. Bouwer-Rice (1976) method was applied for tests in wells screened across 
water table. For the rest tests, KGS (Kansas Geological Survey) (Hyder et al., 1994) 
method was used. All of these three methods have common assumptions as below: 
1) Aquifer has infinite areal extent 
2) Aquifer is homogeneous and of uniform thickness 
3) Aquifer potentiometric surface is initially horizontal 
4) control well is fully or partially penetrating 
5) a volume of water, V, is injected or discharged instantaneously from the 
control well 
Bouwer-Rice method also assumes steady flow while KGS and Butler-Zhan methods 
assumes unsteady flow. Bouwer-Rice and KGS methods are viable for both confined 
and unconfined aquifer, and Butler-Zhan method can only be used in confined aquifer.

4.3. 
Risk Assessment Tool for Vertical Transport through Clay Till 
High concentration of contaminants, such as chloride, ammonium and pesticides, 
were found in the shallow aquifer overlying the clay till near the landfill. As the 
underlying deep aquifer serves as the water resource aquifer, the risk of contaminants 
vertical transport through the low-permeable clay till into the deep aquifer should be 
evaluated, with the concern of the breakthrough time. Therefore, a 1D transport model 
was applied to estimate the vertical transport of the contaminants in clay till under the 
landfill. This simulation helps to decide if it is necessary to do a horizontal transport 
simulation considering the waterworks as receptor. 
4.3.1. 1D Transport Model 
An excel tool of 1D transport model (Chambon, 2009; Danish EPA, 2015) was 
applied, with the assumption of continuous source overlying the low-permeability 
media. Figure 4-3 shows the sketch of the vertical transport modeling scale, where 
landfill is the source of contaminants. The highest value of contaminants 
concentration in the shallow aquifer right below the landfill is taken as the source 
concentration, the output from the model is the contaminants concentration at the 
bottom of clay till in time series. 
 
Figure 4-3. Sketch of the vertical transport modeling scale. 
The DTU V1D tool includes four conceptual models: 1) Presence of vertical fractures 
throughout the low-permeability media, 2) Equivalent Porous Media (EPM) with 1D 
advection/dispersion in uniform low-permeability media, 3) Diffusion only in the 
porous media, 4) 1st-order model for exponentially declining source (only applied for 
source trapped in matrix). In this simulation, as we have constant source and 
assumption of no fracture, only EPM model and Diffusion model were applied.

4.4. 
3D Groundwater Flow Model for Flow Field Simulation 
Since high concentrations of contaminants were found in shallow aquifer under the 
landfill, it is crucial to characterize the migration of these contaminants. As the 
geological heterogeneity in shallow aquifer and adjacent clays, it is difficult to 
simulate chemical transport directly, but the flow field in shallow aquifer can provide 
suggestion on chemical migration trend. Besides, the flow direction and velocity in 
deep aquifer are important to conduct risk assessment in deep aquifer. Therefore, to 
identify the flow field in shallow aquifer and deep aquifer, as well as to assess the 
effect of remediation pumping, a 3D flow model was built using COMSOL 
Multiphysics.  
4.4.1. Conceptual Model 
The model is built for an area of 1140m*1000m, shown as the yellow squared area in 
Figure 4-4 a). It consists of 5 layers, classified with 5 different K values as shown in 
Figure 4-5 and Table 4-6. The model is elevating from -18m DVR90 to the terrain 
including from bottom of deep sand layer to the terrain in Figure 3-3. Remediation 
well 109.292 and two waterworks wells (109.178 and 109.214) are included. 
The model will be set up as steady state model for these reasons: 1) only few wells 
have observed head in an adequate historical period, which is not enough for model 
calibration; 2) the model is large with a high resolution and takes 10-20 min to 
compute, if run for transient condition, it will take more time and memory.  
Wells are working as a continuous pumping well with constant pumping rates. As 
discussed in 4.1 and Appendix 3 and Appendix 4, the average pumping rates were 
calculated based on data from January 2012 to October 2017. For remediation well 
the average pumping rate is 0.96 m3/h; for well 109.178 and 109.214 the average 
pumping rate is 5.54 m3/h and 5.05 m3/h respectively. These average values are used 
in this model.

4.4.2. Model Set Up 
The entire geometry of this model is shown in Figure 4-4 b). The terrain was built with 
interpolation surface. Well 109.178 and 109.214 were installed with two isolated edges 
with the length of well screens. The remediation system was built with 4 horizontal 
edges under the landfill representing the 4 drains, as stated in 3.2, water under the 
landfill is abstracted through these 4 drains. The bottom plane is at elevation = -18m 
DVR90, which is assume to be the bottom of deep sand layer. It was possible to 
interpolate the surface of deep sand layer bottom based on the observed elevations, 
however, the interpolated surface was too close to the edges of pumping wells (109.178 
and 109.214). This kind of geometry cannot be generated correctly with all edges and 
domains applicable in COMSOL. Therefore, to make all components applicable, the 
bottom of deep sand layer was simplified as a plane elevated at -18m DVR90, which is 
the lowest point of deep sand layer in study area.  
The model is a voxel model with no layer physically separated. The different geological 
setting are identified by given different K values. K values are given by two 
interpolation functions: one is geological values with respect to spatial coordinates, 
which is exported from a local geological model constructed by GEUS; the second 
function is K values with respect to geological values. 
To obtain a precise simulation in shallow aquifer, the mesh in shallow aquifer should be 
as fine as possible. However, large amount of mesh elements takes huge virtual 
memory and very long time to compute. Therefore, to refine the model in shallow 
aquifer and at same time reduce the amount of mesh elements, the model was separated 
at z = 13m into two domains, the maximum size of mesh in top domain is 10m and in 
bottom domain is 23m. To refine the vertical mesh, z-direction scale was increased to 5 
in top domain and 10 in bottom domain. The total amount of mesh elements is 3057049, 
and the number of degrees of freedom is 568206. 
 
4.4.3. Boundary Condition 
Under the deep sand layer is Old Baltic till with low permeability. It can be assumed no 
water flow into the Old Baltic till from deep aquifer, therefore a zero flux boundary was 
used at the bottom of the model. 
The vertical boundaries were set with Dirichlet boundary condition, where head values 
are fixed. The head along each boundary were exported from the larger flow model. 
A flux/source surface was used over the terrain, with a flux equal to the recharge, as 
shown in Figure 4-4. According to 4.1 and Appendix 3, the average precipitation at 
Pillemark is 1.8 mm/day. Not all precipitation are infiltrated into subsurface. Rambøll 
(Madsen, 2012) has built a Samsø regional model and simulated for water balance, the 
==PAGE:42==

recharge 175 mm per year was used in Rambøll model, for the whole south Samsø. In 
this model, the same recharge value as in Rambøll’s model will be used, which is 0.48 
mm/day. 
 
4.4.4. Hydraulic conductivities 
The hydraulic conductivities initially applied in the model in Table 4-6 were obtained 
from various sources as described below, and each geological setting is assumed to be 
isotropic. The final K-values used in the calibrated model are discussed in section 5.1.2 
Hydraulic conductivities of postglacial freshwater sand, Tebbestrup meltwater 
sand/gravel and East Jutland meltwater sand were estimated based on grain size 
analyses and slug tests.  
The K value of clay layer (Mid Danish Clayey till and East Jutland clay till) used in this 
model is same as the calibrated value in the larger flow model, which is 1.6E-08 m/s. 
This value is also applied for clay in top layer. 
In the landfill, waste deposits has different K values from landfill liner, and it is highly 
influenced by deposit property such as waste composition, density and stress. To be 
simplified, in this model, landfill is considered with a homogenous geological setting. 
K value of landfill 1E-06 m/s is used in the model, which is a medium value in the 
typical range of K value of landfilled waste (Chen et al., 1994; Reddy et al., 2009). 
For the other types of sand and gravel, where no borehole is screened and slug tests 
cannot be performed, the same value as East Jutland meltwater sand was applied.

4.5. 
Risk Assessment in Deep Aquifer 
As the deep aquifer is a water supplying aquifer on Samsø, the water quality in deep 
aquifer is highly concerned, especially at the point of the waterworks. The decision of 
risk assessment in deep aquifer depends on the vertical transport simulation mentioned 
in 4.3. If the results from vertical transport simulation indicates that chemicals will 
enter in deep aquifer in near future, then a risk assessment considering the waterworks 
as a receptor will be conducted. The risk assessment includes two phases: a chemical 
transport simulation in deep aquifer to calculate the maximum concentration and mass 
discharge at the point of waterworks, and a calculation of concentration abstracted by 
waterworks well. 
4.5.1. Risk Assessment Tool for Transport of Contaminants Released from 
Landfill 
GrundRisk Landfill (Danish EPA, 2018) will be used to simulate the chemical transport 
in aquifer released from the landfill. This tool can take into account both vertical 
transport through clay till and horizontal transport in aquifer, for single source unit or 
multiple source units. In this study, the single unit simulation will be performed with 
continuous source concentration, therefore only the methods for single unit simulation 
will be introduced in this section. 
Figure 4-6 shows a sketch of the chemical transport modeling scale, including vertical 
transport through clay till and horizontal transport in deep aquifer. Based on the results 
from 3D flow model, as discussed in 5.3.6, the flow direction in deep aquifer is from the 
landfill area to well 109.214. Therefore, the point of compliance is the well 109.214 of 
waterworks. The model assumes homogeneous conditions, which means geological 
and hydraulic properties in each layer are constant in space and time, as well as the 
chemical properties. Advection only happens in one dimension in each layer, which 
means only vertical advection in clay till and longitudinal advection towards the point 
of compliance in deep aquifer.

To obtain a better understanding of stratigraphic units, grain size distribution of sand 
and clay samples is analyzed in this section. Table 5-1 presents the fractions of gravel, 
coarse sand, medium sand, fine sand and silt and clay in each soil sample, along with 
the classification. Figure 5-1 shows the grading curves. The soil samples were taken 
from different depth on well 109.297 (B302) and 109.299 (B305). Borehole logs can be

Grading curves of clay samples (B305-5, B305-7, B305-8.5, B305-11.5, B305-14.5 and 
B305-22.5) show great discrepancies from sandy samples. In clay samples, silt and clay 
is dominant with a fraction higher than 50%. Results from well B305 indicates that at a 
shallower clay sample at 5m has a much higher fraction of finer particles than other clay 
samples. Based on the local geological model, the sample at depth 5m is relating to East 
Jutland clayey till, while samples at 8.5m – 22.5m represent Mid Danish clayey till. The 
interface of these two clayey till is at the depth of 6-7m, therefore the sample from 
depth 7m can be assumed to have both East Jutland clayey till and Mid Danish clayey 
till. Grading curves of Mid Danish clayey till are similar to a supra-glacial deposits 
classified as silty to sand till in another Danish site (Putrih Jeza, 2017), however, as the 
curves are incomplete, the similarity is only seen in total sand and gravel classes. 
In postglacial freshwater sand samples (B302-1.5 and B302-2.5), the medium size 
particles like fine sand and medium sand are dominant, while in meltwater sand/gravel 
sample (B305-26), coarse particles gravel and coarse sand dominate. This contributes 
to the significant dissimilarity in grading curves of these two geology, therefore the 
considerable difference between K values for shallow aquifer and deep aquifer are 
expected. 
The grading curve of this sample at depth 3.5m in well B305 shapes neutrally between 
clay samples and postglacial freshwater sand. This can also been found from particle 
composition shown in Table 5-1. Besides, depth 3.5m in well B305 is near the interface 
of East Jutland meltwater sand layer and East Jutland clay layer. Based on these, the 
sample must contain both East Jutland freshwater sand and East Jutland clay.

5.2.1.1. 
Redox Condition 
Data of redox parameters (dissolved oxygen, nitrate, nitrite, manganese, dissolved 
iron, sulfate) in shallow aquifer are illustrated in Figure 5-4 and Figure 5-4. DGU 
number of each monitoring well can be found in Figure 4-1. Due to the lack of 
methane data, methanogenic condition cannot be identified. 
In 1984-2000, only few data at the southern part of landfill were collected. Based on 
these data, in which iron concentration was very high (73 mg/L) and dissolved oxygen 
was lower than 1mg/L, the south part of landfill could be characterized as iron 
reducing condition. 
In 2001-2010, concentration of oxygen higher than 1 mg/L was found in well 109.264 
south to the landfill, which was aerobic condition there. Another well south to the 
landfill (109.291) revealed a reducing condition. At the south part inside the landfill, 
high concentrations of dissolved iron have been observed in well 109.292 and 
109.261, indicating an iron reducing condition.  
In 2011-2016, concentrations of oxygen were all below 1 mg/L inside the landfill, 
indicating anaerobic conditions. At the north part of the landfill, high concentrations 
of nitrate, nitrite and manganese have been detected in well 109.260, which indicates 
a nitrate reducing to manganese reducing condition. At the south part, similar to the 
situation in 2001-2010, high concentrations of dissolved iron in well 109.292 and 
109.261, indicated an iron reducing condition. At the north end outside the landfill, 
based on data from well 109.259, concentration of oxygen greater than 1 mg/L has 
been observed, indicating an aerobic condition. Low concentrations of iron, 
manganese and nitrite also suggest for this. Oxygen concentration higher than 1mg/L 
has also been found in well 109.264, which is south to the landfill, indicating an 
aerobic condition. However, near this wells, well 109.297 and 109.291 revealed 
reducing conditions with low oxygen concentration. 
In general, the redox condition in shallow aquifer can be roughly interpreted as shown 
in Figure 5-2. The characterized redox conditions and covering area are with high 
uncertainty, due to heterogeneous geology, sparse data, and lacking of concentrations 
about other redox parameters (e.g. methane, sulfide).

5.2.1.3. 
Xenobiotic Organic Contaminants 
EU drinking water criteria limits a concentration of 0.1 ug/L for a single pesticide in 
groundwater. 4-CPP, MCPP, and 4-Chloro-2-methylphenol are three most critical 
XOCs which were observed with much higher concentrations than criteria in the 
shallow aquifer. The highest concentration of 4-CPP is 92 ug/L found in 109.261 
borehole inside the landfill on 16-09-2009. The highest concentration of MCPP is 13 
ug/L from borehole 109.261 observed on 16-09-2009. The highest concentration of 
4-Chloro-2-methylphenol is 3.4 ug/L found in borehole 109.261 in February 1994. 
Figure 5-8 shows concentrations of these three compounds in shallow aquifer, and 
concentrations over time are illustrated in Figure 24 to Figure 26 in Appendix 8. 
Similar to chloride and ammonium, boreholes 109.261 and 109.292 located at the 
south of landfill had higher concentrations of pesticides than northern located 
boreholes such as 109.260. This relates to the fact that the northern end of landfill are 
deposited majorly with inorganic waste while southern end deposited with organic 
waste, and the remediation system collected contaminants under the southern end of 
landfill. The decreasing of concentrations over time could be due to declining 
formation and depletion of these compounds. Nevertheless there may be degradation 
that contributes to the long-term decreasing and spatial variation, therefore relative

metabolites were also analyzed. 
4-CPP found in landfill leachate plume usually comes from degradation of 
dichlorprop or impurity of herbicides (e.g. dichlorprop and MCPP). In the shallow 
aquifer, dichlorprop at this site has been measured since 1998, and detected with low 
concentrations (less than 0.1 ug/L). If the origin of 4-CPP is the impurity of 
dichlorprop and MCPP, it is not reasonable that 4-CPP has a much higher 
concentration than dichlorprop and MCPP. Hence, majority of 4-CPP in shallow 
aquifer could be the metabolite of dichlorprop. However, there is no data of 
dichlorprop or 4-CPP before 1998, this conjecture cannot be proved. In wells with 
aerobic conditions (e.g. 109.259, 109.264), the concentrations of 4-CPP were

data, it is impossible to assess the degradation of 4-CPP by analyzing metabolite

5.3. 
Simulation Results from 3D Flow Model 
5.3.1. Model Calibration 
The model was manually calibrated by adjusting K values to fit the observed head 
data, as stated in 4.1 and 4.4.4. K value of clay till layer has been adjusted to fit the 
simulated head difference with the observed difference between two aquifers. K 
values of aquifers were adjusted to fit the head distribution in each aquifer. After 
calibration, the sand layers in top layer are merged, the clay tills in middle clay layer 
has a smaller K value than clay in the top layer, and the K value for deep aquifer is the 
largest value measured from slug tests, as shown in Table 5-3 (Scenario 1). The 
correlation between simulated value and observed value is illustrated in Figure 5-12, 
and Figure 5-12 a) shows that the gradient between the two aquifers is well described 
by the model. Figure 5-12 b) shows that the simulated heads in the shallow aquifer fit 
well with the observed heads, and this provides confidence on the gradient and thus 
the simulated flow direction and velocity in the shallow aquifer are good. Figure 5-12 
c) shows that in the deep aquifer there are a few measurements fitting, but there are 
also some measurements off. The expected effect is that the simulated gradient is 
lower than the observed one. The average error between simulated head and observed 
head are 0.205 m (relative error 1.7%) in deep aquifer and 0.204 m (relative error 
1.0%) in shallow aquifer. In this study, since the observed head has in each well has a 
wide range (wider than 0.44 m in shallow aquifer and wider than 2 m in deep aquifer) 
as shown in Figure 11 and Figure 12 in Appendix 5, the error 0.205 m and 0.204 m 
are very low values compared to the range width of observed head. Therefore, the 
error is acceptable.  
Sensitivity analysis reveals that recharge has a significant effect on the simulated 
heads, as discussed in next section, therefore a second scenario is studied with a lower 
recharge and a lower hydraulic conductivity of clay till. The values of hydraulic 
conductivity and recharge after calibration are shown in in Table 5-3 (Scenario 2). The 
correlation between simulated value and observed value is shown in Figure 5-12, and 
the correlation has very little discrepancy from scenario 1. The average error between 
simulated head and observed head are 0.196 m (relative error 1.6%) in deep aquifer 
and 0.209 m (relative error 1.0%) in shallow aquifer. By changing the K value of clay 
layer and infiltration, numerous combination can be found to fit the simulated head 
with the observed ones, and scenario 2 is just one of them. To find the optimal 
combination, reliable reference of K values of clay and estimation of net-infiltration 
are needed. Vertical fluxes in clay could also be useful. Besides, more observed head 
in the modelling area outside the landfill may also be helpful, because they enlarge 
the calibration into larger scale.

6. Risk Assessment 
The potential risk is often evaluated based on target concentrations (e.g. Dong et 
al., 2015). In this chapter, the measured and predicted concentrations will be 
compared with EU drinking water standard and integrated with the flow field to 
evaluate the risks in two aquifers. 
6.1. 
Risk Assessment in Shallow Aquifer 
In the shallow aquifer, especially at the southern part of the landfill, inorganic 
compounds such as chloride and ammonium, and xenobiotic organic compounds 
such as pesticides 4-CPP and MCPP are present. A remediation pumping well 
removes the leachate under the landfill. According to the results from 3D flow 
model, the pumping work decreased the hydraulic head in the contaminated 
shallow aquifer to prevent the horizontal migration to other sand lenses in the top 
layer. The contaminated groundwater in shallow aquifer under the landfill will be 
abstracted by remediation well or go down to the deep aquifer. Concentrations of 
chemicals have been decreasing over time which could be the results of 
remediation pumping and natural attenuation, indicating a decreasing risk in 
shallow aquifer. In general, groundwater for the shallow aquifer is contaminated 
but will not spread further in the top layer according to the flow field simulation. 
However, the simulation from 3D flow model has uncertainty due to lack of data 
and geological heterogeneity. Well distributed dataset of observed head in the 
modelling area can help to calibrate the model better and find the optimized 
hydraulic conductivities and infiltration. Measured hydraulic conductivities of 
clay and estimated net-infiltration into the model can be reference in model 
calibration. Other hydrogeological properties such as flow velocity and vertical 
fluxes, if it is possible to be measured or estimated, may also help to reduce the 
uncertainty. The heterogeneous geology increase the challenge to collect these 
data. Additionally, the flow model is based on a geological model, but the geology 
in shallow aquifer may be more complex than what the geological model 
described. For instance, the shallow aquifer may have a spatial variation of 
hydraulic conductivity and fractures may exist at the clays contacted to the 
shallow aquifer. 
6.2. 
Risk Assessment in Deep Aquifer 
Deep aquifer has not been contaminated so far, but situation may change as more 
and more contaminants enter deep aquifer in the near future. Therefore, the future 
risk is assessed for a worst case considering the waterworks well 109.214 as the 
==PAGE:80==

receptor. The risk is evaluated by comparing the predicted concentration 
abstracted by waterworks with EU drinking water criteria. The concentrations of 
chloride and 4-CPP abstracted by well 109.214 are calculated based on the mass 
discharge and pumping rate. The values are shown in Table 6-1 along with the EU 
drinking water criteria. 
For chloride, the calculated concentration in well 109.214 is 14.66 mg/L. Taking 
into account the background concentration (30-70 mg/L in Danish groundwater), 
chloride in the water abstracted by waterworks is still lower than the criterion. 
Therefore, it is considered to be safe. 
For 4-CPP, the calculated concentration abstracted by the well is 3.00 ug/L, which 
is 30 times of the criterion threshold, indicating a risk of 4-CPP. However, this 
simulation is based on assumptions for the worst case, such as the whole landfill 
area as the source area, the highest concentration as the continuous source 
concentration, and no degradation. If using a better source term, and considering 
sorption and degradation, the results could be better. Further investigation on mass 
discharge and fate of 4-CPP is needed.

7. Conclusion 
The overall objective is to identify the risk from Pillemark landfill leachate to a 
shallow aquifer and a deep aquifer on Samso island. The study site has a complex 
geology consisting of a deep sandy aquifer, protected by a clay till. A remediation 
pumping system is collecting leachate in the shallow aquifer under the landfill.  
Based on interpretation of the chemical data, the shallow aquifer is contaminated with 
inorganic compounds and xenobiotic organic compounds. The most critical 
contaminants 
are 
phenoxy 
acids 
4-CPP 
and 
MCPP, 
the 
metabolite 
4-chloro-2-methylphenol. The zone under the southern part of landfill has higher 
concentration of contaminants than the northern part of landfill, because southern end 
majorly is deposited with organic waste while the northern end of landfill is deposited 
with inorganic waste, and the remediation system is centralizing the contaminants 
under the southern part of the landfill. There is a degradation of MCPP to 
4-chloro-2-methylphenol in shallow aquifer. In deep aquifer, the concentration of 
chemicals are overall in a low level and below the drinking water criteria thresholds. 
The 3D flow model provides a simulation of flow fields in two aquifers, which support 
for the chemical transport simulation. In shallow aquifer, water flows towards the 
central well in horizontal direction. In vertical direction, water flows downwards, and a 
part of water will be abstracted into remediation well, while the rest will furtherly go 
down and enter deep aquifer. In deep aquifer, flow direction is towards southwest. Well 
109.214 will capture water from the zone under the landfill. However, due to uncertain 
hydraulic conductivities and recharge, as well as the lack of observed data for 
calibration, the simulation has uncertainty. Besides, the remediation system in the 
model haven’t consider the four drains connected to the remediation well, which may 
create discrepancy from the flow field in reality.  
A vertical transport simulation at the beginning of this project found that chloride and 
4-CPP are most critical chemicals, and concentrations of chloride and 4-CPP at the 
bottom of clay can be larger than criteria within 50 years after released. Full scale 
simulations for these two compounds indicate that the maximum concentration of 
chloride in the waterworks is below the drinking water criterion, however, 4-CPP 
concentration will be higher than criterion.  
Based on all the observed and simulated results, it can be concluded that shallow 
aquifer is in risk with high concentrations of contaminants, but the contamination will 
not furtherly spread horizontally. The deep aquifer has no risk so far, however, in the 
future, 4-CPP could be a threat to the water supply well.  
Future work can be started with chemical data collection. More hydraulic heads 
observed from the area outside the landfill can provide a better understanding of 
==PAGE:83==
83 
 
hydrogeology. Hydraulic conductivity of clay is a key parameter that draws large 
uncertainty, thus measurement of hydraulic conductivities of clays is also necessary. 
Besides, estimations of net infiltration in modelling area and chemical mass discharge 
from shallow aquifer under the landfill are helpful for flow field simulation and 
chemical transport simulation. Boundary conditions in 3D flow model can also be 
improved from a well-fitted source. Though the simulations in this study have high 
uncertainty, the approach for assessing risk in this geology has been set up, and more 
reliable results can be obtained if using more solid input parameters.

Appendix 1. The larger flow model 
In my pre-project of a master thesis, a flow model was built with COMSOL 
Multiphysics to perform simulations on hydrology, which is for a larger scale than the 
model in my master thesis. In this report, this model is referred as ‘the larger flow 
model’. 
The larger flow model was built for a 5.12 km2 area including the landfill and 
Hårmark waterworks, with a length of 3.2 km and a width of 1.6 km. The area is 
marked with red rectangle in Figure 1. The model contains three layers: the top san 
layer, the clay till and the deep sand layer. The elevation of each surfaces (terrain, 
bottom of top sand layer, bottom of clay layer and bottom of deep sand layer) were 
export from the geological model and import into COMSOL with interpolation 
functions, and parametric surfaces were built for each layers to generate the geometry. 
Several points of observed heads were selected from a presentation about Pillemark 
fields, and import into COMSOL with an interpolation function. The vertical 
boundaries were set as fixed head condition (dirichlet boundary condition), and the 
value of h was written with this interpolated function. Top surface was set as a 
flux/source boundary, with a recharge of 0.48 mm/day. The bottom surface was a zero 
flux boundary. 
 
Figure 1. The location of Pillemark landfill (yellow delineated area) and Hårmark waterworks 
(blue dots outside the landfill). 
==PAGE:94==
94 
 
 
Figure 2. The geometry of the model. The layers from bottom to top are deep sand layer, clay till 
and top sand layer. 
 
Hydraulic conductivities were estimated based on grain size analysis of soil samples 
collect from borehole B302 (109.297) and B305 (109.299), with an Excel based tool 
HydrogeoSieveXL (Devlin, 2015). However, the results for clay and deep sand layer 
are not reliable because the grain size analysis was only done for size classes larger than 
0.063 mm, which makes an incomplete grain size dataset for simulation. In this model, 
for upper sand layer, the K value estimated based on grain size distribution was used, 
which is 1.33E-4 m/s; for the deep sand, start with the estimated value 2.5E-4 m/s, 
and adjust in calibration. A value of 1E-8 m/s was used as a start for clay layer and 
adjusted until the simulated head difference between two aquifers fitting well with the 
observed value. After calibration, the K value of clay layer is 1.60E-8 m/s and the K 
value of deep sand layer is 1.20E-4 m/s. 
The output from the model suggests the catchment zones of well 109.178 and 109.214 
contains a part of the landfill, and the flow lines from the landfill show that water 
around the west end of landfill will reach in deep aquifer and be abstracted by well 
109.178. The results indicate that the landfill is hydraulically connected with the 
waterworks well 109.178 and 109.214. However, it is not sufficient to conclude that 
there is a risk from landfill to the waterworks, because the model was built without 
the geological setting of the landfill which is supposed to have another hydraulic 
conductivity value, and it is assumed a homogenous sandy layer overlying the clay 
==PAGE:95==

layer but according to the local geological model, the top layer has a heterogeneous 
geological setting.

Appendix 5. Relation between pumping and hydraulic head in two aquifers 
The seasonal variation of hydraulic heads in two aquifers is considered to be relative 
to the pumping works from remediation well which pump water from top aquifer, as 
well as the waterworks which abstract water from deep aquifer. Head data in two 
aquifer from wells near the landfill were collected to estimate the relation between 
pumping rates and head in two aquifer. Figure 11 shows the variation of head in top 
aquifer along with pumping volume of remediation well, which shows the head got 
lower during the periods with more water pumped out. The head data from well 
109.292 is most relative to the pumping trend, because well 109.292 is closest to the 
remediation well. The pumping rate of the remediation is not so high that can cause a 
widely impact on hydraulic head in upper aquifer. Figure 12 shows the head in deep 
aquifer with pumping volume of waterworks. When more water was pumped out, the 
head decreased, and data from all the three wells meet this tendency though 
waterworks are far from these monitoring wells, since the pumping rates of 
waterworks are very high and effect on the hydrogeology in deep aquifer around 
landfill area.  
The difference of head in two aquifer were calculated and plotted together with the 
pumping volumes in Figure 13 from which the larger values of Δh occur when 
pumping more water from waterworks and less water from remediation well. Since 
pumping water from waterworks will decrease the head in deep aquifer and pumping 
from remediation well will lower the head from top 
aquifer, the results make sense. Figure 14 also shows 
a general tendency that when the pumping rate of 
remediation well increases, Δh decreases, and Δh 
increases when pumping rate of waterworks gets 
higher. However, the tendency is not significant with 
respect to pumping volume of remediation well, as 
the R2 is only 0.06. The correlation with respect to 
pumping volume of waterworks is better, which 
indicates that the head difference variation is more 
relative to pumping from waterworks. This result 
make sense because the pumping rate of waterworks 
is much higher than the remediation well.

Appendix 6. Field trip for slug tests  
On March 20th and 21st, I went to Samsø with technician Majken Frederiksen for slug 
tests. Tests were expected to be performed in as many wells as possible in concerning 
area, in order to measure K values for different soil types and investigate the spatial 
variation of K value of the same soil type. Figure 15 and Table 2 show all the wells 
that were possible to perform slug tests, and they were divided into three priority 
classes: the first prior wells which were perfect for slug tests and must be done; 
second ones screened across mixed soil; thirds ones that lacked information of screen 
length and depth which are necessary for calculating K value.  
Among the top prior wells, 109.249 and 109.250 are pumping wells with very large 
diameter which are impossible for slug test. 109.273 has three screens, as shown in 
Figure 16, but the lid of screen 2 could not be removed, therefore we didn’t do slug 
test for 109.273-2. Well 109.260 is located in a swamp as shown in the map, and we 
didn’t found it. Except for these wells, we did slug tests for all the rest in top priority. 
As we didn’t have enough time to test for all the planning wells, the second prior 
wells were discarded, because these wells have screens across more than one soil 
types, so K values measured from these wells cannot be related to any soil type. 
Besides, they are all screened at a part of clayey till, which is low permeable and it 
will take very long time for water level recovery during slug tests. Therefore, we 
chose the third prior wells instead. 
Among the third prior wells, except for the pumping well 109.292, slug tests were 
performed for all of the other wells. Some of these wells have a label on which there 
is information of screen top and bottom. Figure 17 shows an example of these labels. 
However, for well 109.281, 109.282, no label was found.  
As discussed in 4.2.2, slug tests were performed by introducing vacuum condition or 
slug-in water, which depended on the well property. The bladder used for vacuum test 
cannot fit the wells with too large or too small diameter, thus they were done by 
slug-in water. This happened to well 109.273-3, 109.271, and 109.240. 109.289 was 
also done by slug-in water, because it was the last one we did and after hardworking 
for the previous tests the generator cannot support the high load with vacuum machine 
on anymore. Except for these for wells, the others were done by vacuum tests. In each 
well, at least two replicate tests were performed. Figure 18 shows an example of test 
by slug-in water and an example of vacuum slug test.
