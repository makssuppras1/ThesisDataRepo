Abstract
Polyketides are an important group of natural products, as many of these compounds can be
of beneﬁt to humans as either medicines, food supplements/coloring or other purposes. Many
of these compounds originate from hard-to-grow organisms, which increases the need for het-
erologous hosts to facilitate production. In this project, The programmability of a biosynthesis
platform in yeast for production of natural products, using type III polyketide synthase from
plants coupled with type II cyclase/aromatases from bacteria, is explored.
First, the possibility of modifying the polyketide chain length formed by AaOKS in vivo was
investigated, by examining a series of previously in vitro tested mutations when expressed in
the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae.
In the current study, AaOKS with the double N222G+F66L substitutions forms the decaketide
shunt product SEK15 in high ratios in vivo. Contrary to previous studies in vitro, no dodecake-
tide products are detected in the current study in vivo.
Then, the prospect of controlling the ﬁrst ring folding of the polyketide, produced by AaOKS,
the activity of twelve selected cyclases/aromatases from type II PKS systems is investigated.
HPLC-MS analysis reveals that the cyclase/aromatase PdmD causes formation of the C9-C14
ﬁrst ring folded compound UWM1, which is not a detected product of any other tested cyclase,
and ZhuI is demonstrated to catalyse C7-C12 ﬁrst ring folding. Conﬁrming previous observa-
tions reported in literature. The cyclases/aromatases PdmD, TcmN, WhiE-ORFIV and FkmC1
are all found to catalyse the formation of the same unidentiﬁed compound with the theoret-
ical formula C16H10O7, thought to be cyclarized following S-mode C9-C14 ﬁrst ring folding,
based on the previously described natural function of these four cyclases. No detectable activity
is observed from the cyclase/aromatase domains MtmQ, OxyK, NovD-A, NovD-D, NovD-E,
NovP-A and NovP-B in vivo, when presented the octaketide scaffold produced by AaOKS.
The ﬁndings presented in this project extends the chemical space reachable by a programmable
yeast expression platform based on type III polyketide synthases, by introducing the decaketide
scaffold produced by AaOKS with the double N222G+F66L substitutions. Furthermore, if the
structure of the unidentiﬁed compound produced by PdmD, TcmN, WhiE-ORFIV and FkmC1
is conﬁrmed to be C9-C14 folded as hypothesised, it will demonstrate high predictability of the
C9-C14 folding cyclases, making a programmable yeast biosynthesis platform for the formation
of natural products achievable.

Background
2.1
History of Natural Product Discovery
Microbial interactions has evolved in isolated and global niches for millions of years, giving
rise to a large array of bioactive compounds known as secondary metabolites(SM’s). Since they
have evolved to suit some biological purpose, and therefore have a structure likely to ﬁt into
receptors, or affect other biological systems, many of these metabolites are of great interest to
humans as medicines, antimicrobials, pigments and ﬂavourings.[1][2][3]
Bioactive compounds found in nature is popularly called "natural products", and the search for
natural products has been ongoing for more than 200 years, marked by the ﬁrst isolate of mor-
phine from opium in 1805, providing humanity with a potent pain reliever, which is still in use
to this day.[4]
Since then, a lot has happened in the ﬁeld of organic chemistry, and there are now numerous
alternative methods to discover natural products, and even ways of designing new ones.
There was the classical approach of ﬁnding a plant with known bioactivity, and distilling the
active ingredient from biomass, such as with morphine.[4] Then came the approach, of phe-
notypic screening of thousands of compounds from groups of microorganisms and testing their
individual effects, as was the standard procedure of antimicrobial discovery in the group headed
by Waksman and Schats following 1940.[5][6][7]
Several other methods have been developed, based on phenotypic screening, as genetic engi-
neering became an increasingly available method.[7] One such method is what has become
known as combinatorial biosynthesis, where genes related to different SM’s are combined in
one organism to yield a new SM.[8][9]
This brings us to the last couple of decades, where genomics, proteomics, metabolomics,
and several other omics, has granted us a way of identifying secondary metabolite gene clus-
ters(SMGC’s), SM producing enzymes, and important precursors, before we ﬁnd the related
compounds.[7]
This has led to a large array of approaches based on activating these unknown SMGC’s in
the original organisms. There are the pleiotropic approaches such as the "One Strain Many
Compounds" approach or "OSMAC", where a single strain is analysed for metabolite expres-
sion during different culturing conditions, such as aeration, media composition, temperature or
some stressing factors, like metabolites from other organisms or co-culturing.[10][11][12][13]
Then there are the approaches speciﬁc to sets of biosynthetic gene clusters, perhaps the most
well-known is heterologous expression of the entire SMGC of interest in well-deﬁned organ-
isms with activated promoters, which allows observation of both the resulting compound and
intermediates.[14][15][16] This approach is very costly, but the beneﬁts are also many, as this
approach has been further developed, drawing inspiration from the combinatorial biosynthesis
approach to combine parts of separate SMGCs in a single organism to discover new to nature
compounds.[17][18]

2.2
Polyketide Synthases
Polyketides, one of the four main groups of SM’s, contribute to many natural products used
by humans,[19] and in the last couple of decades, our understanding of the polyketide syn-
thases(PKS’s), the enzymes responsible for their production, have increased dramatically.[20]
There are three main types of PKS’s, type I, II, and III, each associated with a speciﬁc set
of organisms and modes of action. Formation of polyketides is in its core similar in all three
types, and is based on loading of a starter molecule, such as acetyl-CoA, followed by sequen-
tial addition of malonyl-CoA or similar substrates derived from malonyl-CoA to yield a longer
polyketide chain, which can then be folded and modiﬁed in a diverse manner of ways.
2.2.1
Type I PKS Systems
Type I PKS’s are characterised as single proteins responsible for the entire production of spe-
ciﬁc polyketides.[21] These PKS’s contain several domains responsible for speciﬁc reactions,
ketosynthase(KS) modules elongates the chain, acyltransferase domains(AT) selects the ketide,
and acyl-carrier protein(ACP) is responsible for holding the growing polyketide chain, and al-
lowing the different domains to perform their modiﬁcations.[21] Speciﬁc to the type I PKS’s is
that reductions of the polyketide can occur while the chain is being synthesised. The domains
responsible for reductions are the keto reductase(KR) domains, dehydratase(DH) domains and
enoyl reductase(ER) domains, catalysing the reactions from keto-group to alcohol, alcohol to
double bond, and double bond to single bond respectively. Once the PKS product is formed, it
is unloaded from ACP, during which the product can be cyclised.[21] Type I PKS’s are subdi-
vided in two groups; iterative and modular PKS’s.
The type I iterative PKS’s are typically found in fungi, and can be subdivided further to re-
ducing and non-reducing iterative PKS’s. Both are characterised by having only one of each
domain, which it then uses repeatedly.[22] The reducing PKS’s contains some of the reducing
domains, resulting in reduced polyketide product, which then cyclise based on the pattern of re-
duction, typically as linear products or macrolides.[23] The non-reducing PKS’s does not have
any of the reducing domains, and therefore produce a full chain of β-ketones, which is then
typically folded in a product template (PT) domain.[23] The catalysed folding pattern is termed
F-mode, short for fungal-mode. F-mode folding is characterised by ﬁrst ring bond formation
between an even numbered carbon and a higher odd numbered carbon, such as C2-C7.[23][22]
The type I modular PKS’s differ from the iterative PKS’s in a couple of signiﬁcant ways.
These PKS’s are typically associated with bacteria, and instead of having a single version of the
KS, AT, and ACP domains, the modular PKS’s have several, and these are only used once pr.
produced polyketide, therefore they are often refered to as molecular assembly lines.[21] The
folding pattern also differs, as unloading is typically facilitated by a thioesterase(TE) domain,
and the cyclisation often results in the formation of macrolides.[24]

2.2.2
Type II PKS Systems
These PKS’s originate mainly from bacteria, and especially actinobacteria.[25] The mode of
action, is based upon the production of a polyketide chain in the enzyme complex known as the
minimal PKS, consisting of a KS domain and a domain named the chain length factor(CLF)
in a heterodimer,(sometimes referred to as KSα and KSβ respectively) and the ACP.[25] The
non-reduced polyketide chains formed by type II PKS are then subsequently folded in a speciﬁc
shape by other separate enzymes named cyclases and aromatases (for simplicity, they are just
called cyclases throughout this project).[26] The folding pattern is notably different from type I
PKS systems, as the type II cyclases form ﬁrst ring cyclisation with a low odd numbered carbon
and a higher even numbered carbon, such as C7-C12. This folding pattern has been termed
S-mode folding, short for streptomyces-mode folding. It is named so because streptomyces is
the largest genus of actinobacteria and many type II PKS systems have been isolated from this
genus.[25][26][27]
2.2.3
Type III PKS Systems
Type III PKS’s are in many ways simpler than their type I and II counterparts.[28][29] They con-
sist of two identical KS domains forming a homo-dimer and usually accepts p-coumaroyl-CoA,
acetyl-CoA or malonyl-CoA as starter units and iteratively extends the chain using malonyl-
CoA.[28][30] Their products are completely unreduced as a result of the lack of KR, DH, and
ER domains, and they have been found in both bacteria, fungi and plants[28]. Despite their
simple nature, they have been tied to the production of precursors to an extensive amount of
natural products.[29][30] However, the longest natural product chain length of type III PKS’s
observed to date contains 16 carbon atoms,[30] whereas the products of type I and II PKS sys-
tems can be as long as 38 and 30 carbon atoms respectively,[31][32] suggesting that type III
PKS’s generally produce shorter polyketides than their type I and II counterparts.

2.3.3
Type III
The combination of relatively simple protein structures, and wide range of products has made
these enzymes very popular in heterologous expression experiments, and indeed, several type
III PKS’s has been successfully expressed in heterologous hosts.[45][46][47] The octaketide
synthase from Aloe arborescens (AaOKS) in particular has in recent years received a lot of at-
tention, as it produces the longest polyketide observed so far from type III PKS’s. It naturally
catalyses the formation of an octaketide (16 carbon atoms) through the sequential condensation
of eight malonyl-CoA units.
Following its ﬁrst characterisation by I. Abe et al.(2005)[30], some important developments of
AaOKS has been reported. Y. Mizuuchi et al.(2009)[29] and K. Wanibuchi et al.(2011)[48] re-
ports a rational approach to increasing the active site cavity volume of the AaOKS by designed
amino acid substitutions. Their ﬁndings show that the substitution N222G, leads to production
of a longer polyketide chain, as they detect formation of the C20 decaketide SEK15 in in vitro
studies. They further expand on this ﬁnding by subsequent substitution of F66L, a position
deeper in the cavity, predicted to further increase space for a growing polyketide. They found
that combination of F66L and N222G substitutions in AaOKS results in the production of the
C24 dodecaketide, TW95a.
These ﬁndings are very exciting, as it is the ﬁrst example of a type III PKS capable of producing
polyketides longer than C16.
There is however a caveat to this PKS, as the reported products SEK4 and SEK4B from the
wildtype AaOKS, are not the only products of this enzyme. Premature release of the growing
polyketide results in production of smaller C12 and C14 polyketides when heterologously ex-
pressed, and SEK4 and SEK4B are also produced by the decaketide and dodekaketide producing
versions in vitro.[35][29][48] SEK4 and SEK4B are the same reported shunt products of some
minimal PKS’s from type II PKS systems, such as the actinorhodin minimal PKS[18][28][49].

This suggests that the AaOKS enzyme produces polyketide scaffolds with sizes and shapes sim-

could catalyse folding patterns of the AaOKS products. Indeed several of such combinations has
recently been reported successful; B. Møller et al.(2016)[50], R. J. N. Frandsen et al.(2018)[51]

ural activity of the cyclases, which demonstrated that the AaOKS produced polyketide scaffold
can be used as starting point for many other programmable biosynthetic pathways.
2.4
Octa-, Deca-, and Dodecaketide Products of Type II and Type III PKS
Systems
The reported octaketides produced by AaOKS can be grouped according to their ﬁrst ring fold-
ing pattern. The C7-C12 ﬁrst ring folding results in the compounds; SEK4(2), dSEK4(3),
ﬂavokermisic acid anthrone(4), and ﬂavokermisic acid(FK)(5). The C10-C15 ﬁrst ring folding

2.5
HPLC-MS Based Identiﬁcation of Compounds
Polyketide products can be very similar in composition and structure, and without the right
equipment, it is difﬁcult to separate them from each other.
High performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) coupled with mass spectrometry (MS) is a
very popular approach to identifying individual polyketide products in a mixture.
HPLC is used to separate compounds in a mixture based on relative interactions with a liquid
mobile phase and interactions with a solid stationary phase. For separation of polyketides, re-
verse phase chromatography (RPC) is the most common choice.[57] In RPC, the mobile phase
is typically a changing gradient of an organic solvent mixed with water and the stationary phase
is typically a tightly packed bed of silica particles, with some form of non-polar surface treat-
ment.[35][48][51][57]
MS is used to measure the mass to charge ratio (m/z) of ionisable compounds in a mixture, and
provides a count of how many times different m/z were detected.[58] Depending on the mode of
ionisation, (negative or positive charge) and the availability of possible acids/bases/salts in the
solution, several adducts of compounds can be observed. An adduct can be described as a com-
pound which have been ionised in a speciﬁc way. A good example could be that during negative
ionisation, a common adduct of a compound would be proton loss, resulting in the measured
m/z corresponding to the monoisotopic mass of the compound subtracted a single proton, or
[M-H] for short. These adducts are all found in the MS spectrum as deﬁned differences from
the monoisotopic mass(M) of a compound.[58][59].
In addition to MS, some instruments allow tandem MS analysis or MS/MS analysis, which
fragments selected ions and measures the m/z of the resulting fragment, which can be used to
infer structural data of the detected ion.[60]
An extra dimension can be added to the data, by coupling HPLC and MS. Through HPLC-MS it
is possible to continuously measure the MS spectrum of the efﬂuent of the HPLC, as it operates,
thereby coupling retention time to the m/z ratios observed at different time points.[61]
HPLC-MS data is typically presented as chromatograms, the three most commonly used types
of chromatograms for HPLC-MS are;
• Total ion chromatograms (TIC), which presents the entire detected count of ions over the
retention time.
• Base peak chromatograms (BPC), which displays the count of the most detected ion at
the given time, over retention time.
• Extracted ion chromatograms (EIC), which shows only the counts of selected ions in a
chromatogram over retention time

HPLC-MS data can be analysed in several ways, one approach is the untargeted metabolomics
analysis, which is based around searching through the entire dataset for potential compounds,
combining both retention time, inferred monoisotopic mass from identiﬁed adducts, and the
MS/MS spectrum to detect possible compounds in the sample. This approach requires no pre-
vious knowledge of a sample or the system it originated from, as the analysis is carried out on
the entire dataset.[60]
A targeted metabolomics analysis, is like a ﬂipped untargeted analysis, where one starts with
a "target" compound (or several), with a known monoisotopic mass and calculated masses of
adducts, and then extracting only the data corresponding to these m/z, thereby asking the ques-
tions "Is this compound present?" and "How much of this compound is present compared to
another?".[62] This approach requires a lot of knowledge of the system, as one has to hypothe-
sise that speciﬁc compounds could be present to actually look for it. Some information on the
compounds in question is also needed, as there typically exist many isomers of a compound,
making speciﬁc identiﬁcation difﬁcult unless some information, like relative retention time of
some compounds, or precise light absorbtion spectra are known in advance.[62][35]
There are some caveats to HPLC-MS based metabolomics, which originate in the way HPLC
and MS works. Some compounds bind much stronger to the stationary phase during HPLC
than others, and while this is great for separating a lot of compounds, it is a problem if some
compounds remain bound in the column until it is purged with pure solvent, as they will not
be detected in this case. The exact opposite case where some compounds cannot bind to the
stationary phase at all is also a problem as they will all elute immediately and be impossible to
tell apart.
The MS adds to the list of undetected compounds, as compounds ionise in different ways at
different conditions, making it impossible to ionise all compounds in a complex sample using
a single method of ionisation.[63] This problem extends to the compounds that are detected,
as the speciﬁc ionisation efﬁciency is different between compounds, meaning that two com-
pounds with the same concentration in a sample can be counted differently in the MS, making
HPLC-MS based quantiﬁcation impossible without using pure compound standards as refer-
ence.[64][63][35]

2.7
A Programmable Platform for Formation of Aromatic Compounds
Programmability is deﬁned as "The capability within hardware and software to change; to ac-
cept a new set of instructions that alter its behavior"(PC Magasine). A parallel can be drawn
to the work presented by Kromphardt[35], who investigated the possibility of a programmable
platform for production of aromatic polyketides in S. cerevisiae, by using AaOKS to produce
the polyketide scaffold, and cyclases to direct folding. In this system, the hardware and soft-
ware is exchanged with enzymes, speciﬁcally AaOKS and cyclases, These enzymes must be
able to perform a predictable catalytic behavior, across many given scenarios for the platform
to be programmable.
One scenario is the changeability of polyketide length, as it would be ideal to be able to reach
several length of polyketide scaffolds, to reach a large chemical space with the programmable
platform. (Figure 2.changeable)
The results presented by K. Wanibuchi et al.(2011)[48] introduces this possibility as different
sizes of polyketide scaffolds could be achievable with the same enzyme, given a few adjust-
ments. However, K. Wanibuchi et al.(2011)[48], only presents data from in vitro analysis,
leaving a knowledge gap in regards to heterologous expression in S. cereviseae.
Another scenario is then presented, as changes in product length of AaOKS, results in changes
in substrate length for the cyclases. Therefore, cyclases must be able to accept different sizes
of substrates, and predictively catalyse the same cyclisation pattern across substrate lengths, for
the proposed platform to be programmable. (Figure 2.predictable)
The beneﬁt of such a programmable pathway, would be that a large chemical space could be
reachable via systematic combination of relatively few enzymes, and that biosynthetic pathways
could be constructed without ﬁrst having discovered the pathway in a natural host.[35]

and if the activity of a cyclase is predictable given different sizes of substrates.

2.8
Scope of This Project
This project is build upon testing of the following four linked hypotheses:
1. Introduction of the N222G and N222G+F66L mutations in AaOKS will lead to forma-
tion of decaketide and dodecaketide products respectively, when the encoding genes is
expressed in S. cerevisiae.
2. The characterized cylcases PdmD, TcmN, WhiE, FkmC1, ZhuI, MtmQ, and OxyK will
predictably catalyze either a C9-c14 or a C7-C12 ﬁrst ring cyclisation, similar to their
reported folding activity on natural substrates, when offered a non-reduced octaketide
chain.
3. The above listed cyclases will catalyse the same observed folding pattern on longer
polyketide chains.
4. The novel cyclases NovD-A -D -E and NovP-A -B, can be expressed in S. cerevisiae and
cause detectable formation of S-mode folded polyketides when offered a non-reduced
octaketide chain.

3.7
Uracil-Speciﬁc Excision Reagent(USER) Cloning and E. coli Trans-
formation
Ampliﬁed fragments were mixed in PCR tubes in a 1:3 molar ratio between backbone and
fragment respectively, with 100ng backbone. 0.5µl USERTM Enzyme from New England
Biolabs was added along with a 1X dilution of CutSMart buffer from New England Biolabs
in a total volume of 10µl. Each reaction mixture was subjected to following program in a
thermocycler: 37◦C for 35 min. 25◦C for 25 min. followed by cooling to 12◦C.
DH5α E. coli competent cells were thawed on ice for 10-20 min. following removal from -
80◦C storage. 30µl E. coli competent cells were carefully added to the PCR tube containing
ﬁnished USER cloning product, and the PCR tubes were immediately put on ice for 10 min.
Two negative controls, one with no PCR fragments added to the USER mix and one with just
the backbone fragment in a USER mix along with a positive control with a veriﬁed plasmid
were included in each batch of USER/transformation steps. Each tube was then subjected to
heat shock in a thermocycler at 42◦C for 90 sec. followed by 5 min. on ice. The contents of each
tube were then plated on LB-ampicilin agar plates and left to incubate overnight at 37◦C.
3.8
Plasmid Ampliﬁcation in E. coli
E. coli culture was innoculated from either glycerol stock or directly from veriﬁed colonies on
transformation plates to 2ml LB-b+ampicillin in 14ml pre-culture tubes, using a 10µl pipette.
The tubes were left for 8-18 hours at 37◦C and 180RPM to become saturated. Plasmid was
puriﬁed from the saturated culture using preps from a Sigma-aldrich GenEluteTM Plasmid
Miniprep Kit, following the standard protocol for high copy plasmid extraction, with the ex-
ception of an added 5 min. incubation at 60◦C with open lids before the addition of elution
buffer to completely evaporate any residual ethanol, and a 5 min. incubation at 60◦C after the
addition of elution buffer, to achieve higher yields. The concentration of DNA in the elute was
then measured in a NanoDropLite from ThermoScientiﬁc.

mixed with 0.5U Bpl1 and 1X cutsmart buffer from new england biolabs in 10µl total volume.
This mix was then incubated for 4 hours before running analytical gel electrophoresis.

3.12
Transformation of S. cereviseae
Strains of S. cereviseae were transformed using the Yeast expression platform developed by the
center for microbial biotechnology in collaboration with KU-life. The parent strain Cen.PK113.17A
was grown overnight in 14ml pre-culture tubes containing 3 ml YPD at 30◦C in a rotary shaker.
The saturated cultures were transfered to shake ﬂasks containing 50ml YPD, which were incu-
bated at 30C in a rotary shaker. After 3-5 hours, the cultures were transfered to 50ml falcon
tubes and then pelleted by centrifugation, and washed twice in 25ml MQ water. The pellets
were then resuspended to a ﬁnal volume of 1 ml. These cell suspensions were then equally
divided in 15 sterile 1.5ml eppendorf tubes pr. shake ﬂask, 75µl in each. The cells were then
pelleted and DNA was added directly to the pellet using a sterile pipette. for plasmid transfor-
mations, 100ng DNA was added and for chromosomal insertions 200-500ng was added. Strains
carrying both a plasmid and chromosomal integration were generated in a single transformation
by adding both linearised fragment and episomal plasmid. 360µl transformation mix was then
added to each tube, this was a mix of 100mM LiAc 33% sterile ﬁltered polyethylene glycol and
278ng/µl boiled ssDNA, followed by vortexing to resuspend the pellet. Each transformation
mix was then subjected to heat shock at 42C for 40 min before pelleting and resuspending in
100 µl MilliQ Water. This suspension was then plated on suitable synthetic complete(SC) drop-
out plates selecting for appropriate auxotrophies; leu, ura, or leu/ura and the plates were then
incubated at 30◦C for at least 40 hours before transformants were tested in colony PCR.

3.13
S. cereviseae Colony PCR
Two methods were used to prepare template for colony PCR of yeast:
1. Sterile toothpicks were used to transfer a small amount of colony from plates to 10µl
milliQ water in PCR tubes. 0.5µl of this simple cell suspension was then used in a PCR
reaction.
2. Sterile toothpicks were used to transfer a small amount of colony from plates to 100µl
200mM LiAc 1%SDS solution. This suspension was subjected to 5 min. at 70C before
addition of 300µl cold 96%EtOH and vortexing. DNA was pelleted 15000G for 3 min and
washed with cold 70%EtOH. The pellet was then dissolved in 100µl MQ water and cell
debris was pelleted. the DNA containing supernatant was then transferred to eppendorf-
tubes and 1µl of this DNA solution was used as template in PCR reactions.
Following template preparation, all yeast colony PCR reactions were as follows: Template was
added to two separate PCR reaction tubes with 10 pMol of forward and reverse primer, 250 µM
of each dNTP and 1U of X7 DNA polymerase in 1X Phire reaction buffer from thermoscientiﬁc.
One reaction tube contained primers designed to give a band of roughly 1600bp in the case of
wild type genotype at the XI-2 site (ID GLS243 and GLS244) and the other tube contained
primers designed to yield a roughly 1000bp band in case of correct integration at the XI-2
site (ID GLS243 and FH13 OR BGHA PAC1 R3). All reaction tubes were subjected to the
following PCR program: 98C for 3min, followed by 8 cycles of 98C - 20 sec, 63C(∆-1/cycle)
- 30 sec, 72C - 1 min:40 sec. then 35 cycles of 98C- 20 sec, 57C- 30 sec, 72C - 1 min:40 sec.
followed by 5 min at 72C and storage at 12C until gel electrophoresis.

3.15
Chemical Extraction
Final OD600 was measured in a Spectrophotometer, and the entire culture liquid was transferred
to 50ml Falcon tubes and centrifuged at 8000G for 5 min. The culture liquid was decanted to
a second 50ml falcon tube and stored at 5C until needed. 25mL extraction solvent (3:1 Ethy-
lacetate:isopropanol w. 1% formic acid) was added to the pelleted cells in the ﬁrst tube. These
tubes were then subjected to 60min of sonication with ultrasound to break the cell membranes.
20mL of the previously separated culture liquid was then added to the solvent, and the tubes
were shaken vigorously to quickly move organic compounds to the solvent phase. The water-
phase was then removed by repeated pipetting with a 2 ml glass Pasteur pipette. The tubes con-
taining the remaining extraction solvent was centrifuged at 8000G for 5 min, before the solvent
was decanted into the tube with the remaining culture liquid. After vigorous shaking, the water
phase was again removed, and the liquid-liquid extraction was complete. Following extraction,
the tubes containing the solvent, was placed overnight in a nitrogen evaporator to completely
evaporate the solvent, leaving the extracted compounds, dry in the bottom of the falcon-tubes.
The compounds were re-dissolved in 300µl methanol and the tubes were subjected to 10 min.
of sonication, before they were transferred to 2ml eppendorf tubes and centrifuged at maximum
tolerance 18000G for 5 min. 150µl of the extracted samples in methanol were then transferred
to HPLC vials and sent to the DTU Metabolomics Core for HPLC-MS and HPLC-MS/MS
analysis.
3.16
Ultrahigh-Performance Liquid Chromatography-High Resolution Elec-
trospray Ionization Mass Spectrometry (UHPLC-HRESIMS) Anal-
ysis
All UHPLC-HRESIMS data was acquired by metabolomics core members, and this described
method was provided by Mario Wibowo from the metabolomics core. All solvents and chemi-
cals used for HRMS and chromatography were VWR Chemicals LC-MS grade, while for fungal
cultures extraction, the solvents were of HPLC grade (VWR Chemicals). UHPLC-HRESIMS
(named HPLC-MS in this report for readability) was performed on an Agilent Inﬁnity 1290
UHPLC system equipped with a diode array detector (DAD). Liquid chromatography of 1 µL
extract was performed using an Agilent Poroshell 120 phenyl-hexyl column (2.1 × 150 mm,
1.9 µm) at 60 ◦C using acetonitrile (ACN) and H2O, both containing 20 mM FA, as mobile
phases. Initially, a linear gradient of 10% ACN/H2O to 100% ACN over 10 min was employed,
followed by isocratic elution of 100% ACN for 2 min, the gradient was returned to 10% AC-
N/H2O in 0.1 min, and ﬁnally isocratic condition of 10% ACN/H2O for 1.9 min, all at a ﬂow
rate of 0.35 mL/min. MS detection was performed in negative ionization on an Agilent 6545
QTOF MS equipped with an Agilent Dual Jet Stream electrospray ion source with a drying
gas temperature of 250 ◦C, drying gas ﬂow of 8 L/min, sheath gas temperature of 300 ◦C,
and sheath gasﬂow of 12 L/min. Capillary voltage was set to 4000 V and nozzle voltage to
500 V. MS data processing and analysis were performed using Agilent MassHunter Qualitative
Analysis B.07.00.

Untargeted Metabolomics Analysis
An untargeted metabolomics analysis was implemented to search for previously unidentiﬁed

by subtracting one from the other.

tide producing strains, or cyclase expressing strains, the m/z was noted, and examined in the
ms/ms spectrum and searched for on the "Chemcalc"[71] website, to hypothesise a molecular
formula.

Plasmid Veriﬁcation via Sanger Sequencing
Alignment of the sequencing results and plasmid maps constructed in benchling, revealed if an
insert was present in the sequenced plasmid and if the sequence was free of mutations, two such
examples are shown in Figure 24.A-B. In some cases, the codon optimised sequence was un-
available in silico, as this optimisation was done years ago. This resulted in a lot of mismatches
in the third amino acid of many codons when performing a sequence alignment. This problem
was solved by translating the nt-sequence to corresponding amino acids to make sure that no
actual mutations were hiding among the many mismatches. An example of this is shown in Fig-
ure 24.C, where it is observed that all in view translations are the same as the template, except
for amino acids after 107, from the forward sequencing read(middle sequence), which looks
like a frameshift caused by a single "A" insertion. In cases like this, where the two sequenc-
ing results disagree, the sequencing chromatogram was used to determine which read seemed
the most reliable. In this particular case, the peaks are much more deﬁned in this part of the
reverse sequencing result(bottom sequence), making this the most reliable read. The pre-made
plasmids p6-p12 contained functional inserts, whereas the pre-made versions of p2, p3, p4, p5,
and p13 were all backbone plasmids with no inserts.
All subsequently constructed plasmids were analysed in the same way and was found to con-
tain no mutations other than nucleotide mismatches due to codon-optimisation. A section of
the TcmN gene in p9 still remains to be sequenced. p6 and p12 were sequenced in previous
work. All remaining alignments of veriﬁed plasmids are available in the appendix (Figures 24

PCR Ampliﬁcation
BB 1, 2, 4, 5, and 6 were all succesfully ampliﬁed in the ﬁrst attempt using an unmodiﬁed
standard PCR protocol used in the BPE group at DTU. The remaining fragments were only ob-

The backbone fragments BB3 and BB11 were needed in high concentrations to allow small vol-

fragment and the corresponding plasmid p16, and strains ST61 and ST62 were discontinued

Discussion
Polyketide products were detected from all strains carrying AaOKS expression, and not from
any of the strains without AaOKS expression, which explains the systematic color difference
observed on plates, in liquid cultures and in extracts with strains expressing AaOKS, as some
polyketides, like ﬂavokermisic acid, are known to absorb light in the visible spectrum.[73]
The in vivo expression of AaOKS with N222G/F66L substitutions presented in this work con-
tradicts the observations of Wanibuchi et al.(2011)[48], as TW95A, the dodecaketide previously
detected in vitro, was not formed in vivo, and no other dodecaketide products were detected.
There is a fundamental difference to the analysis of enzymes in vitro and in vivo. In vitro, one
can optimise the pH to stabilise the active enzyme conformation, and substrates can be in a
very high surplus. In vivo, all parameters are restricted by the heterologous host, so pH and
temperature can be sub-optimal, and the substrates are usually more scarce as they are typically
used by other systems in the cell. In this case, the AaOKS optimum pH was found to be 6.5
and the used buffer was based on potassium phosphate with 1mM EDTA[48]. The pH differs
slightly from the expected range of intracellular pH 4.5-6.6 within S. cerevisiae during batch
fermentation[74]. This difference is very subtle, but as the structure of AaOKS is relatively
small compared to the size of the engineered cavity (748Å3), subtle differences in pH could
potentially have large impacts on structural stability. Combining this with the missing stabiliser
EDTA could potentially mean that the engineered cavity could partially collapse in vivo. How-
ever, the enzyme was still found to produce smaller polyketides, e.g. the decaketide SEK15, so
complete structural collapse seems highly unlikely.
Another, more plausible cause of the lacking dodecaketide, could be based on the observa-
tion that wild type AaOKS naturally produces smaller polyketide products, possibly due to an
instability in product binding[29][35]. It is reasonable that a combination of lesser substrate
availability and less than optimal pH, increases the elongation time of the growing polyketide
product, which increases the "chance" for the growing product to release from the enzyme be-
fore reaching 12 elongations. if this chance is high enough, no dodecaketides will be produced,
simply because of enzyme kinetics. The same explanation could also be applied to the low ratio
of decaketide product observed from the N222G substitution.
A way to test this explanation would be to simulate the internal conditions of yeast in vitro, with
surplus of substrate, and see if the dodecaketide is observed.
If this is indeed the case, future work could be targeted at increasing the availability of the sub-
strate malonyl-CoA in vivo, by metabolic engineering approaches as described by B. Kozak et
al (2014)[75]. Another approach could be to decrease the leakiness of the enzyme, however,
this likely requires the use of high throughput techniques to screen many mutations of AaOKS,
as it is still unclear what determines leakiness in the enzyme.
The observation that the F66L substitution results in formation of SEK15 in vivo is very sur-
prising, as this position is much "deeper" inside the protein than what is considered the edge
of the product cavity of the wild type AaOKS. It must mean that unknown amino acid residues
that make up the edge of the product cavity fold back to ﬁll the empty space left by the F to L

Separation of dSEK4 and dSEK4B during chromatographic analysis is paramount for future
endeavours involving the AaOKS scaffold, as their forced grouping has resulted in a fraction
larger than 25% of the total polyketide products, whose folding pattern could be either C10-C15
or C7-C12.
Since the dehydroform peak contains a plateau, there must be a slight difference in RT, it is
just close enough that the peaks overlap. By changing the gradient elution proﬁle, it should
therefore be possible to separate these peaks further, and possibly create two separate peaks.
A good place to start would be to halt the linear gradient increase of solvent in the mobile
phase, one minute before the usual time of dehydroform elution at around 4-4.5 min, and then
slowly increasing the gradient in small steps, perhaps monitoring for the dehydroform peaks
simultaneously to build the protocol.[57]
Additional information could be gained by also changing the MS conﬁguration to use positive
ionisation instead of the negative ionisation used in this project, as this would facilitate detection
of several other compound adducts.
The double mutant AaOKS(N222G/F66L) in st5, produced a much higher ratio of SEK15, than
both single mutants, with a SEK15 ratio more than 6 times higher than that of st3-AaOKS(N222G).
This observation makes the double substitution a more suitable candidate for future experiments
involving decaketide scaffolds.
This development is exciting, as many of the cyclases tested in this work on octaketides are
known to effectively catalyse cyclisations of decaketide substrates, which calls for another

CPD16 is only an observed product of C9-C14 folding cyclases(PdmD, TcmN, WhiE, and
FkmC1), which suggests that CPD16 might be a C9-C14 ﬁrst ring folded compound. This fold-
ing restricts possible ﬁnal conformations of the compound, and given the theoretical formula
C16H10O7 it was possible to rationalise a chemical pathway and a possible structure of CPD16
from the C9-C14 folded polyketide scaffold.
Following C9-C14 cyclisation, a subsequent spontaneous second ring formation of C6-C15
could occur, followed by third ring formation of C2-C7. Finally, a spontaneous carbonyl ad-
dition at C8 might be possible. This pathway and structure is inspired by the spontaneous
reactions observed in the formation of ﬂavokermesic acid, which also involves two spontaneous
ring formations and a carbonyl addition (Figure 21).
This is very exciting as this means another polyketide scaffold has been added to the chemical
space reachable by combination of type III PKS’s with type II PKS cyclase domains.
This proposed pathway and structure (Figure 21) is highly speculative, as it is based solely on

the assumption that the cyclases catalyse formation of a single ring at C9-C14. If this holds
true, it demonstrates a high predictability of the C9-C14 folding cyclases when the chain length
of the substrate is changed, which will help in development of a programmable platform. Struc-

Assuming that CPD16 is C9-C14 cyclised, it was found that all four tested C9-C14 folding
cyclases (PdmD, TcmN, WhiE, and FkmC1) can catalyse ﬁrst ring formation of the AaOKS
scaffold in vivo, in easily detectable amounts. This in itself is interesting as it means that these
enzymes do not need to perform the second ring C7-C16 cyclisation observed on longer polyke-
tides to form the ﬁrst C9-C14 ring. The ratio of this formation is however very small compared
to that of spontaneously forming products, making up only 2-5% of the total detected polyke-
tide products, which casts doubt on the viability of this combination. There are however many
available techniques for increasing the activity of an enzymatic step.
One approach is to increase the expression of the cyclase via introduction of multiple copies to
the genome. Another approach could be to localise the pathway within a cellular compartment
to increase local concentration of substrate. And ﬁnally protein engineering can be attempted.
Many examples exist, where some combination of these approaches have removed a bottle-
neck in a pathway by signiﬁcantly increasing enzymatic activities in vivo, meaning that even
this small observable activity can be a ﬁne starting point for future pathways to natural prod-
ucts.[80][81][82][83][84]
A short comment is due for data gathered from the TcmN cyclase expressing strains, as the
plasmid used as vector for the integrated fragment was only partly sequenced. Around 500
nucleotides close to the N terminal of TcmN are unveriﬁed, meaning that the cyclase activity
attributed to this cyclase is only completely validated once sequencing of this section has been
performed.
Since activity is observed, no mutations are expected, but if there are mutations, it just means
that TcmN works in vivo in S. cerevisiae with that given mutation.
In addition, it is impossible to know if any of the integrated fragments were integrated with-
out mutations until the genomic site of the strains have been sequenced, however, the cPCR
validation conﬁrms that an integration did occur.
The ﬁndings that ZhuI and PdmD both catalyse ﬁrst ring cyclisation of an octaketide scaffold
conﬁrms the ﬁndings presented by Kromphardt[35], albeit the ratios are not nearly as high as
previously described.
Kromphardt found that ZhuI catalysed close to 100% conversion to C7-C12 folding while
PdmD catalysed around 23% conversion to C9-C14.
It is possible that a separation of dSEK4 and dSEK4B could reveal a lot more C7-C12 folding
activity, making ZhuI activity comparable to previous results by Kromphardt.

2. The yeast strain CEN.PK113.17A was used in this work and a diploid strain based on
strains CEN.PK102-5B and EN.PK111-61A was used by Kromphardt (MAL2-8c SUC2 ura3-
52 his3∆1 leu2-3/112, with MATa or MATα respectively).
These differences do not seem to be radical enough to change activity of PdmD by a factor of
more than four as is observed, yet it is the only known difference between the two experiments.
The lack of UWM1 production by all other cyclases than PdmD suggests that PdmD in some
cases performs an additional action on the polyketide scaffold, exactly what action is impossible
to determine from this work alone, as the alternative structure resulting from C9-C14 folding is
still only speculative.
No cyclase activity was observed from co-expression of AaOKS with any of the cyclases MtmQ,
and OxyK, as well as the novel sequences NovD-ADE and NovP-AB.
MtmQ and OxyK have previously been characterised in vivo, but only in bacteria,[85][86]
and all the novel sequences are completely uncharacterised. This means that there are sev-
eral untested possible causes for the lacking activity of these cyclases. One possibility could
be that they are simply not expressed correctly in the yeast. Another possibility is that they are
expressed, but require another unknown factor to function, which is not present in the yeast.
They could also all be expressed correctly, but not be able to accept an octaketide as substrate.
It might be a possibility that they are active, and catalyse a folding of the octaketide, but the
methods applied for extraction and detection somehow exclude the products. Finally, it is also
possible that the architechture prediction from cDART was inaccurate, and that the novel cy-
clases are actually not cyclases.
Due to time constraints of this project, only the octaketide scaffold of AaOKS was explored
in combination with cyclases, so the predictability of cyclase activity remains untested at the
longer chain length substrates.
However, there is a lot of literature describing cyclase activity on longer polyketide products
from minimal PKS systems,[36][38][26][42] so translation to a type III PKS based platform
seems achievable.

Conclusions
In this work, in vivo expression in S. cerevisiae of AaOKS, and AaOKS with substitutions
F66L, N222G and the combination F66L/N222G were all reported to result in formation of
the octaketide shunt products SEK4, dSEK4, SEK4B, dSEK4B and ﬂavokermisic acid. In
addition, the three substitutions F66L, N222G and F66L/N222G all resulted in the formation of
the decaketide shunt product SEK15, Especially the double substitution F66L/N222G produced
high ratios of this compound. This is the ﬁrst evidence of this in vivo.
HPLC-MS analysis of AaOKS(F66L/N222G) revealed that the dodecaketide product TW95A,
previously observed in vitro, does not form during in vivo expression in S. cerevisiae No cyclase
activity was detected from co-expression of AaOKS with the cyclases MtmQ, OxyK, or the
novel sequences NovD-ADE and NovP-AB.
Co-expression of AaOKS with several type II PKS cyclases/aromatases allowed for formation
and detection of a previously unidentiﬁed octaketide with the theoretical formula C16H10O7,
which occurs in higher ratios when AaOKS is co-expressed with any of the four C9-C14 ﬁrst
ring folding cyclases; PdmD, TcmN, WhiE-ORFIV, and FkmC1. The compound UWM1 was
shown to be speciﬁcally produced by PdmD, as it was not produced in the presence of any other
cyclase.
