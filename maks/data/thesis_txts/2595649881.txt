ABSTRACT
The purpose of this study is to construct a workﬂow for assessment of view outside
the window using virtual reality (VR) environment where quantiﬁcation of the lu-
minous environment using physically-based rendering methods and user assessments
are combined.
View is an important and inseparable part of daylight in buildings. Although it has
an conﬁding eﬀect of visually perceived environment in daylit conditions, it has been
relatively less studied due to complication in experimental set ups. VR environment
allows for view manipulation without limitations of real world and creates a higher
control over the environment. In this study we performed a user assessment exper-
iment using VR where each scene was created using physically-based renderings for
deriving accurate photometric attributes. Reference rooms in two diﬀerent architec-
tural projects were selected as case studies. The visual and lighting conditions in the
room are changed by variable facade conﬁgurations. The geometries of the facade
elements are modiﬁed using parametric design tool Grasshopper for Rhino. Thirteen
diﬀerent scenes were generated for both projects.
Changing the facade conﬁgurations, 26 scenes were created in total. The scenes were
carefully selected to create a variety of contrast and complexity as well as luminous
environment. In order to assess color contrast, a tinted glazing was also chosen as
one of the variables. A 270◦immersive virtual reality environment of these 26 scenes
were generated using Unity, a real time development platform among other VR envi-
ronment. Fourteen participants among the employees of an architectural oﬃce were
invited to participate in the user assessment study. The participants rated each scene
by answering questions that appeared inside each scene, appearing as an overlay
on the same scene. Each scene was ﬁrstly quantiﬁed based on four quantiﬁcation
methods. The four methods are view quality rating, visual connectivity assessment,
contrast and visual complexity assessment. Finally, the results derived from the view
quantifying method are compared with the results collected from the user assessment
session. The validity of the quantifying methods are evaluated and discussed.
The user assessment results showed several trend and preferences regarding the fa-

cade elements. The observed trends indicated that the facades with least obstructions
were preferable. The tinted glass with warm-color tone showed higher preferences of
all conditions. We could also see that there were a tendency to prefer a high-rise
modern and architectonic view as appose to a low-rise city landscape. The latter is
based on simpliﬁed appearance of the buildings in both view types. It was also shown
that quantiﬁcation of the scenes based on view quality as well as view connectivity
have a strong correlation with positive subjective assessments. Although the con-
trast quantifying method and the visual complexity quantifying method didn’t show
strong correlation with the users’ assessment results, a trend could still be observed
that scenes that have higher contrast values (both achromatic and chromatic) have
higher user votes, scenes that have higher visual complexity values (both achromatic
and chromatic) have lower user votes.
Keywords: View Assessment; Image Processing; Virtual Reality; Visual Comfort;
User Assessment; Indoor Environment

Chapter 1
Introduction
The geometrical design of facade plays an important role in building’s indoor envi-
ronment, energy consumption, occupants’ comfort. Consideration on the design of
facade elements also has a major aesthetic value for building projects. Complex ge-
ometries have been considered as a facade solution in several iconic buildings such as
Sunrise tower by Zaha Hadid Architects, Golf’s Tower by Hackenbrioch Architekten.
The potential to integrate ornamental building skin which simultaneously perform as
shading device that also provides acceptable view to the outside (view-out) and day-
lighting performance has been extensively studied by conducting parametric study
and running simulation in DIVA for Rhino [15].
The importance of view-out and visual comfort is a rather new ﬁeld of study within
facade design and indoor environment study. Unlike daylighting indicators which are
better understood and can be simulated and quantiﬁed, view and visual experience
are often considered to be subjective indicators and less studied. While algorithm to
quantify contrast for rendered architectural scene has been investigated[20] and met-
rics such as daylight glare probability[31][11] have been implemented in daylighting
simulation tools, the view out photometrical properties have not been addressed.
The implementation of virtual reality experience in user experience investigation re-
garding visual comfort has been studied using static panoramic virtual reality scene[3]
, such scenes allow for deﬁning the photometric qualities of the scene as close as pos-
sible to the human eye. Despite the extensive usage of rendering plugins such as
Enscape for Rhino that directly display scenes in rhino through virtual reality, such

rendering engines are diﬃcult to customize to reach the same level of human actual
perception of contrast and adaptation.
1.1
Problem statement and objectives
Focusing in the ﬁeld of indoor visual comfort, although a standardized workﬂow for
daylight simulation has been established and implemented in plugins such as Lady-
bug for Grasshopper, ‘view-out’ still remains to be a rather subjective ﬁeld which
lack quantifying methods. Existing requirements for ‘view-out’ in Danish building
standard (DS/EN 17037) only lists the recommending size of view openings and the
view layers a scene should contain(ground/sky etc.).
Therefore, the main goal of this project is to establish a workﬂow of ‘view-out’ quan-
tifying method which include knowledge from visual connectivity, pixel-based image
processing and environmental psychology.
To verify the ‘view-out’ quantifying methods’ adaptability in real building projects,
we implemented this quantifying workﬂow on two of the built projects from Schmidt
Hammer Lassen Architects: Detroit Monroe blocks and Malmo Live, which have simi-
lar facade patterns but diﬀerent urban landscapes. The facade pattern is manipulated
and 26 scenes in total are selected for ﬁnal testing. A set of scores will be generated
from these quantifying methods.
Then we established the workﬂow to generate physically-based rendering images in
the two reference rooms which reﬂect the real daylighting conditions, then the images
are imported to Virtual Reality (VR) devices using Unity. The intention is to invite
participants to take part in a short voting session (10 min per person) to give scores
of these selected scenes based on their subjective feeling in the immersive scenes.
The participants’ vote will be collected during the VR session and compared with the

2.1
The eﬀect of ’View-out’ on human perception
In contemporary building design, facade pattern has evolved from ornamental ele-
ments into complex elements that has further functions. Aesthetical consideration,
energy consideration and indoor comfort consideration have became the three main
factors that inﬂuence facade design.[15]
Environmental inputs and human inputs restrain and support each other to form a
sustainable, comfortable facade design and indoor environment. Concurrently, build-
ing facade are responsible for almost 50% of the heat loss during winter time and over
heating during summer time[28]. Therefore from sustainable perspective, a more en-
closed and insulated facade is desired. However, for occupants inside the building,
they desire to have access to the outside to gain information of the time changing,
weather and the dynamic environment as visual stimuli[12]. But it doesn’t mean
that the occupants desire as large as possible view openings since they need facade
elements to block excessive light and glare for a visually comfortable indoor environ-
ment. Thus ﬁnding the ’sweet-point’ to balance indoor comfort, energy considerations
and aesthetic design in facade pattern design is crucial. The intention of this project
is to construct a workﬂow for view assessment which includes quantifying methods
and user assessment method using virtual reality environment to help ﬁnding this
balanced facade design.

2.2.1
View quality
Windows or apertures facade element as the connection between indoor and outdoor
environment provide important information containing the time of the day, weather,
location and outside activities for occupants. Furthermore, the content of the view
through the window is considered to be a stimuli that aﬀects the occupants’ experi-
ence. Danish standard on Daylight in buildings stated :‘View to the outside should
be assessed from selected reference points corresponding to where people are located
within utilized area. And a view is considered to comprise three distinct layers: a
layer of sky, a layer of landscape, a layer of ground.’[26]
A research by Hellinga and Hordijk[12] has addressed more speciﬁcally that natural
view, distant view and view containing water are preferred by people. Furthermore
they developed an assessment system to quantify the view quality. The score system
ranges from 0pt (low quality) to 12pt (high quality) and the scoring standard is listed
below:
7

2 Background
• What is the character of the view?
Natural landscapes get 4 points. Built view get 0 points.
• Does the view contain natural green?
urban view with natural green get 2 points.
• Which layers are visible?
Four layers: The ground, nearby buildings or greenery, distant city or landscape,
and the sky. The weight factor of each layer is one point.
• Does the view contain natural water?
Views with water gets 2 points.
• Does the view contain nearby cars/traﬃc?
One point is subtracted from the view quality score if nearby cars or traﬃc are
visible.
• How diverse is the view?
Low diversity gets 0 point. Medium diversity gets 1 point. High diversity gets
2 points.
Finally, if the view is dominated by one or more buildings, if the building is not well
maintained the score stays unaﬀected. If the building is well maintained then score
is added/subtracted according to the building’s structure and built time.
The Danish standard ’Daylight buildings’[26] listed more aspects to assess view qual-
ity.
Five aspects are: (1)the size of the daylight openings; (2)the width of the
view(horizontal sight angle); (3)the outside distance of the view; (4)the number of
layers; (5)the quality of the environmental information of the view. Among these,
the (4)and (5) are considered in the above assessment method. In the standard, the

Visual connectivity
Visual connectivity from indoor spaces determines the amount of exposure for the
occupants. In danish standard for shading devices: DS/EN 14501 [27], visual contact
with the outside has been deﬁned as: ’The capacity of the solar protection device to
allow an exterior view when it is fully extended.’.

An analysis framework for evaluating visual connectivity in indoor open spaces based
on three dimensional visibility graph was developed[29]. The method conducts radi-

point could reach other points is recorded as a percentage of connection for each point.
The average score of all points is then calculated. A hot spot graph representing the
internal visual connectivity across the ﬂoor plan is also produced.

2.3.1
Contrast
Contrast is a physical variable that relates to most aspects of light[6], which could
provide information in luminance distribution and variation of a scene. Furthermore,
as contrast associates with modelling in human vision, which is important to reveal
details in the visual ﬁeld, contrast has been used as an important indicator in assessing
digital images quality and then implemented in indoor lighting conditions evaluation
using digital images as medium.
In architectural design, creating lighting contrast is an important way to create visual
interest and the desired ambience. Symbolic work such as Chapel of Light by Tadao
Ando and Museum of the city in Cassino by Steven Holl reveals the power of creating
diﬀerent level of contrast in scenes by manipulating and organizing lights and shadows
to impact the visitor’s’ experience according to diﬀerence usage of the building. Apart
from the signiﬁcance in aesthetics, previous work has also shown the impact of lumi-
nance distribution on occupants’ preference and productivity. An HDR image based
study shows a trend between increased non-uniformity and the participants’ level of
excitement[25]. Wymelenberg and Inanici conducted a pilot study investigating the
impact of adjusting the blind on occupants’ preference and lighting perceptions, it
is concluded that adequate luminance variations create a stimulating and interesting
environment that improves the preference ratings of the occupants, however excessive
luminance variability tends toward creating uncomfortable spaces[30].
Three dimensions of color: Hue, Value (Lightness) and Chroma (Saturation) con-
tribute diﬀerently in contrast perception.
As light or dark is the ﬁrst perception
human grow at infant stage when color perception hasn’t developed, Lightness be-
come the most important factor in existing contrast deﬁnition and measuring metrics,
11
==PAGE:23==
2 Background
therefore gray scale images have been mediums in many existing researches. However,
adaption is the most remarkable property of human vision system (HVS). Contrast
adaptation adjusts sensitivity according to how the ensemble of luminances and chro-
maticities are distributed around the mean, and instead alters colour appearance by
changing the perceived contrast along diﬀerent directions in colour space[13]. There-
fore the implementation of chromatic channels in the algorithm of contrast measure-
ment has lead to more accurate and integrated measures[19][24].
Contrast measurement methods could be divided in to two categories: Global Con-
trast and Local Contrast. Global contrast focus on the brightest area and the darkest
area in an image. Local contrast also takes the composition of contrasting area into
consideration which more thoroughly reﬂects the contrast conditions of the whole im-
age without losing information of the image content. Metrics based on local contrast
and derives a single value for the convenience of rating and evaluating diﬀerent ar-
chitectural rendering images such as spatial contrast metric has been developed[21].
The comparison between diﬀerent global contrast and local contrast metrics under the
theme of architectural rendering images has also been carried[16]. Important metrics
and approaches have been summarized in Table 2.3. However the evaluation method
on architectural rendering images integrating chromatic channels has not been con-
ducted.
As the method for global contrast could not eﬀectively reﬂect the overall conditions,
therefore the assessment approach should be based on local contrast measures. As
the use of multilevel is an important implementation feature to mimic the HVS[24]
and considering the simplicity of calculation, for contrast section we select RAMMG
local contrast method developed by Rizzi et al. in 2004 and integrating the chromatic

2.3.2
Visual complexity
Visual complexity describes the amount of information and diversity of a scene.
Therefore it is an important indicator for deﬁning whether a scene is visually ’in-
teresting/distracting’ or ’dull/calming’.
From human perception perspective, previous research by Olive et al. has deﬁned
visual complexity with several dimensions[14] including: number of objects, clutter,
openness, symmetry, organization, variety of colors. Mathematical approach has also
been investigated in evaluating visual complexity of an image in the ﬁeld of computer
science. Through user assessment approach, a research addressed that regularity, un-
derstandability, directionality and roughness are the most inﬂuential factors aﬀecting
visual complexity evaluation[10]. A complexity measuring method based on the mean
information gain assessing 2D pattern (composed by black, gray and white pixels) is
developed by Andrienko et al[1].
In the ﬁeld of light engineering, the shape and detail visibility of a 3D object is
an important aspects in assessing lighting quality. Relating to the scenario of this
thesis project: assessing the ’view-out’ from a building under daylight, one of the
intention is to investigate the correlation between occupants’ perception of the ob-
jects they see from the windows, more precisely, their ability to identify or to be
distracted from certain objects in the scene. Therefore, shape and visibility of the
objects is one of a solid angle to assess ’visual complexity’ with this background. A
luminance based measures addressed that luminance ratio, mean luminance of the

Virtual reality(VR) as a technology that displays computer generated images speciﬁ-

mounted device (Virtual Research V6), computer graphics and spatial sound eﬀect

Palmer Lucky created the prototype for Oculus in 2010: a kit VR headset that open
the gate of commercial portable and aﬀordable VR devices, the world has seen a rise
of VR products and their implementation in game industry, architecture industry and
psychology related researches. The time line of virtual reality is presented in Figure

Scene generation
The traditional way in architecture industry to use VR devices as a medium to present
3D models is to connect the VR device with the 3D modeling software using plugins
such as ’Enscape for Rhino’, ’Enscape for Revit’ etc. This is an intuitive ’one but-
ton’ approach which could generate immersive scenes quickly. However, it uses the
rendering engine itself to generate the scene, which are not validated scientiﬁcally
regarding its precision of daylighting condition.
In this project, our intention is to generate immersive scene which could be imple-

the reference room at the same time. Rendering light with Radiance from Greg Ward
has the ability to generate static ray-tracing based rendering pictures which repre-
sents the real lighting conditions. Therefore the key to generate immersive scenes was
to ﬁnd the solution to generate immersive scene with static pictures.
Cubemap is a collection of six directions’ textures (front, back, top, down, left,right)
that represents the reﬂections of an environment. The strategy in this project is to
set the viewpoint (the participant’s eye position) in the 3D model. Then capture six
directions’ view by adjusting the camera’s target. After getting the rendered images
of this six view, the six images will be imported to Unity interface. Finally, using
the function ’assets-create-legacy-cubemap’ in Unity, and assigning the six views on
its corresponding position, a seamless immersive scene which represent the real day-
lighting condition is generated. The procedure to generate cubemap is described in

In Danish Standard DS/EN 17037 Daylight in Buildings, recommendation for views
are given from two perspectives. One is the width of view openings, and another is the
quality of view outwards from a given position where the occupants are most possibly
to be resident at. Therefore, before we implement the workﬂow we established in
Chapter 3, we would evaluate the test scenes with these recommendations.
4.1.1
Quantiﬁcation of the scenes based on required width
of view openings
Firstly, the 26 selected scenes are evaluated based on Danish Standard DS/EN 17037
Daylight in Buildings. In Annex C View out, recommended width of view openings
are given as a function of the depth of the utilized area within a space and a horizontal
sight angle of at least 14, 28 and 54 degrees respectively[26]. As shown in Figure 4.1,
the reference room has a width of 16m(b) and a depth of 16m(a), which exceeds the
maximum value in the assessment chart, therefore we take the maximum required
width of the view opening which is 15m. The facade patterns are ﬁrstly evaluated
based on this value (scene 1- scene 7, since scene 8 to scene 13 are based on the above
patterns). The calculation of the total width of the view opening is based on the
principle that the eyesight is perpendicular to the glazing and only the area that is
not blocking the eyesight as described above is contributing to the ’width of view

4.5
Discussions
The established work ﬂow consists of both objective quantiﬁcation methods and user
assessment experiment design in virtual reality environment.
In the quantiﬁcation method section, four methods were developed based on previous
study integrating with modiﬁcations.
For the view quality method, a 14 pt scoring system was established integrating the
existing 12 pt scoring system and the perspectives that was not included in the 12
pt system but are required in building standards. For visual connectivity assessment,
a workﬂow that considers the complex facade elements instead of only the context
buildings were established. Both these two methods show strong positive correlation
with the user assessment results.
For the contrast and visual complexity calculation, chromatic channels were taken
into account based on the existing algorithm. Therefore both achromatic and chro-
matic indicators could be derived. These two didn’t show correlation with the user
assessment results. However they did reveal the trend of users’ preference under these
two subject. Therefore they could be adopted as pre-assessment tools to assist build-
ing designers. Further study needs to be done to develop more accurate contrast and
visual complexity calculation method.
Adopting the existing workﬂow using static images to generate immersive scenes for
user assessment, a more interactive and intriguing experiment procedure was devel-
oped integrating with knowledge from game development. This set of user assessment
approach has been proved to be eﬀective, intuitive and reliable.

Conclusions
4.6.1
Users’ preference for diﬀerent facade patterns
From the data analysis above it could be observed and summarized: (1) The users
have preference for deep and thin vertical facade elements comparing to short and
wide facade elements.
(2) The users have preference for facade elements without
rotation comparing to facade elements that rotated 45◦. (3) Larger distance between
vertical facade elements gain higher scores from the users. (4) The addition of warm-
tone colored glazing could slightly increase the user’s degree of visual comfort which is
matching with the previous assumption. (5) When the facade elements are identical,
the users prefer modern high rising well-maintained city view comparing to low rise
town view.
4.6.2
Correlation between quantiﬁcation methods and user
assessment results
From the correlation analysis above, it could be observed that the view quality and
visual connectivity quantifying method have strong correlation with user assessments.
The contrast and visual connectivity have no evident correlation from the collected
results. However, some trend could be observed from the chart above which could be
useful. (1) Scenes that have higher contrast values(both achromatic and chromatic)
have higher subjective ratings. (2) Scenes that have higher visual complexity val-
ues(both achromatic and chromatic) have lower ratings.
Therefore, the contrast and visual complexity quantifying method could still be imple-
mented in the design process. From the results it could be assumed that users prefer
scenes with higher contrast and lower visual complexity, although this correlation is
not evident.

4.7
Future steps
Some comments from the participants are collected during the experiment session as
shown below:
• ’There could be more decorations in the scenes instead of just a chair’
• ’The rendering looks lacking details which is diﬀerent from what I understand
as architectural renderings’
• ’It is an interesting and inspiring way to look at the facade design in VR googles.
Especially for scene 5 to scene 7, I thought I would rate higher when I could
see the most outside views, but it’s actually good to have something there, feels
more safe and comfortable’
The interviews with the participants after each session were largely consisted of ’com-
plaints’ from the architects about the quality of the renderings. Since in this project
the intention was to study the outside view and the facade elements, therefore no
decorations and details in the room were considered. Moreover, the rendering engine
used in this project is Radiance, which is a backwards ray-tracing rendering engine
that has been veriﬁed scientiﬁcally for creating physically-based HDR images where
the photometric values can be derived. The quality of the renderings were adjusted
to a medium high quality within the time frmae of the project and the computa-
tional capacity available. The rendered scenes allowed for accurate photometrical
understanding of the scene within the range of possible, however, they were from
realistic scenes instead of serving for artistic visualizations. The explanations regard-
ing the type of rendering in these interviews always lead to acceptance and better

understanding of the purpose of the project. However, the impact of using such ren-
derings with empty spaces and compromise details to participants on their rating is
unknown. A more diverse group of participants is thus recommended to eliminate the
eﬀect of ’backround’ or ’profession’ in such studies. Subjects should have diﬀerent
educational background and work experiences to eliminate their pre-understanding
of the experiment. Depending on the introduction and explanation the participants
