Abstract 
Antibody-drug conjugates provide a selective approach to target tumours and reduce adverse 
effects. They consist of an antibody, a linker and a drug (cytotoxic agent). Many of them suffer from 
poor linker chemistry either with premature loss of drug into the blood plasma or inefficient release 
mechanisms.  
This project aimed to provide a novel and alternative linker-strategy which utilizes light irradiation 
to release the drug. For this purpose, two different photolabile linkers based on the ortho-
nitrobenzyl and the nitrodibenzofuran scaffolds have been produced. Both linkers have been 
provided with functional groups enabling the assembly of the conjugate. To enable a comparison of 
their efficiencies of photocleavage, the drug doxorubicin has been functionalized to make the 
assembly with the former mentioned linker possible. The analysis of the synthesized compounds 
has been carried out with NMR, RP-UPLC/MS, IR and melting points.   
A total of 14 different compounds were successfully produced. The former linker was synthesized 
with acceptable yields (≥61 %) with one-exception being a Pd/C catalysed reduction of an oxime 
(29 %). However, the promising reducing agent, NaBH3CN has been suggested. Better yields were 
obtained for the other linker (≥64 %) as several reaction steps occurred quantitatively. Great effort 
successfully resulted in the purification of a product from an Ullmann reaction that coeluted with a 
by-product. Furthermore, an aldol reaction was found superior to a Reformatsky reaction. Lastly, 
functionalization of doxorubicin gave high yields (≥81 %) but inadequate purities. An intelligent 
synthetic pathway which is one step shorter and skips the most challenging step has been 
suggested.

Resume 
Antistof-lægemiddel konjugater er en selektiv måde at målrette et kræftlægemiddel mod en tumour 
og dermed reducere bivirkninger. De består af et monoklonalt antistof bundet til et cellegiftigt 
lægemiddel via en linker. Mange af disse konjugater består af linkere, som resulterer i for tidlig 
frigivelse af lægemidlet til blodet og ineffektiv frigivelse af lægemidlet i lysosomet.  
Projektet fokuserer på en alternativ linker-strategi, der udnytter lys ved en defineret bølgelængde, 
til at frigive lægemidlet selektivt. Derfor er der i projektet blevet fremstillet to photolabile linkere 
baseret på et ortho-nitrobenzyl og en nitrodibenzofuran skelet. Begge linkere er er blevet 
syntetiseret med funktionelle grupper, hvorpå de resterende dele af antistof-lægeimiddel 
konjugatet kan påsættes. For at kunne sammenligne de to photolabile linkeres lyskløvning, er 
doxorubicin blevet funktionaliseret med en carboxysylsyre-gruppe, som kan tages i brug, når den 
førstnævnte linker skal påsættes. Analyserne af de fremstillede stoffer er blevet foretaget med 
NMR, RP-UPLC/MS, IR og smeltepunkter.  
En sum af 14 forskellige stoffer er blevet fremstillet i projektet. Syntesen af den førstnævnte linker 
gav gode udbytter (≥61 %) dog med en undtagelse, som var den Pd/C katalyserede reduktion af en 
oxim (29 %). Derfor blev der fundet et andet reduktionsmiddel,  som ser lovende ud, nemlig 
NaBH3CN. Den anden linker gav endnu bedre udbytter (≥64 %), da flere reaktionstrin forløb 
kvantitativt. Et stort arbejde resulterede i den succesfulde oprensing produktet fra en Ullmann 
reaktion, som coeluerede med et biprodukt. Derudover blev en aldol reaktion fundet overlegen 
sammenlignet med en Reformatsky reaktion. Endelig gav funktionalisering af doxorubicin høje 
udbytter (≥81 %), men utilstrækkelige renheder. En intelligent syntese af stoffet, der fjerner et helt 
reaktionstrin er blevet foreslået.

1. 
Introduction 
For many decades, cancer has been of great interest to the public. It remains the 2nd leading cause 
of death and has a 5-year survival of only 66 % in the United States (Centers for Disease Control and 
Prevention, https://gis.cdc.gov/grasp/USCS/DataViz.html)1. Today the paradigm is combination 
chemotherapy where multiple drugs are used with different mechanisms of action2. However, 
today’s cancer therapy suffers from severe side effects on organs and tissues due to its lack of 
selectivity3. 
With the FDA approval of the first antibody-drug conjugate (ADC), Mylotarg® in 2000, the first step 
was taken towards a new era of cancer therapy with less adverse effects. With a record of 4 
approved ADCs and about 60 in clinical trials, only the tip of the iceberg has been revealed to us4. 
This promising class of chemotherapy has been termed the chemical “magic bullet” because of its 
high specificity towards tumour cells5 with a concomitant expansion of the therapeutic window. 
ADCs are an advanced class of chemotherapeutics which can be divided into three elements, namely 
i) a monoclonal antibody (mAb), ii) a linker and iii) a cytotoxic agent as sketched in Figure 1.

Despite its early success, areas for continued development and improvement still exist. Problems

Thus, this project seeks to produce Doxorubicin-functionalized photolabile linkers for ADCs. For this

2. 
Antibody-Drug Conjugates 
ADCs belong to the group of actively targeted drug delivery where an antibody is employed to target 
a specific antigen or surface receptor present on tumour cells. The strategy is effective because 
some antigens are overexpressed on the surface of cancer cells. An antibody on its own which binds 
to the antigen typically will not to cause any cytotoxicity or treatment efficacy. Thus, ADCs are clearly 
superior since they utilize a highly potent cytotoxic agent in combination with the specificity of the 
antibody. The selectivity for killing tumour cells rather than healthy cells has expanded the 
therapeutic window compared to conventional chemotherapeutics8. The ADC also consists of a 
linker which is very important as it provides distance between the two components, preserving their 
functionalities.  Additionally, it affects the properties and efficacy of the ADC to a high extend9. 
The cancer targets can be divided into two groups: Hematological (cancers in the blood) and solid 
tumours. Targeting hematological tumours with ADCs may be better tolerated than those targeting 
solid tumours. The antigens of circulating hematological tumours tend to be less commonly 
expressed on normal tissues compared to solid tumours which are derived from epithelial cells. 
Antigens on hematological targets may also be expressed on healthy cells, however, these antigens 
are typically found on circulating cell populations with low expression in solid tissues. Furthermore, 
the hematological (healthy) cell populations have a higher regenerative capacity unless these are 
stem cells. Hematological tumours are often more homogenously expressed by antigens than solid 
tumours10. Lastly, solid tumours are difficult to penetrate for reasons including their high interstitial 
fluid pressure (IFP), high cell density, excessive deposition of extracellular matrix and physical 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
11 
 
barriers made of stroma proteins. The higher IFP makes it tedious for traditional chemotherapy that 
works by passive (non-receptor-mediated) targeting to penetrate the tumour but this is alleviated 
by ADCs which due to the binding affinity may stick around for a longer time9. In fact, the majority 
of ADCs in early-stage clinical trials are targeting solid tumours but about half of the approved ADCs 
in late-stage clinical trials are targeting hematological tumours which underpin that it is more 
difficult to achieve success in the therapy of solid tumours10.   
In the coming subsections, the three elements of the ADC; i) the mAb, ii) the cytotoxic agent and iii) 
the linker will be described in more detail. 
 
2.1. 
The Antibody 
The mAb is a huge protein (150 kDa)11 employed to provide selective targeting of tumour cells over 
healthy cells. A mAb is an immunoglobulin (Ig) with a single idiotype produced by immortalized B 
cells (one cell line). The mAb consists of structural repetitiveness. Roughly 110 amino acids in length 
comprise a homology unit and this unit is repeated. The series of repeating homology units is called 
the immunoglobulin domains. The majority of therapeutic mAbs are based on the IgG1 isotope and 
only a few are based on IgG2 and IgG4 due to its fewer number of disulfide bonds9,12. The majority 
of mAbs employed in ADCs are fully humanized, however, chimerized and humanized are not 
uncommon13,14. 
The antibody can be divided into domains. The light chain consists of one variable (VL) and one 
constant (CL) domain whereas the heavy chain consists of one variable (VH) and three constant 
domains (CH1, CH2 and CH3). The variable regions of the heavy and light chains show similarities. All 
the constant regions show strong homology to each other. Each domain has a tertiary structure 
consisting of two β-pleated sheets with a sandwich-like structure and a hydrophobic interior. Within 
each domain, an intrachain, non-solvent exposed disulfide bond is buried between the two β-
pleated sheet structures15. The interchain disulfide-bonds, however, are highly solvent exposed and 
thus more reactive15. In the tertiary structure, loops are held out from the sandwich of the VL and 
these are the complementary-determining regions (CDRs). 
==PAGE:13==

The CDRs comprise the greatest variability in the light chain and is situated around residues 30, 55 
and 95 (moving from the tip of the variable region). These three CDRs are each around 10 amino 
acids in length. The variable region of the heavy chain has another three CDRs which are near the 
CDRs of the light chain. The combining of the light and heavy chain CDRs provide a cleft that 
comprises the antigen-binding site. Furthermore, the CDRs amino acid sequences determine the 
shape and ionic properties of the antigen-binding site and thus define the specificity of the 
antibody12.  The structure of the mAb (IgG1) is roughly Y-shaped and is sketched in Figure 212.

The antibody’s binding-affinity for a specific antigen is utilized in ADCs as previously described. 
Initially the antibody binds to the antigen whereas the ADC will be invaginated by the cell and 
become pinched off to form a membrane-bound vesicle enclosing the ADC. The vesicle is transferred 
to an early endosome (pH 6) that matures into a late endosome (pH 5) and fuses with a lysosome. 
The lysosome has a pH of 4-5 and a high amount of protein digestive enzymes such as cathepsins 
and collagenases besides nucleases and lipid digestive enzymes. Following lysosomal degradation, 
the drug diffuses out of the lysosome where it may reach the target which is typically DNA or 
microtubules and ultimately kill the cell. The overall process is called receptor-mediated endocytosis 
(Figure 3) and includes micropinocytosis, clathrin-mediated endocytosis and caveolin-mediated 
endocytosis9,16,17. Most antibodies are taken up by the clathrin-mediated pathway4.

2.2. 
The Cytotoxic Agent 
Chemotherapeutic drugs are unable to selectively target tumour cells. Therefore, most of them have 
severe side effects on normal tissues and organs. Another problem is tumour resistance to 
chemotherapy e.g. caused by permeability glycoprotein (P-gp) overexpression. The protein is 
situated in the cell membrane and pumps out the chemotherapeutic drug. For instance, DOX readily 
internalizes into the cell by passive diffusion and is negatively affected by tumour resistance caused 
by P-gp18. This type of drug resistance can be bypassed by receptor-mediated endocytosis which 
ADCs utilize3,8.  
Only about 1-2 % of the administered ADC will reach the tumour site, thus the cytotoxic agent must 
be very potent to work in low concentrations 19. In fact, the approved or developing ADCs typically 
use cytotoxic agents with potencies in the picomolar or lower range and are a 100-2000 fold more 
potent than DOX. The mode of action is usually either by interference with the mitotic cycle of the 
tumour cell by inhibition of tubulin polymerization (e.g. auristatins and maytansionids) or disruption 
of DNA (e.g. calicheamicin and duocarmycin) which result in cell death9,17 .  
For reasons previously described, cytotoxic agents used in ADCs that targets solid tumours should 
preferably possess the bystander effect. The bystander effect is the cytotoxic agent’s ability to 
diffuse out of the originally targeted cell and into adjacent cells. Cytotoxic agents that possess the 
bystander effect can diffuse deeper into the tumour where they can be taken up in a different cell. 
This means, that cells which could not be reached by the ADCs, or cells that are antigen-negative 
hence can be affected. An ideal cytotoxic agent to exhibit the bystander effect should neither be 
too hydrophilic (e.g. charged) nor too large to quickly diffuse through cell membranes. The 
bystander effect is shown in Figure 420.

In this project, DOX has been used as a model drug for different reasons. It is one of the most 
important chemotherapeutic drugs and it is widely used in the treatment of breast, gastric, lung, 
ovarian, thyroid, non-Hodgkin’s and Hogdkin’s lymphoma, multiple myeloma, sarcoma and pediatric 
cancers21.  
DOX has been extensively studied, however, its molecular mechanisms that cause cell death remain 
elusive. Consequently, only the two most important mechanisms that work in therapeutically 
relevant doses will be described and these include:  
i) 
Intercalation into DNA and disruption of topoisomerase-II-mediated repair 
 
ii) 
Generation of free radicals which damage cellular membranes, DNA and proteins 
The intercalation can be explained by the planar structure of DOX. DOX forms a covalent bond with 
guanine on one of the two DNA strands which is facilitated by cellular formaldehyde (a methylene 
linkage). This adduct formation has been shown to activate DNA damage and induce cell death. On 
the opposing strand, DOX forms hydrogen bonds with another guanine. The intercalation of DNA 
via adduct formation with DOX can be seen in Figure 522.

The intercalation of DNA pushes apart the flanking base pairs and unwinds the double helix resulting 
in DNA supercoils and increased positive torsional stress. The effect of the torsional stress can 
deregulate processes such as replication, transcription and nucleosome stability. The destabilization 
itself of the nucleosomes provides a mechanism for the action of the drug.  
Additionally, DOX poisons the enzyme topoisomerase IIα which results in double-strand DNA breaks 
and cell death. The enzyme is responsible for releasing torsional stress formed during biological 
processes such as DNA replication and transcription. The enzyme binds DNA supercoils and breaks 
both strands of one DNA duplex, passes the other duplex through the resulting gap and reseals the 
break. DOX is thought to work by trapping the breakage sites and stabilizing the cleavage complex 
thus impeding DNA resealing. 
It has been shown that the cell-killing effect of DOX is still present independently of topoisomerase 
IIα, however, studies suggest that inhibition of topoisomerase IIα could further exacerbate the 
torsional strain caused by DOX. In fact, DOX inhibits topoisomerase IIα which lead to double-strand 
breaks. Unfortunately, DOX is known to cause cardiac muscle failure since it also inhibits 
topoisomerase IIβ which is the isoform of the topoisomerase IIα23.  
Lastly, DOX contain a quinone-hydroquinone moiety which can be easily reduced by a variety of 
enzyme systems, the most significant being the NADPH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide 
phosphate) dependent cytochrome P450 reductase but others include xanthine oxidase and nitric

2.3. 
The Linker 
In this section, the most common linkers for ADCs will be described. The linker in ADCs can be 
divided into the cleavable and non-cleavable type. The cleavable type can furthermore be 
subdivided into the chemically labile linkers such as the acid-labile linkers and the disulfide linkers 
and the enzymatically (protease) cleavable linkers9.  
 
2.3.1. 
Acid-labile linkers 
The acid-labile linkers include hydrazones and silyl ethers. The most common is the hydrazone with 
a plasma half-life of 183 h at pH 7 and 4.4 h at pH 5. This means that the linker get cleaved under 
acidic conditions in the lysosome. One issue with this type of linker is that, it is not sufficiently stable 
in blood plasma and releases the drug too early. The ADC, Gemtuzumab Ozogamicin marketed as 
==PAGE:19==

Mylotarg® (Appendix C) which contain a hydrazone linker was withdrawn from the market for this 
reason6. A simplified example of a hydrazone linker and its cleavage can be seen in Figure 7.

2.3.2. 
Disulfide linkers 
Disulfide bridges can be used as part of a linker to release the cytotoxic agent in the intracellular 
reducing environment. There is a high concentration of the thiol-containing tripeptide, gluthathione 
in its reduced form (GSH) which concentration in the cytoplasm is about 1000-fold higher than in 
the blood. The intracellular concentration is even higher in many tumour cells. Once the ADC is 
internalized in the cell and inside the lysosome, the antibody is degraded by proteolysis prior to 
disulphide-exchange or reduction of the disulphide bridge by GSH6.  The exact mechanism for the 
disulfide-linked drug release is poorly understood. The reaction with GSH, however, is a redox 
process where the disulfide bridge is reduced to thiols and GSH becomes oxidized to gluthathione 
disulfide (GSSG) by formation of a disulfide bond with another GSH. The reaction results in the 
release of drug as can be seen in Figure 825,26.

Disulfide linkers do not quiet solve the problem associated with the mentioned hydrazone linker. In

2.3.3. 
Enzymatically cleavable linkers 
Enzymatically cleavable linkers got a superior plasma stability and release mechanism compared to 
the aforementioned linkers. The most common enzymatically cleavable sequence is the dipeptide 
valine-citrulline (val-cit) combined with p-aminobenzyl alcohol (PAB) which is a self-immolative 
linker (Figure 9). When the tumour-overexpressed lysosomal enzyme, cathepsin B (catB) cleaves the 
amide that links the dipeptide and PAB, it triggers an 1,6-elimination of carbon dioxide and the 
concomitant release of the free drug in its amine/hydroxy form. This strategy is used in the approved 
ADC, Adcetris®19 (Appendix C). A drawback by this particular enzymatically cleavable linker is that it 
has been found to exhibit high aggregation (up to 80 %)6.

2.3.5. 
Conjugation strategies 
The strategies for conjugation to the antibody can be divided into the non-specific and the site-
specific conjugation. The former takes advantage of reactive side chains of lysine and cysteine 
naturally occurring on the antibody. The IgG scaffold has more than 80 lysines in which 20 are 
solvent accessible. Conjugation to lysine leads to a broad range of drug-antibody ratios (DARs) e.g. 
Kadcyla® has an average of 3.5 cytotoxic agents per antibody. The heterogenous mixture is 
challenging to characterize and purify.  
==PAGE:23==

Cysteines on the other hand are less common with 12 intra-chain and 4 inter-chain disulfide bonds 
in IgG1. The significantly more reactive inter-chain disulfide bonds are the main target for 
conjugation. Prior to conjugation, the disulfide bond must be reduced to thiol groups. The limited 
number of possible conjugation sites provide improved dispersity and thus easier characterization 
and purification of the ADCs6.   
The site-specific conjugation mainly employs protein engineering e.g. adding reactive handles or 
unnatural amino acids. Alternatively, ligating enzymes can be used to catalyze bond formation 
between specific amino acid sequences29.  
The major part of ADCs that are either in clinical trials or approved (Adcetris®) utilize the excellent 
reactivity of the mAb’s thiol groups towards maleimide based linkers, however, a few ADCs use a 
thiol group of a (modified) cytotoxic agent to conjugate to the maleimide via a Michael addition. In

3. 
Light irradiation and photolabile linkers 
The previously described cleavable and non-cleavable linkers all have their individual drawbacks. 
Thus, the focus in this report is on the novel application of photolabile linkers in ADCs. Photolabile 
linkers belong to the group of cleavable linkers since the cytotoxic agent can be selectively released 
upon irradiation with a suited wavelength of light. The irradiation induces a fast chemical process in 
the linker causing the release of the cytotoxic agent. On the contrary to many cleavable linkers, 
however, the photolabile linker has superior blood plasma stability. It does not get degraded by 
enzymes, acids or other psychological stimuli making its release of drug more selective. As a 
cleavable linker it promotes the bystander effect. Another important aspect is that light irradiation 
can be selectively aimed towards the tumour site(s) and reduce systemic toxicity.  
The following subsections describe the applicability of light and provides theoretical background on 
one and two photon excitations (OPEs and TPEs respectively) that relate to the properties of the 
two photolabile linkers, PL1 and PL2 which have been synthesized in the project.   
 
3.1. 
Light Irradiation as an Exogenous Stimuli for Drug Release 
Photolabile linkers belong to the group of exogenous stimuli-responsive linkers meaning that they 
are manipulated from a stimulus outside of the body, in this case by light irradiation. The light 
irradiation is preferably benign to normal tissue, shows minimal absorption and interaction with 
biological components and it should penetrate tissues to be applicable in vivo. Radiation of shorter 
wavelengths (high energy) such as X-rays and 𝛾-rays are damaging to normal tissue and therefore 
cannot be used. Another source of light irradiation with lower energy is UV-light, however, this is 
also harmful to normal tissues, gets absorbed by biological components and does not penetrate 
tissues sufficiently. In the even lower energy spectrum, there is near infrared light (NIR) irradiation 
with wavelengths of 750-1000 nm. The longer wavelength enables reduced scattering, deeper 
penetration into the tissue while being relatively mild to normal tissues26. To take advantage of a 
longer wavelength, a TPE is necessary. The next section describes the OPE and TPE routes and how 
a photolabile linker can be evaluated in relation to this.

In a OPE, the fluorophore in the ground state (S0) can absorb a single photon to get to the excited

corresponding to the energy it takes to excite a fluorophore. This energy equals the energy 
difference between the excited and ground states (ES1-ES0). The relation between the wavelength of 
the photon and the energy difference of the electronic states is shown in Equation 1.

The probability for absorption to occur simultaneously is very low, thus TPE requires a much higher 
proton flux with a factor ~106 relative to OPE. Instead of using a high-power continuous-wave (cw) 
laser to achieve this, an ultrashort-pulsed laser is used which generates a higher ratio of peak power 
relative to the average power. In other words, the photons are concentrated into the shorter pulses. 
Therefore, the probability of finding two photons present on the fluorophore at the same time is 
much higher. The most commonly used laser source for TPA is the titanium-sapphire solid-state 
laser with wavelengths between 700-1000 nm and pulses down to 100 fsec30.  
The one – and two photon absorption (OPA and TPA respectively) spectrums are very different due 
to their different excitation routes. This is contrary to the identical emission spectrums. The 
absorption maximum is about 2-fold in TPA30.   
In contrast to a fluorophore which is designed to re-emit light upon excitation, the photolabile 
linkers utilize the excitation light (the photons) to induce a photochemical reaction which 
subsequently releases the caged (inactivated) cytotoxic agent. In fact, re-emission of light is known 
to cause additional phototoxicity, thus, the absorbed photons should be efficiently utilized in the 
photochemical reaction30. For this reason, a high uncaging quantum yield (𝜑𝑢) is desired which 
defines how successfully the absorbed photons translate into releasing the active compound at the 
irradiation wavelength at the absorption maximum as seen in Equation 230.

In this report, the two photolabile linkers, PL1 and PL2 have been synthesized. PL1 is a derivative of 
an o-NB group which is one of the most extensively studied types of photolabile protecting groups

The o-NB group has been equipped with a carboxylic acid handle where a maleimide based 
conjugation handle (for the mAb) can be attached. The linker can be seen in Figure 14.

aromatic moiety. The former mainly affects the photolysis quantum yield whereas the latter affects 
the absorbance35. Even small changes in the structure can render it photocleavable within 345-420

5. 
Conclusion 
This master’s thesis sought to synthesize DOX-functionalized photolabile linkers for ADCs.  Two 
linkers, PL1 and PL2 were successfully produced and some challenges were solved. For the synthesis 
relating to PL1, the yields were generally good ( ≥61 %) with one exception being synthesis of 2 
which gave a modest yield (29 %). The lower yield was mainly attributed to the reduction of the 
oxime with Pd/C, thus a screening to find other possible reducing agents was conducted and 
NaBH3CN showed great promise. Moving on to the synthesis of 4, the solubility of the product in 
MeOH and other solvents was found to be very poor. Therefore, another trituration, rather than a 
recrystallisation, may be a better way to purify the compound. 
The major challenge in the synthesis of PL2, turned out to be the synthesis of 6 because of a 
challenging purification where a by-product coeluted with the product. Although it was possible to 
get the compound clean on smaller scale, the best solution was found to be to accept a slightly 
lower purity of the product (RP-UPLC purity of >95 %).  Furthermore, significant improvements on 
the yields in the syntheses of 7 (90 %) and 9 (quant.) compared to literature50 (61 % and 63 % 
respectively) were made. The synthesis of 8 was found to rely on 2 equivalents of Cs2CO3 to 
complete in quantitative yield. The synthesis of 10b was first attempted with a Reformatsky reaction 
which did either not produce the desired product and in some cases likely destroyed the starting 
material. Instead an aldol reaction was found to work well, however, optimal reaction conditions 
need to be found improve the yield further. 
The functionalisation of DOX occurred in high yields (≥81 %) but was found to require better 
purification strategies, however, literature has shown that the synthesis of 13 can be cleverly 
shortened by one step54. 
A test with e.g. UV-light must be conducted to elucidate which of the photolabile linkers that has 
the most efficient release of DOX. The best linker should be assembled with DOX and a mAb to 
produce an ADC and this should be tested in blood plasma for its stability. As a photolabile linker, it 
is expected that the BPS will be good. 
To sum up, the syntheses of PL1, PL2 and the functionalized DOX have laid a good foundation for 
future development as the syntheses have proven feasible and to occur in overall good yields and 
purities.
