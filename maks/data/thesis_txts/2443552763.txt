This project is done at the department of Biomedical Science at the University of Copenhagen. The
project is a collaboration with the Biotech Research & Innovation Centre and the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark (DTU). This is the ﬁnishing project of the MSc in Medicine and Technology.
The assignment counts for 30 ECTS-points. The project is developed by Malene Eppler Bak in the
period from 1/8-2018 to 1/1-2019.
The goal with the project is to validate whether USPIO labeled T-cells can be visualized in naive
-and trauma mouse models using non-invasive MR imaging. Also to see if the T-cells behave like
normal non-tagged cells. From this, you can achieve greater understanding of the kinetic proﬁle
and migration of the immune cells with respect to brain inﬂammation. By using a well-known
protocol for culturing, incubation and labeling, (CD4+) T-cells are isolated from mouse lymphoid
organs and nodes, and labeled with the negative contrast agent. The cells are injected i.v. to differ-
ent mouse models, and visualized using an optimized MRI scan protocol.

Abstract
Inﬂammation er kendetegnet for mange større sygdomsgrupper som autoimmune sygdomme og
neurologiske lidelser og der er et stort ønske om, at udforske immuncellers adfærd og hvordan de
reagerer under disse forhold. Ved brug af en in vivo celle-tracking model er det muligt, at visu-
alisere og kortlægge migrationsmønstret af speciﬁkke immunceller og deres undertyper. Ved at
undersøge bevægelsesbanen af nanopartikel-markerede celler, ved brug af MRI, er det muligt at
opnå en præcis og detaljeret viden om virkningen af speciﬁkke immuncellepopulationer, der vil
kunne oversættes til nøjagtig patientdiagnostik og fremtidige cellebaserede behandlingsstrategier.
Projektets formål er, at udvikle en protokol der muliggør visualisering af T-celler i både naive -og
traumemus. Der gøres brug af en negativ kontrastagent, USPIO, der skaber signaldæmpning i det
område, det beﬁnder sig. Denne tagges til en kultur af T-celler, der efterfølgende bliver injiceret i
forskellige musemodeller.
De første eksperimenter består af in vivo kontrolforsøg, hvor naive mus bliver injicerede med
de kontrastmarkerede T-celler, for at undersøge effekten af disse over ﬂere dage i udvalgte lym-
feknuder og milt. Det er udfra disse studier, ikke muligt at påvise effekten eller lokaliseringen af
kontrastagent/T-celler. Dernæst bliver det forsøgt at tiltrække de taggede celler til et bestemt om-
råde ved brug af forskellige traumemodeller. Ved at skabe inﬂammation i et bestemt område af
musen er det muligt, at visualisere cellernes akkumulering i vævet og kontrastagentens signaldæm-
pende effekt. Dette gør sig især gældende for en hjernetraume-model. Disse resultater bekræfter at
denne protokol har potentiale til at blive anvendt i andre inﬂammatoriske sygdomsmodeller såsom
for slagtilfælde eller multipel (dissemineret) sklerose.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Inﬂammation is a strong indicator of many of the major diseases such as autoimmune diseases,
neurological disorders, transplant rejection and cancer [1]. A common goal in the medical ﬁeld is
to visualize immune cells during these immune-mediated inﬂammatory diseases to be able to make
a kinetic proﬁle of the cells as well as look at immune cell response relative to disease symptoms.
For a long time immune cells have been visualized by immunohistochemical (IHC) staining where
different proteins of interest or speciﬁc antibodies labeled with ﬂuoroscent (or enzyme) against
immune cells are introduced to a tissue sample giving very accurate results. The drawback of this
method is that a large number of animals have to be sacriﬁced at discrete time points. Despite
a large number of animals, this technique only allows static analysis. For dynamic studies, even
more animals are included because of the need of sacriﬁce at many time points to minimize poten-
tial biological variation [2]. In vivo cell tracking is therefore an emerging method for imaging cells
in their native environment and much progress has been made in this area. The technique makes
it possible to link speciﬁc inﬂammatory reactions with speciﬁc disease symptoms. This enables
reliable correlation between immune cell activity and disease progression or remission. About a
decade ago, the two-photon microscopy was introduced which enabled precise determination of
immune cell movement in lymph nodes and tumours. Unfortunately, this invasive technique with
its poor depth of about 1 mm, has limited applications for small animals.
The oldest technique for tracking immune cells in larger animals and humans is radionuclide label-
ing. However, there are cytotoxicity and patient safety issues that limits the use of this method when
during cell tracking. Also, this technique doesn’t allow anatomical imaging and must be combined
with a modality such as computer tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) which
adds to complexity and cost [1].
From the limitations listed, development of alternative methods, especially using MRI, are now
rising at full speed. MRI has the advantage of not using ionizing radiation and can safely image
deep tissues at high resolution. It has several parameters that can be optimized to regulate image
contrast. To name some, MR images can be weighted according to spin density (concentration of
water), relaxation times (T1, T2, or T2*), chemical shifts etc. MRI is further optimized by the use
of contrast agents that can affect the image contrast [2]. Two of the most common contrast agents
are paramagnetic metal complexes like Gadolinium and (ultra) small paramagnetic iron oxide (US-
PIO) particles. The USPIOs can affect the local ﬁeld inhomogeneity and show up as dark spots on
T2*-weighted images. MRI-based cell tracking involves detecting cells that show such differential

signal/contrast. Sorted or mixed cell populations can be labeled ex vivo by incubation with the
particles in culture media. Another common strategy involves labeling of circulating monocytes by
systemic injection of iron oxide nanoparticles in different experimental models such as ischemic
stroke and EAE, a multiple sclerosis (MS) model. The particles are taken up by the phagocytic
cells often found at inﬂammatory cites. This information can help enlighten the host inﬂammatory
response and provide high diagnostic value for a wide range of diseases.
Often the contrast agent are attached to ﬂuorochromes, creating dual-mode agents that can validate
the in vivo MRI cell tracking with post mortem light microscopy [1].
Several groups have shown that molecular imaging is a crucial diagnostic tool for investigating
in vivo cellular and molecular biology in many disciplines, especially inﬂammatory processes and
associated immune cells, in both humans and animal models. In a review from 2014, they list all
studies reporting in vivo molecular MRI of brain immune interactions in the last 15 years [3]. Out
of these studies more than half investigates macrophages again related to ischemic stroke, EAE and
also brain injury models. Studies have visualized MPIO-labeled macrophages inﬁltrating mouse
brains following a traumatic brain injury (TBI) using MRI. They ﬁnd that macrophages migrate and
accumulate both near the site of injury and elsewhere in the brain following TBI and recruit other
immune cells [4, 5]. Another study ﬁnds that injecting with USPIO can detect inﬁltrating immune
cells (mainly macrophages) in CNS and visualize lesions in the EAE model [6]. Another group
also using USPIO could during brain inﬂammation in MS model track inﬁltrating neutrophils,
macrophages, and activated microglia by longitudinal MRI. It was not possible to track adaptive
immune cells [7].
Only a handful of studies have succeeding in investigate adaptive immune cell, such as T-cells,
using a contrast agent and MRI techniques. It is known that T-cells play a central role in the initi-
ation and termination of inﬂammatory reactions and might be the main pathogenic cells in several
inﬂammatory diseases such as MS [8, 9, 10]. In one study T-cells were labeled with SPIO (Feru-
moxides) complexed to poly-L-Lysine (FE-PLL). The labeled cells were able to induce EAE and be
detected in the mouse spinal cord using in vivo and ex vivo cellular MRI [11]. This was conﬁrmed
by another group, who demonstrated that autoreactive T-cells access the CNS via the ﬁfth lumbar
spinal cord [12]. Another study demonstrated the use of SPIO-Molday ION Rhodamine-B (MIRB)
coupled with in vivo imaging as a valid method to track CD4+ T cells in ischemic brain injury [13].
The objective of this project is to validate whether USPIO labeled T-cells can be visualized in
naive -and trauma mouse models using non-invasive MR imaging. Secondarily, if the T-cells be-
have as normal non-tagged cells. From this, you can create a protocol that can be used to achieve
greater understanding of the kinetic proﬁle and migration of the immune cells with respect to brain
inﬂammation. MR imaging makes it possible for accurate in vivo assessment of the same subject
over time also decreasing the number of animals in experiments. The project will make use of an
earlier produced protocol for cell culturing and labeling and the migration of immune cells will
be monitored using MR imaging. The tagged T-cells will be spatiotemporal observed reﬂecting
the inﬂammation processes in the models. Correlation of immune cell migration over time to the
characteristics of the inﬂammation process, will make it possible to show the impact of speciﬁc
immune cell populations, with high detail and accuracy.

1.1
Background
1.1.1
Primary cell
As mentioned above, the project will be tracking and investigating the primary cell type called T-
lymphocyte (or T-cell). The T-cell is a type of leukocyte that plays a central role in cell-mediated
immunity. These cells carry antigen speciﬁc receptors on their cell surface to allow recognition
of foreign pathogens. There are two main subsets of T-cells, distinguished by the presence of cell
surface molecules known as CD4 -and CD8 glycoprotein.
T-cells are primarily involved in adaptive rather than innate immune responses. Adaptive immunity
is mediated by both CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells and by antibody-producing B-cells. The adaptive im-
mune system has a relatively slow response triggered by the recognition of antigens. The response
improves with time and results in the formation of immunological memory and long-lasting pro-
tection.
T-lymphocytes expressing CD4 are mostly known as T helper cells, and these are noted as be-
ing the most proliﬁc cytokine producers. CD4+ cells can differentiate into different subsets called
Th (T helper)1, Th2, Th9, Th17, Th22, Treg (regulatory T-cells), and Tfh (follicular helper T-
cells), where the phenotypes are characterized by different cytokine proﬁles. These different CD4+
subsets play a critical role in the immune and effector response functions of T-cells. Each subset
releases speciﬁc cytokines that can have either pro- or anti-inﬂammatory functions, survival or pro-
tective function. One example is that Th1-type cytokines produce the proinﬂammatory responses
which aim is to kill intracellular parasites and maintain autoimmune responses. Tumor Necrosis
Factor (TNF) and Interferon-gamma (IFN-γ) are the main Th1 cytokines and have the task of pro-
moting phagocytosis and upregulate microbial killing [14]. Another example is that Th2 releases
IL-4 that is an essential survival factor for B-lymphocytes.
CD8+ (cytotoxic) T-lymphocytes are a subset of T-cells that express a T-cell receptor (TCR) and
are responsible for killing cells directly that are infected, damaged, and dysfunctional, including
tumor cells [15].
T-cells originate in the bone marrow and later develop in the thymus. Afterwards, the mature naive

T-cells leave the thymus after a selection processes and start their circulation between the blood
and lymphatic system. Following a speciﬁc antigen exposure, the naive T-cells become activated.
The activation can happen directly by an antigen binding to T-cell (or B-cell) receptors but most
often the activation involves glycoproteins on the surface of the cells called major histocompability
complex (MHC) molecules. The MHC gene family is divided into class I and II where class I
only can be recognized by CD8 co-receptors while class II can be recognized by CD4 co-receptors.
While display of MHC class I results in destruction of the antigen-presenting cell, the MHC class
II/antigen complex stimulates other immune cells to respond to the antigen.
Following activation, the T-cells will proliferate, and differentiate into effector cells and memory
cells. The effector cells migrate to the site of infection and eliminate the pathogen by cell-mediated
cytotoxicity or cytokine secretion. In order to proliferate, differentiate, and express effector func-
tion, activated T-cells undergo a major shift in phenotype compared to their naive starting point
[16]. The effector cells are short-lived cells, while the memory cells are created for long-term sur-
vival. Memory cells can be located in the secondary/peripheral lymphoid organs (e.g. spleen) or
in the recently infected tissues. The memory cells have three main properties such as the presence
of previous expansion and activation, endurance in the absence of antigen, and increased activity
when re-exposed to antigen. During re-exposure to antigen during the second immune response,
memory T-cells undergo fast grow resulting in a more effective and faster immune response and
thereby elimination of infection compared to the primary immune response.
Unfortunately, activated T-cells not only control infections and tumors but can also contribute to
pathological processes [17]
1.1.1.1
T-cells role in inﬂammatory diseases of CNS
The inﬂammatory response is a complex sequence of events involving many of the chemical media-
tors and immune cells. It’s a cardinal host defence response to injury, tissue ischaemia, autoimmune
responses or infectious agents. Locally, within tissues outside the brain, inﬂammation manifests
by the known features of swelling, redness, heat and pain. It also includes invasion of circulating
immune cells (lymphocytes and macrophages), and induction or activation of inﬂammatory medi-
ators such as cytokines. These inﬂammatory responses have many beneﬁts in infectious states, and
when activated under normal regulated conditions for a limited period of time. However, continued
or needless inﬂammation is the cause of a large number of diseases.
In some cases inﬂammation will target different structures in the the central nervous system (CNS).
Inﬂammatory disease of CNS, is a condition where the brain and/or spinal cord become inﬂamed
that causes irritation and swelling of brain tissue or blood vessels. The brain inﬂammation can
occur for a variety of reasons. By deﬁnition, the diseases are due to primary processes in which
inﬂammation occurs without a normal trigger. For example, immune system malfunction leads to
abnormal inﬂammation and continues with no resolution if not treated [18]. Over a longer term,
this can lead to brain damage. Some examples of diseases are CNS vasculitis (inﬂammation of
the blood vessels in the brain), antibody-mediated inﬂammatory brain diseases (antibodies attack
structures in the brain that lead to inﬂammation of surrounding brain tissue) and demyelinating
conditions, such as multiple sclerosis (MS) [18]. Demyelination is the destruction of myelin, the
protective lipid-rich nerve sheath, resulting in decreased axonal transmission and eventually in ax-

onal disruption leading to sensory, motor, and cognitive problems [19].
As described previously, the T-cells are thought to have a larger role in several inﬂammatory dis-
eases. As an example, the development of MS is related to MHC class II allel expression, suggest-
ing involvement of CD4+ T-cells. It is known that the T-cells are activated and binds to proteins on
the surface of the myelin as they appear foreign. They sets an alarm and secretes proinﬂammatory
cytokines that are toxic to myelin, cause degradation of the BBB, and recruit other inﬂammatory
cells. The inﬂammatory cells and factors contributes to local tissue damage of the myelin sheaths.
The oligodendrocytes that wrap the myelin will try to remyelinate the neurons but will in the end be
overwhelmed by the persistent immune system. Several studies have investigated the experimental
autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE) model, a MS animal model. From its investigations it has
been found that Th1 cells plays an essential role and is thought to be the main pathogenic T-cells in
EAE. Further experiments have shown that others like Th17 cells, seen on Figure 1.1, and myelin
antigen speciﬁc CD8+ T-cells are also active in EAE mice and patients with MS [10, 9].
This project will handle these T helper cells by isolating them from lymph nodes and/or spleen
from a mouse and injecting them intravenously containing a label into naive -and trauma animal
models.

ﬂammatory cells into the CNS. T-cells expand and release inﬂammatory mediators which help
recruit other immune cells to the site of inﬂammation. Activation of local microglial cells and
inﬁltrating cells results in production of different cytotoxic agents which promote myelin break-

rological impairment [10].

1.1.2
Organ of interest
1.1.2.1
Central Nervous System (CNS)
CNS, Figure 1.2, is a part of the nervous system consisting of the brain and spinal cord. It is a
complex network of nerve cells responsible for sending, receiving, and analyze information from
all parts of the body. During the daily life, the spinal cord will send information to the brain using
nerve cells. The brain translates the information and controls the reaction to it. The central nervous
system is divided in white and gray matter which can be seen macroscopically on brain tissue.
The white matter consists of axons myelinated by oligodendrocytes, while the gray matter consists
of the soma of neurons and unmyelinated ﬁbers. Both tissues include a number of neuroglial
cells (where white matter contains more), which are the supporting cells of the CNS. There are
4 different types of neoroglial cells and these have very different functions. These cells account
for more than half of the brain’s weight. One type is the microglia that are a specialized form
of macrophage, involved in the immune system of the brain as well as the clearance of various
metabolites from the brain tissue. The astrocytes can both do clearance of metabolites as well
as transport of fuel and various beneﬁcial substances to neurons from the capillaries of the brain.
Upon CNS injury astrocytes will proliferate, causing gliosis, a form of neuronal scar tissue, lacking
in functional neurons. Oligodendrocytes add myelin sheaths to the axons, which allows better
and faster propagation of electrical signals along the nerves. Lastly, the ependymal cells lines the

The brain requires an enormous amount of blood to maintain normal functions. It requires contant
blood supply to meet optimal glucose and oxygen levels. Its blood supply account for about 15-

In a healthy animal/human the BBB, Figure 1.2, restricts the migration of cells and soluble molecules

expect the labeled T-cells to be present during an inﬂammation in the brain.

1.1.2.2
Lymphatic system
The lymphatic system plays a crucial role in immune responses to foreign antigens and tumors.
When damage occurs to some area of the body, protein fragments are released. The fragments
get picked up by macrophages at the site of injury. The macrophages travel through the lymphatic
system to lymph nodes that drain that region. The lymphatic system is a system of vessels that bring
ﬂuid from between the cells (interstitial ﬂuid) and white blood cells (e.g. T-cells) from the tissues
to lymph nodes, and then from the lymph nodes back into the blood circulation. About 3 L (out of
20 L) of blood ﬂuid is collected every day by the lymphatic system, which is more than half of the
total blood volume. The lymphatic system has no pump but relies on low pressure ﬂow and muscle
activation to slowly press the ﬂuid along. The protein fragments are presented to lymphocytes as
these lymphocytes trafﬁc through the lymph nodes. If the fragment matches to the receptor on the
surface of the lymphocyte, then that lymphocyte becomes activated, leaves the lymph node and go
to injury site to help protect from any bacteria or viruses that have that speciﬁc protein fragments
[23]. The lymph nodes contain T-cells that directly attack invaders and manage the immune system,
B-cells that secrete antibodies into the blood, macrophages that ingest the foreign substances, and
reticular cells that make the stroma scaffolding network that supports all the other cells in the
lymphoid tissues. Lymph nodes are divided into distinct cortex and medulla regions. The outer
part of cortex houses dividing B-cells, while its deeper areas contain mostly T-cells. The medulla
contains both types of lymphocytes. There are between 500 and 700 lymph nodes (the number of
nodes varies from individual to individual) in a human while the mouse only has about 30 with a
variable amount of red -and white blood cells depending on its size and location. Up to 80% of the
total lymph node cell population are T-cells with a CD4+:CD8+ T-cell ratio of 2:1 [24].

are excluded.

2.2
Flow Cytometry
Cytometry is the measurement of physical or chemical characteristics of cells or particles. The ﬂow
cytometry measurements are done as cells in suspension ﬂow individually in a ﬂuid stream through
a beam of light in a ﬂow cytometer instrument. Fluidics introduce the cells for interrogation with
the laser, optics generate and collect the light signals and electronics convert the optical signal to
the proportional electronic signal (voltage pulse) by photodetectors for computer analysis. This
provides discrete measurements from each cell and a distribution of the measured characteristics.
Flow cytometry is dependent on the control, collection, and separation of light.
When the cells pass through the laser they scatter laser light. Any ﬂuoroscence molecules that
are present on the cells will emit ﬂuorescence. The scattered -and ﬂuorescence light is collected by
lenses to steer the light to the appropriate detectors. The system measures both forward -and side
scatter. Forward scatter light (FSC) is proportional to cell size or cell-surface area and side scatter
light (SSC) is proportional to the shape and internal complexity of a cell. Side scatter is normally
collected at 90◦to the laser beam.
With these information, the computer can divide the heterogeneous population into individual pop-
ulation, or look at changes in cell health. It can also detect the presence of a ﬂuorochromes (like
7AAD) and quantify the amount within a cell. Normally, several ﬂuorochromes are used in an
analysis and these can all be detected at the same time, using different detectors, Figure 2.2.

2.4
Magnetic Resonance Imaging
Magnetic Resonance (MR) is a powerful non-invasive imaging modality that has experienced huge
growth over the past decades. MR offers a large number of physical parameters to image and many
instrumental parameters to set for control of image content that can lead to a superior image con-
trast compared to other modalities. MR is primarily used for constructing detailed images of the
human anatomy and physiological processes. The nature of MR is based on the interaction of spins
with three types of magnetic ﬁelds including a main ﬁeld, radio-frequency ﬁeld and linear gradient
ﬁelds [30]. As opposed to Computer Tomography (CT), MR do not make use of any ionizing radi-
ation and is therefore not considered harmful to subjects. MRI is the most important diagnostic and
prognostic technique for assessing neuroinﬂammatory phenomena such as endothelial activation,
BBB permeability alterations and immune cell trafﬁcking. It is currently the only technique that
can investigate the entire CNS in vivo. In this project, it will be used to track and visualize labeled
T-cells in naive mice as well as in different inﬂammation models.
For a technical description on MRI physics, how to create an MR image, and image contrast in
MRI, please go to Appendix A.

Before every scan a localizer sequence needs to be run. This sequence is a low resolution reference

2.4.1.2
Prescan: Shimming and Tuning
Before the real scanning, a prescan need to be performed. This is mostly run as an automated
process, but can be performed manually for improved performance. During a prescan, several
calibrations are done to get optimal execution of the speciﬁed protocol [31]. The steps include
a shimming where small adjustments are made to currents passing through gradients and higher
order shim coils to optimize B0-ﬁeld homogeneity, a tuning where resonance frequency is adjusted
to subject, and dummy cycles where multiple runs of a pulse sequence prior to acquiring data allow
a steady state magnetization to establish [31].

2.4.1.3
T2* FLASH sequence
A gradient echo can be measured by the use of a fast low angle shot (FLASH) sequence, which
is a basic measuring principle in MRI. It uses fast sequence producing signals with low tip angles
(>90◦). The FLASH sequence has the ability to incorporate or remove the effects of transverse co-
herence respectively. This is done by using a so called spoiler gradient after each echo to eliminate
the transverse magnetization (Mxy), by causing a spatially dependent phase shift. This method also
employs frequency-encoding gradient coils with a negative polarity to rephase spins after excitation
as shown on Figure 2.4. T2* relaxation is seen only with gradient-echo (GRE) imaging because
transverse relaxation caused by magnetic ﬁeld inhomogeneities is eliminated by the 180◦pulse at
spin-echo (SE) imaging. As the sequence doesn’t involve refocusing pulses, it is lot faster, using
a smaller repetition time. The overall scan time gets to be a lot smaller than for other sequences
making it suitable for dynamic imaging, for example, to track the inﬂow of a contrast medium
bolus. Because of its speed, it is not troubled so much by motion artifacts [32].

2.4.2
Contrast Agent - USPIO
MRI contrast agents are a group of contrast media used to enhance the visibility of internal body
structures in MRI. As we want to visualize the T-cells speciﬁcally, the project include the use of a
negative contrast agent causing hypointense contrast. Ultra-small particles of iron oxide (USPIO;
5-50 nm) are dextran-coated iron oxide nanoparticles that are internalized into a variety of cells,
including phagocytic cells.
USPIO are superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles that can be directly (endolymphati-
cally), indirectly (subcutaneously), or intravenously administered [32]. The paramagnetic ions are
arranged in a crystal lattice that increases the mobility of the surrounding electrons, resulting in a
considerable increase of magnetic moment. The paramagnetism induces magnetic ﬁeld inhomo-
geneities giving reduction in T1, T2, and T2* relaxation rates. On MRI scans, the iron oxide agents
gives hypointense signal drop out (dark) at all concentrations on T2- and T2*-weighted images, but
may show hyperintense (bright) signal on T1-weighted images in areas of low concentration [34].
The hypointensities are much larger than the volume occupied by the actual contrast agent parti-
cles (blooming effect). This is increasing the visibility of the contrast agent but will interfere with
visualizing the actual anatomical structures. It cannot represent true size of a lesion or labeled cells
[35]. Per mole, iron-based contrast is generally stronger than Gadolinium (Gd), the most common
positive contrast agent (Figure 2.6).

2.4.3
Imaging parameters
The design and detailed speciﬁcations of the imaging sequence depends on several factors. These
factors play an important role in the consideration of the length of signal readout (sampling of MR
signal), image contrast, and signal-to-noise ratio. A MR signal can be degraded by noise which
results from several reasons such as imperfections of the MR system and patient-related issues
(movement). The relationship between the MR signal and the amount of image noise present is
expressed as the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and is deﬁned by Equation 2.4.
SNR

signal amplitude

(2.4)
A high SNR is desirable and dependent on many parameters like slice thickness, ﬁeld-of-view
(FOV), size of image, echo time (TE), repetition time (TR), ﬂip angle, ﬁeld strength etc. Generally,
the SNR increases by the square root of the acquisition time (NEX) and linearly with the voxel
size. SNR increases with increasing FOV and decreases with wider bandwidths.
To achieve a good image resolution, thin slices with a high SNR are optimal. However, thinner
slices are introduce more noise, and the SNR therefore decreases with the slice thickness. The
poorer SNR of thin slices can be compensated for to some extent by increasing the number of ac-
quisitions or by a longer TR which again compensate the overall acquisition time [32].
Another important parameter is the bandwidth. The receiver bandwidth is the range of frequen-
cies collected by an MR system during the frequency encoding and is inversely proportional to the
sampling time. A wide bandwidth enables faster data acquisition and minimizes chemical shift
artifacts but also reduces SNR as more noise is included. Halving the bandwidth improves SNR by
about 30% [32]. With a narrow bandwidth, on the other hand, you can get problems with chemical
shift and motion artifacts, and number of slices that can be acquired for a given TR is limited.
A perfect slice proﬁle is completely rectangular in shape but that is almost impossible to
achieve. Instead, they appear less sharp at the edges and two neighbouring slices can therefore
overlap when closely spaced as illustrated on Figure 2.7. This will result in the RF pulse not only
exciting the slice of interest but also in neighbouring slices. Such interference is known as cross-
talk and produces saturation effects that reduces the SNR [32]. You can therefore, depending on
the sequence used, acquire a small interslice gap between slices.

As mentioned, FOV also has a large impact on SNR and image resolution. When matrix size
is held constant, the FOV determines the size of the pixels within the image such that smaller FOV
gives smaller pixel size. With a constant FOV, a ﬁner matrix (with more pixels) will give a better
resolution and a matrix with fewer pixels results in poorer spatial resolution. Theoretically, a great
image resolution is then achieved by a big matrix containing many data points. A problem is though
that SNR decrease by the size of the voxel. Also, the scan time increases with matrix size and the
sequence inﬂuences the contrast and the perception of different tissues [32].
The scan parameters in a sequence such as TE, TR, and ﬂip angle are also affecting the SNR.
The SNR increases with long TR but the T1 effect is also lost at longer TRs while the scan time
goes up. The SNR can also increase by decreasing the TE but with a too short TE, the T2 contrast
is lost and susceptibility effects occur. The ﬂip angle is proportional to SNR, so when it goes up,
the SNR goes up to a point [32]. Under steady-state conditions, the Ernst angle is the angle for the
optimum SNR, when the ﬂip angle is less than 90◦.
Lastly, an important measurement of image quality is the contrast-to-noise ratio (CNR) that is de-
ﬁned by Equation 2.5. CNR depends on the difference in signal between two areas of interest.

2.4.4
Coil choice and scanning setup
With the help from the team at the NMR Core Facility Center at Panum, two different setups were
constructed and used for the scans. The ﬁrst setup is visualized in Figure 2.8 and made use of a
volume coil making it possible to visualize larger area of the mice. Later, it was tried to use a cryo
probe (surface coil) to get higher sensitivity in the images. This setup used another bed which can
be seen in Figure 2.9.
In general, coils generate the oscillating magnetic ﬁeld B1. They can either be transmit/receive
(typically body coils with birdcage designs) or receive-only (typically surface coils). The latter
requires combined use with a transmitter coil which typically is a body coil built into the scanner.
The big differences between the volume coil and surface coil are the homogeneity and SNR. The
purpose of a volume transmit RF coil is to generate a uniform circularly polarized RF magnetic
ﬁeld throughout the entire imaging volume. The ﬁeld is required to ﬂip the magnetization vector
by a uniform angle. On the other hand, the main purpose of the surface coil is not B1 uniformity,
but to maximize SNR. Therefore, the volume coils have a relatively low SNR since noise is re-
ceived from full volume but a high homogeneity. Surface coils have higher SNR locally but lower
homogeneity than the volume coil [36].
==PAGE:29==
CHAPTER 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

The used surface coil is a cryo probe which is a technology where the coil is cooled with a
stream of He gas. This increases the sensitivity of the probe coil and reduces the level of thermal
noise generated by components of the signal receiver itself as well as the electronic circuits. The
result is a 4 time increase in the SNR compared to a probe at room temperature. The scan time of
the experiments gets to be 1/16 the amount compared to a conventional probe. Also, experiments
can be run with a sample concentration that is 1/4 less in the same amount of time as on a conven-
tional probe [37].

All surgeries and scans on mice in vivo were performed under isoﬂurane anesthesia. During MRI
scan, the animal’s respiration was continually monitored by a small animal monitoring and gating

2.5.2
Inﬂammation: Trauma models
Brain inﬂammation is rapidly activated by infection, trauma, or occur because of chronic neu-
rodegenerative disorders, such as multiple sclerosis. During an immune response in the CNS,
the inﬂammatory cells, microglial and monocytes/machrophages, are activated and transformed to
ameboid brain macrophages. Astroglial cells are also activated and T-cells will begin to inﬁltrate
the damaged area. This inﬂammatory response is managed by the production of several cytokines
and growth factors by both the resident and inﬁltrating cells and increases oxidative stress and cell
death [38].
To be able to investigate how the T-cells react and distribute under an inﬂammatory condition, it is
chosen to perform and analyze different types of trauma models in both brain and other parts of the
body.
2.5.2.1

As a pilot study, a mouse is given a trauma, using a clamp, in the right ear to create inﬂammation.
The other ear functions as control. ∼360.000 labeled T-cells was injected a day before during the
trauma. It is expected to see edema and that labeled T-cells will appear at the site after some time.
MR scans are done to get clear before and after results on the effect of injection of labeled T-cells
and trauma. Scans were done according to Table 2.4.

2.5.2.2
Brain and muscle damage
To study how T-cells accumulate during a brain trauma, an invasive brain damage is executed in the
right side of the brain hemisphere of a healthy mouse. This is also to verify that the labeled T-cells
will act as a normally functioning T-cell, and appear at the site of inﬂammation. The damage is
created by the use of a cryolesion procedure. The mouse are cryolesioned under anesthesia. The
skull is exposed and a focal cryoinjury is done at the surface of the brain by applying a dry ice
pellet for a longer time period as done in [38]. In the same side of the mouse, a muscle damage at
the ﬂank is also done for increased inﬂammation condition. The other side of the mouse, without
any damage, will function as the control. Scans are done 3 days after both injection and trauma
to give time for inﬂammation to rise and labeled cells to proliferate and accumulate at the site.
It is expected to see a signiﬁcant difference in signal intensity between the two areas both on the
T2 -and T2*-weighted images. Unlike previous experiments, the cryo probe setup is now used to
record images with higher sensitivity than with the volume coil. With this technique, it might be
possible to observe clusters of T-cells in the injured area of the brain.
To verify results, the mouse is sacriﬁced and the brain is removed for histological assessment using
protocol described in Section 2.3.
2.5.2.3
Local inﬂammation model
To create a model with an area of increasing inﬂammation without being as invasive as the brain
damage model, a local inﬂammation model is carried out. A local inﬂammation is applied by sub-
dermally injecting a 100 µL oil-droplet emulsion suspending mycobacterium tuberculosis in the
neck of a mouse. It is expected that a part of the immune cells will recognize the mycobacterium
and get activated both in- and outside of the area and create a powerful local inﬂammatory re-
sponse. The model will only create minor bleeding compared to the brain damage model and it
will be possible to distinguish the emulsion from any bleeding caused by the injection. As number
of T-cells are expected to increase over time, because of proliferation, scans are acquired 1, 4, and
7 days after injection. As the emulsion is placed subdermal, the focus area will be around the skin.
Because the skin contains some fat, a higher bandwidth (100-150 kHz) and no fat suppression is
needed, in the MR scans, to avoid any chemical shift artifacts. The cryo probe setup is also used in
this experiment.
All results from the trauma models can be found in Section 3.2. Section 4.1.2 includes a dis-
cussion on the results.

Chapter 4
Discussion
This chapter will contain an analysis and discussion on results achieved during the control -and
trauma model scans. Furthermore, experimental setup and protocols will be discussed.
4.1
Result analysis
Image brightness between the different scan days can sometimes show remarkable difference (as in
Figure 3.4) and it can make it hard to compare the time-course changing between several scan time
points. Measuring the noise level and subtract to the data can normalize it and to make it easier to
investigate the results. This was done for all calculations of relative difference between days in the
different studies.
Since the system calibrates at every startup and adjusts signal gain according to different param-
eters, it is not possible to directly compare the intensity of images acquired at different days. In
order to overcome this, a phantom could be included in every scanning. In a previous study, it
was experimented a bit with different phantoms, consisting of labeled cells embedded in different
percentages of agarose. It was not possible to create a stable and homogeneous solution, which
resulted in consistent intensities across the suspension. Given that the primary aim with this project
was to establish a protocol that can be used to detect and monitor cellular inﬁltrates, without es-
timations of actual inﬂammatory load and number of T-cells, we decided to exclude the use of
phantoms in this set of scans. For future quantitative purposes, a homogeneous water or agarose
phantom should be included in every scan to be used as reference. Agarose is known for having a
T2 relaxation time similar to human tissue.
When inspecting the measured intensity proﬁles, it was considered to do normalization of the gray
values in the ROI by dividing its values by the mean value of a reference tissue such as muscle tis-
sue. The reason why this was not done is that several concerns arises when dealing with muscle for
reference after i.v. cell injection. One is that labeled cells might appear considerably in the muscles
as well which would create signal intensity change and the tissue would no longer be considered
"normal". Also, animal condition and blood oxygen level also affect the signals in the muscles. It
was concluded that no better comparison would come out by using this reference.

4.1.1
Control
From the non-speciﬁc labeling pilot study imaged on Figure 3.1, it is shown how the brain tumor
appears as an abnormal area on both the T2- and T2*-weighted image. On the T2-weighed image
the tumor appears as a high intensity area caused by edema. After injection of contrast agent, a
similar image was measured after 24 h. On Figure 3.2 the tumor now appears completely dark,
on the T2-weighted RARE image, while on the T2*-weighted FLASH image, the contrast agent
appears at the edges of the tumor. This indicates that the tumor has broken down the BBB which
allows free passage to the contrast agents. The contrast agent surrounds and disrupt the signal in
the area of the tumor. It is well known that USPIOs (or labeled cells) can be seen like “circle” shape
distribution surrounding the tumor due to the heterogeneous structure and heterogeneous angiogen-
esis of it [39]. But it can be hard to explain what happened in the dark region in the T2-weighted
image. The interpretation of the MR contrast needs a lot of care because there are so many reason
of change in the contrast. One hypothesis of the hypointense signal in the T2-weighted image is
abnormal angiogenesis in the tumor. If so, it might make sense the blood content have longer T1
rather than USPIO and then it can be seen in brighter signal in T2*-weighting (as seen in the hy-
perintense signal of inner part of tumor).
The overall contrast of the T2-weighted image is bad and signal is lost in many areas because
of the large amount of contrast agents ﬂoating around. It was therefore not possible to visualize
any phagocytic immune cells as done in other studies [6]. For a better image and tracking of cells, a
smaller volume of Molday should probably have been used and some sequence optimization could
have been done to get less noisy images. From this study it is conﬁrmed that the contrast agent does
indeed cause signal attenuation in the areas it is located. It should be noted that this study can’t be
used as a reference of how Molday looks in a naive mouse, as this one had a brain tumor. It would
have been nice to acquire one more scan of a real naive mouse, without any tumor, and inject the
Molday. This would give a more real image on how the contrast agent would appear in the body.
Also, the chance of phagocyte cells picking up the agent would be higher.

differences in the chosen areas of the lymphatic system, before and after injection. Looking at the

4.2
Methodological Considerations
The inconclusive results from the naive mouse studies are for the most part caused by some method-
ological limitations, such as missing reference (or phantom study), the number of mice used in the
different experiments and inconsistency in number of scan days. Reliable results are needed for an
accurate and effective interpretation of images and mapping of the T-cell distribution with respect
to inﬂammation progress.
Because of system calibrations and missing reference in every scan, it is not possible to do an
accurate comparison between the different scan days. It was expected that the control mice would
show some difference in signal intensity between the days in organs of interest, but this could not
be conﬁrmed. Also, using more mice for each experiment with same scan days could have ac-
counted for potential variations between the mice, where possible incorrect results could have been
modiﬁed out, leaving more room for mistakes while giving more reliable results and being able to
do some statistical analysis.
For the cell culturing, incubation and labeling part of the study many challenges occurred as the
cells had trouble with staying alive both before and after injection into mice. Why this was the case
is unknown as the protocol had been used before in other studies. A reason might be due to lack of
experience with laboratory work before starting the project, possibly allowing for more mistakes
to happen. A big problem with the ﬁrst studies was that the number of cells were too low and
better results might have been collected if more cells had stayed alive. It was decided after several
attempts, to get professionals to handle the cells before injection to make sure many cells could
be injected. A lot of time was saved by doing this and much better results were collected in the
following trauma studies.
Even though several optimization were done on both labeling, number of cells and the MR scan
protocol -and coil setup, it was not possible to use MRI alone without some histological assess-

Chapter 5
Conclusion and future work
Several animal models were used to visualize and investigate the migration of labeled T-cells. From
the control studies using naive mice, it was not possible with complete certainty to visualize the
T-cells themselves or the attenuating effect from the contrast agent. This could be caused by a too
low number of cells injected. If T-cells accumulate in the whole body, only small amounts will go
to focus areas and thereby not being picked up by the scanner. Another reason is because the cells
did not survive the injection into the tail vein and therefore degraded instead. The trauma models
including brain damage and local inﬂammation in neck were able to identify clusters of accumu-
lating cells and/or attenuation in signal in areas with inﬂammation, conﬁrming that this protocol
has potential to be used in several other inﬂammatory disease model such as ischemic stroke or
multiple sclerosis.
For future work the aim is to do the injection of labeled T-cells in the multiple sclerosis animal
model (EAE) to see how T-cells in general and its subtypes react and distribute in CNS during
several days. As explained, it was not possible to do this part of the study during the project time,
as necessary animal licenses were missing. Information on the EAE model and a proposal on ex-
periment protocol can be found in Appendix C.
As a parallel study you could do the injection of labeled T-cells in CSF instead of through the
blood. It is known that along with the BBB, the blood-CSF barrier (BCSFB) also play an important
role in brain inﬂammation, as it is a site of T-cell invasion into the CSF. At the same time, it could
also be interesting to look at choroid plexus as studies have proposed that immune cells go into the
choroid plexus stroma from the blood vessels and then enter the CSF. Afterwards, they inﬁltrate
the periventricular areas [22].

Appendix A
MRI description and protocol
In magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), you make use of the signal from hydrogen atoms to generate
an image. A hydrogen nuclei consists of a single proton which posses a positive charge and a
property called spin, meaning it rotates about its axis. As a rotating mass with a positive charge,
the proton additionally has magnetic moment and behaves like a small magnet as seen in Figure
A.1.
Figure A.1 – A hydrogen atom containing an electron cloud, surrounding a nucleus of a single
proton. Protons have an angular momentum (spin) that generates a magnetic ﬁeld [42].
Protons can therefore be affected by external magnetic ﬁelds and electromagnetic waves and,
when it moves, cause a voltage in a receiver coil. When an external magnetic ﬁeld (B0) acts on a
hydrogen nuclei, the spins will align with the direction of the ﬁeld. At the same time the nuclei will
undergo precession which is shown in Figure A.2.
56
==PAGE:63==
APPENDIX A. MRI DESCRIPTION AND PROTOCOL

Figure A.2 – A spinning proton precesses about an externally applied magnetic ﬁeld [42].
This precession is done at a characteristic speed called the Larmor frequency. The Larmor
frequency (ω0) is proportional to the strength (B0) of the magnetic ﬁeld and is given by the Larmor
equation:
ω0 = γ0 ·B0
(A.1)
Where γ0 is the gyromagnetic ratio [MHz/T].
Under steady-state conditions, a slightly larger fraction spins aligns parallel to the main magnetic
ﬁeld, Figure A.3. The protons that align in the same direction as the ﬁeld are in a low energy state
whereas protons aligning in opposite direction are in a high energy state. These opposing protons
will cancel out each others magnetic ﬁeld but because it is easier to line up in a low energy state, a
small amount of parallel protons will not be canceled out. This small difference is what produces
the measurable net magnetization and is represented by the net magnetization vector (NMV).
Figure A.3 – Steady-state condition of magnetization in the presence of a main static magnetic
ﬁeld B0 [30].

A.1
Excitation
Energy can be introduced into this stable spin system by applying an electromagnetic wave of the
same frequency as the Larmor frequency. The electromagnetic wave is generated in a powerful
radio transmitter (coil) and applied to the object to be visualized. This energy absorption process is
called excitation of the spins and results in the longitudinal magnetization (Mz) being tipped away
from the z-axis toward the transverse xy-plane perpendicular to the direction of the main magnetic
ﬁeld [32]. As can be seen in Figure A.4, it is tipped by a radiofrequency (RF) pulse by precisely
90◦. When the transverse magnetization (Mxy) is present, it precesses about the z-axis, creating an
varying voltage of the same frequency as the Larmor frequency in a receiver coil [32]. This gives
rise to the MR signal.
Figure A.4 – Radiofrequency ﬁeld B1 tuned to Larmor frequency and applied in the xy-plane.
Induces nutation of magnetization vector as it tips away from the z-axis [30].
A.2
Relaxation
After the spins have been excited, the signal will begin to decay and the system will return to a
stable state. The decay happens because of two independent processes called T1 relaxation and T2
relaxation.
Following a 90 ◦excitation where Mz(0) = 0, we have that:
Mz = Mo(1−e−t/T1)
(A.2)
As the transverse magnetization decays, the longitudinal magnetization (Mz) is slowly restored.
This is known as T1 recovery or longitudinal relaxation, Figure A.5. The nuclei can return to the
ground state by giving up energy to their surroundings (the lattice) which is why this relaxation is
also called spin-lattice relaxation. The time constant for the recovery is called T1 and depends on
==PAGE:65==
APPENDIX A. MRI DESCRIPTION AND PROTOCOL
59
both the strength of the external ﬁeld and internal motion of the molecules [32]. As greater energy
exchange is required at higher frequencies, this relaxation effect will take longer at higher ﬁelds.
Hence T1 values lengthen with increasing B0. Thermal equilibrium occurs when Mz is restored to
Mo which is when the lowest energy state is reached [30].
Also, after a 90 ◦excitation we have that Mxy(0) = Mo and therefore
Mxy = Moe−t/T2
(A.3)
Right after excitation many spins will be in the state of phase coherence, meaning the spins will
be in phase. This phase coherence is gradually lost after excitation and the individual magnetization
vectors will cancel each other out instead of adding [32]. The vector sum i.e. the transverse magne-
tization will drop down and ﬁnally disappear also stopping the MR signal, Figure A.6. Therefore,
the decay also happens because of so called dephasing. Phase coherence loss appear because of
energy transfer between spins and inhomogenities in the magnetic ﬁeld and by the person being im-
aged. The ﬁrst reason is explained by spins precessing faster or slower according to the magnetic
ﬁeld variations they experience. The overall result is an increasing loss of phase. It is a process due
to pure spin-spin interaction. The dephasing will happen on a time-scale T2. The second reason is
that the inhomogenities will contribute to dephasing making the signal decay even faster than for
T2. This type of decay occurs over the time-scale T2* which is much shorter than T2. The loss of
the MR signal due to T2* effects results in a signal called free induction decays (FID), a damped
sine wave [32]. The FID is just one of four basic types of NMR signals produced in different ways
as shown in Table A.1. More information on some of these can be found in Section A.7.

A.3
Image contrast
Depending on what to investigate, it is possible to inﬂuence the MR image to get different contrasts.
Three features contributes to the intensity of the MR signal and thereby the contrast. These are the
proton density (number of spins per unit volume), and T1 -and T2 time constants. Depending on
which of these three parameters you emphasize, very different contrast and thereby information in
the image is available. This provides a way of discriminate between soft tissues and potentially
make a diagnosis.
A T1-weighted image can be accessed by adjusting the repetition time (TR). TR is the interval
between two successive excitations of the same slice. If the TR is long, more spins will have the
time to rotate back to the z-plane resulting in the growth of the longitudinal magnetization. Hence,
more longitudinal magnetization can be excited with the next pulse making a larger MR signal [32].
When the TR is short, the image is very much affected by T1 (now T1-weighted). Tissue with a
short T1 will relax faster and more signal will be produced at next excitation. These tissues will
appear bright on the image. Tissues with longer T1 will not be able to relax much between two
pulses giving less signal and appearing dark on the image. If TR is too long, all tissues will have
time to return to equilibrium and have similar signal meaning less T1-weighted.
Another time factor is the echo time (TE) which inﬂuences the scale of T2-weighting. TE is the
interval between application of the excitation pulse and collection of the MR signal [32]. With a
short echo time, the signal differences between tissues will be small as T2 relaxation has just begun
and only a small signal decay has happened. With this you get a low T2-weighting. Making the
TE longer, you can distinguish between different signal intensities on resulting MR image. Tissues
with short T2 will appear dark as the signal is almost lost and tissues with longer T2 will have a
stronger signal and appear bright.
Lastly, proton density-weighted (PD) images are images whose contrast is mostly determined
by the proton density of the tissues imaged. They are acquired with a fairly long repetition time
(to minimize T1 effects) and a fairly short echo time (to minimize T2 effects). PD sequences are
especially useful if you want to evaluate structures with low signal intensities such as the bones or
connective tissue structures like ligaments and tendons. Proton density weighting is often used for
high-resolution imaging [32].
A.4
Slice selection and spatial encoding
As a tomographic technique, MR imaging generates cross-sectional images of the human body.
The excitation pulse is therefore delivered only to the slice to be imaged and not necessarily to the
whole body. The excitation of a slice and identiﬁcation of site of origin of a signal within the slice
depends on that the Larmor frequency is proportional to the ﬁeld strength. To actually be able to
excite a selected slice, the magnetic ﬁeld must be made inhomogeneous along the z-direction by
the use of a gradient coil, Figure A.7. Then the Larmor frequency will change slightly along the
z-axis and each slice will get at unique frequency. Gradients are extra magnetic ﬁeld that can add
or subtract to the main ﬁeld. The steepness of the gradient (together with a constant bandwidth)

Also, the slice position can be deﬁned by changing the center frequency of the RF pulse.

respectively [30].
It’s very important to know about the spatial position of a MR signal. You get this informa-
tion by the use of spatial encoding. Spatial encoding consists of two steps: phase encoding and
frequency encoding. Doing phase encoding, a gradient can be applied in the y-direction after the
spins have been excited. This can alter the Larmor frequency of the spins according to there po-
sition along that gradient. This results in a so called phase shift of the spins relative to each other
[32]. When the gradient is turned off, the spins will return to their normal precession rate but will
be behind or ahead relative to their previous state. The phase will vary across the y-axis. With
frequency encoding a gradient can be applied along the x-axis. By increasing the strength from one
side to the other, the Larmor frequency of the spins changes so one side will precess slower than the
other. Collecting the MR signal while the frequency encoding gradient is on, a frequency spectrum

phase encoding together enable a spatial identiﬁcation of each volume element.
Data from the signals is stored in k-space. K-space has two axes with the horizontal axis
(kx) representing the frequency information and the vertical axis (ky) the phase information. K-
space represents the MR image before Fourier transformation (FT) is performed. One measurement
equals one line in k-space and a line is acquired for each phase-encoding step, Figure A.9. The more
lines you have to create, the longer the scan time will take. In order to create more lines in k-space
the amplitude of the gradient strength in the phase encoding should be increased. Chosen frequency
value will determine how many time to sample the data in the frequency direction. This will not
increase the scan time because the frequency are sampled in microseconds at the echo.
Data in the center of k-space determines contrast in the image while the outer lines contains spatial
information.

A.5
Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and image resolution
Images from MRI can be strongly affected by noise resulting from a various of causes like inho-
mogenities, thermal noise, body movement etc. The relationship between the MR signal and the
amount of noise in the image is expressed as the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). A high SNR is de-
sirable in MRI and is dependent on many factors such as slice thickness, ﬁeld-of-view, size of the
image, scan parameters, strength of the ﬁeld etc. The signal will be almost proportional to the ﬁeld
strength as applying a higher magnetic ﬁeld strength increases longitudinal magnetization. This
is because more protons will align along the main axis of the magnetic ﬁeld. This results in an
increase in SNR. This can be used to improve the spatial resolution or scan time [32].
Image resolution is deﬁned as the amount of detail seen in our image and detail is the ability to
distinguish between structures. When looking at an image, it consists of a matrix of pixels or vox-
els of data. The smaller the pixel/voxel, the easier it is to distinguish structures. By changing scan
parameters to create smaller pixels/voxel’s of data, image resolution will increase. One important
parameter to change the resolution is the slice thickness that determines how much tissue to sample
==PAGE:69==
APPENDIX A. MRI DESCRIPTION AND PROTOCOL
63
within one slice. Increasing the slice thickness means including more types of tissues causing par-
tial voluming artifact (image blurring). On the other hand, by decreasing slice thickness you create
a more detailed and a better representation of the anatomy that is sampled. Thinner slices decrease
the size of voxel size and thereby increase resolution.
The image matrix is used to describe the pixel’s/voxel’s of data in the image and hold signal inten-
sity collected from the subject. By increasing the number of frequency and phase lines (with a ﬁxed
FOV), pixels will get smaller. This will increase image resolution. Decreasing the image matrix
will take pixels out of the image which will decrease the image resolution. Changing FOV will
inﬂuence the image matrix as smaller FOV will make pixels smaller to ﬁt and increase resolution.
On the other hand, an increase of FOV will make the pixels expand to ﬁt the larger FOV. This will
decrease the image resolution.
Increase in resolution also means to measure more spatial frequencies than signal frequencies. This
produce an image with a less SNR. So, increasing resolution means decrease in the SNR. When
optimizing the resolution, you start with slice thickness, then change FOV and lastly change matriz
size. Afterwards, the SNR will have decreased but can be compensated for by either increasing
number of averages (increase scan time) or by decreasing the receiver bandwidth (can introduce
chemical shifts artifact).
A.6
Instrumentation
As already mentioned above, the MR scanner consists of several major components. These are
visualized in Figure A.10
Figure A.10 – Diagram of the MR system [30].
The magnet creating the main ﬁeld B0 must be very strong and typically ranges from 0.1 to 3
T. It should be very stable and not allow ﬂuctuations in ﬁeld strength. About 95% of all MR sys-
tems used today have superconducting magnets which are made of niobium-titanium alloy whose
resistance to current ﬂow is eliminated when cooled to near absolute zero. The cooling is done by
cryogens which usually is liquid helium. Imaging process assumes a linear relationship between
ﬁeld and position so ﬁeld inhomogeneity will cause wrong registration of the signal. Therefore,
magnetic ﬁeld homogeneity is a primary consideration in MRI. To achieve an optimal homogeneity,
it is often necessary to make adjustments known as shimming. Shims are used to cancel unwanted
spatial variations of ﬁeld, which improve the overall ﬁeld homogeneity. This can be done either
passively by placing magnetic materials at locations within the magnet and on the outer surface of
the scanner or actively by the use of auxiliary shim coils that are added to the ﬁeld to improve the
overall uniformity [32].

The gradient system consists of three coils that create a linear ﬁeld inhomogenity in both the
x-y-and z direction. It is used for slice selection and spatial encoding. The changing magnetic ﬁelds
generated when the gradients are switched on lead to the common "banging" sound present during
a MR scan. Again, this system must be very stable to prevent any image distortion. The overall
performance of the gradient coils is measured by three parameters: maximum gradient strength,
rise time and slew rate where the slew rate is given by the fraction of peak gradient strength and
rise time (Figure A.11).
Figure A.11 – Key gradient speciﬁcations: Slew rate is deﬁned as peak gradient strength / rise
time [45].
The radiofrequency system consists of a powerful RF generator and sensitive receiver. Making
the system stable ensures that no distortion will inﬂuence the frequency and phase of the signal
giving a non-blurred image [32]. This system contain the transmit -and receiver coils that delivers
pulses that correspond to the resonant frequency of hydrogen atoms and pick up the signal and
amplify it respectively.
A.7
MRI sequences
A sequence is a MR measurement method and there are many of them. From a sequence echoes
or signals are gathered to construct an image. Echoes can be of two types: gradient -or spin echo.
The gradient echoes are undoing of phase shifts from gradient ﬁelds and spin echoes are undoing
of phase shifts from ﬁeld inhomogenities and chemical shift [30].
As mentioned earlier, a single RF pulse generates a free induction decay (FID), but two succes-
sive RF pulses produce a spin echo (SE). The SE represents regeneration of spin phase information
apparently lost during the decay of the FID. By the use of a second RF pulse, certain dephased
components of the original FID can be refocused into a SE [46]. The spin echo sequence, Figure
A.12, is one of the ﬁrst developed sequences that use a 90◦slice-selective RF pulse for the excita-
tion. As dephasing occurs, because some spins precess faster than other, a 180◦RF pulse is applied
to refocus the spins at half of the TE. Thus, after the last half of the TE is done, all spins will be
aligned and an echo is formed. Spin echo sequences gives a great image quality exactly because the
effects of the static ﬁeld inhomogeneities are removed by the refocusing pulse [32]. SE sequences
are still used as the standard sequences for acquiring T1-weighted or PD-weighted images.
==PAGE:71==
APPENDIX A. MRI DESCRIPTION AND PROTOCOL
65
Figure A.12 – Spin-Echo Sequence [30].
The second type of sequence that is used frequently is called a gradient echo (GRE) sequence.
Instead of using pairs of RF pulses to produce an echo, this method employ gradient coils. A
frequency-encoding gradient with negative polarity is applied to rephase the spins after excitation
to get an echo, Figure A.13. Because this sequence doesn’t use a refocusing pulse, it is a lot faster
and a smaller TR can be used. The overall scan time gets to be a lot smaller than for a SE sequence
[32]. Because of this feature it not troubled so much by motion artifacts. Some disadvantages of
this method is that a short TR also limits the time for T1 relaxation which may lead to saturation
and a reduction in SNR. Also because of the lack of a refocusing pulse, the inhomogeneities are
not compensated for and the signal decays with T2*.
Figure A.13 – Gradient Echo Sequence. The echo is peaking at 2τ [30].
The T2* contrast of GRE images is strongly affected by TE, and should be as short as possible
to achieve optimal T1 weighting. A longer TE is chosen to emphasize T2* contrast. This contrast
can be used to detect calciﬁcations or stocks of blood products in for example connective tissues
with a very short T2. When using a very fast sequence with very short repetition times, it is likely
that some of the signal will be left over from every excitation. It is necessary to remove this signal
when a T1-weighted image is needed. This method is called spoiling and is done by turning on the
slice-select gradient one more time to dephase the spins before the next RF pulse is applied [32].

A.8
MR Contrast Agents
MRI contrast agents are a group of contrast media used to enhance the visibility of internal body
structures in MRI. As described above, the image contrast in MRI is a result of differences in the
signal intensity between the tissues. The contrast is set by properties of the tissues and the scanner,
where especially the pulse sequence is a dominant factor. Contrast agents must have a speciﬁc
proﬁle with the right properties to be able to alter the contrast. They consists of paramagnetic,
superparamagnetic or ferromagnetic metal ions that are able to either directly change the proton
density of a chosen tissue or indirectly change the local magnetic ﬁeld or the resonant properties
(T1 and T2 recovery) [32]. The magnetic ﬁelds can be changed by unpaired electron spins of the
agent affects that surrounds hydrogen nuclei of the tissue.
Contrast agents can either be a positive or negative contrast medium depending on how they
affect the signal. An agent that enhances the signal by speeding up the recovery of longitudinal
magnetization resulting in stonger MR signal is positive while a negative medium produces signal
loss by dephasing the protons beyond normal FID and thus shorten T2 further. Examples of a
negative medium are superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles (SPIO).

Appendix C
EAE: Animal model of multiple sclerosis
To study the pathophysiology of multiple sclerosis, several disease models have been developed.
One of the best known and most applied is the experimental autoimmune encephalitis (EAE) animal
model. The EAE model is regarded as a useful general MS model for preclinical therapy develop-
ment. EAE has helped clarify immunopathogenic mechanisms for the development of immunother-
apies. It is an inﬂammatory and demyelinating disorder of the central nervous system (CNS). In
most EAE models, the primary mediators of the autoimmunity are CNS-inﬁltrating CD4+ T-cells
speciﬁc for one or more myelin proteins. Its histopathologically is characterized by demyelination,
axonal damage, and inﬂammation, where the most common cell type is the macrophages [47].
EAE is often induced by immunization with myelin peptides such as myelin basic protein
(MBP) and myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein (MOG). These proteins are normally located at
the myelin sheets in the CNS. Demyelination is produced by injection of the proteins or peptides
from such protein emulsiﬁed in an adjuvant such as complete Freund’s adjuvant (CFA) [47]. The
presence of the adjuvant allows the generation of inﬂammatory responses to the protein/peptides.
In most protocols, mice are coinjected with pertussis toxin (PT) to break down the blood-brain bar-
rier and allow immune cells access to the CNS tissue. This immunization leads to multiple small
disseminated lesions of demyelination and micro-necroses in the brain and spinal cord and the on-
set of clinical symptoms.
EAE can follow either relapsing–remitting (RR) or chronic (CH) disease courses, which can
be said to approximate RR and primary progressive MS pathways, respectively. A short deﬁnition
and time course of the different clinical pathways of MS can be found in Table C.1 and in Figure
C.2. About 85% of MS patients are diagnosed with the RR MS type and 10% with the CH MS type.
As further work for this projekt, we would like to (active) immunized C57B/6 mice (with same
sex and age) with 200 µg MOG35-55 peptide emulsiﬁed in CFA (1:1) on day 0. 100 µl in each
ﬂank. On day 1 and 2, 2 doses of PT of 500ng/mL is injected as illustrated on Figure C.1. This
method will result in a chronic type of EAE where the illness typically will be observed on day
9-14 with a peak of severity at days 12-19. The chronic type of EAE is chosen because it has a
more linear time course with no periods of relapses where the mouse can appear more healthy. This
makes it easier to analyze and track the T-cells as they will appear active all the time.
