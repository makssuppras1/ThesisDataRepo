ABSTRACT 
The building stock is a key factor in reducing the energy consumption in the world and a 
mayor focus towards the energy renovation of existing buildings has been addressed in 
recent years. 
In Denmark, it is especially interesting the renovation of multi-story buildings from 1850-
1950 because of their energy saving potential. Around 75% of the existing Danish 
building stock was erected before 1979, when the first insulation requirements were 
introduced. Ensuring that walls have the proper levels of insulation is fundamental in 
energy renovation of heritage buildings. However the use of external insulation in 
historical buildings is not an option, since the exterior appearance of the building has to 
be preserved. The only option to improve the energy performance of the façade is 
therefore by applying interior façade insulation. Nevertheless, this measure reduces the 
drying potential of the wall and increases the risk of condensation of water vapour that 
penetrates from the interior. The moisture penetration can be avoided by using a vapour 
barrier which will reduce the drying to the interior and most likely lead to moisture 
problems behind the insulation and in the beam ends embedded in the masonry. 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the issues arising from the application of interior 
thermal insulation in traditional Danish buildings from the period 1850-1950, built with 
massive masonry walls and embedded wooden floor beams. 
The investigation used detailed hygrothermal simulations with the software Delphin to 
investigate the risk of mould growth behind the insulation and in the wooden beam 
construction due to altered moisture and temperature conditions when internal insulation 
is applied. Additionally the hygrothermal simulations were combined with the 
measurements from a test apartment located in Copenhagen that was renovated with 
internal insulation, and were later used to simulate worst case scenarios. 
The measurements showed mould growth in the beam ends and in the south-west 
oriented wall. As expected, the results from the worst case scenarios suggested a high 
risk of mould growth at the beam ends and walls, for north and west orientations and 
different rain exposure coefficients. 
Additionally a new method was developed and modelled in Delphin with the purpose of 
finding a general solution to the problems originated in the wall concerning changing 
moisture conditions when adding post insulation.

1 Introduction  
 
1.1 Background 
According to the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD), buildings account 
about 40% of the total energy consumption in Europe (Concerted Action Energy 
Performance of Buildings Directive, 2014) contributing in a large share to greenhouse 
gas emissions.  
The European Union introduced in 2002 the Energy Performance in Buildings Directive 
(Directive 2002/91/EC, EPBD), the main legislative instrument in the area of energy 
efficiency in buildings in Europe, and the recast of the directive in 2010 (Directive 
2010/31/EU) to ensure that all EU countries reduce the energy consumption of their 
buildings by moving towards new and retrofitted nearly-zero energy buildings by 2020 
(Concerted Action Energy Performance of Buildings Directive, 2014). A number of 
measures described in the directive will help the EU to improve its competitiveness and 
security of supply, as well as reducing the carbon emissions. 
A large potential to meet the 2020 reduction targets can be found in the existing building 
stock as new buildings only represent a small share of the total building stock in Europe. 
For instance around 75% of the existing Danish building stock was erected before 1979, 
when the first insulation requirements were introduced (Tommerup, H., & Svendsen, S., 
2006), containing hardly any insulation. 
As the building stock is a key factor in reducing the energy consumption in the world, a 
mayor focus towards the energy renovation of existing buildings has been addressed in 
recent years and is reinforced both in the European level with the aforementioned EPBD 
and to a national level, through the Strategy for Energy Renovation of Buildings 
developed by the Danish Government (The Danish Government, 2014).  
Although the EPBD encourages ambitious renovations, it only covers the renovation of 
existing buildings larger than 1000 m2, however the Danish implementation covers all 
existing buildings, including single family houses (Tommerup, H., & Svendsen, S., 2006). 
The Danish initiatives encourage the energy companies to meet the targeted energy 
reduction through the energy renovation of existing buildings and businesses. The 
strategy establishes some minimum requirements for building components to be met and 
includes the possibility of using more Energy Service Company models (ESCO) as well 
as establishes a green subsidy scheme for energy renovation of housing. The ESCO 
models finance energy renovation projects, eliminating the economic barriers to energy 
renovation. 
The Danish Building regulations from 2010 (BR10, 2010) also refer to the renovation of 
buildings and set new requirements for the thermal transfer coefficients of the different 
elements forming the building envelope, as well as the linear thermal transmittance for 
joints. Nevertheless, buildings worthy of preservation and covered by a law are exempt 
from the provisions. 
In Denmark, it is especially interesting the renovation of multi-story buildings from 1850-
1950 (Figure 1) because of their energy saving potential. They are generally 3-7 stories 
tall, have 45-90 cm of thick solid brick wall that supports wooden floor beams (Figure 2), 
==PAGE:18==
 
14 
have usually wooden ceilings and roof construction which is typically covered with 
traditional tiling and have at least 35º incline (Zagorskas, Paliulis, Burinskien, & 
Gediminas, 2013). Such buildings are generally listed as “Heritage Buildings” by the 
“Culture Authority Register of Protected Buildings” (Kulturstyrelsens register over 
Fredede og Bevaringsværdige Bygninger) (Kulturstyrelsen, 2014). 
The Danish law of “Building Preservation and Conservation of Buildings” 
(Bygningsfredning og Bevaring af Bygninger) assesses if a building is worthy of 
preservation. Among the approximately 4 million buildings in Denmark, around 9000 are 
protected and about 300.000 are estimated to have conservation value (Kulturstyrelsen, 
2014). Heritage buildings are protected both inside and outside, and need the “Culture’s 
Agency” (Kulturstyrelsens) approval before performing any type of modification. 
Heritage buildings have special architectural or cultural heritage qualities that tell about 
important periods of the history of national importance (Kulturstyrelsen, 2012). According 
to “Kulturstyrelsens”, any building at least 50 years old with an architectural or historical 
value must be preserved (Kulturstyrelsen, 2014).

The energy saving measures that can be carried out when renovating existing buildings 
involve insulation improvements of the building envelope (external walls, roof structures 
and floors), changing doors and windows, remove thermal bridges in order to improve 
the airtightness of the building and the installation of an intelligent HVAC system 
(Heating, Ventilation and Air Conditioning).  
In most cases, the buildings erected before the current building codes present high levels 
of air leakage and insufficient or inexistent amounts of insulation, which explains why the 
greatest heat losses in these buildings occur in the external walls and windows 
(Tommerup, H., & Svendsen, S., 2006). Ensuring that walls have the proper levels of 
insulation is fundamental in energy renovation of heritage buildings. Additionally the use 
of external insulation in historical buildings is not an option, since the exterior appearance 
of the building has to be preserved. The only option to improve the energy performance 
of the façade is therefore by applying interior façade insulation. However, the installation 
of interior insulation entails some issues such as the reduction of the drying potential of 
==PAGE:19==
 
15 
the wall caused by the lowering of the wall temperature which increases the risk of 
condensation of water vapour (Morelli, M., & Svendsen, S., 2012). Besides, the pores of 
the bricks forming the façade could close preventing the accumulated moisture to 
evaporate. If this happens, the walls can become damaged after few freezing cycles and 
also moisture problems may occur at the beam ends embedded in the masonry and the 
wall (Zavadskas, et al., 2014).  
Installing a vapour barrier is a conventional solution that prevents the penetration of 
water vapour from the inside, but can also reduce the drying potential to the inside, which 
could be problematic in presence of high wind-driven rain loads (Morelli G, Nielsen, 
Scheffler, & Svendsen, 2010). Consequently, the water from the rain may accumulate in 
the masonry increasing the relative humidity at the beam ends and behind the insulation. 
If a relative humidity above 75%-80% is reached on the wood’s surface, mould growth 
can occur (Harrestrup, 2014) and a higher relative humidity can lead to the decay of the 
wooden beams.  
Thus the results of hygrothermal investigations (Morelli G, Nielsen, Scheffler, & 
Svendsen, 2010) showed that although there is a great energy potential in the building 
stock, renovation measures with internal insulation can cause moisture problems and in 
some cases degradation of parts of the load-bearing structure. 
Other disadvantages associated to the application of interior thermal insulation systems 
are the loss of inner space that involves adding an interior layer of insulation and finish 
material, as well as the complexity of the installation (Zavadskas, et al., 2014). 
Besides the substantial energy saving potential of the eldest buildings, heritage buildings 
represent the cities’ identity and their construction and decorative elements have to be 
preserved. On the other hand, heritage buildings were built in times with different 
lifestyles of the occupants and other comfort standards than the ones found today. 
Therefore, retrofitting of heritage buildings also has an impact in the comfort inside the 
building, suiting modern standards. 
Ultimately, retrofitting of heritage buildings must ensure that the goals of comfort, energy 
efficiency and durability are met. 
 
1.2 Aim of the project 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the issues arising from the application of interior 
thermal insulation in traditional Danish buildings from the period 1850-1950, built with 
massive masonry walls and embedded wooden floor beams. 
Through literature studies it was found that post insulation improves the energy 
performance of the buildings as the energy use for heating is significantly decreased. 
However this measure reduces the drying potential of the wall and increases the risk of 
condensation of water vapour that penetrates from the interior, which will most likely lead 
to moisture problems behind the insulation and in the beam ends embedded in the 
masonry (Morelli G, Nielsen, Scheffler, & Svendsen, 2010) and in the worst case lead to 
structural problems. 
Critical areas in the wall such as the beam end and wall joint, were locally modelled and 
analysed in the simulation tool Delphin, in order to have a better understanding of the 
==PAGE:20==
 
16 
issues originated in these areas. The hygrothermal simulations were combined with the 
measurements from a test apartment in order to validate several theoretical simulation 
models. The goal was to use the validated models to predict how the construction is 
affected by different scenarios such as different rain exposures or orientations.  
Additionally, a new method was developed and modelled in Delphin with the purpose of 
finding a general solution to the problems originated in the wall concerning changing 
moisture conditions when adding post insulation. 
 
1.3 Structure of the thesis 
This section includes a description of the structure chosen to investigate the issues 
related to the application of internal insulation.  
The research was divided in two parts: 
- 
Part 1: Hygrothermal investigation of the application of internal insulation in a 
heritage multi-storey building.  
- 
Part 2: Development of a new general solution 
 
In the first part of this thesis a study of the moisture conditions in the façade and the floor 
construction of a test apartment was carried out to determine the effect of internal 
insulation. 
Temperature and relative humidity measurements of the apartment were registered after 
the renovation process of the façade with internal insulation and were used to validate a 
simulation model with the software Delphin. 
The simulation model was later used to simulate various cases such as worst case 
scenarios. 
The second part of the thesis focused on developing a new moisture-safe solution that 
could be applied in every building where internal insulation is required, no matter the

The solution consists of an air gap between the insulation and the brick wall that is 
constantly ventilated with dry air in order to keep the relative humidity of the construction 
bellow critical moisture levels.

2 State of the art 
2.1 Moisture balance 
Post insulating old load bearing masonry buildings can lead to several moisture related 
problems, such as freeze-thaw damage of the bricks and decay of the wood structure, 
changing a well performing structure into one with a critical moisture balance (Straube & 
Schumacher, 2007).  
Moisture problems can only occur if a moisture source is available and if there are means 
for the moisture to spread. Also, a driving force that causes moisture movement must 
exist. At the same time the material used in the construction must be susceptible to 
moisture damage and has to be exposed for a specific amount of time and exceed the 
critical limit of moisture content of the material (Straube & Schumacher, 2007). 
In general the major sources of moisture are the driving rain, the water vapour in the air, 
the stored moisture and the bound ground water.  
Moisture can be removed from a building element by evaporation of the water 
transported by capillary suction, by air leakage and diffusion, by draining small openings, 
as well as by dry ventilation (Straube & Schumacher, 2007). 
As in practice, it is not possible to remove all moisture sources, the most efficient 
measure is to reduce the time of exposure to the moisture source and to lower the 
probability of reaching the critical moisture content of the materials forming the wall. 
Therefore, there must be a balance of wetting and drying in the building envelope in 
order to not accumulate moisture over time and not exceed the safe moisture content of 
the construction (Straube & Schumacher, 2007).  
Moisture, temperature and time are crucial for the failure or success of a renovation 
project. 
 
2.2 Internal insulation products 
2.2.1 Diffuse closed insulation 
Diffuse closed insulation requires a vapour barrier, which function is to retard the 
migration of water vapour. According to some authors (Morelli, M., & Svendsen, S., 
2012), the moisture penetration can be avoided by installing a vapour barrier near the 
inner surface of the wall, tightened to the beam end in order to prevent vapour 
penetration from the interior, but the barrier also reduces the drying of the wall towards 
the inside. The reduction of the drying potential of the wall can be a problem in the 
presence of wind driven rain, as the rain may accumulate in the masonry (Morelli G, 
Nielsen, Scheffler, & Svendsen, 2010). 
Besides, the implementation of a completely tight layer is a difficult task in terms of 
execution and its durability is very limited (Pavlik, Jirickova, Pavlik, & Cerny, 2005). 
Morelli and Svendsen (2012) placed in their investigations a vapour barrier between the 
gypsum board and the mineral wool (Figure 3) obtaining the aforementioned results, 
while Pavlik, Z et al. went for a less common composition of the building envelope 
==PAGE:22==
 
18 
consisting of the load-bearing structure followed by a water vapour retarder, hydrophilic 
thermal insulation material based on mineral wool, and a water vapour permeable plaster 
(Pavlik, Jirickova, Pavlik, & Cerny, 2005) (Figure 4). This unusual configuration was 
motivated by the necessity to avoid the mechanical damage of the vapour barrier. 
However such system does not work with a traditional water vapour barrier as a great 
amount of water would condense in the insulation layer. Instead, the authors proposed 
the use of a water vapour retarder layer that allows a part of the water vapour to diffuse. 
As expected, the research revealed that conventional insulation materials could not be 
applied in this system because of their limited possibilities of liquid water transport.  The 
investigation concluded that it was necessary to develop a new thermal insulation 
material to satisfy the requirements of the system. The hygric performance of the system 
with a new developed interior thermal insulation was excellent as the relative humidity in 
the critical point of the system was around 44%, far from condensation dangers (Pavlik, 
Jirickova, Pavlik, & Cerny, 2005).

2.2.2 Diffuse open insulation  
Diffuse open insulation also known as capillary active materials, is an innovative solution 
quite extended in the last years. Its purpose is to avoid interstitial condensation and to 
allow the drying out towards the inside, although this is not always the result (Vereecken 
& Roels, 2014). 
A capillary active material is typically made of pores in the range of 0.1-1 µm that absorbs 
the liquid water and redistributes it towards the room (Vereecken & Roels, 2014) (Figure 
5). 
==PAGE:23==
 
19 
 
Figure 5: Transport of moisture towards the interior side and moisture regulating evaporation from the 
internal surface (Remmer UK, 2013). 
 
Capillary active systems replace the traditional vapour tight insulation systems that also 
avoid interstitial condensation but introduce other moisture related issues. Furthermore 
it seems that the hygrothermal performance of a capillary active system is more sensitive 
to modifications of the wall structure than conventional systems with vapour water 
barriers (Vereecken & Roels, 2014). Hence the materials and the configurations used 
can have a great influence on the performance of the system.  
There is a wide disparity of opinions concerning the use of capillary systems. While some 
studies (Vereecken & Roels, 2014) determine that the capillary active system performs 
better than the vapour tight system with respect to the moisture content of the masonry 
wall, other research conclude that that the application of silicate-based materials as 
capillary active insulation without air vapour-tight barrier seems to result in long-term 
water condensation in the insulation layer (Pavlik, Jirickova, Pavlik, & Cerny, 2005).  
In general, the authors that support the efficacy of capillary active systems believe that 
they are a good solution for historical buildings with sufficiently thick walls or walls 
protected against wind-driven rain. However it does not seem to perform properly in the 
case of thinner walls exposed to wind-driven rain, as the moisture can be partially stored 
in the insulation and would enter the room, decreasing the thermal performance of the 
system (Vereecken & Roels, 2014). 
 
2.2.3 High performance thermal insulation 
High performance insulation, currently under research, is a term used for materials with 
a thermal conductivity lower than 20 mW/(mK) (Johansson, et al., 2014) which may have 
a great potential in the retrofit of old buildings, as it enables to save space with slimmer 
elements as a result of its low thermal conductivity. However a lower thermal conductivity 
leads to lower wall temperatures, increasing the risk of moisture problems due to 
increased relative humidity. 
Unfortunately the costs of the newly developed materials remain too high for their 
application to the building industry and their use still entails many disadvantages. 
==PAGE:24==
 
20 
2.2.3.1 Vacuum insulation panels (VIPs) 
Vacuum insulation panels (VIPs) consist of an open porous core of fumed silica 
enveloped of several metallized polymer laminate layers (Jelle, B. P., 2011) (Figure 6). 
The thermal conductivities of the VIPs range from 3 to 4 mW/(mK) in fresh conditions 
and reach around 8 mW/(mK) after 25 years. 
 
Figure 6: Structure of a Kingspan VIP (Kingspan Group, 2014) 
 
The very low thermal conductivities of the VIPs require thinner thicknesses than 
traditional insulation systems, saving space and avoiding to make use of complicated 
and new construction techniques. 
However, one of the many disadvantages of the VIPs is that they cannot be cut or 
perforated at the building site as they lose a large part of their thermal insulation 
performance, increasing to about 20 mW/(mK) (Jelle, Gustavsen, & Baetens, 2010), not 
to mention the high costs that they entail.  
Also Johansson et al. (2014) found that VIPs considerably decrease the temperatures in 
the wall and consequently reduce the drying capacity of the moisture in the brick which 
could lead to high moisture levels in the beam ends. 
 
2.2.3.2 Gas-filled panels (GFPs) 
Gas-filled panels are experimental and there are currently only few products in the 
market with limited applications. They consist of a barrier envelope and a gas between 
reflective layers, that can be air or a heavier gas less thermal conductive than air, such 
as argon, krypton or xenon (Baetens, Jelle, Gustavsen, & Grynning, 2010). 
The thermal conductivities corresponding to the prototype of GFPs are quite high and 
are around 40mW/(mK) (Jelle, Gustavsen, & Baetens, 2010). 
Some authors question the future of the GFPs as other materials such as the VIPs, have 
a better performance. Nevertheless it seems that the application of a low- conductive 
gas could have a potential in the development of this product (Baetens, Jelle, Gustavsen, 
& Grynning, 2010). 
==PAGE:25==
 
21 
2.2.3.3 Aerogels 
According to some authors (Jelle, B. P., 2011) aerogels may have the highest potential 
as a high performance thermal insulation material for building applications. 
Aerogel technologies are still developing and only limited commercial products are 
available. 
Aerogels are dried gels with a very high porosity (Baetens, Jelle, & Gustavsen, 2011) 
and thermal conductivities of 13 mW/(mK) may be reached at ambient pressure (Jelle, 
Gustavsen, & Baetens, 2010). They can reduce up to 90% the heat losses through 
external walls with only 20 mm of the product, however the current cost of aerogels is 10 
times higher compared to conventional insulation materials (Cuce, Cuce, Wood, & Riffat, 
2014). 
 
2.3 Wall configurations 
Different hypothesis of wall configurations of old existing multi-storey buildings have 
been investigated by different authors with the objective of achieving an energy 
renovation without the recurring moisture problems. 
Morelli and Svendsen (2012) found that a way to reduce the moisture problems at the 
beam ends is to stop the thermal insulation 200 mm above the floor and bellow the ceiling 
(Figure 7). 
Additionally, Harrestrup and Svendsen (2015) proved that the effect of the 200 mm gap 
barely decreases the heat savings in the total building and no risk of moisture problems 
was identified in the beam ends.

2.3.1 Ventilated air gap concept 
A different research line studies the possibility of drying out the construction by creating 
an air gap between the masonry wall and the insulation. 
Anastasi (Ueno & Straaten, 2012) developed a solution that involves attaching a 
drainage layer covered with a polyolefin film to the interior of the masonry and applying 
a closed cell foam (Figure 8). Then, an air gap avoids that the drained water is 
redistributed to the immediate area. This application has been developed into a 
commercial product, marketed as a “hygric buffer mat” that allows the drying of the 
masonry. The author suggested the use of a fan to pressurize the cavity for a better dry 
out.  
 
Figure 8: Hygric buffer mat and interior insulation application (Ueno & Straaten, 2012). 
In active pressure control systems (Straube, Ueno, & Schumacher, 2012), outdoor air 
heated to indoor temperature is forced into the air gap. This air maintains the interior of 
the masonry at the ideal indoor temperature, and ensures that air leakage condensation 
cannot occur even with high humidity. It is preferable to use highly vapour-permeable 
insulation because it allows the masonry to dry to the inside as well to the outside (Figure 
9). 
 
Figure 9: Active pressure control retrofit (Straube, Ueno, & Schumacher, 2012) 
==PAGE:27==
 
23 
The method proposed by af Klintberg and Björk (Af Klintberg & Björk, 2012) drains and 
ventilates the air gap with an airflow driven by thermal buoyancy caused by a heating 
cable in the vertical air gap. The heating cable warms the air inside the air gap, resulting 
in a temperature difference between the air gap and the room. If the temperature in the 
air gap is higher than in the room, the density in the air gap would be lower than the 
density in the room. This lower density makes a pressure gradient that creates an upward 
airflow inside the air gap. The air flow enables the system to dry and keeps the relative 
humidity at a low level. The aim of this method is to remove moisture from the 
construction and avoid mould growth (Af Klintberg & Björk, 2008). 
Figure 10 represents an L-shaped test rig that was built by the same authors (Af Klintberg 
& Björk, 2012) to model floor and wall components as a whole. The test rig contains an 
air gap that runs through both floor and wall components with a heating cable placed in 
the lowest part of the vertical air gap. The arrows show the direction of the airflow. 
 
Figure 10: Cross-section of an L-shaped test rig (Af Klintberg & Björk, 
2012). 
 
This same method was applied in a full-scale study of a flooded floor (Af Klintberg, 
Johannesson , & Björk, F, 2008). The flooded floor was dried in nine days when using 
two heating cables, and in thirteen days with one heating cable. The results showed that 
it was possible to dry out a building element and prevent all mould growth provided that 
the indoor relative humidity was lower than 65%.

3.1 Introduction 
To find out whether it is possible to ensure moisture safety in traditional Danish buildings 
with massive masonry walls and embedded wooden floor beams when applying internal 
insulation, measurements needed to be performed in a test apartment undergoing a 
renovation with internal insulation. The measurements were later used to validate a 
theoretical simulation model, which was used for simulating worst case scenarios. 
This research is based on the measurements obtained after the renovation of one 
apartment located in Copenhagen area.  The renovation was carried out under DTU’s 
Civil Engineering department management, which also performed the measurements. 
 
3.2 Methods 
3.2.1 Hygrothermal investigation of the application of internal insulation 
in a test apartment. 
A study of the moisture conditions in the façade and the floor construction of a test 
apartment was carried out in order to determine the effect of internal insulation in the 
moisture levels of the brick wall behind the insulation and of the wooden beam 
construction. 
The optimal method would be to perform measurements on a large sample of buildings 
that would be used to validate theoretical simulation models and test them over a long 
period of time. However due to time and money restrictions it was only possible to 
analyse a single test apartment.  
The alternative method consisted of installing sensors in the beam ends and walls to 
measure the relative humidity and temperatures. The measurements were later used to 
validate a simulation model created with the hygrothermal simulation tool Delphin. The 
purpose of the validation is to use the model and parameterization obtained for a set of 
exemplary cases such as worst case scenarios (Institut für Bauklimatik, 2015). 
The predicted results in Delphin were then compared to the measured values in the test 
apartment. Once the predicted results and the measurements matched, the model was 
validated, and was used to simulate worst case scenarios which enabled to have a 
broader view of the effects of internal insulation and draw more conclusions. 
 
The models were based on real measurements in the building (indoor environment) and 
on real weather data. 
Weather data at the weather observatories 0618000 in Copenhagen Airport and 
0618700 Copenhagen Toldbod was provided by the Danish Meteorological Institute 
(DMI) to use it as exterior conditions in the simulations, in order to obtain more realistic 
results. However there are still many unknown factors such as the solar radiation on the 
façade that will influence the dry out of the wall, or the rain exposure of the wall which 
affects its moisture content.  
==PAGE:29==
 
25 
Besides, carrying out measurements also involved uncertainties in the results due to the 
installation of the equipment and to the equipment itself. 
This means that validating the simulation model can be a difficult task and the model will 
never be 100% accurate, but will still give a good indication of the moisture conditions in 
the construction.

3.2.3 Evaluation of the risk of mould growth 
The moisture performance of the building envelope is essential for the durability of the 
construction. Moisture plays an important role in the decay and deterioration processes 
of the materials in the construction and critical limits for moisture and temperature should 
not be exceeded (Rode C. , 2014). 
 
Different methods can be used to assess the risk of mould growth. 
The assessment can be done with the analysis of simple key values or by the use of 
dynamic models. 
Simple assessment methods range from the simple visual inspection to simple 
calculation criteria, while dynamic models provide results with much more information, 
which also require more expertise to assess. 
 
Visual assessment is generally not possible as the parts of the construction that need to 
be examined are not accessible. Besides, a visual inspection would only be useful if the 
coverage of mould growth on the surface is extended enough to see it with the “naked-
eye”, but will in any case allow a monitoring of the mould growth process. Nevertheless 
mould growth could exist but not be visible (Initial stage of growth). In this cases sampling 
==PAGE:30==
 
26 
can be performed in order to determine which types and concentrations of mould are 
present in the construction. 
 
Measurements or results from dynamic simulations can also be analysed by simple 
reading of the graphs. 
 
A simple but one of the central criteria for assessing the drying capacity of a construction 
is to calculate the amount of accumulated water in the construction during a year (Δw). 
It can be used to assess the risk of mould as the drying capacity will reduce the risk of 
mould growth (Rode C. , 2014). 
Δw is expressed in kg/m2/year, where wend [kg/m2] is the moisture content by the end of 
the year and wstart [kg/m2] is the moisture content in the beginning of the year. A well 
performing construction should have a negative accumulation of water as it should not 
accumulate any moisture.

The VTT model, developed at the VTT Technical Research Centre of Finland (Viitanen, 
et al., 2005) , can be used parallel with hygrothermal simulation models or as a post 
processing tool. It is a mathematical model for the study of mould growth, based on 
several experimental studies on conditions for mould growth on wood and other 
materials. 
The model calculates the Mould Index (M) previously described, and defines the 
minimum value for which mould growth can occur based on the critical relative humidity 
of the materials. For instance, for mould development in wood, the ambient minimal 
critical relative humidity level is 80% (Viitanen, et al., 2010), while the inner surface of a 
brick wall is 85% (Rode C. , 2014). 
 
In this master thesis, the methods used to evaluate the risk of mould growth are first a 
simple evaluation of the measurements carried out in the test apartment, as well as the 
evaluation of the results from dynamic simulations, by simple interpretation of the graphs. 
Then, a more detailed evaluation including the time factor with the use of the VTT-model 
was performed in both measurements and results from the simulations.

Delphin is a simulation program for the coupled heat, moisture, and matter transport in

Germany (Institut für Bauklimatik, 2015). 
Delphin was used in this research for detailed hygrothermal calculations of 1D and 2D 
models. 
Matlab is a numerical computing environment developed by MathWorks (MathWorks, 
2015) that is used in this thesis to predict the mould growth in the construction elements

3.3.2.3 Evaluation of the risk of mould growth in the construction based 
on the measurements 
Relevant literature (Viitanen, 1997) investigating the response time needed for the 
development of the initial stages of mould growth on wood, stated that in continual 
humidity exposures at RH above 80% for several weeks or months, the risk for mould 
growth in pine and spruce sapwood exists when the temperature is between 5 and 50 
°C. Between 0 and 5 °C, the growth of mould fungi is slow and only expected when the 
RH is above 90%. Wood degradation can happen after only a few days when the RH is 
above 95 % and the temperature is between 25 and 40 °C, and it takes approximately 4 
to 8 weeks, when the temperature is between 10 and 20 °C. 
Also, the Danish Building Research Institute (Valbjørn, 2006) ratifies that mould can only 
grow in locations, on surfaces, or in materials where the relative humidity is about 75-
80%. 
The measurements for relative humidity and temperature registered after the renovation 
present values above 75% for the relative humidity at different times as well as 
==PAGE:43==
 
39 
temperatures between 5 to 20ºC during most of the time. Hence there is a risk of mould 
growth, especially at the beam ends, where the relative humidity is the highest. 
A more detailed analysis of the measurements was carry out with the VTT model. 
The minimum value for which mould growth can occur in the beams was set to 80% 
relative humidity in the model, as wood is a very sensitive material, while for the wall 
surface behind the insulation the critical relative humidity level was set to 85% (Table 3).

3.3.4 Model validation 
 
As explained in the previous section, new initial conditions the temperature and relative 
humidity were set in the validation models based on the results obtained in the first round 
of simulations with the DRY files. 
The measurements of temperature and relative humidity inside the test apartment 
(sensor point 1) were used as interior conditions in the validation models. 
The weather data used as exterior conditions in the models was provided by the Danish 
Meteorological Institute (DMI). The measurements were taken at the DMI’s weather 
observatory 0618000 in Copenhagen airport. 
The data consisted of temperature, relative humidity, wind speed and direction, mean 
pressure, cloud cover and precipitation. 
Also measurements of the global mean radiation measured at the observatory 0618700 
in Copenhagen “Toldbod” were provided. 
Sky radiation, direct and diffusion sun radiation were determined through calculations 
based on the relative humidity, temperature and cloud cover in the first case, and global 
radiation in the second. 
Local conditions such as wind-driven rain and solar radiation in the façade were unknown 
parameters, which could be a problem for the validation process. 
It is worth mentioning as well, that the simulations performed in Delphin do not 
completely represent the reality in the 2D model. In a 2D Delphin model the beam is 
modelled as a 1 meter continuous beam. Harrestrup (2014) assumed that the real results 
should be somewhere between the results of a 2D model with beam and the results of a 
2D model without beam. However in this project only the models with beam were 
represented due to time restrictions. 
The results obtained in Delphin for temperature and relative humidity at the walls and 
beam ends were compared to the measured data in the test apartment. Once the results 
of Delphin matched the measurements, the simulation model was validated and was 
used for further research.

The reason of that could be the initial conditions set in the model. The initial conditions

The reference model reaches 100% relative humidity after 60 days of simulation and 
stays constant during all the validation period. The results of the model with 15% of free 
water and 100% relative humidity as initial conditions in the brick wall, start at 85% 
relative humidity and stabilise at 95% after 40 days of simulation, getting closer to the 
measurements. This means that when applying the volumetric liquid moisture content 
condition to the brick wall, the amount of water running through the brick will be limited, 
lowering the relative humidity at the wall.  
The results from the simulation with initial conditions of 80% relative humidity provides 
results very different than the reference model, which means that choosing the right initial 
conditions in the model is very important for the results. For instance in this case there 
is a difference of 20% relative humidity with the reference model during most of the 
validation period. Also the model has greater dynamics and shows a trend identical to 
the measurements.  Therefore a detailed analysis of the initial conditions in the 
construction would be necessary to improve the model and obtained results closer to 
reality. Nevertheless due to time restrictions it was not possible to change the initial 
conditions of all the models and run them again.  
This could also explain why the changes in the rain exposure coefficients have such a 
limited effect on the calculated results. Since the amount of free water in the brick is so 
high, the fact that the wall is exposed to more or less rain does not influence the results. 
Turning to the matter in hand, the relative humidity measured at the south-west wall 
(Figure 34 (c)) is rather constant in the period that can be validated and so are the results 
from the simulation. On the other hand, the measurements at the southern wall (Figure 
==PAGE:52==

34 (d)) present a decreasing trend that was explained in previous sections to be the 
result of the drying out of the wall due to the solar radiation on the façade. However the 
results from the simulations do not present that trend. The same argumentation used to 
explain the dynamics of the relative humidity results in Delphin could be applied here. 
The moisture transport is so slow in Delphin that it is not possible to witness a decrease 
in the relative humidity due to the drying out of the wall in such a short period of time. 
Simulations over a longer period of time would maybe show that drying out of the wall.  
The models were created in an attempt to get results as close as possible to reality, 
however due to time constraints and the difficulty of the task, the models are not perfect. 
Further work in the models will be required in order to improve them. Nevertheless some 
of the differences between measurements and results could be explained by the many 
uncertainties of the model. For instance the solar radiation on the façade that influences 
the drying out of the wall and the rain exposure of the wall which affects the moisture 
content of the wall are unknown. 
Also, the fact that a three dimensional problem was modelled with a two-dimensional 
program implies great uncertainties regarding the simulated results. 
Additionally, the 1D models represent a 0.384 m section of the wall instead of the full 
height of the wall and does not consider the openings in the wall such as windows and 
balconies or corners and therefore ignores any kind of thermal bridge in the façade. 
Besides, the geometry of the models cannot make possible to faithfully represent the 
position of the sensors in the wall. The sensors were installed at a height of 1.5 m from 
the floor. However since the models represent only 0.384 m of the wall, the results of the 
calculations correspond to an averaged value of the 0.384 m of wall surface which might 
affect the accuracy of the results. 
Besides, the exact properties of the existing materials (brick, lime cement mortar and 
lime plaster) are unknown as no samples of the materials were analysed. The properties 
of the materials used in the models belong to the material data base of Delphin and were 
altered in some cases to get closer to the measurements. As for the materials used in 
the renovation, some of the properties of the insulation and gypsum board materials were 
found in data sheets of Kingspan. However some other values were unknown and had 
to be assumed based on the properties of similar materials found in the material data 
base of Delphin.  
Also, it seems like the thermal conductivity of the insulation might not be as small as said 
in the information sheet of Kingspan. According to Kingspan (Kingspan Group, 2014) the 
thermal conductivity of an insulation panel with a thickness bigger than 45 mm is 0.024 
W/mK, which is the used in all the models. However as seen in Figure 37, the calculated 
temperature at the wall’s surface when applying a thermal conductivity of 0.04 W/mK to 
the insulation, provides results closer to the measurements than when using a thermal 
conductivity of 0.024 W/mK. 
==PAGE:53==
 
49 
 
Figure 37: Temperature results with insulations with different thermal conductivities. 
 
Finally, as aforementioned, the initial conditions set in the models have also a great 
influence is the results, especially in the relative humidity calculations. The investigation 
should therefore continue with the variation of the initial conditions in the model. A good 
method would be to simulate a model without insulation (before the renovation) over 10 
years and use the results as initial conditions for the models after the renovation. Also a 
limit to the free water in the brick wall should be set. 
Overall, based on comparison between the measurements and calculations in the 
interface between the brick wall and the insulation, the model seems to provide 
approximate and acceptable results for the temperature and relative humidity in the 
periods when the indoor temperature and relative humidity measurements were 
available.  
Hence the models can be used to simulate worst case scenarios which will not provide 
100% accurate results due to the uncertainties of the models, but will contribute with 
results close to reality and will make possible to compare the different scenarios between 
them in order to analyse their effect in the construction.

3.4.3 Worst case scenarios with Delphin. 
 
After validating the models, worst-case scenarios were simulated using the validated 
models representing the wooden beams (2D) and the interface between the insulation 
and the brick wall (1D). 
The worst-case scenarios were simulated over 5 years with standard weather data 
(Design Reference Year – DRY). 
The models were simulated for north and west orientations as they are the most critical 
orientations.  In Denmark, the most dominant wind-direction is from west, therefore west-
oriented façades are more exposed to wind-driven rain (Harrestrup, 2014). On the other 
hand, north-oriented façades do not benefit from direct solar radiation which reduces the 
drying of the wall (Harrestrup, 2014). 
Furthermore, 1D simulations were carry out for wall thicknesses of 1.5 bricks. 
The simulations were performed for rain exposure coefficients of CR=0.1, CR=0.3 and 
CR=0.5 (CR=0.5 only for the west-orientated wall) for the 1D and 2D models. 
==PAGE:58==
 
54 
Reference models were also created representing the conditions before the retrofitting 
for both 1D and 2D simulations. 
 
3.4.3.1 Interface between the brick wall and the insulation 
 
Figure 42 (a) and (b) shows the mould growth indexes at the interface between the brick 
wall and the insulation north and west-orientated orientations.  
Based on the validation models, both north and west-orientated façades would result in 
high mould indexes. As with the validation models, the rain exposure coefficients have a 
very limited effect on the results and all the scenarios have the same mould index. As 
explained in the previous section the main reason of this might be the initial conditions 
set in the model, where no value for the amount of liquid moisture content was applied, 
resulting in a great amount of water entering the wall through the pores. All the scenarios 
show a constant trend, implying there will be an accumulation of mould growth over the 
years. The mould indexes do not decrease during summer time which means that the 
wall does not dry out at any point of the 5 simulated years. 
The renovated wall result in mould indexes between 3 and 4 indicating visual mould 
covering between 10-70% of the surface. Only the walls without insulation result in a 
mould index equal to zero. The reason of this is the absence of insulation and vapour 
barrier.  
These results question the need to use internal insulation to improve the energy 
performance of the façade. In this case, post insulating changes a well performing 
structure into one with a critical moisture balance. The façade’s performance could also 
be improved by changing the old windows with energy-efficient windows.

4 Part 2: Development of a new general solution: The 
Ventilated Air Gap System 
 
4.1 Introduction 
A controlled ventilated air gap behind the insulation was investigated as a new moisture-
safe solution that could be applied in every building where internal insulation is required. 
The principle consists of a 1 cm air gap located between the brick wall and the insulation. 
Dry air is supplied through a plastic pipe with holes in order to distribute the air in all the 
volume of the gap. The air is removed from the gap through a second pipe with the help 
of a fan. A dehumidifier is in charge of removing the moist from the air supplied to the 
pipe. 
 
4.2 Method 
The method used to develop this new concept is divided in the following parts: 
- 
Calculations for the design of the ventilation system, including the dimensions 
of the pipes, dimensions of the holes and pressure drops in the pipe and the 
holes. 
- 
Estimation of the amount of moisture needed to remove from the air gap in 
order to keep the moisture levels down. 
- 
Election of the dehumidifier: a marketed product was chosen based on the 
amount of moisture needed to remove from the wall surface and air gap. 
- 
Election of a fan to extract the moist air from the air gap. 
- 
Creation of a simulation models and different scenarios in the hygrothermal 
simulation program Delphin to determine the airflow of the supplied air. 
- 
Analysis of the results 
 
4.3 Description of the system 
A controlled ventilated air gap behind the insulation was investigated as a new moisture-
safe solution. 
The principle consists of a 1 cm air gap located between the brick wall and the insulation 
that is constantly ventilated with dry air in order to dry out the inner surface of the brick 
wall. The supplied air is controlled based on relative humidity, temperature and airflow 
velocity. 
The air is supplied through a plastic pipe of 4,5 cm of diameter, placed in the 20 cm gap 
under the insulation.  
As a starting point for this investigation it was assumed it was reasonable to supply the 
air into the gap with a relative humidity of 10% and temperature of 6ºC. Four different 
airflows of 0.3, 0.5, 1.2 and 1.8 m3/m2h were investigated. 
Small holes along the pipe with a 10 cm spacing distribute the air in the wall surface. 
==PAGE:69==
 
65 
It is a close loop system, where the air is supplied through the lower pipe and it is 
removed from the air gap through a second pipe located in the 20 cm gap between the 
insulation and the ceiling. The extraction pipe has the same amount of holes with the 
same spacing in order to guarantee a proper distribution of the air in all the wall surface.  
A micro fan removes the moist air from the air gap. 
A dehumidifier located in a cabinet in the room reduces de level of humidity of the 
supplied air.

4.5 Moisture content in the air gap 
In this section it was calculated the amount of moisture that has to be removed from the 
wall in order to keep the moisture levels under critical levels. 
Moisture can be transferred through the air or materials. It is transported in the air by 
advection, diffusion and convection, while it is transported in materials by capillary 
suction through the pores of the material (Rode C. , 2014). 
In this research only diffusion and convection are considered, assuming that capillary 
suction will not take place in the entire cross-section of the wall. 
 
4.5.1 Vapour diffusion: Water transported through the brick wall 
Diffusion is driven by differences in vapour concentration. The water vapour flows from 
the side of the wall with higher vapour pressure (inside) to the side with lower vapour 
pressure (outside). 
However in composite walls with a vapour barrier at the inner side of the wall, vapour 
diffusion occurs from the exterior to the interior. This is because the vapour barrier 
retards the migration of water vapour from the inside to the outside.

4.6 Dehumidifier

of dehumidifier needed. 
As stablished in 4.5.2, it is necessary to remove 28.08 g of moisture per day. This amount 
is relatively low and conventional dehumidifiers have a too big dehumidifying capacity. It 
is therefore needed the use of special devices and perhaps even the development of 
new products and techniques. 
Two lines of investigation were conducted in this project: the use of a micro-dehumidifier 
and the use of silica gel.

4.6.2 Silica gel  
Silica gel is the most widely desiccant used to remove water vapour because of its large 
capacity for water and ease of regeneration (Wang & Douglas LeVan, 2009). 
It is a prepared material with high porosity (80-99.8%), open structure and large inner 
surface area (500-1200 m2/g) (Haereid, Nilsen , & Einarsrud, 1996) that is commonly 
used as a desiccant packed in permeable bags. Silica gel is also used to dry the air in 
industrial compressed air systems and as preservation tool to control the relative 
humidity in museums and libraries. 
The question is whether it is possible to use the silica gel to dry out the inner side of the 
wall and keep the moisture levels under critical levels. 
Silica gel should be able to work at low temperatures (5ºC), absorb the necessary 
amounts of moisture to keep the relative humidity below 40% and regenerate to 
guarantee its performance. 
A study (Wang & Douglas LeVan, 2009) reported measurements of adsorption isotherms 
for pure water vapour and carbon dioxide on silica gel over a range of temperatures 
(75ºC, 50ºC, 25 ºC and 0ºC). 
In worst case conditions, the relative humidity at the surface of the wall would be 85% 
and the temperature 5ºC. As the experiment was not made for 5ºC, it was assumed that 
the absorption of water by the silica gel would be more similar to 0ºC than to 25ºC. 
At 5ºC and 85% relative humidity the pressure in the system is 0.75 kPa and was 
calculated with a psychometric chart (Appendix ). 
Figure 56 shows that the lower the temperature, the greater the water vapour absorption. 
==PAGE:83==
 
79 
 
Figure 56: Adsorption isotherms of H20 on silica gel at 0ºC and 0.75 kPa. 
 
The curve for 0ºC has no data available for the water absorption at 0.75 kPa which could 
mean that there is a limit in the absorption at such temperature and pressure. However 
by extrapolation, At 0.75 kPa and 0ºC, 1 kg of silica gel would absorb more than 20 moles 
of water or more than 360 g of water.  
The silica gel has to regenerate in order to be useful again as a desiccant. The moisture 
will be released again when the silica gel is heated. It should therefore be contained in 
an insulated box, so that no heat is released to the construction. Also the box should be 
closed to the air gap during the heating process.  
The idea is that the box containing the silica gel will be open to the air gap during the 
drying process and will be closed to the construction during the heating process. Before 
heating up the desiccant, the box will be at room temperature and relative humidity (20ºC 
and 50% RH) which according to the psychometric chart corresponds to a pressure of 
1.1 kPa (Appendix ).

4.9 Hygrothermal calculations 
In order to determine which airflow is necessary to keep the construction under critical 
moisture levels, two worst case scenarios were modelled in Delphin. The assumption 
was that if there is no mould growth under critical conditions, the system would work for 
any type of conditions. 
The worst case scenarios are 1D and 2D models representing the interface between the 
brick wall and the air gap and the beam ends respectively. 
The models were simulated over 5 years with standard weather data (Design Reference 
Year – DRY) and for north and west orientations for the 1D models (as they are the most

air gap, gypsum board, vapour barrier and wood fibre board (Figure 60) were modelled

wall thickness of 1.5 bricks which is the thickness of most part of the wall for the 1D 
simulations.

5 Conclusions and future work 
 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the issues arising from the application of interior 
insulation in old heritage buildings with solid masonry brick walls and wooden beams. 
The measurements in the test apartment showed that the application of internal 
insulation resulted in mould growth at the beam ends and in the south-west oriented wall 
due to its exposure to a bigger amount of wind driven rain and less solar radiation. 
However the measurements were only available for a limited period of time (133 days) 
and mathematical models were modelled in order to study longer periods of time and 
worst case scenarios.  
The validation of the models in order to be able to simulate such scenarios proved to be 
a difficult task as many parameters had to be taken into account and Delphin has a long 
simulation time. The validated models provided higher relative humidities and lower 
temperatures at the inner surface of the wall and the beam ends than the real 
measurements. The results are therefore on the conservative side. 
Based on the results from the validation models and worst case scenarios, the solution 
applied in Meinungsgade does not seem to be safe for both wall and beam ends. 
However such statement is based on unfavourable results which inaccuracy could be 
due to an imprecise set-up of the initial conditions in the models, among other 
uncertainties, that allows a great amount of water enter the pores of the brick wall 
resulting in an increased relative humidity. Besides, the set-up of the models prevents 
the different rain exposure coefficient to have any effect on the results which is not 
realistic. On the contrary to the results, the amount of wind-driven rain and the orientation 
of the walls should have a strong influence in the moisture conditions of the construction. 
Setting the right initial conditions is crucial for the accuracy of the results. Consequently 
it is not possible to state with certainty that the relative humidity would be so critical in 
the construction even after 5 years. A follow up of the measurements should be 
continued in order to keep improving the model. 
At the same time, the development of the Ventilated Air Gap system appears to prevent 
the risk of mould growth in the inner surface of the wall even with small airflows and in 
extreme conditions, but does not seem to work at the beams. In this case, the models 
are probably also in the conservative side as no limits were set to the amount of water 
entering the pores of the brick wall. On the other hand, the system might require bigger 
airflows in order to improve the drying potential of the beams which could be a problem 
for the performance of the system due to the big pressure drop. 
Consequently the main objective should be to understand the many Delphin parameters 
and their repercussions in the results. A special focus should be addressed to the initial 
conditions of the models. It is actually a delicate matter as there is no information on the 
state of the construction and the parameters in Delphin are set based on hypothesis. 
Hence the building should be carefully analysed before applying internal insulation. 
Additionally the sensors in the building are still measuring today, and the measurements 
should be used to validate a longer period of time and create a more accurate model. 
==PAGE:95==
 
91 
Furthermore using a two-dimensional program to analyse three-dimensional problems 
implies many uncertainties. Therefore one should consider the use of 3D tools to 
investigate this matter. 
In any case, the use of internal insulation needs further research as this is the study of 
one single test apartment. Measurements should be performed in a large sample of 
buildings so that the solutions can be documented and developed. A good method would 
be to create hygrothermal models of all the renovated buildings. It would be especially 
interesting that all the buildings would belong to the same period but would have different 
constructional trends. Thus, some sort of guideline could be developed analysing the 
response of the different constructional trends to the use of internal insulation. The 
validation models could then be used to simulate different scenarios, with different 
orientations, rain exposures, materials and insulation thicknesses, in order to find the 
most moisture-safe solution for each trend. 
The research line of the Ventilated Air Gap system should continue towards the 
investigation of new designs that guarantee a low pressure drop and a uniform 
distribution of the air. Besides a real model of the system could be built so that the 
measurements could be used to validate the Delphin models and improve the results. 
Finally it would be interesting to find out if it is technically possible and economically 
feasible to improve the energy performance of old existing buildings and guarantee a 
moisture-safe solution when applying internal insulation and the ventilated air gap 
system. 
Based on this investigation, the benefits of using internal insulation are still debatable. 
As the only reason to use internal insulation is to reduce the heat losses through the 
walls and therefore improve the energy performance of the building, one should consider 
if a safer and more durable solution is possible. Other energy saving measures could be 
applied instead of internal insulation such as upgrading walls and windows and installing 
mechanical ventilation with heat recovery.

the main room of a standard apartment in Copenhagen belonging to the period of 1850-
1950. 
This system, instead of a pipe, uses the gap in the insulation above the floor construction 
along the 5 meters wall to supply the dry air. A plate or elastic material placed on top of 
the insulation gap will drain the air into the air gap. The system is very similar to the 
system with the pipes, as the plate will also have perforations every 10 or 5 cm. A sketch 
of the system can be found in Figure 68.

Based on the results, it is possible to conclude that this system, using the gap in the 
insulation as a pipe and the plate with holes to distribute the air, has smaller pressure 
drops in the system than the system with the pipes, which makes easier to have a uniform 
distribution of the air through all the surface of the wall. 
As seen in Table 20 the system requires bigger diameters of the holes. A 7.7 mm 
diameter every 10 cm would allow a uniform distribution of the air and a total pressure 
drop in the system of around 1.36 Pa, around 42 times smaller than the system with the 
pipes with the same airflow. 
If the perforations are made every 5 cm, a diameter of 5.5 mm will be enough to 
guarantee a pressure drop in the system of 1.3 Pa and a uniform distribution of the air.

1D and 2D models were created to analyse the construction’s performance when 
applying interior insulation.

wood fibre board. 
The 2D models represent the assembly of the floor and the exterior wall. The wooden 
floor beam is perpendicular to the exterior wall and is embedded in the brick wall. The 
models were created without air around the beam.  
The exterior wall was constructed with bricks separated by lime mortar. 
The height of the wooden beam in the floor construction is 132 mm. The floor was 
constructed as 20 mm wooden floor boards. The air gap between the wall and the 
insulations is 10 mm.
