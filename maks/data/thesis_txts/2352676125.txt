Abstract
This thesis deals with the foraging eﬀect of the Bigeye tuna. An existing
model is re-formulated to ﬁt into the scheme of slow-fast dynamics. The necessary
theory from slow-fast dynamical systems is developed and then applied to an
existing model. After the model has been set up, periodic solutions are found
numerically using Matlab for a set value of energy for the system. Lastly, an
optimal energy where the system still emits periodic solutions is estimated in a
conjecture. An upper bound for the conjectured optimal value is presented.
Resumé
Dette projekt beskæftiger sig med Storøjede tunﬁsks søgen efter føde. En
eksistrende model reformuleres, således at det passer ind i et langsom-hurtig
dynamisk system. Den nødvendige teori omkring langsom-hurtig dynamiske
systemer udvikles og derefter anvednt på en eksisterende model. Efter modellen
er sat op, ﬁndes periodiske systmer til modellen ved bryg af Matlab, for en
fastsat energi. Sidst, bliver en formodning omkring den optimale energi systemet
kan antage samtidig med at der stadig ﬁndes optimale løsninger sat op. En øvre
grænse for den optimale energi præsenteres.

Preface
In January 2016, I took a three week class called ’Advanced Modelling - Applied
Mathematics’, this was the ﬁrst time I was introduced to the ’Slow-fast’-approach in
Dynamical systems. The class only lasted three weeks, and I didn’t think that three
weeks was giving the subject the time and justice it deserved. Luckily, it turned out
that Kristian - my supervisor and teacher of the course - knew of a project where the
slow-fast approach might be able to shed some light on the matter at hand. Enter
the Bigeye tuna.
The thesis will work with the model published in [17], and described in [12]. We
will alter the model to ﬁt into the scheme needed to perform slow-fast analysis. First
however, we will state and work with the theory behind slow-fast systems. We will
introduce the notion as well as show how it works on the van del Pol Problem.
Once the theory has been established we will spend some time working with the
model itself and setting up the Layer problem and Reduced problem respectively.
Here the separate dynamics of the two settings will be explored and analyzed.
With the theoretical analysis in order we will begin our search for periodic solutions
in the modiﬁed system. Here we will integrate solutions to the diﬀerential equations
until we reach a Poincaré intersection.
Once the periodic solutions have been established we will begin the search for the
highest energy for which such solutions exist. A few issues popped up during the
implementation process of the periodic solution so no deﬁnitive answer was found. It
was however possible to present a rough estimate which can be examined for future
work, should someone ﬁnd it interesting to explore the matter further.
It must be noted that a large thanks should be extended to both Kristian Uldall
Kristiansen and Uﬀe Høgsbro Thygesen, for having patience with me throughout the
process as well as giving insight when needed.

Introduction
In 2016 an article was published by Thygesen et al. [17] regarding the swimming
patterns of the Bigeye tuna of the coast of Australia. The article presents a model for
the swimming patterns of the Bigeye tuna, based on the hypothesis that the tuna
optimizes its foraging eﬀect. The model is a partial diﬀerential equations model and
numerical analysis conﬁrmed the hypothesis set up.
Figure 1: Phase space of model, taken from [17]
Outside Thygesen et al., a bachelors project was conducted by Takata [12], to see
if an optimal solution could be found for the Hamiltonian function used to deﬁne
the model. The project used the AUTO software in search for all periodic solutions,
and aimed to use this knowledge to pick out an optimal solution. The idea was to
map the value of the Hamiltonian function up against the parameter for each orbit.
This would lead to the periodic solutions disappearing, at some point much like a
saddle-node bifurcation.

1.1
Motivation for Problem
Knowledge about marine animals (in this thesis the Bigeye tuna or Thunnus obesus
[17]) use of its surrounding environment is of the utmost importance in understanding,
as well as predicting, the distributions of the individual species. The Bigeye tuna is
an interesting case as it has been observed to have very distinct vertical migrations
between the surface of the ocean and deeper layers of water. As can be seen from
Figure 2, the Bigeye tuna travels from waters near the surface to deeper waters at
depths around 500 meters below sea level. The pattern shows that at night-time
the Bigeye tuna prefer to stay near the surface where the temperatures are warmer,
whereas it spends the hours during the day to travel between the diﬀerent water
layers to optimize its foraging eﬀect.
The Bigeye tunas ability to dive down to such deep waters where the water can be as
cold as 3◦C, gives it an advantage over other similar marine animals who also have
vertical migration to optimize their foraging eﬀect. The Yellow-ﬁn tuna for example

does not travel as deep as the Bigeye tuna. This gives the Bigeye tuna a strategical
advantage at these lower depths due to the lack of competition from other species.
However, the Bigey tuna has to trade oﬀbetween swimming at the depths where food
concentration is at a premium and being warm enough to function properly and hunt
the nutrition eﬀectively. It seems obvious from the observed data that the Bigeye
tuna forages much better at deeper waters, and is more than willing to swim down to
great depths at the cost of spending time re-heating close to the surface.
So far [17], [18] and [12] has been working with the model without having determined
the optimal energy for the system. Furthermore the dynamics of the model haven’t
been fully described, and these are two things we aim to tackle in this thesis, as will
be formulated in the mail goals of the report next.
1.2
The Thesis
This thesis will still work with the foraging eﬀect of the Bigeye tuna. We will however
be doing it in a slightly diﬀerent way. Namely using the tools from Slow-Fast analysis.
Our ultimate goal will remain the same as for Takata, however we do have a few steps
along the way that we need to get sorted out. First of all:
Goal 1. Identify an optimal periodic solution given an energy of the Hamiltonian
system.
To accomplish this task, we must ﬁrst manipulate the model to ﬁt into the scheme of
the slow-fast analysis, and ensure that the dynamics here are in line with what has
been observed in the original model created in [17]. Next up is to piece together our
analysis of the slow-fast system with that of the original model to be able search for
periodic solutions, and then of course ﬁnally ﬁnd the optimal period solutions for a
given energy.
Goal 2. Assuming Goal 1 is met, ﬁnd an optimal energy that permits periodic
solutions
This resembles the goals of Takata, and are essentially what we hope the slow-fast
dynamical approach will be able to solve for us. Where Takata was forced to use
the AUTO software to carry out simulations the slow-fast application will make it
possible for us to carry out the simulations in Matlab. Thus the purpose of this thesis
can be summarized quickly by

2.2
The Fast-Slow Setting
One could be inclined to ask, why would we want to try the slow-fast approach to
the existing model, what insight to the problem could it give? The idea of using
the slow-fast approach actually came from the phase portrait in Figure 1 on page 1.
This phase portrait had an interesting structure which implied that the slow-fast
method could be a useful tool to describe the dynamics of the model. Figure 7 on
page 13 shows the phase portrait from Figure 1 alone, where some features have been
highlighted.
The green line at the bottom is horizontal, and the ﬂow of the model doesn’t seem to
be visible at the bottom. This could indicate that the dynamics taking place here are
moving at a much slower pace, thus appearing vaguely or not at all in the original
phase portrait. This is our ﬁrst clue that the slow-fast theory might be applicable,
and as it turns out, the rest of the phase portrait gives the same impression.
The other added element to the phase portrait is a black dot at the top of curve,
before the dynamics dictate the trajectory to return to the green baseline. At this
precise point, there is a change in dynamics. The trajectories of solutions either jump
from one manifold to another, or the manifold the trajectories are traveling along
changes. If we, for a second, assume the ﬁrst option of the two, then the black dot

Matlab Application
Throughout this section we will talk about ’forward time integration’ and ’backward
time integration’ respectively. These terms a rather self-explanatory as they cover
exactly what they claim to cover. Here the forward time integration represents the
trajectory along the unstable manifold W u
loc, whereas the backward time integration
represents the trajectory along the stable manifold W s
loc but backward. This is because
our starting point will be the entrance to the Layer problem, and we are interested in
how we reached this point. This is examined by moving backward along the stable
manifold W s
loc, until it intersects the unstable manifold W u
loc. The space where the
intersection occurs, will be called the Poincaré intersection plane, and is deﬁned as
follows:
Deﬁnition 4.1. The Poincaré intersection plane is set the set {(z, Λ, T, µ) | Λ = 0, }
sitting inside the four dimensional space {z, Λ, T, µ}.
The reason we call it the Poincaré intersection plane despite it being a three-
dimensional surface, is that were are not really interested in the value of µ. Our
interest is in ﬁnding a depth and temperature where the trajectories from the stable
manifold and the trajectories from the unstable manifold intersect. Note also that
since we are working in a Hamiltonian system with a constant energy, we can directly
solve for µ if z, Λ and T are given. Therefore, if we have an intersection of the two
trajectories in the z−T plane, then the value of µ coming from the trajectory traveling
along the unstable manifold must equal the value of µ coming from the trajectory
traveling along the stable manifold. To see this let zu, Tu and µu denote the values of
z, T and µ respectively traveling along the unstable manifold, and deﬁne zs, Ts, µs
similarly. Then we have
H(zu, 0, Tu, µu) = H(zs, 0, Ts, µs),
(4.0.1)
but at the intersection, we have zu = zs = and Tu = Ts, and thus
H(zu, 0, Tu, µu) = H(zu, 0, Tu, µs)
(4.0.2)
hence we must have µu = µs as the Hamiltonian function is C1, thus injective. We
can therefore disregard the value of µ and focus on the plane spanned by z and T.

5
Discussion
In this section we will discuss some of the troubles we encountered throughout the
process, as well as look into a few example of what could be the next step in the
process.
5.1
Matlab Ideas and Reasoning
Implementing the slow-fast approach in Matlab and getting useful results turned out
to be a bit more cumbersome than ﬁrst anticipated. For the integration itself Matlabs
own ode45 solver was used. However, in order to ensure, that the code produced
usable results a stop-function was introduced to keep the solutions from running wild.
This function can be found in Appendix B. The function is set up to only stop if
values reach the deﬁned value from below or all angles.
As we wanted to reach our Poincaré intersection plane both from above and from
below, the latter was naturally chosen, however the other option proved useful in
testing out the coding and functionality of both the stop function itself but also
of the entire set-up in general. The purpose of this stop function was to abort the
calculations once the desired area, i.e the Poincaré intersection plane, outlined in
Deﬁnition 4.1 was reached. Note, that the code was set to stop, not only if the value
Λ = 0 was reached but also in case the extreme values of body temperature of the
Bigeye tuna was reached. This not only helped in presenting more visually appealing
ﬁgures but also greatly reduced the computation time, while making error correction
easier.
Finally the program written to carry out the integration was set up with two separate
intial conditions. One for the trajectories traveling along the stable manifold and one
for the trajectories traveling along the unstable manifold. At ﬁrst only one range was
used, but as it turned out that most values yielded a usable, yet rather uninteresting
result, the two separate ranges were set up.
Pictures of the Poincaré intersection were hardly useful, as for larger portions of
the initial conditions the temperature hardly changed at all, thus giving two almost
parallel lines running next to each other as displayed in Figure 22 on the next
page. Thus splitting the initial conditions into two sets TForward and TBackward
respectively we were able to obtain usable results.

5.2
Complications
It turned out that implementing the slow-fast model in Matlab required some more
ﬁnesse than initially anticipated. As we saw in Section 4.1, our system is very sensitive
when it comes to choosing the interval for the initial conditions of T. This caused
quite some trouble at the beginning, as we were not quite sure where the error was
emerged, only that it was coming up. This took some time debugging and caused
some frustration in the late fall and early winter. However, ince this had been solved
the search for the optimal energy of the system began. To begin with, a solution much
lower was considered, namely E = 1.4. This was taken from [12], who estimated for
which values of the Hamiltonian the system would yield periodic solutions.

6
Conclusion
In this section we will brieﬂy reﬂect on what was results were obtained in the report.
This will be done by examining the goals deﬁned in the Introduction. So let us recall
our two goals
Goal 1. Identify an optimal periodic solution given an energy of the Hamiltonian
system.
Goal 2. Assuming Goal 1 is met, can we ﬁnd an optimal energy that permits
periodic solutions.
So let us talk about the two, starting naturally with goal 1, and what we had to do
in order to accomplish it.
6.1
Slow-Fast Application
We developed some useful theory regarding the slow-fast systems, and explored some
of the central ideas behind the system. We were able to take the theory and apply it
in an example of the unforced van der Pol problem. Here, we both set up a slow-fast
system and used it to determine an algebraic expression for the Reduced Problem.
Ultimately, this turned out to not be necessary for our case, as the dynamics of the
Reduced Problem could be directly realized from those of the Layer Problem. We
successfully applied the slow-fast theory on the Bigeye tuna model, and thus were
able to obtain a better understanding of what happens to the Bigeye tuna throughout
its tour of the depths of the ocean. Although the general model was supplied to
begin with, some work went into modifying it to our needs and ensuring that the
modiﬁcations made sense theoretically as well as numerical output made sense. In
general a deeper understanding of the model and its dynamics will hopefully be the
result for any reader familiar with the model beforehand after viewing the problem
through the slow-fast glasses.

6.2
Periodic Solutions
We succeeded in ﬁnding periodic solutions using our redeﬁned model (3.1), and were
thus able to apply the slow-fast approach to the Bigeye tuna problem. Furthermore,
we were able to show in our numerical work the same things we had shown through
theoretical analysis. Albeit, it turned out that the numerical expressions were very
sensitive and took a long time to compute. This hindered the process of searching
for optimal solutions greatly and ultimately resulted in the fact that a distinctive
optimal solution was not found.
An educated guess however was conjectured, and could possibly be proven, disregarded
or, more realistically, improved upon if more time had been available.
6.3
Optimal Energy for Periodic Solutions
Sadly there was not time to search for an exact optimal energy of the Hamiltonian
H as deﬁned by (2.1.1). A conjecture was set up in Conjecture 4.4, stating that
the optimal energy was given by 1.6. As reason behind this conjecture was given a
view of the Poincaré intersection plane when the energy was set to 1.61, where it
could be seen that no intersection actually occurred. Thus 1.61 was established as
an unreachable upper bound, which was later improved to 1.60025, for the optimal
energy. This however was not the case for 1.6 which was used throughout the report,
an energy where periodic solutions could be found. The reason the value 1.6 only was
conjectured was that the intersection in Figure 18 on page 47, where it shows that the
intersection between the forward time integration and the backward time integration
were not at the end of the respective curves. This could indicate that a small change
of the energy would still yield a periodic solution. This was explored further as we
dug into more speciﬁc values for E, and ended up with Equation (4.5.4) on page 48.
If it turns out that the ﬁnal conjecture does not hold, it gives a very good idea of what
the optimal energy is. Raising the energy by 0.00001 from the conjectured maximum
makes periodic solutions impossible as is evident from Figure 21 on page 52.
