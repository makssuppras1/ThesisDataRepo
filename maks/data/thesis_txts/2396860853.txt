Direct numerical simulation of laminar to turbulent transition in transient ﬂows
by Mattia CODA
The objective of this research is the investigation of the phenomenon of transition
from laminar to turbulent regime in transient ﬂows, as a continuation of the previ-
ous study conducted by G. E. Poulsen for his Master Thesis Project ’Direct numer-
ical simulations of solitary wave boundary layers’ (2017). The study is performed
numerically using the open source software OpenFOAM for simulating the ﬂow of
a solitary wave by means of DNS. The solver developed in the previous project, a
standard pimpleFoam software which has been modiﬁed in order to include the ac-
celeration produced by a solitary wave, is used for validating the model by compar-
ing the simulations results for laminar, laminar with vortex tubes and transitional
regimes with the existing laminar analytical solution for transient long waves and
with the results obtained in the previous project. The solver is modiﬁed at a later
time by including a perturbation of the pressure ﬁeld in order to investigate the pos-
sibility of triggering transition to turbulence. Two main perturbation approaches
are discussed: perturbation of the initial condition and continuous perturbation. The im-
plementation of the former is mainly used for developing a better understanding of
the problem. A speciﬁc mathematical expression for the continuous perturbation is
derived and it is demonstrated that, in the transitional regime, the variation of the
perturbation magnitude is able to drive the transition phase of the solitary wave,
allowing to move it up to earlier phases of the cycle compared to previous numer-
ical studies, and therefore to show a behaviour similar to the one experienced in
experimental tests. The ﬂow induced by the perturbation is analyzed by means of a
correlation analysis and ﬁnally the effects when the perturbation is applied at lower
Reynolds numbers are shown.

1 Introduction
Every kind of ﬂow can be classiﬁed into different regimes depending on the condi-
tions of the ﬂow itself. The two main ﬂow regimes are named laminar and turbulent,
which in turn may be classiﬁed into more sub-categories. The main difference be-
tween them resides in the way the adjacent layers of the ﬂuid behave: when the ﬂow
is in the laminar regime, the ﬂuid layers move parallel to each other, while in the tur-
bulent regime the layers mix as a consequence of the chaotic motion assumed by the
ﬂuid particles. The ﬂow regime is generally deﬁned using the Reynolds number Re,
which is a non-dimensional parameter that expresses the ratio between the inertial
forces acting on the ﬂuid particles and the viscous ones. At low Re the viscous forces
overcome the inertial ones, while the opposite happens at high Re. When the ﬂow
transitions from laminar to turbulent regime, instabilities start to grow making its
behaviour less predictable. The turbulent motion produces the mixing of energy
and momentum inside the ﬂow, resulting in higher velocity gradients close to the
surfaces on which the ﬂuid is ﬂowing and consequently larger shear stress. Due to
the different behaviour of laminar and turbulent ﬂows, any engineering application
must take into account the regime into which the ﬂow will be found.
The mechanisms leading to transition from laminar to turbulent regime are very
complex and at present still quite unknown, especially when dealing with unsteady
ﬂows. For this reason in recent years many researches have been carried out on the
topic and are currently being done. The present project focuses on the investigation
of transition inside boundary layers developing in transient ﬂows, with particular
attention on solitary wave boundary layers.
Both experimental and numerical studies have been carried out in the recent past
with the aim of better understanding the processes involved in this phenomenon.
Laboratory experiments generally allow to accurately reproduce the real world envi-
ronment, since the external conditions and the behaviour of the ﬂuid are physically
recreated, but intrinsic limitations exist, mainly given by the equipment required
and the time needed for the investigations. The experimental research is especially
demanding for complex research topics such as ﬂuid dynamics, in which the be-
haviour of the ﬂuid is often modiﬁed due to inﬁnitesimal differences of the initial
settings or due to disturbances occurring during the experimental procedure, which
makes the results uncertain and highly variable. Moreover, data gathering turns out
to be complicated due to the large number of variables involved and the need of
being not invasive in order not to inﬂuence the experiments outcome. Though, con-
ducting experiments allows to experience the physical phenomena as they happen,
which can be signiﬁcant for their understanding.
A numerical approach, on the other hand, broaden the experimental capabilities,
but the accurate reproduction of the real behaviour often represents a difﬁcult task.
The implementation of mathematical models allows to simulate the physics with a
certain accuracy which depends on the model itself. Computational Fluid Dynam-
ics is able to reproduce most of the ﬂuid mechanics phenomena, but highly accurate
models involve high computational costs and produce large amount of data. This

1.1
State of the art
The research on turbulent processes ﬁnds great advantages in the use of a numerical
approach for the reasons previously mentioned. Therefore, many of the recent inves-
tigations on the topic have been carried out numerically, even though experimental
works still represent a very important part of the research.
Turbulence processes in oscillatory boundary layers are similar to the ones occurring
in steady ﬂows, with the main difference that turbulence generation and decay are
regulated by the ﬂuid oscillations. Steady ﬂows have been broadly investigated in
the past, while fewer studies focused on turbulence generation in unsteady ﬂows
due to the higher complexity of the topic.
Points in commons and differences between the turbulence generation in steady and
unsteady ﬂows have been extensively studied by means of Direct Numerical Simu-
lations in [1], with the focus on oscillating ﬂows on a ﬂat wall and on the dynamics
of vortex structures generation inside the boundary layer when transitioning from
laminar to turbulent regime. According to previous experimental studies, wall im-
perfections, as well as other external disturbances, are fundamental for triggering
turbulence in unsteady ﬂows. For this reason, in [1] a wall characterized by small
imperfections has been considered in order to successfully simulate transition to
turbulence. Oscillatory ﬂows, such as the water waves, are characterized by a ﬁrst
phase in which the ﬂuid accelerates that is followed by a deceleration phase: this
behaviour is repeated periodically in time. Low-speed streaks form locally and tem-
porally close to the wall at the end of the accelerating phase of the cycle and their
intensity increases in the early stages of the decelerating phase, eventually breaking
and creating vortexes. The receptivity mechanism has been recently identiﬁed as the
leading mechanism for turbulence generation and maintenance: at high Reynolds
numbers a resonance mechanism is established for which the ﬂow transfers energy
to perturbations that are present in the ﬂow and the ﬂow transitions to turbulent as
a consequence of the growth of these perturbations. In the study reported in [1] this
mechanism is induced by the wall waviness earlier mentioned.
Many researches contributed to the attempts of giving mathematical explanations
of this complex mechanism. As a consequence of the stability analysis described
==PAGE:23==
1.1. State of the art
3
in [2], the interaction between two-dimensional waves, that may be present in the
ﬂow due to the resonance mechanism described above, and three-dimensional dis-
turbances which grow inside the ﬂow is mentioned in [1] as one possible cause for
the turbulence bursts. A second possible explanation for the turbulence triggering
mechanism, given in [3], is identiﬁed with the growth, at sufﬁciently high Re, of
a small perturbation composed by a combination of oblique and two-dimensional
waves characterized by speciﬁc amplitudes and wave numbers.
The mathematical analysis of the turbulence mechanism does not represent the main
focus of this project, but it is important to remember that the creation and develop-
ment of turbulence represents a very complex topic which is at present still quite
unknown, making it difﬁcult to model the phenomenon and therefore creating im-
pediments in the numerical research.
In chronological order, the transition phenomenon has been initially analyzed by
means of laboratory experiments due to the high computational requirements of the
numerical approach. The experimental observations of oscillatory ﬂows, performed
for the ﬁrst time already in 1974 and documented in [4], allowed to investigate the
ﬂow for different conditions and to deﬁne in this way four separate ﬂow regimes
depending on the ﬂow behaviour. The existence of these ﬂow regimes has been con-
ﬁrmed not only by following experimental studies, such as the research presented
in [5], but also by subsequent numerical investigations. The ﬁrst direct simulation
of transition in oscillatory boundary layers has been performed only in 1998 and it
is described in [6], where the ﬂow regimes are classiﬁed as follows:
1. laminar regime, which occurs at low Reynolds numbers and it is characterized
by smooth ﬂuid motion due to the dominance of viscous forces over inertial
ones.
2. disturbed laminar regime, in which the laminar ﬂow presents small-amplitude
superimposed perturbations.
3. intermittently turbulent ﬂow, where turbulence explosively bursts.
4. fully developed turbulent regime, in which turbulence is established during the
whole duration of the cycle.
Each of the mentioned regimes is characterized by a range of Reynolds numbers
which, however, cannot be deﬁned in a precise and univocal way. The behaviour of
the ﬂow, though, determines the distinction between the different regimes. As de-
scribed previously, towards the end of the decelerating phase of the cycle, when the
ﬂow starts to transition, low-speed streaks appear at the bed: the streaks generally
disappear during the accelerating phase without any consequence, but for higher
Re they start to interact and to coalesce with each other, eventually generating co-
herent vortex structures. However, these vortexes generally tend to stretch becom-
ing incoherent structures. Increasing even more the Reynolds number the velocity
components experience random three-dimensional ﬂuctuations and turbulence is
established, often without the appearance of any coherent structure. The disturbed
laminar regime and intermittently turbulent ﬂow are often mentioned respectively
as laminar regime with vortex tubes, due to the shape assumed by the vortex struc-
tures which are formed with the axis perpendicular to the streamwise direction, and
transitional: this terminology has been adopted in [7] and it will be used also in this
project.
==PAGE:24==

Chapter 1. Introduction
The numerical study of transition in unsteady ﬂows is developing quite slowly es-
pecially due to the difﬁculties that are found in triggering turbulence. As discussed
in [7], the laminar and laminar with vortex tubes regimes can be quite easily re-
produced by means of numerical simulations, while for higher Reynolds numbers
it is proven to be complex to recreate the behaviour assumed by the ﬂow in the
experiments.
Due to the fact that experimental research is often limited by the
Reynolds number that can be reached, since expensive equipment and high energy
consumption are necessary, this represents an important issue. Several numerical
studies showed that some kind of perturbation is necessary for triggering turbu-
lence generation, the source of which can be identiﬁed in different phenomena.
It has been demonstrated that the interaction between modes induced by external
perturbations, such as wall imperfections, and temporarily unstable modes, three-
dimensional effects, nonlinear interaction inside critical layers and viscous effects
have play an important role in triggering the turbulence mechanism.
Further numerical investigation has been more recently carried out in [8], where
the solitary wave boundary layer has been simulated using DNS, as it will be done
also for this research. For validating the model used the numerical results of laminar
regime simulations have been compared with the laminar analytical solution and the
experimental measurements presented in [9]: the same comparison has been done
in [7] and it will be also necessary in the present project for proving the correct-
ness of the model implemented. The approach followed in [8] consisted in adding
a divergence-free perturbation to the initial velocity ﬁeld, which does not affect the
simulations when the ﬂow regime is laminar, while it triggers transition to turbu-
lence for higher Reynolds numbers. All the ﬂow regimes have been reproduced
modifying the values of two parameters which deﬁne the problem: a dimensionless
wave amplitude, given by the ratio between wave height and water depth, and the
boundary layer thickness. The ﬁndings showed that a critical value of wave ampli-
tude exists for which, for larger amplitudes, turbulence is generated behind the crest
wave, allowing to describe in detail the phenomenon of turbulence development.
The turbulent ﬂuctuations grow as the wave amplitude is increased, while the time
during which the ﬂow reverses decreases since turbulence enhances the momentum
exchange inside the boundary layer. Turbulent kinetic energy is produced close to
the bottom after the passage of the wave crest and it is diffused in the vertical di-
rection before decaying. In a similar way, the Reynolds stresses start to grow and
reach a peak after which they tend to decrease, showing the explosive generation of
turbulent ﬂuctuations. However, even for the highest wave amplitude considered,
in the numerical simulations turbulence is always generated behind the wave crest,
after the ﬂow reversal. Though, as conﬁrmed by experimental studies, especially by
[10] and [11], at high Re turbulence can be generated already at earlier phases of the
cycle. Speciﬁcally, in [10] and [11] the ﬂow behaviour has been reproduced by means
of an oscillating water tunnel that allowed the description of the coherent structures
which develop inside the ﬂow for a wide range of Reynolds numbers. Laminar, dis-
turbed laminar regime and intermittently turbulent ﬂow have been reproduced with
characteristics similar to the ones presented in previous works and it is found that
turbulence occurrence moves up to lower phase values as Re is increased. However,
due to experimental limitations Reynolds numbers higher than Re = 2.0 · 106 could
not be tested, but the expectation is to possibly spread turbulence towards earlier
phases increasing Re. The tests presented in [10] and [11] will be considered in this
project for validating and analyzing the numerical results.

OpenFOAM
OpenFOAM is an open source CFD software that allows to solve a very broad range
of ﬂuid and continuum mechanics problems and it has been selected for simulating
the aforementioned phenomenon of solitary wave boundary layers. The advantage
of this choice lies in the fact that OpenFOAM utilities, solvers and libraries can be
extended by the users to the extent of physics and programming techniques knowl-
edge.
The procedure of running OpenFOAM can be divided in three separate stages: Pre-
processing, Solving and Post-processing.
The software uses deﬁned solvers, by
means of which the dictionaries created by the user are read, for computing the
solutions of the simulations. Hence, several different ﬁles are involved at each stage
and the main ones are brieﬂy described in this Section. Details about the ﬁles setup
can be found in Appendix A.
2.2.1
Pre-processing
During the Pre-processing stage the simulation is set up: the necessary ﬁles are cre-

tions consist of the solver, which will be illustrated in detail in the next Section, the
folders constant, system, and the initial conditions folder, which are described below.
The constant folder contains ﬁles that include the physical properties for the spe-
ciﬁc application and a subdirectory polyMesh containing a dictionary, blockMeshDict,
which describes the mesh to be generated. Examples of ﬁles containing the physical
properties are transportProperties, where the kinematic viscosity value is speciﬁed,
which is set to ν = 10−6 for this project, and turbulenceProperties, which deﬁnes the
choice of the turbulence modelling method. The dictionary RASProperties speciﬁes
the RAS model when Reynolds-averaged simulation are used, as performed here.
For the project purpose, the waveProperties ﬁle is created, where the values of am-
plitude and period of the solitary wave are set. One more ﬁle, perturbParameters is
added at a later stage when the solver is modiﬁed by including a perturbation of the
pressure ﬁeld which will be later described.

together with more algorithm parameters. Other dictionaries may be deﬁned de-

The solving stage consists in running the simulation, hence in solving the equations
deﬁned in the desired solver. The solver used in this project is essentially the stan-
dard pimpleFoam solver which has been slightly modiﬁed in [7] in order to take into
account the acceleration of a solitary wave by adding it as body force to the mo-
mentum equation. The pimpleFoam solver assumes an incompressible, unsteady and
viscous ﬂow and it allows to perform DNS. The employment of DNS implies that no
turbulence model is used and the whole range of spatial and temporal scales of tur-
bulence is resolved. This is implemented by setting RASModel as simulationType in
the turbulenceProperties dictionary, hence setting the use of Reynolds-averaged sim-
ulation, and switching off the use of turbulence modelling in the relative dictionary
RASProperties.

lowing continuity equation,

• β = 0: the solution does not change between two iterations.
The relaxation factor has been set as β = 1 for the velocity ﬁeld in order to inﬂu-

equations, while at the same time obtaining a smoother convergence rate.

When solving numerically partial differential equations, in order to obtain the con-
vergence it is necessary to satisfy the Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy condition which, in
a n-dimensional case, is described by

where C is called the CFL number. The value of Cmax depends on the solver method
and it is typically set to 1 for explicit methods as the one used in this project. This
condition expresses a ratio between the length travelled by information at each time
step and the relative cell length. If the ratio is higher than 1, the information travels
more than a cell length during one time step and the knowledge of the travel inside
this cell is lost, causing the simulation to miss information and possibly not to con-
verge. Of course, the more the CFL condition is restrictive, the more the simulation
is accurate. However, if Cmax is reduced smaller time-steps are required and there-
fore the computational time increases. Hence, a compromise between simulation
accuracy and computational time is often necessary. In this project the indications
given in [7] are followed and for the most of the simulations Cmax = 0.6 is used
since it has been shown that the numerical results are accurate enough. The value
of Cmax is reduced when higher accuracy is required or increased for reducing the
computational time.

Post-processing
Post-processing includes all the procedures of handling and visualizing the outputs
of the simulations. Several functionalities of OpenFOAM are available at this stage
in order to perform ﬁeld calculations, ﬂow rate, forces and force coefﬁcients calcu-
lations, data sampling and much more, both at run-time and post-run. Third-party
software are also available for data analysis and visualization: MATLAB and Math-
ematica are the computational tools used for data processing and ParaView for data
exploration in 3D.

3 Tests setup
In order to investigate the behaviour of the solitary wave boundary layer and to
reproduce the different conditions in which the ﬂow can be found several different
tests are required. In the present Chapter the general criteria for the deﬁnition of a
particular case study are introduced. In order to setup a simulation three main steps
are followed: choice of the solitary wave parameters which deﬁne the solitary wave be-
haviour, deﬁnition of the computational domain and boundaries in order to reproduce
the external physical conditions in which the phenomenon is intended to be simu-
lated, and the mesh generation, that consists in determining the elements type and
size for discretizing the domain. Depending on the case study the setup procedure
may differ, but the boundary conditions are kept the same for all the simulations of
the present project since they are deﬁned by the problem investigated.

3.2
Domain and boundaries
The computational domain represents the region inside which the ﬂow is simulated:
the region considered in this project is deﬁned as a simple three-dimensional rectan-
gular box. The domain size is modiﬁed depending on the simulation purpose: the
bigger the domain, the less the boundaries may inﬂuence the ﬂow behaviour, but

preliminary domain size considered is [80δ1 40δ1 40δ1], respectively the length in the

is reduced at later stages to [13δ1 20δ1 10δ1] due to computational time requirements.
The following boundary conditions are set at the faces of the computational domain
in order to recreate the phenomenon investigated:
• No-slip condition and zero pressure gradient are set to the bottom surface in order
to simulate the presence of the wall.
• Cyclic boundary is imposed on the sides: what exits the front boundary is

• Slip condition is imposed on the top surface so that viscous effects between the
ﬂuid and the boundary are neglected.
3.3
Mesh generation criteria
The mesh generation is an important phase of the pre-processing procedure for ob-
taining an accurate solution. The computational domain is divided in cells, small
sub-domains, so that the governing equations can be discretized and solved inside
each sub-domain.
A general criteria suggests that the smaller the cells, the more accurate the solution
is, since the computational domain is consequently better discretized. The cells di-
mensions are generally set in order to obtain a solution that is independent of the
cells size itself, hence reaching the mesh convergence. However, as the cells size is
reduced the computational cost is increased, due to the higher number of cells, and
therefore a compromise between solution accuracy and computational cost is often
needed depending on the application and purpose.
Several domain sizes have been used in this project for different simulations, which
will be separately discussed for each case, while the same mesh generation criteria
has been adopted for the entire project. Since in [7] the small eddies have been
successfully reproduced, the same rectangular mesh and grid spacing is considered
sufﬁciently reﬁned for this project purpose. The smallest grid spacing is set in the

5 Turbulence transition
In this Chapter the issue of triggering turbulence transition in Direct Numerical Sim-
ulations is investigated. The case considered for this purpose is test 15 of [11]: it has
already been described how, in the numerical simulation at Re = 2.0 · 106, turbu-
lence develops only at late phases, after the ﬂow reversal, while experimentally, at
the same Re, it generates already at earlier phases. The purpose here is to generate
perturbations in the ﬂow which may activate the turbulence generation mechanism,
reproducing in this manner real disturbances that lead to transition at earlier phases.
Several different approaches have been taken into consideration in earlier investiga-
tions, among which the most common is represented by the implementation of a
wall waviness. However, that is not the case of this project since the wall is not mod-
iﬁed from the ideal case. Two main approaches have been adopted instead, which
will be separately discussed in this Section: perturbation of the initial status and
continuous perturbation of the ﬂow. The domain considered for the simulations is
the same used for the transitional regime tests.
5.0.1
pisoFoam
As mentioned earlier pimpleFoam is the solver used for the present investigation.
Nevertheless, the possibility of using the pisoFoam solver is also investigated. pim-
pleFoam is described in [16] as a large time-step solver for incompressible, turbulent
ﬂow which uses the PIMPLE algorithm with the possibility of applying relaxation
factors for smoothing the solution. Since the purpose here is to perturb the ﬂow in
order to activate turbulence mechanisms, it is believed that pisoFoam is a more ap-
propriate solver: the pressure-momentum coupling is calculated only once, hence
the solution is produced by directly solving the Navier-Stokes equations. No cor-
rection and smoothing of the solution is performed, possibly allowing turbulence to
develop more easily due to the disturbances introduced. For this reason the possibil-
ity of reproducing the aforementioned tests using the pisoFoam solver is investigated.
Speciﬁcally, test 15 is taken into account being the one of most interest for the project
purpose. Hence, the parameter nOuterCorrectors in the fvSolution dictionary is modi-
ﬁed to 1 for replicating the PISO algorithm and the relaxation factors are deleted for
not smoothing the perturbed solution. The simulation had to be run several times
with a decreasing CFL number in order to obtain an accurate solution. Figure 5.1
below show the results for the bed shear stress using Cmax = 0.05.

5.1
Perturbation of the initial condition
In [8] it is described that a divergence-free perturbation is applied at the initially
vanishing velocity ﬁeld in order to facilitate the turbulence transition. The intention
here is to produce a similar perturbation to be introduced into the ﬂow. Since no fur-
ther details regarding the applied perturbation are available, two options have been
taken into consideration: perturbation of the velocity ﬁeld and perturbation of the
pressure ﬁeld. The ﬁrst approach would require the continuity equation, Equation
2.12, to be satisﬁed by the perturbed initial velocity ﬁeld, which is not trivial. For this
reason the second approach is followed and the initial pressure ﬁeld is perturbed. In
this way the mass conservation is not violated and the ﬂow velocity is perturbed as
a consequence of the initial pressure gradients. At a later stage of the project also
an example of valid perturbation of the velocity ﬁeld has been suggested by David
R. Furhman, but it could not be tested in this project. However, this is included in
Appendix C.2.
In order to apply a perturbation to the initial pressure ﬁeld, the package swak4Foam
is employed. Speciﬁcally, the funkySetFields utility is used, that allows to create and
manipulate ﬁelds with mathematical expressions. The related dictionary, funkySet-
FieldsDict, in which it is speciﬁed the ﬁeld that is intended to be manipulated and
the mathematical expression to be assigned, is required to be added in the system
folder: details about the dictionary created can be found in Appendix A.2.3 .

the ﬂow behaviour of laboratory experiments in which early transition has been ex-

perturbation of high magnitude does not represent the cause of early transition in
the experimental results, while a small continuous disturbance would represent a
more realistic situation: hence, the possibility of implementing a different perturba-
tion which can affect continuously the simulation is investigated.
5.2
Continuous perturbation

occurs in real life experiments due to, for example, ﬂuid-structure interactions: the
waviness of the bed, for instance, produces a disturbance of the ideal case that is
continuous in time and it can vary in magnitude. This may be the case of the experi-
ments presented in [11], where a non ideally smooth bed may have caused the early
transition at Re = 2.0 · 106, which instead has not been experienced in the numerical

6 Conclusions
The aim of this research has been the investigation of transition from laminar to
turbulent regime in the boundary layers generated beneath a solitary wave. The
study has been conducted numerically by means of Direct Numerical Simulations
using as starting point the mathematical model developed in [7] for reproducing
this speciﬁc phenomenon in OpenFOAM.
It has been necessary to initially validate the model that was intended to use. First,
the laminar regime of the ﬂow has been studied, since an analytical solution describ-
ing laminar solitary wave boundary layers exists, which allowed to compare the
numerical results with the theoretical ones. After proving that the laminar regime
could accurately be reproduced at Re = 2.6 · 104, the same model has been used for
simulating the laminar with vortex tubes and transitional regimes. As reported in
literature, it has been shown that the vortex structures develop during the decelerat-
ing part of the oscillatory ﬂow cycle as a consequence of the unsteadiness arising in-
side the ﬂow. At Re = 4.4 · 105, the quasi-two-dimensional structures of vortex tubes
could be detected by visualizing the vorticity and bed shear stress ﬁelds in ParaView,
and by analyzing the behaviour in time of the bed shear stress signal which deviates
from the analytical solution when the vortexes are generated. Then, the Reynolds
number has been further increased to Re = 2.0 · 106, value for which, as shown by
the experiments of [11], the transitional regime is established in the boundary layer:
no deﬁned vortex structures form anymore, but the incipient vortexes develop into
turbulent ﬂow. However, although the behaviour of the ﬂow resulted to be modiﬁed
compared to the laminar regime with vortex tubes, turbulence was generated only
at late phases of the decelerating part of the cycle, resulting in a disagreement with
the experimental behaviour of the ﬂow at the same Re value, and therefore showing
an issue in triggering the same transition process.
The issue of triggering transition in numerical simulations is well-known and it has
been often approached by considering wavy walls with the aim of introducing dis-
turbances into the ﬂow and inducing in this way the turbulence mechanism. In this
project a different approach has been used following the example of [8]: a random
divergence-free perturbation is introduced into the ﬂow. The options of perturbing
the velocity and pressure ﬁelds have been both taken into consideration, but for con-
venience only the latter has been ﬁnally pursued. However, a possible expression
for a divergence-free perturbation of the velocity ﬁeld has been mentioned in the
project.
A ﬁrst attempt consisted in perturbing the initial pressure ﬁeld, considering this
equivalent to what has been done in [8], which however did not allowed to signiﬁ-
cantly move transition up to earlier phases of the cycle due to the decay in time of the
perturbation. The implementation of the same perturbation at later phases allowed
to reduce its decay at the phases at which instabilities start to develop and it showed
that the transition could be triggered also at earlier phases. However, despite the
positive results, it is believed that a proper scaling of the perturbation was not car-
ried out at that stage. The perturbation applied has been then modiﬁed in order to
==PAGE:78==
58
Chapter 6. Conclusions
make it continuous in time and therefore solve the decay issue. The scaling has been
also modiﬁed and a new expression of the perturbation was derived, presented in
Equation 5.13, which is believed to be more correct for the purpose, allowing also
to reduce the grid dependence in the vertical direction where the grid grading was
necessarily applied. The perturbation has been directly calculated inside the solver
in order to be modiﬁed at each iteration and it has been characterized with a corre-
lation function related to a characteristic time scale of the ﬂow. Even though issues
regarding the actual magnitude of the applied disturbance are still present, the im-
plementation of different perturbation magnitude showed a clear trend: the higher is
the perturbation introduced, the earlier the ﬂow transitions. This allowed to induce
turbulence occurrence in solitary wave boundary layers before the ﬂow reversal, dif-
ferently from what obtained in previous researches, and consequently to reproduce
a behaviour in the transitional regime that is similar to what is experienced experi-
mentally. The ﬂow induced by the perturbation has been investigated by means of a
correlation analysis that showed that macro time scales which are comparable with
the perturbation time scale characterize the ﬂow.
Moreover, it has been showed that the analytical solution can be still reproduced
when an equivalent perturbation is applied in the laminar regime, meaning that the
solver which includes the perturbation still accurately describes the behaviour of the
solitary wave in this speciﬁc regime. No vortex tube structures have been identiﬁed
when the perturbation has been applied in the laminar regime with vortex tubes
case, while the behaviour of the ﬂow in the transitional regime seemed to resemble
the occurrence of sudden turbulence spots.
6.1
Recommendations for future work
In a future continuation of the present work it would be ﬁrst recommended to solve
the issues regarding the actual magnitude of the perturbation applied, since the stan-
dard deviation of the perturbation ﬁeld does not correspond to the intended value.
This would allow a better characterization of the perturbation implemented.
A second important step would be to make the perturbation grid-independent for
simplifying the comparison of different simulation cases by means of the exact same
perturbation. In order to accomplish this a different approach should probably be
followed, for instance considering a wavy bottom wall. Also the reported pertur-
bation of the velocity ﬁeld seemed to be a valid path for this investigation purpose,
either by applying it as sudden perturbation during the simulation, approach that
produced positive results in the present project when tested with the pressure ﬁeld
perturbation, or by making it satisfy the involved equations even when applied as
continuous perturbation.
As described in this project, pimpleFoam is believed to be the right solver for allowing
the perturbation to naturally grow inside the ﬂow and it could be worth a further
study in case the computational resources would allow it.
Finally, the correlation analysis performed on the ﬂow should be carried on for a
longer simulation in order to analyze the characteristic macro time scales of the ﬂow
and the investigation should be continued by considering a larger computational
domain in order to allow the exploration of the possible occurrence of turbulence
spots once the perturbation is applied.
