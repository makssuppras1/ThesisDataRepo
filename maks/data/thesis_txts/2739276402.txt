Abstract
Many studies have explored the impact of climate change on wind speeds, often extrap-
olating surface winds without fully considering the underlying assumptions. The majority
of these studies apply a power law to 10 m wind speeds to assess changes at hub height,
100 m. The main assumption lies in the shear exponent of the power law. Some stud-
ies use a constant shear exponent regardless of terrain (PL), while others differentiate
between land and water (PL-O). However, this technique can amplify the signal of wind
speed changes, potentially leading to an overestimation. This study aims to investigate
the implications of these assumptions when assessing climate change impacts on Euro-
pean wind resources and power density.
To achieve this, changes in wind speed between a historical period (1995-2014) and a
future period (2051-2070) are analyzed using 13 Coupled Model Intercomparison Project
Phase 6 (CMIP6) climate models and ERA5 reanalysis data. Data are collected with a
3-hour temporal resolution across latitudes 22ºN to 73ºN and longitudes 22ºE to 47ºW,
under the SSP5-8.5 climate scenario representing significant human emissions.
Additionally, this study examines alternative approaches to obtain 100 m wind data. The
first approach involves using a more realistic shear exponent derived from ERA5 reanal-
ysis data (PL-ERA5). Other methods do not rely on the power law. The first method
involves obtaining 100 m winds directly from climate model simulations, using the hyp-
sometric equation to determine the models’ heights and interpolating between the two
nearest available heights (CMIP6-100m). Lastly, two additional methods are introduced
that adjust 10 m wind distributions from climate models to match historical 100 m data:
adjusting the 10 m mean (BC) or adjusting the entire distribution to match the historical
distribution at 100 m (QM).
A study on the variability of shear exponents demonstrates that they cannot be assumed
constant spatially or temporally, as they are strongly linked to land cover. It is also ob-
served that this parameter varies depending on the climate model, further weakening the
assumption of a constant shear.
The results in wind distribution show that using a power law overestimates wind changes,
especially when using a constant shear, highlighting the importance of accurately estimat-
ing this parameter. Results from both methods adjusting the 10 m distribution to match the
100 m data do not overestimate the change as much. The method using the hypsometric
equation is seen as the baseline, as it does not involve the use of 10 m wind simulations.
All these exaggerations are amplified when studying nonlinear variables such as power
density.
The study concludes by identifying the weaknesses of the power law hypothesis but high-
lights its use as an initial assessment tool for wind resource evaluation, providing insights
into how climate change will impact wind resources across European territory, despite
exaggerating projections in certain areas.

Introduction
Since the onset of the Industrial Revolution, human activities have significantly contributed
to the rise in global temperatures. The sixth report of the Intergovernmental Panel on
Climate Change (IPCC) highlights the undeniable link between human greenhouse gas
emissions and the observed increase in global temperatures. Over the last decade, tem-
peratures have risen by 1.1◦C compared to the global average from 1850-1900 [1].
To address this situation and meet the objectives of the Paris Agreement, which limits
global warming to well below 2◦C and preferably to 1.5◦C above preindustrial levels [2],
society is undergoing a process of decarbonizing its energy systems by shifting to renew-
able energy sources. To limit the temperature increase to 1.5◦C, renewable sources must
supply 52%-67% of the total primary energy sources [1].
In the electricity sector, wind, solar, and hydropower are leading the renewable energy
sources for electricity generation. Advances in wind power technologies and wind farm
construction have increased the economic attractiveness of wind farms in many regions
[3]. By 2035, the share of electricity generation from wind power could range from 4% to
more than triple the 2019 share, depending on global CO2 emissions policies. With the
1.5◦C cap, this range widens and could be as high as 34%, according to [4]. Although
the exact values vary significantly depending on the emission reduction measures imple-
mented, it is clear that wind energy is confirming its position as a crucial technology for
combating climate change.
However, investment in wind energy production faces challenges due to uncertainties
surrounding the future revenues of wind energy producers. These uncertainties arise
from the natural variability of the resource and evolving regulatory policies, but also from
the expected effects of climate change on future wind energy production [5]. Thus, it is
crucial to examine future changes in wind resources to better understand and mitigate
potential risks to wind energy investments.
1.1
Objectives of the thesis
Many studies have looked into how climate change impacts on wind speed. Neverthe-
less, the majority of them often extrapolate surface winds without fully considering the
assumptions underlying these simplifications. The most common technique is to use a
power law with a constant shear exponent, regardless of the terrain. This extrapolation
technique might amplify the signal of change in wind speed, leading to an overestimation
of the change.
This study aims to investigate the actual impact of these assumptions. By addressing
these issues, our goal is to improve the accuracy and reliability of assessments on the
effects of climate change on wind resources. We would particularly investigate the impact
on the European wind resource.
It is worth noting that this thesis does not aim to replicate previous studies on the topic of
climate change. Instead, it questions whether the current methods should continue to be
used or if alternative approaches could yield more reliable results.
In order to assess the changes in wind resources and to test the most commonly used
methods, this thesis aims to answer four research questions, which are presented below.

Key Research Questions
1. Is it valid to use a unique shear exponent when assessing the change of wind re-
source due to climate change?
2. What are the implications of mainstream assumptions for wind energy production
projections in Europe? Do current methods overestimate/underestimate the aver-
age wind speed change at 100 meters?
3. Can alternative methodologies ensure more accurate results in the face of climate
change?
4. What are the effects of climate change on Europe’s wind resources? How does it
impact wind power?
1.2
Research methodology
After making a systematic review of the most important articles on how to assess the
impact of climate change on wind resources, the overall procedure followed during the
thesis can be outlined as seen in Figure 1.1.
The process starts with climate data from 13 different CMIP6 models (Coupled Model
Intercomparison Project Phase 6). This climate data includes obtaining wind speeds at
100 m (u100) directly from the CMIP6 simulations, following the steps explained by Andrea
Hahmann et al. in [6] (blue line). The other data used is the 10 m wind speeds (u10). This
data would need to be extrapolated to a 100 m, using one this two approaches.
• Power Law: This method uses the power law for extrapolating the wind to hub
height.

2.1.1
History of CMIP6
The best way to explain the CMIP project is to start by defining what a climate model is.
A climate model is essentially a complex ’computer code’ designed to simulate a digital
Earth, modeling the interactions among different Earth systems: the atmosphere, ocean,
land surface, cryosphere, and biosphere. These models also introduced the effects of hu-
man activities, to predict how our actions might influence the future climate [7]. Figure 2.1
provides a simplify diagram on the interaction of different Earth systems.
Figure 2.1: Schematic view of the components and interactions in a coupled Earth system
model. Adapted from [8]

5
==PAGE:20==
General Circulation Models (GCMs) model the atmosphere and oceans using a three-
dimensional grid, solving equations for fluid motion and energy transfer between grid
points. These GCMs are designed so that different subsystems (as shown in Figure 2.1)
can be run independently or coupled together. For example, the atmosphere module can
be run separately or coupled with an ocean model, with a coupler handling the trans-
fers of energy, water, and momentum at the ocean surface. Further components can be
integrated into a GCM, including modules representing sea ice, atmospheric chemistry
(such as dust and air pollution), ocean biochemistry, vegetation, and carbon cycles. This
integration forms the essence of a climate model [8].
Building on this, the World Climate Research Program (WCRP) initiated the Coupled
Model Intercomparison Project (CMIP) in 1995 to improve the understanding of climate
trends from the past, present, and future using different GCMs [9]. CMIP integrates dif-
ferent models from different research institutions around the world. In this way, CMIP
established a database of Global Climate Models following a common framework. The
different model simulations were used to understand why different models gave different
results even when given identical model input was used, and to understand which results
are commonly agreed upon between models [7].
The initial phases (CMIP1) of the project were simple. The Earth-models simulated the at-
mosphere, the ocean dynamics, the ice-sea interaction and a simple land surface model.
These early models incorporated a simple control scenario, in which human carbon diox-
ide (CO2) emissions increased at a rate of 1% annually [10]. The objective of phase 1
was to document the errors of the coupled models. This was done, comparing the mean
output of the models to current observations to determine how well the Global Climate
Models were simulating the climate, thereby quantifying the systematic error present in
the different models [11].
But where do these systematic errors in climate models come from? They happen be-
cause of a simplification of the global climate models, as we lack the necessary compu-
tational capacity to simulate all the details of Earth system’s interactions. Therefore, the
different earth systems are simplified and simulated independently of each other, then
linked by means of energy, momentum, and mass fluxes. Take for example the inter-
action between the atmosphere and ocean. There is a flux of heat, water, temperature
and net solar radiation. Also, the atmosphere is influence by the land and ice as see in
Figure 2.1. When the model components are coupled together, errors in the fluxes due
to the simplification of the systems result in errors in the climate model’s output variables
(e.g., mean precipitation, wind speed, sea surface temperature), creating systematic er-
rors in the global climate model. These early models often required “flux adjustments” to
overcome these simulation errors and to keep their simulated climate from drifting during
long-term predictions [11].
Since then, CMIP has evolved through five different phases, becoming a major interna-
tional multimodel research activity. More information about the different phases of the
project can be found in the following references [12, 13, 14]. Furthermore, CMIP has po-
sitioned itself as a centerpiece for national and international climate change assessment,
marking a new era in climate science.
For this research, we have used the latest phase of the project, CMIP6. The models used
in this phase no longer require flux adjustments. Furthermore, many additional submodels
have been incorporated to make the model closer to a real-Earth system. This phase aims
to improve understanding of important physical processes in the climate system and the
response of the climate system to external drivers, such as increasing greenhouse gas

2.1.2
Bias on CMIP6
Even though the new models and data used to simulate climate have become more de-
tailed and accurate, they still show consistent differences from real-world observations.
These errors are often called bias. Bias in climate models refers to the systematic differ-
ence between what a model predicts and the actual observed climate properties. These
properties could include mean temperature, variance, or extreme weather events that oc-
cur once every 100 years. ”Systematic” means these differences are not due to random
variations or sampling errors [16].
One of the main reasons for bias, apart from the simplifications of Earth’s systems in
climate models, is the output resolution of these models. Typically, GCMs operate at
a relatively coarse spatial resolution, ranging from about 100 to 200 kilometers per grid
cell. As a result, many regional climate features are not accurately represented, leading
to an incomplete understanding of the physical processes. This can cause errors when
correcting misunderstandings [17, 18].
In recent years, numerous studies and techniques have come out to try to correct this
bias. However, a number of assumptions have to be made about to use them. The main
assumption is to consider bias invariant over time. This means that the methods assume
the magnitude of bias remains the same for both historical and future climate model simu-
lations. In other words, if a model consistently overestimates or underestimates a certain
climate variable in the past, it is assumed that the same overestimation or underestimation
will occur in future projections [19].
Time-invariant bias corrections imply calibrating an empirical transfer function between
simulated and observed climate data and apply this function to model outputs. This pro-
cess is a post-processing step. There are two commonly used approaches:
• Simple mean adjustment: This method estimates the bias as the difference (or
ratio for variables like precipitation or wind speed) between simulated and observed
means over a reference period. The simulated data are then adjusted over a future
period by subtracting the bias or rescaling the data accordingly.
• Quantile Mapping: This method adjusts each quantile of the distribution individu-
The Impact of Assumptions on European Wind Energy Production in the Face of Climate Change
7
==PAGE:22==
ally.
These transfer functions are applied to climate change simulations under the assumption
that the biases do not change over time. Bias correction relies on observational reference
data, which are often model-generated, particularly for gridded data sets.
The time-invariant assumption for the bias simplifies the correction process but may not
always be accurate, as the actual bias can vary over time due to changes in climate dy-
namics and external influences. Therefore, while time-invariant bias correction methods
are widely used, it’s important to recognize their limitations and the potential need for more
sophisticated approaches that account for temporal variations in bias.

Reanalysis data: ERA5
CMIP6 offers a better simulation of climate systems, which are much more advanced and
correct than the previous phases. However, systemic biases persist due to the simplifi-
cation inherent in modeling the complex systems of our planet. Additionally, the spatial
resolution of climate models remains relatively coarse, needing higher resolutions to ef-
fectively study local changes.
Therefore, we are going to use ERA5, the fifth generation reanalysis product generated
by the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). ERA5 is con-
sidered the state of the art in this type of data, providing global climate and weather data
for the past eight decades.
Reanalysis combines model data with observations from around the world into a complete
and robust dataset using the laws of physics. This process is known as ’data assimilation’
and is similar to the processes used by numerical weather prediction centers. In weather
forecasting, data assimilation works by updating prediction models with new observations
every few hours (12 hours in the case of ECMWF) to produce a new estimate of the state
of the atmosphere. This corrected estimate is then used to issue a new forecast.
ERA5 operates on the same principle but at a reduced resolution. This lower resolution
allows for the creation of datasets that span several decades. Unlike real-time weather
forecasting, reanalysis is not constrained by the need to produce timely predictions. This
additional time allows for the collection of more observations and the incorporation of

improved versions of historical data. As a result, reanalysis data provides the most com-
plete picture possible of past weather and climate by blending observations and past
short-range weather forecasts with modern forecasting models to fill gaps in information,
providing a globally, time-consistent gridded dataset that ensures integrity and coher-
ence in representing the main Earth system cycles [20, 21]. Figure 2.3 shows a simple
schematic of how ECMWF generates its reanalysis products.

The ERA5 provides hourly estimates for numerous climate variables at a resolution of

the u (eastward) and v (northward) wind components at 100 m and 10 m heights, repre-
senting averages over the model grid box. By combining both, the speed and direction of
the horizontal 100 m and 10 m wind can be obtained [20].
The ERA5 and CMIP models employ different modeling approaches and use different
inputs, each with its own set of objectives, although in themselves aligned. Understanding
these differences allows us to use both sources without compromise, while ensuring the
reliability of the reanalysis through full validation. However
It should be pointed out that reanalysis data have intrinsic issues which differentiate them

phenomenon.

2.3
Resolution standardization of the models
As seen in section 2.1, various CMIP6 models from distinct research institutions use dif-
ferent resolutions. For the purpose of data comparison between models and result vali-
dation, it is essential that all models share a common resolution or grid. Therefore, after
performing all the necessary calculations on the native grid of each model, it is conve-
nient to define a common grid for all models for future comparison. Following a standard
procedure, a grid of 1◦× 1◦was chosen.
The Earth System Modeling Framework (ESMF) webpage provides a common procedure
and tool for regridding operations. Regridding, also known as remapping, is the method
of changing the grid underlying data values while preserving the qualities of the original
data [24].
Every climate model uses the standard representation of Earth as a sphere, with coordi-
nates expressed in terms of longitude and latitude. On this grid, each point correspond
to a specific geographical location, covering an area where the dynamics of climate pro-
cesses are calculated. These girds are 2D, formed of uniform quadrilateral, making the
transformation to a common resolution grid easier.
Following the discussion on how climate model grids are set up, the purpose of regrid-
ding becomes clear. The grid standardization process involves rescaling the existing 2D
spherical coordinate grid data of the climate model to another 2D spherical coordinate
grid. However, it is crucial to preserve the integrity of the climate simulation outputs dur-
ing the regridding process, as any alteration could result in the loss of significant findings
from the climate models.
Regridding can be broken into two stages. The first stage involves the creation of an
interpolation weight matrix that describes how points in the source grid contribute to points
in the destination grid. The second stage is the multiplication of values on the source grid
by the interpolation weight matrix to produce values on the destination grid [24].
There are numerous kinds of transformations and regridding methods that are appropriate
for different problems. After carefully reviewing all the available methods for the remap-
ping operation provided by ESMF [24], we narrow down our options to two methods:
bi-linear interpolation and first-conservative.
Bi-linear interpolation is a well known way of interpolating climates models to a common
grid. It has been used in many climate change researches, [25], [26], [27]. It works by
assuming that the value of the new grid cell lies between the values of neighboring cells.
To estimate the value in the new grid, bilinear interpolation weights the values between
the four surrounding cells based on the distance to obtain the new value.
The first-conservative method works differently. It also extrapolates to the new grid but
takes into account the weighted overlapping of cells of the old grid into the new one. It
also preserves the integral of the source field across the regridding, ensuring that the flux
is the same in the old grid cell and in the new one. This ensures that the total quantity of
the variable being remapped remains constant, which is crucial for maintaining the signal
for the climate model simulation [28].
The choice of interpolation method depends on how the data will be used and the char-
acteristics of the original variable. Bilinear interpolation works well for smoothly varying
variables in many cases. However, for variables with discontinuous changes, like 3-hourly
data, bilinear interpolation may not be suitable. As explained in [29], when trying to inter-
polate 3-hourly precipitation data from high to lower resolution using bi-linear interpolation,

If we look first at the study regions, the studies have covered a wide range of geographic

As we have seen in section 2.1, climate models have a number of biases that are well

Earlier studies prefer not to include any preprocessing of the climate inputs, as there was
not much agreement on how to process these biases in the simulations.
However, the newer studies have included the use of bias corrected methods to correct
the wind speeds. Among the methods used, it is worth to mention linear scaling and

a more holistic representation of the effect of climate change on wind speed. Typically,
studies take from 5 to 15 different climate models.

We have observed that the most interesting results are eventually the changes in wind at

Within these studies, the method used to extrapolate from 10 to 100 m is what makes
they differ. Also, the extrapolation method can have significant implications in the found
results as they can either amplify or attenuate wind speeds at higher altitudes, leading to
varying conclusions about future wind energy potential.

3.2.1
Power law: definition, assumptions, and weaknesses
Until now, two major assumptions have prevailed regarding the operation of wind turbines.
Firstly, that turbines operates at heights within the surface layer of the atmosphere. The
second assumption, is that the wind field in the swept area of the blades will be the same
as the one encountered at hub height, regardless of the additional height the blades add.
In this study, we will not question these practices and will continue to make these assump-
tions.

stability effects in the wind profile. Atmospheric stability is related to the temperature
profile with height on the atmospheric surface layer. Temperature changes drive buoyancy
in the atmosphere, thus influencing the vertical wind profile [37].

The wind shear exponent (α), plays a crucial role in the vertical extrapolations of wind
speed above the Earth’s surface. Essentially, it describes the vertical gradient of wind
speed. A larger shear exponent means that the wind speed changes faster with height,
while a smaller shear exponent implies a more gradual change. The shear exponent is
always a positive value.
The value of α is highly influenced by the terrain over which the wind flows. In flat, open
terrains, such as plains or oceans, where obstacles are minimal, the shear exponent
usually has a lower value, resulting in a less steep increase in wind speed with altitude
in those topographies. On the other hand, in terrains characterized by hills, forests, or
urban areas, where obstacles interrupt the airflow, the shear exponent tends to be higher,
causing a faster increase in wind speed with altitude.
This terrain dependence highlights the complex interaction between atmospheric dynam-
ics and terrain, stressing the need for accurate characterization of α to better predict wind
behavior in different environments. It is also worth repeating that this shear exponent
assumes neutral conditions of atmospheric stability, and might induce some errors in the
vertical extrapolation of winds, since it does not take into account the effects of buoyancy
produce by temperature gradients that change the wind profile of air masses.
We must also be aware of the limitations of our study, and of the other studies reviewed.
We cannot gather accurate stability data from the climate models simulations. However,
we can do question the spatio-temporal constancy of alpha.
Some of the studies consider a constant shear exponent of 0.14, irrespective of terrain,
while others use a shear exponent of 0.10 specifically over water. The complexity of the
shear exponent motivates to question the validity of the simplifications, raising two key
considerations:
• Whether the shear exponent remains constant across both land and water.
• Whether the shear exponent is invariant over time.

to climate change could be removed with this method.

How to implement extrapolation methods
As explain in the section 2.1, the climate models have a temporal resolution of 3 hours.
The following approach is used to implement the extrapolation methods, applicable to
both future and past periods for any climate model:
1. Select the 3-hour time series for one year of 10 m wind speeds.
2. Apply the extrapolation techniques to the yearly time series.
3. Calculate the yearly mean, summer mean, and winter mean (using December data
from the previous year).
4. Store the averaged data.
This process is repeated for all models and years using different extrapolation methods,
except for Quantile Mapping, which requires a longer time series to operate effectively.
Once the process is completed for all models, years, and methods, the averaged data is
regridded to a common grid.

in practically the same output. Perhaps, the most notable differences between these

method to account for changes in wind speed with height when considering the whole
European domain. This generalized approach smooths out local variations, leading to
comparable results over large areas, as it does not focus on specific areas where local
shear could have stronger implications for wind speed change.

To evaluate the similarity of each method’s ∆U distribution with the baseline, a one-tailed
t-test is going to be used. The one-tailed just means that a specific direction will be tested.
In this case, it makes sense to test whether the mean ∆U predicted by any of the methods
is going to be less than the one predicted baseline, as this would imply an exaggeration
of the wind decline by the methods. We can define the hypothesis to be tested as follows.
• Null Hypothesis (H0)
The null hypothesis states that there is no significant evidence to say that the mean ∆U
predicted by the methods (m) is less than the one predicted by the baseline (b).
H0 : µm ≥µb
(5.7)
• Alternative Hypothesis (H1)
The alternative hypothesis states that the mean ∆U predicted when using the methods
(m) is significant less than the mean change predicted by the baseline (b).
H1 : µm < µb
(5.8)
The decision to accept or reject the null hypothesis will be based on the p-value of the
calculated t-statistics, assuming a 0.05 significance level.
• p-value < 0.05: Reject H0 in favor of H1.
Suggest that there is sufficient evidence that the mean ∆U when using the method
is significantly less than the one predicted by the baseline.
• p-value ≥0.05: Do not reject H0
This suggests that there is no enough evidence to say that the mean ∆U predicted
by the methods are significant less than the mean ∆U predicted by the baseline.

6
Discussion
This chapter assesses the found results, examining the effectiveness of different extrap-
olation methods in assessing European wind energy production under climate change. It
begins with a discussion of the shear exponent in section 6.1, taking about its nature over
land and the different climate model. In this section, it is also compared the effect of using
a realistic shear exponent derived from ERA5 reanalysis data versus a simplified constant
value on wind resource assessments. Despite the observed dynamics, both approaches
yield similar results for land areas.
Further, in section 6.2 it is discussed how the power law method tends to overestimate
negative wind changes over Europe and surrounding waters. This section delves into
the reasons behind this overestimation and examines the two new extrapolation methods
proposed. The chapter concludes with a discussion on the limitations faced during this
study.
6.1
Shear assumption
Throughout section 5.1, it was explored how shear exponent is a dynamic and model-
dependent variable that exhibits spatio-temporal variations for both climate models and
reanalysis data.
The most notable aspect of this parameter is that it remains relatively constant over the
ocean surface in both climate models and reanalysis data, but not over land, which raises
the question of the reasons for such differences.
Despite the dynamics in the shear exponent over land, there is not much difference be-
tween using a power law with a shear exponent derived from the ERA5 reanalysis data
(more realistic) or simply approximating this value to a constant of 1/7 over land and 1/10
over water when assessing the change in wind resource due to climate change (sec-
tion 5.2).
Moreover, although the approximation of 1/10 over the sea holds true for both past and
present conditions, the shear exponent undergoes the most significant changes over the
North Atlantic, making one wonder why this is the case (as seen in Appendix C).
6.1.1
Shear variability across the models
The main reason contributing to the variability of the shear, apart from the wind pattern
changes, lies in the variability of land types over the study periods.
• Over the ocean there is no temporal or spatial change of the shear exponent be-
cause sea will be sea now and in the future.
• Over the land, the terrain cannot be regarded as a fixed parameter; rather, it is a
dynamic quantity.
The elements that make up the land change over time. For instance, let’s consider the
decline in forested areas. Between 1980 and 2000, more than half of the new agricultural
land across the tropics was gained through deforestation [49]. This not only affects bio-
diversity, but also has implications for wind patterns. Different land types can be likened
to different roughness within the atmospheric boundary layer, affecting winds.
The Impact of Assumptions on European Wind Energy Production in the Face of Climate Change
53
==PAGE:68==
With this, it is understood that the shear over land presents variability over time (and
space), but why is this variability differing from model to model? The answer lies in how
each of the CMIP6 models, simulate the Earth’s surface.
The Earth’s surface is difficult to model, as there are many factors to take into account,
some of them with a very high resolution. Climate models characterize the different land
type using 'land cover fractions'. In climate models, 'land cover fraction' refers
to the proportion of a grid cell that is covered by different types of land surfaces, such
as forests, grasslands, deserts, or urban areas. Land cover fraction data are typically
derived from satellite observations or datasets that categorize the Earth’s surface into
different types based on vegetation or land use. The Copernicus Land Monitoring Service
provides various levels of detailed land cover characteristics both in Europe and globally,
using satellite data to monitor changes in land cover. These data are further validated
with ground observations when available [50].
For the long term predictions of the climate models, the different socio-economic scenarios
are used to estimate how this variable will change in the future and here is where the
models differ from one another. On certain areas, there is a general agreement. For
instance, take the example of Poland. The majority of the models seen an increase of the
shear exponent in the future over the Polish territory (see Appendix C).
Forested areas in Poland have been increasing since 1990 due to agricultural land aban-
donment, growing from 28% in 1970 to more than 50% in some areas [51]. This growth of
forest is enhanced by laws enacted by the Polish government that protect nature, such as
the Forest Act of 1992 [52] and the Nature Conservation Act of 2004 [53]. The different
socio-economic pathways expect this trend to continue to a greater or lesser extent in
the coming decades. This increase would be noticeable in the shear exponent, as it is
transitioning from cropland (low shear value) to forested land (high shear value).
This can be observed by assessing the change of the ”land cover fraction” for the different
CMIP6. The data can be retrieved from the World Climate Research Program CMIP6
webpage [42] by searching for the variable "land_cov_frac" under the climate model
names and their variants. Unfortunately, not all climate models have this data publicly
available. Nevertheless, some do. Figure Figure 6.1 shows the change in different crop

The core issue lies in the resolution of the climate models. When using shear exponent in-

These resolutions cover an area of around 100 km by 100 km. The size of the grid cells
remain too large to properly capture terrain variability. An area of 10,000 square kilometers
is huge and can encompass a large range of terrain features such as lakes, cities, and
fields. During the regridding process, the finer resolution data points (10×10 km) are
aggregated to fit into the larger grid cells. This aggregation process involves a loss of
detail. When regridded to a coarser grid, the detailed ERA5 shear exponent is smoothed
out, and the resulting shear exponent values become more uniform across the larger grid
cells. Over the sea, this is not a problem as there is a lot of uniformity in the shear, leading
to a good estimate of its value.
This is clearly seen when comparing the two distributions (Figure 6.3a and Figure 6.3b).
However, even with smoothing, the shear exponent should be larger than 1/7, ideally
closer to 0.17 or 0.20.

6.1.3
Changes in the North Atlantic
Many climate models predict a significant increase in the shear exponent over the North
Atlantic Sea, specifically south of the coast of Iceland.
This predicted increase is not related to changes in land cover fraction or land use, as it
covers a vast area within the open Atlantic Ocean. Instead, it is attributed to the global
circulation patterns of the Earth. The shear exponent is an empirical variable that sim-
plifies the complexity of atmospheric processes. Attempting to explain this observation
purely from a land cover perspective would be incorrect, as it overlooks the larger-scale
atmospheric dynamics at play.
The North Atlantic region, especially the Euro-Atlantic sector of the Arctic, is known for
its high cyclonic activity [54]. Cyclones and storms significantly influence wind patterns
and consequently affect wind shear exponents values in this area. These cyclones are
driven by global circulation patterns, which include the movement of air masses and the
interaction between the ocean and the atmosphere.
Many studies suggest that under the RCP8.5 scenario, this region is going to experience
significant changes in wind patterns [55]. According to Surkova and Krylov [56], this sce-
nario may result in an increase in both average and extremely wind speeds by the end of
the 21st century everywhere over the entire Arctic region. Nevertheless, while it is known
that the projected warming will affect the winds over the Arctic, the challenge is to properly
explain the mechanism of this complex system, supporting our idea that the shear does
not accurately represent the wind changes in this area.
6.2
Effect of the extrapolation methods
In section 5.3 it was seen how the power law tends to overestimate the negative wind
change over Europe and its surrounding waters. This finding leads us to investigate the
reasons for this overestimation.
Moreover, the two new extrapolation methods that do not use the power law provide re-

In this section, we elaborate on the reasons for the overestimation by power-law methods
and their higher variability compared to the CMIP6 100 m winds. Additionally, we discuss
the implications of the proposed new methods.
6.2.1
Overestimation of the power law
Throughout the study, it has been observed that the power law extrapolation amplifies the
negative wind change in future projections for the European continent. This consistent
overestimation raises questions about the underlying causes and limitations of using the
power law for assessing the effects of climate change on wind patterns.
As discussed in section 6.1, a constant shear exponent of 1/7 tends to amplify the ex-
pected wind changes over the sea because wind shear over water typically has much
lower values. The inadequate use of a fixed wind shear exponent, which does not accu-
rately reflect the lower values over sea surfaces, leads to a significant overestimation of
wind shifts. However, even using a more appropriate shear exponent for the ocean, such
as 1/10, the PL-O method still resulted in a more pronounced negative change than the
simulations obtained at 100 m without extrapolation.
On land, the issue is somewhat less pronounced because the wind shear values are
generally in the range of 1/7. The selection of a more realistic shear exponent over land
can mitigate some of the overestimation, but it does not entirely eliminate it. This suggests
58
The Impact of Assumptions on European Wind Energy Production in the Face of Climate Change
==PAGE:73==
that while the choice of shear exponent is a critical factor, it alone cannot account for the
extent of overestimation observed. There must be additional internal causes within the
power law itself contributing to this overestimation.
First of all, the power law is a tool used to extrapolate wind measurements to a time resolu-
tion of 10 minutes based on certain assumptions. It is intended for measuring campaigns
or power curve validations involving in-situ measurements. Setting this context aside, the
power law relies on several key assumptions that need to be met in order to simplify the
complex behavior of wind in the atmosphere. These assumptions are stated below:
1. Neutral stability in the atmosphere
The power law assumes that atmospheric conditions are neutrally stable, which
means that there is no vertical temperature gradient. This implies that there is no
significant temperature difference with height that would cause buoyancy forces to
unsettle the wind profile.
2. Wind remains within the atmospheric boundary layer
The model assumes that the wind profile being analyzed remains within the atmo-
spheric boundary layer (ABL), which is the part of the atmosphere that is directly
influenced by the Earth’s surface and responds to surface forcing with a timescale
of an hour or less.
3. Homogeneous terrain
The power law is often applied under the assumption that the terrain over which
the wind is blowing is homogeneous and flat in all directions, meaning that surface
roughness and the associated shear exponent are constant over the area of interest.
4. Time-invariant wind profiles
The power law implicitly assumes that wind profiles do not change significantly over
time, meaning the relationship between wind speeds at different heights is stable
over the period of interest.
From listing the atmospheric assumptions made by the power law, it is evident that in
the context of climate change and its impact on the wind resource that most of these
assumptions are not being met.
Regarding neutral stability, we are using 6-hour wind speed outputs where atmospheric
stability is clearly non-neutral. Atmospheric stability can vary greatly due to factors such as
diurnal heating, weather systems, and seasonal changes. One might argue that averaging
over the year and the study period averages out stability, which may hold true over sea
but not over land, especially when modeling the net radiative forcing of 8.5 W/m2 by the
end of the century, indicating a warmer Earth.
The same applies to land homogeneity. As seen in section 6.1, there is significant vari-
ability across land grid cells in the model, so assuming homogeneity does not consider the
modeling processes of different climate models. Furthermore, time-invariant wind profiles
are inaccurate due to climate change.
The overestimation observed with power-law extrapolation results from both the inappro-
priate application of a constant shear exponent and the inherent limitations of power-law
assumptions. Addressing these issues requires a more tailored approach that considers
the variable nature of wind shear and atmospheric stability, particularly in different types
of terrain and varying weather conditions.

6.2.2
Implications of the Bias Correction for Extrapolation
The newly developed extrapolation methods that incorporate bias corrections appear not
to be overestimating the change, even when using 10 m winds. These methods mitigate
the overestimation typically seen in power-law extrapolation, producing results that are
more closely aligned with those derived directly from climate simulations.
In the case of the Bias Correction (BC) method, the approach involves adjusting only the
mean of the 10 m wind speeds to match the average of the 100 m wind speeds for the
historical period. Despite this adjustment, the BC method shows slightly larger wind and
power change results than those estimated directly from the climate simulations. The
primary reason for this is that BC merely shifts the mean without changing the overall
distribution of 10 m winds. This in a way is somewhat similar to what power law does,
since it is multiplying the 10 m distribution by a constant. However, this method produces
a less pronounced overestimation because instead of applying the power law without
meeting the conditions, it is using real data at 100 m. For the past it has a clear impact
as the mean aligns with the observational. However, the biggest limitation is to enforce
these historical observational data for the future.
On the other hand, Quantile Mapping (QM) shows results that resemble the most to the
ones obtained from climate simulations without producing this overestimation of the de-
cline in wind speed in Europe. QM adjusts the distribution to match exactly the 100 m
wind distribution from ERA5. This is the only method that does not multiply the 10 m
winds by a constant to get the 100 m winds. The wind distribution is being adjusted quan-
tile by quantile to align with historical observations. For past data, this approach is valid,
as it accurately represents historical wind conditions. However, for future projections, this
method imposes historical data onto future scenarios. Although actual data at 100 m is
employed, ensuring that the wind distributions follow a real 100 m distribution, it compro-
mises future climate projections.
The major assumption when using these methods is that the historical distribution of wind
speeds will remain unchanged in the future, an assumption that may not hold true in the
face of ongoing climate change. Ultimately, it is presumed that the relationship between
10-meter and 100-meter wind speeds observed in the past will persist into the future. Even
when using real data, this critical assumption should not be overlooked when analyzing
results derived from any of these methods or their derivatives

Limitations of the study
Throughout this study, we have attempted to cover the differences between the extrapo-
lation method when assessing ∆U and ∆P due to climate change. It has been observed
that, in essence, studies often extrapolate 10 m winds to 100 m using an unrealistic power
law or by attempting to match the 10 m distribution more closely to historical observational
data at 100 m. This is because many studies choose not to produce 100 m results directly
from the climate simulations.
Results using the power law tend to overestimate, especially when considering changes
in non-linear variables.
It has been demonstrated throughout the study that the assumption of using a constant
shear exponent, or even a constant but different exponent for sea and land, is erroneous.
However, the expected change using one method or another is not as abrupt as initially
thought. This raises questions about potential limitations.

Conclusion
This study investigates the implications of the most common assumptions made to assess
how climate change impacts the European wind resource. In addition, it proposes two new
methods to avoid using these assumptions. To do so, 13 different CMIP6 climate models
have been used for analyzing the changes in wind speed between a historical period
comprising 20 years from 1995 to 2014 and a future period projected by the SSPS-8.5
IPCC scenario, comprising 2051 to 2070, as well as ERA5 reanalysis data.
After a literature review conducted in Chapter 3, it was found that the most common ap-
proach followed by the studies is to extrapolate 10 m winds obtained from the climate
simulations to a height of 100 m using the power law. The assumptions lie in the shear
exponent of the power law, as some studies use it as a constant, while others define dif-
ferent values for sea and land. In Chapter 4, a review of the methods employed by the
studies was done, as well as the proposal of three new methods: one involving a more re-
alistic shear exponent and two bias correction methods; one correcting the mean from the
10 m climate model simulations and the other correcting the whole distribution to make it
similar to the 100 m distribution. It also explains how to obtain 100 m wind results directly
from the weather simulations without the need to extrapolate the 10 m winds.
In Chapter 5 the results of the study are presented, showing how the different methods
predict the change of the wind between past and future, as well as assessing how the
different methods perform against the 100 m wind speed simulations. The effect of the
methods on the sign of the models was also studied. This was conducted for the change
in wind speed and the change in power density, finding that this non-linear variable suffers
the most changes under the different methods.
In Chapter 6 we discuss the main findings of the study regarding the spatio-temporal
variation of the shear and the implications of using a power law. It was seen how the
power law often tends to exaggerate the negative changes because this studies often
don’t fully fulfil the assumptions to use the power law.
In the introduction, we asked ourselves four key questions that this study aimed to answer.
Here are the answers to each one of them:
1. Is it valid to use a unique shear exponent when assessing the change of wind
resource due to climate change?
After calculating the shear exponent for both CMIP6 and reanalysis data, we have con-
cluded that no, a single value cannot be used to assess the wind change at 100 m when
using a power law. Instead, one should define different shear exponents for water and
land, or ideally, use shear exponents derived from reanalysis data.
2. What are the implications of mainstream assumptions for wind energy produc-
tion projections in Europe? Do current methods overestimate/underestimate the
average wind speed change at 100 meters?
The most common assumption is to use a power law without considering that climate
simulation data does not fully comply with the conditions on which this law is based. This
often leads to the power law exaggerating the negative change in wind speed, essentially
scaling the change in wind speeds from 10 to 100 m.

3. Can alternative methodologies ensure more accurate results in the face of cli-
mate change?
In theory, they can. Whenever possible, the model’s own height levels should be used
to obtain winds at different heights and interpolate using ln z between the two heights
closest to the hub. However, since this procedure is complex and not always feasible,
if alternative methods must be used, either quantile mapping or bias correction as they
are using real 100 m wind data to correct the 10 m winds obtained from climate models.
Nonetheless, their limitations must be considered, particularly in predicting wind extremes
and in potentially changing the climate change signal due to assuming that future wind
distributions will mirror past ones.
4. Impact on the Resource. What are the effects of climate change on Europe’s
wind resources? How does it impact wind power?
Finally, we conclude that all methods generally produce similar results when analyzing
the entire European domain. The power law generally tends to overestimate negative
changes in wind speed, although these can be mitigated if a shear exponent derived
from ERA5 reanalysis data is used or if different exponents are chosen for sea and land.
Both Quantile Mapping (QM) and Bias Correction (BC) methods further attenuate the
overestimation. These changes are particularly exaggerated when considering power
density, as this metric involves cubing the wind speed. Any overestimation in wind speed,
therefore, results in a significantly amplified exaggeration in power density.
In conclusion, this study underscores the necessity of continually re-evaluating and adjust-
ing methodologies for wind resource assessments, particularly under changing climatic
conditions. Understanding the limitations and implications of traditional methods, such
as the power law, and considering alternatives is vital for future energy planning and the
development of sustainable wind power resources, ensuring that assessments remain
robust and relevant in the context of climate change.
Although the power law tends to exaggerate changes, especially when used with a con-
stant shear exponent regardless of terrain, it can still provide a first approximation of trends
that may occur due to climate change. Therefore, this method should not be entirely dis-
regarded. However, it is important to be aware of its limitations and to consider more
refined techniques for in-depth studies or when assessing changes for specific future ar-
eas of interest.

Future Work
This study has shown that, although wind distributions over the European continent are
quite similar regardless of the extrapolation method used, there are slight differences in
the magnitude of the changes. This is crucial because for variables that depend non-
linearly on wind speed (such as power density), these differences are amplified, signif-
icantly affecting their projections. Furthermore, when considering the entire European
territory, it has been observed that the use of an unrealistic power-law shear exponent
leads to larger projected changes in wind patterns. Additionally, different extrapolation
methods do not affect all climate models in the same way.
Based on these results, two heuristic approaches might be followed in future research.
The first approach could involve examining how different assumptions and extrapolation
methods influence wind power projections. In this case, conducting an analysis across
the entire European territory would provide a broad and comprehensive perspective. The
central idea would be to identify the optimal locations for wind farms by focusing on the
windiest 25% of each grid cell under future projections. By defining a specific wind turbine
model, it could be calculated the impact of different extrapolation methods on actual wind
turbine performance data. This approach would offer more practical and relevant insights
for wind energy developers, as it would highlight areas where changes in wind patterns
are likely to have the most significant effects. Such an analysis could serve as a valuable
metric, enabling developers to make informed decisions about wind farm placement and
investment, thereby optimizing the efficiency and effectiveness of wind energy production
in the face of changing climatic conditions while at the same time showing the effect of
the different methods in the projection.
The second approach involves conducting an analysis in a smaller area than the entire
European domain, ideally an offshore area, as these are more affected by the different
extrapolation methods used. The goal would be to analyze the dependence of the results
based on the models used and observe how different projections are influenced by the
methods and the number of models. The main objective of this approach is to analyze how
the results depend on the specific climate models used and to observe to what extent the
different extrapolation methods and the number of models used influence the projections
Finally, another important approach could involve validating each extrapolation method
against real-world measurements. Conducting such validations within a smaller, well-
defined area equipped with comprehensive real data could be particularly beneficial. This
targeted validation would provide a clearer and more accurate understanding of the overall
effects and accuracy of the different extrapolation methods. By comparing the projected
wind resource data with actual measurements, it can be assessed the reliability and ro-
bustness of each method. This process would help identify any discrepancies and refine
the models accordingly.

The SSP narratives ([57]) comprise a textual description of how the future might unfold in
terms of broad societal trends. Their main purpose is to provide an internally consistent
logic of the main causal relationships, including a description of trends that are tradition-

complement to the quantitative model projections. By describing major socioeconomic,
demographic, technological, lifestyle, policy, institutional and other trends, the narratives
add important context for a broad user community to better understand the foundation and
meaning of the quantitative SSP projections. At the same time, the narratives have been
