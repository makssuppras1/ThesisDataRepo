Abstract
The wind energy capacity installed on the earth keeps increasing in order to supply the energy
demand which has an increasing trend in general. Since the use of fossil fuels will be limited in the
near future, wind energy as a renewable energy source, have naturally gained popularity. However,
there are many challenges, one of which is icing, for the wind energy sector to be overcome. The
wind energy potential is desirable mainly on the high altitude or on the offshore platforms and these
locations make the topic of icing on the wind turbines crucial for investigation.
The research done in DTU in collaboration with the FORCE Technologies covers several aspects
of icing on wind turbine sections. The numerical models, mainly LEWICE developed by NASA,
for the ice accretion on the aircraft have been examined and they have been tested with the ice
accretion test by using a code inspired from LEWICE. In addition, the air temperature at the test
section of the climatic wind tunnel at FORCE Technologies have been calculated depending on
the input parameters of the test. Finally, a numerical model to calculate the temperature of the
water droplets sprayed by the nozzle has been developed. Since having the micro water droplets
supercooled or not has not been clarified by the literature review done for the project, two models
covering the particles as supercooled or non supercooled have been defined.
The numerical outcomes gathered from the models have been tested in the climatic wind tunnel
with various tests. The tests done in FORCE Technologies can be defined as mapping of the LWC
(Liquid water content) and MVD (Median volume diameter), high speed camera tests to determine
the physical initiation of icing and ice accretion tests to see the applicability of the models developed
for aircraft.

Introduction
Atmospheric icing affects many industries such as power transmission network operation, aviation
and wind energy. That is why, if a wind energy project in a cold climate region is developed, the
risks of icing has to be considered. The main risks and consequences can be listed as shortened
component lifespans, safety issues and decreased annual energy production.
In addition to the
technical aspects, there exists economical consequences of icing as well. For instance, in European
countries energy is sold in a day ahead market where underproduction from the expectations can
create penalties. [1].
Even though there are many existing concerns of installing wind turbines in cold climates, there are
considerable advantages as well. As the density of the air depends on the temperature, having more
dense air will naturally yield to higher energy harvest from the same rotor area of the wind turbine.
[2]. Cold climate, here in the statement above, expresses the condition that the ambient temperature
is lower than the operational limits of the wind turbines and the icing on the wind turbine blades is
expected. The regions defined with the cold climate conditions are generally located in the northern
parts of North America and Europe and in regions where the terrain is mountainous. (icing impacts
ref). Figure 1.1 shows the locations of wind farms located in cold climate regions. [1]
Figure 1.1: Wind Farms Located in Cold Climate [1]

2.1
Basic Aerodynamics of Wind Turbines
Wind turbine is a system in which aerodynamic tangential forces with respect to the blade geometry
are translated as torque to a shaft having a rotational speed in operation. In this chapter, the ba-
sic aspects of the power generation and aerodynamics of the wind turbine sections will be explained.
A wing with the same airfoil cross section is used to test the aerodynamic behavior of a 2D section.
Figure 2.1 shows the stream lines following the path around the airfoil while demonstrating the
leading edge stagnation point, the leading edge and the trailing edge in 2D. Stagnation point occurs
at the location where the local velocity is zero. [4]. Leading edge can be defined as the part of the
airfoil that contacts the airfoil at first. The separated airflow joins back at the trailing edge.
Figure 2.1: Airflow Around a 2D Airfoil [5]

The direction of the forces are basically determined by the direction of the free stream. Drag is
parallel to the direction of the free stream velocity while the lift is perpendicular. Figure 2.2 shows
the diagram of the resultant forces acting on the 2D airfoil section.

2.2
Types of Ice
Atmospheric icing is traditionally divided into three different types depending on the different
formation processes named as precipitation icing, in cloud icing and frost. The features of these
different ice types are explained further below. [7]
• In cloud icing: This is the icing type where supercooled droplets hit a surface below 0°C and
freeze during the collision. The temperature of the droplets can be as low as -30°C and the
reason why they do not freeze is their small size. Accretion types vary depending on the liquid
water content (LWC), median volume diameter (MVD), the temperature, the wind speed, the
duration of icing, chord length of the blade and the collection efficiency. The types of in cloud
icing are listed as below.
– Soft rime: The characteristic behavior of this icing type is the thin ice needles and flakes.
Soft rime icing is expected when the ambient temperature is well below 0°C and the MVD
and LWC are small. In addition, soft rime icing leads to an accretion with low density and
adhesion.
– Hard rime: It is expected at higher wind speeds and higher MVD and LWC. The accretion
has higher adhesion and larger density value than the soft rime.
13
==PAGE:16==
– Glaze: This type of accretion appears in a wet growth. Wet growth is a type of ice growth
where a fraction of the droplets do not freeze at the collision and creates a water film which
freezes in time. Glaze ice has the highest adhesion and density.
• Precipitation: It can be in snow or rain formation. The accretion rate is higher than the in
cloud icing which brings the risk of higher damage.
– Freezing rain: It happens when rain falls on a surface which has a temperature lower than
0°C. Ice density and adhesion are high for this type.
– Wet snow: It is likely to happen when snow is slightly liquid at air temperature between
o and -3°C and it sticks to the surface. The adhesion increases in time.
• Frost: It appears when water vapor freezes on a cool surface. It generally happens in low wind
speeds. The adhesion might be strong.

2.3
Impacts of Icing on Wind Turbines
As discussed earlier in the introduction, having a wind farm installed in a cold climate region have
benefits while it holds many drawbacks due to the ice accretion. In this chapter, effects of ice
accretion on wind turbines, such as wind assessment and wind turbine operation, will be explained.
[11]
• Measurement errors: During the wind resource assessment period, the anemometers, wind
vanes and temperature sensors can be affected by icing. Wind speed errors can reach up to
30% in cold climate conditions. In addition, an ice free anemometer can have an error up to
40% while a normal anemometer has an error up to 60% during icing events.
• Power losses: Ice accretion changes the shape and roughness of the blade which leads to an
adverse effect on the aerodynamic characteristics of the blades and icing results in measurement
errors from turbine instruments such as wrong wind speed or direction which affects yaw and
power control.
Ice accretion on the leading edge of the airfoil sections reduces the power
production and the impact of icing changes from 0.005 to 50% of the annual energy production
depending on the icing conditions and types of the wind turbines or design of wind farms.
• Overproduction: Higher density due to the effect of low temperature can cause overproduction
of the wind turbine.
16
==PAGE:19==
• Mechanical failures: Ice accretion causes an increase in the load on the blades and on the tower,
that creates high amplitude vibrations and/or resonance and a mass imbalance between blades.
In addition, at low temperatures viscosity of the oil, dimensions and mechanical properties of
the components are affected. The mentioned effects can bring possible overheating and higher
fatigue charges on components. One of the most affected components can be mentioned as the
gearbox which will have a shorter lifetime in cold climate conditions.
• Electrical failures: Snow infiltration in nacelle and extreme temperatures cause condensation
in the electronics.
• Safety hazard: If the wind turbine is in a condition where large amount of ice is accreted on
the blades, the accumulated ice can be thrown at a distance around 1.5 times of the combined
height of the turbine and the rotor diameter.
2.4
Explanation of LEWICE Code Developed by NASA
In order to predict the ice shape accreting on the airfoil sections, ice prediction studies have been
reviewed. During the literature review, ice prediction algorithms based on aircraft icing have been
found. One of the most detailed explanations is found for the LEWICE code which has been devel-
oped by NASA. Therefore, the detailed explanation on LEWICE code will be given in this section.
In addition, validation of the LEWICE code will be done for wind turbine sections which are subject
to much lower wind speeds.
LEWICE is an ice accretion prediction code that uses a time-stepping scheme to calculate the shape
of the ice growing on the airfoil section. The potential flow field is calculated by using 2D panel
code. Then, the potential field is used to calculate trajectories of the droplets and the points where
the droplets impinge. By having the points of impingement, distribution of liquid water on the
2d section is calculated. Then the thermodynamic model is used to calculate the ice growth rate.
Depending on the input parameters, the type and shape of the ice are affected by velocity, pressure,
temperature, liquid water content, median volume diameter and icing time.[12] Even though the
code has been used in order to obtain the ice shapes, there exists some assumptions where the code
has to be validated.
The LEWICE code includes four fundamental steps which are listed as below:
• Aerodynamic solver
• Impingement model
17
==PAGE:20==
• Thermodynamic model
• Ice growth model
2.4.1. Aerodynamic Solver
This step is considered as the most time consuming part of the LEWICE code. Even by using
the computation power which has been developing rapidly, having the CFD simulations done takes
unreasonable amount of time. Thus, the code uses a 2D source and doublet panel method.[13] In
addition, a fourth order boundary layer is attached in order to simulate the viscous effects on the
stream facing the airfoil section.[12] Only limited details of panel method is given in this section
because the main focus of the theoretical part of the thesis is to define a thermodynamic model of
icing.

In order to estimate the ambient temperature at the test section, where the items such as a cylinder
or an airfoil wing are subject to tests, a ”macro” model based on the principles of psychrometrics
is developed. This model simplifies the calculations by avoiding the complex calculations, which is
done in a similar test setup, including 3D steady RANS equations for conservation of mass, mo-
mentum and energy. [17]
Psychrometrics uses the thermodynamic properties to analyze conditions and process involving
moist air.[18] In this section, perfect gas relations and their use in heating due to the effect of spray
bar and humidity control will be explained. It is also important to state that, Kuehn showed that
having the perfect gas relations used instead of real gas leads to errors are less than 0.7 %.[19]
Psychrometric charts are introduced in order to make the calculations easily by having the data
read by the engineers for air conditioning problems. A typical psychrometric chart and the terms
to use the chart are presented in Figure 2.13.

There are three different temperature definitions on the chart which are dry bulb temperature, wet
bulb temperature and dew point temperature. Dry bulb temperature is located on the x-axis of
the chart. Dry bulb temperature is generally known as the air temperature. The reason why it is
called as dry bulb is that the air temperature is measured by a thermometer which is not affected
by the moist in the air. The wet bulb temperature is the adiabatic saturation temperature. The

point temperature is the temperature where the air is completely saturated, ie the relative humidity
is 100 %. [21] For saturated air, which is the case in the wind tunnel, all the three temperature
definitions are identical. [22]

LWC and MVD mapping of the Tunnel
In this section, LWC and MVD mapping of the tunnel is explained. For this purpose, Cloud Droplet
Probe (CDP) manufactured by Droplet Measurement Technologies is used. The probe can measure
the size of particles which are between 2 µm and 50 µm. The appearance of the probe can be seen
in Figure 4.2.
Figure 4.2: The Cloud Droplet Probe
40
==PAGE:43==
The CDP uses a laser to brighten the particles and forward-disperse their light.
The detected
dispersed light is then used to calculate the size of particles particles. The CDP gathers the forward-
dispersed photons which are in a cone that is 4 ° to 12 ° from the laser beam. The magnitude of the
dispersed light varies depending on the size, composition and shape of the droplets. The gathered
light is then sent into a 50/50 optical beam splitter and then to a pair of photodetectors which are
named as sizer and qualifier. An optical mask filters the optical signal going to the qualifier. The
probe only qualifies the particles that are in the depth of field and only that particles are sized. In
addition, the amount of unobstructed laser light is gathered in dump spot monitor.[31] The theory
of operation of the CDP can also be followed in the Figure 4.3.

inconclusive due to the complications stated below.
• The zoom of the lenses used in the experiments were not enough to see a single droplet.
• In the experiments, all the active nozzles were used and because of that, the cloud created a
foggy environment where catching a single droplet became impossible.
• There exists a possibility that the lights used in the experiment heated the surface where the
icing is recorded.

5.2
Micro Model
In this section, results of the micro model will be presented. As discussed earlier in theory, the model
has two steps namely kinematic equations and thermal issues. The thermal issues were also covered
for both supercooled and non supercooled particles. In order to show the kinematic behavior of the
particles, an extremely large particle with the diameter of 100 µm is put into the kinematic model.
Due to its higher inertia, it is easier to follow the general behavior of the particles. Figure 5.4 shows
the results of the kinematic model.
Figure 5.4: Kinematic Model Results for an Extremely Large Particle
In the kinematic model, the particle has an initial velocity as 1 m/s as an assumption. As the
tunnel reaches to a 2 m x 2 m section area from 4 m x 4 m the average wind speed in the tunnel
increases. As a result, the velocity in the wind tunnel increases. Due to the velocity difference
in the beginning of the trajectory, Reynolds number is relatively high. Higher Reynolds number
leads to a higher drag force which makes the particle follow the velocity in the tunnel. As the cross
section of the wind tunnel reaches its final shape (2 m x 2 m) the particle velocity follows the final
wind speed. This behavior is seen for all sizes of particles.

Conclusions and Future Work
In this research, the thermodynamics of the ice accretion is studied. As a macro model, ambient
temperature of the wind tunnel at the test section after an adiabatic heat exchange is forecast. In
a micro approach, temperature of a single droplet which has a defined median volume diameter,
initial velocity and temperature is predicted for both supercooled and non-supercooled particles
since a relevant study for a micro sized particle has not been found. In addition, an ice accretion
prediction code for aircraft testing developed by NASA, LEWICE, is studied and a code inspired
from LEWICE is used to check the validity of the model for wind turbine applications. The the-
oretical results have not had a perfect match with the experimental data and the possible reasons
have been listed.
In addition to the theoretical outcomes, median volume diameter and liquid water content map-
ping of the wind tunnel by changing parameters, such as wind speed, temperature, air and water
pressure, has been done. This issue was problematic in the previous studies due to the problems
with the cloud droplet probe. Within this study, the results are clearly stated. In addition, a test
setup with a high speed camera in order to observe the initiation of icing is created. The tests were
inconclusive due to the problems listed in chapter 4.3.
As future work, the points that can be improved with further studies are listed as below.
• The ambiguity in the micro model can be improved by having a test setup to identify the micro
particles as supercooled or not. This test will, then lead to an improvement in the wind tunnel
testing conditions since the requirements of having supercooled particles will be certain after
the tests.
• The macro model results were not able to be validated due to the problems in the wind tunnel.
The model can be tested with an analog or a well calibrated digital thermometer and the
surface temperature can be recorded with an IR camera.
• A better setup has to be created for high speed camera tests. As a suggestion, the camera
can be placed inside the wind tunnel while having the precautions and recordings closer to the
60
==PAGE:63==
surface can be obtained. Instead of having all the nozzles working, a single nozzle targeting
the test surface will be more beneficial.
• In the airfoil ice accretion testing, the test matrix did not include the MVD. The test matrix
can be extended to see the impact of MVD to have a better idea about the validity of the
aircraft ice accretions models in wind turbine applications.
• Due to the problems with the force transducer connected to the airfoil, lift and drag data could
not have been recorded. Since the cloud droplet probe works properly it will be beneficial to
map the lift and drag data with respect to LWC and MVD.
