Project formulation
Microplastics are commonly defined as microscopic plastic debris [1] in a size range be-
tween 0.001 and 5 mm [2]. Because of their omnipresence in the oceans, their bioavail-
ability and their potential harm through ingestion for organisms, microplastics have been
considered one of the biggest environmental issues of the last decades [2]. Therefore, the
interest in the study of microplastics is growing. The purpose of this project is to produce
artificial polyethylene microplastics loaded with dodecane-gold nanoparticles, analyzing
their degradation occurring through artificial weathering and proving their suitability as
model test particles in environmental studies by following their ingestion by freshwater
organisms, Daphnia magna.
The project will start with a literature study looking into three main focuses: polyethylene
degradation, artificial preparation of polyethylene microplastics and analytical techniques
for the study of microplastics, and gold loading techniques.
Based on its results, the
experimental study will be planned. The experimental study will begin with the produc-
tion of weathered microplastics, and loaded weathered microplastics. The microplastics
will be produced from polyethylene pellets, by testing different techniques, including pre-
cipitation, fractionation and milling. The loaded microplastics will be produced by the
use of dodecane-gold nanoparticles, which will be synthesized by following the two-phase
Brust-Schiffrin technique [3]. Then, the artificial weathering will occur through UV light
exposure. Subsequently, the weathering will be quantified through chemical assessment,
using relevant analytical techniques such as FT-IR. Moreover, the artificially produced
microplastics will be used for degradation studies, potentially comparing measurements of
biological oxygen demand (BOD) with those of freely dissolved gold as a proxy for total
mineralisation. Lastly, the weathered microplastics loaded with dodecane-gold nanopar-
ticles will be tested for the quantification of microplastics in animals, where inductively
coupled plasma mass spectrometry, ICP-MS, will be used as analytical technique and
Daphnia magna (D. magna) will be used as animals.

Abstract
The objective of this study was the development of a method for the production of refer-
ence weathered gold doped polyethylene microplastics to be used in microplastics trace-
ability studies. Firstly, the production of polyethylene microplastics was investigated and
a method to produce polyethylene particles in a size range between 6 and 24 µm with the
use of toluene in water emulsion was optimized. Then, physical gold entrapment through
optimized microplastics fabrication by emulsion was investigated by the use of two types
of gold nanoparticles: dodecanethiol functionalized AuNPs and octadecanethiol function-
alized AuNPs. Gold doped polyethylene particles by addition of gold of 0.1, 1, 2 and 3%wt
were produced, where the best result was found to be particles with a size range between 8
and 28 µm and a gold entrapment which was measured to be 0.586±0.101%wt. Different
degradation pathways for the weathering of the produced polyethylene microplastics and
the gold loaded polyethylene microplastics were explored. It was found that degradation
through UV exposure was inhibited both from the presence of water and from the pres-
ence of gold, because of the brown coloration that gold gives to the doped microplastics.
In addition, a procedure of chemical degradation was tried on polyethylene microplastics
and it was found to be able to degrade the microplastics in 2 hours to a greater extent
than what UV exposure can reach in 4 weeks, resulting in the potentially ideal method
to be used for weathering of the reference microplastics. Microplastics dispersion in water
was studied for their future application in traceability studies, and it was found that mi-
croplastics dispersion in the water column decreases with the increasing of the degradation
stage, since the particles agglomerate. Then, a biodegradation analysis with the use of
respirometric BOD measuring system was executed, revealing that little or null biodegra-
dation is seen in polyethylene, that activities unrelated to biodegradation are higher than
biodegradation itself, and created a suspect of toxic effects of the introduced microplastics
in the bio-organisms, which increased with the weathered ones. Lastly, the first attempt of
application of the produced weathered gold doped microplastics in traceability study was
executed through an uptake study in Daphnia magna, based on which, when the result
will be available, it will be understood how to continue the development of the reference
gold doped polyethylene microplastics.

Introduction
Due to the abrupt growth of plastic production and use, since its invention, and the
mishandling of the waste derived from it, plastic litter and in particular microplastics
have become ubiquitous in the terrestrial and water environments.[4–7] Even though the
presence of microplastics in nature has been acknowledged 40/50 years ago, the interest
in their research is recently increased because of their suspected toxicity for fauna and
human health, which could be due to the plastic itself or to the chemicals that it can
transport.[5, 8] Despite the fact that research on microplastics is currently in fast develop-
ment, there are several knowledge gaps that still need to be covered: a common definition
and categorization of microplastics [1], adequate sampling and detection methods [2], un-
derstanding of formation and sources [9], understanding their degradation and transport
[9], and methods to carry out representative and realistic laboratory experiments [9].
For these reasons this study aims to investigate the developing of a model for more re-
alistic microplastics laboratory experiments in traceability studies. For this purpose gold
doped polyethylene microplastics will be produced, weathered, characterized, and utilized
in environmental screening studies. The inclusion of metals in microplastics for their de-
tection, which was already tested in literature [10, 11], is promising because of the high
precision and low detection limit of the existing detection methods for metal tracing, that
could allow detection of realistic environmental concentrations of microplastics, which are
too low to be detected from the currently used analytical methods. Where gold, could
potentially be the ideal metal, since it is inhert and already widely used in environmental
toxicological studies.[12, 13] To reach the aim of the study, firstly, a synthesis method for
the production of polyethylene microplastics with gold traces will be optimized. Then a
method to artificially weather the produced polyethylene gold loaded microplastics will
be investigated together with its biodegradation.
Lastly, the traceability of the artifi-
cially produced weathered microplastics with metal tracer, will be tested in animals by
the use of Daphnia magna, which are the most commonly studied aquatic organisms for
microplastics’ toxicology studies [6], in order to asses the performance of the invetigate
model.

2.1
Microplastics
Plastics are commercial materials derived from synthetic polymers which include other
chemicals (additives, fillers) to improve their performance.[4, 14] They are principally syn-
thesized from fossil fuels, but also, in a smaller amount, from biomass of various origin.[4,
5] Since the beginning of the 20th century, when the first modern plastic, ”Bakelite”,
was developed numerous inexpensive manufacturing techniques have been developed until
reaching a mass production between 1930 and 1950 with the introduction of synthetic rub-
ber, nylon, Teflon, polystyrene (PS), polyethylene (PE) and polypropylene (PP).[5, 14, 15]
Nowadays a wide range of plastic material is produced due to its ease of manufacture, low
cost, light weight, high strength/weight ratio, inertness, high durability, resistance to wa-
ter and degradation.[4] These properties have lead to extensive use of plastic materials in
an almost inexhaustible number of applications, and thus, to massive production, making
the plastic industry one of the major worldwide industries, which consumes approximately
8% of the global oil production.[5, 14–16] Of all the applications, the three major ones are
packaging (39.9%), building and construction (19.7%) and automotive (10%).[17] Despite
all the far-reaching benefits that this commodity provides, plastic has been the subject of
increasing environmental concern.[5] Because, due to its durability, which makes it highly
resistant to degradation, and its massive worldwide production, disposing of plastic waste
is very problematic.[4, 5, 17] Plastics compose 10% of the solid waste generated worldwide,
of which a minor part is recycled, the majority, circa 80%, ends up in landfills, and, from
2 to 10%, enters directly or indirectly in the oceans.[4, 5, 17, 18] Plastic litter is com-
posed from a mixture of polymers and chemicals in a very wide size range that goes from
meters to nanometers.[19] Once dispersed in nature (soil, marine water and freshwater)
plastic litter undergoes different degradation processes: mechanical (erosion, abrasion),
chemical (photo-oxidation, hydrolysis), and biological (degradation through microorgan-
isms); which break them down from macro pieces to smaller and more abundant pieces,
the so-called microplastics.[7, 17, 20, 21]

Microplastics are generally defined as solid plastic debris with a diameter smaller than 5
mm.[4, 8, 15, 17, 18, 20, 22–31] They can be made by all the available plastic types, how-
ever, polyethylene (PE) and polypropylene (PP) are the most common since they are the
most used plastics materials [8, 30], which compose the 80% of plastic production together
with polyvinyl chloride (PVC), polystyrene (PS), polyethylene terephthalate (PET) and
polyurethane (PU) [24, 32]. In addition, they can be of different sizes and shapes like
granulates, fragments, films and fibers, as shown in figure 2.1.[5, 15]

These debris comprise primary and secondary microplastics depending on whether they
are manufactured in that dimension or if they break down to that dimension through a
decomposition process.[8, 15, 17, 23, 34]
Primary microplastics are plastic products that are purposely manufactured to be of mi-
croscopic size.
These can be found in facial cleansers, toothpaste, cosmetic products,
in pharmaceutical products as vectors for drugs, or as air blasting media. In addition,
byproducts of processes like particulate emissions, dust and fibers are also considered pri-
mary microplastics. Lastly, virgin pellets for plastic production, which are usually in a
size range between 2 and 5 mm in diameter, can be considered part of this category, even
though there are discrepant opinions regarding their inclusion.[4, 5]
Secondary microplastics are debris deriving from the fragmentation of larger plastic items
in soil or water. The fragmentation process is caused by the combined action of natural
phenomena like light, water and wind, which can be summarized from the word ”weath-
ering”. The ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sunlight allows oxidation of the polymer
leading to bond cleavage. The water inhibits the photo-oxidation because of its low tem-
perature and partial screening from the light. Lastly, wave action and turbulence could
lead to abrasion and erosion that are also influencing the fragmentation.[4, 5]

Microplastics are ubiquitous in all terrestrial and aquatic environments (ice, marine water,
freshwater, soil, mountains, beaches, seabed, etc.).[6, 7] Their omnipresence makes them
one of the top emerging global issues.[22] Plastic is emitted in the environment through
different processes like atmospheric fallout, indiscriminate disposal, inefficient screening of
wastewater effluents and wrong waste handling; and are then diffused from currents, winds
and animals.[17, 19, 34, 35] It is estimated that around 8·109 kg of plastic escape into the
ocean each year and that the ocean is already containing circa 1.5·1011 kg of which ap-
proximately 2.5·108 kg are floating on the oceans’ surface, where 92% are microplastics.[7,
18, 19] Whilst the soil is estimated to contain between 4 and 23 times the amount of plas-
tic present in the oceans.[17] Even though microplastics presence in the oceans has been
studied for 40/50 years, the interest in research increased only in recent years, because

of its suspected toxicity for fauna and human health.[5, 8] As of now, several potential
harms of microplastics have been studied, such as the ingestion of microplastics by living
organisms, which is to date almost certain [25], the release of harmful substances into
the digestive track [22], bioaccumulation [5], their ability to release and absorb various
pollutants [5, 17, 19] and the proliferation of non-native species [15]. Because of their
small size range, which overlaps with the size of particles ingested by animals and thus
the size of the typical food web, microplastics are considered bioavailable to organisms,
which unintentionally capture them while filter-feeding, deposit-feeding, mistaking them
for prey or ingesting prey containing microplastics.[5, 7, 19] Although plastic is typically
considered as inert it can come into contact with harmful substances of different origin.[5]
The first of these substances are additives, that are intentionally incorporated during
plastic production to enhance its properties. Additives could contribute to make plastic
harmful in two ways: they enhance the durability of plastic, and therefore make its degra-
dation slower, and they could leach out in the environment during plastic degradation,
which could be a problem because of their toxicity that could be a threat for biota and
human health.[5, 19, 35]
In addition, during the degradation or production process, there could be unintentional ab-
sorption of different chemical and pollutants, such as persistent organic pollutants (POPs),
endocrine disruptor chemicals (EDCs), hydrophobic organic contaminants (HOCs), which
are harmful because of their toxicity.[5, 8, 19]

Microplastics analysis
In order to monitor microplastics in various environments, and provide information on their
pollution status, their exposure to organisms and their hot-spot concentrations, reliable
and comparable sampling and analytical techniques are needed. Nevertheless, analytical
methods for microplastics are still in a development stage, as this field of research is very
recent and in development.[2]

four categories: extraction, isolation (or separation), identification and quantification (or
classification).[2] When analyzing artificially produced microplastics, only identification

Extraction is the collection of microplastics from water column, sediments or organisms.
The most used technique consists in the use of nets with mesh width between 5000 µm and

technique that separates the particles by size, in the bigger size fractions, it is possible to
separate the microplastics by naked eye. Lastly, many studies use the combination of the
above mentioned techniques because, since they remove different aspects of the organic
matrix, they are more effective if used together.[23, 26, 27, 36–40]
Identification and quantification consist in finding the types of plastic present in the sam-
ple and their quantities. The identification is very complex, as various sizes, shapes and
polymers in complex environmental matrices must be detected. Thus, there is often the
need of applying a combination of two analytical techniques or more. In general, a com-
bination of physical characterization (like microscopy) and chemical characterization are
used.
The most used and simple method is visual analysis, which allows to classify microplastics
based on their physical features (color, shape, size). It can be done by naked eye or by the
use of microscopy, to magnify the smaller fragments, for example, an optical microscope
and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). However, this analysis is influenced by personal
factors, from the microscopy quality and from the sample matrix, which might be confused
with the microplastics.
Spectroscopy is widely used for chemical characterization, as it can provide the chemical
structure of organic molecules. The different spectroscopy techniques that can be used
are: Fourier transformed infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR), that is effective for carbon based
polymers, micro Fourier transformed infrared spectroscopy (µFT-IR), for smaller parti-
cles; attenuated total reflectance Fourier transformed infrared spectroscopy (ATR-FT-IR),
which produce more stable spectra in case of irregular surfaces; and Raman microscopy,
which allows identification of species present in a very low volume, however, it is very
sensitive to additives and pigments, which interfere with the identification of the polymer.

properties changes depending on their thermal stability. Differential scanning calorimetry

their thermal properties, TGA can also be used in combination with thermo extraction and
desorption gas chromatography mass spectrometry (TED-GC-MS) or thermal desorption
gas chromatography mass spectrometry (TDS-GC-MS), lastly, pyrolysis gas chromatogra-
phy mass spectrometry (Py-GC-MS), detects polymers by comparing them with available

Lastly, tagging methods, consist of the addition of substances into microplastics, which

Even though the research on analytical detection of microplastics is advancing, the cur-
rently used methods are inadequate.
Firstly, sampling, which is a crucial step to guarantee a reliable analysis, lack of stan-
dardization, making results inconsistent and prohibiting the comparison between different
studies.
Secondly, in detection and quantification, there are two major problems:
• The collected microplastics have undergone several and significant degradation pro-
cesses, hence their surface morphology and properties are altered from original poly-
mers. In addition, polymer surfaces in nature are often colonized by microorganisms,
which form a biofilm that screens the polymer detection. The smaller the particles,
the bigger the surface area compared to the volume, the bigger the problem. There-
fore, the current detection methods, in particular spectroscopy, which recognizes
the plastic based on the comparison with the properties of the virgin plastic, are not
reliable. [9, 21, 37, 40]
• The current instrumentation requires a particle concentration significantly higher
than the ones present in nature, because of their detection limit. Thus, it is impossi-
ble to work with realistic concentrations at the moment. The inability of the current
analytical techniques of meeting the research needs are more pronounced when the
particle size decreases. [9, 21, 37, 40]

2.2.1
Microplastics uptake in organisms
Microplastics have a size range which coincides with the size of the food web in a marine
or freshwater environment, therefore they can be easily ingested by many aquatic organ-
isms.[25] The curiosity about whether they are toxic, and what kind of effects they could
have on the organisms that digest them, brought to a wide investigation on microplastics
uptake.[25] The research on this field have been focusing in particular in zooplankton such
as Daphnia magna (D. magna) [6, 22, 24, 25, 30, 47] and mussels [18]. However also other
organisms have been investigated. Mussels are widely used because they can ingest high
amounts of microplastics. They can ingest and accumulate microplastics in field conditions
to a larger extent than crustaceans and benthic deposit feeders, and can concentrate the
microplastics from the surroundings, water or sediments.[18] While D. magna are diffused
because of their important role as primary predator of the phytoplankton and because
they are an important food source for larger predators, which added to their easy han-
dling and tendency to absorb pollutants, make of them the ideal aquatic organisms for
toxicology studies.[6]
D. magna are freshwater zooplankton, typically living in eutrophic ponds and rockpools,
which distinguish themselves from the other planktonic species because of their large di-
mension (5-6 mm in the adult age) and their bright color that makes them very vulnerable
to fish predation. In addition, they have many characteristics that make them ideal for
laboratory studies: short life, high fecundity, fast and parthenogenetic reproduction, wide
distribution, easy culturing, sensitivity to pollutants. Furthermore, their dimension makes
them vulnerable to microplastics uptake and their visible anatomy, illustrated in figure
2.5, makes microplastics detection easier.[6, 48]

2.2.2
Microplastics behaviour in water
To study microplastics behaviour in the natural aquatic environment and creating a realis-
tic scenario it is necessary to disperse microplastics in all the layers of a water column. For
example, in order to study microplastic ingestion in Daphnia magna, the plastic particles
need to be dispersed in the water column so that they can be confused as food web.[25]
However, this is not straightforward since water density is 0.99 g/cm3 and plastics like
low density polyethylene (LDPE) have a lower density (0.90 g/cm3), hence, many plastics
because of their hydrophobicity and the lower density are floating on the water surface.[50]
Characteristics of several particles used in laboratory simulation studies are summarized
in table, 2.4 which shows that the majority of purchased particles already have higher
density than water, and therefore can be immersed in the water column. However, in
the environment, microplastics with a lower density like PE have a higher presence, and
they can enter the water column only after several degradation stages and fouling by
bio-organisms, because of their density.[5, 15] Thus, to have a realistic situation the same
conditions should be reproduced. Most of the studies found in literature do not describe
precisely how the suspension of microplastics in water is obtained. Despite this, some
techniques can be found. One solution could be stirring, shaking, vortexing or sonica-
tion, which is the simplest but often shows particle aggregation.[9, 28] Other studies use
surfactants as Tween 80, 60 or 20, to bring the polymer particles uniformly in solution,
however, this causes the presence of a chemical which is not present in nature, therefore
a control sample should be added to the analysis.[28, 30] Another solution, which is often
used in Daphnia magna ingestion studies, is mixing microplastics with green algae in wa-
ter and letting the bioorganisms foul the microplastics until they allow their immersion
in water.[22, 30] Otherwise, imitating the natural weathering of microplastics would be
another path to follow, and will be further discussed in one of the following sections.

2.4.1
Polymers degradation
Degradation is defined as a change in chemical or physical properties [54] which is caused
by the combination of different degradation agents [55]. Polymers can undergo different
types of degradation, which are distinguished based on the agent of exposure: thermal
degradation occurs when polymers are exposed to high temperature, mechanical degrada-
tion happens when there is exposure to shear action or other mechanical stresses, photo-
degradation takes place when the material polymers are exposed to light (UV, visible
light, ozone, electromagnetic radiations), oxidation occurs in presence of oxygen, hydrol-
ysis happens in case of exposure to moisture or water, chemical degradation takes place
when polymers are exposed to aggressive chemicals and lastly biodegradation is caused by
the action of micro-organisms and enzymes.[14, 54–56] By the combination of these differ-
ent degradation agents, other types of degradation can be obtained, for example from the
combination of oxygen and light exposure the polymer would undergo a photo-oxidation
process [14].
These degradation pathways can be divided into three main categories:
biodegradation, physical and physico-chemical degradation, and chemical degradation, as
illustrated in figure 2.9.[57] When degradation of plastics is due to the normal environ-
mental conditions it is called weathering [14] and it is normally caused by the combination
of oxidation, light, heat aggravated from the presence of humidity, rain, wind and atmo-
spheric pollutants [58].
Figure 2.9: Different types of degradation that affect polymeric materials.
2.4.2
Weathering methods
Plastic weathering can be studied both in natural and artificial conditions. Natural weath-
ering, which is exposure to natural environment conditions, is the most accurate and ap-
propriate test condition. However, the major drawback with direct testing is the exposure
time, which should be equal or longer than the service time of the plastic, which might be
longer than 50 years. Therefore, an accelerated method must be used, which could either
be weathering the sample in extreme climate conditions or weathering the sample in a
laboratory. The accelerated artificial weathering studies are designed by modifying the
20
Weathered PE Microplastics with gold traces for increased environmental
traceability
==PAGE:37==
conditioning parameters: temperature, ultraviolet radiation, humidity, oxygen pressure
and stress. All the degradation processes activated by heat are accelerated at elevated
temperature, however, this may cause additional degradation processes, which would not
take place at lower temperatures. Ultraviolet radiation is the major hazard in natural
exposure, a higher intensity radiation, compared to the natural one, would accelerate the
degradation process, nevertheless, the temperature would increase as well, causing the
drawback mentioned previously. The two commonly used sources of UV radiation are
xenon arc and fluorescent tubes. Controlled humidity conditions and water spray, which
simulate natural rain, can allow the study of erosion, hydrolysis, thermal shock or swell
caused by water, depending on the polymer. The increase in oxygen pressure through
condition chambers can accelerate the rate of oxidation. Lastly, even though stress is
not an aspect of natural weathering, applying loads on the sample in phase of weathering
studies could accelerate the process.[55]
In order to assess the effects of weathering exposure on polymers, different parameters
can be observed: oxygen adsorbtion , bonding state, molecular weight, hydrophobici-
ty/hydrophilicity, thermal properties, physical state and surface topology. The changes
in these parameters can be all observed with existing and common analytical techniques,
such as spectroscopy (FT-IR, ATR-FT-IR), chromatography (GPC, SEC), contact angle
measurement, thermal analysis (TGA, DSC, DTA, DMTA) and microscopy (SEM).[55,
57, 59]
Since PE, is the polymer on which the research is focused, its degradation mechanisms,
and degradation studies will be observed.
2.4.3
Polyethylene
Polyethylene (PE) is a thermoplastic polymer in the polyolefins family which is formed by
long hydrocarbon chains [54], as can be seen from its molecular structure in figure 2.10. It
can be polymerized through different methods which produce products with very diverse
physical properties: linear low density polyethylene (LLDPE), low density polyethylene
(LDPE), high density polyethylene (HDPE) and ultra high molecular weight polyethylene
(UHMWPE).[14, 54] This polymeric material is principally used for the production of
blown film used for packaging, however, it is also used for the production of wires, injection
molded products, coatings, containers, crates, tanks, and, as UHMWPE, for medical uses,
such as artificial hip replacement.[14, 56]

Biodegradation
Biodegradation is a phenomenon caused by microorganisms found in nature which leads
to mineralization. Since microorganisms can adhere to the polymer surface only if it is
hydrophilic, but PE is a hydrophobic material, and biodegradation is only possible for
low molecular weights (<500 g/mol), for this process to occur the prior action of physical
or chemical degradation is needed. This leads to the formation of hydrophilic groups on
the polymer surface which makes the polymer more hydrophilic and decreases its surface
energy. At this point, the organisms start to adhere to the polymer surface and begin their
growth by the use of the polymers’ carbon sources, leading to fractionation of the polymer
into smaller molecular weight molecules, dimers or monomers. The low molecular weight
leads to further accessibility from organisms and microbial assimilation, which further
utilize the polymers’ carbon sources to grow. The ultimate products are CO2, H2O and
microbial biomass under aerobic conditions, CO2, CH4, H2O and microbial biomass under
anaerobic methanogenic conditions, and CO2, H2S and H2O under anaerobic sulfinogenic
conditions.[57, 59, 67]
Different bioorganisms have been studied in literature for this scope, of which, marine
bacteria (bacillus subtilis H1584, kocuria palustris M16 and bacillus pumilus M27), bre-
vibacillus borstelensis, rhodococcus rubber, penicillium simplicissimum YK, extracellular
streptomyces, phanerochaete crysosporium, mucor rouxii, fungi, actinomycetes, and as-
pergillus flavus are an example of the ones that were found to degrade PE.[54, 56, 59,
68]
Figure 2.13 illustrates the biodegradation mechanism of PE, which starts from the results
of the photo-oxidation, that are fractionation of chains, carbonyl groups’ and carboxylic
acid formation. Since these changes make the polymer more hydrophilic and susceptible
to microbes. At this point the microbial attack makes the polymer undergo an oxidation
cycle. The final result will be two carbon acetyl CoA which will enter in the citric acid
cycle giving as final products CO2 and H2O.[57, 60]
From a comparison with the virgin PE FT-IR peaks, it can be seen that the characteristic

cm−1, which represents the alcohol O–H stretching, 3400 - 3200 cm−1, which represents
the water H bond of O–H, 2360 - 2340 cm−1, which represents the CO2 , 1700 - 1725
cm−1, representing the carbonyl stretching vibration, which is mainly due to carboxylic
acid or ketones, 1640 cm−1 with 830 - 880 cm−1, which are due to internal double bonds,
1300 - 1000 cm−1, which represents C–O asymmetric stretching, from the alcohol, and
905 - 915 cm−1, which represents a terminal double bond.[54, 59, 60]

2.4.4
Weathering of polyethylene microplastics
Plastic litter in nature is exposed for long periods to weathering conditions, which by
the exposure to light, water, wind, mechanical stresses and microorganisms, leads to the
fragmentation of plastic litter intro small plastic debris.
This mechanism is known to
be started by UV radiation from the sunlight, which allows the oxidation of the poly-
mer leading to bond cleavage and fragmentation. However, in a marine environment, the
cold water of the sea inhibits photo-oxidation for three reasons: low temperature, ma-
rine microorganisms that adhere to the microplastic surface leading to the immersion of
microplastics in the water column, and light screening which increase the plastic persis-
tence. In addition, water provokes abrasion through wave action and turbulence which
can cause fragmentation because of the mechanical stresses.[4, 5, 32] Even though some
assumption on the degradation process which leads to microplastics’ fragmentation can
be done, the real mechanism of microplastics in natural aquatic environments is still not
fully understood.[69]
For what concerns PE degradation, researches have been focusing mainly on LDPE films
in soil, or UV exposure. However, few studies of laboratory simulation of microplastics
degradation or formation in water can be found.
Da Costa et al.[29] studied the degradation of PE pellets with a diameter between 2 and
4 mm in artificially prepared seawater and deionized water for a period of eight weeks.
This, while doing two comparisons: the first between samples kept in the dark and samples
exposed to UV light radiations, the second between samples analyzed in deionized water
and samples analyzed in artificial seawater. The exposure to UV light was executed with
a UV lamp working at 254 nm with an intensity of 400 W/m2 for eight weeks. The results
showed that no mass was lost, therefore no fragmentation occurred during the eight weeks
of exposure. In addition, the ATR-FT-IR spectra, displayed in figure 2.14, showed that six
weeks was the exposure time where the most considerable oxidation took place, which is
visible from the significant increase of the peaks representing C––O, C–O and OH bonds.
Furthermore, SEM analysis showed that PE pellets passed from having a smooth surface
to having signs of tear and crack lines. Lastly, the two comparisons showed that a small
degradation, non comparable with the one occurring in seawater, is also happening in
deionized water, which could be due to the mechanical agitation, and that UV radiation
did not affect the degradation in a relevant way, proving that the salinity is what influences
the degradation the most. This scarce effect of UV light could be due to the fact that
water increases plastic resistance to UV light as mentioned before, or to the fact that the
UV intensity used was too weak.

As previously mentioned, there is a lack of analytical tools for environmentally relevant
monitoring of microplastics in water, especially when it comes to smaller fractions. How-
ever, Bolea-Fernandez et al.[21] proved that ICPMS is a promising method to analyze
metal doped microplastics, making of metal doped plastic particles a potential frontier for
plastic debris detection and environmental behavior simulation studies. Particles doped
with metal tracers can be very effective especially in case of low concentrations, which
are very challenging to detect since the plastic signal could be impeded from the natural
background and the limitation of the existing analytical techniques. The use of a scarce
metal has several advantages: the samples can be spiked into a big variety of environments
without the worry of having elevated background interference; if the samples are digested
or homogenized the tracer will not be quenched, as it often happens when fluorescent dyes
are used as tracers; and methods to trace metals are already existing and have very high
precision, thus they could also be used to analyze metal loaded plastics materials.[10] This
high traceability would allow to gain a better insight into microplastics transport and
fate.[11]

showed promising results, since the metal concentration scaled linearly with the particle
concentration, demonstrating the even distribution. In addition, it was measured that
even for long exposures (2 months) of particles in sludge or water any significant change
in metal concentration could be detected, showing the stability of the metal entrapment.
Therefore, it was proved that, even if the tracer does not provide information like size,
shape, or polymer identity, it can be a precise tool to quantify microplastics and hence to
study their fate, transport and biological interactions.[10, 11]

3.1
Plan
Due to the restrictions aimed at containing the outbreak of COVID-19 the laboratory
facilities were temporarily closed. Therefore, certain aspects of the synthesis method for
the fabrication of gold doped polyethylene microplastics and the technique for their trace-
ability will be deepened through theoretical research, in order to optimize the successive
experimental phase.
The methodology study will focus on four aspects. First, on the gold loading method, by
investigating different studies involving the production of gold loaded particles or metal
doped microplastics. With the aim of finding the ideal technique to obtain an even distri-
bution of gold among the different particles, the desired gold concentration in each particle,
and the most suitable method to verify gold distribution and concentration. Second, it
will focus on the investigation of different researches about microplastics ingestion studies
in Daphnia magna, for two reasons: finding the adapt digestion method and estimating
the number of particles ingested on average by these organisms based on the particle’s di-
mension. Thirdly, different gold detection methods will be considered, such as inductively
coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM).
Based on the methods that will be found to be the most effective further research will
be done in finding the optimal technique for sampling. Lastly, the gold concentration
to be used in the synthesis of gold doped PE microplastics will be calculated by taking
into consideration the detection limit of the analytical techniques, the average uptake of
microplastics in Daphnia magna during experimental studies and the particle’s dimension.

The metal concentration in the microplastics was determined through TGA and ICP-MS.
Because, TGA measures the mass of a sample overtime during temperature changes, and
since the polymers like PET, PS and PAN decompose at temperatures higher than 300-
400°C, the mass of sample left when 1000°C are reached can be considered to be the
incorporated metal mass. While, ICP-MS measures the atomic ions’ mass in a sample
where sample preparation is designed to leave only metal traces in solution, therefore
only the metal concentration is assessed. The analyses for metal detection showed that in
Schmiedgruber et al.[11], 19%wt of the metal was successfully entrapped, which resulted
in 0.19%wt of In in PET; and in Mitrano et al.[10], 33%wt of the metal added was effec-
tively loaded in the nanoparticles, which means that there was 0.49%wt of Pd in PAN.

measurements on different known amounts of microplastics, if the metal concentration
scales linearly with the amount of plastic, it means that there is an even distribution, as
shown in figure 3.2.

The synthesis of PE microplastics in this study will be done by following and adapting a
protocol found in literature, Balakrishnan et al.[30]. Where PE is dissolved in toluene by
continuous stirring at 95°C, then, water is added to allow the formation of oil in water
emulsion with a ratio 1:10 in weight, and the emulsion is homogenized in order to have
uniform droplets and cooled to allow the PE microplastics formation through gelation of
the polymer inside the toluene droplets. Given this, by considering the literature search, it
seems reasonable to include the gold as previously thought, by physical entrapment. Which
could be done by introducing the gold nanoparticles while the polyethylene is dissolved
in toluene, both because the alkane functionalized or hydrophobic gold nanoparticles can
be dispersed in toluene and should uniformly mix with the polymer, and because since
they are hydrophobic, during the addition of water, and formation of the emulsion, they
should stay in the organic phase together with PE. In this way, the physical entrapment
should be definitive when the emulsion is cooled and the PE chains undergo gelation, and
by stiffening, they keep the gold nanoparticles in their pattern. The entrapment will be
similar to what happens in Schmiedgruber et al.[11] and Tagaya et al.[71].
Of all the procedures mentioned in this section, the most appropriate and accurate anal-
ysis on the gold/metal concentration and distribution in the polymer, have been seen
in the synthesis of metal doped microplastics, Schmiedgruber et al.[11] and Mitrano et
al.[10]. This, because the other studies looked more qualitatively into the metal incor-
poration more than quantitatively. Therefore, a preliminary gold concentration could be
determined by TGA, while deeper analysis on gold distribution among the particles and
gold concentration could be done by ICP-MS. However, also executing qualitative images
through electron microscopy could be interesting.
3.3
Daphnia magna ingestion
Having an estimation of the microplastics uptake in organisms is important in order to
understand the amount of microplastics that will be in one sample and to, therefore,
calculate the minimum gold concentration that the gold doped microplastics should contain
for an effective and reliable quantification of microplastics.
As already introduced in section 2.2.1, Daphnia magna (D. magna) are freshwater zoo-
plankton, widely used in toxicology or traceability studies because of their characteristics.
Their dimension, which is between 5 and 6 mm in the adult age, besides making them
vulnerable to fish predation, makes them prone to microplastics ingestion, especially if con-
sidered that D. magna are unable to distinguish particle size and quality, and therefore
this makes them interesting for studies on microplastics interactions with water organisms.
[48, 75] Various researches have shown that D. magna can readily ingest microparticles
and nanoparticles of different size and shapes, including fibers, beads and fragments.[24,
49, 76–78] The active feeding range of D. magna would be from 200 nm [77] to 50 µm [6],
40
Weathered PE Microplastics with gold traces for increased environmental
traceability
==PAGE:57==
however, particle size between 20 nm and 70 µm [49, 75], fibers up to 1400 µm in length
and 528 µm in width [76], and beads up to 106 µm in diameter [78], have been observed
to be uptaken by D. magna. The uptake of particles outside the active feeding range is
considered to be a passive uptake, which could be due both to the interaction of small
particles with algae and to passive ingestion while drinking water.[49]
The studies looking into microplastics ingestion can be divided into two categories: qual-
itative and quantitative.
The qualitative studies only assess the presence of microplastics in the organism, while the
quantitative studies, in addition to assessing the presence, assess also the amount of mi-
croplastics in that organism. In addition to that, the two categories are used for different
purposes. Qualitative studies are focusing more into toxicological aspects such as assessing
the effect of microplastics on survival, growth and reproducibility of organisms, assessing
the trophic transfer of microplastics (transfer from prey to predator), and verifying the
occurrence of microplastics uptake in fixed conditions.[25, 30, 76]
While, quantitative studies focus on microplastics uptake and body burden dependence
on particle size [49, 77], shape [78], concentration [49] and algae presence [49], but can
also relate the toxicological aspect to the amount of microplastics uptaken [24, 51].
As of now, the number of studies dedicated to qualitative assessment are more numerous
than the ones focused on quantitative assessment, which in my knowledge are five: Rist et
al.[49], Rosenkranz et al.[77] and Canniff et al.[24], which quantified the concentration of
particles in mass, and Frydkjær et al.[78] and Elizalde-Velazquez et al.[51], which quanti-
fied the number of particles. This is probably due to the fact that, in the current state of
the art, the systems for microplastics traceability are still under development, as described
both in section 2.2.1 and 2.2. In tables 3.3 and 3.4 several microplastics ingestion studies
using D. magna as organisms are listed, in order to have an overview of the set-up and
the analysis methods used.
Tables 3.3 and 3.4 reflect the lack in standardization in studies concerning microplastics,
since, as can be noticed, the set-up of the experiments is different in every aspect among the
different studies. These multiple differences, from the standard used (medium and algae
presence), to the D. magna and microplastic concentration, and to microplastics type and
exposure time, makes the comparison of the results difficult. This lack of comparability
is emphasized in the quantitative studies, because of the few studies present in literature.
Given this, it is not possible, as of now, to make a precise calculation of the microplastics
uptake in the set-up that will be used in the current study, however, a rough estimation
can be done. Even though the set-up sees significant variations from one study to the
other, the detection methods are similar, since they are all based on microscopy, except
in Rist et al.[49], where fluorescent spectrophotometry is used.

3.4
Detection methods
Since the use of engineered nanoparticles has grown rapidly because of the huge variety of
applications that they have, and because their environmental fate and effects are unknown,
a variety of studies regarding the uptake of gold nanoparticles in D. magna can be found
in literature.[12, 13, 80–85] These studies, could be a good indicator of which detection
method is more suitable for the detection of gold nanoparticles in D. magna. The most
used methods are ICP-MS [82, 85] or similar (like LA-ICP-MS or other variations)[12] but
also by ICP-OES [81, 84] and electron microscopy [13, 83].
Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) is the most powerful method for
elemental analysis [86], which measures atoms mass with the use of mass spectrometry. It
is widely used for the traceability of metals since it can detect concentrations below 1 ppt,
which is the lowest among the instruments of its category. Traditionally, ICP-MS requires
a careful sample preparation, since to provide robust data it needs the total dissolved
solids to be below 0.2%. Therefore, in the case of solid samples or samples in solid matrix,
it requires digestion in strong acids and subsequent dilution in water. However, there are
alternatives like laser ablation inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (LA-ICP-
MS) that allow the load of solid samples on ICP-MS analysis. This technique uses a small
laser beam to atomize several microscopic spots in the sample and transport it to the
plasma through inert gas flow, thus the complexity of sample preparation is lowered. [64]
In the analysis on metal nanoparticles in D. magna, upgraded techniques of ICP-MS
analysis have been used.[87–89] For example, single particle inductively coupled plasma
mass spectrometry (sp-ICP-MS) or asymmetrical flow field flow fractionation inductively
coupled mass spectrometry (AF4-ICP-MS). For what concern sp-ICP-MS, the detection
occurs on the individual nanoparticle instead of measuring the elemental concentration
representative of the bulk, allowing both the determination of dissolved metal but also the
nanoparticles concentration and size, with the drawback that it could hinder the signal
of the smaller sized nanoparticles.
While, AF4-ICP-MS consists in the integration of
ICP-MS with a high resolution elution technique, which allows separation and sizing of
macromolecules, avoiding hindering of smaller particles signals. [88]
Weathered PE Microplastics with gold traces for increased environmental
traceability

Although these variations are interesting, their use is more complex, expensive and time
consuming, therefore, in this case where simply the atomic gold concentration needs to be
detected in order to quantify the ingestion of microplastics in D. magna, and there is no
interest in analyzing the particles themselves, the basic ICP-MS or LA-ICP-MS are more
suitable.
Inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES), is a good alternative
to ICP-MS, because less expensive and simpler to handle. Its quantification is based on
the measurement of excited atoms and ions at characteristic wavelengths for the specific
element. In addition, it is widely used in environmental analysis, because it is indicated
for high matrix samples, since it has a much higher tolerance to total dissolved solids than
ICP-MS, and it can reach up to 30%. However, it has higher detection limits, therefore it
is less sensitive than ICP-MS, and thus less suitable for the present study. [64, 81, 84]
Electron microscopy (EM) is a well accepted technique for the image analysis of biological
samples, and it is also a widely used technique in nanotechnology for the characterization
of primary particle size and morphology of nanoparticles, but only recently it was also ap-
plied for the detection of nanoparticles in biological samples. However, most nanoparticles
internalization studies executed on D. magna using EM imaging are either inconclusive or
reported limited detection of nanoparticle internalization. [13, 81, 83]
Given this both ICP-MS and LA-ICP-MS could be the most valid techniques to be used
for traceability of gold concentration in D. magna. Where the limit of detection (LOD)
for gold is 0.0009 ng/ml, the limit of quantification (LOQ) is from 10 to 100 times higher
than the LOD, if an accurate quantitative analysis is required, and the upper limit of
detection (ULOD) to have results in the linear range is 1000 ng/ml.[64]

al.[10], in order to digest PAN and PS, attempted several digestion methods on a mix of

density polystyrene. From the several attempts, it was found that aqua regia (3:1 HNO3

Experimental plan
The development of a synthesis procedure for the production of polyethylene microplastics
doped with gold will be executed by optimizing first the synthesis for polyethylene mi-
croplastics alone, and second by optimizing the same synthesis with the presence of gold.
The produced microplastics will then be used for degradation and traceability studies. A
scheme with reactions and analysis which will be executed for this scope is illustrated in
figures 4.1 and 4.2.
The synthesis of PE microplastics will be attempted, firstly, by the dissolution of PE
pellets in emulsion, to stabilize the particles’ dimension, followed by rapid gelation (1.1),
where, the particles’ dimension will be optimized by varying the concentration of surfac-
tant. Otherwise, in case of failure of the first method in giving particles of the desired
size, it will be tried by fractionation or precipitation of PE pellets (1.3) and subsequent
grinding (1.4). The produced microplastics will be weathered by UV exposure in air and
water (in parallel) (1.2) in order to obtain realistically degraded microplastics. Different
exposure times and setups will be used to try to achieve the optimal degradation, which
should be reached when the particles can be evenly distributed in an aqueous media. Both
the virgin and the degraded microplastics will be chemically and physically characterized
to assess the changes that lead from virgin PE to microplastics and weathered microplas-
tics. Gold doped PE microplastics production will start from the synthesis of dodecylthiol
functionalized gold nanoparticles through the two-phases Brust-Schiffrin method (2.1).
The produced particles will be used in the synthesis of gold doped PE microplastics (2.2)
which will be attempted by the use of the procedure optimized previously for PE only
microplastics (1.1 or 1.3 with 1.4). Then, the microplastics will be degraded using the
same weathering method (2.3)optimized for PE only microplastics (1.2). The procedures
will be modified if, due to the presence of gold, variations will take place. The gold doped
microplastics will be then characterized through chemical and physical analyses to verify
the even presence of gold. Lastly, in order to assess the stability of the produced mi-
croplastics in environmental study, gold leaching will be investigated by dispersing the
produced microplastics in deionized water for a long period (one or two months) and by
analyzing through ICP-MS whether the gold leached in water or not (2.4). Since, in order
to ensure appropriate and meaningful interpretation of test results, both in the biological
oxygen demand (BOD) measurement (3.1) and in the ingestion study with Daphnia magna
(D. magna) (4.1), the microplastics must be evenly distributed in the aqueous media, a
particular focus will be placed on this aspect. Given that polyethylene’s density is lower
than water’s density, PE microplastics would float on the water surface, unless additional
Weathered PE Microplastics with gold traces for increased environmental
traceability

measures are taken. Hence, firstly, as described previously, this will be attempted through

5
Results and discussion
In the present chapter, the results of the conducted experiments, described in chapter 7,
will be discussed.
As of now, in microplastics behavior and traceability simulation studies, poor attention
is placed on the choice of the microplastics used, as already mentioned and described
in chapter 2 and in particular in section 2.3. The lack of representativeness of environ-
mentally weathered microplastics and the difficulty of tracing environmentally realistic
concentrations of microplastics led to the investigation of the production of weathered
gold doped polyethylene microplastics. As described in chapter 4 and illustrated in figures
4.1 and 4.2, the development of this reference microplastics started from the optimization
of synthesis of PE microplastics to then investigate the addition of gold in this optimized
process; it continued with the investigation of the ideal weathering method, and then,
their biodegradation and their functioning in traceability studies were assessed.
5.1
Synthesis and characterization of polyethylene
microplastics
The synthesis of polyethylene microplastics was developed starting from a protocol found
in literature, Balakrishnan et al.[30], where the preparation of polyethylene micro or
nanoplastics was executed through an oil in water emulsion. The general procedure con-
sisted in the preparation of an emulsion constituted from: 1%wt LDPE, 10%wt toluene,
2%wt surfactant and 87%wt water. Where firstly LDPE pellets were dissolved in toluene
at 95°C in presence of a surfactant, then water was added after stabilizing the tempera-
ture at 80°C and the formed emulsion was homogenized by the use of both ultra-turrax
and ultrasonication, and lastly, the particles were formed by quickly cooling to 20°C the
homogenized emulsion. This technique relies on the homogenized oil in water emulsion to
form particles that are uniform in shape and dimension, as illustrated in figure 5.1. This
happens, because the polyethylene chains, which are relaxed and mobile while dissolved
in toluene, when water is added, because of their hydrophobicity, will remain into the
toluene phase. Since the toluene, in an oil in water emulsion, thanks to the surfactant
and to the homogenization, will be distributed in the emulsion under the form of uniform
droplets, the PE dispersed in those droplets when cooled will solidify inside those droplets.
Thus, the PE will take the size and shape of the toluene droplets, resulting in as uniform
particles as uniform is the dispersion of toluene droplets in water.

5.2
Synthesis and characterization of gold doped
polyethylene microplastics
The synthesis for the production of gold doped polyethylene microplastics was developed
starting from the final protocol developed for the synthesis of polyethylene microplastics.
The idea was to entrap the hydrophobic gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) in the PE microplas-
tics physically, as shown in figure 5.9. The synthesis technique is based on the formation of
an oil in water emulsion, where, after the dissolution of PE and surfactant in toluene, the
hydrophobic AuNPs are expected to be uniformly mixed in solution. This, because AuNPs
are originally dispersed in toluene, and because they should have the same hydrophobic
behavior of PE given from their functionalization with alkane chains. Then, after water
addition and emulsion formation, the AuNPs are still expected to behave similarly to PE
and remain in the toluene phase, since both are hydrophobic. Thus, after homogenization
and cooling it is expected for the AuNPs to be uniformly entrapped among the polymers
chains in the solidified particles formed inside the toluene droplets in the emulsion.

5.3
Accelerated UV weathering of polyethylene
microplastics
When plastic is dispersed in the environment it undergoes degradation due to exposure
to environmental phenomena (weathering), as described in chapter 2 section 2.4. When
plastic goes through a weathering process it is subject to surface modification, since the
molecular weight of the polymer chains on the surface decreases due to their fractionation
and formations of new functional groups (aldehydes, ketones, esters, alcohols, carboxylic
acids, vinyls), and since small debris leaves the main body due to wearing of the modified
surface, as illustrated in figure 5.16. These changes are a cause of variations in the behavior
of the plastics, therefore, in microplastics studies, a pristine bead cannot be considered
a representative model. For these reasons, in order to produce more realistic reference
microplastics, after the fabrication of PE microplastics, degradation studies were executed.

5.5
Accelerated weathering through chemical degradation
Since weathering trough UV exposure was found to be time consuming and the technique
used for the COD measurement was found to partially digest PE, it was decided to attempt
faster weathering of the produced PE microplastics by adapting the method used in the
COD measurement for this scope.
The COD measurement procedure, called Opened
Reflux Method 5220 B and found in Standard Methods for the examination of water and
wastewater [92], consisted in the digestion of a sample through oxidation by the use of
a boiling mixture of potassium dichromate standard solution and sulfuric acid reagent
solution. After digestion, the remaining unreduced amount of potassium dichromate was
titrated to measure the amount of sodium dichromate consumed and therefore the oxygen
consumed in the oxidation of the sample.
In order to adapt the method to the fast degradation of microplastics, only the diges-

Biodegradation of polyethylene microplastics
In order to observe the biodegradation rate of the produced microplastics and to compare
it among pristine and weathered ones, and among gold loaded and non-loaded ones, a
biodegradation analysis was executed by the use of a respirometric BOD measuring system
and by following the test set-up of the standard 301F from the OECD guideline which
is made for the measurement of ready biodegradability in aerobic aqueous medium[93],
described in section 7.10. To satisfy the standard conditions and to be able to validate
the results certain requirements needed to be fulfilled.
Amount of sample for the BOD measurement (ThOD or COD)
First, the amount of test substance to be added in the test bottle for the measure-
ment needs to correspond to an interval between 50 mgThOD/l or mgCOD/l and 100
mgThOD/l or mgCOD/l as a standard requirement.[93] In order to be inside the interval
75 mgThOD/l or mgCOD/l was chosen as target for the test substance addition. The
mass of sample to be added in the test bottles was calculated with two different methods:
calculation through the theoretical oxygen demand (ThOD) and calculation through the
measurement of chemical oxygen demand (COD). The calculation procedure and results
are displayed in appendix A.3 and shows how the two estimations gave significantly differ-
ent results. Accordingly to ThOD procedure, that is based on the requirement of oxygen to
fully degrade PE, the value of the amount of O2 consumed per gram of substance (ThOD)
Weathered PE Microplastics with gold traces for increased environmental
traceability
85
==PAGE:102==
was found to be 3424.7 mgO2/g, which corresponds to the addition of 8 mg of test sub-
stance in each test bottle. While, according to the COD procedure, which is based on the
measurement of oxygen developed during the sample digestion through harsh chemicals
for 2 hours, as described in section 7.9, the value of the amount of O2 consumed per gram
of substance (COD) was found to be 432.1 mgO2/g, which corresponds to the addition of
63.9 mg of test substance in each test bottle.
The reason behind the different results can be found in the fact that, when the digestion
for the COD measurement was executed, at the end of it, a cluster of PE could still
be found for every repetition. This means that the COD was not calculated on complete
degradation. What was calculated was the mg of oxygen consumed per g of PE to reach the
degradation extent at which exposure to boiling sulfuric acid and potassium dichromate
for 2 hours can bring to, which is clearly not complete degradation. Even though the
COD measurement did not lead a result close to the theoretical value as expected, it
gave an indication on a realistic and detectable degradation extent that PE could reach.
Since even in the presence of harsh chemicals the degradation extent is far from 100%, in
the presence of bio-organisms the degradation will be even lower. Considering that the
amount of sample added will not reach full degradation, as PE is not readily available for
biodegradation, the 8 mg of PE will not develop enough oxygen to reach the limit of 75
mgThOD/l under biodegradation only. Therefore, an addition equal to 8 mg is expected
to give such a low result, which will make it difficult to compare the biodegradation among
the different samples.
The principal scope of this analysis in this study was not to assess the biodegradability
of PE itself, which is known to be poor, but was to compare the biodegradability among
different samples to analyze how a change in a certain parameter could affect it. Therefore,
it was decided to analyze an amount of sample higher than the one indicated from the
ThOD but lower than the one calculated through COD. In this way, a visible result could
be obtained without risking to exceed the maximum detectable value of 75 mgO2/l. Based
on the maximum amount of sample available for this analysis, the test was executed on 13
mg of sample for each bottle. However, in future repetitions, because of the considerations
done, it would be interesting to use 50 or 60 mg of PE microplastics, to be able to see
more clear results.
The biodegradation analysis was executed on pristine PE microplastics (PE), PE mi-
croplastics weathered through UV exposure for 1 week (PE+UV) and gold loaded PE
microplastics weathered for 1 week under UV exposure (PE+AuNPs+UV). However,
the calculations for the sample addition were executed only based on pristine PE mi-
croplastics, since gold oxidizes only in specific conditions, and its functionalization, which
consists in a short alkane chain, it is considered to be negligible compared to PE.

6
Conclusion
The development of reference weathered gold doped polyethylene microplastics to be used
as model test particles in microplastics traceability studies was investigated.
Firstly, a synthesis method for the production of uniform polyethylene particles through
an oil in water emulsion was developed. It was showed that dissolution of PE in toluene
and subsequent formation of toluene in water emulsion in presence of a surfactant, if the
right temperature is kept (95°C) and the homogenization is done through ultrasonication,
leads to the formation of uniform PE particles, with traces of surfactant, with a particle
size between 6 - 24 µm (inside the D. magna feeding range) which can be dispersed in the
water column.
Then, when the gold entrapment in the developed synthesis procedure was investigated,
it was found that the hydrophobic AuNPs used as metal tracer, were not behaving as
polyethylene during the emulsion formation, and were undergoing flocculation, however,
through the homogenization step, it was possible to distribute them in the emulsion and
obtaining gold loaded microplastics. Among the different trials executed with gold addition
of 1%wtPE, 2%wtPE and 3%wtPE both by the use of dodecanethiol and octadecanethiol
functionalized AuNPs none as of now is known with certainty to have led to the aimed
gold entrapment (1%wtPE). However, the better product obtained consisted in gold doped
microplastics with a dimension between 8 and 28 µm with a measured gold entrapment of
0.586±0.101%wt. The uncertainty on whether the desired gold concentration was reached
or not is due to the fact that TGA was revealed to not be a trustworthy method in the
assessment of gold entrapment in these samples, and the ICP-MS results were not available
yet. However, when a clear indication of the gold content of the produced microplastics
will be available, it will be possible to optimize the gold addition and to understand which
functionalization of AuNPs leads to higher entrapment.
The most suitable method for the accelerated weathering of the produced microplastics
was investigated through UV exposure and chemical degradation. For what concerns UV
exposure, it was found that water inhibits the effect of the UV radiation, slowing down the
photo-oxidation process, and that UV exposure in air is most suitable as it is the faster
method. One week was shown to be a sufficient time to observe the full degradation of the
surfactant residue and surface modification. In addition, by comparing the degradation
of gold loaded and non-loaded PE microplastics, it was found that the presence of gold
inhibits the degradation of microplastics since its dark brown coloration absorbs UV radi-
ation, where the inhibitory effect increases together with the gold concentration. For what
concerns chemical degradation, it was found that the exposure of PE microplastics to an
Weathered PE Microplastics with gold traces for increased environmental
traceability
95
==PAGE:112==
oxidizing agent and acid can allow to reach a significant surface modification in only 10
minutes, and 2 hours are sufficient to reach higher degradation than what obtained with
4 weeks of exposure to UV light in air. However, attention in the purification of the de-
graded particle should be placed, to avoid the residue of toxic chemicals. Considered that,
UV degradation is influenced by the presence of gold and it is slower, chemical degra-
dation seems to be the more promising method. However, future trials on gold loaded
microplastics should be done, to confirm this theory.
Investigating the dispersion of the produced pristine and weathered microplastics in water,
showed that weathering, instead of increasing and facilitating the dispersion of microplas-
tics, causes a decrease in microplastics immersion in the water column, and leads to their
floating and adhesion to the walls of the vial. This phenomenon was seen to be higher
with increased degradation extent because of the higher particle agglomeration. Since this
demonstrated the opposite behavior of what expected, it would be interesting to execute
repetitions of the experiment, in order to confirm this new theory.
The executed biodegradability analysis, showed as expected that the produced microplas-
tics are subjected to little or null biodegradation, and that other side activities, like for
example hydrolysis, have occurred during the analysis. In addition, the method was found
to not be optimal for the comparison of the biodegradation process of pristine and weath-
ered microplastics, since the different ability of dispersion in water affects significantly the
results of the analysis. Furthermore, microplastics are suspected to inhibit the inoculum
due to toxic effects. For these reasons it would be interesting to repeat this analysis, with
higher amount of sample, to enhance the possibility of detecting biodegradability, addition
of a bio-compatible surfactant, to have uniform distribution of all the samples in water,
and by executing an inhibition test, to have an answer on whether the micro-organisms
are affected from the introduced microplastics.
Lastly, when the microplastics traceability results will be available, it will possible to
have a better base from which to understand how to optimize the produced reference mi-
croplastics, by understanding what is the real optimal gold content to reach low detection
limits and not modifying the behavior of the microplastics. When this will be optimized,
it would be interesting to further research on how to produce the reference gold doped
microplastics in different particle sizes and trying their application in water and ground
traceability studies.

Methods
Sonication
The homogenization by sonication was executed by the use of a Hielscher UP200S (200
watts, 24 kHz) ultrasonic processor, with the use of a sonotronde S14 with a diameter of
14 mm, an amplitude of 100% and pulse of 50%.
Ultra-turrax
The homogenization was also attempted by the use of an IKA Ultra-turrax T25, set to
20500 rpm.
FT-IR spectroscopy
FT-IR analysis was performed by attenuated total reflectance Fourier transformed infrared
spectroscopy (ATR-FT-IR) through the use of a Thermofisher Scientific Nicolet iS50 FT-
IR fitted with a single diamond crystal attenuated total reflection accessory (ATR), which
did 32 scans per measurement with a resolution of 4 cm−1.
Optical microscopy
Leica DMLB optical microscope with variable zoom and a Leica MC190 HD camera was
used for optical microscopy analysis. The images were then elaborated through Image J.
Thermogravimetric analysis
The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was done by the use of a Discovery TGA machine
from TAInstruments. The temperature increase was done at a rate of 10°C/min from
room temperature to 1000°C under N2 atmosphere. The analyses were done on 1 mg
of sample for hydrophobic gold nanoparticles and 10-15 mg of sample for gold doped
microplastics.
UV-visible spectrophotometry
The UV-visible spectrophotometry was carried out on a POLARstar Omega plate reader
from BMG LABTECH. The analysis set-up consisted in inserting 260 µl of sample inside
the wells of a plate COSTAR 96. Two blanks were done for each repetition, one on the
solvent (toluene) and one on the plate. The measurements were done on a wavelength
between 220 and 1000 nm, with a resolution of 2 nm. The instrument was set in order to
shake the sample for 20 s before the measurement.
Freeze drying
The freeze drying was carried out by the use of a Christ Alpha 1-2 LDplus freeze dryer,
with ice condenser set to -60°C. The procedure consisted of a two phases sublimation
process: the first stage, which is the main drying stage (overnight, 3 mbar), and the
second stage, which is called final drying stage (4 h, 0.01 mbar).

Synthesis of hydrophobic gold nanoparticles
The hydrophobic gold nanoparticles were synthesized by following the two-phase liquid-
liquid Brust-Schiffrin method.[3] Two different functional groups were used, dodecanethiol
and octadecanethiol.
AuNPs-dodecanethiol
A yellow 30 mM aqueous solution of hydrogen tetrachloroaurate trihydrate (6.770 ml)
was mixed with a transparent 50 mM solution of tetraoctylammonium bromide in toluene
(18.052 ml). The red two-phase solution was vigorously stirred for 2 hours, for the tetra-
chloroaurate to be transferred to the organic layer by the use of TOABr as transfer reagent.
Then, dodecanethiol (85.2 µl, 0.36 mmol) was added drop by drop to the organic phase,
resulting in the solution change in color from red to transparent. After 15 minutes of
vigorous stirring, a freshly prepared transparent 0.5 M aqueous solution of sodium boro-
hydride (5.642 ml) was slowly added under vigorous stirring. The previous addition turned
the solution into a dark purple/brown color. The two-phase solution was further stirred
for two hours. Lastly, the organic phase was separated from the aqueous phase and it
was evaporated in a rotary evaporator. The crude product was then purified by dissolv-
ing it into 5 ml toluene and mixing it with 200 ml of ethanol to remove the excess of
reagents. The mixture was kept in the freezer at -26°C for 4 hours. Afterward the dark
brown precipitate was filtered, rinsed with ethanol, and collected by dissolving it into 200
ml of toluene and by evaporating the solvent in a rotary evaporator. The product was
then redissolved in 2 ml of toluene, purified as previously, and further dried in a vacuum
oven. The final product, 0.0763 g (62.54%wt yield), was a brown/black ultra-fine powder,
which contained 0.0520 g of gold (129.89%wt yield). The final functionalized AuNPs were
analyzed through TGA and FT-IR before being redispersed in toluene (3 ml) for further

7.9
Chemical oxygen demand measurement
The chemical oxygen demand (COD) measurement of PE microplastics (PE3.4) was ex-
ecuted by following the Open Reflux Method 5220 B from Standard Methods for the
examination of water and wastewater [92].
Reagents
Three solutions were prepared:
• A standard potassium dichromate solution 0.04167 M was prepared by dissolving
potassium dichromate (6.1295 g), previously dried at 150°C for 2 h, in deionized
water (up to 500 ml).
• A sulfuric acid reagent solution was prepared by dissolving silver sulfate (9.9459 g)
in sulfuric acid (988.1750 ml) by letting it stand for 2 days.
• A standard ferrous ammonium sulfate (FAS) titrant solution 0.25 M was prepared
by dissolving ammonium iron (II) sulfate hexahydrate (49 g) in sulfuric acid (10 ml)
and deionized water (up to 500 ml).
Standardization of FAS solution
The standardization of the standard FAS solution against the standard potassium dichro-
mate solution was executed by diluting with deionized water 25 ml of standard potassium
dichromate solution to 100 ml and slowly adding 30 ml of sulfuric acid while cooling in
an ice bath. 2 ml of ferroin indicator solution were added and a titration was executed by
addition of FAS in an amount sufficient to change the color of the solution from green/blue
to brown/red. The measurement was done in triplicates and the volume of FAS used was
noted for the calculations explained in appendix A.3.1.
COD measurement procedure
20 mg of PE3.4 were introduced in a 500 ml Erlenmeyer flask and diluted to 50 ml with
deionized water. A magnet, for magnetic stirring, and several glass beads with a diameter
of 3 mm, to avoid hard boiling of acids, were introduced in the flask. After the addition
of 25 ml of standard potassium dichromate solution a condenser was assembled to the
flask. 75 ml of sulfuric acid were slowly added through the open end of the condenser.
The solution was stirred thoroughly to prevent local heating of the bottom of the flask.
Then, after covering the open end of the condenser, the solution was let refluxing for 2 h
at 250°C. Afterward, the mixture was cooled and the condenser was washed down with
deionized water and disconnected. After its disconnection the solution was further diluted
until reaching with washing and dilution twice the initial volume, therefore 150 ml of
deionized water were added in total. For the titration, firstly, 2 ml of ferrroin indicator
solution were added, then, FAS solution was added in an amount sufficient to change the
color of the solution from green/blue to brown/red. The volume of FAS used was noted
to calculate the COD value. The blank was executed with the same procedure, with the

7.10
Biological oxygen demand measurement
The biological oxygen demand (BOD) measurement of PE microplastics , weathered PE
microplastics and weathered gold doped PE microplastics was executed by the use of a
close bottle system, where the cup is a benchtop meter control (OxiTop®), and by following
test set-up 301F for ready biodegradability in aerobic aqueous medium from the OECD
guideline [93]. PE3.4 was used as PE microplastics, while PE3.4 exposed to UV light for
1 week was used as weathered PE microplastics and PE+AuNPs7 exposed to UV light for
1 week was used as weathered gold doped PE microplastics. During the test 12 bottles
were used: two replicates for each of the three samples, one abiotic control, two positive
controls and two inoculum blanks. The bottle were placed in an incubator with constant
temperature (20±1°C) under constant stirring (180 - 450 rpm) for 28 days.
Mineral medium
Four stock solutions were prepared:
• 1 l of stock a, a buffer solution with pH = 7.4, was prepared by dissolution of potas-
sium dihydrogen orthophosphate (8.50 g), dipotassium hydrogen orthophosphate
(21.75 g), disodium hydrogen orthophosphate dihydrate (33.40 g) and ammonium
chloride (0.50 g) in deionized water (up to reaching the volume of 1 l).
• 1 l of stock b, a saline solution, was prepared by dissolving calcium chloride dihydrate
(36.40 g) in deionized water (up to reaching the volume of 1 l).
• 1 l of stock c, a saline solution, was prepared by dissolving magnesium sulphate
heptahydrate (22.50 g) in deionized water (up to reaching the volume of 1 l).
• 1 l of stock d, a saline solution, was prepared by dissolving iron(III) chloride hex-
ahydrate (0.25 g) in deionized water (up to reaching the volume of 1 l).
Two batches of 5 l of mineral medium with pH = 7.46 were prepared by mixing stock
a (50 ml) with deionized water (4 l), adding solutions stock b, c and d (5 ml each) and
diluting with deionized water up to 5 l.
Inoculum
The inoculum (activated sludge) was collected from an aerobic wastewater tank, a day in
advance from the analysis, and aerated for 24 h, until its use. The total suspended solid
(TSS) present in the inoculum was measured by extracting three samples of 9.8 ml, each
sample was weighted before and after drying it in a vacuum oven. By the calculations
displayed in appendix A.3.4 the inoculum was found to contain 10.24 gTSS/l and each
bottle was found to contain 16.34 mgTSS/l which is below the limit of TSS allowed (30
mgTSS/l).

A.3
Biological oxygen demand measurement
The amount of samples to be used for the biological oxygen demand (BOD) measurement
was calculated both through the use of the chemical oxygen demand (COD) and the
theoretical oxygen demand (ThOD). In addition, the amount of sodium acetate (NaAc)
to be used in the positive control was calculated through the use of the theoretical oxygen
demand (ThOD). Lastly, the amount of total suspended solid (TSS) in the collected active
sludge was calculated to make sure that it did not exceed the threshold.
A.3.1
Chemical oxygen demand calculation
The molarity (M) of the standard FAS solution was found to be 0.245 M from the stan-
dardization procedure described in section 7.9 and the calculation here. described. The
calculation was based on the average FAS used to titrate the standard potassium dichro-
mate solution (table 7.6) and the following equation:
MFAS solution [M or mol/l] = V standard potassium dichromate solution [ml]
V FAS used for titration [ml]
· 0.2500
In order to calculate the COD from the experimental method described in section 7.9 the
following equation was used:
COD [mgO2/g] = (B −A) · MFAS solution
mCODsample
· 8000
Where, B is the volume in ml of FAS used for the blank, A is the volume in ml of FAS
used for the sample, and mCODsample is the mass of sample on which the measurement
was executed. The value of B was found to be 24.4 ml from the average of FAS used when
titrating the blank samples. The results of the COD calculation are found in table A.4.

in each bottle for the BOD measurement. The OECD 301F standard require a sample
