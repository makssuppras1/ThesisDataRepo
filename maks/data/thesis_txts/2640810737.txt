Abstract 
Sandwich composites are of significant interest over a broad range of industries. 
Optimization of structures, in terms of weight and cost, are continuously researched. As the 
cost of manufacturing of sandwich composites decreases, the demand and its uses will continue 
to increase. There are a near endless possiblity of material combinations that can be chosen. 
Sandwich composites are highly versitile as they can be designed for very specific applications. 
As these materials are pushed further and further to their peak performance, it is critical that 
the material properties are reliably, repeatably, and accuratly determined so that these materials 
can be utilized safely. Of particular interest is the common debonding failure mode. Debonding 
is the separation of the face sheet from the core and it results in considerable loss of structural 
integrity. Several tests have been designed to measure the fracture toughness, or the energy 
required for fracture to commence. This study will focus on the face/core interface fracture 
toughness and mode-mixity of sandwich composites subject to mixed-mode bending. 
At this time there is an ASTM standard for measuring the fracture toughness and mode-
mixity, under mixed-mode bending (MMB), of monolithic laminates with a crack at the neutral 
surface. This standard has been adapted to measure fracture toughness and mode-mixity of 
sandwich composites with a crack at the upper face/core interface, under mixed-mode bending. 
This study begins by determining the minimum crack length 𝑎𝑎 and ligament length (𝐿𝐿−𝑎𝑎) to 
fullfill the assumption of the 2D elasticy equations used to calculate the energy release rate and 
mode-mixity. The equations assume that the stress at the crack tip does not have any 
interference from the ends of the specimen or the distribution of stress below the center roller. 
Through FE analysis, it was determined that for relative face/core thickness of 𝜂𝜂= 𝑡𝑡𝑓𝑓𝑡𝑡𝑐𝑐
⁄
≤
0.05 the minimum crack and ligament lengths are 3x the thickness of the total sandwich 
specimen. As 𝜂𝜂 increases, 𝜂𝜂≥0.1, the minimum crack and ligament lengths become 5x the 
thickness of the total sandwich specimen. These minimum lengths will govern the dimensions 
of an up-scaled MMB testing apparatus, designed specifically for sandwich composites. 
An up-scaled MMB testing apparatus was successfully designed, manufactured, and 
implemented in several tests. Aeronautical test specimens comprised of CFRP woven face 
sheets and 24kg/m3, 6.4mm cell size honeycomb Nomex core specimens were tested on both 
the standard and up-scaled MMB apparatuses and a comarison of the results were made. Since 
there is currently no standard for sandwich MMB testing, the work from this thesis is to help 
with producing an ASTM standard for this test.

1 Introduction 
The field of composites is a rapidly growing industry as the quest to optimize the 
performance of structures continues. Sandwich composites are of particular interest as they 
offer very high strength/stiffness to weight ratios. Industries such as marine, wind, aeronautical, 
automotive, and sport heavily rely on the employment of sandwich composites. For example, 
sandwich panels are often used for ship decks in the marine industry. Many large wind turbine 
blades are comprised of sandwich composites, often glass fiber or carbon fiber is used as the 
face sheets with balsa as the core. Semi-structural commercial aircraft parts such as stabilizers, 
flaps, doors, and floor panels utilize sandwich composites. 
A sandwich composites is defined from the ASTM as, “ a special form of laminated 
composite comprising of a combination of different materials that are bonded to each other so 
as to utilize the properties of each separate component to the structural advantage of the whole 
assembly.” 
1.1 Sandwich Construction and Materials 
A sandwich panel consists of a top and bottom face sheet bonded to a central core. 
Figure 1 [1] shows the typical sandwich construction and nomenclature. 
 
 
Figure 1. Sandwich construction 
 
A wide variety of materials can be used for both the face sheets and the core. Selection 
of the materials is dependent on the application; however, for any structural application it is 
imperative that the load can transfer from the face sheets and the core. The face sheets are

usually made up of high stiffness and high tensile and compressive strength fiber composites, 
and the core is usually a low-density material of several types such as corrugated, honeycomb, 
balsa wood, or cellular foams. Figure 2 shows the different types of core materials used in 
sandwich composites. The face sheets primarily carry the bending moments as tensile and 
compressive stresses but, depending on the application, they may also need to be impact 
resistant, wear resistant, or resistant to environmental effects. The core primarily carries the 
transverse forces as shear stress. The core is also responsible for providing sufficient stiffness 
perpendicular to the faces, and thermal insulation if needed, etc.

1.2 Sandwich Composite Testing Methods 
Most test methods designed to measure the interface fracture toughness of sandwich 
composites are for either mode-I or mode-II. Figure 3 [4] shows several different test methods 
used to determine the face/core interface fracture toughness.

Nearly all the tests listed above came from standardized tests for laminate composites that 
have been adapted to sandwich composites. This thesis will focus on the mixed-mode bending 
(MMB) test. Currently there is no ASTM standard for how to test sandwich composites using 
the MMB test method. The work from this thesis is to be used to help create a standard for how 
to accurately and repeatably measure the mixed-mode interface fracture toughness of sandwich 
composites. 
The MMB test was designed by Reeder and Crews [5] and was initially used for static and 
fatigue crack growth tests in monolithic laminates, with a crack at the neutral surface, under a 
mixture of mode-I and mode-II loading. Here, the MMB test is being adapted to define a 
sandwich mixed-mode bending (S-MMB) test. For the S-MMB test a crack exists at the upper 
face/core interface as opposed to the neutral surface as is done with the standard MMB test. 
The test works by applying a combination of both mode-I and mode-II loading to the specimen. 
The MMB test setup can be seen in Figure 4. The amount of mode-I vs. mode-II loading can 
be controlled by varying the lever arm distance c. The lever arm distance c is constant during 
the entire test, which means a constant mode-mixity is applied at the crack tip for a growing 
crack, which is ideal for fatigue testing.

2.3 Face/Core Interface

elastic material, even if the loading is different from mode-I, the crack will kink in the direction

3 Mixed-Mode Bending Analysis 
Three analytical data reduction methods were implemented and compared to test their 
capabilities and to help determine the ideal data reduction method for a new ASTM standard 
for MMB testing of sandwich composites. A symmetric sandwich beam with a crack at the 
upper face sheet/core interface can be seen in Figure 9 [14]. Note that (d) is the delaminated 
arm, (s) is the substrate arm, and (b) is the base arm of the sandwich beam. 
 
 
Figure 9. Symmetric sandwich beam convention 
3.1 Beam Theory 
The first data reduction method was developed using beam theory and including core 
shear deformation and elastic foundation analysis [4]. The MMB test can simply be analyzed 
as a superposition of the Cracked Sandwich Beam (CSB) test and the Double Cantilever 
Sandwich Beam (DCB) test. When 𝑐𝑐= 0 the MMB test simply becomes the CSB test and the 
loading is mostly mode-II; alternatively, when 𝑐𝑐 increases, the mode-I loading begins to 
dominate. At a sufficiently large 𝑐𝑐 the MMB test would simply become the DCB test. Figure 
10 depicts the static analysis of the MMB testing fixture and how the loading can be 
decomposed into the CSB and DCB tests.

superimposed to determine them for the MMB test.

3.2 Two-Dimensional Elasticity Expressions 
The second data reduction method considers the shear effects and root rotations at the 
crack tip [14]. The main assumption for these semi-analytical equations comes from St. 
Venant’s principle: there is no interference between the stresses generated at the crack tip and 
the edges of the specimen; as well as, the stress distribution of the applied load. This means 
that there exists a minimum crack length 𝑎𝑎𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚 and minimum ligament length (𝐿𝐿−𝑎𝑎𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚) for 
the equations to be valid. It is assumed that the face sheets and core are isotropic and 
homogeneous. Figure 14 depicts the generalized forces that generate crack tip forces. The 
expressions for the strain energy release rate 𝐺𝐺𝐵𝐵 and the mode-mixity 𝜓𝜓𝐵𝐵 under generalized

4 S-MMB Testing Apparatus 
The idea for the S-MMB apparatus was to simply upscale the current MMB apparatus 
to be able to accommodate several different sized specimens with various material 
combinations. The main question is what is the minimum specimen length needed, that will 
not violate the assumed St. Venant’s principle of the analytical equations. The minimum 
specimen length will govern the physical size of the up-scaled apparatus. The minimum 
specimen length could vary depending on the material system of interest. The up-scaled testing 
apparatus should be able to test a majority of material systems; however, analyzing every 
different material system is not feasible. To simply this, one common material combination 
and size was selected from three different industries: Marine, Wind, and Aeronautical. The 
three common material combinations  chosen, from the three different industries, are listed in 
Table 3.

From previous work done with testing sandwich composites on the original MMB 
apparatus, it was determined that doublers would be needed to add additional stiffness to the 
specimens. Doublers are simply additional material bonded to both the top and bottom face 
sheet to add stiffness. Without doublers the deformation of the specimen, specifically the 
delaminated arm on the MMB specimen, becomes too large and can both exceed the limitations 
of the apparatus and/or become non-linear. To ensure proper adhesion of the doublers to the 
face sheets, the white paint on the face sheet needed to be delicately sanded off (without 
sanding into the face sheet itself). Figure 19 shows three specimens with the white paint from 
the original panel, and three specimens that have been correctly sanded.

After the specimens and doublers have been cut to size, appropriately sanded, cleaned, 
and the holes in the hinges have been properly drilled, it is time to prepare them to be glued 
together. Before gluing, it is necessary to add roughness to all the surfaces that will be bonded. 
Adding roughness to the gluing surface provides a stronger bond. The glue used to adhere 
everything together was an epoxy made specifically for composites. Whenever working with 
an adhesive, it is important to take precautions to make sure that the adhesive does not get onto 
the core, or other places it shouldn’t be, as it will affect the fracture toughness, crack 
propagation, and/or mode-mixity. To avoid these issues, tape was applied to all the components 
of the specimen before gluing. Figure 21 (a) shows all the components of the MMB specimen, 
with roughened faces and tape to ensure a proper bond. Once all the specimen components 
have been glued together, they must be clamped and left to cure for 24 hours. Figure 21 (b) 
shows one of the S-MMB specimens clamped together and left to cure.

It was discovered in the first few tests, that gluing the hinges to the doublers was not an 
effective joining method, as the top hinge on several of the tests popped off during the test. The 
gluing process allows lot of human error to enter the specimen preparation. It is possible the 
hinges were not glued effectively, such as: not enough glue, glued in a slightly wrong 
orientation causing eccentric loading, using the wrong type of adhesive, etc. The adhesive was 
designed specifically for composites, so it is possible that using an adhesive for metals would 
be more effective. Ultimately it was decided to drill holes into the doublers and mechanically 
join the hinges to the doublers using screws, as seen in Figure 23. Choosing to fix the hinges 
to the doublers in this way, was chosen because it is very strong, and it fixes the uncertainty in 
alignment. A digital drill press was used to drill the holes, which eliminates much of the human 
error in terms of alignment.

6.2 S-MMB Results and Discussion 
Aside from the first S-MMB test, all the S-MMB specimens were prepared with 5mm 
doublers. One test was performed in force control at a rate of 0.5N/s and the rest of the tests 
were run in displacement control at a rate of 5mm/min. The S-MMB tests were setup using 
MPT [22]. 
 
S-MMB Test 1 
 
The first S-MMB specimen was prepared using 3mm doublers, as was done with all the 
MMB specimens, and the test was performed in displacement control at a rate of 2mm/min. 
The force vs. displacement for the first S-MMB test can be seen in Figure 37. The initial test 
was done using the 100kN load cell, already mounted in the machine, and the crack propagated 
27mm (𝑎𝑎= 142mm). The general rule of thumb is that the load cell is accurate down to 1% 
of the load cell’s capacity, in this case the load cell in the machine can accurately record forces 
at a minimum force of 1kN. As seen in the Figure 37, the loads were considerably lower than 
1kN; therefore, a smaller load cell was required to accurately measure the forces. It was decided 
that a 1kN load cell would be the appropriate size for the S-MMB tests; however, the new load 
cell would need to be calibrated. Figure 38 (a) shows how the calibration of the new load cell 
was performed. The load cell is placed in series with a reference load cell that is verified by 
the manufacturer to be accurate (in this case, the reference load cell was 5kN). To calibrate the 
new 1kN load cell, the machine applies a force to the load cells in series, and the gain of the 
1kN load cell was adjusted until it matches within 1% of the measurements of the reference 
load cell over a range of loads (200N, 300N, 400N, etc.). Figure 38 (b) shows how the new 
1kN load cell was integrated into the S-MMB apparatus: a 100kN load cell (which is no longer 
being used) is connected to the LVDT, the hydraulic grips hold onto the new 1kN load cell, 
and the loading yoke is connected to the new 1kN load cell. The redo test was performed using 
the 1kN load cell; however, it was prematurely stopped on accident. The redo test propagated 
the crack another 13mm (𝑎𝑎= 155mm). The 2x redo test was run successfully, propagating the 
crack another 35mm (𝑎𝑎= 190mm). As seen in Figure 37 (a), there is a lot of noise in the data 
from the three tests. This noise is most likely due to the mismatch in using a large 100kN 
machine with a small 1kN load cell. Therefore, the data was averaged as seen in Figure 37 (b). 
The corresponding observed points of delamination can be seen in Table 13.

It is important to make sure that the doublers do not yield; so, as before with the MMB 
specimen, a strain gauge was placed on the top doubler (just above the initial crack tip 𝑎𝑎𝑜𝑜) and 
another strain gauge was placed on the bottom doubler (just below the center roller). The strain 
measurements can be seen in Figure 39. First, the strain due to the weight of the lever was 
measured by holding the back end of the lever up and then slowly lowering it until the specimen 
was carrying the weight of the lever, these results are presented in Figure 39 (a). Next, the 
strain was measured during the duration of the test, these results are presented in Figure 39 (b). 
The maximum strain due to the weight of the lever is added to the strain measured during the 
test. From Table 6, the yield stress of the aluminum doublers is 𝜎𝜎𝑦𝑦= 150 −180MPa, which 
results in a yield strain of 𝜀𝜀𝑦𝑦= 2143 −2571𝜇𝜇, and from Figure 39 (b), the maximum strain 
experienced on the top doubler ranges from 2259 −2365𝜇𝜇 for the three tests. The measured 
strain is right within the range of yielding, so visual observation is also used to confirm that 
plastic deformation has occurred. Figure 40 shows the delaminated arm of the tested specimen 
lifted from the substrate arm, confirming that plastic deformation has occurred. Plastic 
deformation means that the aluminum has absorbed some of the energy and therefore, an 
accurate energy release rate cannot be calculated from the S-MMB test 1 data. The future S-
MMB tests will be performed using 5mm doublers to avoid yielding.

S-MMB Test 3 
 
The third S-MMB test was performed in force control at a rate of 0.5N/s. Before 
beginning the test, the load needed to be tuned to get accurate results. To tune the load, I simply 
increase the proportional (P) gain and the integral (I) gain until I got acceptable results. A rule 
of thumb is to keep the integral gain 10% of the proportional gain, so I was mainly concerned 
with increasing the proportional gain. This tuning method came from the MTS instructions for 
tuning [23]. Figure 43 shows the results of the load tuning. The red line is the command given 
by the controller and the blue line is the measured data, as the gains are increased the measured 
data converges to the command. It is important to not increase the gains unnecessarily high 
because it could cause instability which would result in sudden and jerky movements in the 
==PAGE:59==

8 Future Work 
The work done in this thesis has shown that the new re-designed S-MMB testing apparatus 
can produce successful test results for static compression tests; however, there is much more 
work that can be done to verify the new testing apparatus. Below is a list of additional work 
that must be done to further study the validity of the up-scaled S-MMB testing apparatus. 
 
- 
Different mode-mixities 
- 
Fatigue testing 
- 
Comparison between the L and W directions of the honeycomb core 
- 
Different material configurations from different industries/for all kinds of applications 
- 
Calibration test of S-MMB rig 
- 
FE analysis considering non-linear effects introduced by the frictionless contact 
between the rollers and the specimen and the large displacements of the delaminated 
face-sheet 
- 
In depth FE analysis to better determine the minimum crack and ligament lengths for 
various material systems 
 
On the analytical side, a closed form solution including the doublers is needed. The brute 
force method used in this study could be a significant source of error. Expansion of the 
analytical equations to orthotropic sandwich layers is also necessary (especially for the material 
system used in this study). The isotropic face sheets and core assumption in the current 
analytical models forced the homogenization of the highly orthotropic honeycomb core, which 
can be a significant source of error. 
 
It was made apparent, once testing began on the S-MMB apparatus, that there is an issue 
with the weight of the lever. The lever (with the hinge clamp, roller, roller holder, and saddle 
attached) is quite heavy. The weight of the lever is placed on top of the specimen and then the 
servo-hydraulic testing machine begins to push onto that. The problem is that the measured 
force will not account for the weight of the lever. To fix this problem, an already tested 
specimen was placed in the fixture and the saddle and loading yoke were tethered together and 
instead of pushing with the machine like normal, the machine lifted the lever until the reaction 
force on the lever, at the center roller, was zero and the force was recorded. This measured 
==PAGE:69==
62 
 
force was used to offset all the force data. There are two issues with this: first, this would need 
to be done for every configuration that is chosen to test and second, this neglects rigid body 
rotations and translations due to the weight of the lever. A proposed solution to this problem is 
a re-designed loading yoke as seen in Figure 50. This loading yoke will tether the lever to the 
testing machine. In this way, the machine can hold the weight of the lever before the test begins. 
This essentially would provide a way to tare the weight of the lever.

9 Conclusion 
Sandwich composites are vastly used in many different industries, often due to the need 
of weight optimization. There is an almost unlimited amount of material systems that can be 
chosen for a sandwich composite. In this thesis a common aeronautical material system was 
chosen. To be able to safely design sandwich composites structures, the fracture toughness is 
a vital material property to know. In particular, the face/core interface fracture toughness is a 
very important parameter as debonding is a common failure mode in sandwich composites. 
Currently there is an ASTM standard for the mixed mode-I/mode-II interlaminar 
fracture toughness of unidirectional fiber reinforced polymer matrix composites. The method 
has been adapted to sandwich composites to determine the interface fracture toughness for 
mixed-mode loading conditions. Currently there is no standard for the sandwich mixed-mode 
bending test. It was discovered that the minimum crack and ligament lengths, to ensure that the 
stresses at the crack tip are free from interference from the specimen ends and the stress 
distribution below the central roller, were a function of the relative face/core thickness 𝜂𝜂. As 𝜂𝜂 
increases the minimum crack and ligament lengths increase. From this discovery, an up-scaled 
MMB testing apparatus was designed, manufactured, and used to perform S-MMB tests. Valid 
test results were obtained, and fracture toughness was calculated using both 1D and 2D 
elasticity equations. The phase angle was calculated using two different data reduction methods 
and the results from both were compared. Fracture toughness and mode-mixity results seem to 
agree nicely among the respective tests (when comparing just the MMB or S-MMB), but there 
are considerable differences when comparing all the MMB tests to the S-MMB tests. This is 
most likely due to the standard MMB specimens being considerably smaller and violating the 
St. Venant’s principle that is assumed in the analytical models. 
These tests allow for human error. The first source of human error is in the cutting, 
sanding, and gluing of the specimens. The specimens need to be cut to precise dimension, the 
sanding must not affect face sheet properties, and if the doublers are not adhered properly, then 
there can be specimen-fixture misalignment or the test could accidentally be measuring the 
disbond between the doublers and face sheets. The second source of human error is matching 
the pictures that are taken at 5 or 10 second intervals with the servo-hydraulic test machine data 
that is recorded at 10Hz. A possible solution to this is using an optical microscope to measure 
the crack growth, similarly to how the DCB-UBM tests are performed. The sources of human 
error exist, but are mitigated with skill and experience in testing. 
==PAGE:71==
64 
 
Other possible sources of error exist: the isotropic and homogenous layers assumption 
of the analytical models, the brute force method used to determine the effective face sheet 
stiffness, large machine/small load cell mismatch, which can cause noise in the data. These 
possible sources of error could also be contributing to the differences in fracture toughness and 
phase angle measurements seen in Section 7. FE analysis will need to be performed to provide 
a benchmark for the test results obtained in this study. Research is still being done on the 
analytical models. Inclusion of doublers and expansion of the 2D elasticity equations to 
orthotropic materials would significantly reduce error. Overall, the up-scaled S-MMB testing 
apparatus produced viable test results and it is a promising method for measuring the interface 
fracture toughness of sandwich composites.

11 Appendix 
The drawings used to produce the large S-MMB testing apparatus are provided. The 
drawings do not include the hardware and things needed to be able to use the S-MMB testing 
apparatus. Table 21 provides a list of the additional things needed to assemble and begin using 
the S-MMB testing apparatus. All the items in Table 21 are off-the-shelf products. The bearings 
are used for the two rollers, and at the load introduction point on the saddle. The length of the 
hinges is too long, but the other dimensions are good; therefore, the ends of the hinges were 
cut to fit into the hinge clamps. The hinges are fixed to the front of the S-MMB specimen and 
secured in the hinge clamps. The retaining rings are used to secure the saddle pins so that the 
bearings on the saddle stay in place. The M12 nut is used to secure the loading pin to the loading 
yoke. Ideally, all the S-MMB apparatus components would be machined from a single block 
of material; however, due to time constraints, many of the components were manufactured in 
pieces and assembled using dowel pins and screws to save time. Washers were used to secure 
the roller holders and saddle to the lever, and as spacers between the bearings in the saddle.
