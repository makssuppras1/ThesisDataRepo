Abstract
Increasing demands for high-performing, energy-efficient, and resilient buildings have forced the
Architecture, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry to pursue a digital transforma-
tion. New technologies as Building Information Modelling (BIM) and Building Energy Mod-
elling (BEM) has revolutionised the means of collaboration, and provided tools to evaluate the
energy performance of a built asset during design. Despite of this, research continues to highlight
that the AEC industry is facing challenges under the digitalisation. Furthermore, that there are
often significant discrepancies between the predicted performance of a design and the observed
performance of the realised building. The latter is known as the Energy Performance Gap (EPG).
Based on the presumption that with increased interoperability between BIM and BEM models
more accurate and detailed energy simulations can be applied in the AEC industry, this thesis
aims to provide a proof of concept that automatically generated BIM-based BEM models hold
the potential to reduce the current EPG.
With knowledge from an exploratory study of EnergyPlus as a thermal simulation engine and
Revit as basis for space object modelling, two new frameworks for automatic BIM-based BEM
modelling are developed. Namely, a framework between Revit and EnergyPlus for automatic
BIM-based modelling with idealised HVAC systems.
Moreover, a framework between Revit
and Spawn of EnergyPlus (SOEP) for automatic BIM-based modelling with detailed HVAC
systems. Common for both frameworks are the development of an application-specific exchange
format schema between Revit and EnergyPlus, a new microservice for mapping and extraction
of semantic data from Revit, and a new microservice for creation of EnergyPlus IDF files.
Based on actual BIM models of a school in Sorø, Denmark, the frameworks are applied and
validated in a use-case study. Through partial calibration of a semi-automatically generated
BIM-based BEM model of the use-case building, it is found that the framework can be used to
determine the EPG, if sufficient Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE) data is available. Addition-
ally, it is partially exemplified that BIM-based BEM models can be used to identify performance
optimization means in turn reducing excessive use of energy.
It is concluded that application specific BIM-based BEM frameworks can increase the interoper-
ability between Revit and EnergyPlus/SOEP. Additionally, that POE-calibrated auto-generated
BIM-based BEM models do hold the potential to reduce the EPG.

Acknowlegdements
This thesis: "Integration of Thermal Building Simulation Tools and Cloud-Based Building Infor-
mation Models," has been written to fulfill the graduating requirements of the Master of Science
in Architectural Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The thesis was
conducted from January to July 2022 by authors Frederik Seeberg (s173796) and Jon Martin
Gosling-Lewis Tangeraas (s173770). The report presents the combined work of both authors.
Table 1 present a division of work on the following page.
The project was undertaken at the request of Ph.D. candidates Mikki Seidenschnur (Rambøll)
and Ali Kücükavci (COWI A/S), under the overall guidance of associate professor Christian
Anker Hviid. The project focused on developing and testing services for the AEC industry as
part of the development project concerning a cloud-based "Virtual Commissioning" platform.
As we both went into the project with little knowledge in programming and no experience in
software development, the research has been both challenging and time-consuming yet intriguing
and engaging. It has been a steep learning curve, which has equipped us with the fundamentals
of a skill-set, which is not common within our field of study, yet proving increasingly beneficial
as the AEC industry continues its digital transformation.
We want to thank Christian Anker Hviid for his overall guidance on the project. Furthermore,
Peter Yde Jantzen (COWI A/S) for his ongoing interest, knowledge-sharing, and input on the
applicability in the industry. Finally, but not least, Mikki Seidenschnur, Ali Kücükavci, and
Esben Fjerbæk Visby for providing us with the opportunity to partake in this ambitious and
innovative project for their continuous supervision and encouragement, and for showing a sincere
interest in parsing on their knowledge.

1.1
Introduction
The latest report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC, 2022) once again
confirmed that radical changes are required if global warming is to be limited to 1.5°C by 2050.
Combined with a projected population growth of 2.1 billion by 2050 and an expectation of
significant migration to urban areas (PRB, 2021), the buildings and construction sector is facing
a vast responsibility. As of 2021, the building and construction sector is responsible for 37% of
the yearly global energy and energy-related CO2 emissions, according to the latest global status
report for buildings and construction, published by The United Nations Environment Programme
(UNEP, 2021). While 10% of the CO2 emissions stem from the fabrication of building materials
and the construction of new buildings, a total of 27% of the emissions are directly linked to
the operation of residential and non-residential buildings. These tendencies and statistics have
led to increasing demands for the Architecture, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry
to drastically reform such that high-performing, energy-efficient, and resilient buildings can be
developed. Progress made within this field has been, and continues to be, strongly linked to the
concurrent digitalisation of the AEC industry. Particularly the emergence of new technologies
such as Building Information Modelling (BIM) and Building Energy Modelling (BEM) (Dodge
Data & Analytics, 2021).
Commonly identified as the foundation of the digital transformation of the AEC industry, BIM
has wholly revolutionised the means of collaboration and efficiency in the industry by defining
a framework for digitally storing and exchanging information between different systems and
stakeholders in a project (ISO, 2018). Similarly, BEM has provided engineers and architects
with tools to evaluate the energy performance of a built asset, validate code compliance, and
study the impact of energy reduction measures during the design phase (NREL, n.d.). However,
research continues to highlight that the industry is facing challenges under the digitalisation
(Bastos Porsani et al., 2021; Elagiry et al., 2020) and that there are often significant discrepancies
between the predicted performance of a design and the observed performance of the realised
building. This discrepancy is commonly referred to as the Energy Performance Gap (EPG) (de
Wilde, 2014; van Dronkelaar et al., 2016).
The causes for the EPG are manifold, interdependent, and related to the design, construction,
and operation phases. Yet, there seems to be increasing consensus among researchers that unrep-
resentative BEM model assumptions and the absence of post-occupancy performance monitoring
are among the roots of the gap (van Dronkelaar et al., 2016; Zou et al., 2018). With the increasing
presence of sensors and smart meters in the built environment, which monitor and log the perfor-
mance of a building in operation, it seems reasonable to assume that the EPG could be reduced
if such data were to be used as continuous feedback for BEM parameter corrections. Further-
more, that these continuously calibrated BEM models were to be used as the basis for ongoing
Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE) of buildings, in turn enabling accurate and knowledge-based
2
==PAGE:18==
system optimisation of a building in operation (Borrelli et al., 2021; Jradi et al., 2018; Menezes
et al., 2012). Such a framework is highly in line with BIM maturity level 3 (Bew and Richards,
2008), and bridging the EPG might thus be dependent on the current challenges of the digital
transformation of the AEC industry being resolved.
1.2
State of the art
The following section contains a literature review and describes the current state of the art on
the topic of the EPG, POE, BIM and BEM interoperability, and existing BIM-based BEM tools.
1.2.1
The energy performance gap and post-occupancy evaluations
The term EPG is commonly used in literature to describe an omnipresent discrepancy or mis-
match between the expected and actual energy performance of a building (de Wilde, 2014; Zou
et al., 2018). The problem was first identified in a technical research paper by Norford et al.,
published in 1994, in which a difference of a factor of 2.5 was found between the predicted and
consumed energy in an office building constructed in the early 1980s. While the identification
of the issue it describes is thus far from new, the building EPG still has no official definition,
nor has the quantification of its magnitude been standardised. However, based on a report by
IPEEC (2019) in which eight studies were compared, the quantification from the following stud-
ies can be highlighted: Frankel and Turner (2008) analysed 90 buildings with a LEED rating
and found that the majority of the buildings deviated by at least 25% from the projected perfor-
mance, with 25% performing notably worse and 30% performing notably better. van Dronkelaar
et al. (2016) analysed 62 non-residential case studies and found an average discrepancy between
the predicted and observed performance of 34% with a standard deviation of 55%. Sidewalk
Labs (2019) analysed 19 multi-unit residential buildings and found that 13% more energy was
consumed in operation than what was predicted through modelling. In general, the review by
IPEEC derived that an estimate of the performance gap magnitude was highly linked to the
study sample size and that the performance gap appeared more significant for non-residential
buildings than residential buildings. No unambiguous conclusion on the actual performance gap
magnitude was obtained from the review.
Furthermore, in a comprehensive literature review in which 227 publications on the EPG were
compared, Zou et al. (2018) derived that the performance gap could be divided into two kinds;
(a) the gap between the predicted and the measured building energy performance, which is the
focus of most research, and (b) the gap between the target from a specification or standard
and the measured building energy performance. Similarly, van Dronkelaar et al. (2016) proposed
three distinctions of the performance gap based on the underlying type of modelling effort, which
in the light of the proposal by Zou et al., can be assessed as a specification of their first instance
(a). These are; (i) a regulatory performance gap, between the predictions from compliance mod-
3
==PAGE:19==
elling and energy measurements, (ii) a static performance gap, between the predictions from
performance modelling and energy measurements, and (iii) a dynamic performance gap between
predictions from calibrated performance models and energy measurements. Meanwhile, de Wilde
(2014) identified that the causes for the EPG are associated with both the design phase, con-
struction phase and operation phase in the building life-cycle. Menezes et al. (2012) highlighted
design assumptions and modelling tools as the discrepancy factors of the predictive performance,
moreover management and control, occupancy behaviour, and built quality as the discrepancy
factors of the actual performance. Similarly, van Dronkelaar et al. (2016) and IPEEC (2019)
outlined uncertainty in modelling, assumption on occupancy behaviour, and inadequate practice
and feedback during operation as primary factors. In summary, all four publications identified
that unrepresentative BEM model assumptions and the absence of performance monitoring are
among the roots of the EPG. Such a conclusion is in alignment with the findings by Zou et al.
(2018), who classified that "Information" and "Behaviour" were the two major influential factors
on the EPG, being identified by 89.1% and 64.3% of the 227 publications reviewed, respectively.
Concerning the strategies for closing the EPG, Zou et al. (2018) highlighted that most research
points toward the adaption of new technologies and methods as one of the most effective measures.
Moreover, that such developments can be divided into four categories; (i) energy consumption
calculations, (ii) collection and analysis of energy-related data, (iii) simulation and modelling
of occupant behaviour, and (iv) controlling of energy systems. Supporting these findings, van
Dronkelaar et al. (2016) stated that "... insights into the operational inefficiencies of a building
can pinpoint underlying reasons for differences between design estimations and actual use. Sub-
sequently, a calibrated model could reintroduce design assumptions to quantify impacts of any
underlying causes and their effect on energy performance.". Similarly, de Wilde (2014) stated
that "efforts are underway to link prediction and measurement in integrated building informa-
tion systems; while this does not necessarily bridge the gap it at least works towards software
environments where data from both sides can be compared and contrasted.". Concerning these
strategies, several publications highlighted the performance evaluation approach POE as a po-
tential key to achieving insight into actual building performance, in turn enabling developments
within both energy calculations, data analysis, occupancy modelling and energy controlling to
be realised (IPEEC, 2019; Menezes et al., 2012; van Dronkelaar et al., 2016; Zou et al., 2018).
Briefly described, POE is a process that evaluates the performance of a realised and occupied
built asset in operation. Through a structured collection of occupancy and performance data,
POE provides the AEC industry with a framework for generating longitudinal feedback on the
in-use performance of building designs (Hadjri and Crozier, 2009). The methodology and ap-
proaches applied in POE vary relative to their intended application. It might either rely on
an anthropological and sociopsychological approach using questionnaires and interviews or a
technology-based and data-driven approach, using building performance data from sensors, me-
ters and sub-meters (HEFCE, 2006; Menezes et al., 2012). Being a framework that, even on a
4
==PAGE:20==
conceptual level, offers great promise in diminishing the current roots of the EPG, its applica-
tion in research is far from new. To demonstrate the current state of the art, the recent use of
POE to evaluate and reduce the EPG by Jradi et al. (2018) and Borrelli et al. (2021) can be
highlighted. Jradi et al. developed a building energy performance monitoring and evaluation tool
called "ObepME" and applied it to a university building in Denmark. With actual data from
the Building Management System (BMS), the tool continuously calibrated a BEM model of the
building. Energy performance simulation results were compared to actual conditions from mea-
surements, allowing the authors to quantify the dynamic EPG (van Dronkelaar et al., 2016) and
evaluate faulty system operation, in turn providing information for a continuous commissioning
process. Jradi et al. evaluated the dynamic EPG of the building to be -2.85%, -3.47% for heating
and electricity, respectively. Similarly, Borrelli et al. (2021) used POE to determine the optimal
control of the HVAC system in a near-zero energy university building in Belgium. A BEM model
with detailed HVAC control was created in Modelica and calibrated using measurement data
from the actual building. By simulating altered control strategies for the heating system and
comparing it to a baseline equivalent to the actual building performance, Borrelli et al. found
that the primary energy demands of the building could be reduced by as much as 46% while
maintaining or slightly improving the thermal comfort in the building. Though the frameworks
proposed by both authors seem prosperous, they require highly detailed BEM models of both
geometries, internal gains and systems. Being yet a manual or semi-automated and thus labour-
intensive workflows, continuous BEM model creation is currently a significant obstacle to the
in-use performance monitoring becoming a well-established practice in the AEC industry in the
context of performance optimisation.
1.2.2
Interoperability of BIM and BEM tools and data formats
The term BIM has no universal definition, but according to the International Organization for
Standardization, it can be defined as the "use of a shared digital representation of a built asset
to facilitate design, construction and operation processes to form a reliable basis for decisions"
(ISO, 2018). BIM is thus primarily a process, though the term does also describe its asset, a
data-rich and object-oriented digital representation of a building. By defining a framework for
digitally storing and exchanging information between different systems and stakeholders in a
project, the implementation of BIM in the AEC industry aims to achieve a more fluid project
workflow and increase collaboration, efficiency, cost and sustainability (Hamil, 2021). Building
Energy Modelling refers to physics-based simulations of the energy use in a building, computed on
a digital representation of said building (EERE, n.d.). Similar to BIM, BEM thus also describes
both a process and its asset. BEM is a multi-purpose tool commonly used in the AEC industry
to evaluate the building performance of new buildings and retrofit designs, validate compliance
with building codes and green building certification schemes, and evaluate real-time building
control and operation (NREL, n.d.). According to the Building Energy Software Tools directory
maintained by IBPSA, 207 BEM tools are currently officially registered (IBPSA-USA, 2021).
5
==PAGE:21==
The vast majority of these tools are not widely applied in the AEC industry (Elnabawi, 2020),
which primarily uses whole-building energy modelling tools developed to accurately simulate the
thermal indoor climate and energy consumption of a multi-zone model.
As both BIM and BEM models are data-rich and object-oriented digital representations of a built
asset, it is commonly identified that BIM holds the capacity to standardise and automate the
production of BEM models (Gao et al., 2019). Such workflows are typically termed BIM-based
BEM, and they have the potential to both reduce cost, save time, and ensure that the conformity
of geometry and inputs are maintained across all building models in a project (Chen et al., 2018).
While BIM-based BEM workflows already exist (covered in section 1.2.3), common misrepresen-
tations of data in the BIM-based BEM models mean that BIM and BEM often-most remain
segregated (Elnabawi, 2020). Having to manually model and update a digital representation of
a building in more than one digital environment, this segregated framework typically results in
errors, simplifications, and BEM only being applied to evaluate energy performance in advanced
stages of a project (Jin et al., 2019). Consequentially, interoperability between BIM and BEM
models remains a frequently reported issue in the literature concerning the digitalisation of the
AEC industry (Gao et al., 2019).
According to Bastos Porsani et al. (2021) the interoperability issues may come as a result of
each of the three processes which constitute the BIM-based BEM framework; (i) BIM software,
(ii) exchange format schema, and (iii) BEM software. To fully understand the underlying causes
for the issues related to each of these processes, the interoperability issues should be seen in the
light of the current digitalisation of the AEC industry, commonly identified by BIM maturity
levels. Initially developed by Bew and Richards (2008), the BIM maturity level model divides the
evolution of BIM into four levels, each representing progressive milestones in the development
of data formats, tools, and project collaboration within the AEC industry. The progression of
BIM through its four maturity stages is visualized in Figure 1.1 and in short it can be described
as follows (BibLus, 2018; United-BIM Inc., 2019).
BIM Level 0
Maturity level 0 represents the lowest maturity of BIM. At this level, all the production and
exchange of information is paper-based.
Though computer-aided design (CAD) is used, the
information stored in the project models is non-interoperable. In turn, this means that BIM
does not promote collaboration between the different project members.
BIM Level 1
At BIM maturity level 1, CAD transitions from 2D to 3D. The management of model information
is standardised within individual project teams.
The collaboration between different project
stakeholders can be described as partial, as each team creates, manages, and distributes its own
models.
6
==PAGE:22==
Figure 1.1: BIM Maturity Levels. Based on original diagram by Bew and Richards (2008).
BIM Level 2
BIM maturity level 2 is often termed "Collaborative BIM" due to the transition from model
standardisation created and managed by individual teams to streamlined project standardisation
established to ensure that information can be exchanged between all project stakeholders and
systems. Different stakeholders and individual team members are still operating on different 3D
models. However, the introduction of common file types means that BIM can be easily exchanged
and that unified BIM models can be created. The common file types also enable workflows to
be developed, which use the data from the BIM model as an input in third-party applications
and tools.
BIM Level 3
BIM maturity level 3 describes the highest order of collaboration within the AEC industry and is
often referred to as "Open BIM". At this stage, it is envisioned that all the project information is
7
==PAGE:23==
integrated into a cloud-based environment, from which all project stakeholders work on a single
centralised project model, making a common data environment (CDE) available to all project
parties. This enables different stakeholders to verify the effects of design changes on all project
disciplines in real-time. By working cloud-based and with interoperable data formats, services
developed by third parties can easily be integrated into the project platforms with bi-directional
data exchange. Furthermore, it will allow built assets to be managed through their life cycles of
design, construction, operation and maintenance, renovation, and end-of-life.
While the BIM maturity levels assess the collaboration and exchange of information in a project,
they do not account for differences in the amount of information stored in a project. Being an
essential aspect concerning the application of BIM, it is instead handled by industry standards,
describing proprietary conditions of BIM object geometries and properties through the terms
"Level of Geometry" (LOG) and "Level of Information" (LOI), together constituting the "Level
of Development" (LOD) of an object (DiKon, 2021).
According to United-BIM Inc. (2019), the AEC industry is currently operating at, or implement-
ing, BIM maturity level 2. The end goal in the industry is to fully transition to BIM maturity
level 3, but the industry is slow to adapt (United-BIM Inc., 2022). The UK Government has
defined some ’key measures’ for reaching BIM maturity level 3. One of the central points is
to "establish new contractual frameworks with BIM to ensure consistency, avoid confusion and
encourage open, collaborative work" (HM Government, 2015). Interoperability is defined as the
ability of two or more systems or components to exchange and use information (IEEE, 1990),
and the development of frameworks that increase the interoperability between different models
and tools in the AEC industry thus plays an important role in reaching BIM maturity level 3.
Concerning BIM-based BEM interoperability, the development of said frameworks should ideally
resolve the following issues.
Interoperability issues related to the BIM software
As it constitutes the foundation of the BIM-based BEM process, the interoperability issues
related to the BIM software are mainly related to the faulty assignment of BIM objects by the
designer, deficient information in individual BIM objects or the BIM environment as a whole.
Research indicates that these issues are associated with both the lack of standardisation for BIM
model generation and the current insufficiency of BIM software to represent the input parameters
required to generate a complete BEM model in the BIM model (H. Kim and Anderson, 2013;
Leśniak et al., 2021).
Interoperability issues related to the exchange format schema
Exchange format schemes are structured representations of a building model and its properties,
exported to a standardised file format from the BIM application. Multiple file formats have
been developed to exchange semantic data and enable interoperability between different soft-
8
==PAGE:24==
ware environments (Akbarieh, 2018). The most common exchange formats in the BIM-based
BEM process are Industry Foundation Class (IFC) and Green Building Extensible Markup Lan-
guage (gbXML) (Bastos Porsani et al., 2021). While IFC aims to provide a universal basis that
allows for improvement and development of tools, processes and information-sharing in the AEC
industry (Gao et al., 2019), gbXML was developed to exchange relevant information required to
generate BEM models (Osello et al., 2022). Exchange format schemes are the core of the BIM-
based BEM interoperability, and issues related to it mainly concern the ability of the schemes
to extract, store and exchange geometry and properties accurately. While IFC can define a 3D
representation of any building geometry, gbXML defines a 3D building construction as a 2D
surface located in the centre-line of the construction. This causes a difference in the building
geometry between the original BIM model and the exchange format. Furthermore, due to its
two-dimensional surface-based representation of three-dimensional geometry, conversion issues
of BIM models with complex building geometry are common for gbXML, limiting its application
to simplified rectangular geometry (Kamel and Memari, 2019; Maile et al., 2007). Both IFC
and gbXML can define information on material properties, thermal zones, and limited data on
HVAC systems. However, location data is only provided in gbXML file format and not in IFC
file format (Kamel and Memari, 2019).
Interoperability related to the BEM software
Concerning the BEM software, interoperability issues are mainly related to the developed application-
specific frameworks for extracting information from the exchange format schema and associating
BEM objects with the corresponding BIM objects and their properties (Kamel and Memari,
2019). With more than 200 BEM applications (IBPSA-USA, 2021) the lack of standardised
BEM structures and components inevitably makes it difficult to ensure accurate BEM represen-
tation from the current exchange format schemes.
To have full interoperability between BIM and BEM, data loss in all three processes must be
assessed and eliminated. Substantial research has been performed to develop frameworks that
simplify the BIM-based BEM process. The most prominent of these frameworks are presented
and compared in the following section.

any issues of interoperability from this test but claimed that the tool should be able to translate
building geometry, constructions, thermal zones and internal loads.

simplification process of the BIM model to complete the data transfer from BIM to BEM, and

reviewed in this section is the ease of use of the different workflows, which also can be considered
an essential part of the BIM-BEM interoperability.

2.1
Introduction to toolchain development
To contribute to the AEC industry transitioning towards BIM maturity level 3, enabling the
broad implementation of continuous commissioning of buildings, a new framework for BIM-
based BEM model creation is developed. The framework aims to improve the interoperability
between Revit as the BIM environment and EnergyPlus/Modelica as the BEM environment.
The framework includes the development of an application-specific data structure utilized as
the exchange format schema between Revit and EnergyPlus.
Furthermore, the development
of two services which (i) extracts and serializes the spatial and semantic data from a Revit
architecture model to the developed exchange format schema and (ii) converts the serialized BIM
data from Revit into an EnergyPlus IDF file. Developed for the Virtual Commissioning platform
(described in Section 1.3.1) and building on its microservice architecture, the new services are
to be integrated with the existing platform services. Namely, the services which (i) extracts and
serializes the semantic data from a BIM system model to a Flow System Class (FSC) (Kukkonen
et al., 2022; Seidenschnur et al., n.d.), and (ii) converts the serialized BIM data from the FSC
hierarchy into a Modelica model (Fjerbæk, 2021). The development of the new services and their
integration with the existing services is intended to result in two new microservice toolchains,
constituting the framework for automatic BIM-based BEM model creation.

Toolchain 1 represents a framework for BIM-based BEM model creation of BEM models with
idealized HVAC systems. The system architecture of Toolchain 1 is visualized in Figure 2.1.

2.2.1
Introduction to EnergyPlus
EnergyPlus is a whole-building energy analysis and thermal load simulation program developed
and maintained by the U.S Department of Energy since 1997 (U.S Department of Energy, 2021d).
It offers numerous capabilities, of which the most noteworthy are; simultaneous and integrated
simulations of thermal zone conditions and HVAC system response, combined heat and mass
transfer models, thermal comfort models, and heat balance-based calculations of thermal loads
(U.S. Department of Energy, 2021c). EnergyPlus is mainly intended as a simulation engine
which uses American Standard Code for Information Interchange (ASCII) text as its primary
input and output. It is based on a modular and object-oriented structure. It uses an Input Data
Dictionary (IDD) file to specify what the ASCII IDF file can contain furthermore what data each
object requires (U.S Department of Energy, 2021d). The modular structure divides EnergyPlus
inputs into groups, groups into objects, and objects into fields. Listing 2.1 shows an example
of the data structure of an IDF input. In the example, "Simulation Parameters" is the group,
"Building" is the object, and "Name", "North Axis", etc. are the fields.
EnergyPlus does not contain or reference external libraries for materials, schedules etc. Except
for weather and climatic data, all inputs are specified in the IDF file.

2.2.3
Representations of location and weather conditions
In EnergyPlus, a given site’s location and weather conditions are specified in the so-called En-
ergyPlus Weather (EPW) file. An EPW file is an ASCII text file containing location identifiers
and weather data in hourly or sub-hourly time steps (U.S Department of Energy, 2021d). The
location identifiers describe the latitude, longitude, elevation, time zone, peak design conditions,
daylight saving period, typical and extreme periods, ground temperatures and holidays. The
weather data contains temperatures, solar radiation, relative humidity, atmospheric pressure,
illuminance, wind direction, wind speed, sky cover, and current weather conditions (U.S Depart-
ment of Energy, 2021a).
In Revit, the project location can be specified from a default list of cities or by entering its latitu-
dinal and longitudinal coordinates. Based on the weather station closest to the chosen location,
weather data is extracted from an external database maintained by the World Meteorological
Organization (WMO) (Autodesk Inc., 2020[b]). The extracted weather data is sparse and not
fit to be used as input in EnergyPlus or any other BEM application.
2.2.4
Representations of building geometry and thermal zones
In EnergyPlus, building geometry is represented by two-dimensional surfaces. Each surface is
defined from various vertices that describe its corners. Based on the order in which the vertices
are entered into EnergyPlus, the orientation of the surface is determined, and its interior and
exterior side is defined. By default, EnergyPlus uses a right-hand coordinate system, applies a
counterclockwise vertex entry direction, and defines the starting vertex position as the upper left
corner of the surface (U.S. Department of Energy, 2021c). The latter if observed parallel to the
surface normal at the exterior side of the considered surface.
Geometry is allocated to three categories in EnergyPlus; building surfaces, fenestration surfaces
and shading surfaces. Building surfaces constitute all primary geometry and can be subdivided
into the following types based on their orientation and heat transfer properties; external and
interzone walls, foundations and interzone floors, and roofs and interzone ceilings. Fenestration
surfaces represent subsurfaces, more specifically exterior or interior doors and windows. Finally,
shading surfaces describe the geometry of both attached and detached static shading elements,
which can be represented at zone, building or site level.

and proprietorial objects which composes a building through so-called thermal zones. Thermal
zones are box-representation of one or more building spaces which share similar temperature
conditions (U.S. Department of Energy, 2021c).
Figure 2.4: EnergyPlus Envelope Hierarchy (U.S. Department of Energy, 2021c).
In Energylus, thermal zones are geometrically formed by a minimum of six building surfaces
that constitute an enclosed volume. As each surface is related to a specific zone, the geometry
of interzone objects (walls, ceilings/floors, windows or doors) must be represented twice to form
two enclosed volumes and describe the surface outside boundary condition for the heat transfer
calculations (U.S Department of Energy, 2021e). Figure 2.5 visualizes the surface-based geometry
representation in EnergyPlus and highlights the thermal zones and interzone surfaces.

2.2.5
Representations of materials and constructions
In EnergyPlus, the physical properties of building elements are represented through construc-
tions, which are compositions of materials describing the thermal characteristics of individual
building element layers. Both constructions and materials may either represent opaque, trans-
parent or air-based elements. The required inputs vary for each element type. Opaque materials
describe an object’s thermal characteristics at a detailed level, covering its conductivity, specific
heat, absorptance (thermal, solar and visible), density and thickness. Transparent materials de-
scribe an object’s thermal and visible properties, covering its transmittance (thermal and visible)
and solar heat gain coefficient. Finally, air-based materials are simplified objects, representing
the thermal resistance of, e.g. air gap layers (U.S Department of Energy, 2021e). As visualized
in Figure 2.4, each building surface must be associated with exactly one construction object,
which contains up to ten references to material objects. In a predefined order, these associated
material objects are specified from outermost to innermost. For interzone surfaces, this implies
that the order of materials in the constructions is reversed for each surface representation, as the
outer material of the surface in one zone equals the inner material of the identical surface in the
adjacent zone (U.S Department of Energy, 2021e). Figure 2.8 shows the difference between the
constructions of an inter-zone surface in each zone it encloses.
Figure 2.8: Order of construction layers for an interzone surface between fictional zones A and B.
In Revit, certain construction elements such as walls, floors and roofs are composed of structural
assemblies, which are divided into layers with associated thicknesses and materials, similar to
the principles applied in EnergyPlus. Revit has an extensive database of predefined materials
that can be assigned to elements, but it also allows the user to import or define new materials.
Revit materials are described through graphical, appearance, thermal and physical information
(Autodesk Inc., 2020[a]). The thermal information of constructions can either be retrieved for the
construction as a whole or individual material layers. The thermal properties of the individual
materials contain; thermal conductivity, specific heat, density, emissivity, permeability, porosity,
reflectivity, and electrical resistivity. The thermal properties of the whole construction include
the heat transfer coefficient, thermal resistance, thermal mass, absorptance and roughness.

==PAGE:41==
2.2.6
Representations of space loads
In EnergyPlus, space loads or internal gains are categorized into heat gains and contaminant
gains, which influence a zone’s conditions. While heat gains are composed of loads from people,
lights and equipment, contaminant gains mainly comprise CO2 production from people (U.S.
Department of Energy, 2021c). Heat gains and contaminant gains are represented through a
combination of peak loads, describing the maximum heat or CO2 production rate. Moreover, a
variety of schedules which varies the peak loads relative to the occupancy and activities in the
zone at a given time (U.S Department of Energy, 2021e).
In Revit, loads can be defined as electrical lighting, electrical power (representing equipment),
or people. The electrical loads can be specified as density or accumulated loads for the entire
space. Similarly, the people load can be specified as the space’s total occupancy or area per
person. Revit does not support the specification of the background CO2 concentration or the
CO2 production rates from occupants. Each space load can be defined directly in the space class
or by selecting a space type with default values from the Revit library.
2.2.7
Representations of HVAC systems
In EnergyPlus, HVAC systems are represented as either idealized or detailed models. Because of
the existing framework for detailed HVAC system modelling in Modelica, detailed HVAC system
representations in EnergyPlus are not explored. Regarding the idealized system representation,
EnergyPlus applies an air load system that can operate at finite or infinite heating and cooling
capacities. The ideal air load system mixes the exhaust air with outdoor air at 100 percent
efficiency to supply the zone with the conditions specified by the heating and cooling set-point
of a zone thermostat (U.S Department of Energy, 2021e).
In Revit, an MEP model’s HVAC systems can be detailed with components that contain infor-
mation about dimensions, constraints, mechanical properties, and component-specific properties.
These components are connected and are allocated to specific zones. Furthermore, spaces can
store information about supply and return airflow. The LOI for Revit MEP components relies
on the family-specific parameters, which often makes the translation of BIM HVAC components
to BEM HVAC object difficult due to non-standardized elements.
2.2.8
Representations of operating strategies and schedules
In EnergyPlus, schedules are essential for accurate modelling of particularly zone occupancy
and occupant behaviour, electric lighting and plug loads, and systems operation. Being a key
modelling aspect, EnergyPlus offers several frameworks for schedule specification. The simplest
and most common framework is the compact schedule, which uses the notions "Through", "For",
and "Until" to specify days, the day types, and the hours of the days for which an assigned
schedule value is valid (U.S. Department of Energy, 2021c).
26
==PAGE:42==
In Revit, operating schedules for occupancy, lighting, and power can be specified for each space
type, along with information such as set points for heating and cooling, humidification, and
dehumidification. Additional schedule types required in BEM are not currently supported.
2.2.9
Representations of simulation parameters and output parameters
In EnergyPlus, the simulation parameters control how the simulation is conducted, whereas the
output parameters determine the simulation results and their format. The simulation parameters
specify global simulation settings such as the orientation of the building, the time step of heat
transfer and load calculations, the details for the solar and shadowing calculations, the simulation
algorithms etc. The output parameters specify the output formatting and simulation results
through "Output Variables". Each output variable is associated with a single EnergyPlus object
and describes an individual simulation result type (U.S Department of Energy, 2021e).
In Revit, BEM application-specific simulation or output parameters are not currently supported.
2.3
Development of Revit and EnergyPlus exchange format schema
Building on the existing hierarchies in EnergyPlus and the knowledge of the BEM parameter
representation in Revit, this section describes the development of an application-specific exchange
format schema for BIM-based BEM model creation in Revit and EnergyPlus.
2.3.1
Proposed data structure
The data structure of the proposed exchange format schema, referred to as the Space Class Hi-
erarchy (SCH), is visualized in Figure 2.9. The data structure utilizes a top-down approach to
arrange the modular object-oriented BIM/BEM data elements into a non-linear tree structure.
The tree structure consists of six major branches, organized in the following order; Simulation
Parameters, Output Parameters, Schedules, Materials, Constructions and Site. The branch and
subbranch hierarchies employed in the data structure deliberately represent an adaptation of the
hierarchies in EnergyPlus. This approach ensures that each object in the data structure can
inherit required field data from an object in the same branch or an upstream branch. This war-
rants that the network of associated input references between different objects can be created,
both when Revit objects are serialized to the SCH and when they are instantiated in Energy-
Plus. Special consideration was given to multi-reference objects: materials, constructions and
schedules. By separating these objects from the Site subbranches, the number of objects that
must be instantiated in EnergyPlus is reduced, as a material can be referenced in more than one
construction, and a construction or schedule can be referenced in more than one surface or zone.

Development of the BIM to Space Class Hierarchy parser
This section describes the development of a microservice that transfers BIM object data from

language C# to access the Revit API.
2.4.1
System architecture
Figure 2.13 displays the system architecture of the BIM parser. The diagram divides the system

shows the exchange of data files.

The BIM data parser is developed as a Revit plug-in that can retrieve all BEM relevant infor-
mation from the Revit project and parse it to a serialized JSON format. The main script for
the Revit plug-in relies on four main components to obtain information from a Revit model,
namely the material mapper, construction mapper, site mapper, and the JSON parser. The task
of the mappers is to retrieve BIM object information for the defined classes in the C# script for
the Revit plug-in. The classes can be described as templates for objects with a defined set of
parameters. The presented SCH dictates the hierarchy of the classes, and their parameters and
relations can be seen in Appendix B. When the BIM object information has been mapped, the
JSON parser transfers the information into a JSON format that can be sent to the platform.

2.7
Limitations and Assumptions related to the toolchains
At the current stage of development, the following limitations and assumptions are related to
services which constitute the toolchains:
• The conversion of BIM geometry from 3D to 2D in the Revit EAM is troublesome and inac-
curate for large models and non-convex geometry. This means that except for simple models,
the geometry of individual surfaces must often be processed before it is parsed to EnergyPlus.
• The algorithm that sorts the surface vertices of an EAM surface into a counterclockwise order is
limited to simple geometry. This means that the order of vertices must be manually processed
for complex surfaces before they are parsed to EnergyPlus.
• For complex geometry, the algorithm that finds the construction associated with an EAM
surface is troubled, as the EAM surface is detected in more than one bounding box. This
means that for complex surfaces, more than one construction is defined.
The inaccurate
construction assignment must be manually deleted from the object model before parsing it to
EnergyPlus.
• The BIM parser is not yet capable of identifying vertical or horizontal ground surfaces. For
surfaces which face the ground, the outside boundary condition must be manually altered from
"Outdoors" to "Ground".
• The BIM parser is not yet capable of identifying vertical or horizontal surfaces as shading
elements. Instead, the parser relies on creating masses to represent shading elements. These
masses must be manually created in the Revit model. All masses are defined as Building
shading surfaces in the object model. Suppose the shading element is a zone shading element
or a site shading element. In that case, the object type must be manually altered in the object
model before parsing it to EnergyPlus.
• Though operating conditions, loads and schedules are to some extent available in Revit, a
mapping process for these data are not yet available in the BIM parser. This implies that
information regarding internal gains from occupants, equipment and lights, operating schedules
and heating and cooling set-points must be manually inserted in the object model for each
zone in the building.
As indicated, the majority of the current shortcomings of the toolchain, namely the BIM parser,
are related to geometry. These shortcomings can be solved by altering the mappers so that the
geometry conversion from the Revit EAM is completely avoided.

energy consumption through meters and submeters. Meters are installed separately for the North
building and the South building, each monitoring (i) the heating energy for radiators, (ii) the
heating energy for the AHU heating coil, and (iii) the electricity for fans, lighting and plug-loads,
at each floor.
The available BMS logs cover the period from the 4th of June 2020 to the 15th of September 2021.
This implies, that all the POE data from the period is either heavily or partially influenced by the
Covid-19 pandemic and the continuous governmental restrictions that public schools endured. To
minimise the influence of Covid-19, the time periods in which the students were home-schooled
were mapped. While it was intended to perform the case study during a month with a high
heating demand, e.g. January, it was assessed that the most suitable month, in which the school
was occupied and data was available, was September 2020.

3.5
Creation of custom schedules representing actual building operation
As highlighted in Section 1.2.1, unrepresentative BEM model assumptions concerning occupancy
and occupant behaviour are commonly identified as a core root of the EPG. Purely assumption-
based BEM modelling principles are applied in compliance modelling and predictive building
analysis in the design phase. However, due to the access to building operation data, the uncer-
tainty regarding these assumptions can be partially or fully avoided in diagnostic analysis, such
as POE. Based on the available sensor data and project documentation, it was attempted to
create dynamic schedules of occupancy, lighting, and equipment loads.

3.6
Creation of custom EPW file of actual weather conditions
In dynamic building energy simulations, applying accurate weather conditions is essential to
ensure that the simulation results are reliable. Consequentially, the weather conditions can be
assessed as boundary conditions of equal importance to the setpoints of the systems described in
Section 3.3. Generally, the meteorological data in the weather files, including EPW files, are so-
called Typical Meteorological Year (TMY) files. These files contain annual data sets representing
typical meteorological conditions for a specific location over an extended period, e.g. ten years.
Even though the TMY format provides a solid basis for predictive analysis, since it accounts for
daily and seasonal variations and represents yearly average climatic conditions, it is not ideal for
diagnostic analysis of previous events since it does not correctly capture meteorological peaks.
Instead, meteorological data representing the actual weather conditions at the exact location and
time of the analysis should be used in diagnostic analysis, such as POEs.
While local weather data can be obtained from meteorological stations, the data must be man-
ually converted into the file format of request. For this case study, a customised EPW file was
manually generated from the data available through the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI)
(DMI, n.d.) and the DTU Climate Station (DTU, n.d.).
Though the original intent was to
acquire the complete data set from the Flakkebjerg DMI weather station, the data extraction
process from DMI was troublesome. Instead, a combination of data from the weather station at
Flakkebjerg and Lyngby was utilised. For each field in the EPW file format, Table 3.7 describes
how the field data was acquired and from which weather station it originated.

The ventilation system simulation shows an imbalance in supply and return airflow. While the

during the heating season. To avoid an unbalanced system, the return dampener could either
be adjusted to reduce the return airflow, or changed to a dampener that can adjust the opening
based on the supply airflow.

Investigation of fluctuating supply air temperature
In instances where the system does not perform as expected, it could be interesting to investigate
how the system control is behaving. A temperature sensor monitoring the ventilation supply air
temperature is located in the ducts just after the heating coil controlled by the heating coil
valve. Figure 3.33 displays the results for the supply air temperature compared with the set
point temperature over 8 days.
Figure 3.33: Simulated supply air temperature and heating coil supply temperature.
The results show that the supply air temperature is usually around 21◦C, but it fluctuates a
considerable amount during occupancy hours, sometimes reaching temperatures above 35◦C.
The system control is investigated over one day to understand why high supply air temperatures
occur. Figure 3.34 displays the supply air temperature compared with the opening of the heating
coil valve, and the opening of the 4 ventilation dampeners associated with the 4 zones in the
model. The opening of the dampeners is included to show when the ventilation supplies air to
the zones.
The results show that the temperature spikes correlate with the valve opening of the heating coil,
where each spike occurs when the valve is closing. The heating coil valve is always open when
the ventilation dampeners are open and share many of the same tendencies regarding when they
open and close. This means that there is a rise in supply air temperature when the dampeners
are closed, indicating that the warm air around 35◦C occurs because there is no airflow. Without
any airflow, the air around the heating coil will heat up for longer than when there is airflow.
The peaks are therefore not representative of the actual supply air temperature. An alternative
solution to avoid this affecting the system control could be to relocate the temperature sensor in
the return air ducts instead and thus remove the possibility of reaching such high temperatures.

Determination of the regulatory energy performance gap
The regulatory EPG, describing the discrepancy between the expected energy requirement from
compliance modelling and the actual energy consumed in operation, was determined from the
energy framework (BE10) and the building energy meters.
Based on the energy compliance
documentation, it was identified that the North part of Frederiksberg Skole was expected to
consume a total of 2627 kWh (0.71 kWh/m2) in heating energy during September. The doc-
umentation did not specify the allocated energy between the AHU and the dedicated heating
system. Similarly, based on the logged meter data of heating energy, it was identified that the
North part of Frederiksberg Skole actually required a total of 11299 kWh (3.09 kWh/m2) in
September 2020. Of the total 11299 kWh, it was found that 7289 kWh (65%) was allocated to
the dedicated heating system, while the AHU heating coil used the remaining 4010 kWh (35%)
to heat the supply air. As visualised in Figure 3.37, the actual heating energy thus exceeded the
expected heating energy by a factor of 4.35, equivalent to a regulatory EPG of +335%.

4.1
Assessment of thesis aims
This thesis aimed to improve the interoperability of BIM-based BEM modelling and to provide a
proof of concept that automatically generated BIM-based BEM models hold the potential to re-
duce the current EPG. The following will assess the fulfilment of the aims from the developments
presented in Chapter 2 and the studies presented in Chapter 3.
Concerning the first sub-aim, to improve the interoperability of BIM-based BEM modelling,
the main objectives were to perform an exploratory study of EnergyPlus and Revit and use that
knowledge to develop a new framework for BIM-based BEM modelling. Applying an application-
specific approach intended to minimise the interoperability issues related to BIM software (Revit),
exchange format schema, and BEM software (EnergyPlus). Based on the application study on
Frederiksberg Skole, the following can be highlighted concerning the improvement of BIM-based
BEM interoperability by the new framework.
Improvement of interoperability issues related to the BIM software
The interoperability related to BIM software remains unchanged as the new workflows do not
provide alternative means of specifying BEM data in BIM models.
Improvement of interoperability issues related to the exchange format schema
The development of an application-specific exchange format schema constitutes the core of the
BIM-based BEM interoperability improvements of the new framework.
Developed solely for
EnergyPlus and based on its existing data hierarchies, the application-specific exchange format
ensures that data from the BIM models are always structured so that each relevant BIM object
in Revit can easily be converted to the corresponding BEM object in EnergyPlus.
Improvement of interoperability issues related to the BEM software
The interoperability issues related to the BEM software are neglected, as faulty data translation
is avoided due to the uniformity of the hierarchies in EnergyPlus and the new exchange format.
In Table 4.1, the interoperability development level of Toolchain 1 and Toolchain 2 is compared to

4.2
Discussion
As indicated by the studies in this thesis, it is indisputable that BIM-based BEM promises great
potential. Both regarding the direct effects of automated energy model creation, as reduction of
modelling errors and time, but equally in making highly detailed energy simulations applicable
outside academia.
As presented in Section 3.7.2, the developed BIM-based BEM framework
successfully generated a coupled whole-building energy model in SOEP, with semantic data orig-
87
==PAGE:103==
inating from Revit. Despite the process not being fully automated, given the current deficiencies
of the framework, this can be considered a significant achievement. Not only because similar ac-
complishments have not been documented in published research but also because the framework
succeeded in performing a task that arguably could not have been performed manually. While
the latter statement may be subject to a comprehensive discussion, the visualisation from the
Dymola GUI in Figure 3.27, may serve as a partial documentation of such claim. As illustrated
in Figure 3.27, the graphical representation of the connection between approximately 6000 sys-
tem components distributed across 84 zones is unfathomable. Having to manually model merely
a fraction of this, the required modelling time would make such a process unprofitable in the
AEC industry. While fully functional BIM-based BEM frameworks could allow complex BEM
models to be frequently applied, significant limitations concerning such processes must first be
addressed.
4.2.1
Discussion of interoperability
As presented in Chapter 1, interoperability issues are identified at the core of BIM-based BEM
obstacles. Non-interoperable models may originate from both BIM software, the exchange format
schema and the BEM software, but the developments performed in this thesis have indicated
that the BIM software is the limiting factor. The exploratory study of EnergyPlus and Revit
highlighted that while both BIM and BEM models are digital representations of built assets, they
differ significantly regarding data representation. Most obvious is the difference in geometry, but
what is more essential is that Revit is currently not equipped to store basic properties required in
EnergyPlus. Noticeable are the existing but idle location and weather specification functionality,
the sparse loads and schedule functionality, and the non-existing ability to specify BEM settings
and request simulation outputs. Given that the exploratory study only considered Revit as the
BIM environment, it can not be deduced whether this applies to BIM applications in general.
Regardless, suppose BIM is truly to be used as a reliable basis for energy modelling. In that
case, the BIM developers are responsible for including the essential BEM parameters in their
BIM applications, even if it does not add additional value to the current employment. Before
this has been achieved, it may be argued that BIM and BEM may not become interoperable.
The preparation of the architecture and systems models of Frederiksberg Skole, presented in Sec-
tion 3.4, highlighted a similar but equally important challenge related to BIM models. Despite
being final project models, neither the architecture nor the system model was equipped to be used
as input in the SCH and FSC parsers. Both BIM models had to be enriched with non-specified
properties for nearly all relevant space and systems objects. Additionally, the LOG of existing
objects had to be lowered in the architecture model, and new objects had to be introduced in
the systems model. This exemplifies that BIM-related interoperability issues are not restricted
solely to the capability of BIM tools to store information, nor the lack of information in different
project phases, but rather actively not including information in BIM models. Moreover, it ex-
emplifies that different BIM model elements require significantly different modelling approaches.
88
==PAGE:104==
The problems with geometry representation could be seen as an issue to be solved by parsers.
However, it could also be assessed as an indication of an industry-wide paradox, where one BIM
model may not serve all its required purposes. This claim contradicts the fundamental strength
of BIM-based BEM; that modelling is universally conducted in one environment. It could there-
fore be argued that BIM-based BEM is not fully applicable before the AEC industry reaches
BIM maturity level 3 and transitions to CDEs. While the industry continues to operate at BIM
maturity level 2, standardisation of BIM modelling for BIM-based BEM could be implemented
to assure that BIM models were to their fullest potential containing the data required in BEM
models. This would undoubtedly also accelerate the implementation of BIM-based BEM across
the industry.
In addition to the interoperability issues related to the BIM software, this thesis found that by
applying an application-specific approach to BIM-based BEM framework development, both the
interoperability related to the exchange format schema and the BEM software can be partially or
entirely eradicated. As outlined in Section 4.1, the strength of the developed BIM-based BEM
framework lies in the custom exchange format schemes. By introducing SCH for space mod-
elling and FSC for system modelling, it was highlighted that both Toolchain 1, but particularly
Toolchain 2, outperforms the existing BIM-based BEM frameworks. Given that this assessment
was performed on a non-exhaustive list of the relevant BIM-based BEM parameters, as pre-
sented in Table 4.1, the comparison of the interoperability development levels may only serve
as an indicative overview of interoperability improvements. Regardless, given that the existing
frameworks, indicatively, failed to achieve the same level of interoperability by mainly relying on
the open schemes as gbXML and IFC, it could be argued that future efforts in BIM-based BEM
development should focus on application-specific schemes. As this thesis only explored interop-
erability between Revit and EnergyPlus/Modelica, it can not be assessed if application-specific
exchange formats are equally successful in BIM-based BEM workflows with other applications.
However, given the similarities between different BIM applications and BEM applications, it may
be suggested that the findings in this thesis could be generally representative.
4.2.2
Discussion of toolchain improvements
Despite successfully developing toolchains that semi-automatically generate and simulate either
a BEM model with idealised systems in EnergyPlus or a coupled BEM model with detailed
representation of systems in SOEP, the framework is far from a finished product. Concerning
the parser limitations presented in Section 2.7, the following improvements may be considered
to increase the framework’s applicability and make it fully automatic.
Improvements of the Revit parser
The main problem regarding the Revit parser is that the current method of generating a simplified
geometry is sufficient. Because the Revit parser utilises the EAM for the building geometry, it is
89
==PAGE:105==
limited to how the Revit EAM can generate a simplified geometric representation of a model. As
observed in the application chapter, the EAM had difficulties generating an accurate geometric
representation of surfaces in the model of Frederiksberg Skole, causing an extensive manual
process to fix it.
This could be avoided by developing a mapper to obtain the geometry of
spaces and surfaces directly from the Revit model itself. Naturally, this is not a simple task
to solve, which is reflected by the fact that the transfer of geometry remains one of the most
prominent factors for the current limitations in BIM-based BEM interoperability.
However,
multiple possible approaches could achieve an accurate geometric representation of a BIM model
in BEM. A promising method could be to utilise the boundary segments of spaces to retrieve
the geometry of the space footprint. The footprint edges and the level height could then be used
to define the remaining surfaces enclosing the space. Another approach could be to retrieve the
geometry of the surfaces in the space geometry element. A limiting factor of this method is that
the geometry of spaces in Revit only stretches to the inside of the surrounding constructions,
causing adjacent spaces to be separated by a distance of the wall, floor, or ceiling thickness.
Avoiding the utilisation of the EAM in the parser would also benefit the construction association
to surfaces. EAM 2D surfaces are generated without any link to the 3D objects they represent,
making it challenging to find the associated construction. The current parser finds most associ-
ated constructions with a location filter but is troubled by floor surfaces with complex geometry,
sometimes linking multiple constructions to one surface. By avoiding using EAM to convert 3D
geometry to 2D surfaces, the geometry and the construction would be attainable from a single
object.
Developing a new geometry mapper could also potentially remove the need for a separate function
to sort the order of surface vertices. As described in Section 2.2.4, EnergyPlus requires that the
surface vertices are sorted in a counterclockwise order to ensure correct surface direction and
surface geometry. The current geometry mapper must remove specific vertices to avoid multiple
vertices being located in the same coordinate.
Because of this, the order of the remaining
vertices is no longer adequately sorted and would cause the geometry in EnergyPlus to be falsely
represented. The current vertex sorting function can sort the vertices of simple geometric surfaces
but has difficulties when the surfaces become more complex. However, a new method of obtaining
surface geometry could work around this by avoiding vertex repetition.
The main problem discovered during the development of the shading mappers was that it could
be challenging to recognise Revit objects representing shading elements, as they can be mod-
elled in multiple ways. The current mappers utilise conceptual mass geometry as site shading
elements and walls without any space association as building shading elements. However, these
methods are insufficient as there can be many variations in using such elements. Ideally, shading
elements would have the possibility to be assigned to a type that could be used for component
identification. This could be solved similarly to the FSC object model, where a set of assets can
90
==PAGE:106==
be implemented to describe the component type and properties. A mapper would then be able
to filter out the shading elements based on their component type and then map the geometry
and shading properties.
As highlighted by Figure 2.10 and Figure 2.11, there is still a significant amount of input fields
that optimally should derive from Revit.
New mappers should be developed to retrieve all
BEM-relevant data available in Revit to improve the BIM-based BEM interoperability.
Improvements of the Space Class Hierarchy and the EnergyPlus parser
While improvements to the SCH exchange format schema and the EnergyPlus parser could be
performed independently, a different approach would be to rethink the requirement of the En-
ergyPlus parser entirely. EnergyPlus is transitioning from the IDF format to a custom data
schema, epJSON, which could replace the SCH. As the data structure of the SCH is in resem-
blance to the data structure of epJSON, minor changes to the BIM mappers would be required.
Using epJSON, the EnergyPlus parser could be avoided entirely, simplifying the BIM-based BEM
framework significantly. This may, however, only be suggested for BIM-based BEM frameworks
with EnergyPlus as the BEM environment.
4.2.3
Discussion of BIM-based BEM for reduction of the energy performance gap
By imitating the steps required in an actual virtual commissioning process with POE data,
Chapter 3 exemplified the potential of BIM-based BEM for performance benchmarking and
optimisation, but more so its adherent uncertainties. Concerning the specific use-case applied in
this study, said uncertainties were comprehensive, affecting each step in preparing the BIM-based
BEM modelling and POE- calibration. Thus inevitably also, the simulation results.
Regardless of the uncertainties, the regulatory EPG of the considered building was successfully
identified from the expected and the measured heating consumption. While it may be argued that
the regulatory EPG can only be used as a rough performance benchmark, it clearly showed its
potential value in the considered application, indicating that the building was using in the region
of 4.5 times more energy than expected. Compared to the magnitudes of the gaps highlighted in
Section 1.2.1, the performance of the considered use-case is the exception rather than the norm.
The regulatory EPG would likely be remarkably lower if applied to a different building. In this
case, it must be stressed that the significant differences in boundary conditions between the
actual operation and the energy frame calculation could, in extreme cases, result in regulatory
EPGs of, e.g. +25% or +50%, despite the building operating as expected. Bench-marking of
building performance should thus often more be based on the more detailed dynamic EPG.
Concerning the dynamic EPG of the use-case building, the studies were limited by the pre-
viously mentioned uncertainties.
Namely, (i) the uncertainty of performing simulations with
91
==PAGE:107==
simplified representations of systems in EnergyPlus rather than SOEP, and (ii) the uncertain-
ties of the boundary conditions describing system control, weather conditions, and the building
operation. As presented in Chapter 3, the former was instated given that the successfully gen-
erated whole-building BEM representation of the use-case building could not be simulated in
SOEP due to convergence errors. The latter originated from the following shortcomings. Due
to insufficient documentation, the system control was based on a screenshot of the BMS system
taken in February 2022, though the dynamic EPG was determined for September 2020. Due
to problems retrieving weather data from DMI, the customised EPW file was partially based
on measurements from Lyngby, 80 km North-East of the considered site in Sorø. Furthermore,
as the building did not have measurements of zone occupancy and a more rigorous division of
sub-meters differentiating between lighting and plug-loads, the heat and contaminant loads were
determined based on dynamic calculations and assumptions.
The non-calibrated dynamic BEM model simulation showed a large discrepancy between sim-
ulations and measurements. Despite not depicting the dynamic EPG, the simulation results
did support the indication by the regulatory EPG assessment; that the building was operating
under significantly different conditions to what was assumed in the energy frame calculations.
Without additional POE data to indicate which boundary condition parameters were erroneous,
a detailed model calibration could not be pursued, meaning that the actual dynamic EPG was
unattainable. However, from simulations with varied operating conditions, namely, load inten-
sity and natural ventilation, it was identified that the building was likely operating with open
windows. Using the varied simulations as partial model-calibration, it was identified that the
dynamic EPG of the considered building was likely within the range of ± 30%. It may be argued
that with additional sensors measuring the, e.g. window opening and actual zone occupancy, the
dynamic EPG could be more accurately identified. Thus, that POE calibrated BIM-based BEM
models could be used to benchmark actual building performance.
When such benchmark models have been established, virtual commissioning of the building
may be used to detect faulty system control and identify optimisations, in turn reducing the
EPG. However, due to the simplified representation of the system control and the influence
of natural ventilation on the dynamics of the HVAC systems, such detailed system analyses
were not pursued in the whole-building simulation in EnergyPlus. Instead, the optimisation
principles were exemplified by analysing various window openings. This highlighted that the
POE-calibrated BIM-based BEM model could be used to reduce the energy requirement while
also assuring that the indoor climate is acceptable.
It did thus also indicate that building
performance optimisation is not limited to the system operation.
Concerning these findings, it must be highlighted that the assessments of the EPG were only
performed for heating. At the same time, it should ideally also be performed for performance
indicators such as electricity for fans, electricity for pumps, electricity for lighting etc. Similarly,
92
==PAGE:108==
rather than considering the total heating energy, the heating energy consumed by room units
should be assessed separately from the heating energy consumed by the AHU heating coil.
In light of the experience gained from the use-case applications, it is suggested that consideration
should be made when applying the BIM-based BEM framework for POE and VC to actively
decide which toolchain will be optimal for the considered application. The strengths of SOEP
are highlighted in its ability to perform detailed simulations of complex system controls, which
would not be possible in, e.g. EnergyPlus. However, Modelica can be challenging to navigate
and requires a certain level of experience for proper utilisation. BIM-based BEM would ideally
simplify this process, but until the interoperability issues discussed in Section 4.2.1 are resolved,
typical BEM applications could be used for less detailed POE-based VC for building optimisation.

Contributions
Even though this report has uncovered the current limitations of the developed BIM-based BEM
framework, it is also important to highlight the contributions that have been achieved from the
research and development in this project. These contributions are listed below.
• Developed an application-specific exchange format schema which improves the interoper-
ability between Revit as the BIM environment and EnergyPlus as the BEM environment.
• Developed a toolchain that can transfer objects and properties from a BIM model in Revit

• Developed a toolchain that can transfer objects and properties from BIM models in Revit
to a CDE and from the CDE to coupled BEM model in SOEP.
• Exemplified that BEM representation of complex buildings and services can be automati-

• Exemplified that POE calibrated BIM-based BEM models holds the potential to identify
the dynamic EPG.
• Exemplified that POE calibrated BIM-based BEM models holds the potential to be used
as the basis for virtual commissioning of buildings in operation.
• Performed developments and studies that are to be used in a journal paper.

4.4
Suggestions for future work
The following suggestions for future work can be highlighted from the research and development
presented in this thesis.
• Develop a new method of obtaining a simplified geometric representation of models in
Revit. This is required to avoid dependencies associated with the Revit EAM, which often
struggles to generate an accurate representation of the geometry of models.
• Improve the current shading element mapper. Shading elements in models are currently
not adequately supported by the parser and need further development to be implemented.
• Develop mappers for all inputs in the SCH that is available in Revit. This should be done
to further improve the BIM-based BEM interoperability, and reduce the requirements for
additional manual processing.
• Develop an SCH object model for missing parameters in Revit objects. This could provide a
method of standardising the required SCH input parameters that are not currently available
in Revit.
• Develop a framework which utilises the epJSON data format.
This would reduce the
framework’s complexity, as it would make the EnergyPlus parser redundant.
• Integrate the framework with a user interface. This would simplify the model validation
processes and enable results visualisations.
• Utilise the framework to further investigate whether it holds the potential to reduce the
dynamic EPG. Multiple applications of the framework are needed for further validation of
the tool.
• Investigate the ability of the framework to perform POE and VC. Such an investigation
could include tests with altered system specifications to assess the accuracy of the POE
and VC.

4.5
Conclusions
The research conducted in this study aimed to improve the interoperability of BIM-based BEM
modelling and to provide a proof of concept that automatically generated BIM-based BEM
models hold the potential to reduce the current EPG. The following is concluded based on the
development of a framework for automatic BIM-based BEM modelling and the application of
the framework on a building in operation.
It is not possible to represent all the essential data required to create an EnergyPlus BEM model
in a Revit BIM model. Despite similarities between the applications, an exploratory study of
EnergyPlus and Revit highlighted that essential parameters as schedules, location and weather
conditions, input parameters, and output variables were either insufficiently represented or non-
existing in Revit. Coupled with user input, Revit might, however, be used for BIM-based BEM.
It is possible to improve the interoperability between Revit and EnergyPlus by developing an
application-specific exchange format schema for BIM-based BEM modelling. By avoiding the
open schemes as IFC and gbXML, it was found that the interoperability issues concerning the
exchange format and the BEM application of BIM-based BEM between EnergyPlus and Revit
could be eliminated. The issues concerning the BIM application were, however, unaffected.
It is possible to semi-automatically generate and simulate a BEM model in EnergyPlus, and
a coupled BEM model in SOEP, with data from a Revit BIM model. Despite not succeeding
in simulating a highly complex BEM model in SOEP, the use-case study exemplified and vali-
dated that both simple and complex BEM model could be semi-automatically generated by the
developed BIM-based BEM framework.
It is possible to identify the EPG of a building with the developed framework if it is calibrated
with POE data. Despite struggling to successfully exemplify the full potential of BIM-based
BEM concerning the identification of the EPG, due to insufficient use-case documentation and
performance data, the developed BIM-based BEM framework did perform as expected, given
that it was only partially calibrated with POE data.
It is possible to reduce the EPG of a building with the developed framework if calibrated with
POE data. While the dynamic simulation model generated by the developed BIM-based BEM
framework was not fully calibrated, it exemplified how varied simulations of boundary conditions
can be used to reduce the energy of a building while also assuring that the indoor environment
is not compromised beyond acceptance.
Thus, the interoperability of BIM-based BEM modelling between Revit and EnergyPlus/SOEP
has been improved. Furthermore, automatically generated BIM-based BEM models do hold the
potential to reduce the current EPG.

Simulation Parameters
Version ("Version"):
Specifies which version of EnergyPlus the IDF file is generated for.
Surface convection alghorithm inside ("SurfaceConvectionAlgorith:Inside"):

Surface convection alghorithm outside ("SurfaceConvectionAlgorith:Outside"):
Specifies the models used for surface convection of the heat transfer surfaces at exterior side.
Heat balance algorithm ("HeatBalanceAlgorithm"):
Specifies which algorithm that is used for heat and moisture transfer through the building surfaces.
Zone air heat balance algorithm ("ZoneAirHeatBalanceAlgorithm"):
Specifies which algorithm that is used to calculate zone air temperatures and humidity ratios.
Simulation control ("SimulationControl"):
Specifies which type of simulation that will be performed. E.g. zone sizing or system sizing.
Timestep ("Timestep"):
Describes the timestep used for heat transfer and load calculations.
Run period ("RunPeriod"):
Specifies the period for which the simulation will be performed.
Contaminant balance ("ZoneAirContaminantBalance"):
Specifies which contaminants that will be simulated in the simulation.
Shadow calculation ("ShadowCalculation"):
specifies the details for lighting and shadowing simulations.
Ground temperatures ("Site:GroundTemperatures:BuildingSurface"):
specifies the monthly average temperature of the ground.
Global geometry rules ("GlobalGeometryRules"):
specifies what geometry rules are applied for when specifying the geometry of a building surface.
Output Parameters
Output variable dictionary ("OutputVariableDictionary"):
Specifies in what format the simulation output variables should be specified.

Output JSON ("Output:JSON"):

Infiltration schedule ("Schedule:Compact"):

Background carbon dioxide concentration ("Schedule:Compact"):
