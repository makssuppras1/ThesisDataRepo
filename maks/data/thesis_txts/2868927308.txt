Abstract
The offshore wind industry is expanding fast in response to the global demand for re-
newable energy. Traditionally, bidding processes for offshore wind projects have focused
primarily on price, with contracts typically awarded to the lowest bidder—an approach that
often overlooks important environmental and sustainability considerations. Nonetheless,
as offshore wind farms expand, there is an increasing awareness of the need to integrate
non-price criteria into the bidding process.
In order to meet regulatory requirements and ensure the long-term ecological balance of
these large-scale projects, non-price criteria— such as project likelihood, supply chain
robustness, system integration and particularly those pertaining to environmental sustain-
ability and biodiversity net gain—are becoming increasingly important.
When environmental factors and biodiversity net gain are incorporated into the bid and
design processes, offshore wind farms can maintain competitive bids while achieving sus-
tainable results. In order to provide a framework that strikes a balance between environ-
mental management and economic viability, this thesis seeks to investigate and optimize
these parameters.
Offshore wind turbines play a critical role in the global change to renewable energy. How-
ever, while economic criteria like cost-efficiency are prioritized in bid processes, integrat-
ing environmental factors, has become increasingly important. By incorporating Biodi-
versity Net Gain (BNG) principles, offshore wind developers can contribute to ecological
restoration while maintaining competitive bids. This study seeks to optimize offshore wind
bidding processes by including environmental criteria such as biodiversity. In this report,
the term “net positive non-price criteria” is used to denote criteria that incentivize devel-
opers to design projects that produce a net positive impact on ecosystem function and
biodiversity.
Net positive impact or net gain are common principles embedded into international safe-
guard standards and policy.
Net positive impact or net gain occurs at the individual
project level through autonomous choices made by individual companies. The idea is that
projects go beyond just mitigating the harm caused by their pre-construction, construction
and operation activities and seek to leave the impacted area in a better condition than it
was found. It requires the adoption of best practices, a focus on direct and cumulative im-
pacts from all activities, a decommissioning plan, consistent and transparent data collec-
tion, careful planning and thorough documentation. It also requires solicitation processes
that reward bids that exceed regulatory standards, that invest in enhancing ecological
function of the affected area and propose innovative projects that build up marine and
aquatic ecosystems.

Introduction
In recent years, there has been growing concern about the impact of human activities on
biodiversity, particularly in the context of large-scale infrastructure projects. Offshore wind
farms, as a crucial part of the transition to renewable energy, are no exception. These
projects can have both positive and negative effects on marine ecosystems, making it es-
sential to consider biodiversity conservation when planning and implementing wind farms.
Integrating biodiversity protection into the decision-making process of offshore wind projects
can help balance the need for clean energy with the preservation of marine life. This ap-
proach, known as Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG), aims to ensure that developments leave
nature in a better state than before. However, the inclusion of non-price criteria like BNG
in offshore wind turbine bidding processes remains an evolving challenge.
This thesis explores how biodiversity considerations, specifically BNG, can be integrated
into the bidding process for offshore wind projects. By focusing on the role of non-price
criteria in project selection, this research aims to contribute to the development of a more
sustainable approach to offshore wind energy development.
1.1
Background
Biodiversity
Biodiversity is the variety of life, including variation among genes, species and functional
traits. [1].
Ecosystem services are the collection of benefits that ecosystems provide to humanity.
Provisioning services involve the production of renewable resources (for example food,
wood, fresh water). Regulating services are those that reduce environmental change
(for example climate regulation, disease control). Ecosystem functions are ecological
processes that control the fluxes of energy, nutrients and organic matter through an en-
vironment. Examples include: primary production, which is the process by which plants
use sunlight to convert inorganic matter into new biological tissue; nutrient cycling, which
is the process by which biologically essential nutrients are captured, released and then
recaptured; and decomposition, which is the process by which organic waste, such as
dead plants and animals, is broken down and recycled.
Biodiversity Net Gain
Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG) makes sure that habitats for wildlife are left in a measurably
better state than they were before the development.
A habitat will contain a number of biodiversity units, depending on things like its size,
quality, location and type. When considering biodiversity value, an ecologist should be
consulted. They will measure the biodiversity value of the existing habitat and advise on
suitable habitat creation or enhancement for the land. The statutory biodiversity metric is
designed to quantify the biodiversity value of a project area before and after development.
It provides a standardized way to measure and compare biodiversity impacts, ensuring
that development projects contribute positively to ecological conservation. The benefits
are to provide a clear and quantifiable measure of biodiversity value, supports transparent
decision-making and promotes the achievement of biodiversity net gain. However, the
limitations are the accuracy of the metric depends on the quality of input data and may
not fully capture all ecological complexities or indirect impacts.

Non-price criteria
The EU State Aid Guidelines for Climate, Energy and Environment came into force in
January 2022, allowing Governments to use non-price criteria for up to 30% of the evalu-
ation in their renewable energy auctions [2]. The Net Zero Industry Act, approved in May
2024, mandates Governments to apply non-price criteria to at least 30% of their volume
auctioned every year per member state. They can decide to apply it as prequalification
or award. If award and if benefitting from state aid e.g. CfD, each criterion shall have
a minimum weight of 5% and a combined weight between 15% and 30% of the award
criteria. Non-price criteria are all other criteria except for price used in a tender, bid or
proposal. Non-price criteria help objectively identify weaknesses and strengths of differ-
ent bids that are not properly captured by a purely economic comparison. They expand
the geographic and temporal scopes of the bid comparison to capture a larger range of
impacts and opportunities. They enable non-economic policy goals to be integrated into
the bidding process. The benefits of the transition can and should be expanded to include
economic, environmental, social and cultural benefits. Non-price criteria are a channel for
maximizing those net benefits.
Non-price criteria can be quantitative or qualitative, but they must be objective, mea-
surable and capable of comparison between different proposals. A properly designed
non-price criterion will encourage innovation and investment in maximizing the potential
benefits of offshore wind, not just procuring the lowest-cost source of energy. European
countries and American states are using non-price criteria in their offshore wind auctions
to advance policy goals. The non-price criteria can be broadly assigned to four cate-
gories: biodiversity protection or environmental impact mitigation; energy system inte-
gration; economic and workforce development; and community benefits (with a focus on
disadvantaged communities) as shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Categories of non-price criteria, taken from [2]
Net positive non-price criteria are an opportunity to recognize and respond to the ecolog-
ical pressures on our marine environments and a tool that legislators and regulators can
employ when soliciting and evaluating offshore wind bids.
Contracts for Difference (CfD)
The European Comission [3] has explained this concept as follows:
“A two-way contract for difference is a contract signed between an electricity
generator and a public entity, typically the State, which sets a strike price, usu-
ally by a competitive tender. The generator sells the electricity in the market
but then settles with the public entity the difference between the market price
and the strike price.
It thus allows the generator to receive a stable revenue for the electricity it
2
==PAGE:13==
produces, while at the same time it provides a revenue limitation for generators
when market prices are high. In a twoway CfD, if the market price is below the
strike price, the generator receives the difference; if the market price is above
the strike price, the generator pays back the difference.”
The main advantages of CfDs over other support mechanisms are better public budget
use and lower operational complexity. The better use of public money comes from the
fact that government budgets to be allocated to CfDs can be better targeted to incentivise
the desired development of clean technologies, without the risk of budget overruns that
have occurred on several occasions with FiT (Feed-in Tariff) schemes, described below.
And by providing a stable revenue profile for projects, CfDs enable private finance to play
a greater role in the required investment in new technologies.
Much of the success of CfDs‘ (in the UK and elsewhere) is due to the fact that the stable
revenue profile, combined with falling technology costs and increasing construction ex-
pertise, has reduced risk perceptions and consequently allowed greater competition for
investment in new projects, reducing the risk premium demanded by lenders. [4]
Different types of support mechanisms: alternatives to CfDs
There are several types of support mechanisms that have been implemented over time
to support the deployment of renewable power generation, from Feed-in-Tariffs (“FiTs”) to
Feed-in-Premiums (“FiPs”).
• Feed-in Tariff (”FIT”): FiTs guarantee a fixed rate for the electricity generated from
renewable sources, usually a price that is typically higher than prevailing market
prices and are paid to renewable energy producers for a specific duration (typically
from 15 to 25 years). A generator receives a fixed price for each MWh of electricity
produced and the fixed-rate tariff is typically set through an administrative process.
The government sets the rules for producers to qualify for the scheme and then
commits to paying the tariffs when production starts. The tariff is calculated based
on the estimated cost of production for a given technology. Due to their simplicity,
FiTs are also more accessible to smaller producers, it serves lower project risk.
• Power Purchase Agreement (”PPA”): The PPA is a contract between the pro-
ducer of power and the buyer. It states the buyer (typically utilities or corporations)
will procure energy from the producer at a fixed price for a specified period of time
(normally anywhere from 5 to 10 years, but long-term PPAs can extend beyond 10
years). Although not limited to renewable projects, this contract is becoming popu-
lar among buyers wishing to procure renewable energy for their sustainability target.
The PPA provides the specific terms of the contract, being amount of power to be
delivered, a negotiated price for that energy, how delivery of the energy is measured
and penalties.
• Feed-in-Premium (”FiP”): FiPs, also known as one-way (or one-sided) CfDs, have
been used in a growing number of jurisdictions since the mid-2010s as a mechanism
to incentivise the development of renewable generation and to overcome some of
the distortions of the previously dominant FiTs. FiPs compensate generators for
low electricity prices up to agreed strike prices and leave the market upside to be
captured by the producers in case the wholesale electricity prices are above the
strike price. This guarantee of a minimum price for renewable projects provides the
necessary de-risking of project revenues to make projects bankable.

Research Question
The research question that guides this study is: ”How can biodiversity net gain be quanti-
fied and utilized in offshore wind farm bidding processes to maximize environmental and
economic outcomes?”
This question is relevant as it attempts to analyze various options to include measurable
biodiversity boost within the bidding processes for offshore wind projects and thereby
promote sustainable energy development while protecting marine ecosystems. Incorpo-
rating BNG as part of the bidding criteria will enable the developers to price in projects that
contribute to environmental policy outcomes, providing longer-term ecological resilience
along with economic viability.
Closely related to this, innovations are also discussed that are key to delivering improved
biodiversity outcomes in offshore wind projects. The study will analyze several relevant
legislative frameworks in markets such as Denmark, The Netherlands and Germany and
the increasing trend of non-price criteria in offshore wind auctions to join environmental
considerations in the competitive bidding process.

2.1.1
Offshore wind development in the EU
The offshore wind development in the EU is guided by several environmental laws, partic-
ularly the State aid rules (Guidelines), Renewable Energy Directive (RED II) and the Mar-
itime Spatial Planning Directive (MSPD). The Guidelines express the schemes to be used
to promote expansion of offshore wind through competitively awarded auctions. However,
auction formats that permit state subsidization of projects, aid awarded via contract for dif-
ferences (CFDs), are limited in the weight afforded to non-price of no more than 30% of
the analysis. Contrastingly, subsidy-free auctions are free to weigh non-price criteria as
the member state wishes. As more member states move towards subsidy-free auction
formats, the use and weighting of non-price criteria are expanding to allow greater con-
sideration of environmental goals. RED II drives the development of offshore wind to
meet Europe’s rapidly scaling renewable energy development commitments. RED II sets
both binding EU-wide and state specific decarbonization metrics and requires expedited
offshore wind permitting. The MSPD is the framework for sustainable use of marine re-
sources and the conservation of marine ecosystems. It coordinates different activities
occurring in Europe’s marine waters.
Tenders involve a formal request for proposals or offers from companies to supply goods
or services. The awarding body specifies detailed criteria and requirements and inter-
ested companies submit their proposals. The decision is made based on predefined cri-
teria such as technical quality, price and sometimes non-price criteria like environmental
impact.
Auctions, on the other hand, are competitive bidding processes where participants bid for
a contract, often focusing more on price competition. In the context of energy projects, auc-
tions typically involve companies bidding to secure the rights to develop projects, where
the contract is often awarded to the lowest bidder or the one offering the best value under
specific conditions.
In the following subsection is presented how the Netherlands has used non-price criteria in
recent tenders, how Germany is proposing to use non-price criteria in forthcoming tenders
and how Denmark is using the non-price criteria. In Europe, most offshore wind sales
occur through the tender process where bids are submitted and evaluated without having
multiple rounds of bidding. The use of non-price criteria to enhance ecological function is
a choice to incentivize nature-positive investments and innovation and it is a choice to act
to address future issues that could impair decarbonization efforts.
2.1.1.1
The Netherlands
The Netherlands is a global leader in offshore wind development and a global leader in the
use of non-price criteria. As the Netherlands has ramped up its offshore wind capacity
procurement target, from 11 GW target to 21 GW by 2030 with potentially between 38
GW and 72 GW by 2050, it has sought to use it auction format to eliminate potential
future conflicts. [7, pages 15–18]
The winning bid receives the lease area, a preliminary study that is sufficient to complete
preliminary engineering design, the interconnection substation and the right to construct
and operate the facility for up to forty years. A mix of price and non-price criteria are used

The two tenders held in 2022 incorporated non-price criteria to address ecological and

Germany
Germany’s offshore wind ambition has grown significantly over the last decade as it moves
towards achieving climate neutrality by 2045. Targets for 30GW of offshore wind by 2030
and 70GW by 2045, represent a significant step up from the current installed capacity of
8 GW. [7, page 19]
The offshore wind procurement process is governed by the Offshore Wind Energy Act
(WindSeeG). The purpose of WindSeeG is to expand the use of wind energy at sea, in
the interest of climate and environmental protection and to consider nature conservation,
shipping and offshore connecting lines. Recently, Germany changed its procurement of
wind energy to accelerate procurement processes and to address concerns over growing
environmental impacts. In 2022, the German Bundestag amended WindSeeG to change
the structure of auctions to allow for the use of non-price criteria and to include new qual-
itative criteria. As Germany moves away from CFDs to a system that allows for zero or
negative bidding by developers, it is expanding the use of non-price criteria. [8]
Germany introduced non-price criteria in offshore wind auctions in 2023. They are running
two types of auctions: the first for non-centrally pre-developed areas and the second
for centrally pre-developed areas. Non-centrally pre-developed areas’ award criteria are

2.1.1.3
Denmark
Denmark, a pioneer in offshore wind development, continues to set ambitious targets,
aiming to reach 13 GW of offshore wind capacity by 2030. Denmark’s commitment to
renewable energy and sustainability is reflected in its use of both price and non-price
criteria in tender processes. [11]
Denmark will use non-price criteria focusing on sustainability and social responsibility in
the upcoming 6 GW of offshore wind energy tenders – Kriegers Flak II, Kattegat, Hesselø
and North Sea I. However, these criteria will be featured in prequalification, while the only
competitive parameter will be the annual adjustment payment to the state. The draft entry
requirements in respect of sustainability include nature positive initiatives, environmental
monitoring, life cycle assessment, environmental product declarations, recyclable turbine
blades and the obligation to decommission the offshore wind farm. [7, page 5]
The sustainability requirements include:
• Nature Positive Initiatives: (Only for Kattegat and North Sea I) - Applicants must
develop and complete nature positive initiatives and provide the Danish Energy
Agency (DEA) a proposal for a plan for competition of the initiatives within 6 months
of the concession’s signing date.
• Environment Applicants must develop and maintain an environmental impact mon-
itoring program for the offshore wind farm.
• Life Cycle Assessment: Applicants must submit a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)
of electricity produced from the offshore wind farm for both the minimum installed
capacity and the overplanting.
• Environmental Product Declarations: Applicants must submit documentation and
comply with the environmental performance of the central components of the off-
shore wind farm by developing Environmental Product Declarations (EPD).

• Recyclable Turbine Blades: Applicants must submit documentation for the En-
vironmental Technology Verification (ETV) by the construction start-up deadline,

• Obligation to decommission the Offshore Wind Farm: The applicant must, at

2.1.2
Real word: failed Tender in Denmark - December 2024
Denmark’s most recent offshore wind tender, launched in April 2024, faced significant
challenges and ultimately drew no bids by the December 5 deadline for sites in the North
Sea. This tender, which offered a substantial 10 GW capacity across six sites, was Den-
mark’s largest to date but lacked the financial support of subsidies, which many industry
players deemed necessary given current market conditions. The decision to forgo sub-
sidies, combined with factors like soaring inflation, rising interest rates and supply chain
issues, led major companies such as Ørsted to withdraw from bidding due to an unfavor-
able risk-reward balance [14], [15].
This outcome marks a setback not only for Denmark but for the offshore wind industry
at large, as it highlights the shifting dynamics within the renewable energy sector, espe-
cially in Europe. The Danish Energy Agency is now planning consultations with market
participants to better understand the underlying reasons for this lack of interest [15].
The broader repercussions of this failed tender could influence future offshore wind projects
in Denmark and across Europe, as developers and investors may reconsider their com-
mitment to large-scale projects without adequate financial support or stable economic
conditions. This may further delay the ambitious goals set by European governments to
transition to cleaner energy.

2.1.3
Analysis of tender non-price criteria: Auction Policies
Analysis of the data provided by EDF concerning non-price criteria in tenders has been
performed in comparison with several offshore wind tenders throughout various countries.
• Bids from China show a clear bias towards using non-price criteria, with the majority
weighting within tenders between 60% and 100%. However, the forms of non-price
criteria are mostly system integration, bidder competency and project deliverability,
rather than environmental mitigation or sustainability measures. For instance, the
Hangzhou Bay Offshore Wind (2023) auction allocated 35.35% to project deliver-
ability and just 4.04% to system integration.
• Denmark has set 0% for non-price criteria in the North Sea Energy Island Phase 1C
(2024) tender, meaning that the auction was entirely price-driven. This seems to im-
ply that some tenders in Denmark are concentrated merely on the cost-effectiveness
of projects at the exclusion of environmental or social impacts. Very strongly among
countries, Denmark has been with a near-perfect environmental record; but it also
seems that some projects get a much higher priority in terms of economic efficiency
than others.
• The Netherlands has extremely well incorporated non-price criteria into its offshore
wind tenders. For instance, in the case of Hollandse Kust West VI (2022), it allo-
cated 30% of the final score against non-price aspects, such as project deliverabil-
ity and system integration, in the tender. Although the IJmuiden Ver Alpha (2024)
tender gives evidence to a further accent on non-price criteria, with 45% attached
to ecological mitigation and 20% to Environmental, Social and Governance (ESG)
dimensions.
• French tenders often have a balance of price and non-price parameters. Thus, for
the AO4 Centre Manche 1 (2023) tender, the score set for price is 70% and the
remaining 30% for non-price parameters.
• U.S. offshore wind auctions place significant importance on non-price criteria. In the
Rhode Island Offshore Wind RFP I (2023), 75% of the score was allocated to non-
price factors, with a focus on local content (50%) and ecological mitigation (25%).
This shows the U.S. focus on maximizing local economic benefits and addressing
environmental concerns in offshore wind projects.
• The U.K. government is considering the inclusion of non-price criteria in the upcom-
ing Contracts for Difference (CfD) Allocation Round 7 (AR7) scheduled for 2025.
This initiative aims to enhance the environmental and social benefits of offshore
wind projects. The Clean Industry Bonus (CIB) is a key component of this approach,
offering additional support to projects that invest in sustainable supply chains. The
CIB application window is expected to open in February 2025. The CIB framework
outlines the criteria and process for this bonus, emphasizing the importance of sus-
tainable practices in the offshore wind sector. [16]

2.2
Environmental Impact
2.2.1
Co-existence in Marine Spatial Planning (MSP)
Marine Spatial Planning aims to balance competing human uses of marine spaces while
ensuring the protection of marine ecosystems. It is a practical approach to achieving sus-
tainable ocean development.
The idea of co-existence in MSP means the need to integrate multiple sectors, such as
energy, conservation and fisheries into a single spatial framework. In Europe, especially
with the expansion of offshore wind energy, marine spaces have become multi-functional.
The co-existence of these uses ensures that renewable energy development does not
come at the cost of marine biodiversity or traditional activities like fishing. These frame-
works promote the integration of environmental goals into the spatial planning of offshore
activities, reinforcing the idea of co-existence. For example, wind farms can contribute
to biodiversity net gain by acting as no-take zones for fish species, enhancing marine
ecosystem resilience. Countries like Denmark and the Netherlands have implemented
co-existence frameworks where offshore wind farms are designed with biodiversity and
fisheries considerations. Denmark’s Energy Islands initiative aims to co-locate offshore
wind turbines, energy storage, and biodiversity protection in a single maritime space. The
Dutch MSP framework encourages the co-existence of offshore wind and marine conser-
vation, with specific non-price bid criteria in offshore wind tenders aimed at promoting
positive environmental impacts.
Integrating Biodiversity Net Gain principles into MSP offers an innovative approach to
enhance co-existence [18].
2.2.2
Nature-inclusive design (NID)
The intention of NID is to reduce adverse effects on affected areas and ensure that mea-
sures to protect nature are included in Offshore Wind Farms development plans and
projects. NID measures can be used to promote rehabilitation of a degraded habitat, en-
hance ecological functioning and increase biological production and diversity in an OWF
area.
Optimized NID scour protection refers to a design applied to a monopile or substation, in-
volving the placement of additional units on or within the standard scour protection layer.
This may include components such as habitat pipes, reefballs, and Eco armour blocks,
which help mitigate soil movement or scouring of the foundation. Scour protection is a
standard design feature in offshore wind farms to prevent the erosion of the foundation,
which could otherwise destabilize the monopile and lead to structural failure.
Optimized NID cable protection layer refers to a design applied to subsea power cables
or cable crossings, specifically incorporating units that enhance the protection of cables.
16
==PAGE:27==
While most of the existing literature on NID and offshore wind farms focuses on bottom-
fixed turbines, particularly in the Dutch sector of the southern North Sea, NID-optimized
cable protection layers are also designed to address additional factors, such as minimizing
environmental impact and improving long-term stability of the cable installation.
Ørsted was awarded contracts for the Borssele 1 and 2 OWFs and worked with Wagenin-
gen Marine Research to introduce formations of concrete pipes to the sea floor, creating
cavities for fish to hide and forage. Vattenfall is also working together with the Technical
University of Denmark – DTU to integrate biodiversity components into life cycle assess-

3.1
Semi-structured interview
In order to collect expert perspectives on incorporating biodiversity net gain, environmen-
tal considerations and innovations in offshore wind tenders, semi-structure interviews
have been conducted. This approach provides flexibility to explore detailed topics while
maintaining structured data collection. It connects theoretical concepts with real-world in-
sights, ensuring a comprehensive understanding of stakeholder views. Semi-structured
interviews have been highlighted as an acceptable approach in qualitative research and
are useful for studying multifaceted issues. The technique brings a level of flexibility to
provide their valuable input outside of this structured framework capturing information that
purely unstructured interviews might miss due to being very detailed and wordy.
It must be specific enough to address the issues (specific, targeted questions) but also
general enough to permit spontaneous elaboration by interviewees.
The interview process starts with building a comfortable rapport with participants to en-
courage open and natural conversation. Flexibility is also key, as interviewers should
be ready to follow relevant tangents that may reveal insights beyond the prepared ques-
tions. With participants’ consent, interviews are recorded, and detailed notes are taken
to capture the information accurately. Ethical considerations, like confidentiality and data
security are also carefully taken into account. The interview template can be checked on

3.2
Interview with the Danish Energy Agency
This chapter analyzes insights from Jan Tjeerd Boom, a key figure in the Danish En-
ergy Agency, regarding the challenges and opportunities associated with the inclusion of
non-price criteria in offshore wind bidding processes. It explores the current policy en-
vironment, the role of environmental assessments and the implications of recent policy
changes.
Denmark has a system of regulations which is in line with the European Union require-
ments, where tenders for offshore wind farms are based on price only as the evaluation
criteria. Boom states that there is a growing political will to adopt qualitative selection cri-
teria and that these variations would be introduced without making changes to the current
regime. This flexibility in the regime suggests to adjust the changing dynamics where sus-
tainability and social concerns are becoming important in the designing of energy projects.
Latest policy changes show a clear move from only depending on subsidies to creating
wind projects that are self-sufficient revenue-generators. This change makes it possible
for project investors to maintain certain investible commitments towards environmental
and social goals within projects, as the government is now more open to funding non-price
components. Boom points out that such a transition enables the inclusion of provisions for
integrated sustainability and social benefits which are usually ignored in public projects.
This tends to show that there is increasing recognition of the value of non-price elements
in public agreements.
According to the Danish system, suitable sites are determined after analyzing already
existing environmental effects to select locations that already cause a minimal environ-
mental influence. In addition, he explains the importance of improving knowledge on the
ecological consequences of offshore wind parks, as all the existing ones are too few to
have a clear picture of the long-term effects.
In his analysis, Boom also makes international comparisons, focusing on the position of
the United States and Germany, in particular. He assesses the reasoning for the non-
tariff barriers employed in different situations such as local content requirements, which
are used to increase public support and develop local suppliers. These conclusions also
stress the different approaches adopted by various countries to achieve economic objec-
tives alongside social and environmental concerns.
The observations made by Jan Tjeerd Boom indicate that there is a great deal of com-
plexity in the interaction between regulation, development of policies and the introduction
of non-price components in offshore wind tenders.
While Denmark has made big progress in integrating environmental and social concerns
in its energy policies, there are still barriers to overcome. Seeking further policy attach-
ment and adaptive policy making will be indispensable to not only protect the natural en-
vironment but also ensure positive social benefits from offshore wind projects. Regarding
the transitions that are being experienced within the energy sector, progressing into new
methods and enhancing the cooperation among all parties will be critical for achieving a
better offshore wind energy development.

3.4
How to estimate the net gain of biodiversity
In estimating biodiversity net gain for offshore wind projects, a number of qualitative and
quantitative way to demonstrate the ecological gains arising from certain conservation
actions are aimed to be undertaken. In bid rounds for offshore wind, where environmental
and social impacts are starting to be as important as economic ones, BNG is one of
the extremely important parameters as it guarantees to increase biodiversity above the
predetermined baseline.
There are also difficulties in calculating BNG within the marine space which include areas
such as time lags in assessing biodiversity recovery as well as complexities in marine
environment. Different factors, such as water quality, migration and ecosystem connection
are among the marine species that may determine the success rate of the conservation
measures taken.
The estimation of biodiversity net gain becomes a measurable activity, helping to imple-
ment these techniques for stakeholders to make trade-offs between development and
ecological concerns. Hence, with efforts such as these, the offshore wind sector can
meaningfully enhance its contributions towards the conservation of biodiversity while net
gains achieved go beyond the legal requirement compliance and serve to build the marine
ecosystems’ resilience.

3.5.2
Artificial reefs
Offshore wind farms are typically built on the seafloor using hard structures like piles,
cables and foundations. However, there is still limited knowledge on the local and large-
scale impacts of these installations on marine environments, especially regarding fish,
marine mammals, electromagnetic fields, noise and benthic communities.
Offshore renewable energy structures, such as scour protections, can enhance biodiver-
sity by acting as artificial reefs, offering shelter and feeding grounds for various marine
species, including fish.
Scour protections around wind farm foundations can alter the seabed, improving species-
rich ecosystems in previously biodiversity-poor areas. Wind farms may increase biodiver-
sity by providing shelter and new habitats, but the design and location of these installations
are crucial to avoid negative impacts on protected species [24].

Piledriving, traditionally used for installing monopile foundations for offshore wind turbines,
generates high levels of underwater noise (up to 180-200 dB), which can harm marine
ecosystems. Depending on environmental factors such as water depth, seabed composi-
tion and sound attenuation techniques, the noise can propagate over distances of several
kilometers, with harmful levels often reaching 10 to 20 kilometers from the source. This
calls for mitigation measures that reduce sound pressure levels (SPL) during the construc-
tion process. The technology used is described as followed:
• Bubble screens: A system of perforated hoses that release compressed air bub-
bles around the monopile. The bubbles create an acoustic barrier that scatters and
absorbs sound waves, reducing the propagation of noise underwater.
• Devices attached to the piling hammer that reduce the impact energy transmission
into the monopile, thereby decreasing vibrations and noise levels.

3.5.5
Gravity-based structures and suction buckets
Monopile, jacket and tripod foundations are the most commonly used types in offshore
wind farm industries, though other foundation types like gravity-based structures and suc-
tion buckets are also used, even though less frequently. While gravity-based structures
and suction buckets are often considered more ”eco-friendly” alternatives due to their
reduced noise and vibration during installation (since they do not require extensive per-
cussive pile driving) their environmental footprint can be more complex.
Gravity-Based Structures are used in offshore wind turbine foundations, primarily in shal-
low waters (up to 50 meters). They rely on their weight for stability on the seabed, unlike
monopiles, which are driven into the seabed. GBS are ideal for solid seabeds, offering
advantages like material cost savings and stability in shallow waters. However, they are
more complex to install and less suitable for deeper waters, where monopiles are typically
preferred due to their simpler installation process and broader applicability in various soil
conditions, [28].

3.6.1
Wind Power Generation
In order to calculate the wind power generation, the turbine’s energy production Eturbine
in MWh is multiplied by the number of turbines nturbines to approximate the wind farm’s
hourly production profile Efarm.
Efarm(t) = ETurbine(t) · nTurbines · (1 −ωloss)
(3.3)
ωloss is the combined fractional loss factor (0 ≤ωloss ≤1), accounting for [32]:
• Wake losses: 5-10% due to reduced wind energy from turbine interference.
• Electrical losses: 2-4% from cables, transformers and transmission.
• Availability losses: 2-5% due to downtime for maintenance or repairs.
• Environmental losses: 0.5-1% from factors like icing or dirt on turbine blades.
• Grid curtailment losses: Typically 0% except in weak grids where it may reach up
to 3%.
• Performance losses: 1-3% from reduced efficiency over time or due to faults.

5.4.4
Assessment of Innovation Relevance for Wind Resource Modeling
The implementation of the six innovations wood wind turbines, artificial reefs, reduced
noise during pile-driving, water replenish holes, gravity-base structures and suction buck-
ets within WAsP depends on their relevance to wind resource assessment and the scope
of the software.
WAsP is primarily designed to model wind flow and predict wind resource potential based
on terrain, obstacles and roughness, while innovations related to foundation design, instal-
lation time and noise reduction focus on the structural, logistical or environmental aspects
of offshore wind farms. For example, Reduced Noise During Pile Driving and artificial reefs
directly impact biodiversity and environmental assessments rather than wind resource po-
tential, making their implementation in WAsP unnecessary.
Similarly, foundation types like gravity-based structures and suction buckets influence
the structural and installation phases but do not alter wind flow or turbine performance
modeled in WAsP. Therefore, while these innovations are critical for optimizing offshore
wind project costs, timelines and environmental impact, they do not inherently affect the
wind flow calculations conducted in WAsP. As such, implementing these innovations in
WAsP is not necessary for wind resource analysis, but their influence could be considered
in separate cost-benefit or environmental models used alongside WAsP outputs.

Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) of
Saint-Nazaire Offshore Wind Farm
In this chapter, the environmental impact of the Saint-Nazaire Offshore Wind Farm is
assessed using the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) methodology. To estimate the envi-
ronmental impacts, the analysis is based on the LCA of a 990 MW wind farm made up
of 66 Vestas V236-15 MW turbines [44]. This approach allows to apply data from simi-
lar, high-capacity offshore wind farms, considering the similar technology and operational
parameters.
The methodology adheres to the ISO 14040/14044 standards [45], focusing on key en-
vironmental indicators such as global warming potential (GWP), biodiversity impacts, re-
source use and acoustic pollution. The functional unit for this analysis is 1 kWh of elec-
tricity generated over the wind farm’s operational lifetime of 25 years, with specific ad-
justments made for the characteristics of the Saint-Nazaire project, including its turbine
model, capacity and installation.
The environmental indicators used in the LCA study include GWP, biodiversity impacts
(particularly marine life) resource use (metals, rare earth elements), and acoustic pollu-
tion during installation. These indicators measure the wind farm’s contributions to climate
change, ecosystem disruption, resource consumption and noise pollution during its lifecy-
cle.
6.1
ISO 14040
ISO 14040 outlines the principles and framework for LCA, including goal and scope defini-
tion, inventory analysis, impact assessment and interpretation. It provides the guidelines
for conducting an LCA study but does not dictate specific methods.
6.1.1
A Four-Phase Approach
1. Goal and Scope Definition:
(a) Foundation of the Study: Establishing clear objectives and the intended ap-
plication of the LCA.
(b) Scope Articulation: Defining the product system to be studied, the functional
unit (quantifiable performance of a product system), system boundaries (pro-
cesses to be included in the study) and any assumptions and limitations.
2. Life Cycle Inventory Analysis (LCI)
(a) Data Collection: Compiling an inventory of relevant inputs and outputs of a
product system.
(b) Methodological Choices: Deciding on approaches for data collection, alloca-
tion methods and handling multifunctional processes.
3. Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA):
(a) Impact Assessment: Associating inventory data with specific environmental
impacts and assessing their magnitude.
(b) Categorization: Impact categories typically include global warming potential,
ozone depletion, eutrophication, acidification and resource depletion.
43
==PAGE:54==
(c) Characterization: Conversion of LCI results into common units for each cate-
gory, facilitating comparison and analysis.
4. Life Cycle Interpretation:
(a) Data Analysis: Evaluating the results in relation to the defined goals and
scope.
(b) Sensitivity Checks: Assessing the reliability and robustness of the results.
(c) Conclusions and Recommendations: Providing a comprehensive summary
of findings with actionable insights.
6.2
ISO 14044
Building on ISO 14040, ISO 14044 provides detailed requirements for conducting an LCA.
It includes specifications for choosing impact categories, category indicators and charac-
terization models. This standard ensures that LCA studies are conducted with rigor and
consistency.
6.3
Goal and Scope of the Assessment
The primary objective of this LCA is to assess the environmental impacts of the Saint-
Nazaire Offshore Wind Farm across its entire lifecycle, from raw material extraction to
decommissioning and recycling. By identifying the key contributors to environmental im-
pacts, the study seeks to highlight areas for improvement in offshore wind energy projects,
with a particular focus on minimizing carbon emissions, resource depletion and environ-
mental disruption.
The system boundary includes all phases of the project:
• Raw material extraction: Mining and processing materials for turbines, foundations
and cables.
• Manufacturing: Production of the turbine components, monopile foundations and
inter-array/export cables.
• Transportation: Shipping of components from manufacturing sites to the installa-
tion location.
• Installation: Offshore activities, including pile driving and turbine assembly.
• Operation and maintenance: Routine maintenance activities and logistics over 25
years.
• Decommissioning: Removal of components, transportation and recycling or dis-
posal.
Impact categories include global warming potential, biodiversity effects (especially ma-
rine life), resource depletion (metals, rare earth elements) and acoustic pollution during
installation.
6.4
Inventory Analysis (LCI)
The LCI phase involves a detailed account of material flows, energy use and emissions
associated with each stage of the lifecycle.

0,7 tons of rare earth elements, mainly neodymium and dysprosium.
The energy-intensive manufacturing processes include:
Steel production: Steel is generated through electric arc furnaces (EAFs), which con-
sume approximately 19 MJ/kg.
EAFs are gradually replacing basic oxygen furnaces
(BOFs) due to their lower CO2 emissions, aligning with global efforts to decarbonize steel
production.
Fiberglass molding: Requires roughly 15 MJ/kg for forming and curing processes. Cop-
per processing: Includes mining, smelting and refining.
Monopile Foundations: The monopiles, used to anchor the turbines to the seabed, are
fabricated from high-strength steel. Each foundation weighs around 900 tons, with an
estimated energy consumption of 20.000 GJ per foundation during production.
Cabling: Inter-array cables, spanning approximately 33 km, connect the turbines, while a
20 km export cable transmits power to the mainland. These cables consist of copper cores
insulated with cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE). Copper production is energy-intensive,
with cable manufacturing consuming about 4.000 GJ per kilometer.
Transportation: Transportation of components to the offshore site contributes signifi-
cantly to green house gas emissions. Components are shipped via a combination of
trucks and ships. Turbine components are transported from the manufacturing facilities
by truck (50 km) and then loaded onto cargo ships for a 2.000 km journey to Saint-Nazaire.
Foundations are shipped 1.500 km from steel mills to the site. Emission factors used for
transportation are 0,2 kg CO2/ton-km for trucks and 0,04 kg CO2/ton-km for ships. These
contribute approximately 2.000 tons of CO2 emissions.
Installation: The installation phase involves heavy-lift vessels for deploying foundations
and turbines. These vessels consume about 10 tons of diesel per day during 20 day op-
erations, emitting approximately 1.000 tons of CO2.
Operation and Maintenance: Over 25 years of operation, the farm requires regular main-
tenance. Crew transfer vessels are used bi-weekly, consuming 2 tons of diesel per trip,
while helicopters are deployed for critical maintenance tasks, consuming 1,5 tons of avia-
tion fuel per flight. Additionally, turbines require replacement of certain components, with
approximately 0,5 tons of spare parts needed per turbine annually. These materials in-
clude metals, composites and lubricants.
Decommissioning: At the end of the wind farm’s operational life, the turbines, founda-
tions and cables are dismantled and transported for recycling or disposal. Heavy-lift ves-
sels are used again, consuming similar energy and emitting comparable CO2 as during
installation.
Recycling rates are high for metals: Steel and copper achieve recovery rates of 95%
and 90%, respectively. Fiberglass blades, however, present a recycling challenge, as
current options are limited, often leading to landfilling. Nevertheless, advancements like
Siemens Gamesa’s RecyclableBlade [47] and GE-Arkema’s recyclable resin technology
[48] are paving the way for more sustainable solutions in the wind energy sector.

Offshore Wind Farm
This chapter highlights the relation with LCA and the implementation of the six innovations
in the study case of Saint-Nazair Offshore Wind Farm. Two indicators have been studied,
the seabed disturbance and the carbon footprint using a monopile as a reference for
comparison.
The carbon footprint indicator for the case study measures the total greenhouse gas emis-
sions throughout its lifecycle, from manufacturing and transportation through installation,
operation and decommissioning. It accounts for emissions caused by the production of
materials, operations of machinery and energy use during construction stages and mainte-
nance. Low direct emissions may be recorded from operation, though indirect emissions
should still be considered.
Seabed disturbance in offshore wind farms refer to physical and environmental changes
affected by the installation and functioning of wind turbines. These disturbances result in
sediment resuspension, habitat loss and noise pollution, which have detrimental conse-
quences on marine ecosystems. Construction work often alters water flow and increases
suspended particles affecting water quality and marine life.

7.6.1
Carbon Footprint (Relative Values)
• Monopile (100): Monopiles have the highest carbon footprint (100) due to the sub-
stantial amount of steel required for construction, the energy-intensive manufactur-
ing process, and the installation methods (pile-driving). Steel is a highly carbon-
intensive material and the monopile foundations’ large size and weight further con-
tribute to the carbon emissions from transport and installation.
• Wind Wood Towers (60): These towers have a significantly lower carbon footprint
(60) because wood is a renewable material with a lower embodied carbon compared
to steel. Additionally, the energy used in manufacturing and transportation is less
than that required for monopiles, and the carbon sequestered by the wood over its
lifetime helps offset emissions.
• Artificial Reefs (80): These structures use repurposed or locally sourced materials
(e.g., concrete, old ships), which reduce the need for energy-intensive steel produc-
tion. While concrete still has a carbon footprint, reusing materials helps lower the
overall impact.
• Noise Reduction (85): Noise reduction techniques, such as bubble curtains or qui-
eter pile-driving methods, reduce the energy needed for installation, thus lowering
the carbon footprint. However, the impact remains higher than other innovations
like Wind Wood Towers because it still relies on traditional piling methods, though
with added measures for noise suppression.
• Water Holes (95): Water replenishment holes are part of a design to enhance the
ecosystem’s resilience, often incorporated into other foundation designs. The car-
bon footprint is high, as it does not fundamentally change the materials used for the
foundation but might slightly reduce the overall impact through innovative installation
methods.
• Gravity-Based Structures (85): GBS use concrete, a material with a significant
carbon footprint, but they do not require piling. The manufacturing process is typi-
cally less energy-intensive than the fabrication of monopiles.
• Suction Buckets (75): Suction buckets rely on a relatively simple installation method
that uses suction instead of pile-driving. This reduces energy consumption during
installation, resulting in a carbon footprint lower than monopiles. The use of steel in
the structure is still a factor, but the reduced installation energy balances the overall
impact.
7.6.2
Seabed Disturbance (Relative Values)
• Monopile (100): Monopiles cause significant seabed disturbance (100) due to the
pile-driving process, which involves driving large metal structures deep into the
seabed, potentially causing vibration, noise and sediment resuspension. This pro-
cess disrupts the marine ecosystem and alters the seabed habitat.
• Wind Wood Towers (100): Although Wind Wood Towers have a lower carbon foot-
print, their seabed disturbance remains the same as monopiles because the piling
method is the same. This is because the installation process may still involve some
level of seabed disturbance, even if the material is more sustainable.
• Artificial Reefs (70): Artificial Reefs can create positive ecological effects by foster-
ing marine life, but they also cause some seabed disturbance during their installa-
tion. However, the disturbance is less than that of monopiles because the materials

==PAGE:72==
used are often repurposed and the installation methods may be less invasive. Ad-
ditionally, Artificial Reefs improve marine habitats by providing surfaces for marine
organisms to colonize, thereby boosting biodiversity and contributing positively to
the surrounding environment.
• Noise Reduction (60): Innovations focused on noise reduction help reduce seabed
disturbance by minimizing the vibration and sediment resuspension typically asso-
ciated with pile-driving. This significantly lowers the seabed impact, although instal-
lation methods can still cause some disturbance.
• Water Holes (90): Water holes may involve drilling or altering the seabed to create
habitats or manage the surrounding ecosystem. This contributes to some distur-
bance, but the environmental benefits might offset the initial impact.
• Gravity-Based Structures (60): Gravity-based foundations typically require mini-
mal seabed preparation and no pile-driving, which results in a lower seabed distur-
bance compared to monopiles. However, the large size of the structures can still
impact the seabed during installation.
• Suction Buckets (45): Suction buckets have the least seabed disturbance because
they use suction to embed themselves into the seabed without causing the vibration
and noise associated with pile-driving. This method is more environmentally friendly
as it minimizes disruption to the seabed and surrounding marine life.
Summary of Key Differences and Reasons
• Carbon Footprint: The innovations like Wind Wood Towers, Artificial Reefs and
Suction Buckets have a lower carbon footprint due to the use of renewable materials
(wood) and energy-efficient installation methods (suction instead of pile-driving).
• Seabed Disturbance: The Suction Buckets result in the least seabed disturbance
because of their installation method, followed by Gravity-Based Structures and Noise
Reduction.
This comparison illustrates that while monopiles are still the most widely used technology,
the alternative innovations in foundation design offer a significant reduction in both carbon
footprint and seabed disturbance. By incorporating technologies like Wind Wood Towers
and Suction Buckets, offshore wind projects such as the Saint-Nazaire Offshore Wind
Farm can reduce their environmental impact, leading to more sustainable and eco-friendly
offshore energy production. Furthermore, these innovations could be integrated into the
bid criteria for future offshore wind projects, where non-price factors such as sustainability
and environmental impact can be considered alongside cost.

Discussion and Analysis
The Bid Impact criteria assess how each innovation influences the competitiveness and
attractiveness of an offshore wind turbine project during the bidding process. This in-
cludes evaluating several key factors such as cost-effectiveness, regulatory compliance,
environmental performance and technical feasibility. The following key factors have been
considered, [60], [61], [62], [63]:
• Cost-Effectiveness: Innovations that reduce both CAPEX and OPEX are rated
higher, as they enhance the economic viability of the project.
• Regulatory Compliance: Innovations that align with environmental regulations,
such as biodiversity and noise pollution standards, are favored.
• Environmental Performance: Technologies that contribute to Biodiversity Net Gain
and minimize environmental harm are prioritized.
• Technical Feasibility: The ease of integration and stability of each innovation in
offshore conditions is evaluated.
• Public and Stakeholder Perception: Innovations that improve public perception
and stakeholder engagement, such as artificial reefs, are considered valuable.

they might offer lower environmental benefits, their bid impact is also less pronounced.

Composite Bid Price Analysis
In this analysis, different offshore wind turbine foundation innovations were evaluated
based on three key factors: Capital Expenditure (CAPEX), Operational Expenditure (OPEX),
and Non-Price Criteria. These factors were normalized to a common scale (0 to 1) to en-
sure comparability, as each has different units and magnitudes. Normalization allows for
an effective comparison between the cost-based and qualitative aspects of each innova-
tion.
The graph illustrates how each innovation would rank in a bidding process for an offshore
wind farm project. Typically, wind farm developers need to submit bids that balance cost
and environmental impact. Innovations that reduce costs (CAPEX and OPEX) while also
offering positive non-price outcomes (e.g., reduced environmental damage or enhanced
sustainability) are more likely to be considered favorable in such bids. The graph, there-
fore, serves as a tool to help stakeholders assess which innovations could lead to better
economic and environmental outcomes for offshore wind projects.

Conclusion
This thesis has addressed the challenge of optimizing bid criteria for offshore wind turbines
by integrating environmental factors, particularly Biodiversity Net Gain, into the bidding
process. In the context of the offshore wind energy sector, incorporating non-price crite-
ria such as sustainability and environmental impact has become increasingly important.
By evaluating six innovative technologies—Wind Wood Towers, Artificial Reefs, Reduced
Noise During Pile-Driving, Water Replenish Holes, Gravity-Based Structures and Suction
Buckets—this study aimed to assess the technical feasibility, environmental contributions
and cost-effectiveness of these innovations within the scope of offshore wind turbine foun-
dation development.
In the research methodology of the thesis, quantitative and qualitative approaches were
combined to assess in what ways BNG can be integrated into bids for offshore wind
projects. It ranged from the assessment of the wind resource with WAsP software to
the LCA that evaluated the environmental impacts, passing through a deep case study
related to the Saint-Nazaire Offshore Wind Farm. In this way, full comprehension and
viability was achieved of the technical and environmental dimensions of offshore wind
innovations.
Expert interviews with the Danish Energy Agency and TotalEnergies, supported this anal-
ysis with practical insights from present industry trends, regulatory priorities and the im-
portance of environmental issues in the context of offshore wind tenders.
A methodology to estimate the environmental and technical feasibility of the innovations in
offshore wind turbine foundations was developed by integrating various assessment tools.
This methodology combines Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) for evaluating environmental
impacts, Wind Resource Assessment using WAsP software and case studies such as
the Saint-Nazaire Offshore Wind Farm. These approaches were used to estimate the
potential contribution of each innovation to sustainability goals, including Biodiversity Net
Gain. The integration of these models allowed for a comprehensive evaluation of how
each innovation would perform in real-world offshore wind farm development, taking into
account both environmental benefits and technical feasibility.
The results indicate that innovations like Artificial Reefs and Reduced Noise During Pile-
Driving are highly advantageous in terms of their environmental benefits. These technolo-
gies not only contribute to biodiversity enhancement but also offer significant potential for
improving the overall sustainability of offshore wind projects. As these innovations are
increasingly valued by stakeholders and regulators, they are likely to play a key role in
enhancing the environmental reputation of offshore wind farms, ultimately giving them a
competitive edge in the bid process.
In contrast, Gravity-Based Structures and Suction Buckets, while technically feasible,
present more mixed outcomes. Although these technologies contribute to cost-effectiveness
due to their installation methods and ability to function in specific site conditions (such as
soft clay soils), their environmental impact is relatively lower compared to other innova-
tions. Nevertheless, their role in reducing overall project costs and ensuring technical
feasibility still positions them as viable options for offshore wind development, particularly
when cost constraints are a priority.
The comparison of Germany, the Netherlands and Denmark revealed explicit priorities for

each nation connected with regulatory frameworks and environmental objectives. To that
end, both Artificial Reefs and Reduced Noise are typical German and Dutch innovations,
revealing strong stakes in biodiversity protection and noise pollution mitigation. Denmark
similarly prioritizes these two innovations but has a very strong focus on noise reduction.
This research underscores the growing importance of integrating environmental consider-
ations into the bid criteria of offshore wind farms. The integration of Biodiversity Net Gain
into the bid process offers a meaningful way to align offshore wind projects with interna-
tional sustainability goals, while also ensuring that the offshore wind sector contributes to
biodiversity conservation. The findings also highlight the increasing role of sustainability
as a competitive factor in bidding processes, which will likely continue to shape the future
of offshore wind energy development.
Further Work
Although this thesis may provide explanations on how innovations can influence the course
of bidding and integration of environmental criteria into the offshore wind turbine project,
there are several research topics that remain.
Another development of interest in the years to come will be data-driven optimization mod-
els able to integrate real performance data from each innovation. Such models would be
capable of making more dynamic assessments of technical, environmental and economic
feasibility from actual project data regarding innovations. This would enhance accuracy
in feasibility studies and push forward decision-making processes at an early stage in the
development of an offshore wind farm.
The second very important line of further research deals with long-term, in-depth analysis
of environmental impacts assessed in innovations. This thesis did consider the potential
of Biodiversity Net Gain, but the reflection on what such long-term technologies would
contribute to the change of landscapes and ecosystems has not been fully accounted for
in the research.
Another topical area is engagement with stakeholders. Whereas this thesis focused on
the technical and environmental issues of innovations, further research might be done
regarding stakeholder perspectives of regulatory bodies, local communities and environ-
mental NGOs. This may help to understand how such groups perceive the importance
of various innovations and, consequently, how such understanding could lead to drivers
of certain technologies and the integration of environmental criteria into the bidding pro-
cess. Stakeholder engagement could also shed light on how policies and regulations
influence innovation adoption, potentially uncovering opportunities for alignment between
regulatory frameworks and technological advancements.
Addressing these avenues for future work will contribute to the offshore wind industry’s
continuing evolution in a manner that balances technological innovation with cost-effectiveness
and environmental protection.

Step 1: Structural Stability of Suction Buckets
The stability of a suction bucket is governed by the interaction between the bucket and
the soil, ensuring that the foundation can withstand environmental loads (e.g., wind, wave
and current forces). The suction force required to install the suction bucket is given by:
Finstallation = σsoil × Abucket
(21)

strength of the soil (assumed to be 0,5 MPa for soft clay), Abucket is the surface area of
the suction bucket.
