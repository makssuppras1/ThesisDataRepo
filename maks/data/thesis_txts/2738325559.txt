Abstract
A complementary characterization of iron-carbon-based surfaces, where the co-deposition
of Wolfram and the introduction of pulsed deposition are analyzed and compared to a gal-
vanostatic Fe-C coating. Annealing to near-operation condition test the thermal stability of
the coating in the wear test where annealing at 240◦C shows improved wear resistance.
The co-deposition of Wolfram shows that Wolfram in the sold-state solution with the iron
α network at 9 w%. The annealed Fe-C-W failed the wear test, which was possible be-
cause of the formation of a new phase observed in SEM that forms during annealing. The
microhardness remains stable in the 700-800 HV range. Nano grains of 20-60 nm are
observed with columnar grain growth. XRD line analysis reveal the crystalite size Fe-C
to be about 10 nm and Fe-C-W to be approximately 8 nm, both of which are growing with
annealing time. Pulse plating parameters are discussed to improve the properties of the
coatings for further research.

1
Introduction
Iron carbon based surfaces are an environmental friendly coating solution that shows
unique properties. The coating is a candidate as an alternative solution to the long-time
industrially used hard chrome coating. In this work a pulse plating method in the depo-
sition process is tested along with co-deposition of wolfram. The use of pulse plating
and co-deposition of Wolfram is expected to enhance the properties of the coating and
change the microstructure and morphology. The coatings are annealed to test for ther-
mal stability at an expected operating temperature. The characterization of the coatings
is based on the mechanical testing of microhardness and the wear test. Microstructure
analysis is performed with X-ray diffraction (XRD), energy dispersive analysis (EDS), light
optical microscopy (LOM), and scanning electron microscope (SEM). Phase analysis and
line profile analysis are performed to investigate microstrain and crystllite size’s and to
analyze the if Wolfram forms new phases. EDS is used to determine the chemical com-
position of the coating with co-deposition of Wolfram. SEM images are used to observe
the topography of the coating, as well as cold-embedded cross sections of the coating to
determine the growth mode. With LOM measurement of microhardness and wear track
analysis is carried out.
Electrochemical deposition parameters are optimized during the experimentation of mix-
ing speed during deposition Layer thickness measurement is performed. In addition an
optimization of the deposition process is performed and recommendation to improve the
coating is proposed for further works.

Literature
In this section, the literature related to the project will be briefly introduced. First, a section
on the literature relating iron carbon coatings: Fe-C followed by a section on iron, carbon
and wolfram coatings. Electroplating using a pulsed current is presented.
2.1
Iron carbon coatings
Iron carbon coatings are obtained with the electrodeposition method using an electrolyte
bath [1]. Iron carbon based surfaces are suitable for industrial applications where wear
resistance and hardness are in demand. The microhardness of Fe-C coating has been
reported in the range of 600 HV to 800 HV in as deposited state [2] [3] [4] [5] [6] and up
to 800 HV and 1300 HV when annealed. The increasing microhardness with annealing is
seen as an outstanding property of the material and a confirmation of its thermal stability.
The microstructure has columnar grains, which are in the nanosized size range of 20 to
50 nm [4] [5] [6].Culumnar grains or ”needle-like grain elongated” structures, as labeled
in [5] are reported to have an aspect ratio of approximately two or three.
The phases present in as deposited state is α-iron (JCPDS card 6-696), and in a pre-
ferred orientation of <311> as reported by [2]. In addition, a nameless phase that does
not belong to the iron phase is observed with phase analysis[3] [7]. The coating shows
leveling effects, which level out unevenness in the substrate. This is a useful property
when applied to damaged surfaces [8]. When the coating is annealed, the microstructure
retains its mechanical properties. Microhardness has been reported up to 1300 HV when
annealed isotherms at 300◦C for 24 hours[3]. When Fe-C coatings are annealed, the
highest microhardness is archived when annealing to 400◦. When exposed to annealing,
oxides and carbides are forming from 250◦C. Oxygen and carbon react with iron and form
oxides and carbides that are small in size and evenly distributed. Oxygen and carbon are
present from the deposition process and are present in microvoids and grain boundaries,
of which there are many with nanosized crystallites in the range of 10 µm.
A compression and bending test of the Fe-C coatings has been conducted by [5] showing
that the coating failed between maximum stresses of 400 and 1800 MPa, with a sudden
catastrophic fracture mode. When investigating the fractures surface, elongated struc-
tures are visible and fragments in powder-like particles of elongated shapes indicate an
intercrystalline fracture. In a bending test the sample breaks into two pieces with a sharp
interface at even lower stresses between 100 and 190 MPa.
Upon annealing the Fe-C coating to 800 ◦, spherical precipitation is observed in the range
of tens of nanometers and up to 1 micron in diameter[5].
2.2
Pulse plating deposition
Electroplating pulses in the deposition process can be a tool to create different microstruc-
tures in coatings. By using a pulse, the growth of the nucleation site is restarted every
cycle, and the grain size is reduced. The work [9] finds that pulse cycles can be used to
create a multilayered coating and control as grain size of as coating according to the pulse
cycle parameters. The pulse can be unipolar or bipolar, changing the current from flowing
in one direction to switch direction with a given cycle. The possibilities are countless. The
settings can impact properties such as impacting the yield stress and the form ability of

2.3
Fe-C-W coatings
Working with a iron carbon wolfram coating, the litterateur is sparse, and with pulsed cur-
rent (PC) where any published literature is not found. Non-published reports from DTU
have been used as a reference point in the project. In the work by [12], experimenting with
the plating parameter is carried out primarily by observing the coating appearance after
deposition. Selected samples have been investigated with X-ray Fluorescence (XRF), X-
ray diffraction (XRD), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and energy-dispersive X-ray
spectroscopy (EDS).
The electrolyte used contained 1,8 L Di water with 80g/l or 144g of Fe2SO4 , 11,3g/l or
20,3g of citric acid, and 3g/l or 5,4g of Na2WO4 sodium wolframate. The parameters
investigated are substrate, pH level, electrolyte age, and plating rate. The results show
the best results when using a fresh bath less than 24 hours and with a pH of 2,9- 3,5. The
plating efficiency is proportional to the pH level. At pH below 3 the plating efficiency is
approximately 35 %, but it improves around the pH level of 3,5, reaching 70 %. With the
high plating rate obtained by the high pH value, the stripes visible on the coating surface.
The stripes can turn into halos-shaped features that have a center with a very thin coating
of bright color A.1. The stripes formations are determined by combinations of parame-
ters, hydroxide evolution, wolfram content, pH, and stirring direction. The stirring of the
electrolyte is important as it moves hydrogen bubbles from the surface of the coating. In
XRD measurements, it is found that the α-ferrite peaks have shifted to the left at a lower
2θ and the conclusion is that Wolfram is in solid solution in the α-ferrite lattice. EDS re-
sults show a wolfram content between 8 and 10 w% in the coatings. In analysis with SEM
many craters is observed in one sample and a hypothesis given is that it related to the
hydroxides species evolution during the electroplating process.
An Fe-W coating is described in [13]. Fe-W coating with Wolfram content of 4 a%, 16 a%
and 24 a% show a rough surface for 4 a% and smooth for higher atomic percentages. In
the as deposited state the microhardness is reported to span from 7-11 GPa (800-1100
HV) for the compositions mentioned above [14]. The electrolyte used contains glycolic
acid and citric acid; beyond iron sulfate and sodium wolframate, it must be assumed that
carbon is incorporated into the coating even though it is not their intention. Carbides and
oxides in reaction with iron and wolfram or both develop with annealing temperatures up
to 800 ◦C. The microstructure has also been found to transition into an amorphous phase
with increasing wolfram content [15]. When the coating is annealed to 400 ◦C in 30 min,
dispersed crystallites of 20 nm diameter appear. This is associated with an increase in
microhardness. It is found that hardness increases with wolfram content in the coating
where a concentration of 0,4 mol is related to the largest microhardness [16]. It is reported
that Fe-W coatings in [17] have a specific wear rate of 11 ∗10−6 for an Fe-W coating with
24 a% of Wolfram. The Specific wear rate is improving further when annealed to higher
temperatures partly due to formation of carbides with wolfram and iron.

3.1.1
Electrolytes
Two electrolyte compositions have been used in this project one is for plating with iron and
carbon and another one when wolfram is added to the composition. The Fe-C electrolyte
is identical to the one used in other research papers [2]. The electrolyte for Fe-C-W is from
a recipe recommended by my supervisor based on a non published report[[12] which is
briefly presented in chapter of Literature.
Fe-C electrolyte bath
To prepare the electrolyte the first step is to heat up 1,6 L DI water to 50 ◦C. The beaker
with the water is placed on a IKU C-mag HS7 heater with magnetic stirring. or an IKU RCT
standard 2, to control the temperature and mixing speed. A thermometer is attached to
the heater and placed approx. 5 cm above the bottom of the beaker. In the beaker are
a small cylindrical magnetic bar to stir electrolyte with the dimension of 8 mm in diameter
and 20 mm long. On the edge of the beaker is a plastic holder to which the the sample
is attached during the deposition process. The approximate distance between the iron
anode and the sample holder is 60 mm.
While the water is heating prepare the chemicals. The citric acid can be added immedi-
ately, but the iron(II)sulfate need to added when the temperature reaches 45 ◦C When
Citric acid is added the solution appear yellow, see figure 3.2d. The color changes again
when iron sulphate is added and the color turn slightly darker with a tint of green. When
the content is dissolved and no particles are visible in the liquid, add additional DI water
to reach a solution size of 1,8L. The pH level needs to be adjusted. The Fe-C solution
need to have reduced the pH level from approximately 2,9 pH in as mixed state to 2,50.
According to [18] the optimal pH value to electroplate Fe-C coatings is between 2,5 and
2,8. The pH level in the solution is then shifted to 2,50 by adding HCl with a pipette while
measuring the pH level. It is always the ambition to reach pH 2,5 as the pH level increases
during the electroplating process. The HCl dissolves very quick and the pH level stabi-
lizes. An iron anode with approximately length off 20 cm long and 5 cm wide is added
to the solution. When a new bath has been mixed, a run in test should be performed by
electroplating for 30 min plating time dc. After every electroplating process the pH need
to measured and adjusted. Since the substrate is manually placed every time with new
copper wire the position deviate with approximately 10 mm and the angles as well. The
beaker is placed in a fume hood for safety reasons.

Fe-C-W electrolytes
Wolfram as a new alloying element was and to find the best plating parameters beyond
the recipe the plating. The first step was to test whether it was possible to reproduce the
results of the previous DTU report [12] using the same parameters as for the Fe-C coating
except for the new recipe:
• iron(II)sulfate FeSO4×7H2O: 80 g/l,
• Citric acid C6H8O7×H2O: 11.3 g/l,
• Sodium wolframate Na2WO4×2H2O: 3 g/l,
• Temperature: 50 °C,
• pH: expected optimum 3.1 and 3.2 at least in between (3 -3.5).
• Current density: 3 A/dm2,
• Stirring: 1 level (240rpm)
It was a recommendation to run it at small electroplating times to begin with of 1 hour, it
was uncertain if the pH and the electrolyte would remain capable at longer plating times.
Additionally, it was recommended to avoid hydrogen bubbles on the surface, as they
would cause craters on the surface of the coating. The recipe for Fe-C-W coatings for
a solution size of 1,8 liter is shown in table 3.1. It contains higher concentrations of iron
sulphate 144 gram and citric acid 20,3 gram. The sodium wolframate content is 5,6 gram.
for the solution of 1,8L.
For the Fe-C-W solution, the citric acid and iron sulphate is added following the same
procedure as for Fe-C described. In addition, sodium wolframate is added to the solution
as the last chemical. The wolfram takes some more time to dissolve. The mixing speed
can be raised during this process. When the solution is mixed, it appears even darker than
that of the Fe-C electrolyte. The natural pH level of Fe-C-W solution is approximately 2,3
and the optimal pH level is 3,10 to 3,20 according to the recipe. The solution is adjusted
by adding NaOH to the solution. Upon addition of NaOH to the solution, small metastable
agglomerations precipitate in the solution. At a higher pH level, the precipitates are stable.
The desired pH level can only be measured when all precipitates are dissolved. In contrast
to the Fe-C solution, the pH level does not stabilize fast and this process can take 20
minutes depending on mixing speed and how much NaOH is added each time. The mixing
speed should be reset to the correct setting for the deposition process.

3.1.2
Dc and Pulse plating
The electroplating process can be divided into two sub-processes, the galvanostatic elec-
troplating and pulse electroplating.
Galvanostatic electroplating
The Galvanostatic electroplating is carried out using a EA-psi5200-10 A power supply that
delivers a constant DC output to the iron anode that is placed in the beaker of 1800 ml.
The plating current used in the project is 3A/dm2 The actual current for the 50x50mm
steel plate used in this project is 0,75A. The use of iron anodes is to control the Fe(II) ion
in the electrolyte and not to produce Fe(III) ions.
Pulse electrodeposition
The Pulse electrodeposition (PED) process is carried out using a Dynatronix DuPr10-3-6
Pulse Power Supply. The setup is similar to Galvanostatic electroplating, except that there
are more electroplating settings that need to be controlled. The pulse TimeON indicated
for how long in the pulse cycle the current is flowing. The pulse TimeOFF is the length of
time a current is paused before a new cycle starts. A pulse cycle time is defined as the
length of TimeOFF and TimeON the pulse on and pulse off times. In figure 3.3 borrowed
from [10] the cycle is defined as current on time plus off time. Ip is the current peak

The first step is to pinch holes in each corner for cobber wire attachment, followed by
measuring the substrate on a scale and measure the weight in grams with 4 digits. The
plate is then covered or masked by tape on the backside. Cut excess tape off at the edges
with a scissor or a sharp knife. The copper wires are then attached to each of the holes in
the two corners as seen on figure 3.4. It is important to ensure a rigid connection between
the copper wire and the substrate to ensure good conductivity. The copper wire is twisted

degreasing for 30-60 sec. The substrate is then sprayed with Di water from a spray where
the cyanide can be washed down into bath again and not carried on to the beaker with
Di water. The activation of the substrate surface is done to achieve the best bonding of
the coating with the substrate. Then the substrate is immersed into H2SO4 for 30 to 60
seconds. When the substrate is removed from the electrolyte, it is immediately placed in
the beaker with Di water. It is now ready to be insert into the electrolyte bath.
When the deposition process is finished the sample is immediately moved in the beaker
with Di water, and immediately cleaned with ethanol. Drying is done with a hair drier using
no heat at ambient temperature. Alternative drying is simply laying the sample on paper
and carefully drying all liquid off with it is also good method.
When samples are drying with the hair drier, the last spot on the coating to dry up is prone
to have blueish corrosion layer. Wolfram samples are more susceptible to corrosion than
Fe-C coatings in the drying process. During drying process the de-masking should also
be done. The best way to hold the sample is to first move the plastic tubes on copper
wires as far from the sample as possible as they contain water that can run on to the
coating. then take the sample up from the beaker and turn the sample up and the copper
wires down while spraying with plenty ethanol immediately followed by blowing cold air on
it. Place the hair drier on a stand from the beginning so de-masking can be done with the
free hand. When the sample is de-masked and dry, the copper wires can be cut off and
the sample placed on a scale. note down the weight. Eventual dendrite formations and
edge that break off is not included in the as deposited value as these do not contribute to
the actual coating thickness that can be calculated. When the sample mass is measured
the the sample is wrapped in paper and pace in the plastic bag labeled according to the
labeling procedure explained in a coming section and placed in an air tight box.

3.2
Annealing
Choosing the right annealing method required many considerations. The annealing of the
coating would best be carried out at temperatures that reflect potential operating condi-
tions since this would reflect the exposure to temperature in use. The sample is prone
to oxidation even at slightly elevated temperatures. Therefore, an inert atmosphere is
required to avoid oxidation of the sample surface. Another important condition is the
dissolution of some as-deposited phases, including the carboxyl group which has been
previously observed to happened at around 259 C according to [4]. At this stage, forma-
tions of carbides and oxides are developed from freed carbon, oxygen, and hydrogen. An
isothermal heating is favored to observe the kinetics of phase evolution. Several heating
techniques have been considered and tested. Vacuum furnace with an argon gas con-
trolled atmosphere, a lead bath in a conventional muffle furnace, and an oil bath. Alter-
natively, a normal air furnace could be used when the coating is protected from oxidation,
being embedded in evacuated glass capsules.

05 edge, BMK 05 E, BMK 07 R1. The heating profile for BMK 05 E was recorded by

Oil bath
An oil bath Julabo SE 6 from Buch and Holm was available. The oil bath is a solution
tested on several samples; initial, it was recommended to only use the oil bath at 190◦C.

at maximum 250 ◦C as working temperature. For safety reasons and because of the risk
of oil aging in recent years with possible property changes, as supervisor recommended

perature as high as possible, the oil bath was tested slowly increasing the temperature
until it reached 240 ◦C in this state. When the oil came in contact with the air, a lot of
smoke developed. In addition, a bad smell of oil was spreading in the laboratory. At that
point, it was decided not to go higher. The slow increase in temperature in the oil bath
was followed by annealing of sub-samples.

observed, probably a corrosion of some kind. This was not ideal and, to avoid it, a sub-
sample was wrapped in stainless steel foil, which was successful. The samples did not
miscolor any longer. The best method discovered was to use 240 ◦C, while holding the
sample in a basket as seen in figure 3.11a, and the oil bath was covered with a lit as good
as possible.
For cooling, a beaker with the same oil as in the oil bath was used at room temperature.
The sample felt cool immediately after it was dipped in oil, and the beaker did not heat
up at all. The cooling process seemed to be very efficient. The oil bath was assumed
to have a uniform temperature everywhere in the bath. For good measure, the samples
were aimed to stay in the middle of the bath every time. To ensure that the oil really was
240◦C the oil bath was heated up and keeped at temperature for long period of time be-

3.4
Mechanical testing
3.4.1
microhardness measurement
Hardness indentation test using a Struers Dura Scan microhardness tester. To find the
ideal force, ISO 14577-4:2016 [22] recommends making a range of hardness indentation
starting with a low weight and increasing it until a deviation of hardness is observed. For
a proper hardness test on coated surfaces, the applied force may cause only deformation
in the coating and not in the substrate. A soft substrate will not be able to support the
indenter, and deformation of the substrate reduces the outcome of the hardness test.
To find the proper indention size, the indention must have only a depth of 1/10 of the
thickness of the coating according to [ASTM]. For Vicker indentations the indention depth
is 1/7 of the diagonals.[23].
The hardness measurement is done for the 6 coatings in as deposited state for the 3
annealing temperatures on the surface a total of 24, including the as deposited samples.
Microhardness is also achieved on the embedded cross section of the 6 coatings. The
hardness measurement is performed on a Dura scan and with 25 gram 3.17a. To mea-
sure the hardness of the coating, it was necessary first to find an estimate for the layer
thickness. The lowest layer thickness measured is 25 µm. This measurement, together
with test indentation, showed cracks at the end of the diagonals when a load of 50 grams
(see figure 3.18b) or more was applied, which concluded that the indentation load must
be 25 gram; this load is also used by other studies [2] see figure 3.18a.
To measure the diagonals the Dura Scan could not provide sufficient images of the in-
dentation of 100x magnification would while correctly focusing and 50x was too small
magnification to measure the diagonals. Alternatively, the LOM was an opportunity but
also a table top SEM was tried out. The table top SEM was available, but the indention’s
were impossible to locate, and the process was too time consuming. The LOM was also
easy to access with top-down view. It was possible to make 100x magnification images.
During the first stage of the project, the expected hardness after annealing was approx-
imately 1200 HV and 800 HV as deposited [2]. With the uncertainties of the Dura scan
and the measurement from image to image, it was expected to be sufficiently small to pro-
vide acceptable results. The first step was to test different loads on the Fe-C-Constant
sample as a baseline. It was difficult to get really sharp images of the indents. 25 gf was
suggested as a good load and it turned out that the use of 25 gf provides diagonals in the
range of 7-10 µm on the samples as seen in figure 3.18a. The automatic measurement
function was unable to recognize the indentations.
A light optical microscope was used at a 100 x magnification lens, and the resolution im-
proved as seen in figure 3.18c. Further test indents were placed on different samples.
Some uncertainty was present. The measured hardness values were sensitive to small
changes in diagonal length. In an attempt to improve the measurements, a tabletop SEM
was used, but the setup time for tabletop SEM and the sample preparation time were too
high. Together with the lack of availability of the table top SEM it is not a feasible solution.

for all the samples is analyzed, it is not possible to prove the existence of any carbide or

fraction that is too low to be detected.
The peak position of the Fe-C increases showing that there is an increasing and then
decreases showing severe stress changes as seen in table 5.8. For Fe-C coatings, there
is a significant increase in 2θ occurs after a short annealing followed by a reduced 2θ
value for the 3 hours of annealing compared to the as-deposited state. An exception is
Fe-C-10-Pulse, where there is very small difference when comparing the as-deposited
over 30 min with 3 hours of annealing. This indicates significant changes in stress in the
coating as a function of annealing.
The 2θ increase the from the as deposited state tensile stress is introduced to the coating
followed by a change to compressive stress. This could show that something, probably
hydrogen, is leaving the interstitial sites causing the lattice to contract and introduce a
tensile stress in the lattice, after 3 hours the tensile stress has been relaxed and tensile
stress could be introduced during the electroplating process, explaining why 2θ is reduced
to a point lower than as deposited.

5.7
Summary of Fe-C coating
Fe-C coating improves wear resistance when annealed, and the effect is already seen
after 10 minutes at 240 ◦C. The pulse plating method with high frequency, as seen with
Fe-C-10-Pulse, has an even better wear resistance than the other coating, when an-
nealed. This trend also continues with friction coefficients. The deposition rate of the
coating decreases when pulse plated. The microhardness does not show any significant
changes when pulsed compared to the galvanostatic method when annealed, the coating
hardness does not change much and is stable at 240 ◦C.
There is variation in the hardness measurements within very few µm can probably not
only be from the contribution the measurement procedure, but is a variation of the micro-
hardness in the coating.
All samples have a preferred orientation of the crystalitte as expected seen as the ab-
sence of 200 peak. There are macrostrains in the coating as the peak position is shifted
to the left indicating a tension in the coating. No other phases are detected than the α-
iron, confirming that the lattice structure is a Bcc of iron. The crystallitte sizes are in the
ranges of 10 nm, and the crystallite size increases for 211 peak to 12-13 nm. Cracks in
the coating appear to cleave in a brittle manner as seen in figure 5.33. Surface cracks
are detected on all samples after annealing, and they correlate with the release of hydro-
gen from the coating. The crack modes observed from the surface are seen in a similar
surface to the crack of the substrate from cross-view.
The microstructure of the coating is confirmed to be nano sized grains 20-40 nm. The
grain growth is in columnar growth mode (FT). Observations of the growth show that it
initiates at the substrate and spreads in an ellipse shape, which needs to be confirmed
by investigation with TEM. Gaps are observed in the coating from topography and also
from SEM images with the cut confirming the presence of the gaps further down in the
coating. These gaps represent a porosity of some kind. There are really no correlations
between columnar grain length and pulse plating as expected according to theory. The
leveling effect of the coating is observed and confirmed by other works[8].

Summary of Fe-C-W Coating
The deposition rate of coating is lower for the pulsed deposition method. The coating layer
thickness distribution is fairly even in the center sub-samples (A-I) and has a thick coating
thickness on the edges. The topography contains a rough waviness in the surface of the
coating in the µm range. In the nanometer range, the grain size is in the range of 20-60
nm for the Fe-C-Constant and 20-40 nm for the Fe-C-W coating. Nanosized grains are
confirmed for both coatings. The agglomerations of grains seen from the topography view
seem to be more dominant in the microstructure of the coating. Columnar grain growth is
observed for both coatings. The ellipse growth shapes are observed.
In the anneal state spheres are observed in the Fe-C-W coating and are increasing in
number with annealing time. Minor sphere-like structures are observed, they have regu-
lar coating microstructure inside the incipient sphere i contrast to the large spheres where
the surface appears white and smooth. EDS analysis shows that the chemical content of
the Fe-C-W coating is uniform everywhere in the coating as well as in the features. The
Wolfram content is stable around 9%. oxygen content in Fe-C-W-500-Pulse is approxi-
mately 17% and in Fe-C-W-Constant it is between 11 and 15 %.
Microhardness is around 700 HV a large increase is seen for Fe-C-W-Constant with an-
nealing, for FE-C-W-500-Pulse is there no effect of annealing. In the wear test, all an-
nealed Fe-C-W coatings show spallation of the surface. The as deposited coating has a
cross sectional area of the wear track of 4535 µm2 for Fe-C-W-Constant and 2132 µm2
which is low.
From the XRD analysis is there a shift for iron 211 in the 2θ position indicating that the
wolfram is in the substitutions position in iron lattice. This is confirmed by phase analysis
showing that there are no other phases than α-iron in the Bcc structure. The crystallite
size is clearly below 10 nm in the as-deposited state and the crystallite size grows with the
annealing time. 2θ position increases showing more tension with annealing time. Cracks
form on the surface of the coating with perpendicular intersections. At microscale HIC
associated stepwise cracks are seen showing directional tensions in the coating according
to HIC theory.

Discussion
6.1
Pulse vs constant current in electrochemical deposition
The electrochemical pulse plating deposition method for coating open for large parameter
space for optimizing and manipulating the coating. The primary idea was to investigate
the the grain growth and nucleation of the α-ferrite phase in the Fe-C coating. By using
pulsed deposition the grain size should be smaller and shorter by increasing frequency
of the cycles in the deposition process. It was expected to see these changes in the mi-
crostructure according the theory [9] [10] this was reflected in the results where the where
crystallite size from XRD peak 211 seen in table 5.8 has not shown any significant trend
that back up the theory. Neither has the SEM images off the cross sections perspective of
the coating showing the growth mode, shown trends following the theory. For Fe-C-1000-
Pulse the theoretical growth length pr pulse is 4,7 nm which is half smaller than the grain
sizes observed. This rate is reduced with increasing the frequency which indicate that
the other important parameters also affect the grain growth morphology and grain size.
Other parameters to alter the properties would be TimeON in relation to the pulse limiting
current density (IL) and the current peak IP . To alter the grain growth morphology it could
possible be done by a higher IP and a shorter TimeON than used for pulsed depositions
[10]. The parameters used for pulsed coating does not change the condition to change
the morphology enough to observe any changes.
6.1.1
Fe-C coatings
To investigate the effect of pulse plating a comparison of galvanotastic deposition method
used on sample BMK 05 and the pulsed samples BMK 07, BMK 09 and BMK 27. The
macroscopic appearance of the coating is seen in figure 5.1. The samples does all have
a similar dark shiny surface appearance as associated with incorporation of carbon[26],
[27], independent of pulse deposition or galvanostatic deposition method as seen in other
works [8].
Layer thickness can be compared according to deposition rates seen in figure 3.6. The
deposition rate slightly decrease using a pulsed deposition method when time contribu-
tion Fe-C-Constant has a deposition rate of 0,0090 g/m and 0,0082 for Fe-C-10-Pulse.
The deposition time is basically the double of Fe-C-Constant when the full duty time is
considered due to the higher frequency of the Fe-C-10 pulse. The time off during the duty
cycle could possible be shortened which would reduce the deposition time.
The growth mode is a columnar grain structure, that can be observed for Fe-C coating.
From investigating the coatings columnar structure there are few differences if any, be-
tween pulsed and galvanostatic depositions. When comparing sample BMK 09 with (Fe-
C-1000-Pulse) in figure 5.25 with sample BMK 27 (Fe-C-10-Pulse) seen in figure 5.27 the
grain sizes is of approximately of the same size. The grain size is in the range of 20 to 50
nm which confirm the nano crystalline grains, observed from other Fe-C-Constant coating
presented in other works [7].
The growth mode in all coatings can be categorized as Field-orientated texture crystallite
growth (FT) [1] [28]. FT growth is associated with a reduced diffusing limiting that creates
an over potential with the present current density in the deposition process.
When investigating the grain growth from a cross section perspective of the columnar
grain, there is a deviating structure near the substrate. The growth of the columnar grain
begins from the nucleation sites and spreads as an ellipse as seen in figure 5.21. This

structure is seen for all Fe-C samples.
The grain size observed for Fe-C coatings from the surface topography in as deposited
state, as seen in figure 5.12, is as narrow as 20-35 nm, where the agglomerations of

to determine any effect of pulse plating with the parameters used in relation to grain sizes

6.1.2
Hypothesis for growth mode in Fe-C coating
When considering the topography of the coating, the columnar grains do not grow to the
same height at the nano level, some grains attract the material in the competition for iron
in the deposition process, as seen in figure 5.20. The amount of metal ions available to
be discharged is limited, which favors grain growth that has grown more. initiation sites
where nucleation has happened first is as seen in figure 5.21. The grain growth is af-
fected by the diffusion limiting current because as edges have more metal ions available
in the electrolyte, in addition to the higher local current density at the edges. The growth
of the plate coating thickness is limited by the reduced current density and the diffusion
limitation of iron ions. The layer thickness distribution follows the distribution when there
is thinner thickness in the center of the coating and thicker layer thickness further away
from the center. Within the 9 subsampels (A-I) of the Fe-C coating have 15- 20 µm.
When investigating grain growth from a cross-sectional perspective, columnar grain growth
is present for all samples. Near the substrate, the nucleation sites have taken place at
specific points, where the grain growth spreads in all directions. As the layer thickness
grows, the grain growth direction turns to a direction perpendicular to the substrate in
the shape of an ellipse. The growth of ellipse-shaped grain bundles is observed in all
samples in the range of 10-15 µm from the substrate, where the ellipse-shaped growth is
replaced by a growth direction perpendicular to the substrate. This ellipse growth is also
recognized in the SEM images of article [2] without being described in the article. In other
articles, it is difficult, but possible, to see ellipses in [5] and [8]. The ellipse-shaped growth
indicates that the growth mode changes due to some changes in the growth condition. If
the growth spread out to begin with, the nucleation rates are rather sparse compared to
the grain growth rate. It is more thermodynamically favorable for continuous growth com-
pared to the nucleation of new sites. The effect of pulse plating deposition method does
not alter the conditions sufficiently to change the thermodynamics of the growth mode.
The driving force to create nucleation sites with a radius greater than the critical size ra-
dius( r∗)of the nucleus is not promoted by the pulsed plating. The ellipse shape of the
growth is an observation that would need to be evaluated with TEM.
The coating topography does not differ when investigating the surface topography of the
pulse samples in SEM. The columnar growth of the deposition of Fe-C coatings is re-
flected in the topography. The columnar grains growth at the surface does not grow to a
height equal to that of a smooth surface on the nanoscale. The surface contains gaps,
as seen in A.35. The gaps are formed when columnar grains lose in the competition for
iron ions. The local current density increases where the grains grow ahead of the other
columns. The higher current at the growth point and the available number of ions may
fulfill the criteria for nucleation. The gaps that are discussed may be irrelevant, yet they
are observed. It is also observed that the cracks run in intergranular in the same area as
the gaps are and possible in the same direction as the columnar grains, relating the gaps
in the coating and the cracks running from surface to substrate with the ellipse growth
mode would explain the weakness the Fe-C (and Fe-C-W) coatings have with crack prop-
agation. This is a hypothesis that would need to be proved.

Conclusion
Introducing pulse plating to enhance the Fe-C coating can be a tool to improve the prop-
erties. The results of pulsed samples show that there begins to be an effect of the pulse
plating parameter when the TimeON is decreased to 10 ms on the properties. From the
theory the applied TimeON is important parameter to control. The results of the wear test
are promising for pulse plating with an iron carbon coating with a low TimeON in the range
of 10 ms as seen with the Fe-C-10-Pulse coating compared to the galvanostatic method.
The microstructure does not change with annealing time at 240 ◦C as seen with the to-
pography and in the cross section view. Neither is there any significant impact of the
annealing or the pulse plating on the microhardness results. There is a slight improve-
ment with pulsed deposition on the wear resistance and a large effect from annealing
when cross section profile of the wear track is analyzed and the friction coefficients. The
deposition rate is reduced when pulse deposition is used.
In the characterization of the coatings the nano sized grain structure is observed also
showing the expected columnar grain growth. The growth initiation at the substrate shows
an ellipse-shaped growth mode; this appears to be a new phenom discovered in the iron
carbon coating and would require further investigation with TEM to be described.
The Fe-C coating contain of a single α-iron phase seen from the phase analysis and their
is an indication of the preferred orientation of crystallites due to the absence of the Fe 200
peak. The crystallites are of 10-11 nm for the Fe-C coating, the crystallite size grows with
annealing time. The coating has a brittle behavior as seen from the fractures. Cracks
formed during the annealing are due to the release of hydrogen.
The Fe-C-W coatings have a different macroscopic appearance than the Fe-C coatings
with a more rough surface. This is reflected in the topography of the surface. When
codepostion of wolfram is confirmed to be substitution positioning α-iron phase due to the
peak shift that matches the woflfram content with a larger strained lattice of α-iron. The
crystallite size is significantly smaller than 10 nm of the Fe-C near 8 nm the crystallite size
grow with annealing time.
The coating does not show the same thermal stability as the Fe-C coatings with the failed
wear test, where the coatings suffer from spallations and cracks in the coating, resulting
in detaching of the coating, exposing the substrate during the wear test.
A mechanism for this is proposed with as formation of an unknown ”dark phase” that is
related to the spherical features observed on the surface. These introduce large cracks
in the coating underneath the spheres. When these spheres and ”dark phases” are ex-
posed to wear, the coating underneath is damaged, and spallation and detachment of the
coating occurs. The coating is brittle as seen with the Chevron and Hertzian cracks in the
wear track, and the penetrating cracks that run from the surface to the coating propagate
as a cleavage fracture.
The deposition parameters of the Fe-C-W coatings are far from optimum because pulse
deposition with Fe-C-W is novel field. From the experimental results, a higher mixing
speed in the electrolyte would improve the deposition rate, which would potentially change
the properties of the coating in combination with experiments with other pulse plating pa-
rameters. Fe-C-w coatings neither improve microhardness as expected

Future Work
The application of the iron carbon-based coatings is limited to the lubricated condition
because of the susceptibility to corrosion. Therefore, a test of the wear resistance in
lubricated conditions is appropriate to conduct. The wear resistance observed in dry con-
ditions provides mostly scientific results, but testing the lubricating coating would be a test
that can convince the industry.
The outcome of the Fe-C-W results is suboptimal, and the coating does not show the same
thermal stability as without wolfram. This challenge can be overcome by optimizing the
deposition parameters and increasing the wolfram content in the coating[16]. The pulse
deposition parameters depend on the deposition processes in the Fe-C-W electrolyte that
appear to have a different optimum.
The columnar grain growth, which has growth in an ellipse shaped mode, must be un-
derstood in relation of crack propagation and fracture. A TEM analysis would be able to
examine the grain structure.
The variation in microhardness observed should be investigated in the perspective of
small nanosized carbide, etc. possible is causing variations within a few micrometers or
it is related to the gaps or porosity’s formed by the columnar grain growth. With the crack
propagating from the surface to the substrate, it could be favorable to use pulse deposi-
tions to form a multilayer structure that is capable of blocking the cracks from penetrating
the coating as seen in [9] where a multilayer with different properties enhances the coat-
ings.
By changing the recipy of the eletrolyte increasing the wolfram content in the Fe-C-W
coating would be a promising approach to improve the Fe-C-W coating. Higher hardness
and better wear resistance have been seen from a Wolfram content higher than used in
the project, and also increasing the pulse plating and using better deposition parameters
starting with the mixing speed could yield promising results.
