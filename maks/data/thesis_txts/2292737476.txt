Abstract
This project is an attempt to create a database with adsorption energies of a high accuracy. The
database should consist of adsorption energies of atoms or simple molecules such as H, N, C, O,
NO, CO and CH adsorbed on single layer 3d, 4d and 5d fcc(111) metal surfaces. The energies
are calculated using the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) implemented in the electronic
structure code GPAW.
The studies show that it is not possible to achieve systematic convergence of the RPA calcu-
lations, within feasible cost, for this type of systems. Additionally RPA calculations prove to
be extra sensitive to the use of the Projector Augmented Wave (PAW) method. The present
GPAW atomic setups will have to be improved to guarantee accuracy.
The calculated RPA adsorption energies, which are not of the desired accuracy, do after all seem
to be sensible for these model systems when compared to other well-known functionals.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Eventually we would like to make cheap high precision calculations of adsorption energies for
systems representing real physics, since this would be an extremely valuable tool in the area of
surface science and speciﬁcally in studies of catalysis. The concept of heterogeneous catalysis is
already exploited in numerous important applications of which the most prominent maybe the
Haber-Bosch process where hydrogen and atmospheric nitrogen is combined to ammonia assisted
by an iron catalyst. The Haber-Bosch process consumes 1-2% of the world’s annual energy
supply and sustains roughly one third of the world’s population through the produced fertilizer.
Catalysts are also used in many environmental precautions where toxic or otherwise harmful
gasses are neutralized. In fact most constructions producing exhaustion employs some sort of
catalyst. Although the use of catalysts is already a huge success there is still a great unresolved
potential in designing catalysts for new processes or improving upon existing catalysts. For
instance fuel cells have the potential to play a role in the energy supply of the future but
only if cheaper catalysts can be found1.
One could go about this in many ways but with
computional atomic-scale simulations we could search for more viable materials in a screening
or try to map the existing catalytic process in greater detail in order to understand it better.
Key measures such as catalytic activity often depend exponentially on the related adsorption
energy thus working towards meV accuracy is crucial for the usefulness of the predictions. All
the theoretical and numerical machinery for reaching meV accuracy is available but given the
size of a real adsorption system it is presently very time consuming to compute. One way out if
this is to develop methods that capture the correct underlying physics while considering less of
the complexity involved in solving the electronic structure problem. The development of such
new methods would beneﬁt from having access to a database with high accuracy adsorption
energies of model systems which could be used as a reference of quality. Transition metals are
especially interesting in surface science, as they represent a wide range of properties.
Here we propose creating a database by starting with the set of adsorptives H, C, N, O, CO,
NO and CH on all the 3-, 4- and 5-d transition metals (except Technetium). The setup of all
systems will be as follows:
 All the metallic surfaces are setup in a 1 x 1 fcc(111) conﬁguration with only a single layer
of atoms.
 Each adsorptive is relaxed at 3 sites: top, bridge and hollow.
With 29 transition metals, 7 adsorptives and 3 sites this amounts to a total of 609 diﬀerent
adsorption systems. The choices to force all surfaces into a fcc structure and only include a
single layer in the surface are obviously unphysical. However, the goal of the project is not to
calculate adsorption energies for physically interesting systems but rather to make high precision
1normally platinum is the best choice.

calculations of systems small enough that a very soﬁsticated method can be applied. Since we
are only interested in creating a benchmarking database the clearly unphysical aspects of the
calculation is happily ignored with reference to the fact that physics is not important, yet!
For a given system, we need a calculation of the total energy of the metal surface and the isolated
adsorptive along with the total energy of the system where the adsorptive is adsorbed on the
surface, see ﬁgure 1.1. Thus the adsorption energy is calculated as
Eadsorption = Eslab + Emol −Eads,
(1.1)
where Emol, Eslab and Eads are the energy of the molecular, surface and adsorption system,
respectively. To compute these energies we propose using Density Functional Theory (DFT)
where the exchange-correlation (xc) energy term is evaluated by using exact-exchange (EXX)
and the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) to the correlation energy. The Random Phase
Approximation (RPA) has proven to give good results for adsorption systems [1][2][3][4][5] by
simultaneously capturing long range van der Waals (vdW) interactions and covalent bonds. The
hope is that RPA will do well for this type of systems as well (we have no way of knowing as there
is no experimental reference data). Aside from hoping that RPA will produce energies close to
the ”true” values we have to make sure that correct RPA adsorption energy is found for each
system. This involves converging the energies to within a certain value wrt. all computational
parameters. The goal is to reach convergence within 10meV since this is the order of accuracy
we want for the adsorption energies. The main work of this thesis will be to ensure that this
criteria is met so that the database really becomes of high quality. The calculations will be
done using GPAW[6], an electronic structure code where DFT and RPA is implemented in the
Projector Augmented Wave (PAW) method .

Chapter 2
Theory
This section covers the theoretical aspects of the calculations. There isn’t developed any new
contributions to the used methods thus the purpose is merely to create an overview of the back-
ground and a connection between the methods.
This theoretical chapter covers the formulation and interpretation of DFT and RPA along with
the connection between the two. The RPA is derived fairly thorough as it is the main interest
of this study. The chapter also touches the implementation of RPA in GPAW as well as some
general considerations regarding RPA. Finally, PAW is introduced as it is relevant to study how
the PAW generated atomic setups inﬂuence RPA calculations.
2.1
Density Functional Theory
DFT[7][8] is the big framework that is the backbone of this project and it is a well-established
and widely used method for solving electronic structure problems. DFT is basically a method
for solving the many-body non-relativistic time-independent Schr¨odinger equation
H |Ψn⟩= En |Ψn⟩,
(2.1)
where H is the Hamiltonian, n is the quantum state label, En are the eigenenergies and |Ψn⟩
are the full many-body eigenstates. The many-body Hamiltonian has the general form
H = Te + Tn + Vnn + Vee + Vne,
(2.2)
where Te and Tn are operators of the kinetic energy of the electrons and nuclei respectively
and Vnn, Vee and Vne are operators of the nuclei-nuclei, electron-electron and nuclei-electron
interaction respectively. Using the Born-Oppenheimer approximation the nuclei are viewed as
static thus eliminating Tn and reducing Vnn to a constant. Vnn together with Vne can be viewed
as an external potential, Vext, in which the electrons move. The Hamiltonian can therefore be
expressed as
H = Te + Vee + Vext.
(2.3)
The crucial element in DFT is the Hohenberg-Kohn theorems[7], stating that
1. The external potential, and thus the ground state, is uniquely determined by the ground
state density, n.
2. The density that minimize the energy functional is the true ground state density.
3
==PAGE:9==
From this follows that the energy can be written as a functional of the electron density and that
minimizing this functional will yield the correct ground state energy. Another important part
of the construction of DFT is the assumption that we can deﬁne a non-interacting system1 with
same ground state density as the interacting system. In the non-interacting reference system it
is straight forward to calculate the kinetic energy which is otherwise diﬃcult to handle. The
energy is expressed as a functional of the electron density
E[n] = Ts[n] + Eext[n] + EH[n] + Exc[n],
(2.4)
where Ts is the kinetic energy associated with the non-interacting reference system, Eext is
the energy due to the external potential, EH is the Hartree energy and Exc is the exchange-
correlation energy. The xc-term is where all the complications from the many-body problem is
put. This includes exchange: repulsion from having electrons of the same spin close together and
correlation: the long range Coulomb interaction where the position of one electron is dependent
on all the others as well as correlation initially ignored when calculating the kinetic energy
in the non-interacting reference system. The correlation missing when calculating the kinetic
energy originates from the Kohn-Sham (KS) approach which assumes that the many particle
wave functions are identical to a Slater determinant of single particle wave functions. However,
this is not true and the error needs to be approximated.
The method for ﬁnding the the ground state energy is to minimize the energy functional wrt. to
a Slater determinant |ΨKS⟩using Lagrange multipliers with constraint that the total number
of particles must be conserved. This yields the KS equations

2.1.1
Jacob’s Ladder
The approximation to the xc-term can be achieved by a range of approaches which can be
characterized and neatly ranked after complexity. Here complexity refers to the computational
eﬀort required by the method which of course should reﬂect a corresponding level of accuracy.
The most common way of ranking the functionals was introduced by Perdew and Schmidt[9] and
is called Jacob’s ladder, see ﬁgure 2.1. At the bottom of the ladder is the Hartree approximation
and at the top we have the ideal of complete chemical accuracy. Pursuing ab initio methods one
cannot avoid eventually facing a problem of inﬁnite proportions as we move towards chemical
accuracy. At the top of the Jacob’s ladder we currently have methods such as RPA, conﬁguration
interaction and coupled cluster, all in principle introducing inﬁnite pertubations of states. In
practice we truncate the inﬁnite sums so that they still provide the desired level of accuracy.

Despite of this, these methods are very heavy and can only be used on systems of a limited scale.
While we wait for computers to get fast enough, we can try to ”cheat” the ladder by constructing
empirical or partly empirical functionals that will bring the accuracy of a higher level down to
a more reasonable level for a limited range of systems. Many such hybrid-functionals already
exist, for instance B3LYP, HSE, PBE0 or BEEF-vdW, but in the case of adsorption systems
there is no satisfactory method because it is diﬃcult to simultaneously catch the physics of the
short range covalent bonding and the long range van der Waals interactions. In order to put
the RPA calculations in a context each system is also calculated with four other functionals,
representing the current ability to calculate the same value at a lower cost: LDA, PBE, RPBE,
and BEEF-vdW.

The Perdew, Burke and Emzerhof functional
The Perdew, Burke and Emzerhof (PBE) functional [12] is a member of a family of functionals
called general gradient approximantions (GGAs) which is located one step closer to heaven on
Jacob’s ladder. The idea behind the GGAs is to expand the LDA by including the gradient
of the density in the evaluation of the xc-term. The GGAs are thus still local approximations
and hence also lack the non-locality required to capture long-range interactions. In short, the
PBE-functional generally does not capture adsorption energies and dispersive interactions very
well. The PBE-functional is, however, known to produce good values for lattice parameters and
bond lengths in metals and molecules, along with reasonably precise cohesive and atomisation
energies [13][14][10]. To sum up, the PBE-functional is good for many things and in this study
it plays a special role as it will stand alone in the calculation of:
 the lattice constants of the metals.
 bond lengths of the molecules.
 the relaxation of the adsorptives on the slabs.
 the wavefunctions to be used as input for the RPA calculation.

2.2
The Random Phase Approximation
The Random Phase Approximation can be introduced in diﬀerent ways, eg. Green’s function
based many-body pertubation theory, Feynmann diagrams, or through the Adiabatic Connection
(AC) and Fluctuation-Dissipation (FD) theorem. The latter is presented here as it can be put in
the context of Kohn-Sham DFT which is a natural starting point for any practical application.
The derivation is inspired by [17]. First the setting of the AC is introduced, then the known
terms of (2.4) are identiﬁed and we are left with a formal xc-term. The xc-term is related to
the density response function through the FDT and ﬁnally the RPA is applied to approximate
the response function.

result is a method that, as mentioned, has proven very useful for quantitative modelling.
2.2.5
The forces and troubles of RPA
As mentioned the great advantage of RPA is that it includes van der Waals forces which are
introduced by the non-locality of RPA3. This strictly derived non-locality is special to RPA
compared to standard DFT functionals. RPA is as such a superior method but since it is much
too demanding for self-consistent implementations we must ﬁrst make a converged DFT calcu-
lation and use the resulting wave functions as input for the calculation of the RPA correlation
energy. What is really appealing about RPA is that it is to a much larger degree ab initio
than the functionals from the lower levels of Jacob’s ladder. Or put in another way; the RPA

The singularity in the RPA correlation energy
In the correlation energy term the singularity appears in the vχ terms. For bandgap systems

The singularity in the exact-exchange energy
The singularity in the exact-exchange energy can be treated by the approaches above however
the exact-exchange energy is not only interesting when working with RPA but with a wide
variaty of ab initio methods, therefore, there has been developed better ways of handling the
singularity. One much used approach for the exact-exchange is to apply a method introduced
by Gygi and Balderschi[26] which deal with the singularity of the second term of (2.33) while
neglecting long the wavelength contribution in the ﬁrst term. The singularity is replaced by an
average around the singularity calculated using an auxiliary function with the same singularity
as the Coulomb kernel. Recently it has been shown that Wigner-Seitz truncation of the Coulomb
potential is a more general method and it observes faster k-point convergence. The Wigner-seitz
truncation is adressed in the next section.
Wigner-Seitz truncation
Recently it has been shown [27] that truncating the Coulomb kernel at the supercell of volume
NkΩis a relatively eﬀective and general way of handling the singularity. A theorectical analysis
considering the Wannier functions of the system reaches the conclusion that a truncation of
the Coulomb kernel on the supercell results in convergence properties determined by the decay
of the Wannier functions. This leads to exponential convergence for insulators. In metals the
valence electrons are delocalized which means the Wannier functions only decay as R−2, where
R is the distance from the considered atomic site. In the end this leads to k-point convergence
following L−2 for zero temperature, where L is the distance between nearest-neighbour k-points
in an equidistant grid. Comparing versatility and cost, the Wigner-Seitz truncation turns out
to be the winner. Although the convergence for metallic systems isn’t as good as we would
like, it is still at least as good as any of the other avaliable methods. Using a minimum-image
convention method, the truncated Coulomb kernel is approximated by an expression where the
singular point now has a well-deﬁned limit. The derivation and practical implementation of the
truncated potential is thus rather technical, however, in order to understand why Wigner-Seitz
truncation works, one can look at it like this: Imagine truncating the Coulomb potential at
box with sides a. In reciprocal space this correspons to neglecting anything inside a box with
sides 2π
a around each G. This way the singularity is avoided. A strict treatment wants to avoid
explicit neglect of the singularity (to ensure good convergence) but we cannot ﬁnd the correct
plane wave representation of the Wigner-Seitz truncated Coulomb potential in a straight forward
analytical manner, hence the use of the minimum-image convention.

2.4
PAW
GPAW, originally called GridPAW, was exclusively a ﬁnite diﬀerence implementation based
on the Projector Augmented Wave (PAW) method. PAW, ﬁrst introduced by Bl¨ochl[29], is the
scheme used to obtain a feasable representaion of the electronic wavefunctions of the atom. PAW
generalizes its predessor the augmented-plane-wave method[30] (APW) and can be reduced to
another earlier method, named the pseudopotential method[31]. The need for an approximate
method arise from the orthogonality of the wavefunctions which results in sharp oscillatory
features close to the nuclei since all the states are non-zero in this region. Such sharp features
are very demanding to represent and requires a very large set of plane waves. Luckily, the wave
functions are smooth away from the core region thus leaving us with a conﬁned problem. See
ﬁgure 2.2 for an example of a PAW atomic setup and [32] for all the standard setups of GPAW.
2.4.1
A PAW introduction
The overall strategy of PAW is to introduce a sphere with radius, rc, where the inner part
deﬁnes the augmentation region and the outer part deﬁnes the bonding region.
The exact
single particle wave functions are then kept in the bonding region while the augmentation
region is approximated with smooth auxilliary functions.
To put this in formal terms, we
consider the Hilbert space deﬁned by the so called all electron (AE) wave functions. The AE
wavefunctions are the full one-electrons Kohn-Sham wave functions4 and are the physically
relevant wavefunctions. We seek a linear mapping taking us from the AE Hilbert space onto

2.4.2
Frozen core approximation
For the sake of completeness it is mentioned that any pracical implementation of PAW rely on
the frozen core approximation. Consider treating all electrons fully for any atom. Then any
system with atoms of high atomic number would be unneassacarilly hard to treat because of
the amount of electrons that had to be considered. Many systems might even be completely
out of range. To avoid this situation we assume that some of the states of the isolated atom are
naturally localized within the augmentation region and that they do not change when forming
bonds. This is in general a good approximation but it can introduce errors in some cases, e.g.
when using GW to calculate band gaps of semi-conductors, including ”semi-core” states in the
atomic setup can make a diﬀerence. Since RPA and GW are somewhat related methods it is not
unthinkable that missing semi-core states in some of the GPAW atomic setups could inﬂuence
the results.

3.2
Preparation of systems
Before going on with the crucial part of this work, converging the adsorption energies to within
a 10 meV precision, a few words on how the systems are setup will be in order. The procedure
for preparing adsorptives, surfaces and adsorption systems is as follows.
 The reference molecules are relaxed in a 10 ˚A × 10 ˚A × 10 ˚A cell with a Quasi Newton
method until all forces are below 0.05 eV/˚A.
 For every surface, the optimum lattice parameter is found. This is done by calculating the
energy of the surface for diﬀerent lattice constants around the experimental value. A 3.
order polynomial is then ﬁtted to all the points and the minimum is found.
 Every adsorbate is then relaxed on the top, bridge and hollow sites on every surface. This
is also done until all forces are below 0.05 eV/˚A.
The parameters of these calculations have not been thoroughly converged, but the choices should
be reasonable which is all we need for our purpose. Should one be interested in these anyway,
the chosen parameters can be found in Appendix G along with the ﬁnal lattice parameters and
surface-adsorbate distance in Appendix H.

3.4.2
Plane wave cutoﬀ
The expansion of the PS wave functions in plane waves has to be complete in principle. In
practice, however, this is a parameter that has to be converged to within the desired precision.
The needed value of the plane wave cutoﬀis generally linked to the smoothness of the partial
pseudo wave functions of the atomic setup. The smoother pseudo partial wave functions the
smaller the nescessary plane wave cutoﬀis. It will be a common thing for all parameters that it
is far too demanding to individually converge each system. Instead the systems are assumed to
be so similar that thoroughly converging one or two systems will be suﬃcient, since the other
systems should exhibit the same convergence properties. One should keep in mind that this is
a technical parameter with no direct physical relevance.
3.4.3
Vacuum and response function cutoﬀ
The unit cell vacuum refers the size of the unit cell which should be big enough that interactions
between repeated images are negligible. For the molecules, the parameter is equal to the length of
the unit cell sides. For the adsorption-system the parameter refers to the distance (z-direction)
from the adsorbate to the repeated image of the surface above.
Slabs are put in unit cells
identical to the corresponding adsorption-system. This parameter is a consequence of the choice
to use periodic boundary conditions. The system we want to model is an isolated surface forming
a bond with an atom or a molecule. In the plane the surface can be considered inﬁnite from
an atomic perspective. Thus the periodic repetition in the plane is a very accurate model but
in the z-direction the repeated images are really not part of the system we want to model,
hence the need to decouple images in z-direction.
Similarly, for the molecules we want the
energy of a completely isolated molecule thus we need to decouple interactions from images in
all directions.
The response function cutoﬀwas explained in section 2.3.4. As the number of plane waves
changes with the size of the unit cell, any given Eχ
cut will result in a varying number of included
bands and plane waves for varying unit cell size. This means that vacuum and Eχ
cut have to be
converged simultaneously.
3.4.4
k-point sampling and Fermi-temperature
Although converging plane wave cutoﬀand unit cell vacuum can be a time consuming process
it is relatively straight forward to converge them by increasing the value. Converging k-point
sampling of metallic systems is, however, so diﬃcult that another convergence parameter, the
Fermi temperature, has to be introduced. As the Fermi temperature by construction change the
convergence of the k-point sampling these parameters correlate strongly and have to be converged
simultaneously. Before going on with the implications of the diﬃcult k-point convergence, let
us look at why metals are diﬀcult to handle.
In principle we have to perform an integration over the BZ in order to construct the correct
density. But in practice we are of course left with a ﬁnite sum of k-points where each k-point is

3.4.5
Previous studies on converging RPA energies
There have been published work on how to succesfully do RPA calculations using GPAW and
the similar VASP4 implementation.
Molecules are diﬃcult in a PW-implementation because of the expensive treatment of vacuum
and the computational cost which scales as ∼V 3. The previous studies report 20meV-50meV
uncertainty on atomization energies for molecules like H2, N2, O2 and CO, [28] and [34]. This is
possible because of the error cancelation between identical unit cells, so we can expect problems
as we are not able to rely on error cancelation for the unit cells of the molecules.
For metals, most of the trouble is experienced when handling k-point convergence. Kresse found
that a total neglect of the singular point for both EXX and correlation is the best choice[35].
Using identical Γ-centered k-points for EXX and correlation each of them converge slowly but
at the same rate and from opposite sides. In this way EEXX

adsorbed on transition (111)-surfaces with 4 layers[34]. Here they also used a (12 x 12) k-point
grid for Cu but observed a Fermi-temperature dependence and found Te = 0.01 eV to be well
converged. They claim an uncertainty < 5 meV per atom. So, it seems that the Fermi surfaces of
slabs are more complicated and additionally we can expect a further complication from choosing
a true 2D surface. The response function of 2D-systems vary more rapidly in reciprocal space
than bulk systems and thus require more dense k-point sampling, [36] and [37].

3.6.5
Conclusion on accuracy of the calculations
It turns out to be diﬃcult to guarantee the accuracy of the of results for the chosen systems.
 First of all, there is the PAW completeness issue which is an issue for any type of system.
The errors might be canceled out when looking at energy diﬀerences but the true error is
diﬃcult to determine and more thorough tests should be performed. A better approach
would be to create a new set of PAW setups designed to guarantee the quality of linear
response function methods.
 Secondly, there is the PAW augmentation sphere overlap which is a result of large cutoﬀ
radii combined with unphysical conﬁgurations yeilding too small bond distances. Again
the true error is diﬃcult to determine but we should deﬁnitely avoid the overlaps if we
want a ﬂawless result.
 Thirdly, it seems very demanding to achieve proper convergence for the isolated molecules
because typical convergence schemes rely on error cancelation which isn’t present here.
Changing the unit cell of the molecules to a unit cell identical with the two other systems
provides extra error cancelation. However, there are still huge problems in obtaining k-
point and response function cutoﬀconvergence. Better convergence might be possible with
more realistic systems but this would mean handling much larger unit cells and increased
number of atoms which requires sacriﬁcing signiﬁcant resources to complete the database.
In section 5.2 we present a set of recommendations on how to proceed in the endeavour to create
quality reference calculations.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and Outlook
5.1
Conclusion
The purpose of this work was to create a reference database with RPA adsorption energies.
This was not achieved because of problems with converging the energies satisfactory wrt. the
computational parameters. Most problematic was the combination of diﬃcult transition metals
in a 2-D structure, Fermi-temperature and k-point convergence. On top of this the atomic PAW
setups of GPAW proved inadequate because the high energy states included in the evaluation of
the response function isn’t properly represented. Another problem related to the PAW setups
is that the unphysical 2D-systems results in structures with overlapping PAW augmentation
spheres.
Nothing decisive regarding the scale of this failure was found, however, it should
deﬁnitely be avoided since the PAW implementation assumes no overlap.
Using not fully converged RPA calculations, it was possible to observe trends which make the
assumption that RPA is good for this type of model system plausible.
However, the many
sources of error disqualify the results from being used as high accuracy reference values.
So, what is the prospect of creating the reference database? The question is answered in the
next section where a promising development in the RPA method is presented along with a
set of recommendations on how to create a high quality reference database of RPA adsorption
energies.
5.2
Outlook
An improved implementation
It should be pointed out that RPA is still a relatively new area of interest for the computational
physics community and as any other young method, RPA is naturally developing rapidly and
in many diﬀerent directions. Both improvements, striving to improve the quality of RPA, and
approximations, reducing the cost of RPA, are in focus. It is thus very diﬃcult to predict the
route that RPA will take in the future but I would like to highlight an approximation that should
excite anyone with less than unlimited resources. The development was presented by the Kresse
group in [38] where they rewrite the non-interacting response function in terms of Green’s
functions. Two Green’s functions, one of the occupied states and one of unoccupied states,
are then calculated at a complexity scaling as NoN 2
P W and NuN 2
P W respectively.
Through
fast Fourier transforms and a contraction of the Green’s functions the response function is
recovered at a complexity scaling approximately reduced by a factor NkN χ
P W compared to the
old implementation. The cost on precision is only in the order of µeV per atom. Using this
42
==PAGE:48==
implementation, the cost of RPA is similar to the cost of exact-exchange calculations and in the
paper they can do calculations on units cells with 216 atoms at modest cost.
Recommendations
This list of recomendations on how to proceed with creating a reference database includes some
points which have been thoroughly argued for in this report, other points are a result of my
intuition and the experience gained from working with the subject.
1. Create a new set of improved setups by introducing extra projectors and partial waves.
The setups should conserve scattering properties up to 400eV-500eV above vacuum level.
The new setups should recover the correct free electron scaling of the response function
and hopefully improve Eχ
cut convergence.
2. Avoid overlapping augmentation spheres either by choosing more realistic systems or by
reducing the sphere cutoﬀradius (or both).
3. Implement the ACFD version of exact-exchange with Wigner-Seitz truncation. This will
hopefully result in error cancelation between the EXX energy and correlation energy.
4. Implement the alternative way of calculating the response refered to above. At the moment
we are stuck with very small systems that are very diﬃcult to converge properly because
choosing larger systems would demand alot of resources. The improved implementation
would open for choosing larger systems where systematic convergence is easier to achieve
without blowing a hole in a supercomputer.
5. Choose systems with 4 layers or more. Others have shown that these systems are, if not
easy, then easier to converge.
6. When the above is done... start over!
