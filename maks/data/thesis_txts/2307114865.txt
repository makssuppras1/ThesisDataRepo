Preface
This Master thesis was prepared at Schneider Electric and at the department of Civil
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements
for acquiring a Master of Science in Sustainable Energy. The thesis were carried and
completed in a time period from January to July 2016. The main supervisor is Hongwei
Li (DTU Byg). The supervisors from Schneider Electric are Janus Christiansen and
Steffen Iversen.
Kongens Lyngby, July 04, 2016

1.1
Introduction
District heating (DH) is considered an essential energy contribution to achieve the target
of European Union in its effort to reduce the greenhouse gas emissions.
DH is also
considered as an asset regarding the energy efficiency, security of energy supply and
the decrease of CO2 emissions. The technology has been well developed in Denmark,
Sweden and Finland for many years and is still expanding. DH networks cover between
80 % and 90 % of the heating demand in the central city areas. In the biggest city of
these countries, main of the DH networks have been installed 50 years ago. The state
of components and pipes are deteriorated due to their long operating period. They are
therefore reaching the end of their lifetime and due to be replaced.
Even though DH is a well known technology, many areas of improvements remain to
be investigated in order to make it more efficient and less costly. DH network represents
a huge investment and maintenance costs due to the installation of the infrastructures
and pipes. Leaks in a network represent a major source of problems because it gives
rise to an increase of energy production of heat and higher electricity consumption of
the pumps due the increased pressure losses in the pipes. Leaks have thus a real impact
on the total NPV costs that include energy costs, maintenance costs, heat losses and
interruption costs. In addition, it is important to cover the energy demand while having
a respectful energy cost for the consumer and producing the energy in an environment
friendly way by wasting as little as possible, hence the incentive of reducing leaks in a
DH network. The same goes for water distribution networks. Leaks in water networks
engender larger consumption of energy from the pumps.
Unfortunately, there is today no affordable technology that can survey the leaks
closely at every time and everywhere. Moreover, the leaks are hardly predictable because
the life time of pipes depends on many factors that contribute to the fatigue life of pipes,
such as corrosion or stress. These factors will deteriorate, on a short or a long term, the
pipes until cracks appear and the failure happens. However, it is known that the level
of operating pressure and temperature has big impacts on the life time of pipes. The
variations of temperature and pressure inside the network create a certain level of stress
depending on their amplitude, range and frequency. These variations are represented
and named as cycles. Each cycle will cause a damage on the pipe and will provoke its
final failure after a certain number of these cycles. The theoretically number of cycles
required to reach the failure of a pipe under a stress range is given by a fatigue life
analysis, which is described by the S-N curve (See Chapter ??).
The goal of this is then to analyze the stress caused by pressure and temperature

2.1
Fatigue
2.1.1
History of Fatigue
The phenomenon of fatigue is known since many times. It has been observed on my
processes that a material could break, for instance after bending a bar many times, it
comes to fail. August Wölher, a German railway engineer, was the first person to find
a correlation about fatigue around 1850 [27]. It characterizes the fatigue behavior of a
material due to cyclic loading. This correlation is translated by a curve which is defined
by stress amplitudes versus number of cycles to its failure. The designation of this curve
took his name, Wöhler Curves, also called S-N Curves or Fatigue Curves. At this period,
railway transports were expending more and more. The incentives of his study were due
to many accidents occurring on railways due to railway axle failures. Thanks to his
investigation, he could anticipate the problem of failure to some critical components
and reduce the number of accidents. He conducted experimental tests on few materials
such as steel and iron to determine their fatigue curve. A. Wöhler is considered as the
precursor of fatigue analysis.
Soon after, engineers started to be concerned about fatigue and performed over
80 studies [18]. These studies were more focused on understanding the evolution and
formation of cracks in materials due to cyclic loading. They have been extended not
only in the railway area but also in other sectors such as in civil engineering or in
aerospace engineering. Later in the 1960’s, these efforts have conducted to two new
methods to predict the life time of materials. One, called the Coffin-Manson approach
which focuses on prediction of crack growing and another called LEFM (Linear Elastic
Fracture Mechanics) that explains how evolve cracks in materials.
A trend is quite obvious today when engineers want to reduce the fatigue on their
structures or components. They will rather investigate the reasons of the failure within
their understanding to decrease the stress cycle than implementing new components
with materials more resilient.
2.1.2
Definition of Fatigue Life
Fatigue life defines the life time of a material to which it can endure during its life a
certain number of stress cycles before its failure. The term ”fatigue” is used because the
failure occurs after a long period of stress or strain cycling. The failures of a pipes can
be translated by the rupture of the welds or at the joints to the junction between two
==PAGE:24==

pipes, or important cracks that conduct to leakages. Depending on the stress level and
the frequency of these cycles, the life time can be lowered or increased. Obviously, life
time differs from the type of materials. Their manufacturing and processes can again
influence on its life time.
The fatigue life time of a material was first predicted by
performing laboratory testings based on statistics. Many tests have to be made in order
to obtain reliable results. A study states that fatigue is the first cause of failure of metals
failure. They estimate it at 90 % of on the overall metal failures. The terms of ”low” or
”high” cycle fatigue analysis are generally used to determine which category of fatigue
the material is subject to. High cycle fatigue analysis is associated with the number of
cycles above 104. It is often used for structures analysis with vibrations. While the term
of low cycle fatigue analysis is associated when the number of cycles are below 104. It
is applied for materials which endure high pressure or temperature level.

2.1.4

2.1.4.1
Definition of S-N Curve
The section ?? clarified how the importance of fatigue analysis and what the fatigue
curve refers to. S-N curves differ from a material to another. They are represented as a
plot of amplitude stress S versus number of cycles to failurs N on a logarithm scale. The
curve of a material was obtained by performing tests under different conditions (stress
level, temperature, frenquency, etc). The usual procedure is to start by performing test
on large stress until the failure of the material, where low number of cycles is expected.
Then they decrease the stress and stop the tests until few material samples resists if the
number of cycles is higher than 107 [17]. Basically a material fails around 108 cycles.
Once the tests done, the curve can be drawn for the stress amplitude applied with the
corresponding number of cycles to failure.
As general rule, S-N curve will expose a
negative slope. Greater the stress amplitude is, less the number of cycles. Nowadays,
finite element method (FEM) are used to define the S-N curve.

2.2
Rainflow Counting Method
2.2.1
Approach
The rainflow cycle counting method (RFC) is became nowadays the method the most
popular tool in industry for fatigue analysis.
This technique is used to assess the
cumulative damage on materials due to stress from long random stress histories. The
stress histories is translated by cycles and the RFC method allows to convert these
cycles into damage on materials. Peak and valley (reversals) define the maximum and
the minimum stress of a cycle. This analysis is usually performed over a long period of
time and requires enough process data to calculate the fatigue over this period. Also
called rainflow counting algorithm, this fatigue analysis method can be applied in many
sectors such as in aviation, aerospace, building or again wind turbine. This algorithm
has been developed by M. Matsuishi and T. Endo in 1968 which is based on Palmgren
and Miner’s linear cumulative damage rule. The RFC method is called this way to
describe the process of the water going down of a pagoda roof, as illustrated in Figure
?? and ??.
2.2.2
Example of RFC Method: M. Matsuishi and T. Endo
Based on Matsuishi and Endo’s method, several similar techniques for complicated
loading history have emerged since, for both uniaxial load time history and multiaxial
load time history. Here, only uniaxial load time history will be discussed. Within this
context, few algorithms have been developed such as the three-point and four-point cycle
counting techniques. Beneath, an example of rainflow cyclic method from Matsuishi and
Endo extracted from Y. Lee and T. Tjhung’s jounral [14] is described. The solutions is
given just after.
• Procedure for RFC method:
1. Rotate the loading history 90° such that the time axis is vertically downward and
the load time history resembles a pagoda roof.
2. Imagine a flow of rain starting at each successive extremum point.

2.3
Systems of Leak Detection
The importance of detecting leaks in district heating networks is essential in term of
environmental and economics aspects. The water losses mean waste of energy and so
money. Regarding Swedish district heating companies, the losses of water is huge. In
Sweden, the losses represent several million of cubic meters of water per year [10]. There
is a lot at stake. The networks have to be renovated.
Leakage tools have already been implemented to monitor leaks present in an existing
network. The common way is to use an airborne thermography. It has the same principle
than the thermal camera to locate the thermal losses for buildings. A plane flies over
the city to map the pipelines network to detect the heat losses and leaks. The thermal
map shows emitted radiation of the network to indicate the concentration of heat losses.
==PAGE:38==
20
2 Background
The main leaks will be located and the pipes will be then renovated of replaced, whilst
the minor leaks will be monitored in the future. The airborne thermography has the
advantage to be able to cover huge area and everywhere, where some locations cannot be
accessed from the ground such as industrial or private sites. Moreover, from the thermal
maps problems of pipe insulation can be detected. This technology is definitely a good
way to prioritize the renovation of networks according to the significance of leaks [11] [7].
Pipe manufacturers such as Logstor propose new systems for leak detections in real
time through a surveillance system [15]. They developed new types of pipe where they
incorporate a copper sensor wire inside the insulation. This wire allows to detect the
moisture and the insulation prevent the water from the ground to come inside. If a
leakage occurs, the wire will detect the water.
The previous technologies exposed are usually used for ”water” leakages in district
heating network. When steam is used instead, the detection by ultrasonics [25] is a
technology more compatible with and more reliable. The theory behind this is that the
leakages provoke ultrasounds with more or less intensity depending on the flow going
through the cracks. The ultrasounds intensify when the source of leakage get closer.
Leaks from the consumer side are quite common, especially at the heat exchangers or
storage tank in the buildings. In order to localize them, uranine additive is used. This
substance that has the particularity to be green fluorescent with the contact of the day
light, is a added in the water of the network. Thus, the leaks can be detected and the
maintenance can be made on the infrastructures.
These methods have shown good figures. Depending on Hot Cool magazine [12], the
city of Odense in Denmark has opted for using several techniques of leak detection to
decrease their leaks in their district heating network. By using manual checks in the
network every five years, thermographic camera every two years and the uranine additive
every three years, the amount of waster water has considerably dropped. In 1990, they
registered losses in the range of 700 m3/years/km. Today, it represents around 80.

3.1.2.1
Introduction
Within a programme of research, the International Energy Agency (IEA) has performed
a study involving several stakeholders such as institutes, engineering consultancies companies,
universities, institutions, associations, agencies or communes [2].
The goal of this
research was to estimate the number of full temperature cycles that a preinsulated
pipe could endure during his lifetime in a district heating network. The purpose of this
study was to give a recommendation of full temperature cycles for future design of DH
systems. General conclusions are drawn regarding the effect of factors on the S-N curve
and where the pipes endure the most stress in the network. In that way, they have
investigated several DH networks located in Denmark, Sweden, Germany, Korea and
The Netherlands.
3.1.2.2
Methodology
First, the procedure was to retrieve a large bunch of data for temperature. Data loggers
was previously installed in the network and gave data for a period of one year with
1 minute time step. The data retrieved contains supply and return temperatures at
strategic locations: service pipes (consumer side), transmission pipes (production side).
These locations have been made in order to analyze the full temperature cycles for
different pipe diameters and types.
Once the data gathered, they processed all the
measurements by using the method called rainflow cyclic counting in order to calculate
the number of full temperature cycles. The temperature cycles are calculated following
the Palmgren-Miner cumulative damage rule:

Here are presented the main conclusions of this research that be helpful to oriented other

on pipe location and whether it is a return or supply pipe. By comparing the number

temperature cycles is at the return service pipes.

Even though the greatest number of full temperature cycles is at the service pipes,
more attention has to be made at the tee connection, between the main pipe and the
service pipe. The tee component presents weakness and so more risk of failure than the
pipe in itself.

By using Palmgren-Miner cumulative damage theory, it is considered then that few

Approach
In order to determine the fatigue endured by the pipes through temperature variations,
the common method of rainflow cyclic counting will be used as described in Section
??. In Section ??, an equation has been explained which allows to translate the thermal
stress from the temperature variations via temperature history. The fatigue accumulated
during its life is defined by the full temperature cycles N0 and is calculated with the
Palmgren-Miner’s theory written in equation ??.
While the fatigue life of a pipe is
determined with the S-N curve that is a function of number of cycles.
The right method to define accurately the life time of a pipe would be to evaluate
individual pipe by defining the corresponding S-N curve of the material and applying
the stress (temperature history, pressure history, etc) and external effects (ground
temperature, corrosion, etc). This would have necessitated to use a finite element method
(FEM). That implies numerical techniques and thus use computational software such as
ANSYS or COMSOL. This method allows to obtain the right S-N function because it
calculate the stress concentration factors, which is the driver of the function. However,
an entire network cannot be assessed by FEM for reason of time. A general tool has
to be used to define the thermal fatigue for the entire network, hence the reputation of
the simplicity of the RFC. It is easy to implement and gives good outcomes according
to many studies performed in the industry sector.
It has to be reminded that the goal of this master thesis project is to present an
easy and reliable tool that can be developed and implemented in TERMIS. The flow
chart in Figure ?? presents the way of calculating the full temperature cycles in offline

the time series in the algorithm. The code will process the data and rank the cycles in
function of their amplitudes. Finally, the Palmgren-Miner’s rule is used to obtain the

The study of stress analysis is performed on the network with two scenarios named: Real
Time (RT) and Temperature Optimization (TO).
Real Time:
The RT scenario is simulation launched on a typical and single day during
winter. The data retrieved are measurements at the plant from the database. It allows
to reproduce a scenario by including in TERMIS these measurements at the plant for
this specific day: mass flow, supply temperature, return temperature, supply and return
pressure.
Temperature Optimization:
For environmental and economic reasons, DH networks
try to decrease the supply temperature at the plant to minimize the heat losses from
the pipes while providing the energy demand to the consumers. Two different approaches
exist for optimizing the supply temperature: they can either reduce their energy production
by lowering the supply temperature, or they can operate their plant by lowering their
operating costs. In the second approach, it requires to include fuel prices and energy
costs. The first approach is considered as environmental and the second one economical.
In TO scenario, only the first approach will be analyzed.
Note that to have good
outcomes of a TO simulation in order to be able to compare the results with the RT
scenario, it has to be ensured that the plant of the RT scenario was not already operating
with temperature optimization. Otherwise the results would be equivalent.

4.3
Discussion
In this section, several aspects are approached and discussed to clarify the methodology
and the results obtained. First of all, the results presented above are given for certain
DH network, and so the results cannot be generalized for every DH networks.
The RFC method appeared as a convenient tool to measure thermal cycles for complicated
temperature histories where the cycles change all the time. These cycles are converted
then into damage via the Palmgren-Miner’s rule that gives a number of full temperature
cycles depending on the value set for the variables b and ∆Tref. The values chosen
influence a lot the results. As mentioned in IEA research, it appears that big variations
creates more damage on pipes than small variations when the Palmgren-Miner’s rule is
used. The result of the number of FTC declines when the value of ∆Tref and b increase.
The results from the simulations have to be interpreted with attention because it does
not reflect entirely the reality.
Indeed, the consumption profile at the consumer is
the same at any nodes and this has an impact on the entire network.
The energy
consumption at the consumer is actually a fraction of the total demand. Consequently,
spontaneous consumption is not simulated since the consumption profile is set. In reality,
big variations should appear on the return pipes in both scenarios. So more thermal
stress is supposed to be involved in the return pipes. The only method to show and
calculate the damage is to installed data loggers at the consumer sides and record the
temperature histories.
The there is a suspicious about the results of the number of full temperature cycles in
the supply pipes for TO Scenario. They are in general higher compared to RT Scenario,
especially at the consumer side. The reason of this could be explain by the first cycle
presents in the temperature histories. By taking for example Pipe ”29599_” (See Figure
??), there is a drop of temperature from 71 to 65 °C. This cycle is definitely responsible of
the most of the cumulative damage calculated. The reason of this drop can be explained
==PAGE:58==
40
4 Stress Analysis: District Heating Network
by the looking at the energy demand and the setting during the same period. Indeed,
the temperature initial of temperature optimization is set at 72 °C and the power is
low. At the pipe ”29599_” the temperature starts at 71 °C due to heat losses through
the pipes and the effect of temperature optimization begins then at this point. The
temperature drops until 65 °C but it can be seen that the temperature does not drop
more than 2 °C/h because it is actually the maximum temperature change set at the
plant. The drop of temperature has of course an impact on the mass flow at the plant.
To provide the same energy, the mass flow at the plant has to increase. Therefore, if
the temperature initial was lowered, the thermal stress in the network would have not
been that high. In addition, the consequences would not be the same if this simulation
would be performed on a larger period. The effect of this big cycle would be attenuated
over a longer period.
It is seen that operating the plant in temperature optimization is among other parameters
constraint by the maximum temperature change set at 2 °C/h to minimize thermal stress.
The thermal stress could be further reduced by lowering this value but the purpose of
temperature optimization would then without interest. The supply temperature would
vary just a little. The energy produced will be not that different by operating the plant
like in RT Scenario.
It is explained above that some results are questioned because a large variation appears
at the beginning of the temperature optimization. Wide variations create definitely more
stress and can is is explained by the Palmgren-Miner’s rule. Larger is the value of ∆Ti,
larger the fraction ∆Ti / ∆Tref will be. The exponent b will increase exponentially this
fraction.
In this project, the results were extrapolated on 30 years while the simulation were
performed on one day with 5 minutes time step because of the limits of Termis. The
extrapolation of the results leads obviously to a lack of FTC. For instance, when the
operating temperature changes over the seasons. The supply temperature at the plant
depends on the total demand which is a function of the outside temperature and wind
speed. Over the seasons, the supply temperature will then vary. The resulting supply
temperature can reach 90 °C the worst the day in winter while in summer the temperature
can be around 65 °C. The thermal stress engendered by the change of operating temperature
is not shown in our daily simulation.
Simulate a period on one year would be an
appropriate time in order to extrapolate on many years. It would be expected that the
results of FTC would be slightly the same each year.
Above, it is demonstrated that the time of simulation is important to get relevant results
but the time step of the simulation has also a role to play. In IEA research, they did a
sensitivity analysis regarding the results of FTC and the time step chosen. Larger is the
time step and lower is the FTC. By comparing the results of 1 minute and 60 minutes,
the number of FTC is lowered to 56 %. A change is also present by using 2 minutes
time step instead of 1 minutes.

5.4
Discussion
This study attempts to analyze the stress caused by the pressure change. The method is
based on the hoop stress that allows to calculate the stress at a given pressure. However
this relation does not give any figure about the pressure change, hence the usage of the
cumulative hoop stress change. It adds up the hoop stress in absolute value for a time
series. This method can be used as an indicator to interpret the pressure change but
not to determine the fatigue of pipe.
The scenarios simulate different operating strategies that could be applied in real cases
but some of them present some limits and constraints such as the reservoir. In Scenario
2, a constant water mass flow is applied at both plants. When the total mass flow of the
plants exceeds the total demand, the water is stored in the reservoir. At the opposite, if
the total mass flow is below the total demand the water stored in the reservoir is used
to provide the rest of the demand. However, the total demand profile varies a lot during
peaks hours. Reservoir with huge volume needs sometimes to be implemented to avoid
a reservoir totally filled or emptied. Both cases are inconceivable because an excess of
mass flow would lead to over pressure in the network with a consequence of pipe failures.
Otherwise the total demand is not reached.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
6.1
General Conclusion
In this thesis project, Arhus DH network was investigated to define the effect of temperature
optimization on pipe fatigue. Two scenarios were performed in order to compare the
number of FTC by using the rainflow counting method. The first scenario (RT Scenario)
represents the network in its real state where no energy saving measures are implied to
the plant. Then, the second scenario (TO Scenario) defines a scenario where the supply
temperature is optimized to reduce the energy produced at the plant. The effects of
the operating plant on fatigue pipe is analyzed. The number of FTC is calculated via
the Palmgren-Miner’s theory and the two scenarios are then compared. It is found that
temperature optimization leads to more thermal stress in the supply pipes compared to
the RT Scenario.
The large pipes are less impacted though.
The number of FTC
remains relatively constant from one scenario to the other.
On another hand, TO
Scenario dissipates totally the thermal stress in the return pipes regardless the effect
of spontaneous consumption commented in the discussion Section ??. From a general
point of view, the stress is mainly located in the supply pipes.
Regarding water distribution networks, three operating strategies have been carried out
to demonstrate which one was the most convenient to reduce pressure change. The most
reliable strategy was found and as expected it is obtained by pressure optimization. The
method of cumulative hoop stress change was applied to measure the pressure change
in the network. This approach has its limits since it is used as an indicator of pressure
change and not as a fatigue analysis method. It is therefore not an effective tool because
the results cannot be translated into fatigue and the lifetime of pipes cannot be processed.
However, this method shows that one strategy improves the cumulative hoop stress
change by using one strategy. Scenarios 2 and 3 reveal a reduction of 57.7 % and 90
% comparing to scenario 1, respectively. According to the energy consumption of the
pump in the zone studied, pressure optimization leads to higher consumption because
of the pressure level but induce less pressure change.
Finally, this thesis has demonstrated the impacts and behavior of pressure and temperature
fluctuations in water utility networks by operating the plants in different ways. The
operating strategies have been discussed along this project to clarify their influence on
the network. This project gives an insight on stress analysis but the correlation between
fatigue and lifetime have not been made. The methods exposed have found limits and
have to be used carefully. It also has to be remained that the results are obtained for
==PAGE:72==
54

two specific case studies and therefore the results can differ from other projects.
6.2
Improvements And Future Works
The results and the discussion showed how the change of the thermal stress and pressure
stress was affected by the different operating mode of the plants. The scenarios used
reproduce the reaction of the network depending on the plant set up and the energy
demand. However, enhancement remains to be done in this project. For instance, the
fact of having the same energy demand profile in the network energy does not reflect the
reality. It engenders the same fluctuations of pressure or temperature in all the pipes in
the network but at different ranges. Various consumption profiles should be applied at
the consumers or a function should be created and applied to the consumers in order to
simulate spontaneous consumption.
The Palmgren-Miner’s theory allows to define the number of FTC to translate it in
lifetime for pipes. To insure the reliability of this method which is used to foresee the
lifetime of DH pipes, a statistical analysis could be performed to confirm that the pipe
failures are correlated to temperature cycles. A complete history of pipe maintenance
would be required on an entire network on several years with information such as
the location, the operating time, the characteristic of the pipe and the reason of the
maintenance. The reason of replacing or repairing pipes can be multiple. It can be
due to leaks, insulation deterioration, age etc. It is expected that the theory of the
cumulative damage due to temperature variations is confirmed but other parameters
have to speed up or slow down the deterioration of pipes such as the velocity and the
geometry of pipes. Even though Y. Chang et al claimed that the main factor of stress
is the temperature, these other aspects have to be taken into consideration.
The cumulative hoop stress change has been used as a tool to measure the pressure
change but presents some limits since it cannot be converted into fatigue.
Another
method has to be considered to determine the fatigue life with pressure variations as
fatigue parameter.
Once this theory confirmed, it would be interesting to inspect after the economic aspect
of it. By considering that some operating modes are reducing more thermal and pressure
stresses than others and so increasing the lifetime of pipe, it raises few questions and
are presented as new research areas: What would be the savings on the maintenance
cost by lowering stress ? Is it profitable to invest in variable speed pumps for operating
pressure optimization in water distribution networks ?
