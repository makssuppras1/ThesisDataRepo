Executive summary 
 
 3. 
 
Management is an essential part of running a young growing company (YGC) and proper management 
is critical to its survival and growth. Choosing the best managerial strategy for an YGC is difficult and 
varies from project to project. It should be based on appropriate strategies for project management 
methodologies (PMMs) and task management tools (TMTs), as they provide structure to a project, 
facilitate communication, establish expectations, enhance quality and promotes consistency. This study 
investigates and identifies leadership challenges for an YGC and includes a discussion of the managerial 
importance of skills, delegation and knowledge sharing. Based on these challenges PMMs and TMTs 
are analyzed with the aim of identifying which PMMs and TMTs are appropriated for an YGC in 
general. As the requirements differ across YGCs one particular case i.e., Sportyfriends will be applied to 
identify the best fit of PMM and TMT. 
First finding of this thesis is, based on literature review, that appropriate PMMs for YGCs is Agile and 
Scrum as they are easy to manage, flexible for change and provides an overall plan. The second finding 
is based on group- and individual interviews with SF’s employers here six main criteria were identified 
by SF (flexibility, time limitation, documentation and education, meeting and communication, 
teamwork and prior experience). SF’s criteria have been used to compare Agile and Scrum. Agile offers 
written documentation and regular meetings opposite to Scrum that cannot offer written 
documentation and is dependent on daily meetings. Based on the case study the best fit of PMM for SF 
is Agile for the time being as the sales team cannot have daily meetings. A future strategy for SF is 
changing to Scrum when it is possible for the sales team to hold daily meetings. The methodology of 
Scrum offers high flexibility, improved teamwork and increased delegation of responsibility. Moreover, 
selecting Scrum would be in accordance with the none-formalized methodology that the CEO of SF 
currently uses. In all circumstances it is important to acknowledge the importance of using a formalized 
PMM. Third finding is Asana and Basecamp being appropriate TMTs for YGCs. This is based on 
online research and theory on challenges for YGCs. Seven TMTs have been selected and analyzed 
(Asana, Accelo, Jira, Wrike, Basecamp, Pipedrive and Monday). To identify the best fit of TMT the 
interviews of SF employees and the TMTs Asana and Basecamp have been analyzed applying the Value 
Proposition Model. They differ in regard to the demands of SF (price, real-time communication, 
scalability, integration and user-friendliness). In regard to the highest prioritized demand, price, it can 
be concluded that Asana is the best TMT for SF at the current stage. When SF grows and need more 
than 15 users a new evaluation is recommended as demands and prioritizations for the company 
changes over time.  
The implementation part consists of a workshop with an Interactive SWOT Methodology where the 
two selected TMTs, Asana and Basecamp have been tested and discussed. The conclusion of the 
workshop is development and actions plans regarding price, scalability, integration, user-friendliness, 
missing email communication and Slack with additional features. These findings recommend staying 
with Asana. When looking at the new findings in regard to the risk of losing data and improving 
teamwork the development and actions plans recommend changing to Basecamp.

Introduction 
 
 5.  
Management is an essential part of running a young growing company (YGC) and proper management 
is critical to its survival and growth. Project leaders are constantly being challenged to deliver successful 
and effective results despite limited time and resources. Due to this pressure, well organized 
management is crucial and a fundamental and vital component in any organization (Fuller-Love, 2006). 
New entrants have the possibility of focusing and capture new growth opportunities, rather than 
following historical trends. It is important to focus in finding the optimal way from the beginning 
regarding cost savings, so the company does not have to cut later on or “die” before getting started for 
real (Probst, 2011).  
Management can simply be described as getting things done by other people. Management is a process 
regarding development, storage, retrieval and dissemination of information. Moreover, the expertise 
within an organization can support and improve its business performance (Gupta, 2000). Leaders have 
responsibility for enabling an organization to achieve its objectives. They are therefore responsible for 
planning, organizing and controlling organizational activities. Leaders set and manage budgets and 
identify ways of making sure that the budget is kept. Moreover, leaders are in charge of human 
resources, time, materials, economics, etc. (Gupta, 2000). Leaders operate with a plurality of activity, 
constantly having to switch their attention from one subject, problem or person to another. They live in 
an uncertain world, where relevant and useful information is often mixed up with gossip and 
speculation (businesscasestudies, 2018). 
Project management needs careful organization, coordination, delegation and knowledge sharing of the 
individual project member’s skills in order to secure effective and efficient work processes. 
Organizations need to realize that knowledge is a crucial resource for organizations and should be 
managed carefully. To optimize on these areas helps the leader to create overview and develop 
competences and motivation for team members (Gupta, 2000). A leadership tool will ideally contribute 
to motivate the employees. Motivation has a larger effect on productivity and quality of work than any 
other managerial aspect of the project. Mind motivation is an ongoing activity particularly important 
during crises or peaks in project work (Joana Geraldi, 2017). Knowledge about the specific challenges 
for an YGC is necessary for success. Moreover choosing the best managerial strategy for an YGC is 
difficult and varies from project to project, it should be based on knowledge regarding project 
management methodologies (PMM), task management tools (TMT), delegation and knowledge sharing 
(Matassa, 2006). 
Managerial challenges are a big issue for companies of all size. Large companies’ uses a lot of time and 
energy on their PMM as it is part of shaping the company. The wrong methodology can cause large 
issues within the company and in worse case make a company fall apart. Yet it is as important for a 
small or YGC company maybe even more important as a small or YGC is more fragile (Fuller-Love, 
2006). A review of the PMM research literature showed a general acceptance that methodologies are 
important to YGCs (Cray, 2009). YGCs are often in a vulnerable economic status and changes all the 
time during their growth phase. Moreover, it is extremely important to find the optimal TMT for a 
company to get success (Fuller-Love, 2006). There has not been carried out a lot of research regarding

YGC in relation to PMM and TMT, why the topic is interesting and relevant (Davila, 2004) (Mishra,

It is important to select the appropriate PMM and TMT as they provide structure to a project. PMMs 
and TMTs facilitate communication, establish expectations, enhance quality and promote consistency. 
Selecting the correct methodology and tool among the hundreds available can be an overwhelming task 
(Cray, 2009).  
 
5.1 
Problem definition 
This study investigates and identifies leadership challenges for an YGC including a discussion of the 
managerial importance of skills, delegation and knowledge sharing. Based on identified challenges for a 
YGC PMMs and TMTs are analyzed with the aim of answering the question: Which PMMs and TMTs 
are appropriated for YGCs in general and what is the best fit of PMM and TMT for one particular case 
of Sportyfriends (SF) an YGC?  
 
5.2 
Definitions 
In this thesis an YGC is defined as a company where the manager is independent and often also the 
owner/main entrepreneur of the company. The company is developed with capital and ownership by 
individual or small group of investors or partner (Fuller-Love, 2006). Being a young company requires 
being newly started with the possibility of applying for funds for start-ups which often means that the 
company has to be less than three years old (Innovationsfonden, 2019) (Borean, 2019) (Pre seed, 2019) 
(Capnova, 2019) (Syddansk Innovation, 2019). 
In this thesis appropriate is defined as being capable of handling challenges and continuously changes 
in an YGC. 
 
5.3 
Boundaries 
First boundary in this thesis is to investigate only YGCs. The reason for this is that an YGC is 
innovative, open minded and most important the participation can actually make an impact. Moreover, 
the constant change in an YGC causes an interesting shift from being a company without a formalized 
PMM and TMT to a company which success is depending on applying the best fit of PMM and TMT. 
Additionally, only little research regarding YGC in regard to PMMs and TMTs exists (Davila, 2004).  
Second boundary is regarding selection of PMMs as there are around 1000 different types of PMMs 
(Charvat, 2003). The selection of PMMs has been carried out with regard to the degree of flexibility and 
online research (Appendix 1).  
Third boundary is regarding selection of TMTs as there is a plurality of different TMTs. The selection 
of TMTs has been done by online research alone (Appendix 2). When identifying TMTs only electronic 
tools will be selected for analysis as electronic tools is the future in a modern company.

a discussion of the managerial importance of skills, delegation and knowledge sharing. Based on the

Theory 
 
 6.
 
Knowledge about leadership is important for success in an YGC and it is linked to the skills and 
personal background of the leader. Organizations need to accept the importance of knowledge not only 
to stay competitive, but also to become innovative (Gupta, 2000). Skills include finance, marketing, 
human resources, strategy, planning, operation management- and the individual character of the leader 
(Gupta, 2000). Each decision is influenced by both reason and motion but the weight given to each of 
these elements during the decision making process can vary widely depending on the person (Blundel, 
2008). Moreover, management also includes softer management skills such as communication, 
decision-making and teambuilding (Fuller-Love, 2006). These all has influence on which managerial 
strategy to choose. Good management and management tools can make a company more efficient and 
thereby increase the chances of success (Fuller-Love, 2006). 
Having a strong and competent leader is important for an YGC. The leaders must have knowledge 
about his/her own strengths and weaknesses, awareness of experience and moreover knowledge about 
the team member’s skills to be able to carry out correct delegation of work and responsibilities (Fuller-
Love, 2006). Another important factor for optimization of the companies’ efficiency and effectiveness 
is awareness of the importance of knowledge sharing (Hayes, 2014). When choosing the optimal 
managerial strategy, the leader has to decide which PMM and TMT is the best fit for him/her and for 
the specific company (Fuller-Love, 2006). 
In the sections below the leadership challenges in an YGC from the early development stages through 
the growth phases of the company, including the often seen crises will be presented. Additional, 
delegation and knowledge sharing will be described as it is of outermost important for the leader’s 
choice of managerial strategy, this include the choice of PMM and TMT. Furthermore, different PMMs 
and TMT will be presented (Fuller-Love, 2006). 
 
6.1 
Challenges in an YGC 
The model of Scott and Bruce have been included as it defines the growth stages an YGC will go 
though as it develops (Bruce, 1987). Each stage has its own distinctive character and managerial 
challenges (Table 1). It is the anticipation of these challenges and the management of the changes they 
cause, that will ensure success and survival. Moreover, knowledge about these challenges will help to 
decide which PMM and TMT that is the best fit for an YGC.  
The model can be used by the leader to identify the present situation of the company. It is an indicator 
of what strategies appear suitable at various stages in the growth of a company. The transition from one 
stage to the next can cause crises that tend to be disruptive. Managers have to act proactive rather than 
reactive towards these changes to minimize disruptions or crises. Prior knowledge of what generates 
crises and what to expect in each stage will ease the process of change. The models main purpose is to 
recognize what will be required from the leader in the different stages as a company progresses to the 
next stage of its development (Bruce, 1987).

Stage 1 - Inception: In this stage the basic features will be one operation unit in a single market with 
limited distribution channels and funds. To further development of the company the owner has to 
accept demands regarding finance, energy and time. In this stage the major focus changes from 
establishment to profitability. This requires a different attitude and concentration of effort. The 
administrative demands changes which create a need for formalization of systems and record keeping 
(documentation). This results in new demands regarding the skills of the manager and the features of 
the TMT. A crisis may appear if the manager cannot recognize and handle these demands (Bruce, 
1987).  
It is important to beware of how to handle the first crises in an YGC from being the only one knowing 
anything to be aware of the importance of delegation, education and employing. Even though the 
development of modern technologies and managerial tool have let to the need of fewer people in 
managing the organization, different skills are required at the different stages of growth. To optimize 
delegation knowledge about skills of all the team members are mandatory (Bruce, 1987) (Fuller-Love, 
2006). 
It is important to realize that a small firm is not a “scaled down” version of large firms as the leader in a 
small firm has much greater awareness of ongoing activity in the company. A challenges it therefor that 
the leader does not see the need for procedures to miniature performance (Fuller-Love, 2006). 
Developing an YGC is very time consuming and together with the increased administrative load a call 
for delegation of supervisory tasks is needed to avoid bottlenecks, backlogs and confusion (Bruce, 
1987). 
Stage 2 - Survival: The Company is potentially a workable business entity at this stage. As the business 
expands the finances emphasis will transform to working capital and the need to finance increased 
inventories and receivables. It is quite normal that the YGC at this stage is in need for more capital. If 
the company has established credibility a creditor financing such as a bank overdraft or short-turn loan 
may be used. Otherwise an external investor or governmental fond can help with more liquidity to

secure the survival of the firm. The product line is normally still single or limited and marked expansion 
is the way to growth with the need for expansion of channel distribution (Bruce, 1987).  
In this stage there are four major risks for crises. Firstly, overtrading with uncontrolled growth, if it is 
not properly handled it will lead to minimizing the business. To minimize this tread growth must be 
limited this may result in the company staying in the survival stage. For the company to develop into 
the stage 3 the growth must be controlled with documentation. This requires control systems and 
organization in a higher level. Secondly, the company will face increased challenges with complexity of 
expanded distribution channels operation in new geographical arears or selling to a broader type of 
customers. A way to solve this issue is to hire a supervised supervisor but this may lead to other issues 
why restructuring the current management style is a better solution (Bruce, 1987). Here the supervisor 
emphasis on delegation and coordination from a distance. New types of customers might demand the 
company to adapt it practices. Thirdly, when a new competitor enters the market there will be a change 
in the basis for competition, placing a greater emphasis on price at the expense of the differentiation. 
Economies of scale is necessary if the company chooses the price path and with it the need for 
modernization and expansion. Fourthly, the need for a more advanced information system will likewise 
increase (Bruce, 1987). 
Stage 3 – Growth: The Company should at this stage be profitable and normally the generated cash 
goes back into the company for further development and to help finance the increased working capital 
demands. Liquidity can be a major problem and despite the acceptance of the company’s products in 
the market place the company may be forced to fold. If sufficient opportunities occur, it may be 
relevant to sell the business prior to stage 4 at a substantial capital gain. Coordination is now in focus 
for the functional manager leading to a higher level of formal organization structure and a system that 
can scale up and is flexible for changes. An accounting system is at this point a requirement. To grow 
the company should initialize formal research and further development to expand the product range. 
Crises in this stage emerge from attention of larger competitors which often result in pressure on price. 
The company can respond by forfeit market share and compete on a differentiation basis with another 
focus for example on greater and more personal service. This strategy effectively means staying in 3th 
stage.  
Another solution is to expand into new markets or tailor more specific products that will be more 
useful for specific groups of customers. As a new company on a market it is not needed to target as 
large customer base as well-established companies. Key issues facing management will be financing the 
growth and maintaining control of operations. The organizational structure would have to change again 
and this time to a more professional rather than an entrepreneurial approach why flexibility and 
scalability must be given a high prioritization (Bruce, 1987).   
Stage 4 – Expansion: In this stage there is also focus on funds to secure further growth. A successful 
company track-record opens up for possibility of long term funds such as equity partners or long term 
debt. Characteristics of this stage are that the business has professionally established and systemized all 
general control such as management, budget, accountant and policy. It is important to recognize that a 
new introduced person to the company often does not have the same commitment as those who have 
been with the business from the early stage. This can lead to crises because of different cultures (Bruce, 
1987).

If the company is continuously successful operated, it can expand further or it can be sold out with a 
capital gain. The professional managers will take over more and more and the decentralization will 
continue because of that the entrepreneur will often be forced to take a new role as watchdog and 
planner instead of CEO. This can also create a crisis as it is not his nature that allowed him to set up 
the business in the first place (Bruce, 1987).  
Stage 5 – Maturity: The Company is at this stage still growing and can no longer be defined as an 
YGC. Management key issues are expense control, productivity and finding growth opportunities. 
Major investments are into marketing effort and maybe plant upgrading and maintenance (Bruce, 
1987).

(Table 5). 
 
6.2 
Individual skills and delegation 
In an YGC the leader often have to acquire all skills themselves and be able to educate or train 
employees as they do not have the economy to hire help (Fuller-Love, 2006). The leader for an YGC 
has to operate in a highly complex environment with a small number of staff and the approach is 
usually to employ someone already possessing the relevant skills and thereby save cost on education. In 
YGC teambuilding and communication are more developed as employees work closer together in small 
teams and with good communication. Teambuilding help communication, creativity, innovativeness 
and motivation (Blundel, 2008). Small companies often use ad hoc based indoor training but as they 
grow more advanced training may be needed (Fuller-Love, 2006). 
Many small businesses do not have the necessary management and leadership skills. Particularly skills 
related to managing and motivation of people are important and if these are missing the company fail 
to reach their growth potential. Successful entrepreneurs are often those that have successfully gathered 
a top entrepreneurial team that wants to work hard for a small cost as they believe in the vision of the 
company (Bruce, 1987). When a small company grows bigger than around 10 persons, it becomes 
impossible for the leader to stay an expert on all aspects why he/she has to acknowledge the need for 
appointing a supervisor or professional manager (Fuller-Love, 2006). 
People are the most valuable asset in any project. When people know what they are supposed to do, 
they can work much more effective and efficient. In an YGC the project is often uncertain and prone 
to change why flexibility and communication are outermost important (Bruce, 1987). The activity, 
priority and time for executing will change all the time. Keeping control of all these changes at a high 
level of detail is exhaustion and frustrating to all parts. Too much time is often spent on 
communicating changes and to secure that everyone will get it right. A well-functioning communication 
and documentation system is therefore needed (Blundel, 2008). Another issue is nonrealistic 
expectations and promises of a smooth process. This leads to frustration, not caused by the changes 
but because of the difference between reality and the expected. This frustration can be minimized by a 
clear strategy with a picture of the progress of the entire company and overview of own and others 
tasks including workload (Bruce, 1987). A clear management strategic can help the leader structure

projects and thereby increase the company performance. Moreover, an issue is that the work never goes 
as planned. In this case the team members have to use their own judgement to adjust the task 
accordingly. It is therefore important to have skillful and trustworthy team members that have enough 
directions to choose the right decisions. Moreover, effective practices for coordination between tasks 
must be applied (Joana Geraldi, 2017). 
People have different qualities, competences and skillset. The leader needs to have an overview of what 
the members like to do and which skillset they possess. This is in order to delegate the right task to the 
appropriate team member (Tracy, 2018). Another essential task for a successful leader is to delegate 
enough tasks to create time to carry out tasks only the leader can manage. Successful performances 
based on good delegation create confidence and encouragement. Thus it is a good idea to delegate easy 
manageable tasks to new staff and to staff that needs a boost of success. It is important to be open for 
discussion and participation from team members when delegating tasks. As there is a direct relation 
between how much people are invited to talk about the job and how they understand, accept and 
become committed to it (Blundel, 2008). Another way to motivate and create competences is to 
delegate the responsibility for the entire task. The leader also needs to be clear about time, deadlines, 
money and other human resources in addition to delegation, responsibility and tasks (Tracy, 2018). 
Moreover, outcome must be defined clearly and should be measurable to give the responsible team 
member a better understanding of expectations and encourage commitment (Tracy, 2018). 
Delegation is a complex process where the leader assigns new tasks to team members, giving 
responsibility for decisions formerly handled by the leader. Delegation is an essential element of 
effective management and it offers a number of potential benefits, both to the leader and the team 
members (Tracy, 2018). When used correctly delegation will improve the speed and quality of 
decisions, reducing overload for the manager, enrich the team members’ job and motivation. Moreover 
delegation provides opportunities for the employees to develop leadership skills (Yukl, 1999). 
Knowledge about the team member’s skills, strengths and weakness are important for correct 
delegation of tasks and responsibilities. Correct delegation leads to effective management which will 
optimize the company (Tracy, 2018).   
 
6.3 
Knowledge Sharing 
Knowledge is interesting in terms of how knowledge can exist within an organization, as well as in the 
daily life of everyone. Knowledge has in the resent years been accepted as a significant organizational 
resource (Maryam, 2001). It is important to be aware how knowledge is generated, and how knowledge 
can be shared and reused, in the most efficient way whether this is through a TMT, digital social 
networks, face-to-face encounters or a combination. Knowledge is moreover important with regard to 
competitiveness and innovation (Carneiro, 2000).  
In order to properly understand knowledge a categorizing of knowledge has proven to be a useful 
(Figure 2) (Davies, 2013). Explicit knowledge is codified knowledge. Codified knowledge means that 
the knowledge can be transferred by using a code i.e. verbal or written language or that it is already 
available in its codified form. Implicit knowledge is knowledge that has the potential to become

An optimal leadership tool should be able to collect, increase and share all internally possessed

Regarding transferring information it is relevant to focus on the “push and pull strategy” (Levy, 2015). 
A company has to decide if they want to push information towards the employees, or if the employees 
themselves should pull the needed information out. The push strategy has the advantage of ensuring 
that all employees get the same basic knowledge and therefore having a common and shared 
understanding of e.g. norms, culture, work procedures, etc. The risk is that too much information 
might end up exposing employees to irrelevant information and essentially wasting their time. The pull 
strategy eliminates this risk by letting the employees choose the pulled information in respect to what, 
when, and where (Trap W. S., 2015). However, there are prerequisites that have to be in place for the 
pull strategy to function: A good organized TMT that can obtain explicit knowledge and a social 
environment for locating implicit and tacit knowledge. These two types of information can be 
combined and should be selected in regards to the context of the situation.

6.5 
Project management methodology (PMMs) 
There are many PMMs to choose from including hybrids and offshoots of the different types, each 
with their own set of rules, principles, processes and practices all with the purpose to maximize the use 
of resources and time (Charvat, 2003). One of the earliest and most well know PMMs is the Waterfall 
model. The Waterfall model is simple and follows its steps sequentially, ending in a functioning system. 
In this way it provides the coherent and disciplined approach which has been used by all future 
methodologies. Some of many future developed methodologies were slight variants of the Waterfall 
methodology others more drastic departures (Cray, 2009). Each company must identify an appropriate 
methodology for managing their project. The chosen methodology serves as blueprints and the choice 
of method will have influence on the success of the company (Catchpole, 1986) (Boyer, 2018). Projects 
vary in scope and requirements, which means that the right methodology to implement, will vary and it 
might change over time. Management methods are used continuously, respectively over a longer period 
of time, unlike disposable analytical techniques that solve a problem or situation (Muslihat, 2018).  
A good PMM should be able to take the available data / information and use it to create a system and 
communicate the details regarding the system to the stakeholders. This can be described as the 
following three characteristics: (Catchpole, 1986) 
1) Use available information  
2) Provide a means for adequate communication among the stakeholders 
3) Encompasses all activities needed to develop a system 
PMM can assist in improving the way of handling a project to a more effective and efficient way. This 
requires recognition of organizational priorities, the character of the leader including prior experience 
and a deeper understanding of different PMMs. Project management experience is important, and this 
is where a project leader's knowledge of the pros and cons of each methodology can assist companies 
in choosing the methodologies that allow them to maximize outcome (Cray, 2009). 
The process required to assess, document and select the right PMM for each project is detailed, time-
consuming and complex initially, but worth it in the end (assuming the most appropriate project 
management methodologies have been selected) (Cray, 2009). There are a number of different types of 
project management software which have different benefits and provide value for teams of varying 
sizes and types. One of the ways to classify PMMs is by the de degree of flexibility and responsiveness 
to change (Cray, 2009) (Wilson, top5projectmanagement.com, 2018) (Guntamukkala, 2006).

Project management software is related to different task management software, created to enhance the 
efficiency. Such software helps businesses implement and execute multiple projects within the required

Agile 
The PMM Agile is a value-centered method of project management that allows projects to get 
processed in small phases or cycles (Figure 3). The methodology is flexible and can handle complex 
projects that exhibit dynamic traits, the goal being to continuously adapt to abrupt changes from client 
feedback (O'Regan, 2017). It is best appropriate for small iterative and incremental software projects 
made up of a highly collaborative team or a project that requires frequent iteration (Table 2). It is a type 
of process where demands and solutions evolve through the collaborative effort of self-organizing and 
cross-functional teams and their customers (nutcache, 2018) (Muslihat, 2018). 
Agile is based on four fundamental values: (Jovanovic, 2018) 
1. Individuals and interactions: In Agile development, self-organization and motivation are 
important, as are interactions like co-location and pair programming. Structure is not firmly 
defined.  
2. Working software: Demo working software is considered the best means of communication 
with the customers to understand their requirements, instead of just depending on 
documentation. 
3. Customer collaboration: As the requirements cannot be gathered completely in the beginning of 
the project due to various factors, continuous customer interaction is very important to get 
proper product requirements. 
4. Responding to change: Agile Development is focused on quick responses to change and 
continuous development.

The agile teams work in close collaboration with each other and are most often located in the same

Agile emphasises on collaboration, flexibility, continuous improvement, and high quality results

Scrum 
Scrum moves control from a central scheduling and dispatching authority to the individual team 
responsible of the project. The higher complexity of the project the more important it becomes to 
delegate decision making (Schwaber, 2004). Scrum relies on a self-organizing cross-functional team to 
take the feature from idea to implementation (O'Regan, 2017).  Scrum is based on iterations, where the 
team looks at the requirements, considers the available techniques and evaluates on its own skills and 
capacities. With Scrum the planning time is shorten down to a minimum to kick of the project. In this 
process an estimation of what must be done and which result is expected (Product Backlog) (Figure 5). 
The result of the iteration is an increment of a product (finish). The operation part between start and 
finish is called the Sprint. Small teams will be assembled to focus on specific projects independently 
(Sprint Backlog). Doing the Sprint the Scrum Master have daily meeting to evaluate progress or results 
and reprioritize backlogged tasks (Schwaber, 2004). The Scrum Master is the leader of a specific 
“Scrum”-project that is facilitated within the scrum sessions. The Scrum Master is the expert on the 
Agile process and acts as a coach to the team, thereby helping the team to achieve a high level of 
performance. The role differs from that of a project manager, as the Scrum Master does not assign 
tasks to individuals or provide day-to-day direction to the team (O'Regan, 2017). An entire iterative 
period is normally within a time frame of about 30 days. The result is an iterative process that shortens 
the feedback loop between customer and developer and between goal and implementation and also 
between investment and return (Schwaber, 2004). 
 
 
Figure 5: Demonstrating the Scrum methodology 
Scrum is based on the Agile framework that was explained earlier (O'Regan, 2017). It is a way to 
prevent delays in product launch because the entire process focuses on team collaboration. This is 
opposite to the traditional Waterfall method that reveals a lengthy process where planning alone could 
take a couple of months before moving to the next stage (Cray, 2009). The advantages and 
disadvantages with the PMM Scrum are shown in Table 4.

6.6 
Task management 
Task management is an activity in which an individual or leader tracks a task throughout its life cycle 
and makes decisions based on the progress. Moreover, delegating subtasks to team members, and 
setting deadlines to make sure projects get done on time (Mishra, 2013). Task management often make 
use of software tools that help effectively organize and manage tasks by using functions such as task 
creation, planning and assignment, tracking, reporting and overview. Task management will help the 
leader to analyze the efficiency of an individual, department or company (Techopedia, 2018). 
Task management software empowers team members to work more efficient and effective. A good 
TMT keeps track of all tasks in one place and thereby provides a good overview that can be used to 
track personal, group or shared tasks. This makes it possible to save time and secure that all tasks will 
be taken care of within deadlines (Mishra, 2013). With a centralized task management point, it is 
possible to track and identify a team or an individual team member based on what has been done, 
monitor the time a task is taking and to determine efficiency. This information can be used to balance 
workloads, forecast bottlenecks and guard against delays and missed deadlines (Techopedia, 2018). 
There are several major ways to manage tasks, some of which were created to help software developers, 
and some for general purposes. Some of the most efficient tools are developed by developers as they 
desperately need to stay organized and work quickly (Mishra, 2013). The most common way to create 
an overview of all tasks for a TMT is with a Gantt chart or Kanban board (Patankar, 2016).  
==PAGE:25==
Sissel Trap Wiegandt - 2019 
 
Technical University of Denmark 
Page | 24 
 
 
Figure 6: Demonstrating a Gantt chart (Dansk Byggeri, 2014) 
Gantt chart provides an overview of project timeframes. It outlines time from left to right, and the 
temporal extent of the activities is indicated by spelling, hence the name bar chart (Figure 6). The 
activities are listed above and down and have milestones for each area of action. A Gantt chart shows 
the status of the work by a change in the Gantt rods (Attrup & Olsson, 2008). 
 
Figure 7: Demonstrating a Kanban board 
A Kanban board gives a transparency of the work flow and an overview of all tasks (Figure 7). 
Typically, a Kanban board shows how work moves from left to right, each column represents a stage 
within the value stream. Work items are represented visually on a Kanban board, allowing team 
members to see the state of every piece of work at any time and who is responsible for the different 
tasks (Huang, 1996).  
To help the employees prioritize their tasks it is possible to set goals and know what are the most 
relevant tasks to finish first. This helps to organize a good and healthy work structure. A way to do this 
is to give every task a severity or/and a deadline and at the same create a time estimate of task (Huang, 
1996).

Asana organizes and gives overview of the team's work in one place including goals, calendars, files,

comparison has been carried out with six identified (flexibility, time limitation, documentation and 
education, meeting and communication, teamwork and prior experience). SF’s criteria have been

Flexibility: In the interview Morten, CEO, clarified that SF is in need of a PMM that is flexible. The 
reason for this is that SF is an YGC that changes all the time both organizational and strategical. 
Morten says: “…that is a tough assignment as the agenda changes all the time”. Both Agile and Scrum 
can handle flexibility and it is possible to change priorities and requirements easy. Why it is not possible 
to point at any of the methodologies as better than the other. 
Time limitations: Time limitations are also an important factor for SF. Morten says: “missing time as 
being a young growing company is the biggest challenges”. Both the sales department in the group 
interview and the sales manager, Axel, elaborates on this issue. Clarifying that time limitations are an 
important issue. At the group interview Lars says: “start-ups do not have a lot of resources and time”. 
In relation to time limitation the Agile methodology has the advantages of having little planning 
requirements and only regular meetings. Scrum on the other hand is depending on both more planning 
and daily meetings. There are pros and cons for both methods. Having to plan more is time consuming 
but might save time in the end as mistakes can be detected and corrected earlier on in the process. The 
same applies for the time consuming meetings. Having daily meetings takes time but mistakes will get 
caught earlier and fixed before the mistake requires additional time and challenges. Regarding the issue 
of time limitation, it is not possible to recommend one of the methodologies for SF. 
Documentation and education: Morten emphasizes that PMM need to be able to handle 
documentation as the sales team needs references when selling to different customers and for 
education. Axel emphasize on this, saying: “We should do some more knowledge sharing” and 
“become more active on our Slack channels and explain our success.” Moreover, the sales team needs 
written documentation (explicit knowledge) to be able to learn from each other. Agile has minimum 
rules and only little documentation is generated. Scrum on the other hand has no writing 
documentation, which is good as it saves time but will be very important when the YGC grows bigger. 
Written documentation is important for education and knowledge sharing as mentioned in the 
interviews but also for establishing common ground for the company and to ease transfer of 
technology to new team members. Regarding documentation, it is therefore reasonable to recommend 
SF to use the methodology of Agile. 
Meetings and communication: The SF sales department do not have daily meetings but meet once a 
month. The sales team does not have more frequently meeting as most of the employees only work 
part time. SFs solution to this is that the sales manager has weekly Skype meetings to follow up with all 
employees from the sales department. Morten says: “The sales manager has weekly meetings with the 
sales employees to follow up on exact tasks and better communication”. Axel mentions in the 
interview, that one of his managerial challenges is, that he does not meet his sales people on a daily 
basis. Agile is depending on regular meetings, opposite to this Scrum is depending on daily meeting to

the customer’s jobs, gains and pains (Osterwalder, 2014).

Customer jobs: Things the customer are trying to get done either a task they are trying to complete, a 
problem they are trying to solve or a need they are trying to satisfied. When investigating the job it is 
important to take the customers perspective. There are three main types of jobs and a supporting job: 
- 
Functional jobs: Performing or completing a specific task or solving a problem 
- 
Social jobs:  Gain status, power or how to be perceived by others 
- 
Personal jobs: Seek a specific emotional state, feeling good or secure 
- 
Supporting jobs: Purchasing and consuming value either as consumers or as professionals 
Pain: Things that annoys or trouble the customer before, during and after trying to get a job done. 
Pains include negative emotion, undesired costs, obstacles and situations or risks such as potential bad 
outcome or no outcome at all. (Osterwalder, 2014)  
Gain: The positive outcome that the customers hope to achieve. It is about how the customers measure 
a job well done. Gains could be savings, user-friendliness, increased turnover, service, positive 
emotions, functional utility and social gain (Osterwalder, 2014).  
 
Value proposition 
Next part in the model is to investigate the value proposition part in the following order: Product and 
services, pain reliever and gain reliever. These should all be ranged in accordance to their importance to 
the customer (Osterwalder, 2014).  
Products and services: All products and services that is offered so the customer can get their functional, 
social, personal or supporting job done. These can be either: 
- 
Tangible: Manufactured goods, face-to-face service 
- 
Intangible: Copyrights, quality assurance 
- 
Digital or virtual: Downloads, online recommendations 
- 
Financial: Investment funds or financing services 
Pain reliever: The value created to the customer by the product or service which alleviates the customer 
pains. Pain relievers eliminate or reduce negative emotions, risks, undesired costs and situations.  
Gain creators: The gains that the product or service creates for the customer. Gain creators generate 
benefits that the customer expects, desires or would be surprised by, this include functional utility, 
social gains, positive emotions, and cost savings (Osterwalder, 2014).

The implementation part consists of a workshop for the two selected TMTs, Asana and Basecamp. The 
reason why workshop methodology has been chosen is that is can be used as a forum where ideas and 
new implementations can be demonstrated and discussed in a creative environment without limitations. 
Moreover, it gives the participants a change to reflect and discuss (Rasmussen, 2011). This might result 
in additional findings to the earlier preformed interviews. An advantage by preforming a workshop is 
that the facilitator has time to analyze the data from the workshop. Strength of a workshop is described 
by Lauge Rasmussen: “Interactive matters help people to develop ways of perception that integrates 
practical experience, reflective through and creative imagination. Moreover, they can be applied on 
both simple and complex problems” (Rasmussen, 2011).  
The implementation part can moreover be used to validate the findings of the best fit of TMT for SF 
that has been identifies earlier in this study through the interviews and the Value Proposition Model. 
 
Before preforming a workshop, a framework for the workshop needs to be settled. This includes 
limitations such as, setting rules, clarifying subject, time limitation, place and participants. Additionally, 
it is important to create an open environment where the participants feel safe and dare to open up for 
their ideas and thoughts. Organizational and mental obstacles need to be removed for the participants 
to be receptive to changes (Rasmussen, 2011).  
 
There are several methods to perform a workshop such as Casual mapping, Community Building 
through Dialog, Chronical Workshop, Improvisional Theatre, Search Conference and Interactive 
SWOT Methodology. The last mentioned has been selected for this workshop based on the chosen 
limitations.  
 
Limitations of the workshop:  
Purpose: To evaluate the two chosen TMTs (Asana and Basecamp) based on discussion. 
Ground rules: It is not allowed to criticize others opinions.  
Subject: TMT for the sales department 
Time: 2 days of 3 hours 
Place: SF’s work office  
Participant’s background: Knowledge regarding Asana and SF. Moreover, the participants have same or 
similar work goals.

10.2 The SWOT Inventories  
The SWOT Inventories starts with an introduction where the methodology and terminality of the 
SWOT is explained. It is important that the participants are aware of the participatory and none 
hierarchical SWOT approach (Rasmussen, 2011). A basic requirement is that everybody understands 
that all participants are considered equal regardless of their education, background, ethnicity, sex or age. 
Afterwards the two main components of the SWOT Inventory are defined. These are indicators of the 
internal situation and external situation (Rasmussen, 2011).  
 
The internal situation consists of existing strengths and weaknesses.  Strength can be defined as internal 
assess of e.g. technology, knowhow, motivation or entrepreneurial spirit to exploit external 
opportunities or to fight off external threats. Weakness can endanger the company or obstructs the 
exploitation of opportunities. This includes symptom weaknesses for the company (delays in payment, 
high cost structure and poor monitoring), hygiene factors (lose of leadership skills, internal 
communication) or structural weaknesses (poor manufacturing skills, information technologies, dis 
loyalty) where following categories are look at: 
- 
Input: Resources (financial and human) 
- 
Process: Procedures, technologies and attitude 
- 
Output: Quality of product and services, prices and distribution 
- 
Overhead: Location, communication and management 
- 
Support: Accountability, administration and IT 
 
The external situation consists of opportunities and threats such as political, social and cultural, 
economic and technological trends. An opportunity can be describes as any external factor which is 
positive for the future for the company. A threat can be describes as an unfavorable external factor that 
can influence the company negatively. Threats might be “structural” such as a declining labor force or it 
can be “market related” e.g. change in consumer interests, increased oil - or electricity prices. 
 
The identified strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats should then be ranged regarding their 
importance to the company. This ranking helps the participants to engage in a discussion regarding the

Conclusion   
 12. 
The conclusion answers the research question: Which PMMs and TMTs are appropriated for 
YGCs in general and what is the best fit of PMM and TMT for one particular case of 
Sportyfriends an YGC? Based on theory and interviews the research question regarding which PMMs 
and TMTs are appropriate for YGCs in general can be answered. A case study has been included to 
answer what is the best fit of PMM and TMT for one particular case of SF an YGC. To provide a 
better overview the answer to the research question will be given first regarding PMM and then 
regarding TMT. 
 
12.1 Conclusion on PMMs 
General: Based on the theory for YGCs and an online research, three PMMs have been selected and 
analyzed: Agile, Waterfall and Scrum. The Waterfall methodology is not appropriate for YGCs as is has 
a lack of flexibility. Moreover, it does not identify technological or business bottleneck and working 
software is late in life cycle. Agile and Scrum has the advantages of being easy to manage and flexible 
for change. Their biggest disadvantage is that new team members may be challenged due to minimal 
documentation. Agile and Scrum provides an overall plan but is depended on high individual 
dependencies and responsibilities. As both are normality for an YGC this should not be an issue. 
Therefor it can be concluded that Agile and Scrum are both appropriate PMMs for YGCs. 
Case study: In conclusion on which PMM that is the best fit for an YGC a case study with SF has been 
introduced. Six main criteria have been identified and ranked based on a group- and individual 
interviews with SF’s employers, the CEO and the sales manager. SF’s criterias are flexibility, time 
limitation, documentation and education, meeting and communication, teamwork and prior experience. 
These criteria have been used to compare the two appropriate PMMs for YGC Agile and Scrum.  
In conclusion both PMM can handle the flexibility requirement. As both Agile and Scrum has the 
possible to change priorities and requirements easy. Therefor it is not possible to select the best fit of 
PMM for SF based on the criteria flexibility. Regarding time limitations, it is neither possible to identify 
the best fit of PMM for SF as both methods have pros and cons. Scrum has more planning and daily 
meetings compared to the Agile methodology which is more time consuming but might save time in 
the long run. Regarding written documentation (explicit knowledge), which is important for education, 
knowledge sharing and for establishing common ground Agile only generated little documentation. 
Scrum on the other hand has no writing documentation. Regarding documentation, the best fit of 
PMM for SF is therefore the Agile methodology. Regarding meetings and communication Agile is depending 
on regular meetings, opposite to Scrum which is depending on daily meeting to identify issues well in 
advance. Since daily meetings are not a possibility for SF for the time being with their many part time 
employees. The best fit of PMM for SF is therefore the Agile methodology. Regarding teamwork, daily 
meetings would improve teamwork and force the individual team members of SF to take more 
responsibility. This would agree with the skills and entrepreneurial spirit of the employees. Additionally, 
it would encourage knowledge sharing and education. The best fit of PMM for SF for current stage

would be Agile but for a later stage where daily meetings are a possibility Scrum would be the better fit. 
Regarding prior experience with PMMs neither the CEO nor the sales manager have any. Currently the

basis. The sales manager also has an overall plan but as he plans on weekly/monthly basis he leans

Based on the six identified criterias of SF, it is only possible to recommend based on documentation,

12.2 Conclusion on TMTs 
General: Based on the theory on challenges for an YGC and online research, seven TMTs have been 
selected and analyzed: Asana, Accelo, Jira, Wrike, Basecamp, Pipedrive and Monday. The challenges 
and combined demands for an YGC has made it possible to select between these seven TMTs and 
identified Accelo, Asana and Basecamp as appropriate for an YGC. Prioritization of the demands for 
an YGC varies depending on which stage the YGC is in. Price is often the most important factor for an 
YGC as the budget is limited especially in the early stages. For that reason, price has been given the 
highest priority in this thesis. Accelo can therefore be excluded as it is the most expensive TMT of the 
three selected. In conclusion the TMTs appropriate for an YGC in general is Asana and Basecamp. 
Case study: To answer the question regarding the best fit of a TMT for a case study of SF an YGC the 
interviews of SF employees and the TMTs Asana and Basecamp have been analyzed applying the Value 
Proposition Model.  
The connection between the customer profile of SF and the value proposition of the two TMTs shows 
that they differ regarding price. Asana offers the cheapest solution with few users and is free up to 15 
users. Asana is more expensive compared to Basecamp in a later stage when SF expands to more than 
15 users. Regarding real-time communication, the best fit is Basecamp as Basecamp offers a product with 
real-time communication. Asana only partly offers real-time communication. Regarding scalability and 
integration, the best fit is Asana as Asana both has the capability of scalability and offers easy integrating 
with other software systems. This is opposite to basecamp, which only offers one all-inclusive solution. 
Moreover, Basecamp cannot integrate with other software system. Regarding user-friendliness, the best fit 
is Asana as Asana is already used by SF. This is an advantage, as SF saves time and has prior 
knowledge.  
In regard to the highest prioritization of demands it can be concluded that Asana is the best TMT for 
SF at the current stage where SF has less than 15 users. When SF grows and needs more than 15 users

Validation  
The validation of the findings, identified earlier in this study through the interviews and the Value 
Proposition Model regarding the best fit of TMT for SF, is based on a comparison with the result from 
the implementation workshop with the Interactive SWOT Methodology.  
 
The Interactive SWOT Methodology presents the new findings: 
- 
External communication being missing email communication 
- 
Real-time communication where Slack provides additional features 
- 
User-friendliness with focus improving teamwork and exploring new features 
The validation shows that the findings are consistent in both methods regarding price, knowledge being 
lost (communication), scalability, integration and user-friendliness. The reproducibility between the 
results from the Value Proposition Model and the workshop results validates the finding that Asana is 
the best fit of TMT for one particular case of SF an YGC.

Reflection    13. 
 
A research like this will no matter the result have an influence on the awareness of the importance of 
selection of both PMM and TMT for a company. Moreover, it will help to create awareness of the 
challenges in identifying an appropriate PMM and TMT and awareness of how time consuming the 
process is.  
A reflection that has surfaced during the process of finalising this thesis is that it is one thing to get a 
company’s attention and awareness regarding the choice of PMM and TMT, but another thing to create 
awareness of the importance of securing a correct implementation.  
All big companies have selected on a PMM and TMT but smaller or young companies may not use a 
PMM or TMT in their early development stage or they might use some kind of non-formalised PMM 
and TMT. Moreover good implementation of managerial changes is mandatory to have success and a 
change manager could be a good idea to help securing a good implementation. The optimal way of 
implementing a change depends on the management of the company, the employees and the stage of 
readiness/attitude towards a change. An implementation of either a PMM or TMT will provide the 
company with a totally new way to think and act, it will give the company a source of motivation and it 
will be a part of securing the company for the future.
