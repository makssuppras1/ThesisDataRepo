Abstract 
 
Today’s efficiency of the biomass-based power plants is confined to heat exchangers steam output 
temperature of 540 ℃, beyond which the material undergoes heavy high temperature corrosion attack 
induced by potassium chloride (KCl). The current study aims at finding new materials solutions to 
increase the exploitation limit of current heat exchangers to a maximum steam output temperature of 
600 ℃, without compromising on the corrosion resistance of the material. 
This thesis was carried out to investigate the influence of low and high temperature nitriding in 
combination with pre-oxidation treatment on model and commercial alloys, taking the inspiration based 
on the results from former PhD student Sunday Chukwudi Okoro [46], which showed an improvement 
in the corrosion resistance of the investigated FeCrAl alloy after the nitriding treatment. The emphasis 
was given to alloys with relatively higher Al content due to the inherent ability of a thin and slow 
growing alumina oxide formation, which is thermodynamically more stable with oxygen and has lower 
affinity to reaction with KCl in contrast to Cr2O3, according to the research conducted by former PhD 
student Saeed Kiamehr [14]. In addition, Al has lower affinity to nitrogen in contrast to Cr, which can 
provide the ability to avoid the formation of CrN and subsequently undesired depletion of Cr from the 
matrix.  
In order to verify the nitriding impact against high temperature corrosion induced by KCl, alloys were 
exposed under conditions resembling to high temperature corrosion in biomass-firing power plants. The 
exposure was conducted at 600 ℃ for 168h in flowing N2(g) + 5%O2(g) + 15%H2O(g) with synthetic 
KCl deposit.  
Nitrided samples were investigated with Light Optical Microscopy (LOM), Scanning Electron 
Microscopy (SEM), Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy (EDS), X-Ray Diffraction (XRD) and 
Microhardness test. The KCl corrosion exposed sample were investigated with SEM and EDS. The pre-
oxidized sample were investigated with XRD and SEM. 
Results showed that the nitriding treatment did not show an improvement against high temperature 
corrosion attack induced by KCl on the investigated model alloys, current statute of the art TP347HFG, 
and FeCrAl alloy. The pre-oxidation treatment showed presence of a thin and slow growing alumina 
oxide on all samples, however, failed to protect the underlying material.

1. Introduction 
With the advent of the Industrial Revolution came an increase in fossil fuels consumption and the 
corresponding decline of biomass as a source of energy. In recent decades biomass has regained its 
attention as a renewable and sustainable source of energy due to environmental concerns. CO2 
emissions generally have been considered climate neutral provided that the bioenergy system is carbon 
flux neutral. The CO2 emitted upon combustion of the biofuel roughly speaking equals the amount of 
CO2 sequestered by the biomass [1]. 
Figure 1.1. Biomass combined Heat and Power (CHP) plant design 
 
Biofuel is not as energy efficient as fossil fuel. Setting a limit for the output steam temperature of a 
biomass boiler is a trade-off between fuel energy consumption and material degradation [2]. High 
temperature corrosion induced by the deposition of corrosive species on hot sections of the superheater 
tubes known as “fireside” drastically reduce the lifespan of the material due to accelerated corrosion, 
setting a limit on the exploitation of current heat exchangers to a maximum steam output temperature 
of 540℃ [3].

1.1 Description of the problem 
During the combustion of biomass, higher corrosion rates are commonly observed on the fireside than 
on the steam-side of the superheater tube as depicted in Figure 1.2. Flue gasses produced during the 
combustion process are quite aggressive and comprise high amounts of alkali, sulfur, and chlorine 
species in the form of gasses and particles. Low melting temperature particles transported with the flue 
gasses and corrosive gasses adhere to the fireside of the superheater tube and cause corrosion along with 
limitation of heat transfer to the steam [4]. Accumulation of ash deposits can lead to fouling, 
subsequently yielding conditions for slagging [5]. 
 
Figure 1.2. Schematic drawing of the cross section of a superheater tube with the deposit 
 
Since the late 1980s, Denmark has been utilizing wheat and barley straw as a source of biofuel [5]. 
Straw comprise high amounts of K and Cl, along with other elemens [33]. During the combustion 
process, corrosion deposits rich in KCl are formed on the tubes and can be accounted as 80% of the total 
ash-deposits along with fly-ash particles comprised of K-Ca Silicate [6]. The addition of S has proven 
to have a beneficial effect on the corrosion resistance, due to conversion of KCl to K2SO4 according to 
Equation 1.1 [31][32]. Coal contains more S than straw and Denmark has been co-firing coal with 
biofuel from the early days, but now aspires to withdraw from coal by 2023 as a source of fuel for CHP 
generation according to the local news declared by the Danish green energy giant Dong Energy A/S on 
the 2nd of February 2017.

Potassium chloride can react with oxides or SO2 by releasing the Cl2. The released chlorine can diffuse 
through the protective oxide and react with the elements of the metal, especially with Cr due to its higher 
affinity to chlorine, and thus the so-called “chlorine-cycle” can be initiated [36]. The presence of 
H2O(g) during the biomass combustion results in the release of gaseous HCl in high levels, which result 
in the formation of Cl2 according to the Deacon reaction in Equation 1.2 [37].

Lately work by Pettersson and others [34][35] have shown that there is a direct reactivity between KCl 
and Cr-rich oxide by forming non-protective K2CrO4 instead of protective Cr-rich (Fe, Cr)2O3 oxide. 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
In addition, the work performed by Asteman and others [38][39] have shown that the presence of 
H2O(g) during the biomass combustion results in Cr depletion from the oxide layer due to the formation 
of volatile chromic acid Cr2O2(OH)2(g). 
Superheater tubes with steam output temperature above 540 ℃ promote evaporation of chromium 
chlorides and chromium oxychlorides from the surface leading to a porous structure with high corrosion 
rates [3]. 
In straw-fired boilers, in relation to K-S-Cl chemistry the formation of aerosols has an inherent 
supplementary impact on corrosion reaction [6]. 
The purpose of this study is to find new materials solutions in order to increase the steam output 
temperature up to 600 ℃ as an alternative to well-known austenitic stainless steel TP347HFG without 
comprising on the rate of degradation of the material, ultimately improving the efficiency of biofuel.

2. Theory 
This chapter summarizes a concise literature review on mechanisms taking part during high temperature 
biomass induced corrosion along with nitriding in terms of surface modification technique as being the 
main emphasis in search for a new materials solution to mitigate the alkali chloride-induced high 
temperature corrosion. The pre-oxidation treatment with the intention to form a protective Al2O3 scale 
will be discussed as well. 
 
2.1 High temperature corrosion 
High temperature corrosion is an acknowledged issue in industrial equipment and machinery, which 
oftentimes can set a limit for material selection and its performance. High temperature corrosion can be 
described as a chemical attack from gases, solid/molten salts, or molten metals, characteristically at 
temperatures above 400 ℃.  
2.1.1   Oxidation [7] 
Oxidation is a corrosion reaction in itself and is characteristic in oxygen containing environments at 
elevated temperatures. The majority of industrial environments comprise adequate oxygen activities 
resulting in oxidation as part of the high temperature corrosion reaction regardless of the prevailing 
mode of corrosion. Inherently, oxidation can act as a barrier against corrosion attack by forming a 
protective oxide scale in aggressive environments for instance containing sulphur, halogens or alkali 
metals.  
In industrial processes where heat generation is due to combustion, a great deal of high temperature 
processes employ excess air to ensure absolute combustion of the fuel. Combustion products are mainly 
composed of O2, N2, CO2, and H2O; and even though metal alloys are oxidized by oxygen the latter two 
may affect the oxidation behavior. 
Thermodynamically speaking, the formation of an oxide is feasible when the oxygen potential in the 
medium is greater than the threshold oxygen partial pressure required to form a given oxide. The 
standard free energies of formation of various oxides can be read from the Ellingham diagram in Figure 
2.1.  Chromium and Al oxides are substantially more stable than Fe, Ni, and Co oxides. Addition of Cr 
and Al in low concentrations can result in internal oxidation of the alloy, while increasing the 
concentration above a critical value results in external oxidation, leading to formation of a slow growing 
oxide scale that protects the alloy from rapid oxidation. 
2.1.2   Pre-Oxidation [40][42] 
The oxidation resistance of high-temperature alloys relies on the formation of a protective surface oxide, 
thus the pre-oxidation treatment prior to alkali chloride-induced high temperature corrosion can 
potentially provide a beneficial effect. The protective surface oxide in high temperature alloys should 
be stable, continuous, slow growing, free from cracks and pores, adherent and coherent. The Cr-rich 
oxide experience accelerated reactivity with KCl at high temperatures, therefor 𝛼-Al2O3 comes close to 
fulfilling the requirements. Alumina grows at a slower rate than Cr2O3, is thermodynamically more 
stable with oxygen, and provides a substantially higher degree of protection in the oxidizing 
environment. The slow growth rate of 𝛼-Al2O3 is related to its highly stoichiometric structure and large 
band gap, thus making electronic conduction troublesome. Transport processes in oxide scales have a 
substantial effect on the rate of oxidation and resistance to penetration by aggressive species, stress 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
development, grain growth, and creep behavior. The presence of Cr in Al containing alloys facilitates 
the formation and preservation of a complete 𝛼-Al2O3 by acting as a getter and avoiding internal 
oxidation of the Al. In addition, the presence of Cr stabilizes 𝛼-Al2O3 in lieu of 𝛾-Al2O3, and reduces 
the threshold for the development of continues protective Al oxide. 
The drawback of 𝛼-Al2O3 is its susceptibility to crack formation and spallation. The oxide failure can 
be caused by stresses applied during service, growth stresses, and thermal variations that lead to 
compressive stresses due to the difference in thermal expansion coefficient between the alloy and the 
oxide.

2.1.3   Chlorine corrosion 
Salt-forming elements in the form of gasses either as reactants, products or contaminants, readily react 
with most metals to form volatile metal halides. Halides are widely encountered in fossil and biomass 
utilizing power plants, waste incinerators, and polymer decomposition mills [8]. A global convention 
has been established regarding chlorine compounds as being the primary corrosive species in fossil, 
biomass, and waste-to-energy (WTE) power plants [9].  
In chloride-containing combustion environments, corrosion is driven by oxidation-chlorination rival 
reactions, which depend on whether conditions are oxidizing or reducing [7]. The presence of chlorine 
changes the morphology of the oxide scale by creating porosities, which significantly accelerates the 
rate of corrosion by subsurface penetration. The complex corrosion mechanism involved is widely 
accepted as active oxidation, where Cl penetrates the oxide scale, Cl2 are formed at the oxide/metal 
interface, Cl2 are evaporated and converted into oxides [41]. The reaction kinetics governing the rate of 
chloride transportation by diffusion through pores and cracks in the protective oxide scale depends on 
the magnitude of oxides and chlorides formed, where corrosion can abide a linear or paralinear rate law 
[7][9].

interactions taking place during chloride-containing combustion as depicted in Figure 2.2, where Na 
and K can take part [9].

2.1.4   Ash/salt deposit corrosion 
Fireside corrosion is primarily taking place underneath deposits composed of mixtures of ash and salts. 
Deposition processes can vary, but generally are due to physical flyash deposition, vapor condensation, 
and gaseous reaction of different species with already formed deposits. The adhesion of deposits may 
be induced by fusion of molten or semi-solid corrosion products. Deposits rich in chloride in the lower 
section of the boiler are fractionally converted to sulfates at higher elevations in the convection section 
due to their inherent higher stability under oxidizing conditions [9]. Thermodynamic potentials of the 
environment adjacent to the metal surface underneath the deposit may be altered by the deposit. For 
instance in an environment containing both oxygen and sulfur, oxygen activity tends to be lowered and 
activity of the sulfur increased leading to difficulties in a protective oxide scale formation [7]. 
2.1.5   Molten salt corrosion 
Material in contact with molten salt or ash-salt mixture is subjected to molten salt corrosion also known 
as hot corrosion. Corrosion can appear in form of uniform thinning, pitting, internal or intergranular 
attack. Molten salts are good fluxing agents and can readily remove an oxide scale from a metal surface. 
The presence of oxygen and water vapor in the salt accelerate the dissolution of the oxide scale by 
molten salt corrosion [12] 
The aggressiveness of chlorides is to a great extent due to their low melting points, especially when 
combined with alkali and heavy metal chlorides that form low melting eutectics [13]. In Table 2.1 
several salt systems along with their eutectic temperatures can be observed in relation to KCl-induced 
corrosion. The likelihood for formation of all eutectic salt systems during waste incineration in unlikely, 
but the values provide a reasonable indication of the low melting points of fused deposits on superheaer

2.2 Nitriding 
As a surface modification technique, nitriding can distinctively enhance mechanical properties of the 
material (metallic origin), e.g. wear and fatigue, along with improved corrosion resistance. The 
pronounced property improvements are due to the high hardness, internal stresses, and modified 
chemistry in the nitrided zone. In order to obtain a case-hardened surface, a medium acting as a nitrogen 
donor is of need. Process designation is derived after the medium employed to donate, e.g. gas nitriding, 
salt bath nitriding, and plasma nitriding. Emphasis will be given to gas nitriding as the only nitirding 
process involved in the current work.  
Gaseous treatment is advantageous due to its ability in precise control of the thermodynamic conditions. 
The chemical potential of nitrogen at the surface of the component to be nitrided can be varied over a 
wide range in a controlled manner, whereas such precision cannot be achieved in the case of other 
nitriding processes mentioned before [18]. 
In gas nitriding, nitrogen rich gas such as ammonia (NH3) is generally utilized as a donor. When 
ammonia comes into contact with the surface of the heated workpiece it dissociates into atomic nitrogen 
and hydrogen. The adsorbed nitrogen at the surface can either diffuse into the substrate or transform 
into nitrogen gas by recombining with another nitrogen molecule. Classical nitriding is performed in 
the temperature range of 480 - 530 ℃ and for the period of 20-80. In pure iron and carbon steel, the 
improvement in tribological and anti-corrosion properties is attributed to the development of a 
compound layer consisting of iron-nitrides (ε and γ’); improvement in fatigue properties is attributed to 
the development of a diffusion zone consisting of precipitated iron-nitrides (α” and γ’) emerging upon 
either slow cooling or aging (subsequent to quenching), and can be seen in Figure 2.4 alongside with 
iron-nitrogen phase diagram in Figure 2.5 [17]. 
Figure 2.4 Schemtic cross-section of nitrded region [20]. 
 
Thermodynamic principles and kinetics in detail can be found in publications from Marcel A.J. Somers 
[18] and others, whereas the kinetics of compound layer formation are depicted in Figure 2.6 and are 
tersely as following: 
1. Nucleation and growth of the γ’-phase, which depends on the nitrogen content at the surface. 
2. Nucleation and growth of the ε-phase on top of the γ’-phase. 
3. Formation of a ε/γ’-bi-layer by coalescence of dual phase nuclei, the growth of which is time 
dependent and is influenced by nitrogen diffusion through the layer. 
4. Possible porosity formation due to inherent metastability of iron nitrides with respect to Fe and 
N2 near the surface at grain boundaries, which are energetically favourable nucleation sites with 
the highest nitrogen content. 
5. Formation of pore channels by coalescences of the pores with respect to nitriding time.

2.2.1   Nitriding of steels 
Addition of alloying elements to the iron-carbon system results in a different scenario in respect to the 
nitriding layer. Many alloying elements are stronger nitride formers than iron and as a result, a harder 
diffusion zone can develop on condition that alloying elements are present in solid solution prior 
nitriding. A high concentration of nitride forming alloying elements in the matrix leads to decreased 
growth of the diffusion zone due to increased binding capacity of the nitrogen, and the compound layer 
should generally be thinner, if it is developed at all. The improved hardness of the diffusion zone is both 
due to the precipitation of hard nitrides as well as induced compressive stresses, because of misfit 
between nitrides and matrix [18]. 
Alloying elements have different interaction parameters, some being weak nitride formers, e.g. Si, and 
some being strong nitride formers, e.g. Ti and V, as depicted Table 2.2. The interaction parameter is 
normally derived from the (negative of the) ratio of the change of chemical Gibbs energy divided by the 
energy required (product of misfit strain and interfacial Gibbs energies) for precipitation of nitrides [21]. 
Table 2.2. The Me-N interaction parameter “I” for 
precipitation of Me-nitride in a ferritic matrix. 
 
2.2.2   Nitriding of stainless steels 
The acclaimed corrosion resistance of stainless steel is attributed to its ability to spontaneously form a 
protective passive layer of chromium oxide on top of the surface. The shortcoming is in its low hardness 
and poor tribological properties. Under wrong conditions stainless steel can suffer extensive wear, e.g. 
galling. Therefore, thermochemical surface modification treatments began to be applied. Wear is 
improved at the expense of decreased corrosion resistance due to formation of Cr-nitrides, which 
unfortunately results in Cr depletion from solid solution. In time, the problem was overcome with low 
temperature nitriding by keeping nitrogen in solid solution, resulting in the formation of expanded 
austenite with improved hardness in the diffusion zone. Proper tailoring of process parameters, e.g. time 
and temperature, can eliminate the formation of Cr-nitrides in the diffusion zone. Nitriding at higher 
temperatures above the stability range of Cr-nitrides is an alternative, resulting in high diffusion depths, 
but decreased hardness. Both approaches are depicted on TTT diagram in Figure 2.7 [22]. 
Addition of alloying elements with higher affinity for nitrogen compared to Cr can yield further 
improvement in material properties due to formation of other alloying element-nitrides instead of Cr-N.

2.2.3   Process parameters and their impact 
Nitrogen uptake in the material can be controlled by tailoring the gas. Introduction of hydrogen to 
ammonia reduces nitrogen uptake in the material by reducing nitrogen potential (activity) and is 
represented in Figure 2.8 from the experimental work performed by Somers and Christiansen [23]. 
Figure 2.8. Thermogravimetric uptake curves for nitriding of AISI 316 at 445 ℃ in different 
𝑁𝐻3and 𝐻2 gas mixtures, where 𝐾𝑁 denotes nitriding potential. The uptake for KN = ∞ 
reflects the maximum possible uptake of nitrogen at 445°C for gaseous nitriding [23]. 
 
In pure iron and carbon steels the diffusion zone development is mainly rate controlled by the inward 
diffusion of nitrogen into the matrix. One can even have a uniform concentration depth profile by 
increasing the time, theoretically speaking, but maximum nitrogen uptake may never be exceeded even 
by increasing the time as it is bound to nitriding potential. In the presence of alloying elements with 
affinity for nitrogen in the matrix, the precipitation kinetics affect nitriding kinetics and, thus, the 
development of the diffusion zone. It can be assumed that all alloying elements with strong affinity for 
nitrogen do not precipitate instantaneously upon the arrival of nitrogen. The concentration of dissolved 
nitrogen does not abruptly drop to zero at any point in depth, instead, a gradual change in dissolved 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
nitrogen can be observed. When the dissolved nitrogen has reached its saturation level at certain point 
and place, then, it can be assumed that supreme nitride precipitation has occurred, meanwhile the 
nitriding front has progressed further into the bulk [21]. 
Different kinds of nitrogen (Type I, II, and III) [21] that may be present in the matrix can have diverse 
effects on nitriding kinetics. Chromium-nitride with NaCl-type crystal structure has a plate-like 
morphology, due to large linear misfit with the matrix, has nitrogen of type III and is the only one being 
mobile that contributes to the nitrogen diffusion process. The type III nitrogen is dissolved in the ferrite 
matrix at octahedral interstitial sites. The elastically strained ferrite matrix surrounding nitride platelet 
is tetragonally distorted and is associated with a hydrostatic stress component of tensile nature that leads 
to a thermodynamically induced enhanced solubility of nitrogen [23].

After nitriding exposure samples were embedded in cross-section using Struers Pronto Press-20 for hot 
mounting with DuroFast resin followed by MultiFast resin on top due to its lower price. Embedded 
samples were grinded and polished using an automatic Struers Rotopol-22 grinder/polisher and a

After KCl corrosion exposure samples were manually embedded in cross-section using EpoFix resin 
along with EpoDye for cold mounting with 24 hour curing time in order to fully preserve the corrosion

Tegramin-25 using SiC abrasive paper x500, x1000, and x4000 grit followed by polishing with 3 μm 
and 1 μm using a Struers DP-stick P containing polycrystalline diamonds. 
Samples were etched with Kalling’s 2 reagent (CuCl2 5g, HCl 100ml, C2H6O 100ml) for 2-3 seconds 
in order to reveal microstructural details that would not be evident on the as-polished samples. 
Prior to SEM investigation samples were carbon coated using Quorum Q150R∈ along with carbon fibre 
E428 in order to avoid charge build up that inhibit image formation due to non-conductive samples. 
3.4   Experimental set-up for nitriding 
Nitriding was performed using NETZSCH STA 449 F4 Jupiter thermobalance at temperatures of 450 
℃ and 550 ℃ in pure ammonia with the gas flow of 100 ml/min as a starting point. Subsequently, the 
gas composition was altered to a mixture of ammonia and hydrogen with the a gas flow of 50 ml/min at 
450 ℃ in order to reduce the nitriding potential. Selected temperature range will keep substitutional 
solute atoms immobile and allow only the diffusion of interstitial atoms, which would not have been the 
case at temperatures above 580 ℃ [22]. 
3.5   Experimental set-up for pre-oxidation 
Pre-oxidation was performed in pure oxygen under controlled ambience using NETZSCH STA 449 C 
Jupiter thermobalance at temperature of 650 ℃ for 24 hours with the gas flow of 50 ml/min. 
3.6   Experimental set-up for high temperature corrosion 
In order to imitate conditions relevant to biomas fired power plants employed in Denmark, samples 
were exposed at temperature of 600 ℃ for 168h in flowing N2(g) + 5%O2(g) + 15%H2O(g) with 
synthetic KCl deposit (applied on top of the samples prior the corrosion test exposure) under controlled 
ambience with oxygen gas flow of 10 ml/min and nitrogen gas flow of 160 ml/min. In biomass power 
plants fireside deposits are comprised of KCl and K2SO4, where KCl is known to be the most aggressive 
[25], and therefore is given the emphasis in the experiments.  
3.6.1   Synthetic deposit 
KCl powder was dissolved in isopropanol to make a saturated KCl solution. The resulting slurry was 
applied on top of the specimen from one side by means of brushing resulting in a layer with a thickness 
of approximately 1 mm. The sample was then heated to 150 ℃ in order to evaporate the isopropanol, 
leaving only KCl adhered to the surface.

3.7 Characterization techniques 
The following sub-chapter will present the equipment employed in the experimental investigation along 
with concise literature review. 
3.7.1 Light Optical Microscopy (LOM) 
In the current work an Olympus GX41 Light Optical Microscope (LOM) was used for surface imaging. 
LOM uses visible light and a series of lenses to magnify images of small samples. Light from a mirror 
is reflected through the specimen into the objective lens, which generates the first magnification. The 
generated image by the objective lens is further magnified by the eyepiece lens, which is acting as a 
magnifying lens. 
3.7.2 Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)[44] 
In the current work a JEOL JSM-5900 Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) with Energy Dispersive 
Spectrometer (EDS) connected to INCA system was used for surface imaging and analysis. For SEM 
imaging and EDS analysis and acceleration voltage of 15kV and high beam current was used. SEM 
operates by scanning the surface of the material with a focused beam of electrons, which are generated 
by an electron gun and focused with a series of electromagnetic lenses. The emitted electron beam is 
condensed to a fine probe for surface scanning down to a diameter of 10Ȧ . The interaction of high 
energy electrons with atoms at the surface lead to emission of new electrons, which are collected by a 
detector. SEM can be operated by either detecting secondary electrons SEs or backscattered electrons 
BSEs. The inelastic scattering of electrons from atoms in the specimen produces SEs, which are 
deflected at small angles and offer the best image quality of surface topography. The elastic scattering 
of electrons from atoms in the specimen produces BSEs, which are deflected at large angles and provide 
information about specimen composition. Elements with higher atomic number generate more BSEs 
and hence a brighter image. SEM equipped with EDS can provide a chemical analysis of the material 
by generating X-ray photons upon the interaction of a focused beam of electrons with the surface. The 
energy of generated X-ray photons corresponds to transition energy which is distinctive for each 
element. The interaction volume for electron and X-ray emission is illustrated in Figure 3.2

4.1.1 Nitriding at 550 ℃ 
In spite of beneficial ease of control, which gaseous treatment provides there is an obstruction, coming 
from the stability of the passive film. The problem can be overcome by surface activation, by either 
removing or transforming the oxide layer before or during treatment. The method employed in the 
experiment is in-situ, meaning that activation is taking place in the furnace prior to surface hardening. 
The exact details of the method will not be disclosed due to confidential reasons as being intellectual 
property of DTU. 
The model alloys nitrided at 550 ℃ shown in Figure 4.1-4.3 developed a thick and hard diffusion zone. 
However, the high nitriding potential had an influence on the appearance of the formed surface layers. 
At the surface region in Figure 4.1 (d), cracks and pushed out grains are visible due to the high 
composition-induced stresses and limited ductility. Visual inspection of the samples after gaseous 
treatment reveal a dull appearance that can be attributed to the increased surface roughness as a result 
of protruding grains. The micrographs reveal what may seem to be a uniform crack along the interface 
of the diffusion zone with the underlying material, but is more likely an accumulation of carbon, which 
is originally present in the alloy, pushed by the advancing nitrogen front [23].  
 
The finely dispersed precipitates with varying size form and characteristically white in color are 
composed of Nb, C and N (investigated with SEM-EDS, but not included in the results), which are 
initially present as Nb-carbides prior nitriding. The dark orbicular features are inclusions, which form 
by reactions of components dissolved in the melt. These could be oxides or suplhides formed by 
deoxidizers added to the melt.

4.1.3 Hardness measurements 
Hardness variation through the diffusion zone from top to bottom is visible for the model alloys (the 
TP347HFG at 550 ℃ seems to have uniform hardness) due to the difference in nitrogen concentration, 
see Figure 4.20-4.21. Some samples exhibit uniformity in hardness depth profile and some exhibit 
spiking; one cannot be certain whether the grain boundary, intermetallic, carbide, or nitride was 
unintentionally hit during measurement. In both nitriding conditions samples OC-D and OC-F exhibit 
superior hardness in contrast to sample OC-B, presumably due to higher aluminum and niobium content 
resulting in increased nitride formation. Depending on the nitriding conditions samples OC-D and OC-
F exhibit discrepancy in hardness (in some places OC-F has higher hardness than OC-D at 550℃, at 
450 ℃ OC-F has lower hardness through the diffusion zone). The TP347HFG has the softest diffusion 
zone in both nitriding conditions due to having only niobium (no presence of Al) in minor concentrations 
in contrast to model alloys. The hardening for TP347HFG comes from CrN as niobium is bound to Nb-
carbides. The FeCrAl alloy has the hardest diffusion zone due to having AlN and CrN in high quantities. 
The last dots in Figure 4.20-4.21 are taken with a substantial distance outside the diffusion from the last 
hardness measurement (still in the diffusion zone) close to the interface of the diffusion zone and the

4.1.4   General discussion 
The high hardness values measured in the diffusion zone are superior for samples nitrided at 450 ℃ as 
opposed to 550 ℃, presumably because higher precipitation phase fraction can be attained at lower 
temperatures, see Figure 4.22. At higher temperatures decreased formation of nitrides is favored with 
the ability to coarsen with time, resulting in drop in hardness. At lower temperatures increased formation 
of nitrides is favored without the ability to coarsen. 
The solubility of nitrogen in the matrix is reduced at lower temperatures and therefore nitrogen is 
confined to stay in the form of precipitates. The latter statement can serve as an explanation for sample 
OC-B nitrided in pure ammonia becoming brittle, as depicted in Figure 4.12.  
Figure 4.22. Precipitate phase fraction as a function of temperature 
 
In remark to hardness-thickness relationship in the nitrided experiments, samples with higher hardness 
tend to have a narrower diffusion zone as observed in Figure 4.5, 4.16, 4.20-4.21 for model alloys and 
TP347HFG. This can be understood through the formation of more nitride precipitates in the samples 
with higher alloy constituents, which also results in lower penetration depth of the nitrogen. In addition, 
composition-induced stresses in the expanded austenite originating from the misfit of the alloying 
elements with the parent lattice contribute to a reduced solubility of nitrogen. The diffusion in a ferritic 
matrix as in FeCrAl alloy is faster than in austenitic matrix [29], therefore the FeCrAl alloy is exhibiting 
high hardness (due to increased nitride formation) in conjunction with wide diffusion zone.

4.2   Pre-oxidation 
The recorded X-ray diffractograms show that model and commercial alloys after pre-oxidation 
treatment shown in Figure 4.23, 4.25, 4.27-4.29 have developed an iron oxide scale, see Figure 4.30-
4.34 for the recorded X-ray difractograms. The scales formed are complex, normally consisting of 
discrete layers and different microstructures. The oxide scale typically consist of three phases: 
hematite (Fe2O3), magnetite (Fe3O4) and wustite (FeO), arranged in order of increasing oxygen 
content towards the surface. Hematite and magnetite make up the outer layers of the scale and due to 
their composition, make up the larger mass of scale present. Thickness of the layers and their grain 
structure are sensitive to oxidation conditions, material composition and surface finish [27]. According 
to iron-oxygen equilibrium phase diagram shown in Figure 4.24, wustite is not stable below 570 ℃, 
upon cooling to room temperature it decomposes to magnetite or, under very slow cooling conditions, 
to magnetite and pro-eutectoid iron [28]. The micrographs for the non-nitrided samples (reference) show 
some morphology near the surface, presumably due to induced deformation during sample preparation, 
e.g. grinding/polishing.

4.3 KCl corrosion of non-nitrided samples 
To allow proper evaluation of the influence of the nitriding process on the corrosion behavior of model 
and commercial alloys, the KCl corrosion exposure of the non-nitrided samples is first presented. During 
the finalizing SEM-EDS investigations for the non-nitrided samples the JEOL JSM-5900 low vacuum 
SEM went out of order, therefor the author resorted to lower resolution desktop SEM The Phenom 
ProX. The SEM-EDS analysis is presented only for sample OC-B, as the SEM-EDS analysis from the 
Phenom ProX is not trustworthy. 
The corrosion products shown in Figure 4.35 are separated into two layers: layer 1 is located above the 
presumed original surface of the alloy, and layer 2 is located below. The SEM-EDS mapping shown  in 
Figure 4.36 show that corrosion products in “spongy” layer 1 comprise enrichment in Fe and O, with 
Cr and Al in the inner region. Layer 2 comprise depletion in Fe, with Cr and Al in the outer region, O 
is partly present in the outer region, presumably indicating on the presence of internal oxidation and 
grain boundary attack from the outer oxide (layer 1). The depletion of elements in layer 2 happens in 
decreasing sequence towards the surface as was investigated by TEM+EDS point analysis in the 
depletion zone close to the oxide in to be published paper by Malede, Montgomery, Dahl and Hald [42] 
on AISI 310 exposed to KCl corrosion in N2(g)+5%O2(g)+15%H2O(g) for 168h at 600 ℃. The high 
density porosity formation in layer 2 can presumably indicate on the active oxidation corrosion 
mechanism that took place during the KCl corrosion exposure, as the porosity formed explicitly near 
the surface region with Fe, Cr and Al depletion. The confirmation of the chlorination cycle requires 
characterization equipment with higher resolution, such as TEM+EDS analysis inside the hole and near 
the matrix and comparing the chlorine signals. The blocky corrosion products shown in Figure 4.35 
(a,b) in side the bulk of the material are comprised of KCl. The occurrence of such products is generally 
a consequence during sample preparation, where KCl diffuses from the surface into the matrix.

4.4.2 SEM-EDS analysis 
The SEM-EDS analysis shown in Figure 4.41-4.51 demonstrated Fe depletion in the diffusion zone, 
primarily in the area with selective corrosion attack, and Fe enrichment in the outer oxide with traces of 
Cr, Al and Ni. The areas with Cl corrosion products show Fe depletion with heavy depletion in Cr, Al, 
and Ni. Areas with severe Cr and Al depletion experience enrichment in Ni concentration. The partially 
affected areas in the diffusion zone could have been Cr-oxidized with additions of Al, and Al-oxidized 
with additions of Cr due to internal oxidation. The adjacent presumably unaffected areas could be 
enriched in Ni, Cr, and Al. 
Sample OC-B nitrided at 550 ℃ shown in Figure 4.41 show enrichment in Cr with traces of Al 
underneath the area of selective corrosion attack. The intermittent thin layer is presumably Cr rich oxide 
as it is rich in oxygen. There is no apparent source of the Cr rich oxide, e.g. no intergranular corrosion 
from the outer oxide. Hence, it can be assumed that the diffused Cl species underneath have reacted 
with Cr to form CrCl2 and when the evaporated CrCl2 has reached the region with substantial partial 
pressure of oxygen, the Cr has oxidized. The same type of theory could be applied for Al. The region 
with Cr rich oxide is completely depleted in Ni, which is common for Cr and Al oxide formation.

4.4.3   General discussion 
Ranking the samples by means of successful corrosion protection (to some extent): 
1. OC-D nitrided at 450℃  (One tiny island of Cl corrosion product; selective corrosion present) 
2. OC-F nitrided at 450℃  (Some Cl corrosion products; selective corrosion present) 
2. Other samples (Heavy Cl corrosion products, selective corrosion, heavy grain boundary attack) 
In comparison to the non-nitrided samples after KCl corrosion exposure, the nitrided samples did not 
show an improvement against alkali chloride-induced high temperature corrosion. The outer spongy Fe 
rich oxide with traces of Cr and Al was present on all samples. The outer oxide was thicker in 
comparison to non-nitrided samples and as a result, the zone with selective corrosion attack was thicker, 
consequently leading to the region with higher Fe depletion. Regions with severe Cl corrosion attack 
with traces of K (only on few samples) were present in the bulk. Samples nitrided at 550℃ behaved 
worse than the 450 ℃, due to the presence of high density crack formation and pushed out grains, which 
resulted in the accelerated ingress of corrosive species that attacked the underlying bulk. In the 450 ℃ 
nitriding series sample OC-B behaved much worse than OC-D and OC-F, the corrosion response was 
quite similar as in the 550 ℃ nitriding series (for model alloys). Severe corrosion attack of the OC-B at 
450 ℃ can be attributed to lower Al content (3.03Wt%) in comparison to OC-D (4.11Wt%) and OC-
F(4.17Wt%). One could speculate on either CrN formation or increased CrN formation in sample OC-
B at 450 ℃, leading to decreased Cr content in solid solution with resulting decrease in corrosion 
properties. 
The improved corrosion resistance among the model alloys in sample OC-D in the 450℃ nitriding series 
can be attributed to its thinner diffusion zone and fewer crack formation as is evident by the 
micrographs. The micrographs in OC-D at 450 ℃ show what may seem to be the presence of thin and 
slow growing Cr and Al oxides. According to the Ellingham diagram in Figure 2.1, Al requires less 
oxygen partial pressure and oxygen activity to form the protective oxide in contrast to Cr. The formation 
of thin and slow growing alumina below chromia can potentially block the unwanted diffusion of 
elements towards the surface. 
Oxides may appear in layer like structure where each layer corresponds to one oxidized element such 
as chromia (Cr2O3), alumina (Al2O3), the same oxidized element, but with different stoichiometry such 
as hematite (Fe2O3), magnetite (Fe3O4), or a combination of oxidized elements known as spinel 
(M3O4, where M could be Cr, Fe, Ni, Al). In order to confirm which type of oxide is present, thorough 
XRD analysis is required. 
The zone with inner selective corrosion attack, show differences in grey contrast, which can be 
attributed to the difference in Ni concentration. Nickel has low affinity to oxygen and can be left 
unaffected during oxidation due to slower kinetics, whereas other elements will be oxidized 
preferentially. The brighter areas can very well be unaffected even though they show presence of 
oxygen. When the area measured during SEM-EDS is less than 1 μm, the quantative element analysis 
may not be precise, as the signals from adjacent areas will be collected. The SEM is not efficient for 
measuring lighter elements, e.g. oxygen, nitrogen, carbon.  
Voids and porosities in the underlying material oftentimes can be mistaken for oxides; therefore, XRD 
analysis with additional laborious work is required, comprising complete removal of the outer oxide by 
means of grinding/polishing and leaving only the matrix with the desired features to be investigated.

4.6.3 General discussion 
The pre-oxidation treatment of the nitrided samples, except the FeCrAl alloy, displayed no apparent 
improvement against alkali chloride-induced high temperature corrosion in comparison to the nitrided 
samples without the pre-oxidation treatment. The presence of heavy Cl enrichment with traces of K 
inside the material was present under both investigated conditions. The pre-oxidation treatment for 
sample OC-B and OC-D nitrided at 550 ℃ displayed more severe Fe depletion in the zone with selective 
corrosion attack in contrast to the non-pre-oxidized samples, see SEM-EDS mapping shown in Figure 
4.41-4.41 in comparison with Figure 4.61-4.62. The pre-oxidation treatment for sample OC-F nitrided 
at 550 ℃ displayed a significant improvement in corrosion resistance, in the zone with selective 
corrosion attack in contrast to the non-pre-oxidized sample. The pre-oxidation treatment for sample OC-
F at 550 ℃ displayed the signs of a thin, slow growing Al and Cr rich oxides at the surface, however 
failed to protect the underlying material against the corrosive species, see SEM-EDS mapping shown 
in Figure 4.43 in comparison with Figure 4.63. The pre-oxidation treatment for TP347HFG nitrided at 
550 ℃ displayed more severe Fe depletion in the zone with selective corrosion attack in contrast to the 
non-pre-oxidized sample. However, the Fe depletion zone was slightly thinner in the pre-oxidized 
TP347HFG at 550℃, and no severe Cl corrosion attack was present throughout the whole sample (big 
black morphological features), see SEM-EDS mapping shown in Figure 4.44 in comparison with Figure 
4.64. The pre-oxidation treatment for sample OC-B nitrided at 450 ℃ displayed an improvement in 
corrosion resistance in contrast to the non-pre-oxidized sample, as no heavy Cl corrosion attack was 
present throughout the whole sample (big black morphological features). The zone with selective 
corrosion attack in the pre-oxidized OC-B at 450 ℃ was thinner, but more affected (area wise); the 
sample displayed the signs of a thin, slow growing Al and Cr rich oxides at the surface, see SEM-EDS 
mapping shown in Figure 4.45 in comparison with Figure 4.46. The pre-oxidation treatment for sample 
OC-D and OC-F nitrided at 450 ℃ displayed a diminishment in corrosion resistance in contrast to the 
non-pre-oxidized samples, as the samples developed high-density porosity formation (small black 
morphological features) under the zone with selective corrosion attack, see SEM-EDS mapping shown 
in Figure 4.46-4.47 in comparison with Figure 4.67-4.68. The pre-oxidation treatment for both sample 
OC-D and OC-F at 450 ℃ displayed the signs of a thin, slow growing Al and Cr rich oxides at the 
surface, however, on the non-pre-oxidized OC-D sample at 450 ℃ the thin, slow growing Al and Cr 
rich oxides at the surface appeared more pronounced.  
The pre-oxidation treatment of the FeCrAl alloy at 450 ℃ displayed an improvement against Cl 
corrosion attack in contrast to non-pre-oxidized sample. The representative micrographs show no 
presence of the big black morphological features, see SEM-EDS mapping shown in Figure 4.51 in 
comparison with Figure 4.70.

5. Conclusion 
The effects of low and high temperature nitriding on model and commercial alloys against alkali 
chloride-induced high temperature corrosion have been studied. The following conclusion can be made 
based on the obtained results. 
The nitriding treatment at 450 ℃  and 550 ℃ with different nitriding potentials showed no apparent 
improvement against alkali chloride-induced high temperature corrosion in comparison to the non-
nitrided samples. The “spongy” non-protective outer oxide was present on both tested model and 
commercial alloys. The area underneath the surface comprised a zone with inner selective corrosion and 
intergranular corrosion attack. The presence of Cl and K (to some extent) corrosive species was present 
in the material under both investigated conditions. The nitriding treatment at 450 ℃  better corrosion 
resistance (to some extent) in contrast to the 550 ℃ nitriding treatment. The pre-oxidation treatment 
prior to KCl corrosion test exposure displayed the signs of a thin and slow growing alumina oxide 
formation, however, failed to protect the underlying material.

6. Future work 
Although the results of the present work have not shown any improvement against alkali chloride-
induced high temperature corrosion, they indicate on the need for additional research in order to fully 
evaluate the influence of the nitriding process on the corrosion behavior. 
The nitriding potential at 550 ℃ was clearly too high as evidenced by better performance in corrosion 
resistance for model alloys at 450 ℃ and severe intergranular corrosion attack at 550 ℃. The pre-
oxidation treatment in sample OC-F at 550 ℃ showed a substantial improvement in the zone with 
selective corrosion attack, however, due to the presence of high-density crack formation the sample 
suffered severe intergranular corrosion attack. Consequently, systematic studies focusing on nitriding 
process parameters along with pre-oxidation treatment parameters are required. 
The improvement in the zone with selective corrosion attack after the pre-oxidation treatment was not 
observed on sample OC-D at 550 ℃ as compared with sample OC-F at 550 ℃ even though the Al 
content in both samples is relatively similar, e.g. Al (4.11Wt%) for OC-D and Al (4.17Wt%) for OC-F. 
Consequently, systematic studies focusing on the influence of Nb additions with Al are required, e.g. 
Nb (1.01Wt%) for OC-D and Nb (2.53Wt%) for OC-F.
