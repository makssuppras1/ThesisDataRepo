Abstract
Hydrotreated Vegetable Oil (HVO) is a process of conversion of oils and fats into various renewable fuels,
like green diesel, renewable jet fuel and bio propane. These renewable fuel products are similar to their
conventional counterparts obtained from crude oil, therefore can be used in existing transport and aviation
sector without any modifications. HVO process is also gaining acceptability in industry because it can be
integrated in an existing petroleum refinery with few modifications. The oils and fats used in the HVO process
needs to be pretreated first in order to remove unwanted impurities. These impurities can poison the catalyst
in hydrotreatment reactor if not removed. Pretreatment process mainly includes acid/special degumming,
bleaching/adsorption and deacidification. The sequence and number of steps may vary based on source and
characteristics of oils and fats used. Almost all the new HVO plants and the cogeneration plants are based on
pretreatment of oils and fats.
The aim of this thesis is to study hydrolysis of oils and fats as a pretreatment option for HVO process. The
oils and fats are treated with water in a countercurrent column, operating at high pressure and temperature
to form faty acids and glycerol. The faty acids are purified and sent to the hydrotreating unit where it is
reacted with hydrogen to form green diesel and renewable jet fuel. One major diﬀerence of using fat spliting
with hydrotreating (case study 1) compared to pretreatment and hydrotreating (case study 2) is the hydrogen
consumption. The faty acids have a smaller molecular structure compared to triglycerides and therefore will
require less hydrogen than triglycerides when treated in a hydrotreater. But, when these cases are compared
based on overall process economics, including Net present value (NPV) and Discounted cash flow rate of
return (DCFR) considering time value of money, case study 2 is most favourable. For a feed capacity of 1000
tons/day of crude palm oil, the economic parameters of both the case studies are given below.

Introduction
Global atention is increasingly focused on the need to accelerate clean energy transitions in order to mitigate
the risks of climate change. The Paris Agreement has an objective of holding the increase in the global average
temperature to well below 2°C. With its major emissions footprint, the oil and gas industry is at the heart of
the mater. Countries around the world are implementing policies and legislation’s to improve eﬀiciency and
cut carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions.
Europe has adopted one of the most ambitious renewable energy policies in the world. The Renewable
Energy Directive (RED) European Union (EU) 2018/2001, establishes an overall policy for the promotion and
use of energy from renewable sources. The overall EU target for Renewable Energy Sources consumption by
2030 has been raised to 32%. Whereas the transport sub-target require fuel suppliers to supply a minimum of
14% of the energy consumed in road and rail transport by 2030 as renewable energy.
Biofuels are therefore important in helping the world meet its greenhouse gas reductions targets and
achieving the temperature goals of Paris climate agreement. At present biodiesel derived from oils and fats via
transesterification processes dominates the biofuel market. Biodiesel or Faty acid methyl esters (FAME) have
physical properties similar to those of conventional diesel. Because biodiesel have poor cold flow properties
and higher oxygen content, it is mainly used as an additive to the conventional diesel, with blending upto
5-7% (B5 −B7)1.
In order to overcome the shortcomings of biodiesel, hydrotreating of oils and fats has emerged as a
commercially proven solution to solve this problem in the coming decades [1]. Hydrotreating of oils and fats
is also commonly referred to as Hydrotreated Vegetable Oil (HVO) or as Hydroprocessed Esters and Faty
Acids (HEFA)[2]. In this process triglyceride’s containing oils and fats is treated with hydrogen to remove
oxygen and split the triglyceride molecule into propane and n-alkanes. The renewable fuel products of this
process are characterized as Green Diesel (GD), Renewable Jet Fuel (RJF), bio propane or bio LPG and bio
naptha [3]. These green fuels have identical structure, physical and chemical properties compared to their
petroleum counterparts. Also, the various fuel products of the HVO process are compatible with the existing
infrastructure and the fuels have smooth access to the existing market.
Note: [1] B5-B7 is the blending ratio of biodiesel with conventional diesel.

Background study
Hydrotreatment of vegetable oil plants can utilize a wide range of feedstocks containing triglycerides, which
includes almost all types of seed oils, palm oil and palm kernel oil. But also includes non edible oils like Tall
oil, Waste Cooking Oil (WCO), jatropha oil, camelina oil, babassu oil, algae oil, animal fats of category 1 and
2, faty acids and acid oil [4].
In the first part of a HVO process, crude oils and fats are pretreated in a series of steps to remove various
impurities like phosphatides, metals, free faty acids (FFA), other insoluble impurities present in the oil. Some
of the major pretreatment steps mainly includes special degumming, bleaching/adsorption and deacidification.
In special degumming the feedstock is treated with phosphoric acid or citric acid to remove the phosphatides
from the oil using high speed separators. Whereas, in bleaching/adsorption activated carbon or bleaching earth
is used to remove various insoluble impurities, metals, remaining phosphatides from the oil. Deacidification is
the process of removing FFA from the degummed, bleached oil. Deacidification, utilities vacuum and high
temperature to separate the low volatile FFA from high boliling triglycerides. The purified oils and fats are
then sent to a hydrotreating unit. A simplified production process of pretreatment and hydrotreating is shown
in figure 2.1.

Hydrotreating is a catalytic process in which the pretreated oils and fats is reacted with hydrogen over a
bi-functional catalyst (NiMoS2/Al2O3) to form various mixtures of n-alkanes (CnH2n), typically in the carbon
range between C15-C18 and boiling point in the range of 270°C - 315°C. Being n-alkanes the fuel properties of
these renewable fuels are beter than the conventional petroleum fuels and FAME. The renewable fuel has the
advantages of a higher cetane number, lower aromatic content, lower sulfur content, and potentially lower
greenhouse gas emissions. This allows blending in any desired ratio without any concerns regarding fuel
quality.
A hydrotreating plant involves two sets of process hydrotreating and hydroisomerization. Hydroisomeriza-
tion is also known as hydrocracking [5]. Hydroteating is a non-destructive hydrogenation in which hydrogen
first reacts with the unsaturated carbonyl group of the triglyceride molecule, later more hydrogen is added to
crack the glycerol structure to form faty acids (FA) and bio propane. The faty acids then undergo three sets
of reaction to remove the carboxylic acid group from the straight chain alkanes i.e Decarboxylation (DCX),
Decarbonylation (DCN) and Hydrodeoxygenation (HDO) based on the selectivity of the process.
In the HDO route, the faty acids reacts with three moles of hydrogen to produce one mole of hydrocarbon

Finally, the long chain n-alkanes mixture is hydroisomerized/hydrocracked to produce a mixture of iso-

Green Diesel and Renewable Jet Fuel.

Faty acid methyl esters is obtained by a transesterification process that converts all kinds of vegetable oils
and animal fats into ester type biodiesel fuel. The only advantage of this process is that it can be conducted at

3.4
Hydrogen Production
When using oils and fats in a hydrotreater to produce renewable diesel, the refinery hydrogen balance should
be in place, as the hydrotreating reactions consume a large amounts of hydrogen ranging from 400-500
Nm3/m3. In this study hydrogen production is not simulated and the cost of production of hydrogen is used
from the European Commission World Energy Outlook 2050 for large plants.
The two main processes for producing hydrogen in industry are methane steam reforming (MSR) and
Electrolysis. Methane steam reforming coupled with amine sweetning plant can produce hydrogen with purity
upto 99% V/V. However, the process parameters needs to be carefully defined in order to optimize capital cost.
Almost all HVO production facilities from table 2.2 utilize methane steam reforming to meet the hydrogen
requirements. Natural Gas or Shale Gas is perhaps the most common feedstock for steam reforming, but also
various low boiling saturated hydrocarbons that have a low sulfur content, refinery gas, liquefied petroleum
gas (LPG), and low-boiling naphtha can also be used and makes the MSR process flexible.

3.4.2
Water Electrolysis
Water electrolysis is a process that splits hydrogen from water using an electric current. The process can be
used on both large and small scale. On a large scale, the process may be referred to as power-to-gas, where
power is electricity and gas is hydrogen. Electrolysis does not produce any emissions other than hydrogen
and oxygen. The electricity used in electrolysis can come from renewable sources such as hydro, wind, or
solar energy. If the electricity used in electrolysis is produced from fossil fuels, then the pollution and carbon
dioxide emissions produced from those fuels are indirectly associated with electrolysis.
Recently two big HVO projects have selected water electrolysis as a means to produce hydrogen, SkyNRG
in the Netherlands and ST1 in Sweden. SkyNRG in developing one of the first dedicated European Sustainable
Aviation Fuel (SAF) production plant in association with Shell. The plant will be located in Delfzijl, Netherland
and is scheduled for commissioning in 2022. The plant will produce 100,000 tons of SAF a year. The energy
company ST1 is building a new bio refinery in Gothenburg, which is planned to open in 2022 and have an
annual production of 200,000 tonnes of renewable fuels [25] [26] [27] [28] [29].
Conclusion
Methane steam reforming (MSR) is the most widely used process to produce hydrogen. Natural gas or shale
gas is treated with steam at high temperature and pressure in a autothermal reactor to produce mixture of
CO and H2 called synthesis gas, the gas is then further purified in an amine sweetning plant. The cost of
hydrogen for a 1000 tons/day plant is 1.32 USD/kg.

Objectives and Scope
The present study is aimed towards studying hydrolysis of oils and fats also referred to as fats spliting
as a pretreatment option in a HVO process. The hydrolysis of oils and fats utilizes high temperature and
pressure to react the triglyceride molecule with water to produce faty acids, glycerol and water. Faty acids
are separated from a solution of glycerol and water using distillation and are sent to a hydrotreating unit.
Based on the literature survey, patents of Lurgi GmbH [42] and North Carolina State University [43] [44]
describes the processes for hydrolysis of oils and fats to form faty acids and converting the faty acids in a
hydrotreating unit to produce green diesel and renewable jet fuel.
Lurgi GmbH (Now Air Liquide) is supplying faty acid production units since 2000 and therefore process
of Lurgi GmbH [42] was followed to develop a flowsheet of hydrolysis and hydrotreating on the process
engineering simulator AVEVA Pro/II 10.2. During the process design stage major atention is paid to:.
• Raw Material Selection
• Energy Eﬀiciency (Pinch Analysis)
• Capital Cost Reduction
• Sensitivity Analysis

Case Study 1: Fat spliting and Hydrotreating
Hydrolysis of oils and fats is an old process and dates back to 1813 when Michel Eugène Chevreul described
oil and fats as substance consist of glycerol and one, two, or three faty acids. It was also known that reaction
with water will separate fats into its components [47]. In 1853 Tilghman [48] discovered, that the reaction of
fat and water to form faty acids and glycerine, can be carried out by mixing the fat with water. Afer the
mixing then subjecting tile mixture to a high degree of heat while the pressure is maintained suﬀiciently high
to prevent vaporization of the water. Aferwards, in 1890 the Twitchell’s method came, where he devised
a relatively simple atmospheric pressure boiling method employing a reagent or catalyst to speed up the
hydrolysis for producing faty acids and glycerine directly from fats. This method was widely used for a
long period because it can be performed in atmospheric pressure and temperature of 100 °C, but the waste
processing remained a problem.
As an alternative, high temperature, non-catalytic process of triglyceride hydrolysis was initiated in 1939
by Proctor and Gamble[49], and was developed in 1949 by Colgate-Palmolive-Peet and Emery Industries,
known as Colgate-Emery process [50]. The process requires a temperature of 250 °C and a pressure of 50
bar, this is higher compared to the Twitchell process. In the Colgate-Emery Process fat and water react
counter-currently in a column at about 260 °C and about 50 bar. Heat exchange between faty acid and water
takes place in the top portion of the column and between fat and sweet water in the botom part.
In the thermal hydrolysis of triglycerides, the triglyceride containing feedstock is heated in presence
of water to break the bonds in the triglyceride molecule specifically between the faty acids chain and the
glycerol molecule. In general one mole of triglycerides reacts with three moles of water to give three moles of
constituents faty acids and and one mole of glycerol. The overall reaction is given in equation 5.1
Triglyceride + 3H2O −→3FFA + Glycerol
(5.1)
The thermal hydrolysis remains the commercial process used for faty acid production until the present
time.
5.1
Fat Spliting: Preliminary Flow Diagram
The preliminary flow diagram of fat spliting was developed based on the process described in Lurgi’s patent
[42]. The thermal hydrolysis of oils and fats comprises of series of steps, in the first step the oils and fats are
introduced at the botom of the spliting column, water is introduced at the top of the column. The spliting
column is at a temperature of 220 - 260°C and operates at a very high pressure of 25 - 55 bar, so that the
water inside the column is at a subcritical state and does not flash to steam [51]. At these conditions water
is soluble in the fat phase, and the step-wise reaction of spliting of triglycerides proceeds without use of a
catalyst. The ratio of water to oil plays a important role in the completion of the reaction and for the reaction
kinetics, a water to oil ratio of 3.5:1 is used during the simulation in Pro/II to achieve the required conversion
of 98-99% [51] [52]. The basic flow diagram is shown in figure 5.1, the flow diagram is made with inspiration
from Lurgi GmbH handbook [53].

5.5
Thermodynamic Model Selection
It is important to select a thermodynamic model which predicts the thermodynamic properties at various
conditions correctly. A thermodynamic model may be valid for the whole flowsheet or only for some units.
Specific thermodynamic options at unit level will definitely increase the reliability of the results.
The CAPEC 5.8 LIPID Library of Alfa Laval is used in the Pro/II simulations, it already has all the
necessary properties of triglyceride components (OOO, OPO etc), faty acids, glycerol and water. For the
other components n-alkanes, i-alkanes, CO2, CO and H2, Pro/II SIMSCI databank was used which has all the
necessary properties. The simulation does not include any non-library components and therefore there was
no need to specify the components as pseudo-components and use the group contribution method for their
critical properties. For the simulation of Hydrolysis and hydrotreating process, we have following possibilities
based on literature study and Pro/II Property Selection Assistant.

5.6.1
Hydrolysis Section
Palm oil (TAG) at a temperature of 60 °C and a flowrate of 42,000 kg/hr is pumped by a positive displace pump
(P1) at a outlet pressure of 45 barg. The incoming oil is heated in a steam heater (HEX1) where the temperature
is increase to 250 °C before entering the hydrolysis reactor (R1). Superheated steam at a temperature of 260
°C and pressure of 45 bar is entering the reactor at the top. The reactor (R1) is a continuous counter-current
reactor, operating at high temperature of 260 °C and pressure of 45 barg, it does not need any catalyst to
accomplish the necessary contact between the reactants. The steam to oil ratio is kept at 3.5 based on various
studies in literature [76], [77], [78]. The fat spliting reaction takes place in reactor (R1) forming faty acids
and glycerol in water. The mixture is then cooled in heat exchanger (HEX2) before flashing in Flash tank (FT1)
operating at 65 °C and 0.01 barg. The thermodynamic model for the hydrolysis section is UNIFAC whereas for
38
==PAGE:45==
the flash tank (FT1) the thermodynamic model SRK is used in order to use flash tank (FT1) as three-phase
separator. In the flash tank (FT1) water (water_recy) is removed from the top, the second stream of faty acids,
un-reacted TAG and water (FA+TAG) is removed from the side. The third stream leaving (FT1) is glycerol-water
mixture (S_5), also called sweet water. The faty acid stream (FA+TAG) is then heated to a temperature of
90 °C in a steam heat exchanger (HEX3) before feeding the stream to the packed column (T2). The packed
column (T2) is operating under vacuum (0.01 barg) and having Sulxer Mellapak 250X packing. In the column
(T2) various unreacted TAG, water, Glycerol mixture is removed from the FA. The FA are then sent to the
hydrotreating section.
The glycerol-water stream (S_5) is purified in packed column (T1). Such a separation system has a low
pressure drop, small liquid holdup, and large surface area per unit volume of packing. Type of packing used is
Mellapak 250X, with material of construction being SS316L. The calculation algorithm used in the packed
column was Chemdist. The purity of glycerol stream (glycerol) obtained at the top was 99.54% (w/w). The
water obtained at the botom of the column is recycled back to reactor (R1).
5.6.2
Hydrotreating
The faty acids stream (Faty_acids) from column (T1) are transferred via centrifugal pump (P3) to heat
exchanger (HEX5) where the temperature of incoming faty acids is increased to 320 °C. The hydrogen is
coming to the plant at a temperature of 50 °C and 10 barg, which is compressed stagewise in compressor (C1)
and (C2). The stream of hydrogen (S_10) is then heated in a heat exchanger (HEX4), where the temperature is
increased to 320 °C. The FA’s stream (S_13) is then mixed with the stream of hydrogen (hydrogen_1) in mixer
(M1). The mixed stream of FA’s and hydrogen is then fed to hydrotreating reactor modelled as conversion
reactor (R2), (R3) and (R4) operating at 320 °C and 30 barg. The unsaturated FA’s like oleic acid (C18H34O2),
linoleic acid (C18H30O2) etc are converted to saturated FA with the same number of carbon atom i.e stearic
acid (C18H36O2). The reaction of hydrogenation is given in section 5.4.2. The saturated FA palmitic acid and
stearic acid are then reacted with hydrogen over a solid bimetallic sulphided NiMo on alumina (NiMo/ γ
Al2O3) or sulphided CoMo on alumina (CoMo/ γ Al2O3) catalyst. Two set of reactions are taking place HDO
and DCN/DCX. These reactions for FA are already explained in detail in section 5.4.4. The n-alkanes (C15-C18)
formed in reactor (R3) and (R4) are then sent to the hydroisomerization reactor (R5), which is operating at 400
°C and 30 barg, to hydroisomerize n-alkanes to i-alkanes (iso-diesel range) improving the cold flow properties
of the fuel. The catalyst used in hydroisomerization typically are Pt/SAPO-11/Al2O3 or Pt/ZSM-22/Al2O3.
The reactions conditions in hydroisomerization, also leads to hydrocracking, where n-alkanes (C15-C18) are
broken down to iso- alkanes mainly in the aviation jet fuel range (C9-C10).
The fuel mixture stream (S_18) is then sent to a gas turbine (EX1) to decrease the pressure of the gas
stream from 30 barg to 5 barg and generate power to be used in the refinery. The stream (S_19) is then cooled
in a heat exchanger (HEX7) to a temperature of 86 °C. The cooled stream is then flashed in a flash tank (FT2)
which is modelled as a three phase flash separator. The temperature in flash tank (FT2) is at 10 °C and at 1
barg, separating stream (H2+CO2+CO) from the top, stream (water_decant) from the botom and the side
stream (S_23) containing green diesel, aviation fuel and water.
5.6.3
Separation Section
The stream (S_22) is then heated in a steam heat exchanger (HEX7) to a temperature of 60 °C before flashing
in flash tank (FT3) to remove any traces of CO2 and CO before entering the distillation column. Distillation
column is operating at a pressure of 1 barg, condenser at 130 °C and reboiler at 280 °C. The lower boiling
aviation fuel is removed at the top of the column and the diesel is removed from the botom.

5.8
Heat integration and Pinch Analysis
Heat integration is performed using the Pinch Analysis Spread sheet of IChemE (Institute of Chemical
Engineers, United States). The whole process sheet (hydrolysis and hydrotreating) is considered for heat
integration, a minimum approach temperature of 10°C is considered. Based on the energy and mass balance
from flow sheet figure 5.7, the process has a potential for Heat Integration, as most of the heat exchangers
are heaters and uses saturated and superheated steam to heat the incoming feed streams. This provides a
possibility of using heat from a hot process stream to heat-up the cold streams when the plant is running at
steady state. To achieve the maximum heat integration in the process pinch analyses was done to minimize
the energy consumption of steam in the process. The stream summary of various heat exchangers from
flowsheet figure 5.7 with supply temperature and target temperature as given in table 5.23.
Because of the process schematics, the hot stream afer axial gas turbine (EX1) is available at 308 °C, which
provides an opportunity to design a heat economizer network to eliminate the steam usage in the process.
The feasible cascade was calculated using the mass flow rate and specific heat capacity from PRO II, the
detailed calculation are shown in Appendix B.6. The pinch point was available at 298 °C.

Sensitivity Analysis
In this section a sensitivity analysis for the optimized and heat integrated flowsheet figure 5.9 is performed.
The studied profitability metrics are the net present worth, NPV, and the discounted cash flow rate of return,
DCFR. These two parameters takes the time value of money into account. To evaluate the NPV the cash flow
for the life of the project is discounted into the present worth. The sum of the total discounted cash flow is
then defined as the NPV. The cash flow for Case Study 1 is found in table 5.34 and 5.35. The DCFR for the
project is the interest rate where the NPV will be equal to zero. It is desired to have a large DCFR meaning
that the project is profitable. Both the DCFR and NPV factor are taking into account when evaluating the
sensitivity analysis. In order for the project to be feasible the value of NPV and DCFR should be larger than
zero. If the value of NPV is less than zero, that means that the project is losing money. The DCFR value can
not be negative, when the value of NPV is negative, the DCFR value is small or zero. Figure 5.10 shows how
the NPV is aﬀected by increases and decreases from the base case for the estimated values of FCI, WC, palm
oil price and diesel price.

6.4
Special Degumming (Alfa Laval Process)
In Special degumming, crude oil is heated to a temperature of 70-80 °C before phosphoric acid or citric acid is
added and mixed vigorously in a mixer. The acid reacts with the hydratable and non-hydratable phosphatides,
metal impurities (calcium, magnesium, iron) present in the oil, to precipitate the impurities. Diluted lye, at
carefully controlled temperatures and pH is added to the crude oil to agglomerate the impurities to form
viscous large gummy particles. The oil is then sent to a retention tank with agitator for completing the reaction
and a proper mixing of crude oil with the acid and the lye figure 6.4. The residence time in the retention
tank varies from 30-45 minutes, and depends on the impurities in the feed oil and the required quality of the
processed oil. The gums formed in the oil are then separated using a centrifugal separator which separated
the viscous part from the oil. The cleared oil is then mixed with hot water to remove the traces of various
impurities and are separated in the second centrifugal separator figure 6.4.
Figure 6.4: Acid Degumming Courtesy: Alfa Laval
6.5
Bleaching/Adsorption (Alfa Laval Process)
The prime focus in the bleaching processes is the removal of a wide range of diﬀerent impurities, such as:
• Contaminants such as soaps and trace metals.
• Traces of phospholipids.
• Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) and other pollutants.
65
==PAGE:72==
In the bleaching process, the previously degummed oil or low phosphorus crude oils are first treated
with citric/phosphoric acid in a high speed acid mixer. The acid is added to precipitate any small quantity of
phospholipids or metals present in the oil. The oil is then sent to a bleacher, where activated carbon/bleaching
earth is added. The bleacher can be mechanically agitated or steam agitated. The slurry mixture is then
filtered in a vertical pressure leaf filter where all the impurities with the activated carbon are removed. The
clean oil is then sent to deacidification or can be sent to a hydrotreating unit based on the oil requirement. A
simplified flow diagram of bleaching process is shown in figure 6.5.
Figure 6.5: Adsorption/Bleaching, Courtesy: Alfa Laval
6.6
Deacidification (Alfa Laval Process)
Deacidification is a vacuum distillation process in which oils having high percentage of FFA is passed through
a flash vessel at very low pressure and relatively high temperature, in order to remove FFA from the oil.
Deacidification is mainly used for low quality oils having high percentage of FFA upto 50%.
In the deacidification process, figure 6.6, the feed oil is first sent to a deaerator where air is removed under
vacuum in order to protect the oil by preventing oxidation. Afer leaving the deaerator, the oil is regeneratively
heated in a special heat exchanger, the economizer, by the hot oil leaving the flash vessel. This ensures that as
much heat as possible is recovered from the hot oil. The oil then proceeds to a final heater where it is brought
up to the exact temperature required for faty acids to evaporate, normally using high-pressure steam.
When the oil has reached the designated temperature, it is fed to the flash vessel. In the flash vessel it is
exposed to a combination of vacuum and high temperature that removes volatiles, including free faty acids
(FFAs), that have a higher vapour pressure than the oil itself. The faty acids vapours are then condensed in
the scrubber, where cold faty acids acts as the cooling medium.

i.e., hexadecane and octadecane, are the products of hydrodeoxygenation reaction (with H2O as secondary
product). In most cases, the extent of both pathways is known from the experimental data and the catalyst
utilized. The product distribution is also influenced by the reaction pressure since at a high hydrogen pressure
the HDO will be the preferred pathway. On the other hand, at a lower hydrogen pressure the DCN/DCX
reaction will be enhanced.

6.11
Pro/II mass and balance implementation
Steps similar in case study 1 will be followed as given in section 5.6. The plant capacity chosen for developing
the flowsheet is similar to case study 1, which is 1000 ton/day of feed. The yearly target production is 355,000
ton/year. Palm oil was chosen as the feedstock similar in case study 1. The thermodynamic model chosen for
simulation of hydrotreating section is SRKM.
In the previous section we have seen that oils and fats have high oxygen content, and in order to convert
oils ans fats to drops in fuels, the oxygen has to be removed. In oils and fats oxygen is mainly present in the
form of an ester, carboxylic acid or hydroxyl group.
The pretreated triglycerides feed (TAG) is pumped from a storage tank by pump (P1), which is a positive
displacement pump with a discharge pressure of 30 barg. The feed is heated in the feed heat exchanger (E1)
to a temperature of 300°C and is mixed with the incoming stream of hydrogen at the same temperature and
pressure in the mixer (M1).
The preheated mixture is then sent to hydrotreating reactor (R1) operating at 300 °C and 30 barg, the first
stage of the reactor (R1) is packed with a high pore capacity alumina infused with low activity hydrogenation
metal. The first stage of the packed bed acts as a guard/grading against impurities, especially phosphorus
73
==PAGE:80==
and metal particles, for the subsequent packed beds. Commercially available TK 709 catalyst from Haldor
Topsøe is a low activity grading catalyst, with high voids and large size. The second packed bed consists of a
bimetallic sulphided NiMo on alumina (NiMo/ γ Al2O3) or sulphided CoMo on alumina (CoMo/ γ Al2O3).
The n-alkanes (C15-C18) formed in reactor (R1) are then sent to the hydroisomerization reactor (R2), which is
operating at 400 °C and 30 barg, to hydroisomerize n-alkanes (Diesel Range) to i-alkanes (iso-diesel range)
improving the cold flow quality or hydrocracked to iso- alkanes aviation jet fuel (C9-C10).
A gas turbine, (E1) is used to decrease the pressure from 30 barg to 5 barg, and a heat exchanger is used
to decrease the temperature before flashing in flash (F1) at 70 °C and at 1 barg, separating propane (C3H8),
CO2, CO and H2. The liquid stream containing green diesel, aviation fuel and water are then separated in a
distillation column shown in figure 6.8.
Pro/II uses Fenske scheme to predict the minimum number of stages based on total reflux. The minimum
reflux can then be determined by the Underwood method, where a minimum reflux = 2 is used. Based on
the reflux then the theoretical stages, actual reflux, condenser and reboiler duties can be calculated by the
Gilliand correlation.

Cogeneration and Expansion Scenario
In case of cogeneration, oils and fats are mixed with conventional light gas oil (LGO) (C12-C18) with boiling
point in range 200-365 °C, approximately in ratio of 30 % (oils and fats) with 70% (LGO) and is fed to a existing
hydrotreater. This case can be considered as an expansion in a existing refinery having a hydrotreater. Oils
and fats have diﬀerent concentration of free faty acids (FFA) leading to diﬀerent acid value. The FFA can
cause potential corrosion issues in the upstream portion of a hydrotreating unit. It is also evident that the
impact of pure faty acid mixture on the metallurgy of hydrotreating section will be more compared to the
oils and fats.
8.1
Corrosion in HVO Process
Corrosion in a HVO process is a very complex and extensive subject. The pretreatment, hydrotreating and
hydroisomerization process handles vegetable oil, hydrocarbons, water, salts, solid impurities and chemicals at
both low and high temperatures. The materials involved are mainly carbon steel, SS 304, SS316, SS316L (L:low
carbon) and special alloys. It is not possible, within the scope of this study, to cover all possible corrosion
mechanisms. The corrosion study detailed below are based on current industrial practice and simulation
process conditions. The aim of this section is to highlight the importance of material chemistry in designing
of a new HVO facilities or in case of cogeneration.
8.1.1
Importance of study
Corrosion analysis is needed to prevent major accident caused by corrosion in the process industries. Prevention
of corrosion is key point to assure process safety in the HVO process. Corrosion analysis can be used to
predict lifetime of equipment and prepare the replacement to be installed at plant turnaround. Corrosion
analysis can be done by using API 581 to predict corrosion mechanism based on impurities in feed, operating
temperatures, and equipment materials [85]. In this study the focus is mainly on corrosion caused by:
• TAN (Total Acid Number) & sulphur concentrations.
• chlorine concentration.
• faty acids concentration.
8.1.2
TAN and sulphur concentrations
Corrosive properties of crude oil in a petroleum refinery is determined by measurement of sulphur content
and total acid number (TAN). TAN numbers exceeding 1.5 to 1.8 mg KOH/g in the crude is considered to
be corrosive, however corrosion problems can occur in crudes with TAN numbers as low as 0.3, for several
reasons, including velocity and the nature of the acidic species present [86] [87].
The TAN is a measurement of acidity that is determined by the amount of potassium hydroxide (KOH) in
milligrams that is needed to neutralize the acids in one gram of oil. It is an important quality measurement of
crude oil. The TAN value indicates the potential of corrosion problems in a crude oil refinery. It is usually
the naphthenic acids in the crude oil that cause corrosion problems, this type of corrosion is referred to as
naphthenic acid corrosion (NAC).

8.1.3

In case study, fat hydrolysis as an alternative to pretreatment processes, is seen. The faty acids mixture from
fat spliting section is sent to hydrotreating section where the temperature ranges from 300-330 °C. The faty
acids are mainly mixture of lauric, palmitic acid, oleic acid, stearic acid, linoleic acid and linolenic acid.
Faty acids can be defined as organic acids with more than six carbons atom in length with the general
formula RCOOH, where R is a hydrocarbon chain and COOH is the carboxylic group. Oils and fats have
diﬀerent concentration of free faty acids (FFA), shown in table 8.3. At the lower temperatures, the acids
may be considered as harmless polar oils. However, when the products are heated to the high temperatures
necessary for processing and production, significant corrosion of steel can occur. The FFA (%) are converted to
acid number (AN) in order to compare the oils and fats to corresponding conventional crude oils.
