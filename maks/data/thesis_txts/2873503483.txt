Abstract
This work is an exploratory analysis that investigates the influence of practitioner tech­
nique on needle electromyography signal characteristics and examines whether Motor
Unit Potential duration maintains diagnostic independence across different recording ap­
proaches. The study analyzes a comprehensive dataset of clinical EMG recordings col­
lected from 4,322 patients by seven medical practitioners using two distinct recording
systems. These recordings were originally performed for clinical diagnostic purposes and
preserved, providing access to complete signal data, typically unavailable, rather than
processed metadata alone. Moreover, the dataset encompasses both normal patients
and those with neuromuscular pathologies to investigate different clinical scenarios. The
method employs multi­domain feature extraction spanning temporal, frequency, ampli­
tude, and energy characteristics, followed by both supervised and unsupervised machine
learning approaches to identify potential practitioner­specific patterns in signal acquisition.
The analysis reveals limited natural grouping among practitioners with unsupervised clus­
tering methods.
Conversely, supervised classification approaches demonstrated mild
identification possibilities, achieving improved performance over random chance while
maintaining variability in individual practitioner identifiability. Investigation of Motor Unit
Potential duration relationships demonstrates a clear weak correlation between individual
signal features and MUP duration measurements. These findings confirm that MUP du­
ration operates as an independent diagnostic measure unaffected by practitioner­specific
recording methodologies.
Current literature employs signal processing and machine learning tools mainly for diag­
nostic classification or movement recognition, and most studies utilize limited datasets
without access to previously recorded clinical signals, creating a lack of investigation into
different approaches for performing needle EMG procedures. The unique preservation of
complete EMG signal recordings addresses this gap, enabling retrospective analysis. The
findings validate the diagnostic reliability of established EMG parameters across different
practitioners and recording systems, supporting current clinical protocols while identifying
opportunities for procedural standardization and improvements in clinical neurophysiology
practice.

Introduction
1.1
Electromyography Background
Electromyography (EMG) is a fundamental diagnostic technique in clinical neurophys­
iology that measures the electrical activity of muscle fibers to evaluate neuromuscular
disorders. With this method, clinicians can have direct access to the functional status of
motor units, the basic functional components of the neuromuscular system [1]. This tech­
nique enables medical doctors to distinguish between different pathological conditions
affecting muscles, peripheral nerves, and motor neurons, facilitating correct diagnosis
and appropriate treatment planning. [2].
Needle electromyography constitutes the most clinically relevant form of EMG examina­
tion, due to its ability to record electrical activity directly from muscle tissue through the
insertion of needle electrodes within specific muscle regions to sample individual motor
unit activity [1] [3].
1.2
EMG Signal Characteristics and Diagnostic Applications
EMG signals are bioelectric recordings that range from microvolts to several millivolts in
amplitude, with frequencies between 10­500 Hz (most energy in the 50­150 Hz range) [4].
The electrical signals are strongest close to muscle fibers and weaken with distance from
the recording electrode [5], which makes needle placement crucial for accurate record­
ings. These signals have a complex profile since motor units fire asynchronously and
the MUP is determined by the current source, the tissue electrical characteristics and the
recording setup [4].
Motor Unit Potentials (MUPs) represent the combined activity of muscle fibers within about
2.5 mm of the recording electrode. Duration is measured from the start of the signal to its
return to baseline, while amplitude reflects the peak­to­peak voltage of the main spike [4].
The amplitude mainly comes from fibers closest to the electrode, typically involving fewer
than 20 fibers, while duration reflects a broader area of muscle fiber activity [4].
Motor Unit Potential duration is a valuable diagnostic parameter because it reflects the
temporal dispersion of muscle fiber action potentials within individual motor units [6]. My­
opathic processes typically reduce MUP duration because muscle fiber loss decreases the
number of contributing action potentials, while neuropathic conditions create motor units
with increased territorial spread because of reinnervation of denervated muscle fibers and
temporal dispersion, caused by slow and variable impulse conduction in the reinnervated
terminal motor, that prolongs MUP duration [2]. The clinical significance of MUP duration
analysis extends beyond pure diagnostic categorization, as this parameter demonstrates
constancy against technical variations, enabling longitudinal monitoring of neuromuscu­

categories require completely different therapeutic approaches [7]. Myopathies primarily

analysis of MUP duration has become more sophisticated through computerized systems
that automatically measure multiple potentials and provide statistical comparisons against
control populations. [9, 10].
1.3
Evolution from Qualitative to Quantitative Analysis
The historical development of EMG analysis has undergone a transformation from subjec­
tive, qualitative interpretation to objective, quantitative measurement systems. Tradition­
ally, EMG analysis at institutions like Rigshospitalet relied on manual visual recognition
of motor unit potentials, with diagnostic accuracy depending on individual electromyo­
grapher knowledge [8]. This approach introduced significant inter­observer variability,
as different physicians could interpret the same EMG signals differently based on their
subjective judgment. The development of advanced automatic decomposition systems
EMGPAD has enabled a quantitative approach that significantly reduces this practitioner­
dependent bias [8].
1.4
Problem Statement
Needle EMG signals depend mostly on three factors: the patient, the practitioner and the
physiology of the Motor Unit. [1] [11].
Despite advances in quantitative EMG analysis, the clinical procedure still depends on
individual practitioner technique and knowledge, and this can introduce systematic varia­
tions that could influence diagnostic outcomes [12]. In particular, needle EMG examina­
tion requires numerous physician­dependent choices, including muscle selection strategy,
needle insertion technique, electrode positioning, and continuous control of the practice
[1]. Each electromyographer develops individual approaches for these procedural ele­
ments, from the initial muscle selection guided by clinical history of the patient, to the
precise needle insertion technique.

2.1
Recording System Comparison and Technical
Specifications
This work can be considered a retrospective analysis of EMG data [16], since the dataset
employed comprises electromyography recordings stored in binary format (.bin files),
collected from 4,322 patients in two distinct hospital institutions. What makes this study
unique is that the recordings were made for clinical purposes, rather than for this specific
investigation.
The two hospitals used different EMG acquisition systems, each employing distinct
methodological approaches that impact signal characteristics and data processing re­
quirements. The EMG Recording System 1 (EMG1) system, an old system utilized in one
room at Blegdamsvej Hospital, implements a ”forward” recording methodology where the
procedure follows a doctor­controlled protocol. During EMG1 sessions, the examining
physician inserts the needle electrode, establishes communication with the patient, and
manually initiates a 10­second recording period when optimal conditions are achieved.
This approach gives complete procedural control to the clinician.
In contrast, the Keypoint (or Cadwell­based) equipment employed at Glostrup Hospital
utilizes a ”backwards analysis” approach. This system continuously records EMG activ­
ity and retrospectively selects the optimal 5­second segment from the recorded buffer,
ensuring consistent signal quality through automated selection algorithms. In this case,
quality control is transferred from manual assessment to systematic analysis, potentially
resulting in more standardized signal characteristics across different practitioners.
The frequency range characteristics further differentiate these systems, with EMG1 sup­
porting a frequency range from 2 Hz to 10 kHz, while Keypoint systems have different
low­frequency cutoffs of 5, 10, or 20 Hz extending to high frequencies of 10 kHz. This
variation in frequency response may introduce systematic biases when comparing record­
ings across institutions, so it will need careful consideration during signal processing.

2.3
Patient classification (normal vs abnormal)
In the protocol employed for the recordings utilized in this investigation, muscles were
classified into diagnostic categories based on the calculated percentage deviation val­
ues of MUP duration. Specifically, muscles were designated as ”Normal” when the mean
MUP duration fell within the ±20% range of age­matched reference values, which corre­
sponds to the 95% confidence interval for healthy muscle tissue [8]. Values exceeding
+20% deviation typically indicate neuropathic conditions characterized by motor unit rein­
nervation and increased temporal dispersion, while values below −20% deviation suggest
myopathic processes involving muscle fiber loss and reduced motor unit complexity. For
each patient, the percentage deviation was calculated using a standardized formula and
then matched with established reference values for the patient’s specific age range and
the examined muscle group, derived from normative databases of healthy control sub­
jects. These reference values represent laboratory specific data obtained from investiga­
tions on healthy control subjects, utilizing mean duration measurements from recordings
performed with the same procedural methodology employed in routine clinical evaluations
(see Fig. A.1 in the Appendix A for detailed reference tables).
This study analyzes recordings from both normal and abnormal muscles, focusing on the
complete range of clinical conditions and does not limit the investigation to pathological
cases exclusively. However, using the ±20% threshold, the patient population demon­
strates a predominantly normal physiological profile with 1,966 normal patients (75.2%),
527 neuropathy cases (20.2%), and 122 myopathy cases (4.7%).

3.2
EMG Signal Processing and Feature Extraction
The primary keywords used for the literature search included: ”needle EMG”, ”surface
EMG”, ”motor unit action potentials”, ”electromyography”, ”EMG signal processing”, ”EMG
denoising”, ”EMG filtering”, ”EMG feature extraction”, ”multi domain analysis”, ”multi do­
main feature extraction”, ”amplitude analysis”.
In standard clinical practice, needle EMG is the preferred method for recording MUPs, due
to its ability to isolate activity from individual motor units. Signal processing steps such
as filtering, denoising, and baseline correction are critical for extracting reliable features.
Commonly used techniques include time­domain measures (e.g., MUP amplitude, dura­
tion, number of phases), frequency­domain analyses, and wavelet­based decomposition
[17].
3.2.1
The Need for EMG Signal Processing
EMG signals represent the electrical activity generated by muscle fibers during contrac­
tion, but raw EMG recordings contain complex information that requires processing to
extract meaningful clinical and physiological insights [18]. Analysis of EMG signals has
been a topic of interest in recent years, but classification studies focus especially on sur­
face myoelectric signal patterns [19]. The analysis of SEMG signals is usually based on
analyzing several factors, such as amplitude, time, and frequency­domain properties [20].
The fundamental challenge of this method lies in the fact that raw signals capture a mix­
ture of motor unit action potentials, noise, overlapping motor unit contributions, electrical
interference, and movement artifacts that pollute the physiological processes [21], making
it difficult to isolate the true muscle electrical activity from unwanted signal components.
That is why processing techniques such as denoising become critical to separate mean­
ingful physiological information from interfering elements and ensure proper interpretation
of neuromuscular activity patterns [22].
3.2.2
Rationale for Feature Extraction
Feature extraction from EMG signals serves multiple essential purposes in clinical neuro­
physiology. The primary rationale lies in the need to transform complex, multi­dimensional
signal data into quantifiable parameters that reflect specific physiological processes and
pathological changes [23].
In feature extraction, the raw input signal is transformed into a set of compact repre­
sentative signal features [19]. Furthermore, extracted features serve as input data for
advanced analytical methods, including machine learning algorithms and statistical clas­
sification systems [24] [20], since these computational approaches require numerical data
rather than raw signal waveforms.

3.2.3
Multi­Domain Feature Extraction Strategy
EMG feature extraction typically spans multiple signal domains because different charac­
teristics of the signal reveal distinct aspects of neuromuscular function, which can facilitate
signal classification. This multi­domain approach becomes necessary because no single
signal parameter can capture the full complexity of motor unit behavior and muscle ac­
tivation patterns [23]. Each domain provides complementary information about different
physiological processes occurring during muscle contraction.
The effectiveness of multi­domain feature extraction has been demonstrated in compara­
tive studies where combinations of temporal, frequency, and energy­based features con­
sistently outperform single­domain approaches [25].

4.1
Signal Pre­processing
Given the exploratory nature of this study, the pre­processing approach was intentionally
minimal to preserve the raw characteristics of data and to minimize any risk of introducing
artifacts or bias through excessive signal manipulation. The main purpose of this phase is
to ensure compatibility between the data acquired from the two distinct recording systems
in Blegdamsvej Hospital and Glostrup Hospital to make them comparable for this work.
By minimizing redundant artifacts unique to the recording system, the biases will mostly
be features of the signals.
Previous studies investigating signals and their characteristics (such as power spectrum
features and motor unit firing patterns) used similar filtering approaches, often limited to
application of a basic band­pass filter (e.g., 20­30 Hz to 10­20 kHz), as pre­processing
settings can significantly alter waveform morphology [5].
4.1.1
Signal Filtering Process
Signal manipulation was implemented in MATLAB R2021a, chosen for its compatibility
with existing EMG analysis tools used in clinical practice, and all needle EMG signals
underwent identical pre­processing procedures to ensure consistency.
Raw signals stored in binary format were loaded and a second­order Butterworth band­
pass filter (20­5000 Hz) was applied with normalized cutoff frequencies [20 5000]/(fs/2).
The filtering operation uses the function filtfilt() for zero­phase digital filtering, which
processes signals in both forward and reverse directions to eliminate phase distortion.
[46]
The 20 Hz high­pass cutoff eliminates baseline drift and noise related to possible needle
movement [5]. These values were chosen specifically to balance the different frequency
ranges adopted from the two recording systems: EMG1 system has a lowest recording
frequency of 2 Hz, while the KEYPOINT has 3 different lowest ranges of 5 Hz, 10 Hz and
20 Hz.
Moreover, the 5000 Hz low­pass cutoff captures the full spectrum of needle EMG ac­
tivity including high­frequency motor unit potentials. The reason for this high threshold
is that unlike surface EMG, needle recordings are able to register contents with high fre­
quency due to their selectivity and proximity to muscle fibers [47]. This method preserves
the physiological properties of needle EMG signals while improving their quality for later
feature extraction and analysis, allowing comparison of the two distinct datasets.
In addition, the second­order Butterworth filter applies only moderate roll­off at the cutoff
frequencies [48], removing noise while avoiding the introduction of harsh transitions that
could hide motor unit action potentials near the filter boundaries. In this pre­processing,
spontaneous motor unit firing variations, high­frequency signal components up to 5 kHz
and spectral features like sudden increased power were not filtered out because they are
not considered noise. On the contrary, these can be interesting features that can char­
acterize signal variability and facilitate the successive work of classification and eventual

4.2
Feature Extraction
The fundamental objective of this work is to identify potential clustering patterns among
different practitioners through analysis of their EMG recordings. To achieve this, a set of
signal features that serve as descriptive attributes of each procedure has been extracted
from each recording. These extracted features will be the input dataset for subsequent
analysis in this study.
The feature selection strategy deliberately spans multiple signal domains, including tem­
poral dynamics, frequency content, amplitude distributions, and power characteristics to
ensure comprehensive signal representation. The rationale for this choice is that ana­
lyzing the combination of these descriptors, rather than examining individual features in
isolation, can provide meaningful information about the procedural approaches employed
by different practitioners. Individual features may show overlapping ranges between prac­
titioners, while the combination of multiple signal characteristics from different domains
provides complementary information that improves classification performance, as demon­
strated by the superior accuracy achieved with combined feature sets compared to indi­
vidual features [49].
By combining these features I could maximize the descriptive power of this work and in­
crease the likelihood of detecting subtle but meaningful differences in recording practices
among the seven different practitioners.
The Teager­Kaiser Energy Operator (TKEO) approach was specifically chosen because
it computes instantaneous signal energy and has been demonstrated to improve EMG
change point detection and muscle activity monitoring, particularly in challenging signal
conditions [25, 50]. The adaptive thresholding mechanism ensures robust performance
across signals with varying baseline noise levels and activation intensities. These mea­
sures were included because they can capture complementary aspects of motor unit be­
havior.

Extraction Process
The feature extraction pipeline operates following an iterative loop structure that sequen­
tially processes each EMG recording by loading the binary signal file, applying the pre­
processing filters mentioned above, extracting the complete feature set, and storing the
results before continuing with the subsequent signal.
The amplitude and temporal analysis operates directly on filtered signals to preserve the
full dynamic range of muscle electrical activity. The TKEO implementation computes in­
stantaneous signal energy through squared amplitude approximation for computational
efficiency, with adaptive burst detection applied using signal­statistics­based threshold­
ing. For the Envelope Extraction: A dual approach using both absolute value x[n]|| and
Hilbert transform hilbert(x)|| was implemented (enabling movement onset/offset detection
and burst amplitude analysis). For the frequency­domain descriptors, spectral analysis
using Welch’s method with 2­second analysis windows and 50% overlap for power spec­
tral density estimation was used. This configuration was selected empirically because it
provides optimal balance between frequency resolution and statistical reliability through
averaging across multiple time segments, while the window length captures sufficient
cycles of typical motor unit firing rates. The overlapping approach reduces variance in
spectral estimates without significantly increasing computational overhead [54].
Power distribution metrics quantified spectral spread, and logarithmic transformations are
applied to those ones because in this way power­based features contribute appropriately

4.3.1
Exclusion Criteria
The standardization of the dataset was necessary to ensure consistency across the dataset
and to minimize bias factors that could compromise the project.
As shown in the data description, the recordings were captured at varying sampling fre­

every signal with the specific practitioner. For this reason, all the recordings without
doctor identification included in the metadata were not considered in the dataset.

4.4
Unsupervised Clustering Approach
This first part of the study aimed at assessing whether EMG signal structure shows natural
practitioner­specific grouping in the signal space, without using any label or prior knowl­
edge about the doctors, and whether those patterns are influenced by dataset balancing
and clinical subpopulation filtering. Therefore, unsupervised learning was employed [55].
This investigation employed three distinct iterations to progressively isolate the different
sources of variability present in clinical datasets:
• Iteration 1: Analyzed the complete dataset in its original form, including the sig­
nificant practitioner imbalance from the dataset. This decision was made because
exploratory work benefits from understanding the natural distribution of clinical data
before applying corrections. In this way I could have a clear answer to the specific
research questions which aim at observing natural data behavior.
• Iteration 2: Used a balanced dataset to remove this imbalance, so that every doc­
tor could have the same number of data. However, this correction still contains
two distinct bias factors that can confound the analysis: biological factors, including
intrinsic muscle characteristics and pathological conditions, and procedural factors
related to practitioner­specific recording techniques and signal acquisition patterns.
• Iteration 3: Used only the dataset including only normal patients, removing patho­
logical conditions from the analysis, leaving practitioner technique as the primary
remaining source of systematic variation.
Both the complete dataset and the subset containing only normal patients required bal­
ancing to address the pronounced practitioner imbalance. Systematic sampling was cho­
sen because it is straightforward to implement and ensures even distribution across the
ordered dataset [56].
The implementation defined the target sample size as the minimum practitioner count,
which was 1,287 signals per doctor. For each practitioner, the sampling interval was
calculated as total signals per practitioner/target sample size. A random starting point was
selected uniformly within the first sampling interval, and every k­th sample was selected
according to the calculated interval. After this process the datasets were balanced and
data ready for the consequent analysis.

erates through four sequential stages, each designed to progressively refine the balancing
process [62].
First, the degree of imbalance (d) is calculated for each minority class relative to the ma­
jority class using Equation 4.4:
di = nmaj −nmin,i
nmaj
(4.4)
This quantifies the relative under­representation of each class, providing the foundation
for determining how many synthetic samples are needed to restore balance.
The number of synthetic samples (Gi) required for each minority class is then determined
using Equation 4.5:
Gi = (nmaj −nmin,i) × β
(4.5)
The balance control parameter β is set to 0.9 after empirical evaluation. This value main­
tains near­perfect class distribution while preserving the natural variability characteristics
of EMG signals, avoiding the over­balancing that occurs with β = 1.0.

4.5.2
Support Vector Machine Classifier
Support Vector Machine (SVM) algorithms are chosen for the doctor classification task
because they perform well with high­dimensional datasets and provide a unique global
optimal solution through constrained quadratic programming [63].
SVM has been successfully applied to medical diagnosis applications and biological data
processing. Moreover, EMG signals contain measurement noise and artifacts and this is
why the soft margin approach in SVM becomes significant. Its flexibility allows the model
to maintain reliable performance without forcing perfect separation of all data points, be­
cause it can tolerate some misclassified samples, avoiding noisy measurements ruining
the overall classification results [64]. Lastly, the doctor identification problem involves
distinguishing between multiple medical practitioners simultaneously, which requires a
multiclass classification approach, such as SVM.
The datasets undergo stratified partitioning into training (80%) and testing (20%) subsets,
maintaining proportional class representation across both partitions to prevent sampling
bias. Three kernel functions are evaluated: linear, polynomial, and Radial Basis Func­
tion (RBF). Each kernel addresses different assumptions regarding the separability char­
acteristics of the feature space, so the optimal choice depends on the data structure [65].
Hyperparameter optimization is conducted through exhaustive grid search with 5­fold
cross­validation applied to the training partition. In this way parameter selection remains
independent of the final test evaluation.
The SVM parameters were optimized through systematic testing to find the best balance
between accuracy and speed.
The linear kernel demonstrates superior performance
among the evaluated options. The parameter C is evaluated across [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10,
100]; higher C values demand fewer classification errors but may overfit, while lower C
values create a more flexible boundary that tolerates some mistakes [66]. The model
achieves optimal performance with C = 100, obtaining a cross­validation score of 0.4680
and test overall accuracy of 0.4654.

• Specificity:

­ combines precision and sensitivity, where preci­

Sensitivity and specificity are considered the most important parameters for evaluating
classification performance of this work because they provide objective, inherently stable
measures of test validity that are independent of disease prevalence or dataset composi­
tion. As demonstrated in diagnostic testing literature, ’sensitivity and specificity should be
considered inherently stable measures of test performance for all intents and purposes’
and ’do not depend on disease prevalence,’ making them ideal for comparing classifica­
tion approaches across different dataset configurations [69, 70].
In this work, for practitioner identification applications, sensitivity and specificity represent
the most clinically relevant performance. In the doctor­specific evaluation, sensitivity mea­
sures each practitioner’s individual identifiability, since it calculates the proportion of their
procedures that can be correctly attributed to them. High sensitivity indicates distinctive
procedural signatures that enable reliable recognition, which is essential for applications
requiring confident practitioner identification. Specificity quantifies the system’s ability to
avoid false attributions by correctly rejecting procedures performed by other practition­
ers. This metric is important to evaluate the models to understand incorrect practitioner
attributions.

Discussion
This chapter discusses the implication of the EMG signals classification among the seven
different practitioners. The main findings are discussed ins Section 6.1, the methodolog­
ical consideration are explained in Section 6.2, while Section 6.3 concludes with the limi­
tations of this study.
6.1
Interpretation of Main Findings
6.1.1
Practitioner discrimination patterns and signal distinctiveness
The supervised classification results reveal similar patterns for both the SVM and Random
Forest models.
The observed high specificity values indicate that the models excel at avoiding incorrect
practitioner assignments; when the system determines that a signal should not be at­
tributed to a specific doctor, this decision is correct approximately 90% of the time. The
models effectively learn exclusion patterns. Conversely, the lower sensitivity values re­
veal that the models correctly identify only 40.2­44.5% of signals that truly belong to each
practitioner, indicating that positive identification capabilities are constrained.
The general variability in individual practitioner performance metrics (especially in sensi­
tivity and F1­score) shows heterogeneous signal characteristics across the cohort. This
pattern suggests that actual practitioner identification is possible. However, this individu­
ality is limited because the majority of practitioners exhibit moderate to limited sensitivity
values while maintaining high specificity scores. This performance distribution suggests
that most practitioners use similar EMG recording methodologies that generate overlap­
ping signal characteristics, making positive identification challenging while preserving re­
liable negative classification capabilities.
These results are consistent between the SVM model and the Random Forest model.
Moreover, considering balanced training data and the absence of significant differences
between the complete and normal datasets, low sensitivity indicates that certain practi­
tioner pairs exhibit naturally overlapping decision boundaries that limit the discrimination
capabilities of the model. The diagnostic testing literature emphasizes that ”sensitivity
and specificity do not depend on disease prevalence” and represent inherent test char­
acteristics [69]. Therefore, it is probable that this variability pattern indicates that the
classification challenge represents an intrinsic data characteristic.
The clinical interpretation of these findings reveals that practitioners performing elec­
tromyography procedures do have detectable technical variations in their recording method­
ologies. These variations are mild, indicating actual standardization of the protocol, which
maintains reliability across practitioners, but the differences suggest opportunities for
technique consolidation. The detection of practitioner­specific signatures suggests that

current standardization protocols, while effective, have not achieved complete method­
ological uniformity. The optimal scenario would be the complete absence of detectable
practitioner­specific patterns, ensuring that diagnostic outcomes remain independent of
the performing clinician. This methodological homogeneity would guarantee that patient
diagnosis and subsequent therapeutic interventions are determined exclusively by patho­
physiological factors and not influenced by the operator.
6.1.2
Feature importance consistency across dataset configurations
The iterative dataset refinement revealed interesting stability in feature importance rank­
ings across complete, balanced, and normal patient subsets.
In particular, Principal Component Analysis identified the same feature domains as
primary contributors: amplitude­based characteristics dominated PC1, burst­related pat­
terns characterized PC2, and frequency­domain features defined PC3. Random Forest
(which provides robust feature selection through its use of multiple decision trees and
bootstrap sampling) feature importance rankings maintained similar hierarchies regard­
less of dataset composition. A possible interpretation is that these features capture as­
pects of EMG signal acquisition procedures rather than artifacts or pathological conditions.
However, the extraction of features from different domains created a high­dimensional
space that, while comprehensive for exploratory analysis, may have diluted discrimina­
tive signals with redundant or noisy variables that complicated classification algorithms.
From a procedural perspective, the amplitude­based characteristics dominating PC1 are
quantitative and complexity descriptors of the signal that provide information about the
number of active muscle fibers. Investigating these features might suggest that practi­
tioners differ in their ability to achieve optimal muscle activation levels, which may reflect
variations in patient instruction techniques or comfort in achieving voluntary contractions
[52]. The burst­related patterns characterizing PC2 are typical of non­stationary con­
tractions and can indicate tremors (which can be increased in neuropathic conditions).
Practitioners with higher PC2 scores may demonstrate superior ability to maintain sta­
ble recording conditions and minimize patient movement artifacts through effective com­
munication and positioning techniques. The frequency features and zero crossing rate
metrics defining PC3 might be indicators of procedural quality markers, with lower spec­
tral centroids and reduced zero crossing rates indicating controlled, experienced needle
manipulation technique, whereas higher frequency content and increased zero crossing
variability could reflect suboptimal procedural execution characterized by tremor or erratic
needle movements.
This interpretation cannot be considered precise. A more focused investigation, consider­
ing only specific features, would have produced results that are more easily interpretable.
6.1.3
MUP duration independence: diagnostic reliability
Classification with Random Forest did not show significant improvement by adding MUP
duration as a possible discriminative feature, showing that this information does not con­
tribute to possible doctor clustering. Moreover, regression analysis demonstrated that

mean MUP duration measurements operate independently of practitioner­specific signal
characteristics, with EMG features explaining only 4.47% of MUP duration variance. Fur­
thermore, correlation analysis revealed universally weak linear relationships between in­
dividual signal processing features and MUP duration.
The clear independence of MUP duration from practitioner­specific recording character­
istics is the main finding of this work. Since MUP duration serves as the primary discrim­
inative parameter for distinguishing normal from pathological muscle conditions in clini­
cal practice, this significant result validates that diagnoses are practitioner­independent
within the studied hospitals and across the seven practitioners. Consequently, patient
outcomes depend only on the pathophysiology of the muscle and are not influenced by
the specific methodological approach of each examiner. This result provides evidence of
high standardization in diagnostic reliability across different practitioners.
The direct clinical interpretation is that even if there are variations in how the EMG
procedure is performed, the diagnosis is not affected by these technical differences. Fur­
thermore, procedures performed by different doctors on the same muscles will lead to
the same clinical diagnosis, which can potentially result in standardized treatment deci­
sions, a reduced number of incorrect diagnoses, and improved reliability for patient care
continuity.
6.2
Methodological Considerations
6.2.1
Feature dimensionality and pathologic conditions
The study performed classification analysis on a large feature space. Segmented feature
analysis would have reduced the dimensionality and possibly enabled more focused eval­
uation of discriminative power across signal domains. While this approach would have
reduced the exploratory power of the investigation, it could have identified the minimal
feature set necessary for reliable practitioner identification.
An additional methodological consideration concerns the patient stratification criteria em­
ployed for normal versus abnormal subset extraction. The classification threshold of mean
MUP duration within ±20% of reference values was selected to establish a comprehensive
dataset. However, this relatively broad classification window potentially includes muscles
with mild pathological characteristics or neuromuscular alterations that may introduce bi­
ological bias into the dataset.
6.2.2
Outlier Practitioner Analysis and Methodological Implications
The exceptional distinction of Doctor 5 across all analytical approaches suggests either
advanced technical expertise or distinct procedural methodology that creates recogniz­
able signal signatures.
Important information should be considered while analyzing these results. In the signal
processing phase of the method, every single recording (stored as a binary file) was pro­
cessed individually, and the features were extracted and stored one by one. This process

49

did not account for a difference that characterizes the method of Doctor 5. In particular,
it was not considered that the recordings from this practitioner were performed using two
different channels. This means that the EMG was conducted using two distinct needles
simultaneously. Even though the two needles produce two different signals that can be
treated independently, there might be overlapping information about firing patterns from
the same Motor Unit (MU).
To handle the difference of this particular methodological procedure, a validation test to
compare the firing patterns from different needles should be performed. Specifically, the
different firing patterns across the two channels should be identical for the same motor
unit to avoid over­representation of the same MU [8]. This validation step was excluded
from this study due to the specific analytical requirements involved: it would necessitate
detailed parameters including inter­potential intervals and firing rates, which are funda­
mental components of motor unit analysis and motor unit potential processing. The scope
of this investigation was limited to analyzing mean MUP duration as input metadata, mak­
ing comprehensive Motor Unit Potential characterization beyond the intended research
boundaries.
In addition, the over­representation of signals from Doctor 5 can be attributed to the
double­channel recording approach. This imbalance did not compromise the outcomes of
this study because of the balancing methodologies implemented to address the potential
bias introduced by the uneven practitioner representation.
6.3
Limitations of the Study
6.3.1
Generalizability constraints
The sample of seven practitioners represents a narrow subset of clinical EMG practice that
may not generalize beyond the specific contexts studied. Practitioner recruitment from
two hospitals introduces bias that could reflect local training protocols, equipment main­
tenance practices, or clinical standards. Results obtained from this specific practitioner
subset cannot be representative of the broader population of clinical electromyographers
without additional validation studies.
6.3.2
Technical and equipment limitations
Systematic differences between EMG1 and Keypoint recording systems introduce tech­
nical confounds that bias correction procedures may not fully eliminate during the filtering
phase of signal processing.
6.3.3
Patient­related confounding factors
EMG signal characteristics depend on patient cooperation, muscle tension, and physio­
logical state during recording [1, 11]. Factors such as patient anxiety, involuntary move­
ments, or communication difficulties can introduce variability that may result in different
recording patterns attributed to practitioner technique rather than patient factors.
The biological heterogeneity naturally present across the population also creates sys­
tematic bias that might challenge practitioner discrimination. Variations in subcutaneous

Conclusion
This study investigated whether needle EMG signal characteristics are influenced by prac­
titioner technique and whether mean Motor Unit Potential duration depends on practitioner­
specific recording approaches. The findings reveal patterns of practitioner influence that
vary across different analytical approaches and individual medical professionals.
7.1
Research Questions Resolution
Returning to the central research question:
Do individual practitioner techniques in needle EMG recording create detectable signal
variations, and does this variability affect the diagnostic reliability of Motor Unit Potential
measurements?
The findings demonstrate a varied relationship between different practitioners and EMG
signal characteristics. Needle EMG signals do exhibit practitioner­specific influences, but
these effects are highly heterogeneous and require deeper analytical approaches for re­
liable detection. Only one practitioner showed evident singularity across all the analyses,
while the others presented moderate or limited identifiability.
Conversely, mean MUP duration measurements clearly appear to operate independently
of practitioner technique, with signal processing features explaining less than 5% of MUP
duration variance. This independence validates the clinical utility of MUP duration as a
robust physiological parameter that depends only on the muscles. Consequently, this
supports its continued use as a diagnostic criterion.
1. Practitioner Discrimination from Signal Analysis with ML algorithms: The anal­
ysis demonstrates that differentiation among medical practitioners using needle EMG
recordings is moderately achievable across individuals. The discrimination accu­
racy exceeds random chance clustering while remaining limited in performance.
Doctor 5 demonstrated distinctiveness across all models, while others showed mod­
erate or limited identifiability.
In particular, unsupervised clustering revealed highly limited natural grouping among
different doctors, while supervised classification methods distinguished practition­
ers but the performance metrics suggest that the clustering is moderate. The most
robust discrimination emerged when focusing on normal patient populations, sug­
gesting that pathological conditions introduce confounding variability that obscures
technique­specific signatures.
2. Feature Importance and Clinical Relevance: EMG signal processing features
demonstrated general discriminative power, with amplitude­based characteristics
(RMS amplitude, peak­to­peak measurements) and temporal burst patterns emerg­
ing as the most influential for practitioner identification. The consistency of feature

genuine differences in recording methodology, although there is no clear identifica­
tion of the most discriminative features.
3. Differences among healthy and unhealthy subjects: When analyzing datasets

differentiation compared to datasets with mixed pathological conditions. This pat­
tern was consistent across both supervised and unsupervised learning method­
ologies. However, statistical analysis revealed no significant differences in perfor­

dataset types.
4. MUP Duration Independence Assessment: The correlation analysis provides
strong evidence that MUP duration is largely independent of practitioner­specific
signal characteristics.

Future Work
This study provides a foundational framework for practitioner identification using EMG
recordings, but several methodological refinements and expanded investigations can im­
prove technical robustness and clinical applicability.
8.1
Feature Selection Improvement
The current 48­dimensional feature space requires targeted modifications to improve clin­
ical interpretability and reduce computational complexity. Redundant features should be
removed, and there are several techniques to maintain a high degree of variability and in­
formation content by reducing the dataset. The reason is that ML algorithms can perform
better with less polluted data.
Moreover, the correlation structure and interdependencies among EMG signal processing
features identified with the ablation experiment should be taken into account to determine
redundant parameters and optimize feature selection for practitioner identification sys­
tems.
8.2
Pathological Condition Analysis
Investigation of abnormal muscle datasets could be valuable for understanding practi­
tioner adaptation to diverse clinical scenarios. Future analysis should separately exam­
ine neuropathic and myopathic patient populations, where high MUP duration variability
creates distinct signal characteristics that may reveal practitioner­specific diagnostic ap­
proaches.
The pathological subset analysis would provide insights into how practitioners modify their
examination techniques when encountering abnormal muscle conditions. This investiga­
tion could reveal whether practitioner discrimination patterns remain consistent across dif­
ferent pathological contexts or whether adapting the technique to specific disease cases
causes additional discrepancies among medical doctors. From a broader perspective,
this analysis would be the base for training protocols for complex diagnostic scenarios.
8.3
Practitioner Investigation and Outlier Exclusion
Given the significant discriminating performance of Doctor 5 across all other practitioners,
a thorough individual investigation could be interesting to show the precise procedural el­
ements influencing signal distinctiveness. A feature comparison of these signal properties
with established EMG protocols could reveal either advanced technical expertise or non­
standard methodological modifications that enhance or compromise diagnostic quality.
Also, the exclusion of Doctor 5 from the analytical pipeline would reveal clustering be­
havior among the remaining six practitioners without the influence of exceptional signal
54
Do overall signal frequency components influence Motor Unit Potential Size?
==PAGE:63==
distinctiveness that can confound the performance of the models. This analysis would
determine whether subtle practitioner differences emerge when the dominant outlier is
removed, or whether the remaining practitioners demonstrate increased procedural ho­
mogeneity that makes discrimination more challenging.
If clustering performance decreases substantially after Doctor 5 removal, this would indi­
cate that the remaining practitioners employ highly standardized procedural approaches.
Conversely, if secondary clustering structures emerge among the remaining practitioners,
this would suggest that Doctor 5’s performance masks technique variations that become
apparent only through focused analysis.
8.4
Implementation of Procedure Feedback System
Lastly, future work should develop practitioner­specific feedback systems based on iden­
tified discriminative features. The main goal would be enabling objective assessment of
examination quality and consistency during signal recording. These systems could sup­
port training standardization initiatives for novice neurologists and provide quantitative
objective metrics for evaluating procedural competency.
