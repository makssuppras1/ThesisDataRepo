Abstract
Electrochemical migration (ECM) is a critical reliability concern in modern electronic
devices, particularly as miniaturization and growing power demands increase the risk of
corrosion-related failures.
This thesis investigates the ECM mechanisms and dendrite
formation on printed circuit boards (PCBs) with immersion tin (ImSn) surface ﬁnishes.
To study the ECM and the dendritic growth potentiostatic and galvanostatic test were
conducted at various potential/current applied, under a micro volume water layer.
A
digital microscope was used to capture in-situ sequential images of dendritic growth. Sev-
eral characterization techniques, including Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical Emission
Spectroscopy (ICP-OES), Scanning Electron Microscopy/ Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy
(SEM/EDS), and X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS), were employed to analyse the
samples post-testing. Additional pH analysis was carried out to study the pH gradient
and its evolution over time.
The results indicate a correlation between electrical bias and the extent of corrosion and
amount of dissolution, with dendritic growth occurring only at higher voltage/current
levels. Tin dissolution is preferred in the ﬁrst stage of the process, followed by tin passiva-
tion which leads to a reducing dissolution rate. Hydrolysis is responsible for the majority
of charge consumption, inﬂuencing pH gradients and dendrite composition. The study
conﬁrms that Sn-Cu dendrites exhibit a mixed composition of metallic structures and
oxides/hydroxides, with pH and bias level playing a key role in their formation. These
ﬁndings provide valuable insights into ECM mechanisms, relevant for improving reliability
in electronic devices.

signiﬁcant advances in electronic devices. This progress, combined with the desire for
smaller and more eﬃcient designs (see ﬁgure 1.1), has led to the miniaturization of the
Printed Circuit Board (PCB) assemblies. Simultaneously, the need for increased power in
electronic devices, has resulted in higher voltage levels being applied to PCBs.
The combination of the shrinking distance between components on the PCB and the rising
voltage applied, presents a signiﬁcant challenge to system reliability. This is particularly
critical in terms of corrosion risk.
As a result, improving the reliability of electronic
devices has become a major topic. A transition towards electriﬁcation has interested most
industries such as renewable energy, transportation, power conversion and storage. This
transition has resulted in a strong need for reliable systems to support these industries,
which are heavily dependent on these technologies.

1.1
Corrosion in electronic devices
“Corrosion can be deﬁned as the degradation of a metal due to a reaction with its envi-
ronment” [2].
Historically, corrosion has been associated with metals; however, it can aﬀect all kind of
materials, including polymers and ceramics. The corrosion mechanisms that will inﬂuence
the degradation of the diﬀerent types of material will depend on the nature and character-
istic of the category taken into consideration. If polymers are sensible to UV light, instead
ceramics are prone to corrosion due to high temperatures and aggressive chemicals. Nev-
ertheless, the common eﬀect corrosion has on all materials, is the decrease of mechanical
properties and an alteration in the chemical structure, leading to reduced functionality,
loss of material or failure [3].
Among the various applications where corrosion can be highly detrimental, electronic
devices are one of them. The application and vast use of electronic devices in many aspects
of our life and the introduction of these in countless applications has demanded them to
function and survive in harsh environments.
The multiple metallic parts in the PCB
are vulnerable to corrosion due to the exposure to humidity, air pollutants and corrosive
gases [4]. The exposure to humid environments, combined with varying temperatures,
causes corrosion problems to the susceptible material combinations used in PBCs. Clearly,
electronic devices are rarely in direct contact with water, however the major risk is the
humidity in the air which, with temperature ﬂuctuations, can easily undergo condensation
and its deposition on the PCB leads to the formation of a water layer. This thin water
layer is responsible for the creation of interconnections among components, that leads to
the start of corrosion mechanisms. The miniaturization of electronic devices in recent
years has facilitated the role of the water layer, enabling connections to be made over
shorter distances [4, 5].

reduced spacing between electronic components and the increasing voltage applied to the

Research Motivation
This MSc thesis investigates the electrochemical migration (ECM) mechanism in elec-
tronics. A thorough understanding of these fundamental corrosion processes is lacking

2.1
Printed Circuit Boards
Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs) are the core element of electronic devices as they allow
the connection between components in the electronic assemblies. Many active and passive
components are mounted on the PCB, and the spacing between these units is a crucial
aspect from the corrosion and reliability point of view. The demand for increased func-
tionalities in electronic devices, coupled with their miniaturization, translates into the
requirement to mount a greater number of components on a single PCB. These compo-
nents have also become signiﬁcantly smaller over time, resulting in more compact and
densely packed boards. The consequence of this progress, while enhancing device capabil-
ities, presents a tough challenge to make the PCB reliable and resistant to corrosion.
2.1.1
Structure & Components
A PCB is constituted by four essential parts:
• a non-conductive substrate or laminate;
• conductive printed circuits;
• a solder mask layer;
• mounted components. [12]

ﬁbreglass embedded in epoxy resin with brominated ﬂame retardant in the epoxy matrix
[13]. The printed conductive circuits generally consists of copper. The laminated copper
is placed onto the laminate and then, after protecting the “artwork” of copper lines and
connection with an etch resistant material, it is exposed to an etching process.

Soldering
The assembly process includes diﬀerent steps, a fundamental one being soldering. Solder-
ing is a metallurgical joining method to connect the components to the laminate board
and its copper lines, creating the mechanical and electrical connection between them [16].
In the SMT, after the components have been placed on the board, they are soldered by
two types of soldering processes: wave soldering or reﬂow soldering [17].
Wave soldering is a method that was ﬁrst extensively used for pin-through-hole connec-
tions. In this process the PCB is ﬁrst sprayed with a foam ﬂux, then passed through a
heating element, and ﬁnally it is passed over a solder bath. At the end of the process the
bonding between the components is accomplished. However, the presence of components
on the bottom of the board interfere with the laminar ﬂow of the solder bath leading to
some shadowing eﬀects that cause defects in the soldering joint [16, 17].
To overcome the problems presented by the wave soldering technique, the reﬂow soldering
was introduced and became the most used joining method in SMT processes. In the reﬂow
soldering a solder paste is applied to one of the mating surfaces, usually the solder pads
laying on the board, by using a stencil like shown in ﬁgure 2.6. The solder paste consists
in a pre-blended mixture of solder powder and a ﬂux, which is quite viscous to ensure it
stays in place after application. The ﬂux has the function to remove oxides from both
mating surfaces upon heating, creating clean solder joints. Afterwards, the components
are placed on the board by a pick-and-place system. Finally, the board goes through a
reﬂow oven with a well deﬁned heating proﬁle which allows the paste to melt and form
the solder joint upon solidiﬁcation [16, 17].

2.1.3
Solder pads
Another important aspect of the soldering process is the forming of the solder joint which
requires more than simply melting and solidiﬁcation of the solder paste, and here the base
metal plays a crucial role. To form a metallurgical bond, the base metal from the solder
pads and the solder paste need to mix at the atomic level to form intermetallic compounds
(IMC) [19].
Solder pads are generally made of the same base material characterizing the underlying
copper circuit.
Afterwards, they undergo a surface ﬁnish process to make them more
suitable for soldering. The coating material used for the surface ﬁnish process holds the
function of protecting the copper underneath from oxidation and increase the solderability
and wettability of the surface. Surface ﬁnishes are rather thin to not impact on ﬁnal volume
and weight of the PCB. Researcher have been looking into the properties of surface ﬁnish
aiming at improving the performances of this layer reducing the unwanted formation of
IMC which can cause cracking issues hindering the soldering performance [20].
Common surface ﬁnishing processes include: ENIG (Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold),
ImSn (Immersion Tin), HASL (Hot Air Solder Leveling), Sn-Ag surface ﬁnishes. However,
HASL is no longer used as it contains lead (which is now banned for toxicity reasons, more
information will be illustrated in next paragraph regarding this issue). A lead-free version
of HASL has been introduced as a replacement (LF-HASL) [20].
For this study, the surface ﬁnish of highest interest is the ImSn since it is the one applied
over copper on the test samples. ImSn coating oﬀers a good solderability and allows for
a simple deposition method. The process for applying the ImSn coating consists of three
phases:

ageing [21].
It is important to remember that, if on one hand IMC growth is wanted to create a
strong bonding at the solder joints, on the other hand uncontrolled growth and whiskers
development can lead to mechanical instability and cracking issues in the solder since the
typical intermetallic compounds are brittle in nature [24].

Lead-free solder alloys
The right material to join the components on the board needs to posses many properties

electronics assemblies. Historically, tin-lead (Sn-Pb) eutectic solder alloys have been the

2.3
Typical mechanisms of electronics corrosion
Electronic devices are a complex system, composed of diﬀerent materials therefore sus-
ceptible to many types of corrosion. Some, such as galvanic corrosion, are also typical
of conventional corrosion mechanisms, whereas others are characteristic of printed circuit
boards due to their intrinsic properties.
2.3.1
Galvanic corrosion in electronics
When two metals are in physical contact and connected by an electrolyte, galvanic corro-
sion can take place. A galvanic cell works like an electrochemical cell with the less noble
(or more reactive) metal acting as anode, and the more noble one not taking part in the
corrosion process with its own ions but providing a surface for the cathodic reaction to
happen[37, 31].
Although the probability of galvanic corrosion is high due to the use of many diﬀerent metal
in a closely packed space, this corrosion mechanism inside electronic devices is typically
associated to the ageing of the device rather than a short-term failure issue. However, a
practical example can be found in the ENIG surface ﬁnish which consist in diﬀerent metals
layered in the following order from bottom to top: copper, nickel and gold. The top layer of
gold is relatively thin and can lead to porosity that expose the nickel underneath. Looking
at the electrochemical series 2.1, the potential diﬀerence is large and this leads to the fast
formation of pits in the nickel layer due to the large cathodic area oﬀered by the gold top
layer. In the worst cases the corrosion is so deep that it can reach the base copper leading
to failure of the surface coating [38].

Electrochemical migration consists in ionic migration resulting from an electrochemical
process. The result of this migration and redeposition of conductive metal ﬁlaments is
called a dendrite.
A necessary condition is the presence of a water/electrolyte media
connecting the two biased points to allow the ions transport. This layer may be either a
continuous thin water layer or an invisible adsorbed layer, which generally has a thickness
ranging from 1nm to 10 µm. Another critical factor, as stated in the deﬁnition, is the
applied voltage bias, that can vary depending on the application generally going from 2
V to 450 V, but reaching 1000 V in speciﬁc cases[3]. When both of these conditions are
met dendritic growth can occur [40].
These conditions reﬂect the formation of en electrolytic cell (previously mentioned and
shown in ﬁgure 1.2b.
The basic mechanism for ECM consist in an electrochemical process with a transfer of
charge between the two parts that show a potential diﬀerence. The process consist of two
reactions: cathodic (reduction) and anodic (oxidation). The polar electrolyte, hence the
thin water layer, fulﬁls the function of closing the circuit by ion conduction. Therefore, the
electrolyte composition plays a large role in this process, because if the resistivity changes
due to contamination of the water layer, the increase in conductivity leads to a higher
charge transport between the two electrodes, translating in a higher corrosion rate.
The anode and the cathode are deﬁned areas where diﬀerent reactions take place:
• Anode: is the oxidation reaction site and corresponds to the positive potential site.
The anodic reaction consists in the metal dissolution into ions with release of elec-
trons. Water dissociation will also happen for voltages over 1.23V [41]. The reactions
are the following:

(2.6)

process.
• Cathode: is the reduction reaction site and is the negative potential site. At the
cathode many diﬀerent reactions based on potential and pH of the solution can be
favoured. Here a couple of possible reactions are illustrated:

The build-up of the thin water layer depends on the humidity and temperature of the

Stages of ECM
The ECM mechanism can be divided in three steps as illustrated in ﬁgure 2.11 and they
are:
1. Dissolution of metal
2. Ionic transport
3. Deposition of metal ions [42].
Figure 2.11: Schematic representation of the dendritic mechanism and growth: (0) clean surface,
(1) forming of the electrolyte layer, (2) metal’s ion dissolution, (3) ion’s movement and transport,
(4) deposition and formation of dendrites [42].
Each of them will be further explained in the following sections.
1. Dissolution of metal
In this stage the metal dissolution reaction will occur at the anode:
M −−→Mn+ + n e−
(2.10)
The metal releases metallic ions and free electrons which will fuel the other half-reaction at
the cathode. Possible cathodic reactions can be reduction of oxygen or hydrogen evolution.
The latter can be very visible and will appear as bubbling in the electrolyte in the areas
corresponding to the cathode. The dissolution of metal is crucial for the ECM process as
it provides the metallic ions that will be necessary for the dendritic growth [42].
2. Ionic transport
Once the metallic ions are dissolved in the solution, they will migrate to the cathode by
the eﬀect of the electric ﬁeld, the diﬀusion or the convection.
The electric ﬁeld that is generated when the voltage is applied is responsible for the
electrical migration of the ions. The intensity of the electric ﬁeld depends on the distance
between the biased points and the voltage applied as follows:

E = V

[5]
(2.11)
where E= electric ﬁeld, V= applied voltage, d= distance. Shorter distances and higher
voltages produce stronger electric ﬁelds.
The velocity of the ions will depend upon three parameters: the intensity of the electric
ﬁeld, the mobility of the ions (related to their size and charge) and the properties of the
electrolyte (such as pH and temperature). Another important factor related to the elec-
trolyte is the conductivity, a better conductivity will favour the ion migration transport.
The dissolving species will accumulate close to the anode, on the other side the cathodic
reactions will produce a high amount of hydroxides. This concentration gradient will lead
to a diﬀusion process in the solution.
Lastly, the convection is not an clear eﬀect in a thin water layer, but it can be induced
by the intense bubbling due to the hydrogen evolution at the cathode.
The constant
formation and collapse of these bubbles causes a convection eﬀect in the electrolyte aiding
the ion movement [42].
3. Deposition of metal ions
At the cathode the opposite reaction for the metal deposition will happen:
Mn+ + n e−−−→M
(2.12)
The dendritic formation consists of two consecutive phases: initiation and growth. The
initiation requires an overpotential, which is usually represented by the bias voltage applied
to the system. Once the growth has started, the propagation stage proceeds at a faster
pace. The dendrite, growing, will have a higher electric ﬁeld at the tip and this, together
with the reduced distance, promoting a preferential deposition of the metallic ions at the
tip. The typical structure of a dendrite is tree-like propagating from cathode to anode.
The moment the dendrite connects the anode to the cathode, shorting occurs leading to
intermittent or deﬁnitive failure of the device. An extremely high LC will pass through the
dendrite depending on the dendrite nature itself and its conductive properties. A dendrite
can be made of both pure metal and/or hydroxides of the metal, and pH conditions play
a role in deﬁning what type of species are stable when the deposition happen [42, 3].
With the miniaturization of devices and higher component density on the board the dis-
tances are reduced making a fertile environment for dendritic growth, increasing the risk
for ECM corrosion failure in modern devices [3].

Test methods
Corrosion comes in various types and forms, therefore many testing techniques are nec-
essary to study these mechanisms. Corrosion testing techniques are classiﬁed in three
categories: laboratory tests, ﬁeld tests, service tests. Many techniques also involve the
attempt of accelerating the corrosion eﬀects to study the targeted phenomena in shorter
times. Electrochemical methods are essential to study the corrosion processes, especially
in electronics [46].
In the particular case of electrochemical migration three tests are mainly used in experi-
mental testing:
• Thermal Humidity Bias (THB). This test is performed in a chamber where humidity
and temperature parameters can be controlled and the sample is connected to a
power supply to apply a bias. Condensation eﬀect can be studied with this test.
The parameters are chosen based on the speciﬁc requirements of the tests which can
reproduce the application environment or accelerate test conditions. To monitor the
experiment an optical microscope can be connected to visually record the changes
over time, or electrical output can be measured to collect leakage current, insulation
resistance or voltage change.
• Water Drop (WD). In this case, the biased points are connected by a water droplet
deposited on the sample. This drop is usually distilled water, however it can also
contain some deﬁned electrolyte. Its volume can vary depending on the distance
between the two electrodes. The volume of the droplet can be of great importance
as the ECM is inﬂuenced by edge eﬀects. Similarly as before, an ammeter can be
connected to collect the current output.
This test is usually conducted at room
temperature.
• Thin Electrolyte Layer (TEL)[47]. This test is conducted in similar conditions as
the WD, where the main diﬀerence is the shape of the electrolyte. If before it was
a droplet, here is deposited as thin water layer. As before, in situ images are taken
by employing a digital microscope and the electrical output signal is recorded and
stored.

2.5.2
Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy works with a similar system of lenses as the LOM, with
the exception that the focused beam consist in electrons rather than light. This allows for
a much higher magniﬁcation (up to x500000) and better resolution (up to 0.5 nm). The
electron beam is generated and accelerated towards the sample by a positive electrical

electron beam collides on the surface of the specimen the interaction and eﬀect cause by

When the beam hits the surface, the electrons are scattered and adsorbed into the upper
layer of the material, losing energy along the way. The interactions have a typical shape of
a teardrop, as shown in ﬁgure 2.13, which extends in depth to a maximum of 10 µm. The
electrons can interact with the specimen on diﬀerent levels, giving various output signals
collected by speciﬁc detectors. The signals collected are then interpreted and converted
into an image. The signals are classiﬁed based on the types of interaction they have with
the sample and consequently they provide diﬀerent kinds of information:
• Backscattered Electrons: these are electrons that are redirected by interactions with
the nuclei of atoms in the specimen.
Their deﬂection back toward the detector
depends on the size of the nuclei they encounter. Regions containing elements with
higher atomic numbers appear brighter in the resulting image, as larger nuclei are
more likely to scatter electrons eﬀectively. This makes backscattered electrons useful
for distinguishing areas with varying atomic numbers. These electrons have high
energy.
• Secondary Electrons: these electrons have a low energy and are collected by a special
detector. Having low energy they come from very low depth and therefore charac-

These electrons are generated by inelastic
interactions with the atoms of the sample.
• Auger Electrons: these are a special type of low energy electrons and their analysis
is surface-sensitive. The Auger electrons are emitted by the atoms as a result of a
relaxation: when hit with a high energy electron beam, a second electron from the
core shells of the atom can be emitted causing an electron from an outer shell to
fall down to ﬁll the hole left behind from the emitted electron. This transition frees
a certain amount of energy that is then absorbed by a third electron and this is
ejected. This third electron is called Auger.
The parameters that characterize the shape and size of the electron-specimen interaction
depends on the accelerating voltage of the beam and the average atomic number of the
substrate. One one hand, the higher energy the colliding electrons have, the bigger the
interaction volume will be; on the other hand the bigger atomic number and the denser
the sample is, the smaller the interaction volume will be.
The sensitivity of this instrument requires the use of vacuum to analyse the sample. In
the absence of vacuum, the electron beam would be scattered and lose energy by the
interaction with the air molecules resulting in a lower-quality image [50, 51].

XPS is a surface characterization technique widely used for measuring elemental compo-

The behaviour of the Sn-Cu system under a constant applied voltage was examined through
potentiostatic testing, providing insights into dendritic growth, corrosion mechanisms, and

This section presents the results, detailing the current response,
metal dissolution, and the mechanism of electrochemical migration.
Literature has shown that voltage bias strongly inﬂuences the ECM mechanism [61]. Volt-
age aﬀects multiple stages of ECM, including ion transport, dissolution, and dendrite
formation. For example, the electric ﬁeld, directly correlated with voltage, plays a key
role in driving ion migration, accelerating the overall process. This is why studying this
process under varying voltages can provide insight into the electrochemical mechanisms
at diﬀerent conditions.

Chapter 5
Discussion
Failure in electronic devices presents a signiﬁcant reliability concern, but often the root
cause is left unclear. Understanding electronic failure is challenging as it involves an inter-
connection between electrical and material sciences. Electrochemical Migration (ECM) is
a perfect example where the failure symptom consists of an electrical output, but the root
cause lies in corrosion science. In the case of a PCB, multiple components are mounted in
a close space, and depending on their function and input need, they might be supplied by
diﬀerent voltages. Thus, that is the starting point of this research, where a potentiostatic
test is carried out applying multiple voltage levels to the PCB sample.
Overall, a general observation is that a higher voltage causes an increase in current elec-
trical output. This aligns with Ohm’s Law, which states that for a constant resistance, a
higher voltage results in a higher current. However, when considering additional electro-
chemical factors, the phenomena becomes more intricate and challenging to understand.
To dive deeper in this electrochemistry mechanism, ﬁrstly the potentiostatic test is dis-
cussed, by the voltage levels. Then galvanostatic test are discussed.
5.1
Low voltage potentiostatic test
Low voltage levels applied, namely 1V and 2V, showed no dendritic growth in the tests.
This is related to the above mentioned voltage levels which lie in a very narrow range below
or slightly above the hydrolysis value (which corresponds to 1.23V [41]). Therefore, the
electrolyte conductivity, which is extremely low due to the very pure water used for testing,
is maintained low, with no release of ions in the solution. For this reasons, ion dissolution
rate is slow leading to a limited concentration of metal ions for ECM, preventing dendritic
growth.
5.2
High voltage potentiostatic test
At higher voltage levels (5V, 10V and 48V), higher current outputs are observed, with
leakage current (LC) peaks corresponding to dendritic short circuits. When an increasing
voltage is applied, it leads not only to higher electrical current response in the system,
but also to shorter failure time. The average time to failure decreases from 11.9 minutes
for 5V, to 8.66 minutes for 10V, to just a few minutes for 48V. This indicates a clear

57
==PAGE:72==
correlation between the applied voltage and the dendritic growth. This is caused by two
factors, which are interconnected: the metal dissolution and the hydrolysis.
The metal dissolution is seen in the ICP results, which, up to 10V, show a clear increasing
trend for tin dissolution. Additionally, it is seen that tin is the ﬁrst element to dissolve
preferentially in the electrolyte, which is most likely due to the greater surface availability,
as a result of the ImSn coating deposition on top of copper. Tin also seems to reach a
dissolution limit, which can be related to a passivation ﬁlm responsible for protecting the
metal. This is due to the fact that tin is known for forming a stable passive layer which
reduces the dissolution rate [35].
Coming to the hydrolysis contribution, here its inﬂuence is visible from the in-situ micro-
scope images revealing the bubbling, and also from the pH gradient study. From the water
splitting reactions (oxygen evolution at the anode 2.5, and hydrogen evolution at the cath-
ode 2.8), an acidic environment is created towards the anode and a basic towards the cath-
ode. This evolving pH gradient will inﬂuence the reactions happening in the system, and
looking at the Pourbaix diagrams it can be estimated which reactions are more favoured.
From literature, tin dissolution, forming either stannous ions ( Sn −−→Sn2+ + 2 e−) or
stannic ions (Sn2+ −−→Sn4+ + 2 e−), is favoured in the anodic acidic environment. On
the other hand, when the ions are redepositing at the cathode, the stannous ions will tend
to react forming hydroxides in neutral environment (Sn4+ + 4 H2O −−→Sn(OH)4 + 4 H+)
[40].
Coming back to the current output, the high current can lead to further information when
considering its relation to charge consumption in the process. The higher the current, the
larger the amount of charges ﬂowing though the system. Similarly to tin dissolution, up
to 10V, the total charge consumed is increasing with the voltage applied (Figure 4.7).
The resistance of the dendritic structure is also evaluated though the study of its mor-
phology and chemical composition, thanks to SEM-EDS and XPS. SEM images show
a transformation of the dendrite morphology from 5V, where more sharp and branched
structures are observed, to 10V represented by more bulky and dense dendrites. From
literature this morphologies are related to the nature of the corrosion products, where
metallic ﬁlaments are more sharp, while bulky formations are oxides or hydroxides [69].
Chemical composition is studied with XPS, where the spectra conﬁrmed the coexistence
of metallic and oxides/hydroxides compounds.
In relation to the hydroxides, their solubility product constant (Ksp) should also be con-
sidered. The Ksp will aﬀect which component will reach the maximum solubility in the
electrolyte, and therefore will be more incline to precipitate and redeposit forming the
dendrite. In table 5.1 are summarized values for Ksp for copper and tin hydroxides in
pure water. The lower solubility of tin favours its precipitation and deposition at the
cathode.

resistance, since the heat energy produced when the current ﬂow through the ﬁlament is
directly related to it (see relationship 3.4). From this relationship, it can be realized that
the heat produced increases for high resistance paired with high current, creating a risk
of burning of the dendrite. If the heat generated is suﬃcient to evaporate the electrolyte

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This research has provided a detailed investigation into the mechanisms of Sn-Cu den-
drite formation due to electrochemical migration (ECM) on printed circuit boards with
immersion tin ﬁnishes. Through both potentiostatic and galvanostatic testing on a two-
pad ImSn PCB conﬁguration, the study reveals that the system response and growth of
dendritic growth are highly sensitive to the applied electrical conditions.
Following the Ohm’s law, higher electrical inputs corresponds to higher dissolution and
shorter failure time. Dendritic growth was observed only above an empirical threshold
value, highlighting the role of electrical bias in triggering and sustaining corrosion pro-
cesses. This mechanism is valid only up to a certain voltage/current level, above which
distinct behaviour start, such as violent convection, pH neutralization and dendrite crack-
ing.
The dissolution rate is increasing by applying higher electrical bias. Tin is preferentially
dissolving due to the higher surface availability, but its dissolution is slowed down after
the ﬁrst initial phase as it seems to undergo passivation and form a protective layer.
The chemical composition and morphology of the dendrites indicate the presence of both
metallic structures and oxide/hydroxide compounds. This formation is inﬂuenced by the
pH gradient in the electrolyte, which itself is determined by hydrolysis reactions. The
nature of the dendrite directly impacts the resistance of the ﬁlaments, playing a crucial
role in heat dissipation and the potential risk of dendrite burning.
In summary, this study provides important insights into ECM mechanisms and oﬀers an
experimental basis for understanding dendrite formation. This knowledge is essential for
developing better strategies to prevent dendrite-related failures in electronic devices.
6.1
Future work
The basis for understanding the mechanism behind ECM are studied in this project.
However, additional studies are necessary to improve the testing conditions to match real
applications environment.
Environmental conditions
The eﬀect of electrolyte contamination has been discussed in the background theory pre-
sented in Chapter 2, but it has not been investigated in this project.

Another important area for future research is the application of higher voltages. To meet

Combining the insights from this project with further research will provide a strong knowl-
