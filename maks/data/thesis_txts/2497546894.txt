Abstract 
The study aims to close the gap between theory and the practical implementation of a circular 
economy in the campus development of the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The method 
consisted of four main stages. First, an exploratory theoretical research in the literature on circular 
economy. Secondly, an overview of DTU campus, including published information, internal 
documentation and insights from several meetings with relevant actors from campus service at DTU. 
Findings, such as enablers and disablers of circular economy, awareness, and identifying current 
examples of circularity, were part of the study to define DTU baseline in terms of circularity. Thirdly, 
an analysis between the review in the literature and the overview of DTU was done to discuss the 
implementation of a circular economy in campus development. System thinking, ReSOLVE 
framework, network management, a proposal of a CE indicators framework and several strategies 
were identified as the tools and methods to implement CE thinking in campus development; these 
were discussed and analyzed. Finally, the work resulted in a roadmap to implement CE thinking in 
campus development at DTU, the conditions that must be met to transition and a proposal to report 
and monitor on some KPIs. In conclusion, the study helps to address the knowledge gap in the 
implementation of CE thinking in higher education within campus development, and it serves as a 
case study.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
The construction of a building refers to all phases across the value chain that make up that building. 
With all the inputs and outputs, from the structure to the ‘stuff’ inside. The construction industry has 
a very high impact on the environment and the limited resources on Earth, and is responsible for the 
following (Realdania 2019): 
▪ One-third of the waste (The Danish Ministry of Environment and Food 2018) 
The waste hierarchy adopted by Denmark is the following, first pre-treatment for reuse, secondly 
recycling, followed by incineration and finally landfilling. In Denmark, construction and demolition 
waste correspond to 86% recycling (mostly down-cycling1), 7% Landfilling and 6% incineration 
(Deloitte et al. 2015). Waste from construction and demolition includes concrete, asphalt, wood, and 
stone. However, this accounts for structural elements mostly. There are other types of waste coming 
from the skin, services and ‘stuff’ inside a building. Moreover, waste is created in all phases in the 
value chain. 
At present day, the construction industry produces tonnes of waste, in Denmark, it represents one-
third of all waste in the country (The Danish Ministry of Environment and Food 2018). Therefore, 
there is tremendous potential to recover value in materials and resources. However, in Denmark, 
material is mostly recovered for low value (The Danish Ministry of Environment and Food 2018). 
▪ Depletion of natural resources - 40% of materials consumption is used for construction 
(Realdania 2019) 
Demand in construction is rising at an incredibly fast pace. One main reason is the increasing 
population, 2.4 billion more people in 2050. Other reasons include the population shift from rural to 
city areas, and countries, like India, are becoming developed nations in the next decades. The 
equivalent to the floor plan of New York will be built every month to meet this demand (Gates, B. 
2019). This high demand could lead to shortages of some of the main raw materials use for 
construction and have a direct effect on rising raw materials prices. Currently, in Denmark, the cost 
of raw materials is about 50% of production (Guldmann, E. 2016). It is estimated that Denmark could 
gain 6 billion Euros annually by becoming circular, savings of up to 50DKK per working hour 
(Guldmann, E. 2016). 
The second most-consumed resource in the world after water is sand, a key element in the production 
of concrete in the construction, the evidence is showing that the world is facing sand shortages and 
criminal bands are getting involved in illegal markets, leading to enormous consequences at the 
environmental and human level (BBC 2019). Between 2011 and 2013, China has used more sand 
than the United States did in the entire 20th century (BBC 2019). Hence, the prices of such resources 
could go up significantly in the years to come. 
In Europe, people live as if the world would have 2.8 times the current natural resources (WWF 
2019). Brundtland (1987) stated, "Sustainable development is the development that meets the needs 
of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs." 
(International Institute for Sustainable Development 2019). From its definition, the consumption of 
natural resources in the construction industry is currently unsustainable.

Even though this is a staggering number, the insufficiency of materials for the construction industry 
is not there yet, hence it is not the primary motivation for Denmark to shift to a CE.  
▪ 25-40% of the world's CO2 emissions 
The main motivation to transition to a CE is the reduction of CO2 emissions, where Denmark must 
reduce by 70% in 2030 to meet the new Climate Act target towards net zero in 2050 (State of Green 
2019). A large proportion of greenhouse gas emissions comes from this sector. A lot of energy is 
needed across all phases in the value chain, from the extraction of raw material, processing, 
distribution, manufacturing, construction, use, and demolition. 
People's health and wellbeing depend on the healthy state of the planet, hence limiting and reducing 
our ecological footprint in the built environment is essential.  Actions need to be taken forward to cut 
down the waste being generated in the construction and avoid further extraction of new raw material 
and pursue a reduction of greenhouse gas emissions.  
Circular Economy 
The field of Circular Economy (CE) has become very popular over the past decade, giving rise to an 
increasing number of articles and has opened discussions among experts. CE has become a key focus 
in Europe, and it is particularly strong in the construction industry. CE is revolutionary by concept, 
even though there is not a unified definition, there is a similar understanding of the theme.  
‘‘CE deﬁnition as a new economic model based on regenerative and restorative cycles aiming at 
decoupling economic growth from resource depletion and environmental degradation, increasing the 
overall system resilience and creating and preserving economic, environmental and social value to 
maximize human well-being’’ (Fonseca et al. 2018). ‘‘Meaning less input and use of natural 
resources. Increased share of renewable and recyclable resources and energy. Reduced emissions. 
Fewer material losses or residuals. Keeping the value of products, components and materials in the 
economy’’ (Antikainen, R. et al. 2018).  
It is clear from these definitions that achieving a CE could reduce significantly the negative impacts 
of the industry. Currently, the world is only 9.1 % circular, leaving a massive 'Circularity Gap' (Circle 
Economy 2018). The question then is: how to transition from this theoretical definition into actual 
implementation?  
Universities are the role models of society, and they play an essential role in promoting sustainability. 
They are responsible for giving an example and inspiring students to solve the issues the world is 
facing. As Mendoza, F. et al. (2019) mentioned in his work ‘‘Higher Education Institutes represent 
an area of particular interest for CE implementation due to their socioeconomic relevance for the 
service sector and their influential role in supporting sustainable development in cities and regions 
worldwide (Ferrer-Balas et al., 2008)’’. For this reason, the area of university campus development 
will be investigated. 
Case study 
The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) is not only a technical leader university but also has a 
strong focus on sustainability, creating new and innovative solutions to tackle the most challenging 
problems described by the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (UN SDGs). DTU as an 
organization, continuously acts and redesigns itself to become a more sustainable environment 
wherever it is possible, from waste management, energy and resource consumption to the 
transportation footprint. DTU is expanding, where new high-quality facilities and buildings are being 
built to set an inviting campus for the 11,300 students and 6,000 employees and growing (DTU 2019). 
==PAGE:11==

Therefore, the development of a CE strategy embedded into the sustainability practice is of interest 
to a growing organization like DTU to become more sustainable, reduce impacts, and achieve a higher 
level of performance with less resource consumption in current and new constructions. According to 
the sustainability policy for DTU's campuses, ‘‘DTU must be at the forefront of the transition to a 
CE’’ (DTU Policy 2019). In alignment with the strategy launched by the Danish Ministry of 
Environment and Food in 2018. 
Campus Service (CAS) is the department within DTU responsible for the operation, development, 
and maintenance of DTU buildings, areas, and facilities. DTU CAS is responsible for answering the 
need and demands of students and researchers in the most sustainable way possible. Campus 
development has an impact on the environment and therefore must be managed. Therefore, the report 
will focus on DTU campus development as a case study. To set a boundary, campus development 
refers to all the construction projects of current and new buildings taking place in the university. 
Prior work 
The application of CE principles in the construction industry remains a question to be answered 
(Adams, K. et al. 2017). There has been a research initiative in Europe that examines and designs 
circular solutions, called Building Materials as Banks (BAMB). Also, powerful tools such as Building 
Information Modelling (BIM) have proven to be helpful for buildings to become more circular (Yun-
Tsui et al. 2019). Innovative ideas towards a CE have developed to a certain extent, such as 3R, 
modular design, resource management, sharing economy and international building passports. 
However, few studies that focus of the application of CE in campus development have been done. 
Two papers have shown a study of the implementation of CE at the campus level; Birgit H. et al. 
(2019) and Mendoza et al. (2018). Birgit H. et al. (2019) introduces a framework for the application 
of circular principles in campus development at Dutch Universities. Moreover, Mendoza et al. (2019) 
propose a methodological framework for the implementation of CE thinking in higher education 
institutions. Both pieces of work have been used and adapted to develop the project plan (Appendix 
A: Project Plan). 
A gap in the literature 
According to Birgit H. et al. (2019) ‘‘construction, development and redevelopment projects usually 
focus on limited aspects of circularity and are separate from each other. In the area of sustainable 
development of the physical campus, there is a need for the elaboration of practice-oriented strategies 
for an integrated approach. There is no systematic overview available of implementation strategies 
for the principles of a CE in area development or campus development’’. 
‘‘The University of Edinburgh (2015) evaluated the implementation of CE thinking in curricula 
development, outreach activities, and campus management. The findings revealed that the CE 
concept was not yet fully embedded within current campus operations due to an overall lack of 
awareness and understanding of the CE concept among staff, including conservative and rigid 
governance structures and operating models, insufficient policies, limited stakeholder collaboration 
and decision-support frameworks, as well as time and financial constraints.’’ (Mendoza, F. et al. 
2019). 
‘‘Another relevant challenge to facilitate the implementation of a CE in the university includes the 
lack of access to appropriate decision-support frameworks, data gathering systems and key 
performance indicators. Appropriate target setting, effective KPIs and appropriate CE and 
sustainability strategies are also essential for driving meaningful changes. However, HEIs’ 
sustainability management and reporting systems need further development by embedding CE 
thinking fully’’ (Mendoza et al., 2019)

Research question 
The question that this project will address is How can the Technical University of Denmark 
effectively implement circular economy thinking at campus development - new and current 
buildings and constructions? 
The case study will focus on the main campus in Lyngby, one of the six locations across Denmark. 
To answer the research question is vital to understand: 
▪ Where is DTU campus standing in terms of circularity? 
▪ Investigate current activities and trends towards a CE in the construction industry. 
▪ What is the role of campus service in the development of DTU? 
▪ What practices, strategies, methodologies, and frameworks, related to a CE, are being 
implemented by DTU? Which ones could be applied to foster the transition? 
And, implement the project plan described (Appendix A: Project Plan). 
Impact – Innovation and application 
Many authors state the enormous benefits of adopting a CE in the three sustainability pillars, the 
environment, economy, and society. CE could help organizations and companies to reach UN SDGs. 
Also, solutions to mitigate the negative impacts of the construction industry are needed. Furthermore, 
it has been found that a gap exists in circularity in campus development, ‘‘Campus organizations 
want to get to work with circularity, but do not know exactly how to put it into practice.’’ (Birgit, H. 
et al. 2019). Therefore, to address this gap, the focus research area is the implementation of CE 
thinking in the campus development at the Technical University of Denmark, Lyngby campus. 
The contribution from this piece of work is expected to add knowledge regarding the implementation 
of CE thinking in higher education campuses and suggest a proposal of a holistic approach to 
systematically apply CE principles on new and current buildings and constructions at DTU. 
Therefore, reduce the circularity gap in campus development. This project will cover the 
informational perspective and give place to a qualitative assessment. The aim is to provide DTU CAS 
a roadmap together with tools, frameworks & guidelines, to become more circular.

Chapter 2: Method 
The method carried out was partially similar to the one proposed by Mendoza et al. (2019); and a 
framework for facilitating the implementation of a CE in higher education institutions, also done by 
Mendoza et al. (2018). In addition, the work from Birgit H. et al. (2019) as previously mentioned was 
used. These pieces of work were adapted to fit the following methodology and answer the research 
question. Furthermore, the project plan designed to answer the research question and fulfill this work 
can be found in Appendix A: Project Plan. 
The method can be divided into four main stages: a) literature review b) background and foreground 
analysis of DTU c) the implementation of CE thinking d) recommendation. The first two stages 
correspond to the exploration phase, which was an iterative process. The review in the literature was 
done to understand the CE in the construction industry, the methods and tools, how to monitor 
progress and the relevant CE strategies. Then, the background analysis was done to gather 
information about the sustainability policies, strategies, construction projects, and annual report. 
While the foreground analysis consisted of a stakeholder engagement through semi-structured 
interviews and document collection to gather inside knowledge. Next, the implementation of CE 
thinking phase was a correlation of the elements in the exploration phase in order to design a more 
systemic approach to support the decision-making process towards a CE; the tools, frameworks, 
strategies and communication were adapted to the specific case of DTU. Finally, the recommendation 
phase corresponds to the creation of a roadmap to implement CE thinking in campus development, 
the conditions and a starting point for reporting and monitoring CE.

The methodology followed to produce this literature review was broken down into the following 
steps: 
Step 1: A search was done using search tool DTU Findit, a service from DTU library, and Google 
Scholar, limited to books, articles, and journals. In order to define whether the academic paper was 
relevant, the abstract was read. Step 1 was an iterative process through various sessions. 
Step 2: Relevant pieces of work were referenced and noted in a word document looking for the 
following: claims, overviews, conclusions, definitions, gap identification, and disagreements. 
Step 3: A categorization process took place to group those pieces of work relevant to one of the 
following areas listed above. 
The review consisted of an exploratory theoretical research, with a broad scope, in order to achieve a 
more holistic understanding of the subject and was not limited to the construction sector alone. To 
address the limited literature available, technological talks and panels from industry experts that give 
their view and opinion on the subject, both in presence and online, were a fundamental piece of 
research to reflect upon CE principles and understand how they can be implemented in practice. 
Second stage – Chapter 4: Background analysis and Chapter 5: Foreground analysis 
Background analysis 
Baseline information gathering - The background analysis was done to evaluate whether CE thinking 
is currently consolidated in the policies and strategies of the organization.  Information and data 
published by DTU in DTU Inside, annual report, DTU Avisen and DTU website was gathered. It was 
done to get an overview of DTU campus and of the organization's sustainability management; to 
analyze DTU sustainability strategies, policies, CE activities in the building projects and reporting 
mechanisms. The aim of this analysis is to get an overview of the know-how, capacity and resources 
of DTU towards embedding CE in the sustainable management system. 
Foreground analysis 
Stakeholder engagement - The purpose of this stage is to engage those stakeholders with a sustainable 
impact in the campus development; and understand about the effectiveness of current systems and 
strategies. Also, serves to identify challenges and opportunities of implementing CE thinking in their 
processes. Data was collected by conducting several semi-structured interviews with the management 
in CAS, and documentation provided by the sustainability coordinator in CAS, who was contacted in 
the first place through my master thesis supervisor. Internal stakeholders who influence the built 
environment at DTU CAS were identified and contacted by email to meet in person. A list of 
questions was produced (Appendix B: Interviews/meetings) and topic areas were identified relevant 
to the stakeholder, this was used as a guideline during the meeting. The interviews took place at DTU. 
A lot of freedom was given to the actor to answer the questions or speak about a topic. Follow up 
questions were done as well to further explore new insights. Meetings were recorded, to avoid relying 
on memory and second opinion, and lasted for an hour approximately. Some of the meetings led to 
contact with other relevant actors. The meetings were typed down in word document and analyzed to 
extract relevant points such as: enablers and disablers of a CE, examples, and awareness of CE.

Chapter 3: Literature Review 
This exploratory research consists of defining what a CE means in the construction industry and how 
its principles can be integrated into campus development. The research includes scientific literature 
and practical research on the CE. It gives an overview of the construction industry, presents current 
activities and trends towards CE in the construction industry. As well as reviewing and defining a

Circular Economy 
This sub-chapter includes some definitions of CE by different authors and introduces the Ellen 
MacArthur Foundation (EMF). Also, it presents a more critical reflection on the principles of a CE 
and the relation to the construction industry. 
What is a Circular Economy?  
There is no single and precise definition of the term CE in the literature (Birgit H. et al. 2019). Most 
authors tend to link CE concepts to sustainability, and associate CE to the concept of cradle to cradle 
(Birgit, H. et al. 2019). Other similar concepts linked to CE include Closed-loop economy, zero waste 
economy, green supply chain management, performance economy, cradle to cradle, industrial 
symbiosis (Kalmykova, Y. 2018). Some definitions of the term CE by different authors have been 
included and discuss how they reflect on the construction. 
Ellen MacArthur Foundation (EMF 2019) 
The EMF is a key player in the transition to a CE. The foundation has put much work to spread the 
awareness and potential of a CE in different industries. Moreover, they have developed material to 
assist companies, organizations and academia in the transition to a CE. Figure 2 shows one of the 
models that represent a CE. 
The EMF, based the CE on three principles:  
1) Prevent waste and pollution through design  
2) Prolong the usage phase of products and materials 
3) Regenerate natural systems (Renewable energy, farming, water cycles) 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
 
Figure 2 The Butterfly model from Ellen Mac Arthur Foundation (EMF 2019). 
The butterfly model from EMF is the most utilized representation of a CE. A critical remark in the 
model is the differentiation between both flows, technical and biological. These two must be 
separated in order to maintain value. Hence, preserving natural resources, optimization of resources 
and a system that is effective by design (EMF 2019). The model describes well the concept of closing 
the loop and achieves a more system thinking view of the concept.  The model could help in designing 
towards a CE, designing the way decisions are made along the value chain in a more systemic way 
towards circularity. 
It can be said that biological elements (left) can achieve a perpetual loop, however, is not the case of 
technical ones (right). Therefore, by design one must prioritize biological solutions over technical 
ones or design them to be easily separated. Design for many iterations close to infinity to be as circular 
as possible and theoretically eliminate the end of life (EoL) phase. How? By adding loops were waste 
is dissipating and keeping value at the highest level.  
Heyes, G. et al. (2018) proposes a definition well in agreement with the one presented by EMF and 
adds that renewable energy is the only driver of materials and technical nutrients. The complexity of 
how current buildings are designed makes it extremely difficult to separate the technical and 
biological elements from each other. Designing to prevent or minimize leakages of toxic chemicals 
and waste is essential to become circular (Heyes, G. et al. 2018). In a CE economic gain is found 
within the value chain in existing products and materials, not in the consumption of finite resources 
(Heyes, G. et al. 2018). Design plays a significant role, from the selection of materials, designing for 
assembly and dismantle and a system to make information easily accessible. 
Kirchherr et al. point out that the CE should be a fundamental systemic change (Birgit H. et al. 2019). 
It is highlighting the importance of system thinking. According to the author's analysis ‘‘A CE is an 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
economic system based on business models that replace the concept of ‘end-of-life’ with reducing, 
reusing and recovering raw materials in production, distribution and consumption processes (Birgit 
H. et al. 2019). The introduction of business models in the definition outlines the importance to place 
the right incentives to all the stakeholders involved and work towards these circular principles.  
Van Buren et al. (2016) points out the following ‘‘focal points: reducing the consumption of raw 
materials, designing products in such a manner that they can easily be taken apart and reused after 
use (eco-design), prolonging the lifespan of products through maintenance and repair, and the use 
of recyclables in products and recovering raw materials from waste flows’’. Some activities in the 
definition, such as maintenance and repair, are well familiar in the built environment, also referred as 
refurbishment or renovation, and are activities of prolonging the use at a lower cost compared to 
replacing by a new one.  
When it comes to the 17 UN SDGs, achieving a CE has a positive impact on all three sustainability 
pillars, environmental, economic, and social. Although a CE addresses stronger the economic and 
environmental development goals, by creating a synergy between them (Realdania 2018). It is 
important not to lose attention in the social pillar. A CE has a positive impact on the accessibility of 
resources to a broader spectrum and the creation of new jobs. Overall, a CE is a more resilient 
economy to resource security, social justice, improve human health and less environmental 
degradation (Realdania 2019). Many authors suggest that the main underline principle of a CE is 
waste prevention. A CE considers the activity and geography to promote a design with smaller loops, 
therefore more efficient use of resources and increasing profits (Heyes, G. et al. 2018). Ideally, an 
economic framework that regenerates and restores, to avoid environmental impact and depletion of 
limited resources, hence decoupling economic growth from the latter (Realdania 2019).  
Currently, the construction industry presents a focus on technology development to improve eco-
efficiency in reducing greenhouse gas emissions, e.g., the successful implementation of advanced 
technical solutions to become more energy efficient. For example, improving insulation to reduce 
energy consumption or more efficient lighting, leading to lower running costs, where the economic 
incentives are placed. This is an important element within a circular system, but it is not the case for 
material and product circularity (Eberhardt, L. C. M. 2018). Investigation on material efficiency is 
needed. Although eco-efficiency2 is good, sometimes it can result in a rebound effect3, causing a 
higher environmental burden. A CE by design works towards eco-effectiveness4, and consequently, 
the system becomes more eco-efficient (Kalmykova, Y. 2018). This occurs because the design 
philosophy aims to optimize a system rather than a single component (Birgit H. et al. 2019). 
Achieving a ‘circular building’ means to follow the principles of a CE during all phases of the 
building (Pomponi, F and Moncaster, A. 2017). In the construction industry, it is very challenging to 
extract value from waste, since demolition is not as planned and organized as construction (Jensen, 
K. G. 2017). 
A vital principle of a CE is to attempt to recover value in the tightest loops – prolonging the use phase. 
For this to happen, the value must be higher than the wider loops. When it comes to technical 
nutrients, maintenance, reuse, refurbish, recycle, and finally, energy recovery (Realdania 2019).

Reflection on a Circular Economy 
There is no singular definition; authors put more weight on some aspects more than others; however 
they do remain very similar in context. A CE is a system designed to minimize waste in the form of 
energy, greenhouse gas emissions, materials and other resources, and to maximize the value of these 
elements across the value chain. At the same time, the structure and processes of an organization 
directly affect the principles of a CE (Birgit H. et al. 2019). The implementation of a CE is greatly 
favored by an Environmental Management System certification, such as ISO14001 (Fonseca et al. 
2018). 
For a practical approach is preferable to focus on the principles the EMF proposes, rather than the 
definition itself. Furthermore, investigate the strategies, frameworks and parameters that are in line 
with these principles and butterfly model, and the environmental management system.  
Building elements 
To transition to a CE, a holistic view from different perspectives is required, specifically in research 
and planning and understanding what composes a building. Planning refers to the action of defining 
the activities and time to reach a specific goal. Time is a critical element in this phase since it is 
strictly proportional to the resources. In a linear economy, once the product is delivered to the 
customer, the responsibilities tend to diminish; in most cases, the user or customer is now the main 
responsible for that product. Now, the new user or customer must know and become aware of the 
lifetime of the product in order to plan accordingly.  
The built environment is overly complicated due to the significant number of materials involved, each 
with its life cycle, processes behind and change of use (Eberhardt, L. C. M. 2018). It is important to 
remark that (GXN 2018) has identified six different layers of a building, as shown in Figure 3: the 
outer structure, the skin, the installation of services, the interior space and the stuff inside. Each layer 
with a different life cycle and flows of resources, like raw material, energy, water, air, land use, traffic, 
and waste (Birgit, H. et al. 2019).

Sustainable Building Certifications 
There are currently dozens of certification bodies in the built environment with a focus on 
sustainability. A collaborative work between the Danish Building Research Institute and the 
innovation company of 3XN Architect – GXN (Jensen, K. G. et al. 2018) has produced a guide to 
Sustainable Building Certifications. The ten certifications selected for analysis and comparison are 
Active House, BREEAM, DGNB, Green Star, HQE, LEED, Living Building Challenge, 
Miljöbyggnad, Nordic Swan and WELL; chosen for their relevance to the construction industry in 
Denmark (Realdania 2018). Since DTU CAS aims to launch the DGNB Certification body across the 
new buildings at DTU campus, most focus will be given to this certification body. 
DGNB stands for the German Sustainable Building Council. It is the only certification body with 
balance weight in all three sustainability pillars; in fact, it is the one that best represents the economic

solution house’ case study. A lot of CE principles have been applied and documents have been 
produced to enlighten ‘‘sustainable transparency, design for disassembly and material 
accountability’’. 75 green solutions were implemented focused on ‘‘material circularity, reusability

The number of DGNB sign-ups has increased considerably. DGNB projects have over 100 indicators 
on how to build sustainability. A significant limitation of this certification is that it does not 
differentiate between a certain threshold, 65% - 85%, e.g., the building remains gold level in this 20% 
range. Hence, it can limit improvements and not motivate them to improve further. 
In 2018 the DGNB was the first one to introduce a framework to assess and measure the CE principles 
across buildings, Frameworks and tools. Moreover, the Building Research Establishment 
Environmental Assessment Method (BREEAM), presents good instruments to assess the 
environmental impact, such as the use of LCA and ECO Cost, which are in line with the principles 
of circularity (Birgit H. et al. 2019).

System Thinking 
System thinking is a holistic approach to integrate every part as a whole and its interconnections in 
the context of a more extensive system. A way to analyze the relationships and interactions between 
the component of one action over time (Summerton, L. et al. 2019). Instead of isolating components, 
it allows for understanding the subject. Therefore, such an approach aims to gain knowledge on the 
factors that affect the transition to a CE rather than focusing on a ‘niche area.’ (Summerton, L. et al. 
2019). The use of a value chain map to visualize a system is of good use for this purpose. An 
integrated strategy approach to systematically linked all the phases and actors across the whole value 
chain is an interesting approach to become circular.

Life Cycle Thinking 
Life Cycle Thinking is a way to understand the whole life cycle of a product, upstream and 
downstream, from raw material extraction to EoL, as seen in Figure 4. It combines elements from a 
CE and the triple bottom line11, where impacts from social, economic and environment are considered 
and how to minimize these impacts to transition to a CE (Summerton, L. et al. 2019). LCA, Life 
Cycle Cost Assessment (LCCA) and MFA (Material Flow Analysis) are tools that assist the decision 
making in the built environment towards sustainability with regards to the triple bottom line. 
Moreover, the literature presents research studies extended to a CE (Pomponi, F and Moncaster, A. 
2017). It is a time-consuming process and requires expertise. 
LCA 
The environmental impacts of a product or service across the entire life cycle are assessed using LCA. 
It is a well-established technique to decide between product A and product B in terms of their impact 
on the environment (Corona, B. et al. 2019)(Eberhardt, L. C. M. 2018). In addition, LCA is an 
appropriate tool to assess the sustainability of the three pillars of a new solution or a CE strategy 
(Fonseca et al. 2018).

replacement or a new acquisition.  An LCCA could be decisive when to do a refurbishment of a 
building or construct a new one. The analysis provides a cost calculation of the product's entire life

DGNB CE guideline 
DGNB has designed a framework to foster the integration of CE aspects into the construction. It 
works on a bonus scheme, where points are added whether an activity is being taken forward 
(Lemaitre et al. 2019). Consisting of twelve CE bonuses and the range of activities (Appendix E: 
DGNB Guideline). The framework resulted in a guideline to incentivize stakeholders, specifically 
planners, and manufacturers in the construction industry, to integrate CE principles in the 
construction. The idea behind this is to remark the potential economic model and assist in the 
transition to a CE. A critical remark is to consider the deconstruction of the building early in the 
planning phase.  
The maximum achievable is 100 points from 120 in total, 10 from each criterion. The weighting is 
not explicit what is based on. What does a +1 bonus point indicate? Does it measure the reduction 
of the impact on the environment or the cost savings? Also, the criteria work better for new 
constructions rather than current building mass. Moreover, the challenge of such ‘weighing’ 
framework is as stated by Rahla, K. et al. (2019) is to ‘‘avoid opportunistic behavior and arbitrary 
measurements which can generate ambiguity’’. 
Monitoring and track progress in a CE for campus development 
In order to track and monitor progress towards a CE, a metric system is essential, such as key 
performance indicators (KPIs). It allows measurement and progression over time with its 
development and if possible, and desirable, a benchmark with the sector. However, it is a time-
consuming process and includes heavy data collection and data management.  
Many authors are proposing a variety of different parameters and framework to measure circularity

2019). Also, the level of complexity, time, resources and data availability makes it difficult to apply 
some of these tools at campus level; tools such as Material circularity indicator (EMF & Granta 2015). 
The problem is that a well-established and comprehensive way to track and monitor the transition 
towards a CE is lacking (EMF 2019). In fact, instruments to measure circularity performance 
indicators are not present in campus development (Mendoza, F. et al. 2019). In agreement with  Birgit,

DTU Campus 
CAS is the organization responsible for the operation, development, and maintenance of DTU 
buildings, areas and facilities. With the primary objective of providing researchers, employees and 
students the best possible working and studying conditions while keeping it attractive. Resulting in 
an asset to different businesses, citizens and community (DTU 2016).  
There is a pleasant architectural scene by combining nature, forest, gardens, sports facilities and a 
central avenue within the building mass. ‘‘A scenic campus has a positive impact on learning, well-
being, and stress reduction, and offers benefits in relation to adapting to climate change.’’ Original 
buildings date back to the 1960s and 1977s. Currently, DTU is under transformation regarding 
buildings and infrastructure (CAS 2019). 
There is a considerable investment from DTU to transform the physical environment. It is expected 
to create a superior atmosphere for the development of DTU activities. It is creating the best possible 
conditions to foster research, teaching, and innovation across researchers, students and companies. 
This development encompasses 20 new buildings, improving current building mass and green areas. 
Resulting in an inviting campus for the 11,200 students and 6,000 employees and growing, as more 
people want to either study or carry research at the University, in alignment with the increasing 
demand of engineers from the society to solve the challenges the world is facing. On average, 400 
new students and researchers are welcomed to DTU per year, which means that in 2050, the number 
of students and researchers will double (CAS 2019). This does not necessarily mean double the 
amount of resources are needed to accommodate this future need. DTU has the vision to have a 
flexible, robust and future-proof utilities infrastructure and energy system, for experiments and 
research.

CE drivers at DTU 
DTU as a leading technical university with an explicit sustainable focus, could ask: What are the 
drivers to transition to a CE? The following drivers have been identified: 
▪ Becoming more sustainable 
Transitioning to a CE is a step forward towards the 17 UN SDGs in alignment with DTU strategy and 
Danish Government (2018). In construction and campus development would mean lesser greenhouse 
gas emissions, less resource consumption, and reduction of waste. 
▪ Branding 
Achieve a higher value perception by potential students and researchers (WCEF 2019). CE has 
become a very trendy and popular term in the last couple of years, some sectors like in the plastic 
industry have taken advantage. For example, Coca-Cola along the MacArthur Foundation is a good 
example where it has mobilized the whole industry. The implementation of CE across the university 
campus could mean a positive movement in the same direction to DTU, resulting in students enrolling 
with a ‘circular mindset’. (Assuming that a sustainable campus influences the student’s choice of 
university).  
▪ Cost reduction through higher utilization of resources  
More intensive use of the current resources available would mean more value extraction without 
resulting in extra consumption.  
▪ Avoiding regulative costs of environmental pollution and waste 
It is thought that being at the forefront of a CE is a risk mitigation strategy to environmental pollution 
and waste problems that can arise in a more regulatory future. Even though prices of resources are 
rising (WCEF 2019), they might still be too ‘cheap’ (too cheap to use nature) (Masi D. et al. 2017). 
Tax reform society to use nature - a tax reform society could be underway. Or higher taxes to those 
that produce more waste. It is worth mentioning that from Mendoza, F. et al. (2019) case study ‘‘All 
the workshop participants agreed that the development of a corporate CE procurement strategy could 
reduce costs of maintenance, waste management, energy and carbon emission tax’’ 
 
▪ Mitigate the risk from the limited resources and the increasing pressure in the coming 
years 
It is expected that a CE would lead to less resource consumption; therefore, lower expenditure on 
new materials and products to satisfy the university needs. It includes saving material costs, 
dampening prices and volatility (Kalmykova, Y. 2018). 
▪ Resource efficiency  
This aspect is mostly achievable with the latest technology developments. Through a CE DTU would 
become more resource-effective which leads to greater efficiency.

Chapter 6: Implementation of CE thinking 
This chapter aims to apply the frameworks and tools that were identified in Chapter 3: Literature 
Review and the findings from Chapter 4: Background analysis and Chapter 5: Foreground analysis 
to design a more systemic approach to transition to a CE. The chapter will serve as a scope of a CE 
management system, including those practices that are thought will drive change towards CE in the 
campus development. Furthermore, the identification of appropriate strategies was done and a 
proposal of CE indicators framework with an original methodology to identify a suitable monitoring 
system is achieved. Finally, the communication aspect is discussed according to GRI standards. 
Framework and tools 
It is important to note the following remark: ‘‘Case studies demonstrating how CE frameworks can 
drive change within organizations from the service sector are scarce (Johannsdottir, 2014; Heyes et 
al., 2018).’’ (Mendoza, F. et al 2019). Having mentioned this, a range of frameworks and tools that 
are expected to drive change towards a CE at DTU campus development will be analyzed and 
discussed. 
System Thinking  
It is crucial to describe the system of DTU CAS and the development of new buildings, to underline 
where CE can play a significant role. The value chain map will be presented to describe such a system.  
 
 
Figure 6 shows the value chain showing the resource flow: materials (There is a distribution factor between phases) 
The objective is a circular system to minimize waste, energy, and emissions and maximize resources 
value by the implementation of R activities; dissipations will still occur due to the technical elements, 
where some phases present more than others. 
The dimensional organization and process are as follows: On the strategic level, the campus has a 
vision and a strategy for the area to be developed. At the tactical level, the design and procurement 
are specified by the project manager, architect, designer, and constructor altogether. Finally, the 
project is realized by the contractor, supplier and producer. During the use phase, rebuilding and 
==PAGE:44==

refurbishment there is interaction with the CAS, DTU employees, students and maintenance team 
(DTU 2019)(Birgit, H. et al. 2019).  
It has been identified that Sub-system A is where DTU has the main control and direct impact, 
especially the use phase. On the one hand reduces the waste coming from construction, use, 
demolition and disassembly phases; increases resource efficiency. On the other hand, increase the 
implementation of Rs activities, in order to keep materials at a high value. 
Furthermore, DTU has also control and impact on sub-system B, but to a lesser extent. It is important 
to notice that the choice of suppliers and designers will have a direct effect on the subsequent phases. 
Therefore, the importance of a ‘circular design,’ in sub-system b, to make it simpler to implement the 
Rs activities, keep materials at a high value, and reduce waste. And, the choice of suppliers with 
strong alignment on circularity and collaboration. Identifying key actors who show strong 
involvement in sustainability/circularity as themselves across the supply chain is fundamental for this 
systemic change. As Corona, B. et al. (2019) stated, ‘‘New stakeholder groups need to be 
acknowledged, for example, third parties (proﬁt or non-proﬁt organizations) engaged in speciﬁc 
recirculation activities. Dealing with their own interpretations, abilities, limitations and contextual 
situations.’’  
The sub-system C is more difficult to have an impact from DTU position, but it can be done by setting 
the right standards, procurement and require suppliers/subcontractors to follow a code of conduct. 
Since DTU has the power to choose those product manufacturers that are responsible for raw material 
consumption to the stakeholders from sub-system B. A boundary has been set after the extraction 
process of raw materials where DTU has little influence.  
For this reason, a close collaboration between the different actors is essential. It was acknowledged 
by one of the employees from CAS the following, ‘‘From building 112, it has been noticed that a 
strong collaboration towards sustainability has been present from the very beginning, including DTU 
CAS, the maintenance team, architect firm, engineers and the contractor.’’ In terms of circularity, it 
is important to involve other actors as well, such as the external company carrying demolition and 
product manufacturing. 
Therefore, it is vital to generate co-creation through partnerships to implement solutions towards a 
CE. Solutions will involve different parts of the value chain; identification of this is important to 
optimize the potential. Currently, it has been identified a very high level of commitment to 
sustainability in campus development from CAS employees. It must reach every level of the 
organization and across the value chain.

Methodology 
A review in the literature has been done to understand what each indicator means and what it 
measures in the Construction industry. Important to note that DTU campus has both characteristics 
of the micro (product) and meso (industrial park) level, therefore both levels were investigated (Geng. 
et al. 2011). Circular metrics were identified from an extensive list of KPIs produced by Kravchenko, 
M. et al. (2019), also some other indicators where included and adapted from Circle house project, 
DGNB and EMF cases. KPIs were classified in different classes. Furthermore, a categorization 
process took place as follows:

Analysis and discussion 
The CE indicators framework accounts for the three sustainability pillars and every indicator is 
aligned with the principles of CE. The group assignment is not binding; for example, the activities 
belonging to the Social group will have an impact on environmental and economic aspects as well. 
The ‘activities’ indicator corresponds to the social pillar since it aims to create awareness among the 
internal stakeholders through introductory courses, but the impact this can have encompasses all other 
pillars. 
As mentioned by Birgit, H. et al. (2019) ‘‘Translating ambition into concrete content aspects makes 
the abstract subject of circularity more understandable and accessible.’’ Hence, translating 
objectives to more detailed requirements by asking questions in the right direction towards CE. In 
order to overcome one of the challenges remarked by Wbcsd (2018), who stated that ‘‘story-telling 
and effective communication of CE initiatives is a challenge’’. An approach that has characteristics 
of a flexible and standarised process is desirable (wbcsd 2019) 
Usually, one buys products that identify with their values. Therefore, asking for products in line with 
circular principles should be a standard for an organization that wants to transition to a CE. DTU 
should not limit their procurement to time delivery, quality and price. ‘‘For example, challenge 
suppliers to know what the material embodied carbon is’’ (Sandilands, A. 2019). Since the 
construction industry lacks detailed information about the components of materials and products, a 
starting point is to ask questions, such as: What is in the product? Where does it come from? Can I 
apply any of the Rs activities from R-list? If so, how? (DIF Measure the CE 2018). 
In addition, as Fonseca et. al. (2018) stated, many organizations are not reporting external pressures 
to adopt CE, such as demanding ‘‘green suppliers and products’’, hence becoming a major obstacle 
according to previous researchers. Asking these leading questions could generate the right pressure 
to tackle this major obstacle for the adoption of CE. For example, ask the contractor for a forecast. Is 
waste reusable? Biodegradable? Compostable? If it neither of this, it will become waste with a cost; 
therefore, is it waste being bought? Asking these types of questions achieves higher retention of value 
of materials (DIF 2018).  
The criteria used by the expert committee to assess the solutions of the circular construction challenge 
(Realdania 2019) were also used to produce the right questions from DTU perspective ‘macro level’. 
A framework has been added and adapted from (Realdania 2019), Table 11; it can be used to align 
stakeholders with the same circular mindset. Also, the questions have been linked to specific the 
specific framework or tools that can assist the process.

Conclusion 
One of the conclusions is that it may be too costly to quantify many of these KPIs or gather data. A 
common framework is needed to breakdown CE complexity and demand a CE across stakeholders. 
It is suggested to included as an internal management framework. From the position of DTU, as 
clients, project managers must ask questions towards circularity to the external stakeholders. The 
value chain map is a good complementary tool for a system thinking approach, to identify the right 
stakeholder who should be able to answer and be responsible for. The strategies have been included 
for each different indicator. Moreover, such a framework should aim to empower internal 
stakeholders in the organization to take on their roles and transition to a CE (Wbcsd 2018). 
The main drawback of not processing, calculating and submitting data collection inhibits a monitoring 
mechanism of CE and leads to a lack of reporting. A long-term strategy to gain more knowledge and 
awareness in CE is to implement a reporting mechanism, which can also lead to a potential 
benchmarking and continuous improvement by identifying problematic areas and improve decision 
making (Geng, Y. et al. 2011). Moreover, there is a cost analysis, of processing these data, that must 
be done as further research.

and avoid diesel machinery by using machinery that runs on electricity (Danish Architecture Centre 
2019) 
 
The main aim of these strategies is to target CE principles; avoiding waste, keeping the value of 
products at the highest level possible and enhancing biological cycles. 
Important to note that the material efficiency measure is not clear whether DTU has place actions 
towards this strategy, there are some examples like building using prefabricated for student 
accommodations and an example of a modular building approach which is more efficient. However, 
the policy and guide do not touch on this aspect. 
The means to apply and implement these strategies is through increasing awareness among 
stakeholders and create a sustainable mindset among them. One of the reasons the sub-policy and the 
process guide designed by the sustainability coordinator at DTU CAS are essential. Another critical 
point is that many of these strategies cannot be carried out ‘alone’; partnerships are needed to 
collaborate with; hence, the importance of network management. Finally, the business case for each 
of the strategies needs to be investigated and analyze its feasibility, both economically and 
technically.

Chapter 7: Recommendation 
Conditions to transition to a CE 
The conditions DTU must meet to develop a campus following the principles of a CE will be 
described. The findings from work done by Birgit H. et al (2019) are used to discuss where DTU is 
standing and what the organization must do. ‘‘The main conclusions are that circularity is an 
organizational issue, complexity must be reduced, and integral policy and specialized knowledge are 
required. Five recommendations towards an integrated strategy for circularity in campus 
development are given’’ (Birgit H. et al. 2019). 
1) ‘‘Ensure a consistent policy and support from all departments by formulating an integral 
strategy for circularity.’’ 
This approach is done at the sustainability level but is limited in the implementation of CE principles. 
The strategic policy could further implement the principles of CE, such as stating the reduction of 
waste, maximize resources by prolonging the use of products/buildings and enhancing natural cycles. 
In addition, some of the CE strategies identified could be embedded in the policy. More concrete 
actions need to be specified surrounding a CE in campus development.  
2) ‘‘Make sure you also have an implementation plan: Develop the proposed system into a useful 
instrument by specifying substantive requirements and concrete objectives for the tactical and 
operational level.’’ 
A CE plan is lacking. Requirements can be set using the CE indicators framework, Table 10. And, 
the objectives could be defined according to DTU baseline, ambitions and context in Denmark. 
Ideally, define the objective with a target and specified a time horizon. 
3) ‘‘Reduce complexity and make the subject of circularity accessible and understandable. Set 
clear priorities in the initial phase.’’ 
Although this has been done to certain extent with the introductory course on LCA and the 
introduction of DGNB. Regarding the awareness among employees at CAS, everyone did relate to 
the definition of a CE in one way or another; however this is due to the umbrella concept. Introductory 
courses on CE should be taken by DTU employees to get more familiar with the topic. To overcome 
the ‘many’ definitions in the subject is better to refer to the principles of CE proposed by EMF. The 
priorities actions should be in line with the strategy and objectives. The CE indicators framework is 
also though to reduce complexity and improve communication between internal & external 
stakeholders. 
4) ‘‘Let go of the automatism of existing frameworks and create space and ﬂexibility for a 
different approach in the development process.’’ 
It has been found out from the interviews that the CE activities, found in the ReSOLVE framework, 
are done in a natural approach, and it is a natural part of the process of decision making due to the 
high sustainability mentality. However, a hybrid approach could be more appropriate to make it 
simpler and less time consuming, but not limit the innovation through long and rigid processes. 
ReSOLVE framework is a good approach to familiarize with CE activities and get inspiration from 
implemented cases. Also, a way to stored difficulties and challenges when implementing and build 
knowledge around the employees. The set of questions derived from the CE indicators framework, 
Table 10, serves as a flexible approach to ask respective stakeholders to be aligned in this transition 
to a CE. As, well as Table 4 for a macro level perspective. 
==PAGE:66==
58 | Page 
5) ‘‘Think and work more in network contexts. To be able to close cycles at the local level, 
consciously establish links between development projects on campus at different scales and 
in different life cycles.’’ 
DTU has shown examples and frameworks of applying LCT in their decision making. In terms of 
recommendation the network management tool proposed applies very well. It aims to link different 
circular projects and solutions to campus development. And, aims to involve more stakeholders with 
a circular mentality and stronger collaboration along the value chain. Also, to check in a short time 
whether it’s a ‘first movers’ approach or it has been implemented before, also addressing one of the 
concerns found in the interviews. Prioritize to work only with those who perceive sustainability at 
the same or higher level than DTU, meaning active involvement in a CE as themselves across the 
supply chain, system thinking applies.

and the Innovation & Sector services could be relevant at a later stage.

For a successful implementation of CE thinking in campus development at DTU the following aspects 
in these six steps are expected to be covered.  
First, a good understanding of the CE principles is required through an introductory course. System 
thinking must be achieved, using value chain map and understand the impacts and influence of CAS 
and its boundaries, to address the problem in collaboration rather than alone. Furthermore, completing 
the ReSOLVE framework with successful cases would be appropriate to gain awareness of a CE and 
to have a framework that is more ‘complete’ for later use. It is suggested to apply the framework in 
a building per building case to differentiate which buildings have more CE solutions integrated.  
Secondly, the CE baseline is rather complex to define for a campus and building structures due to 
their complex nature, however identifying the ‘strong’ and ‘weak’ buildings in terms of integrated 
CE solutions is an appropriate approach to move in the right direction. Also, it is appropriate to 
discuss elements in the ReSOLVE framework, the strategies and the leading questions from the CE 
indicators framework, Table 10. The right mindset across all actors in the organization is essential for 
the transition, and it has been found out that DTU is very strong in this aspect when it comes to 
sustainability, the next step would be to illustrate CE actions, as shown above, in the policy and 
process guide. 
 
Fourthly, the right question has to be asked to the stakeholders who have an impact on the final 
product/building, when sitting down with the designer, architect or maintenance team; also, alignment 
in the strategies chosen is needed among external and internal stakeholders. And, utilize the 
ReSOLVE framework for inspiration to select good initiatives and as a tool to quickly identify if a 
project covers circular principles or not. The fifth step is about learning outcomes of ‘good practice’ 
and improves on the process. As an original roadmap, it will need to adapt and change to fit the 
organization. Finally, it is expected that such an implementation would make it easier to start 
collecting data to build a monitoring and reporting KPI system. 
 
The roadmap has been thought to match CAS specific resources, capabilities and activities. An 
approach that takes a relatively short amount of time and does not involve expensive softwares. Is 
about meetings, workshops, and applying the suggested tools in a robust manner, enhancing broad 
collaboration and input.

Chapter 8: Conclusion 
The substantial impacts from the construction industry have been remarked; a third of the waste 
generation, 40% depletion of natural resources and the contribution to greenhouse gas emissions by 
25-40%. A gap in circularity on-campus development has been identified and a methodology was 
designed based on previous work on higher education institutions, to stand in the shoulders of 
knowledge in a subject that is in its infancy. A method consisting of a literature review, foreground 
& background analysis of DTU, as a case university, the implementation of CE thinking in campus 
development and finally the recommendations to address the research question. The methodology 
successfully arrives to a resulting roadmap to implement CE thinking in campus development; 
however, it does not test its performance. In agreement with Kobza, N. and Schuster, A. (2016) who 
stated that ‘‘the transition to a CE needs to be gradual and well thought’’. And, it is important to 
think and address how circular we can think in the coming years to meet the UN objectives.  
The key points from the literature review are to focus on the principles rather than the definition due 
to the many definitions together with the ‘umbrella concept’. The informational inhibitor has been 
identified to be the one DTU can have major impact to foster change. Hence, a wide range of tools 
and frameworks were identified, CE strategies and a way to monitor & track progress were 
investigated to the specific case. 
What are the main findings? It was found in the background analysis that the university campus 
corresponds to a meso level scale with some characteristics of a micro-level scale as well. The drivers 
for DTU to transition to a CE were identified: to become more sustainable, branding, cost reduction 
through higher utilization of resources, avoiding regulative costs of environmental pollution and 
waste, and to mitigate the risk from limited resources & the increasing pressure in the years to come. 
It has been found out that the strategy policy is aligned with a CE. However, further improvements 
have been described, by highlighting concrete actions and reference to the principles of a CE in the 
strategic policy and ultimately reflect them in the process guide. Furthermore, DTU could improve 
their communication by informing more about the current challenges and impacts from DTU 
development, to onboard more stakeholders specially students and researchers to research and 
develop solutions. With regards to reporting, in a CE is key to have the accountability of the materials 
consumed, also avoid a sole focus on efficiency. 
With regards to the foreground analysis, it has been found that the level of awareness in sustainability 
is very high among CAS employees and the main entrance to a CE was the introduction course on 
LCA and the launch of DGNB certification. DGNB was identified as an enabler in implementing CE 
thinking, a more integral and holistic approach to all buildings. It shows a supportive and systematic 
framework to implement CE in a new building; however it has not been implemented yet. And, some 
of the disablers which were pointed out in the interview can be overcome with innovation from Rs 
activities, specifically the ones at the top such as re-imagining and redesigning. Then, if the value 
cannot be perceived by the current user, other stakeholders who will find value must be found. It is 
fundamental to have an adaptable and flexible approach in design to adapt to new needs and trends. 
The need for a more systematic framework to simplify CE thinking has been included as well. Also, 
better planning and scheduling with digital platforms are needed, to avoid relying on space and 
generating waste. There is also a need to replicate successful cases and systemic approaches.  
Furthermore, the key points from the process guide are the following. It is expected from the project 
manager to acquire relevant knowledge from reference projects, colleagues and stakeholders. 
Moreover, to achieve the best quality out from the least resources, in line with a CE. Also, life cycle 
thinking and design economic analysis need to be implemented in their decision making. Finally, 
acknowledge experience from other projects. The process consists of seven steps: planning, offering, 
==PAGE:70==
62 | Page 
construction, design, execution, handling over and post-occupancy. It is also specified whether digital 
tools and frameworks are applied to improve decision making.  
The implementation of CE thinking in campus development has been addressed by identifying the 
following frameworks and tools: System thinking, ReSOLVE framework, Network management, CE 
indicators framework, the CE strategies and communication. System thinking is an overall and 
holistic view of the organizational boundaries and areas of direct and indirect impact. The same 
approach could be further applied to narrower and more specific cases, e.g., looking just at the flow 
of furniture; hence, identifying relevant actors and opportunities to apply Rs activities towards 
circularity. ReSOLVE framework can be embedded in the process guide to assist PM in making 
decisions, replicate successful cases and fill DGNB criteria.  And, it has been acknowledged many 
examples of circularity in campus development in the ReSOLVE Framework, many of these 
examples come from a ‘natural’ approach; however, they are classified as an incremental 
improvement, rather than radical.  Network management is a tool that enhances collaboration with 
those stakeholders that have a ‘circular’ mindset and communicate experiences surrounding a CE, to 
build further CE solutions. Then, the leading questions from the CE indicators framework are 
expected to help align stakeholders and demand for an extra effort in the pursuit of a CE. Finally, 
several CE strategies that are being implemented by DTU have been identified, discussed briefly 
including observations and potential improvements. Currently, there are strategies and policies at 
DTU, to some extent, that are in line with the principles of a CE. Moreover, those CE strategies that 
could be implemented by DTU are presented. Finally, the reporting was assessed with the GRI 
reporting standards both quality and content, where better communication needs to be in place and if 
possible, develop a materiality matrix; to onboard stakeholders to solve challenges in campus 
development and account for resource consumption.  
The recommendation chapter includes five concrete actions on how to improve the integrated strategy 
built from the recommendations by Birgit H. et al (2019). And, actions designed and aim to improve 
the exchange of information among internal and external stakeholders, an obstacle reported by Winas 
et al. (Fonseca et al. 2018). The roadmap consisting of six steps with key activities and integration 
with the frameworks and tools that were identified. Constructions are known for a lot of 
documentation, requirements, data and time-consuming processes. It was not the purpose of this work 
to have one extra sheet with a requirement checklist. It is expected to suit the organization's resources 
and objectives. And, a simplified reporting mechanism was suggested, by identifying KPIs relevant 
to the transition to a more circular campus. These KPIs are expected to be straightforward to account 
for.  
Is the research question successfully addressed? The research question has been addressed to some 
extent. The project resulted in a roadmap that contains several key activities together with frameworks 
that have been identified and built specifically to implement CE thinking in the development of a 
university campus. Also, the report serves to understand where DTU baseline is and define the 
directions the university should take to transition to a CE.  
To what extent? Due to the scope of the study and time limitations, it was not possible to test the 
roadmap. This leaves unanswered questions: whether the application of such a roadmap would 
guarantee the implementation of CE in campus development? This can be confirmed by working on 
the Limitations and further improvements, and Future Work.  
 
What is the impact? This work can serve as a guide for campus development in any Higher Education 
Institution with campus to implement the principles of circularity. It serves to understand what a 
Nordic University leader in Sustainability is currently doing and what it can do to think more in a CE 
==PAGE:71==
63 | Page 
context. Furthermore, the roadmap could also be potentially implemented in any construction 
development in the service sector that aims to transition to a CE. 
 
Limitations and further improvements 
▪ Although interviews were conducted with CAS service, the study has not included the 
perspective form, other stakeholders, closely related to DTU.  Getting to know other 
perspectives both internal and external could give other points of view in the implementation 
of a CE. In alignment with system thinking in a CE. The perspectives from AUS and 
maintenance team could have been interesting and fitting in this work. Also, the perspective 
from higher management positions, such as the board, executive and governor. Finally, 
external stakeholders’ perspectives on the subject could have given some powerful insights 
as well. 
▪ Workshops were not part of the study; to avoid conflicting views and move forward. However, 
activities such as this can be useful to get more insights, opens discussions and offers other 
points of view. To cover this gap, findings from other projects such as the one from Mendoza, 
et. al. (2019) and Birgit H. et al (2019) were used to reflect in the analysis and build in the 
case study.  
▪ CE is still in the early phase of research, many of the activities may follow circular principles 
but the real impact has not been studied nor assess yet. This is very important to assess, to 
avoid a higher environmental burden by applying CE strategies. The application of LCA could 
help with this limitation. 
▪ The analysis is qualitative, and it was not possible to quantify it due to the scope of the study. 
The materials aspect could be further investigated, by taking a narrower approach to a specific 
case, for example looking at the furniture or window frames of a building. The EMF proposes 
some guidelines on how to measure circularity in a specific product. Also, MFA could be 
applicable.  
▪ Although the economic aspect was somehow considered when developing this work, the 
business models were not analyzed in detail therefore the economic analysis is somehow 
lacking in the report.  
Future Work 
Further work could be defined by a) looking at another university, region, and investigate it as another 
case study to compare findings and results b) Implement the roadmap and identify further barriers 
and opportunities and implement constructive feedback in an iterative manner c) Investigate the social 
impact of implementing specific CE solutions d) Feasibility of CE solutions, economic and 
technical18. As Mendoza, J. et al. (2018) suggest, building a business case on CE solutions is essential 
to buy in stakeholders e) Narrow down to specific cases of CE, such as designing a more systemic 
decision making for the renovations f) Investigate fundamental questions: the reduction of energy 
consumption in a construction use phase? Vs. The consumption of raw material? Energy efficiency 
focus vs resource effectiveness? 
 
18 CE opportunity prioritization matrix (Mendoza, F. et al. 2019) could be used in a workshop scenario to identify if an

Reflection 
It was challenging work, it was not a straight-line journey; instead it was one with curves and sinuous 
road. The main reflection I do in this extensive work is that decisions must be made along the journey 
even though one does not have the full picture. It is better to learn while doing. Although the literature 
review sometimes felt like endless reading, however interesting, there was no progress if boundaries 
are set and concluding points are written after each read or attended the seminar. Another important 
remark is the choice of a broad subject such as the construction which was narrowed into campus 
development. The issue with a broader area to cover is the depth of the analysis and assessment, there 
are full of small obstacles that without smaller lenses are very difficult to overcome. Having 
mentioned this, from one point one must start, whether it is to investigate a CE of an individual 
product being the windows used, or specific furniture just to give an example. Otherwise encompass 
a wider spectrum as the campus itself, involving all constructions and develop thinking for a 
mesoscale magnitude; where the number of variable changes abruptly, and one must work with that. 
Also, the focus could have been narrowed to one particular strategy, digital tool or systemic 
framework for renovations and work in a greater ‘isolation’ from the system. I have chosen to cover 
an area and to address a research question where my skills and capabilities could be of better use. 
Having said that, it is a subject that requires a multidisciplinary approach. 
Last but not least, the work done is not perfect, there are many limitations and further work which 
could have made the work a more ‘complete’ one. Moreover, many other questions have emerged 
from this work and opens this fresh subject further. Finally, I will say that I am satisfied with the 
results and fulfilling learning experience the master thesis was.

Take backs to DTU: IN GREEN 
• How can a university campus contribute to a more resilient city? Preserve the crucial ecosystems 
and green areas while at the same time facilitating an urban development. Strengthening the 
potential for increased biodiversity for bees, insects and birds. New water systems are created in 
the area to take care of surface water and improve microclimate, while outdoor environments are 
being designed to strengthen the distribution pathways of plants and animals. 
• How do we use wood for large projects? Building in wood decreases CO2 emissions significantly 
during construction and lifespan of the building. And, improves indoor climate and acoustics. – 
Spatial efficiency by using all spaces as learning environments. 
• How do we make large scale public buildings CO2 neutral? Middelfart Townhall – certified as a 
DGNB Platin building - became Denmark’s most sustainable public building – integrating a 
unique mix of sustainable solutions like solar panels, leftover wood and smart cooling systems. 
It’s beautiful, sustainable and communal. Saves on maintenance budgets. 
More info: https://ramboll.com/sustainability/solutions/projects/middelfart-city-hall 
 
- Electricity generated by 700m2 solar panels on the roof. 
- Walls and ceilings are constructed of materials to reduce noise and create optimal working 
environment. 
- Surplus heat created by people or computers is used as district heating for the homes in the city. 
- Food from the canteen is turned into natural gas. 
• How can an ecosystem approach facilitate urban living? In the construction phase the project 
team has committed to avoid diesel machinery by using machinery that runs on electricity. Green 
space will cover 75% of the site. Including a large central garden and green roofs. 
• How do we build the places we need from the things that we throw away? Mad Arkitekter 
addresses the building industry’s massive waste problem, by reusing building components will be 
able to reduce material emissions by 90%.  
• How do we build more while consuming less? Upcycle studios - Upcycling not only saves 
resources, it also makes the finished product significant cheaper. 
 - 75% of windows come from abandoned buildings. 
 - Upcycle concrete are cast from very durable concrete waste from the construction of Copenhagen 
metro. 
• How do we use local natural resources for safer transportation? Transportation can be difficult 
during wintertime. Lowering the city costs for snow removal and supporting the existing heating 
infrastructure. Create year-round mobility and make use of an environmentally friendly natural 
resource.
