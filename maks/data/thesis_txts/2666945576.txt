Abstract
The scope of the following Master thesis is to make an optimization of the work processes for
the element production for Fehmarn Link Contractors - the contractors for the Femern tunnel
project.
The study covers construction practices from the third and fourth industrial revolution. The
third industrial revolution covers automation where industrial robotics improved line produc-
tion of tunnel elements. The fourth industrial revolution is split into an assessment of the
maturity of the company, which gives an opportunity for the formulation of a solution using
The Internet of Things to improve Supply Chain Management in the company. Furthermore,
the development of Augmented Reality is examined where the application of innovative tools
will support construction workers in their daily work. A safety suit which improves work en-
vironment safety and limits the workers fatigue and potential injuries. This kind of physical
augmentations is shown to have beneﬁcial eﬀects on work process eﬃciency.
The study investigates the maturity level of Industry 4.0 at Femern A/S, and identiﬁes their
ambitions and needs of action to exploit their potential in a digitalized forward-moving world.
An existing framework is used to evaluate Femern A/S readiness towards the digital transfor-
mations. For the assessment of the maturity level, clear deﬁnitions were necessary concerning
the term Industry 4.0 in order to fully investigate the current status quo of the company and
what possible solutions are available.
The solutions examine introducing robots for automatic rebar production processes, utilizing
tracking technologies for rebar in real-time, using Augmented Reality tools for quality check,
and the application of exoskeletons to assist craftsmen in their work and to limit injury.
The problems identiﬁed were found with the lack of use in technological advancements within
the construction industry in general, and the company itself. Through NASA’s methods for
system engineering, tools are applied in terms of the technological readiness level, in order to
assess whether the technologies are ready to be implemented.
Flow diagrams are implemented in order to show the value of what values are added from
implementing Industry 4.0 in a tunnel project. This is compared with Industry 3.0 practices
and the methods that make most sense for the company are applied.
The research conclusion of the Master thesis found that the advanced technologies are used in
other industries like production, and in order for Femern A/S to transition from their beginner
maturity level managerial changes are necessary. Through automated processes the costs and
beneﬁts of using robots has been evaluated and the tunnel project will have a decrease in man-
hours by 33%. Through awareness about digitilization and its beneﬁts, pilot projects and using
an agile approach Femern A/S will slowly adapt to the changes that the world is increasingly
moving towards.

1.1
Background
The idea of connecting Eastern Denmark and the neighboring Germany by a ﬁxed connection
has existed since early 1990’s.
A bridge was the initial proposal. A feasibility study was then conducted in the late 1990s,
followed by further proposal exploration in the early 2000s.
In June 2007 German and Danish oﬃcials agreed to construct a bridge. In Copenhagen in
September 2008 the German and Danish transport ministers signed the agreement for the
bridge to link the two countries.
In the following year, the German and Danish authorities approved the project construction.
The plan for the projected ﬁnish date of the bridge was 2018. Nevertheless additional feasibility
studies conducted by Femern A/S determined that a tunnel would actually be a better choice.
It would be much more environmentally-friendly, would not be dependent upon the weather
conditions, and importantly it would have lower construction risks. [1]
Over the next couple of years the client designed the plans for the tunnel and ensured the
objectives from German and Danish authorities were obtained. In 2019 initial construction
works in Germany began. In the beginning of 2020 the Danish authorities activated the con-
struction contracts which initiated the start of the tunnel construction in January 2021. To
date, many parties still remain against the tunnel, especially in Germany during the latest ap-
proval steps. However, in November 2020, after years of political discussions and many lawsuits
the ﬁnal milestone was achieved when a top German court dismissed the lawsuits against the
construction of the tunnel.
The Fehmarnbelt is approximately a 18 km wide strait at the Baltic sea, between the Danish
island of Lolland and the German island of Fehmarn.
See map in Figure 1.
The planned
immersed tunnel will connect the two islands. The tunnel will contain a dual track railway and
a four lane motorway, with emergency lanes in both directions. The length of the tunnel is
anticipated to be 18.2 kilometers, which will make it the longest immersed tunnel in the world.
Completion of the tunnel construction will take approximately 8.5 years.

Figure 1: Location of the planned tunnel between Denmark and Germany
Denmark is already familiar with these kinds of projects and investments.
In July 2000 a
ﬁxed connection between Sweden and Denmark was opened. It is a combined bridge and tun-
nel connection, consisting of the Øresund tunnel between Copenhagen and an artiﬁcial island
Peberholm. Additionally it includes the Øresund bridge, a combined beam bridge and cable-
stayed bridge between Peberholm and Lernacken south of Malm¨o. The connection consists of
a motorway and a double railway track. The length of the connection is 15.9 km. The Øresund
tunnel is 4 km long. This tunnel was built with the same building principles and methods as
will be used in the construction of the Femern tunnel. The exception being that the Øresund
tunnel elements were much smaller and at that time it is reasonable to conclude that the tech-
nology was not as developed as it is today.
The budget for Femern is estimated to be 55.1 billion DKK (2015 price level) with a re-
serve of 7.3 billion DKK (approximately 14%). Financial expenditures of the project has been
determined according to the same model as the Great Belt and Øresund connections. In the
long term it is the users that are ﬁnancing the construction of the tunnel, as well as the main-
tenance. The proceeds from the travel fees through the tunnel will be used to payback the loan
used to ﬁnance the tunnel construction. Repayment of the loan is estimated to take 36 years.
The EU ﬁnancial support is 10%. [2]

Introduction to company and organizational unit

1.3
Tunnel Construction
Figure 3: Illustration of tunnel
The construction solution that will be used to construct the Femern tunnel is a traditional
method for immersed tunnels. It is the same method that has been used in construction of the
Øresund tunnel. Overall construction principles and order is as follows:
• Excavation of a trench at the bottom of the sea where the tunnel will be located
• Production of concrete elements in the factory in the harbor in Denmark
• Tunnel elements will be towed to the right location at sea
• Elements will be sunk to the bottom and placed in the trench
• Elements will be connected together and the water will be pumped out
• The trench and the tunnel will be covered with gravel and sand. The sea bottom will be
as if it is untouched after the construction.
In the ﬁrst step the 60m wide and 16m deep trench will be excavated at the bottom of the
Baltic sea. Here the pre-fabricated tunnel sections will be immersed in the trench 45m below
the surface of the sea. Tunnel segments will be pre-fabricated at the harbor in the production
halls which will soon be built. The production halls will consist of 5 production lines. The
tunnel consist of reinforced concrete elements which will be cast on site. On the harbor of
Rødby, three halls will be constructed for execution of reinforcement cages and casting of the
concrete elements. Production of the elements will be a continuous process, where 9 segments
will be cast in the production hall and joined together into an element. The element will then
be pushed to a dry dock, which will be ﬁlled with water so the element is mostly submerged
and ﬂoating. The element is then towed by 4 ships to its location and submerged to the right
position.
In total the tunnel will consist of 89 prefabricated elements from a factory in the harbor
of Lolland. There are two type of elements, split up into 79 standard elements and 10 special
elements. The tunnel element is 42.2 meters wide and 8.9 meters tall. Furthermore, the section

Element production
As mentioned, the elements will be made of unusually strong reinforced concrete. This material
is the strongest construction material and is being used for constructions that are exposed to
extreme loads. The combination of steel bars as the reinforcement and the concrete makes it
possible to exploit the beneﬁts of both materials. The concrete has the ability to absorb the
pressure forces and stresses. Steel on the other hand is perfectly suitable to receive the tension
forces and stresses.
As the tunnel will be exposed to high pressure and tension stresses at every part, it is indis-
pensable to apply reinforcement steel bars in every part of the tunnel. The steel reinforcement
bars will be made by assembling steel bars into reinforcement webs. This makes it possible to
absorb tension in both vertical and horizontal direction.
The webs will be placed together and shape the form of the tunnel that will be cast with the
concrete in the next phase.

2.3
The structure of the construction industry
The construction industry is mostly build up by companies that are organized around projects.
Projects are normally split up into diﬀerent phases where each phase can consist of diﬀer-
ent types of stakeholders, diﬀerent people who make the decisions and thereby other views on
what is important. The result may be project parties concerned about their own interest where
stakeholders are trying to re-distribute costs to other stakeholders in the following phase(s).
The project owner puts in money to ﬁnance the project.
The project consists of the con-
struction costs but also design costs where a lot of planning is needed to make sure design is
coordinated in the best way possible. This is done by engineers and architects. Those designer
need to follow certain building codes set by the government. Furthermore they need to make
sure that they satisfy the client’s requirements, which is done on the basis of inputs gained
from manufacturers, suppliers and contractors.
Besides the direct construction cost there are cost both directly and indirectly set out by the
public. These are things such as requirements on which materials to use, establishment of park-
ing basement or other initiatives that are not directly beneﬁcial to the project owner. Indirect
costs come from regional or municipal district plans. These could be the height of a building
or restrictions of how much area a construction project can use. District (or regional) plan
requirements are in addition to the national construction legislation. [11]

Main Context - Reinforcement Production Process
4
Main Context - Reinforcement Production Process
Reinforcement production consists of many steps, from ordering the reinforcement, to having a
complete cage ready for casting. This report will ﬁnd and analyze new and innovative processes
that can optimize the production of rebar cages for the tunnel elements. The Øresund Tunnel
will be used as reference, as it was a similar construction process but smaller. [13]
4.1
Current process
4.1.1
Order and shipping
The ﬁrst step in that must be taken before the reinforcement production can begin, is to order
the necessary materials. The Tunnel needs thousands of tons of reinforcement. Multiple orders
will be done over time and the reinforcement will then be shipped by ships to Rødby harbor.
Keeping track of the reinforcement is demanding work, especially in this project.
A time
scheduled is to be followed in order to know when to order the reinforcement and what type it
should be.
4.1.2
Reinforcement processing
After the reinforcement has arrived to the site, it has to be processed before it can be used.
The reinforcement rebars must be cut and bend for the right shapes and sizes to the tunnel.
Flat surfaces as bottom, top slab and wall consist of larger matts where the rebars are tied
together with a certain distance between single pieces.
Cutting and bending
The reinforcement will be cut and bend by workers, by using manual rebar benders.
The
workers inserts each rebar manually and uses the bender, to bend the rebar to the necessary
shape. The workers will follow a plan depending on what type of reinforcement is needed.
Reinforcement mats
The mats consist of reinforcement bars tied together with wires. The reinforcement bars are
placed next to each other on a row, and tied together using steel wire.
4.1.3
Execution of Reinforcement cages
The segments of Femern tunnel are made up of reinforced concrete elements. The reinforcement
goes through several processes to be build up into a cage. The reinforcement cages also contains
a frame system, which helps for stabilization in the tunnel. The reinforcement cage is made up
of three parts, which are the base slab, walls and roof slab.
Figure 11 below shows the reinforcement cage of the Øresund tunnel, which also includes a
frame system.
25
==PAGE:26==
4

Figure 11: Reinforcement cage with frame, Øresund Tunnel
Base slab
The base slab consist of longitudinal and traversal bars placed on top of each other. The bars
will be tied together, and rolled to a big roller mat. The roller mat will be lifted by a crane
and placed in the right position. Workers will then manually roll out the mat, so it is really
for a second layer above it.
When the longitudinal and transverse rebars have been placed, the intersections between them
will be tied. This is repetitive work that has to be done on all intersections, and requires the
workers to bend down to perform the work.
Walls
The reinforcement for the walls construction will be made with steel frame.The purpose of the
frames is to keep the rebars with the correct distance between each other. The Steel frames
are made to hold the longitudinal rebars above each other with the correct distance.
The
frameworks are made by an external supplier and shipped to the Femern project. There are 6
walls on each segment.
Roof slab
The roof slab is executed exactly like the base slab. It will then be lifted by a crane, and placed
on top of the walls. The reinforcement cage is then ﬁnished and will be cast with concrete.
4.1.4
Production Hall
The reinforcement work and concrete casting is going to be performed in a production hall.
The rebar will be transported from the harbor to the production hall where the process of
producing tunnel elements can begin.

Processes of producing segments are split up into diﬀerent locations in the hall. The rebar cage
will be constructed on a moving belt. When the bottom slab is ﬁnished the element will move,
so the walls can be constructed and then the roof slab. The ﬁnished rebar cage will then stay
in the buﬀer area, then move to the casting bed, where concrete will be cast on the rebar cage.
The segments will then move to the curing area, where they will be joined together to become
a full element, before moving to the upper basin. [14]
4.2
Optimization in the process
Optimization suggestion to the construction processes are focused on the most time consuming
phases of the construction of the rebar cage for the tunnel elements. Based on the experience
from Øresund tunnel the most time consuming phases in the assembling of rebar cage for the
tunnel are horizontal directed surfaces. These surfaces in the tunnel are primarily the bottom
and top slab.
Eﬃciency is depended on well provided knowledge and eﬀective instruction to the person-
nel. Intelligent training is essential in eﬃciency of the processes and safety of the workers.
Human work is a big part of above mentioned activities included in the rebar cage produc-
tion. As this is a 18 km long tunnel constructed of similar elements, many activities will be
repetitive in a 8,5 years long project. Big potential is found in increasing the eﬃciency and
safety improvement by supporting the workers in those actives. Due to the size of the project,
the used methods for supply of rebar are very old-fashioned and unmanageable.

7.2
History of Automation
The ﬁrst idea of automation dates to ancient Greece, around 400 BCE. The ﬁrst known written
use of automaton was in the book of Homer’s Iliad. This is hard to imagine, since there were no
machines or computers at that time. According to Greek mythology, Talos was an Automaton
made of Bronze. His job was to protect the island of Crete from invaders and roamed the island
three times a day.
Figure 18: Ancient Greek coin - Talos Automaton
The real history of automation happened much later than ancient Greece, and began relatively
closer to our generation, which was the ﬁrst industrial revolution. In the 1700s machines like
the steam engine were created, which marked the beginning of automation. Many work tasks
could suddenly be performed much easier with machines. This did however bring uncertainties
among people, as they were afraid that machines would take over their jobs.
Even though early automation can be dated back to the ﬁrst industrial revolution, the term was
not used at the time. The word ’automation’ derives from automaton and was ﬁrst popularised
in 1947. The word automation can be attributed to vice-president of the Ford Motor Company
D.S. Harder in the 1950’s. Harder described automation as a philosophy of manufacturing. At
that time there were huge development in the industry, and Ford established a department for
automation. It was the idea of using assembly lines that came from Henry Ford, which made
production much faster. This idea was, however, not fully automated, as it required a lot of
manual work in the assembly line and can be classiﬁed as semi-automation.
It was ﬁrst in the second half of the 20th century, in 1969, that automation had its great
breakthrough. That year marked the beginning of the third industrial revolution, where the
focus was on automated production, computers and electronics. It was an end of an era where
the focus used to be mass production and semi-automation, to suddenly having robots that
could fully automate many work processes. [23,24]

7.3
Introduction to Automation
Automation is a technology that describes a process being performed automatically with non
to minimal human interaction.
The Oxford dictionary deﬁnes automation as ”The use or introduction of automatic equipment
in a manufacturing or other process or facility.” [25]
Some scientists deﬁne automation as applying machines or systems to execute tasks that in-
volve more elaborate decision-making. [24]
Automation can be applied in something as simple a light switch.
A normal light switch
will require manually ﬂicking the switch to turning the light bulb on or oﬀ. This process can
be automated by adding a sensor that detects motion. When the sensor detects that someone
has entered the room, it will automatically turn the light switch on, and when it no longer
detects anyone in the room, it will turn the light switch oﬀ.
This describes automation in its simplest form, since automation can also be applied at much
larger and more complex scales, for instance with robots. This could require the use of more
complex technology like machine learning and big data. Robots can then be set to do many
work tasks like welding, cutting and picking & placing items.
Many companies such as the automotive industry are investing greatly in automation as it can
have valuable advantages. One of the key features of automation is executing repetitive work.
By making a robot do repetitive work, a company can save many man hours, money and time.
A robot does not need time to learn a task, but can do it instantly, so there is no learning
curve and this can save a lot of time. Another advantages of automation is a decrease in risk
of injuries. By having fewer workers work on diﬃcult tasks, a foreseeable reduction in injuries
is the result.
Even though there are many advantages to automation, there are still some key disadvantages.
Robots come at a cost; hence it is up to the company to assess if it is worth the investment.
Sometimes, a robot may also want to be used for a single project and can possibly increase
project cost. There is also the possibility of malfunctioning robot, which can be expensive
to repair and may delay the work. Another aspect to consider is the potential of job losses,
because robots can replace manual jobs completed by workers
Robots are an important part of automation, but is not the only option in the ﬁeld of au-
tomation. There are three diﬀerent varieties of automation as shown in Figure 19 below.

7.3.1
Types of automation
When dealing with automation, especially robots, it is important to understand that all of
them do not work in the exact same way. And there are in fact, three types of automation that
these robots and machines can be categorized under. These automation types are as follows:
• Fixed automation
• Programmable automation
• Flexible automation
Fixed automation works in simple operations and in ﬁxed sequences. This machine is ﬁxed
to a speciﬁc work procedure and not capable of bigger changes. This is an advantage when
executing repetitive work, as they have a high production rate.
Programmable automation is a machine capable of changing sequences to accommodate
a wide variety of products. The change in sequence is done by a program that requires cod-
ing in order to change the machine sequence. This enables the machine to be very ﬂexible but

Flexible automation is part of programmable automation. Flexible automated machines
are also capable of changing to a variety of sequences. The diﬀerence here is that with ﬂexi-
ble automation, a short amount of time is lost in the change of sequence. However, the lack
of program-ability gives ﬂexible automation a smaller variety of sequences, compared to pro-
grammable automation.

7.3.2
Reason for automation
After establishing the base knowledge of automation, it is important to ask the big question:
why automate? There are several reasons to automate, which are as follows: [24]
• Feasibility: Certain tasks are not possible to perform manually due to the limitations
of the human body. This could be like working in small scale, for instance working with
nano-particles.
• Productivity: Computers and automatic machines can work at very high capacity and
speed, which are beyond human capabilities. For instance placing millions of colored dots
on a TV.
• Safety: Automated systems can work well in environments that are unsafe for humans.
For instance in extreme temperatures, with poisonous gas or radiation.
• Quality & economy: With the help of automation, the user can secure better quality
and consistency of the end product. This can save on cost compared to manual labor.
• Importance to individuals, to organizations, and to society: Automation can
remove the need for middle managers and middle agents, which reduces or eliminates the
agency cost, thus reducing some of the bureaucracy.
• Accessibility: Automation can improve accessibility for all people, which includes dis-
abled people.
• Additional motivations: Additional motivations is the ability to use automation for
convenience, advantages of modernization and improvement in life quality.

Guidelines here include guidelines for manufacturing robots and installation. In this case, the

• Safeguarding devices: Workers operating with the robot should be safeguarded at all

– Mechanical limiting devices
– Non-mechanical limiting devices
– Presence-sensing safeguarding devices
– Fixed barriers
– Interlocked barrier guards
• Awareness devices: Awareness devices can be chains with warning signs and whistles.
They should bring awareness to the hazardous environments.

• Safeguarding operator: The operator of the robot should be protected from all hazards
linked to the work. When the robot is running, should all safeguard devices be on, and

7.10
Widening the search theory
In case of an event in form of an accident, it can be tracked to multiple factors. The theory
behind it is called extending the scope and is described in the book ”Beyond aviation human
factors”.
A major accident may at ﬁrst glance seem to be caused on the individual or individuals that
are associated with the event, which is mostly not the case. The event is linked not only local
human cause but distributed widely in the organization can often be traced back several years
before the event happened.
Figure 34 shows the diﬀerent factors contributing to a major event occurring. The ﬁgure shows
that a major event is not only triggered locally by a worker but can be tracked to major failures.
These failures can then be tracked to the top of the organization.
The top of the organization is mentioned as top-level, while workers having direct impact are
in the ”sharp end”.
64
==PAGE:65==
7
Automation
Figure 34: Active and latent failure to cause an event
• Organizational processes contains major processes set by the top-level management.
They are responsible for high-level decision making based on external economic and po-
litical factors. Organisational processes are always required when working in hazardous
conditions, and sets the local working conditions and defenses, barriers and safeguards.
• Local working conditions are factors that inﬂuence the eﬃciency and reliability of
the workers when doing their job. The organizational process has direct inﬂuence on the
local working conditions, both positive and negative. When bad decisions from top-level
management like unsafe working acts are made, it can lead to bad working conditions,
which can end up in critical events.
• Defences, barriers and safeguards are designed to protect people from the eﬀects of
risks and hazards.Defences, barriers and safeguards can be classiﬁed in to two categories.
The ﬁrst one being the function served. This includes creating awareness of the risks and
hazards, detect and warn of unusual conditions and possible hazards and protect workers
and work place from damage.
The second category is modes of application within an organization. This includes in-
structions, supervision, protective equipment and training.
• Active failures are failures like violations and errors directly impacting on the event.
These failures are done by workers that are in the sharp end. Active failures are mostly
detected by the defences and will often not have consequences.
65
==PAGE:66==

Automation
• Latent failures are failures that can have exist a couple of years before a major accident
occurs, without any noticeable bad outcomes. Latent failures are loopholes in the system’s
defences, barriers and safeguards. Combined with active failures or local triggers or often
all three, latent failure can lead to major accidental events.
• Event is the case where the risk occurs. The event will then pierce through the defences,
barriers and safeguards, either completely or partially. The event could cause uncontrolled
hazards and potential victims.
• Local triggers are triggers such as atypical system states, local environmental conditions
or adverse weather. These triggers will make the event more likely to happen, and they
directly eﬀect the event.

7.10.1

Widening the search model is utilized when an event occurs. In this case, a hazardous event
has not happened, so the whole model cannot be made. However, there is an element that
can be identiﬁed and changed to minimize hazards. Defences, barriers and safeguards can be
optimized to prevent latent failure.

Making a model, one can understand the situation that is being dealt with. However, models
do not represent the real world case precisely. A good model that describes defences, barriers
and safeguards in a given situation is the Swiss cheese model.

Knowing the possibility of multiple risk factors, it is crucial to go through a risk management
process. This process consists of 5/6 steps which as it can be seen in ﬁgure 36. [43]
Figure 36: Risk management cycle
Identiﬁcation
The ﬁrst step in the risk management cycle is to identify potential risks in the project. The risks
will then be reviewed and analyzed. The project team has identiﬁed several risks connected to
the work performed with the robots, and have been collected in a list.
68
==PAGE:69==

Quantiﬁcation & Qualiﬁcation
After identifying potential risks, a two step process will be performed. The ﬁrst thing is to
quantify the consequences and probabilities of the risk. This means ﬁnding out how likely it is
that the risk will occur and how big the consequences will be in that case. Consequences can
be in form of cost, injuries and others.
The second step is to qualify the kind of risk. This means ﬁnding other properties of the task
that can have the potential risk. These properties can then help with the next step in the risk
management cycle, which is decision.
Quantiﬁcation and qualiﬁcation can be classiﬁed under one term, which is risk analysis. A
risk analysis can be performed either as quantitative or qualitative. A quantitative risk analy-
sis can not be performed, due to requirements that can not be fulﬁlled, as described in chapter
7.4. Qualitative risk analysis is described in the PMI as ”the process of prioritizing individ-
ual project risks for further analysis or action by assessing their probability of occurrence and
impact as well as other characteristics”. A key feature of qualitative risk analysis is that it
focuses on high-priority risks [17]. Qualitative risk analysis will be performed as a risk assess-
ment, where all the identiﬁed risks are assessed and given a rating on their level of risk. Risk
assessment is performed in chapter 7.11.2.
Decision
After the information from quantiﬁcation and qualiﬁcation have been collected, the decision
making process can be performed. In the decision making process, the risk is assessed whether
it is acceptable in its current state, or need to be reduced.
This is the step in risk management cycle that takes most time, since it requires a lot of
discussion.
Reduction (when required)
The step of reduction is fully dependent on the previous step of decision making. When the
decision making process is ﬁnished, there will be decided whether reduction of the risk is
required, or if it is acceptable in its current state. In case reduction is not required, this step
can be skipped.
Monitor
Femern A/S will have to make ongoing monitor and evaluation of potential above mentioned
risks. The ﬁrst thing to do is to identify the risk owner, as he is responsible for monitor and
evaluation of the risk. This task consists of assessing whether the risk has been reduced or
removed, completely.
If the risk has not been removed or reduced enough, the risk owner
will have to assess the risk with the project manager to remove the risk or reduce it enough.
Depending on the magnitude of the risk, the risk management cycle can start over if necessary.

Feasibility study
A feasibility study is an analysis of a solution or idea, in terms of how feasible it is. A feasibility
study will tell about how successful the solution will be, by analysing diﬀerent factors like
economically or operational feasible the solution is.
Conducting a feasibility study consists of going through diﬀerent types of feasibility. This is
called a TELOS feasibility, and is an acronym of the ﬁve feasibility it contains, which are as
follows: [52–55]
• Technological feasibility: Is it possible to implement the technology in its current
state? Are there any constraint in technological resources?
• Economic feasibility: Will the solution be proﬁtable for the overall project? Is there
interest in the solution which will bring in funds?
• Legal feasibility: Is the solution legal? Does it break any laws or come in conﬂict with
other agreements?
• Operational feasibility: What changes are needed either long term or short term for
the solution to be successful? Is training for the workers required?
• Schedule feasibility: How will the solution aﬀect the schedule of the project? Will the
solution be able to be implemented when it is needed?

Prior to getting an in depth examination speciﬁc application of 4.0 within the company, it is

Figure 42 below shows the development of the industrial revolutions.
Figure 42: The era of the ﬁrst to the fourth industrial revolution
During the mid-1700s, it was the time of the ﬁrst industrial revolution. At this time people
started using steam to power the equipment. Essentially, it was the introduction of machines.
At ﬁrst it was introduced in the textile industry, where people manufactured products such as
cotton whining in very traditional manual processes. By introducing the steam powered equip-
ment, this process became mechanized. After this period, the machinery was spread out and
the machines were able to replace basic human tasks and functions. This had a huge impact,
with about a 400 to 500 percent increase in productivity. The introduction of steam powered
equipment also changed the location where products were made. Production moved from small
houses where looms were being made and became more centralized in the cotton factories.
Concluding the ﬁrst industrial revolution, it was a centralization of manufacturing. [57]
About a century later, the world moved into the second industrial revolution, where the
world saw the light of electrical engineering. The utilization of the electricity introduced a new
way of approaching the manufacturing industry. The doors for mass production was opened
and the ﬁrst moving conveyor belt was introduced. The car producer Ford was a primary pro-
ponent of this method. With Ford’s Model T manufacturing greatly accelerated, from a small
manual process where people produce multiple car elements, to where someone is working at
the production line on one speciﬁc part used for the overall process. This is called the division
of labor Where people are focusing on individual parts of an overall production.

Asset tracking
Lots of industries suﬀer with asset tracking within their Supply Chain Management (SCM).
The term SCM is a term originating from the 1990s. A lot of deﬁnitions of the term has circled
around - though there is a common agreement that is is about managing and optimizing the
material and information streams from supplier to the end-user. It involves tracing the prod-
ucts, processes, people, information, resources that all together will transform a raw material
into a product that satisﬁes a need. [82]
A common known technology used for SCM is Radio Frequency Identiﬁcation (RFID) and can
be an enabler for IoT. The technology uses radio waves for identiﬁcation and and tracking of
objects. According to an article there are various methods proposed by researchers to track
asset, including bar-coding, GPS, WIFI, RFID, etc. [83] The study compares the diﬀerent types
of tracking methods [83] and their diﬀerences in terms of cost, resistance to environment, ﬁt of
diﬀerent project sizes, etc.
The study concluded that a solution with a combination of bar-codes for identiﬁcation and
GPS for location would be the most optimal one for the given project. The conclusion is based
on the environment that construction sites are exposed to. As the bar-code method is already
applied within the industry and would require minimal eﬀort to the current stage and for the
GPS it provides an accuracy that is suﬃcient.
As indicated in the study the result is an automated or semi-automated process where paper
collection is removed from the tasks. Furthermore it will increase the accuracy of where mate-
rials are placed and thereby the need to search for material.

It has been 20 years since a similar construction project took place in Denmark, the Øresund

The study that is described in this publication has shown that applying ”assistive augmen-

technology as direct instruction for workers in diﬀerent assembling processes. The study was

This solution supports the worker during the assembling process as well as after the process.

where a robot has been programmed based on a recorded movements by a human worker and
transferred to motion sequences and adapted into the robot’s system. In the evaluated study
this method has been combined with interactive surfaces and AR to ﬁnally create a system
that can provide interactive projected instruction for humans.

Beginning of the technology development
The ﬁrst TRM is shown above at the Figure 77. At the late 60’s and early 70’s the digital era
had its breakthrough. Many companies, universities and institutions experimented with digital
technology. Researchers were hoping to create something groundbreaking and innovative while
studying and exploring digital technology.
The digital technology was a symbol of science
ﬁction. At that time, the science ﬁction movies were an inspiration to the inventors. They
were hoping to invent technologies such as digital notebooks, speaking robots, maid robots for
home use and diﬀerent sorts of vehicles. Potential beneﬁts at that time were the driver for
the researches and explorations. That led to the invention of Sketchpad [115] and it was a
beginning to the digital drawings that we know today.
Development paths which are categorized at the ﬁgure are explained as follows.
1. The breakthrough technology in the digitization of civil engineering processes was the
Sketchpad. A technology that made the ﬁrst digital drawing a reality. First digital draw-
ings were mentioned by Ivan Edward Sutherland in his PhD thesis at MIT (Massachusetts
Institute of Technology). [116] This brought an important value to the designers. The
eﬃciency in production of drawings rose signiﬁcantly.
2. Within the civil engineering the ﬁrst digital drawings were a very important point. It
pushed further investigations and inventions. Quite quickly after that the ﬁrst attempt
to combine the digital drawings with reality was made. The ﬁrst working prototype of
HMD was rolled out. The hardware as the Sketchpad was invented by Ivan E. Sutherland
and it was so called Cube in the air. [117] This invention took place in 1968. [118] He
experimented with a head-mounted three dimensional display.
This historical device

9.15
History of Exoskeleton
The term Exoskeleton derives from Greek ”exo” which means outer and ”skeletos” meaning
skeleton. It is as the name suggests, a skeleton on the outside, which is in contrast to an
endoskeleton, which is a skeleton on the inside. The Exoskeleton originates from the animal
kingdom, as some animals have their skeleton on the outside. These animals have a hard shell
on the outside, with ﬂexible joints and muscles on the inside. Exoskeletons works like armor
and protects the animals against predators, as well as protecting them from getting wet from
the rain.
Figure 80 below shows an X-Ray of a scorpion.
Figure 80: X-Ray of scorpion
The Figure 80 clearly shows that the skeleton of the scorpion in on the outside, and covers the
whole body. The ﬁgure also shows that the scorpion is completely hollow on the inside.
The development of robotic Exoskeletons for human use was ﬁrst mentioned in the second
half of the 20th century in 1965. General Electric developed an Exoskeleton called the Hardi-
man, which could augment the user to lift heavy weights.
It was not until the beginning of the 21st century, that Exoskeletons began to appear on the
market. Exoskeletons started as products developed to aid disabled people in their movement.
With this technology, people on wheel chairs because of weak limb damage could suddenly walk
again.
Later on, Exoskeletons where further developed for military use, to augment the user’s strength
and endurance.

9.16
Introduction to Exoskeleton
Heavy lifting and one-sided repetitive work is a challenge in many companies. Many of these
functions are increasingly being remedied by robots and other automation, so that employees
can be freed up for other value-producing work. The construction industry is an industry where
hard physical work is often performed with the risk of strain and wear and tear on the body.
New technology such as Exoskeletons may be able to alleviate many of the problems of repet-
itive hard physical work processes. Work environment plays an important role for well-being
and safety in the workplace. And the Exoskeleton could be the solution to help craftsmen
become less worn out, sore and tired. Exoskeletons are an attached mechanism you take on
e.g. the upper body, and aims to relieve the area on where the Exoskeleton is located.
Diﬀerent body support areas that Exoskeletons can be used:
1. Shoulders: an Exoskeleton at the shoulders acts as a support, primarily in and around
the shoulder joints. When using the Skeleton, the load around the shoulder complex is
reduced by adjusting the skeleton with a speciﬁc angle and auxiliary force. This reduces
the risk of overload or the development of shoulder injuries.
Figure 81: Exoskeleton that supports shoulders
2. Back: Placing an Exoskeleton with relief in the back signiﬁcantly increases the user’s
ability to work in positions that strain the back, primarily the lower back, when lifting or
bending in forward positions. In addition, the load is reduced by torques on the lumbar
spine.

Figure 82: Exoskeleton that supports back
3. Legs: Some postures require you to squat repeatedly, and to relieve the knee joint and
avoid straining the thigh muscles, you can use Exoskeletons speciﬁcally built for the legs.
In addition to the diﬀerent places an Exoskeleton can be attached, there are also diﬀerent
types, such as motor driven Exoskeletons or those called passive Exoskeletons.
Figure 83: Exoskeleton that supports legs
The principle of passive Exoskeletons is basically that they do not add any extra energy to
you, but instead try to maintain a balance in the body’s natural position. With this solution,
wear and disease due to overloads will be reduced. At the same time, employees will be able to
perform more and thus increase eﬃciency and capacity. And it is also a good signal that you
’invest’ in a good working environment for your employees so that they do not get worn out in
the labor market.

The 10 steps are furthermore divided into three phases of the timeline:
• Preparation Phase
In the preparation phase the beneﬁts to the strategy are outlined. Relevant stakeholders
are introduced to the solutions on a workshop where it is evaluated if there is experience
in the ﬁeld or a need for training from new employees. Stakeholders should be involved
as much as possible, mostly due to 2 reasons. Feedback from the workers on the solutions

on the project on a daily basis - therefore feedback is essential from them, in order to
make sure that everyone is on the same page.
Furthermore by evaluating the preparation phase of the solution, minor or fewer changes
may only be experienced during the implementation phase.
• Implementation Phase
Project managers make sure that workers knows how to use the new solution. In case there
is something they are missing, the workers should be able to get solid exploration and
thereby adapt to the information given. Through agile management and weekly meetings,
continuous feedback from the workers is given, highlighted on either a technology roadmap
or a Kanban board. Through the meetings the project manager will be able to see new
opportunities or limitations if a given solution is to be upgraded or in implementing a
new one.
According to the PMI by having interactions on a regular basis the project will minimize
potential of risks, trust is build and adjustments will be supported earlier in the project
cycle which will have a positive impact on project costs and thereby the overall success
of the project. [17]
• Post Implementation Phase
After the implementation phase, the post phase follows. In this phase the new solution
and working methods are becoming standard in the company. Procedures and training
material is provided for the new solutions and old training material is removed for the
purpose of not making confusions. New workers should be given training according to
the new solutions, why a program is important. A R&D department is set up where their
primary function is going to maintain, update and make sure that the solution is con-
stantly improving based on new technologies/methods of working. Through continuous
reviewing workers process based on data estimates and weekly meetings, a good workﬂow
and stability in the company is ensured.

Discussion
Major ﬁndings
The research in this Master thesis aimed to optimize on the production process of Femern
tunnel, with focus on the reinforcement process. The research focused on three major areas,
Automation, Industry 4.0 and Augmentation.
Based on research for these three areas, it was found that every focus area had its advan-
tages and disadvantages.
The research on automation showed three robots that can be used to automate several repeti-
tive tasks in the production process. The research showed fairly positive on multiple analysis,
including cost-beneﬁt and risk assessment. The solution is also very favorable by Femern A/S,
as they suggested it from the beginning.
Research on Industry 4.0 showed a potential use of RFID technology making it possible to track
objects. The tool connects processes on diﬀerent levels, giving the company a tool to track
reinforcement through the Supply Chain, it does not require lots of work, and with the newest
technology is cheap to implement. Before this tool was chosen, the maturity of the company
was assessed which in overall concluded low maturity in regards to Industry 4.0, but ambitions
towards improving the Supply Chain. The solution showed positive results in terms of tech-
nological maturity towards this project and has been something that Femern A/S experienced
was missing from an earlier project. Due to diﬀerent software and stakeholders in the project
it makes it complicated to integrate diﬀerent systems and would most likely require a decision
from the top management level for a standardization. Furthermore the culture is shown to be
a hinder from succesfully utilizing the use of digital technologies why there is a need to bring
awareness about the topic.
The research on augmentation showed two diﬀerent tools for assisting workers.
The tools
consist of assisting in guiding the workers in the performance of their tasks, and giving them
physical support in intense labor work, respectively.
Through interviews, literature review and questionnaires it was conﬁrmed that the culture
in the construction industry is conservative. This is the main cause of workers not adapting
to the new technologies, since in terms of technological maturity - the technology is there and
can be implemented if needed. It was found that many workers lacked the knowledge about
the digital technologies and that they even existed.
The PESTEL analysis on Industry 4.0 furthermore showed the importance of environment
and governance in the transition to Industry 4.0. The results matter when setting up strategies
for a company as they can be both be seen as values towards the workers but also barriers that
have to be worked through in order to make digitilization possible.

Discussion
The most optimal solution combines the use of single tasked robots assisted by an opera-
tor for reinforcement execution processes, RFID technology for Supply Chain trace-ability,
Augmented Reality for training of workers, and Exoskeletons for minimizing injuries during
the construction process.
To fully integrate Industry 4.0 in the company, it will require changes in the six core-dimensions
as assessed in 8.10. It will require several steps for each core-dimension to be at Industry 4.0
level, which is far from Femern A/S’s ambitions.
Femern A/S is not interested in Industry 4.0 just for the name, but for something that gives
the company value. It is clear they have ambitions of becoming more digitalized, but if it does
not add up to the beneﬁts, then they would rather use their current methods.
Even though the research showed that there is great potential in optimizing the production
process, there will still be risks to consider. In terms of any given solution, there should always
be a redundancy. Femern tunnel project is at the moment planned to be executed exactly
like the Øresund tunnel. Suggestions in the research includes major changes in the production
hall, which might lead to potential risks. Though the risk assessment showed mostly low risk
regarding implementation of automation, it is a risk that still exists and should be taken into
consideration. In case the solution is adopted and it suddenly is not possible to execute, there
needs to be a backup plan to switch over to the original plan of making the production process
manually. This is also present for the implementation of the tracking system.
Automating the production process is not planned to completely exterminate manual labor,
but will decrease it by 33%. This means that the production can still run at 67% of its capacity
without automation. The production will then not stop in case the solution fails, and can still
run, but at a lower capacity to begin with. Femern A/S should agree with FLC to have a
couple of foremen with their craftsmen ready as backup, in case it is required.
Through the various analyses it seems that implementing automation in the production hall,
comes exclusively with beneﬁts. Risk is mostly low, cost-beneﬁt ratio is high and it seems
feasible. Furthermore, are accidents regarding robots low, and there are clear safeguards and
rules to follow, in order to stay safe when working with robots. This argument is also further re-
inforced through various literature. BMW’s manufacturing plant switched its welding division
to almost being completely run by robots. This has increased eﬃciency and reduced fatigue
by workers. As mentioned previously, the industrialization has not been present to the same
degree in construction, when compared to other ﬁelds. That creates new challenges for project
managers who will now consider a construction project as an industrial production. There
could be a valuable opportunity to turn the focus towards other concrete element factories.
Robots are also drastically falling in price, and have more than halved in price in the last
30 years. Combine this with labour cost, which has more than doubled in the last 30 years, it
is clear that automation has many beneﬁts.

Conclusion
In Denmark, the construction industry makes up almost 80% of Danish economic wealth. (Dan-
ish construction 2016). Therefore, it is extremely important to make an active eﬀort to increase
labor productivity in construction projects. As the Femern tunnel project is one of the world’s
largest and Europe’s most important infrastructure projects, it presents a great opportunity
to further the expansion of construction industry technology. It has a duty to be the market
leader in optimization and to be the ﬁrst link in the chain of new innovative solutions that will
pave the way for eﬃcient and sustainable construction processes.
This Master thesis aimed to optimize the production process for the Femern tunnel, with
focus on the reinforcement production.
The optimization focused on three main areas, which were Automation, Industry 4.0 and
Augmentation.
The research for the three focus areas was based on qualitative analysis methods, where data
was collected through literature review, meetings and conferences.
Research for automation focused on the possibility to establish automated systems in the
reinforcement production process for Femern tunnel project.
Automation is the act of executing work processes automatically, with limited to non human
interaction. The analysis focused on construction industry automation, which deals with robot
based automation. Diﬀerent robots where introduced for diﬀerent tasks to automate, and a
complete plan for reinforcement production process. These robot are Arm-Tec SpinMaster,
which is used to make reinforcement matts, Advanced construction robotics TyBot, used to tie
the intersections in the nets, and Schnell Coil 20 & APPS, for producing stirrups and placing
them at the desired spot based on stirrup type.
The analysis aimed to ﬁnd out whether and how advantageous automation would be for the
tunnel production process, and at which degree is the solution feasible.
Analyses tools and methods for this chapter were picked based on the factors mentioned above.
These included analysis of risk, cost-beneﬁt and feasibility.
The analysis conducted for automation showed fairly positive results all around. It was found
out that implementing automation in the reinforcement production process will decrease the
man hours per ton of reinforcement by a third. Furthermore is the risk of implementing au-
tomation mostly low, and implementation feasible in multiple aspects taken into consideration,
with minimal obstacles.
Industry 4.0 marks the fourth industrial revolution, and can be seen as the introduction of
IT to industrial manufacturing.

Technologies that push the revolution forward is the use of big data, IoT, Artiﬁcial Intelligence

a solution for the core dimension that Femern A/S had largest ambitions towards, and what
they wanted to change the processes for - the Supply Chain. It was found that in order for the
company fully recognize the values of Industry 4.0, the lean approach could be a basis for it. By
applying the lean approach of looking at the values that comes with changing processes, it can
eliminate processes in the Supply Chain that causes time delays. The use of Radio-frequency
Identiﬁcation (RFID) censor technology was introduced adding value to the Supply Chain in
a future state presentation.
Further analysis was made to access the maturity of the RFID technology, including a technol-
ogy readiness level and a risk assessment. The analysis gave a clear view on the maturity of
the technology that could be implemented in the company, as well as the risks associated with
implementing it.
By the added value it gives to the company’s supply chain, the technology used for tracking
reinforcement, could be applied for future projects as well. With Femern A/S as implementer
of this solution, it could eventually evolve into more advanced integrated technologies that
many other stakeholders in the construction Industry could beneﬁt from.
The development of the Augmented Reality as an innovative tool in the construction indus-
try is remarkable. This is the beginning of an interesting journey which cannot be precisely
predicted. All the factors that have been presented in this thesis indicate that this technology
will contribute to the processes in the construction sector in many ways. Beneﬁts are primarily
gained by faster management processes at the construction site. Instruction of the workers,
safety training and quality control are much more eﬃcient. This provides gains in the overall
the construction processes, which are within the scope of this thesis and pushes for the adaption
of Industry 4.0 technologies.
Physical fatigue of workers can be minimized by application of Exoskeleton which is able to
support the muscles that are exposed to the biggest strain during construction processes. This
solution provides beneﬁts that mostly can be seen in the long-term. The Exoskeleton helps
to avoid serious injuries that would be visible only after years of the users’ service. The most
recognizable beneﬁt gained by the workers is less exhaustion after work. Those beneﬁts are
essential to the company as this solution can minimize sick working days of the craftsmen.
As indicated from the thesis it is seen that the technological maturity gained is not what
is stopping the implementation of RFID and exoskeleton, it is an organizational as well as
managerial challenge, but still manageable.

Conclusion
It was found that the requirements from above are a strong driver for innovative solutions.
The ICT executive order has sparked the development of a more digitized approach to con-
struction industry. Similarly, Covid-19 restrictions have generated many new solutions in a
number of diﬀerent sectors. Based on these examples the project team suggests for Femern
A/S to set speciﬁc requirements for the usage of a cloud-based platform suited for the construc-
tion industry. In that way the Femern A/S would minimize the risk of the cost increase during
the construction processes. Since these two mentioned platforms are based on customers’ expe-
rience, the Femern project would contribute to the development of future technologies, which
soon may become the standard operating procedure in the construction industry.
Femern A/S can beneﬁt tremendously from implementing automation in the reinforcement
production process. It meets their need of ﬂattening and decreasing the learning curve, which
leads to key advantages. The decreased learning curve will lead to fewer craftsmen working on
the production, which results in fewer injuries. Furthermore, Femern A/S will beneﬁt from a
signiﬁcant price drop in the reinforcement work. Implementation will be expensive, but will be
very beneﬁcial in the long run.
Femern A/S should however stay cautious because risk is always present, and possible conse-
quences may occur. It is then recommended to consider the Swiss cheese model to strengthen
their defenses, barriers and safeguard and to use widening the search theory if a hazardous
event occurs.
The cost and beneﬁts for Industry 4.0 is not there in all areas at Femern. To have a smart
factory where machines, robots and censors are working together is not just a small step and will
require additional capacities which the organization will need to pay to develop and maintain.
As the Femern tunnel is mass production, Industry 3.0 or just automation would be more
suﬃcient for production of tunnel elements than investing in the capabilities to make customized
products - a beneﬁt that comes with Industry 4.0.
For implementation of Industry 4.0 at the last maturity level, Femern A/S has to undergo
a long-term strategic transformation, and account for workers in a conservative industry by
highlighting the value that digitalized changes can give the company. The change of the industry
requires awareness of the digital solutions and by using lean thinking approaches, the company
will get a better view on the beneﬁts that digitilization has on the industry. Furthermore it
will help them becoming more agile and adapt quicker to changes from unplanned events.
Lastly, Femern A/S has an indirect responsibility to be a role model towards smaller companies
in the sector, pushing the construction industry forward towards better productivity and less
injuries.
