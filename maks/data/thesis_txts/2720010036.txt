Abstract
Dimethachlor is a herbicide applied on oil rape seed, and in various countries its metabolites
are found in the groundwater. In Denmark it was approved until 1990 and recently its metabo-
lites, especially dimethachlor ESA and CGA 369873, have been detected in several Danish ab-
straction wells, including the Slimminge well ﬁeld in Central Zealand. Pesticide pollution can
either stem from diffuse or point sources, of which for the later three are identiﬁed in the catch-
ment area. Residues of pesticides can stay in agricultural soils for decades and thus potentially
be long term diffuse sources. The focus of this work is to review the literature on dimethachlor
and its metabolites, and to assess their impact of the point and diffuse sources on the water
quality in the Slimminge abstraction wells.
Soil samples from both point and potential diffuse sources were taken and analysed for
residues of dimethachlor and its metabolites with a high performance liquid chromotography
(SPE-LC-MSMS). The results were compared to site characterization data, including ground-
water concentrations and borehole descriptions. For the diffuse sources, ﬁelds on which rape
seed was planted during the approval period of dimethachlor, were found by analyzing aerial
pictures. A total of 22 spots on two agricultural ﬁelds were sampled within the upper 1 - 1.5 m,
and two soil cores were taken to the depth of 4.88 m. For a mass balance the vertical contam-
inant mass discharge (CMD) at the sources was compared to the mass discharge entering the
abstraction wells.
At the point sources the main contamination of the water consists of dimethachlor OA and
dimethachlor ESA, and is present in the deep clay layer, right above the limestone aquifer. The
soil samples were strongly dominated by residues of the mother compound within the ﬁrst
5 mbgs. All sites have contamination hotspots and at site 2 the spill of dimethachlor is likely
to have happened more recently. At the agricultural ﬁelds, no residues of the compound were
detectable. The CMD from the point sources was not sufﬁcient to explain the magnitude of
contamination in the abstraction wells. Furthermore, during the transport in the groundwater
dimethachlor is likely degraded into CGA 369873 and dimethachlorcarbonyl sulfonic acid, and
dimethachlor OA into non-extractable residues. Especially for the OA metabolite the exact
transformation products and pathways are unknown.
Summarizing, no contribution to the contamination from the agricultural ﬁelds could be
concluded and based on the mass balance further point sources are expected in the catchment
area. The CMD is based on a simple approach, whereby the highest uncertainties stem from the
inﬁltration rate and the size of the contaminated area. More knowledge is needed regarding
the transformation of dimethachlor metabolites especially under anaerobic conditions. Further
investigations should be conducted at the known point sources, especially site 3, and the catch-
ment area should be examined on additional point sources. The tolerated concentration for
dimethachlor metabolites are much higher in other countries, raising the debate if the Danish
law is too protective or if other regulations are too low to the protect groundwater.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Contamination of groundwater with pesticides and their metabolites poses a threat for the
drinking water supply, particularly in Denmark, where it solely relies on groundwater abstrac-
tion [1, 2]. In the past the detection of pesticides or their metabolites has already lead to the
closure of drinking water abstraction wells in both Denmark and other European countries [3].
These closures are closely linked to the respective water quality criteria. In the EU the ground-
and drinking water is not allowed to contain more than 0.1 µg/L of individual pesticides or
relevant metabolites and not more than 0.5 µg/L for the total sum of these substances [4, 5].
In Denmark similar rules are applied, with the differences that all and not only the relevant
metabolites are included in the quality criteria [6].
Several compounds of the chloroacetanilide herbicide group are frequently detected in
groundwaters all over the world including Europe and the USA [7–11]. Findings in surface
and groundwater in Germany were clearly dominated by the metabolites of this group [8].
Also in Denmark these pesticides and especially the compound dimethachlor are gaining more
attention. In 2020 one of its metabolites called dimethachlor ESA was detected in 2.1% of the
groundwater sampled in the national monitoring program GRUMO. It is even within the top
ten most detected pesticides in Danish drinking water supply wells, posing a potential risk for
the drinking water quality. Furthermore, the degradation compounds dimethachlor ESA and
dimethachlor OA have recently been added to the list of compounds to be monitored in Danish
groundwater. [11]
Despite its current relevance, dimethachlor was used in Denmark more than 30 years ago.
The compound was introduced in 1977 and approved in Denmark from 1980 - 1990 in form
of the product Teridox 500EC [11–13]. In this time span a total of 161 tons were sold and the
possession of the compound was ﬁnally banned in Denmark in 2012 [14]. The herbicide is
designed as a pre-emergence treatment on oil seed rape plants [13]. The pesticide was not
expected t leach to groundwater due to its relative high adsorption and quick degradation [15].
A generally higher risk for the groundwater is expected to stem from the degradation products,
as they are usually more mobile and persistent than the mother compound [13].
When it comes to groundwater contamination, there are two main types of sources namely
point and diffuse sources. In general a point source is characterized by a small affected area,
high concentrations and strong horizontal and vertical concentration gradients. Typical ar-
eas for pesticide point sources are locations for pesticide storage, ﬁlling and washing sites.
1
==PAGE:14==
Chapter 1. Introduction
[15, 16] Pesticide point sources can potentially be remediated by methods using contaminant-
immobilization, separations or destruction [17].
A diffuse source on the other hand is deﬁned by a large affected area with lower contam-
inant concentrations and limited vertical and horizontal concentration gradients in the soil.
They typically occur on agricultural ﬁelds, where pesticides are applied regularly in a diluted
from. For these sources, remediation actions are normally not used due to the large affected
area. Pesticides can adsorb to agricultural soils making them potential long-term leaching
sources. The retardation factor depends on the soil and pesticide type [18]. Even after 20
years without the use of pesticides, several different pesticide residues can be detected on agri-
cultural ﬁelds [19], showing that legacy pesticides can have long-term effects.
A few articles have already addressed the occurrence of chloroacentanilide herbicides and
their degradation products in soil including alachlor, metolachlor and metolachlor ESA [19, 20].
However, no research about dimethachlor residues in soil was found, indicating two important
knowledge gaps. Firstly, it is unexplored which metabolites are present in the soil and how high
the concentrations of the mother compound and the degradation products are. Secondly, the
risk of these residues contaminating groundwater aquifers is not well investigated.
Dimethachlor metabolites have been detected in the Slimminge well ﬁeld, located in Cen-
tral Zealand in Denmark [21]. To determine where the contamination stems from, three point
sources in the area were identiﬁed earlier, by analyzing water samples from the sites [22–24].
As the mother compound was applied to the many agricultural ﬁelds around the abstraction
wells, these could potentially leach over a long time and thus contribute to the contamination in
the well ﬁeld. The aim of this thesis is to enhance the understanding of dimethachlor residues
acting as long-term sources for dimethachlor metabolites and to assess the risk of groundwater
contamination by point and diffuse sources in the Slimminge well ﬁeld. The speciﬁc objectives
of the thesis are to
• review literature on dimethachlor, its behavior in the environment including its degrada-
tion process and identify the most relevant metabolites and their expected behaviour in
the environment
• analyze three dimethachlor point sources regarding the geology and contamination dis-
tribution and interpret the spatial distribution of dimethachlor and its metabolites in soil
and groundwater
• identify relevant agricultural ﬁelds and collect, compare and interpret data about con-
taminant concentrations in soil samples
• link the sources to the well ﬁeld by calculating a mass balance from the point and diffuse
sources, to determine the resulting impact for the abstraction wells
By this, new knowledge about the possible relevance of agricultural ﬁelds as a long-term
sources for pesticides and the risk for underlying groundwater aquifers can be generated. From
here possible connotations for other European countries, still applying dimethachlor and for
similar compounds from the chloroacetanilide herbicides can be elaborated.

2.1.1
Use and history
Dimethachlor belongs to the class of chloroacetanilide herbicides and is used pre-emergence on
oilseed rape plants to control annual grasses and broad-level weed species. It is the active com-
pound in the product Teridox 500EC, which is an emulsiﬁable concentrate with a dimethachlor
concentration of 500 g/L [13, 15]. It is applied once per year with a vehicle-mounted or a boom
sprayer in to a clean seed bed. The intended application rate is 1.5 kg/ha and the herbicide has
to be diluted into 100 - 400 water L/ha. [13] Dimethachlor is a selective herbicide and it acts
by inhibiting elongase-enzymes and therefore stopping the synthesis of very long chain fatty
acids (VLCFA) [13, 25].
In Denmark Teridox 500EC was approved and commercially available from 1980-1990. [13,
14] In this period a total of 161 tons were sold, with raising sales over the years peaking in 1990
with 88.600 kg of sold product, as it can be seen in Figure 2.1. [15] Regarding the high sales
in the late 80’ies a continuous application until the mid 1990’ies is likely. The possession of
product in Denmark was banned in 2012 as a result from the regulation EC 1107/2009 [14].

Environmental behavior of dimethachlor
Physical-chemical properties of dimethachlor
The IUPAC name for dimethachlor is 2-chloro-N-(2-methoxyethyl)acet-2‘,6‘-xylidide [13]. It
belongs to the class of chlororoacetanilide herbicides, which main structure consists of dialky-
lanin with different side groups [11], as it can be seen in Table 2.2. Other herbicides of this
family are acetochlor, alachlor, butachlor, dimethenamid, dimethenamid-P, metazachlor, meto-
lachlor, pethoxamid, propachlor and s-metolachlor [26].
The physical-chemical properties of dimethachlor and the respective sources are listed in
Table 2.2. In the following the values are described and interpreted according to the guidelines
of the Pesticide Properties Data Base (PPDB) [27]. Considering the melting point of around
46°C, dimethachlor is present in solid state under normal environmental conditions. The solu-
bility in water of 2.3 g/L is high, and distinctively higher than of the other chloroacetanilides
herbicides which range from 20 mg/L for butachlor [28] to 1.5 g/L for dimethenamid-P [29].
However, the water solubility does not reach the desired concentrations for ﬁeld applications,
such that the compound is typically brought into the environment in the form of emulsiﬁable
concentrates, which are diluted prior to use (see Section 2.1.1).

dimethachlor. Furthermore, for the structurally very similar compound metolachlor organic
matter has been shown be the predominant adsorbent [31], such that the same can be assumed
for dimethachlor.

Transformation and persistence of dimethachlor
Neither hydrolysis nor photolysis contribute signiﬁcantly to the degradation of dimethachlor
[12, 13]. The degradation of the compound has been observed in both aerobic and anaerobic
soils [13, 32], and the degradation can be expected to be governed by microbial activity. For
the structural similar compounds alachlor and metolachlor the dominance of the microbial
degradation pathway has also been shown in several studies [33–35].
In soil a ﬁrst-order degradation of dimethachlor is observed [13, 32, 36].

For the degradation of dimethachlor in the subsoil or in groundwater, no studies could
be found. However, studies for other compounds in the chloroacetanilide group suggest a
generally slower degradation under these conditions. Alachlor’s degradation rate for example
is lower in the subsoil than in the plough layer and higher with more biomass and microbial

most of the laboratory experiments [13, 32].
Summarizing, the ﬁndings of the half-lives indicate dimethachlor to be non-persistent in
most cases, but more a persistent behaviour under certain circumstances is possible. Favorable
conditions prolonging the stability of dimethachlor could for example be lower temperatures
and low microbial activity. These conditions are for example given during the winter months
and in deeper soil layers, in which the average groundwater temperature is around 9°C [38]
in Denmark. Furthermore, the microbial activity is generally declining with depth. In com-
bination with the temperatures it can be expected that most dimethachlor will be degraded
in the top soil and it to be more persistent if it reaches lower soil layers. In Section 2.2 the
dimethachlor metabolites formed under degradation in soil are listed and described.
2.1.3
Toxicity of dimethachlor
To humans, dimethachlor is of moderate toxicity at oral intake and of low toxicity by dermal
uptake. In the body the compound is absorbed moderately rapidly and distributed widely.
However, due to its complete metabolization and rapid excretion, it shows no potential for
accumulation in humans. The compound is not genotoxic, but after tumors reported in a rat
study the EFSA proposed dimethachlor to be classiﬁed as ’Harmful, Limited evidence of a
carcinogenic effect.’ The acceptable daily intake for dimethachlor (ADI) was evaluated to be
0.1 mg/kg bw. [13]
In the environment dimethachlor has more severe effects. It is classiﬁed as very toxic to aquatic
organisms and not bioaccumulative. Short-term EC50 values for aquatic organisms varied form
3.9 mg/L in ﬁsh, to 24 mg/L in daphnia to as low as 0.0054 mg/L in algae. The compound
showed a low toxicity for bees, and is considered to be a low long-term risk to earthworms and
soil organisms. [13]

2.2.1
Formation of Metabolites
Around 20 metabolites are described in the literature and in Figure 2.2 the known degradation
pathways in soils as described by ’enviPath’ [39] are shown in a ﬂow-diagram. Under aerobic
conditions four degradation pathways (labeled A, B, C and D) are known. Route A and B
are so-called gutathione pathways, as they are expected to be formed via the conjunction to

this antioxidant. In the ﬁrst step of route A, the metabolite dimethachlor ESA (also called
CGA 354742) is formed, which is one of the two most dominant dimethachlor metabolites as
described by the literature. This degradation pathway occurs often in microbial active soils
and plays an important role for detoxiﬁcation. [39] In an experiment under aerobic conditions
15.8 % of the originally applied dimethachlor had transformed to dimethachlor ESA after 14
days [13].
Oxidation of this compound leads to dimethachlorcarbonyl sulﬁnic acid [39]. In the past,
this compound was often incorrectly labeled as CGA 373464, which in fact is a different degra-
dation product. This mistake stems from an EFSA report in 2008 and this mix up of the com-
pounds is therefore likely in former reports and analyses [40]. Dealkylation/desulfonation/hydrolosis
of dimethachlorcarbonyl sulfonic acid lead via CGA 369873 to the proposed compound CGA 37734
and ultimately to the formation of CO2 [39].
The second major metabolite is dimethachlor OA (also called CGA 50266), which accounted
for 12 - 35 % of the original amount of dimethachlor under aerobic conditions after 4 - 63 days
[13]. Dimethachlor OA is formed under the degradation pathway ’D’ via the compound CGA
39981. By oxidation of CGA 39981 also the compound SYN 530561 can be formed.
The compounds SYN 528702, which is formed under pathway ’C’, and CGA 102935, which
can be part of all the named pathways, contained 6 - 9 % of the original radio label added to
dimethachlor in an experiment after 42 -120 days. [13] Thus they make up a much smaller
fraction of the metabolites formed especially in comparison to dimethachlor OA in the study
conditions.
Under anaerobic conditions dimethachlor can be degraded into CGA 42443, which under
aerobic conditions will be oxidized to CGA 39981. [39] Also the formation of dimethachlor OA
and dimethachlor ESA was observed under anaerobic conditions, but to a lesser extend. [13]
López-Ruiz et al. (2020) [36] monitored the soil concentrations of the dimethachlor and its
metabolites in hermetically closed ﬂasks. The soil was spiked with Teridox 500EC equivalent
to an application rate of 1.5 kg dimethachlor/ha (7000 µL Teridox/kg soil) onto two different
soils (sandy loam and loam). The type of conditions were not explicitly named in the study,
but can be expected to (partially) be anaerobic (see Section 2.1.2). The analyzed compounds
were dimethachlor OA, SYN 528702 sodium salt, CGA 39981 and CGA 42443. All of them
were detected, with the maximum concentrations being reached 70 days after application (the
total experiment lasted 110 days). The highest concentration were found for CGA 42443 with
up to 1074.4 µg/kg in loam, followed by dimethachlor OA (979.9 µg/kg in loam) and CGA
399981 (705.4 µg/kg in loam). Furthermore, three formerly unknown transformation products
are described, of which two seem to form in soils. One of the newly described compounds was
detectable already at day 0 and as its concentration declined afterwards, it could be an impurity
of Teridox 500EC. The degradation pathway for the remaining compounds is unknown and
they are temporarily named metabolite m/z 198 and m/z 238 [36]

Mobility of metabolites

Transformation and persistence of metabolites
For the main degradation products dimethachlor ESA and dimethachlor OA, laboratory tests
have been conducted to determine the half-life in soil, and under photolysis and hydrolysis.

therefore shows a low to high persistence, with the average indicating a non-persistent behav-
ior, when following the guidelines of the PPDB [13, 27].

Groundwater monitoring - chloroacetanilide compounds and their metabolites
Findings of other chloroacetanilide herbicides are much more described in the literature and
their presence has been proven in groundwater in for example Spain, Germany and the US
[7–9]. In a study by Reemtmsa et al. (2013) [8], which examined ground- and surface water in
Germany, the ﬁndings were clearly dominated by metabolites of chloroacetanilide herbicides.
These include metabolites from acetochlor, metolachlor, metazachlor and propachlor [8].
Often the ESA and OA metabolites are detected e.g. metolachlor ESA in Swiss groundwater
with concentration up to 0.97 µg/L [10] and alachlor ESA which was present in 1.5 % of Danish
abstraction wells [11]. Other studies exemplary include metazachlor OA, alachlor ESA and
acetochlor ﬁndings in Polish groundwater [15] and metolachlor ESA which was present in
11 % of US groundwater samples with concentrations up to 2.5 µg/L [7]. Furthermore, also
the mother compounds such as alachlor, acetochlor and metolachlor have been detected in
different European groundwater aquifers [9, 15, 46].
2.3.2
Soil concentrations
For dimethachlor and its metabolites no ﬁeld studies could be found addressing the concentra-
tions in soils. Two studies conducted by Riedo et al. (2021), (2022) [19, 47] focused on pesticide
residues on agricultural ﬁelds and grassland sites. In the study of 2021 [19] metolachlor-ESA
was detected at an average of 3.1 µg/kg and a maximum of 4.1 µg/kg, and s-metolachlor was
found at a median concentration of 2.5 µg/kg and a maximum of 79 µg/kg. Unfortunately, it
was not clearly stated whether these results stem from organically or conventionally managed
ﬁelds. No residues of alachlor, acetochlor and metolachlor-OA and were detected.
In the study from 2022 [47], alachlor was found on a grassland sites with concentrations
up to 10 µg/kg and s-metolachlor was detected on grassland sites, and on both conventional
and organic agricultural ﬁelds with respective concentrations up to 2.9 µg/kg, 17 µg/kg and
3.7 µg/kg. In this study no residues were found neither of metolachlor OA, metolachlor ESA
nor acetochlor. Metolachlor and s-metolachlor are more persistent than alachlor and dimethachlor
[12, 48–50], such that the detection of these compounds do not necessarily indicate that also
dimethachlor is likely to be found on agricultural ﬁelds.
2.4
Legal rules regarding dimethachlor and its metabolites in drink-
ing and groundwater
2.4.1
EU
There are three relevant regulations regarding pesticide and metabolite residues in groundwa-
ter which are the pesticide regulation (Regulation (EC) 1107/2009), the drinking water direc-
tive (Directive (EU) 2020/2184) and the groundwater directive (Directive 2006/118/EC). Each
metabolite falls be classiﬁed as either ’relevant’, ’non-relevant’ or to be a ’metabolite of no-
concern’ [51] In the drinking and groundwater directives the quality criteria for pesticides and
their relevant metabolites are given with 0.1 µg/L for single compounds and with 0.5 µg/L
17
==PAGE:30==
Chapter 2. Dimethachlor and Metabolites
for the total sum of pesticides and their relevant metabolites (Directive (EU) 2020/2184 [4]
and Directive 2006/118/EC [5]). For non-relevant metabolites the quality criteria varies from
0.75 µg/L to >10 µg/L, dependent on several assessments of the compound [52].
A metabolite is deﬁned as ’any metabolite or a degradation product of an active substance, safener
or synergist, formed either in organisms or in the environment’. It is declared as a ’relevant metabo-
lite’ if it for example poses a higher or equal risk to organisms than the parent compound (Reg-
ulation (EC) 1107/2009 [53]). Under certain conditions, metabolites are classiﬁed as products of
’no-concern’ and need no further assessment. However, this does not apply for the metabolites
of dimethachlor (except for the CO2), so that they are either relevant or non-relevant metabo-
lites. (Sanco/221/2000 [52])
The national laws can include own deﬁnitions and quality criteria, as long as they are
in compliance with the EU regulations. Therefore, the legal framework regarding pesticide
residues in ground and drinking water varies across the member states. In Germany for ex-
ample the quality criteria for non-relevant metabolites in groundwater is 1 µg/L or 3 µg/L
depending on the available toxicological data, while it is set to 0.9 µg/L in France.
The same is true for the deﬁnition of relevant and non-relevant metabolites, such that the
metabolites of dimethachlor are classiﬁed differently across the countries. In Germany the
compounds dimethachlor ESA, CGA 369873 and CGA 373464 are non-relevant metabolites,
whereby the ﬁrst two metabolites are part of the list of recommendations for pesticide metabo-
lite monitoring in German groundwaters [54, 55].
2.4.2
Denmark
In Denmark the ’Miljøstyrelsen’ is responsible for the approval and regulation of pesticides
[51]. The Danish regulations regarding the deﬁnition of relevant metabolites is stricter than in
other EU countries, leading to more metabolites being monitored [56]. Furthermore, the quality
criteria of 0.1 µg/L and 0.5 µg/L account for pesticides and all their metabolites, whereas the
EU regulations only considers relevant metabolites(BEK nr 1383 af 03/10/2022 [57]).
There is no real deﬁnition for relevant metabolites, but the so called ’Drikkevandsbekendt-
gørelsen’ includes a list of them [51]. This lists includes dimethachlor ESA, dimethachlor OA,
(2,6-dimethyl-phenylcarabamoyl)-methansulfonysre and [(2,6-dimethylphenyl)(2-sulfoacetyl)
amino]acetic acid. Many of them have recently been added to the list and must consequently
be monitored in the drinking and groundwater. (BEK nr 1383 af 03/10/2022 [57])
As for the relevant metabolites, there is no clear deﬁnition for non-relevant metabolites, but
instead a non-complete list is foun found in the ’Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen’ (BEK nr 1383
af 03/10/2022 [57]). This list does not include dimethachlor metabolites, but metazachlor ESA
and metazachlor OA, which are degradation products of the chloroacetanilide metazachlor.
The classiﬁcation between relevant and non-relevant metabolites has no inﬂuence on the al-
lowed concentration, but impacts the water works possibility to keep operating if this criteria
is exceeded. If non-relevant metabolites are present in too high concentrations, the water works
can apply for an exemption, which can be given for up to three years. This exemption can be
renewed up to two times, after which the water work would have to close if the problem was
not resolved within these nine years. (BEK nr 1383 af 03/10/2022 [57])

The pre-quaternary deposits consist of three layers which are Late Cretaceous (Maastrichain)

Maastrichian chalk has a thickness of 1000 - 1500 m [61], the Danian limestone of 15 - 50 m

of hardened limestone, the three layers in the middle consist of hardened limestone with thin
layers of clay and marl, and the upper two layers resemble the middle layers with fewer parts
of limestone [63].
The morainal cover is located directly on the pre-quaternary layer in most places and occa-
sionally the two layers are separated by meltwater sand or gravel [62]. The Quaternary deposits
consist of clayey tills and show a general thickness of approximately 15 m [59, 62].

Summary of ﬁndings
At all three sites dimethachlor metabolites were detected in the groundwater. The scope of

4.3
Deﬁning of sampling ﬁelds
The ﬁelds to be sampled should fulﬁl the two requirements of having been treated with dimetha-
chlor and being located in the catchment area of the Slimminge well ﬁeld. The latter one could
easily be checked with the model by Schøller (2018) [59] as shown in Figure 3.4. The ﬁrst re-
quirement was more difﬁcult to investigate because dimethachlor was used more than 25 years
ago (see Section 2.1.1) such that documentation of the application is rare.
In order to still identify relevant sampling ﬁelds, aerial photos from the area were examined
for visible rapeseed ﬁelds. The photos used for the investigation stem from the database jarweb
[77], which is run by the region of Zealand, and the website called ’Danmark set fra Luften’
[78]. In the beginning the focus was on pictures from 1980 - 1995 as in this time period the
use of dimethachlor can be expected (see Section 2.1.1). Unfortunately, most of the available
pictures were either black and white or taken before or after the rapeseed had bloomed in its
characteristically yellow, so that only few rape seed ﬁelds could be determined. On top of
that, the years for which pictures exist are quite limited. As oil rape seed has a suggested crop
rotation scheme of four years [79, 80], it is quite likely that many of the relevant ﬁelds were not
determined with only considering pictures from 1980 - 1995.
Therefore, also pictures from 1996 - 2021 were taken into account, on which several rape-
seed ﬁelds are clearly visible. Even tough dimethachlor was most likely not used in this period,
the pictures show the suitability of rapeseed cultivation on certain ﬁelds, increasing the likeli-
hood that it was also seeded there during the period of interest. Furthermore, by turning the
colored pictures of identiﬁed rape seed ﬁelds into black and white (bw), the grey shade could
be compared to the grey shades in the bw pictures from other years. For the year 1999 two
pictures exist one with blooming rapeseed ﬁelds and one taken in summer. After the bloom in
spring the rapeseed ﬁelds showed a distinctive bright green in summer. As many of the other
photos were not taken during the bloom, the bright green colour could be used to identify pos-
sible rapeseed ﬁelds instead of referring to yellow. Examples for this are shown in the Figures
4.1 - 4.3.
Lastly, information about the ﬁelds was added by identifying the ﬁeld owners with the
database jarweb [77]. Currently three point sources contaminated with dimethachlor in the
area are known. The pesticides which had been handled at the farms were most likely used on
the ﬁelds, so that ﬁnding the ﬁelds which belong to the point sources can help ﬁnding relevant
ﬁelds. All the used aerial pictures are included in the Digital Appendix.

4.5.1
Sample preparation
The soil samples from the hand-augering were prepared for the chemical analysis in weeks 46 -
48. First, the plastic bag was kneaded by hand, to achieve a ﬁrst homogenization. From this
a sub-sample from different areas in the bag was transferred into an aluminium tray, where
the soil was further homogenized by hand. Afterwards, parts of the homogenized soil was
placed in small plastic vials, which were closed and stored at 10 °C until all the soil samples
were homogenized. The remaining soil in the aluminium tray was transferred into a plastic
bag, closed with a zipper and stored at 10 °C, until possible further examinations such as the
grain size analysis.

washed with ethanol and dried with a paper towel. The aluminium tray was also cleaned with

4.5.2
Chemical analysis
The soil pesticide concentrations were measured by a SPC-LC-MSMS (Soil Phase Extraction
Liquid Chromotography Triple Quadruople Mass Spectrometry). The soil phase was extracted
from the dry soil with a water:methanol mixture and further details of the analysis is given in
Appendix C. The hand-augered samples were analyzed for dimethachlor, dimethachlor ESA,
dimethachlor OA, CGA 373464 and CGA 369873. Furthermore, the also the compounds DMSA,
DMST, tolyﬂuanid, DMS, DPC, MDPC, chloridazon and bentazon were analysed for. Due to
time limitations the soil core samples were only analyzed for dimethachlor.
4.6
Calculation method
4.6.1
Comparison of concentration at point sources and agricultural ﬁelds
A simple calculation was made to compare the order of magnitude of dimethachlor ﬁndings
on the farms to the ones from the agricultural ﬁelds. The goal is to estimate a worst-case soil
concentration on the ﬁeld, based on the results from the point sources. Under the conservative
assumptions that the degradation rate and sorption are the same on the ﬁeld and on the farm,
the difference between the two types of sources solely relies on the initial mass of dimethachlor
per area at the time of contamination. Further assumptions for this are the same inﬁltration
rate and that both locations have been contaminated at roughly the same time and for the same
number of times. By knowing the initial mass of dimethachlor per area for both types of sources
and the current soil concentration at the point source, the concentration at the diffuse source
can be estimated.
For the calculations the following estimations have been made. Teridox 500EC has a dimetha-
chlor concentration of 500 g/L and is sold in canisters of 5 L [81]. If a canister tipped over with
an open lit circa 1 - 2.5 L might have spilled. At the measurements conducted by WSP on site
1 [22], it can be seen that there are around 15 m distance between the hotspot well B2 and

To evaluate the impact of the different sources on the well ﬁeld, a mass balance between the

source and the ones entering the abstraction wells was estimated and compared to each other.

abstraction per year (m3/yr), Cwell is the concentration in the abstracted water (mg/L) and
CMDwell (g/yr) is the contaminant mass discharge for the whole well, so the mass of contami-
nant entering the well each year.
CMDwell = Q · Cwell
(4.2)
For the sources the vertical mass discharge was chosen to estimate the CMD and the concept is
shown in Figure 4.14. The inﬁltrating water is expected to vertically transport the contamina-

following assumptions:
• the contamination is located in the saturated zone
• the concentration and the contaminant mass discharge are constant over time
• the contaminants are not further degraded
• quasi steady state conditions have been reached
• the concentration is uniformly distributed in the deﬁned sub-areas around the boreholes.

Results and discussion
The results chapter is divided into four main sections. First, the soil concentration from the
point sources site 1 and site 2 are presented (there are no soil samples available from site 3).
The water (presented in Section 3.4) and soil samples are compared among each other and
among the sites. Afterwards, the agricultural ﬁelds around Slimminge are described. This
includes the past use of dimethachlor, geology and ﬁndings of pesticide residues on the ﬁelds,
followed by a discussion of the differences between the point sources on the farms and the
diffuse sources on the ﬁelds. Thereafter, the link between the different sources and the well
ﬁeld is made to evaluate which of the sources poses the biggest risk to the drinking water
quality in the area. Lastly, both a local and broader, more international perspective on the
relevance of dimethachlor is given, based on the results from the diffuse and point sources
examinations.

show contamination hotspots because the concentrations differed strongly among the bore-
holes. When comparing the three sites, the magnitude of the metabolite concentrations and the
depth with the highest contamination vary.
At site 1 and 3 the main part of dimethachlor metabolites appears to be present in the lower
fat clay and the limestone layer at around 14 - 21 mbgs. Samples from the shallow aquifer
showed none to very low concentrations of the degradation products at these point sources.
In contrast to this, at site 2 the main contamination was found in the shallow aquifer in the
clay layer (around 5 -9 mbgs). The highest concentration for dimethachlor OA at this site was
64 µg/L, which is nearly two times as high as the maximum concentration found at site 1
(35 µg/L). Furthermore, only at this site the mother compound was found with concentrations
reaching 1.19 µg/L. At the hotspot area around B2 and B8 at site 1 (see Figure 3.8) groundwater
samples were also taken in the shallow aquifer (borehole B10) showing low metabolite con-
centrations. Therefore hotspot areas at lower depth seem unlikely to have been missed at the
other sites. Also the geologies at the sites are very similar, that they cannot explain the contrast
between the sites.
Instead, the differences between the sites might be caused by more recent spills of dimetha-
chlor containing pesticides at site 2 compared to site 1 and 3. If the pesticide entered the ground
later, it would have had less time for the vertical transport to the deep aquifer. Additionally,
dimethachlor would have been exposed to the degrading conditions in the soil for shorter,
such that more of the original compound could be left. The same is true for the degradation
compounds, so that maybe not enough time has passed to reach similar concentrations as at
site 1 or 3. In Figures 5.4 and 5.5 the relative concentrations of the compounds in the hotspots at
site 1 and 2 are shown. The relative composition is noticeably different between the sites, with
for example dimethachlorcarbonyl sulfonic acid making up 8 % of the found compounds at site
1 but only 0.7 % at site 2. This observations supports the theory of a more recent spill at site
2 because dimethachlorcarbonyl sulfonic acid and CGA 369873 are degradation compounds
from dimethachlor ESA. More time can lead to more formation of these compounds and thus
enhance their relative concentration especially in regards to dimethachlor ESA.
On the other hand, the reduced dominance of dimethachlor OA can not fully be explained
with the current knowledge about the compounds. The mean half-lives for dimethachlor ESA
and dimethachlor OA suggest the latter one being slightly more persistent [13]. This however
would lead to an increasing relative concentration in regards to the compound dimethachlor
ESA, but the opposite is observed. At the supposedly older site 1 58.3 % of the mass found
in the hotspots is dimetachlor OA to 84.2 % at site 2. The reported half-lives were measured
under aerobic conditions, such that they are probably not representing the behaviour under
anaerobic conditions accurately. Furthermore, the observed half-lives of dimethachlor OA had
a very large range from 6.8 - 254.4 days [13], so that the metabolite could have been less per-
sistent at the conditions at the point sources. Lastly, the impact of the soil type on the detected
concentration could not be determined due to interactions with other factors such as the depth
and the location of hotspots.

compounds (see Section 2), it is likely that they are transported downwards into the aquifer as

the difference does not stem from the sample type but from which compound was measured.

surface as seen at site 2. At site 1 dimethachlor was found in the soil at borehole B6 and no
shallow water sample exists for this sampling spot. With a total of only three soil proﬁles for
two sites, having missed a possibly shallow or deeper laying hotspot is imaginable.

Another difference between the sources is the frequency of (possible) contamination. While
on the individual ﬁeld oil rape seed can only be planted with several years of break [79, 80]
dimethachlor might have been handled up to every year on the farm. Normally several ﬁelds
are cultivated by one farm, such that it is likely that at least one of them is planted with rape
seed every year. Therefore, dimethachlor was possibly handled at the same spots every year
at the farm, whereas the crop rotation on the ﬁelds led to years without input here. Therefore,
the soil on the farms is likely exposed to more frequent and higher concentrated dimethachlor
spills, which could be reﬂected by the higher soil concentrations.
Furthermore, the geology is different between the source types. While the ﬁrst layer at the
farms mainly consists of ﬁllings such as stone (see Section 3.4), the top layer of the ﬁelds is
an organic carbon rich plough layer. Dimethachlor will thus sorb more to the top layer on the
ﬁelds (see Section 2.1.2) and be transported vertically at the farm. Higher microbial activity and
aerobic conditions in the plough layer, will enhance the degradation at the agricultural ﬁeld.
A similar behavior was shown for alachlor, which has a lower degradation rate in the subsoil
than in the plough layer [33].
Also uptake by plants [13] could enhance the quicker dissipation of dimethachlor on the
ﬁeld. Furthermore, dimethachlor is non-volatile (see Section 2.1.2) that gaseous loss from either
of the sources after the spill or application is unlikely. Nevertheless, through spraying the
compound on the ﬁeld, potential more gaseous loss can happen in comparison to the point
sources. In earlier studies, dimethachlor has already been detected in air samples in [86].
Besides the differences mentioned above also a too low sample depth should be considered.
As mentioned above, dimethachlor sorbed to the organic fraction in the plough layer has the
highest likelihood of being degraded. The samples from the point sources furthermore showed
the highest concentration at the redox transition zone, which at the ﬁeld is most likely present
2.5 - 3.5 mbgs. There might have been insufﬁcient sampling as only two samples were taken at
this depth.

Dimethachlor OA
At the point sources dimethachlor OA was found frequently and at high concentrations. In
the abstraction wells however, it was either always below the detection limit or only present
at low concentrations. The concentrations in the well ﬁeld align with the few available studies
examining groundwater samples (see Section 2.3). The ﬁndings at the point sources however,
resemble more the results obtained from laboratory experiments or leaching studies, which
lasted a maximum of 2 years [13]. The difference between the point source and the abstraction
well is most likely explained by the transformation of dimethachlor OA. The metabolite can
56
==PAGE:69==

form non-extractable residues(NER), degrade to CO2 [39] and is very unlikely to sorb to soil [12,
13]. Unfortunately, there are knowledge gaps regarding the exact degradation pathways and
the composition and behaviour of the NERs, such that is unclear how exactly dimethachlor OA
is transformed between the point sources and abstraction wells.
Dimethachlor ESA and GCA 369873
Both at the point sources and in the abstraction wells dimethachlor ESA had a high detection
frequency and concentration. This aligns with the groundwater monitoring studies, in which
dimethachlor ESA and CGA 369873 seem to be the most common compounds. Interestingly,
dimethachlor ESA was concluded to be non-persistent in degradation experiments [13] but it
is still detected in the groundwater. Therefore, this metabolite might be much more persistent
than initially expected, especially because the behaviour under anaerobic conditions was not
examined yet. In Appendix E a simple calculation based on the known half-life is given, esti-
mating the concentration of dimethachlor ESA 30 years after a Teridox 500EC spill. The results
also show that the concentration is far below the detection limit of 0.01 µg/L such that the
known half-life may not depict the reality accurately.
Just as dimethachlor ESA, CGA 369873 is detected very frequently in both the point sources
and the abstraction wells. However, there is a substantial difference regarding the relative con-
centrations of these compounds. The ratio of the average concentrations of dimethachlor ESA
and CGA 369873 is 1.4 for the well ﬁeld, 8.08 for site 1 and even 48.6 for site 3. This shows a
stronger dominance of dimethachlor ESA at the point sources in comparison to the abstraction
wells. When expecting the degradation of dimethachlor ESA into CGA 369873 (see Figure 2.2)
between the point source and the abstraction well, a change of the ratio can be explained.
Dimethachlorcarbonyl sulfonic acid
Dimethachlorcarbonyl sulfonic acid is rarely mentioned in the literature and plays a minor role
in the abstraction well contamination. In three of the abstraction wells it has only been detected
once during the measurement campaign from WSP [22]. Also the ﬁndings in well 212.1389 are
rather low. In the point sources however, it is detected nearly as frequently as CGA 369873 and
often with higher concentrations, making it the third or fourth most relevant compound.
This discrepancy to the literature can be due the rare measurements of the compound in
the conducted studies. Furthermore, CGA 369873 is a product of dimethachlorcarbonyl sul-
fonic acid so that, depending on the respective degradation rates, a higher concentration of
dimethachlorcarbonyl sulfonic acid can be realistic. To fully undermine this theory, more infor-
mation have to be gathered or produced regarding the half-lives of those metabolites.
Dimethachlor
At site 2 also dimethachlor was detected, which is not present in neither the well ﬁeld nor
is it described in other groundwater monitoring studies. As explained in Section 3.4.4 the
detection at the site could be due a more recent contamination. Dimethachlor, which is non-
persistent, will most likely be transformed before it reaches the well ﬁeld. This is also plausible
57
==PAGE:70==

as all samples from deeper soil layers do not contain dimethachlor. The dimethachlor found
in the soil samples, can effect the water quality by desorption and by be being transformed
into metabolites. The newly formed degradation products are more mobile and are thus more
likely to be transported to the well than the mother compound. Furthermore, the metabolites
sorb less, which could explain why mainly dimethachlor was found in the soil samples.
SYN 528702, CGA 102935 and CA 42443
Lastly, more than 5 % of the initial dimethachlor was respectively transformed into CGA 102935
and SYN 528702 in degradation experiments [13]. SYN 528702 was frequently analyzed in the
point sources put only detected once with a concentration of 0.01 µg/L. It might therefore be
degraded quickly enough that it does not have an impact on the well ﬁeld contamination. CGA
102935 on the other hand was not analyzed, such that no conclusion about this metabolite is
possible. The compound CGA 42443, which is exclusively formed under anaerobic conditions
was neither analyzed at the point sources nor in the abstraction wells.
Summary
Summarizing, the highest concentration at the point sources are caused by dimethachlor OA
and dimethachlor ESA, while the dominant compounds in the abstraction wells are dimethachlor
ESA, dimethachlorcarbonyl sulfonic acid and CGA 369873. All in all, there are many aspects
aligning between the point source, the well ﬁeld and the information available in the litera-
ture. The biggest difference stems from the very high concentrations of dimethachlor OA at
the point source and the absence of it in the well ﬁeld, which might be due to transformation
of the compound. Furthermore, the difference of the relative importance of dimethachlor ESA
and CGA 369873 can be attributed to the conversion of dimethachlor ESA, whereby more in-
formation about persistence of these compounds are needed.

Discussion of the pesticide mass balance
The used calculation methods and assumptions might not accurately reﬂect the reality in well
catchment area. Firstly, the long transport time of several years (probably even decades) be-
tween the sites and the abstraction wells was not considered. The pollutants reaching the
abstraction well today will have leached from the source a long time ago. As the main con-
tamination at the site was found in deep soil layer, the pollution’s center of mass might have
already left the point sources. This center of mass might be reaching the abstraction wells to-
day, while the point sources represent lower concentrations. Therefore, the used method might
have underestimated the impact of the point source.
Furthermore, the rather simple method for calculating the mass discharge might be a good
ﬁrst estimate, but not detailed enough to accurately display the impact of the source on the
well ﬁeld. For site 1 for example, WSP calculated the horizontal mass discharge and arrived
at values of similar range to what was obtained by the vertical CMD in this study (WSP:
dimethachlor OA 5.59 g/yr, dimethachlor ESA 1.6 g/yr, total metabolites 8.75 g/yr). At ﬁrst
62
==PAGE:75==
Chapter 5. Results and discussion
glance, the magnitude of the vertical discharge method therefor seems to be in accordance.
However, when WSP calculated the horizontal mass discharge, three mayor hotspots at the
north at the site had not been discovered yet. In their calculations, the used concentration for
one part of the vertical transect was there likely too low. If the horizontal mass transfer would
be re-calculated including the information about the new hotspots, it will likely be larger. Thus,
it will have larger values than the ones obtained by the vertical CMD in this study, indicating
that the used method might underestimate the CMDs.
The biggest uncertainties of the method include the estimated area of the contamination
and the inﬁltration rate, which both could in- or decrease the estimated CMD value if chosen
slightly different. At site 1, one area area with a higher uncertainty is located north-west of the
farm, where only one borehole was taken (see Section 4.6.2). In general the results from site 3
are highly uncertain as only very few measurements were taken at this site.

Evaluation of the indicators for the Slimminge well ﬁeld
Five out of six indicators for point sources are (potentially) fulﬁlled for at least on of the abstrac-
tion wells in the area. One indicator is when abstraction wells in close proximity to each other
show different magnitudes in contaminant concentration. This is true for the abstraction wells
212.1349 and 212.1389, which are located at distance of 150 m. Here the average concentration
of dimethachlor ESA is 3.45 times higher in well 212.1389. In the same well, hexazinon was
detected once as the only mother compound in the well ﬁeld, which can also indicate a point
source. For this well and 212.1678 the data about the indicator ’High concentrations (at least
one >1 µg/L)’ were inconclusive because the concentrations for anionic detergents was only
given as a sum. Furthermore, it was difﬁcult to assess if the indicator ’Same compound varies
in concentration’ was true for the study area because not all compounds were measured in the
same frequency. In well 212.1389 dimethachlor ESA was under the detection limit of 0.01 µg/L
at the end of December of 2021 and at 0.16 µg/L only one and a half month before. On the
one hand, as this is a difference of more than a magnitude it could indicate the variance of the
compound’s concentration in the well. On the other hand, one point of reference is not enough
to proof the indicator.
With regards to the diffuse source indicators, three out of the four wells nearly always meet
the requirements for the indicator of ’low concentrations’. The remaining three indicators for
diffuse sources were not fulﬁlled or not applicable.
Summary and discussion of the inﬂuence from point and diffuse sources
Overall, more indicators imply point sources to the the origin of contaminating, but some of
the requirements are only fulﬁlled by single wells. Therefore, it might be more beneﬁcial to
consider the wells individually instead of assessing the overall well ﬁeld. Abstraction well
212.1734 should be excluded from this, as it is a rare horizontal well for which the method may
not be suitable. For well 212.1389, most of the indicators signal the contamination to stem from
point sources. For well 212.1349 and 212.1678 the results are more ambiguous, whereby the
indicators for 212.1678 hint slightly more towards point sources.
Summarizing, it seems likely that there are undeﬁned point sources in the area. Further-
more, the impact of known sites might currently be underestimated, due to the transport re-
lated time relay, the uncertain spill size, uncertain inﬁltration rate and limited data availability
especially for site 3. From the agricultural ﬁelds no contribution to the contamination could be
concluded. Limitations of the analysis however, might have hindered to estimate the impact
of the diffuse source. The sampled ﬁelds and depths might not represent the risk from the re-
maining ﬁelds accurately because the use dimethachlor use on these ﬁelds was likely but not
proven. Moreover, especially the deeper layers of soil might have been under sampled.
Furthermore, former laboratory experiments concluded a high percentage of dimethachlor
transforming into non-extractable residues [13], which are not be detectable with the used
chemical analysis. In general parts from non-extractable residues are potentially re-mobilizable
[41], such that these fraction might pose a relevant risk of contaminant leaching. Under which
conditions this can occur for dimethachlor and its metabolites is currently unknown. Therefore,

further work in the Slimminge catchment area and research about the behaviour of dimethachlor

5.4
Perspective
5.4.1
Further work in Slimminge
In the Slimminge well ﬁeld dimethachlor metabolites were the only detectable compounds
among 109 analyzed pesticides in the water outlet in 2022 [21]. They are thus one of the biggest
threats for the drinking water quality and the majority of their origin is still undeﬁned. The
most important aspect to understand the contamination of the well ﬁeld is therefore to gather
more information about the location of origin.
Point sources
Point sources seem to be the dominant source in the well ﬁeld, and it is therefore important
to ﬁnd potentially more of them and to reﬁne the knowledge about the known sources. Al-
ready applied tools like investigating the historical background, interviews, visits and screen-
ing studies can help to identify potential point sources in the area [16]. A starting point could
be abstraction well 212.1678, just north of the motorway. Currently no point sources are known
for this well, and the smaller catchment area (in comparison to well 212.1734) makes screening
easier. A ﬁrst investigation could include the area between abstraction well 212.1678 and ﬁeld
B (small sampling ﬁeld at the east, see Figure 5.9) and parts further southeast of this.
The catchment area of the horizontal abstraction well 212.1734 is much bigger than for the
remaining three abstraction wells, so that a ﬁrst screening of the area might be more com-
plex. On the other hand, there are many monitoring and closed wells especially in the area
between well 212.1734 and Slimminge as shown in Figure 5.9. An investigation for this well
could be started by taking water samples from the monitoring wells and analyzing them for
dimethachlor metabolites. As the wells already exist, the sampling will also be cost-efﬁcient.
Furthermore, more information about the pollution, especially for site 3, is needed to esti-
mate the impact for the well ﬁeld more accurately. As it is common practice, hotspots at the
sites may be located most efﬁciently, by ﬁrst detecting the areas on the farm where spraying
tools were washed. These are usually the most contaminated parts of a farm when it comes to
pesticides [16] and will most likely also be relevant for dimethachlor residues.
Mass balance and further models
For a more accurate mass balance, the estimated CMDs can be improved with more data from
the sites and a more accurate inﬁltration rate. Especially on site 3 more samples should be taken
as so far only four spots were analyzed. As the metabolites can be transformed into each other,
a mole based approach of the mass balance might be better. However, as the center of mass of
the plume might have already left the area of the point sources, even the reﬁned CMDs might
not accurately portray the point sources.
65
==PAGE:78==

Therefore, setting up further models, could help to better understand the importance of
each of the sources. First, the original amount of dimethachlor spilled at the sites could be es-
timated by calculating the concentration at the site over time after a spill. The most likely spill
scenario can be evaluated by modelling different scenarios and including sorption, desorption,
inﬁltration, degradation (for both dimethachlor and its metabolites) and comparing the results
to the current found concentrations on the site. The ﬁnal result should be checked for credibil-
ity, by comparing it to experiences of pesticide spills at farms (see for example Miljøstyrelsen
[16]) and by taking into account the amount of dimethachlor used at the farm, if the numbers
are known.
A ﬂow-and-solute-transport model would consider the transformation of the metabolites
better and thus me more accurate than the mass balance. The model should include disper-
sion, seepage velocity, sorption, desorption, degradation, and the estimated original spill(s), to
assess the mass discharge arriving at the abstraction wells today and in the future.
Remedial actions
If the updated and new models indicate an exceedance of the quality criteria in the abstrac-
tion wells, actions for protecting the drinking water need to be discussed. This could include
remediation for the most impacting point sources. Regarding the known sources, this could
especially affect site 2 because the contamination shows much higher concentrations at a shal-
lower depth, making remediation more accessible. Further possibilities to deal with and to
prevent too high pesticide concentrations in drinking water are discussed in Section 5.4.3
Diffuse sources
If the additional information about the point sources still cannot explain the magnitude of
contamination in the well ﬁeld, the impact of the agricultural ﬁelds should be examined again.
Here other ﬁelds on which dimethachlor use was likely, should be sampled. One good starting
point could be the ﬁeld directly to the east of well 212.1678 (see Figure 5.7) as in 1987 rape seed
was planted here.
Also for the diffuse sources more investigations such as interviews can be made, to know
if dimethachlor was used on the ﬁelds. Besides soil also water samples could be taken. With
the lower detection limit they might be more promising in detecting dimethachlor residues. At
the point sources, the highest dimethachlor concentration in the soil were detected around the
redox transition zone. New campaigns at agricultural ﬁelds should thus take more samples
from this depth as well.
If metabolites or dimethachlor are found on the ﬁelds, the mass balance should be up-
dated to include the CMD form the ﬁelds. As diffuse sources cover large areas, remediation
techniques like for point sources are not applicable. The drinking water quality would more
efﬁciently be improved by treatment after the abstraction. A further discussion about this is
given in Section 5.4.3.

Missing knowledge about the behaviour in the environment
For a complete understanding of the contamination at the Slimminge well ﬁeld, futher knowl-
edge about the behaviour of the metabolites in the soil is fundamental. This includes, the
degradation pathways of dimethachlor OA and dimethachlor ESA under anaerobic conditions.
Laboratory degradation experiments could help to close this knowledge gap. Furthermore, the
possible role of the non-extractable residues should be investigated. Many aspects about these
residues are unknown for example the re-mobilizable fraction, the persistence, the chemical
structure(s) and the desorption. After more information was gathered, they could be imple-
mented into the above named models.
Monitoring of further metabolites
Lastly, it could also be of interest to monitor the compound CGA 42443 at both the abstraction
wells and the point and diffuse sources. This compound is only formed under anaerobic con-
ditions and has currently not be included in any of the investigations. As large parts of the soil
are expected to be anaerobic, this compound may play a relevant role in assessing the contam-
ination in the catchment area and to potentially close the mass balance. In return, metabolites
which were never detected e.g. CGA 373464 could be excluded form the analysis if resources
need to be saved.
5.4.2
Signiﬁcance of chloroacetanilide herbicides in Denmark and abroad
Relevance of dimethachlor
The presence of dimethachlor metabolites in water abstraction wells is not only a problem in
Slimminge. In ﬁve other water works operated by HOFOR degradation products of dimetha-
chlor were already found in the water outlet in 2022 [71]. As rape seed was planted in all
regions of Denmark [83], there is a risk for other water works in the country to be faced with
a contamination originating from dimethachlor. To elaborate which wells might be affected,
the proximity to point sources, namely farms on which Teridox 500EC was used, should be
taken into account. To transfer the insights gained from the Slimminge well ﬁeld to other parts
of the county, the effect of different geologies on the transport of dimethachlor has yet to be
examined.
In other countries the use dimethachlor is still approved until the end of 2023 [87]. How-
ever, this must not necessarily lead to a much higher risk for the groundwater quality than
what is currently detected in Denmark. The main contributors for groundwater pollution with
dimethachlor metabolites seem to be point sources. These are located at farms, especially where
the spraying material was cleaned after use [16]. Since the 1980’ies stricter rules about the
cleaning procedure and pesticide handling have been implemented, such that this source of
contamination is much less likely today [80].
67
==PAGE:80==
Chapter 5. Results and discussion
Furthermore, the quality criteria for pesticide metabolites are in generally less strict in the
other EU member states [51]. The detection of dimethachlor metabolites in ground- and drink-
ing water samples, will therefore ﬁrst lead to actions at much higher concentrations. In Ger-
many for example, the quality criteria for dimethachlor ESA in groundwater is 30 times higher
than the allowed concentration in Denmark [54, 57].
Common characteristics among chloroacetanilide herbicides
Besides dimethachlor, also other chloroacetanilide herbicides can endanger the groundwater
quality. There are several common characteristics among chloroacetanilide herbicides. First
of all, many of them are applied pre-emergence [48, 50, 88, 89], which can increase the risk of
washing the pesticide into the subsurface due to higher inﬁltration in comparison to soil with
a plant cover. Furthermore, most of them are currently not approved in the EU [28, 48–50, 88–
90], such that the problems seen today stem from past applications. In the 1990’ies 1000 - 2000
ton/years of alachlor were applied in Western Europe and 500 - 1000 tons/year of metazachlor
and metolachlor [91]. A problem occurring with legacy pesticides is that ﬁnding relevant point
sources is more difﬁcult due to often lacking documentation of the use. Additionally, it might
be too late for remediation actions at the point source as the majority of the contamination
might already have entered deep soil layers or groundwater aquifers. Moreover, Denmark
faces the problem of acetochlor and metazachlor metabolites being found in groundwaters,
without the active substances ever having been approved in Denmark [15]. Locating point
sources will be much more difﬁcult as no ofﬁcial records of the substances will be held.
Another common issue are the many possible metabolites, which make it difﬁcult to mon-
itor the full extend of the contamination. Furthermore, little knowledge exists about the be-
haviour of especially the degradation products under anaerobic conditions. This makes it dif-
ﬁcult to model and estimate the impact that possible point or diffuse sources can have on the
groundwater quality.
Differences between chloroacetanilide herbicides
Even though the compounds are structurally very similar, they show slightly different be-
haviour in the environment, such that the knowledge of dimethachlor is not easily transfer-
able to other compounds. For example acetochlor, metolachlor and metazachlor are slightly
more persistent and at the same time less mobile than dimethachlor [12, 48, 88, 92]. There-
fore, it is difﬁcult to estimate whether they are more or less likely to be transported into the
groundwater based on their characteristics alone. In fact, acetochlor, alachlor, dimethenamid
and metolachlor have been detected in groundwater studies [7, 9, 15, 46]. For dimethachlor,
however, only the metabolites and not the mother compound were found in earlier studies and
in the abstraction wells in Slimminge.
Also, the metabolites of these compounds are highly relevant, in regards to groundwa-
ter contamination. The transformation into the OA (oxoacetic acid) or ESA (ethane sulfonic
acid) degradation products is possible for all chloroacetanilide herbicides [15]. Interestingly,
68
==PAGE:81==
Chapter 5. Results and discussion
the behaviour of the ESA metabolites can differ strongly between the chloroacetanilide herbi-
cides. Dimethachlor ESA for example is assessed to be non to moderately persistent (DT50=11 -
23 days), whereas metolachlor ESA and metazachlor ESA are much more persistent with half-
lives of up to 400 days. Regarding mobility however, dimethachlor ESA, metolachlor ESA and
metazachlor ESA behave similarly as they are all very mobile [13, 48, 92]. In the Slimminge
well ﬁeld dimethachlor ESA has a dominant role. As metolachlor ESA and metazachlor ESA
are even more persistent, it can be expected that they can be a risk for underlying groundwa-
ter aquifers, if the respective mother compound was used. In a wide groundwater screening
in the US, metolachlor ESA was detected in 11.4 % of the samples with concentrations up to
2.5 µg/L [7]. In Denmark, the compound was never approved for application [93], so the risk
of contamination is much lower for Danish groundwater.
Summary of the relevance of chloroacetanilide herbicides
Summarizing, chloroacetanilides and their metabolites are detected in groundwaters world
wide, proving a wide spread pollution. The compounds share common characteristics like
moderate mobility and low persistence in soil and a generally higher persistence for the ESA
metabolites. The monitoring and assessment of contaminations can be difﬁcult, due to the
many possible metabolites and the fact that the active substance was often used several years
or decades ago. Nevertheless, the surveillance in groundwaters is necessary, to correctly assess
the groundwater quality and ensure safe drinking water for the future and now.
5.4.3
Protecting drinking water from contamination in Denmark
The Slimminge well ﬁeld shows how legacy pesticides can endanger the groundwater and
therefore the drinking water quality. As these are not the only compounds putting the drinking
water at risk, the following part focuses on options, to ensure safe drinking water in the future.
Three main aspects are discussed which are
• protecting the drinking water source,
• treating the drinking water,
• and deﬁning new standards for water quality.
Protecting the drinking water source
In Denmark protecting the drinking water source means protecting the groundwater. One op-
tion are ground water protection zones around the abstraction wells, in which the use and
handling of pesticides and other possible contaminants are forbidden or strictly regulated. Dif-
ferent kinds of protection zones already exist in Denmark, with rules being stricter with de-
creasing distance to an abstraction well [94]. Problematic are persistent compounds. As they
can be transported in the groundwater over a large distance, they could lead to very large pro-
tection zones in which their use is forbidden. This can be seen in Slimminge, where some point
sources are located more than a kilometer away from the well ﬁeld.
69
==PAGE:82==
Chapter 5. Results and discussion
In general, and not only limited to the protection zones, the use of fewer and better pesti-
cides and chemicals can be beneﬁcial to protect different environmental compartments. Ideally
the compounds and their degradation products are not toxic (to non-target organisms) and not
persistent in both top and deep soil layers.
Furthermore, stricter rules about handling pesticides can avoid spills and thus help protect-
ing the groundwater. One positive example is the mandatory collection of washing water at
farms nowadays, instead of the uncontrolled seepage into the soil as it has been practice before
[80]. This prevents point sources from forming. If point sources are be identiﬁed of putting
drinking water abstraction wells into risk, the soil and groundwater at the source can poten-
tially be remediated. In general remediation of pesticide contaminated soil can be achieved
through contaminant-immobilization, separation or destruction [17], and a suitable technique
for the respective pesticide and conditions has to be found. In general, diffuse sources cannot
be treated in the same way because they affect a much larger area.
Furthermore, monitoring wells installed in the catchment area but in a distance to the actual
abstraction well, can help to indicate early signs of a shift in the groundwater quality. The
wells could also be of advantage to indicate were point sources are possibly located. However,
installing monitoring wells can be costly. Furthermore, in Denmark the monitoring list for
compounds in groundwater (national groundwater monitoring GRUMO) is slower updated
than in other countries e.g. Switzerland and England, where the list is revised regularly if
necessary [95].
Treatment of drinking water
Another option for securing clean drinking water are the drinking water treatment facilities. In
Denmark the groundwater is generally only aerated and ﬁltered before being distributed to the
consumers. In certain cases, also the use of e.g. active carbon ﬁlters, the use of chemicals and
UV light is allowed. [96] Installing additional treatment has the advantage of having a more
immediate impact, than e.g. the ban of certain chemicals as it as shown for the Slimminge well
ﬁeld. Furthermore, treatments with active carbon can remove several different pollutants at
once, whereas bans and clean-ups at point sources only target certain compounds. However,
metabolites of dimethachlor have very low sorption tendencies that activated carbon might
not be able to capture them and different technologies should be used for them. Additional
treatment steps will also increase the production cost of drinking water. Moreover, it treats the
symptoms but not the source of the problem.
Reﬁning quality standards for (drinking) water
Another option could be to revise quality criteria for different water uses. In Denmark, water of
drinking water quality is utilized for washing dishes, clothes and even for ﬂushing the toilet.
Revising the water quality for different water uses might lower the cost of producing large
amount of water with very high quality. If two water systems were installed in households, one
could contain high quality drinking water and the other water with high enough quality for the
remaining applications in the household. In newly planned housing areas, the buildings could
70
==PAGE:83==
Chapter 5. Results and discussion
e.g. use collected (and potentially slightly treated) rain water for activities requiring lower
quality water. As the water works would need to produce less drinking water, this could lower
the overall expenses of treatment and distribution.
Lastly, the quality criteria of drinking water could be altered. With the quality criteria of
0.1 µg/L for non-relevant metabolites, the regulation in Denmark is much stricter than in the
other European countries, in which for some cases even concentrations > 10 µg/L are permit-
ted. It could be discussed, if the for non-relevant metabolites the quality criteria should be
raised to higher allowable concentrations.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Recently, dimethachlor metabolites have been detected in the abstraction wells from the Slim-
minge well ﬁeld [64], which provides drinking water to parts of the Greater Copenhagen Area
[59]. The residues pose a risk for the drinking water, for which the quality criteria regarding
pesticides and their metabolites is set to 0.1 µg/L for individual compounds [57]. In Den-
mark, dimethachlor is a legacy pesticide because the compound’s approval stopped more than
30 years ago. In this work the impact on the Slimminge well ﬁeld of farms and agricultural
ﬁelds as potential long term sources for dimethachlor and its degradation products was as-
sessed.
Firstly, the expected behaviour the compounds in the environment was reviewed in the lit-
erature. Dimethachlor is mobile in the subsurface [12, 13], whereby sorption to organic matter
plays the most important role affecting the mobilty. The degradation is governed by microbial
activity and under most circumstances dimethachlor will be non-persistent [12, 13, 32]. The
few existing studies about the behaviour under anaerobic conditions point towards a lower
degradation rate [13, 36], but too little data is available for a sound conclusion. Dimethachlor
is of low to moderate toxicity for humans and highly toxic to aquatic organisms [13].
The mother compound can degrade into a variety of metabolites. Under laboratory exper-
iments the dominant fractions were dimethachlor OA, dimethachlor ESA and non-extractable
residues. Under anaerobic conditions also the compound CGA 42443 was formed [13, 39]. In
the few groundwater monitoring studies however, almost exclusively the detection of dimetha-
chlor ESA and CGA 369873 was reported. No studies could be found about dimethachlor
residues on soil samples. Dimethachlor ESA and dimethachlor OA are less toxic, more mo-
bile and slightly more persistent than the mother compound under aerobic conditions [12, 13,
44, 45]. There are knowledge gaps about the fate of most other degradation products in the
environment. Futhermore, there is too little available information about the behaviour under
anaerobic conditions for all compounds, to fully assess their behaviour in the environment.
Three point sources were already identiﬁed in the Slilmminge well ﬁeld and the available
data on geology, water concentrations and soil concentrations were analyzed and interpreted.
All three sites showed hotspots contaminated with dimethachlor metabolites. Most relevant in
the water samples were dimethachlor OA and dimethachlor ESA. The highest concentrations
were generally found in the lower fat clay layer just above the limestone aquifer in around
14 - 21 mbgs. In the soil samples mainly the mother compound was found and the detection
was limited to the upper 5 meters of soil. Due to the higher sorption tendency of dimethachlor
72
==PAGE:85==
Chapter 6. Conclusion
it is more likely to be found in the soil samples then the metabolites. The higher persistence
and mobility of the metabolites lead them to travel further into the ground than the mother
compound. Furthermore, as dimethachlor ESA was only detected in sand lenses, this could
indicate either a higher sorption tendency to sand than to clay or a better extraction from this
soil type. The pollution at site 2 most likely happened more recently which resulted in higher
concentrations, shallower depth for the contamination hotspot and the presence of the mother
compound dimethachlor in the water samples.
Relevant sampling ﬁelds were found by examining historical and more recent photos of
the area. Many of the ﬁelds around Slimminge are suitable for the cultivation of oil rape seed,
which was the target plant for dimethachlor. On the two sampled ﬁelds neither dimethachlor
nor the analyzed metabolites were detected in the upper 5 m of soil. In comparison to the point
sources, the ﬁelds were likely exposed to less frequent and lower concentrated dimethachlor
applications. Furthermore, the higher microbial activity and organic carbon fraction in the
topsoil could have lead to higher degradation rates, such that dimethachlor residues were not
detectable on the agricultural ﬁeld. The lack of residues could also be due to a too low sampling
depth or the uncertainty if dimethachlor was applied on the ﬁelds.
Lastly, the impact of the different sources on the well ﬁeld was evaluated by a simple
mass balance. Overall, the pesticide contaminant mass discharges leaving the point sources
were lower than the contaminant mass reaching the abstraction wells. In those wells, mainly
dimethachlor ESA and CGA 369873 are causing the contamination, while the sources release
primarily dimethachlor OA and dimethachlor ESA. The occurrence of CGA 369873 in the ab-
straction wells can be explained with the degradation of dimethachlor ESA [39]. Also dimethachlor
OA is expected to transform between the point source and the abstraction well e.g. into non-
extractable residues. The origin of more than 90% of the contaminant in the abstraction wells is
still unknown. Most likely, the pollution stems from other point sources, but with the current
knowledge also an inﬂuence of the agricultural ﬁelds or the underestimation of mass discharge
from the known point sources cannot be ruled out.
Future work should focus on getting more reﬁned information about the contamination at
the known point sources (especially site 3), implement a ﬂow-and-solute transport model in-
cluding sorption, desorption and degradation, and screening the area for more point sources.
The already existing monitoring wells in the area can be used for the screening of the area.
Furthermore, more research on the behaviour under anaerobic conditions and the relevance
of non-extractable residues is needed. The compound CGA 42443, which is only formed un-
der anaerobic conditions, has never been tested for and could therefore be included in future
analyses.
The metabolites and its degradation compounds are also contaminating the groundwater in
other Danish areas and European countries. As the quality criteria for the metabolites is much
higher in other countries, the contamination will likely not be taken as serious as in Denmark.
The case of the Slimminge well ﬁeld proves how legacy pesticides pose a risk to the drink-
ing water quality in Denmark. There are many possibilities for protecting the drinking water
source from protection and on of the most effective ones is to strictly regulate the compounds
approved for application.

Appendix A
Background
A.1
Use in Germany
Contrary to Denmark, dimethachlor is still approved for use in 15 countries of the European
Union (EU) such as Germany, France and Poland [12]. In Germany the compound was sold
as the part of the product BRASAN® for 20 years and as part of COLOZOR® TRIO since 2008
[25], of which the authorization ends in December 2023. The products COLOZOR® UNO and
COLOZOR® UNO FLEX solely contain dimethachlor and are authorized until the end of 2023
and 2022 respectively [87]. Unlike TERIDOX 500 EC, COLOZOR®UNO FLEX can be applied
both pre-emergence and early post-emergence for winter oil seed rape, at loads to maximum
1 kg/ha every third year [25].
A.2
Photolysis and hydrolosis of dimethachlor
Neither photolysis nor hydrolosis seem to be relevant for the degradation of dimethachlor.
[12, 13]. The aqueous hydrolysis half-life at 20°C and a pH of 7 is 200 days, which makes the
compound persistent against this type of degradation. The aqueous photolysis half-life could
not be determined because dimethachlor was stable (at pH 7) under these conditions. [12, 13]

Analysis of pesticides by SPE-LC-MSMS 
Mikael E. Olsson 
DTU Sustain 
January 2023 
Principle 
The pesticides (Appendix A) are analyzed by High Performance liquid Chromatography – Triple Quadrupole Mass 
Spectrometry (1290 Infinity II- 6470 LC-QQQ, Agilent Technologies). Soil samples are extracted with 
water:Methanol (50:50) followed by solid phase extraction. Water samples are analyzed directly.  
Solid phase extraction (SPE) 
5 g dry soil is extracted using 20 ml Methanol:Water (50:50) at room temperature for 24h. The extract is centrifuged 
at 3000g for 10 minutes and the supernatant is diluted with water to of 200 ml, resulting in a Methanol concentration 
of 5%. The extract is acidified to pH 3 using HCl prior to column loading. 
1g / 6ml ENVI-18 SPE columns (Supelco) are conditioned with 2 ml Methanol, 6 ml Ethyl acetate, 4 ml Methanol 
and 4 ml water. The sample is loaded at 10 ml / min and after loading the column is vacuum dried for 1h. Elution 
is achieved by 1 ml Ethyl acetate, 3 ml n-Hexane and 6 ml Methanol. The extract is evaporated under a gentle stream 
of Nitrogen to near dryness and reconstituted in 1 ml 10 mM Ammonium acetate. 
 
Chromatography 
Chromatographic separation is achieved on a 2.1 x 100 mm, 1.8 micron reversed phase column (Zorbax 
EclipsePlus C18  RR,  Agilent  Technologies). To analyze for all compounds two different chromatographic 
methods are used.  
 
For Group A 0.1% Formic acid is used as aqueous  phase  (A) and Acetonitrile with 0.1% Formic acid as 
mobile phase (B) using the following gradient:   100% A for 2. Min, then up to 70% A in 0.5 min which is held for 
2.5 min followed by a column regeneration time of 3 min. The flow is kept at 0.5ml/min and the column temperature 
at 35 °C. 5 ul sample is injected and the Multi-wash function is utilized. 
 
For Group B 10 mM Ammonium acetate with 0.1% Acetic acid is used as  aqueous  phase  (A) and 10 mM 
Ammonium acetate with 0.1% Acetic acid in 90% Methanol is as mobile phase (B) using the following gradient:   
99% A for 2.5 min, then up to 50% B in 0.5 min which is held for 5 min and then up to 100% B in 2 min followed 
by a column regeneration time of 6 min. The flow is kept at 0.5ml/min and the column temperature at 35 °C. 10 ul 
sample is injected and the Multi-wash function is utilized. 
 
Detection 
Detection is achieved in dynamic Multiple Reaction Monitoring (dMRM) with sheath gas at 400 °C at 12 l/min and 
the capillary voltage at 4500 V. For Group A the source temperature is 300 °C and 10 l/min nebulizer gas flow at 
35 psi and for Group B the source temperature is 290 °C and 13 l/min nebulizer gas flow at 40 psi. The polarity 
(ESI), collision energy voltage (CID), fragmentor voltage and cell acceleration voltage are optimized for each 
compound (Appendix A).

Appendix E
Results
In Table E.1 a simple degradation calculation is shown. It was calculated how high the con-
centration would be after 30 years if all of the dimethachlorpresent in Teridox 500EC was con-
verted into dimtheachlor ESA. A ﬁrst order degradation rate was assumed with the values
presented in the EFSA study [EFSA]. From the results of the calculation it can be seen that the
remaining dimethachlor ESA would be far below the detection limit. On the one hand, as this
compound was found at point sources, where the pollution would also be exposed to dilution
by inﬁltrating water, it is possible that the real half-life should be higher. On the other hand
dimethachlor will not be transformed instantly into dimethachlor ESA, so that the residues of
dimethachlor ESA found today, might have been formed just in the last couple of years from
remaining dimethachlor residues in the soil.
