Abstract 
 
Currently, polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA) production at large scale is unsustainable due to the 
high cost of substrates required for pure culture conditions. Open cultures methods utilizing 
mixed microbial consortia (MMC) are being advanced, allowing for integration of multiple 
sources for biomass (Kourmentza et al., 2017). At the same time, wastewater treatment plants 
(WWTPs) are generating large amount of waste activated sludge (WAS) which gets 
incinerated and/or applied to agricultural soil with very high associated costs (Dagerskog et 
al., 2020). Instead, WAS can offer a stable supply of bacterial biomass for PHA accumulation, 
in cases where streams rich in volatile fatty acids (VFAs) are locally available (Werker et al., 
2022). The main constraint in its optimization arises from the inherent degree of enrichment in 
PHA accumulators that varies with the source (Pei et al., 2022a). 
 
In this frame, this study investigated the potential of WAS for direct accumulation using 
mixtures of acetic acid (HAc) and butyric acid (HBu) as feedstocks. HBu was used as model 
substrate against acetate to evaluate the hypothesis of a lower oxygen yield on substrate and 
its effect on the accumulation. First, a set of pulse respiration response experiments was 
conducted to estimate an average oxygen yield on substrate (Yos) for different mixtures. Then 
selected mixtures were applied for accumulation experiments under nutrient limitation.  
The respiration response of the biomass was estimated from serial feast-famine pulses and 
the observed Yos was proven to be related to pure polymer storage. Pure HBu exhibited the 
lowest Yos among all mixtures (0.18-0.20 mgO2/mgCOD) and HAc the highest (0.30-0.33 
mgO2/mgCOD). Mixtures with HBu between 20% and 70% showed a systematic decrease in 
Yos compared to HAc within a range of 0.23-0.26 mgO2/mgCOD. Overall HBu always 
exhibited the lowest energy requirements for PHB synthesis.   
In the accumulation, increased PHB yield, PHB content and productivity was observed for 
cases were HBu was utilized, in contrast to an inferior performance in pure HAc. A maximum 
PHB content of 0.62 gPHB/gVSS was achieved with HBu, compared to 0.56 gPHB/gVSS in 
the 50%mixture and only 0.45 gPHB/gVSS in HAc. In the presence of HBu, always significant 
active biomass growth was detected in the system, and it was assumed to be directly related 
with the increased PHA yields and contents. In the case of mixture active growth was promoted 
but at a lower extent.  It was concluded that under the conditions examined, lower energy 
requirements for PHB synthesis had a systematic positive effect on PHB accumulation 
possibly promoting selective growth of PHB accumulators. Further experiments were 
considered necessary to adequately support these observations.

Introduction 
1.2 
Polyhydrxoyalkanoates (PHA)  
Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) are microbial storage polyesters accumulated naturally by 
different groups of bacteria as an intracellular reserve of carbon and energy under imbalanced 
growth conditions (van Loosdrecht et al., 1997). In the presence of an external substrate 
source microorganisms can either utilize it for growth or divert it to intracellular storage. 
Accumulation is preferably enhanced under conditions of alternating presence or absence of 
a carbon source, also known as dynamic conditions or feast-famine.  As a general mechanism, 
microorganisms respond to this regime by accumulating PHA when an excess of carbon is 
present (feast) and then utilize it upon the depletion of substrate (famine)(van Loosdrecht, 
1997). At the same time, microorganisms that can accumulate PHA increase relatively to other 
microbial populations that lack this ability. The degree of enrichment (DE) of a mixed microbial 
biomass, is often used to describe the fraction of those PHA-storing bacteria (PHA 
accumulators) over the total microbial population therein (Pei et al., 2022a). Besides the 
carbon source, nutrient limitation (nitrogen, phosphorus) and the presence/absence of an 
electron acceptor can trigger similar mechanisms (Dias et al., 2006; Li et al., 2016).  
Polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB) is the most found and investigated PHA and is a homopolymer of 
poly-3-hydroxybutyrate P(3(HB)). Other typical examples include the poly-(3-hydroxyvalerate) 
PHV and the co-polymer poly(3-hydroxybutyrate-co-3-hydroxyvalerate) PHBV. Depending on 
the available carbon source and the metabolic pathways implemented by the bacteria for PHA 
synthesis, different monomers are formed. Acetate and butyrate used either as sole substrates 
or mixtures only produce the 3(HB) monomers (Lemos et al., 2008). Up to date, around 150 
different PHA monomers have been identified suggesting the possible combinations and 
different types of polyesters than can be formed (Rhem et al., 2003).  
 
1.3 
PHA production 
Currently, commercially available PHAs are produced by pure culture methods (Kourmentza 
et al., 2017). These are conducted with defined bacterial strains (isolates, engineered strains) 
and high purity substrates, thus demanding sterile conditions to prevent contamination 
(Estévez-Alonso et al., 2021a). In 2023, around 105 kt of PHA were produced globally, mainly 
segmented among packaging materials, agriculture and horticulture, coating and consumer 
goods applications (Ishimwe, 2023).  
PHA production by pure cultures is unsustainable due to the high cost of raw materials and 
maintenance of aseptic conditions, lower yields, susceptibility to degradation of resulted 
polymers and difficulty of extraction. An additional limitation is the high price of the marketable 
biopolymers (2.2 -5.5 €/kg) compared to the price of petrochemical plastics (e.g. less than 1 
€/kg) (Sabapathy et al., 2020). However, due to their biodegradable and renewable nature, 
PHAs are becoming increasingly important towards the substitution of conventional plastics 
and minimization of CO2 emissions. To overcome these challenges, new methodologies 
focusing on the utilization of a wider range of substrates for accumulation and open culture 
approaches using mixed microbial consortia (MMC) are being developed (Morgan-Sagastume 
et al., 2016; Kourmentza et al., 2017).

1.4 
Accumulation with MMC 
MMC strategies utilize consortia of microorganisms of unknown composition and subject them 
to dynamic feeding conditions with volatile fatty acid (VFA)-rich substrates (Werker et al., 
2022). By engineering specific bioprocess conditions, PHA accumulators that are naturally 
occurring in the MMC can be selectively favored relatively to non-PHA accumulating biomass 
(Albuquerque et al. 2011). 
Up to date PHA production with MMC is only performed at pilot scale with two dominant 
technological approaches, namely enrichment accumulation or direct accumulation (Estévez-
Alonso et al., 2021). Enrichment accumulation involves the production of a highly functional 
biomass (enrichment) in a SBR and the subsequent accumulation in a separate reactor. 
Accumulation takes place by implementing a selected feeding regime to the previously 
enriched MMC under growth limiting conditions to maximize the biomass PHA cellular content. 
The types of starting inoculum include microbial communities of various sources, such as lake 
sediments (Mohammed et al., 2022), activated sludge (Sakai et al., 2014; Wen et al., 2010; 
Pei et al., 2022; Estévez-Alonso et al., 2021), or isolated strains (Chaundry et al., 2010; Matias 
et al., 2009). Enrichment accumulation typically results in high PHA content (0.37-0.89 
gPHA/gVSS) due to the higher degree of enrichment of the starting biomass (Valentino et al., 
2018; Albuquerque et al., 2011; Johnson et al. 2009a; Marang et al., 2013; Jiang et al., 2011; 
Carvalho et al., 2022). On the other hand, direct accumulation relies on the inherent PHA 
storage capacity of the biomass source by directly subjecting it to accumulation without any 
enrichment. Generally, it provides lower PHA content (0.2-0.5 gPHA/gVSS) (Novelli et al., 
2021). 
Some cases in literature have achieved improved performance for direct accumulation using 
WAS. Cavaillé et al., (2013) reported PHA contents up to 0.57 gPHB/gVSS by applying 
phosphorus limiting conditions. Estévez-Alonso et al. (2021) and Pei et al. (2022a) reported 
PHA contents in the range 0.60-0.64 gPHA/gVSS using various WAS sources and/or addition 
of Ca(OH)2 proving that the PHA accumulation potential of WAS can be improved under the 
applied  conditions. These indicate that direct accumulation performance has the potential to 
achieve comparable results to enrichment strategies, if specific bioprocess conditions are 
accordingly implemented. This holds significant relevance for industrial applications and 
requires further investigation.  
Moreover, MMC can be made to accumulate PHA using feedstocks rich in volatile fatty acids 
(VFAs).  Besides synthetic VFA solutions, industrial streams can be a source of readily 
available chemical oxygen demand (COD) for the same cause. The acidogenic fermentation 
of industrial waste feedstocks transforms the soluble organic content in non-defined volatile 
fatty acid (VFA) mixtures (including acetate, butyrate, valerate, propionate) which can be 
available on-site for accumulation (Sekoai et al., 2023; Werker et al., 2022). VFA-rich 
feedstocks have been investigated as substrates for PHA accumulation from activated sludge 
among which sugar cane molasses, brewery wastewaters, candy factory effluents, paper mill 
and olive mill effluents and the organic fraction of municipal solid waste (Korkakaki et al., 2016; 
Kourmetntza et al., 2015; Dias et al., 2008, Albuquerque et al., 2007b;).

A successful accumulation strategy should aim to create a selective environment that can 
maximize the PHA productivity and final PHA content by simultaneously minimizing any

inhibitory effects (Ahmadi et al., 2020; Serafim et al., 2008). Inhibition can be related to the 
presence of salts, high concentrations of nutrients and/or substrates (as VFA) (Korkakaki et 
al., 2016). Among the operational parameters investigated in literature, the substrate 
composition, concentration and feeding strategy together with nutrient availability have been 
proven to affect the PHA productivities in both direct and enrichment accumulation processes 
in pilot and lab scale (Serafim et al. 2004; Chen et al., 2015; Estévez Alonso et al., 2021; Wen 
et al., 2010; Valentino et al., 2015; Bhalerao et al., 2020). 
Different substrate feeding regimes involve the addition of substrate in batch, fed-batch, 
continuous or dynamic feeding mode to sustain microbial growth and optimize product 
formation. In fed-batch strategies, the substrate is fed in periodic intervals by a pulse-wise 
regime. The addition of substrate is commonly based on the dissolved concentration DO which 
requires monitoring throughout the whole accumulation process. A typical supply in MMC is 
implemented using very concentrated synthetic VFA mixtures to avoid radical volume changes 
within the reactors (Kourmentza et al., 2017). Although this cannot be attained in real 
fermentation effluents due to their diluted nature, fed-batch strategies have been proven 
efficient in lab-scale MMC accumulation with high productivities and PHA yields even 
comparable to pure cultures. That was even proven for cases where WAS was the starting 
inoculum for either enrichment or direct accumulation. Serafim et al., (2004) by applying 3 
pulses of 1.9 gCODHAc/L in enrichment accumulation resulted in 0.78 gPHA/gVSS. Estévez-
Alonso et al. (2022) provided PHA content up to 0.42 gPHA/gVSS after 142 accumulation 
feast-famine pulses on WAS targeting a maximum substrate level of 150 mgCOD/L when a 
typical range of continuous feeding could only provide a maximum of 0.30 gPHA/gVSS. 
Specific process conditions have been reported to systematically improve the accumulation 
performance for WAS. A novel strategy was suggested by Morgan-Sagastume et al. (2017) 
proving that the integration of a pre-treatment step prior to accumulation by three feast-famine 
(acclimation pulses) could improve the PHA accumulation potential of a WAS biomass by over 
50%. Similar observations were also made by Bengtsson et al (2017), recommending that 
implementing 3-4 aerobic feast-famine acclimation pulses on a VFA-rich substrate can provide 
a physiological adaptation of the biomass. This adaptation was always coupled with increased 
specific substrate uptake rates, PHA productivity and PHA storage during the subsequent 
accumulation. Also, Estévez-Alonso et al. (2021) suggested a standard protocol for direct 
accumulation using WAS, by comparing continuous vs pulse-wise feeding. Their findings 
indicated that the combination of an acclimation step with pulse wise feeding resulted always 
in the highest PHA content and that excess presence of substrate, triggered respiration on 
stored polymer thus resulting in lower final PHA contents. Considering these improvements, it 
is possible that direct accumulation can be further optimized to reproducibly provide increased 
PHA yields.  
 
1.6 
Butyrate as substrate 
Butyrate is a very promising substrate for PHA production because it has higher theoretical 
product yield compared to acetate, 0.60 gCODPHA/gCODHBu vs 0.43 gCODPHA/gCODHAc (Shi 
et al. (1997). This is mainly supported by the metabolic pathway of butyrate transformation to 
PHB that requires less ATP per C-mol than acetate as shown in Table 1.1 (Marang et al., 2013; 
Shi et al., 1997). Moreover, it is a major constituent of multiple industrial feedstocks and 
acetogenic fermentation broths occasionally comprising up to 44% and 50% of the total VFA 
therein (Marang et al., 2013; Johnson et al., 2009; Liu et al., 2013; Battista et al., 2022). These 
characteristics suggest that by valorizing industrial streams even with a lower fraction of

1.7  
PHB biosynthesis 
PHB can be directly formed from the central metabolite acetyl-CoA (van Loosdrecht, 1997). 
The principal mechanism driving the biosynthesis of PHB in a wide range of studied gram-
positive and gram-negative bacteria involves 3 sequential enzymatic reactions (Figure 1.2). 
Initially, 2 acetyl-CoA units are reversibly condensed to acetoacetyl-CoA by a b-ketothiolase 
(phaA) (1). An acetoacetyl-CoA reductase (phaB) then catalyzes the reduction of acetoacetyl-
coA to R-3-hydroxybutyryl-CoA using NADH reducing power (NADHàNAD+) (2). PHB is 
synthesized in a last step through polymerization of R-3-hydroxybutyryl-CoA by a PHB 
synthase (3) (Shrivastav et al., 2013; McAdam et al., 2020; Shi et al., 1997). This pathway is 
suggested for most fatty acid substrates that are first transported into the cells and 
metabolized through beta-oxidation mechanism.

The energy required, expressed as ATP equivalents, for the conversion of each VFA to PHA 
vary with the composition of the substrate and the number of carbon atoms thereof. Acetate 
is directly activated to acetyl-CoA (van Leeuwen et al., 1997; Wang et al., 2017; Marang et 
al.,2013; Johnson et al., 2009b) while butyrate, can be directly activated to hydroxybutyryl-
CoA and then polymerized to PHB (Dias et al., 2006).  
For PHB synthesis, 1 mol of VFA consumes 2 mol of ATP, one for active transport of the 
substrate into the cell and one for its conversion to acetyl-CoA (Dias et al., 2008).  However, 
the per-mol equivalence suggest lower ATP requirements for butyrate, specifically 0.75 mol 
ATP/cmolHBu compared to 1 ATP/cmolHAc in the case of acetate (Table 2.) This also applies 
for VFAs with a higher number of carbon atoms. This implies that lower energy is required to 
produce one cmol of PHA by VFAs with longer carbon chains (Wang et al., 2017). The 
activation of any VFA substrate to acetyl-CoA produces NADH2 which is subsequently 
consumed in oxidative phosphorylation for ATP generation. PHB is considered a NADH2 sink 
as its production requires NADH2 (van Leeuwen et al., 1997). If the fraction ATP required for 
growth is limited (as during feast-famine) then ATP is expected to accumulate, resulting in 
accumulation of NADH2. Eventually that will accelerate biosynthesis of PHB, providing a 
possible link between the reducing power requirements and PHB production (Reis et al., 
2003).

Although most substrates share the same metabolic pathways for biosynthesis of PHB, 
alternatives can dominate for certain microorganisms and for substrates mixtures (Reis et al., 
2013; Shi et al., 1997). Shi et al. (1997) using Alcaligenes eutrophus as model microorganism 
noticed higher flux distributions to PHA synthesis than to the TCA cycle when butyrate and 
acetate were present together in the feedstock. They calculated a higher PHB yield on butyrate 
than on acetate (0.60 gCODPHA/gCODHBu vs 0.43 gCODPHA/gCODHAc) concluding that butyrate 
is more efficient to produce PHB from an energetic point of view. Marang et al. (2013) also 
proved that butyrate was preferably taken up by microbial enrichment cultures for PHA 
production resulting in higher substrate uptake rate and PHA yield compared to acetate. 
Besides the energy requirements, potential synergetic substrate uptake patterns could be of 
relevance for PHA accumulation conducted with VFA mixtures (Reis et al., 2003).  
Mixtures with up to 4 different VFAs have been studied as substrates for accumulation and 
metabolic models have been established to follow the different VFAs uptake rates. However, 
little research has attempted to establish a link between the energy requirements for the 
substrate assimilation and the accumulation potential. Recent work from Estévez-Alonso et 
al. (2022) and Xing et al. (2023) suggested that creating conditions for reduced oxygen yield

on substrate (Yos) combined with nutrients limitation can promote the selective growth of PHA 
storing microorganisms in direct accumulation of WAS. However, they only studied the 
independent effect of either acetate, or butyrate or salts (KOH, Ca(OH)2) as triggers for the

COD will be utilized for anabolism and another will be oxidized to produce energy.  This latter 
is expressed as oxygen yield on substrate, directly related to the oxygen requirements for the 
full substrate oxidation.  
Based on the reported metabolic pathways and stoichiometry of butyrate activation it can be 
assumed that butyrate can potentially be metabolized with lower oxygen requirements and 
simultaneously enhance more selective growth of PHA accumulators. However, this requires 
further investigation with mixtures of acetate and butyrate and simultaneous observation of 
the resulting Yos and active growth occurrence. In this frame, a systematic evaluation of 
butyrate and acetate is suggested as a first step towards a better understanding of the 
connection between enhanced PHA accumulation and energy requirements for PHA 
synthesis. 
 
 
Aim of the project 
The aim of this thesis is to identify and describe the operational strategies needed by PHA 
accumulators to grow competitively and with concurrent PHA storing activity in direct 
accumulation process. The threshold to achieve this metabolic condition for combined growth 
and storage response of PHA accumulators will be defined, to support that WAS can be 
directly used for PHA accumulation.    
 
 
Hypothesis 
Literature findings indicate that that the yield of oxygen on substrate for PHA accumulation is 
0.19 mgO2/mgCODHBu for butyrate and 0.25 mgO2/mgCODHAc for acetate (Marang et al., 
2013). When a substrate is taken up by PHA accumulators it may be interpreted that fraction 
of it (Yxs) is utilized for biomass growth (represented by both PHA and active biomass) and 
another fraction (Yos) is directed to energy production with O2 consumption. This latter is the 
Yos that represents the total amount of oxygen required for the full substrate oxidation. If a 
lower fraction is going to energy production, then a higher flux is expected to be directed to 
biomass growth. Thus, the following hypothesis can be formulated:  
If the Yos on acetate does not represent a condition for selective growth, while Yos for butyrate 
does, then there is a threshold, given a mixture of butyrate and acetate, where a sufficient 
condition is reached to support selective growth of PHA accumulators in WAS.

2 Materials & methods 
2.1 
Experimental setup 
All experiments were performed in the same setup comprised of two parallel 1-L double- 
jacketed glass fed-batch reactors. The airflow was set at 5.5 L/min (MV-302, Bronkhorst, 
Germany) and kept constant throughout all experimental duration. Agitation was fixed at 100 
rpm by standard three-bladed turbine (R60, CAT Scientific, Germany). Reactors’ temperature 
was maintained at 25.0 ± 0.05 oC by circulating water bath (DC 10/P14, Thermo scientific). 
Dissolved oxygen (DO), pH and temperature measurements were taken every 10 seconds, 
by online probes (COS81D, CPS11D, XXXM Endress & Hausser, The Netherlands) connected

2.2.1 Respiration response experiments (RR) 
RR were conducted to estimate an oxygen yield on substrate, Yos, from the different substrate 
mixtures. In each experiment, a total of 14 feast- famine pulses were applied. Feast conditions 
were generated with a pulse input to reach a maximum substrate level of 150 mgCOD/L, and 
the duration of the feast was monitored by changes in respiration based on dissolved oxygen 
trends. The famine period was dynamically adjusted to be four times longer than each 
respective feast time. The duration of each feast/famine cycle ranged from 1 to 3 h. In each 
experiment, thickened sludge was diluted with tap water to nominally 2 gVSS/L and was left 
to aerate overnight for 10 hours to establish a baseline for background endogenous 
respiration. Nitrification was inhibited by the addition of 40 mg/L allylthiourea (ATU).  
To establish a baseline for comparison among experiments conducted at different dates and 
with varying sludge age, the three first feast-famine cycles were always applied with HAc as 
substrate. After the end of the three feast-famine cycles on HAc, the feedstock was shifted 
from HAc to the selected HAc/HBu mixture as listed in Table 2.1 and the system was left to 
run for additional 11 pulses on the new substrate. The first pulse after change of substrate was 
always excluded from the data treatment to avoid misinterpretation of noise and signal 
disturbances. The oxygen yield on substrate, Yos (mgO2/mgCOD) was determined per pulse 
for all reference and subsequent pulses to extract average values and relative trends. Only 
the pulses that showcased a relatively stable Yos, SSUR and SOUR profile were considered 
for the average calculation. 
Each time, before starting the feast-famine cycles, a gas out-gas in test was conducted in both 
reactors to determine the endogenous respiration rate (rend) and the oxygen volumetric mass 
transfer coefficient, 𝐾!𝑎 (min-1) according to the methodology described by Tribe et al. (1995). 
More specifically aeriation was switched off (gas out) and the DO concentration was monitored 
for a short period of time (5 min) until it reached 0.5 mg/L ensuring not exceeding the critical 
DO concentration. The aeriation was subsequently restarted, and the system was left to 
equilibrate at the initial DO level (about 8 mgO2/L). The variation with time of the DO during 
the gas out period was used to estimate the specific microbial oxygen uptake rate 
(mgO2/L/min/gXa) (level of endogenous respiration), while the re-aeriation data during the 
gas-in period were used to determine 𝐾!𝑎. 𝐾!𝑎 was unique for each reactor and for each 
experiment. It was determined per pulse and the average value of all pulses and for each run 
were presented and used for the analysis (further details in paragraph 2.3)

A total of 2 PHB accumulation experiments were conducted to evaluate the effect of lower Yos

2.3 
 Analytical techniques 
During all RR and PHB accumulation experiments, mixed liquor biomass was sampled at 
selected time points to follow biomass growth, storage compounds, nutrients, and soluble 
COD (sCOD).  
Two 12 ml grab samples were collected at each selected time points in 15 ml falcon tubes, 
one serving for VSS/TSS and one for polymer characterization. 12ml of mixed liquor were 
directly acidified to pH 2 by the addition of 200 μL 3M HCl and was subjected to thorough 
mixing by vortex (4 min). Both samples were centrifuged at 4,750 rpm at 4oC for 10 minutes 
for suspended solids separation. The harvested settled biomass pellet of the non-acidified 
sample was transferred to pre-weighted ceramic crucibles and was used for total solids and 
ash determination according to standard methods (drying at 105 oC and ashing at 550 oC). The 
acidified settled biomass was oven dried at 105 oC after discarding the supernatant. The dried 
biomass pellet was ground by hand using a mortar and pestle and was stored until further 
polymer analysis.  
The supernatant from the non-acidified samples was immediately filtered with syringe filter 
(pore size 0.45 μm, EPTF, Millipore, Ireland) to remove the biomass and was stored at 4 oC 
until further liquid analysis. HAc and HBu concentrations were determined by ultra-high 
pressure liquid chromatography, UHPLC (Dionex Ultimate 3000R, Thermofischer Scientific 
with Phenomenex Rezex organic acid H+ column, 300x7.8 mm), ammonium and phosphate 
concentrations were determined by ion chromatography, IC (Metrohm Compact IC Flex 930, 
Metrohm, Switzerlands) and sCOD by the LCK314 Hach-Lange (Germany) test kits. 
PHB content was estimated as gPHB/gTS and gPHB/gVS with respect to total solids and 
volatile solids respectively by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA2, Mettler- Toledo) as described 
in Chan et al. (2017). The method determines sample residual moisture, PHB contents 
alongside organic and inorganic fractions.  More specifically, representative pre-weighed 
samples of ground dried biomass (3-5mg) were inserted to the TGA (80 oC) followed by purging 
with nitrogen gas (50 ml/min). The temperature was increased to 105 oC (10oC/min) and 
retained for 15 min whereof moisture could be determined. Temperature was then increased 
to 550 oC and retained for 30 min. The polymer mass was estimated by the rapid mass loss 
between 225 oC and 350oC which is characteristic for PHB. At 550 oC the purge gas was 
changed to air and ash content was determined by the weight loss at 550 oC with air 
atmosphere. The PHB content of the biomass expressed as weight percentage PHB of VSS 
(%gPHB/gVSS). The determined PHB content was subtracted from the VSS to obtain the 
active biomass (Xa) inside the reactor.

affect the microbial community and their functions (Pala-Özkök et al., 2012). The use of a 
younger sludge might have a different microbial composition resulting in variations in Yos, as 
different microbial consortia can dominate therein and hence, respond to the substrate input 
differently (Reis et al., 2003). The performance of all RR in relation to the reference HAc pulses 
is presented in Figure 3.1.3.

could provide same average Yos on HAc. This was confirmed for SSUR and at a smaller 
degree for SOUR of the reference pulses as presented in paragraph 3.1.4. That was

This variation in the Yos resulting from HAc injections likely stemmed from the complex 
interplay of microbial dynamics, adaptation, completion and environmental factors within the 
mixed sludge biomass. In complex biological systems like in wastewater treatment processes, 
the variations in Yos on HAc can be linked to complex interactions within the mixed microbial 
community that is responsible for HAc but also any other substrate utilization (Wang et al., 
2017, Pala-Özkök et al., 2012). Microorganisms are highly adaptable and can acclimate to 
dynamic environmental conditions such as substrate availability. It is possible that during the 
initial HAc pulses the microbial community was adapting to the immediate influx of HAc leading 
to lower Yos. Considering that all experiments were conducted with MMC, the sludge biomass 
consists of very diverse microbial populations that have not been thoroughly examined for the 
purposes of this study.  
 
Overall, a systematic decrease in the Yos was observed with the presence of HBu. The Yos 
on HAc generally fluctuated in a range that was always higher than the rest of the substrate 
mixtures. The Yos on HBu regardless of the sludge age was always the lowest reported among 
all experiments, The Yos represents the cumulative amount of oxygen that is consumed for 
the full oxidation of a substrate. It is an indicator of the rate of ATP release per carbon mol of 
substrate. The rate of ATP release is affected by the metabolic pathways involved in the 
substrate utilization, such as beta-oxidation, TCA cycle, electron transport chain and the 
oxidative phosphorylation (Abbott et al., 1973).  ATP is utilized for maintenance and anabolic 
processes such as growth and/or polymer storage. A lower value of Yos, as in the case of pure 
HBu, indicated that a lower amount of substrate is respired to CO2 resulting in a higher residual 
fraction of COD (1-Yos) available for anabolic activities. For all cases, no active growth (Xa) 
was detected during the RR and Xa was maintained at approximately 2 gXa/L (Table 3.1.2, 
Appendix A). This suggested that during the feast, the substrate was mainly used for PHB 
storage. Stored PHB was subsequently consumed during the famine phase. No contribution 
to the Yos was expected by active growth or respiration on stored polymer. PHB accumulation 
was low up to  pulse 6 (0.05 gPHB/gVSS) and gradually increased by the end of the trials (up 
to 0.55 gPHB/gVSS) in all experiments (Table 3.1.2, Appendix A). This increase in PHB did 
not cause any proportional trend in Yos at any experiment. This indication verified the 
assumption that during these RR experiments, endogenous respiration was constant and that 
the determined Yos is only related to the respiration on the external added substrate.  
The Yos on HAc and/or HBu estimated in this study are generally higher than Yos of other 
respirometry studies found in literature. Werker et al. (2022) reported an average Yos on HAc 
of 0.26 ±  0.02 mgO2/mgCODHAc using enriched biomass with a similar COD:N:P ratio. Lower 
Yos have been reported by Marang et al (2013) using a theoretical Yos on HAc of 0.25 
mgO2/mgCODHAc (0.25 mol/Cmol) and Yos on HBu of 0.15 mgO2/mgCODHBu (0.19 mol/Cmol). 
These studies investigated the effect of pulse-wise feeding on pre-enriched cultures that are 
expected to be highly selected on PHA accumulators. The higher Yos values found in this 
study are acceptable due to the direct utilization of biomass which comprises of unidentified 
consortia. It can be interpreted that the Yos estimated from enriched cultures is the minimum 
theoretical value that a MMC could exhibit, as it is not considering antagonistic activity of non-
selected microbial communities.

The values reported for Yos are presented as an estimation of the respiration response that 
the specific biomass can have upon the exposure to a selected readily biodegradable 
substrate. They are specific to the utilized type of WAS and the conditions applied (feeding 
regime, nutrient limitation). It is recommended that further testing with more WAS sources 
should be applied to generalize these observations. The observed Yos values were considered 
representative of the studied system and were used to describe relative trends.  
The Yos on HAc generally fluctuated in a range that was always higher than the rest of the 
substrate mixtures. The Yos on HBu regardless of the sludge age was always the lowest 
reported among all experiments, verifying that the uptake of HBu was conducted with the least 
oxygen consumption. All the mixtures exhibited a shift in comparison to the reference HAc 
pulses (lower Yos), suggesting that feeding on mixtures with a fraction of HBu triggered a 
respiration response with lower oxygen requirements than pure HAc. For a better investigation 
of the factors triggering this effect an analysis of the pulse duration, the specific substrate 
uptake rate (SSUR), the specific oxygen uptake rate (SOUR), the polymer yield on subtsrate 
(YPS) and the specific rate of polymer production (RPX), was conducted for each experiment. 
 
Feast duration  
The feast duration (di) per pulse relatively to the feast duration of the very first pulse decreased 
gradually throughout the duration of the RR experiments, both regarding the reference HA 
pulses and the mixture pulses.  The average feast duration of the reference pulses across all 
trials was 33.55 ± 3.08 min (1st), 27.16 ± 1.82 min (2nd) and 23.02 ± 1.58 min (3rd) and the 
ANOVA analysis concluded that this profile was the same across all reference pulses (p-
value=0.956). Certain mixtures initiated an adaptation period, wherein the feast duration 
fluctuated after the change of substrate and required a longer period and a larger number of 
pulses before stabilization. A typical example of an adaptation pulse is shown in Figure 3.1.4b 
(Appendix A). The stabilization was interpreted as uniform SSUR, SOUR but also constant di 
after a certain number of pulses. Further comment in paragraph 3.1.3.  The average di for 
pulses on pure HAc was 18.65 ± 0.74 min. Direct change from HAc to HBu caused fluctuations 
in the processing time which was stabilized only after the 8th pulse, amounting to an average 
of 19.22 ± 1.01 min. Mixtures with a higher fraction of HBu (60%, 70%) required the longest 
adaptation period among all experiments, but eventually resulted in the shortest feast duration 
(14.14 ± 0.17 min for 60% and 13.25 ± 1.68 min for 70%.  Pure HAc, pure HBu and mixtures 
with a small fraction of HBu (20%, 30%, 40%) were metabolized more efficiently (the pulse 
was processed more optimally in terms of the biochemical processes involved). This is 
probably related to the different metabolic pathways used by the consortia of the MMC and 
which are further induced in the presence of a larger fraction of acetate in the mixture.

of substrate (adaptation pulse, Figure 3.1.4b, Appendix A), which resulted in varying values of 
SSUR and SOUR. The variability in SOUR was evident but at a relatively lower order of 
magnitude than SSUR (Figure 3.1.5b, Appendix A). The duration of this adaptation period

utilized in the present study. A younger sludge, comprised of a more active community might 
demonstrate greater variability in response to changes in substrate availability and the 
environmental conditions (Reis et al., 2003). On the other hand, an older sludge is more likely 
a stable and resilient community with an adapted metabolic activity (Pala-Özkök et al., 2012).  
 
Mixture pulses 
RR on pure HAc and pure HBu was linked to the lowest SSUR among all trials with an average 
value of 3.85 ± 0.12 mgCOD/gXa/min and 4.40 ± 0.28 mgCOD/gXa/min respectively. All 
mixtures resulted in improved SSUR (Figure 3.1.5a & Figure 3.1.5b, Appendix A). Similar 
indications have been reported by Albuquerque et al. (2013), where the co-presence of 
multiple substrates in MMC have enhanced co-metabolism patterns and increased rate of 
substrate uptake. In this case, a possible interpretation would be that in the presence of both 
HAc and HBu, the different microbes of the MMC can adapt to their preferred carbon source, 
allowing for action of other microbes that have more restricted substrate assimilation patterns 
(no substrate competition). It was not possible to extract a clear conclusion on the effect of the 
increasing HBu fraction. Notably, HBu exhibited the lowest SOUR among all blends (0.85 ± 
0.05 mgO2/gXa/min). All mixtures exhibited increased SOUR than the pure substrates, with 
the highest SOUR (1.58 ± 0.03  mgO2/gXa/min) reported for the 50% mixture (batch B). No 
clear trend was observed with the fraction of HBu.  A lower respiration rate for HBu can be 
related to both its effect on cellular metabolism and the substrate preferences in the mixed 
culture (Dias et al., 2008; Reis et al., 2003). Moreover, HAc is often reported as a more 
universal substrate than HBu, making it a more versatile carbon source for mixed cultures 
(Lemos et al., 1998).

The achieved PHB contents in this study (0.56-0.60 gPHB/gVSS) are higher than reported for 
conventional direct accumulation on WAS. However, few researchers have reported 
comparable results for the case of WAS subjected to similar acclimation (3 feast-famine pre-
pulses) and accumulation with acetate as feedstock. Pei et al (2022a) reported contents 
between 0.55-0.66 gPHB/gVSS using WAS from different WWTP. Alonso et al. (2022) 
achieved up to 0.64 including Ca(OH)2 in the batch experiments and applying comparable 
nutrient limitation. The latter research established a connection between the lower energy 
requirements for PHB synthesis expressed as lower oxygen yield (as occured by the addition 
of Ca(OH)2) and a promoted simultaneous PHB production and selective biomass growth. In 
the present study, a systematic increase in active biomass and biomass PHB content 
correlating with the fraction of HBu in the feedstock was observed. 
This improved effect on biomass yield for the pure HBu compared to the mixture and the pure 
HAc can be related to the observed Yos both in the initial and the end phase of the 
accumulation. At the beginning of the accumulation where biomass growth was limited, the 
Yos reflected the energy requirements for pure polymer storage. At the same time, literature 
and other relevant studies verify that HBu has less energy requirements for PHB accumulation 
than HAc, specifically 0.75 mol ATP/CmolHBu vs 1 mol ATP/CmolHAc (Marang et al., 2013). This 
implies that PHB synthesis from HBu has less energy requirements than HAc. As also shown 
in paragraph 3.1, for cases of limited biomass growth, HBu was always linked with lower Yos 
than pure HAc and/or mixtures including HAc. The same observation was made here, where 
the accumulations that run on pure HBu also provided the lowest Yos throughout the whole 
experimental duration. The HAc had the highest Yos and the performance of 50% mixture 
varied in between the two.

(Figure 3.2.5). As the accumulation progresses growth is occurring in parallel to PHB

SSUR 
The SSUR profiles during the accumulation experiments is presented in Figure 3.2.6 (Appendix B). 
Pure HBu consistently exhibited higher SSUR values throughout the experimental period. The 
SOUR of the experiments is presented in It varied at a maximum range of 4.9-5.3 
mgCOD/gXa/min (5-10 hours) and gradually decreased towards the end of the experiment. 
On the contrary, the 50% mixture showed a decreasing trend over time with the highest range 
reported in the first 5 hours (4.9-4.6 mgCOD/gXa/min). Acetate generally exhibited the lowest 
SSUR which varied between 2.4 and 4.1 mgCOD/gXa/min. The ranges for SSUR during the 
accumulation on acetate are comparable to those reported by Pei et al. (2022) for direct 
accumulation in WAS (0.83-2.28 mgCOD/gXa/min) but also Morgan-Sagastume et al. (2017) 
using acetate for acclimation and accumulation of activated sludge biomass after enrichment 
(mgCOD/gXa/min). Marang et al. (2013) reported similar ranges for butyrate uptake (4.5 
mgCOD/gXa/min) on accumulation on pre-enriched culture. Regarding accumulation on 
mixtures of HBu and HAc few reports are only referring to enrichment cultures and study the 
fraction of each VFA on the cumulative SSUR (Albuquerque et al., 2013). In the present study, 
while no direct correlation between the specific HBu or HAc fractions on SSUR was found, it 
was evident that HBu was always associated with higher SSUR. This was further supported 
by the feast times observed during HBu accumulation pulses (14.3 ± 2.8 in A1 and 15.12 ± 
2.18 in A2) compared to pure HAc (21.8 ± 3.8 min) and the 50% mixture (22.33 ± 2.62 min). 
This suggests that HBu is more readily to be converted to PHB, likely due to the differences 
in metabolic pathways and microbial substrate preferences. HBu undergoes a shorter 
metabolic pathway for transformation to PHB (direct activation to hydroxybutyryl-CoA) 
compared to HAc resulting in higher carbon uptake rates and PHB yields (Marang et al., 2013). 
In scenarios where both substrates are present, bacteria exhibit a strong preference on HBu 
over HAc, with HAc uptake being favored only after HBu depletion (Wang e tal., 2017). This 
phenomenon might imply that substrate uptake may serve as the limiting step in PHB 
production. To follow the substrate consumption profiles additional sampling and detailed 
modelling of the flux distributions should be implemented. In that case it would be possible to 
argue in more detail about the exact kinetics of HBu vs HAc uptake when present together. 
The fact that the 50% mixture maintained higher and more consistent SSUR than the HAc 
may indicate that acetate acts as a co-substrate for the other VFAs uptake. Similar observation 
was made by Fradinho et al. (2014) suggesting that the presence of acetate accelerated the 
consumption of butyrate and propionate and generally had higher cumulative SSUR than the

Another possible interpretation would be in connection to the metabolic pathways. The acetate 
to PHB pathway involves the formation of acetyl-CoA, then acetoacetyl-CoA to result in the

that under certain conditions fraction of both substrates flows either way but at different rates, 
hence we cannot completely ignore their occurrence (Dias et al., 2008). This however is 
expected to be related to the specific process conditions, and the dominant limiting parameters 
(e.g. nutrients, energy) and further detail metabolic modelling would be required to support the 
exact mechanism of substrate assimilation. Similar statements have been made by Reis et al. 
(2021) and Predella et al. (2010) to explain the simultaneous increase in SSUR and polymer 
synthesis rates in accumulation of mixed substrates (acetate, butyrate, valerate).

For a more accurate evaluation of the measurements, COD mass balances were constructed 
for the whole experimental cycle and for each accumulation. Figure 3.2.7 shows the calculated 
electron balances for all 4 systems at different time points during the accumulation. At 30 h, 
for the HBu reactor (A1), 92.09 % (0.04%) of the electrons corresponding to the consumed 
substrate were traced among the active biomass (Xa), the produced PHB and the oxygen 
yield (Yos). The Yos in this case represents the amount of oxidized substrate to CO2. For the 
50% reactor case, 98.59% (0.01%) of the electrons were recovered in the same electron 
acceptors with an improved recovery towards the end of the accumulation. The HBu (A2) had 
a recovery of 92.04 (3.52%) with 60% traced in the PHB biomass at 30 h (Figure 3.2.5c) while 
in the HAc reactor 90% (0.03) were recovered 43% represented by the Yos at the end of the 
accumulation.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
In all experiments, there was a small increase in the carbon flux directed to CO2 over time. 
This is probably related to the increased flux through the TCA cycle. It has been reported that 
in conditions of substrate excess and restricted PHB synthesis, this carbon is continuously 
dissipated to produce surplus energy (Escapa et al., 2012). Both pure HBu and 50% mixture 
exhibited increasing Yos throughout the experiment, coupled with decreasing substrate uptake 
rate. This observation was more significant in the 50% mixture suggesting that the utilization 
of substrate by the biomass was diminishing over time. This trend aligns with the previous 
statement, suggesting that upon cell saturation, the substrate continues to be taken up but is 
primarily spilled to produce surplus energy rather than being utilized for biomass generation. 
Moreover, the carbon flux to PHB was higher during the first 8 hours and started decreasing 
towards the end of the experiment in both experiments. This was linked with the increased 
biomass yield in the system towards the end of the accumulation. For the HAc case a clear 
decrease in the flux to PHB was observed from 8 h onwards and was related to the increasing 
active biomass therein. The same observation was noted in the 50% but at a lower degree.  
This can imply the improved performance of HBu, that sustained higher PHB yields during the 
accumulation. The gap in the electron balance was higher at the beginning of the accumulation 
and improved towards the end implying that the observed fluxed became more consistent with

be assumed that the growth observed was selective for PHB accumulators, contributing to the 
higher PHB content in the biomass. Further analysis is recommended in this direction to prove 
the selective growth of PHB rich biomass.

Conclusion 
The combination of respiration response and accumulation experiments that were performed 
in the present work concluded that the presence of HBu was beneficial for PHB accumulation 
in WAS both as sole and as a mixture substrate.  
The PHB contents achieved were in the range of 0.58-0.62 gPHB/gVSS which are generally 
among the highest reported for direct accumulation in WAS (Pei et al., 2022; Estévez-Alonso 
et al., 2022). 
These observations expand the possibilities for utilizing WAS as a versatile source for PHA 
production at industrial scale. An optimum substrate for direct PHB accumulation should allow 
the biomass to grow and simultaneously store PHB at increased rates throughout the whole 
course of the accumulation. In this work, HBu was the substrate that systematically promoted 
this effect in contrast to pure HAc that had the lowest productivity and PHB yield at the end of 
the accumulation. Although it was not possible to identify the selectivity of growth, increased 
growth was always observed in all experiments with a higher HBu fraction. Even lower 
amounts of HBu (50%) were proven capable of triggering the same effects with concurrent 
increased final PHB biomass content (up to 0.58 gPHB/gVSS).

From the series of RR experiments it was possible to identify the oxygen yield on substrate, 
Yos for the different pure substrates and mixtures. Under these conditions and with minimal 
active growth, the Yos reflected the energy requirements for pure polymer storage. Pure HBu 
showed repetitively the lowest Yos among all studied mixtures (0.18-0.20 mgO2/mgCOD) and 
HAc the highest (0.31-0.33 mgO2/mgCOD). Based on this observation it was verified that HBu 
has lower energy requirements for PHB synthesis compared to HAc as also reported in 
literature (Marang et al., 2013; Shi et al., 1997). All mixtures (20%-70%) systematically 
provided decreased Yos compared to HAc and their absolute values varied between 0.23 
mgO2/mgCOD and 0.26 mgO2/mgCOD. It was not possible to identify a relation between the 
fraction of HBu and its relation to the Yos. However, a qualitative observation of lower Yos with 
the presence of HBu was always made and was linked to improve substrate uptake profiles. 
These findings suggested that the mixtures facilitated the respiration response of WAS. 
However, these observations were only relevant to the specific system and conditions applied 
(feeding regime, famine WAS, nutrient limitation) and further verifications is necessary for 
generalization of the approach.  
Last, an attempt to observe the influence of those lower energy requirements on the 
accumulation performance was made. Lower Yos values were observed throughout the 
accumulation for all cases for pure HBu and the 50% HBu mixture compared to HAc. The 
initial phase was comparable to the condition described by the RR experiments which further 
supported the initial assumptions. The main distinctive effect was that pure HBu always 
completed the accumulation with the highest COD consumption and the highest substrate 
uptake rate. That also reflected to a highest PHB yield, productivity and PHB content. The 
main differentiating characteristic was the level of active growth in the system. Accumulation 
with pure HBu had a biomass growth up to 2.19 gCODXa compared to 1.12 gCODXa in the 50% 
mixture and only 0.79 gCODXa in the pure HAc. Although it was not possible to identify the 
selectivity oft this growth, most probably it I was in favor of the PHA accumulating biomass 
considering that the PHB yield and the biomass PHB content were sustained.  Even lower 
amounts of HBu (50%) were proven capable of triggering the same effects with concurrent 
increased final PHB biomass content (up to 0.58 gPHB/gVSS) and suggested the potential 
benefit of utilizing mixtures.  
In connection to the hypothesis of the present work, it was verified that a lower Yos always 
provided higher biomass growth and increased PHB contents. It was not possible to identify a 
threshold that this improved performance was observed, due to some practical complications 
(analyzed in Outlook and Appendix C).  
Further work needs to be conducted in this direction to establish connection between the lower 
energy requirements for PHB synthesis and the accumulation potential.  It is recommended 
that additional experiments are planned to evaluate more HBu/HAc ratios for accumulation 
but also alternative feedstocks.

Outlook 
 
In connection to the experimental plan followed in this work, two sets of accumulation results 
are missing from the Results & discussion paragraph. This was due to unforeseen failure of 
the TGA instrument that was used for the PHB determination. However, the VSS analysis was 
completed and will be followingly presented in this paragraph, to try and identify the possible 
outcomes. 
The two studied ratios were 60%(HBu)-40%(HAc) and 70%(HBu)-30%(HAc) and the collected 
data up to the point of the report are presented in Appendix C. These mixtures were selected 
as cases that could provide improved PHB accumulation potential in relation to the highest 
fraction of HBu and reduced energy requirements for PHB storage as analyzed above. The 
most interesting observation was that the 50% mixture showed a comparable VSS/COD profile 
with the pure HBu, while all other mixtures declined significantly from the reference already by 
the mid-time of the accumulation (Figure C.2). This indicated that the efficiency of COD 
utilization by the biomass was decreasing. To verify what happened in 60%, 70% the solid 
analysis is required. In contrast to the initial hypothesis, if this trend is true, it would suggest 
that a higher than 60-70% HBu fraction is not necessarily beneficial for the overall course of 
the accumulation. Also, that a minimum of 50% HBu (or else) is potentially adequate to provide 
improved accumulation performance (increased PHA and biomass yield, high PHA content). 
This would entail great potential for the upscale of the accumulation of WAS. It would imply 
that feedstocks with a moderate HBu fraction would be adequate to maximize the PHA 
productivity, if the same biomass and same conditions are implemented.  
In this direction, it is recommended to conduct further accumulation experiments to examine 
more ratios of HBu and HAc to identify the lower limit of HBu that can trigger this effect. By 
adequately justifying the link between lower Yos and the lower energy requirements for PHA 
synthesis, it will be possible to generalize this observation.  Potentially, identifying alternative 
ways to lower the energy requirements for PHA accumulation would further support the 
findings of this work.  
In the same context, the implementation of detailed metabolic modeling to support the 
empirical observations is considered necessary. A kinetic model adapted to the conditions of 
the current study will allow for better investigation of the main drivers affecting the specific 
PHA accumulation process, considering the stoichiometry of all metabolic reactions (both 
substrates uptake, PHB production, growth, catabolism, oxidative phosphorylation, PHB 
consumption) and balances for the conserved moieties (ATP, NADH2, acetyl-CoA). 
Considering all factors such as substrate competition, cell growth, maintenance, product 
formation, it will help predict the substrate competition between acetate and butyrate in direct 
PHA accumulation and adequately support the reported observations in a more systematic 
manner. Moreover, the ATP surplus (if present) could be quantified and directly used as an 
interpretation of the observed results. 
For a better investigation of the antagonism among the different microbial consortia but also 
the identification of the selectivity of growth, a microbial community and microscopic analysis 
is necessary to identify and quantify the presence and/or prevalence of PHA accumulators. 
The degree of enrichment (DE) in PHA accumulators has been identified as a key parameter 
that maximizes the PHA accumulation potential of WAS (Pei et al., 2022a). Staining and

Evaluation of results for missing experiments 
The average COD consumed by the reference reactors in 30 hours across all trials was 16.70 
± 0.44 gCOD. From the mixture results for COD, a notable observation was that the amount 
of COD consumed differed significantly between the 70% and the rest of the mixtures. This 
was the only experiment that behaved similarly to pure HBu (with a distinctive differentiation 
from the rest after the 10th hour). Pure acetate, 50% or 60% had similar requirements in COD. 
This was an unexpected outcome as the initial hypothesis was that the higher the HBu fraction, 
a behavior closer to the pure HBu would occur. Regarding the VSS evolution in the reactors, 
a clearer correlation between the higher amount of HBu and the increased VSS concentration 
was observed. The highest final VSS concentration was detected for the pure HBu (as 
expected) followed by the 70%, the 50%, 60% and pure HAc. A possible interpretation would 
be that in cases where HBu was present in higher abundance, then a higher active biomass 
concentration would occur, reflecting to higher VSS concentration. However, it is not clear 
whether this increased VSS is due to an increase in the active growth or an increase in the 
biomass PHB content (as VSS= PHB + Xa). To clarify this observation the complete solids 
analysis is required. This is planned to be completed upon the reparation of the instrument 
and the results will be then interpreted in a comprehensive manner.   
Figure C.1: Accumulation results for all planned experiments: (a) Evolution of COD and (b) evolution 
VSS.  
50% and 60% mixtures had no distinctive profiles for the biggest part of the accumulation 
except the end. An indication that the 50% mixture could sustain continuous VSS production 
was observed, while the 60% HBu reached a plateau around the 30th hour. The reason behind 
this difference remains unclear. However, that might imply that at lower ratios, the metabolic 
fluxes of both substrates are more optimally balanced and that active growth and PHA 
accumulation are sustained for a longer time. This was the case of 50% HBu mixture (as 
shown in paragraph 3.2.2). However, further analysis is required to identify the dominant 
phenomena for the 60% mixture.  
To follow the rate of VSS change during the different accumulation experiments, the rate of 
VSS produced over the COD consumed was calculated and presented in Figure C.2. The 
most interesting observation was that the 50%HBu mixture showed a comparable VSS/COD 
profile with the pure HBu, while all other mixtures declined significantly from the reference 
already by the mid-time of the accumulation. This indicated that the efficiency of COD 
utilization by the biomass was decreasing. This was indeed the case for pure HAc, which as 
analyzed in paragraph 3.2.1 had a limited active biomass growth. To verify what happened in 
60%, 70% the solid analysis is required. In contrast to the initial hypothesis, if this trend is true,
