Abstract
The European Union General Data Protection and Regulation (GDPR) legislation aims to
protect the privacy of EU citizens and the ﬁnes imposed by the GDPR are a strong incentive
for organisations and researchers to devise a solid culture adapted to GDPR compliance.
An
interesting aspect of the GDPR is the limitation of processing of personal information.
Many
limitations primarily revolve around the data subject’s consent to the processing of their data. It
is therefore of interest to investigate how to design a model that can incorporate the notion of a
consent and impose the GDPR limitations on processing of data by enforcing the presence of a
valid consent. While there are multiple ways to devise such a system, this project focuses on the
Attributes Enhanced Role Based Access Control (AERBAC) model. The thesis explores how the
AERBAC model may be extended to secure against unlawful processing of personal data without
the presence of a valid consent, denoted secondary use.
The foundation of this project is the idea that the attributes used by AERBAC to enforce access
control to data can be used to prevent secondary use by annotating objects that contain personal
data with attributes that denote the consented purposes of the objects and annotating subjects
with attributes that represent the subject’s current activities. By matching the activities of a
subject with the consented purposes of an object, it can be veriﬁed if the subject is performing
any activities that would violate the lawful processing of the requested data, before AERBAC
grants the subject access to the requested data.
This project shows that it is indeed possible to extend AERBAC to incorporate the desired
secondary use-prevention and does so by segmenting the extension into three primary domains
that are initially addressed separately.
Each domain is thoroughly analysed and a solution to
each domain is proposed. It is shown how these domains interact and how they can be combined
to form a complete solution denoted the extended AERBAC model. The project also addresses
that the extended AERBAC model is shown to have some limitations, in particular regarding
lack of support for protection against secondary use not involving the requirement of a valid
consent. Other limitations include the amount of manual work required to implement the extended
AERBAC model and the model’s limited ability to autonomously detect and monitor the subject’s
current activities.
Keywords:
GDPR, Attributes, Access Control, ABAC, AERBAC, Lawful Processing

Introduction
With the growth in pervasive computing and the Internet of Things, companies have expanded from the desktop
into all aspects of our lives. Many companies proﬁt from targeted advertising, and in doing so, have converted
the users of their services from consumers into products, whose personal information is sold to companies
that seek to impose targeted sales against the user. Due to this change in business models, the rate at which
personal data is gathered, aggregated and analysed has soared and the control of one’s personal data has moved
from the user to the data aggregators, spearheaded by Big Tech giants such as Google, Apple and Amazon.
In response to the invasion of privacy of its citizens, on May 25th 2016, the European Union implemented
the GDPR (General Data Protection and Regulation) legislation, which seeks to return the control of the
citizen’s personal information from private corporations back to the original data owners. To help enforce
this legislation, signiﬁcant ﬁnes for violations were implemented on 25 May 2018.
Since then, companies
can be held responsible for GDPR violations, for example on January 21st 2019, the french CNIL (National
Commission on Informatics and Liberty) imposed a 50 Million euro ﬁne on Google on the basis that Google
lacked transparency, provided inadequate information and lacked valid consent regarding ad-personalisation.2
This shows how much of an impact a GDPR violation can incur, and has spurred the interest in developing
mechanisms to assist in enforcing GDPR inside organisations. There are many aspects of GDPR, and many
tools that can achieve compliance with one or more of these aspects respectively. One interesting approach is
the use of Access Control.
The principle of Access Control predates computers and computer science and has existed in the physical
world as security in the form of walls, locks and guards etc. in order to monitor and control access to areas
and objects.
With the rise of computing and digital systems, the concept has been adopted by computer
scientists, and today, computer security is heavily reliant on AC (Access Control) mechanisms to secure the
conﬁdentiality and integrity of data.3
Due to the reliance on Access Control cyber security, research on
how to improve both the security-level and performance of access control mechanisms is of great signiﬁcance
and importance. Technological advancement carries with it new features and possibilities for users, but also
generates new security threats and concerns. This dynamic technological development encourages perpetual
co-development of security features alongside technological advancements, including the development of new
or improved Access Control models. One such example is the release of the European GDPR, which has had
a major impact on cyber security considerations, especially since the ﬁnes for violating the EU GDPR were
implemented.
The EU GDPR has thus sparked motivation in several ﬁelds of computer science, of which
just some areas are Cloud Technology[18], Geolocation[9] and Big Data [10]. Among the many ﬁelds of study
that are looking into adaptation and implications of GDPR is the ﬁeld of Access Control models, and how
AC models can be used to ensure GDPR compliance. This area is interesting because GDPR is mainly a
privacy-related topic, and AC is designed to authorise access in order to ensure data conﬁdentiality which is
closely related to privacy.
An important aspect of GDPR is the restriction that, under many circumstances, the processing of data
containing personal information requires consent by the data owner.
Whenever personal information has
been collected for one or more consented purposes and then is subsequently processed with a non-consented
purpose, that processing is denoted secondary processing or secondary use4. This dissertation investigates how
this particular aspect of GDPR, namely the prevention of secondary processing, can be enforced using Access
Control models.
The fundamental idea of this thesis, is that attributes that are traditionally used by the ABAC (Attributes-
Based Access Control) model can be used to achieve prevention of secondary processing. This can be achieved
by building an AC model that supports attributes in a way that allow the attributes to describe the lawful
processing of any object with personal information and then match those attributes with the current activity
of any user that requests access to the objects, to enforce that the object can only be accessed by the user, if
the user is performing an activity for which the object has a matching consent.

(Role-Based Access Control) model in a manner that mitigates the limitations of both of the two individual

In AERBAC’s current state[24], the model does not readily support secondary use prevention. The model was
not originally developed with GDPR in mind, for which reason the main goal of this thesis is to investigate
the possibilities of extending AERBAC to support secondary use prevention and to propose one such extended
AERBAC model.

1.1
Aim
Whether ethically or ﬁnancially motivated5, both the private and the public sector in the EU have seen a grow-
ing interest in being compliant with the European GDPR. Naturally, in the wakes of this growing interest, is
the development of IT systems that can handle GDPR compliance on behalf of the organisations autonomously.
The motivation for automating GDPR compliance is two-fold: eﬃciency and assurance. Automating GDPR
compliance adds eﬃciency by alleviating the workload of the users of the systems, just like any other automa-
tion is intended to reduce the required amount of human labour. The second beneﬁt, assurance, is the ability
to guarantee both employees and management within an organisation that they are GDPR compliant, which
can reduce stress and risk of reprisals on the individual employees and on the company as a whole. Addi-
tionally, assurance relates to assuring the data owners that the given organisation is taking steps to protect
their data, which can enhance trust in the organisation. While a full-scale GDPR compliant system would
be the ultimate goal to pursue, this is far too ambitious, and not likely to be possible due to organisations
implementing unique architectures by combining platforms and custom software. This dissertation narrows its
focus from being completely GDPR compliant, to compliance with a segment of the GDPR: The section Right
to restriction of processing6, states: Where processing has been restricted under paragraph 1, such personal data
shall, with the exception of storage, only be processed with the data subject’s consent or for the establishment,
exercise or defence of legal claims or for the protection of the rights of another natural or legal person or for
reasons of important public interest of the Union or of a Member State. The GDPR article revolves around
restricting data processing, with one aspect of particular importance: the subject’s consent. This leads to the
following deﬁnition:
’Secondary use’ is the processing of personal information, without the presence of an informed consent for
that processing purpose, given by the data subject to whom the personal data pertain.
The goal of this dissertation is to investigate how secondary use can be prevented by the use of Access Control
models.
In particular, the focus will be to investigate how the AERBAC[24] can be extended to support
secondary use-prevention and to evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of AERBAC in this context, ie.
answering how well the proposed AERBAC extension suﬃces to cover secondary use-prevention. This paper
initially establishes the fundamental understanding of Access Control models, including AERBAC and its
heritage, and from there, designs and proposes an extended AERBAC model that can solve the secondary
use-requirement.
AERBAC makes access decisions by using policies that relies on conditions that must be satisﬁed in order to
grant a subject access to a given object. Conditions primarily revolve around evaluating Boolean expressions
containing both object and subject attributes. The hypothesis of this dissertation is that it is possible to
determine the consented purposes of objects and translate them into attributes and that any subject that
requests access to one such object can have their current activity determined and annotated as attributes as
well. Furthermore, it is hypothesised that AERBAC can use these attributes to make access decisions in a

1.2
Scope
Access Control as a concept has very wide applicability. For this reason, it is important to deﬁne the scope of
the area of Access Control that is to be considered in this dissertation:
Consent-related aspects of GDPR
GDPR states multiple exceptions to the requirement that personal data can only be processed with a valid
consent (Art 6.), while ultimately these exceptions are important to consider, the primary focus of this disser-
tation is to provide a paper-based Proof-of-Concept that consent-mandated processing can be controlled using
a modiﬁed version of the AERBAC model. Section 11.3 addresses some of the exceptions to consent-mandated
processing and how to incorporate these into extended AERBAC design.
Physical versus digital access control
Access Control can be implemented as either physical or digital access control or as a combination of the
two7. While all three areas are highly interesting in the context of AERBAC and AERBAC’s capabilities, this
dissertation is limited to only considering secondary processing in the digital realm and therefore only digital
access control. This also means that while AERBAC supports environmental attributes that can be used to
make access decisions based on sensor input, the environmental attributes are not considered relevant and are
also omitted from further study.
Solution recipients
Access control can be applied at various levels of restrictiveness and across many diﬀerent platforms and
purposes.
Varying from extremely restrictive, military grade access control implemented in governmental
intelligence agencies and ﬁnancial institutions to private end-user access control such as submitting personal
credentials to authenticate and obtain access to one’s personal desktop. Since personal computers are not
covered by the same GDPR restrictions8, the focus of this thesis is the organisational levels which fall under
GDPR’s deﬁnition of ’enterprise’(Article 4.(18)). Furthermore, since the automating of tasks increases in value
as the scale at which it is applied goes up, the larger organisations that can beneﬁt from the solution proposed
in this thesis, the better. This argument is further enhanced by the consideration that AERBAC is intended

Activity detection
As explored in section 10, identiﬁcation of a subject’s current activity can involve several techniques of varying
complexity. The most complex being to use techniques such as Neural Networks, AI or Machine Learning to
automatically detect the current activity to using static mapping and manually asking the user to state their
current activity. The diﬀerent approaches vary in degree of complexity at the beneﬁt of automation as the
complexity increases. However, due to limitations of time and to focus on the hypothesis, the detection of the
subject’s current activity is considered important but exactly how it is carried out is of less relevance. For this
reason, the most complex approaches are considered out of scope in this dissertation. Investigations as to how
to apply the complex detection mechanisms instead remain a suggested future ﬁeld of work.
Subject limitation
As previously mentioned, the most complex and fully automated approaches to activity detection are considered
out of scope which means that the subject must, to some degree, be involved in the process of determining
their current activity.
A subject in the context of access control can be both autonomous processes and human users or processes
acting directly on behalf of a user. However, due to the involvement of the subject in activity detection, the
scope is limited to subjects that are either human users, or processes acting directly on behalf of a user in such
a way that the human user can be involved in the activity-detection process.
If the solution is to support ’passive’ processes or other abstractions of processes that must be able to work
without the involvement of a human user, the system needs to have a lot of exception-handling and innate
decision-making10.
Implementational Proof-of-Concept
Due to time-constraints, no code or implemented PoC will be developed alongside this dissertation and an
implementational PoC is therefore considered as ’future work’. Instead, this dissertation aims to provide the
overall ideas and concepts as well as the paper-based model design in such a way that a software engineer can
extract the technical requirements and, in turn, implement the model based on this.
Platform applicability and consent collection
This dissertation is intentionally not limited to speciﬁc platforms and tools in order to keep it compatible with
as broad a selection of platforms as possible. One exception is that the solution proposed in this dissertation
will assume that input can be provided to it based on web-interfaces for collection of consented purposes of
submitted personal information. The speciﬁcations regarding this are kept at a high abstraction level to allow
any type of web application to be used for consent-collection.

Basic Access Control Principles
The concept of Access Control is old, and most people have faced access control in multiple forms during their
every day life. Examples of access control include using keys to lock and unlock a door, access cards to access a
workplace, presenting a driver’s license to the police or tickets to a train conductor. In each case, the principle
is the same: to restrict, control and enforce access to either a service, action or object, through diﬀerent means
in such a way that unauthorised access is prevented. In computer science, access control is used to secure the
conﬁdentiality and integrity data. This is usually done by limiting certain actions or operations in such a way
that only people who are authorised can perform the restricted actions. This usually involves three elements:
a subject who intends to perform an operation on some object. This could for instance be to ensure that only
a user with administrator privileges can create and delete users in a system. In this case create and delete are
the operations and the objects are all user-objects, for which access control is exercised. A user must have the
’tag’ administrative privileges as the condition which must be met, if the user is to be allowed to exercise those
actions on user-objects.
Access Control can be carried out in a multitude of ways to ensure that a subject is authorised to perform
a speciﬁc operation on a speciﬁc object. The following sections will present some of the original and basic
AC models leading up to more modern approaches and ﬁnally the model which this dissertation focuses on,
AERBAC. The diﬀerent AC models will not be exhaustively addressed but will be used to both provide an
overview and an understanding of the basic AC concepts, but also show how AERBAC still relies on many of
the same basic principles that the original access control mechanisms do. Access Control models vary greatly
but most of them share some common concepts. Figure 2.a depicts the overall outline of access control models
of which the components show are generally present in all access control models: Subjects are the users, or
processes acting on behalf of them, policies contain the permissions in the system and hence speciﬁes the rules
and conditions that are to be enforced. Policies are moderated by policy administrators. The reference monitor
is the access control ’core’ which evaluates access requests submitted by the subject and grants or denies access
to the requested objects based on the policy. The process of evaluating an access request is also often referred
to as ’making an access decision’.

2.2
The Access Control Matrix
One very basic and fundamental way of representing a policy is an ACM (Access Control Matrix)[13]. An
ACM is a table that lists subjects as rows and objects as columns. Each entry is the permissible actions that a
subject has for the given object. In practice, the ACM is usually used to extract and represent the permissions
in one of two ways: (i) Dividing the ACM into columns such that each column lists all the permissions for
all users for one object is denoted an ACL (Access Control List). (ii) Dividing the ACM into rows such that
each row lists all the objects and associated permissions for one per user is denoted the capabilities of that
user.
ACLs and Capabilities thus contain the same information and are both derived from an ACM, and
the diﬀerence between the two is whether they are user- or object-centric. From the example Access Control
Matrix shown in table 1, capabilities and ACLs can be derived: Capabilities are represented by each row, for
instance Alice’s capabilities are: (RW, Object X) and (O, Object Y) ie. that Alice may perform read and write

2.3
Access Control Lists
ACL is one of the most simple and widely used access control mechanisms. This is mainly due to its simplicity,
which makes ACL quite intuitive to comprehend and likewise, evaluating access control checks is a simple
operation comprised of a direct table look-up.
As described in section 2.2, an Access Control List is a list of permissions which are associated with an object,
usually via some object identiﬁer. The list maps subjects13 to a set of permissible actions.
Upon accessing an object, the system needs to retrieve the associated ACL via the object’s identiﬁer, and then
look for an entry in the ACL that matches the subject’s ID. If the subject’s ID is in the object’s ACL, and
the requested action is a part of the set of permissible actions for that subject, then permission to perform the
requested operation on the object is granted.
ACLs are still in use in most modern operating systems such as Windows[17], Unix-based[5] and Mac-OS [1].
The main reason for ACL’s success in the area of operating systems, is the complexity requirements of ACLs
for objects within local operating systems: Local desktop operating systems usually have a low amount of
users, meaning that enumerating an object’s ACL is not too cumbersome when handling access requests. In
13Subjects are denoted principal in Windows ACL terminology[12].
14
==PAGE:19==
addition, the systems list of users is relatively static. This means that maintaining the ACLs and updating
them when users are created and removed is feasible. On the other hand, the arguments that support ACLs
for desktop-level access control, also becomes the obstacle when scaling: Using ACLs in large systems such
as enterprise servers or web applications which are prone to a large amount of users and frequent changes to
the list of users, maintaining ACLs becomes cumbersome and handling access requests becomes a slow process
when enumerating huge lists of users and permission-sets. Especially when considering that modern ACLs
usually have more advanced features such as the ability to specify both DENY and ACCEPT permission-rules
as well as default ’catch-all’ rules across a large set of diﬀerent actions, opposed to the classic Unix actions:
read, write and execute. Thus, the advantages of ACL for private computing become disadvantages when scaled
to enterprise-level: Managing ACL structures quickly becomes cumbersome in unison with a growing number
of users in the system. This gave birth to the addition that ACLs could deﬁne user groups, ie. sets of users,
rather than having to deﬁne individual users. ACLs based on user groups are denoted ACLg. ACLg is the
predecessor to role-based AC (RBAC) which is further addressed in section 3. In this context, it is worth noting
that John Barkley[2] has shown how ACLg (ACL with only groups being permitted as subject) is equivalent
to RBACm (RBAC minimal model), and hence a speciﬁc variant of RBAC.
Note that this dissertation addresses ACL in the context of ﬁle system management, ACLs also have another
application: Firewalls. Many basic stateless ﬁrewalls, such as IPtables are built using ACLs which specify
simple chains of rules to be used to evaluate if a packet is to be permitted through. Network-level AC is not
considered a part of the scope in this dissertation since the goal is to restrict access to ﬁles subjected to GDPR.
2.4
Mandatory and Discretionary Access Control
In Access Control, there are two fundamental approaches to policy design: Discretionary Access Control (DAC)
and Mandatory Access Control (MAC14). Having established the notion of an ACL in section 2.3, ACLs can
be designed using either MAC or DAC:
DAC [27] is used for instance in Unix-OS ACLs where the ACL of each object is controlled at the discretion
of the object owner. In this type of system, the owner can override or modify access control rules that the
ACL is enforcing. While this is ﬂexible, it also relies on the user; the object owner could modify the rules in a
way that could compromise security, whether it being intentional or not. This is a typical vulnerability when
dealing with non-tech-savvy users who could be coerced to grant permission to a malicious process. Think of
the Android OS for mobile phones which uses ’permission’-pop-ups that the phone’s owner is confronted with
when installing new apps, prompting for ’access’-rights to certain areas of the phone like camera, microphone,
photos, etc. The above is an example of DAC in which the user, for better or for worse, acts as the ﬁnal
authority and has the ability to modify the ACLs at their own discretion.
MAC [26] is traditionally used when more restrictive security is required and was originally developed for
securing assets for the US military. Where DAC is controlled by the individual data owners, MAC is controlled
in a centralised manner and can only be modiﬁed by the individuals with explicit permission to the centralised
MAC policy. This means that MAC supports enforcing general rules that are applied globally within a system
and that cannot be overridden by any local users. This is obviously less ﬂexible but also eliminates the risk of
coercion or mistakenly modifying rules.
Due to the distinction between MAC and DAC, an enterprise environment will usually deploy DAC in the local
desktop environments of employees while MAC is applied on a network- and server-level and used to secure
extra sensitive assets and vaults. The real world applicability of MAC and DAC is therefore not a binary
consideration but rather a synergy with applying MAC and DAC interchangeably to achieve a structure that
is ﬂexible in dynamic and less critical environments, and rigid in business critical segments.
Every AC model use either or a combination of the two principles. For instance, Role and Attribute-based
access control presented in the following sections are usually controlled centrally in enterprise environments
and are thus often categorised as MAC while AC models for personal computing use DAC. MAC is therefore
the principle used going forward, due to the scoping of this project.

3
Role-Based Access Control
RBAC (Role-Based Access Control) was formalised by David Ferraiolo and Rick Kuhn (1992) [6] and has since
then been adopted as an access control standard by NIST in 200415. RBAC as an Access Control paradigm
is likely one of the most intuitive and easy to understand, in addition to being eﬃcient and cost-beneﬁcial[8]
to implement. However, RBAC faces some limitations in modern enterprise environments which have spurred
the desire to develop improved Access Control models such as ABAC and, in extent, AERBAC.
Roles: From the notion of user groups, introduced by AC models such as ACLg, as presented in section 2.3,
it is not far-fetched to start naming each user-group by a shared identiﬁer such as a shared functionality or
property that the particular group members have in common. In this way, an identiﬁable user-group became
known as a role and members of the user-group are considered to have the speciﬁed role. Using the concept of
roles, rather than creating policies where permissions are associated directly with users, permissions are now
associated with roles and the membership of those roles in turn determines the permissions of the user. This
essentially introduces the role as an abstraction-layer between users and permissions. Permissions in such a
system are thus centered around roles and Role-Based Access Control is trivially considered to be role-centric
in its policy management.
It is easy to see why this can be very powerful under the right circumstances, since this approach allows the
creation of rules that apply to roles rather than to individual subjects. Thus enabling the use of generalisation
to reduce the amount of rules that need to be created, kept up to date and iterated through when handling
access control requests.
It should be mentioned, that the roles used in RBAC should NOT necessarily be the same as the organisa-
tional use of the notion role. For instance an organisational role such as HR employee might not be directly
translatable into a single, corresponding AC role but instead may correspond to a set of roles that can change
from employee to employee. This is due to the fact that AC mechanisms seek to achieve least privilege and
segregation of duties16, these goals are best achieved with narrow and speciﬁc roles that are too speciﬁc to
encapsulate all the duties of an organisational employee role. It is therefore often considered bad practice to
have a one-to-one relationship between organisational- and AC-roles. However, it must be clariﬁed that, in
order to make this dissertation more accessible and comprehensible for the reader, most examples and cases
through the dissertation uses organisational roles as equivalent to AC roles in examples. This could be exem-
plifying the role ’HR employee’ in cases, even though the use of such a role in AC context is discouraged. It is
important to keep this in mind when considering the examples provided through this dissertation.
3.1
RBAC policy rules
The general format of how RBAC policies are structured is as a sequence of rules or permissions. Rules are
usually formally represented in an if-then-structure with a default ’catch-all’ rule at the end of the policy. The
if-then-rules evaluate some condition represented by a logical expression (Boolean) and grant access based on
the outcome of the evaluated expression. Traditionally, the rules use a binary (2-valued) policy result output:
either grant or deny access. However, it should be noted that nothing prevents the system from using an n-
valued output. Once a policy is invoked, each rule is iteratively checked and ﬁnally, if no condition-expression
evaluated to true, a default rule states whether access should be granted or denied.17. Thus, the general format
for each policy rule becomes either of the following four statements, with the constraint that there can be only

3.2
RBAC advantages
Opposed to capabilities and ACL, RBAC gives the ability to create fewer policies that applies to many users
rather than a permission per user. While models such ACLg and similar AC models also allow for speciﬁcation
of more than one user per policy, the power in segregating the user-groups from the policies and referencing
them using roles, means that policies can be generalised to cover groups of users that share the same security
clearance. In addition, ALCs are associated with each object, where RBAC associates its policies with each
role. In systems with many users who can easily be generalised into a few roles, this is obviously powerful in
terms of policy management: Creating a new user in ACL would require each object’s policy to be revised, and
adding the user to the appropriate policies, while RBAC simply requires assigning the user a role by adding
the user to the appropriate user-group. This greatly reduces the tediousness of managing policies which no
17
==PAGE:22==
longer need to name speciﬁc users in each policy. In turn, this reduces the chance that errors18 are introduced
since micromanagement of large policies is considered prone to human error.
An additional advantage is that the reduced size of policies, caused by introducing roles, can improve perfor-
mance when evaluating access requests. This is due to fewer rules being required to satisfy the same policy
and hence fewer conditions that need to be checked upon handling access requests.
Advantage summary:
• Intuitive - division by role is easy to comprehend
• Easy to use - grouping by shared tasks allows management at a higher level
• Fast - access checks are fast and with little chance of error, compared to the corresponding signiﬁcantly
large ACL- and capability-policies
• Computationally light - when adding/removing users due to central management of membership to
user-groups being the only required work.
3.3
RBAC disadvantages
Due to RBAC being widely used in the industry there are also many sources regarding the disadvantages of
RBAC. For instance, NIST lists some of the core limitations related to RBAC in [13], and [24] discusses the
RBAC limitations that are mitigated by AERBAC. This dissertation presents some of the primary limitations
of RBAC, based on the interpretation of multiple sources.
RBAC’s ability to deﬁne permissions for roles, rather than at user-level, gave rise to the advantage of easier
policy management over more senior models such as ACL. The advantage of easier policy management by
generalising users into roles comes at the cost that managing permissions for individuals, or for very speciﬁc
or niche requirements, becomes cumbersome and this introduces perhaps the primary limitation of RBAC:
coarse granularity19. The problem of coarse granularity is somewhat of a paradox, since RBAC was intently
designed to be more coarse than its predecessors. However, in modern systems the need for ﬁner granularity
has increased and, as such, the drive behind RBAC’s conception is now considered one of its limitations. It is
important to note that the granularity issue is only prevalent when the system grows suﬃciently large, and for
this reason, RBAC is still very popular in smaller systems, such as personal desktop computers.
Only contested by the granularity limitations in terms of severity, is the coupled issue known as the Role Explo-
sion-problem. Role Explosion occurs when a large RBAC-based system tries to accommodate ﬁne granularity
in order to achieve segregation-of-duties and to adhere to the principle of least privilege20 ie. to limit a user’s
permissions to those that they speciﬁcally require to perform their tasks and nothing else and to have one user
have multiple roles one for each category of tasks. If a system is to support many separate duties, the number
of roles tend to grow exponentially, leading to an infeasible amount of roles in terms of role management. In
order to illustrate the Role Explosion-problem, consider a variant of the example used in [24]:21
Consider an online video streaming platform with subscription-based user-accounts. The platform is initially
dividing its user-accounts into two types (roles) by their age: adults and children. In RBAC, this implies child
and adult as roles. In order to make policy management simpler, role-inheritance is used so that adult inherits
permissions from child ie. the users with the adult-role can view all videos available to any of the two roles,
while the child-role is restricted to the objects identiﬁers speciﬁed in its own policy.
The platform is also extended to support a premium user account-type, which is essentially still either child
or adult but with the added ability to view new releases. In RBAC this would require the creation of two
new roles: child-premium and adult-premium. Additionally, if the platform is to support the ability to have
promotional periods, in which regular users are given temporary ’premium’-status, two additional roles would

user-groups with diﬀerent access constraints in an environment where there is cross referencing between the

A third limitation of RBAC is the fact that RBAC relies on explicit object identiﬁcation in its policies. This
means that systems with a large amount of objects cause large permissions in which vast numbers of object
identiﬁers need to be explicitly stated. This can be an issue both because the time it takes to carry out an
access control check is increased when the reference monitor needs to iterate through more object identiﬁers
to look for a match and it can make policy reviewing more cumbersome. If this is coupled with role explosion,
it is easy to imagine how having to specify large sets of object identiﬁers across a large list of roles, caused by
role explosion, will quickly hamper the system’s performance and the ability to manage policies eﬃciently.
RBAC is static. RBAC relies solely on policies that associate user-groups with sets of object identiﬁers that
members of those user-groups can access. This means that RBAC is not capable of considering anything but
the identiﬁer and thus can’t consider information such as the context in which the object is requested, ie.
information such as the time of the day, the date of creation of the object or geographical location from which
the access request is made, etc. Without this type of information it only makes sense to carry out the access
check at the initial time of the request. It makes no sense to re-evaluate access request decisions at a later
point, since there are no variables that could have changed since the initial request.22
RBAC is one-sided in the sense that RBAC considers the user’s roles and the permissions related to those roles,
but the objects that are referenced by each permission are based solely on the object identiﬁers. Hence, no
information regarding the object is taken into account, such as object meta-data. For instance, if a speciﬁc role
should only be able to access objects if they are freshly generated, while it requires elevated privileges to access
the same data once its creation-date supersedes a given threshold. This can’t be expressed in RBAC which
would then rely on the policy for the user that should only access fresh objects to be periodically reevaluated
and have the expired objects ’removed’ from the permissions.
While access control between enterprise environments is considered out of scope, it is worth noting that RBAC
relies on pre-provisioning to support cross-connections between two or more enterprise environments: If a
subject from one enterprise environment is to access another environment’s systems, that subject needs to
have a user-account created at that site separately and have the correct roles assigned in order to perform
their tasks in that environment. Other AC models such as Attribute-Based AC have the ability to work across
enterprise environments as long as the subject possesses the correct attributes. The diﬀerence can be seen if
you consider RBAC as a bouncer at a night club with a list of guests. If a patron approaches the bouncer and
asks for access to the VIP lounge, they need to already be on the list. In a similar fashion, RBAC relies on the
user to be assigned user-groups, in every environment that they access. While another approach is to grant
access based on something that the subject can present to the AC model: A patron showing a VIP bracelet
to the bouncer which is similar to the approach of ABAC. If multiple clubs agree on the same VIP bracelet,
a patron could in turn enter any of the collaborating clubs with the same bracelet without ever being on any
individual club list.
Limitation summary:

• Coarse granularity
• Role-Explosion (scalability)
• Reference by explicit object ID
• Static and no context
• One-sided

3.4
Role hierarchies
In order to improve RBAC, many modern implementations use role hierarchies. Role hierarchies build on the
principle of inheritance, ie. the ability for a role to inherit the permissions of one or more other roles and then
potentially add its own speciﬁc permissions.
A simple example would be to consider a company in which the root-role is ’employee’ and then assign a set
of basic permissions which are generalised and should be enforced for all employees of the company. Deﬁning
speciﬁc roles such as accountant, canteen chef, CEO, etc. each of these roles can inherit the permissions of
’employee’ and then add their own role-speciﬁc permissions. In this way, roles and their relationships can
be presented with an inheritance relationship, removing the need to explicitly specify redundant permissions
that are inherited. RBAC was oﬃcially presented in 1992 in [6], while in 1996, [28] introduced the notion of
inheritance by a relationship similar to PO (Partially Ordered) sets[29].
While PO-sets can be a powerful tool to build security lattices that can help formalise security policies, this
dissertation does not use PO-sets in its proposed solution to secondary use-prevention. Even so, it is beneﬁcial
to understand the concept that roles can inherit permissions in this way. In addition to role-inheritance, PO-
sets can be used to deﬁne security lattices which can be used for MAC (Mandatory Access Control) systems
and in turn, used to formulate strict security requirements in a system. A security lattice can be used to
distinguish diﬀerent security classiﬁcations of objects such as objects containing personal information versus
those that do not. In this way, the relationship between multiple classiﬁcations of objects can be formally
represented. This way of expressing security classiﬁcations using PO-sets plays a large role in designing the
security-classiﬁcations that form the backbone of IFC models. A short outline of the core concepts of partial
orders has been included in appendix C and section 12 will brieﬂy outline the areas of the proposed solution
that could be further extended by applying PO-set theory.

Attribute-Based Access Control
Due to the limitations of RBAC, as discussed in section 3.3, other AC models have been proposed with
the intention of mitigating the limitations and especially the prominent Role-explosion problem.
One of
the most popular AC models that have risen in the wakes of RBACs limitations is ABAC (Attribute-Based
Access Control) [14]. ABAC makes access decisions by evaluating conditions imposed on attributes rather
than checking for user-membership. ABAC involves the requesting subject’s attributes, the attributes of the
requested object(s) and the possibility to include environmental attributes. An attribute is essentially one
or more values or properties that are associated with each subject and object in a system. One of the key
diﬀerences from RBAC is the fact that since attributes are associated with both objects and subjects, ABAC
is capable of considering the context or state of the objects when making access decisions. In contrast, RBAC
is a subject-centric model that revolves around the identity and associated role of each subject and bases its
permissions solely on these. This gives ABAC much greater ﬂexibility and the ability to include more advanced
and elaborate access decisions with a much ﬁner granularity than RBAC.
Simply put, an attribute can be any value that can be associated with an entity. For example, many objects
contain some form of meta-data or a data header, each piece of information in such a header is essentially
an attribute that describes properties of the data. This could be the creation-date, author, ﬁle-size, etc. An
attribute can also be custom. ie. manually speciﬁed and annotated a given object. This could be to specify the
attribute ’End of life’ which holds a data as the attribute value that speciﬁes at what point the object should
be deleted. Usually an attribute is generated and annotated an object at the point of creation of the object,
but in practice there are no constraints that dictate this, and thus, an object may arbitrarily be subjected to
attribute modiﬁcations at any given time. An attribute can be represented by an attribute name alone, but
is usually both a name (attribute identiﬁer) and one or more associated attribute values. This format can be
represented in a tuple-style: (name, values). In the dissertation, the following notation is used to refer to the
attribute-value of a speciﬁc attribute, att, for a given object, o:23
att(o) = attribute-value
(5)
Since ABAC has ﬁner granularity than that of RBAC, ABAC can essentially be used to enforce the same
role-based constraints that RBAC does: A simple case would be to create a ’role’-attribute that consists of the
attribute identiﬁer: role, and some string-value that speciﬁes the speciﬁc role of a given subject. Classical role-
values include administrator, superuser, guest user, regular user, observer, etc. By annotating each subject with
one or more role-attribute and then specifying for each object, which role-values that are valid to permit access
to, this essentially correlates to RBAC and thus, it is relatively easy to convert a RBAC implementation to an
ABAC implementation. The relationship is not directly mirrored the other way around since ABAC uses much
more advanced permissions, RBAC’s simple permissions won’t be able to incorporate ABAC functionality.
In addition to subject- and object-attributes, ABAC also has the ability to include environment-attributes,
such as geographical location, the ambient temperature or any other sensor-related input.
The inclusion
of environment-attributes has greatly increased ABAC’s ability to encompass a security paradigm known as
context awareness or Ambient Intelligence (AmI) [11]. Context awareness can be used to create more elaborate
policies since the knowledge regarding the environment can be taken into account but also faces limitations in
the sense that all sensor data need to be interpreted, including considerations as to inaccuracies and thresholds.
As discussed in the scope in section 1.2, the focus of this dissertation is the internal context and as such,
environment attributes and the possibilities added by these are not considered to be of relevance for the time
being.
Attributes can be placed in a shared repository to enhance control and ability to review and monitor attributes.
This is beneﬁcial when network availability and proper bandwidth can be ensured. If network availability
ﬂuctuates, maintaining local copies of data and their attributes may be required, which in turn can synchronise
with repositories when the network is available.
A property of ABAC is that attributes can be either dynamic or static: static attributes do not change during
a session, and would thus not need to be checked again after the ﬁrst check during session initiation. Static

On the other hand, dynamic attributes
are assumed to be relatively likely to change during a single session and thus need to be re-evaluated during
session run-time. Dynamic attributes could be the time of the day or the room temperature of the physical
access point, but also attributes that relate to digital-properties, such as other active processes within a session
or whether other events need to be triggered in the system in order to allow access (temporal conditions).
Intelligently designed ABAC systems distinguish between static and dynamic attributes and keep a record of
the current session’s attributes in a way that ensures that an attribute re-veriﬁcation event is triggered when
any access control check is carried out which involves dynamic attributes, while the registry simply provides
any static attributes to the access control checking module without any subsequent periodical veriﬁcation.
Changes to dynamic attributes can therefore cause a condition to evaluate to false after the initial access has
been granted and therefore prompt the system to revoke the subject’s access to the object mid-session.
It is not hard to imagine that, while dynamic attributes can be very powerful, they are also computationally
heavy. This is due to the fact that the attributes have to be fetched from the entities dynamically, possibly
either at set time-intervals or at every subsequent access control request, but also that verifying an attribute
can be resource demanding. For this reason, it is recommended to evaluate the need for each attribute to be
classiﬁed as dynamic to ensure that no static attributes are evaluated dynamically, wasting resources.
It is important that the system can trust in the integrity of the attributes provided to it. That is, that attribute
values can’t be forged or spoofed and provided in order to cheat the system to allow access falsely. This could
for instance be, if the ABAC model uses a system-time attribute, the user might simply change the time of
the system24 and thus manipulate the system-time-attribute. It could also be if the user is able to forge an
’administrator-role’-attribute, making the system believe that the user in question has their role-attribute =
administrator. Obviously, this is an important security issue, but luckily the issue is not new in particular for
ABAC: RBAC and other AC models have faced the same issue of credential and token forgeries. One of the best
solutions is to use certiﬁcates, or use MACs (Message Authentication Codes) or similar mechanisms in order to
ensure that the attributes have in fact been generated by the correct modules and that their integrity is intact.
But this further enhances the previous point regarding the resource demands of dynamic attributes: If the
attribute itself not only needs to be fetched from memory and evaluated using the permissions but also needs
to be cryptographically secured and subsequently veriﬁed for integrity, it adds to the resource requirements of
both generating and verifying dynamic attributes. Meanwhile, static attributes only need to go through this
process once they are impacted by integrity-controls to a signiﬁcantly lower degree.
ABAC policies are based on Boolean logic in order to create permissions. As presented in section 3.1, RBAC
policies can also be expressed by simple Boolean expressions, however, the complexity of the guard in RBAC
conditions is very low, since it is a simple check on a user’s role. In ABAC, it is easy to realise that the
complexity of the Boolean expressions can be much more elaborate. The format of ABAC policies is still
structured in an if-then-statement format, however, the guard can be signiﬁcantly more complex, since only
one’s imagination is the limit on how to create conditions based on the available attributes in the session. This
means that conditions can involve anything from checking for particular role-attributes ´a la RBAC, all the
way to complex checks including multiple attributes which must all satisfy some given constraints. This could
be a speciﬁc time of day, geographical location and temporal requirements such as considering the presence of
particular processes or particular states within the session.
Due to ABAC being policy-centered, opposed to the subject-centered RBAC, ABAC can also be referred to as
Policy-Based Access Control. However, I personally do not believe the use of policies to be the central trait in
ABAC because the notion of a policy pre-dates ABAC, and policies are used in many other AC models. Hence,
it is more intuitive to refer to this AC model as Attributes-Based Access Control, having attributes as its core
functionality while keeping in mind that this enables ABAC to be policy-centered opposed other models such
as RBAC.
ABAC is centralised in the sense that policies can be controlled centrally and the polices can be independent of
the internal business logic in the sense that a central policy can ’generalise’ conditions such as: ”any object with
attribute X = value Y has permission Z”, which means that ABAC policies do not need to consider speciﬁc
subjects and objects and list any object identiﬁers explicitly in the policies but instead refer to conditions

• Dynamic access control checks
• Context awareness
• Fine granularity

4.1
ABAC disadvantages
While ABAC is in many regards considered superior to RBAC, ABAC too faces limitations that can discourage
the use of it. The complexity of ABAC policies is signiﬁcantly more comprehensive than RBAC since making
rules that use attributes intelligently is diﬃcult, especially compared to policies that solely use group member-
ships such as RBAC. Keeping the full overview of the policies is not easy and it can be hard to ensure that the
rules actually enforce the corresponding NLP (Natural Language Policy) correctly. This is by far the largest
concern regarding ABAC since the ability to perform policy reviewing is critical to ensure the safety-property,
as mentioned in section 2.1. This limitation is increased as ABAC is scaled to larger organisations: Just like
for RBAC’s Role-Explosion problem, ABAC policies risk either becoming too vague and general or too speciﬁc
and numerous to manage properly.
While environmental attributes based on sensor input can be included in ABAC policies, the reliability and
error margin of ambient attributes is particularly diﬃcult to control and it can be a daunting task to properly
ﬁlter and evaluate sensor input correctly in a way that the output is precise enough for the ABAC model to
make safe decisions.
As discussed in [14], the maintenance costs of ABAC may exceed the beneﬁts long term and therefore ABAC
should not indiscriminately be the AC model of choice. This means that ABAC is not universally beneﬁcial
but should be analysed with each business case in order to ensure that the cost of risk is higher than the cost
of security. ABAC is most suitable in environments that already have a rich pool of attributes and where
access decisions can truly beneﬁt from the ﬁne granularity oﬀered by ABAC based security. In extent of this
notion, [14] also highlights the cost of adapting or transitioning into ABAC from a previously used model
such as RBAC, which may incur signiﬁcant costs due to the task of generating, annotating, maintaining and
deprecating the attributes that the resulting policies may require. Furthermore, it is considered inevitable to
have occurrences of denial of access during the transitional period until policies are adjusted correctly.
In short, ABAC’s primary limitations result from the high complexity introduced when a ﬁne granularity
is desired: Policy reviewing becomes diﬃcult which in turn causes adaptation to and maintaining ABAC
increasingly resource demanding.

AERBAC is an abbreviation for Attributes-Enhanced Role-Based Access Control and is an AC model built as
an extension of the NIST core RBAC framework, as proposed in [7]. Therefore, it retains RBAC’s role-centric
focus, using the same notions of roles and sessions and is still structured by associating users with roles, which
in turn map to policies, consisting of permissions. Roles are associated with permissions in a one-to-many
fashion, which creates structured way of organizing policies. In order for a subject to access an object, the
subject must have one or more relevant roles and, just like RBAC, the role must in turn have a relevant
permission for the object. Once a session is initiated, the session is associated with the given role(s) which in
turn links the session to the permissions associated with that role. This means that once a subject requests
access to an object, the only permissions taken into consideration, are the permissions that are linked with the
role(s) in the given session.27 At this point AERBAC distinguishes itself from RBAC:
While permissions are associated with roles, the permissions are not structured as simple RBAC permissions
consisting of operators and roles but they are instead structured as ABAC permissions: By using attributes
and attributes-based Boolean logic, the AERBAC permissions achieve the granularity of ABAC while each
permission is associated with a given role similar to RBAC, making policy reviewing easier than pure ABAC.
AERBAC policies are based on the three attribute-types presented in the ABAC-section (section 4): objects,
subjects (users) and environment attributes. Each of the three attribute-types are referenced using a corre-
sponding attribute-function (OATT, UATT and EATT respectively). Attribute-functions are functions that
return a corresponding attribute-value28 which means that in the case of objects and subjects, the functions

For instance, one might have an OATT deﬁned: ’objectClassiﬁcation(o)’ which takes an object-identiﬁer, o,

AERBAC permissions are constructed by specifying an operation followed by an object expression. An object
expression is a Boolean-expression that includes object attribute functions, OATT. Permissions use the object
expressions to match a permission to a set of objects. In this way, contrary to RBAC, an AERBAC permission
does not explicitly state which objects it permits access to, but rather provides an expression that can be
evaluated on any object in the system. Objects with attributes for which the expression evaluates to true, will
be considered accessible by the operation stated in the permission. Object expressions are explained in more
detail in section 5.2.
The combination of the roles with attributes creates a dual-layer access control: While ultimately it is the
attribute-dependent permissions which determine whether a user has access to an object or not, the permissions
are bound by the user’s role.
Therefore, for access to be granted, the user’s role must contain a relevant
permission, which in turn must satisfy any attribute-based conditions.
While it may seem ineﬃcient and
counter-productive to structure the access control checks in this two-layer fashion, it optimises access control
checks in much the same way as a hash-map does: By ordering permissions by associating them with roles, only
the sub-set of permissions mapped by that particular role needs to be checked, and thus, AERBAC achieves
faster access control checks by reducing the number of permissions that need to be considered. Consider the
following example:
A scenario, where the role administrator has the permission (delete, objectClassiﬁcation(o) = classiﬁed). Ie.
that subjects with the role ’administrator’ may perform the delete-operation on objects that have the value of

annotated to the provided object-identiﬁer. To evaluate the expression, the object expression substitutes any

vided object is accessible by that permission. Upon requesting access to perform the ’delete’-operation on the

’administrator’.
The reason that AERBAC uses object expressions is due to the power of abstraction, which proves to be
eﬃcient: When querying for a multitude of objects, one can consider object expressions as a ﬁlter, in which
the user can provide object attributes: ”give me all objects for which the objectClassiﬁcation-attribute is

the system to generalise, and not have to specify individual object identiﬁers in its permissions and its queries.
Permissions can be extended with conditions, which are similar to the structure of the permission’s object
expressions in their structure, but which also support the inclusion of user- and environment attribute func-
tions. This means that a condition can be constructed of Boolean expressions that consider all three types of
attributes. The reason that conditions are appended to permissions and not fused with them, is that querying
for objects should only involve attributes that the objects themselves can provide. It would not make sense to
query for objects with an object expression that uses user attributes: ”give me all objects for which the user’s
attribute is ’logged in’”.

5.3
AERBAC Notation
As a general notation-rule, used through this dissertation, sets are represented using squared brackets, [], while
tuples use regular parenthesis, (). In both cases, elements are comma-separated.
Attributes are preﬁxed u, o or e for user-, object- and environment-attributes respectively.
Attribute values can be either atomic values or a set of atomic values.
Attribute functions fetch speciﬁc attribute values from a given user, object or from the set of environment
attributes. The three types of functions are denoted UATT, OATT and EATT.
Attribute function naming generally copies the same name as the attribute(s) that they fetch, aﬃxed with
() to signify that they take a function input. For instance, in the case of an object-attribute representing the
creation-date of an object, oCreationDate, the corresponding OATT is denoted with the same name as well as
any required input: oCreationDate(o) returns the value of the attribute oCreationDate for object o, where o
is an object identiﬁer.
Attribute functions are deﬁned to Return null, if the attribute does not exist for the speciﬁed user or object.
ie. in the case that no oCreationDate-attribute is annotated the object o, oCreationDate(o) = null.
For enhanced readability, I introduce the notation that the returned value from attribute functions may be
represented as a tuple that speciﬁes the attribute name followed by the returned value(s). Hence, oCreation-
Date(o) = (oCreationDate,value).
In practice, AERBAC still only returns the attribute value(s) but including the attribute name in the notation
can prove useful to avoid confusion if an attribute-function is not named directly after the attribute-name.
5.4
AERBAC Strengths and Weaknesses
AERBAC states four advantages over traditional pure RBAC and ABAC[24]:
• Fine-grained access Control (compared to RBAC)
• Context-aware access30 (compared to RBAC)
• Easy Auditing (compared to ABAC)
• Policy Modiﬁcation Visualisation (compared to ABAC).
These strengths arise from the approach of adding ABAC permissions and conditions to RBAC’s role-structure
which combines the ﬁne granularity of ABAC and the easier policy reviewing of RBAC. Note that a hybrid
such as AERBAC still has limitations in the sense that while it is has a ﬁner granularity than pure RBAC, it
retains a role-centric structure that keeps it from achieving the same granularity as pure ABAC. At the same
time, while AERBAC might have easier policy reviewing than pure ABAC, the permissions and conditions are
still potentially complex and more diﬃcult to review than pure RBAC. In this way, AERBAC positions itself
in between the two ’extremes’ represented by pure RBAC and ABAC in the granularity-review trade-oﬀwhich
is visualised in ﬁgure 5.a.
Furthermore, just like for pure ABAC, AERBAC retains the limitations of potentially being resource demanding
due to the potentially large amount of attributes and the required attribute management and maintenance.
AERBAC can mitigate this by varying the complexity and granularity based on identifying the most critical
domains:
Low risk domains can be secure using simple attributes and roles, hence relatively easier and forgiving policy
management while more restricted areas can be secured using more complex and ﬁne-granularity policies. In
this way, less restrictive domains do not incur heavy resource cost and can retain the strengths of RBAC while
the use of complex attribute functions can be used in areas where the need for security supersedes the need to
minimise resource costs. This imposes the notion that while AERBAC is more powerful than pure RBAC and
ABAC, it must also be applied with caution and consideration regarding the domains to which it is applied.

6.2
Ownership
An interesting observation is how the EU GDPR empowers the data subject as the central owner and controller
of their own data. This is covered in the GDPR articles 12 - 23, ’Rights of the data subject’. The reason why
this empowerment of the data subject is interesting is that traditionally in IT, the notion of a data owner is
usually the association of a user-identiﬁer with each object in the system, specifying who ’owns’ that object.
By default, most systems assign the creator of an object as the owner, and depending on the system, the
owner can then reassign ownership. The reason ownership is interesting is that it is usually used to enforce

access control by means of restricting anyone but the owner and any people that the owner identiﬁes, from
performing operations on an object. However, in GDPR, there is no direct link between being the owner of
data and being the controller. In other words, a company’s (controller) might collect personal information
from data subjects (the owners) and store it in their systems. Traditionally, the moment the data is collected
from the data subjects, their information is stored as objects in the company’s system and thus, the company
has ’ownership’ of the data. This is in direct conﬂict with GDPR specifying the data subject as the owner,
implying that even if a company has created the data objects, the ownership remains at the individuals for
whom the personal information pertains. This change in the understanding of ownership has the following
direct impact that ownership is now ambiguous in access control and it is important to distinguish the legal
data owner of the information within an object from the ’creator’ that has generated the object. This has the
following implications regarding data owners:
• Ownership can’t be used in the traditional sense to enforce access control34
• Object ownership is speciﬁed by the creator of the object while the object’s content has another legal
owner derived from the personal information contained within the object
• Legal ownership can’t be re-assigned or changed, unless the personal information within is destroyed and
replaced with the personal information of the new owner which in practice makes no sense.
It is worth noting that while legal ownership of a given object could be theoretically shared, the implications
would be quite complex, since it would imply that a single object contains the personal information of multiple
subjects. In this case, being compliant with the GDPR becomes diﬃcult since the consent of all owners is
required and any single owner could request the destruction of their information but without processing any
of the other owner’s data. In practice it would likely be infeasible to manage a set of owners while staying true
to GDPR, and thus, it is much simpler to enforce the constraint that data objects should only pertain to a
single data subject and thus implicitly, state a single, static data owner.

for another, secondary purpose which lies outside the consent given by the data subject. The following articles
are of particular relevance to secondary processing:

shall, in accordance with Article 89(1), not be considered to be incompatible with the initial purposes (‘purpose

Sub-paragraph 5.1.(b) frames the core of the reason for the deﬁnition of secondary processing as a breach

while 1.(f) relates to leaks that would expose data to external processing. For now, the goal of the extended
AERBAC is primarily internal security and 5.1.(b) is therefore prioritised above 5.1.(f). In regard to this, note
that a security breach should already be mitigated by the implemented access control policies in such a way that
an attacker’s ability to access data is limited by the privileges she has managed to obtain. Breach prevention is
therefore primarily concerning the overall AC policy design and not in particular a concern regarding secondary
use-policies.

design of the aspects of the extended AERBAC solution that deals with consent collection as well as deﬁning
the overall requirements to constraints that must be enforced to achieve prevention of secondary processing.
Article 6, Lawfulness of processing, relates directly to consent by listing several lawful processing purposes,
of which one is consent-based and the remainder can be viewed as exceptions to consent-based processing.

restriction in Article 6 could therefore roughly be considered a Natural Language Policy and be parsed into
AERBAC conditions. If the translation from article 6 to AERBAC conditions could be made on a one-to-

model such that the aspects of the solution that involves consent abide by these requirements.

1.
Personal information: Personal information is any information directly related to the identity of a
person. In the context of AERBAC, all personal information exists as data objects. Every object that is
deemed to contain personal information is subject to GDPR compliance requirements and thus, in extent, is
subject to being included in AERBAC’s access control checks to ensure that a consent is present. Therefore
it is important to deﬁne exactly what information qualiﬁes as being personal information. From this follows
that it is intuitive to enable the reference monitor to be able to distinguish every data object in the system as
either bound by consensual requirements or not. By deﬁnition, this dissertation refers to any object containing
personal information as a sensitive object35 By contrast, any object that does not contain personal information
and therefore is not covered by constraints imposed by GDPR, is referred to as a benign object. This is further
addressed in section 9.1.
2. (Data) Processing: In computer science, processing a data object is equivalent to applying operators
to the object, for instance read-, write- or execute-operations. In GDPR, processing data is a more abstract
term that does not concern itself with the speciﬁc computational operators that are applied to the object but
instead regards the contexts or purposes that the object may be processed for. For instance, in GDPR a given
object may have a permission to be processed for ’statistical analysis’-purposes with the intent to analyse client
traﬃc on a website. In this case, carrying out ’client traﬃc analysis’ may involve numerous computational
operations on the data, all of which are allowed as long as the operations are applied with the intent to carry
out the ’traﬃc analysis’ purpose. For this reason, it is not possible to translate GDPR purposes into a set of
corresponding computational operators. Instead, GDPR’s notion of consented purposes must be translated to
a domain that can be parsed by the reference monitor, in this case AERBAC. Therefore the approach is to
describe consented purposes by attributes.
Once the consented purposes are represented as attributes, the allowed processing of a data object in accordance
with GDPR can be ensured by restricting all36 computational operations on a sensitive object via the AERBAC
policies. These restrictions can be imposed by conditions that use attributes to describe the consented purposes
of the object and compare those attributes to the current processing purpose (current activity) of a subject.
While this might seem trivial due to already being presented in section 1.1, it is an important observation
related to the design-considerations when extending AERBAC with secondary processing prevention. How
consented purposes are translated into attributes is addressed in section 9.
As discussed in section 6.3, secondary processing or secondary use is the processing of data objects, containing
personal information, without the explicit consent given by the data owner, specifying that particular process
as an allowed processing of the data. In GDPR terms, secondary use implies violation of article 5 and 6,
since secondary processing is the antithesis of GDPR’s deﬁnition of lawful processing. In AERBAC, secondary
processing is implicitly prevented by restricting operations on sensitive objects by the conditions listed in article
6, Lawfulness of processing.

(ii) speciﬁc, (iii) informed and (iv) unambiguous.37 (i) freely given indicates that the data subject must not
be forced to give their consent. This is not a term with particular interest in the context of this dissertation.

Requirement Speciﬁcation
Prior to the thesis, a brief survey[30] was carried out in order to identify problem domains and their prospective
challenges40. The primary objective of [30] was to identify the problem-domains and challenges associated with
extending AERBAC for secondary use prevention, as well as giving a brief introduction to preliminary ideas
and solutions. The dissertation identiﬁed the following primary goals:
1 Annotation of an object with a consented purpose
2 Translation of the consented purpose into appropriate object-attributes
3 Deriving a subject’s current activity
4 Express a subject’s current activity as subject-attributes
5 Extend the policies with appropriate conditions matching subject activity- and object purpose-attributes.
This dissertation largely builds on the same ﬁve problem domains as [30] but slightly refactored:
Due to the close relation between the original problem domains one and two as well as between domain three and
four, these four domains have been concatenated to two domains. Problem domain one and two both relate
to consent and objects. These two domains are concatenated and denoted the ’Consent’-domain. Problem
domain three and four both relate to detecting current activities of a subject, which are concatenated and
denoted the ’Activity’-domain. The ﬁfth domain relates to designing policies (permissions and/or conditions)
that can prevent secondary use using attributes. The goals of this domain are unchanged but the domain
has been re-prioritised as the ﬁrst priority since the other domains become much easier to address once the
permissions and conditions have been deﬁned. This domain is denoted the ’Conditions’-domain. The three
new domains address the following:
1 Conditions-domain
– Deﬁning conditions and permissions such that the reference monitor can use AERBAC attributes
to prevent secondary use
2 Consent-domain
– Identiﬁcation of objects that need a consent, ie. objects that contain personal information
– Determining the purpose for which this personal information is collected
– Collecting the appropriate identiﬁed consents for each object
– Representing the collected consents in a way that enables translation from a consent into appro-
priate attributes
– Annotating the correct objects with the correct consent-attributes
3 Activity-domain
– Deﬁning the notion current activity
– Detecting a current activity of a subject
– Annotating the current activity as attributes to the subject
– Dynamically monitoring an annotated current activity
The reason for the reordering is that in order to design how objects and users are to be annotated with
attributes, it is of great relevance how the policies match up.
The problem can therefore be approached
from either direction: Either by (i) designing both an object-consent-solution and a current activity-solution
individually and have them ’meet in the middle’ by designing a policy that satisﬁes the outcome of the two
other domains by matching their attributes, or by (ii) specifying the policy and associated condition design, ie.
how secondary use is in practice enforced by the reference monitor, and then have each of the two other domains
match up against this. While both approaches are viable, specifying the policy ﬁrst and then determining how
to derive the correct attributes has been determined to be the best approach since the expected outcome of
the Consent- and Activity-domains rely on the policy- and condition-design. The three domains have been
sketched in ﬁgure 7.a where they have been related to the classical AC model in ﬁgure 2.a.

Policy and Condition Design for AERBAC
This section addresses how the policies and, in extent policy conditions, should be designed, in order to propose
a solution to the ﬁrst problem-domain, as speciﬁed in section 7. It is important to note that ultimately the
conditions used in the extended AERBAC for secondary use-prevention should incorporate all aspects of the
processing of data, as speciﬁed in Article 6, Lawfulness of processing and as discussed in section 6. For now,
the focus is mainly on deﬁning policies that are compliant with GDPR and with consent (Art. 6.1.(a)).
Note that this section relies on the AERBAC notation presented in section 5.3.
8.1
Comparing Purposes with Activities
Since the problem domain related to policy design is addressed ﬁrst, it is left completely up to the design
of the policy to specify the expected input that must be made available to the permissions and conditions.
In AERBAC’s case, the input is the available attributes in the system, and hence, this section can freely
specify the desired attribute-functions and determine the format of the corresponding attributes returned by
the functions. In turn, it is left up to the two other problem-domains to accommodate the requirements to the
attributes stipulated by this domain.
The goal of a secondary use-prevention policy based on Art 6.1.(a) is to be able to match the current activity
of a subject who has submitted an access request, with the consented purposes of a sensitive object that the
subject wants to access. Access should be granted if the current activity matches the consented purposes. For
now, assume that the Consent-domain can provide attributes that represent the consented purposes of objects,
denoted purpose-attributes, and that the Activity-domain can provide attributes that represent the current
activities of subjects, denoted activity-attributes.
Comparing activity- and purpose-attributes is a central
aspect of the design of the policies and should be subjected to further investigation:
The most straight forward approach to comparing the two attribute types, is to enforce the requirement that
each activity-attribute must have a directly comparable and corresponding purpose-attribute and vice versa.
This corresponds to a one-to-one relationship between the two attribute types. This implies the notion that
if an object can be consented for the purpose ’targeted ads’, then there must exist one and only one activity-
attribute that matches this purpose such that an object consented for only ’targeted ads’ can only be accessed
iﬀ41 the subject has the corresponding activity-attribute.
There are more advanced approaches than deﬁning a one-to-one relationship. It is easy to imagine a system in
which one activity may correspond to multiple consented purposes, and similarly, scenarios where one purpose
matches multiple activities. Other conventions could therefore be to allow many-to-one, one-to-many or even
more complex many-to-many relationships instead of the simple one-to-one relationship described above. These
relationships may open up for more powerful policies by being less restrictive, and have the ability to generalise
by deﬁning hierarchies to match the two attribute types more easily.
However, these approaches are also
signiﬁcantly more complex to manage and policy reviewing may become much less transparent since these
advanced relationships need to be taken into account. For this reason, the extended AERBAC model is, for
now, designed with one-to-one relationships between consent- and activity-attributes while it is noted that the
possibility to extend this approach further exists.
The assertion of equality between the object- and the subject-attributes, purpose and activity, is achieved
by comparing the attribute-values returned by their respective attribute-functions. For instance, an activity-
attribute named uAccounting should be directly compared to the purpose-attribute named oAccounting. This
comparison can in practice be carried out by the use of AERBAC’s atomic-type attribute, which returns an
atomic-value when the attribute-function is invoked. An atomic value can for instance be a string-value. In
order to enable equality-assertions, the value-ﬁeld can be used for the assertions, by adding the consented
purpose or current activity as a string value:42

While the use of atomic-type attributes as shown above can solve the ability to assert equality between purposes
and activities, there is a major caveat that needs to be taken into account:
Even if atomic-type attributes can support equality assertions, it is a problem that each attribute needs its
own attribute-function to retrieve the attribute-value from a given object. Following the example above, this
implies the presence of the functions: uAccounting() and oAccounting(). This quickly becomes infeasible at
scale, since not only does every purpose/consent-pair need two attribute functions deﬁned, but the policies,
and in extent, conditions that are used by the reference monitor, also need to explicitly include each purpose
and activity attribute by calling their respective functions. This means the policy reviewing can quickly grow
infeasible. To exemplify this, think of a scenario with two consented purposes research and ads for the object,
o, and the same two current activities for a subject, u. Using this scenario, the atomic-type approach would
require four attributes and four attribute functions:
oResearch(o) = (oResearch, research)
oAds(o) = (oAds, ads)
uResearch(u) = (uResearch, research)
uAds(u) = (uAds, ads)
(9)
The four attributes in eq. (9) all need to be included in the policies, along with any other consent and activity
also deﬁned in the system, in order to ensure that secondary use is prevented. It is easy to see that only a few
possible consented purposes would cause the policies to grow very quickly.
The alternative to atomic-type attributes, is the second attribute-type that AERBAC supports: set-type at-
tributes. set-attributes contain a set of atomic values such that when invoking the corresponding attribute
function, a set of atomic values is returned. Just like for atomic-type, this dissertation uses the notation that
attribute-functions used for set-type attributes return a tuple consisting of the two elements: (name,values[]).
Set-type attributes can be utilised by deﬁning a single attribute that contains all consented purposes of a
given object as a set of values, oPurposes, and a single attribute that does the same for all current activities,
uActivities. This approach means that only two attribute-functions are ever needed; one OATT, oPurposes(),
and one UATT, uActivities(). These functions return the entire set of consented purposes and current activities
of an object or subject respectively as string values.
Using set-type attributes instead of atomic-type, any number of consented purposes and current activity pairs
can be reduced to only two attributes and their corresponding attribute-functions needing to be deﬁned,
regardless of the total number of purposes and activities. Following the example shown in eq. (9), these two
attribute-function pairs can be reduced to:
oPurposes(o) = (purposes, [research, ads])
uActivities(u) = (activities, [research, ads])
(10)
As shown in the example in eq. (10), the approach of collecting all purposes and activities in a single object- and
a single subject-attribute respectively is very powerful, as it allows policy conditions to be constructed using set-
logic and set operators, which simpliﬁes the process of condition-speciﬁcation vastly. The conditions can now
be constructed by calling the two functions oPurposes() and uActivities()43 and comparing the elements in the
returned sets of atomic values by asserting equality using ﬁrst order logic, string comparison and set-operators.

interesting consideration is to determine at which point the comparison between oPurposes and uActivities
should take place. Recall that a permission consists of an operation followed by an object expression. Since

8.2
Extending the Global Condition
The limitation of cGlobal in eq. (15) is that it does not distinguish between sensitive and benign objects.
Since benign objects do not need a consent, there would be no consented purposes annotated a benign object,
meaning that oPurposes(o) = null. Invoking (15) when oPurposes(o) = null would cause the assertion to fail
and thus either invoke an exception or outright deny access to the object in question. Hence, a convention
regarding benign objects is needed.
One solution could be to use the universal-set operator, U, and annotate all benign objects with oPurposes(o)
= U. In this way, all values except ∅for uActivities would satisfy cGlobal in (15). This is not a satisfactory
solution since: (i) it enforces that any operation on purely benign objects requires activity detection to assign
a value to uActivities such that uActivities(u) ̸= ∅, (ii) it requires all benign objects to be identiﬁed and
annotated such that oPurposes(o) = U, which could involve a massive amount of benign objects, which would
be redundant and ﬁnally (iii) it would mean that cGlobal is invoked for all objects, including all benign objects
which is a resource waste since evaluating cGlobal for benign objects is redundant. For these reasons, it would
be much more eﬃcient to detect if the object is benign at an earlier point and skip further evaluation if this
is the case45, without having to have manually identify and annotate benign objects.

Skipping evaluation of cGlobal can be achieved in two ways: (a) Introduce a check at the permission-level,
which acts as a guard towards evaluating any conditions including cGlobal or (b) introduce the guard at
condition level.
(a) Permission guard:
Recall that AERBAC uses permissions that in turn have conditions, as presented in section 5.2. This means
that a check could be introduced in all permissions such that every permission checks if the given object is
sensitive and if so, invoke all the regular conditions of that permission in addition to cGlobal. If the object is
benign, only the original conditions of that permission are invoked, skipping cGlobal.
In practice, distinguishing between benign and sensitive objects can be achieved in two ways: (i) Introduce
a new Boolean-style attribute that distinguishes sensitive objects, oSensitive = true or false. This attribute
then needs to be annotated to all objects that can be evaluated in AERBAC or (ii) distinguish objects by
the convention that the presence of oPurposes implies sensitivity and hence that when oPurposes = null

8.4
AERBAC Condition Design Summary
The following is a recap of the solution that has been derived and discussed through section 8:
• The activities currently performed by a subject, u, can be expressed as string-values in a set-type attribute
such that:
uActivities(u) = (activities,A[]), where A[] is the set of all current activities.
• The purposes for which a data owner has given consent for a given object, o, can be expressed as string
values in a set-type attribute such that:
oPurposes(o) = (purposes,P[]), where P[] = the set of all consented purposes.
44
==PAGE:49==
• For each possible element in activities[] there is a corresponding possible element in purposes[] and vice
versa, ie. a one-to-one relationship
• oPurposes(o) is expected to be callable for all objects, following the conventions in table 5.
• AERBAC syntax is extended with short-circuiting ’logical AND’- and ’logical OR’-operators: && and ||
It is a strict requirement that the language implementing AERBAC can support these short-circuiting
evaluations and that evaluation is carried out from left to right.
• If all the above stated conditions are met, the global condition, cGlobal in eq. (16) can be appended to
all existing conditions as described in section 8.3
This solution suﬃces for cGlobal to enforce either the presence of a consent for all the current activities of the
requesting subject or that the requested objects are benign for access to an object to be granted. This covers
the goal of compliance with secondary use prevention as stated in GDPR Art. 6.1.(a).
Note that the choice of using one-to-one relations between consent and activity and directly comparing by
asserting equality is a simple solution, but it has the advantages of creating easier policy reviewing and
gives the opportunity the create a very simple global condition, cGlobal rather than more complex solutions.
Additionally, this solution can easily be extended to accommodate more abstract processing purposes and
activities, by substituting the attribute values in sets by for instance process IDs so that very speciﬁc processes
can be granted access to a corresponding consent. This correlates to the philosophy that, like RBAC, it is a
goal to keep policy reviewing as fundamentally simple as possible, which is achieved by introducing a single
global policy.

9
Objects and Consent
This section addresses the Consent-domain, as deﬁned in section 7, ie. exploring possible solutions on how
to associate objects with a consent in attribute-format that satisﬁes cGlobal, as presented in eq. (16). This
includes the steps from the submission of a consent-form by a data subject to interpretation and translation
of the consent-form into the corresponding attributes and ﬁnally annotating the relevant objects with the
consent-attributes.
In order to annotate an object with a consented purpose, it must be determined which objects need a consent,
and how consent is deﬁned and represented in a way that can be parsed by the model and ﬁnally, how consent
is gathered from the data subject and associated with the correct objects as attributes.
9.1
Objects Needing Consent
As already touched upon in previous sections, one important consideration is the distinction of sensitive and
benign objects. Following the classiﬁcation of an object as either sensitive or benign, it must be ensured that the
object is compliant with the conventions presented in table 5 to ensure that the objects are handled properly
by cGlobal. The condition-design outlined in section 8.4 builds on positive identiﬁcation of sensitive objects
by stating that all sensitive objects must be tagged with the oPurposes-attribute. Note that this does not
mean that access control should only be applied to sensitive objects. The distinction between sensitive and
benign objects means that the AERBAC model should enforce regular access control, as it normally would, to
all objects, but in addition to the regular access control checks, also enforce the condition that a valid consent
must be given for access to be granted when the object in question is ﬂagged as sensitive. In other words,
the dashed box in ﬁgure 7.b should be invoked for both benign and sensitive objects, while objects ﬂagged
as sensitive should also be subjected to the secondary use-condition that ensures that the correct consent is
present and that it matches the user’s current activity.
9.2
Identifying Sensitive Objects
Identifying sensitive objects is a matter of determining whether a given object contains personal data. This
can roughly be done in two ways: (i) dynamical analysis of each object in the systems databases or (ii)
predetermining object-types that are expected to be sensitive by analysing the APIs.
This dissertation does not propose methods for identifying objects as sensitive because any method will depend
greatly on the speciﬁc implementation. Instead, the following assumption is made:
Any organisation subject to GDPR is assumed to have carried out analysis to determine the sensitive objects
in their system, since GDPR mandates that the organisation is aware of the data they collect and must be able
to produce consent for the collected data. Note that the collection of personal data can be covered by other
lawful processing purposes that do not involve consent, as stated in GDPR Art. 6, however, even if subject
to alternatives to consent-based processing, the organisation must be able to document why they do not need
consent, and as such, it is assumed that even under such circumstances, an organisation is aware of all sensitive
data in their system.
While it is assumed that all sensitive data is readily identiﬁed, the data might not be classiﬁed in a way that
makes it directly translatable to oPurposes-attribute values that can be used by the extended AERBAC model.
For the purposes of this thesis, it is therefore assumed that any organisation seeking to adapt the extended
AERBAC model has already identiﬁed all sensitive objects in some way. Exactly how objects are identiﬁed
as sensitive is not relevant in this dissertation. The interesting part is determining how to standardise the
classiﬁcations already made by each organisation such that the extended AERBAC model can be applied
as universally as possible by proposing how the identiﬁed sensitive object can be correctly annotated an
oPurposes-attribute.
In extent, it is assumed that the organisation is already able to link a valid consent with each object, presumably
by referencing a consent in each sensitive object or by referencing each sensitive object in each consent,
46
==PAGE:51==
depending on the associativity in the database. This assumption is based on the GDPR requirement that
the organisation must be able to document a consent or a legal reason for the absence of a consent for all
sensitive objects. Utilising the association between each sensitive object and a consent is therefore assumed to
be possible in all cases where an organisation is already compliant prior to adapting to AERBAC.
Consider the conventions in table 5, which state that any benign object is identiﬁed by the absence of the
oPurposes-attribute. This means that any object that has not been checked for sensitivity won’t have oPurposes
annotated either, and hence, by default, all unclassiﬁed objects are assumed to be benign. The advantage of
this is that only the objects considered to be sensitive need to be modiﬁed to adapt to the extended AERBAC
model and benign objects do therefore not introduce any additional work. The goal is therefore that each
object which is identiﬁed as sensitive is annotated with an oPurposes-attribute. If the attribute-value is set
to ∅, the object is classiﬁed as sensitive, but with no valid consents added and thus would not be accessible in
the extended AERBAC model. It is therefore desired to utilise the presumed link between the object and the
existing consent and deﬁne consent is translated into oPurposes-values. This is addressed further in section
9.3.
To exemplify how an organisation may classify objects as sensitive, consider the following imagined case:
Case: Privacy Inc
The enterprise, Privacy Inc is a privatised organisation who needs to collect some personal information
from its clients, in order to provide their phone text messaging-services. Speciﬁcally, Privacy Inc requires
the client’s name and their phone number.
Clients access Privacy Inc’s services via a web-browser,

In terms of identifying sensitive objects, this case is straight forward: All data (name and phone number)
submitted via the web-form by the data subject are identiﬁed to be sensitive and therefore must be associated
with a consent. In other cases, identifying sensitive objects might not be as trivial. For instance, if the input
is unrestricted text where the client can submit free text, such as a help desk support ticket. A support-ticket
could potentially contain sensitive information, but it won’t always be the case. In these circumstances, the
safest option is to ﬂag any potentially sensitive input-ﬁeld as sensitive, and provide the option for staﬀto
remove the ﬂag upon manual inspection at a later point, should the need arise. One can then ensure that
consent is always gathered by asking the client to consent before they can submit a support ticket, regardless
of it being actually sensitive or not. It should be noted that the collection of the consent is addressed in more
detail in section 9.3.
In conclusion, when it comes to perimeter detection of data, the solution is to specify the input ﬁelds for data
that can be submitted as being sensitive and ensuring that a consent is given and provided along with the
data. The submission of a consent is further addressed in section 9.3.
In many cases regarding data collection, an initial consent pertains to an ongoing collection of data rather
than a single submission. This could be the collection of session-data using cookies, for which a consent might
be given once while subsequent data collection could occur every time the user connects to the web-service. If
this is the case, a more dynamic approach to object identiﬁcation is needed: A session identiﬁer can be used
to track subsequent data collection and link it to an initial consent which is still assumed to be present for the
data to have been collected in the ﬁrst place.

• Objects are classiﬁed as either sensitive or benign
• Organisations are assumed to have identiﬁed all sensitive objects in their system
• The original classiﬁcation is either directly stored with the object as attributes or variables or stored by
referencing a registry of sensitive objects by listing the object identiﬁers
• For all sensitive objects covered by Art 6.1.(a), it is mandatory that a valid consent is present and it is
assumed that a link to the correct consent exists
• To accommodate the extended AERBAC model, classiﬁcation as sensitive is implied by the presence of
the oPurposes-attribute, which must adhere to the conventions stipulated in 5

9.3
Collection of Consents Using Consent-forms
The collection of a consent is very speciﬁc depending on the particular implementation and APIs involved. For
this reason, practical aspects of consent-collection are considered out of scope of this dissertation. Albeit, it is
still of importance to understand some of the more abstract aspects of consent-collection, and for this reason,
this sub-section will address this issue by outlining a basic format of representing a consent-form that can be
signed and submitted by the data subject. In order to scope the collection of consent and align it with the
other questions raised in section 9, this section will primarily revolve around collection of consent on the basis
of the case, Privacy Inc, deﬁned in section 9.1, ie. a collection of consent by submitting a web-form with some
predeﬁned purposes.
Note that it is an important goal to ensure that the design of the AERBAC consent-form is as easy as possible
to translate into attributes that can be used by AERBAC. For this reason a simple data-structure is likely the
most optimal approach.
As stated in section 6.1, consent must be:
• Freely given - the user needs to actively perform some operation in order to submit a consent
• Explicit, and easy to understand - the purposes must be explicitly stated in a natural language
• Informed - the consent-form must list ALL of the consented purposes.
Even though the above statements regarding consent are the legal requirements, there should be a clear
correlation between the legal requirements and how consent is deﬁned in AERBAC. In order to make the above
requirements to consent-collection more tangible, consider that people who surf the web are often presented
with consent-forms when connecting to new sites. Most websites require a consent to their cookie-policy and
consent to how they gather data from the session. It is important to note that there are two segments of GDPR
that can be contradictory in this context: For the consent to be informed, the user must be told exactly what
they consent to, implying that the consent-form must be detailed and explicit. But on the other hand, the
information regarding the consent must be explicit and clear, which implies that the consent-form should not
be cluttered with too much information and make it diﬃcult for the user to understand what they consent to.
This duality can easily become contradictory if too much data is collected for too many purposes.
The way many websites get around this contradictory issue, is to present a brief overview in simple, nat-
ural language with only a few very generalised purposes. The consent-form then gives the user the option
to review each general purpose in detail and micromanage the speciﬁc aspects of the consent.
See for in-
stance the following consent form, which at the time of writing, is presented to the user upon connecting to
https://www.speedguide.net, shown in ﬁgure 9.a46. As can be seen from this ﬁgure, the initial consent-form
is quite vague and not very informative, and it relies on the user’s own interest in their privacy to actively
select to review the details of the consent by clicking ’Show Purposes’ which provides the detailed list. This is
a typical format of consent-forms used for web applications due to it covering both the goal of being informed
and simple by dividing the consent into two segments.
In accordance with this approach of a segmented consent-form, by deﬁnition, I denote the simple and overall
statements of purpose as general purposes. General purposes are vague and can be applied widely. Likewise,
purposes that are speciﬁc and have a ﬁner granularity than general purposes, I denote as atomic purposes. A
general purpose can be thought of as representing a set of atomic purposes and an atomic purpose must always
be associated with a general purpose.
It is important to note, that for many consent-forms in production, the general- and atomic-purposes in the
same consent-form does not always directly correlate. For instance, on speedguide.net’s consent-form, there

It should be noted that some general purposes might have the same, or very similar atomic purposes in each
their sets of corresponding atomic purposes. This is an interesting consideration since it gives rise to a potential
conﬂict: If two general purposes both have the same atomic purpose, what happens if a data subject agrees to
one general purpose but not to the other? Essentially this would mean that the data subject both agrees and
disagrees to the same atomic purpose. In this case, what choice takes precedence? There are multiple aspects
to this, where the simplest could be to extend the form with a legal note stating that conﬂicting purposes
will, either favor the positive or the negative consent by default, depending on design. However, in a more
intelligently designed system, one might desire to distinguish between the two atomic purposes. Consider for
instance if the two general purposes: personalised ads and user experience, these could map to multiple atomic
purposes, of which they for example could have one in common: location, ie. the geographical location of the
user’s device or access-point. If the user consents to user experience including all related atomic purposes but
does not consent to personalised ads, the atomic purpose ’location’ is indeed conﬂicted, but only as long as
the atomic purpose is considered out of its context. If the atomic purpose is kept in relation to its originating
general purpose, it makes complete sense to state that ’location’ may be tracked for ’user experience’-purposes,
but not for ’personalised ads’-purposes. This gives rise to an important deﬁnition in the consent-form design:
While general purposes each correlate to a sub-set of atomic purposes, each atomic purpose must always be
related to its originating general purpose in a many-to-one manner. Ie. once a consent-form is submitted, one

involves the ability to uniquely identify each atomic purpose: Instead of one ’location’ atomic purpose, use two
atomic purposes ’location for user experience’ and ’location for personalised ads’. This approach eliminates the
risk of conﬂicting atomic purposes, but also risks dramatically increasing the number of atomic purposes and
their complexity. In conclusion, in order to avoid conﬂicting atomic purposes, the consent-form must either be
kept intact by always associating each atomic purpose with its parent general purpose or each atomic purpose
must be speciﬁc enough to exist uniquely in one general purpose.

a sub-set of associated atomic purposes. The user can either consent to all the general purposes collectively,

consent-form. The consent-form could be structured in the form of sets, [], of purposes following this format:47

which are collected along with the consent. In this way, each atomic purpose lists the objects that are covered
by that purpose. By deﬁnition, objects listed inside atomic purposes in this way are sensitive objects. The
reason that each atomic purpose lists its associated objects is due to the fact that this makes it signiﬁcantly
easier to determine which objects that should be annotated with consent-attributes and also to link the correct
consent-form to its associated objects. Obviously, this adds a constraint that generating each form takes a bit
more work, but it should be negligible since ensuring GDPR consent should already involve the identiﬁcation
of all data that is collected, this format thus only states that the identiﬁed data to be collected should also
be explicitly stated in the consent-form.

desirable to either hide the object identiﬁers in the presentation layer or to create a function that can fetch each

considerations that relate to the speciﬁc front-end design of a particular implementation and are therefore
left open for now. The key note is that the AERBAC back-end can expect to be provided consent-forms as

sensitive data should have at least one consent-form template associated with it. This means that since most
organisations are likely to have a multitude of diﬀerent input sources, they are likely to need multiple diﬀerent
consent-form templates deﬁned and managed.

source. This means that consent-forms are distinguished by whether they are templates or a signed instance
of a template. Since the user can manage their consents, a signed consent-form does not need to contain all
the purposes of the template. This means that a signed consent-form represents a subset of the purposes of its

In order to make the process of attribute-annotation more transparent, the attributes are to be annotated by
external referencing. That is, via an external registry48 that lists the sensitive objects by their object identiﬁers
mapping a set of oPurposes-values. This is done by storing a table which uses the object-identiﬁers as primary
key and then a column for the oPurposes-attribute with the corresponding rows being the attribute-values.
This table is populated by parsing the consent-form which links consented purposes to objects. This task is
carried out by the CAT module which is presented in the next section. The attribute registry is stored in a
database external to the CAT module and can be used for both providing a quick overview of the purposes
that have been consented to by the data subject as well as help identifying potentially sensitive objects that
need a consent (empty attribute-value ﬁeld). The table is denoted the oPurposes-registry.

objects that have been identiﬁed as sensitive but do not have a valid consent, should have (purposes,[]) anno-

The Current Activity of a Subject
This section focuses on the Activity-domain, as presented in section 7. Initially, the notion of a ’current activity’
is deﬁned. Once the current activity of a subject is determined, it is of interest to consider how the current
activity is represented as attributes so that they match the oPurposes-attributes that are deﬁned in section 9.
Finally, it is addressed how a change in the current activity of a subject can be monitored and handled.
10.1
Deﬁning Current Activity
With the goal to match the current activities of subjects with consented purposes of objects in mind, a current
activity can be perceived as any activity performed by a subject which involves objects that require consent.
In other words, any permissible action in the system that would involve sensitive objects should be associated
with a current activity and hence, ’activities’ involving purely benign objects are not of interest and those
activities are therefore omitted from further study in this section.
An activity in the context of computing could intuitively be translated into computational operations. Viewing
activities as operators can be used to bridge the gap between activities and AERBAC policies because AERBAC
permissions uses operators. Imagine a solution in which activities are translated into operations and thereby
become easy to constrain via AERBAC permissions. However, this solution is NOT adequate as the deﬁnition
of activities is not that trivial: An activity involving data is the purpose for which the subject is processing
that data. ie. activities should be viewed as processing purposes50. A processing purpose can include all
combinations of computational operations on the data involved and thus, there does not exist a direct link
between an activity and a particular set of operations. For instance, an activity could be to fetch phone-
numbers and names of a set of clients from a database in order to ring them and perform a survey. In this case
the read-operator could be used to access the numbers and names and the purpose for which the read-operation
is carried out on the data is ’to ring clients and perform a survey’. Even if the read-operator is a part of the task
’ring client’, it does not mean that the read-operator can be viewed as an indication that the current activity
is to ring clients, because the operator is not exclusively related to that task. However, the knowledge that the
task of ringing clients includes the read-operator could be used is a part of a pattern matching approach in
order to detect the current activity of a subject. Pattern matching is part of the more automated approaches
to activity detection addressed in section 10.3, and enhancements using pattern-recognition are also brieﬂy
touched upon in section 10.3.7 (Customising the static mapping).
As stipulated in the design of the global condition, cGlobal, it is a requirement that the current activities of
a subject must ultimately be translated into uActivities-attribute values. In turn, these values must directly
correlate to oPurposes-attribute values. It is interesting to investigate a direct approach to deﬁning the total set
of current activities within a given system: The translation from current activity to the uActivities-attribute,
is the most straight forward if the current activities are derived in the same way as the oPurposes-values
were: by parsing the consent-form. When the consent-form templates are designed (see section 9.3), the data
controller must specify both the general- and atomic-purposes that make up the consent-form which in turn
are translated into oPurposes-attribute values. Since the goal is to match activities with purposes, it is quite
intuitive to simply make each general- and atomic-consented purpose equivalent into a corresponding general-
and atomic-activity. For instance, the atomic purpose ’ring the customer’ could just as easily be viewed as
a current activity. Therefore, if ’ring the customer’ is a consented purpose, it should also be considered as a
potential current activity and thus be included in the set of possible ’current activities’. This approach has
three advantages:
1 Specifying all permissible ’current activities’ is done concurrently and implicitly as the consent-forms
are being designed, ie. no extra work is required in addition to the work already required to specify the
consent-forms.
2 Since the list of general- and atomic-purposes is also considered the list of potential ’current activities’
there is a direct one-to-one correlation between each purpose and an activity, thus, the ability to match
purposes and activities in AERBAC conditions becomes relatively trivial.
50Although processing purposes is a more accurate term in this context, this dissertation uses current activity instead,
in order to not confuse a current processing purpose with a consent for a particular processing purpose.

3 The one-to-one matching of purposes and activities ensures that there always exists at least one activity
that can access a sensitive object as long as the sensitive object has at least one consented purpose. If
this was not ensured, there could exist ’dead ends’ where a sensitive object only has consented purposes
for which no corresponding current activity exists, which would essentially make it impossible to retrieve
the object through AERBAC’s reference monitor.51
The reason that Consent-forms suﬃce for constructing the list of all possible ’current activities’ is due to
the fact that any activity that does not involve sensitive objects is not relevant for this study and since the
consent-forms must cover all processing purposes for sensitive objects, they also cover all relevant activities in

The practice of categorising consented purposes by general and atomic can be mimicked for activities in the

10.2
Concurrent Activities
Allowing multiple simultaneous activities in a single session is inherently supported by the design of the global
condition, cGlobal in its current format, eq. (16). Because cGlobal works by checking for the membership of all
elements in uActivities in oPurposes. In this way, a subject is allowed to process an object if all the subject’s
current activities are permissible for the accessed object.
The reason cGlobal is designed with concurrent activities in mind is to achieve greater ﬂexibility for activity
detection: Activities are classiﬁed as either general or atomic of which general activities each represents a set
of atomic activities. The reason general activities are desired as the current activity is because the shorter
the list of possible activities is to choose from, the less work is required to determine the current activity of a
subject. Hence activity detection becomes much easier if it can be carried out at the level of general activities
rather than atomic activities, which are likely much more diﬃcult to distinguish and exist in greater numbers.
Because each general activity in practice is simply a placeholder for a set of atomic activities, the only way
to allow a general activity to be assigned as a subject’s current activity, is to allow a set of atomic activities
values in the uActivities-attribute and therefore uActivities must be a set-type. In conclusion, allowing general
activities as the current activity implies the support of multiple values in uActivities ie. support of concurrent
activities.
With the notion that concurrent activity support is desired, cGlobal was designed with this in mind, but it
quickly becomes apparent that the intersection-approach used in the earlier iterations of cGlobal 54 does not
suﬃce:
If a subject, Alice, is determined to have activities X and Y concurrently: uActivities(Alice)=[X,Y] and Alice
requests access to an object, o, which is consented for purpose X only: oPurposes(o)=[X], the intersection-
version of cGlobal would grant Alice access to o, but if the activity detection is not able to distinguish X and
Y, then there is no way to check if Alice is actually only processing o for purpose X. She could easily also
be processing with purpose Y. In order to avoid this issue, cGlobal checks for a full membership, ⊆, of all
uActivities-values and hence in the above example, cGlobal (16) would not allow Alice access to o.
54Cf. eq. (11).
59
==PAGE:64==
However, allowing concurrent activities in this way runs the risk of severely limiting the amount of objects that
can be accessed: The more concurrent activities a subject has, the fewer objects will have a set of purposes
that satisﬁes cGlobal. This could be troublesome if for instance a subject is carrying out some processing that
requires having access to all objects of a particular type, this could for instance be to carry out statistical
analysis. If Alice has uActivities(Alice) = [X,Y,Statistics] then Alice is restricted in the sense that all objects
with oPurposes() = [Statistics], [X,Statistics] or [Y,Statistics] would not be accessible since cGlobal requires
consent for both X, Y and Statistics at the same time. This is potentially troublesome in the sense that Alice’s
statistical analysis would then miss a series of objects. This can be handled in multiple ways; Alice could for
instance be notiﬁed that a series of objects where denied because of concurrent activities blocking access and
be asked to remove X and Y from her list of current activities to obtain access to all the objects. Another
approach is to use a principle that this dissertation denotes as ’session narrowing’. This principle is devised to
dynamically reduce the set of values in uActivities as a subject obtains access to objects and works by applying
the following operation following every successful access request of a subject, u, to an object, o:
uCurrentActivities(u) = uCurrentActivities(u) ∩oConsentedPurposes(o)
(17)
While session narrowing can be used to reduce the number of concurrent activities of a subject as the subject
accesses more restricted objects, this method does not suﬃce when activity detection is factored in55: If the
detection of a user’s current activities is at the resolution of general activities, and hence the system can’t
distinguish atomic activities, at least not those belonging to the same general activity, session narrowing does
not provide any security in terms of secondary use prevention:
If uActivities(Alice) = [X,Y] and Alice requests access to o with oPurposes(o)=[X] and cGlobal is redeﬁned
to using ∩, Alice would be granted access to o. If session narrowing is then applied: uActivities(Alice) = [X].
Since narrowing is applied following the successful request, Alice can now only access objects consented for X
but if the activity detection can’t distinguish whether Alice is performing X or Y then, even if narrowing sets
uActivities(Alice) = [X], there is no way to verify that Alice is not actually performing activity Y. And hence,
session narrowing would only be symbolic but not actually enforce anything.
The conclusion regarding session narrowing is that even if concurrent activities are disallowed or at least
restricted using session narrowing, the ability to detect and distinguish activities from one another is the
absolute bottleneck and the granularity of the detection mechanisms thus dictates the granularity at which
secondary processing can be prevented.
It is for this reason that it does not suﬃce to use the intersection version of cGlobal and the reason that
it makes sense to check for the partial ordering uActivities ⊆oPurposes instead.
In this way, even if the
detection of activities can’t distinguish activity X from Y, as long as all accessed objects can be processed
for both purposes, the AERBAC model does not have to care about which activity is in fact applied to the
object. In a sense, this is a semi-blind access mechanism, because it is able to distinguish activities at the
general-level while it can’t be trusted to distinguish atomic activities, at least not those belonging to the same
general activity. It is therefore blind when distinguishing individual atomic activities.
As already mentioned, restricting access using the ⊆-operator approach risks limiting the number of accessible
objects, and hence, in systems where access to all objects with one speciﬁc consent are required, it is recom-
mended to only have the activity that matches that consent active. Session narrowing could be applied to
achieve this or the use of concurrent activities might be limited in some other ways such as only allowing spe-
ciﬁc combinations of concurrent activities etc. Therefore concurrent activities should be viewed as an optional
extra.
10.3
Detection of a Subject’s Current Activity
In section 10.1 it was determined how the Task list should be designed and used to list the collection of all
possible current activities in a system. This section addresses how a given subject may have determined which
of the activities from the task list, that they are actually performing, such that the detected activities can be
expressed as the subject uActivities-values.

There are many ways to detect a subject’s current activity. In order to order these, this thesis distinguishes the
diﬀerent methods based on the level of automation by dividing the methods into three categories. The methods
vary in degrees of complexity in terms of implementation with the trade-oﬀbeing the degree of automated
detection versus required human interaction. The methods are:
1 Manual
The user is asked to manually state what their current activity is
2 Static Mapping
Predeﬁned mappings of certain processes and patterns to speciﬁc activities are statically implemented
to infer the user’s activity
3 Dynamic Mapping
Techniques such as Machine Learning or neural networks are used to detect the user’s activity.
As addressed in the scope-section 1.2, dynamic mapping is too comprehensive to addresses properly within this
thesis. In this regard, recall that the overall goal of this thesis is to investigate how AERBAC can be extended
to accommodate secondary use prevention, with this in mind, exactly how a current activity is detected is a
secondary goal that needs to be satisﬁed in order to provide AERBAC with some of the attributes that it
needs to enforce secondary use prevention. For this reason, it is deemed that, for now, a less complex solution
is satisfactory while it must be noted that future work could easily involve adding extra modules with the
capability to dynamically detect activities and provide these as attributes to AERBAC.
10.3.1
The Manual Approach:
The manual approach is the most simple and could for instance be to prompt the user by displaying a list
of ’possible activities’ ie. the Task list, and ask the user to pick one or more activities from the list. This
approach has the following advantages (+) and disadvantages (÷):
+ Accurate - the system does not need any form of guesswork when determining the current activity
+ Simple - extremely easy to implement and maintain
+ Non-refutability - the manual selection can ’commit’ a user to agreeing to them performing a speciﬁc
activity. This can be held against them should malicious or secondary-use behaviour be detected at
which point they can’t refuse, due to them having speciﬁed their current activity explicitly themselves
÷ (Human) Error-prone - Easy to circumvent by either intentionally or unintentionally selecting a ’wrong’
activity
÷ Intrusive - it requires the user to manually choose from a potentially large list
÷ Insecure - there is no way to verify that the user is truthful about their stated current activities, this
is somewhat mitigated by logging and the non-refutability advantage, but can still prove troublesome if
a restrictive conﬁguration is desired
÷ Static - no continuous detection is carried out that would enable the system to detect a change in
activity unless the system periodically prompts the user to verify their current activity which would be
very intrusive.
The disadvantages are too great to perceive this simple approach as a viable solution, considering that it has
been identiﬁed that it is a goal for the extended AERBAC solution to minimise intrusiveness56. Furthermore,
it is identiﬁed in section 10.4 that dynamic reevaluation of the current activity in a session with access to
sensitive objects is required to avoid accessing an object with one activity and then switching activity and
process the object under the new activity.
56See the Usability-requirement listed in section 7.

10.3.2
The Static Mapping Approach:
An alternative to the manual approach is to use static mapping.
Before exploring this approach, it is of
relevance to consider the data available that can be used to determine a current activity using static mapping.
Recall that access requests in AERBAC are evaluated by considering the session through which a subject
requests access to one or more objects. By having the subject making their session available to the reference
monitor when submitting an access request, AERBAC’s reference monitor can evaluate:
• The attributes of the user that owns the session
• The attributes of any objects already granted to the session
• The list of processes that are currently active in the session.
In addition to the above data, the reference monitor can also access environment attributes that are externally
accessed relative to the session, however, for now, they are considered irrelevant due to the focus being to
detect the user’s current activity within the session, which the environment is not likely to reveal much about.
Note that in advanced scenarios, environmental attributes might be able to contribute to activity detection
since certain tasks could be performed from certain physical locations such as a server-room. In such cases,
using dynamic activity detection, it would be possible to determine certain patterns in environment attributes
such as location or what day of the month that salaries are usually computed etc. These patterns could be
used to contribute to a more accurate and advanced detection of activities. However, for now, the focus is
narrowed to only consider the session and the data available using the session.
Of the data available in a given session, I argue that what is likely to reveal the most about what the user is
currently doing, is the processes that are active in the user’s session. Even if it was determined that the user-
or object-attributes were more crucial in order to determine the current activity, it is the active processes that
mandate what types of processing is actually available to the user within the session. For instance, if the subject
and the subject’s accessed objects were used to determine that the user’s current activity is Accounting but
the only active process in the session is the Solitaire application, then there can be no ’Accounting’ processing
going on, since solitaire can’t perform any meaningful operations on accounting data objects.
Hence, the
active processes are assumed to be both signiﬁcant indicators of the current activities in a session as well as the
gate-keepers of what potential processing purposes that are available in the session. This leads to the notion
that considering the processes in a session can give a strong indication of the current activities or the intended
activities in the session.
A static mapping approach can be to predetermine speciﬁc combinations of processes in a session that corre-
sponds (maps) to speciﬁc activities. If all activities can be uniquely identiﬁed in all scenarios, this mapping can
be used to automatically detect the current activity within a session. The advantages (+) and disadvantages
(÷) of detecting the current activities by purely static mapping are:
+ Automatic (non-intrusive) - in optimal conditions, no human interaction is required
+ Dynamic57 - if non-intrusive, every change in a session can invoke a renewed evaluation of the current
activity
÷ Infeasible - exhaustively mapping all activities can be comprehensive both initially and to maintain
÷ Rigid - A static map can be very inﬂexible to accommodate the individual needs of each member of
staﬀas well as changing situations and procedures
÷ Inaccurate - unless the mapping is extremely exhaustively designed, this approach is prone to error.
However, the accuracy increases with the number of custom and speciﬁc-purpose applications used in
the organisation. Larger organisations are therefore assumed to generally achieve greater accuracy.
It is clear that many of the advantages and disadvantages of static mapping are complementary to those of
manual determination of a current activity. However, the advantages of static mapping rely on a lot of work

carried out to generate the mapping and to maintain it. Without proper maintenance, a static mapping would
quickly seize to be beneﬁcial and instead become inaccurate and risk blocking legitimate access requests.
A note on the granularity of detecting a current activity:
As described in section 10.2, cGlobal supports concurrent activities within a session in order to allow for a
session to have its associated uActivities-attribute equal to a general activity rather than at the level of atomic
activities. This is advantageous since it simpliﬁes the detection of a current activity if the detection is made
at the level of general activities rather than atomic activities. The advantage is both due to the list of general
activities likely being signiﬁcantly shorter58 than the list of atomic activities, and that most likely general
activities are signiﬁcantly easier to distinguish from one another. For instance, the atomic activities: ’Research
project A’ and ’Research project B’ might involve exactly the same processes, tools, methodologies and staﬀ,
but the data involved may have been gathered over two separate periods of time and therefore consent may
have been given to either one or the other project separately. Automatically detecting whether a subject is
performing one or the other project might be near impossible59 and therefore it is very beneﬁcial to detect
activities at a higher abstraction level such as at the level of general activities. For this reason, static mapping
carried out at the level of general activities would make the process of generating the mapping signiﬁcantly
easier and also likely increase accuracy due to general activities being more distinctive from one another.
If static mapping is used for detecting the general activity of a session, it implies that the granularity of this
approach alone becomes relatively coarse and relies on some sort of manual decision making in regards to
the individual atomic activities associated with the detected general activity. This leads to the notion that
the combination of the two approaches could potentially yield a hybrid approach that, in a similar way to
how AERBAC combines RBAC and ABAC, mitigates some of the disadvantages of each of the individual
approaches.
An important reason why static mapping is viable is due to the fact that organisations intended to use the
extended AERBAC model are deﬁned to be large enterprises, as deﬁned in the scope, section 1.2.
Large
organisations are assumed to use a fair amount of custom or speciﬁc-purpose software, which means that the
ability to distinguish activities on the basis of active processes become viable. If an organisation primarily use
general-purpose applications, the ability to distinguish activities by the active processes in a session diminishes
and the viability of static mapping is drastically reduced. This consideration is also addressed in section 12.9.
10.3.3
Combining Static Mapping and Manual Selection
In a hybrid approach, static mapping is carried out in order to estimate the most likely general current activity
and rank the Task list accordingly. The ranked Task list is subsequently presented to the user, asking them
to conﬁrm what their general activity is and allow them to specify any speciﬁc atomic activities subsequently
related to the chosen general activity. Compared to the advantages and disadvantages of the two individual
methods, the hybrid has the following properties:60
• Intrusiveness - the hybrid retains the intrusiveness of the manual approach but alleviates it by ranking
the list such that a user is likely to have the correct activity on top and thus spend less time during each
interrupt.
• Dynamics - the use of static mapping enables the system to detect if the session’s active processes
changes signiﬁcantly ie. at the level of general activities and thus re-prompt the user to verify their
current activity.

• Feasibility - while solely relying on static mapping requires rigorous work to maintain acceptable accu-

the user conﬁrms their activity, this makes the work of specifying the mapping much more forgiving. The
hybrid still involves much more preliminary work and maintenance than the manual approach, however.
• Flexibility - Similarly to the Infeasibility-disadvantage for purely static mapping, the hybrid method
is much more ﬂexible than pure static mapping as it allows for manual overrides by the user.
• Accuracy - the hybrid retains the accuracy of the manual approach and reduces unintentional human

activity does not match the estimation.

10.3.4
Annotating a Current Activity
Since an object can be consented for a single atomic purpose, it is paramount for satisfying cGlobal (16), that
the current activity can be speciﬁed at the granularity of any single atomic activity, since cGlobal would then
otherwise prevent them from accessing objects that are only consented for a subset of the atomic activities
of a general activity. Since the ranked Task list resulting from the previously mentioned procedures is only
concerning general activities, there is a need to address how to further narrow the detected general activity to
a subset of its related atomic activities. In order to achieve this, the approach relies on manually prompting
the subject that has ownership of the session for which the ranked Task list is generated.
As will be addressed in regards to detecting changes to the current activity in a session in section 10.4, it
is an implicit requirement that while a subject can have any number of atomic activities as the elements in
uActivities, all atomic activities must be associated with the same general activity. This is because a change
in activity can only be automated at the level of general activities and as such, the session should be restricted
to one general activity at the time so that a change in general activity can be detected and prompt the correct
response, such as re-evaluating accesses to granted sensitive objects.
In order to annotate the atomic current activities of a subject, the subject is presented with the ranked Task-
list as generated by the aforementioned procedures. This list could be presented in many ways, one obvious
being as a drop-down or a nested check-box menu. Once a general activity is selected (most likely the top-
most activity in the list) the user is presented with the sub-tasks related to that general activity. The user is
permitted to select multiple objects from this list (think of a check-box style selection) as long as they belong
to the same general activity. The list of sub-tasks can easily be fetched using the Task list and once the user
has selected one or more sub-tasks, these sub-tasks are annotated the uActivities-attribute as the set of current
activities.
At this point, annotation is trivial since the user has speciﬁed the exact sub-tasks that are to be annotated.
Recall that the session directly states the user-identiﬁer of the user in question and the chosen sub-tasks
correspond directly to their uActivities-attribute values, and as such directly represent the set of string-values
that must be associated with the user’s own uActivities-attribute.
Since the activity detection mechanism presents a list of possible general activities to the subject, it could
simply be enforced that the subject can only select one general activity at the time from this list. A new choice
in the list should remove and override any previous values in uActivities and therefore the user can only have
one general activity at the time. Annotation of new uActivities-values should therefore replace the existing set
of values, not be added to an existing set.
10.3.5
Enhancing the Activity Detection Process
In its current format, the hybrid solution can be used to detect a general activity of a subject with acceptable
satisfaction, and use this to assist the user in selecting speciﬁc atomic activities. This approach is intrusive
and relies on proper design and maintenance of the dependencies set to remain accurate, the design can be
enhanced further by introducing Role-to-Activity association:
AERBAC is role-centric which means that permissions are ordered by an associated role which limits the
set of permissions that have to be audited when an individual with a given role submits an access request.
This advantage could also be applied to the detection of a current activity for a subject: As already brieﬂy
mentioned in section 10.1, a general activity could roughly correspond to an organisational role while each
related atomic activity correlates to the individual tasks associated with that role. Assuming corporate roles
can be roughly translated into AC roles64, each role can in turn be associated with the general activities that
64Note that, as discussed in section 3, directly translating organisational roles to AC roles is generally considered bad
practice since the organisational roles are usually too large. However, for now assume that this is not the case.
67
==PAGE:72==
this role is expected to perform.
This is advantageous because it limits the set of ’possible activities’ by the subjects role and hence, detecting
the current activity of the subject is carried out among a much smaller subset of potential activities, making
activity detection both faster and also more accurate. Furthermore, presenting general activities to a member
of staﬀthat is completely unrelated to such a task is redundant. For instance, when a janitor authenticates at
his work PC to order more soap deliveries, there is no need to ask him about his current activity and proceed
to list choices such as ’legal counselling’ since he is not likely to hold any roles and permissions to any legal
counselling objects anyway.
This implies creating a Role →General Activity-mapping which speciﬁes permissible activities for that role.
Since a session is always associated with one or more roles, the Task-list can be reduced to only contain the
general activities that are associated with that role. For instance, the general activity ’Marketing’ could be
mapped to only the roles that actually are expected to perform PR-related work and hence ’Marketing’ should
not exist in the Task-list for staﬀwith any other roles than ’PR’. Since most roles presumably only have a very
small subset of the full Task list in a large organisation, this has two advantages:
(i) The accuracy of activity detection is greatly enhanced since the list of candidate activities is reduced
which means that activities which might otherwise overlap with the true current activity won’t be present. For
instance, following the example in table 10, if the only active applications are Excel and Notepad, the activities
’Accounting’ and ’Sales’ are equally likely, while if these two tasks are associated with two diﬀerent roles, only
one of them would actually be considered for any given session if the session is only associated with one of
those roles at the time.
This means that both the speed and accuracy at which activity detection is carried out using static mapping as
well as the speed at which the subject can select the correct activity from the presented list of ranked activities
are increased.
(ii) The security is increased by separation of duties.
Some tasks may leak information through their name alone: If for instance a task exists called ”Merger of
company A and B”, the task-name it self would be highly sensitive, if the merger in question has not yet been
publicly announced. Naming-conventions can mitigate this by either obscuring sensitive names using aliases
or enforcing benign naming-policies, or alternatively, restricting the ranked task list by role association, as
discussed above, could prevent disclosing task-names to roles that are not associated with the task and as such,
prevent leakage of sensitive task-names beyond their restricted roles.
While increased security is beneﬁcial, it must be noted to take care not to substitute regular access control
policies with features such as the Role-to-Activity association discussed above:
The extended AERBAC is intended to prevent secondary processing and it is a clear assumption that regular
Access Control is carried out in addition to the extended AERBAC’s prevention of secondary use, either by
AERBAC it self, or by an already existing AC model. It is therefore not a goal for the extended AERBAC
model proposed in this dissertation to discuss how to enforce regular Access Control. In other words, the
extended AERBAC model ensures that, if a subject can access objects according to organisation policies, and
a requested object is sensitive, then the object must also have a consented purpose that matches the current
processing purposes of the subject. This means that the Role-to-Activity association presented in this section
must be viewed as an extra feature, but should not substitute any original access control policies.
The disadvantages of this enhancement are the added work of specifying and maintaining the association
between roles and tasks, and the risk of such an association being redundant since the polices should already
limit the tasks that can be performed by a given role implicitly by the permissions for that role. Due to the
disadvantages, this enhancement is considered an ’optional extra’ which might have varying degree of success
depending on the speciﬁc implementation and should be subject to further investigation with the speciﬁc
implementations in mind before adding the enhancement.

10.3.7
Additional Considerations and Remarks
Manually populating Dependencies sets
It should be noted that, even if the job of populating the Dependencies sets is manual, it is not crucial that
each set exhaustively includes of ALL involved applications related to a given task. Due to the way the weights
are applied, it is more important to include ’key applications’, while including redundant and general purpose
applications that could be involved in any task, does not have much relevancy or could even cause misleading
’noise’, which leads to the following pitfall:
If only one, or at most a few Dependencies sets are given an entry which is in fact redundant, it would distort
the ability to predict activities correctly. For instance, stating that the ’Accounting’-task involves the use of
a ’web browser’-application. Software such as web browsers are so general purpose that they could essentially
be a part of almost any activity or could easily be instantiated for benign purposes concurrently and therefore
a person populating the Dependencies sets might overlook explicitly stating in each set that this dependency
exists. If a single entry is then given such a general purpose entry, such as stating that the Accounting-activity
uses a web browser, then, whenever a session has a web browser-application active, would be used as a strong
indication that the session is doing Accounting. This leads to the following requirement for the static mapping
approach to work:
It is important to identify general purpose applications and any redundant applications such as OS applications
etc. and either ensure that they are present in all Dependencies sets or that they are removed from all sets.
Alternatively, introduce a ban-list that lists all general purpose applications which subsequently are forcibly
omitted from the Dependencies matrix.
Customising the static mapping While the static mapping method and hence the Dependencies matrix
is designed as a one-size-ﬁts-all, it might be determined that individual users have varying preferences and
work diﬀerently and thus use diﬀerent combinations of processes for the same task.
The hybrid approach
could be optimised by allowing individual customisation based on user-feedback. Ultimately this should be
achieved by using ’learning algorithms’ that could manipulate the Dependencies matrix. For instance, if for
an individual user it is consistently estimated that they are performing Accounting while they are in fact
performing Sales. If automated, the model could pick up on this consistent deviation from its estimations and
address it by scoring Accounting higher or than Sales for speciﬁc patterns or the user could be allowed to
manually state that the currently speciﬁc set of active processes correlate to Accounting which could modify
the user’s local version of Accounting’s Dependencies set. The user could potentially also be given the ability
to ’add’ or ’remove’ processes from the Dependencies matrix if, for instance, a developer uses another IDE
65Assuming the optional feature of associating roles with tasks as described in section 10.3.5 is desired.

10.4
Detecting a Change
To prevent secondary use, it does not suﬃce to only verify the user’s current activities at the point of granting
access to objects, since this would allow a user to access an object with a legitimate activity and subsequently
change their activity and process the object while performing the new activity. For this reason, the uActivities-
attribute is classiﬁed as dynamic, in contrast to the static oPurposes-attribute66. How the uActivities-attribute
is dynamically evaluated is addressed in this section.
Thus far, the activity detection procedures presented in section 10.3 have mainly revolved around determining
the current activities of a subject at the point of submitting access requests to sensitive objects, and the
procedures do not take any existing value of uActivities into consideration. The proposed procedure in section
10.3.6 is not built for continuous monitoring because the procedure prompts the user (step 5 and 6), asking them
to specify their current activity. Prompting the user continuously or with short intervals, without reasonable
suspicion that the current activities have changed, would be much too intrusive. Furthermore, the procedure is
not equipped to determine if the current activity of a subject has changed and there is currently no speciﬁcation
on how to trigger a change detection-procedure. The procedure in section 10.3.6 is therefore not satisfactory
in its current format in achieving complete protection against secondary use and there is a need to determine
how a continuous monitoring procedure can be implemented, which can detect changes in an already existing
uActivities-attribute value to ensure compliance following a successful access request. It is noted that while
the user can be involved in the process of monitoring their activities, the goal is to minimise their involvement
by only including them in the process once reasonable suspicion of a change in activity has been inferred.
Note that ABAC models are inherently built with the intention to dynamically evaluate attributes by detecting
changes and handling a change appropriately. It is assumed that AERBAC is conceived with dynamic handling
of attributes in a similar manner to the NIST ABAC framework67 and as such, how the extended AERBAC
speciﬁcally handles a change in uActivities is considered out of scope. The scope of the procedure given in
this section is therefore to determine that the current activity has changed and subsequently that uActivities-
attribute should be updated and what the updated value should be. It is assumed that once uActivities is
updated, some built-in ABAC trigger is invoked that handles the dynamic attribute change by reevaluating
access to all currently accessed objects, and takes action by removing invalidated objects from the given session
accordingly.
A note on accuracy, sensitivity and intrusiveness: The higher the desired accuracy in detecting changes, the
more sensitive the automated change detection mechanism must be. The more sensitive the detection becomes,
the more frequent it is that the procedure will either reach false and true positives. False positives mean that
the user is unnecessarily confronted with a prompt to verify their activity. The sensitivity should be determined
individually by organisation. In evaluating the desired sensitivity, it should be considered how easy it is for the
organisation to generate a Dependencies matrix with a high number of unique dependencies since this lowers
the number of false positives by increasing the conﬁdence rating, coupled with the organisation’s tolerance
thresholds regarding the risk of secondary use breaches, weighed against the added intrusiveness of a more
sensitive detection mechanism.
It is the goal of the proposed procedure to borrow as much functionality from the previous activity detection
mechanisms as possible68, while adding the capability to continuously or periodically monitor the session’s
active processes, and determine if it is likely that uActivities has changed and take action accordingly. This
goal implies the need to deﬁne: (i) how a change is detected, including how change is deﬁned, (ii) a procedure
66Dynamic and static attributes are discussed in the ABAC section 4.

regarding how a detected change is handled and (iii) when the change detection procedure should be executed
ie. deﬁning a trigger mechanism.
10.4.1
Deﬁning an Activity Change
Since the method for detecting a change should not involve a user needlessly, it is important to consider the
degree of autonomous evaluation of a change. Considering the procedure in section 10.3.6, the autonomous
segment of activity detection in this procedure is at the level of general activities, achieved by using the
Dependencies matrix and the active processes in a session. If the detection of a change in current activities is
to be achieved autonomously, a change can therefore only be measured at the level of general activities as well.
If detection was to be made at the level of atomic activities, the user would have to be involved every time the
change detection procedure is invoked and not only when there is a positive outcome, which is undesirable.
It is for this reason that it is mandated that all concurrent atomic activities must be from the same general
activity, since change detection would otherwise not be able to detect a change between the atomic activities.
A change in general activity can be determined by considering the Dependencies matrix and the ranked Task
list:
Recall that AERBAC can access the session, including the attributes of both the session and of the user who
owns the session. This means that a change detection procedure, can at all times determine what the user’s
uActivities-attribute is. uActivities yields a set of atomic activities which in turn, by deﬁnition all correspond
exclusively to the same general activity. The general activity obtained from uActivities can be thought of as
the expected current activity. To detect a change, the model can re-calculate the ranked Task list, using the list
of active processes in the session and the procedure for estimating and ranking a session’s current activities,
as presented in section 10.3.3. The procedure should then compare the resulting snapshot of the ranked Task
list with the expected current activity to determine if the current ranking matches the ranking from when the
current value of uActivities was set. If not, it is deemed likely that the user has changed their current general
activity and steps must be taken to prevent secondary use accordingly.
A change by comparing the expected general activity and the snapshot ranked Task list can be deﬁned in
multiple ways, some of the simplest being the following: Assume that the AERBAC model retains knowledge
of the expected general activity’s ranked Task list, using this ’old’ Task list, compare it to the freshly generated
Task list and decide that the current activity is likely to have changed in one of the following ways:
1 Whenever the weighted sum of the expected general activity has changed
Assuming that the dependencies matrix is relatively extensive, and as soon as it involves just a single
general purpose entry, this deﬁnition is too sensitive and involves way too many interruptions from false
positives
2 Whenever the ranking of any task in the list changes
If the dependencies matrix is large, many processes will be listed as some sort of dependency and it is
highly likely that under such circumstances, there may be many small ﬂuctuations in the exact weights,
especially among the low-ranked tasks at the bottom of the list. If each ﬂuctuation results in prompting
the user, the intrusiveness would be too high and this deﬁnition does therefore become too sensitive
On the other hand, if the Dependencies matrix is short such as the list being reduced signiﬁcantly
using the Role-to-Activity assignment mentioned in section 10.3.5, and if almost exclusively unique
dependencies are present in the remaining activities, this deﬁnition is the most accurate since this would
detect any change
3 Whenever the ranking changes such that the expected general activity has dropped in ranking
This deﬁnition is ’blind’ to the surrounding tasks but is also only triggered when the likelihood of activity
change is relatively large and thus has a lower sensitivity than deﬁnition one and two
4 If the expected general activity is not the highest ranked, a change could also be whenever any process
is moved above the current task or whenever a higher ranked task changes weighted sum
Assuming that the user is most likely to chose the highest ranked task from the ranked Task list when
prompted and that anytime a current activity is chosen by the user that is not the highest ranked in the
Task list presented to them, the choice is logged, this deﬁnition is negligible.
71
==PAGE:76==
The goal is to chose the deﬁnition that will detect a change as accurately as possible and hence a high number
of true positives and true negatives while keeping the amount of false positives (unnecessarily disturbing the
user) and false negatives (change in activity undetected) low. This will ensure minimisation of unnecessary
user-interrupts and thus a low intrusiveness while also ensuring compliance with secondary use prevention
by detecting if an activity changes. Judging from the arguments for each proposed deﬁnition listed above,
deﬁnition 2 is the best approach if the Dependencies matrix can be assumed to be relatively small and if it has
a prevalence of unique dependencies. Deﬁnition 3 is considered to be the best if the Dependencies matrix is
large and with ﬂuctuations in ranking among irrelevant processes. Since the deﬁnition thus becomes dependent
on the speciﬁc implementation, choosing the correct deﬁnition of a change is not straight forward. It is decided
to go with deﬁnition 3 to apply the ’broadest’ and least sensitive of the two approaches and noted that using
deﬁnition 2 instead may be advantageous for some organisations, depending on speciﬁcs pertaining to each
individual organisation.
Therefore, a change of current activity is deﬁned as follows:
If the rank (position) of the current general activity has dropped from the old to the new ranked Task list, it
is deemed that it is likely that the current general activity has changed.
An advantage of this approach is also that the model does not need to store the entire old ranked Task list in
memory but only store the rank of the current general activity. Activity change can quickly be determined by
simply comparing the old rank-value with the new rank in a freshly generated ranked list.
There are two signiﬁcant caveats to the notion that it is only the ranking of the currently chosen activity that
is considered:
1. Since a change can’t be determined at a ﬁner granularity than general activity, the detection mechanisms
cant distinguish atomic activities belonging to the same general activity. A user can therefore perform secondary
use if they pick a consented activity, access an object legitimately and then switch from the consented atomic
activity to a non-consented atomic activity where both atomic activities belong to the same general activity.
Such secondary use can’t possibly be detected as long as the detection mechanisms can’t monitor at the level
of atomic activities.
2. If for instance the chosen activity is ranked #1 with weight = 10.0 and the second place changes from
weight 0.33 to 9.9 this would not be a change in ranking but a severe change in the conﬁdence rating of #1,
since the diﬀerence between #1 and #2 has changed from 9.67 to 0.1. On the other hand, the chosen activity
when the user is prompted could be at #99 with weight 0.0 and subsequently upgraded to #1 with conﬁdence
10. Therefore the use of conﬁdence-rating can be used instead of considering changes to the ranking but this
becomes troublesome as it is not given that the highest ranking activity is also the one that the user chooses
when prompted. This is deﬁnitely an area that should be given much more in-depth study in order to propose
a mathematically sound way of determining if it is likely that the current activity has changed suﬃciently to
warrant re-prompting the subject for conﬁrmation.
In conclusion, for optimum functionality, only detecting changes by considering the ranking does not suﬃce,
and especially when the detection is only at the level of general activities. It must be taken into account that,
because the user is asked to choose their current activity from the list, it is in no way guaranteed that the
chosen activity is the top ranking and may even be the lowest ranked with a very low conﬁdence rating. In
worst case this could mean that the user selects an activity with a weighted sum of 0.0 with multiple other
activities in the list with the same weighted sum of 0.0, while there may exist tasks with very high weights.
Ultimately, if the third method for activity detection ie. fully automated activity detection was in place and
hence no use of static mapping and manually prompting the user was necessary, it would be just as easy to
detect a change as it was to detect the original activity and the user would never have to be involved until it
is deemed that their current activity conﬂicts one or more session objects.
10.4.2
Triggering the Change Detection Procedure
As previously mentioned, the change detection procedure needs to be either continuous or at least periodical
in intervals that are short enough that a change in activities will be caught before secondary processing can
take place. The easiest way to ensure that a change in activity is caught as fast as possible, without needlessly
triggering the change detection procedure, is to add a trigger which is triggered whenever a session’s list of
72
==PAGE:77==
active processes changes. Because redundant- and OS-processes are not assumed to have an impact on a user’s
ability to perform secondary processing, it would be redundant to trigger from any change in the list of active
processes, and it suﬃces to only trigger if there is a change involving a process marked as a dependency. In
practice this can be achieved by extracting the full list of dependencies from the Dependencies matrix and scan
the list of active processes in the session for any dependencies, whenever a new process marked as a dependency
is either terminated or started, the trigger is activated.
In addition, to limit the number of redundant triggers, the trigger should only be active while the session has
access to sensitive objects. It is redundant to verify the user’s activity if no sensitive objects are accessed.
It is assumed that, at the point of accessing any sensitive object, the procedure for activity detection in
section 10.3.6 has been executed and that access to the sensitive objects could not have been possible without
uActivities having been assigned at least one valid atomic activity and hence uActivities ̸= null ∧uActivities

10.4.3
Monitoring the Value of uActivities
The procedure for monitoring the value of uActivities, and in extent a change in activity, is triggered according
to the deﬁnition of the trigger as discussed above.
Once triggered, the procedure evaluates the session to
determine if it is likely that the uActivities-value does no longer match, as described in the change-deﬁnition
above. If it is deemed likely that the user’s general activity has changed, the user is presented with a freshly
generated ranked Task list and asked to conﬁrm their current activity. The uActivities-attribute is then updated
according to the user’s input. An important note is that if the user states that they are still performing their
original activity, it is the new ranking of the general activity that is stored for subsequent evaluations, since
every subsequent evaluation would otherwise result in a redundant user-prompt. The procedure for monitoring
the uActivities-attribute is presented formally below, but ﬁrst an example is provided to show how the procedure
works. For simplicity, this example deals solely with general activities:
A subject requests access to an object consented for the purpose ’Sales’ following the scenario resulting in
table 11. The user would be prompted with the ordered list of tasks which lists that the activity Sales is the
most likely. Assume that the user conﬁrms the estimated activity by choosing Sales as the current activity.
The system stores uActivities as Sales as well as storing the knowledge that the rank of Sales was #1. If the
user then terminates the Word-application, the trigger would be activated and a reevaluation of the estimated
current activity would result in the weighted sums: wSum(Sales) = 1.33 and wSum(Accounting) = 1.33. At
this point it is equally likely that the subject is processing the data for Sales as for Accounting which could
be left to the beneﬁt of the doubt and hence no conﬁrmation prompt is sent to the user. If the subject then
terminates the EMS application, the trigger is invoked again and the change detection procedure determines
that Sales is now weighed at 0.33 while Accounting is now the highest ranked with the weight 1.33. Following
the deﬁnition of a change, such a shift in the ordering of the ranking indicates that the subject is now likely
executing Accounting-activities rather than Sales. The user is then prompted with the new ranked list and
asked to conﬁrm their activity. In this case, the user is still performing sales and only closed EMS by mistake,
so the user states that Sales is still their current activity. uActivities is thus unaltered but the stored rank of
the current general activity is changed to #2 instead.
If the user had instead conﬁrmed that their current activity indeed had changed, then in turn the uActivities-
value is updated accordingly, meaning that all sensitive objects granted to the session need to be reevaluated.
How the reevaluation of accessed objects is carried out is left up to the speciﬁc implementation of the access
control system with the only requirement being that, once the uActivities-attribute has changed, all sensitive
objects must be reevaluated, and any mismatch should be handled according to the implemented policy.

11.1
Formal Language Extensions
In order to achieve the desired behaviour of cGlobal in eq. (16), the AERBAC model must be extended with
support of short-circuiting operators, and in particular support of a logical OR short circuiting operator denoted
||.
78
==PAGE:83==
The ||-operator ensures that benign objects are omitted from further evaluation by cGlobal by skipping evalua-
tion if the ﬁrst part of cGlobal evaluates to true, which is the case whenever the given object is benign. Since a
short-circuiting logical OR-operator is mandated, it makes little sense to omit the inclusion of a short-circuiting
logical AND-operator, &&, as well. While the &&-operator is not strictly required, it proves useful for op-
timising condition-evaluation. For instance, && can be used when appending cGlobal to existing conditions
when the AERBAC polices are extended with cGlobal, cf. section 8.3. It should not involve much added
work to extend the language with a logical AND-operator, once a logical OR-operator is created, and as such,
both operators are added to the extended AERBAC model. Note that the existing logical AND- and logical
OR-operators , ∧and ∨could possibly be short circuiting already, depending on the implementation. If this is
the case, there is no need to add && and || to the AERBAC language. However, to ensure that it is perfectly
clear that there is a need to support short circuiting operators, the two operators are added explicitly to the
formal language while it is noted that they can be omitted if the original logical-operators are indeed short
circuiting.
Section 10.4.4 outlines the formal approach to detecting a change in uActivities.
The required AERBAC
extensions deﬁned in this section are primarily the functions session processes and session objects. Both of
these functions are relatively trivial functions that retrieve data from a session.
As introduced in this section, the relationship between objects and oPurposes-attributes is formally given
by OPA (Object-to-Purpose Assignment) while the relationship between users and uActivities is represented
by UAA (User-to-Activity Assignment). In practice, the OPA is essentially the oPurposes registry and the
functionality required to read and write data in the registry. UAA is currently not speciﬁed in terms of whether
it should be centralised in a registry or be locally deﬁned at each user. How UAA is implemented it is left
to be decided during implementation design, the recommendation is to implement UAA in a similar fashion
to the similar mappings such as the URA72, to ensure consistency. An outline of the formal extensions of the
AERBAC language is provided below.
The formal AERBAC language in appendix A is to be extended with the following:
• Entry 1.1 is appended with the terms PROC, PURP, and ACT denoting processes73, purposes and
activities74 respectively
• Entry 1.14 (new) presents OPA (Object-to-Purpose Assignment)
• Entry 1.15 (new) presents UAA (User-to-Activity Assignment)
• Entry 1.16 (new) presents session processes, introduced in eq. (22)
• Entry 1.17 (new) presents session objects, introduced in eq. (19)
• Entry 2.1 is extended with the short circuiting operators, || and &&
The above extensions are reﬂected in the extended AERBAC formal language, presented in appendix D. The
extensions are referenced by the above entry-index and use a blue font to highlight the changes. Notice that
no existing parts of the AERBAC language are modiﬁed or removed, and hence that the extended AERBAC
only adds to the existing language.
It is worth noting that earlier versions of cGlobal, presented through section 8, involve operators that are cur-
rently not supported by AERBAC. For instance the intersection- and union- operators, ∩and ∪. Additionally,
there is no mention of support of the universal-set operator, U, and as such, if previous iterations of cGlobal are
to be supported, the AERBAC language is to be further extended to support such operators. Because these
operators are currently only used in deprecated iterations of the cGlobal, they are currently not included.

11.2.4
Post Analysis
The result of the Bapple-case is that Alice successfully obtains access to the objects bobMail and bob, which
correspond to the sensitive data that Alice needs to perform the desired task ’email - sales’, as shown in the
consent-form in listing 6. Likewise, Alice is denied access to the sensitive object bobPhoneNo since Bob has
not consented for his phone number to be used for anything except the ’text - satisfaction’ sub-task, which is
not Alice’s current activity.
This case shows how the extended AERBAC model is capable of preventing illicit access to sensitive data by
matching the user’s activity to the object’s consented processing purposes. Some alternative scenarios can
further highlight the extended AERBAC model’s capabilities and limitations:
Concurrent activities: Scenario change: If Alice had chosen to be performing both of the Sales sub-tasks:
[’email - sales’, ’ring client’]: This would not have given her access to any additional sensitive objects pertaining
to Bob, and in fact have limited her even further:
Consequence: Due to the consent that Bob has given, cGlobal would still yield true for the object bob since
bob is consented for both activities. However, cGlobal will fail for both BobMail and BobPhoneNo since none
of the objects are consented for both of Alice’s activities. This is a limitation that could prevent Alice from
legitimate access. The limitation arises because the system can’t distinguish between atomic activities in the
current design which means that cGlobal requires each uActivities-value of Alice to match a oPurposes-value
for the requested object. If this limitation was not used, the current design of activity detection would have
no way to ensure that Alice does not perform secondary processing by processing each object for the opposite
activity, ie. processing BobMail for ’ring client’ and BobPhoneNo for ’email - sales’, which would be in breach
with secondary use.
Conclusions: This is an intended limitation of cGlobal to prevent secondary use, but runs the risk of permitting
legitimate access. The mitigation is to either allow Alice to manually override the access denial by stating that
she is aware of the consents regarding Bob’s data or to ask Alice to reevaluate her current activities such that
she is only performing one of the two atomic activities.
Activity change:

Scenario change: Alice could for some reason desire to change her activity from Sales to Optimisation after
having obtained access to the objects bob and bobMail. Alice’s motivation to change activity does not have to
be intentionally malicious but could simply be because Alice’s boss shows up and asks her to quickly provide
him with the latest results of her work with a project related to Optimisation. In her hurry, Alice prioritises
to help out her boss and changes her current activity to accommodate his request.
Consequence: In this case, Alice could potentially76 be using Bob’s personal information for a purpose for

In extent of the above scenario; if Alice terminates the EMS process, the change in active processes should
invoke a reevaluation of the ranked Task list by the Activity Monitor module. The module evaluates that the

activities, although less likely than before. At this point the Activity Monitor module could log the drop in
weighted sum for Alice’s current activity, Sales, but does not take further action.
If Alice then runs the Survey-application, the weighted sums are reevaluated again, in which case the weights
of Client Satisfaction and Optimisation both are increased by 0.5, causing Sales to drop below Optimisation
in the ranked Task list. This change in ranking could indicate that Alice is now more likely to be performing
another activity than Sales and Alice is therefore prompted, asking her to conﬁrm her current activity. If
Alice conﬁrms that her current activity indeed has changed from Sales to Optimisation, AERBAC updates
Alice’s uActivities-attribute and revokes already granted access to invalid sensitive objects accordingly. In this
case the objects bob and bobMail are not consented for any Optimisation sub-tasks, meaning that both objects
would be revoked.
If Alice had not closed EMS, starting both the Survey- and the Adobe suite-applications would cause Opti-
misation and Sales to be ranked equally likely with a weight of 2.00 each. In this case, the user is given the
beneﬁt of the doubt and no prompt to conﬁrm the current activity is triggered. This means that under these
circumstances, while Alice may not abuse Bob’s data, there is the potential since she could now include Bob’s
data in Optimisation-related processing. This highlights a limitation of the current solution to how activity
detection is carried out.
11.3
Additional lawful processing purposes and AERBAC
In the current state of the extended AERBAC model, the solution is built around the assumption that secondary
use is prevented by ensuring that a given user is only performing processing activities that are consented for
the sensitive object accessed by the subject. As mentioned in section 6.3, article 6 lists several processing
purposes that do not require a consent and thus, these exceptions to the general rule of consent-enforcement
are not covered in the current extended AERBAC model.
While extensively analysing all cases of exceptions to consent is too large a task, this section shows how many of
the alternative processing purposes can be incorporated into the extended AERBAC solution. The processing
purposes that do not require consent are listed in GDPR article 6, some of them are:
1.(c) Processing is necessary for compliance with legal obligation to which the controller is subject.
For instance, if there are legislative requirements such as a requirement that an insurance company must
obtain and store the social security number of its clients, then a consent for such purposes is not required.
1.(d) Processing is necessary in order to protect the vital interests of the data subject or another natural
person.
76The extended AERBAC solution is intended to prevent illicit processing by detecting the potential for illicit pro-
cessing, and hence access is restricted based on the potential of abuse. This is in order to have a pro-active prevention.
85
==PAGE:90==
For instance, this could be hospitals that need to process medical records of patients to ensure proper
treatment, for which no explicit consent is required. This could also apply if the subject is incapable of
giving consent themselves (Art. 9.2(c)).
1.(e) Processing is necessary for the performance of a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise
of oﬃcial authority vested in the controller.
For instance, services that are vital to public interest such as infrastructure, for example electricity and
water supply companies. They may be obligated to process data such as home addresses in order to
provide their services.
The above mentioned exceptions are a subset of lawful processing other than consensual processing. There
are many other lawful processing purposes, for instance Art. 9 goes into detail regarding processing related to
special categories that are exempted from consent requirements, and Art. 10 concerns processing of personal
data relating to criminal convictions and oﬀenses respectively.
In its current format, the extended model represents a theoretical Proof-of-Concept that it is indeed possible
to prevent secondary use when concerning consent-related data ie. data for which an explicit consent has been
given by the data owner. Ultimately the extended AERBAC model should be able to incorporate all lawful
processing purposes including the examples listed above. The additional lawful processing aspects could be
incorporated in multiple ways:
Adding policy exceptions: The power of attributes could potentially incorporate all these requirements by
specifying the correct policies. cGlobal in its current format, eq. (16), covers consensual processing, but adding
more rules to it in an ’else if’-style could enable cGlobal to apply to all the other scenarios for which consent
is not required:
Notice in eq. (16) that it currently in natural language reads: if the object is benign OR there is a consent,
then grant access. The other lawful processing purposes can be expressed in a similar fashion by extending
the expression. For instance, a user can be given an attribute stating that they are medical personnel and the
data object an attribute that states that the object contains medical record information that may be vital:
uMedicalPersonal(u) = true and oMedicalRecord(o) = true. Then cGlobal could simply have appended the
condition: ’|| (uMedicalPersonal(u) ∧oMedicalRecord(o)).
Additional conditions and permissions could be added to ensure that the object can’t be arbitrarily obtained
just because the user is medical staﬀand the object is a health record, this could be for instance that the
subject must have recently been registered at the same medical clinic as the medical personal user. In this
way, complex ’exceptions’ can be appended to cGlobal so that it no longer only distinguishes between benign
and sensitive objects and whether the given sensitive objects have a valid consent, but also consider all the
other lawful processing purposes.
The limitation of this approach is that there may be a huge amount of work involved to specify all the extra
condition-extensions but also to ensure their correctness and to build a system that can ensure that the proper
attributes are annotated and kept up to date accordingly. Furthermore, if cGlobal is to be applied globally
(for every object included in every access request) then a large and computationally demanding cGlobal would
likely hamper the system by slowing response-times for access requests signiﬁcantly.
Redeﬁning the meaning of oPurposes-attributes: Currently oPurposes is synonymous with consented
processing purposes.
If oPurposes were to be generalised to instead be synonymous with lawful processing
purposes of which consent-purposes is a subset, oPurposes could be used to describe all purposes. For instance
an oPurposes-value could be ’vital medical record’ in accordance with the previous example. Then cGlobal
would not need to have any additional expressions appended since the secondary use prevention mechanism
still only has to revolve around matching oPurposes with uActivities. The extra work would instead be to
detect these activities correctly, which no longer only involves the active processes in a session. For example, in
order to ensure that medical personnel can always access vital medical records, they can have the uActivities-
value ’vital medical record’ annotated. In this way, the extra work required for this approach to incorporate
the full secondary use perspective is to deﬁne each lawful purpose as oPurposes-values and how to structure
permissions correctly and then determine how to ensure that the correct staﬀor correct situations result in
the correct annotation of uActivities.

==PAGE:91==
The caveat regarding this approach is that uActivities is quickly reduced to a ’one-size-ﬁts-all’ attribute which
is not desirable. For instance, medical staﬀcould be allowed to access medical records without consent but
should only be allowed to do so in the vital interest of the data subject or another natural person. This means
that medical staﬀmight not by default be given uActivities = ’vital medical records’, but instead should
have additional factors included such as whether the member of staﬀis currently working in the ER or trauma
department and whether the data owner of a given medical record is in fact at the same ER. Such circumstances
are speciﬁc and unique for each lawful purpose and can’t necessarily be divided into black and white points of
view such as the current state of oPurposes and uActivities does.
Override-functionality: The main focus of this dissertation is to address secondary use in its primary case:
when consent is required. For the this solution to be extended with the additional lawful processing purposes,
the model needs to either be extended via extra policies or by deﬁning more alternative activities in addition to
consented activities, as shown for medical records above. Alternatively, the model should be implemented with
an override-function. This would mean that whenever access to an object is denied due to cGlobal evaluating
to false, the user could be alerted and told that the denied access is due to lack of consent, which the user
could be given the option to override. An override could then be ﬂagged for review and logged. In this way,
the currently proposed solution could be used by the medical personnel who would simply have to override the
denied access request and obtain access to a sensitive medical record. This would then be subject to review at
which point the medical staﬀcould state that it was in the vital interest of the data owner and explain why
that was the case. The obvious caveat regarding this is the potentially large overhead of bureaucracy required
if a user makes too many overrides77 and it is also worth noting that a ’one-size-ﬁts-all’-approach might not be
desirable and therefore some aspects of secondary processing might be more readily solved using cGlobal while
other aspects are more readily solved using other approaches such as the Decentralised Labelling Model[21].
In conclusion, the current extended AERBAC model as is, is a theoretical PoC that documents how AERBAC
can be extended to incorporate the consent-related aspect of secondary use, which is the default case regarding
lawful processing of personal data. This model could be further enhanced to include other lawful processing
purposes in addition to the consent-related secondary use prevention, as shown above. However, since the
extended AERBAC model is not inherently made with these additional purposes in mind, incorporating these
exceptions might either overly complicate the design of policies or may overly simplify otherwise very com-
plicated scenarios by reducing them to a simple match between a purpose- and an activity-attribute. It is
considered a relatively large additional task to include the additional lawful purposes in an eﬀective way and
this is therefore considered as ’future work’ and AERBAC might not be the best possible platform through
which to enforce alternative lawful processing.

Discussion
Many choices and key areas of dispute have already been addressed in the relevant sections, in which impli-
cations, limitations and alternatives have been presented and compared. This section provides an overview
of the most important reﬂections regarding the considerations and design-choices presented throughout this
dissertation and elaborates on those.
12.1
Core Domain-speciﬁc Considerations
The overall goal of secondary use-prevention is achieved by addressing three core aspects of the problem, divided
into three sub-goals deﬁned in section 7. These three separate domains are initially addressed individually, and
ultimately put together to provide a complete solution to the ultimate goal. While being addressed separately,
it is quite clear that the design of a solution to each domain relies heavily on the design of the other two. For
instance cGlobal dictates the ’output’ of the uActivities- and oPurposes-attributes from the two other domains,
while the consent-form designed initially for oPurposes has proven to be very useful in determining the values
of uActivities due to the close link between activities and purposes. The key-takeaways from each domain
were:
Conditions: It was decided to approach the policy-goal by initially deﬁning a single global condition that
could be enforced for all objects and hence be directly appended to all existing policies. This condition is
improved in an iterative manner by continuously detecting the weaknesses of the current state, reaching the
ﬁnal design in eq. (16). The resulting solution to this domain has the major advantage that policy reviewing
won’t be aﬀected beyond the original complexity of AERBAC policies and the conventions regarding benign
and sensitive objects ensure that the model can work without altering any benign objects which makes it
easier to transition to the extended AERBAC model. This domain is overall believed to be very solid with
no identiﬁcation of any apparent weaknesses other than the minor limitation that having multiple concurrent
activities may severely limit the number of available objects. However, this is considered a necessity to ensure
proper prevention of secondary use.
Consent: Consent was determined to require a relatively signiﬁcant amount of manual labour in terms of
implementing the extended AERBAC model in such a way that the consents are represented as the deﬁned
consent-form templates in section 9.3. This is a relatively signiﬁcant assumption since the ability to represent
consents in this way is a strict requirement for the solution to work. However, since GDPR already imposes
the requirement to identify all sensitive objects and handle their consent, it is assumed that organisations must
have some form of representation or data structure for consents prior to transitioning to AERBAC and thus
states the precondition that the organisation must identify a way to convert the existing consent-data to the
AERBAC consent-form data structure. The extra work added to convert the existing consent to the AERBAC
consent-form is therefore assumed to likely be a daunting task but still manageable. As long as this assumption
holds, there are no signiﬁcant challenges or assumptions regarding consent collection and object annotation.
Especially due to oPurposes-attribute being static, meaning that no monitoring is required. Furthermore, the
manual labour is conﬁned to the initialisation-phase and will not require the active involvement of the data
subject78 or system users. While the consent-domain is believed to be relatively sound, it is noted that one
can always improve the data-representation of data such as consent-forms, and it is therefore recommended
to consider further investigation on consent-form design as a potential ’future work’. Improvements to the
consent-form design could empower this area of the AERBAC model and potentially both alleviate some of
the manual work in the initialisation phase while adding the potential for extending the model with additional
features, this is further addressed in the ’future work’ segment of the conclusion in section 13.1. It is also noted
that the simple matching of purposes and activities in a one-to-one manner could potentially be improved:
Conditions could be customised to consider speciﬁc processing purposes and perhaps match purposes and
activities in a many-to-many manner instead. This could be to deﬁne a set of activities that all correspond to
the same general purpose and then annotate objects with general purposes instead. The risk is to have less
78While the data subject is required to review and consent to the data collection by submitting a consent-form, this
is already mandated and AERBAC thus adds no extra work on top of the existing work.
88
==PAGE:93==
transparent conditions and more diﬃcult policy reviewing while the safety-property is at risk of being broken.
These caveats are the primary reason why the current, simple solution is chosen.
It is also observed that the consent-form templates form the basis of the entire extension. See for instance the
extended AERBAC ﬁgure, ﬁg. 11.a, in which the templates are at the root of the extensions pertaining to the
Consent- and Activity-domains. The advantage is that the consent-forms are intentionally kept simple and
in a format that resembles XML which makes them relatively easy to use universally and to incorporate into
most existing systems that use web-applications or micro services.
Activity: Activities are generally more troublesome to incorporate than consents, this is primarily because the
oPurposes-attribute is static while uActivities is dynamic and thus require reevaluation and change detection,
and also because activities are ’detected’ while purposes are speciﬁed directly via consent-forms. Purposes
are therefore quite rigid in the sense that they build on strong, predeﬁned contracts that can be translated
directly into tasks while activities result from evaluating the session. Since this evaluation is attempting to
be deterministic by using the Dependencies matrix, it attempts to add determinism to human behaviour,
and assuming human behaviour to be deterministic is a fallacy. Therefore, the uActivities-attribute was de-
termined to be the most eﬃciently implemented using a semi-automatic process that relies on static mapping
to initially detect an assumed activity and then have the subject manually verify the result. This is most deﬁ-
nitely a weak spot in the AERBAC model that could be vastly improved by adding stronger methods to further
automate the process, such as using machine learning. Because the current activities are manually selected
by the user from the ranked Task list, intentional abuse is NOT prevented by the current solution. However,
intentional abuse could be detected by logging access requests and reviewing those that present themselves as
anomalies. An anomaly could be if a user claims to be performing an activity that is currently ranked very
low and hence is unlikely. This would not prevent secondary use, but would mean that secondary use could be
detected by reviewing the logs. However, care must be taken not to introduce a large overhead in session- and
anomaly-reviewing.
Another important aspect of the Activity-domain is that this domain relies on dependencies to be speciﬁed
for each task to carry out the static mapping, which is used for both activity detection and in particular
for activity monitoring. Several concerns have already been pointed out regarding dependencies, one concern
is the importance of consistency when adding general purpose applications or ensuring that general purpose
applications are properly banned from all Dependencies sets, to avoid unnecessary ’noise’. Another concern is
that when populating the Dependencies sets, it is important to strive towards being able to uniquely identify
each task by trying to obtain as many unique dependencies as possible. This is a manual task and the accuracy
of activity detection relies greatly on how well this task is solved. Alternative designs that are more automated
are greatly desired to mitigate the issues outlined above, but for now, several solutions are proposed that can
help enhance the current solution by mitigating the concerns, including (i) manual conﬁrmation of an activity,
(ii) logging of choices and anomalies, (iii) manual overriding of an estimated activity and especially (iv) the
option to create Role-to-Activity associations. Role-to-Activity association will likely reduce the amount of
available tasks in a given session greatly and thus help increase the chance of achieving uniquely identiﬁable
tasks based on dependencies and also make it easier for the subject to pick the correct current activities.
Creating such a mapping requires extra work and maintenance, but it can prove very powerful under certain
circumstances.
The following sub-sections pertain to speciﬁc key considerations regarding the extended AERBAC model. They
are represented in an arbitrary order which does not attempt to prioritise or structure the diﬀerent considera-
tions in a particular way.
12.2
Manual Labour and User-Interaction Consequences
One of the biggest limitations of the extended AERBAC model is the dependency on human interaction during
activity detection and the manual work required to initialise and maintain consent-forms and dependencies.
Generating the consent-forms can be a daunting task for any organisation, especially if the organisation employs
multiple input sources and collect a large amount of sensitive information. GDPR stipulates that gathering
consent for all sensitive data is mandatory and the added workload is therefore not originating from AERBAC
but from GDPR. The only ’extra’ work required by the organisation is therefore to ensure that their current
consent-gathering mechanisms can be translated into the AERBAC consent-form templates.
89
==PAGE:94==
Generating the dependencies can potentially be a very large task, depending on the number of general activities
and applications that the organisation uses. However, it is assumed that most large organisations, for whom the
extended AERBAC model is intended, have relatively standardised processes with speciﬁc and single-purpose
applications deployed. It becomes signiﬁcantly easier to generate the dependencies if each general activity can
be mapped to a single specialised application. As already mentioned in section 10.3.7, due to this assumption,
it is not necessary to exhaustively include every involved application related to a given task. However, it is
important to either exclude the use of general purpose applications from the dependencies or to ensure that if
a single task lists a general purpose activity explicitly, then all other activities should also list that dependency.
In terms of required user-interaction, the user will as a minimum be required to manually select their current
activity when the ﬁrst access request to a sensitive object is submitted. As long as no change to their current
activity is detected, the user will not be required to conﬁrm their current activity again. In the worst case,
the static mapping may detect changes to the ranking of the current general activity very often and cause the
user to have to conﬁrm their activity a lot of times. While this is not in breach with secondary use prevention
it can be of great nuisance to the user, which is undesirable. By ranking the task-list before asking the user
to manually pick their current activity79, the user is less prone to mistakenly selecting the wrong activity
from the list, and the time it takes them to locate their current activity in the list is reduced, assuming the
current activity is ranked as one of the top-ranking on the list. Furthermore, a do not ask me again- or a
time out-option could be added to the prompt, allowing the user to avoid unnecessary disturbances. The risk
is obviously that the activity of the user could then change without being registered and subsequently cause
secondary use breaches. Manual overrides and timeout features are therefore to be used with great care.
12.3
Activity Detection
Generating Dependencies sets: Currently the Dependencies sets are generated ﬁrst and from them, the
matrix is created. In a practical sense, this process could be changed:
1 Create an empty Dependencies matrix ﬁrst, and use the task list to generate the activities-column in
the matrix
2 List all software available to employees in the system in the top row of the matrix
3 Manually go through each ﬁeld in the matrix and insert ’1’ whenever applicable
This approach eliminates the generation of Dependencies sets by making them redundant, simply skipping
them. This dissertation uses Dependencies sets and then generates the matrix from these because it is assumed
that in large systems, no one is likely to be able to ﬁll in the entire matrix and therefore it is easier to divide
this manual process into Dependencies sets, which could be manually generated by the diﬀerent members of
staﬀthat normally carry out these tasks. This gives ﬂexibility and removes the need to have a central authority
that manages this task. However, there is still a need to make global modiﬁcations that apply to all sets, eg.
when the company switches licenses and decides to use an alternative product for speciﬁc tasks, for instance
if another text-editor than Word as suddenly used instead. In such cases, it is necessary to support a global
’substitution’ of all occurrences of a given application across all Dependencies sets.
Using weighted sums: Weighted sums are in essence an inverse frequency analysis based on the number of
occurrences of a given application in the Dependencies matrix. Using the simple weighted sums to detect rank
activities and to detect a potential change in activity in a session, is perhaps a too simple approach.
For instance, it may not suﬃce to only consider the active applications but also which speciﬁc sub-processes
and APIs that a given application is using. Pattern matching such as detecting and monitoring certain patterns
observed for individual members of staﬀcould also be relevant: If a given user always starts by running EMS
and oftentimes forgets to close EMS as the activities change, keeping it unused in the background, the relevancy
of EMS when calculating weighted sums could be reconsidered and adjusted accordingly.
For now, weighted sums are deemed to be adequate but it is noted that much more complex, eﬃcient and
accurate approaches could replace the current activity detection mechanism. For instance, more advanced
mathematics could be used to perform statistical analysis such as Machine Learning and pattern matching
79or multiple concurrent atomic activities
90
==PAGE:95==
to automate detection. Furthermore, conditions such as running SAP- and EMS-applications simultaneously
could be prohibited by analysing an organisation’s Dependencies matrices. Calculating weights could alter-
natively be improved: single-purpose items could be given a very high weight (above 1) to ensure that they
rank high in the weighted sum while thresholds for ’general purpose’ apps could be deﬁned, ie. following the
example, if a weight is below 0.25 it might be ’too general’ for instance Notepad, and thus should be modiﬁed
to a weight of 0 or have the column removed altogether. HOWEVER; this should lead to a statement that
regardless of the math used to calculate weights, the underlying method of manually creating Dependencies
sets is so inaccurate that it is a mathematical bottleneck and thus, improving the ’weight calculations’ would
not overall improve accuracy very much, and therefore a ’simple’ inverse frequency calculation, such as the one
currently employed, might suﬃce. On a side note, since ultimately the automated approaches should be used
instead of weighted sums, spending time improving the calculation of weighted sums is redundant.
As shown in the post-analysis of the Bapple-case, in section 11.2.4, regarding activity changes, the current
approach is limited since only a change in ranking invokes a prompt to conﬁrm one’s activity. In practice this
means that Alice could be performing an activity with wSum = 10 while all other activities has wSum = 0,
and then suddenly change her active applications such that another activity becomes equally likely with weight
10. This would not trigger a conﬁrmation-prompt since they are at the same value. This can be argued to be a
severe limitation. The reason that change detection is based on a change in ranking is to prevent the user from
being overloaded with prompts all the time. This is because it would not only be of great nuisance and hamper
the worker’s ability to perform their tasks, but also risk promoting circumvention: Consider for instance if
members of staﬀdiscover some very broad and central activity consented for almost all purposes, and learn
which applications are a part of that application-set, a simple script could boot a series of dependencies for
that activity and the user would only have to state the performed task once, at the initial access request. Due
to the high weight of this task, the ranking never changes regardless of the user’s actual current activity. In
this way, by the user being dishonest, the use of the extended AERBAC for secondary use prevention will
be invalidated since the current activity remains static and the actual underlying activity is never tracked.
Since it is in the model’s best interest to not cause that kind of intentional and dishonest circumvention, the
invasiveness of the model must be minimised which is the core reason behind the very simple checks for change
in ranking.
In conclusion, for now it is considered adequate to use simple weighted sums and detect a ranking change and
base activity detection on this while it is noted that more complex and automated detection mechanisms are
direly needed to reach the full potential of the extended AERBAC model.
12.4
Improving the Policy-Domain
While cGlobal is considered to be quite sound, there has been identiﬁed an area in which the policy-domain
could be improved:
Currently, cGlobal disallows ALL operations on sensitive objects without the correct corresponding activities
attribute-values, but the policies could be extended to distinguish between benign- and sensitive-operators.
This could be to classify operators by whether they in truth are counted as performing ’processing’ on the
data: Copying, storing or encrypting an object can for instance be considered as benign-operators. If an object
is sensitive but the operator applied to it is benign, there would be no need to have a legitimate activity
attribute-value since no consent would be required.
By classifying each operator in the system as benign or sensitive, policies could be extended by only applying
cGlobal to permissions that involve sensitive operators. This allows benign operators to be applied to sensitive
objects without ever having to invoke cGlobal. cGlobal would then only be invoked and enforce secondary
use-prevention if both the object and the applied operator are sensitive. This two-dimensional classiﬁcation of
sensitivity could drastically improve the ability to access sensitive objects in benign ways, which enable many
tasks to be performed that would otherwise require an activity-attribute.

12.5
Alternatives to Attributes
Intuitively, since each role is likely to be directly associated with speciﬁc tasks, there likely exists an implicit
mapping of roles to general activities for most organisations. Hence, it could be feasible to ’cut out the middle-
man’ and simply let each role dictate what objects can be accessed based on the consented purposes of each
object. Similarly, ACLs could be used the other way around to denote the consented purposes for a given
object by listing what user-groups can access each object, provided that a user-group can be mapped to a set
of tasks.
Both RBAC and ACL are thus candidates for alternative approaches to preventing secondary use. The reason
AERBAC is chosen over these approaches is because of the power of attributes: Attributes are extremely
ﬂexible and can be used to create complex and accurate Boolean expressions to be used in conditions and,
in extent, policies. This means that the granularity and ﬂexibility of using attributes is signiﬁcantly better
than that of using roles. Attribute based approaches also hold other advantages over RBAC such as dynamic
access checking which further enhances the argument that attributes are a superior approach to secondary use
prevention. Furthermore, attributes-based AC models can ultimately be designed entirely as ’black boxes’ in
the sense that they can solely consider the attributes without any explicit speciﬁcation of object and subject
identiﬁers. This means that attributes-based AC can be implemented without any knowledge of the related
system other than the assumption of the presence of certain attributes.
In extent of the previous discussion of attributes versus roles, it is also worth noting why AERBAC is chosen
over pure ABAC: AERBAC is devised to mitigate many of the limitations of either RBAC and ABAC alone.
AERBAC provides a hybrid solution which is more powerful and therefore a better candidate than pure ABAC.
Hence, once it is accepted that attributes are superior to ACL- and RBAC-approaches, AERBAC is a stronger
candidate than pure ABAC. It should be noted that there are alternative hybrid models80 that, compared to
AERBAC, may be equally viable for secondary use prevention. This remains to be determined.
12.6
Alternative to Access Control
As touched upon in section 2, there are two diﬀerent data control paradigms of which one is Access (or Domain)
Control and the other is IFC (Information Flow Control). While Access control works by perimeter control,
IFC works by specifying how data can ﬂow through a system.
These two approaches are fundamentally diﬀerent in terms of securing data and since this dissertation proposes
a solution to secondary use-prevention by means of access control, it is interesting to consider if a similar solution
could be achieved using IFC:
Attributes-based AC models work by considering the attribute-values of objects which is very similar to how
security labels work in IFC. Furthermore, this dissertation introduces the sensitivity-classiﬁcation to objects
containing personal information. Classiﬁcation of data in this way is very closely related to how IFC uses
security classiﬁcations to enforce ﬂow-based access control. Classiﬁcation in this way are essentially partially
ordered sets of classiﬁcations in a hierarchical structure: {benign,sensitive}. It is therefore relatively easy to
imagine an IFC-based solution that looks very similar to the currently proposed extended AERBAC. It can
be argued that Attributes in a sense blur the line between domain control and information ﬂow control due to
the many similarities between attributes in AC and labels in IFC.
In short, I ﬁrmly believe that a very similar and at least as secure solution could be derived using for instance
the IFC approach known as DLM (Decentralised Labelling Model) [21], compared to using AERBAC. One
key diﬀerence is that DLM is designed with security labels in mind and it can be very powerful compared to
AERBAC because DLM has ﬁrm methodologies used to prove that its policies are secure.
On the other hand, IFC approaches are limited in the sense that they are based on the assumption that the
entire platform is trusted and require a full overview of the entire system in order to perform IFC analysis
and deﬁne the ﬂow-policies correctly. In large enterprise systems, IFC is therefore discouraged due to the
large initial workload required to design and implement IFC. IFC is usually preferred in smaller, segmented
areas of an enterprise, where IFC can be used for very speciﬁc and specialised purposes.
This forms the
80These are referenced in section 5 and include [3][15][16][20].
92
==PAGE:97==
primary argument that AC-based approaches to GDPR enforcement are better since GDPR usually covers large
domains of an enterprise and therefore can’t necessarily be eﬃciently limited to small, specialised segments
of the system. Furthermore, as discussed in section 12.5, the AC models can work almost entirely oblivious
to the surrounding systems, which IFC can’t. The consideration therefore becomes a trade-oﬀconsideration
between stronger security (IFC) versus easier implementation and maintenance (AC). Thus, AC is likely the
better choice in more generalised circumstances such as those of this dissertation.
12.7
Adding PO Theory
As already touched upon above, there are several aspects of the proposed solution that use PO (Partial
Ordering) and display similarities with IFC. For example, cGlobal, eq. (16), uses the subset-operator and thus
implies a PO-relationship: uActivities ⊆oPurposes. Similarly, the security classiﬁcation of benign and sensitive
objects imply the PO-relationship: benign ⊆sensitive.
Denning [4] introduced the labelling of objects and subjects by security classiﬁcations and security clearances
respectively.
This approach remove the need for central policies and obtain safety by enforcing that the
relationships deﬁned by the partial orderings are kept instead. This could be that a subject must have sensitive-
clearance to access objects classiﬁed as sensitive and that any subject can access benign-classiﬁcation objects.
A subject with benign-clearance can therefore only access objects classiﬁed as benign.
In this way, it can
be introduced that subjects by default have benign-clearance, and that escalation of privileges to sensitive-
clearance can be achieved by the subject having the correct uActivities-values such that they abide by the PO:
uActivities⊆oPurposes. The reason this approach is not pursued further is because the approach gives rise to
some challenges when dealing with the fundamental requirements pertaining to consent:
Security classiﬁcation and clearances that are traditionally expressed using PO-sets can’t be directly translated
to the consent-domain. This is because each object has its own unique consents that are provided by a unique
data subject. In this way, a user can’t be granted access to process an arbitrary sensitive object just because
they hold the sensitive-clearance, since this clearance does not give any assurances that the speciﬁc object is
in fact consented for the activities of the subject. The current design of cGlobal, that each activity of a subject
must have a corresponding consented purpose for a given object to be accessible, can therefor not be readily
expressed using partial ordering. The approach of applying PO-set theory is therefore not pursued further at
this point. If PO-set theory is to be applied to the current solution, fundamental aspects of the solution such
as the design of cGlobal would likely have to be completely revised.
One way to implement PO-sets could be to create a security label for each possible combination of consented
purposes in a hierarchical manner by structuring each general purpose as a parent with each child being its
associated atomic purposes in a giant security lattice. Whether this is feasible and how exactly this is done
has not been further investigated in this dissertation and it remains an open area, subject to further studies
to determine how and if PO-sets can be introduced, and if introducing PO-sets would even be beneﬁcial by
contributing anything other than soundness-proofs.
12.8
Session Narrowing and the High-Water Mark Principle
Section 10.2 presents the principle denoted as session narrowing. This approach, formalised in eq. (17), can
be used to reduce the set of concurrent activities in a session when objects with only a subset of the activities
are accessed. As concluded in section 10.2 this approach does not secure against secondary use since narrowing
of the session does not change the fact that activity detection is at the level of general activities and thus,
removing a subset of atomic activities is more symbolic than added security. The idea of dynamically altering
the attributes in the session depending on the objects that are accessed is however an interesting topic, and
surely could help to provide increased security if a sound solution can be derived.
Another alternative that could be used with a similar goal of constraining the session is the High-water mark
principle[32]. This principle was originally proposed in 1962 and can be viewed as a predecessor to some of
the early IFC approaches. The principle essentially states that the security classiﬁcation of an object should
be elevated to reﬂect the security level currently granted to the user in the session. In the context of this
dissertation, the approach would implicate that any benign object currently accessed in a session should be
93
==PAGE:98==
elevated to sensitive with its oPurposes-value equal to the collection of oPurposes-values for all sensitive objects
already in the session. This prevents ’leakage’ by ensuring that no sensitive data is transferred from a sensitive
object to a benign object which can subsequently be accessed without being detected by cGlobal. The high-
water mark principle can therefore be useful in restrictive scenarios to prevent leakage of sensitive information.
The drawback is the risk that a large amount of benign data risks being elevated to sensitive with all sorts
of oPurposes-values annotated and thereby in turn deny legitimate access to the benign objects. While the
high-water mark principle can be powerful in environments where a very restrictive approach is desired, it also
runs the risk of being too restrictive and cause a ripple-eﬀect in escalating the classiﬁcation of benign object
to a point where everything is sensitive and consents are required.
12.9
Scope Alterations
The intended users of the extended AERBAC model are deﬁned in the scope in section 1.2 to be larger
enterprises. One interesting consideration is to consider the applicability of the proposed extended AERBAC
model if it is used in smaller organisations:
Smaller organisations are likely to have an easier time performing policy reviewing due to the nature of the
organisations size usually implying a smaller IT-architecture and less data to review.
On the other hand,
smaller organisations are usually not suﬀering from Role-Explosion issues and will thus usually be reluctant to
spend the required resources on adapting and maintaining advanced AC systems such as ABAC and AERBAC
when RBAC can suﬃce for their needs.
Similarly, smaller organisations are likely to have fewer general activities and they might have an easier time
during the initialisation-phase by dealing with much smaller consent-forms. On the same note, smaller or-
ganisations are more likely to use more standard licensed software opposed to customised applications used in
large organisations, thus, while the number of tasks may be smaller, the ability to achieve unique distinction of
dependencies can be hard to achieve. For instance, a small accounting ﬁrm using solely Excel spreadsheets and
Word for all their tasks would make the current approach to activity detection impossible since the applications
become too general-purpose.
Thus, it is quite clear that several of the design-choices made throughout this dissertation have had larger
organisations as the primary target and that many of these choices might not be the best choice for smaller
enterprises. There are no technical aspects of the proposed extended AERBAC solution that explicitly exclude
smaller organisations from using the model, but the disadvantages and resource demands of the model are
deemed to be likely to exceed the beneﬁt of adapting the model if the target organisation is below a certain
size and this implicitly limits smaller enterprises from beneﬁting from the extended AERBAC model’s secondary
use prevention. Exact speciﬁcation of such thresholds is very diﬃcult to provide in general terms and relies
on evaluating each speciﬁc case.
12.10
Security Considerations
Throughout this project, several features are mentioned that can act as mitigating factors to some of the draw-
backs of the diﬀerent aspects of the solution. For example, the limitations to autonomous activity detection
can be mitigated by adding manual overrides. Adding manual overrides is however a security concern since
any time a security decision can be manually overridden, it opens to possibility of a compromise or abuse. As
brieﬂy discussed in section 6.3, GDPR distinguishes between internal (Art. 5.1.(b)) and external (Art. 5.1.(f))
breaches of processing. In section 6.3 it is argued that the extended AERBAC model is primarily concerned
with the internal aspects of a breach, since secondary use internally should be prevented by cGlobal while
external secondary use should be prevented by other means. Manual overrides of cGlobal are therefore not
likely to have a large impact on external breaches, as long as it is only the cGlobal-part of the permission
that can be overridden. However, it is important that any organisation implementing the extended AERBAC
model is aware that features such as manual overrides can compromise security, even if only internal security
is aﬀected.
Similarly, monitoring and ﬂagging anomalies and outliers can help to identify a breach and be eﬀective ad-
ditions where manual overrides are allowed. But the level of security requirements is important to take into
94
==PAGE:99==
consideration: If reviewing anomalies and outliers is a manual task, a breach will only be detected retrospec-
tively. Thus, secondary processing in breach with GDPR may very well take place, and even if discovered,
won’t exempt the organisation from a possible ﬁne for breaching GDPR. Monitoring is therefore primarily
well suited for optimising internal processes and conﬁguring the AERBAC model accordingly by analysing the
anomalies and overrides submitted by the system users.
12.11
Minor Remarks
The following remarks are presented in a Q&A style:
Q: Should AERBAC be applied as an add-on or fully replace existing AC measures?
A: This question is mentioned in section 7: With the current design of cGlobal, the condition can be globally
appended to any existing policy, and therefore the extended AERBAC model can technically be used either as
an add-on or fully replace existing models. The consideration of which approach is best, is therefore left up to
external factors that depend on each individual scenario:
As discussed regarding the ABAC model in section 4.1, the cost of adaptation or transition from one AC model
to ABAC can be daunting. In comparison, the cost of transitioning from other AC models to AERBAC may be
even steeper and leads to the consideration that the cost of transitioning can be mitigated if AERBAC is added
as an add-on to existing security measures instead of replacing them. In this way, the extended AERBAC is
solely used for secondary use prevention while keeping any original policies enforced via the existing AC
measures. However, this might be a half-measure which can hurt the organisation long-term, compared to
fully transitioning from the original AC model into solely using AERBAC. If the organisation is already using
policies that use attributes, adapting AERBAC might be signiﬁcantly easier, since the policies in principle
would only need to be altered by appending cGlobal. Because every organisation and their IT-architecture is
diﬀerent, it is not for this dissertation outright to state which of the two approaches is the best, and this is
left to the individual organisation to decide. It should be noted that while both approaches have merits, it
is recommended to prioritise a full transition to AERBAC to avoid having a heterogeneous architecture and
ending up with legacy systems that are hard to maintain and separate.
Q: What if atomic activities weren’t uniquely associated with a single general purpose?
A: In practice AERBAC’s cGlobal-condition doesn’t rely on whether atomic purposes are unique, as long as
all atomic activities are elements in the oPurposes-attribute of any accessed object, the origin of each activity
does not matter. However, detecting a change will be aﬀected since changes are tracked at the level of general
activity. With the current format of activity change detection, it is therefore mandated that all concurrent
atomic activities belong to the same general activity for activity detection to work.
Q: What if short-circuiting is not possible?
A: If this is the case, there are other, less attractive alternatives to ensuring that benign objects are not
subjected to cGlobal, as discussed in section 8.2, for instance using a permission guard instead. The increased
resource demand of policy reviewing by doubling the permission-sets however leads to the conclusion that
short-circuiting is the better alternative to a simple and universal global condition, but it relies on the notion
that short-circuiting operators must be supported.
Q: What if detection of activities is made at the level of atomic activities?
A: In this case, the activity detection would not have to involve the user, unless the user desires to override the
access decision ie. the user disagrees with the detection result. This would also mean that session narrowing
could successfully be implemented and as such, that a user could initially be detected to carry out a general
activity and then dynamically have their uActivities-attribute adjusted as the user accesses more restricted
sensitive objects. In other words, if detection could be made at the level of atomic activities, one of the largest
drawbacks of this project, user involvement in activity detection, would be eliminated, and the solution would
be much more ﬂexible and less intrusive.

Conclusions
Section 1.1 presents the central hypothesis that forms the ultimate goal of this dissertation: To investigate how
secondary use can be prevented using attributes enforced through the AERBAC model and its policies. It is
concluded that it is theoretically possible to extend the AERBAC model, as presented in section 11, to cover
consent-related secondary use as deﬁned in section 6.3.
As shown by the Bapple-case in section 11.2, the consent-related aspects of the GDPR, and in particular article
6.1.(a), can be solved entirely by the extended AERBAC model proposed in section 11. As addressed in section
11.3, it is important to note that the extended AERBAC model has been designed primarily with consent-
related secondary use-prevention in mind. Since there are multiple exceptions to consent-related processing,
the extended AERBAC model should be further enhanced to accommodate all the lawful processing purposes,
if the goal of complete secondary use-prevention is to be achieved. As previously discussed, there are multiple
ways to achieve a more complete prevention, but care should be taken not to either create a massive and
computationally heavy cGlobal or to use the current approach of matching purposes with activities in an
abusive and unintended manner.
The primary thesis of this dissertation was divided into three sub-goals in section 1.1. Each of these sub-goals
have been achieved in their respective problem domain:
• Sub-goal one relates to objects and consent and hence problem domain two in section 9
• Sub-goal two relates to subjects and activities and is solved in problem domain three in section 10
• Sub-goal three is solved by modifying AC policies by appending cGlobal, as addressed in section 8.
In retrospective, it can be observed from the results in the discussion in section 12.1, that dividing the goal into
multiple domains that are dependent on each other makes it apparent that each subsequent domain depend on
the previous domains. The domains addressed lastly are prone to stricter requirements and more complicated
solutions than the ﬁrst domain. For instance, the conditions-domain stipulates requirements to the format
and annotation of uActivities and oPurposes. The consequence is that the conditions-domain is quite easily
solved by keeping it simple. Since this domain is addressed ﬁrst, the solution to the condition-domain did not
need to consider the other domains and the resulting cGlobal is very simple and eﬃcient. The consent-domain
is slightly more complicated due to the requirements introduced in the conditions-domain and ﬁnally, the
activity-domain is by far the most complex and demanding domain since it relies on requirements and design
decisions made throughout the other domains. When adding the fact that uActivities needs to be dynamic,
the activity-segment is doubtlessly the weakest spot of the combined solution, having the most urgent need for
further development to achieved a sound combined solution.
One primary limitation of the proposed extended model is that the current activity of a subject involves
manual interaction. This means that intentional abuse is NOT prevented by the current solution. Logging and
reviewing sessions that present themselves as anomalies81 can mitigate this, but introduce even more undesired
manual work in turn. Furthermore, as long as secondary use-prevention is not automated, it can prove diﬃcult
to have automated processes act as the subject and be audited using the model. This is because processes acting
as subjects need the ability to respond to the prompt in which they state their ’current activity’. To mitigate
this, autonomous subjects need to have their current activities predetermined so that they can respond to the
AERBAC activity prompt. This means that, for now, the model primarily covers human users and processes
with predetermined ’current activities’, ie. processes that are designed with the extended AERBAC model in
mind.

Future work
There are several possibilities of enhancing the extended AERBAC model in its current state. All the areas
that have been identiﬁed to beneﬁt from future work have been discussed in section 12, some of the primary
areas that can beneﬁt from further investigation are:

1 Enhancing the activity detection
Using more sophisticated methods such as Machine Learning and pattern matching algorithms to reduce
the amount of required human interaction by enabling decisions to be made autonomously. This will
both reduce the impact on the user and allow processes to act as subjects that are not necessarily aware
of their own ’current activity’ explicitly. Such solutions would also be able to drop the use of the weighted

Adding the lawful processing purposes that exist as exceptions to consent-based restrictions, as discussed

Creating an implementation PoC for instance in XACML (eXtensible Access Control Markup Language),
possibly by extending the existing AERBAC policy PoC presented by Rajpoot in his PhD. thesis [23]82.

and considerations in the industry.

While the current consent-form is perfectly adequate for the needs of this dissertation, the design is

Partially Ordered sets
PO (Partially Ordered) sets[29] can be represented as Directed Acyclic Graphs and follow three general rules:
1 reﬂective: all elements are related to themselves (implicit relation arrow)
2 asymmetric: no two elements can have a bi-directional relation (arrows can only go from one to the
other, not both ways)
3 transitive: if a →b and b →c then a →c
Consider the following example: A company sets the ’root’ role as employee, which for this example will have
no permissions, ie. their permission-set, { }, is empty: Employee = ∅. The following three roles then represent
diﬀerent basic, core roles in the company: Accountant with permission-set: A, Legal Staﬀ= S and Developer
= D. The company then employs ﬁrst-line managers (FL managers) to manage developers and accountants
and thus, the FL managers inherit the permissions of both Developers and Accountants: FL Manager = A,D.
Additionally, the company CEO supervises the FL managers and also manages legal staﬀdirectly, CEO =
S,A,D. This example is visualised in ﬁgure C.a. Keep in mind that the three PO set rules must be enforced:
The accountant can’t have the permissions of the developer or vice versa, since this would be in breach with
the asymmetry rule.
