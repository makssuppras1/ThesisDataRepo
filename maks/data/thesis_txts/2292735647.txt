Abstract 
Feline infectious peritonitis (FIP) is one of the most important lethal viral diseases in cats and yet 
the pathogenesis behind the disease is not fully understood. FIP is an immune mediated disease 
that causes the development of granulomas in several internal organs and in some cases massive 
effusions in the body cavities leading to the death of the cat. 
Feline Coronavirus (FCoV), from which FIP origins, is a highly prevalent virus among cats causing 
either no or mild symptoms. This virus has been investigated extensively in order to find a specific 
mutation that, in some circumstances, can induce the virus to become highly virulent and lead to 
FIP. Failure in finding this mutation has led scientists to speculate about the existence of two 
strains of the virus with a different virulence. Other studies have instead looked more into the 
immune response of the cat itself and found out that it plays an important role in the 
pathogenesis of the disease. To date, no accurate diagnostic test is available neither a cure nor any 
vaccination has been found.  
In this veterinary master project the diversity of FCoVs in Danish cats succumbing to FIP was 
investigated mainly focusing on the role of the viral genome in the pathogenesis. The aim was to 
create a method for full genome sequencing of FCoVs using the next generation sequencing 
platform, Ion Torrent. We now offer a useful assay for full genome sequencing of future FIP cats. 
This approach was adopted to look at the feline infectious peritonitis virus (FIPV) within the cat 
itself as well as to compare it to other FCoV sequences derived from cats infected with feline 
enteric coronavirus (FECV) and FIPV available at the NCBI GenBank. This comparison was applied 
to identify possible FIP specific mutations in the cats.  
Another aim of this study was to find the most effective extraction kit for looking at the viral 
microbiome in tissue and feces of domestic and wild cats using the next generation sequencing 
platform Illumina. Several sample prepared with different extraction kits were sent for 
sequencing, but only the fecal sample prepared on the QIAsymphony RNA kit was obtained before 
the end of the study. The data was assembled using de novo assembly, which gave us the 
opportunity to search for novel viruses and investigate whether another virus could contribute to 
the pathogenesis of FIP.

1. Literature study  
A literature study was conducted with focus on the development in the research regarding the 
pathogenesis of Feline Coronavirus, and more specifically the genomic variation of the virus. The 
general features of the virus and the disease that follows with the virus are also described, 
together with a section entirely on the current diagnostic methods. The literature study thereby 
gives an introduction for understanding the complex pathogenesis of FCoV, and a general 
knowledge of the virus as well as the lethal disease that can develop from an infection. Moreover, 
a section regarding the theories behind the methods, used in this study, is describedl. Most 
sections were written based on a combination of reviews and original publications, whereas the 
section on the pathogenesis was based solely on original publications.  
1.1 Taxonomy and genomic variation  
Feline coronavirus (FCoV) belongs to the family Coronaviridae within the order Nidovirales. The 
Coronaviridae family consists of 4 subfamilies: alfa-, beta-, gamma- and deltacoronavirus. Feline 
coronavirus belongs to the subfamily alfacoronaviridae, along with other well known viruses such 
as canine coronavirus, human coronavirus and porcin transmissible gastroenteritis virus. In other 
subfamilies the renowned SARS-coronavirus and the avian infectious bronchitis virus can be found 
(Hagemeijer, et al., 2012). 
Coronaviruses are generally known to be fast evolving, as they are RNA-viruses that depend on the 
RNA polymerase for their replication. The polymerase causes several point mutations, with an 
average mutation rate of 1 mutation per 10.000 nucleotides. The consequence of this high 
mutation rate is that coronaviruses always appear in the single individual as a group of 
quasispecies. If mutations appear in a specific location, they can lead to biological changes. Along 
with these point mutations, coronaviruses are also known for their ability to transfer big 
fragments of nucleotide sequence, when a co-infection between different genera of coronavirus 
occurs. These mutations are called recombinations, and such mutation can lead the emergence of 
novel viruses. Most human circulating coronaviruses have assumingly evolved from mammalian 
coronavirus and of those the bat coronavirus has been postulated to be the main contributor. It is 
presumed, for example, that SARS-CoV originates from the bat, and a homology of 87-92 % 
between the bat SARS-like-CoV and the human SARS-CoV has been shown (Battilani, et al., 2003;

1.2. Viral properties 
 
 
Figure 2 – The genome of FCoV (Drechsler, et al., 2011) 
Feline coronavirus is a single stranded positive sense RNA virus. The genome is nearly 30kb long, 
representing one of the largest and most complex genomes among RNA viruses. The structure of 
the genome is shown in Figure 2. The genome is surrounded by an envelope, which has the 
significant protruding spikes typical of coronaviruses. The capsid coats the RNA and together they 
form a spiral shaped nucleocapsid. The genome is divided into a part that encodes the non-
structural proteins and a part that encodes the structural proteins. The structural proteins consist 
of four open reading frames (ORFs) which form the spike-
protein(S), the envelope-protein (E), the membrane 
protein (M) and the nucleocapside (N) seen in Figure 3. 
Additionally five ORFs encode the accessory proteins 3a-c 
and 7a-b. The function of 3a-c remains unknown, whereas 
7a-b translates into small secretory glycoproteins. The 
non-structural proteins consist of two ORFs which encode 
the genes for the replication including the polymerase 
(Rottier, 1999; Drechsler, et al., 2011; Borschensky and 
Reinacher, 2014).  
The spike protein, being a class 1 fusion protein, is responsible for the virus uptake in the host cell 
and possibly also for the host cell tropism (it is still being investigated). The spike protein in divided 
into a “S1” domain, responsible for receptor binding, and a “S2” domain, responsible for the fusion 
with the target cell. The spike protein depends on activation by cellular proteases for the uptake in 
the host cell, and a functional furin cleavage site is described to be at the border between the

receptor binding site (S1) and the fusion membrane site (S2) (Bosch, et al., 2003; Chang, et al., 
2012; Porter, et al., 2014).  
When the virus enters the host cell, the transcription-replication complex of the genome occurs in 
relation to intracellular double membrane vesicles (DMV) in the cells cytoplasm. The structural 
proteins are synthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum and afterwards transported to the DMV, 
where the virus-particle is assembled. Since single stranded positive sense RNA constitutes the 
genome, the RNA can be directly synthesized into proteins, as it resembles messenger RNA 
(mRNA). This occurs for the proteins needed for the RNA-synthesis, whereas the accessory and the 
structural protein are synthesized by discontinuous transcription. When the virus exits the DMV, it 
buds off the membrane, thus receiving a coating of double lipid membrane. The virus then goes 
further through the Golgi apparatus, where it is subjected to final modifications, and finally exits 
the host cell by exocytose (Rottier, 1999; Hagemeijer, et al., 2012).  
1.3. Epidemiology 
FECV, the non-virulent pathotype of FCoV, is the most common fecal pathogen in domestic cats 
(Drechsler, et al., 2011). In a recent study investigating 100 cats from a shelter, it was discovered 
that 94 % of the cats had FECV in the feces, and, of this amount, 58 % of them had diarrhea. This 
example represents the general occurrence and virulence of FECV seen in multicat households 
(Sabshin, et al., 2012). FECV can persist in dry environments for up to seven weeks, and spreads 
horizontally between cats by a fecal-oral route, resulting in the litter box being the main source of 
transmission. Additionally it was discovered that FECV can be present in saliva, showing therefore 
that drinking bowls and grooming could be a possible way of transmission. Due to cats being kept 
in high numbers and closer contact (sharing rooms and litterboxes etc.), FECV is highly prevalent in 
multicat households. Furthermore, a poor development of immunity towards FCoV makes it 
possible for a single cat to be reinfected (Pedersen, et al., 2004). When a cat becomes infected 
with FECV three things can happen: 
1. The immune system fights the virus, which is cleared from the cat after 6-8 months 
2. The cat excretes the virus in the feces continuously during the first 4-6 months and 
thereafter becomes a carrier of the virus with periodically shedding. 
3. The cat becomes a carrier and excretes virus continuously for more than 18 months

If a cat is infected with FECV, there is always a risk of developing FIP. FIP mostly occurs 
sporadically, but is also known to arise in shelters epizootically. The sporadically/enzootic FIP 
occurs in 5-12% of cats infected with FECV (Addie and Jarretto, 1992). If an epizootic outbreak of 
FIP occurs, a much higher mortality (3-49%) is observed over a period of four years. The epizootic 
outbreaks are thought to be dependent on multiple factors such as overcrowding, a rise in number 
of kittens and general stress in the population.  
FIP is mostly observed in kittens and young cats, and is known as one the most common causes of 
death in cats under 2 years. FIP is uncommon in cats of 5-10 years of age. Risk factors of FIP are: 
- 
Individual FCoV titter 
- 
The frequency of FCoV shedding 
- 
The number persistent shedders of FCoV in the household 
- 
The number of young cats and the amount of time that they stayed in the household 
- 
The cat being infected with feline leukemia virus (FeVL) 
- 
The cat being infected with feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV) 
The higher risk of developing FIP in cat being infected with either FeVL or FIV is due to their 
immune system already being overloaded or suppressed. A higher risk of developing FIP has also 
been observed in pure breed cats, males and sexual intact individuals (Marioni-Henry, et al., 2004; 
Pedersen, 2009). 
FIPV does not seem to spread horizontally since experiments, trying to infect cats with FIPV 
derived from the feces, were not successful. A case of placental transfer has been observed in one 
cat that developed FIP during pregnancy, but otherwise, no transmission pattern has been 
observed for FIPV (Rottier, et al., 2005; Kipar, et al., 2006; Pedersen, et al., 2012).  
1.4. Pathogenesis  
The pathogenesis concerning the development of FIP has been a topic for debate, since it was first 
described as an important disease in cats in the sixties (Holzworth, 1963). Two main theories have 
been developed. The most generally accepted is the “Internal Mutation Theory”, where it is 
believed that when an individual cat becomes infected with the non-virulent FECV, the virus will in 
some cases develop into the virulent FIPV, due to mutations in certain places of the viral genome.

This theory is based on coronaviruses high ability to mutate. The mutations leading to the 
development of FIP are believed to occur in a place(s) in the genome, which alter the cell tropism 
and thereby allow the virus to efficiently spread systemically through monocytes/macrophages 
(Stoddart and Scott, 1989; Rottier, et al., 2005). An alternative hypothesis is based on the belief 
that two strains of the virus are circulating the environment, as either avirulent or virulent. A cat 
will develop FIP symptoms only if infected with the virulent strain (FIPV) (Brown, et al., 2009). 
The progress made in the research of the pathogenesis of feline coronavirus, mainly concerning 
two mentioned theories, is described in the following sections. 
1.4.1. The Internal mutation Theory 
The first time scientists began to suspect that it was only small mutations, which separated FECV 
from FIPV, was in 1984. A group of scientists carried out an experiment on SPF cats that were 
inoculated with two different strains of FCoV: one strain was avirulent (FECV) and the other one 
gave rise to FIP symptoms (FIPV). They examined the antibodies from both groups of cats via 
immunoblotting, immunofluorescence assay and in vitro-neutralization test and found no 
difference between them (Pedersen, et al., 1984).  
1.4.1.1. The 7b gene 
The first gene, that was thought to be significant to the development of FIP, was the accessory 7b 
gene. A group of scientists performed an experiment sequencing the 3’end of the viral genome, 
using in vitro strains of both FIPV and FECV. The results revealed a deletion of 238bps in the 7b 
gene of FECV. This finding challenged the “Internal Mutation Theory”, because FIPV were 
supposed to show mutations compared to FECV, and not the opposite. The fact that FIPV could 
have had an insertion was deemed unlikely, as similar sequencing of CCV did not show this 
deletion (Vennema, et al., 1992) .  
A later study revealed that the deletion in the 7b gene was not apparent when looking at the two 
different virus strains in vivo. They examined the 7b gene of 16 cats infected with FECV, where 
four of them developed FIP. The 7b gene were intact and similar in all cats, and therefore the 
authors suggested that the previous deletion found in FECV, where due to the adjustment to the

cell culture, and that 7b on the contrary was an important virulent factor for both FECV and FIPV 
(Herrewegh, et al., 1995). 
Another study supporting the Internal Mutation Theory was performed using 19 cats chronically 
infected with feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV) and 20 of their siblings without the disease. All of 
the cats were orally infected with FECV. Two of the cats with FIV developed FIP, and after 
extracting the virus (FIPV), it was inoculated in the peritoneum of two SPF cats. These two SPF cats 
developed FIP, thereby confirming the diagnosis of FIP. The 7b gene of the two FIP cats was 
compared to each other and with the 7b gene of the FECVs of the other cats. The genetic 
correlation showed a 99% homology between the different strains. The study also found proves of 
immunosuppressed cats having a higher risk of developing FIP. The immunosuppressed cats also 
showed a higher and longer fecal shedding of FECV (Poland, et al., 1996).  
In 2009 it was concluded that the 7b gene did not play an important role in the virulence of FCoV. 
A study investigated the 7b gene of 20 cats with wet FIP and 20 clinically healthy cats infected with 
FECV. The study showed that 32 of the 40 cats had an intact 7b gene, and eight cats had small 
deletions in the 7b gene. Three out of eight were FIP cats. This study disclosed that even cats with 
deletions in 7b could develop FIP, and therefore the interest in the 7b gene decreased (Lin, et al., 
2009a).  
1.4.1.2. The 3c gene 
The subsequent place in the genome that was under suspicion of being a determinant in the FECV 
to FIPV development was the accessory 3c-gene. A study by Vennema et al. conducted in 1998 
examined six pairs of cats in three different geographical regions, each consisting of one cat 
infected with FIPV and one cat infected with FECV. They also considered supplementary FIP cases 
from a cattery in California. The 3’ end of the genome was sequenced and phylogenetic tests on 
these sequences showed that the FIPV/FECV pairs from the same region were exceedingly similar, 
compared to the FIPV/FECV pair from a different region. Moreover, a deletion in the 3c gene 
causing a truncation of the gene was found in 11 out of 13 FIP cats, showing that FIPV had possibly 
mutated from FECV. They also found two FIP cats from the same house to have different deletions 
in the 3c gene, which confirmed the idea that mutations causing FIPV develop in the individual cat 
and is not transmitted horizontally between cats (Vennema, et al., 1998). All this evidence

supported the Internal Mutation Theory and findings of this study were later confirmed by 
another group of scientists (Pedersen, et al., 2009).  
A study in 2010 investigated 28 FIPV cats and 27 FECV cats. The 3c gene was sequenced and found 
always intact in the FECV cats, while 78 % of the FIP cats had a truncated 3c gene. The authors 
postulated as a consequence of these findings, that the 3c gene was an important gene for the 
replication of the virus in the intestine for FECV, while it was unessential for FIPV. The feces of FIP 
cats were also examined for FCoV and the scientists found out that some cats had FCoVs with an 
intact 3c gene. They explained this by asserting that the cat had a superinfection with FECV 
(Chang, et al., 2010). 
Based on the previous study, that discovered FIP cats to have FCoV with an intact 3c gene in the 
feces, a study was set up to test if this FCoV could be transmitted horizontally. FCoVs with an 
intact 3c gene were again found in the feces of a number of FIP cats, and the authors called this 
FIPV with an intact 3c gene, instead of considering the possibility of a superinfection. FCoV with 
intact 3c inoculated oronasally into SPF cats did not cause FIP. However, the virus was later found 
in the intestine. The tissue of cats with FIP was infectious to the SPF cats after both oronassaly and 
intraperitoneal inoculation regardless of the 3c gene being intact or not, but only the FIPV with 
intact 3c gene was shed in feces after oronassaly inoculation. The SPF cats infected oronasally with 
FECV all shed FECV in their feces, consistently with the epidemiology of FECV. Surprisingly one out 
of three cats infected intraperitoneally with FECV, shed a low level of FECV in the feces later, 
suggesting that FECV is able to spread systemically, however not to a great extent. The authors 
concluded that the acquisition of macrophage tropism determining the difference between FECV 
and FIPV “might be relative rather than absolute”, and that it is possible for FIPV to be shed in the 
feces, when having an intact 3c gene, but it is not infective (Pedersen, et al., 2012).  
In a recent study a group of scientists from Germany sequenced the 3c gene of 28 cats that had 
succumbed to FIP. It was possible for them to obtain a full gene sequence of the 3c protein in 19 
of the cats. Five out of 19 cats had an intact 3c gene certainly indicating that 3c cannot be the 
single factor determining the virulence of FECV and FIPV cats (Borschensky and Reinacher, 2014). 
An additional study also discovered that two out of 19 FIP cats had an intact 3c gene. They had ten 
paired samples consisting of an ascites sample and a fecal sample of the same FIP cats. The feces

1.4.1.3. The spike protein 
An additional gene that was investigated, in order to find a possible connection to the 
pathogenesis of FCoV, was the spike gene. As mentioned earlier in section 1.2, this gene encodes 
the typical spikes that appear on the surfaces of all coronaviruses. The spike protein is responsible 
for the entry of the virus in the host cell, as it facilitates membrane fusion and receptor binding.  
In 2005 a group of scientists decided to test if a mutation in the spike gene of FCoV could be 
responsible for an altered host cells tropism. To examine this, the spike gene of a FECV strain 
serotype 2 was transferred into a FIPV strain, making a recombinant hybrid of FIPV. The 
recombinant FIPV were inoculated into a cell culture of macrophages.  It was observed that the 
recombinant FIPV had a reduced ability of infecting the macrophages, as only 2 % of the cells were 
infected after ten hours. On the contrary a control done with the primary FIPV strain, grew rapidly 
and destroyed the entire cell culture (Rottier, et al., 2005).  
A groundbreaking study towards understanding the pathogenesis of FIP was completed in 2012. 
Opposed to several other studies concerning FCoV, these scientists performed full genome 
sequencing of 11 FCoVs of each pathotype. The specimens were derived from SPF cats that were 
inoculated with serotype 1 FECV or FIPV and developed symptoms accordingly. They identified 
two point mutations (A→T and A→C) at position 1058 of the spike gene, which altered the amino 
acid from methionine seen in the FECVs to leucine seen in 91,6 % of the FIP cats. Almost all of the 
FIP cats that did not have a mutation at position 1058, had another point mutation (T→ G) at 
position 1060 in the spike gene, which changes the amino acid from serine, found in all the FECV 
cats, to alanine in the FIP cats (4,2%). In total they discovered a 95,8 % correlation, between 
mutations in these two locations and the virulent pathotype, FIP. This could finally be the specific 
mutation causing FIP, searched for throughout the years. However, if only small and simple 
mutations were responsible for the conversion of FECV to FIPV, why were FIP not more prevalent? 
The authors made a calculation estimating how often this mutation statistically would occur. The 
result was that the mutation should arise once in every 1,5 x 104  genomes being created. They

108 genome equivalents of FCoV per every microliter of feces. On the basis of this calculation, the 
prevalence of FIP should be much higher than what is observed. The authors therefore concluded, 
that other factors, such as additional mutations or host factors, must affect the pathogenesis of 
FIP as well (Chang, et al., 2012). 
An additional study, focusing on the role of the spike gene in the pathogenesis, was done in 2013. 
11 FIP cats and 30 cats infected with FECV were included in the study. All the cats had their spike 
gene sequenced and analyzed. The study revealed that the entire FECVs S1/S2 cleavage sites were 
highly conserved, and that FIPVs on the other hand were considerably more variable in this region. 
The study further revealed that 10/11 FIP cats harbored mutations in the S1/S2 furin cleavage site, 
and that the mutations were different in all the cats, which supported the Internal Mutation 
Theory. The effect of these mutations on the furin cleavage was checked using human furin in a 
cell culture. Most mutations caused a negative effect of furin cleavage, but the authors suggested 
that the mutations in the S1/S2 site could contribute to the spike protein being more open, and 
therefore more accessible for cleavage by other proteases (Licitra, et al., 2013).  
A recent study performed in 2014 was setup to search for the mutation in position 1058 of the 
spike gene, found by Chang et al. (Chang, et al., 2012). The scientists had a total of 112 samples 
from a combination of FIP cats and clinically healthy cats. The samples contained both tissue and 
fecal samples from each cat. The samples were initially subjected to quantitative real time PCR. 
The results showed that in the group of cats with FIP, 95 % of the tissue samples and 81 % of the 
fecal samples were positive for FCoV, whereas the clinically healthy cats had 22% of the tissue 
samples being positive for FCoV and 60 % of the fecal samples. The FCoV positive samples were 
subsequently subjected to pyrosequencing of a 153 nucleotide long fragment, comprising position 
1058 of the spike protein. The FIP cats, that did not show a leucine substitution at position 1058, 
had a conventional PCR performed for a fragment containing position 1060 of the spike protein. 
The authors found, similar to the study done by Chang et al., a substitution from methionine to 
leucine in 91 % of the tissue samples of FIP cats. Unlike the previous study 89 % of the non FIP cats 
tissue samples had leucine in position 1058. Moreover, 9 % of the tissue samples originating from 
FIP cats had methionine at position 1058. Based on these results the authors concluded that the

1.4.2. Circulating avirulent and virulent strains 
An entire new hypothesis regarding the pathogenesis of FCoV surfaced in 2009 (Brown, et al., 
2009). A group of scientists formulated a hypothesis which suggested that the virus causing FIP 
(FIPV) and the enteric virus (FECV) were two distinct strains circulating the environment. This 
hypothesis implied that only cats infected with the virulent strain (from an outside source) would 
develop disease. The scientists conducted a study on samples from 56 cats, collected during a 
period of two years. Eight of the cats were diagnosed with FIP and 48 cats were infected with 
FECV. They selected four genes of the FCoV genome to sequence; the M-gene, the 7b gene, the 
gene encoding the polymerase and the S gene. After sequencing the genes, a phylogenetic test

FIPV was observed, as would have been expected if the Internal Mutation Theory were accurate.

1.4.3. Wet and dry FIP 
When a cat becomes infected with the virulent pathotype FIPV, the reaction of the immune 
system will determine if and how disease develops. In general there are two pathological forms of 
FIP: wet and dry. The wet form is characterized by effusion in the body cavities. Especially 
abdominal effusion/ascites is a key symptom of FIP. The dry form, without effusions, is 
characterized by granulomas in different organs and is harder to diagnose clinically, as no specific 
clinical signs are observed.  
The immune response plays a crucial role in the development of FIP. Especially the cellular 
immune response seems to be a key factor in fighting and suppressing the disease, as the 
following responses to FIPV have been observed:  
- 
Cats that develop wet FIP often get acutely sick, whereas cats with the dry FIP often 
develop chronic disease. The chronic disease is defined by granulomas which confines the 
virus. These granulomas are developed by a cellular immune response. 
- 
Some cats disposed to FIPV never develop disease. These cats often have a very strong 
cellular immune response towards the virus.

1.5. Diagnosis 
The diagnosis of FIP is often difficult since no current diagnostic test is available for diagnosing FIP. 
This result in the diagnosis being composed of several different factors, including signalement, 
anamnesis and paraclinical parameters. Together these features can indicate a probable diagnosis 
of FIP, but diagnosis is never exact.  
The following factors in signalement and anamnesis can be suggestive of FIP: 
The age of the cat can be an indicator of FIP, since FIP is often seen in kittens and young cats less 
than five years of age. However, cats over ten years also have a higher prevalence of FIP. Centain 
breeds are predisposed to FIP such as Birmans, Bengals, Himalayans, Ragdolls, Abyssinians and 
Rexes. Also cats originating from a mulitcat household are in higher risk. The anamnesis often 
reveals a history of nonspecific signs such as anorexia, fever, weight loss, depression, seizures, eye 
disease and ataxia. In some cases the cat will present in the clinic with acute effusion in the 
abdomen, which is a significant evidence of FIP. However, the presence of effusion in body cavities 
is not equal to FIP, as 50 % of cats with effusions suffer from another disease. Involvement of the 
eyes and CNS seems to be more frequent in cases of dry FIP.

1.6. Treatment, prevention and control 
Currently it is generally accepted that FIPV is derived from FECV, and contrary to FIPV a general 
knowledge concerning the transmission of FECV is acknowledged. One option for avoiding FIP 
would be to minimize the transmission of FECV through management of multicat households. As 
FECV spreads mainly through a fecal-oral route, using the same litterbox along with common and 
food and drinking bowls, should be avoided. Since FECV can survive in a dry environment up to 
seven week, indirect spread via employees and their clothing is also possible. However, FCoV is 
highly susceptible to cleaning agents and disinfectants, and therefore proper cleaning and good 
employee hygiene would have a huge impact on the virus persistence.   
Specific drugs for treating FIPV are not available. The treatment of FIP is therefore mostly 
symptomatic, making use of corticosteroids and non-steroid anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). 
Draining of the effusion in the abdomen in wet FIP cases and subsequent intraperitoneal injections 
of corticosteroids can also be performed.  
Scientists have additionally tried to use antiviral drugs against FIP. An inhibitor of one of the main 
proteases encoded by coronaviruses called 3CL has been developed and has shown inhibition of 
the viral replication of FCoV in vitro. The immune suppressor Cyclosporine A has also shown to 
inhibit replication of FCoV in vitro, but in vivo studies are still missing. Another way to fight the 
virus infection is by inhibiting the production of viral protein. Ribivirin is a broad spectrum triazol 
nucleoside, which has this effect. This drug works in vitro, but in vivo difficulties obtaining a

therapeutic level has presented, due to serious side effects such as hemolysis. Inhibitory peptides 
that block the region in the spike-gene responsible for fusion with the host cell have shown great 
results, inhibiting the replication by 97 %, however, so far, it has only been done in in vitro studies 
(Hartmann, 2005; Pedersen, 2014a).   
Many studies have tried to identify a vaccination for FIP, but many problems have occurred. One 
of the problems is antibody dependent enhancement (ADE), which causes the cat to become even 
more and faster sick, when infected with FIPV after vaccination, compared to non-vaccinated cats.  
Another problem is that vaccines only are developed for serotype 2, as it is the only serotype able 
to grow in cell cultures. The vaccine, on the American market, is intranasal and based on mutant 
DF2-FIPV strain. It stimulates the cell mediated immune response in the upper airway of the cat. 
The problem with this vaccine is that it is only beneficial, when the cat is seronegative for FCoV. 
Additionally, the vaccine is only effective if given cats older than 16 weeks, and by that time most 
kittens are already seropositive for FCoV (Hebben, et al., 2004; Hartmann, 2005; Addie, et al.,

1.7. PCR 
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is the process of amplifying DNA in vitro, which is beneficial for 
the continuous work of the DNA in interest. The process has 3 steps:  
1) Denaturation of the DNA to single stranded molecules – this step occurs at a temperature 
of 92-96°C. 
2) Annealing, where the primers hybridize to the complementary DNA in each flank of the 
target in interest (the 3’ hydroxyl ends of the primers face the target DNA). Here the 
highest temperature possible is used, compared to melting temperature of the specific 
primers, normally around 50-60°C. 
3) Extension/elongation of the primer by the DNA-polymerase, normally at a temperature 
around 70°C. The polymerase incorporates one of the four nucleotides (A, T, C and G) in 
extension to the primer, according to the base-pairing principle. 
==PAGE:29==
Veterinary Master’s Thesis 
 
Pia Ryt-Hansen 
 
 
DTU VET 
29 
 
An overview of the PCR process can be viewed in Figure 13. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
These 3 steps make out one cycle, which is repeated around 40 times. The amount of DNA 
generated rises exponentially, since the product of one cycle can be used as template for the next 
cycle.  
The primers are one of the most important things for the PCR to function, as they are responsible 
for having the specific fragment of DNA in interest sequenced. It can be difficult to design primers, 
as many properties need to be taken into account. Ideally primers should have a CG content over 
50 %, be a least 20 nucleotides in length and have an annealing temperature around 50-60°C. 
Additionally, the primers should be found in conserved areas of the genome, so that no 
mismatches occur, when the primers bind to the template. Achieving primers that maintains all of 
these properties can be challenging, as some genomes have a high AT content and have a lot of 
variation in the nucleotide sequence from sequence to another.  
The polymerase, used in the PCR, is heat resistant and originates from Thermus aquaticus(Taq), 
thus making it possible to perform the PCR in elevated temperatures. High temperatures makes it 
more likely to avoid the same amount of mismatched annealing to non-target sequences, which 
would occur if the polymerase was working under lower temperatures (e.g. room temperature).

FCoV is a single stranded RNA-virus, and as the Taq polymerase is only able to work with a DNA 
template, an additional step has to be added to the PCR process. This step is implemented before 
the PCR cycling. For converting the RNA into cDNA, a technique called reverse transcription PCR 
(RT-PCR) is performed. In this process a reverse transcriptase enzyme is used for translating RNA 
to cDNA. This cDNA is then used as a template for further amplification, as described before. This 
step can either be done as “one step” in the same tube as the rest of the PCR-reaction, or in “two 
steps” where the cDNA is first created and then transferred to another tube in which the PCR-
reaction occurs (Arnheim and Erlich, 1992; LifeTechnologies, 2015d). 
1.8.rtRT-PCR 
To quantify the amount of a specific RNA/DNA in a sample, a quantitative real time PCR method 
can be used. This method utilizes the process of PCR of a specific region in the DNA sequence. If 
the sample constitutes RNA, a reverse transcriptase step is performed before the PCR. The amount 
of DNA that is produced during PCR reaction is measured by 
labeling of the one or several compounds of the reaction with 
fluorescent dyes. Using a TaqMan probe is one of the methods 
available. In this method, two specific primers bind to the 
template DNA and a TaqMan-probe binds in between the two 
primers. The TaqMan-probe consists of complementary DNA to 
the template, and in each end it has a quencher and a reporter.

The reporter sends out a fluorescent signal, which is absorbed by the quencher, when these two 
are in close proximities. When the polymerase starts to amplify the specific sequence between the 
two primers and reaches the TaqMan probe, the probe breaks in two, separating the reporter and 
the quencher. Since the reporter and the quencher are no longer in close proximity, the 
fluorescent signal is not absorbed, and the reporter now emits a measurable fluorescent signal. 
This process can be seen in Figure 14. This emitted signal is measured by a detector, which 
measures the amount of fluorescent signal emitted in each cycle of the PCR. The more 
amplification of template that occurs, the more fluorescent signals will be emitted. As discussed 
earlier, the PCR will at one point happen exponentially, and in this phase it is possible to quantify 
the amount of DNA in a given sample. This is done by considering the dissociation curve of a 
graph, created by the computer that measures the fluorescents. An illustration of a dissociation 
curve can be seen in Figure 15. The curve consists of the logarithm (log 10) of the amount of 
fluorescent signal on the y-axis and the cycle number of the PCR on the x-axis. On the graph there 
is a set threshold of fluorescence, which is showing that an actual exponential amplification of 
DNA is occurring and not simply background noise. The background noise is visualized by making a 
no template control (NTC). Estimating the amount of DNA in the sample is performed by reading 
off, at which exact cycle number the on the x-axis, the curve crosses the threshold, and this cycle 
number is termed the Cycle threshold value (Ct value). The more DNA is present in the primary 
sample, the faster the threshold will be reached, and the lower the Ct value, as it will not require 
many PCR cycles to reach a high level of fluorescence. If the Ct value is over 40 cycles the sample is 
considered to be negative for the DNA of interest (if there is 40 cycles in the PCR). To identify the 
efficiency of a rtRT-PCR assay, a dilution series of the sample is made, and performed on the given 
assay. From the resulting Ct values, a standard curve is made, as seen in graph of Figure 16. The x-
axis is made up of a logarithmic scale (log 10) resembling the concentration of the different 
dilutions of the sample, and the y-axis consists of the Ct values. The Ct value at each dilution is 
placed in the graph. If the efficiency of the assay is a 100 % a linear graph will appear, since the 
assay is producing an exponential amount of copies, compared to the first dilution. If the efficiency 
is 100 % the slope of the graph would be -3,3 equal to a 10 fold dilution of the sample resulting in 
the Ct values of each dilution being 3,3 apart. Many factors can influence the amplification of the

1.9. Sangers sequencing 
A way to determine the specific nucleotide sequence of a genomic fragment is by using Sangers 
sequencing method. The method is dependent on “chain terminating inhibitors” in a primer 
extension process. These chain terminating inhibitors are analogs to deoxyribonucleotide 
triphosphate(dNTP), however modified so they do not contain a  3’-hydroxyl group. This 
modification assures that the polymerase will not be able to extend the complimentary strand 
further, after an incorporation of one of these. The modified dNTPs are termed 
dideoxyribonucleotide triphosphate(ddNTP) and exist for all four nucleotides. The proportion of 
ddNTPs and dNTPs, for the primer extension process, is regulated so that an incorporation of the

occurs, but with a different ddNTPs in each tube, will reveal the entire sequence of the template.  
The original Sangers method created by Frederick Sanger was set up in the following way: Four 
tubes, each containing the DNA-sequence of interest, a primer, the polymerase, the four dNTPs 
(dATP, dCTP, dCTP og dTTP) where one of them was labelled with fluorescent 32P, and one ddNTP, 
was prepared. The polymerase then bound to a hybridized primer and began to synthesize a 
complementary strand until it inserted a ddNTP. This resulted in many different fragment lengths. 
When the fragments were transferred to a gel electrophoresis they separated by length. The 
fragments were visualized on the electrophoresis due to the one dNTP, which were labeled with 
32P. The wells in which Sanger put a certain ddNTP e.g. ddATP revealed where in the sequence the 
nucleotide was, compared to how far it went on the gel electrophoresis. When the results of all 
the tubes were viewed together, a pattern of different fragment length was seen, ideally being 
separated by only one base in length (Sanger, et al., 1977).  
Modifications to the Sanger sequencing method have been made over time, especially to make 
the process automatized. Currently all four ddNTPs are labelled with a different fluorescence, and 
the primer extension process is carried out as usual, but with all the reactions occurring in a single 
tube. Later the fragments go through a capillary electrophoresis, and the nucleotides are struck by 
a laser, which makes the nucleotide emit fluorescence. The specific florescent signal is detected 
and sent to a computer, where the entire sequence is recorded automatically, as seen in Figure 17 
(Metzker, 2005; Hutchison,Clyde A.,,III, 2007).

1.10. NGS-methods 
Sangers sequencing is now known to be a “first generation sequencing” method, and the 
advanced methods, which were developed later, are termed “next generation sequencing”(NGS) 
methods. The main difference, which differentiates the two generations, is the ability of NGS to 
make giant parallel analysis, with a high throughput, at a very low cost. The cost per million bases 
using the NGS platform Illumina HiSeq is 0,07 US dollars whereas the cost of Sangers sequencing is 
2400 US dollars. One of the few disadvantages of NGS is conversely the high throughput, which 
equals to an enormous dataset, which needs to be stored and analyzed. NGS platforms can make 
reads along the size of 76-400bp, whereas Sangers sequencing has a read length of up to 900bp. 
However, the number of reads obtained in total, still makes NGS superior. NGS can generate over 
600 GB of sequences in one run, whereas Sanger instruments only generate around 100Kb per 
run. The speed of the sequencing is also significantly different. The Sanger sequencer model 
AB3730xl gives an output of 2,88 megabases per day, whereas the next generation system 454 by 
Roche can give an output of 14 gigabases per run, and one run takes approximately ten hours. 
Every platform has a different coverage, telling how many times a nucleotide has been sequenced, 
which gives an estimation of how confident the data is.  
The NGS technologies can be used on amplicons, 
where a reference sequence is already availble, but 
can also be used for de novo sequencing, where it is 
possible to obtain sequences from all the different 
genomic material of a sample. This can for example 
be utilized to search for novel viruses in a given a 
sample.

Before the actual sequencing, a library preparation of DNA or RNA in interest is needed. In this 
process the sequences are divided into fragments, and adaptors are ligated onto the ends of the 
fragments. Thereafter an amplification step is performed, ensuring that enough templates are 
available for further sequencing. The sequencing which happens after the library preparation is 
different depending on which platform is being used. The two platforms used in this study are 
described below: 
The Ion Torrent employs semiconductor sequencing.  This process 
utilizes emulsion PCR, where a single library template bound to a 
microbead is captured in an oil droplet which contains polymerase, 
primers and dNTPs as seen in Figure 19. These droplets fit unto a 
microwell or a glass plate inside a microchip, where the amplification 
of the template will take place. This microchip takes advantage of the fact that every time the 
polymerase inserts a nucleotide to the extending sequence, a proton released. This proton gives 
rise to a small change in pH, which makes the microchip active. The microchip is continuously 
flooded by one nucleotide at a time. If the nucleotide, that the chip is flooded with, is inserted by 
the polymerase, a proton is released, and the microchip becomes active and then registers which 
specific nucleotide has been incorporated. If the nucleotide flooding the microchip, does not 
match the template, no signal will be detected. In the case of two or more of the same nucleotides 
being incorporated after each other, the microchip will detect a higher change in pH, and can 
thereby count how many of the same nucleotide is incorporated. This technology is unique to any 
other NGS method, as it does not utilize fluorescent dyes, thus making the sequencing more 
affordable and fast. Another platform of next generation sequencing is the Illumina MiSeq/HiSeq.

1. Own studies 
2.1. Introduction 
FIP is a very important and lethal disease caused by FCoV, which is commonly seen in kittens and 
young cats originating from a multi cat household, where the virus is highly prevalent (Pedersen, 
2009). Currently, veterinarians do not have a useful diagnostic tool for making a confident 
diagnosis of FIP (Addie, et al., 2009). This is a problem due to the outcome of the diagnosis of FIP 
which is always euthanization. The main reason for this problem of diagnosis is that the 
pathogenesis of FIP is not yet fully understood as described in Section 1.4. For a long time it has 
been debated whether FIP arises from an internal mutation in the far less pathogenic pathotype 
FECV or if there are actually two different viruses circulating the environment. The Internal 
Mutation Theory is by far the most accepted one, even though no 100 % specific mutation 
associated with FIPV has been discovered. Recent studies have shown that a point mutation in the 
spike gene might be a contributing factor to the development of FIP or at least to the systemic 
spread of FCoV (Chang, et al., 2012; Porter, et al., 2014).  
The aim of this study was to investigate FCoVs sequences from Danish cats, by using next 
generation sequencing technologies. The study was the first study done in Denmark where full 
genome sequences of FCoVs were obtained and studied.  
The first objective was to develop a method for quantifying FCoV in different samples, such as 
isolates, tissues and feces. Secondly, an effective method for a conventional PCR, making full 
genome sequencing possible on the Ion Torrent possible, was discovered. Thereafter, the obtained 
full genome sequences were aligned against other FCoVs, to see if any specific mutation(s) 
associated with FIPV could be found.  Furthermore de novo sequencing was carried out on both 
cat tissue and fecal samples, using next generation sequencing technologies. These samples had 
been prepared using different extraction kits, to discover which kit was the best for the further de 
novo sequencing. The result of the de novo sequencing was used to look for potential virus 
associated with the virulent pathotype of FCoV and for investigating the virome of the cats.  
These methods can hopefully be used in the future at the DTU VET. Especially the method for 
sequencing FCoV on the Ion Torrent might be used farther for future surveillance of FIP cases,

For full genome sequencing on Ion Torrent sequencer a total concentration of 1000ng of the 
different PCR-products were needed. Due to the fact that the PCR products (fragments) were

genome, the individual fragments amounted. This amount was then converted into ng of PCR 
product needed from each fragment. Thereafter, the concentration of each fragment measured 
on the spectrophotometer was counted in, by dividing the amount of product needed, with the 
actual concentration of the PCR product. The PCR-products were then mixed with a TE-buffer 
which contained Tris-buffer and EDTA for stabilizing the DNA (LifeTechnologies, 2015g). The full

the DNA templates were the same length. At this stage the lengths of the fragments were 
measured on the BioAnalyzer (Agilent Technologies) to see if the desired length had been reached. 
If the length was as desired, a purification step was initiated using magnetic beads. Then a heating 
step was executed for the adapters to bind to the ends of the templates. The 3 samples (Isolate 2, 
Cat 1 Karlslunde and Cat 2 Holstebro) were at this stage also given a specific barcode, so that they 
could be told from each other later, since all the samples were pooled together for the actual 
sequencing. An additional purification step, using magnetic beads was then completed, and a PCR-
amplification of the templates with adaptors was performed. The lengths of the templates were 
measured a second time on the Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies) to see if the adaptors had 
bound to the template. If the desired lengths were obtained, two additional purification steps 
using magnetic beads would follow. The samples were then pooled, making a total template 
concentration of 26 pmol, and transferred to the Ion OneTouch™ 2 machine (Life Technologies), 
which made the base for an emulsion PCR, creating oil droplets. One droplet would ideally contain 
one sphere, one template, polymerase and dNTPs so that a clonal amplification of the template 
could occur, giving eventually one sphere bound to many equal templates. Next a further 
purification step followed where the templates were purified/enriched with the Ion One Touch ES 
(Life Technologies) into a neutralization buffer. Hereafter, the quality of the sample was 
determined using an IonSphere™ Quality Control assay, by looking at both the concentration of 
template in both the enriched and non-enriched samples. If the concentration and the difference 
between the two samples were as desired, the enriched sample was sequenced in the Ion Torrent 
sequencer using an Ion 316 chip (LifeTechnologies, 2014; LifeTechnologies, 2015b; 
LifeTechnologies, 2015c).

2.3.4.1. Analysis of the full genome sequences from Ion-Torrent 
The data constituting the total reads of the 3 samples originating from Isolate 2(Sample 1), Cat 1 
Karlslunde(Sample 2) and Cat 2 Holstebro(Sample 3) were imported using the “Ion Torrent Import” 
function in the program CLC Genomics Workbench version 6.5. Afterwards, the total reads from 
each sample was assembled in 2 different ways. First, the reads were assembled using the toolbox 
function “De Novo Assembly” with CLC’s default settings and with the setting “Create Report” 
under “Result Handling” to create an assembly summary report. Thereafter the reads were 
assembled using a reference sequence, using the toolbox function “Map Reads to Reference” with 
CLC’s default settings, creating a “Consensus assembly”. The EU186072 strain was chosen as the 
reference strain since it is the longest of the serotype 1 FCoV strains in the NCBI GenBank.  
To discover whether the three FCoVs sequences of this project were serotype 1 or serotype 2, a 
search was carried out in the sequences. The sequence of the two primers Iffs and Icfs (Addie, et 
al., 2003), which are specific for either FCoV or CCoV, were searched for in the three FCoV 
sequences. This was performed using the function “Find Sequence”. 
An alignment of the three FCoV sequences(Concensus assembly of the Sample 1, de novo 
assembly of Sample 2 and Consensus assembly for Sample 3) and four existing FCoV serotype 1 
was created using the FECV serotype 1 strains: FJ938060, FJ938053, HQ012371 and the FIPV strain 
DQ848678. The command “Create Alignment” under the option “Alignments and Trees” was used 
in line with CLC’s default settings. The alignment was created to observe changes in the amino acid 
sequence specific for the FIPVs compared to the FECVs. 
When looking for “variant detection”/quasispecies, the raw data (containing the total reads) of 
each sample were mapped against its own consensus sequence, assembled either using “de novo 
assembly” or “map reads to reference”. The result of this mapping was then used for the function 
“Quality-based Variant Detection”. This function searches for different variants in all the reads 
compared to the reference sequence. These different variants can consist of SNVs, MNVs, 
replacements, deletions or insertions. When looking for a variant, the program also takes into 
account the quality of the neighboring bases next to the given variant, and thereby estimates 
whether the variant is probable compared to the specific area of the genome. A “maximum gap 
and mismatch count” is also set, so that, in case of mismatches occurring in the neighborhood 
==PAGE:53==
Veterinary Master’s Thesis 
 
Pia Ryt-Hansen

bases, the variant will not be included in the final variant result. These values were chosen to be 
set as default. An average coverage also needs to be chosen, depending on how many reads were 
available in the dataset. If the coverage of a region in the genome is low, then, the probability of a 
specific variant being found in this region is also low. This value was also set to be as default. A 
“minimum frequency” was chosen too. This value sets a threshold for the minimum number of 
reads that displays a variant in a given position, compared to the reference sequence. This value 
was first set to be 8 % which gives us a good probability of having PCR and sequencing errors 
discarded (CLC bio, 2015). Later a “minimum frequency” of 5 % was chosen to see the difference 
between the two results.

2.4.2.8 Qiagen Gene Read rRNA Depletion Kits 
This kit was used on two of the samples chosen for the de novo 
sequencing for testing the effect of removal of ribosomal RNA 
(rRNA) from the samples. rRNA is the most representative RNA 
in a total RNA sample, constituting 80 %. This can be 
problematic because this RNA is often not of significant value 
and can be disturbing in sample and therefore a waste of 
sequencing resources.  
This kit is designed to remove rRNA with an efficiency of up to 
99,9 %. The kit removes all types of rRNA, and retains other 
RNAs. This happens through a hybridization of a specific 
oligonucleotide (DNA) to rRNA of different sizes. The bound 
oligonucleotide is recognized by a specific antibody, and this

The GeneRead rRNA Depletion Kit was used as follows:  
1. The BioMag Protein G Beads were vortexed and 125µL for each number of samples was

2. The supernatant was removed, so only the beads were left in the reaction tube. 
3. For each sample another tube containing the depletion mix were done. The mix is made up 
by 10µL hybridization buffer, 5µg RNA(26-28µL in our case), 10µL HMR rRNA Depletion

volume of 100µL. 
4. The depletion mix were mixed thoroughly and incubated for 70°C for five minutes. 
5. The mix was transferred into the 2mL reaction tube containing the beads, and vortexed to 
resuspend the beads.

2.4.5. Next generation sequencing – Illumina MiSeq library preparation: 
The first samples sent for de novo sequencing were prepared following the TruSeq stranded total 
RNA preparation protocol (Illumina, 2015), shortly described as follows.  
The RNA was first denatured and then analyzed on the BioAnalyser (Agilent Technologies) to 
visualize what sizes of RNA were present in the samples. The samples that had been through the 
rRNA depletions kit were purified using RNA XP cleanup. The other samples were dried down to a 
volume of around 10µL on the Eppendorf Concentrator plus. All the samples were then subjected 
to enzymatic fragmentation, and a first strand cDNA synthesis was performed using the kit 
Superscript II RT and random hexamers primers (LifeTechnologies, 2015e). After this, a further 
second strand cDNA synthesis was carried out and the adaptors were ligated to the double 
stranded cDNA templates.  A purification of these cDNA templates was repeated twice, and an 
enrichment step using PCR was performed to amplify the templates which had an adaptor bound 
at both ends. The final library then needed to be checked against the Agilent Technologies 2100 
Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies) for validation of the template size, assumed to be around 260 
bps. If the samples showed the right fragment size, they were pooled together in a concentration 
of 150ng each and sequenced on the Illumina MiSeq (Illumina). 
Six samples were sent, at a later stage, for de novo sequencing. Four of the samples were prepared 
on the QIAsymphony DSP kit. Before library preparation of these, the samples were subjected to 
double stranded cDNA synthesis, so that a DNA preparation protocol could be used instead. This

3. Discussion 
This study was divided in two parts. The first part was mainly focusing on determining a method 
for the full genome sequencing of FCoVs (obtained from suspected FIP cats), so that the sequences 
later could be examined for their genomic variation. The second part of the study focused mainly 
on discovering the best nucleic acid extraction kit of samples meant for later de novo sequencing. 
3.1. Discussion on the examination of the diversity of FCoV in Danish cats: 
We confirmed a semi quantitative rtRT-PCR method for relative quantification of FCoV in body 
fluids, feces and tissue, and noticed that the FIP cats had a high quantity of FCoV in the internal 
organs and in the effusions. On the contrary, it was shown that the amount of FCoV in feces was 
very low in Cat 1 Karlslunde suggesting that it was either not infected with FECV at the time, or 
had a very low infection level. This could possibly suggest that FIPV originates from an outside 
source, since none of the primary virus was found in the feces. However, this has been previously 
explained by the cats having cleared the primary infection with FECV before any FIP symptoms 
developed, possibly due to the fact that the immune system was on high alert in the beginning of 
the FIPV infection. It is a bit suspicious that the primary virus seems to have “disappeared” in 
some individuals by the time they develop FIP.  According to the Internal Mutation Theory only 
some of the FCoVs have developed the mutation enabling them to infect the tissue, and that 
should not eliminate the non-mutated virus from the intestine. A key scientist devoted to FIP has 
described this as a “positive selection” for the mutated virus (Pedersen, 2014a), which can be 
deemed questionable since the virus in the intestine still has the same chances of survival as 
before the mutation. The rtRT PCR results showed that the amount of FCoV in the tissue of FIP 
cats was very high. It was almost at the same level as the amount of virus in the isolates, which 
indicates that the virus shedding in the tissue is massive during the development of FIP. The fecal 
samples of cats from the cat shelter at Kattens Værn, confirmed that the infection pressure in 
multicat household is high as predicted. All three cats from the shelter were positive for FCoV, and 
had no symptoms of disease. The opposite was seen in some random fecal samples from wild cats, 
which were all negative for FCoV, probably due to a low infection pressure in nature. The eye 
==PAGE:73==
Veterinary Master’s Thesis 
 
Pia Ryt-Hansen 
 
 
DTU VET

chamber fluid of a highly suspected FIP cat showed to be negative for FCoV. This might be due to 
the special eye-blood barrier or to the fact the cat was not infected with FCoV. Cat 5 Glostrup, that 
tested negative for FCoV, probably had effusions in the abdomen due to its recent pregnancy. This 
could be a clear representation of how high number of cats is misdiagnosed with FIP and 
euthanized.  
A method for full genome sequencing of FCoV was also created. Using only four primer pairs, 
99,65 % of the FCoV genome was obtained, compared to the longest FCoV sequence (EU186072) 
in NCBI. In the process, the designed primers was proven to be superior to the published primers 
for full genome sequencing created by and published by Balint et al. (Balint, et al., 2012). 
Additionally, the primers that we created were expected to work for both serotypes at the same 
time, as the locations of the primers were found in conserved areas of both serotypes. The 
primers were chosen so that they could be conveniently used as an assay for later examination of 
the spike gene of suspected FIP cats. For this reason our fragments, which includes the spike gene, 
are so large in size that the entire gene can be sequences in either one or two fragments. We 
could also have chosen to divide the spike gene into smaller fragments, making it more likely for 
the polymerase to amplify the entire fragment, but due to big variation of this gene, it was difficult 
to find a conserved location for the primers. In the process of developing a conventional PCR 
method for the full genome of FCoV, we proved that a two-step PCR method (using the First 
Strand SuperScript III kit to produce cDNA  followed by PCR using the Accuprime kit), worked 
considerable better for FCoV, when compared  to the one-step method tried out first. Several 
factors may have contributed to that result. Firstly, the first step of the two-step method uses 
both random primers and primers hybridizing to the poly-a-tail of the FCoV for creating the cDNA. 
This makes the likelihood of having all of the FCoV genome transcribed into cDNA higher. 
Secondly, using the cDNA in the second step as a template makes it more likely for the specific 
primers to have a template to bind to. Moreover the Accuprime utilizes a reaction mix, which gives 
a high fidelity, high specificity and high yield. It is unsure which factor that caused the significant 
difference seen between the one-step and two-step method, or if it was the presence of all the 
factors together. However, a clear picture of the two-step method being superior, when working 
with FCoV, has been shown. Earlier studies have struggled with the conventional PCR of FCoVs and 
they have ended up using semi nested or nested PCR (Chang, et al., 2012; Borschensky and 
==PAGE:74==
Veterinary Master’s Thesis 
 
Pia Ryt-Hansen 
 
 
DTU VET

Reinacher, 2014). We hereby offer a new method for the conventional PCR of FCoV, avoiding the 
use of extra primers. If problems arise with obtaining the PCR products of some fragments, we 
have also shown that the use of pQE-primers can be very helpful. However, the effect of pQE-
primers is not paramount, as no difference was seen when using these primers for Fragment 5 of 
Cat 2 Holstebro. 
When aligning our full genome sequencing of the three FCoVs with other FCoV of both 
pathotypes, we observed clear evidence of the same point mutation in the spike gene specific to 
the cats with FIP as found by Chang et al. This mutation is, as proven by the study of Porter et al., 
probably coding for the systemic spread of FCoV, and therefore it would have been ideal to also 
have some FCoV full genome sequences of the tissue from the FCoV positive cats from the shelter. 
This would have allowed to whether the mutation was specific to systemic spread of the virus and 
not to the FIPV pathotype. An additional finding made when looking at the alignment of the FCoVs 
was the discovery a big deletion in Cat 1 Karlslunde’s 5’end of the spike gene. This deletion was 
very similar to the deletions around 700 bases found in two previous studies (Terada, et al., 2012; 
Chang, et al., 2012). Since the deletion is not observed in all FIPVs, it could simply be an indication 
that the 5’ end of the spike gene does not have a major role in the virulence of FCoVs. Mutations 
in the ORFs encoding the polymerase, specific to the FIP cats, were also found in our study. 
Mutations in this gene have in previous studies been regarded as irrelevant (Drechsler, et al., 
2011; Chang, et al., 2012). However, even though no previous studies have shown this, one could 
consider that a mutation in the polymerase of the FIP cats could lead to a possible 
hyperproduction of the virus. By the vast amount of virus present it does not seem that these 
mutations are hindering the replication rate significantly. In order to confirm that these mutations 
were specific to the FIP cats, we should have had full genome sequences from the tissue sample of 
healthy FCoV positive cats from the shelter, to discover if these cats would have the same 
mutations in the tissue FCoVs.  
When working with the large amount of data that is generated from the next generation 
sequencing platforms, special caution shall be adopted, to handle the data correctly. For example 
the way of working with the reads from Ion Torrent can induce a significant difference in our final 
consensus sequence. When mapping the reads to a reference, the reads somehow become

“forced” to correspond to the reference and therefore they become prone to more errors. But 
when using de novo assembly, which assembles a sequence by overlapping reads, it was not very 
likely in our case to obtain a full genome sequence. With our data from sample 1, it was only 
possible to obtain around 22kbs of coherent sequence, when using de novo assembly. However, 
when mapping the reads to the reference, a full genome was obtained, and therefore the 
sequence originating from the mapping to reference was used in the later alignment.  
The mutation rate of RNA viruses such as coronavirus gives a high likelihood of finding 
quasispecies. When looking at our results from the “Quality based variant detection” we see that 
variants of the same virus are present. The single nucleotide deletions observed in the results are 
probably due to sequencing errors, because such deletion would change the reading frame. We 
minimized the risk of sequencing errors in our primary conventional PCR assay, by utilizing an 
enzyme with a high level of proofreading; therefore it is more probable that the errors originate 
from the sequencing of Ion Torrent. The discovery of SNVs and MNVs could indicate the presence 
of quasispecies. SNVs/MNVs were found in all our samples but in very different amounts. Sample 3 
which originated from Cat 2 Holstebro, had around 50-70 times as many SNVs/MNVs as the two 
other samples. The exact reason for this pattern is not clear and due to the time constraints of this 
project, it was not possible to further investigate these aspects. We assume that it might be due to 
a worse quality of this sample caused by polymerase errors in the sequencing process. However, 
the first amplification of the fragments, that was done using the conventional PCR assay 3, showed 
great results. Additionally the lower concentration of the final PCR products of Cat 2 Holstebro 
was taken into account before sending the sample for sequencing on the Ion Torrent platform, so 
there should not have been a significant difference between the qualities of the PCR products of 
the different samples. We found evidence that quasispecies were present in the FIP cats and in the 
isolate. When quasispecies are evident, it is more difficult to look for specific mutations, as there is 
not just one single FCoV sequence specific for one cat, but many different variants of the same 
virus.  
When examining the studies in literature regarding the pathogenesis of FIP, it is noticed that all 
the in vitro studies have been done with FCoV serotype 2, since it is the only serotype that can be 
successfully grown in a cell culture. This means that we do not have a representative picture of

how FCoV acts in vitro compared to how the distribution of FCoVs is in real life. Serotype 1 is, as 
previously mentioned, the most prevalent serotype in nature and it has an entirely different spike 
gene. Studies like the one performed by Rottier et al., used the serotype 2 spike gene to test 
whether mutations in this gene could be deemed responsible for an altered host tropism (Rottier, 
et al., 2005). The results of this study do not give us a clear explanation of what could be 
happening in serotype 1 FCoVs, when they have an entirely different spike gene which also utilizes 
a different receptor at cell entry. The same applies the other way around, e.g. the mutations in the 
spike gene found by Chang et al. (Chang, et al., 2012) only sequenced serotype 1 FCoVs and the 
same goes for the study done by Porter et al. (Porter, et al., 2014), who designed primers based on 
the ones Chang et al. used.  
When considering the work done on the possible role of the 3c gene in pathogenesis and its role in 
the host cell tropism it must be deemed highly unlikely that this gene could be a determining 
factor, as both FIPVs with mutations in this gene are found in the feces, and FIPVs with an intact 3c 
gene are found systemically. The study which was set up to prove that FIPV could not be 
transmitted horizontally, did not consider that the FCoV with an intact 3c gene found in the feces 
of FIP cats, could be FECVs caused by a superinfection, as suggested by Chang et al. This would 
explain why this FCoV did not cause FIP in the SPF cats, but instead caused them to shed FCoV 
with an intact 3c gene in the feces after oronasally inoculation (Pedersen, et al., 2012).  
In general, the spike gene seems to be a likely place for a significant mutation due to its role in the 
uptake of the virus in the host cell and thereby the cell tropism. Even though FECV has been found 
systemically it still seems that the main difference between FECV and FIPV is the ability to spread 
systemically, due to the fact that FIPV is found in much higher levels than FECV in organs. Two 
mutations in the spike gene have been found to correlate with the pathotype FIP by Chang et al. 
(Chang, et al., 2012), but as the authors themselves discussed, these two nucleotide changes 
should occur reasonably often and therefore it contradicts the observed prevalence of FIP. A later 
study ended up showing that these mutations were not correlated with FIP, but rather the ability 
of FCoVs to spread systemically. The ability of systemic spread is somehow related to FIP, since 
FIPV does seem to spread more effectively than FECV, but the exact mechanism still remains 
unknown. The fact that no scientist thought of examining the tissue of FECV infected cats before

2012 and again in 2014 is remarkable, since the Internal Mutation Theory is based on the clear 
distinction of FECV being in the intestine and FIPV spreading systemically. The study done by Wang 
et al., showing clear evidence that FIPV is able to spread horizontally between cats, is very 
controversial and it questions the generally accepted Internal Mutation Theory (Wang, et al., 
2013). This could be explained by there being a possible unknown difference between serotype 1 
and serotype 2. It could also be a special case of the mutations causing the FCoV to be so different, 
that it becomes able to spread horizontally. Finally the scientist of the Internal Mutation Theory 
could have been wrong all along. However, all previous studies have not shown a phylogenetic 
pattern that fits with FIPV being a different strain from FECV and able to spread between cats. 
Most studies have shown that there is only a very small difference between FECV and FIPV, and 
also the prevalence of FIP does not concur with the ability of the virus to spread horizontally. 
Additionally, no one has been able to distinguish FECV and FIPV a 100 % by anything else than the 
symptoms of the cat. One could be tempted to think that there is no difference between these 
two pathotypes genetically and it is only a question of how the immune system of the cat reacts to 
the virus when it spreads systemically. Some factors are evident that could suggest that the 
immune system might be the main factor in determining which cats develop FIPV and which do 
not. The fact that pure breed cats have a higher risk of developing FIP could suggest that these 
cats might have a weakened immune system due to the extensive breeding programs. Another 
fact is that there has also been shown a correlation between stress in the environment, which can 
also lead to a weakened immune system, and the development of FIP. At last it has been seen that 
some cats do not develop FIP even if they are inoculated with the virus (Pedersen, et al., 1981; 
Brown, et al., 2009). One thing that is sure is, that FIP is an immune mediated disease that thrives 
on a humoral response to the virus. It is not known whether it is the antibody-antigen complexes, 
the activation of macrophages by the FC-receptor or a combination of both that lead to the 
development of disease. The cellular immune system also plays an important role, and the 
activation of monocytes has been proven to cause the effusions consistent with wet FIP. The cats 
that develop wet FIP have strong clinical symptoms and die fast, whereas some cats develop dry 
FIP and have a more chronic disease process. Both forms have the same granulomatous and 
pyogranulomatous lesions, and both have a similar immune response with both the cellular and 
humoral immune system being activated. No clear difference in the immune response and in the

infection level explains why some cats develop perivasculitis/vasculitis and thereby effusion 
(Paltrinieri, et al., 1998; Kiss, et al., 2004). As mentioned, the activation of monocytes is the main 
cause of the effusions (Kipar, et al., 2005), but if the same immune response is seen in both wet 
and dry FIP cases, why do only some cats that develop effusions? It seems that the cats with dry 
FIP are more capable of restraining the virus, and therefore develop a more chronic disease 
pattern. This is an example of yet another peculiar feature of FIP. The determining factor(s) of the 
development of FIP still remains unknown.  
3.2. Discussion of the nucleic acid extraction kits for samples meant for de novo sequencing novo 
sequencing:  
Our results from the rtRT-PCR of the different elutions indicate that the most effective kit for 
extracting viral RNA from tissue was QIAsymphony DSP kit whereas the QIAsymphony RNA kit 
performed best for feces. If we look at the amount of tissue used for the different kit we also 
notice that the QIAsymphony DSP kit contained the highest amount, around 2,5-18 times higher 
than the other kits. This kit had a 1-3 log higher yield than the other kits, showing a significant 
difference between the kits. In the QIAsymphony DSP both DNA and RNA from viruses and 
bacteria is extracted and perhaps this lack of selectivity, makes as the RNA less likely to be 
damaged, compared to a kit that uses DNase treatment. The QIAsymphony DSP kit is not 
developed for extracting RNA and DNA from cells, but instead it is specific for viral and bacterial 
RNA and DNA in body fluids. Due to this fact, one should not think that the QIAsymphony DSP kit is 
the best for extracting viral RNA in the tissue. This can perhaps be explained by our FIP cat having 
body effusion in both the thorax and the abdomen. It could be possible that our tissue samples 
were containing a fair amount of effusions and therefore, resulting in the QIAsymphony DSP kit, 
being the most effective kit for extracting viral RNA from tissue, even though it was actually 
extracting viral RNA from the effusion. The second best kit for extracting viral RNA from tissue was 
the QIAsymphony RNA kit, which is normally the kit used for analyzing tissue samples at DTU VET. 
The least effective kit for extracting viral RNA from tissue according to the Ct values was the 
QIAcube HT 96 Cador Pathogen. However, it must be stated that the machine showed problems 
with collecting the right amount of sample from the Eppendorf tubes, due to foam production as a 
consequence of the high protein content of the samples. For the QIAsymphony RNA kit, which

Ct values might have been caused by different inhibitors of the samples and not by the kits

material is of interest. If it is only RNA, there is no reason for using sequencing on material DNA.

the genetic material from the cat cells can be avoided to use sequencing capacity on and thereby 
coverage on.

4. Conclusion and perspectives 
In this study a new method for full genome sequencing of FCoV was developed. The method is a 
two-step conventional PCR, applying Superscript III First Strand kit for the cDNA synthesis and 
==PAGE:81==
Veterinary Master’s Thesis 
 
Pia Ryt-Hansen 
 
 
DTU VET 
81 
 
Accuprime Taq DNA Polymerase High Fidelity kit for the PCR. It has further been proven that this 
method can be combined with the use of pQE-primer if problems arise.  
We generated three full genome sequences of FCoV, and found the same mutation as in the study 
done by Chang et al. (Chang, et al., 2012). This mutation is probably a factor of FCoVs ability to 
spread systemically, as indicated by the study of Porter et al. (Porter, et al., 2014). Mutations in 
the polymerase, specific to the FIP cats, were also observed and a possible significance of these 
was discussed. No specific mutations clearly determining the FECV to FIPV development have been 
identified, and therefore the determining factor of the pathogenesis of FIP still remains a mystery. 
Evidence of viral quasispecies was observed, and this should be taken into regard, when looking at 
full genome sequences obtained from a next generation sequencing platform. Our three FCoV 
serotype 1 sequences can hopefully be used in the future research of FIP, along with our methods 
used for obtaining them. The rtRT-PCR Assay 3 is ready to be used at the DTU VET for making 
diagnosis of FIP in future possible insurance cases were the diagnosis of FIP is questioned.  
The aim, to successfully look at the diversity of FCoV in Danish cats, has not entirely been 
achieved, as we did not obtain a representative amount of FIP cats. However, we can compare our 
samples to the FCoVs sequences obtained in the rest of the world. Moreover this is the first study 
that has produced insight on FIPVs (serotype 1) in Danish cats. The FIPV samples resembled other 
FIPV samples in the NCBI, as similar mutations in the spike gene were observed.  
For the extraction of nucleic acid on both tissue and fecal sample, we found that the QIAsymphony 
DSP kit was the most effective for extracting RNA from tissue and that the QIAsymphony RNA kit 
was the most effective for the fecal samples, when using FCoV as a reference. The best kit for 
nuclec acid extraction of a sample, meant for later de novo sequencing of viruses, has not yet been 
found. However, it was revealed, that the presence of DNA in a sample being used for the TruSeq 
RNA library protocol for de novo sequencing, inhibits the function of the kit. We can therefore 
conclude that when information of both viral DNA and RNA is needed, the RNA should be 
translated into cDNA and follow a DNA library preparation protocol (if preparing the samples on 
the QIAsymphony DSP kit). We have also shown that when using the QIAsymphony RNA kit for 
samples meant for de novo sequencing, a large amount of data on the bacteria, is acquired. This 
hinders the search of viruses, and therefore a comparison of the virome of the domestic cat and

avoiding having all the sequencing capacity used on bacteria, a sterile filtration is recommended

Information provided by the clinics on the different cats and our pathological findings dissection of Cat 1 
Karlslunde: 
Emil(Cat 1 Karlslunde)  
Signalement: neutered male, 2 years and 9 months old, mixed breed.  
Anamnesis and clinical examination: This case was referred to Karlslund dyrehospital from another 
clinic on the 10th of February with the suspicion of liver failure. The day after the cat was 
euthanized and picked up for further examination at DTU-veterinary institute. In Karlslunde 
dyrehospital the cat presented as weak and dehydrated and with conjunctiva lightly icteric and 
icteric palatum molle.  
Imaging: An ultrasound was done and a lot of free fluid was found in the thorax along with a 
smaller amount of fluid in the abdomen. A thoracentesis was done and the fluid was found to be 
yellow and stringy. Paraclinical test: On the blood sample a Albumin/Globulin ratio of 0,5 was 
seen, along with high ALAT. The kidney values were low(Urea and creatinine).  
Pathological findings: In our dissection of the cat a number of lesions suggesting FIP were found. 
The thorax was filled up with the yellow/orange gelatinous fluid which was stringy and a 
serohemorrhagic fluid was seen in the pericardium. The lungs were collapsed and had multiple 
circular focal lesions which were 2-3mm in diameter and resembled pyogranulomatous lesions. 
The same types of lesions were also seen subpleurally. In the thorax diffuse fibrinous pleuritis 
covering the inside of the costae and the diaphragm was seen. The abdomen had around 30-40ml 
of yellow/orange fluid, and there was a general fibrinous peritonitis. The liver was swollen and had 
rounded edges. It also had focal circular lesions around 1-3mm in diameter also resembling 
pyogranulomatous leasions. The texture of the liver was rubber-like and the whole liver was 
covered in fibrin. The mesenteric lymph node was enlarged having a size of 1,5cm x 4 cm, and it 
had what resembled pyogranulomatous lesions. The texture was firm and dry when you cut into it. 
The spleen was also swollen and the kidney was found to be lightly svullen, dry and the cortex of 
one was yellow. A general icteric color was seen e.g. in the subcutaneous and abdominal fat, skin, 
ears and in the conjunctiva.
