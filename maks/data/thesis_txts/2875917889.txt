Abstract 
Plastic pollution is one of the most important environmental and human health challenges that 
humanity faces today. More specific, Polystyrene (PS) is thoroughly studied plastic polymer, which 
has been detected in soils, water and even within the human body. To assess its potential health 
impacts there is need for a reliable and systematic framework. In this thesis, I critically examined the 
applicability of the PlasticRiskCat framework, which is a weight of evidence-based tool developed 
under the EU PlasticHeal project. Additionally, I also evaluated if methods assisted by AI such as 
PlasticRiskCat and the SciRAP AI tool can assist researchers and evaluators in a health hazard 
assessment. In the thesis conventional SciRAP reliability and relevance scoring is combined and 
compared with scores evaluated by Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT and Google 
Gemini to automate literature screening. During the screening more than one thousand PS 
toxicological studies were downloaded and divided across twelve health endpoints, the same that 
PlasticRiskCat evaluates.  To evaluate the compatibility of GPT based tools in literature screening I 
performed a T-TEST and a Bland Altman plot.  The results showed that the Ai assisted evaluation 
aligned with the manual assessment(P=0.41), with average disagreement of 17 to 13 points calculated 
as upper and lower limits of agreement, showing strong correlation and agreement. Additionally, the 
GPT based tools showed reproducibility with (P=0.76) and an average disagreement of 6.9 and 7.7, 
substantially lower than the previous comparison showing higher correlation and reproducibility. I 
also compared the manual evaluation which also shower high correlation (P=0.96) but also the 
biggest average disagreement of 16 points showing high correlation but also higher disagreement 
than that of the first scenario. The studies that scored the highest in reliability and relevance were 
then chosen for the GPT based tools Health Hazard assessment. The evaluation was performed in 
three scenarios one that had no additional data input, one with specific studies and with the highest 
in reliability and relevance studies identifies by the Google Gemini script, the evaluation was 
performed twice and a comparison between the two evaluations was also performed. They showed 
substantial differences in their results; to quantify the differences and assess the reproducibility I 
performed a Euclidean distance, cosine similarity and Mean Average Error (MAE) methods. The 
methods showed that the only reproductible scenario was the third with the previous two being unable 
to produce consistent results. The results furthermore showed that SciRAP GPT based tool produced 
consistent evaluations even when compared to manual evaluations, demonstrating a potential for 
assisting researchers in literature assessment of large scale. Additionally, a correlation between the 
quality of data and the accuracy and reliability of the hazard profiles was evident in the results. I 
conclude that the AI integration can assist human experts at doing a plastic health hazard assessment, 
but always under a human evaluator.

ramme.I dette speciale har jeg kritisk undersøgt anvendeligheden af PlasticRiskCat-rammen, et

vurderingseksperter i en sundhedsrisikovurdering.I specialet kombineres og sammenlignes

sprogmodeller (LLMs) som ChatGPT og Google Gemini med henblik på at automatisere

studier, som blev fordelt på tolv sundhedseffekter – de samme som PlasticRiskCat vurderer.For at 
vurdere kompatibiliteten af GPT-baserede værktøjer i litteraturscreening udførte jeg en T-test og 
et Bland-Altman-plot. Resultaterne viste, at den AI-assisterede vurdering stemte godt overens med 
den manuelle vurdering (P = 0,41) med en gennemsnitlig uenighed på 17 til 13 point, beregnet som 
øvre og nedre grænser for enighed, hvilket indikerer en stærk korrelation. Derudover viste de GPT-
baserede værktøjer høj reproducerbarhed (P = 0,76) med en gennemsnitlig uenighed på 6,9 og 7,7 – 
markant lavere end i den første sammenligning – hvilket indikerer en højere korrelation og 
stabilitet.Den manuelle vurdering viste ligeledes en høj korrelation (P = 0,96), men også den største 
gennemsnitlige uenighed på 16 point, hvilket peger på høj korrelation men lavere konsistens end i 
det første scenarie.De studier, der opnåede de højeste scorer for pålidelighed og relevans, blev 
derefter udvalgt til de GPT-baserede værktøjers sundhedsrisikovurdering. Evalueringen blev udført 
i tre scenarier: ét uden yderligere datainput, ét med specifikke studier og ét med de mest pålidelige 
og relevante studier identificeret af Google Gemini-scriptet. Evalueringen blev gentaget to gange, og 
resultaterne blev sammenlignet.De viste betydelige forskelle i resultaterne; for at kvantificere disse 
og vurdere reproducerbarheden anvendte jeg Euklidisk afstand, kosinus-lighed og Mean Average 
Error (MAE). Analyserne viste, at det eneste reproducerbare scenarie var det tredje, mens de to første 
ikke kunne levere konsistente resultater. Resultaterne viste desuden, at SciRAP GPT-
værktøjet producerede stabile vurderinger, selv når de blev sammenlignet med manuelle 
evalueringer, hvilket demonstrerer et stort potentiale for at støtte forskere i litteraturvurderinger i stor 
skala. Endelig var der en tydelig sammenhæng mellem datakvaliteten og nøjagtigheden og 
pålideligheden af risikoprofilerne. Jeg konkluderer, at AI-integration kan støtte menneskelige 
eksperter i udførelsen af plastrelaterede sundhedsrisikovurderinger – men altid under menneskelig 
kontrol.

1. Plastic Pollution 
 
Plastic pollution is one of the biggest environmental challenges of the 21st century, in 2019 global 
production exceeded 459.75 metric tons with globally only 9% of it being recycled (Geyer et al. 
2017, OECD 2022). Plastic is an excellent source for a plethora of different applications due to its 
low cost by being a byproduct from oil extraction and its durability (Narancic et al, 2020). Their 
durability is also a key reason why plastics have become such a major problem, since there are no 
known natural mechanisms for complete biodegradation, plastics simply fragment into increasingly 
smaller pieces, which are classified as microplastics and nano plastics depending on their size 
(Pfohl et al. 2022, Cai et al., 2023). As a result, plastics accumulate in terrestrial and aquatic 
ecosystems, as well as in organisms themselves posing a serious problem for ecological and potential 
human health risks (Li et al. 2023).Plastics and more specific MNPs find access to the environmental 
and the human body by a variety of different pathways including landfills, wastewater discharge, 
mismanaged waste with the last one making 39% of all plastic waste in non-OECD countries (OECD, 
2022).  When the fragmentation of the plastic due to their innate durability reaches (<5 mm) the 
plastic reaches the point where is characterized as micro plastic and nano plastics when it is bellow 
(<1 µm) (EFSA, 2019). The fragmentation happens from the following mechanisms:  
photodegradation and abiotic processes (Song et al, 2022). Microplastics have been shown to 
adversely affect multiple human systems, including the digestive, reproductive, and respiratory 
systems (Chartres et al., 2024). 
Marine plastic pollution is a well-recognized problem; the received attention was caused due to its 
impacts and well documented harm to wildlife. Furthermore, sea birds, fish and marine mammals 
either consume the plastic, or they become entangled in the plastic debris. Some of the organisms are 
injured and dying as an effect of this problem (Thushari & Senevirathna, 2020).   
Terrestrial pollution by plastics is not an as discussed as its marine counterpart but it has been 
observed that soils in agricultural regions, where sewage sludge is used, have a significant amount 
of MNP accumulation (Ramage et al., 2025).   
Airborne MNPs have furthermore been detected in both outdoor and indoor air, urban dust, and even 
precipitation, indicating a widespread dispersal risk of inhaling these particles (Wu et al., 2025; 
Islam et al., 2024). 
Plastics do not biodegrade in the conventional sense; instead, they persist for decades to centuries, 
continually fragmenting without mineralizing. Even the biodegradable plastics often require 
industrial composting conditions and may not degrade effectively in nature. 
Polystyrene (PS) is one of the most used plastics in the world today, it is made up of monomer 
styrene. The three forms of Polystyrene are: film, rigid foam, and solid plastic in which PS is 
available. In 1839, a German pharmacist Edward Simon accidentally discovered polystyrene, and a 
century later, it was used commercially. Among various polymers, polystyrene is frequently used in 
microplastic toxicology studies, and evidence suggests that exposure can impair gut barrier function, 
reproductive outcomes, and respiratory health (Chartres et al., 2024).  
 
 
==PAGE:11==
Specifically, PS has been observed in high quantities inside the human body 4.8 µg/ml (Leslie et al., 
2022), for that reason research focused on evaluating potential health risks PS and other MNPs may 
have on the human health. More than a thousand papers were published the last decade evaluating 
all potential risks of PS in human body, due to the number of publications the data produced by the 
research was often scattered and makes it difficult to distinguish between quality studies and noise. 
Furthermore, due to these problems regulating this industry and guiding future research is proved to 
be difficult as a researcher would need to find quality studies over hundreds and thousands of papers.  
These challenges hamper traditional chemical risk assessment of plastics such as PS (SAPEA, 2019). 
Consequently, different risk frameworks had been developed specifically for plastics. One of these 
frameworks is the POLYRISK risk assessment framework for MNPs, developed with the goal to to 
develop a modular human health risk-assessment framework focusing on inhalation exposure (see 
figure 1) (Vogel et al., 2024). POLYRISK is one of the five EU projects part of CUSP cluster 
(alongside PLASTICHEAL, AURORA, IMPTOX, and PlasticFate), these projects develop 
frameworks and together they generate research on the health impacts of MNPs and to assist in the 
future European regulatory frameworks (CUSP-Research, 2025). The POLYRISK framework is 
divided in three steps. In the first step, a check if the substance is truly a polymer as also the size of 
it is taken into consideration. After answering this question and if there is potential human exposure, 
we move to step two where we check if the polymer is inhaled, if it reaches the lungs, the 
dissolvement of the substance and potential leaching of hazardous chemicals. Finally, we move to 
step three were the morphology if it’s a fiber or a particle with the next step being optional. In Figure 
1 the logic tree applied by the framework provides further details. POLYRISK focuses mainly on the 
inhalation route with a potential extension to other exposure routes (Vogel et al., 2024.) 
Another risk assessment framework that was developed specifically for plastics is PlasticRiskCat 
that was developed by the EU project PlasticHeal (Hansen and Sherpa 2025). PlasticRiskCat follows 
a weight of evidence approach and integrates state of the art technologies such as AI assistants to 
evaluate specific MNPs health risks across 12 health endpoints such as carcinogenicity and 
reproductive toxicity. In figure 2, the logic tree that PlasticRiskCat follows can be studied.  Compared 
to POLYRISK, it does not evaluate only the risks of inhaled MNPs, but all risks across all exposure 
routes. PlasticRiskCat does this by having trained AI assistant to assess the reliability and relevance 
of scientific papers published within each specific endpoint (Hansen and Sherpa 2025).  
The advantages that the PlasticRiskCat framework is that it provides the possibility for basing an risk 
evaluation on all available published research. This makes understanding the practical applicability 
of PlasticRiskCat important to understand e.g., how might PlasticRiskCat be applied on plastics such 
as PS and what are the pros and cons of applying PlasticRiskCat on PS. 
In figure 1 and 2 the two Risk Assessment logic trees are presented as it is important to visualize the 
difference in the approach of the two methods to get a better understanding of the thought process in 
later chapters.

2. Methodology 
In this Chapter, the methodology used is presented, and key decision are discussed about e.g., why 
PlasticRiskCat and SciRAP was chosen and the specific choice of AI software used. 
2.1 Selection of Risk Evaluation Framework 
First, the framework chosen for the risk assessment of PS is PlasticRiskCat. This framework is 
currently the only available systematic tool that evaluates the health risks in humans of MNPs across 
12 health categories. One of the key differences in similar frame works is that PlasticRiskCat unlike 
POLYRISK takes into consideration all exposure routes ensuring that no relevant route with potential 
hazard is overlooked (Vogel et al., 2024).  
In addition, the weight of evidence approach (WoE) used by PlasticRiskCat covers better the MNP 
evaluation compared to the classic chemical risk assessment as the chemical risk assessment needs 
standardized toxicity tests (OECD guidelines). WoE allows the addition of all available research 
even using the heterogeneous data assigning greater weight to higher-quality, more relevant studies 
while taking into less consideration the low quality and less relevant ones.  (SAPEA, 2019) 
Another critical factor in choosing PlasticRiskCat is the emphasis this framework gives on study 
reliability and relevance scoring, performed in accordance with SciRAP criteria. The integration of 
this type of scoring ensures the transparency and reproducibility of how evidence is judged. This is 
crucial for such a rapidly developing field like MNP toxicology (de Ruijter et al., 2020).  
Additionally, it should be mentioned that the search is conducted primarily with open-source studies, 
to mitigate any potential copy rights claims that may our research fall under. Key words are then 
used to choose relevant studies for our corresponding categories with filters that keep only the desired 
area of study for example only toxicological studies.  
Studies conducted on human, In vivo and in vivo studies are gathered, then saved in their 
corresponding health category file and are prepared for the next step.   
Furthermore, PlasticRiskCat requires a high quality and high in relevance studies, that’s why a 
literature screening examining the toxicologic effects of the choosing polymer should be conducted. 
To gather the information, we need to divide the studies into relevant categories.  
Finally, the use of AI-assisted tools for the screening and scoring gives us a chance to evaluate further 
the use of AI in assisting researchers and policy makers understanding complex problems like MNPs 
where systematic review of all available research is extremely difficult.

2.3 Relevance and Reliability Check  
When all available data was downloaded and renamed a relevance and reliability method was used 
for the evaluation of the studies.  This was done so I could first and foremost select the best available 
studies to train PlasticRiskCat with and to evaluate if an Ai tool could have the similar, reproductible 
and trustable results.  
For this evaluation there were three promising methods: SciRap, ToxRTool, and Klimisch that were 
originally considered in the view of the type of studies I would research, in my case being toxicology, 
the strength and weaknesses of each method and the automation potential each tool had. In the end 
the SciRap method was chosen for several factors. SciRap was designed for studies in the field of 
toxicology specifically, it provides reliability and relevance criteria this aligns with our goal evaluate

the papers in a reliable and reproductible manner. In contrast, the Klimisch scoring scheme on the 
other hand, is more subjective relying on an expert’s judgment to place the studies into broad

or no checklist ToxRTool is also but fixed and categorical, this limits its ability to rank studies.,

The last criteria of choosing SciRAP was the automation potential. The criteria that SciRAP follow

et al 2021). Finally, Klimisch, is very difficult to be automated since it has not shown the same 
alignment with WoE approach as SciRAP has.

Bland Altman Plot 
The second analysis I applied to my the 4 different SciRAP samples I evaluated was a Bland Altman 
plot. In the sample there are 4 different SciRAP evaluations, one was evaluated by me, for the second 
i asked a fellow MSc student to evaluate the same papers and for the third and fourth samples I used 
a specialized AI agent trained in SciRAP criteria. I chose to do two human and two AI evaluations 
so I could compare them directly and test both reproducibility and agreement between methods. 
 I chose the Bland Altman plot to understand if the samples agree with each other and not only if 
they are different. In contrast with the t test this method shows us exactly where each study is in the 
plot giving us the chance to visualize how far the assigned to each study scores are from the mean 
bias and if some of them are scores outside the higher and lower Limits of Agreement (LoA). An 
additional advantage of this method would be the bias each method (human evaluated or AI) would 
possibly have into repetitively assigning a lower or higher score.   
In the plot of Bland Alman, we have the mean of the two scores in the x axis and the difference Ai – 
Manual or Ai-Ai on y axis. Two additional parts of this plot are the Mean bias visualized in my 
results as a red line and the two limits of agreement the lower and higher limit (Bland & Altman, 
1986). 
Mean Bias is the average of all the differences between the two samples in my case these are AI and 
human evaluated scores (manual) and AI and AI scores. When the line is above zero, AI tends to 
score higher overall. When it is below zero then AI tends to score lower. As close this line is to zero, 
the closer AI and manual scores are on average. 
Limits of Agreement are the two blue and green lines that show the range where about 95 % of all 
differences are expected to fall, this takes into consideration that these differences follow normal 
distribution. This assumption is justified by the central limit theorem which state that the sampling 
distribution of the mean difference tends to normalize with the increase of the samples size (Kwak 
& Kim, 2017). The upper LOA is the highest expected normal difference whereas the lower LOA is 
the lowest expected normal difference.

3 PlasticRiskCat Framework 
PlasticRiskCat is a framework for hazard evaluation of NMPs developed by the EU funded project 
called PlasticHeal (Hansen and Sherpa 2025). PlasticRiskCat it is a first-tier hazard assessment 
framework that can be used to systematically and transparently evaluate what is known about the 
human hazards of micro and nano plastics (MNP). The outcome of using the PlasticRiskCat 
framework on a given type of micro and nano plastics is a radar plot that shows the results of a 
evaluation of the strength of evidence regarding:  
1. Carcinogenicity   
2. Reproductive toxicity  
3. Immunotoxicity  
4. Metabolic effects  
5. Neurotoxicity  
6. Respiratory toxicity  
7. Cardiovascular disease  
8. Gastrointestinal effects  
9. Organ-specific toxicity  
10. Genotoxicity including mutagenicity 
11. Oxidative stress  
12. Endocrine-related 
The PlasticRiskCat framework consists of five overall steps:  
1. Define the scope of the PlasticRiskCat evaluation 
2. Gather all relevant data and information 
3. Evaluate the reliability and relevance of this information 
4. Use weight-of-evidence approach to assign strength of evidence for each endpoint 
5. Fill in radar plot 
Step 1: Scope Definition. 
Micro and Nano plastics are not a group of substances that have the same physio-chemical 
characteristics. MNPs showcase different shapes, sizes, source and paths through which find their 
way to the human organism. That’s why it is important to define the scope of MNPs that will be 
subjected to the PlasticRiskCat. More specifically a definition of the polymer followed by the form 
of it, size and exposure route should be conducted. Furthermore, the scope is then showcased in the 
title of the chart produced by the tool.   
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Step 2: Gather all relevant data and information 
For the gathering of relevant data and information I followed the same method as it is described in 
chapter 2.1 and 2.2, for reference she the above chapters.  
Step 3: Evaluate the reliability and relevance of this information 
Step 3 of the PlasticRiskCat framework is an evaluation of the reliability and relevance of the studies 
to guarantee a high quality PlasticRiskCat evaluation. As not all studies showcase the same amount 
of relevance and quality, only studies of sufficient quality and relevance should be used.  
The PlasticRiskCat framework relies on the SciRAP tool developed by Stockholm University and 
the Karolinska Instituted in Sweden has been chosen as the most appropriate for that process. SciRAP 
have developed a tool for each type of studies such as in vitro, in vivo etc. At DTU, Hansen and 
Sherpa (2025) have developed AI agents to evaluate any given study. The agents check the relevance 
of the studies and their reliability, the reliability happens through a check of the study design, 
Figure 6: PlasticRiskCat chart Scope in brackets. 
==PAGE:24==
appropriate documentation and the use of controls. Relevance by how applicable the data are 
to the substance, exposure, and endpoints. 
Step 4: Use weight-of-evidence approach to assign strength of evidence for each endpoint 
In step 4, a weight-of-evidence approach is to be used to assign strength of evidence for each 
endpoint. PlasticRiskCat is developed with the weight of evidence principles recommended by 
regulatory agencies for chemical hazard assessment. The weight of evidence approach entails 
using multiple independent lines of evidence in combination to draw conclusions, rather than relying 
on any single study alone. Furthermore, by combining the collective knowledge from in vitro, in vivo 
etc. studies we can understand the problem from multiple angles. This way the bias and limitations 
of a single studies mitigated as there is a plethora of information, especially for important 
contaminants such as micro and nanoplastics and specifically in the case of this thesis, namely PS.  
In a weight-of-evidence approach, different types of studies do not carry the same amount of 
weight. The PlasticRiskCat the following hierarchy is suggested: 
• 
Highest weight is given to human data: Epidemiological studies and human biomonitoring 
data are the most important, because they a direct show of the effects of the chemical on a 
human subject. It should be noted that this type of data is sparse till now, but any data from 
such papers would carry the highest weight.  
• 
Moderate weight is given to animal in vivo studies: For a study conducted in an animal to 
be a part of this category a series of prerequisites must be met, including Clear 
documentation of study design with endpoints and methodologies. Appropriate controls to 
ensure validity. Ethical and regulatory compliance such as adherence to OECD guidelines. 
Sufficient sample size to allow for meaningful interpretation of results. Transparent reporting 
of dosing with exposure routes. Finally, the experiments should be conducted on different 
species of mammals then there is strong evidence of correlation of effect to also humans  
• 
Lower weight is given to In vitro: In vitro cell based studies that are conducted in different 
non mammalian species such as zebrafish, daphnia and algae although important to see if a 
substance has toxicological effects do not carry the reliability for effects in humans due to 
the vast differences between the species.  
• 
Lowest weight is given to in silico computational: Computational modeling serves as 
supportive information rather than definitive evidence. 
PlasticRiskCat to give a better understanding of a potential danger a MNP may have used a semi 
quantitative strength of evidence scale for each endpoint. In contrast with the binary hazard 
detected or no hazard we assign a score from 1 to 5 based the collected evidence indicating a potential 
effect on that endpoint. This five-point scale is adapted from the European Environment Agency 
guidelines for expressing evidence certainty. 
• 
5 Very Strong evidence: The likelihood that the MNP causes this effect is >90% (It is backed 
by consistent, high-quality evidence). 
• 
4 Strong evidence:  70–90% certainty. Clear indication of hazard, though some uncertainty 
may remain. 
• 
3 Moderate evidence: 50–70% certainty. Limited data suggesting an effect, needing caution 
but not definitive proof. 
• 
2 Weak evidence: 30–50% certainty. Some studies hint at a hazard but overall evidence is 
inconsistent. 
==PAGE:25==
• 
1 Very Weak evidence: <30% certainty. Little to no compelling evidence of hazard for this 
endpoint.

4. SciRAP 
Evaluation of the reliability and relevance of the scientific studies that feed into the PlasticRiskCat 
evaluation is a key step of the PlasticRiskCat methodology. In PlasticRiskCat, SciRAP evaluation is 
used as a methodological framework and that why is chapter is dedicated to introducing the key

4.1 Introduction to SciRAP 
SciRAP (Science in Risk Assessment and Policy SciRAP) is a tool for the evaluation of the reliability 
and relevance of scientific ecotoxicological, toxicological and epidemiological studies. SciRAP was 
developed by the University of Stokholm in collaboration with the Institute of Environmental 
Medicine at Karolinska Institute to help bridge a perceived gap between regulatory bodies, science 
and academia when it comes to the use, structure and transparency of health and environmental 
studies. To achieve this goal, SciRAP is based OECD test guidelines and already developed methods 
for evaluating the quality of scientific studies such as the Klimisch method. (Beronius 2025)). 
SciRAP was developed in 2014 and subsequently upgraded in 2018 and the applicability of SciRAP 
has gradually been expanding so that it now includes:  
• 
ecotoxicity studies (the CRED tool)  
• 
ecotoxicity studies of nanomaterials (NanoCRED)  
• 
ecotoxicity studies in sediment and soil (CRED for sediment and soil)  
• 
ecotoxicity studies investigating behavioural effects (EthoCRED)  
• 
in vivo toxicity studies  
• 
in vitro toxicity studies  
• 
in vitro toxicity studies of nanomaterials  
• 
observational epidemiological studies 
 (Molander et al. 2021). 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
SciRAP developed several tools for different types of research such us :  
Common of all the different evaluations methods under SciRAP, is that the focus on evaluating the 
reliability and relevance of studies based on OECD reporting standards as this approach was better 
than the traditional Klimisch method. The main advantage compared to the Klimisch method is that 
it provides more transparency and better guidance (Roth et al. 2021).  
Each SciRAP tool has two sections. The first section focusses the reporting quality that requires the 
users of SciRAP to assesses if the scientific reports on a range of SciRAP criteria (see figure 8 on 
the reporting criteria for SciRAP in vitro). The second section focusses on the methodological quality 
of a given study and here the “trustworthiness” of the methodology followed in the scientific study 
is evaluated. For instance, questions such as are whether an appropriate solvent (vehicle) has been 
used that is not expected to interfere with the results of the study at the concentration used. For each 
criterion, the evaluator of a given study must assess whether the criteria is either: “fulfilled”, 
“partially fulfilled”, “not fulfilled”, “not reported” or they can decide to remove the criteria all 
together, if the criteria is considered irrelevant.

4.2 Automation SciRAP evaluations within the PlasticRiskCat 
Framework 
One significant limitation of existing methods for evaluating the reliability and relevance of scientific 
studies is that they must performed manually by experts in the field (e.g., in vivo or ecotoxicological 
studies) and given the vast number of scientific studies that are published each year on, for instance 
plastics, is task is very time- and resource consuming. Last year along more than 7,000 health and 
environmental studies were published on plastics alone. As machine learning and natural language 
processing abilities have emerged and continue to grow, we wanted to explore the possibility of 
automatically completing the required literature screening and evaluation of reliability and relevance 
when it comes to application of the PlasticRiskCat methodology. the formal nature of SciRAP 
scheme seems amenable to algorithmic treatments and could markedly accelerate processing of broad 
sets of toxicology data.  
The structured question form of SciRAP makes partial automation a plausible possibility. The 
possibility of digital screening tools to include SciRAP by expressing study reliability scores 
numerically, an AI model can concentrate on learning from the most reliable data. Furthermore, an 
LLM such as ChatGPT or Gemini could be used to read a paper and produce responses to SciRAP 
scores. Since SciRAP requires explicit documentation of rationale, an AI could even present paper

6.Discussion 
The results of this thesis as also the methods used to produce them reveal a promising potential for 
the use of AI assisted hazard assessment as also their integration into the methodology itself. If we 
look the AI assistant tool for the relevance and reliability as also the PlasticRiskCat tool, we see that 
their integration into the methodology can enhance the resulting Hazzard assessment.  
6.1 Similarities between SciRAP evaluations 
First, the SciRap assistant tool showed a very similar results with the one conducted by me, even 
though the differences in the results may change he final assessment of the paper this is something 
that could be mitigated by a stronger phrasing in prompt. 
A key element for the evaluation of PlasticRiskCat ability to conduct a thorough and reproductible 
plastic Risk assessment is the relevance and radiality evaluation. For this process the SciRAP tool 
was used as it showed the best alignment with the framework’s goals.   
For PlasticRiskCat tool to evaluate the risk in the 12 health categories more than 1000 papers were 
downloaded, this would make it impossible for me to use SciRAP to test all of them, so I used a GPT 
trained to use SciRAP criteria. I then choose more than 20 studies were I also applied the criteria 
manually and compared the results. With the help of a MSc student a second evaluation of the same 
papers was independently conducted, in the same manner a second AI evaluation was conducted in 
separate computer using the same version of the tool.    
In more detail the AI SciRAP tool showed reproducibility of its results as showed in figure 16, AI vs 
AI results have the lowest mean bias and disagreement with the LoA being the lowest among all 
scenarios. This agreement in results comes in contrast with the SciRAP conducted by us with the use 
of SciRAP Excell tool.  
Furthermore, we can also understand by the mean bias of the Bland Altman plot that the AI scores 
only -2.17 lower than human evaluator. In the resulting score so we can see that the AI a slightly 
more rigorous evaluation than the human.  
Human evaluators at the same time even though that they disagree more in the evaluation they also 
have high rates of agreement. By observing the lowest and upper limits of agreement we see that in 
the comparison between the two manual evaluations the difference is the highest among all scenarios 
with of 16 points. A difference of that magnitude can be impactful in deciding which papers will be 
used in the final hazard assessment. 
On the other hand, when comparing the two AI evaluations the LoA are the lowest among all three 
scenarios, showcasing strong agreement in the resulting evaluation.  
Additionally, the T.test show an additional layer of agreement between human evaluator and AI tool, 
this agreement shows that in general the method works in principle. Using such agents so in research 
which demand a high number of papers to be evaluated is the most appropriate way to use these 
tools. This is since the more studies we have the higher the chance that the AI and human would 
agree.  
==PAGE:47==
The question comes in if we trust the resulting AI evaluation even if there is reproducibility of its 
evaluation. That’s why It should be noted that these changes in results may prove problematic for 
the final hazard risk assessment. In more details if the AI tool applies a high reliability score into a 
paper, that will then be used for the assessment, if the AI had by mistake given this score due to its 
inherited problems such as hallucination the hazard assessment will be conducted with incomplete 
or false data. 
To counter the above problem an additional layer of evaluation should be added to the overall 
framework. As the AI applies reliability scores into the plethora of produced research, a human 
evaluator with expertise into toxicology will reevaluate the papers with the highest reliability and 
relevance score. By doing that we decrease the amount of time scientist would need to spend into 
deciding which papers are of reliability and relevance. At the same time the AI’s inherited problems 
are mitigated  
 There is evident consistent in AI evaluation which gives as a promising integration of such tools 
into frameworks such as PlasticRiskCat. 
6.2 PlasticRiskCat Radar Plots 
Continuing we have the resulting radar plots and three methods of evaluation of them, the results 
here also show a promising picture for the aspect integration of AI assisting tools into the hazard 
assessment frameworks.  
Starting with the fact that the scenario with the best papers was proved to be the most consistent 
and reproductible out of all scenarios. This shows that high quality data and a rigorous evaluation 
of data, with a situationally a prompt with very specific gaudiness can result in useful insights.  
For the three scenarios we can see that the only one with consisting of results was the radar plot 
produced by the high-quality papers. In the other two scenarios the picture vastly changes:  
In more details by comparing the Euclidean distance, cosine similarity and MAE, gave us valuable 
insights into understanding if the the choosing of the papers have additional effects into the quality 
and reproducibility of the results.  
By using this method, it is shown that by training PlasticRiskCat we achieve a reproductible and 
consistent radar plot. Between all the hazard assessments the only one with the same result were the 
trained tool. The highest difference was between the trained in selected research, that’s an unexpected 
result as the expected highest divergence should be between the two untrained models as these had 
the highest potential for AI hallucination.   
Due to the specific language input that PlasticRiskCat tool has it counters shows that the untrained 
model produces the second most correlated results. The difference in correlation and reproducibility 
between untrained and trained in specific papers can be explained because the specific papers guide 
its time the AI in different results. In more detail when we chose the highest in reliability and 
relevance research, we also increased the chance of these papers to agree with each other. This is 
because there is much higher chance for papers of this quality to agree with each other than random 
research which could diverge from the consensus. 
==PAGE:48==
Cosine similarities couldn’t produce valuable infights due to the similarities in shape these 
evaluations had. By expanding in this subject, the overall shape of the evaluations remain more or 
less the same, this is because even if the score in the case of the tool without any papers and in the 
case of the tool with the best papers even if the score decreases by one point  in carcinogenicity for 
example it will also be 1 higher in one of the other hazard endpoints. This causes the cosine similarity 
to produce very similar results.  
That’s why a third method was important to also implement, this method was MAE. With the use of 
MAE, it becomes clear how big the differences between the methods were and the effect the 
additional papers had for the resulted plots.  
It becomes very clear when observing MAE that the additional papers influence the resulting radar 
plot as also the reproducibility of the results. The tool which takes the information from the web 
doesn’t reproduce the same results, this may be due to the vast information there is in the web. This 
makes AI interprete research not necessarily with interest for PS being interpreted as important. By 
providing data to the AI and guiding it via a very specific prompt the resulting radar plots become 
more similar between them.  
This scenario happens only with high in reliability data and not with random papers, an explanation 
on why we don’t see reproducibility in the resulting radar plot from random papers could be 
explained from a potential disagreement of these papers between them. In more detail, research of 
high reliability many times results in very similar findings, this is because they follow a very strict 
process which can be identified and be evaluated. On the other hand, papers of lower reliability may 
not have disclosed all aspects that could make their results differentiate.  
In the case of training the tool in specific papers these papers may disagree highly between them, so 
one or two papers that diverge from the consensus can have great impact in the resulting hazard 
assessment.  
The highest MAE can be observed in a no data evaluation and best data evaluation. This was to be 
expected as the tool ceases to take random data from the web but starts to evaluate only the one, we 
provide to the system. This increases the chance that the AI can comets the same conclusions again 
and mitigates potential hallucinations.

6.3 Application of the SciRAP Framework on PS Studies 
By applying the SciRAP criteria on the PS data we collected it became evident that the reliability 
and relevance evaluation plays decisive role on the resulted hazard assessment. By applying the 
criteria to the totality of available studies we observed that a big portion of the produced research 
does not meet the methodological and reporting standards. Additionally, it became evident that by 
including only the high in relevance and reliability data into our GPT based tool, the uncertainty of 
the of the assessment was reduced. At the same time by this was the only scenario with reproductible 
==PAGE:49==
result, producing an identical radar plot. This shows that a GPT based tool can do quality evaluation 
under properly guided prompt engineered structured criteria.  
6.4 Application of the PlasticRiskCat Framework on PS 
The GPT based tool PlasticRiskCat produced a reproductible radar plot only when had high in 
reliability and relevance data input. After the evaluation PS was associated with very strong 
evidence for neurotoxicity, reproductive toxicity, oxidative stress, and organ specific toxicity, 
and strong evidence for metabolic, endocrine, immune, respiratory, and gastrointestinal effects. This 
evaluation frames PS as hazardous for the human health across many health endpoints.  
 
6.5 Integration of AI into Hazard assessment.  
All results lead to the same conclusion, that AI version of PlasticRiskCat and SciRAP can be very 
useful tools to be used by researchers for identifying best available data and for showing a trend in 
the resulting assessment.  
For the AI to achieve that a rigorous evaluation of the data must be performed, the evaluation must 
be conducted by a human. The AI will do the initial screening of all papers, the evaluate then will 
take the best papers and will apply a second round of human screening choosing the papers that 
human the human decided.  
Additionally in the case of the final risk assessment it should be clearly expressed that these results 
must be produced only by very thoroughly screened data of the highest reliability. Additionally, I 
would recommend dedicated research in the choose papers by which we conducted the final risk 
assessment from a human. 
The human evaluation will give us the final picture of how the resulted radar plot would compare to 
one produced by a toxicology expert who had the same data as the AI  
6.6 Projects findings alignment with similar research  
As AI is a newly introduced tool most research is similar to this thesis but not identical, from 
publications such as Sonnenburg et al. (2024) we can observe that most researchers came to similar 
conclusions with me. In Sonnenburg et al. (2024) an attempt to extract toxicological data using LLM 
was made, the researchers compared a GPT version specifically fine-tuned in toxicological language 
and compared it to a regular GPT. The findings showed that the fine-tuned GPT outperformed the 
regular GPT, this comes in alignment with my findings.  
Additionally, Nykvist et al (2025) showed that a prompt-based approach for literature screening can 
be successfully applied in screening literature based on their abstracts and titles. In their research a 
regular GPT was used, successfully screening literature and by sequence providing support to the 
idea that AI can reduce literature screening time from researchers.  
==PAGE:50==
As highlighted by Hartung and Kleinstreuer (2025) AI evaluations such us the one performed by our 
GPT based tools are depended on their specific version of the GPT, this may create problems in the 
evaluation. A rephrasing of the prompt should then be considered or the return in previous versions 
of the GPT, most available GPTs give the ability to the user to choose between versions that may 
align better to their specific needs.  
From Blümmel et al (2024) validates the claim that LLMs can be used as tools that assist researchers 
in information extracting. The project compared the AI outputs with the traditional manual outputs; 
the AI showed good extraction quality and improved the overall efficiency as researchers could focus 
on a fraction of the data and not the totality.  
Finally, Hartung et al. (2024) showed that AI faced challenges when reporting  heterogenous data 
such as in vivo or in vitro and NAMs. To address this issue, our study applies a category specific 
GPT based tool. Each one is tailored to the criteria  of the respective category  , that way improving 
the consistency and interpretability of AI assisted tools.

7. Conclusions 
 
In this thesis, I set out to test the PlasticRiskCat framework using the full body of available health 
effect literature on PS. additionally, to evaluate the potential LLMs in screening literature and 
assessing the relevance and reliability of these studies. Finally, I also examine how less reliable or 
relevant studies may influence the final PlasticRiskCat evaluation of PS. 
Both PlasticRiskCat and the AI SciRAP tools managed to effectively assess the overall data into 
meaningful radar plot hazard assessment and to assess the reliability and relevance of all available 
data on PS.   
First the LLMs gave a very close approximation of the quality of available research on PS by 
applying the SciRAP criteria. Although there were some disagreements between a manual evaluation 
and an AI assisted one, the use of the AI assisted tool cannot completely replace human evaluation. 
Rather, it can help the evaluator differentiate data of interest to those studies and findings that did 
not fulfill all the necessary criteria. Furthermore, LLMs can successfully assist toxicological 
evaluators into prioritizing research to be evaluated and used.  
Additionally, PlasticRiskCat successfully produced reproducible results when reliable and consistent 
data was given to it. The results showed that the tool is successful in doing the hazard assessment 
only when was provided with the most reliable and relevant data. In other cases, the results were 
neither consistent nor reproductible. The result radar plot from the scenario of most reliable and 
relevant data input showed clear the hazardous potential of PS. With very strong evidence for health 
hazard in the following categories: Neurotoxicity, Reproductive toxicity, oxidative stress and organ 
specific toxicity.  PlasticRiskCat can so be used as an assisting tool by evaluators for visualizing 
where research is enough and where additionally data should be collected. PlasticRiskCat so should 
only be used as an assistant tool to a specialist and cannot replace human evaluators.
