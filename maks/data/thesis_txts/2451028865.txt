Abstract
The aim for this thesis is to study and test acquisition, preprocessing techniques and
mathematical models with regards to their ability to reduce ring artifacts arising from
flat-field estimation errors in CT reconstructions.
A mathematical model is formulated for an acquisition technique where the object
is shifted slightly in each measurement. Analysis of the mathematical model showed
that the technique simply ”smears out” the flat-field estimation errors in the sinogram.
The reconstructions obtained with this technique had reduced the ring artifacts signifi-
cantly but they appeared rather noisy compared to the other ring reducing techniques
and did not obtain the lowest error measures in most of the simulated experiments.
The preprocessing technique proposed in [52] applied smoothing to the sinogram to
reduce the ring artifacts. However, the simulations showed that the technique was
prone to introduce significant errors as a consequence of the smoothing. The second
preprocessing technique proposed in [39] combines wavelet and Fourier filtering to
reduce the ring artifacts and delicately picks out the flat-field estimation errors and
filters these. The experiments showed that this technique was less prone to introduce
new artifacts and errors and managed to obtain low relative errors in the simulated
experiments.
The three techniques have in common that they try to manipulate the sinogram to
reduce the ring artifacts. In [2] a mathematical model is proposed. The model is
based on the statistical nature of the measurements and takes the systematic errors
induced by flat-field estimation errors into account. The mathematical model was
compared to the three ring reduction techniques in a simulated experiment in which
the mathematical model reduced the ring artifacts the most.
The preprocessing techniques and the mathematical model were tested on real data,
namely a neutron data set provided by Manchester University [19] and a X-ray data
set recorded at I12-JEEP beamline, Diamond Light Source [20]. The experiments
showed that the mathematical model performed best in the case of a low signal to
noise ratio whereas the preprocessing technique based on wavelet Fourier filtering was
preferred for the experiments with relative high signal to noise ratio.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
X-ray computed tomography (CT) is a non-invasive scanning technique used to ac-
quire morphological information of the interior of objects, without cutting or damag-
ing them. CT scanning is widely used in different sciences and is especially known for
its application in medicine, where it allows doctors to acquire images of the interior
of the body for diagnostic purposes [46]. In addition CT has applications in various
other sciences such as material science [34], archaeology and aviation security [31].
In X-ray CT a source sends X-rays at a given intensity through an object.
The
X-rays are attenuated as they pass through the object and the attenuated X-rays
are measured by detectors placed opposite to the source. The source and detectors
are rotated around the object such that measurements are collected from multiple
projection angles.
These measurements are collected in an array referred to as a
transmission sinogram.
The transmission sinogram is normalized by the intrinsic intensity of the source. The
reconstruction models are often based on the assumption that the intrinsic intensity
is known but in practice the intensity is estimated. The intensity is estimated by
measurements without an object in the scanner. Such measurements are called air
scans [15], flat-fields [2], or white-fields [48]. The flat-field measurements are often
uncertain due to the statistical nature of photons and the flat-field estimation may
introduce errors in the reconstruction as a consequence. The flat-field estimation er-
rors are systematic and appear as concentric ring artifacts in the reconstruction [5,
45]. The ring artifacts can have severe impact on the reconstructions, especially when
the acquisition time or the dose is limited [2]. The impact of the ring artifacts are
illustrated in Figure 1.1. The figure illustrates how ring artifacts in CT reconstruc-
tions may obscure image details [45]. Other factors that may lead to ring artifacts
are miscalibrated or dead detector pixels on the charge coupled device (CCD) [2].
Neutron CT is non-invasive scanning technique where the source sends out neutrons
[37]. Neutron CT is based on the same model assumptions whereby the same tech-
niques and reconstruction models can be applied to neutron data [33]. Reconstruc-
tions based on neutron imaging suffer from flat-field estimation errors just as the
X-ray reconstructions and ring reduction is a pressing issue in neutron imaging as

There are different approaches for reducing the ring artifacts in CT reconstructions.
A popular method is an acquisition technique where the object is shifted slightly
in each measurement [18]. The otherwise systematic flat-field estimation errors are
”smeared out” by the shifting and the result is a reconstruction where the flat-field
estimation errors no longer look systematic to the human eye.
Another common
approach to reduce ring artifacts is to apply preprocessing to the sinogram.
The
preprocessing methods manipulate the sinograms in an attempt to reduce potential
flat-field estimation errors.
The mentioned acquisition and preprocessing techniques are often used in practice,
but we are not currently aware that their impact on the reconstructions have been
analyzed. Moreover, the techniques do not address the underlying problem with the
flat-field estimation errors as they try to manipulate the sinogram to fit the reconstruc-
tion model. In [2] a mathematical model denoted Striped Weighted Least Squares
(SWLS) is proposed. The model is based on the statistical nature of the measure-
ments and takes the systematic errors introduced by flat-field estimation errors into
account.
The reconstruction models assume that the flux is constant throughout the scan, but
there might be a change of the source intensity also referred to as a change of flux. In
this case, the sinogram will not be normalized properly as a consequence. The errors
introduced by the change of flux can be corrected for by computing the scaling factor
describing the change of flux throughout the scan.

Outline
This thesis focuses on studying and testing the acquisition technique, preprocessing
techniques and the mathematical SWLS model with regards to their ability to reduce
ring artifacts. They are tested in simulated experiments along with experiments with
a neutron data set and an X-ray data set.
Chapter 2 introduces the mathematical theory for X-ray CT and neutron CT along
with the basic statistic properties. In Chapter 3 a mathematical model for the ac-
quisition technique is formulated and analyzed. Moreover, two types of preprocess-
ing techniques are introduced, namely a preprocessing technique based on a sorting
and smoothing of the sinogram denoted Pnlm and a preprocessing technique based
on combined wavelet and Fourier filtering denoted Pwff. The mathematical model
SWLS where the flat-field estimation errors are taken into account is presented and
derived. Lastly, a model for the change of flux is formulated. All three ring reduction
techniques are analyzed and tested on simulated experiments in Chapter 4. They are
evaluated based on their ability to reduce ring artifacts visually, combined with a
quantitative assessment in terms of relative attenuation error (RAE). The ring reduc-
tion techniques are tested for different reconstruction methods, namely the filtered
back projection (FBP), Simultaneous Algebraic Reconstruction Technique (SART)
and Weighed Least Squares (WLS). The SWLS model is tested against the ring re-
duction techniques on both simulated and real experiments. Simulated experiments
with the change of flux model are conducted. Chapter 5 presents the data experi-
ments, namely the neutron data set provided by Jakob S. Jørgensen, The University
of Manchester [19] and the X-ray data set recorded at I12-JEEP beamline, Diamond
Light Source [20]. The results are discussed in Chapter 6 and the conclusion is pre-
sented in Chapter 7.
The main contributions of this thesis are:
• formulation of a mathematical model for the acquisition technique, Chapter 3,
• analysis of the acquisition model in comparison to the model for the regular CT
scan, Chapter 3,
• comparison of the ring reduction techniques and the SWLS model on simulated
data, Chapter 4,
• comparison of the preprocessing techniques and the SWLS model on the neutron
data set provided by Manchester university [19] and the X-ray data set made
available by [52], Chapter 5,
• formulation of an optimization model correcting for change of flux during the
scan, Chapter 3.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In this chapter the geometry and physics of 2D Computed Tomography (CT) are
described. The mathematical theory behind the Radon transform and its connection
to the parallel beam geometry is explained. Moreover, the filtered back projection
also known as the inversion formula for the Radon transform is derived. Some basic
statistical distributions are introduced along with statistical point estimations such
as the maximum likelihood estimate and the maximum a posteriori estimate.
2.1
X-Ray Computed Tomography
There are several different experimental set-ups for the 2D CT-scanners, most com-
mon are the parallel beam and the fan beam geometry [6]. The parallel beam geometry
was used in early scanners and is used today in large-scale synchrotron facilities. The
data sets in this thesis are gathered by scanners using the parallel beam geometry.
Therefore the theory will be derived for the parallel beam geometry. Moreover, the
data acquired using parallel-beam geometry has a direct interpretation in terms of the
Radon transform of the object. If we consider a single experimental set-up, this means
that analytic and iterative methods may be easily compared. The analytic method
could for instance be based on the inversion formula for the Radon transform.
The experimental set-up for the parallel-beam geometry is illustrated in Figure 2.1.
The source sends out r parallel X-rays at a fixed projection angle θ ∈[0◦, 360◦[. The
rays are attenuated as they pass through the object and a detector measures the
attenuated X-rays. The X-ray attenuation depends on the attenuation coefficient of
the material. Different materials will be discernible by looking at the intensity values
of the reconstruction, this also means that it is possible to image the internal structure
of the object. The source and the detectors are then rotated around the object such
that measurements from p projection angles are obtained.
The measured data is
called a transmission sinogram and is usually represented as a matrix Y ∈Rr×p
where entry (i, j) corresponds to the measurement from the i’th detector at the j’th
projection angle. A mathematical model for the attenuation of the X-rays can be
formulated by Lambert-Beer’s law.

2.2
Neutron Computed Tomography
Neutron imaging is a non-invasive scanning technique similar to X-ray imaging. The
physics of neutron imaging is characterized by Lambert-Beer’s law as for X-ray imag-
ing [33]. However, neutrons interact differently than X-rays with materials due to
their zero charge [12]. X-rays are in general attenuated more by denser materials
whereas the neutrons are attenuated more by lighter materials such as hydrogen. In
this way the two techniques provide different material contrasts and reveal different
material properties [32]. The same reconstruction models can be applied to neutron
data as to the X-ray data and the most commonly used method is the filtered back
projection which is an inversion formula for the Radon transform.

A strength of the acquisition technique is that the data is not manipulated in the
sense that there is nothing removed by later processing. Thus it is not expected that
this acquisition technique will introduce new artifacts since the error source is simply
distributed differently throughout the data. A drawback is that the technique cannot
be applied after the scan and can therefore not be applied to already obtained data
sets. Another drawback is that it may increase the scan time by up to 100%. The
scan time might be expensive and time demanding thus in these cases the method
might not be applied due to the extra cost. Moreover, it is not possible to apply this
technique for all scanners as it is only a selection of scanners that have this function
as a build-in option. Lastly, a drawback is that this acquisition technique does not
address the underlying problem since it simply smears out the errors. Hence it is
expected that the reconstructions based on this technique might appear more noisy
and may still contain some degree of ring artifacts due to the remaining structure of
the relative flat-field in Figure 3.3b. Therefore it is expected that a reconstruction
model which addresses the underlying problem might be able to perform as well as
well, if not better, than this method.

3.5 Sorting and Smoothing Preprocessing Technique
27
3.5
Sorting and Smoothing Preprocessing Technique
A preprocessing technique processes the sinogram before applying a reconstruction
algorithm. Two types of preprocessing will be considered in this thesis, a sorting
and smoothing technique proposed in [52] and a combined wavelet Fourier filtering
technique proposed in [39].
Both techniques tries to reduce the ring artifacts by
reducing the stripes in the sinogram.
The first preprocessing technique uses a combination of sorting and smoothing in
attempt to reduce the stripes in the sinogram. This preprocessing technique can be
described by 3 steps. First each row of the sinogram is sorted by its gray-scale values.
A median filter is then applied to each column of the sorted sinogram. The median
filtering is performed with the Matlab function medfilt1 which applies an nth-order
one-dimensional median filter. The idea is to go through each column entry by entry
and replace each entry with the median of neighboring entries. Lastly, the smoothed
sinogram is resorted to obtain the final preprocessed sinogram.
The technique is
denoted Pnlm and reconstructions obtained using this technique will be denoted with
the prefix Pnlm.
The three steps of the preprocessing method is illustrated in Figure 3.4 along with
the final preprocessed sinogram. The amount of stripes are significantly reduced in
the preprocessed sinogram in Figure 3.4d compared to the unprocessed sinogram in
Figure 3.4a. Figure 3.4e depicts the differences between the unprocessed sinogram and
the Pnlm sinogram. The figure confirms that the preprocessing method has reduced
the stripes in the sinogram.
However, the figure also reveals that the smoothing
technique has smoothed out some of the details in the sinogram since the figure does
not only consist of stripes. This observation is further supported by considering the
difference between the baseline sinogram and the Pnlm preprocessed sinogram shown
in Figure 3.4f. In the ideal case these two sinograms should be the same resulting in
a zero difference but from the figure of the differences it can be seen that some of the
structure of the sinogram has disappeared as a consequence of the smoothing. Thus
the Pnlm preprocessing technique method may reduce the ring artifacts but it might
introduce other artifacts in the process. Especially the presence of sharp edges may
lead to streak artifacts in the reconstructions [52].
The strengths of the algorithm are that it is easy to understand, easy to implement,
the computational cost is low and it only requires a single parameter n. The main
drawback of the method is that the median filter might introduce new artifacts, es-
pecially in presence of sharp edges. The severity of the introduced artifacts depends
on the parameter n and it is not trivial how to choose n. Chosen too low the method
fails to reduce the stripe artifacts in the sinogram sufficiently, but chosen too large
the method will introduce new streak artifacts in the reconstructions. However, the
new introduced streak artifacts might be easier to reduce with further processing than
the original ring artifacts from the flat-field estimation error [52].

3.7
Simultaneous Algebraic Reconstruction Technique
FBP has been the method of choice for CT imaging due to its low computational
cost. However, in recent years the iterative methods have been revisited [30, 10] due
to the increase in computational power. The simultaneous algebraic reconstruction
technique (SART) [3] is a method which is well suited for X-ray CT as it can be
applied to limited data and noisy measurements which FBP struggles with [41]. The
SART method belongs to a class of projected gradient methods of the form

The matrices are given by D = V −1 and M = W −1,
where V is a diagonal matrix with row sums of A in the diagonal, and W is a diagonal
matrix with the column sums of A in the diagonal [26].
A strength of the iterative methods is that they provide more flexible algorithms [10]
compared to the analytic methods. They allow for regularization and constraints,
where examples of constraints suited for CT are box constraints and non-negativity
constraints [26]. The main disadvantages of the iterative algorithms are the computa-
tional cost and the need for a stopping criteria. Moreover, the SART method might
converge to a solution corrupted by noise if it is not regularized [30]. For the sim-
ulated experiments, the SART reconstruction with the minimum relative error and
the reconstruction for the maximum number of iterations will be shown such that the
best and worst case of the algorithm can be investigated.
The SART will be used to evaluate the acquisition technique S and the two preprocess-
ing techniques Pnlm and Pwff as the SART method is used in practice by physicists
and CT manufactures. The SART model is not based on the physics of the X-ray CT
and thus it might be beneficial to consider the statistical models.

reconstructions are shown for the converged problem or the maximum number of

whereas SWLS and S-WLS has the same RAE after the maximum number of itera-

the difference images in Figure 4.21 we see the same behavior for the S-WLS as for the
previous experiments, namely that there is some clear structure visible which is hard

Acquisition Technique
A mathematical model for the acquisition technique was formulated in (3.13) to ad-
dress how the technique affects the reconstructions. The projection sinogram in the
model was linearized to isolate the flat-field estimation error. The result was the
Gaussian model in (3.16) which showed that the relative flat-field estimation error
only differed by a permutation matrix from the regular scan. Hence based on the
Gaussian model, it was expected that the flat-field estimation errors would have the
same magnitude in the sinogram domain for both types of scans. This analysis sup-
ports the presumption that the acquisition technique does not address the issue but
simply redistributes the flat-field estimation errors throughout the sinogram.
This presumption was supported in the simulated experiments in Figure 4.8 since
the RSE measure was the same for FBP and S-FBP. The FE and RAE measures

was reduced meaning that the contribution from the flat-field estimation error in
the reconstruction had decreased.
Hence the simulated experiments showed that
the flat-field estimation errors are reduced in the image domain even though they
have the same magnitude in the sinogram domain. The simulated reconstructions
for the acquisition technique appeared to have reduced the ring artifacts the best
in almost every simulated experiment. However, the reconstructions appeared very
noisy and did not obtain the lowest RAE or FE for most of the simulations. Their
noisy appearance can be explained by considering the reconstruction of the flat-field
estimation errors in Figure 4.4b. The figure reveals how the flat-field estimation errors
are ”smeared out” through the reconstruction and thereby appear as random errors.
It was shown in Chapter 3 that the Fourier transform of the reconstructions can
be used to reveal otherwise hidden ring structures in the reconstructions. At first
glance, the acquisition technique seemed to have eliminated the ring artifacts in the
reconstructions. However, masked ring structures in the reconstructions were revealed
by considering the Fourier transform of the reconstructions and ˆu(z), examples of this
are depicted in Figure 4.3b and 4.5b. Hence the flat-field estimation errors for the
acquisition technique are more systematic than first assumed by solely evaluating the
reconstruction by its appearance.
The SART experiment showed that if the iteration sequence is stopped at the mini-
mum RAE then the acquisition technique obtains the lowest RAE among all the ring
reduction methods. A similar result was obtained in the WLS test in Section 4.3,
where the acquisition technique obtained the lowest RAE for the experiments with
regularization.
The acquisition technique has not been tested on real data as the data sets available for
this thesis have both been recorded by a regular scan. An experiment was conducted
where an object was scanned twice, first by a regular scan and then by a scan where the
object was shifted in each projection. However, the generated data had a relatively
high SNR whereby the potential flat-field estimation errors were negligible. Thus
the data could not be used to compare the acquisition technique to the other ring
reduction methods as there were no ring artifacts in the data acquired by the regular
scan.
The acquisition technique proved a stable ring reduction method as it did not in-
troduce new artifacts or modify features in the phantom in any of the simulated
experiments. In addition there are no parameters involved in the technique whereby
the technique is easy to use and understand. Hence it seemed as it might be a de-
scent ring reduction method especially when using a reconstruction model capable of
handling the potential extra noise. The technique’s main drawback is the acquisition
technique requires the rotation to stop for the shifting and acquisition. In the fast
tomography imaging setups at synchrotrons the data is acquired continuously while
rotating.
Thus the acquisition technique eliminates the possibility for fast scans.

Pnlm Technique
The preprocessing technique Pnlm was analyzed in Chapter 3. The analysis showed
a possible drawback for the technique, namely that the smoothing applied to the
sinogram might ”smooth out” some of the features in the sinogram. The preprocessing
technique requires only a single parameter but this parameter must be chosen with
care since if chosen too low the ring artifacts in the reconstruction are not reduced
whereas chosen too large there is a high risk of introducing new streak artifacts or
other errors in the reconstructions.
The experiments in Chapter 4 and Chapter 5 showed that the Pnlm technique in-
troduced streak artifacts in several of the experiments. Moreover, in the simulated
FBP experiments the Pnlm technique introduced other errors such that the Pnlm re-
constructions had different intensity distribution than the phantom. This type of
introduced error appeared very difficult to detect as the errors was masked in the
reconstructions. The Pnlm technique performed slightly better for the iterative meth-
ods as there were less new artifacts introduced in the Pnlm reconstructions for SART
and WLS in Section 4.2 and 4.3, respectively. However, there are some significant
errors introduced in the SART experiment depicted in Figure 4.16c.
The neutron data set had a relative high number of sharp edges in the sinogram and
as a result the Pnlm technique removed a notable amount of data as the difference
between the Pnlm preprocessed sinogram and the unprocessed sinogram depicted in
Figure 5.7d showed the contour of the entire sinogram. This resulted in a FBP recon-
struction with visible streak artifacts close to the edges of the Pnlm reconstruction.
The X-ray data set had a relative few number of edges present in the sinogram. The
Pnlm technique showed to be better suited for this type of data set as it did not
introduce new visible artifacts in the reconstruction.
To summaries the Pnlm technique did not obtain the lowest RAE for any of the
simulated experiments and all of the reconstructions contained some degree of ring
artifacts while still introducing new artifacts. The X-ray data experiment showed
that the technique performs better for sinograms with fewer edges. The technique
seems very sensitive to the parameter choice and should be used with care as the
experiments have shown that it is not always possible to detect the introduced errors
by solely looking at the reconstruction.

6 Discussion
Pwff Technique
The Pwff preprocessing technique isolates the high frequent stripes and apply a damp-
ing filter only to these coefficients whereby all other coefficients remain unchanged.
The analysis of the technique in Chapter 3 showed that the difference between the
Pwff preprocessed sinogram and unprocessed sinogram consists only of stripes and
therefore is less prone to introduce new artifacts compared to the Pnlm technique.
The experiments in Chapter 4 and 5 showed that the Pwff technique managed to
reduce the ring artifacts without introducing notable new artifacts. However, ring
artifacts were still visible in several of the Pwff reconstructions. The Pwff technique
struggled the most with the FBP grains experiment in Figure 4.2c as it in this exper-
iment introduced a darker circle in the center which was hard to classify as an error
without knowing the ground truth. However, the technique obtained the lowest RAE
for this experiment. The Pwff technique obtained the lowest RAE in most of the
simulated experiments even though it did not appear to reduce the rings as effective
as the acquisition technique.
The Pwff technique was tested on the two data sets and performed best for the
neutron experiment with all data channels depicted in Figure 5.7e and the X-ray
data experiment in Figure 5.13e. However, the Pwff technique struggled to reduce
the ring artifacts sufficiently for the neutron experiment with only a subset of channels
depicted in Figure 5.8e.
The strength of the Pwff technique is that the risk of introducing new artifacts is
greatly reduced compared to the Pnlm technique. Moreover, it is relatively cheap to
compute. The main drawback of this method is that it is not trivial how to choose
the parameters and that several of the reconstruction still contained some degree of
ring artifacts.
SWLS model
The SWLS model was tested on the simulated experiment with the grains phantom
with and without regularization. The unregularized SWLS reconstructions is depicted
in Figure 4.23a and the SWLS model proved efficient in reducing the ring artifacts
as the SWLS reconstruction did not appear to contain any ring artifacts. In addition
the SWLS model obtained the lowest RAE along with the S-WLS reconstruction but
seemed to be less affected by ring artifacts than the S-WLS. This observation was
supported by considering the difference between the phantom and the computed re-
construction in Figure 4.23b and its Fourier transform in Figure 4.23c. Neither of
these images contained ring structures indicating that the SWLS model had success-
fully reduced the ring artifacts in the reconstruction.
The experiment was repeated with TV regularization to avoid the solution to converge
==PAGE:121==

to a solution heavily influenced by noise. The TV regularized SWLS solution did not
obtain the lowest RAE as the RAE for the regularized experiment was 0.1 whereas the
S-WLS reconstruction obtained a RAE of 0.09. However, they performed very close in
terms of RAE and the SWLS has the visual advantage that the SWLS reconstruction
appeared less affected by ring artifacts.
The SWLS model is more complex and requires more computational power than the
FBP model combined with preprocessing. Thus the SWLS model was not tested on
the neutron data experiment with all the data as the FBP with Pwff preprocessing
proved sufficient to eliminate the ring artifacts. However, the SWLS model proved
the most efficient in reducing the ring artifact for the neutron data experiment using
only a subset of the data channels in Figure 5.9d. Considering the X-ray experiment
in Section 5.2 it can be seen that the SWLS reconstruction did manage to reduce the
ring artifacts, but it did not perform better than the FBP with Pwff preprocessing.
Thus there is not much to gain by applying the SWLS model for this experiment due
to its computational cost. However, this experiment has a high SNR and thus it was
expected that FBP model combined with preprocessing would be sufficient to reduce
ring artifacts.
Change of Flux
The neutron data suffers from a change of flux which inspired the formulation of the
optimization model for the change in flux. The change of flux model in (3.42) was
tested on simulated data. The model proved capable of estimating the true scaling
when non-negativity constrains were added to the model which is illustrated in Fig-
ure 4.28. The simulated experiment opened up for possible improvements such as
changing the regularizing term to a regularize the gradient and not the coefficients.
However, this is left as future work. The change of flux model was applied to the neu-
tron data set, where the unprocessed sinogram along with the flux corrected sinogram
are shown in Figure 5.6. The figure shows that the number of negative background
pixels have been greatly reduced by applying the flux correction obtained from the
optimization model. Thus it seems as the proposed model for flux provides a qualified
method for correcting for the change of flux in simulated experiments and in practice
as well.
Future work
A possible extension of the SWLS model could be to consider other priors for the
flat-field. A possible choice could be to obtain an estimate of the the flat-field from a
simple FBP reconstruction with preprocessing and use this estimate as a prior instead
of the gamma distribution. However, this is left as future work.
All the data experiments are evaluated by visual inspection.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
A mathematical model was successfully formulated for the acquisition technique.
Analysis of the model showed that the flat-field estimation errors are perturbed for
each measurement, and as a result they appear less systematic in the sinogram. As a
consequence, the norm of the flat-field estimation error was not reduced by the per-
turbation but simply distributed differently throughout the sinogram. However, the
errors in the reconstructions arising from flat-field estimation were reduced compared
to a regular scan. They appeared random at first glance, but it was shown that they
were actually systematic as the reconstruction contained mask ring structure.
The acquisition technique performed well in terms of visually reducing the ring arti-
facts in the experiments and obtained the second lowest relative error in all simulated
experiments. The technique did not introduce new artifacts which both preprocess-
ing methods were prone to, especially the Pnlm preprocessing technique. The pre-
processing technique Pwff performed better than Pnlm in all simulated and its main
strength was that it did not seem to visually introduce new artifacts or errors like
Pnlm.
However, it did struggle to reduce the ring artifacts sufficiently for several
of the experiments. To conclude the acquisition technique performed best overall
among the ring reduction techniques on the simulated experiments especially then
using SART or WLS with regularization.
The SWLS model was compared to the ring reduction techniques. The experiment
showed that the SWLS reconstruction and its errors had significantly less ring struc-
ture compared to the ring reduction techniques. In addition, the SWLS reconstruc-
tions obtained the second lowest relative error along with the acquisition technique.
However, the ring artifacts were visually reduced the most in the SWLS reconstruc-
tion. Thus it can be concluded that the SWLS model performed best in the simulated
experiment.
For the real data experiments, the Pwff preprocessing method proved best for the two
experiments with a relatively low noise level, but the SWLS performed best for the
more challenging neutron experiment with only a subset of the data. Hence based on
the data experiments it can be concluded that it seems that the SWLS is preferable for
more challenging data sets, whereas the FBP combined with the Pwff preprocessing

Lastly, a mathematical model for the change of flux was successfully formulated and
the simulated experiment showed that the model was able to estimate the correct
scaling of the sinogram. The model was applied to the neutron data set where a
change of flux had happened during the scan.
The model successfully estimated
the scaling factors and greatly reduced the negative values in the sinogram and in
reconstruction as well.
