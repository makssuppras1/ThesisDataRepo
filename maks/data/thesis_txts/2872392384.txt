1
Introduction
1.1
Project motivation
In recent years, the penetration of renewable energy sources into the electrical grid has accelerated, driven by
their decreasing Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE), advancements in disruptive technologies, and efficiency
improvements. Among these sources, photovoltaic (PV) energy has experienced particularly rapid growth
and is now one of the most widely deployed renewable technologies globally [1, 2, 3, 4].
This growth has been supported by the ongoing development of high-efficiency crystalline silicon modules,
the widespread adoption of bifacial modules, improvements in power electronics such as more powerful smart
inverters, and a favorable learning curve that drives down system costs [1, 2, 3, 4].
Since photovoltaic energy is the present and the future, is essential to seek more efficient ways of operating
and managing these solar parks. Inefficient O&M practices can lead to substantial energy losses and financial
setbacks. These techniques could mean accumulated wasted energy if the plant is not operated properly.
For instance, a study analyzing 15 PV plants in Spain and Italy over 15 months found that energy losses
due to inefficiencies (through real operation and maintenance) accounted for between 22.34% and 27.58% of
the net energy yield [5].
Underperformance due to various factors, including inadequate maintenance, has been estimated to cause
losses up to $4,696 per megawatt annually in the U.S., amounting to billions in lost revenue across the
industry [6].
In the case of solar parks, the maintenance and operation have relied on two methods: preventive and
corrective maintenance [7].
Preventive maintenance refers to scheduled inspections and interventions aimed at reducing the probability
of system failures and performance degradation. This includes activities, such as periodic inverter testing,
visual inspection damage or corrosion, and other additional services, such as thermographic inspections to
detect hot spots, and electrical testing of system components (such as insulation resistance tests or IV curve
measurements). By identifying potential issues early, preventive maintenance helps ensure stable system
performance and enhance component lifetimes [7].
On the other hand, corrective maintenance is performed actively due to detected failures or performance
issues. This may implicate replacing failed modules, repairing inverter faults, or addressing electrical discon-
nections and grounding problems. While corrective maintenance is necessary to restore system functionality,
it is often costlier and associated with downtime-related energy losses, particularly if faults are not promptly
identified [8].
To support these maintenance strategies, most PV plant operators rely on continuous monitoring of system
performance, with a common baseline being the analysis of data from the maximum power point (MPP)
tracking systems embedded in string inverters or at the plant level. Monitoring parameters such as string-
level DC power, AC output, and MPP voltage and current trends allows operators to detect deviations from
expected performance. This MPP-based monitoring is widely adopted due to its ease of implementation,
low data complexity, and robustness under normal operating conditions [7, 9].
Nevertheless, MPP-based strategies lack a complete diagnosis of the possible issues in the power generation.
For example, MPP data may indicate reduced string output, but it cannot easily distinguish between soiling,
partial shading, module degradation, or electrical faults such as diode failures or contact resistance issues
[10]. As a result, operators may need to conduct time-consuming field investigations or rely on additional
diagnostic tools, to localize and classify faults with greater precision. Recently, thanks to new effortless
techniques (such as remote inverter-based IV curves and infrared thermography), new services have appeared
to provide a broader picture of the state of the photovoltaic plant. These services can shed light on the root
cause of common issues in the PV plant at the DC level.
Current-voltage (IV) curve analysis has been an established diagnostic method for identifying performance
issues at the PV string or module level [10, 11, 12]. Traditionally, IV curve measurements are conducted
using portable IV tracers during on-site inspections. This method is particularly effective for detecting a
1
==PAGE:9==
wide range of faults, including mismatch losses, cell degradation, shading effects, interconnection failures,
potential-induced degradation (PID), and bypass diode faults [10, 12].
Historically, IV curve measurements have been performed during scheduled preventive maintenance or as
part of corrective investigations following observed performance losses in system-level monitoring data [7].
However, the conventional IV curve measurement process is labor-intensive and time-consuming, as each
string must be measured individually using field equipment, often requiring manual disconnection and con-
trolled irradiance conditions [11]. This operational difficulty has limited the frequency with which IV curve
diagnostics can be employed in large-scale PV plants.
In recent years, advances in inverter technology and power electronics have introduced new possibilities for
IV-based diagnostics. Some inverter manufacturers, such as Huawei and Sungrow, now offer inverters with
built-in capabilities to perform remote IV curve scanning at the string level [13]. This innovation enables
plant operators to obtain IV curves without field intervention, reducing effort and enabling more frequent
and systematic fault detection.
By providing a wider diagnostic information than MPP-based monitoring alone, remote IV curve analysis
represents a promising tool for O&M strategies in large PV plants. It offers the potential to enable early
fault detection, optimize maintenance planning, and ultimately improve plant yield and profitability.
However, measured string IV curves must be obtained under certain conditions. For example, irradiance
levels typically need to exceed 400 W/m2 in the plane of array (from 600 W/m2 for inverter-based IVs [13]) to
ensure that the measurement results are accurate and representative of real system performance [14]. In low
irradiance conditions, IV curves may not reveal latent faults clearly due to increased measurement noise and
altered curve shape [11]. Also, the curves must be the same within 5% for stable irradaince conditions[14].
Furthermore, while IV curve analysis provides valuable insight into string-level performance, it cannot al-
ways pinpoint the exact root cause of observed underperformance or faults. For instance, similar IV curve
anomalies may result from module degradation, soiling, partial shading, or electrical connection issues —
phenomena that can present overlapping signatures in the IV curve [10, 14].
1.2
Objectives
This project aims to explore the benefits, advantages and drawbacks, and the possible applications of current-
voltage curves based of inverter (IV curves) over MPP monitoring data for the operation and maintenance
of utility-scale solar parks.
To study these, the main objectives of this thesis are:
• Inspect the operation and maintenance methodologies in utility-scale solar parks and assess
points of improvement.
• Examine the diagnostic parameters in the IV curves and develop fault detection methods
based on these parameters.
• Utilize the IV curves models and compare the results with the measured IV curves to develop fault
detection strategies.
• Analyze and compare IV curve (inverter based) fault detection methods with traditional
maximum power point (MPP) to further characterize faults that are visible for MPP data and
identify faults that are invisible for MPP.
• Provide practical recommendations and study the feasibility of incorporating IV-based diagnostics
into existing O&M workflows.

2
State of the art of O&M methods and IV curves
2.1
Introduction to Operation and Maintenance (O&M) procedures in large
PV plants
The Operation and Maintenance (O&M) procedures of a solar park begin once the construction phase is
completed and the plant has been commissioned and connected to the electrical grid. The transition from
construction to O&M is marked by the handover process, where the plant undergoes approval testing to
verify that it meets the contractual performance requirements (e.g., power output, quality standards, grid
compliance). From this point onward, the focus shifts to the correct functioning of the plant providing high
levels of technical, safety and economic performance during its operational lifetime, typically 20–30 years [9].
To ensure those standards, associations such as Solar Power Europe and the International Energy Agency
(IEA) have developed O&M best practice guidelines for the companies that supply these services around
the world[9, 7]. Depending on each region, the guidelines may vary because the different challenges that the
power plan faces [8]. In this work, an European/American best practices point of view has been studied for
consistency and relevance to the studied case.
According to the O&M best practice guidelines [9], some of the most important tasks in the operation of a
PV power plant include:
• Plant Performance Monitoring and Supervision: Continuous monitoring of key performance
indicators (KPIs) and system operation to detect deviations, optimize energy yield, and support pre-
ventive maintenance.
• Optimization of O&M: Implementation of data-driven processes and tools to enhance the efficiency
and effectiveness of maintenance activities and asset management.
• Power Plant Controls: Management of plant-level and inverter-level controls to ensure grid compli-
ance, power quality, and safe operation under varying environmental and grid conditions.
At this point, the O&M team is involved to a greater extent in all of the tasks previously mentioned, being
responsible for routine operations, performance optimization, and the execution of both preventive and
corrective maintenance actions. In this report we will only develop those directly related to the operational
activities linked to the O&M team. If interested, the document [9] discusses them thoroughly.
The operation and maintenance of the plant is made by a specialized service provider. This provider may/may
not belong to the owner of the plant. In both cases, to guarantee the correct operation in the plant, a contract
takes place between the owner and the O&M provider. This document will be the commonplace that indicates
the responsibility, the services, prices, among other clauses.
In this contract, a thorough description of the several services is provided. Among the most important ones,
it can be found:
• Technical and Contractual Availability: Performance guarantees based on system uptime and
availability metrics.
• Spare Parts Management: Policies and procedures for inventory control, procurement, and replace-
ment of spare parts.
• Preventive and Corrective Maintenance Services: Detailed description of scheduled maintenance
tasks and response procedures for unplanned faults or failures.
• Performance Reporting: Regular reports on plant performance, fault history, and maintenance
activities.
• Safety and Compliance: Measures to ensure safe working practices and compliance with applicable
standards and regulations.

2.1.1
Plant performance and operation
When it comes to a utility-scale solar park, there are several actors and systems involved in ensuring reliable
and efficient energy production (Figure 1). The actors are the O&M Control Room, the technicians,
the grid Operator (Energinet) and the trader. On the system side, the systems in the power plant
are the control unit, the monitoring web portal and the database to manage the events in the solar
plant.
Let’s break it down into the several systems and the parties involved in them.
The control unit enables remote supervision and control of the plant’s output. Both the trader and the
grid operator can interact with the control unit as required. The grid operator may request an active power
limitation — a temporary reduction in plant output — to maintain grid stability and safety during certain
grid conditions. Similarly, the trader may manage curtailments in response to electricity spot market or
contractual obligations under a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA). Since the detailed market mechanisms
and grid interactions are outside the scope of this thesis, they will not be discussed further here.
In parallel, the O&M control room continuously monitors the operational status of the solar plant. In case
of any event that may damage energy production, the control room can intervene or dispatch maintenance
tasks. To support this, a monitoring platform collects and displays real-time data from all plant equipment
(from string, inverters to transformers) providing a comprehensive overview of plant performance. According
to [9] (Section 9), an effective monitoring system should ensure accurate, timely data acquisition, proper
alarm configuration, and reliable communication between plant systems and the control room (Section 2.1.2).
This management system (usually called webportal [9]) can be set to trigger some alarms in case that some
of the equipment is not working properly. The alarms comprise all the equipment related to the energy
production and provide a first-level indication of potential faults on site. When an alarm is triggered, the
control room reviews the corresponding data and creates or updates a work order in the central database
(often implemented as a Computerized Maintenance Management System, CMMS). Each work order specifies
the the nature of the fault, its location, required parts and priority level.
Field technicians receive their assigned work orders on mobile devices, travel to the site, carry out repairs
or preventive tasks, and then update the work-order status. Those updates flow back to the O&M control
room so engineers can close out the ticket and confirm the fix.

2.1.2
Power Plant Performance Monitoring and Supervision
The monitoring and supervision of a utility-scale PV plant is a critical part of ensuring its optimal per-
formance and early fault detection. According to the SolarPower Europe O&M Best Practice Guidelines [9],
a well-designed monitoring system must allow for detailed follow-up of the entire energy conversion chain,
from the DC-side of the PV array to the AC-side of the grid interface. The O&M team is responsible for
monitoring quality, as well as the retrieved data.
The modern inverters are able to report detailed DC-side metrics—string voltages and currents, as well
as AC-side measurements such as phase voltages, currents, real and reactive power, power factor, internal
temperatures, and inverter status (including any derating or fault codes)
Data loggers (Section 9.1 [9]) are the brain of these inverters, retrieving all the info (usually one datalogger
for all the inverters placed in one substation) upstream. The data retrieving intervals (ranging from one to
fifteen minutes) are done via standard protocols like Modbus/TCP or RS-485 (sections 9.2-9.9 [9]). These
data loggers also gather meteorological inputs (pyranometers, ambient temperature sensors) and readings
from energy meters.
The sensors (previously plugged into a meteor box) are connected to a PLC (Programmable Logic Controller),
which is responsible for normalize these analog (4–20 mA, 0–10 V) and digital signals into Modbus registers,
enforce galvanic isolation and surge protection. Once the info is collected, batches of data are securely moved
to central servers or a cloud environment (over Ethernet, 4G/LTE or fiber).
All of these edge measurements feed into the central monitoring web portal, the O&M team’s cockpit for
visibility, analysis and reporting. This portal ingests raw feeds from any inverter, PLC or data logger format
and normalizes them into a unified schema.
Within the portal itself, alarms are raised for communication losses (inverters, loggers or PLCs going offline)
and performance deviations (inverter trips, zero-current strings, sudden PR drops) (section 9.10.8 [9]).
O&M engineers validate and, if necessary, escalate these into formal work orders; technicians then carry out
corrective or preventive actions on site and update the portal to close the loop.
2.1.3
Maintenance
Maintenance of a solar power plant typically begins with the contractual framework agreed between the asset
owner and the O&M provider. These contracts define both technical availability (the percentage of time the
plant must be generating under predefined, ideal conditions) and contractual availability (which may allow
for scheduled outages, force-majeure events or grid curtailment).
Within that framework, the O&M provider’s obligations around spare-parts management are explained:
minimum stocking levels on-site or at regional warehouses; guaranteed lead times for replenishment; and
replacement thresholds under warranty or service-level agreements. The contract will also enumerate the
suite of services the provider offers—ranging from routine preventive and corrective maintenance to the
response time of the provider.
Among the maintenance activities, the contract differentiates the several types of maintenance and the
responsibility for the O&M provider to act accordingly.
Preventive Maintenance
It consists of scheduled, periodic tasks designed to keep every component (from modules and combiner boxes
to inverters and transformers) operating at peak efficiency. Typical preventive activities include torquing
and tightening electrical connections; replacing air filters and seals in cabinets; calibrating pyranometers and
temperature sensors; verifying inverter cooling-fan operation; and applying the latest firmware or control-
logic patches. Each of these tasks is performed on a predetermined cycle (monthly, quarterly or annually)
and documented so that trends in performance or wear can be spotted before they translate into downtime
or underperformance.
SolarPower best practices provides the suggested time periods between periodical inspections. If interested,

Annex E [9] offers more information.
Corrective Maintenance
Corrective maintenance covers all unplanned interventions required to restore a fault or failure. When an
inverter trips, a string goes offline, or a combiner-box fuse blows, the O&M team mobilizes field technicians
to diagnose and repair the issue. Response times, priority levels and escalation paths are previously discussed
by the service-level agreement in the contract (e.g. 24-hour response for critical inverter failures, 48-hour for
module-string repairs).
Predictive Maintenance
Even though it is optional and it is not well-implemented in the industry, it is becoming very popular among
the services provided by the O&M companies, crunching data-driven analytics. By mining historical SCADA
data the O&M team can anticipate component wear and intervene just before a failure would occur. This
approach can extend component life and save even more unplanned downtime off the calendar.
Extraordinary Maintenance
Finally, out of the scope of the contract, the extraordinary maintenance refers to interventions driven by
exceptional events: severe storms, flooding, fire, theft or vandalism, or grid-level incidents that exceed normal
operating conditions. In case it happens, the bill should be covered by the asset owner.
Additional Services
In addition to core maintenance activities, many O&M providers offer a range of additional services designed
to enhance plant performance or asset value. These may include performance optimization studies, advanced
monitoring and analytics, IV curve testing, thermographic inspections, soiling loss analysis, repowering
solutions, and training programs for asset owners.
2.1.4
Key performance indicators
In the operation and maintenance of utility-scale PV plants, Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) serve as
essential tools to monitor the health of the plant, verify compliance with contractual obligations, and assess
the effectiveness of the O&M provider. Among the many possible KPIs, four core metrics are universally
considered the most critical: Reference Yield, Performance Ratio, Technical Availability, and Contractual
Availability.
These KPIs offer a balanced view of plant performance by capturing both the physical energy conversion
efficiency (Reference Yield, Performance Ratio) and the operational reliability and service quality (Technical
and Contractual Availability). These KPIs are also widely standardized across the industry, required in
practically all O&M contracts and linked to both energy yield and revenue performance (section 10 [9]).
These metrics are sent to the asset owners monthly.

2.2
IV Curve-Based Diagnostics in PV Systems
Current-voltage (IV) curve analysis is one of the most powerful diagnostic tools available for detecting and
classifying faults in photovoltaic (PV) systems. While traditional monitoring based on maximum power
point (MPP) data can indicate performance deviations, it often lacks the granularity needed to identify the
root causes of such issues. IV curve analysis provides a much wider picture of system behavior by capturing
the detailed electrical response of PV modules or strings under known conditions.
As highlighted in the SolarPower Europe O&M Best Practice Guidelines [9] and the IEC 62446-1 standard
[14], IV curve analysis is recognized as a standard approach for evaluating system performance, detecting
early signs of degradation, and verifying system quality during commissioning and maintenance.
This section provides an overview of IV curve-based diagnostics, beginning with the fundamentals of IV curve
interpretation, followed by a discussion of measurement techniques, both traditional and modern (remote
inverter-based). Key parameters extracted from IV curves are then reviewed, along with their diagnostic
significance. Finally, common fault types detectable via IV curve analysis are described, showing how this
method complements and enhances conventional O&M practices.
2.2.1
Fundamentals of IV curve Analysis
The current-voltage (IV) curve of a photovoltaic (PV) string or module captures its complete electrical beha-
vior under given conditions. The IV curve provides a more detailed view of system performance than simple
MPP measurements, allowing operators to identify specific fault signatures and degradation mechanisms.
The European Standard IEC 62446-1 [14], particularly Annex D, provides clear guidance on how to inter-
pret deviations in the IV curve and their diagnostic implications. Similarly, practical guidance from field
experience, such as the comprehensive framework presented by [12], complements the standard by addressing
common measurement issues and advanced troubleshooting strategies.
In a healthy system, the IV curve has a smooth shape with three characteristic regions: a near-horizontal
portion at low voltage (current-dominated), a sharp “knee” where maximum power is extracted, and a near-
vertical portion at high voltage (voltage-dominated) [14] [12]. The exact shape depends on cell technology;
for example, crystalline silicon cells typically exhibit sharper knees than thin-film modules [14].
When deviations from this normal shape occur, they can reveal a wide range of issues, as shown in Figure 3.
The possible causes of such deviations are summarized in Table 1, adapted from both [14] and [12].
Both references agree on the root cause that provokes different shapes along the IV curve. However, [12]
explains more in depth what could be the causes if there were any measurement failures (such as sensors
misalignment).

2.2.2
Measurement techniques for IVs
The quality and reliability of IV curve-based diagnostics depend fundamentally on the measurement tech-
nique used to acquire the IV data. In field applications, IV curves can be collected using several different
methods, each with its own advantages and limitations in terms of accuracy, operational effort, and diagnostic
value.
In recent years, inverter-based IV scanning has emerged as an important innovation, enabling the remote
acquisition of IV curves from large PV plants in a more efficient and scalable manner [13].
Instead of
relying on time-consuming manual testing of individual strings, these systems allow operators to trigger an
automated IV sweep across all strings connected to the inverter, significantly reducing the effort and cost
required for comprehensive diagnostics. As modern PV plants grow in size and complexity, these effortless
technique will provide an easy way to analyze every string in a short period of time.
IV tracer
An I-V tracer directly connects and measures the current and voltage of a PV module or string by sweeping
through a range of voltages (from 0 V to VOC) and recording the corresponding currents, thus generating the
I-V curve. IV tracers are the reference standard for IV measurements and are widely used in commissioning,
preventive maintenance, and troubleshooting. The advantages of tracers are:
• Provides direct and accurate measurements.
• Portable and suitable for on-site testing.
The main disadvantages for this technology are:
• Requires temporary disconnection of the PV
• One measured string at a time.
This IV tracers are discussed in depth in [15], and [12].
Inverter based IV sweeping
11
==PAGE:19==
Recently, some of the services provided by inverter manufacturers are the remote scanning of the IVs. This
begins with the command initiation by the user, followed by the IV scanning of the inverter (sweeping the
voltage across each string from 0V to VOC). After retrieving and collecting the data, an algorithm compares
the shape and features, classifying every IV and detecting fault pattern [13].
The main advantages for this novel way of measure are:
• Allows fast and simultaneous measurement of many strings.
• Integrated into normal plant operation—no need to disconnect the system.
• Precision of 0.5% in current and voltage.
• Fast implementation.
But, the disadvantages are:
• Currently supported only by certain inverter platforms.
• Diagnostic resolution may be lower than that of a high-end dedicated IV tracer.
On-board IV Curve Monitoring via Power Optimizers / Module Electronics
The IV curve can be extracted by varying the load connected to the PV source and recording voltage and
current at each point to plot the I-V curve. It is commonly used in laboratories or controlled environments.
It is useful for research purposes, but time-consuming compared to the other methods [16].
2.2.3
Features of IV curves
The IV curves can supply not only a visual representation of the module/string performance, but also many
distinctive characteristics, points or regions along the curve. These are defined as features. And they give
information about the electrical behavior and thus, the health of the string. These features are obtained by
either:
• direct extraction of characteristic points from the measured IV trace (e.g. Isc, Voc, Pmpp), or
• derived calculation using the curve shape or slope (e.g. Rs, Rsh, FF, First Derivative).
Below is a detailed overview of the main I-V parameters and derived metrics.
Open-Circuit Voltage (Voc)
The open-circuit voltage, Voc, represents the maximum voltage a PV device can produce when no current
flows (I = 0). It corresponds to the rightmost point on the I-V curve. A significant drop in Voc may indicate
potential induced degradation (PID), shorted bypass diodes, or damaged modules.
Short-Circuit Current (Isc)
The short-circuit current, Isc, is the maximum current output when the terminals are shorted (V = 0). It
appears at the leftmost point on the I-V curve. It is proportional to irradiance. Low Isc typically points to
soiling, shading, or cell degradation.
Voltage at Maximum Power Point (Vmpp)
Vmpp is the voltage at which the power is maximized, indicating the point of peak power delivery. This value
is lower than Voc and is affected by temperature and internal resistance. Vmpp is also sensitive to temperature
and series resistance.
Current at Maximum Power Point (Impp)
Impp is the current corresponding to Vmpp, where power output is at its maximum. Reduction in Impp is
often due to shading, mismatch, or cell degradation.

2.2.4
Fault detection methods using IVs
The I-V curve encapsulates essential signatures of PV system health.
Fault detection using I-V curves
relies on identifying deviations in the curve shape or extracted features. Below are the most prominent and
production-relevant fault detection techniques.
Feature-Based Thresholding
Feature-based methods analyze deviations in key electrical parameters. Thresholds are defined for para-
meters such as:
• Series resistance increase ⇒Rs ↑, FF ↓
• Shunt resistance drop ⇒Rsh ↓, curve rounded near Isc
• Shading/mismatch ⇒irregular knee or multiple steps
Kongphet et al. [17] propose comparing characteristic I-V features (e.g., Voc, Isc, Pmpp) with healthy reference
curves generated by an analytical or hybrid model. Fault indicators are residuals between real and reference
curves.
Model-Based Residual Methods
These approaches estimate an ideal I-V curve (from single or two-diode models) and calculate residuals
between real and modeled outputs. Faults are flagged when residuals (RXid,ft) exceed thresholds [18]:
RXid,ft = ∥Xid −Xft∥,
RXid,nm = ∥Xid −Xnm∥
where:
• Xid = ideal,
• Xnm = normal,
• Xft = faulty conditions
Classification via Curve Shape Analysis
Shape-based methods categorize faults by analyzing morphological patterns in the IV curve, a strategy well-
supported both in industry standards and in scientific literature. Certain characteristic distortions in the IV
curve shape can be linked to specific fault mechanisms:
• Rounded knee ⇒degradation (e.g., increased shunt resistance or cell aging) [19].
• Flattened top ⇒partial shading or mismatch between modules [12].
• Sharp drops or steps ⇒open or broken bypass diodes [20].
These fault signatures are also documented in the IEC 62446-1 standard [21] and practical guides such as
[12]. Shape-based classification remains an effective and interpretable approach, particularly when combined
with expert knowledge or automated classification workflows.
Deep Learning Methods
Liu et al. [22] employ convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained on simulated I-V curves represent-
ing different failure modes (e.g., near/far shading, PID, cracks) to classify faults automatically without
environment correction:
14
==PAGE:22==
• Trained on synthetic I-V data
• No irradiance/temperature input required (normalized curves)
• Suitable for smart inverter deployment
Hybrid Approaches
Combining model-based and data-driven techniques enhances robustness:
• Use of analytical models to generate reference curves (hybrid modeling)
• Extraction of features (Vmpp/Voc, Impp/Isc) as Machine Learning (ML) input
• Transfer learning on field data improves generalization [22]
In addition to these techniques, signal processing approaches such as wavelet transform, Fourier analysis,
and principal component analysis (PCA) have been used to extract features from I-V curves that may be
imperceptible through visual inspection alone. Wavelet transform, in particular, allows for multi-resolution
analysis of the I-V curve to detect transient or localized faults. Fourier analysis reveals hidden frequency
components associated with electrical noise or instability, while PCA can highlight variance in the data
linked to fault conditions. These methods serve as complementary tools to traditional electrical parameter
thresholds and are especially effective when integrated with machine learning models [23].

Microcracks were induced in PV modules by controlled mechanical stress testing [25]. A total of 8 mod-
ules were subjected to mechanical stress by having an operator kneel, walk, or hop on the modules. This
approach was used to simulate real-world mechanical stress events, such as transport, installation, or wind-

Following the stress tests, electroluminescence (EL) imaging confirmed the presence of microcracks with

In terms of electrical performance, the outcomes are:
• Isc did not show a consistent change attributable to microcracks, in agreement with prior findings that
cracks often affect Fill Factor and Pmpp more than short-circuit current.
• Pmpp decreased noticeably with increasing crack severity, both at high and low irradiance[25]. This
decrease in Pmpp was confirmed to be a useful electrical indicator of microcrack presence.

String 7.3: Ribbon damage and Short Circuit, Open Circuit Bypass Diodes
Ribbon damage modules were created through a controlled test procedure ([25]). Eight modules were

3.2
Explored Methodologies and fault detection strategies
With the datasets described in the previous section, the next step is to define the methodologies for fault
detection and to implement appropriate alarm strategies. The primary objective is to perform a comparative
analysis of the capabilities, strengths, and limitations of using I-V curve-based diagnostics versus traditional
MPP-based monitoring for fault detection in PV systems.
To achieve this, different fault detection approaches will be applied to both datasets. The I-V data and MPP
data will be processed and analyzed using complementary techniques, ranging from simple threshold-based
alarms to more advanced model-based and feature-based analyses. The ultimate goal is to assess the added
diagnostic value that I-V measurements can provide over conventional MPP-based approaches, particularly
in terms of fault classification capability and practical deployment considerations in large-scale PV plants
[27, 12].
3.2.1
Fault Detection Strategies Based on MPP Data
Monitoring of PV plants at utility scale has traditionally relied on analysis of Maximum Power Point (MPP)
data [9]. This is the most widespread technique because it is inherently supported by all commercial string
inverters and SCADA systems, and it can be applied cost-effectively across very large PV systems. The
primary advantage of MPP-based monitoring is its simplicity and scalability, making it suitable for systems
ranging from a few megawatts to gigawatt-scale portfolios.
Benchmark Practices in O&M
Typical O&M practices rely on a set of standardized alarms and warnings, which are configured based on
MPP and environmental data [9]. These alarms are designed to detect gross system faults (e.g., disconnected
strings, major inverter faults) and to provide early warning of potential underperformance.
Alarms: These are intended to indicate critical faults that require immediate intervention. A common
benchmark condition used in many O&M contracts is as follows (even though the set conditions can be
applied to other variables):
If, for a period of 60 minutes, the total DC current produced by a string remains below 0.1 A,
while the inverter input current exceeds 5 A, and irradiance is higher than 50 W/m2, an alarm
is triggered.
21
==PAGE:29==
An equivalent condition can also be formulated in terms of DC power, comparing string-level power to
inverter-level totals.
The explanation for this condition is that when sufficient irradiance is available, a normally functioning string
should produce a minimum expected current. A sustained failure to meet this threshold strongly suggests a
gross fault (e.g., open circuit, inverter DC input failure, disconnected string).
Warnings: In addition to alarms, O&M providers often implement performance warnings, which are not
critical but indicate possible degradation or partial faults. For example:
If a string consistently underperforms relative to other strings under similar irradiance, a warning
is triggered.
This approach allows detection of gradual degradation (e.g., soiling, PID, partial shading), which may not
trigger alarms but still impacts long-term yield.
Interpretation of MPP Features
The information available from MPP data provides useful, though limited, diagnostic insights. MPP data is
based on empirical and theoretical correlations between electrical parameters and environmental conditions
[28].
• Current vs Irradiance: A linear relationship is expected. Deviations (non-linearity, saturation, or
sudden drops) suggest faults such as shading, PID, or interconnection issues.
• Voltage vs Irradiance: Voltage typically decreases slightly with temperature and varies less with
irradiance. Significant voltage drops may indicate module degradation or high series resistance.
• Power vs Irradiance: Useful for detecting underperformance trends. A lower-than-expected power
curve suggests losses due to various factors (soiling, mismatch, electrical faults).
The main advantages of MPP-based fault detection are its seamless integration with SCADA and inverter
platforms, its low-cost implementation across large PV fleets, and its ability to enable fast detection of gross
faults, such as disconnected strings or inverter DC input failures. However, this system also has important
limitations.
It provides limited diagnostic depth and cannot accurately classify specific fault types.
Its
sensitivity to subtle degradation mechanisms, such as partial shading, early-stage PID, or minor mismatch
is relatively poor. Moreover, the interpretation of MPP data can be mistaken by environmental variability,
especially changes in irradiance and temperature, which complicate the task of distinguishing true faults
from normal operational fluctuations.
In this project, feature thresholding and comparison between strings are applied to MPP data from the DTU
test array to establish a baseline for fault detection performance. The results of this analysis will serve as a
reference when evaluating the added value of I-V curve-based diagnostics in the subsequent section.
3.2.2
I-V Fault Detection Methodology
I-V curve analysis offers a more detailed view of PV string performance than conventional MPP monitoring,
as discussed in Section 2.2.3. The I-V curve provides access to multiple electrical parameters (features)
that can be compared across strings or against expected values. In this project, two main fault detection
strategies are applied: comparison of extracted I-V features across strings and statistical comparison between
measured I-V curves and modeled I-V curves.
A widely used modeling approach is based on the Single Diode Model (SDM), which describes the I-V
behavior of a PV cell or module as an equivalent circuit with a current source, diode, series resistance, and
shunt resistance. The DeSoto model is a well-known parameterization of the SDM that incorporates the
effects of temperature and irradiance on key parameters [29]. In this project, the DeSoto model is used to
generate reference I-V curves for comparison with measured data.

In this section, the methodologies described in Section 3.2 were applied to real-world data in order to assess

For the MPP analysis, several techniques were used to detect abnormal behavior at the string level in

4.2
Fault detection methods for IV
As described in Section 3.2.2, one of the main strategies for identifying faults in string-level IV curves is
to compare measured feature values under clear-sky conditions with simulated features generated using the
DeSoto model under the same environmental inputs. This approach enables the establishment of consist-
ent, data-driven thresholds that help isolate significant deviations while minimizing false detections due to
measurement uncertainty or varying irradiance.
In the following analysis, we first apply this method to clear-sky days to calculate the mean bias error (MBE)
between measured and modeled features. These results serve as a reference baseline. Then, we extend the
same calculations to the full DTU IV dataset to evaluate how the presence of faults and non-ideal conditions
affect the diagnostic indicators. This comparison highlights the robustness and limitations of the IV-based
method when deployed across broader operating conditions.

Conclusions
This thesis studied how smart IV curve diagnostics can improve operation and maintenance (O&M) proced-
ures in large-scale photovoltaic (PV) plants. The traditional approach for monitoring is based on MPP data,
which is easy to collect and already available in most plants. However, it has limitations when it comes to
detecting complex or early-stage faults. The goal of this project was to compare both MPP and IV-based
methods using real data from a test facility at DTU and from two utility-scale solar parks, and to provide
practical insights for future O&M strategies.
One of the main findings is that MPP monitoring is useful for detecting large faults like a disconnected string
or inverter shutdown. These are usually caught through simple alarms based on low current or power during
high irradiance periods. This method is fast and works well as a first-level check. But, as seen in the results,
MPP data cannot give enough information to know the root cause of the problem. It cannot distinguish
between different fault types like PID, shading, soiling, or cracked modules.
IV curve analysis, on the other hand, gives a much deeper view of the electrical behavior of each string. In
this thesis, the IV curves were measured using both field IV tracers (at DTU) and remote inverter scans
(in the utility-scale parks). The curves were analyzed by extracting key features such as Imp, Isc, V oc,
V mp, fill factor (FF), series and shunt resistance (Rs and Rsh), and the first derivative, and comparing
them to expected values from the DeSoto model. This made it possible to detect not just whether a string
was underperforming, but why. For example, a high Rs can point to ribbon damage, while a low Rsh may
indicate PID or microcracks.
The case study at DTU showed clearly that IV features could separate healthy and faulty strings. The string
with PID and cracks showed a loss in fill factor and current, especially at low irradiance, which matches the
behavior described in the literature. The string with short/open circuit diodes and ribbon damage showed
reduced voltage and power output, and this was also reflected in the residuals when compared to the model.
In contrast, these faults were hard to detect using only the MPP data.
In the utility-scale solar parks, IV measurements were taken remotely with Huawei inverters. The strings
were analyzed using residuals over the features, grouped by transformer. This approach helped to identify
outliers in large datasets and showed that several strings had performance anomalies likely caused by shading,
damaged modules, or disconnections. Some strings showed multiple outliers across voltage, power, and FF,
and these often matched with low energy production in the MPP data. In some transformers, groups of
faulty strings were found together, which might be linked to site-specific issues like vegetation or terrain.
The IV data, even though it was limited to a single day, was still very useful in finding these patterns.
Another important result is that IV curves do not always need to be measured under perfect clear-sky
conditions. This is important for practical O&M workflows because it means IV scans can be scheduled
more flexibly and used more often.
However, IV-based diagnostics also have some limitations. They require more complex processing and data
handling, and a good physical model of the string behavior.
They also need some level of expertise to
interpret the results correctly, especially when multiple faults are present at the same time. In addition, not
all inverters support IV scanning yet, although more manufacturers are starting to offer this feature.
In general, the conclusion of this thesis is that IV curve analysis is a valuable addition to existing O&M
methods. It should not replace MPP monitoring but work together with it. MPP-based alarms are still
useful for quick detection and day-to-day supervision. IV diagnostics can then be used to investigate further
when performance issues are detected, or as part of scheduled preventive maintenance. The combination
of both methods leads to better fault detection, reduced need for field inspections, and better planning of
maintenance actions.
From a practical point of view, plant operators should consider integrating IV scans into their regular
monitoring routines, especially in large plants where on-site inspection is expensive. Thresholds for each IV
feature should be set based on reference models, statistical results from nearby strings or known healthy
strings. Diagnostic tools should include automatic residual analysis and feature comparison. In the future,
machine learning methods could help classify fault types based on IV curve shapes, but for now, feature-based
59
==PAGE:67==
and model-based methods are effective and interpretable.
All in all, IV-based diagnostics are ready to be used as a smart and scalable tool in utility-scale PV plant
O&M. They provide extra information that is not visible in MPP data and help reduce both downtime and
operational costs. Their integration into digital O&M platforms will help operators make better decisions
and ensure higher energy yield from their assets.
