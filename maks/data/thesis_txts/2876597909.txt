Abstract
Optimizing fed┬¡batch fermentations is a long┬¡standing challenge: the system is nonlinear, the un┬¡
derlying kinetics are uncertain, and every experiment is costly. This thesis explores a combined
framework of optimal control theory, non┬¡parametric modelling, and Bayesian optimization with
the objective of improving the performance of fed┬¡batch processes.
The dynamics of the fermentation were firstly analysed through an ODE model, revealing how
strongly the feed rate shapes the process performance. This motivated the use of PontryaginÔÇÖs
Maximum Principle to search for an optimal feeding profile. Because there was no suitable JAX┬¡
based solver, a custom two┬¡point boundary value solver was developed and validated, although the
fermentation problem itself proved to be as numerically unstable as reported in the literature.
To overcome the rigidity of classical kinetic models, Gaussian Processes were introduced as flexi┬¡
ble surrogates. A GP trained directly on the kinetic data accurately reconstructed the true growth
and production rates, while a GP┬¡ODE approach trained on full fermentation trajectories illustrated
both the promise and the difficulty of learning kinetics from indirect measurements.
Finally, Bayesian Optimization was used to navigate a simplified two┬¡phase feeding strategy, ef┬¡
ficiently identifying improved operating conditions. Together, these results form the first steps
toward a unified framework that combines optimal control, machine learning, and probabilistic
optimization for next┬¡generation bioprocess design.

Introduction
One of the main challenges in fermentation is optimizing the process. Every biotechnologist
dreams of producing more product in less time, ultimately reducing costs and improving efficiency.
In industrial reality, however, companies often lack the time and flexibility to systematically op┬¡
timize fermentations. Instead, they rely on practical experience, adjusting parameters based on
previous batches and maintaining conditions that ÔÇ£worked well last timeÔÇØ. In many cases, process
optimization remains an exercise in trial and error, rather than a scientific strategy.
Yet, what if the perfect ingredients for optimization are already within our fermenters? Behind
every fermentation lie mathematical equations that describe how biomass grows, substrate is con┬¡
sumed, and product is formed. These equations define a dynamical system that can be guided and
optimized using a principle developed decades ago during the Cold War: PontryaginÔÇÖs Maximum
Principle (PMP) [1]. PMP, a fundamental result in optimal control theory, provides a systematic
way to determine the best possible control, such as an optimal feed rate, that drives a system to┬¡
wards a desired goal while respecting process constraints such as maximum volume or flow rate.
In a fermentation context, this typically means optimizing the feed strategy that maximizes the
titer, yield, or rate of the desired product.
Fermentation is one of humanityÔÇÖs oldest biotechnological tools, with evidence of its use dating
back over 10,000 years [2]. In the nineteenth century, Louis Pasteur famously defined fermentation
as la vie sans airÔÇölife without air. Pasteur demonstrated that fermentation was a biological process
carried out by living microorganisms, such as yeast, in the absence of oxygen [3]. While this vie
sans air focused on the anaerobic metabolism of yeast and bacteria, modern biotechnology uses the
term fermentation more broadly. Today, fermentation refers not only to anaerobic metabolism, but
also to the industrial operation of cultivating microorganisms in bioreactors to produce biomass,
metabolites, enzymes, or recombinant proteins. Fermentation, in its current meaning, has thus
expanded from a metabolic definition to an industrial one.
Fermentation processes can be run in three classical operational modes: batch, continuous, or fed┬¡
batch. Over the past three decades, the use of fed┬¡batch reactors has grown significantly across
biotechnology, chemical, and pharmaceutical industries, owing to the significant advantages they
offer [4]. Fed┬¡batch mode is an operational technique where substrate is fed to the bioreactor
without removing the culture fluid, causing the volume of the reactor to increase over time [5].
It can be seen as an intermediate strategy between batch and continuous modes. Typically, once
the maximum reactor volume is reached, the feeding is stopped and the process continues as a
batch operation until the target conversion is achieved. The widespread adoption of fed┬¡batch
strategies stems from their primary advantage: the ability to precisely control the concentration of
key substrates in the bioreactor. This control mitigates common issues found in batch processes,
such as substrate inhibition and overflow metabolism, by maintaining the substrate at a low, optimal
concentration. Furthermore, this controlled and gradual feeding of nutrients avoids one of the main
disadvantages of continuous systems: the wash out of cells faster than they are reproduced [6]. By
manipulating the feed rate, it is possible to regulate microbial growth and metabolic activity, thus
maximizing product yield and productivity over conventional batch or continuous operations [7].
Metabolite maximization in fed┬¡batch fermentation is one of the central challenges in biochemical
engineering and has therefore received significant research interest [8]. Bioprocess optimization is
typically expressed in terms of three key performance metrics: titer, the final product concentration═¥
rate, the volumetric productivity═¥ and yield, the efficiency with which the substrate is converted

into product. Improving these metrics leads to direct economic benefitsÔÇöeither by reducing the re┬¡
quired reactor size for a given production target, which lowers capital investment, or by increasing
productivity in a fixed reactor volume, which decreases operational costs [4].
The substrate feed rate thus emerges as the natural control variable for optimizing fed┬¡batch fer┬¡
mentation, as it directly governs the concentration of the limiting substrate. However, formulating
the problem with the feed rate as the control variable yields a singular optimal control problem
that is difficult to solve [9]. According to PMP, the problem is singular because the manipulated
variable appears linearly in the mass┬¡balance equations and, therefore, in the Hamiltonian function
[8]. PMP does not bring a simple solution for this singular interval, and therefore maximizing the
Hamiltonian is not sufficient to find the optimal solution. For this reason, several techniques have
been proposed to transform the singular control problem into a non┬¡singular one. For example, Ya┬¡
mane et al. [10] used the specific growth rate as the control variable═¥ however, this approach cannot
account for physical constraints on the flow rate and is unsuitable when growth is non┬¡monotonic.
Likewise, Guthke and Knorre [11] considered substrate concentration as the manipulated variable
but disregarded physical constraints on feeding rate and volume. Since these alternative methods
present significant issues for practical implementation, the feed rate profile remains the logical
choice for the control variable. See [4][9] for a further discussion of this question.
Solving this optimization problem requires a mathematical model that accurately captures the dy┬¡
namics of the system that takes place inside the fermenter. Classical models rely on kinetic ex┬¡
pressions such as those proposed by Monod or Haldane, which assume that the forms of specific
rates of growth, substrate uptake, and product formation are perfectly known. However, this over┬¡
simplification of complex biological processes often leads to a significant Model Plant Mismatch
(MPM), making it unlikely that the optimal calculated policy will perform as expected in a real┬¡life
implementation [12].
To overcome these limitations, this project explores non┬¡parametric modelling using Gaussian Pro┬¡
cesses (GPs). Unlike traditional kinetic models, GPs do not impose fixed functional forms but
instead learn the underlying relationships directly from the data. This flexibility allows the model
to capture the nonlinear dependencies and uncertainties inherent in biological systems [13].
However, learning the kinetics is only one part of the optimization problem. The second challenge
is to efficiently search for the large, highly nonlinear space of possible feed profiles. Exhaustively
testing all candidates, either experimentally or through repeated dynamic simulations, is expensive.
Bayesian Optimisation (BO) provides an elegant solution to this issue. By combining a probabilis┬¡
tic surrogate model with an acquisition function that strategically selects the next most informative
experiment, BO identifies promising operating conditions with few evaluations [14]. In a fermen┬¡
tation context, this means that the Gaussian Process (GP) not only models the kinetics, but also
can drive a data┬¡efficient search for improved feeding strategies.
By combining the adaptability of GPs, the exploration of Bayesian Optimisation, and the rigor of
PMP, we propose a hybrid optimization framework capable of learning kinetic behaviour from
data and identifying optimal feeding strategies for various performance objectives. The culmina┬¡
tion of this research is the iterative optimisation process (Figure 1.1). This cyclical methodology
transforms the traditional trial┬¡error strategy into a rigorous, data┬¡driven workflow. First, a fer┬¡
mentation is run and state measurements are collected. These data are then used to update the
non┬¡parametric surrogate model, which learns and refines the underlying kinetics of the system.
BO is used in parallel to efficiently explore promising regions of the feeding strategy space. Fi┬¡
nally, the GP┬¡based kinetic model is embedded in the PMP framework to compute the next optimal
feed profile, which is then applied to the process. By repeating this loop, the system gradually con┬¡
verges toward increasingly efficient operating policies.

2
Theoretical Framework
This chapter presents the theoretical foundations of the project. It is divided into two main parts: (1)
the formulation of the optimal control problem for fed┬¡batch fermentation using PontryaginÔÇÖs Max┬¡
imum Principle, and (2) the use of Gaussian processes to model specific rates as non┬¡parametric
functions, together with the Bayesian tools needed to train them and employ them for decision┬¡
making.
First, a fed┬¡batch fermentation is expressed as a system of Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs)
that describes how each component of the process evolves over time. Keeping in mind that our
main goal is to find the optimal feed rate, PMP is implemented to provide a set of necessary con┬¡
ditions for the solution to be optimal. The principle requires an optimal control and trajectory to
satisfy a Hamiltonian system, which is a two┬¡point boundary value problem, and a condition that
the control Hamiltonian is maximized. When the Hamiltonian is defined, it is evident that our
control variable, the feed rate, appears linearly with respect to the Hamiltonian. This fact leads to
a singular control problem that is difficult to solve. The Hamiltonian is formed by one coefficient
that does not affect the feed rate and another coefficient that multiplies the feed rate. This second
coefficient is known as the switching function and will dictate the feed rate values at every point.
As we are dealing with a singular control problem, PMP does not give us a simple solution for the
case where the switching function hits 0, that is, when we enter the singular interval. However,
the singular feed was obtained by sequentially differentiating the switching function until the feed
rate explicitly appears. All in all, we ended up knowing how the feed rate should behave at each
time point in order to be optimal, thus obtaining an optimal feed profile.
Regarding the modelling of specific rates as non┬¡parametric models, Gaussian Processes were used.
In this chapter, the basis of Bayesian inference is presented. Gaussian processes will serve us to
define a prior distribution or belief over functions, allowing the incorporation of observed data that
would be our fermentation measurements. This incorporation will update our belief of our specific
rates, making it possible to model the relationships between specific rates and fermenter conditions
without specifying an explicitly parametric form for these relationships. The GP is described by
the mean and kernel functions, which define the space of functions that the GP considers plausible.
However, the GP depends on the kernelÔÇÖs hyperparameters that must be inferred from the data.
To accomplish this, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are introduced as a Bayesian
tool for sampling from the posterior distribution of these hyperparameters, enabling uncertainty
quantification of the non┬¡parametric rate functions. Finally, BO is presented as a framework that
uses GP as a surrogate model to guide the selection of new experiments or control policies.
Together, these two theoretical pillars provide the foundation for the iterative optimisation method┬¡
ology developed in this thesis.

2.2
Optimization by PontryaginÔÇÖs Maximum Principle
PMP is a fundamental tool in optimal control theory, providing the necessary conditions to deter┬¡
mine the best possible control strategy that most effectively guides a dynamical system from one
state to another [15]. Conceptually, PMP extends the ideas of variational calculus, which seek
functions that extremize (minimize or maximize) a given functional, by introducing control inputs
and enforcing the dynamics of the system through adjoint (or costate) variables. Developed in
1956 by Lev Pontryagin and his students, the principle first gained fame through its application
to rocket optimization problems, such as maximizing terminal velocity under strict fuel and thrust
constraints [16].
In the context of fed┬¡batch process described in Chapter 2, PMP offers a rigorous framework for
dynamic optimization: it involves the definition of a performance index to be maximized, the
identification of the control variables, and the incorporation of system constraints.

2.3.1
Simple Shooting Method
In this method, the missing boundary conditions are guessed: either the initial conditions on the
adjoint vector ╬╗(0) or the final conditions on the state variables x(tf). By doing so, we will have
either the initial values or final values for both the state and adjoint variables [4].
We can consider the case of guessing the initial conditions on the adjoint variables and integrate
forward from 0 to tf the state and adjoint differential equations (Figure 2.2 a). If the calculated
values are in agreement with the given values, the guessed values are correct and the resulting
feed rate is the optimal. If the values do not match, one must iterate on the initial values of the
adjoint vector. We will need to iterate on the initial values of the adjoint vector until the final value
calculated of the adjoint by integration agrees with the final conditions given by PMP.
However, we should consider that it is difficult to guess reasonable initial values of the adjoint
variables since no physical insight is available on them [9]. For that reason, we could also guess
the final values of the states and integrate backward in time from tf to 0 (Figure 2.2 b). Following
the same philosophy as in the previous case, we will need to adjust our final state guesses until the
values of the initial states agree with the state vector x0.

The multiple shooting method is an extension to the simple shooting method. This method consists
in breaking up the trajectory into equal subintervals, on each of which an initial┬¡value problem is
defined. The solutions are then adjusted in successive iterations until the boundary conditions
and continuities at the end of the subintervals are satisfied [19]. This method is typically more
successful than simple shooting. In multiple shooting, segments become shorter═¥ therefore, the
relationship between the decision variables and the objective function and constraints becomes
more linear [20]. However, it is important to note that, in this case, you need to guess the value of
states and adjoints at the beginning of each segment or shooting node. This might be delicate for
the case of adjoints, as we do not have a clear insight of how their values should evolve over time.

Gaussian Processes for Non┬¡parametric Modeling
The main challenge in process control and optimization is to build an accurate model of the process.
Traditionally, the models used for fermentation have been mechanistic models. Mechanistic (or
parametric) models are mathematical models that describe process outcomes as functions of a set
of parameters. However, such models often lead to mismatches with real process behavior and
reproducibility issues, due to the oversimplification of complex biological processes [12]. Conse┬¡
quently, different non┬¡parametric modeling methods have been applied in process manufacturing,
such as linear latent variable techniques of Principal Component Analysis (PCA), Partial Least
Squares (PLS) [21] and the non┬¡linear approach of neural networks [22]. Unlike parametric mod┬¡
els, nonparametric models do not assume a specific predefined functional form, but instead are
free to learn it from the training data [23].
Bayesian statistical approaches have recently emerged as valuable solutions to address numerous
modeling challenges within the field of process engineering [12]. Bayesian inference is a type
of statistical inference in which observations are used to update or infer the probability that a

hypothesis may be true [24]. In this way, the Bayesian framework enables a natural integration of
prior knowledge with experimental data from actual processes, which typically results in improved
performance compared to conventional non┬¡Bayesian techniques [25].
Gaussian Processes are non┬¡parametric Bayesian regression models that define a probability dis┬¡
tribution over functions, allowing them to model complex, nonlinear relationships in data while
naturally incorporating prior beliefs and quantifying prediction uncertainty [26]. When GPs are
applied to real process data, especially in high dimensional or complex scenarios, direct analytical
inference can become computationally hard. In such cases, MCMC methods are widely employed
for posterior inference in GP models, enabling efficient sampling from the posterior distribution
over functions or parameters, and providing a robust way to capture uncertainty in both prediction
and model structure [27]. GPs can also serve as surrogate models in BO to find the global optimum
of expensive black box functions, which in this context could be used to guide the selection of the
next experimental run.
In this project, GPs are developed to model growth (┬Á), substrate consumption (¤â) and product
formation (¤Ç) specific rates.
2.4.1
Bayesian inference
Statistical inference consists of using numerical data to draw conclusions or estimates of quanti┬¡
ties that cannot be directly observed [24]. Bayesian procedures are increasingly becoming popular
for building statistical models for real┬¡world problems. Bayesian inference combines information
collected from experimental data with the prior knowledge that one has before performing any
experiment. In this approach, we combine any new information available with the prior informa┬¡
tion to build the basis for the statistical procedure. In this way, we can say that Bayesian statistics
provide a natural method for updating uncertainty in the light of evidence [28].

A Markov chain is a stochastic process that satisfies the Markov property characterized as memo┬¡

By collecting thousands of these samples, we can build a histogram that empirically approximates
the posterior distribution for each hyperparameter, allowing us to find the most likely values and,
crucially, quantify our uncertainty about them.
2.4.4
Bayesian Optimization (BO)
Bayesian optimisation addresses the problem of optimising an objective function using the fewest
possible function evaluations. BO consists of two main components: a Bayesian statistical model
or surrogate model for modelling the objective function, and an acquisition function to decide
where to sample next [14]. By combining these two elements, Bayesian optimization can find the
global optimum of ÔÇ£black boxÔÇØ functions that do not have an analytical expression and are expen┬¡
sive to evaluate. This optimization is perfectly suited for biotechnology. For example, in previous
research projects in this field, this optimization method was used to find the optimal fermentation
medium that gave the best result of the performance index [30]. This is an ideal study case, as
the relationship between media components and the performance index is analytically unknown
and expensive to evaluate (e.g., all the resources and time to check all possible media component
concentrations). However, our study case is a non┬¡standard Bayesian optimization problem, as our
surrogate model is a GP┬¡ODE system, where the ODE system models the fermentation dynamics,
using GPs to simulate the kinetic rates.
In the context of Bayesian optimization, GPs are particularly well suited to serve as surrogate mod┬¡
els, especially for biological applications. Gaussian processes can learn smooth response functions,
include prior beliefs about the system, incorporate process noise in its implementation, and obtain
confidence in its predictions by associating higher uncertainty with unexplored parts of the design
space [30].
The most widely used acquisition function is Expected Improvement (EI). The EI function bal┬¡
ances exploring unknown areas and exploiting known good areas by calculating the expected value
of the improvement over the current best result [31]. The exploitation┬¡exploration trade┬¡off is possi┬¡
ble as there are two scenarios that can both produce a high EI value: (1) the input value is predicted
to be good on average (exploitation) or (2) the input value has high uncertainty: the GP thinks that
the value might be good (exploration). EI can be mathematically formulated as:

4.5
Overall conclusion on PMP┬¡Based Methods
The systematic investigation presented in Sections 4.3 and 4.4 leads to two critical conclusions:
1. Numerical intractability. Classic PMP┬¡based shooting methods, in all tested formulations,
were not a robust reliable tool for solving this fermentation optimal control problem. The
underlying 8┬¡dimentsional TPBVP is pathologically stiff and ill┬¡conditioned, causing solvers
to crash or converge to incorrect and suboptimal minimum.
2. Fundamental Model Dependency: A more profound limitation is that this entire PMP
framework is fundamentally dependent on having a perfect and pre┬¡defined analytical model
of the process kinetics (Eq. 3.3). In any real┬¡world industrial or experimental setting, such a
perfect model is never available. The true kinetics are unknown and must be inferred from
the data.
These two challenges motivate a completely different methodological philosophy. The following
sections, therefore, explore a non┬¡parametric approach using GPs. The goal of this next phase is not
to solve the stiffness problem, but to develop a method that addresses the more critical real┬¡world
problem of the model. This GP┬¡based method is designed to find an optimal control policy without
requiring prior knowledge of the underlying kinetic functions, using only sparse experimental data.

Conclusions and future directions
The goal of this project was to develop a strategy to optimize fed┬¡batch fermentations by combining
classical optimal control theory with non┬¡parametric modelling of kinetics functions, with the final
aim of including Bayesian optimization to guide the optimization process.
Preliminary analyses demonstrated that fed┬¡batch systems can be effectively modelled by using an
ODE system that describes its dynamics and that the feed rate has a strong non┬¡linear influence
on fermentation performance. This was especially relevant to justify the choice of this parameter
as the control variable. The constant feed rate case revealed that static strategies are inherently
suboptimal, and the brute force discretization further showed that optimization landscape is highly
complex, non┬¡linear, and non┬¡intuitive. Although finer time discretizations would, in principle,
approximate the optimal solution, the resulting computational burden makes PMP essential.
The goal of applying PMP was to find the feed profile that is optimal in the whole trajectory. The
application of PMP led to a two┬¡point boundary value problem, in which initial state conditions and
adjoint final conditions must be satisfied simultaneously. As no JAX┬¡compatible TPBVP solver
existed, a custom solver was successfully developed and validated using BratuÔÇÖs problem.
Several PMP┬¡based formulations were then tested on the full metabolite optimization problem
(originally described by Shin & Lim [4, 8]), using both state┬¡based and time┬¡based controls as
well as single and multiple shooting schemes. Most attempts resulted in numerical instabilities,
reflecting the intrinsic difficulties previously reported in the literature [9] [4].
However, the substrate┬¡inhibition┬¡only variant remains promising. In that reduced case, analytical
results from the literature show that the costate vector can be eliminated from the singular feed
expression, which could substantially increase numerical stability [45, 9]. Exploring this reformu┬¡
lated problem is a natural next step. Another potential avenue is to revisit the origins of the PMP
application: the GoddardÔÇÖs rocket problem, which shares structural similarities with the metabolite
optimization problem and has well┬¡established numerical solution strategies [46, 47, 16]. If solv┬¡
able within the JAX┬¡based TPBVP framework, these methods could be directly transferred to the
fermentation problem, bringing the spacecraft and bioreactors closer than one might think.
Gaussian processes were used to model the systemÔÇÖs kinetic functions. Two methods were inves┬¡
tigated: (1) direct GP regression on the kinetic┬¡rate data, which successfully recovered the under┬¡
lying kinetic functions, and (2) a GP┬¡ODE framework trained on the state trajectories from four
different feed┬¡rate experiments. The latter, more realistic approach produced a biased posterior,
indicating that the sampler became trapped in a local minimum. Longer warm┬¡ups and step size
controller could be tested to overcome this problem in the future.
Finally, a Bayesian optimization framework was implemented to guide the selection of operating
conditions in a simplified two┬¡phase feed strategy. Although successful, this approach was only
evaluated on a grid of 25 experiments. Extending the experiment count would improve the BO
