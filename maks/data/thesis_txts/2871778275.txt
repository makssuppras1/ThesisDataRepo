Abstract
This thesis presents the design, fabrication, and evaluation of a novel thermoresponsive oral drug
delivery system that addresses the limitations of current gastrointestinal therapies. The proposed
platform integrates a poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) based electrospun ﬁbrous membrane with 3D-
printed microneedles to enhance mucosal adhesion, prolong intestinal residence time, and enable
controlled drug release in response to physiological temperatures.
The ﬁbrous membrane was fabricated via electrospinning and thermally crosslinked to ensure
mechanical stability, while the microneedle frame was produced using stereolithography 3D printing
with biocompatible resins. Upon exposure to body temperature (37°C), the PNIPAM membrane
undergoes a conformational change, enabling the device to self-crimp into a tube-like structure
with omnidirectional microneedles surrounding its outer surface, which mechanically anchor and
mechanically anchor into intestinal tissue without the need for orientation-dependent attachment.
Comprehensive in vitro and ex vivo studies were conducted to assess the structural integrity,
thermoresponsive behavior, cytocompatibility, and drug release characteristics of the platform using
model drugs such as doxorubicin and curcumin. While the device demonstrated strong mucoadhesive
performance and biocompatibility, thermal crosslinking was found to limit drug release eﬃciency
which needs further optimization in future research
Despite these challenges, the ﬁndings validate the platform as a promising candidate for novel oral
delivery systems. Its tunable mechanical and thermal properties, combined with modular fabrication,
support future development for targeted drug delivery applications.

1
Introduction
1.1
Oral Drug Delivery
Oral drug delivery remains the most popular and preferred method due to its convenience: it does
not require sterilization, has a long shelf life, is relatively inexpensive to produce, and is often favored
by patients. Approximately 90% of pharmaceutical formulations for human use are administered
orally.[1],[2],[3].
After ingestion, orally administered drugs travel through the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, a muscular
tube approximately 6 meters long comprising four main regions: the esophagus, stomach, small
intestine, and large intestine (colon). The drug rapidly passes through the esophagus into the
stomach, where absorption is limited because of the small surface area and thicker mucus layer. The
rate of gastric emptying—a↵ected by dosage form, fed or fasted state, and food composition—controls
how quickly the drug enters the small intestine.[1],[4].
The small intestine, with its large surface area and rich blood supply, is the primary site for drug
absorption. Drugs cross the intestinal mucosa and enter the bloodstream via the hepatic portal vein,
where they may undergo ﬁrst-pass metabolism in the liver before reaching systemic circulation (see
Figure 1). Drugs not absorbed by the time they reach the colon are typically eliminated in feces.
Figure 1: Illustration of the small intestine highlighting the lumen. [5]
Numerous barriers restrict drug absorption along this path. The intestinal mucosal layer serves
as a protective barrier, limiting drug permeability, while the underlying epithelial layer regulates
what enters the bloodstream, often preventing large or hydrophilic molecules from passing through.
Traditional oral dosage forms, such as tablets and capsules, su↵er from limited retention time in the
intestine and poor mucoadhesion, reducing drug absorption and overall therapeutic e↵ectiveness.
1.2
Intestinal residence and mucoadhesion
To address these challenges, various strategies have been employed to enhance intestinal residence
time and mucoadhesion, including surface modiﬁcation of drug carriers and the use of microneedle
2
==PAGE:10==
arrays that physically anchor drug delivery devices into the mucosal tissue. Microneedles facilitate
drug uptake by penetrating the mucus barrier and enabling localized delivery.
However, current microneedle designs often feature needles arranged unidirectionally, requiring precise
orientation against the mucosal surface for e↵ective attachment. Some systems rely on external
magnetic ﬁelds or other forces to correctly orient the device within the dynamic GI environment,
complicating practical application.
This project proposes a novel approach that leverages the thermo-triggered self-crimping properties
of three-dimensional microneedle-ﬁber composites fabricated with PNIPAM. At physiological body
temperature, the PNIPAM-based ﬁbrous membrane undergoes a shape transformation that produces
an omnidirectional microneedle device. This self-crimping e↵ect ensures the microneedles engage
e↵ectively with the mucus regardless of the device’s initial orientation, eliminating the need for
external orientation controls.
The di↵erence between conventional oral drug delivery and this thermoresponsive system is illustrated
in Figure 2. Traditional drugs pass through the GI tract with minimal interaction with the intestinal
wall, resulting in poor adhesion and rapid transit. In contrast, the thermoresponsive system self-
crimps at body temperature, improving mucoadhesion and enabling the drug to remain longer at
the absorption site, thus enhancing therapeutic eﬃciency.
Figure 2: The di↵erence between conventional drug delivery (A) and the thermoresponsive oral
system (B) in the digestive tract. In (A), traditional oral drugs pass quickly with limited mucosal
interaction. In (B), the thermoresponsive platform self-crimps at body temperature, improving
mucoadhesion and retention time. [Illustration made in BioRender]
By integrating 3D microneedles with this thermoresponsive ﬁbrous membrane, the device combines
physical anchoring with stimulus-responsive behavior. This synergy enhances intestinal adhesion, pro-
longs residence time, and improves the bioavailability and therapeutic eﬃciency of orally administered
drugs in the complex and dynamic gastrointestinal environment.
Furthermore, the platform’s polymeric ﬁbers not only enhance adhesion to the intestinal mucus but
also enable the direct loading of sensitive drugs such as biologics like insulin and doxorubicin, or
anti-inﬂammatory agents like curcumin into electrospun ﬁbers or 3D-printed microneedles. This
approach o↵ers controlled release proﬁles, protects the drugs from degradation in the harsh gastric
environment, and improves stability during gastrointestinal transit. This structural change promoted
by PNIPAM ensures the drug remains in the intestinal lumen longer, optimizing absorption and
therapeutic outcomes. The increased residence time, facilitated by the omnidirectional microneedles
3
==PAGE:11==
anchoring the device in place, further promotes absorption by sustaining close contact with the
intestinal epithelium.
2
Project Overview
This project aims to develop and evaluate a novel oral drug delivery system with omnidirectional
microneedles that enhances intestinal residence time, improves mucosal adhesion, and sustained
drug release, ultimately increasing bioavailability and therapeutic eﬃcacy. The system integrates
3D microneedles with a thermo-responsive polymeric ﬁbrous membrane, forming a multifunctional
platform that adapts to the dynamic GI environment.
2.1
Motivation
Many drugs, especially biologics and poorly soluble compounds, face challenges such as enzymatic
degradation, poor mucosal adhesion, and limited residence time in the gastrointestinal tract, resulting
in low bioavailability.
This project aims to develop a novel oral drug delivery system that combines 3D microneedles with a
thermoresponsive PNIPAM-based ﬁbrous membrane. The device is designed to mechanically anchor
to the intestinal wall and enhance mucosal adhesion at body temperature, thereby prolonging its
residence time in the small intestine. By increasing local retention, the system promotes sustained
drug release and improved absorption of therapeutics that are otherwise poorly bioavailable via the
oral route. The primary motivation is to improve the eﬃciency of oral delivery by enhancing their
interaction with the intestinal epithelium.
2.2
Hypothesis
The integration of 3D microneedles with a PNIPAM-based thermoresponsive ﬁbrous membrane
will signiﬁcantly improve intestinal residence time and mucosal adhesion of an oral drug delivery
device. This enhanced interaction with the intestinal epithelium will promote localized and sustained
drug release, leading to increased absorption and bioavailability of therapeutic agents such as
doxorubicin (DOX), and curcumin. Furthermore, the platform will demonstrate structural stability,
biocompatibility, and scalability, supporting its potential as a broadly applicable oral delivery system
for biologics and poorly soluble drugs.
2.3
Research Aim
The aim of this Master’s thesis is to develop and characterize a novel thermoresponsive ﬁbrous drug
delivery platform for oral administration by integrating electrospun PNIPAM-based membranes with
a 3D-printed microﬁbrous frame. This platform is designed to respond to physiological temperature
changes, enhancing mucoadhesion and enabling controlled drug release in the intestinal tract. The
project will involve the fabrication and optimization of the thermoresponsive membrane, structural
and mechanical characterization, drug loading and release studies, as well as in vitro and ex vivo
biocompatibility and performance assessments, with the ultimate goal of improving oral drug
bioavailability and therapeutic eﬃcacy.
An overview of the fabrication and integration process is shown in Figure 3. The process begins
with the preparation of the polymer solution with crosslinker and catalyst, where the materials are
4
==PAGE:12==
dissolved in organic solvents like dimethylformamide (DMF) and tetrahydrofuran (THF). As the
solvents are mixed, a uniform solution composite is formed and loaded into a syringe. Electrospinning
is then used to produce ﬁbrous membranes, which are subsequently cross-linked in an oven to stabilize
their structure.
In parallel, microstructures are designed using SolidWorks and fabricated using an stereolithography
(SLA) 3D printer. After printing, the parts are cleaned using a sonicator bath to remove excess
resin, followed by UV curing to fully solidify the printed microstructures. Finally, the 3D-printed
microframe and the electrospun polymer membrane are integrated to create the complete functional
system, as illustrated below in Figure 3.
Figure 3: Schematic of the fabrication and integration process. Polymer solution is prepared,
electrospun into ﬁbrous membranes, and thermally crosslinked. Simultaneously, microstructures
are designed in SolidWorks, 3D printed via SLA, cleaned in a sonicator, and UV cured. The ﬁnal
membrane is then integrated with the printed microframe.
The integrated polymer membrane is aligned and punched using a 0.6mm diameter tool to match
the features of the 3D-printed frame. Once aligned, the polymer membrane and printed structure
form a single drug delivery system. Due to the thermoresponsive properties of the polymer, the
integrated system can be self-crimp to form the ﬁnal geometry of the drug delivery device. The ﬁnal
assembly process is illustrated in Figure 4..
Figure 4: Final integration of the polymer membrane with the 3D-printed frame. The membrane
is punched using a 0.6mm biopsy punch, aligned, and bonded to the printed microstructure. The
structure is then rolled to create the complete drug delivery system.

Experimental Setup
The following subsections provide a brief overview of the equipment and materials used throughout
the project.
3.1
Electrospinning
Electrospinning is a technique which is used for producing ultraﬁne ﬁbers from a wide range of
natural and synthetic polymers. The process relies on the application of a high voltage electric
ﬁeld to a polymer solution, which is ejected through a small nozzle (needle) towards a grounded
collector. As the charged polymer jet travels through the air, the solvent evaporates and solid ﬁbers
are deposited on the collector. These ﬁbers will then form a non-woven ﬁber mat with diameters
ranging from nanometers to a few micrometers.
It is widely applied in drug delivery systems, where it enables controlled and targeted release of
therapeutic agents. This controlled release is possible because drugs can be encapsulated within the
ﬁbers, and the polymer matrix can be engineered to degrade or swell over time, allowing gradual
di↵usion of the drug. Additionally, the high surface area-to-volume ratio of electrospun ﬁbers
enhances interaction with the surrounding environment, making release kinetics tunable by adjusting
ﬁber composition, structure, and morphology. This versatility allows for sustained, localized, or
stimuli-responsive drug delivery.[6],[7].
A typical electrospinning setup consists of four main components: a high-voltage power supply, a
syringe pump, a syringe with a spinneret (blunt-tip), and a ﬁber collector, as illustrated in Figure
2. In this setup, a polymer solution prepared with a volatile solvent is ejected from the syringe at
a controlled rate while subjected to a strong electric ﬁeld. The applied voltage induces a charge
within the polymer liquid, causing the droplet at the needle tip to elongate into a conical structure
known as Taylor’s cone. When the electrostatic repulsion surpasses the surface tension forces, a ﬁne
jet is ejected from the cone tip, initiating ﬁber formation.
Figure 5: Electrospinning setup and jet formation process, highlighting key stages from Taylor cone
initiation to ﬁber deposition on the collector. [Illustration made in BioRender]
6
==PAGE:14==
The morphology and quality of the resulting ﬁbers depend strongly on the delicate balance between
ﬂow rate, applied voltage, and the distance between the spinneret and collector. Incorrect tuning may
lead to bead formation or irregular ﬁbers, while optimized parameters ensure uniform, continuous
ﬁber production. As the polymer jet travels toward the collector, rapid solvent evaporation combined
with bending instabilities leads to the thinning and solidiﬁcation of the ﬁbers, as depicted in the
sequential stages in Figure 5.
Typically, ﬁbers are randomly deposited onto the grounded collector surface. However, for certain
specialized applications, aligned ﬁber structures are required. This can be achieved through the use
of rotating spin collector, or by introducing auxiliary electrodes to manipulate the electric ﬁeld and
minimize jet instability, enhancing ﬁber alignment along a preferred direction.[8],[9].
3.2
PNIPAM
PNIPAM is a thermoresponsive polymer known for its lower critical solution temperature around
32°C, which aligns closely with human body temperature. Below this threshold, PNIPAM remains
hydrophilic and water soluble, while above it, the polymer becomes hydrophobic and collapses. This
unique temperature-dependent behavior makes PNIPAM highly suitable for biomedical applications
such as controlled drug delivery systems.
Despite its advantages, PNIPAM lacks suﬃcient mechanical strength and environmental stability
on its own, particularly in the harsh conditions of the gastrointestinal tract. To overcome these
challenges, PNIPAM must be combined with other components. The inclusion of a crosslinking
agent such as Open-structured Polyhedral Oligomeric Silsesquioxane (OpePOSS) enhances the
polymer’s mechanical robustness and stability in aqueous environment. In addition, a catalyst like
2-Ethyl-4-methylimidazolium acetate (EMI) is introduced to facilitate e↵ective crosslinking under
moderate thermal conditions, making the ﬁnal material more durable and functional in biological
environments.[10]
With its temperature-responsive behavior and improved mechanical properties through crosslinking,
PNIPAM becomes a strong candidate for nanoﬁber fabrication. These functionalized microﬁbers
will be produced using the electrospinning process, which is discussed in the next section[11].
3.3
DOX and Curcumin
DOX and Curcumin are two widely used drugs in the treatment of cancer and inﬂammation. DOX
is a widely used chemotherapeutic agent e↵ective against various cancers, including breast, ovarian,
and lung cancer. Curcumin, a curcuminoid derived from turmeric, possesses anti-inﬂammatory,
antioxidant, and anticancer properties. These pharmacologically active compounds are well-known
globally due to their therapeutic properties and widespread use in treating cancer and inﬂammatory
diseases.[12]
Despite its potential, curcumin faces challenges related to solubility and bioavailability, which limit
its clinical application. These limitations hinder its e↵ectiveness in treating certain conditions,
making research into improving its delivery mechanisms a crucial focus for enhancing its therapeutic
potential.[13],[14]
Electrospun microﬁbers have emerged as a versatile platform for delivering both DOX and Curcumin.
The unique characteristics of electrospun microﬁbers — such as high surface area-to-volume ratio,
tunable porosity, and the ability to encapsulate drugs within biodegradable polymers — make them
ideal carriers for controlled and sustained drug release. Several studies have reported successful
loading of DOX into electrospun microﬁbers, which enhances targeted delivery to tumor sites while
7
==PAGE:15==
reducing systemic toxicity. Similarly, Curcumin-loaded electrospun microﬁbers have been developed
to improve its solubility and protect it from rapid degradation, thereby enhancing bioavailability
and therapeutic eﬃcacy.[15],[16]
Additionally, electrospun microﬁbers can be used to load di↵erent types of drugs, such as DOX
or Curcumin, within the same ﬁbrous system. This allows ﬂexibility in designing drug delivery
platforms depending on the therapeutic need. These ﬁbers can help achieve controlled release, reduce
side e↵ects, and improve patient compliance by adjusting the system to match the speciﬁc drug’s
properties and mode of action.
Overall, the use of electrospun microﬁbers as drug delivery vehicles for DOX and Curcumin represents
a promising strategy to overcome the limitations of conventional formulations and improve clinical
outcomes.
3.4
3D Printing in Drug Delivery Systems
3D printing is a technique that constructs three-dimensional objects layer by layer from a digital
model, typically created with computer-aided design (CAD) software. In biomedical research, 3D
printing o↵ers unprecedented control over the fabrication of complex structures such as tissue
sca↵olds and drug delivery systems, making it a valuable tool in developing patient-speciﬁc therapies.
3D printing is increasingly used in the biomedical ﬁeld, especially for drug delivery systems. It
allows for precise control over the structure and release proﬁle of drug-loaded materials, enabling
customized designs tailored for speciﬁc therapeutic applications.
Di↵erent 3D printing technologies are available on the market, each suited for di↵erent applications
and materials.[17]
3.4.1
Fused Deposition Modeling
Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) is one of the most accessible and cost-e↵ective methods. It
involves the extrusion of thermoplastic ﬁlaments such as PLA, ABS, or PETG through a heated
nozzle, which deposits material layer by layer. While FDM is easy to use and relatively inexpensive,
it has limitations in resolution and surface quality. Moreover, its compatibility with biocompatible
materials is restricted, which limits its applicability in drug delivery and tissue engineering.[18]
3.4.2
Stereolithography (SLA)
SLA is one of the oldest and most widely used 3D printing methods, known for its high printing
resolution and ability to produce complex geometries with smooth surfaces. SLA uses a laser
projector to focus a laser beam on a liquid resin, photopolymerizing it layer by layer to form a 3D
structure.
The resolution of SLA is determined by the diameter of the laser beam. A laser directs light to two
mirror galvanometers, which move in the X and Y planes to focus the light onto the resin, curing it
layer by layer. The printing time for SLA is longer due to the limited surface area of resin exposed to
the laser beam and the need for layer-by-layer printing [19]. For this project, the FormLabs 3B/3B+
3D printer is used, which employs SLA technology to create high-resolution 3D printed structures.
8
==PAGE:16==
3.4.3
Selective Laser Sintering (SLS)
SLS employs a laser to fuse powdered materials, such as polymers or metals, into solid structures. It
o↵ers good mechanical strength and does not require support structures during printing. Nevertheless,
it is expensive and has limited availability of FDA-approved biomaterials, which restricts its use in
direct biomedical applications.[20]
3.4.4
Inkjet bioprinting
Inkjet bioprinting is another emerging technology that prints droplets of bioinks containing living
cells. This technique excels at printing soft hydrogels and biological structures with high precision.
Despite its advantages in tissue engineering, it su↵ers from low mechanical strength of the printed
constructs and demands complex optimization of the bioink formulations.[21]

on the need for high-resolution sca↵olds. SLA technology provides access to resins that are both

the surface ﬁnish and dimensional accuracy of SLA-printed parts make them ideal for integration
with electrospun ﬁbers, ensuring better control over drug release kinetics. Compared to FDM and
other methods, SLA o↵ers superior performance for biomedical applications, particularly in cancer

3.6.1
Optical Microscopy
Optical microscopy is a widely used imaging technique that utilizes visible light and a system of
lenses to magnify and visualize small-scale structures. In this study, two di↵erent types of optical
microscopy were employed. A reﬂected light optical microscope was used in the IDUN lab for
imaging the surface morphology of electrospun membranes. This technique illuminates the sample
from above and captures light reﬂected from the surface, making it suitable for non-transparent
materials. In contrast, a transmitted light (brightﬁeld) microscope was used in the cell
lab, where light passes through the sample to generate contrast based on di↵erences in sample
thickness and density. This setup allowed for the visualization of cell shape, distribution, and overall
morphology, providing qualitative information about the cultured CaCo-2 and HeLa cells.[24].
3.6.2
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
SEM is a powerful imaging technique used to study the surface structure of materials at high
magniﬁcation and resolution. Unlike traditional optical microscopes, which are limited to resolving
objects larger than approximately 200 µm, SEM can achieve much higher resolution. In this project,
a table-top SEM available in the IDUN labs was used, which is capable of achieving resolutions
down to 10 nm and magniﬁcations of up to 1 ⇥105, enabling detailed visualization of micro- and
nanoscale surface features.[25].
Figure 10: Schematic illustration of the basic components and working principle of a SEM. Electrons
emitted from an electron gun are focused onto the sample, and secondary electrons generated from
the surface are detected to form a detailed image.[26]
The working principle of SEM involves an electron gun that emits a beam of electrons onto the
surface of a sample. This electron beam is focused through a series of lenses and apertures, ensuring
that it remains highly concentrated as it scans the sample (see Figure 10). The scanning process
12
==PAGE:20==
occurs within a vacuum environment to prevent the electron beam from interacting with molecules
or atoms in the air, which could otherwise degrade the image quality.
As the electron beam interacts with the sample’s surface, various signals are generated, including
secondary electrons, backscattered electrons, and X-rays. These signals provide valuable information
about the sample’s surface morphology, composition, and structure. The detected signals are then
processed by the microscope’s detectors and converted into high-resolution images displayed on a
computer screen.[25].
SEM is especially useful for analyzing the topography, size, and chemical composition of materials,
making it an essential tool in ﬁelds such as material science, biology, and nanotechnology.[27].
3.6.3
Coherent Raman Scattering Microscopy (CARS)
CARS microscopy (see Figure 11) is an advanced, imaging technique that allows detailed chemical
imaging of biological and material samples. It operates by using two or more laser beams—typically a
pump beam and a Stokes beam—which interact with the sample. When the energy di↵erence between
these beams matches a speciﬁc vibrational mode of the target molecules, a coherent anti-Stokes
signal is generated. This anti-Stokes signal, which has a shorter wavelength (higher energy) than the
excitation beams, is strong, directional, and allows for high-contrast imaging with excellent spatial
resolution.
CARS is particularly useful for visualizing molecules such as lipids and proteins within cells and
tissues without the need for ﬂuorescent dyes or chemical labels, making it highly suitable for studying
live biological samples. In this study, a combined CARS and confocal ﬂuorescence microscopy
setup was used. While CARS was employed to examine the chemical composition and structure of
the samples, the integrated confocal ﬂuorescence function enabled live/dead cell imaging through
conventional ﬂuorescence staining. This dual capability allowed us to correlate structural and
biochemical information with cellular viability, making the technique highly valuable for assessing
both material properties and biological responses in the context of our drug delivery system.

Material and methods
This section outlines the materials, fabrication processes, and characterization techniques used to
develop a thermo-responsive oral drug delivery system. As the delivery system are made of di↵erent
layers, this section will focus on each layer individual.
4.1
Electrospinning and ﬁbrous membrane characterization
The polymer PNIPAM has an average molecule weight (Mv) ⇡40,000 Da and was obtained from
Polysciences, Inc. DMF and THF were readily available in the laboratory for general use and were
used without any further puriﬁcation. OpePOSS were supplied by Hybrid Plastics and EMI served
as crosslinker and catalyst, respectively.
In order to ensure the fabrication of a consistent and high-quality material, a number of carefully
controlled processes were employed in the preparation of the microﬁber membrane.
Prior to
electrospinning, a polymer solution was prepared as the ﬁrst step in the process. The electrospinning
parameters were optimized to achieve the desired morphological and mechanical properties of the
resulting ﬁbers.
A polymer solution was prepared by dissolving PNIPAM in a 1:1 (v/v) mixture of DMF and THF at
45% w/v. OpePOSS and EMI were added as crosslinker and catalyst, respectively, in a weight ratio
of 100:20:0.3 relative to PNIPAM. To ensure homogeneity, PNIPAM was gradually added under
continuous stirring at room temperature overnight until fully dissolved.
15
==PAGE:23==
For drug loading, DOX was incorporated into the polymer solution at a concentration of 0.22 mg/mL,
resulting in a total of 1.1mg DOX in 5mL of solution. The precise calculations for the solution
can be found in Appendix B. The drug was then added after complete dissolution of the polymer
components and stirred until uniformly dispersed. To avoid clumping, the polymer was introduced
to the solvent gradually under continuous stirring. The stirring was maintained at room temperature
for several hours, as determined by the polymer’s solubility characteristics.
Once the polymer and DOX were fully dissolved into a homogeneous solution, the mixture was
transferred into airtight plastic syringe ﬁtted with a 20-gauge needle. The syringe was then mounted
onto a custom-built electrospinning setup, as shown in Figure 11, and connected to a syringe pump
programmed to dispense the solution at a controlled ﬂow rate.
With the syringe in place, electrospinning was carried out under optimized conditions based on
literature guidelines [7]. An appropiate voltage was applied between the needle and the collector,
generating an electric ﬁeld that induced the formation of a stable Taylor cone and a continuous
polymer jet. As the jet traveled toward the collector, solvent evaporation resulted in the deposition
of solid microﬁbers.
The tip-to-collector distance (working distance) was maintained at a distance to ensure e↵ective
solvent evaporation and ﬁber formation. Depending on the intended ﬁber orientation, either a
stationary ﬂat collector (Figure 14A) was used for randomly oriented ﬁbers, or a rotating drum
collector set at a speed of 75 RPM (Figure 14B) was used to produce aligned ﬁbers.
Figure 14: Prototype electrospinning setup used in the experiment. (A) System conﬁgured with
a ﬂat stationary collector for membrane formation. (B) System conﬁgured with a rotating drum
collector to produce aligned microﬁbers. Both setups utilize a syringe pump to control the ﬂow rate
of the polymer solution.

Post-Processing of Nanoﬁber Membrane
Following electrospinning, the microﬁber membrane was carefully removed from the collector and
air-dried under a fume hood to ensure complete removal of any residual solvent. To further enhance
its mechanical stability and robustness, the membrane was subjected to thermal treatment between
120–160°C. This heat treatment facilitated relaxation of the polymer chains and initiated crosslinking
within the ﬁbers, thereby strengthening the membrane.

LDH Assay
Another assay performed was the LDH assay, which is useful because it is simple, sensitive, and
provides clear data on cell viability and toxicity. It works by detecting LDH, an enzyme released

that LDH, an intracellular enzyme, is released into the surrounding medium upon damage or lysis of
the cell membrane.

resulting NADH can be detected spectrophotometrically by measuring the absorbance at 490 nm,
providing an indirect measure of cell damage.[30].

4.4
Dox drug release
To evaluate the drug release proﬁle of DOX-loaded ﬁbers, ﬁve individual samples were prepared.
Each sample consisted of approximately 0.0200 g of membrane ﬁber immersed in 1 mL of phosphate-
bu↵ered saline (PBS) (see Figure 18A). The experiment was conducted at 37°C using a water bath to
simulate physiological temperature, as illustrated in Figure 18B . The experiment was also performed
at room temperature using samples of the same weight.
At predetermined time points, 200µL of PBS was withdrawn from each sample and immediately
replaced with an equal volume of fresh PBS. This approach ensured the maintenance of sink
conditions, a state in which the concentration of the drug in the release medium remains signiﬁcantly
below its saturation solubility. Maintaining sink conditions is essential for accurately assessing drug
release, as it prevents saturation of the medium and allows continuous di↵usion of the drug from the
membrane matrix.
Sampling was performed every 15 minutes during the ﬁrst 3 hours, followed by hourly sampling at
4 and 5 hours, and a ﬁnal sample was taken at 24 hours. Each collected aliquot was transferred
to a microplate and analyzed using a microplate reader. Absorbance was measured at 480 nm, or
ﬂuorescence readings were recorded in the 480–590 nm range to assess drug release over time.

4.6
Integration of polymer membrane with 3D-printed microneedles
The idea of using a biocompatible double-sided tape, speciﬁcally a pressure-sensitive adhesive layer
(PSA) [31], was implemented to enable seamless integration with the 3D-printed structure. To
ensure a precise ﬁt between the tape and the structure, the cutting process was carefully planned
and executed. The exact dimensions and shape required were derived directly from the SolidWorks
sketch used to design the component. This approach guaranteed that the tape would perfectly match
the geometry of the structure, maintaining a 100% dimensional correlation.
The double-sided tape used was a pressure-sensitive adhesive layer (PSA) (ARcare 90106, Adhesive
Research, Limerick, Ireland). Using the digital sketch, the design was transferred to the plotter
cutting machine, which then cut the PSA layer accordingly. This method ensured that the tape
followed the exact contours of the 3D-printed piece, eliminating any potential misalignment or excess
material.
The polymer membrane was then carefully positioned and adhered onto the PSA layer already
attached to the 3D-printed structure. Once secured in place, any excess membrane material was
trimmed o↵using scissors to ensure a precise ﬁt and a clean ﬁnish.
Finally, the entire assembly was inspected to conﬁrm proper alignment and bonding between all
layers, resulting in a robust and seamless integration.

Ex vivo experiment with animal tissue
The experimental setup, shown in Figure 23, consists of a metal plate onto which a 24 cm section of
pig intestine is mounted. The intestine is carefully incised and opened to expose the inner mucosal
layer, allowing for direct observation and interaction with the mucus surface.
Water is introduced into the system using a syringe connected to a pump, which controls the ﬂow
rate and pressure of the water through the sample area. In this setup, a constant water ﬂow of
20 mL/min was applied, and the syringe used had an inner diameter of 29 mm.
To investigate interactions with the mucosal layer, various 3D-printed microstructures are applied to
the surface. These microstructures include plain microﬁbers as well as microﬁbers embedded with
microneedles of varying heights:
• 0 mm (blank microﬁber, no microneedles)
• 0.3 mm microneedles
• 0.6 mm microneedles
• 0.75 mm microneedles
These microneedles are designed to interact mechanically with the mucus layer. In particular,
structures with microneedles tend to exhibit stronger adhesion to the mucus surface, potentially
enhancing residence time and mechanical stability compared to smooth microﬁbers.

Results & Discussion
This section presents the experimental ﬁndings obtained from the development and characterization of
the thermo-responsive oral drug delivery system, followed by a critical discussion of their implications.
The results are organized according to the main objectives of the study, including membrane
fabrication and characterization, 3D-print integration, cytotoxicity evaluation, and drug release

Each section begins with a description of the key results, supported by relevant ﬁgures and tables,

5.1
Polymer Membrane and Electrospinning
The electrospinning process was initially performed using a voltage of 12 kV, a tip-to-collector
distance of 14 cm, and an infusion rate of 1 ml/h, as recommended by several relevant literature
sources [7]. This setup resulted in the formation of a signiﬁcant number of beads, as shown in
Figure 24A, indicating that the chosen parameters were not optimal. To reduce bead formation,
experiments were conducted with varying voltages, infusion rates, and distances. Based on the
results, it was concluded that a voltage of 7.5 kV, a distance of 14 cm, and an infusion rate of 1
ml/h produced improved outcomes. Although fewer beads were observed under these conditions (see
Figure 24B), they were still present in considerable amounts. Furthermore, when the ﬁber membrane
was thermally treated at 130°C for 4 hours, it resulted in a melted membrane.

Analysis of the ﬁber samples showed a clear di↵erence in morphology and dimensions depending

uniform electrospun ﬁbers. The diameter distribution was analyzed using ImageJ, based on 100 ﬁber
measurements, which revealed a narrower distribution for the improved ﬁbers—indicating better
reproducibility and control in the electrospinning process.

E↵ect of Thermal Treatment on Membrane Integrity and Water Response
The electrospun membrane was thermally treated at 130°C for 4 hours to initiate crosslinking. In
the ﬁrst attempt, using old PNIPAM, the membrane melted completely, leaving only the aluminum
foil (Figure 26). Panel A shows the membrane before treatment, and panel B shows the result after
four hours at 130°C.

In the next attempt, the correct PNIPAM was used. The membrane was placed in an oven at 130°C
for 4 hours. However, after the treatment, the membrane was found to be cracked and had broken
into small pieces. SEM analysis (see Figure 27) revealed that the polymer had contracted irregularly,
causing the ﬁbrous membrane to melt and take on a slimy appearance.

This result was assumed to be caused by several factors: (1) too little solution electrospun, resulting
in a thin layer that could not withstand heating; (2) use of a plate collector, which may have caused
the ﬁbers to layer unevenly, increasing the risk of cracking during heating; (3) incorrect ratio of

==PAGE:36==
PNIPAM, OpePOSS, and EMI, possibly resulting in less crosslinker; and (4) degradation of the
crosslinker (OpePOSS) and catalyst (EMI) because they are stored for too long or not kept properly.
Due to suspicion that too little crosslinker was added, a new solution with a ratio of 100:20:0.3
(PNIPAM:OpePOSS:EMI) based on studies [34] was prepared (see Appendix B), compared to the
earlier ratio of 100:15:0.3. Despite this adjustment, the resulting membrane still broke into pieces
after treatment, as shown in Figure 28. To investigate the e↵ect of ﬁber alignment on membrane
structure, the plate collector was replaced with a spinning collector, set at 12 kV, 14 cm distance,
1 ml/h infusion rate, and 75 RPM, as shown in Figure 14. This change was intended to further
compact the ﬁbers and produce a thicker, denser membrane, which was expected to help prevent
cracking.
Figure 28: Thermally treated membrane showing a crack.
Regardless of collector type, the ﬁber membranes were always deposited layer-by-layer, creating
issues during thermal treatment as the crosslinker activated unevenly.
The plate collector was preferred to create a thicker membrane, since the spin collector required
covering a larger area. Studies showed promising results at 120°C with the 100:20:0.3 ratio [35], so
the oven was tested at 120°C for 4 hours. The membrane did not crack, but dissolved during the
room temperature water test, making it unsuitable for body temperature (37°C).
New OpePOSS and EMI were then purchased, as the previous ones appeared to be expired, which
according to our assumption could be one reason for the observed degradation. The new EMI was
liquid, unlike the older solid form. A fresh solution was prepared and thermally treated at 120 °C for
4 hours. The resulting membrane showed less cracking but still dissolved in water tests. Thermal
treatment at 130 °C for 4 hours produced similar results, with cracking and no resistance to water,
which could be due to degradation of OpePOSS and EMI.
The temperature was raised to 140°C, but the oven couldn’t keep a steady temperature, which

5.1.3
Drug release
To determine the maximum theoretical drug concentration available for release from the drug-loaded
polymer matrix, the weighed amount of polymer used in each sample was multiplied by the known
drug loading ratio. The drug loading ratio was calculated from the total amount of drug incorporated
in the polymer batch.
For Sample 1, a 10.4 mg piece of drug-loaded polymer was weighed and placed into 1 mL PBS for in
vitro release testing. The drug loading ratio was 1.1 mg of dox in the whole piece of polymer. The
maximum possible concentration of drug in PBS was calculated using the following equation:
Cmax = polymer weight (mg) ⇥drug loading (mg/mg polymer)
volume of PBS (mL)
This value represents the maximum amount of drug that can be theoretically released from the
sample under perfect release conditions. It was used as a reference to calculate the cumulative drug
release at each time point.
Figure 35: (A) Standard calibration curve used to determine DOX concentration based on absorbance
measurements at 480 nm. (B) Cumulative drug release proﬁle of DOX from PNIPAM ﬁber membranes
over time, showing the gradual release behavior under the given experimental conditions.
To ﬁnd out how much DOX was released, we made a standard curve using known amounts of DOX.
We started with 25 µg/mL and diluted it by half several times until about 0.03 µg/mL. This helped
us measure the drug amount in our samples based on the absorbance readings 35 A.
33
==PAGE:41==
We tested drug release at room temperature and 37°C to see how temperature a↵ects it. At room
temperature, the release slowly increased and reached about 30 µg after 5 hours. At 37°C, the release
was much lower — only about 1.6µg after 5 hours. 35 B.
This was not what we expected. Although PNIPAM becomes hydrophobic above its LCST (32°C),
slow and prolonged drug release has been demonstrated [36], where the relatively higher temperature
was supposed to accelerate drug di↵usion from the ﬁbers. However, in our case, we only observed
very slow drug release over 5 hours.
One possible reason is the heat treatment at 130°C to crosslink the membranes. We think the
crosslinking might not have worked well, making the polymer structure too tight or uneven, which
may have blocked the drug from releasing properly — especially at 37°C.
The polymer may have collapsed too much at 37°C, becoming too dense and compact, which
physically blocked drug release.
So overall, more drug was released at room temperature than at 37°C due to the hydrophilic nature
of the PNIPAM membrane. This suggests that drug release from the ﬁbrous membrane didn’t work
eﬃciently as planned, and we will need to improve the crosslinking process and ﬁber design to
achieve better results.
To further test the e↵ect of the membranes on HeLa cells, we used conditioned media collected from
membrane samples incubated at 37°C. This setup allowed any released DOX to mix into the media
before applying it to the cells. We tested four groups: a control group with just cells, a group with
conditioned media from non-DOX membranes, a group with conditioned media from DOX-loaded
membranes, and a toxic control.

As shown in

cytotoxicity, comparable to the TCP control. This indicates that the ﬁber material itself was not

These ﬁndings support that some DOX was released from the membranes at 37°C, which aligns
with earlier drug release results (see Figure 51B). However, the overall cytotoxic e↵ect remained
modest, likely due to limited DOX di↵usion. This may be attributed to the dense crosslinking of the
PNIPAM structure and its temperature-induced shrinking behavior above its lower critical solution
temperature (LCST), both of which can hinder drug release by reducing membrane permeability
and water uptake.
When the PNIPAM is too heavenly crosslinked, the DOX can get stuck inside the dense structure
and can’t move out into the surrounding enviroment.
PNIPAM shrinks and becomes highly hydrophobic when the temperature rises above its transition
point. At 37°C, this shrinkage likely blocks the pathways through which the drug would normally
di↵use. As the structure contracts, less water can enter the membrane, reducing the dissolution
of the drug and its ability to di↵use out. If the pores or channels are mostly closed, DOX cannot
escape into the medium as expected.
So, the combination of strong crosslinking and polymer collapse at 37°C made the membrane too tight
and less permeable. Even though the system was supposed to release the drug at body temperature,
the current design didn’t work well enough under those conditions.
5.2
3D-printed microneedles
The ﬁnalized 3D-printed microstructures successfully met the intended design and performance
criteria. Structures featuring microneedles atop microﬁbers were fabricated using iterative design
improvements, focusing on ﬂexibility, resolution, and mechanical stability.
Initially, print attempts using Gray Resin V4 resulted in highly brittle structures that, although
printed with high resolution, lacked ﬂexibility and broke easily. Switching to BioMed Clear V1
improved ﬂexibility, but print resolution was insuﬃcient. Ultimately, BioMed Flex 80A was chosen
for its ideal balance of ﬂexibility, resolution and biocompatibility, enabling successful fabrication of
complex structures.
A key challenge in fabrication was ensuring adhesion of microﬁbers to the supporting frame. Designs
with freestanding microﬁbers frequently detached during printing. To address this, microﬁbers were
embedded directly into the frame, signiﬁcantly improving print success and post-treatment durability.
Optimized support placement further minimized stress during the printing process.
After reﬁning the parameters, microﬁbers with diameters of 300 µm, 400 µm, and 500 µm were
successfully printed. Attempts to fabricate thinner ﬁbers (100 µm and 200 µm) failed due to breakage
during removal from the build platform. Thus, 300 µm was selected as the minimum diameter for
mechanical robustness and print ﬁdelity.
Microneedles were then integrated into the microﬁber structures, with each needle designed to be
300 µm in diameter and height. A total of 32 microneedles were placed along each microﬁber. This
conﬁguration is intended for biomedical applications, such as improved adhesion to mucosal surfaces
(see Figures 37).

Mechanical Testing of BioMed Flex 80A Microﬁbers
To evaluate the mechanical properties of the microﬁbers, a tensile test was conducted using BioMed
Flex 80A samples. The experiment involved cyclic loading and unloading, with force (in Newtons)
and displacement (in millimeters) recorded at a frequency of 200 data points per second.
Figure 41 shows a representative force-displacement curve from the test, highlighting the elastic

5.4.3
LDH Assay
To further evaluate the cytotoxic potential of the materials developed in this study, an LDH (lactate
dehydrogenase) release assay was conducted following 24 hours of incubation with Caco-2 cells. This
assay serves as a sensitive indicator of cell membrane integrity, where the release of LDH into the
culture medium is directly proportional to the extent of membrane damage—and thus, cytotoxicity.
As shown in Figure 45, all test conditions—control (TCP), ﬁber-conditioned medium, and 3D-material-
conditioned medium—demonstrated very low LDH release, with cytotoxicity values remaining well
below 2%. In stark contrast, the positive control group (treated with a lytic agent to induce full
membrane rupture) showed 100% LDH release, validating the assay’s sensitivity and dynamic range.
According to the ISO 10993-5 standard for the biological evaluation of medical devices, materials
that induce less than 30% cytotoxicity are classiﬁed as non-cytotoxic. All tested samples in this
study clearly fall within that category.[38].

Ex vivo experiment
The adhesion performance of the 3D-printed microﬁbers with di↵erent microneedle heights was tested
using the pig intestine setup described earlier (Figure 23). The intestine measured approximately
24 cm in length. Four types of ﬁbers were tested: a control group with smooth microﬁbers (0 mm
microneedles) and three groups with microneedles of 0.3 mm, 0.6 mm, and 0.75 mm in height. Each
test was repeated four times for every ﬁber type to ensure consistency.

Microﬁbers without microneedles (0 mm) moved easily through the entire intestinal tract under a
water ﬂow of 20 ml/min, with a syringe diameter of 29 mm, showing no adhesion to the mucus layer.
The 0.3 mm microneedles provided slightly better interaction, remaining in place for an average of
12 seconds compared to 9 seconds for the control group. However, both ﬁber types failed to resist

a higher ﬂow rate of 99 ml/min to better simulate physiological conditions, the pump was limited to
a maximum speed of 20 ml/min and thus prevented us from increasing the ﬂow rate further.
In contrast, ﬁbers with 0.6 mm and 0.75 mm microneedles showed signiﬁcantly improved adhesion.
The 0.6 mm ﬁbers were consistently stopped around 11 cm along the intestine, while the 0.75 mm
ﬁbers stopped earlier, at around 7.5 cm. This suggests that taller microneedles increase mechanical
interlocking with the mucus, preventing the ﬁber from sliding through.
Although 0.75 mm microneedles performed best in terms of adhesion, their longer length increases
the risk of breakage during production, which may lead to uneven microneedle height. In comparison,
the 0.6 mm microneedles still o↵er strong adhesion while being more reliable to produce consistently.

shorter distances, conﬁrming stronger attachment to the intestinal wall. The error bars demonstrate

balance of strong adhesion and reliable manufacturing quality.

5.6
The Final Device
The ﬁnal product consists of a two-part, thermo-responsive orally administrable device designed for
controlled drug release in the intestine. It comprises an electrospun PNIPAM-based ﬁber membrane
combined with a 3D-printed ﬂexible structure capable of folding under physiological conditions (see
Figure 47). The concept behind the design was to create a smart and foldable drug carrier that
could change shape in response to temperature ﬂuctuations, thereby achieving increased retention in
the intestinal mucosa.
Figure 47: The ﬁnal device features a polymer membrane overlaid with the 3D-printed structure
spaced 1 mm apart, matching the 1 mm scale bar in the image.
5.6.1
Physical Appearance and Structure
The ﬁnal product appears as a thin, ﬂexible, sandwich-like structure. The electrospun ﬁber membrane
serves as the active drug-carrying component, while the 3D-printed layer made from Bioﬂex material
is added to provide mechanical support and enable folding.
At room temperature, the device remains ﬂat and extended.
When the temperature reaches
approximately 37 °C, as in the intestine, the PNIPAM ﬁbers shrink, inducing a distinct mechanical
deformation where the structure begins to fold due to the anisotropic mechanical restriction imposed
by the top 3D-printed microﬁbers. This feature allows the system to change shape and thereby
enhance retention within the intestinal mucosa.

5.6.3
Challenges and Considerations
Despite promising mechanical and structural properties, several challenges arose during development:
• Crosslinking of PNIPAM ﬁbrous membrane needs further optimization, which is crucial for its
thermo-induced structure change.
• Thermal crosslinking at 130 °C likely resulted in an overly dense polymer structure, limiting
the di↵usion of DOX from the ﬁbers at body temperature.
• The ﬁber membrane’s porosity was reduced after temperature change from room temperature
to 37°C, which may have further restricted drug transport.
• Mechanical integration between the ﬁber membrane and the 3D structure required careful
optimization, as delamination or deformation could occur after folding and temperature
exposure.

6
Conclusion
This project successfully demonstrated the design, fabrication, and characterization of a novel
oral drug delivery platform that integrates a thermoresponsive PNIPAM-based electrospun ﬁber
membrane with a 3D-printed ﬂexible microstructure featuring omnidirectional microneedles. The aim
was to develop a system capable of enhancing mucosal adhesion, prolonging intestinal residence time,
and enabling sustained drug release ultimately improving the bioavailability of sensitive therapeutic
agents.
The ﬁnal device, a two-part sandwich-like construct, was designed to remain ﬂat at room temperature
and fold in response to physiological temperatures (approximately 37 °C). This thermally induced
folding behavior enhances mechanical anchoring and mucoadhesion within the intestinal environment.
Structural and morphological analyses conﬁrmed the successful integration of the electrospun
membrane with the 3D-printed support structure, with SEM imaging revealing a porous architecture
suitable for drug loading.
However, several challenges emerged during development. Notably, drug release at 37 °C was
lower than expected. Thermal crosslinking at 130 °C likely led to excessive densiﬁcation of the
polymer network, thereby restricting the di↵usion of DOX. Moreover, the shrinkage of PNIPAM
membrane at 37 ￿resulted in highly reduced porosity and obviously overhigh hydrophobicity,
which also signiﬁcanlty impact drug release. Furthermore, layer-by-layer patterns resulting from the
electrospinning process persisted even after thermal treatment, potentially impacting the structural
stability of the membrane and its further integration with 3D- printed microneedles.
Mechanical integration between the ﬁber membrane and the 3D-printed structure also required
careful optimization to prevent delamination or deformation, particularly during thermal folding.
Despite these challenges, the ﬁnal device demonstrated good structural stability, thermoresponsive
behavior, and potential for improved mucosal retention.
According to our in vitro cell experiments, the materials used in this platform exhibited biocompati-
bility, supporting their suitability for biomedical applications.
In conclusion, this work provides a strong proof of concept for a smart, foldable oral drug delivery
system with tunable mechanical and thermal properties. While further optimization is needed
to enhance drug release kinetics and address ﬁber layer uniformity, the platform o↵ers promising
potential for the oral administration of biologics and poorly soluble drugs, with future applications
in targeted therapies.
7
Future Perspectives
While the developed device demonstrates promising structural and functional characteristics, there
remain opportunities for further optimization and reﬁnement. A key focus for future work will be to
improve drug release kinetics at physiological temperature (37°C). The current system exhibited
suboptimal release, likely due to the dense polymer network formed during thermal crosslinking and
limited porosity of the electrospun ﬁbers after temperature change. Additionally, the ratios between
the ingredients used in the ﬁber formulation could have a↵ected ﬁber morphology and drug release
behavior, indicating a need to optimize the composition.
Moreover, alternative crosslinking methods for PNIPAM ﬁbers can avoid the need for high-
temperature oven treatments. For example, UV-induced crosslinking of PNIPAM-based copolymers
has been demonstrated as an e↵ective approach to achieve stable networks at lower temperatures,
preserving ﬁber morphology and reducing thermal degradation risks [39],[40]. This method allows
48
==PAGE:56==
rapid and controlled crosslinking by exposing the ﬁbers to UV light, initiating polymerization within
copolymer segments without compromising sensitive drug payloads. Further optimization of the
thermal curing process and membrane fabrication will be carried out to ﬁne-tune the porosity and
ﬁber structure. By adjusting parameters such as electrospinning conditions, crosslinking time, and
temperature settings, the goal is to create a ﬁber network that stays structurally stable while still
allowing the drug to di↵use e↵ectively at body temperature.
In the current method, loading the drug into the electrospun ﬁbers still has some limitations. The
drug release proﬁle needs further optimization, and the thermal crosslinking at 140°C is not suitable
for thermo-sensitive drugs like curcumin. Because of this, alternative strategies should be explored.
One option is to coat curcumin onto the microneedles by dissolving it in a suitable liquid and
applying it through dip-coating, where the microneedles are dipped into the solution and dried, or
drop-casting, where small drops are placed directly onto the surface and left to dry. Both methods
are done at room temperature and avoid exposing the drug to heat. Another possibility is spraying
curcumin onto the polymer membrane using a ﬁne mist, either manually or with an airbrush or spray
system. This can provide even coverage and allow the drug amount to be controlled by adjusting
the number of sprayed layers.
These methods can potentially improve drug stability and release control, but there was not enough
time in this project to experiment using curcumin or DOX.
Another possibility for improvement involves the exploration of alternative resins for the 3D-printed
sca↵old. Speciﬁcally, testing materials with increased elasticity, such as BioMed Elastic or comparable
ﬂexible biocompatible resins, could enhance mechanical integration between the ﬁber membrane
and the printed frame. These materials may reduce delamination and deformation after thermal
exposure and folding, while potentially allowing for more dynamic shape changes under physiological
conditions.
The long-term goal is to adjust the material and device design so the oral drug delivery system can
be customized and scaled up. It should work with di↵erent kinds of drugs, including biologics and
those that don’t dissolve well. More in vivo tests will also be needed to prove that the system is
safe, e↵ective, and practical to use.

(b) Transfer the suspension into a T-75 culture ﬂask.
(c) Incubate at 37 °C in a humidiﬁed incubator with 5% CO2.
(d) Monitor cell morphology and conﬂuence daily.
(e) Change the culture medium every 2–3 days.
4. Subculturing (Passaging):
(a) Upon reaching 80–90% conﬂuence:
i. Aspirate old medium.
ii. Rinse cells with 5 mL DPBS to remove serum traces.
iii. Add 2.5 mL trypsin solution and incubate at 37 °C for 3–5 min.
iv. Observe cell detachment under a microscope.
(b) Neutralize trypsin by adding 8 mL of complete medium.
(c) Transfer the suspension into a sterile 15 mL centrifuge tube.
(d) Centrifuge at 1300 rpm for 5 min.
(e) Discard the supernatant and resuspend the pellet in fresh medium as required.
(f) Repeat the splitting procedure every 3–4 days.
5. Cell Seeding for Cytotoxicity Assays:
(a) Once a suﬃcient number of cells were obtained:
i. Seed approximately 1 ⇥105 cells per well in a 96-well plate.
ii. Allow cells to adhere and form a conﬂuent monolayer prior to treatment.
6. Quality Control:
(a) Throughout culturing, monitor morphology, conﬂuence, and tight junction integrity.
(b) Ensure epithelial-like characteristics are retained prior to assay preparation.

D.1 CCK-8 Assay Procedure
1. Medium Removal and Washing:
• Remove the culture medium from the 96-well plate.
• Wash cells gently with phosphate-bu↵ered saline (PBS), then remove the PBS.
2. Preparation of CCK-8 Medium:
• Prepare a working solution for 25 wells using a total volume of 2,500 µL (100 µL per
well).
• Dilute the CCK-8 reagent at a 1:10 ratio by mixing 250 µL of CCK-8 with 2,250 µL of
fresh complete DMEM.
• Ensure the solution is mixed thoroughly.
3. Addition of CCK-8 Medium:
• Add 100 µL of the prepared CCK-8 solution to each well.
• Add 100 µL of the same solution to blank wells (without cells) for background correction.
4. Incubation:
• Incubate the plate at 37 °C for 4 hours.
5. Supernatant Collection:
• After incubation, transfer 90–100 µL of the supernatant from each well to a new 96-well
plate for absorbance reading.
6. Medium Replacement for Continued Culture:
• In the original plate, add 100 µL of:
– Fresh DMEM for control (TCP) wells.
– Conditioned medium for experimental wells (e.g., ﬁbers or 3D printed devices).
• Return the plate to the incubator for continued culture.

D.3 LDH Cytotoxicity Assay
Principle: The LDH assay measures lactate dehydrogenase released from damaged or lysed cells,
indicating cytotoxicity based on membrane integrity.
Sample and Cell Preparation
• Sterilization: Expose electrospun ﬁbers and 3D printed samples to UV light for 30 minutes
on each side.
• Preconditioning: Soak samples in complete medium for 24–25 hours at 4 °C.
• Cell Seeding: Seed cells at a density of 1 ⇥104 cells/well in 96-well plates with 100 µL of
medium.
• Experimental Groups:
– Group 1: Control (TCP)
– Group 2: Electrospun ﬁbers
– Group 3: 3D printed devices
• Incubate for 24 or 48 hours at 37 °C with 5% CO2.
LDH Assay Procedure
1. Gently mix the culture medium in each well.
2. Transfer 100 µL of the supernatant from each well to a new 96-well plate.
3. Controls:
• Low Control: Untreated TCP cells
• High Control: Cells treated with lysis bu↵er (for maximal LDH release)
• Blank: Medium without cells
4. Add 100 µL of prepared LDH reaction mix to each well.
5. Incubate for 30 minutes at room temperature, protected from light.
6. Add 50 µL of Stop Solution (if provided).
7. Measure absorbance at 490nm.

Drug Release Procedure
The drug release study was conducted to evaluate the release proﬁle of Doxorubicin from the
developed membrane ﬁbers. This allows for the assessment of release kinetics and the potential
suitability of the system for oral drug delivery applications. The release experiment was performed
over a 5hour period using PBS at 37°C, with samples collected at multiple time points to generate a
detailed release curve.
Materials Required
• Doxorubicin-loaded membrane ﬁbers
• Phosphate-bu↵ered saline (PBS), pH 7.4
• 15 mL Falcon tubes
• Analytical balance
• Micropipettes and tips (200 µL capacity)
• 96-well microplate
• Microplate reader (for absorbance and ﬂuorescence)
• Water bath set to 37 °C
Procedure
1. Preparation of Materials Pre-warm PBS in a water bath at 37°C. Gather all materials and
equipment.
2. Sample Weighing Using an analytical balance, weigh approximately 0.0200g of each
Doxorubicin-loaded membrane ﬁber. Prepare ﬁve samples in total.
3. Sample Loading Place each weighed membrane ﬁber into a separate 15mL Falcon tube. Add
1.0mL of pre-warmed PBS to each tube to fully submerge the sample.
4. Incubation Place the tubes in a 37°C water bath to mimic physiological conditions. Gently
swirl each tube at the start to ensure uniform distribution.
5. Timed Sampling At the designated time points, withdraw 200 µL of the PBS solution using
a micropipette. Time points:
• Every 15 minutes for the ﬁrst 3 hours
• At 4 hours and 5 hours
• Final sample at 24 hours
6. PBS Replacement After each withdrawal, add 200 µL of fresh pre-warmed PBS to maintain
constant volume and ensure sink conditions.
7. Transfer to Microplate Transfer each 200 µL sample into a well of a 96-well microplate.
Include blank PBS as a control in separate wells for background correction.

8. Absorbance and Fluorescence Measurement Measure absorbance at 480nm (characteristic
peak of DOX). Record ﬂuorescence using 480nm excitation and 590nm emission for improved
sensitivity.
9. Data Documentation Record all absorbance and ﬂuorescence readings. Subtract background
values using the PBS blank.
10. Data Analysis Calculate cumulative DOX release at each time point. Generate a release
proﬁle curve (DOX released vs. time). Analyze release kinetics to assess the performance of
the thermo-responsive system.
11. Calibration Curve Prepare a calibration curve using known DOX concentrations. Use this
to convert absorbance/ﬂuorescence readings into actual concentrations.
