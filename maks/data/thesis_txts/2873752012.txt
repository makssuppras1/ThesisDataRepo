Abstract
While inference-time scaling through search has revolutionized Large Language Models, translating
these gains to image generation has proven elusive. Recent attempts to apply search strategies
to continuous diffusion models have shown limited benefits, with simple random sampling often
performing better. This thesis demonstrates that the discrete, sequential nature of autoregressive
image models provides the missing foundation for effective search in visual generation. I present a
comprehensive study of how classic search algorithms, particularly beam search, can dramatically
improve autoregressive text-to-image generation.
Through experiments on DrawBench and T2I-CompBench++, I show that beam search achieves
superior image quality while requiring only 54% of the function evaluations compared to random
search. On compositional tasks, my approach drives improvements of up to 17.4 percentage points,
with an average gain of 11.3 p.p. across all categories. Remarkably, a 2B parameter autoregressive
model equipped with beam search achieves gains 2× larger than those of search applied to a 12B
parameter diffusion model, allowing the 6× smaller model to achieve superior final performance in
every category and demonstrating that architectural choice matters more than scale for enabling
effective search.
To understand these efficiency gains, I conduct systematic ablation studies revealing why this advan-
tage exists: the discrete token space enables early pruning of unpromising paths and computational
reuse. However, attempts to verify intermediate visual states for further efficiency gains face fun-
damental challenges. My analysis of verifier dynamics shows clear trade-offs between speed and
capability, with lightweight models sufficient for attribute binding but heavyweight vision-language
models necessary for complex spatial reasoning.
This work establishes a new paradigm for autoregressive image generation where progress comes
not from larger models but from smarter inference. By demonstrating that models can “think harder”
through search, I open a path toward more efficient and accessible high-quality image generation.

What if the next leap in image generation comes not from building larger models, but from teaching
smaller ones to think more carefully? This thesis demonstrates that a 2B parameter autoregressive
model, when equipped with intelligent search algorithms, can outperform a model six times its
size while using significantly less computational search budget. As shown in Fig. 1.1, this finding
suggests a potential shift in how we approach generative modeling for image generation, from pure
scale to strategic computation.
The field of generative modeling has achieved remarkable progress through a well-proven recipe:
scale. Text-to-image models have evolved from abstract sketches to photorealistic imagery by
training increasingly larger models on massive datasets [2, 3, 4, 5]. This approach has established
diffusion models as the dominant architecture, delivering state-of-the-art quality through their
continuous latent space representations [6, 7, 3, 8].
Yet the landscape of generative modeling is evolving. In language modeling, a complementary
paradigm has emerged where systems achieve dramatic performance gains not through more param-
eters, but through more sophisticated inference-time computation [9, 10, 11, 12]. The success of
models like o1 [9], o3 [13], DeepSeek-R1 [10], and Gemini 2.5 Pro [14] demonstrates through rein-
forcement learning that how a model “thinks” can matter as much as its size. Other systems leverage
sophisticated search algorithms and process reward models [15, 16, 17, 18] to achieve expert-level
reasoning through computation rather than scale, with [11] also demonstrating that small, generally
available models can outperform large models with compute-optimal inference-scaling. This raises
a fundamental question for the future of generative AI: will the ability to leverage inference-time
computation become as important as raw model capacity?
This success in language naturally raises the question of whether similar gains are possible in image
generation. For this domain, however, the answer has proven elusive. Recent attempts to apply
strategies like noise trajectory search to diffusion models have yielded unpromising results, with
simple random sampling often performing better [1].

This thesis explores a promising alternative: autoregressive image models. Their discrete, sequential
generation process, often viewed as a computational disadvantage compared to parallel diffusion,
may actually be their hidden strength [19, 2, 20, 21, 22]. By representing images as sequences of
discrete tokens, these models create a search space remarkably similar to language, where classic
algorithms like beam search can effectively navigate toward better solutions. Fig. 1.2 illustrates
how this architectural difference fundamentally changes the scaling dynamics.
To validate this hypothesis, I present a comprehensive study of search algorithms applied to autore-
gressive image generation. My approach combines state-of-the-art discrete visual models [21] with
sophisticated verifiers, from lightweight preference models to powerful vision-language models,
that guide the search process toward high-quality, compositionally accurate images.
The results challenge conventional assumptions about model scaling:
• Efficiency through search: On DrawBench [4], my beam search approach surpasses the
quality of search on a 12B diffusion model while using less than half the function evaluations,
demonstrating that strategic computation can substitute for both model scale and brute-force
sampling.
• Compositional performance gains: On challenging multi-object scenes, guided search deliv-
ers improvements of up to 17.4 percentage points (p.p), with average gains of 11.3 p.p. across
all T2I-CompBench++ categories, addressing long-standing weaknesses in compositional
generation.
• Architecture matters more than scale: Despite using a 6× smaller model, my autoregres-
sive approach achieves 2× larger improvements than search applied to a 12B parameter
diffusion model, while also outperforming the 12B model in all compositional categories,
suggesting that architectural compatibility with search may be more important than raw
parameter count.

• Understanding the boundaries: Through systematic ablations, I reveal both the promise

These findings suggest we may be approaching an inflection point in generative modeling. Just as the
field once shifted from convolutional networks to transformers when scale became paramount [23],
we may now be witnessing the early stages of a transition toward architectures that can effectively
leverage inference-time computation. The question is not whether one architecture will replace

work and charting potential paths forward for the field.

2
Related Work
This chapter reviews the key research areas that form the foundation for my work on search-guided
autoregressive image generation. I begin by surveying the landscape of modern visual generation
(Sec. 2.1), covering the dominant diffusion paradigm and the recent resurgence of autoregressive
models, which provides the architectural basis for my approach. Following this, I review the
extensive progress made in test-time scaling in Large Language Models (Sec. 2.2), where inference-
time search has proven highly effective, establishing the key principles that motivate my investigation
in the visual domain. Finally, I examine the emerging field of test-time scaling in image generation
(Sec. 2.3), summarizing recent efforts to adapt these techniques to both continuous and discrete
models.
2.1
Visual Generation
Diffusion models have emerged as dominant for high-quality visual generation, achieving state-of-
the-art results through iterative denoising processes. Key architectures like DDPM [6], DDIM [7],
Stable Diffusion [3, 5], DiT [8] and flow-matching models [24] demonstrate high-quality image
synthesis capabilities, with subsequent work extending to video generation through models like
Sora [25]. These models capture complex data distributions through gradual noise addition and
removal, enabling controllable generation through techniques like classifier-free guidance [26] and
ControlNet [27].
Autoregressive image generation has roots in PixelRNN and PixelCNN [19, 28], which intro-
duced pixel-by-pixel generation but suffered from severe computational limitations. VQ-VAE [29]
established discrete tokenization, enabling efficient autoregressive modeling over compressed repre-
sentations. DALL-E [2] successfully combined autoregressive transformers with discrete tokens
for text-to-image generation, though raster-scan approaches still faced computational inefficiencies
and violated natural bidirectional dependencies. Recent work addresses these limitations. Visual
AutoRegressive modeling (VAR) [20] redefines autoregressive learning as coarse-to-fine “next-scale
prediction” rather than standard “next-token prediction”, with Infinity [21] extending this approach
through bitwise token prediction and infinite-vocabulary tokenizers. This sequential modeling
method has been shown to enable scalable learning for large vision models [30]. Alternatively, Lla-
maGen [22] demonstrates that vanilla autoregressive models can achieve competitive performance
by refining the original “next-token prediction” without specialized visual inductive biases. Beyond
discrete tokenization, emerging approaches eliminate vector quantization entirely, using diffusion
procedures for continuous-valued autoregressive generation [31]. Additionally, new integrations
incorporate native image generation directly within language model architectures [32, 33]. These
advances establish autoregressive models as competitive alternatives to diffusion approaches, with
emerging scaling properties that bridge language and vision domains.
2.2
Test-Time Scaling in Large Language Models
Test-time scaling has emerged as a powerful tool in large language models, where allocating
additional computational resources during inference yields performance improvements. Large
language models achieve performance improvements through both internal and external methods.
Internal methods include models like OpenAI o1/o3 and DeepSeek-R1, which demonstrate gains
on reasoning benchmarks by employing reinforcement learning-trained internal reasoning processes
and self-refinement [9, 13, 10]. These models learn to naturally extend their “thinking time”
during inference through learned chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning. s1 [34] builds on internal
self-refinement by forcing computational budgets through generation termination (stopping if the

strategies [42], while approaches like repeated sampling demonstrate that simple strategies can
yield substantial improvements [43]. This approach is particularly promising for smaller models,
with [11] demonstrating that scaling inference computation allows models to achieve performance
rivaling models 14× larger.
2.3
Test-Time Scaling in Image Generation
Recent success of test-time scaling in language models [11, 15, 10] has motivated exploring similar
approaches for image generation, where models can improve outputs by allocating additional com-
putation during inference rather than increasing model size. Early autoregressive image generation
models like VAR [20] and Infinity [21] enable discrete token-based generation amenable to language
model techniques, while diffusion models operate in continuous latent spaces requiring specialized
adaptations.
Internal methods. Preference optimization techniques adapt reinforcement learning approaches
to align image generation with human preferences. Wallace et al. [44] introduce Diffusion-DPO,
reformulating Direct Preference Optimization for diffusion models using the evidence lower bound
to derive differentiable objectives from human preference data. This approach significantly improves
visual appeal and prompt alignment. Subsequent work explores curriculum-based variants [45] that
gradually increase training difficulty, and entropy-regularized approaches [46] to mitigate overfitting
and reward hacking. For autoregressive models, Tong et al. [47] provide a comprehensive comparison
of DPO versus Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), revealing that GRPO exhibits stability

and computational efficiency while DPO shows higher sensitivity to reward model variations.
Building on these foundations, Jiang et al. [48] propose T2I-R1 with BiCoT-GRPO, implementing
bi-level CoT reasoning that distinguishes semantic-level planning from token-level generation,
achieving improvements through joint optimization of both reasoning levels with ensemble reward
signals.
External methods. Test-time scaling without model modification primarily relies on sampling and
verification strategies. The simplest approach is best-of-N sampling, where multiple candidates are
generated and ranked using reward models such as ImageReward [49] for text-image alignment,
CLIPScore [50] for semantic correspondence, aesthetic predictors [51] for visual appeal or VLMs for
sophisticated selection [52, 53]. Advanced verification approaches incorporate Chain-of-Thought
reasoning, with Guo et al. [53] introducing PARM (Potential Assessment Reward Model) for adaptive
step-wise evaluation in autoregressive generation through clarity judgment, potential assessment,
and best-of-N selection strategies. More sophisticated approaches explore search over generation
trajectories. Singhal et al. [54] propose a general framework for inference-time scaling and steering
of diffusion models. Ma et al. [1] systematically evaluate inference-time scaling for diffusion models
beyond increasing denoising steps, introducing search frameworks over noise trajectories with three
algorithms shown in Fig. 2.1. Their experiments reveal that noise trajectory search strategies are
consistently outperformed by simple best-of-N sampling, suggesting fundamental limitations in
applying verification techniques to continuous latent space models. For flow models, Kim et al. [55]
address deterministic sampling limitations through SDE-based generation, interpolant conversion
from linear to Variance-Preserving trajectories, and Rollover Budget Forcing for adaptive compute
allocation across timesteps. SANA-1.5 [52] demonstrates the effectiveness of repeated sampling
with VLM-based selection, achieving improvements and showing that smaller models with inference-
time scaling can outperform significantly larger models, much in line with [11]. Zhuo et al. [56]
propose ReflectionFlow, an inference-time framework that integrates noise-level, prompt-level, and
reflection-level scaling, using explicit textual feedback to iteratively refine diffusion model outputs.
Recent work extends these principles to video generation, with Liu et al. [57] introducing Video-T1
that reframes video generation as search over noise trajectories using Tree-of-Frames approach for
computational efficiency.

3
Methodology
This chapter lays out the methodological foundation for applying search algorithms to autoregressive
image generation. I begin by presenting the architecture of the foundational generative model,
Infinity (Sec. 3.1). Following this, I detail the suite of verifiers used to provide varied quality signals
to guide the search (Sec. 3.2), and establish my framework for measuring computational cost to
ensure fair comparison between strategies. To conclude, I provide a formal definition of the three
search algorithms at the core of my work, random search, Greedy Token Optimization, and beam
search, illustrating how each approach navigates the generative search space (Sec. 3.3).
3.1
Autoregressive Image Generation
Autoregressive models for image generation follow the same fundamental principle as large language
models: predicting the next element in a sequence based on previous context. However, applying
this paradigm to images presents challenges, as images lack the inherent sequential structure of
natural language and require careful tokenization to convert 2D visual data into discrete tokens
suitable for autoregressive modeling.
3.1.1
Visual AutoRegressive Modeling (VAR)
Traditional autoregressive approaches for images [2, 58] flatten 2D image tokens into 1D sequences
using raster-scan ordering, leading to three key limitations: violation of mathematical premises due
to bidirectional spatial dependencies, disruption of spatial locality, and computational inefficiency
with O(n6) complexity.
Visual AutoRegressive modeling (VAR) [20] addresses these limitations by redefining autoregressive
learning as ”next-scale prediction” rather than ”next-token prediction”. VAR employs a multi-scale
approach where images are encoded into K resolution scales R = (r1, r2, . . . , rK), with each
scale rk ∈[V ]hk×wk (where V is the vocabulary size) representing tokens at increasingly higher
resolutions. The autoregressive likelihood is reformulated as:

3.1.2

While VAR successfully addresses the structural limitations of image autoregression, it still faces
constraints from discrete tokenization. The vocabulary size V in traditional VQ-VAE approaches

vocabularies (e.g., V = 232) require prohibitively large classifier parameters (8.8 trillion for
h = 2048), while small vocabularies limit reconstruction fidelity.

models including SD3-Medium [5], while achieving 2.6× faster inference speed.
For this work, I employ Infinity as the base generative model due to its competitive image quality,
fast inference speed, and autoregressive structure that naturally enables integration with search
strategies, an avenue yet to be explored in image generation. Fig. 3.1 illustrates the fundamental
difference between Infinity’s progressive scale-based generation and traditional diffusion denoising
processes.
3.2
Verifiers
Evaluating generated images is difficult, and in order to guide search, comprehensive verification is
necessary. Images are complex, and reducing evaluation to one dimension is extremely challeng-
ing [62]. Following the work in [1], I guide search with three different verifiers, as well as adding
them together as an ensemble verifier. This allows for both sophisticated evaluation, and comparison
to previous work using continuous models. In addition, I experiment with the LLaVA-OneVision
Vision-Language Model [63], finetuned in [53] to verify prompt alignment.
CLIPScore is a reference-free evaluation metric that leverages CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image
Pre-training) [64] to assess image-caption compatibility. CLIP is a multimodal model trained on
400 million image-text pairs from the web using contrastive learning. The model consists of an
image encoder and a text encoder that map visual and textual inputs to a shared embedding space,
where semantically similar image-text pairs have high cosine similarity.
CLIPScore [50] computes the compatibility between an image v and candidate caption c by extracting
their respective embeddings and measuring their cosine similarity:
CLIPScore(c, v) = w · max(cos(c, v), 0)

3.2.1
Defining and Measuring Computational Budget
The choice of compute budget metric is crucial for fair comparison across different search strategies.
Prior work has employed varying definitions: some approaches using random search/best-of-N
define budget simply as the number of generated images [52, 53], while Ma et al. use NFEs (Number
of Function Evaluations) [1] which in their definition is the number of diffusion steps. Kim et al.
also uses NFEs, but take a more advanced approach, implementing rollover budget forcing [55],
that adaptively allocates NFEs across timesteps.
Autoregressive image generation faces a unique consideration: once tokens are generated and locked
during the sequential process, they do not require re-computation in subsequent search iterations.
This creates an asymmetry where random search must generate complete sequences from scratch,
while greedy token optimization and beam search can reuse previously computed tokens.
To provide a comprehensive and transparent evaluation, I report results using two distinct computa-
tional budget definitions: the number of generated images and the Number of Function Evaluations
(NFEs).
• Number of generated images: This metric reflects the total number of complete image
sequences evaluated by the verifier. It serves as a simple and transparent measure of search
depth and allows for a direct 1-to-1 comparison with other best-of-N studies, regardless of
the underlying model architecture.
• Number of Function Evaluations (NFEs): This metric accounts for the actual computa-
tional work performed. For the Infinity model used in this work, I define one NFE as a single
forward pass of the transformer to predict the tokens for one of its 13 progressive scales.
This allows for a precise measurement of resource utilization within my experiments, clearly
capturing the token reuse advantages of guided search strategies over random search.

It is crucial, however, to contextualize the NFE metric when making comparisons to studies on
different architectures. An NFE in my autoregressive framework is not directly equivalent to an
NFE in a diffusion model (i.e., a full denoising step). Therefore, any cross-study comparison of
NFE counts should be interpreted as an order-of-magnitude approximation, not a direct comparison
of FLOPs. I adopt this dual-metric approach to offer both a clear, high-level comparison of search
depth via the number of images and a precise, fine-grained analysis of computational efficiency via
NFEs. This ensures my findings are presented with both transparency and rigor.

This chapter presents a systematic evaluation of my proposed search strategies. I begin by estab-

the critical trade-offs between different verifiers (Sec. 4.2). Following this, I present my primary

4.1.1
DrawBench
DrawBench serves as a foundational benchmark for evaluating the effectiveness of search strategies.
Introduced by Saharia et al. [4], DrawBench comprises 200 carefully curated text prompts distributed
across 11 distinct categories, each designed to probe specific model capabilities that are relevant
for assessing the impact of inference-time scaling. The set was also used by [1], allowing for
comparison with inference scaling using continuous models.
The benchmark’s categories encompass a comprehensive range of generative challenges: color
attribution (e.g., “A blue colored dog”), numerical accuracy (e.g., “Three cats and one dog sitting
on the grass”), spatial reasoning (e.g., “A car on the left of a bus”), text rendering (e.g., “A sign that
says ‘Deep Learning’ ”), and compositional understanding including conflicting object interactions
(e.g., “A horse riding an astronaut”). Additional categories test the model’s robustness to linguistic
variations, including misspelled prompts (“Rbefraigerator”) and rare vocabulary (“Artophagous”).
This varied structure makes DrawBench suitable for evaluating search strategies, as the categorical
organization allows for fine-grained analysis of how different search methods perform across a suite
of compositional challenges. The benchmark’s moderate size of 200 prompts is a good balance
between comprehensive coverage and computational feasibility, enabling thorough evaluation of
multiple search configurations while remaining small enough for searches with higher budgets.
For this work, I employ DrawBench to assess how random search, greedy token optimization, and
beam search perform across these diverse generative tasks. The results will be used to select optimal
search strategies for further verification.

Verifier hacking is a prevalent issue when scaling inference through search, a trend also observed
by Ma et al. [1]. I find similar behavior in my experiments, where the search process overfits to a
verifier’s inherent biases. This phenomenon arises because verifiers often have narrow objectives;
optimizing for one metric, such as aesthetics, can degrade another, like prompt adherence (Fig. 4.2).
For instance, an aesthetic verifier might ignore key prompt details to generate a more visually
appealing image. Furthermore, per-sample verifiers can induce mode collapse by causing the search
to converge on a narrow set of high-scoring samples, which harms diversity-sensitive metrics. The
ensemble verifier balances competing objectives by aggregating rankings from multiple sources,
or employing constrained, local search algorithms that are less prone to aggressively exploiting a
single bias.

Ablation Studies and Analysis of Alternative Strategies
To better understand the factors driving the performance of the proposed search strategies, I con-
ducted several ablation studies to investigate alternative approaches. These experiments explore
more complex heuristics for budget allocation and verification, providing valuable insights into the
problem’s underlying challenges.
4.5.1
Analysis of a Heuristic-Based Dynamic Budget Allocation
A potential enhancement to tree search strategies is to allocate the computational budget dynamically,
focusing resources on steps where the model is most uncertain. I hypothesized that the variance of
verifier scores across candidate tokens would serve as a good proxy for this uncertainty. A high
variance would suggest a complex decision point where more exploration is needed, while low
variance would imply that the top candidates are similar and fewer samples are required.
Experimental setup. To test this hypothesis, I implemented a variance-based dynamic allocation
strategy. The core of this method is a heuristic designed to distribute a fixed total number of
candidate slots (130) across the 13 generation steps. The number of candidates allocated to each
step was set to be proportional to the variance of verifier scores observed at that step, relative to
the average variance across the entire generation process. As illustrated in Fig. 4.6, this approach
methodically concentrates the search on the initial, high-variance steps.

4.5.2
On the Challenge of Training Intermediate Verifiers
A natural approach to improving search efficiency is to verify partial generations, enabling the early
pruning of unpromising trajectories. This strategy has proven highly effective in language models
through Process Reward Models (PRMs) [15, 11, 18], which evaluate intermediate reasoning steps.
I investigated whether a similar approach could work for autoregressive image generation.
Key challenge: The stochastic completion problem. Unlike LLMs where intermediate steps are
often semantically coherent, my autoregressive model generates progressively refined visual features.
An intermediate state at step k does not map to a single final image, but to a wide distribution of
possible completions. My experimental setup, which labels an intermediate state with the score of
just one random completion, provides an incredibly noisy supervision signal. This is analogous to
evaluating a chess opening based on the outcome of a single, randomly played game; the signal is
too unreliable for effective learning.
Experimental setup. I attempted to train step-specific verifiers that could predict final image
quality from partial generations. My approach was as follows:
• I generated 10 candidate images for prompts from the T2I-CompBench++ training set using
the Infinity-2B model.
• From this pool, I constructed 52,380 within-prompt image pairs for contrastive learning.
• Each partial image was labeled with the final ImageReward score of its corresponding com-
pleted trajectory, using this as an inherited supervision signal.
• I fine-tuned separate models for each generation step, initializing from the public ImageReward
weights.
• The models were trained for 10 epochs using the original contrastive ranking loss, an effective
batch size of 128, and a cosine learning rate schedule with a peak LR of 1 × 10−5.
The attempt to train intermediate verifiers failed in two instructive ways, each confirmed by the
validation metrics shown in Fig. 4.7:
Early-Stage Failure (Step 1): The verifier for early, abstract image states completely failed to learn.
Fig. 4.7B shows its validation accuracy oscillating around 50%, no better than random guessing.
The corresponding validation loss in Fig. 4.7A is noisy and shows no meaningful convergence. This
confirms that the supervision signal is too distal and abstract to be useful at this early stage.

large improvement on the majority of pairs drives the average loss down. However, this over-
specialization corrupts the model’s general features, causing it to misclassify a few “hard” or
ambiguous pairs that it previously got correct. This leads to a net drop in the total accuracy, even
as the average loss improves.
Implications. This negative result highlights a key difference between autoregressive image and
text generation, a challenge conceptualized in Fig. 4.8. The attempt to train a “visual PRM” failed
due to the stochastic and noisy relationship between intermediate states and final outcomes. My
findings suggest that for practical and reliable search in autoregressive image models, evaluating
only completed images remains the most robust strategy. This motivates my focus on developing
efficient search algorithms like beam search, which are designed to minimize the number of full,
expensive trajectories that need to be evaluated.

Discussion
This chapter steps back from the empirical results to synthesize their broader meaning and impli-
cations. I begin by discussing the key findings regarding search efficiency and verifier dynamics
(Sec. 5.1), before turning to a critical examination of this work’s limitations (Sec. 5.2). Building
on this analysis, I then outline promising directions for future work (Sec. 5.3) and conclude by
considering the broader societal and ethical impact of these advanced generative methods (Sec. 5.4).
5.1
Key Findings
My experimental results offer several key insights into the effectiveness and underlying mechanics
of scaling autoregressive models at inference time. The findings are grouped into three main themes:
the fundamental economics of scaling, the critical role of the verifier in guiding the search, and the
inherent architectural advantages of autoregressive models for guided search.
5.1.1
The Economics of Test-Time Scaling
Logarithmic scaling of verification scores. My experiments reveal a fundamental law governing
the relationship between computational budget and generation quality. The expected maximum
verification score across all tested verifiers follows the relationship E[maxi≤k si] ≈α log(k) + β,
where k is the number of samples. This logarithmic scaling holds consistently, suggesting it is
an intrinsic property of the image generation space. This behavior, which aligns with established
scaling laws in machine learning [69, 70], indicates diminishing returns and establishes a clear
motivation for developing more compute-efficient search strategies than simple random sampling.
Simple heuristics for budget allocation can be inefficient. While guided search is superior to
random search, not all heuristics for allocating compute are effective. My attempt to dynamically
allocate the search budget based on verifier score variance proved counterproductive. The strategy
was significantly less compute-efficient, requiring 40% more NFEs for the same number of evaluated
paths, and ultimately underperformed a simpler fixed uniform budget approach (Tab. 4.6). This
result provides a valuable insight: score variance alone is a noisy and insufficient signal for budget
allocation, and a consistent search breadth at each step appears to be a more robust and efficient
strategy.
5.1.2
The Critical Role of the Verifier
Verifier choice creates distinct optimization trade-offs. While budget size sets a theoretical limit
on performance, the choice of verifier is a dominant factor that shapes the final output. When guiding
with ImageReward, I observe balanced improvements across metrics. In contrast, single-aspect
verifiers demonstrate aggressive optimization biases, as seen when Aesthetic Score optimization
achieves high visual appeal at the cost of prompt adherence (Fig. 4.2). This highlights the challenge
of “verifier hacking”, where the search process overfits to a verifier’s narrow objective.
Task complexity determines the optimal verification strategy. My evaluation on T2I-CompBench++
reveals that verifier effectiveness is task-dependent. For attribute binding tasks, the lightweight
ImageReward is highly cost-effective, while heavyweight models like LLaVA-OneVision provide
a decisive advantage on complex spatial reasoning. The practical implication of this verifier-task
dependency was illustrated in my temperature-tuning experiment (Sec. 4.4.1). When I increased
sampling diversity, the performance of the ImageReward-guided search improved on numeracy
tasks where it showed competency on simple prompts. Conversely, its performance on spatial
tasks, where it is weaker, actually decreased. This demonstrates that the verifier acts as a critical
bottleneck: increased diversity in the search space is only beneficial if the verifier is capable of
identifying the improved solutions within it. This reinforces the conclusion that optimal systems

31
==PAGE:38==
must employ adaptive verification, reserving expensive but more capable models for prompts that
demand genuine visual reasoning.
Intermediate generative states lack a reliable verification signal. My attempt to improve effi-
ciency by training step-specific, intermediate verifiers failed in two instructive ways (Fig. 4.7). First,
the verifier for early steps (e.g., Step 1) completely failed to learn, with accuracy hovering around
50%. This demonstrates that the supervision signal from the final image score is too distal and
noisy to be useful for abstract, early-stage features. Second, the verifier for late steps (e.g., Step
10) started with high accuracy but saw its performance degrade during fine-tuning. This suggests
that finetuning a reward model for process based rewards is very challenging, and simple transfer
learning from the domain of ”completed” images is not a sufficient strategy. It also highlights a
fundamental difference from LLMs, where intermediate steps can be semantically coherent and
verifiable.
5.1.3
The Architectural Advantage of Autoregressive Search
Search strategies dramatically improve efficiency. My findings demonstrate that for autoregres-
sive models, how the computational budget is spent is as important as its size. Both GTO and
beam search consistently outperform random search. On DrawBench, beam search with 195 images
surpasses random search with 390 images, while using only 27% of the function evaluations. This
efficiency stems directly from the discrete, sequential generation process, which allows guided algo-
rithms to prune sub-optimal paths early and reuse computation—advantages currently inaccessible
to continuous latent space models.
Discrete search provides a key advantage over continuous models. This architectural benefit
leads to a step-change in performance scaling, standing in sharp contrast to findings in diffusion
models where search offers limited gains over random sampling [1]. My direct, multi-benchmark
comparison makes this concrete: the 2B autoregressive model with beam search not only achieves
performance gains larger than those from search on a 12B diffusion model (Tab. 4.7), but it also
reaches a higher absolute score on every verifier metric on DrawBench and in every category on
T2I-CompBench++. This demonstrates that the discrete token space provides a fundamentally more
tractable and effective domain for guided search, unlocking quality improvements that have been
elusive in continuous diffusion architectures.
Beam search exhibits superior scaling at higher budgets. While both GTO and beam search
are highly efficient, beam search scales more effectively. At lower budgets, their performance is
comparable. However, with more compute, beam search’s lead widens (Fig. 4.4), reflecting its
ability to maintain multiple parallel hypotheses and avoid the local optima that can trap a purely
greedy search. This establishes beam search as the optimal strategy when quality requirements
justify larger computational budgets.
5.2
Limitations
While the results herein demonstrate the effectiveness of search strategies for scaling autoregressive
image generation, this work is subject to several important limitations that frame the scope of my
conclusions.
Model and architecture specificity. My experiments focus exclusively on the Infinity model
family, utilizing its specific discrete tokenization scheme and hierarchical generation process. The
effectiveness of beam search may partially depend on these architectural choices, and it is an open
question how well these efficiency gains generalize to autoregressive models with different tokenizers
(e.g., the ViT-VQGAN used in Parti [71]) or alternative generation paradigms like masked modeling
(e.g., Muse [72]). The 13-step progressive trajectory creates a particular search landscape that may
not be representative of all models.

==PAGE:39==
Verifier imperfection and bias. My search strategies are fundamentally guided by proxy models
for human preference. Despite efforts to mitigate “verifier hacking” through ensembles and analysis,
the problem remains partially unsolved. The results show that even ensemble approaches can
exhibit optimization biases. More fundamentally, current verifiers are trained on specific data
distributions that may not align with all use cases; a verifier excelling at photorealism may provide
poor guidance for artistic tasks. For instance, my temperature-tuning experiment (Sec. 4.4.1) showed
that increasing sampling diversity actively worsened spatial reasoning scores, as the search process
amplified the ImageReward verifier’s known weakness in that area. Furthermore, the computational
cost of sophisticated verifiers like LLaVA-OneVision (up to 36× slower than lightweight alternatives)
creates a stark trade-off between search quality and inference speed, a practical barrier for many
applications.
Inherent search strategy constraints. Beam search, while effective, is a heuristic with known
constraints. As a greedy algorithm, it can only make locally optimal decisions at each generation
step and cannot backtrack to correct early mistakes, a significant limitation when foundational errors
in composition or object count occur in the initial low-resolution stages. My results also reveal a
minimum budget threshold is required for guided search to explore sufficient search space with
uniform budget allocation across steps. Finally, the use of a fixed beam width is suboptimal, as
simple prompts could be solved with a narrow beam while complex prompts would likely benefit
from wider exploration, motivating the need for dynamic allocation strategies.
Practical computational economics. The reported efficiency gains, measured in NFEs, do not fully
capture the wall-clock time and memory overheads of practical deployment. The analysis assumes
verifier computation is negligible, but for heavyweight models like LLaVA-OneVision, verification
time constituted over 60% of the generation time in my experiments, significantly eroding the
effective speedup. Moreover, beam search requires maintaining multiple partial generations in
GPU memory, an overhead not measured here that could be prohibitive for larger models or higher
resolutions. Realizing these efficiency gains in practice would require sophisticated engineering to
decide when the search overhead justifies the expected quality improvement.
Lack of large-scale human evaluation. Ultimately, the automated metrics and benchmarks used
in this work, while state-of-the-art, are still proxies for true human judgment of image quality and
prompt fidelity. A comprehensive human preference study would be required to definitively validate
whether the “optimal” images found by these verifier-guided search strategies are genuinely superior
to those from simpler methods in the eyes of human evaluators. While beyond the scope of this
thesis, this remains the gold standard for evaluation.
5.3
Future Work
Building on the findings and limitations of this work, several exciting avenues for future research
emerge. I identify three primary directions: advancing the search and verification algorithms
themselves, formalizing the economics of how compute is allocated, and expanding the search
paradigm to more dynamic and interactive applications.
5.3.1
Advanced Search Algorithms and Intermediate Verification
Intermediate verification with process-based rewards. My ablation studies show that training
a verifier on partial images using a “distal” final reward is ineffective. The clear next step is to
design verifiers that provide a more direct, fine-grained signal. This points toward creating a
visual equivalent of a Process Reward Model (PRM) [15]. Such a model would not be trained on
final outcomes, but on the quality of the generative process itself, learning to reward promising
intermediate steps. Solving this challenge would unlock the ability to prune the search tree much
earlier, leading to dramatic efficiency gains.
Scaling Autoregressive Image Generation Through Inference-Time Search
33
==PAGE:40==
Beyond beam search. While effective, beam search is a relatively simple heuristic. More sophis-
ticated methods like Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), which have proven successful in other
domains with large search spaces, could be adapted for image generation. This approach would draw
a direct parallel to advancements in LLM reasoning, such as Rest-MCTS* [18], which leverages a
PRM to guide its search. In this context, the intermediate verifier described above would serve as a
“visual PRM”, enabling an MCTS algorithm to intelligently balance exploration and exploitation,
potentially discovering novel, high-quality image compositions that beam search might miss.
Beyond unimodal search. A further enhancement is to move towards multi-modal search, where
the search algorithm refines the image tokens and text prompt tokens simultaneously. If the model
struggles with a concept, the search could explore minor rephrasing of the prompt that might be
easier for the model to understand, while co-optimizing the image generation. This would create a
powerful system that finds the best possible image-text pair, rather than just the best image for a
fixed text. Other work validate the idea of prompt optimization, with [73, 74, 75] all attempting
prompt optimization for text-to-image generation. Even the Infinity paper [21], utilizes prompt
rewriting in their experiments, though without any direct optimization.
5.3.2
The Economics of Compute: Optimal and Adaptive Allocation
Adaptive verification frameworks. My results show a clear trade-off between fast preference-based
verifiers and slower reasoning-focused models. This motivates the development of an adaptive
verification framework, as conceptualized in Fig. 5.1. In this system, a lightweight prompt classifier,
such as NVIDIA’s DeBERTa-based [76] complexity classifier1 would first analyze an input’s
compositional complexity and route it to the most appropriate verifier, for example ImageReward
for simple attribute tasks, and LLaVA-OneVision for complex spatial or numerical reasoning.
Furthermore, my findings on temperature tuning (Sec. 4.4.1) suggest this framework could also be
extended to adapting the sampling parameters. This would create a hybrid system that optimizes
the performance-per-compute trade-off on a per-prompt basis.
Compute-optimal scaling of inference. This work focuses on the effectiveness of search for a
fixed model size. A critical open question, inspired by work on compute-optimal training [11], is to
formalize the compute-optimal allocation between model size and inference-time search. Given a
fixed total compute budget, is it better to use a larger base model with a small search budget, or a
smaller, faster base model with a more extensive search? What about the verification framework
(Fig. 5.1)? Answering this would provide a principled framework for co-designing models and their
inference algorithms, moving beyond the current paradigm of scaling them independently.
5.3.3
Interactive and Multi-Turn Generation
From static prompts to interactive editing. The current paradigm for all strategies is a static,
one-shot generation from a single prompt. A powerful and more user-centric future direction is to
develop search strategies for interactive, multi-turn editing. This would require a fundamentally
new type of verifier that operates on differentials. Instead of verifying a single image against a
single prompt, this model would need to evaluate the quality of an edit, taking the previous state
as context: V (pold, iold, pnew, inew). Such a verifier would learn to reward changes that accurately
reflect the user’s new instructions (e.g., “now make the car blue”) while preserving the quality of
the previous image. Successfully developing this would enable a new class of intuitive and powerful
creative tools that allow users to collaboratively refine images with the model through dialogue.

5.4.1
Positive Implications and Advancing the Field
A new paradigm for image generation scaling. The central implication of this work is that it
provides strong evidence for a paradigm that complements the dominant approach of scaling model
parameters. My results show that a smaller model, when paired with intelligent and computationally
intensive inference-time search, can achieve superior gains in compositional tasks compared to
a substantially larger model. This suggests that progress in generative models need not come
solely from building ever-larger models, but can also emerge from developing more sophisticated
“thinking” processes (smarter inference algorithms). This opens a new direction for research where
the co-design of models and their inference strategies can lead to more efficient and capable systems.
Democratizing access to high-quality generation. A direct consequence of this new paradigm is
the potential to democratize access to state-of-the-art generative capabilities. By demonstrating that
smaller models can be elevated to higher performance levels through search, this work provides
a path for researchers, artists, and smaller organizations, who may lack the resources to train or
deploy massive frontier models, to still achieve excellent results. This can help level the playing
field and foster broader innovation in the generative modeling ecosystem.
5.4.2
Risks and Ethical Considerations
Amplification of verifier bias. A significant risk of highly effective search strategies is their
potential to massively amplify the inherent biases of the verifier models used for guidance. These
verifiers are imperfect proxies for human values, trained on specific distributions of data that may
contain cultural, stylistic, or representational biases. A powerful search algorithm will relentlessly
optimize for a verifier’s preferences, “hacking” it to find outputs that maximize the score. If widely
adopted, this could lead to generated content becoming more homogenous and systematically
perpetuating the underlying biases of the reward models at a massive scale.
Environmental cost and the rebound effect. While my methods are more compute-efficient for
achieving a certain level of quality, their very effectiveness could lead to a “rebound effect” on total
energy consumption. By making it feasible to generate hundreds of candidates to find one perfect
image, these techniques may encourage a massive increase in the total volume of computation
performed. This paradox, where greater efficiency leads to greater overall consumption, highlights
the need to consider the entire system’s energy footprint, not just algorithmic efficiency in isolation.
Accelerating high-fidelity malicious content. The improved compositional control demonstrated
in this work is a double-edged sword. The same capability that allows the model to correctly
place three specific objects in a scene also lowers the barrier for creating highly specific and
convincing misinformation. The ability to precisely follow a complex, malicious prompt is a
critical component for generating photorealistic fake images for harmful purposes, from political
disinformation to targeted harassment. As these methods improve, the potential for misuse grows in
tandem, underscoring the need for robust detection and responsible deployment practices.

Conclusion
Inference-time search has emerged as a powerful paradigm for enhancing the reasoning and task-
solving abilities of Large Language Models. However, translating this success to the domain of
image generation has proven challenging, with leading continuous diffusion models showing limited
gains from guided search strategies. This thesis addresses this critical gap. I have demonstrated that
the discrete, sequential nature of autoregressive models provides a uniquely suitable foundation for
these search techniques, establishing guided search as a powerful and compute-efficient method for
dramatically improving the compositional quality of generated images.
My experiments provide comprehensive evidence for this thesis. The central finding is that an
autoregressive model’s architectural compatibility with search can overcome a massive deficit in
scale. My results show that a 2B parameter model, guided by beam search, not only achieves superior
quality with 73% fewer function evaluations than random sampling, but ultimately surpasses the
absolute compositional performance of a 12B parameter diffusion model in every tested category.
This outcome is driven by a 2× larger performance gain from search, demonstrating that for these
tasks, architectural choice is more impactful than a six-fold increase in parameters. Furthermore,
my work elucidates the critical role of the verifier, showing clear trade-offs between speed and
capability, and revealing how search parameters like temperature can amplify a verifier’s inherent
strengths and weaknesses.
Despite these strong results, I acknowledge key limitations that frame this work. The entire process
remains fundamentally constrained by the imperfection and inherent biases of the verifier models,
whose flaws can be amplified by the very search processes they are meant to guide. Moreover, while
strategies like beam search are vastly more efficient than random search, the absolute computational
cost of performing deep search remains a practical barrier for real-time applications.
Ultimately, this thesis demonstrates that the paradigm of inference-time scaling can be successfully
translated from language to vision, with the right architectural foundation. By showing that a 2B
model can be guided to outperform a model six times its size, this work establishes that the path
to more capable and efficient image generation lies in the co-design of models and their inference
algorithms. As the field’s focus shifts from pure pattern replication to compositional reasoning, the
ability to “think harder” through search may prove more valuable than simply being bigger, making
an architecture’s amenability to search a critical factor for future progress.

Scaling Autoregressive Image Generation Through Inference-Time Search

==PAGE:50==
While inference-time scaling through search has revolutionized Large Language Models, translating these gains
to image generation has proven elusive. Recent attempts to apply search strategies to continuous diffusion models
have shown limited benefits, with simple random sampling often performing better. This thesis demonstrates
that the discrete, sequential nature of autoregressive image models provides the missing foundation for effective
search in visual generation. I present a comprehensive study of how classic search algorithms, particularly
beam search, can dramatically improve autoregressive text-to-image generation.
Through experiments on DrawBench and T2I-CompBench++, I show that beam search achieves superior image
quality while requiring only 54% of the function evaluations compared to random search. On compositional
tasks, my approach drives improvements of up to 17.4 percentage points, with an average gain of 11.3 p.p.
across all categories. Remarkably, a 2B parameter autoregressive model equipped with beam search achieves
gains 2× larger than those of search applied to a 12B parameter diffusion model, allowing the 6× smaller model
to achieve superior final performance in every category and demonstrating that architectural choice matters
more than scale for enabling effective search.
To understand these efficiency gains, I conduct systematic ablation studies revealing why this advantage exists:
the discrete token space enables early pruning of unpromising paths and computational reuse. However, attempts
to verify intermediate visual states for further efficiency gains face fundamental challenges. My analysis of
verifier dynamics shows clear trade-offs between speed and capability, with lightweight models sufficient for
attribute binding but heavyweight vision-language models necessary for complex spatial reasoning.
This work establishes a new paradigm for autoregressive image generation where progress comes not from
larger models but from smarter inference. By demonstrating that models can “think harder” through search, I
open a path toward more efficient and accessible high-quality image generation.
