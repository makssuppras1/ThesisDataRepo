Abstract
This project investigated the possibility of using reinforcement learning in sewer systems,
preventing negative effects of the formation and emission of hydrogen sulphide. The re-
search was conducted using a Simulink model, simulating an artificial sewer system using
the end-of-pipe solution for injection of iron sulphate, precipitating sulphide in the waste
water.
The focus of this study was to compare the performance of reinforcement learning agents
quantitatively with appropriate baselines and alternative control methods. Therefore, a
new reward function was derived and was used as a performance metric for comparing the
different methods being tested. The reinforcement learning methods tested in this study
consist of both tabular and deep Q-learning. The former being applicable when the con-
tinuous signals from the sewer system are discretised. The signals included into the state
were chosen based on the derived reward function, and a crude time series analysis of their
correlation structure. The linear quadratic regulator, a common control approach, was
applied by modelling the signals based on the results of the correlation structures. The
baselines were generated by simulating and measuring the performance when injecting a
constant flow of iron sulphate into the waste water pipe, relative to the flow of the waste
water itself.
Bugs in the simulation were revealed during the the project and two out of three were
resolved. The third bug was unfortunate as it introduced uncertainties onto all the per-
formances.
The results from this study show the difficulty of outperforming the generated base-
lines. The best constant baseline performance was −153.06, while the highest achieved
performance was −151.15, obtained by testing pre-defined control strategies. The best
Q-learning result was −151.53, but, since the reward did not converge, it was concluded
that Q-learning did not succeed in beating the best constant baseline. A multi-armed
bandit, a reinforcement learning method which does not depend on any state, proved to
be capable of learning the best constant baseline action within ten training days. It was
not expected that the unresolved bug was the reason for not outperforming the baselines.
The overall discouraging results verify that it is hard to apply reinforcement learning
to control problems. A more thorough analysis of the simulation is required if the aim
is to implement a reinforcement learning approach in the real world. The conclusions
suggest that further testing is needed to identify under which conditions the simulation
is actually controllable: When can the baselines be surpassed consistently?
If this is
answered sufficiently, and further realistic conditions imposed on the simulation show that
the system is controllable, one could aim to use transfer learning to reduce convergence
time for a real system.

Introduction
The requirements of having an efficient cleaning process of waste water have led to merging
smaller sewer systems into larger networks [1]. A small part of such a network is depicted
in Figure 1.1. The purpose of these networks is to collect and transport waste water from
cities and land districts to a waste water treatment plant. When having to transport waste
water over large distances, gravitational pipes are not sufficient, and parts of the system
need to consist of pressurised pipes. The conditions in the pressurised pipes can lead to
several problems; these problems are related to the formation of sulphide in a biofilm
on the inner surface of the pressurised pipe, due to microbial and chemical processes [1]
[2]. The formation of sulphide requires anaerobic conditions which are frequent when the
pressurised pipes stretch across large distances.

 The temperature in the system.

After sulphide has been produced in the biofilm it can be transported further downstream
by the waste water and thus into the end pit, in which hydrogen sulphide (H2S) can be
emitted from the waste water phase into the pit atmosphere. Emission of H2S might cause
the following negative effects:
 Odour complaints from neighbouring households.
 Degradation of the pit, the following downstream pipe(s), and other objects such as
metal ladders, electronics connected to the pit, etc.
These effects are based on emission of H2S in the end pit or the following downstream
gravitational pipe, as depicted in Figure 1.1, and are very dependent on the physical
system and its location. Especially, the former depends heavily on whether or not the
end pit is located in the countryside or in an urban area. The latter depends on several
factors, one of them being what material the pipes are made of.
Concrete pipes are
vulnerable to exposure of H2S, as sulphuric acid can be produced if oxygen is available:
H2S + 2O2 →H2SO4
(1)
which is usually the case in the end pit and the gravitational pipe following the pressurised
pipe [1] [2] (see Figure 1.1). A simple stoichiometry of the reaction between the sulphuric
acid and the concrete pipe wall could be written as follows [2]:
H2SO4 + CaCO3
(Cement)
→H2O + CO2 + CaSO4
(Gypsum)
(2)
If plastic pipes are used, then no corrosion of such pipes occur, but this will make the
odour problem even worse. This is due to an effect based on conservation of mass, and/or
the problem is moved further downstream to another part of the sewer that might still
consist of concrete pipes. The price of replacing a pipe is not insignificant and is affected
by the physical location as well. For instance, it is cheaper to make such a replacement
in a field compared with below street level in a city. These considerations will be the
foundation for the choice and design of a reward function in section 3.
The emission of H2S can be reduced if considerations are taken during the construction
phase of the sewer, as sewer drops and turbulence will emit more H2S from the waste
water phase into gas form [2]. But if such precautions have not been taken, or if being
insufficient, other options for reducing the emission of H2S exist. The chemical equilibrium
of how much H2S is in gas form is given in (3) [1] [2]:
H2S(g) −−⇀

The equilibrium is pushed to the right when increasing the pH in the waste water. This
method is efficient but have a few drawbacks [1]:
 The problem is moved if the pH increases further downstream.
 A high pH can be a problem for the treatment plant.

Another method is to utilise that hydrogen sulphide reacts as a weak acid with heavy
metals, and thus precipitate is produced which helps bind sulphide in the waste water [1]
[2]:
Fe2+ + HS−→FeS + H+
(4)
In this project, iron sulphate (FeSO4) will be the source of Fe2+.
Drawbacks of this
method is that it does not work for low pH values, and that the method itself decreases
the pH value. Thus, for practical reasons, this method is combined with adding base,
keeping the pH value in the desired range.
This project will focus on the control approach, namely adding heavy metals to control
the H2S emission. The injection of heavy metals into the system has been done previously
in one of the pits to the left in Figure 1.1 [2]. This causes problems if waste water coming
from the other pit contains a large concentration of H2S, and can lead to a very high
emission in the end pit. Therefore, it is interesting to add the heavy metals further into
the pressurised pipe, after the two pipes have been merged into one. This solution is
called ’end-of-pipe’. The controller/agent in the figure is illustrating such a solution. A
control approach is defined commonly as having an objective in terms of a cost function
which is to be minimised based on a set of sequential decisions. One can optimise over
such a cost function if having a model for the dynamical system:
˙x(t) = f(x(t), u(t), p1, t) + e1(t)
(5)
y(t) = g(x(t), p2, t) + e2(t)
(6)
where x is the underlying states of the system, ˙x is the temporal derivative of the states,
u is the control variable, y is the observed/measured state of the system, p is parameters,
and e is stochastic noise. The states in the sewer system are the concentrations for the
chemical components that affect H2S, and H2S itself. The dynamics in f are very complex
and are hard to engineer in a real physical system. The goal for Grundfos is to be able to
control such a system optimally at several locations for customers, perhaps having slightly
different preferences with respect to how much odour and deterioration of the sewer pipes
they might tolerate, while minimising the amount of added heavy metals. In a tradi-
tional control approach, an engineer is often required to tune parameters in a dynamical
model to achieve the best possible correspondence with the physical system, and hence
use model-based control strategies, e.g. model predictive control. This is intractable and
is why reinforcement learning is considered as an alternative approach to being able to
control and suppress the emission of H2S from sewer systems.
Reinforcement learning (RL) is based on the concept of learning by interacting with an
environment where the overall goal in RL is to maximise future reward [3]. The learning
can start from scratch without knowing the system dynamics, and the agent attempts to
learn an optimal policy based on its experiences by receiving the reward feedback pro-
vided by the environment. The way an agent is interacting with its surroundings is that
it finds itself in a given state, st, from where it will choose an action, at, with respect
to its current policy, πt. This will yield a reward, rt+1 and a new state st+1, and so on.
The policy being followed must face the trade-off between exploring and exploiting the
current knowledge of the agent. The reward can be either deterministic or stochastic and
it depends on the action taken and/or the new state. Also, there are cases where the agent
does not have access to all the underlying states from the system, but only a subset of
5 of 100
==PAGE:7==
these. A system like this is called partially observed and relates to the y equation from the
control part above. Although there are different notations in control and RL problems,
they can have a great many things in common; they both depend on underlying states,
they have controllable/manipulative actions, and they optimise over a cost function or
reward function. The reward function can be defined as minus the cost function. A great
challenge in the system is that there is a significant and non-constant delay from taking
an action to the time it is measurable, see the yellow logger in the end pit in Figure 1.1.
A simulation tool is required for a proof-of-concept phase in a project like this, since the
learning time in a real physical system is impractically long. Furthermore, there are eco-
nomical and health related risks if failing to suppress the emission of H2S. Grundfos has
developed such a simulation in Simulink [4] which will be the foundation for this entire
project. The simulation has not been verified quantitatively versus a real system, since
it is hard to tune parameters within the complex model, but hopefully learning from the
simulation tool can give indications of what might and might not work in practice. Fur-
thermore, if the simulation succeeds in generalising data from the real world nicely, then
transfer learning methods might be considered to reduce convergence time drastically in
a real system. Hopefully, this will be the case, as RL methods have proved previously to
require lots of data to converge [5] [6] [7].
Before this project started, Grundfos had made an initial test on whether or not this sys-
tem could be controlled by an RL approach. The indications from this test were that it
seemed possible, since the reward converged to a constant. A great issue was the simula-
tion time required to obtain such convergence. Furthermore, the results were made using
a bugged simulation, and the chosen optimisation problem was based on the intuition of
suppressing high emission of H2S without adding too much iron sulphate. The mathemat-
ical expression for this reward function was hard to tune and relate directly to a physical
system, though. Due to these issues, no performance baselines of controllers/agents were
available for comparing the results produced in this project. The research questions posed
for this project were defined as follows:
 How can the performance of a controller be quantified?
 What are the limitations of the currently available approach?
 Are there benefits of using an adaptive linear controller as opposed to the current
approach considered by Grundfos?
The contributions made in this project are listed in the following:
 Fixing and uncovering bugs in the simulation based on an analysis of the input-
output relations (see section 2).
 Deriving a new reward function formulation based on an economical approach for
maintaining the sewer network. This new reward function is tunable as one can
adjust how important the H2S suppression is versus the cost of adding iron sulphate
(see section 3.2).
 Suggesting a method for testing different policies (see section 4.5).
 Producing constant baseline results for comparing different control strategies and
reinforcement learning methods (see section 5.1).

2.1
Qualitative Description of Underlying Model
The purpose of the simulation is to model the change of concentration for several chemical
components, including sulphide. The general convection-diffusion equation, for a scalar
concentration, c:
∂c(x, t)
∂t
= ∇x · (D(x, t)∇c(x, t)) −∇x · (q(x, t)c(x, t)) + R(x, t)
(7)
is a partial second order differential equation. In this general form, the states, x can be
multidimensional; D is the diffusivity; q is the velocity field at which the concentration,
c, follows; R is the source/sink term, depending on whether it is positive or negative,
respectively.
The Simulink model has a state vector c ∈R10; one element being the total sulphide
and another one Fe. The other states are related to the chemical and microbiological
processes. The total sulphide is defined as:
s(−II) = cH2S + cHS−
(8)
and will be used interchangeably with ’sulphide’ throughout the report.
The model in Simulink is more simple than (7), as it is a one-dimensional convection
equation:
∂c(x, t)
∂t
= q(t)∂c(x, t)
∂x
+ R(x, t, c(x, t), q(t))
(9)
9 of 100
==PAGE:11==
assuming an incompressible flow and no diffusion. The partial differential equation (9)
is then discretised by splitting each section of the pressurised pipe into n elements. One
such section could be Main Pipe 3. By equipping (9) with initial conditions, c(x, 0) =
c0 ∀x ∈S, the problem is defined.
The elements in R are coupled rate equations denoting the formation or deformation of a
chemical component on the inner pipe wall or in the waste water. Furthermore, the rate
equations in R depend on physical parameters such as the temperature, pH, flow velocity,
etc. [4] [2] [1]. The rate equations are based on the theory presented in [2]. The actual
equations from the Simulink model are listed in the internal document from Grundfos [4].
They are not explicitly listed here due to confidentiality.
One major discrepancy between a real waste water system and the simulated model, is
that the Simulink model ’measures’ the sulphide (total sulphide) concentration directly
in the waste water (see the yellow star in Figure 2.3). For a real system, however, a logger
is installed in the end pit, and therefore measures the concentration of H2S in gas form,
also depicted in Figure 2.3. As briefly described in the introduction, there is a non-trivial
relation between the sulphide concentration in the waste water and the H2S (gas) concen-
tration in the end pit, e.g. depending on turbulence, etc.
The feedback to a controller or an agent is delivered by sending a time series measuring
the H2S concentration in the end pit. This could be six hours, for instance, denoted as
’step hours’. The measurements are collected using a fixed sampling rate, one measure-
ment every 15 seconds, yielding 1440 data points for one time series (6 step hours). Later
in this report, it is tested if reducing the step hours to one hour gives quicker and more
flexible learning and control. A potential problem with a reinforcement learning agent
can be if it misinterprets a bad action as being good. This is caused by the significant
and non-constant delay for the feedback reward received from the system.
Data produced from the Simulink model have been compared with data gathered from
a physical system (measured by a logger), and the tendencies match qualitatively. From
what has been communicated from Grundfos during this project, it is hard to make a
direct comparison between the simulation and a real system. This is due to:
 The measurement methods being significantly different.
 The parameters in the simulation model needing to be tuned with respect to data
from a real system.

2.3
System Outputs and System Dynamics Debugging
When the seed problem was resolved, the state outputs from the simulation were anal-
ysed to verify that the outputs were in correspondence with the physical understanding
of what should happen when injecting iron sulphate into the system. The initial results
are illustrated in Figure 2.7. By looking at the total sulphide signal, one can see that it
responds to the injection of iron sulphate, but a critical observation is that the curves for
’Iron’ and ’Action Setting’ are very alike. Furthermore, iron sulphide, the product from
the chemical reaction between the sulphide and iron, stays at a concentration of zero;
recall the chemical reaction (4). This led to further analysis of the C++ functions and,
by doing so, two changes in the code were applied. The results are shown in Figure 2.8
where the signals act as expected.
One can notice that the system has not reached a steady state configuration in the end

Quite late in the project it was discovered, based on the k-nearest-neighbour plots in
section 5.1, that there was an issue with the waste water flow measured in Main Pipe 2 at
the agent. When performing simulations with identical inflow seeds and identical action
settings, the flow was the same across all runs2, but if another action setting scheme was
used, small deviations occurred. This was investigated quite thoroughly by extracting the
waste water flow from all the pipes, and comparisons of input-output from function-boxes
were made. This tested the built-in map of inputs-to-output when two pipes are merged:
qout = qin,1 + qin,2
(14)
The flow expression was tested for numerical discrepancies across the function block in
Simulink, responsible for merging the pipes ’Connect 1’ and ’Connect 2’, but no mistakes
occurred during these tests. In the end, it was concluded that it had to be the action
setting that caused the unexpected feedback in the system. A physical explanation could
be if a downstream pipe acted as a bottleneck, i.e. if the flow velocity gets too high, but
no justification for this was found in either the Matlab initialisation script or the C++
code files. Nonetheless, it was tested whether a bottleneck could be the reason for the
unexpected feedback. The tests were conducted on the results from the constant baselines
presented in section 5.1, where a different constant action setting is used throughout each
simulation. If a bottleneck is apparent, then one would expect the accumulated flow in
Main Pipe 2 to be less, compared with a simulation using a lower action setting. The
results obtained when testing this hypothesis can be seen in Figure 2.9. In the top plot,
the total volume is equal to multiplying the accumulated flow with the time step. By
zooming in on the top plot (see the bottom plot), an indication of the spread can be seen.
The data show five out of 36 different, constant baselines for a single seed3 (see section
5.1). The hypothesis of having a bottleneck is rejected since the coloured curves intersect
several times in the zoom in plot; there is no systematic dispersion due to the different
constant action settings. All the test data are not presented here, but, the main point
being that the problem has not been solved. This can be seen in the top plot in Figure

regarding the replication issues. Note that these flow discrepancies are replicable, but the

3.1
Design of General Reward Function
An issue in designing the reward function for this project is that a reliable model for what
happens at the end pit is not available, by means of how much H2S is emitted at the end
pit. This is due to the fact that the pump flow, transporting the waste water into the end
pit, is mostly an on-off signal, and therefore splashing effects occur at the end pit. Such
turbulence will release more H2S from the waste water, which is why the shape of the pit
is also of importance to reduce such emission. This is one of the reasons why Grundfos
settled on using (15) in the proof-of-concept phase.
The general overall cost function is defined as the extra cost of money to maintain a
physical system. The cost is assumed to be additive and is defined as:
C := Ccorrosion + Codour + CFe
(16)
R := −C
(17)
The corrosion cost is the cost for having to replace a downstream gravity pipe section
and/or the end pit5. The odour cost arises when neighbouring households call the local
municipality and complain, at the cost of human resources. Finally, the cost of Fe is the
price of buying FeSO4 including its transportation from the distributor.
For a specific site/location/system, such a cost function’s individual components can be
modelled and estimated, and, by doing so, a defined optimisation problem can be achieved.
One can then choose whether it should be a stationary problem or a time varying problem6.
3.2
Simplification of the General Reward Function
To choose and make use of a realistic but also relevant reward function, simplifications
and assumptions of the terms in (16) must be performed.
The odour term in (16) is now elaborated slightly. To disturb and bother neighbours, a
certain amount of H2S must be emitted. This concentration will then spread out due to
diffusion and convection, in terms of wind. But the number of complaints will also vary,
as individuals might react differently to odour. For example, they might not complain as
often initially as they would in case of an ongoing problem. If this assumption is taken,
then consecutive H2S emissions must be considered in the odour term.
Generally, it is hard to predict the dynamics of this term quantitatively without any data,
leading to the simplification of the cost function, (16), by omitting the term concerning
odour. Furthermore, it is assumed that the prices are stationary.
This section thus redefines the cost function to be based on corrosion and injected ferrous
iron:

4.2
Deep Q-Learning With OpenAI
Tabular Q-learning is not applicable for a system with continuous states if the state dis-
cretisation is omitted. Instead, a function approximator such as a neural network can be
used. Deep Q-learning refers to using a deep neural network as a function approximator
to map states and actions to a Q-value. Deep Q has been used to beat professional human
game testers in Atari games [5]. The network structure in [5] takes in the states as input
but gives an output for each valid action. The output is the score for the respective action
and can be used to choose an actions according to the policy, π. Double Q-learning has
been shown to reduce maximisation bias and improve performance [6]. Prioritised expe-
rience replay has been proven to make the learning phase more efficient by putting more
emphasis on important transitions instead of sampling uniformly from the data buffer [7].
The algorithm being used in section 5.5 is the deepq algorithm from OpenAI [14]. It
was wrapped into the code set-up used in this project, by means of the simulation envi-
ronment, the training set-up for different hyperparameters, and the testing of extracted
policies. The deepq algorithm was executed with many of the default settings, includ-
ing the duelling network structure and double Q-learning. Some of the experiments were
done with prioritised experience replay, but most of the results in section 5.5 are produced
without it. The settings used for each result-run are listed in a table within the respective
result section.
Since the number of training steps in the simulation environment used in this project is
far lower than environments from OpenAI Gym [15], the learning was started after one
day of simulation instead of 1000 training steps. The update of the Q-target network was
updated every 5th day. The tabular methods from above produce results using a fixed ε,
but here it is linearly annealed from 1 to a pre-defined value over the first 80 % of the
training steps; the pre-defined value is listed in the respective hyperparameter table in
the result sections.
The state inputs being used to produce the results will start by being the discrete states,
using state discretisation (see section 4.1.3). This is done to compare the tabular Q-
learning methods with the deep Q approach, see section 5.5.1. After the comparison,
discretised time and continuous sulphide states are being used (see section 5.5.2). There
are only two inputs to the deep Q network if time bucket (TB) is 1, one for the time and
one for the sulphide state (see section 4.1.3). On the other hand, if time TB is larger
than one, the number of inputs are 1+TB, one for the time and TB sulphide states. The

4.5
Performance Evaluation
One of the objectives for this project was to come up with a way of evaluating the per-
formance of different strategies and approaches for controlling the waste water system
introduced above. This is critical for comparing the results produced by e.g. tabular
Q-learning and deep Q-learning.
The reward function derived in section 3.2 will be used as a metric when making a per-
formance evaluation of a given policy. Recall that the policy denotes the strategy for how
much and when to inject FeSO4 into Main Pipe 2 (see Figure 1.1 or Figure 2.2). The
following criteria are used for evaluating a policy:
1. The accumulated reward obtained when testing the policy.
2. The learning/convergence time.
3. The stability of the learning curve.
4. The adaptability in case of a change in the system.
The matter of adaptability was not in focus in this project, as beating the baseline turned
out to be a major challenge itself. The simulation set-up is split into two parts:
1. Training a policy.
2. Testing a policy.
The training phase is done for a pre-defined number of training days where the policy
is saved each day. The training phase needs to trade-off exploration and exploitation
making it tedious to rely on the performance for this phase only. This calls for external
simulation tests by using the derived policies greedily. By doing this, one will get a more
clear indication of the capabilities of the obtained policy, and avoids a subtle dependence
on the Python seed, for instance. Testing a policy in this way can only be done when
simulating the system, and not in an online learning scenario in a physical system. It is
merely meant as a way of comparing several algorithms in a fairly manner.

the constant baseline. The states are specifically the sulphide signal and the waste water
flow signal. The third part of the result section (see section 5.4 and 5.5) presents results
for tabular Q-learning and deep Q-learning, both using state information from the second
part. The fourth, and final, part of the result section tests the LQR controller using
regularisation (see section 5.6).
The action setting resolution for the constant baselines is used across the individual Q-
learning approaches to make fair comparisons. In the end a better action setting resolution
is used in the deepq algorithm as it turns out to perform better than the tabular methods.

In this part, the moving average is set to zero. The time states are encoded in four inter-

setting is altered based on whether a certain time interval is set to be important. Note
that for k > 0 the iron sulphate injection will be increased, whereas it will be decreased
for k < 0. The four time intervals are now each being treated as being important (’1’) or
not important (’0’). A sequence of such four numbers defines the policy. Since the action
setting is based on information from the previous state, a sequence of 0100 will indicate
that the action from 12:00-18:00 will be adjusted according to k. This gives 24 = 16
combinations that have to be tested. These combinations are plotted in Figure 5.22.
The only sequence yielding better performance for positive k is 0001, which corresponds
to increasing the injection of iron sulphate during the night (00:00-06:00). 0110 shows
that it is suitable to turn down the action setting from 12:00-00:00, whereas 1100 sug-
gests turning down the action setting from 06:00-18:00. The results thus indicate that it
is favourable to keep the action setting higher during the night compared to midday. This
can be due to the fact that the waste water flow is larger during the day, meaning that
the cost of injection iron sulphate gets higher compared to the cost of the sulphide.
From the analysis put forward in the section on correlation structure and feature engi-
neering, it seems as though there is potential for beating the best constant baseline by
adjusting the action setting according to the underlying states. Though, overall, only min-
imal improvements were achieved when comparing with the best baseline performance.
Due to this, it can be hard to ensure that the performance of these methods actually do
a better job than a constant iron sulphate injection.
Since the waste water flow is non-controllable, and it is periodic in time, it will not be
considered as a state itself, as it is assumed that the structure can be learned through a
time state.

5.5.2
Continuous States and Discrete Actions
The previous section used the deepq algorithm with discrete states as input. The constant
baseline did not got surpassed, and we now turn to continuous states for the first time in
the Q-learning setting.
Discrete Daily Time and Continuous Sulphide
The states being considered in this section consist of both the time and sulphide concen-
tration. The reason for keeping both signals in the states is that the results for Train # 8
did not yield large negative spikes (see Figure 5.36) as was the the case for the correspond-
ing states in fitted Q-learning (see Figure 5.30). The hope by keeping both is that the
agent gets the opportunity to learn underlying dynamics. The sulphide state is now con-
tinuous, but is still compressed from a full time series over the step into a single float value
(by taking the average, see bottom part of section 4.1.3). The time is still discretised and
mapped into a discrete value (daily time setting). The reason for still using discrete time is
that the agent should be able to use the periodicity from the system. The time is discrete
and not continuous since that the feedback signals are received at the same times each day.
In the following, a time bucket setting (TB) of one and six will be considered. Until now,
TB has been equal to one to avoid the explosion in the number of discrete states. The
information given to an agent is the same in the following two cases:
 Step hours is one and time bucket is one.
 Step hours is six one and time bucket is six.
The differences between the two cases are:
 How often the agent gets the information (measuring a state and receiving a corre-
sponding reward).
 How often the agent is allowed to choose a new action.
Both relate to the choice of step hours. The time bucket option is therefore a way of
getting a finer resolution of the signal(s) from a given step in the system. This enables
giving the agent a timely trend in the signal(s). The hyperparameters are listed in Table
18.

5.6
Linear Quadratic Regulator
The final method to be considered is the LQR with regularisation. This method makes
use of a continuous scalar sulphide state and calculates an optimal15 continuous action
setting. The approach is described in detail in section 4.4.
The LQR method requires a linear model for the sulphide state and a prediction model
for the waste water flow. The reason for them being separated is that the waste water
flow is not affected by the action setting16. The goal for the LQR controller is to exploit
the models and use them to optimise the action setting and thus achieve a low cost (the
same as a high reward). The overall goal for the modelling part is then to be able to
predict the obtained reward based on the sulphide and the waste water flow.
The modelling was done by following the Box-Jenkins procedure [16]. The model fit-
ting process, one step predictions, and forecasts were done by using the Python library
’statsmodels.tsa.stattools’. Firstly, the modelling of the waste water flow will be presented
which yields acceptable results. Secondly, the sulphide modelling and its results is pre-
sented, but the overall dynamics are not captured by the linear model. When having
these, a test of being able to predict the reward is being conducted, and finally the LQR
is used to make performance results for comparison with the methods already presented.

The overall question at this point is why the system does not seem to allow a significantly
better performance than the best constant baseline. The question goes back to ’what
is significant’? This is hard to answer since an optimal controller of the system is not
available to yield the best possible reward, as previously mentioned. But from the results
presented for the methods from above it is clear that learning a policy from scratch ca-
pable of beating the best constant baseline is surprisingly hard. The pre-defined control
strategies (see section 5.3.2 through 5.3.4) and the LQR method (see section 5.6) rely on
conducting tests in a hyperparameter space which is not possible in a real physical system.
The issue for the Q-learning methods is that the reward curves from the full convergence
plots were unstable (see section 5.4 and 5.5). When the deep Q-learning method managed
to surpass the best constant baseline performance, it was not consistent in all following
steps (see Figure 5.41).
At this point, let us take a step back and go through the underlying arguments for training
the Q-learning agents as has been done. The training was chosen to be done continuously
for a pre-defined number of training days. This was done by using the same seed as for
Test run # 1; as the agent was trained further into the future compared with the test, it
was not considered a problem. This has not been rejected, as Test run # 1 has not been
an outlier with respect to its performance when comparing with the other four test runs.
One could also have performed the training on the first 15 days only and then reset the
environment and repeat this procedure (change the setting to be episodic). This approach
was not considered since this would not be the case in a real physical system and it might
over-fit the policy to the dynamics within these 15 days. The goal for the agent is to
learn a good policy by generalising the system dynamics it encounters. It might be the
case that initialising the simulation for one day only is not adequate (if the conditions in
the sewer pipes have not reach realistic values). But no observable indications have been
detected on this matter. The choice of initialising the system for one day only was made
based on looking at the signals from the replication issues section (see Figure 2.8).
The reason for testing the two different step hours settings is that it is expected that the
agent is forced to choose a constant injection setting of iron sulphate if step hours is too
high. The results indicated that a step hours of one was too low due as the performances
were lower than for step hours of six generally. The thoughts on this matter is that each
individual sewer system might have a setting of step hours that is more suitable when
91 of 100
==PAGE:93==
control the system. Step hours should allow the agent to respond to the dynamics of the
system, while still assigning the feedback reward to the relevant measurements of hydro-
gen sulphide. This goes back to the problem of the retention time of waste water in the
sewer system (the significant and non-constant delay in the system).
The most important question is whether the system actually is really ’controllable’ based
on the states being feed to the agent during the learning phase. Several different con-
figurations for the state have been tested on the Q-learning methods, but no significant
improvement compared to the best constant baseline performance was obtained. One
could add more history of the sulphide signal as a state (not just a compressed value of
the time series from the previous time step). A problem in doing so is regarding to the
initialisation of the system, since it is done for one day only. This was circumvent for the
flow of waste water in the LQR method since it does not depend directly on the action
setting. But the initialisation period should be extended if adding more sulphide history
to the state. This is also the reason for not doing it in this report, as it requires a huge
rework of all results.
Imposing more realistic conditions on the artificial sewer system can be done by:
 Varying the concentration of the incoming waste water (this has been kept constant,
only the amount of waste water has been varied).
 Including a rain inflow to the inflow pits (this increases the waste water flow but
also decreases the concentration within the waste water).
 Accounting for a decrease in pH when injecting iron sulphate.
 Adding seasonality for the temperature.
But since the system cannot be controlled properly in its current configuration it does
not make sense to move on and add more realistic conditions to the simulation. A way
of analysing and backtracking why the artificial sewer system is not controllable, can be
to simplify the simulation until it is actually controllable. This can be done by removing
the lower branch of Figure 1.1 and thus end up having a single inflow pit. Furthermore,
the noise on the inflow can be stripped as well to see whether this improves the ability
in controlling the system. The magnitude of the noise has not been chosen based on a
physical system. Either of these suggestions can be done individually or in conjunction.
Note that new baselines are required if changing the system.
As is quite clear at this point, many issues have to be dealt with before considering ap-
plying reinforcement learning in a real physical sewer system. If the simulation fails at
generalising real world sewer systems it seems very unlikely that this will be the case.
Choosing the appropriate hyperparameters, state representations, and available action
settings seem practically impossible if such a simulation is not available.
If the above mentioned issues are resolved and the simulation complexity is generalising
a real physical sewer system adequately, one might apply transfer learning and apply an
agent in the real world.

7
Conclusions
This project was initiated to investigate the possibilities of controlling an artificial sewer
system implemented in Simulink.
The objective in controlling the system is to avoid
the negative effects caused by emission of hydrogen sulphide from the waste water. The
action to avoid such emission is to inject iron sulphate into the pressurised pipe using
the end-of-pipe solution. The project builds on an initial proof-of-concept test made by
Grundfos, indicating that a reinforcement learning approach enables convergence of the
reward signal to a constant. This initial test was done using a bugged simulation and
another reward function than used for this project.
The question of how to quantify the performance of a controller was pursued by deriving
a new tunable reward function and establishing an external and consistent test procedure.
The reward function was derived based on an economical approach for maintaining a sewer
system from being corroded as a result of emission of hydrogen sulphide. The corrosion
rate function used in the reward function was found in an empirical research paper for
corrosion of concrete sewer pipes. The reward function depends on the emitted hydrogen
sulphide, the waste water flow, and the action setting for injecting iron sulphate into the
pressurised pipe. The reward function can be tuned to make the corrosion more expensive
than the price of injecting iron sulphate, and vice versa. The test procedure was chosen
as a methodical approach enabling evaluation of the performance of a controller/agent
acting in the artificial sewer system. The policies tested in the sewer system were set as
greedy, allowing fair comparisons of the different methods. Each method was tested for
its respective policy of injecting iron sulphate for 15 days for five fixed different seeds.
A crucial part of evaluating different methods is to compare with relevant baseline(s).
Such baselines were created by testing several policies, each using a constant injection
of iron sulphate, also referred to as constant baselines. The best constant baseline per-
formance was −153.06 kr. and a multi-armed bandit reinforcement learning method was
shown to be able to learn the best constant action setting within ten days of training.
Most reinforcement learning agents choose an action based on a state of the system. The
signals affecting the reward function were analysed based on the results from the constant
baselines. The analysis consisted of calculating the sample auto-correlation of the signals.
The sulphide signal showed a daily and a weekly structure conditioned on the action set-
ting. The flow of the waste water also yielded a daily and weekly structure, and since it is
not affected by the action setting it was encoded by using time-of-day and/or time-of-week.
Pre-defined control strategies were tested using each signal individually. The best per-
formance when testing for waste water flow dependence was −151.15 kr. whereas it was
−151.66 kr. and −151.86 kr. for sulphide and time dependence, respectively.
Tabular Q-learning methods such as basic Q-learning and fitted Q-learning were trained
and tested on the artificial sewer system. A one-level tiling method was used to map the
continuous signals to discrete states, as required by tabular methods. The highest achiev-
able performance from testing the tabular methods was −153.06 kr., the performance for
the best constant baseline. The policy used to obtain this performance was the constant
action setting policy. Fitted Q-learning using sulphide as the only state yielded the best
93 of 100
==PAGE:95==
performing tabular method.
A deep Q-learning method using deepq, an algorithm from OpenAI, was tested for both
discrete and continuous states separately. By using discrete states, results qualitatively
similar to the ones from tabular methods were generated. The best obtained performance
was once again −153.06 kr. It performed more consistently when both daily time and sul-
phide were included into the state compared with fitted Q-learning, though. When using
continuous states, the first results from Q-learning surpassed the best constant baseline.
The highest performance was −151.53 kr. using a policy that decreased the injection of
iron sulphate at the end of the week. The number of training days were extended, but no
indication of improvements was gained.
The final method tested was the linear quadratic regulator where the derived bilinear re-
ward function was regularised into being quadratic. A linear model of the sulphide signal
was fitted and future predictions of the waste water flow were used in the optimisation
iterations. The highest achieved performance was −151.86 kr. for a set of regularisation
coefficients. Patterns could be observed both within the course of a day and within the
course of a week. The reason for this was the future predictions of the waste water flow.
The linear model for the sulphide was not doing well, meaning that the reward was ob-
tained by only having knowledge of the waste water flow.
The indications from the above mentioned methods are that:
 The injection of iron sulphate tends to be cheaper/more efficient during the night.
 The weekly periodicity of the sulphide signal can be used to decrease the action
setting in low activity periods.
All the methods have shown a high sensitivity to the performance with respect to hyper-
parameters such as the exploration/exploitation trade-off, learning rate, and step hours.
The performance also depends greatly on the resolution and the size of the action space.
The smaller action space tended to learn the constant baseline since it only had one action
setting below the best constant baseline action setting (zero action setting). The larger
action space consisted of a total of 36 actions and made the learning process hard for
the agents, despite the fact that multiple actions were apparent below the best constant
baseline action setting. It seemed as if more of the policies achieved a better performance
than −153.06 kr. when choosing eight actions surrounding the best constant action set-
ting from the large action space.
During the project, flaws within the simulation were discovered. Firstly, there was a bug
preventing the simulation from replicating identical signals even though the set-up was
reused. Secondly, another bug generated signals that did not correspond with the under-
lying physics. These problems were resolved during the project. Thirdly, a final bug was
discovered late in the project; running a simulation for different action schemes resulted
in unexpected feedback to the upstream flow of waste water. This is unfortunate as it
adds a non-quantified uncertainty to all obtained results. That being said, this is not
expected to be the reason for not beating the best constant baseline.
Still, there are many problems and concerns to be resolved before applying a reinforce-
ment learning approach to a real physical sewer system. First of all, the simulation needs
94 of 100
==PAGE:96==
to be reconsidered as there is a bug introduced by the chosen action setting causing un-
certainties for the obtained results. The derived reward function only takes corrosion in
the following gravitational pipe into account, but the end pit is also at risk of corroding
due to turbulence, releasing more hydrogen sulphide from the waste water. The cost of
odour complaints should also be included in the reward function, when applicable. The
problem with classifying if an obtained reward is significantly better than the best con-
stant baseline, is that it is not known how big the reward gap is from the best constant
baseline performance to the best possible performance. This optimal reward can only
be found when having a truly optimal controller. Since the best constant baseline has
not been truly beaten it is paramount to reconsider the approach going forward. The
simulation can be simplified by removing the lower branch of the artificial sewer system
and/or discarding the noise on the inflow. The purpose of doing this is to make sure that
the artificial sewer system is actually controllable.
