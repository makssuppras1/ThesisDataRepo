Abstract
This project seeks to illustrate the properties of the Scenario Based Damage Assessment
(SBDA) method, ﬁrst presented in 2016 [3]. The method was proposed as a way of using
the changes in modal parameters, found in structures exposed to structural changes, to lo-
calize damages. The original paper describing the method bases its damages on changes
in mass. However, changes in stiffness is an even more frequent type of damage. There-
fore, this thesis aims to account for the use of SBDA as a mean of localizing damages in
the form of changes in stiffness.
The research conducted in this thesis is based on both numerical simulations and ex-
perimental testing. First off, the properties of the SBDA method is examined by numerical
testing. This is done by simulating the modal properties of a test specimen with the use of
Finite Element (FE) software. This includes both the damaged and undamaged specimen
required for SBDA.
Then, testing was carried out on a series of small wooden specimens. The damages
induced on these specimens were made by making a small cut, thus changing the stiffness
and the modal parameters of the specimen.
Ultimately, the results of both the numerical and the experimental testing will help
determine the use and limits of the SBDA method, bringing it closer to commercial ap-
plication.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
One of the fundamental pillars of modern society is the civil engineering structures such
as buildings, bridges, wind turbines, drilling platforms and many others. These existing
civil and mechanical structures are, by the nature of time, in constant decay. For many
years this decay is of little importance, but over time, degradation of these structures are
unavoidable. When these structures were designed, they were designed to withstand the
forces of nature and usage through their application for a number of years. Approaching
the limitation of their service years, the structures will be in increasing risk of failure. But
just how close to failure will in many cases remain unknown.
In these cases the engineering ﬁeld of Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) comes of
interest. Through various damage assessment methods it is possible to determine the state
of the structure and assess whether reconstruction or prolonging of service life is more
suitable. In recent years the branch of Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) has gotten
increasing attention as a way of automating SHM. The ﬁeld of OMA is still relatively
new in engineering and despite of its potential, still presents certain difﬁculties. Thus,
new methods of OMA damage detection are regularly being formed. These methods
covers various steps of the damage detection deﬁnition proposed by Rytter[1]. Rytter
describes four levels of damage detection, which are deﬁned as:
• Level 1 Detection - Is the structure damaged?
• Level 2 Localisation - Where is the damage?
• Level 3 Quantiﬁcation - How severe is the damage?
• Level 4 Prognosis - What is the remaining service life of the structure?
Effectively being able to answer these questions proves, yet today, still a challenge.
Among these challenges is the difﬁculty in making accurate and repeatable measurements

on complex structures[2]. Thus, research in the ﬁeld of Structural Health Monitoring is

Much of the work done during this thesis is based on a method presented in a Ph.d.
thesis from 2016 called "Scenario Based Damage Assessment" (SBDA) by Jannick Hansen
[3]. The work presented in this thesis accounts for a new method of localising (level 2),
and partially quantifying (level 3) damages on a simple cantilever structure (a windmill
wing). Apart from a theoretical presentation of the method, the thesis included experi-
mental work with added mass as damage cases.
Wrapping up the original thesis on SBDA, a few suggestions for further work were
made. Among these were, the number of possible scenarios are a very limiting factor
for the accuracy of the method. Solving this issue could greatly increase the ability to
do precise damage localisation. Additionally, the thesis were focused only on changes in
mass as structural changes. It was suggested to look into the case of changes in stiffness,
as this is a very common damage type.
1.1
Scope of Work
The scope of this project is ﬁrst and foremost to demonstrate the possibilities and limita-
tions of the SBDA method as presented by Hansen [3]. In particular, the localisation of
damages as changes in stiffness rather than changes in mass. The change in stiffness is a
very common type of damage, which can occur in many different forms in the world of
construction.
During the work and implementation of SBDA, it is of interest to determine if the
method in some way can be improved. Be it by increasing the accuracy, the robustness or
in some other way make it more efﬁcient.
1.2
Structure of the Report
This report will start with a brief introduction to the main concepts of the Scenario Based
Damage Assessment theory as introduced by Hansen [3]. This introduction will be kept
short, with the sole purpose of enabling the reader to understand the remaining work and
discussion in this thesis. For a detailed account of the SBDA method the reader is referred
to the original thesis by Hansen [3].
Following this, is a presentation of the modiﬁcations made on the original method of
SBDA, as well as a presentation of a new method of using the changes in modal parame-
ters to localise damages.
The theory presented is then tested through various numerical simulations. Adding
errors to the models, will give an indication of the limitations of the method.

Theoretical Background
2.1
Scenario Based Damage Assessment
To use modal parameters in localizing and quantifying damages to a structure, a prior
knowledge of the effects of the perturbation is required. This knowledge can be obtained
by modelling the scenarios using FEM software. For example, cracks and yielding can be
modelled as changes in stiffness. Thus, a relation between the experimentally measured
changes, and the changes imposed in the FEM software can be constructed as
∆c = S∆u
(2.1)
Where ∆c is a column vector containing measured changes, S is a matrix, column-wise
containing the predicted modal parameters with respect to each perturbation scenario,
and ∆u which links the changes of ∆c to a column of S by having non-zero values in the
corresponding entries[3].
∆c can be obtained experimentally and S numerically, which leaves ∆u to be deter-
mined. Solving equation (2.1) for ∆u yields
∆u = S−1∆c
(2.2)
Where ∆u will reveal which modal parameters are affected by the current state of the
experimental specimen, enabling the localization and quantiﬁcation of the damage on the
structure. Successful implementation of this method requires a well constructed and well
conditioned matrix S.

CHAPTER 3
Modiﬁcation of Theory
Through a numerical study, the SBDA method as presented by Hansen [3] proved to have
certain shortages affecting the robustness of the method. These shortages are only affect-
ing the robustness of the method in a pragmatic manner. For theoretical and simulation
purposes the modiﬁcations presented in this section will have little impact on the robust-
ness. However, considering the experimental robustness of the method, the following
shortages and proposed methods of improving them were identiﬁed.
3.1
Number of Modes Required
An apparent issue that arises when one is to apply the theory of SBDA on physical struc-
tures is the difﬁculty in detecting modes. For numerical models, including FE models,
extraction of modal parameters from any given number of modes is quite easy. Restricted
only by the number of DOF’s included in the model. However, these numerical modal
parameters are to be compared to the modal parameters of the physical model which are
much more difﬁcult to detect. The problem arises because the system of equations intro-
duced in equation (2.2) is required to be over-determined. This means that if one were
to consider three different scenarios, four modes are required. If the damage localisation
is to be done with some precision, one might set up 10 or 100 scenarios instead of three.
This would require the identiﬁcation of at least equally many +1 modes, which in practice
is not possible.
The proposed solution to this problem will from here on be referred to as Battle-
Royale-SBDA (BR-SBDA). The main concept of this method is to use the original for-
mulation of SBDA, but only considering two scenarios at a time, rather than considering
all scenarios at once. By doing this the number of modes required to keep the system of
equations over-determined is reduced to minimum three at all times.
The change vector ∆c and the sensitivity matrix S are found similarly to the original
formulation of SBDA. But instead of solving equation 2.2 for the entire system, a set

based on mass change given by Parloo et al. [9]
What is seen in equation (3.12) is that two formulations of determining the dimension-
less frequency change can be utilized. Both the left and right hand side of the equation
can be used to construct the sensitivity matrix S and the change vector ∆c used in equa-
tion (3.3). The pro of the LHS of equation (3.12) is that no FE matrices are required to
determine the frequency changes, thus avoiding the uncertainties associated with using
these.

3.6
Using MAC-values as Scenario Selector
A new method of selecting the most likely scenario is proposed in this section. The
method is devised in collaboration with the main supervisor Rune Brincker. This will be
referred to as the MAC-SBDA method.
Instead of solving the system of equations seen in equation (2.13) and (3.3), the se-
lection of the most probable scenario is based on the Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC)
value. The MAC value describes the closeness of two vectors. Vectors consisting of many
coherent DOF’s are not easily compared. Instead, a statistical indicator such as a corre-
==PAGE:28==
16
CHAPTER 3. MODIFICATION OF THEORY
lation measure can be used. Two vectors a and b are to be considered. The MAC-value
between these two vectors are given as (see Brincker [6])
MAC(a, b) =
|aHb|2
(aHa)(bHb)
(3.13)
The MAC values found will lie in the range 0-1, where 0 corresponds to no consistent
correspondence, and 1 representing consistent correspondence. The MAC value describes
the angle between the two vectors in the relation given as
MAC(a, b) = cos2(θ)
(3.14)
where θ is the generalized angle between the two vectors. Thus if the angle θ = 0
then the MAC-value = 1, corresponding to a full correlation between the two vectors. If
θ = π/2 the MAC-value is equal to 0, meaning no correlation between the two vectors.
These MAC-values are found by comparing the experimentally determined changes
in frequencies, stored in the vector ∆c, with the frequency change vectors created numeri-
cally, stored in the columns of S. The change vector ∆c and Sensitivity matrix S is created
similarly to the SBDA methods, see table 3.1. The individual columns in the sensitivity
matrix S, represents the change in frequency for each scenario generated. Thus the need
for having a matrix disappears as it is no longer needed to solve the system of equations
by the pseudo-inverse of S. For the sake of simplicity, the use of S as a matrix to store all
scenarios is kept, despite it serving no mathematical purpose.
This method of selection has the advantage that the MAC-values are very easily com-
pared between the individual scenarios. As each column of the sensitivity matrix is ex-
tracted and compared to the change vector ∆c, the number and types of scenarios gen-
erated will have no impact on the total outcome of the method. This is in contrary to
the SBDA and BR-SBDA method. These methods are solved collectively as a system of
equations, where the number of modes and scenarios included will affect the value each
scenario.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
The results presented both in the numerical and experimental analysis shows potential for
the method as a means to localise damage. In several cases, the method was able to quite
accurately identify the location of the damage. Even with an FE model of questionable
accuracy the location of the damage could be limited to a speciﬁc section of the structure.
Was the method applied on a structure like an Offshore Wind Turbine (OWT), the loca-
tion of the damage could potentially be reduced from a range of 120m (The full height
of the OWT), to a range of 30m, 15m or even as low as 5m. So even if the method pro-
vides inaccurate results, the potential for saving man hours in the form of physical Non
Destructible Testing (NDT) inspection is huge.
The results did also show that the method is not without its shortcomings. The place-
ment of the damage was shown to have a rather large impact on the ability to accurately
localise the damage. Including scenarios for the top ﬂange of the structure proved to be
a challenge and they were thus not included in the experimental work. This is of course
a limiting factor of the robustness of the method. However, both for the test specimen,
as well as a physical structure of similar geometric design, it could be argued that dam-
ages near the bottom of the structure are more signiﬁcant in terms of structural health
than damages near the top of the column or the ﬂange. The higher stress levels in the
lower parts of the structure makes it more susceptible to damages, as reduction of carry-
ing capacity in this part of the structure makes it vulnerable to collapsing mechanisms.
Thus the loss of accuracy in low stress zones of the structure might not be essential to the
pragmatic use of the method.
Besides the complications of the damage location, there are many more different types
of damages yet to be included. Loose bolts might not behave similarly to cuts in wood
and even the cuts themselves will impact the structure differently depending their orienta-
tion. Through this thesis, only one type of damage was modelled, but for large structures

it should be considered whether more types of scenarios at the same location would be
beneﬁcial.
Another point of discussion of the method in its current formulation is the fact that
the method will always determine a location for the damage. This is present for both
the SBDA and the MAC-SBDA method. Both methods as presented in this thesis bases
the location of the damage on the highest value found between given scenarios. Despite
the quality of the simulated scenarios, one of these will inevitably resemble the vector
∆c more than the others. This will occur no matter the quality of said scenario. This
should lead to extra caution when generating scenarios. If the scenarios generated are too
coarse the changes in frequencies will not resemble the actual damage response and thus
the localisation is likely to fail. When the ﬁneness of the simulated scenario increases,
the changes between scenarios will become smaller. As these changes in frequencies ap-
proach the threshold of natural frequency detection (see appendix C) the risk of identify-
ing wrong damage locations increases. Thus the creation of scenarios becomes a balance
between accuracy and noise.
Not only the quality of the scenarios are important to the quality of the outcome of the
method. All inputs used will have a signiﬁcant impact on the quality of the method. This
includes the scenarios, the overall quality of the FE model and in particular the quality of
the data retrieved experimentally. As seen in the experimental section and mentioned by
Farrar and Worden[2] it is very difﬁcult to continuously perform accurate measurements.
Farrar and Worden mentions it in context of large complex structures, but even for a
simple structure in lab conditions some measurements might still be compromised.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Through this thesis, the method of Scenario Based Damage Assessment was presented
and applied both numerically and experimentally. The aim of the thesis was to illustrate
the possibilities and limitations of the method as it was presented in its original form.
In addition to this, it was of interest to improve the method in terms of robustness or
accuracy if possible.
Through numerical modelling it was shown possible to utilise the method of SBDA
to localise changes in stiffness of an FE model. Using identical reference and damage
models, the localisation was possible with great precision. However, as more realistic
conditions were simulated, the performance of the method decreased. The simulation
of realistic conditions consisted of differences between the Finite Element reference and
damage model (representing the physical model).
To increase the performance and robustness of the method, several modiﬁcations were
introduced. These included, among others, modiﬁcations to the construction of the sen-
sitivity matrix, reducing the number of modes required and an alternative approach to se-
lecting the most probable scenario. The new way of selecting the most probable scenario
used the Modal Assurance Criterion and is thus referred to as the MAC-SBDA method.
The basic principles of the SBDA method were kept; constructing a range of scenarios
and comparing the changes in modal parameters to the changes measured/simulated. But
instead of solving the over-determined system of equations containing the sensitivity ma-
trix and the change vector, the MAC values were found between the vectors containing
the natural frequencies.
The method was then tested experimentally using a small wooden T-structure. Through
three different damage cases of varying damage signiﬁcance the method provided very
promising results overall. All three damages were localised within a few centimetres
precision for most of the damage sizes. However, the method did fail to localise the dam-
age in some cases. This is likely due to some of the factors affecting the performance
41

42
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
of the method discussed in the thesis. The factors affecting the results includes: Poorly
performed experimental tests, deviations between FE model and physical structure, sim-
ulated damage scenarios not resembling actual damage cases enough.
7.1
Further Work
The work done throughout this thesis, proves that the method of SBDA has the potential
to become more robust and precise by introducing a range of modiﬁcations. The newly
presented MAC-SBDA method proved very promising, but is far from ready to be applied
commercially. Thus the properties of this method should be further explored.
As it was shown during the experimental part of the thesis, the quality of the damage
test varied enough so that the damage localisation failed at times. This type of error is
unacceptable if the method is to be applied to large complex structures. However, doing
another test immediately after the failed test might have localised the damage better. For
this reason, it could be of interest to investigate the method together with an automated
system of data sampling and using statistical considerations.
Damage localisation as this thesis revolves around, is the second level of Rytters four
levels of SHM. Level one, detection, was brieﬂy covered during the experimental phase.
However it could be of interest to investigate whether the method of SBDA/MAC-SBDA
could be utilised on the third level of Rytters SHM, the damage quantiﬁcation. When
the damage has been localised, would it then be possible to determine the magnitude and
severity of the damage using a scenario based approach? In continuation of this, it could
be of interest to see if a non-linear method of determining the sensitivity of the modal pa-
rameters, would be a better estimate for quantiﬁcation. To this end, the Bernal projection
equation as introduced in this thesis could be used.
Lastly, performing experiments similar to the ones conducted during this thesis on a
more complex structure could bring the method closer to commercial appliance. A more
complex structure would challenge all input parameters; Quality of the FE model, deter-
mination of natural frequencies and scenario simulations. Successfully implementing the
method on a larger, more complex structure would increase the chance of implementing
the method on a full size structure, such as an offshore wind turbine, or an oil drilling
platform.

The geometric design
The geometric design of the structure has several properties making it interesting. The
ﬁrst property to point out is the simplicity of the structure. Apart from a clamped column,
this structure is one the simplest structures available. Consisting solely of a column and
a transverse ﬂange the behaviour of the structure will resemble that of a simple column.
What differentiates it from the column, is that the ﬂange increases the rotational mass in
one direction slightly. This should, in theory, ensure that the two ﬁrst mode shapes can be
distinguished from each other.
Secondly, the geometry and the dynamic behaviour will behave much like a mono
pile structure such as an Offshore Wind Turbine (OWT) [10]. In particular, the ﬁrst two
frequencies of bending will resemble the two ﬁrst modes of the OWT. The latter modes
will also resemble the behaviour of the OWT, disregarding the modes associated with
dynamic behaviour of the OWT’s ﬂaps. The closeness of the two ﬁrst modes, both for the
test specimen and the OWT, can results in special damage cases where the two ﬁrst modes
are switched in terms of arrangement by lowest frequency. This should be considered
when comparing mode shapes from before and after the damage has occurred.

Material design
The reason the material for the specimen is chosen as wood is mainly because of the ease
of which damage can be inﬂicted. Combined with the low cost and ease of acquiring new
specimens.
The downside of choosing wood, as the material for testing, is mainly the fact that
wood is an orthotropic material. This means that the mass and stiffness will vary between
each test specimen, and even along a single specimen. Modelling this kind of material in
an FE model is extremely difﬁcult to do.
The connections in the specimen is made by bolting the pieces together with M8 bolts.
Similarly, the bottom base plate is connected to the column by a bolt.

Flaws of the FE model
An FE model is in nature, only an approximation of a theoretic or physical specimen.
It will never resemble the test specimen completely, but increasing the similarity greatly
affects the results of the SBDA method. There are however, three major differences be-
tween the FE model and test specimen used in this thesis:
• Accelerometers are not included in model
• Material is difﬁcult to model
• Boundary conditions are poorly modelled.
Accelerometers are not included in the FE model. The B&K accelerometers weighs
approximately 7.5g (including some weight of the wire) and with 10 sensors this adds up
to around 75g. This is quite a lot considering the mass of the specimen varies between 450
and 800g, depending on the density of the wooden specimen. The weight of the sensors
are included in the model by increasing the overall density of the model. This corresponds
to "smearing" the sensors all over the specimen. This will of course not act exactly as the
==PAGE:67==
55
physical specimen, considering the mass inertia of the sensors will be different based on
the placement on the specimen.
Another approach that could be used is by extracting the effect of the sensors from
the experimental results. This can be done using Bernals equation [6].
Orthotropic material like seen in wood is almost impossible to model perfectly. The
variations in the wood is much more delicate than what can practically be achieved in
an FE model. Thus the material model used will have to be average, if not for the en-
tire model, then at least for a section of the model. For this model, the same material is
used throughout the entire model. This means that the local variations of density and E-
modulus will not be applied. By doing a proper average, the behaviour of the orthotropic
specimen can be modelled quite well, disregarding local anomalies.
The Boundary Conditions are in the FE model, chosen as perfectly clamped by re-
stricting all six DOF’s (Displacement and rotation in three axis). Though the wooden
specimen is indeed clamped to the heavy base plate, the connection between column and
wood base plate is made using a bolt. This connection will allow for small rotations and
the bottom of the column, thus not reﬂecting the behaviour of the modelled specimen.
This can be mitigated in several ways. One way is to apply a spring BC in the bottom
of the column instead of a restrained BC. This will require a bit of work to adjust the
stiffness of the modelled spring to properly match the stiffness of the actual BC. Alter-
natively, a small element can be made in the FE model where the modulus of elasticity
can be altered separately from the rest of the model. This would result in a similar effect
as the spring BC. For the model used in this thesis, the model has been kept completely
simple with the clamped BC. By altering the E modulus of the entire model, the resulting
natural frequencies of the ﬁrst ﬁve modes can be made sufﬁciently close to the experi-
mental model so that the results become quite good.

APPENDIX B
Test and Test Setup
The test performed on the wooden T-structure is a simple OMA test, performed in labora-
tory conditions. The reason that the test is performed under such conditions is to eliminate
the inﬂuence of the environment on the modal properties of the specimen. Temperature,
humidity etc. can impact the modal properties, which could lead to false damage local-
isation. Many publications has already adressed this issue including, but not limited to:
Handawi [14], Slocak [15].
The purpose of the test is to identify the natural frequencies and mode shapes of the
structure.
Equipment
The equipment used for the testing during the work of this thesis includes:
• Wooden specimen
• Heavy base plate
• Clamps
• Accelerometers + mounts
• Analog to Digital Converter
• NI Signal Express (Software)
The accelerometers used for these test are piezoelectric accelerometers of type 4508B
from Brüel & Kjær[16]. These type of sensors work when a small mass inside the ac-
celerometer is pushed against the crystal. The voltage generated by the piezoelectric
material in the sensor will change, depending on the force applied by the mass. This
voltage can then be converted to a corresponding acceleration.

Setup and testing
The setup consists mainly of the wooden T-structure clamped to a heavy base plate. From
here, the necessary amount of sensors are applied and connected to the ADC and com-
puter to capture the signals.
An allen key is used to excite the specimen carefully and intelligently. The excitation
is randomized in time and space, in order to excite all the mode shapes, and provide reli-
able data in the analysis. Further, the test person may not induce a force to the specimen
while scratching the specimen. This is undesirable as it may introduce fake stiffness to
the structure by restricting the otherwise natural response.
Data sampling
When designing the test itself, a couple of very important parameters are to be decided.
First off is the amount and placement of sensors. Increasing the number of sensors will
allow for more detailed data and the determination of more natural frequencies and mode
shapes. However, the response of the higher modes are quickly drowned out by noise,
and thus adding more sensors might not in practice increase the number of modes that
can be identiﬁed. On top of that, the sensor will add mass to the structural system. This
will undoubtedly inﬂuence the modal parameters and the used number of sensors should
therefore be carefully considered. Before engaging in experimental activities the place-
ment of sensors were discussed with the supervisors. From experience, it was known
that it is rarely possible to identify more than the ﬁrst ﬁve to ten mode shapes. Thus the
amount of sensors and their placement are selected to effectively identify the ﬁrst ﬁve
modes.
Secondly, there are some settings regarding the sampling that are important for the
data retrieved. The sampling frequency, is one of the main factors inﬂuencing the data.
Here it is important to consider that the highest frequency expected should be well rep-
resented. For the wooden T-structure the second order bending modes (mode 4 and 5)
expected frequencies would be less than 200. To ensure that frequencies in this range is
