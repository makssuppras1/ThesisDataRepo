ABSTRACT 
Under the right conditions, microalgae are known to produce harmful blooms that cause major 
fish kill worldwide. In 1988, an extensive bloom in Skagerrak caused the killing of both wild and 
caged fish, which had detrimental economic consequences for fish farms. The sinner was the 
microalgae Prymnesin polylepis, but the toxic components were never identified, and no chemical 
components in P. polylepis has ever been characterized. 
In this thesis, investigation to discover the fish-killing toxin(s) has been pursued in two ways. 
First, by bioguided fractionation with RTgill-W1 cell bioassay, and second, by chemical 
characterization of some chlorinated metabolites.  
Prior to the analysis, dereplication with molecular networking identified a class of 
phosphocholines (PC) in P. polylepis, a membrane lipid detected in other eukaryotic organisms. 
MS/MS analysis and identification of distinctive fragment ions could substantiate PC in P. 
polylepis, and additionally proved molecular networking as a useful tool in dereplication.  
The crude extract of P. polylepis exhibited toxicity in RTgill-W1 cell bioassay. The most significant 
dose-response effect from semi-preparative HPLC derived fractions revealed three major 
metabolites as possible toxins. One of the metabolites was putatively assigned as the well-studied 
and previously proven cytotoxiccompound Loliolide. Another one of the metabolites was also 
identified with Dynamic Cluster Analysis, and the isotope pattern indicated a compound with two 
chlorine atoms. Interesting, the molecular mass resembled other chlorinated ichthyotoxins such 
as prymnesins and karlotoxins.  
With spectroscopic analysis (LC-HRMS and NMR) it was possible to identify other chlorinated 
metabolites, and the thesis includes a full planar structure elucidation of a penta-chlorinated 
metabolite from P. polylepis, a new compound named polylepin A. Two hexachlorinated 
analouges to polylepin A, were also detected and addressed, and these compounds showed 
structural similarities to other biological active marine natural products. 
Altogether, in this master thesis both approaches have given new insight into the chemistry and 
likely bioactive compounds in P. polylepis.

RESUME 
Under de rigtige betingelser kan mikroalger lave skadelige algeopblomstringer som resulterer i 
omfattende fiskedød verden rundt. I 1988 opstod der er voldsom opblomstring i Skagerrak, som 
dræbte både vilde og opdrættede fisk. Årsagen var mikroalgen Prymnesium polylepis, men de 
fiskegiftige stoffer blev aldrig karakteriseret og ingen kemiske komponenter i P. polylepis er 
nogensinde blevet karakteriseret.  
I dette speciale er der anvendt to metoder i forsøget på at finde de fiskegiftige stoffer. Først ved 
bioguidet fraktionering med RTgælle-W1 celle bioassay og bagefter ved karakterisering af nogle 
chlorinerede metabolitter.  
Forud for analysen blev en stofklasse af phosphocholiner derepliekret i P. poilylepis vha. 
molekylær netværk, som er et membran lipid i eukaryote organismer.  MS/MS analyse og 
identificering af karakteristiske fragment ioner underbyggede dette og beviste samtidig, at 
molekylær netværk var et anvendeligt redskab i dereplikering. 
Råekstraktet fra P. polylepis viste sig at være giftigt i gællecelle assayet, Den mest signifikante 
dose-respons effekt indikerede, at tre forskellige metabolitter kunne være indblandet i den 
observerede effekt. En af metabolitterne var interessant, da den samtidig blev identificeret i 
Dynamic Cluster Analysis og isotopmønstret indikerede 2 chlor i dens stuktur.  Molekylemassen 
var også bemærkelsesværdig eftersom den mindede om andre kendte klorerede ichthyotoxiner 
karlotoxin og prymnesin.   
Med spektroskopiske analyseteknikker (LC-HRMS og NMR) var det muligt at identificere andre 
chlorinerede metablitter i P. polylepis, og i specialet fremvises en plan strukturopklaring af en 
pentachloreret metabolit, polylepin A.  Der blev også identificeret to hexachlorerede analoger til 
polylepin A, og disse metabolitter udviste strukturelle ligheder med andre biologisk aktive stoffer 
fra marine organismer. 
Specialet viser at begge metoder har givet indsigt i kemien samt mulige bioaktiviteten i P. polylepis

1 Introduction to thesis 
 
In 1988 during the late spring, an extensive bloom of the algae Chrysochromulina polylepis 
occurred in Scandinavian waters. The bloom influenced most aquatic life and as the algal front 
moved towards the Norwegian coast, it became a severe threat to the fish farming industry. 
During the following month, it spread along the Norwegian and Swedish coastlines, and covered 
major parts of the Kattegat and Skagerrak, a strait running between Norway, Sweden and 
Denmark. The bloom caused killing of both wild and caged fish. Several millions of $US were lost 
on the fish farms. Very little was known about the alga and its progression was closely watched 
and reported in both television and newspaper. [1][2] No fish killing bloom of C. polylepis has 
been detected since. 
 
Algal blooms are a normal part of the productivity in the ocean but since 1966 a number of toxic 
blooms have been reported. [1] The harmful algal bloom (HAB) is associated with rapid 
proliferation and high biomass accumulation of the toxic algae. [3] HAB has gained attention due 
to its impact on public health, fisheries and aquatic ecosystem. As fish farming is an economically 
important industry in many countries, also in Denmark, it is of high interest to monitor and 
control HAB.  The exact mechanism of how fish are affected and the toxins responsible for these 
effects are still in a preliminary discovery phase, which makes it a difficult task to cope with HAB. 
  
Subsequent to the bloom in 1988, the toxicity of C. polylepis was considered to be a group of toxins 
rather than a single component, but they were not identified. The symptoms observed in the fish 
included damage to the permeability of the gill epithelia, discoloration, changes in behavioural 
patterns, the loosening of organs all in addition to death. [2] Later, C. polylepis has proven to 
exhibit haemolytic activity in phosphor-limited cultures, thus suggesting the production of toxins 
with haemolytic activity. [4] Screening for bioactive compounds in C. polylepis has suggested the 
haemolytic activity to be related to certain lipid classes, but this was later invalidated. [5] 
Despite an effort to unravel the mystery, the toxic component of C. polylepis remains unclear. To 
accommodate the need to avoid major fish killings in the future, there is clearly a need to identify 
the chemically toxic principle of C. polylepis. To understand the chemical profile and possibly 
bioactive compounds of C. polylepis, biological test should be accommodated with chemical 
characterisation in the identification.

2 Introduction to algal secondary metabolites 
 
In the great Tree of Life, life can be divided into three domains: bacteria, achaea and eukaryote. 
Eukaryote being the most complex cell type of the three can be further divided into different 
kingdoms. This gives rise to six kingdoms: Animalia, Plantae, Fungi, Protista, Archea and Bacteria. 
Sometimes Archea and Bacteria are joined as one kingdom called Monera resulting in five 
kingdoms [6]. Algae are sometimes classified as plants, but they are primarily classified as 
Protista, which is a kingdom of organisms that are unicellular or unicellular-colonial and who 
form no tissue.  
In classical taxonomy, after being classified into a kingdom the organism can be further 
subcategorized six times into phylum, class, order, family, genus, and ending with the species.  
 
2.1 Algal organisms 
Algae represent a large group of different aquatic organisms. Aquatic organisms encompass both 
marine, brackish and fresh water algae. Algae figure in the food chain as the very beginning of 
nutrition, and plays a key role in the aquatic environment. 
The diversified aquatic environment harbours a large variety of algae. Algae has adapted to 
different environments and this has resulted in great diversity regarding size, morphology, and 
pigments that makes them difficult to classify and group. [7] Some algae are photosynthetic, some 
are mixotrophe, and some do not have chloroplasts. A simple division can categorize algae into 
macroscopic- and microscopic algae. To simplify it further, macroscopic algae are seaweeds and 
microscopic algae are phytoplankton. Seaweed are simple cells collaborating in a connective 
tissue as multi-celled organisms anchored to the seabed. They can range up to 70 m, and can 
provide a tremendous biomass. Seaweed includes algae such as brown, red, and green algae. 
Phytoplankton is single celled microscopic organisms that colonizes and floats on the surface of 
the water. Phytoplankton fits nicely into the kingdom Protista as single celled organisms that are 
not animal, plant or fungi. Some microalgae are in the limelight due to their role in HAB.  
 
2.1.1 Chrysochromulina or Prymnesium? 
The algal sinner in the harmful bloom during the outbreak in 1988, was reported as 
Chrysochromulina polylepis. [1] The alga belongs to the order of prymnesiales (haptophyte). In a 
study of prymnesiales, a revision of the taxonomy based on ribosomal DNA sequences and 
morphological data reclassified Chrysochromulina polylepis as Prymnesium polylepis. [8] Thus, in 
the subsequent presentation and discussion of the toxic specie, it will be referred to as P. polylepis.   
==PAGE:10==
Page 9 of 106 
 
P. polylepis figures in the aquatic environment as phytoplankton. It is a single cell organism of 
approximately 10 m. It has two equally long flagellae, and between the flagellae it has a filiform 
organelle, called the haptonema, as illustrated in Figure 1. [2] The haptonema is like a little horn, 
and is common within the algae order haptophyte. Another type of microalgae within the 
haptophyte order that has also been associated with HAB is Prymneisum parvum. [3][9] 
Flagellated species such as p. parvum and p. polylepis, accounts for 90% of HAB. [10] 
 
 
Figure 1 illustrates P. polylepis with its two flagellae and the haptonema in the middle. [11] 
 
2.2 Natural products 
Secondary metabolites from organisms as plants, fungi and algae are classified as natural 
products, and the word is used interchangeably. The interest in natural products was in the 
beginning mainly due to their pharmaceutical effect. The complexity of secondary metabolites 
has proven to be a pool of potential drug candidates. In algae, other features are also being 
explored such as pigments, ingredients for cosmetics, biofuel as well as a nutritional supplements 
in the food industry. [12] 
 
2.2.1 Primary and secondary metabolism  
Primary metabolites include proteins, carbohydrates, fat, and nucleic acids. All organisms relay 
on primary metabolites to sustain growth, development and reproduction.  Energy is released 
when the metabolites are degraded through metabolic pathways, as for instance carbohydrates 
that are catabolized in the citric acid cycle. Primary metabolism is essentially the same 
throughout all living organisms, despite the many differences between them. Secondary 
metabolites are different because they possess more distinct and unique features in each group 
of organisms, or even in individual organisms. They are much more limited in their distribution 
than primary metabolites. Most often, they prove beneficial to the organism as part of defence 
==PAGE:11==
Page 10 of 106 
 
strategies. [13]  
Secondary metabolites provide the same vital function as primary metabolites. They play a role 
in avoiding predators and it is logical to state their importance as otherwise natural selection 
would have precluded the high cost of metabolic energy used in their construction. It is also 
important to state that there is no strict line between the two categories of metabolite and a grey 
area exist where the classification can overlap.  
 
2.3 Production of secondary metabolites 
Secondary metabolites show great chemical diversity often with a high degree of complexity. 
Despite this, secondary metabolites are derived from a limited selection of building blocks. 
Specific intermediates utilized in respective metabolic pathways are the foundation of 
biosynthesis. These pathways include acetate, shikimate, mevalonate, and then there is a group 
of alkaloids. They will briefly be presented in the succeeding section.   
 
2.3.1 Biosynthetic pathways, building blocks, and products 
The limited selection of building blocks is derived from primary metabolism. The ones utilized in 
the pathways mentioned above are acetyl-CoA, shikimic acid, mevalonic acid, and 
methylerythritol phosphate.  
Acetyl-CoA is utilized in the acetate pathway and the products are fatty acids and polyketides. 
Polyketides will be presented individually and more thoroughly in the subsequent section as 
many algal toxins stem from this pathway. 
Shikimic acid is utilized in shikimate pathways and the products are aromatic amino acids. 
Mevalonic acid and methylerythritol phosphate are utilized in mevalonate pathway and the 
products are terpenoids and steroids. 
In additional to these building blocks there are also amino acids, which among other things are 
an important nitrogen source. Nitrogen containing compounds are named alkaloids, and are also 
a group of secondary metabolites.  
 
An additional feature in biosynthesis of secondary metabolites is the possibility of combining 
building blocks, thus increasing the diversity and complexity. However, it also makes biosynthesis 
elucidation less straight forward. The most common and widely implemented strategy for this 
task is by isotope labelling. Sometimes a retrosynthetic approach in which the structure is 
dissected into its constitute parts is a sufficient way to determine the origin of the building blocks. 
[13]

2.3.2 Polyketides in the acetate pathway 
The acetate pathway constitutes a large class of secondary metabolites with great diversity, 
namely polyketides. It is also responsible for the generation of fatty acids and many aromatic 
compounds. The core of the biosynthesis relies on the condensation of two units, one acetyl-
Coenzyme A (CoA) and one malonyl-CoA via the Claisen reaction, as illustrated in Figure 2. The 
figure illustrates the nucleophilic attack on the carbonyl at acetyl-CoA by malonyl-CoA. It is 
thought that the anion is generated at malonyl-CoA by spontaneously decarboxylation, thus 
decarboxylation facilitates Claisen condensation. The mechanism of chain elongation can go on in 
a series of reactions resulting in a poly--keto ester chain, as illustrated in Figure 2. Polyketides 
are characteristics with their backbone with oxygen on every second carbon. Acetyl is derived 
from glycolysis, the breakdown of carbohydrates, and is then acetylated to CoA. Malonyl-CoA is 
derived by carboxylation of acetyl-CoA, a conversion that increases the reactivity of the reaction 
and makes it more favourable than condensation of two acetyl-CoA units.  
 
 
Figure 2 illustrates the core mechanism of polyketide biosynthesis. Acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA reacts in a 
Claisen condensation, several reactions generates a poly--keto ester chain. [13] [14] 
The complexity of polyketide metabolites is a result of enzymatic modification during the 
elongation of the poly--keto ester chain. The enzymes are placed in a sequence manner that 
results in a multifunctional enzyme, with all enzymes in one complex called polyketide synthase 
(PKS). Different types of PKS has been identified in different organisms, called type 1, type 2, and 
type 3 PKS. The type of PKS in algae is unclear but based on the size, functionalities, and 
complexity of the secondary metabolites, it has been described as type 1. [15] However, the 
chemical aspects of chain construction and elongation are mainly the same in all PKS. 
The assembly line in the construction of polyketides with PKS include an acyl carrier protein 
(ACP), an acyl transferase (AT), a dehydrase (DH), an enol reductase (ER), a ketoreductase (KR), 
a ketosynthase (KS), and a thio-esterase(TE). The domains work in a collaborative fashion and 
they all carry out different functions, which are illustrated in Figure 3.  
==PAGE:13==
Page 12 of 106 
 
The ACP carries acetyl-CoA brought by AT. ACP activates acetyl-CoA and delivers it to KS. An 
extender unit malonyl-CoA is brought to ACP by AT, which can react with the starter unit at KS, 
illustrated in the first reaction in Figure 3. The two entities are condensed in a Claisen reaction, 
as described above, at the KS enzyme. The elongation can proceed in further addition of extender 
units providing a non-reduced polyketide, as a poly--keto ester chain seen in Figure 2. 
Alternatively, the chain can be altered at other enzyme-domains, all illustrated in Figure 3. The 
first possible enzyme is a KR, which reduces the β-keto group to a β-hydroxy group. Following 
KR, the chain can proceed to a DH which removes the hydroxyl group by eliminating H2O and 
leaves a double-bond behind. Lastly, the chain can by altered by an ER which reduces the double 
bond into a single bond. The chain can re-enter the elongation cycle again at any time. The result 
will be either a highly or partially reduced polyketide, giving rise to a diverse range of compounds 
based on the simple starter unit. The polyketide is released from the domain by TE. A minimal 
domain contains a KS, AT, and ACP to catalyse the carbon chain. Methyl groups can also be 
introduced in the chain, either by using another starter unit or by a malonyl transferase (MAT). 
The structural diversity arises from the combination of different enzyme domains.  
 
 
Figure 3 illustrates the assembly line of PKS. [16] Different modification enzymes contributes to the complexity 
and diversity of polyketides, such as KR, DH and ER. 
2.4 Microalgal secondary metabolites 
Microalgal natural products are profoundly interesting because of their association with HAB that 
threatens the fish farming industry and cause public health issues. Microalgal secondary 
metabolites that are toxic to humans are named phycotoxin. Microalgal compounds that are toxic 
to fish are named ichthyotoxins. Secondary metabolites are known to be involved in defence 
strategies in organisms, and the production of microalgal toxins are likely a result of such 
==PAGE:14==
Page 13 of 106 
 
strategy. The evolutionary origin of toxins is possibly a consequence of predator-prey 
interactions. Examples from each group, phycotoxins and ichthyotoxins respectively, will be 
presented in the subsequent section to illustrate the structural diversity that rules within algal 
toxins.  
 
A characteristic feature of secondary metabolites arising from marine organisms is the high 
halogenation ratio, which will be mentioned briefly.  
 
2.4.1 Microalgal toxins 
Phycotoxins are accumulated up through the food chain, and were first recognized in secondary 
sources such as contaminated clams or mussels associated with disease in humans. The 
mechanism of action is often related to an effect on ion channels in the cell membranes or 
enzymatic pathways. [3] Some of the most studied phycotoxins, as a consequence of the severe 
symptoms they present, will be presented together with their chemical features. All examples are 
illustrated in Figure 4. Classifying the toxins based on their chemical features instead of their 
symptoms creates a greater understanding of the functional diversity within phycotoxins. The 
phycotoxins have been described and grouped into five groups which are 1) linear and 
macrocyclic ethers, 2) ladder-frame polyethers, 3) macrocyclic imines, 4) tetrahydropurines, and 
5) secondary amines. [3][17]  
 
An example from group 1 is the diarrhetic shellfish toxins Okadaic acid (OA). OA damages the 
intestinal system and the mode of action has been described to be through inhibition of protein 
phosphatase activity, which results in hyper phosphorylation of certain proteins. [17] It is a 
spirofused molecule with seven ether rings with intervening carbon units. It has four hydroxyl 
groups, and a carboxylic acid that makes it polar. OA originates from the polyketide biosynthesis. 
From group 2, the structural term ladder-frame polyethers means a structure with multiple cyclic 
ether rings, that are arranged in a ladder-frame fashion. An example from group 2 is brevetoxins, 
which are known as neurotoxin shellfish toxin. It is subdivided into A and B types depending on 
the backbone, which consist of either 10 or 11 fused cyclic ethers respectively. The cyclic ethers 
range in size within type A and B, an example of type B is Brevetoxin2 (PbTx-2) is illustrated in 
Figure 4. Brevotoxin also originates from the polyketide biosynthesis, 
Group 3 only contains four toxins, all of them has a macrocyclic structure in which one of the rings 
has an imine functionality. They are all classified as neurotoxins and are described to interact 
with acetylcholine receptors, which are protein-receptor complexes in the cell membrane of 
certain neurons. [17] One example is gymnodimine. which also originates from the polyketide 
biosynthesis.  
==PAGE:15==
Page 14 of 106 
 
An example from group 4 is the paralytic shellfish toxin saxitoxin (STX), which is a potential 
neurotoxin that blocks nerve and muscle cells resulting in signs of paralysis. [18] The toxin is 
comprised of a tri-cyclic purine with hydroxyl groups, which makes it polar. Several analogues to 
STX have also been identified and due to its symptoms, it is one of the most studied groups of 
phycotoxins. Many of the species that produce these toxins has also been related to fish killings 
during HAB, but has yet to be identified as the responsible component. [17] With nitrogen it its 
structure it can be grouped as an alkaloid.  
An example from group 5 is Domoic acid (DA), which is a small compound with a secondary amine

3 Analytical chemistry 
 
3.1 Sample preparation 
Sample preparation is the step before chemical analysis. In this context, it is prior to the 
purification of secondary metabolites. 
 
3.1.1 Extraction (liquid-liquid) 
In extraction the analytes of interest are extracted from the matrix, based on polarity. In liquid-
liquid extraction two immiscible solvents with different polarity are used, and the secondary 
metabolites will be present in the solvent they share polarity with. [28] The two solvents are 
added, sometimes stepwise, and are followed by shaking or centrifugation to disrupt the cells. 
Centrifugation helps the cell remnants to sediment. A phase separation will occur, most often in 
a water/organic phase, and the desired phase is isolated. A few considerations are important. 
First, it is important to choose the right solvent, thus a solvent with affinity towards the analytes. 
Second, it is important to choose solvents that will give a significant distribution difference of the 
desired analytes in one of the two phases. In other words, the solvent must have specificity 
towards the analytes when compared with the matrix. The extraction can also be done stepwise, 
in which one solvent is used at the time. For instance, in algal extraction acetone is used first to 
extract apolar metabolites such as pigments, and then methanol is used to extract the semi-polar 
secondary metabolites. If many extraction steps have been used it is important to up-concentrate 
the sample again by removing the solvent on a rotary evaporator under vacuum or by nitrogen 
evaporation.  
 
3.2 Chromatography 
Chromatography is a technique that separates compounds in a mixture, dependent on chemical 
features. Column chromatography is a two-component system including a mobile phase and a 
stationary phase. The mixture is transferred through the column by the mobile phase, and 
compounds are separated based on their interaction with the stationary phase. Compounds with 
high affinity will adhere stronger than compounds with lower affinity. The separation is a result 
of equilibration of the compounds between the stationary and mobile phase respectively. 
The stationary phase is a fixed, solid material and can have different compositions and chemical 
properties. The mobile phase can be either liquid or gaseous giving rise to two different 
techniques named liquid chromatography (LC) and gas chromatography (GC). The choice of 
technique depends on the type of compound involved in the separation. For instance, in GC it is a 
prerequisite that the compounds are sufficiently volatile. In this section, only LC is discussed. 
==PAGE:19==
Page 18 of 106 
 
3.2.1 Liquid Chromatography 
LC can roughly be divided into two groups: column interaction and non-column interaction. In 
column interaction chromatography, the compounds interacts with the column either by 
adsorption, partition distribution, charge properties or specific affinity and are separated based 
on these properties by competing with solvent molecules for a site on the stationary phase.  Non-
column interaction separates compounds based on their size. [28] LC based on adsorption can be 
further divided into two subgroups: normal phase (NP) and reverse phase (RP). NP utilizes polar 
interaction between compound and stationary phase, and RP utilize nonpolar interactions.  
In this section RP is presented thoroughly, and NP will be briefly mentioned. However, the basics 
behind column chromatography are the same in all the techniques, namely the separation of 
compounds based on chemical features.  
 
3.2.2 Reverse phase column mechanism of action 
In RP the stationary phase is nonpolar, and the solvent is more polar. The analytes will adsorb to 
the stationary phase based on polarity, thus nonpolar analytes will bind stronger than polar 
analytes. The mechanism of the mobile phase is to displace analytes from the adsorption site, and 
it will happen if the adsorption properties of the solvent exceeds the adsorption properties of the 
analytes. Thus, polar analytes will elute first, and nonpolar analytes will elute in the end. The 
interactions in play can be van der Waals forces. For complex mixtures the most efficient elution 
is performed with a gradient, in which two miscible solvents are used in different ratios to 
constantly change the polarity of the mobile phase. An alternative to gradient elution is isocratic 
elution in which only one concentration is used. Using one concentration cannot accommodate 
for the entire polarity range in a complex mixture, thus gradient elution is preferred in a 
separation strategy. In RP, the gradient of the mobile phase changes from more polar to less polar. 
The mechanism is effected by equilibrium, and whether the analytes prefer to be in the mobile 
phase or at the stationary phase. Retention time is the time the analyte stays on the column, and 
is directly proportional to the hydrophobic interactions.  
In NP the mechanism is reversed, hence the name. A polar stationary phase is used, and a mobile 
phase gradient going from less polar to more polar is used. With this system, nonpolar analytes 
will elute first, and polar analytes will elute in the end. NP is useful if very polar compounds 
cannot be retained on RP. The interactions in play are hydrogen-bonds, thus stronger than the 
forces in RP. Sometimes RP is also referred to as partition chromatography, and NP as adsorption 
chromatography to differentiates between the different binding properties. 
 
==PAGE:20==
Page 19 of 106 
 
Polarity is affected by functional groups in the compound. LogD is a term that states the solubility 
of a compound in octanol in relation to water, and thus its polarity. A small solubility value will 
indicate a polar compound, a high value will indicate a nonpolar compound. 
 
3.2.3 High Pressure Liquid Chromatography efficiency   
In High Pressure Liquid Chromatography (HPLC), high pressure is used to force the mobile phase 
through the column. This allows the implementation of small particle size, and thus a better 
resolution, which will be defined shortly. Additional to HPLC is ultrahigh pressure liquid 
chromatography (UHPLC). UHPLC implement even smaller particles and higher pressure. 
The aim in the separation process is a fast equilibration between the stationary and the mobile 
phase of the analyte.  
Two important terms in HPLC are resolution and efficiency. Resolution is an expression of how 
good the column selectivity is, hence how well the column separates similar compounds to give 
baseline separated peaks. Efficiency is the columns ability to elute narrow and symmetric peaks, 
and is determined by the number of theoretical plates. [28] The number of theoretical plates 
express how many equilibration steps an analyte undergoes and a high number means better 
efficiency. The particle size is the predominant factor regarding plate height and efficiency. When 
the particle size decreases the number of theoretical plates increases. 
The term is introduced in the van Deemter equation (1). The constants A, B and C depend on the 
column and the stationary phase.

The A term is an expression of multiple paths. The solute can move in different paths through the

consequently gives rise to peak broadening. Small particles create a uniform dispersion and gives 
fewer paths, which in turn gives a smaller A term. 
The B term is an expression of longitunal diffusion as the solute moves along the column axis. The 
diffusion happens as solutes moves from a high concentration to a low concentration. The faster 
the solute travels, the less diffusion will happen. Thus, B is inversely proportional to the linear 
velocity µx and a higher µx reduces the term and gives a smaller H. 
Lastly, the C term is a matter of equilibration time. The solute must transfer from the mobile phase 
to stationary phase to reach equilibrium and will depend on the distance and velocity. The slower 
the velocity, the more time the analyte will have to reach equilibrium. The broadening happens if 
mass transfer is slow relative to the mobile phase velocity. The distance however, can be affected

3.2.4 High Pressure Liquid Chromatography instrumentation 
The HPLC system consists of an injection site, a solvent delivery system, a column, and a detector, 
as illustrated in Figure 6. Analytes are detected as they elute from the column, and the output is a 
chromatogram with intensity in relation to time. 
The column is the heart of HPLC and is responsible for the separation. The inner part of the 
column is coated with the stationary phase. The material is spherical microporous particles of 
silica. In HPLC the sizes typically range from 1.7-5m.  Small particles give better resolution (A-
term), but the downside is the increase in pressure, as a result of greater solvent resistance. The 
pores inside the particle increase the surface area, which increases the area of analyte adsorption. 
In RP the silica particles are reacted with ligands to make a bonded stationary phase. The 
covalently attached ligands are apolar and can be octadecyl (C18) or hexyl phenyl among others. 
The octadecyl (C18) phase is illustrated in Figure 6.  The first gives rise to van der Waals 
interaction and the latter gives rise to - interactions. 
The column is heated throughout the experiment. A temperature increase will decrease the 
solvent viscosity, thus reducing the pressure. Too high temperatures however, will degrade the 
column. The column can obtain different dimension with variable length and diameter. A long 
column will give longer separation time and better resolution, but a long separation time also 
gives rise to more diffusion (B-term). Additionally, it is not always desirable to have a long run 
time in a time effective perspective. A narrow column requires less sample and facilitate a faster 
mass transfer (C-term).

and a detector. [29]

To protect the column, it is protected with guard column which is a small pre-column made of the 
same stationary material. Its role is to capture impurities and highly adsorbing analytes so they 
do not stick to the column.  
Before the column is the injection site where sample and solvent enter the column. The injection 
system often consists of a loop with several entries that can control the flow into the column. In 
modern HPLC the injection site is automatic, and the instrumentation holds an autosampler that 
provides constant samples for analysis.  
To provide the flow through the system of sample and solvent and to maintain the high flow 
required in HPLC, a sufficient pump system is connected. In HPLC the pump should provide a 
pressure up to 400bar, and in UHPLC up to 1000 bar. The pressure increases because the particle 
size decreases in UHPLC. Furthermore, the pump should be able to provide the system with a 
solvent gradient, thus be able to mix solvents in a specific ratio. One way to deliver a gradient 
system is by a binary pump. Each pump delivers one specific solvent that is mixed with the other 
to the right gradient before it enters the column. To avoid air in the system, solvents are degassed 
before analysis. Air can interfere with the column and pump, and give noise in the detector output. 
Furthermore, ultrapure HPLC solvents are required in the instrumentation to prevent column 
degradation, and avoid background noise in the chromatogram. In the end of the setup we find 
the detector, which can be a diode array detector (DAD) and/or a mass spectrometer (MS). Both 
will be presented later.  
 
The choice of column regarding length, diameter and particle size depends on the purpose of the 
experiment. Analytical columns have a narrower diameter and small particles around 1.7-5m, 
which provide high resolution and efficiency. Only small amounts can be loaded in narrow 
columns. The aim is to get full separation between compounds based on their polarity. 
Preparative columns are broader and have bigger particles, higher than 10m, and as a result 
much more compound can be loaded. The loading capacity depends on the dimensions of the 
column. The separation is less critical, and the aim is to get fractionations of compounds rather 
than separation.  
 
3.3 Bioguided purification strategy of natural products  
Natural products often exist in a complex matrix of other metabolites. After extraction, the 
approach is to divide all metabolites into smaller fractions using preparative chromatography. 
Natural products often cover a high chemical diversity as a consequence of their multiple 
biosynthetic pathways described earlier. Explorative solid phase extraction (E-SPE) columns can 
be utilized to exploit the different chemical entities in such complex matrixes. E-SPE are small 
==PAGE:23==
Page 22 of 106 
 
columns with stationary phases similar to LC but in small scale. The stationary phases are 
composed of different materials giving rise to a range of orthogonal selectivity and can be used to 
explore the optimum purification strategy. [30] Column selectivity ranges from polar/apolar, to 
ion exchange properties, to size variation. The elution is stepwise, and the number of fractions 
can vary from each column. [31] 
E-SPE can also be used in combination with bioassays. A classical approach in the purification of 
novel natural products is by an activity-guided purification strategy. Bioassay is an analytical 
method to detect potency, and the type of assay can vary dependent on the type of potency that 
is detected, for instance toxicity.  
In ichthyotoxic perspective, fish appear as the ideal bioassay. However, it is neither sustainable 
nor ethical to use fish throughout a purification process as many tests are performed. Instead, 
living cells or tissues are used such as gill cells. [32] Chemical toxicity to gill cells are detected as 
a change in viability. It is translational to whole biological system under the assumption that gill 
cells are in the first line of attack. Suffocating gill cells can induce mucus secretion, as a protective 
response, that suffocate the fish. [32][3] Early studies from P. polylepis has also showed damage 
to the permeability on the gill cell epithelia. [2] The change in viability is detected with a reagent, 
which is converted to a fluorescent compound by living cells. Thus, low fluorescent activity is a 
result of low viability. Another example of a bioassay is the Artemia Saline bioassay, which 
previously has been used to test the toxicity of P. polylepis. [33] A. Saline are brine shrimp, but 
they are more closely related to water fleas than true shrimp. Contrary to the gill cells, A. Saline 
are whole organisms. A desired parameter when using bioassays is LC50, which is the 
concentration that causes the death of 50% of test animals/cells.  
One strong advantage with E-SPE bioguided fractionations is the sensitivity of biological assays 
to extract information about the toxins. [3] E-SPE is useful in the preliminary phase at is provide 
range of information without using large amounts of the extract.  
 
3.4 Spectroscopy 
3.4.1 Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy 
Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy (UV or UV-vis) refers to adsorption of light emerging from the 
ultraviolet and visible range in the electromagnetic spectrum. Absorption spectra are obtained 
by sample irradiance and subsequent detection of the remaining light, which has not been 
detected. 
Absorption of electromagnetic radiation causes excitation of an electron from the ground state to 
a higher state of energy. The electron moves from an occupied orbital with high energy (HOMO), 
to an unoccupied orbital with lowest energy (LUMO). The absorption energy corresponds exactly 
==PAGE:24==
Page 23 of 106 
 
to the energy difference between the two orbitals. The absorption energy is represented by its 
wavelength. 
The most observed transition includes compounds containing -electrons and non-bonding 
electrons like lone pairs. A conjugated system will decrease the energy gap between LUMO and 
HOMO, and the absorption energy appears at higher wavelengths. Substitution such as methyl, 
hydroxyl or halogen contributes to the electron density in the system can decrease the energy 
gap same way. The group of atoms influencing the absorption are collectively called 
chromophore. [34] Molecules have many exited modes of vibration and rotation, which give rise 
to more transition states. However, as these transitions happen in a small energy window, the 
differences are not recorded and instead, the absorption band appears as an envelope rather than 
a sharp peak. [34][35] 
 
The instrumentation setup consists of a light source, the sample, a polychromator, and a DAD. To 
cover the entire range of UV-light (110-400nm) and visible light (400-800nm), both deuterium 
and tungsten lamps are used respectively. A polychromator disperse the light into its component 
wavelengths. Different wavelengths reach different parts of the detector, but are all recorded at 
once. The output is a spectrum with intensity in reference to time. [36] 
 
 
3.4.2 Mass spectrometry 
Mass spectrometry is a separation technique that separates molecules based on mass-to-charge 
ratio (m/z). Hence, a prerequisite for the analysis is to get the analytes in a charged state. The 
technique operates under a high vacuum, which makes it ideal for analysing molecules after GC 
separation, as the analyte is already in the gas phase. For the same reason challenges are faced 
when MS is merged with LC, as the analyte is in a liquid state. However, HPLC often works with 
small volumes, as a consequence of working with narrow columns, which makes it compatible 
with the MS interface. 
A general composition of an MS instrument is an ion source, a mass analyser, and a detector. 
The first unit in MS instrumentation is the ion source which is the site of ionization.  
 
3.4.2.1 Ion source 
Different ionisation techniques can be applied for this purpose such as chemical ionization, 
electron ionization, and electrospray ionization. Only electrospray ionization (ESI) will be 
presented. 
ESI is a soft ionization method that operates under atmospherics pressure. The atmospheric 
condition makes the source an ideal interface between the high pressure conditions in HPLC and 
==PAGE:25==
Page 24 of 106 
 
the high vacuum conditions in MS. The goal of the source is to evaporate the sample into a gas 
state, from a liquid state, and at the same time apply charges to the analytes.  
The analytes elute from the LC instrument in a liquid state and enters the spray chamber via a 
nebulizer with a stream of nitrogen as a carrier gas. A strong electric field is applied to the 
nebulizer by creating a potential difference between the end of the nebulizer and a counter 
electrode in the spray chamber, as illustrated in Figure 7. As a result, the exiting sample together 
with the nitrogen flow generates an aerosol of charged droplets guided by the applied voltage. 
When the droplets are subjected to dry hot nitrogen gas, solvents will evaporate and make them 
unstable. The high surface charge per unit volume will eventually cause combustion due to high 
Columbic repulsions between the droplets. After several evaporation and combustion steps, the 
charged droplets are reduced to charged molecules and will eventually leave their cargo of ions 
in the gas phase. [37] [38] The free gas-phase ions are attracted towards the mass spectrometer 
with an opposite potential. The ions are guided via a glass capillary at lower pressure out of the 
source. At the end of the capillary, the ions are further directed through skimmer cones at even 
lower pressure. [39] The skimmer cones direct the analytes into the octopole region, which in an 
ion bridge that provides ion transmission over wide mass range. Another lens direct the ions 
towards the quadrupole, which has a narrower mass range than the octopole and focuses the ions 
into the mass analyser. The interface between the ionsource and the mass analayser is illustrated 
in Figure 8. The quadrupole is presented in the next section.  
ESI can be performed in positive or negative mode, generating positively or negatively charged 
analytes respectively. It depends on the applied electric field. The ionization potential can vary 
among analytes in a sample, some analytes are easier to ionize than others. For this reason, MS is 
not a quantitative measurement of the amount of analyte.  
 
Figure 7 illustrates the action in ESI positive mode. In ESI, the droplets gets charged as solvent evaporates and 
due to columbic explosions, it will eventually create small charged molecules. The applied voltage will guide 
the ions. [40]

3.4.2.2 Ion focusing interface (Quadrupole) 
The quadrupole focuses the ions into the mass analyser, as illustrated in Figure 8. It is constituted 
of four solid rods arranged in a square and is parallel to ion beam. Ideally, the rods are hyperbolic 
in the cross section to enhance ion transmission. A radiofrequency voltage is applied to the rods 
in pairs of two, with a polarity inverted from one rod to the next. An oscillating electric field is 
generated in the region between the rods and the ions are separated based on the stability of their 
trajectories in the path. The technique utilizes that ions have different stable trajectory paths. A 
trajectory of an ion is considered stable if it never collides with the rods, thus only ions with the 
correct mass-to-charge ratio pass within the field of the quadrupole. If ions collide with the rods 
they are neutralized and expelled from the analysis. The oscillating electric field focuses a narrow 
range of ions with different mass-to-charge ratios. For instance, ions with high masses are lost. 
Quadrupole has a higher focusing efficiency than other multipoles. [41] 
 
3.4.2.3 Mass analyser 
Many different mass analysers exist, that can determine the mass-to-charge ratio. The choice of 
mass analyser can vary between experiments and depends on how accurate the result is needed 
to be. For instance, a quadrupole can also be used as a mass analyser. It is robust and cheap but 
has a low accuracy. Time of flight (TOF) mass analysis is more sensitive and provide high mass 
accuracy that makes it reliable to determine elemental compositions of new compounds.  
In TOF the ions are separated based on their velocity. The technique relies on the equation of 
kinetic energy stating that lighter molecules travels faster than heavy molecules if they are 
subjected to the same kinetic energy. The kinetic energy is explained in equation 2.

The beam of ions passes into the TOF analyser. A pusher in the mass analyser accelerates the ions

away. This means that ions with same mass-to-charge ratio can obtain different velocities. It will 
reduce the mass resolution and cause peak broadening in the chromatogram. A way to 
circumvent this is by introducing a reflector in the end of the tube, which is like a mirror that 
reflects the ion back again. For instance, when two ions with different kinetic energy but same

reflector deeper, thus returning to the flight path later than the ion with the lower velocity, and

The flight tube is about 1 m. long and the vacuum conditions allow the ions to have a free path of

of mass, as the flight time is changed. The detector is a microchannel plate with a set of 
microscopic tubes, and each tube works as a multiplier. When an ion hits the surface, an electron 
escapes and begins the process of electrical signalling amplification through the tube. The 
electron cascade produces a current that is detected at the end of the plate as an electrical signal. 
[42] The output is called a total ion chromatogram (TIC) and is a collection of all detected ions 
within a given scan. TOF provides high resolution (HR) and high mass accuracy down to 1-5ppm 
mass error. With high HR-MS the exact mass for each isotopic peak of an analyte is determined. 
The monoisotopic peak is the exact mass of the most predominant isotope of each element, 
abbreviated A. A+1 is the isotopic peak that takes +1 isotopes into accounts such as 13C. The 
intensity of A+1 is relative to the A peak (100%) and reflects the natural abundancy of the isotope. 
For instance, 13C has a natural abundancy 1,01% and the intensity of A+1 relative to A can be 
proportional to the number of carbons in the analyte. The A+2 peak takes the +2 isotopes into 
account such as halogens and sulphur. Collectively, A, A+1, A+2 etc. are termed the isotopic 
pattern. The effect of different isotopes in the isotopic pattern is presented in section 3.6.1 about 
data processing.

3.4.2.4 Tandem MS/MS (collision cell/hexapole) 
In tandem MS/MS an additional stage of mass analysis involving a fragmentation process is 
added. The fragmentation takes place in the collision cell, which is placed before the mass 
analyser TOF, but after the quadrupole as illustrated in Figure 8. In the collision cell, which is a 
hexapole, the ions are subjected to an inert collision gas like the carrier gas. The most intense ions 
are selected in the quadrupole and focused to the collision cell. As default it is the three most 
intense ions per scan that are chosen. Ions are bombarded with a gas beam perpendicular to its 
path, and are accelerated through the collision cell with different potentials. As default the 
acceleration potential is 10, 20, and 40 eV. A higher potential will induce a higher degree of 
fragmentation. Fragment ions produced in the collision cell are sent to the TOF. MS/MS mode is 
less sensitive than MS mode. Fragmentation can also happen outside the collision cell, in the 
source. For instance, the analyte can collide with residual solvent or air which makes them 
fragmen, and also some compounds are more labile and tends to dissociate easily. Fragmentation 
is a useful tool in structure elucidation, as the fragmentation can be a characteristic fingerprint of 
the compound. When MS/MS mode is not acquired, the hexapole functions as ion transmission 
prior to the TOF.   
 
3.5 NMR 
Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) is a spectroscopic method and an important tool in chemical 
analysis. It is a powerful tool in terms of providing information in structure elucidation of organic 
molecules such as natural products. The technique was successfully demonstrated in 1946, and 
the discovery and the subsequent work was rewarded with a Nobel prize in physics in 1952. [43] 
The active NMR nuclei presented in this section are 1H-atoms and 13C-atoms.  NMR spectroscopy 
can be acquired in one and two dimensions respectively. Table 1 provides an overview over the 
NMR techniques presented in the section.  
 
3.5.1 One-dimensional NMR 
In one-dimensional (1D) NMR only one time domain is obtained, and it is the simplest experiment 
to perform. The most commonly 1D experiment are 1H-NMR and 13C-NMR. 1H-NMR is more 
sensitive than 13C-NMR. The spectra illustrate the intensity of the peaks in relation to their 
chemical shift. Only 1H-NMR is presented 
 
1H-NMR 
From the spectra acquired it is possible to determine the number of protons, the chemical shift, 
the multiplicity, and the coupling constant.  
==PAGE:29==

The signal intensity is proportional to the number of protons in the compound and integration of 
the signals can provide the total number of protons. The chemical shift H is a valuable source of 
information regarding the chemical environment of the nuclei and interpretation of H is useful in 
structure elucidation. The last important information to extract from the 1H-NMR spectra is the 
multiplicity of the signal, and the coupling constant. The multiplicity is a result of spin coupling in 
which a proton is effected by the spin property of neighbouring protons. The signalling proton 
peak divides into sub peaks dependent on the number and type of neighbouring protons. The 
number of neighbouring protons are represented in the number of splitting in the resonance 
peak. The type of neighbouring protons it represented in the strength of the coupling to 
neighbouring protons. The strength is measured as the difference in hertz (Hz) between a 
splitting peak and it is called the coupling constant (J). The coupling is a through bond 
phenomenon and will depend on the angle between the two C-H bonds in play, and the number 
of intervening bonds between the two C-H bonds. A better overlap gives a stronger coupling, and 
fewer intervening bonds will give a stronger coupling. Thus, interpretation of the splitting pattern 
can determine the number and type of neighbouring protons.  
1H-NMR spectra provide many useful information in structure elucidation. One disadvantage is 
signal overlap, which happens as the molecule complexity increases. 
 
3.5.2 Two-dimensional NMR 
In two-dimensional NMR, an additional time frame is applied in the experiment to yield higher 
resolution. The first application of two-dimensional NMR was in 1975, but the idea was presented 
years before. [44] The 2D spectra illustrate two coordinate axis of chemical shifts, and the 
magnitude of the signal is represented as a contour plot. It maps out interactions within the 
molecule, and the aim is to identify structural fragments and subsequently unify them. 
Experiments include homonuclear and heteronuclear correlations respectively.  
 
Correlation spectroscopy (COSY) 
Proton correlation spectroscopy is one of the most applied and simplest 2D experiments. COSY 
correlations can unravel complex coupling systems, which can be impossible in 1D spectra due to 
signal overlap. The spectrum corresponds to a plot of 1D 1H-NMR on both axis and if two nuclei 
couples to each other it will give rise to a cross peak. A cross peak contains precessional 
frequencies of both coupled nuclei, giving the chemical shift of one of the protons along the 
vertical axis, and the chemical shift of the other proton the horizontal axis. The cross peak is a 
result of the J-coupling, and only protons that are directly coupled give rise to a cross peak. The 
coupling can be either geminal 2JHH, vicinical 3JHH, or allylic 4-5JHH couplings respectively. By 
following the cross peak correlation around the spectrum, fragments of the structure can be 
==PAGE:30==
Page 29 of 106 
 
resolved based on proton-proton coupling, this is called a spin system. If the structure of the cross 
peak is sufficient, the J-coupling (Hz) can be determined, however this is not always the case. 
As singlets provides no correlation information, they can be suppressed in the experiment by a 
Double Quantum Filter (DQF). DQF also reduces dominant diagonal peaks, and together gives a 
less dense diagonal that makes interpretation easier. [45] DQF takes longer time, and some 
sensitivity might be lost.  
 
Total correlation spectroscopy (TOCSY) 
Total proton correlation spectroscopy allows the detection of full spin systems from individual 
protons.[46] The experiment permits distinct protons to couple to each other as long as carbon 
bearing protons between them can transfer the signal. Cross peaks can be generated between all 
members of a coupled spin system which can lead to a complete correlation of a spin system. 
Contrary to COSY, TOCSY records indirect correlation, which is correlation between nuclei that 
are not J-coupled. TOCSY is an extension of COSY correlations as the spin information of a nucleus 
can be transferred up to five bonds away. The technique is useful if COSY has a crowded area in 
which important couplings are lost. It is not a prerequisite all protons in a spins system gives rise 
to a cross peak. 
 
Hetero Single Quantum Correlation (HSQC) 
Heteronuclear correlation spectroscopy utilizes that hetero nuclei also couple to each other. [47] 
Some of the exploited nuclei are 1H and 13C and HSQC allows the assignment of carbons to a 
specific proton. As already mentioned, the sensitivity of 13C experiments is lower than 1H 
experiments. A way to circumvent this is by transferring the proton property to its attached 13C, 
which happens in HSQC. An important feature is the detection of diastereotopic protons, which 
has the same carbon shift but the proton shift can vary. One drawback is that quaternary carbons 
are not detected, as they have no proton to transfer the signal.  
 
Hetero Multi Bond Correlation (HMBC) 
Heteronuclear correlation spectroscopy utilizes that hetero nuclei also couples to each other. 
HMBC identifies long range coupling between protons and carbons. Contrary to HSQC, the proton 
spin property is transferred 2, or 3 carbons away. [48] Though weak, the spin property can also 
be transferred 4 carbons away. The major strength of HMBC is that it allows the coupling of 
proton to quaternary carbons. The 3JCH coupling is expected to be the strongest, and 2JCH and 4JCH 
are expected to be weaker. However, it can still be difficult to distinguish between them and it is 
not a prerequisite that all 3JCH are present. HMBC is still an important tool in the final assembling 
of the structure.  
==PAGE:31==
Page 30 of 106 
 
Heteronuclear Single Quantum Coherence-Total Correlation Spectroscopy (HSQC-
TOCSY) 
In HSQC-TOCSY, the second dimension has been resolved into the chemical shift of the carbon 
instead of the proton as in TOCSY. [48] It is a very powerful tool to resolve overlapping protons, 
as the chemical shift of carbon is often better resolved. As the long range proton couplings are 
dependent on the time lock in the TOCSY experiment, it is not a prerequisite that all are shown in 
the spectrum.

3.6 Data processing 
Dereplication is a procedure used to identify already known compounds in a crude extract 
mixture. It is an important strategy in the preliminary phase of natural product isolation as it 
focuses the screening toward unknown compounds, thus eliminating isolation of well-studied 
natural products. Traditional dereplication is often based on chromatography combined with 
spectroscopic methods and database searching. [49] Chromatography provides the necessary 
separation of a mixture prior to analysis. Spectroscopic methods as UV and MS provide structural 
information about the chromophore, the presence of conjugated double bonds, and the accurate 
mass. Newer technique is the high throughput dereplication driven by algorithms utilizing full MS 
spectrums at once. It speeds up the process using the whole spectrum as a query, contrary to 
traditional dereplication in which only one compound is analysed at a time. Both methods will be 
presented in the section. Two different techniques are presented regarding high throughput 
dereplication namely molecular networking and Dynamic Cluster Analysis. [50][51]

3.6.1 Traditional dereplication 
The task after data has been acquired from LC/ESI-MS is to tentatively assign molecular ion in 
chromatographic peaks in the spectrum. Finding compounds in databases requires 
unambiguously establishment of the mass, hence assigning the molecular ion [M+H]+ correct. 
Deriving the molecular formula gives an even more specific database search. The molecular ion 
can be assigned through identification of adduct patterns arising from characteristic mass 
differences,  m/z, to other ions in the spectrum. The most commonly observed adduct patterns 
will be mentioned. [49] In ESI+ the most common adduct, other than [M+H]+, is [M+Na]+ and it is 
observed +21.9820Da (22Da) from the molecular ion [M+H]+. The mass difference arises from 
the subtraction of a proton from the monoisotopic mass of sodium 22.9820Da because the 
observed mass difference in the spectrum is between [M+H]+ and [M+Na]+ and not between 
neutral M and [M+Na]+. The same thing applies for other observed adducts such as potassium, 
+37.9637Da (38Da), and ammonium, +17.0344Da (17Da), which gives rise to [M+K]+ and 
[M+NH4]+ adducts respectively. The mobile phase solvent can also contribute to adducts. 
Acetonitrile has a monoisotopic mass of 41.0265Da (41Da) and can bind [M+H]+. It is a neutral 
molecule, hence the observed mass difference between [M+H]+ and [M+H+MeCN]+ is the mass of 
acetonitrile. The analyte can also undergo neutral losses, for instance the loss of water is most 
often observed and corresponds to a mass differences of -18.0106Da (18Da). Sometimes the 
molecular ion is not present, or very weak, and the mass difference between other adducts can 
become applicable. For instance, a mass difference of five mass units between two ions indicate 
the presence of [M+Na]+ and [M+NH4]+. Most natural products are detectable in ESI+ but some are 
inefficient in accepting a positive charge making them poorly ionisable in ESI+. In such cases, ESI- 
can be used instead. ESI- gives different adduct patterns than ESI+. [49] 
Prior to adduct pattern analysis, the isotope pattern should be considered for two purposes. 
Firstly, the isotope pattern reveals the charge state of the compound, secondly, it also reveals the 
presence of, if any, halogens or sulphur. The charge state is established based on the spacing 
between the monoisotopic ions in the isotope pattern. The mass is given as mass-to-charge ratio 
m/z, and will be affected if z=1 or z=2. If z=2 it will reduce the mass by half because M is divided 
by two. Additionally, the spacing will be 0.5 mass units instead of 1. If the compound is double 
charged, every mass differences between adducts will be reduced by half if the adduct is also 
double charged. Consequently, the mass difference to a sodium adduct is 11 mass units, and the 
neutral loss of water corresponds to a mass decrease of nine etc. The isotope pattern represents 
the elemental composition and can reveal the presence of halogen and sulphur due to their 
different isotope abundancy. 37Cl, 81Br, and 34S are all +2 isotopes and have a natural abundancy 
of 31.98%, 49.31%, and 4.44 % respectively that gives rise to a notable A+2 peak. [52] Tentative 
analysis of the intensity of A+2 relative to A can reveal type and number of +2 element. 
==PAGE:33==
Page 32 of 106 
 
 
Another challenge when a peak is tentatively assigned is the presence of several dominant ions. 
If the ions do not have an obvious adduct relationship, deconvolution can establish if they are 
related. With deconvolution, extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) are overlaid and the retention 
time and peak shape are compared. If the ions are related, they will elute with the same retention 
time and possess the same peak shape.  
 
When [M+H]+ is assigned, other chemical characterizations can accommodate the compound 
elucidation such as UV, retention time, and isotope pattern. A built-in feature in Qulitative 
Analysis masshunter workstation can calculate putative elemental compositions based on the 
exact mass. Often it presents a range of formulas in which many can be excluded based 
contradictories between either UV and double bond equivalence (DBE), or isotope pattern and 
sulphur/halogens. The accurate mass or elemental composition can be used as a query in a 
database such as Antibase or Marinlit.[53][54] Sometimes it is possible to dereplicate a 
compound solely relying on MS, but if the database provides a sheer number of possibilities, 
orthogonal strategies can be implemented such as UV. 
 
3.6.2 Molecular networking 
An alternative to the manual dereplication strategy just presented is Molecular Networking (MN), 
an online-based based automatic dereplication strategy. [55] MN is an approach that utilizes the 
chemical information hidden within fragmentation spacing in MS/MS data. It is a comparative 
study where MS/MS spectra from unknown compounds are compared with MS/MS spectra from 
known compounds. Identical precursor mass and identical fragmentation patterns between 
unknown and known spectra makes it possible to identify the unknown compound. Successful 
dereplication requires a library of several of MS/MS spectrums from known. An applicable tool 
to use is Global Natural Products Social Molecular Networking (GNPS). GNPS has a collection of 
over 221,000 reference library spectra from 18,163 compounds that can be compared to. [50] An 
additional feature that makes MN a powerful alternative to traditional dereplication is the 
organization of MS/MS data based on similar chemistries. With this feature, experimentally 
acquired MS/MS data are compared to each other to capture putative analogues.[56] The premise 
is that structurally similar molecules will fragment in similar ways because the structural 
architecture and functional groups dictates dissociation fragmentation. In a dereplication study 
with a complex mixture containing many unknown analytes this can be a useful tool and a quick 
way to organize MS/MS data. Thus, MN with GNPS has two important qualities, firstly it is able to 
make automatic dereplication against a large database library with reference spectra, and 
secondly, it captures related analogues within the experimental data based on similar 
==PAGE:34==
Page 33 of 106 
 
fragmentation patterns. The strategy behind the two qualities is the same, namely alignment and 
comparison of fragmentation patterns.   
 
When MS/MS data has been searched against the reference library in GNPS, the result can be 
visualized in Cytoscape, a tool designed to visualize correlations in large datasets. [57] Each 
MS/MS spectra is represented as a node labelled with its precursor mass as illustrated in Figure 
9. Due to mathematic merging of MS/MS spectra prior to GNPS processing, the labelled precursor 
mass can vary from the original one observed in the spectrum with few ppm. [55] Edges 
connecting the nodes represent relatedness between molecules, hence analogues are clustered 
together, also illustrated in in Figure 9. The thickness of the edges shows how well compounds 
are aligned, but can also be expressed with a similarity score referred to as the cosine score. It 
ranges between 0 and 1, where 1 represents completely identical spectra. When data is processed 
with GNPS the lowest tolerant level of cosine score can be implemented.  
The library and experimental spectra are also provided with an alignment score between 0 and 
1, in which 1 means they are identical. Additionally, the reliability of the annotation of the library 
spectra are scored with one of three values; gold, silver, and bronze, in which gold is the best and 
means the structure has been assigned using NMR, together with other relevant techniques.    
MN as a dereplication strategy depends on the existence of related fragmentation patterns. This 
approach is limited if the mass spectrometer inherent low fragmentation, or the compounds have 
poor ionization ability.  
 
 
Figure 9 illustrates an example of a cluster generated by GNPS. Each node represents an MS spectrum. The pink 
colour indicates the spectrum has been matched and assigned to a structure. Yellow nodes remain unknown.

MN can be easily integrated into existing NP workflow, as MS/MS can be acquired concurrent 
with LC-MS analysis. MN can be used as the initial interpretation of a complex mixture with many 
compounds, or as a more specific tool to search for analogues of compounds with known 
bioactivity.  
 
3.6.3 Dynamic Cluster Analysis 
Dynamic cluster analysis (DCA) is an automated unbiased technique that can identify compounds 
with +2 elements such as Cl, Br, and S. [51] The DCA program filters all ions recorded within a 
chromatogram that have a certain intensity. The technique relies on three features namely, the 
A+1 to A+2 isotope spacing, the monoisotopic mass, and the A:A+2 intensity ratio. The program 
filters the chromatogram in a fully automated way by using the three parameters, and can search 
specifically for compounds containing either chlorine or sulphur. The spacing between A+1 and 
A+2 has not been utilized so far, but has shown to be more accurate compared to the A to A+2 
spacing to discriminate sulphur and halogens. The algorithm roughly splits the compounds into 
three groups. Compounds containing Cl and/or Br, compounds containing S and no halogens, and 
the rest based on the three features. The filter-setup used to identify halogens are called DCA-Hal 
in the program.

5 Results and discussion 
5.1 Project outline 
 
In this project, two approaches were used to characterise secondary metabolites in the 
ichthyotoxic microalgae P. polylepis, in an effort to elucidate its chemical profile. Dereplication 
was performed in the preliminary phase in an effort to elucidate already known compounds. After 
that, the project divided into two; a bioguided fractionation strategy and an analytical 
spectroscopy strategy. They were conducted in parallel studies, but will be presented 
individually. The bioguided fractionation procedure is presented first, followed by chemical 
characterisation of chlorinated metabolites. 
Prior to this thesis, no chemistry was known from the algae. However, the group had already 
purified one chlorinated compound and acquired NMR data, but the structure was not given, and 
nothing was known about its possible bioactivity.

5.2.3 Molecular Networking 
With GNPS MS/MS data from the crude extract (E1) was searched against the GNPS spectral 
library. This approach identified 51 compounds with a precursor mass tolerance of 2Da and a 
fragment ion mass tolerance of 0.5Da. Throughout the analysis, each spectrum was referred to as 
“node” and for simplicity its associated precursor mass was referred to as its integer number. 
Some of the molecular ions identified during traditional dereplication were matched with 
reference spectra in MN including A1, A2, A5a and A9. A2 and A5a appeared in the same cluster 
that consisted of 19 nodes in which 13 were identified as phosphocholines (PC), one as 
sphingomyelin (SM), and the rest did not result in any hits in the GNPS spectral library. All 
thirteen spectral libraries had been assigned with gold quality, which meant they had been 
correctly assigned. The cluster is illustrated in Figure 18 together with the polar head group of 
PC and SM. The structural similarities in the head groups explained why they were clustered 
together. They were both phospholipids with one or two acyl chains. The pink colour in Figure 18 
represents identified nodes and the yellow colour indicates unidentified nodes. The results were 
presented in Cytoscape with a library name and a formula, for instance A5a was presented as 
PC(16:0/0:0) and C24H50NO7P. The name indicated the PC had one chain with 16 carbons without 
double bonds. All the results within the cluster are listed in Table 4. The list of all 51 results is in 
appendix 8.1.  
To validate the GNPS results, MS/MS spectra were manually interpreted in reference to their 
suggested structure. Results with a mass error above 100 ppm between the spectral library mass 
and the experimental mass were considered irrelevant and not included in the validation. Six out 
of seven GNPS results could be confirmed by this approach. In Table 4 the confirmed nodes are 
color-coded pink. To illustrate the approach, A5a was used as an example, see below.

5.3 The pursuit of the fish toxin 
The crude extract (E3) was tested in RTgill-W1 cell bioassay to confirm the toxicity of P. polylepis. 
When gill cells were subjected to methanol and acetone extracts in the same concentrations, the 
methanol extract showed a higher mortality rate. The difference was more significant at lower 
concentrations, which is illustrated in Figure 20.  
 
Figure 20 illustrates the crude extract after extraction with acetone and methanol. The graph illustrates the 
methanol fraction is the most toxic and reaches 100% mortality rate. The acetone extract has lower mortality 
rate than the methanol extract, especially at lower concentrations, but still shows a high mortality rate at 80%.  
 
The result confirmed the toxicity of P. polylepis and suggested the toxin(s) was better dissolved 
in methanol. This is consistent with p. parvum, where the toxic component prymnesin is extracted 
from the methanol extracts. [23] The dual toxicity effect of both methanol and acetone extracts in 
RTgill-W1 bioassay indicated the toxin(s) could be spread in both extracts. A pooled fraction 
exhibited a mortality rate in between the ones observed for methanol and acetone extract 
respectively, which is illustrated in Figure 21. The shape of the curve indicates the toxicity levels 
of when the concentration decreases. 
 
Figure 21 illustrates a dose-response curve of a pooled sample with the methanol extract and acetone extract. 
It exhibits a mortality of 95%.

Based on the significant mortality rate observed in both the acetone- and methanol extract, the

5.3.2.3 Peak 4 
Another peak worth taking into account despite its low intensity, was peak 4. The predominant 
ion was m/z 1323.5735. It was notable because it was also identified in DCA. It was single charged,

identified with DCA, although with much lower intensity. With deconvolution it was clear they 
were related, and m/z 1323.5735 was assigned as [M+H]+. The complex isotope pattern made it 
difficult to determine an elemental composition, but based on the A+2 peak the compound was 
expected to possess two chlorines. The ion was only observed with high injection volumes, and 
due to sample limitation it was not possible to acquire MS/MS data under standard conditions on 
our Agilent system. However, the high mass of the compound was interesting as it resembled

5.3.3 Final remarks and discussion of bioassay as a bioguided technique  
The sensitivity increased markedly when using RT-gill-W1 cell assays instead of A. Saline. The 
mortality increased from being observed in four fractions to all fractions, despite the samples in 
RTgill-W1 cell assay were eight times as diluted than in the A. Saline assay. This indicated that 
RTgill-W1 cells were more sensitive to P. polylepis than A. Saline.  
The scattered toxicity observed for E-SPE and HPLC semi-preparative fractions in RTgill-W1 cell 
bioassays gave suspicion of false positives. Biological high concentrations were considered as a 
relevant source of error. Too high concentration can result in damaged gill cell which means the 
observed mortality is caused by high biomass instead of ichthyotoxicity. This has previously been 
described as a the fish-killing reason during HAB. [3] In this experiment, all fractions were diluted 
in the same volumes and then the whole sample was used  to give the same biological equivalence 
==PAGE:60==
Page 59 of 106 
 
in the test. The primary reason for this approach, contrary to the approach in which all fractions 
are tested in same concentration, was that dilution could dilute out the toxin(s). In this way the 
toxin(s), if present, was tested in the highest possible concentrations. Additionally, it appeared as 
an adequate method in a screening experiment. 
Based on these observations however, the concentration was considered to be a critical factor 
when P. polylepis is tested in RTgill-W1 cell assay. Methanol was also considered to have a 
contribution to the scattered effect. The very hydrophobic analytes from HPLC semi-preparative 
fractions needed more methanol; 2-3% instead of 1%, hence higher concentrations of organic 
solvents were introduced in the test. Concentrations below 5% were considered safe, but it has 
been proven that samples tested with DMSO exhibited slightly higher phycotoxicity than samples 
without. [60] The best way to accommodate this source of error was with a blank on the side, but 
that was not done.  
Additional to the sample perpetrations, disrupted cells could also cause false positive. This could 
have happened when the plates were inverted over the sink, hence this should be carried out 
carefully. In this experiment, random checks of the wells were performed in the microscope that 
excluded this source of error.  
To conclude, it was difficult to address if the scattered toxicity was a consequence of false 
positives or in fact a very toxic algae. The scattered toxicity was unexpected as the RTgill-W1 
assay previously had shown to be insensitive to P. parvum. [71] The observed effect pointed 
towards the need to develop a sufficient toxicity test-strategy for P. polylepis. It also suggested 
that other bioassays might be applicable.   
A consequence of the scattered effect in both E-SPE and semi-prep HPLC fractions was that the 
likelihood of making erroneously decisions increased. In order to use bioassays as fractionation 
guide, the results should guide in a clear direction. Despite this, the result pointed towards three 
interesting metabolites.

5.4 Prymnesins or not? 
As a different approach in the pursuit of the fish killing toxin in P. polylepis, a more targeted 
strategy was implemented. P. parvum are known to produce prymnesins, the toxin suggested for 
major fish kill during HAB. [24] As P. parvum and P. polylepis belongs to the same genus, it was 
obvious to clarify if P. polylepis were able to produce the same toxin. Prymnesins are 
characteristic in their supersized ladder-like polyether structure with molecular weight between 
1600 and 2400 mass units. The toxin often appears in the chromatogram as a double charged 
analyte [M+2H]2+ with an m/z between 800 and 1200. [23] Additionally, they can be identified 
with the fragmentation ion due to the neutral loss of the glycone moiety. EIC of 16 identified 
prymnesin A- B- and C- types suggested no presence of prymnesins in the crude extract of P. 
polylepis. [23] In a second attempt to identify prymnesins, DCA was used. Prymnesins has a 
chlorine in its structure and is a candidate for the DCA-Hal filter. On our Dionex LC Bruker system, 
prymnesin-like compounds elute within 4-7 minutes. They are considered prymnesin-like if they 
are double charged, and has an m/z value between 800 and 1200. [51] No analytes were found to 
fall within the specific criteria of prymnesins. 
 
Neither EIC of masses from identified prymnesins, nor filtered search of halogenated compounds 
with DCA gave an indication of prymnesins in P. polylepis. However, DCA revealed many 
halogenated metabolites within a given intensity. In reference to the structure of prymnesins and 
the presence of chlorine, a hypothesis was raised if the chlorine was a characteristic feature of 
toxins in the genus Prymnesium.  Based on this hypothesis, the results from DCA were used as a 
targeted strategy to identify chlorinated compounds in P. polylepis as possible toxins.  
 
5.4.1 Identification of other chlorinated compounds by DCA 
DCA reported 68 halogenated compounds with an intensity of at least 25x103, which was the 
lowest intensity limit to avoid analytes that were also identified in the blank sample. To search 
for the most intense chlorinated compounds the cut-off intensity was adjusted. With the 
requirement of an intensity of at least 106 five analytes were identified. None of them had been 
identified with MN, which made them candidates as novel compounds.

5.4.1.1 Compound 1/polylepin A 
Among the five analytes identified with DCA, the most intense one was m/z 676.0836 which is 
illustrated in Figure 25. Based on adduct pattern analysis m/z 676.0836 was assigned to [M+H]+. 
The compound was named polylepin A. One fragmentation ion at m/z 596.1246 was not assigned 
as an adduct, but deconvolution revealed it was related to polylepin A. The mass difference 
corresponded to the exact mass of a sulphate ester SO3-. The mass spectrum had been recorded 
on HRMS, which made the calculation masses exact and thus substantiates the assignment of SO3 
in the compound. The fragmentation was expected to originate from the hydrolysis of a sulphate 
group SO4. In Figure 25 the EIC of m/z 676.0836 is overlaid with the BPC to illustrate its presence 
within the crude extract. The mass spectrum displays the mass of the A+2 peak instead of the 
monoisotopic mass A, because A+2 was more intense than A. The distinct isotopic pattern can be 
explained in terms of halogenation. Based the DCA-Hal analysis it was known polylepin A had 
either bromine and/or chlorine in its structure. Additional, in-source fragmentation revealed a 
sulphur from the sulphur ester. Both halogens and sulphur could contribute to the isotope 
pattern, but due to sulphurs low isotope contribution the compound was expected to be highly 
halogenated. The first interesting thing to note about the isotope pattern was its extension up to 
an A+8 peak. This indicated the presence of at least four isotopes of 34S and/or 37Cl and/or 81Br, 
in which three of them had to be 37Cl and/or 81Br in order to get the +8 peak.  
The combination was not expected to be 1S and 3Br as Br gives rise to a symmetrical isotope 
pattern due to its 50:50 distribution of 79Br and 81Br. Thus, if bromine was present, it was assumed 
to be in combination with chlorines to accommodate the asymmetric isotope pattern. 
Furthermore, the neutral mass 675.0836 indicated an uneven number of nitrogen, hence at least 
one nitrogen was present in the compound. Based on the criteria of at least one sulphur, four 
oxygen, 1 nitrogen, and three Cl and/or Br, the formula predictor presented numerous elemental 
compositions within a mass error of 3ppm. Few could be excluded based on their DBE of 0, but 
too many still remained and it was not possible to determine the correct formula based on MS 
data alone. NMR data was required to aid the elemental composition elucidation. 
polylepin A had already been purified, see appendix 8.4, and NMR data were acquired from that 
sample.

5.5 Structure elucidation 
This section includes a full structure elucidation of polylepin A based on NMR and MS/MS data.  
 
5.5.1 NMR polylepin A 
In order to elucidate the structure of polylepin A the following NMR data had been acquired: 1H- 
NMR, DQF- COSY, HSQC, TOCSY, HSQC-TOCSY, and HMBC. The final structure is illustrated in  
Figure 26. Furthermore, Table 7 provides a full list of the assignment including the chemical shifts  
of carbons and protons, and HMBC correlations. See appendix 8.5 for all the spectra used. 
 
Figure 26 illustrates the final structure of polylepin A. All carbons are assigned with a number, and the 
protons at the carbon are related to the same number. All chemical shifts are listed in Table 7. 
 
First, the elemental composition was determined. HSQC- and HMBC correlations furnished 24 
carbons which could be included as an additional criterion. The elemental compositions were 
now reduced to ten suggestions within a mass error of 3ppm. Two of them could be excluded 
because they had less than four Cl and/or Br and/or S. The experimental isotope pattern of 
polylepin A was revised again and compared to the theoretical patterns of the eight suggested 
formulas. Based on these comparisons, polylepin A could be assigned with the elemental 
composition C24H38Cl5NO8S. The isotope ratio is given in Figure 41. The calculated m/z of 
C24H38Cl5NO8S was 676.0834 which gave a mass difference of 0.3ppm from polylepin A and made 
the formula reliable. The double bond equivalent was calculated as four.  
 
Preliminary observations prior to structure elucidation 
The HSQC spectra revealed that the structure included two methyl groups, nine methylene in 
which eight of them were diasterotopic, and nine methines. Based on HMBC-correlation, four 
tertiary carbons were present. One of them was assigned as a carbonyl based on the carbon shift 
at δc 174ppm, which indicates an amide, a carboxylic acid or an ester. Two of them resigned in 
the olefinic range at δc 132ppm and 137ppm respectively, and the last one resigned at δc 104ppm.  
Moreover, 1H-NMR showed two downfield protons at δH 6.42ppm and δH 6.85ppm respectively 
indicating olefinic protons. The multiplicity was a doublet of doublet (dd) consisting of a large 
and a small coupling constant. The dd was only clear enough at δH 6.42ppm to be measured, and 
==PAGE:65==
Page 64 of 106 
 
revealed a coupling constant at 11Hz and 1Hz suggesting a cis or trans and an allylic coupling 
respectively.  
The high chemical shift observed in both C-NMR and H-NMR confirmed the presence of 
deshielding elements consistent to oxygen, chlorine and nitrogen observed in the elemental 
composition.   
 
Five detected spin systems based on DQF-COSY correlations 
The DQF-COSY technique allows proton-proton coupling through a 3JHH correlation. The 
inspection of DQF-COSY disclosed five spin systems and are illustrated in Figure 27. The olefinic 
spin system needed help from HMBC correlations to be assigned correct. the two downfield 
protons at δH 6.42ppm and δH 6.85ppm revealed HMBC correlation to the quaternary olefinic 
carbons, at δc 132ppm and 137ppm. A way to include two sp2 carbons in addition to the ones 
assigned to δH 6.42 and δH 6.85 was by two conjugated double bonds as illustrated in Figure 27, 
spin system 2. The diene explained the coupling constant of 11 Hz, which corresponded to the 
3JHH between H-6 and H-7. The small coupling constant of 1 Hz fitted a 4JHH allylic coupling from 6-
H to a proton at C-4. The presence of a diene also included an additional double bond and together 
with the carbonyl that added up to three, thus only one double bond was left to be resolved. 
 
Figure 27 illustrates the five spin systems detected in the DQC-COSY spectrum. Each spin system is assigned 
with a number 1-5. 
 
One of the major challenges in DQF-COSY was different protons with identical or very similar 
chemical shifts.  
 
Assembling the five spin systems into a long chain based on TOCSY, HSQC-TOCSY, 
and HBMC 
TOCSY and HSQC-TOCSY was important techniques to extend the spin system from the DQF-COSY 
correlations, especially in the crowded areas. The techniques enabled the assembling of some of 
the fragments in Figure 27, in association with HMBC correlations.  
From the HSQC-TOCSY spectrum which are illustrated in Figure 28, it was observed that C-12 
from spin system 3 and C-13, which had not been assigned to any spin system, shared many of 
the same correlations. This indicated that they were adjacent to each other. Another important 
thing to note was the correlation of H-14 to C-12 and C-13, also illustrated in Figure 28. H-14

Additional, H-15 showed a 3JCH correlation to C-13.

Assembling of functional groups to the carbon backbone based on various 
experiments and calculations 
With the backbone in place, the remaining elements that needed to be positioned included five 
chlorines, one sulfate group, four oxygen and three protons. The carbons with a missing element 
included carbon 3, 5, 8, 10, 14, 16, and 20. It is illustrated in Figure 30 with an “x”.  
From general table values it was given that carbons bonded to chlorine or oxygen resonated 
approximately in the same ppm range 13C-NMR (35-80ppm). [72] Chlorinated carbons were only 
expected to resonate 5ppm upfield in reference to oxygenated carbons. This made the positioning 
challenging as all the carbons mentioned above had a carbon shift within 60-75 ppm.  
 
In an effort to identify non-hydroxyl bearing carbons over hydroxyl bearing carbons, a deuterium 
experiment was performed. An HSQC spectrum was recorded in MeOH-D3. [73] The introduction 
of protons in the solvent reintroduced the proton in the hydroxyl groups, which should affect the 
chemical shift of the carbons bearing it in reference to ones recorded in MeOH-D4.  
Only one significant change in 13C-ppm was observed at C-20, indicating a hydroxyl group at C-
20. The result could be explained in terms of tautomerism. Under these conditions the hydroxyl 
proton at C-20 could be able to tautomerise with the carbonyl at C-22, which affected the chemical 
shift illustrated in Figure 31. This was the only position in the structure where tautomerism could 
==PAGE:68==
Page 67 of 106 
 
happen and explained why C-20 was the only carbon affected. In Figure 32, C-20 is assigned to a 
hydroxyl group. 
 
 
Figure 31 illustrates the effect of MD3OH solvent. The green signals are from the first experiment with 
MD3OD, and the pink signals are from the second experiment with MD3OH. Only C-20 exhibits a change in the 
chemical shift. 
 
As only one hydroxyl group was revealed with the above experiment, ACD I-lab was used to 
calculate putative carbon shifts. With a simplified model the chemical shift of C-23 with either 
three chlorines or three hydroxyl groups were predicted to be 105ppm and 115ppm respectively. 
The calculations indicated a tri-chlorinated carbon that could be substantiated in the literature.   
A range of tri-chlorinated compounds had been isolated from Dysidea, a marine sponge, see 
Figure 42. [74][75] An NMR study from such compound assigned the tri-chlorinated carbon to δc 
105.1 ppm [76], which was in proximity to C-23 at δc 104ppm. This enhanced the assignment of 
three chlorines at C-23. Additionally, the chlorinated moiety from the isolated compound was 
positioned in the same fashion as C-23, constituting a tri chlorine-ethyl together with C-1 and C-
2.  
 
Calculation for the carbons in the diene, C-5 and C-8, were performed in the same way. The 
calculation predicted carbon-shift around 150ppm for chlorinated bonded vinyl carbons. 
Oxygenated vinyl carbons were calculated to have a chemical shift at around 180ppm. As C-5 and 
C-8 resonated around 135ppm they were assigned with a chlorine, as 180ppm was too far off. In 
Figure 32 the positions of chlorines are illustrated. 
 
Elucidation of the ether-ring based on HMBC correlations 
An important structural restriction at this point was that only two oxygen and one proton were 
left to be assigned besides the sulphate group, but the structure had four missing spots including 
C-3, C-10, C-14, and C-16. All of them possessed a high chemical shift, indicating the attachment 
of a deshielding element. It suggested that two of them had to be connected to the same oxygen 
==PAGE:69==
Page 68 of 106 
 
in a ring fashion. Additionally, it would account for the last double bond equivalent. Several 
possibilities existed in ring formation over an oxygen atom including four-, six-, eight-, nine-, and 
fourteen-membered rings. The six-membered ring generated between C-10 and C-14 was 
energetically most favourable. To confirm this hypothesis, any HMBC correlation, even weak, 
between these two carbons were examined. The proton at δH 3.22ppm (H-10) had a 3JCH to C-14, 
which was not included in the beginning because it was weaker. The presence of an ether ring in 
marine natural product is not unusual, a whole class of phycotoxins has been categorized based 
on their poly-ether ring conjunction. [3] The putative structure based on NMR data is illustrated 
in Figure 32. 
 
Figure 32 illustrates the backbone of the putative structure, which is based on NMR interpretation. The 
position of chlorines are substantiated by theoretical calculations as well as other studies in the literature.  
 
By comparing the chemical shifts in the ring to other similar structures with a six-membered 
ether ring, C-10 and C-14 appeared to resonate at lower ppm in polylepin A. Other structures 
showed chemical shifts ranging from 67ppm to 75ppm, which was higher than C-10 and C-14. 
[77] [78] The lower chemical shift of 60ppm for C-10 and C-14 opened the possibility of a nitrogen 
atom in the ring instead of an oxygen, and a carboxylic acid instead of an amide at C-22. 
Compounds with a piperdine ring and carbons corresponding to C-10 and C-14 showed chemical 
shifts of 56-57ppm, which were a bit lower than the observed shifts in polylepin A. [79] Regarding 
the carbonyl, C-22 resonated at 174ppm which was consistent with both an amide and a 
carboxylic acid. Thus, it was difficult to assign C-10, C-14 and C-22 unambiguous with NMR data. 
The putative structure was continuing assigned as in Figure 32, but the observations were kept 
in mind throughout the structure elucidation.  
 
At this point, only C-3 and C-6 were missing an element. It was impossible to distinguish between 
the sulfonated and hydroxylated carbons, as they were both bonded to an oxygen which gave rise 
to the same chemical shift in 1H-NMR and 13C-NMR. The limitation in NMR made MS/MS a feasible 
tool. MS/MS was also relevant to confirm the current structure to enhance the elucidation.

Final remarks on the structure and perspective 
In an effort to elucidate if polylepin A had an amide or a secondary amine polylepin A was 
attempted to react with AccQ●tag, which has previously shown to work with prymnesin. [23] 
polylepin A was reacted with AccQ●tag alongside with glycine as a positive control. polylepin A 
did not react with AccQ●tag which indicated the presence of an amide, as a secondary amine was 
expected to react.  
 
==PAGE:73==
Page 72 of 106 
 
polylepin A had many stereocenters which had not been determined. The stereochemistry of C-
2, C-3, C-10, C14, C-16, and C-20 could be determined with NOESY correlations. NOESY allows 
spatial correlations between protons that are in proximity of each other. Optical rotations are also 
a relevant technique to assign the stereochemistry. Optically active compounds will have a 
characteristic rotation of plane polarized light and makes it possible to differentiate between 
enantiomers. 
 
In the end, it is worth mentioning the UV profile of polylepin A, illustrated in Figure 25. It had UV 
absorption around 250nm and the only contributor to UV absorption in the structure was the 
diene part of the molecule. It was expected that the chlorine atoms had a small contribution. The 
low energy system in the diene gives rise to an UV-profile in the UV-vis range, and explained the 
colourlessness of polylepin A.  
 
Lastly, the polylepin A eluted in two fractions from the aforementioned semi-prep HPLC 
experiment, namely F5 and F6. The amounts where too small to be measured, <0.1mg, thus the 
concentration tested in RTgill-W1 bioassay were assigned as <62.5 μg/mL. F5 and F6 exhibited a 
mortality of 70% and 80% respectively at the start concentration and were considered 
interesting in an ichthyotoxic context. It was inconclusive to make a dose-response curve as the 
correct concentration was unknown. Polylepin A should be tested in a pure form to establish a 
validate toxic effect, but due to time and mass limitation it was not prioritised.

5.6 Analogues to polylepin A 
In reference to the interesting structure of polylepin A, it was interesting to see if any analogues 
were present P. polylepis. Different approaches were used to study this. MN was an obvious tool 
to see if polylepin A clustered to any other compound. Another approach was to search for similar 
chemical characteristics like MS/MS fragmentation pattern. Lastly, DCA was used to identify 
other types of chlorinated compounds, this is included in Appendix 8.7. 
 
5.6.1 Identification of polylepin B 
In MN polylepin A clustered with another compound at m/z 710.0441 with a cosine score of 0.9 
similar chemical entities. The compound at m/z 710.0441 was assigned as [M+H]+ based on the 
adduct pattern, as illustrated in Figure 37. It exhibited similar mass spectrum as polylepin A, and 
was abbreviated polylepin B. The mass difference between the two compounds was 33.9605 
corresponding to the subtraction of a proton and addition of a chlorine. This was substantiated 
by the isotope patter, which is illustrated more clearly in Figure 41. The figure illustrates a small 
increase in the A+4 peak in polylepin B compared to polylepin A, which was the result of an extra 
chlorine atom. Polylepin B eluted approximate one minute after polylepin A in the chromatogram, 
having the same UV spectrum. Altogether, this suggested the elemental composition 
C24H37Cl6NSO8. 
 
Figure 37 illustrates the mass spectrum of m/z 710.0441 polylepin B and all the adducts.  
 
In a first attempt, MS/MS data was used to compare the structural similarities of polylepin A and 
polylepin B. In Table 8 some of the most predominant fragment ions are presented. It was 
observed that they possessed many of the same neutral losses that indicated that polylepin A and 
polylepin B were analogues of each other. Interestingly, the same loss of C10H13Cl5O was observed, 
==PAGE:76==
Page 75 of 106

5.6.3 Discussion of structural features in polylepin 
As already mentioned, a range of tri-chlorinated compounds has been isolated from Dysidea, a 
marine sponge, such as the compound groups diketopiperazine and dysidin illustrated in Figure 
42. [74][75] Another example of a tri chlorine-ethyl compound is herbamide, which has been 
==PAGE:79==
Page 78 of 106 
 
isolated from several marine organisms. Interestingly, herbamide B, which has been isolated from 
cyanobacteria Lyngbya majuscule, has a specific stereochemistry that makes it biological active. 
It has shown in vitro activity against the parasite L. donovani with an EC50 of 5.9 M. [80] 
herbamide B is illustrated in Figure 42, and it resembles polylepin from C-1 to C-8 with few 
variations. 
 
Figure 42 Herbamide, a natural product isolated from several marine organisms. In possess a tri chlorine-

Other tri chlorine-ethyl containing compounds has also been isolated from L. majuscule. 
Interestingly, L. majuscule is listed as a harmful micro algae at IOC-UNESCO taxonomic list and 
has been found to contain over 70 biological active components. One of them are barbamide, 
which is structurally similar to herbamide B with a tri chlorine-ethyl incorporated in the 
structure. Bioassay of barbamide shoved ichthyotoxicity with an LC50 at 25 µg/mL. [81]  
 
The combinations of the sub-structure tri chlorine-ethyl with neither vinyl chlorides nor a 
sulphate group were reported in Marinlit and Antibase. The sulphate group in itself, however, 
was identified in over 1000 compounds in Marinlit. In compositions with one or more chlorine 
atoms, 21 compounds were identified. An example of compounds with several chlorines and a 
sulphate group was chlorosulfolipid. Chlorosulfolipids have a structural role in algal membranes, 
but they have also been described in context with DSP. [26] 
Polylepsin resembled some of the chlorosulfolipids isolated from different algae in their poly-
chlorinated carbon backbone. For instance, the most commonly known chlorosulfolipid 
Danicalipin isolated from the algae Ochromonas Danica has a backbone of 22 carbons, six 
chlorines and two sulphate groups. [82] One of its analogues deviates with the replacement of a 
sulphate group against a proton. In a brine shrimp bioassay they showed toxicity with an LC50 of 
0.27 and 2.2 g/mL respectively. [83] In that study, chlorine was not related to the toxic effect, 
however the presence of sulphate indicated an increased toxic effect. 
Malhamensilipin A is another chlorosulfolipid isolated from the algae Poterioochromonas 
malhamenis and is illustrated in Figure 43. It has a backbone of 24 carbons, six chlorines in which 
==PAGE:80==
Page 79 of 106 
 
one of them is a vinyl chlorine as in polylepin, and two sulphate groups. [84] Malhamensilipin A 
has been demonstrated to be an inhibitor of protein tyrosine kinase, which is an important 
enzyme in the signalling pathway mediating the effects of growth. [85]  
 
Figure 43 illustrates the structure of Malhamensilipin A. It resembles polylepin as it has a backbone of 24 
carbon, six chlorines and a sulphate ester. 
 
Two last compounds worth mentioning in this context is karlotoxin, isolated from K. veneficum 
and prymnesins from P. parvum. Karlotoxin exist in different analogues, one with a vinyl chlorine 
and one with a sulphate group at two different positions. KmTx2 with a vinyl chlorine and no 
sulphate is illustrated in Figure 5. As mentioned, it is the only toxin that has been directly related 
to kill fish and it has shown LC50 of 0.38 µg/mL in gill cell assay. In a study however, the 
sulfonation indicated a reduction in the toxicity contrary to the chlorosulfolipid study. The vinyl 
chloride on the other hand was assigned to amplify the effect in combination with the length of 
the lipophilic arm. [86] Prymnesin-B1 also possess a vinyl chlorine, as illustrated in Figure 5 and 
the effective concentration EC50 has shown to be 6nM RTgill-W1 cell assay. 
 
From this short presentation, the biological activity of isolated marine natural products with 
chlorines and sometimes also sulphate was illustrated. The biological activity ranged from anti-
infective drugs to phycotoxins and ichthyotoxins. A specific toxic character of tri-chlorine ethyl, 
chlorine atoms, and the sulphate group has been ambiguous, but the various examples suggest a 
possible biological activity within them. In the same way, the entities are suspected to be 
intimately related to the unknown function, if any, of polylepsin.  
Additionally, a concurrent observation was that all heavy chlorinated molecules, with three or 
more chlorines, and in combination with a sulphate listed in Antibase and Marinlit are isolated 
from algae. This supported the origin of polylepin as marine metabolites.

6 Conclusion  
 
Prior to the thesis, no metabolites from the microalgae P. polylepis had been chemically 
characterized. This thesis addressed different chemical compounds in P. polylepis which were 
identified by various techniques. By using molecular networking as a dereplication tool with 
GNPS library spectra, it was possible to identify PC in P. polylepis, a group of lipids which possibly 
originated from the cell membrane. The example of PC also statute MN as an excellent tool to 
extend dereplication in its ability to cluster analogues.  
The crude extract of P. polylepis exhibited toxic responses in RTgill-W1 cell line. The toxic 
complexity increased when E-SPE and semi-preparative HPLC fractions were tested, and it gave 
a scattered toxicity in all fractions, which was unexpected. The effect was primarily ascribed to a 
matter of high concentrations. The most potent fraction with a dose-response curve that showed 
100% mortality at the lowest tested concentration, 7.8μg/mL, revealed three major metabolites; 
Loliolide and a choline-related metabolite and a high molecular-weight chlorinated compound. 
The first has previously been related to cytotoxicity. The latter was also identified in DCA and the 
isotope pattern indicated two chlorine atoms in the formula. E-SPE experiment also indicated the 
compound had a cation, such as an amine. Although speculative, the metabolite shared 
similarities to other known ichthyotoxins such as karlotoxins and prymnesins. All three 
metabolites were considered relevant in future work, but the first and the latter were apparently 
most interesting. Additionally, due to the wide-ranging biological activity reported about 
Loliolide, it appeared as an outmost priority to confirm if P. polylepis can synthesise this 
compound and if it can, then in which stereospecific conformation.  
In a parallel study to the bioassays, another chlorinated compound was identified in the pursuit 
of the fish toxin. Using NMR and HR-MS it was possible to elucidate the full planar structure of the 
novel compound polylepin A. It revealed a distinct structure that possessed five chlorines, a 
sulphate ester, an ether ring and a terminal primary amide. Three of the chlorines were 
positioned in a tri-chloro-ethyl fashion, a substructure which can also be found in other biological 
active compounds isolated from the marine environment. It all speculated that polylepin A could 
possess unique biological activities. In RTgill-W1 cell line, the fraction with polylepin A exhibited 
80% mortality at a concentration lower than 62µg/mL. The compound should be tested as a pure 
compound to confirm the effect. 
In addition, two other analogues of polylepin A were identified, polylepin B and C. They had 
similar carbon backbones but differed by one additional chlorine, and one of them was missing 
sulphate ester, defining a new compound class of linear chlorinated metabolites in P. polylepis. 
Altogether, this thesis presented new insight into the chemistry and likely bioactive compounds 
in P. polylepis.

8.4 Appendix: purification polylepin A 
Extraction and isolation 
 
Algal cultivation. C. polylepis P-039 (kindly provided by Bente Edvardsen) were 
maintained in autoclaved seawater with f/2 nutrition kept at 15 °C and an irradiance of 
250 μmol photons m−2 s−1. The culture was harvested in late exponential phase (200.000 
cells/mL) 20 L at a time by continuous centrifugation using an Avanti Series equipped 
with a JCF-Z rotor (3500 G, 40 mL/min, Beckman Coulter).  
Extraction and Isolation. A total of 40 L C. polylepis P-039 (approximately 8×109 cells) 
were extracted with acetone (4×40 mL) and MeOH (4×40 mL) and dried separately. The 
compound was attempted retained on a 10 g Sepra SAX column (Phenomenex) but was 
unsuccessful. Instead, the fractions generated from this purification attempt were pooled 
and subject to C18 fractionation. The acetone and MeOH pools were separately loaded 
onto a 10 g C18 column and eluted in a step-wise MeOH-H2O gradient in 10 % point MeOH 
increments from 10 % to 100 % MeOH with each fraction being 150 mL. The compound 
eluted in the 70 % MeOH fraction. Final isolation was achieved using semi-preparative 
HPLC using a 250×10 mm LUNA(2) C18 column (Phenomenex) using A: mQ H2O + 50 
ppm TFA and B: MeCN + 50 ppm TFA. The compound was eluted using a gradient from: 
40-50 % B in 2 min, then increased to 55 % B in 8 min, then increased to 58 % B in 1 min 
and finally increased to 100 % B in 1 min. A final round of semi-prep chromatography 
was necessary in order to remove a phthalate impurity. This was achieved on a 150×9.4 
mm Zorbax phenyl column (Agilent) using a linear gradient from 40-80 % B in 20 min of 
the above-mentioned solvents.

8.7 Appendix: Identification of Compound 1  
 
Within the five most intense halogenated ion, besides polylepin A and B, was the compound at 
m/z 700.3700, abbreviated compound 1. It was not clustered with anything in MN. The 
differences between polylepin A, B, and C, and then compound 1 was the isotopic pattern and the 
mass defect. Both thing indicated a different elemental composition, thus a different compound. 
It had a sodium adduct which made is possible to assign compound 1 as [M+H]+. The natural mass 
699.37 indicated the compound had at least one nitrogen, or another uneven number of nitrogen. 
From the isotope pattern ratio, the compound was expected to possess two chlorines. Bromine 
was excluded based on the A+2 peak, which was approximately 60% of the molecular ion, see 
Figure 46. It was difficult to derive a formula that fitted these criteria, and the isotope pattern. 
 
 
Figure 46 illustrates the isotope pattern of compound 4. The A+2 peak indicates a double-chlorinated 
compound. 
During a purification strategy, no UV absorption appeared and the isolation had to be based on a 
time-dependent manner. The compound was only partially purified, and due to time limitations 
not finished.
