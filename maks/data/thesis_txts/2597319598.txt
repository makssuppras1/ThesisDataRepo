Abstract 
Data collection methods of benthic sampling using Van Veen grabs have previously been 
employed to investigate both the physiology and distribution of the benthic isopod, Saduria 
entomon (S. entomon). This paper investigates the spatial and temporal distribution of this 
species over time and space in the central and southern Baltic using an alternative method – 
fisheries survey trawling data of bycatches of S. entomon. The results of analysis from this 
study confirm that this gear and its bycatches can resolve significant spatial patterns in 
distribution and biomass and which are consistent with the known geographical distribution 
seen in previous studies using alternative, targeted sampling methods.  
Results from GAM analysis reveal that variables which are temporally non-varying in time, 
longitude and latitude and depth, have a significant influence on the distribution of S. 
entomon.  In general models having these variables explained more variability in distribution 
and biomass than direct measures of the hydrographic environment (temperature, salinity, 
oxygen concentration).  
Time series analysis revealed fluctuations from year to year in Catch per unit effort (CPUE) 
and probability of occurrence, however very little seasonal variation was apparent. 
GAM models used explained up to 54% of the deviance in probability of occurrence based on 
presence and absence models, while models having CPUE as response variable and using only 
positive values explained up to 78% of the deviance. Model results revealed how multiple 
environmental conditions (temperature, salinity and oxygen concentration) affect distribution 
and biomass and could explain up to 63 % of observed deviances.   
The distribution and abundance of S. Entomon was identified to be constrained by abiotic 
factors, in particular salinity, temperature and depth. Decreases in abundance were identified 
with increasing salinity. CPUE declined at temperatures greater than 8 ̊C. While optimum 
depths of between approximately 50 – 70m encountered the highest CPUE. 
These results may be able to explain how distribution and biomass of S. entomon might change 
in the future under changing ecosystem conditions.  In general, distribution and biomass data 
for S. entomon collected through fisheries survey trawls complements existing grab-sampling 
datasets.  Such data opens new possibilities for investigating the ecology of this species and 
how it interacts with the food web and surrounding abiotic environment.

Introduction 
 
One of the main sources of prey for cod in the Baltic sea is the benthic species Saduria Entomon 
(henceforth known as S. entomon), which is a benthic isopod (SPECIES INFORMATION 
SHEET Saduria entomon, 2013). S. entomon has been estimated to account for 40 – 50% in 
weight of an adult cod diet during a period between the 1960s and 1980s in the Central Baltic 
based on analysis of stomach content data (UZARS, 1994) and is the most common food source 
for cod alongside sprat, herring and other invertibrates (Sparholt, 1994). It has furthermore 
been reported that the concentration of this prey in cod stomachs has declined in recent years 
in the Eastern Baltic, suggesting a change in the Baltic ecosystem is taking place (Neuenfeldt 
et al., 2019) although much about the abundance of  S. entomon and its importance to 
predator’s diet as a benthic food source for cod is still unknown (Casini et al., 2016). 
Furthermore, S. entomon is also a major prey item in flounder (Platichthys flesus) stomachs. 
For example, diets of flounder greater than 20cm in length had 30 – 70% in weight as S. 
entomon and consumed three times as much S. entomon as cod during the period 2015 - 2017. 
(Haase et al., 2020) 
Variability in S. entomon abundance and spatial distribution are hypothesized to be related to 
abiotic conditions (especially bottom oxygen, salinity and temperature) and the abundance of 
predators, such as cod and flounder (Haase et al., 2020)(Borecka, Janas and Kendzierska, 
2016). Changes in abundance and distribution of S. entomon due to expansion of hypoxic 
areas could also have led to changes in diets, growth and condition of cod and a switch towards 
pelagic prey (Neuenfeldt et al., 2019).  
Existing data covering parts of the Baltic, collected by analysis of benthic Van Veen grab 
samples, is available for S. entomon for further investigation. However, in general these data 
have limited spatial distribution due to a limited number of repeated sampling points 
concentrated in relatively small areas; furthermore most of the benthic grab samples are 
limited to certain time periods and seasons. (Gogina et al., 2020) The available data for 
estimating distribution and abundance of S. entomon in the Baltic is therefore limited in time 
and space.  
The aims of this study are therefore; 1) to assess whether data collected using fishing trawling 
gear is a suitable method for analysing the time and space distribution of S. entomon and 
whether it can improve existing data collection methods and analysis of the species ecology; 
and 2) investigate whether these methods can be used to help understand factors affecting the 
temporal and spatial variability in distribution and abundance. These objectives are important 
not only for ecologists’ understanding of the species but also for fisheries management as the 
==PAGE:5==
 
 
 
5 
species has an important role in food web dynamics with consequences for commercial fish 
species. 
This project will use data from bottom trawl surveys to estimate the abundance and spatial 
distribution of S. entomon over several years and during different seasons (spring, fall). 
Variations in density will be compared with oceanographic factors including temperature, 
salinity and oxygen. Statistical analyses will assess whether an effort-standardized index of 
abundance (Catch per unit effort;CPUE) changes over - or between years and seasons and 
whether environmental conditions can explain the species distribution.  
In order to investigate the species distribution, time series of abundance and spatial 
distribution will be investigated. Generalised Additive Models (GAM) are important for 
ecological modelling where the available data is sparse, over large geographical areas (such as 
is the case with survey trawling data) and can be used to predict the distribution and optimum 
environmental conditions based on existing knowledge of the ecology. (Drexler, 2016). 
The thesis reads as follows; First an introduction to S. entomon and its ecology, as well as a 
description of the location of the study area and the oceanographic conditions in play (section 
1). Next follows the methods section covering the data collection and statistical methods used 
in the analysis (section 2). A summary of the results (section 3) and finally a discussion of how 
successful the fishing trawl data is for describing the species distribution and future 
recommendations will be covered (section 4).  
 
1.1 Saduria entomon Ecology 
 
S. entomon is a brackish – freshwater species which is generally encountered at cold 
temperatures of between 0.5 and 10 ̊C and is commonly found in fine muds or sandy sediment 
habitats. (Leonardsson, 1986)(Gogina et al., 2020) Based on the results of laboratory 
experiments it can also survive at temperatures of up to 20 ̊C (Borecka, Janas and Kendzierska, 
2016), but the effect of temperature on reproductive success and other demographic rates is 
unknown. In the southern Baltic the marine S. entomon’s  optimum salinity is 9-14 parts per 
thousand and the Baltic population is unable to adapt to freshwater environment (Borecka, 
Janas and Kendzierska, 2016).  
The Baltic is a semi enclosed sea and in general, due to connectivity with the higher salinity 
North Sea via the Kattegat and the Øresund, average salinity increases towards the west to 
salinity levels above the species favourised salinity range. Therefore, the species is seldom 
encountered west of the island Hiddensee, located to the north east of the German Baltic coast 
==PAGE:6==
 
 
 
6 
due to salinity levels greater than what the species prefer (HAAHTELA, 1990). As well as being 
a key prey species for cod and flounder, S. entomon is also a predator which feeds on other 
invertibrates including insects, as well as bivalves and crustaceans such as the amphipod 
pontoporeia affins, while juveniles are known to feed on “… oligochaetes, smaller P. affins, 
nematodes, microcrustaceans…” (Leonardsson, 1991) and therefore plays an important role 
in the food web.

The species is commonly encountered at depths of between 50 – 85m (Borecka, Janas and 
Kendzierska, 2016) but has also between recorded at depths of between 10 and 30m, 
particularly the juveniles which are often separated from adults in their distribution as well as 
coastal populations (Gogina et al, 2019)(Leonardsson, 1991). The maximum depth of S. 
entomon is generally determined by the depth at which O2  is less than 2mg/L (HAAHTELA, 
1990).  
S. entomon can survive at lower oxygen levels but only for limited periods of time. S. entomon 
tends to migrate, particularly the juveniles, into shallow water in the winter and then return 
to their habitat during the summer (HAAHTELA, 1990). It has also been suggested that the 
species is intolerant to high levels of illumination and the migration during the summer may 
be a result of either changes in light levels, seasonal changes in temperature or even 
cannibalism (HAAHTELA, 1990) (Leonardsson, 1991).

1.2 Site location and oceanography  
 
The Baltic is a semi–enclosed estuarine sea. It receives a high volume of fresh water input 
relative to saline water due to the volume of river discharge relative to the limited, intermittent 
exchange of water with the high salinity North Sea via the Kattegat. The subject area of this 
study is located in the central Baltic sea and is divided into a number of separate basins 
including the Bornholm Basin, Stolpe channel and Eastern Gotland basin, through which a 
high volume of the incoming salt water from the North Sea and Kattegat passes. The area of 
investigation, determined by the available data, is defined by longitudinal positions 13-20  ̊E 
and latitudinal positions 54-58.5 ̊N (figure 2). 
 
Figure 2. Site location including BITS station locations and extent of data collection 
area* 
 
*The BITS data are those from Danish and Swedish fishery research surveys. 
 
The formation of a halocline at a depth of around 50m in the Bornholm Basin is caused by a 
density gradient as a result of the outflowing fresh surface water overlying high salinity water 
flowing in at depth from the North Sea via the Kattegat which enters the basin via the 
Bornholm strait (Jakobsen, 1996) (Rak and Wieczorek, 2012). The highly dense bottom water 
leaves the basin and moves eastward via the Stolpe channel (Jakobsen, 1996).  
==PAGE:8==
 
 
 
8 
The deep water in the basins show very little seasonal temperature variation and the physical 
properties of the deep water are strongly influenced by major episodes of water flowing into 
the basins. These inflow events mixing with the layers above are somewhat restricted by the 
strong stratification between the horizons (Rak and Wieczorek, 2012).  The most recent inflow 
event occurred in 2014, however the effects of this event only lasted for a short period of time. 
Prior to that the most recent inflow was in 2003 (Szymczycha et al., 2018). Large areas of the 
Baltic are therefore permanently stratified, including the Gotland deep (Conley et al., 2009) 
while the area of the Baltic which is hypoxic has increased in the Bornholm and Gotland basins 
steadily in the past hundred years or so. During hypoxic events the deep basins form one large 
area with hypoxic conditions. (Carstensen et al., 2014) 
The permanent stratification in the basins in turn results in anoxic or hypoxic conditions at 
depths below the halocline and these conditions mean that the physiology of  S. entomon is 
affected by  physiological stress including changes in the production of haemocyanin which is 
an important protein for transporting oxygen in the haemolymph (Borecka, Janas and 
Kendzierska, 2016). Furthermore, osmoregulatory problems can also occur. S. entomon as a 
bottom dwelling species is therefore particularly sensitive to anoxic conditions 
(KRISTOFFERSSON, 1990). 
 
1.3 Expectations 
 
The anticipated results from this study would be to find spatial and temporal, including 
seasonal, patterns in distribution of S. entomon based on the analysis of the fishing survey 
trawling data. Seasonal differences may be present due to the yearly migration in the 
population and yearly changes in density due to fluctuations in abiotic factors. From the 
results of statistical analysis, expectations would be a high probability of occurrence and CPUE 
at optimum levels of salinity, oxygen and depth ranges based on the principles discussed in 
section 1.1. The response to temperature, would likely be an optimum at cold temperatures but 
may be more difficult to predict because although S. entomon is generally acclimatised to cold 
conditions it can be found at a wide range of temperatures. (Borecka, Janas and Kendzierska, 
2016)

2 Data and Methods 
This section will introduce the dataset as well as the data treatment process (Section 2.1) and 
the statistical models used in the analysis (section 2.2).  
2.1 Data Treatment 
 
2.1.1 
Survey data 
 
S. entomon data were collected during the Baltic International Trawl Survey (BITS) cruises on 
the Dana and Argos research vessels which sailed in spring (February, March) and autumn 
(November). The Danish data was collected between the period of 2007, while the Swedish 
data was collected between the period 2010 – 2019. 
The data is thus comprised of fisheries research haul data. The International standard trawl 
for Baltic Demersal Survey trawl type used was a TV3 x 930 x 80mm and 16mm in codend. 
(Nielsen, 2001)  
The data collected by BITS on Dana prior to 2010 has been left out of this analysis due to 
inconsistent sampling and recording of S. entomon prior to this time (pers. communication 
Marie Storr Paulsen, Head of Section for Monitoring and Data, DTU Aqua). Data collected on 
Argos and later on Dana by the Swedish portion of BITS before 2013 has been excluded for 
similar reasons (M. Casini, SLU Aqua, pers. communication.). 
For each haul the location, weight in kilograms (kg) and number of Saduria was recorded, as 
well as information describing the type of gear, locations and fishing time. A summary of the 
raw data variables and definitions from the Danish BITS can be found in the Appendix table 5 
and 6.  Each haul on the BITS is assigned gear quality and haul type variables by the cruise 
leader.  The gear quality variable is defined as valid or invalid, and the haul type variable has 
several classifications (e. g. normal bottom haul, acoustic calibration haul, etc.; Table 6) 
Only bottom hauls assigned to the “valid” category were used in this analysis, therefore hauls 
for acoustic calibration purposes and hauls where the oxygen content recorded was below 
0.5ml/L at the bottom have been excluded from the analysis. Gear quality information was 
available for all years, but haul type information was only available from 2014 onwards. 
However all hauls which had a missing haul type entry were categorized as valid hauls by the 
cruise leaders (pers. communication Henrik Degel, Section for Monitoring and Data, DTU 
Aqua), and were therefore included in analyses.  
The Danish data were collected across 3 different subdivisions of the Baltic – (ICES SD 24, 25 
and 26; Figure 3) respectively as characterized by the International Council for the Exploration

10
of the Sea (ICES). The majority of the Danish data (88%) was within SD 25 and Swedish data 
within SD 24 – 28.

Catch per unit effort (CPUE) was calculated by calculating the swept area using the doorspread 
(or Otterboard distance) in kilometres multiplied by the distance of the haul converted into 
kilometres from nautical miles. The final CPUE units were Kilograms per kilometre squared 
trawl area. Where doorspread entries were absent the doorspread was approximated by using 
a linear regression calculation of the natural logarithm of depth versus the doorspread from 
the available entries. The regression formula equated was then used to calculate an 
approximation for the missing doorspread entries. The regression calculation can be found in 
the appendix (figure 21). All hauls with a duration of less than thirty minutes were removed 
from the data set, to allow for consistent sampling between stations. This method was applied 
to 61 missing entries (approx. 7% of the total). 
S. entomon catch data was converted into CPUE by calculating the swept area from the 
otterboard distance and the distance covered during the trawl using the same method as the 
Danish data (figure 22). In the Swedish BITS the data was in the format of Kg/hour and had 
to be converted to a swept area basis to match the Danish BITS data.  This was done by first 
calculating the biomass of S. entomon caught by multiplying the weight caught per hour by 
the fishing time.  The biomass caught was then converted to CPUE in kg per km2 using haul 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
 
11
information (otterboard or doorspread distance and haul distance) in the Swedish BITS 
database. In the Swedish BITS database, there were several duplicated entry values of CPUE 
for different length classes of Saduria; these were filtered out leaving one unique CPUE per 
station which included all size-groups caught by the haul. Where doorpsread entries were 
missing the swept area was calculated by the same method as the Danish data.  
Once the data sets from both countries were converted to similar units,the data were combined 
into one combined data set for subsequent analyses. For all analysis the natural logarithm of 
CPUE was used for analysis (ln CPUE). 
 
2.1.2 Depth data 
 
Depth data were downloaded from the General Bathymetric chart of the oceans and comprised 
of  32-bit float values (GEBCO - The General Bathymetric Chart of the Oceans, no date). The 
data included topography for land above sea level. Therefore, in order to visualize contrast in 
depth on maps all values above zero were filtered out leaving only values at or below sea level. 
In order to visualize the distribution of S. entomon seasonally and by depth, individual maps 
were produced to show Saduria presence and absence overlaid on top of bathymetry for the 
period of 2010 – 2019 for the Danish data, and 2013 – 2019 for the Swedish data. Additional 
maps were created showing CPUE in order to visualize the spatial density of the species. 
(figures 5 and 6) 
 
2.1.3 Substrate data 
 
Data containing the substrate type for each haul station were obtained from the EMODnet 
geology portal (Data products | Geology, no date). For each station the latitude and longitude 
positions were matched to the nearest geology data point by interpolation to obtain the 
substrate type. Substrate data is included in the analysis of Saduria distribution as a factor or 
categorical variable. The substrate was defined by the five following categories: 1. Mud to 
muddy sand, 2. sand, 3. mixed sediment 4. coarse sediment and 5. rocks and boulders. The 
categories were defined by the relative proportions of mud, sand and gravel. There are 3 
categories of geology data based on the classification by EMODnet, folk 5, 7 and 16 with an 
increasing level of detail in categorization towards folk 16 (See Appendix figure 23 for more 
details) However, folk level 5 was selected for the analysis due to missing entries in folk 
categories 7 and 16 for the Saduria haul stations in the data base.

2.1.4 Environmental data 
 
Conductivity, temperature and density (CTD) and oxygen data were obtained from the same 
cruises as the haul data from BITS cruises between the period of 2010 - 2019. For the Danish 
data, the S. entomon survey trawling data was matched up with the corresponding 
hydrographic stations by station ID number. For each of the variables, the stations were 
matched to the trawling data by splitting both sets of data into individual seasons and 
matching hydrographic station numbers provided by the DTU Aqua fiskeline software with 
the corresponding CTD station numbers. All values above the deepest measurement were 
filtered out leaving values corresponding to the maximum pressure to allow for analysis of the 
benthic environment. The database contained several missing entries in the period between 
2012 and spring 2014. This period was therefore not included in analyses of hydrographic 
conditions on distribution and abundance of S. entomon. 
The Swedish BITS CTD and oxygen data were obtained from the ICES database. It was not 
possible to obtain the station numbers which were equivalent to the trawling stations, 
therefore the data were matched by date and by calculating the nearest station from the 
Euclidian distance. It was deemed not necessary to account for the curvature of the earth in 
this calculation due to generally relatively small distances between the paired locations. In 
both the Swedish and Danish data sets there were some cases which had large distances 
between the trawling and CTD stations. A threshold of a decimal degree distance of 0.08 was 
selected based on analysis of histograms; consequently, all CTD and oxygen values at distances 
greater than this threshold from the haul position were replaced with NA values and excluded 
from the analysis. Due to some anomalies between the depths recorded in the fishing survey 
vs the CTD data, any data points which differed in depth by a more than 10m were also 
excluded from analyses involving environmental variables.

2.2 Analysis  
 
In this section a description of the data analysis is described using Generalised Additive 
models (GAM) as well as time series regressions, to investigate seasonal and yearly changes in 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
14
distribution. The overall results of the statistical GAM models can be found in table 3 and 
figures 7 – 9. The time series results are included in figures 13 -17. 
 
2.2.1 Generalised Additive Models 
 
A two stage delta-gamma analysis was used to analyse the S. entomon data (Stefánsson, 
1996)(Orio et al., 2019) The first step used was a binomial with link (logit) model, in order to 
determine the presence and absence of S. entomon data. This approach was used due to the 
large number of zero values in the data set which do not follow a normal distribution 
(Stefánsson, 1996)(Orio et al., 2019). The second step estimated the mean abundance at 
locations where S. entomon was present. 
S. entomon biomass estimates were converted into a binomial count (0 being absent and 1 
present) and therefore all biomass entries with a value greater than 0 corresponded to a binary 
entry of 1. Stage 2 of the model involved log transforming the CPUE data for only the hauls 
where S. entomon was present (positive stations) (binomial entry of 1) and estimating a model 
using a gaussian distribution with an identity link to derive the CPUE response (Parra et al., 
2017). These models were selected due to the distribution of the response variable as detailed 
in (Stefánsson and Pálsson, 1997).  
Figure 20 in the appendix shows the distribution of log transformed CPUE for locations where 
S. entomon was encountered; this demonstrates a normal or gaussian distribution, while 
figure 19 shows the distribution of the CPUE for the complete data set of all logged CPUE 
values with the addition of 0.001 to all entries. This clearly shows the non-normal distribution 
as the “… equal variance assumption normally applied to linear models is not satisfied” 
(Stefánsson, 1996). This therefore justifies the use of the binomial model selection (Warton1 
and Hui1, 2011). 
For  stage 1, the modelled response probability was determined as the natural logarithm  of 
the probability of the species being present as a proportion of the total number of samples (p) 
(Stefánsson, 1996). The probability of S. entomon being present at a given location was 
modelled as a function of the potential explanatory variables depth, longitude, latitude, year, 
salinity, temperature, oxygen and substrate type, where 𝛼 is the intercept,  𝛽1. . 𝛽𝑖  are the 
parameters of the model and 𝜀 is the error term (Eq. 1). These variables were selected for the 
model based on ecological principles discussed in section 1.1 which previous studies indicated 
could affect the distribution of the species.

By adding smooth terms to the variables for longitude, latitude, depth, salinity, temperature 
and oxygen the model became a Generalised Additive Model (GAM). The model was created 
using the Generalised Additive Model (mgcv) (GAM) package in R using a binomial error 
distribution and logit link (Parra et al., 2017). Categorical or factor variables (substrate type, 
year and season) were added to the model without smooth terms. The GAM model is able to 
identify both linear and non–linear trends (Stefánsson and Pálsson, 1997)(Casini et al., 2016). 
The final model results are summarised in the table 4 in the results.  
For each model the null model was run initially. Under the null model the parameters have 
zero effect on the response and the deviance is explained purely by the intercept (Guisan and 
Zimmermann, 2000). Variables were added to the model one at a time. This approach is 
referred to as a forward stepwise model selection (Parra et al., 2017). Variables were retained 
in the model which resulted in a lower Aikaike’s Information Criterion (AIC). (Parra et al., 
2017). Any terms which were insignificant or had low evidence of increased explanatory power 
(Burnham, Anderson and Huyvaert, 2011) were removed from the model at each step. The 
deviance explained was calculated for each model and parameters were removed as necessary 
during the process. The level of significance was set at a p value of 5% for all, and all terms 
with a p value of less than 5% were kept in the model (Casini et al., 2016). Where interaction 
terms were included in the models, the maximum value of the knots was limited to a k value 
of 20 and for independent smooth terms k was limited to a value of 3 in order to limit the 
degrees of freedom of the models and allow for responses that conform with reasonable 
ecological principles (Orio et al., 2019).  Residuals were analysed for diagnosis of fit, and 
results are included in the appendix. 
Due to the sparsity of available CTD and oxygen data in some years, particularly prior to 2015, 
the final dataset was subdivided into three subsets of data which contained different sample 
sizes and allowed analyses having different time-space coverage. The largest dataset contained 
all entries of haul data, including the record where no environmental data were available (n = 
1307); a second dataset included only those hauls where temperature and salinity data were 
available (n = 983) and a third dataset included only those hauls which contained oxygen 
concentrations, temperature and salinity measurements (n = 668).   
In the majority of cases, when environmental variables were included in the models with the 
temporally non-varying variables for longitude and latitude, these terms did not improve

model performance (details in the results section 3.1.2 and general discussion in section 4). 
Therefore, separate models were tested with exclusively environmental variables (salinity, 
temperature and oxygen) along with depth. 
The models which included environmental variables were compared with the models without 
environmental variables using the same sample size in order to compare model performance 
using AIC criteria. For each sample size one final delta-gamma model was selected (therefore 
6 models in total). A summary of the final models and comparisons between models is 
provided in table 4. 
Two additional simple models were used to create a map of probability of presence using test 
data with depth, longitude, latitude and substrate information at a high resolution. The 
probability of occurrence calculated by the binomial model was used to create a map of 
predicted probability (figure 10). The predicted values from the first model were then

2.2.2 GAM model validation 
 
In order to test the potential predictive skill of the GAM models, each model of probability of 
occurrence and ln CPUE was split into training and test sets. The final eight models were run 
on training dataset which comprised of data from 2010 – 2017. The test data were from 2018 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
17
– 2019; the training model based on 2010-2017 data was then used to predict values to 
compare with the observations from 2018-2019. The predicted values from the test data set 
were then compared to the observed values using Spearman’s rank correlation (rs) for the 
gaussian models. The threshold for a pass test for the Spearman’s rank was set at rs > 0.1 (Parra 
et al., 2017). The binomial models were validated by calculating the area under the curve 
(AUC) of the receiver operator characteristics (ROC) curve calculates the proportion of correct 
predictions (equivalent to binomial count of 1) and incorrect predictions (equivalent to a 
binomial count of 0) (Elith et al., 2006). The threshold was set to 0.5 and therefore if the 
probability of the response variable Y = 1 is greater than 0.5, then Y is equivalent to a value of 
1 (S.entonom being present)  
 
 
2.2.3 Time series analysis 
 
Time series of the survey data between the period 2010 – 2019 were plotted to allow for 
assessment of whether the population density and CPUE is stable or changes over time and if 
there were seasonal or interannual patterns. Both the probability of occurrence and the mean 
natural logarithm of CPUE were calculated for each year for the period 2010 - 2019. Time 
series were split into the quarters spring and autumn to allow for assessment of possible 
differences among seasons in the data. Probability of occurrence was calculated by dividing 
the number of stations which had Saduria by the total number of stations sampled in each 
quarter. For each time series the standard error was calculated by the sample standard 
deviation divided by the square root of the sample size. 
Time series were plotted for 4 different regions. The coordinates for the separate areas are 
specified in figure 4 and table 2 below. The areas chosen were arbitrary and based on a visual 
interpretation of the three main centres of highest density of the species distribution (further 
information on this is provided in the results section 3.1.2 and discussion section 4).  
In some seasons the total number of stations with data collected were low, therefore these 
seasons with less than 5 stations were removed from the analysis. GAM models were used to 
derive the yearly trend by predicting the ln CPUE in each of the areas. A thin plate regression 
spline was added to the variable year. Quarter was included in the time series model as a 
categorical variable.  
To assess the difference in density between the highest density areas, the mean and standard 
error for each series area was calculated for the period 2010 – 2019 for both spring and autumn

and plotted in bar charts (figure 16). This was calculated for both the ln CPUE and probability 
of occurrence. A one-way Analysis of Variance in means (ANOVA) was then used to assess the 
mean ln CPUE and probability of occurrence between the three areas. The null hypothesis that 
all three areas show no difference in variability was compared to the alternative hypothesis 
that there is a difference in means (‘6 ANOVA Contents’, no date).  (See section 3.2.6 for 
further details). The null hypothesis was rejected following the ANOVA test and the treatment 
variable Series area was found to be significant and therefore a Post hoc Duncan’s multiple 
comparisons test was performed to find which of the area means differed from the others. 
(Shaaer, 1999) 
Observations of the mean yearly salinity, temperature and oxygen from the CTD and oxygen 
data were plotted to assess whether environmental conditions changed over time. Data was 
split into two intervals by depths of between 30 – 60m and 60 – 90m.  Over 90% of the data 
was within these depth ranges based on a review of depth histograms.

numbers were 3 (delta) and 7 (gamma) and sample size n = 983 model numbers were 5 (delta)

3.2.1 Binomial Models 
 
The binomial GAM models accounted for 31-55% of the deviance (see table 4). The deviance 
explained differed slightly between models but was relatively consistent for models containing 
the three different sample sizes. The variables which were temporally non-varying, namely, 
longitude, latitude and depth were always found to be significant in the binomial models. As 
can be seen from figure 7 the predicted fit of the log probability is compared to depth and the 
probability of presence decreases with depth, while the 3D plot of the interaction between 
longitude and latitude shows two peaks with a significantly high predicted probability of 
occurrence. The small peak coincides with the high density encountered in the spatial 
distribution in figure 6 to the north east of the Bornholm basin while the larger peak represents 
the high density encountered along the west of the East Gotland basin. 
Analysis using the smaller dataset containing environmental variables salinity, temperature 
and oxygen showed that these variables were not retained in the binomial models for 
probability of occurrence due to an insignificant contribution (p-value >0.05). Separate 
models were tested with exclusively environmental data (salinity, temperature and oxygen) 
along with depth but without the temporally nonvarying variables longitude and latitude. In 
these models, environmental variables were significant and explained between 31 – 35% of the 
deviance (models 4 and 6). However, these models which included only environmental 
variables generally had higher AIC values and therefore the temporally nonvarying variables 
(longitude, latitude and depth) were more successful at predicting probability of occurrence. 
The partial residual plot of depth (figure 7) shows a predicted reduction in probability of 
presence and with increasing depth and with a steep decline at depths of greater than 60m. 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
23
However, there is a low level of confidence at greater depths due to limited amount of data 
points at depths greater than 60m, which is illustrated by the wider confidence intervals. 
 
3.2.2 Positive only models of ln CPUE using Gaussian distribution 
 
The results for the gaussian models predicting ln CPUE from present only data accounted for 
between 50% and 78% of the deviance explained. The present only ln CPUE models were more 
successful at retaining environmental variables than the binomial models, in that in these 
models the temporally nonvarying variables longitude and latitude did not improve the 
models and were not retained in the models (models 7 and 8) and the environmental variables 
(plus depth) explained up to 63% of the deviance. This would suggest that the ln CPUE of S. 
entomon is determined by a combination of interactions between longitude, latitude, depth, 
salinity, temperature and oxygen. 
The exception to this was Model 2, which analysed the largest sample size. In this model 
longitude and latitude were retained and the model explained 78% of the deviance, even 
though environmental variables were not available, for the full 1307 observations. As can be 
seen from figures 8 and 9 the predicted fit of ln CPUE for the variables; depth, salinity, 
temperature and oxygen were compared to observed values. Salinity was highly significant in 
the models tested and a decrease in the response of ln CPUE was found as salinity increased. 
Temperature had a less definitive effect on ln CPUE and differed depending on the sample size 
used. For model 7 (figure 8) ln CPUE was inversely proportional to temperature and for Model 
8 (figure 9), ln CPUE reached a maximum at temperatures of 6 – 8 ̊C and declined as 
temperatures increased above 8 ̊C.

mud and < 90% sand. However, the number of sampling locations of mixed sediment and mud 
to muddy sand were much greater in number than the other sediment types (See model 
outputs in appendix for more details). 
Similarly, season was not significant in any of the models as a factor variable. The interaction 
between longitude, latitude and year was significant in the binomial models 1 and 5. This 
suggests there could be some variability in distribution from year to year. (Further analysis of

4 Discussion 
 
In summary, based on the results of spatial distribution analysis there are three main centres 
of density which are located to the north east of the Bornholm basin, the south west of the East 
Gotland basin and further north to the west of the East Gotland basin. The results of the GAM 
analysis provide further evidence of a highly significant spatial distribution by confirming that 
the variables which are temporally non-varying, namely longitude, latitude and depth are 
highly significant as predictors of probability of occurrence. Models of ln CPUE of positive only 
values reveal that salinity and temperature are highly significant and ln CPUE is negatively 
correlated with with these variables. Oxygen does not respond as anticipated in the models 
based on the ecology of S. entomon and the models predict a decrease in ln CPUE with 
increasing oxygen concentration. This is probably due to the multicollinearity of oxygen with 
other variables (depth, temperature and salinity). Analysis of time series reveal that some 
years (notably between 2014 -2016) have an increased probability of occurrence and ln CPUE. 
Finally, the areas 1 and 2 have statistically significant higher probability of occurrence and ln 
CPUE. 
 
4.1 Spatial distribution and assessment of sampling application 
 
Based on the results of spatial and temporal distribution, fishing survey trawling methods are 
a promising way of collecting S. entomon data with a high spatial coverage relative to benthic 
sampling data. The spatial distribution of S. entomon from the survey data revealed high 
abundance to the north east of the Bornholm basin, the north of the Stolpe channel and along 
the length of western edge of the East Gotland basin reaching as far north as the island of 
Gotland and is reflected in the model predictions from the interaction between longitude and 
latitude. These results match the geographical distribution of (Gogina et al., 2020) based on a 
number of benthic grab sampling events and (SPECIES INFORMATION SHEET Saduria 
entomon, 2013) who mapped and modelled the distribution of S. entomon during the period 
between 1981 and 2009. Results here concluded that the areas 2 and 3 had both a high CPUE 
and probability of occurrence. The CPUE density abundances in this study also revealed a low 
density in the Bornholm basin and low abundance towards the west as salinity levels 
increased.  These results are similar to the modelled distribution presented by (Gogina et al., 
2020) based on benthic grab sampling methods and support the finding that the BITS surveys 
potentially can provide quantitative estimates of distribution of this species.  
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
37
 
In terms of the actual densities recorded using this method, the maximum CPUE of around 
37kg/km2 (figure 6) converts to 0.037g/m2. This is therefore somewhat lower than studies 
which used benthic grab sampling (previous studies recorded on average < 5 g/m2) in the 
southern Baltic (HAAHTELA, 1990). The difference is perhaps expected due to the difference 
in sampling methods; for example the mesh size of the BITS nets is too large to retain small 
individuals of S. entomon, and some larger individuals may be able to detect the oncoming 
gear and avoid being captured by burial. In addition, the nets integrate possible smaller-scale 
patchiness and aggregations because they are towed over large distances (typically 1.5 nautical 
miles) whereas benthic grab sampling captures S. entomon over small areas. Despite these 
differences, low densities of <0.1 g/m2 were also recorded by benthic grab sampling in the 
Bornholm basin which reflects the spatial distribution in the analysis here in relative terms. 
(HAAHTELA, 1990). Therefore, the data collection method used here cannot resolve smaller 
scale spatial variations in biomass or probability of occurrence but are potentially useful for 
larger scale distributional and abundance investigations. These results show that BITS can 
provide additional ecosystem and foodweb relevant data which can complement benthic 
sampling data and with little or no additional economic cost.   
4.2 GAM analysis 
 
The partial effects of the relationship of CPUE to salinity, temperature and depth conform to 
what we know about the biology of the species and this provides support for the conclusion 
that trawling survey data can be used to study the temporal changes and the spatial variations 
of this species in the offshore areas. S. entomon distribution is strongly influenced by depth 
with an optimum depth range of approximately 50 – 70m. This is in keeping with previous 
studies by (HAAHTELA, 1990), however the majority of the stations had a maximum depth of 
greater than 30m so the models are only accurate within the range of the depths sampled. The 
optimum temperature from the models of between approximately 2 – 8 ̊C is consistent with 
previous findings by (Gogina et al., 2020) where the highest biomass was generally recorded 
at temperatures of less than 15̊C.  
The results of (Borecka, Janas and Kendzierska, 2016) revealed that S. entomon is particularly 
sensitive to low salinity conditions. Based on laboratory experiments, the production of a 
respiratory pigment haemocyanin increased with decreasing salinity as a result of increased 
metabolism, in particularly at extreme low salinities of 1 PSU. The minimum salinity in the 
data set here is 6.7 parts per thousand, therefore it is not possible to assess this effect at 
extreme low salinities.  However, it may partly explain why there is a high confidence in the 
predicted CPUE vs. salinity at a value of approximately 8 parts per thousand, well above the 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
 
38
critical low salinity levels shown by the high confidence intervals. It is likely that the species 
would also be encountered at lower salinities than the data extends to because as a brackfish 
– freshwater species it can tolerate low salinities of  < 1 parts per thousand and other forms of 
the species have been recorded in Swedish lakes, although the distribution of the Baltic form 
does not span into fresh water (HAAHTELA, 1990).   
Results here show that as salinity increases above 8 – 9 parts per thousand, CPUE begins to 
decrease. It has been reported that the species can survive at higher salinities of up to 20 parts 
per thousand reported by (HAAHTELA, 1990) but normally cannot move to deeper, more 
saline water because it is constrained by the low oxygen levels at these higher salinities due to 
the long term hypoxia which constrains the species to shallower depths in the Baltic (SPECIES 
INFORMATION SHEET Saduria entomon, 2013). This may be one reason for the apparent 
decline in predicted CPUE with increasing salinity encountered here, although as previously 
mentioned it is not commonly encountered in higher salinity regions west of the Baltic such 
as the Kattegat, therefore the ln CPUE response to salinity here matches the species 
distribution documented in the literature.  
Oxygen did not conform to the anticipated outcome based on the known physiology of the 
species. The results of the partial residuals suggest that CPUE decreases with increasing 
oxygen, which should not be the case. As previously mentioned, physiological problems occur 
with osmoregulation in hypoxic environments at these depths and expanding hypoxic areas is 
a threat to the species (KRISTOFFERSSON, 1990) (Borecka, Janas and Kendzierska, 2016). 
Despite this, some studies show that S. entomon are capable of surviving in low levels of 
oxygen of 3mg/L (KRISTOFFERSSON, 1990) and results of (Gogina et al., 2020) also found 
that large biomass can be encountered at low oxygen levels of around 4mg/L in cold, deep 
water. However, this still does not explain the high CPUE predicted here for oxygen values of 
between 0 and 2 mg/L. The model prediction for oxygen has a wide range of confidence 
intervals, particularly at hypoxic levels of oxygen between 0 – 2 mg/L. The model may 
therefore be inaccurate at predicting the response to oxygen at low levels due to the sparsity 
of data points and the high CPUE predictions at approximately 0mg/L may be outliers. One 
further consideration is that the CPUE data here does not contain any values where the oxygen 
level is less than 0 mg/L, therefore the model is unable to predict the expected CPUE decline 
at 0mg/L. If the data set had contained these low values then perhaps a decline in CPUE at 
low oxygen levels of below 0mg/L would be predicted by the model. 
One further explanation for the discrepancy may be a result of the co-linearity between the 
variables oxygen and depth. Oxygen is negatively correlated with both salinity and depth and 
therefore one problem with the model might be the predictive capability, particularly at depths 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
 
39
of over 50m, below the halocline where there are low levels of oxygen due to the inverse 
relationship between these two variables.  
In all models, the predicted temperature response showed a decrease in probability of 
occurrence and CPUE at temperatures greater than approximately 6̊C, which is in keeping 
with the known ecology and studies by (Gogina et al., 2020). As previously mentioned, S. 
entomon has been able to withstand high temperatures of up to 20̊C under laboratory 
conditions (HAAHTELA, 1990). Other studies have shown that an increase in temperature has 
the ability to affect osmotic pressure at salinity levels which overlap with the range of salinities 
encountered here, which in turn may affect osmoregulatory ability. This may explain the 
decline in CPUE at temperatures greater than 6 ̊C, however contradictory evidence has also 
been found in studies of other crustaceans. (GILLES, 1983).  
In terms of substrate, the result of model 1a is in keeping with the results of (Leonardsoon, 
1986) which found that the species inhabits areas which are not hard and which it can burrow 
through. 
 
4.3 Additional factors – prey and predation 
 
Analysis of time series and season revealed some yearly variations in probability of occurrence 
and CPUE. Predation pressure was not included as a variable in this report, however these 
yearly variations may be attributed to fluctuations in the distribution and biomass of both cod 
and flounder. The yearly fluctuations in S. entomon density such as the decrease in density 
after 2016 may perhaps be attributed to an increase in the density of flounder. Flounder 
population density has increased in the past 20 years in the southern Baltic in studies of areas 
which overlap with the time period of data collected here (Haase et al., 2020). At the same 
time, the concentration of S. entomon in cod stomachs has declined during this period with 
the expansion of hypoxic areas (Neuenfeldt et al., 2019). Studies have shown that in recent 
years flounder may have outcompeted cod for the resource of S. entomon. This is confirmed 
by the analysis of stomach content by (Haase et al., 2020).  
Both cod and flounder are sensitive to oxygen depleted areas where the benthic community is 
affected and the prey and are more sensitive to oxygen depletion than the benthic prey they 
consume (Diaz and Rosenberg, 2008). Food resources for demersal fish are therefore limited 
by the extent of hypoxic areas, however the expansion of these areas means that flounder as a 
specialist benthos feeder is able to outcompete cod in areas where benthic resources are 
available while cod have shifted towards pelagic prey sources (Haase et al., 2020). The spatial 
distribution encountered here may be partly explained by the distribution of flounder. 
==PAGE:40==
 
 
 
40
Similarly, data on the availability of S. entomon’s prey such as P. affins may be a factor in 
explaining yearly variations although we do not have available information on whether there 
have been changes in the available prey sources for S. entomon to understand the food web 
dynamics with certainty. 
The map below shows the distribution of flounder between the period 2010 to 2019 in quarters 
1 and 4 and of flounder ages 1 – 9 collected from BITS for fishing hauls over 30 minutes long. 
There appears to be some overlap in spatial distribution of flounder with S. entomon where 
the highest density areas of the benthic isopod are. High densities of flounder are concentrated 
around the Bornholm basin. This may be one explanation for the low ln CPUE and low 
presence of S. entomon in this area. This could not be statistically tested in this paper but could 
be investigated further in future studies. 
Figure 18 Distribution of flounder caught ages 1 – 9 between 2010 - 2019 
 
The low density of S. entomon in the Bornholm basin may also be attributed to the 
concentration of Eastern Baltic cod which has high reproduction in this area (Köster et al., 
2008). Population of cod have shown a general decline since the 1970s (Eero et al., 2015). 
Despite the general decline there was an increase in abundance in the late 2000s although 
stocks did not increase to the same geographical extent as it had in the past, encompassing 
areas such as the East Gotland basin (Eero et al., 2015). The stock has therefore not expanded 
further to the north east, which may be due to anoxic conditions in the basins (Köster et al., 
2008). This could be one explanation for why there is a higher abundance of S. entomon 
further to the north east of the Bornholm basin and along the western side of the East Gotland 
basin further to the north east.

4.4 Temporal analysis 
 
Trends in seasonality were not apparent in the results of either the time series or the spatial 
analysis GAMs. It is well documented in the literature that coastal S. entomon show seasonal 
migration patterns, particularly the younger adults and juveniles which migrate into deep 
water in the summer and to shallow in the winter (HAAHTELA, 1990).  Juveniles are often 
segregated across space from the adults. This separation over space may be due to cannibalism 
which results in juveniles migrating to shallow water to escape the larger animals 
(Leonardsson, 1991). It has been suggested that juveniles remain in shallow areas up until they 
reach a size of 10mm and then migrate back to deeper areas before they finally return to 
shallow areas to spawn (Leonardsson, 1991). In deep areas, such as the Bornholm basin and 
East Gotland basins, within the offshore study site recruitment is relatively constant 
throughout the year whereas in shallow areas it is more seasonal (Leonardsson, 1991). This 
could be one explanation for the lack of seasonality found in the time series because 
seasonality is not statistically significant here due to the limitation of the data set which covers 
only depths greater than approximately 22m. Furthermore, since the majority of migrants are 
juveniles or younger adults the seasonal patterns may not be reflected in the data. One 
explanation for this may be due to the mesh size used in the hauls which may not be small 
enough to capture the smaller juveniles. The mean weight per individual captured in the 
Danish and Swedish BITS is 2g. Length measurements were not available in this study, 
however growth experiments in the Bothnian sea have shown that juveniles grew from 3 – 
4mm up to approximate length of 20 – 25mm after the first year of growth, while maturity 
was reached for females and males at 29.1mm and 36mm respectively (Leonardsson, 1986). 
As an example of length to weight comparisons, experiments by (Sparrevik, 1999) recorded 
pre-moulting S. entomon individual mean weights of 0.76g - 1.58g corresponding to mean 
individual lengths of 30mm – 40mm. This would suggest based on the minimum and 
maximum individual weights encountered in this study of 1 – 5g that fisheries trawling method 
may not be able to capture the smallest size classes. However, a more detailed assessment of 
length class in the same study area of the Baltic studied here would be required to confirm this 
assertion. 
It has been reported that the abundance of S. entomon has declined in the past due to low 
oxygen (SPECIES INFORMATION SHEET Saduria entomon, 2013). Prey availability was not 
considered in the analysis here, however the peak density recorded in the time series in 2015-
2016 may be a result of increased availability of prey such as amphipods and bivalves. Another 
explanation for the decline in CPUE or probability of occurrence between 2015 and the present 
could be a result of expanding hypoxic areas and therefore a lower availability of prey sources. 
The decrease in ln CPUE is matched by a decrease in oxygen concentration observed in the 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
 
42
time series results between 2015 – 2019  and is part of an ongoing trend and is likely to be a 
result of increased stratification as a result of reduced major inflow events, leading to 
expansion of hypoxic areas, as described by (Reusch et al., 2018).  
One of the limitations of the time series data is that the time range of data is quite short only 
covering 10 years, therefore long-term variations caused by environmental conditions cannot 
be analysed with this data set. The results of the longitude, latitude and depth models, 
although reliable for interpreting the distribution of the species over the short term during 
stable climatic-hydrographic conditions, cannot be used to infer how the distribution of the 
species may change in the future due to hydrographic and climatic variability. It seems likely 
that the distribution over longer, dynamic timescales is likely to be determined by the 
interaction between temperature, salinity and oxygen as well as other unquantified factors 
such as prey availability and threat of predation. Therefore, the models which contain 
environmental variables are potentially more useful in the future as climate changes even if 
the R2 coefficients and predictive quality is lower. 
 
4.5 Prediction maps 
 
The map of predicted probability of occurrence was somewhat successful in predicting the 
probability of occurrence. The area of highest density from the actual observations, along the 
western edge of the East Gotland basin was predicted with a probability of occurrence of 
between 0.8 -1 (80 – 100%). There are some errors in the predicted probability of occurrence, 
see map (fig 10). This is because generally none of the bottom trawls occurred at depths of 
shallower than 25m. Therefore, coastal areas cannot be considered accurate.  
The trend of a reduction in oxygen concentration over the period 2015 – 2019 may explain the 
lower CPUE and probability of occurrence during this period.  As the benthic habitat becomes 
uninhabitable for benthic fauna and there have been reported declines in the species 
(SPECIES INFORMATION SHEET Saduria entomon, 2013). Studies by (Johansson, 1999) 
have shown that predation by S.entomon on amphipods was greatly reduced at low oxygen 
conditions of below 33% saturation. Despite this, the time series covers a short-term period 
and the changes may simply be a result of inter-annual variability in hydrographic conditions. 
Longer term trends in the environmental conditions are needed for a more detailed 
assessment of the benthic conditions and monitoring of population numbers in the future.  
 
4.6 Relevance of study and future recommendations 
 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
 
43
Future recommendations for the advancement of studies in the ecology of S. entomon would 
involve collection of S. entomon length measurements data on surveys in addition to the 
numbers and weight measurements. This would allow for an assessment of whether the mesh 
size used in bottom trawling surveys creates a bias towards larger individuals. Assessment of 
links to cod ecology could be investigated by analysis of stomach contents. Furthermore, 
expanding the spatial coverage of the data collection over a larger geographical area would 
allow for a better understanding of the species distribution and interactions in the food webs. 
As pressure from anthropogenic threats such as climate change result in loss of habitat and 
prey availability for commercial fish species such as cod and the expansion of hypoxic areas, 
projected future distribution of S. entomon may expand towards the south and west due to a 
reduction in salinity as a result of climate change. Such a  change in distribution may be 
beneficial to cod in the future (Gogina et al., 2020) This prey is important, particularly for the 
juvenile cod because there is no alternative prey of similar size in the benthic 
environment.When resources are scarce cod must find alternative food sources in the pelagic 
environment. (UZARS, 1994)(Neuenfeldt et al., 2019). The possibility of a match between 
predator and prey in the future as a result of changing environmental conditions may occur 
(Gogina et al., 2020) The information provided from survey trawling data could therefore be 
used to understand future projections of cod stocks and the extent of their habitat. 
 
 
5 Conclusion 
 
The data produced by this study confirms that new scientific knowledge can be obtained from 
fisheries surveys, thereby increasing their ecological importance and value. A high spatial 
coverage can be achieved through this method which compliments benthic sampling.  
High densities of S. entomon were present, particularly along the western side of the East 
Gotland basin and were confirmed by significant spatial patterns.  
Results from GAM analysis reveal that the methods used here can explain the spatial and 
temporal distribution and factors affecting the species abundance. The temporally non-
varying variables, longitude and latitude and depth have a significant influence on the 
distribution of S. entomon, and that trawling survey data can potentially be used to predict the 
probability of occurrence accurately under contemporary environmental conditions. Models 
also reveal significant decreases in CPUE with increasing salinity and decreases at 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
 
44
temperatures of greater than approximately 8 ̊C, while oxygen results did not respond as 
anticipated and CPUE showed a decrease with increasing oxygen levels.  
Time series analysis revealed fluctuations from year to year in CPUE and probability of 
occurrence, however very little seasonal variation was apparent. 
The models used explained up to 54% of the deviance explained based on presence and 
absence models, while models predicting CPUE using positive only values explained up to 78% 
of the deviance explained. The analysis here is only applicable to depths greater than 
approximately 25m due to a paucity of data at shallow depths. The predictive quality of the 
models is accurate but caution should be used when using the models to make predictions of 
species density in shallow water areas.
