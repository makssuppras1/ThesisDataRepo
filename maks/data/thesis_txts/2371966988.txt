Abstract 
The emergence of resistance to artemisinin-based antimalarial drugs in Plasmodium falciparum in 
Southeast Asia poses a threat against malaria control and elimination. It is therefore crucial to 
understand the interconnectedness of parasite populations and be able to monitor resistance 
spread. Recently, a barcoding system based on geographically restricted single-nucleotide 
polymorphisms (SNPs) in mitochondria and apicoplast genomes of P. falciparum was developed. 
Combined with SNP detection in the artemisinin resistance marker, K13, use of this system could 
offer important insights into parasite population structure and diversity and possibly detail the route 
of drug resistance spread. 
This study aims to explore the diversity of SNPs in the mitochondrial genome and to identify SNPs 
in the K13 gene of P. falciparum samples obtained from malaria patients in India (Ranchi District), 
Tanzania (Korogwe District) and Senegal (Podor, Richard Toll, Kaolack and Ndoffane districts). It 
further aims to test the predictive power of the barcoding system based on the samples used in this 
study. Extracted DNA was amplified using PCR and subsequently Sanger sequenced. For the K13 
gene, a total of 199 sequences were generated (92 from India, 48 from Tanzania and 59 from 
Senegal). For the mitochondrial genome, a total of 237 sequences were generated (93 from India, 
48 from Tanzania and 96 from Senegal). SNPs were identified by comparing to reference genomes.  
None of the SNPs detected in the K13 gene in this study are likely to be associated with artemisinin 
resistance, suggesting that P. falciparum populations in the studied areas are still effectively 
susceptible to artemisinin. Further, this study has detected geographically restricted SNPs in the 
mitochondrial genome.  These will be incorporated into the barcoding system, thereby expanding 
its usability. Other detected SNPs are not geographically restricted. Furthermore, a number of SNPs 
are reported here for the first time. Finally, the present study has shown a great genetic variation in 
the mitochondrial genome of P. falciparum between two high-transmission seasons in Ranchi, India 
as well as an apparent large within-country diversity of P. falciparum in Senegal.

Resumé 
Fremkomsten af resistens over for artemisininbaserede antimalaria lægemidler i Plasmodium 
falciparum i Sydøstasien udgør en trussel mod kontrol og udryddelse af malaria. Det er derfor 
afgørende at forstå sammenhængen mellem parasitpopulationer og at være i stand til at overvåge 
resistensspredningen. For nylig er der udviklet et stregkodesystem baseret på geografisk 
afgrænsede enkeltnukleotidpolymorfer (SNP'er) i mitokondrie- og apicoplastgenomer af P. 
falciparum. Kombineret med SNP-detektion i artemisininresistensmarkøren, K13, kan anvendelse af 
dette system sandsynligvis give vigtig indsigt i parasitpopulationens struktur og diversitet og 
muligvis give indsigt i resistensspredningens geografiske rute. 
Dette studie har til formål at undersøge diversiteten af SNP'er i mitokondriegenomet og identificere 
SNP'er i K13-genet fra P. falciparum prøver fra malariapatienter i Indien (Ranchi distrikt), Tanzania 
(Korogwe distrikt) og Senegal (Podor, Richard Toll, Kaolack og Ndoffane distrikter). Studiet sigter 
yderligere efter at teste stregkodesystemets evne til at forudsige den geografiske oprindelse af de 
prøver, der anvendes i dette studie. Ekstraheret DNA blev amplificeret ved anvendelse af PCR og 
efterfølgende Sanger-sekventeret. For K13-genet blev i alt 199 sekvenser genereret (92 fra Indien, 
48 fra Tanzania og 59 fra Senegal). For mitokondriegenomet blev i alt 237 sekvenser genereret (93 
fra Indien, 48 fra Tanzania og 96 fra Senegal). SNP'er blev identificeret ved at sammenligne med 
referencegenomer. 
Det er usandsynligt, at de SNP'er, der er påvist i K13-genet i dette studie, er associeret med 
artemisininresistens, hvilket tyder på, at artemisinin fortsat er effektivt mod P. falciparum 
populationer i de undersøgte områder. Endvidere har denne undersøgelse detekteret geografisk 
begrænsede SNP'er i mitokondriegenomet. Disse vil blive inkorporeret i stregkodesystemet og 
derved udvide dets anvendelighed. Andre registrerede SNP'er er ikke geografisk afgrænsede. 
Desuden rapporteres her for første gang en række nye SNP'er. Endelig har det foreliggende studie 
vist en stor genetisk variation i mitokondriegenomet af P. falciparum mellem to 
højtransmissionssæsoner i Ranchi, Indien samt en umiddelbar stor diversitet af P. falciparum inden 
for Senegals grænser.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Malaria – the disease 
1.1.1 Cause of malaria disease  
Malaria is caused by the protozoan parasite Plasmodium. Human disease is caused by five different 
species of Plasmodium: Plasmodium falciparum, P. malariae, P. ovale, P. vivax and P. knowlesi [1]. 
The malaria parasite is typically transmitted by female mosquitos of the genus Anopheles. However, 
malaria can also be transmitted from an infected mother to her child during pregnancy or at birth 
or by blood-borne transmission (e.g. blood transfusion, transplantation and needle-sharing) [2]. 
1.1.2 Life cycle of the malaria parasite  
The malaria parasite has a complex multistage life cycle occurring within two living beings; the 
vector mosquito and the vertebrate hosts (Figure 1). When an infected mosquito bites, sporozoites 
present in the salivary glands of the mosquito enter the bloodstream and are carried to the liver, 
where they invade hepatocytes [3]. They undergo a process of multiple nuclear division, followed 
by cytoplasmic division. When the divisions are complete, the liver cell ruptures and several 
thousand merozoites are released into the bloodstream.  
The released merozoites penetrate red blood cells in which they adopt a characteristic “signet-ring” 
morphology, and are now called trophozoites. Inside the red blood cells, the trophozoites start to 
undergo nuclear division followed by cytoplasmic division. The red blood cell then ruptures and 
releases new merozoites, which infect new red blood cells. Over the next circa 48 hours, the parasite 
continues this cycle of mitotic replication in red blood cells. With each replication, instead of 
undergoing nuclear division, some merozoites within the red blood cells develop into male or female 
gametocytes.  
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
When a mosquito vector ingests the blood, the male gametocytes differentiate to produce several 
individual gametes. The gametocytes become detached and penetrate the female gametocyte, 
which elongates into a zygotic form. This form penetrates the midgut wall of the mosquito and 
settles as an oocyst. Within the oocyst, numerous sporozoites are formed. When the oocyst is 
mature and ruptures, these sporozoites are released into the body cavity of the mosquito. From the 
body cavity, the sporozoites travel to the salivary glands of the mosquito, ready to enter the next 
human host, the mosquito bites.

1.1.3 Clinical features of malaria disease  
Malaria is an acute febrile illness [2]. The symptoms of the disease typically develop within 10 days 
to four weeks following the infection, coinciding with the time at which the merozoites first burst 
from the red blood cells. In some people, however, symptoms take several months to develop. The 
most severe form is caused by P. falciparum with clinical features including:  high fever, shaking 
chills, profuse sweating, headache, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, anemia, muscle pain, convulsions 
and bloody stools. Moreover, P. falciparum malaria can cause a number of life-threatening 
complications, such as: cerebral malaria, respiratory failure, acute renal failure and/or severe 
anemia [2]. The progression to these complications can be rapid and potentially fatal. It is therefore 
important that malaria patients are assessed and treated rapidly. 
1.1.4 Global burden of malaria  
Malaria remains a major threat to human health with an estimated 212 million cases occurring 
worldwide in 2015, most of which occurred in Africa (90 %) [5]. The estimated number of deaths 
from malaria was 429.000 the same year, with 92 % occurring in Africa. 70 % of these were 
estimated to have occurred in children under the age of five years. Almost all deaths (99 % of the 
global total) resulted from infection with P. falciparum (Figure 2). P. vivax was estimated to be 
responsible for 3100 deaths in 2015, with most occurring outside Africa.

1.1.4 Reduction of global burden  of malaria 
In the year 2000, the world was in the midst of an escalating malaria public health disaster. That 
year marked a turning point in multilateral commitment to malaria control and since then, sub-
Saharan Africa has experienced unparalleled levels of malaria intervention coverage [6]. In the years 
since, international financing for malaria control has increased twentyfold [5]. This has enabled 
widespread upscaling of the main malaria control interventions of today: insecticide-treated bed 
nets (ITNs), indoor residual spraying (IRS) and prompt treatment with artemisinin-based 
combination therapy (ACT). These increased efforts have substantially reduced the global burden of 
malaria; the number of malaria cases has fallen 22 % since 2000 and 14 % since 2010 [5]. 
Correspondingly, the number of deaths owing to malaria had decreased 50 % since 2000 and 22 % 
since 2010 [5].  
1.2 Resistance against antimalarial drugs  
1.2.1 Emergence of resistance  
Today, one of the biggest threats against the recent reduction of the global burden of malaria is the 
emergence and spread of parasite resistance to antimalarial drugs. P. falciparum has through history 
showed an impressive capacity to develop resistance to nearly all of the currently available 
antimalarial drugs. This has significantly hindered malaria control efforts at great cost to human 
health and life [7]. 
The antimalarial drug chloroquine developed in 1934 quickly proved to be one of the most 
successful drugs against infectious diseases. The tremendous success of chloroquine led to heavy 
use of the drug through the decades to follow. Eventually, this led to development of chloroquine 
resistant malaria parasites. Reports suggest that resistance emerged first in 1957 along the 
Cambodia-Thailand border area and then in Colombia [8]. Resistant strains spread steadily from 
Thailand through Southeast Asia and India in the 1960s and 1970s. When resistance was first 
==PAGE:15==
 
5 
 
detected in Africa in the late 1970s, it spread across the continent within a decade. This resulted in 
a resurgence of malaria morbidity and mortality [9].  
The high levels of resistance to chloroquine forced many countries to switch their first-line therapy 
to the sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine (SP) drug combination [10]. However, resistance to SP developed 
rapidly and was quickly widespread [11]. Importantly, it was later shown that the parasites causing 
illness and death in Africa had their genetic origins close to the Thailand-Cambodia border [12], [13]. 
Due to increased resistance to chloroquine and SP, mefloquine was introduced as first-line 
treatment in Thailand in 1984. Despite of careful regulation of its use, resistance towards this drug 
developed within six years and is also evident in neighboring countries, such as Vietnam, Burma and 
Cambodia [14], [15]. 
1.2.2 Southeast Asia: t he epicenter of antimalarial drug 
resistance 
Historically, Southeast Asia has been the center of the emergence of resistance to antimalarial 
drugs. The reason why resistance to almost all antimalarial drugs is detected first in Southeast Asia 
has never been fully explained. A conventional explanation, however, is that heavy use of 
antimalarial drugs in Southeast Asia may have presented parasite populations in this part of the 
world with more opportunities to acquire resistance [16].  
A reason for the heavy usage of antimalarial drugs in Southeast Asia might be that transmission of 
malaria is much lower in Southeast Asia. Therefore, fewer people have naturally acquired immunity 
to falciparum malaria there compared to Africa [17]. Consequently, when people from Southeast 
Asian are infected, they more often show symptoms compared to people from Africa, and more 
often resort to treatment.  
Southeast Asia is also where the multidrug-resistant P. falciparum clones are found. Some clones 
from Indochina are in fact resistant to every common antimalarial drug [16]. There are several 
potential explanations for the emergence of multidrug-resistant parasites. One is that meiotic 
recombination probably has permitted concentration of independently acquired resistant traits in 
single clones [18]. An alternative explanation is that some parasites that have developed drug 
==PAGE:16==
6 
 
resistance in the past are predisposed to acquire resistance to new unrelated antimalarial agents 
[16].  
1.2.3 Mainstay of treatment today: a rtemisinin -based 
combination therapies  
Artemisinin is a compound produced by the plant Artemesia annua. Artemisinin and its derivatives 
are a powerful class of antimalarials: they achieve rapid clearance of the asexual parasites that cause 
the symptomatic blood-stage infection, but also reduce the numbers of gametocytes - the sexual-
stage parasites responsible for transmission [19, 20]. Artemisinin derivatives include dihydro-
artemisinin, artemether, sodium artesunate and arteether (Figure 3). 
 
Figure 3: Chemical structure of artemisinin and its derivatives, dihydroartemisinin, artemether, sodium artesunate and 
arteether [21].  
Artemisinins are usually combined with a second, structurally unrelated and more slowly eliminated 
partner drug. Partner drugs include SP, lumefantrine, mefloquine, amodiaquine, piperaquine and 
chlorproguanil/dapsone. The most common used of such artemisinin-based combination therapies 
(ACTs) 
are 
arthemether-lumefantrine, 
artesunate-amodiaquine 
and 
dihydroartemisinin-
piperaquine [22]. ACTs are expected to allow fewer opportunities for development of resistance. 
Provided the partner drug is effective, the ACTs ensure high cure rates and are generally well 
tolerated.  
==PAGE:17==
 
7 
 
Since 2001, the WHO has recommended the use of ACTs in countries where P. falciparum malaria 
has developed resistance to the conventional antimalarials [23]. Today, artemisinins are the 
cornerstone of first-line combination therapies against uncomplicated malaria since they are the 
only class of antimalarials to which no widespread resistance has been detected.  In the recent years, 
access to ACTs has expanded substantially. By the end of 2014, ACTs had been adopted as first-line 
treatment policy in 81 of 91 malaria endemic countries [5].  
Regretfully, P. falciparum resistance to artemisinin has now been detected in five countries in the 
Greater Mekong subregion [24]–[30]. Resistance to artemisinin is defined as delayed parasite 
clearance. It is thus a partial resistance and patients with delayed parasite clearance are usually still 
able to clear their infections. However, the efficacy of ACTs requires both the artemisinin and the 
partner drug component. When artemisinin loses potency, the partner drug is exposed to far greater 
selective pressure for resistance. When the parasites also are resistant to the ACT partner drug, the 
patients are not able to clear their infections.  
Unfortunately, high treatment failure rates with the ACT combination artesunate + mefloquine were 
reported on both sides of the Thai-Cambodian border in the early 2000s [31] and in Cambodia, high 
treatment failure rates have been detected for four different ACTs (Figure 4).

1.2.4 The risk of spread or de novo emergence of  
artemisinin resistance  
The risk of ACT-resistance spreading from Southeast Asia to Africa, as happened previously with the 
chloroquine- and SP-resistance, is extremely concerning. Artemisinins are already an essential 
component of treatments for multidrug-resistant malaria [23]. If we lose artemisinins to resistance, 
we may therefore be faced with untreatable malaria. Since no alternative to the artemisinins is 
expected to enter the market for at least several years, it is crucial that their efficacy is preserved. 
Aware of the peril of spreading ACT resistance in southeast Asia, the WHO developed a “Global Plan 
for Artemisinin Resistance Containment” (GPARC) [32]. One of the main goals of GPARC is to 
increase monitoring and surveillance efforts to evaluate the threat of artemisinin resistance.  
Apart from the risk of artemisinin resistance spreading from Southeast Asia, there is also the risk of 
de novo emergence of resistance. In fact, though resistance to chloroquine was detected first in 
Southeast Asia in 1957, it independently emerged in South America in 1959 (Figure 5) [33]. Similarly, 
resistance to SP also emerged independently in South America. Moreover, it was recently shown 
that highly artemisinin-resistant parasites could also emerge independently in Southeast Asia [34]. 
It is therefore important, not only to monitor artemisinin resistance spread from Southeast Asia, but 
also to surveil malaria strains around the world for potential de novo emergence of artemisinin 
resistance.

1.3 Studying single-nucleotide polymorphisms 
1.3.1 Single -nucleotide polymorphisms  
In order to preserve the efficacy of the artemisinins, it is essential to understand parasite population 
interconnectedness as well as to be able to monitor spread and emergence of artemisinin 
resistance. For both of these purposes, studying single-nucleotide polymorphisms can be useful.  
Single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) are the essential element of DNA sequence variation, also 
termed polymorphism. An SNP is a variation at a single nucleotide position, where each variation is 
present to some appreciable degree within a population (e.g. > 1 %) [35]. If an SNP occurs within a 
gene, the gene is described as having more than one allele. This may lead to a change in the amino 
acid sequence. SNPs can, however, also occur in noncoding regions of DNA.  
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
With regard to drug resistance, the detection of SNPs in molecular markers is of great value and can 
provide important information for detecting and tracking antimalarial drug resistance. Moreover, 
specific SNPs can sometimes be used as genetic markers to identify the geographic origin of 
infections.  
1.3.2 Molecular m arkers of antimalarial drug resistance  
Molecular markers for drug resistant malaria are based on genetic changes that confer parasite 
resistance to antimalarial drugs. For instance, polymorphisms in the gene encoding the P. falciparum 
chloroquine resistance transporter (Pfcrt) confers resistance to chloroquine  and polymorphisms in 
the gene encoding P-glycoprotein homologue 1 protein (Pfmdr1) modulates this resistance [36]. 
Pfmdr1 has also been found to be implicated in resistance to mefloquine, artesunate, lumefantrine 
and quinine [15], [36]–[38]. Furthermore, polymorphisms in the gene encoding dihydrofolate 
reductase (DHFR) confers resistance to antifolate drugs (such as pyrimethamine) and in 
dihydropteroate synthase (DHPS) confers resistance to sulphadoxine [39], [40].  
These molecular markers have been validated as tools for resistance surveillance and have been 
used to guide national malaria treatment policies [42], to monitor changes in parasite drug 
susceptibility [43] and in the control of a malaria epidemic [44]. Unfortunately, resistance to 
chloroquine and SP had already severely compromised the efficacy of these drugs by the time the 
molecular markers for resistance to these drugs were discovered, and they were therefore of little 
operational value. However, a molecular marker artemisinin resistance has recently been found [45] 
and the use of it as a surveillance tool thus has the potential to help contain artemisinin resistance 
and prolong its usability before it is too late.

1.4 Molecular marker for artemisinin resistance  
1.4.1 Kelch13 propeller polymorphism as molecular marker 
for artemisinin resistance  
A molecular marker of artemisinin-resistant P. falciparum is the propeller domain of the Kelch13 
(K13) gene. In a genome-wide association study, Ariey et al. (2014) showed that mutations in this 
domain were associated with artemisinin resistance in vitro and in vivo [45]. They found strong 
correlation between the presence of a mutant allele, in vitro parasite survival rates (Figure 6) and in 
vivo parasite clearance rates, making K13 mutations important determinants of artemisinin 
resistance. Further, they found that mutant K13-propeller alleles cluster in provinces in Cambodia 
where resistance is prevalent.  
 
Figure 6: Survival rates of Cambodian parasite isolates in the RSA0-3 h (ring-stage survival assay), stratified by K13-
propeller allele. Mutant parasites with the mutations C580Y, R539T, Y493H and I543T have significantly higher survival 
rates than wild-type parasites [45]. 
Since this study, multiple groups have associated the same as well as additional K13-propeller 
mutations with low parasite clearance in countries in Southeast Asia [24], [34], [46]–[49].  Only SNPs 
found within the propeller-domain of the K13 gene (Figure 7) have been associated with resistance. 
Several studies have found K13 propeller polymorphisms in African countries. However, none of the 
polymorphisms associated with resistance in Southeast Asia have been observed.

1.4.2 Molecular mechanism of artemisinin resistance  
The molecular mechanism of artemisinin resistance is not fully understood. However, toxicity of 
artemisinin derivatives depends principally on their pro-oxidant activity [51]. The reported role of 
some kelch-containing proteins to regulate cytoprotective responses to external stress, such as 
oxidative stress, is therefore intriguing [52]. The K13 propeller domain shows homology with the 
human Kelch-like ECH-associated protein 1 (Keap1), which is involved in cell adaption to oxidative 
stress [53].  
One hypothetical model (Figure 8) of artemisinin sensitivity and resistance, based on the function 
of the human Keap1 protein is as follows: the wild-type K13 binds a putative transcription factor 
and delivers it to ubiquitin ligase, which targets it for proteosomal degradation [44], [53]. This 
transcription factor is responsible for the expression of genes involved in counteracting oxidative 
damage, such as proteins that refold oxidatively-damaged proteins. When wild-type K13 senses 
oxidants (such as artemisinin), it undergoes a conformational change and releases the transcription 
factor. Thereby, the transcription can initiate the expression of the counteracting genes.  
In wild-type parasites this response is not sufficient to counter-act the oxidative effect of 
artemisinin, hence the sensitivity to the drug. In resistant parasites, however, the mutations in the 
K13-propeller causes destabilization of the interaction between K13 and the transcription factor 
==PAGE:23==
 
13 
 
[48]. This leads to a constant expression of the genes counteracting oxidative damage, allowing the 
parasite to better withstand the oxidative damage caused by artemisinins.  
 
Figure 8: A proposed mechanism of artemisinin sensitivity and resistance in Plasmodium falciparum [54]. In artemisinin 
sensitive parasites, wild-type K13 (green) binds a putative transcription factor (blue) and targets it for degradation. In 
artemisinin-resistant parasites, mutant K13 (red) cannot bind the transcription factor, allowing it to upregulate the 
expression of genes involved in counteracting oxidative stress. 
1.4.3 K13 resistance marker has transformed artemisinin 
resistance monitoring  
Discovery of the K13 artemisinin resistance marker has revolutionized approaches to the monitoring 
of artemisinin resistance, allowing molecular surveillance in remote areas. Many molecular 
surveillance studies have greatly expanded the map of K13 propeller polymorphism. Aiming to 
support the WHOs “Global Plan for Artemisinin Resistance Containment”, the WorldWide 
Antimalarial Research Network (WWARN) has collected data from 39 of these molecular 
surveillance studies, in total summarizing K13 propeller polymorphisms from 64 countries [55].  
Using this data, they have constructed the ART Molecular Surveyor (Figure 9), an interactive map 
that summarizes K13 propeller polymorphisms by location and year. The aim of the surveyor is to 
provide a clear and standardized visualization of the data available to date, which can be used in the 
design of monitoring strategies. In order for the surveyor to be a reliable tool for artemisinin 
resistance monitoring, it must be perpetually up to date. A global effort is therefore needed to 
continuously integrate K13 polymorphism data from around the world.

1.5 Molecular marker for geographic origin  
1.5.1 
Polymorphisms  
in 
organellar  
genomes  
as 
geographical barcode  
Apart from being able to monitor resistance spread and emergence, it is crucial to understand the 
interconnectedness of parasite populations and be able to trace the source of imported infections. 
Recently, a barcoding system has been developed which can be used to distinguish geographic 
origins of P. falciparum parasites [56]. This system uses barcoding of SNPs in mitochondria and 
apicoplast genes of malaria parasites.  
Previous barcoding approaches of P. falciparum were based on nuclear SNPs [57]. These approaches 
are, however, constrained by frequent recombination of the nuclear genome which disrupts multi-
locus SNP associations. Preston et al. [56] suggested that, due to the strict maternal inheritance of 
the mitochondrion and apicoplast genomes, it might be possible to create a barcode from 
polymorphisms in these genomes that is geographically informative and stable over time.  
The mitochondrion genome of P. falciparum is a 6-kb linear extrachromosomal DNA molecule which 
exists in multiple copies in each parasite [58]. It is transmitted in female gametocytes only and does 
==PAGE:25==
 
15 
 
not recombine along lineages. The apicoplast genome is a 35-kb circular molecule which is also 
inherited maternally. There is strong evidence that these organelles are co-transmitted during 
gametocytogenesis [56], [59]. This means that SNP variation in the two organelles combined can 
give extended possibilities for identifying geographically specific haplotypes.  
Preston et al. conducted a repetitive haplotype classification analysis using SNP variation in these 
two organelles combined [56]. With the data from this analysis, they constructed a 23-SNP barcode 
(Figure 10) that can predict the geographical origin of a sample with 92 % accuracy. 
1.5.2 Limitations and future prospects of the organellar 
barcode 
The ability to determine the geographical origin of P. falciparum isolates could potentially have great 
practical value in containing drug resistance and eliminating malaria. However, due to scarcity of 
sequence data from some regions, the barcode in its current form lacks data representing the Indian 
sub-continent, Central America, southern Africa and the Caribbean. Moreover, due to high genetic 
diversity in Africa, it is still very difficult to distinguish samples from different parts of Africa. Thus, 
there is also a need for more data from Africa.   
If the barcoding system were to be fully and successfully developed, use of it might offer great 
insights into the evolution of parasite population structure and diversity. For instance, this system 
could provide a means to pick up potential increase in presence of Asian parasite strains in Africa or 
it could be used to track the source of an imported outbreak. Such valuable insights could help direct 
the focus of resistance spread control and thereby help speed up the eradication of malaria.

1.6 Objectives of this study 
1.6.1 Main objectives 
The rationale behind the current study rests in the expectation that the combination of identifying 
polymorphisms in the P. falciparum K13 propeller and in the organellar genomes of the parasites 
could give important insights into P. falciparum population structure and diversity and thereby help 
detail the route of artemisinin resistance spread. The main objectives of the study can be 
summarized into the following points: 
1) Identification of SNPs in the K13 gene in P. falciparum samples from India, Tanzania and 
Senegal. 
a. Using this data, determine whether artemisinin resistance is likely to be present in 
the examined areas. 
2) Identification of SNPs in the mitochondrial genome in P. falciparum samples from India, 
Tanzania and Senegal.  
a. By comparing with findings from other studies, determine whether SNPs could be 
geographically restricted. 
b. Assess the validity of the barcoding system in its current form by testing whether 
the barcode is able to identify the geographic origin of the samples in this study. 
3) Explore an aspect of temporal evolution of P. falciparum by analyzing SNPs in both the K13 
gene and the mitochondrial genome in samples from peak transmission seasons from two 
consecutive years in India.  
4) Explore an aspect of within-country diversity of P. falciparum by analyzing SNPs in both the 
K13 gene and the mitochondrial genome in samples from four different sites in Senegal. 
1.6.2 Steps to fulfill main objectives  
The main objectives of the study will be fulfilled by working through the following steps: 
a) Using polymerase chain reaction (PCR), K13 and mitochondrial sequences in Indian, 
Tanzanian and Senegalese P. falciparum samples will be amplified. 
==PAGE:28==
18 
 
b) Using a commercial sequencing service, PCR products will be sequenced. 
c) Using sequence analysis software, sequences will be analyzed and SNPs will be detected. 
d) Detected SNPs will be compared with relevant observations from other studies, and the 
significance of the findings of this study will then be assessed. 
1.6.3 Choice of samples  
This study will analyze P. falciparum samples from India, Tanzania and Senegal. The reason for 
choosing India as a sample site is that the organellar barcode lacks data representing the Indian 
subcontinent. Furthermore, India’s geographical position between Southeast Asia and Africa could 
represent a site through which artemisinin resistance potentially could spread. It would therefore 
be convenient to 1) be able to monitor whether artemisinin resistance is present in this area and 2) 
be able to see if imported infections have their origin in this area.  
Indian samples from peak transmission seasons from two consecutive years will be analyzed, 
enabling this study to explore an aspect of temporal evolution of P. falciparum populations in this 
area.  
The motivation for choosing samples from Tanzania in East Africa and Senegal in West Africa is that 
it is still very difficult to distinguish samples from different parts of Africa. There is therefore a need 
for denser data representation of organellar polymorphisms in P. falciparum from Africa.  
With regard to artemisinin resistance surveillance, it is crucial that K13 polymorphisms are 
continuously monitored, especially in Africa, since emergence of artemisinin resistance there would 
be devastating. Moreover, if artemisinin resistance were to spread from Southeast Asia to Africa, it 
is likely that it would arrive in East Africa first due to its geographical position and frequent air travel 
to the area. Additionally, resistance to chloroquine and SP therapies in Africa were detected first in 
East Africa [60]–[62], making East Africa a particularly important site to focus resistance monitoring 
to.  
Senegalese samples from four different sites in Senegal will be analyzed, allowing this study to 
explore an aspect of within-country diversity of P. falciparum in West Africa. 
==PAGE:29==
 
19 
 
1.6.4 Choice of genetic sequences  
In this study, it was chosen to look for SNPs in the entire P. falciparum K13 gene (2181 bp). However, 
due to time limitation, the entire mitochondrial and apicoplast genomes will not be sequenced and 
analyzed. Therefore, after discussions with colleagues at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical 
Medicine who were the main authors of the Preston et al. (2014) publication [56], this study has 
chosen to focus on a 2215-bp long sequence of the nearly 6-kb long mitochondrial genome. This 
sequence was chosen because several earlier studies [56], [63]–[65] have detected polymorphisms 
within this sequence in P. falciparum.

2. Materials and Methods 
2.1 Samples 
2.1.1 India 
Indian samples were collected as part of a previous study in Dumargarhi village in the Ranchi District 
in the state of Jharkhand in eastern India [66]. During the study period from May 2014 to April 2016, 
four cross-sectional malaria prevalence surveys were carried out; two during peak transmission 
season (October-December) and two during low transmission season (June-August). In the present 
study, samples from the peak transmission seasons in 2014 (CSS1) and 2015 (CSS3) were used. The 
sample type was filter paper blood spots, from which extracted DNA solutions were provided for 
the present study in agreement with the authors.  
2.1.2 Tanzania 
Tanzanian samples were collected during a previous study  in Korogwe District in Northeastern 
Tanzania [67]. Blood samples were collected in years 2013 through 2016 from children admitted to 
Korogwe District Hospital who were blood smear positive for P. falciparum. In the present study, 
DNA was purified from 84 of these blood samples using a QIAamp DNA Mini Kit from QIAGEN 
(Hilden, Germany) according to manufacturer’s protocol. 
2.1.3 Senegal 
Senegalese samples originate from a study  conducted in 14 health facilities across Senegal [68]. 
During the study period from July 2013 through 2014, positive rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) were 
collected and stored. Extracted DNA solutions were made available for the present study in 
agreement with the authors [69]. Samples used in the present study are from the central part of 
==PAGE:31==

Senegal (Kaolack and Ndoffane districts) and from the northern part (Richard Toll and Podor 
districts).  
2.1.4 Controls 
For all PCRs carried out in this study, positive and negative controls were used. Positive controls 
used were DNA purified from malaria strains: 3D7/NF54, 7G8, FCR3 and K1. Negative controls used 
were: DNA purified from a Danish malaria-negative blood sample and ultrapure water.

2.4 Agarose gel electrophoresis  
In order to verify PCR amplification, PCR products were run on ethidium bromide gels with 1.2 % 
agarose (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). Samples were mixed with 6X DNA loading buffer before 
loaded into gel. Bands were visualized using ImageQuant LAS4000 UV detector (GE Healthcare Life 
Sciences, Chalfont St. Giles, UK) and fragment sizes were determined using Gene Ruler 100 bp Plus 
DNA ladder from Thermo Scientific (Waltham, MA, USA). Mitochondrial PCR products were 
approved for sequencing if bands were detected in all three nested mitochondrial PCRs. Likewise; 
K13 PCR products were approved for sequencing if bands were detected in both nested K13 PCRs. 
2.5 Preparation for sequencing  
PCR products were sequenced using Eurofins Genomics’ Plate Seq Service. Samples were prepared 
according to the company’s sample submission guide [72]. First, DNA concentrations of the PCR 
products were measured using NanoDrop 2000 Spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, 
MA, USA). PCR products and primers were subsequently diluted to specified concentrations (5 ng/µL 
and 10 pmol/μL, respectively) and added to the sequencing plate in specified amounts (15 µL sample 
and 2 µL primer). Primers used for sequencing were the same primers used for amplification of 
nested fragments (Table 2 and Table 4). All fragments were sequenced in both forward and reverse 
direction.

of the reads at the specific position and by comparing with the reference sequence

4. Discussion 
4.1 SNP findings in the K13 gene 
4.1.1 K13 SNP findings are not associated with resistance  
Collectively, four different SNPs were found in the K13 gene (NCBI RefSeq: XM_001350122.1) in the 
samples from India, Tanzania and Senegal. Located at bp positions 521, 523, 566 and 764, none of 
them are within the propeller region which extends from bp position 1294 to bp position 2115 [50]. 
All SNPs that have shown to be associated with artemisinin resistance in Southeast Asia are within 
the propeller region [24], [34], [45]–[47], [49] and the assumed resistance mechanism relies on a 
conformational change in the K13 propeller [51], [75]. Therefore, the K13 SNPs found in this study 
are unlikely to be associated with artemisinin resistance. This suggests that P. falciparum 
populations in the studied areas are still effectively susceptible to artemisinin, if SNPs in the 
propeller region of K13 are the sole marker of artemisinin resistance. 
The fact that no K13 SNPs associated with artemisinin resistance were found in this study is in line 
with earlier reports describing polymorphisms in P. falciparum in samples from the same countries, 
but different regions: northeastern India (Tripura, Mizoram and Arunachal Pradesh) [76], western 
Senegal (Dakar) [77], [78] and eastern Tanzania (Dar es Salaam) [77]. To compare, the samples used 
in this study were from eastern India (Ranchi district), northeastern Tanzania (Korogwe district) and 
central- and northern Senegal (Kaolack, Ndoffane, Richard Toll and Podor districts). The SNPs 
detected in the mentioned studies were, similarly to the SNPs detected in the present study, all 
different from the artemisinin-resistance associated SNPs observed in Southeast Asia [24], [34], 
[45]–[47], [49]. 
==PAGE:46==
36 
 
4.1.2 Most prevalent K13 SNP: A566C  
The most prevalent K13 SNP found in this study in both Indian, Tanzanian and Senegalese samples 
is A566C, while the other detected SNPs occurred only in one sample each. This SNP is non-
synonymous and causes an amino acid substitution of lysine with threonine at codon position 189. 
This SNP was found in 17.4 % of the Indian samples, with no major difference in the prevalence 
between the two peak transmission seasons. Further, it was found in 10.4 % of the Tanzanian 
samples and 22.0 % of the Senegalese samples. This SNP is geographically widespread, and has 
earlier been detected in Uganda [79], Senegal [78], Malawi [80], the China-Myanmar border [81], 
Bangladesh [34] and Northeastern India [76] (Figure 17).  
 
Figure 17: Geographical distribution of the Plasmodium falciparum Kelch13 A566C single-nucleotide polymorphism 
(SNP). In the present study, the A566C SNP was found in both Indian (17.4 %), Tanzanian (10.4 %) and Senegalese 
samples (22.0 %) (callouts with text). Earlier studies (empty callouts) have detected the SNP in Senegal, Uganda, Malawi, 
Bangladesh, Northeastern India and along the China-Myanmar border [34], [76], [78]–[81].  
 
The P. falciparum K13 gene consists of three domains: a “Plasmodium specific domain”, a BTB-POZ 
domain and a Kelch propeller domain. The A566C SNP is positioned in the so-called “Plasmodium 
specific domain” of the K13 gene. As the name suggests, this domain does not show homology with 
other known Kelch-containing proteins [45]. Hence, little is known about this domain and it is 
therefore difficult to ascertain why the mutation is so widespread.  
==PAGE:47==
 
37 
 
A566C was the only K13 SNP detected in the Indian samples, while one and two other SNPs were 
detected in the Tanzanian and Senegalese samples, respectively. The fact that more SNPs were 
detected in the African samples suggests a higher genetic diversity of P. falciparum in Africa 
compared to India. This difference in genetic diversity between the sample sites will be discussed 
later (see section 4.3).  
4.1.3 Artemisinin resistance may be caused by different 
SNPs in Africa and Asia  
None of the K13 SNPs observed in this study are associated with resistance to artemisinin, indicating 
that there is still no resistance towards artemisinin in the studied areas. However, it is important to 
realize that the SNPs that are associated with resistance in Southeast Asia may not be determinants 
of resistance in Africa or other areas of the world. For instance, the high-level usage of ACTs in Africa 
may cause positive selection for resistant parasites, which could lead to de novo emergence of 
artemisinin resistance in Africa, possibly with different mutations conferring resistance as compared 
to Southeast Asia.  
An example demonstrating the likelihood of this scenario is an SNP at amino acid position 578 within 
the propeller region, which has been observed in several African countries: Congo (1.2 %) [82], 
Gabon (1.1 %) [82], Ghana (1.0 %) [82], [83], Kenya (2. 8 %) [82], Mali (1.1 %) [84] and Uganda (1.0 
%) [85]. This SNP was shown not to be associated with delayed parasite clearance in Mali [84]. 
Nevertheless, it is suspected to be a determinant of resistance in Africa for several reasons. First, it 
is located two amino acids before the C580Y SNP, which is the SNP that associates with 85 % of 
artemisinin treatment failure in Southeast Asia [34], [45]. Secondly, computational modeling 
suggests that the A578S SNP could lead to a conformational change disrupting the function of the 
K13 propeller, similar to the C580Y SNP [86]. Findings such as A578S suggest that we should be 
aware that an SNP could be a determinant of resistance in Africa, though it is not found to be in 
Southeast Asia. 
==PAGE:48==
38 
 
4.1.4 Drug resistance in Africa versus Asia  
Host immunity plays an important role in emergence of resistance. Immunity to malaria is acquired 
through repeated exposure to malaria parasites [17]. People living in higher-transmission areas 
therefore tend to have a higher degree of immunity. People with high levels of antimalarial 
immunity rarely experience disease symptoms, even though their blood may contain an amount of 
parasites that could prove lethal to a malaria-naïve visitor [17]. Parasites with resistant phenotypes 
can therefore potentially be masked in these high-transmission areas. This means that it likely will 
be very difficult to establish the effect of potential resistance-conferring mutations, if they were to 
arise de novo in Africa [87].  
The high degree of immunity in Africa also means that many infections not are treated. The selective 
pressure is therefore lower, and resistant phenotypes are thus not selected for. Moreover, in high-
transmission areas, there are more polyclonal infections. This means that parasites with different 
phenotypes (such as drug-resistant and not drug-resistant) more often have the opportunity to 
undergo recombination to increase parasite diversity [87]. Therefore, a drug-resistant phenotype 
can more easily be lost between two generations in Africa.  
Combined, these conditions mean that it most likely will take longer time before a potential 
association between a mutant genotype and in vivo artemisinin resistance is observed in Africa. It is 
therefore important to investigate the potential involvement of K13 SNPs in artemisinin resistance 
by other means than in vivo assessment, such as computer modelling. In this way, SNPs that could 
potentially alter the conformation of the K13 propeller might be identified. This would allow malaria 
control activities to react quickly if the prevalence of such a suspected SNP suddenly started 
increasing in a given area. 
However, since it remains uncertain whether K13 polymorphisms can act as a molecular 
determinant of resistance in Africa, it is highly important that molecular monitoring of resistance is 
combined with routine assessment of in vivo efficacy of ACTs. 
==PAGE:49==
 
39 
 
4.1.4 Other markers of artemisinin resistance than K13  
Apart from polymorphism in K13, mutations in other genes may be involved in conferring resistance 
to artemisinin in P. falciparum. A recent study [88] found increased ring-stage survival rates for P. 
falciparum isolates from Cambodia that harbored no K13 mutations. Another study in the UK 
reported K13-independent treatment failure in imported cases of P. falciparum malaria which had 
been treated with artemether-lumefantrine [89]. The findings of these studies indicate that 
artemisinin resistance monitoring should not focus entirely to K13 polymorphisms. Effort should be 
made to identify genetic loci involved in conferring artemisinin resistance in the absence of K13 
mutations. This might ultimately result in additional artemisinin resistance markers, which could 
make the possibilities for molecular surveillance of artemisinin resistance more comprehensive and 
reliable.  
4.1.5 Uncertainties related to the method  
When assembling the two K13 nested sequences to the reference sequence, some samples had a 
small span of low coverage (only one read) in the overlap between the two sequences. This low 
coverage could result from too small an overlap between the two nested sequences or poor-quality 
readings in the ends of the sequences that consequently would be trimmed off.  If the samples had 
SNPs in this small span of low coverage, they could potentially have been missed or interpreted as 
sequencing mistakes. Fortunately, none of the K13 SNPs shown to be associated with artemisinin 
resistance [24], [34], [45]–[47], [49] are positioned in this span, and any such SNP has therefore not 
been missed. In order to overcome this disadvantage of the method, a protocol could be designed 
in which the overlap between the two nested sequences is bigger.

4.2 SNP findings in the mitochondrial genome  
4.2.1 Findings in the Indian samples  
In the Indian samples, five different SNPs were detected in the mitochondrial genome. The most 
prevalent SNPs were T2175C (32.6 %) and G1367A (33.7 %). These SNPs have only been detected 
before in India and Bangladesh [65] (Figure 18), and are thus likely to be geographically restricted 
to this area. In correspondence with colleagues from London School of Hygiene and Tropical 
Medicine [90], the main authors of Preston et al. (2014) [56], it was confirmed that these two SNPs 
are geographically informative of the origin of the sample and the two SNPs will in fact be included 
in the updated version of the barcode.  
Another SNP, G1511A, was present in 4.4 % of the samples. This SNP is most likely not geographically 
restricted, as it has also been detected in Cameroon [90] (Figure 18). G1692A was detected in 9.8 % 
of the samples. This is a quad-allelic locus and has been detected in many geographical areas 
including Brazil, Nigeria and Cameroon [63] (Figure 18). In this study, it was also detected in Tanzania 
and Senegal. This SNPs is therefore not geographically informative neither. 
A827G was also detected in 4.4 % of the samples. According to published literature, this SNP has 
not previously been detected. Therefore, there is a possibility that this SNP is geographically 
restricted. Whether the SNP is geographically restricted to, for instance, the Indian sub-continent 
should be backed up by the detection of the SNP in samples from other sites of area.

4.2.2 Variation in prevalence of SNPs between seasons in 
India 
In the Indian samples, the prevalence of the two SNPs T2175C and G1367A differed significantly 
between the two peak transmission seasons. This difference between seasons raises the question 
of whether the mitochondrial genome is in fact stable over time. However, it is possible that the 
parasite sub-population exhibiting the G1367A SNP for some reason survived the low transmission 
season between the two high seasons better than the sub-population exhibiting the T2175C SNP. If 
this is the case, it might be that the mitochondrial genomes of the individual parasite sub-
populations are, in fact, stable and the difference in the overall prevalence of the SNPs owes to 
different survival rates of sub-populations across the low season. This could for instance be due to 
a bottleneck caused by some sort of environmental change. Such events can sometimes alter the 
variation in the gene pool of a population in a random manner.

4.2.3 Findings in the Tanzanian samples  
In the Tanzanian samples, 12 different SNPs were detected in the mitochondrial genome. The most 
prevalent SNPs were G1692A (10.4 %), G863T (4.4 %) and T1214C (4.4 %) (Figure 19). The rest of the 
SNPs were detected in only one sample each. As mentioned, SNPs at locus 1692 are not 
geographically restricted, as they have been detected in many different geographical areas. G863T 
is not geographically restricted, as it earlier has been detected in both Ghana, Malawi and Laos [90]. 
T1214C, however, has earlier only been detected in the neighboring country, Malawi [90]. There is 
therefore a possibility that this SNP could be geographically restricted to East Africa.  
G2363A was only detected in a single Tanzanian sample. It is nevertheless an interesting finding as 
it is geographically restricted to East Africa and is already part of the barcoding system developed 
by Preston et al. (2014) [56]. Although geographically restricted, the frequency of the SNP is low, so 
it can only discriminate about 1-3 % of the East African population [56].

Another interesting finding in the Tanzanian samples is C772T. This SNP was also only detected in a 
single sample. However, combined with other SNPs in the mitochondrial genome and the apicoplast 
genome this SNP is part of the barcode for discrimination of samples from both Southeast Asia and 
Oceania [56]. This could mean one of two things: either this SNP cannot be used to discriminate 
samples from Southeast Asia/Oceania after all or the strain is imported from one of the two 
mentioned areas.  
The presence of artemisinin resistance in Southeast Asia makes the latter possibility a disturbing 
scenario. Whether the strain matches one of the haplotypes from Southeast Asia cannot be 
confirmed nor dismissed in this study, since only part of the mitochondrial genome was sequenced, 
and the barcode consists of SNPs in both the mitochondrial and the apicoplast genomes. Although 
it is possible that the strain is imported from Southeast Asia, it has most likely not imported 
artemisinin resistance, since no SNPs associated to artemisinin resistance were observed in this 
study. Nevertheless, the sample containing this SNP is a perfect example of the relevance of 
interplay between molecular drug resistance markers and geographical barcodes.  
4.2.4 Findings in the  Senegalese samples  
In the Senegalese samples, 11 different SNPs were detected. SNPs at bp position 1692 were 
detected in 35.4 % of the samples. This locus is known to be quad-allelic [56], [63], meaning that all 
four nucleotides have been observed at this locus. This also became apparent in this study, as three 
different versions of the SNP were detected in the Senegalese samples: G1692A, G1692T and 
G1692C (Figure 20).  
The second-most prevalent SNP in the Senegalese samples was C1637A (7.3 %), which, according to 
published literature, is reported here for the first time. This SNP could therefore be geographically 
restricted to West Africa. However, as with the A827G finding in the Indian samples, this hypothesis 
should be supported up by the detection of the SNP in other sites of Senegal or West Africa in 
general.

4.2.5 Within-country genetic diversity of P. falciparum  in 
Senegal 
The mitochondrial SNPs detected in the different sites in Senegal differed greatly both with regard 
to SNP loci and frequencies (Figure 21). This indicates a high genetic diversity of P. falciparum among 
different populations in Senegal. To obtain a more exact metric for genetic diversity among the 
populations, the dataset could be analyzed to calculate Wright’s F-statistics (FST) [91]. This was, 
however, beyond the scope of this study.  
The apparent high within-country genetic diversity of P. falciparum in Senegal is consistent with a 
high rate of malaria transmission in Senegal and matches the results of a recent study conducted in 
a neighboring country, Mali [92]. Using microsatellite markers, another method for measuring 
genetic diversity, this study found a particularly high genetic diversity level of P. falciparum in Mali.  
High genetic diversity may constitute an obstacle in malaria control efforts. It has been shown that 
the genetic diversity of P. falciparum is indicative of the ability of the parasites to adapt to their 
hosts by selection of beneficial traits, such as antigenic variability and drug resistance [93].

Moreover, as will be discussed later (see section 4.3), it is more difficult to define geographical 
barcodes for areas of high genetic diversity.

4.2.6 Evaluation of the validity of the organellar barcoding 
system 
The barcode in its current form lacks representation of the Indian sub-continent [56]. It would hence 
not be possible to determine the geographical origin of the Indian samples in this study.  
In the Tanzanian samples, one SNP (G2383A), which is also a part of the barcode for distinguishing 
samples from East Africa, was detected in a single sample. However, an SNP (C772T), which is part 
of the barcode for distinguishing samples from Southeast Asia and Oceania, was also detected. 
While this could be a sign of an imported infection, it could also indicate that the barcode in its 
current form is not sufficiently informative on the geographical origin of P. falciparum samples.  
==PAGE:56==
46 
 
For distinguishing samples from West Africa, the barcoding system only uses SNPs in the apicoplast 
genome. Naturally, since this study only sequenced part of the mitochondrial genome, none of these 
SNPs could be detected in the Senegalese samples.  
To sum up, with the SNPs detected in the present study, the barcode would only be able to identify 
the geographical origin of a single of the 237 analyzed samples. However, due to a time and 
economical limitations, this study was limited to only analyzing a part of the mitochondrial genome. 
The barcode consists of SNPs in the entire mitochondrial genome as well as the apicoplast genome. 
Therefore, it is not possible to say whether the barcode in its full form would be able determine the 
geographical origin of the samples in this study. To evaluate this, it would be necessary to look for 
SNPs in the rest of the mitochondrial genome and the apicoplast genome of the studied samples.  
4.3 Genetic diversity of African and Indian P. 
falciparum  
In the mitochondrial genome, as well as in the K13 gene, far more SNPs were detected in the 
samples from Africa (Tanzania and Senegal) than in the Indian samples. The present study also found 
a high variety of mitochondrial polymorphisms in P. falciparum within a single African country, 
Senegal (Figure 21). Moreover, apart from the geographically widespread G1692A in the 
mitochondrial genome, no SNPs were shared among the Tanzanian and Senegalese samples. 
Collectively, this data suggests a much higher genetic diversity in the African samples as compared 
to the Indian samples.  
A mathematical measure of the genetic diversity of the different populations could be obtained, for 
example by calculating the Shannon entropy [94], allowing a more accurate comparison of the 
diversities. This was, however, beyond the scope of this study.  
The apparent high genetic diversity in the African isolates compared to the Indian isolates is 
consistent with suggestions that P. falciparum had already infected humans and expanded greatly 
before they migrated out of Africa and followed our ancestors in their expansion throughout the 
tropics [64], [95]–[97].  Studies suggest that this human migration out of Africa produced sequential 
==PAGE:57==
 
47 
 
genetic bottlenecks for P. falciparum, explaining the lower genetic diversity outside of Africa [63], 
[96].  
Also supporting the out of Africa model, a study by Tanabe et al. (2010) [96] showed a strong 
negative correlation between within-population genetic diversity of P. falciparum and geographic 
distance from sub-Saharan Africa was documented. This means that the larger the distance from 
Africa, the lower the genetic diversity of P. falciparum is. The fact that the present work found a 
lower variety of polymorphisms in P. falciparum in India compared to Africa matches very well with 
this negative correlation.  
The high level of genetic diversity in Africa combined with high malaria transmission rates results in 
a higher degree of meiotic recombination between different genotypes [98]. This, in turn, maintains 
the high genetic diversity of P. falciparum diversity in Africa. Due to this sustained higher genetic 
diversity of P. falciparum in Africa, it will most likely be more difficult to identify geographically 
restricted SNPs in African countries, which the findings of this study also suggests. Preston et al. 
(2014) [56] also experienced difficulty in discriminating P. falciparum samples within the African 
continent. Thus, in order to be able to identify populations from different parts of Africa, efforts 
should be focused in obtaining denser data representation of mitochondrial polymorphisms from 
this continent.  
4.4 Future perspectives 
4.4.1 Continued monitoring of in vivo ACT efficacy and  K13 
polymorphisms is necessary  
Even after the setup of the WWARN ART Surveyor [55], monitoring artemisinin resistance remains 
a substantial challenge. The global community needs precise and timely intelligence in order to 
prevent or delay the spread and/or emergence of artemisinin resistance. There are still many areas 
where there is no information on the efficacy of ACTs nor information on the presence of K13 
polymorphisms. Furthermore, in order to effectively track the emergence and spread of antimalarial 
==PAGE:58==
48 
 
drug resistance in real-time, continued sampling from areas where data already exists is necessary. 
The global community must therefore continue to collect and share data on K13 polymorphisms as 
well as data other antimalarial resistance markers.  
In this regard, it is important that molecular surveillance data is shared prior to publication. A 
publication reviewing data from SP resistance surveys found an average lag time of five years from 
the date of survey to the date of publication [99]. Such delayed sharing of molecular artemisinin 
resistance surveillance could result in a huge cost to public health. 
As discussed earlier, it remains uncertain whether K13 polymorphisms can be used as a molecular 
marker for artemisinin resistance in Africa at all and there may even be other markers for 
artemisinin resistance than K13 [88], [89]. It is therefore important, that we, apart from molecular 
surveillance, keep monitoring in vivo efficacy of ACTs. Some countries already have routine 
surveillance, while others have no monitoring system [99]. In order to achieve good regional 
surveillance and maximize the likelihood of early resistance detection, sentinel surveillance sites 
should be much more widely dispersed and guidelines should be made that advise how frequently 
and with what coverage surveillance surveys should be undertaken [100]. 
4.4.2 More data is needed on organellar single -nucleotide 
polymorphisms 
In addition to the areas covered in this study, data on mitochondrial and apicoplast SNPs is still 
needed for many other areas for an effective geographical barcode to be constructed. Due to the 
scarcity of sequence data, the barcode in its current form [56] still lacks representation of Central 
America, southern Africa, the Caribbean, and areas of the Indian sub-continent. Moreover, due to 
high genetic diversity in Africa, there is a need for a denser data representation from the continent; 
a fact that also became apparent in the present study.  
In this study, only a fragment of the mitochondrial genome was analyzed. However, the detection 
of SNPs in this fragment is not sufficient for the discrimination of samples from different 
geographical areas. Therefore, a search for geographically restricted SNPs in the remaining part of 
the mitochondrial genome as well as in the apicoplast genome should be carried out for the samples 
==PAGE:59==
 
49 
 
used in this study. This could help define a geographical barcode for the Indian sub-continent and 
improve the predictive power of the barcode for East and West Africa.  
Once the barcode can distinguish samples from all these areas, it should be used in practice. The 
application of the barcode has the potential to improve management of imported malaria cases and 
reduce the risk of local epidemics resulting from further transmission. This will be valuable for local 
malaria elimination and resistance containment programs.  
4.4.3 Predicting resistance markers for drugs not yet on 
the market 
The molecular markers for resistance towards chloroquine and SP were of little operational value 
since resistance had severely compromised the efficacy of the drugs by the time the molecular 
markers were discovered. The future prospects for the containment of artemisinin resistance are 
much more optimistic since the K13 molecular marker for artemisinin resistance was discovered 
before resistance had had a chance to become widespread. If K13 polymorphisms are continuously 
monitored worldwide, the use of this surveillance tool still has great potential to help contain 
artemisinin resistance and prolong its usability.  
In order to be even more on the forefront of containing antimalarial drug resistance, the next step 
could be to try to predict potential resistance markers for antimalarial drugs that are still in the drug 
development pipeline. If the target of the drug for instance relies on binding to a protein, it could 
be investigated which mutations would alter the conformation of the binding site, thereby inhibiting 
binding of the drug. By doing so, awareness could be focused to the emergence and increase in 
prevalence of certain SNPs. Thereby, it could be possible to monitor resistance towards a drug from 
its first day in the market.  
4.4.4 Maybe it is time to take resistance profiling to the 
“next generation ” 
In order to effectively monitor the emergence and spread of antimalarial drug resistance it is 
important that SNP-profiling is continuously carried out for samples from around the world with 
==PAGE:60==
50 
 
regard to both drug resistance markers and geographical barcodes. There is thus a requirement for 
sequencing of many genes in many samples as a permanent and ongoing process. Using Sanger 
sequencing, this can be a costly affair. It may therefore be relevant for research facilities to shift to 
a sequencing method more suitable for large-scale screening.  
Next-generation sequencing (NGS) is highly suited for large-scale screening, as it allows for analysis 
of millions of sequencing reactions at the same time. Of great relevance to the present work, a yet 
unpublished proof-of-concept study recently described an NGS-based method for combined high-
throughput resistance-profiling and mitochondrial barcoding of malaria infections [101]. The 
method uses Illumina’s Miseq next generation sequencing technology combined with custom dual 
indexing for track-keeping of sequencing pertaining to a specific infection.  
The method was found to be a reliable platform for high-throughput surveillance of emergence and 
spread of antimalarial drug resistance [101]. Importantly, it was shown to be affordable: the 
estimated price for sequencing of all relevant molecular markers of antimalarial drug resistance as 
well as geographical barcodes is $4 per sample. In comparison, the cost of Sanger sequencing of the 
K13 gene and a fragment of the mitochondrial genome for one sample in this study was

5. Conclusion 
This study set out to identify SNPs in the K13 gene and the mitochondrial genome in P. falciparum 
samples from India, Tanzania and Senegal. Further, it was a goal to assess whether the detected K13 
SNPs were associated with artemisinin resistance and whether the detected mitochondrial SNPs 
were geographically restricted. This goal was accomplished. None of the SNPs in detected in the K13 
gene are likely to be associated with artemisinin resistance. Some of the SNPs detected in the 
mitochondrial genome are geographically restricted and will be incorporated into the geographical 
barcode, while, in line with expectations, a greater number of detected SNPs are not geographically 
restricted. Furthermore, a number of SNPs detected are reported here for the first time. These may 
or may not be geographically restricted. In addition, the present study has shown a significant 
difference in prevalence of mitochondrial SNPs in P. falciparum between two high-transmission 
seasons in Ranchi, India as well as an apparent large within-country diversity of P. falciparum in 
Senegal.  
In summary, the findings of this study are of relevance to artemisinin resistance containment 
activities: they suggest that the P. falciparum populations in the studied areas are still effectively 
susceptible to artemisinin and that they will contribute to an expansion of the geographical barcode 
system developed by Preston et al. [56].
