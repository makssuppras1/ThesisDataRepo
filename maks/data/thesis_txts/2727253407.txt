Abstract
Background: Navigating an airway in a structured manner during flexible bronchoscopy is
essential for an efficient and thorough procedure, and implementing feedback during training
ensures competence prior to a clinical setting. Realistic airway phantoms extracted from medi­
cal images allows for patient­ and scenario­specific training.
Objective: The purpose of this project is to develop a realistic bronchial phantom that can
evaluate the user’s performance based on structured navigation.
Methods: A optically and tactically realistic phantom was created from segmenting the airway
from chest CT scan and 3D printing on a PolyJet 3D printer. A detection software was developed
to automatically evaluate training based on structured progress by using the bronchoscope
camera and creating a image classification model that can detect identification markers installed
at the bronchial segment ends of the phantom.
Results: Assessments by clinicians indicated that a realistic phantom would be effective in
patient­ and scenario­specific training prior execution in a clinical setting. Performance tests
demonstrated that the classification model successfully identified some of the bronchial seg­
ments.
Conclusion: With proof­of­concept established, the bronchoscopy simulation device can con­
tinue development for implementation in bronchoscopy examination training.

Introduction
Bronchoscopy is an essential procedure that doctors use to look directly into a patient’s airways
and lungs. This procedure is performed by pulmonologists, who may also be referred to as
bronchoscopists. (Both terms will be used interchangeably throughout this report.) There are
two types of bronchoscopes: rigid and flexible. The rigid bronchoscope is only used to view the
larger airways, thus the flexible bronchoscope is used more often since it can move down into
the smaller airways such as the bronchioles [1].
Bronchoscopy can be used for diagnostic or therapeutic purposes. It can help diagnose and
treat lung cancer, airway blockage, inflammation and infection, an interstitial lung disease caused
by consistent cough, cough of blood and vocal cord paralyze, and more. The flexible broncho­
scope is often used to perform a biopsy, insert medicine into the lungs, or place a breathing
tube in the airway to help give oxygen and suck out secretion [1]. In Figure 1.1, a flexible bron­
choscopy examination is illustrated.
Figure 1.1: An illustration of flexible bronchoscopy performed by a pulmunologist [2]
1.1

1.1.1
Tracheobronchial anatomy
The upper airway consists of the oral cavity, nasal cavities, oropharynx, hypopharynx, and lar­
ynx. The lower airway includes the trachea, bronchial tree segments, and the smaller, distal
bronchiole airways [3]. For this project, we focus only on the trachea and bronchial segments.
The trachea, also called the windpipe, is a tube structure that runs from the cervical region to the
thoracic region. The trachea splits in the inferior part at the carina into the right main bronchus
and the left main bronchus, and each main bronchi can divide further up to the 24th order [4].
However, for the purposes of this project and in line with the standard bronchoscopy procedure,

The trachea and main bronchi have horseshoe­shaped, incomplete hyaline cartilage rings that
run perpendicular to the length, as seen on Figure 1.3. There are about 18 ­ 22 of these rings
along the trachea that covers the anterior and lateral sides, with a length of approximately 80%
of the total tracheal circumference, while the posterior side is flat [6, 3]. Each ring is approx­
imately 4 mm in height and 2 mm apart. The tracheal wall averages to approximately 3 mm,
while the bronchial walls become thinner with every subsegment [6, 7].
1.1.2
Bronchoscopy procedure
The bronchoscope handle has a controller for distal end movement, a working tunnel port, and
a suction valve and suction port. In the procedure, the insertion tube enters the patient and has
a belonging working tunnel through which sampling fluid or tissue and instruments can pass
through. At the distal end of the bronchoscope is the camera and light source.Figure 1.4 is an
example of a flexible bronchoscope, with a tube inserted into the suction port on the handle and
a biopsy tool emerging from the distal end [8].
Figure 1.4: Flexible diagnostic bronchoscope [8]
The bronchoscopist may stand either at the head of the patient with an upside­down viewpoint
of the patient (seen on Figure 1.1 in chapter 1) or at either side of the head, facing the patient.
The bronchoscope handle is typically held by the left hand directly over the entry point, while
the right hand controls the distal end of the bronchoscope and handles syringes and biopsy
equipment, which are inserted in the working tunnel port [5, 8]. The bronchoscope enters via
the nose or the mouth, into the larynx, then trachea. As standard procedure, the pulmonologist
must examine each bronchial segment, regardless of knowing the target [8]. Figure 1.5 shows
bronchoscopic images at various points in the tracheobronchial anatomy, where the ridges from
the cartilage rings can be seen and felt by the bronchoscope when passing through the trachea
and main bronchi.

1.1.3
Complications during bronchoscopy examination
Bronchoscopy is generally a safe procedure, with serious complications occurring in less than
1% of cases and low mortality rate of 0.02% [9]. However, as with any medical procedure, there
are still some risks associated, such as bleeding, infections, hole in the airways (bronchial per­
foration), irritation of the airways (bronchospasm), irritation of the vocal cords (laryngospasm),
and air in the pleural space leading to pneumothorax [1].
Research has shown that inexperienced bronchoscopists have a higher complication rate and a
lower success rate to obtain positive biopsy material. Furthermore, international guidelines indi­
cate that basic competency is obtained after a trainee has performed at least 100 procedures in

ing pace, which may require more or fewer procedures [5]. Thus, it is crucial for pulmonologists,
particularly novice operators, to gain confidence in their skills and thereby mitigate risks towards
patient safety [11, 12].
1.1.4
Current methods for bronchoscopy training
In its early history, bronchoscopy training was conducted using the ”see one, do one, and teach
one” method, in which trainees observe the procedure and practice directly on a patient in a
clinical setting under the guidance of experienced professionals [13]. Many training programs
across the world and smaller hospitals in Denmark still conduct teaching this way [5]. This
method, due to lack of experience, increases risk of complications on the patient and stress for
the trainee [13]. Additionally, this may also foster distrust in doctors, which in turn discourages
people from seeking early medical attention that is essential for positive outcomes.
There are also several training methods that do not involve inexperienced pulmunologists prac­
ticing bronchoscopy on humans. Training using live animals allow for practice on similar anatomy
and scenarios on living beings, but this raises issues with ethics, costs of raising the animals,
and potential damage to the bronchoscope. Advances in technology have allowed for the use
of virtual reality (VR) that give performance feedback in real­time and phantom airway in a man­
nequin, which does not provide feedback. While these inanimate models cannot mimic real­life
scenarios, such as breathing movements or resistance to the bronchoscope, they provide a
safe, ethical way to gain experience and confidence in navigating the airways and performing
sampling and biopsy procedures [13].
CAMES bronchoscopy training program
The Simulation Center at the Copenhagen Academy for Medical Education and Simulation
(CAMES) developed a program for teaching and certifying pulmonologists in bronchoscopy
examination in Denmark and around the world. CAMES is one of four recognized teaching fa­
cilities across the world that have validated tests using a series of training assessment tools,
which demonstrates that pulmonologists significantly improve their bronchoscopy skills after
the three­day course at CAMES [14]. The training program focuses on theory, including lung
anatomy as seen from inside and outside and sampling techniques, and practical experience
using virtual reality and simulation with a phantom.
In the virtual reality training, seen on Figure 1.6a, the trainee is given a score, evaluated based
on the time used during the examination, how many segments are found in the correct order,
how many times the bronchoscope hits the wall, sample collection, and other factors [5, 8]. VR
training, however, uses tools specifically made for this method, not a real bronchoscope. The
virtual reality program at CAMES has three levels of training:
1. Navigation: Timed trials following a blue dot down the airways.

not provide automated feedback but, instead, is observed and assessed by the instructor.
However, neither of these training methods reflect the complete real­life case of bronchoscopy
examination. The virtual reality does not have the experience of interaction with realistic air­
ways nor does the user train with the actual tools in a bronchoscopy procedure. The phantom
does not have a realistic structure and material and does not give real­time feedback when per­
forming a bronchoscopy examination [10, 5]. The standard phantom also cannot incorporate
patient­specific scenarios. Thus, there is a need for developing a realistic phantom with incor­
porated feedback for pulmonologists to train on.
1.1.5
Assessing bronchoscopy competence at CAMES
CAMES has conducted multiple studies assessing factors that affect the quality of a bron­
choscopy examination and validating the simulated training pulmunologists are performing.

Thus, teaching has been focused on the combination of virtual reality and phantom simulation,
as described in subsection 1.1.5. A series of studies have been conducted to validate that train­
ing improves pulmonologist performance when executing a bronchoscopy examination. Overall,
CAMES and literature on the field have proven that receiving feedback during training signifi­
cantly improves pulmonologists skills concerning bronchoscopy examinations with the next trial.
Virtual reality
Virtually reality (VR) is a tool that is used in a wide range at CAMES, due to various scenarios
that can be simulated and reflect basic to real­life cases of patient situations [15]. In a study by
Konge et al., two experienced bronchoscopists compared the performance of 14 senior consul­
tants, 14 trainees, and 14 medical students using a set of 28 criteria/checklist points. The results
validated that VR training improved the novice operators’ performance and, thus, is valid and
reliable for training purposes [16]. Furthermore, CAMES constructed a pass/fail criteria based
on two of their bronchoscopy assessment tools: assessment of VR and assessment of Clini­
cal Bronchoscopy Based on Video Recordings. In the VR assessment, the contrasting­group
method was used to compare 14 experienced operators and 14 novice operators performing
VR bronchoscopy. In the video assessment, 7 experienced bronchoscopists evaluated perfor­
mance based on technical ability and theoretical knowledge. The results indicated that pulmu­
nologists who had performed less than 50 operations would fail the test [15].
Assessment form
In addition, a similar study had been conducted where an assessment form was developed for
bronchoscopy in clinical settings or practicing on a phantom. The assessment tool was devel­
oped by 5 physicians, 3 clinical experts, 1 expert in educational measurements, and 1 person
with experience in both fields. The clinical expert group included three key elements of bron­
choscopy competence in the assessment form: (1) demonstrating technical ability, meaning
moving around the bronchial tree in a gentle way, (2) demonstrating anatomical knowledge,
meaning the operator performs a systematic and thorough visualization of all 19 bronchial seg­
ments, and (3) describing the procedure and the findings. The study incorporated a scale to
evaluate the performance, due to the complexity of findings and performance [10].
Motion analysis of bronchoscope position
A motion analysis study focusing of the trainees movements during a bronchoscopy examination
was performed by Colella et al. Here an automated motion analysis system demonstrated that
experienced bronchoscopists consistently kept the bronchoscope handle stabilized, thus min­
imizing complications to the patient and bronchoscope damage. This indicates that automatic
feedback on correct movements could be incorporated during training to help novice broncho­
scopists improve their skills. However, the sensors used for this study are now unavailable so
it has not been incorporated into the teaching program at CAMES. Furthermore, the teaching
staff at CAMES found the technological setup challenging, so implementation should also focus
on user­friendly setups [9].

Structured navigation progress
In a study conducted by Cold et al., novice, intermediate, and experienced bronchoscopists
were asked to enter all bronchial segments in a phantom simulation while the principal inves­
tigator observed and pressed a button that recorded the time and order each segment was
reached. The results showed that those who progressed from one segment to the next im­
mediate segment (i.e., B1, then B2, and so on, regardless of starting on the right or left side)
were more thorough and efficient in their examination. This is the first study to demonstrate the
importance of a structured navigation path to ensure segments are not missed for assessing
competence with flexible bronchoscopy [17]. Thus, structured progress navigation is incor­
porated in the CAMES bronchoscopy training program [8]. However, manually logging each
progression is an inefficient process and there exists no automated bronchial segment recog­
nition and detection for phantom simulation.
Fiber optic cables for light detection
CAMES Engineering incorporated fiber optic cables at the end of each bronchial segment of
a phantom to evaluate structured navigation. The phantom used was manually designed, not
resembling realistic anatomy, and 3D printed in PLA with open ends where the cables were
attached with colored tape to prevent external light from leaking in. Each cable led to a box with
a distinct spot that would light up when the light emitted from the bronchoscope reached a par­
ticular segment. A separate camera recorded the light box during a bronchoscopy procedure
and the timestamp of the bronchoscope location was evaluated from this. However, there were
many issues with this setup. With a sensor for each of the 19 segments, there were too many
wires extruding from the phantom as well as the external hardware connects to the cables. Ad­
ditionally, there was light leakage within the phantom that sensors from multiple segments were
detecting light from the bronchoscope, thus there were multiple spots in the light box indicating
segment recognition. The cables were attached with tape, as this was an early prototype, but
this caused the sensors to be easily detached from the phantom. Complications with the setup
and insufficient results suspended further explorations of this method. This was purely a exper­
imental setup and not a published study. Details of this experiment were disclosed by MBSS,
with videos of verification tests and remnants of the setup at CAMES Engineering.
Future of clinical bronchoscopy
A review by Nilsson et al. focused on simulation training of bronchoscopy as it is practiced to­
day, but, as the procedure is continuously evolving, the training of bronchoscopy needs to adapt
to these changes. For example, the technology for biopsy taking with endobronchial ultrasound
(EUS) may soon be integrated into a standard bronchoscope, which will necessitate the inte­
gration of simulated endobronchial ultrasound (EBUS) training with standard bronchoscopy, in­
cluding training in recognizing the ultrasound anatomy of mediastinal lymph nodes and vessels.
Navigated bronchoscopy, in which preoperative computed tomography (CT) scans are used to
guide biopsy taking, is also increasingly used and thus needs incorporation in simulation train­
ing as well. The currently available simulators are mostly focused on basic bronchial anatomy
and scope maneuvering. Future simulators should incorporate decision­making scenarios to
allow for complex skills training beyond basic proficiency [18].

1.2
Objective
The objective of this project has two parts: (1) create a realistic phantom model of the tra­
cheobronchial tree and (2) detect that the trainee is examining all 19 bronchial segments in a
structured, consecutive manner. A more realistic phantom than the standard one used today
would give the trainee more practical experience in navigating airways using the actual bron­
choscopy equipment and, therefore, more prepared in conducting bronchoscopy in a clinical
setting. Segment recognition and detection ensures that the trainee inspects each segment in
the structured manner, so that they are more likely not to miss checking any segment, ensuring
thoroughness and efficiency. This would allow the operator to train independently without the
presence an instructor for feedback.

Segmentation of airway from CT scan
An open­access database of chest CT scans for over 1000 COVID­19 patients were taken from
the Harvard Dataverse repository [19]. Due to the large quantity of datasets available, several
datasets were randomly chosen and segmented for the airways using the AirwaySegmentation
extension on 3D Slicer. This automated the segmentation process by selecting a point on the
trachea, after which the algorithm automatically retrieves the convoluted kernel and segments
all the pixels with an intensity below the threshold of the selected point to obtain the airway seg­
mentation [20]. This algorithm, however, could only provide a rough segmentation with minimal
branch extensions, so it was only used for screening purposes of potential CT scans to segment
from. Among the datasets, it was found that Subject 100 provided the most optimal detail and
resolution of the airways based the clarity of the CT images and the automatic segmentation.
Thus, modelling and prototyping were derived from this dataset.
The CT images were manually segmented on 3D slicer to extract the airway. The process for

2.4
Material selection
In this section, we examine the mechanical properties of the airways and several different ma­
terials in order to determine the material for the phantom. As a starting point, the elastic modu­
lus of a human airway were compared with materials capable for 3D printing. Additionally, the
compatibility of the material for use in bronchoscopy training was also considered. We have col­
laborated with 3D Lab Denmark to print the tracheobronchial phantom with a PolyJet 3D printer.
2.4.1
Mechanical properties of the trachea
A study by Safshekan et al. investigated the mechanical properties of the trachea of young and
old people. Tensile tests were performed on the tracheal cartilage in groups based on age, sex,
and position in the trachea (cranial vs caudal). Figure 2.7 shows the resulting elastic modu­
lus for each group. The elastic modulus ranged from 12.178 ± 1.265 MPa in young people to
20.540 ± 1.840 MPa in old people. The study observed no statistically significant difference
based on sex or position in the trachea [21].
Another study by Roberts et al. compared the Young’s modulus of different layers of the tra­
cheal cartilage. The outer layer was 13.6 ± 1.5 MPa and the inner layer was 4.6 ± 1.7 MPa
[22]. Furthermore, a high Young’s modulus reflects the elasticity of intact cartilage [23]. Several
other studies have shown that healthy cartilage young modulus is in the range of 5 ­ 30 MPa
[24, 25, 26].

3.2.1
Using the bronchoscope camera and computer vision
A simple way of applying a detection system for bronchial recognition during training is using
the built­in camera of the bronchoscope by recording a video of the bronchoscopy training. A
laptop can be connected to the bronchoscopy imaging system to record the video, which is a
simple, minimal setup that the user can operate independently. The video recordings from the
camera can then be used to perform computer vision detection using simple unique markers on
each bronchial segment. Through the recordings, the order and the timestamps of segments
examined can be extracted. Because the marker would be incorporated into the detachable
segment ends, the system must be able to recognize the markers regardless of orientation.
Here, we discuss different markers for image recognition.
Colors
Using colors incorporated inside the phantom segments is a simple implementation for image
recognition. An advantage of this method is that it is an easy way to produce for the developer
with 3D printing. One option is to use 19 different colors. However, the color variation may be
too close together within color spectrum for consistent and accurate detection. To mitigate this,
4­5 colors in unique combinations can be applied. A disadvantage is that the trainee may be
able to recognize a pattern to the system. Furthermore, it is not an optically realistic airway,
since the colors would in interfere with the naturalistic colors.
Shapes
Another form that can be used to perform computer vision detection is the use of shapes at
the end of a segment. Like color recognition, either 19 distinguishable shapes or a few unique
shapes in different combinations and positions can be applied. The latter method may be less
obvious for the user to discern a pattern but would still be possible to distinguish. These mark­
ers would not be optically realistic, but since the markers would be small and only located at the
segment ends, it would not completely interfere with a naturalistic appearance. These markers
would be simple to implement into the segment ends but would require a high resolution printer
for multiple small shapes.
Colors and shapes combination
A combination of colored shapes allows for more variety in creating the markers as fewer unique
colors and shapes are needed to make different combinations. Random colored shapes vari­
ations are simple to generate with software. These markers would make this less discernible
but not impossible for the trainee to find a pattern. As the area to place these patterns in the
segment ends are quite small (6.5 mm diameter), implementing such small shapes ( 2 mm max­
imum) in different colors may be difficult. If the segments caps should be created with silicone
molds at CAMES, as mentioned earlier, this would have to be done by hand. If the caps should
be 3D printed, the printer must have a very high resolution. Like the shapes option above, it
is optically unrealistic but may not interfere with a naturalistic appearance due to its location at
the end of a segment.

QR Codes
Another detection marker considered were QR codes incorporated at the segment ends. These
can be generated easily with software and, unlike the colors and shapes, would be completely
indistinguishable to the trainee. However, incorporating this into the segment ends can only
be done by 3D printing with high resolution and no option to make this with silicone molds at

3.2.2
Attaching sensors to the phantom
Fiber optic cables
As discussed in subsection 1.1.5, fiber optic cables at each segment end have previously been
used to detect the light emitted from the bronchoscope. There were several issues with this
setup, and thus it was discarded. Since our phantom has removable ends, a better way to
connect the cables and prevent light leakage could be explored. A benefit to this system would
be that there is no obvious pattern for the user to discern during training. However, with many
wires extruding from the phantom as well as external hardware, this is complicated to setup and
practice with.
Magnetic sensors
The bronchoscope has wires along the scope itself, which emits a small magnetic field. Thus,
an option for the detection system could be use of magnetic sensors, which can be attached to
the detachable segment ends to detect when the bronchoscope end is within a certain range.
However, the sensor must have high accuracy and be able to distinguish between segments that
are close together, which may cause issues with detecting others segments nearby. Depending
on the available magnetic sensors in the market, it may be a wired or wireless connection and
requires another hardware to receive the signal. A wired connection, like the fiber optic cables,
adds complications to setup with multiple wires extruding from the phantom. A wireless sensor
can possibly be connected via bluetooth, which may also require additional software develop­
ment for the project.
Pressure sensors
A previous study has investigated a method with attached pressure sensors to the end of a
bronchoscope to evaluate the bronchoscopy operators performance, which showed that novice
bronchoscopists hit the bronchial wall more often than experience bronchoscopists [31]. How­
ever, using this method requires that the bronchoscope hit the wall, which would cause compli­
cations to the patient during the procedure and could damage the bronchoscope. Additionally,
in order for this system to detect segments, additional sensors and hardware would be have to
be installed, thus causing the same problems as the other sensors method. Thus, this method
is not viable for bronchoscopy training.

Magnetic endoscopic imaging
In a previous study conducted by CAMES, magnetic endoscopic imaging (MEI) was used for
colonoscopy to track the location of the colonoscope and give automated feedback and provide
unbiased evaluation [32]. This method can give the position of the endoscope in space, which
can then be used to visualize the pathway of colonoscopy, but this requires additional software
development to be able to provide the location in relation to the anatomy. There exist some
models with magnetic imaging for bronchoscopes, however, this equipment has a very high
cost. Because the bronchoscope has a smaller diameter than a colonoscope, it would be diffi­
cult to incorporate this technology into a bronchoscope. Therefore, this method was determined
as not feasible to produce for the scope of this project.
CNN and motion sensors
Another approach could be exploring a more complex computer vision method based on a con­
volutions neural network (CNN) and position tracking with a sensor incorporated in the camera
or purely relying on CNN. However, this technology is yet to be explored and demands expertise
and knowledge on neural networks and thus is beyond the scope of this project.

The four possible outcomes are True Positive (TP), True Negative (TN), False Positive (FP type
1 error) and False Negative (FN, type 2 error) [33]. TP indicates that a value is predicted positive

FP indicates that a value is predicted positive but it is false and last FN indicates that a value
is predicted negative and it is false. Therefore, the confusion matrix is very useful since it can
help identify the percentage of accuracy, precision, recall, and more.

6
Discussion
In this chapter, we discuss the considerations and results for the phantom, the detection algo­
rithm, and the bronchoscopy training device.
6.1
Tracheobronchial phantom
6.1.1
Segmentation
Several automated algorithms were considered for segmenting the airways. There are auto­
mated airway segmentation algorithms with high accuracy, including segmentation of some
sub­segments, that have been developed. The EXACT’09 study, developed by Lo et al., cre­
ated a common reference standard of segments and evaluated airway tree extraction algo­
rithms from 15 other research groups. They then combined elements from these algorithms that
was able to extract a more complete airway tree, an average of 78.8% of tree length detected
from 20 datasets based on the reference standard tree [35]. One participating research group
was Feuerstein et al., who developed an algorithm that enhances branch edges and adaptive
cuboidal volumes of interest to improve tracing of the airway tree. An average of 73.3% of the
tree length was detected based on manual labelling of clinicians [36]. Another participating re­
search group was van Rikxoort et al., who used a wavefront propagation based on furcation,
radius, and other factors for region growing and increased the threshold where there are no
”leakage” of outside the airway wall. Their algorithm detected an average 57.0% of tree length
[37]. Garcia­Uceda developed an automatic airway segmentation algorithm using a U­Net deep
CNN for segmenting biomedical images. While not a participant in the EXACT’09 study, they
tested their algorithm with the same dataset and detected an average of 70.3% of the tree length
[38]. This algorithm was available as open source, which we attempted to use. However, there
were issues with compatibility with different software versions and with the computer processing
power available. Therefore, it was decided to use 3DSlicer for airway segmentation since it was
sufficient in extracting the 19 bronchial segments, which are the focus of this project. Ideally,
it would be beneficial in the future to use the Garcia­Uceda algorithm, and thus save time and
obtain a more detailed phantom.
6.1.2
Segment labeling
A study by Petersen et al. came across a challenge that has to be considered, when manually
labeling bronchial segments from airways extracted from CT scans. Clinicians compared air­
way measurements over time by taking 2 CT scans of the same patients and manually labeling
the segments. It was found that there was inconsistency among experts when manually label­
ing segments of the same patient from different CT scans, which the investigators attributed to
anatomical heterogeneity and changes during breathing [39]. Therefore, this should be taken
into consideration when designing a phantom from the same patient’s CT scans taken at differ­
ent times and making classification models.

6.1.3
Choice of material
For phantom material selection, there exists some studies that have created anthropomorphic
phantoms from medical images with rigid and non­rigid materials. Kim et al. 3D printed an
airway model from MRI and CT images with rigid acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) ma­
terial [40]. Shirotani developed a realistic torso with tissue substitute materials, in which the
trachea was composed of a polyurethane base with ester of phosphoric acid additive but does
not discuss how the organs were molded nor the durability of the phantom [41]. Hermosilla et al.
developed a thyroid­neck anthropomorphic phantom made of ICRU­44 polyester resin as equiv­
alent of human soft tissue for nuclear medicine purposes but does not discuss the durability [42].
Park et al. developed a tissue­engineered trachea scaffold that mimics the mechanical behavior
of native trachea, including flexibility, radial strength, and luminal patency, for the purpose of
direct implantation to aid tissue regeneration. Their method of development involves 3D printing
a sacrificial mold to create a scaffold of a polymer solution of poly­(L­lactide­co­ϵ­caprolactone)
(PLCL) and gelatin. However, at room temperature, PLCL changes properties and gelatin is
unstable, and the study does not address the durability of this polymer blend at room temper­
ature due to its purpose for in vivo use [43, 44, 45]. These materials have shown promise as
tissue substitutes for an airway. However, these materials and methods were deemed too com­
plicated to implement in our project, and, therefore, were removed from consideration.
The CAMES Phantom Lab is capable of creating silicone molds of different stiffnesses. How­
ever, silicone has a high coefficient of friction, is tacky, and is prone to sticking to itself causing
blockage [46]. When rubbing the side of a bronchoscope on a silicone sample, the broncho­
scope was indeed sticking to the silicone and was difficult to move smoothly, unlike during clin­
ical bronchoscopy. Thus, silicone was removed from consideration for the phantom because
contact with the bronchoscope hinders movement.
CAMES has previously collaborated with 3D Lab Denmark, which has a Stratasys J5 MediJet
PolyJet 3D printer that can print anatomical models in a variety of material mechanical proper­
ties with high accuracy for training and education. Therefore, we determined that printing with
the J5 MediJet and its printing materials was the most efficient and cost­effective method. Five
samples of materials were provided, and clinicians at CAMES were then consulted for the best
resemblance to a human airway, which is discussed in subsubsection 2.4.3.
6.1.4
Evaluation of the phantom
After first conducting a bronchoscopy examination on the final phantom, the phantom had some
narrow pathways so markers in those segments could be not tested by the detection software,
as discussed in subsection 3.5.2. This case occurred even in the verification of the PLA phan­
tom, as discussed in section 2.5, of bronchoscopy examinations and visual inspection before the
phantom went into final production. Due to the unavailability of the clinicians, the bronchoscopy
examination was done by biomedical engineers before sending the model to production. The

==PAGE:51==
segmented airway was also re­checked in 3D Slicer, and there appeared no visible errors with
the segmentation. Since the model was segmented from a patient’s CT scan and scaled to
match measurements from the CT scan, this could just be the natural airway structure of the
patient and was assumed to be the true size. Several CT datasets were segmented, however,
this CT scan gave the best segmentation, so development continued with this model. The CT
scan was also taken from a COVID patient, so there could have been an obstruction or conges­
tion in their airway that made the airway narrower. Additionally, due to the rigid PLA material of
the early prototypes, it was difficult to tell at the time that the bronchoscope would not be able
to pass through. The narrow parts of the airway model were adjusted so that a bronchoscope
can pass through, though it was not 3D printed nor tested due to time constraints.
It was intended to compare the final phantom with a pig’s airway but, due to time constraints,
this was not possible. However, it could have been beneficial to perform mechanical tests and
compare the results. Since our airway phantom is meant to resemble the human airway in ”look”
and ”feel” during bronchoscopy, rather than actually replicating the airway, it was determined
that assessment by experienced clinicians, who have direct knowledge concerning texture and
appearance of the airways, was an acceptable validation method. Notes from our consultations
of the final phantom can be found in Appendix E. Overall, the anatomical representation of the
phantom got positive assessments from the clinicians. While the clinicians noted anatomical
anomalies for this patient, they determined that a more conventional bronchial structure would
be beneficial for general training while having the option of patient­specific training would be
useful.
6.2
Bronchial segment detection system
6.2.1
Selection of detection of markers
When selecting the markers for segment detection, several types were considered. QR codes
were, ideally, the best option for detection markers because developing classification model
would not be needed. The preliminary tests in the bronchoscopy training box revealed many
issues with recognizing the QR codes, such as the resolution of the printed images and camera
angle. While it is possible that a PolyJet printer could print higher­resolution images of the QR
codes on the caps, it was not guaranteed that this would also resolve the camera angle issues.
Therefore, alternative detection markers had to be explored. In hindsight, caps could be pro­
duced with QR codes at 3D Lab Denmark and thus a test could have been performed, but due
to time constraints, this was not executed.
6.2.2
Training the classification model
Using the combination of the colored shapes as the detection markers, there were several as­
pects to consider in training the classification model. One consideration was that the figures
might be too difficult to distinguish. Therefore, it was important to use sharp colors and shapes
that easily could be distinguished. However, the final result after the caps were printed at 3D
Simulation of a bronchoscopy device for training purposes
43
==PAGE:52==
Lab Denmark, a few of the caps were sometimes harder to distinguish by ourselves, thus po­
tentially making it harder for the classification model to distinguish as well, although this would
be beneficial for the user that the markers could be hard to distinguish. Some of the markers,
as viewed in Figure 3.3, were difficult to classify due to being unable to isolate them on camera
or due to partial images. In hindsight, fewer or more distinct shapes and colors could have been
selected. Using only black and white or RGB colors would have higher contrast and, therefore,
be more distinguishable for computer vision. Fewer shapes and making them larger would also
be easier to present on the camera. However, the drawback is that these would be more rec­
ognizable for the bronchoscopist. Furthermore, a lot of predictions mistakenly classified some
segments as the wall of the phantom due to the wall’s prominent presence in most of the images.
In order to compensate for the classification model’s lack of optimal performance, we incorpo­
rated a class that counts if a prediction is made three times in a row, as described in subsec­
tion 3.5.3. The count of how many predictions were tested on various video recordings and the
final script and classification seem to provide the best feedback when the prediction was made
three times in a row. In hindsight, it could have been better to investigate this in a more detailed
examination together with the confusion matrix (accuracy, precision, recall, and f1 score) that
was performed with the classification model.
In order to improve the future classification model, several ideas and methods could be further
explored. Some of our suggestions are mentioned here. This classification problem could po­
tentially have been resolved by comparing two classification models. One model is trained to
detect markers and another that is trained to detect whether there are one or two markers in a
frame. The two classification models would then be used together, and could potentially have
increased the performance of the classification model.
In subsubsection 5.2.1, it was illustrated that a majority of the data was labeled as ”wall”, in­
dicating that the classification model was assigning images that were difficult to distinguish as
the wall of the phantom. It is shown in Figure 5.2 that the wall had a precision of 86.65% and
a recall of 92.36%, indicating the model is biased towards the wall. Since the training data was
constructed the same way as the test data set, a huge amount of the training data consists of
the wall, thus, the model is more biased towards classifying a frame as a wall. A simple solution
for improving the model classification could potentially be to remove a lot of the frames that are
labeled wall so that some of the markers might be classified better.
Another option for classification could have been to recognize the figures as objects and not
classify them straightaway. However, these options were not further explored due to the time
constraints for this thesis. In hindsight, it could have been beneficial to explore other simple
detection methods such as blipper, Data Matrix, Maxicode, and others. This could potentially
resolve the detection problem if these methods would be able to be detected with the small
camera attached to a bronchoscope.

6.2.3
Data quality and quantity
One of the many ways to obtain a proper classification model is to have relevant data to make
proper predictions. A greater sample of images could have been selected in order to potentially
increase the model’s accuracy. In order to obtain relevant data, various levels of experience
in performing bronchoscopy were used. Some of the collected data, however, did not end up
being of high quality since the experienced bronchoscopists would pass the markers too quickly
during the procedure. While the operators were instructed to pause clearly on the markers, it
was not done consistently throughout the procedure. On the other hand, some of the operators
came so close to the markers that the data was very blurry or bright and, thus, very difficult to
identify. Furthermore, a few of the images obtained were a bit dark. The lighting was some­
times inconsistent due to an automatic setting to adjust the intensity of the bronchoscopy light.
To improve the classification model, the data could be improved by performing post­processing
on the videos, such as noise reduction and adjusting the contrast, exposure, and white balance.
6.2.4
User experience analysis
The user interface was evaluated over two rounds, as described in subsubsection 5.2.3. Ide­
ally, more subjects could have participated to get more feedback with understanding the system,
since only two subjects were tested in each round. Having feedback from people with varying
proficiencies with adapting to new technology would be useful in designing the UI and the user
guide to be more instinctive. After consultation with the senior bronchoscopist during the first
round, many improvements were made to the user guide and user interface, such as clarify­
ing the instructions and simplifying the display. The updated user guide can be found in ??.
The results improved significantly with the updated user guide. Additionally, the senior bron­
choscopist expressed that he desired something simpler, such as a link to click on in order to
evaluate performance. This could be executed by setting up a network server or coding an app,
however, this was out of the scope of this project.
6.3
Bronchoscopy simulation training device
As seen in Table 5.4, there is no significant result for automatic detection in comparison with
manually logging segment detection. The classification model does not predict correctly in the
right order. Potentially, it could have been beneficial to try to vary the count of images that should
be predicted consecutively. However, it was proven that it did not make much of a difference
in the validation dataset nor the test dataset. The video recordings did not provide optimal data
for the model, since there were often two segments seen at the same time, especially in two
of the three videos. The participant also viewed some of the segments too quickly that there
were not enough frames to analyze. In the third recording, the participant was again asked
to isolate each marker. The time measurement between the segments was recorded with our
code, however, it seemed irrelevant to compare the timestamps, when the classification model
did not predict any of the segments in the correct order.

The limitations of the phantom and the software required the clinicians to modify their usual
procedure, such as being unable to check some segments, manipulating the bronchoscope to
isolate the markers, and pausing at each segment, thereby compromising the natural rhythm
they have developed through their experience. The software, at its current state, cannot predict
a segment with too few frames if the operator is moving through too quickly. While this might be
sufficient for novice operators who perform the procedure slowly, the software will be less effec­
tive in evaluating their performance as they improve and perform more efficiently. For experi­
enced bronchoscopists who are demonstrating to observers or would like to further practice their
skills in a simulated setting, this device would be insufficient in evaluating their performance, as
was the case with the experienced clinicians during the validation tests. The device should be
designed to meet user needs rather than having the user, especially experience ones, having to
adjust to the performance of the device. Therefore, improving the phantom design and the clas­
sification model efficiency should be further explored in order to accommodate more skill levels.
6.4
Project management
Though out the journey of the production of the final device goal settings were made weekly.
However, we learned that it can be difficult to estimate time span of a task, when these issues
have not been encountered before, thus the time plan was extended. However, in hindsight, it
could have been very beneficial, even in a small team, that one person was designated as the
product manager. The product management role could have been established more clearly and
one in the team could have been in agreement in charge of following regularly up on deadlines
and making sure requirements were fulfilled days before deadlines.

7
Conclusion
An airway phantom extracted from a CT scan provided an optically realistic alternative for bron­
choscopy training compared to the standard phantom. This would allow bronchoscopists more
practical experience to gain skills and confidence prior to performing bronchoscopy in a clinical
setting with real patients. The classification model was able to correctly detect some of the
bronchial segments. However, there were some issues with obtaining good quality data to train
and test the model with. Nonetheless, proof­of­concept was established for automatic detection
of bronchial segments during training, so further development of the phantom and the detection
software can be continued to aid with bronchoscopy training.
7.1
Future work
Training for biopsy procedures with the phantom
Early in the development phase, a clinician at CAMES expressed that it would be beneficial to
be able to perform various biopsy procedures during training as they are currently done in VR.
Thus, removable caps and standardized segment ends were designed in order to accommo­
date training for biopsy procedures. However, the phantom developed for this project was not
tested with biopsy procedures due to time constraints. Therefore, future work should include
testing with BB and MB by placing obstructions to be removed, and testing for fluid leakage
should also be done by flushing and removing liquid to demonstrate if BL and BAL biopsies can
be incorporated into training with this phantom since the caps were not verified to completely
seal the ends. Since EBNAB and TBNAB require puncturing with a needle, this should also be
taken into consideration with improving the design of the phantom.
Improving segmentation and modelling
Segmentation of the airways from the CT scans and adding features on the model were a te­
dious and time­consuming process. Due to technical issues and cost constraints, we were not
able to use better existing segmentation software and had to semi­manually segment the air­
ways using a basic medical imaging software. Manually adding features individually, such as
the hyaline cartilage rings and the standardized cylindrical segment ends, were repetitive tasks
that took time. Improving the efficiency of these processes would allow more time for patient­ or
scenario­specific models for training and may also ensure consistency within the phantom. This
process could possibly be automated using Python combined with Blender, thus a script would
automatically cut off the ends, add cartilage rings, and create standardized segment ends. This
would be beneficial for producing more realistic phantoms from other patient data for training.
Improving image recognition
Refining the data: The most simple and efficient way to improve data quality is by conducting
post­processing on the images and removing the data labeled as wall so that the the model

==PAGE:56==
will be less biased towards predicting as wall. Furthermore, it might be sufficient to create the
shapes on the caps into black and white, thus, the model does not have to compare colors.
Synchronise the classification model and manual logging: Further investigations could be
conducted if it is possible to incorporate the classification model together with the manual logging
video program that was used in section 5.3 and, thus, increase the accuracy for Carlene.
QR codes: QR codes can be explored again with the test setup with new printed QR codes on
the caps, form 3D lab Denmark and post­image processing the images.
CNN: As mentioned in subsubsection 3.2.3, a purely CNN system could be used as a recog­
nition system, thus, markers do not to be used. However, this would be very time­consuming
to develop for the scope of this project. Furthermore, AMBU has been working on using only
neural network to recognize the location of a segment in an airway. However, their algorithm
in its current state can only be used on the same standard phantom. It could be possible to
explore the CNN method for recognition with our realistic phantom.
Other methods for detection
Security alarms: Another interesting method that could be explored is the technology used
in security alarms in retail stores. Small sensors that transmit the electrical signal and a small
electronic article surveillance (EAS) antenna could be incorporated on the phantom. Each alarm
has its own unique code and, thus, it would be possible to distinguish each segment.
Laser and sensors: Another solution for providing feedback to the operator could involve a
future technology involving micro laser measurements and micro motion sensors, which have
not yet been invented. The laser would be able to measure the distance at any given time to
the phantom walls and, thus, indicate if the operator hits the walls. However, the laser could
interfere with the phantom’s realism, thus, it would be beneficial to match the color of the laser
and the phantom to the same. The micro motion sensors would be able to work through Blue­
tooth or wireless WiFi, thus enabling a smooth surface and recognition.
User interface
Set up and network server or produce an app that makes the user interface experience more
user­friendly. Furthermore, the user display output at streamlit could be illustrated more user
friendly, by illustrating all images of the segment and coloring a frame around each image. Red
appear around the image if the segment has not been detected in the right order and green if it
has been detected in the right order.
Task analysis user study
A complete task analysis study should be performed for the entire process, from setting up the
laptop for recording until getting the evaluation results, in order evaluate the ease­of­use of the
device.

Detecting sub­segments
Sub­segments branch out of the 19 bronchial segments so training for additional identification
would be beneficial for bronchoscopists. The standard phantoms and our realistic phantom do
not include these sub­segments and, thus, training for sub­segment detection can only be done
in VR. Incorporating detection of sub­segments would allow for a more in­depth training for the
course participant at CAMES and other training programs. As the airways differ from patient to
patient, improving the segmentation and modeling would allow better extraction of the airways
from medical images for patient­specific training. Alternatively, since the design of the phantom
in this project have standardized ends, sub­segments can also be designed to attach without
having to design an entirely new phantom.
Material compatible with EBUS
With the increasing use of the EBUS bronchoscope, a material that is compatible with ultra­
sound could be considered so that bronchoscopists can also train with this technology with a
phantom simulation.
Environmental impact
It is important, when designing a product, to consider sustainability and its environmental im­
pact. Since it is planned to create more phantoms in the future, it would be beneficial to consider
how the chosen material can be reused or recycled or to consider other materials with less neg­
ative environmental impact.

C
Creating the hyaline cartilage rings
Because the segmented airway is an irregular shape, the standard shapes offered in Blender
could not be used to create the tracheal rings, thus, the rings had to be created manually.
The step­by­step process of creating the hyaline cartilage rings in Blender are illustrated in
Figure C.1 and described corresponding with the images. These steps should be done before
adding thickness (solidifying) the airway.
Figure C.1: Process for creating tracheal rings
(a) Create a 2 mm thick cylinder. Place the cylinder on the part of the trachea or bronchus
where the ring is to be located and rotate as needed so that it follows the curvature of the
airway.
(b) Select the cylinder and add the modifier ”Boolean”. Select ”Intersect” and select the airway
as the ”Object” to get an outline of the airway for that specific location. After completing this
step, the ring is specific to this part and cannot be moved.
Steps (a) and (b) are repeated with a 4 mm thick cylinder, placed at exact the same location
with the same rotational values as the 2mm, prior to proceeding with the next steps.
(c) Decrease the size of the 4 mm outline in the x­ and y­axes slightly (approximately 95%
scale) until the 2 mm outline just overlaps the 4 mm outline.
(d) Move the 4 mmm outline posteriorly until the posterior of the 2 mm outline disappears.
Selecting first the 4 mm and then the 2 mm, use the Boolean tool ”Difference” to create the
C­shaped ring on the anterior and lateral sides of the airway. The ring cannot be modified
after this step.
(e) Steps (a) through (d) creates a single cartilage ring.

Standardizing the bronchial ends
Figure D.1 illustrates the process to standardize the bronchial end, the following steps describes
the process corresponding to the images. These steps should be done before adding thickness
(solidifying) the airway.
Figure D.1: Process for standardizing the bronchial ends
(a) Create a mesh plane, approximately 10 mm by 10 mm, enough so that it overlaps the
cross sections of the segment end to be cut off. Add the modifier ”Solidify” and change the
thickness to 0.001. Place the plane at the spot where the segment is to be cut off.
(b) Select the airway and add the modifier ”Boolean”. Select ”Difference” and select the plane
as the ”Object”. In ”Edit Mode”, select the faces of the segment to be removed, press ”P”,
and separate by ”Selection”. Hide the segment end for the remainder of this process.
(c) Create a cylinder with a diameter of 6.5 mm and length of 5 mm. Align the cylinder to the
segment end, ensuring that it is centered and is as close as possible without overlapping.
(d) Select the airway and add the modifier ”Boolean”. Select ”Union” and select the cylinder as
the ”Object”.
(e) In ”Edit Mode”, create a face for the inner part of the cylinder by selecting the all edges of
inner part and pressing ”F”.
(f) Select both faces of the cylinder and the end of the segment. Press ”F” to connect them.

Introduction to bronchoscopy
On 7 February 2023, we attended an introduction in bronchoscopy course, which is the first
part of the CAMES training program, taught by pulmunologist PFC, who talked about the bron­
choscopy procedure and demonstrated the use of the equipment, VR training, and the phantom.
Comments from PFC:
• No consensus on how many bronchial segments, usually 18­20.
• No feedback for phantom training, unlike VR.
• VR does not use the real bronchoscope or tools (forceps, brush, scissors, etc.). VR uses
tools that resemble the real thing but they is specially made for VR.
• Phantom biopsy training is dropping a bead (random location) and finding and extracting
it. It would be nice to do other types of biopsy training with our phantom .
• Standard phantom too stiff.
• Phantom training can only focus on main bronchial segments, while VR can go into some
subsegments (e.g., L6a, L6b, etc.).
Comparison of carina sample materials
LK, 28 April 2023
• A95 resembles the cartilage stiffness, A50 resembles the non­trachea parts.
• A70 can be used if only printing with a single material. The rings are still stiff enough
that there will be ”bumps” when moving the bronchoscope along while the airway is still
flexible.
• A95 can be used for training to suture the tracheal cartilage. It would be nice to have
trachea phantoms with this stiffness for training.
PFC, 1 May 2023
• A95 is similar to cartilage and A50 is similar to the other parts between the cartilage
• A70 is a good compromise as it is in the middle. Still has some flexibility so it can still
move a little bit with the tip of the bronchoscope – studies with endoscopes have shown
that the esophagus can move a little bit if pressed but no studies for airways yet.
