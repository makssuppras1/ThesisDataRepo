Abstract 
During the last years the intensity of infections (INF) with the parasitic nematode liver worm 
Contracaecum osculatum have increased in the Eastern Baltic cod (EBC). The increase of INF with 
C. osculatum is related to the current increase of its final host which is the grey seal Halichoerus 
grypus. The EBC are currently suffering from heavy infections and the safety of the EBC liver is 
continuously threatened by several stressors. The aim of this study was to 1) identify the intensity 
of the infection of C. osculatum in livers of EBC, 2) evaluate any possible alterations of the liver 
tissue of EBC caused by C. osculatum and 3) discuss how C. osculatum may affect the function of 
the cod liver and hereby the condition of the EBC. 
In this study histological examination was used to evaluate pathological lesions caused by C. 
osculatum in EBC liver tissues. These findings show that C. osculatum may compromise the 
physiological function of the liver of EBC. The livers of EBC were found heavily infected by the C. 
osculatum third stage larvae, and INF was found significantly to be related to the intensity of the 
inflammation in the liver caused by C. osculatum. Interestingly, another worm species was found 
in the bile ducts of EBC livers and EBC with the highest INF of C. osculatum were also found to 
have significant more bile duct worms compared to EBC with low INF of C. osculatum. The fat 
vacuole patterns were found irrelevant with INF of C. osculatum and probably more related to 
the nutritional status of EBC. This statement was supported by a clear loss of fats in non-infected 
wild EBC liver tissues in comparison with healthy cod from the Blue Planet. Moreover, the 
continually increase in the INF of C. osculatum, the current deteriorating oxygen conditions and 
the reduction and changes in prey availability for EBC may be some of the answers for the 
currently poor nutritional condition of the EBC and increased natural mortality.

1. Introduction 
Contracaecum osculatum nematodes are also known with the name liver worms (Zuo et al., 
2017). C. osculatum is very widespread in marine environments and individuals occur fx. both as 
adults in seals and as the third-stage larvae in crustaceans and fish species (Køie and Fagerholm 
1995; Zuo et al., 2016; Nadolna et al., 2017). In the Baltic Sea area, the population of the grey 
seal Halichoerus grypus has dramatically increased counting more than 30000 individuals since 
2000s (ICES WGMME 2018) The last years H. grypus are found heavily infected with the C. 
osculatum larvae in the Baltic Sea (Lunneryd et al., 2015) and functioning as the final host of C. 
osculatum (Köie and Fagerholm 1995; Nadolna et al., 2018).  
During the last decades lots of studies have focused on the relationship between the H. grypus 
presence and numbers of infected Eastern Baltic Sea cod (EBC) with C. osculatum (Haarder et al., 
2014; Zuo et al., 2017; Zuo et al., 2018). This is due to increase in infections of EBC with third-
stage C. osculatum larvae where the highest prevalence and intensity are found in the Eastern 
Baltic Sea region (Mehrdana et al., 2014; Horbowy et al., 2016; Sokolova et al., 2018).  EBC is the 
only documented paratenic host in the Baltic region (Köie and Fagerholm 1995).   
EBC gets infected when eating other infected prey items such as sprat sprattus sprattus which is 
the most common transport host of C. osculatum (Zuo et al., 2016; 2017). Intensity of infections 
increases with length of the fish revealing that number of C. osculatum individuals accumulate 
over time in EBC (Horbowy et al., 2016; Zuo et al., 2016). The larvae of C.osculatum end up being 
encapsulated in the liver of EBC resulting in possible organ tissue and cell alterations (Buchmann 
and Mehrdana 2016; Zuo et al., 2018).  The purpose of the encapsulation is a usual reaction of 
the host to isolate and eliminate the larvae from the liver tissue (Buchmann 2012). Recent studies 
revealed inflammatory reactions (elicit of macrophage) around of the encapsulated C.osculatum 
larvae in the liver tissue of the EBC (Zuo et al., 2017). 
In addition to these alterations there is a hypothesis that the presence of C. osculatum in cod 
liver  in combination with, deteriorating oxygen conditions in the Baltic Sea and changes in prey 
items may negatively affect the physiology and health of EBC (Eero et al., 2015; Horbowy et al., 
2016; Sokolova et al., 2018). The EBC stock has been under dramatic changes in the last decades 
and individual nutritional status has decreased and cod above 50 cm are rare to find (Eero et al., 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
2015). In general, like in mammals, the liver in teleosts is a multifunctional organ and it has a 
major role in detoxification and in metabolism of fats and carbohydrates (Bruslé and Anadon 
1996). Consequently, any possible malfunction of the liver might have a direct influence in the 
survival of the cod (Horbowy et al., 2016). Furthermore, raw caned liver is considered to be an 
important dish of many northern European countries’ traditional cuisine, including Denmark 
(Nadolna and Podolska 2014). Subsequently, cod and its products such as cod liver oil have a 
large economically value on the fisheries market in north Europe (Nadolna et al., 2014). However, 
due to the recent increase in the intensity of EBC liver infections there are worries regarding the 
suitability of these products for human consumption (Buchmann and Mehrdana 2016).   
Even though there are several studies regarding the infection of EBC liver with C. osculatum 
(Nadolna et al., 2014; Sokolova et al., 2018; Zuo et al., 2018), it remains uncertain how and at 
what level C. osculatum may affect the health condition of the individual cod. In the present study 
using histological examinations I investigated how C. osculatum affects the liver tissue of EBC in 
relation to different infection intensities. Based on my findings, I consequently evaluated a 
scoring system based on relevant and possible criteria for the observed changes in the liver.

2. Background  
2.1 Histopathology of fish parasitic infections   
Parasitic infections are very common in fish, specifically in wild fish populations which inhabit 
areas where environmental criteria are met for intermediate hosts and transmission of parasites 
(Feist and Longshaw 2008). The diversity of parasites is unique, having evolved through ancient 
fish hosts since the Devonian period c (Feist and Longshaw 2008) approximately four hundred 
million years ago. It is important to be familiar with the appropriate molecular techniques as well 
as the life cycle, morphological and anatomical characteristics in order to complete an 
identification of the parasites’ taxonomic group (Rohde 2005; Woo 2006).   
Pathology is a crucial diagnostic specialty which relies heavily on the pathologists’ expertise, the 
quality of the reference materials as well as the accurate description of clinical signs and 
histopathological characteristics (Feist and Longshaw 2008).  Even though histopathology is a 
descriptive diagnostic method it may offers great guidelines of prognostic values for a thorough 
diagnosis (Feist and Longshaw 2008). Therefore, it provides an opportunity to assess how 
individual fish as well as whole populations can be affected by parasite infections (Lester 1984). 
Some of the most common histopathological lesions that are regularly observed by parasites 
might be acute inflammation or chronic inflammation and degenerative changes (as for instance 
necrosis) in the tissue vessels (Feist and Longshaw 2008).   
The immune system of every host responds differently to the various stages of the parasite, and 
the cellular response typically tries to isolate and eliminate the specific parasite. A mixture of 
effective parasite replications and extreme host reactions are often the most severe pathologies 
(Feist and Longshaw 2008).  
It has been investigated (Ferguson and Needham 1978; Feist 1997) that prior exposure to a 
specific parasite can produce a degree of immunity. Although the same infection can develop 
again, this decreases the symptoms and tissue responses. Thus, it is crucial to be able to know a 
specific samples’ previous infections but, although this could be feasible for aquaculture species, 
it is virtually impossible for fish in the wild (Feist and Longshaw 2008).  
Nonetheless, for most of the parasite-host interactions, mortality is hard to predict, as it is not 
rare to see fish with severe infections that they do not show any visible symptoms (Feist and 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
Longshaw 2008). The pathological alteration diagnosis is just one factor of assessing the impact 
of parasitic infections on an individual level. It is important before assigning parasites as driving 
forces for population structure that the complexity of the effect of sublethal infections on the 
host must be considered (Feist and Longshaw 2008).  
Subsequently, a multi-disciplinary study involving scientists from a variety of different fields of 
expertise is required in order to thoroughly evaluate the role that the diseases have on fish 
populations (Feist and Longshaw 2008). It has been proposed  that the parasites suspected of 
affecting the host survival, fish age, and size are some of the factors that should be assessed and 
determined in order to find a link between parasitism and its pathology and effect on population 
level (Feist and Longshaw 2008). 
 
2.2 Life cycle of C. Osculatum 
C.  osculatum belongs to family Anisakidae (book). 
The life cycle of C. Osculatum includes eggs, four stages of larvae and one adult stage. It has 
several intermediate and transport hosts and one documented paratenic host (figure 1) until it 
reaches its final host which is H. grypus (Köie et al., 1995; McClelland et al. 1990). Initially, 
fertilized free eggs of C. Osculatum are introduced in the sea waters through the seal faeces (Köie 
et al., 1995). If the egg moults two times inside the egg a third stage larva (L3) is produced and 
liberated into the sea afterwards (Köie et al., 1995; Højgaard 1998; McClelland 2005). If the egg 
moults one time inside the egg the larva becomes a second stage larva (L2). Both L2 and L3 stage 
larvae can be found in crustaceans and use them as intermediate hosts. 
According to Köie and Fagerholm’s (1995) laboratory studies, crustaceans like nauplius, calanoid 
and Cirripedia larvae are likely demonstrated to have a major role in C. Osculatum’s life cycle 
functioning as intermediate hosts. They are the main food source of small fish, which get infected 
by eating the infected crustacean (Köie et al., 1995). Several studies have proved the presence of 
third stage C. Osculatum larvae in the liver of several Baltic Sea fish species (Zuo et al., 2016). 
Some of the most common transport hosts of C. osculatum are S. sprattus, Hyperoplus 
lanceolatus, Clupea harengus, Pleuronectes platessa (Köie et al., 1995, Nadolna et al., 2018).  
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
Initially, all these species are included in the diet of EBC (Pachur and Horbowy 2013). Therefore, 
by feeding on them EBC are getting infected with the third stage larvae and acts as the only 
documented paratenic host in the life cycle of C. osculatum (Köie et al., 1995; Zuo et al., 2018). 
Because of the predominant intensity of C. Osculatum in Baltic cod livers the parasite is also 
known with the name “liver worm”, however it has been also observed in other organs too 
(Mehrdana et al., 2014). After eating the infected EBC, H. grypus will become the final host, in 
which C. osculatum will complete its last two molts and mature into adult into the seal’s stomach 
(Köie et al., 1995). However, according to Valtonen et al. (1988) most probably only third stage 
larvae longer than 4-5 mm will be able to survive and mature in the final host. Several studies 
have shown high intensity of four larvae stage and adult specimens located in the H. grypus 
stomach (Lunneryd et al., 2015, Zuo et al, 2018).

2.3 Presence of C. Osculatum in cod liver 
In his recent study about host size and infection dynamics of C. Osculatum (Zuo et al., 2016 
demonstrated that there was a slight or no presence of C. osculatum specimens in cod with 
length size lower than 30 cm. However, individuals with length up to 40cm were found highly 
infected most probably because of their different diet preferences (sprat, herrings etc.). 
In the period 2011 to 2014, there was a substantial increase in the prevalence and the intensity 
of C. osculatum (Haarder et al., 2014). The degree of the infections tend to increase with the 
length of the cod for up to 60 cm. Though, followed by a decrease after that point indicating a 
decrease in the survival rate of large and heavily infected cod (Horbowy et al., 2016).  In contrast 
with the 1987-1993 year period in which only 4% of the fish were found to be infected with C. 
Osculatum, Nadolna and Podeska (2014) recorded significant increases in the prevalence of C. 
Osculatum infections in the southern Baltic cod which were tested in 2011 with 11% occurrence. 
More studies suggested that the prevalence of C. Osculatum infections in cod sampled east of 
Bornholm has increased significantly to around 100% (Haarder et al., 2014; Mehrdana et al., 
2014). Mehrdana et al. (2014) and Sokolova et al. (2018) also recorded that the largest prevalence 
in C. Osculatum infections in EBC were found in the eastern Bornholm region close to H. grypus 
colonies. The intensified infection of cod livers by C. Osculatum parasites was also proposed as a 
consequence of the effective re-introduction of H. grypus in the Baltic sea region (Buchmann and 
Kania 2012; Haarder et al., 2014).  C. osculatum in their third stage are semi-transparent to grey 
in color and are approximately 2-3cm in length and 1mm or less in width and only a portion of 
the worm is clear in EBC  livers because the rest of the parasite is embedded in the tissue under 
the liver surface (Buchmann and Mehrdana 2016). The worm can stay in an inactivated state for 
more than 3 years inside the liver, since its larva are encapsulated in the dense cellular layers of 
the host (Buchmann and Mehrdana 2016).  
 
2.4 Effects of C. osculatum in fish 
Serious inflammatory reaction with  tissue deformity, which is at first seen as attracting 
inflammatory cells and partly encapsulating the worm in the liver of the cod, are presumed to 
affect the normal function of such species merely because larvae are present in tissues 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
(Buchmann Mehrdana 2016). Therefore, many parasites are found on the surface of the organ in 
an encapsulated state which can be clearly seen when parasites are extracted by peeling off the 
cod’s liver surface layer. The physiological conditions, health and survival state of the host can be 
affected by C. osculatum presence (Buchmann and Mehrdana 2016). 
 In Antarctic icefishes with high loads of C. osculatum larvae, decreased body weight 
measurements have been reported suggesting a host effect caused by the parasite (Santoro et 
al., 2013). The cod in the Southern Baltic region might already be infected with hundreds third 
stage C. osculatum larvae (Mehrdana et al., 2014; Haarder et al., 2014; Nadolna and Podolska, 
2014), which can in turn greatly influence the integrity and function of the cod’s organs as well 
as disturb its growth (Mehrdana et al., 2014; Zuo et al., 2016). Even in well-nourished cods, 
despite that the tissue disorders from C. osculatum are substantial, the effects become more 
pronounced when the liver has decreased in size (Buchmann and Mehrdana 2016).  
Shulman’s and Petrushevski’s study (1955) of C. osculatum infection in EBC in the periods of 
1940s and 1950s had already recommended a negative association between the parasite and the 
fish. Nonetheless, the possible parasite effects on the health status of the cod livers are difficult 
to separate from more manageable factors like food availability (Buchmann and Børresen 1988). 
In order to evaluate and prove the exact effect of the parasite, extensive laboratory tests are 
needed (Buchmann and Mehrdana 2016).  
 
 
2.5 The nutritional condition of Baltic cod 
It is a known situation that since the 1990s the nutritional status of the adult EBC has been 
decreasing (Eero et al., 2015). The suspected reasons for this situation are the poor fish prey 
abundance in the main region of cods (Eero et al., 2012b), the benthic prey deficit due to the 
oxygen depletion at the bottom of the sea (Carstensen et al., 2014), the increasing regions that 
suffer from low oxygen aquatic environments that can affect the growth of cod (Casini et al., 
2016), and the growing situation with parasite infections (Buchmann and Kania 2012; Haarder et 
al., 2014; Mehrdana et al., 2014; Nadolna and Podolska 2014). The number of natural predators 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
for cod in the Baltic sea, as the H. grypus for instance, over the last decades has been relatively 
low (Horbowy et al., 2016). From the early 2000s, the number of H. grypus has almost tripled, 
estimated to approximately 28 thousand individuals in 2012 for the entire region of the Baltic 
(Harkonen et al., 2013).  
It is frequent that aquatic ecosystems and fish stocks suffer major changes over the years, thus 
the recent developments in cod in this regard are not an exception (Horbowy et al., 2016). For 
instance, before the collapse of the cod stocks in Labrador and Newfoundland in Canada there 
were also reports of increased parasite infestations likely due to seal population (Brattey et al., 
1990). 
 
2.6 The Liver of teleost 
Being the largest gland in the body, the liver provides several complex functions similar to 
mammals. The roles involve nutritional assimilation, detoxification, bile development, metabolic 
homeostasis which involves synthesis of plasma proteins, carbohydrates, lipids and vitamin 
storage (Genten 2009). Portal vein and hepatic artery are the main sources which provide blood 
to the liver (Wallig et al., 2017). The hepatic artery provides oxygen and energy, whereas the 
portal veins are responsible for several metabolic processes or elimination of substances 
consumed by the gastrointestinal tract (Wallig et al., 2017).  
In general, the liver has a lobular structure identical in all animal species (Roberts 2012).  The 
main cell type in the liver are hepatocytes (60% of the hepatic cells), shaped as cords, which 
separate the sinusoids with the biliary (canaliculi) network (Eurell and Haensly 1982; Bombonato 
et al., 2007).  Hepatocytes have polygonal shape, they are located in the liver parenchyma and 
are usually loaded with fats and glycogen. When the diet is less than optimal the cells diminish 
and the liver is loaded with yellow ceroid pigments (Genten 2009,). Hepatocytes are highly 
important for the function of the liver as they are mainly responsible for the urea cycle, the 
metabolism and for the bile formation (Genten 2009; Wallig et al., 2017). However, all cell types 
are equally important for the smooth function of the liver and take part in toxicological responses 
(Wallig et al., 2017).  
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
Bile ducts also arise in the parenchyma of the liver occurring between neighboring hepatocytes, 
where bile canaliculi is excreted into the bile network (canaliculi), (Aughey and Frye 2001). The 
bile duct is filled with hepatic plasma membranes and from there it flows to a small bile duct in 
the portal area. In the portal areas small sized bile ducts are lined with cuboidal epithelium while 
the large ones with columnar epithelium (Aughey and Frye 2001). 
In fish liver sinusoids are less frequent, they are randomly distributed among the hepatocytes 
and they are surrounded by endothelium cells with a very discrete central nucleus (Roberts 
2012). In addition, Macrophagic Kuppfer cells are likely not present in teleost’s livers, while 
according to Hinton et al. (1976) there are assumptions based mostly on morphological 
characteristics that cells similar to Kuppfer might be present in some teleost livers (Varichak 
1938).  
 
3. Aim 
The aim of the present study was to 1) identify the intensity of the infection of the livers, 2) use 
histology to evaluate any possible alterations of the liver tissue of EBC caused by C. osculatum 
and 3) discuss how C. osculatum may affect the function of the cod liver and hereby the condition 
of the EBC.

4. Materials and Methods 
 
4.1 Sampling  
 
For this study, the total number of 69 Eastern Baltic cod (EBC) fish were caught by trawl east of 
Bornholm, close to the harbour of Nexø in October 2018 (Fig. 2). For the histological analysis 30 
EBC out of the 69 were chosen based on examinations of the liver surface to estimate the 
approximate numbers of nematodes in order to get a high variation in infection intensity. It is 
known that infection intensity is positively correlated with the fish length in the eastern Baltic 
Sea area size range of the 30 EBC was therefore 32 to 51 cm total length [TL] (Horbowy et al., 
2016). 
 
Fig.2. ICES Subdivisions (SD) in the Baltic Sea. The sampling took place in SD 25 which corresponds to the 
eastern Baltic cod. 
 
While on board on the ship, EBC were euthanized, and while the fish were freshly dead the livers 
were removed to collect three to four pieces of the liver tissue from three different sites of the 
liver (middle-top & two edges) (Fig.3A). Different sites were chosen for a more accurate 
estimation of the infection in the whole liver. Moreover, samples were collected from the top for

immediately put in tubes with formalin for later histological analysis. Moreover, general

for subsequent laboratory analysis to estimate the numbers of nematodes in the livers. The same 
procedure as above was carried out for three cod delivered by the Blue Planet. These cod were 
in optimal nutritional condition and had been at the Blue Planet since they were very small. They 
were therefore used as control for comparison between a healthy cod and EBC.

4.3 Histopathological Preparations 
For the histopathological analysis the liver tissue samples were fixed for several months in 10% 
neutral buffered formalin. In this way, the formalin by penetrating the liver tissue will cause 
chemical reactions that will harden, maintain and protect the tissue during the later processes, 
and prevent degradation. After fixation the tissue samples were trimmed and placed in cassettes 
and were then placed in a Leica Peloris™ rapid tissue processor for conventional processing to 
paraffin wax.   
The end goal of the processing was to embed the tissue in paraffin wax, but because the paraffin 
wax was considered a very hydrophobic substance, the removal of most of the water was 
necessary to avoid any infiltration with the wax. This was achieved by gradually increasing the 
ethanol concentration until all water was replaced by ethanol. From absolute ethanol the 
samples were transferred to a so called clearing medium because ethanol was also considered 
highly incompatible with wax (Mumford 2007). The tissue was cleared with xylene to create a 
compatible matrix to support the penetration of paraffin into the tissue.  
Therefore, the tissue samples were infiltrated with paraffin wax. The last step was to embed the 
tissue samples into paraffin blocks, where the tissues were carefully placed in a mold and filled 
with melted wax. The molds were cooled with the cassette as lid and the paraffin wax tended to 
be cooled.   
Once cooled, the tissue blocks were placed in the microtome machine and carefully sliced into 
floating tissue ribbons at 3μm (Fig 5A). Immediately the sections were put on the glass slides 
using albumin as agglutinant. Thereafter, all the glass slides were stained with Hematoxylin and 
Eosin (H&E) for the evaluation of the tissue structural features. In addition, an extra set of slides 
were also stained with Picro-sirius Red staining to examine the collagen thickness of the tissue 
sections. Moreover, 3 H&E and Picro-sirius Red stained tissue samples taken from healthy fish 
from the Bl å Planet aquarium (Copenhagen), were used as controls. For the prevention of biases 
and a more accurate approach all the initial ID of the slides were covered (blinded). When bliding 
the slides, a random number where given to the slides and the fish number were hidden for the 
examiner until all examination for histology where done (Fig. 5B). The examination of the slides 
was done with a Leica microscope at 50x, 100x, 250x and 400x magnification.

addition, inflammatory reactions unrelated to the nematode’s larvae were also examined. A total 
number of 30 Pico-sirus stained histology glass-slides containing 3 to 4 liver tissue sections each, 
were also investigated for the collagen’s condition of the liver surface.

4.5 Criteria for the inflammation categories 
The evaluation of the scaling for the inflammation was based on any presence of pathological 
lesions as well as their frequency in the cod liver tissues, both lesions related and unrelated to C. 
osculatum larvae. C osculatum related lesions defined as any alterations in the liver tissues with 
a visible C. osculatum larvae and lesions next to the larvae, like any bleedings in the tissue or 
tunnel formations with tissue debris close to the C. osculatum.  
Lesions were considered unrelated to C. osculatum if they were found far away from the larvae 
or in areas with no presence of the C. osculatum larvae. These lesions could be either because of 
a microbial infection or due to other parasites. The inflammation categorization was assigned 
each slide was based on the presence of perivacuolar and periductular inflammation as well as 
the size (large/small) and the frequency of granulomas in the liver tissue. The inflammation for 
each slide was grated based on:  
Category 1: No inflammatory reactions in the liver tissue. 
Category 2: Slight inflammatory reaction, either with foci of perivacuolar/ periductular 
inflammation or the formation of one to two small enclosed granulomas (Fig. 6A). 
Category 3: Inflammatory reactions with presence of few small granulomas, and macrophages 
aggregations (Fig.6B). 
Category 4: Multiple focal inflammatory reactions with large granulomas formation and many 
macrophages aggregations (Fig.6C). 
Category 5: Severe disseminated inflammation with formation of multiple large granulomas 
connected by macrophages aggregations and lymphocytic reaction around the vessels and bile 
ducts (Fig.6D).

4.8 Statistics and data handling 
For all the samples the infection intensity (INF) which describes the number of C. osculatum per 
gram of liver tissue, was calculated and used in the statistical analysis. This was done to account 
for any differences in liver size as a small liver is considered to be more affected in functioning 
compared to a large one. By applying the Kruskal Wallis statistical test I tested how INF changed 
with inflammation, vacuoles patterns and bile duct worm groups. 
For the calculation of the INF the corrected liver weight was used to account for the mass of the 
nematodes and estimated by the equation: 
                  Corrected liver weight = total liver weight −sum of weight of the nematodes  
Where large nematodes were assigned 0.009 gram per nematode and small nematodes were 
assigned 0.004 g per nematode (pers. Communication M. P. Ryberg). From here INF could be 
calculated by the formula: 
Infection intensity (INF) = Total number of nematodes
Corrected liver weight
 
For the statistical process of the data R version 3.5.1 (2018-07-02) in R studio (Version 1.1.456) R 
Core Team (2018) was chosen. The statistical analysis was implemented by using a Kruskal Wallis 
test, which is a rank-based test and it can be applied to one-way data with more than two groups. 
The test describes the likelihood of an observation in one group to be higher than the others 
(Kruskal and Wallis, 1952). Differences were considered statistically significant at P-values < 0.05. 
For the determination of statistical differences among the groups a Dunn test using rank sums 
was applied (Dunn 1964).,

5. Results 
Nematodes were observed in 27 out of the 30 livers and a total of 960 nematodes were collected 
from these. All nematodes were identified as C. osculatum species during the microscopic 
examination of the cephalic and caudal shape-ends (Fagerholm 1982). In addition, a total number 
of 13 cod were found heavily infected by larvae bile duct worms. 
  
5.1 Microscopic analysis 
5.1.1 Effects of C. osculatum in the liver   
Specimens of the third stage larvae of C. osculatum were detected encapsulated by a granuloma 
formation in the cod liver parenchyma (Fig.9A). In the center of the granuloma a free space 
around the larvae were seen, in this space extensive tissue damage and debris from tissue cells 
were also found. In the histological slides there was often recorded several tunnels with debris 
(Fig.9B). These lesions were most likely related to the larvae movement in the liver tissue. Severe 
focal inflammation with the formation of multiple peripheral granulomas connected with 
macrophages aggregations to each other were also found (Fig.9C). Moreover, a case of a likely 
dilation of a vein with congestion was observed, presumably due to compression of the blood 
flow in the vein by the neighboring larvae (Fig89.D). However, it could also be a result of a huge 
tunnel formation with haemorrhage in the tissue. Moreover dilated bile ducts including bile duct 
worms and inflammation reaction between them (Fig.9E).The collagen of the liver capsule was also 
examined for any possible damages especially close to the C. osculatum larvae and were 
undamaged in all sections and with normal thickness (Fig9.F).  
 
5.1.2 Inflammation intensity 
The larvae were encapsulated in the tissue and was surrounded by numerous macrophage 
agglomerates (MA). Large tracks of tunnels with cellular debris and blood and damaged vessels 
filled with blood cells were also spotted.  Severe focal inflammation with the formation of 
multiple peripheral granulomas connected with macrophages aggregations to each other were 
also found.

6. Discussion  
In summary, this study showed that infections with C. osculatum caused lesions and damage to 
the liver tissue of EBC. This was reflected by damages in the liver tissue caused by the specimens 
of C. osculatum that were found cellular encapsulated by a granuloma formation, large tracks of 
tunnels with tissue cellular debris and hemorrhages, as well as damaged vessels. Furthermore, 
significant differences between infection intensity (INF) of C. osculatum and intensity of 
inflammation in the liver was found. The intensity of inflammation increased with INF as the most 
infected EBC were found in the category 5 group. No significant changes were found in the 
vacuole pattern in relation to INF with C. osculatum. Very interestingly, I found another parasite 
in the bile ducts of the liver. It was not possible to identify the parasite, but it was most likely a 
trematode or mixozoan. The intensity of these bile duct worms increased significantly with 
increase in INF of C. osculatum. Prevalence of C. osculatum in the livers was 90% with mean 
intensity 35.5 ± 7.0 of C. osculatum per EBC. 
 
6.1 Histological analysis 
The histological results of this study are in agreement with the notion that C. osculatum may 
cause lesions in the liver tissue of EBC. In my study the inflammatory reactions around the 
infection area were characterized from slight to severe and had a major role for the 
categorization of the inflammation. In support of this, Zuo (2017) showed that the encapsulated 
C. osculatum caused inflammation with macrophage and fibroblast-like cells released in the EBC 
liver tissue. Reactions in fish are very usual in the parasite-host interactions and have recently 
been recorded both for C. osculatum and for other parasites (Buchman 2012). Elarifi (1982) 
investigated the liver tissues of the whiting Merlangius merlangus and found them heavily 
infected by Contracaecum spp. larvae. In the liver parenchyma he found lesions in the tissues 
from the encapsulated larvae such as destroyed liver vessels, tissue cells and inflammatory 
reactions with fibroblasts and macrophages.  
The liver fatty composition showed no significant differences between the vacuoles patterns and 
INF. Though, I found that the EBC livers had very low fat with micro-little vacuoles as the 
dominant pattern, indicating that EBC were starving.  The three control cod from the Blue Planet 
==PAGE:36==
35 
 
analyzed in my study showed that a healthy cod have a very fatty liver and that the dominant 
pattern of vacuoles is mainly big (macro) vacuole pattern with lots of large well-shaped fat 
vacuoles.  On the other hand, the 3 non-infected EBC did not show the same vacuole pattern as 
the cod from the Blue Planet, as they had small or no fat vacuoles . Subsequently, the vacuolation 
observed in all the control EBC is very unusual for the cod species (Fujita et al. 1986) and  this is 
most likely an indicator of low lipid accumulation in the liver. This means that the EBC currently 
are not in a good nutrition state and they are probably starving (if time try to find a reference for 
changes in fat vacuoles vs starvation in fish). 
Cod are wild fish and may carry several parasitic species or other microbial/ bacterial related 
diseases that may cause alterations to its liver (Perdiguero-Alonso et al. 2008). Besides of the C. 
osculatum, few tissue sections were also spotted with lesions most likely caused by bacterial 
infections, while interestingly bile duct worms were also spotted in 13 liver tissues. High 
significant differences between the INF and the bile duct worms were recorded. When INF in EBC 
was high they tended to contain more bile duct works compared to livers with low INF as they 
were assigned none or few bile duct worms. Bile duct dilation and inflammatory reaction around 
the infected bile ducts was observed most likely due to the presence of the larvae inside them. 
The information regarding bile duct parasites in the cod liver is very limited and even though 
there is the notion that the bile duct worms might be related to trematode or myxozoan species, 
further investigation is required for the identification and the effects of this bile duct parasite. In 
a recent study Borucinka et al. (2017) found the presence of myxozoa in the bile ducts of the 
southern Baltic cod, and lesions like biliary hyperplasia, biliary fibrosis and slight hypatocellular 
necrosis. The lesions and the morphology of the myxozoan were similar to the bile duct worm of 
my study. In addition, Hemmingsen & Mackenzie (2001) found several species of biliary myxozoa 
in the liver bile ducts of gadoid fish in the North Atlantic Sea and described the same lesions as 
Borucinka et al. (2017) and the lesions found in my study.

6.2 The effect of the liver function 
As the liver is responsible for many of the metabolic processes like carbohydrates, lipids and 
vitamin storage (Bruslè and Anadon 1996), C. osculatum may likely challenge both the function 
and the nutritional status of the infected EBC livers (Mehrdana et al., 2014; Zuo et al., 2016). A 
recent study carried out on EBC from the same area as cod used in my analysis showed that the 
liver lipid content and the albumin content were highly decreasing with the increase of INF (pers 
communication, M. P. Ryberg). Albumin is the main protein of blood plasma and controls the 
osmotic pressure of the blood (Culler and Lögdberg 2007). Subsequently, any decrease may 
indicate possible malfunction of the liver and result in poor health condition. In my study I found 
large tunnels with debris from hepatocytes and damaged veins and arteries in the liver tissues 
most likely caused by the movements of C. osculatum inside the tissue. Such lesions may have a 
negative effect on the metabolism and physiological function of the liver in EBC. The hypatocytes, 
are mostly responsible for the formation of the hypatic bile ducts as well as the metabolic 
procedures and urea (Genten 2009), while the hepatic arteries and veins are the main blood 
sources providing blood and oxygen to the liver and helping the metabolism. Borucinkka et al. 
(2017) found similar migration tunnels in the cod liver tissues, hemorages and hepatocellular 
necrosis likely related to migration tunnels of Anisakis larvae, suggesting that the cod were in a 
poor health condition. In a recent study, Marnis et al. (2019) showed that EBC liver infected by 
C. osculatum had different expressions of genes related to metabolism, growth and immune 
system compared to uninfected cod. Subsequently, infections of C. osculatum and other parasites 
may negatively affect the liver with multiple ways and as the liver is a critical organ for the energy 
storage, metabolism and the immune response it may compromise the survival of the host.   
6. 3 Individual nutritional condition of the cod - Link between population  
The cod collected in this study were suffering a lot from poor nutrition and from several infections 
in their livers both related to C. osculatum and unrelated. In a recent study Horbowy et al. (2016) 
showed that the infected EBC were in very bad condition compared to the non-infected EBC. 
Furthermore, the heavily infected cod were found in such poor health condition that recovery 
was very difficult.  
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
Decrease in condition of cod is related to increase in natural mortality (Dutil & Lambert 2000). 
The reasons that EBC individuals are found in poor condition and large EBC are gone are probably 
a combination of several factors. The increased natural mortality especially in large size EBC is 
expected to be related to some extent to the currently increased level of infections by C. 
osculatum in EBC (Swain & Benoît 2015; Horbowy et al., 2016). Larger EBC are more infected by 
parasites as C. osculatum accumulates in the liver over time (Zuo 2016; Horbowy et al., 2016) 
Besides of the increasing intensity infection with parasites, EBC are also suffering from low 
availability and changes in prey possible as a result of increase in hypoxic areas within the Baltic 
sea which also seems to explain the decreased condition of EBC (Eero et al., 2012b, Casini et al. 
2016, Reusch et al. 2018, Neuenfeldt et al. 2019). In summary, EBC is already suffering 
dramatically from lack of food availability and hypoxic environments, and the currently highly 
infections from C. osculatum and other parasites, may lead the population to even lower health 
condition and distress.

7. Conclusion 
In this study the infection of EBC by C. osculatum was examined by two different approaches. 
Firstly, by applying histological examination methods I investigated and noted all the pathological 
lesions that the C. osculatum cause to the infected liver tissues of EBC. Secondly, I proved 
statistically that there is a significance between the INF and the inflammation intensity caused by 
C. osculatum and between the INF and number ofbile duct worms. Summarizing the results, I 
here show that lesions caused by C. osculatum may have in combination with changes in prey 
items and increase in hypoxic wateran effect on the helath of EBC. Although my research agrees 
with previous studies, it is unknown to what extent C. osculatum may affect the function of the 
liver. For that reason, further investigations should be applied.
