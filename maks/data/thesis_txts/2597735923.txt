Abstract
Spectral albedo influence on bifacial photovoltaic front and rear side irradiance calcula-
tions came more to attention recently, with increasing prominence in bifacial technologies.
In this study, ground spectral albedo data for grass and white polymer material sheet were
recorded over a period of four months total with high resolution spectroradiometers placed
in Riso campus at Technical University of Denmark. Additionally, albedo of the same cov-
ers were measured using standard pyranometers and reference cells. This work confirms
the findings of many previous spectral albedo studies, that spectral albedo is not static,
but varies in time, based on many factors such as sun’s position in the sky, time of day,
humidity, presence of dew, aerosol loads, age and composition of cover etc.
A view factors modelling tool - PVfactors was expanded to include spectrally resolved
hourly diffuse horizontal, direct, and global horizontal irradiance values as well as spec-
trally resolved albedo. Using real spectral and spectrally flat albedo data, as well as
EQE measurements of common bifacial photovoltaics technologies such as IBC, n-PERT,
PERC, bifacial arrays were modelled and bifacial gains were calculated for different albedo
scenarios. Study concludes that error introduced to modelled bifacial gain values by ex-
cluding spectral albedo could be as high as 4% for the timeframes investigated. This
study also suggests some possible approaches to minimise the error. Study finds that er-
ror is dependant on factors such as PV array geometry, timeframe investigated, spectral
resolution and width of spectrum measured. Additionally, it was concluded that refer-
ence cells are better choice for albedo measurements than pyranometers as alternative
to spectroradiometer.
Finally, mismatch factors and spectral impact are calculated for the cases investigated in
this study, showing that spectral mismatch for backside surfaces for grass could be as
high as 20%.

Introduction
Bifacial cell (Bifi) technology inception reaches the very beginnings of the photovoltaics
(PV) technology as a whole, when IBC photovoltaic cells with an efficiency of 5% were
processed in Bell labs in 1954, with a bifacial character [1], [2]. However, in the years
following, as Bifi option was more costly, monofacial PV remained the dominant PV tech-
nology, while Bifi cells were employed in high-end applications such as the space industry.
As PV technology was growing and evolving, together with it, more competitive Bifi tech-
nology options were entering R&D departments of universities and companies in early
2000. It took another decade for the interest to ramp up further and reach the PV indus-
tries attention with a construction of a 1,25 MWp PV park in Japan by PVG Solutions in
2013 [2]. After this successful installation garnered approval of the technology’s feasibil-
ity, it inspired industry to diverge resources to conceptualise different type of photovoltaic
installations, and inspired many to conduct research into topic of Bifi PV technology.
Extensive research in Bifi PV showed that gains in energy yield might increase up to
30%, depending on the albedo of ground cover, geographic location, and installation’s
geometry [3]. With the rise of Bifi technology, came a need to predict the energy gen-
eration output capacity of the PV installation in question, so project revenues could be
estimated. Naturally as Bifi technology branches from the conventional PV technology,
many methods and models previously used are valid and useful for the modelling of Bifi.
Main defining Bifi difference from monofacial PV is the energy generation capability of the
rear PV surface. Therefore, to predict and model Bifi energy generation, rear side plane
of array irradiance (Rpoa) must be taken into account as well as factors effecting it.
One specific problem, for modelling and predicting Bifi PV output and performance is
ground albedo properties. Typically, for modelling purposes one would use a spectrally
averaged albedo for the ground reflections and therefore irradiance is assumed to be
reflected equally for all the light wavelengths. However, as the ground below a PV module
reflects light selectively, the spectrum of light reaching the PV module - particularly the rear
side - differs from that of the sun. Furthermore, PV cells are spectrally responsive devices
and their output varies with the relative shape of the spectral irradiance absorbed by the
device. This difference does not have a significant effect on monofacial PV performance,
as the ground reflected light reaching the active front side area, makes up only a small
fraction of the total global plane of array (Gpoa) irradiance. Depending on the module tilt
angle, the differences in accounting for spectrally resolved versus spectrally flat albedo,
is just 0,04% in energy yearly for a monofacial module tilted at 25°, while with increasing
module tilt it can be up to 2,4% for 40° tilt [4].
In contrast, for Bifi PV modules, a great amount of the light reaching the back surface
is reflected light coming from the ground cover. Since this reflected light is spectrally
selective, the extent to which it impacts Bifi PV yield predictions should be considered
with care
The introduction of Bifi modules, many commercially used software packages such as
pvsyst, SAM, PVSol, and others have introduced Bifi modeling capability. In addition,
open-source libraries such as PVfactors [PVfactors], bifacialvf [5] have been developed.
These solutions however lack the capability to analyse spectral albedo effects. This is un-
derstandable, as high resolution spectral time series data are hard to come by, especially

in advance for an area under investigation for potential PV park. Therefore, research
into how spectral effects impact Bifi PV performance, will provide valuable insights for Bifi
modeling and applications.
In this study, high resolution spectral global horizontal, direct, and reflected irradiance data
were gathered over a 5 months, at DTU Risø campus near Roskilde in Denmark. Three
units of MS711 spectroradiometers manufactured by EKO instruments in Japan. Spectral
albedo of grass and white woven polymeric tarp was recorded during this period. PVfac-
tors, a two dimensional view factors model developed by the SunPower corporation was
modified to include spectral irradiance and albedo modeling capability. This expanded
capability permits the calculation of Bifi gains under different albedo scenarios, ranging
from full time series spectral albedo to typical spectrally flat albedo values. The calcula-
tions were done for typical Bifi cell technologies such as nPERT, PERC, and IBC, which
were characterised in this study using real PV cell samples. Finally, spectral mismatch
factors and spectral impact were calculated for these technologies.
1.1
Motivation
Over the past decade, the solar industry experienced explosive growth in capacity and
energy generated worldwide. At the beginning of 2020, total installed capacity was esti-
mated at 583,5 GW, while it was just 40 GW in 2010, and merely 1,4 GW in year 2000 [6].
This growth was a result of many interlinked factors and their effect to the solar market.
The most important is arguably changes in policy and given incentives by the governments
supporting new solar projects, which stem from a goal to be more independent from tradi-
tional energy generation methods and fluctuating price of fossil fuels [7]. These incentives
together with the shifting public opinion on approach to energy sustainability, drove the
many commercial and residential projects to be built, with almost a certain promise of
financial gain in most cases, while heightened demand for solar technology pushed its
further development resulting in consistently falling PV prices.

The average silicon module price fell from 100 USD/W in 1975 to less than 1 USD/W in
2015. This drop in price was driven and accelerated from 2007 on-wards by adopting
mass production methods to manufacturing of PV modules and adopting public R&D ap-
proach providing technological improvements to the industry [8]. However, prospects of
further PVccell, module, and installation costs are unknown. Some sources claim further
reductions as new technologies such as perovskites are being developed, where other
state that the price must plateau [9].
In general, renewable energy technologies, including solar PV - in some instances -
reached a level where they are cheapest energy generation option [10]. All the afore-
mentioned factors, together with expected growth in electrical energy consumption by
15% to 49% depending on sector/country in question [11], as well as African countries
adopting solar power both on and off-grid in rapid pace [12] will further push PV technol-
ogy development and installed PV capacity growth.

With the increasing prominence of Bifi PV, the market share of Bifi modules employed in
the field is forecasted to grow to 35% by 2027 according to the The International Tech-
nology Roadmap for Photovoltaics (ITRPV). This growth means that research into Bifi
specific modeling is crucial for energy yield predictions to accurately define a PV project’s
bankability. As mentioned earlier, the aspect of the ground albedo component might skew
the predictions, by not accounting for spectral characteristics. Therefore, it is important
to get insight and quantify the potential for improved accuracy of the Bifi performance
models by accounting for spectral albedo effects.

1.2
Goal and steps of the study
The standard approach of using spectrally flat albedo for Bifi PV modelling, while the PV
cell is a spectrally responsive device, and the reflectance of ground is spectrally selective,
raises a question, are these neglected effects significant and important to Bifi modelling?
With this question in mind, this work raises a hyphothesis that there is a potential for im-
proved Bifi modelling accuracy with the inclusion of spectrally resolved albedo. Therefore,
the goal of this study is calculating the potential for improved accuracy in Bifi PV energy
predictions by including spectral albedo.
The study was done in the context of DTU, meaning that absolute findings may not be
universal, but tied to the location where the experimental work was done.
To reach the main goal, the work was divided into the following steps:
• First, spectral albedo of the grass and a white tarp (WT) was measured over the pe-
riod of 5 months. Time-series albedo data were analysed and compared to publicly
available spectral albedos from SMARTS. Three units of high resolution spectrora-
diometers from EKO instruments were used to record spectral GHI, DNI, and RHI
irradiances.
• Second, the two dimensional view factors model PVfactors was modified to allow
for spectrally resolved irradiance and albedo components.
• Then, different scenarios of ground albedo were analysed using the modified PV-
factors model. Bifi gains were calculated for these scenarios and compared to the
base case, for different Bifi PV cell technologies.
• Finally, spectral mismatch factors and spectral impact were calculated for these Bifi
PV cells.

2.1
Bifacial PV modelling tools
With increasing adoption of Bifi technology, the need to accurately model the back side
irradiance emerged. View factors models as well as ray tracing simulation models were
identified as potential ways of analysing Bifi PV. Some of the first tries to model with
these methods, were using popular general purpose finite element analysis stools such as
COMSOL [14]. In addition, researchers started working on in-house view factors models
to use for rear side irradiation calculations [15].
During 2017, couple of today’s well known modelling tools were developed and made
available as open source code for Bifi rear side irradiance modelling. Namely, NREL’s
Bifacialvf [5] view factors model, a wrapper for Radiance [16] suite of tools for ray tracing
adapted for Bifi PV calculations, as well as view factors model PVfactors [17] by Marc
Anoma from SunPower. The aforementioned models take different approaches to cal-
culate RPOA, using either two dimensional view factors model or ray tracing. All of the
tools were validated with real experiments - to varying accuracy - before publishing the
tool’s source code. First iteration of modelled PV array with PVfactors was found to be
within 2% agreement to the measured RPOA energy yield, whereas bifacialvf was found
to be equally accurate. Two dimensional view factors models take an assumption, that
the modeled rows are relatively long and edge effects can be disregarded. On the other
hand, ray tracing approach evaluates irradiance for three dimensional geometry scene,
therefore application is more complicated, but potentially more precise than view factors
models. The open source availability as a Python code online, allows these tools to be
checked by wider public, and for authors to implement bug fixes and improvements in
timely matter, making these models widely used both in academia and corporate setting.
Alternative simulation tools, such as MoBiDiG VF, pvsyst, SolarFarmer, Solar Advisor
Model (SAM), Plant predict, Prism, Daidalos, and others using either view factors or ray
tracing approach, were updated to be able to model Bifi PV. Prominence of all the available
tools vary.
2.2
Validation of Biﬁmodelling tools
Numerous studies since have tried to compare and benchmark the available Bifi tools.
Study by Silvana Ayala Pelaez et al. found that most of the models - despite varying com-
plexity - agree amongst each other, with best agreements to the measured Bifi modules
for lower PV ground clearance cases. Study has found maximum absolute error of 2%
for bifacial gain (BG) of a system for all cases [18]. Validation for a bigger field installa-
tion, and different ground covers was carried out by Amir Asgharzadeh et al. Study has
found that view factors models can estimate BG up to 1% in absolute accuracy, if modeled
correctly, while ray tracing approach might potentially be even more precise [19].
A comparison of eight available Bifi PV tools, and their modelled RPOA results for 420kWp
Bifi PV installation was carried at DTU by Nicholas Riedel et al. According to the study,
both open-source tools and commercially licensed software give sufficiently accurate re-
sults of rear side irradiance predictions, on par to the accuracy that can be expected for
modelling monofacial PV installations [20].

2.3
Variables inﬂuencing bifacial PV performance
With the available modelling tools and Bifi testing installations, researches constantly give
insight into the factors influencing Bifi PV perforance. Amir Asgharzadeh et al. performed
a study, showing that module tilt, module height above the ground, and albedo were some
of the most important factors effecting the BG. BG increases with height up to a point, af-
ter which it saturates. Another finding of this study is that self shadowing of the ground
by the modules, reduces the performance of bigger installations, showing that geome-
try also has influence over the Bifi PV performance [21]. Study by Matthieu Chiodetti et
al. further looked into albedo influencing PV calculations, and concluded that for Bifi PV,
taking albedo as a time-static value introduces error of up to 2,1% in temperate climates
and 1,1% in Mediterranean due to the effect solar zenith angle (SZA), cloudiness (diffuse
ratio), rain patterns has on reflectance of the cover under the Bifi PV. They found, that
albedo values may vary substantially over the period of a year, and time-series albedo
data should be used for modelling instead. Based on the findings, variable albedo model
was proposed which can improve the Bifi PV modelling accuracy [22]. Work by Xingshu
Sun et al. further showed the importance of local albedo data need for sufficiently accu-
rate Bifi modelling. In their work, researchers have used satellite derived irradiance and
albedo data for Bifi PV performance modelling and corresponding BG, however their de-
veloped framework is only for quick estimations of relevant Bifi PV installation parameters
worldwide. In order to get the accurate calculations, ground based irradiance and albedo
data are needed [23].
2.4
Albedo studies in context of BiﬁPV
Realisation that constant albedo assumption is negatively influencing accuracy of the Bifi
PV simulations, sparked an interest into albedo of different ground covers and their de-
pendency on the irradiance scene. Study by Mehreen Gul et al. analysed spectrally
flat localised albedo values and their change with ageing, solar elevation, rain patterns,
cloud covers. While analysing 7 different materials study has found that every variable
has a different influence on all the ground covers analysed. Study concludes that every
material must be characterised separately over long observation time to draw sufficient
information about the albedo characteristics of a cover [24]. As floating PV has entered
the picture recently, albedo of water surfaces was studied, finding that usually acceptable
water albedo of 0,2 leads to overestimation of BG. Time-series albedo values for water
are usually much lower with it being highest in evening and morning, while much lover
throughout the day [25]. Bifi topic sparked an renewed interest in albedo research by PV
scholars with some researchers concluding that monthly albedo values might be sufficient
for accurate Bifi modelling [26]. Also, it is still debatable whether use of satellite or ground
based measurements should be employed for wide-scale Bifi modelling.
2.5
Albedo measurements in broader context
In fact, research in albedo, although relatively neglected in PV scene before introduction
of Bifi, is still a topic of interest for climate and remote satellite sensing sciences. As
climate models solve for energy balance of the incoming sun radiation and energy leaving
the earth surface, accurate albedo predictions were always of interest. Many studies
were done on this topic, from analysing albedo of ground covers of naturally occurring
vegetative surfaces by using planes [27], to satellites [28] and analysing influences of
dew, aerosols, wind, and diurnal cycles, as well as recent studies refining the findings
and making more accurate measurements and predictions [29].

Spectral impact is weighted with irradiance measurements at the site, to account for high

To calculate the MM and spectral impact factors one needs to have spectral data of the

2.8
Main takeaways
Literature review could be summed up to couple of main points:
• There is a wide range of tools with two main approaches to Bifi modelling - view
factors and ray tracing. Most of the tools are useful, with accuracy dependant on
the complexity of the tool or method. However, it could not be definitely said that
one tool is superior compared to others.
• Albedo is not a constant but a variable property, which depend on many different
factors such as SZA, presence of dew, rain patterns, ground cover ageing, cloudi-
ness, aerosols, nature of the cover, wind influence etc.
• Reflected light has changed spectral characteristics compared to those of an in-
coming irradiation. This spectrally resolved albedo plays a significant role in energy
generation potential for Bifi PV modules, and it also depends not only on the ground
cover characteristics, but spectral response of the PV technology itself.
• Bifi modelling software can be expanded to incorporate spectral irradiance and
albedo effects.
• There is a lack and difficulty in acquiring high resolution spectrally resolved irra-
diance and reflection data. More conventional methods to record albedo can be
employed such as reference cells or pyranometers, to a certain degree of accuracy,
with a reference cell giving better results as its spectral responsitivity is closer to
that of a PV technology used in the field.
• Many studies have shown the effects under synthetically generated AM1.5G spec-
trum, leaving a possibility for a field study, analysing real recorded global and re-
flected spectral irradiance data.

Broadband GHI, DNI, DfHI irradiance data from the weather station was filtered using
Baseline Surface Radiation Network (BSRN) from World Radiation Monitoring Center rec-
ommended quality check test flow. The full quality check procedure and its’ recommended
limits to check are given in the appendix B. According to BSRN, pyranometer measure-
ments are to be filtered first for physically possible limits for the broadband irradiance,
filtering out lower than possible and then maximum possible irradiance, dependant on the
solar irradiance constant. Then, extremely rare limits are filtered out, using tighter criteria
for the filtered data. Finally, radiation components are compared using empirical relations
to sumSW, which is total DHI and DNI weighted by the cosine of solar zenith angle.
In addition to BSRN filters, broadband irradiance data was further filtered by emitting mea-
surements lower than 0, timestamps where clear sky index (CSI) calculated was greater
than 1,05, as such condition are possible, with clouds boosting GHI irradiance momen-
tarily,as well as checking for unrealistic diffuse irradiance fractions, filtering for outliers of
0 and 1,05 limits.
Broadband irradiance data was meticulously checked as it is used further to quantify vari-
ability of the sky irradiance over time, to catch timestamps where irradiance conditions
were changing rapidly, meaning that it is very likely that albedo calculated using spectro-
radiometers’ measurements will be unrealistic, as explain previously.
Variability index (VI) was chosen to be used as a metric for quantifying and identifying
acceptable, irradiance stable timestamps, to filter out spectral irradiance data [47].
Original intention of the VI is to quantify variability in irradiance over the period of time at
a site, for inter-comparison of potential sites or timescales. This metric is useful for clas-
sifying days or other time periods in which variability effects can be compared, classifying
sites in terms of the timing, frequency and magnitude of variability, and possibly used as
a metric for future variability forecasting.
Essentially, VI is calculated by comparing measured GHI to CSI differences from one
timestamp to another, calculating a vector for each, and comparing GHI and CSI vector
lengths to one another for every time delta. The convenient metric, as one number is then

dataset. Daily albedo plots were made for all the days, with only select wavelength albedo

evening albedo values, especially for near IR band, has downwards trending albedo val-

ments, especially at the edge of silicon detector limits starting at around 1050nm. Ad-
ditionally, despite spectroradiometer having a glass dome to minimise instrument cosine
response, with increasing SZA, reliability of measurements decrease at increasing rate.
As the whisker phenomenon was consistently starting at greater SZA 70° angles, data
with higher angle were dropped from the dataset.

and unrealistic albedo values.

3.7
EL imaging and EQE of bifacial PV cells
Since Bifi solar cell samples might have incurred damage at some point of their life, before
doing external quantum efficiency measurements were EL imaged to see if any regions
might be negatively affected or of a low quality. EL imaging is one of the techniques to
investigate PV samples in a non-destructive manner. Electric current is passed to the
cell using current-voltage supply, essentially putting a solar cell into the desired mode on
the IV curve, however not as a generator, but as a consumer of energy. Cell working in
this mode excites electrons in the junction and when they jump, emit light in near infrared
spectrum. IR light is being emitted at a range of 800nm to 1400nm, with a peak at around
1150nm for this excitation.
For EL imaging silicon based detector camera was used with a removed infrared filter. As
Si based detectors are responsive to the wavelength range 300nm-1200nm, visible light
filter up to 900nm was used, to block out significant amount of light, to be able to detect
1000nm – 1100nm light that is used to produce EL image. Analysis of EL images would
show which areas of the sample are electrically degraded or completely disconnected, by
showing as dark areas, due to light not being emitted or emitted at reduced capacity from
these regions if they are disconnected affected in any way. Governing standard IEC TS
60904 13 “Photovoltaic Devices Part 13: Electroluminescence of Photovoltaic Modules”
EL images of all samples were done in a dark room, in an imaging lab in Riso. Half cut
cells, as essentially being 2 cells at reduced area connected in series, were allowed to
pass twice the voltage [V] than the full-sized cell and half the current [I]. The light was
collected over a period of time to have a brighter image, but not for too long as to not
induce too much noise in it so that image comprehension would decrease. As the cell
samples, were of different quality, current and voltage to produce best possible quality

3.10
SMARTS spectral simulation
Spectroradiometers installed in the field for this study measure global horizontal and re-
flected horizontal spectrum in wavelengths from 300nm to 1100nm , while EQE measure-
ments done were in a range of 300nm to 1200nm as silicon based solar cells measured
are responsive to this spectral band.
This mismatch in spectral data ranges is inherently due to limitation in resources allocated
to this study as pricey equipment from weather station was relocated to the PV field, while
additional new spectroradiometer piece was installed already adding to the cost of the ex-
perimental setup. In order to extend the range of spectral measurements band, additional
pieces of equipment would be needed to be installed, more than doubling the costs of the
setup. In order to evaluate the possible effect this mismatch has on the calculations done
in this study, solar irradiance spectrum was simulated using Nrel developed SMARTS
(Simple Model of the Atmospheric Radiative Transfer of Sunshine) model.
One option considered to alleviate this flaw in the study was to emulate the missing spec-
tral band with SMARTS then joining it together with spectral measurements from the field,
however SMARTS model is developed only for investigating clear-sky, while spectrora-
diometer in the field works under many different irradiance and atmospheric conditions,
with many timestamps measured being partially clouded or totally overcast conditions.
Therefore, an approach to investigate the weight of missing spectrum against the whole
spectrum band of interest, was chosen. After the determination of the weight this spec-
trum has on the calculations, choice was made to extend the measured spectrum adding
the missing band. The procedure is described in the following section.
SMARTS version 2.9.5 was used for simulation, set up by following the steps defined in
user instructions provided with the software [50].
Simulated site was described as following, trying to emulate conditions present at Riso
site where experiment is set up. Carbon dioxide levels were set to 390 ppm [54], aerosol
model - Rural, with turbidity of 0,084 at aerosol optical depth at 500nm, extraterrestrial
spectrum by Gueymard 2004, regional albedo settings were set to generic bluegrass (lawn
grass) as the measurement site is maintained and periodically cut as well as located in
rural area. Spectral range was simulated for 1nm steps between 300nm and 1200nm
wavelengths. US standard Atmosphere 1976 was used as reference atmosphere, solar
constant - 1366,1 W/m2, site pressure - 1013,25 millibar.

absorbed in any significant amount [49]. Albedo is much more spread out in the near IR

counter intuitive, as one could argue that use of spectrally resolved albedo could improve
modelling accuracy. However, it is more important to use timeseries values with some
spectral resolution if possible to yield best accuracy in Bifi modelling.
Spectroradiometer is not absolutely necessary to minimise modelling error, however with
the data from it used properly, it could help reach very accurate modelling results. On the
other hand, if spectral values are just used to calculate time static spectral albedo values,
it is better to use reference cell or even pyranometer for acquiring the data.
It is better to use reference cell to pyranometer for recording albedo values, as broad-
band pyranometer sensor would probably lead to underestimation of the energy values
calculated, due to pyranometer recorded albedos being lowered by inclusion of broader
ground cover spectral data.

5
Conclusion
5.1
Main conclusions
Results of this study shows that albedo of ground is not a static quantity as it is usually
used in PV modelling. Albedo of ground is spectrally selective, so ground reflects every
wavelength of light uniquely, and this reflectance capability changes with many factors
influencing it. Factors include solar zenith angle, cloudiness, clearness, aerosols, rain and
wind patterns, age of cover, humidity, dryness, shadowing, slope of ground, composition
of strands in cover, geometry of the scene.
Grass cover was shown to exhibit greater variance in albedo values with time than man
made ground material. Seasonal influence in albedo values of covers was not evaluated
here as experiment had covered just a fraction of the year.
Using real measured spectral albedo values, as well as EQE measurements to calculate
spectral response of Bifi PV technologies such as IBC, PERC and, n-PERT, BG were
calculated. Calculations were done for scenarios where spectral nature of ground cover
reflected irradiance was fully included for front and back PV surfaces as well as using
ground albedo values from other instruments, lower spectral resolution, static spectral
values, or spectral albedo values used characterising the cover with two albedos, one for
overcast and one clear sky days.
Differences in potential error for albedo scenarios were expressed as ∆BG, where full
timeseries spectral albedo calculation results are compared to results of the other albedo
scenario.
It was found that potential for error increases with lower PV module tilts, lack of tracking,
and PV back side spectral response if it is high and close to spectral response of a front
side.
Incompleteness of spectral measurements (300nm - 1100nm vs 300nm-1200nm) for spec-
tral albedo calculations were shown to not induce great ∆BG error. Differences were
found to be negligible, potentially allowing use of narrower band spectroradiometer for
spectral albedo measurements. This finding is not definitive and relative just in scope of
this study.
Error in ∆BG due to exclusion of spectral albedo data, was shown to be in range of 0,11%
for a scenario where ground spectral albedo was defined for clear and overcast days, all
the way up to 4,16%, if using time static spectral albedo from SMARTS to model horizontal
Bifi PV arrays, with comparable front to back spectral responses.
Results of a study show, that use of timeseries Pyranometer albedo data, leads to under-
estimation of BG values, whereas reference cell to overestimation, but the latter shows
more promising results in minimising modelling error as spectral response of reference
cell is much closer to that of a PV device compared to pyranometer. It is highly proba-
ble, that reference cells are better device to use instead of spectroradiometers for albedo
research than pyranometers.
A very high resolution spectroradiometers were used in this work. Scenarios were anal-
ysed in this work, where lower resolution spectral data was used for modelling and it led
to similar BG results, showing that potentially, lower resolution data could be also used for
Impact of Spectral Albedo on Bifacial PV Performance Assessment
65
==PAGE:78==
Bifi PV reflected light modelling. Extent of lowering amount which would still be acceptable
is out of scope of this work.
BG calculation scenario, where time static spectral albedo data was used, led to worst
error in BG predictions. It underperformed even compared to calculations done using
albedo values measured by pyranometers, although it might look sensible to use spectral
albedo values. What is more important in minimising the error in modelling reflected light
is timeseries data where albedo changes with changing irradiance conditions, whereas
sensor type used to record this information just further lowers the potential for errors.
Inclusion of spectral albedo to Bifi PV front and back side irradiance calculations definitely
has a potential to increase the accuracy of Bifi PV modelling.
MM and SI factors were calculated in this study, showing that MM varies with time and SI is
dependant on the type of ground cover. For natural grass surfaces, spectrally insensitive
measurements must be corrected for MM as otherwise it can lead to back side energy
underestimation by 20%.
5.2
Future work
This work had touched a lot of topics going towards the completion, as well as introducing
some potential research questions.
During the study, AOI losses were assumed to be same for all Bifi PV cell samples, and
a typical AOI of a commercial Bifi module was used. Ideally, samples should have been
measured for AOI losses in a lab. With this improvement, results of this study would have
been in slightly greater accuracy.
All the samples modelled were of silicon. Inclusion of other PV materials would have pro-
vided more insight into broader Bifi PV modelling. Results of cells having bigger spectral
response window or significantly shorter would have provided very interesting results.
This study was carried over a comparatively short period of time, so seasonal variations
and their effects were not recorded. This work could potentially be continued, to measure
spectrally resolved albedo values for longer periods, with more ground materials to investi-
gate, ideally at the same time. Potentially lower resolution and quality spectroradiometers
could be used to cut on budget requirements of such study.
This work has mentioned presence of front MM models, and lack of back side ones too.
Although, as shown in this study, albedo values are highly variable and depend on many
factors, there is a potential for inception of back side MM model, which would be very
helpful as it was shown that spectral albedo values should be accounted in calculations,
but measurement of this spectral data is a complicated task.
This study lacks in in depth validation of the methods used to calculate results in this
study. Potentially, arrays of Bifi PV should observe and compared to modelled results in
a thorough manner to confirm the the theoretical approach used.
Finally, tool extensions developed here could be packaged into their own tool, allowing
others to do bifacial PV modelling with spectral capability. With some additional code,
python written programs could allow to do discretization of the PV arrays, allowing to
analyse the edges of PV panel arrays. Then spectral mismatch factors could be calculated
as well spatial non-uniformity of spectral irradiance and compared to those modelled with
other Bifi PV modelling approaches such as ray tracing.

BSRN recommended quality checks
 
 
BSRN Global Network recommended QC tests, V2.0 
 
C. N. Long and E. G. Dutton 
 
Define: 
 
SZA = solar zenith angle 
 
µ0 = Cos(SZA) 
 
 
NOTE: In the formulas below, if SZA > 90O, µ0 is set to 0.0 in the formula 
 
S0 = solar constant at mean Earth-Sun distance 
 
AU = Earth – Sun distance in Astronomical Units, 1 AU = mean E-S distance 
 
Sa = S0/AU2 = solar constant adjusted for Earth – Sun distance 
 
Sum SW = [Diffuse SW + (Direct Normal SW) X µ0] 
 
σ = Stephan-Boltzman constant = 5.67 x 10-8 Wm-2 K-4 
 
Ta = air temperature in Kelvin [must be in range 170K < Ta < 350K] 
 
Global SWdn: SW measured by unshaded pyranometer 
 
Diffuse SW: SW measured by shaded pyranometer 
 
Direct Normal SW: direct normal component of SW  
 
Direct SW: direct normal SW times the cosine of SZA; [(Direct Normal SW) x µ0] 
 
LWdn: downwelling LW measured by a pyrgeometer 
 
LWup: upwelling LW measured by a pyrgeometer

Ratio of Global over Sum SW: 
(Global)/(Sum SW) should be within +/- 8% of 1.0 for SZA < 75O, Sum > 50 Wm-2 
(Global)/(Sum SW) should be within +/- 15% of 1.0 for 93O > SZA > 75O, Sum > 50 Wm-2

Diffuse Ratio:
