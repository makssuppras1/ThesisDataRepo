Abstract
Financial market forecasting has been one of the most attractive research topics over
the last few decades. The recent development of machine learning has proven to be an
effective tool in financial applications.The thesis will focus on the financial market fore­
casting and asset allocation in a quantitative manner. The constituents of S&P 500 are
studied and we forecast the movement of the stocks in 2019 and develop the portfolio
dynamically. Two different methods are adopted for this purpose: One is applying a so­
phisticated recurrent neural network, Long­short term memory (LSTM), to forecast the
undervalued and overvalued stocks and developing long­only and long­short portfolios
accordingly; the other one is applying model predictive control (MPC) to dynamically op­
timize a portfolio based on forecasts of the mean and variance of returns from a hidden
Markov model (HMM). As a more complicated model, the LSTM network shows a slightly
better performance than HMM­based MPC and the extra computational effort pays off.
The short position based on the LSTM network loses money throughout the study period,
which proves that shorting should not be a proper strategy for a steadily growing market.
Besides, LSTMs combined with a fixed portfolio strategy show no clear pattern in asset
allocation and no preference to certain undervalued stocks through time, while a clear
pattern has been observed from the result of MPC with HMM that some of the stocks are
more appreciated than others. Transaction costs have a dramatically adverse impact on
the profitability of the strategies that the financial characteristics are vastly different before
and after transaction costs. Furthermore, the above methods have been proved effective
in the past research and real practice but cannot achieve a satisfactory result in the most
recent period, which is an evidence that the stock market has been increasingly influ­
enced by the models and thus profitability deteriorates as time goes by. Finally, a number
of attempts to improve the model have been conducted and the results have proven their
effectiveness.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Financial market forecasting has been one of the most attractive research topics over the
last few decades. The forecast not only aims to predict financial indicators or trends of
the financial market but also provides insight for the investors to make decisions. For
this purpose, a feasible research path is to capture the pattern of the historical data with
the help of numerical models and conduct analysis or forecast according to our practical
demands.
The recent development of machine learning has proven to be an effective tool in finan­
cial applications, such as neural networks for portfolio optimization (Zhang, Zohren, and
Roberts 2020), natural language processing for the sentiment analysis of the financial
market (Muthukumar and Zhong 2021, X. Li et al. 2019), financial market forecast (Gun­
duz 2021,Fischer and Krauss 2018), bankruptcy prediction (Kou et al. 2021). Besides, the
statistical models for financial applications have shown that currently they are still pow­
erful tools in the analysis and forecast of financial markets, such as GARCH for Bitcoin
regime changes (Ardia, Bluteau, and Rüede 2019) and dynamic asset allocation using
Hidden Markov models (Fons et al. 2021).
The thesis will focus on the financial market forecasting and asset allocation in a quan­
titative manner. For this purpose, the constituents of S&P 500 are studied and the two
kinds of models mainly adopted in the thesis are shown as follows.
• Neural Networks: Long­short Term Memory (LSTM) is a kind of recurrent neural
networks for learning order dependence in sequential prediction tasks. The LSTM
network is deployed to forecast the movement of stocks and based on which we
determine the portfolio dynamically by longing the undervalued stocks (long­only
strategy) or pair trading (long undervalued stocks and short overvalued stocks) ac­
cording to the forecast. The methodology for LSTMs is primarily motivated by Fis­
cher and Krauss (2018) but some adjustment is needed because we will study a
more recent period.
• Generative models: Hidden Markov models (HMMs) will be adopted as a generative
model to simulate the underlying regime of the financial market. Model predictive
control (MPC) is used to dynamically optimize a portfolio based on forecasts of the
mean and variance of returns from a hidden Markov model with time­varying param­
eters. The methodology is primarily motivated by Nystrup, Madsen, and Lindström
(2018) and due to differences of the dataset, some adjustment is also demanded.
Furthermore, different setups will also be discussed to reveal the characteristics of the
models in the financial application as a guidance for future work. Finally, based on the
primary methods proposed by the above­mentioned researchers, a couple of ideas to im­
prove the LSTM network will also be attempted for overcoming some broadly recognised
limitations.

2.1
Long­short term memory networks
The introduction of the Long­short term memory network (LSTM) follows the descrip­
tion given by Graves (2013), Olah (2015) and Fischer and Krauss (2018). Essentially,
the LSTM networks belong to the class of recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Thus, we
will start with briefly introducing the RNN structure and then illustrate its limitations and
demonstrate how the LSTM was introduced.

the RNNs only have ”short­term” memory.

cated but effective model, the LSTM network, was created. (However, they have not been
completely solved)

2.1.6
Techniques to prevent overfitting
We should pay attention to overfitting during the model training, where the model performs
exceptionally well in the training set while performs poorly in the validation set. An intuitive
explanation is that the model learns and fits not only the pattern but also the noise of the
training data.
There are a number of techniques to prevent overfitting, of which the ones covered in this
section are L1/L2 regularization, dropout and early stopping.
L1 & L2 Regularization
One approach to prevent overfitting is adding penalty items in the loss function to control
the magnitude of the parameters. The two most commonly chosen penalty items are L1
and L2 regularization.

Dropout
Another overfitting­preventing technique, exclusively in the field of deep learning, is dropout
(Nitish Srivastava 2014). The primary idea comes from bagging, an ensemble algorithm
to improve the stability and accuracy of machine learning models. It is briefly illustrated
in fig. 2.6
During the training, the values of the nodes are set to 0 with probability pdropout in each
iteration, which is equivalently creating a collection of thinned sub­networks. At the test
time, it is not efficient and feasible to take an average of the predictions from abundant
thinned networks (unlike Random Forest). The idea is to use a single neural net without
dropout at the test time. The weights are scaled back based on the dropout probability.
In other words, as a unit is retained with probability 1−pdropout, the weights of the unit will
be multiplied by 1 −pdropout at test time. (Nitish Srivastava 2014)
Early stopping
Early stopping is a form of regularization to avoid overfitting in the training by an interactive
method. The idea is very simple and illustrated in fig. 2.7. It originates from the fact that
the training loss decreases steadily but the test loss starts to rise again after some epochs.
Therefore, we should record the set of parameters of the lowest test loss as the training
goes on. The benefit of early stopping is that the learning dynamics is not damaged
and the only cost is saving the parameters every time when the best model is detected
regarding the test loss. The extra time consumed is negligible.

3.2
Performance review of LSTM
3.2.1
Trading strategy
The two following trading strategies are adopted.
• Long­short strategy: 2k stocks in the portfolio, k stocks for long position and k stocks
for short position. This arbitrage strategy follows Huck (2009).
• Long­only strategy: k stocks in the portfolio and are held in long position. It is
adopted to compare with the above pair trading strategy.
Positions are closed after one period.
We should also clearly define the method to select the stocks from the forecast. As sug­
gested by Fischer and Krauss (2018) and Krauss, Do, and Huck (2017), the returns larger
than or equal to the cross­sectional median are labelled as 1. For all models, we forecast
the probability for each stock to outperform the cross­sectional median in period t + 1
(class 1), given the information up until time t. Then we rank all stocks in period t + 1
in descending order of this probability. The top of the ranking corresponds to the most
undervalued stocks that are expected to outperform the cross­sectional median. As such,
for a long­short strategy consisting of 2k stocks in the portfolio, we go long top k stocks
and short flop k stocks in the ranking. For a long­only strategy, we only go long top k
stocks.
A potential problem and its solution
The forecast has been constructed as a classification problem that we predict if the return
at t + 1 will be larger than the corresponding cross­sectional median return computed
over all stocks. However, this setting has some intrinsic problems that the model with the
lowest loss is not equivalent to the most profitable model that we want to obtain.

In this section, we will thoroughly discuss about the financial performance of the LSTM and

• Mean return (long): The geometric mean of the daily return for the k long stocks.
• Mean return (short): The geometric mean of the daily return for the k short stocks

• Mean return: The geometric mean of the daily return for the portfolio. For long­only
strategy, it equals to the mean return (long).
• Minimum: The minimum return in the trading period.
• Median: The return under which 50% of returns are found when they are arranged
in increasing order.
• Maximum: The maximum return in the trading period.
• Share > 0: The portion of the positive returns.
• Standard dev: The standard deviation of the daily returns, which is a measure of
risk.
• VaR 5%: Value at Risk is a way to quantify the risk of potential losses for a firm
or an investment. For VaR 5%, the portfolio return will fall below the value with a
probability of 0.05.
• CVaR 5%: Based on Value at Risk, Conditional Value at Risk quantifies the amount
of tail risk that an investment portfolio has. It is the average of the returns that
fall below the VaR 5%. A visual comparison of the definition of VaR and CVaR is
presented in fig. 3.9.
• Max. drawdown: Maximum drawdown is the maximum observed loss from a peak
to a trough of a portfolio, before a new peak is attained. A visualized illustration is
presented in fig. 3.10.
• Sharpe ratio p.a.: The Sharpe ratio of the returns, which measures the performance
of an investment such as a security or portfolio compared to a risk­free asset, after
adjusting for its risk. The formula is shown as follows.

Attention­LSTM
Attention mechanism has been an important issue in deep learning for the past few years.
It was first proposed in the paper ”Attention is all you need” (Vaswani et al. 2017). A well­
known deep learning architecture built with an attention mechanism is Transformer (Wolf
et al. 2020) based on which a more complex model Bidirectional Encoder Representations
from Transformers (BERT) astonishingly achieved a decent result in 11 Natural Language
Processing tasks (Vaswani et al. 2017). In the field of time series forecast, the attention
mechanism has been adopted in travel time prediction (Ran et al. 2019) and prediction of
PM2.5 concentration (Y. Li et al. 2019).
In this section, we apply the attention mechanism to the output of the LSTM. We want to
enhance the capability of the LSTM network in long­term memory.
The comparison of LSTM and Attention­LSTM is shown in fig. 3.13. Here we use H =
{h1, h2, · · · , hT } to denote the matrix of hidden states generated by the LSTM cells. The
idea behind the attention mechanism is that we do not solely use the information from
the last hidden state but from all time steps. For this purpose, we do not treat the hidden
states equally but assign each of them with different weights such that the model will pay
more attention to the more important (i.e., higher weight) hidden state to generate the final
output.
There are a number of ways to quantify the attention and obtain the weights. In a direct
manner, the weights should reflect the similarity between the last hidden state and all
the previous ones (i.e., the similarity of two vectors). One of the most commonly used
similarity metrics is dot product. Then the vector of attention is calculated as in eq. (3.4).
To guarantee that all entries of α are summed to 1, we should normalize the vector by
applying the softmax function.
α = HhT
(3.4)
The final output of the temporal attention layer is the affine transformation of the concate­
nation of the weighted context vector ct = Hα and the last hidden state. Finally, the
sigmoid function is applied for a classification problem.
o = σ(W [ct, hT ] + b)
(3.5)
The configuration of the above Attention­LSTM only yields a few more parameters than
the original LSTM network. The number of parameters of Attention­LSTM is 2520 (Original

To update the HMM parameters, the input sequence should be long enough to not only
capture the market dynamics but also help obtain a stationary Markov Chain. Therefore,
we use 600 days to update the HMM model and simulate the return at the next time step.
In detail, we start with feeding the data of the past 600 days before the first available day
in 2019 (2019­01­02) into the model and simulate. After that, we move one day ahead
and train again with the data of the past 600 days (i.e., sliding window) and simulate at
the next day (2019­01­03). The transaction cost we adopt is 0.10%. The simulation is
iterated until the last day of 2019.

local optima, we should in general try to fit the model with various initializations and select
the highest­scored one.

• Number of hidden states for HMMs.

3.4
Comparison of LSTM and HMM­based MPC
In essence, the two models follow two different methodologies that a direct comparison
is inadequate as the number of assets in the portfolio is static in the implementation of

4
Conclusion and future work
4.1
Main conclusions
In this thesis, a number of contributions towards financial market forecasting and asset
allocation are made.
We applied Long­short term memory (LSTM), one of the most
renowned recurrent neural networks, and hidden Markov model (HMM), a generative
model that has commonly been applied in financial engineering. The performance of
the two models in the test period of 2019 has been fully compared and the characteristics
of both models have been discussed, respectively. The following conclusions have been
drawn from this thesis.
• By comparing the LSTM with three benchmark models, Logistic Regression, Ran­
dom Forest, and Maxout Network, the LSTM model shows not only the best perfor­
mance but also a clear advantage in almost all evaluation metrics. As the LSTM is a
more complex model demanding for more computational resources and takes much
longer time to train, the extra cost paid off. However, no model beats the SPX index
after transaction cost. At the same time, we have observed that transaction cost has
a big impact on the profitability of the strategy by comparing the financial character­
istics of all models before and after transaction costs. The LSTM network can beat
the index before transaction costs but cannot after transaction costs. Therefore, re­
ducing the trading frequency and thus alleviating the adverse impact of transaction
costs can be seen as a meaningful approach to optimize the portfolio strategy. A
fixed and naive implementation to close its position at each time step is inadequate.
• The result from this thesis and previous research (Fischer and Krauss 2018) has
shown that the market has been increasingly influenced by the models and the prof­
itability deteriorates as time goes by. Furthermore, only the data from the open and
free source is used in this thesis. It is easily obtained and many people can work it
on. This fact indicates that if we only rely on free data and widely deployed models,
it could be hard to beat the market.
• A long­short strategy is not a proper one in the period when the market value grows
continuously. The SPX grew by nearly 28% in 2019. Correspondingly, the numerical
results show that the short position in the long­short strategy ends up loss.
• A number of ideas have been attempted based on the traditional LSTMs. We have
observed improved financial performance for the study period, but there is a long
way to go to beat the market (i.e., SPX index). A more radical modification of the
model may result in further improvement.
• In the thesis, we carefully discuss the risk aversion and trade­aversion for the Model
Predictive Control (MPC) with hidden Markov models (HMMs). We found that with
no risk aversion, the model takes the most aggressive strategy that it holds only one
or two stocks in the portfolio, while with higher risk aversion, trading is more frequent
and the investment is more diversified. And the trade­aversion control can serve as
an additional force apart from transaction costs to compress the model’s inclination
to trade.
• The LSTM is applied combined with a predefined equal­weight strategy that at every
time step, a fixed number of stocks are held in a long position in the portfolio. Model
Predictive Control (MPC) is performed based on the generated returns from HMM.

In order to compare the performance of the two models, the risk­aversion of the

no preference to certain undervalued stocks through time, while a clear pattern has

4.2
Future work
In this thesis, we have obtained a lot of meaningful conclusions. However, due to the
limited available resources, we cannot completely follow the research pipeline of the pre­
vious publications and make a thorough and comprehensive comparison with them. The
following work can be finished when more resource is acquired.
• In this thesis, only 50 out of 505 stocks in the S&P 500 constituents was randomly
selected because the laptop is only equipped with 8GB RAM and 2GB video mem­
ory. In Fischer and Krauss (2018), the authors applied LSTM networks to all S&P
500 constituents and trained the model on NVIDIA GPUs. Therefore, when a more
powerful computer or cluster is available, it is a good choice for us to model with all
S&P 500 constituents at one time.
• In Fischer and Krauss (2018), the authors obtained all month­end constituent lists for
the S&P 500 from Thomson Reuters from December 1989 to September 2015. They
used the constituent list and dataset of the different study periods for eliminating
survivor bias. However, the historical data of companies that have gone bankrupt or
been acquired by others is too expensive to obtain. As a result, we could not finish
the same work as the authors did. Therefore, if there is a reliable data provider
for the historical data, we could extend our existing work to a longer horizon and
evaluate the models in different historical periods.
The thesis can be seen as the first step towards more sophisticated models for financial
market forecast. At this point, the following approaches are proposed and can serve as
possible choices for the next step.

tion mechanism to the output of the LSTM layer. In sentiment classification of natural
languages, Wang et al. (2016) proposed a more advanced attention mechanism which
may benefit time series forecast. The new architecture is shown in fig. 4.1.

• Debt­to­Equity Ratio: a metric dividing a company’s total liabilities by its shareholder

• Return on Equity: a metric dividing net income by shareholders’ equity.
• Price­to­Book ratio: a metric that compares a company’s market value to its book

perspective, we can reinterpret the task as to recommend the undervalued stocks to earn

are widely used in the field of recommendation system can be introduced. For example,
we may rank the stocks based on the return at each time step and then obtain a matrix

at the next time step. Although the example is merely a naive application of a common
model in the recommendation system, we can still say that there are many good ideas
that we can borrow to build up a stock recommendation system.
