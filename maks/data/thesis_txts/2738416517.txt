Chapter 1
Introduction
Peptides and proteins are vital biomolecules that play crucial roles in numerous
biological processes. Proteins are large, complex molecules composed of one or
more long chains of amino acids, folded into specific three-dimensional structures
that determine their functions within the body. Peptides, on the other hand, are
shorter chains of amino acids, typically ranging from 2 to 50 in length, and are
derived from proteins through enzymatic breakdown or synthesis. Both proteins
and peptides are indispensable in physiological processes, serving as enzymes,
hormones, structural components, and signaling molecules.
1.1
Amino Acids: The Building Blocks
Amino acids are the fundamental units that build peptides and proteins. There
are 20 standard amino acids, each with unique properties and functions, forming
the diverse sequences that determine the specific structure and function of the
resulting peptides and proteins. These amino acids include:
• Alanine (Ala, A)
• Arginine (Arg, R)

• Asparagine (Asn, N)
• Aspartic Acid (Asp, D)
• Cysteine (Cys, C)
• Glutamic Acid (Glu, E)
• Glutamine (Gln, Q)
• Glycine (Gly, G)
• Histidine (His, H)
• Isoleucine (Ile, I)
• Leucine (Leu, L)
• Lysine (Lys, K)
• Methionine (Met, M)
• Phenylalanine (Phe, F)

• Tryptophan (Trp, W)
• Tyrosine (Tyr, Y)
• Valine (Val, V)
Amino acids link together via peptide bonds, forming polypeptide chains that
fold into functional proteins or remain as shorter peptides.
1.2
The Importance of Peptides and Proteins
Peptides and proteins are fundamental to human nutrition, health, and food
science due to their diverse roles and beneficial properties.

1.2.1
Nutritional Value
Peptides and proteins provide essential amino acids necessary for growth, repair,
and maintenance of body tissues.
The digestibility and absorption of food-
derived peptides are often high, making them significant contributors to the
overall nutritional profile of food products.
1.2.2
Bioactive Properties
Peptides exhibit various bioactive properties that can promote health and pre-
vent diseases. These bioactive peptides, released during food processing or di-
gestion, include:
• Antimicrobial Peptides (AMPs): AMPs are crucial in combating the
rising issue of antibiotic resistance due to their ability to kill microorgan-
isms without inducing resistance. They disrupt microbial cell membranes
or interfere with intracellular functions, providing rapid antimicrobial ef-
fects. For an in-depth review, see "Comprehensive Assessment of Machine
Learning-Based Methods for Predicting Antimicrobial Peptides" by Xu et
al.
• Antioxidant Peptides: These peptides protect cells from oxidative dam-
age by neutralizing free radicals.
• Antihypertensive Peptides: These peptides help lower blood pressure
by inhibiting angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE).
• Immunomodulatory Peptides: These peptides enhance the immune
response and regulate inflammation.
• Opioid Peptides: These peptides exhibit pain-relieving properties by
interacting with opioid receptors.
1.2.3
Functional Properties
Peptides also enhance the functional properties of food products, contributing
to their texture, stability, and sensory attributes:
• Emulsification: Peptides can stabilize emulsions in products like salad
dressings and sauces.

for products like whipped creams and mousses.
• Gelation: Peptides facilitate gel formation, important for the texture of

1.2.4
Flavor Enhancement
Peptides influence the taste and flavor of food products by acting as flavor pre-
cursors. They undergo reactions during cooking to develop desirable flavors.
Some peptides also possess inherent taste-modifying properties, such as sweet-
ness, bitterness, or umami, enhancing the sensory experience of foods.
1.3
Peptides in the Milk Industry
The milk industry is a significant sector where peptides play a crucial role. Milk
contains various proteins, such as caseins and whey proteins, which are rich
sources of bioactive peptides.
These peptides are released during the diges-
tion of milk proteins or through fermentation and enzymatic hydrolysis during
dairy processing. Milk-derived peptides enhance the nutritional value of dairy
products by providing essential amino acids and promoting overall health.
In the context of infant nutrition, milk peptides are vital in infant formula,
mimicking the functional properties of human milk. They aid in the develop-
ment of the immune system, enhance nutrient absorption, and promote healthy
growth in infants. These peptides provide essential amino acids and bioactive
compounds that are crucial for infant development, offering benefits similar to
those found in natural breast milk.
1.3.1
Health Benefits
Milk-derived peptides exhibit numerous health benefits, including:
• Antihypertensive Effects: Peptides such as lactotripeptides (e.g., IPP
and VPP) inhibit angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), helping to lower
blood pressure.

Literature Review
2.1
Overview of Machine Learning in Biological
Classification
Machine learning (ML) has emerged as a transformative technology in biologi-
cal classification, offering powerful tools for analyzing complex biological data.
ML techniques enable the development of predictive models that can learn from
existing data and make accurate predictions on new, unseen data. In biological
classification, ML has been widely applied to various tasks, including the iden-
tification of genes, proteins, and peptides, prediction of protein structure and
function, and classification of diseases [1, 2].
ML algorithms, such as support vector machines (SVM), random forests (RF),
and neural networks, have been particularly effective in handling the high-
dimensional and heterogeneous nature of biological data. Feature engineering
and selection are critical components in ML workflows, allowing the extrac-
tion of relevant information from biological sequences. With the advent of deep
learning, the ability to automatically learn complex feature representations from
raw data has further enhanced the accuracy and applicability of ML models in
biology [1, 2].

2.2
Machine Learning in Peptide Classification
Peptide classification, particularly the identification and functionality predic-
tion of antimicrobial peptides (AMPs), has significantly benefited from advance-
ments in machine learning techniques. The complexity and diversity of peptides
necessitate sophisticated computational approaches to complement traditional
experimental methods, which are often labor-intensive and time-consuming.
This section reviews the key machine learning methodologies applied to peptide
classification, emphasizing their evolution, implementation, and performance
[1, 2, 3].
2.2.1
Conventional Machine Learning Approaches
Early efforts in peptide classification utilized conventional machine learning al-
gorithms, such as support vector machines (SVM), random forests (RF), and
k-nearest neighbors (k-NN). These methods rely heavily on feature engineer-
ing, where domain knowledge is used to extract relevant features from peptide
sequences.
2.2.1.1
Support Vector Machines (SVM)
SVMs are popular due to their robustness in handling high-dimensional data.
They have been widely used in tools like CAMP, which applies SVMs along with
other algorithms (RF, ANN) for AMP prediction. The incremental feature selec-
tion (IFS) and recursive feature elimination (RFE) methods are often employed
to optimize the feature set, improving the classifier’s performance [1, 4].
2.2.1.2
Random Forests (RF)
RF classifiers are favored for their ability to handle large datasets with many fea-
tures. They aggregate the results of multiple decision trees to enhance predictive
accuracy and control over-fitting. Tools like AmPEP and dbAMP utilize RF
algorithms to classify AMPs by employing a diverse range of sequence-derived
and physicochemical features [1, 5].
==PAGE:15==
2.2 Machine Learning in Peptide Classification
9
2.2.1.3
k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN)
The k-NN algorithm, used in models like iAMP-2L, classifies peptides based on
the closest training examples in the feature space. Fuzzy k-NN variants have
been introduced to account for the inherent uncertainties in peptide classifica-
tion, improving the model’s robustness [1, 6].
2.2.2
Feature Engineering and Selection
Feature engineering is a crucial step in conventional ML approaches, involving
the extraction of meaningful features from peptide sequences. Commonly used
features include amino acid composition (AAC), dipeptide composition (DPC),
physicochemical properties, and structural attributes.
2.2.2.1
Amino Acid Composition (AAC)
AAC features represent the frequency of each amino acid in a peptide sequence.
They are simple yet effective in capturing essential characteristics for peptide
classification [2].
2.2.2.2
Dipeptide and Tripeptide Composition (DPC, TPC)
These features extend AAC by considering the frequency of consecutive amino
acid pairs or triplets, providing more detailed sequence information [2].
2.2.2.3
Physicochemical Properties
Properties such as hydrophobicity, charge, and molecular mass are critical for
distinguishing between different peptide functionalities. Tools like CS-AMPPred
integrate these properties with sequence-based features to enhance predictive
performance [2, 7].

2.2.3
Deep Learning Approaches
The advent of deep learning (DL) has revolutionized peptide classification by
enabling the automatic extraction of complex features directly from raw se-
quences. DL models, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and
recurrent neural networks (RNNs), have shown remarkable success in bioinfor-
matics applications.
2.2.3.1
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
CNNs are adept at capturing spatial hierarchies in data, making them suitable
for peptide sequence analysis. Tools like Deep-AmPEP30 utilize CNN architec-
tures to extract features from peptide sequences and classify them with high
accuracy [3, 9].
2.2.3.2
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)
RNNs, including long short-term memory (LSTM) networks, are designed to
handle sequential data, making them ideal for modeling peptide sequences. The
AMP Scanner v.2 leverages LSTMs to predict AMP functionalities by learning
the dependencies between amino acids in a sequence [3, 10].
2.2.3.3
Attention Mechanisms
Recent advancements incorporate attention mechanisms into DL models, al-
lowing the network to focus on the most relevant parts of the input sequence.
AMPlify, for instance, combines Bi-LSTM and multi-head scaled dot-product
attention to enhance the classification accuracy of AMPs [3, 11].
2.2.4
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful tool in peptide clas-
sification due to their ability to model complex relationships within biological
data. GNNs, such as Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) and Graph Atten-
tion Networks (GATs), can effectively process graph-structured data, capturing
intricate dependencies and interactions.
==PAGE:17==
2.2 Machine Learning in Peptide Classification

2.2.4.1
Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs)
GCNs are used to analyze graph-structured data, making them suitable for mod-
eling the spatial relationships in peptide sequences. They have been applied in
various bioinformatics applications, including protein-protein interaction pre-
diction and drug discovery [12].
For instance, GCNs have shown success in
capturing the complex structural patterns in protein-protein interaction net-
works, providing insights that were previously unattainable through traditional
methods.
2.2.4.2
Graph Attention Networks (GATs)
GATs utilize attention mechanisms to weigh the importance of different nodes
and edges in a graph, enhancing the ability to capture relevant features. The
sAMPpred-GAT model, for instance, uses GATs to predict AMPs by incorporat-
ing structural information from predicted peptide structures [3]. By leveraging
the attention mechanism, GATs can dynamically focus on the most critical parts
of the graph, improving the interpretability and performance of the predictive
models.
The application of GNNs in peptide classification offers several advantages:
• Structural Information Utilization: GNNs can naturally incorporate
structural information of peptides, which is crucial for understanding their
functionality. This is particularly beneficial for AMP prediction, where the
spatial arrangement of amino acids significantly influences antimicrobial
activity.
• Enhanced Feature Representation: By transforming peptide sequences
into graph representations, GNNs can capture complex relationships and
dependencies that are often missed by traditional ML methods.
• Improved Predictive Performance: Studies have shown that GNN-
based models, such as sAMPpred-GAT, outperform conventional ML and
DL models in various metrics, including accuracy, precision, and recall
[3, 12].

2.3
Evaluation and Benchmarking
Evaluating the performance of ML models in peptide classification involves met-
rics such as accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and the area under the receiver
operating characteristic curve (AUC-ROC). Cross-validation techniques, includ-
ing K-fold and leave-one-out cross-validation, are commonly used to ensure ro-
bust performance estimates [1, 3].
Independent benchmarking studies, like the one conducted by Xu et al., provide
comprehensive comparisons of various ML and DL approaches, highlighting their
strengths and limitations. These studies are crucial for identifying the most
effective models and guiding future research directions [1, 2].
2.4
Challenges and Future Directions
Despite significant advancements, peptide classification faces several challenges,
including data imbalance, feature redundancy, and the need for more compre-
hensive datasets. Future research is expected to focus on:
• Integrating Multi-omics Data: Combining genomic, proteomic, and
metabolomic data can provide a more holistic view of peptide functionality
and improve prediction accuracy [3, 12].
• Developing Sophisticated Feature Selection Techniques: Advanced
feature selection methods can help in identifying the most relevant fea-
tures, reducing noise, and improving model performance [12].
• Enhancing Interpretability of DL Models: Developing models that
provide biological insights into how predictions are made can enhance the
trust and applicability of these models in real-world scenarios [3, 12].
In conclusion, machine learning has transformed peptide classification by offer-
ing powerful tools to predict peptide functionalities accurately. As ML and DL
techniques continue to evolve, their application in peptide research promises to
uncover new insights and drive innovations in biotechnology and computational
biology [1, 2, 3].

Chapter 3
Methodology
3.1
Graph Representation of Data
Some data are naturally presented in graph form, such as networks of people,
molecules, and social interactions. For instance, in molecular structures, each
atom can be considered a node, with connections (edges) representing chemical
bonds. These bonds are crucial features that determine the molecule’s proper-
ties. Similarly, proteins and peptides are chains of amino acids linked by peptide
bonds, where the interactions between these amino acids govern their function-
ality. By representing a peptide as a graph, consisting of nodes (amino acids)
and edges (interactions), we can uncover the key features that dictate its behav-
ior. This graph-based representation is particularly useful in neural networks
for tasks such as classification.
3.2
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
In the realm of machine learning, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) stand out as
a transformative technology designed to handle graph-structured data. Unlike
traditional data forms, graphs consist of nodes (vertices) and edges that capture
==PAGE:20==
14
Methodology
the relationships between nodes. This unique structure is prevalent in many real-
world applications, such as social networks, biological networks, and knowledge
graphs. GNNs are tailored to exploit these structures, making them essential for
tasks that require understanding and leveraging the intricate interdependencies
within data.
3.2.1
Understanding Graph Neural Networks
Graph Neural Networks extend the capabilities of traditional neural networks
to non-Euclidean domains, specifically graphs. A graph G = (V, E) consists
of a set of nodes V and a set of edges E connecting these nodes. Each node
v ∈V can be associated with a feature vector xv, and edges (u, v) ∈E can have
associated features euv. GNNs operate by iteratively updating node representa-
tions (embeddings) through a process called message passing or neighborhood
aggregation. This allows nodes to incorporate information from their neighbors,
thus capturing the local and global structure of the graph.
3.2.2
Importance of GNNs
The significance of GNNs lies in their ability to process relational data, which
is common in various domains. Unlike traditional neural networks that handle
Euclidean data like images and text, GNNs excel in scenarios where the rela-
tionships between data points are as crucial as the data points themselves. This
relational approach enables GNNs to capture complex dependencies and inter-
actions, leading to enhanced performance in tasks such as node classification,
link prediction, and graph classification.
3.2.3
Benefits of GNNs
1. Capturing Local and Global Structures: GNNs can simultaneously
capture local neighborhoods and global graph structures, providing a com-
prehensive understanding of the graph.
2. Permutation Invariance: GNNs respect the permutation invariance
property of graphs, ensuring that the output does not depend on the
ordering of nodes or edges.
3. Scalability: Various GNN architectures are designed to scale with large
graphs, making them suitable for big data applications.

3.2.5
Types of GNNs
• Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs): Extend the concept of con-
volution to graphs.
• Graph Attention Networks (GATs): Use attention mechanisms to
weigh the importance of neighboring nodes.
• Graph Recurrent Networks (GRNs): Use recurrent neural networks
to update node embeddings iteratively.
• Graph Autoencoders (GAEs): Used for unsupervised learning to en-
code graph data into a lower-dimensional space and decode it back.

3.3.2.1
Detailed Explanation of AlphaFold
AlphaFold employs a deep learning approach to predict the three-dimensional
(3D) structure of proteins from their amino acid sequences. It uses a neural
network architecture that integrates information from homologous sequences,
multiple sequence alignments, and structural templates to generate accurate
models.
AlphaFold’s significance in this study cannot be overstated. We used AlphaFold
to generate structural models for our peptides, and its predictions were validated
against experimentally determined structures available on the RCSB Protein
Data Bank (PDB) website. The PDB is a comprehensive resource for 3D struc-
tural data of biological macromolecules, and it provided us with validated 3D
shapes for comparison.
Upon evaluation, AlphaFold’s predictions closely matched the validated struc-
tures, leading us to select it as the primary tool for our study.
AlphaFold
outputs the five best models for each peptide, and we chose the first model for
further analysis. These models are provided as PDB files, which contain the 3D
coordinates of atoms in the protein.
3.3.3
Graph Construction from AlphaFold Models
To construct the peptide graphs, we utilized the coordinates from the AlphaFold-
generated PDB files. Specifically, we focused on the Cα (alpha carbon) atoms
for calculating distances and constructing the graph.
Here is a step-by-step
breakdown of the process:
1. Distance Calculation: We calculated the Euclidean distance between
the Cα atoms of each pair of residues.
A threshold distance of 10 Å
(Ångströms) was used to determine whether an edge should be created
between two nodes (residues). If the distance between two Cα atoms was
less than or equal to 10 Å, an edge was established between the corre-
sponding nodes.
2. Angle Calculation: In addition to distances, we calculated three angles
to capture the geometric relationships between residues. These angles were
also used as edge attributes, providing additional structural information
for the GNN to leverage.
3. Graph Representation: Each peptide was represented as a graph where:

To generate PSSMs, we employed Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA)

the NR database (O’Leary et al., 2016) instead of the nrdb90 database.

peptide sequence. Each entry in the PSSM represents the log-likelihood of
