Abstract
In recent years, various initiatives have been taken in the maritime sector, aiming the
CO2 capture and storage, nevertheless without wide commercialization in new buildings
or ship retrofitting. The scope of this study is the introduction of Molten Carbonate Fuel
Cell (MCFC) on board a vessel as part of its exhaust gas system as a new approach of
decreasing GHG emissions from the exhaust, such as CH4 and NOx, while capturing CO2
and generating electrical energy, by adopting a currently in┬¡use stationary application for
employment on board. The sustainable electric power provided, as well as the technical
water recovered from the MCFC can be used to meet the vesselÔÇÖs energy demands. The
MCFC is developed by FuelCell Energy, and the candidate vessel studied is a Stena Bulk
Suezmax tanker. All calculations were performed under the assumption that the vessel
runs on LNG and has a dual fuel ME┬¡GI MAN ES engine. During this project, the energy
and mass conservation concept was utilized for evaluating the MCFC operation. As a
result, a Waste Heat Recovery ÔÇô Energy Balance Analysis was performed, which included
the required energy for the MCFCÔÇÖs and capture and liquification systemsÔÇÖ heating and
cooling processes, as well as the available energy entering and exiting the fuel cell stack.
The vessel ownerÔÇÖs technical department (Stena Teknik) provided appropriate data, as
well as relevant information concerning the engine manufacturer (MAN ES) and fuel cell
manufacturer (FuelCell Energy) was retrieved from public literature, with the aim to fa┬¡
cilitate the analysis of the Waste Heat Recovery ┬¡ Energy Balance Analysis. Moreover,
this information was then utilized to determine the efficiency of the MCFC and the CO2
capture and liquification systems. Based on this analysis, the dimensioning of the MCFC,
as well as a proposal of layout and piping system to fit in the engine room were depicted
in two flowcharts, one for each approach of CO2 cooling studied. The first approach con┬¡
cerned the LNG passing through the Cryogenic CO2 Separation / Liquification system,
in order to offer its cooling power, and the second approach concerned the CO2 mixture
passing through the Fuel Gas Supply System (FGSS), in order to be cooled by the LNG,
simultaneously with the LNG heating and conversion to NG.
Concluding this study, MCFC characteristic size was estimated to be 13 m2 in cross sec┬¡
tion and 10 m in height. The preferred approach of CO2 cooling is the second, which
removes 53% CO2. ISO containers are also suggested as an alternative option of CO2
storage, as it is estimated to have the ability of 80% CO2 removal. Furthermore, the pos┬¡
sibility of NOx removal and CH4 slip reduction was rendered possible due to chemical
reactions and the internal reforming property of the MCFC. In addition, the annual electric
energy production of the MCFC system, based on the data provided for the Suezmax
tanker, was calculated at 17.2 GWh/year, which results in making the replacement of an
auxiliary engine by the fuel cell feasible, saving this way 4,734,354 Ôé¼ annually. Finally,
the annual CO2 removal was estimated at 11860 MT CO2/year by utilising the second ap┬¡
proach of CO2 cooling, corresponding to OPEX savings of 621,228 Ôé¼ annually from CO2
capture, as opposed to 1,881,600 Ôé¼ loss per year when using MGO without a CO2 capture
system. Taking the OPEX savings into consideration, an expected return of investment
can even occur in the first 1 ┬¡ 2 years. As future considerations, real life experiments must
be conducted in order to ascertain the real NOx and CH4 slip reduction, treated CO2 rate
and electric energy production during MCFC operation. Moreover, a Techno┬¡econ analy┬¡
sis and a risk assessment should be performed, aiming the thorough study of all aspects
of MCFC implementation on board.

Introduction
Carbon Dioxide CO2 is one of the most crucial greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and
a major contributor to global warming. The International Maritime Organisation (IMO)
has formed appropriate regulations in order to achieve at least 40% reduction in CO2
emissions across international shipping per transport unit in 2030, pursuing attempts to
at least 70% reduction in 2050. IMO also pursues a yearly decrease in GHG emissions
from international shipping by at least 50% by 2050, as compared to 2008 [1]. Some
of the most important regulations established by IMO are the Energy Efficiency Design
Index (EEDI), the Energy Efficiency eXisting ship Index (EEXI) and the Carbon Intensity
Indicator (CII). The EEDI is a mandatory regulation for new vessels since 2013 and is a
technical indicator, aiming the adoption of more environmentally friendly equipment and
engines. For each ship type and size, a minimum energy performance level per capacity
mile is required, which new vessels must reach in order to fulfill this regulation [2]. On
the other hand, the EEXI regulation is implemented in order to minimize existing ship
greenhouse gas emissions and is linked to the vesselÔÇÖs technical design. Vessels must
obtain EEXI certification once throughout their lifespan, but no later than the first annual
survey in 2023 [3]. Finally, the CII regulation, which will take effect in 2023, indicates a
shipÔÇÖs efficiency in transporting products or passengers and is expressed in gr of CO2
emitted per cargo capacity and nautical mile. The vessel is then assigned a yearly rating
spanning from A to E, with the grading requirements becoming increasingly strict as 2030
approaches [4].
As of 2018, maritime transport emits more than 1 billion tonnes of CO2 per year, which is
accountable for about 2.9% of global GHG emissions [5]. Zero┬¡carbon or carbon┬¡neutral
fuels for shipping are being developed, but accessible quantities will be limited for many
years, and construction of fuel distribution infrastructure has yet to begin. Thus, the inter┬¡
est in carbon capture and storage solutions (CCS) in shipping has increased, in order to
comply with the sustainable shipping guidelines. Although several initiatives have been
taken in the maritime industries in recent years, nevertheless, none of these projects
have been widely commercialized on new building or the retrofit of existing ships. The
Molten Carbonate Fuel Cell is introduced as a newfound way of reducing some of the
GHG emissions from the exhaust of the vesselÔÇÖs main engine, such as CH4 and NOx,
whilst capturing the CO2 and producing electrical energy, by adopting a currently in┬¡use
stationary application and implementing it on board [6].
1.1
Green House Gas Emissions
Some of the key GHG emissions produced by maritime transport are CO2, methane CH4
and nitrous oxide N2O. The aforementioned emissions are considered to be sources of an┬¡
thropogenic pollution and direct greenhouse gasses [7]. There is also the indirect GHG
category, in which nitrogen oxides NOx, the non┬¡methane volatile organic compounds
(NMVOCs), sulfur dioxide (SO2) deriving from a larger category called sulphur oxides
SOx, and the carbon monoxide (CO) are classified [8]. GHG emissions are considered to
be significant contributors to global climate changes, since they trap heat and increase the
temperature of the Earth (also know as global warming). In addition, air quality and pollu┬¡
tion, water and soil contamination, destruction and poisoning of flora and fauna, chronic
complications in human health and even death are some of the numerous consequences
of the GHG emissions [9].

1.2
Alternative Fuels
Alternative fuels aid with the shift from traditional motor fuel sources, such as gasoline
and diesel, to alternative energy sources. Initiatives promoting alternative fuels are a rec┬¡
ommended technique for reducing emissions, such as GHG emissions, including volatile
organic compounds (VOCs), since these sources contribute significantly less to air toxicity
than conventional fuels [10]. Pollutant levels and emission reductions differ depending on
the alternative fuel, and exposure to pollutants varies by geographic area. Therefore, un┬¡
derstanding the local context can help with judgments on the best alternative fuel strategy,
since there is no one┬¡size┬¡fits┬¡all solution for these initiatives [10].
DNV [11] has recognized LNG, LPG, methanol, and hydrogen as the most viable alterna┬¡
tive fuels for shipping, which are analysed below:
ÔÇó LNG: LNG stands for Liquified Natural Gas (CH4) and it is a hydrocarbon fuel, with
its primary constituent being methane, which as written previously has low carbon
content and high CO2 reduction capability. It also has the same composition to natu┬¡
ral gas used in homes, power production, and industry [11]. This fuel is sulphur free,
which means that it produces no SOx, complying that way with the modern regula┬¡
tions that restricts the sulphur content of fuel oil used on ships operating outside of
authorized emission control zones to 0.50% m/m (mass by mass) since 2020 [12].
LNG must be stored in insulated tanks because of its boiling point, which is ┬¡163
oC at 1 bar of absolute pressure. In addition, its energy density per mass is around
18% higher of the one of Heavy Fuel Oil (HFO), as opposed to its volumetric density,
which is only 43% of HFO. For this reason, the LNG cylindrical storing tanks occupy
three to four times the volume as compared to a typical prismatic fuel oil. LNG is one
of the most environmentally friendly fossil fuels accessible today, since it emits no
SOx and has low particle and NOx emissions, which are lower than those of HFO.
Nonetheless, the methane slip challenge addressed previously must be taken into
account when assessing the CO2reduction potential of LNG as a marine fuel [11].
ÔÇó LPG: LPG stands for Liquified Petroleum Gas and is considered to be any mixture
of propane and butane in liquid form. When those two are mixed together, particular
saturation, pressure and temperature properties can be enabled, depending on the
quantity of each substance in the mixture. Propane has a boiling point of ┬¡42 oC
and butane, depending on its form, has a boiling point of ┬¡0.5 to ┬¡12 oC. Both can be
liquified in moderate pressures and so, their storage needs specific propane tanks
with valves equipped to keep a balanced pressure to below 25 bar. Due to its low
volumetric density, LPG is stored in larger tanks in comparison to those of HFO.
In addition, LPG can emit 16% less CO2 than HFO, resulting in a pollution mitiga┬¡
tion and also a 10┬¡20% of NOx emissions reduction. However, propane and butane
have three to four times the global warming potential of CO2, which has to be taken
into account. Finally, LPG may be obtained from two sources: as a byproduct of oil
and gas production or as a byproduct of oil refinery. It may also be produced from
renewable sources, such as a byproduct of renewable diesel manufacturing [11].
ÔÇó Methanol: Methanol is the most simple alcohol, with the least carbon and the most
hydrogen content of any other liquid fuel. It is a fundamental ingredient for hundreds
of vital chemical compounds used for building materials, it is utilized as a transporta┬¡
tion fuel and it constitutes a hydrogen carrier for fuel cells. It can derive from a variety

such as biomass, CO2 and hydrogen [13]. When generated from natural gas, a

ÔÇó Hydrogen: H2 is manufactured by a variety of methods, including electrolysis of

drogen is utilized in many industrial processes and is now being studied as a viable

a low┬¡emission alternative fuel for maritime transportation. Hydrogen can be stored
in either cryogenic, compressed gas or chemically bound conditions, having a boil┬¡

can get up to 15 times the volume of stored HFO. In addition, it can eliminate GHG
emissions when used in an internal combustion engine, but not NOx, since these
emissions cannot be avoided when combustion is involved. However, all GHG emis┬¡
sions, including NOx, can be totally avoided when hydrogen is combined with marine
fuel cells for vessel energy production. Zero┬¡emission ships are achievable if H2 is
produced using renewable energy or natural gas, along with carbon capture and
storage [11], [13].
1.3
Alternative Technologies
In light of the constant need of GHG emissions reduction in shipping, there are some new┬¡
found technologies, which can either assist in treating the GHG emissions before being
released to the atmosphere, or avoid using fuels that produce them and thus, produce the
power needed for the vessel in an alternative way. Among the new technologies, DNV
has distinguished battery systems, wind┬¡assisted propulsion, and fuel cell systems to have
considerable promise for ship applications [11]. There is also the example of the molten
salt reactor (MSR), which is a type of nuclear reactor in which the primary coolant and/or
fuel is a molten salt solution. However, this technology, although promising, has only
been used two times in operations, rendering it still under discussion [14]. Although the
first two examples of the distinguished DNV technologies are widespread, the newfound
idea of implementing a fuel cell on board can benefit the vessel in two ways. Firstly, the
CO2 emitted by the vessel can be captured and stored and secondly, the electric power
produced by the fuel cell can be used to fulfil various vessel needs. This gives the op┬¡
portunity to study whether the fuel cell produces an adequate amount of electricity, which
might also be able to replace an auxiliary engine.

The identified as most promising methods for ship applications according to DNV [11] are
the following:
ÔÇó Batteries: Batteries enable the direct storage of electrical energy for propulsion,
developing this way several opportunities to enhance the power system. In general,
systems that use batteries are known to be more controllable and easier optimized,
as well as safer and more fuel efficient [11]. Recent advancements in battery tech┬¡
nology, as well as reduced costs as a result of rising global demand for batteries,
make this technology appealing to shipping. Fully electric ships offer a significant
advancement in power system design, although they are currently only practical in
limited applications, such as ferries and short┬¡sea transport [13]. A vesselÔÇÖs abil┬¡
ity to operate entirely on electricity is often restricted by the size or expense of the
requisite battery installation. As the environmental impact is concerned, batteries
produce zero emissions when used for ship operation, however emissions are pro┬¡
duced when they are manufactured, which renders them as controversial [11]. Fur┬¡
thermore, lithium┬¡ion battery recycling has shown to be practical, with various firms
offering this service, resulting in a reduction of the environmental footprint. The em┬¡
phasis lies on the recovery of aluminium and copper, however the rather low intake
of recycled or used batteries limits the full potential of such recoveries [13].
ÔÇó Wind┬¡assisted propulsion: Apart from human strength, wind has been the major
energy source, utilized to drive ships for thousands of years, since it is an endless
source of energy. As efforts to reduce pollution and minimize climate change grow,
wind┬¡assisted propulsion is now recognized as a promising means of lowering ship
fossil fuel consumption and the commercial shipping industry is taking a fresh look
at wind power, at least as a supporting role. There is currently practical experience
with two methods, namely the kites and the Flettner rotors. The kite method gen┬¡
erates an aerodynamic force on the ship due to the wind, which results in a pulling
force that requires less engine power consumption by the vessel. On the other
hand, the Flettner rotors consist of vertical cylinders that spin and produce lift due to
the wind, forming this way aerodynamic thrust (Magnus effect). There are no direct
fuel expenditures associated with using wind┬¡assisted propulsion. However, most
systems require an additional source of energy in order to operate. For example,
before their employment, kites have to be inflated, launched, controlled and also
after their operation, they need to be retracted, using external devices. In the case
of Flettner rotors, the aerodynamic thrust force can be only generated when the
auxiliary motors are turned on. In the aforementioned circumstances, the quantity
of energy required for the operation of the wind┬¡assisted methods is insignificant
in comparison to the power produced by the external and auxiliary machines [11].
There is an additional wind┬¡assisted propulsion project announced to be available
in 2024, called the ÔÇØAquas EkranoplanÔÇØ vessel developed by RDC aqualines, which
is based on fixed┬¡wing technology. According to this technology, the vessel gains
lift from the movement of air across aerodynamically constructed wings, which are
firmly connected to its shaft, thus giving the ability to the ship to float over the water
surface. However, its capacity is low and it still needs to be tested and evaluated
in the near future before objective conclusions can be drawn about this initiative [15].
ÔÇó Fuel cells: Through electrochemical oxidation, fuel cells turn the chemical energy
stored in a fuel directly into electrical and heat energy, which can then be used for

propulsion or other on┬¡board purposes. For their chemical reaction, all fuel cells
require a hydrogen┬¡rich fuel. Aside from pure hydrogen, the reformers in fuel cells
are used to convert different fuels such as natural gas, methanol, or diesel into
hydrogen┬¡rich fuel for the cells. Fuel cells outperform conventional engines in terms

chemical mechanism, operating temperature, efficiency, and fuel compatibility. Al┬¡

shipÔÇÖs primary engine. However, there are other types of fuel cells, such as the

(LT┬¡PEMFC) and the High┬¡Temperature Proton Exchange Membrane fuel cell (HT┬¡
PEMFC), which only capture the CO2 from the fuel used for their operation [11].

1.4
Objectives
The aim of this project is the introduction of Molten Carbonate Fuel Cells (MCFC) on board
of a ship, as a part of its propulsion system, with the purpose of Carbon Capturing from the
exhaust gas received by the shipÔÇÖs main engine. In addition, sustainable electric power,
as well as technical water will be produced from the process of the CO2 capturing, which
can be used to satisfy a large amount of the vesselÔÇÖs energy needs. Finally, the fuel cell
will be examined regarding its ability to capture the methane in the exhaust gas due to
methane slip in the engine and also its ability to break down a substantial part of NOx in
its process.
For this purpose, the MCFC analysed in the present project is developed by FuelCell
Energy company and the candidate ship to be analyzed is a Stena Bulk Suezmax tanker.
Since a standard Suezmax tanker operating in HFO requires a scrubbing system for the
exhaust gas prior to entering the MCFC, an LNG┬¡fueled version of this tanker, including
both propulsion and fuel cell running on LNG, is preferably considered. The choice of this
vessel is done primarily due to its size, which will provide larger space for the capture
system and liquid storage of the captured CO2 to be accommodated, while giving the
possibility to evaluate the methane slip challenge due to the LNG fuel. The Suezmax is
also preferred due to its large daily consumption, which makes the return of investment
for the MCFC quicker.
During this project, a detailed analysis of the thermal and electrical energy produced by the
MCFC was performed, by utilising the 1st Law of Thermodynamics, and more specifically
the Law of Conservation of Energy. As a consequence of this, a Waste Heat Recovery ÔÇô
Energy Balance Analysis was applied for the calculation of the required energy needed for
the heating and cooling processes of the MCFC, as well as the available energy entering
and exiting the fuel cell stack. Based on this analysis, the dimensioning of the MCFC
could be achieved and a proposal of layout and piping to fit the fuel cell into the engine
room could be accomplished. In addition, the possibility of NOx removing and methane
emission reducing were evaluated, as well as the replacement of an auxiliary engine by
the fuel cell was considered. Finally, the annual electric energy production of the MCFC
system, the annual CO2 removal rate, as well as the annual OPEX savings from the CO2

GHG Emissions Health and Environmental Effects
The direct GHG emissions, such as CO2, CH4, N2O, the indirect GHG emissions, such
as NOx, NMVOCs, SO2 and CO, but also the large group of chemicals SOx, are caused
by human factors and are key contributors to environmental degradation [9]. The direct
greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4 and N2O) originate from sources that are created entirely by
the so called reporting entity, whereas the indirect greenhouse gasses (NOx, NMVOCs,
SO2 and CO) do not directly originate from the reporting entity, but are in some way con┬¡
nected to it [7]. Nevertheless, the three principal GHG emissions in maritime transporta┬¡
tion are the CO2, CH4 and NOx. In addition, SOx chemicals are emitted during maritime
operations and thus, they are also worth of mentioning.
More specifically, the three key GHG emissions, as well as the SOx chemical group are
analysed below [9], [16]:
ÔÇó CO2: Carbon dioxide emissions derive from both natural and anthropogenic sources
and are accounting for approximately 76% of the wold emissions. More specifically,
the sea contains approximately 50 times more CO2 than the amount of the atmo┬¡
sphere, and also 19 times more CO2 than the one absorbed by the terrestrial bio┬¡
sphere [17]. Decomposition, ocean discharge, and respiration are some examples
of natural sources. However, most human sources, including cement manufacture
(4%), deforestation (9%) and fossil fuels combustion (coal, oil, natural gas) (87%),
cause critical impacts to the environment and to human health [18]. CO2 is accumu┬¡
lated into the atmosphere, forcing this way climate changes and global warming by
changing the energy balance of earth and space. This phenomenon happens be┬¡
cause CO2 captures the reflected solar energy, in other words the infrared radiation,
and does not allow their return to space, causing this way the temperature to rise.
As a result, the glaciers and sea ice are melting, the sea levels are rising, agricul┬¡
tural and animal species are getting extinct and intense weather conditions occur.
Some of the human health effects of carbon dioxide can be nausea, dyspnea, heart
dysfunctions, eye and skin damages, as well as death when in high concentrations.
ÔÇó CH4: Methane is a natural gas, which is used as a burning fossil fuel and is the
second most prevalent anthropogenic GHG emission after CO2, accounting for ap┬¡
proximately 20% of world emissions [19]. When CH4 is produced from a non┬¡fossil
source, it can even be beneficial for the environment, as it can reduce the carbon in
the air. However, when it is produced by human activity, such as oil and natural gas
combustion, landfills and agricultural activities, coal mining etc., it can widely affect

ÔÇó The reformer output is introduced in the anode of the SOFC, while air is introduced

ÔÇó The O2 of the air turns into Oxide ions, through its reaction with the electrons inserted

4.2.4
Possibility of NOx and Methane Slip Reduction
One of the additional benefits of installing a MCFC on board, except for CO2 capture and
water and electric power production, is the NOx and CH4 slip reduction it promises.
As NOx is concerned, it is claimed by [33] that the MCFC is capable of breaking down NOx
components in exhaust gas, by using as elimination mechanism the ability of the NOx to
react with CO2ÔêÆ
3
and O2 at the cathode, with the aim of creating nitrates and nitrites that
transfer through the electrolyte to the anode side, via electrochemical reactions. In turn,
at the anode, the nitrates and nitrites react with H2 and CO2, creating in that way inert N2.
Something worth mentioning is that at high NOx concentrations of more than 200 ppm as
cathode input, fuel cell tests run by [33] showed a more than 70% NOx elimination. How┬¡
ever, these fuel cell test concerned a power plant and thus, proper experiments should be
run in order to accurately estimate the NOx reduction of the exhaust gases of a vessel.
As CH4 is concerned, it is supported that a big amount of the methane in methane slip,
as estimated in table 4.2, will be reformed under the high exhaust gas temperature at the
cathode of the MCFC, in the same manner as it is reformed at the anode side. The high
temperatures of 600 ┬¡ 700 oC, as well as the low pressure of 1 bar activate the internal
reforming reaction, which decomposes the CH4 into H2 and ethane C2H6. Consequently,
the presence of water droplets in the exhaust gases produces CO2ÔêÆ
3 , which also cross
the electrolyte and pass to the anode side in order to become CO2 and thus be captured.
However, in order to accurately estimate the methane slip removal rate of the MCFC,
proper experiments should be run.

4.5.2
Layout and Piping System Proposal
On the next page, a portrayal of the layout and piping system of the MCFC in the engine
room can be displayed, along with the principal processes of Carbon Capture and Stor┬¡
age. The portrayal was attempted for the second approach of LNG cooling, namely CO2
through the Fuel Gas Supply System (FGSS) approach fig. 4.6, after the reconsideration
of the first attempt in fig. 3.1 in section 3.3, which portrayed the first approach of LNG
cooling, namely the LNG through the Cryogenic CO2 Separation / Liquification system.
The second approach of the LNG cooling was selected since it was proven to be more
efficient and includes the same layout and routes of all other system inside the engine
room, except for the installation of the cryogenic CO2 separation and liquification system.
As described before, in fig. 4.6, the exhaust gas (orange line) exits the engine and gets
heated up in the catalytic oxidiser, with the aim of reaching 600 oC and subsequently
entering the cathode side of the MCFC. The CO2 passes to the anode side of the MCFC
(light green line) in the form of CO2ÔêÆ
3 , and then exits the anode side as a mixture of H2O,
CO2 and H2 residue. The CO2 anode exhaust mixture follows a cooling, liquification and
compression process, in order to be later stored in the CO2 storage tank.
More specifically, the anode exhaust cooling process includes the water recovery (blue
line) from the exhaust mixture, which enters a heat recovery unit and assists in steam
production (brown line). This steam enters the mixer and is combined with LNG (red
line), with the aim of entering the anode side of the fuel cell. This water recovery takes
place with the assistance of fresh water as a cooling mean (light blue line). After the
anode exhaust cooling, the CO2 mixture passes through the cryogenic liquification and
separation system, which is placed this time inside the FGSS, with the aim of the CO2
to be liquified by the cryogenic LNG (red line) simultaneously as the LNG is converted
to NG in the vaporizer system of the FGSS. In this way, to warm CO2 will also assist in
the heating of LNG and thus requiring less power for the LNG vaporization. The CO2 is
liquefied by compression at 10 bar and by the cryogenic power offered by the LNG (red
line) at ┬¡50 oC and thus separated from the residual exhaust (pink line), which includes H2
and CO. The residual exhaust is then recycled back to the catalytic oxidizer and in turn in
the MCFC, so that the residual CO can be removed. The H2 is believed to be minimal in
the residual exhaust mix, however, if the amount is respectable, the H2 can be recycled
back to the anode side of the fuel cell and assist in water production. Consequently, the
CO2 final compression process takes place, where the pressure of liquid CO2 is increased
by a pump, in order for it to be stored in the CO2 storage tank.
Simultaneously, the CO2 and NOx depleted exhaust (dark green line) exits the cathode
side and is lead to the pre┬¡heat unit, with the aim of assisting with the heat up of the LNG
that enters the anode (red line). Subsequently, the CO2 and NOx depleted exhaust enters
a heat recovery unit, heats up the water recovered in order for the steam to be produced
and is then released to the atmosphere. Before being released, the heat energy carried
after heating the water is turned in electric power, which can be used for other vessel
purposes.

Conclusions
The projectÔÇÖs initiative is centered on the introduction of a MCFC on board a vessel as
a part of its propulsion system, aiming to the CO2 capturing of the exhaust gas received
by its main engine. For this reason, the vessel ownerÔÇÖs (Stena Bulk) technical depart┬¡
ment (Stena Teknik) provided appropriate data, as well as relevant information concern┬¡
ing the engine manufacturer (MAN ES) and fuel cell manufacturer (FuelCell Energy) was
retrieved from public literature, which were utilized in performing a Waste Heat Recov┬¡
ery ┬¡ Energy Balance Analysis and determining the required energy for the MCFCÔÇÖs and
capture and liquification systemsÔÇÖ heating and cooling processes, the enthalpy change of
these procedures, as well as the available energy entering and exiting the fuel cell stack.
In addition, two alternative approaches were proposed and compared, concerning the
cooling and liquification of the CO2, namely the LNG passing through the Cryogenic CO2
Separation / Liquification system and the CO2 mixture passing through the Fuel Gas Sup┬¡
ply System (FGSS). Furthermore, based on this analysis, the carbon removal rate and the
different MCFC application options, such as the replacement of an Auxiliary Engine by the
electric power produced by the fuel cell and its NOx and methane slip reduction ability,
were determined. Moreover, the dimensioning of the MCFC, as well as a proposal of
layout and piping system were depicted in two flowcharts. Finally, OPEX savings from
the MCFC implementation were estimated. This projectÔÇÖs concluding remarks may be
summarized below:
ÔÇó The processes which consume energy during their operation are the CO2 compres┬¡
sor, the pump which guides the fresh water to the anode exhaust cooling system,
as well as the pump, which circulates the water recovered to the HRU for steam
production.
ÔÇó The processes which produce energy during their operation are the MCFC, the fresh
water, which has obtained thermal energy by cooling the CO2, as well as the CO2
depleted exhaust, which is still left with a lot of energy after heating the mixture of
NG and steam and is capable of offering a great amount of electric power for the
vessel purposes.
ÔÇó As the average power of the Suezmax vessel increases in the laden condition, so
does the CO2 production, namely 3663.7 kg/h, and thus, the MCFC maximum power
production grows as well, namely at 1375 kW. This leads to an increase of the Total
Electric Power produced at around 2.8 MW. In addition, by an increase in the aver┬¡
age power of the Suezmax vessel, there is a simultaneous increase in the treated
CO2 rate.
ÔÇó The 1st Approach concerning the LNG passing through the Cryogenic CO2 Sepa┬¡
ration / Liquification system is proved to provide with less cryogenic power that the
2nd Approach concerning the CO2 mixture passing through the Fuel Gas Supply
System (FGSS). As a result, the treated CO2 rate is significantly greater in the 2nd
Approach, namely at 53% for laden condition, rather than the 1st Approach, which
is 16%.
ÔÇó A sensitivity analysis including both pressure and temperature cases of installing
exchangeable ISO containers for the CO2 storage showed a tremendous increase
in treated CO2 rates. However, the 39 bar and ┬¡10 oC case of ISO containers at

Future Considerations
This chapter is a supplementary step to improve the MCFC and the capture and liquifica┬¡
tion systemsÔÇÖ working processes on board. Extensive studies might assist in increasing
the knowledge of the functioning, the requirements and the additions that the aforemen┬¡
tioned processes necessitate. The following are some suggestions to be utilised for future
work:
ÔÇó Real life experiments on a small or large scale to exactly calculate the performance
data, the emissions, as well as the treated CO2 rate. In addition, these experiments
can provide the real numbers of NOx and CH4 slip reduction during the MCFC op┬¡
eration.
ÔÇó Experiments must take place in order to determine the quantity of the H2 in the resid┬¡
ual exhaust fuel separated from the CO2 at the cryogenic liquification process. This
will indicate whether the amount of H2 is of great value to be recycled as additional
fuel back to the anode of the MCFC or be used for other vessel purposes.
ÔÇó A study can be conducted on whether the exhaust gases from the auxiliary engines
and the boiler can be also inserted to the MCFC for CO2 capture, when in use.
ÔÇó Since the information concerning the MCFC was restricted, additional study can be
conducted to examine the limitations of residence time and exhaust gas speed in
the fuel cell, which determine the size and the amount of exhaust gas that can be
inserted to the fuel cell. The aim of this study is to define the precise values of these
two parameters and will lead to a more accurate dimensionalization of the MCFC.
ÔÇó Techno┬¡econ analysis, with the aim of discovering whether the MCFC on board is
a financially beneficial concept for the shipping company. In addition, a Life Cycle
Cost Analysis (LCCA) can determine if this new idea is cost efficient for retro┬¡fit or
new building vessels in the long run.
ÔÇó A risk assessment should be conducted, in order to identify possible risks and eval┬¡
uate the possibility of danger if one of the these risks emerges. Some suggestions
to mitigate the risks may include the exhaust gas system optimisation in order to
minimise back pressure, dead weight and light weight increase/impact considera┬¡
tion, as well as electric energy response of the battery when the auxiliary engine is
switched off or when there is unstable electric power.
ÔÇó Test to learn if the use of MCFC can assist in reducing the cargo VOCs (Volatile or┬¡
ganic compounds) in crude carriers, which have both short┬¡ and long┬¡term negative
health consequences, by transferring them from the cargo area to the anode side
of the fuel cell for internal reforming.
