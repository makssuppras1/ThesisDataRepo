Abstract
The effect of flow transients on the response of an Airborne Wind Energy System (AWES) was
investigated using a virtual crosswind flight model. The study aimed to improve understand-
ing of the interactions between unsteady atmospheric flows and AWES. Different sampling
techniques, helical and centreline, were compared to assess their ability to capture turbulence
encountered by the AWES. Helical sampling provided a more representative measure, while
the low coherence between the two methods indicated that centreline sampling fails to ad-
equately reflect the turbulence experienced by the system. It was concluded, that without
pre-configuration, a typical profiling Lidar can not accurately represent the transients experi-
enced by an AWES system. To reflect the system’s physical averaging of rapid fluctuations, a
second-order Butterworth low-pass filter was applied. A 0.3 Hz cut-off frequency was found to
best capture the systems behaviour, while minimising noise. Acceleration calculation methods,
using Lidar wind velocity data, were evaluated. It was shown that finite difference methods tend
to underestimate P90 acceleration values compared to Fourier based calculations. The influence
of base period on P90 accelerations was also examined, with results showing minimal impact on
the overall distribution. The AWES response to steady and unsteady wind conditions, varying
turbulence intensity (TI), and flow accelerations was analysed. The stream-wise wind velocity
component was established to have the greatest impact on apparent wind velocity, angle of
attack (AoA), and lift-to-drag ratio. Increased TI led to higher AoA and aerodynamic forces,
with flow acceleration events causing significant force spikes and critical AoA exceedances. The
need for active control strategies during flow acceleration events, at moderate to high wind
velocities, was established. Finally, turbulence experienced along helical and figure-of-8 flight
paths was compared, showing similar aerodynamic effects but differing temporal patterns.

1.1.2
Power Production
As power production is central to AWES, the typical operational cycle used to generate elec-
tricity will be briefly outlined. Ground generation AWES usually operate in a pumping cycle
for power production, Figure (1.1). This cycle has three main phases: reel out, reel in and
transition phases [63]. During the reel out phase, the AWES flies in a pattern crosswind, which
maximises the tension force, and therefore energy generation. This phase continues until the
tether reaches its maximum length at which point the AWES is steered towards the xwzw
plane. At this stage power generation has ceased and tether length remains constant. Before
fully reaching the xwzw plane, the system transitions to the reel in phase, during which the
tether is retracted. This process consumes energy, as the ground station drum must counter the
AWES’s pulling force. However, the energy consumed during retraction is significantly lower
than the energy produced during reel-out phase, though it is necessary to keep the system under
tension. Once the tether is retracted to its minimum length the AWES is directed towards the
xw axis. Before reaching this axis, the crosswind manoeuvrers are reinitiated, restarting the
power generation cycle.

1.2.1
Velocity measurement for the system
Accurate velocity measurement is critical for the control, eﬀiciency, and safety of AWES. Know-
ing the velocity of the AWES relative to the surrounding air and the ground, enables precise
flight path control, power estimation, and aerodynamic analysis. Several techniques are avail-
able for velocity measurement, each with different advantages and limitations depending on

Pitot-Static Tubes
A pitot-static tube is a device which can be used to measure airspeed. It operates by sampling
the local stagnation pressure P0 at the central hole, and the local static pressure P from multiple
holes positioned circumferentially around the probe body. P is usually taken as the mean of the
measurements from these holes. Using Bernoulli’s equation the local mean velocity U parallel
to the probe can then be determined, where ρ represents the air density, Equation (1.3).

Some advantages of pitot tubes over other measurement techniques include their temperature-
insensitivity, robustness and minimal requirement for calibration. However, pitot tubes are
less effective at low velocities or in supersonic flows. Additionally, clogging can compromise
measurement accuracy.
Global Positioning System
Global Positioning System (GPS) is a satellite-based navigation system, which can be used
to measure velocity relative to the Earth’s fixed coordinate system.
It operates through a
system of satellites orbiting the Earth, which continuously transmit signals containing their
position and time. Ground control stations monitor these satellites, ensuring their accuracy
and updating their orbital data. A GPS receiver detects the signals from the satellites. As the
receiver moves the frequency of these signals will shift, due to the Doppler effect. By analysing
these shifts the receiver can calculate its velocity in three dimensions.
1.2.2
Reference Frames
Understanding reference frames is imperative in flight dynamics as they provide a basis for
analysing the motion of an aerial system. A common way to illustrate the concept of reference
frames is to imagine a person in an airplane watching a movie on the screen in front of them [5].
If the airplane banks (tilts), the person may feel an acceleration, but the screen will appear to
remain in the same position relative to them. This is because both the person and the screen
are in the same reference frame, the airplane’s reference frame. However, from the perspective
of an observer on Earth, the airplane’s banking motion causes a change in the orientation of
both the person and the screen. While frames of reference are arbitrary, it is convenient to use
common ones.
1.2.3
Common Reference Frames
First one needs to define a coordinate system to work with. The North-East-Down system
(NED) is common for aircraft [36]. Then a reference frame is needed, attached to the earth.
Since an AWES device operates over a small area, the curvature of the earth can be neglected.
It is also assumed that the atmosphere does not move relative to the earth [29]. Thus, a plane
tangent to the surface of the earth is defined and called the Local Tangent Plane (LTP). In
the NED convention, the north axis will point north towards the local meridian and the east
axis will point east towards the local parallel. The down axis is perpendicular to the other two
and points towards the earth. Figure (1.2). The origin of the NED frame can be chosen at any
point on the surface.

Wind and Atmospheric Conditions
1.3.1
Turbulence
Turbulence is irregular and chaotic fluctuations in wind speed and direction. In the atmospheric
boundary layer (ABL), turbulence is primarily determined by surface friction and surface heat
flux [56]. For wind energy engineering, turbulence intensity is important in structural design
and aerodynamic loads calculation. It’s defined as mean wind speed (¯u) over the standard
deviation of wind speed (σu): I = σu
¯u [46]. A 10-minute averaging period is typically used to
calculate the mean wind speed.
Although this equation proposes that turbulence intensity is not directly linked to mean wind
speed, turbulence intensity generally declines as wind speed increases [20]. This is mainly due
==PAGE:22==
INTRODUCTION
8
to the relative increase in shear production with speed, in comparison to other transport terms
[31]. This is also partly due to the small denominator. However, this pattern differs offshore,
where turbulence tends to intensify at higher wind speeds due to wave induced mechanical
turbulence [7]. As noted previously, turbulence also depends on surface heat flux, which affects
the stability of the ABL. Three main stability classes exist: stable, neutral and unstable.
Gryning et al. [22] further subdivide these into seven classes based on Obukhov length, Table
(1.1). Kelly and Gryning simplified and clarified the definition of stability classes by proposing
the use of the inverse Obukhov length, L−1 [34]. This approach was motivated by two main
reasons.
Firstly, L−1 approaches zero when there is zero heat flux, and secondly, it offers
greater analytical convenience. Turbulence intensity is typically greater in unstable conditions,
due to increased buoyancy, and weaker in stable conditions. Stability also affects the vertical
homogeneity of the boundary layer. During stable conditions, the boundary layer experiences
limited mixing, causing turbulence intensity to remain uneven [7].

1.3.2
Flow accelerations
Wind gusts
Wind gusts are rapid changes in wind speed [68]. Some literature [27], along with the IEC61400-
1 standard [13], define gusts in terms of a maximum wind speed for a specific duration. However,
in this study gusts are defined as accelerations, similar to Kelly [32]. Wind gusts form due to
turbulent eddies and the outflow of downbursts. Their intensity depends on several factors,
including surface roughness, orography, stability and mesoscale climate [28]. Wind gusts are
not consistently defined, with sampling average and moving average windows differing between
research fields [28] [66]. Wind gusts not only pose risks to AWES but also to traditional HAWT,
aviation, and harbour shipping [58] [53] [24]. The perception of wind gusts varies based on the
observers perspective. An AWES moving upwind encounters shorter steeper gusts, while one
moving downwind would experience more gradual gusts [7]. Thus, the position of the AWES
along its flight path is crucial in determining the loads it will experience.
Wind ramps
Wind ramps are events characterised by a significant increase in wind speed over a period of
seconds or minutes. They generally have a larger rise time than wind gusts and are often
related to the passage of cold fronts [44], though they can be driven by numerous different
meteorological processes [26]. Two base quantities are associated with wind ramps: rise time
∆t and ramp amplitude ∆U [33]. These can be used to describe the ramp acceleration ∆U
∆t
which has a greater impact on the loads than each quantity individually [25].
1.3.3
Lidar
Lidar stands for “Light Detection and Ranging”. The types of lidar used in wind energy are
usually based on the Doppler shift, which describes the change in the frequency of a wave due
==PAGE:24==
INTRODUCTION
10
to the relative motion of source and observer. The lidar device emits laser beams which are
backscattered by molecules or aerosol particles. The backscattered light can be detected and
analysed by measuring the Doppler shift, which allows the calculation of horizontal wind speed
and direction at each measurement height [17]. Doppler lidar can either emit their beam in a
pulsed or continuous wave approach.
Lidar systems can also vary based on their profiling approach. Horizontal profiling lidar scan
at a fixed altitude to collect data over a broad area. On the other hand, vertical profiling
lidar capture data by scanning in the vertical plane, providing height-specific measurements.
Finally, scanning lidar utilise a rotating or oscillating mirror to generate a 3D view. A vertical
profiling lidar, ZX300, was used in this study. It worked by emitting a continuous wave laser
beam at a selected height, covering 50 different azimuth angles [39]. For each height, the system
performed a complete rotation in 1 second, after which a new height could be selected, Figure
(1.7). Up to 10 heights could be configured, in addition to the pre-fixed height of 38 m [64].
Figure 1.7: Schematic of continuous wave lidar [50].
Carrier to Noise Ratio (CNR) is used to assess the quality of the signal received from a lidar.
It compares the returned signal to the level of noise [23]. Low CNR can cause wind speed
uncertainty to become too large, rendering the data unreliable [21]. Four parameters most
significantly impact CNR: aerosol backscatter, turbulence, humidity and precipitation [2]. Re-
garding aerosol backscatter this typically decreases with altitude, as the primary source of
aerosols is the earth’s surface [41]. Cloud cover can also affect the backscatter as it’s increased
in the lower part of the cloud and thus, reduced at higher altitudes [54].
Lidar can have bias in its measurement of wind speed. Unlike a cup anemometer which calcu-
lates a scalar average of wind speed, lidar calculates a vector average of the velocity components
[11]. This causes lidar to typically measure slightly lower wind speeds compared to the cup
anemometer. Homodyne detection also introduces uncertainty due to 180-degree ambiguity in
the measured wind direction. This ambiguity can be corrected by using external references or
heterodyne detection systems.

1.4.1
Spectral theory
Spectral theory is an important tool in wind energy. The spectrum represents the amount of
kinetic energy contained in wind speed variations, at a given frequency. The high frequency
part of the spectrum corresponds to turbulence, while the low frequency part is related to
fluctuations of the mean wind. Between the two maxima there is a local minimum called the
spectral gap [2], which is from approximately 10 minutes up to several hours. This gap exists, as
on an hourly timescale wind speed typically remains relatively stable under normal conditions.
Spectral theory is built on the concept of the covariance function Rxy(τ), Equation (1.17).
Here τ is the time lag and E is the expectation value. This function describes the relationship
between two random variables, i.e. how they change together over time. Two types of covariance
exist: auto-covariance and cross-covariance. Auto-covariance measures how a signal is related
to itself, while cross-covariance measures the relationship between two different signals.
RXY (τ) = E [(X(t) −E[X(t)])(Y (t + τ) −E[Y (t + τ)])]
(1.17)

(FT) of the covariance function, Equation (1.18). Here ω is the angular frequency and i is the
imaginary unit. This result is important, as it allows the transformation of a signal from the
time domain into the frequency domain and vice versa, using the Inverse Fourier Transform
(IFT).

Calculating acceleration from velocity
There are two main ways to calculate acceleration from velocity time series: finite differences

Filtering
When analysing time series and spectra it is important to filter signals to remove unwanted
noise, and to smooth out variations that occur faster than the system can effectively respond to.
As the well-known adage in computer science puts it: “garbage in, garbage out” - underscoring
the importance of pre-processing to ensure meaningful results. Two common filters used are
low-pass and high-pass filters. As the name suggests, low-pass filters allow low frequency signals
to pass through, while attenuating high frequency signals. High-pass filters do the opposite,
Figure (1.8). Both filters have a cut-off frequency fc which is defined as the point where the
filter starts to attenuate the signal. The cut-off frequency chosen is dependent on the engineer’s
specific requirements, however it must always be below the Nyquist frequency. This is defined
as half the sampling rate of a discrete time signal, and is the highest frequency that can be
accurately represented in a sampled signal.

1.5
Problem Setting
1.5.1
Gap identification
Flow accelerations pose a challenge to AWES as they increase the risk of failure due to extreme
loads and affect the stability of the system in crosswind flight. Both of these challenges can be
addressed by adjusting the angle of attack, using control systems. However, if this control is
not active, the device sacrifices eﬀiciency, making it less profitable, and it may also experience
reduced reliability. To allow for active control, it is crucial to study these flow accelerations, to
identify which ones impact the system and to understand the extent of their impact. Although
there is some research on flow accelerations for HAWT [32], to the best of the author’s knowledge
there is minimal research on their effect on AWES. Due to their smaller size and flight paths,
AWES encounter and are affected differently by turbulent structures, and exhibit a unique
response, highlighting the need for research in this area.

Stall
Stall is a sudden reduction in lift when an airfoil reaches a critical angle of attack. In AWES,
stall can be particularly dangerous as it compromises flight stability and safety. Kitemill’s
KM1 system has a critical angle of attack of approximately 10◦. Although stall is immediate,
its effect on the system develops over time. Therefore, it was defined that the AWES would
only be considered stalled if the critical angle of attack was maintained for 2 second or longer.
2.1.2
Derivation of Crosswind Flight
An aerodynamic model was developed, to predict the forces on the AWES and its dynamics
during crosswind flight. An AWES has three core components: the ground station, the tether
and the airborne device. In this model, the ground station was assumed to have no effect on
the atmospheric flow and is thus, neglected. The tether was considered straight. Tether drag
was neglected since it has negligible effect on aerodynamic forces, which were the primary focus
of the analysis. The wing was modelled as a rigid body point mass. For simplicity, a steady-
state solution was investigated, meaning the external forces acting on the kite are considered
in equilibrium. The model was a 3 degree of freedom model, only considering translational
motion.

2.3.3
Low-Pass Filtering: Cut-off Frequencies
Using the sampling method described in Section (2.3.2), the AWES is assumed to be a point-
like structure. In reality, the movement of air at different points on the AWES is not perfectly
correlated and high frequency variations in wind speed do not instantly change drag and lift
==PAGE:43==
METHODOLOGY
29
forces [6]. To account for this, a second order (O2) Butterworth low-pass filter was applied
to the sampled wind field, suppressing high-frequency components that the AWES physically
averages due to its mass, path and area. The choice of cut-off frequency is important as its
selection has a direct impact on the characteristics of the filtered signal and consequently, the
aerodynamic response of the system. Thus, a range of cut-off frequencies was investigated using
McWilliams et al.’s “turbulent_box_generation_example” [42].
The spectra for the sampled u-component are shown in Figure (2.14). Both the raw spectra and
the smoothed spectra (using logarithmic binning and averaging) are presented. The spectra
were multiplied by frequency so that broad trends peaks in the energy distribution were easier
to identify. The unfiltered wind field displays a distinct spectral peak at approximately 0.1
Hz, indicating dominant turbulent structures in the inflow. Another smaller peak can be seen
just around0.2 Hz. It is evident that reducing the cut-off frequency progressively attenuates
high frequency content. While this reduces noise, it may also eliminate important features
that affect the AWES and are critical for effective system modelling. Integrating the unfiltered
spectrum provides information about the energy distribution. 78% of the energy content lies
below 0.3 Hz, with 42% lying in the 0.1–0.3 Hz band. Thus, applying a low-pass filter with a
cut-off frequency of 0.3 Hz closely approximates the filtering effect corresponding to an AWES
response time of ∼5 s, and it retains the majority of wind dynamics while suppressing high-
frequency noise. To demonstrate that the filters are functioning as intended, one can plot the
coherence between the unfiltered spectrum and the filtered spectra, as shown in Figure (2.15).
The coherence was calculated by dividing the time series into segments of length 256. A drop-off
in coherence at the chosen cut-off frequency confirms the filter’s effect.

sampling were applied to the Mann box and compared. The helical sampling was based on
Equation (2.27), while the centreline sampling was along the x-direction at the centre of the y-z

progression, allowing them to sample corresponding x-positions simultaneously for consistent

Lidar Data Analysis
The initial approach for this study was to use concurrent Lidar and AWES data to assess how
the device responded to transients. However, due to data unavailability, a modelling approach
was instead adopted. Despite this, some of the preliminary work carried out as part of the
original approach proved valuable to the final study. Thus, it is discussed here. In this chapter,
Lidar data is used to investigate the effect of different calculation methods and base periods on
P90 values.
3.1
Effect of Acceleration Calculation Method and Base
Period on P90 Values
Initially, to extract accelerations from the velocity time series two different methods were con-
sidered: the finite difference method and calculating accelerations directly in Fourier space.
Both methods are described in detail in Section (1.4.2). To compare the two acceleration cal-
culation methods, Lidar measurements from two different days were analysed. For both cases,
P90 of accelerations were computed using a base period of 3 minutes. From Figure (3.1) & (3.2),
one can see that using finite differences results in an underestimation of the P90 acceleration
statistics. This behaviour is expected, as the finite difference method is a discrete approx-
imation of the derivative, which acts like a high-pass filter. Therefore, it tends to amplify
high-frequency noise while attenuating or distorting lower-frequency components of the signal.
In contrast, calculating acceleration directly in the Fourier domain provides a more stable and
accurate representation of the signal [32]. Thus, for this report, accelerations will always be
calculated directly in Fourier space.

Response Analysis to Turbulence
In this chapter, it was investigated how the model responds to a range of turbulent inflow
conditions. Three different cases were studied: steady versus unsteady wind, varying turbulence
intensities, and flow accelerations.
4.1
Steady vs Unsteady Wind Conditions
Initially, the model was tested for three steady mean stream-wise wind velocity components
U NED
mean : 9.5 m/s, 12.5 m/s and 15.5 m/s. The other steady velocity components were set as
zero. For each speed, Mann turbulence boxes were generated using filtered parameters from the
dataset for significant acceleration events [42]. Wind speed and directional data, and thus Mann
parameters, were available at two heights: 100 m and 160 m. The dataset at 160 m was used.
This choice was motivated by the fact that the AWES operates at higher altitudes, making it
preferable to use wind speed data measured closer to its operational height. It should be noted,
that for the most extreme events at 160 m, sudden increases in wind speed associated with
acceleration were generally observed to coincide with comparable fluctuations at 100 m [32].
This correlation supports the hypothesis that the ABL depth, during these events, was near
160 m. Therefore, in reality, during these events the AWES would likely be operating either in
the strong stable inversion layer, where turbulence is suppressed, or in the troposphere. From
this dataset the selected boxes had an unscaled turbulence intensity of approximately 0.07. The

converted to the spatial scale. The grid resolution was limited by the computational capacity of
the author’s system but was deemed suﬀicient for the intended level of analysis. The turbulence

acceleration event in Section (4.3) could be captured during sampling. A shorter simulation

Next, the effect of turbulence intensity (TI) on the AWES model was explored. A U NED
mean of 12.5
m/s was selected, and three different TIs were considered: 0.0493, 0.0678, 0.0881. Furthermore,
to study the impact of high TI on the system, the final Mann box was scaled to give a TI of
0.15. Scaling the Mann box introduces challenges because each set of Mann model parameters
correspond to fixed spectral properties and TI. There are two common approaches to scaling
boxes. The IEC recommends scaling the turbulence field directly rather than adjusting the
parameters [12]. While this approach is convenient, it can negatively influence the accuracy of
the model. Therefore, in this report αϵ2/3 was scaled to obtain the chosen TI, as recommended
by Dimitrov et al. [15]. This will only influence the variance but not the shape of the spectrum.
The AoA was calculated following the same approach as before, using the filtered helically
sampled data. The response of the AoA to different TI is shown in Figure (4.7). One can
observe that an increase in TI corresponds to larger fluctuations in AoA. This is expected, as
higher TI leads to greater fluctuations in the apparent wind experienced by the AWES. Since
the AoA is governed by the apparent wind angle, its variability increased accordingly. The
critical AoA is only exceeded in the case of TI = 0.15. In this case, there are four exceedances
lasting 2 seconds or more which may be suﬀicient to induce stall.

To ensure the acceleration event is present, the turbulence box must be constrained based
on the time series of the extreme acceleration event.
Constraining involves modifying the
generated turbulence field so that it matches measured time series data at specific spatial
locations. In this case, only the u and v components were constrained, as time series data
was only available for these components. Since Hipersim requires constraints on all velocity
components, the w component was constrained using the associated raw turbulence.
This
resulted in minor deviations caused by interpolation, and the constraint mechanisms used
by the solver.
This also assumed that w is unaffected by u.
However, in turbulent flows,
non-linear interactions cause energy to transfer between velocity components. Despite this,
the assumption is considered reasonable for a simplified model, where the primary goal is
to approximate general flow behaviour, and the u component has the greatest effect on the
device. The constraints were applied to the centreline of the turbulence box, since the time
series measurements were recorded at this height. Furthermore, since the Mann turbulence
model generates homogeneous turbulence, applying the constraints at the centre of the domain
preserves the statistical symmetry of the field.

The unfiltered parameters capture the turbulence characteristics of the specific event, whereas

4.3.2
Comparing Constraint Locations
The location of the constraints can significantly influence the turbulence experienced by the
AWES. This is because the difference between the unconstrained and constrained fields is
greatest near the constraint and diminishes with distance, approaching zero in regions far from
the constrained area [16]. Thus, improper application of the constraints could cause sampling
to miss a substantial portion of acceleration events.
In Section (4.3.1), the centreline was
constrained since the time series was measured here and this approach preserves statistical
properties of turbulence throughout the box. However, because the box is sampled helically,
there is a case for constraining points on the helix, which should allow the sampling to capture
stronger turbulence variations. Due to grid resolution limitations, the exact top of the helix
did not coincide with a grid point in the turbulence box. Therefore, for comparison purposes,
the grid point closest to the top of the helix (z = 216 m, y = 160 m) was constrained along the
xNED-axis and compared to the standard centreline constraint.
Figures (4.19) and (4.20) show that the influence of constraint location on AoA and Fz is
not constant over time. At certain time stamps, the upper-helix constraint results in higher
values, while at others the centreline produces a stronger response. This variability arises from
the helical nature of the sampling path. Constraining a point on the helix can intermittently
align with regions of intensified turbulence, capturing stronger events, but can also correspond
to regions of weaker turbulence when the measurement point is farther from the constraint.
This demonstrates the importance of selecting appropriate constraint locations when using
Mann turbulence fields to model AWES response. Thus, it is recommended to validate the
modelled wind field and resulting response against real-world measurements from Kitemill’s
system. However, this validation could not be carried out within the scope of this project. Such
validation would help identify the spatial locations, that most accurately represent appropriate
constraint points.

Chapter 6
Conclusions & Future Work
The effect of flow transients on the response of an AWES was investigated in this thesis. A
virtual crosswind flight model was developed to perform the analysis. The main conclusions
are presented below, followed by a brief outline of potential future work.
How do different sampling techniques and filtering, influence the turbulence the
AWES experiences?
Three different sampling strategies were examined: helical, centreline at UNED
kite , and centreline
at UNED
box . It was concluded that helical sampling, despite introducing more noise into the signal
due to its rotational motion, is necessary to accurately capture the turbulence experienced by
the AWES. Compared to both centreline techniques, it better captures high-frequency fluc-
tuations. The low coherence between helical and centreline sampling methods indicates that
centreline sampling is not suﬀiciently representative of the turbulence experienced along a he-
lical flight path. Furthermore, this also suggests that a typical profiling Lidar cannot represent
transients that an AWES encounters, without appropriate data configuration.
To account for the AWES’s physical averaging of high-frequency wind fluctuations, a second-
order Butterworth low-pass filter was applied. A cut-off frequency of 0.3 Hz provided a good
balance between noise reduction and preserving system dynamics. This cut-off frequency would
vary depending on the AWES studied.
How does acceleration calculation method affect P90 values?
Stream-wise acceleration values obtained using the finite difference method were compared to
those calculated directly in Fourier space. It was found that finite differences often underes-
timated P90 accelerations. These findings are consistent with Kelly [32]. Base periods were
determined to also influence P90 values, with shorter periods capturing more transient peaks
and localised turbulence, while longer periods reduced the effect of short-term variations.
What type of wind conditions cause the AWES to exceed critical angles of attack
and what loads will it experience?
The AWES response to three different conditions were studied: steady versus unsteady wind,
varying TI, and flow accelerations.
In the presence of unsteady winds, increases in U NED
mean
led to higher AoA and greater aerodynamic forces. However, for the Mann turbulence boxes
used in this study with at TI of 0.7, this did not cause any relevant critical AoA exceedances.
Furthermore, the spread of stream-wise accelerations experienced by the AWES were seen to
increase with U NED
mean , following the findings of Kelly [32]. It was also found that the stream-wise
72
==PAGE:87==
CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK
73
component of the wind velocity has the greatest effect on the apparent wind velocity of the
AWES, and consequently on the AoA, L
D and Fz.
Increases in TI also caused an increase in AoA, aerodynamic forces, and sampled stream-wise
accelerations. A linear relationship was observed between the standard deviation of stream-
wise accelerations and TI, just like Kelly [32]. Circumferential position was also investigated
for different TIs. Fz was seen to have no clear relationship with circumferential position, while
AoA exhibited larger variance during the descending phase of the loop.
Finally, the effect of flow acceleration events (gusts) on the system were studied, by constraining
Mann turbulence boxes. Constraining at different points was found to vary the magnitude of
the response. Flow accelerations were observed to have a large effect on the AWES, with the
critical AoA exceeded 27 times for both events at 12.5 m/s and 15.5 m/s. For these U NED
mean ,
Fz was found to spike significantly with loads of 2–3 kN above the mean value, corresponding
to a 40–60 % increase. Furthermore, transient events caused a larger spread of stream-wise
accelerations particularly for 12.5 m/s which approximately doubled its standard deviation.
These findings led to the conclusion that sophisticated active control would be necessary for
the system to eﬀiciently deal with events at moderate to high wind speeds, though at lower
wind speeds it would likely be less important. However, further research is needed on this topic.
How does flight path affect the relative velocity fluctuations (‘apparent turbulence’)
the AWES encounters?
The turbulence experienced along a helical flight path was compared to a figure-of-8 flight
path.
Both paths were found to exhibit similar magnitude AoAs and aerodynamic forces,
albeit at different times. Helical motion experienced slightly larger variations in stream-wise
accelerations, though the impact of these on the system were potentially mitigated by the
simplicity of the path.
Future Work
Several open questions remain in this study and the scope for future research is wide in this
area. For instance, developing a more advanced model that accounts for variable pitch and
side-slip could improve the accuracy of the results. Additionally, studying a larger range of
flow accelerations and refining the position of constraints through real-world validation would
be valuable next steps. For practical implementation, it would also be beneficial to incorporate
a virtual lidar system or use real-world data to assess whether flow accelerations can be detected
and responded to in real time, with a re-configuration of the Lidar system. Finally, investigating
how different AWES configurations, such as soft kites, respond to turbulent conditions and flow
accelerations would also provide useful insights.

Tether length was analysed to detect power production. It was assumed that as the tether
increased in length, the device was in a power production phase. On the other hand, when
the tether length was decreasing consistently it was in retraction phase. Take-off and landing
period were also discounted from the analysis. All of this was implemented in Python, and
more detailed description is now provided. This was only done for high wind and a method is
still required for low wind.
