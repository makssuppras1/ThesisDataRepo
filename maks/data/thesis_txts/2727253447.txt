Abstract
This thesis focuses on estimating flood response times using the Detrending Moving-average Cross-correlation
Analysis (DMCA)-based method and identifying and separating time series into single events using the DMCA-
Event Separation Routine (DMCA-ESR) in Vejle catchment in Denmark. The research aims to investigate the
performance of these methods, variations in response time, and the relationship between response time and
catchment characteristics. The study uses streamflow data from the Danish Environmental Protection Agency
and gridded rainfall data from the Danish Meteorological Institute.
The findings indicate that the DMCA-based method effectively estimates the response time in the Vejle catchment,
with response times ranging from 2 to 8 hours depending on the catchment size. Larger catchments generally
have slower response times, indicating a relation between catchment size and flood response. Seasonal variations
in response time were observed, with faster response times during the low baseflow season compared to the high
baseflow season. The study also found that response times during drought periods were faster compared to periods
with steady rain contributions, indicating the influence of antecedent moisture conditions.
However, the performance of the DMCA-ESR method in identifying and separating events showed limitations
and uncertainties, resulting in unrealistic response time estimations. Further refinement and improvement of the
DMCA-ESR method are recommended.
Extrapolating flood response times to ungauged locations for the purpose of producing a national map for Denmark
should be approached with caution due to deviations in response time estimations from year to year and season to
season. The study recommends the need for further research, improvement of methods, and examinations in other
catchments and geographical areas to enhance the accuracy and reliability of flood response time estimations.
In conclusion, this thesis contributes to the understanding of flood response time estimation and event identi-
fication in Vejle catchment. Further research and improvement of the methods are recommended to enhance
their accuracy. Additionally, investigations in different catchments and geographical areas would provide valuable
insights for the validation and generalization of the findings and enable extrapolation to ungauged stations.

Sammenfatning
Denne afhandling fokuserer på at estimere oversvømmelsesrespons tider ved brug af metoden baseret på De-
trending Moving-average Cross-correlation Analysis (DMCA), og at identificere og splitte tidsserier til enkelte
hændelser ved brug af DMCA-Event Separation Routine (DMCA-ESR) for stationer i Vejle opland i Danmark.
Studiet har til formål at undersøge disse metoders ydeevne, variationer i responstid og sammenhængen mellem
responstid og oplandskarakteristika. Undersøgelsen anvender strømningsdata fra Miljøstyrelsen og 1x1 km gitter
nebørs data fra Danmarks Meteorologiske Institut.
Resultaterne indikerer, at den DMCA-baseret metode effektivt estimerer responstiden i Vejle opland, med respon-
stider på mellem 2 og 8 timer afhængig af oplandets størrelse. Større oplande resulterer generelt i langsommere
responstider, hvilket tyder på en sammenhæng mellem oplandets størrelse og oversvømmelsesrespons tid. Sæ-
sonbestemte variationer i responstiden blev observeret, med hurtigere responstider i sæsonen med lav baseflow
sammenlignet med sæsonen med høj baseflow. Det blev også fundet, at responstider i tørkeperioder var hurtigere
sammenlignet med perioder med konstante regnbidrag, hvilket indikerer at responstiden blev påvirket af oplandets
fugtforhold.
DMCA-ESR-metodens ydeevne til at identificere og adskille hændelser viste imidlertid begrænsninger og usikker-
heder, hvilket resulterede i urealistiske estimeringer af responstider.
Yderligere forbedringer af DMCA-ESR-
metoden anbefales.

kort for Danmark bør behandles med forsigtighed på grund af afvigelser i estimaterne af responstiderne fra år til
år og fra sæson til sæson. Studiet anbefaler behovet for yderligere undersøgelser, forbedring af metoder og under-
søgelser i andre oplande og geografiske områder for at forbedre nøjagtigheden og pålideligheden af estimaterne af
oversvømmelsesrespons-tiderne.
Specialet bidrager til forståelsen af estimering af oversvømmelsesresponstid og hændelsesidentifikation i Vejle op-
land. Yderligere forskning og forbedring af de anvendte metoder anbefales for at øge nøjagtigheden. Derudover
ville undersøgelser i forskellige oplande og geografiske områder give nødvendig indsigt til validering og generalis-
ering af resultaterne og muliggøre ekstrapolering til ungauged stationer.

Introduction
In recent years, there has been an increase in the frequency of flooding events, mainly caused by the occurrence of
extreme weather events and the change in land cover and use. [Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2012]. Land cover changes such
as urbanization or deforestation have proven to increase runoff, and the recurring of extreme weather events such
as heavy rainfall is expected to increase due to anthropogenic climate changes [Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2012] [Bauer
et al., 2015].
In July 2021, Germany experienced extreme rainfall in the state of Rhineland-Palatinate which led to severe
flooding in the country. The floods swept away houses and buildings, and more than 200 people lost their lives
[Saadi et al., 2023]. The high precipitation was considered to be a product of climate change, and the events
showed that the developed Western countries are not spared from the effects of climate change Saadi et al. [2023].
Following the flood events in Germany, the Danish state allocated funding to the Danish Meteorological Institute
(DMI) to perform research and develop a comprehensive national flood warning system with the objective to
prevent a similar incident in Denmark [Fonseca, 2023]. By investing in the research and development of a flood
warning system, the Danish state aims to improve preparedness, thereby minimising the risks associated with
flooding, and preventing a similar catastrophe in Denmark [Fonseca, 2023].
When establishing a robust and effective flood warning system, multiple factors must be considered. One of
these factors that could be considered is the catchment response time. The response time represents the time
between a rainfall event within a catchment area to the time when the streamflow reaches its highest level in the
corresponding streams [Giani et al., 2021]. It can give important information about the flood dynamics in the
catchments and help to predict the time of potential flooding.
Estimating the response time is a complex task. The first time the response time was estimated dates back to over
150 years ago initially starting with the rational method [Beven, 2020]. The reason behind the complexity is among
others the numerous definitions of the response time and the multiple factors that can influence the results, and
needs subjective decisions for the estimation. However, in a recent study a new method was developed to estimate
the response time based on the Detrending Moving-average Cross-correlation Analysis (DMCA) originally used
in economics [Giani et al., 2021]. The goal of the DMCA-based method was to create a method that did not
cause any subjective decisions, and which could estimate the response time based on multiple events at a time.
This method is applied in this thesis to estimate the response time in the Vejle catchment. Furthermore, another
method is introduced to identify and separate the time series into rainfall-streamflow events. The method is based
on the DMCA-based method and is called DMCA-Event Separation Routine (DMCA-ESR). The two methods
are combined, using the DMCA-ESR to identify and separate the events, and following the DMCA-based method
is applied to estimate the response time for each event.
In this thesis, the aim is to investigate and estimate flood response times at some gauged locations in Denmark.
The research will be based on the Danish Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA) streamflow data and DMI’s

Background
2.1
Catchment Response Time
Understanding the response time of a system is important when analyzing the hydrological dynamics or associated
flood events [Beven, 2020]. Overall, the response time refers to the duration it takes for the streamflow or water
level to react to a rainfall contribution. Estimating the response time can give valuable knowledge about the
behaviour of the hydrological system in the catchment [Grimaldi et al., 2012]. This section will elaborate on
the different types of response times, and the challenges combined with the estimation of them [Beven, 2020]
[Grimaldi et al., 2012].
When discussing response times in hydrology there can be referred to different types based on the studied system
e.g., catchment response time, channel response time or groundwater response time [Grimaldi et al., 2012]. In
this study, the response time is referring to catchment response time. Hence, it refers to the time it takes for
precipitation to transport through the system and eventually reach the catchment outlet [Giani et al., 2021].
Estimating the response time can be challenging because of the dynamics and complexity of the catchment [Allnutt
et al., 2020]. Hydrological systems are complex as it not only involves the input rainfall but also factors such as
infiltration, evapotranspiration, or surface runoff. It can also be affected by spatial variabilities such as the soil
type, land cover and elevation [Gericke, 2019].
There are multiple different methods to estimate the response time. Some of these are hydrological modelling
which uses models to estimate the response using input data such as rainfall and catchment characteristics, then
there are isotopic tracers which researchers use to trace the water throughout the system [Giani et al., 2021].
Response times can also be estimated by using remote sensing and Geographic Information Systems (GIS), as
these provide valuable information, which can be used to identify the response time [Gericke, 2019].
2.1.1
Defining response time
The time of concentration has had an important role when analyzing catchment responses for more than 150 years,
where the rational method was introduced Beven [2020]. According to the International Glossary of Hydrology, it
is defined as the duration taken for a water particle to travel from the farthest point in the catchment to the outlet
Beven [2020]. However, when estimating the time of concentration it can be challenging and variations in the
definitions are needed, depending on the method. The different definitions and their estimations were examined
in the study "Time of concentration: a paradox in modern hydrology" [Grimaldi et al., 2012], and it was found
that the different estimation methods for time of concentration resulted in estimations that varied by up to 500
% [Grimaldi et al., 2012].
Numerous definitions have been proposed to describe the time of concentration when the rainfall and runoff data
are available for the estimation Allnutt et al. [2020]. Below is a list of definitions taken from Allnutt et al. [2020],
each denoted by a different letter corresponding to the associated illustration in Figure 1:

2.2
Influencing factors on the response time in streams
There is a variety of factors which can influence the response time in streams including catchment characteristics
such as size, slope, or land cover [Gericke, 2019]. In larger catchments, the response time is usually longer, as the
water must travel over larger distances before it accumulates at the outlet [Gericke and Smithers, 2016]. Usually,
the larger catchment will also have higher flow values, as the larger areas can accumulate more rainfall, and thus
more runoff [Gericke and Smithers, 2016]. Catchments with steeper slopes tend to have a faster response time,
as the water flows faster downhill. The size and shape of the stream itself can also impact the response time [Liu
et al., 2021]. In deeper and wider streams, the response time tends to be faster compared to more narrow and
twisting streams, here the response times are usually slower [Liu et al., 2021].
The response time can also be influenced by upstream conditions such as reservoirs, dams, or lakes [Vanderkelen
et al., 2021]. Reservoirs both human-made and natural lakes can store large amounts of water. This can prevent
the flow from increasing after large rainfall events as the reservoirs can retain the water, thereby regulating the
flow [Vanderkelen et al., 2021].
The soil composition plays a crucial role in water flow as it determines the speed of the infiltration and how much
water can be absorbed [Gericke, 2019]. The varying structure of the soil, such as particle sizes and pore space,
has a great influence on the infiltration and storage of water [Schumann, 1998]. Fine textured soils, such as silt
and clay, have smaller pores and low permeability, resulting in slow infiltration. Coarse textured soils, such as
gravels and sand, have larger pores and higher permeability, leading to faster water movement through the soil.
The water holding capacity is generally higher in the finer textured soils. [Schumann, 1998]
The land cover or land use can also influence the response time, as it can either speed up or slow down the runoff
[Gericke, 2019]. In urban areas with large amounts of impervious surfaces, e.g., concrete and roofs, the runoff will
be fast since there is no infiltration [Gericke, 2019]. This can lead to shorter response times, and an increasing risk
of flooding. Opposite, are the vegetated areas such as forests or fields. The natural and vegetated areas decrease
the runoff due to multiple different factors such as infiltration, the surface roughness slowing down and spreading
out the surface runoff, and the interception of plants and vegetation [Gericke, 2019]. Hence, resulting in a higher
response time.

5.1
DMCA-based method
There are, as mentioned in section 2.1, multiple ways to estimate the response time between rainfall and stream-
flow. In A Practical, Objective and Robust Technique to Directly Estimate Catchment Response Time, a study by
Giani et al., a method to estimate the response time based on the Detrending Moving-average Cross-correlation
Analysis was presented. DMCA was originally used in economics to give an understanding of the correlation of
variables at a timescale [Kristoufek, 2014]. Giani et al. were the first to use the method in hydrology. In the
paper Giani et al. tested the method on various catchments to see if the DMCA-based method could be used as
a way of estimating the response time [Giani et al., 2021].
Overall, the DMCA-based method makes the noisy rainfall data comparable with the smooth flow data. Contrary
to other methods estimating the response time, the DMCA-based method can evaluate multiple events at a time
[Giani et al., 2021]. The method can be used on single events but this requires the user to moderate the data.
Using the method for single events will be introduced later in this section.

5.2
DMCA-ESR method
There are multiple ways to identify a rainfall-flow-event but many of them require subjective decisions creating
room for potential misinterpretations which can lead to mistakes. This could be parameters which have to be
defined according to the specific study area, it can require different criteria for the rainfall time series, such as
minimum rain depth, or it can require separation of baseflow [Giani et al., 2022].
In An Objective Time-Series-Analysis Method for Rainfall-Runoff Event Identification, Giani et al. have developed
a method which is able to separate the rainfall-runoff events by analyzing the rainfall time series and the streamflow
time series and the contained signals within. The method was developed with the motivation of creating a method
where it was not required for the hydrologist to make subjective decisions. The method can be used with time
series of any resolution if the time delay between the rainfall and stream flow series can be registered. The ability
to identify events is crucial when investigating the significance of flood events [Giani et al., 2022].

consists of 10 steps, which are described below but also visualized in Figure 13 and 14.

Third Step: “Selection of cores of rainfall and stream flow events” [Giani et al., 2022]
The core of an event is defined as the period where the time series is unsteady. The rainfall and stream flow are
connected if they share the same event. The core is found when the bivariate fluctuations are different from zero.
When the bivariate fluctuations are zero it means that either both time series are zero, or one of them is. This
method “cleans up” the small contributions which might occur during the time series. There must be at least two
consecutive zeros in the bivariate fluctuations for it to be regarded as an event break, as the bivariate fluctuations
will consist of a zero when it changes from negative to positive.
Fourth Step: “Identification of the end of rainfall events” [Giani et al., 2022]
In step four, the core and rainfall fluctuations are used to identify the end of the rainfall event. First, the end
of the rainfall event is assumed to be at the end of the core. The end of the rainfall event has to be fitted to
the actual end of the rainfall, this is done considering three possible approaches, which are visualized in Figure
13 under step 4. The first case is that the rainfall has already ended when the core ends, and therefore the end
is moved backwards until it registers a non-zero value. The second case is that the rainfall event has not ended
yet. Here the end is moved forward until the rainfall is lower than Rmin. The third case occurs if the core ended
because the fluctuations of stream flow were zero. In this case, the rainfall will move back until it reaches the
Rmin and then it will continue to be moved backwards until the rainfall value is bigger than Rmin.
Fifth Step: “Identification of the beginning of rainfall events” [Giani et al., 2022]
The beginning of the rainfall event will most likely align with the beginning of the core. Should this not be the
case, the beginning of the rainfall event will be adjusted following one of the three cases, which can be seen in
Figure 13 under step 5. The first case would be if the beginning of the rainfall is before the beginning of the core.
If this is the case the start will be moved forward until the first non-zero value is registered. The second case is
if the rainfall starts after the core has started and the start of the rainfall event will then be moved backwards
until a value lower than Rmin is registered. In the third case, the steadiness of the streamflow is considered. If
the stream flow fluctuations are zero before the start of the core, the rainfall starts should be moved back until a
value lower than Rmin is registered.
Sixth Step: “Check on rainfall events” [Giani et al., 2022]
In this step, the generated rainfall events are checked. This is done to ensure, that the beginning of the event lies
before the end of the event, and the rainfall is higher than Rmin. Is this not the case, the events are removed.
Seventh Step: “Identification of the end of streamflow events” [Giani et al., 2022]
When the end of the streamflow event is identified one of two methods is used based on how the core ended.
In the case that the core ended because the rainfall fluctuations were zero,the end of the streamflow is moved
forward until the end of the positive streamflow fluctuations is registered. If the core ended when the streamflow

Eighth Step: “Identification of the beginning of streamflow events” [Giani et al., 2022]
Like in step 7, the beginning of the streamflow event is identified using one of two methods. The first applies if
the beginning of the rainfall and the beginning of streamflow are at the same timestep. The streamflow is then

Ninth Step: “Check on streamflow events” [Giani et al., 2022]
As in step 6, the events are deleted if the streamflow starts after the end. However, in this step the events are
also deleted if they start before the rain events.
Tenth Step: “Check on rainfall-streamflow events” [Giani et al., 2022]
Lastly, the events are checked using the core to make sure there are no overlapping events. Is this the case the
two overlapping events will be converted into one event.

6.1
Sensitivity to Rmin in event separation technique
As mentioned in Section 5.2 the DMCA-ESR method aims to create an objective event separation tool, and the
only subjective decision to be taken is the minimum rainfall intensity. Besides the rainfall time series and the
streamflow time series, the Rmin is the only input in the DMCA-ESR method. When the method was tested in
the article by Giani et al. [2022], the authors chose an Rmin value of 0.2 mm/h for an hourly resolution. They
found that the optimum values were between 0.02 and 0.2 mm/h for data with an hourly resolution, as it did not
result in any significant difference when separating the events [Giani et al., 2022].
This section will contain a sensitivity analysis of Rmin to test which minimum rainfall intensity will be most fitting
for the continuation of this study. Ten intensities scaled linearly between 0 to 1 mm/h were tested as well as 1.5
mm/h, so 11 intensities in total for all five stations. These intensities were chosen to see how the method would
react to different values.

tolerance is estimated based on the Rmin. For the Rmin of 0.1 mm/h there were 4 events, two of them having a

For the event around the 13th of January 2019 in Figure 17b, it looks like the rainfall event was split in the middle
of the event. This happens when the minimum rainfall intensity is set too high, as the method adjusts the event
to fit with the beginning and end of a rainfall event based on the Rmin. When the Rmin value is too high, all of
the rain contributions might not be registered, which is the case for the event around the 13th of January. The
rainfall events are longer with a lower Rmin, which is also because the method registers more rainfall contributions
than higher Rmin values.
The same situation occurred during the event around the 17th of January for both minimum rainfall intensities
modelled in Figure 16. While the split of the event using an Rmin value of 1.5 mm/h was likely due to the value
being too high, the same does not necessarily explain the situation for Rmin of 0.2 mm/h. Instead, the separation
could be related to the starting point of the rainfall event. If the rain event began in the middle of the core of
the event, the beginning of the rainfall event had to be moved backwards until a value lower than the minimum
rainfall intensity was detected. When a lower value was detected, the beginning of the rainfall event would be
moved to that timestep (see section 5.2 or Figure 13). This is likely what occurred in the event around the 17th
of January. Appendix II shows the same plot zoomed-in on the beginning of the rain event from which it can be
seen that the small rainfall contribution was what caused the rainfall event to be split up in the middle of what
looks like a rainfall event.

7
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the estimation of flood response time using the Detrending Moving-
average Cross-correlation Analysis (DMCA) method and identify and separate the time series into single events
using the DMCA-Event Separation Routine (DMCA-ESR) in the Vejle catchment. The research questions focused
on the performance of these methods, the variations in response time, and the relation between response time
and catchment characteristics.
The findings indicate that the DMCA-based method is capable of estimating the response time (Tr) in the Vejle
catchment, and found that the Tr varied between 8 hours for the largest sub catchment to 2 hours for the second
smallest. Hence, it was observed that larger catchments generally exhibited slower response times, suggesting a
relationship between catchment size and flood response.
The method showed better performance with long time series compared to individual events. The uncertainties in
the estimations of Tr for single events appeared to be connected to the event identification separation performed
by the DMCA-ESR method and the modifications which was applied to the single events. The modifications were
required when using the DMCA-based method on single events.
Seasonal variations in the response time were also observed, with the low baseflow (summer months) season
resulting in faster response times compared to the high baseflow (winter months) season. This finding indicates
that the antecedent moisture condition of the catchment influences the response time. Additionally, during the
low baseflow season, more intense rain contributions were observed, even though there were fewer days with rain
compared to the high baseflow season. A high-intensity rainfall contribution during a low baseflow period resulted
in fast response times.
Furthermore, the response time during drought periods was found to be faster compared to periods with steady
rain contributions. This riods of dryness influence the catchment’s ability to respond fast to rainfall contributions.
Regarding the DMCA-ESR method, it was noted that the event separation process combined with the DMCA-
based method, resulted in estimations of the response time which was either too fast or too slow considering the
size of the catchment. The reasons why the estimations resulted in unrealistic response times, were a combination
of inaccurate event separations and inadequate modifications of the single events. Sometimes the events were
separated too early or too late, or in some cases, the event identified an event which probably would not be
considered as an event.
This indicates the need for further refinement and improvement of the DMCA-ESR
method.
Based on the above findings, it is essential to exercise caution when extrapolating flood response times to ungauged
locations for the purpose of producing a national map for Denmark. While the DMCA-based method showed
promise in estimating response times, and there were found relations to catchment characteristics. The estimations
still showed deviations in the estimated Tr from year to year, and season to season, which could affect the
Spring 2023
Page 55 of 61
==PAGE:57==
extrapolation. Especially, the limitations in the DMCA-ESR method and uncertainties associated with single-
event analysis should be taken into consideration.
In conclusion, this study contributes to the understanding of flood response time estimation and event identifica-
tion in the Vejle catchment. Further research and refinement of the methods used in this study are recommended
to improve the accuracy and reliability of the methods. Additionally, examinations and response time estimations
in other catchments and geographical areas would provide valuable insights to generalize and validate the findings
and to be able to extrapolate response times to ungauged stations.
