Large-Scale Extraction of Biomedical Relations from the Literature
Author
Luca Dorigo (s200237)

Abstract
Abstract
Over one million biomedical papers are published annually, making it impossible for doctors
and researchers to keep up with ever-growing quantities of information. While the scientific
community agrees that the future of medicine will be driven by automated and artificially
intelligent aids to diagnosis, drug discovery, and treatment, one of the major hindrances to
that future lies in the difficulty of extracting and integrating this wealth of information into
a form that is easily processable by computational tools — for the last century; information
was primarily published as unstructured text.
In this thesis, I investigate how biomedical information can be programmatically extracted
from the literature and integrated into a large Knowledge Graph that can be used to aid with
diagnosis, research, and human and automated reasoning. In particular, I focus on identi-
fying mentions of biomedical entities in text and linking those mentions to corresponding
concepts in a biomedical ontology. This task can be decomposed into several sub-problems.
The first is identifying which text spans are potential mentions of an entity — this is usually
called Named Entity Recognition (NER), and was the focus of one of my previous research
projects. The second sub-problem, known as Entity Linking (EL) and the main focus of this
thesis, is to determine which ontology concept, if any, corresponds to the mentioned entity.
One particularly challenging aspect of EL called Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD) is to
determine the appropriate concept in the case of homonyms or abbreviations that can refer
to more than one concept.
After reviewing modern Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD) and Entity Linking (EL) ap-
proaches and the recent breakthroughs in Natural Language Processing (NLP) that made
them possible, I implement a technique described in “Low Resource Recognition and Linking
of Biomedical Concepts from a Large Ontology” and show that it improves precision by over
20% compared to string-similarity-based approaches while retaining similar levels of recall. I
then introduce and implement several new ideas, one of which further improves the system’s
performance.

1.1
Motivation
The human body is an enormously complex system, and many factors hamper physicians’
abilities to diagnose and treat diseases. One important factor is the limited working memory
of the human brain, which can only hold around three to five pieces of information at any
given time, while a patient may present with tens of observations or blood results [2]. An-
other is the infeasibility of keeping up with discoveries — over one million papers are added
to PubMed1 every year [3]. Finally, I can also mention the very long-tailed distribution of
diseases — the CDC lists over 13.000 distinct disorders and diseases, the majority of which
are classified as “rare” [4]. Doctors are therefore likely to encounter only a tiny subset of this
list in their day-to-day practice.
Over the last few years, I have been very interested in studying how computers can help pal-
liate some of the difficulties highlighted above — in particular, in how to extract information
from vast amounts of biomedical literature and present it in a way that can help doctors
with diagnosis. In previous projects, I developed a system to extract relations between men-
tions2 of biomedical concepts3 from the biomedical literature. I then attempted to use those
relations to build an extensive knowledge graph4 that reflects our current understanding of
human biology.
In this thesis, I focus on a very specific problem that has proven to be a bottleneck in my

1.2
A Small Word on Notation and Naming Conventions
As this entire thesis centres around disambiguating ambiguous words, it would be unfor-
tunate if the thesis itself used ambiguous or unclearly defined words. As I discovered, the
literature on this subject is rife with similar but distinct ideas that are referred to using
different terms in different publications: “words”, “names”, “entities”, “concepts”, “terms”,
“mentions”, “phrases”, and so on.
To avoid confusion, I do my best to be very consistent in how I call things — and in particular,
I try to use an internally coherent naming scheme even when citing papers that might use
a different nomenclature. Every time a concept is used for the first time, it is defined in a
footnote, and the list of definitions I use is reprised in the glossary.

Previous Work
As I mentioned, this thesis is the continuation of a few previous projects — my Bachelor’s

1.3.1
Bachelor’s Project: Biomedical Relation Extraction
The objective of the Bachelor’s project [5] was to extract relations between biomedical
concepts from the biomedical literature.
More formally, the goal was to extract triples
(m1, r, m2), where m1, m2 are potential mentions of biomedical concepts (that is, spans of
text that may or may not correspond to a specific concepts) and r is a relation type — e.g.,
association, causation, production, and so on.
In brief5, the main idea was to use Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to derive
the grammatical structure of sentences. The derived structure could then be used to identify
triples of (subject, predicate, object) that were likely to correspond to the relations
of interest.

It is important to stress that the mentions produced by the system I developed are just
spans of text: at this point, there is nothing that allows resolving different surface forms6 to
a unique concept, or to disambiguate between homonyms7.
1.3.2
Summer Course: Scaling Up, and Linking Mentions to an
Ontology
Over a two-month project course that took place in the Summer 2021 [6], I attempted, to-
gether with another DTU student, to solve two important problems8. First, how to scale up
the system I developed in my Bachelor project to allow it to run on the entirety of PubMed.
Second, how to link the extracted mentions to an existing biomedical ontology9, which is
known in the literature as the Entity Linking (EL) problem. Finding a solution to the EL
problem is essential in order to build and analyse a knowledge graph — indeed, a knowledge
graph is only meaningful if its nodes refer to unique concepts. The main idea was to re-train
existing Word2Vec embeddings so that the embeddings for surface forms of the same concept
would have a high cosine similarity to one another. To determine the concept corresponding
to a mention, the system would find the concept in the ontology whose embedding has the
highest cosine similarity to the mentions’ embedding.
Even though we successfully processed all of the PubMed database, extracting around eleven
million triples, the approach we used to link mentions did not work well enough to produce
a knowledge graph of sufficient quality. Therefore, since linking mentions to unique concepts
is essential for any further analysis of the resulting knowledge graph, we implemented a
simpler alternative based on string similarity. Even though the recall was much lower, as
some concepts have wildly different surface forms (e.g., “vitamin d” and “cholecalciferol”),
we hoped that the precision would be sufficient to generate a usable knowledge graph. Al-
though precision was higher than our initial approach, the next project revealed that it was
insufficient to build a high-quality knowledge graph.

1.4
Objectives
This thesis’ primary objective is to take a deeper dive into the topic of Entity Linking (EL)
and to develop an approach that is both performant and scalable. There are existing openly
available systems for linking mentions to ontologies and, in particular, to the Unified Medical
Language System (UMLS) (the most extensive biomedical ontology, which I will introduce in
chapter 2). However, the entire Natural Language Processing (NLP) field is evolving rapidly,
particularly due to the advent of Transformer-based language models. Consequently, state-
of-the-art EL approaches from just a few years ago are now vastly outperformed by new
techniques that rely on newer NLP methods. None of those newer techniques has yet made
it into openly available and easily usable systems. In this thesis, I attempt to implement
and improve some recent techniques and investigate whether they can be used to build a
high-quality knowledge graph.

Preliminary Notes
In this chapter, I introduce background information on various topics I will refer to through-

which is the ontology for which I chose to develop my Entity Linking system — it is a

2.1
The Unified Medical Language System
The UMLS has been described as a vocabulary, a thesaurus, a meta-thesaurus, and an ontol-
ogy. Indeed, it attempts to fulfil all of those roles, resulting in a large and complex system
that can be somewhat confusing on a first approach. Since the UMLS is such a central
component of this thesis, it is a good idea to take some time to understand its design.
The UMLS is ongoing effort by the US National Library of Medicine (NLM) to aggregate
information from a wide variety of different sources, which they call source vocabularies —
examples include SNOMED CT [8] and the Human Phenotype Ontology [9].
The central elements constituting the UMLS are UMLS concepts, which identified by unique
CUIs. The NLM defines UMLS concepts as “specific meanings which can be referred to by
different words or phrases1” — this is very similar to the definition I gave for concepts, but
for clarity, I will use the phrase “UMLS concept” to refer specifically to those concepts that
appear in the UMLS.
Each UMLS concept is associated with one or more UMLS atoms, identified by AUIs. An
UMLS atom is a surface forms for the UMLS concept which is used to refer to that con-
cept in one of the source vocabularies. For example, the UMLS concept “Vitamin D” with
1I use phrase to refer specifically to a span of text consisting of one or more words.

CUI C0042866 lists the UMLS atoms “Vitamin D” from the MESH vocabulary, with AUI
A0133136, and “Calciotropic hormone” from the SNOMED vocabulary, with AUI A3003081.
The main objective of the UMLS is to connect as many atoms as possible to a single UMLS
concept.
While each UMLS atom represents a unique string (surface form) in its source vocabulary,
the same surface form can appear in multiple source vocabularies. In the UMLS, those sur-
face forms are simply called strings and are associated with a unique SUI.
Finally, the UMLS identifies surface forms that are lexical variations2 each other. To mark
that a surface form is a lexical variation of another, they are assigned to the same UMLS
term and given a unique LUI. Figure 2.1 shows an example of how these different identifiers
are connected.
For each UMLS concept, a single UMLS atom is chosen (based on some priority rules) as
the preferred term — this is the UMLS atoms that is used to identify the UMLS concept in
the UMLS. Finally, it is worth mentioning that each UMLS concept is classified into one or
more semantic types. Those form a hierarchy of general categories, going from very generic
(“Physical Object”) to more specific (“Cell Component”).
2.2
Conclusions From Previous Projects
In the introduction, I mention that when attempting to detect spurious concepts and rela-
tions in the knowledge graph I built, I identified a few key problems in my data that arose
from the way I linked mentions to concepts.
The Entity Linking approach I took in my previous project course[7] was relatively straight-
forward. It relies on QuickUMLS [10], a Python library that implements a fast fuzzy string-
matching algorithm to retrieve the UMLS concepts which has a surface form that is most
similar to a given string. The mention would then be linked to that concept, with some
simple heuristics used in cases there were multiple surface forms with identical similarities.
I briefly discuss the two main problems deriving from this approach.

Literature Review
In this chapter, I conduct an in-depth literature review on the topic of Entity Linking (EL),
as well as on the closely related field of Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD).
Since WSD is actually a sub-problem of EL and is an older field with many good quality
surveys, I use it to lay some of the theoretical groundwork before diving into the literature
around EL. I initially also wrote a review of the field of Author Name Disambiguation (AND),
but as it ended up being less relevant to the rest of the thesis, I decided to relegate it to
Appendix A.
Note that the terminology used within these fields shows a lot of variance from paper to
paper. For clarity, I try to always explicitly map the terminology used in each field to the
definitions I use in this thesis. I also try to use internally consistent definitions within each
field.
Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD):
WSD is the problem of determining, for words
that have multiple possible meanings, which meaning corresponds to mentions of the word
in a given text. Here, what is intended by meaning is usually the equivalent of concept in
the context of this thesis, and what they call words or word phrases are just surface forms
— i.e., the words by which a given meaning can be expressed.
Entity Linking (EL)
Finall, EL is the problem of, given an ontology containing a set of

3.1
Word Sense Disambiguation
Word Sense Disambiguation refers to the problem of determining the meaning of a specific
instance of a word in context, for words that can have more than one meaning (known as
polysemous words). WSD is an essential aspect of NLP and is one of the most challenging

3.1.1
Theoretical Framework
Navigli [12] formalises WSD as follows: suppose a text is modeled as a sequence of words,
T = (w1, w2, . . . , wn). WSD is the task of determining a mapping A from words to meanings,
whereby each word is mapped to a subset of the possible meanings for that word (typically,
only the one most relevant meaning is retained):
A(wi) ⊆MeaningD(wi)
Where MeaningD is the set of possible meanings for wi according to the dictionary1 D.
Navigli [12] identifies four important aspects of all WSD techniques:
Choosing the set of possible meanings for a word:
In the WSD literature, the set
of possible meanings for a given word is usually enumerated in dictionaries or ontologies. It
is important to note that what constitutes “different meanings” can vary widely depending
on the granularity level at which meanings are considered. On the one hand, some words
have entirely unrelated meanings (e.g., “bass” the fish and “bass” the musical instrument).
However, there are also much subtler cases where two mentions of a word might refer to the
same underlying object or entity but might have different meanings depending on how that
word is used or referred to. Navigli [12] gives the example of the word knife. While referring
to the same exact physical object, the meaning might vary between “kitchen appliance” and
“weapon”, potentially even within the same sentence.
This aspect is particularly relevant to consider in the biomedical context and when working
with the UMLS because many biomedical word phrases can have subtly different meanings
depending on the context in which they are used, and those meanings correspond to distinct
UMLS concepts. To give an example, in the UMLS, the surface form “Orexin” can refer,
amongst others, to the family of orexin neurotransmitters (“Orexins”, CUI C1113688), to the
gene coding for these neurotransmitters (“HCRT Gene”, CUI C1415500), and to the human
variant of orexin proteins (“HCRT Protein, Human”, CUI C1527908). Whether those should
be considered separate concepts in the resulting knowledge graph is debatable, but whatever

Classification method:
The last component of WSD is the actual classification of a word
between its possible meanings. Here, Navigli [12] differentiates between techniques among
two main aspects. First, the techniques can go from entirely supervised (using labelled cor-

knowledge-poor. Additionally, Navigli [12] divides classification approaches between token-
based, where each mention of a word is assigned a specific meaning, and type-based, where it
is assumed that a word has a consistent meaning throughout a single text.
3.1.2
Classical Approaches to WSD
Navigli [12] is the most cited, thorough and well-written survey of WSD methods. However,
the techniques it describes are somewhat dated because the entire NLP field has seen seismic
changes in the last years with the advent of Word2Vec-like embeddings and, more recently,
attention-based and Transformer-based approaches.
Nonetheless, it lays the groundwork for a lot of later work on WSD. I summarise the survey
here, glossing over some of the more outdated or irrelevant aspects and diving deeper into
the original papers whenever I feel the approach might be relevant to me. I will focus on
more recent developments of WSD in the following subsection.
Supervised Approaches
The majority of supervised approaches described by Navigli [12] consist of standard clas-
sification techniques adapted to the WSD problem.
Since, for the most part, they are
extremely well-known methods often encountered in introductory courses to statistics and
machine learning, I only mention how they are used for WSD without dedicating too much
time detailing how they work.
Decision lists:
First described by Rivest [16], decision lists are some of the earliest ap-
proaches to training classifiers for text — in the case of WSD, classifying mentions of words
amongst possible meanings. They consist of weighted sets of rules that assign a given score
to each possible meaning of a word depending on features of the word and its context. For
instance, for the word “bass” with possible meanings of “fish” and “musical instrument”, there
might be a rule saying that if “bass” is preceded by “eat”, then the “fish” meaning has a high
score, while if it is preceded by “play”, the “musical instrument” meaning should have a high
score.
Decision trees:
Decision trees are a widely used technique for classification tasks. Each
internal node of the tree corresponds to a split of the data into two subsets based on the
value of a specific feature (e.g., “Is the word a verb” or “is the word preceded by this other
word”) and leaves of the tree correspond to a specific meaning of a word. They are trained
by identifying which features to split on to obtain leaves that are as pure as possible (i.e., all
words ending up in a given leaf should ideally correspond to the same meaning). Although

Naive Bayes:
Naive Bayes uses Bayes’ formula to estimate the probability for a given
meaning of a word given its context.
To do so, it relies on the simplifying assumption
that the features of the context vector are independent. This allows computing the joint
conditional probability for the meaning given those features as a product of the conditional
probabilities for the meaning given each feature. The meaning assigned to each word is thus
the one with the highest estimated probability given its context.

Neural networks and deep-learning-based approaches have seen enor-

densely connected networks, with separate networks being trained for each word to disam-

meaning of the word. In the following section, I go into much more depth on modern neural-
network-based approaches.
Exemplar-based methods:
Exemplar-based techniques, the most common being kNN
(k-Nearest Neighbors), are methods that choose the meaning for a word based on the meaning
of the k most similar words in the training set. According to Navigli [12], when their review
was published, exemplar-based methods were amongst the best-performing ones for WSD.

Unsupervised Approaches
Generally speaking, unsupervised approaches to WSD work by clustering instances of words
without assigning them to a specific meaning in a dictionary but ensuring that instances
within a cluster correspond to the same meaning. These approaches are often called Word
sense discrimination or, in more recent literature, Word Sense Induction (WSI) [17]. Al-
though it can be viewed as a distinct task from WSD, [12] states that it is a subproblem of
WSD as WSI results are commonly used as the initial step for WSD (the second step being
to label the obtained clusters to assign them to a meaning).
Navigli [12] identifies three principal approaches to WSI: context clustering, word clustering,
and co-occurrence graphs.
Context clustering:
Context clustering methods rely on the distributional hypothesis:
that is, the idea that the meaning of a word can be derived by looking at the distribution of
the words surrounding it in large corpora of text. Early attempts at using the distributional
hypothesis include Schutze [18], which represents a word wi in a vocabulary of size N by
a vector wi of size N such that the element wij corresponds to the number of times that
words wi and wj co-occur in the same context. The vector representation for a specific
instance of a word can then be constructed as, for instance, the average of the vectors of the
words surrounding it, and those representations can be clustered with common clustering
approaches. Approaches relying on the distributional hypothesis saw a major boom with the
advent of Word2Vec [19] in 2013, which represents words as much denser vectors and enabled
dramatic improvements in many downstream NLP tasks. More recently, Transformer-based
techniques have been used to produce vector representations that capture more and more
information about words and their contexts.
Word clustering:
unlike context clustering, word clustering techniques rely on the fea-
tures (typically syntactic features) of single words to cluster them into different meanings.
A popular technique (Lin [20]) works roughly as follows. For a target word to disambiguate,
w0, an ordered list of the most similar words to w0, {ws1, . . . , wsn}, is found based on their
syntactic features (for instance, if w0 is a noun that is often the subject of verb X, then
{ws1, . . . , wsn} might be other nouns that are also often used as subjects of X). Starting
from the root w0, the words ws1, . . . , wsn are then arranged in a tree structure such that
each successive word wi is added as a child of the word already in the tree to which wi is most
similar. The tree branches rooted in w0 are then considered to be w0’s separate meanings.
To give a concrete example, the word “cast” might have a subtree corresponding to nouns
that are often the subject of “plays” and noun modifiers of “movie” (the most similar word
in this branch might be “actors”); as well as a subtree corresponding to verbs whose object
is often “dice” or “stone” (here the most similar word may be “throw”). Figure 3.2 shows an
example of such a tree constructed for the word “duty”.

The idea behind selectional preference techniques is that specific
meanings of a word impose constraints on the semantic type of other words to which it relates
grammatically. To reprise the example in Navigli [12], the verb “eat” expects a subject that is
of type “animated entity” and an object of type “edible”. As a consequence, in the sentences
“My parents eat bass for dinner”, the word “bass” is more likely to have the “fish” meaning
(which is of type “edible”) than the “musical instrument” meaning. The obvious problem
with this type of approach is that it requires vast amounts of information on the semantic
types of all meanings of words. Most techniques of this type use WordNet [14] to provide
this information; however, according to Navigli [12], they tend to perform worse than other
approaches.

Finally, the last family of knowledge-based WSD techniques

1. Similarity-based approaches generally define a similarity measure between two mean-

function of the path length between the two meanings in an ontology. The selected
meaning for a word is the one that is most similar to the meanings of the context
words.
2. Graph-based approaches rely on more global graph structure to disambiguate meanings.
Typically, they use lexical chains — that is, sequences of words in a text related by
relations such as is-a or part-of. For example, a text might contain the words “Rome”,
“city”, and “inhabitant”, which form a lexical chain with relations “is-a” and “part of”.
Roughly speaking, WSD is performed by attempting to build such lexical chains and
selecting the most appropriate meaning that matches those already in the chain.
It is interesting to observe that these two sub-families correspond to the two main types
of similarity discussed in the networks embeddings literature: the first corresponds to ho-
mophily, i.e., the idea that nearby nodes are similar to one another, while the second is
akin to structural equivalence, i.e., the idea that “similar” nodes have similar patterns of
attachment.
3.1.3
Recent Developments in WSD
Once again, I loosely follow a survey of WSD methods, “Recent Trends in Word Sense Dis-
ambiguation: A Survey” by Bevilacqua, Pasini, Raganato, et al. [13], that outlines modern
techniques for WSD which have gained in popularity in the last years. Compared to ear-
lier approaches, these techniques differ by heavy use of deep neural networks, both for text
or graph embeddings and for classification purposes. According to Bevilacqua, Pasini, Ra-
ganato, et al. [13], most of the highest-performing modern techniques rely on supervised
models, with a few interesting knowledge-based models that rely on non-annotated informa-
tion from ontologies like BabelNet and additional text corpora.
Knowledge-Based (Unsupervised) WSD
What Bevilacqua, Pasini, Raganato, et al. [13] call knowledge-based methods are techniques
that only use information in ontologies such as WordNet or BabelNet, as well as eventual pre-
trained language models and additional non-annotated text corpora, to disambiguate words.
Although they are significantly less performant than supervised methods, these techniques
are very easy to re-implement in different languages (since they only require a ontology and
pre-trained model in the target language — which BabelNet and many other sources pro-
vide). Additionally, they do not require an annotated set of disambiguated words, which
means they may be the only WSD methods I can apply in my case.
The two best-performing approaches in this category are SyntageRank [23], which relies
on a specialised version of the PageRank algorithm, and SREF [24], which uses BERT (a

pre-trained language model) to learn representations for Synsets6 in WordNet and compares
those representations with the representation of word instances in text.
SyntageRank:
SyntageRank is a technique based on Personalised PageRank (PPR)7,
which relies on the WordNet network, augmented with additional information found in Syn-
tagNet [25], which is a similar network to WordNet but whose focus is on syntagmatic relation
— that is, relations that indicate that two words often appear together in a sentence. Ac-
cording to the authors, adding syntagmatic relations is necessary because PageRank is very
sensitive to the type of relations present in the network. They empirically showed that results
are much better when syntagmatic relations are added. Given a word w to disambiguate
with context words C = {c1, . . . , cn}, SyntageRank works as follows. First, candidate mean-
ings (nodes in the ontology) are selected for all words in C — this is necessary because
context words can themselves have more than one possible meaning. Then, the candidate
meanings for all context words are used as the starting nodes to perform PPR. PPR yields
a probability distribution over the entire network: the selected meaning for the target word
w is the one amongst the set of candidates for w that was assigned the highest probability.
SREF:
Synset Relation-Enhanced Framework (SREF) [24] is a very different technique
that relies on pre-trained language models (specifically BERT [26] — a much more thorough
description of Transformer-based language models will follow in a further chapter) to learn
representations for each meaning of a word, and disambiguates word instances based on the
meaning that is most similar to their context representation. To do so, SREF uses WordNet
to retrieve a set of synonyms for each meaning (called synset) as well as the short definitions
associated with those meanings (called the gloss). As those synonyms and definitions are
often too short to compute appropriate representations, SREF then finds usage examples on
the web. The representation for a meaning is computed by concatenating the synset, gloss,
and web examples and using that as input of a BERT model. The context representation is
simply the BERT model’s output for the context around the word to disambiguate.

ones [13], they have the obvious disadvantage of requiring a large annotated dataset of dis-
ambiguated words to train a model. It is worth noting that there is a subtle distinction that
is not always clearly addressed in the literature between methods that require annotated
samples for all words that need to be disambiguated and methods in which the annotated
data is used to train a generic model that, when presented with a mention of a word and a
list of possible meanings, can choose the most appropriate meanings for the mention even
if that word was not encountered in the training data. Only this latter type of approach is

each meaning s, an embedding corresponding to that meaning is computed by averaging the
embeddings for the contexts of all words that have that meaning in the training data. To
disambiguate a word, the vector for each possible meaning of the word is then compared to
the vector for the word itseld to disambiguate and the one with the highest cosine similarity
is chosen as the meaning.
Other techniques also include information about synonyms and definitions that can be found
in ontologies:
Sequence classification:
One way to leverage information about synonyms and their
definitions (often called glosses in the literature) is to treat the WSD problem as a sequence
classification task [29]: for a given word w with context c, a scoring function scores all
triplets (w, c, G(s′)), where s′ is a meaning of w and G(s′) is the gloss of s′. The meaning
with the highest score is the most likely meaning of w.
Span extraction and generative approaches:
There are several possible variations
of the previous technique. In span extraction [30], the target word is concatenated in the
text with all possible glosses of its synonyms. The model’s objective is to identify the span
corresponding to the most likely meaning. In generative approaches [31], generative language
models (e.g., GPT-2) are fine-tuned to generate the gloss of a word given a word and its
context.

3.2. Entity Linking
3.2
Entity Linking
Entity Linking (EL), sometimes called Named Entity Disambiguation (NED), refers to the
problem of mapping free-text mentions of concepts to the corresponding concept in an on-
tology. It is closely related to the problem of WSD, as distinct concepts can share the same
surface forms. The central objective of this thesis is very close to EL — the only difference
being that in my case, I already have a list of candidate mentions to be linked, while in many
EL approaches, relevant mentions first need to be identified in text.
In this section, I give a brief overview of the main theoretical aspects of the EL problem, then
describe in detail a few different approaches to EL in the biomedical field and specifically
for linking to the UMLS.
3.2.1
Theoretical Aspects
Formally, the EL problem can be defined as follows. Given a set of mentions M and an
ontology with a set of concepts C, the goal is to find a mapping M →C that maximises the
number of correct mappings [34]. In most cases, it is expected that some mentions m ⊆M
have no corresponding concept in the ontology. These mentions are said to be “unlinkable”
and are usually mapped to a special “NIL” concept.
Shen, Wang, and Han [34] identify three main aspects of EL: Candidate Entity Generation,
Candidate Entity Ranking, and Unlinkable Mentions Prediction.
Candidate Entity Generation
The first step is to retrieve a subset Cm ⊂C of concepts that might correspond to the men-
tion m. Techniques to do this range from simple string-matching approaches (which Shen,
Wang, and Han [34] call dictionary-based techniques) to more sophisticated approaches that
use semantic similarity measures and leverage the context of m.
According to Shen, Wang, and Han [34], dictionary-based approaches are the most popular.
Typically, these techniques involve building a dictionary mapping surface forms (i.e., syn-
onyms, lexical variations, and abbreviations) to possible concepts. Shen, Wang, and Han
[34] give an example of how such a dictionary might be built from Wikipedia — leveraging
redirects, disambiguation pages, backlinks, and bold phrases at the beginning of articles.
Table 3.1 shows an example of a small portion of such a dictionary. It is worth noting that,
in my case, this work was already done. Indeed, the entire point of the UMLS is to aggregate
synonyms, abbreviations and lexical variations of medical terms.
Given the dictionary and the mention, candidates are retrieved either by exact string match-
ing (simply retrieving the possible concepts associated with the mention string) or using
various string similarity measures.

Candidate Entity Ranking
Since Em typically contains more than one concept, the second step is to rank concepts in

so I do not go into more details here as the approaches and considerations are essentially the
same as for WSD.
Unlinkable Mention Prediction
Finally, it is necessary to determine whether the top-ranking concept is, in fact, a match for
the mention — if not, m might not have a matching concept in the ontology.
A simple approach taken by many EL systems is to leverage the score computed in the
previous step for ranking mentions and apply a threshold — any mention with no candidate
concepts above the threshold is considered unlinkable. The threshold is typically learned
from the data.
The second most common approach is to train a supervised classifier that takes the tuple
(m, etop) and predicts whether etop is a match for m — if not, m is considered unlinkable.
Typically, the tuple contains the usual features used in WSD for ranking candidates (word
and context embeddings, lexicographic features, etc.), but some systems integrate specific
features such as the score of the top-ranking concept and whether or not the concept is
recognised by NER systems.
Finally, another interesting approach is explicitly adding a NIL concept to the ontology and
including it as a candidate for all mentions. The NIL concept is typically given features that

“Hierarchical Losses and New Resources for Fine-grained En-

Murty*, Verga*, Vilnis, et al. [36] present an interesting technique that tackles both Entity
Linking and Entity Typing (i.e., assigning categories to mentions). I choose it as one of
the approaches to investigate more in-depth because it specifically addresses UMLS linking,
is one of the rare techniques that specifically leverage hierarchical information present in
knowledge bases such as the UMLS, and is by the authors of the MedMentions dataset [37],
which use it to compute baseline performance on MedMentions. Since I will also rely on
MedMentions for my experiments and performance evaluation, this provides an interesting
comparison.
Murty*, Verga*, Vilnis, et al. [36] tackle three similar albeit separate problems. I illustrate
them with the sentence “Albert Einstein was a renowned physicist” (where the mention
of interest is “Albert Einstein”):
1. Mention typing, i.e., assigning fine-grained types to specific mentions of concepts. Here,
“Albert Einstein” might have types “Physicist” and “Deceased Person”.
2. Entity typing, i.e., assigning fine-grained types to concepts as a whole based on the
set of all their mentions. For instance, “Albert Einstein” could also have the types
“German Citizen”, “American Citizen”, and “Author” (the first two can clearly not be
true for a single mention).
3. Entity linking, i.e., assigning a unique concept to a given mention. For instance, if
linking to Wikipedia, “Albert Einstein” would be linked to the page “Albert Einstein”.
Mention and Context Representation
Murty*, Verga*, Vilnis, et al. [36] encode mentions and their context as follows. First, each
token is represented by its pre-learned embeddings (these are frozen and do not change
during model training). To specify which tokens are part of the mention and which are
part of the context, as well as add positional information to their model, they concatenate
token embeddings with position embeddings — tokens that are part of the mention are
given a position of 0, tokens that come before have a negative position, and tokens that
come after have a positive position. The context is encoded by a single convolutional layer
with tanh activation function and max-pooling regularization. The mention is encoded by
simply averaging the embeddings of the tokens within it. Finally, the mention and context
are encoded together by concatenating and passing them through a two-layer feedforward
neural network with tanh nonlinearity. Figure 3.4 shows the structure of the network for an
example sentence.

“Low Resource Recognition and Linking of Biomedical Concepts from a Large Ontology”
was published by the team behind MedMentions [37] and currently achieves state-of-the-art
results for Entity Linking on that dataset. It is an end-to-end Entity Linking system that
does both NER (i.e., determining which spans of text correspond to concepts) and Entity
Linking and disambiguation. This approach is particularly interesting to study because of
its relevance to this project’s scope — it uses the same dataset, aims to link to the UMLS,
and is a modern state-of-the-art technique for Entity Linking. It consists of three separate
phases that mirror the three aspects of EL described in subsection 3.2.1.
Candidate Generation
Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al. [1] generate both candidate mentions, i.e., spans of text that
might correspond to a concept, and candidate concepts, i.e., for each candidate mention, a list
of UMLS concepts that might correspond to that mention. Candidate mentions are generated
by taking all possible spans of tokens up to length K within a sentence and stopping at stop-
words. For every candidate mention m, the set of candidate concepts LM(m) = {e1, . . . , en}
is generated using lexical similarity by taking both n-gram and word-based similarity into
account — the details of how this is done are not particularly interesting, and I omit them
here for brevity.
Each candidate concept ej ∈LM(m) is represented as a tuple of features (sei, aei, Eei, Tei, tei),
where:
1. sei is the similarity measure between the candidate span m and the candidate concept
ei
2. aei is the matched surface form for the candidate concept ei
3. Eei is the preferred term for the concept in the UMLS
4. Tei is the semantic type of the candidate concept ei
5. tei is the type for the matched surface form — for instance, synonym, acronym, canon-
ical name, etc.

Candidate Ranking
For each pair of a mention and one of its candidates (m, ei), ei ∈LM(m), the authors
compute the logit (score for that pair) L(m, ei) as follows:
L(m, ei) = FF (B(m, ei), Emb(tei), sei, Emb(binsS(se)))
Where:
1. FF is a feedforward neural network
2. Emb(x) is a trainable embedding for value x
3. binsS(sei) is a projection from the similarity score into bins
4. B(m, ei) is the representation for the candidate span, its context, and the candidate
concept obtained with a pre-trained BERT language model.
The input for the BERT model consists of the candidate mention, centred within its context,
concatenated with the name of the candidate concept.
To train the model, the logits for all possible candidate concepts are normalised using Soft-
Max (to obtain a probability distribution) and optimised to minimise cross-entropy.
Span Selection
The last part of the system has to determine which of the candidate mentions, which are now
coupled with their top-scoring candidate concept and its corresponding predicted probability,
are actual mentions of the concept. This is done with a separate model with a very similar
architecture to the one used for candidate ranking. For a tuple (m, ei, p), where m is the
candidate mention, ei is the candidate concept, and p is the predicted probability, the logit
for the span is computed as follows:
S(m, ei, p) = FF (B(m, ei), Emb(tei), sei, Emb(binsS(se)), p, Emb(bins(p))))
The model is trained to optimise a custom loss function (omitted here for brevity). It can
be used at inference time in two different ways: either in a thresholded version where any
mention for which the probability is above a certain threshold is considered to be linked to
the concept, or in a “greedy” way where only the most likely, longest, and earliest-starting
non-overlapping mentions are considered linked to the concept.

Now that I presented an overview of the theoretical background for WSD and EL, as well as

My data consists of a large set of relation groups extracted from the biomedical literature.
Each relation group can be seen as a triple P1, r, P2, where P1 and P2 are sets of phrases and
r is a relation between them. Each phrase in a set of phrases Pi has the same root noun
(e.g., “headache”), with additional adjectives or modifiers that make it more specific. For
instance, the phrase “postprandial headaches in children” would result in the set of phrases
{“headaches”, “headaches in children”, “postprandial headaches”, “postprandial headaches in
children”}. The goal is to find the UMLS concept(s) that match the phrases in the set of
phrases Pi. Ideally, the most specific phrase possible should be matched, but as that is not
always possible, part of the difficulty is to determine which less-specific phrases should be
matched or not.
To keep complexity manageable for the thesis, I will not consider the problem of selecting
the most optimal phrase to match. Instead, I simply consider all possible phrases extracted
from the literature and focus on the task of aligning them to the UMLS. As such, the
problem is relatively close to a typical Entity Linking problem, in which NER (i.e., identifying
candidate mentions) has already been performed. Therefore, for each phrase, the task can
be decomposed in the same three steps described in subsection 3.2.1:
1. Candidate generation: An initial set of possible UMLS concepts must be identified
for each phrase.
2. Candidate ranking: The candidate concepts need to be ranked based on how close
they are to the actual concept being mentioned in the phrase.
3. Unlinkable mention detection:
For each phrase, it is necessary to determine
whether or not the best candidate is actually a match.

3.4.1
Performance Measures
WSD and EL publications use a wide variety of performance measures to report their results.
While similar, there are subtle differences in how those measures are calculated and reported
— for instance, in WSD, it is assumed that every word must correspond to an underlying
person or meaning, while in EL, the mention may have no corresponding concept. Since my
problem is most closely related to EL, I focus on performance measures for EL in this section.
The most commonly used metrics are precision, recall, accuracy, and F1-score [34]. Although
these are very common measures that are not restricted to the WSD and EL domains, I
briefly describe each for reference in the rest of the thesis. It is important to note that the
Entity Linking problem, as presented in the previous section, is best understood as three
33
==PAGE:40==
3.4. Performance Evaluation for WSD and EL
separate subproblems of candidate generation, ranking, and unlinkable mention prediction,
whose performance can be measured independently. While most papers report the system’s
performance as a whole, accuracy, precision and recall are typically defined for single classi-
fication or information retrieval problems, not composite problems such as Entity Linking,
so interpreting them requires some care.
In particular, it is important to note some subtle differences between the notions of true posi-
tives, false positives, false negatives and true negatives used in binary classification problems
and those used here. Here, there are actually five possible scenarios:
1. The mention is unlinkable, and the system correctly predicts that it is unlinkable.
2. The mention is unlinkable, and the system incorrectly links it to a concept.
3. The mention is linkable but, the system incorrectly predicts that it is unlinkable.
4. the mention is linkable and, the system correctly links it to the correct concept.
5. The mention is linkable but, the system links it to the wrong concept.
Cases 1-4 correspond to the usual outcomes of a binary classification problem — true nega-
tives, false positives, false negatives and true positives. Case 5, however, is unique to Entity
Linking systems: when computing the metrics described below, linkable mentions that were
linked to the wrong conceptcount both as a false positive (because there is a match that
should not have been there), and as a false negative (because the correct match for the
mention is missing) [41].

separate blocks allows to improve each component separately and better understand the
overall performance of the system.
Candidate generation:
Candidate generation is evaluated only on linkable mentions —
candidates generated for unlinkable mentions are irrelevant. If Em ⊆E is the set of candi-
dates generated for each mention m, the most important and commonly reported metric is
the accuracy — i.e., the percentage of sets Em that contain the true concept13. An additional
important aspect is the size of the generated sets Em, which impacts efficiency — taking
the entire vocabulary E as candidates trivially results in 100% of candidate sets containing
the true match. However, in cases where each candidate has to be evaluated separately, the
resulting system can be very inefficient.
Candidate ranking:
Although candidate ranking was described in terms of assigning a
score to each candidate, usually, it is really only the highest-ranking candidate that is of
interest, as that is the one that would typically be considered as the matched concept. Con-
sequently, this step is usually evaluated in terms of accuracy — that is, the proportion of
mentions linked to the correct concept. Note that in theory, this part should only be eval-
uated on mentions that do have a corresponding concept— identifying unlinkable mentions
happens in the next step, so unlinkable concepts should not be considered when evaluating
candidate ranking. Similarly, those concepts for which the candidate generation step did not
generate the correct concept as one of the candidates should not be considered for evaluating
the candidate ranker’s performance.
Unlinkable mention detection:
Detecting unlinkable mentions can be viewed as a sim-
ple binary classification problem, where the system predicts whether or not a mention is
unlinkable. The usually reported metrics are, as is the case for the whole EL problem, pre-
cision and recall (and F1 score). Here, too, when reporting the performance of the unlikable
mention detector, it makes sense to only consider the ground truth in this step: all that
matters is whether the chosen candidate is, in fact, the correct concept for the mention —
whether it is incorrect because the mention was not linkable in the first place, because the
candidate generator did not generate the correct candidate, or because the wrong candidate
was chosen by the candidate ranking step is irrelevant.

behind them. This chapter is not essential to the rest of the thesis, and the reader can safely
skip it if already familiar with the introduced concepts.
I start by introducing attention mechanisms, the primary technique that allows models to
learn word representations that capture information about the context in which the words are
used. Then, I describe Transformer models, a family of models that efficiently use attention
mechanisms to train very large models on enormous text corpora. Finally, I focus on BERT,
BART and GPT, three related families of PLMs often used in EL/WSD systems.

5.1.1
Sequence-to-Sequence Models
Attention Mechanisms are usually described in the context of Sequence-to-Sequence (STS)
models2: models that take a sequence of things as input and produce another sequence of
things as output. While things can be any type of data element — words, pixels, time-series
data points, and so on — in this section, I limit the description to textual STS models,
which take a sequence of words as input and produce another sequence of words as output.
Typical applications of this type of model include machine translation or question answering.
In general terms, STS models predict the conditional probability for an output sequence
given an input sequence, p(y|x), where x is the input sequence x1, x2, . . . , xn and y is the
output sequence y1, y2, . . . , yk[44]. A STS model consists of two components: an encoder,
which computes a representation s of the input sequence, called the context3, and a decoder,
which computes output element yk one at a time by using the context s as well as the output
sequence up to that point y1, y2, . . . , yk−1. This decomposes the conditional probability as
follows:

Encoding:
Input words are processed one by one. For a given word wi, the RNN is given
as input a representation of wi4 as well as the hidden state accumulated up to that point,
hi−1. The RNN produces an output for each word, which is discarded, and a new hidden
state hi. This new hidden state is used to process the next word until the end of the input
sequence. The context passed to the decoder is the final hidden state, s = hn.

Similarly, the decoder is a RNN that is initialised with an initial hidden state
of s. At each step, the decoder produces a vector of probabilities over each word in the
vocabulary, p(yj|hj−1) = SoftMax(g(hj−1)), where g is a function that maps the hidden
state to a vector with the size of the vocabulary. The word with the highest probability is

5.2
Transformers
At the time they were introduced, the attention mechanisms described in the previous sec-

The paper “Attention is all you need” by Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, et al. [46], which be-

called Transformer, which relies exclusively on attention mechanisms and simple feedforward
layers. Aside from a sizeable increase in performance, the Transformer architecture has the
big advantage that it does not rely on sequential computations like RNNs, which means
that training and inference can happen on massively parallel processors (GPUs) and thus
dramatically increase the speed of training and inference.
I start by describing how Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, et al. [46] extend the attention mech-

then describe the architecture of the Transformer model in more detail.
5.2.1
Self-Attention
Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, et al. [46] extend the attention mechanism described in the pre-
vious section to be used both on the encoder and decoder side. The intuition behind this
is that when generating embeddings for a specific word, not all words around it are equally
important. The attention mechanism is thus used to “focus” on the relevant words to learn
more useful embeddings. The use of attention in the encoder is called self-attention because
the attention mechanism is applied to the current input vector rather than to a separate one.
In the Transformer literature, attention mechanisms are usually described and implemented
slightly differently than those introduced earlier. Suppose that there are two words, w1 and
w2, with embeddings x1 and x26. Just like in the original attention mechanism, the objective
is to score each word (say, w1 against all other words, so w1 and w2) to determine which
other words are most important when generating the embedding for w1.

normalised score. It is important to note that k, q and v can be of lower dimension than
the input embeddings (for reasons that will become apparent when I explain multi-headed
attention). This is why the layer’s output is given by the product of the scoring vector by
the value vector rather than the product of the scoring vector by the embeddings — those
two vectors are of different dimensions.
5.2.2
Multi-Headed Attention
As I mentioned above, the vectors k, q and v usually have lower dimensionality than the
input embeddings. This is because Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, et al. [46] introduce an ad-
ditional mechanism called multi-headed attention, which allows the model to learn multiple
attention patterns simultaneously and greatly increases the model’s capacity: for instance,
one “head” might produce attention scores that highlight low-level grammatical features (for
instance, by giving high scores to subject-verb pairs), while another head might correspond
to coreferences (for instance, in the sentence “The dog is wet because it played in the rain”,
the “it” and “dog” pair might be given a high score).
To implement this, the model learns N separate triplets of weight matrices (Wk, Wv, and
Wq). Each triplet corresponds to a different attention head. The dimension d of the resulting
key, query and value vectors is chosen such that d · N is equal to the dimension of the input
embeddings. Afterwards, the N scaled value vectors are concatenated to form the output of

5.2.3
Encoder
The overall architecture of Transformers, like other STS models, is structured as an encoder
and a decoder. The encoder consists of N identical layers, each consisting of one multi-
head self-attention sub-layer and one feedforward sub-layer. A residual connection is added
between each sub-layer7, and the output is normalised using layer normalisation. If the input
to a layer is X, then the output of the layer is:
LayerNorm (LayerNorm (X + SA(X)) + FF (LayerNorm (X + SA(X))))
Where X is the input to the layer, SA is the multi-head self-attention sub-layer, and FF is
the feedforward sub-layer. Crucially, the self-attention and feedforward layers can be com-
puted in parallel — i.e., separately for each word in the input.
In the encoder, the query, key and value vectors are all computed from the current layer’s
input vector — either the output from the previous layer or the initial embeddings in the
first layer.
Decoder
The decoder is similarly composed of a stack of N identical layers, with similar layer normal-
isation and residual connections between every sub-layer. Each layer consists of the following
three sub-layers:
1. A masked multi-headed self-attention sub-layer. This is very similar to the one in the
encoder, except that the scores for all words on the right of the word that is being
predicted, wi, are set to zero. The idea is that the decoder should have information
available about the outputs produced before wi, but not about words coming after it.
2. An encoder-decoder multi-headed attention sub-layer. In this layer, the queries come
from the previous layer, while the keys and values come from the encoder. This is
similar to the attention-based models I introduced earlier, whereby the decoder can
“focus” on specific parts of the input sequence when producing an output.
3. Finally, like in the decoder, a feedforward sub-layer allows for nonlinear transformations
of the output.
7“Residual connection” means that the inputs to layer X are added to the outputs of X. This is done to
let the model keep information about earlier layers.

The final part of the decoder consists of a simple scoring layer called Generator, a linear layer
with a softmax activation that outputs a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary.
This layer uses the information from the decoder to predict the most likely vocabulary word.
Positional Encodings
One final important aspect of the Transformer architecture is that of positional encodings.
Unlike RNN-based attention models, in Transformers, all words are processed independently
from one another. This means that the model cannot know the order of the words in the
input sequence.
Since the order of words clearly carries essential information about the
meaning of a text, an additional mechanism is needed to let the model take word order into
account.
This mechanism is implemented by adding a specific pattern (the positional encoding) to
the input embeddings. A trivial way to do so would be to simply add the index of the word
to the embedding vector. However, this would disproportionately perturb the value of word
embeddings in the latter parts of the sentence. Instead, a more complex encoding based on
the sum of two sinusoid functions of the index with different frequencies is used. The exact
details of the implementation are somewhat complicated and not particularly relevant, so I
do not go into more details here.
5.3
BERT, BART, and GPT
The most famous Transformer-based PLMs in use today are, by far, BERT (Bidirectional
Encoder Representations from Transformers [26]), GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transform-
ers [47]), and, more recently, BART (Bidirectional Auto-Regressive Transformer [48]). These
models are straightforward to understand as they are variations on the basic Transformer
architecture described previously — as one of the papers openly states, “BERT is conceptu-
ally simple and empirically powerful.”. I do a quick roundup of their specificities and uses
in the following sections.
5.3.1
“Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training”
GPT, introduced by Radford, Narasimhan, Salimans, et al. [47], was groundbreaking not so
much for the techniques it uses (which, as I will describe shortly, are a very simple variation
on the Transformer architecture) but for showing that pre-training a Transformer model on a
large corpus of text could yield dramatic improvements in performance for downstream tasks.
They call this process of adapting a large pre-trained model to a specific task fine-tuning.
During initial training, the model optimises a generic loss function on a large unlabeled text
corpus. It is then re-trained with a task-specific loss function on a much smaller supervised
dataset.

5.3.2
“BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for
Language Understanding”
BERT was introduced one year after GPT by Devlin, Chang, Lee, et al. [26]. It is conceptu-
ally very similar to GPT, with the key difference that BERT does not use a mask for tokens
to the right of the current position. This lets it use information from the entire sequence
when computing embeddings but limits its use for text generation.
Architecture-wise, BERT is not particularly interesting — it uses the exact same structure
described in “Attention is all you need”, with 12 layers of encoder-only Transformer sub-
layers8.
The paper presents two models — one with 12 layers and the same number of
parameters as GPT, meant to be used for performance comparison with GPT, and one with
24 layers and thrice the number of parameters.
The most interesting contributions of BERT are the tricks it uses for pre-training the model.
Unlike GPT, it is not possible to just predict the “next” token in a sequence — the model
uses multiple layers of bi-directional attention, so predicting a single token based on the
surrounding text does not work because each token “knows” itself indirectly from the repre-
sentation of the surrounding tokens (the exact mechanism is a bit complicated to understand,
see [49] for more information).
Instead, Devlin, Chang, Lee, et al. [26] use masked language modelling to train their net-
work: each target token in a sequence is replaced by a specific <MASK> token, which the
model must then predict. This is different from masking in the attention layer because the
token to predict is entirely absent from the input sequence — so the model cannot “cheat” by
using the representation of the surrounding tokens to infer the representation of the missing
token.
The authors add a second training objective to the pre-training phase they call next sentence
prediction. In this step, the model is presented with a piece of text and a possible next
sentence, which is the correct one in 50 % of cases. This additional objective forces the
model to learn longer, inter-sentence dependencies that the authors show are very beneficial
for specific types of downstream tasks. For this, they use a special input representation with
two particular out-of-vocabulary tokens: [CLS] and [SEP]. [CLS] is added to the beginning
of all sequences processed by BERT. It is intended to capture information for use by the

5.3.3
“BART: Denoising Sequence-to-Sequence Pre-training for Nat-
ural Language Generation, Translation, and Comprehension”
BART is a more recent model that generalises BERT, GPT and other recent architectures
and pre-training techniques.
Like BERT and GPT, BART uses the same encoder-decoder structure described in “Atten-
tion is all you need” — with N encoder layers and N decoder layers. On a high level, this
means that BART can attend to all input tokens at once but can only produce output tokens
based on already produced tokens.
The main innovation in BART is that they set up training as a generic de-noising task:
the model is given a text to which some type of noise was added and must reconstruct the
original text. BART’s authors describe five types of noise in the paper but stress that the
main idea is that the setup is generic and that many other types of noise functions can be
used. The noise functions they use are:
1. Token Masking which is the same as in BERT.
2. Token Deletion in which random tokens are deleted in unknown positions, and the
model must predict not only the tokens but also the positions from which they were
removed.
3. Text Infilling which is similar to (1) but where multiple tokens are replaced by a
single <MASK> token.
4. Sentence Permutation in which the text’s sentences are randomly permuted and
must be re-ordered by the model.
5. Document Rotation where the text is rotated by a given amount of tokens, and the
model must find the actual start of the document.
Like BERT, BART can be used on a wide variety of downstream tasks by fine-tuning and
directly using the features as inputs for downstream tasks. The authors show that the specific
type of noising function used strongly impacts performance in different types of tasks.

Training the Network
The feedforward network is initialised with random weights, and the weights of the BERT
model used to compute representations for the mention-candidate pairs are downloaded from
HuggingFace. I use, like the paper, an Adam optimiser with a learning rate of 2×10−5. While
the paper uses “a linear warmup for 10% of the batches, followed by a linear decay”, they
do not specify the warmup and decay parameters, so I start by using a constant learning rate.
Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al. write “Each input batch to the span linker consists of all
candidates for one mention, with KM = 502 as the batch size.”. It is worth noting that
here, the meaning of “batch” and the way the loss function (cross-entropy) is applied are
somewhat different from their usual use in multi-class classification. Usually, a single input
(e.g., a picture or a sentence) is used to generate a vector of predicted probabilities over the
possible classes. The loss function (usually cross-entropy) is applied to the vector of prob-
abilities. Here, however, each input (i.e., a mention-candidate pair) is used to generate a
single scalar value (the score for that pair). The loss is defined as the cross-entropy between
the (normalised) scores and a one-hot-encoded vector v such that vi = 1 for the correct
candidate and vj = 0 for all other candidates.
What the authors call a “batch” would thus normally not be called a batch but rather a single
input. Therefore, the batch size is actually 1, and thus the training method is closer to SGD
than mini-batch gradient descent — the model’s weights are updated once for each mention3.
The final aspect to mention is that, like the authors, I use the training and validation splits
provided by MedMentions (consisting of approximately 120.000 mentions for the training
set and approximately 40.000 for the validation set), and I use early stopping based on the
model’s accuracy on the validation set.

6.3. Candidate Ranking
Training on GPU:
The obvious first step was to move the training process to GPU.
By simply offloading the main computations (i.e., processing with BERT and training the
feedforward model) to a graphics card, I was able to reduce the training time to 25 hours
per epoch — still a lot, but much more reasonable.
Loading data in separate processes:
Profiling the training process showed that a sig-
nificant amount of time was spent in-between batches while loading and preprocessing the
inputs to be fed to the model. To speed this up, I moved the data loading to a separate
process, which allows the CPU to load new data while the GPU is busy with the previous
batch. This further reduced the training time to about 15 hours per epoch.
Disabling gradient computations for the BERT model:
while not entirely clear
from the paper, it appears that only the weights of the embedding and feedforward layers
are updated during training — the outputs of the BERT model are used as static feature
vectors. This means that it is possible to freeze the weights of the BERT model, avoiding
the need for a backward pass (during weight update) over all of the BERT model’s weights.
This reduced the time to approximately 6 hours per epoch.
Mixed-precision training:
Modern GPUs contain specialised Tensor Cores that can
dramatically speed up some common operations used by neural networks. This requires
storing some of the model weights at half precision (16-bit floats rather than 32-bit floats),
which has the added advantage of reducing the model’s memory footprint, but might decrease
the model’s accuracy and ability to learn. Since it is widely used in Transformer networks, I
decided to use it in my case, too — further reducing the time to approximately 3 hours per
epoch.
Pre-generating BERT embeddings:
Since the weights of the BERT model are frozen,
the BERT representation used as input for the feedforward network is the same in every
epoch of training. This means that it is possible to pre-compute BERT embeddings for all
mention-candidate pairs and cache them to avoid re-computing the same information several
times. Additionally, small experiments and tweaks to the model architecture generally do
not require changing the embeddings — meaning that precomputing the BERT outputs can
be done once and then re-used many times. With this final optimisation, the training time
is reduced to just ten minutes per epoch, with an initial preprocessing time of about three
hours.

Initial run:
The first training run I attempted is shown as a blue line on the figure. The
model achieved an accuracy of around 65% on the validation set. The model quickly stopped
learning (the validation accuracy and losses plateaued after roughly two epochs).
Linear warmup and decay:
As I explain in subsection 6.3.2, I initially used a constant
learning rate because the description given by the authors of how they implemented their
linear warmup and decay was insufficiently detailed. After some time spent implementing
the model, however, it became clear that the authors were also using the HuggingFace library
and that they referred specifically to a function from that library that linearly increases and
decreases the learning rate of the optimiser over the course of the training. As seen on the
orange line, this had relatively little effect — the training losses are more or less identical,
and the validation accuracy increased by about 1% (which could be very well explained by
minor stochastic differences).

is present:
While not explicitly mentioned in the paper, I suspect that the mentions and
sets of candidates used to train the model were restricted to only those sets generated in the
candidate generation step in which the true matching concept was present. Indeed, not doing
so would lead to having mentions for which all labels would be “0”, but since, by definition,
the SoftMax function used to normalise the scores produces a probability distribution over
all possible labels (which can thus not all be zero), it would be impossible for the model
to generate the correct outputs in those batches.
Indeed, as shown by the green curve,
only training on the sets of candidates in which the true matching concept is present led to
significantly higher accuracy.

speed up training (because it can better take advantage of GPU parallelism) and convergence
speed (because gradient descent is performed over a few averaged steps and is thus less

a problem in how one of the input features was computed, i.e., string similarity between
the mention text and the matched surface form. By default, QuickUMLS splits the string
to be matched into words and attempts to match any subsequence of words with a surface
form: the similarity it reports is the similarity between the subsequence and the match,
not between the entire string and the match. This means that, for example, “Vitamin D
Deficiencies” might match “Vitamin D” with a similarity of 1, which is much higher than
the similarity between “Vitamin D Deficiencies” and “Vitamin D deficiency” even though the
overall similarity should be higher in the latter case. Correcting this behaviour in the can-
didate generation step so that the string similarity is computed between the entire mention
and the matched surface form resulted in a substantial increase in performance, reaching an
accuracy of 81%.

the [CLS] token. Therefore, while I am unsure whether this was indeed what the authors
did, I also experimented with using the average outputs. This resulted in a small increase
in performance, reaching an accuracy of 82%.

The accuracy I reach is lower than that reported by the authors, which claim a final accuracy
of 85%. I have two hypotheses for this gap:
Surface form preprocessing:
Unlike the authors, I do not preprocess UMLS surface
forms to remove meta-information and disambiguating context. This might mean that the
similarity score used as input by the candidate ranker is not always correct. For instance,
suppose the mention is “vitamin D” while the surface form for the correct match is “Vitamin
D (supplement)”. In the authors’ case, the “(supplement)” part would be removed, yielding
a string similarity of 1, while in my case, the similarity would be much lower. Since the
similarity score has a significant impact on the model’s prediction (as evidenced by the large
change in performance after fixing how similarity is computed in one of the experiments), it
is reasonable to think that the lack of preprocessing might reduce overall performance.
Lack of positional encoding for the mention:
The BERT model currently has no way
of knowing which tokens are part of the mention and which are part of its context. The men-
tion is usually centred within the context, but it can be of variable length and is sometimes
not centred at all (if it is too close to the beginning or end of the text). Suppose multiple
concepts are mentioned within the context. In that case, it might be very hard for the model
(and indeed even for a human annotator) to know which of them should be considered when
comparing with the candidate concept. The authors make no explicit mention of this, but
one sentence in the paper indicates that they may indeed take this into account: “a trained
vector is added to the embeddings of each mention token at BERT’s input.”. I did not
initially understand what this referred to, but it might be that the trained vector is meant
to signal to the model that those tokens belong to the mention.
Unfortunately, both of these hypotheses require a lot of additional effort to verify. The first
one would require a lot of work to effectively identify and remove meta-information and
disambiguating context from surface forms. Implementing the second one would make it
impossible to pre-compute BERT outputs (as the inputs would be different in each epoch),
thus making training and experimenting extremely slow.
Since my performance is reasonably close to the original results, and since I would rather
dedicate time to experimenting with some new ideas, I decide not to test those hypotheses.

Span Selection
The last part of Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al. [1]’s approach is to train an additional
network which, for each mention, takes the highest-ranking candidate according to the can-
didate ranker and outputs a score that reflects whether that candidate is actually a match
for the mention or not. Once more, I briefly re-summarise the network architecture, then
describe a few important aspects of the implementation and finally discuss the training and
performance of the model.

6.4. Span Selection
The network architecture for the span selector is as follows:
S(m, e, p) = FFS (BS(m, e), Emb (te) se, Emb (binS (se)) p, Emb (binL(p)))
Where:
1. p is the predicted probability for the pair (m, e) by the candidate ranking step
2. FFs is a new feedforward network with a similar structure to the one used for candidate
ranking
3. Bs(m, e) is BERT’s pooled output for an input representation specific to the span
selector, which I will describe shortly
4. te is the name type
5. se is the string similarity between the candidate and the matched concept, computed
in the candidate generation step
6. bins(se) and binl(p) are binned versions of the string similarity and predicted proba-
bility, respectively
7. Emb (te), Emb (binS (se)), and Emb (binL (p)) are embeddings of, respectively, the
name type, string similarity bin, and predicted probability bin.
6.4.1
Implementation
Virtually all aspects of the implementation of this network are identical to those in the
candidate ranking step, so I do not repeat the description.
The only notable difference
is in the input encoding for the BERT embedding: the structure of the input is almost
identical, with the only difference being that this time, the second sentence in the BERT
input representation uses the matched surface form a instead of the preferred term ep:
[CLS]C(m)[SEP]CS(e)[SEP]
Where CS(e) = ”Te, a eC”. Here Te is the semantic type of e (formatted just like in the
candidate ranking step), a is the specific surface form that was matched, and eC is the dis-
ambiguating context present in the preferred term — which I ignore. I am not entirely sure
why the authors chose to use the matched surface form a here: it seems that it carries only
very little information that can be used to determine whether or not the concept is an actual
match for the mention.
Other than this, the only difference with the candidate ranking step is that there are two
additional features: the probability predicted by the candidate ranking model and an em-
bedding for the binned version of that probability.

6.4.2
Training
The model’s training happens similarly to the candidate ranking step, with one important
difference in how the loss is defined.
Loss Function
Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al. [1] train their model so that the score for a candidate should
be positive for correct matches and negative for incorrect matches. For this, the authors use
a loss function which they call a thresholded max-margin loss and define as:
LS(m, e, p) =
 W+ × max(0, M −s)
if (m, e) is ground truth
max(0, M + s)
otherwise
Where:
1. m, e, p is a triple consisting of a mention, its top-ranking candidate, and the probability
for that candidate predicted by the candidate ranking model.
2. W+ is a hyperparameter that controls the weight given to positive examples. Larger
values of W+ mean that the optimiser gives a bigger penalty to correct matches that
the span selector deemed incorrect than to incorrect matches that the span selector
deemed correct.
3. s is the score generated by the span selector for the pair (m, e).
4. M is the margin hyperparameter, which the authors fix to 1. As I will explain below,
this essentially ensures that the model’s weights are only updated for the samples it is
least confident about.
67
==PAGE:74==
6.4. Span Selection
If like me, the reader is unfamiliar with this type of loss, a clearer intuition for its behaviour
can be gained by considering what happens in the following four scenarios. Assume that
M = 1, and W+ = 2:
1. If (m, e) is ground truth (i.e., it is a correct match):
(a) If the score given by the model is > 1, then M −s is < 0, so the loss becomes 0,
and the weights are not updated.
(b) If the score given by the model is < 1, then M −s is > 0, so the loss becomes
2 −2s — the lower the score, the larger the loss.
2. If (m, e) is not ground truth (i.e., it is an incorrect match):
(a) If the score given by the model is < −1, then M + s is < 0 and the loss is 0, and
weights are not updated.
(b) If the score given by the model is > −1, then M + s is > 0 and the loss becomes
1 + s — once again, the higher the score the larger the loss.
As mentioned, the margin M ensures that the model only learns from samples for which it
is not “confident” enough. Take a correct match as an example: the resulting loss will be
0 as long as the score is greater than 1 — but if the model is either wrong (s < 0) or not
sure enough 0 <= s < 1, then the loss will be nonzero, and the weights will be updated
accordingly.
I am not certain why this approach is desirable over a “normal” binary classification loss
based on binary cross-entropy (using a sigmoid activation function) — but as of now, I
simply replicate the authors’ approach.
Training Loop
The model is trained with the Adam optimiser, using a linear warmup on 10% of the steps
and with a final learning rate of 2 × 10−6. It is trained on batches of 64 samples, wherein
each sample is a single mention and its top-ranking candidate. To reproduce the authors’
results, I experiment with giving the hyperparameter W+ values 1, 2, 5, 10, 20. Since there is
substantially less computation involved in this step (only one sample per mention compared
to the 50 samples per mention in the candidate ranking step), I let all experiments run for
20 epochs.

6.4. Span Selection
Figure 6.4 shows the results for the span selection step for various values of W+. There are
a few things worth noting.
First, it is good to clarify what the precision, recall, and F1 metrics actually measure: even
though this step is sometimes called unlinkable mention prediction, here, I consider a posi-
tive to be a correctly linked mention, and negatives are the incorrect matches. Therefore,
precision is the proportion of candidates deemed correct by the span selector that is actually
correct. High precision means that the model will let few false positives through — in other
words, if I only keep candidates that are deemed valid by the model, few of those will be
incorrect. Conversely, recall is the proportion of correct matches that the model has deemed
correct: high recall means most of the correct matches are let through by the model.
With this in mind, it is interesting to consider the effects of the parameter W+ on the model
performance. Since W+ determines the weight given by the loss function to matches erro-
neously classified as “correct” relative to those erroneously classified as “incorrect”, it stands
to reason that increasing the value of W+ should increase the model’s recall and decrease its
precision. As it is “punished more” for failing to validate a correct candidate than for failing
to reject an incorrect one, the model is encouraged to be lenient and let through more false
positives in order to be sure to catch as many true positives as possible.
This effect is easily observable in Figure 6.4. While the value of W+ yielding the highest F1
score is 2, what constitutes an optimal value is, of course, use-case dependent. Notably, in
my case, I am more interested in high precision than in high recall. I can extract millions
of mentions from PubMed. However, as I saw in previous projects, even a small proportion
of incorrectly linked mentions can cause my data to become unusable, so it is acceptable
to sacrifice recall in order to increase precision. Consequently, I experiment with two addi-
tional values for W+ that were not part of Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al. [1]: W+ = 0.2 and
W+ = 0.5.
While the F1 score is noticeably worse, especially for W+ = 0.2, the model reaches a precision
of around 91% while retaining a recall of around 30%: this is surprisingly good, as I will
show soon when comparing to the baseline. For further experiments, I only consider W+
values of 1 and 0.2 — the first as a comparison to Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al. [1]’s results,
the second as the likely most interesting high-precision model in my case.

7.1
Thoughts and Possible Approaches
In the following, I give four main ideas on how the existing system might be extended. As I
lack time to try all four of them, I offer some thoughts as to what the expected difficulty of
implementing each of them might be, as well as the expected performance gains, to better

7.1.1
More Training Data
Currently, the training data available to the model is limited to mentions that were explic-
itly annotated in the MedMentions [37] dataset. While substantial and currently the largest
available dataset of this type, by the authors’ own admission, MedMentions is still signifi-
cantly limited — in particular, only about 1% of existing UMLS concepts are found in the
corpus.
To work around this problem, most existing EL approaches, including the one I implemented
by Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al. [1], rely on generic features that are not specific to those
concepts encountered in the training set — in other words, they are not learning to classify
mentions of specific UMLS concepts, but rather to build a generic binary classifier that can
take information about a mention on one side and a UMLS concept on the other and decide
whether the mention is of the UMLS concept or not.
Whether it is to enable concept-level classifiers or simply to increase the amount of data
available to train existing methods, and potentially, in our case, to allow for deeper and
77

more powerful neural networks to be used, vastly increasing the amount of training data is
likely to result in substantial improvements to model performance.
Proposed Approach
Of course, simply stating that a larger dataset might improve performance without offering
a way to come by such a dataset is of limited interest — manually curating a dataset such as
MedMentions requires hundreds of hours of expert annotations and is clearly not a feasible
approach.
Fortunately, it might be possible to automatically curate a much larger dataset by using the
lists of surface forms contained in the UMLS.
First, consider that if a surface form of an UMLS concept appears verbatim in a span of text
and that it is known that that surface form is not a surface form of any other concept, then
there is a very high likelihood for that span of text to be a mention of the UMLS concept.
This assumes that the UMLS is exhaustive1 and that the span of text is not a non-medical
English word with an unrelated meaning that also happens to be a surface form of an UMLS
concept. Considering the size and maturity of the UMLS, the first assumption seems rea-
sonable, while the latter might warrant some verification. In any case, such concepts can
be filtered out using a list of common English words or more advanced lexical resources like
WordNet[14].
Given a unique surface form, it is possible to search any biomedical corpus for all spans of
text where that surface form appears verbatim, thus obtaining a substantial list of mentions
for that surface form. However, these mentions are of limited interest: by definition, they are
very easy to link since the candidate generator is guaranteed to find a single candidate with
a similarity of 1. At the same time, if the surface form is a surface form of more than one
concept, it is not possible to use this approach since the found mention could be referring to
any of those concepts.
Rather, the idea is to find mentions of the unambiguous surface forms and replace them
with the ambiguous surface forms to generate “synthetic” mentions. To illustrate how this
might work, consider the UMLS concept VDR Gene2, for which the UMLS lists the possible
surface forms vdr gene, vitamin d receptor, vitamin d receptor gene, and NR1I1 3. Of those
four:
1. vdr gene is also a surface form of VDR Gene (procedure)
2. vitamin d receptor is also a surface form of Vitamin D3 Receptor, VDR protein,
human, and many more

3There are actually a lot more possible surface forms — I only list those four for the sake of brevity.

With only this information, it might, in many cases, be difficult even for a human to decide

Of course, the model can get a lot of information from the text of the preferred term —

information about the contexts in which the preferred term appeared. However, it is likely
possible to increase the information available to the model or present it in a way that is more
amenable to learning by training the neural network.

Semantic type of the target UMLS concept

concept carries a lot information about the candidate. Currently, the semantic type is made

feature.
Providing BERT with a definition or example usage for the candidate UMLS

Currently, as explained above, only the preferred term is used as input to BERT.

synonym for the VDR Gene (procedure). Thus, the model might gain from being presented
with an example usage for the concept (which can be found in the literature by using a similar
approach to that for generating synthetic mentions described in the previous section) or by
being given a definition (which the UMLS provides for many but not all UMLS concepts).

for the target UMLS concept:
currently, the mention and the target concept are given
together as input to BERT, separated by the [SEP] token. BERT was indeed intended to
be used for classification in this manner, but in its “basic” pre-trained form, BERT was only

BERT was designed to be fine-tuned for a specific downstream task — in other words, the
BERT model itself is meant to be re-trained with a specific objective function tailored to the
task at hand. Instead, because of time and hardware constraints, I merely use the outputs

from a “basic” BERT model to train a much smaller neural network to rank candidates and
perform span selection. An alternative approach might be, instead of using a single vector
containing the BERT representation for both the mention and the target concept, to use
two separate representations, one for the mention and one for the target concept. This way,
the task of classifying “whether the two sentences refer to the same concept” is entirely the
responsibility of the (trained) neural classifier, and BERT is used exclusively for computing
a dense representation for each of the two inputs.
Pros and Cons
The difficulty and gains of this approach depend entirely on which type of additional infor-
mation I choose to provide to the model. Adding an embedding for the semantic type is
very straightforward, but I also expect relatively few benefits — after all, the semantic type
is already available to the model, albeit in a perhaps less usable form.
Providing usage examples and definitions is likely to yield the greatest benefits. However, it
is also the hardest to implement: finding usage examples would need to follow the approach
described in the previous section (albeit only for those UMLS concepts that appear as can-
didates, not for all UMLS concepts), and is likely to be very slow in a naive implementation
or require a lot of effort to implement efficiently.
Finally, it is hard to know the impact of separating the input representation in two — I found
little mention of this in the literature. While merely changing the input representation to
use two BERT representations is easy, it will likely require significant tuning of the structure
of the classifier neural network (as it would result in almost doubling the number of input
parameters).
To be done properly, this, in turn, would require a very computationally
expensive search over possible network architectures.
7.1.3
Adapting the Model or Loss Function to Take the Hierarchical
Structure of the UMLS Into Account
So far, nothing of what I have done leverages the hierarchical information embedded in the
UMLS. In the literature review, I found two main approaches that take advantage of this
type of knowledge. First, systems that use hierarchical or graph structure to define or modify
a loss function. The intuition is that if a mention is matched to a concept that is close to
the true concept in a knowledge graph or a hierarchical ontology like the UMLS, then the
loss should be lower than if it was matched to a completely unrelated concept.
Second, approaches such as [36], which learn embeddings for UMLS concepts and make
learning is-a relationships between UMLS concepts a secondary learning objective (the idea
being that the embeddings will contain information about the hierarchical structure, which
will be used when linking mentions).
I briefly discuss how each of these approaches might be implemented.
82
==PAGE:89==
7.1. Thoughts and Possible Approaches
Including hierarchical information in the loss function:
currently, the loss function
used for both the candidate ranker and span selector simply considers whether the chosen
candidate is the same as the correct concept or not. However, the optimisation process
might be better guided by a finer-grained loss function that takes into account how close the
chosen candidate is to the correct concept. Intuitively, it is better if the system links the
mention “vitamin D3” to the concept “vitamin D2” than if it links it to the concept “brain
cancer”, even though those are strictly speaking both incorrect. There are several possible
definitions of “closeness”, such as shortest path length or distance from the closest common
parent. One possibility that makes intuitive sense would be to use the shortest path length
in case the matched candidate is a parent of the correct concept and infinity otherwise. This
would model the idea that it is acceptable to select a broader concept than the one that is
actually mentioned (or at least better than a completely unrelated one). However, this needs
to be verified experimentally because it might also be the case that it would lead the model
to be less precise and prefer choosing UMLS concepts that are “higher up” in the hierarchy
(and thus more likely to be closer to a lot of concepts) over more specific ones.
Including information about the hierarchical structure when learning embed-
dings for UMLS concepts:
In my literature review, I describe the approach taken by
Murty*, Verga*, Vilnis, et al. [36] to do Entity Linking while taking the hierarchical structure
of the UMLS into account. To recapitulate, the idea is to learn an embedding eci for every
concept ci in the UMLS, and at the same time, train a classifier which, given a mention in
context and the concept ci, computes a score that should be high if the mention is a mention
of the concept ci. This is similar to the approach taken by Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al.
[1], which I have implemented, but differs in the fact that it learns unique embeddings for
every UMLS concept, while Mohan, Angell, Monath, et al. [1] relies on a pretrained language
model to compute embeddings for the UMLS concepts.
At the same time, to capture the hierarchical structure of the UMLS, Murty*, Verga*, Vil-
nis, et al. [36] add a secondary objective to the model that attempts to learn a bilinear
mapping A between concepts such that eciAecj should be high if ci is a parent of cj, and low
otherwise. A is not used at inference, but since eci and ecj are learned at the same time as
A, it forces the model to include information about the hierarchical structure of the UMLS
into the concept representations eci, which can then be used by the classifier when linking
mentions.
The described approach cannot be directly applied to the model I currently implemented.
Indeed, I do not currently learn embeddings for the UMLS concepts, but only a common rep-
resentation for both the mention and the candidate concept. Using the approach I describe
in subsection 7.1.2, I could theoretically modify my model to instead learn embeddings for
the mentions and UMLS concepts separately, and thus apply the same idea as in Murty*,
Verga*, Vilnis, et al. [36]. There is, however, another issue with this approach: unlike Mo-
han, Angell, Monath, et al. [1], Murty*, Verga*, Vilnis, et al. [36] need to learn embeddings
for all concepts that they wish to be able to link to. Since MedMentions only contains

mentions for a small subset of the concepts in the UMLS, this approach is not currently
feasible. It might be implemented after getting more training data in the way I describe in
subsection 7.1.1.
Pros and Cons
The first approach of modifying the loss function to weigh the loss by the distance between
the predicted concept and true concept would likely require a significant amount of work
and experimentation to get working properly. It requires finding an efficient way to check
the distance between two concepts4, and experimenting with different distance metrics. It
is also unclear how much of a benefit this would bring to the model: intuitively, it is more
likely to help with convergence (by producing a smoother loss function) than with the final
performance.
The second approach might have more impact on the final performance, however, its im-

counteract this, I tried to increase regularisation by doubling the amount of dropout between
layers — this led to a marginal improvement but an overall performance that was still worse
than that of the baseline.

4. Writing a simple parser to read local abstracts: abstracts are stored locally as XML fils,

slow considering I only need to extract the abstract text. I replaced it with a much
simpler (and faster) one that extracts the abstract’s text and ignores all additional
metadata.
All this brought the time required to generate examples down to around 8 hours, which is
much more reasonable.

that the majority of concepts for which it was not possible to find examples are relatively
obscure concepts that are unlikely to be the correct match.

The performance for the candidate ranking step using the usage examples, shown in Fig-

exactly why; however, one possibility could be that the two very different types of input
(“None Found” rather than the usage example) might prevent the model from learning prop-

original model, lack of positional encodings might mean that the model “does not know
what to focus on” — which is made worse by having now a lot more context that might
confuse the model.
For previous experiments, I did not focus on the span selection step because the under-
whelming results of the candidate ranking had me doubtful of any useful impact on span
selection. Here, however, I was curious to see the effects of using usage examples on the span
selection step as well: the fact that, as I mentioned, the proportion of incorrect matches lack-
ing an example usage is much higher than for correct examples indicates that presence or
absence of examples in itself contains information about whether an example is correct or not.
Indeed, the results shown in Figure 8.7 are promising: recall is improved in all cases, and
precision is either identical or slightly improved. The previous top-performing high-precision
span selection model (W+ = 0.2, input normalisation, pooled outputs) had, for a target pre-
cision of 91%, a recall of 34%. By contrast, the new top-performing W+ = 0.2 model (input
normalisation, averaged outputs) has a recall of 37% for the same precision — a small but
significant improvement.
While I conclude my experiments here, there are, of course, many more experiments that
one could run to try and include usage examples. One idea could be to remove concepts for
which there are no examples, rather than using a dummy string “None Found”. Another idea
could be to use a different feature for usage examples rather than just appending them to the
current input representation. In this vein, it could also be possible to obtain a more accurate
representation by averaging several examples for each concepts. Finally, each of those ideas
might warrant a new step of hyperparameter tuning, which I have not done here.

Chapter 9
Conclusion and Further Work
This thesis was motivated by the need for a better way to match potential concepts extracted
from biomedical literature to an ontology, with the goal of building a large knowledge graph
of known biomedical relations. I started with a literature review in which I looked both at my
main problem of Entity Linking and at the closely related field of Word Sense Disambigua-
tion.
After dedicating some time to understanding Transformer-based language models,
which form the basis for most of the top-performing modern techniques in those fields, I
focused on replicating an existing technique that claims state-of-the-art results for the prob-
lem of Entity Linking on the UMLS. I initially failed to obtain comparable performance to
that described by the techniques’ authors, but after experimenting with a few aspects that
were not explicitly mentioned in the original paper, I was able to obtain similar results. I
then took some time to think about possible ways to extend the model to improve perfor-
mance and decided to implement two of them: first, I tried replacing the language model
used by the system (BioBERT) with one that was supposedly better suited to biomedical
Entity Linking (SAPBERT). Second, I attempted to give more information to the model by
encoding more information about concepts that might correspond to the mention and by
giving the model examples of how a concept is usually used. Unfortunately, neither of those
approaches successfully improved the system’s performance.
In this last section, I reflect a bit on how well my objective was achieved and what I learned
along the way. I also discuss what went well, what did not, and what some possible further
experiments and improvements could be.
9.1
Is the Problem Solved?
While the Entity Linking system I developed is without a doubt a substantial improvement
over what I used in previous projects, whether the current system is performant enough to
be used to build the biomedical knowledge graph I want to obtain needs to be verified on
the actual data.

Adding a “pre-selector” to the model:
As I mention in the previous section, one of
the weakest points of the model is that currently, the model attempts to link any mention of
a UMLS concept. In general, this is indeed the objective in Entity Linking systems. In my
case, however, the objective is to build a knowledge graph of relations between biomedical
concepts, and it is important that the included concepts be specific enough that the relations
actually carry information. There are two cases where the current system can go wrong.
First, the sentences from which the relations (and thus the concepts) are extracted are not
specific enough. For instance, in the sentence “In the first group, inoculation was associated
with improvement in symptoms”, the RE system might identify the relation (inoculation,
ASSOCIATION, symptom reduction) — but both concepts are meaningless on their own,
as they refer to information that was likely introduced earlier in the text (i.e., which drug
was inoculated, and which syptoms decreased ). Second, if the text makes a claim about a
very specific concept, but the EL system incorrectly links only a smaller part of that concept
(which would result in an incorrect relation).This is a rather subtle problem because in some
cases, it is not entirely clear how much “specificity” is needed: in the phrase “acute pancreatic
cancer in caucasian patients”, what would or would not be acceptable mentions? “cancer”
is too vague, “pancreatic cancer” might or might not be specific enough, “acute pancreatic
cancer” is good, and “acute pancreatic cancer in caucasian patients” is unlikely to appear in
the UMLS. This could be at least partially addressed by adding an additional model that
“screens” mentions to establish whether or not they are specific enough to be included in the
knowledge graph.
Foregoing the UMLS entirely:
so far, I have assumed that the ideal way to construct
a knowledge graph was by having the nodes correspond to concepts defined in a biomed-
ical ontology. However, while manually evaluating the results for the relations extracted
from the literature, it was clear that a lot of concepts were either entirely absent from the
UMLS, in which case the information would be lost, and a lot of other concepts were the
combination of two or more existing UMLS concepts: for example, “staphylococcus aureus
infection of the upper respiratory tract” is a combination of the concepts “Staphylococcus
aureus infection” and “Upper Respiratory Infections”. This is problematic because it is not
clear how to represent this information in the knowledge graph.
One possible approach would be, instead of relying on an existing ontology to which concepts
can be matched, to build a knowledge graph from the ground up by directly inferring whether

9.1.2
What Went Well, What Did Not, and What I Learned Along
the Way.
As is often the case with this type of project, my expectations for how I would be spending
the majority of my time were rather far removed from reality. In particular, I had to ded-
icate a lot of effort to profiling and optimising various aspects of the data preparation and
model training pipelines to make them run in a reasonable time. Reproducing an existing
technique described in a paper also took significantly more time than I had hoped, and it
was frustrating that I ended up spending most of my thesis on that rather than on experi-
menting with my own ideas. With that being said, this project was a tremendous learning
opportunity all along — there is much to be learned from trying to reproduce other people’s
work, and all the profiling and optimisation forced me to become very familiar with aspects
of machine learning that I had not been exposed to before, such as GPU optimisation and
memory management.
It was also satisfying to end up with a system that is a marked improvement over what
I started with, and that may, perhaps, someday be used in a larger context of biomedical
knowledge extraction for computer-aided diagnosis.

Appendix A
Author Name Disambiguation (AND)
Author Name Disambiguation (AND) refers to the problem of identifying individual authors1
in publication databases (Scopus, PubMed, etc.) — resolving variations in how their names2
are written and identifying distinct authors that share the same name. While similar to
WSD, it has some specificities that set it apart — in particular, unlike for words, disam-
biguating names cannot rely on the context of surrounding words (although it can leverage
the contents of the articles) — rather, AND techniques more often rely on network-based
information contained in author co-authorship relations.
While it was interesting to get a sense of the literature in this field because it is a highly
active area of research that might offer some interesting ideas applicable to my problem, in
retrospect, I do not use any of the techniques described in this section — so to shorten an
already long thesis, I decided to leave it as an Appendix.
A.1
General Considerations
Ferreira, Gonçalves, and Laender [56] give the most widely cited survey of automated meth-
ods for AND. Although it is somewhat dated (it was published in 2012), it provides good
background information on the literature in this area. In the following sections, I summarise
their review and the theoretical aspects of AND, and include where appropriate additional
information from more recent surveys such as [57], which covers advances made between
2010–2016, as well as some information from the “Related Work” section of Santini, Gesese,
Peroni, et al. [58], which gives a good overview of the latest advances in the field. I then
select two interesting papers and delve deeper into the techniques they present.
Ferreira, Gonçalves, and Laender [56] define the AND problem as follows. Consider a set of

Each author name attribute is a reference rj to a real author. The goal of AND is to produce
a function that partitions the set of references r1, . . . , rm into disjoint sets a1, . . . , ak, such
that each partition ak contains all and only all the references to the same author. There
are two principal difficulties to AND, which are the same encountered in biomedical Entity

multiple authors with the same name.
Ferreira, Gonçalves, and Laender [56] proposes a taxonomy for the different approaches to
AND. On one hand, the techniques can be classified according to the general approach —
either author grouping or author assignment. On the other hand, the techniques can be
divided based on the information they leverage. Here, Ferreira, Gonçalves, and Laender
[56] distinguish between three possibilities: techniques that only use citation information,
techniques that rely on additional web information, and techniques that leverage implicit
information (for instance, semantic analysis of the titles or abstracts of publications).
A.1.1
Types of Approach
Ferreira, Gonçalves, and Laender [56] describe two general approaches to AND: Author
Grouping, in which citations of a specific author are grouped together by clustering them
according to some similarity measure, or Author Assignment, in which references are directly
assigned to a specific author. This latter category is further divided into two sub-categories
based on the way that references are assigned — with some techniques being based on
classifiers, and others on clustering methods.
Author Grouping
Author Grouping techniques revolve around the idea of clustering ref-
erences to the same author according to some similarity measure, with the objective to
maximise cluster purity (i.e., clusters should only contain references to a single author) and
minimise fragmentation (i.e., authors whose references are split amongst separate clusters).
Ferreira, Gonçalves, and Laender [56] identify three general ways to define similarity func-
tions:
1. Predefined functions that are tailored to a specific collection of records — typical
examples include string similarity or set similarity measures.
2. Learned similarity functions that use machine learning techniques to learn a function
that maps pairs of references to either one (if they refer to the same author) or 0 (if
they do not).
3. Graph-based similarity functions which leverage (usually) co-authorship networks to
define similarity in various ways.
Author Assignment
Author Assignment methods explicitly construct a model for each
author (based, for example, on the probability for a given author to publish in a specific jour-

Two Case Studies
A.2.1
“A Knowledge Graph Embeddings based Approach for Au-
thor Name Disambiguation using Literals”
“A Knowledge Graph Embeddings based Approach for Author Name Disambiguation using
Literals” is a recently published paper by Santini, Gesese, Peroni, et al. [58] that describes
an approach to AND that combines network embeddings (i.e., information derived from co-
authorship and citation networks) with text embeddings (i.e., information contained in the
article themselves) and also with various types of metadata. While it cannot be applied
directly to my problem of matching UMLS concepts (as it relies on specific information
contained in bibliographical records), it is an interesting example to describe in some more
detail due to its use of multimodal embeddings as well as general techniques like blocking
and clustering that can be applied to other tasks.
On a high level, Santini, Gesese, Peroni, et al. [58]’s approach consists of three steps. First,
they construct multimodal graph embeddings, which are a dense vector representation of

A.2.2
“On Disambiguating Authors:
Collaboration Network Re-
construction in a Bottom-up Manner”
Li, Zhu, Zhou, et al. [61] present an interesting approach to AND, which they frame as the
problem of deriving the author co-authorship network, starting from the set of author names
associated with each paper.
A co-authorship network is defined as a graph G = (V, E, P), where the vertices V represent
authors, and an edge (u, v) ∈E represents a co-authorship relationship which is associated
with a set of papers P uv corresponding to all papers co-authored by u and v. It is easy to see
that, indeed, AND is equivalent to the problem of deriving a co-authorship network starting
from ambiguous author names associated with each paper.
For reasons I will detail further, the approach detailed by Li, Zhu, Zhou, et al. [61] is not
directly applicable to the Entity Linking problem, but it offers a few interesting ideas that
can be translated to my problem. I will summarize their approach and give some thoughts
to how it can be applied to the Entity Linking problem.
Li, Zhu, Zhou, et al. [61] claim that existing embedding-based state-of-the-art approaches
to constructing co-authorship networks work in a top-down manner: initially, all identical
names are assumed to refer to the same person, and an initial network is constructed like this.
All papers associated with a given name are then embedded into dense vectors, which are
clustered — with clusters being assumed to correspond to separate authors. This, according
to the authors, has two main issues: first, that the entire system needs to be re-trained to be
able to process any new papers. And second, that part of the information used to construct
the embeddings comes from the initial, low-quality co-authorship network.

Li, Zhu, Zhou, et al. [61] propose a different, bottom-up approach for building a collaboration
network consisting of two steps. Initially, they build what they call a stable collaboration
network (SCN), which can be thought of as a network where authors sharing the same name
are matched with very high precision (i.e., nodes are likely to represent a single author) but
low recall (i.e., there might be many nodes corresponding to the same author). The obective
is to be able, in the second step, to leverage high-quality network similarity measures based on
this initial network to construct a more accurate network by merging nodes that correspond
to the same authors.
η-Stable Collaborative Relations
To build the initial high-confidence network, they
leverage the observation that the probability for two authors to collaborate on n papers,
given that they collaborated at least once, is much higher than what would be expected if
collaborations were random. In other words, successive collaborations are not independent
— authors that collaborated once are much more likely to collaborate again.
Furthermore, given that a1, a2 are two different authors that share the name a, and likewise,
b1, b2 are two different authors that share the name b, then the probability that both a1, b1
and a2, b2 collaborate on a large number of papers is extremely small. Conversely, if the
names a and b appear together in a large number of papers, there is a high probability that
a and b refer to the same two persons in all those papers.
Based on this observation, they extract a list of what they call η-stable collaborative relations
— that is, pairs of author names (a, b) such that a and b appear together in at least η papers.

Stable Collaborative Network
The list of η-stable relations is used to construct the

Suppose the current SCN contains a single relation (a, b), and that the relation (ai, ci) is
currently being added. If the relation (bj, cj) also appears in the list of η-stable relations,
then authors ai and bj are assumed to be the same as the ones already in the network a and
b, respectively. Therefore, the node c is added to the network to form a triangle with a and
b. If the relation (bj, cj) does not appear in the list of η-stable relations, however, then ai
is considered to be a different person than a, and a new pair of nodes a, c is added to the

Global Collaborative Network
Once the SCN is constructed, the second step is to find
a way to determing which nodes of the SCN refer to the same author and can thus be merged.
This is done by training a probabilistic model that takes various similarity measures into
account to determine whether two nodes with the same name are likely to refer to the same
author or not. The specific similarity measures used are not so important to describe here,
but it is worth noting that they fall into three categories:
1. Graph similarity measures, based on the topology of the SCN.
2. “Venue” similarity — for two nodes a1 and a2, it is possible to determine two sets of
publishing venues (journals, conferences. . . ) in which the neighbourhoods of authors
a1 and a2 have published papers. Venue Similarity measures are then computed as set
similarity between these sets.
3. Topic similarity — an author is likely to write papers in the same general field. Topic
similarity measures are computed using word embeddings of paper titles published by
the neighbourhoods of the two nodes.

A.2.3
Closing Notes
As I mention earlier, the approach in Li, Zhu, Zhou, et al. [61] cannot directly be applied to
the problem of matching entities to the UMLS, but their ideas might be adapted to fit the
problem. While there is no direct equivalent for “co-authorship” relations, it may be possible
to use a similar idea by leveraging co-occurrence — just like authors that collaborated
once are more likely to collaborate again on other papers, related entities are likely to
appear together more often than a random distribution would predict, so similar “stable
co-occurrence networks” may be constructed.
