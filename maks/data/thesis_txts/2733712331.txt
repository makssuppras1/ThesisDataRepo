Abstract
Approximately 1.1 million people suffer from a stroke in Europe each year, with an ex-
pected increase in the upcoming years. As a result, the need for adequate post-stroke
rehabilitation becomes more and more apparent. Home-based rehabilitation provides a
valuable solution to this problem, allowing patients to recover from the comfort of their
homes. Mirror therapy is an example of such a therapeutic approach, which, although
effective, lacks immersiveness and a personalized approach. The conceptualization of a
robotic hand, controllable by the stroke patient, stems from a desire to enhance the ef-
ficacy and experience of therapeutic interventions, particularly in comparison to conven-
tional mirror therapy. Enabling stroke patients to control a robotic hand has the potential
to enhance immersion levels, resulting in a more engaging and lifelike rehabilitation expe-
rience within the convenience of their homes. This approach aims to provide a dynamic
and interactive approach, allowing individuals to actively participate in their rehabilitation
journey. The goal of this thesis is to build a robotic hand using 3D-printed parts and hobby
appliances, controlled by computer vision, to research the sense of embodiment a patient
experiences during the rubber hand experiment. Two controlled within-subjects crossover
studies are conducted in this thesis to validate whether participants report a sense of em-
bodiment and examine if participants perform better during memorization tasks with the
help of the robotic hand. The results show that participants report a higher sense of em-
bodiment with the active robotic hand as well as a significantly increased performance
in the memorization task (F1,56 = 4.047, p < .05) & (F1,56 = 19.463, p < .05). In con-
clusion, the development of a robotic hand for stroke rehabilitation presents a promising
solution to enhance the effectiveness of home-based therapies, addressing the growing
need for post-stroke rehabilitation. The results of controlled studies demonstrate not only
an increased sense of embodiment among participants using the active robotic hand but
also a significant improvement in performance during memorization tasks, highlighting the
potential impact of this innovative approach on the rehabilitation process.
Keywords: Stroke Rehabilitation, Embodiment, Rubber Hand Illusion, Body Ownership
Illusion, Human-Robot Interaction

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The impact of cerebral stroke is felt worldwide. In Europe, 9 million people currently live
while dealing with the ramifications of a stroke, and this number is expected to increase
by 12 million in 2040 [1]. Equally disconcerting is the mortality toll, with annual stroke-
related fatalities in Europe numbering between 30 to 170 per 100.000 of the population [2].
Declining death rates due to better and quicker treatment means that there will be more
people surviving their strokes and living with the consequences. Therefore, the projected
overall expense associated with stroke in the EU, encompassing both healthcare and
non-healthcare expenditures, which stood at approximately 45 billion euros in 2015, is
expected to increase significantly [2]. This troubling situation is not exclusive to Europe;
rather, it is reflected in different parts of the world. In the United States, for instance, an
annual tally surpassing 795,000 individuals fall victim to strokes, of which approximately
610,000 are initial or recurrent incidents [3]. These figures are especially concerning when
considering that strokes constitute a leading cause of enduring disabilities of a severe
nature.
Despite the troubling statistics and the clear gravity of the problem, therapeutic inter-
ventions can significantly improve the disabilities resulting from stroke. Within a mere
three months of therapy, patients typically experience a reduction in disability levels [4].
Nonetheless, research indicates that a substantial proportion of stroke survivors continue
to struggle with chronic physical or cognitive impairments even after three months of con-
ventional therapeutic regimens [5]. Furthermore, in terms of upper limb functionality, ap-
proximately 65% of stroke survivors remain incapable of reintegrating their affected hand
into routine activities upon completion of therapy [6].
This highlights the importance of late-stage stroke rehabilitation, which takes place be-
yond the initial six-month phase and plays a crucial role in the patient’s journey toward
regaining independence. Typically conducted within the stroke survivor’s home environ-
ment, this phase lacks a defined endpoint and can extend over an extended duration,
sometimes spanning years [7]. The effectiveness of late-stage rehabilitation heavily re-
lies on the individual’s level of motivation, effort, and the goals they set for themselves
[8]. Consequently, individuals in this predicament often require ongoing assistance to
perform activities of daily living (ADL), such as bathing, dressing, eating, and toileting [9].
Moreover, sustaining motivation and obtaining suitable guidance becomes increasingly
challenging as the patient distances themselves from immediate caregivers.
The integration of technology can be a promising helping hand to alleviate this predica-
ment. Technological and robotic-based therapies have garnered increased attention and
have undergone rapid evolution in recent decades. Research and development in the
field of rehabilitation robotics have made substantial strides, particularly in the domain of
neurorehabilitation for post-stroke patients [10]. The conceptualization of a robotic hand,
controllable by the stroke patient, stems from a desire to enhance the efficacy of thera-
peutic interventions, particularly in comparison to conventional mirror therapy. Traditional
mirror therapy (Figure 1.1), while valuable, has limitations in its ability to provide a fully
immersive and personalized experience for individuals undergoing rehabilitation [11] [12].
The integration of a robotic hand controlled by the stroke patient holds the potential to
elevate immersion levels, creating a more engaging and realistic rehabilitation process

1.1.1
Thesis project context
This thesis project was conducted in close collaboration with the ReHyb project (www.
rehyb.eu), serving as its overarching framework. ReHyb is a European international re-
search project consisting of multiple consortium members from across the continent. It is
financially supported by the Horizon 2020 Framework Programme of the European Union.
This thesis was formulated within a research group housed at the Department of Technol-
ogy, Management, and Economics at the Technical University of Denmark, which is also
an integral part of the ReHyb project.
The primary objective of the ReHyb project is the development of a personalized upper-
limb device that assists individuals during home-based rehabilitation, optimizing training
efficiency through serious gaming and enhancing the user experience by aiding in activi-
ties of daily living (ADL). This project encompasses a diverse array of disciplines, ranging
from robotics and AI and cognitive science to psychology, among others.
1.2
The thesis project
As part of a specialized course at the Technical University of Denmark, the author con-
tributed to the development of a proof-of-concept (POC) 3D-printed robotic hand. The
hand is assembled with 3D-printed parts provided by the InMoov project for its structure,
complemented by Parallax servo motors that serve as its actuators. This combination
makes it a cost-effective and easily obtainable device. While this system, in its current
form, may not find direct application in rehabilitation, it serves as a valuable tool for ex-
ploring if a piece of even simple equipment may create an immersive experience with
potential utility in a rehabilitation context. Notably, the system is capable of mimicking the
movement of a human hand with the help of computer vision.
Throughout this master’s thesis project, we will investigate the effects of embodiment
concerning a 3D-printed robotic hand during stroke rehabilitation therapy sessions. The
thesis aims to ascertain whether a sense of body ownership illusion can be induced over
a robotic hand within a controlled setup similar to the rubber hand experiment, utilizing
the robotic hand as a substitute for the rubber hand [15]. With the help of the robotic
hand, a higher level of engagement can be achieved since the participants actively con-
trol the hand themselves using computer vision, potentially leading to a higher sense of
embodiment. Additionally, participants’ accuracy in memorization tasks with the help of
the robotic hand will be examined and compared to conventional videos of memorization
tasks. To accomplish these objectives, the thesis will conduct a controlled study, involving
the design and execution of experiments with participants, followed by the evaluation of
the obtained results.
1.3
Contribution to existing research
A study by Kalckert & Ehrsson [16] found indirect evidence of a facilitatory effect between
the active and passive movements they defined in their experiment setup. In this study,
Kalckert & Ehrsson refer to a passive and active hand while conducting an experiment
based on the rubber hand illusion. In their setup, they make use of an active hand setup
by having the participant control a wooden hand. This is done by placing the participant’s
right hand in a box with the fake hand on top. With the help of a thin wooden stick, the
participant’s right index finger was connected to the fake hand, enabling the participant to
lift up the right index finger of the fake hand. Results showed that the passive condition
took significantly longer to elicit, which supports the notion of the relatively rapid onset of
the moving rubber hand illusion.

potential for implementation in clinical settings.
Since this research utilizes affordable, readily accessible equipment, it can trigger and
motivate the creation of similar low-fidelity systems. These systems, in turn, have the po-
tential to enhance immersion and embodiment in stroke rehabilitation, thereby contributing
to a more engaging rehabilitation experience.

and,
”Does the use of the robotic hand as auxiliary support lead to an improvement

The answer to this research question leads to another question:
”Is a 3D robotic hand a viable alternative to traditional stroke rehabilitation
methods like mirror therapy?”

2.1
Stroke rehabilitation
Stroke rehabilitation is a multifaceted process aimed at restoring function and improving
the quality of life for individuals who have suffered a stroke. This comprehensive approach
involves several key steps, which are typically tailored to the specific needs and abilities
of the stroke survivor. These steps encompass various therapeutic modalities, medical
interventions, and support systems [17] [18] [19] [20] [21] [22]. This section gives an
overview of the steps taken during stroke rehabilitation.
Stroke rehabilitation starts with an initial assessment and diagnosis that encompasses a
thorough examination of the patient’s medical history, physical condition, neuroimaging
results, and functional deficits. This evaluation aims to determine the stroke’s type, loca-
tion, and severity. Subsequently, the focus shifts to medical stabilization, prioritizing the
stabilization of the patient’s overall condition, managing risk factors, and addressing any
complications that may arise.
In the acute care phase, the primary objective is to minimize brain damage and pre-
vent secondary complications. This often necessitates hospitalization in specialized units,
where interventions such as thrombolytic therapy are administered. Early rehabilitation,
initiated within 24 to 48 hours post-stroke, emphasizes the prevention of complications,
improvement of mobility, and addressing basic daily activities.
This phase integrates
physical, occupational, and speech therapies.
The rehabilitation process maintains a commitment to ongoing, comprehensive assess-
ments to adapt goals and interventions based on the patient’s evolving needs. Intensive
rehabilitation therapies follow, targeting specific impairments. Additionally, psychosocial
support is provided to address the emotional and psychological aspects of recovery.
A key goal of the rehabilitation process is to prepare patients for community reintegration,
involving training in various activities to regain independence. Long-term management
strategies may encompass continued therapy or periodic reassessment, lifestyle modifi-
cations, and ongoing medical oversight. Lastly, supportive care plays a crucial role by
offering education, resources, and support networks for both stroke survivors and their
families, encompassing community-based programs and caregiver training.
2.2
Recent rehabilitation therapies
Stroke rehabilitation encompasses a range of evidence-based therapies designed to fa-
cilitate recovery and improve functional outcomes following a stroke, such as physical
therapy (PT), Occupational Therapy (OT), Speech and Language Therapy, Cognitive Re-
habilitation, Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy, Mirror Therapy, Virtual Reality and
Gaming, Electrical Stimulation and teletherapy [23] [24] [25] [26] [27] [28] [29] [30] [31].
These therapies are tailored to address the specific physical, cognitive, and communica-
tion deficits that stroke survivors may experience. Among the diverse array of rehabilita-

2.2.1
Mirror therapy
Mirror therapy is a therapeutic technique used to alleviate sensory and motor deficits,
particularly in the affected limb, by employing the principle of visual illusion. A mirror is
strategically placed to reflect the image of the unaffected limb (Figure 2.1), creating the
illusion that the affected limb is moving without difficulty [32]. Mirror therapy capitalizes
on the brain’s neuroplasticity, promoting the rewiring of neural pathways associated with
motor function and sensory perception [33]. By visualizing the affected limb in a nor-
mal state, the brain is encouraged to reestablish connections and improve motor control.
Mirror Therapy is primarily used for stroke survivors with upper limb impairments, includ-
ing weakness, reduced range of motion, and pain. It has shown promise in improving
limb motor function, reducing pain perception, and enhancing overall upper limb recovery
[34]. This study aimed to investigate the impact of combining Task-based mirror therapy
(TBMT) with conventional rehabilitation therapy on upper limb motor function, motor re-
covery, activities of daily living, and spasticity in subacute stroke patients with moderate
to severe impairments, comparing it to the effects of occupational therapy.
Both the TBMT group and the control group exhibited improvements in upper limb motor
function and activities of daily living as assessed by the Brunnstrom Assessment, Fugl-
Meyer Assessment (FMA), and Modified Barthel Index (MBI) scales. However, it’s worth
noting that a statistically significant difference between the two groups was observed only
in the FMA assessment, with the TBMT group showing superior results (P<0.05).
These findings align with the results of a previous study conducted by Mirela Cristina et
al. [35], which focused on 33 subacute stroke patients with mild to moderate upper limb
impairments. In that study, patients were divided into an experimental group receiving
TBMT and a control group, both undergoing an 8-week treatment regimen.
2.2.2
teletherapy
Telecommunication Therapy, also known as telerehabilitation or remote rehabilitation,
leverages technology to deliver rehabilitation services remotely. This approach allows
stroke survivors to access therapy from their homes or other convenient locations through
video conferencing or telehealth platforms [37].
Telecommunication therapy provides
stroke survivors with access to expert therapists and rehabilitation programs, overcom-
ing geographical barriers and improving accessibility to care. Furthermore, it enables
therapists to remotely assess progress, monitor compliance, and modify treatment plans
as needed [38]. Telecommunication therapy is valuable for individuals with stroke who
may face challenges attending in-person rehabilitation sessions due to geographical re-
moteness, mobility issues, or other constraints. It facilitates ongoing therapy, promotes
adherence to treatment plans, and enhances overall convenience [39]. The average age
of the participants in this study was 73.6 years, with a standard deviation of 9.9 years. The
delivery and remote monitoring of exercise through the system were found to be feasible.
All participants successfully completed the study, surpassing the recommended number
of sessions by an average of 125% and the prescribed number of repetitions by 104%.
Participants expressed high levels of satisfaction with the system, rating its usability at
78%, enjoyment at 70%, and acknowledging significant benefits with an 80% approval
rating. Importantly, there were no reports of adverse events during the study.In terms of
physical performance, the mean difference between pre-and post-intervention total short
performance scores was 1.4 (95% CI 0.79, 2.00), indicating a positive impact of the inter-
vention.

2.2.3
Virtual Reality and Gaming
VR and Gaming interventions involve the use of immersive, computer-generated envi-
ronments and interactive games to engage stroke survivors in rehabilitation exercises.
Studies suggest that these interventions make therapy more enjoyable and motivating
[40] [41]. VR and Gaming interventions provide real-time feedback, challenge stroke sur-
vivors with progressively complex tasks, and offer a dynamic and stimulating rehabilita-
tion environment. They encourage active participation and repetition, which are essential
for neuroplasticity and skill acquisition [42]. VR and Gaming interventions are versatile
and can target various aspects of stroke rehabilitation, including motor function, balance,
cognitive skills, and coordination. They have shown promise in improving motor recovery,
balance, and cognitive function while maintaining patient engagement and motivation [43]
[44] [45]. In the study conducted by Afsar et al., the use of virtual reality is researched to
gain further understanding of how it can pose as a viable solution in stroke rehabilitation.
The core idea behind their approach is that simulating familiar tasks through repetitive
functional exercises can assist and amplify the process of neuroplastic activation. The
outcomes of this review strongly advocate for the integration of Fully Immersive Virtual
Reality (FIVR) into the rehabilitation protocols designed for stroke patients. Their findings
show significant improvements in the Brunnstrom stages and scores on the Fugl-Meyer
upper extremity assessment, Box&Block Test, and Functional Independence Measure
for both the experimental and control groups when comparing post-treatment results to
baseline. Specifically, the experimental group demonstrated significantly greater improve-
ments in Brunnstrom stage-upper extremity and Box&Block Test scores compared to the
control group. In contrast, the Brunnstrom stage-hand, Functional Independence Mea-
sure gains, and Fugl-Meyer gains exhibited similar progress between the two groups.
As a result of these observations, they conclude that FIVR stands out as a versatile and
comprehensive approach that can be tailored to the specific abilities of individual patients,
leading to positive outcomes [43].
Demeco et al. conducted a systematic review of the use of VR in stroke rehabilitation.
They analyzed 12 randomized control trials with a total of 350 participants, with a mean

Efficacy of Mirror Therapy
Upper limb stroke rehabilitation remains a challenging endeavor due to the myriad of mo-

of upper limb stroke rehabilitation remain subjects of scrutiny and ongoing investigation

Efficacy of Mirror Therapy
Mirror therapy’s efficacy in upper limb stroke rehabilitation is grounded in its capacity to
harness neuroplasticity, the brain’s intrinsic ability to rewire itself in response to sensory-
motor experiences. Through a seemingly simple yet cleverly designed setup —an optical
mirror positioned to reflect the unaffected limb as a ’virtual’ image of the impaired limb—
mirror therapy prompts the brain to perceive visual feedback indicating the execution of
movements by the weakened limb. This perceived movement, often described as a ’mir-
rored reflection’ of the functional limb, serves as a potent stimulus for neural rewiring,
ultimately facilitating motor recovery [51].
One of the areas where mirror therapy has demonstrated particular promise is in the mit-
igation of upper limb paresis, a debilitating consequence of stroke that often hampers
daily activities and independence. Clinical observations and research findings have indi-
cated that mirror therapy may enhance motor function in the affected upper limb [28]. By
promoting neural plasticity, the therapy fosters the re-establishment of motor control and
coordination, allowing patients to regain a measure of functional independence [52].
Another noteworthy aspect of mirror therapy lies in its potential to alleviate pain associ-
ated with the enduring effects of upper limb stroke [53]. Many stroke survivors endure
chronic pain, with neuropathic pain and complex regional pain syndrome being common
afflictions [54]. The mechanism behind mirror therapy’s pain-relieving effects resides in
its capacity to induce sensory and motor congruence. By offering visual input that aligns
with perceived movement, mirror therapy appears to mitigate pain sensations, offering
relief and improved quality of life for individuals grappling with post-stroke pain.
Applicability and Limitations
While mirror therapy holds considerable promise, it may not have universal applicability
in the context of upper limb stroke rehabilitation. The therapy’s success hinges on the pa-
tient’s ability to perform exercises correctly, which, in some cases, necessitates guidance
and practice under the supervision of a skilled healthcare professional [55]. This requi-
site guidance ensures that patients engage in mirror therapy with precision, optimizing its
potential benefits, while mitigating the risk of incorrect execution. Furthermore, the ap-
plicability of mirror therapy may be different from person to person. Some people do not
respond as well as others to mirror therapy [56]. Factors such as the nature and severity
of the condition being treated, an individual’s overall health, and their willingness to com-
mit to the therapy regimen can all influence its effectiveness. It’s essential for healthcare
professionals to carefully assess each patient’s specific needs and monitor their progress
closely to determine whether mirror therapy is the right approach for their rehabilitation

Embodiment
Embodiment, a foundational concept in cognitive science and neuroscience, represents
a profound exploration of the intricate relationship between the human body and the mind
[86]. Central to this notion is the assertion that the corporeal form and the cognitive pro-
cesses of an individual are profoundly intertwined, challenging the age-old Cartesian du-
alism that posited a stark separation between the body and the self [87]. At its essence,
embodiment asserts that our cognitive faculties, sensory perceptions, motor actions, and
even abstract cognitive processes are not isolated phenomena but are fundamentally
shaped and influenced by our bodily experiences and interactions with the external world
[86]. An integral facet of this concept is the notion of ”body ownership,” which asserts that
individuals possess a profound sense of ownership over their own bodies. This sense
of ownership extends beyond mere physicality; it influences our conscious experiences,
profoundly influencing how we perceive and interact with the world around us. In light of
this, embodiment underscores the holistic integration of the body into our cognitive and
perceptual processes, emphasizing the inseparability of physical and mental dimensions
in shaping our understanding of the world and our very selves [88].
Embodiment’s roots trace back to philosophical inquiries into the nature of perception and
cognition, gaining traction in the latter half of the 20th century as a central theme in cogni-
tive science and neuroscience. Notably, theorists like George Lakoff and Mark Johnson
have contributed significantly to the conceptual development of embodiment, contending
that abstract thought processes are often grounded in the sensorimotor experiences of
the body [89]. This perspective challenges traditional cognitive theories that portray the
mind as a disembodied entity, proposing instead that our cognition is inherently grounded
in our bodily interactions with the environment [90].
In considering the versatile nature of embodiment, it is evident to explore how factors
such as skin color and the spatial orientation of body parts contribute to this intricate re-
lationship. Research has delved into the influence of skin color on the perception of body

thoughts into tangible experiments, unlocking a new dimension of understanding regard-

information, effectively tricking the brain into embracing the virtual limb as part of the self.

3.1
Understanding Hand Tracking Fundamentals
A Python script was written to successfully track and analyze a person’s hand, delivering
a signal that denotes the number of fingers raised. Known as hand pose estimation, hand
tracking stands as a fundamental task in computer vision, aiming to precisely and reliably
detect and follow the movement and configuration of human hands in real-time [101] [102].
The central objective of hand tracking involves the estimation of the 3D pose and motion
of the hand based on 2D image or video data [103]. This encompasses the determination
of positions and orientations of hand joints and the overall shape of the hand. The hand
tracking process starts with receiving image or video data through cameras or depth sen-
sors [104]. These sensors capture the visual aspects and movement of the hand from
varied perspectives, supplying crucial visual cues for subsequent analysis. Following
data acquisition, diverse feature extraction techniques come into play to identify relevant
information for hand tracking [105]. These features may involve hand edges, contours,
texture, or even a disparity map of the hand against the background [106].
The detection of features serves to emphasize distinctive aspects of the hand, facilitating
subsequent algorithms in the detection and tracking of the hand’s pose. Once hands are
detected, the next step is to precisely and accurately locate them within the image or
video frames. Localization techniques may encompass geometric analysis, model fitting,
or regression-based approaches [107] [108]. These methods estimate the positions of
hand joints, including fingertips, knuckles, palm center, and the hand’s overall shape and
orientation.
Following accurate localization, the hand-tracking process is initiated. This involves cap-
turing changes in hand poses over time, typically across consecutive frames, by estab-
lishing a correspondence between hand landmarks in different frames. Techniques like
feature matching or optical flow are employed for this purpose [109] [110].
To represent hand poses effectively, various models can be utilized, such as skeletal
models, mesh models, or point clouds [111]. In the context of this project, a skeletal model
was chosen to represent the hand as a set of interconnected joints, while mesh models

offer a more intricate surface representation, and point clouds are used to represent the
hand as a collection of 3D points in space [112] [113].

The Arduino setup
The setup includes an Arduino Uno microcontroller paired with 5 Parallax Inc 4 servo
motors, an Adafruit 16-channel servo shield soldered to the Arduino, and a specialized
power cable delivering 3A at 5-6V. Acting as the central processing unit, the Arduino Uno,
an open-source microcontroller board, oversees and coordinates the movements of the
servo motors. Despite the availability of the Raspberry Pi microcontroller, the Arduino Uno
was chosen for its cost-effectiveness and accessibility. The Parallax servo motor was
selected due to its high quality, accurate positional control, and quick responsiveness,
even though it may be comparatively bulkier than other, smaller hobby servo motors.
Establishing a connection between the Arduino and Parallax servo motors involves ap-
propriate pin allocation. The control wire of the servo motor, responsible for transmitting
positional instructions, links to one of the digital pins on the Arduino. Additionally, the
servo motors require a dedicated power source for optimal functionality, with a specific
power cable chosen for this purpose to administer the correct amperage.
To initiate the setup, the Arduino Uno is powered on by connecting it to a computer through
a USB cable and the external power supply. Following this, the Arduino Uno code is
uploaded to the microcontroller, enabling the execution of specific instructions and control
over the servo motors. Upon successful initialization, the Arduino Uno processes the
code and transmits signals to the Parallax servo motors. These signals, often in the
form of pulse-width modulation (PWM) signals, dictate the desired position, velocity, and
direction of the servo motor [115]. The Parallax servo motor decodes the signals, aligning
the motor shaft with the designated angular position and translating digital instructions
into direct motion, i.e., opening or closing a finger. This process is made possible by the
Arduino Uno’s ability to produce accurate PWM signals [115].

Construction of the 3D printed hand
All of the plastic parts were 3D-printed using the open source prints from InMoov [116],
using an Ultimaker S5 3D printer, with the exception of a few minor parts. First, all of
the parts were sanded down to remove any excess print material, as well as all holes
were redrilled so that they were slightly bigger and reduced friction. The build started with
gluing together the forearm pieces and assembling the servo bed. The servo bed had to
be sanded down to fit and accommodate 5 HK15298 servo motors. After making sure that
the servo motors were tightly secured and the cables properly managed, the assembly of
the fingers could begin. Each finger is made up of 5 phalanges plus a fingertip. Several
spare fingers were printed to try out different constructions. It was important that the finger
could smoothly operate when opening and closing while limiting side-to-side movement.
After trying many different materials to join the joints together, the choice was made to use
a standard 3mm printing filament. The holes in the phalanx were redrilled to accommodate
the 3mm filament and then secured to the joints of the fingers by using a heat gun and
pressing down the plastic, creating a button of sorts. Next, all of the wires were guided
through the fingers, into the palm of the hand. Two strings were used per finger, one to pull
the finger open, the other to close it. A few types of wire were tried; standard rope, guitar
strings, nylon braided fish wire, and steel wire. The purpose of the wire was to quickly
and robustly open and close the fingers, so the wire should be strong, durable, and have
as little slack as possible. The nylon braided fish wire was used for exactly that reason,
having the least amount of slack, while reducing the risk of breaking after repetitive use.
Next, the wires could be pulled through the wrist and attached to the forearm. The wires
were then attached to a round pulley screwed directly to the servo motor. Again, a few
pulley systems were tested and created, with eventually the choice leading to using a
round pulley wheel instead of a bar. The round pulley wheels’ benefit was that a channel

3.8.1
Problems and improvements
During testing of the robotic hand, one major flaw was found, caused by three problems.
Although the motors reacted to the signal sent by the computer vision algorithm, their
response was very slow and unpredictable, leading to sloppy movement of the fingers
themselves, with some fingers not even being able to be opened and closed. Three
solutions were found to make sure that the robotic hand operated as fast and as smoothly
as possible. First, the HK15298 servo motors were replaced by Parallax Inc 4 servo
motors, which contributed to faster and more accurate response time as well as better
position. Second, an Arduino servo shield was added to make distributing power and
PWM signals easier, providing much-needed coordination to the setup. As a result of
this setup, a more powerful external power supply was needed, which would solve the
problem of some servo motors not being able to operate at the same time. A special
adjustable charger was used to supply the Arduino setup with 5-6V and 3.0A. However,
the problem with the fingers not opening and closing faster and smoothly still resided,
caused by slack in the wires. To combat this issue, a tightening system was devised,
using elastic bands and Teflon cable housing. A lot of room was left between the wrist
and servo bed in the forearm, making it the ideal location to add a tightening system. A
small plastic plate was attached to the side of the servo bed with two elastic bands tied
to it. As it turns out, elastic bands made for the children’s toy fruit of the loom proved to
be the right diameter and tensile strength. To avoid the nylon braided fish wires cutting
into the elastic band, Teflon cable housing was put around the elastic bands. With this
improvement, the wires would be automatically tightened whether they were opened or
closed, leading to a latency of only 90 ms between detection of the finger and activating
movement in that finger.

Controlled study
This project aims to find out whether it is possible to provoke a sense of embodiment
with the usage of a 3D-printed robotic hand similar to the rubber hand experiment. Two
controlled crossover studies are conducted in this thesis to validate whether participants
report a sense of embodiment and examine if participants perform better during memoriza-

were also asked to share any comments they had about or during the experiment. These
additional insights are important to take into account for further improvements or research
in the field of Human-Computer Interaction.
The study consists of two separate experiments, both of which include a test and control
study. The participants were divided into two groups. Group 1 first completed the control
study and then the test study of both experiments and vice-versa for Group 2. The ex-
periments were performed right after each other, while always starting with experiment 1
first.
4.1
Experiment 1
The objective of this experiment is to examine whether or not participants report a sense
of embodiment with the robotic hand. The participant is placed at a table with their hands
resting on the table, palms facing upwards. A divider is placed at the participant’s right
shoulder to occlude the view of the right hand. The participant is asked to wear a long-
sleeved shirt backward so that the left arm is in the right arm sleeve. The right arm is
situated behind the divider and is not inside the sleeve of the backward shirt. The remain-

the robotic hand by opening and closing their fingers, which got recorded by the camera
and sent to the microcontroller in the robotic hand.

4.1.1
Measuring the illusion
The proprioceptive drift (PPD) is used by many studies to gauge whether the rubber hand
illusion has been experienced by the participant. The proprioceptive drift is a localization
bias of the real hand towards the artificial hand and occurs when the rubber hand illusion
is properly executed [118]. Nonetheless, the proprioceptive drift is not yet accepted as the
undisputed indicator of embodiment [119] [120]. The choice was made not to implement
the PPD given the scope and schedule of this thesis.
An alternative approach to gauging embodiment is with the help of physiological data. In
these experiments, the artificial hand is subjected to perceived threats. Sensors are linked
to the original hand which exhibits signals analogous to conditions where the original hand
might be exposed to threats [120]. Again, the choice was made not to implement these
indicators given the scope and schedule of this thesis.
Longo et al. have devised a questionnaire designed to quantify subjective embodiment
[121], a tool extensively employed in the academic literature. The questionnaire was
validated using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) against alternative measures of the
illusion. Initially developed with ten items for assessing the rubber hand illusion, the ques-
tionnaire by Longo et al. can be adapted to align with the parameters of this thesis. Numer-
ous other experiments involving virtual hand scenarios have adopted the questionnaire
by Longo et al. as a foundational tool for their evaluations, often presenting rephrased
and occasionally translated questions [122] [123].

4.1.3
Latency
Research has shown that the rubber hand illusion can be disturbed if there is a delay
between the perceived and experienced stimuli, thus it was crucial that the robotic hand
had as little latency as possible. Shimada et al. discovered that a latency of 300 ms
between visual and tactile stimuli causes a significant decrease in the rubber hand illusion
[124]. Furthermore, other studies report that the latency has to stay under 150 ms to avoid
detection of visuomotor delays [125] [126]. The latency for the robotic hand was measured
to be 90 ms, well below the lower limit of visuomotor delays.
4.2
Experiment 2
The objective of this experiment is to examine whether participants perform better on a
memorization task with a robotic hand or a pre-recorded video of a human hand. The par-
ticipant is placed at a table with their hands resting on the table, palms facing upwards. For
the control study, an iPad is placed in front of them, while during the test study (Figure 4.3),
the robotic hand is placed in front of them (Figure 4.4). The iPad is used to show different
videos portraying a human hand that performs certain finger movement sequences. The
robotic hand is operated by the researcher and is used to perform certain finger move-
ment sequences. The sequences are divided into three categories: easy, medium, and
hard. The easy sequence consists of approximately 3 moves, the medium sequence of
approximately 6, and the hard sequence of approximately 9. The sequences are similar
for both the test and control study. However, the order of appearance is completely ran-
dom for each participant, using a Latin Square design. The participant is presented with
5 sequences per category, with 15 sequences in total. For each study, the participant is
shown a sequence and then asked to replicate this sequence with their right hand.
4.2.1
Data collection
During the experiment, the accuracy of the participants’ replication is measured. For
example, if the participant is presented with a sequence of hard difficulty and gets 7 out of
9 movements correct, they will be awarded a score of 77.8% for that specific sequence.
All of the sequences for each study and each participant are measured and scored on
accuracy. An analysis of variance was used to confirm if there was a significance in
results between both variables since the data could be ranked on a continuous scale with
equal distance between adjacent values.

4.2.2
Objective measurement of task difficulty based on number of
operations
One noteworthy aspect of consideration is the use of the number of operations as an ob-
jective measure of task difficulty. By quantifying the cognitive load through the amount
of operations, we established a metric for evaluating the complexity of sequences un-
dertaken by participants. However, data showed that the amount of operations is not
a completely adequate measurement of task difficulty. Two medium sequences had to
be updated from medium to hard difficulty, while two hard sequences were assigned the
medium difficulty. We assume that the reason participants scored notably higher on se-
quences 11 and 13 compared to the other sequences, is because the gestures were
familiar and easier to perform given the positions of the fingers. Contrastingly, replicating
sequences 6 and 8 was challenging because participants had to hold unnatural finger
poses. A table with the average score for each sequence can be found in Appendix D.
4.3
Pilot experiment
The pilot experiment was conducted with a few friends and was quite similar to the actual
experiment setup. Initially, the choice was made to use a rubber or wooden hand to use
for the control study. However, since we wanted to accurately compare the passive stage
of the experiment versus the active stage, we decided to use the robotic hand during the
passive experiment as well. For experiment 1, it turned out that stimulating the fingers
of the participant and robotic hand at the exact same time was quite difficult, so some
time was taken by the researcher to improve and ensure that the stimuli were applied
simultaneously. Furthermore, the pilot participants reported that the position their hands
were in, was uncomfortable to sustain for longer periods. In the experiment, participants
were seated at a height-adjustable table for comfort, with their right hand resting on foam
padding. The divider was also made height-adjustable, to ensure that it was still placed
at their right shoulder. A couple of small paintbrushes were tested during the passive
stage, and eventually, the choice was made to use a square paintbrush approximately
1 centimeter in width, since this gave the most tactile feedback. The only change made
for experiment 2 was the difficulty of the sequences. Originally, we wanted to have the
hard sequence consist of 15 movements, the medium of 9, and the easy of 5. The hard
sequence was proven to be extremely difficult, with even the medium task causing dif-
ficulties. The decision was made to shave down the difficulties to 9 movements for the
hard category, 6 for the medium and 3 for the easy category.

6
Discussion
6.1
Experiment 1
The results of experiment 1 show that the robotic hand successfully elicits similar out-
comes to the original results of the rubber hand experiment by Botvinick and Cohen [15]
and that the active robotic hand creates more immersion and a sense of embodiment
compared to the passive robotic hand. Between the control and test study, participants
reported an overall higher sense of embodiment with the active hand and even significant
results were found regarding the cosmetic appearance of the robotic hand and the fact
that participants felt like they had more than one right arm or hand. Furthermore, when
comparing the data from the control study, similar results were found in the answers of the
participants collected by Botvinick and Cohen when they implemented the rubber hand
experiment, which implies that the robotic hand could be a successful tool to help stim-
ulate ownership over the hand along with the added benefit of being able to operate the
hand as opposed to only passive stimulation. Data gathered during the qualitative part of
the interview show that the illusion was very convincing for most participants:
”The active movements affected my senses much more, I felt like it was part
of my body but not my right hand, just like a third hand or something.”
”It felt ’scary’ to turn away from the table to do the survey because I felt like
the arm would follow my shoulder and fall off the table.”
and;
”At some points, it really felt like my real right hand had disappeared.”
However, participants also mentioned how certain characteristics of the robotic hand took
them out of the illusion, especially when it triggered their senses:
”I noticed when the start of the brush stroke was just a millisecond off, that
[sic] was a strange feeling, as what I saw didn’t match my feelings.”
”Some of the moments it really felt that the robotic hand was my right hand.
But some of the time still feel the touch is [sic] coming from my real right hand.
I think it could be a much stronger feeling if the robotic hand visualizes more
like a human hand.”
”The way that the fingers slammed back to the table, even though my real
fingers moved slowly, took me out of the illusion a bit.”
”After some minutes the robotic hand started feeling a little bit more like a real
hand, the only thing I considered was distracting me and avoiding ’truly’ feeling
the robotic hand, was the noise the robotic fingers made when opening, as it
made me confused by hearing a hit without feeling something.”
This highlights the fact that both mechanical and cosmetic upgrades are needed to further
enhance the connection and embodiment of the robotic hand. Integrating soft robotics
can significantly enhance the hand’s dexterity and flexibility by using pneumatics and
compliant materials, allowing for more natural and adaptive movements that mimic human
hand gestures. Soft robotics can contribute to a more lifelike and user-friendly robotic
interaction. With the incorporation of a soft body design, it becomes feasible to introduce
a latex skin layer onto the robotic hand, creating a remarkably realistic appearance akin
Preliminary Studies on using a 3D-Printed Robotic Hand for Stroke Rehabilitation
47
==PAGE:58==
to that of a human hand. This addition not only enhances the visual aspects but also
contributes to a more authentic and human-like interaction experience. However, a study
suggests that there is no discernible difference between rigid robots and soft robotics when
it comes to human-robot interaction [128], as there is a wide variation in what participants
perceive as ’normal’.
As a potential improvement to enhance the robotic hand mechanically, increasing the de-
grees of freedom and enabling partial closure of the fingers, moving beyond the current
binary state is suggested. The robotic hand has been designed to accommodate a sixth
servo motor within the wrist, dedicated solely to accurately rotating the wrist to mimic
human hand movements. This addition not only addresses issues some participants ex-
perienced, such as hand cramping during Experiment 1 but also introduces the benefit of
allowing patients to freely rotate their hands, reducing potential distractions. Additionally,
given the current binary functionality of the robotic hand, it would be beneficial to track
hand movements more precisely by measuring finger closure in percentages. This ad-
justment would enable the robotic hand to extend the fingers proportionally to the patient’s
movements, resulting in an even more accurate representation of the patient’s hand. Nev-
ertheless, the fact that the robotic hand could be triggered by a small movement also has
benefits. It highlights an advantage of the system of the robotic hand. By enabling full-
finger movements even with minimal user input, the system promotes a profound bodily
illusion, akin to the effects seen in mirror therapy. This inherent adaptability offers sub-
stantial therapeutic advantages, as it allows for individualized threshold adjustments for
each finger. For instance, if a patient can only manage slight movement in their pinky, the
system can enhance sensitivity by lowering the threshold for that specific finger. More-
over, as the patient gains more finger control, the system can easily accommodate by
increasing the threshold.
The active engagement with the robotic hand is suggested to have significantly contributed
to the amplification of the illusion of ownership and agency experienced by the partici-
pants. Within the framework of the rubber hand illusion, the active operation of the robotic
hand created a dynamic and reciprocal interaction, allowing participants to not only per-
ceive the visual and audible stimuli but also to actively manipulate and control the move-
ments of the artificial limb. This active involvement introduced a multi-sensory integration,
encompassing visual, audible, and proprioceptive cues, thereby possibly reinforcing the
participants’ sense of ownership over the robotic hand [129].
The concept of agency played an important role in shaping the participants’ subjective
experiences. By allowing participants to initiate and execute movements with the robotic
hand during the active part of Experiment 1, the experiment facilitated a sense of agency
that exceeded the limitations of traditional paradigms associated with the rubber hand
illusion (section 4.4). Research suggests that the direct correspondence between the
participants’ intentions and the actions performed by the robotic hand created an inte-
gration of the robotic hand into their body schema, leading to an enhanced perception of
ownership [130].
Furthermore, the active engagement introduced a novel dimension to the participants’
embodiment experience by integrating motor planning and execution into the illusion. As
participants executed intentional movements with the robotic hand, the sensory feedback
generated from these actions was integrated into their overall perception of the artificial
limb as an extension of their own body. It is possible that this integration not only fostered
a stronger connection between the participant’s body schema and the robotic hand but
also induced a perceptual shift, wherein the boundaries between self and non-self be-
48

==PAGE:59==
came blurred, reinforcing the illusion of ownership, as suggested by a meta-analysis of
23 experiments related to the rubber hand illusion and movement [131].
The observation that participants reported a sensation of possessing more than one right
hand or arm during the utilization of the active robotic hand introduces an intriguing aspect
to the findings of the study. The active engagement with the robotic hand appears to have
induced a perceptual expansion, wherein some participants incorporated the robotic hand
into their bodily self-representation. This sensation of possessing multiple right hands
suggests plasticity in the neural mechanisms underlying body schema, allowing for flex-
ible and dynamic integration of external robotic elements [132]. The significance of this
phenomenon lies not only in its potential to reshape our understanding of body percep-
tion but also in its practical implications for the development of prosthetic technologies and
physical interfaces in stroke rehabilitation. The ability to induce a sense of multi-limbed
embodiment has the potential to enhance the functionality and acceptance of physical
rehabilitation tools and artificial limbs, paving the way for innovative approaches in the
design of assistive devices and immersive experiences.
The tendency of participants to feel a stronger cosmetic attachment to the active robotic
hand as opposed to the passive robotic hand can be attributed to several factors. Firstly,
the active engagement with the robotic hand provides users with a heightened sense of
control and agency, fostering a more personalized and interactive experience. The abil-
ity to operate and manipulate the active robotic hand allows users to actively shape its
movements, creating a more dynamic and responsive interaction. This active involve-
ment likely contributes to a greater feeling of ownership and integration, influencing the
cosmetic attachment by making the robotic hand feel like a natural extension of one’s
own body. Additionally, the active engagement with the robotic hand introduces a novel
layer of personalization. Users may develop a sense of attachment to the active robotic
hand as they actively shape its movements to align with their preferences and intentions.
This personalization aspect, coupled with the interactive nature of the active robotic hand,
possibly contributes to a more individualized and user-centric experience, fostering a cos-
metic attachment that goes beyond the passive observation of a static limb.
The reported feeling of the real hand being ”mechanical” could be linked to cognitive dis-
sonance arising from the simultaneous operation of the robotic and real hands. The brain,
confronted with conflicting sensory inputs from the active robotic hand and the participant’s
actual hand, might attempt to reconcile this incongruity by interpreting the real hand as
having mechanical qualities. Ehrsson [133] and Makin et al. [134] explain that the illusion
emerges as a result of resolving the initial conflict between visual and somatosensory
representations of the hand. To solve this, the integration of visual, tactile, and proprio-
ceptive signals is involved, culminating in a unified multisensory perception of the robotic
hand as if it were one’s own. Exploring these cognitive processes could contribute to a
deeper understanding of the mechanisms involved in body representation and the brain’s
capacity to adapt to novel sensory experiences.
Moreover, the reported sensation of the real hand being perceived as artificial has practical
implications for the development of immersive technologies with stroke rehabilitation. It
suggests that the integration of robotic elements into the body schema can influence not
only the perception of the artificial limb but also the self-perception of the biological limb.
This has implications for the design of prosthetics and physical interfaces, emphasizing
the importance of considering the holistic impact on body perception when implementing
such technologies.

showed that the passive condition took significantly longer to elicit, which supports the
notion of the relatively rapid onset of the moving rubber hand illusion. The onset time
was not recorded with the participants of this study but could be an explanation as to why
participants felt a higher sense of embodiment with the active robotic hand.
6.2
Experiment 2
The participants’ higher accuracy in the memorization task with the robotic hand suggests
multiple factors coming together and contributing to their enhanced performance. The
physical presence of the robotic hand could have triggered the embodiment effect, cre-
ating a deeper level of cognitive engagement as participants mentally associated them-
selves with the hand, although this has not been tested and examined with the partici-
pants. The provision of feedback through the physical interface facilitated a more immer-
sive, higher engagement, and active learning experience, potentially offering additional
cues for memory retrieval. Research in the field of physics education provides evidence
supporting the idea that active participation and involvement of the body during the en-
coding process have a positive impact on learning [135]. The three-dimensional repre-
sentation of sequences by the robotic hand could have contributed to improved spatial
processing, aiding participants in creating accurate mental representations. Along with
the integration of visual and auditory elements in the robotic hand, resulting in a multi-
sensory learning experience, the robustness of information encoding could have been
enhanced. Marian et al. [136] propose that ”audio-visual associations in the environ-
ment and our previous experience jointly contribute to visual memory, strengthening vi-
sual memory through exposure to auditory input”. It very may well be that participants
scored higher on the robot memorization task since the mechanical noise of the robot
hand could have acted as an audible mnemonic device. Furthermore, the introduction of
novelty and interest associated with the robotic hand may have heightened participants’
motivation to memorize accurately. The reduced potential distractions and increased fo-
cused attention during interaction with the physical interface could also have played a role
in the observed superior performance.
Although participants were not asked to comment on their experience during experiment
2, many reported after the experiment that they felt like they performed better on the iPad
memorization task, even though the results showed otherwise. The disparity between
participants’ perceptions of their performance on the iPad task and the objective results,
which indicated superior performance with the robotic hand, can be attributed to several
cognitive and psychological factors. Firstly, participants’ confidence in their abilities might
have influenced their subjective evaluation. Individuals may have felt more familiar and
at ease with the conventional nature of iPad videos, possibly leading to an overestimation
of their performance in that condition. Secondly, there could be a cognitive bias known
as the Dunning-Kruger effect at play [137], where individuals with lower abilities tend to
overestimate their performance. If participants found the iPad task more straightforward
or conventional, they might have perceived it as easier and assumed better performance,
even if the objective measures suggested otherwise. This however is very unlikely, since it
can be assumed that the participants had similar abilities based on the fact that they have
the same level of education, as well as it can be assumed that all participants are familiar

with technology. Furthermore, the novelty associated with the robotic hand might have
contributed to a perception of increased difficulty, leading participants to underestimate
their performance. The introduction of a new and unconventional instructional medium
can create a cognitive bias where individuals perceive challenges or complexities that
may not align with their actual performance. Additionally, participants’ expectations or
prior beliefs about technology and learning mediums might have influenced their post-
experiment evaluations. If there was a preconceived notion that traditional screen-based
instructions are more effective, participants might have been inclined to believe they per-
formed better with the iPad, irrespective of the actual results. It’s also worth considering
the potential influence of social desirability bias, where participants might have wanted to
align their self-perception with what they perceived as socially desirable or expected by
the experimenters.
6.3
Limitations
The study’s findings should be interpreted in the context of several limitations. The re-
liance on a predominantly student sample may restrict the external validity of our findings,
prompting caution in extending outcomes to more heterogeneous populations, especially
stroke patients. Additionally, the binary functionality of the robotic hand, confined to either
fully open or closed positions, raises concerns about its ability to authentically replicate the
complex spectrum of natural hand movements. While self-report measures are a valuable
component of our assessment of the Body Ownership Illusion (BOI), exclusive reliance
on subjective accounts warrants consideration. Future investigations should incorporate
diverse and objective measurement techniques for a more comprehensive analysis, such
as measuring proprioceptive drift or assessing participants’ embodiment by analyzing their
physiological data during the experiment [120].
The deliberate exclusion of participants familiar with BOI concepts, although intended to
minimize bias, introduces a potential selection bias. Participants possessing prior knowl-
edge of BOI may approach the experiment with different expectations or perceptions,
influencing the outcomes in unpredictable ways. The reported instances of participant
discomfort during pilot experiments emphasize the importance of addressing potential er-
gonomic challenges. Despite efforts to mitigate discomfort through adjustments, the im-
pact of ergonomic factors on participant experiences remains a significant consideration
that could affect the validity of our results.
Moreover, the measured latency of the robotic hand at 90 ms lies below a range where
previous literature suggests possible disturbances in illusions with delays as low as 150
ms. This raises questions about the potential influence of latency on the observed results
and the generalizability of our findings, given that some participants mentioned that they
noted a small delay, although it did not take them out of the rubber hand illusion.
Some participants mentioned that they were very well aware of the appearance of the
robotic hand and its movement. One participant noted:
”The color and look of the hand took me out of the illusion, but in glimpses, I
kind of had it.”
Another participant mentioned:
”The robotic hand did not move as realistically as my own hand, like semi-
closed fingers. Instead, it closes all the way or not at all; I could identify the
breakpoint from where it would close and where it would not.”

6.4
General discussion
While VR lenses immerse patients in visually rich and adaptable environments, promoting
spatial awareness and remote accessibility, physical robotic hands mimic human move-
ment to elicit the rubber hand illusion, fostering a tangible sense of embodiment and pro-
viding useful visual and physical feedback. This section compares using VR and the
robotic hand in stroke rehabilitation.
The utilization of VR lenses in stroke rehabilitation introduces a novel aspect to stroke
rehabilitation by capitalizing on immersive 3D environments Figure 6.1. The inherent
spatial awareness and depth perception facilitated by VR lenses prove useful in honing
hand-eye coordination and navigating three-dimensional spaces. However, a study by
Höhler et al., warns that the design of augmented reality games should be adapted to the
patient’s impaired visuospatial perception, since they have a reduced ability to accurately
perceive distances and three-dimensionality in an AR environment [138].

A counterargument could be given that the setup of the camera pointing to the patient’s im-

possibilities, the rehabilitation process could be even further personalized to the patient’s
need. Incorporating hand tracking from various devices like glasses, smartwatches, or
stationary cameras could further enhance the interactive and immersive aspects of the
rehabilitation system discussed in the context of stroke rehabilitation. Integrating these
technologies can provide additional benefits and flexibility in monitoring and guiding users
during rehabilitation exercises.
The use of glasses with integrated hand tracking technology offers a hands-free and in-
tuitive interaction method. By capturing hand movements directly from the glasses, users
can engage in rehabilitation exercises without the need for additional controllers. This ap-
proach enhances the naturalness of the rehabilitation experience, allowing users to focus
on movements and tasks without the hindrance of holding specific devices. Specifically,
the patient could continue to wear these glasses throughout the day.
Smartwatches equipped with advanced sensors and hand tracking capabilities can serve
as convenient input devices for monitoring hand movements during rehabilitation. Users
can receive real-time feedback on their hand gestures and exercises through the smart-
watch interface. The portability of smartwatches allows for continuous monitoring outside
of rehabilitation sessions, enabling a more holistic approach to the rehabilitation process.
Utilizing stationary cameras in the rehabilitation environment provides a non-intrusive and
comprehensive means of capturing hand movements. Computer vision algorithms can
analyze the data from these cameras to track and interpret various hand gestures and
motions. While testing the hand tracking algorithm for this thesis, a human hand could
still be accurately detected from the other side of the room. Hypothetically, if the patient
finds the smartwatch and glasses cumbersome to wear and has difficulty setting up a cam-
era during each rehabilitation session, a solution could be offered by installing cameras in
the patient’s room. Granted, concerns regarding privacy could be raised. This approach
would allow for a more spatially aware rehabilitation experience, with the additional ben-
efit that stationary cameras can facilitate remote monitoring by healthcare professionals,
promoting consistent feedback and progress tracking.
By considering hand tracking technologies from glasses, smartwatches, and stationary
cameras, the rehabilitation system becomes not only technologically advanced but also
versatile, adaptable, and well-suited to the diverse needs of stroke survivors. This inte-
gration holds the potential to redefine stroke rehabilitation by leveraging emerging tech-
nologies for improved patient outcomes.

7
Conclusion and future work
During this thesis, a successful prototype robotic hand was built using 3D-printed parts
and appliances from a hobby shop. The hand was able to mimic the opening and closing
of a human hand with a latency of only 90ms, making it suitable to be used as a tool to
research embodiment with active versus passive stimuli as well as memory retrieval.
The results show that the robotic hand successfully elicited the rubber hand illusion by
participants, with a reported higher sense of embodiment in the active robotic hand. Ad-
ditionally, a significant difference was found between the passive and active hand when it
comes to appearance and resemblance and the fact that some participants experienced
the feeling of having an extra arm. The results from experiment 2 showed that the robotic
hand yields a significantly higher accuracy in memorization tasks compared to instructions
given from an iPad screen.
The results from this thesis show that the robotic hand could be a valuable and useful tool
for stroke patients during their rehabilitation process, adding a personalized experience
with the help of active stimulation combined with low costs.
7.1
Future work
To further improve the robotic hand a couple of improvements and possible studies are
suggested.
Enhancing Anatomical Realism with Soft Robotics To further enhance the robotic
hand’s realism and user experience, we recommended exploring the integration of soft
robotics. Soft robotics could provide a more natural look and feel, addressing concerns
raised by participants in the study:
”The way that the fingers slammed back to the table, even though my real
fingers moved slowly, took me out of the illusion a bit.”
,
”After some minutes the robotic hand started feeling a little bit more like a real
hand, the only thing I considered was distracting me and avoiding ’truly’ feeling
the robotic hand, was the noise the robotic fingers made when opening, as it
made me confused by hearing a hit without feeling something.”
and
”It would be nice if it looked more like a real hand”
. As some participants mentioned, the abrupt movements of the robotic fingers distracted
them during the experiment, potentially hindering them from experiencing the illusion.
Soft robotics can enable smoother and more lifelike finger movements, simulating the
gradual and precise motions of human fingers. This improvement could greatly enhance
the immersion and effectiveness of the rehabilitation process.
Implementing Machine Learning and AI Algorithms
Incorporating machine learning and artificial intelligence (AI) algorithms into the robotic
hand’s control system has the potential to greatly improve the robotic hand’s performance.
These technologies can enable the robotic hand to adapt and personalize its assistance

based on individual patient needs and progress by adjusting the range of motion of the
fingers and the detection criteria of the patient’s hand. Furthermore, machine learning
algorithms can analyze patient data, including movement patterns and rehabilitation out-
comes, to dynamically tailor the rehabilitation program. Exploring machine learning and
AI in this context could lead to more precise and personalized experiences for a stroke
patient during their rehabilitation.
Long-Term Impact Studies
While the initial study provided valuable insights into the use of the robotic hand during
a brief experimental setup, conducting long-term impact studies is essential. Long-term
assessments can help evaluate the sustainability of the improvements achieved through
robotic-assisted rehabilitation. Researchers can track patients’ progress over extended
periods to assess the durability of motor recovery and functional improvements. Long-
term studies can also investigate whether the benefits observed during the rehabilitation
period persist and lead to lasting improvements in patients’ daily lives. Understanding the
long-term impact is necessary to determine the full potential of the robotic hand in stroke
rehabilitation.
Customization and 3D Printing
Customization plays a vital role in optimizing the robotic hand’s utility for individual pa-
tients. Leveraging 3D printing technology and scanning techniques, it is possible to cre-
ate highly customized robotic hands tailored to each patient’s specific anatomical needs.
3D scanning can generate precise maps of a patient’s hand, enabling the design and
fabrication of personalized components. This level of customization could enhance the
connection between the patient and the robotic hand while also ensuring that the transi-
tion from looking at the patient’s own hand to the robotic hand is as seamless as possible.
Future research can explore the integration of 3D printing and 3D scans to further improve
the user experience with the robotic hand.
Gamification and VR Integration
To make the rehabilitation process more engaging and motivating, integrating gamifica-
tion and VR elements into the robotic hand’s interface should be considered. Gamification
can transform therapy sessions into interactive and enjoyable experiences, encouraging
patients to participate actively. VR environments can immerse patients in virtual scenar-
ios that mimic real-life activities, making rehabilitation more engaging and relevant. By
incorporating gamification and VR, researchers can enhance patient adherence and mo-
tivation, potentially leading to better outcomes. Future studies can explore the design and
implementation of gamified and VR-integrated rehabilitation programs using the robotic
hand.
Clinical Trials and Collaboration with Experts
To advance the research and development of the robotic hand, collaboration with ther-
apists and medical experts is extremely important. Conducting clinical trials with stroke
patients in real-world rehabilitation settings is the next logical step. These trials can pro-
vide valuable data on the effectiveness, safety, and user experience of the robotic hand
in diverse patient populations. Furthermore, involving therapists and medical experts can
lead to a deeper understanding of the clinical implications and practical challenges associ-
ated with robotic-assisted rehabilitation. Collaborative efforts can facilitate the refinement
of the robotic hand’s design and functionality based on expert insights and patient feed-
back.
