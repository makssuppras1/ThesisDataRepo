Resumé 
 
Der er i de seneste årtier generelt kommet mere fokus på indeklima. Både indeklima på skoler, men 
også i virksomheder, og det er også blevet generel viden, at indeklimaet kan påvirke de ansatte, 
både psykisk og fysisk. Men er det også økonomisk fordelagtigt for en virksomhed at investere i at 
forbedre indeklimaet? Det er det, der er blevet undersøgt i denne opgave.  
Metoden til at beregne, hvorvidt en investering i forbedring af indeklimaet er rentabel for virksom-
heden, har været at skabe en sammenhæng mellem, hvordan forbedring i indeklima forbedrer pro-
duktiviteten målt i effektive arbejdstimer og dermed virksomhedens økonomi. Dette er gjort ud fra 
tidligere studier, både danske og udenlandske, der beskriver sammenhængen mellem forskellige 
indeklimaparametre og produktivitet. Ved at sammenholde den økonomiske profit med udgifterne 
til renovering og forbedring af indeklimaet fås en tilbagebetalingstid, som gør det muligt at vurdere, 
hvornår en given renovering er rentable for virksomheden. Ud fra tidligere studier er sammenhæn-
gen mellem produktivitet og følgende indeklima parametre blevet undersøgt: CO2-koncentrationen, 
inde temperatur samt forholdet mellem vinduesareal og gulvareal. Derudover er der lavet 9 forskel-
lige renoveringsforslag og beregnet udgifterne dertil, som alle forventes at kunne forbedre indekli-
maet på et eller flere af de førnævnte parametre. Alle renoveringer og forbedringer er lavet med 
udgangspunkt i en standardbygning fra 1980’erne, hvilket også gør resultaterne mest brugbare for 
andre virksomheder siddende i en bygning fra samme periode. 
Resultaterne viser, at det er mest rentabelt for virksomhederne at øge vinduesarealet. Derudover 
viser resultaterne også, at tilbagebetalingstiden generelt er lavest, når man kigger på gevinsten i 
produktivitet ift. forbedret inde temperatur sammenlignet med gevinsten ift. CO2-indhold. Samt at 
det er mest rentabelt at gå efter en renovering, der stræber mod at opnå et indeklima i klasse A 
eller B, hvor tilbagebetalingstiden ligger på 2-6 år, hvorimod en stræben mod klasse C og D har for 
høje udgifter ift. den mulige gevinst, og derfor har tilbagebetalingstider på 5-40 år. 
Metoden blev testet på virksomheden Christoffersen & Knudsen A/S’s kontorbygning. Måleresulta-
terne af indeklimaet blev i metoden brugt til at undersøge hvilke renoveringsforslag, der ville være 
mest fordelagtigt for Christoffersen & Knudsen A/S at investere i. Igen viste resultaterne, at tilbage-

Introduction 
The indoor climate is well-known to have an impact on human wellbeing, concentration and health. 
Most companies also know that a good indoor environment improves the working environment for 
their employees. However, for many companies it is difficult to establish the economic benefits for 
the company. Renovation and optimization of the indoor environment can be a costly affair and for 
most companies it is more likely that an investment improving indoor environment is implemented 
if it results in an economic benefit.  
The purpose of this project is to investigate whether it is financially worthwhile for a company to 
invest in improved indoor climate in order to increase the performance of its employees. 
The improvements of indoor environment are focused on four parameters: CO2-concentration, tem-
perature, ventilation rate and daylight. All these parameters are generally known to affect the well-
being and productivity at workplaces. The improvements regarding indoor climate are prepared us-
ing the indoor climate simulation software IDA ICE. With IDA ICE the existing indoor climate and the 
improvements are assessed which makes it possible to predict the gains in productivity and perfor-
mance. The economical side of the project is prepared by using Molio’s price data books program 
Sigma, which is used to calculate the construction costs of an indoor climate improvement. This is 
used to compare the improved indoor climate and performance with the renovation costs, in order 
to evaluate if it is profitable to invest in improved indoor climate or not.

1 Theory/background 
Indoor climate, in general, refers to two branches, the thermal indoor climate, which can be trans-
lated to the thermal comfort of the body, and the atmospheric indoor climate also known as air 
quality. Thermal comfort is defined as the state of mind that expresses satisfaction with the ther-
mal environment. Comfort is a mental perception which is affected by the heat balance which 
again is affected by all the thermal comfort parameters. The air quality includes all the remaining 
parameters that affects the human body, such as CO2 and volatile organic compounds (VOCs). To-
day, it is also common to include other parameters to the indoor climate evaluation. Particularly, 
the two parameters noise and light both have a significant impact on how we perceive the indoor 
climate. [1] 
Even though the outdoor climate gets a lot of attention, it is only approx. 10% of our life we spend 
outside at the northern latitudes. Accordingly, 90% of the life is spend inside [3]. This supports the 
importance of a great indoor climate and explains why the indoor climate has a huge influence on 
people’s health and wellbeing throughout life. 
It is well-known that people perceive indoor climate differently. That is why discomfort such as 
draught, noise, overheating, cold and odour are subjective perceptions. Thus, the indoor climate is 
a very complex subject, the building standards etc. is made to accommodate the majority of the 
population, well aware that some percentages will be dissatisfied.  
In relation to performance and sick-leave for occupants in office buildings, this project focuses 
mainly on the thermal- and atmospheric indoor climate and daylight conditions.  
1.1 Earlier studies in the field  
In recent years, more focus has targeted the effects of bad indoor environment. This has led the 
wish to determine the consequences of bad indoor environment on socio-economic aspects. The 
interest in this topic has led to several published studies around the world, and they all have the 
same in common, namely that they show a large potential for the socio-economy by investing in 
improved indoor environment in offices [2] [3].

A guidebook based on a collection of different studies was published by REHVA (Federation of Eu-
ropean Heating, Ventilation and Air Conditioning Associations) in 2006 and it is one of the first guide-
books made with focus on the link between indoor environment and socio-economic consequences 
[2]. The guidebook reviews the available information to find out whether there is solid scientific 
evidence that the indoor environmental quality affects office work. Furthermore, the guidebook 
illustrates how to quantify the effects of indoor environment on office work, and how to include 
these effects in the calculation of building costs. The main results of the study are listed below: 
• Doubling the outdoor air supply rate can reduce illness and short-term sick leave prevalence 
by approx. 10% and increase the performance of office work by roughly 1,5%. 
• Every 10% reduction in the percentage of workers dissatisfied with air quality can increase 
the performance of office work by roughly 1%. 
• Reduction in temperatures above 24°C by 1°C can increase the performance of office work 
by roughly 1.5% and increase of too low temperatures up to 20°C can increase the perfor-
mance of office work by 2%. 
• Increasing the ventilation rate from 12 to 24 l/s/person are estimated to save USD22.8 bil-
lion per year nationally due to decreased short-term sick leave.  
Another study made by US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) [4] estimates that tens of billions 
of dollars are lost every year due to bad indoor environment in offices impacting the health of em-
ployees. A study conducted together with Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health [3] shows a 
direct connection between air quality in office buildings and employee productivity, increased cases 
of absenteeism, higher operational costs, and chronic health issues like asthma. Furthermore, the 
study found a cognitive score improving by up to 101% in “green building conditions” (GBC), i.e., 
conditions where ventilation rate is increased and VOC and CO2 levels are lowered. Finally, the study 
found that office buildings with healthy air quality have up to 35% fewer absences. 
1.2 Thermal comfort 
Thermal indoor climate is acceptable if the body does not experience thermal discomfort caused by 
the general heat balance or local discomfort such as draught at high air speeds with either high or 
low room temperatures. The body must be in thermal balance, which means that the body is neither 
feeling too hot nor too cold. Thermal comfort includes the parameters that affect the heat balance

humidity [1]. 
1.2.1 Activity and clothing 
The human body converts energy all the time. The basic metabolism of humans is different as well 
as the general energy conversion. The energy conversion is measured in W/m2, at a given activity 
level, met. This can be seen in table 1. When the body converts energy the temperature of the body 
rises, and the person feel warmer.  
 
  
 
 
 
 
 
 
Besides the activity level, also the clothing insulation influences how a person perceives the thermal 
environment. The more clothes a person is wearing the more he or she is protected from cold air 
and high air velocities. Furthermore, if a person is wearing multiple layers of clothing, he or she will 
feel warmer. In figure 1 the insulation of clothing (clo) can be seen for different kinds of outfits. The 
clothing level has for instance been included when determining which indoor air temperatures are 
accepted in winter- and summertime. The air temperature is accepted to be a bit higher in the sum-
mertime because it is expected that people wear less clothes and therefore the level of clo is lower, 
and the other way around in the wintertime [1].

1.2.2 Air temperature 
The air temperature is the measured air temperature in the living area close to the person, but not 
the heated air which is very close to the skin. Depending on whether the general position for the 
persons in the room is sitting or standing, the temperature should be measured either in 0,1 m, 
0,6/1,1 m and/or 1,7 m above the floor [16].  
The skin temperature is the parameter that is easiest for people to evaluate and very often what 
people mean when they find the indoor climate discomforting, although it is a combination of all 
the thermal comfort parameters that affects the skin temperature. It is difficult to accommodate 
everyone’s needs when it comes to indoor air temperature.  As mentioned, how people perceive 
the air temperature is very different from person to person. The amount of clothes, Clo level, and 
the activity level, met, can be different and the overall psychology of the person can vary [1].  
1.2.3 Mean radiation temperature 
The mean radiant temperature is defined as the uniform temperature of the surfaces of an enclo-
sure that results in the same radiant heat transfer between occupant and surfaces as in the actual, 
non-uniform surroundings. What people experience and feel relating to thermal comfort in a build-
ing is related to the influence of both the air temperature and the temperature of surfaces in that 
space, represented by the mean radiant temperature [1].  
1.2.4 Air velocity and asymmetric radiation

skin due to high air velocities. High air velocities often appears when the air change is too high in

experienced if the person in the room is exposed to asymmetric radiation. For instance, if the outer 
wall is not insulated and therefore might be very cold. This gives a very low temperature close to 
the wall compared to the rest of the room which can cause discomfort for the person sitting close 
to the wall [1].  
1.2.5 Humidity 
Humidity may at high levels cause moisture nuisances such as the growth of mould and mildew 
while users will experience that it is difficult to get rid of body heat, while at low relative humidity 
individuals will experience irritation due to dry mucous membranes. A mechanical ventilation sys-
tem can help controlling and regulating the humidity of a room and achieve an acceptable indoor 
climate [1]. 
1.2.6 PMV/PDD 
Often, not everyone in a room finds the thermal indoor climate satisfying as people's sense of tem-
perature and draught vary as do the personal influences, such as clothing and activity level. The 
thermal indoor climate therefore strives to satisfy as many people as possible. The Danish professor 
P. Ole Fanger developed a method for assessing the thermal indoor climate and comfort called the 
PMV / PPD model. PMV and PPD mean respectively ‘Predicted Mean Vote’ and ‘Predicted Percent-
age of Dissatisfied’. PMV is an index which predicts the average voting value of a group of people 
listed in a 7-step scale. -3 corresponds to very cold and +3 corresponds to very hot. 0 corresponds 
to the heat balance thermal neutrality. The PPD index predicts the expected proportion of people 
who, according to the PMV index, will be dissatisfied by either feeling the thermal indoor climate as 
too cold or too hot based on the thermal comfort parameters. The relation between PPD as a func-
tion of PMV is illustrated in figure 2 [1].

1.3 Air quality 
Indoor air quality includes all the non-thermal aspects of the indoor air which affects and influences 
humans’ comfort and health. Firstly, the air affects the body through respiration, where the purpose 
is to get the essential oxygen for the metabolism and to abduct the produced carbon dioxide (CO2). 
When inhaling the lungs surface, which is around 100 m2, get exposed to all the components of the 
air, and therefore it makes sense that the air quality has a huge influence on the health and comfort 
for humans [1].

1.3.1 CO2 and bio influencer  
People asses the indoor air quality with two senses, smell and vision, meaning that the air quality 
affects the mucous membranes in nose and eyes. If a room has a bad smell or the air feels enclosed 
it might be because of high bio effluences, which means that people will qualify the indoor air quality 
as bad. At the same time, if our eyes hurt because of for example smoke, we will also perceive the 
indoor air quality as bad. 
Often people find that the indoor air quality is bad because of low oxygen-level in the room. How-
ever, the human body is not very sensitive to oxygen concentrations within normal levels and the 
indoor CO2-concentration is much more interesting to investigate. Previously, humans were pre-
sumed to be the main source to indoor air pollution, today we know that in modern buildings there 
are other relevant pollution sources, too [1].  
1.3.2 VOC 
Most materials in buildings release gasses, and they are called VOCs (volatile organic compounds). 
Some materials release gasses in the beginning and then decrease over time, while others release 
throughout their entire lifetime. Some VOCs can be smelled but most of them are not noticeable 
for humans. VOCs are associated with the symptoms such as dry throat and nose, itchy and irritated 
eyes, headache, and fatigue which many people experience in both new and existing buildings. This 
is another reason for implementing mechanical ventilation to remove high volumes of VOCs from 
the room and improve the indoor air quality. Nowadays, there is also much more focus on the use 
of materials with low gas exhaust. The most known VOC is formaldehyde, which has a characteristic 
smell and gives irritation in eyes, throat, and nose. It is used a lot in plywood and chipboards and 
authorities in many countries have therefor set restrictions for the content of formaldehyde.  
Thousands of different VOCs exist and it is almost impossible to distinguish them from each other. 
Most test equipment can only measure the total amount, but not the individual compounds [6]. 
1.3.3 Visual indoor climate 
The visual indoor climate includes lighting level, light quality, amount of daylight, visibility, glare, 
and electric lighting. Daylight access and vision have an influence on the individual's circadian 
rhythm and mental condition. Studies show that the ability to achieve satisfying daylight conditions 
and access to unobstructed views is one of the most valued elements in a great indoor climate.

When designing offices, working stations are most often placed by a window to make the best pos-
sible use of daylight. Orientation, geometric shape, and size of the window have an influence on 
daylight, the need for cooling, and the need for heat and energy. Although, a large window section 
provides a greater incidence of daylight, it will in many cases result in a greater heat demand due 
to a large heat loss in wintertime. In the summertime, however, large window sections will result in 
higher temperatures which means increased cooling needs. In order to reduce the cooling needs in 
the summertime, direct and diffuse sunlight can be shielded by implementing interior or exterior 
sun protection. Direct radiation is the light that comes directly from the sun in sunny weather. Dif-
fuse radiation is the light in cloudy weather. This light passes through the clouds and are reflected 
to the surroundings. Diffuse radiation is preferred in office spaces, as the light is cooler and more 
natural than direct radiation, and it decreases the risk of glare [8].  
The Building Regulations have specified two methods to document that the daylight conditions in 
living and working areas are satisfying. The two options are called the 10% rule and the 300-lux rule. 
When applying the 10% rule, it is documented that the window glass area without shading condi-
tions corresponds to at least 10% of the relevant floor area. The given window glass area must be 
corrected for reduced light transmittance and shading from the surroundings. However, this does 
not always mean that a sufficient daylight condition is achieved in all zones. 
When applying the 300-lux rule, it must be demonstrated that the intensity of the interior lighting 
from daylight is at least 300 lux in half of the relevant floor area for at least half of the daylight hours 
[7]. The 300 lux correspond to an overcast sky with a daylight factor of 2.1%. For walk areas and 
staircases the lightning requirement is 100 lux corresponding to a daylight factor of 0.7% according 
to DS/EN 17037 [8]. For workspaces, the relevant floor area is defined as the part of the room where 
the working surface is located. The study of the illuminance of daylight must be carried out for the 
critical space using simulation tools that uses hourly values for weather data from DRY 2001-2010, 
as it must be simulated for each hour during a year. Meeting rooms do not have daylight require-
ments.

There is a lot of literature on the topic. In general, it is the applicable Building Regulations which 
must be complied to. 
2.1 The Building Regulation 
At the time of this project, it is the Building Regulations 2018 (also mentioned as ‘BR18’ [9]) which 
sets the framework for all types of building constructions. The building regulations specify the re-
quirements of the building act and aim to ensure that construction is carried out health-, safety- 
and energy satisfactory. Out of BR18’s 22 sections dealing with technical regulations, four are di-
rectly related to indoor climate and ventilation - and certain others shed light on indoor climate-
related issues, such as moisture and pollution. Section 17 and 18 deal with ‘Sound conditions’ and 
‘light and vision’, respectively, including reverberation time and daylight. Section 19 is about the 
thermal indoor climate, including installations for heating and cooling and the energy consumption 
for these. Section 22 ‘Ventilation’ deals with the requirements for ensuring satisfactory air quality 
and humidity conditions in relation to application, including energy, control as well as operation and 
maintenance. Each section contains a number of paragraphs, which describes topics within the 
overall area. E.g., it says in §447 ‘Ventilation in other buildings and rooms’: 
“The size of the ventilation can e.g., be determined on the basis of category II in Annex C in DS 447 Ventilation in buildings 
- Mechanical, natural and hybrid ventilation systems, category II in Annex B in DS / EN 16798 or by ensuring that the 
CO2 level at the dimensioning conditions is below 1000 ppm, where persons are the major source of pollution. It must 
always be ensured that the ventilation is sufficient to ventilate away other contaminants.”  
(Bygningsreglementet, 2018) 
The Building Regulations are updated regularly in updated versions where e.g., a tighter energy 
frame is implemented. An important thing to emphasize is that a building just has to comply with 
the Building Regulations at the time of the construction. It means, that buildings built, where older 
Building Regulations were applicable, will probably not comply with todays desired indoor climate 
standards. In addition to the Building Regulation, there are a lot of standards and instructions which 
are updated together with the Building Regulation to ensure that they do not contradict the require-
ments listed in the Building Regulation.

3 Methodology 
The purpose of this project is to investigate the connection between indoor climate, performance, 
and economy in office buildings. To do that a connection between them all is needed. The connec-
tion between indoor climate parameters and performance is taken from earlier studies and is pre-
sented first. These studies present a given performance for a given indoor climate parameter value 
during a total amount of effective working hours. A given indoor climate parameter value means a 
given performance in percentage and thereby also an amount of loss in effective working hours. 
After presenting the relation between indoor climate parameters and loss in effective working 
hours, relevant renovation proposals for improving the indoor climate will be presented. Based on 
these proposals IDA ICE [10] simulations are made, which output gives expected indoor climate val-
ues for the different renovation proposals. The costs for these renovation proposals are then calcu-
lated with Sigma, Molio’s price data books [11], and the total cost is assumed for each of them. The 
changed output values from IDA ICE for the indoor climate makes it possible to predict a change in 
productivity for all the given renovation proposals. Held together with the construction cost it is 
possible to predict a profit for every renovation proposal and thereby also the payback time. 
3.1 Indoor climate parameters and performance

CO2, temperature, ventilation rate and daylight, influence the employee performance while the in-

the indoor climate and occupant well-being. Many of the other parameters has not been investi-

3.3 IDA ICE – Reference model 
The method to determine the connection between indoor environment and the renovation cost is 
based on a reference model in the energy- and indoor climate simulation program IDA ICE. The 
program is a simulation tool, developed to be used in the design phase of building design- and en-
ergy performance. The version used is IDA ICE 4.8 [10] and the simulation period used in the project 
is from 1st of January to the 31st of December. The purpose of the indoor climate simulations in IDA 
ICE is to determine the improved indoor climate and performance for each renovation proposal and 
compare it to the cost of each specific renovation. 
In the following section, relevant inputs to the building modelling will be described. The section will 
deal with material properties, window properties, internal heating supplements, building systems 
and other important parameters so that a realistic image of the building's indoor climate as well as 
energy consumption can be processed and analysed. The simulations made will be based on a 
weather file regarding the reference year DRY 2013 Copenhagen/Kastrup [13], which is loaded into 
IDA ICE. Furthermore, the reference model is based on information about the building regulation 
from 1982 [14]. Figure 10 illustrates the building geometry used in the project.

3.3.3 Internal heat gains 
The internal heat gains correspond to that heat inside the building that comes from people, lighting, 
and equipment. The office is assumed to only be in use on weekdays during the working hours be-

for the building. However, the working hours per employee is set to 1672 hours a year, where holi-
days are extracted [15]. This number of working hours is also used later when determine how many 
working hours that are effective, when different renovation proposals are investigated. 
The reference model is 100 m2. By adding 10 employees to the model means that each employee 
has 10 m2 of working space, which meets modern recommendations. The employees are assumed 
to be sitting at a desk and by having an activity level equal to 1,2 met as well as a clothing insulation 
of 0,8 ± 0,25 clo. 0,8 clo equals to a person in long pants, long-sleeved shirt, and thin sweater while 
the variation of ± 0,25 clo, is due to the fact that clothing is dependent on the season as well as the 
individual comfort [16].

3.5 Renovation proposals – Economy (Sigma) 
The overall purpose of the renovation proposals is to determine a cost for a given renovation. To 
investigate whether a renovation is profitable it is necessary to know the cost of the given renova-
tion. The renovation proposals which include ventilation has been simulated in IDA ICE to find the 
==PAGE:54==

ventilation rate and thereby the size and subsequently the cost of the ventilation system. Further-
more, the IDA ICE simulation has also given the amount of hours in the given categories, which is 
needed later to determine the profit in performance improvement.  
To calculate the cost of the given renovation proposals Sigma is used. Sigma is a software which 
calculates construction prices [11]. The software presents all the building professionals which each 
contain a lot of different renovation and renewal parts. To get the cost as accurate as possible it is 
necessary to find the specific renovation part or new construction part for all the steps of the reno-
vation and then put in the amounts.  All the renovation and renewals contain all the needed parts 
of the specific construction including the wages.  
3.5.1 Ventilation system and components  
A ventilation system primarily consists of ducts. But all additional components have to be included 
to find the total price. We know that the bigger the ventilation rate, the bigger the ventilation sys-
tem, and thereby more components.  To calculate the number of the different components for a 
given ventilation rate, an association related to the air flow has been made for all the components.  
The highest air flow rate found in IDA ICE, 900 l/s (Renovation proposal 3 Cat A), has been used as 
setpoint in order to make the association. In this way a linear equation can be found to determine 
all numbers for all fixtures for different air flows. 
It has been assumed that renovation proposal 3 with the highest air flow of 900 l/s, contains the 
largest number of components and that the rest of the renovation proposals will require less com-
ponents. Renovation proposal 3 is assumed to need 6 fire dampers and 100 meters of ducts. Besides 
that, it is assumed to need 8 supply- and exhaust air terminals which is found by using a circular 
supply air terminal with a throw length of 2 meters and thereby each air terminal is approx. supply-
ing 12,5 m2 each, by calculating the area of a circle. It is assumed that the number of silencers, VAV 
– dampers and T-pieces are the same as air terminals. To make it possible to calculate the amount 
of ducts and components for all other air flows (renovation proposals) associations has been made 
for all components. However, to make it realistic the number of air terminals, fire dampers, silenc-
ers, VAV-dampers, and T-pieces, can never be lower than 2 no matter the air flow. All the associa-
tions can be seen on table 41, and the calculated amounts has been used as input for the Sigma 
calculations.

3.6 Profitability  
In the previous chapters the relationship between indoor climate parameters and performance was 
presented. Then the different renovation proposals were defined and simulated in IDA ICE. The sim-
ulation then gave a distribution of hours in different categories, either CO2-concentration or tem-
perature intervals. This distribution now makes it possible to determine how much better the indoor 
environment has become and thereby also how much the performance has improved. An example 
of how the calculations have been conducted will now be showed for renovation proposal 3. 
Table 43 shows the results for renovation proposal 3. The number of hours in the different temper-
ature classifications for renovations directed for either category A, B, C or D can be seen in the first 
row. All renovations are based on improvements from the reference model.  
The second row is the amount of time in the given classification given in percentage. Third row is 
the temperature that is used related to the performance. This leads to the 4th row, which is the 
performance related to the temperature in the given classification.  
5th row is the sum of loss in effective working hours and the following formula has been used.

In the 6th row the connection between the performance and the economy is made. It is assumed

and also payback times.  
The loss in effective working hours is multiplied with 215 DKK/hour, and thereby the loss in eco-

tion the renovation aims for the better the indoor climate gets and thereby the loss in effective 
working hours declines.

4 Study Case  
The indoor climate of an existing building has been analyzed in addition to the reference model 
made in IDA ICE. Many assumptions have been made during the simulations, why it is interesting to 
showcase the method on an existing building and see if it gives comparable results. Two indoor 
climate measurements have been conducted, one in September and one in December, in order to 
review the indoor climate for both summer- and winter period. A questionnaire has been completed 
by the employees, in order to understand how the employees, experience the indoor environment. 
Thus, to investigate how to improve the performance of the employees and finally to discuss if it is 
profitable for the company to invest in improved indoor climate.    
4.1 Introduction to Christoffersen & Knudsen A/S 
An office building in Rødovre, Denmark, has been selected in this project. The office building is lo-
cated in an industrial district and is occupied by Christoffersen & Knudsen A/S. It is a construction 
company that deals with water and heating services. The company has in total 114 employees, and 
the office building accommodates 20 administrative staff. The office building was erected in 1958, 
however, major renovations on the building envelope were conducted in 1984, why the Building 
Regulation 1982 will be considered. In addition, modern windows were installed in 2011. Infor-
mation about the building’s construction structure, window type, service system etc. were discov-
ered at the inspection of the building together with the CEO of the Christoffersen & Knudsen A/S. In 
case of missing information, some reasonable assumptions were made based on BR82 and BR18

5 Discussion  
The purpose of this project was to investigate the connection between indoor climate, performance 
and the economy in office buildings, and to establish if some renovations are more profitable than 
others. The payback time is used to distinguish how attractive the individual renovation proposals 
are. The results show that the highest gain in profit from better performance is obtained by improv-
ing indoor air temperature and increasing the W/F ratio.  
To assess how valid the results are, it is necessary to conduct a sensitivity test. This is done to test if 
the results are realistic but also how sensitive the method is. 
5.1 Sensitivity analysis  
The calculated payback time and profit gives an overview on how attractive it is to renovate a build-
ing from the 1980’ies into a building with a better indoor climate. The payback time makes it possi-
ble to choose the most profitable renovation proposal. However, the process has indicated potential 
errors, e.g. by using a trend line based on already sorted data and choice of reference studies on 
indoor climate and performance. Applying the method on the study case building also shows that 
some parameters change the payback time significantly. To test what parts of the method that is 
unstable and affects the results significantly, a few sensitivity tests have been made.  
First the employee price has been tested. The employee value for Christoffersen & Knudsens A/S is

5.2 Pros, cons and errors 
Overall, the results shows that there is a connection between the performance and the four indoor 
climate parameters (temperature, CO2-concentration, ventilation rate and window/floor ratio) and 
that the indoor climate affects the economy of companies. It is seen that the better indoor climate 
the better the performance. However, to obtain improvements renovations are needed and that 
has a financial cost. It is only when improved indoor climate and increase in performance combined

with the costs that it is possible to evaluate the profitability. Applicable for all results are that their 
profit and payback time vary with the kind of indoor climate parameters investigated. Generally, 
the payback time is lower for the performance affected by the temperature, but also window/floor 
ratio increase has a very high performance improvement and thereby a very low payback time.  
It is important to be aware that an improvement of one parameter can negatively affect another 
parameter. For instance, when increasing the window/floor ratio the amount of daylight and view 
increases and the performance improves, but at the same time the indoor temperatures will in-
crease affecting the performance negatively (see Table 49). Contrary to the impact of increased W/F 
ratio ventilation implementation affects the indoor temperature and the Co2 concentration and 
subsequently the payback time positively. This emphasizes the importance of ventilation and that 
it is not possible to look at one parameter alone if a pleasant indoor climate is wanted.

6 Conclusion 
The overall investigation of the correlation between the indoor climate, performance and econ-
omy in office buildings has made it possible to get an estimate of expected profits and payback 
times.  
The results showed that it is necessary to consider more than one indoor climate parameter if a 
good indoor climate is wanted. However, the most rewarding renovation is the implementation of 
ventilation. Ventilation has a positive effect on indoor air temperature and is also the only param-
eter which can lower the CO2 concentration. Also, W/F ratio increase has a very positive effect on 
performance but has to be implemented in combination with e.g. ventilation, to keep the indoor 
air temperatures down. The renovations aiming for improving the indoor temperature is the most 
profitable, in general, when considering all parameters.  
The payback time is generally lowest when looking at the gain in productivity in terms of improved 
indoor temperature compared to the gain in terms of CO2 content. It is most profitable to implement 
a renovation that targets an indoor climate classification A or B, where the payback period is 2-6 
years, whereas renovating for classification C and D has too high costs compared to the possible 
gain, and therefore has payback periods of 5-40 years. 
It can be concluded that an optimisation of the indoor climate will be profitable for many compa-
nies but the exact profit and thereby also exact payback time will vary between companies and be 
difficult to predict. Hopefully, the fact that there is a profit to gain, small or large, will convince 
more companies to invest in better indoor climate, both for the companies’ financials and espe-
cially for the well-being of the employees.
