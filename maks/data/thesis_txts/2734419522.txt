Abstract
Malicious actors poses an increasing threat to both individuals and companies. Despite
an increasing rise in the use of containerized technology not many lightweight solutions
monitoring distributed systems running as containers are offered. This thesis examines
to what extent it is possible to detect anomalies in applications running as containers
through application of artificial intelligence, using the models Random Forest, OC-SVM
and LSTM-Autoencoder. To examine this, an end-to-end intrusion detection system built
as a distributed system, integrated directly into other distributed systems to detect anoma-
lies in network traﬀic, has been implemented. The system has been used to gather data
used for tests, investigating how it is possible through the use of artificial intelligence to
detect abnormal behavior in network traﬀic in containerized systems. The experiments
has shown results concluding that it is possible to create a lightweight system that can be
integrated into a distributed system and gather network traﬀic to detect anomalies with
the use of artificial intelligence. Furthermore, it has highlighted challenges that must be
addressed when bridging the gap between tests of artificial intelligence based models in
controlled environments and using them in a real-time running system.

Introduction
In recent years the evolution of digital systems as well as the increasing amount of digital
devices connected to the internet has caused a rise in malicious actors and cyber crime.
According to AAG [1], and Astra [2], two companies specializing in IT security tools, ran-
somware is a growing threat recording around 236.1 million ransomware attacks occurred
globally in the first half of 2022[1], while the cost of cybercrimes is projected to reach $10.5
trillion by 2025[2]. Meanwhile, the count of cyber crime has risen from 0.17 billion in 2001
to 6.9 billion in 2021, and will predicted to be at approximately 10.26 billion in 2031[2].
AV-Test, the independent IT-security institute, also showed that the total amount of mal-
ware has also only seen a rise throughout the years[3].
In early years the monitoring of malicious behavior in systems was typically done by
manually monitoring events and logs. However, due to the increase in devices and the
amount of data running systems, manually monitoring such systems would be an almost
impossible task. The consequences of this was the development of automatic monitoring
systems, also referred to as intrusion detection systems (IDS).
Over the years, significant research and development efforts have been invested in the
field of intrusion detection systems to enhance their performance. Broadly speaking, the
key priorities for an eﬀicient intrusion detection system is to maintain a high level of
detection accuracy while minimizing the occurrences of false positives. Meanwhile, the
continuous development of artificial intelligence has caused an interest in researching its
capabilities for intrusion detection aiming at creating sophisticated and high-performing
systems.
However, a notable issue arises as much of this research is isolated and focused on spe-
cific datasets. Many artificial intelligence driven models exhibit impressive results when
trained and tested on well-known datasets, yet there is only little talk about any practical
diﬀiculties when these are implemented in real-world operational systems, where the data
may not be known in advance.
Addressing the gap between research and practical implementation is crucial for advancing
the field of intrusion detection, and there is a need to develop artificial intelligence driven
intrusion detection solutions that not only show promising results in controlled settings
but also demonstrate practicality, adaptability, and eﬀiciency when deployed in dynamic,
real-world environments.
Meanwhile, when examining system infrastructures of companies, there has been a rise in
popularity of using container technology when developing distributed systems[4].
Enterprise solutions such as Snyk[5], a tool to scan images for vulnerabilities, and Dy-
natrace[6], a platform providing cloud monitoring, mainly rely on static analyses such as
image and code analysis. However, there exists a very little to none range of intrusion
detection systems relying on container introspection that are easily implemented and de-
ployed alongside distributed systems.
According to Infosecurity Magazine, zero-days, hence vulnerabilities not known to software
vendors, has also been on a rise[7]. This reveals challenges in the rise of new patterns of
malicious behavior. To address these challenges, an exploration into anomaly-based detec-

by detecting deviations from predefined baselines, enhancing the ability of the system to

1.1
Project Scope
As cyber security is a broad field involving a variety of different perspectives, threats can
manifest in several ways. The author has chosen to focus solely on detecting anomalies
in network traﬀic. However, ideally the system is able to handle further expansion and
thereby reach beyond of solely detecting anomalous behavior in network traﬀic, but also
detect anomalies by analyzing for example files and processes running in the containers.
Throughout the thesis, artificial intelligence based models have been implemented together
with different preprocessing methods, such as encoding and feature scaling, to create a sys-
tematic comparison of the models. The models chosen for tests have been chosen based on
research and the workings of each model. These models have been implemented, trained
and tested with known public datasets, as well as datasets created within this thesis.
The models have been tested in an controlled environment and implemented into a run-
ning intrusion detection system implemented throughout the thesis.
The system implemented is a proof-of-concept of an end-to-end intrusion detection sys-
tem running in a container network, that will gather network traﬀic within the network,
analyze the data based on a trained model, and present the results to the user through an
user interface.
The intrusion detection system will be set up on a remote server hosted on AWS to
imitate a realistic cloud-based environment. Furthermore, test containers running test
applications has been set up, to create a dataset used to train the chosen models, which
will later be used in the fully implemented intrusion detection system. This shows insights
into the entire flow, where a user would deploy the intrusion detection system, and train
on their own provided data, to suit the model to each use case, creating an anomaly based
solution, where the baseline of normality is always based on the independent system in
which the intrusion detection system resides.
In summary, the goal of this thesis is to implement a system that employs artificial intel-
ligence to detect outliers in a network of containers running in a remote cloud network,
identifying the strength and weaknesses of various artificial intelligence based models for
this use case, as well as show and discuss the advantages and weaknesses of such a de-
tection system. The thesis will thereby demonstrate a possible solution to a cloud-based
intrusion detection system and how it would adapt artificial intelligence as a means of
decision support. It will try to solve the challenges of implementing an eﬀicient detection
system that can be generalized for a broad range of systems.

1.1.1
Approach
Throughout the thesis, the author has taken several steps to address the objective of the
study. The following approach was followed throughout the thesis.
• The author has done research on existing intrusion detection systems, specifically
those implemented into cloud networks as well as research into the field of artificial
intelligence to study the performance of the models throughout several use cases
• Based on the former research, a few models and processing methods has been
adapted, and implementations were done to test the models in an isolated envi-
ronment with known datasets.
• Based on these implementations, systematic tests based on datasets, models and
preprocessing has been done. This has worked as an iterative process, where several
scenarios has been tested.
• Simultaneously, an end-to-end intrusion detection system has been developed, that
adopts a model used for detecting anomalous behavior in a realistic running envi-
ronment, monitored through an user interface.

Background
The following chapter will introduce the necessary knowledge behind the implemented
system and design choices to the reader. This includes a description of intrusion detection
systems, container technology as well as artificial intelligence and relevant models.
2.1
Intrusion Detection Systems
An intrusion detection system (IDS)[17] is a security mechanism designed to monitor and
analyze system activities for signs of malicious activities. There are two main types of
intrusion detection systems, namely network-based intrusion detection system and host-
based intrusion detection system.
A network-based detection system will monitor the network traﬀic in the system where
the intrusion detection system is located. These detection systems can look at features
like number of packages sent towards the network, the source of the packages, and similar
features to investigate whether the network traﬀic might be malicious. Network-based
detection systems are great to provide the overall insights in activities within the system.
A host-based detection system resides at each host, and can see what files are opened,
what processes are run, and other relevant information connected to a particular host.
These are great to monitor each host in details, but does not provide a comprehensive
overview of the system.
The goal of an intrusion detection system is to distinguish benign from malicious be-
havior, which can be achieved by two primary methods; Signature-based detection and
anomaly-based detection.
Signature-based detection systems works by identifying patterns (signatures) in the data,
that can indicate malicious behavior. These patterns are based on a manually specified
rule set, and are highly deterministic towards detection within the configured rules. This
can provide high accuracy, however, the rules must be maintained frequently as attack
methods evolves in a rapid rate[7], which can be a tedious process. This can make the
signature-based detection systems unable of detecting new types of attacks that differ
from existing attacks, or they might be vulnerable to evasion techniques. Such evasion
techniques could include splitting the payload, manipulating protocols and using other
ports.
A solution to this issue, is to instead look at the behavior of the system, and create a
baseline for what is normal in each specific system. By doing this, the detection system
will not need to rely on any rule sets or patterns, and does not need to know what mali-
cious versus benign behavior is, but will instead know if some behavior changes drastically,
as change in behavior can be a sign of malicious activity. These type of detection systems
are known as anomaly-based intrusion detection systems, where anomalies are detected
within the data.
The advantage of anomaly-based detection systems is the ability to detect novel patterns,
but a consequence might be a lower accuracy and/or a higher false positive rate, as the
detection method relies on the system to analyse patterns, and detect outliers in this data.

2.2
Container Technology
A container is an executable unit running application code and its dependencies, packing
it into one environment resulting in easy deployment. Packing up all dependencies makes
it easier to deploy across different devices and platforms as there are no requirements for
packages or dependencies installed on the host running the containers[18].
This causes containers to be a lightweight solution, and has caused container technol-
ogy to become a popular solution resulting in a continuously increasing market size and
adoption[19][4]. Types of containers include Docker, rklet and Containerd. This thesis
will solely focus on the use of Docker containers as Docker still remains the most popular
platform[4].
An otherwise very popular way of making applications independent from hosts have been
the use of virtual machines. The two technologies are very similar, however they do dif-
fer in some ways. The key difference being, that a virtual machine virtualizes an entire
machine down to the hardware layers, whereas containers only virtualize software layers

OSI Model
The Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) model[26] describes seven standard layers that
computer systems use to communicate over a network.
As the implemented intrusion
detection system is network-based, it makes sense to describe the 7-layer OSI model to
understand how a network packet is built. The seven layers is as follows: Physical Layer,
Data Link Layer, Network Layer, Transport Layer, Session Layer, Presentation Layer and
Application Layer and an illustration can be seen in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: OSI Model[26]
Layer 1, known as the Physical Layer, is responsible for the physical connection details
such as cables, connectors, voltages, and signaling. Its primary function is to establish the
transmission of raw binary data over a physical media.
The Data Link Layer (Layer 2) establishes and terminates a connection between two nodes
physically linked within a network. It divides packets into frames sending them from the
source to the destination. The layer is constructed of Logical Link Control (LLC) that
identifies network protocols and Media Access Control (MAC) using MAC addresses to
connect devices and determine permissions for transmission and reception of data.
Layer 3, known as the Network Layer, serves two primary purposes. It divides segments
into network packets and later reconstructs them upon reaching the recipient.
It also
manages packet routing by identifying the optimal path through a physical network. This
layer uses network addresses, which is typically Internet Protocol (IP) to route packets to
a destination node.
The Transport Layer processes data transmitted from the session layer, segmenting it
during transmission. It is responsible of reassembling these segments upon reception, con-
verting them back into usable data for the session layer. Additionally, the transport layer
manages flow control by aligning the data transmission rate with the receiving end’s con-
Cloud-based Intrusion Detection System with AI-driven Decision Support
9
==PAGE:17==
nection speed. Furthermore, it does error control, verifying the accuracy of received data
and requesting retransmission if errors are detected.
Layer 5, known as the Session Layer, establishes communication sessions between devices,
as well as terminating the connection when communication is finished. The session layer
also has the capability to establish checkpoints during data transfers, enabling devices to
resume the transfer from the last checkpoint in case of any interruptions.
Layer 6, referred to as the Presentation Layer, is responsible for preparing data for the
Application Layer. It specifies the encoding, encryption, and compression methods for
proper reception on the receiving end. This layer also takes data transmitted from the
Application Layer and prepares it for transmission across the Session Layer.
Layer 7, known as the Application Layer, is utilized by end-user clients like web browsers.
It employs protocols enabling software to transmit and receive information, presenting the
data to end-users in a meaningful way. Some instances of the Application Layer protocols
include Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP), File Transfer Protocol (FTP) and Domain
Name System (DNS).
The flow of the data, hence going from layer 7 to 1 or from layer 1 to 7 depends on
whether the data is transmitted or received.

2.5
Anomaly detection
Anomalies, also sometimes referred to as outliers, are values or data points that is signifi-
cantly different from the main part of the dataset. This is based on a predefined baseline
of normality. Anomaly detection is the detection of anomalies, which is done through
analysis to identify data points that deviates from the baseline. Anomaly detection is
based on two assumptions; First is that the anomalies in the data only occur rarely. Sec-
ond, is that the features of the anomalous data is significantly different from the normal
data. Anomaly detection can both be supervised, semi-supervised as well as unsupervised,
depending on the labels available in the data.
Before outliers can be identified, the normal behavior of a system must be modeled,
hence creating a baseline for what is normal behavior. There are several approaches for
doing this, whereas the most common techniques can be categorized into three main areas:
clustering, nearest neighbour and classification[30][32].
In the clustering method, the normal data points are assumed to be clustered together
while outliers will lay outside of this cluster. The outlier score is measured as the distance
between the data point and the nearest center of a cluster.
With the nearest neighbour method, the normal data is assumed to lay close to each
other in dense ”neighborhoods” while outliers will have a longer distance to their nearest
neighbor. The outlier score is therefore based on the distance from the nearest neighbor
of the data point.
In classification, the classifier learns, through supervised learning, what distinguish nor-
mal and anomalous data in the feature space. The outlier score is measured by probability
that a data point belongs to the normal class.
As data gets more complex, so does the different kinds of anomalies it can have. It is
important to know these variations before approaching a method, to understand what one
want to achieve. These types include point anomalies, contextual anomalies and collective
anomalies[32].
Point anomalies are individual instances that are significantly different from the rest of
the data.
Contextual anomalies are instances that are anomalous in a specific context but not neces-
sarily anomalous in other contexts. This could include time of day, where network traﬀic
might be considered anomalous if it occurs at unusual times.
Collective anomalies are groups of instances that are anomalous if seen in a collection,
even though the individual instances may not be anomalous. This could be the amount
of network traﬀic within a given time frame, which could represent a DDoS attack.
Within network traﬀic it is often seen that data points can be both contextual, col-
lective and point anomalies. Hence, more sophisticated approaches are needed for the

2.6.1
Random Forest
Random Forest is a supervised decision tree based machine learning algorithm, that com-
bines the output of multiple decision trees to get one single result, and is able to handle
both classification and regression problems[35][36].
A decision tree starts by making a decision based on a ”question” related to the value
of the features, and from there making a series of decisions based on a series of questions.
All of these questions and decisions make up nodes in the decision tree, and is that way
splitting the data down the tree. The data will, through the decisions arrive at a final
decision, denoted by leaf nodes in the tree, representing the outcome, hence the classifica-
tion for classification tasks. An illustration of a decision tree can be seen in Figure 2.5.
The aim of decision trees is to split the data into several ”decision branches” such that
the data can be classified as accurate as possible, and metrics such as the Gini Index[37],
often used for classification tasks, can be used to evaluate the quality of the split, hence
how well the split would be for classification based on the data.
The Gini Index[37] is used to select which features should be further split in the deci-
sion tree, which is determined by how pure the split of the given feature will be, meaning
whether the decision will result in one label or another. The purity of a feature split
refers to how well the split separates the data into groups with similar labels. The goal is

To achieve this, Random Forest utilizes bagging and feature randomness.
Bagging is the process of creating multiple subsets of the training data by randomly
sampling with replacement. Each subset, known as a bootstrap sample, is used to train a
separate decision tree. The purpose is to expose each tree in the ensemble to a different
version of the training data, allowing them to capture different aspects of the overall pat-

Feature randomness involves introducing randomness in the selection of features consid-

which features are most important when making decisions. Instead of considering all the
features every time, each tree randomly picks only a subset of features to consider at each

feature randomness is used to make each tree look at a different subset of features at each
split.
Combining bagging and feature randomness, ensures that the Random Forest model builds
several trees that learn from different perspectives and use different features, thereby re-
ducing the risk of overfitting.
For classification tasks, the final prediction of the Random Forest is determined by a
majority vote amongst the individual trees. Each tree votes for a class, and the class with
the most votes becomes the predicted class, creating the ensemble effect by aggregating
the predictions of each decision tree. This helps improve the generalization of the model
and reduce the overfitting that might occur in individual trees.

Within the input layer there are no computations, and is simply used to pass the in-

2.7.2
Recurrent Neural Networks
A recurrent neural network (RNN), is a type of deep learning algorithm that uses se-
quential data or time series data. Recurrent neural networks are distinguished by their
“memory” as they take information from prior inputs to influence the current input and
output, making it somewhat context aware[48][49].
Recurrent neural networks share parameters across each layer of the network.
While
feed forward networks, which is a network where the data only flows one way, have differ-
ent weights across each node, recurrent neural networks share the same weight parameter
within each layer of the network. This decreases the number of parameters, and hence al-
lowing the model to generalize the sequences of varying lengths. The weights are adjusted
through the processes of backpropagation and gradient descent like feed forward networks.
An illustration of a recurrent neural network against a regular feed forward neural network
can be seen in Figure 2.11.
Figure 2.11: RNN vs Feed Forward Network[49]
In standard neural networks, the inputs and outputs are independent of one another, but
sometimes, such as when predicting the next record of a series, the prior records are nec-
essary, and therefore the previous records must be remembered. RNNs have an internal
memory that stores all information about calculations, and the most important compo-
nent of RNN is the hidden state, which remembers specific information about a sequence.
This particular feature enables the use of context for problem solving, and for anomaly
detection this is a powerful feature as it enables the detection of contextual anomalies.
Essentially, an unfolded recurrent neural network is a feed forward network with an extra
parameter, which is the value stored for the hidden state. An unfolded RNN can be seen

2.7.3
LSTM
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)[51] is a type of recurrent neural network meant to
solve the issues of vanishing and exploding gradients, and is often used for supervised
tasks. As it is a recurrent neural network, it is good for handling sequential data predic-
tion tasks. Whereas a regular recurrent neural network only has short-term memory, the
LSTM model is good for handling long-term dependencies and can grasp the order depen-
dence for a longer time, which is important for problems that requires context awareness
over a longer period.
It varies from traditional RNNs as traditional RNNs only has a single hidden state, which
can make it diﬀicult to learn long-term dependencies. LSTMs solves this by introducing a
memory cell. The memory cell works as a container holding information for an extended
period of time, and thereby learning long-term dependencies in sequential data.
This memory cell is controlled by three gates; An input gate, a forget gate and an output
gate. By utilizing these gates, it is possible to control what information to add to, remove
from, as well as output from the memory cell. Hence, the LSTM network can selectively
save or forget information as it flows through the network. The information is thereby
contained in the cell, and memory manipulation is done by these gates seen in Figure 2.13.

The forget gate
The forget gate is used to remove information that is no longer useful to the state in the
memory cells. Two inputs xt and ht−1, which represents the input at the current time
step and the previous cell output, respectively, are given to the forget gate and multiplied
with weights associated with the forget gate as well as an addition of a bias. This result
is used as input to an activation function that gives binary output 0 or 1. If the output is
0, the information will be forgotten, and if the output is 1, the information will be kept
in the cell for future use.
This can be formulated as follows:
ft = σ(Wf[ht, xt] + bf)
The input gate
The input gate ensures the addition of useful information to the state in the cell. The in-
formation starts off by being regulated using a sigmoid activation function, and the values
to be added is filtered using xt and ht−1 similar to the forget gate. Afterwards, a vector is
created using a tanh activation function which results in an output Lt between -1 to +1,
that contains all possible values from xt and ht−1. The values of this vector are multiplied
with the aforementioned filtered values it to obtain the useful information desired to be
added. The equations for the input gate are as follows:
it = σ(Wi[ht−1, xt] + bi)
Lt = tanh(WL[ht−1, xt] + bL)
The previous state Ct−1 is multiplied by ft, thereby disregarding the information that was
previously chosen to be forgotten in the forget gate. Afterwards the chosen useful infor-
mation it ∗Lt is added, representing the updated value, which is adjusted to the amount
at which it was chosen to update each state
Ct = (ft ∗Ct−1) + (it ∗Lt)
The output gate
The output gate is responsible for extracting useful information for the current state and
present it as an output.
A vector is generated by applying the tanh activation function on the state cell. The
information is then regulated using a sigmoid activation function, and filter by the values
that must be remembered using xt and ht−1, just like seen with the forget gate and the
input gate. The values of the vector are multiplied with the filtered values and sent as an
output which is used as input to the next cell. This is represented by the equation:
ot = σ(Wo[ht−1, xt] + bo)
2.7.4
Autoencoders
Autoencoders[52] are an unsupervised learning technique, typically used for dimensional
reduction, in which neural networks are employed for the task of representation learning.
Representation Learning is a process in machine learning where algorithms extract pat-
terns from the data to create representations that are easier to understand and process by
extracting the most important features.
By the use of an encoder and decoder, the networks are designed to learn a compressed
22
Cloud-based Intrusion Detection System with AI-driven Decision Support
==PAGE:30==
representation of input data, and later reconstructing the compressed data into the origi-
nal input. The encoder compresses the input into a lower-dimensional latent space, while
the decoder reconstructs the input from this representation. Autoencoders can be used for
a variety of applications including anomaly detection, and feature learning. Especially the
reconstruction phase shows an invaluable role when autoencoders are used for anomaly
detection tasks, as the lack of ability in the reconstruction phase is a sign that novel data
has occurred.
Autoencoders can also be used as part of a broader model, where the autoencoder is
used as a feature extraction model. When the model is trained, the decoder can be dis-
carded and the model up to the encoder can be used. The output of such a model is a fixed
compressed representation of the input data, which can be used as an input in another
model thereby using the autoencoder for reduction of dimensions in data.
2.7.5
LSTM-Autoencoder
Long Short-Term Memory Autoencoders (LSTM-Autoencoders) is a fusion of the two
beforementioned models, LSTM and Autoencoders. It combines the sequence modeling
capabilities of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks with the reconstruction capa-
bility of autoencoders[53].
The encoder component of LSTM-Autoencoders utilizes LSTM units to process sequential
input data, effectively capturing the temporal contexts and encoding them into a com-
pressed representation in a latent space. This ensures that the model can retain relevant
information from an entire sequence of data, making it well-suited for tasks involving con-
textual patterns such as network traﬀic.
In the decoding phase, the LSTM-Autoencoder employs LSTM units to reconstruct the
data from the learned latent representation. The LSTM units in the decoder ensure the
generation of outputs that preserves the temporal dependencies present in the original
input.
The anomaly detection capabilities of LSTM-Autoencoders works by training the model on
normal data sequences, so that the model learns to reconstruct them accurately. Anoma-
lies, which deviate significantly from learned patterns, will not be reconstructed accurately
and will result in a higher rate of reconstruction errors. The performance of the model is
thereby evaluated based on the model’s ability to recreate the input sequence.
The flow can be summarized as follows:
Encoding Phase
The input sequence is passed through the encoder, which consists of LSTM layers, which
capture and compress the relevant information from the input sequence into a fixed-size
representation, known as the latent space.
Latent Space
The latent space is a compressed representation of the input data. It contains the most
important features or patterns that the autoencoder has chosen to be relevant for rep-
resenting the input. The dimension of the latent space is usually much lower than the
dimension of the input data.
Decoding Phase

autoencoder, which consists of LSTM layers in reverse order. The decoder attempts to

can effectively capture and represent the essential features of the input data in the latent
space. The goal is to minimize the reconstruction error, ensuring that the decoded output
matches the original input as much as possible.

Design
For the design of the implemented intrusion detection system, the flow from data gathering,
data preprocessing, classification and monitoring is considered, creating a proof-of-concept
of a complete end-to-end environment for intrusion detection in cloud environments. The
intrusion detection system is a network-based detection system, that can be deployed into
a container network, to monitor the traﬀic within the network, and thereby establish the
behavior in the system and ultimately detect any abnormal behavior that may occur.
3.1
Use case
The implemented intrusion detection system has been developed with a use case in mind;
A company having one or more distributed services developed as micro services running in
containers. It is seen that a big part of companies develop micro services and a major part
of companies have adapted containerization technologies[19][59]. It is therefore reasonable
to assume that there is a use case of distributed services deployed as containers running
in companies.
Such applications and services can be targeted in multiple ways, as they have multiple
attack surfaces. Sari Sultan et al[60] provided a survey of the issues and challenges within
container security, which has been considered throughout the design of this intrusion de-
tection system based on container introspection.
Some of the possible attack scenarios include insecurely built or outdated container images
which may contain vulnerabilities that attackers can exploit to compromise the application
or gain unauthorized access. This can result in exploits such as remote code execution
like seen with the ShellShock vulnerability[61].
Such attacks might alter the behavior
of containers, causing them to do malicious behavior such as trying to access sensitive
information, like embedded clear-text secrets, or to be part of botnets. Furthermore, if
the containers are running an insecure internet-phased application, the container could in
several ways be vulnerable to several exploits like a Denial-of-Service attack.
An insecure container can also be used to target other containers through inter-communication
between containers. A container could, for instance be used to create requests to other
containers, exploiting any potential vulnerabilities of those containers.
Generally, the attack surface and threats can be put in connection with the CIA triad[62].
The CIA triad, encompasses the concepts Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability, and
serves as a base for information security.
Risks towards the confidentiality concept can arise through container image vulnerabili-
ties, where attackers might exploit insecure dependencies, gaining unauthorized access to
sensitive data within the container.
The concept of integrity could be compromised if malicious actors is to manipulate the
integrity of application code running in the containers.
Lastly, risks of compromising availability may arise from Denial-of-Service attacks target-
ing containerized applications, overwhelming the application, or sending malicious pay-
loads, causing them to fail.

The implemented intrusion detection system aims to detect such attack scenarios, by
detecting sudden distinct shift in behavior in the container. If the container suddenly

detection system solely focuses on network traﬀic, the detection of attack scenarios is nar-

When creating the model for OC-SVM, a lot of parameters can be tuned according to the

The number of features corresponds to the number of features in the encoded data given,
meaning for this use case there is 124 features to consider, as the output of the one hot
encoded data has 124 features.
This requirement to match the input shape with the size of the data, adds a layer of
comxplexity if implemented into a running system, as there must be a mechanism to en-
sure the data fed to the model is always at a fixed size.
The layers of the model follow the encoder-decoder architecture, where the input is en-
coded into a smaller size, and then reconstructed back to its original size.
The first layers after the input layer are LSTMs-layers compressing the input down from
size [(, 10, 124)] to size (, 32), meaning the latent space will be of 32 dimensions. The
LSTM layers all use the ReLU activation function. These layers are used for capturing
temporal dependencies in the input data, while the ReLU activation function introduces
non-linearity to the model.
Next a Dropout layer is implemented.
Dropout is a regularization technique where a
random fraction of input units are set to zero during training. It helps prevent overfitting
by reducing the reliance on specific neurons, making the network more stable[73].
The RepeatVector layer repeats the input n times.
For this model it has been set to
the amount of timesteps, meaning the output of this layer will match the size of the next
LSTM layer[74]
To decompress the size, further LSTM layers are employed.
It decompresses the size
back to the original size. Similarly, the ReLU activation function is used for these layers.
This will capture the dependencies of the reconstructed output similar to the first LSTM
layers which captured the dependencies of the input.
Similar to the second block, the second Dropout layer helps prevent overfitting by ran-
domly setting a fraction of input units to zero during training.
Finally, the Dense layer is applied which is a layer that is deeply connected with its
preceding layer which means the neurons of the layer are connected to every neuron of its
preceding layer. This works as the output layer. The Dense layer is the output layer and
will result in the original size. Hence the output of the Dense layer should be the recon-
structed data. The TimeDistributed wrapper is applied around the Dense layer, which
is commonly used in recurrent neural networks. It allows the Dense layer to be applied
to each time step independently. If the input is a sequence of data, the TimeDistributed
wrapper layer applies the specified layer, hence the Dense layer, to each time step of the
sequence[75][76].
In summary, the model takes a 3D input tensor with shape (Batch Size, time steps, number
of features), and runs it through an encoder layer which consists of stacked LSTM layers.
The purpose of the LSTM layers is to keep the contextual representation throughout en-
coding. The data will be encoded into a latent space, which is a compressed representation
of the input data that is learned by the encoder. The compressed data will then be decom-
pressed by the decoder that will reconstruct the data into the original size. Throughout
the process of reconstructing/decoding, the reconstruction loss can be measured. This

measures how well the model can reconstruct the data. When the model is trained and
has a desired performance, based on the reconstruction error, the model can be used for
classification. When classifying, one will input test data to the model, and the data will be
compressed and then reconstructed. When reconstructing, the reconstruction error will be
measured. If novel new data occur in the test data, the reconstruction error will be high,
as the model will not be able to reconstruct it, and hence it can be assumed that novel
data, hence an outlier, has occured. One can set a threshold for how high the reconstruc-
tion error can be when classyfing. If the reconstruction error is above this, one can assume
that the input contains outliers. Hence, using this fusion, it employs the capabilities of
LSTM to learn the contextual representations of the data, and the autoencoder to learn
the representations of the features, and recreate them, to provide the possibility of novel
detection.
Activation functions
It has been chosen to use ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit)[77] in the LSTM layers which is
denoted by
f(x) = max(0, x)
If the input x is positive, ReLU returns x. Otherwise, 0 is returned. ReLU provides three
key components to the network. It introduces non-linearity to the network, allowing it to
learn complex patterns and relationships in the data. It is computationally eﬀicient, as
the function involves simple thresholding, making it faster to compute compared to other
activation functions. It can introduce sparsity in the network because it sets all negative
values to zero, which can be beneficial in terms of memory eﬀiciency and can lead to a
more precise generalization. As it only returns 0 or positive values, this also prevents
vanishing gradient.
Loss function and optimizer
For the LSTM-Autoencoder model, the input will be encoded to a latent space that will
then be reconstructed, and the reconstruction loss is measured. This loss can be measured
in several ways, however for this use case, mean square error (MSE)[78] has been used.
MSE is a computation of the average squared difference between the true value and the
values predicted from the model and is formulated as follows
MSE =

original data and the reconstructed data.
The optimizer used is Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) which is an optimization
algorithm that adjusts the learning rates for each parameter individually, making it adap-

Feature scaling
For the OC-SVM model and the LSTM-Autoencoder model, feature scaling has been done.
This has not been done for the Random Forest model, as feature scaling does not serve
any benefits towards decision tree based models, as decision trees make decisions based
on feature values by selecting the best split at each node. The decision-making process in
decision trees relies on comparing the values of features within the same node, and scaling
does therefore not provide any value towards the decision making.
The implemented LSTM-Autoencoder use the ReLU activation function and ReLU can
be sensitive to the scale of input features. Thereby will standardizing or normalizing the
input features help ensuring that the values fall within a certain range, preventing issues
like vanishing or exploding gradients during training.
Furthermore, OC-SVM relies on the kernel function to map input data into a higher-
dimensional space where a hyperplane is constructed to separate normal instances from
potential outliers. Common kernel functions, such as the Radial Basis Function (RBF),
involves distance calculations between data points. The feature scaling ensures that all

5.2
Tests and Evaluation
During the thesis there has been a systematic approach of testing models and their hy-
per parameters. Time has been spent on researching methods and what is suitable for
the problem, however a majority of the tests have followed an ”trial-and-error” method,
that often is necessary in artificial intelligence based solutions due to the varying inde-
pendent factors, where it is not always transparent what causes a model to perform well
or perform poorly.
This process have somewhat been automated, by making it easily
available to switch out datasets, encoding methods, models, and so on. Rather than ap-
plying an automated cross-validation method such as the GridSearchCV[85] method from
scikit-learn, that automatically finds the optimal hyper parameters by cross validation
and grid search, the systematic test approach instead makes it is possible to add a layer
of transparency to what affect the performance of the model. However, for a practical
implementation it would make sense to use an automated tool likeGridSearchCV to find
the best hyper parameters when training the model.
The different components being tested are:
• Encoding method
• Models and their hyper parameters
• Datasets
• Feature extraction
Several metrics have been taken into consideration when evaluating the performance of
the models depending on the models.
5.2.1
Machine Learning Models
For the Random Forest and OC-SVM models, accuracy, precision, F1 and recall has been
calculated[86].

The accuracy metric is calculated by the total number of correct classifications divided by
the total number of classifications.
Accuracy =
TP + TN
TP + TN + FP + FN
where TP is True Positives, TN is True Negatives, FP is False Positives and FN is False
Negatives.
This metric can be insuﬀicient in evaluating the performance of an anomaly detection
model, as it can be misleading in some situations. In a dataset that is highly scewed,
hence having a very low amount of anomalies, the model achieves high accuracy even if it
classifies everything as normal behavior.
Therefore the two metrics, precision and recall is introduced to provide a better mea-
surement of the performance of the model. Precision is defined as the number of true
positives divided by the number of true positives plus the number of false positives, hence
it measures the accuracy of the positive predictions made by the model. A high precision
indicates that when the model predicts a positive class, it is likely to be correct.
Precision =
TP
TP + FP
Recall is the number of true positives divided by the number of true positives plus the
number of false negatives, hence measures the ability of the model to capture all the
relevant instances of the positive class. A high recall indicates that the model is good at
identifying most of the positive instances in the dataset
Recall =
TP
TP + FN
Depending on the use case or application, it may be desirable to optimize for either pre-
cision or recall.
Optimizing for high recall may be more appropriate when the cost of a false negative is
high, which is typically the case within cyber security, where false negatives can be critical.
The F-score helps to measure recall and precision at the same time.
F1 = 2 ∗Recall ∗Precision
Recall + Precision
The F1 metric indicates balance between precision and recall, making it a valuable metric,
especially when both false positives and false negatives needs to be minimized. A high F1
score indicates a model that performs well in terms of both precision and recall.
5.2.2
LSTM-Autoencoder
To evaluate the performance of the LSTM-Autoencer model, the performance in recon-
structing input sequences is calculated. The loss measures the difference between the input
sequence and the reconstructed output. For this thesis it is computed using mean square
error presented in Section 3.6. Lower reconstruction loss indicates better performance. It
is also the reconstruction loss that is used to predict whether some data is anomalous,
where a threshold is set on the reconstruction loss, and if the loss is greater than the
threshold, the data is considered anomalous.

Discussion
This chapter will summarize the key aspects and findings of the thesis and how it affects the
research questions defined. The chapter will contain small discussions of various findings

6.1
Summary
The findings of the thesis shows that there are several possibilities to gather useful data
that can be used for anomaly detection in a cloud based intrusion detection system by
focusing on container introspection. It was chosen to solely focus on network traﬀic data
between containers, and a real world dataset was created based on a test application de-
ployed to an instance running on AWS. Furthermore, two public datasets NSL-KDD and
USNW have been used for experiments in controlled settings, before applying the models
to the running intrusion detection system. Utilizing various artificial intelligence mod-
els for anomaly detection highlights the impact of different datasets and their quality on
model performance. Therefore, implementing a baseline behavior for each monitored sys-
tem enhances the effectiveness of the intrusion detection system. The presence of noise in
datasets highlights the importance of data cleaning, preprocessing, and feature extraction
techniques like PCA before inputting the data into the models.
During the experiments done isolated in Google Colab it showed that different approaches,
datasets and hyper parameters highly affected the models, and that a test with the Ran-
dom Forest algorithm on the NSL-KDD achieved a F-score of 97% while the same test on
the UNSW dataset only achieved a F-score of 48%, underlining that one model might
work in a specific way to some data, but must be changed to perform eﬀiciently on other
data. Meanwhile, the training and prediction times overall were relatively low, which was
promising for the further implementation.
Looking at the results, it showed that Random Forest generally performed best, and es-
pecially on the UNSW dataset, the Random Forest model achieved higher results than
OC-SVM. Meanwhile OC-SVM performed with various results, with the best results clas-
sifying data in the NSL-KDD dataset, and did not perform well with the UNSW dataset. Both
models achieved up 100% accuracy, precision and recall on the PCKSN dataset, which can
be a sign that the anomalies are very distinct to the normal behavior, hence enabling the
models to easily classify the data.
While the models showed great results in an isolated test environment with predefined
datasets, a layer of complexity was added to implement the models into a running in-
trusion detection system focusing on container introspection. These challenges included
avoiding any bottlenecks when classifying, continuously sending data to the model, and
general design questions of connecting the data gathered with the analysis module when
running in a containerized application. Furthermore, several security risks had to be con-
sidered during the choice of data gathering method.
The final product was an intrusion detection system running as a containerized distributed
application. The focus on this implementation was to create a lightweight system that
can easily be deployed into an system running in one or more containers. Thereby the
intrusion detection system would be deployed as a ”side-car” container to the rest of the
target system.

6.2
General discussion
Despite the variable results of OC-SVM, the flexibility of the model showed to be be a
valuable in an eﬀicient system, and trying out other unsupervised algorithms could be
beneficial for the system. Using a supervised algorithm means that training the model
for each particular system, would require an extensive work in creating datasets for each
system, whereas OC-SVM, as seen, enables the user to create the dataset by simply leav-
ing their system running while collecting the data, providing an easier manageable system.
However, the OC-SVM still contains certain limitations.
For this particular use case
each network record will be represented as a data point in the feature space. As the data
is somewhat clustered by the decision boundary, it means that anomalous behavior will
be based per data point, meaning the model would only be able to detect point anoma-
lies. Thereby, there is no consideration towards any contextual anomalies in the behavior,
which provided the motivation of implementing LSTM.
Although, the implementation of a deep learning model also increased the complexity of
training the model. In general deep learning models require a lot of data of good quality,
and depending on the nature of the data, it might be diﬀicult to create good datasets for
these models. However, related work presented in Section 1.3 did show promising results
on the NSL-KDD dataset, and it was therefore assumed that it should be possible when
testing at least this dataset.
Only using network traﬀic as data for the purpose of detection might not be enough
data and hence might not be representative to the overall detection. The system would
benefit from a hybrid solution using both network based and host based intrusion de-
tection techniques to get a more detailed overview of the behavior. The initial research
and testing showed that both file monitoring and process monitoring could be added as a
means of data gathering for anomaly detection as well.
Results showed that the performance of the models was very dependent on the dataset, and
this can make it somewhat diﬀicult to guarantee performance of a model in an unknown
system. The implemented solution was made to create an easily managed anomaly-based
intrusion detection system. However, for the model to analyse data accurately based on
the specific baseline for each system, the model must be trained for each system. By
applying the use of proper data cleaning, processing and tools such as SearchGridCV it
can be easier to mitigate the challenges of unknown and different data in systems. De-
pending on the data, it needs different preproccesing and cleaning which must be taken
into consideration when implementing the end-to-end application into a running system.
Similarly, the choice of features gathered does present some limitations. Features of each
network record is captured, however the raw data payload is omitted. For a more sophis-

6.3
Key challenges
This section will present the key challenges of the implemented system.
6.3.1
Running in privileged mode or same network namespace
During capturing of network traﬀic, it was found that that capturing container had to
either run in privileged mode or to be on the same network stack as the target containers.
Both of these solutions will increase security risks, in that containers will either be run
elevated, or will not be isolated from each other, as they are all put on the same network
stack as the Packet Sniffer container. By having the container run in privileged mode,
the security risks include accessing the host with privileged access if an attacker breaks
out of the container, also referred to as ”container escape”[87].
Putting the containers on the same network stack includes the risk exploiting possible
vulnerabilities in containers through the possibility of inter-communication between con-
tainers. Considering these risks, it was chosen to run the container on the same network
stack, instead of running the Packet Sniffer in privileged mode. Ultimately, it was cho-
sen to use Scapy as the network gathering tool, however, according to the documentation
Scapy does contain certain limitations, as it not fast, hence it might miss packages in a
rapid environment with a lot of data, and it is advised to use tcpdump[88]. As this can be
a crucial factor to the intrusion detection, it should strongly be considered how this can
be solved.
6.3.2
Malicious data is unknown
The implemented anomaly-based detection approach currently suffers from only indicating
that the system has started to behave differently than the learned normal behavior. This
may not necessarily be due to malicious behavior, but can also be a sign of outage in
the system or change of functionality. The implemented system might therefore instead
benefit from a hybrid model where both outliers are detected, and a supervised learning
approach is adapted for the purpose of detecting specifically malicious behavior.
6.3.3
Continuously adaption
Anomaly detection can sometimes have low latency, meaning it may be necessary to often
update existing models as new data becomes available. It is also important to note that
behavior in systems often change, and it might be necessary to adapt the baseline of normal
behavior relatively often in the system. This can take up a lot of resources and time, if
manually having to train the model. Therefore the system will benefit from continuously
adaption of the model and baseline.
6.3.4
Preprocessing
For the experiments within this thesis, it was chosen to use one hot encoding and binary
encoding, which showed great results. However, one hot encoding suffer from that it cannot
handle new unseen data. As the encoder is trained on particular data, it will create the
dimensions of the data based on the unique values of the categorical data. This means if
a new unique value occurs, the one hot encoder would not be able to handle it. This is a
crucial weakness to consider in a system in which the purpose is to detect anomalies. As
binary encoding is creating columns based on the binary values of the unique values, new
unseen data can be handled to a higher degree than with one hot encoding. However, a
situation might occur where new unseen data would have to be handled in more bits, hence
dimensions, than the encoder is trained with. This could occur if the encoder is trained
with three unique values of a categorical feature, hence representing it in two dimensions
64

with the highest binary value being 11. However, if a fourth unique value would occur
during classification, the fourth unique value must be represented with three bits, hence
three dimensions, as the value would be represented as 001. This also means that having
more initial dimensions when using the binary encoder, would leave more room for new
data.
6.3.5
Issues with LSTM-Autoencoder
The LSTM-Autoencoder ended up presenting several challenges during implementation
and tests. The nontransparent nature of working with artificial intelligence poses chal-
lenges in debugging the performance the models, which especially came forward during
the implementation of the LSTM-Autoencoder, and several changes were made through-
out the implementation.
Comparing with any related work, it should be possible for the LSTM-Autoencoder model
to learn the representations in the NSL-KDD dataset, showing that the it is not necessar-
ily the data quality being the issue, as this can otherwise be the case, as deep learning
models, especially LSTM, requires a lot of data, to properly learn the relations in the data.
Looking aside of the results, and looking at the LSTM-Autoencoder from a practical im-
plementation, there is an added layer of complexity compared to the two other models in
regards to classification of continuously gathered data. Both Random Forest and OC-SVM
does not require any specifics in terms of shape of input data, while the LSTM-Autoencoder
has the input shape defined, that the input data must adhere to when classifying the data.
Furthermore, as the LSTM layers of the LSTM-Autoencoder is a supervised learning
algorithm, this model face the same challenge as with Random Forest, where the user
must provide a labeled dataset for training.
A significant challenge might arise when implementing the LSTM-Autoencoder into a
containerized application, as neural networks are known to be both more complex and
slower. Hence it might affect the performance of the containerized intrusion detection
system. As the LSTM-Autoencoder never got to fully work, this could not be tested,
however it is an interesting aspect to consider.
6.4
Choice of models
Both the OC-SVM and Random Forest showed promising results in training and pre-
diction time, hence qualifies as good candidates to be used in a containerized intrusion
detection system, where the container being lightweight is a priority. Furthermore, none
of these models have much requirement to the input, so if the data is preproccessed with
same encoders and PCA the model was trained with, the input can be fed to the model.
Oppositely, the LSTM-Autoencoder has a requirement in terms of the shape of the in-
put data, adding a layer of complexity towards how the data should be fed to the model.
Furthermore, as neural networks are more heavyweight than traditional machine learning
algorithms, this might cause too much overhead in the system in consideration to the prob-
lem, meaning the computational requirements might be too heavy compared to the results
it can achieve. The LSTM-Autoencoder was chosen because of the contextual awareness,
and the support of novel detection. However, it is important to consider how suitable the
model is on the bigger scale, especially when only considering network traﬀic. Collective
and contextual anomalies might be detected with other methods, like looking at the rate
of packets within a given timeframe, or look at network activity on a particular time of day.

6.5
Research Questions: Summary of Findings
The research and experiments of the thesis has been focused around the initial research
question. This section will sum up the findings in relation to the research questions.
How can an environment be set up to collect a real cloud data set for an
IDS?
The experiments included both local and a remote setup, with the latter implemented on
AWS through an EC2 instance running Linux. The local setup, was installed with Docker
and WSL, and exhibited no noteworthy difference in performance when compared to the
AWS deployment.
This implies that deploying the intrusion detection system locally,
through a Cloud Service Provider (CSP), or on a user-owned server would yield consistent
performance and setup requirements, given equivalent host resources. Leveraging Docker’s
network handling capabilities the creation of an application for data gathering lead to the
capturing of network traﬀic within a monitored distributed system of implemented test
applications, enabling data collection for subsequent analysis.
Additionally, the development of an application, capable of controlling network traﬀic,
demonstrated the feasibility of generating a real-life dataset.
What will be the main challenges when implementing an IDS in cloud based
environments?
Developing an intrusion detection system in cloud-based environments posed challenges,
particularly in ensuring effective data gathering without compromising security, while cre-
ating a lightweight design.
Furthermore, a challenge can be the resources available, which requires careful consid-
eration. Containers are designed to be lightweight, and the system must run with low
latency to minimize waiting time when detecting anomalies. Designing the intrusion de-
tection structure must therefore account for resource constraints. Similarly, there is a
potential bottleneck in the Analysis Engine, especially if the analysis takes too long.
To mitigate this, a two-container approach was implemented, with distinct responsibili-
ties for data gathering and analysis. Rapid data gathering for a short duration ensures
a manageable amount of data sent to be analysed, which allows for rapid analysis and
classification, promoting live anomaly detection.
What will be the main challenges when employing artificial intelligence in
an IDS?
The main challenges of employing artificial intelligence in an intrusion detection system
is adapting to the unknown data quality in advance. The key lies in creating a model
that can generalize well to fit into diverse running systems, where the system can adapt
the model and configure hyper parameters in a flexible way. This involves designing the
intrusion detection system to dynamically adjust and learn from the specifics of the system

it is deployed in.
The use of artificial intelligence also increase the requirements of consideration towards the
use of resources, and it is important to avoid any bottlenecks occuring during classification.
How can we create a lightweight and easily deployable IDS in cloud based
environments?
Creating a lightweight and easily deployable intrusion detection system is achieved through
the utilization of Docker and container technology. Through the development of several
containers using Dockerfiles and Docker-compose, the intrusion detection system becomes
easily deployable within a distributed system. Simple configuration adjustments in the
Docker-compose file facilitate simple integration. The choice of Docker enables users to
deploy the intrusion detection system with minimal setup requirements. Additionally, the
adoption of flexible models such as OC-SVM enhances the ease of baselining the detec-
tion system for a specific system. This approach allows the intrusion detection system to
initiate data gathering and, at any point, update the model based on the collected data,
offering adaptability without imposing significant setup burdens on the user.

7
Conclusion
The main goal of this thesis was to examine to which extent an easy and lightweight solu-
tion can be created for the purpose of monitoring anomalous behavior in a containerized
distributed system using artificial intelligence.
To examine this, a series of tests were
conducted and presented in Section 5.3. A full stack end-to-end intrusion detection sys-
tem was implemented and several artificial intelligence based models including Random
Forest, OC-SVM and an LSTM-Autoencoder was implemented for the experiments. The
implementation and results showed that it was possible to a great extent to implement a
lightweight solution, that can easily be integrated into a running distributed system and
gather network traﬀic that can be used to create a baseline of normal traﬀic for each sys-
tem and use machine learning models to detect anomalies in the monitored system. Both
public datasets have been tested and a dataset created with a implemented test application
have been used for experiments. It was found that the data gathering methods available
creates security vulnerabilities, which must be considered. The experiments showed that
the results of performance depended on the datasets, and Random Forest generally per-
formed better on the UNSW dataset with a precision of 98% and accuracy of 91%, while
OC-SVM performed best on the NSL-KDD with precision of 90% and accuracy of 84%. The
LSTM-Autoencoder encountered several challenges throughout the implementation and
this implementation has been left for future work.
Implementing the machine learning models into a fully running system, showed that when
adapting machine learning models, a generalized method for data handling is needed, and
model and hyper parameters should be considered dynamically depending on the data
collected. Meanwhile, it was possible to employ artificial intelligence for the use of de-
tection in containerized distributed system without any issues of bottlenecks occurring,
which resulted in a smooth continuous classification.
The final implementation did show some issues when classifying data as it resulted in
a high rate of false positives, while the LSTM-Autoencoder also presented several chal-
lenges during initial tests.
The final product is an end-to-end intrusion detection system deployed as containers,
in a network of target containers to be monitored. Due to the use of Docker and Docker
compose, the system is easy deployable, and is capable of baselining to the target system
almost automatically, easing the process of creating datasets while still being capable of
having the model baselining to the specific system.
7.1
Future Work
The preliminary work have presented valuable insights into a solution of intrusion de-
tection within the domain of container applications, effectively bridging the gap between
artificial intelligence research and practical implementation. However the final outcome
benefits from further enhancements, presented in this section.
The intrusion detection system benefits from adding more data gathering methods like
process and file monitoring to create a hybrid of a network based intrusion detection and
a host based intrusion detection system. This combination of both data gathering meth-
ods can help in getting a more advanced look into the containers running. Initial data
68

gathering experiments showed that both of these are possible.
As the implemented system only detects anomalous behavior, it does not have any knowl-
edge towards what malicious representations are, and the system might benefit from having
a hybrid between anomaly detection and supervised learning where known malicious data
is also learned, which could eventually be implemented into a two-phase application, where
the system is initially detecting anomalous behavior, and based on this data detecting any
malicious behavior in the detected anomalous data using multi-class classification.
Finally, some work still remain for the LSTM-Autoencoder, and further debugging should
