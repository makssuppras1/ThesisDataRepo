Abstract
To further advance laboratory automation, a bridge is required to connect the unordered
human workspace with the structured world of automation equipment. This work focuses
on 96-well plates, commonly used in research labs, and presents a robotic system to
transfer these plates, for example, from a laboratory workbench to a liquid handler. The
developed system aims to minimize the time researchers spend manually handling tasks
by automating the pick-and-place operations of 96-well plates.
This study explores the integration of computer vision and robot manipulation in research
laboratories using commonly available and cost-effective hardware. The robot observes
its environment with a camera, detecting plates using a You Only Look Once (YOLO)
model trained on mainly synthetic data. The depth and segmented images are combined
to accurately estimate the position of the plate using point cloud registration, allowing for
precise grasping by the parallel gripper. Optical character recognition (OCR) is employed
to determine the correct plate orientation by identifying the characters A to H marking
the wells, addressing challenges of visibility on transparent and translucent plates with
embossed, engraved, and printed characters. The robot then places the plates on au-
tomation equipment, such as liquid handlers, without spilling any liquid.
Results demonstrate a robust detection model capable of adapting to varying laboratory
conditions. Additionally, a pick-and-place routine specifically tailored to 96-well plates is
developed with an accuracy of 92% for placing the plate in ┬▒2 mm. The work brings nov-
elty to the state of the art by the OCR on embossed, engraved and printed characters on
transparent and translucent plates, achieving a 97% success rate in correct plate rotation
determination with a simple ring light setup. Additionally, the work serves as a first inves-
tigation for future improvement in optimizing camera positions for character visibility using
active vision techniques.
The project delivers an easy extendable setup based on the Robot Operating System
(ROS) that is tested under real-world conditions, successfully replicating researcher tasks
in laboratories with an overall success rate of 87%. This proof-of-concept highlights the
potential of combining computer vision and robotic manipulation in laboratory settings,
facilitating future enhancements such as integrating a mobile base for automating routine
tasks.

1
Introduction
This thesis explores the opportunities for systems that use computer vision and robotic
manipulation to fulfill tasks in life science laboratories. For that, commonly available lab-
oratory hardware is integrated to perform human tasks in a laboratory. More precisely, a
robot arm works with a vision system and artificial intelligence to handle 96-well plates.
This section provides an overview of the broader scientific context surrounding the core
research issue. It then examines the motivation of the project, followed by an explanation
of the research questions. Finally, the organizational structure of this thesis is outlined.
1.1
Problem Formulation and Proposed Solution
The steady integration of autonomous systems into diverse work environments has signif-
icantly influenced the productivity, efficiency, and quality of various industrial operations.
Recent advancements in technology sectors, such as robotics and artificial intelligence,
have boosted the automation of tasks that previously required human intervention. De-
spite the growing incorporation of automated equipment in life science laboratories over
recent years, a substantial amount of manual labor remains necessary. And researchers
often find themselves conducting experimental protocols manually within research facili-
ties.
Automation technologies have demonstrated their reliability and efficiency in maximiz-
ing productivity by replacing human workers performing repetitive and monotonous tasks
with faster and more precise machinery. However, the pharmaceutical industry, due to
the necessity for standardization, stringent regulatory approvals, and extensive research
facilities, has seen a slower pace of technology adoption compared to other sectors. Ac-
cording to a report by McKinsey & Company about the future of the US biopharma industry

How can different tools from robotics work together to achieve an automation pipeline

Computer Vision: Computer vision is used in several steps of the pipeline. As a first step,

2.1
Unmanned Systems in Research Laboratories
Autonomous systems describe systems that operate without the need for human control
by being capable of making decisions on their own [3]. Furthermore, systems that do not
require the physical presence of a human are referred to as unmanned systems [4]. Those
systems are used for various applications, from automation of manufacturing processes
to pharmaceutical research laboratories. Recent advancements in autonomous systems
include the integration of vision systems, sensors, and artificial intelligence.
Automation of laboratories started in the late 1970s and early 1980s in response to evolv-
ing healthcare needs and technological advancements. Emerging initially as a response
to demographic shifts and regulatory changes early laboratory automation systems were
seeking to streamline processes and enhance efficiency in biomedical research and clin-
ical diagnostics. Over time, the field evolved from hardware-driven systems to software-
based approaches [5].
Types of robots used include articulated robots, autonomous mobile robots, and humanoid
robots. Examples of articulated robots can be collaborative robots, Cartesian, and SCARA
robots. While mobile robots are already widely used in industry, the technology of hu-
manoid robots is not commonly found in industry yet. Additionally, automation equipment
like liquid handlers exist. These are specialized robotic systems designed to automate
tasks in laboratories. Regardless of the choice of robot, the goal of integrating automa-
tion systems is to increase the production rate and efficiency of workflows. Additionally,
tedious and repetitive tasks do not have to be executed by humans.
However, in contrast to industrial environments, research laboratories face several differ-
ent challenges that make automation difficult. These reasons range from the variability
of protocols and the need for easy-to-customize systems that can adapt to different re-
quirements to the complexity of experiment setups. For research laboratories, additional
benefits of automation are improved safety and reproducibility of data [6]. The recent de-
velopment of automation systems has led to a laboratory automation interim technology
gap [6]. The term describes the limited choice between either manual labor or expen-
sive automation systems, hindering the widespread adoption of automation in laboratory
settings. Looking specifically at laboratory processes such as for example cell culturing,
while there are high-cost, fully automated systems available, there is a lack of commer-
cially available low-cost intermediate-level automation options [7, 8]. Recently the term of
self-driving laboratories (SDL) has emerged. The term refers to the concept of automated
laboratory workflows where various experimental processes and tasks are performed au-
tonomously without the need for constant human intervention [9]. The right approach to
automation depends on the requirements of the system and available resources.

2.2.2
Vision Based Pick-and-Place Tasks in Research Laboratories
Pick-and-place tasks can either be executed in static environments where the location
of the objects is known and can be hard-coded or the location of the objects needs to
be detected first. Therefore, visual input from a camera is used to determine the pose
and grasping points of the object. Example for the first case are most robots in industry.
Pick-and-place tasks are used in manufacturing for loading and unloading components,
packaging, and the assembly of parts. Requirements for these robots are safety and
precision as well as robustness. Therefore, these robots work under defined conditions
with constrained poses of the objects to pick. In research laboratories where robots need
to deal with changes in the environment, the pose of the object needs to be determined
first.
Therefore, artificial intelligence, vision systems, and other sensors are combined with
robot arms. Common systems integrate an RGB camera with a depth sensor. More
information about vision-based robotic grasping can be found in reviews [18] and [19]. A
short summary of the sub-parts of vision-based pick-and-place operations is presented in
the following.
Object Recognition
Object detection methods from images have evolved significantly over time. Initially, the
2D methods relied on template matching using descriptors like Scale Invariant Feature
Transform (SIFT) [20], as well as machine learning models such as Support Vector Ma-
chines (SVM) [21]. More recently, deep learning methods have gained importance, uti-
lizing deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs). An example is You Only Look Once
(YOLO), a model that outputs bounding boxes and segmentation masks for detected ob-
jects [22].
For 3D object detection, traditional methods focus on extracting local 3D shape descrip-
tors, followed by voting or correspondence-based techniques to identify objects. Addi-
tionally, similar to the 2D detection, deep learning methods exist [23].
When using deep learning models for object detection, large datasets are needed to train
the model. Creating these datasets can be time-consuming and difficult. Therefore, syn-
thetically created datasets can be used for training. Recent advancements in rendering
make it easy to generate scenes with varying conditions like light and background to cre-
ate diverse labeled images on which to train deep learning models. For example, it was
shown that the accuracy of neural networks can significantly be improved by using syn-
thetic training data for the detection of transparent PET bottles [24]. Different methods to
generate synthetic data exist. One method involves synthetically generating 3D objects
and environments [25]. Another method for generating synthetic datasets is the usage
of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to produce images [26]. A third approach
combines real-world data to construct images [27]. The choice of the suitable method
depends on the specific requirements of the detection application.
Object Pose Estimation
Object pose estimation algorithms aim to determine the objectsŌĆÖ six degrees of freedom
(6-DoF) pose. For known objects, geometric models and feature matching algorithms
can be used for pose estimation [28]. These template-based methods for 6D object pose
estimation utilize pre-labeled templates to match observed objects. In 2D image-based
approaches, templates are 2D projections of known 3D models that get aligned using
methods like template matching or correspondence-based techniques. Deep learning

variants that employ neural networks like PointNet show accurate estimation results by
processing point cloud data to estimate object poses [29]. In 3D point cloud-based meth-

on establishing a feature correspondences between observed data and a model. Addi-

votes to pixels or 3D points to determine the objectŌĆÖs pose. For more information, Guan

Transparent objects pose a particular challenge to pose estimation because of their unique
visual characteristics, making it difficult for depth sensors to produce accurate depth infor-
mation due to the reflection and refraction of light. Recent research has focused on using
deep learning to estimate the 3D geometry of transparent objects from single images, en-
hancing robotic manipulation in lab settings [32]. Three deep convolutional networks are
used to infer surface normals and predict the masks of transparent surfaces and occlu-
sion boundaries. Another approach is to use neural radiance fields to grasp transparent
objects [33]. Their ability to model complex scenes with transparency enables accurate
representation and detection of transparent objects. To tackle the complexity of having dif-
ferent classes of lab equipment, TransNet implements category-level transparent object
pose estimation [34]. In the initial stage, TransNet focuses on estimating the category-
level pose of transparent objects by employing localized depth completion. This step is
followed by surface normal estimation in the next stage, which serves to enhance and
fine-tune the accuracy of pose estimation for transparent objects.
Grasping Pose Estimation
After estimating the pose of the object, the gripperŌĆÖs grasp position is determined. Grip-
per types vary widely, from suction devices to dexterous hands used for pick-and-place
tasks. Suction-based grippers are unsuitable for laboratory environments due to their in-
effectiveness with liquids in open containers. A variety of techniques exist for grasp pose
estimation for electrical grippers. One approach is geometric algorithms that analyze ob-
ject geometry and stability criteria to identify suitable grasp points. Deep learning methods
exist that predict the optimal grasp pose from point clouds [35]. Additionally, simulation-
based methods exist where the grasp scenarios are modeled to optimize the grasp points
considering physical constraints. Furthermore, sensor-driven techniques utilize tactile or
force feedback to validate and enhance the reliability of identified grasp points [36].
Finally, over the last years, reinforcement learning methods for pick-and-place tasks were
researched [37]. For reinforcement learning tasks, the optimal action is learned through
trial and error to maximize rewards received for successfully picking up and placing ob-
jects, thereby refining their strategies over time to maximize task completion efficiency.
All in all, this review shows that many different algorithms exist for pick-and-place tasks.
The choice of the optimal algorithm depends on the formulation of the problem, the object
to grasp, the environment and many other factors.
Existing Solutions
Pick-and-place tasks in research laboratories face unique challenges due to the dynamic
and unstructured nature of the environment: The images that get analyzed to detect ob-
jects of interest may be taken under varying conditions. In general, a robot needs to be
capable of navigating in changing environments when deployed in a laboratory setting.
Additionally, robots in research laboratories must collaborate with human researchers,
making safety a crucial concern. Additionally, the lack of standardized processes presents
another challenge. As mentioned before, lab equipment varies widely and is often made
of transparent materials like glass or plastic, further complicating the tasks.

Optical Character Recognition (OCR)
Optical Character Recognition (OCR) describes technology that can convert characters
like letters and numbers on images into machine-encoded text [41]. These systems exist
for different languages, fonts, and styles. Examples of applications range from automatic
number plate recognition, document digitization, and digital archiving to the reading of
labels for industrial workflows.
Different technologies can be found in literature for character recognition. They can mainly
be divided into conventional methods and deep learning methods. Examples of conven-
tional methods are Support Vector Machines (SVM) or Artificial Neural Networks (ANN).
For deep learning methods, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) or Recurrent Neural
Networks (RNN) showed sufficient accuracy [41, 42]. The choice of technology depends

embossed and engraved letters presents additional challenges to the process. This is
because those characters are made of the same material and color as the background.
Hu et al. showed that CNNs can be used to improve the contrast between embossed
characters and background [46]. The findings were verified by detecting embossed letters
on soles. The images were taken under controlled conditions on a conveyor belt with an
additional light source to highlight the difference between characters and background.
Additionally, Zhang et al. showed that You Only Look Once (YOLO) can be used to detect
convex and concave characters with varying distributions in the scene [47]. Experiments
were conducted with a self-made dataset of images from a factory line handling embossed
characters on steel.
2.2.4
Active Vision
The term active vision was first mentioned in 1987 [48]. Active vision describes the con-
cept of manipulating the constraints of observed phenomena to enhance the perceived
outcome [49]. It demonstrated that an active observer could solve basic vision problems
more efficiently than a passive observer by controlling sensor motion. In a seminal work
presented in 1987 by Aloimonos et al., it was shown that controlled sensor motion signifi-
cantly reduces the complexity of classical vision problems [48]. This technique decouples

the process of determining unknowns such as surface orientation, reflection parameters,
and lighting distribution at each point in the image. Additionally, it was shown that ac-
tive vision can help with classification tasks by improving the prediction accuracy [50].
Moreover, active vision can help with depth estimation, object tracking, and control tasks
[51].
Next-Best-View
A subcategory of active vision is the problem of the next-best view. Next-best-view (NBV)
algorithms determine from where the next image of a scene should be taken to capture
the most informative data about an environment or object. The concept is used in au-
tonomous exploration, object recognition, and 3D reconstruction. The primary objectives
of NBV algorithms include maximizing coverage, minimizing uncertainty, and optimizing
the quality of reconstructed models. Therefore, common metrics for evaluating NBV ap-
proaches include information gain, surface area coverage, model completeness, and the
cost of sensor movement [52, 53].
When reviewing next-best-view algorithms, approaches can be divided into model-based
and non-model-based approaches [53].
Model-based tasks focus on pre-determined
sensor placements using CAD or voxel-based models. Voxel-based methods divide the
space into a grid and compute the information gain for each voxel, selecting the viewpoint
with the highest gain.
An example of how a model-based approach is used for inspection with robot manipula-
tion is presented by Tarabanis et al. [54]. A machine vision planner is proposed that finds
the optimal sensor pose, ensuring easier object detection by guaranteeing that the object
is not occluded, resolvable, focused, and entirely within the field of view. The planner
takes the CAD model of the object and a model of the camera as input. The task results
in a non-linearly constrained optimization problem that is expressed as the weighted sum
of objectives. The optimization is solved in a real-world experiment with a quadratic pro-
gramming method. In general, it is shown that most NBV planners can be formulated as
constrained optimization problems [53].
Another example of NBV planning for sensors in an industrial setting is presented by
Ellenrieder et al. [55]. Given a sensor pose, the optimal illumination pose for inspection
of non-Lambertian metallic materials is determined based on an estimate of parameters
for a mathematical model of the reflectance of non-Lambertian material with the help of
a bidirectional reflectance distribution function. The constraints for optimal illumination
are modeled as a convex optimization problem that is solved with a linear programming
method. The method determines the optimal three-dimensional illumination pose.
On the other hand, non-model-based tasks emphasize real-time planning for unknown
environments. The goal is to increase the efficiency and model completeness with fewer
sensing operations. An example of a model-free approach is presented by the work from
Chu and Chung [56]. Their work proposes a camera control method that autonomously
adjusts the camera position and configuration based on changes in the surroundings, en-
suring continuous visual information for a target object. Therefore, a cost function mea-
suring the visibility and manipulability is maximized by integrating real-time feedback to
dynamically update scene information.
A recent example of NBV algorithms is the work proposed by Burusa et al. [57]. The work
focuses on next-best-view algorithms to accurately perceive plant nodes in tomato green-
houses despite occlusion. This research addresses this as a local NBV planning prob-
lem, optimizing camera viewpoints to improve perception accuracy. A gradient-based
NBV planner using differentiable ray sampling to estimate local gradient directions for

periments show that this approach significantly improves 3D reconstruction and position

Recent developments show the integration of learning algorithms like reinforcement learn-
ing [58]. A robot arm holding an object uses the Soft Actor-Critic deep reinforcement

The approach improves object classification by learning the best poses for the robot arm

This section explains the implementation of a vision-based pick-and-place system that

ŌĆó Modularity: This project is a subsystem of a bigger project. Therefore, it is essential

3.1
System Overview
The objective of the system is to have a physical robot perform a pick-and-place task with
96-well plates. For this project, the robot is mounted on a table. To investigate the envi-

camera streams images where a deep learning model detects if a multi-well plate is visi-

image, the grasp point for the plate is estimated via point cloud reconstruction. Addition-

Software
Robot Operating System (ROS)
All components are controlled by the Robot Operating System ROS [66]. ROS is an
open-source middleware platform that was started in 2007. At its core, ROS provides a
flexible framework for building distributed robotic systems. Its modularity makes it easy to
switch out components and reuse subsystems. Additionally, many different packages for
robotics, like trajectory computation and standard algorithms for navigation, exist. ROS
supports digital twins and simulation, which makes development easy.
ROS Master: The ROS Master is a centralized component that facilitates communication
between nodes in a ROS system. It provides services for registering nodes, advertising
and discovering topics and managing message passing. The ROS Master helps to co-
ordinate the interaction between nodes and ensures reliable communication across the
system.
Nodes: ROS has a node-based architecture. A node is a standalone process performing
a specific task. The nodes need to register themselves within the ROS master. To com-
municate with other nodes, they can subscribe to other nodes and publish information
themselves.
Topics: Nodes can publish messages to a topic and other nodes can subscribe to that
topic to receive the message. This publish-subscribe system makes it easy to add or re-
move single nodes. The type of a message has to be fully defined, either by an existing
data type or a custom-built one.
ROS is structured into packages, which makes sharing resources convenient. A pack-
age contains the source code, message definitions, configuration, and launch files. This
project uses ROS Noetic for Ubuntu 20.04 LTS.
Open Source Computer Vision Library (OpenCV)
Open Source Computer Vision Library (OpenCV) is an open-source computer vision li-
brary with real-time support [67]. It supports algorithms for image processing, computer
vision, and machine learning. The support of different programming languages like Python
and C++ makes it easy to use.
Open3D
Open3D is an open-source library for 3D data processing [68]. The library provides tools
for point cloud, meshes, and depth map processing. Therefore, 3D reconstruction or point
cloud registration is made easy.
MoveIt
MoveIt is an open-source motion planning framework within the ROS ecosystem [69].
Motion planning algorithms allow robots to compute a sequence of valid configurations
that transition from an initial state to a desired goal state. Furthermore, MoveIt supports
inverse kinematics solver and collision detection. Inverse kinematics is the calculation
needed to determine the joint configuration of a robot for a given end-effector pose. Ad-
ditionally, the robot can be visualized with Rviz. Rviz provides an interactive 3D demon-
stration of the trajectory and other ROS topics.
NVIDIA Sim
NVIDIA Sim is designed to simulate complex physical phenomena using GPU acceler-
ation. It includes features like PhysX for real-time physics simulation, Flex for fluid dy-
namics and soft body simulation, utilizing CUDA for parallel computing on NVIDIA GPUs.
NVIDIA Sim can be used to create realistic simulations across various domains, from
gaming and virtual reality to engineering and scientific research. Additionally, NVIDIA
Sim Replicator can be integrated, making it easy to render and capture scenes repeat-
edly [70]. Therefore, it is, for example, a suitable software for generating synthetic data.

3.2.1
Data Generation
Due to the absence of an annotated dataset for 96-well plates, creating one manually by
capturing and annotating images is impractical due to its labor-intensive nature. More-
over, manually created datasets are biased towards the specific environment where the
images were captured. Therefore, the dataset to detect 96-well plates should be gener-
ated synthetically. The use of artificially generated images in the training of deep learning
models is a widely adopted and significant technique as explained in Section 2.2.2. For
this project, the object that the model should detect can be made out of different materials
like plastic with different transparencies. Therefore, it is assumed that 2D modeling is
not sufficient. In 3D models, light interactions with transparent surfaces can be simulated
realistically, capturing phenomena such as refraction and reflection. Additionally, CAD
models of plates are openly available and easy to create.
Therefore, a modeling approach is used with NVIDIA Sim and NVIDIA Sim Replicator
from the NVIDIA Omniverse. By generating the data synthetically, precise control over
variability in the data is achieved. Additionally, the annotation effort is minimized, and the
issue of biases present in real-world datasets is mitigated. The approach of using syn-
thetic data enhances model robustness and optimizes dataset quality for improved per-
formance in diverse scenarios. Inspiration was taken from other projects that implement
a synthetic data generation pipeline to train deep learning models [71]. The contribution
of this project is to implement a solution that generates synthetic data with 96-well plates
tailored to training a detection model for segmenting plates in research laboratories.

are either embossed or engraved on the plate and text is visible on the other side of the

3.2.2
Plate Detection Model
After setting up the synthetic and real-world dataset, a YOLOv8 model is trained. You
Only Look Once (YOLO) is a real-time instance segmentation and object detection and
recognition model [22]. The model was created by Ultralytics. An API is provided that
makes it easy to train and test the model with different settings. Additionally, data aug-
mentation can be applied during the training pipeline. Because of its easy usage of data
augmentation and the availability of pre-trained models, it accommodates the challenge
of having sparse datasets. The concept of using pre-trained models is called transfer
learning. Instead of using a model with randomly initialized weights, the model is already
trained on different datasets. The learned features can then be transferred to the sparse
dataset.
The model is divided into three main components: the backbone, neck, and head. For
more information, please refer to Section A.1.1. In summary, the last layer produces the
modelŌĆÖs outputs, including bounding boxes, segmentation masks and confidence scores.
All in all, YOLO fits the requirements of the detection algorithm by allowing real-time pro-
cessing. Additionally, it is capable of handling sparse datasets by augmenting data and
enabling the availability of pre-trained models. Therefore, it is assumed that the model can
adapt to different light conditions and backgrounds and is suitable for detecting opaque,
transparent and translucent 96-well plates. The contribution of this project is to train a
pre-trained YOLO model with help of the YOLO API on a self-created dataset to detect
96-well plates.
For detecting multi-well plates on images, the model returns a bounding box, segmenta-
tion mask and the confidence of the detection. To achieve this, a YOLOv8 nano segmen-
tation model is chosen to be trained for 100 epochs with early stopping of patience 10.
Early stopping is a regularization technique used during the training phase. It prevents
the model from overfitting, which means that the model memorizes the dataset too well
and, therefore, does not generalize enough to perform well on unseen data. It can be
detected when the performance on the validation set degrades while it still improves on
the training set. Patience determines after how many epochs of no improvement of the
validation accuracy the training is stopped.
Furthermore, augmentation is applied to the training data. The YOLOv8 default data aug-
mentation is used. In summary, color and light augmentation, geometric augmentation,
and scene augmentations are applied. Color augmentation is, for example, a change of
hue or saturation of the image. This type of augmentation simulation helps the model to
generalize even under varying light conditions. Geometric augmentation is, for example,
rotation, translation, and flipping. This helps the model to work under different viewing
perspectives or if the object is partially occluded. Scene augmentation describes, for ex-

ample, the combination of images to create a new scene or random erasing of regions

If a plate is detected in the scene, the robot arm picks up the plate and places it in a

3.3.1
Picking
When designing the picking algorithm, several assumptions were made based on the
laboratory environment and the handling of plates. The algorithm is tailored to the specific
conditions found in laboratories. First, it is assumed that other objects do not cover plates.
This is because standard laboratory practice requires that the workspace is kept clean
to avoid contamination, which could compromise the integrity of experiments or tests.
Secondly, it is generally assumed that plates are laid flat on the table. Additionally, the
plates are not moving.
The project specifically focuses on handling 96-well plates. These plates have a fixed
width and length, but their height and the appearance of their sides can vary. Only con-
sidering plates, simplifies the pick-and-place operation since the grasp pose is directly
determined by the plate pose. Therefore, no grasp pose estimation algorithm has to be
28
Robot Perception and Manipulation in Life Science Laboratories
==PAGE:43==
added. For this proof-of-concept project, the plate is always grasped on the long side.
Considering all these points ensures that the picking algorithm is robust and adaptable to
the typical conditions encountered in a laboratory setting.
From these assumptions, the following requirements can be derived:
ŌĆó Robustness: The algorithm should work in different laboratories independent of
where the laboratory is located or what light conditions are present. Still, it is as-
sumed that a light source is present.
ŌĆó Accuracy: For the system to be robust, the pick-and-place operation must be exe-
cuted with sufficient accuracy to ensure that other equipment can continue to use
the plate without interruption.
ŌĆó Adaptability to unseen plates: The algorithm should be able to generalize and pick
up new plates it has not seen before. Therefore, an algorithm should be chosen
where it is not required to have a database of possible plates.
The input to the picking algorithm is the RGB image plus the depth map and the seg-
mentation mask of the detected plate as described in Section 3.2. First, the segmentation
mask is used to segment the RGB and depth data. Therefore, a binary mask is generated
from the polygon coordinates. The segmented RGB image and depth data are then used
to construct a point cloud. After outliers are removed, the center of the plate and its axes
are estimated.
First, an approach is used where the surface of the plate is detected. Recognizing the
largest surface area is assumed to be robust, particularly in the context of transparent ma-
terials where noise and outliers pose significant challenges. That is because transparent
objects allow light to pass through, causing refraction and distorting depth measurements.
Transparent objects also lack sufficient surface reflections needed for accurate depth cal-
culation. Additionally, background interference and material properties can disrupt accu-
rate depth detection. Furthermore, it assumed that the presence of 96 small wells on the
plate causes additional noise. Therefore, focusing on the largest surface minimizes the
impact of individual noisy points or small clusters of outliers. Additionally, it makes the
algorithm easy to adapt to plate variability. Plates may vary in height and orientation, but
the largest surface detection method can adapt to these variations.
Second, as discussed in Section 2.2.2, registration is a widely used algorithm for grasp
point estimation if the object to grasp is known. Since all multi-well plates have approxi-
mately the same width and length, the algorithm is considered to be suitable for the pose
estimation of 96-well plates. First, a template of a plate to match the sensed plate is cre-
ated. Since the height of the plates varies, the template represents the top part of the
plate. The goal is that, in the end, the template point cloud is aligned with the sensed
one such that the grasp point can directly be determined by the registered point cloud. A
process is used where the template point cloud is first coarsely aligned with the sensed
point cloud, and then the alignment is refined.
As shown, surface detection and registration are known methods for pose estimation of
objects. The project contributes by implementing the algorithms for grasp point estimation
of opaque, transparent, and translucent 96-well plates. The implementation is done with
PythonŌĆÖs Numpy and Open3D library, where the algorithms are already available.
For the first approach, the Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC) algorithm is used [73].
The algorithm is an iterative method to estimate model parameters in the presence of
outliers. A random subset of the data points is sampled and fitted to a model. After that,

Optical Character Recognition (OCR)
Different methods for optical character recognition exist. Additionally, diverse challenges
are encountered in the context of optical character recognition on 96-well plates since the
letters can be engraved, embossed, or printed.
One challenge is contrast. When images are taken, the characters appear differently
depending on various factors, such as lighting conditions, the material of the plate, and the
appearance of the engraving or embossing. This variability in appearance of characters
can affect the accuracy of optical character recognition.
Another significant challenge is visibility. The embossed and especially the engraved
characters are not always visible to the camera. The visibility depends on the angle of
light shining on the surface and the angle of the camera. An example of the visibility of
engraved characters can be seen in Figure 3.25. The images are taken under different
camera poses. The angle of the camera around the x-axis is increased steadily. As can
be seen, there exists camera poses where the characters are visible on the plate and
poses where no indication of characters is present.
Figure 3.25: Visibility of engraved characters under changing camera pose.
The multi-well plates can be divided into three categories: Opaque, translucent, and trans-
parent. Figure 3.26 shows how light interacts differently on different materials. When light
shines on an opaque surface, it is mostly absorbed or reflected. The reflected light does
not pass through the material. This type of surface is advantageous for optical character
recognition, providing high contrast for engraved or embossed characters, making them
easier to detect and read.
In the case of translucent surfaces, light partially passes through while being scattered in
various directions. While some light is reflected, the scattering can reduce the contrast of
the engraved and embossed characters, making them more challenging to detect. How-
ever, the characters can still be visible under optimal lighting conditions where the angle
and intensity of light are controlled.

Existing Methods
Studies showed that tesseract and easyOCR show accurate results for license plate
recognition [77]. It is hypothesized that the problem of license plate recognition can be
somehow adapted to the problem of character recognition on multi-well plates. Conse-
quently, tesseract and easyOCR are tested in this thesis as an example of existing OCR
models [44, 45].
Tesseract comes with different built-in configurations. After trying out different configura-
tions, it is chosen to detect as much text as possible in no particular order. Additionally,
tesseract suggests different preprocessing steps for the image to improve the detection.
For example, the text should be horizontally aligned what is already handled by the pre-
processing step. Furthermore, the model works better on binarized images with minimum
noise. Therefore, edge detection is applied with morphological operations to achieve seg-
mentation of the characters. As discussed, the characters in the images are presented
in different light conditions and different contrasts between the background and charac-
ters. Therefore, there was no sufficient method found to reliably binarize the images
while ensuing visibility of the characters. Therefore, tesseract is evaluated on the prepro-
cessed images assuming it would not perform optimaly, and another existing OCR model
is tested.

CNN
Since the problem can be seen as a constrained problem where only a subset of char-
acters is detected, it is evaluated how well a self-trained CNN performs. The CNN han-
dles the problem as a classification task between 9 different classes: A to H and no ŌłÆ
characters. This was chosen over binary classification because the model is expected to
handle ambiguity better. When training the model to classify characters or noŌłÆcharacters,
it could easily happen that the model interprets the text on the side of the plate where
no characters are present as characters. While the use of CNNs for image classifica-
tion is well-established [46], this projectŌĆÖs contribution lies in training a CNN on a self-
made dataset tailored for character recognition on 96-well plates. The CNN architecture,
adapted from a previous study for 34759 Perception for Autonomous Systems in Spring
23, achieved a high accuracy of 98.7% on the MNIST dataset, demonstrating its potential
for this project.
The CNN architecture is composed of three convolutional layers followed by fully con-
nected layers. The three convolutional layers are responsible for extracting the first low-
level features like edges, the second for detecting more complex patterns, and the third
for capturing more complex features.
To reduce the spatial dimension of the feature
maps, max-pooling is applied after each convolutional layer. After these layers, two fully
connected layers are applied, introducing non-linearity through the Rectified Linear Unit
(ReLU) activation function. After that, dropout is applied to prevent the model from over-
fitting. The final layer has nine output neurons corresponding to the nine classes. It
produces the class scores for classification. The architecture of the model makes it com-
putationally efficient while choosing enough layers to learn relevant features.
To train and test the model a different dataset is recorded. One recording was done in the
morning and one in the evening to cover varying light conditions. After the preprocessing,
every image is cut into 8 subimages showing the characters A to H. For every image, an
augmented image is generated to have more variability in the dataset.
The chosen augmentation techniques include random brightness adjustment for handling
lighting variations, random hue saturation value adjustment to adapt to color changes,
random Gaussian noise addition for handling noisy images, random shadow simulation for
realistic lighting conditions, and image blur to prevent reliance on specific sharp features.
While data augmentation helps increase variability, it cannot fully replicate the diversity
and complexity of real-world conditions. Additionally, the dataset is limited to a few plates.
This limited variety may not encompass the full range of potential variations in plate de-
signs, textures, or materials, potentially leading to poor generalization to plates with dif-
ferent characteristics. All in all, it is assumed that a limitation of the model is its high
dependency on the training dataset.
In total, the model is trained on 5870 images. The exact number of images per character
varies since some of the images were sorted out due to non-visibility of the characters on
the image. In summary, 1640 images for no character are present and around 530 images
per character.

Template Matching
Template matching describes a method where a smaller template image is searched in a
larger image. The template is compared to the image while sliding it over the larger image.
To compare the template to the larger image, normalized cross-correlation is used what
means that first the similarity between template and image are measured through cross-
correlation and second the similarity is normalized. By normalizing the cross-correlation,
this method can effectively handle variations in the contrast between the template and the
input image, ensuring that the matching process is more robust to variations in lighting
and contrast.
A limitation of template matching applying normalized cross-correlation is the sensitivity
to noise. High levels of noise can affect the correlation results, leading to False Positives
or reduced accuracy in matching.
Another limitation is that the method assumes that the template and the matching regions
in the larger image are of the same size and orientation. It does not inherently handle
rotations or scaling. This limitation can be considered irrelevant to this project since the
characters have a known location and orientation. Furthermore, the letters are of known
size.
Another limitation is that if the character appearance changes drastically by, for example,
choosing an italic font, the template matching will fail. This limitation is considered to be
not crucial for the project since a big change like this is not expected from the vendor.
First, the templates are set up. To avoid introducing bias, the characters are generated
in a LibreOffice document and then captured via screenshots. Different fonts that look
similar to the font on the plates are tested. The best performing one Lato Heavy with font

Additionally, more preprocessing steps are added on top of the input image to improve
template matching: First the image is resized, then, if the mean brightness of the image

of the pixel intensities in the input image. Then, the cumulative distribution function is

Integration of OCR and Active Vision
When the optical character recognition algorithm is integrated into the hardware, three
different approaches are tested. The three approaches are displayed in Figure 3.31.
First, the characters are recognized under static conditions. A light source is permanently
installed so that the characters are always visible from a specific camera pose if the plate
is placed at a defined location. Considering the whole system with a mobile robot, the
robot would move around to detect the plate, pick it up, and place it onto the mobile base.
On the mobile base, the light is installed with a defined place for the plate. Next, the
correct rotation is determined, and the mobile base can drive to the delivery location.
Second, the project aims to work independently in unconstrained environments. There-
fore, the plate is detected, and from the 3D pose estimation of the plate, a grid of poses
around the plate is sampled. The camera visits the poses until characters are detected
or the algorithm terminates.
Third, similar to the second approach, the plate is detected. Instead of sampling camera
poses, a more advanced algorithm chooses the next-best camera pose until characters
are detected or a maximum number of poses is visited. As previously discussed in Sec-
tion 2.2.4, various algorithms exist for determining the optimal next pose based on CAD
or 3D models of an object [54, 53]. Additionally, research has been conducted to find
the best illumination pose to improve material visibility [55]. These methods rely on the
physical description and symmetries of the objects. However, in the context of enhanc-

individual components can be easily replaced or modified as needed. For instance, the
main controller for character recognition can be easily switched between using static light
conditions and gradient ascent. Returning to the initial system requirements, this modu-
larity simplifies the integration of a mobile robot into the ROS framework, allowing for a
more flexible and scalable system architecture.
Regarding robustness, the subsystems are designed to perform reliably under varying
conditions, such as different laboratory environments or light conditions. The system is
also capable of handling different plate types. However, integrating all subsystems into
a cohesive whole system presents significant challenges due to error propagation. To
mitigate this, if a subsystem fails, the robot is programmed to return to its initial position
and retry the task. This approach enhances the overall reliability and usability of the
system in real-world scenarios. The results for testing of the full system are presented in
Section 4.4.
Lastly, simplicity is a key consideration in the design of the system. Only components
that are commonly available in automation laboratories are used, avoiding the need for
specialized equipment. This ensures that the system is more accessible for replication
and future development.

5
Discussion and Outlook
In this chapter, the research questions are discussed, and a potential outlook for future
work is given.
All in all, this thesis contributes to laboratory automation by demonstrating the develop-
ment of a robust 96-well plate detection model trained primarily on synthetic data, the
feasibility of automated pick-and-place operations for these plates in real laboratory con-
ditions, and a novel method integrating character recognition to determine plate rotation,
challenging how vision systems and robot manipulation can work together in a real-world
setting. These achievements help to reduce manual tasks and enhance productivity in
life science laboratories through effective integration of automation technologies by using
easily available hardware.
How can different tools from robotics work together to achieve an automation pipeline
where a robot arm picks and places randomly placed plates?
The research question on how different tools from robotics can work together to achieve
an automation pipeline where a robot arm picks and places randomly placed plates is
addressed by developing a proof-of-concept prototype. This prototype demonstrates the
systemŌĆÖs capability to handle a variety of 96-well plates while accurately determining their
correct rotation. The system includes a collaborative robot arm, an RGB-D camera, and
both integrated and external ring lights. These components are configured to operate
seamlessly together through the Robot Operating System (ROS), resulting in an easily
extendable system.
A significant challenge in this project was ensuring the seamless communication and in-
tegration of the computer vision, deep learning, and motion planning components. This
integration was achieved through extensive testing, calibration, and fine-tuning within the
ROS framework. Precise calibration of the camera and robot arm was essential to ensure
accurate detection and placement.
The system was tested under real-world conditions and achieved an overall accuracy of
87%. Key metrics such as grasping accuracy for pick-and-place operations and detection
accuracy for the YOLO model were used to evaluate the performance. A YOLO model,
trained mainly on synthetic data, successfully detected 70 out of 70 plates during real-
world testing. The grasp point for the pick-and-place operation was estimated through
point cloud registration, achieving an accuracy of 92%, with plates being placed within
┬▒2mm. Motion planning, combined with the robotŌĆÖs force feedback, enabled the smooth
placement of the plates without spilling any liquid. During testing, no fluid was spilled. Ad-
ditionally, optical character recognition (OCR) was integrated into the system to determine
the correct rotation of the plates without requiring additional expensive hardware, relying
only on the existing camera setup and an extra light source. However, it is important to
emphasize that the system was evaluated on a subset of possible scenarios, although
variance in the testing scenarios was introduced, for example, by using different types of
plates.
Future research will focus on improving motion planning so the robot arm can react to oc-
clusion maps from the RGB-D camera to avoid obstacles and plan around them. Existing
algorithms can be integrated with ROS to address this limitation, as the current system
does not plan around the occlusion map despite having the capability to sense it [78].
80
Robot Perception and Manipulation in Life Science Laboratories
==PAGE:95==
Especially when the system is working alongside humans, reactive obstacle avoidance
will be crucial to ensure safety in the laboratory.
Additionally, the grasp point estimation pipeline can be further refined. A limitation of
the current implementation is that the system was not extensively tested with partially
occluded plates. It is assumed that if the plate is heavily occluded, the detection and the
grasp point estimation will fail. Therefore, training of the YOLO model with synthetically
created images of partially occluded plates can be done. Then, the registration for not
fully reconstructed point clouds can be tested.
The current results indicate that a noisy point cloud affects the accuracy of plate detection
by the depth sensor. One solution is to use multiple sensor measurements to reconstruct
the point cloud rather than relying on a single shot. Deep learning methods can also be
employed for the accurate reconstruction of transparent and translucent plates [32]. Tech-
nologies like TransNet can improve depth completion and 3D geometry reconstruction of
transparent objects [34]. This enhancement will be particularly valuable if the system is
extended to handle more types of plates or other lab equipment in the future. Another ap-
proach could involve using more accurate depth-sensing technologies, such as LiDARs
or time-of-flight cameras.
For generalizing the system to different types of plates, the force threshold for placing
plates should be adaptive based on the plateŌĆÖs weight. The sensor can estimate this
threshold during the pick-up process. Although initial testing shows that the detection and
grasp point estimation works with 96-deep-well plates, more extensive testing is needed
for different types of plates. A potential limitation of the current implementation is that the
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) might fail on 6-well plates due to the larger wells
leading to incorrect plane estimation.
All in all, it should be mentioned that assumptions were made about the plates and labo-
ratory environment. The assumptions are extensively discussed in the methodology part
of the thesis. Examples include the appearance of the plate, the background of the labo-
ratory benches, and light conditions in the laboratories. If these assumptions do not hold
anymore, the system needs to be reevaluated and extended.
Future work includes integrating the system with a mobile base, which will be the next step
toward testing the systemŌĆÖs collaboration with humans in a laboratory setting. User feed-
back will be essential to identify additional system requirements. Evaluating the safety of
the system will be crucial, especially considering the mobile base with a manipulator, to
ensure it operates safely around researchers in the lab.
How can computer vision techniques be utilized to determine the rotation of a plate
autonomously?
Accurate determination of multi-well plate rotation is crucial to avoid errors in sample han-
dling and data acquisition. The appearance of 96-well plates depends on the manufac-
turer. Therefore, the characters on the side of the plate are used to determine the correct
rotation of the plate. However, challenges arise from the diverse appearance of these
characters and their visibility issues caused by light interactions with the platesŌĆÖ transpar-
ent and translucent materials.
The thesis first investigates three different methods for optical character recognition (OCR).
The first method investigates existing models for OCR that showed low accuracy and im-
practical processing times for real-world applications. Next, a self-trained CNN is evalu-
ated, but it has been found to be sensitive to varying lighting conditions, which change
the appearance of the characters. Last, template matching is evaluated, showing high
Robot Perception and Manipulation in Life Science Laboratories

==PAGE:96==
accuracy in character recognition and a low rate of False Positives.
Still, choosing template matching as the OCR method poses limitations to the system. If
the appearance of the characters changes significantly, template matching fails. For ex-
ample, its current implementation does not generalize to 384-well plates that have char-
acters from A to P that are significantly smaller than the characters A to H on the 96-well
plates. To enhance generalization across different plates, as future work either a plate
classifier that selects the templates to match could be included or different deep learning
methods could be used. For example, during implementation, it was seen that models
like Google Lens recognized the characters with high accuracy. Its API would make it
easy to integrate, but it is not freely available [43].
Next, the OCR is integrated into the system to investigate a method with a stationary
light source and two different active vision methods. First, OCR is evaluated under the
stationary light source, which achieves an accuracy of 97% of correctly detected rotations.
This approach is robust under different light conditions but requires a fixed space for the
additional light source.
Therefore, active vision algorithms were assessed. First, a fixed sampled grid of camera
poses was used. This approach has limitations due to its fixed poses relative to the plate,
potentially missing the poses where characters are visible. Moreover, the algorithmŌĆÖs long
execution time and dependency on the plateŌĆÖs visibility pose further challenges, especially
in cluttered environments where camera poses may be obstructed or characters occluded.
As a next step, an objective function is formulated that gives a continuous estimation of
the visibility of characters. The first testing showed that the objective function gives valid
estimations. Next, an optimization algorithm is used to maximize the objective function
relative to the camera pose. Results show that in future work a different optimization
approach should be used. Gradient ascent suffers from convergence to local maxima
and its non-exploratory behavior, as seen in the results of this project. The function that
models the objectives functions values gives a good first overview but is not suited for
online learning and should therefore be exchanged by a regression model or a different
machine learning model.
Another challenge, even with alternative optimization techniques, is that adapting next-
best-view algorithms to solve character visibility in a 2D scenario is not straightforward
since most of the algorithms rely on a 3D reconstruction of the scene where, in simula-
tion, the next-best-view can be determined. The approach to generating and constantly
updating a model of the scene was shown to be time-consuming, especially considering
the high-dimensional space of the camera pose. Therefore, future work should inves-
tigate how feasible a simulation of the problem is, particularly considering how plates,
characters, and lighting interact. If a simulation of the problem is feasible, reinforcement
learning and simulation-based optimization approaches can be considered. Additionally,
that helps resolve the problem of the method, taking additional time to record values to
get an initial model of the scene.
In summary, exploring the adaptation of active vision algorithms to the novel challenge of
determining optimal camera poses for character visibility on plates in real-world settings
is valuable. However, it is shown that these algorithms are not yet ready for practical use.
At present, using a stationary light source is considered more reliable based on current
research findings.

Conclusion
This thesis explores the integration of computer vision and robotic manipulation to perform
pick-and-place tasks involving 96-well plates in life science laboratories. The proposed
system serves as a subsystem with the potential for future integration with a mobile base.
The primary goal of this system is to assist researchers in transferring plates with sam-
ples from the lab desk to automation equipment such as liquid handlers. This system
effectively bridges the gap between the unstructured, human-centric environments of lab-
oratory workspace and the structured settings of automated equipment. By facilitating this
transition, the system supports the evolution from human-driven laboratories to collabo-
rative environments where humans and automation work together, giving the potential to
enhance efficiency and productivity for researchers.
The highlights of this thesis include several key achievements. First, the detection and
segmentation of plates in laboratory environments were successfully trained on primarily
synthetic data generated for this project, achieving an accuracy of 100% when integrated
into the system. Testing demonstrated that the model, trained on synthetic data, can
generalize effectively to unseen plates and environments.
Second, the thesis compares two different algorithms for grasping plates, highlighting the
challenges of detecting plates made from various materials in real-world environments.
The system achieved an accuracy of 86% in placing plates within a boundary of ┬▒1 mm
using registration. Additionally, the integrated force sensor of the UR robot arm proved to
be highly effective for the smooth placing of 96-well plates where the maximum possible
weight is known.
Third, the thesis presents a novel approach to detecting embossed, engraved, and printed
characters on plates. After evaluating suitable methods for optical character recognition
(OCR), the integration of the detection method was tested under stationary conditions and
with active vision. The OCR achieved an accuracy of 97% under a stationary light source.
The thesis also explores the integration of active vision in real-world lab environments,
demonstrating both its advantages and limitations. A grid of sampled points showed that
the approach can miss the camera pose that makes engraved characters visible under
certain light conditions. Additionally, an objective function was developed to estimate
the visibility of engraved characters, and an optimization algorithm was used to find the
maximum visibility for these characters.
Additionally, the practical aspect of the project involved integrating the mentioned tech-
nologies and implementing them on a real robot arm in a laboratory setting. The system
reached a Technology Readiness Level (TRL) of 6, indicating validation of performance
in an environment that simulates real-world conditions, though not yet fully in the ac-
tual operational environment. Ensuring all components worked together robustly within a
framework that is easy to extend and modify is another achievement of the project.
In conclusion, this project demonstrates the technical feasibility of using existing automa-
tion equipment for pick-and-place operations in laboratory settings. It also showcases the
potential of integrating vision systems with manipulators to reduce manual intervention,
enhance operational efficiency, and improve overall productivity in life science labora-
tories. By exploring the application of computer vision algorithms in real-life scenarios
with real hardware, this thesis lays the groundwork for future advancements in laboratory
automation.

A.1.1
You Only Look Once (YOLO)
The core concept of YOLO involves dividing the input image into a grid and predicting
bounding boxes and class probabilities for each cell within that grid. The model is divided
into three main components: the backbone, neck, and head. The backbone extracts
features from the image, like edges and textures. Following the backbone, the neck com-
ponent further refines the extracted features. This process involves various operations
such as feature fusion, spatial pooling, and dimensionality reduction. The neck compo-
nent, therefore, improves the detection accuracy by enhancing the feature representation
and incorporating contextual information. The head is the final segment of the network
responsible for producing the modelŌĆÖs outputs, including bounding boxes and confidence
scores for object detection.
To train the model, the API requires a specific format of the dataset For the YOLO format,
every directory has two sub-folders, one for images and one for labels [22]. The labels
are stored in text files where every single image has its text file. The structure is describes
as
/

...

image001.txt
...

The data.yaml file serves as a configuration file for YOLO and tells the YOLO API where
to find the test, validation, and train folders, as well as how many classes are used with
the naming of the classes.
The label files are structured as text files. The image and label are matched through a
unique name. For object detection with bounding boxes, every row represents a new
label. A row consists of
[class, xcenter, ycenter, width, height]
(A.1)
where all values are scaled by the image width and height. For segmentation masks,
YOLO uses
[class, x1, y1, x2, y2, ..., xn, yn]
(A.2)
as a format where xi and yi represent the bounding coordinates of the segmentation mask.
