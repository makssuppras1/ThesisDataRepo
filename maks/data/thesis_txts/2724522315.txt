Abstract
Worldwide 46% of all habitable land is used for agriculture and 26% of all CO2 equivalent emissions
come from the food sector. With this impact, the food sector is a great contributor to the ongoing climate-
and biodiversity crisis that humanity is facing. An effective solution to this is a shift towards a more plant-
based diet. To make this transition easier, new meat alternatives (MAs) are needed. Wheat bran (WB) is
one of the most abundant sidestreams worldwide. Annually, 130M tons are produced with a nutritional
potential to cover 11% of the world’s protein intake, and maybe this potential could be used for MAs. This
thesis will try to answer how it can be feasible to use WB in MAs both from a technical and commercial
perspective. This is done through a literature review, where the characteristics of WB and MAs are
mapped. On the basis of this, ideas are generated to find market opportunities for WB in MAs. The biggest
potential is found to be in protein ingredients made from WB as protein is by far the most dominating
nutrient in MAs on the market. One of the suggested ideas for this is to increase the protein content of
WB by fermentation supplemented with nitrogen. The technical feasibility of this is investigated through
experiments, where WB is fermented with Rhizopus sp. at different nitrogen (ammonium chloride) levels
and fermentation times. The content and composition of total and free amino acids are investigated by
an LC-MC analysis. The protein content didn’t increase after 48 hours of fermentation at any of the
nitrogen levels. However, the amino acid profile improved in regard to the content of essential amino
acids. The flavor was greatly affected by the fermentation, which was proven by a GC-MS analysis that
measured volatile compounds in the samples. In the last part of the thesis, the business potential of a WB
protein concentrate (WBPC) was evaluated following a method from the book ’Where to Play’. From
this analysis, a startup or company solely producing and selling WBPC can’t be suggested. However, a
biorefinery concept seems to have potential, where WBPC is one out of multiple high-value compounds
extracted. This would however require further investigation. On the basis of the work done in this thesis,
wheat bran in meat alternatives wasn’t proven to be feasible.

Introduction
1.1
Problem and motivation
Worldwide 46% of all habitable land is used for agriculture and 26% of all CO2 equivalent (CO2e)
emissions come from the food sector [1]. This contributes significantly to the ongoing climate- and
biodiversity crisis that humanity is facing. But by changing what we eat these numbers can be lowered
significantly. A powerful way to do this is to eat less meat and more plant-based. The difference between
the environmental impact of two protein sources becomes very clear when we compare dried chickpeas,
a common plant-based protein source, to minced beef, a widely consumed animal protein source. In this
comparison, minced beef emits 16 times more CO2e and takes up 34 times more land per kg of product
produced than dried chickpeas. This means that producing minced beef has a much larger impact on the
environment compared to dried chickpeas[2]. So why don’t we just cut the meat? Meat has a fundamental
role in many food cultures and many people do not have the cooking skills or knowledge of how to eat
more plant-based[3]. To make this transition easier for people, new plant-based products and ingredients
are needed. These have to be convincing in terms of flavor, price, nutrition, and texture among others.
Combined with fully utilizing the food recourses we already have, we could produce more food from
less land. Luckily, new technologies are allowing us to utilize byproducts as food that once was deemed
as feed[4]. Wheat bran, a by-product from flour production, is one of the most abundant sidestreams of
the food sector with a nutritional potential to cover 11% of the world’s protein demand[Appendix A.1].
Currently, it’s mainly used as feed for animals, but maybe it could be used in meat alternatives?
1.2
Research Questions
This thesis will try to answer:
• How can it be feasible to use wheat bran in meat alternatives both from a technical and commercial
perspective?
This will be done through a feasibility study where this is further investigated. The main focus will be
on the Danish market but parallels will be drawn to other markets. To help answer this question, three
research questions are made, representing the different parts of the thesis:
1. What are the main characteristics of meat alternatives and wheat bran?
2. How is it most optimal to use wheat bran in meat alternatives?
3. What is the business potential for the most promising opportunity found in research question 2?

Literature review
This chapter investigates and presents knowledge and research about our food system, meat alternatives,
and wheat bran by reviewing state-of-the-art scientific literature.
2.1
A more sustainable food sector
The global temperature is rising, leading to a rapidly changing climate, which is mainly caused by CO2e
emissions from human activities[5]. This is the result of a world population of around 8 billion people
that needs housing, clothing, transportation, and most vital of all water and food[6], which has made
the food sector account for 26% of all CO2e emissions[1]. We share our world with 42.8B livestock[7],
and these also have many of the same needs as humans in order to thrive and grow, such as water and
feed. Sometimes, the line between food and feed is very clear, but in many cases these two categories
are fundamentally indistinguishable, meaning that animals are fed with food so they can turn into our
food[8]. From a resource efficiency perspective, this can be a very poor utilization of the food, perfectly
described by the energy and protein feed-to-food conversion efficiencies. For every 100 calories a beef
cow consumes, only 3% are converted into edible products from the animal. It’s the same number for
protein, while for poultry the numbers are 13% and 21% [9]. Therefore, if a farm animal is fed something
that could as well be food, it would be way more efficient for humans to eat it. This wouldn’t be a problem
if there were an infinite amount of resources and arable land on our planet, but that’s not the case. Of
the 106M km2 labeled as habitable land, 46% is used for agriculture. While livestock takes up 77% of
agricultural land for feed production and grazing, it only accounts for 18% of the global calorie supply
and 37% of the global protein supply as seen in Figure 2.1[1].

used for this [1]

sidestreams from food production, such as wheat bran and whey[8]. The last mentioned used to be a

2.2
Meat alternatives
Meat alternatives go under many names; meat analogs, meat substitutes, mock meats, meatless meats, and
many more. The meat alternative category can be divided into two subcategories: Those products trying
to look and taste like meat and those that can just replace meat without looking or tasting like it. The first
mentioned is often referred to as meat analogs and a widely used technology to make them is through low
or high-moisture extrusion, which can give plant proteins the fibrous texture known from meat. Closely
related to the category of meat alternatives is alternative proteins, which is used to describe nonanimal
protein used to make meat alternatives. These proteins can be more traditional plant-based proteins such
as soy or wheat or newer plant-based proteins such as rapeseed protein, but they can also be made from
algae or microbial biomass, such as fungi and yeast[13]. Protein ingredients for meat alternatives are
usually divided into three main categories depending on the protein content: flour (<60%), concentrate
(60%-90%), and isolate (>90%), either used in powder form or as input for extrusion. Low moisture
extrusion is typically us to produce a texturized vegetable protein (TVP), which is a dry but rehydratable
product that is used as an ingredient in meat analogs such as patties, sausages, and minced. High moisture
extrusion uses the same proteins but the meat-like product comes straight from the machine such as plant-
based strips. Many plant proteins have unwanted flavors, which partly can be removed by extrusion. The
companies Beyond Meat and Impossible Foods are among the most well-known companies using the
power of extrusion to create meat analogs[14].
The other mentioned category, the meat alternatives that don’t mimic meat, includes products such as
tofu, tempeh, and seitan but legumes can also be used as a protein-rich alternative to meat[15]. In tofu,
the texture comes from the bond of coagulating soybean proteins[16]. In tempeh, a fungus is grown
mainly on soybeans, and the mycelium web of the fungi will bind the beans together and give a new
texture[17].
2.2.1
Nutritional equality
The main nutritional attribute of meat is protein, therefore protein is often the dominant macronutrient
in meat alternatives. Besides being a source of protein, meat is also a valuable source of iron, vitamin
B12 as well as other B complex vitamins, zinc, selenium, and phosphorus[18]. The protein in meat is
considered to be of high biological value meaning that it both contains a high amount of essential amino
acids and that the protein has a high digestibility score. This can be expressed by the PDCAAS score.
The PDCAAS score can maximum be 1, which is the case for egg protein, and 0.92 and 0.91 for beef
and soy protein respectively[19]. Meat intake often comes with a high amount of saturated fat which
is linked to a higher risk of cardiovascular disease. However, this varies a lot depending on the type of
meat and cut[18]. Many meat alternatives on the market cover both the macro- and micronutrients of
meat. Most plant-based meat alternatives(PBMA’s) are even a healthier choice than traditional meat due
to their higher fiber content and lower levels of saturated fat. For salt, PBMA’s are healthy alternatives
to processed meat but have more salt than unprocessed meat. In general plant-based meat have higher
levels of iron compared to meat but vitamin B12 varies depending on the product category. However,
the bioavailability of iron and the protein quality are not labeled on the package of PBMA’s but will be

2.2.2
Danes and meat alternatives
The yearly sales of meat alternatives in Denmark per capita have doubled five times from 2006 to 2020,
from 0.1kg to 0.5kg. For processed meat, these numbers are 21kg and 20kg, and for fresh meat, 46kg and
43kg. This indicates that even though sales of meat alternatives are rapidly rising, the numbers are still
very small compared to meat sales. Many studies point out that consumers want to eat more plant-based
and sustainable, but their dietary habit doesn’t seem to be changing significantly changing in the period
above[21]. This is supported by the yearly survey from Madkulturen, where 69% of Danes answered that
they are willing to change their behavior and habits for the sake of climate, environment, or sustainability.
However, only 19% of Danes agree with striving to cook food without or with very little meat for the sake
of sustainability. Meat is the main ingredient in many of the most widespread dishes in Denmark, and
this might be why Danes are not very willing to actually cut down on meat in practice[3]. The top three
reasons keeping Danes from buying more plant-based alternatives is taste, price, and texture presented in

2.3
Wheat Bran
2.3.1
Origin of Wheat Bran
Wheat bran (WB) is a side-stream generated from the production of refined wheat flour, where only the
inner part of the wheat kernel (shown in figure 2.4 below), the starchy endosperm, is a part of the final
product. The remaining parts from the milling are collected at different stages of the process, where
WB is the most significant one being around 25% of the whole wheat kernel. It mostly comprises the
outer and inner pericarp, and the aleurone, and will also contain some of the starchy endosperm and the
embryo[23]. Despite the increasing evidence of the health benefits of wholemeal products, consumers
still prefer products of refined wheat flour, which leads to great amounts of WB. The main reason why
WB isn’t utilized more widely in the baking industry is because of its gritty texture, bitter and pungent
flavor, and coarse mouthfeel of bread caused by the bran. At a more practical level, processing dough
with WB is technologically very challenging. When mixed with refined flour, WB has been shown to
negatively affect the quality of bread making, the dough mixing properties, and the structure of wheat
dough[24]. Around 70% of all wheat is used for food and with a yearly production of 740M tons, around
130M tons of WB is generated each year. In the production of 100% whole grain wheat flour, WB isn’t
produced as the flour comprises whole wheat kernels [25].
Figure 2.4: The anatomical parts of a wheat kernel[26]

2.3.3
Nutritional characteristics of WB
The nutritional composition of WB mainly consists of dietary fibers, other carbohydrates, and protein as
seen in the table2.2 below. It is also rich in minerals, vitamin B6, thiamine, folate, vitamin E, and some
phytochemicals, especially antioxidants such as phenolic compounds. However, the bioavailability of the
nutrients is greatly affected by the fiber-rich food matrix and the absorption of some minerals might also
be affected by the so-called anti–nutrient phytic acid [28]. Several components in WB are associated with
health benefits, such as soluble dietary fibers, which has shown to improve gut health. Another compound
beta-glucan helps to reduce blood cholesterol levels, which may reduce the risk of heart disease. High
levels of arabinoxylan are to be found in WB as well and are linked to a reduced risk of type 2 diabetes[23].

2.4
Processing: Utilizing the nutrients
The ’super fine WB’ previously mentioned is made by a process called super fine milling or micronization.
It’s an effective process to disrupt the WB fiber matrix and not only improves protein digestibility but
also protein extractability, releases phenolic compounds, and changes functional properties[31]. This is
just one of many methods to fully utilize the nutrients in WB suggested by the literature on this topic. The
main principle for most of these processes is the same - the fiber matrix needs to be disrupted to make
the compounds within more accessible. From here, the enhanced WB can be used directly or the desired
compound can be extracted by varying techniques depending on it being protein, lipids, antioxidants, etc.
2.4.1
Mechanical and heat treatments
Milling in various forms has been used to change the properties of WB[23]. It can both be done to improve
the bioavailability of nutrients and is also a feasible method to overcome the negative effects of coarse
bran on food quality. Dry fractionation methods, such as electrostatic separation and air classification
have also been studied[25]. Either in combination with milling or by itself, heat treatments such as
autoclaving and steam explosion increase the amount of soluble arabinoxylan and reduce the amount
of phytic acid. Microwaving reduces phytic acid levels and increases available protein. Extrusion with
different parameters changes the properties of WB fibers and can reduce mycotoxin levels and increase
the amino acid content[32]. Ultrasound has also been shown as a tool to disrupt the fiber structure of WB
and led to increased concentrations of different compounds[33].
2.4.2
Microbes and enzymes
Fermentation with different microbes, such as lactic acid bacteria (LAB) and yeasts, can be used to in-
crease protein extractability and modify the nutritional composition of WB[25]. A combined fermentation
of these microbes can also increase the amount of free amino acids, change the flavor of the bran, reduce
phytic acid content, and more[34]. Fungi, such as Rhizopus oryzae known for tempeh production, are
also capable of improving the flavor and texture when used to ferment WB and following used for bak-
ing cakes[35]. The microbes are able to change the composition and properties of WB as they are able
to produce enzymes that break down nutrients and compounds, that can change the pH. Commercially
produced enzymes can also be used directly to break down the fiber matrix and release compounds. The
aleurone layer of WB contains different digestive enzymes intentionally to be used for the germination
of the wheat seed. However, by chemical acidification, these enzymes, called endogenous enzymes, can
be activated and lead to an increase in protein liberation and solubilization[25].
2.4.3
Extraction
Typically the treatments above are used as a former step to extraction, and this has been done with a
high protein yield and high purity of the final concentrate. In the case of the activation of the exogenous
enzyme prior to an HCl extraction, 75% of the proteins in WB were solubilized and extracted from the
bran after 24 hours of incubation. For the non-treated bran, 14% of the bran protein was solubilized by
the same extraction method. When fermented with a mix of LAB, yeast, and commercial enzymes for
24 hours, 52% of the proteins were solubilized, but in vitro, studies showed twice as good digestibility
compared to the use of only incubating with endogenous enzymes. Protein isolates from cereal bran by
alkaline extraction typically have a protein purity of 67%-90% and a protein extraction yield of 18%-
34%[25]. This is confirmed by a specific case where milling followed by alkaline extraction gave a final
protein concentrate with 80% protein and an extraction yield of 34%[26]. Another case using water,

3.2.1
Process combination: Higher protein for extrusion
For showing how the processes could be combined, one example could be a combination of fermentation,
separation, or extraction with extrusion. Extrusion is widely used among plant-based meat alternatives
to mimic the fibrous meat texture by applying pressure and heat to plant proteins resulting in a restruc-
turing of the proteins as mentioned in section 2.2. The protein content needs to be rather high for this
to be possible, and protein meal, concentrate, or isolate are typically used with a protein content ranging
from 50%-90% on a dry basis. However, research shows that extrusion still can work in a mixture of
oat fiber concentrate and pea protein isolate with a high fiber content of 33% and 40% protein[38]. As
the protein content of WB is 16.2% it would need an increase. By microbial fermentation of WB, the
microbial biomass could potentially leverage the protein content. However, this would require an addi-
tional nitrogen source and a microbe able to convert this into new amino acids or proteins if the selected
microbe can’t fix nitrogen from the atmosphere. By separation, the WB undergoes milling or grinding
and then a separation process where the particles are separated according to their size. This is described
in a report[39] from Danish Technological Institute, where they obtain a concentrate from WB with 82%
higher protein content compared to the initial WB. This is probably still too low for the formation of
fibrous proteins by extrusion and would need optimization. However, protein could be concentrated
through an extraction process as seen in the literature review. In this case, the WB might also go through
a milling/grinding process at first to reduce particle size, then probably an enzymatic treatment to break
down the fiber-rich matrix and make some compounds more available as seen in the literature review.
Another way to do the last mentioned could be through heat treatments, ultrasound, or fermentation.

didn’t change. Under the frying, it was observed, that the WB tempeh had a very high oil absorption

Greatest nutrient opportunity
For the potential use cases, there are both protein and fiber-focused ideas, but where is the biggest poten-
tial? In Figure 2.2 it is clear that protein is by far the greatest nutrient in plant-based meat alternatives,
which makes sense as many try to mimic meat. However, in WB the greatest nutrient category is fiber,
so if WB were to be used with its current nutritional composition, it would be in very low amounts, else
the fiber content of the product would exceed the market average too much. So this implies that the
greatest potential is in the protein-focused ingredients, and this will be further explored. From Figure
3.1 this could be in the form of a ’Protein ingredient’ from WB or ’Microbial protein’. The microbial
protein idea could be implemented in several ways, but the most significant step would be fermentation.
Hereby, the microbe would grow on WB as for the previous tempeh case, and from here it could be
extracted or separated from the WB, which would lead to a microbial protein or maybe a mix of WB
protein and microbial protein depending on the method of separation. This would differentiate from just
a pure WB protein ingredient. Another interesting ingredient could be a fermented WB (FWB), where the
protein content is raised by the growth of the microbial proteins, and the FWB is the protein ingredient
by itself without the need for extraction. In the earlier mentioned special course, a literature review was
done(see appendix A.6), to investigate whether the protein content would increase by fermentation or
not. The different sources of this literature review measured the protein content in different ways and it
was, therefore, difficult to conclude if this was possible. Some measured soluble protein and some total
protein, and some used the Kjeldahl method to measure the nitrogen content, while others used other
methods. In general, the protein content shouldn’t be able to increase, without an additional nitrogen
source, as protein can’t be made without nitrogen[40]. However, some of the sources in this literature
review seemed like it was possible to raise the total protein content by fermentation with the addition of
organic[41] or inorganic[42] nitrogen. So this is a market opportunity to explore, high protein fermented
WB abbreviated PFWB as a protein ingredient for meat alternative producers. However, the principle
would need additional confirmation through other research findings or by conducting experiments.

Technical
In this chapter the technical feasibility of the high Protein Fermented Wheat Bran (PFWB) as an ingre-
dient in meat alternatives will be investigated through experiments. This means that first of all, the aim
will be to see if the protein content can be raised with nitrogen-supplemented fermentation at different
concentrations of ammonium chloride as the nitrogen source. The effect of fermentation time will also be
investigated. This will be done by analyzing the amino acid content and profile of the fermentation sam-
ples. The volatile compounds of the sample are analyzed as well, as this can give an indication of whether
and how the flavor is changed by fermentation. As a start, a short introduction to food fermentation, in
general, is given.
4.1
Food Fermentation
For over 6000 years, microbes have been used to preserve food by fermentation. This has made a broad
array of microorganisms a deeply rooted part of our food culture, where fermented foods and drinks such
as beer, bread, wine, cheese, sausage, pickled vegetables, and many more are staples. At a basic level, it’s
a food transformation process by microorganisms, where long-chain carbohydrates and proteins are bro-
ken down to simple sugars, and amino acids, or converted to other metabolites by enzymes produced by
the organisms. The line between rot and fermentation can be difficult to control and it’s well described
in the book ’The Noma Guide to Fermentation’: ”Rot is a club where everyone gets in: bacteria and
fungi, safe or unsafe, flavor-enhancing or destructive.” However, controlling a fermentation process can
be done by regulating pH, oxygen, temperature, salinity, and more [43].
The purpose of fermentation can be many but the five primary ones are preservation, culinary enrich-
ment, nutritional enrichment, detoxification, and cooking efficiency[44]. For the case of PFWB, the
main reason will be nutritional enrichment, but also culinary enrichment as the flavor of WB will change,
hopefully, for the better.

supplementation of inorganic nitrogen[42], and here Rhizopus Oligosporus was also used. It has been

one source states that Rhizophus species can metabolize inorganic nitrogen, and that inorganic nitro-
gen is preferred over organic nitrogen for an economically feasible large–scale production[48]. Another
source states that filamentous fungi can synthesize all the different amino acids needed from inorganic
nitrogen[49]. As Rhizopus species are filamentous fungi, this could mean that it’s technically feasible to
produce the PFWB ingredient, and the following experiments will show if AC can be used to increase
the protein content of WB with Rhizopus Oligosporus.
4.2.1
Regualtion - Novel food
Is WB fermented with Rhizopus Oligosporus a novel food? When developing a new product on the Eu-
ropean market, there’s a chance that the product will be classified as a novel food. This refers to the
regulation procedure of new foods in the EU, and if classified as a novel food, the approval process can
take 18 months or longer[50]. From a business perspective, this would increase time-to-market signifi-
cantly, and years of research and development(R&D) and loads of money could be lost if the ingredient
got rejected for approval. Even though both WB and the tempeh culture, Rhizopus oligosporus, are non-
novel and approved for consumption, the combination could be novel and require approval. Therefore,
the novel food department at the Ministry of Food, Agriculture and Fisheries of Denmark was contacted
and asked if WB fermented with Rhizopus oligosporus would be recognized as a novel food. The answer
was that if WB (not novel) is fermented with Rhizopus Oligosporus (not novel), then the Danish Food
Authority will not consider the overall product as a novel food, and it will be up to the potential selling
company to ensure that the fermented food is safe to eat for consumers. However, it cannot be certain
that this applies at an EU level until an official assessment has been done through something called an
Article 4 request. This is good news for selling a potential product in the Danish market as the initial
market. If a promising ingredient is developed, an Article 4 request should be made, to be sure that all of
the EU could be a potential market.

4.3.2
Pre-treatment
To ensure minimal contamination from unwanted microorganisms, the WB was sterilized in an oven at
121◦C for 20 minutes. To allow quicker heating of the WB, 2 kg was vacuum-packed distributed amongst
three vacuum bags, and placed in the oven. This method can’t be recommended, as the WB came out as
a brick that needed to be crumbled back to normal size by hand.
4.3.3
Solid State Fermentation
After sterilization, the WB was mixed with tap water in a ratio of 1:1. Next 5g/kg of Rhizopus spores was
mixed into the wet WB (WWB) by hand with gloves. 3g/kg was recommended on the package but the
amount was increased to 5g/kg to have a higher risk of Rhizopus being the dominant organism. Next, the
WWB was divided into four parts. Three of these parts had AC added to them, 1g, 2g, and 4g per 100g
WWB respectively, while the last part didn’t receive any AC. The four different formulations were then
packed into perforated plastic bags with 100 g in each bag. Eight bags were made for each formulation
for 0h, 24h, 48h, and 72h of fermentation time and duplicates of each. This gave a total amount of 32
bags. They were sealed with tape and marked with a recognition system outlined as ”T24N0D1” meaning
24 H of fermentation with 0g nitrogen source (AC) duplicate 1. This recognition system will be used to
describe the samples in the following sections but without referring to the duplicate.
The solid-state fermentation (SSF) was carried out in an Isotherm Forced Convection Laboratory Incu-
bator set to 30◦C. Samples were taken out at the four time-stamps and stored at -20◦C until ready for
analysis.

Analysis
Below, the methods used for analysis are presented.
4.4.1
LC-MS (amino-acids)
To detect the total content and composition of total amino acids and the free amino acids in the fermented
samples, a Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) was made. In the test of the total con-
tent of AAs, all protein is hydrolyzed (broken down) to AAs, so the total content of AAs equals the
total content of protein + other AAs. The method of these tests can be found in appendix A.4 and A.4
respectively. Due to a limitation on the capacity of the machines, all 32 samples weren’t analyzed, and
only one duplicate per sample was analyzed. Samples, T0N0, T24N0, T48N0, T72N0, T48N1, T48N2,
and T48N4 went through the analysis of total amino acids, while T0N0, T48N0, and T48N1 for the free
amino acids.
4.4.2
GC-MS (Votalite compounds)
Volatile compounds play an important role in the flavor of food as they contribute to its aroma and taste.
These compounds are responsible for the unique and distinct flavors of different foods. The taste buds in
the mouth can detect only five basic tastes - sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami. Therefore, the sense of
smell is crucial in determining the overall taste experience of food, as it is capable of detecting thousands
of different odorants. Volatile compounds are released from the food during cooking and processing,
and they can also be generated by microbial fermentation during food production[51]. Therefore, the
volatile compounds in fermented and unfermented WB were analyzed through GC-MS. The method of
the analysis can be found in appendix A.3. For the same reasons as for LC-MS, only T0N0 and T48N0
were analyzed

4.5.6
Sources of error
It would have been interesting to have done an LC-MS for non-fermented samples with nitrogen added,
to see if AC interferes with the LC-MS machine. Regarding the suspicious decrease of MET for T48N4
there might be an explanation. The content of MET is the lowest of all AAs, and therefore, it might
be more exposed to uncertainty. When investigating the data more thoroughly, one of the quadruplicate
values of MET for T0N0 is 8.4 times higher than the average of the other three. But the average of all
four is used to show the data. If this high value is removed, it actually shows an increase in the content of
MET of around 200% for all fermentation times. And even with the addition of AC, the increase is still
at around 200%, except for T48N4, where it’s 100%. Another thing to consider is that the composition
of the starter culture is unknown. On the package it just says ”Rhizophus spores”, but it’s unknown if
these are Rhizopus Oligosporus, Rhizopus oryzae, or another Rhizophus strain even though these two
are the ones typically used for tempeh. Contamination of the samples might also have occurred from
organisms transported by air or by hand when preparing the samples. These potential organisms could
have influenced the results above. For future experiments, this could be further investigated by mapping
the organisms in the starter culture and samples by microscopy.

experiments, which implies that it might work. Another company, Bosque Foods[70], seems to be using
the same principles, even though they are even more secretive about their process. MOA is targetting the
B2B plant-based meat analog market and would therefore be direct competition, while Bosque Foods is
developing its own meat alternative products. Another potential competitor is Evergrain by ABInBev, a
startup making a protein powder from brewers’ spent grain (BSG), which has a very similar nutritional
profile to WB. Their target market seems to be plant-based drinks and sports nutrition, but of course,
there is a risk of them also focusing on meat alternatives in the future. Many more startups probably exist
in this market and more will most likely emerge in the future. The competition in the protein market,
in general, is expected to be moderate to high owing to the presence of numerous players[61], which is
assumed to be the case for the market for protein ingredients for meat alternatives as well.
Another thing to consider is that as WB is a byproduct of wheat flour production, its existence is 100%
dependent on wheat production. If wheat crops get a disease locally or globally, the WB supply could be
influenced. To hedge against this risk, the protein production process could be developed as a platform
style, able to shift to other sidestreams. Another great external risk is that the flour mills are the producers
of WB and if they develop or license a technology to incorporate WB back into the flour, this could be a
great competitive thread. But, this is also an interesting market opportunity, that could be further investi-
gated in the future. The market for wheat flour was valued at 160.7B USD in 2020[71], thereby 49 times
larger than the protein ingredient market for meat alternatives at 3.3B USD, and could hold interesting,
maybe less challenging, and more impactful market opportunities.
5.2.4
The final score
By a recap of the analysis above, the potential of the market opportunity of producing WBPC will be
evaluated. It doesn’t meet any unmet needs of the customer, and at the moment it isn’t known if WBPC
has any unique functional properties. The compelling reason to buy is therefore seen as ’Low’. The SOM
is rather small at 9.1M USD, however, this is only 5% of the SAM and 0.28% of the TAM. It could be
possible to obtain a bigger portion of the TAM and the market is growing with a compounded annual
growth rate of 10.2%. With this in mind, the market volume is considered to be ’Mid’. Lastly, regarding
the economic viability, a WBPC company is expected to be ’Low’ as the protein content of WB is too
low compared to soybean meal used for soy protein production. This would require more WB to produce
1 kg of product but also a much higher production capacity. All in all, the potential of a business solely
producing WBPC is judged as ’Low’.
To judge the overall challenge, the implementation obstacles are evaluated first. The technology for ex-
traction is already developed and from the literature review, decent extraction yields have been obtained
with a high protein content in the extract. However, as earlier explored, the extraction yield needs to be
very high for this to be profitable and the process would require further development. More importantly,
the final ingredient needs to fulfill the requirements of the customers, such as a ”neutral taste”, which
makes the development more difficult. With this in mind, the implementation obstacles are scored as
’High’. Time to revenue is closely linked to the score of implementation obstacles, as the development of
the technology will take time. Another thing prolonging the time to market is the product development
period of the customers, which might want the properties of the ingredient changed before implementa-
tion. Therefore, time to revenue is ranked as ’High’. Lastly, due to a highly competitive protein ingredient
market and the low protein content compared to other sidestreams, the external risk is scored as ’High’
as well.

Conclusion
In this thesis, it was investigated how it can be feasible to use wheat bran in meat alternatives both
from a technical and commercial perspective. Through a literature study, the characteristics of wheat
bran and meat alternatives were mapped. It was found that meat alternatives on average had a protein
content of 15.4%, while only containing 4.8% of fiber. The protein content in wheat bran was found to be
rather high at 16.2% but with a much higher fiber content at 40.2%. Without a change in the nutritional
composition of wheat bran, the high fiber content would therefore limit the usage of wheat bran in meat
alternatives. Among multiple ideas generated using a mindmap approach, it was therefore decided to
further inspect three potential wheat bran protein ingredients with leveraged protein content. These ideas
were referred to as ’WB Protein’, ’Microbial Protein’, and ’PFWB’ respectively. WB Protein was defined
as extracted protein from wheat bran. Microbial Protein required a fermentation where a microbe should
be grown on the surface of wheat bran and later separated from it. PFWB stands for Protein Fermented
Wheat Bran and would require a nitrogen-supplemented fermentation process where the protein content
would be raised. PWFB was chosen as the most promising one, as the complexity was deemed as lower
than the other ingredient ideas and no byproducts would be generated with this approach. However, the
technical feasibility of PFWB couldn’t be completely proven by existing literature, so this was further
investigated through experiments. In the experiments, different samples of wheat bran were fermented
with fungi, Rhizopus sp., at different levels of nitrogen supplementation and different fermentation times.
Afterward, the samples were analyzed through an LC-MS analysis to detect changes in the amino acids
and protein content. The technical feasibility of PFWB couldn’t be proved through the experiments as the
nitrogen supplementation didn’t lead to any protein increase after 48 hours of fermentation. Therefore,
PFWD wasn’t further investigated. Instead, the business potential of WB Protein was investigated, as
the technical feasibility of this ingredient was already proven in the existing literature. It was found that
the meat alternative producers mainly operate with three protein powder types; flour, concentrate, and
isolate. It was assumed that WB Protein could be formulated into a WB Protein Concentrate (WBPC)
that could cover all three powder types in the market. However, it was found that WBPC didn’t meet any
particular customer needs that weren’t satisfied by the current ingredients on the market. Also, it wouldn’t
be able to compete on price with protein from other byproducts as the protein content of wheat bran is
much lower than that of soy- and rapeseed meal used to produce soy- and rapeseed protein. Therefore, it
was concluded that a startup or company would be better off choosing another market opportunity than
solely producing and selling WBPC. But, throughout the analysis, a new opportunity arose. This was a
Biorefinery Concept, where WBPC would be one out of multiple high-value compounds extracted from
wheat to generate multiple revenue streams. This market opportunity was evaluated to involve high-risk
and high reward. However, this concept would need further investigation to judge its true feasibility. On
the basis of the work done in this thesis, wheat bran in meat alternatives wasn’t proven to be feasible.

Future steps
Wheat bran in meat alternatives wasn’t proven to be feasible in the work of this thesis. However, other
interesting findings were made that could benefit future research. Some of these are listed and explained
below:
• By fermenting wheat bran with Rhizopus sp., the total amino acid content was increased by 1.4%
after 24 hours of fermentation. However, after 48 hours and 72 hours, the total amino acid content
decreased by -16.4% and -25.5% respectively compared to unfermented wheat bran. After 24 hours
the essential amino acid profile was significantly improved, but after 72 hours it was worse than for
unfermented wheat bran. This implies that fermentation can be overdone, and it could potentially
destroy the nutrients of the food fermented. It should also be considered when labeling fermented
foods, as the protein content can’t be expected to be the sum of the ingredients.
• Wheat bran could be used in many other ways by using the findings in this thesis. It could be
promising to use it in bread together with the rest of the wheat kernel where it originally belongs.
Transportation and supply chain wise this could also make good sense with the right business
model. The market opportunity could be to develop a process, which could be licensed to the
mills. This would still require a change of flavor, texture, and baking properties. This could be
done by super-fine milling, endogenous or exogenous enzyme treatment, and fermentation. With
the right parameters and organisms, wheat bran could potentially be added back to the flour this
way. It wouldn’t generate significant amounts of byproducts and could have a huge impact on
utilizing a large amount of wheat bran and let people benefit from the health benefits of wheat bran
by simply eating bread.

A.3
GC-MS method
The method described below is written by Sanne Kjærulf Todorov, PhD-student at the DTU Nationale
Food Institute, and sent to me by e-mail on the 9th of March 2023.
”GC-MS is a method to detect volatile compounds in a sample. Triplicates of 1 g of sample was measured
for T0N0D1 and T48N1D2. Each sample was added to a DURAN round bottom flask. 20 mL DI water
and 30 uL internal standard (1-pentanol 4-methyl) was added to each flask. The flasks were placed in 45
°C waterbaths and an adsorbent tube containing tenax-GR 60/80 was attached to the headspace exit of
the flask for volatiles collection. The flask was bubbled through with nitrogen gas at a flow rate of 240
± 10 mL/min for 30 minutes. Afterwards the adsorbent tube was dried for 20 minutes with a nitrogen
flow of 40 ± 5 mL/min. The adsorbent tube was analyzed using a TurboMatrix650 sample tube system
coupled to a Hewlett Packard mass selective detector. The analysis was run using standard parameters
for detection of ions with a molecular mass between 50 and 550. Standards of 36 volatile compounds
were run in 5 different concentrations to make calibration curves.”
A.4
LC-MS: Total amino acid
The method described below is described in detail in an internal document [73] from the DTU Nationale
Food Institute. However, a summary is given below with small changes compared to [73]

==PAGE:44==
LC-MS is here used to detect the total amount of 16 different amino acids in a sample. Quadruplicates
of 50 mg were measured and put into glass vials for samples T0NO, T24N0, T48N0, T72N0, T48N1,
T48N2, and T48N4. 1 mL of 12M HCl is added to the vials before they are closed tightly. Leave in oven
for 18 hours at 110 ◦C. Take vials out of the oven and cool them down to room temperature. Filter the
hydrolysates into 4 mL vials through 0.22 µg cellulose acetate syringe filter. Mix 100 µL of this into a
new 4 mL vial, then add 1.5 mL 0.2M sodium carbonate followed by 1.6 mL of 100mM of ammonium
formate. This gives a dilution factor of 32. The liquid was transferred from the vial to a 1mL vial suitable
for the Agilent Technologies Quadrupole LC-MS machine.
A.5
LC-MS: Free amino acid
The method described below is described in detail in an internal document [74] from the DTU Nationale
Food Institute. However, a summary is given below with small changes compared to [74]
LC-MS is here used to detect the total amount of 16 different amino acids in a sample. Triplicates of
50 mg were measured and put into glass vials for samples T0NO, T48N0, and T48N1. 5 mL of 0.1 M
HCl is added to the vials, and then centrifuges at 5000 g for 5 minutes. 250 µL of the supernatant is
now transferred to an HPLC vial together with 1 mL of ammonium formate. Now they are ready for the
Agilent Technologies Quadrupole LC-MS machine.
