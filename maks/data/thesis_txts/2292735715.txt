Abstract
Investigation of the time-lens induced demagniﬁcation factor.
The purpose of this thesis is to evaluate the possibility of compressing the
spectra of individual pulses in a 1.28TBd high-speed OTDM signal below the
130GHz limit of coherent receivers. The idea is to achieve the spectral compres-
sion through an optical Fourier transform (OFT) system. The foundation for
this evaluation is partly simulations based on the nonlinear Schr¨odinger equa-
tion, together with an experimental demonstration of the concept.
The thesis begins with a derivation of the fundamental mathematics governing
OFT. This is followed by a presentation of the simulation results. These show
that the desired compression is possible under the condition that a broad tem-
poral chirp is applied in the OFT process. Hereafter follows the results of the
experiment. Here a successful OFT-indcued compression is shown to bring the
bandwidth of a 20GBd OTDM signal below the 130GHz limit. This serves as
a proof-of-concept of spectral compression of an ODTM signal. Furthermore,
The experience gained from conducting the experiment facilitates a discussion
of the practical conditions required to obtain OFT induced spectral compression
of high quality. It is found that several challenges are faced, including that of
having access to optical waveguides with the desired zero dispersion wavelength
and slope. Also using a chirping element capable of delivering a precise linear
chirp together with ensuring a highly aligned OTDM signal, was found to as-
sumable play an important role in achieving a high quality of the compressed
signal.
In conclusion, the conducted research indicates, that it is possible to compress
the bandwidth of single pulses in a highspeed OTDM signal below the 130GHz
limit of coherent detectors. More research involving simulations or experiments
which actually includes the coherent detection process is, however, needed be-
fore the idea of using coherent detection to monitor high-speed OTDM signals
can be ﬁnally conﬁrmed.

Introduction
Research show, that Internet traﬃc has been growing exponentially for many
years and is expected to continue doing so [Tkach, 2010].
For instance, on
YouTube alone, in every minute, more than 300 years of video is being watched
and over 300h new content is being uploaded [Youtube, 2015]. This demand
drives the research in long-haul optical ﬁber systems where the goal is to trans-
mit the highest data throughput over the longest distance, preferable without
signal regeneration. In this discipline it is important to maximize the spectral
eﬃciency [Winzer, 2012] and, at the same time, minimizing the average trans-
mitted energy per bit [Willner et al., 2014], corresponding to optimizing the
signal to noise ratio and thereby allowing for more information to be embedded
in ﬁbers. High data transmission rates in single channel optical waveguides is
a rapidly developing ﬁeld. Optical time domain multiplexing (OTDM) has lead
to symbol rates of 1.28TBd [Mulvad et al., 2010], while advanced modulation
formats has made it possible to encode many bits of information into each sym-
bol. This has lead to single-laser signals with bit rate up to 10.8Tbit [Richter
et al., 2011] for single channel wave guides, while 43 Tbit/s has been achieved
for multi-channel wave guides [Hu et al., 2014]. With the fast evolving data
transmission rates also follows a demand for eﬃciently monitoring and process-
ing of the signals in real time with sub picosecond resolution [Foster et al., 2008].
Conventional techniques for measuring the optical waveform, such as ultrafast
optical sampling oscilloscopes, are based on the cross correlation measurements
between the measured signal and a short optical pulse [Dorrer, 2006]. These
techniques therefore needs several repetitions of the optical waveform to gener-
ate its proﬁle, and fast ﬂuctuations with durations comparable to the sampling
pulse width will hence not be detected. It is therefore desirable to make ”single-
shot” detections of the signal in which only one symbol is required for evaluating
the waveform.
This thesis seeks to evaluate the possibility of monitoring the signal quality of
high-speed OTDM signals by use of an optical Fourier transform (OFT) sys-
tem and a coherent detector. An OFT system is based on the combination of
a dispersive element and a phase modulator (PM), which together eﬀectively
transfers the temporal pulse shape to the frequency domain, or the spectral
shape to the time domain. The underlying mathematics of an OFT system is
directly analogue to that of a spatial imaging system consisting of a parabolic
lens followed by free space diﬀraction [Kolner et al., 1994]. Here the light exiting
the parabolic lens is the spatial Fourier transform (FT) of the light entering the
lens. Equivalently to the spatial analogue, an OFT system can be considered
to perform a FT on the temporal envelope of the incoming light. Furthermore,
like a spatial lens can magnify or compress an image, so can an OFT system
also magnify or compress a waveform in time. Based on these properties, it
is proposed that the quality of ultra fast OTDM signals can be monitored by
propagating the signal through an OFT system, and then measuring the full
electric ﬁeld of the individual pulses by a coherent receiver. Such a measure-
ment could potentially be performed single-shot allowing for detection of the
the un-averaged waveform, which could be useful e.g. when monitoring ultra
fast OTDM signals.

1.1
Objective of the thesis
The objective of this thesis is to evaluate the possibility of using an OFT system
to bring the bandwidth of individual pulses of a 1.28TBd OTDM signal below
the 130GHz limit of the coherent receivers limit.
This is achieved through
construction of an experimental OFT system together with simulations of an
OFT system based on the nonlinear Sch¨odinger equation (NLS). Due to the time
constraints of this thesis, it has not been possible to make an experimental OFT
system with a full-scale 1.28TBd signal. Instead the experiment is conducted by
use of a 20GBd OTDM signal with a full width at half maximum (FWHM) of
1.5ps, which is comparable in size to the symbol rates used in modern 1.28TBd
systems (200fs-300fs) [Mulvad et al., 2010] [Richter et al., 2011].
The main purpose of the experiment is a proof-of-concept of spectral compres-
sion of an ODTM signal. This should also facilitate a discussion of the practical
conditions required to obtain OFT induced spectral compression.
An actual demonstration of the compression of a high-speed OTDM signal is
attempted through the NLS-based simulations. The results of the experiment
together with the simulations should provide the base for evaluating the possi-
bility of making single-shot coherent detections of a 1.28TBd OTDM signal.

The two remaining systems Hybrid of ”noncoherent and diﬀerentially coherent
detection” and ”Coherent detection” does both allow for utilization of all four
DOF, and are thus suitable candidates for making full electric ﬁeld measure-
ments. Starting with the ”Hybrid of noncoherent and diﬀerentially coherent
detection”, it can tolerate very high phase noise tolerance and has found sig-
niﬁcant use in the early 1990s. It is, however, unsuited for detecting (QAM)
modulated signals, which currently is the most promising modulation scheme
for high bit rates [Ip et al., 2008]. The last and most advanced of these four
detection methods is coherent detection. Here the receiver computes decision
variables based on the recovery of the full complex electric ﬁeld including phase
information. This allows easy utilization of all four DOF. The disadvantages of
coherent detection, compared to the 3 other detection methods discussed, is the
complexity and the cost of the system [Ip et al., 2008]. This summarizes the
reasons for why it is the bandwidth of a coherent receiver which dictates the
compression factor which is sought to be obtained in this thesis.

3.4
Space-time duality for temporal imaging
The construction of the relevant OFT systems for this thesis is based on a du-
ality between the mathematics which describe the paraxial diﬀraction of beams
conﬁned in space, and the dispersion of narrow-band pulses in dielectrics [Kol-
ner et al., 1994]. In this subsection it will be shown how this duality leads to
the conclusion that a quadric phase modulation (PM) in time (a chirp) is the
analog of a thin lens in space. This analogy makes it appropriate to term an
optical component performing a quadric PM a time lens.
When having established the diﬀraction - dispersion analog together with the
time lens - thin lens analog, it will by shown how a combination of a time lens
and a dispersing element can create the TD analog of a spatical imaging system.
Such an imaging system can allow for expanding or compressing (magnify or
de-magnify) pulse shapes or spectral distributions, while maintaining the overall
shape of the envelope.
In the earlier sections the symbol T = t −z/vg has been used to distinguish the
moving time frame T from the static time frame t. It is, however, no longer
necessary to distinguish between those frame of references, so from this section
and on, the time frame will be the moving frame given by T, but in order to
follow standard notations the time will be denoted as t.

3.4.3
Electrooptic PM and parametric time-lenses
There exists several components capable of performing a quadric PM and hence
can be called a time-lens. A well-know example is an electrooptic PM (EOPM)
driven by a sinosoidal signal [Kolner et al., 1994] [Berger et al., 2000]. The as-

to a parable that it can work as a parabolic PM [Kolner et al., 1994], see ﬁgure
5. This method does, however, have an upper limit to the obtainable chirp rate
due to the maximum current capacity of the phase modulator.
Another way of creating a time-lens is by facilitating a parametric wave-mixing
process, such as degenerated four-wave-mixing (FWM), by use of a chirped
pump [Mulvad et al., 2011].
Here the generated idler will eﬀectively be the
phase-modulated signal except for a shift in frequency. In a degenerated FWM
process the conversion eﬃciency is proportional to |Ip|2, where Ip is the electri-
cal ﬁeld intensity of the pump. It is therefore important that the pump is ﬂat
in time and frequency in order not to distort the signal [Hansryd et al., 2002].
The pump can be constructed by sending high power pulses through a disper-
sion ﬂattened HNLF, which creates a super continuum [Mulvad et al., 2011].
The self-phase modulation in the HNLF will result in a broad spectrum which
is then ﬁltered out so that it obtains a ﬂat square-shaped spectrum [Mulvad
et al., 2011]. It can then be chirped and broadened by GVD in a SMF. The
chirp obtained by this process is described by (64). The length of the SMF ﬁber
is chosen so that the desired chirp is obtained. By chirping through a FWM
process the signal actually obtains the double chirp rate of the pump because
of the |Ip|2 scaling of the conversion eﬃciency [Hansryd et al., 2002] Mulvad
et al. [2011]. The length of the SMF ﬁber is chosen so that the desired chirp is
acquired.

The result of this theory chapter is, provides arguments for why it is potentially

The slowly varying envelope approximation
The dispersing elements of the temporal imaging systems, described in section
3.5, are all assumed to be based on pure a pure dispersive medium, meaning
that the Taylor expansion of the propagation constant β is terminated at β2.
Most dielectric media do, however, possess some degree of higher order disper-
sion, usually in the form of β3 [Agrawal, 2007]. The restriction of considering
no higher terms than β2 is, as mentioned earlier, based on the slowly varying
envelope approximation which is equivalent of assumption narrow band pulses.
So even though the contributions from β3 exists in a medium, the eﬀects can
be ignored for most pulses, as long as they are narrow band [Agrawal, 2007]. It
can, however, deﬁnitely be discussed if the pulses considered in this thesis are
narrow banded. According to (18), the condition for narrow band pulses is

The assumption of instantaneous χ3-response
Another source of error is potentially the assumption of instantaneous χ3-
response to electric ﬁelds, which was expressed by (20) in the process of deriving
the NLS equation. As mentioned earlier this leads to a neglecting of a special
kind of ﬁeld-induced molecular vibrations called the Raman eﬀect. This is, ac-
cording to [Agrawal, 2007], important for very narrow pulses. For silica it occurs
over a timescale of 60-70fs which is a factor ∼15 smaller than the time scale of
the 1.5ps pulses. From this consideration and [Agrawal, 2007], the unaccounted
Raman eﬀect should deﬁnitely be remembered, as a source of error in relation
to this thesis. The generalized NLS equation (32) does take the Raman eﬀects
into account, but it has been beyond the time constraints of this thesis to also
include these contributions in the derivation of the results.

5
Experimental work
This chapter concerns the experimental work of the thesis. The purpose is a
proof-of-concept of an OFT induced spectral compression of single pulses in
a high-speed ODTM signals. This should facilitate a discussion of the practi-
cal conditions required to obtain OFT induced spectral compression and thus
provide knowledge for the later discussion of the maximal possible spectral com-
pression of a 1.28TBd OTDM signal.
The most relevant possible experimental system for this thesis would involve
a full scale 1.28TBd OTDM signal such as described in [Mulvad et al., 2010]
or [Richter et al., 2011]. Such a system would, however, require more time in
the laboratory and more equipment than was available for this thesis. It has
therefore been sought to test the time-to-frequency OFT conversion on a OTDM
signal which resembles the full scale OTDM signal as much as possible under
the given conditions. By doing this, it is hoped that the experiment can provide
usable results for a discussion of the potential of making single-shot coherent
detections of a 1.28TBd OTDM signal.
The experiment conducted can be described as a spectral compression of a
20GBd OTDM signal with pulse widths of ∆tFWHM 1.5ps by use of an time-to-
frequency OFT system with a FWM based time lens. The system is described
in details in the following section.
5.1
Description of the OFT system
The experimental system as a whole is illustrated on ﬁgure 16. As can be seen
from the ﬁgure, it can be divided into 6 separate sections. Section 1 contains
the signal source, which is split into 3 channels, one for the signal, one for
the pump and one for driving the electrooptical phase modulator. Section 2
describes how the signal by a FWM process gets wavelengths converted. This
is needed to separate the wavelength of the signal from the wavelength of the
pump.
In section 3 the symbol rate of the signal is doubled by an OTDM
process. Section 4 concerns the chirping of the pump by use of an electrooptical
phase modulator. Section 5 describes the dispersion of the signal which is the
ﬁrst actual part of the OFT system, and section 6 concerns the chirping of the
signal through a FWM process which is the end the OFT system. Here the time-
to-frequency converted signal is measured by an OSA. The decision of chirping
though a FWM process instead of just phase modulating the signal directly, is
primarily because, as discussed in section 3.4.3, one then eﬀectively obtains the
double chirp rate of the one provided by the phase modulator. Furthermore,
the limits of the electrooptical phase modualtion does, as will be demonstrated

5.2
Experimental results
With the purpose the experiment being to make a spectral compression of the
20GBd OTDM signal, it is necessary to decide on how to measure the input
spectral width.
This can be determined from the the spectral proﬁle of the
20GBd OTDM signal, as shown on ﬁgure 22. Here the width is found to be
∆λ ≈3.2nm ± 0.5nm, where the uncertainty originated from the breathing
because of incoherent cross talk between the pulses. This spectral width diﬀers,
however, from the one expected from pulses of temporal width ∆tFWHM =
1.58±0.1 ps, as was measured on ﬁgure 21 which, by the condition of transform-
limited Gaussian pulses would imply a spectral width of ∆λ = 2.2nm.
This incongruency could be ascribed to the fact that the OTDM process is
not optimal, partly because of the breathing of the system, and partly because
of improper alignment. It is very possible that a more better aligned OTDM
process would have resulted in a more narrow spectral width of the multiplexed
signal. The width of ∆λ ≈3.2nm ± 0.5nm is, however, the obtained result, and
therefore it is this spectral width, which is termed as as the input spectral width
to the OFT.
A measurement of the spectral proﬁle after the ﬁnal HNLF ﬁber in the OFT
system is shown on ﬁgure 28. Here the pump is in the middle with a unchirped
signal on the left and the chirped idler on the right. It is clearly seen, that
the idler, upon this last operation of the OFT system has been signiﬁcantly
compressed.
A zoom on the idler, as shown with blue line on ﬁgure 29, reveals a 3dB spectral
width of ∆λ = 0.67 ± 0.2 nm, where the ±0.2 nm is included for taking the
breathing of the system into account. For comparison, the idler as a result of
FWM between an undispersed 20GBd OFT signal and a pump, which is not
phase modulated, is shown with red line on ﬁgure 29. It is by comparison seen,
that the combined eﬀect of the dispersion in the SMF and the chirping by FWM
results in a compressed idler.
With an expected spectral width of λ = 0.4nm and an achieved width of λ =
0.67 ± 0.2 nm, the system has performed a spectral compression which is a bit
smaller than the theory predicted.
The diﬀerence of ∼0.2nm between the theoretical an practical compression can
be argued to originate from factors such as non optimal length of SMF ﬁber for
the dispersing part of the OFT, non quadracity of the phase modulation and
non optimal alignment of the OTDM signal

6
Results and discussion
The evaluation of the possibility for bringing the bandwidth of individual pulses
in a 1.28TBd OTDM signal below the 130GHz limit of the coherent receivers
available at the Technical University of Denmark, has been accomplished by
ﬁrst deriving the theoretically important equations governing OFT systems.
The relations showed that the wanted spectral compression was possible in the
conditions which the derivations were based on. The conditions included the
assumption of pure dispersive materials and components capable of modulating
the light with a perfect quadric phase modulation. This ideal scenario works ﬁne
for the making of simple predictions, but since none of these ideal components
are realisable in practise, the exact evaluation required more knowledge. At this
point, the method of solving the problem analytically had it’s limitation since
including more physical relevant parameters would practically make the system
analytical unsolvable.
Therefore two other methods for evaluation of the spectral compression was
conducted; split-step simulations based on the NLS equation and a practical
experiment. With the simulations it was possible to include more of the relevant
parameters which was omitted in the ideal cases considered by the analytical
approach. This investigation showed that it was possible to obtain an OFT
induced demagniﬁcation by applying a broad chirping range, which results in a
splitting of the OTDM signal into a number of diﬀerent frequency components,
given by the number of temporal pulses overlapped by the chirping range. Each
of these frequency components then represents a tributary of the 1.28TBd signal.
The simulations of this process showed that a correctly designed OFT system
would be capable of compressing each single frequency components enough for
allowing isolation of that frequency by ﬁltering. This could then, potentially
allow for coherent detection of the tributary.
When realizing an actual OFT system further limitations will be introduced in
the form of signal noise and restrictions of optical materials with the desired
parameters.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
To further investigate the the process of compressing through an OFT systems,
a real system was realised is the laboratory. This experiment succeeded in hav-
ing a 20GBd OTDM signal compresses below the 130GHz bandwidth limit of
the coherent receiver. Although it was not a full scale OTDM signal, the exper-
iment allowed for gathering impressions of the possible challenges, which would
be faced if the compression of the full scale OTDM signal should be accomplished
as predicted by the simulations. It was found that several challenges were faced
in the practical realization of the OFT system including that of having access to
optical waveguides with the right slope and zero dispersion wavelength allowing
for optimal FWM process. A highly aligned OTDM signal was also found to
assumable play an important role in achieving a high quality of the compressed
signal. In general, the OFT conversion must also be assumed to improve if one
had access to a dispersing medium with more exact quadracity of the dispersion
and a time lens capable of delivering a more precise linear chirp with a high slope.
The perspective of the results of this thesis is primarily that it could lead to
an useful and relatively simple way of monitoring high-speed OTDM signals
by single-shot coherent detections, e.g. in the form of a coherent receiver with
a tunable bandpass ﬁlter. This method would, however, probably suﬀer from
interference from the neighbouring channels due to the dispersion step of the
OFT. A way to overcome this interference could be to demultiplex the wanted
tributary in advance of the OFT. This could e.g. be done by time gating with a
nonlinear optical loop mirror such as used in [Mulvad et al., 2009]. The draw-
back of this is, that it greatly complicates the OFT system compared to the
solution of the sliding ﬁlter. Both the solution of the sliding ﬁlter and the time
gate could, potentially, prove to be a useful way of checking the quality of high
speed OTDM signals with sub picosecond resolution.
With a measurement technique such as this, capable of detecting small changes
in ultra short pulses, it is also a possibility that an OFT combined with a coher-
ent detector can be used to characterize properties of optical materials rather
then monitoring the quality of OTDM signals.
Using this technique, mate-
rial related phenomena happening on the sub picosecond scale, such as Raman
oscillations or nonlinearities in connection with short high-power pulses could
potentially be characterized.
The simulation results, which provides the argument for why it is considered
possible to achieve the desired spectral compression, are based on the approxi-
mations which applies to the NLS equation. This includes the slowly varying en-
velope approximation and the assumption of instantaneous χ3-response, which
lead to a neglecting of the Raman oscillations. With the simulations concerning
300fs pulses, it seems right to question the exact validity of this. According to
[Agrawal, 2007] one has to have pulse width above a few ps for being completely
free of the inﬂuence form Raman oscillations. It is thus not unlikely that diﬀer-
ent results would have been obtained, if the generalised NLS equation had been
used as a base for the simulations. One argument that points at the validity of
the simulations is that in [Mulvad et al., 2011] a time-to-frequency conversion
has already been shown to work but with a greater compression then the one
sought in this thesis. An argument could therefore be, that if it was possible for
Mulvad et al. to demonstrate a more dense compression, then the one desired

in practice by making an experiment comparable to the one simulated in this

While goal of this thesis was solely to evaluate the possibility of spectrally com-
pressing the signal enough for the bandwidth limit, the coherent receiving part
of the system also needs some attention. Further research in combining OFT
systems with coherent receivers could therefore beneﬁt from including the actual
process of coherent detection of the OTDM signal. This would allow for testing
the quality of the whole detection system and thereby make comparisons to
the quality of other methods for resolving temporal proﬁles such as frequency-
resolved optical gating (FROG), autocorrelation and ultrafast optical sampling
oscilloscopes.
In conclusion, it is estimated, that the possibility of using an OFT system to
bring the bandwidth of individual pulses of a 1.28TBd OTDM signal below the
130GHz limit of the coherent receivers limit is possible.
This estimation is,
however based on all the approximations which applies to the use of the NLS
code, and further research or an actual experimental realization is necessary
to ﬁnally conﬁrm the prospects of monitoring the signal quality of high speed
OTDM signals by coherent detection.
