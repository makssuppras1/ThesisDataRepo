Abstract
This Master’s Thesis presents the numerical reproduction in HAWC2 of experimental results for a Tetra-
Sub 10MW floating wind turbine. It is a continuation of the experimental campaign conducted in 2021
by DTU Wind Energy as part of the FloatStep project. A 1:60 scaled model of the DTU 10MW RWT
mounted on the lab-scaled TetraSub concept foundation, was numerically simulated under the same en-
vironmental conditions of the campaign. These included regular waves, irregular waves, focused waves
and a combination of wind and waves conditions. The numerical study was focused on the in-plane
responses, surge, heave and pitch. In an effort to replicate the basin tests, the hydrodynamic damping
had to be calibrated by trial-and-error method for each sea state, in ESYSWamit module of HAWC2.
Second-order diff-frequency loads were included in the simulations with the use of Newman approxi-
mation method. The comparisons were illustrated in time series, PSD and exceedance probability plots.
Firstly, the model was validated against experimental decay tests and showed a good match in terms
of response and in the estimation of natural frequencies of the system. Secondly, regular waves were
examined. These were the only cases of waves that did not include second order loads. The numerical
results for regular waves were quite satisfactory in all in-plane degrees of freedom. Afterwards, initial
irregular wave only simulations demonstrated the uncertainty around the calculated second-order loads
of the TetraSub floater, thus a filter was used on top of Newman approximation for very low frequencies.
In general, the numerical model would under predict surge and heave responses while over estimating
pitch, which was more affected by the second-order frequency loads. The tensions of the three moor-
ing lines were also underestimated mainly due to missing forcing in model.
The calibrated model
for focused waves showed promising results by capturing the wave’s energy spectrum and the majority
of natural frequencies’ peaks as seen by PSD plots, however further research is needed on focused waves.
The parameters for Mann model module of HAWC2 were calibrated based on experimental measure-
ments. The wind only simulations mainly focused on obtaining the correct center of buoyancy for the
tilted numerical system as the linear WAMIT analysis would lead to discrepancies for the mean pitch of
the floater. From wind only cases the existence of friction in the experimental setup was made obvious,
as the numerical blade pitch would constantly have greater mean values, since mechanical friction of
the generator was not modeled. The performance of the model under wind and wave conditions would
typically under estimate the wave’s frequency spectrum, due to the assumption of fixed hydrodynamic
coeﬀicients throughout the range of simulated environmental conditions while demonstrating the diﬀi-
culties uBEMT might face when calculating the aerodynamic loads of a floating wind turbine, especially
in the above rated region and surge DOF. Finally, two different control strategies implemented in the
campaign, were also numerically tested in wind speeds at and above rated. The first one was a detuned
Basic DTU Wind Energy Controller and the second one a novel controller with a tower-top velocity
loop. Both aimed at dealing with the effect of pitch instability. The numerical implementation of those
showed good results in following the responses of rotor speed and blade pitch angle. The model was
able to track the heavy influence in pitch natural frequency that the faster tower-top loop controller
would cause on the system’s motions. On the other hand, the numerical results exhibited the heavy
influence from wind spectrum that the detuned controller has, and the less dynamic behaviour it causes
on the responses of the system especially for rotor speed and blade pitch angle.
Keywords: Floating wind turbine, TetraSub floater, Numerical reproduction, wind, waves, HAWC2

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
The gift of wind energy
A first-of-its-kind, legally binding, global climate-change agreement was adopted at the Paris climate
conference in December 2015 [8]. The Paris Agreement main objective is to achieve climate-neutrality
before the end of the century. More specifically, the goal is to limit global warming below 2◦C, while
pursuing policies that would limit it even further at 1.5◦C. In order to prevent global temperature
rise, net-zero CO2 emissions have to be gradually achieved. Hence, renewable energy sources like solar,
hydro, biomass, geothermal and wind, that are environmental-friendly, should have a major role in
electricity generation.
Seven years after the agreement, wind energy both onshore and offshore, seems to be one of the
most promising renewable energy sources. Even during the two years of COVID-19 pandemic, wind
energy showed exceptional resilience. The year 2020 was the best year in history of global wind energy
with a year-over-year (YoY) growth of 53% [9], while in 2021, there was 93.6 GW of new installation,
bringing the cumulative wind power capacity to 837 GW [1]. Some of the countries that lead the world
in total onshore installations are China, the US and Germany, while key representatives for offshore
wind energy are China and Europe, namely Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands and the UK as seen
in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Total onshore and offshore installations. Figure taken from [1].
The year 2020 may have been the best in the history of wind energy, but 2021 was the record year
for offshore wind energy with 21.1 GW becoming grid connected worldwide, bringing the total offshore
wind capacity to 57.2 GW as seen in Figure 1.1. The majority of offshore wind comes from bottom
fixed offshore wind turbines (BFOWTs). A BFOWT would typically have and operational water depth
of approximately 50 m, therefore, locations with shallow waters like the North Sea, Irish Sea and Baltic
provide the best conditions for such technology to flourish [10].
Unsurprisingly, countries situated
around the aforementioned areas, like Denmark, the UK, Belgium, Germany and the Netherlands
account for 99% of European offshore wind installed capacity. Shallow waters, however, is a luxury
not many countries have. The Mediterranean Sea or the coast lines of Japan and the US have greater
average depths. This in combination with social pressure to keep wind turbines out of sight might make
both onshore and BFOWTs an unfeasible option. As a result, floating offshore wind energy becomes
an attractive solution.

1 Introduction
1.2
Floating offshore wind energy
As the efforts to decarbonise and abide by the goals for net-zero emissions continue, along with any
social upset the more traditional wind turbine systems may cause, opportunities in deeper waters have
to be sought. Thankfully, according to [11], 80% of the world’s offshore wind resource potential lies
in water depths of more than 60 m. As a consequence, BFOWTs become less attractive and floating
offshore wind turbines (FOWTs) can be game-changers. In general, some of the advantages of floating
offshore wind and FOWTs are [12]:
• Access to deeper waters and unlocking the full wind resource potential
• Stronger and less turbulent winds leading to higher capacity factors
• Lower visual and acoustic impact
• Less sensitive to local variations in depth or seabed conditions which aids mass production and
lowers the Levelised Cost of Energy (LCOE)
• Easy relocation, removal and decommission since most concepts can be towed to the installation
point
The interest in floating offshore wind is growing exponentially. The Global Wind Energy Council
report forecast a growth of floating offshore wind from 17 MW in 2020 to 16.5 GW in 2030 [11].
Moreover, the LCOE of floating wind turbines is expected to drop from around 200 €/MWh in 2020 to
approximately 40 €/MWh by 2050. [13] In 2021 alone, Norway commissioned the 3.6 MW TetraSpar
floating substructure at the Metcenter Test site [14], during the same year the 50 MW Kincardine
project in Scotland which utilises five Vestas V164-9.5MW turbines and the Principal Power Windfloat
platform was also introduced, making it the largest floating offshore wind site to date [15]. Pioneers
in floating offshore wind outside of Europe, commissioned new floating projects as well, such as the
5.5MW floating prototype at Yangxi Shapa III offshore wind farm in China [16]. The aforementioned
projects sum up to 57MW of floating wind capacity being commissioned in 2021 alone, bringing the
total capacity to 139MW [1].
1.2.1
Stabilization mechanisms and floating concept substructures
The main components of a FOWT substructure can be summed up to three. The floater, necessary for
structural purposes and buoyancy, the mooring lines combined with anchor system for station-keeping
and thirdly, the dynamic power cable for grid connection [2]. Companies around the globe are trying to
develop different designs, in an effort to dominate the market. An initial classification of those designs
can be based on the primary mechanism employed to achieve static equilibrium.
Those stabilising
mechanisms are:
• Mooring stabilised: Mooring lines under high tension restore the tilted structure to its equilib-
rium.
• Ballast stabilised: Ballast is typically a long structure at the bottom of the floater, used to
increase the distance of the center of gravity from the center of buoyancy by lowering the former.
Hence, creating the necessary restoring pitch moment when the system tilts.
• Waterplane stabilised: This mechanism counters the overturning moment by making use of the
large second moment of area with respect to the rotational axis. A large second moment of area
can be obtained either by the waterplane area being wide enough or by smaller cross-sectional
areas located at a distance from the axis of rotation.
==PAGE:19==
1.2 Floating offshore wind energy
3
Based on the stabilising mechanisms, floater designs can be classified in four main categories:
• Tension Leg Platform (TLP): TLP is a mooring stabilised floater with a central column to
mount the wind turbine. At the bottom of the center column there are multiple tendons connected
under high tension created by the excess buoyancy of the floater. Since mooring lines keep the
system stabilised, a TLP is dependent on the correct selection of them as well as the anchor
system and soil conditions. Some TLP floater designs would be the Pelastar [17] and Bluewater
Floating Wind TLP [18].
• Spar buoy: Spar concept typically consists of a long cylindrical member at the bottom of the
floater as a ballast in order to achieve stability. The draft of a Spar platform is quite big, so
assembly cannot be done in a harbour due to limited depth. Boats and crane vessels are utilised
in order to transfer and mount the wind turbine at the point of installation, which increases
the overall cost. Mooring lines are attached to the system for station-keeping. A well-known
commercial Spar buoy concept would be the Hywind Scotland, which was the world’s first floating
offshore wind farm developed by Equinor [19].
• Barge: Barge is based on waterplane stabilisation mechanism with very low draft that makes for
an easy installation. Floaters of this kind have a damping pool however, due to the larger motions
they experience [20]. An example of barge concept is Floatgen 2MW from Ideol, installed in 2018
on the Atlantic coast of France [21] .
• Semi-submersible: Yet another buoyancy stabilized floater concept like the Barge. Semi-sub
designs, however, usually have three columns placed on the edges of a triangle to achieve stability.
The wind turbine can be either mounted on top of one of these columns or be supported by a
fourth one. This can be located in the center of the triangle or be offset between the other columns.
Like the barge, semi-sub has mooring lines attached for station-keeping. Additional heave plates
can be added at the bottom of each column to dampen heave response in case of extreme sea
states. Grid connected installations of semi-sub type are Windfloat Atlantic Phase 1 [22] off the
coast of Portugal in 2019 and the previously mentioned Kincardine project in Scotland [15].

1.2.2
Challenges of floating wind energy
The wind energy potential a FOWT system can unlock, has motivated pioneering companies to develop
revolutionary prototypes and some of them have already been commissioned.
Yet, the LCOE of a
FOWT still remains more than three-times that of a BFOWT [25]. Consequently, such projects are
in need of government support in form of funding. Moreover, energy and investment planning policies
have to be enforced in order to form a security net for all involved parties.
Except for the financial and political challenges, floating wind energy faces multiple technical issues
as well. The Floating Wind Joint Industry Project (JIP) [26] has identified quite a few challenges. To
name a few, mooring system implementation, in terms of new materials and complexity of installation,
electrical systems, involving the dynamic power cables and existing infrastructure and transportation
challenges, related to the size of harbours, and vessels available. Furthermore, one of the main challenges
in deploying commercial-scale floating offshore wind is the need for validated numerical tools. Numerical
tools should be further improved in order to predict the complex dynamics of a FOWT and the wave-
structure interaction. Undoubtedly, experimental campaigns in lab-scale or full-scale, are necessary in
order to validate the numerical models. Nevertheless, a final validated numerical setup could reduce
by a lot the Capital Expenditure (CapEx) of a developer and therefore reduce the LCOE of FOWT.
Finally, floating offshore wind sets challenges to other wind energy fields as well, that require further
research and development. For instance, aerodynamics in terms of turbulence, loads and wakes and
control theory of wind turbines [2] .
1.3
Motivation and thesis structure
The research presented in this Thesis addresses some of the technical issues of Section 1.2.2 and more
specifically, the numerical modeling of the complex dynamics of FOWT system. This Master’s Thesis is
a direct continuation of an experimental campaign conducted in 2021 as part of the FloatStep research
project [5]. During the campaign, a 1:60 model of the DTU 10MW Reference Wind Turbine (RWT)

conditions at the test facilities of Danish Hydraulic Institute (DHI) and for different control strategies
so as to investigate the phenomenon of pitch instability.
The aim of the current research is to simulate the experimental wind and wave conditions tested in
[5], in an effort to acquire a validated numerical model that can eﬀiciently match the dynamic responses
of the physical system. In order to achieve that, the aerodynamics, hydrodynamics, mooring and control

2.1
Structural dynamics and Equation of Motion
One of the major differences between FOWTs and BFOWTs in terms of structural dynamics, is the
existence of rigid-body modes present in the former. A FOWT by definition floats in water regardless
of the stabilisation method, thus any exerted loads cause the entire system to move as a rigid body.
The simplest method of modeling the dynamic behaviour of a FOWT is by considering it a rigid
body with six degrees of freedom (DOFs), having three rotational and three translational motions. As
a matter of convention, surge is the translation in the direction of wind and wave, sway is the side to
side motion and heave corresponds to the vertical motion up and down. The rotational motions around
those three DOFs are called roll, pitch and yaw respectively. The DOFs are visualised in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Floating turbine motions represented as a rigid body. Figure taken from [2].

2.2
External loads on floating wind turbine
The EOM described in (2.2) provided an overview of the dominant external loads exerted on a FOWT
system. In principal, there are also other external loads that might influence the dynamic response of
a FOWT , due to its exposure to harsh environmental conditions. For instance, earthquakes, lightning
and ice are some of the less common categories of loads that might have to be modelled if necessary.
In general, the most important loads are aerodynamics, hydrodynamics and mooring reactions. A brief
description of those is given below.
2.2.1
Aerodynamic loads
The Blade Element Momentum Theory (BEMT) [27] is the simplest yet effective method of calculating
steady-state aerodynamic loads and estimating power and thrust curves when the operational parame-
ters of a wind turbine are known. These include the wind speed, the rotor speed and blade pitch angle.
In reality, however, the wind seen by a blade is stochastic, unstable and has time and spatial variations.
Hence, a more sophisticated model is required in order to capture the more complex aerodynamics. This
model is called unsteady Blade Element Momentum Theory (uBEMT). Moreover, the vibrating blades
and floater’s movement also affect the apparent wind seen by the blades, thus affecting the aerodynamic
loading. Consequently, uBEMT must be coupled with a structural model of the wind turbine in order
to develop an aero-elastic code that would compute more faithfully the behaviour of a physical wind
turbine. An example of the velocity triangle seen by a blade is shown in Figure 2.2. V0 is the free
stream velocity, Vrel is the relative velocity seen by the blade, W is the induced velocity, ˙θr is the
rotational velocity, ublade, is the blade’s velocity and uf is floater’s velocity caused by the translational
and rotational velocity components.
The aero-elastic code is often further improved in terms of aerodynamics by including models like
dynamic inflow, dynamic stall, Glauert’s correction, tower shadow effect or wind shear. Details on
these models can be found in [28]. Overall, there are several industry-standard softwares for aeroelastic

2.3
Control theory of wind turbines
Control systems are an essential part of wind turbines whether FOWT or BFOWT. The main goal of a
control system would be the stable and above all, safe operation of wind turbine. For this reason, the
purpose and functionality of a control system varies depending on the existing conditions. Under nor-
mal operation conditions, the aim would be to maximize power output or to keep power and rotational
speed within set operational limits in order to reduce the loads. A well-functioning controller though,
should be able to provide safety systems like emergency shutdown and alarms. The main features of a
control system would be torque, pitch or yaw control. The most important aspects of control theory
will be discussed briefly.

FOWT control strategies
So far the basics of control theory have been mentioned. These are applied to both fixed and floating
wind turbines. Nevertheless, a direct application of classic control strategies to floating turbines might
lead to a phenomenon called pitch instability. Pitch instability occurs due to the pitch motion of the
system especially when operating slightly above the rated wind speed and results in high amplitude
motion. If left uncontrolled pitch instability may lead to system failure.
Considering a typical thrust-wind speed curve, in the above rated region the gradient is negative.
This means that when a FOWT pitches forward into the wind then the blades’ relative wind speed
increases which leads to a decrease in thrust. On the other hand, when a FOWT pitches backwards, in
the downwind direction, then the relative wind speed decreases, which results in an increase of thrust
force applied at the rotor. In both cases the turbine’s motion is amplified and can lead to unstable
operational conditions since the aerodynamic damping is negative (in phase with the motion).
==PAGE:31==
2.4 Related literature review
15
As a result, the control strategies for FOWT should be adjusted. One solution would be to detune a
standard controller. This means changing the natural frequency of the controller, thus its proportional
and integral gains, at a value lower than that of pitch frequency. In so doing the effect of pitch instability
can be prevented. However, the pitch frequency of a FOWT is typically very low and this would result
in a very slow controller as well.
A slow controller cannot properly react to fast changes in wind
speed, for instance in wind gusts, and this leads to overshoots in rotor speed that might damage the
generator. Moreover, a slow controller reaction to wind changes would also affect the loads exerted
on the structure, possibly increasing their extreme value. Even though rotational overshoots could be
handled by decreasing the generator’s rated speed, this would also take its toll on the power output.
So, detuning a controller might be a first relatively easy approach in dealing with pitch instability, but
there are many aspects to be taken into account.
Another way of dealing with pitch instability was presented in [41]. The proposal was the addition
of a tower velocity loop into a conventional (fast) PI controller, meaning that a new gain is multiplied
by the tower-top fore-aft velocity and added to (2.11) (or (2.12)) resulting in :

2.4
Related literature review
The opportunities and advantages of floating offshore wind have been presented in Section 1.2 followed
by the complex dynamics that define a FOWT in Sections 2.1 to 2.3. For these reasons, both academia
and industry have shown a strong interest in developing numerical tools and conducting experiments
that would eventually aid for a better understanding of floating technology. Numerical tools can be
fast and cost-eﬀicient, but in order for them to be trustworthy as well, comparison to experimental
measurements is necessary. Therefore, researchers around the globe have been trying to validate their
numerical tools. In this section, some of the validation efforts throughout the years will be presented.
In 2013, the university of Maine tested a 1:50 scale model of the NREL 5MW RWT mounted on top
of the DeepCwind floating platform [42]. The experiments were conducted at the Maritime Research In-
stitute Netherland’s offshore wave basin and they included regular waves-only, irregular waves-only and
a combination of wind and waves test conditions. The numerical tool used to validate the experiments
was FAST, that was able to predict accurately the responses for wind-only conditions when comparing
time-series plots, Power Spectral Density (PSD) plots and general statistics. For the wave cases, the
linear wave energy frequency response was adequately captured but second-order difference-frequency
responses were not always present in the simulations.
In 2016, a 1:60 version of the DTU 10MW RWT mounted on a TLP floater was tested at DHI in
wind and wave conditions [43]. The aero-elastic code used for the validation was Flex5, with Morison
equation and unsteady BEM being integrated in the code. The numerical results were in good agree-
ment for surge and heave responses while pitch, nacelle acceleration and mooring line tensions appeared
to have some discrepancies. It was also observed that the addition of wind improved the accuracy of
the model. During the same year, at NREL the aero-elastic code FAST was utilised to compare the
measured response of Statoil’s Hywind Demo spar offshore wind turbine [44]. The numerical model
was set up to similar wind and wave conditions and produced statistically similar wave responses to the

experimental data. Only yaw and roll response at low frequencies seemed to deviate.
One year later, the triple spar campaign was underway. It featured a 1:60 DTU 10MW RTW with
the Triple Spar floater [45]. The case studies included both wind and wave simulations, regular, ir-
regular and focused. Moreover, three different control strategies were implemented, fixed blade pitch,
land-based controller and a tuned one to counteract the pitch instability issue. The numerical simula-

in good agreement with the experimental responses and nacelle acceleration. This gave the necessary
motivation to expand the research for irregular waves and wind conditions.
In 2018, the University of Tokyo put their in-house code, NK-UTWind, developed with the help
of ClassNK, to the test by comparing it to a 1:50 scale semi-submersible floater with 2MW wind tur-
bine located at the Ocean Engineering Basin of National Maritime Research Institute of Japan [46].
The study was mainly focused on wind and wave misalignment even though aligned cases were also
examined. Simulations were able to reproduce the floater’s motions well and showed no coupling of the
floater motions under misaligned cases. The energy of the responses was mainly in the natural frequency
of each degree of freedom and wave spectrum. At the same year, a numerical model of a 1:50 scaled
5MW OC3-Hywind wind turbine setup in FAST, was validated against experimental measurements of
the physical system taken at State Key Lab of Ocean Engineering at Shanghai Jiao Tong University
[47].The examined cases included wind and wave conditions, both regular and irregular. Some of the
conclusions of the said study were that FAST could simulate well the responses within the wave fre-
quency range of the in-plane degrees of freedom. However, the energy in surge natural frequency was
not well captured, thus affecting the mooring line tension responses as well. Moreover, some differences
between the responses were observed with respect to the second-order difference frequency range.
In 2019, a numerical study of 1:60 DTU 10MW RWT on TetraSpar platform was performed [48].
The code of the model was HAWC2 and various wind and waves conditions were examined. After
calibration the numerical model seemed to under predict the in-plane motions in some cases especially
for milder sea states. Furthermore, some uncertainties in the wind field inputs of the model were noted
due to manual calibration.
Finally in 2020, a 1:40, 750 kW semi-submersible model was experimentally tested in the Ocean
Engineering wide tank of the University of Ulsan and validated based on FAST code [49]. The model
was re-scaled from the initial campaign [50] due to slamming phenomena in extreme wave conditions.
Surge and heave responses in regular waves were well matched even though mean response of pitch were
slightly underestimated probably due to non-linear hydrodynamic loading in the low-frequency response.
Lastly, a 1:35 froude scaled 5 MW turbine on a TLP platform was numerically reproduced in [51]. The
FOWT design was called AAUE-TLP which had the advantage of individual pitch control. Some of
the most interesting findings of the said study were that the numerical model could not adequately
estimate the 1P and 3P excitation from the rotating experimental rotor in wind-only tests. In addition,
the theoretical aerodynamic performance was overestimated compared to the experimental one. The
same trend continued for the wind and wave testings.

Lab controller tuning
One of the main goals of the 2021 campaign was to design and test different control strategies in order
to investigate the effect of pitch instability in the above rated wind speed region. Hence, it is deemed
necessary to briefly mention the two control strategies that were eventually selected for the conduction
of experiments as those controllers were used in the numerical simulations as well.
The first thing that should be mentioned is that both controllers follow constant torque control for
the full load region (see Section 2.3). The main controller used for the wind and waves simulations was
based on the Basic DTU Wind Energy Controller [56]. However, this version had different KP and KI

will be named like that for the remaining of this report.

implemented, was the addition of a tower top gain, Ktt, on the fast but unstable basic controller. This
tower top gain resulted in a faster controller than A003 but still able to cope with pitch instability [5].
Both controllers were manually calibrated during the experiments by using a HAWCStab2 onshore
turbine model. Calibrating further the gains of the selected controllers was not part of this project,

Numerical Model in HAWC2
The Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation, commonly knows as HAWC2 [29],
was the aero-elastic code used for the implementation of the numerical model. Parts of the model such
as the scaled DTU 10MW RWT or mooring lines module were already created by assistant professor
Pegalajar-Jurado Antonio, while other parts such as hydrodynamic elements or wind field implementa-
tion were part of this dissertation. Nevertheless, the structure of the numerical model was thoroughly
studied. Software’s manual [57] was extensively examined for setting up modules related to hydrody-
namic and aerodynamic loading. The former were obtained through ESYSWamit module and Morison
equation drag term in order to capture viscous effects. The latter were calculated based on the uBEMT
of HAWC2. In this chapter important aspects of the methodology that was followed will be presented.

In Section 3.2 a brief description of the TetraSub floater parts was given. The dimensions of each

coordinates in HAWC2 coordinate system as described in 4.1.1. Afterwards, it was decided to model

the floater’s geometry were disregarded in an attempt to not increase the complexity of the numerical
model.
In principal the application of WAMIT hydrodynamic outputs in ESYSWamit module does not
require any part of the floater to be modelled in HAWC2 as the data can be imported directly and
applied on a chosen node of the modeled structure. WAMIT, however, being a potential flow solver is
not able to capture any viscous forcing or damping [32]. Therefore, the aforementioned floater parts were
modeled as hydro-elements [57] in HAWC2 in order to calculate the drag-term of Morison’s formula and
thus capture viscous effects. In so doing, drag coeﬀicient, Cd and axial drag coeﬀicient Cdaxial needed to
be specified for each element. Even thought the geometry of the heave plates was not directly modeled
in HAWC2, their effect was taken into account by increasing Cdaxial of the cylindrical tanks and center
column.
Inspiration for estimating the values of axial and transversal drag coeﬀicients was drawn by [59].
There are three main non-dimensional parameters when describing the flow induced by the oscillation
of a body [60]. Those are Reynolds number (Re), Keulegan-Carpenter number (KC) and frequency

The aerodynamic blade loads for the cases with wind, were calculated using the uBEMT code of HAWC2.
Thankfully, the aero-elastic code offers a variety of options when it comes to aerodynamic modelling.

4.2.1

The first step in trying to match the experimental and numerical wind fields was to figure out the wind
shear during experiments. In this part the methodology that was followed will be explained.
During the experimental campaign, wind calibration tests were conducted for four different mean

were selected to cover the range of ECs in terms of hub-height wind speed. (see Tables 3.3 and 3.5)
Therefore, the experimental data for each mean wind speed were available in seven different heights,
thanks to the two configurations of wind probes and five different horizontal positions (P1 to P5) as it
was explained in Section 3.4 and can be seen in Figure 3.4. The coordinates in height (y) and horizontal
distance (x) are shown below for convenience.
– x = [-1.8, -0.9, 0, 0.9, 1.8] m
– y = [0.25, 0.85, 1.45, 2.05, 2.65, 3.25, 3.85] m

positions, ranging from −1.8 m to 1.8 m. Looking at Figure 3.4, it is noticed that only one wind probe
is available at that height, from configuration 2. Another example would be the position at hub height.

4.2.2
Mann box parameters calibration
The next step after calibrating wind shear for the numerical model, was to approach the experimental
wind frequency spectrum. For this goal to be achieved, an investigation about power spectral density
(PSD) of experimental measurements and wind speed spectra produced by the wind module of HAWC2
was carried out. In so doing, the necessary inputs in the numerical model were determined. Once more,
as in Section 4.2.1, the data from wind calibration tests were utilised.
The turbulence generator code used, which is a build-in feature of HAWC2, is the Mann model.
The basic principles of Mann model is isotropic turbulence in neutral atmospheric conditions and the
energy spectrum is given based on the Von Karman spectrum [57]. Isotropic turbulence results to vortex
structures being circular, because the properties of turbulence like the length scale and variance remain
identical for all three directions.
For setting up the Mann model in the numerical simulations, there were a few parameters to be
considered. First of all, the center of the Mann box was placed exactly at the rotor center at hub height
and its dimensions were set in such way that the 4 m x 4 m combined outlet of the wind generator
was covered.(see Section 3.4) Since the Mann box is centered at hub height, the decision was made to
utilize the wind measurements acquired from the wind prob set at hub height as well, but this time
only configuration 1 from Figure 3.3 was used. In addition, in an effort to increase robustness of results,
three different positions P2, P3 and P4 were considered. (see Figure 3.4) The positions at the edges P1
and P5 were disregarded for the same reasons as in Section 4.2.1.
Apart from placing the Mann box correctly there are a few key inputs that need to be discussed.
The first one is length scale L, then αϵ2/3 parameter where α is a scaling constant and ϵ the dissipation
rate [66] and finally the shear blocking factor Γ. The initial suggested values according to HAWC2
manual for these parameters would be:
– L = 33.6
==PAGE:47==
4.2 Aerodynamics and wind field generation
31
– αϵ2/3 = 1
– Γ = 3.9 for non-isotropic turbulence and normal atmospheric conditions
Nevertheless, these suggested parameters cannot be directly implemented in the numerical model,
since they refer to a full-scale model and normal atmospheric conditions. Therefore, length scale had
to be adjusted and Γ was set to 0 as preliminary calibration attempts proved that such value gave
more accurate results and would better match the wind field generated by the wind generator. Fi-
nally, the HAWC2 command dont_scale was activated, so that the turbulence intensity be influenced
indirectly by calibrating αϵ2/3. To sum up, the inputs that were eventually calibrated were L and αϵ2/3.
At this point the methodology and general idea behind the Mann parameters calibration should be
explained. It has already been mentioned that the experimental data came from the wind calibration
test as in Section 4.2.1. It was also mentioned that, only the data of the wind probe at hub height
and at three different horizontal locations were utilised from configuration 1. This meant that for every
location out of the three, the PSDs were calculated and then averaged in order to get the allocation of
energy at frequency domain. Since three different locations were used for exporting the experimental
PSDs, this meant that the numerical simulations had to be executed with three different seeds every
time, so that the comparison between numerical model and experiments would be less biased.
In theory, the averaged numerical and experimental PSDs should match as close as possible both in
terms of energy content (total PSD area) and in terms of frequency distribution. The latter means that
even if the areas were identical but the frequency distribution between them was off, then the numerical
spectrum was deemed inadequate. Furthermore, initial calibration attempts revealed that the energy
content of the Mann box for higher frequencies than 1 Hz seemed to be substantially lower than that
of the experiments. Hence, the frequency range of interest was limited to [0 −1] Hz. The range itself
might seem small, however, it is approximately two times higher than the highest natural frequency of
the system. Consequently a good match of averaged PSDs within the [0 −1] Hz range was accepted
and any difference below 5% of the PSD areas ratio was deemed adequate.
In Tables 4.3 to 4.6 some of the calibration trials are shown for the four different mean wind speeds
at hub height. The last case (Case 4) in every table, was the one selected for any further simulations
with wind at the corresponding mean wind speed, as the ratio was under 5%. When calibrating a
new wind speed, the parameters from the previous one were the first ones to be tested as can be
verified by comparing Case 1 to Case 4 of previous mean wind speed. In general, it was noticed that
by increasing wind speed both the length scale L and parameter αϵ2/3 had to be increased. Since the
option dont_scale was activated in HAWC2, the produced turbulence intensity was not constant and it
was necessary to be checking its value, thus the values of mean turbulence intensity are also presented.
The selected parameters would produce turbulence intensity levels of 3% to 3.41% which agrees with
the observations made in [5], of low turbulence intensity within the rotor area of around 3%.

The EOM in (2.2) contains a damping matrix B which consists of different sources, namely aerodynamic,
hydrodynamic and structural. The hydrodynamic damping can be decomposed in a radiation term and
a viscous term as seen in (4.3):
B = Baero + Bhydro + Bstruct = Baero + Brad + Bvisc + Bstruct
(4.3)
From (4.3), Brad was based on the radiation-diffraction solution from WAMIT analysis while Bvisc
was calculated by the drag term of Morison’s equation (2.4) as such:

The calibration was carried out in ESYSWamit module of HAWC2 by adding hydrodynamic damp-

Based on (4.5), the linear damping term is propor-

This meant that damping elements in the main diagonal of matri-
ces Blin and Bquad as [B11, B22, B33, B44, B55, B66] were added, influencing directly the DOFs :
[surge, sway, heave, roll, pitch, yaw] respectively. However, due to the offset of the center column be-
tween the back sets of tanks of the floater (see Figure 3.1) there was strong coupling between the DOFs,
which lead to the addition of cross-coupled elements during the calibration.
The methodology of calibration is briefly described here and will be extensively described in the re-
lated chapters. Initial calibration attempts began from the decay tests, where both linear and quadratic
hydrodynamic damping were added in multiple DOFs. Afterwards, wave-only conditions were tested
and calibrated, moving on to wind-only cases and finally wind and waves combined. For any case that
included waves there were a couple of assumptions made. Firstly, it was noticed that the effect of
added linear damping was much greater than that of quadratic. Therefore, one assumption was that
any quadratic terms resulted from decays, remained fixed for the subsequent simulations. Secondly,
since wave heading was 0◦, emphasis was given on calibrating the responses of the in-plane motions
i.e. surge, heave and pitch. Thus, the main elements that were altered B11, B33, B55 and their cross-
coupled elements like B15, B35 for surge-pitch and heave-pitch respectively, in an attempt to keep the
calibration process simple yet effective.
All other cross-coupled out-of-plane elements that resulted
from calibration of decay testing (see Chapter 5) were disregarded. In general, the calibration of the
aforementioned elements was executed manually and numerical responses for the in-plane motions were
compared to experimental measurements in time and frequency domain. Some important statistics
were also monitored, mainly the error of standard deviation (σ) of responses. The hydrodynamic damp-
ing implementation aimed to reduce the error of σ and therefore, achieve a close match in the energy
content of numerical and experimental responses. The energy content is related to the area under the
curve of PSD plots. Additionally, σ is related to the variance of the signals as σ2, which in turn is equal

Second-order hydrodynamics implementation
The initial approach with regards to wave loads, was to take into consideration only the first-order wave
forcing. This approach, however, lead to discrepancies between the numerical model and experimental
results in most ECs. The effect of second-order loads especially those at diff-frequencies range cannot
be ignored and should be included for an accurate reproduction of low frequencies motion of the system

As a result, the second approach would utilize the full quadratic transfer
functions (QTF) taken from WAMIT with the aid of assistant professor Antonio Pegalajar-Jurado.
The implementation of full QTFs however, lead to undesired responses which damping calibration alone

experimental responses much more eﬀiciently, thus was selected for all numerical simulations.

CHAPTER 5
System identification
In this chapter the system identification will be presented. This includes calibration of decay tests
and estimation of natural frequencies of the TetraSub 10MW floating wind turbine in all DOFs. The
methodology followed during the decay calibration will be discussed. Moreover, decays of numerical
model in surge, heave and pitch will be presented since the overall analysis of the current project
focused on the in-plane DOFs. Finally, the resulting added damping matrices, linear and quadratic,
will be shown.
5.1
Decay tests and natural frequencies
Free decay tests were conducted in order to identify the six natural frequencies of the floater. In most
cases an initial displacement was imposed to the structure and then released back to equilibrium position.
For instance, surge and sway decay tests were executed by attaching ropes in the physical model and
then pulling in the respective direction. Similarly, a rope was attached to the tower-top and then pulled
for pitch and roll decay tests, while heave testing was performed by manually pushing downwards from
the nacelle [5]. The decays were performed in still water and the turbine was not operating at the time.
Decays were simulated in HAWC2 as well. The first step was to obtain the initial displacements of
the physical model. This was achieved by applying external forces or moments to the corresponding
DOFs in the model. The rotor was considered locked and wave kinematics were not included to abide by
the still water conditions of the lab. After achieving the initial displacements, the focus was on obtaining
the correct natural frequencies. This meant that calibration of damping elements, both diagonal and
off-diagonal ones, was necessary as discussed in Section 4.3. Damping was introduced in the form of
both linear and quadratic matrices. Apart from calibrating damping elements, viscous damping from
Morison equation was also included as explained in 4.1.2. It should be noted that the added viscous
damping from Morison equation in the case of decays, came from the neglected quadratic term of (4.4),
which is proportional to ˙ξ2, since no wave kinematics exist for decays.
Even though all 6 DOFs were calibrated and their respective natural frequency was obtained in the
numerical model, only the three in-plane DOFs surge, heave and pitch decay tests will be presented
in full. Decay results for the out-of-plane motions will only be shown in the corresponding DOF of
the decay test.
The full decay analysis of the out-of-plane DOFs can be found in Appendix A. In
Section 4.3 it was mentioned that each sea state had to be calibrated individually, as also stated in
[35]. Nevertheless, the linear and quadratic matrices were the same for all six decay tests, since the still
water condition of each decay can be considered as a single sea state.
In Figures 5.1 to 5.3 decay comparisons are presented. The black line corresponds to the experimental
measurements while the orange one represents the numerical results.
The initial numerical results
without implementing damping through drag term of Morison’s equation and without calibrating, are
also presented in dashed blue line. Apart from the time series, PSD plots are also shown to demonstrate
the effect of resonance in the corresponding natural frequencies and provide information about cross
coupling effects. In addition, the mean value is removed for the three mooring lines (ML) in their
corresponding plots illustrating the tensions. The reason being a slight offset between the numerical
model and the experimental measurements. This small offset can be explained by considering the way
the pretension of 20 N was applied manually during the lab experiments, which might lead to some
uncertainties in acquiring the exact value for every decay test. Obviously, such issues do not arise in
the numerical problem where a pretension of exactly 20 N can be applied in every simulation.

In this chapter the results of the numerical simulations will be compared to the experimental measurements of
2021 campaign. The first comparisons to be presented will be the wave-only cases. Those are divided in regular,
irregular and focused waves. Afterwards, wind-only simulations will follow and their contribution to the final

6.1
Data post-processing
The results will be illustrated with time series of the responses, PSD plots and exceedance probability plots
depending on the examined case. To achieve that, a universal MATLAB script was set up that would load
both the results of HAWC2 and the corresponding experimental measurements. All data were transformed to
the global coordinate system, as demonstrated in Section 4.1.1. There was no filter used on the experimental
data, apart from when illustrating the wind turbine signals of rotor speed and fore-aft shear force, as will be
discussed in Section 6.3. For those, a 3rd-order low-pass Butterworth filter with a fcut = 4 Hz was implemented.
Finally, a moving average filter with 10 leading and lagging points was used for the PSD illustrations of both
experimental and numerical results, so as to obtain a better insight of the dominating frequencies in the time
series.

Wave-only cases
The response of the TetraSub 10MW to wave-only conditions is the first to be analyzed. In order to simulate
the experimental results as faithfully as possible, the wave elevation measurement from WG8 (see Figure 3.2)
had been imported to HAWC2 environment. This specific wave gauge was located at the same distance from
the wave maker as the TetraSub floater and 4 m away from it, therefore the wave field that was measured is
considered undisturbed from the system’s induced motions. In addition, the wind turbine was not operating
during the wave-only experiments, hence was considered locked in the numerical model as well.

It was stated in Section 2.2.2.1 that irregular waves can represent real-life sea states more eﬀiciently than regular
waves. The superposition of several waves with different wave heights and frequencies for irregular waves to be
created, is a stochastic process. As a result, apart from the time series and PSD plots that have been presented
so far, in the cases of irregular waves the exceedance probability P plot is added. Probability of exceedance is
defined as:
P = p (X ≥xi) = 1 −

N + 1
(6.1)
A MATLAB function was implemented that would receive a signal’s time-series and would locate the peaks
based on a minimum peak-to-peak distance, which was set at 1 s. In so doing, the maximum peaks are located
and stored. Then the peaks are sorted in ascending order and assigned an index i in order to compute exceedance
probability (6.1) of each peak based on its index i and the total number of peaks N.
The irregular states that were examined in this stage were: EC 6, EC 11 from 2017 campaign (see Table 3.3)
and all the ECs from 2021 campaign (see Table 3.5). Before going on to presenting some of the results however,
a few assumptions that were made during the simulations have to be mentioned. Firstly, as in the case of
regular waves, the surface elevation measurement from WG8 was imported into HAWC2. Secondly, for the
all the cases of irregular waves, second-order hydrodynamics were included. It was stated in Section 4.4 that
the initial approach of full QTFs was disregarded due to some nonphysical responses especially for pitch. This
specific DOF had the lowest natural frequency and was thus, more affected by the diff-frequency second order
loads. As a consequence the Newman approximation was used.
Newman approximation considerably improved the responses of the numerical model, still the very low-
frequency excitations caused by the second-order loads, persisted and calibrating linear or quadratic damping
alone did not seem to influence that region. As a result a high-pass, 3rd-order Butterworth filter was implemented
in the second-order loads calculated by Newman approximation.
The filtering provided the opportunity to
approach the experimental results more eﬀiciently. The diﬀiculty was selecting a lower limit fcut for the high
pass filter. Inspiration was drawn from [48] were the region of interest for calibrating wind field was:
frange = [0.7fnat_low −1.3fnat_high]
where fnat_high is the highest natural frequency of the system and fnat_low is the lowest.
For the case of
TetraSub 10MW the lowest natural frequency is that of pitch at 0.147 Hz. Based on this and also after running
all the simulations without any filter to acquire an overview of the energy content at low frequencies, it was
decided to apply fcut = 0.1 Hz. In Figures 6.5 and 6.6, the pitch response of EC C from 2021 and EC 11 from
2017 are shown as examples, with and without filtering of low-frequency signal. It is quite obvious that the
implementation of such high pass filter has affected the numerical response and drastically improved the energy
content comparison in the PSD plot.

The next step after calibrating the responses for wave-only conditions, was to evaluate the performance of the

For the numerical simulations emphasis was given to the base line controller A003 for wind-only simulaitons.
The D008 controller was mainly tested at the above rated region. Both controllers were imported in HAWC2
as external DLL files, which were provided by senior researcher Fanzhong Meng who was responsible for setting
up the controller during the experimental campaign of 2021.
The first approach when simulating wind-only cases was to make use of the calibrated damping matrix from
decays as shown in (5.1), since there were no incident waves. However, the results did not have the desired match.
This demonstrated the possible diﬀiculties that have to be faced when simulating a FOWT using uBEMT, so
it was decided to implement a few changes to the damping elements of ESYSWamit module. Nevertheless, the
main focus of wind-only simulations was not the calibration of damping per se, but the calibration of center of
buoyancy (CoB) of the floater. The reasoning behind calibrating the center of buoyancy will be explained.
It was observed in [5] that the TetraSub floater experienced quite large mean pitch angles of around 10◦at
rated wind speed. These large pitching angles meant that the water plane area of the floater, that is the area of
the surface intersecting with water, was constantly changing as the system was tilting. The TetraSub belongs
to the semi-sub category of floaters, thus it is water-plane stabilised (see Section 1.2.1). Therefore, the second
moment of area is changing with the changes in water plane area and so does the hydrostatic stiffness of the
system. This dependency of hydrostatic stiffness on the position of the floater, in terms of large pitch angles, is
non-linear.
As for the numerical analysis, the initial WAMIT results obtained at the equilibrium position could no longer
represent the tilted system. Consequently, it was decided to have a new WAMIT analysis provided by assistant
professor Antonio Pegalagar-Jurado. The mean pitch angle of 8.7◦was selected, which was determined based
on the mean pitch angles of the three wind-only tests for wind speeds below,at and above rated. Nevertheless,
WAMIT assumes small displacements and models hydrostatic stiffness linearly [58]. As a result, even with the
new WAMIT analysis introduced, trying to represent the highly non-linear hydrostatic pitch restoring coeﬀicient
of the physical model within a broad range of pitch angles by assuming linearity, still led to discrepancies
between the numerical and experimental results. Consequently, calibration of the distance of CoB from the
center column was chosen as a way to affect the pitch restoring moment of the system and thus the hydrostatic
restoring coeﬀicient based on (6.2):
C5 = mg(zb −zg) + ρgI11ξ5
(6.2)
where zb is the distance of the CoB from the center column, zg is the distance of the center of gravity, I11 the
second moment of area about y-axis (in global coorinate system) and ξ5 the pitch response.
The aim of CoB calibration was to achieve a maximum error of 5% in mean pitch response. In Table 6.11,
the effect that calibrated CoB had on the measured mean pitch of the model is shown. The experimental mean
values are also presented for comparison. From a maximum initial error of almost 61% for the lowest wind
speed, the model has approached the experimental results for pitch with a maximum error of 2.6% occurring at
the highest wind speed.
As for the extend of changes that had to be made to the original CoB, Table 6.12 demonstrates that the
calibrated values deviated by less than 5%. Therefore, it was assumed that it would have no major impact on

Wind and waves cases
The final step after having calibrated the wind field parameters, hydrodynamic damping from waves-only cases
and also the center of buoyancy from wind-only simulations, was to evaluate the performance of the model
under normal operating conditions, i.e. for simultaneous wind and wave conditions. The wind and wave cases

5 min was removed from PSD plots and statistics calculation.

A few interesting observations arise from Figure 6.21 regarding EC 3 when illustrating the wind turbine
signals. Firstly, the blade pitch is constant and set at 0.79◦as expected, since the wind speed is below rated
and the turbine operates in the optimal CP tracking region. Secondly, the numerical sensor of fore-aft shear
even though it follows the same trend as the experimental measurements, it underestimates the energy content
within approximately [1−1.5] Hz. This is were the majority of the wave’s spectrum is located. The explanation
for this would be the fact that the fore-aft sensor measures the total force in the horizontal direction, thus
not only measuring thrust force. The tilt of the rotor creates a gravitational load that is also included in the
total measured force. Additionally, the floater’s dynamic motion especially in the fore-aft direction, i.e. pitch,
provokes inertial effects from acceleration that are also accounted for. Therefore by comparing the numerical
and experimental responses in pitch, from Figure 6.17, it can be seen in the PSD plot of pitch DOF, that the
model underestimates the energy content from wave frequency region. This underestimation of pitch energy
content in wave’s spectrum, is possibly depicted in the shear fore-aft PSD plot as well.
A final interesting
observation about fore-aft shear sensor was the divergence in the mean value of the force. The lacking wave
excitation might explain the dynamic miss-match of the two signals, but the offset in mean value is believed
to be derived mainly from thrust. Given that both numerical and experimental systems have approximately
the same tilt angle in Figure 6.17, the explanation for this offset may lay in the aerodynamic performance of
the model. In [68], it was stated that the characteristic of the lift coeﬀicient is not linear in the low Reynolds
number regime, especially around 50.000 and the aerodynamic coeﬀicients cannot be analyzed using a single
aerodynamic derivative dCL/dα for angles of attack 0◦up to the critical angle, as it is usually the case for
the high Reynolds numbers the full-scale turbines operate.
As shown in Section 4.1, the average Reynolds
lays within this area of 50.000 for the experiments. The current numerical model, however, assumes linearity
for the calculation of aerodynamic coeﬀicients, while the physical blade was designed based on interpolation of
coeﬀicients along its segments. This possible miss-match of numerical and experimental aerodynamic coeﬀicients
would affect the thrust and it is believed to be a valid reason the sensors have a mean offset. Further research
is needed on this matter to assert a solid conclusion though.

Major differences can be noticed in the wind turbine signals, namely the rotor speed and blade pitch angle.

contrary, when blade pitch is determined by controller D008, it is dominated by pitch natural frequency as in
Figure 6.23(b). In both cases the numerical model was able to reproduce the measured signals pretty accurately.

CHAPTER7
Conclusion
In this Master’s Thesis a numerical model of a 1:60 scaled DTU 10MW RWT mounted on the TetraSub concept
floater, provided by SOT, was investigated and validated against experimental measurements.
Those were
acquired from the campaign conducted in 2021 as part of the FloatStep research project [5]. The goal was to
replicate the wind and wave conditions measured in the test basin of DHI, in an effort to acquire a numerical
model capable of matching the dynamic responses of the lab-scaled physical system. The aero-hydro-elastic
code used for the numerical simulations was HAWC2.
Firstly, a theoretical overview of the dynamic modelling of a FOWT was presented, along with the major
external loads exerted on the structure. Two different control strategies were also briefly analyzed, a classical
control strategy most commonly used in wind turbines and a more novel strategy suitable for FOWTs including
a tower-top velocity loop to counter the effect of pitch instability in the above rated region. The next step
was to present the experimental setup of the 2021 campaign as this provided useful insights in setting up the
numerical model. A few key components were discussed, including the scaled model of the DTU 10MW RTW
and the TetraSub floater. Moreover, some information was given on the test facility, which revolved around the
wave basin and wave gauges and the wind generator with the wind probes used for wind data acquisition. The
environmental states that were tested were also mentioned. These formed the basis of the numerical simulations
and included ECs with waves-only, regular, irregular and focused, wind-only tests and the combination of wind
and waves which were the major investigation of the current project.
Having presented the experimental setup, a description of the numerical implementation of it followed. The
difference among the various coordinate systems was demonstrated like the one HAWC2, ESYSWamit module
and experimental data used. Attention was given especially to the numerical modelling of the TetraSub floater
in HAWC2. The most major components of it, i.e. the three sets of tanks and center column were modeled
as hydro-elements [57] to account for viscous effects via the Morison’s equation drag term while the PF solver
WAMIT outputs were utilised include first- and second- order wave excitations. Before running any simulations,
a few important aspects of aerodynamics and hydrodynamics implementation were clarified. Regarding the for-
mer, the numerical wind field had to match as closely as possible the experimental one. Therefore, calibration
both of wind shear and parameters of wind module of HAWC2, called Mann model, were necessary. Wind
shear was manually calibrated based on the power law used internally in HAWC2. The numerical results were
compared to experimental measurements from wind probes, indicating that a wind shear exponent α = 0.38
would suﬀiciently reproduce the wind shear present in the lab. The calibration of Mann parameters was based
on matching the energy content of power spectral density plots of the measured data for four different mean
wind speeds at hub height. The selected numerical parameters achieved a similarity with a standard deviation
error of 1.47% to 2.97% compared to experiments. As for hydrodynamics, the methodology of extra linear
and quadratic damping in ESYSWamit module of HAWC2 was described along with the second-order loads
imported. A full QTF analysis was available, but it was decided to use Newman approximation instead, due to
nonphysical results of the former that subsequent calibration attempts did not seem to have an effect on.
The first area of focus was system identification. The numerical model simulated the decay tests in all six
DOFs and the natural frequencies of the system were obtained. All six numerical decays had a good match with
the experimental ones. This was proved by the estimation of natural frequencies where all six of them were
under the initial target of 5%. More specifically, the numerical natural frequencies deviated just by 1.15% to
1.98% with the only exception being surge natural frequency with an error of 3.57%. The linear and quadratic
extra elements for the decays were presented indicating a strong coupling between the DOFs.
Afterwards, the main simulations began.
The focus of this Master’s Thesis were the responses of the
TetraSub 10MW in the in-plane direction i.e. surge, heave and pitch. Therefore any out-of-plane cross coupled
elements were disregarded. The extra quadratic damping terms were considered constant and only extra linear
damping was calibrated as it had a more profound effect on system’s responses. The first ECs to be simulated

were regular wave-only conditions. Regular waves cases were the only cases that did not include any second
order loads. For most cases the numerical model was able to accurately reproduce the experimental results
like ECs G,H and I of Table 3.4 while EC J demonstrated the importance of damping calibration when the
wave frequency matches one of the system’s natural frequencies. In this case, surge natural frequency was close
to wave’s frequency, therefore, after calibrating B11 and B15 elements for surge and surge-pitch coupling, the
responses were suﬀiciently matched.
After the regular wave-only simulations, irregular waves were examined. The examined cases were EC 6, EC
11 from 2017 (see Table 3.3) and all of 2021 cases (see Table 3.5). Initial attempts with Newman approximation
proved that there was high uncertainty about the second-order loads of TetraSub 10 MW. Low frequency excita-
tions, in most cases, even by using Newman approximation that typically underestimates the loads compared to
full QTF analysis [35] proved to be unrealistically high compared to experimental measurements and calibration
of damping did not seem to have an effect on such low frequency region. Hence, it was decided to implement
a 3rd-order Butterworth high-pass filter with a fcut = 0.1 Hz in the second-order loads before importing them
to HAWC2. This approach drastically improved the results. The numerical PSD energy content by means of
standard deviation and probability of exceedance of the responses was monitored and compared to experiments
in an effort to get a close match. This was achieved by manually adjusting the extra linear damping elements
for each case.
In general for the irregular wave cases, the numerical responses were capable of following the trends of the
experimental measurements. The surge absolute standard deviation error ranged from 0.87%-9.85% across all
cases, with 95th percentile absolute error being from 10%-20%. Heave response standard deviation varied from
1%-14% for most cases, with EC A showing the worst percentage of around 20% due to some underestimation
of the wave’s energy spectrum and the cross-coupling effect from pitch. The 95th percentile varied from 2.5%-
20%. Generally, surge and heave responses were slightly underestimated by the model, the main reason being
the missing excitation force from the wave frequency spectrum. Is some cases like EC C or EC D the wave’s
energy was captured more effectively compared to EC A and EC 6. This could possibly be explained because
the hydrodynamic coeﬀicients Cd and Cdaxial were considered fixed for all environmental conditions and were
not calibrated for each individual case. As for pitch, having the lowest natural frequency of the system, made
its calibration more challenging since it was mostly affected by second-order loads. Therefore, pitch response
was typically overestimated, its standard deviation would vary from +7%-+28%. The missing wave forcing,
especially for surge was evident in the extra linear damping term B11. This element would have negative values
across all ECs, ranging from 0 to −18
kg/s. This in turn would lead to underestimation of tensions in the
mooring system, since mooring lines were heavily influenced by surge motion and the incident wave.
Such negative damping values had to be examined in the corresponding decay test, to check whether they
would result in an unrealistic addition of energy in the system and unrestrained responses. However, the decay
results proved that the system remained suﬀiciently damped. One final interesting observation, regarding the
irregular waves simulations and the second order implementation, was the effect the mean value of second-order
loads had on the numerical results. The DOF of pitch was especially influenced by that. When filtering the
second-order loads with a high pass filter, the mean value of the signal would be rejected. Nevertheless, in an
effort to stay true to the original loads, having already filtered out the low frequencies, the mean valued was
added again. This influenced the severe sea states the most. For instance the 50-year sea state EC E would
have a mean pitch of −0.202◦with mean second-order load filtered out, while mean pitch would reach −7.364◦
with mean second-order load added back to the filtered signal.
A special case of waves, called focused waves was always examined. Only a couple of cases were tested
however, namely EC 6 and EC 11 from 2017. The initial simulations would make use of the calibrated damping
elements from irregular waves cases, but the negative values for surge damping element would lead to a miss
match between the model and the physical system. Therefore, some calibration was executed, using positive
values this time to dampen out the motion. The model would capture the extreme wave excitation and follow
the PSD peaks in the corresponding DOFs especially for surge and pitch. Heave’s cross-coupling with pitch was
underestimated.
After the investigation of wave-only cases was over, numerical simulations for wind-only cases were executed
for three different wind speeds below, at and above rated. The initial plan of using the damping from decays
for those tests, since there were no incident wave, led to big discrepancies with the experimental results. This
was an indication of the challenges that uBEMT faces when calculating the loads for a FOWT system, thus
was decided to implement a few changes via hydrodynamic damping. During the wind-only simulations the two
different control strategies were introduced, the baseline detuned controller and the faster tower-top velocity
loop controller. Yet, the main focus of wind-only simulations was to calibrate the CoB of the system. The
==PAGE:89==

reasoning behind this calibration was the fact that the numerical model would overestimate the mean pitch of the
floater. This phenomenon was because of the modelling of hydrostatic stiffness linearly, one of WAMIT analysis
assumptions, while the physical model would have a highly non-linear hydrostatic pitch restoring coeﬀicient for
the wide range of tilt angles it experienced. Even though, a new WAMIT analysis was introduced in HAWC2
for a mean tilt angle of 8.7%, still the mean numerical pitch would vary between 17% −61% depending on the
case, from the measured data. Therefore, by calibrating the CoB of the model by less than 5%, it was able to
match the experimental mean pitch angle of the floater within 0.1% −2.6%.
After some calibration the numerical model was able to follow the trends of the physical one, in terms of
responses, yet pitch seemed to be more heavily influenced by the numerical wind field compared to measure-
ments. Mooring line tensions, on the other hand, were better tracked for wind-only simulations than waves-only
for most cases other than the lowest wind speed of 1.1 m/s. For this specific case, it seemed that the experi-
mental ML 2 and ML 3 were heavily influenced by out-of-plane DOFs of roll and yaw, having sharp peaks in
0.22 Hz and 0.29 Hz respectively, along with 1-P excitation at around 0.92 Hz, none of which were captured
by the model. When comparing the wind turbine signals of rotor speed, blade pitch and shear force in the
fore-aft direction, it was noticed that numerical blade pitch would be overestimated in the model, for the same
rotational speed. This was attributed to the existence of mechanical friction in the physical model, which was
not modelled in HAWC2, therefore friction and the counter-moment it created, would make the physical model
pitch less for the same rotational speed to be achieved. From examining the fore-aft shear force, it was noticed
that the numerical model does not capture the 1-P excitation that the physical one had. This 1-P excitation
is believed to be mainly caused by the existence of imbalances in the lab-scaled blades which does not exist in
the HAWC2 since they were identical.
The final part of this Master’s Thesis was dedicated to evaluating the performance of the model under
normal operating conditions i.e. wind and waves combined. The main focus once more was the in-plane DOFs
and the baseline controller. The simulations involved cases at low wind speeds, rated and above rated regions.
In general the model demonstrated good results when compared to the measurements. The standard deviation
error for surge would lay between 0.34% −15.3% while heave DOF was between 0.74% −14.24% for all tested
cases with the baseline controller. As for pitch standard deviation error would be 2% −11% for most cases
except for EC A which deviated by 30% as it seemed to be more heavily influenced by the numerical wind field.
The energy contents of PSD plots might have been close but some discrepancies were also obvious. For instance,
the wave’s frequency spectrum would be slightly underestimated by the model. Once more this was attributed
to the use of fixed hydrodynamic coeﬀicients for all sea states. Another reason would be that not all parts of
the TetraSub floater were modeled as hydro-elements in HAWC2, thus missing out on forcing introduced by
Morison equation drag-term. Moreover, it was observed in all DOFs and especially surge, that for increasing
sea state, the energy content at very low frequencies would be higher for the physical model. The main reason
for this is believed to be the under-prediction of aerodynamic loads of the FOWT from uBEMT, especially in
above rated conditions where large oscillation frequency occurs as was noticed in [67]. In addition, the lack of
actual wind speed measurements at hub during the wind and waves tests, increases the uncertainty with respect
to the calibrated Mann Box in HAWC2. It should also be pointed out that below rated wind speed simulations
indicated that the numerical rotor speed would have a greater mean value than the physical one. This could be
explained by the fact that the model over predicts the aerodynamic torque, operating under different optimal
K value, thus having a higher rotor speed. The physical model can not follow this behaviour due to mechanical
friction.
Lastly, the effect of the two different control strategies on the TetraSub 10MW system responses was exam-
ined. EC 5, which was slightly above rated at 1.68 m/s, was presented to show the aforementioned influence.
By comparing PSD plots, it was shown that the floater’s pitch response would be dominated by pitch natural
frequency when the tower-top loop controller was used and have slightly higher oscillations compared to the
baseline controller. This would also be the case when comparing rotor speed and blade pitch. In the case of
D008, the signals were heavily influenced by pitch natural frequency, as this novel control strategy is driven by
floater’s pitch motion. Whereas the baseline A003 controller would be more influenced by the wind spectrum.
The numerical implementation of both controllers was able to accurately reproduce the aforementioned effects
both for D008 and the sharp peak in pitch PSD and the wind influence of A003, even though there was some
over estimation in the latter caused by the numerical wind field. On top of this, the time series plots of the
signals, demonstrated a much more dynamic behaviour of rotor speed and blade angle when operating under
the fast D008 controller. On the other hand, the detuned, A003 would keep the blade pitch more constant due
to its slower reaction to wind changes. Both of these effects were also successfully captured by the numerical
controllers during the comparisons.

7 Conclusion
Suggestions for further research
It was quite obvious from the very first numerical simulations, the distinctive particularity the TetraSub
10MW has when it comes to second-order loads calculation. Newman approximation and filtering were utilised
in an effort to make the responses more in-line with the experimental measurements. However, comparisons
with other mid- or even high- fidelity engineering tools is suggested in order to derive concrete conclusions.
As far as the current model is concerned, a more thorough investigation of focused and especially misaligned
wave cases is recommended.
The former have a highly non-linear nature, however both the current model
setup and WAMIT analysis oriented more towards the more common irregular sea states thus making focused
waves an interesting topic for future research. As for the latter, even though they were out of the scope of
this Thesis, measurements for misaligned waves are available. An investigation of the model’s effectiveness on
such conditions would reveal the extent of cross-coupled effects that dominate the floater’s motions, including
out-of-plane DOFs. Furthermore, the calibration of wind field was executed manually through trial-and-error
in order to match the power spectral density of the wind in the lab. In order to minimize the uncertainties
around numerical wind field, it would be worth examining whether the constraint turbulence approach can be
implemented. This method can be used to simulate turbulence boxes constraint by measured data [69], which
may lead to more accurate results if applicable by the existing wind data.
From an experimental point of view, the fact that a TetraSub 10MW model in now available will also aid
any future experimental campaigns. The controllers’ gains were set up manually by trial-and-error during the
campaign, based on a HAWC2 model of the 1:60 TetraSpar floater having a 1:60 DTU 10MW RWT mounted
[5]. The existence of a TetraSub 10MW model would mean that the range for proportional and integral gains as
well as the tower-top velocity gain can be narrowed down more effectively. This would lead to a faster and more
accurate acquisition of the optimal combination of control parameters, which in turn will result in improved
controller performance.
