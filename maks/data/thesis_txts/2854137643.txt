Abstract
Floating oﬀshore wind farms have emerged as a promising solution for harnessing renewable en-
ergy in deep-water regions. However, wake eﬀects caused by turbine interactions signiﬁcantly
impact the overall eﬃciency of wind farms. Unlike ﬁxed-bottom turbines, ﬂoating oﬀshore wind
turbines possess repositioning capabilities, providing an opportunity to mitigate wake losses and
optimize wind farm performance.
In this thesis, the layout of the FOWF and the mooring system conﬁguration of each turbine
were adjusted to maximize annual energy production.
The optimization results showed that
without safety constraints, the annual energy production could be increased by 3%. Incorporating
practical constraints, such as avoiding mooring line overlap and ensuring suﬃcient anchor spacing,
slightly reduced the improvement to 2.994%, while enhancing system safety and operational
reliability. Meanwhile, a preliminary calculation indicates that shared anchors are economically
feasible.

1.1
Background
The global shift towards sustainable energy is accelerating, driven by both environmental and
economic pressures. The International Renewable Energy Agency predicts that renewable energy
could supply 90% of the world’s electricity needs by 2050, with wind energy playing a central role
in this transition [1]. Oﬀshore wind energy, in particular, has garnered increasing attention due
to its high potential for large-scale power generation, especially in regions with limited onshore
wind capacity[2]. As noted by the Global Wind Energy Council, oﬀshore wind installations are
expected to increase sixfold by 2030, driven by technological advancements and policy support[2].
Unlike onshore wind farms, oﬀshore wind farms beneﬁt from higher and more stable wind speeds,
leading to greater energy yield. According to WindEurope, oﬀshore wind farms also have the
potential to minimize land-use conﬂicts and visual impacts, making them a favorable option for
countries with dense populations or limited land availability[3]. Moreover, advancements in tur-
bine technology, such as the development of larger rotor diameters and more eﬃcient generators,
have further enhanced the competitiveness of oﬀshore wind energy[1].
Annual build-out of oﬀshore wind should ramp-up signiﬁcantly towards the end of the decade.
It is anticipated that installations over 2024-2030 to take the EU to 393 GW by the 2030. The
EU2030 target is 425 GW[4].
Figure 1.1: 2024-30 annual onshore and oﬀshore wind power installations in the EU[4]
However, the design and optimization of ﬂoating oﬀshore wind farms introduce new challenges
compared to traditional bottom-ﬁxed designs. Factors such as mooring line dynamics, platform
stability, and site-speciﬁc wind conditions require more sophisticated modeling and optimization
techniques[5]. Additionally, the costs associated with ﬂoating platforms and deeper water instal-
lations remain a barrier to widespread adoption, though these costs are expected to decline as
the technology matures and economies of scale are realized[6].

One-Dimensional Momentum Theory
The core of wind energy lies in eﬀectively capturing and converting its kinetic energy. Wind
turbines achieve this by slowing down the air that ﬂows through their blades, transforming part

of the kinetic energy into mechanical energy, which can then be converted to electricity. To max-

analyze the changes in speed and pressure as it passes through the turbine. In this context, the
one-dimensional momentum theory provides a fundamental framework to describe the interaction
between the wind turbine and the airﬂow, deriving the theoretical power limit of a turbine—the
Betz limit.
The 1D Momentum Theory assumes that the turbine blades can be idealized as an "actuator

deeper understanding of the physical limits of wind energy utilization, oﬀering theoretical support
for the design and optimization of wind power systems.

1.4
Floating Oﬀshore Wind Turbine
Figure 1.4: Diﬀerent FOWT design concepts. Source: [9]
Oﬀshore wind energy represents a sustainable source of renewable energy, generated by capturing
wind forces in oﬀshore environments where unobstructed airﬂows yield higher and more consistent
wind speeds. Recently, oﬀshore wind has gained signiﬁcant traction due to its enhanced power
potential compared to onshore wind. Before the advent of ﬂoating structures, the deployment
of wind turbines was limited to shallower waters and simpler seabed conditions, reliant on ﬁxed
foundations. However, with the development of ﬂoating platforms, anchored to the seabed by
ﬂexible anchors, chains, or steel cables, turbines can now be positioned further oﬀshore in deeper
waters. This advancement oﬀers multiple beneﬁts, such as access to steadier wind resources, min-
imized visual impact, and fewer restrictions on acoustic noise. In recent years, various FOWT
designs have been proposed, featuring diverse support platforms, anchoring, and mooring sys-
tems. These designs build upon ﬂoating support structures pioneered in the oﬀshore oil and gas
industry, including semi-submersibles, tension leg platforms (TLPs), and spar buoys[10]. The
following provides a concise overview of several types of ﬂoating wind turbines[11]:
The spar-type ﬂoating wind turbine is speciﬁcally designed for deployment in deep-water envi-
ronments. It consists of a ﬂoater, a tower, and a rotor-nacelle assembly. The ﬂoater typically
comprises a steel or concrete cylindrical structure ﬁlled with water and gravel ballast, which en-
sures that the center of gravity remains well below the center of buoyancy. This conﬁguration
enhances the turbine’s stability and mitigates the risk of capsizing. Such a design enables the
turbine to maintain an upright position on the water surface, providing signiﬁcant resistance to
pitch and roll motions. Furthermore, the spar-type turbine can yaw freely to align with the wind
direction, thereby optimizing energy capture and enhancing overall eﬃciency.
The TLP type ﬂoating wind turbine is a ﬂoating wind power generator featuring a ﬂoating
platform structure that supports the turbine. The TLP is held in place by vertical tendons (also
Design Optimization of Floating Oﬀshore Wind Farms
4
==PAGE:12==
known as tethers), which are anchored using template foundations, suction caissons, or pile-driven
anchors. These pre-tensioned tendons provide the platform with restoring stability. Compared to
spar-type, semi-submersible, or pontoon-type ﬂoating turbines, the TLP type exhibits a relatively
lower dynamic response to wave action. However, it may experience a phenomenon known as
"pull down," where the draft increases as the platform moves away from its equilibrium position.
The semi-submersible ﬂoating wind turbine is a type of ﬂoating wind energy converter featuring
a structure composed of several large column tubes interconnected by tubular members. The
turbine can be mounted on one of the columns, positioned on multiple columns, or placed at
the geometric center of the columns, supported by lateral bracing structures. The column tubes
provide ballast and are partially ﬁlled with water, ensuring both buoyancy and stability, thus
classifying the design as “column-stabilized.” This conﬁguration oﬀers excellent stability, and the
relatively shallow draft allows for greater site selection ﬂexibility. Semi-submersible wind turbines
are secured in position by a mooring system and can generally be assembled onshore.
Figure 1.5: The 6 degrees of freedom of an oﬀshore ﬂoating wind turbine platform. Source: [12]
The performance of ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines is inﬂuenced by displacements occurring across
6 DOF. These displacements encompass three translational movements: surge (X), sway (Y),
and heave (Z), and three rotational movements: roll, pitch, and yaw, as illustrated in Figure 1.5.
The 6 DOF motions arise due to the combined eﬀects of environmental forces and the system’s
restoring forces acting upon the ﬂoating wind turbine[13].
1.5
The Wake of Turbine
Wind turbine wakes are one of the most important aspects in wind power meteorology because
they decrease the power production and increase the loading of downstream wind turbines[14].
The wake can be divided into two regions: the near wake (2–4D downstream) and the far wake
(>4D downstream) where D is the diameter of the rotor[15]. The near wake region is signiﬁcantly
inﬂuenced by the presence and speciﬁc characteristics of the wind turbine, resulting in a highly
complex and anisotropic ﬂow structure. In contrast, the far wake is less aﬀected by the turbine
itself and more by general turbine parameters, such as power and thrust coeﬃcients, leading to
a less complex ﬂow structure.
Two primary physical phenomena are of interest in the wake: (1) the momentum (or velocity)
deﬁcit, which leads to a decrease in power output for downstream turbines, and (2) the height-
ened turbulence levels, which induce unsteady loading on the downstream turbines[14].

1.7
Thesis Objectives and Organization
Objectives
The unique characteristics of ﬂoating wind farms, such as their ability to reposition turbines
and adapt to varying environmental conditions, present signiﬁcant opportunities for innovative
solutions that enhance energy production and mitigate wake-induced losses. By leveraging the
capability of ﬂoating turbines to adjust rotor positions in response to changing environmental
factors, eﬀective wake management can be achieved, leading to increased overall power output.
This potential for improved performance serves as a key motivation for further exploration and
development of this topic in my thesis.
This study aims to advance the understanding of ﬂoating wind farm optimization by investi-
gating the dynamic interactions between wind turbines and their surrounding environment. It
emphasizes the integration of aerodynamic and hydrodynamic eﬀects to capture the complexities
of turbine behavior under varying conditions. Furthermore, the research incorporates advanced
optimization techniques to address challenges such as wake eﬀects, mooring system conﬁgura-
tions, and layout design, with the ultimate goal of enhancing energy production eﬃciency and
system reliability in real-world oﬀshore wind farm scenarios.
Organization
This thesis comprises four main components. The ﬁrst chapter outlines the methodology, pro-
viding a comprehensive derivation and detailed explanation of the theories and methods essential
for the subsequent chapters. The second chapter focuses on the steady-state 6 DOF responses
of FOWTs under varying wind conditions, derived through OpenFAST simulations, which form
the basis for constructing a data-driven motion model. The third chapter elaborates on the in-
tegration of the data-driven motion model into PyWake for wind farm simulations. Finally, the
fourth chapter addresses the optimization of FOWF layout and mooring system conﬁguration,
leveraging the repositioning capabilities of FOWTs, accompanied by a thorough analysis of the
optimization outcomes under various practical constraints and scenarios.

OpenFAST
OpenFAST is a simulation tool designed for multi-physics, multi-ﬁdelity modeling of wind tur-
bine dynamics. Essentially, it serves as the core framework, integrating computational modules
for aerodynamics, oﬀshore hydrodynamics, control and electrical systems, and structural dynam-
ics. This setup allows OpenFAST to conduct comprehensive, nonlinear aero-hydro-servo-elastic
simulations in the time domain. OpenFAST supports the analysis of various wind turbine con-
ﬁgurations, such as two- or three-blade horizontal-axis rotors, pitch or stall-regulated systems,
rigid or teetering hubs, upwind or downwind rotors, and lattice or tubular towers. Wind turbines
can be modeled both on land or oﬀshore, whether on ﬁxed-bottom or ﬂoating foundations[24].
Key Features
• Supports Various Wind Turbine Types: OpenFAST enables the simulation of multiple
wind turbine types, including both onshore and FOWT, adapting to diverse geographic
locations and environmental conditions. Particularly for FOWT, OpenFAST accurately
simulates complex dynamic responses under challenging marine conditions.
• Multi-Physics Coupling Modules: OpenFAST integrates multiple physics modules, each
dedicated to modeling speciﬁc aspects of wind turbine dynamics. This multi-physics ap-
proach includes: Aerodynamic Module (AeroDyn), Structural Dynamics Module (Elasto-
Dyn), Control and Electrical Systems Module (ServoDyn), Hydrodynamics Module (Hydro-
Dyn), etc. By coupling these modules, OpenFAST can accurately represent wind turbine
behavior in real-world environments, accounting for varying wind speeds, wind directions,
and marine conditions. Six DOF Motion Simulation: OpenFAST supports 6 DOF motion
simulation, making it particularly suitable for analyzing FOWT. The six degrees of freedom
include three translational motions (surge, sway, and heave) and three rotational motions
(roll, pitch, and yaw), enabling OpenFAST to model the complex dynamic responses of
FOWT under wave and wind loads.

Working Principles
OpenFAST operates through a modular architecture, where each module is responsible for a
distinct physical process. This design not only enables independent development and updating
of modules but also allows users to select and conﬁgure diﬀerent modules according to speciﬁc
simulation requirements. For instance:
• AeroDyn Module: AeroDyn is a time-domain wind turbine aerodynamics module that is
coupled in the OpenFAST multi-physics engineering tool to enable aero-elastic simulation
of horizontal-axis turbines.
• HydroDyn Module: HydroDyn is a time-domain hydrodynamics module that has been
coupled into the OpenFAST wind turbine multi-physics engineering tool to enable aero-
hydro-servo-elastic simulation of oﬀshore wind turbines.
• ServoDyn Module: The Structural Control module within ServoDyn is used to simulate
tuned mass dampers and tuned liquid column dampers.
This module also provides an
option for applying a force time series load at the location of the Structural Control node.
• ElastoDyn Module: The ElastoDyn module computes structural responses, covering the
elastic deformation of blades, towers, and foundations.
These modules communicate with one another through a coupling algorithm to synchronize physi-
cal quantities, resulting in a cohesive dynamic response. Outputs from each module are exchanged
with others to form an integrated simulation, allowing, for example, aerodynamic information
from AeroDyn to inﬂuence structural dynamics in ElastoDyn, while structural feedback also im-
pacts aerodynamic loads.
This modular coupling design ensures that each module can evolve independently without dis-
rupting the simulation workﬂow. Data exchange between modules is synchronized through time-
stepping and numerical algorithms, enabling OpenFAST to perform nonlinear, multi-physics
coupled simulations in the time domain.

All2AllIterative Engineering Wind Farm Model
In the All2AllIterative method, the calculation of each turbine’s eﬀective wind speed, tur-
bulence intensity, and wake deﬂection is performed iteratively. This process accounts for wake
deﬁcit, turbulence characteristics, yaw eﬀects, and wake deﬂection. The steps are as follows:
Figure 2.12: All2AllIterative iteration procedure. Source:[29]
Initialization:
Set up turbine parameters and environmental conditions, including pitch angle. Initialize the
wake models (Bastankhah, Niayifar, and Zong), turbulence model (Crespo and Hernández), and
deﬂection model (Jimenez Wake Deﬂection Model for calculating wake deﬂection).
Iterative Calculations:
• For each turbine, calculate the wake eﬀects on downstream turbines. Use the deﬂection
model to account for rotor-tilt-induced wake deﬂection, adjusting the position of the wake
relative to downstream turbines.
• Utilize wake model to calculate the wake deﬁcit, and use the turbulence model to estimate
the wake’s turbulence intensity.
• Aggregate the wake deﬁcits, turbulence intensities, and deﬂections from multiple upstream
turbines to determine the eﬀective wind speed, turbulence intensity, and wake position for
each downstream turbine.
Convergence Check:
After each iteration, check if the wind speed, turbulence intensity, and wake deﬂection for all

and actual environmental conditions. Consequently, it is necessary to develop a model that can

accommodating a variety of wind conditions that may not have been directly simulated.

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
The scope of this thesis encompasses the optimization of FOWF design with the objective of
maximizing AEP. This goal is realized through the development of a data-driven motion model,
its integration with PyWake to facilitate accurate FOWF simulations, and the application of ad-
vanced optimization algorithms to reﬁne wind farm layouts and mooring system conﬁgurations.
The optimization process showed that disregarding safety factors led to a 3% AEP improvement,
corresponding to an additional 145.55 GWh annually for the simulated wind farm. Incorporating
practical constraints, such as avoiding mooring line overlap, slightly reduced the improvement
to 2.992%. Further analysis identiﬁed anchor proximity as a risk factor, prompting the intro-
duction of an anchor spacing constraint. This adjustment not only improved system safety but
also facilitated escaping local optima, resulting in a 2.994% AEP improvement—slightly higher
than the overlap-constrained optimization. A preliminary analysis was conducted to evaluate the
economic feasibility of implementing shared anchors. The feasibility is inﬂuenced by factors such
as the optimized wind farm conﬁguration, inﬂation rates, and the increased costs associated with
technical requirements. Nevertheless, the overall assessment indicates that shared anchors are
economically viable.
This study successfully combined the repositioning characteristics of FOWTs to optimize the
layout and mooring system conﬁguration of a speciﬁc FOWF, providing insights into the balance
between energy production and system safety under practical constraints.
6.2
Future Work
In the OpenFAST simulations, several practical factors, such as ocean currents and wind-wave
misalignment, were excluded to simplify the problem. Addressing these factors represents an
opportunity for future research to enhance the ﬁdelity of the simulations.
Within the PyWake framework, the minimal variation in yaw observed in the data-driven motion
model led to the exclusion of yaw misalignment eﬀects during the simulation process. However,
in real-world scenarios, yaw misalignment plays a signiﬁcant role in wake dynamics and could
serve as a valuable focus for future optimization studies.
The optimization results reveal that the addition of constraints led to an increase in AEP, in-
dicating that the previous solutions were likely trapped in a local optimum. To mitigate this
issue, sensitivity analyses on parameter settings could be conducted to reduce the likelihood of
local optima in various optimization problems. Moreover, the application of shared anchors in
this study was implemented by directly modifying the outputs of the optimized conﬁguration.
Incorporating shared anchor considerations directly into the optimization process—evaluating
56
==PAGE:64==
whether their use leads to superior solutions—could further enhance the ﬁnal results.
Finally, economic models were not integrated into the current optimization process.
Future
research could beneﬁt from incorporating economic models to conduct feasibility studies for spe-
ciﬁc wind farms. While this approach may increase model complexity, the potential beneﬁts from
improved optimization outcomes are likely to outweigh the challenges.
