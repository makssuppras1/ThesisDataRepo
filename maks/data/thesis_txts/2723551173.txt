Abstract
Abstract
This study outlines how to auralize the spatial sound in a room through headphone
playback. A hypothesizing virtual loudspeaker array encloses the captured sound field,
formulating the classical underdetermined inverse problem.
Two solutions of driving
signal for the virtual loudspeaker array, i.e., the volumetric reconstruction, are solved by
imposing the L-1 norm and the L-2 norm side constraints, respectively. They are compared
in an ideal two-dimensional simulation and the auralization of a realistic room impulse
response. Except for the L-curve method, a tailored regularization parameter, namely the
average noise power, is derived for the L-1 norm problem solved by the FOCUSS algorithm.
The result reveals that the common practice, including the least square solution and
the denser grid, may cause a blurred spatial image, supported by the listenerвҖҷs informal
report.
The FOCUSS algorithm combined with the tailored regularization parameter
successfully improves the normalized mean square error and the spatial similarity in the
broad frequency and space area.
Thus, the study proves the sensibility of promoting
sparse solutions in a damped room, and the solution seems to provide a better listening
experience.
Nevertheless, both methods fail to determine the direct sound direction,
leading to a wrong perception of the sound sourceвҖҷs location. The problem appeals to an
elaborate design of the inverse problem, e.g., introducing the knowledge of the direction
of arrival and a targeted rather than arbitrary placement of the virtual loudspeakers.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The endeavor to accurately capture and reproduce spatial sound reproduction dates back
to the advent of the audio industry [1]. The initial work focused on the multiple chan-
nelsвҖҷ systems to record and playback the sound, following the investigation of the stereo
audio system. One key problem of modern research arises from the implementation in
cinema: how to simultaneously preserve the direction of the sound source and the am-
biance? The quadraphonic sound was redesigned to meet this requirement, and one of the
decoders, termed Ambisonics system, was proposed in this period. Instead of recovering
the sound field in the whole space, the Ambisonics system creates a sweet-spot area near
the listenerвҖҷs ear. After experiencing the popularity of the 5.1-channel surround system,
the rapid development of digital data storage and hardware ushered in the modern sig-
nal processing method. These methods include the Wave Field Synthesis (WFS) [2], the
parametric encoding method like Vector-Base Amplitude Panning (VBAP) [3, 4], higher-
order spherical harmonics (HOA) [5, 6], and the linear inverse problem with the numerical
encoding [5, 7]. The technology development put the idea of reconstruction forward to
auralization. The term auralization could be summarized as a technique that [8вҖ“10]:
вҖў identifies the transfer function between the source (or virtual source) and the mea-
surement position to reproduce the unknown environmental sound from the numerical
data;
вҖў includes the function delineating the head and torso effect during the sound propagation
and the targeted microphoneвҖҷs recording;
вҖў allows the audience to perceive an in-situ experience at a given position of the modeled
space when listening to the playback sound through the microphone or the headphone.
The definition suggests that the sound field reconstruction is insufficient for the auraliza-
tion since free field propagation analysis neglects the audience in the room. Though the
Duplex theory seems to be an elegant explanation of a free-field spatial location [11], the
confusion cone and the ability to localize the target on the median plane imply that the
monoaural cue or spectral coloration plays a pivotal role in the localization [12]. It has
been shown that the human head and shoulders cause the diffraction for sound higher
than 200 Hz, while the tiny structure within the ear canal brings about distortion in
the high-frequency range [10]. A straightforward way of quantifying these effects is to
measure head-related transfer functions (HRTFs). HRTFs make it possible to separate

This study focuses on the classical application of reproducing the binaural listening expe-

idea mentioned above, the practical implementation of the headphone auralization of the
sound in the room can be separated into the four stages [10, 13]:
вҖў Record or synthesize the sound field. The impulse response (IR) containing the spatial
information is stored in numerical data.
вҖў Encode sound field. The sound field will be expressed as a series of the basis func-
tionвҖҷs combination, and then the captured signal is expressed by the basis functionвҖҷs
coefficient.
вҖў Decode sound field.
Depending on the basis function, it has divergent methods to
incorporate HRTFs in the sound fieldвҖҷs decoding to generate binaural room impulse
response (BRIR). It will be discussed in subsection 2.5 and subsection 5.3.
вҖў Generate the reconstructed binaural signal of the room.
Note that the binaural recording directly generates the binaural signal, merging different
stages. Though it can produce plausible binaural signals, it also reduces the flexibility
of factoring in the head rotation or individualization [13]. Besides, the parametric model
will decode the sound field into the diffuse and non-diffuse components. The decorrelation
technique and the VBAP are employed to process them further [4], but it is far from this
studyвҖҷs topic.
1.2
Scope of the study
This thesis focuses on implementing auralization to reconstruct the spatial sound in the
room, and it only covers the last three stages of the auralization. The work outlines the
frame of auralization based on the volumetric reconstruction from the inverse problem
model. The frame is concise, encoding and decoding the sound field, but each step has
multiple issues.
The perfect encoding of the sound field relies on the continued integral along the surface
enclosed in the target area [2, 17]. However, the realistic application must discretize the
integral to a microphone array distributed on the enclosed surface [18]. As a result, spatial
aliasing is unavoidable, and its severity depends on the relative length of the microphoneвҖҷs

interval to the sound wavelength [19]. Further, different basis functions have their specific
problems. For example, HOA needs a spherical array to record the sound field. An open
field array may give rise to multiple forbidden frequencies [6], while a rigid sphere limits
the highest available harmonics order [13]. Alternatively, an arbitrary microphone array
is employed to encode the sound field. Then, it defines multiple virtual sources with
the plane wave [20] or monopole source [18] assumption, which formulates the inverse
problem. Nevertheless, the signal captured by the microphone is usually contaminated
by the measurement noise.
Thus, an accurate solution becomes impossible, and the
approximation technique termed regularization is the main solving approach [21].
Another issue is that the roomвҖҷs reverberation is complex. The measured impulse response
is usually cast into direct sound, early reflection, and late reflection [7, 22]. The fine
structure of the first two components makes a sparse solution more attractive, while the
late reflection corresponds to the diffuse component. If all components are solved by a
less elaborated model, like the sparse solution assumption, it may perform poorly even
using a large number of microphones [20].
In the decoding stage, the main issue is how to incorporate the influence of the listener
into the biaural rendering. The directivity calculated by HOA can be used to sample
HRTFs spatially [13, 23]. Or the direction and the magnitude of reflections are solved and
convolve with HRTFs. Nevertheless, HRTFs are measured on the spatial grid according
to specific distribution rules, like Gaussian distribution and the equal angel distribution.
The measurement grid of the basis function may not match the sampling grid of HRTFs.
So, the HRTFs interpolation is implemented before generating BRIR. The approximation
introduces the spatial cueвҖҷs loss [23], influencing the perception of the auralized binaural
signal.
This work employed the impulse response dataset Ref [24, 25] measured on the planar
aperture to encode the sound field while the technology implementation stems from Ref
[5, 26, 27]. The 2D dimension measurement limits the encoding method to the numerical
solution of the inverse problem. Therefore, the appropriate solution balancing the error
of approximated solution and encoding the noise becomes the key problem of this work.
Based on the discussion above, this study attempts to:
вҖў Realize a volumetric reconstruction of the impulse response by solving an inverse prob-
lem.
вҖў Verify the efficacy of the classical L-2 norm problem, which forms the benchmark result.
вҖў Considering the measured roomвҖҷs characteristics, an L-1 norm problem is formulated

вҖў The reconstructed room frequency response from the solution of the L-1 norm and L-2

вҖў The binaural music signal is created by the BRIR. An informal listening test provides
the impression of auralization.

Theory
Figure 1 delineates the technology roadmap of this work, and this section introduces the
technical details of each unit. The monopole source model is first discussed, leading to the
definition of the virtual loudspeaker and the inverse problem formulation. The volumetric
reconstruction of the sound field is realized by solving the virtual loudspeakerвҖҷs driving
signal with the room frequency response measurement on a planar aperture. However,
the ill-posed problem requires appropriate regularization strategies. Thus, the L-1 norm
and the L-2 norm regularization problems are formulated in this section. Each strategyвҖҷs
solution provides the driving signal of the virtual loudspeaker, and the regularization
parameter selection will be fully investigated. Finally, it shows how to obtain the BRIR
from both the solution of the L-1 norm and the L-2 norm problem. The interpolated
HRTFs are factored in this stage.

2.1
The inverse problem formulation
As mentioned in subsection 1.1, sound field reproduction can be realized from different
perspectives. This thesis does not consider the physical boundary or perception when
encoding the sound field. In other words, it only attempts to solve the coefficient of the
defined basis function to encode the sound field as accurately as possible, which can be
cast into the category of the numerical method.

2.1.2
Definition of the inverse problem
The sound field can only be measured at finite positions, so Equation 1 is discretized
during realistic practice [5, 18]. The discretized monopole source implementation could
be found in the attempt to reproduce impulse response [28] and the plane wave with few
loudspeakers [19, 27]. Recently, Fernandez-Grande [26] proves the efficacy of the dense
monopole sound source grid in reproducing the sound field in the near field. This model
is later expanded to encode the impulse response based on the room impulse responseвҖҷs
(RIR) fine structure in the time domain [7, 22]. Similarly, Ref [29, 30] proposes that
summation of the monopole source and a homogeneous component can reconstruct the
sound field. Following the previous research, this study assumes a spherical grid of the
monopole sound source encloses the area where microphones capture the sound field. It
means that only the interpolation within the enclosed space is considered in this study.

2.4
Diffuseness index
The perfect diffuse sound field is generated by distant sources of random noise evenly
distributed over all directions [44]. Therefore, the perfect diffuse sound field possesses the
following characteristics,
вҖў The sound signal captured by the microphone should be the same at each measurement
position, termed completely homogeneous [44].
вҖў The correlation between two points should only depend on the distance, which is a sinc
function [7, 22, 46].
вҖў Incident sound power for each direction is constant, i.e., an isotropic sound field [47].
The second characteristic has been utilized to evaluate the sound fieldвҖҷs diffuseness [48],
but the few used microphones make the result sensitive to the chosen measurement po-
sition. Another idea is to encode the sound field into the spherical harmonics. Then the
correlation between different orders of the spherical harmonics is employed to evaluate
the sound fieldвҖҷs diffuseness [46]. Unfortunately, the thesis uses a sound field dataset mea-
sured on the planar area, so the spherical harmonics encoding cannot be implemented.
Finally, an isotropic sound field does not have the net transport energy, leading to the
method of the parametric encoding of the sound field [47, 49]. This method needs the

soundвҖҷs velocity data to calculate the sound intensity, which doesnвҖҷt contain in the used
dataset.
The technical limitation mentioned above makes this study choose a method using the
square of the beamforming output to approximate the sound powerвҖҷs distribution on the
measurement grid [46]. The initial method uses spherical harmonics, which cannot be

beamforming method is introduced in the next section to obtain the directivity.
2.4.1
DaS beamforming
Intuitively, the sound field measurement on a planar aperture can be encoded by circular
harmonic. High-order circular harmonics are required for the correct sound field encoding
in high frequencies. Take the dataset used by this thesis, for instance. The simulation
shows that a circular area with a 0.15-meter radius requires at least 15 orders of circular
harmonics to encode the used sound field measurement up to 5000 Hz. Nevertheless,
the high-order circular harmonicsвҖҷ beamforming result contains many sidelobes [50]. Be-
sides, the dataset is measured by an omnidirectional microphone in the free field so that
the radial function causes multiple forbidden frequencies. Those two problems make cir-
cular harmonic expansion hard to implement for this dataset. Therefore, a Delay and
Sum (DaS) beamforming is chosen to test the regularization parameter selection method
derived in subsection 2.3 [51].
The basic idea of DaS beamforming is introducing a time delay for each measurement
signal to reinforce the measured sound signal from the incident direction other than the
noise or irrelevant direction [51]. Then, all delayed measurement signals sum up to probe
in which direction the largest output is achieved, i.e., incident direction.
The planar
aperture used in the dataset makes a uniform circular array be the preferable choice for
beamforming. However, the uniform circular array cannot promise that the beamforming
output pattern is independent of directions in 3D space [52]. Thus, the elevation angle
is set to 90в—Ұ. The assumption requires that the angle of the source relative to the z-axis
should be larger than 30в—Ұ, and the sound from a direction other than a 90в—Ұelevation
angle is sufficiently attenuated [52]. In this study, the elevation angle between the source
and z-axis is about 76.2в—Ұ, so the source and the measurement position are close to being
on the same plane. Besides, the damped room absorbs most sound energy within a few
reflections, especially in high frequencies. It could expect that DaS would be able to
detect the sound sourceвҖҷs direction if only considering the sound wave propagates in the
plane, which is proved in Figure 22.

3.2
Discussion
The experiment above supports the argument that the L-2 norm problem with the least
square solution tends to distribute the energy evenly across the virtual loudspeaker [34].
The spatial blurring is observed in Figure 5, Figure 14, and Figure 15. The non-ideal

L-curve deteriorates the weak wavefront problem. Though using the noise to the signal
power ratio as the regularization problem improves the performance, it cannot change the
character of distributing energy across the space inherited in the Tikhonov regularization
method.
Comparison between Figure 5 and Figure 8 indicates that a larger distance among micro-
phones may attenuate the spatial blurring and improves the capability of identifying the
arriving direction of the incident plane wave. The larger distance would bring a larger
time difference between the captured signal among microphones. The FFT operation will
transfer the time shift into the rotation of the frequency domain, so it helps to attenuate
the overlap among the frequency response of different microphones. Compared to a 0.1
m radius, a 0.3 m radius circle will impose the Tikhonov regularization solver to dis-
tribute less uniform energy among virtual loudspeakers to reduce the regularization error.
However, a sparse microphone grid gives rise to a lower spatial Nyquist frequency, which
makes it arduous to solve the broadband frequency problem. If a dense microphone grid is
used to attenuate spatial aliasing for the broad frequency problem, the underdetermined
problem setup leads to a dense virtual loudspeaker grid. As a result, the spatial blurring
problem further deteriorates (Figure 15).
Furthermore, solving the inverse problem of encoding the sound field in the frequency
domain will вҖңovershadowвҖқ the weak sound reflection. The FFT transformation of the
Hanning pulse generates a broad frequency response, and the frequency response of the
Hanning pulse with a larger magnitude overwhelms the one with a small magnitude.
Thus, the weak captured sound is difficult to be solved in the frequency domain, though
the captured Hanning pulse has an evident structure in the time domain. The Tikhonov
regularization method does not endeavor to reweight the solution, and the solution can
still identify the wavefront of the incident Hanning pulse with the smallest magnitude.
However, the other two Hanning pulsesвҖҷ also project their shadow in the solution. In
Figure 11 (a), the shadow of the other two Hanning pulses is observed in the first snapshot
of the third row. Conversely, the FOCUSS algorithm attempts to emphasize the larger
component in the initial solution (i.e., W1 = I). As a result, the projected shadows are
endowed with a larger magnitude, and the wavefront is blurred in the whole reconstruction
area. Thus, the FOCUSS algorithm fails to identify the third Hanning pulse wavefront in
the third row of Figure 11 (b).
In practice, RIR consists of direct sound, early reflection, and late reflection. The di-
rect sound and the early reflection have a large magnitude and an impulse-like shape.
32
==PAGE:44==

Based on the aforementioned discussion, the late reflection will be overshadowed by the
other two parts when sound field encoding is realized in the frequency domain. Tikhonov
regularization may be able to solve the late reflection component, whereas the FOCUSS
algorithm would fail. On the other hand, the dense grid is not a sensible choice when
solving tremendous reflections in a room by the Tikhonov regularization method.
It
might be desirable to solve the possible incident direction first and places a sparse vir-
tual loudspeaker array in a narrow spatial area [7]. In contrast, a dense grid of virtual
loudspeakers can help the FOCUSS algorithm to probe at least the component with the
largest magnitude. The reweight strategy is advantageous in solving the dominant sound
fieldвҖҷs component. However, it becomes a downside when identifying the weak component
since the FFT of the dominant sound component incurs a broad frequency response, the
one emphasized during the iteration. Thus, the diffuse component is hard to be solved
by the FOCUSS algorithm.

4
Implementation of Auralization
This section outlines the procedure and the technology used to auralize the anechoic music
signal. The details of the measurement data will be discussed.
4.1
Procedure
This study uses an open dataset to realize the auralization in an enclosed space where the
measurement occurs. Note that the ratio of the average noise power to the sound source
power is unknown in the realistic problem, so L-curve method will be used to select the
regularization parameter for the L-2 norm problem. Following the structure described in
Figure 1, the main procedures of the auralization include:
вҖў Encode the sound field with Tikhonov regularization and the FOCUSS algorithm
using the measured frequency response at the selected position in Ref [24, 25]. The
regularization toolbox implements the L-curve method and the Tikhonov regulariza-
tion in Matlab [54]. The FOCUSS algorithm is realized by the iteration procedure
described in subsection 2.4.
вҖў This study uses the HRTF measured by Bernschutz [55].
The dataset contains
the measurement at 2702 nodes of a Lebedev grid, whose measurement detail is
delineated in Ref [55]. The measured HRTFs are interpolated on the position of the
virtual loudspeaker by the spherical harmonic expansion [6, 23].
вҖў The FFT operation transfers the interpolated HRTFs into the frequency domain.
вҖў The driving signal multiplies with HRTFs in the frequency domain and sum up
the multiplication result across the whole space to reconstruct binaural frequency
response. BRIR is obtained by the IFFT of the binaural frequency response.
вҖў To evaluate the reconstruction quality, Inter-channel Time Difference (ICTD) and
Inter-channel Level Difference (ICLD) are calculated for the reconstructed BRIR.
вҖў BRIR convolves with the anechoic recordings to generate the binaural music signal
in the room.
Since the anechoic recordings have over a 1-minute length, block
convolution is used at this stage [53].

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
This study aims to outline the structure of the volumetric reconstruction based on the
room frequency response captured on a planar aperture. The L-2 norm and L-1 norm
underdetermined inverse problem is formulated, then solved by the Tikhonov regular-
ization and the FOCUSS algorithm, respectively. An ideal 2D simulating experiment is
first held to probe the two solving approachesвҖҷ characteristics. The knowledge obtained
from the experiment is verified by the impulse response measured on a planar aperture
in room 019, building 352, DTU. The solution to the inverse problem provides the vir-
tual loudspeakerвҖҷs driving signal, regarded as the direct sound and reflections. Following
this idea, the solved driving signal multiplies with the interpolated HRTFs to generate
the binaural frequency response. Then, BRIR is obtained by the IFFT operation of the
reconstructed binaural frequency response. Finally, based on the linear-time invariant as-
sumption, BRIR convolves with the anechoic signal to generate the binaural music signal.
An informal listening test shed the first light on the impression of auralization.
The result verifies the proposed auralization processвҖҷs feasibility but also unfolds some
latent deficiencies in the common practice. In the simulation experiment, the L-2 norm
imposes the smallest least square constraint on the driving signal to obtain the unique
solution. However, it also introduces the preference for a uniform driving signal. Though
the wavefront is not concentrated and ripples exist, the simulation result shows that the
Tikhonov regularization method can solve the direction of the single or multiple incident
sound (Figure 8(a) and Figure 11 (a)). However, it relies on a small number of virtual
loudspeakers and the overlap between the incident direction and the loudspeakerвҖҷs loca-
tion. The wavefront is vague if the Hanning pulse does not come from one loudspeakerвҖҷs
position. The increase in the loudspeakerвҖҷs number, i.e., the denser grid, cannot help
the Tikhonov regularization find the correct incident direction but further deteriorates
the spatial blur image (Figure 15 (a)). The informal listening test also reports a blur
impression of the cello sound auralized by the Tikhonov regularization.
Conversely, the FOCUSS algorithm successfully strengthens the wavefront but also am-
plifies the overshadowing problem. In other words, the dominant impulse component,
e.g., the direct and the early reflection, will incur a broad response when transferring into
the frequency domain. The FOCUSS algorithm puts a larger weight on the dominant
