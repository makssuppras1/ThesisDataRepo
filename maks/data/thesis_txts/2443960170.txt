Abstract
Introduction
Pediatric sarcomas are a rare and complex cancer type consisting of several subtypes
with various characteristics and with a dismal prognosis. Unidentiﬁed characteristics and subtypes
might be found in diagnostic imaging studies leading to improved survival through stratiﬁcation of
tumors and thereby allowing the possibility of a more personalized treatment. In this project, deep
learning is used to develop and implement an algorithm for segmentation of pediatric sarcomas in
PET/CT scans with the ﬁnal aim of paving the way for stratiﬁcation studies.
Methods
Retrospectively, 58 pediatric patients with biopsy proven sarcomas had a whole body 18F-
FDG PET/CT scan performed. Additional, 51 veriﬁed soft tissue sarcoma PET/CT data, obtained
from The Cancer Imaging Archive (TCIA), was included.
Each sarcoma was manually delineated
by a nuclear medicine specialist and presented as the true label reference. The PET/CT data along
with the tumor references were used to train a deep learning network to automatically identify and
segment sarcomas.
The applied network was adapted from the U-Net where the input consists of
three adjacent 2D PET slices and three adjacent 2D CT slices.
The output of the network is a
segmentation map corresponding to the center-slice of the input, and consists of two images presenting
a segmentation of the two classes (tumor/background). The developed and implemented network was
optimized by investigating hyperparameters playing a substantial role in the algorithm.
The ﬁnal
model was evaluated by a K-fold cross validation with K=5. Furthermore, the inﬂuence of subtypes
and TCIA data was explored. The tumor segmentations were evaluated based on a voxel wise dice
score, precision, and sensitivity.
Results
Through a hyperparameter search, the ﬁnal model was determined with the following set-
tings: Batch size=8; learning rate=1e−02; number of ﬁlters at 16, 32, 64, 128, 256 at block level 1, 2, 3,
4, and 5, respectively; and dropout rate at 0.1, 0.2, and 0.3 at block level 1-2, 3-4, and 5, respectively.
The ﬁnal model had a mean performance on the entire pediatric sarcoma data set of a dice score at
0.36, a precision at 0.57, and a sensitivity at 0.42. Results in terms of pediatric sarcoma subtypes
revealed that especially one of the subtypes (Ewing sarcoma) showed varying characteristics, and was
the most challenging subtype for the algorithm to segment. The additional TCIA data had no eﬀect
on the model performance.
Conclusion
The developed deep learning network was trained to segment pediatric sarcomas, and
managed to recognize and segment the majority of these highly heterogeneous and complex tumors.
This work revealed a crucial need for more pediatric sarcoma data, and especially regarding unique
cases. This study creates the foundation for further work towards eventually paving the way for risk
stratiﬁcation studies that may reveal new characteristics of the pediatric sarcoma subtypes.

Introduktion
Pædiatriske sarkomer er en sjælden og kompleks kræfttype best˚aende af undertyper
med forskellige karakteristika med en d˚arlig prognose. I billeddiagnostikken af disse sarkomer kan der
m˚aske ﬁndes uidentiﬁcerede karakteristika og undertyper, som kan give ny viden, der kan forbedre
overlevelsen gennem stratiﬁcering af tumorer og dermed mere m˚alrettet behandling. I dette projekt
er Deep learning brugt til at udvikle og implementere en algoritme, der kan segmentere pædiatriske
sarkomer p˚a PET/CT scanninger, hvor det endelige form˚al er at bane vejen for stratiﬁcerings-studier.
Metode
Retrospektivt, har 58 pædiatriske patienter med biopsi-veriﬁcerede sarkomer f˚aet foretaget
en helkrops 18F-FDG PET/CT scanning. Et supplerende PET/CT datasæt, best˚aende af 51 veriﬁ-
cerede bløddelssarkomer fra The Cancer Imaging Archive (TCIA), blev inkluderet. Hvert sarkom blev
manuelt delineeret af en nuklear medicinsk specialist og repræsenterer en true label reference. PET/CT
data samt tumor referencen blev brugt til at træne et deep learning netværk for automatisk at kunne
identiﬁcere og segmentere pædiatriske sarkomer. Det anvendte netværk var adapteret fra U-Nettet,
og dets input best˚ar af tre tilstødende 2D PET slices samt tre tilstødende 2D CT slices. Netværkets
output er en segmentering svarende til inputtets center-slice, der best˚ar af to billeder, som indeholder
en segmentering af hver klasse (tumor/baggrund). Det udviklede og implementerede netværk blev
optimeret ved at undersøge hyperparametre, der har væsentlige roller i algoritmens funktioner. Den
endelige model blev evalueret ved en K-folds krydsvalidering med K=5. Indﬂydelsen af sarkomunder-
typerne samt det ekstra TCIA data blev endvidere undersøgt. Tumor-segmenteringerne blev evalueret
baseret p˚a en pixelvis dice score, præcision og sensitivitet.
Resultater
Igennem en undersøgelse af hyperparametrene blev den endelige model fastsat med
følgende opsætning: Batch size=8; learning rate=1e−02; antallet af ﬁltre i blok level 1, 2, 3, 4 og
5 var henholdsvis 16, 32, 64, 128 og 256; og dropout med en rate p˚a 0.1, 0.2 og 0.3 i blok level 1-2,
3-4 og 5. Den endelige model præsterede samlet set p˚a hele det pædiatriske sarkom datasæt med en
gennemsnitlig dice score p˚a 0.36, en præcision p˚a 0.57 og en sensitivitet p˚a 0.42. Resultater baseret
p˚a undertyper viste at særlig ´en undertype (Ewing sarkom) udviste varierende karakteristika og var
den sværeste for algoritmen at segmentere. Den ekstra TCIA data viste sig ikke at have en eﬀekt p˚a
modellens præstation.
Konklusion
Det udviklede deep learning netværk blev trænet til at segmentere pædiatriske sarkomer
og form˚aede at identiﬁcere og segmentere størstedelen af disse meget heterogene og komplekse tumorer.
Det har vist sig nødvendigt at have et større datasæt repræsentativt for de unikke tilfælde af pædi-
atriske sarkomer.
Dette studie danner grundlag for videreudvikling, der p˚a længere sigt kan bane
vejen for risikostratiﬁcerings-studier for potentielt at afsløre nye karakteristika af de pædiatriske sarko-
mundertyper.

Preface
This Master’s Thesis is written to obtain a Master of Science degree in Biomedical Engineering at the
Technical University of Denmark and University of Denmark. The project is completed in collabora-
tion with the Department of Clinical Physiology, Nuclear Medicine and PET at Rigshospitalet.
The interest for this project originates from our passion for combining two scientiﬁc ﬁelds; the technical
engineering ﬁeld and the medical ﬁeld. This combination is interesting, as you get to work with real
life data, and as it enables to do research within a ﬁeld, where the outcome may change human lives.
In this project, a deep learning network is developed to automatic detect and segment pediatric
sarcomas on PET and CT imaging data. Diﬀerent models with varying parameters are set up in order
to determine the model that results in the best possible segmentation.
This project was conducted from August 2018 to January 2019, and presents a workload of 30 ECTS
each. It is directed readers with similar background and similar or higher educational level.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Pediatric sarcomas are a rare and an aggressive type of cancer, accounting for 7-10% of pediatric
cancer. The aggressiveness of this cancer is reﬂected in the poor prognosis and the extensive treatment
protocol. This includes combinations of surgical procedures (e.g. amputation), chemotherapy, and/or
radiotherapy to improve survival [1]. The children surviving the disease most often experience severe
side eﬀects such as lifelong psychological, physical, and/or medical diﬃculties [2].
Treatment of pediatric sarcomas is planned according to the cancer subtype. However, due to the
dismal prognosis and the heterogeneous response to treatment, questions about undiscovered charac-
teristics and subtypes are raised, which may aid as decision support for the treatment and prognosis.
Risk stratiﬁcation studies could contribute with new knowledge about pediatric sarcomas and improve
survival through more personalized treatment.
Delineation of sarcomas in medical image modalities may provide valuable information for a study
of pediatric sarcomas by paving the way for stratiﬁcation studies. The delineation is primarily ac-
complished on Positron Emission Tomography (PET), Computed Tomography (CT), and Magnetic
Resonance Imaging (MRI) images, and is a necessity for precise staging, planning of treatment, image-
guided radiotherapy, and response to treatment [3]. However, as image modalities contain a large and
often unused amount of data, which contain important qualitative information, it may be possible to
extract data features that are not visually interpretable.
Data features drawn from a tumor volume can potentially reveal the complexity of pediatric sar-
comas [4]. These features are extracted from the delineated tumor, where a delineation should be as
precise and accurate as possible, which is why the segmentation process is a crucial step of extracting
quantitative information from imaging modalities [4].
An accurate delineation is challenging and requires a trained specialist, as the level of expert knowledge
and experience have high impact on the segmentation accuracy [3][5]. It is diﬃcult to convey distinct
segmentation rules due to the heterogeneity of the sarcomas, and the large variability of their location,
size, and shape, which also induce inter- and intra-operator variability. In order for the specialist to
perform manual segmentation close to the ground truth the process becomes a time-consuming and
tedious task [6].
Another consideration is that a high number of subjects are required in a risk stratiﬁcation study

Diagnostic work-up and treatment
The diagnosis for all pediatric solid tumors is determined by
a tumor biopsy. Staging is crucial for treatment planning and based on tumor size and location along
with possible presence of metastasis, and determined by a X-ray, a CT, an MRI, and/or a PET/CT
or a PET/MRI, where PET/CT and PET/MRI are the highest international standard [11].
Treatment of pediatric sarcomas follows the international treatment protocol, where RMS is treated
following the European pediatric Soft tissue sarcoma Study Group (EpSSG) [15]. The treatment of
sarcomas combines chemotherapy, surgical procedure, and/or radiotherapy; some cases require ampu-
tation to remove the tumor [11][13][14].
The prognosis of the sarcoma subtypes depends on primary tumor site, tumor size, surgical re-
sectability of primary tumor, tumor histology, and presence of metastases [1].
Late complications
Longtime surviving patients are at risk of late complications due to the treat-
ment.
Chemo- and radiotherapy are intense treatments for both children and adults.
Radiation
damages the DNA of exposed cells, while chemo is a drug targeting and inhibiting rapidly dividing
cells that are a main characteristic of cancer cells [16]. Because children are growing and thus have
more frequent cell division than adults, they are more sensitive to chemo- and radiation therapy [17].
Late complications vary from psychological and social issues to physical and medical side eﬀects.
Psychological or social issues may be caused by stress or depression and are typically related to e.g.
amputation. Physical problems can be caused by amputation or lifelong musculoskeletal dysfunction
due to chemotherapy. Medical complications include chronic diseases such as heart disease and kid-
ney failure.
Furthermore, secondary cancer could be a late complication caused by late eﬀects of
radiotherapy [2].
2.2
Imaging in Oncology
PET, CT, and MRI are imaging modalities used in oncology to stage and evaluate response to treat-
ment. Usually, they are applied in combination where PET/CT or PET/MRI are combined in hybrid
scanners. The ﬁnal diagnosis requires a biopsy with histology.
Positron Emission Tomography
PET utilizes positron-emitting radioactive isotopes that label a
molecule as a tracer. The tracer is intravenously injected and distributed by the blood stream to the
target organ or tumor. A scanner registers the location of the uptake of the decaying isotopes, which
depends on metabolic processes in each cell.
A typical tracer in oncology is 18F-FDG (ﬂuorine-18 ﬂuorodeoxyglucose), where FDG is a glucose
analogue and 18F is the radioactive isotope. The tracer accumulates in tissues and organs that have
particularly high glucose metabolism such as the brain and many tumors. The tracer accumulation
in tumor cells are caused by increased glucose uptake and metabolism due to a high cell division rate
and upregulated glucose transporters [18].
PET images provide physiological information representing the tracer uptake in the body and have
low spatial resolution, cf. Figure 2.2a. Thus, PET is often applied in combination with CT or MRI to
obtain the combination of physiological information and anatomical precision, cf. Figure 2.2c.
Computed Tomography
CT uses a rotating X-ray beam to produce projections of a subject from
360°orientations around the subject. By computational reconstruction, 2D cross-sectional images are
created and further stacked to become a 3D representations of the scanned object [18].
A CT image represents the attenuation of the X-ray beam through tissues, Figure 2.2b. Thus, the
higher tissue density, the more attenuation of the X-rays. CT provides anatomical information, where

intelligence to perform a certain task, and is typically implemented using Machine Learning (ML) [21].
ML uses statistical techniques that enables a machine to learn and optimize its performance in a certain
task. Developing and implementing ML for tasks that can be diﬃcult for humans to solve, such as
advanced mathematical calculations, are straightforward to implement as it includes a set of deﬁned
rules. However, the development and implementation of ML techniques for tasks that are trivial for

accomplish such tasks [21].

2.4
Deep Learning
Deep learning is based on feature learning and inspired by the biological neural network of the brain.
It evolved from the concept of perceptrons, which is an algorithm representing the human neuron with
all its connections. From the concept of a single perceptron, multi-layer perceptrons were developed
and later evolved to various Neural Network (NN) architectures [22]. Through time, the concept of
DL has developed and additional operations have been introduced to improve the performance of DL
networks [24][25].
The success of DL has increased concurrently with the increasing amount of labelled data available,
and with the acceleration of parallel computation due to implementation of the Graphic Processing
Unit (GPU). The GPU makes it possible to train larger models with a high number of parameters
within a limited time-frame [21]. Lately, DL has attracted a lot of attention in the ﬁeld of computer
vision, mainly due to the development of new eﬃcient NNs. One of these is the Convolutional Neural
Network (CNN) that achieved state of the art recognition in 2012 at the annual ImageNet ILSVRC
challenge, and is favourable in image classiﬁcation and semantic segmentation, where the latter is a
pixel wise segmentation [7][8][9][26].
In the following, principles behind a DL network are introduced, starting from the concept of the
perceptron.

Backpropagation is a process where the loss is propagated backwards to previous

Regularization
Regularization is a technique used to modify a NN during training. If the model is not trained enough,
underﬁtting can occur. Conversely, if the model learns details and noise too well, the model exhibits
poor performance on unseen data due to overﬁtting [33].
Regularization prevents overﬁtting and improves the overall performance when validating the
model. Diﬀerent methods of applying regularization exist, where predeﬁned parameters, referred to as
hyperparameters, are deﬁned in each method to control the regularization-degree [33].

Dropout
Dropout is a frequently used regulator, interpreted as a stochastic method adding noise to the hidden
units of the NN, which eventually minimize the loss function [33]. Former studies have shown that
dropout results in a signiﬁcantly lower generalization error [25].
Dropout is applied in training by temporarily removing units from the layers in the NN, cf. Figure 2.7.
This implies that all incoming and outgoing connections to the units are left out. The dropout units are
chosen randomly based on a probability, which is the hyperparameter of the dropout function [25][33].
Applying dropout results in diﬀerent possible network combinations of the full network. For every
iteration, a new network combination that consist of a new set of units is trained. Dropout strengthen
the model, as the algorithm is trained on various cases of the total network and diﬀerent output is
achieved [25].

2.4.4
Convolutional Neural Networks
CNNs are a class of multi-layered NNs that are specialized to recognize patterns in images, and hence
perform well in classiﬁcation and segmentation problems. The input to these networks is multidimen-
sional, originating from an image made of pixels. All of the above principles apply to a CNN, however,
CNNs diﬀer from regular NNs by always containing convolutional layers.
CNNs can be constructed diﬀerently by applying additional operation layers. A typical CNN is con-
structed by one or multiple convolutional layers, followed by a ReLU activation layer to introduce
non-linearity to the network [29], and a pooling layer for downsampling. These consecutive operations
can be repeated till the desired depth of the network. The ﬁnal segmentation or classiﬁcation output
is obtained by applying one or more fully connected layers, where the last layer is always fully con-
nected to the number of prediction classes. Fully connected layers have full connections to all output
activations in the previous layer. A Softmax classiﬁer can be applied to the output, which assigns a
probability distribution of the output to either class, i.e. each pixel is mapped to diﬀerent prediction
classes [28].
Additional operations are possible to apply in a CNN, which may deﬁne the type of CNN network.
A CNN applied for segmentation contains upsampling layers to perform a pixel wise classiﬁcation and
segmentation, cf. Section 2.4.4. The relevant operations for this project are presented in the following.

Convolutional Layer
A convolution is a mathematical operation where a ﬁlter weight matrix of a given size is used to calculate
new ﬁltered values of each pixel within an image to output a feature map. A 2D convolution can be
applied on both single-channel and multi-channel images; multi-channel images refers to successive 2D
images presenting the same object. In medical image segmentation these channels could represent an
axial slice from diﬀerent image modalities such as PET, CT, and/or MRI.
The ﬁltered values are calculated by the dot product between the values of the ﬁlter and the
covered pixels in all channels, cf. Figure 2.9 [36][28]. If several ﬁlters were chosen, each ﬁlter operates
independently on the input; the dot project between the values of each ﬁlter and the covered pixels
in all channels is calculated independently. The resulting feature map would consist of the number of
feature channels corresponding to the chosen number of ﬁlters.
Figure 2.9 illustrates a multi-channel convolution where one 3×3 ﬁlter is applied to a 5-channel
image [37].

Convolutional ﬁlters detect speciﬁed patterns in an image that depends on the values in the ﬁlter
weight matrix. Each ﬁlter value corresponds to a weight parameter of the network. Filters operating
in the initial layers of the network are low level ﬁlters that detect e.g. edges, squares, and circles. The
deeper in the network, the more sophisticated a ﬁlter becomes, e.g. detecting high level features such
as faces and organs [28][36][37].
Pooling Layer
Pooling is used to downsample the input and typically applied between successive convolutional layers.
It reduces the amount of parameters and network computation by reducing the spatial size of a given
input. This means that the applied convolutional ﬁlter in the subsequent layer covers a larger area
of the original input image. Thereby, higher level detection ﬁlters extract larger and more complex
features. Additionally, pooling contributes to avoid overﬁtting [28].
For the concept of pooling, a 2×2 ﬁlter is applied to the input in Figure 2.10 with stride 2, where four
pixels are pooled to one in the resulting feature map. The stride deﬁnes the step size of the ﬁlter when
sweeping over the input [37][28].

2.5
U-Net
A U-Net is a u-shaped CNN, cf. Figure 2.13, which has been proven favourable to solve medical image
segmentation problems in DL [29]. The U-Net is constructed of an encoding- and a decoding part that
down- and upsamples the input through several operations [9].
An image contains many diﬀerent patterns, which can be diﬃcult for an algorithm to detect and classify
by the original image. For a successful segmentation of a certain pattern, the algorithm needs to look
deeper into details of the image, which is accomplished by downsampling the image and applying
several diﬀerent ﬁlters [9][38].

Encoding
The encoding consist of four blocks and one base block. Each encoding block is constructed
by two 3×3 convolutions, followed by a non-linear ReLU transformation, and a 2×2 max pooling layer
with stride 2. The downsampling increases the degree of details within each pixel, however, reduces
the anatomical localization [37][38].
By the ﬁrst convolution, a chosen number of ﬁlters are applied on the input image. Each feature
channel in the resulting feature map indicates where in the image, if at all, a pattern like the one
described by the ﬁlter is present [9][38]. Down through the encoding path, the number of convolutional
ﬁlters are increased to detect various complexity patterns within the image [37][35].
Decoding
From the base block, the feature map is up-sampled through four blocks. Each decoding
block is constructed by a 2×2 up-convolution with stride 2; a concatenation of the feature maps from the
encoding path; and two 3×3 convolutions followed by a ReLU transformation. The number of feature
channels is halved in every up-convoluting by choosing a lower number of convolutional ﬁlters [37].
The concatenation ensures maintenance of anatomical localization when upsampling the feature
maps. The decoding expands from low spatial resolution and high detail orientation, to a high spatial
resolution segmentation map where the number of output segmentation maps correspond to the number
of prediction classes [37][38].

In DL, normalization is important to ensure cooperation between implemented operations, such as
initialization of weights, regularization, and optimization.
Z-normalization standardizes data according to the mean and standard deviation of a data popu-
lation. Data is transformed to have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one. In the sense of
imaging processing, z-normalization changes the range of pixel values within the image according to
the population. For one data point or one pixel the z-normalization is given by [39][40]:

2.7
Patch Sampling
Image data can be given as input to an algorithm in several ways, e.g. 2D/3D slices or 2D/3D patches
of the image. A 3D image as input is computationally demanding, and therefore 2D slices or 2D/3D
patches are often applied.
J. Xu et al.
[41] presents a 2.5D method where the input consists of
adjacent slices. This method provides 2.5D structural information to the algorithm and thus more
textural information to learn from [9][41].
A patch sampling method is often used when having a small data set, as it creates more input
samples. A patch is a smaller part of the original input, and can be in 2D and 3D, where 3D contributes
with local context in the depth of the 3D image [9].

Data Acquisition

Mask
A mask was deﬁned for each PET/CT scan representing the region of the patient. This was
done to only predict inside the patient and to train the algorithm strictly based on pixel values within
the patient area.
Each mask was deﬁned based on knowledge of HU within a CT. The space surrounding the patient
in a CT image is air that has a HU at approximately −1000, and thus a threshold at −900 was chosen.
Hence, the mask is a matrix of 1 and 0; values higher than −900 equals 1, and values lower equals
0. As the CT and PET per study ID are resampled to each other, the mask is the region of interest
within both scans.
Normalization
A mean and a standard deviation were calculated from each of PET- and CT within
the deﬁned mask.
Each PET and CT ﬁle was z-normalized according to the mean and standard
deviation for PET and CT.
Slice packages
A procedure was implemented to ease the data load when training the algorithm.
NumPy ﬁle ”packages” for each study ID were created, where a NumPy ﬁle ”package” was prepared
for every slice in each PET/CT data set containing the input to the network (PET, CT, mask, and
label) during training, cf. Section 3.6.
Data split
The data was divided into two sets: A training and a validation/test set. The validation
set was also used for testing due to the relatively small data set. A split ratio of 80/20 was chosen; 80%
of the full data set was assigned to the training and 20% was assigned to validation/test. Furthermore,
it was ensured that each of the subtypes was presented in both sets, where a similar split ratio at 80/20
was applied to each subtype, each randomly assigned to both the training and validation/test set. The
main data split applied was denoted ”K1”, cf. Appendix B, where 46 of the pediatric sarcoma study
IDs were assigned to the training set and 12 were assigned to the validation/test set.
The soft tissue sarcoma data from TCIA was solely included in the training set, as it was used with
the intention of giving the network more data to learn from. This resulted in additional 51 patient
data in the training set.
A 5-fold data split was made to implement a 5-fold cross validation of the ﬁnal model, where all
pediatric sarcoma study IDs are presented in one of the ﬁve folds.

3.6
Segmentation Model
A 2D segmentation model adapted from the U-Net was developed, cf. Figure 3.3. The initial model was
built up of four encoding blocks, a base block, and four decoding blocks. Each encoding block consisted
of two convolution blocks and max pooling. A convolution block consisted of 3×3 convolution, batch
normalization, and a ReLU layer. The base block consisted of two convolution blocks. Each decoding
block consisted of a 2×2 up-convolution layer with stride 2, a concatenation from the same level in
the encoding, and two convolution blocks. The ﬁnal layer consisted of a 1×1 convolution followed
by a softmax activation function to map each pixel to a probability in the resultant output with two
channels, tumor or background. A summary of the model layers and its parameters is seen in Appendix
C.

3.6.1
Hyperparameter Optimization
To ﬁnd the best performing segmentation model, a hyperparameter search was performed using the
total data set, i.e. both pediatric sarcoma data and TCIA data. During the hyperparameter search,
the K1 data split was applied, cf. Appendix B. Study IDs not appearing in the K1 data split were used
for training including TCIA data. Each network setup in the hyperparameter search was trained with
800 iterations. The initial network setup used a BS of 16, [8,16,32,64,128] ﬁlters through the block
levels, and without dropout. The best performing model during training was chosen according to the
lowest validation GDL calculated at every 10th iteration.
The hyperparameter optimization was performed in the following order: Loss function and LR, BS,
number of ﬁlters applied in each block level, and dropout. In each step, the parameter(s) in question
was varied while the others were ﬁxated. The optimal parameter was found, and ﬁxated for the next
parameter search.
Loss function and learning rate
A grid search of the loss function and LR was executed. Two loss
functions (GDL and CE) and ﬁve LRs (1e−05, 1e−04, 1e−03, 1e−02, and 1e−01) were investigated.

with 800 iterations for each fold in order to present a model representative for the entire data set. The
validation/test set for each fold is documented in Appendix B.
Evaluation of the ﬁnal model performance on the full data set was based on performance of each
fold, and was subsequently analyzed according to pediatric subtypes and body regions. When evalu-
ating the ﬁnal model according to pediatric subtypes, the segmentation performance of the respective
subtypes from all ﬁve folds were assembled and metric values were calculated for each of the subtypes.
Furthermore, each pediatric sarcoma was categorized to a body region according to the location of
primary tumor, which was either a region including brain, head/neck, thorax, abdomen/pelvis or ex-
tremities. Evaluating the performance in terms of body regions, the segmentation performance in each
of the ﬁve folds that belonged to e.g. the thorax were assembled and metric values calculated for this
body region.
Lastly, the ﬁnal model was trained using the K1 data split through 10,000 iterations to ensure that
the loss had fully converged. The two best models, determined by the validation loss, along with the
last model were evaluated.
Post-processing
The predicted stack of slices obtained from each study ID during the 5-fold cross
validation were converted back to the clinical system. The NumPy ﬁles were converted to a MINC,
resampled to their original dimensions, and converted back to DICOM. This was done in order to
let the clinician evaluate the performance of the model and to include them in development of the
algorithm.

The Inﬂuence of Data
The TCIA data was downloaded and included to enhance the data set and thus improve the perfor-

algorithm excluding this data. The algorithm was trained using the ﬁnal model setup, and the K1

The pediatric sarcoma data consists of several subtypes, each having diﬀerent properties that may
have an eﬀect on the overall performance of the model.
The inﬂuence of each pediatric sarcoma subtype was evaluated. The group Others was decided to
exclude and TCIA data was included for training in all models. The subtypes ES, OS, and RMS were
excluded one at a time, which resulted in four models that were trained, validated, and tested only
including the following subtypes:
• Subtype model 1 includes ES, OS, and RMS (+TCIA in training)
• Subtype model 2 includes ES and OS (+TCIA in training)
• Subtype model 3 includes ES and RMS (+TCIA in training)
• Subtype model 4 includes OS and RMS (+TCIA in training)
The K1 data split was used for this investigation where pediatric sarcomas belonging to the excluded
subtype (when not listed in the above) were removed from the training and validation/test set. Each
of the above models was compared to the ﬁnal model including the full data set.
3.8
Developmental Journey
Throughout this project, a lot of experience has been gathered and used for decision support, which
has given a fundamental intuition of the operating U-Net. These ongoing experiences are not reported
in the results in Chapter 4, as they are per se not self-standing results. Though, they have been a
great part of the developmental research process, and thus we found worth to report as a part of the
method.
Size of data set
At the project start, imaging data of 109 pediatric sarcomas were intended to be
included. However, 51 of the imaging data showed not to contain primary tumor, which was why
these could not be included for the purpose of tumor segmentation. The already small data set was
thereby nearly halved. Moreover, pediatric sarcomas are a complex type of cancer and a small data
set may complicate the algorithm’s ability to learn all of its specialties. Hence, the data from TCIA
was decided to be included due to the very small data set.
The reconstructed pediatric imaging data had diﬀerent x-y-dimensions and pixel spacing, which
was necessary to change due to the required algorithm input size of a 400×400 matrix. The pixel
spacing was not a requirement from the algorithm, but diﬀerent pixel spacing entails diﬀerent ﬁeld
of views and resolutions of the images and could possibly complicate the learning process. Thus, we
thought it necessary to rescale to one ﬁeld of view to enhance the algorithm’s ability to learn e.g.
anatomical proportions. It would be favourable to have a standardized reconstructed data set to avoid
manipulating operations.

Data load
The data per study ID consists of a stack of 2D slices, which initially were saved as one
3D matrix NumPy ﬁle. First, the most obvious approach was to randomly load a 3D NumPy ﬁle
from the training set and then randomly draw out a single input slice to the algorithm (incl. adjacent
slices). This way of loading data showed to be very time consuming, as it took much longer time for
the Central Processing Unit (CPU) to load the input data than for the GPU to train the algorithm
with one input. Hence, the approach to load the data was reevaluated.
The new implemented loading approach included the explained ”slice packages”, cf. Section 3.6,
which to a great extent reduced the time of one training session of 800 iterations from approximately
16-17 hours to 1-2 hours.
Sampling strategy
The data sampling strategy has been a consideration throughout this project.
At the start of this project, a 3D version of the U-Net architecture was considered using overlapping
3D patches of size 32×32×32 as input. This method was the initial approach as it addresses class
imbalance and increase the amount of input data. However, the overall anatomical context was reduced
and challenges arose due to the complexity of pediatric sarcomas. The volume of the pediatric sarcomas
ranges from 3.7 cm3 to 4302.8 cm3 with a mean of 250.2 cm3. Thus, a great part of the sarcomas were
too large to be contained within the patch; examples are shown in Appendix F. This would impede the
algorithms’ ability of recognizing and separating textural patterns and borders of tissue and organs.
A larger patch could have been chosen, but this would highly extend the computational cost. Thus,
the 2.5D approach was preferred where the 3D-aspect manifest by including three adjacent slices. This
was a conscious decision despite reintroducing class imbalance and reduced local context.
Randomization
In this project, each model was trained one time. However, experiences showed
that ideally each model should have been trained a minimum of three times in order to present the
most representative model in the results. Unfortunately, this would require much more time than this
project has allowed.
Although, few of the models was trained twice with similar model setup and data split to observe the
outcome. These models is not included in the results, but they have contributed to the understanding
of the algorithm’s ability to learn and the uncertainty in the results. These models are presented in
Section D.4 in Appendix D along with an explanation of what is observed. In brief, these extra training
sessions support the importance of working with a representative large data set in order to present a
robust and reproducible outcome model.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this Master’s Thesis, deep learning was used for segmentation of pediatric sarcomas with the ﬁnal
aim of paving the way for stratiﬁcation studies. May deep learning aid in the decision making of the
most optimal treatment for each individual child with a sarcoma in the future? In the following, results
are considered and discussed in relation to state-of-the-art, strengths, and limitations.
Chapter 4 presented results, which showed the ﬁnal model to have a mean performance on the entire
pediatric sarcoma data set of a dice score (DS) at 0.36, precision at 0.57, and sensitivity at 0.42.
The results showed a correlation between segmentation performance and tumor location, and fur-
thermore between segmentation performance and subtype. Tumors within the thorax had a higher
segmentation performance compared to other body regions. An investigation of pediatric sarcoma
subtypes revealed varying inﬂuence on the model performance where ES showed to be diﬃcult to seg-
ment when the model was trained only including ES and one additional subtype group. Subsequently,
TCIA data did not have an inﬂuence on the model performance.
5.1
Segmentation Model
Parameters in the conﬁgured model were investigated to determine the best performing model. Results
from the hyperparameter search are discussed in the following.
Two loss functions along with ﬁve LRs were explored in a grid search, and the best model was chosen
by a trade oﬀbetween a high score in the evaluation metrics and a model that is capable of good
generalization.
Comparing the model performance of the two loss functions showed that the GDL outperformed
the CE and where GDL showed to handle the tumor-background imbalance better. In a tumor seg-
mentation task, the background is often easier to predict with a high accuracy, as it often takes up
more space within the medical image compared to the tumor. This class imbalance is penalized in the
GDL where an invariance correction of the two classes is performed by adding a weighting factor to
the loss of each class.
Selecting an adequate LR required some considerations. Between every iteration, the learnable param-
eters are updated to minimize the loss; a large change (large LR) risks to bring the model away from
the global minimum. Contrary, a small LR may require more iterations prior to convergence of the
loss. Moreover, it is possible that the system ends in a local minimum, and the algorithm may not be
45
==PAGE:64==
Chapter 5.
Discussion
able to take steps large enough to withdraw from it.
Even though LR=1e−05 showed better generalization performance, higher metric values were fa-
vored, as the generalization can be improved by adjusting hyperparameters in the regularization-terms.
LR=1e−02 was chosen, which is thought to be a high LR. However, as we use the Adam optimizer,
the LR adapts over time, which may entail that the algorithm avoids deviating from a global minimum
trajectory.
Investigation of the BS pointed equally towards both BS of 8 and 16, even though we expected that
a larger BS would result in a better performance.
This was assumed as a high BS induces more
regularization in terms of batch normalization, and simultaneously the number of iterations can be
decreased due to a sooner convergence of the loss. In addition, a low BS may introduce more noise
to the system, especially if the BS is not representative of the entire data set, which is manifested by
ﬂuctuations of the loss, which can be observed in Figure D.1 in Appendix D. This points towards that
a higher BS should be favoured, however, due to the model uncertainties described in Section 3.8, both
BSs were applied in the search of the number of ﬁlters.
Increasing the number of ﬁlters in each layer of the network also increases the number of learnable
parameters. We suspected that the highest number of ﬁlters would show the best performance, as the
model would be able to comprehend more features from the complex data set. However, results showed
that Filter 32 led to an underestimation of the tumor, which may be explained by an inadequate number
of iterations and/or that the algorithm does not require more feature detectors (ﬁlters) to improve its
performance. As BS of 8 combined with Filter 16 showed the overall best performance, this model was
chosen.
Adding dropout to the model expanded the computational cost, which was unexpected as the
number of learnable parameters is reduced when units are left out of the network. This could be
explained by the manual implementation of dropout in the network.
However, the better model
performance in terms of generalization revealed the regularization strength of adding dropout.
5.2
Final Model
The ﬁnal model was trained on the entire data set by a 5-fold cross validation and the obtained results
are discussed in the following.
Considering results from the cross validation, a great variety in performance was observed between
the ﬁve folds, despite approximately even distributions of subtypes in each fold. Furthermore, a high
variance in performance was observed within each of the subtypes, cf. Table D.7 in Appendix D,
which demonstrates the pronounced diversity between the tumors within the same subtype. This may
indicate that the size of the data set may be too small to be representative for each subtype.
Results analyzed in terms of body regions revealed a strong correlation between performance and tumor
location; the best performance was observed for tumors within the thorax. The empty space (air) in the
lungs may ease the identiﬁcation of tumors compared to other body regions such as abdomen/pelvis
where organs and tissues may camouﬂage possible anatomical changes and where e.g. kidneys and the
bladder are located and typically appears as hotspots in the PET.
Tumor segmentations near the brain often resulted in a high sensitivity, but low DS and precision,
which was caused by segmentation of the brain, cf. Figure 4.7. The brain typically appears as a hotspot
in a PET image caused by high glucose metabolism and thereby a high tracer uptake. Therefore, it
46
==PAGE:65==
Chapter 5.
Discussion
was not surprising that the brain was also segmented, as a detection of the tumor has not been
accomplished prior to segmentation. Thus, the algorithm was not expected to discard other typical
hotspots as the brain.
Segmentation of tumors in the extremities was anticipated to perform well, however, a tendency
was observed that the model did not segment the entire tumor when bone was included, examples are
seen in Figure 4.11, D.12, and D.15. The clinician determines bone involvement by identifying if a
periosteal reaction is present, which can be observed as a diﬀuse pattern around the inﬁltrated bone
in the CT image, cf. Figure 4.11. This diﬀused pattern combined with limited cases and the low SUV
in the tumor inﬁltrated bone may explain why the algorithm has not been able to segment the entire
tumor in these cases.
The tumor of study ID 13 had a SUVmax of 5.7 g/ml, however, the SUV was much lower in the
non-segmented areas of the tumor, cf. Figure 4.8. Observing the relation between the DS and SUV
in Figure D.18, a trend is observed, showing that a SUV< 5 g/ml makes it diﬃcult for the algorithm
to segment the tumor. In such cases, local context has shown to be important. The three adjacent
PET and CT slices have shown not to be suﬃcient in this case due to the large variety in the tumor
characteristics and SUV from slice to slice.
The tumor segmentation of study ID 68 in Figure 4.10 scored over average performance. However,
the performance was negatively inﬂuenced by the faulty segmented area present in the left kidney seen
in the axial slice. These false positives were observed in all segmentations, primarily in hotspot areas.
This is assumed reasonable, as the algorithm has only been trained with 800 iterations, which may be
insuﬃcient in order to learn the entire background that accounts for the greatest part of the data set.
For example, if the brain covers approximately 5% of the axial slices in a full body scan, a training
with a BS of 8 along with 800 iterations would entail that the algorithm has only been exposed to 160
slices including brain. Consequently, the lack of training samples from these diﬃcult background areas
induces false positives in the results.
When the ﬁnal model was trained with 10,000 iterations, the precision remained similar throughout
the iterations while the DS and sensitivity were improved, cf. Figure 4.16. This suggests that the
algorithm quickly learns to recognize tumor (fairly high precision), but with presence of false positives
and false negatives, indicated by lower DS and sensitivity in the lowest iterated model. Throughout
the 10,000 iterations the amount of false positives and false negatives were reduced causing the higher
DS and sensitivity.
From Figure D.21, Appendix D, the loss is estimated to converge around 2000 iterations. However,
since the data consist of 4091 slices with tumor pixels (excl. augmented data), using a BS of 8 requires
approximately 4000 iterations before the algorithm has been exposed to all tumor slices, as the input
is background slices 50% of the time. Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that the improvement in
the evaluation metrics at 10,000 iterations can be attributed to ﬁne tuning and a reduction of false
positives and negatives.
These results indicate that the algorithm should have been trained by a larger number of iterations.
Furthermore, the training of the model should have been continued above the 10,000 iterations to
investigate if false positives and negatives could have been further reduced.
5.3
The Inﬂuence of Data
The inﬂuence of training the algorithm with TCIA data and subsets of the pediatric sarcoma data set
are discussed in the following.

Comparing the two models with and without TCIA data, no distinct diﬀerences in the performance
were observed. In general, the more data available, the better in order to introduce the algorithm to
as broad variety of data as possible. Thus, our expectation was that TCIA would have had a greater
improving impact on the model performance. In this case, including TCIA did not appear to contribute
with any new and extra characteristics to learn from. This may be explained by the fact that TCIA
solely consist of soft tissue sarcomas, and may only be representative for RMS and thereby for less
than half of the pediatric sarcoma data.
This contributes to the concluding remarks that the algorithm is in need of more specialized data,
by which we mean data representing characteristics and unique cases of the pediatric sarcoma subtypes.
The subtypes of the pediatric sarcomas are known to vary in size, heterogeneity, and location, which
is why we expected them to be diﬃcult for the algorithm to learn and thereby segment. When the
algorithm was trained with a subset of pediatric sarcoma subtypes, the subtype group Others was
purposely excluded due to the lack of deﬁning characteristics. However, omitting this group showed a
slightly decrease in model performance (ES-OS-RMS, Figure 4.19), indicating that Others may include
tumors sharing characteristics with under-represented tumors among the other subtypes.
We expected that the inclusion of only ES-OS would exhibit an increase in performance, as they
are bone sarcomas. However, it was the OS-RMS model that had the overall best performance, while
ES-OS and ES-RMS both performed poorly in comparison. To all appearances, ES may be a more
diverse subtype compared to OS, while the segmentation of RMSs may be enhanced by the inclusion
of the TCIA data. ESs often contain both bone and soft tissue involvement whereas OS more often
only involve bone. The OS subtype is in general ”hotter” due to an often higher SUV compared to
ES, that is again higher than RMS, which have shown to also inﬂuence the segmentation performance.
5.4
Strengths and Limitations
Strengths and limitations of the model is discussed in the following, which also form the basis for
possible optimization and investigations in future work.
It would be expected that in order to make it possible for an algorithm to recognize and segment a
speciﬁc tumor type, a unique and identiﬁable set of characteristics should be well deﬁned; e.g. location,
tumor size, histology, tracer uptake, and patient age and size. However, the subtypes altogether can
be located in almost all parts of the body, and can emanate from both bones, muscle, and connecting
tissue. Moreover, the tracer uptake and anatomical structure are seen varying depending on location
and tumor conditions. Thus, the task of learning the individual characteristics of the subtypes showed
to be a challenge, and may explain why it was diﬃcult to produce reproducible models. However,
despite all of these challenging factors, the developed model has proven to handle the task of identifying
and segmenting a great part of the pediatric sarcomas.
Including all pediatric sarcoma subtypes have revealed to be a strength as results showed a more
generalizing model. Cases, which did challenge the model in tumor segmentation were observed espe-
cially when the tumor had a low tracer uptake, e.g. study ID 57 (ES) in Figure 4.9 and 74 (OS) and
87 (Others) in Appendix B. This indicates that the algorithm may be more inﬂuenced by the PET
than the CT, and therefore lack the ability to recognize anatomical abnormalities.

The optimal combination of hyperparameters changes depending on the network architecture and the
data. Hence, no distinct rules are speciﬁed in order to choose the hyperparameter values, besides
intuition along with observed tendencies and performances.
Various methods exist to optimize these parameters. In the initial search, we used a simple grid
search, which gives a coarse overview of the performance and enables to observe and learn general
tendencies. However, other methods such as a random grid search takes a range of hyperparameters
and test them randomly, which may reveal more optimal combinations of hyperparameters, as real
numbers may not be the best choice. A random grid search requires a higher number of searches to
represent the full picture of possible combinations, and therefore, this approach was not chosen, due
to time constraints.
In the evaluation metrics for the entire stack of slices including both slices containing tumor and non-
tumor, cf. Appendix E, a large amount of segmented false positives were observed. As this project
has focused on the task of segmenting tumors, these false positives have not been considered a direct
limitation. However, it was desired to implement a fully automatic algorithm in which it would be
favorable to expand the algorithm to manage false positive in order to minimize the clinician’s time
used to correct for these.
In the long run it may be beneﬁcial that the model is able to ﬁnd and segment other ”hotspots”
than just the tumor such as metastasis. Suggestions could be to apply an algorithm that is developed
to classify and separate other known anatomical ”hotspots” prior to detection and segmentation of the
tumor and possible metastasis.
All pediatric sarcoma segmentations were imported back to the clinical system, which is an important
outcome of the research in order for the clinicians to eventually apply results of a research project like
this, and furthermore if it should be possible to implement and use in the clinic.
5.5
Comparing to Literature
To the best of our knowledge, no studies on DL models applied to pediatric sarcoma data exist. How-
ever, two studies which have applied similar image modalities, evaluation metrics, and input strategy
as this project are discussed for comparison.
In a recent study by Han [46], a liver lesion segmentation algorithm was developed using a data
set consisting of 200 abdominal contrast-enhanced CT scans. Han used a combination of the U-Net
and ResNet and applied the 2.5D input method using ﬁve adjacent slices. Initially, a coarse liver
segmentation on all slices (whole body) was performed by lowering the resolution of the images. This
was followed by a second model that only reprocessed slices from the segmented liver region with the
original image resolution in order to detect and segment the liver lesion. The proposed model was
reported to have a mean DS of 0.67 resulting in a ﬁrst place in the Liver Tumor Segmentation (LiTS)
challenge at the time of the International Symposium on Biomedical Imaging (ISBI) 2017 conference
[46].
Another study, reported in the Medical Image Computing and Computer Assisted Intervention
(MICCAI) 2018 PET segmentation challenge paper [47], developed a 3D version of the U-Net seg-
menting in a tumor bounding box. The input was given as 3D patches and consisted of 176 cases
including simulated, synthetic phantom data, and a clinical data set of 25 cases of lung and head/neck
cancer. This method won the PET segmentation challenge achieving the highest accuracy at 0.67,
49
==PAGE:68==

deﬁned by 0.5 × sensitivity + 0.5 × precision, on the clinical data set [47].
The results from the developed algorithm in this project were particularly lower than the scores ob-
tained in these studies. However, a main diﬀerence is that their data sets consisted of tumors located
in a speciﬁc area, which could ease their segmentation task compared to our approach. Furthermore,
the beneﬁt of conducting Han’s detection prior to segmentation makes it possible to become special-
ized within the structure of the liver. Moreover, Han used the 2.5D input method, but included ﬁve
adjacent slices compared to our three adjacent slices, which may have enhanced the local context. The
PET tumor challenge was not challenged by detecting the tumor, as the input data only consisted of
a deﬁned tumor bounding box. Thereby, the approach was not depending on anatomical context.
The segmentation task in this project was challenging due to the tumor complexity and the various
tumor locations. Therefore, it may be reasonable that our accuracy is lower, as our approach used
the original image without the need to deﬁne the tumor area ﬁrst. However, comparing our results
based on regions, a DS at 0.6 along with a accuracy at 0.62 (0.5 × sensitivity + 0.5 × precision) for
the thorax was obtained, which is close to that of referred studies. This attests that it may not be
the developed algorithm, but again the amount and complexity of the data that are the challenge of
achieving a good performance with a high generalization.

5.7
Future Perspectives
Future perspectives are contemplated and discussed, which lay the foundation for further work and
considerations to be made to optimize the conﬁgured algorithm.
One challenge for the segmentation algorithm was experienced to be the segmentation of false posi-
tives. It is desirable to lower the segmentation of hotspots and other false positives to improve the
overall performance of the model, which could be approached by developing an algorithm favourable
for detection of tumors prior to the segmentation. Furthermore, it is worth to investigate if the false
positives may be further reduced by increasing the number of iterations to more than 10,000.
The implemented algorithm for segmentation of pediatric sarcomas may be further optimized by in-
vestigating the impact of other hyperparameters, e.g. the size of the convolutional ﬁlter and the depth
of the network. A random grid search could be carried out including combinations of other hyperpa-
rameters and an expansion of the parameters already investigated in this project.

Thoughts and experiences regarding the input sampling strategy obtained in this study have already
been mentioned.
Future considerations have included to sample in sagittal and coronal planes to
imitate 3D anatomical context. If the algorithm received a sagittal tumor slice from the middle of
the body, it would contain (besides the tumor) the brain, bladder, and heart; all in one slice. Thus,
to investigate if the algorithm would be better to identify anatomical structures, as it ”sees” them in
context with the tumor.
Another consideration was to implement a 3D U-Net and sample with 3D patches greater than
32×32×32 to maintain local context and combine this with a detection of the tumor prior to segmen-
tation. Thus, the computational demanding task of training the algorithm when using larger patches
would be constricted to only become specialized within the tumor area.
The imaging data input applied in this project has been PET and CT slices. However, it is known
that MRI scans have been used in the hand-drawn delineation process to support the registration on
the PET/CT slices. MRI may be useful for additional image guidance as the sarcomas involve both
soft tissue and bones, which both are well contrasted in MRIs. Thus, it is worth to consider expanding
the model input to also include MRI slices, which could provide new and more anatomical information
for the algorithm to learn from.
Finally, a future perspective is to someday apply the algorithm on a larger patient study to be able to
segment tumors not relying on individual drawing skills at diﬀerent institutions and avoid interobserver
variability. This would aid in risk stratiﬁcation studies, and thereby be able to give more personalized
treatment by discovering new patterns in tumor types.

Conclusion
The work of this Master’s Thesis has developed and optimized a deep learning algorithm to automatic
segment tumors of pediatric sarcoma patients on PET/CT images. The algorithm was based on a 2D
U-Net architecture favorable for medical image segmentation.
The optimal combination of hyperparameters was found through several investigations and grid searches.
The resultant ﬁnal model managed to segment the majority of the pediatric tumors despite a complex
and limited amount of training data. A correlation was observed between segmentation performance
and subtypes and furthermore between segmentation performance and the location of the tumor.
The work with the pediatric sarcoma data set revealed high variation of characteristics within each
subtype. Thus, the need for more data representing unique cases are crucial to improve the perfor-
mance and generalization of the model.
Further optimization and validation are warranted prior to implementation and use in the clinic.
However, if improvements prove successful and reliable, an automatic segmentation approach would
have a great impact on and pave the way for identiﬁcation of undiscovered characteristics of existing
pediatric sarcoma subtypes.
