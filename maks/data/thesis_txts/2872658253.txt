Abstract 
Glucagon is secreted by the α-cells in the pancreas, where it travels to the liver and induces glucose 
production. Thus, glucagon is a key hormone in regulating plasma glucose. However, a dysregula-
tion of glucagon levels is often seen in obesity and metabolic diseases such as metabolic dysfunc-
tion-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) and diabetes, resulting in hyperglucagonemia. A 
potential impaired hepatic glucagon elimination may play a role in these elevated glucagon levels. It 
is acknowledged that the kidneys excrete glucagon. However, whether the liver plays a role in the 
hepatic elimination of glucagon and its mechanisms is not yet established. 
Hence, this project aims to investigate the potential role of the liver in the elimination of glucagon. 
It will further be assessed whether enzymatic cleavage or receptor-mediated internalization is the 
primary route of elimination. To explore this, in situ liver perfusions were conducted on male lean 
mouse models. Moreover, glucagon elimination was also assessed in diet-induced obese (DIO) 
mice. Comparing hepatic glucagon elimination between these models will help evaluate how obe-
sity-associated metabolic changes may affect glucagon clearance. 
In summary, between 9 and 20% of glucagon was eliminated by the liver in male lean mice. Fur-
thermore, we suggest that glucagon is primarily eliminated by receptor-mediated internalization ra-
ther than enzymatic cleavage by dipeptidyl peptidase (DPP) IV and neprilysin (NEP) in the liver of 
male lean mice. Additionally, it was observed that this elimination was impaired in male DIO mice. 
These findings suggest that obesity influences the glucagon elimination in the liver, contributing to 
hyperglucagonemia.

1 Background 
1.1 The history of glucagon 
In 1922, Kimball and Murlin identified a pancreatic substance that increased blood glucose in rab-
bits and dogs. They named this substance glucagon for glucose-agonist (1). Later in 1953, glucagon 
was purified and crystallized (2), and in 1957, the amino acid sequence of glucagon was determined 
as a 29-amino-acid-long peptide (3). This led to the development of the first glucagon radioim-
munoassay (RIA) by Roger Unger and colleagues in 1959 (4), allowing the possibility of measuring 
glucagon concentrations in plasma. Approximately twenty years later, Raskin and Unger discovered 
that diabetes patients exhibited increased plasma glucagon concentration (5). These findings, along 
with earlier research, highlighted the physiological importance of glucagon for the homeostasis of 
blood glucose levels. In recent years, this growing understanding has expanded glucagon’s role in 
glucose regulation. Glucagon agonists are being investigated as weight-reducing agents in combina-
tion with two other hormone peptides, glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) and glucose-dependent in-
sulinotropic polypeptide (6). Hence, the importance of glucagon in therapeutic medicine has never 
been greater.  
 
1.2 Processing and secretion of glucagon 
Glucagon is derived from proglucagon, a 160-amino-acid-long peptide, encoded in the GCG gene, 
and is well conserved across species. Proglucagon is expressed in pancreatic α-cells, intestinal L 
cells, and in a subset of neurons from the brain stem (7, 8). 
In the α-cells, glucagon is cleaved from proglucagon by prohormone convertase (PC)-2. This enzy-
matic activity controls the glucagon production and secretion. By contrast, in the brain and the in-
testine, differential processing of proglucagon by PC-1/3 gives rise to several other peptides, 
namely GLP-1, glucagon-like peptide 2 (GLP-2), glicentin, and oxyntomodulin (8). The importance 
of PC-2 in processing proglucagon to glucagon was observed across studies, as glucagon was unde-
tectable in PC-2-deficient mice (9, 10), confirming the role of PC-2 in processing proglucagon to 
glucagon.  
One of the key factors influencing glucagon secretion is the concentration of glucose. α-cells sup-
press glucagon secretion during elevated plasma glucose concentrations. In contrast, increased glu-
cagon levels occur at low glucose levels (11). Other factors, such as amino acids, also play a pivotal 
role in the secretion of glucagon. For instance, arginine, alanine, glycine, and proline provide stim-
uli to glucagon secretion (12). Hence, a protein-rich meal would increase glucagon secretion, 
whereas a carbohydrate-rich meal would inhibit glucagon secretion (13). This nutrient-dependent

Page 2
 
regulation of glucagon highlights its role in maintaining metabolic balance after food intake. In ad-
dition to nutrient signals, it has been reported that neural and endocrine factors influence the secre-
tion of glucagon as well (14, 15), underscoring the complexity of its regulation.  
 
1.3 Glucagon measurement  
The plasma concentration of glucagon differs depending on whether it is measured in peripheral or 
portal blood. This is due to the pancreas draining its blood into the portal vein (Figure 1). Further-
more, it also facilitates the transport of nutrient-rich blood from the gastrointestinal organs to the 
liver. Circulating glucagon levels measured in the peripheral blood are between 5 and 50 pM, de-
pendent on fasted and fed conditions. However, portal glucagon levels are 10-fold higher than pe-
ripheral blood, meaning that the glucagon concentration reaching the liver can range between 50 to 
500 pM (16).

However, one of the biggest limitations of studying glucagon is the challenge of obtaining reliable 
and accurate measurements from glucagon assays due to cross-reactivity. Sutherland and de Duve 
identified that the glucagon-like compounds from the gut could interfere with glucagon measure-
ments (17) and later showed to be functionally different from pancreatic glucagon (13). Therefore, 
it is important to highlight the methodological and technological advances allowing reliable and ac-
curate measurements of glucagon concentrations in plasma.  
At least five glucagon-like molecules circulate in the body. The number may be even higher due to 
incomplete or additional protein cleavage, which challenges the accuracy of glucagon assays. One 
==PAGE:10==
Page 3
 
of the earliest and well-known antibodies targeting glucagon, developed by Unger (4), targets the C-
terminus of glucagon, as illustrated in Figure 2. Due to the targeted area, no major cross-reactivity 
occurs. Only glucagon 1-61 can interfere with the C-terminal-targeting antibody, which is found in 
low plasma concentrations. However, studies have found that individuals with chronic liver disease 
have elevated levels of glucagon 1-61 (13), which can cause higher cross-reactivity. Therefore, the 
selection of an appropriate analysis method is crucial.  
Antibodies targeting the mid-region of glucagon will bind several other proglucagon by-products, 
such as glicentin, glucagon 1-61, and oxyntomodulin, resulting in higher cross-reactivity. Lastly, 
the N-terminal targeted antibodies also cross-react with other glucagon-like molecules, such as ox-
yntomodulin (13). An alternative method to the mentioned is the sandwich enzyme-linked immuno-
sorbent assay (ELISA), which is based on antibodies targeting both N- and C-termini. This method 
is very precise and accurate, as the assay is based on two antibodies binding to each end of the mol-
ecule. Therefore, this assay also allows measurement of very low glucagon concentrations down to 
2 pM (18).

1.4 Functions and actions of glucagon 
Glucagon promotes its biological action through binding and activation of the glucagon receptor 
(GCGR), a seven-transmembrane G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) located on the cell surface 
(8). As GCGR is predominantly expressed in the liver (19), glucagon primarily acts on hepatocytes. 
However, GCGR is also expressed in several other organs, including the kidney, and throughout the 
gastrointestinal tract (8). The GCGR was first described in 1971 by Rodbell and his colleagues as a 
part of the adenylate cyclase system (20).  
In response to glucagon stimulation, downstream signaling modulates various physiological pro-
cesses, enhancing certain pathways while suppressing others, as illustrated in Figure 3. Upon gluca-
gon binding to the GCGR on the hepatic cell surface, cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) lev-
els rise. This leads to the activation of protein kinase A (PKA) that phosphorylates multiple targets, 
including glycogen phosphorylase kinase, which in turn activates glycogen phosphorylase, promot-
ing glycogen breakdown via glycogenolysis. The production of glucose 6-phosphate in glycogenol-
ysis is then converted into glucose by glucose-6-phosphatase, resulting in increased glucose produc-
tion. While glucagon signaling promotes glycogenolysis, it also inhibits glycogen synthesis in the 
liver by inactivating glycogen synthase (21), thereby shifting hepatic glucose metabolism toward 
glucose production and release.

travels to the liver and stimulates gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis, and the urea cycle. The upregulation of 
gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis leads to increased glucose production, resulting in elevated blood glucose levels. 
On the other hand, glycolysis is decreased due to reduced fructose 1,6-bisphosphate levels and decreased pyruvate 
levels as a result of activated pyruvate kinase. Furthermore, lipid and amino acid metabolism are also enhanced, leading 
to a reduction in lipid and amino acid levels (7). Created in Biorender.com.

Glucagon stimulation promotes gluconeogenesis as well. Firstly, the activated PKA upon glucagon 
stimulation phosphorylates cAMP response element-binding (CREB) protein, leading to the upregu-
lation of the phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase (PEPCK). This enzyme catalyzes the conversion 
of oxaloacetate to phosphoenolpyruvate. Next, the glucagon-activated PKA phosphorylates 6-phos-
phofructo-2-kinase/fructose-2,6-bisphosphatase (PFK-2/FBPase-2), which will reduce the levels of 
fructose-2,6-bisphosphate (F-2,6-P2). Since F-2,6-P2 normally inhibits fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, 
the decrease of F-2,6-P2 promotes gluconeogenesis by allowing the conversion of fructose-1,6-
bisphosphate to fructose-6-phosphate. In parallel, PKA also phosphorylates pyruvate kinase, result-
ing in inhibition of the enzyme. This leads to a reduced conversion of phosphoenolpyruvate to py-
ruvate, causing inhibition of glycolysis. And finally, glucagon increases glucose-6-phosphatase ex-
pression and activity, promoting the conversion of glucose-6-phosphate to free glucose, the final 
step of gluconeogenesis.  
The glucose levels in the blood circulation rely on glycogenolysis during fasting for less than 12 
hours. However, as glycogen stores are used up, the maintenance of normal blood glucose levels 
depends on gluconeogenesis (7, 21). The glucose-raising effect of glucagon has been confirmed in 
several studies, where administration of exogenous glucagon showed increased glucose levels in 
dogs (22) and in humans (23).  
As mentioned earlier, glucagon secretion is stimulated by a protein-rich meal. This is because 
amino acids are strong stimulators of glucagon secretion. Upon GCGR binding, glucagon promotes 
the amino acid uptake by increasing the expression of amino acid transporters (24). Additionally, 
glucagon increases hepatic amino acid metabolism due to increased capacity and rate of the urea 
cycle (25) while also upregulating the transcription of several enzymes for ureagenesis (26). Urea-
genesis prevents hyperammonemia, which is caused by increased amino acid catabolism by con-
verting toxic ammonia into urea, a nontoxic molecule excreted in the urine (24). Glucagon also 
stimulates lipid metabolism in the liver and enhances the cholesterol clearance from the circulation 
(27). Moreover, glucagon stimulates β-oxidation, a process where long-chain fatty acids are de-
graded into acetyl-CoA, and inhibits triglyceride (TG) synthesis, reducing hepatic TG content (24).  
These diverse effects of glucagon are coordinated through the liver α-cell axis, where the secretion 
of glucagon is regulated by its actions in the liver in a feedback manner. An increase in plasma lev-
els of amino acids stimulates glucagon secretion, which in turn regulates the hepatic amino acid up-
take by promoting transport into the hepatocytes and supporting the amino acid metabolism via ure-
agenesis and gluconeogenesis (28). This results in a lowering of plasma amino acids and normaliza-
tion of glucagon secretion. Moreover, low levels of circulating glucose stimulate secretion of gluca-
gon, resulting in increased hepatic glucose production. In return, high levels of glucose inhibit fur-
ther secretion of glucagon, leading to decreased glucose production and normalization of the liver-
alpha cell axis. The last component of the liver α-axis is the regulation of hepatic lipid metabolism. 
==PAGE:13==
Page 6
 
However, the mechanisms behind this regulation and whether lipids regulate glucagon secretion 
from α-cells are not established (24).  
 
1.5 The elimination of glucagon 
After glucagon is secreted, it undergoes elimination from the blood circulation. In humans, pigs, 
mice, and dogs, the half-life is between 2-6 minutes, dependent on the species (29-32). It is widely 
acknowledged that the kidneys are a major site of glucagon excretion, as supported by multiple 
studies (30, 33, 34).  
Several mechanisms contribute to the fast elimination of glucagon, such as enzymatic cleavage. The 
enzyme dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP IV) is a serine protease and is widely expressed in several or-
gans, including the intestine, kidney, liver, and islets. Additionally, it exists both in membrane-
bound and soluble forms. Both forms are active and exhibit enzymatic activity. DPP IV preferen-
tially cleaves its substrate at position two from the N-terminus, containing a proline or alanine resi-
due. However, peptides with glycine, valine, serine, threonine, or leucine at the second position can 
also be cleaved (35). DPP IV hydrolyses glucagon 1-29 to glucagon 3-29 and then glucagon 5-29 in 
human plasma (36, 37).  
A study from Baumeier et al. (2016) reported DPP IV expression in both mouse and human liver 
samples. Furthermore, they observed that diet-induced obese (DIO) mice had increased expression 
and release of DPP IV and concluded that their observations indicate that the liver plays an essential 
role in regulating circulating levels of DPP IV (38). Despite these reported enzymatic activities, 
Deacon and colleagues did not see any significant degradation of glucagon by DPP IV in porcine 
plasma. Additionally, the authors did not find the liver to play any role in glucagon elimination 
(30).  
Another enzyme involved in glucagon cleavage is the insulin-degrading enzyme (IDE), which is a 
zinc metalloprotease. Its name is based on its ability to bind and degrade insulin, but it has also been 
shown by X-ray crystallography and in vitro that it binds and degrades glucagon (39). It is ex-
pressed in various cell types and is primarily localized in the cytosol, but has also been found in in-
tracellular vesicles, organelles, on membranes, and secreted into the extracellular space (40). IDE 
cleaves glucagon into glucagon 1-16, glucagon 1-17, and glucagon 18-29 (41, 42). However, there 
is conflicting evidence regarding its role in glucagon cleavage, as other researchers did not observe 
any changes in plasma glucagon in IDE-deficient mice (43). 
Glucagon is also a substrate for the enzyme neutral endopeptidase 24.11, also known as neprilysin 
(NEP). NEP is a membrane-bound zinc metallopeptidase that has been reported to play an essential 
role in the breakdown of glucagon in pigs (44), mice, and humans (45). Kjeldsen and colleagues 
==PAGE:14==
Page 7
 
demonstrated that recombinant NEP cleaves glucagon within 15 minutes of incubation. They inves-
tigated the fragments of glucagon generated by NEP cleavage and identified five fragments, namely 
glucagon 1-21, glucagon 1-19, glucagon 1-18, glucagon 5-18, and glucagon 1-13, which did not ac-
tivate, bind, or antagonize the glucagon receptor signaling in vitro. Furthermore, they observed that 
NEP inhibition reduces the degradation of glucagon due to NEP-mediated glucagon cleavage in 
mice (45).  
Findings imply that the internalization of other GPCRs within the same family as the GCGR, such 
as the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R), is crucial for its functionality in signaling and cross-talk with 
other receptors (46). The GLP-1R is regulated through ligand-mediated receptor internalization and 
is either recycled back to the cell surface or degraded (47). Given these mechanisms, it is plausible 
that a similar process also applies to GCGR and is a route of hepatic glucagon elimination. Some 
studies have proposed that GCGR, together with its ligand, glucagon, was internalized in vivo in 
rats (48-50). Furthermore, other studies suggested that internalized glucagon is proteolyzed within 
hepatic endosomes before and after ATP-dependent endosomal acidification. Before acidification, 
an endosomal serine protease mediates glucagon proteolysis, which is at neutral pH, whereas after 
acidification, endosomal cathepsins B and D mediate the glucagon proteolysis (51, 52).  
Despite numerous studies on glucagon elimination, it is still debated whether the liver plays a role 
in the elimination of glucagon across the liver, and which organ is responsible for the glucagon 
elimination in humans. Grøndahl et al. (2024) suggested that the kidneys are the main site of gluca-
gon elimination in humans and not the liver, as the clearance was reduced in subjects with chronic 
kidney disease and not in individuals with liver cirrhosis (53). Table 1 summarizes the findings of 
hepatic elimination of glucagon and evidence that this could be due to internalization and/or enzy-
matic cleavage.

1.6 Hyperglucagonemia in diseases 
Since the first observation of hyperglucagonemia in 1978 (5), increased plasma glucagon levels 
have been further reported in various diseases, including obesity, type 2 diabetes, type 1 diabetes, 
different fatty liver diseases, and kidney diseases (13).  
Type 1 diabetes is a chronic autoimmune disease caused by immune-mediated destruction of pan-
creatic β-cells, leading to insulin deficiency. As a consequence, patients diagnosed with type 1 dia-
betes require lifelong insulin therapy to maintain plasma glucose control and prevent life-threaten-
ing complications. Despite insulin deficiency, individuals with this disease frequently exhibit ele-
vated fasting plasma glucagon levels (63). 
In contrast, type 2 diabetes is characterized by a combination of β-cell dysfunction and insulin re-
sistance, leading to an insufficient insulin response and secretion (64). In healthy subjects, hyper-
glycemia suppresses glucagon secretion, however, this regulation is impaired in individuals with 
type 2 diabetes. Instead, they have a limited α-cell response to hyperglycemia. Hence, plasma glu-
cagon levels remain inappropriately increased despite high levels of blood glucose (65), resulting in 
increased hepatic glucose production in both the fasting state and postprandial (66). The role of ele-
vated glucagon levels causing hyperglycemia was supported by Hædersdal and colleagues, who 
showed that treatment with a GCGR antagonist lowered fasting glucose levels in type 2 diabetes pa-
tients (67). Furthermore, this was additionally confirmed by Grøndahl et al. (2021), where the au-
thors investigated whether impaired elimination of glucagon contributed to the high levels of gluca-
gon. They concluded that the glucagon elimination was preserved in type 2 diabetes (29) and there-
fore did not play a part in hyperglucagonemia observed in type 2 diabetes. Therefore, fasting ele-
vated glucagon levels are suggested by most researchers to be due to an over-secretion of glucagon. 
Obesity is associated with fat accumulation in the liver, which is the primary target organ of gluca-
gon. A study observed that obese individuals with hepatic steatosis had high fasting plasma levels 
of glucagon (68). Even slightly elevated liver fat is also linked to increased glucagon levels (69). 
Notably, up to 80% of type 2 diabetes patients appear to have fatty liver disease, where hyperglu-
cagonemia is most commonly observed (24). These findings are supported by Gar et al. (2018), who 
reported that hyperglucagonemia was not present in all individuals at high risk for type 2 diabetes 
but only within a subgroup who also exhibited characteristics of metabolic syndrome (70). Another 
study showed a correlation between liver fat and glucagon levels in obese patients with type 2 dia-
betes, all having hepatic steatosis. After a 7-week low-calorie diet, the plasma glucagon was signifi-
cantly decreased along with a reduction of body mass index (BMI) and liver fat (71).  
Fasting hyperglucagonemia is linked to factors other than liver steatosis. Metabolic dysfunction-
associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) describes a spectrum of liver disorders. It is diagnosed 
==PAGE:18==
Page 11
 
when hepatic steatosis is present, along with at least one indicator of metabolic dysfunction such as 
elevated BMI, impaired glucose regulation, high blood pressure, increased TGs, or low levels of 
high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (72). Steatosis is graded based on the percentage of hepatocytes 
containing medium and large lipid droplets, with a threshold of more than 5%, indicating the pres-
ence of steatosis (73). Individuals with type 2 diabetes also diagnosed with MASLD have been 
shown to exhibit elevated fasting plasma concentration of glucagon (74). Noteworthy, studies have 
observed that patients with MASLD had elevated fasting hyperglucagonemia independently of type 
2 diabetes, suggesting that MASLD may be the main driver of hyperglucagonemia rather than type 
2 diabetes (75, 76).  
Additionally, individuals with MASLD and obesity, glucagon resistance towards amino acid catab-
olism was associated with the downregulation of key hepatic enzymes involved in amino acid me-
tabolism (68). This could suggest that a potential disruption of the α-cell axis in these conditions is 
due to either lower hepatic GCGR expression or impaired GCGR signaling (77), leading to altered 
regulation of glucagon.  
Taking all this together, these studies highlight the central role of glucagon in the liver and that im-
paired liver function is associated with hyperglucagonemia. Consequently, this leads to increased 
glucose production, contributing to hyperglycemia.  
 
2 Rationale and aim 
Over the last century, there have been numerous studies on glucagon and its role in diseases, partic-
ularly in conditions with increased plasma glucose. While hyperglucagonemia is observed in vari-
ous diseases such as diabetes and liver diseases, the exact mechanisms causing hyperglucagonemia 
remain unclear. However, it has been suggested to be due to increased secretion, decreased degrada-
tion, elimination, and/or inaccurate measurement due to cross-activities with other glucagon-like 
molecules in assay analysis (13).  
Despite the substantial number of studies, the focus of glucagon elimination has been limited. Most 
researchers have focused on glucagon secretion as the consequence of hyperglucagonemia. It is 
widely accepted that the kidneys play a crucial role in the clearance of glucagon (53). Nevertheless, 
the liver’s role in glucagon elimination has not been sufficiently explored. Evidence suggests that 
several enzymes, including DPP IV (36, 37), IDE (39, 40), and NEP (44, 45), are important in the 
degradation of glucagon. Furthermore, studies have shown that the GCGR internalizes together with 
glucagon upon stimulation (49, 51). However, findings across studies were inconsistent, as others 
have reported no elimination by the liver (30, 58) 
==PAGE:19==
Page 12
 
Thus, this thesis aims to investigate the potential role of the liver in the elimination of glucagon. 
Should it be confirmed that the liver plays a role, the underlying mechanisms are hypothesized to 
involve either enzymatic degradation, receptor-mediated internalization, or both. This will be as-
sessed by performing in situ liver perfusion in DIO and lean mouse models. If altered liver-specific 
elimination of glucagon can be identified in DIO mice, it will uncover new insight into the causa-
tion of hyperglucagonemia in disease.

3.1 Experimental animals 
All animal experiments were performed with the permission from the Danish Animal Experiments 
Inspectorate, Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark (permit 2021-15-0201-00929) in ac-
cordance with the EU directive 2010/63/EU, the National Institutes of Health (publication No. 85-

Male C57BL/6JRj mice were acquired from Janvier Labs. They were housed in groups of up to 6 
mice per cage with continuous access to standard chow (Brogaarden, catalog no. 30404) and water. 
All mice were maintained on a 12 h/12 h light-dark cycle with lights on from 07:00 – 19:00. Mice 
were acclimatized for a minimum of one week after arrival prior to the experiments. To induce obe-

3.2 In situ liver perfusion  
The liver perfusion buffer was a Krebs-Henseleit bicarbonate (KHB) buffer (118 mM NaCl [Sigma 
Aldrich, catalog no. S1679], 4.7 mM KCl [Merck, catalog no. 1.04936.0500], 1.2 mM MgSO4 
[Sigma Aldrich, catalog no. 230391], 1.25 mM CaCl2 [Sigma Aldrich, C8106], 1.2 mM KH2PO4 
[Merck, catalog no. 1.04877.1000], and 25 mM NaHCO3 [Sigma Aldrich, catalog no. S-5761]), 
which was prepared and stored at 4°C until use. The buffer was used within a month from the day 
of preparation. Glucagon (Bachem, catalog no. H-6790.0005) was dissolved in 1 mM phosphate-
buffered saline buffer with 1% bovine serum albumin (BSA) (Sigma Aldrich, catalog no. A2153) 
and prepared in aliquots with a final concentration of 20 µM. The aliquots were stored at -20°C un-
til use.

Page 13
 
For the liver perfusion, glucagon solution was prepared in a 20-fold higher concentration than the 
desired final concentration, reaching the liver. This was due to a dilution into the KHB buffer dur-
ing the perfusion, resulting in a 20-fold dilution of the glucagon solution. Furthermore, glucagon 
solution was prepared in KHB buffer containing 1% BSA as carrier protein to avoid adhesion to 
tubing and glassware.  
The liver perfusion system consisted of a reservoir containing KHB buffer, which was heated to 
37°C and continuously aerated with 95% O2 and 5% CO2, a roller pump for buffer flow, a bubble 
trap to catch air bubbles in the system, a thermocirculator to control the temperature and computer 
software to measure the pressure at the portal vein. The buffer was aerated for at least 30 minutes to 
provide sufficient oxygen to the liver and maintain a pH of 7.4 – 7.5.  
The mice were anesthetized by injection of ketamine (100 mg/kg) and xylazine (10 mg/kg). Each 
mouse was transferred to a heated operating table, placed on its back, and the lack of reflexes in re-
sponse to toe pinch was confirmed. Next, the mouse was sprayed with 70% ethanol to ease the han-
dling of hair during the operation. The abdomen was then cut with scissors to expose the abdominal 
cavity and the liver. At the portal vein, a catheter was inserted, and a perfusion tube was subse-
quently connected to it, where the buffer was infused at a flow rate of 0.8 mL/min. Next, the dia-
phragm was dissected, and another catheter was inserted into the inferior vena cava to collect the 
output solution. The infusion rate was then increased to 3.5 mL/min, resulting in a pressure of ap-
proximately 10 mm/Hg.  
The liver was perfused with KHB buffer alone (equilibration period) for 30 minutes before initiat-
ing the experiment. The prepared glucagon solution was transferred into a syringe and placed into a 
syringe pump set to deliver a continuous infusion at a rate of 0.175 mL/min. Approximately 1 mL 
sample volume was collected in 12x75mm minisorp tubes from the tube connected to the portal 
vein (called input samples) or the inferior vena cava (called output samples) and immediately 
placed on ice. The samples were stored at -20°C until further analysis. The whole experimental liver 
perfusion setup can be viewed in Figure 4. The collected samples were analyzed using sandwich 
ELISA and RIA to quantify glucagon concentrations. Mouse livers were collected immediately af-
ter liver perfusions and snap frozen on dry ice. All livers were stored at -80 °C until analysis of TG 
content and enzymatic activity.

3.4.1 Sandwich ELISA 
Sandwich ELISA kit (Mercodia, catalog no. 10-1281-01) using antibodies recognizing both COOH- 
and NH2-termini was performed to quantify the concentration of glucagon in samples collected 
from liver perfusions. Samples and standards were transferred into a 96-well plate, which was pre-
coated with a capture antibody. A detection peroxidase-conjugated antibody was then added to the 
wells and incubated overnight (18 - 22 hours) at 4°C with shaking (700 rpm), allowing antigen

binding. After incubation, the wells were washed 6 times with 700 µL wash buffer and subse-
quently tapped on against absorbent paper to remove excess buffer. The provided substrate solution 
(3,3’,5.5’-tetramethylbenzidine) was added to the wells and incubated on the bench for 30 minutes, 
allowing the enzymatic reaction between the bound conjugate antibody and substrate. The reaction 
was stopped using an included stop solution, resulting in a yellow color, where the intensity de-
pends on the glucagon concentration. The absorbance was immediately measured using a micro-
plate reader (Tecan Magellan Pro), reading at 450 nm, and concentrations were determined based 
on a standard curve. 
 
3.4.2 Radioimmunoassay 
For further glucagon concentration determination, RIA was performed in collaboration with Jens J. 
Holst at the University of Copenhagen, Copenhagen, Denmark. This assay is based on competitive 
binding kinetics, where radiolabeled glucagon and unlabeled glucagon from samples compete to 
bind to an antibody. 
Firstly, the standard curve was prepared with the perfusion buffer, KHB, and with the following 
concentration of glucagon: 0, 2.5, 5, 10, 20, 40, 80, 160, and 320 pM. Phosphate buffer (40 mM 
phosphate med 0.1% albumin) was used in preparing radiolabeled glucagon (I-125) for the assay. 
150 µL sample was transferred into minisorp tubes 12x75 mm, mixed with 50 µL antibody, and in-
cubated at 4°C. After 24h, when antibodies and antigens had formed complexes until equilibrium, 
100 µL radiolabeled glucagon (approximately 5000 counts per minute) was added to each tube and 
incubated at 4°C for another 24h. The antibody-bound fraction of glucagon was isolated from the 
unbound glucagon (labeled and unlabeled) by adding plasma-coated charcoal, which captures the 
unbound fraction under incubation at 4°C for 30 min. This was followed by centrifugation at 4°C 
for 25 minutes at 3000 rpm, where the supernatant contained the antibody-bound glucagon. Thus, 
the supernatant was decanted into counting tubes, and the remaining pellet was discarded. The sam-
ples were measured using a gamma-counter (3 minutes per sample), which detected counts per mi-
nute of radiolabeled glucagon. The glucagon concentrations were determined by the sigmoidal 
standard curve, where at very low analyte concentration, most antibodies are bound to the labeled 
glucagon, resulting in a high signal and plateau, and vice versa. Hence, the linear part of the stand-
ard curve provides the best range for quantification. Two different antibodies were used with the 
code names 4304 (79) and 4305 (80), targeting the mid-region and COOH-terminus of glucagon, 
respectively. Antibody 4304 binds to the 6-15 amino acid sequence of glucagon and antibody 4305 
binds to 19-29.

3.4.4 NEP activity in liver tissue and plasma 
NEP activity in pooled human heparin plasma was measured to test if the enzyme inhibitor sacubi-
trilat was able to inhibit NEP. Additionally, the activity was measured in liver tissue to investigate 
whether there were any differences in enzymatic activity between lean and DIO mice. This was car-
ried out by an in-house two-stage enzymatic assay, where in the first reaction, NEP in the samples 
cleaves the substrate, Suc-Ala-Ala-Phe-AMC (7-amino-4-methylcoumarin), between Ala and Phe, 
generating Phe-AMC. In the second reaction, aminopeptidase M cleaves between Phe-AMC, releas-
ing the fluorophore AMC, which can be detected using fluorescence measurements.  
Firstly, all reagents were prepared. The substrate solution (5 mM Suc-Ala-Ala-Phe-AMC [Bachem, 
catalog no. 4006306]) was made by dissolving 5.6 mg of Suc-Ala-Ala-Phe-AMC in 195µL dime-
thylforamide (Sigma Aldrich, catalog no. D4551) and then diluted in 1850 µL assay buffer (0.1 M 
Tris-HCl [Sigma Aldrich, catalog no. T1503] in MilliQ, pH = 7.6). Aminopeptidase M (Sigma Al-
drich, catalog no. L5006) was prepared by transferring 110 µL into an Eppendorf tube and centri-
fuged (7000 x g for 3 minutes). The supernatant was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended in 
550 µL assay buffer. This was stored at 4 °C until use, where the solution was diluted in 500 µL of 
10 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) (Sigma Aldrich, catalog no. E9883). 
Next, the liver tissue was prepared and homogenized based on the protocol from Miners et al. 
(2008) (81). Tissue was crushed while frozen using a mortar, and 200 mg of tissue was collected

dic group] and 10 μM phenylmethanesulfornyl fluoride [Sigma Aldrich, catalog no. 93482]) were 
added to the tubes. The tissue was then homogenized using a tissue lyser at 6000 oscillation/min for

plasma treated with saline (P-value = 0.831) (Appendix 8.5 and 8.6). The enzymatic activity of NEP 
was also measured in liver tissue post-perfusion from DIO and lean mice. The NEP activity was

5 Discussion 
Glucagon plays a critical role in regulating several metabolic processes and promoting glucose pro-
duction. Hyperglucagonemia may be caused by a potentially impaired glucagon degradation. If glu-
cagon degradation is impaired, it can lead to sustained glucagon signaling, resulting in elevated he-
patic glucose production. However, the potential hepatic elimination of glucagon is not fully under-
stood. To address this knowledge gap, it is important to determine whether glucagon is eliminated 
by the liver and identify mechanisms driving hepatic glucagon clearance. Here we demonstrate that 
glucagon is eliminated by the liver in male lean mice during liver perfusion, and its elimination is 
impaired in male DIO mice. Furthermore, the elimination of glucagon is mainly driven by receptor-
mediated internalization rather than enzymatic cleavage. 
 
5.1 Glucagon is eliminated by receptor-mediated internalization rather 
than enzymatic cleavage  
The sandwich ELISA, used in these experiments, targets both the N- and C-termini of glucagon, 
meaning it can only detect intact, full-length glucagon molecules (82). If glucagon is enzymatically 
cleaved, the sandwich ELISA will not detect the glucagon fragments. If glucagon is internalized via 
its receptor, the intact peptide is removed from circulation, also reducing the ELISA signal. In con-
trast, the RIA assays target epitopes located in the mid-region (RIA 4304) and C-terminus (RIA 
4305) of glucagon. Therefore, RIA can detect both full-length glucagon and certain degradation 
fragments, depending on the specific cleavage site. Interestingly, both RIA analyses (Figure 6B and 
C) showed similar results with a significant decrease in the output group compared to the input 
group. The agreement between the two independent analyses strengthens the reliability of the ob-
served trend. The percentage decrease of glucagon after liver passage is observed to be highest in 
the sandwich ELISA than in the two RIA analyses. This could be due to the fact that the ELISA is 
more sensitive to intact glucagon, whereas RIA could detect both intact and glucagon fragments, 
leading to a smaller decline. Since the reduction in glucagon levels after passing the liver is seen in 
all three assays, it indicates that glucagon is mainly taken up by hepatocytes or other target cells and 
receptor-mediated internalized rather than being degraded extracellularly by enzymes. These find-
ings support the hypothesis that receptor engagement plays a key role in glucagon clearance and 
may provide insight into the underlying mechanisms of hepatic glucagon processing.  
When comparing absolute values of glucagon across assays, it was observed that a much higher 
concentration of glucagon was measured with RIA compared to sandwich ELISA in the same sam-
ples (Figure 6). Thus, the glucagon standard used in RIA was measured with the sandwich ELISA 
to determine the glucagon concentration. The same standard was used in both RIAs. The sandwich 
==PAGE:37==
Page 30
 
ELISA revealed an approximately 50% lower concentration of the standard than expected, which 
could indicate that the RIA analysis in general determines glucagon concentrations to be higher. 
This could explain the high concentration of glucagon measured with RIA. Moreover, the RIA cali-
bration standard is not traceable to an international reference, as compared to the standard used in 
the ELISA, which may also contribute to the variability. 
A limitation of this study was that the samples were collected in a single tube. As a result, they were 
initially thawed for analysis with sandwich ELISA, then refrozen and thawed again later for both of 
the RIA analyses. Such non-optimal storage conditions can compromise the stability of glucagon 
and must be handled and stored with caution. As demonstrated by Albrechtsen et al. (2015), gluca-
gon concentrations were significantly reduced when stored at -20°C for one month by almost 50%, 
highlighting the importance of preventing long-term storage freezing. During short-term storage at 
room temperature, glucagon was significantly reduced after 3 hours. In contrast, glucagon was sta-
ble on ice for 24 hours (83). Based on this, the samples were stored on ice while conducting liver 
perfusion to limit the degradation of glucagon. Furthermore, samples were analyzed within a month 
to prevent further degradation. Importantly, all input and output samples were handled identically, 
allowing direct comparisons. For future studies, it would have been preferable to collect samples in 
separate tubes for each assay.  
 
5.2 Impaired hepatic glucagon elimination 
The findings in this thesis indicate that glucagon is eliminated by the liver of lean mice, and not in 
DIO mice (Figure 6 and Figure 11B). This suggests that there is an obesity-associated impairment 
in glucagon clearance, which may contribute to hyperglucagonemia, commonly seen in individuals 
with metabolic diseases or obesity. If hepatic glucagon clearance is reduced in obesity, as suggested 
in this thesis, it may represent an underestimated factor in glucagon dysregulation.  
Elevated cholesterol levels are associated with obesity (84). Notably, evidence from McGlone et al. 
(2022) indicated that hepatocyte cholesterol content influences glucagon sensitivity and signaling. 
They identified several potential cholesterol-binding sites through simulations, indicating that it can 
interfere with the receptor function (85). Therefore, it may be possible that elevated hepatic choles-
terol levels in DIO mice contribute to the observed impaired glucagon degradation, as glucagon 
cannot bind the GCGR and be internalized. However, this must be investigated further, as the cho-
lesterol content was not investigated in this project. 
Lower glucagon concentrations were measured with sandwich ELISA in the input samples from the 
first mouse on each experimental day, during which three to four liver perfusions were performed 
per day. This was observed in both male lean mice and DIO mice (Figure 6A and Figure 11B). 
==PAGE:38==
Page 31
 
One possible explanation is that glucagon may have bound to the inner surface of a new syringe, 
resulting in a reduced amount of glucagon in the infused solution. Since the same syringe was re-
used, its inner surface was coated with glucagon, preventing further loss of glucagon during subse-
quent liver perfusions that same day. To minimize peptide loss due to surface binding, the syringe 
was pre-coated with glucagon from the prepared solution before the liver perfusion in age-matched 
lean mice (Figure 11A) and mice infused with glucagon along with enzyme inhibitors (Figure 9). 
This showed a markedly lower variation in glucagon concentration. 
A general key limitation of the studies is that all experiments were conducted exclusively in male 
mice, which introduces a sex bias. As biological processes can vary between males and females, the 
findings may not fully represent the physiological response in female mice, limiting the generaliza-
bility of the results. Hence, it would be valuable to include female animals in future studies. 
 
5.3 Comparative insights from insulin clearance 
The impairment of hepatic glucagon elimination draws a direct parallel to the reduced hepatic insu-
lin clearance seen in subjects with hepatic steatosis (86, 87) and type 2 diabetes (87). Insulin is pri-
marily eliminated in the liver (88), and up to 77% is removed in the first-pass metabolism in dogs 
(89). However, high-fat feeding reduced the first-pass metabolism clearance of insulin to 56% after 
six weeks, and after 12 weeks, it was further reduced to 43% in the canine model (90). This reduc-
tion of insulin clearance contributes to hyperinsulinemia, leading to a systemic metabolic dysregula-
tion. This is similar to the glucagon impairment seen from our DIO mice study (Figure 11B) which 
may contribute to the hyperglucagonemia seen in subjects with liver steatosis (68) and diabetes (63, 
65).  
 
5.4 Enzymatic activity in liver tissue 
The NEP and DPP IV inhibitors used in liver perfusion (Figure 9) were examined in pooled human 
plasma to confirm their ability to inhibit the enzymes. The RFU of the background in plasma con-
taining enzyme inhibitors was higher than the actual sample. As a result, enzymatic activity could 
not be calculated, so only the raw RFU values are reported (Figure 7A and Figure 8A). Statistical 
calculations on the RFU values between plasma containing inhibitors and the control showed that 
the NEP worked efficiently. Due to the low sample size in the assessment of DPP IV inhibition, the 
statistical calculations were not conducted. However, the RFU values were lower in plasma contain-
==PAGE:39==
Page 32
 
ing inhibitors, indicating inhibition of DPP IV. Furthermore, since the values of the plasma with in-
hibitors were lower than background samples, it could indicate that the enzymatic activity was close 
to zero, which also points towards an effective inhibition of NEP and DPP IV.  
The enzymatic activity of NEP in liver tissue was confirmed in both DIO and lean mice (Figure 
7B). However, two samples were excluded due to a pipetting mistake, resulting in a sample size of 
8 instead of 10. The results of NEP activity showed no significant difference between the DIO mice 
and the lean mice, indicating that the elimination of glucagon across the liver is not driven by NEP 
cleavage. On the contrary, the DPP IV activity was significantly lower in liver tissue from DIO 
mice compared to lean mice (Figure 8B). Notably, the data from DPP IV activity in tissue are ex-
trapolated. The inclusion was considered reliable and included due to a good linear fit of the stand-
ard curve (Appendix 8.8). However, normalization of the results to protein content rather than tissue 
weight would have been more appropriate, given that the DIO mice exhibited more fat accumula-
tion in the liver, potentially confounding comparisons based on tissue mass alone.  
The enzymatic activities of both DPP IV and NEP in liver tissue from DIO and lean mice showed 
high variability, leading to a high standard deviation. This could be due to biological reasons. For 
instance, liver tissues contain subregions, known as liver zonation, which differ in metabolic func-
tions and enzymatic activity (91). Technical variation could also play a role, such as uneven tissue 
homogenization, since uneven tissue disruption could cause variable enzyme release.  
Findings from Baumeier et al. (2016) showed elevated levels of hepatic gene expression of DPP IV 
in mice with high weight gain and fatty liver, and a correlation was seen between the gene expres-
sion and DPP IV release (38). Furthermore, it was reported that NEP activity was increased in indi-
viduals with obesity, type 2 diabetes, and MASLD (92, 93), which is conflicting evidence to the ob-
servations during this thesis. Given the continuous flow of the perfusion system, it was uncertain 
whether the enzyme had sufficient time to interact with glucagon. Therefore, in future research, fur-
ther investigation on the enzymes is needed. For instance, to determine whether membrane-bound 
DPP IV can cleave glucagon, glucagon could be added to homogenized tissue, and the mixture ana-
lyzed to see if cleavage occurs.  
The peptide fragments identified by LC-MS/MS indicate enzymatic activity consistent with the 
cleavage by DPP IV, resulting in glucagon 3-29 and IDE, producing glucagon 1-16, glucagon 1-17, 
and glucagon 18-29. Glucagon was not detected in the input sample. This could be due to plastic 
absorption, as the internal standard also was lost. Hence, only the output sample results were calcu-
lated and showed that 3% was glucagon fragments compared to the intact peptide. This indicates 
that only a small fraction of glucagon is processed by enzymatic cleavage. Furthermore, this could 
also explain the lower significance between input and output samples in RIA 4304 (Figure 6C). As 
this antibody targets the 6-15 sequence of the glucagon, it can recognize all fragments. In contrast, 
==PAGE:40==
Page 33
 
the 4305 antibody detects the 19-29 sequence of glucagon. Since some of the glucagon was cleaved 
into the fragment glucagon 18-29, it could not bind to this fragment, resulting in a lower glucagon 
concentration.   
Taken together, these data indicate that enzymatic cleavage does not play a dominant role in the he-
patic elimination of glucagon. Hence, the hypothesis that glucagon is degraded by enzymatic cleav-
age in the liver is rejected. Instead, the data may point toward receptor-mediated internalization, 
which should be investigated further to confirm this hypothesis. To validate this in future studies, 
we could conduct experiments with GCGR knock-out mice or perform liver perfusion in lean mice 
and infuse a GCGR antagonist with the same experimental setup as with enzyme inhibitors. If no 
significant difference between input and output is seen, it will support the internalization hypothe-
sis.   
Lastly, the altered glucagon elimination observed in male DIO mice may be due to altered receptor-
mediated internalization, which should be investigated further. Moreover, IDE should be studied to 
determine the role of IDE in glucagon elimination, as this was not assessed in this project.  
 
5.5 MASLD in a DIO mouse model 
The DIO mice were fed a high-fat diet to induce hepatic steatosis. Hepatic lipid accumulation is a 
hallmark of MASLD and can induce cell damage and inflammation. A scoring system for rodent 
models to establish the extent of fat accumulation in the hepatic cells has been proposed using histo-
pathology (94). However, the mice used in this thesis were not examined for MASLD through his-
tology analysis, as it was out of scope. This is a limitation to the study, since having a better under-
standing of the mice’s health status would allow a better comparison of DIO and lean mice, and a 
better understanding of hepatic diseases. According to earlier research data from the research group 
had shown that the same mouse models did not exhibit any fibrosis and inflammation after 20 
weeks of feeding the same high-fat diet (data not published). However, in this project, it was ob-
served that the TG content was significantly higher in the DIO mice compared to lean mice, reflect-
ing hepatic steatosis. Additionally, the visual differences between lean and DIO mice support the 
presence of hepatic steatosis in DIO mice. This is still relevant, as even slightly elevated fat levels 
in the liver were associated with hyperglucagonemia (69).  
In future studies, further investigation in MASLD mouse models could be conducted to examine 
glucagon elimination in these models. A way to induce fibrosis could be by including a hepatotoxin 
(95), which could make the model more representative of MASLD, and then validating MASLD 
histologically (73).

5.6 Liver perfusion advantages and limitations 
As previously mentioned, one of the major challenges in studying glucagon is the cross-reactivity 
with other glucagon-like peptides (13). While glucagon is synthesized and secreted in the pancreas, 
the glucagon-like peptides are secreted in the gut (8). Since this project is investigating the liver in 
situ, isolated from other organs, the interference from other glucagon-like peptides is not expected 
to pose a significant issue. Hence, this experimental design offers a clear advantage for studying 
glucagon within the liver. However, this isolation also results in no interactions between the liver 
and the surrounding organs. This is a limitation of the model, as the absence of inter-organ commu-
nication can influence liver function. Moreover, due to the lack of blood flow, the liver did not get 
access to all the components present in the blood, such as hormones, plasma proteins, or immune 
cells that influence liver function in vivo. This also imposes limitations on the model.  
Moreover, all liver perfusions were performed on mice that were anesthetized with ketamine and 
xylazine, agents that have been suggested to interfere with glucagon secretion (82). Although this 
thesis focuses on the elimination of exogenously administered glucagon, these anesthesia-induced 
changes could potentially affect hepatic physiology and the clearance mechanisms. Thus, future 
studies should consider another anesthetic protocol or specifically investigate the impact of keta-
mine and xylazine on liver function.  
Considering that the liver is isolated from circulation during liver perfusion, receiving only oxygen-
ated buffer, it is important to confirm that the cells remain viable. In earlier studies of the liver dur-
ing liver perfusion, it was shown that there was a decrease in the partial pressure of oxygen and an 
increase in the partial pressure of carbon dioxide in buffer output, indicating that the liver respired. 
Furthermore, it was reported that the hepatocytes were responsive to amino acid, glucose, and glu-
cagon stimulation, indicating that the cells were alive. Additionally, the liver was to be respiring 
and responsive for at least 3 hours (96).  
Despite the presented limitations, they can also be seen as advantages. Especially when considering 
the simplicity of the model and the ability to isolate the liver function. The fact that the model al-
lows researchers to focus especially on hepatic processes without interference from other organs or 
other external factors can provide a clearer understanding of the role the liver may play.  
The KHB buffer used in the perfusions is a simple buffer that mimics physiological conditions, only 
containing essential salts. The composition without proteins reduces background interference in 
other analyses. Hence, this buffer is ideal for downstream experiments that require high sensitivity 
and precise quantification, such as LC-MS/MS. 
==PAGE:42==
Page 35
 
Furthermore, the limited duration of the perfusion simplifies the study of acute liver functions. This 
allows a highly controlled experimental setup and is easier to manage than long-term in vivo stud-
ies. In addition to this, it also makes it easier to identify liver-specific responses and mechanisms. 
Moreover, this method allows for the direct identification of cleavage products originating from the 
liver, rather than from cleavage occurring in the circulation.  
 
6 Conclusion 
In conclusion, we have shown that glucagon is eliminated by the liver in healthy male mice. This 
was investigated by conducting in situ liver perfusion. Using three different assays with different 
epitope targets to determine the glucagon concentration, hepatic glucagon elimination was consist-
ently observed, ranging from 9 – 20%. Furthermore, LC-MS/MS revealed that only 3% of the total 
effluent solution from liver perfusion contained glucagon fragments. During liver perfusion with 
infusion of NEP and DPP IV inhibitors, hepatic glucagon elimination across the mouse livers was 
still observed. These results imply that glucagon is cleared by receptor-mediated internalization ra-
ther than enzymatic cleavage by NEP and DPP IV.  
Moverover, observations in this thesis point towards a previously underappreciated impairment in 
hepatic glucagon elimination in obesity, shown in male DIO mice. These findings link to the 
dysregulation of glucagon in several metabolic diseases, such as obesity, diabetes, and MASLD, 
where hyperglucagonemia is often reported. These findings suggest that obesity influences the glu-
cagon elimination in the liver, contributing to hyperglucagonemia. However, the exact mechanisms 
of hepatic glucagon elimination remain unclear, and further assessment of the mechanisms is 
needed. Ultimately, identifying the predominant pathway could enhance our understanding of glu-
cagon’s role in metabolic regulation, helping to develop new therapeutics in disorders where gluca-
gon plays an important role.
