ABSTRACT 
 The present MSc project shows the proposed R&D methodology for investigating the granular 
flow in rotary kiln reactors.  
The current use of granular materials has led the kiln reactor configuration becoming critical 
when it comes to granular material processing. However, the large number of interesting 
phenomenon while material is passing through the kiln has motivated the scope of this project.  
For that purpose, LIGGGHTS Open-Source Software implements a modelling framework based 
on Discrete Element Method (DEM) to accurately predict the granular behaviour in rotary kiln. 
The first goal, mapping the flow regimes, Froude and granular Bond number would be proposed 
since they are able to characterize the granular behaviour in rotary kilns for a wide range of powder 
materials. 
However, due to the wide variety in terms of particle size and particle density of granular 
material, a very interesting phenomenon-the particle segregation phenomena- would be studied as 
a second major goal. Therefore, a deep study of how particle size and particle density driving 
forces affect the homogeneity of granular mixtures would be carried out. Subsequently, in order 
to mitigate the particle segregation, new potential lifter configuration for powder industry would 
be implemented in the simulation to study the how the segregation pattern is modified, and a more 
intense mixing degree is trigger. 
As to finalize the master’s thesis, an overall discussion of how the proposed methodology, 
process engineering simplifications have influenced the results achieved would be carried out.

1. INTRODUCTION 
The present MSc Thesis project has been an industry collaboration between one of the leading 
companies in the production of high quality batteries for the automotive sector, Northvolt AB with 
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU). Before entering in the technical report, it is necessary first, 
showing the thesis motivation and defining where this project is allocated in Northolt AB to 
understand its contribution to the engineering field and second, give an overall idea of the project 
outline. 
1.1 Motivation 
After the Paris Agreement in 2015, all parties involved accorded setting up some goals to 
address the challenge of climate change and its negative impact on the society. Among those goals, 
keeping the global temperature rise this century below 2 ºC above pre-industrial levels and  
achieving net-zero CO2 emission by 2050 are highlighted as a major goals. In this last 
environmental goal is where Northvolt AB arises in 2016, more concrete its contribution is in the 
process known as vehicle electrification or e-mobility. This process encompasses the increasing 
needs by the automotive sector to carry out the shift from traditional fossil fuel engine-car to the 
environmental friendly electric car. 
 Thus, the shift towards the e-mobility begins with the production of high-quality batteries. 
Since the automotive battery production industry is relatively modern, it is therefore in the early 
stage of development and hence not all the process involved are perfectly understood and 
optimized for battery production. 
It is in this early engineering stage where this project is allocated, more concrete in the need to 
understand how to handle the powder material where the cathode active material is produced (the 
kiln reactor). In consequence, a good understanding of the flow regimes, the phenomenon 
associated and how process variables affect the process unit responsible (kiln reactor) for the 
crucial step in battery production can be further used to optimize the production process in terms 
of energy and raw material consumption and its environmental consequences attached as metal 
depletion or CO2 footprint.

1.2 Project outline 
The MSc project presented is part of one of the Northvolt R&D process engineering projects. 
Therefore, since there is no preliminary study performed by Northvolt AB in this field, a general 
study from scratch about the granular behaviour in rotary kiln was conducted. A simplified pilot-
scale modelling framework defined in LIGGGHTs Open-Source Software would be used for 
modelling granular flow. LIGGGHTs was selected since can implement the Discrete Element 
Method (DEM) approach to accurately simulate a cross-sectional snapshot of the granular flow 
which also make it possible to accurately predict the granular interactions in rotary kilns. 
 The first section of this project covers the state of art in kiln technology where a general 
overview of kiln reactor has been provided as well as its classification which would introduces the 
kiln background to the lector. Afterwards, the theoretical heat and mass transfer mechanisms as 
well as mixing and segregation governing mechanisms in kiln operation would be shortly 
explained in order to understand  why is important to properly characterize the flow regimes and 
how crucial is to comprehend the segregation phenomena in powder handling.  
 In the following section, the modelling framework, boundary limits and material properties as 
well as the flow sheeting process are presented in detail. Then, based on previous statements, the 
R&D methodology for investigating the granular flow in rotary kiln reactors is presented in a 
progressive way. 
 First, it would start with a preliminary study of the main kiln process parameters (particle size, 
rotational speed and filled kiln volume). Afterwards, mapping the flow regimes (also known as the 
bed behaviour diagram) would be carried out for first, being able to characterize the granular 
behaviour in rotary kiln and second, compare the simulated data with the literature data proposed 
by Boateng [1]for model validation. Once the model is defined and validated, some local 
sensitivity analyses of the particle parameters would be use to understand how the flow regime      
(bed behaviour diagram) is affected and later be extrapolated to cover other particle natures ranges. 
Since Northvolt AB uses kilns for manufacturing the cathode active materials for batteries, a 
high mixing degree is required to produce a homogenize and high-quality product. Subsequently, 
due to kilns are used to work with a particle mixture diverging in size and density, the segregation 
phenomena would be studied. Then, because of segregation is a spontaneous undesirable 
phenomenon in rotary kilns, a well-known process engineering solution, internal lifters 
configuration would be proposed for first analysing its influence over the flow regime region (bed 
behaviour diagram) and secondly for avoiding segregation phenomena.

 At the end of the project, the conclusion chapter would summarize the main conclusions

2. LITERATURE REVIEW 
This chapter is dedicated to cover the state of art in kiln technology from the theoretical point 
of view. We would cover from the governing heat and mass transfer mechanisms to the 
implications in the current material processing. To describe the theoretical background and kiln 
technology applied in the thesis, a description of the evolution of its applications and the 
classification that has evolved through time would be carried out. 
 
2.1 Kiln Background 
 First, rotary kiln reactors are common rotary process equipment widely used in several 
industries due to their high range of applicability. The widespread usage of rotary kilns reactor 
configuration can be associated with the ability to manage several solids or slurry feedstocks for 
example pharmaceutical compounds, metal-oxide ores, limestones, or hazardous waste. Besides 
those feedstocks of different nature, the versatility of this rotary process unit is related to the 
capability to handle a vast range of particle sizes and a broad range of operating conditions 
depending on its purpose. For example, powder material ranging from microns up to millimetres 
or even centimeters, and regarding the process operating condition, kilns can work as a drier, 
process mixing unit or as a reactor, depending on the goal of the kiln, the process operating 
condition and the kiln dimension may change. 
 Those mentioned characteristics have led to the kiln reactor to become one of the most 
polyvalent process units in pharmaceutical, food, cement or as it is going to be studied in this 
Master Thesis, the key role in the metallurgical processing industry, more concrete in the 
metallurgical powder ores or raw material processing. [2], [3], [4], [1]. 
Historically, from early stages, the kiln technology has been mainly associated with the cement 
industry, the cement process or clinkering process which was conducted in a batch reactor. Then, 
due to the lack of up-scaling the process, in XIX century Portland cement company patented a kiln 
reactor able to operate continuously and easy to up-scaling, that was the moment where the concept 
==PAGE:17==
 
5 
 
of rotary kiln reactor was first used.[5]. There, in the clinkering process, the initial role of the kiln 
was and is currently the reaction step where the lime, chalk, clay, and chemicals react at high 
temperature where the calcination and the sintering of the raw material occurs and hence the 
cement is produced. The high temperatures demanding on clinkering process has led to being the 
critical step in the cement industry since it represents the main hotspot of the process in terms of 
energy demand. Besides, in earlies stages of the kiln technology, the ability of handling solid 
material under vast range of operating conditions showed to the engineering field the great 
potential of implementing kiln technology in other industries [1], [3]. 
 As a result, nowadays, this process unit is being used for other additional purposes including 
mixing or drying due to kilns can achieve the specifications required with relatively low 
economical investment and a high level of replicability in powder products [1],[2],[3],[4] . 
 Even though rotary kilns are commonly used, they also have some disadvantages during the 
normal operation, specifically when solids powders are involved in. Dust generation and low 
thermal efficiency are highlighted as a major operational problem. Then, literature reported that 
the implementation of internals such as lifters or hanging metal chains demonstrates an increase 
in energy efficiency, resulting in economical savings.  
Dust generation in vibratory or rotatory process vessels, represents a source of risk in daily 
operation. It can dangerously affect human health due to dust generation and more concrete the 
small particle matter that may contain hazardous powders can be breathed in by humans. Besides, 
dust generation was reported that may block intake airflow of rotary support process units as 
pumps. As a result, most industrial plants that handle powders material  proposed to reduce the 
risk of dust generation by the implementation of cyclons and filters to reduce the amount of 
dangerous particle matter (PM) [6]. 
Before entering in the classification, rotary kiln reactors are tumbling in a horizontal direction, 
this kiln disposition in question has evolved in terms of operating conditions and internal geometry 
depending on the industrial application. Even though, the kiln structure in all applications is the 
same, long horizontal cylindrical dumbbell shape vessels with a certain inclination to enhance the 
movement of particles alongside the kiln rotary axis where the solid is retained in the lower part 
of the cylinder while the gas is passing through the cylinder contacting the solid material during 
the rotary movement [1],[3]. This Gas-Solid contacting nature has led them to operate in parallel 
or concurrent flow and, the addition of internals such as lifters or tumblers are regularly used in 
==PAGE:18==

2.2 Rotary Kiln Operation 
During the previous section, the history, applications and, classification of the kiln reactor in 
the current industry were carried out. Finally, we concluded that rotary kiln reactors are one of the 
most widespread operational units in the industry due to their vast range of applications. [8]  
However, it is important to mention the reactor’s importance in the chemical industry. Since 
reactors are considered one of the most crucial steps during the process design as well as in the 
daily-plant-running operation from the energy consumption, economical and, risk assessment 
point of view[6]. As a result, the kiln reactor unit requires a complete study focused on the heat 
and mass transport phenomena to understand the reaction kinetics, thermodynamics, and safety 
for better optimization of the process. The study of heat and mass transfer mechanism was gathered 
in the term Bed-Phenomenon as Boateng first used [3]. This term includes simplified equations of 
heat transfer and motion within the Gas-Solid phase. Additionally, Boateng highlighted that during 
the normal operation of kilns, the bed-phenomenon would be responsible for the overall 
performance of the operation. For that purpose, bed-phenomenon science would study mainly how 
the solid-phase behaves during the rotary kiln operation. 
Then, Persin and Minaev reported that the considerations on heat and mass transfer phenomenon 
are mostly dependant on the motion experienced by the solid granular material in rotary kilns[9]. 
The motion of solid matter inside rotary kilns is divided into two main movement contributions, 
the axial and the transverse(radial) motion. First, Barr et al.  studied the transverse motion whereas 
the movement occurs in the perpendicular direction of the rotating kiln is used to determine the 
flow regimes prevailed [10] [11]. Regarding the axial direction, Perron and Bui  found that the 
axial rheological analysis of the rotating kiln is used to determine the residence time of the granular 
material in the reactor [12]. 
Consequently, due to the importance of the granular motion for heat and mass transfer 
phenomenon, the scope of the present Master Thesis will focus on modelling the granular flow in 
the transverse direction of a rotating kiln.

2.4 Mass Transfer Mechanisms 
Before entering into detail, it is required to address the mass transfer mechanisms, or as it is 
stated in rotary kiln technology, the granular bed motion mechanisms [1], [3]. The bed motion is 
used to understand the fundamentals of granular motion inside rotary kilns for later predict the 
granular flow regimes and segregation phenomenon. 
The literature and more specifically Boateng reported the dynamic behaviour of granular 
material consists of both an axial and a transverse component. The axial motion component is 
defined as the motion of the particle parallel to the rotary axis, thus it will be used to determine 
the residence time inside the reactor. On the other hand, in the transverse component, the granular 
motion occurs in the perpendicular plane to the rotating axis[13].This transverse component has 
huge importance in determining transport processes such as flow regime, segregation or heat 
transfer characterization. As a result, those mentioned implications would directly affect the 
reaction and hence, the overall performance of the process.  
Even though both contributions of the dynamic behaviour of the granular material are important, 
the aiming of this thesis is to simulate the transverse motion and its implications in the granular 
bed phenomenon. However, it is necessary to discuss theoretically the axial motion since some 
designing process engineering decisions are influenced by the flow in the axial component. 
 
2.4.1 
Axial Bed Motion 
The axial motion is theoretically defined as the flow of particles parallel to the rotary axis, 
graphically this motion is represented in the z-direction of the Figure 7[1]. The forward advance 
in the axial direction is responsible for some operational and economical process engineering

Based on the process engineering implication of the axial motion, it is required to determine the

Besides, generating and enhancing the forward advance of the bed in the axial direction, the

tried to address how the granular material behaves in the transverse direction. This dynamic 
behaviour of the granular bed inside the rotary kiln is commonly known as flow regimes.

Literature defined the stagnant layer as the part of the granular bed which moves in the same 
direction and at the same rotational velocity as the wall. On the other hand, the active layer is 
defined as a uniform thick layer at the surface of the granular bed characterized by its constant 
motion in the opposite direction to the rotary cylinder as it can be seen in Figure 9 . Besides, 
regarding the velocity absolute value the active layer’s is greater than the stagnant layer’s. These 
transverse granular layers would be used to characterize flow regimes, more concrete rolling and 
cascading motion as it will be later discussed. 
 
 Then, once the active and stagnant layer are defined, the following flow regimes can be 
accurately described as follows: 
 
Slipping motion: It is a transverse bed motion that occurs when the filling degree is smaller than 
10% and, the Froude number is smaller than 10-4. In this flow regime region, the granular bed 
remaining at rest and, the stagnant layer has no motion when the system achieves the steady state. 
Besides, the bed forms a small angle of deflection as can be seen in Figure 8. So, the bed slips 
against the wall and hence it behaves as its name says, slipping motion. J. Mellmanm noted that 
this flow regime region does not have an industrial applications since their transfer coefficients are 
not affected [16]. 
 
Slumping motion: It is a transverse bed motion that occurs when the filling degree is smaller 
than 30% and, the Froude number is ranging between 10-5 <Fr<10-3. At these conditions, the bed 
behaviour becomes unstable, more concrete at the shear wedge layer. As a result, the bed starts 
having a swinging motion or oscillation motion at the shear wedge when the cylinder rotates. 
==PAGE:29==
 
17 
 
Additionally, this flow regime is englobed in the Rolling-Cascading group which is mainly used 
in some applications such as a rotary kiln, mixers, or rotary dryers.[16] 
Rolling motion: It is one of the most common bed behaviour patterns that takes place when the 
Froude number range is ranging between 10-4<Fr<10-2 and the filling degree is around or higher 
than 5% and lower than 18% of the total cross-section area. The rolling motion is characterized 
because the top layer of the bed known as the active layer is in constant discharge onto the bed 
surface and hence, the active layer of the bed is continuously rolling down. This represents a 
constant motion of the active layer and particles are constantly renewing. As the slumping motion, 
this flow regime is englobed in the rolling-cascading motion, the application already mentioned in 
slumping is also applicable to this regime[1], [16]. 
Cascading motion: As the rolling motion, is one of the most common industrial scale flow 
regimes that it can be achieved when the filling degree is higher than 10% of the total cross-section 
area and for Froude number ranges between 10-3<Fr<10-1. This motion's main characteristic is the 
wave or cascade shape that the active layer of the granular bed achieved. This wave shape is 
obtained because particles in the larger part of the bed are transported upwards due to the rotation 
speed of the wall.  The upwards region is considered a plug-flow region due to its transport 
mechanics characteristics. On the other hand, this flow regime is included in the rolling cascading 
motion and the applications already mentioned in rolling and slumping motion are also 
applicable.[1], [16] 
Catacracting motion: It is one of the transverse bed motions mainly used in ball mills due to the 
high rotational speed that the cylinder achieves let the top-layer be transported upwards to be flung 
into the gas space.  This throw-off into the free space lets the particle gain kinetic energy that 
would be used in milling applications. This flow regime can be observed when the filling degree 
is higher than 20% of the total cross-sectional area and the range’s Froude number is between       
10-1<Fr<1 [1], [16]. 
Centrifuging motion: It is one of the most undesirable transverse bed motion because it has no 
industrial use. It is high energy demanding flow regime because the rotational speed needs to be 
sufficiently high to put particles in contact with the wall as a uniform film. This regime is observed 
at any filling degree when the centrifugal force experienced by a particle is at least equal or greater 
than the gravitational force. In terms of the Froude dimensionless number, the values where the 
centrifugal to gravitational ratio are greater or equal to 1.[16]

2.5 Mixing and Segregation Mechanisms 
Granular mixing is one of the most common processing routine steps in a broad range of 
industries where the granular material is involved, for example, metallurgical-powder, cement, or 
pharmaceutical industry. The importance of having a mixing phenomenon is related to obtaining 
a homogeneous output for normal and stable downstream operating conditions. Even with the wide 
practical applications of granular mixing, there is still a lack of information due to the mixing and 
segregation phenomena mechanism in granular bed are notably complex. Thus, this section would 
cover the main mechanisms of both phenomena, mixing and segregation since they are industrially 
scaled used to predict optimal operation conditions in rotary vessels such as kilns or mixers 
[17],[18]. 
  
2.5.1 
Segregation Phenomena 
This segregation phenomenon is defined in granular science as the process where a bi-disperse 
granular bed that is formed by particles with a difference in size, density, shape would tend to 
separate into two regions, reducing the homogeneity of the granular mixture[19]. This granular 
phenomenon is an undesirable effect because it would modify the heat and mass transfer 
mechanisms since the granular bed would be formed by two regions instead of one homogeneous 
region. Those deviations mentioned before due to the bed separation in particles from the same 
nature can lead to have untreated or unreacted raw material since not all particles are in contact 
with the other nature particle. 
Then, in order to understand and how to avoid this phenomenon, Van Puyvelde  explains in his 
doctoral thesis the main mechanism that affects the segregation percolation, inertia and, buoyance 
mechanisms.[13] 
 a) Percolation or dynamic sieving is a mechanism that separates particles by size and it is 
defined as the ability of small particles to sneak or pass through the voids between larger particles 
that the granular bed contains[13]. As a result, the small particle would tend to stay in the cavities 
where the larger particles would form the cavities as it is shown in  Figure 11        
==PAGE:32==
 
20 
 
 
Figure 11 Percolation mechanism[13] 
 
On the left side of Figure 11 represents the final stage of the percolation mechanism where the 
small particle filled the voids formed by the larger. On the other hand, right side of Figure 11 
shows the process where the small particle in this case the darker one sneaks through the voids of 
the bed until it reaches the final stage of the percolation occupying the cavities.          
b) Marleen Arntz defined the inertial mechanisms as the resistance that any moving or stationary 
particle experiences against the change in the state of motion. The inertial segregation mechanism 
is enhanced at high rotational speeds or in other words when the centrifugal forces start being the 
predominant force [20]. As a result, based on the centrifugal nature of the mechanism, when the 
bi-disperse granular bed contains particles with a difference in densities, the inertial mechanisms 
would enlarge the contribution to the segregation of the system. 
 
c) Buoyancy mechanism or convective mechanism as Marleen Arntz noted, it only prevails 
when the bi-disperse bed contains particles with a difference in dentistry. This mechanism explains 
why objects float when they are immersed in a fluid, but in the case of granular material, the 
buoyance principle is applied to particles in a mobile bed where the less-dense particle tends to 
rise while the denser tends to go deep into the granular bed[20].

4.1 Discrete Element Method (DEM) 
During the lasts decades, the granular material has become popular due to its wide range of 
applications in material processing by many industries as pharmaceutical, mineral processing or 
food handling. Then the demand by the engineering field for a new mathematical model to address 
the industrial problems where the granular material is involved has led to develop an algorithm to 
describe and predict the mechanical behaviour and hence, optimize the industrial applications.  
 As a result, the recent advance in computing power gave the possibility to Cundal and Strack 
in the late 70s to develop a computing method for modelling granular material, the Discrete 
Element Method also denoted as DEM [29]. 
DEM method is a numerical modelling technique based on applying Newton’s second law of 
motion to a discrete body in the system at a concrete time-step. Therefore, by setting up an 
individual force balance plus an inertial balance acting on each discrete body, finite force 
displacements, body rotation and multi-contact interaction among discrete neighbourhood bodies 
can be used to determine the position and, velocity by applying the following governing equations 
[29].

4.2.1 
Hertz-MidlinModel 
It is a combination of two mechanic basic models, the Hertzian or Hertz- model and Midlin 
Model. Initially, the Hertzian model was developed by Hertz in 1882 for solving contact 
interaction between two elastic surfaces, but nowadays it can be further used for modelling 
physical contact between two or more particles. 
Hertzian model is a non-linear-spring contact model focused on non-adhesive contact bodies. It 
considers the elastic forces between bodies to determine the contact stress. Therefore, it represents 
the contact force between two curved elastic bodies which eventually generate a normal pressure 
force over each body that would derive into contact stress between both surfaces generating elastic 
interactions[33], [34]. On the other hand, it neglects the adhesion and friction forces between 
particles even tough, it strongly depends on the contacting surface geometry of the bodies 
involved. As a result, the assumptions of the Hertzian model are[33]: 
• 
The surfaces are smooth and frictionless. 
• 
The contact area is small compared to the size of the bodies. 
• 
The bodies are elastic and isotropic. 
 
More in detail, the physics behind those assumptions are [33]: 
• 
The surface in contact is continuous and non-conforming which results in a homogeneous 
material with constant material properties. 
• 
The strain, as well as the pressure load, are static and hence, the energy dissipation due to 
the particle vibration can be neglected while heat conduction appears as the main factor of energy 
dissipation. 
• 
Each solid body has its own and invariant dimensions, and it is assumed to be an in-elastic 
body. As a result, it is possible to disregard the change in shape or the deformation of the bodies 
in contact. 
==PAGE:46==
 
34 
 
• 
The dimensions of the bodies are much larger than the dimensions of the contact surface. 
Considering those assumptions, Hertzian Model can be used to describe the normal contact 
forces on soft elastic bodies. However, due to the complexity of implementing the non-linear 
model into practical application and the lack of tangential contribution. Mindlin and Deresiewicz 
added his contributions to the initial Hertz model and nowadays it is already known as the Hertz-
Mindlin model.  
As a result, Hertz-Mindlin model is a non-slip contact model mainly used for modelling contact 
forces between non-cohesive particles and friction particles. This model is based on the previously 
described Hertz model combined with the improvements done by Mindlin. Hertz-Mindlin model 
established two components of the force exerted over the body, the normal and tangential 
component. Hertz contributes to determine the force in the normal direction while Mindlin add the 
non-slip improved model in the tangential direction. 
The governing equation of the Hertz-Mindlin model contains two main components that 
consider the normal and the tangential direction where the force is exerted respectively on the 
body:

transverse strain or transverse elongation to the axial strain or axial compression would represent 
the Poisson ratio [35], [36].

particle collision without friction. It can also be defined as the ratio between the velocity of the 
particle after the collision divided by the velocity before it, or in other words, the difference in 
kinematic energy of the particles during the collision would be transformed into heat energy[37]. 
d)Shear modulus(G): It is a measurement of the material shear stiffness behaviour under elastics 
forces or, in other words, it physically described the response of the material under elastic shear 
stress force [35].

The rolling friction model is used to determine the rolling drag or resistance opposed to the 
motion of a body when this one rolls on a surface. Even there are several mathematical models in 
the literature to define the rolling friction force, LIGGGHTS software bases the rolling friction 
model on Hertz-Mindlin model because it assumes that the particles in contact are smooth and 
elastic but, it differs from Hertz-Mindlin on how rolling friction is implemented [38].

more accurate relative rotational velocity of two particles in contact when a constant torque in the

4.2.3 
Johnson, Kendal and, Roberts model, JKR model 
 
Johnson, Kendal and, Roberts’s cohesion model or JKR contact model was developed due to 
the lack of information in modelling dry/wet adhesive powders materials. For that purpose, JKR 
contact model combines previous research from Hertz-Mindlin model with Van der Waals 
interaction forces between bodies into a new mathematical model [40], [41], [42]. 
Consequently, JKR model assumes some statements from Hertz-Mindlin model for example: 
• 
No-slip condition in the tangential elastic force, normal and tangential dissipation force,  
• 
Soft, smooth and elastics bodies  
Furthermore, JKR adhesion theory has successfully outlined explaining the influence of the 
surface energy of the material to bring particles in contact. The assumptions made by JKR in terms 
of geometry allows achieving a better approximation to the reality. First, it assumes that the contact 
region between two colliding particles is smaller than the radii of the hemispheres in contact [40], 
[41]. Then, the Van de Waals attractive forces start increasing so, the deformation increases 
proportionally and hence, the overlap region at the equilibrium too[43]. However, at the same time, 
the system fulfils the assumption from the Hertz-Mindlin model which states that the dimensions 
of the solids are much greater than the contact dimensions. As a result, the adhesion force 
generated is known in the JKR model as the pull-off force. 
   
==PAGE:52==

Then, LIGGGHTS uses gran_cohesion_sjkr model which represents another force contribution 
to the overall force balance set up by the DEM methodology. However, this project used a 
modified version of the sjkr known as sjkr3 which is an optimised version where the surface energy 
of the material for parallel computing simulations is manually implemented. The governing 
equation of the pull-off or cohesion force determined by sjkr3 may be expressed as a function of 
the following physical parameter as follows [33] [39][44]: 
a) Surface energy ( ) : It is a material property directly related to the energy required to increase

5.2 Segregation phenomena 
This chapter shows how to characterize the main kiln segregation driving forces, particle size 
and particle density.  
Then, for evaluating the segregation phenomena, it is first required to define what is a mixed system 
and what is a segregated system. Van Puyvelde and, Marlen Arntz [13][20] defined a mixed system 
when the standardised mixing index(previously defined in entropy mixing calculation chapter) 
which goes from 0 to 1, is greater than 0.9. On the other hand, since it is difficult to characterize 
how segregated a system is, an in-house scale based on several degrees of segregation would be 
defined. This scale comprehends mixing index values from 0 up to 0.9. As a result, three different 
degrees of segregation depending on whether the two phases are separated or not . Given a mixing 
index range between 0.9-0.8 a low segregated system is considered. Given a mixing index range 
between 0.8-0.6 an intermediate segregated system is considered while mixing index below 0.6 
would be defined as a fully or highly segregated system. 
Then, the methodology selected would evaluate each driving force individually and quantify the 
radial mixing based on the entropy-like method.

the large particles would remain at the outer layers while the small would reach the bed core or the 
inner part of the bed, in consequence, the bi-disperse bed tend to segregate. 
 
Furthermore, it is possible to observe that the final mixing index when the system reaches the 
steady state depends largely on how large the driving force is. In consequence, it is possible to 
classify the degree of segregation achieved in the steady state as a function of the driving force. 
When the driving force is dparticle-large/dparticle-small < 1.25 which is the case of bi-disperse system 
composed by 4mm and 5 mm, the system reaches a low segregated system. Secondly, driving force 
range 1.5< dparticle-large/dparticle-small <1.75 which comprehends the systems composed by particle 
from 4 and 6,7 mm reaches an intermediate segregated degree. Last, when the system driving force 
dparticle-large/dparticle-small > 2 which in this case represents system composed by particles of 4 mm and

have the same size, percolation or dynamic sieving can be neglected since these mechanisms 
explain the bed segregation by particle size difference and does not explain the segregation 
phenomena in presence of particles with different density. However, buoyancy or convective 
mechanism and inertial mechanism are proposed to explain particle density driving force.

lighter in the outer part of the granular bed. In all systems, the centrifugal force exerted on both

density are more sensitive to this force because they present less mass and therefore it is easier to 
reach the necessary force for flowing in the outer part of the bed. Alternatively, heavier particles 
would flow in the inner part of the bed. The second mechanism that explain the segregation in 
particle density is the buoyancy or convective mechanism. Here, the buoyance forces in the mobile 
bed let the lighter objects float while the heavier sink into the granular bed.

7. CONCLUSIONS 
The presented project has shown the applicability of systematic methodology based on local 
sensitivity analysis and the best engineering practice, as well as a successfully study of the granular 
flow in a rotary kiln reactor was carried out. 
Unfortunately, due to the extremely computer power demanded to implement the DEM 
approach, some the process engineering decisions were taken to simplify the system and reduce 
the computer power bottleneck attached to it. However, they have been resulted appropriate to 
balance between the existing computer power and the accuracy of the results obtained. 
Regarding mapping the bed behaviour diagram, it has been proved that dimensionless Froude 
number and drum filling degree can be used for predicting the bed behaviour patterns. However, 
the cohesive nature of some powder materials has led to define an additional dimensionless 
number, the granular Bond number (Bo). Then, the combination of Froude number, granular Bond 
number and drum filling degree has resulted in a new powder bed behaviour diagram that can 
cover all powder natures with high accuracy. Even the new bed behaviour diagram covers most of 
powder’s nature, local sensitivity analyses also showed how material parameter affects the bed 
behaviour diagram in order to adapt the new bed behaviour to any material processed. 
Additionally, segregation has shown to be an undesirable phenomenon since it reduces the 
homogeneity of granular mixtures when there is a difference in particle size and particle density. 
Then both driving forces have concluded that the higher the driving force the higher is the system 
tendency to segregate. Besides it has been proved that segregation or mixing index when the 
system achieves the steady state is function of particle sizes ratio and particle densities ratio 
respectively and not dependant on the absolute value of each magnitude.  
 Last, the new potential lifter configuration for powder industry have shown first, that there are 
no behaviour patterns able to characterize the granular flow in rotary kilns. Secondly, that it can 
avoid segregation phenomena when particle size and particle density driving force is prevailing in 
the system. 
==PAGE:105==
 
93 
 
 Overall, the present MSc Thesis provides a reliable study of granular flow inside rotary kilns 
itself, and the steps needed to take it to replicate them. 
However, possible improvements for further research can be taken by implementing the heat 
transfer model in LIGGGHTS’s code to understand how it is affected by the bed behaviour patterns 
and, the possibility of reproducing the methodology in a high-performance computer for reducing 
the computer power bottleneck regarding the calculations and post-processing.

9.1 Software selection methodology 
The main limitation of the Discrete Element Method (DEM) is the computational power since 
DEM simulations are highly intensive in terms of computational time due to it requires solving the 
differential governing equations to provide information about the particle tracking and velocity of 
each discrete body at each time-step.  
So, choosing a software package to run DEM simulation is firstly considered. Then, an initial 
evaluation of the different Open-Source Software available has been performed according to the 
following parameters:  
• 
The efficiency of the algorithms for calculating pair-wise interaction forces between 
discrete bodies. 
• 
Allowability to overlay force fields. 
• 
Heat Transfer Analysis. 
• 
Coupling calculation method. 
According to these evaluating parameters, Table 5compares 4 different widely used Open-Source 
Software for granular modelling based on whether the software is capable or not to perform them 
individually.

Preliminary results for small particle size (4 mm) 
From Table 6 it could be observed a defined bed-behaviour pattern when the Fr number is equal 
or greater than 1·100 (Fr≥1) for all filled volume assessed since particles tend to get closer or get 
adhered to the wall. This centrifugal bed-behaviour pattern takes place as a cause of the high 
particle velocity greater than the gravitational force; thus, centrifugal motion occurs.  
 Then as the Fr number decreases, the flow regime pattern changes. For example, given a                    
Fr =1·10-1 when 5% volume is filled, particles do not have enough velocity to centrifuge. However, 
the pattern behaviour looks like rolling motion since the top-layer particles have more velocity 
(particles highlighted light blue colour in Table 7). When 15 % volume is filled, the top part of the 
bed start having a distinctive wave-shape corresponding to the cascading motion. If the filled 
volume is increased up to 25% or 35 %, the pattern behaviour follows the catacracting motion 
since the top part of the bed gains enough speed to jump out of the bed. 
Given Fr =1·10-2, when 5 % and 15 % volume are filled, the bed-behaviour shows the same the 
rolling motion pattern. On the other hand, when 25% and 35% volume are filled is clearly visible 
the wave-shape bed behaviour pattern characteristic of a cascading motion. Besides, looking at the 
velocity profiles table, more concrete at 35% filled volume it is possible to differentiate the active 
and stagnant layer previously described. 
Finally given Fr = 1·10-3, when 5% volume is filled, sliding behaviour pattern is obtained where 
particles slip into the wall according to the kiln rotational speed. Meanwhile when 15% and 25 % 
volume is filled, the slumping behaviour pattern takes places since a synchronized oscillatory 
motion at the shear wedge occurs when the cylinder rotates. Then, when 35% volume is filled a 
clearly rolling motion is achieved since it is possible to differentiate the active and stagnant layer 
due to the velocity profile from Table 7. 
==PAGE:115==
 
103 
 
Additionally, it is possible to evaluate the difference in the particle motion and particle velocity 
between active and stagnant layer. When Froude number is below 1, the active layer (appears in 
the Table 7 and Table 9 in red), indicating rolling downwards with high velocity, opposite to the 
stagnant layer, coloured in blue indicating the upwards movement. However, right under the 
active layer the velocity changes sharply due to the active layer is rolling down while the 
stagnant is rolling up. Therefore, according to our simulation is pointing towards the hypothesis 
that the layer highlighted in dark blue located in between the stagnant and active layer 
corresponds to the core layer since it has theoretically no motion. In this case, the velocity is 
1·105 and 1·102 order of magnitude lower than the active and stagnant layer, respectively. 
Preliminary sensitivity analysis for large particle size (10 mm) 
From Table 8 it could be possible to observe that for Froude number ranges between                      
1·10-3≤ Fr ≤ 1·100 not all the literature patterns could be modelled. 
Given Fr = 1·100 when 5% and 15 % volume are filled, the bed behaviour pattern governing in the 
system is the rolling motion since the top layer is continuously rolling down and, hence discharging 
particles onto the bottom bed layer. Alternatively, when 25% and 35% volume are filled a clear 
wave-shape bed behaviour pattern characteristic of a cascading motion prevails in the system. 
 Given Fr = 10-1, Fr=10-2 and Fr=10-3 the bed behaviour pattern achieved when 5 % and 15 % and 
later for 25% and 35 % volume is filled, are essentially the same. When 5% and 15% volume is 
filled, sliding and slumping motion coexist since the pattern prevailing in the system follows a 
combination between oscillatory motion at the shear wedge at the same time as slumping when 
the cylinder rotates. Then, when 25% and 35 % volume is filled, the bed behaviour pattern 
predominating in the system is the same, the rolling motion.  
 
Overall, from these preliminary sensitivity analyses, it has been proved that the particle size is a 
critical to model the bed-behaviour-map. The large particle (10 mm diameter) cannot be used for 
modelling since it can not to predict most of the literature bed behaviour patterns. Meanwhile, the 
small particle (4 mm diameter) can preliminary be used to determine the flow regimes inside rotary 
kiln since it has modelled all bed behaviour pattern at similar Froude values compares with the 
literature Froude ranges.  
Moreover, given Fr ≥ 1·100 the governing behaviour pattern in all volume filled ranges is the 
centrifuging motion. In contrast, for Fr ≤ 1·100 and, depending on the filling degree, the behaviour 
==PAGE:116==
 
104 
 
pattern may change from sliding up to catacracting. When around 5% volume is filled, the bed 
behaviour patterns could be sliding and rolling. Then, when 15% up to 25 % volume is filled the 
preliminary proven bed behaviour patterns could be slumping, rolling, cascading and, under 
certain conditions Catacracting. Meanwhile, when around 35 % volume is filled, rolling, cascading 
and catacracting bed behaviour pattern could be observed. 
As a result, from preliminary sensitivity analysis it is possible to conclude that 4 mm particle size 
would be suggested for carrying out further analysis in the present MSc Thesis project.
