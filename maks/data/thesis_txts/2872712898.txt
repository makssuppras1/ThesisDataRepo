Summary
Abstract
Background: Deep learning in medical imaging is challenged by limited
data, heterogeneous image characteristics, and privacy constraints, often leading
to class imbalance in training datasets. Generative AI offers a promising solution
by synthesizing realistic, annotated images with controllable properties. This the-
sis investigates conditional data generation for 3D medical imaging, focusing on
meningioma segmentation. Manual tumor annotations remain time-consuming,
inconsistent, and diﬀicult to scale. State-of-the-art models still underperform
on small tumors (<3 ml), limiting early detection and clinical utility. We hy-
pothesize that this performance gap stems from an underrepresentation of small
tumors in training data and explore generative methods to synthesize such cases,
aiming to improve dataset balance without relying on large-scale manual anno-
tation.
Methods: We propose two complementary diffusion-based synthetic data
generation pipelines to mitigate class imbalance. (Track 1) is a conditional la-
tent diffusion model that generates paired 3D MRI volumes and meningioma
masks explicitly conditioned on tumor size. (Track 2) is a mask-based inpaint-
ing method that implants synthetically generated meningiomas into healthy or
non-meningioma brain scans to simulate realistic and double-pathology cases.
We evaluate their impact by training nnU-Net models on various combinations
of real and synthetic data and testing on clinical-grade meningioma cohorts. Ad-
ditionally, we assess generalization to double-pathology scenarios and explore the
potential of zero-shot repurposing of Track 1 to generate “healthy twin” images
for use in residual-based anomaly detection.
Results: Track 2 augmentation improved segmentation performance on tu-
mors under 1 ml (Dice: 68.0% vs. 64.2%) and outperformed the baseline model
on the final test set (Dice: 87.3% vs. 85.7%; Recall: 95.9% vs. 94.5%), and suc-
cessfully recovering 48 of 61 previously missed tumors. The Track 2 pipeline
also generalized to synthetic menigioma-glioma double-pathology cases, improv-
ing Dice from 61.5% to 81.4%. Further Track 1 demonstrated the feasibility of
training segmentation models on fully synthetic data with only a modest drop
in Dice (79.1% vs. 82.1%), and its image generator achieved 53.1% Dice in a
zero-shot anomaly detection proof-of-concept experiment.
Conclusion: This work demonstrates how diffusion-based synthetic augmen-
tation can improve segmentation of small lesions by addressing class imbalance
without altering model architecture. The proposed methods generalize beyond
meningiomas and offer a scalable path toward more robust and clinically appli-
cable segmentation models.

Resumé
Baggrund: Dyb læring inden for medicinsk billeddannelse udfordres af be-
grænsede data, heterogene billedkarakteristika og privatlivsbegrænsninger, hvil-
ket ofte fører til klasseubalance i træningsdatasæt. Generativ AI tilbyder en
lovende løsning ved at syntetisere realistiske, annoterede billeder med kontrol-
lerbare egenskaber. Denne afhandling undersøger betinget datagenerering til 3D
medicinsk billeddannelse med fokus på meningiomsegmentering. Manuelle tu-
morannoteringer er fortsat tidskrævende, inkonsistente og vanskelige at skalere.
State-of-the-art modeller præsterer stadig under niveau på små tumorer (<3 ml),
hvilket begrænser tidlig detektion og klinisk anvendelighed. Vi antager, at dette
præstationsgab stammer fra en underrepræsentation af små tumorer i trænings-
data, og udforsker generative metoder til at syntetisere sådanne tilfælde med det
formål at forbedre datasætbalancen uden at være afhængig af manuel annotering
i stor skala.
Metoder: Vi foreslår to komplementære diffusionsbaserede syntetiske data-
genereringspipelines for at afbøde klasseubalance. (Spor 1) er en betinget latent
diffusionsmodel, der genererer parrede 3D MR-volumener og meningiommasker
eksplicit betinget af tumorstørrelse. (Spor 2) er en maskebaseret indmalings-
metode, der implanterer syntetisk genererede meningiomer i raske eller ikke-
meningeom hjernescanninger for at simulere realistiske og dobbeltpatologiske
tilfælde. Vi evaluerer deres effekt ved at træne nnU-Net-modeller på forskelli-
ge kombinationer af reelle og syntetiske data og teste på meningiomkohorter af
klinisk kvalitet. Derudover vurderer vi generalisering til dobbeltpatologiske sce-
narier og udforsker potentialet for zero-shot genbrug af Spor 1 for at generere
”raske tvillinge”-billeder til brug i residualbaseret anomalidetektion.
Resultater: Spor 2-augmentation forbedrede segmenteringsydelsen på tu-
morer under 1 ml (Dice: 68,0% vs. 64,2%) og overgik baselinemodellen på det
endelige testsæt (Dice: 87,3% vs. 85,7%; Recall: 95,9% vs. 94,5%) og gendannede
med succes 48 af 61 tidligere oversete tumorer. Spor 2-pipelinen generaliserede
også til syntetiske meningiom-gliom dobbeltpatologiske tilfælde, hvilket forbed-
rede Dice fra 61,5% til 81,4%. Yderligere demonstrerede Spor 1 muligheden for
at træne segmenteringsmodeller på fuldt syntetiske data med kun et beskedent
fald i Dice (79,1% vs. 82,1%), og dens billedgenerator opnåede 53,1% Dice i et
zero-shot anomali-detektions proof-of-concept-eksperiment.
Konklusion: Dette arbejde demonstrerer, hvordan diffusionsbaseret synte-
tisk augmentation kan forbedre segmenteringen af små læsioner ved at adressere
klasseubalance uden at ændre modelarkitekturen. De foreslåede metoder genera-
liserer ud over meningiomer og tilbyder en skalerbar vej mod mere robuste og
klinisk anvendelige segmenteringsmodeller.

Chapter1
Introduction
Meningiomas are the most common intracranial tumors in adults, accounting for 41%
of all primary brain and central nervous system (CNS) tumors and over 56% of non-
malignant CNS tumors, with an incidence of 9.7 per 100,000 individuals (Ostrom
et al., 2023). These typically benign, slow-growing tumors originate from arachnoid
mater cells involved in cerebrospinal fluid production. Many meningiomas are discov-
ered incidentally during scans for unrelated symptoms, with a reported prevalence of
0.9–2.5% among individuals undergoing magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) (Ostrom
et al., 2023; Solheim et al., 2014). MRI is the diagnostic gold standard, providing
detailed information on tumor size and location. However, MRI analysis is challenged
by intensity inhomogeneity, scanner variability, and differences in acquisition param-
eters (Tustison et al., 2010; Nyúl et al., 2000). Additionally, small meningiomas may
resemble blood vessels, complicating detection.
For asymptomatic and benign cases, the European Association of Neuro-Oncology
(EANO) recommends annual MRI follow-up for five years after diagnosis, and less
frequently thereafter (Goldbrunner et al., 2016). Due to their variable growth and
irregular morphology, response assessment in clinical trials is diﬀicult, and no standard
criteria exist.
Surgical intervention is typically considered upon progression, often
based on 1D or 2D measurements. However, volumetric measurements have shown
stronger associations with overall survival (Huang et al., 2018). Although evidence
remains limited, interest in volumetric assessment is growing, particularly for detecting
subtle changes in small or complex tumors such as those with dural tails (Huang et al.,
2018). Nevertheless, manual segmentation remains time-consuming, labor-intensive,
and subject to intra- and inter-rater variability (Berntsen et al., 2020; Binaghi et al.,
2016), which is especially pronounced for small or atypical tumors, and in cases with
comorbidities, post-treatment artifacts, or other factors that alter tumor appearance
on MRI.
This motivates the need for automated or semi-automated segmentation methods
to reduce radiologists’ workload and improve segmentation quality, ultimately enhanc-
ing diagnostic accuracy by minimizing human variability and enable earlier detection
of tumor progression. Moreover, accurate tumor delineation is essential for data-driven
research, where imaging serves as a key biomarker.
Deep learning, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), has demon-
strated strong performance across medical imaging tasks such as segmentation, synthe-
sis, denoising, and anomaly detection (Galić et al., 2023). Unlike traditional methods
that rely on handcrafted features, deep learning learns representations directly from
data, reducing subjectivity and improving accuracy. State-of-the-art meningioma seg-
mentation models trained on the public BraTS 2023 challenge dataset (LaBella et
al., 2024) have achieved Dice scores (a measure of lesion overlap) of approximately
86% on the test set. However, in clinical practice, models trained on non-public data
are often preferred, as public datasets are typically cleaned and limited to pathology-

specific cases, lacking the diversity of real-world settings where multiple pathologies
may coexist (Yu et al., 2022; Ahluwalia et al., 2023).
One such state-of-the-art model, trained on non-public clinical-trial data (Bouget
et al., 2021) and referred to as the Bouget model, is used in a semi-automated pipeline
during initial research. In this workflow, physicians first review its meningioma seg-
mentations and then manually refine those predictions to produce the final volumetric
delineation for potential use in clinical practice. This U-Net-based architecture is heav-
ily inspired by the nnU-Net framework (Isensee et al., 2018) with incorporated global
attention mechanisms. Based on five-fold cross-validation using 600 MRI datasets, it
achieved an average Dice score of 81.6% and a detection precision of 98%. However,
tumors smaller than 3 ml were occasionally missed, resulting in an overall recall of
93%.
Inspired by the findings of (Bouget et al., 2021), we hypothesize that the limita-
tion lies not in the model architecture itself, but in the broader challenges of class
imbalance and limited training data (Hellín et al., 2024). These issues are particu-
larly pronounced in medical imaging, where datasets are often small, heterogeneous
in acquisition, and subject to privacy constraints. Expanding the training dataset
with additional cases containing sub-3 ml tumors may therefore improve the model’s
performance in detecting the smallest lesions. However, acquiring such data is often
impractical due to the need for extensive manual annotation.
A promising alternative is to leverage recent advances in generative Artificial Intel-
ligence (AI) to synthesize and augment training data. In particular, diffusion models
have shown superior performance over earlier generative methods such as generative
adversarial networks (GANs) and variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Ho et al., 2020).
Further improvements, such as latent diffusion models, where the diffusion process
operates in a compressed latent space, have made high-resolution 3D medical image
generation feasible (Rombach et al., 2021; Pinaya et al., 2022).
By generating realistic synthetic images conditioned on dataset-specific features
such as tumor size, generative models can produce tailored datasets that supplement
real data, mitigate class imbalance, and improve model robustness.
Recent stud-
ies have even shown that segmentation models trained entirely on synthetic data
can maintain reasonable performance (Fernandez et al., 2022; Fernandez et al., 2024;
Ruschke et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024).
1.1
Aim of the Study
This project investigates methods for conditional data generation to synthesize anno-
tated 3D medical images for use in imbalanced datasets. As a specific application, we
focus on meningioma segmentation and aim to address limitations in current state-
of-the-art models, particularly their reduced performance on small tumors below 3
ml, as reported by (Bouget et al., 2021). To this end, we propose two complemen-
tary data generation pipelines: (Track 1) a conditional latent diffusion framework that
generates full-volume 3D MRIs alongside voxel-wise meningioma masks, explicitly con-
ditioning on tumor volume to mitigate class imbalance; and (Track 2) a mask-based
diffusion in-painting strategy that “transplants” size-controlled synthetic meningiomas
into otherwise healthy or non-meningioma scans to create heterogeneous and poten-
tially dual-pathology cases that better reflect real-world variability.
We evaluate clinical utility by (a) quantifying nnU-Net segmentation performance

1.2
Related Work
1.2.1
Early Efforts in Generative Modeling
The use of synthetic data dates back to early statistical methods (Rubin, 1993), with
modern approaches evolving rapidly through deep learning (Drechsler et al., 2023).
Among the earliest deep learning breakthroughs were Generative Adversarial Networks
(GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014), known for generating sharp images but challenged
by training instability and mode collapse. Variants like Wasserstein and conditional
GANs improved robustness and realism (Arjovsky et al., 2017; Mirza et al., 2014).
In parallel, Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma et al., 2013) offered stable
training by modeling a latent distribution, but often produced blurrier outputs. En-
hancements such as adversarial/perceptual losses (Plumerault et al., 2020; Johnson
et al., 2016) and discrete latent spaces (e.g., VQ-VAEs (Yu et al., 2022)) improved
fidelity by reducing oversmoothing.
1.2.2
Diffusion Models: A New Paradigm in Generative Modeling
Diffusion models have recently emerged as a powerful alternative to GANs and VAEs.
First introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015 and popularized through Denoising Dif-
fusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020), these models define generation
as a gradual denoising of pure noise. While highly effective, DDPMs are computation-
ally expensive due to their many sampling steps. Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models
(DDIMs) (Song et al., 2020) mitigate this by using a deterministic, non-Markovian
sampling process that retains the original training objective but accelerates inference
by 10–50×, requiring no retraining.
A key advancement for high-dimensional data like medical imaging was the La-
tent Diffusion Model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2021), which compresses images into a
lower-dimensional latent space before applying the diffusion process. This significantly
reduces memory and compute demands while maintaining image fidelity. LDMs have
enabled the synthesis of full-resolution 2D/3D medical images and laid the ground-
work for conditional generation in complex domains. Notably, (Pinaya et al., 2022)
showed successful conditioning on demographic covariates and released the large-scale
LDM100k dataset, enabling large-scale training without requiring access to sensitive
patient data.
1.2.3
Diffusion-Based Generation of Image–Label Pairs
Our study focuses on generating paired image–label data for downstream supervised
segmentation tasks. To this end, Fernandez et al., 2022 introduced brainSPADE, a
two-stage pipeline using latent diffusion models. The first stage generates semantic
label maps that segment the brain into tissue classes (white matter, gray matter, and
cerebrospinal fluid), while the second stage translates these maps into realistic MRIs
using spatially adaptive normalization (SPADE) (Park et al., 2019). This approach
was extended to 3D in brainSPADE3D (Fernandez et al., 2024), which supports class
conditioning on pathological phenotypes (e.g., white matter hyperintensities, tumor
contrast states) and MRI contrasts. The authors demonstrated that nnU-Net models

1.2.4
Diffusion-Based Inpainting for Data Augmentation
An alternative strategy for synthetic data generation involves diffusion-based mask
inpainting models, first introduced by Lugmayr et al., 2022. These models enable aug-
mentation of real images by synthesizing and inserting plausible content into masked
regions, providing a promising approach for tumor implantation.
In a related direction, Hu et al., 2023 proposed a non-diffusion method for in-
serting liver tumors into CT images using simple heuristics. Their method generates
ellipsoidal tumor labels that are deformed, textured, and blended into the image. Al-
though this technique does not directly apply to the more complex appearance of
brain tumors in MRI, we adopt a similar idea for label generation. Specifically, we
implement a heuristic-based method that simulates random tumor growth to create
more irregular yet relatively rounded shapes.
Building on diffusion-based approaches, Chen et al., 2024 introduced TumorDiff,
a latent diffusion framework in which real images are encoded and a downsampled
mask is used to zero out part of the latent representation. The model is then trained
to reconstruct the masked region, allowing it to synthesize tumors during inference.
Applied to CT scans of the liver, kidney, and pancreas, this method improved down-
stream segmentation performance by up to 10 percent in Dice score when synthetic
tumors were inserted into real images, outperforming the heuristic method proposed
in Hu et al., 2023.
Our method builds on similar principles as TumorDiff, but introduces several key
modifications. While TumorDiff relies on a latent diffusion model, we instead employ
a standard DDPM that operates directly in image space.
Moreover, rather than
conditioning on the entire image, we restrict the inpainting process to localized patches.
To compensate for the reduced spatial context, we integrate SPADE conditioning using
anatomical label maps to guide the generation process.

Synthetic Healthy Twin Frameworks for Anomaly Detection
An emerging approach in medical anomaly detection involves generating a synthetic
“healthy digital twin” of the input image and identifying anomalies via residual dif-
ferences.
For example, Wolleb et al., 2022 proposed a weakly-supervised method
using DDIM and classifier guidance to denoise tumor-containing images into tumor-

labels.

we investigated whether it could be repurposed for anomaly detection in a zero-shot
setting. By omitting the tumor label from the input segmentation map, the model
generates a structurally similar brain without the tumor, potentially producing a

2.1
Clinical Context: Intracranial Pathologies Relevant to This Study
The primary clinical focus of this thesis is on meningiomas. However, gliomas and
multiple sclerosis (MS) are also briefly introduced, as these conditions are incorporated
into some of the data generation pipelines to simulate multi-pathology cases.
2.1.1
Meningiomas: Pathology, Diagnosis, and Monitoring
Meningiomas are extra-axial tumors arising from arachnoid cap cells and are the
most prevalent primary brain tumors in adults, with a higher occurrence in females
(13.2/100,000) than in males (5.8/100,000) (Ostrom et al., 2023). Thus, there are
often more females in clinical meningioma datasets. Menigiomas are typically benign
and slow-growing, often located near the cerebral convexities, falx cerebri, or skull
base. Most tumors exert mass effect on adjacent brain structures rather than infiltrat-
ing parenchyma, which distinguishes them from intra-axial tumors such as gliomas
(Yarabarla et al., 2023).
The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies meningiomas into three grades
based on histological criteria and mitotic activity: Grade I (benign), Grade II (atypi-
cal), and Grade III (anaplastic/malignant) (Louis et al., 2016). While Grade I tumors
account for the majority (80%), Grade II and III tumors exhibit higher recurrence rates
and poorer prognoses. Despite their generally benign nature, meningiomas exhibit in-
tratumoral heterogeneity, with rare cases transforming from low-grade to high-grade
tumors (Bi et al., 2017).
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is the gold standard for non-invasive diagnosis,
offering excellent soft-tissue contrast and the ability to delineate tumor boundaries and
assess mass effect. Meningiomas often present as well-circumscribed, homogeneously
enhancing masses on post-contrast T1-weighted sequences, frequently exhibiting a
dural tail. However, visual diagnosis is challenged by several factors: small tumors may
resemble vascular structures, lesions near bone may suffer from susceptibility artifacts,
and post-operative or post-radiation changes may obscure tumor boundaries.

2.2.
MAGNETIC RESONANCE IMAGING (MRI)
While many patients are asymptomatic at diagnosis, tumor monitoring is essential.
For benign meningiomas, the European Association of Neuro-Oncology (EANO) rec-
ommends active surveillance via annual MRI scans for at least five years post-diagnosis
(Goldbrunner et al., 2016). Radiological progression is typically assessed using 1D or
2D measurements. Volumetric analysis has also been proposed as a more reliable alter-
native, enabling finer-grained tracking of tumor evolution. It is particularly valuable
for complex morphologies and when assessing treatment response.
2.1.2
Gliomas
Gliomas are intra-axial brain tumors arising from glial cells, including astrocytomas
and glioblastomas. On T1-weighted contrast-enhanced MRI, high-grade gliomas often
exhibit heterogeneous enhancement and necrotic cores, which can resemble irregular
or atypical meningiomas. Their infiltrative growth leads to indistinct margins, further
complicating differentiation. While typical imaging patterns differ, visual overlap with
meningiomas may occur in complex or atypical cases.
2.1.3
Multiple Sclerosis (MS)
Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a demyelinating disease characterized by white matter lesions
that can visually resemble tumors on MRI. Although MS lesions typically lack mass
effect, they may transiently enhance on T1-weighted contrast images during active
phases, occasionally mimicking small tumors and complicating distinction in mixed-
pathology settings.
2.2
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a widely adopted imaging technique in neurology
and neurosurgery, primarily due to its excellent soft tissue contrast. This makes MRI
particularly helpful for evaluating brain structures, identifying vascular abnormalities,
and detecting or monitoring tumors such as meningiomas. Compared to Computed
Tomography (CT), MRI provides clearer seperation of both the tumor and surrounding
tissue, enabling more precise assessments of size, location, and potential involvement
with critical brain regions. Additionally, MRI does not use ionizing radiation, which
is advantageous for repeated follow-up scans and reduces long-term radiation risk.
Despite these benefits, MRI exams can be more time-intensive and costly than CT.
Patients with ferromagnetic implants (e.g., certain pacemakers or aneurysm clips) may
be contraindicated for MRI due to the strong magnetic field. Moreover, patient motion
and fluctuations in magnetic field homogeneity can produce artifacts that sometimes
compromise the reproducibility of quantitative measurements. Figure 2.1 presents a
cutaway diagram of the MRI acquisition process together with an example 3D volume,
illustrating the modality’s capacity to capture fine anatomical detail.
2.2.1
MRI Imaging Sequences
MRI exploits nuclear magnetic resonance in hydrogen protons, primarily in water. In
a strong magnetic field, protons align with the field and are briefly disturbed by a
radiofrequency (RF) pulse. As they realign, they emit RF signals, which are spatially
mapped via Fourier transforms. Varying sequence parameters, mainly repetition time
(TR) and echo time (TE), emphasizes different tissue properties through T1 and T2
relaxation. T1 reflects realignment with the magnetic field, while T2 reflects signal
decay due to dephasing. Adjusting these parameters yields varying image contrasts.

2.3
Neural Network Basics: From Core Layers to U-Nets
Deep learning models are built by combining a few key building blocks. At the most
basic level, fully connected layers apply a learned linear transformation z = Wa +
b to an input vector.
For image and volume data, these are usually replaced by
convolutional layers. Convolutions use small filters that slide across the input and
share weights across space, which makes them ideal for processing images.
After each linear transformation, a non-linear activation function is applied. The
most common choice is ReLU (max(0, x)), though smoother alternatives like SiLU
are becoming more often used (Elfwing et al., 2017). Neural networks are trained by
minimizing a loss function through mini-batch gradient descent. Instead of computing
gradients over the full dataset, updates are made using small batches, which improves
eﬀiciency and generalization. During backpropagation, gradients are computed using
the chain rule:

squared values (Kingma et al., 2017).
To improve training stability, normalization layers are used.
Batch Normaliza-
tion normalizes across the mini-batch but is less effective with small batches. Group
Normalization, which normalizes within each sample over grouped channels, is more
reliable for small-batch 3D models (Wu et al., 2018). Instance Normalization further
simplifies this by normalizing each channel independently per sample, and is particu-
larly useful in tasks like style transfer (Park et al., 2019).
To reduce the spatial size of feature maps, models often use strided convolutions
or pooling operations. This helps the network focus on larger patterns in the data.
Upsampling methods like nearest-neighbor interpolation are used in the reverse direc-
tion to recover spatial resolution. These are often followed by convolutions that refine
the upsampled features.
All these elements come together in the U-Net architecture, which is widely used
for tasks like image segmentation, see Figure 2.3. U-Net has an encoder that gradually
reduces spatial resolution while increasing the number of feature channels. Each stage
typically uses convolution, normalization, and activation layers. The decoder then
upsamples the features and combines them with feature maps from earlier layers in the
encoder using skip connections. These connections help preserve spatial details that
might otherwise be lost. This simple yet powerful design has made U-Net a popular
choice in both biomedical image analysis and generative modeling (Ronneberger et al.,
2015).

2.4
Variational Autoencoders
Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) extend the standard autoencoder framework by
learning a probabilistic latent space that enables sampling and generation of new data
(Kingma et al., 2013). A conventional autoencoder consists of an encoder that com-
presses the input x into a latent representation z, and a decoder that reconstructs x
from z. Architecturally, this corresponds to a U-Net without skip connections, trained
to minimize reconstruction loss, typically measured as mean squared error.
VAEs reinterpret this latent space in probabilistic terms (see Figure 2.4). Instead
of mapping x to a fixed point in latent space, the encoder learns to approximate the
posterior qϕ(z|x), often parameterized as a multivariate Gaussian with diagonal vari-
ance N(µϕ(x), σ2
ϕ(x)I). The decoder models the likelihood pθ(x|z) of reconstructing
x from a latent sample z. This probabilistic formulation is crucial for generative mod-
eling: by learning a distribution over the latent space, new samples can be generated
by sampling z ∼p(z) and decoding through pθ(x|z). Thus, VAEs enable not only
reconstruction but also the synthesis of entirely new, coherent data samples.

Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) require hundreds to thousands of

2.7.4
Classifier-Free Guidance for Enhanced Control
Even with powerful conditioning modules, sampling can drift toward low-frequency
artefacts or ignore weak signals. Classifier-free guidance (CFG) (Ho, 2022) introduces
a simple and lightweight training-time trick that enables adjustable control at infer-
ence. The model is trained on both conditional and unconditional inputs by randomly
replacing the conditioning vector c with a null token (e.g., all zeros), allowing it to
learn both modes within a single network. At inference, one obtains the conditional
noise prediction ϵθ(xt, c) and its unconditional counterpart ϵθ(xt, ∅), and extrapolates:
ˆϵθ(xt, c) = (1 + w) ϵθ(xt, c) −w ϵθ(xt, ∅),
(2.23)
where w is the guidance scale. Setting w = −1 recovers standard unconditional
generation. For w ≥0, we have conditional generation: w = 0 corresponds to standard
conditional generation, while larger values of w extrapolate beyond the model’s direct
outputs, amplifying the effect of the conditioning signal. Increasing w strengthens the
fidelity to the conditioning at the expense of sample diversity.

Chapter3
Materials and Methods
This study investigates methods for improving meningioma segmentation accuracy by
generating synthetic training data designed to mitigate class imbalance, particularly
the underrepresentation of small meningiomas. To this end, two complementary tracks
of approaches will be investigated:
• Track 1: Synthetic Data Generation
We will generate fully synthetic brain MRIs with meningiomas, paired with
corresponding label maps. This will be done using a two-stage conditional latent
diffusion pipeline (see Section 3.2). By explicitly controlling tumor size, we can
synthesize balanced datasets tailored to underrepresented cases.
• Track 2: Real Data Augmentation
We will augment real brain MRIs by inpainting synthetic meningiomas into se-
lected regions using an adapted denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM)
for mask inpainting (see Section 3.3). Our proposed model allows precise con-
trol over tumor location and enables synthetic augmentation of cases with co-
occurring pathologies. For example, it can insert meningiomas into subjects with
multiple sclerosis or other brain tumors such as gliomas, where real training data
are scarce and current meningioma segmentation models may fail to generalize.
As a downstream task, we train multiple segmentation models (see Section 3.4).
Each model is trained on a different combination of real meningioma datasets and
tailored synthetic datasets generated using the pipelines developed in Track 1 and
Track 2. The goal is to evaluate segmentation performance across these configurations.
In doing so, we aim to quantify the benefit of using synthetic and augmented data to
improve accuracy.

3.1.3
Initial Data Harmonization and Preprocessing
To consolidate data from multiple sources, we implemented a standardized prepro-
cessing pipeline that mirrors the steps applied to the BraTS-MEN-2023 dataset.
All MRI volumes were resampled to a uniform isotropic resolution of 1 mm3, using
bilinear interpolation for the image data and nearest-neighbor interpolation for the
corresponding segmentation maps to preserve label integrity.
Following resampling, all datasets were registered to MNI space (MNI152 NLIN
2009a symmetric) using rigid-body transformations implemented in ANTs (Fonov et
al., 2009; Avants et al., 2011). For MRI datasets not already skull-stripped, we applied
HD-BET (Isensee et al., 2019). While this skull stripping is not ideal, since it can
remove tumors near the skull, this step was necessary to maintain compatibility with
BraTS-MEN-2023, which is our largest meningioma dataset.
Consequently, this
choice inherently limits our analysis to tumors not affected by skull stripping.
For deep learning training, MRI intensities were normalized by clipping to the
[0.5, 99.5] percentile range, followed by min-max normalization to the range [0,1]. We
deliberately avoided z-normalization, bias correction, or other intensity adjustments to
allow the model to learn and reproduce the inherent variability in raw clinical scans.
If normalization is required for specific downstream tasks, it can be applied to the
synthetic outputs prior to use.
As a final preprocessing step, all data were downsampled to an isotropic resolution
of 1.5 mm3 and center-cropped to a matrix size of 96 × 128 × 96. This downsampling
was necessary to ensure full brain coverage within a single volume while fitting the
memory constraints of the available GPU hardware and avoiding undesired patch-
based processing approaches (Mechrez et al., 2016).
3.1.4
Obtaining Reference Label Maps
Not all meningioma datasets used in this project were accompanied by the same quality
of ground truth segmentation labels. The BraTS datasets include expert-annotated
label maps, which we treat as reference ground truth. Notably, the BraTS-MEN-
2023 dataset contains three tumor subclasses, one of which represents surrounding
FLAIR hyperintensity. In contrast, the other datasets provide only a single tumor class
without subclass differentiation. To harmonize the label definitions across datasets, we
converted the BraTS-MEN-2023 segmentations into a single-class format by merging
class 1 (enhanced tumor core) and class 3 (non-enhanced tumor core), while excluding
class 2 (surrounding FLAIR hyperintensity).
Ground truth segmentations are not available for the RH-MEN-QA cases. How-
ever, for the RH-MEN-QA-2+3 subset, where the Bouget model performed well, we
use the raw Bouget segmentations as pseudo-ground truth reference labels for com-
puting validation metrics. For RH-MEN-QA-0, no reference labels are available, and
this subset is therefore evaluated using only qualitative assessment (QA) scores.
The RH-MEN-TEST set does include expert-annotated ground truth segmen-
tations, which were manually refined by radiologists.
However, it is important to
note that these annotations were not created from scratch; instead, they were based
on Bouget model predictions, potentially introducing a bias in favor of the baseline
model.
In addition to tumor labels, we obtained full-brain segmentations into 33 anatom-
ical classes using SynthSeg (Billot et al., 2023). The tumor segmentation was then
superimposed onto the anatomical map to produce a comprehensive 34-class label

3.2
Track 1: Synthetic Data Generation
3.2.1
Short Overview and Mathematical Notation
Track 1 focuses on developing a synthetic MRI generation pipeline to create brain im-
ages with meningiomas and corresponding segmentation labels for use in downstream
supervised segmentation algorithms. The approach builds on prior two-stage pipelines
(Fernandez et al., 2024; Ruschke et al., 2024) using latent diffusion models (LDMs)
(Rombach et al., 2021): a mask generator that produces semantic label maps (includ-
ing tumor regions) and an image generator that synthesizes realistic MRIs conditioned
on these labels, refer to Figure 3.4.
To enhance control over tumor characteristics, the mask generator incorporates a
conditioning variable that explicitly controls tumor size in the generated images. This
approach is inspired by previous methods that condition on anatomical features such
as brain volume, ventricle volume, and sex (Pinaya et al., 2022; Ruschke et al., 2024).
The resulting model enables the generation of diverse, high-quality fully synthetic
datasets with customizable tumor size distributions. This has the potential to mitigate
the class imbalance often observed in real datasets, particularly for underrepresented
cases such as very small or very large tumors.
The model assumes access to a dataset D = {(xi, si, ci) | i = 0, . . . , N}, where
each data point consists of: a normalized 3D MRI image x ∈[0, 1]H×W ×D, a one-hot
encoded segmentation mask s ∈{0, 1}34×H×W ×D containing 33 SynthSeg label classes
and one meningioma class, and a min-max normalized tumor-to-brain volume ratio
c ∈[0, 1]. Our method involves training four distinct neural networks. Two of these are
autoencoders that map MRI segmentation masks and MRI images, respectively, into
latent representations: zmask

3.2.2
Model Architectures
For the autoencoder and denoising-diffusion network architectures, we based our de-
signs largely on the generative models available in MONAI’s open-source codebase
(Pinaya et al., 2023). MONAI supplies modular, state-of-the-art implementations of
variational autoencoders and U-Nets, which we adapted to our needs. The final model
architectures used in this project are presented below.
3.2.2.1
The Autoencoders
Mask Autoencoder
The Mask Autoencoder (AE) is based on a variational autoen-
coder (VAE), following the approach of AutoencoderKL from Rombach et al., 2021.
However, because our model operates on very sparse and discrete one-hot encoded
segmentation masks, we adapted the standard architecture, which is designed for con-
tinuous image inputs, to suit our domain.
To handle the sparse input, we introduce a simple, learnable lookup table that
embeds each one-hot encoded class vector into a 64-dimensional vector. This results
in a dense input tensor of shape 64 × H × W × D, which is fed into the main model
architecture (see Figure 3.5). The backbone consists of three resolution levels, each
separated by downsampling and upsampling blocks that halve the spatial dimensions
at each level. The core components are pairs of residual and attention blocks, described

Image Autoencoder
The Image AE shares the same encoder–decoder backbone as
the Mask AE, with only a few key modifications. Since the input is a continuous-valued
MRI image rather than a discrete one-hot mask, no embedding layer is needed. Simi-
larly, inference post-processing is replaced by a simple clamping to the valid intensity
range [0, 1].
Further, we set the latent dimensionality to Limage = 8 to provide suﬀicient ca-
pacity for reconstructing fine-grained intensity details, rather than just discrete labels.
This means the image latent is compressed by a factor 8/(1 × 43) = 1/8. To incorpo-
rate anatomical context, each upsampled residual block in the decoder is conditioned
on the segmentation mask. This is achieved by replacing the standard Group Normal-
isation layers with SPADE layers; see Section 2.7.3 for background on SPADE-based
semantic conditioning.

3.2.5
Training
All training is conducted using Distributed Data Parallel (DDP) across four NVIDIA
A40 GPUs, with an Adam optimizer (β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999).
Autoencoder Training
The autoencoders are first pretrained on the LDM100K
dataset for 20 epochs using a batch size of 1 per GPU (effective batch size of 4 due
to DDP). A OneCycle learning rate schedule is used, peaking at 5 × 10−5, to promote
rapid initial convergence (Kingma et al., 2017; Smith et al., 2018). Fine-tuning is
then performed on the BraTS dataset for 100 epochs using a multi-step learning rate
schedule: 1 × 10−5 for the first 80 epochs, 1 × 10−6 for the next 15, and 1 × 10−7 for
the final 5 epochs. To improve generalization, we apply 10% dropout and mild data
augmentations during training, including random rotations (±10◦), zoom scaling in
the range [0.9, 1.1], and random coronal flips with corresponding left-right swapping
of SynthSeg label classes.
For adversarial training, we adopt an incremental strategy to stabilize learning in
the early stages. Specifically:
1. Epochs 0–3: The adversarial loss is disabled, and the discriminator weights
are frozen. During these initial epochs, the generator focuses on learning basic
intensity reconstruction using only the LL1, Lperc, and LKL terms.

3. Epochs 6–onward: We enable the adversarial loss for the generator, complet-

Denoising U-Net Training
We pretrain the denoising U-Net on the LDM100K
dataset, using precomputed latent codes zµ and zσ obtained from the pretrained
autoencoders. This avoids re-encoding full images at each step, allowing for eﬀicient
training. The U-Net is trained for 250 epochs using a multi-step learning rate schedule:
1×10−6 for the first 150 epochs, 1×10−7 for the next 75, and 1×10−8 for the final 25
epochs. Fine-tuning on the BraTS dataset follows a similar schedule over 500 epochs:
1 × 10−6 for the first 375 epochs, 1 × 10−7 for the next 100, and 1 × 10−8 for the final
25 epochs.
The Mask noise predictor is trained simultaneously for both conditional and uncon-
ditional modes, enabling classifier-free guidance (CFG) during sampling for stronger
conditional generation (see Section 2.7.4). Conditioning is controlled using the binary
flag in the 2D context input. During training, we apply a context dropout strategy;
with a probability of 10%, a conditional context vector [c, 0] is replaced with an uncon-
ditional context vector [˜c, 1], where ˜c ∈[0, 1] is sampled uniformly at random. This
prevents the model from relying on unconditional context information to reconstruct
the output. The binary flag thus clearly signals whether the model should condition
on the provided context or ignore it.

3.2.6
Inference
All inference and sampling experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA Titan RTX
GPU with 24 GB of memory, allowing for a batch size of four images. To accelerate
generation, we adopt the deterministic DDIM sampler (Section 2.6.1), which decouples
the number of denoising steps from the original 1000-step diffusion trajectory. We
experiment with different numbers of inference steps, but unless otherwise specified, we
report results using 200 inference steps, as this setting did not noticeably compromise
sample quality.

3.2.8
Experimental Hyperparameter Tuning
To optimize the components of our pipeline, we conducted a series of empirical experi-
ments to tune key hyperparameters. Given the number of configurations tested during
early prototyping, we present detailed results only for the most informative settings.
Mask Generator.
We investigated several variations to improve mask generation
quality. These included adding a pretraining stage versus training from scratch, re-
placing sparse one-hot encodings with learnable lookup embeddings, and including
or excluding a Dice loss term. We also evaluated the effect of standard data aug-
mentations and tested increasing the weight of the tumor Dice loss by a factor of
10 to emphasize accurate tumor reconstruction. Architectural modifications included
doubling the latent dimension Lmask from 4 to 8 and halving the number of residual–
attention blocks per resolution level.
Image Autoencoder.
For the image autoencoder, we tested reducing the latent
dimensionality from Limage = 8 to Limage = 4, as well as halving the number of residual
attention blocks. We also evaluated the effect of including or omitting perceptual and
adversarial loss terms to assess their individual contributions to reconstruction quality.
Diffusion Models.
For the diffusion models, we compared two strategies for ob-
taining latent training samples: using the mean latent vector µz versus sampling from
the full Gaussian distribution N(µz, σ2

3.3.1
DDPM Framework for Mask Inpainting
Our proposed denoising U-Net operates on a context window x0 ∈RHw×Ww×Dw,
where Hw, Ww, Dw denote the spatial dimensions of the window, selected to be
smaller than the full image shape. In our experiments, we set Hw = Ww = Dw = 32,
matching the latent resolution used in the Track 1 LDMs. With more available GPU
resources, larger context windows could be considered to provide more context to the
model.
The context window is normalized from [0, 1] to [−1, 1], which we empirically found
beneficial for the DDPM training.
The model also takes as input a binary mask
m ∈RHw×Ww×Dw, which defines the inpainting region.
This mask is created by
setting all voxels within an L1 distance less than or equal to a radius R from the
center voxel to 1. For our experiments, we use R = 8, resulting in a cubic inpainting
region of side length 16. We found this to be a good trade-off: it provides suﬀicient
context outside the mask while leaving enough space to insert tumors of a meaningful
size. At our input resolution, the inpainting region has a volume of approximately 14
ml, allowing space for a spherical lesion of up to 7.2 ml.

We use a U-Net architecture similar to the Track 1 image denoising U-Net, which
also conditions on a label map using SPADE layers. The main architectural modifica-
tion is adapting the input layer to accept three channels instead of one. We retain the
same hyperparameters, including two residual blocks per resolution level and channel
sizes [256, 512, 768].
However, we found that the noise schedule used in Track 1 performed poorly in
this setting. Specifically, it caused the context window to become excessively noisy
within just a few initial diffusion steps. This issue arises because the new data range is
[−1, 1], and brain tissue intensities are concentrated within a narrow subrange, while
extreme values correspond to background or high-uptake regions. To address this, we
adopted a scaled linear beta schedule with βstart = 0.0001 and βend = 0.02, where
the key advantage is that βstart is substantially lower than the 0.0015 used in Track 1.
Visual inspection confirmed that, under this schedule, the signal remains largely intact
during the early forward diffusion steps, enabling more favorable learning conditions.
3.3.2
Training
All aspects of the training procedure largely followed the Track 1 LDM image model.
The main difference was the use of the RH-MS dataset for pretraining instead of
LDM100K, and increasing the number of pretraining epochs from 250 to 750 to
compensate for the smaller dataset size. The multi-step learning rate schedule was
adjusted proportionally to maintain the same relative timing.
During pretraining, we uniformly sampled window centers anywhere within the full
brain mask so the model could learn to inpaint across all brain regions. During fine-
tuning, we instead centered each window on the BraTS tumor to focus the network on
the newly added tumor class, and to be consistent with our inpainting pipeline, which
always inserts a synthetic tumor label at the center of each window.

3.3.4
Experiments
We compare our framework with two key modifications:
1. Adjusted noise schedule. We present the effect of replacing the original β
schedule used in Track 1 with a modified version with a smaller initial β and
thus slows down the initial signal decay. This adjustment is intended to better
accommodate the narrower intensity range typically observed in normalized MRI
volumes.
2. Pseudo-3D convolutions in the U-Net. Instead of full 3D convolutional
layers, we implemented pseudo-3D layers, in which a 3D convolution is factorized
into a 2D convolution over the axial planes followed by a 1D convolution along
the axial (depth) axis.
This decomposition reduces computational cost and
parameter count while still capturing both in-plane and through-plane features,
as described in Qiu et al., 2017.
The three configurations (3D Track 1 scheduler, 3D with adjusted scheduler, and
Pseudo-3D with adjusted scheduler) are evaluated after pretraining by computing
MAE, SSIM, and PSNR on 100 randomly sampled context windows from RH-MS vali-
dation patients, comparing the regions before and after the inpainting operation. This
is complemented by a qualitative assessment of how seamlessly the synthetic regions
blend with the surrounding real tissue. We then fine-tune the best-performing config-
uration on BraTS-MEN to learn tumor inpainting. To demonstrate its performance,
we show representative examples of synthetic tumors of different sizes implanted into
the RH-MS dataset.
3.4
Downstream Tasks
To evaluate and demonstrate the practical utility of the Track 1 full synthetic data
generation and Track 2 data augmentation pipelines, we designed the following down-

(a) Improving meningioma segmentation performance with synthetic data
We evaluate whether synthetic examples generated by our Track 1 and Track 2
pipelines can mitigate class imbalance and data scarcity in meningioma segmen-
tation. Using the state-of-the-art nnU-Net segmentation framework (Isensee et
al., 2018), we train segmentation models on (1) exclusively real BraTS-MEN
images, (2) purely synthetic images, and (3) hybrid sets combining real and syn-
thetic images. Our aim is to determine whether segmentation networks can be
effectively trained using only synthetic data and to quantify the additional gains
from augmenting real data with synthetic samples.

moving the tumor label from the input segmentation map, prompting the model
to generate a synthetic healthy version of the scan.
3.4.1
Real, Synthetic, and Augmented Datasets
We prepare four distinct datasets for training the downstream segmentation models,
each assigned a unique identifier from 1 to 4:
(1) Real meningioma dataset (n = 1500): Contains all available real menin-
gioma cases from BraTS-MEN-2023 and BraTS-MEN-2024.
(2) Fully synthetic dataset (n = 1500): Generated using the Track 1 pipeline
with brain-to-tumor ratio conditions sampled from a kernel density estimate
(KDE) of the empirical distribution observed in BraTS-MEN. Samples that
failed to generate tumor labels were excluded and resampled. This approach
ensures a diverse set of synthetic images spanning the full spectrum of tumor
sizes, and not just small tumors.
(3) RH-MS with synthetic meningioma implants (n = 2934): The complete
RH-MS dataset, augmented with a single small synthetic tumor per scan using
the Track 2 inpainting pipeline. Tumor volumes are sampled uniformly between
0.1 and 4 ml, resulting in a large dataset specifically targeting small tumors.
(4) BraTS-GLI-2023 with synthetic meningioma implants (n = 1350): The
full BraTS glioma dataset, augmented with small synthetic meningiomas placed

This dataset supports training models to segment
meningiomas in multi-pathology scenarios, particularly where meningiomas and

4.1.2
Image Autoencoder
We also evaluated several image autoencoder variants. Table 4.2 shows selected con-
figurations, testing LDM100K pretraining, adversarial and perceptual losses, and
varying latent dimensions. All models use two residual blocks per level and mild aug-
mentation, which we omit from the table as their effects mirrored those in the mask
autoencoder: fewer blocks reduced performance, while augmentation gave slight gains.
The table shows that pretraining improves all reconstruction metrics, despite be-
ing performed on a non-contrast healthy dataset. Adding an adversarial loss slightly
increases pixel-wise errors (RME, RMAE) but improves PSNR, SSIM, and MS-SSIM,
suggesting more realistic textures. Including a perceptual loss yields modest gains
in pixel-wise metrics (though still slightly below the L1-only model) and further en-
hances texture-related scores, with MS-SSIM significantly increasing from 0.963 to
0.989. Reducing the latent dimensionality from 8 to 4 degraded performance, so we
retained a size of 8 (double that of the mask autoencoder) to better capture texture
details. We selected the model using pretraining, adversarial and perceptual losses,
and a latent dimension of 8 as our final configuration, prioritizing improved texture
metrics over minor losses in pixel level accuracy. As a sanity check, SynthSeg applied
to the reconstructed images yielded an average Dice of 0.933 compared to label maps
derived from real images, confirming that the model preserves semantic content well.

Our experiments highlight three main insights. First, sampling latents from the
latent distribution during training leads to better performance than using the mean,
improving both visual fidelity (FID, KID) and sample diversity (PW-SSIM). Second,
the scaled linear noise schedule consistently outperforms the standard linear variant,
likely due to slower degradation during early diffusion steps.

number of DDIM inference steps slightly lowers diversity, but using as few as 50
steps still produces samples that outperform those generated with a 1000-step linear
schedule, while greatly reducing runtime.

Track 2: Synthetic Data Augmentation
In Track 2, we investigated diffusion-based mask inpainting by comparing three DDPM
setups. We tested whether performance could be improved by (i) reusing the scaled
beta schedule from Track 1, (ii) lowering the initial noise level in a modified schedule,
and (iii) replacing all 3D convolutional layers with pseudo-3D alternatives. As shown
in Table 4.4, the model using full 3D convolutions with βstart = 0.0001 consistently
outperformed the others across all metrics.
However, inpainting is not a typical reconstruction task.
The objective is not
to recover a ground truth image but to plausibly fill in missing regions in line with
the surrounding anatomical structures. For this reason, we interpret the quantitative
metrics with caution and instead turn to qualitative examples in Figure 4.7 for further
insight. When using the larger beta schedule from Track 1, we observed visible noise
artifacts in the output. These are likely caused by the high βstart, which causes rapid
signal degradation during the early diffusion steps, limiting the model’s ability to
recover fine details. In contrast, the modified beta schedule led to significantly cleaner
reconstructions, with synthetic regions blending more seamlessly into the surrounding

Discussion
This study demonstrates that diffusion-based synthetic data augmentation can sub-
stantially improve meningioma segmentation performance, particularly for imbalanced
datasets with an underrepresentation of small tumors. Augmenting real data with
synthetic implants (Track 2) yielded the most consistent improvements, raising Dice
scores for sub-1 ml tumors from 64.2% to 68.0%, and outperforming the baseline on
the final test set (Dice: 87.3% vs. 85.7%, Recall: 95.9% vs. 94.5%). Remarkably, the
model recovered 48 of 61 tumors missed by the baseline. Track 2 also enabled gener-
alization to synthetic multi-pathology cases (Dice: 81.4% vs. 61.5%). Although less
accurate, the Track 1 pipeline demonstrated the feasibility of training segmentation
models on fully synthetic data (Dice: 79.1% vs. 82.1%), and showed proof-of-concept
performance in zero-shot anomaly detection (Dice: 53.1%). Together, these pipelines
offer a scalable, architecture-agnostic solution to class imbalance and model robust-
ness. They integrate smoothly into existing segmentation frameworks and, in settings
with imbalanced data, may provide greater gains than engineering more complex ar-
chitectures. Additionally, both approaches are easily transferable to other pathologies,
enabling more diverse datasets, capturing rare condition combinations, and ultimately
pushing segmentation models toward clinically reliable performance.
The project was initially motivated by the baseline model from Bouget et al., 2021,
which failed to detect a tumor in 62 of 263 cases in the RH-MEN-QA dataset (RH-
MEN-QA-0). We trained a standard nnU-Net on BraTS-MEN data augmented
with RH-MS data containing small, synthetically implanted meningiomas (Track 2),
and then had an expert radiologist evaluate its predictions on the 62 baseline failure
cases.
Of these, 55 were confirmed as true lesions and 7 were ambiguous.
In the
confirmed subset our model produced clinically acceptable segmentations in 33 (60
%), moderate results in 10 (18 %) and failed in 12 (22 %). Further on the RH-MEN-
TEST final test set, our model achieved a Dice of 87.3 % versus 85.7 % for baseline,
driven by higher recall (95.9 % vs. 94.5 %). We missed only one tumor compared
to six by the baseline and never failed on any tumor that the baseline detected. Our
model therefore identified 48 additional lesions: in RH-MEN-QA-0, 20 were under 1
ml, 21 between 1–27 ml, and 2 exceeded 27 ml; in RH-MEN-TEST, 4 were between
1–3 ml and 1 between 1–27 ml. These results show that an nnU-Net trained on a
dataset enriched with small tumors significantly outperforms the baseline model in
detecting small meningiomas, supporting the hypothesis that the baseline model was
trained on data underrepresenting this subgroup.
Notably, the baseline model achieved higher precision on the test set (83.1% vs.
81.5%), suggesting that our model has a higher false positive rate. This may be due
to a slight tendency of our model to oversegment tumor boundaries, as also noted by
the radiologist in several cases from the RH-MEN-QA-0 test set (see Appendix F).
We suspect that the percentile clamping applied during MRI intensity preprocessing
reduced the contrast between enhancing tumors and surrounding CSF. Replacing this
step with simple min–max normalization in future iterations may help better boundary

When comparing the baseline model’s performance on our test set with the per-
formance originally reported by the authors in Bouget et al., 2021, which are based
on five-fold cross-validation on a non-public dataset, the original study reports 81.6%
Dice, 98.0% precision, and 93.0% recall. In contrast, our evaluation yields higher Dice
and recall scores but substantially lower precision. This discrepancy is likely due to
differences in annotation protocols and case distributions between the datasets, espe-
cially regarding lesion size and complexity. This observation suggests that the high
precision reported by Bouget et al., 2021 may not generalize well to external datasets,
though further investigation is needed given the lack of public access to their training
data.
Nevertheless, the Dice scores we report for both the baseline and our models
on RH-MEN-TEST (85–87%) align with the best-performing models on the pub-
lic BraTS-MEN leaderboard, which typically report Dice scores around 86% on the
oﬀicial BraTS-MEN test set.
Fernandez et al., 2024 demonstrated that up to three-quarters of the training data
could be synthetic without significant performance loss compared to using only real
data (Dice scores: 72.2% vs. 72.8%) in white matter hyperintensity segmentation.
Scores drop to 36.2% when using only synthetic images. Although not directly com-
parable to our task, as meningioma segmentation is generally easier due to clearer
tumor boundaries, the study provides useful context. We did not experiment with
varying proportions but trained models using a number of synthetically generated im-
ages comparable to the size of the real datasets, which proved effective in line with
the findings of Fernandez et al., 2024.
We evaluated the impact of adding synthetic data to our nnU-Net segmentation
models by using the network trained on all 1,500 BraTS-MEN cases as a real-data
nnU-Net baseline, then augmenting subsequent models with synthetic small-tumor
cases. On the RH-MEN-QA-2+3 validation set, the nnU-Net baseline model suc-
cessfully detected all tumors. This is unsurprising, as the set includes cases that were
already well handled by the baseline from Bouget et al., 2021, and the BraTS-MEN
dataset used for training contains a relatively large number of sub-3 ml tumors. How-
ever, despite the strength of our pure real-data baseline, adding synthetic small-tumor
examples (using track 2) yielded modest overall gains (82.7 % vs. 82.1 % Dice; 73.4
% vs. 72.9 % precision; 98.3 % vs. 97.8 % recall, for synthetic-augmented vs. real-only
training, respectively), driven primarily by a notable improvement on tumors under 1
ml (Dice increasing from 64.2 % to 68.0 %). As an additional test we used the real-data
baseline to predict tumors on the 33 cases (from RH-MEN-QA-0) that our synthetic
augmented nnU-Net segmented perfectly; 18 of these had Dice scores below 90% when
compared to the augmented model’s predictions and 5 below 50%. However, the real-
data baseline did not completely miss any of these tumors. Hence, while the public
BraTS-MEN dataset alone mitigates much of the small-tumor under-representation
observed in the the baseline from Bouget et al., 2021 training data, our Track 2 tumor-
implant augmentation pipeline may offer additional improvements for segmentation
of the smallest tumors.
While incorporating synthetically implanted meningiomas via the Track 2 pipeline
improved nnU-Net performance, augmenting the real-data baseline with fully syn-
thetic samples from Track 1 did not yield further gains (Dice 80.7% vs.
82.1%).
Nonetheless, a model trained exclusively on Track 1 synthetic data achieved a Dice

of 79.1%, only slightly below the baseline. The performance gap was primarily due
to reduced precision (69.0% vs. 72.9%), while recall remained comparable (97.5% vs.
97.8%). The limited benefit of Track 1 augmentation may stem from its condition-
ing on tumor-to-brain volume ratios matching the BraTS-MEN distribution, which
was not specifically tailored to small tumors (where improvements are most needed).
Although small lesion generation was possible, Track 1’s coarse conditioning often
resulted in tumor-free outputs for small input values, making it impractical and re-
quiring extensive resampling. This limitation directly motivated the development of
Track 2, which enables precise control over tumor sizes below 7.2 ml. While Track 2
was more effective for augmenting datasets with small tumor cases, it requires access
to real MRI scans. This dependency, however, could potentially be bypassed by using
the Track 1 pipeline to generate tumor-free synthetic brains as inputs to the Track 2
inpainting process.
The limited control in Track 1 contrasts with more reliable conditioning on larger,
consistent anatomical structures, such as the ventricular system shown in Ruschke et
al., 2024 and replicated in Appendix D. Small tumors are harder to condition on due
to their size and variability in location, making them more likely to be ignored during
denoising or lost during decoding. This issue is likely compounded by the LDM100K
pretraining set, which contains only healthy brains, biasing the model towards tumor-
free generation. One potential improvement would be registering BraTS tumor labels
to the pretraining set to incorporate tumor knowledge earlier in training.
Regarding architectural choices for the Track 2 inpainting diffusion model, we
found that a full 3D convolutional architecture outperformed the pseudo-3D approach.
This contrasts with the findings of Durrer et al., 2024, which favored pseudo-3D.
However, the models in Durrer et al., 2024 operated on full images and implemented
relatively lightweight 3D U-Nets with fewer feature channels than their pseudo-3D
counterparts. In our study, we limited the input to smaller context windows or patches
rather than full images, which allowed us to use deeper 3D U-Nets with more feature
channels. The reduced spatial context was compensated by conditioning the model
with semantic label map conditions. Therefore, it was not surprising that 3D models
performed better in our setup.
For synthetic label generation in Track 2, we proposed a heuristic random growth
algorithm to simulate tumor shapes. This method produced more irregular and mor-
phologically diverse labels compared to approaches using deformed ellipsoids, such as
in Hu et al., 2023. Our method thus avoided hand-crafted shape priors and prioritized
diversity, although the resulting boundaries were sometimes visually coarse. Alterna-
tively, we considered registering real BraTS tumor labels for use as synthetic labels,
but this would not increase shape diversity in a training set that already contains
BraTS data. We also explored diffusion-based inpainting of tumor masks directly into
SynthSeg maps (see Appendix C). However, this approach proved challenging, likely
due to operating in discrete label map space rather than the smoother latent space
used in Track 1. In the end, we thus opted for the simpler sampling method, as small
tumors often lack distinct morphologies and further efforts in diffusion-based label
generation seemed unnecessary.
A further advantage of the Track 2 pipeline is its ability to synthesize multi-
pathology scenarios by implanting meningiomas into scans that already contain other
pathologies or post-operative artifacts. We demonstrated this capability using a syn-
thetic test set of glioma patients with implanted meningiomas. Models trained with-

Returning to the Track 1 model, we found that our image generator could synthe-
size semantically consistent ”healthy twin” brain scans by reconstructing from Synth-
Seg label maps (not containing meningioma labels), enabling residual-based anomaly
detection. This approach achieved a Dice score of 50%, which improved slightly to
53% after fine-tuning on the RH-MS dataset that does not contain meningiomas. This
suggests that models trained on BraTS-MEN may carry tumor-related bias, some-
times generating meningioma-like tissue even without an explicit tumor label. This
may result from deformations in SynthSeg masks near tumor regions, which the model
may implicitly associate with tumors. A further limitation is that SynthSeg can mis-
label tumor tissue as cerebrospinal fluid due to their similar MRI intensities, leading
to low residual contrast in the anomaly maps (see Appendix G). Moreover, because
the healthy-twin reconstructions are derived from label maps rather than the original
MRI, they require intensity post-processing.
By contrast, diffusion-based methods
(such as DDIM sampling with classifier guidance on partially noised MRI images, as
proposed in Wolleb et al., 2022) achieve up to 70% Dice on BraTS-2020. Although
no anomaly-detection algorithm matches a fully supervised segmentation model (per-
forming at around 85% Dice), anomaly detection requires no manual labels and can
identify multiple lesion types simultaneously. Hence, although our image generator
(despite not being specifically trained for healthy-twin generation) underperformed, it
nonetheless points to an interesting direction for future work.
One limitation of our data generation pipelines is the use of 1.5 mm3 downsam-
pled MRI volumes, a choice driven by GPU memory constraints.
Alternatives to
downsampling include processing the images in patches or employing a coarse-to-fine
approach, in which a coarse MRI volume is first synthesized and then enhanced by a
super-resolution model. We did not adopt patch-based methods because they increase
inference time and may introduce stitching artifacts. We deferred super-resolution to
future work, as it represents a straightforward additional step. Nevertheless, improved
resolution will be desirable in future studies, since clinical MRI scans in the transverse
plane often achieve 0.5 mm2 in-plane resolution.
All segmentations produced by the baseline model from Bouget et al., 2021 were
generated on full-resolution MRI images and subsequently downsampled to match the
1.5,mm3 resolution of our synthetic data. This downsampling limits the validity of
direct comparisons between the baseline’s predictions and our model’s outputs. More-
over, our validation set was biased, as it used the baseline model’s own segmentations
as reference annotations and included only cases that the baseline segmented well.
For the final test set, expert annotations were used to refine the baseline’s predictions;
however, this process may have introduced bias, particularly in regions with greater
ambiguity.
Additionally, the supplementary test set of diﬀicult cases was selected

based on known failure cases of the baseline model, further introducing potential bias.
A more rigorous evaluation would require an independent test set annotated from
scratch by expert radiologists. Although we considered using a subset of BraTS-
MEN as a test set, we ultimately chose a fully external, clinically acquired dataset to
prioritize real-world variability. We thus accepted the trade-off of having potentially
biased ground-truth annotations.
Another limitation concerns the statistical assessment of performance differences
between models. In this report, we performed hyperparameter tuning and model eval-
uation using a single hold-out validation split. From a machine learning standpoint,
it would be preferable to retrain and evaluate models across multiple hold-out splits,
for example via cross-validation, to obtain a more robust estimate of performance
and reduce the influence of favorable or unfavorable data splits. However, implement-
ing such an approach would substantially increase training time and computational
demands, making it infeasible within our time constraints.
An additional challenge in the evaluation of generative models is the limited avail-
ability of reliable quantitative metrics. In this study, we employed the Fréchet In-
ception Distance (FID) and Kernel Inception Distance (KID) to assess visual fidelity,
and pairwise Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM) to quantify sample diver-
sity. However, these metrics are not always well-aligned with human visual perception
Barratt et al., 2018. Moreover, such metrics are primarily useful for relative model
comparisons rather than as absolute benchmarks of quality. Consequently, in Track 2,
we relied primarily on visual inspection for model selection, particularly focusing on
the blending of boundaries between real and implanted synthetic regions, an aspect
not easily captured by current quantitative metrics.
Finally, our models have been trained exclusively on contrast-enhanced BraTS-
MEN data, which limits their generalizability. Future work should incorporate more
diverse data sets, including local scans that exhibit multiple pathologies, post-operative
artifacts, and variability in scanner hardware and patient demographics. Based on our
experience fine-tuning Track 1 models from non-contrast to contrast-enhanced images,
we anticipate that straightforward transfer learning would enable generalization to
other MRI sequences, such as T1-weighted, T2-weighted, and FLAIR images.

Chapter6
Conclusion
This thesis presents a flexible, diffusion-based framework for addressing class imbal-
ance in medical image segmentation, with a particular emphasis on improving the
representation of small lesions.
By developing and evaluating two complementary
strategies, one based on fully synthetic image generation and the other on anatom-
ically guided inpainting, we demonstrate that generative models can be effectively
integrated into existing segmentation workflows without modifying the network archi-
tecture. Applied to the task of meningioma segmentation, these approaches improved
performance not only on underrepresented small tumors but also in more complex
scenarios involving multi-pathology cases.
While the two synthetic data pipelines
offer different strengths, with one enabling full dataset synthesis and the other allow-
ing precise lesion control within real anatomy, they both support the development
of more robust and generalizable segmentation models.
This is particularly useful
in settings with limited or imbalanced training data and may extend beyond their
immediate application to meningioma segmentation. Looking ahead, enhancing the
anatomical realism of generated lesions, supporting higher-resolution synthesis, and
validating on larger, clinically curated datasets will be important steps toward bridg-
ing the gap between research and real-world clinical applicability. Overall, this work
adds to the growing evidence that generative AI can play a valuable role in making
medical imaging models more adaptive, fair, and clinically ready.

AppendixD
Conditional Generation on Ventricular Volume
In early prototyping, our Track 1 pipeline did not reliably follow the tumor size con-
ditioning input. We suspected this was due to the diﬀiculty of conditioning on small
structures like meningiomas, which take up little space in the image and vary in lo-
cation. These features may not be well captured in the latent space, making ”small
tumor” hard to distinguish from ”no tumor.”
To investigate, we ran a sanity check by conditioning on a more prominent and
consistent anatomical feature: the ventricular-to-brain volume ratio.
This task is
known to be easier to learn (Ruschke et al., 2024) and served to validate both our
hypothesis and the implementation itself.
We changed only the conditioning input, using the pretrained model before any fine-
tuning. Results in Figure D.1 show a strong correlation between input and generated
ventricular volume, with much lower variability than tumor-based conditioning.
Figure D.2 shows representative samples across different conditioning values. The
model clearly adjusts ventricular size in line with the input.

AppendixE
Inpainting Experiments on SynthSeg Maps
Figure E.1 presents preliminary results on the feasibility of developing a DDPM for
direct mask inpainting into SynthSeg-derived multi-label maps.
While the model
showed some ability to inpaint masks, it also produced noticeable artifacts. Training
was challenging due to the discrete, one-hot encoded nature of the 33-class label space,
which proved more diﬀicult than diffusion models operating in continuous [−1,1] ranges
or latent spaces. Nonetheless, the results indicate some potential, which might be
unlocked with further hyperparameter tuning or extended training. Ultimately, we
did not pursue this direction further, as small tumors were unlikely to significantly
alter the predicted SynthSeg label map. Therefore, simply superimposing a tumor
label was deemed suﬀicient. Therefore, our heuristic-based label generator provided
a simpler and more practical solution for generating tumor labels, compared to the
potentially overcomplex training and inference process of this inpainting method.
Figure E.1
Four samples are shown from all three spatial views, displaying 64 × 64 × 64 patches (context windows for
the DDPM) extracted from full SynthSeg label maps. The central 32 × 32 × 32 region was masked out and
inpainted using a preliminary DDPM model with DDIM sampling and 200 inference steps. While the qualitative
results appear relatively promising, we observe hallucinations outside the brain, particularly in the third sample. In
the fourth sample, the model fails to capture the complexity in the core brain region. This suggests that applying
inpainting directly to SynthSeg label maps would require substantial additional effort in hyperparameter tuning
and model refinement.

AppendixG
Anomaly Detection: Tumor–CSF Confusion
Figure 4.15 shows four representative BraTS-MEN validation cases processed us-
ing our zero-shot anomaly detection pipeline (Track 1 image generator fine-tuned on
BraTS-MEN). The method’s effectiveness depends on whether SynthSeg mistakenly
labels tumor regions as cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Since meningiomas and CSF often
appear similar, such mislabeling leads the synthetic healthy twin to reconstruct tumor
areas as CSF, causing the anomaly map to overlook them.
