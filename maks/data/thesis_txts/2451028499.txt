ABSTRACT
Antimicrobial-resistant bacteria is becoming an emerging global threat. The increased dosage
of antibiotic in treatment of recurring infections, as observed in the lethal disease cystic fibrosis
(CF), and the absence of new antimicrobial agents are a major concern. Targeted delivery can be
a solution to decrease the concentration and the frequenct usage of antibiotics. Nanotechnology
offers drug microcarrier systems, which can overcome potential obstacles and deliver the drug
directly to the target.
In this project, the properties of three different polymers, poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA),
polyethylene glycol (PEG), and chitosan were analyzed. SU-8 microcontainers were utilized as
drug carrier and loaded with the antibiotic ciprofloxacin hydrochloride (CIP). The release profile
of CIP were measured with the polymers either as co-loaded in a 50:50 w/w ratio, and as a coating
on top of the microcontainers and CIP. PLGA coating offered a slow release, while PEG and chi-
tosan offered a fast release. All three mixtures of polymer and CIP displayed a relative fast release.
Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa) were grown overnight in a microtiter plate to create a
biofilm and simulate the bacterial environment in CF airways. This setup was used to test PLGA
ability to reduce the local pH, thus enhancing the bactericidal effect of CIP, and to test PEG and
chitosan abilities to exhibit antimicrobial effects. The subsequent bacterial disintegration studies
were analyzed on an optical screening system called oCelloScope. None of the polymer coatings
provided a significant beneficial effect to CIP after 20 hours. PLGA and PEG showed a potential
of improved bactericidal effect when co-loaded with CIP and Chitosan showed no effect. PLGA in
a powder form without CIP provided no bactericidal inhibition, however PEG and chitosan in a
powder form without CIP showed a potential inhibitory effect against P. aeruginosa.
A segmentation program has been developed to distinguish the polymers even further. The tool
provide a better insight of bacterial disintegration. although, it was not applicable in these experi-
ments.

1 INTRODUCTION
The expanding threat from antimicrobial-resistant bacteria and increasing transmission across in-
ternational borders are becoming an emerging global health issue [1]. These trends are a combina-
tion of an extensive and increasing use of antimicrobial agents, increasing dosages for areas more
difficult to reach like the pulmonary system, and the paucity of new antimicrobial agents [2,3]. A
targeted antimicrobial treatment to lower the concentration of antibiotic is a significant prognostic
factor, particularly for seriously ill patients [4]. A solution for targeted delivery can be found in the
field of nanotechnology. Carrier systems consisting of polymer or lipid based nanoparticles can
deliver antibiotics, while simultaneous protecting the drug from the environment [5].
1.1 Cystic ﬁbrosis
Cystic fibrosis (CF) is a lethal, autosomal, recessive, and multisystemic disease. 70,000 - 100,000
people are estimated to be suffering from CF worldwide, with the highest prevalence in Caucasian
countries (Figure 1.1). The current median life expectancy of CF patients is 30-40 years. However,
recent advantages in the field have lead to better knowledge, diagnostics, and treatment of the dis-
ease. This means that newborns with CF are now anticipated to have a life expectancy of over 50
years [6].
Figure 1.1 – The distribution of cystic fibrosis occurrences in the world in 2017 [7].
CF is caused by a mutation in a gene that encodes the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance
regulator (CFTR) protein. This protein is mostly present at the apical membrane of the respiratory,
digestive, and reproductive epithelium [8], which is why CF affects multiple organs. Of these, the
most predominant organs are the lungs, the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, the pancreas, and the liver.
The CFTR protein is an anion channel and is responsible of transporting of chloride, bicarbon-
ate and sodium across the cell membrane. At least 2000 different mutations have been identified,
although not all are CF-causing and most of them are rare [9].

1.1.1 Respiratory system
The respiratory system is continuously exposed to infectious agents during normal inhalation. The
epithelium layer is a critical component of the lungs’ innate immune defence. It consists of ciliated
cells, secretory cells, and a chemical barrier which comprises of different mucin and antimicrobial
proteins [8]. The mucus layer can be divided into a low viscosity gel on the bottom and a high vis-
cosity gel on top, serving different functions. Most inhaled particles and pathogens will get trapped
in the high viscosity layer, where the cilia are able to move the low viscosity layer beneath. This
drives the whole mucus layer towards the larynx, where it can be cleared through the stomach or
by coughing out the phlegm [8].
The CFTR channel plays a vital role in regulating the hydration of the low viscosity layer through
an osmotic gradient. The impaired CFTR channel from CF patients reduces the low viscosity layer
drastically leaving the high viscosity layer closer to the cilia, resulting in a collapse of these (Fig-
ure 1.2). This leads to a flawed mucus clearance from the airways. The deficiency of an innate
immune defence will lead to a favorable environment for opportunistic pathogens such as Pseu-
domonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa). Furthermore, recurrent and persistent bacterial infection will
inevitable lead to neutrophilic inflammation and tissue damage. The alternation between infection
and inflammation eventually leads to bronchiectasis, reduced gas exchange, and respiratory fail-
ure. Respiratory problems and irreversible lung damage are some of the key problems regarding
CF casuing 90 % of deaths in CF patients [8,9].
Figure 1.2 – The Cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator channel plays a vital
role in regulating airway surface hydration through the osmotic gradient by transporting
chloride and sodium. An abnormal CFTR protein weakens the osmotic gradient in the fluid
and reduces the volume of the low viscosity layer. The mucus layer draws closer to the ep-
ithelial cells and collapses the cilia. This results in a reduced mucus clearance of pathogens,
among other things [9].

1.1.2 Mucus
Mucus is produced by goblet cells and submucosal glands. It is an adhesive and heterogeneous
gel that hydrates the airway and protects it by trapping bacteria and particulates. It consists pre-
dominantly of two mucins (MUC5AC and MUC5B) [10], which are scarce in CF airways [11,12].
It should be taken into consideration in mucolytic therapies, that the mucus in CF patient only
contain a scarce amount of mucins. The major components in the mucus are polymerized DNA
and filamentous (F-)actin due to neutrophil degradation (Figure 1.3) [13,14]. This combination of
DNA and actin also enhances the formation of bacterial biofilm [15]. The combination also make
the mucus more viscous, which characterizes CF sputum, but it can be observed in other respira-
tory diseases like bronchiectasis [16].
Currently, only a few drugs have been accepted for mucolytic treatment in CF and although these
are meant to ease the lives of patients, the viscous phlegm is a recurrent problem when patients are
off the medicine [17].
Figure 1.3 – The difference between a healthy lung and an inflamed cystic fibrosis lung. The
neutrophils and the DNA infuse the thick mucus with more stickiness and thickness, re-
stricting airflow through the airways [18].
1.2 Antibiotics
Antibiotics are the drug that is most often used in connection with the treatment of bacterial res-
piratory infections. They can be used for prophylactic, eradication of early infections, attenuation
of chronic infections, and if there is an acute exacerbation [19]. The type of antibiotics depends
on the type bacteria. As seen in Figure 1.4, the most common bacteria in early childhood of CF
patients is Haemophilus influenzae and partly Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus). In the adolescence
and adulthood the most prevalent bacteria is P. aeruginosa [20].

==PAGE:12==
Figure 1.4 – Abundance of bacterial strains according to age. The figure shows that infants
and young children (0-11 years) have a higher abounds of Haemophilus influenza compared
to any other strain included in this figure. However, when the patients are at the adolescence
age, S. aureus and P. aeruginosa dominate the lungs, where P. aeruginosa is the most aboun-
dant [20].
The most common prophylactic treatment of CF patients in Europe is a type of antibiotics known
as β-lactams, which is only used to reduce the prevalence of S. aureus and to prevent an infection
with a secondary bacteria [21]. Early eradication of especially P. aeruginosa is in focus in order to
try to avoid or decrease the chance of a chronic infection. Although, a complete eradication is only
achieved approximately 80 % of the time [21].
When an infection becomes chronic, the aim often switches from eradicating the bacteria to sup-
pressing the bacteria with the purpose of mitigating the inflammatory response. Antibiotics are
administered intravenous (IV) or inhaled. The latter is recommended for better patient compli-
ance and will simultaneously improve lung functionality [22]. However, this method will result in
a prolonged systemic antibiotic treatment, which will increase the risk of bacterial resistance and
antibiotic hypersensitivity reactions in the patients [23–25]. The acute exacerbation is most often
treated with a synergic combination of antibiotic administered IV. This reduces the risk of bacterial
resistance [21].
1.2.1 Ciprofloxacin
Ciprofloxacin is an antibiotic which is commonly used against P. aeruginosa [21]. As seen in
Figure 1.4, P. aeruginosa is the most prevalent bacteria in CF patients at the age of 11 or older [20].
Therefore, for this age group, ciprofloxacin seems to be the best choice of antibiotics. It has low oil
and water solubility, which results in slow permeation to lipoidal cell membranes. Increasing the
solubility leads to increased permeation and subsequently enhanced antibacterial efficacy [26].
Page 4 of 62
==PAGE:13==
Figure 1.5 – Chemical structure of ciprofloxacin [27]
Ciprofloxacin is a fluoroquinolone, which is effective against gram-positive as well as gram-negative
bacteria [28]. Fluoroquinolones are characterized by the fluorine atom in their chemical structure
(Figure 1.5), which greatly increases the potency of the drug [28]. The mechanism of action is
inhibition of the DNA gyrase (topoisomerase II) and topoisomerase IV. Both topoisomerase are
similar in structure, but they have different functions [29, 30]. DNA gyrase is an enzyme that
reduces the topological strain of double-stranded DNA during replication, by introducing negative
supercoiling which favors strand separation and DNA replication [31].
Topoisomerase IV is
likewise an enzyme and it allows the two newly formed inter-linked chromosomes to be separated
so they can be segregated into two new bacterial cells. This process is also called decatenation [32].
Fluoroquinolones, including ciprofloxacin, bind to the complex of DNA and the topoisomerases
causing a conformational change of the enzyme. This results in a breakdown of DNA and the
bacteria will therefore be unable to replicate and hence die [30]. An illustration of the mechanism
of action is shown in Figure 1.6.

1.3 Bioﬁlm
Biofilm is an intricate community of cells attached to a surface and encased in an extracellular ma-
trix, consisting of polysaccharides, nucleic acids, proteins, and cellular debris [33,34].
Figure 1.7 shows an overview of a biofilm formation cycle. The first step when forming biofilm
is when a bacterium undergoes transition from a planktonic stage to irreversible attachment to a
surface or substratum. When enough bacteria are clustered together, they start to secrete polysac-
charides (exopolysaccharides) and the maturation of the biofilm begins. The architecture of the
biofilm varies from flat biofilm to mushroom-shaped biofilm filled with cells encased in the matrix.
The architecture determines the distribution of chemical gradients and protects the bacteria against
the surrounding environment, including antimirobial agents [33–35]. The bacteria are capable of
leaving the biofilm, if e.g. the nutrients become scarce or the population becomes too crowded. It
can either happen by a separation of matrix containing bacteria or the bacteria can become mo-
bile and tunnel out of the matrix. This process enables the bacteria to colonize new surfaces and
reinitiate the biofilm process. Initiation, development, and dispersion of the biofilm are regulated
through quorum sensing (QS) [35,36].
In CF patients the polysaccharide in the biofilm is alginate produced by P. aeruginosa [21]. P. aerug-
inosa produces a large mushroom-shaped biofilm which will alter the metabolism of P. aeruginosa,
since an oxygen deficit develops, thus creating an anaerobic environment. This can create problems
Page 6 of 62
==PAGE:15==
for antibiotic treatments, for example the transport of tobramycin into the cell relies on oxidative
metabolism. This neutralizes the detrimental effects from the antibiotics [37].
Figure 1.7 – The formation of a bacterial biofilm. The planktonic bacteria undergoes a
transition from free flowing to an irreversible attachment to a surface. They create a ma-
trix consisting of exopolysaccharides, which encompass the bacteria, and the maturation of
the biofilm begins. At the saturation point, the bacteria can become motile and leave the
biofilm, and the cycle can start anew [38].
1.3.1 Pseudomonas aeruginosa
P. aeruginosa is an encapsulated, rod-shaped, gram-negative bacterium. It is an opportunistic
pathogen, which is found ubiquitously in nature.
It can grow in both anaerobic and aerobic
environments, as it is a facultative anaerobe. It uses QS to coordinate the behavior within the
population and it is intrinsically resistant to multiple antimicrobials, partly due to numerous efflux
systems, which can pump the antibiotic out of the cell. It is not excessively virulent, although it is
capable of comprehensive colonization and can aggregate into biofilms, which further contributes
to its antimicrobial resistance [33,39].
P. aeruginosa reacts to a hypoxic condition by increasing the alginate production, which fortifies
the physical barrier to charged antibiotics [40].
The plethora of different CTFR mutations and different bacterial flora in the airways makes it diffi-
cult to create an in vitro test that does mimic the way a patient would respond [21].
PAO was isolated in Australia 65 years ago. Its derivative PAO1 is currently the most commonly
used reference strain for P. aeruginosa. The PAO1 genome has been completely sequenced and it is
only resistant to chloramphenicol. PAO1 is therefore the obvious choice for easier reproducibility of
research. However, a slight diversity between the laboratory strains of PAO1 exits. This microevo-

1.4 Microcontainer
The human body has a lot of physical and chemical barriers, that can degrade or hinder a poten-
tial drug from reaching its target destination. This is a problem that can potentially be mitigated
with the use of microdevices, which serves as a delivery vesicle. Microfabrication of these devices
create the ability to produce and alter the drug carrier into having the perfect shape and size and
to induce a controlled release when the target destination is reached. Thereby, overcoming the ob-
stacles throughout the body, such as in the gastrointestinal tract or the pulmonary system [42–44].
Microcontainers are an example of such a microdevice. They are polymer-based cylindrical shaped
devices with a cavity capable of protecting a drug from the outside environment [45] (Figure 1.8).
These microcontainers offer an advantage of unidirectional release, which can potentially secure a
more controlled release of the drug, an increase in the local drug concentration, and avoids a po-
tential luminal drug loss [46].
The microcontainers can be created using SU-8. It is an epoxy resin-based polymer, which com-
prises of polycarbonate, cyclopentenone, and a photo acid initiator such as triarylsulfonium hex-
afluoroantimonate [47]. SU-8 has stable mechanical, thermal and chemical properties, which is
favorable during production [48]. It can therefore provide molecules protection against pH depen-
dent changes and enzymatic changes [45,49]. It is important to keep in mind that microcontainers
still have to be biodegradable to ensure excretion, so they do not accumulate in the body.
Figure 1.8 – SEM image of a single SU-8 microcontainer [50].
1.4.1 Microcontainer filling
There are different methods to load the SU-8 microcontainers with a drug, such as inkjet print, su-
percritical impregnation and powder embossing (Figure 1.9) [49,51,52]. Inkjet printing has been
proven successful in dispensing precise volumes, but can be very time consuming [53]. Another
problem is drug precipitation and the nozzle resulting in clogging. It is therefore necessary to not
Page 8 of 62
==PAGE:17==
have any water insoluble compounds in an aqueous solution [54]. Impregnation offers accurate
and reproducible loading, but is not possible with all drugs, as it requires a solubility of the drug in
a polymer matrix [46,55].
In powder embossing, a shadow mask is aligned to fit between the microcontainers on a chip to
minimize residual powder between the microcontainers. This is followed by embossing of the pow-
der into the cavities of the microcontainers [52]. A similar method is by using a stencil to cover the
area between the microcontainers, followed by manually pressing the powder into the cavities with
a brush or similar tool. The flaw in this method is a considerable waste of drug and it is suboptimal
for viscid powders [52,56]. The best suited method is one which has a homogeneous loading with
minimal waste. This depends on the properties of the drug and any potential excipients [52,55].
Figure 1.9 – A representative of a selection of different methods for loading microcontainers
with a drug [57]
1.4.2 Spray coating of microcontainers
The microcontainer protects its content from the outside environment, except from the cavity of
the microcontainer where the drug is vulnerable to the external surroundings. This problem can be
solved by sealing the microcontainer with a lid [57]. The lid also functions as an ancillary approach
to add functionalities and excipients by using a functional polymer or similar substance [58].
This provides a more controlled release of the drug, as the coating can be time-, pH, or enzyme
dependent and release the drug at a specific region [56]. Additionally the coating can enhance the
absorption rate and prolong the drug efficacy [45].
Different coating techniques can be utilized.
One method applies the coating solution man-
ually to the sample by dipping the sample into the solution [59]. Another method applies the
coating in a stirring coating pan [60]. However, these methods do not provide a uniform distribu-
Page 9 of 62
==PAGE:18==
tion of the coating layers. Alternatively, spray coating provides a good deposition of polymer on
both planar or non-planar surfaces [61]. The polymer solution is pumped and nebulized through
a small orifice, while a piezoelectric transducer produces mechanical vibrations inside the spray
nozzle [62–64]. The subsequent small droplets of the solution are aimed and dropped towards
the targeted substrate (Figure 1.10). The small droplets coalesce with the substrate. The substrate
is usually placed on a heating plate where the temperature can be controlled to adjust solvent
evaporation rate. Spray coating offers a customizable technique with multiple parameters, such as
speed and area coverage, which can be modified to meet the requirement and the properties of
the solution. Additionally, it offers a good reproducibility, precise conformal coatings and good
transfer efficiency between the nozzle and the target [64].
Figure 1.10 – A schematic overview of the spray coating nozzle. The polymer solution is ac-
tuated by applying compressed air and ultrasound to the spray nozzle. The polymer solution
is pumped out of the small orifice at the bottom and enforced to the focus region [65].
1.5 Polymers
Polymers have been extensively studied for biomedical applications. They have an essential role
in the advancement of drug delivery systems, tissue engineering scaffold, and cell culture sup-
ports [66–68]. Polymers have certain characteristics when used as carriers or in conjugation with a
drug that may provide a variety of possibilities, such as Controlled drug release, enhanced drug sol-
ubility, minimized drug degradation, reduction of drug toxicity, targeted delivery, and improved
drug efficacy. However, the most desirable traits are low toxicity, biodegadability, as well as bio-
compatibility [67,69]. There are naturally derived polymers like starch, chitosan, alginate, and col-
lagen, and there are synthetic polymers such as poly(akrylcyanoacrylates), poly(anhydrides), and
polyesters [70].

==PAGE:19==
1.5.1 Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid)
Poly(lactic acid) (PLA), poly(glycolic acid)(PGA) and in combination, their corresponding
copolymer poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid)(PLGA) are some of the most studied synthetic aliphatic
polyesters.
This means that the properties, formulations, and production methods are well
described [71]. They are biodegradable, biocompatible, and approved by both the Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) and the European Medicines Agency (EMA). One of the main applications
of PLGA is to protect the drug from the environment in the human body. The drug can be
hydrophillic or hydrophobic small molecules as well as macromolecules [72].
PLGA is degraded by hydrolysis cleavage of the ester links into small oligomers and then further
into two monomers, PGA and PLA (Figure 1.11). These two monomers are further metabolized in
the body and eliminated as carbon dioxide and water [73]. The life-span of PLGA depends on the
ratio of PGA and PLA, where PGA is more hydrophillic than PLA. A higher ratio of PGA reduces
the hydrophobicity and attracts more water, resulting in a faster degradation. However, it has been
proven that a 50:50 ratio has a faster degradation time than a higher ratio of PGA, unless its a 100
% PGA [73,74]. The intermediate degradation products may reduce the pH locally, which in turn
can induce an inflammatory response from the immune system. Furthermore, it may accelerate
the degradation rate of the polymer, resulting in premature loss of properties [75,76].
Studies have shown that PLGA displays promising properties in CF environment, as it can
penetrate the negatively charged, compact mucus [77]. Additionally, PLGA was shown to be safe
against CF bronchial epithelial cells in vitro [5]. When some antibiotics are incorporated in the
polymer, PLGA shows either an enhanced antibacterial effect or equally effective as the antibiotic
alone in a P. aeruginosa biofilm [77,78].

1.5.2 Polyethylene glycol
Polyethylene glycol (PEG) is another FDA approved polymer and is one of the most utilized
polymers in drug delivery. It is non-biodegradable and relatively chemical inert. Under normal
conditions it is uncharged and amphiphilic and can therefore be dissolved in both water and
organic solvents [79]. PEG can either be branched or linear (Figure 1.12) and is therefore available
in multiple molecular weights (MW) [80]. The hydrophilic and flexible nature that PEG can obtain,
benefits the stealth properties of PEG. This makes any compound conjugated to PEG (PEGylated)
more invisible to the body’s own clearance of particles. This happens through steric hindrance
and less van der Waals, electrostatic, ionic, interactions, which prolongs circulation time of the
molecule [79,81,82].
The same stealth attributes with few intermolecular interactions are applicable in the mucus.
PEG can effectively penetrate and diffuse into the mucus layer and accumulate on the epithelial
surface [83]. A high density of PEG on the surface and a low MW increases the time for penetra-
tion through the mucus. Whereas a higher MW shifts the properties from mucopenetrative to
mucoadhesive. The higher MW of PEG has longer chains, which increase the sum of hydrogen
bonding with the mucus layer [84,85]. A dense coating of low MW PEG shows the same properties,
with an increases penetration rate in CF sputum [86].
A study has shown that PEG conjugated to an antibiotic showed a decrease in efficiency against
planktonic P. aeruginosa, but was more effective against bacterial biofilm compared to the
antibiotic alone [87].

1.5.3 Chitin and chitosan
Chitin, poly(β-(1→4)-N-acetyl-D-glucosamine) (Figure 1.13) is a natural polysaccharide.
It
is synthesized by a vast amount of organisms and is the most abundant polymer in the world
after cellulose. The two primary sources are from crustaceans, shrimp and crab shells. Chitin is
called chitosan when around 50 % of its acytel groups are removed (deacetylated) in an acidic
aqueous medium, as it is insoluble at neutral and alkaline pH values [88]. Chitosan is a copolymer
comprising of glucosamine and N-acetylglucosamine (Figure 1.13) and is therefore accessible in
multiple MWs and degree of deacetylation (40-98 %). It is regarded as non-toxic, biocompatible
and slowly biodegradable and is approved by FDA and EMA as a food excipient. Chitosan has
the ability to swell through imbibition of water. This allows for a controlled slow drug release,
which can increase the bioavailability of a drug in e.g. the GI tract [89, 90]. In the swollen and
protonated state, chitosan exhibits mucoadhesive properties as the positive surface charge forms
a complex through ionic and hydrogen bonding with the negatively charged mucus [89]. The
protonated form also has the ability to open epithelial tight junctions. This facilitates the transport
of hydrophilic macromolecules through the epithelial cell layer [91]. Additionally, the positive
charge assists in keeping an anti-biofilm activity in gram-positive bacteria when it is conjugated
with antibiotics.
The chitosan conjugation also makes the antibiotics more accessible in the
bacterial biofilm, making the antibiotic more effective towards the bacteria [92].

1.6 Scanning electron microscope
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) can visualize objects down to the micrometer range. An elec-
tron beam is emitted from an electron gun with an energy ranging from 1-30 kV. The beam is
focused by condenser plates before it passes through a pair of coils and an aperture and scans the
surface in a raster fashion. The atoms at the surface of the specimen are affected by the electron
beam resulting in an emission of secondary electrons. These secondary electrons and reflected or
backscattered electrons from the beam itself can be detected and mapped. This provides a high
resolution image of the specimen. The quality and resolution can be modified changing the voltage
of the beam and therefore which electrons that are detected [93]. The high quality images enable
the study of morphology of both loading and coating of microcontainers [94].
1.7 Detection method of bioﬁlm
1.7.1 oCelloScope
The oCelloScope™(BioSense Solutions ApS, Farum, Denmark) is a high-throughput optical detec-
tion system (Figure 1.14A). It offers the same accuracy as standard optical density (OD) measure-
ment, which is used when measuring the concentration of bacteria [95]. In addition, it is less labor
intensive and time-consuming compared to the conventional microscopy [96].
The oCelloScope can be utilized in several applications for real-time cell monitoring such as cell
morphology, cell migration and proliferation, spore germentation, antimicrobial susceptibility, and
growth kinetics [95–97].
The oCelloScope detection system generates a series of images by scanning through the fluid sam-
ple, with a digital time-lapse microscopy. The system taking images consists of a lens, a digital
Page 14 of 62
==PAGE:23==
camera unit, and an illumination unit. The optical axis of the imaging system has an incline of 6.25
° relative to the horizontal plane (Figure 1.14B), which facilitates the scanning of different volumes
and extracts phase information [95]. The phase information aids in recognizing structures in the
different images and gives a 3D effect. The 5-megapixel camera and the lens are designed to abso-
lute magnification factor of X4, a focus depth of approximately 10 µm and an optical resolution of
1.3 µm, which is the same as a x200 magnification in a standard light microscope. The recorded
images form a stack. Image processing and digital analysis from any time-lapse experiments are
conducted automatically in MATLAB integrated in the oCelloScope [95].
Background corrected absorption (BCA) and the total absorption (TA) are two algorithms used
in the growth kinetic analyses. The whole acquired image stack is analyzed and they are based on
light absorption, which are principally the same as OD measurement. The BCA algorithm has a
higher sensitivity compared to OD measurements, even at high or low cell concentrations. It cor-
rects the background intensities compared to the first time point images. A threshold is calculated
and allocates pixels into ”objects” and ”background”. The growth curves are then generated based
on ”objects”, diminishing the effects from the background [98]. The TA algorithm is similar to
standard OD measurement. However, TA is less sensitive compared to BCA since the background
is not deducted. Another algorithm used by the grow kinetic software is segmentation and extrac-
tion of surface area (SESA). This algorithm identifies all ”objects” based on their contrast to the
background and calculates the surface area covered by the objects. It does not use light absorbance,
mitigating any shadow effects from e.g. air bubbles in the well and condensation on the microtiter
plate lid. It has high accuracy at low cell concentration, but the accuracy starts to decline when
more than 20 % of the image is covered by objects [98]. The segmentation and extraction of aver-
age length (SEAL) are similar to SESA, but is more specifically designed to detect filamentation of
rod shaped bacteria. It determines the average length of the filamentation based on the ”objects” in
the image. The SEAL algorithm is inaccurate when cells or filaments are overlapping. Both SESA
and SEAL only analyze the best focus images [98].

1.8.1 Microdissolution
Microdissolution is a method to investigate drug release of a compound through spectrophotomet-
ric analysis. Most analyes have an absorbance in in the UV range of 200-720 nm. Microdissolution
measure the drug release with optical probes, which detects the light absorbed by the analytes [99].
Microdissolution is valuable for solubility, release and dissolution profiles. A UV probe with a
mirror attached is submerged into a temperature and stirring controlled medium, where the sam-
ples are present, and the probe is emitting light. The irradiance of the light beam decreases when
the concentration of the analyte increases. The absorbance is proportional with the concentration
and can thus be calculated.

5.1 Fabrication of microcontainers
The microcontainers were fabricated with a similar approach as outlined by Tao et al. [101]. The
material is spin coated onto a polished silicon wafer, which can have an additional layer consisting
of teflon or titanium-gold for easier removal of microcontainers after the fabrication [101, 102].
The microcontainers are created by using photolithography. This means that A light will transfer
a pattern from an optical mask to the SU-8 photoresist, which creates the desired pattern in two
steps on the surface (Figure 5.1). This pattern can modified by size and shape to fit the coveted
need [50,101]. The finished wafers with microcontainers were cut into squares and distributed by

5.2 Filling procedure
The loading procedure of both the CIP and the different polymers were the same. The chips were
weighted prior to loading in order to determine the quantity of substrate in the microcontainers. A
distinctive shadow mask that fits between the microcontainers on a chip was aligned with the chip,
which reduces the spillage. The powdered substrate was gently pressed into the microcontainers
through the holes in the shadow mask (Figure 5.2). This was repeated a few times for maximum
loading of the substrate before the removal of the shadow mask. Any excess residues between the
microcontainers were removed and the loaded chip was assessed by SEM. If a microcontainer was
lost during the process, the weight was deducted for a more precise calculation of the drug load.
PEG, PLGA, and chitosan had large particle sizes and were therefore ground down manually with
a mortar and pestle prior to loading. All of the co-loadings were carried out in a 50:50 w/w ratio.

6.2 Spray coating
One of the aims of this project was to analyze the impact of the polymers, PEG, PLGA, and
chitosan as a coating material on microcontainers containing CIP, on bacterial growth, compared
to a physical mixture. Two thicknesses of each polymer were evaluated to determine the best
coverage of the microcontainers.
As seen in Figure 6.1, the crystal structure of CIP can be relative large. This could be an issue, as
the uniformity of the content of the microcontainer provides a foundation for the spray coating, as
this uniformity further enables a uniform polymer coating. On the contrary, a non-planar loading
may skew the coating, which results in a differential release from the microcontainers.
SEM images provided a visual assessment on the uniformity, coverage, and morphology of each
coating. It was the only tool used to determine which coating that should proceed to release studies
and testing on the oCelloScope.

Lb medium is not an optimal choice when testing a drug with a drug which absorbs light at at
the UV-range of 200-300 nm, since LB has close to a 100 % light absorption at that range (data
not shown). Neither can LB be used with a non-antibiotic drug. The work environment is mostly
non-sterile and the 37 °C LB medium with rotation is the ideal condition for bacterial growth.

6.4 Further discussion
The considerable difference between the release of PLGA and PEG coating is shown in Figure 6.12.
Both coatings offer a situational release profile. PEG coating offers a rapid release. A burst release
carries the risk of local or temporal drug overdose, which may be toxic for the patient. In addition, it
is economically inefficient if the drug is released before reaching the targeted destination [108,109].
Although, it may be beneficial when the target is ever-changing like the constant flow of mucus.
Here it is necessary for a release before the microcontainer or drug vesicle is secreted or carried
away from the target. A burst release may also be essential when treating infections. PLGA coat-
ing retards the release of CIP and offers on the other hand a slower controlled release. This is
usually a desired trait, which may lead to a prolonged pharmaceutical effect and an enhance drug
absorption [110]. It may be a problem when treating an infection if the release is below the mini-
mal inhibitory concentration (MIC) or minimal biofilm eradication concentration (MBEC) [111].
Both of the coatings ends around a 100 % release of CIP, but there is still a significant difference
between PLGA and PEG after 18 hours (P = 0.004).
The release profile with the chitosan coating was excluded since the results were invalid. The reason
could be due to the liquid pressure on the lids on top of the SU-8 microcontainers. When chitosan
swells, the liquid flows beneath the lid and pushes the lid off of the microcontainer. This may be
enhanced if chitosan has a weak affinity to SU-8 and it is therefore easily detached from the micro-
containers. Another reason could be if chitosan is being protonated be either CIP or a component
in the LB medium. A study has demonstrated that protonated chitosan is soluble in a non acidic
saline solution [112]. The LB medium did contain a relative high salt concentration (10 g/L NaCl),
Page 35 of 62
==PAGE:44==
and a post-release pH measurement could had established if the pH range was in the acidic part.

6.5 oCelloScope
After the scrutinization of the loaded and coated microcontainers, it was time to evaluate the
effect on bacteria. Conventional biofilm flow-cell systems combined with confocal laser scanning
microscopy usually takes several days to setup, sterilize, inoculate and waiting for a maturation
of biofilm [113]. It is very sensitive for air bubbles, since there is a constant flow and the air
bubbles can damage the structures in the biofilm.
The oCelloScope on the contrary offers a
relative high-throughput, non-invasive, real-time monitoring of microbial growth and inhibition.
In addition, the oCelloScope software generates time-lapse videos of the acquired images. This
could potentially make it easier to account for any unexpected results and it is faster and easier
to make adjustments compared to the flow-cell systems. One of the aims for this study was to
determine the oCelloScopes capability to screen for planktonic bacteria as well as surface adhering
bacterial clusters. This could potentially result in a high-throughput testing to assess new ideas
and combination in the combat against bacterial biofilm before being applied to the more time
consuming flow-cell and a 4-day maturation of a biofilm.
The oCelloScope offer a few algorithm to measure bacterial growth/inhibition. The SEAL algo-
rithm would be an evident choice since P. aeruginosa is a rod shaped bacteria, and this algorithm
is specifically designed to detect these.
However, the SEAL algorithm is progressively more
inaccurate when the cell concentration increases [98] and is therefore not an applicable choice.
The high cell concentration is also the reason why the TA and the SESA algorithm is not a choice.
The SESA algorithms accuracy is decreasing when more than 20 % of the image is covered [98],
and it is expected to have a 100 % coverage in at the initiation of the measurements. This leave
Page 36 of 62
==PAGE:45==
the BCA algorithm to be the only choice. It also offers the ideal qualities since it performs well
at high concentrations and it uses the background from the first image as a point of origin, thus
generating growth/inhibition curves based in changes compared to the first image. The BCA and
TA algorithms have been validated with conventional OD measurements by Fredborg et al. ( [95]).
The term ”growth inhibition” is a bit misleading, since the bacteria has been grown O/N to a
plateau state without any antibiotic added. The CIP loaded microcontainers is added afterwards
without any addition of new LB medium. All of the experiments is measured at room temperature
without agitation, leaving the bacteria in tough conditions.
Consequently, the growth curves
generated will be referred to as disintegration curves, since the bacterial growth inhibition is
minimal and it is more a disruption in the bacterial clusters.
6.6 Optimization of oCelloScope methods
Prior to any comprehensive experiments, it was necessary to be acquainted with the oCelloScope
and to figure out the most efficient way to do the disintegration experiments. The tilted detection
system was could give a 3D image of a depth of approximately 50 µmL in a 96 well microtiter plate.
Anything above this threshold could result in shadows in the images and irregularities in growth
kinetics. When working with the small volumes in microtiter plate parafilm is paramount, since
they dry out otherwise. There were also a visible difference when using row A and H, coloumn 1
and 12 in comparison with the rest of the rows and coloumns (data not shown), which could be due
to a more accessibility to oxygen. These were accordingly avoided. The oCelloScope had a initial
focus point, which could be different for each experiment. In order for easier repeatability, this was
modified. The length of the setup were tested up to 48 hours. This increased the risk of evaporation
and condensation on the lid. In addition, each disintegration curve plateaued out, resulting in an
insignificant variation between 20 hours and 48 hours. A 5 minute interval during the first hour
was also tested to verify that nothing important happened there. 1 hour interval demonstrated the
best results and simultaneously keeping the data quantity low. The number of images taken at each
interval were experimented with 3 - 50 images per interval. There was not any visible difference in
the growth kinetic. However, a more images results in a higher resolution and a bigger picture in
focus, which is crucial during segmentation.
The illumination experiment was necessary due to the fact that the images gets lighter over the span
of 20 hours. A bright illumination at start, results in overexposed images and likewise underexposed
images of the illumination was to dark (Figure 6.13). The optimal setting was found to be between
290 - 310.

6.7.4 Disintegration curves further discussion
Figure 6.18 displays the three different polymer coatings together. It is shown that chitosan (−▲−)
and PLGA (−●−) exhibit the same slower release compared to PEG. Although, if the release was to
slow, so the concentration of CIP was below the MIC, then there would be an enhanced of biofilm
formation instead of an inhibition [118]. The optimal concentration would be above MBEC and
MIC and below the toxicity limit [111]. However, there were no instances of enhancement of biofilm
observed in any of experiments. There were no significant difference between any of the coatings
after 20 hours.

The reason why a relative slow cell lysis in all of the oCelloScope disintegration curves with CIP
were observed, could be due to that ciprofloxacin prevents successful DNA replication in bacteria.
It takes a longer time for the cell to die compared if the antibiotic targeted and disrupted the cell
wall instead [119]. This may also had been the reason why there was not observed any substantial
difference between the polymers and CIP with a negative BCA value in the taken images.
Only a few images had inhibition zone around the microcontainer(Appendix A.5). It appears to be
when the microcontainer was upside down and stuck at the bottom on the biofilm. This way CIP
was released directly into the biofilm at a slower pace, which in return resulted in an inhibition
zone.
The bacteria in these experiments were easily accessible, and there were only observed the antimi-
crobial effect of CIP together with the different polymers. If the biofilm had been surrounded

There were a few source of errors which could affect the outcome of each individual iteration. When

7 CONCLUSION
Pulmonary administration of nanoformulations with a targeted drug delivery and a high drug load-
ing are in a rapid development and a promising tool in treatment for CF patients and in the combat
against antibiotic resistance. The SU-8 microcontainers loaded with CIP and afterwards separately
either coated or loaded with the three polymers, PLGA, PEG and chitosan, offered each an indi-
vidual release profile with different surface properties, with a potential of mucus penetration or
adhesion to the thick CF mucus.
PLGA showed a slow controlled release of CIP when applied as a coating, but with a more rapid re-
lease when co-loaded with CIP. The polymer in itself exhibited no antimicrobial properties against
P. aeruginosa. PLGA as a coating did not have an increased antimicrobial after 20 hours based on
this study. However, it appeared to have a low degradation rate in the LB medium. As the co-loaded
PLGA showed a slight potential of an improved bactericidal effect.
PEG offered an enhanced rapid release of CIP, both as a coating and as a co-loading. The coating
showed no significant increase against bacteria. The co-loading seemed to have an improved bacte-
ricidal effect. In addition, PEG alone may impede the bacterial growth and provided antimicrobial
activities to a certain degree.
The co-loading with chitosan showed a similar release as the co-loading with PLGA, in the initial re-
lease. The chitosan coating revealed, that LB medium was a poor buffer used for release studies. As
it absorbs light at varying wavelength by itself, obscuring the genuine results from the analyte mea-
sured. Chitosan showed no bactericidal effect in combination with CIP. However, it occasionally
showed a bactericidal effect when administered as a powder form loaded in the microcontainers.
There were no significant difference in bacteria disintegration after 20 hours between the three coat-
ings.
The advantages of the oCelloScope over other methods such as a flow-cell system, are a faster
portable system, which requires a significantly lower sample volume. It can analyze 96 iterations
or sample combinations at once. Throughout this thesis it was established that the oCelloScope
provides a high throughput screening of the disintegration of P. aeruginosa, in both the planktonic
stage, in form of turbidity, and as surface adhering clusters. However, it was insufficient when
segmenting images with a large bacterial coverage. A new segmentation tool has been developed,
which enables the possibility to segment images covered with a bacterial clusters and biofilm.

8 FUTURE PERSPECTIVE
The oCelloScope has proven a useful tool for analyzing the effect of antibiotic against bacteria. This
project provides a basis for the effect of three polymers, PLGA, PEG, and chitosan, in combination
with SU-8 microcontainers and CIP against P. aeruginosa clusters and biofilm. Future studies
could examine different combinations of the three polymers. To see e.g. if a chitosan or PLGA
coating prolong the release of a mixture of CIP and PEG, or if the polymers have a beneficial effect
on each other or in combination with CIP. In addition to polymer combination, a combination of
different antibiotic could be a possibility.
There was only investigated one medium in this study. Another choice could be the artificial
sputum medium which is formulated to mimic the sputum of CF patients. In addition, the mucins
in the medium renders it possible to observe the interaction between the polymers and the mucus.
The company behind the oCelloScope, BioSense Solutions, is currently working on a fluorescence
detection system. This enables the opportunities of different fluorescence tagging, for instances
green fluorescent proteins and Live/Dead Staining of bacteria, which in return, facilitates a better
quantification of bacteria.
In the long term, it would be necessary to use microcontainers made of a biodegradable material to
avoid an accumulation of microcontainers in the lungs. The polymer could be an already existing
one, such as polycaprolactone which is already approved by the FDA. Another problem which needs
to be addressed is the size of the microcontainers. As these are currently too large and must be
reduced before it can be properly inhaled.
These research proposition may lead to an sanguine future for CF patients and in the combat against
antimicrobial resistance.
