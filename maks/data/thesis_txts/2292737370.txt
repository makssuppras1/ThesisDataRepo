Abstract 
Bacterial resistance against commonly used antibiotics is becoming a real and global health threat. 
There is an urgent need for development of new antibacterial agents able to target resistant strains. 
One such compound is NXL101, which is part of a family called Novel Bacterial Topoisomerase 
Inhibitors (NBTIs). NXL101 shows promising properties of antibacterial targeting, but 
unfortunately the compound had to be withdrawn from phase I clinical trials because of cardiac 
arrhythmia caused by hERG channel inhibition 
 
The presented project describes the synthesis of 14 new NXL101 analogs, in an effort to further 
enhance the Structure Activity Relationship (SAR) data acquired to date. The main focus of the 
work in this project has been to investigate the synthesis of new types of core scaffolds, i.e. replace 
the central carboxy substituted piperazine with differently substituted homopiperazines. The 
quinolone and linker moieties have been kept constant, while the west chain moiety was varied for 
each specific core.  
The 14 new analogs have been evaluated for antibacterial activity against Staphylococcus aureus 
and Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) values were determined. All compounds were also 
evaluated for hERG binding. The obtained results showed that the most promising compounds are 
the unsubstituted piperazine and homopiperazine analogs.

Resumé	  
Bakteriel resistens mod gængse brugte antibiotiske midler er ved at blive et reelt og globalt 
sundhedsproblem. Dette medfører et øjeblikkeligt behov for, at udvikle nye antibiotiske midler, der 
kan bekæmpe antibiotika resistente stammer. Et potentielt stof til at ramme de resistente stammer er 
NXL101, som er en del af en familie kaldet ”Novel Bacterial Topoisomerase Inhibitors” (NBTIs). 
Til trods for NXL101 gode antibakterielle egenskaber, måtte udviklingen stoppes efter fase I 
kliniske forsøg på grund af hjertearytmi, hvilket skyldtes inhibering af hERG kanalen. 
 
Det præsenteret speciale beskriver syntesen af 14 nye NXL101 analoger, som led i en indsats på at 
udvide den indsamlede data om ”Structure Activity Relationship” (SAR). Hovedfokus i dette 
projekt har været undersøgelsen af nye kerner, dvs. at udskifte den centrale carboxy substitueret 
piperazine med forskelligt substituerede homopiperaziner. Quinolin og linker delen har været holdt 
konstant mens vest kæden er blevet varieret for hver kerne. 
De syntetiserede analoger er blevet evalueret for antibakteriel aktivitet imod Staphylococcus aureus 
og “Minimum Inhibitory Concentration” (MIC) værdier er blevet bestemt. De syntetiserede 
stofferne blev også evalueret for binding til hERG kanalen. De mest lovende stoffer ud fra de 
opnåede værdier blev fundet til at være de usubstitueret piperazine og homopiperazine analoger.

Chapter 1 Introduction 
Bacteria influence all living things. They can have a positive influence and help us survive or they 
can be harmful and even deadly. All living things live with a diverse bacterial flora, that influences 
everyday life. Most of the bacteria are present in the gastrointestinal tract, where the majority live in 
symbiosis with the human host and overall contribute positively to human health.1 
Almost all individuals will at some point be exposed to a pathogenic bacterial infection. Such 
infections can in some cases be relatively harmless and be cleared by the host’s own immune 
system. The human immune system has developed, throughout the evolutionary history, a very 
wide and complex system of defensive mechanisms. This allows the body to clear itself of many of 
the encountered pathogenic bacteria, but if the immune system is weakened or a new powerful 
bacterium is encountered this can have deleterious effects. 
Antibiotics were discovered in the 1940’s as a strategy to kill pathogenic bacteria that the host’s 
immune response could not kill itself.2 Since the first discovery of antibiotics many more classes 
have been discovered and used. The use of antibiotics has not only proved important for human 
health, but also for livestock production. However, the extensive use of antibiotics has resulted in 
the development of resistance in several bacterial strains towards our current arsenal of antibiotics. 
An example of such a strain is the methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), which has 
been widely reported in mainstream media and the demand for development of new classes of 
antibiotics is urgent.3 Although MRSA has taken up a lot of column space in the newspapers other 
resistant bacteria have also evolved. Especially the ones, which can be encountered in hospitals, 
represent a great risk since the infected host is most likely already weakened or have undergone 
surgery.  
The antibiotic resistance of the bacteria towards a certain antibiotic is not only caused by overuse of 
said antibiotic. By nature, bacteria are very versatile and adapt quickly to changing environments, 
thus antibiotic resistance will develop at some point or another.   
The development of new antibiotics is of great importance, in order to fight the antibiotic resistant 
bacteria with upgraded arsenals. Unfortunately, the blockbuster drugs are not within antibiotic 
research in the medicinal industry, due to the short treatment period antibacterial drugs has, 
relatively to other diseases. Hence, the money earned pr. patient is relatively low.4 Companies had 
also practically stopped their research of new antibiotics, essentially because the need for antibiotics 
==PAGE:17==
 10	  
was already addressed by the existing drugs on the market, and that there was little room for more 
potent or safer drugs. These factors combined have caused the amount of antibacterial drugs going 
into clinical trials to drop dramatically over the past decades, as only five new classes of 
antibacterial drugs have been approved in the period 2000-2012 and only very few new drugs were 
in the pipeline Figure	  1.1.4 Hopefully, this will change in the future. New initiatives are currently 
stimulating antibiotic research. Of these can be mentioned the initiative launched in 2010 called the 
10 X ’20 Initiative, which aims to have 10 new antibiotics on the market by 2020.5

Today a widely used class of antibiotics are the quinolones. The subclass called fluoroquinolones is 
especially used clinically as a last measure against bacterial infection and clears the infection by 
inhibition of topoisomerase II enzymes of the bacteria. These compounds are often used when the 
bacterial infection acquired is suspected of being multiresistant.6 Unfortunately, bacteria have 
begun to show resistance towards fluoroquinolones as well.  
Another newly discovered class, which also targets topoisomerase II enzymes, are the novel 
bacterial type II topoisomerase inhibitors (NBTIs). Within this class one newly developed 
compound called NXL-101 shows great potential for inhibiting both bacterial topoisomerase IV and 
gyrase. However, NXL-101 development had to be discontinued due to induction of cardiac 
arrhythmia in healthy subjects, which was caused by the compounds affinity towards hERG channel

developments projects before.  
The herein synthesized compounds were tested and evaluated for their antibiotic activity and their 
affinity for inhibition of the hERG channel. Together these results and previous experiments will 
hopefully show the structure activity relationship (SAR), which can help in further optimization of 
analogs.

Chapter 2 Biological Background 
Life on earth as we know it, can be categorized into two subcategorises: Prokaryotes and 
eukaryotes. The human race and all higher life forms belong to eukaryotes while all bacteria and 
archaea belongs to prokaryotes1. 
Prokaryotes are unicellular microorganisms, which are significantly different compared to 
mammalian cells. The greatest difference between the two being the separate nucleus and more 
complex nature of eukaryotes, while prokaryotes have a circular DNA, which is coiled up in the 
cytoplasm of the cell. These differences are exploited in the development of antibiotics.  
There are several different ways to characterize bacteria; the most widely used is Gram staining, 
characterizing Gram-negative or Gram-positive bacteria. The Gram characterization is based on the 
difference between the cell wall structures. Gram-negative bacteria have two distinct layers, an 
inner membrane and an outer layer of polysaccharides, whereas Gram-positive bacteria only have 
one layer of membrane, which is much thicker. The cell wall is of great importance for prokaryotes 
as it provides structural stability and without them bacteria would die of lysis.7 
Prokaryotes are also smaller than eukaryotes and therefore have a smaller cell volume; this allows 
the procaryotic cells to support a more rapid growth. The faster rate of growth coupled with the fact 
that bacteria are haploids allow them to mutate and adapt to changing environments at a fast rate, 
which can cause development of drug resistance. Drug resistance in bacteria can also develop by 
genetic transfer which occurs by direct transport of genetic material from one bacterial cell to 
another.7 
 
2.1  
Antibiotic Strategies 
There are important considerations to take into account when choosing the target within the bacteria 
for antibiotic treatment:  
• The target must be essential to the bacterial organism, so that blockage or inhibition will 
lead to decreased growth or kill the organism.  
• The targeted protein or enzyme must be conserved over bacterial species to provide the 
antibiotic drug a broad range.  
==PAGE:21==
 14	  
• The targeted enzyme or protein must have no structural resemblance with enzymes or 
proteins in the mammalian host in order to prevent toxicity from clinical use.8   
In the development of antibiotics, it is possible to exploit and target various metabolic functions in 
bacteria which all comply with the requirements listed above; 
• Inhibition of cell metabolism. These drugs inhibit an essential enzyme in microorganisms 
that is not present in the host. It is also possible to produce drugs, which show selectivity 
against enzymes present in both the bacteria and the host as long as there are significant 
differences in the structure between the two.  
• Inhibition of cell wall synthesis. As mammalian cells do not have cell walls such drugs do 
not affect them. 
• Interactions with the plasma membrane. Some antibacterial drugs interact with the plasma 
membrane permeability, which has fatal outcome for the bacteria.  
• Disruption of protein synthesis. Essential proteins and enzymes, which are required for 
bacterial survival, can no longer be made. 
• Inhibition of replication, transcription and translation. This prevents cell division and/or the 
synthesis of essential proteins.7

2.2  
Bacterial Topoisomerases 
When a cell replicates it is necessary to unwind the compact DNA and make space for DNA 
polymerase, which conducts the replication. During replication, the double stranded DNA is split by 
helicases just before the replication fork. As the replication fork moves along the DNA strand, 
positive supercoiling is generated (Figure	  0.2). Positive supercoiling can be visualized by having 
two strings that are intertwined with a person holding in each end. If one person starts separating 
the strings the tension will build in the opposite end. At some point it will be impossible to separate 
the strings any further. The same mechanism applies during DNA replication which will cease and 
might even break the DNA unless the positive supercoils are removed. 
To counteract the problem of positive supercoiling, enzymes such as gyrase and topoisomerases can 
introduce negative supercoiling and induce relaxation in the DNA, which stabilizes the replication. 
Topoisomerases can be categorized into two main classes, type I and type II, and further 
categorized into subclasses A, B and C. Type I topoisomerase work by passing one strand of the 
DNA through a break in the opposing strand. Type II work by passing a region of duplex from the 
same or a different molecule through a double-stranded gap generated in the DNA.10 Both Type I 
and Type II are ATP dependent in their functions. 
 
Figure 0.2: The movement of the replisome and the replication fork causes the formation of positive supercoils. Figure 
from.13  
 
One characteristic for all topoisomerases is that they contain a nucleophilic tyrosine, which binds 
and cleaves the DNA strand. Prokaryotic gyrase and topoisomerase IV are both type IIA 
==PAGE:23==
 16	  
topoisomerases, and compose two subunits A and B that associate to form A2B2 complexes. 
Moreover eukaryotes do not express gyrase, and topoisomerase IV is composed by a single subunit 
that are active as homodimers. This means that there are distinct structural differences, which can 
be exploited in drug targeting. 11,12,13,14 
Generally, when looking at bacterial gyrase and topoisomerase IV, the A subunit is involved in 
interactions with the DNA. It is within this subunit the active nucleophilic tyrosine, responsible for 
DNA cleavage, is located. The B subunit is responsible for ATP binding. When analysing the two 
types of enzymes it can be seen they have similar amino acids sequences and tertiary structure and 
perform many of the same functions, but they also possess their own distinct functions e.g. gyrase is 
the only known topoisomerase, which is capable of inducing negative supercoiling.  
  
Figure 0.3: Type IIA topoisomerase cleaves a duplex DNA string called the G-segment and passes another duplex DNA string 
called the T-segment through the gap in a reaction coupled to ATP. The G segment is then religated and released. The figure 
also shows the approximate positions of inhibition of the enzymatic reaction.13,15 Figure from. 15 
 
The actual bacterial type IIA enzymes regulate the DNA topology by creating a four base paired 
staggered break in one DNA duplex, called the GATE or G-segment. Then another duplex is passed 
==PAGE:24==
 17	  
through, called the transport or T-segment, the earlier mentioned break. Religation and fusion of the 
G-segment ends the enzymatic reaction. The entire enzymatic reaction can be seen in Figure	  0.3. 
The break in the G-segment occurs when binding of the DNA brings together the catalytic 
nucleophilic tyrosine from the A subunit and magnesium-binding residues from the B subunit. This 
forms two catalytically competent cleavage sites, four base pairs apart. Cleavage is then 
accomplished when the catalytic tyrosine attacks the scissile phosphate forming a phosphotyrosine 
bond, thus breaking the DNA backbone.

N-heterocyclic functionality at C7. The second generation agents had significantly broader 
antibacterial spectrum and are still widely used. Since then also a third and a fourth generation have 
been developed and been introduced to the market.  
The structural basis of quinolones action was unknown until 2009, over 40 years after the first 
quinolones were introduced, reflecting the challenge of co-crystallography these complex enzymes 
together with quinolone and DNA. Quinolones were shown to be inserted within the cleaved DNA 
at the two active sites, thus preventing DNA relegation and stabilizing the topoisomerase IV 
enzyme-DNA phosphotyrosyl complex (Figure	   0.5). The water Mg2+ bridge explains the 
importance of the keto acid, which is required in quinolone antibacterials. When the quinolone is 
bound, the Mg2+ ion mediates interaction between the quinolone and the protein. The magnesium is 
modelled with an octahedral coordination sphere with two oxygens from the quinolone and four 
water molecules.17 Two of the water molecules make hydrogen bonds with ParC (A subunit) Ser84 
and Glu88 (Figure	   0.5), thus providing evidence for why mutations at these residues often are 
implicated in quinolone resistance. The two amino acids may also partly explain why quinolones 
are not active in human topoisomerase type IIA as these are not conserved here.15,18

DNA. The quinolone achieves this by having nonplanar groups at C1, C7 and C8 This means that 
the structure is more of a wedge shape which fits into the DNA. Normally the wedge shape would 
not fit into the DNA, but when bound to the enzyme and cleaved, the DNA has a tendency to have a 
slightly distorted shape. The distorted shape also helps point polar atoms on the amino bases 
towards Mg2+ coordinated water molecules. Thus, the Mg2+ coordinated by water also mediates 
interactions with the DNA and may have a role in the antibacterial inhibition17. It is worth

2.4  
Bacterial Resistance to Quinolones  
Pathogenic bacteria can show resistance towards fluroquinolones in three ways, where the most 
common and explainable form is caused by spontaneous mutations in the target structure. The 
mutation usually happens in the A subunit in the area between amino acid 67 and 106. In certain 
pathogens, serine 84 has been shown to be critical for binding the inhibiting quinolone. Pathogens 
have been shown to mutate Ser84Thr. This means introduction of a more bulky side group resulting 
in a tenfold lower susceptibility to fluroquinolones. Another way for pathogens to develop resis-
tance to fluroquinolones is through modifications of the efflux pumps situated in the cytoplasmic 
membrane of the bacteria. Efflux pumps have the ability to pump out toxic substances from the cell 
against a concentration gradient at a cost of energy. Resistance, by the help of efflux pumps, has 
been shown to be achieved by overproduction of the efflux pump coding mRNA. 
The last form of resistance came as a big surprise when discovered. Fluroquinolones are completely 
synthetic and unnatural products that could not be imagined as substrates for degrading or 
modifying enzymes originating in living organisms. Thus it came as a big surprise when plasmid 
==PAGE:27==
 20	  
borne resistance was discovered. It was first reasoned that it could not be plasmid borne gyrA which 
had been mutated and introduced into the cell e.g. by conjugation, since when introduced into the 
cell there would still be plenty of none mutated gyrA, which is expressed by the host chromosome, 
that would be susceptible to fluroquinolones. It was found that a new gene named quinolone 
resistance (qnr) could explain the resistance. This new protein coded by the qnr gene was able to 
protect the gyrase by binding to the enzyme, thus disallowing the drug from binding. The protein 
works by having a three-dimensional structure that fits into the DNA-binding pocket of gyrase. This 
binding probably leads to slower replication for the bacteria, though this is more desirable for the 
bacteria than being killed by the quinolone. This clearly shows the extraordinary ability of bacteria 
to adapt to environmental changes that the use of antibiotics has meant to the microbial world.20 
 
2.5  
Novel Bacterial Topoisomerases Inhibitors (NBTIs) 
With an offset in the antibiotic resistance to quinolones and other compounds, pharmaceutical 
companies have put a lot of effort into the development of new antibiotics. This effort led to the 
discovery of a new family of antibiotics targeting DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV called the 
Novel Bacterial Topoisomerase Inhibitors (NBTIs). This family has an inhibition mechanism 
similar to quinolones, but are structurally and mechanistically different.21 
Inhibition by this family of antibiotics is not associated with the same stabilization of the cleaved 
DNA-enzyme complex as quinolones. Rather structural studies have shown that NBTIs bind and 
inhibit DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV before DNA is cleaved (Figure	  0.3). The inhibition is 
achieved by binding midway, between the two active sites of the enzyme, in the DNA. The 
quinoline-carbonitrile group of the compound is situated between two central base pairs of the 
stretched DNA and the oxathiolo-pyridine group occupies the non-catalytic pocket, which opens up 
between the two GyrA subunits. The pocket is largely hydrophobic and the interactions between the 
antibiotic and the hydrophobic pocket is stabilized by van der Waals interactions.22

2.6  
hERG Channel inhibition 
The hERG (the human Ether-à-go-go-Related Gene) channel is expressed in tissues throughout the 
body including the heart, neurons and smooth muscle. The exact role of the channel in many of 
these tissues is unknown, but it is known to have an important function in the heart where it has a 
vital role in the human cardiac electrical activity and a very defined open and closed state. The 
protein is a membrane channel that is selective for the efflux of K+ ions. The family of potassium-
selective channels have a wide variety of structures and functions that experience conformational 
changes in different ways. Some are voltage-gated such as the hERG channel, which have varying 
permeability to K+ ions dependant of transmembrane voltage-dependant conformational changes. 
Other K+ selective channel pumps are dependent on stimuli activating compounds. 
The importance of the gene became evident when it was discovered that inherited mutations in 
hERG caused long QT syndrome (LQTS), a cardiac repolarization disorder, that predisposes 
affected individuals to arrhythmia.23 The disorder is a well-known risk factor for the occurrence of 
torsade de pointes (TdP) which can cause ventricular fibrillation, leading too sudden death in the 
absence of prompt medical intervention.24

2.6.1  
Role of the hERG Channel: The Cardiac Action Potential 
The hERG channel has a vital role in the action potential in human ventricular cardiomyocyte. The 
ventricular cardiomyocytes constitutes the heart and the action potential is the mechanism by which 
the heart contracts and relaxes and is a continuous and very complex process. The cardiac rhythm 
can be divided into 5 distinct phases (phases 0 to 4) (Figure	  0.8).25  
Phase 0 is triggered by an electrical stimulation, typically from an adjacent cell through gap 
junctions. This causes a rapid depolarization, due to a large inward flow of Na+ ions.26 
Repolarization is a much slower process, which occurs in three phases. Phase 1 proceeds rapidly 
and lasts only a few milliseconds. In this phase a short burst of outward current of K+ creates a brief 
repolarization. Phase 2 follows which is a slow repolarization also called the plateau. This 
repolarization takes longer because the channels, which move the K+ ions have a longer activation 
time, and because of a continues inward current of Ca2+. The intake of Ca2+ provides the optimum 
excitation-contraction coupling. The slow repolarization moves over to phase 3, where the most 
important component is to have a rapid delayed rectifier K+ current through the hERG channels, 
thus restoring the K+ balance inside the cardiomyocytes to allow another circle of the action 
potential.25

2.6.2  
Drug-induced blockage of the hERG channel 
The hERG channel can be blocked by a wide range of pharmaceuticals, thus inducing LQTS with a 
potential fatal outcome. Although LQTS is a relatively rare side effect caused by drugs, inhibition 
of the hERG channel represents an unacceptable risk. This is particularly true for drugs used in 
treatment of non-life-threatening conditions.27 
Test of hERG channel inhibition is now an important aspect in drug discovery and development, 
but because of the simplicity of the experimental model, the results must be evaluated with caution. 
It is estimated that approximately 40-70% of new molecules developed as potential therapeutic 
agents, when assayed for affinity of hERG channel inhibition, test positive and are thus abandoned 
early in the development phase.28     
The promiscuous susceptibility of hERG to pharmacological blockade is thought to result from 
particular structural features of the channel. Structure-function analysis and sequencing of the 
hERG channel, shows that the hERG channel has a larger pore cavity compared to other K+ 
channels. The larger pore cavity results in the hERG channel being able to accommodate a range of 
drug sizes and structures. An Ala-scanning mutagenesis has been conducted to identify the residues 
of drug interaction in the hERG channel. The mutagenesis analysis is performed by individually 
mutating each residue within the pore module to alanine and test the resulting mutant against potent 
hERG blockers. The conducted analysis shows that two polar residues located at the base helix 
(Thr623 and Ser624) and two aromatic residues located in the S6 domaine of the hERG subunit 
(Tyr652 and Phe656) decreases the affinity of potent antiarrhythmic drug.25  
The two polar residues cannot easily explain the promiscuous blocking of hERG as these are highly 
conserved in the family of potassium channel proteins. However, the two aromatic S6 residues are 
not conserved. It is likely that these residues are able to form cation-π interactions or π stacking 
with the inhibiting drugs thus blocking the channel. Interestingly, not all compounds are completely 
attenuated in the mutants where one of the aromatic residues is missing, thus suggesting for some 
compounds that interactions with one of the residues is sufficient.  
==PAGE:32==
 25	  
The majority of inhibitory compounds block the hERG channel from the intracellular side of the 
membrane by a mechanism related to the inactivation of the protein. A closely related protein, the 
EAG K+ channel, found in fruit flies, has many of the same key residues lining the inner cavity as 
hERG. However, most drugs show greater affinity for the hERG channel than the EAG channel. A 
key difference between the two channels is the inactivation and conformational change that the 
hERG channel experiences during the action potential. There are two suggested reasons as to why 
inactivation increases drug binding, either the inactivation lowers the concentration of K+ ions 
within the filter, hence reducing the electrostatic repulsion between drug and ion, or it optimally 
positions all residues involved in drug binding.29  
The hERG channel models will never be completely accurate. The obtained results from the hERG 
model are measured in a simplified system, which has some limitations that must be taken into 
account. In vivo, the inhibition of hERG does not always prolong the QT interval or induce TdP. 
The ventricular action potential is a very complex mechanism and drugs can have additional 
activities e.g. block Ca2+ channels thus counteracting the effect of hERG channel blockage. None 
the less, the current method is more amenable to high-troughput screening than assessing changes 
of the QT interval and arrhythmia risk via animal models.  
 
2.6.3  
Pharmacophore model of hERG inhibition 
As a result of hERG channel inhibition a wide variety of drugs e.g. thioridazine and grepafloxacin 
have been withdrawn from the market and several others have restrictions of use.30 A withdrawal of 
a drug can be very costly, for medicinal companies, and can result in loss of lives before it is known 
if a drug can cause QT prolongation. 
To ensure no drugs, which might inhibit the hERG channel are marketed, several pharmacophore 
models have been deduced building on structure-activity relationship of known QT-prolonging 
compounds.31 The known inhibitors have a very wide range of structures, thus it was difficult to 
find a common pharmacophore model.  
The pharmacophore model below shows that some central residues (Tyr652 and Phe656) have an 
important role in the drug-protein interactions as these residues have the possibility of making π-
stacking interactions between the amino acid side chains and the aromatic fragments of the drug 
molecule. Polar residues (Thr623 and Ser624) have been shown to have an pronounced effect in the 
binding polar tails present in many potent hERG blockers. Furthermore, the central protonated 
==PAGE:33==

residues, thus further strengthening the drug-protein interaction. Figure	   0.9 show the 
pharmacophore model imposed on the hERG channel and how NXL101 fits the model.

The model may be incorporated into the design of new NBTI analogs. However, it is not the only 
way to try to improve the hERG binding affinity. Previous experiments show that this can also be 
achieved by introduction of a group with a negative charge at physiological pH. The introduction of 
a zwitterion into the molecule gives a neutral charge and has been shown to give an almost tenfold 
lower binding affinity of the hERG channel.29  
Despite the described trends in the pharmacophore model, the variety of drugs that can bind to the 
hERG channel illustrates the complexity of the channel and that it is impossible for all the 
compounds to bind in the same way. None the less, the model is a useful tool together with in vitro 
tests to measure the hERG affinity of novel compounds. Consequently it can be predicted to some 
extent whether or not a compound will induce toxicity in humans. However, the models will never 
be 100% accurate and LQTS might surface in clinical trials leading to the termination of the 
development.

Other NBTIs from the Literature 
Several new NBTI series have been reported in the past years in an effort to find a clinical 
candidate and more than 100 patents have been filed.36 Figure	  0.13 depicts some of the promising 
new NBTI candidates developed. The basic scaffold of the NBTIs cannot be altered too much as 
this might also affect the antibiotic effect of the compound. This is a limiting factor that has to be 
overcome by changing the moieties attached to the core structure, and hopefully develop a 
compound, which retains the antibiotic effect and has a low affinity for hERG binding. Thus, a 
significant amount of the developed new compounds are based of the scaffold of GSK299423 2.6. 
There are also compounds inspired by other developments, such as 2.7, which is based on 
AVE6971 2.11.38 Although the core structure cannot be altered too much, some compounds with 
different cores have been investigated such as the pyran-based 2.20.41 This compound showed great 
antibacterial properties, however hERG binding affinity is also a problem for these types of 
compounds.  
When trying to further optimize 2.6 it was suggested with 2.7 that the introduction of 
conformational constraint and removal of metabolic targets would improve the overall biopharma-
ceutical properties. Compound 2.7 showed good antibacterial activity, but the hERG binding 
affinity did not show much improvement.38 Compound 2.19 showed both good antibacterial 
properties and a low hERG bindning affinity and according to the authors this compound is a 
candidate for clinical trials although information about this claim have not been found. The most 
promising NBTI compound is 2.18. The candidate shows high potency against Gram-positive 
bacteria and low hERG inhibition. In may 2014, this was the first NBTI to be approved for phase II 
clinical trials and is potentially the first NBTI to be approved for clinical use.37

Chapter 3 Project Description and Strategy  
This thesis is part of a project initiated by the Nielsen group in 2011. The ultimate goal of the 
project is to synthesize a pre clinical antibiotic candidate based on the scaffold of NXL101. The 
introduction of a simpler core-scaffold would also ease the synthetic work required for the 
production of a library of analogs needed for a thorough SAR study. The development of 2.4 had to 
be discontinued after phase I clinical trials because of prolonged QT in healthy subjects. The hope 
is that by doing a widespread SAR study of analogs similar to 2.4, the ideal drug candidate can be 
found. The candidate should be a potent antibacterial drug without significant hERG inhibition.

1st Generation NXL101 Analogs 
As discussed previously the synthetic route to compound 2.4 (Figure	   0.12) is complex and the 
overall yield is low. The compound showed interesting antibacterial properties but hERG channel 
inhibition lead to LQTS in healthy subjects. Inspired by this, the Nielsen group wanted to address 
some of the synthetic challenges that NXL101 faced. In order to perform an extensive SAR study of 
NXL101 analogs, two simplifications were made to easier obtain new analogs. A new central 
scaffold was designed by removing one of the stereocenters and introducing a piperazine in the 
central core instead of piperdine. Furthermore, the hydroxylgroup on the α-carbon of the linker to 
the quinolone was removed. The simplified molecule 3.1 with two less stereogenic centres can be 
seen in Figure	  0.2. The simplification also meant that a solid phase support could be coupled to the 
acid on the central core, and by orthogonal protection group strategy, selective coupling to the core 
could be performed. The solid phase support strategy has since been phased out as the strategy 
required a large excess of valuable building blocks in several reactions steps.   
 
Figure 0.2: Simplified structure of NXL101 developed by the Nielsen group. 
Some of the synthesized 1st generation NXL analogs can be seen in Table 1. The consequence of 
replacing the core piperidine and the hydroxyl group on the α-carbon was satisfyingly that the 
antibiotic activity of compound 3.2 was retained although the hERG inhibition increased around 
twofold. The increase in hERG inhibition and the decrease in MIC for 3.2 was thought negligible 
compared to the advantages that the new synthetic route offered. Compound 3.2 was later renamed 
NNXL. Surprisingly the reintroduction of the hydroxyl group resulted in a decrease in antibacterial 
activity (3.3 and 3.4). The substituents on the core piperazine were then studied and the biological 
results indicate that the strongest antibiotic activity is achieved when the carboxylic acid is installed 
in the C2 position with an R-configuration (3.2, 3.5 and 3.7). The intermediate methyl ester analog 
3.6 was tested as well, as the final step in the synthesis of these analogs is the hydrolysis of the 
mentioned 3.6, the compound showed both poor antibacterial effects and high hERG affinity.

3.3  
3rd and 4th Generation NXL101 Analogs  
Following the promising biological properties of some of the aliphatic compounds in the second 
generation analogs (3.13 and 3.20) further work went into investigating the optimal length of linear 
aliphatic chains and different ring sized cyclic aliphatic moieties which became the third generation. 
The synthesized analogs with linear aliphatic chains varied between 1-11 carbons and the most 
promising new analog was the pentyl chain, which also showed great antibacterial properties 
(MSSA MIC = 0.8 µg/mL, MRSA MIC = 1.6 µg/mL), but unfortunately also had a high hERG 
affinity (73% inhibition at 30 µM). Different aliphatic rings with variable sizes were also 
investigated, but overall none of the produced third generation analogs had improved properties 
over the compounds (3.13 and 3.20) they were based upon. 
Furthermore, fluorinated aromatic compounds were also investigated with different number of 
electronegative groups at variable positions. But once again none of the investigated compounds 
showed any potential leads. Lastly a reinvestigation of the linker between the piperazine and the 
quinolone moiety was conducted. This series of compounds incorporated different linkers with 
different properties, cis and trans double bonds, and a varying number of hydroxylgroups with 
different stereochemistry at the α and β position from the quinolone. Generally, variations on the 
linker lowered the antibiotic activity of the analogs, however a hydroxylgroup on the α-carbon was 
tolerated, but no antibacterial activity were observed for diol analogs.   
 
3.4  
Structural Modifications of the Piperazine Core 
Thus far the NNXL project in the Nielsen group has yielded many analogs. The main focus of the 
investigation has been the west chain of the NNXL (Figure	  0.3) as this structural element of the 
scaffold has been shown to have a large impact on the biological properties of the compounds. The 
west chain both influences the antibacterial properties and the hERG binding affinity, thus a 
thorough investigation of this part of the molecule is very important in the SAR study.  
Recently a reinvestigation also explored the properties of the linker between the piperazine core and 
the quinolone part of the molecule. In this part of the molecule there is a distinct difference between 
NNXL and NXL101 as a hydroxylgoup and thereby a stereocentre have been removed in the 
simplification of the molecule. The importance of one or two hydroxylgroups on the linker was 
studied, and thus far it seems as if the simplified version has the best biological properties.

4.1  
C2 Carboxy Substituted Homopiperazine NXL101 Analogs 
This chapter covers the synthesis of the general homopiperazine 3.21 with the carboxylic acid 
positioned at the C2 position. The full synthetic scheme is shown below in Scheme	  0.1.  
There were a few challenges for this synthetic strategy. Because of the two amines in the starting 
material, orthogonal protecting groups had to be utilized in order to conduct the reactions 
chemoselectively. This requirement for orthogonality is fulfilled by the two amine protection 
groups tert-butyloxycarbonyl (Boc) and carboxybenzyl (Cbz), where the former is acid-labile, while 
the latter can be cleaved by hydrogenolysis.ii Once protected, the generated carbamates are electron 
withdrawing, thus reducing the basicity and nucleophilicity of the amines. 
Furthermore, because the first reductive alkylation was performed on a primary amine to yield 
compound 4.3, it was important to ensure that double alkylation did not occur.

Scheme 0.2: Two step synthesis for compound 4.10 used in the synthesis of 3.21. 
For the first reductive alkylation at N1, compound 4.10 (Scheme	  0.2) was produced by a two step 
synthesis. First step was protection of a single alcohol of propanediol. This was achieved by having 
a large excess of propanediol compared to TBDPSCl, after single protection the separation of the 
product from the excess propanediol was easily achieved by column chromatography. The second 
step was oxidation of the free alcohol to the corresponding aldehyde 4.10. For this reaction a Swern 
oxidation was chosen. The Swern reaction utilises the formation of a reactive electrophilic sulphur 
compound from the reaction of DMSO and oxalyl chloride. This newly formed salt reacts with the 
free alcohol from the propanediol and produces a sulfonium salt, which is stable enough for 
deprotonation by DIPEA thus forming the aldehyde, used for the reaction with 4.2 after a aqueous 
workup.  
The synthesis of 4.3 was accomplished by adding one equivalent of 4.10 to 4.2. This results in the 
formation of the imine (iminium ion if a secondary amine is the nuclephile) in situ which after 
reduction by the mild reducing agent Na(OAc)3BH yields the desired amine as seen in Figure	  0.1. 
For the reduction of the imine it is important to use a chemoselective reducing agent such as 
Na(OAc)3BH and not NaBH4, as the imine is in equilibrium with the primary amine and the 
aldehyde. If NaBH4 was used the aldehyde would be reduced to the corresponding alcohol.

Reductive alkylation at N4 and Challenges of Purification 
The six-step synthetic route to 4.7 seen in Scheme	  0.1, gave an overall yield of 18% and about 500 
mg product. The penultima steps to get the C2 homopiperazine NNXL analogs were the N4 
reductive alkylation, and hydrolysis of the ester to yield the carboxylic acid. To produce as many 
analogs of 3.21 as possible it was decided to split 4.7 into 30 mg batches and perform the reductive 
alkylation on each batch using different aldehydes, as 5–10 mg of the final analogs would suffice 
for NMR characterization and biological testing. As the reductive alkylation and hydrolysis of 
NNXL analogs had been performed many times before previously in the group, 5 aldehydes and the 
free amine were chosen and reacted without any test reactions. The first five chosen west chains for 
3.21 can be seen in Figure	  0.3. 
 
Figure 0.3: The five chosen aldehydes for the first N4 reductive alkylation on 3.21. 
	  
The N4 reductive alkylation was performed by adding the selected aldehyde to a mixture containing 
4.7 and the reducing agent Na(OAc)3BH. The reaction was usually completed overnight, and the 
mixture was subjected to an aqueous workup, after which the hydrolysis was conducted in a 
THF:water (2:1) solution with LiOH as the base. The hydrolysis was usually also completed 
overnight, and AcOH was added to neutralize the base. The reaction mixture was then purified by 
preparative HPLC to give the product.  
Unfortunately the first six compounds decomposed. After the hydrolysis preparative HPLC was

4.6  
Biological Test Results and Evaluations 
The synthesized analogs seen in Table 4.1 have been subjected to two types of biological tests to 
evaluate the antibacterial properties of the compounds as well as the inhibitory effect on hERG. To 
test for the antibacterial properties a whole cell broth microdilution assay was used to determine the 
minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) and a predictor fluorescence polarization assay was 
used to measure the hERG inhibition. The hERG channel inhibition has either been reported as the 
concentration of 50% inhibition or (EC50) or the percentage of inhibition at 30 µM (% blockage at 
30 µM). From the collected results of these assays it would then be possible to rank the synthesized 
compound according to their antibacterial activity and their degree of unwanted hERG inhibition. 
These results can, together with the previous work performed by the Nielsen group, be used in the 
SAR evaluation of the NXL101 analogs and hopefully lead to the design of more potent 
antibacterial analogs with less off-target binding.  
 
4.6.1  
Determination of MICs 
Minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) is by definition the lowest concentration of an 
antimicrobial agent that inhibit the visible growth of a microorganism after overnight incubation.46 
Thus, it is desirable to have a low MIC value when developing new antibacterial compounds. The 
test performed to measure the MIC values is very simplified and many factors can influence the 
measured MICs compared to how the compound would perform in vivo. However, the determined 
MIC values are a good indication of the antibacterial potency of an analog.  
The compounds were only tested against Gram-positive bacteria, as the activity of NXL101 analogs 
have been shown to be generally low against Gram-negative bacteria. The two strains used to test 
the antibacterial activity of the compounds developed in this thesis were the S. aureus strains 
MSSA RV37 and swine MRSA CC398. Of the two strains, MRSA is not susceptible to methicillin 
(β-lactams), while MSSA is susceptible.   
Post Doc. Tim Holm Jacobsen from the group of professor Michael Givskov, Department of 
international health and Immunology University of Copenhagen performed the MIC determinations. 
The testing was performed as a whole cell broth microdilution assay. In this type of assay the same 
type of bacterial cell culture is subjected to decreasing concentrations of the investigated 
==PAGE:69==
 62	  
compound. After one day of incubation the MIC value is then determined by visual inspection. The 
lowest concentration of compound that prevents visible growth represents the MIC value.47 
The concept of the dilution assay is shown in Figure	  0.14. The experiments were performed in 96 
well microtiterplate and all results were determined by double determination. First, 100 µL growth 
medium is added to all 12 wells in a row. To the first well is then added 100 µL from a 200 µg/mL 
solution of the test compound, thus the concentration of the test compound is 100 µg/mL. The 
mixture is stirred and 100 µL is extracted and added to the next well, the process is repeated 
throughout the row except for the last well, which acts as a reference. Following this, 100 µL of 
incubated cell culture is added to each of the wells giving a concentration of 50 µg/mL of the test 
compound in the first well. The range of concentrations of the test compound is thus between 50 
µg/mL and 0.05 µg/mL as seen in Figure	  0.14. After 24 hours of incubation the MIC values are 
determined, it is very important to do double determination and include the reference, as many 
technical factors can influence the MIC values.48

4.6.2  
Determination of hERG Channel Inhibition 
The testing of hERG channel inhibition of the prepared NXL101 analogs was performed by LEO 
Pharma A/S. The testing was unfortunately not completed in time for the submission of this project. 
A short description of the method is given below in case the results are obtained before the defence. 
The best test for hERG inhibition is the so-called Patch-clamp electrophysiology, unfortunately this 
assay is too expensive for high throughput tests. The assay used by LEO Pharma A/S, a PredictorTM 
hERG Fluroscence Polarization Assay Kit, which works by having a membrane containing hERG 
channel proteins and a high-affinity red fluorescent hERG channel ligand called a tracer. The hERG 
channel inhibition is determined by the fact that when the tracer is free in solution and excited with 
plane-polarized light, the emitted light from the tracer will be depolarized because the molecules 
tumble rapidly. Contrary if the molecule is bound to the hERG channel the tracer will be more 
locked and the emitted light will remain more polarized. Consequently, when the investigated 
compound is added to the solution the tracer and the compound will compete for the hERG channel 
binding, thus giving a more depolarized light if the compound has a high affinity for the hERG 
channel50.   
 
Figure 0.15: Principal by which hERG inhibition is determined by LEO Pharma. Figure from.50 
Since 2011 two methods have been applied to determine the hERG channel inhibition of the 
NXL101 analogs. The early analogs were measured by a titration curve and the exact concentration 
for when the inhibitory effect is 50% is measured. The currently used method is a more simple and 
less time consuming method, where the membrane is subject to one known concentration of the test 
analog (30 µM), and the value determined is the percentage of inhibition of the hERG channel.

homopiperazine compounds are a bit surprising, as the substituted analogs showed poor potency. It 
was speculated that the introduction of the 7-membered ring, would make the core of the molecule 
larger. Thus a shorter west chain (1- or 2-atom linker) should give a more optimal configuration and 
fit into the described binding pocket (Chapter 2.5). This was however not the case for the 
substituted homopiperazine and non-substituted homopiperazines as they all showed equal or better 
potency with a 3-atom linker. Instead, the potency of the non-substituted homopiperazines suggests 
that the carboxygroup is responsible for the lack of potency in the substituted analogs.  
The obtained results imply that the lowered antibacterial activity of the substituted analogs is 
caused by steric hindrance of the carboxylic acid or interactions with amino acid residues adjacent 
to the binding pocket. However, the lower potency of the substituted homopiperazine analogs can 
also be explained by a poorer membrane permeability. 
Both non-substituted homopiperazine and piperazine shows promising antibacterial properties.

Chapter 5 Conclusion and Future Work 
	  
The main focus of this project has been to synthesize new NXL101 analogs based on a simplified 
scaffold, NNXL, obtained by removing two stereocenters. In an effort to further expand the SAR 
data acquired to date, the main focus of the work has been to synthesize new types of core 
scaffolds. The substituted homopiperazine core with a carboxygroup positioned at the C2 position 
(3.21) was successfully synthesized while the synthesis of a homopiperazine with a C5 positioned 
carboxygroup (3.22) was unsuccessful. Furthermore, non-substituted analogs of piperazine and 
homopiperazine were synthesized to investigate the importance of the carboxy group (3.32 and 
3.24), and the possibility of developing a preliminary SAR model consisting of very simple 
analogs. For all analogs the quinolone and linker part have been kept constant while the core and 
the west chains have been varied. This method resulted in 14 new synthesized analogs.  
The synthetic routes for the C2 and C5 substituted analogs were very similar. However, only the C2 
substituted analogs were successfully synthesized yielding 7 C2 substituted homopiperazine 
analogs, which were sent for biological testing. The overall yield of the synthetic route prior to the 
last reductive alkylation was 18% and the yield of the final analogs were between 14-34%. The 
analogs proved to be less stable than piperazine core analogs and an optimized purification process 
was developed.  
The synthesis of the C2 and C5 substituted homopiperazines relied on a ring closure to form the 
homopiperazine core. However, during the synthesis of the C5 analogs an unforeseen 6-membered 
ring closure caused the formation of the undesired byproduct 4.35 and because of time constraints 
an alternative route could not be explored. The synthesis of C5 substituted analogs only yielded the 
undesired product 4.35, which was sent for biological testing.  
The 6 non-substituted analogs of piperazine and homopiperazine 4.42-4.47 was easily obtained 
from the Boc protected piperazine and homopiperazine. From the Boc protected starting material 
only a two-step reaction was needed to obtain the analogs. 
From the 14 analogs sent for biological testing the overall SAR knowledge obtained in this project 
can be summarized as following; 
 
==PAGE:75==
 68	  
• Introduction of the substituted homopiperazine core lowers the antibacterial potency. 
• Non-substituted homopiperazine and piperazine core analogs retain high antibacterial 
activity. 
• A 3 atom linker on the west chain yields the most potent analogs. 
Three of the best performing analogs are shown in Figure	  0.1. The non-substitutet analogs 4.43 and 
4.46 possesse the lowest determined MIC values against MSSA and MRSA. With analog 4.23 
performing best of the synthesized C2 analogs, further explorations of these compounds seem 
pointless.

Based on the present work, several experiments can further expand the SAR analysis. The synthesis 
of the C5 substitutet analogs would be interesting, as these analogs could possibly be more potent 
than the C2 analogs. Furthermore, the synthesis of the C5 analogs would also confirm whether or 
not the substituted piperazine analogs constitute the most potent core. Further investigations of the 
non-substituted homopiperazine and piperazine cores would show if these analogs can be used as a 
very simple model for SAR studies.   
However, further investigations heavily depend on the results of the hERG binding affinity test for 
the 14 new analogs which have yet to be obtained.

6.1  
General Method 
Unless otherwise stated, all reactions were run under normal atmosphere and all glassware was 
dried over a Bunsen flame under vacuum, before contact with any of the reactants or solvents. All 
flasks were equipped with a rubber septum, through which transport of chemicals were performed 
with needle-tipped syringes. All reactions were followed on thin layer chromatography and 
reversed-phase ultra performance liquid chromatography mass spectrometry.  
All solvents were of HPLC grade quality or purified using an Innovative Technology PureSolv 
MD7 System. All commercially available reagents were used without further purification. In vacuo 
evaporation of solvents was performed using a rotary evaporator conncted to a membrane pump at 
various temperatures and pressures.  
All compounds were generally characterized by TLC, melting point, 1H NMR, 13C NMR, IR, MS 
(ESI), HRMS (ESI) and whenever appropriate optical rotation, when the amount of compound were 
limited, certain methods were chosen. 2D NMR spectras (COSY, HSQC) were also recorded to 
help verify the structure. 
Analytic TLC were done be Mereck aluminium sheets covered with silicagel C-60 F254. The plates 
were either visualized under UV-light and/or stained by dipping in a developing agent followed by 
heating. KMnO4 ((3 g) in H2O (300 mL) along with K2CO3 (20 g) and 5% aqueous NaOH (5 mL)) 
and/or ninhydrin (10 g in 300 mL EtOH) were used as developing agents. Melting points were 
measured using a Thomas Hoover capillary apparatus. Column chromatography was performed 
using glass columns packed with Matrex 60 Å silicagel (35-70 µm particles), as stationary phase. 
The liquid phase is specified in the experimental procedures. Analytic HPLC was conducted on a 
Waters Alliance 2695 reversed-phased high-performance liquid chromatography system using a 
Symmetry® C-18 column (d 2.5 µm, 4.6x75mm, column temp: 25 °C, flow 1 mL/min) with 
detection at 215 nm and 254 nm. Eluents A (0,1% TFA in H2O) and B (0,1 TFA in MeCN) were 
used in a linear gradient (100% A to 100% B) in a run time of 13 min.

(operating at 400 MHz for proton and 101 MHz for carbon), was processed via the NMR software
