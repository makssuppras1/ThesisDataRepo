Abstract 
Large-scale experiments in 2009 and 2014 showed that the indoor environment in many Danish 
schools were well below the standards and recommendations. This could affect the pupils as several 
studies have shown that the indoor environment could influence the pupil performance and well-
being negatively. In this master’s thesis the data from the latest Large-scale experiment of 2021 is 
being analyzed and discussed. 
The pupils of 1227 participating classes had to perform physical measurements of their air quality, 
temperature, lighting, and noise, and complete a performance test and well-being survey. They 
repeated this two times on two days with a week separation. On day 1, the pupils acted as usual 
without any changes, and on day 8, the pupils were outside, and the windows were open during 
recess. 
This master’s thesis analyses the results and compares the performance test accuracy and well-
being of the pupils with the four measured indoor environment parameters. The results showed that 
the indoor air quality had improved slightly in this experiment compared to the experiments in 2009 
and 2014. The measured CO2 concentration was below the recommended maximum limit of 1000 
ppm in 48% of the participating classes on day 1, and 66% of the participating classes on day 8, 
showing that opening the windows during recess, improved the air quality. However, since the 
experiment took place in November and December, the measured operative temperature decreased 
from day 1 to day 8, showing that the thermal environment was influenced negatively by the opening 
of the windows. The results also showed that many classrooms had insufficient lighting, even when 
there was no solar shading. Sound pressure level measurements showed that there was too much 
noise in the classrooms. Only 3% of the participating classes measured a sound pressure level below 
the maximum recommended 50 dB(A).  
There was no clear linear correlation between the individual indoor environment parameters and 
pupil performance, but results showed that the performance increased from an average of 89,8% to 
90,2% from day 1 to day 8 possibly indicating a multivariate correlation. It was also seen that the 
older the pupils were, the higher was the average of the performance test accuracy, and that the 
girls had a higher performance compared to boys. There was a more direct correlation between the 
indoor environment and the well-being survey, where the results showed that more pupils found the 
air quality bad when the CO2 concentration increased. There was also a slight correlation between 
the performance test accuracy and the well-being survey, showing that pupils who felt they had a 
headache, could not concentrate, or did not want to perform, performed slightly worse than the pupils 
who felt no symptoms.  
Both the large-scale experiment and long-term measurements in six classrooms in Odense showed 
a correlation between the ventilation method and the CO2 concentration. The results showed that 
when a classroom was equipped with both supply and exhaust the CO2 concentration was generally 
lower than classrooms who had only natural ventilation.  
Two of the classrooms where the long-term measurements took place were recently renovated and 
showed an improved indoor environment in terms of atmospheric- and thermal environment. The 
two classes were a small part of the plan of renovating all 34 public schools in Odense. A study on 
the number of renovation tasks in schools in Odense in recent years, showed that the number of 
renovation tasks in 2021 were equal to the number from 2013-2018. The same amount of renovation 
tasks is planned for 2022 and 2023 in the large initiative “Velfærdens Fundament” by Odense 
Municipality. This is a part of their vision saying that “in 2025 there will be significantly less 
classrooms with a bad air quality and too much noise in Odense.”

Resumé 
Masseeksperimenter i 2009 og 2014, viste at indeklimaet i mange danske folkeskoler ligger langt 
under standarderne og anbefalingerne. Dette kan påvirke elevernes præstation og trivsel viser flere 
studier. I dette kandidatspeciale vil resultaterne af det nyeste masseeksperiment 2021 blive 
analyseret og diskuteret.  
I masseeksperimentet deltog 1227 klasser som hver især skulle foretage målinger på luftkvalitet, 
temperatur, belysning og lydniveau, og eleverne skulle gennemføre en koncentrationstest og en 
trivselstest. Klasserne gentog dette over tog dage med en uges mellemrum. På dag 1 skulle eleverne 
gøre som de plejer i frikvarteret før lektionen, og på dag 8 skulle eleverne være uden for klasselokalet 
imens vinduerne var åbne i klasseværelset inden lektionen. 
I dette speciale kortlægges indeklimaet i de deltagende klasser, samt undersøges sammenhængen 
mellem indeklimamålingerne, koncentrationstesten og trivselstesten. Resultaterne viste at 
luftkvaliteten i de deltagende klasser var forbedret en lille smule i forhold til masseeksperimenterne 
i 2009 og 2014. Den målte CO2 koncentration var under det anbefalede maksimum på 1000 ppm i 
48% af klasseværelserne på dag 1, og 66% af klasseværelserne på dag 8. Dette understregede, at 
når vinduerne åbnes i frikvarterende forbedres luftkvaliteten i klasseværelset. Da eksperimentet 
foregik i november og december, betød dette også, at temperaturen i klasselokalerne var lavere på 
dag 8 end på dag 1. Resultaterne viste også, at der i mange klasselokaler er for lidt belysning selvom 
solafskærmningen ikke er trukket for. Derudover viste målingerne af lydniveauet, at der var alt for 
meget larm i klasserne. Kun 3% af de deltagende klasser lå under den maksimalt anbefalede værdi 
på 50 dB(A).  
Resultaterne af koncentrationstesten viste ikke nogen direkte lineær sammenhæng mellem 
indeklimaet og præstationen, men der var en forbedring på det gennemsnitlige antal rigtige svar fra 
89,8% til 90,2% fra dag 1 til dag 8, hvilket kan indikere, at der er en multivariat sammenhæng mellem 
flere variable. Derudover viste resultaterne også, at jo ældre eleverne var desto højere var deres 
gennemsnit, og at piger havde flere korrekte svar end drengene i alle årgange. Der var en mere 
tydelig sammenhæng mellem indeklimaet og trivslen, hvor resultaterne viste at desto højere CO2 
koncentrationen var, desto flere elever syntes at luftkvaliteten var dårlig. Der var også en lille 
sammenhæng mellem præstationen og trivslen der viste, at hvis eleverne følte, at de havde 
hovedpine, ikke kunne koncentrere sig, eller ikke ønskede at præstere, så var deres antal af rigtige 
svar også lavere.  
Både masseeksperimentet og langtidsmålinger som blev foretaget i seks klasseværelser i Odense, 
viste at der var en sammenhæng mellem luftkvaliteten og ventilationsmetoden i klassen. En større 
del af de klasser, der havde et ventilationssystem med tilførsel og udsugning af luft, havde også en 
lavere CO2 koncentration end de klasseværelser, der kun havde naturlig ventilation.  
To af de klasseværelser hvor der blev foretaget langtidsmålinger var for nyligt renoveret, og de 
tilsvarende resultater viste også at de havde det bedste indeklima i forhold til luftkvalitet og 
temperatur. Disse to klasseværelser er en del af en større plan fra Odense Kommune, om at 
renovere samtlige 34 folkeskoler i Odense. En undersøgelse på antallet af renoveringstiltag i Odense 
Kommune viste at der i 2021 var lige så mange renoveringer som i årene fra 2013 til 2018. Antallet 
af renoveringstiltag viser sig at fortsætte i 2022 og 2023 i projektet Velfærdens Fundament, hvor der 
er afsat 500 mio. kr. til at forbedre indeklimaet i folkeskolerne. Dette er en af flere projekter, som skal 
sørge for at sikre visionen, at der ”i 2025 er væsentligt færre klasseværelser med dårlig luftkvalitet 
og for meget støj i Odense Kommune.”

1 Introduction 
The Danish pupils spend more time in classrooms than ever before. To increase general 
competences and stimulate individual engagement, a large school reform implemented in 2014, 
resulted in pupils spending from a total of 8.000 hours in school to 11.000 hours in 2019 (OECD, 
2014; 2019). The school system spans over 10 years of mandatory education where pupils spend 
more than 20% of their awaken hours in schools, making it an important task to ensure the quality 
of the indoor environment to take care of pupil health and well-being. 
 
Figure 1: Weekly hours per class separated into the three stages in the current school system. (Indskoling - 1st to 3rd , 
Mellemtrin – 4th to 6th, Udskoling – 7th to 10th). Source: From 1988 – 2003 (Ministry of Children and Education). From 
2003 – 2007 (UNI∙C Statistics & Analysis, 2009), 2009-2014 (Ministry of Children and Education, 2009-2014), 2014-2020 
(Ministry of Children and Education).   
The hours spent in school per week has changed a lot through the last couple of decades. Figure 1 
shows that from 2014, the hours spent in school per week was extended with 12 hours compared to 
2010. As almost 90% of the Danish schools that are built, were constructed before 1995 according 
to Clausen, et al., (2017), the pupils may be exposed to an unhealthy indoor environment for longer 
periods than ever due to the condition of the schools.  
In 2009 and 2014 large-scale indoor climate experiments were carried out in Danish Schools, where 
13% of the Danish schools participated the first year and 11% the second year. The experiment in 
2009 showed that only 44% of the Danish schools were fulfilling the recommendations in terms of 
air quality, while in the neighboring Scandinavian countries Norway and Sweden, studies showed 
that 79% and 84% of schools fulfilled the air quality recommendations (Andersen, et al., 2009). In 
2014 the number had decreased to 40% in Danish schools (Clausen, et al., 2014).  
 
1.1 Objectives 
In the late autumn of 2021, the large-scale experiment was repeated. This time with an extended 
focus on the indoor climate influence on pupil performance and well-being. Data from 321 schools 
will be investigated and the results will be analyzed and discussed in this master’s thesis. The main 
themes that will be investigated is based upon background knowledge about indoor environment in 
schools and its influence on pupil performance and well-being. The thesis focuses on the following 
hypothesis: 
The indoor environment in many Danish schools is in poor quality and it influences 
performance and well-being of the pupils.

2 Indoor Environment 
The main purpose of this section is to create an overview of what indoor environment is in theory 
and to explain what the main factors are when focusing on schools. This is done by introducing the 
four fundamental indoor environment parameters and create a general understanding for them. 
By searching through the latest literature in the field, an overview of the indoor environment influence 
on performance and wellbeing is investigated. This is done so the results later can be compared to 
the found literature and evaluated according to indoor environment regulations, standards, and 
recommendations in the industry.   
Finally, this section also investigates the consequences and socioeconomic gains of a bad or good 
indoor climate and gives an overview of political initiatives and strategies from Odense Municipality 
on what is done to improve the indoor environment in the public schools.

2.1 The Atmospheric Environment 
2.1.1 Indoor Air Quality 
The atmospheric environment in the indoor climate refers to the air quality in a room. It is an important 
topic as it relates to the health and well-being of the pupils in a classroom. A bad air quality can have 
both short-term effects, such as irritation of the eyes, nose and throat, and long-term health 
consequences such as respiratory diseases as described by the Danish Healthcare Services 
(Jørgensen, 2020). 
 
2.1.2 Pollutants influencing indoor air quality 
The air quality is determined by the exposure to a wide spectrum of air pollutants from building 
materials, furniture, equipment, and human metabolism (Fanger, et al., 1992). The pollutants consist 
of different types of gasses, particles, and micro-organisms in different sizes such as Radon, 
Formaldehyde, fine dust particles, PM10 and PM2.5, VOCs (volatile organic compounds) and human 
bioeffluents that are released from surfaces and humans into the room air. The concentration of the 
pollutants determines the health risk, and some are more dangerous than others, ranging from

sensory irritation to cancer (WHO, 2010). The VOCs are usually emitted with higher temperatures 
and a high relative humidity. (Jørgensen, 2019). 
 
2.1.3 CO2 concentration as an indicator for indoor air quality 
Studies have shown that when the CO2 concentration is high associations can be drawn to causing 
headache, fatigue, eye, nose, and respiratory tract symptoms, e.g., Myhrvold, et al., (1996) who 
found that there was a correlation between symptoms like headache and tiredness and a rising CO2 
concentration. The CO2 concentration itself, is usually considered harmless at these concentrations, 
but the correspondingly rising presence of the other harmful indoor pollutants such as emissions 
from building materials, furnishing materials and occupant clothing are usually considered the 
causative agents as written by Zhang, et al., (2017) who examined the effects of human exposure 
to only CO2, which showed no statistically significant effects on acute health symptoms. However, 
exposure to the combination of human bioeffluents and a CO2 concentration at 3000 ppm reduced 
the perceived air quality and increased the intensity of reported acute health symptoms.    
The CO2 concentration is therefore a simple way to measure the air quality in a room based on the 
number of occupants. It works well as an indicator for the pollution of bioeffluents and shows how 
well a room is ventilated. The bioeffluents are generated by the metabolism of the occupants based 
on their activity level and surface area. A sitting adult typically produces 20,4 L/h, while children 
produce a little less. 
When measuring the CO2 concentration, 380-420 ppm of the total concentration is considered as 
the background concentration in the ambient air.  
 
2.1.4 Ensuring an acceptable indoor air quality   
To ensure an acceptable indoor air quality, source control and pollutant dispersion is required, which 
means the application of low-emission materials and products, the venting of pollutants to the 
outdoor air, and ventilation control. (WHO, 2010) This goal can be achieved through different 
ventilation methods.  
 
2.1.5 Ventilation Methods 
There are two main methods of ventilation, natural- and mechanical ventilation. It is possible to 
combine them in different hybrid configurations depending on the building typology, season, or 
configuration (Hviid, et al., 2014). The Danish school building typologies can be separated into six 
categories as explained in (ICIEE, 2016) where each category describes a building typology typical 
for a specific period. Each typology requires different ventilation solutions when renovating and 
modernizing the school buildings and therefore the two main methods of ventilation can be used in 
various ways. In Figure 3, three ventilation methods are illustrated which are usually seen in 
classrooms.

Natural ventilation (③) is usually the simplest solution and was the primary solution until around 
1995, where the building regulations introduced the indoor climate section (Building Regulations, 
1995). Natural wind driven air flows and pressure differences are the main drivers of natural 
ventilation and works through either manually or automatically controlled window openings or vents. 
This ventilation type can work as a one-sided configuration, cross-ventilation configuration, stack 
ventilation (like in an atrium which serves several floors), or as a top-down principle with a central 
shaft used for cold air supply. Unfortunately, as found in the large-scale experiment of 2009 
(Andersen, et al., 2009) and large-scale experiment of 2014 (Clausen, et al., 2014), almost 70% of 
schools with natural ventilation had an unacceptable air quality. 
The different configurations have both pros and cons, but natural ventilation works best when two 
opposed openings drive the wind, for instance with cross-ventilation or a solution with opposed 
overhead openings, where windows in the facade work as supply and the overhead windows work 
as exhaust. According to the building regulations 2018, the requirement of mechanical ventilation 
can be dispensed if measures like cross ventilation is implemented (Domestic Affairs and Housing 
Comittee, 2018). One of the main consequences of natural ventilation is the risk of draught, which 
can be the cause of not opening the windows when the temperature is below 18 ˚C, which results in 
insufficient air supply (Maagaard, et al., 2021). 
Mechanical Ventilation (①, ②, ④) is more complex but can be suited for many situations with 
different building typologies. Mechanical ventilation is driven by a mechanical air handling unit with 
a fan installed. The system can either be centralized or decentralized (②) as seen in Figure 3. A 
centralized system can cover many sections of a building, where a decentralized system is installed 
per zone. There are two types of controls for mechanical ventilation with supply and exhaust. Firstly, 
the constant air volume control (CAV) which delivers a constant air flow to a zone, and the variable 
air volume (VAV) which is usually demand controlled and is coupled with sensors with setpoints for 
the air quality, temperature, or CO2 concentration.

2.2 Thermal Environment 
2.2.1 Thermal Comfort 
The thermal environment in a room describes the conditions that influences an occupants’ sensation 
of feeling warm or cold. When the heat balance of the body is in a neutral state, the occupant is 
thermally comfortable. An occupant can feel thermal discomfort when exposed to thermal 
asymmetry, which is caused by unwanted heating or cooling of parts of the body, or when exposed 
to air movements locally cooling the skin, known as draught.  The heat balance is influenced by the 
activity level, the clothing level, and the surrounding thermal conditions in the space (Nielsen, 2016). 
The thermal conditions include the air temperature, combined air velocities, relative humidity and 
mean radiant temperature. 
 
2.2.2 Operative temperature as an indicator for the thermal environment 
The operative temperature is typically used as a simple indicator for the thermal environment. The 
operative temperature can be estimated as the average of the air temperature and mean radiant 
temperature at low air velocities. It can also be measured with globe thermometers, which measures 
the combined effect of radiation, air temperature and air velocities. These three parameters are 
influenced by the building envelope and the building systems. 
 
2.2.3 Building envelope and systems influencing the thermal environment 
Figure 4 presents some of the contributors to the three parameters when looking at classrooms. 
 
Figure 4: Contributors to the thermal environment. 1: Heat transmission to/from other rooms, 2: Equipment, 3: Lighting,

envelope.

thermal environment. Depending on the insulation level and the thermal mass of a building they 
determine how sensitive the thermal environment is to quick variations from the outdoor 
environment. A heavy construction will be less sensitive compared to a light construction, due to a

larger heat accumulation in the components. Maagaard, et al., (2021) describes that the thermal

Equipment and technical building systems (②, ④) also contribute to the thermal environment. A 
classroom is typically equipped with a projector which has a heat emission of around 250 W, and a 
smartboard with 175 W (Christensen, 2018).  The ventilation system (⑤) supply air can supply a 
room with both cooling and heating, depending on the outdoor air temperature during the seasons 
and the components in the air handling unit. Artificial lighting (③) also affects the room temperature 
in buildings depending on the type. LEDs usually have a lower heat emission of 4-6 W/m2, compared 
to fluorescent tubes, 6-8 W/m2 which many schools use today.  
The occupants (⑥) also emit heat according to their activity level and clothing insulation. Only the 
dry heat loss from the occupants will contribute to temperature changes in the room, the rest, which 
is classified as moisture emissions, will only contribute if the moisture level results in condensation 
on the windows. The dry heat loss of occupants is estimated at around 100 W per person. (Vorre, et 
al., 2017)  
Finally, solar radiation (⑦) is also an influential factor. If a classroom is oriented towards south, solar 
radiation can result in overheating of the room. The amount of solar radiation entering the room can 
be limited by having windows with a low solar heat gain coefficient, but this can affect the light 
transmission through the window, which in the end affects the visual environment. 
 
2.3 Visual Environment 
2.3.1 Daylight, artificial lighting, and view 
The visual environment describes the daylight, artificial lighting, and the occupants’ view from their 
work area. Normally humans prefer daylight compared to artificial lighting, and therefore the 
placement of the work area is very important (Nielsen, 2016). The daylight in a building is mostly 
dependent on the facade, window area, and in some cases, also the roof, where skylights are placed.  
Artificial lighting is used whenever there is insufficient daylight, to ensure that the task area and 
surroundings are well lit. The lighting should only be turned on if it is needed and should resemble 
natural daylight as much as possible through the color temperature. A high color temperature is often 
perceived as cold light, while a very low color temperature is perceived as warm light. 
 
2.3.2 Illuminance as an indicator for lighting conditions 
The illuminance describes the amount of light falling on a surface per area. It is measured in the unit 
called lux and is usually used as the indicator for the lighting conditions in a room. An outdoor 
illuminance of 1000 lux indicates an overcast sky, while the interval between 32.000 lux and up to 
100.000 lux indicates direct sunlight. The illuminance can be measured with lux meters or 
applications for the phone, where the camera lens is covered with a white membrane.

2.4 Acoustic Environment 
2.4.1 Sound and Noise 
The final indoor environment parameter is the acoustic environment. Especially in classrooms the 
acoustic environment is important to consider, as there are many occupants gathered in a single 
room. The pupils should be able to concentrate, listen and comprehend what the teacher is saying.  
Sound travels through rooms as waves and reflects on surfaces. Inside rooms there are two 
distinguishments, direct sound and reverberation. The direct sound is sound waves from the source

2.4.2 Sound pressure level and reverberation time as indicators 
Sound can be measured as the sound pressure level with the unit decibel (dB). The decibel scale is 
a logarithmic scale where every increase of 3 dB is a doubling of the sound. The A-weighting is 
introduced to approximate the way humans hear. The sound pressure level is used as a simple 
indicator where 60 dB(A) is considered normal speech, measuring at the mouth of the speaker. The 
reverberation time is an indicator of how many seconds it takes for a sound to be reduced by 60 dB. 
Usually, the quicker the sound is reduced the better is the acoustic environment at dampening the 
sound.  
 
2.4.3 Sources of noise and solutions to reduce the noise level 
It is important to distinguish between what is unwanted noise and what is informative sound. Too 
much dampening of the noise level can affect a pupil’s comprehension of the information from when 
the teacher is speaking (Maagaard, et al., 2021). Figure 6 presents some of the factors affecting the

2.5 Indoor Environment influence on performance and well-being 
In this section, the indoor environment influence on performance, well-being and absenteeism in 
schools are investigated through a literature study. Each indoor environment parameter has their 
own section.  
 
2.5.1 Relationship between indoor air quality and performance and well-being 
In a study by Shendell, et al., (2004), the influence of a high CO2 concentration on student attendance 
in American schools in Washington and Idaho were investigated. It was found that a 1000 ppm 
increase in CO2 could be associated with a relative 10% to 20% increase in student absence. Toftum, 
et al., (2011)  describes that this corresponds to a decrease in the absence between 2.5% and 5% 
in Danish schools, which would correspond to the Swedish school standards. 
In another study by Kjeldsen, et al., (2013) it was found that there could be an association between 
the student absence and type of ventilation. The study showed that the student absence was higher 
in schools with natural ventilation (4.4%) compared to schools with supply and exhaust (4.3%), and 
schools with only exhaust (4.2%). 
Two studies in Denmark and one in England showed that pupils would perform better in terms of 
speed in solving simple mathematical problems with an increased ventilation rate. The studies were 
later confirmed in large scale experiments in schools in USA (Wargocki & Wyon, 2016). In the Danish 
study two classrooms at two schools were investigated. The number of correct answers in four 
performance tests increased with 6.3% in addition, 4.8% in number comparison, 3.2% in grammatical 
reasoning and 7.4% in reading and comprehension, when the ventilation rate increased from an 
average of 1.7 l/s, pr. person to 6.6 l/s, pr. person (Petersen, et al., 2015). 
Wargocki, et al., (2020) determined a linear relationship between the relative performance and the 
ventilation rate when looking at the interval between 2 and 7.5 l/s pr. person, meaning that a higher 
ventilation rate would lead to a higher relative performance. They also determined a relationship 
between the performance and CO2 concentration described by a 3rd degree polynomial presented in 
Figure 7, where it is seen that the performance decreases with almost 5% when the CO2 
concentration increases from 900 ppm to 2400 ppm. The Optimal performance was assumed to be 
at 900 ppm. The optimal performance for the ventilation rate was at a fresh air supply of 7.5 l/s pr. 
person.

2.5.3 Relationship between performance, well-being, and visual environment 
The lighting level, the variability of the lighting and the view from the pupil’s task area has a great 
influence on the performance and wellbeing. In a study by Barkmann, et al., (2012) the effect of 
variable illuminance on reading speed and comprehension in two classrooms each, in two separate 
schools, was studied over a period of nine months, where one class in each school served as an 
intervention group while the other as a control group. The results showed that the children made 
20.8% less errors with an illuminance of 1000 lux compared to the children in the standard lighting 
room with an illuminance of 300 lux.  
In another field intervention study by Hviid, et al., (2020), 92 pupils, in four classrooms, aged between 
10-12 years, were tested in concentration, logical reasoning, math and processing speed while 
subjected to different ventilation rates and lighting conditions. Several scenarios were set up varying 
ventilation rate and lighting color and the results showed that a combined increase in the illuminance 
(from 450 lux to 750 lux), an increase in lighting temperature (2900 K to 4900 K) and an increase in 
ventilation rate (3.9 l/s, pers to 10.6 l/s, pers) would increase the processing speed (6,6%), 
concentration (8.3%) and math skills (11.8%). 
Mott, et al., (2012) found that when 84 third graders were exposed to focus lighting (1000 lux, 6500 
K) compared to normal lighting (500 lux, 3500 K), reading fluency performance increased with 36%. 
This study suggests that if adjustable lighting is used in classrooms for specific tasks, such as oral 
reading, the performance can be improved. 
Recent research written in the new DS/EN 12464-1:2021 describes that the variability of the 
illuminance and color with seasons and time of day can help enhance people’s well-being. The idea 
is that the variability will resemble the natural light and stimulate the cone photoreceptor inputs to 
specific neurons in the eye that regulates the circadian rhythm, “which makes good lighting” (Nodell, 
2020). The circadian circuitry responds to short wavelength blue, long wavelength oranges and 
yellows and contrasting lights during sunrise and sunset. The standard suggests adjusting the 
lighting so that during periods the lighting level will be higher than the recommended or temporarily 
slightly lower, depending on the time of day. This is especially important for room types occupied in 
extended periods, like classrooms. The variability of light was already introduced in the earlier 
version from 2011 but expanded in the 2021 version with more info.   
With regards to the need of a visual green scenery from the classroom windows, another field 
intervention study was performed by Li & Sullivan (2016) where high school students were assigned 
to random classrooms with or without views onto green landscapes. The students performed normal 
classroom activities and stayed in the classroom during breaks. The study showed that classrooms 
with views onto green landscape caused significantly better performance of tests of attention and 
quicker stress recovery.

2.5.4 Relationship between performance, wellbeing, and the acoustic environment 
In a study by Jamieson, et al., (2004), where 40 young children aged 5-8 years were tested in the 
ability to understand speech while listening in a normal classroom environment, it was shown that 
almost all kids had trouble understanding speech when the noise level was 65 dB(A). However, at 
lower levels only kindergarten and first graders had trouble understanding speech, while in a quiet 
environment all kids performed well. They concluded that the youngest kids were most susceptible 
to the effects of noise, but also tend to be in the noisiest classrooms. 
 
2.6 Self-estimated indoor environmental parameters importance 
Besides the research on how the performance is affected objectively through testing and monitoring 
the occupants, research on self-estimations from occupants are also important to explore. When 
exploring this type of qualitative information, the importance of other factors than the indoor 
environment parameters show up, which is important for the overall satisfactory when performing 
schoolwork or office work.       
Spanning over a period of 10 years a large analysis with 50,000 subjective responses from 
occupants in office buildings in the US, by Wargocki, et al., (2012) showed that satisfaction with the 
workplace and self-estimated performance relied on several of the indoor environment parameters. 
The temperature, noise level, air quality and visual comfort all played a significant role when self-
estimating the performance, while the amount of space was the most important factor when it came 
to workspace satisfaction.

2.7.3 Dimensioning the ventilation rate 
There are three main methods when dimensioning the ventilation rate with focus on the air quality, 
according to the DS/ISO/TR 17772-2:2018 standard. The first is the perceived air quality method, 
which considers the emission from occupants and building materials. The second method calculates 
the ventilation rate based on diluting an individual substance, which for example could be for diluting 
CO2, while the third method is based on pre-defined ventilation rates.  
The ventilation rate can also be dimensioned with focus on the thermal environment which considers 
the need for ventilative cooling. This method is often the one used when dimensioning the ventilation 
rate in larger buildings with many occupants like schools. Often when using this method, the required 
ventilation rates calculated using the three other methods will also be fulfilled.

2.7.5 Evaluating the thermal environment 
The thermal environment can be evaluated with the PMV and PPD indices. The PMV (Predicted 
Mean Vote) index provides an estimation of the thermal sensation for the body as a whole. The PPD

occupants in a room. The indices and the calculation of them are thoroughly described in DS/EN

the sensation of feeling cold, and +3 is the sensation of feeling hot, 0 is a neutral sensation. The 
PPD is described as a function of the PMV and is lowest when the PMV is 0 and highest when the 
PMV is either -3 or +3, meaning there is a large amount of people being dissatisfied among a large 
group. Table 6 shows the minimum and maximum values for each climate category given in the

The indoor environment can be summarized by the recommendations based on the TAIL index. This

2.9 Socioeconomical advantages with an improved indoor environment 
The performance, well-being, and health of pupils in their primary school years can have 
consequences on their educational possibilities later in life if exposed to an unacceptable indoor 
climate throughout their school years. It has already been explored how different indoor environment 
parameters affects pupils’ performance and well-being negatively, and in the following the 
socioeconomical aspect will be the focus.    
Compared to Sweden and Norway, the number of Danish classrooms which exceed a CO2 
concentration of 1000 ppm for longer periods is too high. In a collaboration between Slotsholm, DTU 
and DREAM-Gruppen it was estimated that an increased ventilation rate also increased the results 
of PISA-scores with 10 points (Slotsholm A/S, 2012). They refer to an OECD-study from 2010 which 
showed that countries with better PISA-scores also experience a larger growth. According to their 
calculations an increase in fresh air supply in the Danish classrooms, so that it matches the Swedish 
classrooms, will cause a yearly BNP growth of 1.3 billion kr., and an improvement of the public 
finances of 270 million kr. 
In a comprehensive study about socioeconomical advantages with an improved indoor climate by 
Madsen, et al., (2019), a literature study was made on what effect an increasing education time had 
on pupils’ acquisition. They found that there was a correlation between the two, and that an 
increasing education time also increased learning. One study by Bingley, et al., (2018) showed that 
when education time was increased with 4.6% from 1st to 9th grade, the result was a 2,8% increase 
in final grade. Another study by Aucejo & Romano (2016) showed that when increasing the 
educational year by 10 days, test results increased with 0.8-1.7%. However, the same study showed 
that when reducing absence by 10 days, test results increased with 2.9-5.5%, meaning that focusing

2.10 Responsibility for indoor environment in schools 
The public schools are institutions belonging to the municipalities. The municipal council ensures 
that all children, below the age of 18, in the municipality, can receive free education in the public 
schools. The municipal council makes decisions regarding the economic boundaries, school 
structure, school districts and the general inclusion with the local communities. 
In every public school a group of representatives are elected as the school board, who takes care of 
the school interests. The board consists of representatives from the parents, the teachers, the pupils 
and sometimes up to two stakeholders from the local community. The school board is an 
intermediate communication step from the parents to the local council member in the Children and 
Youth Administration. The school board approves the school budgets and educational resources. 
According to the Public-School Law, the school board can operate within the limits and goals given 
by the city council. It is their job to monitor all parts of the school, except personal cases. They can 
give statements and ask questions to the city council about the school and the other way around. 
(Ministry of Children and Education) 
In Odense the school boards consist of 7 parents, 2 teachers and 2 pupils. The school board election 
takes place the year after the local elections (Kommunalvalg) which means that the school board is 
elected to a 4-year period.

with the preparation of improving the indoor climate in schools. Odense was one of the municipalities 
participating and has developed a model which includes the different stakeholders and the

3 Method 
This chapter presents the methodology used in the process of gathering data, analyzing, and 
presenting them. Section 3.1 describes the large-scale experiment of 2021, section 3.2 describes 
the data-processing of the data gathered from the large-scale experiment, section 3.3 describes 
three case-studies where long-term measurements were done in six classrooms, and section 3.4 
describes the mapping of school renovations in Odense.  
The following software was used for data processing: Spyder (python), Microsoft Excel and 
HOBOware.    
   
3.1 The Large-scale experiment of 2021 
The large-scale experiment took place in November 2021 with 321 schools, distributed over 1227 
teams participating, amounting to around 13% of the schools in the country. The experiment was 
performed using the same instruments as the previous large-scale experiments from 2009 and 2014. 
The instruments were packed into kits which were sent to the individual schools. Astra was in charge 
of this year’s experiment.  
 
3.1.1 Large-scale experiment details 
The pupils were the central point during the experiment and had to perform physical measurements 
to map the indoor climate in the classrooms. They also completed a survey, where they evaluated 
their own perceived health and well-being, as well as a concentration test to measure their 
performance. The teachers answered a small questionnaire on the overall physical appearance of 
their classroom. The measurements and surveys were done two times spanning two days with a 
week’s separation.  
The experiment was set up, so that some teams started with one type of behavior, and other teams 
started with another type of behavior. The two types will be referred to as day 1 and day 8.   
On day 1 the class had to act as usual with no changes to their indoor environment. 
On day 8 all the pupils had to be outside the classroom before the lecture. During that time, windows 
had to be open but closed again when the lecture started.  
The physical measurements included measuring the CO2 concentration, the operative temperature, 
the illuminance in four zones in different scenarios, the sound pressure level, and reverberation time. 
Guidelines and validation of the apps and measuring equipment used in the experiment were done 
by (Nielsen & Bruun Nielsen, 2020) in their bachelor project in the spring of 2020. The physical 
measurements were reported in a formular on Astra’s website.   
  
3.1.2 New in this experiment 
Compared to the large-scale experiments from 2009 and 2014, this time the pupils had to complete 
more measurements, making it a more thorough study on the indoor environment parameters 
compared to earlier. The performance test and well-being surveys were also new in this experiment, 
to study the correlation between the indoor environment, performance, and well-being.

3.1.3 Building checklist  
As a part of the experiment the teacher filled in a formular with the characteristics of the classroom. 
It included things such as the size of the classroom, the orientation, types of lighting, ventilation 
method, and more. These parameters were used when analyzing the study results as they each may 
have had an influence on the data. 
 
3.1.4 Methodology for the physical measurements 
The CO2 concentration was measured using a 100 ml plastic syringe connected to a plastic pipe, 
which, on the other end, was connected to a CO2 measurement pipe. The plastic syringe had to be 
empty of air when the measurement started. The plunger was then slowly pulled until 50 ml was 
shown on the scale of the plastic syringe, this would take approximately one minute. Immediately 
after, the CO2 concentration was read on the measurement pipe. The concentration was multiplied 
by two as only 50 ml was pulled into the syringe instead of 100 ml. The final concentration was then 
noted in the formular on the website.  
The operative temperature was estimated using a tennis ball, a temperature strip, and a small 
spear. The temperature strip was taped onto the tennis ball, which was attached to the end of the 
small spear. The setup was taped to the back of a chair and left for a few minutes. The globe 
temperature could then be read on the temperature strip and noted in the formular.  
At low air velocities and when the difference between the mean radiant temperature and the air 
temperature is lower than 10 K, the globe temperature is a good estimate of the operative 
temperature.  
The lighting level was measured using the Swedish app called “ljus” for Iphone. It was chosen 
amongst other apps to be the most accurate according to (Nielsen & Bruun Nielsen, 2020), and was 
validated using the Extech SDL400 Light Meter. The app showed small deviations as seen in Table 
10, which shows the average deviation at different lux intervals compared to the Extech Light Meter.

A total of 17 measurements were done inside the classroom, four in each zone for each scenario,

The sound pressure level was measured using an app called “Noise exposure”. Validation of the

blackboard in the classroom. When the measurement ended, the app calculated the average sound 
pressure level which was then noted in the formular. 
The reverberation time was measured using the app “APM tool lite” and balloons provided in the kit. 
The app measured how long it took for the sound to decay by 20 dB. The balloons were inflated so 
they approximately had an equal volume of air inside. The balloons were blown individually at 
different locations, resulting in five measurements. The app then calculated the average 
reverberation time (T20). The shown number was then noted in the formular on the website.

3.2.4 Performance Test 
The performance test dataset contained of more than 24.000 submissions from pupils. For each 
pupil the Team-ID, their pupil number, and date and time was recorded when completing the test, 
making it possible to follow their performance on both days.  
A Python script was created to sort the data. The script removed exact duplicate submissions and 
looked for places where a pupil had repeatedly submitted either “True” or “False” on all statements 
as they were deemed an invalid submission. The “True” or “False” was changed to the binary 
numbers 1 and 0 for easier comparison and evaluation of the data.  
The script then merged the performance test results with the physical measurements depending on 
whether it found a relation between the date and time of the two submissions. This relation was 
based on that the state of the indoor climate for the physical measurement’s submission and the 
performance test was only comparable if they were both submitted within a certain time of each 
other. It was chosen that the physical measurements could not be submitted more than half a day 
before the performance test was taken but could be submitted by up to two days after.  
The two-day boundary was selected based on that it was found to be the average of the minimum 
number of days between the physical measurements for the two scenarios. It was expected that the 
classes performed the two scenarios with a week separation in between, but as this was not the 
case, this decision was made.

3.4 Mapping of school renovations in Odense 
To get an overview of the condition of the schools in Odense, a large study of renovation tasks from 
the past years were conducted. Excel was used to keep track of the renovation tasks from 2013-
2021. To gather the data, several sources were used. 
 
3.4.1 Weblager (Web archive) 
Weblager is a large website which many municipalities use to archive building cases. This includes 
both public and private building projects. These cases could be locked if they contained sensitive 
personal data but was accessible with a login.  
The responsible in Odense Municipality was contacted in September 2021 and in the end of October 
2021 it was possible to get a login. Only four schools could be accessed at a time, which meant that 
once the cases for the first four schools were studied, four new schools were accessible, and so on. 
When searching for any building project, either the address, cadastral number, or property number 
was needed. Once this was known, a list of cases would show for the specific property. The cases 
corresponded to larger projects on the property since the construction year, which included some 
renovation cases. 
When studying the cases, tasks specifically aimed at indoor environment changes were noted. This 
included the type of task and year of the issued permit. It was noticed that not all schools had cases 
on every change and supplementing with other sources was needed. 
 
3.4.2 Odense City Council meeting summaries  
The city council writes meeting summaries after every meeting. The summaries included 
construction budgets for the schools. Every meeting summary from 2013 to 2021 was investigated 
with the focus on four keywords: ‘School (Skole)’, ‘Construction (Anlæg)’, ‘Renovation (Renovering)’ 
and ‘Modernization (Modernisering)’. When a match was found, it was investigated if it was a relevant 
renovation task. If yes, the type and year was noted. The finds were also compared to the list of 
already found tasks from the web archive to ensure that no tasks were written twice. 
As the meeting summaries included construction budgets and initiatives from past school 
renovations from 2013, it was possible to get an overview of what goals, initiatives and construction 
budgets the municipality had planned for the schools. When specific schools were mentioned, it was 
noted, and to which larger project it belonged to, e.g., Velfærdens Fundament.

4.10 Summary of large-scale experiment results 
From the large-scale experiment 2021, it was seen that the CO2 concentration was still too high in 
most of the classrooms. Only 48% of the classrooms participating in the experiment had a CO2 
concentration below the recommended 1000 ppm. It was seen that when the windows were opened 
before the lecture started, the measured CO2 concentration was below the recommended 1000 ppm 
in 66% of the classrooms showing an improvement in the air quality. 
The temperature measurements showed that the operative temperature was within the 
recommended 21-25 ˚C in 80% of the classes when measuring the normal conditions in the 
classrooms. This percentage decreased to 68% when the windows were open before the lecture, 
which meant that the thermal environment became worse. 
The measured illuminance when lighting was on and there was no solar shading showed that in 38% 
of the classrooms, the average illuminance was below the minimum recommended 300 lux. The 
view from the classrooms were mostly school yards and playgrounds, but there were also many 
classes who reported, that they had views onto green scenery.  
The average sound pressure level was measured in the classrooms and the results showed that 
there was too much noise in 97% of the classrooms. 
The individual indoor environment parameters showed no direct linear correlation with the 
performance test results, where more than 50% had 23 out of 24 correct answers on both days, 
indicating that the pupils did not find the test very difficult. On day 1, the pupils had an average of 
89,8% correct answers and on day 8, the pupils had an average of 90,2% correct answers indicating 
that there is a correlation, but it could be a multivariate correlation between several parameters. The 
girls performed better than the boys, while the average performance generally increased the older 
the pupils were.  
The indoor environment had an influence on the well-being survey. Pupils found the air quality worse 
when the CO2 concentration was higher, and it was seen that when the windows had been open 
before the lecture, more pupils found it too cold in the classroom compared to the normal behavior. 
There was no clear correlation between the calculated average illuminance and the perceived 
lighting in the classroom, but it was seen that when there was an increase in the measured sound 
pressure level, the pupils perceived the noise level in the classroom to be worse. 
There also seemed to be a small correlation between the well-being survey and the performance 
test. There was generally a lower performance accuracy when the pupils evaluated that they had a 
headache, had tired eyes, could not concentrate, or did not want to perform.

7 Discussion 
In the following some of the main themes of this thesis will be discussed as well as the main results 
and the associated sources of error.   
 
7.1 Literature research 
In general, when doing literature research, it was important to read through the discussions of the 
individual studies and investigate the conditions of which their findings were based on.   
In the relationship that was found between the performance and CO2 concentration proposed by 
Wargocki et al. (2020), it was written that it was a crude estimate, and it should not be applied to 
dynamic or variable conditions. The relationship was based on the assumption of a steady state 
concentration in the classroom, good air mixing, sedentary activity and was only valid between the 
range of 900 ppm and 2400 ppm. However, classrooms seemed to be dynamic environments when 
investigating the long-term measurements for the six classrooms, which meant that the relationship 
was difficult to use as a comparison. The study was rather used as an indication that there is a 
relationship between the performance and the air quality, and an increasing CO2 concentration 
influences it negatively. The same thing could be discussed in the found relationship between 
classroom temperature and performance found by Wargocki, et al., (2019). It was also written that it 
was a crude estimate which worked under given conditions.  
Both relationships were used in the new industry guideline by Maagaard, et al., (2021)  where it was 
extrapolated that the performance would increase to almost 110% if the temperature decreased 
below 20 ˚C in the classroom. This seemed unlikely since the relationship would probably follow a 
U-shape, meaning that the performance would decrease with lower temperatures. Figure 50 also 
showed that up to 22% of the pupils felt too cold when the operative temperature went below 20 ˚C 
on day 8. The optimal performance that was set arbitrarily in the studies were set differently in the 
industry guideline, where they wrote that the optimal performance was set at 22 ˚C and 1000 ppm. 
In Wargocki, et al., (2019) and (2020) they were set to 20 ˚C and 900 ppm.  
The industry guideline also concluded that it was more beneficial to focus on the thermal environment 
by decreasing the temperature with 1˚C instead of decreasing the CO2 concentration 100 ppm, but 
this depends on the season. During the summer period it is probably true, since that is where the 
thermal environment has its ‘worst case scenario’ with high temperatures, but during winter the 
atmospheric environment has its ‘worst case scenario’ since the windows are usually closed, and in 
that situation the air quality should also be a priority. 
Regarding the socioeconomical consequences of a better indoor environment, the Coronavirus has 
changed the way people work. In Madsen, et al., (2019) it was described that when a pupil in 1st – 
6th grade was sick for one day it would correspond to a 0,31 day decrease in labor supply. However, 
this could correspond to an even lower decrease now since more parents have gotten used to work 
from home due to Coronavirus lockdowns. Of course, this only relates to the type of work, which can 
be done from home.

7.3 Large-scale experiment results 
The large-scale experiment on indoor environment was repeated for the third time since 2009. It is 
important to emphasize that in the experiments of 2009 and 2014 the same methods were used to 
measure the CO2 concentration and temperature, which meant that the experiments were 
comparable for these parameters. It is also important to remember that this experiment was 
completed during the winter season, while the other two were completed in the autumn. The overall 
results might have looked different if this experiment also was completed during autumn.   
The results of the measurements and the evaluation of them were unconditionally based on trust 
that the pupils and teachers performed them methodically correct. Since there was no supervision 
from experts, there was no way of knowing whether they were done correctly or not. Several sources 
of error turned up when analyzing the results. 
Firstly, the submitted values for the average reverberation time turned out to vary a lot. It was 
determined that realistic reverberation time values would be between 0 to 1 second, and everything 
above would be deleted. The resulting data spanned from everything in between 0 to 90 seconds, 
and many classes reported values above 1 second. Nielsen & Bruun Nielsen (2020) described in 
their bachelor project that the app was sensitive to when the phone was moved around to the four 
positions in the classroom. Their results also showed that when testing the app, it would typically 
show reverberation times of more than 1 second in some octave bands, while it was measured to 
under a second using a control software. This could have been the cause of the large number of 
unrealistic measurements. It also must be pointed out that the measurements for the average sound 
pressure level took place in a lecture where the pupils walked around and did measurements, talking 
to each other, which possibly does not reflect an average lecture, where they might sit still and listen 
to the teacher.    
The next parameter which seemed to cause issues was the measurements of the illuminance. In the 
experiment the teams had to perform 17 measurements on both days. Four measurements in four 
scenarios and one outside. In the data-processing phase it was decided that all measurements below 
1 lux would be discarded and that there was no upper limit. The decision not to have an upper limit 
==PAGE:90==

83 of 106 
was based on the fact that if the sun was shining directly on the device, and the illuminance was 
measured at the window, it was expected to see a large number. This also meant that it was hard to 
pinpoint non-measured values that was inputted just for the sake of writing something in the formular. 
Values such as ‘1234...’ were removed as it was expected these were non-measured values, but 
other than that, it was difficult to determine. However, when investigating the results, it seemed that 
the values made sense compared to the different scenarios. Scenario A showed the largest number 
of classes within the recommended illuminances compared to the other scenarios, which made 
sense, since there was no solar shading, and the lighting was on. 
The measurements of the CO2 concentration included that the pupils had to multiply the reading of 
the syringe by two. Some schools emailed Astra with a message saying that they forgot to multiply 
the reading. These measurements were changed manually, but there was an unknown number of 
classes who never contacted Astra if they forgot to multiply by two, or classes who did not realize 
that they forgot it. This has probably resulted in some schools reporting CO2 measurements that 
were half as much as it was supposed to be. 
 
7.3.1 Performance test and well-being survey  
In the performance test the pupils had an average of around 90% (21-22 out of 24) correct answers 
on both days. Only 1% of the pupils had less than 12 out of 24 correct answers. This indicated that 
the pupils found the performance test easy. This could also be the reason why the individual indoor 
environment parameters did not seem to influence the pupils as much as what was expected. The 
largest improvements from day 1 to day 8 were seen on the two hardest questions where there was 
an improvement of 2,3% and 1,7%. If the performance test had reflected these harder questions, it 
was possible that a larger effect of the air quality improvement could have been seen. This was 
uncertain as there were many other factors influencing the performance, as was seen in the results 
from the well-being survey. There was a slight increase in pupils with below 75% correct answers 
when they e.g., had a headache, did not want to perform, or could not concentrate. 
A significant source of error was the assumed illuminance when the pupils completed performance 
test and well-being survey. The illuminance was calculated based on whether it was written in the 
measurement submission that the sun was out or not, and the lighting was on or off. From the data 
there was no way of knowing which scenario was actually used while they were doing the test and 
survey. 1024 out of 1278 had stated that the lighting was on, and the sun was not out during the 
physical measurements, which meant that the average illuminance of scenario A used the most in 
the comparison with the performance test and well-being survey.   
The second source of error by using that method was that it was the average illuminance in the four 
zones. However, the illuminance could have been very different in the zones closest to the window, 
compared to the illuminance in the other side of the classroom.  
Also, the possible reason why the lighting did not influence the performance that much could be due 
to the pupils using laptops or tablets to do the test. These probably have a well-lit screen, which 
ensures that it is possible to see what is on there even though the classroom might have had an 
average illuminance below 300 lux.    
There was a total of 13 well-being survey questions. The questions asked the pupils how they were 
feeling “right now”, which also could have led to some issues when comparing some of them with 
the measurements. Mainly one of the questions was interesting, which was about the noise in the 
classroom. The noise level was measured as an average over the entire lecture, which indicated the 
==PAGE:91==

general noise level in the classroom, but it can be assumed that while the well-being survey was 
completed there would be quiet in the classroom since everyone were concentrating on that. It meant 
that the relation between the average sound pressure level and the perceived noise level could be 
wrong, even though it showed a correlation.  
 
7.3.2 Coronavirus and other complications 
During the experiment of 2021 the coronavirus was a great factor when it came to the number of 
classes responding to the different tasks. It was seen that out of the 1227 registered classes only 
371 (30%) finished all the tasks. In a feedback letter about the experiment from Astra (Appendix 
13.1) it was written that many teachers had been absent due to illness with coronavirus and that 
many teachers could not overcome doing the experiment since there were so many other things they 
had to respond to.   
Some teachers from the small grades also wrote that the experiment was too difficult to do, which 
meant that they gave their spot to a class with older pupils.  
Unless this was informed to Astra there was no way of knowing from the data, that a grade of older 
pupils had performed the measurements and tests for the younger pupils. However, Astra has done 
a lot to help and guide the teachers via phone and e-mail.  
 
7.3.3 Large-scale experiment results for Odense schools 
Unfortunately, very few of the registered Odense classes finished the experiment. Of 42 registered 
classes only 18 submitted the physical measurements for the experiment. The results generally 
showed that the indoor environment was slightly better in the schools compared to the national 
results.  
  
7.4 Long-term measurements 
In the evaluation of the long-term measurements, it was decided that the occupancy hours were from 
8-15 based on the industry guideline (Maagaard, et al., 2021). Since this could vary from day to day 
in the schools, the figures which were supposed to only include occupied hours, consisted of 
measurements from outside the occupancy hours as well. This did not change the principle of the 
outcome of the results much, since the results still showed that the classroom without mechanical 
supply and exhaust had the worst indoor environment and the renovated classrooms with supply 
and exhaust had the best indoor environment. 
 
7.5 Mapping renovations tasks and budgets for Odense schools

schools. They found that the year of 2014 was the year with most renovation tasks from the period 
of 2005-2016, which was the direct opposite of what was found in this thesis. Their sources were

8 Conclusion 
 
86 of 106 
8 Conclusion 
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate whether the indoor environment influenced pupil 
performance and well-being. It mapped the indoor environment of more than 600 classes who 
participated in the large-scale experiment of 2021, where pupils completed physical measurements, 
a performance test, and a well-being survey. The results showed that only 48% of the participating 
classes measured an indoor air quality below the threshold of 1000 ppm, when the pupils acted 
normally. When windows were opened and no pupils were in the classroom during recess, the air 
quality improved. The CO2 concentration was then measured to be below the threshold in 66% of 
the participating classes. It was seen that the classrooms with a CO2 concentration higher than 1000 
ppm were primarily classes using natural ventilation, while the classes below were primarily using 
supply and exhaust.  
The operative temperature was within the recommended limits between 21-25 ˚C in 80% of the 
classrooms when the pupils acted normally, and 68% of the classrooms when the pupils had been 
outside during recess and the windows were open. This showed that the atmospheric environment 
improved while the thermal environment worsened. The average illuminance was within the 
recommended limits of 300-2000 lux in 60% of the classrooms while the lighting was on, and 35% 
of the classrooms when the lighting was off, showing that an improvement in the lighting was needed 
to ensure better lighting in classrooms. The noise level was the worst performing parameter showing 
that only 3% of the classrooms measured an average noise level within the recommended 30-50 
dB(A), the rest measured above 50-70 dB(A) and 70-80 dB(A). 
There was no clear direct linear correlation between the individual indoor environment parameters 
and pupil performance in the experiment. However an improvement in the performance from 89,8% 
on day 1 to 90,2% on day 8 showed that there could possibly be a multivariate correlation with 
several parameters instead of individually. For every grade, on both days, the girls performed better 
than the boys, and the performance generally increased with the age of pupils.  The results from the 
well-being survey showed that there was a correlation between the perceived indoor environment 
and some of the measured parameters. The results showed that when the CO2 concentration was 
high, more pupils perceived the indoor air quality as bad. The same thing was seen on day 8, where 
the windows were open during recess. More pupils felt slightly cold when the operative temperature 
was below 20 ˚C. Finally, there was a slight correlation between the perceived well-being of pupils 
and their performance. Pupils who responded that they had a headache, did not want to perform, or 
could not concentrate, had a slightly lower performance compared to pupils who did not have a 
headache, wanted to perform, or could easily concentrate.  
  
Based on the indoor environment in the participating schools in Odense and long-term 
measurements in six classrooms it was shown that there were still schools in the municipality where 
the indoor environment was inadequate compared to today’s standards. The long-term 
measurements showed that a balanced mechanical ventilation system ensured a better indoor air 
quality compared to the classrooms without ventilation. Improving the air quality by replacing 
ventilation systems were one of the strategies Odense Municipality used to achieve their vision of 
having significantly less schools with a high CO2 concentration and less noise in 2025. The mapping 
of renovation tasks in Odense also showed that in the years 2021-2023 all the 34 public schools 
would go through extensive renovations in the large project called Velfærdens Fundament where 
500 million kr. were allocated for the purpose.

9 Future research and recommendations 
 
87 of 106 
9 Future research and recommendations 
9.1 Research with the current data 
- 
Multivariate analysis 
Since there was no clear linear correlation between the individual indoor environment parameters, 
but there was an improvement in performance from day 1 to day 8, there could possibly be a 
multivariate correlation between several parameters. A multivariate analysis was not done in this 
thesis but could show the results from a bigger perspective.  
 
- 
Daylight and lighting 
Since the data provides a lot of measurements with different scenarios of the daylight and lighting 
conditions in the classrooms it is obvious to look further into this. The classes also mentioned what 
type of lighting they used, which way the windows were oriented and what type of scenery was 
outside the classroom.  
         
- 
Difference between private schools and public schools 
The data could be used to research whether there is a difference between the indoor environment 
generally measured in private schools compared to public schools. This thesis described who has 
the responsibility for the indoor environment in public schools, but it mapped the indoor environment 
for both private schools and public schools. It would be interesting to see how exactly private schools 
prioritize the indoor environment, and what the differences are when investigating these results.  
  
9.2 Future large-scale experiments 
In the large-scale experiment of 2021, there seemed to be many tasks compared to the previous 
years. This was primarily due to the large number of measurements for the illuminance in the 
classroom. The focus on the indoor environment influence on performance and well-being could 
mean that the measurements should reflect the conditions which the pupil is in at the exact moment 
of doing the test and survey.  
However, it is also important to remember that these experiments are also made for the purpose of 
teaching the pupils about indoor environment, with hands-on experience.  
To strengthen the expected correlation between the indoor environment and performance, the test 
could be changed, or the difficulty could be increased. The well-being survey question regarding the 
noise level could be based on how noisy it has been during the lecture instead of currently. 
The guide for the app measuring the reverberation time in the classroom might have to be revised 
to get more accurate submissions. 
Since the outdoor temperature can have an influence on the indoor environment and on the well-
being of the pupils, it could be an interesting parameter to measure. It could be investigated whether 
winter and summer conditions had a negative or positive influence on their well-being.
