The continental slope region north of Svalbard is a highly advective and dynamic area where
the warm and saline Atlantic Water (AW) meets the cold and fresh Polar Surface Water of
Arctic origin. This thesis analyses mooring data comprising hydrography, currents (2012-
2015 and 2019-2022), and dissolved oxygen (DO) and pH (2019-2020 and 2021-2022) from
the 800 m isobath at 81.54 âĶN, 30.86 âĶE to characterise seasonal and inter-annual variability
in DO and pH, and relate it to the physical forcing. Hydrographic measurements reveal
a cold and fresh surface layer overlaying a warm and saline AW layer. The AW is most
pronounced in winter (September to January), where stronger inïŽows create thicker AW
layers covering the majority of the water column. Beneath the main AW layer cooled and
modiïŽed Atlantic water masses reside. Five main drivers of variability in DO and pH are
identiïŽed to be photosynthesis, respiration, water masses and circulation, solubility, and air-
sea gas exchange. The pH has sparse and less robust data, but the available data suggests
that the variability is mainly based on water mass diïŽerences with lower pH in the AW due
to higher anthropogenic dissolved inorganic carbon in the source waters. The DO shows
clearer shifts in drivers, and exhibits strong seasonality in the near-surface. During the dark
and ice covered period the dominant driver is water mass eïŽects, and associated temperature
dependent solubility. The bloom causes the strongest increase in DO. The onset of the bloom
is detected in late June in 2019-2020 and early July in 2021-2022. In both years the area
is still ice covered, and the under ice bloom is driven by the light availability penetrating
through the shrinking ice cover. In the measurement closest to the surface (2019-2020) the
bloom increases the DO concentration to an annual maximum in mid-August of 495 Âĩmol/L
and supersaturates the water to 132%. After the peak of the bloom the increased respiration
from the higher concentration of organic matter drives the DO values close to winter values.
The deep part of the measurements show less seasonality driven variability. The main drivers
are water masses and circulation. Episodic inïŽows of more Atlantic like water masses brings
more DO, but lower pH into the area. This highlights the dual role of AW at these depths. The
increased inïŽow of AW to the Arctic regions, and the subsequent AtlantiïŽcation, are projected
to increase in the coming decades. Additionally, Arctic ampliïŽcation causes temperatures
to rise faster than the global average. The continental slope of Svalbard is a sentinel to
the perturbations this will pose on the biogeochemistry. The results of this thesis support,
that the increased inïŽow of AW have acidifying eïŽects on the waters, and that increasing
temperatures cause a solubility driven decrease in DO. This multi-parameter time series
analysis, contributes to the monitoring of changes occurring, and being projected to occur,
during the coming decades in the Arctic Ocean.

Introduction
The Arctic is experiencing rapid changes in the marine environment through multiple inter-
connected processes. Air temperatures are increasing faster than the global average, a process
referred to as Arctic AmpliïŽcation (Serreze and Barry, 2011). The sea ice cover is declining
and thinning within the Arctic Ocean (Stroeve and Notz, 2018). The inïŽow of Atlantic Water
(AW) is increasing, causing an AtlantiïŽcation of the Arctic and weakening the cold halocline
layer, which has previously insulated the surface waters from the underlying AW (Polyakov
et al., 2017).
The ocean plays a vital role in mitigating climate change by taking up 25-30% of the
anthropogenic carbon dioxide (CO2) emitted to the atmosphere (Le QuÃĐrÃĐ et al., 2016). The
uptake of anthropogenic CO2 has caused a decrease in the ocean pH (Chierici and Fransson,
2009) by 0.1 since the industrial revolution (Doney et al., 2009). The cold polar waters are
an especially eïŽective carbon sink for atmospheric CO2 due to the higher solubility of CO2
in cold water. However, the Arctic Ocean is also particularly vulnerable towards lowering pH
both due to the higher solubility of CO2 in colder waters, as well as due to the naturally lower
alkalinity in freshwater from melting sea ice, glacial melt, precipitation and river run-oïŽ
(Jones et al., 2023). As the Arctic Ocean takes up more CO2 the buïŽering capacity will
decrease, causing a greater eïŽect on the pH. The Arctic Ocean has already experienced the
largest pH decrease globally (Garcia-Soto et al., 2021). The buïŽering mechanisms are related
to the calcium carbonate (CaCO3) saturation, which decreases with ocean acidiïŽcation, which
threatens calcifying organisms. Projections indicate that the high latitude oceans will be the
ïŽrst to become undersaturated with respect to CaCO3 (Orr et al., 2005).
At the same time the Arctic Ocean is experiencing an increasing inïŽuence of AW entering
through Fram Strait and the Barents Sea (Polyakov et al., 2017). These waters bring heat, salt,
nutrients (Renner et al., 2023) and higher levels of anthropogenic CO2 (Ericson et al., 2014;
Ulfsbo et al., 2018) into the area. Presently, the melt water induced surface waters are markedly
fresher, and uphold a strong halocline, which insulates the AW layer from the surface, and
hence the sea ice and the atmospheric interface. However, the halocline is observed to be
weakening (Polyakov et al., 2017). If this stratiïŽcation breaks down, the heat stemming from
the AW would be enough to melt the entirety of the Arctic sea ice (Timmermans and Marshall,
2020). The nutrients provided by the AW, and the disappearance of sea ice both inïŽuence
the primary production (Renner et al., 2023), which has cascading eïŽects throughout the
ecosystem (SÃļreide et al., 2010).
Changes in the physical environment have implications for the biogeochemistry, and pH
and dissolved oxygen (DO) are valuable indicators of the state of the marine environment.
pH is an indicator of the carbonate chemistry and the eïŽects related to ocean acidiïŽcation.
The DO concentration is driven largely by biological processes such as photosynthesis and
respiration, as well as physical processes including ventilation and mixing. In a changing
Arctic, it is crucial to understand the interplay between physical drivers and biogeochemical
responses to understand how the marine environment will develop in the future.
The area north of Svalbard is a sentinel to the Arctic Ocean and can be used as an indicator
for the future changes of the Central Arctic Ocean. It is a dynamic and highly advective area
with inïŽowing AW from Fram Strait, which encounters the colder and fresher polar waters
(Lundesgaard et al., 2022). As the area is seasonally ice covered, it is diïŽcult to access, and
time series measurements from the area are scarce.

2.2
Dissolved oxygen
In the ocean, DO can be altered through physical, chemical and biological processes. In the
Arctic Ocean this is additionally inïŽuenced by the presence of sea ice, the strong halocline,
and circulation.
2.2.1
Physical processes
Oxygen can enter the ocean at the surface through air-sea gas exchange (Garcia-Soto et al.,
2021). Under ideal conditions, an equilibrium would be established between the air and
the ocean, and the surface waters would become fully saturated. The concentration of DO
in the surface waters would hence equate the solubility. The solubility depends mainly on
temperature, salinity, and atmospheric pressure (Eveleth et al., 2014). Increased temperature
and salinity both decrease solubility, whereas increased atmospheric pressure increases the
solubility. However, the gas exchange does not always equilibrate, since this takes â 10-14
days in the open ocean, and other processes might alter the water or its position on shorter
time scales (Eveleth et al., 2014). Additionally, sea ice functions as a semi-permeable cap
between the ocean and the atmosphere, and hence limits air-sea gas exchange. However,
partial equilibration is occurring through channels in the ice, and in the vicinity of leads and
open water (Timmermans et al., 2010).
The dissolved oxygen saturation (DOsat) is a measure of the dissolved oxygen concentration
(DOconc) relative to the solubility, and the fully equilibrated surface water would have a DOsat
of 100%, if no other processes occurred. Since air-sea gas exchange only happens at the
surface, the surface waters generally have higher DOsat, than deeper waters.
However,
circulation and vertical mixing can ventilate the deeper waters, and the DOsat can thus be
used as an indicator of the time since the water mass was last in contact with the atmosphere
(Eveleth et al., 2014).

Globally the oxygen levels are decreasing, and the main driver for oxygen loss at the
surface is the decrease in solubility due to warming of the ocean and hence decreased air-sea
gas exchange of oxygen (Garcia-Soto et al., 2021). The main drivers in the Arctic Ocean
have been found to be solubility and stratiïŽcation, and more locally around Svalbard there is
a greater inïŽuence by multi-/decadal variability and changes in source waters (Stramma and
Schmidtko, 2021).
2.2.2
Biological processes
Biological processes have a major impact on the DO. Primary production at the surface
produces oxygen through photosynthesis, which can cause supersaturation of the surface
waters during the productive season. When there is sea ice coverage this supersaturation
can persist for longer, since the surface is not in direct contact with the atmosphere, which
limits outgassing (Eveleth et al., 2014). The strong halocline in the Arctic Ocean additionally
limits vertical mixing, and causes the supersaturation to be contained mainly within the mixed
layer (Timmermans et al., 2010). Conversely, respiration decreases the DOsat, and is not only
performed by autotrophs, but also by most heterotrophs (Sarmiento and Gruber, 2006). This
means that respiration occurs throughout the year and at all depths. Waters at depth will hence
become undersaturated over time, unless they are ventilated by oxygen-rich surface waters.
The spring bloom in the Arctic is driven by the limitations in light availability due to the
seasonality in solar radiation and the sea ice cover. However, this is given that the nutrients
have been replenished during the winter period of net respiration and deep mixing. The onset
of the bloom is thus tightly linked to the presence and thickness of the ice, as well as potential
snow cover on the ice, as these can all limit the light availability in the pelagic zone. At high
latitudes, the spring bloom often starts as an under ice bloom and then shifts into a pelagic
bloom once the ice starts to melt, which can be as late as July-September (Leu et al., 2011).
2.3
pH
Globally, the pH in seawater is generally between 7.9-8.4 (Pilson, 2012), and therefore the
seawater is alkaline. However, the ocean is becoming less alkaline and has experienced a
global decline in surface pH of 0.1 since the industrial revolution (Doney et al., 2009). This
corresponds to an approximate 30% increase in [H+] (Fransner et al., 2022). The decrease
in pH is primarily driven by the increase in anthropogenic CO2, of which the ocean takes
up 25-30% (Le QuÃĐrÃĐ et al., 2016). However, as the CO2 uptake in the ocean increases, the
buïŽering capacity decreases, and the ocean absorption rate is expected to decrease (Ericson
et al., 2014). The variability in pH is driven by biological primary production and respiration,
physical mixing, air-sea CO2 exchange, and ocean CO2 chemistry (Chierici and Fransson,
2009).
The term ocean acidiïŽcation is used to describe the decrease pH and also in CaCO3
saturation, which amongst others limits the ability of calcifying organisms to maintain their
shells and skeletons (Orr et al., 2005).

Similarly to the DO the CO2 is inïŽuenced by the potentials of air-sea gas exchange. When
the sea-ice melts, the surface is exposed to the atmosphere, causing an increase in the air-sea

the global average (Zeebe and Wolf-Gladrow, 2001).
The AW has a higher level of anthropogenic CO2, than polar waters (Ericson et al., 2014).
The anthropogenic CO2 concentrations within the AW is increasing, and the subsequent
accumulation within the Eurasian Arctic Ocean can be attributed to the Atlantic source
waters. The inïŽow of AW is suggested to have a major impact on the acidiïŽcation of the

Biological processes
Photosynthesis and respiration have opposing eïŽects on CO2 compared to O2.
During
photosynthesis CO2 is absorbed by the primary producers causing an increase in pH, and
conversely respiration releases CO2, thus decreasing the pH (Chierici and Fransson, 2009).
However, the correlation between biological productivity and pH is not linear, since the CO2
reacts in the chain of the carbonate system, and the change in pH is hence largely dependent
on AT.

3.3.3
Chlorophyll-a
Primary producers, such as phytoplankton and microalgae, contain photosynthetic pigments,
with chl-a being the most common and essential for photosynthesis. Since chl-a is present
in all phytoplankton, its concentration can be used as a proxy for phytoplankton biomass
and primary production. Chlorophyll pigments absorb mostly blue and red light and reïŽect
green. When excited by light, chl-a emits a faint ïŽuorescence (Townsend, 2018). The chl-a
concentration is estimated through measuring this ïŽuorescence. The instrument sends out a
beam of light, and detects the returning light, which is emitted by the excited particles. From
this the chl-a concentration is estimated through the instrument software. The instrument
is not calibrated, and hence the focus will be on relative changes during the time series
measurements.

3.4
Currents
Acoustic Doppler Current ProïŽler (ADCP) data has only been analysed for the focus periods
in 2019-2020 and 2021-2022. The ADCP emits high frequency sound signals in various beam
directions. Suspended material in the water column causes backscatter, and the instrument
analyses the shift in frequency using the Doppler eïŽect to determine the current direction
and speed. A key assumption is, that the suspended material moves with the same horizontal
velocity as the water. The output from the ADCP processing are the u and v components of the
measured current, which measure the east- and northward components, respectively. Various
ADCP instruments send out sound signals with diïŽerent frequencies. Higher frequencies
give a higher resolution, but in return the reach is lower than for instruments with lower
frequencies.
Comparing the ADCP data across years, showed that the 2012-2015 data had markedly
diïŽerent current speeds compared to later years. The 2020-2021 data had evident compass
calibration issues.
Due to these quality and uncertainty concerns, the ADCP data from
2012-2015 and 2020-2021 were taken out of the analysis.
The frequencies of the ADCPs used for the analysis of the 2019-2020 and 2021-2022

5.1
Data limitations
The data used in this project stem from diïŽerent autonomous sensors on moorings that have
been deployed for 1-2 years in a harsh environment. This creates some data limitations that
should be taken into account for the further discussion.
During the service cruises when the moorings were recovered, they were also mostly
redeployed. They were therefore not re-calibrated, and all of the instruments had only been
factory calibrated prior to their ïŽrst deployment. However, the 2012-2015 and the 2019-2020
data have been quality controlled (Sundfjord et al., 2017, 2020, 2022).
All of the instruments were mounted on mooring lines. As the area is highly advective
and experiences strong currents, the mooring is experiencing lateral current drag, causing the
line to tilt and leading to downward displacement of the instruments of up to 121 m. This
depth variability is most pronounced nearest to the surface and decreases with depth, due to
the proximity to the anchor. This means that the instruments are not consistently measuring
at the same depth and hence the changes in the variables, are in some cases not related to
temporal variability, but rather to depth variations, and associated diïŽerences in the vertical
distribution of water masses.
The setup of the instrument placements on the moorings varied between years (Table
3.1). The ïŽrst periods (2012-2015) had similar setups with CTDs mounted at â 30 m, â 50
(2012-2013), â 100 m, â 200 m, â 400 m and â 750 m. The later periods (2019-2022) had
more instruments and closer placements in the upper â 300 m. Beneath this were two Solo
instruments, which measured only temperature at â 400 m and â 600 m. Another CTD was
placed near the seaïŽoor at around â 880 m. This variation in instrument placement, causes
a bias in terms of describing the accurate inter-annual changes. The placement diïŽerences
could e.g. inïŽuence whether the AW core is observed across the years. A comparison of
the maximum temperatures should thus be made with caution, since the instruments capture
diïŽerent parts of the water column across the years.
As the solo instruments used in 2019-2022 only measure temperature, the vertical position
has been interpolated from the pressure of the surrounding instruments, as described in
Section 3. The position of these instruments are thus not precise. Additionally, the salinity
has been interpolated between the other instruments in order to calculate the TC to make the
temperatures comparable to that of the other instruments. The solos were only incorporated
in the linearly interpolated plots in Figures 4.1 and 4.2. As these plots are linearly interpolated
between the data points, there is already an associated uncertainty of the vertical location
of the isothermals between the instrument locations. Despite the uncertainties related to the
measurement interpretations the solos thus increase the accuracy of the vertical temperature
distribution compared to the interpolated alternative. In 2020-2021 the solos failed, and were
not used.
During the deployments there were several incidents when instrumentation failed. In
the 2013-2015 deployment the uppermost CTD failed after the 13. February 2014, and the
remaining deployment period does not measure the near-surface. The upper instrument in
2020-2021 also failed after the 9. April 2021. This makes it more diïŽcult to determine the
inter-annual patterns in the near-surface layer. The pH sensor on the SeapHOx at the near-
surface in 2021-2022 showed values ranging 2-8 after the 11. April, and these measurements
were removed. The full seasonal cycle of the near-surface pH, can therefore not be described.

5.2
Intro to processes
In order to determine what drives the changes in pH and DO in the two focus periods some
of the essential processes will be discussed in the following sections. These are biological
processes; primary production and respiration, physical; water masses and circulation, and
biogeochemical; air-sea gas exchange and solubility. Subsequently, these processes will be
combined to try to describe the interplay between them, and how they inïŽuence the observed
variability. The remaining variability will be explored speculatively. Lastly speculations will
be made towards how these variables might change with projected climatic changes, and what
that indicates for the state of the marine environment.
5.3
Primary production
Primary production is only expected to be detectable at the near-surface measurements, since
photosynthesis can only occur in the photic zone. The main signal of primary production
would be expected to be an increase in DO. Primary production could also result in an increase
in pH, due to the uptake of CO2. However, this might be less evident, due to the non-linear
correlation between CO2 concentration and pH.
5.3.1
Near-surface layer
In the 2019-2020 data set, there is a substantial increase in DO from the end of June until
the annual maximum of 495 Âĩmol/L on the 11. August, followed by a decrease throughout
the remainder of the time series in September (Figure 4.4 (D)). This increase in DO is so
signiïŽcant, that the most plausible explanation would be the onset of the bloom. This is
supported by the fact, that the increase occurs at a time, where the sea ice has started thinning,
and the sun has fully returned to the area. Light is the main limiting factor for the Arctic
bloom, and sea ice has a blocking eïŽect on the light penetration. Once the sea ice starts to
melt, and the snow cover on top of the sea ice disappears, the light penetration increases,
and becomes available to primary producers. Leu et al. (2011) describe that the bloom in
the Arctic typically initiates with an ice algae bloom. Once the ice algae bloom is over, the
ice becomes more transparent, which allows more light to penetrate through the ice, which

can be utilised in the pelagic bloom. The magnitude of the ice algae bloom is lower, than
for the pelagic bloom, but it can dominate the spring production. Since the increase in DO
starts prior to the ice free season, the onset of the bloom in 2020 is likely driven by ice algae.
Since the near-surface measurement is taken at 22 m, which lies within the surface mixed
layer, it is reasonable to expect that some of the DO produced by an ice algae bloom would
be detectable at that depth. There might additionally be an under ice pelagic bloom after the
ice algae bloom, which could be speculated to cause the spike in DO observed in mid-July.
However, it is diïŽcult to distinguish the contribution of the ice algae and phytoplankton using
only DO measurements.
Comparing this to the DOconc in 2021-2022 shows an apparent vertical diïŽerence between
the two measurement locations. The 2021-2022 near-surface measurement has a median
depth of 46 m, compared to 22 m in 2019-2020. Since the primary production occurs close
to the surface the signals in the DOconc are less pronounced at 46 m, and the bloom is not
immediately evident from the DOconc in Figure 4.8 (D). However, the DOsat in Figure 4.8 (E)
shows an increasing shift in early July. Since the DOsat describes the DOconc relative to the
physically driven solubility it is a good indicator of biologically driven DO increase. During
the increase in DOsat the TC and SA increase. If all other factors were kept constant, this
would be expected to cause a decrease in DOsat. Since the opposite is observed, this further
supports that the increase in DOsat is driven by a biological increase in DO. The opposing
trends of the solubility and photosynthetic signals can mask the relatively weak DO signal,
that would otherwise have been observed in the DOconc. It is not possible to determine if an
associated increase in pH occurs, since the pH measurements became erroneous prior to the
bloom.
In 2021-2022 the chl-a was also measured, and shows an increase already in late June.
This supports the presence of primary producers, and that a bloom is occurring. The increase
in chl-a is prior to the observed increase in DOsat, which is puzzling, since the chl-a indicates
a presence of phytoplankton at the measured depth, which would be assumed to produce
oxygen. However, there might be a delay in the DO signal. The increase in chl-a occurs
while the area is still mainly ice covered, but the SIT and SIC have both started to decrease,
which gives rise to light penetration. Similar to the 2019-2020 this early part of the bloom is
expected to be supported by ice algae and under ice phytoplankton growth.
The timing of the increase in chl-a is similar to the increase in DO in the 2019-2020 season.
This could be interpreted as the two years having similar bloom onset times. However, these
are two diïŽerent variables, and it might be more correct to compare the DO between the years,
which would indicate, that the bloom starts â 2 weeks later in 2021-2022, compared to 2019-
2020. Comparing the SIC and SIT between the two years shows that in late June and early July
the 2019-2020 period still had a â 90% SIC and an almost 2 m SIT, compared to an â 80%
SIC and â 1 m SIT. If sea ice, and the its eïŽects on light availability, are the main drivers
of the onset of the bloom, it would have been expected, that the 2021-2022 bloom would
initiate earlier, than in 2019-2020. However, snow depth is not included in the data, and could
potentially be thicker in 2021-2022, and hence limiting the light availability. Additionally, the
sea ice algae and under ice bloom in 2021-2022 may simply not be immediately detectable at
these depths.
Another factor inïŽuencing primary production is the availability of nutrients. The inïŽow

5.4
Respiration
Opposed to primary production, respiration occurs all year and throughout the water column.
Respiration is expected to be observed, as a decrease in DO over time. This is most easily
observed in the DOsat, since the DOconc is also inïŽuenced by variability related to solubility.
At depth the changes in DOsat can indicate when the water mass was last at the surface, where
oxygen can be replenished through air-sea gas exchange and primary production. Respiration
would also lead to a decrease in pH, since it releases CO2.
5.4.1
Near-surface layer
During the pre-bloom period in 2019-2020 and in 2021-2022 there is an overall decreasing
trend in DOsat. This indicates, that respiration dominates over photosynthesis, and that there
is a net consumption of oxygen. This would be expected, since photosynthesis is not occurring
in the dark and ice covered periods. The respiration rate is modest, and in both years the DOsat
decreases by â 5 percentage points during the pre-bloom measurements. After the bloom the
respiration increases drastically due to the higher concentration of organic matter. This drives
the decrease of 132% to 92% occurring in less than two months.
5.4.2
Bottom layer
At the bottom there is no photosynthesis, and the biological processes are expected to be
dominated by respiration.
The ventilation of the bottom waters are driven by physical
processes related to mixing and inïŽow of oxygen-rich water masses. Respiration is expected
to decrease the DOsat within the water mass, but the trend within the data is not necessarily
a linear decrease, since the area is highly advective, and there is a high turnover rate of the
water masses.
The initial DOsat is lower at depth, than at the near-surface, which would also be ex-
pected, since the near-surface is closer to the oxygen sources of photosynthesis and air-sea
gas exchange. In 2019 the initial DOsat is 84% and in 2021-2022 it is 83%. During the
observational period the DOsat varies between 82-87% in 2019-2020 and 81-85% in 2021-
2022, which means, that it is generally slightly lower in 2021-2022 compared to 2019-2020.
Both years exhibit a net decreasing trend with episodes of rapid increase. This supports
that respiration decreases the DOsat over time, but that it gets replenished through episodic
ventilation. The respiration rates between the years are comparable, and the DOsat diïŽerence
between the years is more likely driven by variations in the DOsat of the source waters, as
well as the inïŽow rates.

5.8
Future projections
The observations from two yearly deployments on the continental slope of Svalbard reveals
how tightly the DO and pH respond to shifts in water masses, temperature changes, and
biological activity. Extrapolating those sensitivities into a rapidly changing Arctic, can help
illuminate some of the projected changes and their eïŽects. In this way the Svalbard slope
area can be used as an early warning system for impending transitions in the Arctic.
5.8.1
Warming, sea ice retreat, and AtlantiïŽcation
The Arctic is projected to keep warming at rates faster than the global average (Serreze and
Barry, 2011), the sea ice extent is decreasing and the ice is getting younger and thinner
(Stroeve and Notz, 2018), and the inïŽow of AW is increasing, bringing additional heat, as
well as salt and nutrients into the area (Polyakov et al., 2017). These changes will all have
impacts on the DO and pH.

tightly linked to the temperature dependent solubility eïŽects.

Conclusion
Physical and chemical data were collected from a mooring on the Svalbard continental slope
on the 800 m isobath during two yearlong deployments from 2019-2020 and 2021-2022.
Additional hydrographic data were collected in 2012-2015 and 2019-2022. These data are
analysed to determine the variability of DO and pH, and their drivers.
From this ïŽve main drivers are identiïŽed: photosynthesis, respiration, water masses and
circulation, solubility, and air- sea gas exchange. Their relative contribution to the DO and pH
changes varies across depth and season. The near-surface exhibits the strongest seasonality.
During the dark and ice covered winter the DO exhibits strong correlation with temperature
indicating that solubility eïŽects are the dominant process governing the DO during this period.
The seasonally most drastic increase in DO happens during the bloom which is detected in the
DO signal in late June in 2019-2020 and in early July in 2021-2022. The onset of the bloom
occurs while the area is still ice covered, but is driven by the light availability penetrating
through the thinning ice cover. The annual maximum occurs after the complete melt of
the sea ice, and reaches 495 Âĩmol/L in 2019-2020. The shallower measurement position in
2019-2020 compared to the 2021-2022 deployment captures a greater impact of the bloom in
the DO signal, and indicates the the lower 2021-2022 measurement at a median depth of 46
m may be on the lower boundary of the bloom depth. After the peak in DO the respiration
rate exceeds the primary production, which has decreased, and the DO levels return to near
winter values. The measurements from the bottom of the continental slope show a markedly
diïŽerent pattern. The values of DO are generally lower, and the variability is less pronounced.
The main driver of both DO and pH at these depths are related to water masses. Episodically
there are inïŽows of water masses that resemble the AW more than the residing water masses.
These more Atlantic inputs have more recently been ventilated at the surface and carry higher
levels of DO. However, they also carry more of the Atlantic high DIC values, and hence low
pH.
With projected climate change, AtlantiïŽcation and sea ice melt these variables are pro-
jected to change further in the coming decades. The warming inïŽuences solubility, decreasing
the DO and the mitigating eïŽects of CO2 uptake from the atmosphere. The timing of the
bloom is projected to change, as the sea ice cover declines, having potential cascading eïŽects
