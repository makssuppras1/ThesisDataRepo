Abstract
1
Abstract
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is a non-invasive method that induces neural ac-
tivation by inducing magnetic pulses to the brain. TMS has been widely used to investigate
brain activity, although the different output measures used are each associated with spe-
cific limitations. Most recently, the limitation of obscured EEG signal using TMS-EEG has
been addressed. Beck, Christiansen et al. discovered the possibility of recovering otherwise
obscured EEG signals from as early as 2 ms. This led to the discovery of the immediate
TMS-evoked potentials (i-TEPs), which arrive at 2-6 ms and suggest a local "immediate"
response of the stimulated cortex. It has been proposed that the iTEPs consist of two com-
ponents. However, it remains unknown whether and how these components contribute to
the generation of motor evoked potentials (MEPs) and whether they change when the TMS
coil orientation is changed. Understanding this could provide further insight into cortical
processing in the brain after TMS.
To investigate the potential link between iTEPs and MEPs, EEG and EMG data were pre-
processed, and the iTEPs were separated into a fast, synchronized component and a slow,
unsynchronized component. Features were extracted from both iTEPs and MEP peak-to-
peak amplitudes to examine how iTEP characteristics differ with current direction and how
they relate to motor output. Machine learning models were trained to predict MEP ampli-
tudes from iTEP features, with model interpretability and uncertainty estimated through
kernel-based regression techniques. Model performance was validated using 10-fold cross-
validation and permutation testing, and generalizability was assessed by testing on a second
dataset.
Differences between coil orientations were observed in the MEPs, the iTEPs, and the feature
relevance computed using automatic relevance determination (ARD). Models showed low
prediction error and strong performance within datasets, as confirmed by cross-validation
and permutation testing.
Testing the models on the opposite dataset showed very poor
model performance.
The synchronized and unsynchronized components of the iTEPs were successfully isolated
and found to vary with current direction, in line with previous reports. Machine learning
models trained on iTEP features showed strong predictive performance for MEP ampli-
tudes within datasets, and feature relevance varied between current directions. However,
the poor performance of models on new data suggests that further work is needed to deter-
mine whether a global model of the relationship can be established.

Introduction
4
Introduction
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is a non-invasive method that induces neuronal
activation by delivering magnetic pulses, which induce an electrical current in the brain.
TMS is used both clinically and in research to modulate and investigate brain activity [10],
[11].
The bulk of TMS research has focused on the motor system due to the ease with which
motor output can be measured. When TMS is applied to the motor cortex at sufficient
intensity, it elicits a motor evoked potential (MEP), which can be recorded with EMG. How-
ever, because the MEP is an indirect measure of cortical activity confounded by other inputs
along the signaling pathway, very little is known about the direct effects of TMS on the brain
[1].
The TMS-evoked activity can also be measured from implanted electrodes in the epidural
space of the spinal cord. When the motor cortex is stimulated at the appropriate intensity,
a series of waves, known as I-waves, can be recorded. These waves are thought to originate
from transsynaptic activation of pyramidal tract neurons (PTNs) in the motor cortex [1].
I-waves provide a more direct but invasive way to assess TMS-evoked activity.
To avoid the implanted electrodes while studying more direct cortical effects, TMS can
be combined with electroencephalography (EEG). After TMS, negative and positive deflec-
tions caused by the stimulation will be seen in the average EEG trace. These are called
TMS-evoked potentials (TEPs) and reflect cortical excitability [12].
However, the TMS
pulse results in artifacts that disturb the EEG signal. As a result, the first 10 milliseconds
(ms) have been obscured and therefore excluded from analysis [6]. Consequently, the peaks
often studied with TMS-EEG do not reflect direct cortical activity, as they arrive later than
10 ms after the pulse.
In a recent paper by Beck, Christiansen et al.
2024 [6], they have successfully reduced
the artifacts present in the early EEG signal, making it possible to recover the signal as
early as 2 ms. Within this initial window, from approximately 2 to 6 ms, the immediate
TMS-evoked potentials (iTEPs) can be observed. The iTEP consists of 2-3 peaks super-
imposed on a slower wave, suggesting that iTEPs comprise two distinct components. The
latency and frequency of the iTEPs suggest a local "immediate" response of the stimulated

One factor known to influence the characteristics of both MEPS, I-waves, and iTEPs is
the direction of the induced current in the cortex,which depends on the direction of the
current in the magnetic coil. It has been shown that different current directions activate
distinct neuronal networks in the motor cortex, evident as different MEP latencies and dif-
ferent I-wave composition [1]. Interestingly, differences in the iTEP characteristics when
changing the current direction of the stimulation appear similar to those found in I-waves.
This supports the hypothesis that the iTEPs are the cortical origin of the I-waves. However,
as the measures were derived from a combination of both fast and slow iTEP components,
the results should be interpreted with caution [6].
Further investigation of the iTEPs has been conducted, supporting the possibility of iTEPs
being an excitability index of the stimulated motor cortex, and with results corroborating
the proposal that the iTEPs consist of two distinct components [13].
Furthermore, a significant positive correlation between iTEP features and MEP peak-to-
peak amplitude has been found using data from a single subject [7].
If the iTEPs are the cortical origin of I-waves, a relationship is expected between the iTEPs
and the MEPs.
While preliminary findings have demonstrated an association between specific iTEP com-
ponents and MEPs, it remains to be investigated whether a more generalized or global
relationship exists across subjects and conditions. Specifically, the structure of the relation-
ship between the different components of the EEG signal occurring 2–6 ms after TMS and
the resulting MEPs remains unclear, as does the extent to which this relationship is affected
by direction of the induced current.
This thesis aims to investigate how different components in the immediate TMS-evoked
EEG signals (iTEPs) contribute to the motor output measured through MEPs elicited with
biphasic TMS with two different (dominant) current directions.
To this end, I explore
whether machine learning models can be utilized to describe this relationship and whether
such models can be generalized to new data.
Within this overarching aim, I address the following research:

5.1
Transcranial magnetic stimulation
TMS is a non-invasive method introduced in 1985 by Barker et al. [14]. TMS works by
delivering magnetic pulses generated in a magnetic coil. The magnetic pulses are generated
by discharging a capacitor, which produces an electrical pulse that runs through a copper wire
coil inside a plastic case. The TMS coil generates a magnetic field that, when placed on the
head, penetrates the skull, perpendicular to the coil’s plane, without significant attenuation,
and will induce an electric field in the brain.
At the neuronal level, if the electric field induced by TMS is above the threshold of neuronal
activation, it can elicit an action potential. The most likely sites of excitation are axon ter-
minals and bends, especially in the crowns and lips of the pre- and postcentral gyri, where
the electric field is the strongest. The activations spread both within cortical micro-circuits
and to remote connected brain regions [1].
The intensity required to elicit action potentials depends on the coil orientation and di-
rection of neurons. See Figure 1.

IV, and V. From [1].

5.1.1
TMS-EMG
Most TMS research has focused on the motor system due to the accessibility of measuring
an output using EMG. Stimulating the ’hand knob’ (M1-HAND) in motor cortex, above a
certain threshold, it is possible to elicit a MEP which can be measured using EMG from
electrodes placed on the skin [1] (see Figure 3). The ’hand knob’ is the part of motor cortex
responsible for the voluntary hand movements [10]. Using this for exploring TMS-induced
neural activation results in an easily measurable readout of MEPs, as the motor cortex has a

superficial anatomical location and offers direct corticospinal output to spinal motor neurons.
However, the MEP output is not a direct measure of cortical activity, as it is affected by
synchronization and the physical properties of cell populations at the cortical, spinal, and
muscular levels [1]. The MEPs provide a high signal-to-noise ratio readout of corticospinal
excitability, but lack specificity regarding the exact neural circuits or mechanisms involved.
The MEP is typically a triphasic waveform in neurologically intact individuals and contains

[15]. In this project, I used the peak-to-peak amplitude as the target variable to be predicted.
Since the MEP is a well-studied measure, the peak-to-peak amplitude serves as a reliable
feature for investigating new phenomena.

5.1.2
I-waves
The effect of TMS on the motor system can also be investigated using invasive recording.
When recording from implanted electrodes along the spinal cord after stimulation of the mo-
tor cortex, it is possible to record the so-called indirect waves (I-waves). These can be seen
in Figure 5.1.2. The waves are a series of 1-5 descending volleys in the corticospinal tract.
The earliest wave arriving is called the direct wave (D-wave) and is thought to originate from
direct activation of the fast PTNs. The I-waves, on the other hand, are believed to originate
from indirect, transsynaptic activation of the PTNs [16].
It is hypothesized that the I-waves are the result of activation of several layer 5 PTNs rather
than repetitive firing of a single neuron, since a single PTN is most likely to contribute to
only one I-wave. Through computational modeling, it has been demonstrated that the spike

While I-waves provide valuable insights into corticospinal activity, their measurement re-
quires invasive spinal recordings. To investigate cortical responses more directly and non-
invasively, EEG can be used to record neural activity at the scalp level.
5.1.3
EEG
EEG is a non-invasive method that records voltage potentials resulting from current flow in
the brain using electrodes placed on the scalp. EEG only detects a small portion of the variety
of electrical activity in the brain and primarily synchronous postsynaptic potentials rather
than action potentials [18], [12]. This is due to the short duration of action potentials, which
reduces the likelihood of the action potentials summing to a readable signal [12]. The activity
recorded is the sum of synchronized activity in populations of cortical neurons (pyramidal
cells). An excitation of the neurons creates an extracellular voltage that is more negative in
one place than elsewhere. This results in a dipole where one region is positively charged and
another negatively charged. The positively charged region is called a source, whereas the
negatively charged region is called a sink. The electrodes of the EEG will detect the sum of
these negative and positive charges. If the electrode is closer to either the source or the sink,
the charge from this part will dominate the recording in that electrode. This will result in
negative deflections in some electrodes while positive deflections in others, representing the
same dipole. However, the EEG electrodes will not be able to record charge from a single
dipole but rather from a population of dipoles. Since the electrodes measure the sum of
the charges of the dipoles, they must lie parallel and be synchronously active for the EEG
to detect a signal [19]. When using TMS, neurons are synchronously activated, making it
well-suited for combining TMS and EEG.
5.1.4
TMS-EEG
By combining TMS and EEG, you can study the neural TMS-evoked brain activity. This
could, for example, involve examining the change in power spectrum of the EEG signal or
analyzing the EEG waveforms. When stimulating with TMS, a series of positive and negative
deflections induced by the TMS (TEPs) will appear in the EEG data. The studied TEPs
arrive from 10 ms and forward and thus are not a measure of direct cortical activity. TEPs
are named after their polarity and latency, e.g., N15 (negative deflection at 15 ms), P30
(positive deflection at 30 ms), and N100 (negative deflection at 100 ms) [12]. An example
showing TEPs is found in Figure 4.

5.1.5
iTEPs
When analyzing TMS-EEG, several limiting factors are present. Among these are the arti-
facts induced by the stimulation. This includes instrumental artifacts, such as the TMS-pulse
artifact and decay artifact, as well as muscular artifacts caused by stimulation-induced mus-
cle activation. In 2024, Beck, Christiansen et al. demonstrated that using high-frequency
EEG and a TEP-optimized hot spot, it is possible to recover signals as early as 2 ms. This
had previously been impossible due to the mentioned artifacts. When stimulating the motor
cortex, three peaks superimposed on a slower wave appear in the EEG data between 2-6 ms
after stimulation. These are called the immediate transcranial evoked potentials (iTEPs). It
has been hypothesized that the iTEPs are the cortical origin of the I-waves [6]. This finding
enables the study of early cortical responses, providing insight into the intracortical circuitry
involved in voluntary motor control.
5.1.6

When changing the coil orientation used for stimulation, the readout also changes. This is
due to a difference in the activation of cortical circuits. The site of activation when stimulat-
ing the motor cortex using anterior-to-posterior (AP) coil orientation is thought to be more
anterior than when using posterior-to-anterior (PA) coil orientation. The effects of changing

EEG preprocessing
Working with TMS-EEG, there is no established standard for preprocessing, resulting in
various approaches being employed. The difference in preprocessing introduces variability
since different preprocessing pipelines alter the data differently. Brancaccio et al. have tested
the three main preprocessing pipelines and their differences. They did so using a synthetic
TMS-EEG signal to precisely and quantitatively observe what the pipelines do to the data.

5.3.1
Filtering
One of the crucial steps in preprocessing is filtering. This is used to increase the signal-to-
noise ratio, enabling a clearer signal that can be more easily interpreted. However, it also
carries the risk of altering the signal in a way that may lead to false results.
A common issue with causal filters is temporal dispersion. To minimize temporal dispersion
due to filtering as much as possible, a zero-phase filter was used in this project [24]. A zero-
phase filter is an acausal filter, meaning it uses both past and future signals in the filtering
process. By applying the filter in both the forward and reverse directions, zero-phase filtering
reduces the shift in latency. However, filtering both ways introduces the possibility that later
components may affect earlier ones. Acunzo et al. have shown that the cut-off using high-
pass filtering should be as low as possible, with 0.1 Hz as the highest value, to avoid filtering
artifacts that result in systematic biases, such as a late component affecting an earlier one,
leading to incorrect interpretations [24]. One should therefore consider the possibility that
the iTEP signal may be affected by later TEPs when using a too high high-pass cutoff.
5.4
Machine learning
For further interpretation of the processed data, machine learning model were used to model
the relationship between the iTEP features and the peak-to-peak MEP amplitudes.
Machine learning can be used to train models with data. The goal of the training is to obtain
a model that, with the past experience from the given data, has ’learned’ how to generalize

==PAGE:24==

in future situations [9]. In the case of this report, the machine learning models are given
features from the iTEPs and the corresponding peak-to-peak amplitude of the MEPs. The
goal is then for the model to learn how the features from the iTEPs relate to the MEP peak-
to-peak amplitudes. This should enable the model to predict MEP peak-to-peak amplitudes
using iTEP features from new data.
Machine learning can be divided into supervised and unsupervised learning. In supervised
learning, both the input and the true output are given to the model. This means the model
knows the ground truth and can then fit the model to this, in the best possible way. In
unsupervised learning, there is no ground truth given, and the model therefore uses different
methods to find patterns in the input data that make it possible to get the desired output.
In this report, I used supervised learning, since both the input (features of the iTEPs) and
the output (peak-to-peak amplitude of MEPs) are given to the model to train [9].
To ensure the trained model can predict output from new inputs, it is essential to con-
sider the risk of overfitting. If a model has enough degrees of freedom, it will be able to fit
the training data almost perfectly, resulting in very low error values. However, this will lead
to poor prediction using unseen data. Figure 50 shows an example of three different models
(red lines) fitted to data (blue circles). Then, new data points are introduced (green squares),
and although the model furthest to the right fits the training data the best, it does not fit
the new data points the best. The model is overfitting to the training data, resulting in a
bigger error for unseen data. Therefore, it is essential to look for overfitting and desirable to
achieve the lowest possible training and validation errors. The validation error is the error
obtained by testing the model on unseen data. A visualization of the training and validation
errors is shown in Figure 10, where model 3 performs the best on the training data but has a
much higher validation error, suggesting the possibility of overfitting. Model 2, on the other
hand, has a low training error and a validation error that is only slightly higher. Therefore,
model 2 would be the best choice in this case [9].

5.4.1
Gaussian Process Regression
For this project a Gaussian process regression (GPR) was chosen as the machine learning
model to train.
Using regression, a model is fitted to some input values to be able to predict a continuous
response [9]. Using the simplest regression, a linear regression, an assumption that the out-
Page 15 of 62
==PAGE:26==
Master’s thesis
5
Background
put is given by a linear combination of input features has to be made. However, this is not
always the case. Therefore, a non-linear model can be used, which is non-linear with respect
to the input data, but not with respect to the model’s parameters (weights). A limitation of
this approach is that it typically produces a single best-fitting function for the training data,
without providing information about the model’s confidence in its predictions. However,
several functions may fit the same training data equally well, and the one selected may not
generalize optimally to new, unseen data [25].
These complications can be addressed using GPR, which provides not a single function
but a distribution of functions that fit the data points. This leads to the predictions being a
mean of these functions, with a confidence interval being the variance of the functions [25].
Knowing the confidence interval of the predictions is an important tool when using ma-
chine learning. This helps the user understand the model’s uncertainty and, based on that,
make an informed decision about using the prediction. An example from [25]: If a classifi-
cation model is trained to classify pictures of dogs and cats, it should, with high certainty,
classify a picture of a cat as a cat. However, if the model is presented with a picture of a
duck, the prediction of the class, cats or dogs, will be somewhat random, since the picture
does not fall into either of the categories. The model should then predict the picture as either
a cat or a dog, but with high uncertainty. This way, the user knows that the prediction is
rather random.
The Gaussian process depends on the smoothness assumption being that if two variables
are strongly correlated, then if a point x producing an output y is very close to x’, it should
produce an output y’ very close to y [25].
Going back to the fact that the GPR does not predict a single function but a distribu-
tion of functions. This means that we are able to predict y’ for any x’ without using just
one model, but instead averaging across all the possible models [25].

which Gaussian process the functions should be drawn from, a kernel is selected before the
regression process. This kernel generates the covariance matrix of the Gaussian distribution,
which describes the shape of the distribution and the characteristics of the functions to be
predicted. Drawing from a Gaussian process implies that if two outputs, y and y’, are highly

The EMG data in dataset 2 required preprocessing before it was suitable for extracting MEP

exclusion of trials.

and filtfilt() in MATLAB with a bandwidth of 2 around 50 Hz.
The offset removal was performed using a script created by Mads J. Madsen, DRCMR,

6.2.3
Removal of TMS artifact and cubic interpolation
The TMS-pulse artifact is removed to avoid ringing artifacts when filtering [30]. Earlier,
when working with TEPs, the removal and interpolation of the TMS-pulse artifact could be
between -2 and 5 ms [8]. However, since the iTEPs arrive already at 2-6 ms, the TMS-pulse
removal and interpolation range needs to be shortened while still removing the pulse artifact.
In her work, Sveinsdóttir found a time range from -1.2 ms to 2.1 ms to be suitable for TMS-
pulse artifact removal and interpolation working with immediate signals [7]. During the
extraction of features from the iTEPs (see Section 6.4), for this project, the latency of peak
1 was found to be 2.1 ms in several cases. To minimize the influence of TMS-pulse artifact
removal and interpolation on the extracted value of peak 1, the range was adjusted to range
Page 25 of 62
==PAGE:36==
Master’s thesis
6

from -1.2 ms to 2.0 ms.
After removing the data within the time range of the TMS-pulse artifact, the removed
data were replaced with interpolated values. A cubic interpolation was chosen to avoid hard
edges from a linear interpolation, which can cause ringing effects when filtering [12]. The
pulse artifact was removed and interpolated using the TESA toolbox in EEGLAB.
6.2.4
Filtering
Filtering of the EEG data can, among other things, be used to remove high-frequency noise
[12]. Earlier research working with the later TEPs has typically employed a low-pass cut-off
frequency of around 90 Hz [8], which is significantly lower than the one used for the iTEPs,
since these consist of higher frequencies. Especially when wanting to isolate the synchronized
component, the relevant frequency band lies well above those explored in most prior TEP
studies. Therefore, it is not possible to rely heavily on what has been done in earlier TEP
research. However, earlier work with iTEPs from Beck, Christiansen et al., Stango et al.,
and Sveinsdóttir inspired the chosen filtering bands.
For this project, the EEG data were band-pass filtered using several different frequency
bands for comparison and separation of different components (see Section ??). The bands
used to obtain the results in Section 7 are: 0.1 Hz - 2000 Hz, 0.1 Hz - 1000 Hz, and 0.1 Hz
- 200 Hz.
A Butterworth bandpass filter was used for the filtering. Using the Butterworth filter in-
cluded in the TESA toolbox, the data is filtered across concatenated trials to minimize
edge artifacts and computational time. However, to ensure that no artifacts from filtering
are spread across trials, a function for filtering was created that filters trials one by one.
This was accomplished using MATLAB’s butter() function, along with the filtfilt() function,
which performs zero-phase filtering by filtering the data in both forward and reverse direc-
tions. This ensures that the event latency does not shift as would be the case using a causal
filter, which would shift the signal in time [31].

channel will then affect the new average reference, making it even more important to exclude

6.2.6
Methods for extraction of features
Previous work has supported the hypothesis that iTEPs consist of two different components.
To quantify the contribution of each component to the MEPs, these components must be
separated first.
Earlier methods for extracting features of the iTEPs have been proposed.
Beck, Chris-
tiansen et al. extracted the peak and trough amplitudes, latencies, and a peak-to-trough
measure from the full EEG signal.
Using this method, it is not possible to separate the contribution, which may be substantially
different, from the different components to the MEPs.
Stango et al. used time-frequency analysis to calculate the immediate TMS-related power
(i-TRP). This showed maximal activity in two frequency bands, leading to the fast (syn-
chronized) iTRP (fi-TRP) and the slow (unsynchronized) iTRP (si-TRP).
However, this does not provide a way to separate the EEG signal into the two components.
A method for separating the components could be to utilize the frequency bands where
maximal activity was found by Stango et al. and filter around those frequency bands to
isolate each component.
This method was investigated. However, issues regarding filtering artifacts were encoun-
tered. As described in Section 5.3.1, issues can arise using a too high cut-off for high-pass
filtering. The effects of this are visualized in Figure 39 in Appendix A.
Therefore, an additional method has been proposed for separating the iTEP components.
6.2.7
Subtraction of slow wave
To avoid filtering artifacts when separating the two components, the slow wave (unsynchro-
nized) was subtracted from the broadband filtered data. This was achieved by first filtering
the data as described in Section 6.2.4, using a range of 0.1–1000 Hz for the broadband sig-
nal and 0.1–200 Hz for the slow-wave component. Subsequently, the slow-wave signal was
subtracted from the broadband signal, leaving the high-frequency component (synchronized

data). Further description and visualization of this method can be found in Appendix B.
Since it may not only be slow-frequency signals contributing to the underlying slow wave,
but also somewhat different, unsynchronized signals with varying frequencies, the two com-
ponents will be referred to as synchronized and unsynchronized.
6.2.8
Test of frequency range
Since an increased power was found in the paper by Stango et al. within a narrower frequency
band than the one used in this project, a test of model performance across different frequency
ranges was conducted. This resulted in better model performance using a frequency band
of 200-1000 Hz compared to 500-1000 Hz for the synchronized component. Results can be
seen in Appendix B.2. This implies that there may be neural information important for
generating MEPs within the frequency band of 200-500 Hz. Given the indications that the I
waves arise from a population of neurons rather than single neuron firing [17], it would make
sense for the iTEPs, if these are the cortical origin of the I-waves, to consist of information
in several different frequency bands.

rent direction, linear mixed-effects models were used. Direction was used as the fixed effect,
and subject as the random effect.
Even though the linear mixed-effects models accounts for non-independence due to repeated
measures for example across subject, it does not account for the short-range autocorrelation
introduced when using a running average. This presents the risk of getting overconfident
p-values [32].
To account for this problem, the mean of the data points for each subject was used, resulting
in one value per subject, for fitting the linear mixed-effects models. In this way, no autocor-
relation was present; however, this results in a loss of power due to the reduced number of
samples [32].
Maximization of power and within-subject variability can be obtained using all the values
to fit the models. Therefore, the models were fitted to all values as well, while keeping the
limitations described above in mind.
Trying to find a middle way between these two methods, three values per subject were in-
troduced. The fifth, the middle, and the fifth-from-last. This reduces the autocorrelation
compared to using all the values. Furthermore, it introduces some within-subject variability
and higher power compared to taking the mean of all values within a subject. However,
this method also has its limitations. The three values chosen are arbitrary, and there is no
way to ensure they capture the true variability. Additionally, the correlation might still be
present and therefore not resolve the independence issue.
Since all of the methods have limitations, the statistical results should be interpreted with
caution. The p-values from fitting the models shown in Table 2 are all Bonferroni corrected
to control for multiple comparisons.

6.4.2
Extraction of peaks on trial basis
The iTEP feature extraction was performed on a trial-by-trial level. This was done to keep
the variability, which is highly desirable to include, as it represents the true variability in
physiological signals caused by factors such as excitability state and underlying oscillatory
activity. However, the variability can also be caused by technical factors and slight changes
in coil position, which are not of interest, meaning the variability does not solely reflect
physiological sources.
The features extracted were the three absolute peak values and two absolute trough values
from the synchronized component, as well as the absolute peak value from the unsynchro-
nized component. Since using both the positive peak and negative trough can cause ringing
artifacts [33], only the positive peaks were included in further analysis to avoid including
artifactual data in the features.
Due to the high variability between trials, the peak finding was challenging. For each dataset,
a time window was identified for each peak and each trough. Within these windows of time,
the max() function in MATLAB was used to find the peaks, whereas the min() function was
used to find the troughs. During this peak-finding process, additional trials were discarded.
It became apparent that some trials had artifacts within the epoch but outside the iTEP
range, which affected the reliability of the iTEPs. Exclusion criteria of trials were as follows:
1. Trials where one or more peaks were missing were excluded.
2. Trials where the peak/trough finding windows had to be changed in such a way that they
became unfit to find peaks and troughs in several other trials were excluded.
3. Trials with a negative peak value in the unsynchronized component, a peak before 2.0
ms, or not a clear slow wave, were excluded.
This led to the exclusion of subjects as well, since the trial exclusion process reduced the
number of trials below a chosen level of 40 trials pr subject.

6.5
Choosing and training a machine learning model
For modeling the relationship between the extracted iTEP features and the amplitudes of
the MEPs, a machine learning approach was employed. For this approach, a model was
selected, trained, and validated on one dataset, and lastly, tested on the opposite dataset.
A GPR was chosen for the modeling of the relationship between the iTEP features and the
MEPs. The choice of this model has been made due to several advantages. First and fore-
most, GPR provides probabilistic prediction, which gives uncertainty bounds. This ability
to quantify uncertainty is especially valuable when modeling, without knowing the ground
truth about the relationship.
Furthermore, it is possible to model nonlinear, complex relationships, which provides flexi-
bility in modeling [25]. However, the GPR does not provide a simple closed-form equation
for interpretation. To enhance the model’s explanatory power, ARD was introduced.
The GPR works with prior beliefs, kernels, which means it can model relationships with
relatively small sample sizes. With larger sample sizes, GPR is more computationally ex-
pensive.
Other models, such as neural networks or support vector machines, could have been chosen
to do the modeling as well. However, the running time using neural networks is significantly
longer, and support vector machines require a larger amount of data. Furthermore, they
lack uncertainty quantification [11].
For comparison, Bayesian linear regression was tested as well, but was not found suitable for
modeling the relationship between the iTEP features and the MEP amplitudes, which may
not be linear.
6.5.1
Kernels
Different kernels for the GPR were tested. Firstly, a Squared Exponential kernel, as it is the
default kernel when using GPR, and was therefore chosen as the initial kernel to test. The
Squared Exponential kernel is simple and predicts smooth functions. However, the Matérn
kernels, especially Matérn 5/2, are a more popular choice for real data and are therefore
expected to provide a better fit to the data. The Matérn kernels are flexible and can model
a larger variety of smooth functions [25]. Additional kernels were added to test whether
the assumption about which kernel would perform the best was true: the Matérn 3/2 and
exponential kernels, which are both part of the Matérn kernels. ARD was used with all
kernels to be able to extract the relevance of each feature.

6.6
Prediction and feature relevance
The trained model was used to predict the MEPs, providing another visual of the model’s
performance. Furthermore, the relevance of each feature was examined using length scales
determined in the ARD process. These length scales tell the model how much a feature
needs to change before the model expects the output to change significantly.
This means that if the length scale is small, even a slight change in the feature will signifi-
cantly alter the output. Whereas if the length scale is large, the feature will need to change
a lot more for the output to change. This means that in the feature relevance plot, the
height of the bar is determined by the reciprocal of the length scale: a high bar indicates
a small length scale, meaning that only a slight change in input will significantly alter the
output, indicating high relevance. Conversely, a lower bar reflects a larger length scale and
thus lower feature relevance. This also means that if one feature has a larger range than the
others, it will seem less relevant, as a relatively large change in the input may be required
Page 33 of 62
==PAGE:44==
Master’s thesis
6
Methods
before a change in the output occurs. However, this is accounted for by standardizing the
data.
6.6.1
Confidence interval
One of the advantages of using GPR is the ability to assess the model’s confidence. The
predictions of the MEPs were plotted together with the confidence interval to provide a
visual representation of the model’s confidence.
6.6.2
Permutation test
To further test the model’s performance, a permutation test was done. The MEP values were
randomly shuffled within subjects, and a model was trained on the permuted data. This
was done 1000 times, and the R2-values of the models trained on the permuted data were
computed. In this way, it is possible to get a measure of how likely it is that the model’s
performance is a coincidence.

8
Discussion
Through conservative filtering followed by signal subtraction, I here succeeded in separating
the "unsynchronized" slow and "synchronized" fast components of the iTEPs. The sepa-
rated components exhibit similar characteristics to those previously reported for iTEPs. I
compared the iTEP evoked with two different (dominant) current directions. The analysis of
the isolated synchronized component yielded results that align with previous research. This
includes a larger first peak when using APPA current direction relative to PAAP, and a larger
second and third peak when using PAAP current direction relative to APPA. The isolated,
unsynchronized component differed qualitatively between current orientations; however, this
observation was not statistically supported in this relatively small sample size. Furthermore,
machine learning models were successfully trained using features from the iTEPs to predict
MEP amplitudes within each of the two datasets. All models demonstrated strong predic-
tive performance within datasets, with confidence intervals providing an estimate of model
certainty and permutation tests confirming significantly higher R2 values for non-permuted
data. Feature relevance from each model was extracted, and, qualitatively, they differ de-
pending on the current direction. Interestingly, and contrary to expectations, the models
performed poorly when tested on the opposite dataset, indicating that they do not accurately
and/or solely explain the global relationship between iTEP features and MEP amplitudes.
8.1
Differences across current directions are similar to what has
previously been reported, and iTEP features might explainthe
latency difference of MEPs
Differences in MEPs and iTEPs have been previously reported when using different current
directions. Similar differences can be observed in the data used for this project.
In Figure 19, the mean of all MEPs from all subjects separated by current direction is
displayed. It appears that the latency of the MEP induced using APPA current direction
is slightly shorter compared to using PAAP. Similar latency difference has previously been
reported using PA and AP current directions [22]. As the same relative intensity has been
used for each current direction and, when combined with the similarity in amplitudes of
the mean MEPs, this indicates that it is reasonable to interpret the latency differences as
reflecting the predominant activation of distinct interneuronal networks rather than merely
differences in motoneuron recruitment. This suggests that biphasic stimulation activates
Page 51 of 62
==PAGE:62==
Master’s thesis
8
Discussion
different neuronal circuits depending on the direction of the current.
It is important to note that biphasic and monophasic stimulation affect the measured outputs
differently, as a consequence of the difference in the activation of the neuronal networks. For
the I-waves, it has been observed that the biphasic pulses elicit a more complex pattern of
the D- and I-waves, reflecting a mixture of those typically observed with monophasic stim-
ulation. Nevertheless, differences between biphasic current directions have been found to
resemble those seen in corresponding monophasic configurations (see Section 5.1.6), particu-
larly when using sufficiently high stimulation intensities, supporting the notion that different
neuronal circuits are engaged changing biphasic current direction [35], [36].
Given the indication of latency differences in MEPs and the suggestion that this is a result
of activation of different neuronal networks, the following will examine how this differential
activation is reflected in the iTEPs.
Figure 20a shows the mean iTEPs, derived from EEG data from all subjects filtered with a
frequency band of 0.1 - 2000 Hz, separated according to current direction. Visual inspection
suggests a larger peak 1 in the APPA current direction, and larger peaks 2 and 3 in the
PAAP current direction. This is also what is reported by Beck, Christiansen et al. [6].
Looking at Figure 20b displaying the synchronized component, the same characteristics are
visible: Peak 1 appears larger in APPA current direction and peak 2 and peak 3 appear
larger in PAAP current direction. This suggests a meaningful separation of the components.
Additionally, a fourth peak is visible in the synchronized component of the PAAP current
in the PAAP current direction. The fourth peak has not been chosen as a feature for this
project. However, since the fourth peak is visible in the PAAP current direction and not in
the APPA it could have an influence on the MEPs. This is discussed further later.
Continuing with the unsynchronized component of the iTEPs shown in Figure 20c, the
PAAP wave appears larger and to have a later peak than the APPA wave. The later ar-
rival of the peak of the unsynchronized component in the PAAP current direction matches
the latency difference observed in MEPs. These findings suggest that the unsynchronized
component of the iTEPs may contribute to explaining the latency difference of the MEPs.
However, the difference in the unsynchronized component was not statistically significant
when linear mixed-effects models were fitted using mean values from each subject, indicat-
ing that these findings should be interpreted with caution.

Furthermore, it has been proposed that the difference in latency of the MEPs using dif-
ferent current directions is due to the location of activation [1]. Using AP current direction,
the threshold for activation is the lowest more anteriorly than when using PA. This may lead
to a difference in the time it takes for the excitation to travel from the site of stimulation to
the corticomotoneuronal cells.
8.1.1
Quantification and statistical analysis
For further quantification and statistical analysis on the observed differences described above,
features were extracted from the different components of the iTEPs in each current direction.
In Figures 21 and 22, box plots displaying the differences between current directions within
each of the extracted features of the iTEPs are shown.
Linear mixed-effects models were fitted to the extracted features to get a statistical measure
of the difference between current directions. Since a running average was used before ex-
tracting the peak values from the different components, autocorrelation is introduced. This
results in a dependence that is not accounted for using linear mixed-effects models. Different
approaches were applied in an attempt to fit the models in the best possible way despite the
introduced dependence.
An approach using the mean within each subject was used to eliminate the unwanted depen-
dence, however this limits the power of the model and does not use the advantage of linear
mixed-effect models being the ability of using all trial-level data while still accounting for
subject-level differences via random effects.
Linear mixed-effects models were also fitted to all values, and, aiming for a compromise
between trial-level and subject-level analysis, they were additionally fitted to three selected
values per subject: the fifth, the middle, and the fifth-to-last trial. However, this is not a
robust method for selecting representative values, as the chosen trials are arbitrary and may
not adequately capture the subject’s variability.
The Bonferroni corrected p-values from the linear mixed-effects models fitted to different
numbers of values are shown in Table 2. The p-values are as expected, lower the more data
points, hence the more power is introduced to the model. Since none of the methods are
ideal, the highest p-values, obtained using the mean values, are used for the following inter-
pretation.

Beck, Christiansen et al. [6] extracted peak values from the almost raw signal, comparing
the values between current directions with intensity adjusted to the RMT of the respective
current direction. Their results showed significant effects of current direction on peak 1 and
peak 3 after fitting linear mixed-effect models.
Using the within-subject mean, fitting the linear mixed-effects models show similar results
as found in the paper by Beck, Christiansen et al. [6]. Furthermore, an additional peak
value extracted from the unsynchronized component was also tested. It did not show any
significant difference between current directions when using the mean within subjects.
The fact that the separated iTEP components lead to observations and statistical results
comparable to those previously found of the non-separated iTEPs suggests that the sep-
aration was successful. This allows investigating each component individually, potentially
offering deeper insights into the cortical processes underlying iTEPs and their associated
neuronal activity.
8.2
Successful prediction of MEP amplitudes within dataset 1
Two GPR models, using data from dataset 1 for each current direction, were successfully
trained. No signs of overfitting were present, and low RMSE and high R2 were found using
10-fold cross-validation. To verify that the good error values did not happen by chance,
permutation tests were performed. These showed likewise very good R2 values for the non-
permuted data and significantly worse for the permuted data. This suggests the relationship
between iTEP features and MEPs can be modeled using machine learning. Since the iTEPs
precede MEPs temporally, these findings further suggest that the iTEPs either represent or
reflect neural processes involved in generating the descending signals that excite the mo-
toneurons.
The models trained using both current directions performed really well predicting MEPs
from the iTEP features. Comparing the model performances between the two current direc-
tions for dataset 1, it is clear that the model trained on PAAP data qualitatively performs
better than the one trained using APPA data.
This is interesting compared to the results of Stango et al. [13]. They showed no significant
association between the TMS-related power of the unsynchronized component and the MEPs
in the APPA current direction. These results suggest that the unsynchronized component
does not contribute significantly to the MEPs.

RMSE of the model trained on APPA data may result from the unsynchronized component
not having a significant association with the MEPs. The same features, however, are ex-

the amplitude of the unsynchronized component - does not have a significant association

8.4
Trained models performed poorly with new data
The models trained on data from dataset 1 were tested with data from dataset 2 and per-
formed poorly. Then, a model was trained on data from dataset 2, which was tested with
data from dataset 1. It performed poorly as well.
The results from predicting MEP peak-to-peak amplitudes from the opposite dataset than
the models were trained on, show very low R2-values and high RMSEs, indicating poor model
performance. Looking at Figures 37 and 38, showing scatterplots of the predicted versus the
true MEP values, it is very clear that the predictions of MEPs are not very good.
Examining the predictions from the models trained on dataset 1, there is a slightly better
fit when using the APPA model than the PAAP, which is expected since the data in dataset
2 was collected using the APPA current direction. However, the difference is so small that
it is not possible to conclude that one model has a better fit, as it could be a coincidence.
The poor model performance when introducing new data could be due to the models not
explaining an actual physiological relationship. However, there may be several other factors
that limit the models’ ability to predict MEPs on the new data. Some of those will be
discussed below.
One might be the choice of model. Choosing GPR for modeling comes with limitations
that may impact the models’ ability to predict MEPs from new data. Firstly, there is no
modeling of subject-level variation. The GPR treats all observations as if they were from
Page 58 of 62
==PAGE:69==
Master’s thesis
8
Discussion
a single population, disregarding the fact that the different values originate from different
subjects. Furthermore, there is no built-in way to handle correlated inputs when using GPR.
Since the synchronized and unsynchronized components are not independent, this introduces
an issue, which may lead to reduced generalizability.
Looking into the more technical site, stimulation intensity may be a limiting factor in the
models’ ability to predict MEPs on new data. Examining previous work, Sveinsdóttir [7]
found a significant positive correlation between iTEP features and MEP amplitudes using
data with 105% RMT, but not when using data with 115% RMT. For this project, data with
110% RMT has been used. The precision and confidence of the prediction might increase us-
ing data with 105 % RMT, since it seems the relationship between iTEP features and MEPs
is stronger using this lower stimulation intensity. This increase in precision and confidence
of the model might lead to better predictions when introducing new data. If the relationship
between the iTEP features and the MEPs is stronger using lower RMT intensities, it would
likely be easier for the model to capture the patterns of the true relationship. Furthermore,
the direction-specific effects decrease with increasing stimulation intensity above a certain
level, as the induced current results in activation of less specific neuronal elements [1]. This
supports the idea that the relationship between iTEPs and MEPs becomes stronger at lower
intensities, as a more specific neuronal area can be targeted.
In addition to stimulation intensity, methodological differences between datasets may also
affect the observed relationship. These are described in Sections 6.1.1 and 6.1.3 and include
different coil sizes. A 70 mm coil was used for dataset 1 and a 35 mm coil for dataset 2. The
smaller coil used for dataset 2 results in more precise stimulation. This means that using the
70 mm coil in dataset 1 may have resulted in stimulation of additional neurons contributing
to the iTEPs and/or the MEPs, thereby creating a different relationship between the two.
Figure 34 shows the distribution of the iTEP peaks extracted from datasets 1 and 2, respec-
tively. The variance in peak values in dataset 2 is much smaller than in dataset 1. This also
applies to the MEP amplitudes visualized in Figure 35. The difference in variance might
trouble the model. Regardless, it reveals a difference in data distribution that may, among
other things, be caused by different coil sizes.
The preprocessing of the data might also contribute to the difference in data distribution
and the models not being able to predict MEPs well from the opposite dataset. Most steps
were performed similarly across the two datasets. However, channel and trial exclusion were

8.5
Future work
A comparison has been conducted between iTEP features, MEPs, and current direction.
However, further investigation testing the extraction of different features of the iTEPs and
MEPs, such as latencies, might give more insight into the relationship between the iTEPs
and the MEPs. Additionally, statistical testing with more values without the averaged de-
pendency should be conducted to confirm the significance of the differences.
The poor performance of the models using features from a new dataset highlights the need
for further work to confirm whether the relationship between the selected iTEP features and
MEP amplitudes can be effectively modeled using GPR. Additionally, further investigation
of feature relevance within each current direction is needed.
This could include using data with a lower stimulation intensity relative to current directions
to investigate if this introduces a clearer relationship and, consequently, a more generalizable
model.
Furthermore, using two new datasets where as many factors as possible are kept steady
for training and testing. This would provide grounds for exploring the possibility of model-
ing the relationship with fewer unknowns. If this results in better model performance when

Conclusions
9
Conclusions
The aim of this thesis was to investigate how different components of the iTEPs contribute
to MEPs elicited by biphasic TMS with APPA and PAAP current directions. Furthermore,
the thesis examined whether this relationship could be modeled using machine learning, and
whether such models generalize across different datasets.
A successful separation of the iTEP components enabled analysis of how they contribute
differently to cortical processing. Visual inspection suggested that iTEP components differ
between current directions, a finding supported by statistical analysis, which revealed sig-
nificant differences in peak 1 and peak 3 of the synchronized component.
Using features from the separated components, GPR successfully predicted MEPs within
individual datasets, suggesting it is possible to model the relationship between iTEP fea-
tures and MEPs using machine learning. This supports the hypothesis that iTEPs reflect
cortical processes underlying the generation of descending motor signals.
To explore how individual features contributed depending on current direction, feature rel-
evance was assessed using ARD. It showed that different iTEP components seem to play
distinct roles in predicting MEPs depending on the current direction used. Using the APPA
current direction, the earlier peaks of the synchronized component appear to be the most
influential; however, this differs slightly between datasets. In the PAAP current direction,
the second peak of the synchronized component and the unsynchronized component seem
to be the key contributors. These results support the idea that the iTEP components con-
tribute to explaining the difference in MEP latency and that they result from the activation
of two distinct networks. Notably, the results indicate a considerable contribution of the
unsynchronized component to MEPs in the APPA direction, which is opposite to what was
suggested by Stango et al.
Finally, when the trained models were applied to new data, their predictive performance
decreased substantially. This highlights a limitation in the generalizability of the models
and points to the need for further methodological refinement.
These findings underscore the potential of iTEPs as a non-invasive index of corticospinal
excitability, but also emphasize the need for further methodological development before gen-
eralizable models can be established.
