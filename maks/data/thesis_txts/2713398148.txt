Project description 
PKC manufactures dextran derivates with different functionalities. In this project, new dextran 
derivates with new functionalities will be investigated. To obtain new functionality changes in the 
polymer, a “two-step” side-modification will be investigated. The first step will be the attachment of 
a functional linker to the backbone. In the second step, functional groups are further attached to the 
functional linker to achieve new properties. As part of this effort, the following work packages must 
be completed.  
1. Conduct a literature search for linkers with an alkyne moiety as the non-participating 
functional group on Dextran. Conduct a literature search for linkers with an alkyne moiety as 
the non-participating functional group on dextran.  
2. Investigate a minimum of three different linkers, where Glycidyl propargyl Ether is 
mandatory and the remaining two are optional. 
3. Investigate how the degree of substitution can be controlled. 
4. Attempt different modifications of the produced dextran-linkers via e.g., CuAAC. 
5. Assess how these side-chain modifications are analyzed. 
If time allows it, the following objectives could be investigated. 
1. Investigate the same linkers on other biopolymers as the backbone. 
2. Analyse these side-chain modifications.

Abstract 
This project is based on the synthesis of side-chain functionalized dextran’s. Initially, a functional 
linker with a terminal C≡C bond has been introduced by either etherification or esterification of 
Dextran 10.  
The first etherification-reaction has been performed via. Williamson’s ether synthesis with 4-bromo-
1-butyne or Propargyl bromide. The Sn2 reaction was first attempted with 4-bromo-1-butyne but 
there was no indication of the linker from the analysis. However, the synthesis succeeded with 
Propargyl bromide, which led to different conversions rate depending on the reaction conditions.  
The second etherification-reaction has been performed by a nucleophilic ring-opening reaction with 
Glycidyl propargyl ether, which showed higher conversion rate compared to Propargyl bromide. It 
has been possible to obtain different degree of substitution depending on the base concentration, 
reaction time, temperature, and molar ratio from the reaction with Glycidyl propargyl ether.  
The third side-chain functionalization was performed by reacting 5-hexynoic acid to a CDI-activated 
Dextran 10, which was the only ester-derivate compared to the two ether-derivates. All experiments 
led to formation of lumps. The most possible reason to formation of lumps is cross-linking of Dextran 
10 with unreacted CDI which leads to insoluble products and formation of lumps. In contrast, the 
remaining product, separated from the lump, resembled the product. 
The following step was the introduction of new functionalities to dextran via Copper(I)-catalyzed 
Alkyne-Azide Cycloaddition. The prepared side-chain modifications bearing a terminal C≡C bond were 
reacted with two different azides: Methoxypolyethylene glycol azide and Rhodamine B-PEG2K-N3. 
The click reactions with both azides had occurred but due to more hydrophilic character obtained 
from Polyethylene glycol, the product was not able to be precipitated in the attempted solvents and 
thereby residues from the starting materials were not removed. 
Finally, two side-chain modification of particles were prepared from Glycidyl propargyl ether and 
Propargyl bromide reacted with Degradable dextran microspheres A (DDMA). Those DDMA-
modification were reacted successfully with both Methoxypolyethylene glycol azide and Rhodamine 
B-PEG2K-N3.

Resumé 
Dette projekt er baseret på syntesen af sidekædefunktionaliserede dextraner. Indledningsvis er en 
funktionel linker med en terminal C≡C binding blevet indført ved enten etherificering eller 
esterificering af Dextran 10. 
Den første etherreaktion er udført via. Williamsons ether syntese med 4-brom-1-butyn eller 
propargylbromid. Sn2-reaktionen blev først forsøgt med 4-brom-1-butyn, men der var ingen 
indikation af linkeren fra analysering af produktet. Syntesen lykkedes derimod med Propargylbromid. 
Den anden etherificeringsreaktion er blevet udført ved en nukleofil ringåbningsreaktion med 
Glycidylpropargylether, som gav højere substitution sammenlignet med Propargylbromid. Det har 
været muligt at opnå forskellig grad af substitution afhængigt af basekoncentrationen, 
reaktionstiden, temperaturen og molforholdet fra reaktionen med Glycidylpropargylether. 
Den tredje sidekædefunktionalisering blev udført ved at omsætte 5-hexynoic acid til en CDI-aktiveret 
Dextran 10, som var det eneste esterderivat sammenlignet med de to etherderivater. Alle forsøg 
førte til klumpdannelse. Den mest mulige årsag til dannelse af klumper under syntesen, er 
krydsbinding af Dextran 10 med ureageret CDI, hvilket fører til uopløselige produkter og dannelse af 
klumper. Derimod lignede det resterende produkt den ønskede modificerede dextran. 
Det følgende trin introducerede nye funktionelle grupper til dextran via kobber(I)-katalyseret Alkyn-
Azide Cycloaddition. De fremstillede sidekædemodifikationer med terminale C≡C-bindinger, blev 
omsat med to forskellige azider: Methoxypolyethylenglycolazid og Rhodamine B-PEG2K-N3. 
Klikreaktionerne med begge azider lykkedes, men da PEG-grupperne medfører et mere hydrofilt 
produkt, var det ikke muligt at udfælde produkterne i de anvendte opløsningsmidler. Urenheder blev 
derfor ikke fjernet. 
Til sidst blev der fremstillet to sidekædemodifikationer på partikler ved at reagere 
Glycidylpropargylether og Propargylbromid med DDMA. Disse DDMA-modifikationer lykkedes med 
både Methoxypolyethylenglycolazid og Rhodamine B-PEG2K-N3.

1. Introduction 
pK Chemicals A/S (PKC) is a Danish Company established in 1964 under the name Pharmacia, where 
modified biopolymers have been produced most of the time. The company has changed its name 
through the years and the products manufactured have increased significantly. Today PKC 
manufactures biopolymers and modified biopolymers in the production sites in Køge and Hårlev. PKC 
manufactures dextran’s and dextran derivates, which is the majority of the production, but lately, 
they have started their production of hydrolyzed starch as well.  The reason for that is the production 
of degradable starch microspheres (DSM-A), which process has been developed and optimized in the 
latest years. The development of new and existing products is in high demand at PKC, and so is the 
investigation of biopolymers and their modifications.  
The general use of polymers has increased markedly over other types of material due to their many 
possible applications in industries of electronics, healthcare, packaging of foods, and many others. 
The rapid growth of conventional polymers and new technologies have made them prevalent in daily 
life. Due to the increasing cost of petroleum and other fossil-based fuels, alternative methods of 
creating polymers are necessary. Furthermore, there has been a growing concern about the impact 
of environmental pollution from fossil-based fuels and the waste from petrochemical products. 1 
The interest in more environmentally friendly materials and sustainable developments has 
expanded, and so has the interest in biopolymers as an alternative to conventional polymers. 
Biopolymers are produced or derived from a living organism and have the potential to create a 
sustainable industry. Biopolymers are a sustainable alternative to synthetic polymers from petroleum 
because they are naturally degradable and because they are renewable sources. Biopolymers are still 
in development since they are a new generation of materials with a lot of undiscovered properties 
and applications.  
Biopolymers can be modified in many ways as a key tool for amending their chemical and physical 
properties. The most common way is by direct attachment and modification to the backbone, but a 
2-step modification is another possibility. Introducing a functional linker with new functionalities 
allows for other types of reactions and makes it possible to introduce new desired properties.   
The scope of this project is to obtain knowledge about biopolymers and modified biopolymers with 
green chemistry and sustainability in mind. Thus, the synthesis will be executed under aqueous 
conditions, if possible, to obtain a greener synthesis pathway.

2. Literature study  
2.1 Biopolymers and polysaccharides 
Biopolymers are naturally occurring polymers produced by the cells of living organisms. They consist 
of monomeric units which are covalently bonded to form the larger molecule of the biopolymer. The 
classification of biopolymers can be done differently and based on their origin, they can be 
distinguished into microbial, natural, and synthetic biopolymers. They can also be classified to be 
either biodegradable or non-biodegradable but typically, the classifications are based on the type of 
monomers where we find: Proteins, nucleic acids, and polysaccharides. All three groups of 
biopolymers contain good properties within different areas. However, this project will focus on 
polysaccharides and modified polysaccharides due to the availability of those at PKC.2 
Polysaccharides are the most abundant and naturally macromolecular polymers which exist in many 
living forms. They have important biological functions in their pristine structure and are a promising 
candidate in biomedical and pharmaceutical fields due to their physicochemical properties, which 
can be physical or chemically modified easy to enhance their properties. Polysaccharides are made of 
monosaccharide units linked by glycosidic bonds. If the chemical structure only consists of the same 
monosaccharide, the polysaccharide is classified as a homopolysaccharide. However, some 
polysaccharides consist of different monosaccharides and are hence called heteropolysaccharides. 
Scheme 2.1 shows examples of mono- and polysaccharides.   
 
Scheme 2.1: Example of a monosaccharide and a polysaccharide. 
Dextran, cellulose, starch, and chitosan are some of the most common polysaccharides on earth. 
They are obtained from different renewable sources like plant origin e.g., cellulose, microbial origin, 
e.g., dextran, and animal origin e.g., chitosan. The availability of polysaccharides makes them perfect 
as starting materials for chemical modifications. Polysaccharides and their derivatives are used in 
building materials, food, pharmaceuticals, textiles, fuel, cosmetics, and many other fields. In the food 
industry, they are often used for the preservation of confections, softener in baked goods, and as

thickeners in dressings. In the cosmetic industry, they are widely used as viscosity enhancers, 
stabilizers, and moisturizers. 3 
2.1.1 Cellulose and starch 
Cellulose is the most abundant polysaccharide and biopolymer since it is found widely in nature due 
to the major constitution of cell walls of plants. Regarding the chemical structure, cellulose is one of 
the simplest natural polysaccharides and consists of single repeating units of glucose, which are 
linked through β-1,4 linkages. Cellulose can be completely hydrolyzed to glucose units, which consists 
of two secondary alcohols and one primary alcohol.4 The structure of cellulose is shown in Scheme 
2.2 
 
Scheme 2.2: Structure of cellulose, showing the beta-linkages between carbon 1 and 4 between the glucose units. All 
substitutions (not hydrogen) are equatorial. 
Starch is another abundant polysaccharide that occurs naturally in granules in seeds, roots, and 
stems in many types of plants including wheat, potatoes, and corn. Structurally starch differs from 
cellulose since the glucose units are linked through α-1,4 linkages and because starch consists of two 
polysaccharides, amylose, and amylopectin. Amylose is a linear structure and resembles cellulose, 
where amylopectin is highly branched through carbon 6, which is shown in Scheme 2.3. The ratio 
between amylopectin and amylose in natural starch is normally 3:1. 4 
 
Scheme 2.3: Structure of starch consisting of amylose and amylopectin, showing the alpha-linkages between carbon 1 and 4 
between the glucose units. All substitutions (not hydrogen) are equatorial. 
 
==PAGE:13==

Cellulose and starch are applied in many industries including the medical industry. However, the 
highly branched structure of amylopectin and the high solubility capacity in starch, give cellulose and 
starch very different properties. Cellulose can be suitable for plastics and fibers because it would not 
dissolve in water. On the other hand, starch can be useful in foods because animals have the 
necessary enzyme to hydrolyze α-linkages.4 
A large number of derivates have been synthesized for cellulose and starch. Most polysaccharides 
are structurally very similar, but can differ in e.g., monomer type and linkages between monomers, 
permeability, amount of primary and secondary alcohols, and branching. However, a modification 
method working on one polysaccharide will work on other polysaccharides too, due to their 
similarity. There are many types of modifications applied to especially cellulose, but a lot of 
modifications have been applied to starch as well. Both polysaccharides have two secondary alcohols 
and one primary alcohol, and most modifications will include reactions with the primary alcohol as it 
is less sterically hindered. Another common type of polysaccharide is dextran, which structurally 
varies a lot from cellulose and starch since it is based on another monomer and only has three 
secondary alcohols.

2.1.2 Dextran 
Dextran is a heterogeneous class of glucans made by polymerization of α-D-glucopyranosyl from 
sucrose. The common feature of dextran is the α-D-1,6 linkage between each α-D-glucopyranosyl 
unit in the backbone and with branches from α-D-1,3 linkages. Commercially dextran is synthesized 
by the bacterium Leuconostoc mesenteroides starting with the fermentation of sucrose. The 
bacterium can grow on most mediums containing sucrose and a few inorganic salts and a nitrogen 
source. Depending on the source and substrates the structures of dextran are varying in molecular 
weights and branching.  There are several commercially available dextrans but the most important 
are dextran 40 and dextran 70. The numbers refer to the molecular weight of the dextran in the 
preparation. Commercial dextran has a degree of branching of about 5 %, where about 85 % of the 
sidechains are single or double units of α-D-glucopyranosyl.5 The structure of dextran is shown in 
Scheme 2.4. 
 
Scheme 2.4: Structure of dextran showing the alpha-linkages between carbon 1 and 6 between the glucose units and the 
branching from carbon 3. 
The molecular weight of dextran’s can be controlled by hydrolysis. By adding a strong acid to the 
polysaccharide chain, the chain breaks into monosaccharides and oligosaccharides and the average 
chain length will decrease. The addition of the base will terminate the acid hydrolysis and the desired 
length of the chain can be obtained.  
Dextran’s are neutral polysaccharides that are easily soluble in water, formamide, and glycerol, but 
the solubility depends on the molecular weight and especially the branching because it affects the 
interaction with water through hydrogen bridges. Some fractions of dextran require heating to bring 
the fraction into solution. Dextran’s are biocompatible and biodegradable and stable for more than 
five years, which is important considering dextran as starting material for medical substances and 
other applications. 3

2.1.3 Application of dextrans 
Dextran’s are neutral and hydrophilic polymers that can be fully degraded in biological systems. They 
are not broken down by human digestive enzymes but degraded by bacteria in the large intestine. 
They have been described and patented many times in foods but are not permitted as additives in 
Europe and USA. However, they are widely used in pharmaceutical and biomedical applications, 
where they can be degraded by the dextranase enzyme from the tissue. 5 
Dextran’s are widely used in the improvement of moisture retention and viscosity. The applications 
vary from dextran to dextran because of the difference in molecular weight and branching, and they 
are often divided into high molecular weight dextran’s >40 kDa and low molecular weight dextran’s 
<40 kDa. Low molecular weight dextran’s are typically used for clinical applications.6 Dextran can 
prevent protein opsonization and is thereby used for surface modification which is used in cancer 
therapy. 7 
Clinical grade dextran with high molecular weight, 40.000, 60.000, and 70.000 g/mol are often used 
for replacing moderate blood losses. Technical dextran’s are common in many industries. Fractions 
with low molecular weight distribution can be used as the SEC standard for molecular weight 
determination. Dextran’s are also an ingredient in many cosmetics due to their moisturizing 
properties. 7

2.2 Chemical modification of polysaccharides  
In the field of green chemistry, modifications of polysaccharides are central. Polysaccharides are 
produced in large amounts and have a large structural diversity, which makes them perfect as 
starting materials for different modifications.8 Polysaccharide modifications can be divided into three 
major categories as shown in  Figure 2.1. 
 
Figure 2.1: Modification methods of polysaccharides divided into chemical, physical, and enzymatic modification. Chemical 
modification is further divided into degradation, substitution, and cross-linking. 
The above figure shows the most common methods of providing improved properties of 
polysaccharides. Chemical modification of polysaccharides is the most common and known 
modification method, but there has been a growing interest in the field of physical modification such 
as temperature treatment and radiation of polysaccharides because they are often simple and cheap. 
Enzymatic modifications have recently replaced some chemical methods since they are safer and 
healthier, which is especially important in the food industry where they are often applied. However, 
the possibilities in chemical modifications are much bigger compared to the others, and with an 
increased focus on green chemistry, this category of modification will be further investigated. 9 
Chemical modification is an important approach to tailor polysaccharides and thereby change the 
biological activity by introducing hydrophobicity, acidic, basic, or other functionality into the 
structures.8 The simplest polysaccharides including dextran and starch consist of repeating 
monosaccharides with hydroxyl groups as the only functional groups. Some polysaccharides also 
consist of acids or nitrogen-containing functional groups, but those will not be considered. The 
number and positions of the hydroxyl groups vary from one monosaccharide to another. Depending 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
17 
 
on the reaction and amounts of reactants, a different number of hydroxyl groups can react with the 
monosaccharides. The term, degree of substitution (DS) of a polymer is the average number of 
substituents groups attached per base unit. In the case of dextran, the base unit is α-D-
glucopyranosyl which has three reactive OH-groups. The DS can thereby range from zero (no 
substitution) to three (full substitution). 
The DS is an important property regarding the modification of polymers but can be difficult to control 
since polysaccharides are long, sometimes branched molecules with different molecular weights. 
Figure 2.2 shows the many possible substitutions of the repeating units in polysaccharides. In many 
reactions, further substitution can take place on the initial modification. 8 
 
 
Figure 2.2: Different substitutions patterns in polysaccharides. 8 
Polysaccharides can be chemically modified through homogeneously dispersed molecules or on the 
surface to obtain different functionality. Derivates capable of forming hydrogels and micelles are also 
possible by different modification techniques, which showcases the many possibilities for chemically 
modifying polysaccharides.8 Some of the most common modification methods are shown Scheme 
2.5. 
==PAGE:18==

Scheme 2.5: Common chemical modification techniques of polysaccharides. 
Scheme 2.5 shows the most conventional chemical modification techniques of polysaccharides 
containing only hydroxyl groups. The methods are esterification, etherification, grafting, oxidation, 
and cross-linking. Oxidation of polysaccharides is a very common method for converting both 
primary and secondary hydroxyl groups to aldehydes or carboxyl groups. The oxidation can be 
performed in different ways to produce different types of structures whose physicochemical 
properties strongly depend on factors such as the acidity of the oxidation medium and the nature of 
the oxidizing reagent.10 In general the oxidation of polysaccharides leads to better water solubility 
and lower viscosity which can be exploited in further reactions. Oxidations of primary alcohols can be 
done chemically or by enzymes and lead to the conversion of aldehydes or further to carboxylic 
acids. Another possible reaction is the oxidative cleavage of 1,2-diols. Both reactions are shown in 
Scheme 2.6.

Oxidation is a challenging topic due to the presence of multiple hydroxyl groups on the 
monosaccharides, which makes it difficult to perform selective reactions. A lot of studies have been 
done to improve the selectivity of oxidized products and especially the oxidation of cellulose. One of 
the most selective reactions reported is the reaction of cellulose with liquid or gaseous nitrogen 
dioxide, leading to the conversion of the primary alcohols.11 Oxidized cellulose can be used in the 
medical industry as a hemostatic during surgery. 10 
2.2.1 Analysis of polysaccharides and dextran  
The most fundamental understanding of polysaccharides and their derivative’s functions and 
properties are given from structural analysis and knowledge about substituent distribution. The 
understanding of the relation between structure and properties such as viscosity and solubility can 
help improve those properties in the preparation of modified polysaccharides. Structure analysis of 
polysaccharides differs a lot from analysis of small molecules since most polysaccharides have a high 
level of diversity and irregularity, which can make the structural analysis very complex.  
The structure analysis usually starts with the determination of DS, which can be done by titration, 
GLC after depolymerization, and NMR spectroscopy. NMR spectroscopy can also give sight into the 
structure of polysaccharides, which makes the method obvious regarding structural analysis of 
polysaccharides. The structure of dextran and its derivates are usually determined by 1H-NMR and

2.2.2 Chemical modification of dextran 
In the last few decades, a lot of dextran esters have been synthesized. Esterification of 
polysaccharides can lead to inorganic or organic esters. Regarding dextran, phosphates and sulfates 
have gained the most interest among inorganic esters, because of the improved water solubility. 
Dextran phosphates can be achieved by treatment with polyphosphoric acid in formamide and 
sulfates can be achieved by sulfating agents like chlorosulfonic acid and sulfur trioxide. However, the 
application of sulfur complexes and organic bases can be favorable since the above-mentioned can 
react strongly with water. In the field of organic esters, the use of dextran esters with short aliphatic 
chains is limited because the reaction with acetyl chlorides or anhydrides does not lead to pure and 
soluble products. However, dextran esters of longer aliphatic chains such as butyrates are easily 
prepared by using carboxylic acid anhydrides in heterogeneous solutions with pyridine. The different 
chain length of the carboxylic acid and the DS influences the solubility of the dextran. 8,7 
Dextran ethers are not as common as esters. However, the introduction of ether-type moieties leads 
to more stable dextran derivatives. The carboxymethylation of dextran in water/organic solvent 
mixed with monochloric acid is one of the most produced dextran ethers. The derivative is an 
important substance in the preparation of heparin-like polymers used to interfere with blood 
coagulation.  
Cross-linking has also received attention as a modification strategy for dextran and can be achieved 
by physical interactions and/or chemical reactions. By cross-linking dextran with epichlorohydrin the 
formation of two commercial products named Sephadex and Debrisan arises. Those products are 
used for gel filtration and wound cleansing. Furthermore, the reaction of bifunctional reagents e.g., 
diisocyanates with dextrans or acylation reactions leads to the formation of hydrogels. Hydrogels 
have received increased attention due to their properties as efficient matrix systems in polymer 
separation.  7 
In the preparation of modified dextran’s and other polysaccharides, regioselective reactions have 
been investigated. The purpose of regioselective reactions is to design well-defined structures and 
achieve a degree of substitution close to one. If this happens, further modifications can be 
introduced at specific positions to obtain the structure relevant for the purpose. Some 
monosaccharides contain both primary and secondary hydroxyl groups, which allows some reactions 
to happen at specific positions. In this context, the use of protection groups can also be considered.  
==PAGE:22==
 
 
22 
 
The practical work with regioselectivity is not easy with polysaccharides as monosaccharides are 
covalently linked to each other. The desired regioisomer can be purified from other components in 
monomeric structures by chromatography or crystallization. This is not possible if two 
monosaccharides with correct and incorrect modifications are linked by covalent bonds.  8

2.3 Introduction of a functional linker to polysaccharides 
The following sections cover some common modification techniques to introduce a functional linker 
to the polysaccharides by reacting the hydroxyl groups in the monomer. The most common way to 
let the hydroxyl groups react is with the saccharide oxygen as a nucleophile.  This category describes 
reactions where saccharide oxygen acts as a nucleophile and is retained in the synthesized product. 8 
2.3.1 Etherification  
Etherification describes the reaction of an alcohol with an alkylating agent in the presence of a base. 
The base is used to deprotonate the alcohol to give the alkoxide ion, which then acts as a 
nucleophile. The overall reaction is shown in Scheme 2.7. 
 
Scheme 2.7: General reaction of alkylation of polysaccharides. 
The Williamson Ether synthesis is the most widely used method to form ethers. The reaction is an 
Sn2 reaction where the alkoxide ion from the polysaccharide displaces a halide ion from an alkyl 
halide to give an ether. The synthesis is the easiest and perhaps the fastest way to create ethers, but 
they can be limited by competition from elimination reactions. Benzylation and alkylation are two 
widely used reactions of Williamson Ether synthesis. 12 A common way to add a nitrile group to a 
polysaccharide is by cyanoethylation. The addition of acrylonitrile leads to Michael addition of the 
polysaccharide and the product is very used as a precursor for amino-functionalized 
polysaccharides.13  Another very common and straightforward method is the reaction with epoxide. 
In the presence of a base, this leads to a nucleophilic ring-opening reaction, which is also a very 
common way to synthesize ethers of polysaccharides. 14 All the above-mentioned etherification 
reactions are shown in Scheme 2.8. 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
24 
 
 
Scheme 2.8: Common etherification methods.15 
Etherification is a widely used modification strategy for a lot of reasons. The reactions have the 
advantage of comparatively easy access to most reagents. The reagents are usually more easily 
handled than reagents used in esterification. Furthermore, ethers are much more resistant to further 
reactions than esters, which is crucial for a 2-step modification technique. In contrast etherification 
typically involves strongly basic conditions e.g., NaOH or NaH. Thus, reactions with base-sensitive 
moieties and most difunctional reagents are not possible. Further, tedious steps, modest yields, and 
long reaction times can be associated with the etherification of polysaccharides depending on the 
pathway. 8 
The reactivity of etherification in homogeneous reactions of polysaccharides is in the position order: 
[HO-] 6>2>3/4 for polysaccharides with one primary hydroxyl group. The reactivity of dextran’s 
depends on the equatorially oriented secondary hydroxyl groups: [HO-] 2, 3, and 4.  The reactivity of 
HO-2 is high due to higher acidity because of proximity to the anomeric center and the position order 
is: [HO-] 2>4>3. The level of branching influences the reactivity of dextran’s. If the level of branching 
at HO-3 is 5 %, there will be 5 % less HO-3 and 5 % more 6-OH, and then the end-groups cannot be 
neglected anymore. 3 
Williamson ether synthesis 
Etherification of polysaccharides via. Williamson’s ether synthesis has been described successfully in 
many papers.  Among alkylation reactions, methyl ethers of polysaccharides are one of the most 
important and have proved to be valuable derivatives due to their stability towards alkali and acids. 
In the early fifties, methylation of polysaccharides was prepared by the reaction with sodium and

2.3.2 Esterification 
Esterification is the equilibrium reaction of acids and alcohols to form esters. The reaction is an 
acylation which is very common in laboratory and living organisms. The alcohol reacts with a 
carboxylic acid to form the ester as shown in Scheme 2.12.  
 
Scheme 2.12: Esterification reaction. The reaction is reversible.   
There are several pathways to synthesize esters which include esters from carboxylic acids, acid 
chlorides, and acid anhydrides, which require strong acids. Another milder approach is the Steglich 
esterification using DCC and DMAP, which allows the conversion of acid-labile substrates.  However, 
a lot of other approaches with different solvents can be used in reactions of esters. The methods are 
all shown in Scheme 2.13. 
Esterification of polysaccharides is an effective solution to achieve thermal plasticity, water 
resistance, and solubility in organic solvents. It is a common modification approach that is very 
straightforward and has the advantage of comparatively easy access to most reagents. 21 
Some difficulties regarding the esterification process, depending on the pathway, is the requirement 
of strong acid, which makes the reaction impossible if there are any acid-labile groups present.  
Furthermore, most steps in the mechanism are reversible where the most stable species (the ester) 
will dominate at the end due to the Le Chateliers princip. In the meantime, the system will seesaw 
through the mechanism steps, which can take a very long time. When the ester is formed water will 
be produced as well, which pushes the reaction towards the reactant and dilutes the reaction 
mixture.  
Esterification of polysaccharides gives access to a wide range of products. The commercial processes 
of esterification are carried out under heterogeneous conditions, because of polysaccharides’ high 
viscosity and costs of solvents. However, a lot of procedures involve homogenous conditions due to 
higher DS. Concerning the reactivity of esterification of polysaccharides, the major differences are 
due to electronic reasons. The rate of esterification is usually in the position order: [HO-] 6>2>3/4 for 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
28 
 
polysaccharides with one primary hydroxyl group. In the case of dextran with only secondary 
hydroxyl groups the acylation reactivity is: [HO-] 2>4>3.22

carboxylic acid with CDI and hence a homogeneous synthesis with the polysaccharides. The method 
is particularly suitable for biopolymers because the by-products formed are CO2 and imidazole. 
Imidazole is soluble in many solvents including water and alcohol and thereby easy to remove.

Afterward, the reaction with polysaccharides gives the ester product.7

2.4.1 Click chemistry – Copper-catalyzed alkyne-azide cycloaddition (CuAAC) 
The introduction of click chemistry has changed the way material design problems are approached 
due to a lot of reasons. Click reactions are in general reactions with easy elimination of the by-
product, employment of mild reaction conditions, water compatibility, simple purification process, 
and formation of a single product in high yield. Outstanding properties like these, make click 
reactions green and facile. 
In 2002, the well-known Copper-catalyzed 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition reaction between alkyne and 
azides was discovered by Meldal and Sharpless. The cycloaddition reaction is similar to the classical 
Huisgen cycloaddition reaction, where an organic azide reacts with an alkyne at temperatures around 
120°C to form a triazole ring.  However, the reaction proceeds faster in the presence of Cu(I) and the 
only product formed is the 1,4-disubstituted ring in contrast to classical Huisgen where 1,5-
disubstituted triazole regioisomer is formed too. A 1,5-disubstituted triazole can be formed 
selectively in the same reaction but the presence of a ruthenium (II) catalyst. Scheme 2.17 shows all 
three cycloadditions of alkynes and azides. 3 
 
Scheme 2.17: Three different reactions of azide-alkyne cycloadditions. The classical Huisgen reaction yields two different 
regioisomers and is very slow compared to CuAAC and RuAAC yielding one regioisomer each. 
The catalyst chosen is most often copper due to the reaction rate being much faster than most other 
catalysts. Most often, Cu(I) is added as a salt or generated by in situ reduction of copper (II) salts in a 
reductive environment. The latter is often chosen due to high purity of the active catalyst, low cost, 
and stability to oxygen. 26 
The CuAAC reaction yields 1,4-disubstituted-1,2,3-trizoles shown in Scheme 2.17. Triazoles are an 
important class of heterocycles with applications in material science. They have several applications 
as corrosion inhibitors, photostabilizers, photographic materials and dyes. Click reactions have many 
advantages as described earlier, but there are still many challenges remaining. The final product can 
==PAGE:33==

be contaminated by copper particles under homogeneous conditions since the separation of the 
catalyst are very difficult and often requires chromatographic purification. To overcome this 
problem, recent works have shown great results using heterogenous catalytic systems which 
provides easier handling and easier separation of the catalyst from the product. 26 
Since the establishment of CuAAC, a lot of polysaccharides have been modified with this chemistry 
including dextran. S. C. Abeylath al. has prepared O-pentynyl dextran with 5-chloro-1-pentyne in a 
Sn2 reaction and hence synthesized three different dextran-products via click chemistry. Those three

2.4.2 Surface modification of microparticles  
Another widely used application of click chemistry is modifications of microparticles. Microparticles 
are solid powders synthesized from biodegradable proteins or polymers and can be divided into 
microspheres and microcapsules. The spherical microparticle is solid or hollow and has no fluid inside 
which separates them from microcapsules.  Microparticles have many applications in material 
science and technology including liquid chromatography, affinity bio separation and absorbents.  The 
applications largely depend on the type of polymer, porosity, size, and cross-linking. The cross-linking 
improves the mechanical stability of the polymer and prevents disintegration in solvents.  
One of the most important properties is the colloidal stability of the microparticle. Surface 
functionalization can be applied to ensure the microparticle is compatible with its environment and 
that the colloidal systems remains dispersed. By modifying the surface of the microparticle with click 
chemistry, any desired functionality can be obtained. One major advantage compared to other 
modifications strategies of microparticles is that click chemistry can be applied to many different 
colloidal particle systems.28   
Microparticles play an important role in drug delivery systems, especially small-molecule 
pharmaceuticals, due to their swelling, permeability, and small sizes. The drug can be encapsulated 
or entrapped in the microsphere because of the strong network formed by the cross-linking. Here 
they can deliver and locally release drugs in a very controlled way compared to other delivery 
systems. 29 
PKC produces degradable starch microspheres A (DSMA), which is microparticles based on starch. 
However, the synthesis can also be performed with dextran as polymer (DDMA) due to the similarity 
of starch and dextran. The most common way to produce the microparticles is in a batch-emulsion 
technique which is also used in the process of DSMA. This technique exploits that immiscible oil and 
an aqueous precursor generate droplets, which can be cross-linked by a cross-linker to form the 
microparticle in the presence of a surfactant to stabilize the emulsion. This is illustrated in Figure 2.6.

Derivatives of polysaccharides are of great interest because of their applicability and their many 
properties. Dextran’s are widely used in especially medical applications since they are biocompatible

dextran derivatives regarding their applications and physiological activity, but only very few contain

3. Project plan  
This project aims to investigate a two-step modification of a biopolymer, which in this project will be

arises from the terminal hydrogen from the alkyne at 2.95 ppm. The terminal hydrogen is split by the 
hydrogens across the triple bond even though it is not the neighbor carbon, due to the allylic 
coupling. The relation between the terminal hydrogen and the anomeric hydrogen from Dextran 10 
was used to determine the DS.  Some of the products were analyzed in 13C-NMR, which also 
confirmed the reaction from the two new peaks at 79.37 and 82.47 ppm, corresponding to the two 
carbons in the alkyne, see appendix 9.1.3.  
The first synthesis with PB (A5) was performed in 1:1 molar ratio between Dextran 10 and PB. The 
temperature was 35 °C and the mixture reacted for 18 hours giving a DS = 0.23. In order to control 
and improve the DS, the reaction conditions were varied. One experiment (A7) was performed at 
55°C for 3 hours, but all other conditions remained the same, see Table 4.1. The DS decreased 
markedly to a DS = 0.12 indicating that the reaction time at 3 hours were not enough or/and the 
higher temperature damaged the reaction.  To confirm which factor influenced the reaction the 
most, new experiments with only one variable could be done, but time did not allow for this 
investigation.  
The time did allow for the investigation of using NaBH4 in the reaction. NaBH4 reduces the aldehydes 
in the polysaccharide to primary alcohols leading to more reactive groups. However, it was seen that 
removing the reducing agent from the reaction, and keeping all other factors the same, the DS 
increased from 0.23 to 0.33. Furthermore, two experiments with a molar ratio of 1:3 between 
Dextran 10 and PB were performed (A6 + A9), one with and one without NaBH4. Both experiments 
got a higher DS due to the higher amount of linker, but again the DS became highest for the 
experiment without NaBH4.

4.2 Side-chain modification of dextran via. nucleophilic ring-opening reaction 
According to the project plan, another two side-chain modification should be investigated. One of 
them is the nucleophilic ring-opening reaction which is common in the preparation of modified 
polysaccharides. This method has been described in many papers with water as solvent, but with 
different bases and different concentrations, where the majority used NaOH in 0.1 M or 0.5 M 
solution. In section 2.3.1, two methods were described for the preparation of Dextran-AGE and 
Dextran-GPE.19,20 The papers have been an inspiration for the preparation of this side-chain 
modification of dextran. Glycidyl propargyl ether (GPE) was chosen as linker and the reaction scheme 
is shown in Scheme 4.3.

which is shown in Table 4.3.

4.3 Side-chain modification of dextran via. CDI coupling  
Besides the two side-chain ether modifications, the project plan also stated to investigate a side-
chain modification of dextran with an ester-derivate. The most traditional esterification from 
carboxylic acids requires strong acid to form the ester. To avoid the harsh conditions another milder 
approach was attempted. The investigation was performed with 5-hexynoic acid (HA), which was 
prepared in DMF with CDI to activate the linker before reaction with dextran. This pathway was also 
chosen because the by-products formed were CO2 and imidazole. Thereby the purification process 
only required water and ethanol to separate the by-products from the product and to remove 
remains of DMF. The reaction scheme is shown in Scheme 4.5.  
 
Scheme 4.5: Reaction scheme of 1) Activation of 5-hexynoic acid with CDI. 2) Reaction of activation acid with Dextran 10. 
In the first experiment with HA (C1), the synthesis was reacted in 1.5 hours before addition of 
Dextran 10 (Step 1). Dextran 10 was then added (Step 2) but it was observed that after one hour of 
reaction time, some of the reaction mixture started to form a lump. The most likely reason for this 
lump is due to cross-linking of Dextran 10 with unreacted CDI which happens if CDI is not completely 
consumed before addition of Dextran 10. This could lead to insoluble products and formation of 
lumps.  
After 4 hours of reaction time, the product was precipitated in ethanol, and it was observed that 
some of the product still was a lump and some of the product was a free substance. The product was 
purified 3 times in ethanol and analyzed in FT-IR an 1H-NMR. Both methods showed that DMF was 
not separated from the product in the purification, and it was then decided to change the 
purification process in the next experiment (C2). Instead of purification 3 times in pure ethanol, a

hours to avoid cross-linking. However, the lump was still formed, but first after 2 hours.

4.4 Side-chain modification of DDMA  
From the project plan it is seen that if time allowed, the side-chain modification would be 
investigated with cross-linked dextran (DDMA) instead of dextran. There are pros and cons when 
working with particles. The chemical synthesis is relatively straightforward for both dextran and 
dextran particles. However, particles will never be part of a full homogenous synthesis, which can 
influence the reactivity which also depends much on the size of the particles. The purification step 
also differs since the modified dextran must be precipitated, which can be challenging working with 
modified polysaccharides. On the other hand, particles can easily be purified on a filter.

4.5 Copper-catalyzed Alkyne-Azide Cycloaddition of Dextran-GPE and MPGA 
As proposed in the project plan, the prepared modified dextrans, bearing a reactive carbon-carbon 
triple bond, should be reacted with methoxypolyethylene glycol azide (MPGA) in a copper catalyzed 
alkyne-azide cycloaddition. A variety of different applications could be targeted by this chemistry by 
choosing different azides with different properties. The click-reaction has due to its many advantages 
such as mild reaction conditions, selectivity and high yields received a lot of attention during the past 
years.31 However, the chosen azide reacted with the synthesized Dextran-GPE were a PEG-azide due 
to the many favorable properties. PEGs are often used as thickeners, softeners, and moisture 
carriers, and are furthermore soluble in many organic solvents but also in water. This is favorable 
since the modified Dextran-GPE are soluble in water too, making the click-reaction possible in water. 
The reaction scheme for this click reaction are shown in Scheme 4.7. 
 
Scheme 4.7: Synthesis of Dextran-GPE-MPGA by CuAAC reaction of alkyne groups along the backbone of Dextran-GPE with 
MPGA. 
By reacting Dextran-GPE and MPGA in a molar ratio of 6:1 (F3), it was expected to achieve full 
conversion of MPGA. This expectation was complicated to assess due to some difficulties occurring in 
the precipitation. None of the tested solvents were able to precipitate the product.  The precipitation 
was firstly attempted in ethanol since all the above explained side-chain modifications of dextran 
were precipitated in ethanol. Even ice-cold ethanol was not able to precipitate the product. Both 
heptane and toluene and different mixtures of all 3 solvents were attempted for the precipitation, 
but the product was dissolved in all of them. The next attempt of isolating the product would have 
been to freeze dry the product after dialyzes against water, but this was not done due to limitation of 
time. However, it was decided to analyze the product by 1H-NMR, 13C-NMR, HPLC and FT-IR in order 
to verify the reaction see appendix 9.1.8. The following Figure 4.16 compares 1H-NMR for the

5. Conclusion 
The main objective of this project was the preparation of three different side-chain modifications of 
dextran bearing a terminal C≡C bond. The first modification was performed by Williamson’s ether 
synthesis with BB and PB as linkers. Several reaction conditions were attempted with both linkers, 
but due to the more hydrophobic character of an extra carbon in BB, no reaction occurred. In 
contrast, the synthesis was successful with PB. Several reaction conditions were attempted, and by 
running the reaction for 18 h in 35 °C with a molar ratio of 1:3 of Dextran 10: PB, the highest DS was 
obtained (DS = 0.44). Furthermore, it was observed that the DS decreased markedly for experiments 
performed with NaBH4. 
The next modification was a nucleophilic ring-opening reaction with GPE, which worked from the first 
experiment. Thus, a lot of different reaction conditions were attempted. The reaction was very 
sensitive to the NaOH concentration, which should neither be too high or too low, but the reaction 
time and temperature also influenced the DS of the Dextran-GPE products. However, the highest DS 
was obtained by running the reaction for 18 h in 35 °C with a molar ratio of 1:3 of Dextran 10: GPE 
(DS = 1.25).  
The last modification was a side-chain modification of dextran with HA via. CDI coupling, which was 
the only ester derivate compared to the 2 ether-derivates. All experiments with this CDI coupling led 
to formation of lumps. The most possible reason to formation of lumps is cross-linking of Dextran 10 
with unreacted CDI which can lead to insoluble products and formation of lumps. However, it was 
observed that the remaining product, separated from the lump, resembled the product. 
The three different side-chain modifications are compared in three different parameters: DS, easy 
handling, and the sustainability of the materials. Both PB and GPE were successful linkers in order to 
prepare a dextran bearing a terminal C≡C bond. Both methods were performed in the same reaction 
conditions with water as solvent and purified in ethanol. Nevertheless, the highest obtained DS were 
obtained with GPE caused by the more hydrophilic character compared to PB leading to a more 
homogenous system. The modification with HA by CDI-coupling were both less sustainable and 
harder to handle than the two other side-chain modifications.  
Secondly, the prepared side-chain modifications were investigated by reacting the prepared dextran-
modifications bearing a terminal C≡C bond with two different azides MPGA and Rhodamine B in a 
CuAAC reaction. From the analyzes it was concluded that the reaction had occurred, but due to more 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
71 
 
hydrophilic character obtained from PEG, the product was not able to be precipitated in the 
attempted solvents and thereby residues from the starting materials were not removed.  
Thus, two side-chain modification of particles were prepared from GPE and PB reacted with DDMA. 
Those DDMA-modification were reacted successfully with the same two azides, which could easily be 
purified.

6. Further work 
This master project has investigated different ways to chemically modify dextran and crosslinked 
dextran with the purpose of introducing a terminal alkyne. The successful modifications were further 
used in click reactions with two different azides. The first modification step was achieved with both 
GPE, PB and HA.  
The epoxy-linker showed great results from the start in both NMR and FT-IR, which resulted in a lot 
more investigation and optimization of this synthetic pathway. However, a lot more investigation 
would have been interesting concerning the control of DS and optimal conditions for the reaction. 
One interesting thing could be to see how big conversion rate was possible by increasing the amount 
of GPE.  Furthermore, it would have been interesting to try another epoxy-linker to compare the 
reactivity.  
The Sn2 reaction with 4-bromo-1-butyne never showed any sign of conversion and PB had a lower 
conversion compared to GPE. By changing the solvent from water to DMSO or another less polar 
solvent, a higher conversion rate could maybe have been achieved.  
The ester-derivate was synthesized in DMF, which is much less sustainable compared to water 
applied in the 2 ether-modifications. The practical work with the synthesis was also complicated 
compared to the two ether-modifications. However, a solvent-free iodine catalyzed acetylation 
would have been interesting to investigate.  
In the next step, the click reactions with the two azides were investigated. Only few experiments 
were synthesized since the product was not precipitated. The next attempt of isolating the product 
would have been to freeze dry the product after dialyzes against water, and then investigate the 
reaction conditions and new azides with other relevant properties.

Glycidyl propargyl ether (GPE, >90%), 4-bromo-1-butyne (BB, 97%), propargyl bromide (PB, 80% in 
toluene), 5-hexynoic acid (97%), 1,1’-Carbonyldiimidazole (CDI, >97%), methoxypolyethylene glycol

L-ascorbate (>99%), and the following solvents: ethanol, toluene, dimethylformamide (DMF) were 
purchased from commercial sources. None of the chemicals were further purified before use. 
Dextran 10 (10.000 Da), degradable dextran microspheres A (DDMA) and purified water (PW), were 
produced at pK Chemicals A/S. 
1H and 13C-NMR were recorded on Magritek Spinsolve 80ULTRA multi X (80 MHz) using purified water 
and DMSO as solvents. The residual solvents peak was used as reference. Infrared analyses were 
recorded on Perkin Elmer Spectrum Two, FT-IR spectrometer with lithium tantalate (LiTaO3) as MIR 
detector with SNR of 9.300:1 and spectral range of 8.300-350cm-1.  
The molecular weight of some experiments was determined using a size exclusion HPLC column. The 
samples were analyzed on a Water UltrahydrogelTM linear column with a refractive index detector 
from Shodex RI-501. The eluent used in the column was (0.1 M NaNO3, 400 ppm NaN3) and the 
injection were performed with a flowrate at 0.6 mL min-1 and a temperature at 27.1°C.  
Some products were furthermore investigated with Dino-Lite Edge AM4115T-GRFBY digital 
microscope. This microscope views objects involving fluorescent objects using 2 different sets of 
LED’s, 4 Blue LEDs at an excitation of 480nm and 4 Yellow LEDs at an excitation of 575nm. 
All experiments are conducted at 1000 RPM unless else is stated. Furthermore, the dextran used is 
the same for all experiments and has a molecular weight of approximately 10.000 Dalton. In the first 
modification step, the molar ratio is between the repeating units in Dextran 10 and the linkers. A 
molar ratio given as 1:1 will optimally give one linker per monosaccharide corresponding to a DS=1.
