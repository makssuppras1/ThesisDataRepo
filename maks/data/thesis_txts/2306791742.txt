Abstract 
Flooding is the most frequent natural disaster in Europe, and it poses severe risks to the public health 
and the economy of the communities living in areas where it strikes. Floods are caused by extreme 
rain events which, according to many authors, are going to increase in frequency and strength in the 
future. In the city of Copenhagen, the sewer system is in charge of conducting the waste water from 
the city to the wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) and, in case of cloudburst, to drain the water from 
the surface and send the possible excess water to Combined Sewer Overflow (CSO) structures. These 
2 objectives, though, works one against each other, since the first aims to reduce the CSOs, while the 
second aims to remove the water from the city and therefore increase CSOs. For these reasons the 
utility company HOFOR, has developed a real-time control (RTC) set-up, called “cloudburst button” 
which is supposed to reduce the risk of flooding in the city, by opening the gates towards the receiving 
water bodies.  
The goal of this project was to optimize the sewer system of the city of Copenhagen in order to reduce 
the risk of flooding by improving the performance of the cloudburst button. Moreover, it was tried to 
minimize CSOs to the harbor for smaller rain events. 
The model of the sewer system of Copenhagen was studied in order to identify the issues connected 
with the present condition. The cloudburst button was found not to be able to reduce significantly the 
flooded area, compared to a scenario with the cloudburst button off, and the CSOs were found to be 
relatively high for small rain events. The model was systematically modified in order to simulate 
possible improvements to the system. The new model was then examined and new modifications were 
made in a loop that was repeated 4 times. First, the baseline scenario was modified by raising the crest 
level of strategic weirs, in order to reduce the water flowing throughout them. At the same time, orifices 
with gate were placed next to the weirs where orifices were not already present, in order to be able to 
control CSO structures which were previously uncontrollable. These orifices with gate were controlled 
by the cloudburst button. After, the RTC was modified so that the cloudburst button would overrule 
all the other control actions. Moreover, the controlled opening of 2 gates was delayed in order to reduce 
CSOs. Subsequently, 2 bottlenecks, creating an obstacle for the flow towards the CSO structures, were 
bypassed with 2 orifices with gate that would open with the cloudburst button. Finally, 4 pipes were 
modified, increasing their diameter, after finding out they were causing a reduced flow produced by 
the difference of the pipes themselves and the downstream branch. 
It was found that the modifications to the model reduced the CSOs volume by 10 times, compared to 
the baseline scenario, for small rain events (i.e.  Chicago Design Storm (CDS) rain with 1 year return 
period with a duration of 90 minutes). At the same time, the attempts to enhance the flow through the 
sewer to reach the outlets successfully reduced the flooded area by almost half for a large rain event 
(i.e. CDS rain with 20 years return period with a length of 90 minutes), from 41,518 m2 to 23,049 m2, 
and to reduce its volume from 6,146 m3 to 3,093 m

Outline of the report 
This thesis is subdivided into 6 main chapters. 
The first chapter gives an introduction to the problems faced in the project, i.e. flooding 
and combined sewer overflows (CSOs). The guidelines framing the project are also 
presented, along with a description of the cloudburst button. 
In Chapter 2 are described the general information about RTC and the programs used.  
Chapter 3 consists of 2 subchapters: the first shortly presents where the project takes

The methodology followed in the project is presented in chapter 4. 
Chapter 5 describes the results obtained in this project, which are divided into 6 
subchapters.  
The conclusions are finally presented in chapter 6 
1.2. 
Cloudburst and flooding 
Floods are defined by the European Environmental Agency (EEA) (2001), as situations 
of extreme runoff during which human lives, property, and infrastructures are 
threatened.  
In Europe, flooding is the natural disaster that occurs more frequently than any other 
and is also the one that causes most economic damage (EEA, 2001). The risk of 
flooding is severe also in Denmark, since in Copenhagen, during summer 2010 and 
2011, the city suffered from a serious flood two years in a row (Copenhagen 
Municipality 2011). Damages caused by the 2011 downpour in Copenhagen was 
estimated to cost 5 to 6 billion DKK (Copenhagen Municipality 2012). Other than 
merely economic damage, floods are also dangerous for public health. People that gets 
in contact with flooding water have 5 times higher chances to get diarrhea, and there is 
a significant increasing trend of gastroenteritis in people living in flooded houses. 
Floods increase also the chances to catch respiratory diseases by 6 times (Wojcik et al., 
2013).  
The Danish Metrological Institute (DMI) defines a cloudburst as a rain event of 15mm 
of rain depth or more in 30 minutes or less (Hansen, 2015), while the downpour which 
caused the 2001’s flood was close to 100 mm in 1 hour.  Normally the risk of flooding 
is a combination of natural and artificial factors (e.g. vegetation cover, climate change, 
faster runoff through deforestation, urbanization, river channelization) or by increasing 
the chances of exposure of humans by building cities on flood plains (EEA, 2001).   
In Copenhagen, the majority of the sewer system consists of a combined type, which 
deals both wastewater and stormwater in the same pipes (Copenhagen Municipality 
2012) and most of the actual sewer system was established between 1860 and 1910 
==PAGE:12==

(Sørensen et al. 2006). This extremely complex sewer system is the only drainage 
system the city of Copenhagen has got, and it has not the capacity to handle extreme 
rain events, nor the city is capable of handling large quantities of surface water 
(Copenhagen Municipality 2012). This causes overflows to the harbor for small-
medium rain events, and floods and damage on the surface for extreme downpours, as 
the system cannot cope with all the amount of water, nor the city is able to handle the 
surface water (Copenhagen Municipality 2012). 
1.3.

of Copenhagen intends to improve the environmental quality of its surface water bodies 
in order to allow bathing conditions by enhancing the treatment and handling of

maximum of 2-3 times every bathing season, which consists of the months of June, July 
and August. These are the months that are expected to experience more severe 
cloudbursts in the future (Copenhagen Municipality, 2011). In Table 1 a description of

the inner lakes of Copenhagen and the channels of the harbor (Copenhagen

1.4. 
Climate change 
Future weather predictions suggest that there will be an increase in extreme rainfall 
events, and there is already evidence which proves this, which poses the need for an 
improvement of the current design of the urban drainage system (Madsen et al., 2009). 
At a global level, in the last century the sea has already risen by about 17cm, and 
according to some projections it is going to rise between 18 and 59cm from the level at 
1990 before 2090, according to the 2007 Fourth Assessment Report of the 
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) (Hallegatte et al., 2011).  
The average temperature in Europe has risen by 1.3 °C compared to the pre-industrial 
level. There has been an increase in heat waves and forest fires in central Europe, while 
the northern countries have experienced an intensification of heavy rains, such as 
cloudbursts and flooding. These extreme natural events are likely to increase the risk 
for the public health and can cause significant economic losses (E.C., 2013).  
In Denmark, the recent extreme precipitations, connected with the 2010 and 2011 
floods, have raised the population awareness about the risk that climate change brings 
(Olsen et al., 2015).  
Although there are some uncertainties in how climate change is going to impact urban 
drainage systems (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al.,2013), there is a need to take action in order 
to adapt the sewers to such extreme events, making the cities at risk more climate-
resilient and flexible, as the European Union (EU) is trying to do with the EU 
Adaptation Strategy (E.C., 2013; Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al.,2013). 
1.5. 
Cloudburst button 
When an extreme weather event as a cloudburst takes place, there is a high risk of 
flooding connected with the large amount of water generated, if the sewer system is not 
able to handle the storm water, and the surface is not structured to conduct the water 
from the city safely. In a city like Copenhagen, this can cause enormous damage to lives 
and property (EEA, 2001).  This factor drives the need for an emergency plan in case 
of a cloudburst event. 
The sewer system of Copenhagen is already regulated by a Real Time Control (RTC) 
system consisting of a combination of local regulatory control and rule-based control 
which act at a regional or global level. The main goal of RTC is to minimize CSOs 
(Mollerup et al., 2016). In case of exceptional rainfall, though, the priority of the sewer 
==PAGE:14==
Introduction 
4 
 
system becomes to remove as much storm water as possible from the areas potentially 
at risk of flooding. For example, Clavagueram de Barcelona, the wastewater utility of 
the city of Barcelona, has found that the two primary objectives of their work, avoiding 
floods and minimizing CSOs, works one against each other. Avoiding floods has the 
highest priority, and therefore the utility is considering to implement in their RTC a 
layer for managing the objectives, which will allow the switching between the two 
priority, based on the weather forecast (Mollerup, 2015).  For the same reasons, and 
since it is believed that the level of complexity of the control is already too high 
(Mollerup et al., 2016), there is a need of a control capable of overruling the existing 
RTC leading the water from the sewer to the receiving water bodies.  
The cloudburst button is the name given to the set-up of the sewer system used to cope 
with extreme rain events. It consists of a sort of RTC, that can be operated manually or 
automatically. When “pressing the button”, the system adapts in order to handle the 
incoming cloudburst water, and in particular it opens the gates towards the harbor, 
emptying the pipes in order to clear the pipe’s volume and to be able to lead the water 
from the city out as soon as possible (HOFOR, 2013). In case of cloudburst, either 
manually, knowing the weather forecast, or automatically, following an alarm setting, 
these gates would open, reducing the risk of flooding in the city. 
The functioning and efficiency of this set-up are being investigated in this project, and, 
in case the system was found not to be able to cope with the rain events with the return 
period required by the guidelines, it will be attempted to improve the system. 
1.6. 
Aims of the project 
In this thesis, it was tried to accomplish 2 main goals:  
 Improving the efficiency of the cloudburst button in order to reduce the risk of 
flooding for the city of Copenhagen. 
 Minimizing the CSOs to the receiving water bodies.

2. Theory background 
The present project was carried out using the DHI program MIKE FLOOD, and one of 
the main problem considered in this thesis consisted in the attempt to optimize the RTC 
of the sewer system of Copenhagen. For these reasons, a short description of RTC and 
the theory background of the program are presented in the following chapters.  
2.1. 
RTC 
A sewer system is used optimally when, before a CSO takes place, the whole retention 
capacity is used. This cannot be guaranteed by some fixed gates and weirs (Marinaki 
and Papageorgiou 2005). An urban wastewater system is real-time controlled if its 
variables are monitored and, at the same time, used to operate actuators during the flow 
process (Schütze et. al., 2004). RTC can be a cost-efficient tool to solve the problem of 
handling large volumes of wastewater and storm water without building expensive 
structures such as basins and/or expanding existing structures. Many combined sewer 
systems could improve their performance, in relation to CSOs, using RTC. Some sewer 
systems are not designed ideally since they are often built in existing cities and the 
structures are often placed where space was available, instead of where it would have 
been ideal (Mollerup, 2015).   
Basically, a RTC is made of control loops where hardware components including 
sensors (e.g. rain gauges, water level gauges)  monitor the process evolution, actuators 
(e.g. pumps, gates) influence the process, controllers adjust actuators to achieve 
minimum deviations of the controlled process variable from its desired value (set-
point), and data transmission systems transmit data between the different devices. In a 
feedback loop control, an actuator is activated if there is a measured deviation from a 
set point. In a feedforward loop, the controller is activated in relation to the prediction 
of the deviation from a set point, in order to avoid the deviation. A 
feedback/feedforward loop is a combination of the 2 (Schütze et. al., 2004).  
The typical RTC for sewer system today is decomposed horizontally, based on spatial 
decomposition. This means that the control problem is managed locally at the sub-
catchments. This way of controlling the sewer can be inefficient if the sub-catchments 
are interacting, and adding logic switches and cascade feedback loops can create 
complexity and confusion. Another method for decomposing the control problem is 
vertically, as shown in Figure 1, with a time-dependent control hierarchy, as proposed 
in Mollerup, (2015).  
==PAGE:16==
Theory background 
6 
 
 
Figure 1: Time-dependent control hierarchy for sewer systems (Mollerup 2015). 
The control is divided into 4 layers: management of objectives, optimization, 
coordinating control and regulatory control. 
 The management of objectives layer is at the top of the hierarchy and defines 
the overall aim for the operations of the sewer system. It can take into account 
seasonal changes as bathing season or diurnal changes in energy cost to update 
the objective functions.  
 The task of the optimization layer is to determine the optimum operation in the 
control layers. Since the status in the sewer system can change rapidly during a 
rain event, there is a need of a frequent update of the set-point. The optimization 
could, therefore, provide a trajectory of set-points.  
 If control loops are interacting, or there are some constraints in the controlled 
variables, a coordinating layer is needed. Its role is to decouple interacting 
loops and manage constraints. 
 The last layer consists of a regulatory control layer. Its function is to make 
sure that the set-points or the trajectories are followed, and the disturbances (any 
input that influences the controlled variable) are rejected (Mollerup 2015). 
In the present project, the cloudburst button can be placed in the management of 
objectives layer. In fact, pushing the button changes the present objective of avoiding 
CSOs to the avoidance of flooding.

2.3.1. Mouse surface runoff models 
 There are different runoff computation models available in MOUSE:  
 Model A- Time/Area Method, 
 Model B- Non-Linear Reservoir (kinematic wave) Method, 
 Model C- Linear Reservoir Method, in the Dutch and in the French variants, 
 UHM- Unit Hydrograph Model 
For surface runoff computations in MOUSE, any of the methods above could be used, 
as long as the necessary data are available. Still, only one of the methods can be used 
for each simulation. (DHIa, 2014).  
The model of the Copenhagen drainage system uses the Time/Area method. The 
amount of runoff is based on three parameters: the initial loss, the size of the 
contributing area and by a continuous hydrological loss. The initial loss represents the 
minimum amount of rainfall that causes a runoff. The impervious areas in the catchment 
are those which do not allow water to infiltrate into the ground. The hydrogeological 
loss represents the evapotranspiration and the imperfect imperviousness.  The shape of 
the runoff hydrograph is controlled by the concentration time and by the time-area (T-
A) curve, which represent the reaction speed of the catchment to a rainfall event, and 
the shape of the catchment. 
The computation of the runoff is done by dividing the catchment area in a number of 
cells, in a form of concentric circles with a center point at the point of outflow, 
depending on the simulating time step, Δt, as in ( 1 ):

2.3.3. MOUSE control module 
The MOUSE control module is capable of advanced Real-Time Control (RTC) 
simulations for urban drainage systems. It allows the setup of a series of controllable 
devices and operational logic for interdependent regulators based on real-time 
information about the system state. The controllable devices can be pumps, weirs, 
orifices with gate or weir and valves. They operate under a set of simple logic rules and 
control functions, which allows a schematized definition of any size and form of 
decision tree by the use of the operators AND, OR, NOR and OR in any combination. 
A simple RTC is schematized in Figure 5. RTC is useful in cases where a flexible 
redistribution of water in space and time improves the situation of the system. 
Therefore, the role of RTC is to optimize the use of the system, maximizing the benefits 
in terms of performance and minimizing the need of supplementary works.

 Sensors are physical devices located in specific places in the system, which provide

Based on the information provided by the sensors, some logical conditions can frame 
the boundaries of certain operation situation. This consists of a series of independent 
logical tests on the variables information sent by the sensors, where the actual value is 
tested against a certain set-point. When all the conditions are fulfilled, the logical 
condition is evaluated as “TRUE”, while if only one is not valid, the condition is 
“FALSE”. In MIKE URBAN, a hierarchy is followed by the actuators to determine 
their actions, i.e. the first condition of the series to be true has the highest priority. 
It is also possible, in MIKE URBAN, to use an external water level in a DFS0 format 
to simulate an active RTC from the user, i.e. applying the external water level to specific 
nodes, where the water level can be set to be higher/lower a certain threshold and 
therefore allowing the user to decide when and how to influence the RTC. 
Based on the status of the condition, a control action relates the input from the sensors 
and the set-point or the setting for the controlled device. In case a flow variable is the 
parameter to control, the control decisions are taken after considering the set-point 
value of the parameter and its actual value. The control algorithm is based on the 
numerical solution of the “continuous control problem” equation and is commonly 
regarded as PID (Proportional-Integral-Differential) control. The selection of the 
variable is limited to a few flow variables (e.g. discharge, water level) and since the 
variable are harder to control the further they are from the controller, due to time lags, 
diffusion and interferences, it is best if it is found in nearby the controller (DHIc, 2014). 
2.4. 
MIKE 21 
The hydrodynamic model in the MIKE 21 Flow Model is based on the numerical 
solution of the two-dimensional shallow water equations: the depth-integrated averaged 
Navier-Stokes equations. It simulates unsteady two-dimensional flows in one layer 
(vertically homogeneous) fluids and has been applied in a large number of studies (DHI, 
==PAGE:22==
Theory background 
12 
 
2015). The special discretization of the equations is done using a cell-centered finite 
volume method. The space of the model is discretized by dividing it into a grid of non-
overlapping elements or cells The special discretization can be done by using a 
rectangular grid or a flexible mesh. To be able to handle a discontinuous solution, 
MIKE 21 uses an approximation of the Riemann solver for the computation of the 
convective fluxes. The time integration of the shallow water equations is performed 
with an explicit scheme.

3. Case study area 
3.1. 
The city of Copenhagen and the Lynetten catchment 
Copenhagen is the capital city of Denmark and house 569,557 inhabitants, and in the 
Greater Copenhagen Region the first quarter of 2016 numbered a population of 
1,280,370 individuals (Statistics Denmark, 2016). Copenhagen is situated on the 
eastern coast of Zealand and for a small part on the island of Amager.  
The Lynetten wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) covers the central area of the city of 
Copenhagen and has an area of about 7,600 hectares. Most of the sewer was established 
between 1860 and 1910. It wasn’t until 1980 that the Lynetten WWTP was put into 
operation (Sørensen et al., 2006), and since then a series of reservoirs and pipe 
reservoirs were commissioned to reduce the pollution of the harbor channels by 
minimizing CSOs and allowing bathing quality. The total storage volume is 
approximately of 153,000 m3 (Löwe et al., 2016). In the 1990s the utility company 
managing the sewer system decided also to implement an RTC in the western part of 
the system, in order to optimize the use of the large pipes in that area. Still today, many 
pumps and gates only have local regulatory control, but more and more are being 
replaced or complemented with rule-based controls that can coordinate a larger number 
of operators and optimize the functioning a larger region of the sewer system (Mollerup 
et al., 2016).

The first step, in order to enhance the sewer system, is to identify the problem. As 
explained in the previous chapters, the problem dealt with in this project was the need 
to optimize the sewer system by reducing CSOs and to enhance the cloudburst button. 
The setup of the model consists in finding the right conditions to run accurate 
simulations of the problem.  In this case, the model obtained from Krüger A/S was 
provided without external water levels for the control of the cloudburst button (chapter 
5.1) and the sea water level, which had to be created. The time step for the simulation 
was modified in order to obtain stable simulations, and the sensitivity of some overflow 
parameters was analyzed in connection to the flooding behavior. 
The next step consists in running 2 simulations, one with the cloudburst button on and 
one with the cloudburst button off, with a large rain event (20 years return period) and 
a simulation with a small rain event (e.g. 1 year return period) with the cloudburst 
button off (the cloudburst button must stay off if there is no cloudburst). In chapter 5.1 
this step was followed in order to study the baseline scenario. The RTC functioning is 
investigated in these simulations in order to understand its relation to different 
conditions of the system. A water balance is also performed in order to understand the 
flow conditions of the sewer system in the different scenarios. The flow direction is 
useful to understand the direction of the flow at different moments. The outlet 
discharge is a fundamental parameter to consider in order to establish the behavior of 
the modeled system in case of small rain events, where the goal is to minimize it, and 
for big rain events, where instead is needs to be as large as possible. The study of the 
floods depth and area is, nevertheless, crucial to assess the status of the model and 
setting the goal of possible change in the system. Therefore, its maximum volume and 
area were investigated (for a depth above 10 cm). 
Changes are implemented after having analyzed all the results and having obtained an 
understanding of the model. These modifications are done in strategic positions, 
considering the most realistic and cost-effective solutions for the problem considered. 
Among others the most important are raising the weirs crest level, so to reduce the 
CSOs for small rain period. This solution could though generate more flooding for 
bigger rain events, which acts against what Schütze et. Al. (2004) refer as safety rule, 
stating that the worst-case scenario would be to fall back to the starting system status. 
Therefore, orifices with controllable gates need to be implemented next to the weirs, if 
orifices are not already there, where they can be open when the RTC goal becomes 
avoiding floods. The RTC system could also be modified, especially after inserting 
new structures. The last thing would be to removing bottlenecks in the sewer system, 
which could be done by enlarging pipes or connecting parts of the system in areas where 
the water cannot escape efficiently.  
This loop is repeated until satisfied with the results, and conclusions can be made.

Looking at the water balance of the baseline scenario (chapter 5.1.1) the irregular 
discharge pattern of the pipe 9 was investigated.  
In MIKE VIEW the whole portion of the system from the outlet to pipe 9 was studied, 
and in Figure 33 a portion of the studied area is presented. 
==PAGE:51==
Results and discussion

It can be seen that the pipe has an abrupt change in the slope, which creates a sort of 
bulge on the general pipe trajectory, i.e. the slope changes from about 1 ‰ to about 43 
‰. The same was observed on the sewer section downstream of pipe 10. Under small 
rain events, the bulge structure has the positive effect of holding the water upstream

Pipe 10 and pipe 9 are part of the two main branches of the sewer sending the water to 
the CSO structure 16. The 2 bulges described previously were connected with two 
orifices with a gate, called Bypass 1 for the right branch and Bypass 2 for the left one. 
They both had a width and a height of 3 meters and were made in a way that would 
connect the manhole right before and the one right after the convexity. The goal of these 
two orifices was to allow the flow of the water before it reached the bottom of the 
highest point of the bulge. The water would therefore not be accumulating upstream, 
and the coming storm water would have a larger volume available in the pipes and 
fewer changes in the flow, and therefore, less energy losses. The gates were controlled 
with the cloudburst button so that they would open only in case it was necessary.

5.6. 
Summary of the results 
In the first part of this chapter a description of the baseline scenario was presented, 
where it was shown that the current situation did not efficiently drain the storm water 
towards the outlets for a large rain event (i.e. a CDS rain with a return period of 20 
years). At the same time, for small rain events (i.e. 1 year return period), the model 
showed the presence of CSOs into the harbor.  
In Mod_1, the weirs crest level was risen, in order to reduce the CSOs volume. At the 
same time, an orifice was added where missing, in order to be able to open the gates 
and discharge the storm water to the harbor in case of a cloudburst. The orifices were 
controlled by the cloudburst button. The modifications to the system showed to be 
effective since the total discharge went from 5556 m3 to 936 m3. At the same time, for 
a large rain event, the flooded area and volume increase were not significant. 
In Mod_2, it was tried to enhance the model by overruling the RTC of some orifices 
which showed an inefficient control.  The goal was to allow the water to pass through 
the gates as soon as the cloudburst button was on and to reduce the CSOs by delaying 
the opening of some orifices. The CSOs volume was almost halved, but the flooding 
area and volume were not reduced significantly. 
To reduce the flooding, in Mod_3, it was tried to remove 2 bottlenecks, which were 
preventing the water from flowing freely towards the outlet. The bottlenecks were 
bypassed with 2 orifices provided with gates, which would open with the cloudburst 
button. As a result, the discharge from the outlet involved increased by almost 3,000 
m3, and the flooded surface with a depth higher than 10 cm became 29,642 m2, while 
in the baseline scenario it was 41,518 m2 leaving undisturbed the discharge for smaller 
rain events when the cloudburst button was closed. 
With Mod_4, it was tried to increase the capacity of the pipe below the 2 bypass before 
described, by increasing the diameter of 4 pipes so that they had the same diameter as 
the pipes downstream since they were causing a severe head loss. As a result, the 
discharge from the outlet involved increased by more of 3,000 m3compared to Mod_3, 
and the surface flooded was reduced to 23,049 m2.

6. Final remarks 
6.1. 
Conclusions 
The purpose of this study was to investigate the possibility of an optimization of the 
RTC system called “cloudburst button”, in order to reduce the flooding in the city of 
Copenhagen. Moreover, it was tried to minimize the CSOs for small rain events, 
without increasing the flood risk for larger return period rain events.  
In order to accomplish these goals, the baseline scenario was investigated, in particular 
by looking at the water balance of a focus area, which was selected for the presence of 
large flooded areas and for the possibility to cut it easily from the rest of the sewer 
system, the flow direction in its pipes, the CSO structures discharge and the flooding 
area and depth. The knowledge obtained by this analysis allowed to modify the system 
systematically. For every modification, this analysis was repeated, creating a loop that 
was followed until satisfied with the results.   
It was found that increasing the crest level of some strategic weirs reduced the CSO 
caused by small rain events (i.e. CDS rain with a return period of 1 year) to the harbor 
from 5,556 m3 to 936 m3. At the same time, orifices with gate were coupled to weirs 
which had no possibility of RTC, which allowed the system not to worsen the risk of 
flooding.  On the other hand, changing the RTC so that when the cloudburst button was 
in on-mode a group of gates would open, did not reduce the risk of flooding as expected. 
Nevertheless, the opening conditions for some gates was changed, in order to delay 
their CSO in the case of small rain events, and it allowed to half the CSOs volume. 
After, 2 bottlenecks in the pipe system, consisting in a bulge through which water was 
not able to flow freely, were bypassed with 2 gates, which were set to open when the 
cloudburst button was in on-mode. This allowed the sewer system to remove the storm 
water more efficiently, reducing the maximum flooded area for a large rain event (i.e. 
CDS rain with a return period of 20 years) from 41,518 m2 to 29,642 m2, and its volume 
from 6,146 m3 to 3,923 m3. Finally, it was found that increasing the diameter of 4 pipes 
in beside a head drop, caused by the difference of the above-mentioned pipes and the 
downstream branch, reduced the flooded area to 23,049 m2.  
Since only modifications that are considered economically realistic were carried out, 
and seen the results obtained, the goals of the project can be considered achieved. 
6.2. 
Future investigations 
Some recommendations are presented as suggestions in case further developments are 
to be made on this subject: 
 Perform an economic feasibility study of the modifications proposed in this 
paper; 
 Investigate a way to predict when the cloudburst button needs to be pushed, in 
order to avoid unnecessary opening of the cloudburst button. This could be 
based on rain forecast data combined with rain gauges measurements. Knowing 
the intensity of the rain, combined with for example the knowledge of the
