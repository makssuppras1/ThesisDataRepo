Abstract
In a world with an increasing penetration of intermittent electricity genera-
tion and price levels, the need for ﬂexible long-storage technologies is rising.
The concept of Power to Gas (PtG) is one of the new technologies that could
potentially support the future energy system in running entirely on green
energy.
A feasibility study has been carried out to investigate the opportunities
of PtG in 2030. With reference to other studies, a mathematical optimisation
model has been developed to determine the most favourable business case
of PtG from a private economic point of view. The concept of Power to Gas
to Power (PtGtP) has been investigated as well as the possibility of green
hydrogen production to different customers. Furthermore, the possibility of
disconnecting the PtG facility from the electricity grid and fuel it with private
wind power is addressed.
The results show that PtGtP with the use of underground storage as well
as steel tanks requires strong regulation, thus this concept is determined
infeasible. On the contrary, the high value of green hydrogen in the mobility
and industry sector as well as gas sector (only at low production costs)
determines the concept of PtG feasible under speciﬁc circumstances. The
capacity size of the facility and yearly production are mainly determined by
the average electricity input price and the volatility of electricity prices. The
most beneﬁcial system solution is when the facility is disconnected from the
electricity grid and fuelled with private power. However, the feasibility of
this system must be ﬁnally proven by further studies.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Climate change is probably humanity’s greatest challenge. Within the last
hundred years, we have signiﬁcantly improved our standard of living, but
it has had a high cost regarding the carbon footprint on Earth and the
greenhouse gases derived from energy production amongst other things.
However, what is the use of our increased standard of living if our lifestyle
is not in harmony with the planet that we inhabit. We must do something to
ﬁght climate change!
Many green alternatives have already displaced conventional power
plants all over the world.
This development has been highly successful
in making electricity generation greener, by which the number of green
initiatives is expected to increase within the next many years. On the other
hand, the increasing penetration of intermittent generation has resulted in
volatile electricity markets and low spot prices which makes it difﬁcult for
conventional electricity generators to stay proﬁtable.
One solution is long storage technologies which can store electricity at
times of ample generation (low prices) and discharge when production is
scarce (high prices). The most common energy storage technology is lithium-
ion, but this technology is limited in its ability to store large quantities of
electricity for longer periods. On the other hand, Power to Gas (PtG) is a new
technology which could potentially support the future energy system with
ﬂexibility in production and storage of green electrolysed hydrogen. PtG
offers the opportunity to integrate large quantities of renewable electricity
by taking advantage of ﬂuctuations in generation and price levels.
This
is achieved by converting and storing the electricity as green hydrogen
which can be used in different energy sectors such as the electricity, heating,
gas, industry and mobility sector.
However, the technology has yet to
be commercialised, and feasible business scenarios must be investigated to
identify the attractiveness of PtG in Denmark.

The aim of this project is to identify the most feasible business case for
a PtG facility in Denmark at different locations and with different market
designs.
The objective is to assess the possibilities for the technology in
a Danish context rather than to demonstrate the technology itself.
The
objective is also to identify the most proﬁtable operation scenario as well as
the main barriers and drivers for future commerciality.
Numerous of researchers have already investigated PtG from many
different points of view. Grond et al. [1] investigates the state of PtG in
terms of technology readiness with the key ﬁnding being that the current
readiness level depends on the speciﬁc process’ layout, but based on many
successful demonstrations projects, technology is evolving and with it the
commercialisation of the technology. The Flex4RES project [2] investigates
the technical coupling between energy markets with the key ﬁnding being
that Denmark is a country that contains the system conditions required
to make PtG suitable because of well-developed energy networks, sizeable
storage and a high amount of wind. ECN and DNV-GL [3] explores the role
of PtG (including drivers and bottlenecks) in the future Dutch energy system
with the key ﬁnding that PtG is cost-ineffective from a short- and medium-
term public perspective and that a positive private business case for speciﬁc
applications of PtG may still prove feasible.
Common to most studies is that they examine the technical feasibility of
PtG, the current framework or the future role of PtG in different countries.
This project does not inquire deeply into the technology itself but instead
focuses on the feasibility of investment from a private economic point of view
as well as the operation scheduling under different regulations. Furthermore,
this project determines the potential for private wind farms operators by
connecting a PtG facility directly to a wind farm.
The project covers an investigation of expected conditions in 2030 at two
locations – Lille Thorup in Northern Jutland and Esbjerg in Western Jutland
– with three different system simulations:
• Lille Thorup - Location 1: Lille Thorup is close to one of the two salt
caverns in Denmark with cheap and a large storage capacity, by which
it becomes interesting to investigate the potential of interseasonal
storage. Lille Thorup is a pure Power to Gas to Power (PtGtP) concept
where the hydrogen is converted back into electricity (and heat) to meet
an excess electricity demand.
• Esbjerg – Location 2A: Special about the location in Esbjerg are the
many hydrogen customers, by which the ﬂexibility of PtG becomes
interesting. The concept in Esbjerg investigates the hydrogen offtake
in the electricity, heating, gas, industry and mobility sector.

• Esbjerg – Location 2B: Another advantage of Esbjerg is the high
amount of green wind power.
This opportunity is investigated at
Location 2B where the facility at Location 2A is disconnected from the
electricity grid to fuel the facility with green and cheap electricity and
to avoid expensive taxes and grid tariffs.
Common to every system simulation is that they can all provide a PtG
system integration, although the characteristics in their energy systems are
otherwise different. The key activity in this study has been the development

investment planning of the three system simulations based on real data.
Even though many assumptions are made to simplify the project, the overall
feasibility of PtG in a Danish context can still be determined.
An introduction to PtG and the maturity are presented in Chapter 2

with a few recommendations and suggestions for future work in Chapter
6 (Conclusion).

CHAPTER 2
Key aspects
In this chapter, the ’underlying magic’ of PtG is presented based on current
literature. This includes an evaluation of the maturity, technology, hydrogen
customers and current regulations.
2.1
Potential of PtG
The feasibility of PtG has already been investigated in a number of studies
through simulations, mathematical models, small demonstration projects
and expert interviews. Common to all literature is that energy storage is
needed to balance ﬂuctuations from renewables, and PtG could be the key to
create a fully sustainable energy system.
In Schaaf et al. [4] and Grond et al. [1] investigations of available storage
technologies are presented, and PtG is compared to other technologies such
as batteries, compressed air energy storage, pumped hydro storage and
ﬂywheels. In [4] a comparison of the charge/discharge time and storage
capacity of the different energy storage systems is made. Looking at the case
of storing large quantities of intermittent electricity, the technology requires
an elevated capacity combined with high charge/discharge periods. Since
hydrogen is a chemical secondary energy carrier, PtG is the best possible
storage technology. In [1], it is emphasised that PtG is distinguished from
other storage technologies by the characteristic that hydrogen is produced
as an intermediate product. Hydrogen can subsequently be processed as a
gas or be converted back into electricity (and heat) by fuel cells. This makes
hydrogen a valuable energy carrier for sectors such as gas, electricity, heating,
industry and mobility.
PtG sounds like one of the most promising storage technologies.
However, in Park et al. [5], PtG is evaluated to be economically infeasible.
This is mainly because of the high investment cost (CAPEX), the – sometimes
4
==PAGE:12==

– high prices of input electricity (OPEX) and the low hydrogen selling prices.
As a result, other sources of value (such as the production of by-products
and ancillary services) could be included with an advantage. Furthermore,
PtG has to be assessed in relation to the whole energy system. As presented
by Dansk Energi [6], the integration of electrolysis and hydrogen in the
energy system could increase the total system efﬁciency signiﬁcantly. Some
technologies – such as PtG – are expensive in isolation but induce an overall
system optimisation because of beneﬁcial integration capabilities. PtG also
has a high security of supply because of a non-existing need for bio resources.
On the other hand, the security of electricity supply is challenged because of
the ﬂuctuation of power production from renewables.
2.2
Technology
PtG is still a relatively new technology, and in general, new technologies
induce uncertainties that imply risks for developers, manufactures, vendors,
operators and end users [7].
In the following paragraphs, the key
technological choices are elaborated as well as the maturity of electrolysers
for PtG, hydrogen storage and fuel cells. Figure 2.1 shows the concept of PtG
and all its stages – from the production of hydrogen to the ﬁnal offtake.
Figure 2.1: Graphic overview of the concept of PtG.
Input electricity is used as fuel for hydrogen production. The hydrogen
produced in the electrolyser (up to 100 bar) can either ﬂow directly to the
natural gas grid and industry through pipelines, to smaller storage tanks
distributed to end users by truck or to a larger storage. Before it is stored
in the distribution tanks or storage, the hydrogen is compressed to around
700 bar to improve the energy density by volume. The hydrogen can also
ﬂow from storage to a fuel cell which re-convert the hydrogen into electricity.
==PAGE:13==
CHAPTER 2. KEY ASPECTS
6
Lastly, the excess heat from the electrolyser and fuel cell can be used in the
heating sector.
Input electricity
The source of electricity input has a high impact on the economic and
environmental feasibility of PtG. A high price on input electricity requires
an even higher hydrogen selling price.
Furthermore, an electricity input
based on mainly fossil fuel entails a higher Global Warming Potential (GWP).
The GWP is a measure of the estimated contribution to global warming
when directly emitted into the atmosphere. A high GWP represents a high
impact. In Reiter et al. [8], the ecological performance of PtG is investigated,
and it is concluded that hydrogen production based on solar and wind
powers reduces the GWP by respectively 73% and 95% compared to steam
methane reforming. If the production is based on the electricity mix from the
EU-27 countries, the GWP increases by 147% compared to steam methane
reforming.
Another option is to disconnect the PtG facility from the electricity grid
and source it directly from a renewable energy asset by a private wire. This
ensures that the input electricity is cheap and green and, furthermore, that
the facility avoids paying transmission and distribution charges to a third
party.
Because of the high network tariffs in Denmark, this could save
the facility a notable amount of money. In Dansk Energi [11], the network
tariff (44 DKK/GJ in 2015) is assessed to exceed CAPEX and OPEX for
small facilities. Depending on the future design of tariffs, this may change
signiﬁcantly. However, the disconnection from the grid also decreases the
ﬂexibility to export any excess electricity production and, likewise, to import
grid electricity in cases of downtime of the renewable asset.
Electrolysers for hydrogen production
The core element of PtG plants is the electrolyser which transforms the
input electricity into hydrogen. The principal concept of electrolysis is to
split water (H2O) into hydrogen (H2) and oxygen (O2), using electricity as
energy input. There are three main categories of electrolysers today, each
with its own characteristics and potential: alkaline (AEC), proton exchange
membrane (PEM) and solid oxide electrolysis (SOE). Today, AEC is the most
commercialised technology followed by PEM and then SOE.
Through an expert elicitation study, Schmidt et al.
[12] investigated
the future expectations of capital cost, lifetime and efﬁciency of the three
types of electrolysers.
One requirement for electrolysers in PtG facilities
is that they can operate with intermittent power sources, such as the fast
response of system components, enabling dynamic operation or short cold-

Hydrogen storage
The aim of hydrogen storage is to make it possible to produce hydrogen
when the electricity input is cheap (and the demand is low) and, in reverse,
use the hydrogen storage in periods with high electricity prices. The bigger
the storage, the more ﬂexible the operation of the electrolyser becomes and
the more hours with high prices can be evaded. However, this also requires
an oversized electrolyser to meet the capacity needed to use the hydrogen
storage. In Dansk Energi [11], it is stated that the electrolysers in Denmark
are between 5% and 60% bigger than regular electrolysers, and the optimal
storage capacity is between 5 and 60 full load hours of production. Both
have a major inﬂuence on the investment cost of the PtG facility by which a
trade-off between a ﬂexible production and the capacity of the electrolyser
and storage must be decided.
There are two main categories of storage opportunities: underground salt
caverns (previously used for storage of natural gas) and steel buffer tanks.
Storage of hydrogen in salt caverns is signiﬁcantly cheaper than in buffer
==PAGE:15==
CHAPTER 2. KEY ASPECTS
8
tanks. On the other hand, salt caverns are dependent on the geographical
location of the PtG facility and therefore still under development.
In
Denmark, two natural salt caverns exist, one in Stenlille and one in Lille
Torup [13].
Some literature, such as Bünger et al. [15], also identify the natural gas
grid as a storage capacity because of the collaboration between the gas and
power sectors. However, as mentioned in Skøtt et al. [14], no guidelines
for the amount of hydrogen that the Danish gas grid can contain exist as it
depends on the end users. At the moment, about 2% of hydrogen is injected
into the Danish natural gas grid.
Fuel cell
Stored hydrogen can also be used as an input fuel in a fuel cell to convert
the hydrogen back into electricity (and heat) to meet an excess demand. This
is also known as ’Power to Gas to Power’ (PtGtP) or ’interseasonal storage’
between surplus periods and deﬁcit periods. On the other hand, the extra
process – the conversion from hydrogen to electricity – in the supply chain
causes an additional loss in efﬁciency. In [1], the overall efﬁciency of PtG and
PtGtP are assed and reported to be in the range of 65% in the case of PtG
and 35% in the case of PtGtP. However, as mentioned in [1], the efﬁciency
is only relevant in the narrow perspective of PtG in which the technology
is solely applied on direct power storage. Efﬁciency is less important in a
wider perspective of the whole energy system – and thus to obtain a fully
sustainable energy system.
2.3
Green hydrogen customers
To create a proﬁtable business case, the hydrogen selling price has to exceed
the hydrogen production price. Because hydrogen is a chemical secondary
energy carrier, hydrogen can be applied mainly in ﬁve different sectors: the
electricity sector (re-conversion), the heating sector (excess heat), the gas
sector, the industry sector and the mobility sector. Different for each market
is the market price. In Bünger et al. [15], the maximum possible hydrogen
production cost is calculated in each market for the year 2030. The costs are
presented in Table 2.2. By comparison, the production price for hydrogen
from PtG (with renewable electricity and bulk storage) is predicted to be 7.3
EUR/kg-H2 [15]. Furthermore, the surplus heat from the electrolyser and
fuel cell process can be changed into heat for district heating. According to
Energitilsynet [16], the average Danish heating price was 94 EUR/MWh in
2017.

Many experts have tried to predict the hydrogen market, but the future
attractiveness of green hydrogen will remain uncertain as well as the future
market size and future offtake prices.
2.4
Regulation
Although PtG has a high potential of storing large quantities of intermittent
electricity, regulations and laws are one of the keys to unlock the signiﬁcant
opportunities and to encourage the prevalence of PtG. The Danish energy
sector is regulated by different rules for grid connection, operation and
market.
This includes the cost of using the electricity grid, service
applications and subsidy schemes.
Taxes and tariffs
Energinet – the Danish TSO – handles the taxation of the ’energy motorways’
such as the electricity grid. Furthermore, the Danish government has added
taxes to the consumption of electricity as well as production of district
heating from excess heat. Based on reports from SKAT [19] and Energinet
==PAGE:17==
CHAPTER 2. KEY ASPECTS
10
[20], the current major taxes and tariffs include power and heating taxes,
network tariffs, system tariffs, distribution tariffs and PSO1.
Service applications
An additional income to consider is the income from support markets such as
the regulation and reserve market. In Wind Europe [21], PtG is compared to
other storage technologies in relation to service applications. The comparison
is based on charge/discharge time and capacity. PtG is found to be able to
deal with grid support (e.g. congestion) and system support, such as demand
response and seasonal storage, but unable to deliver frequency support and
dynamic voltage support. On the other hand, Ravn et al. [22] argue that
PtG facilities are able to provide frequency regulation (within 25 and 150
seconds). PtG is still a relatively new technology by which the exact value
of the system support is still under development.
Subsidy schemes
One way to promote new energy initiatives is by subsidy schemes. Different
schemes are applied for different technologies and is either focused on
investment or generation and based on price or quantity [23].
According to the Danish Parliament [24] and recent Danish climate
policies, there is no direct governmental support for PtG in Denmark.
However, the development of hydrogen technologies is indirectly supported
by the government as well as larger companies through few tax reductions
and R&D projects. In [18] – the newest Danish climate proposal – a plan
is formulated for the promotion of green mobility as well as the hydrogen
infrastructure. Another initiative of the Danish government is an exemption
of car registration tax for FCVs (which is up to 150% of the purchasing price
according to SKAT in [25]). Furthermore, Brintbranchen [26] run different
research projects on hydrogen (e.g. HyLAW and Future Gas) founded by the
European Commission and Danish Energy Agency respectively.

System simulation
In the previous chapter, the fundamentals of PtG were presented.
The
key aspects are utilised in this chapter to develop three suitable system
simulations for PtG based on two real locations.
The locations have
different characteristics and are studied to determine the most beneﬁcial
circumstances for PtG in Denmark.
The locations have been selected on
the grounds that they can both provide a PtG system integration, but that
the characteristics in their energy systems are otherwise different. Figure
3.1 shows the geographical location of the two facilities, Lille Thorup and
Esbjerg, as well as the national transmission gas grid.

4.4
Regulation affecting PtG
Essential to the feasibility of PtG in Denmark is also the national regulatory
framework.
According to Brintbranchen [26], Denmark has – in general
– favourable conditions for PtG. However, means of initiatives must
be launched to secure the penetration of green hydrogen production in
Denmark. One of the big challenges is the categorisation of green hydrogen,
e.g. hydrogen produced by electricity from the power grid with an unknown
amount of renewable energy.
Therefore, an international certiﬁcation of
hydrogen is necessary to promote hydrogen as a green fuel in the future.
However, this study does not look further into this discussion.
The regulatory framework addressed in this study is divided into two
main sections:
regulation on the producer side and regulation on the
consumer side. An overview of the evaluated parameters is presented in
Figure 4.4. Every parameter is assumed unchanged between today and 2030.
Regulation on the production side
Electricity grid tariffs:
The Danish grid tariffs covers grid operating,
depreciation, ﬁnancing and administration costs regulated by the Danish
Energy Agency (Energinet) [20].
The tariffs are different, depending on
whether the PtG facility consumes electricity from the grid or produces
electricity for the grid. Every tariff is presented in the following bullets, while
a summary of the used tariffs is presented in Table 4.14.

Regulation on the consumption side
While the regulation on the production side affects the feasibility of PtG
directly, the regulation on the consumer side affects PtG indirectly. Currently,
no initiatives for PtG exists (except exemption of car registration tax for
FCVs), but future regulation will most likely be inspired by already existing
initiatives for other green technologies.
Public subsidies according to its use: As previous mentioned, biogas
is promoted through public subsidies according to its use [54].
Support
schemes for biogas include the use for electricity production, injections into
the natural grid, industrial purposes, transport fuel and heating purposes. If
these support initiatives were applicable for PtG, the demand and hydrogen
selling price of green hydrogen would increase as well as the total value of
PtG.
Bio-tickets:
Another support scheme is bio-tickets which ensure an
intermixture of biofuels (5.75%) in conventional transport fuel.
The bio-
tickets act on the market as green certiﬁcates and are traded when a company
meets the intermixture requirement from EU. Thus, the bio-tickets would
increase the hydrogen demand on the consumer side. However, biofuels are
deﬁned as a liquid or gaseous fuel based on biomass for use in the transport
sector.
Hydrogen produced with green electricity is not covered by this
deﬁnition as electricity is not a liquid or gaseous fuel and the production
is based on electricity and not biomass.
As the future for the regulatory framework on the consumer side is very
uncertain, the public subsidies according to hydrogen’s use and bio-tickets
are not included in this analysis.

Results
This chapter covers the results of the mathematical optimisation model for
each system simulation, which includes the objective (proﬁt) of each model,
the electrolyser, storage and fuel cell capacity and the operation scheduling
over a year with an hourly resolution assuming known energy prices.
Many tests have been done to investigate the behaviour of the models
and interesting outcome areas. Consequently, this chapter does not focus
on the same base scenario for every system simulation, but it does focus on
several scenarios selected speciﬁcally for each location. However, common
to all scenarios is that the electricity spot price, gas price and regulatory
framework are the main parameters explored.
The results are presented
for each system simulation where every section starts with a presentation
of the selected scenarios followed by the results and a discussion. Lastly, key
ﬁndings are presented. The chapter is rounded off with an overall discussion
of the results where the potential of each customer sector is addressed.
The results presented in this study are also discussed in relation to
other studies of PtG. Additionally, a discussion of the method and model
assumptions are presented throughout the following chapter along with the
impact of speciﬁc parameters.
All scenarios have been performed in GAMS, and every model is
included in Appendix D (Model ﬁles).

The results show that PtG in Lille Thorup requires strong regulation
to become proﬁtable. One of the reasons can be found in the round-trip
efﬁciency of the system. With an electrolyser efﬁciency of 58%, a storage
efﬁciency of 90% and a fuel cell efﬁciency of 50%, the round-trip efﬁciency
becomes 26%. This requires a signiﬁcantly higher spread between the buying
and selling price of electricity.
As an example, if 1 MWh electricity is
produced and sold to the grid for 80 EUR/MWh, the maximum possible
electricity buying price to get a positive proﬁt is 4.7 EUR/MWh, including
taxes and tariffs and without support. If the heating revenue is included, the
maximum buying price is 14.3 EUR/MWh. In other words, high efﬁciencies
and high price volatility are needed to improve the business case in Lille
Thorup. This is also reﬂected in the results where the lowest needed support
is found in Scenario 3. On the other hand, the highest needed support is
found in Scenario 2 where the price is high and the volatility is low.
Looking closer at the impact of the heating price, a price increase would
increase the maximum possible selling price of electricity.
With a 30%
increase in the heating price1, the maximum buying price becomes 17.2
EUR/MWh for 1 MWh electricity sold to the grid for 80 EUR/MWh. This is
an increase of 2.9 EUR/MWh compared to the selected heating price. Thus,
a higher heating price decreases the impact of price volatility.

Key ﬁndings
• PtG in Lille Thorup is a very uneconomic venture and requires
strong support (tariff exemption and investment support) to become
proﬁtable.
Among other things, this is down to a low round-trip
efﬁciency.
• Large electrolyser and fuel cell capacities are needed to take advantage
of the ﬂuctuation in the overall electricity generation and price levels
which will require large investments.
• If a great amount of support is provided, Lille Thorup is most proﬁtable
at low spot prices and high price variations (Scenario 3).
• Looking at a three-year period, the similarity between the yearly price
proﬁles determined the proﬁtability of the facility. The more similar
proﬁles, the more proper sizing of the processes and the higher proﬁt.
• As the electrolyser and storage capacity are dependent on each other,
the full potential of the large underground storage cannot be utilised
due to a high electrolyser investment cost.

5.3
Overall discussion
Looking at the results from an overall point of view, the concept of PtGtP
seems unproﬁtable under the investigated framework. On the other hand,
positive revenues are discovered for the concept of PtG in the natural gas
sector, mobility and heating sector - and most likely also the industry sector,
Hydrogen injected into the natural gas is only possible at exceptional
high price peaks (Location 2A) or at low production costs (Location 2B).
When injecting hydrogen into the gas transmission grid, the facility must be
located close to the gas transmission grid with high ﬂow rates to ensure that
the hydrogen dilutes the natural gas. As the amount of injected hydrogen
at Location 2A is small and insigniﬁcant, the gas grid is evaluated as an
unimportant customer by which the facility location is not restricted by the
location of the gas grid. On the other hand, the main customer at Location 2B
(Scenario 3 and 4) is the gas grid, by which the facility location is restricted
by the gas grid location.
Identical for all scenarios is the fact that no hydrogen is purchased by
the industry sector because of high distribution costs.
However, another
business opportunity for the industry sector can be developed with the
ﬁnding of the possibility of gas grid injection. The industry selling price is
higher than the average gas price (54 and 34 EUR/MWh), but the distribution
cost renders a proﬁtable hydrogen sale to the industry sector impossible.
Imagine that the hydrogen sold to the natural gas grid is sold directly to the
industry instead (distribution is avoided), the proﬁt would with certainty
increase. This requires that the facility is located close to a big hydrogen
consuming industry (e.g. reﬁneries, ammonia production or a production
of other chemicals) and that the facility enters into cooperation with one of
these industries. However, this would also reduce the risk of the business
case as the hydrogen demand in the industry sector already exists and the
facility has a ﬁxed customer with a ﬁxed purchase and price. As the focus on
green initiatives is still increasing, it is conceivable that green hydrogen will
==PAGE:61==
CHAPTER 5. RESULTS
54
become more sought after in these big industries with which the value of PtG
would increase and perhaps so would the price of green hydrogen.
The mobility sector is the most favourable costumer at Location 2A and
2B because of high selling prices. However, the facility should be careful
to not only focus on the mobility sector as this sector contains a high
risk because the breakthrough for hydrogen-powered vehicles is lacking.
However, this can change signiﬁcantly towards 2030 in either a positive or
a negative direction.
The heat sales also improve the feasibility of PtG signiﬁcantly by
providing an additional revenue. However, the main driver of the business
case should not be the possibility of district heating, instead, a facility
location next to a district heating grid is clearly preferred.
Lastly, it must be mentioned that this study is based on many uncertain
assumptions about future developments in prices, demands and infrastruc-
ture, which is why the results only are valid for the presented framework.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate the feasibility of PtG in a Danish
context and based on a mathematical optimisation model tested on real data
for two speciﬁc locations. The model was constructed to determine the yearly
proﬁt, the size of the facility and the level of hydrogen production as well as
to determine the energy sales to different end customers.
The results showed that PtG as a pure PtGtP concept (with the use
of underground storage as well as steel tanks) requires strong support to
becomes proﬁtable, by which this business case is determined infeasible
under the investigated circumstances. High capacities are needed in Lille
Thorup to take advantage of the ﬂuctuations in power generation and price
level, whereas high investments are essential. These investments cannot be
covered by the revenue from the electricity market, by which much support
– consisting of tariff exemption and CAPEX and OPEX support – is needed.
On the other hand, a ﬂexible system solutions investigated at Location
2A and 2B are much more economical.
The ﬂexibility of Esbjerg is not
fully utilised as the hydrogen is sold solely to the gas grid or the mobility
sector.
By removing or signiﬁcantly reducing the distribution cost, a
potential for PtG in the industry sector is discovered.
However, the
amount of hydrogen injected into the natural gas grid at Location 2B most
likely exceed the intermixture requirements which possibly challenges the
business case in that system simulation. Furthermore, the dependency of
the mobility sector increases the risk as hydrogen-powered vehicles are not
fully commercialised.
Another uncertainty of Location 2B is the future
regulatory framework for infrastructures as well as the wind farm proﬁt of
fuelling the PtG facility rather than producing electricity to the grid. On the
other hand, Location 2A highly need a certiﬁcation of green hydrogen to
ensure the sustainability of the whole process. Furthermore, the feasibility
of Location 2A increases signiﬁcantly with tariff exemption, and a new
regulatory framework would be a great advantage.
Thus, one system

simulation (Location 2A or 2B) cannot be assessed to be more valuable than
the other because of different advantages and disadvantages.
Additionally, it can be concluded that low average spot prices and
volatile spot prices have a high impact on the PtG facility operation. This
is supported by the hydrogen storage where hydrogen is charged at low spot
prices and discharged at high spot prices. Obviously, the hydrogen selling
price also has a major effect on the hydrogen production. Hydrogen is sold
to the end customer willing to pay the highest price. Electricity grid tariff
exemption is evaluated as the strongest regulatory instrument to promote
green hydrogen in Denmark, but does not necessary entail a higher hydrogen
production.
The main barriers of PtG in Denmark are the uncertainty in the future
selling prices and demands which increases the risk.
Furthermore, the
current regulatory framework does not help the commercialisation of neither
green hydrogen in general or the technology of PtG. On the other hand,
it is expected that the volatility of electricity spot prices would increase
in the future which is one of the main drivers for PtG. Another driver is
the increased demand for green initiatives that can support the increased
penetration of intermittent electricity generation. Even though PtG in some
scenarios is uneconomical from a private economic point of view, PtG might
be proven feasible from a social economical point of view.
To wrap it all up, the business case of using PtG to help the electricity
market as a regulating power plant seems infeasible. On the other hand,
the business case of PtG facilities selling green hydrogen to the mobility or
industry sector seems much more feasible as the selling prices are higher, the
demands can be predicted and the green hydrogen demand in these sectors
will most likely increase towards 2030.
6.1
Future work
As the results do not fully explore all aspects of PtG, an extension of this
project could be made to support the ﬁndings and to explore other concepts
for PtG in Denmark.
One example is to develop an in-depth analysis of the potential of green
hydrogen in the mobility and industry sector.
Thus, potential customers
(e.g.
speciﬁc reﬁneries) must be investigated to determine the value of
green hydrogen in these sectors. Another example is to further investigate
the system solution for a PtG facility fuelled by private wind power. This
includes an analysis of the operation scheduling of the wind farm to yield to
highest proﬁt.
