Abstract
Due to environmental pollution issues, focus has been put worldwide on the reduction of
fossil fuels based power generation and the utilisation of diﬀerent renewable energy mixes.
However, the increase in the penetration rates of renewable energy and power electronic
based generation in the power systems has aﬀected their total inertia making them more
vulnerable to disturbances and blackouts related to frequency instabilities.
In this thesis, a co-located hybrid power plant comprised of a wind power plant, solar
photovoltaics plant and battery energy storage system is modelled. In addition, a control
system is developed with the aim to regulate the active power of the hybrid power plant
facilitating this way its capability to provide frequency support services to the power system.
DigSilent PowerFactory software has been utilised for the development of the respective
models.
The obtained results show that the generic control system developed in this thesis enhanced
the hybrid power plant controllability regarding its active power generation both under
normal and fault conditions.
The model capability to eﬀectively generate the imposed
active power references under both constant and turbulent weather conditions is ﬁrstly
examined and validated under normal operation. Then, its ability to participate in the
regulation of frequency related contingencies is assessed when an active power imbalance
event is applied to the power system. The observed improvement in the frequency response
is attributed to the active power reserve released when a frequency instability occurs. Thus,
the outcome of this study is that the developed control system enables the hybrid power
plant to successfully provide primary frequency support services in a power system with
reduced inertia, allowing for participation in the frequency ancillary services market.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Over the last years, the need for reducing Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Emissions’ rates has
been widely increased. For this reason, international agreements (Kyoto Protocol, Paris
Agreement) have been signed worldwide by many countries, having as a goal the limitation
of the global temperature increase rate and the climate change [1]. According to the EU
targets, Renewable Energy Generation in Europe has to reach the amount of at least 27%
of the total energy consumption by 2030.
In Denmark, the policies on climate change
mitigation have been formulated partly in compliance with these international agreements
enacted by both the United Nations (UN) and the European Union (EU) and partly by
the Danish national target decisions instituted in the energy sector [1]. According to the
Danish climate policies towards this direction and the Danish government platform in 2015,
Denmark has set the target to be independent of fossil fuels by 2050 [2].
The aforementioned policies along with their market acceptance have resulted in the increase
of the penetration rates of Renewable Energy Sources (RES), such as wind and solar, to
the power systems [3].
During the last years, a general trend of combining RES with
a storage system has been developed, aiming for the utilisation of their complementary
nature and their operating conditions, resulting in an improved system eﬃciency and larger
power reliability [4]. Many economical investments have been made towards this direction,
allowing for the green transition to be facilitated and the RES integration into the power
system to expand rapidly, making their cumulative energy cost more competitive with
conventional generation technologies [5].
Currently, the combination of wind and solar power with electricity storage for utility-scale
hybrid power plants (HPPs) has attracted interest from many manufacturers [4]. Apart from
the eﬀorts for new HPP constructions, many industries turn to the idea of overplanting,
with the aim to increase the use of the existing infrastructure. In particular, the transition
of wind power plants (WPPs) into HPPs has become a general trend in the industry today.
This concept, known as wind-based utility-scale HPP, is aiming for the proﬁt maximisation
from diﬀerent markets (i.e. capacity market, energy market and ancillary services market)
[4].
A wind-based utility-scale HPP can have several beneﬁts compared to an individual tech-
nology power plant [4] [5]:
1. Increase in the capacity factor -
when the evacuation factor is limited and for
overplanting situation.
2. Decrease in variability - when individual technologies are combined together due
to their complementary nature. As RES are deﬁned by weather conditions and not
1
==PAGE:14==
by demand, they may cause additional stress to the electrical grid, curtailment of the
renewable generators and low (or even negative) power prices.
3. Increase in availability -
when individual technology proﬁles are combined to-
gether due to their complementary nature. This may result in higher annual energy
production (AEP) and capacity factor (CF) as the excess energy production, which
otherwise would be curtailed, is stored.
4. Cost reduction and Revenue increase - the optimal utilisation of the electrical
infrastructure of RES can lead to the cost reduction due to the HPP design (CAPEX).
Operational costs (OPEX) can also be decreased.
5. Increase in ancillary services capability - the utilisation of storage systems in
order to provide ancillary services facilitates the HPP entrance in new markets and,
hence, its revenue raise.
6. Increased dispatchability and ﬂexibility using storage for the revenue max-
imisation from market - when overplanting, the reduction of the power curtailment
amount is prompted.
The attention in this master thesis project is directed towards co-located utility-scale HPPs,
which combine diﬀerent power generation technologies, such as wind, solar photovoltaics
(PV) and battery energy storage system (BESS), as depicted in Figure 1. The term ”co-
located” refers to the fact that the HPP diﬀerent technologies are connected to the same
substation, which is the Point of Common Connection (PCC) between the HPP and the
external grid [5]. An HPP has typically higher controllability, as its diﬀerent power tech-
nologies are owned by the same company [4]. Contrary to a traditional hybrid power system,
the electrical load is not involved into the HPP owner concerns [4].
Figure 1: Co-located HPP connected to the grid.
In the electrical design of an HPP, a deep understanding of the electrical infrastructure, site
conditions, grid codes, etc. is required by the HPP owners [4]. The challenges imposed due
2
==PAGE:15==
to the high RES penetration in modern power systems are mainly related to the dynamic
security and reliability of the power systems’ operation.
A balance between generation
and consumption of electricity must always be maintained within the power system. This
balance is reﬂected to the system frequency. In case of a supply-demand imbalance, the
system frequency is aﬀected and if this violates some predeﬁned frequency range, then the
stability and the security of the power system is threatened. Consequently, the HPP power
output quality must satisfy the grid code requirements imposed by the transmission system
operators (TSOs) in order for this supply-demand balance to be maintained [6].
The fulﬁllment of the grid requirements can be achieved by means of appropriate control
strategies. In this context, this research work proposes and develops a generic HPP con-
troller for frequency control support from a co-located HPP, seen as a single power plant
from the grid point of view [7]. This control system is thus implemented to ensure the HPP
capability to participate in the Frequency Ancillary Services (FAS) market for the provision
of primary frequency support to the power system. In this respect, the BESS role is crucial
due to its ability to perform a frequency raise service by releasing power or a frequency
reduction service if it has the capacity to store electricity from the grid for a certain period
of time [5].
1.2
Objectives
The overall objective of this thesis is to investigate frequency support capability of a co-
located HPP. The main focus is to develop a dynamic model and control strategy for
co-located HPPs for provision of frequency containment reserve support.
In this context, the following main tasks are targeted:
1. Modelling and control of the WPP.
2. Modelling and control of the solar PV.
3. Modelling and control of the BESS.
4. Performance evaluation of the individual technology models by means of simulation
tests.
5. Synthesis of the HPP compounded by the WPP, solar PV plant and BESS.
6. Performance evaluation of the complete HPP model by means of simulation tests.
7. Development of a generic HPP control system which is responsible for the active power
output regulation and ensures that the HPP has the capability to provide primary
FAS.
8. Performance evaluation of the HPP control system developed by means of simulation
tests.
3
==PAGE:16==
The dynamic modelling of the HPP and all the simulation tests are realised by utilising
the DigSilent PowerFactory software. PowerFactory is a power system analysis software
application used for the analysis of generation, transmission, distribution and industrial
power systems.
1.3
Assumptions and Limitations
The following assumptions and limitations have been considered during this research work:
• The model and control system developed for the HPP are only suitable for frequency
control studies.
• The technical requirements to be satisﬁed regarding the system frequency and the
quality of the active power output are based on the Danish grid code and the EU
Commission regulations [1] [2].
• The performance of the HPP models are evaluated based on the model performance
at the PCC.
• The frequency controller uses local measured frequency, meaning that the frequency
is always measured at the PCC. The frequency, though, is assumed to be the same
within the whole power system.
• Apart from the HPP, conventional generators also participate in the system frequency
regulation.
• Due to the action of the Automatic Voltage Regulator (AVR) functions of the grid
generators, the voltage levels are considered to be constant during the simulation
tests.

2.1
Frequency in the Power System
As already mentioned in 1.1, the balance between electricity generation and consumption
is required in order for the frequency stability not to be violated. This is achieved by the
automatic and constant response from fast generating units and its objective is to avoid
frequency deviations from its nominal value (50Hz or 60Hz) when a power imbalance occurs
in the power system [6]. Frequency values lower than the rated indicate a higher than the
generation consumption, whereas values higher than the rated frequency correspond to a
lower than the generation consumption.
Generally, the TSOs are responsible for the frequency to be kept constant at its rated value.
This balance is typically ensured by utilising functions such as the activation of additional
generation or the disconnection of loads (load shedding) [8]. The grid codes impose the
frequency ranges and the duration within which the generating power plants will continue
to operate, either with or without modifying their power production [6].
The regulation of the frequency is realised through three consecutive steps which are illus-
trated in Figure 2, where a frequency imbalance occurs at A point. The ﬁrst step of the
frequency response (A-B) is called Fast Frequency Response (FFR) and reﬂects the power
plants’ capability to regulate frequency by temporarily providing active power support to
the system [9]. This control stage is traditionally related to the emulated inertial response
or to the fast primary frequency response by renewable power plants, the ﬁrst of which can
be extracted from the kinetic energy stored in the wind turbine rotors; on the other hand,
the fast primary frequency response requires an active power reserve both by WPPs and
solar PV plants [9]. The second stage of the frequency regulation (B-C) is called Frequency
Containment Reserve (FCR) and refers to the automatic response activated in a generation
plant which is responsible for frequency regulation and power balancing [9] [10] [11]. The
frequency rises gradually over a period of a few seconds and according to [10] and [11], it has
6
==PAGE:19==
a full activation time of up to 30 seconds. Finally, the third stage (C-D) is called Frequency
Restoration Reserve (FRR) and is responsible to modify the active power setpoints of the
generation plants in order for the frequency to be restored back to its nominal value and
the power system balance to be maintained [10]. This stage can last from seconds up to 15
minutes after an imbalance event occurs [10].
Figure 2: Frequency regulation schematic [10].
The balance between electricity supply and demand is achieved by the TSO buying ancillary
services from electricity generators and consumers [12]. In the Nordic area, the highest
permissible variations in frequency is in the range of ±0.2%, meaning that the range within
which the frequency is considered to be in steady state (in balance) is between 49.9-50.1 Hz
[13].
2.2
Wind Turbines
Due to the constant penetration of the wind power into the power system, diﬀerent wind
turbine concepts have been developed over the last years [14]. Wind turbines can be divided
into diﬀerent categories based on their speed and power control abilities.
Wind turbine generators can be classiﬁed into ﬁxed speed and variable speed. A general
overview of the diﬀerent characteristics of ﬁxed and variable speed wind turbines is presented
in [15] and [16]. Due to their ability to continuously adjust their rotational speed to the
wind speed, obtaining this way the maximum aerodynamic eﬃciency over a wide range of
wind speeds and providing a better power quality, variable speed wind turbines are used in
this thesis.
The most common wind turbine concepts are Fixed Speed concept (Type 1), variable speed
wind turbines (VSWT) with variable rotor resistance (Type 2), VSWT with partial-scale
frequency converter (Type 3) and VSWT with full-scale frequency converter (Type 4) [14]
[15] [17]. Type 3 is also referred as the Doubly Fed Induction Generator (DFIG) concept.
From these, the most common used in WPPs are types 3 and 4, the conﬁgurations of which
7
==PAGE:20==
are illustrated in Figure 3. Although Type 3 wind turbines were the dominant type in the
past years, Type 4 using PMSG is becoming more and more popular in large scale WPPs
[14]. In [18] [19] [20] a comparison of DFIG (Type 3) and PMSG (Type 4) based wind
turbines regarding their eﬃciency and performance is presented.
The fact that Type 1 and Type 2 wind turbine concepts are directly coupled to the grid
implies an inherent inertial response provision to the system [21]. However, this response is
restricted and uncontrollable and thus, Type 1 and 2 hardly contribute to the system em-
ulated inertial response. However, with the aid of a pitch controller and through deloading
control, both these concepts have the capability of providing a constant amount of available
aerodynamic power as a reserve margin to support the primary frequency regulation ser-
vices [21]. On the other hand, Types 3 and 4 are decoupled from the grid due to their power
converter-based interface, therefore modern wind turbines do not inherently contribute with
inertia to the system, as the mechanical energy stored in their rotors remains isolated from
the power grid [10] [22]. However, the implementation of suitable control methods in or-
der for the inertial response of these wind turbine types to be emulated makes them more
attractive, even compared to conventional generators, due to their faster, more accurate
and ﬂexible active power regulation [21]. In the present thesis, Type 4 PMSG based wind
turbine concept is utilised.
Figure 3: Type 3 and 4 wind turbine conﬁgurations. Types C and D stand for Types 3 and 4 respectively.
WRIG, WRSG and PMSG stand for wound rotor induction generator, wound rotor synchronous generator
and permanent magnet synchronous generator respectively [15].
Lastly, wind turbines can be classiﬁed according to the power control they perform, which
aims to lower the turbine damage risk due to high wind speeds. If the turbine runs at a
maximum speed and a high wind gust occurs, the aerodynamic torque can take a critical
value and a runaway situation can be caused. The most common power control strategies
are Stall and Pitch control and are thoroughly described in [14] and [15]. In this research,
pitch control is applied for the power control of the wind turbines and the turbine blades are

[14] [15].

3.1
Wind Power Plant Model
The WPP modelling has been based on the aggregated method described in [31]. This
method is commonly used in cases of large-scale WPPs where the concern is the entire
WPP impact on the power system [31]. The most important beneﬁt of this method is that
it simpliﬁes the system, as it avoids the use of a multitude of wind turbines and also, it
reduces the computation time substantially [31]. The objective of the aggregated model is
to represent a large WPP by rescaling the power capacity of a single wind turbine [31] [32].
In Figure 7, the generic schematic of the aggregated model is shown, where N refers to the
number of the wind turbines. In this thesis, N is considered to be equal to 60 wind turbines.
The input of the WPP is assumed to be the average of the wind speeds for the diﬀerent
wind turbines [32].
DigSilent Powerfactory oﬀers a built-in aggregation method for the
electrical equipment of the system (i.e. generator, power converter, transformer, capacitor,
inductance) of the wind turbine. Therefore, the generator and the transformers have been
modelled by a speciﬁc number of parallel generators and transformers respectively, while
the rest of the electrical equipment (i.e. power converters, capacitance and inductance)
have been upscaled accordingly to the increased power ﬂow [31]. In this thesis, the nominal
apparent power of the WPP, the generator side converter and the grid side converter have
been considered to be equal to 132MVA, 150MVA and 150MVA respectively.

Transmission model
The Transmission model represents the mechanical part of the wind turbine, where only
the drive train is taken into consideration, as it is the part which mainly contributes to
the interaction with the grid and has the most relevant inﬂuence on the power ﬂuctuations
[31]. The drive train is responsible for the conversion of the aerodynamic torque Trot of the

MPPT control model
MPPT control block represents the Maximum Power Point Tracking and generates the op-
timal value of the active power given as a reference to the Controller 2, where the active
power control is realised. It consists of a predeﬁned look-up table, which shows the relation
between the optimal active power and the generator speed and provides the optimal aero-
dynamic power by keeping the power coeﬃcient Cp at its maximum value until the nominal
rotational speed is reached, i.e. while the wind turbine operates in the power optimisation
zone (see Section 2.2.1).
Damping control model
The Damping control is achieved through a bandpass ﬁlter and a phase compensator. As
depicted in Figure 9, this block receives the generator speed signal as an input and generates
a DC voltage reference value for Controller 1. The damping controller controls the speed
oscillations by using the DC-capacitor as a short-term energy storage. The reference DC
voltage Vdc,ref reﬂects these speed oscillations.

As explained in Section 2.2, the target of the pitch controller is to change the pitch angle of
the blades in order to control the power output of the wind turbine. In Figure 11, the pitch

and the servomechanism model comprised of both pitch angle and rate-of-change limiters.
The input of the PI controller is the error between the reference generator speed and the
generator speed signals. The pitch angle controller limits the rotor speed when the generator
power reaches its rated value, by limiting the mechanical power extracted from the wind.
The gain scheduling control of the pitch angle purpose is to compensate for the non-linear

Hybrid power plant control system
In this section, the generic control system (brieﬂy presented in Section 3.5) is described
explicitly. The control system task is to regulate the active power of the hybrid power plant
facilitating this way its capability to provide frequency support to the power system and
therefore, to participate in the FAS market in the same way as the conventional power plants.
The overall block diagram of the HPP controller is illustrated in Figure 30 , visualising all
the components that constitute the controller. The control system is separated into two
major parts, the Main controller and the Dispatch parts.
Figure 30: Hybrid power plant control system conﬁguration.
The HPP controller receives as inputs the TSO orders regarding the control function to be
applied as well as the active power reference, which are formed in a way that the power
system requirements are met. In addition, a frequency controller is also incorporated into
the Main controller, which receives the frequency measurements at all times through a
Phased-Locked Loop (PLL) device. In this way, any possible frequency deviation from its
rated value (50Hz) is detected and translated into an active power signal which has to be
included on top of the active power reference stemmed from the TSO requirements. The
summation of these two active power references constitute the total active power reference.
This signal is passed through a Proportional-Integral controller (PI controller) along with
the actual active power signal reaching the PCC and measured through a measurement
device, with the aim to zero the error between them, securing this way that the ﬁnal active
power reference is followed with the minimum possible error.
Subsequently, the active power reference derived from the PI controller is fed into the dis-
patch block, which through the appropriate algorithm, allocates this power to the respective
control systems of the subplants. As shown in Figure 30, the available active powers of the
WPP and solar PV plant, the corrective active power reference from the frequency con-

==PAGE:44==
troller and the SOC of the BESS are also used as inputs to the Dispatch part of the control
system. PI controllers are also used for the individual active power reference signals sent
out to the subplants’ controllers. Finally, measurement and communication delays have
been also used for the measurements and for the communications between the HPP con-
troller and the controllers of the individual technologies. In Figure 30, the measurement
and communication delays are not depicted. The diﬀerent parts of the control system are
presented in detail in the following chapters.
4.1
Control functions block
In this section, the diﬀerent control functions used for the representation of the TSO re-
quirements are presented.
Firstly, an active power reference signal is elaborated in the
control functions block, based on the control function required by the TSO. Each control
function can be activated at any moment. In this thesis, four active power control functions
are considered, having as a main target the HPP to support the grid by providing primary
FAS. These functions, along with their characteristics, are described below [7]:
1. No power limitation control: The HPP is required to generate the maximum
available active power coming from the renewable generation technologies. The BESS
is required not to generate active power in normal conditions in order to maintain its
capability to provide frequency regulation (if its state of charge permits) for the case
when an abnormality in the system frequency occurs.
2. Delta control: The HPP is required to operate with a continuous reserve power
capacity in relation to its total available active power coming from the WPP and
solar PV plant. As the target of the HPP in this research is to have the ability to
provide primary FAS, the greatest advantage of this method is that such a reserve
capacity facilitates the provision of frequency regulation actions when needed.
3. Balance control: Here, the reference active power signal is adjusted downwards
or upwards compared to the total available active power of the HPP, based on grid
requirements. Hence, a constant value is set as a total active power reference from
the TSO.
4. Power gradient limiter: Finally, a power gradient limiter is also included in the
applied control functions, which has the responsibility to set how fast the total active
power production can be adjusted upwards and downwards. By limiting the rate in
HPP power output, the power gradient limiter ensures that the conventional power
plants of the power system can also adjust their outputs with respect to the fast
changing weather conditions (i.e. wind speed, solar irradiance) [6].

Frequency controller
In this section, the control rationale applied in the frequency controller block is described.
A droop and derivative (ROCOF=df/dt represents the rate of change of frequency) control
structure is applied as presented in [10].
As illustrated in Figure 32, the controller consists of two stages (droop control and ROCOF
estimation), which both act in a similar way: when the deadband is cleared, a constant
gain is applied, which will be added or subtracted from the reference signal according to
the contingency happening in each case. The frequency is measured at the PCC in order
deviations from its nominal value to be detected and corrective actions to be realised. Thus,
the HPP must be able to generate either more or less active power in order to compensate
for a possible frequency deviation. For this reason, the corrective active power output DP
coming out of the controller is added to the active power reference signal derived from the
control functions’ block. If there is no frequency deviation from its nominal value, then the
output of the frequency controller DP will be equal to 0.

4.3
Dispatch strategy
In this section, the logic of the algorithm developed in the Dispatch block is analysed. As
already mentioned, the dispatch block rationale is to distribute the active power reference
signal derived from the PI controller to the individual controllers of the subplants, based on
several criteria. In Figure 30 it can be noticed that the dispatch block receives the available
active power signals from the WPP and the solar PV plant, the active power reference of
the HPP derived from the PI controller, the corrective power quantity DP stemmed from
the frequency controller and the SOC of the BESS. The Dispatch algorithm can be divided
into two main states:
• Active Power Maximisation (APM) state, where the control function used is the ”No
power limitation” control function. The purpose of the dispatch function in this case
is to maximise the power output of the renewable generation plants and at the same
time, ensuring that the BESS has the capability of participating in the frequency
regulation in case of a frequency related instability, if its SOC allows for this, i.e. if
SOC>0.
• Active Power Limitation (APL) state, where the curtailment control functions are re-
quired. The term ”limitation” refers to the fact that the HPP always operates with a
lower active power output than its total capacity. Here, the total active power refer-
ence is allocated by giving priority ﬁrstly to the PV and then to the WPP. In case of
excess active power compared to the total renewable power plants’ available one, and
if the SOC allows for it (i.e. if SOC<1), the BESS is charged. On the other hand,
in case that the active power reference is lower than the total available power of the
HPP, and if the SOC allows for it (i.e. if SOC>0), the BESS is discharged.

”No power limitation” control under frequency instabilities

observed which reaches its Nadir point (0.9945p.u.) after almost 4s and stabilises again in
the value of 0.9979p.u. after a time of around 20s since the event started.
In the ”No power limitation” control case, the WPP and solar PV plant do not participate in
the frequency regulation as they already operate at their available active power levels and no
possibility for temporary overloading is provided from the models. Hence, this temporary
overloading power reference received from the frequency controller, is undertaken to be
produced from the BESS, provided that its SOC is suﬃcient. In Figure 44, it is observed
how the active power reference is diﬀerentiated from the actual active power at the PCC,
regarding both the size and trend of the curves. The reason why this happens is quite
obvious, as the applied load step event of 90MW is very large to be regulated by the 10MW
BESS of the HPP, considering that, in this case, it is only the BESS who is responsible for
the provision of frequency control service. Consequently, even though the BESS is required
to generate more than 10MW active power, its active power output cannot be higher than
its capacity. This limitation is achieved by the PI controller, which, as already said, limits
the total active power reference sent to the Dispatch block according to the HPP active
power capacity. However, as the HPP is connected to the strong grid, the grid conventional
generators, not being congested and implementing a larger active power reserve, compensate
for this active power production realising the same droop.

6
Conclusions and Future Work
6.1
Conclusions
This thesis has been carried out with the aim to develop a dynamic model for a co-located
HPP comprised of WPP, solar PV plant and BESS. Furthermore a generic control system
has been developed in order to enable the HPP capability to participate into frequency
control services.
The ﬁrst task in this thesis has been focused on the modelling and the performance eval-
uation of the individual units that the HPP consists of. In this respect, several simulation
tests have been performed deducing that the models of the renewable generation units (i.e.
WPP and solar PV plant) are strictly dependent on the weather conditions (i.e. wind speed
and solar irradiance) and their ﬂuctuating nature. Therefore, any changes in the weather
conditions are directly reﬂected in the active power output of these technologies. Addition-
ally, the BESS model performance has been tested in order to validate its storage capacity,
which in this thesis has been considered equal to 20MWh, and, also its ability to get charged
or discharged depending on the state of charge (SOC) value.
The next step has been the synthesis of the HPP model, which ﬁrstly has been set to operate
at its full active power capacity based on the weather conditions applied each time to the
model. Subsequently, HPP has been successfully reinforced with a generic control system,
on top of the local controllers already existing in the models of the individual subplants,
with the purpose of achieving a higher controllability in terms of its active power output
and enabling the HPP to provide primary frequency ancillary services. The HPP control
system has been built up taking into consideration the TSO requirements regarding the
control functionalities under which the HPP has to operate depending on the power system
needs.
In addition, the developed frequency controller has assured that any frequency
deviation in the system is detected eﬀectually and the corrective actions are assigned to the
total active power output in order for the power system balance to be recovered. Based on
this, the dispatch function sends the active power reference signals to the local controllers
of the individual technologies. These signals are then forwarded to the points of coupling
(PoCs) of the subplants. The accuracy with which the actual active power signals follow
the reference ones is satisfying, due to the PI controllers’ eﬀect on the system.
From the simulation tests’ procedure, it has been concluded that the HPP has acquired the
capability of providing frequency control services to the power system. The HPP control
system is suﬃcient for the participation of the HPP into the frequency regulation of the
power system. The comparison between the base case, where no generic control system is
applied, and the developed case where the ”No power limitation” control function is ordered,
has shown that the HPP control system has resulted in an improved frequency response
for the power system. The BESS has been proved to be an advantage for the provision of
58
==PAGE:71==
frequency ancillary services from the HPP, as it is the one which supports the system in case
where no reserve capacity is ordered for the other units. Its small capacity, though, is not
suﬃcient for all the frequency related imbalance contingencies. This is perfectly acceptable,
considering that the aim of the developed control system has been to advanvce the HPP
active power control aiming for its participation to the primary frequency ancillary services
market, which eﬀectuates an economic beneﬁt for the HPP owner.
In conclusion, the outcome of the present thesis can be summarised to the following:
• The dynamic model of an HPP compounded of a PMSG based WPP, a solar PV plant
and a lithium-ion based technology BESS has been created and can be utilised for
frequency control studies.
• The generic control system developed and applied to the HPP has achieved to fulﬁl
its control requirements under both normal and fault conditions. The imposed active
power references have successfully been generated by the HPP in all the study cases,
under both constant and turbulent weather conditions.
• The reinforcement of the HPP with the generic control system brought about an
improved frequency response for the power system. In particular, the active power
reserve imposed for all the control functions has enabled the HPP with the capability
to participate in the ancillary frequency services market, by being released when an
under-frequency related imbalance occurs.
• Given the total available active power of the HPP, the comparison between diﬀerent
active power reserve requirements from the TSO has shown that the lower the active
power reference ordered by the TSO, the better the response of the HPP for under-
frequency instabilities and the same load step event is.
• The sensitivity analysis regarding the droop value of the frequency controller has
shown that the raise of this value leads to a worse frequency response of the power
system and a lower active power output for the HPP.
6.2
Future work
In this thesis, the goal for the dynamic modelling of an HPP and the development of its
generic control system for active power and frequency regulation has been accomplished.
This control system can be utilised by many other renewable power plants for frequency
support to the power system. However, further research is recommended to be conducted
for the optimisation of this control system in order to be applied in a real HPP integrated
into the power network. The aspects which are proposed to be further investigated in the
future are the following:
59
==PAGE:72==
• Further develop the HPP frequency controller to also include other frequency control
stages as presented in Section 2.
• Develop an optimal dispatch while including techno-economic criteria.
• Optimise the size of both the solar PV plant and the BESS.
• Investigate the impact of communication delays on HPP capability to provide fre-
quency support.
• The internal active power losses of the HPP should be calculated with larger accu-
racy as in [10] and then attributed to the proportional and time constants of the PI
controllers of the control system in order to ensure an optimised power injection to
the PCC.
