Abstract
AZO is a promising replacement for ITO as a transparent conducting oxide. However, good electrical properties
of AZO are mostly limited to speciﬁc regions over a substrate and are highly sensitive to deposition parameters.
This has largely been suggested to be caused by the presence of negative oxygen ions during deposition and
subsequently, high presence of atomic oxygen atoms in the ﬁlm.
This study was conducted to compare the highly sensitive pressure parameters between 2 and 5 mTorr of AZO
with ITO and GZO. Characterisation techniques were analysed to compare the properties and determine the
cause for poor electrical properties of AZO thin ﬁlms. RF magnetron sputtering used to deposit the thin ﬁlms
onto soda lime glass substrates.
It was discussed that the electrical properties of AZO and GZO show high spatial variations suggested to be
caused by the inﬂuence of negative oxygen ions. Upon analysing the DC self bias voltages, it was concluded
that the presence of higher bias voltage during deposition was directly correlated to the formation of high sheet
resistance peaks opposite the erosion tracks at higher deposition pressure of 5 mTorr. This was further shown
to be not true in case of ITO which is suggestive of a low concentration of energetic ions during the sputtering
process.
Further characterisations revealed that due to the incoherence of plasma formed at low pressures and the presence
of negative ions caused lower deposition of target material (caused by displacement of incident particles from
target) and occasional resputtering at the centre region of AZO and GZO layers. This was partly the case for
ITO where the incoherent plasma was only responsible for the low deposition rate but no resputtering occurred
suggesting presence of negative ions as the reason for resputtering.
Similarly, annealing and acid etching of selected samples gave more evidence of the presence of negative oxygen
ions during deposition and later the higher concentration of atomic oxygen at localised regions of AZO and
GZO thin ﬁlms. This was further supported by the absence of these characteristics from ITO thin ﬁlms.
Hence, the presence of negative oxygen ions during the deposition of AZO is suggested to aﬀect its electrical
properties which hinders its wide adaptation in the semiconductor industry. However more research into the the
spatial chemical composition is needed to fully understand these eﬀects.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
1.1.1
Need for Transparent Conducting Oxides
Transparent conducing oxides (TCO) are thin ﬁlm materials composed of metal oxides used heavily in the
electronic industry, often with thickness in the nanometer (nm) scale. Oxides are generally doped with another
metal which are termed as TCO materials. Due to their favourable optoelectronic and conducting properties,
as well as their transparency in the visible light region, these materials have a wide range of applications.
Applications of TCO range from conducting layers in light emitting diodes (LED), touch enabled display
devices, low emissivity windows, smart windows, and solar cells (photovoltaics).[1][2] LEDs and especially
organic LEDs (OLED) use TCO as an electrode layer on on one or both sides of the pn-junction. Similarly,
touch enabled display devices have two layers of TCO electrically separated by non-conducting dots present
above the LED or OLED display screen with constant electrical charge and a speciﬁc change (by a ﬁnger)
in that electric charge is then is detected as an input.[3] Low emissivity windows consist of glass coated
with layers of TCO that absorb UV and infrared wavelengths, essentially blocking UV and infrared photons
with it’s transmission properties.[4] Smart windows change the light transmission by application of heat and
electricity which is controlled by the TCO layer on the glass surface.[5] Solar cells, similar to LEDs, use TCO
as transparent conducting layers for mainly heterojunction silicon photovoltaics and thin ﬁlm photovoltaics due
to their transparency and good conductivity.[6] Predominantly, the material with the best properties and market
domination in the semiconductor industry is Indium Tin Oxide (ITO) with resistivity in the region of 10-4W푐푚.
1.1.2
Fabrication of Transparent Conducting Oxides
TCO thin ﬁlm layers are made up of groups of grain structures, a grain is a group individual structures arranged
in the same direction, these grains form grain boundaries at the interface between two grains due to the change
in crystal direction arrangement. A crystal is formed of a group of molecules arranged in a deﬁnite pattern.
Electron transport predominantly occurs through these grain boundaries and optical transport through the grains
as illustrated in ﬁgure 1.

The fabrication process of TCOs largely depends on it’s application. There are numerous ways of fabricating
these layers. Chemical vapour deposition where the material reacts on the substrate surface to form a thin ﬁlm

layer, spin coating and solution processing are all chemical methods of deposition.[7][8][9] Physical vapour
deposition (PVD) is another method where the transport of molecules occurs from a target bulk material to a
substrate, where these molecules of the material deposit to form a thin layer. There are numerous methods of
PVD, such as material evaporation where the bulk material is heated in a crucible and the high energy molecules

magnetron sputtering, Radio frequency sputtering, High impulse magnetron sputtering, Reactive ion magnetron
sputtering and Ion assisted magnetron sputtering.[1]
Predominantly, metal oxides are deposited by radio frequency (RF) magnetron sputtering and have proven
to be economical and result in high uniformity over large substrate areas.[11] This method dominates the
semiconductor industry for TCO fabrication additionally due to it being a low temperature process which is not
limit the substrate material selection. This method is further discussed in section 2.1.
1.1.3
Indium Tin Oxide
ITO is the dominant transparent conducting oxide used in the semiconductor industry. The main reason for
it’s popularity is due its excellent material properties with resistivity in the range of 10-4W푐푚and high average
transmittance above 88%. Typically, ITO is composed of 90% Indium (In) and 10% Tin (Sn). The heavy doping
of Tin (at 10%) further increases the charge carrier density which is responsible for the low resistivity of the
material.[1]
While Tin is a relatively abundant element and is used in lower quantities compared with Indium, the demand
for ITO is continually rising especially due to the rise in LED device production from the early 2000s. Since the
main element in ITO is Indium, over 50% of total Indium supply is consumed by the semiconductor industry.
This demand is rising as more applications of ITO emerge. This rise in demand has caused the price of Indium
to increase signiﬁcantly over the years. More importantly, as a rare-earth metal, Indium is a scarce metal at
0.1푝푝푚on the earth’s crust and used heavily in technological applications as solders and soft alloys. Physically,
Indium is a soft ductile material with a low melting point of 156.6°퐶. Naturally, it is present in small quantities
in Zinc Sulphide and is generally isolated as a by-product of Zinc reﬁnement.[12]
1.1.4
Replacing Indium Tin Oxide
Today, the extremely high demand and low recycling of Indium (>1%) makes this highly unsustainable for
continuous use especially to meet the demand for new generation photovoltaics necessary to curb the eﬀects of
climate change. Currently, most of the photovoltaic market is dominated by single junction Silicon photovoltaic
technology which do not use TCO as layers. Single junction silicon photovoltaic devices have however reached
close to it’s theoretical eﬃciency limit determined by Shockley-Queisser Limit.[13] The Shockley-Queisser
limit shows the maximum theoretical eﬃciency that a solar cell with a single p-n junction can have. To pass
belong that limit multiple junctions need to be fabricated on the same plane and new photovoltaic technologies
need to be developed which are highly dependent on TCOs.[14][15]

The role of photovoltaics is crucial for a sustainable transition into renewable energy sources by replacing fossil
fuels. Hence ﬁnding an alternative source of ITO is crucial for advancement in more eﬃcient and sustainably
produced photovoltaic devices.
This case is also true for meeting the ever increasing demand for LEDs.
Additionally, a cost eﬃcient and more abundant composition TCO has the potential for widespread applications
such as low emissivity windows to improve building insulation and, more importantly, in smart technologies,
such as applications in Internet of Things (IOT) devices within optoelectronics, an emerging ﬁeld which is
limited caused by the high cost and rarity of ITO.
From the sustainability perspective, research on alternative TCOs cover Goal 9: Industry, Innovation and In-
frastructure, Goal 11: Sustainable Cities and Communities, Goal 12: Responsible Consumption and Production
and Goal 13: Climate Action of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals. By enabling fabrication of

life cycle, and increasing output by utilising more abundant materials as opposed to rare materials covers Goals
9, 11, 12 and 13 of the UN Sustainability goals.
1.1.5
Problem with Aluminium doped Zinc Oxide
Over the years, many alternatives to ITO have been studied predominantly by doping Zinc Oxide (ZnO) due to
it’s abundance in nature at 132 ppm for Zinc and 49.4% for Oxygen. Tin Oxide (SnO2) has also been studied
but the relative resistivities were much higher compared to ITO. Intrinsically ZnO is an n-type semiconductor
with a band gap of 3.4 eV and has a relatively high resistivity at 1-100W푐푚which occurs due to low oxygen
deﬁciency in the structure. Further n-type doping of ZnO has been studied by adding group III elements like
Gallium (Ga), Indium (In) and Aluminium (Al) with the best results with Gallium and Indium in low quantities
of 5% with resistivity in the region of 10-3W푐푚.[16][17]
Despite Gallium showing promising electrical properties with high light transmittance in the visible light region,
Gallium, like Indium, is also a rare earth element at 5.5푝푝푚and is even more expensive than that of Indium.[12]
Hence, Aluminium as a dopant has been studied as an alternative since the 90s. ZnO doped with Al2O3 (AZO)
typically at 2% to 2.5% Al2O3 concentration lowers the resistivity close to ITO values however, this is limited
to a few locations on the fabricated layer. Whereas, majority of the areas exhibit higher resistivity. Moreover,
the resistivity of AZO is highly sensitive to fabrication parameters.[18]
This eﬀect has been largely suggested to be caused by the accelerated negative ions originating from the target
surface toward the substrate colliding at high energies inducing defects onto the crystal structure. This damage
to the crystal structure introduces oxygen to the deposited region, which further reduces the mobility of electrons
increasing the resistivity at the region. These high energy negative oxygen ions originate with high intensity
at the erosion tracks of the target due to the high energy concentration induced by the magnetron which are
mirrored to the substrate, explaining the heightened resistivity at these regions.[19]

Project Design
The objective of this work is to perform a comparative analysis on the inﬂuence of negative ions in the
optoelectronic properties of AZO, GZO and ITO thin ﬁlm with the aim to ﬁnd the cause of poor electrical
properties of AZO in comparison with ITO.

Thin Film Deposition
RF magnetron sputtering method is the dominant deposition technique for TCO materials in the industry used
with rotating substrates for high uniformity in the ﬁlm and very good target utilisation. This method is used
for its diversity in material selection as this process works well at low temperatures which many substrates are
sensitive to. For spatial analysis of deposition of AZO, the deposition process needs to be non-rotating and
target parallel to the substrate, as depositions made by inclination or rotating the substrate cannot show the
growth mechanism of the ﬁlm and inﬂuence of the erosion tracks on to the sample. Since most multi target
deposition tools employ rotation and inclination to factor for uniform material distribution and negating the
eﬀects of erosion tracks, a non rotating and parallel sputtering tool must be used.
Annealing
Annealing of selected samples is done to analyse the eﬀect of rearrangement of the crystal structure on its
properties at fairly low temperatures. This procedure involves heating the samples in a furnace to allow slow
cooling which regrows the crystals, usually promoting increased crystal sizes.
Accelerated Degeneration Test
This test is to identify the stability of the deposited materials upon exposure to a mild acid simulating the
eﬀects of acid rain and pollution in the air and winds, rudimentary degradation. It is also expected that due to
the complex process of deposition, spatially, diﬀerent characteristics must exist in the crystal structure due to
diﬀerent etching rates depending on the orientation of the crystal.[20]
Characterisation
Sheet resistance is an electrical property measured using a 4 point probe with a spatial resolution of 1mm which
determines the opposing force to the ﬂow of current.
Further, thickness of the deposited layer must be measured to determine the deposited layer thickness. This is
also measured at a spatial resolution of 1mm.
Resistivity of the sample is calculated with sheet resistance and ﬁlm thickness of the deposited sample.
Transmittance measurement measures the amount of light that passes through the sample and additionally
determined the spectral absorption throughout the sample.
The band gap is an important parameter of semiconductors that reveals the characteristics of material interaction
with electron energy states. No energy states can exist in this gap. The band gap compared with other material
properties can reveal more information about the sample.
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) characterisation can reveal the grain structure for the samples that diﬀer
depending on the deposition parameters and further treatment on any given sample.
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is performed on selected samples to reveals its elemental composition
and bonding states.

Method and Materials
2.1
RF Magnetron Plasma Sputtering Deposition
Sputtering is a physical vapour deposition technique which uses a working gas to transport material from the
target to the surface. Simply, a target material acts as the cathode and the substrate the anode. The sputtering
chamber is pumped to vacuum to increase the mean free path of electrons to be able to gain enough energy
between two collisions and ionise the gas. This is done by using high vacuum pumps usually in two stages, ﬁrst
a low vacuum pump reaches low vacuum ( 100푚푇표푟푟) and then a high vacuum pump usually a turbomolecular
pump or diﬀusion pump coupled with the low pressure pump further pumps the pressure to high vacuum
( 10-3푚푇표푟푟) to minimise the reactive molecules of mainly air and water. On the next step, an inert working gas
in introduced, usually Argon, at a constant rate into the chamber which controls the overall pressure of the system
along with a pressure control valve that dictates the ﬂow inside the chamber. An electric ﬁeld is then applied
which creates a high potential between the cathode and the anode resulting in energy transfer to the electrons.
These high energy electrons above a speciﬁc threshold ionise the Argon creating a plasma. The positive ions
are then accelerated toward the cathode and detach particles from the surface (target). These particles are
transported toward the anode and are deposited on the surface (substrate). The mean free path is the distance a
particle has to travel to collide with another particle. In case of sputtering, the mean free path is approximately
0.5푚푚resulting in high particle interaction needed to sustain a plasma and continuous deposition. The pressure
needed is in the range of 1 to 100푚푇표푟푟. In most cases, high ion current densities energy ions toward the
cathode are needed to increase the sputtering yield. This is achieved by placing a permanent magnet behind the
cathode, this magnetic ﬁeld forces electrons to gyrate along the magnetic ﬁeld lines and increase their ionisation
rate. The resultant increase in plasma density can sustain a larger ion ﬂux reaching the surface of the target
resulting in high sputter rate.[11]
Depending on the target material, diﬀerent operation modes are possible. Direct current (DC) can be used
for conducting targets while dielectric targets need radio frequency (RF) bias to ignite the plasma and ensure
ion bombardment on the target.
During RF operation, the high mobility electrons are able to follow the
electromagnetic ﬁeld and gain enough energy as to sustain the plasma. The low-mobility positive ions are not
able to follow completely the RF ﬁeld and this will result in a cathode potential having a DC component. This
DC component translates in a positive sheath that accelerates ions toward the cathode and produce sputtering. In
more detail, at the ﬁrst RF cycle the target is negatively charged causing the ionised working gas to bombard the
target surface and release material from it’s surface. These are still present until the end of the cycle. The second
cycle also attracts electrons toward the target surface ensuring cancellation of positive charge accumulation. The
target material is placed in contact with a copper plate between the toroidal permanent magnets with north pole
on the edges and south pole in the centre. The temperature of the magnetron and target material is controlled
with channels within the copper plates with ﬂowing water to dissipate the heat from the cathode. The presence
of permanent magnets induces a strong ﬁeld near the target surface which traps the electrons to prevent substrate
bombardment enabling quicker deposition. RF magnetron sputtering is the dominant method for the deposition
of transparent conducting oxides. The deposition results in smooth surface without the need for substrate heating
which is especially needed in semiconductor devices that cannot withstand the high processing temperatures.
The ﬁeld switching nature of RF magnetron sputtering makes is popular among semiconductors and insulators.

Annealing of Samples
When a material is deposited by sputtering method, the material grains of certain size and structure or can be
amorphous. Due to the bombarding nature of sputtering, the substrate and the deposited layer structure may
be destroyed in the process. To rearrange the crystal structure, it is essential to heat the sample in a furnace
and then gradually bring back to room temperature. This process regrows the crystal, in essence, arrangement
of molecules takes place once sample reaches gained energy state (molecules have higher tendency to move).

growth, for some materials, enhances their electrical and optical properties caused by this rearrangement in
molecules. The heating takes place in a furnace and depending on the material and substrate varies in the

are surrounded by insulating walls that trap the heat inside the furnace. The chamber is heated through heating
coils around the walls. The substrates are placed on a fused silica tray suspended through one side of the
furnace to avoid local temperature variations. There are provisions for using the furnace in vacuum or in the
presence of speciﬁc gases to make provisions for minor chemical reactions. The furnace used for the annealing
was PEO-604 furnace manufactured by ATV Technologie GMBH located inside the cleanroom facility at DTU

Film Thickness
The thickness of the deposit ﬁlm is crucial for understanding the correlation with the deposition pressure and

the deposition pressure is not uniform and correlated with its electrical properties.[21]
Thickness of thin ﬁlms can be measured by numerous ways, mostly involving the eﬀect of thin ﬁlm interference
for higher accuracy. The tool used to measure the thickness of the deposited samples is based on the same
principle. Filmetrics f20 is a thin ﬁlm analyser manufactured by Filmetrics Inc. In thin ﬁlm interference,
photons interacting with a thin layer cause destructive and constructive interference caused by the combination
of reﬂected light from the surface and reﬂected light from the interface between the thin layer and the thick
substrate. The combination of these wavelengths causes peaks and troughs when observed by a spectrometer.
This eﬀect is visually observed as colourful patterns. Since light is a mixture of photons oscillating at diﬀerent
energies, a spectrometer probes this spectrum of incoming light into its separate energies or wavelengths (휆)
which all have the same speed in any given medium. When these oscillations are in phase (when peaks and
trough align) the resultant wave increases in intensity while the reverse is true for destructive interference where
the oscillations are exactly half a phase shifted. For a waves to cause complete constructive interference, the
thickness of the thin ﬁlm must be quarter the wavelength and for waves to be completely destructive the ﬁlm
must be half the wavelength. This causes colour shifts between diﬀerent thicknesses of thin ﬁlms. The thin ﬁlm
analyser utilises these factors by analysing the recorded spectrum generated by the thin ﬁlm by a light source
kept at a very low angle to the normal allowing the reﬂected light to be directed toward the spectrometer. A
software analyses the spectrum and ﬁts the peaks and troughs to estimate the thickness of the thin ﬁlm. Some
approximate knowledge of the material being measured is needed for accurate detection. The setup diagram
is shown in ﬁgure 5. Filmetrics F20 analyses the spectrum in the visible light and near infrared wavelengths

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) an imaging tool for the micrometer and nanometer scale.

2.7.7
Band Gap
Electrons within any material exists at speciﬁc energy states. The band gap reveals energy states a material
has between the valence band and the conduction band where no electron states can exist. This gap in energy
state is the minimum energy an electron needs to move to the conduction band. In conductors the conduction
band is lower than the valence band which allows the electrons to move freely between states in the material.
Materials with very high band gaps are insulators and those with lower gaps are called semiconductors. A
semiconductor is a material which becomes a conductor or insulator depending on the applied energy this can
be heat (phonon) or light (photon). When electrons at the low energy state (valence band) cross the threshold to
17
==PAGE:25==

Magnetron Plasma Sputtering
reach the next allowed high energy state (conduction band) the material becomes a conductor from an insulator.
The band gap thus is an important property in TCO materials, closely related to its resistivity. Materials can
either have a direct band gap where the valence and conduction bands are at the same momentum planes or an
indirect band gap where the valence and conduction bands are at diﬀerent momentum planes. Direct band gap
materials require only a photon to reach the conduction band while an indirect band requires a photon and a
phonon to reach the lowest conduction state. This property makes direct band gap materials better at emitting
and absorbing light.[1]

2.7.8
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)
XPS is used to characterise the surface properties of a material. In this tool, an x-ray photon generating source is
incident on the surface of the sample which interacts with the atoms and ejects electrons from the atomic shells
called photoelectrons as they are ejected by the incident photon. These ejected photoelectrons have speciﬁc
energies depending on the atom and the atomic shell it was ejected from. This loss in energy from the initial
energy of the incident photon is analysed by an electron analyser which generates a spectrum with intensities
on the y-axis. Each photoelectron loses a speciﬁc amount of energy due to the inelastic collision between the
photon and the electron. These known photoelectron peaks are analysed and the elemental analysis is made.
In addition to individual atoms, this process also shows variations in peaks depending on the bonding made by
these atoms which gives light to the chemical state of the atom. With this spectra, the chemical and elemental
characteristics can be obtained. The interaction volume in XPS is limited to 5푡표10푛푚since electrons ejected
any deeper are absorbed by the material. Since these samples eject electrons, the characterisation must be in
ultra high vacuum ( 10-6푚푇표푟푟) to increase the mean free path of the electrons to enable interaction with the

acid. The etching was carried out after a preliminary etching rate was established with dummy samples. These
tests were conducted to have a rough understanding of the etch rate and due to the destructive method of the test,
and shortage of samples the etching was not repeated which limits the accuracy of the etch rate. For AZO at 5
mTorr, the sample was immersed for 100 seconds and GZO was immersed for 25 seconds. These times were
selected to approximately result in similar amount of removed material from both samples. However this was
not the case with the whole samples immersed, mainly due to the variations in the etching rate of the sample at
diﬀerent positions depending on the crystal structure of the sample surface.
The material removal of AZO sample was higher than approximated by the tests. However, a pattern is clearly
visible from the etched thickness in Figure 20a where more material was etched at regions opposite the erosion
tracks of the target (-10 mm and +10 mm). The other regions had roughly the same etched material. This
eﬀect is more noticeable with GZO etching in Figure 20b. The etched material at the erosion tracks increased
signiﬁcantly with sharp peaks, showing the adverse eﬀect of etching rate due to erosion tracks. The etch rates of
both samples in Figure 20c clearly shows the diﬀerences between AZO and GZO. AZO has a lower etching rate
with slightly higher rates at the erosion tracks. However with GZO, the rate of etching is much higher especially
between the erosion tracks. Conversely, while AZO showed similar etch rate at the edges and the centre, GZO
at the edges had an extremely low etching rate at below 0.2 to 0.5 nm/s compared to 2 to 3 nm/s of AZO at the
edges. The plot for resistivity for AZO was omitted due to it’s erratic characteristics caused by the high material
removal. GZO however showed increase in resistivity at the erosion tracks from approximately 10-2 W.cm to
4x10-3 and 7x10-3 W.cm. Generally, the resistivity increased with etching. The erosion tracks clearly correlate
with the sheet resistance and ultimately the resistivity of the sample suggesting an inﬂuence of erosion tracks
on the sample and elevated etching rate.

Discussion
DC Self Bias Voltage
The overlapping of DC self bias voltage between presputter position and 휙= 60푚푚in case of ITO can be caused
by the inﬂuence of the deposited material present on the surface of the shutter plate and be aﬀected by other
factors. Hence this eﬀect needs to be further studied. Overall, all materials show similarities in the bias voltage
near the substrate surface. The sharp rise while the pressure increases after the dip is present in all materials
and sputtering conditions. This sudden dip between 5 and 4 mTorr pressures explains the improvement in sheet
resistance of the layer as the DC self bias voltage directly aﬀects the concentration of oxygen ions deposited
onto the sample. This increase in deposition is highest at the erosion tracks due to higher energy ions incident
from this region in the target material, depositing at the substrate opposite the erosion tracks depicted in ﬁgure
15.
Resputtered regions
The resputtering had an adverse eﬀect on the resputtered regions with extremely high sheet resistance and poor
optical properties. While this eﬀect was random in nature in case of AZO, for GZO it was more prevalent and
virtually all 3 and 4 mTorr samples had this eﬀect. This eﬀect is represented by the low transmittance spectra
at speciﬁc regions plotted in ﬁgure 17b. The slightly higher range of DC bias observed during the deposition of
GZO from table 2 further explains the higher resputtering in comparison with the deposition of AZO.
Film Thickness
From the results, the dip occurring at 2 mTorr suggests the inﬂuence of the ineﬃcient plasma formation at
pressure causes lower material deposition at 2 mTorr and further eﬀects the sample by resputtering in case of

AZO and GZO. This low material deposition at the centre region is highly minimised at 3 mTorr and higher
pressures, however the resputtering issue remains and is enhanced further. Lower deposited material at the
erosion tracks for 5 mTorr AZO and GZO samples suggests the inﬂuence of negative ions while deposition.
Sheet Resistance
It is suggested that increased sheet resistance at the centre of the samples of AZO deposited at 2 mTorr and
slightly at 3 mTorr is a result of the incoherent shape of the plasma, which occurs at low pressures, is inﬂuenced
by the presence is highly energetic negative ions. A combination of low pressure and presence of energetic
ions causes this resputtering and prevents the target material from deposition at the surface. Additionally, the
humps of sheet resistance formed at 4 and 5 mTorr samples of AZO and GZO are also caused by the high
energy ions originating from the erosion tracks from the surface of the target material even in the presence of
coherent plasma. The lower hump at 2 mTorr GZO sample, correlating with higher thickness at the region
when in comparison with AZO, suggests lower inﬂuence of the negative ions while at 5 mTorr, the energy of
the negative ions originating from the erosion tracks is higher in comparison. These high energy ions are not
present during ITO deposition hence the elevated sheet resistance at the erosion tracks at 4 and 5 mTorr are not
visible. Moreover, the elevated sheet resistance at the centre in case of 2 mTorr and slightly at 3 mTorr suggested
to be caused by the incoherent state of the plasma at these low pressures.
Resistivity
The lack of any peaks in resistivity plots for ITO deposited samples suggests that the negative ions have a lower
impact on the thin ﬁlm growth. Material deposition of ITO at these pressures has very low sensitivity. GZO
had much lower resistivity in comparison to AZO and resulted in smooth distribution for 2, 3 and 4 mTorr. 4
mTorr resistivity of AZO was comparable to the resistivity of GZO at 5 mTorr and overall, the uniformity of
resistivity was not good for AZO at 3 and 4 mTorr unlike GZO depositions.
Transmittance
The transmittance of AZO at 3 mTorr is high an uniform above 88% throughout the sample. This high quality
of transmittance is comparable to the transmittance of GZO however due to the eﬀect of resputtering some
regions have low transmittance caused by the removal of deposited material. GZO was more susceptible to this
eﬀect that AZO but ITO had no eﬀect of resputtering. ITO however has a parabolic distribution through the
sample with higher transmittance at the centre of the sample. This eﬀect can be explained by correlation with
the sample thickness (14c) which is lower at the centre due to plasma incoherence.
Annealing
The eﬀect of annealing showed ﬂattening of sheet resistance for AZO and GZO deposited at 5 mTorr. Annealing
is expected to increase the grain size of the material, which is attributed to the higher transmittance property.
The change in sheet resistance can be attributed by the redistribution of atoms and ﬁlling the defects, during
grain growth, that may be caused by the bombardment of energetic negative oxygen ions into the deposited
ﬁlm These implanted oxygen atoms opposite the erosion tracks can diﬀuse to spread over a larger area, a vital

characteristic of the annealing process. This can further be suggested by observing the lack of change in sheet

Band Gap
The band gap of annealed samples was comparatively lower than their as-deposited constituents. This can be
explained by larger grain size after grain regrowth of the materials since the band gap in the presence of larger

centre of the sample.[32]

erosion tracks, further suggesting the diﬀerence in the chemistry of those regions. GZO showing higher etch

atomic oxygen concentration at those regions.

presence of more oxides for 3 mTorr AZO sample than at 5 mTorr, however on comparison with GZO, which
had lower ratios did not show any diﬀerence between 3 and 5 mTorr samples. Further characterisation needs to
be conducted to determine a correlation. In case of Zn/Al ratio, at 3 mTorr signiﬁcantly higher amount of Al

roughly translates to the ratio from the analysis.[33] However the etching process used to remove contamination
front the surface is known to occasionally change the chemical state of the elements hence further studies need
to be conducted to analyse this eﬀect.

4
Conclusion
This study aimed to study the inﬂuence of negative oxygen ions in the optoelectronic properties of AZO compared
with GZO and ITO thin ﬁlms. These negative ions are reported to occur during the deposition process of the
thin ﬁlm. To study this eﬀect, deposition of these thin ﬁlms were performed onto 50푚푚x 10푚푚Soda Lime
Glass substrates due to its very good optical properties, needed to further characterise these thin ﬁlms. RF
magnetron sputtering method was used to deposit the thin ﬁlms without any rotation of the substrate to maintain
spatial information of the plasma process. The deposition parameters were selected from previous work which
suggested optimised parameters for good properties but these results were highly sensitive the the deposition
pressure. Hence depositions at 2, 3, 4 and 5 mTorr pressures for each material were made while other parameters
were kept constant with power at 30푊, sputter distance (Z) at 35푚푚and shutter plate opening (휙) at 60푚푚.
The deposition process revealed resputtering at low pressure (2, 3 and 4 mTorr) AZO and GZO samples. This
was explained to be due to the combination of incoherent structure of the plasma at low pressures and slightly
higher DC self bias voltage than at ITO which had no resputtering at these pressures. However, the DC bias
at 3 and 4 mTorr pressures have the lowest values, compared to their 5 mTorr constituents, and thus correlates
with lower sheet resistance which is suggested to be caused by the low concentration of negative oxygen ions.
Moreover, at 5 mTorr, AZO and GZO have humps with high sheet resistance opposite the erosion tracks while
not humps are formed in case of ITO. This suggests the high concentration of negative oxygen ions originating
from the erosion tracks due to the high density of the plasma in case of AZO and GZO at and lack of these
negative ions (correlating to much lower DC bias compared to AZO and GZO at 5 mTorr) than in case of ITO
at 5 mTorr. The lack of resputtering regions for AZO and GZO at this pressure while in the presence of oxygen
ions is explained by the high stability of plasma hence stating that resputtering occurs as a combination of both
high concentration of negative ions and incoherent shape of plasma.
2 mTorr samples further suggested that the plume-like shape of the plasma prevented deposition of target
material at the centre region resulting in higher sheet resistance in case of AZO, GZO and ITO. Additionally,
the plume-like shape of the plasma caused a high concentration of negative oxygen ions at the centre resulting
in oxygen deposition, this was correlated with the increased resistivity at the centre for AZO but did not have
an adverse eﬀect for GZO. The much lower thickness for ITO at the centre region can be attributed to only the
presence of incoherent plasma and suggests no inﬂuence of negative oxygen ions. One could argue that the
deposition of target material was higher in for GZO at the centre than AZO. However this eﬀect needs to be
further studied to understand the role of negative oxygen ions at this pressure.
The eﬀect of annealing at 400°C on 5 mTorr samples was further linked to the impact negative oxygen ions
have on the deposition process. Annealing of 5 mTorr AZO and GZO samples revealed ﬂattening of the sheet
resistance humps which was suggested to be caused due to diﬀusion of the highly concentrated atomic oxygen
atoms opposite the erosion tracks, as discussed previously. More evidence to support this claim was by analysis
of sheet resistance of the annealed 5 mTorr ITO sample which had no eﬀect on the annealing process, likely due
to a lack of negative oxygen ions in the sample. It must be noted that while annealed samples for ITO at 2, 3, 4
and 5 mTorr had no change in their sheet resistance, both AZO and GZO annealing processes resulted in erratic
sheet resistance values at 2, 3 and 4 mTorr with only 5 mTorr annealed sample giving any meaningful results,
possibly due to the inﬂuence of resputtered regions. This was not further investigated and should be looked into
to better understand the eﬀect of oxygen concentration over a range of pressures.

The transmittance of the measured materials at 3 mTorr showed high valued for AZO and GZO at above 88%
and 85% respectively. However ITO had a parabolic trend correlating with the thickness of the ﬁlm, this was
attributed to the sample thickness which is lower at the centre due to the incoherent plasma distribution. The
narrowing of band gap from before and after the annealing process, which was the case for all materials was
attributed to the increase in grain size from small grains or even amorphous state of the sample which occurs
during deposition process. Here it was suggested that as the grain size grows, the energy states where electrons
can exist overlap in bulk materials resulting is lower overall band gap.
From the degradation test of 5 mTorr samples by 10% solution acetic acid revealed variations in etch rate with
position. Higher etch rate was found for GZO while both AZO and GZO had highest rates at opposite the
erosion tracks. Upon comparison of sheet resistance for GZO, it was found that the higher etch rates increased
the sheet resistance. More importantly, rate of etching at the erosion tracks can be attributed to it’s diﬀerent
chemistry and suggest that the etching was higher due to higher concentration of atomic oxygen present in those
regions, especially considering that ITO was not etched even when left for longer duration. The higher etch rate
of GZO can also be compared to the higher DC self bias voltage. This however needs further work to completely
understand the mechanism.
XPS analysis showed no signiﬁcant relations for the tested 3 and 5 mTorr samples and annealed 5 mTorr
samples for AZO and GZO. The only relation emerged was that for the 3 mTorr AZO sample, the Zn/Al ratio
was lower which meant that the Aluminium content was roughly at 5% atomic wright. This suggests the better
electrical properties which was shown to increase with increase in Aluminium content in the AZO ﬁlm. More
work however needs to be conducted to completely understand the composition of the materials and numerous
inﬂuences may aﬀect the results.
Looking broadly, characterising the thin ﬁlms revealed numerous properties of those thin ﬁlms that suggest
high inﬂuence of negative oxygen ions during deposition and concentration of atomic oxygen afterwards.
AZO is suggested to be highly sensitive largely to the above stated inﬂuences. GZO however with its better
electrical properties is susceptible to these inﬂuences. But ITO seems to have less of an eﬀect to these inﬂuences
predominantly due to the low concentration of negative ions during the deposition process. To further understand
the eﬀects more work needs to be conducted on spatially derived chemical compositions.
