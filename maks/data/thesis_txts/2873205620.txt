Abstract
This thesis addresses the translational gap between bioinformatics and clinical oncology
by presenting a prototype software application designed to empower clinicians and sci­
entists to independently analyze curated transcriptomic data. Using the investigation of
SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vaccination in Acute Myeloid Leukemia
(AML) as a proof­of­concept, this work demonstrates the application’s utility in streamlin­
ing target discovery.
Methods involved the analysis of bulk RNA sequencing data from TCGA LAML and Leuce­
gene cohorts against healthy GTEx controls, followed by cell­type deconvolution and
single­sample gene set enrichment analysis (ssGSEA) to infer expression correlation with
immune cell populations. Three publicly available single­cell RNA sequencing datasets of
AML bone marrow were integrated and analyzed to assess cell­type­specific expression.
Finally, protein isoforms were aligned to identify conserved regions for in silico peptide­
MHC binding prediction.
The analysis of bulk RNA­seq data revealed that SIGLEC15 is significantly overexpressed
in both AML blood and bone marrow samples compared to healthy tissues. In bone mar­
row samples, SIGLEC15 expression correlated positively with an M2 macrophage signa­
ture and negatively with signatures for M1 macrophages, Tregs, and CD8+ T cells, sug­
gesting its role as a tumor microenvironment antigen (TMA). However, single­cell analysis
showed that SIGLEC15 expression was sparse and not significantly enriched in any spe­
cific malignant or immunosuppressive cell type, highlighting the technical limitations and
complexities of scRNA­seq data. A conserved protein region suitable for vaccine design
was identified across all protein­coding isoforms, though in silico predictions yielded only
weak binders for the HLA­A*01:01 allele.
In conclusion, while bulk transcriptomic data supports SIGLEC15 as a potential immunother­
apeutic target in AML associated with an immunosuppressive phenotype, the inconclusive
single­cell results underscore the need for multi­modal analysis beyond transcriptomics
for robust target validation. The developed application serves as an effective proof­of­
concept for a tool that can accelerate preliminary transcriptomic investigations.

Introduction
Cancer represents an increasing global health challenge, with an estimated 20 million
new diagnoses and 9.7 million deaths worldwide in 2022 alone. Projections indicate that
this burden will grow substantially, reaching over 35 million new cases annually by 2050,
driven by demographic shifts and increasing risk factors[1]. A principal reason for the per­
sistent difficulty in treating cancer lies in its biological complexity, which is rooted in hetero­
geneity, manifesting as inter­patient heterogeneity, where tumors from different patients
are molecularly distinct, and intra­tumor heterogeneity, where a single tumor comprises
a group of genetically diverse subclones. This diversity is a fundamental mechanism of
therapeutic resistance, as treatments may eliminate dominant drug­sensitive cells while
leaving rare, resistant subclones to drive disease relapse, and so to be effective, cancer
therapy must be tailored to the specific patient’s disease[2].
In response to this challenge, precision oncology has emerged, aiming to match treat­
ments to the unique molecular characteristics of an individual’s tumor. This approach
relies on predictive biomarkers, such as specific gene mutations or differences in gene
expression and usage, to divide patients into groups and guide the use of targeted ther­
apies within these groups[2]. The precision oncology framework is built upon a foun­
dation of bioinformatics, the discipline responsible for analyzing the massive and com­
plex datasets generated by high­throughput omics technologies like Next­Generation Se­
quencing (NGS). A typical bioinformatics workflow, such as for RNA­sequencing data,
involves a sophisticated, multi­step pipeline of quality control, alignment, quantification,
and normalization before meaningful biological insights can be extracted. The power
of bioinformatics extends to advanced applications, such as computationally identifying
patient­specific neoantigens from tumor DNA to design personalized cancer vaccines, a
process that is almost entirely computational[3].
Despite its potential, a significant translational gap prevents the routine application of
bioinformatics discoveries in the clinic. This gap comes from a fundamental disconnect
between the worlds of the clinician, who has the biological questions, and the bioinfor­
matician, who has the computational tools. The standard interaction between the two
often becomes a slow, iterative cycle of requests and replies where the clinician is a pas­
sive recipient of reports, slowing down exploratory data interaction that drives discovery.
This is compounded by significant technical barriers, as most bioinformatics tools require
specialized programming skills. This imposes educational barriers amplified by a lack of
cross­training between disciplines and data barriers related to the complexity and security
of patient information. The problem is a lack of tools that empower the domain expert, the
clinician or biologist, to explore data interactively[4][5].
To address this, this thesis presents a prototype software application designed to em­
power clinicians and bench scientists to perform independent analysis of curated bioin­
formatics data. While public web platforms can be used for interactive data exploration
of large public datasets[6], a need remains for easily deployable tools for project­specific
data. Our prototype functions as an interactive interface where a user inputs a gene
and disease of interest. The application then queries a local repository of pre­processed
data and generates a suite of interactive visualizations. By providing these analyses on­
demand through a user­friendly interface, this work serves as a proof­of­concept for a
new class of tools that can place the power of bioinformatics data exploration directly
into the hands of the domain experts best positioned to interpret its clinical relevance,

Theoretical Background
2.1
Acute Myeloid Leukemia
Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML), also known as acute myelogenous leukemia and acute
nonlymphocytic leukemia (ANLL), is a cancer originating in the bone marrow, character­
ized by the rapid and uncontrolled proliferation of abnormal myeloid cells spreading to
the bloodstream and distant tissues. These malignant cells, termed leukemic blasts, are
immature hematopoietic precursors that fail to differentiate into functional, mature blood
cells[7]. This aberrant clonal expansion of myeloid hematopoietic precursors within the
bone marrow disrupts normal hematopoiesis, leading to deficiencies in mature blood cell
populations. A shortage of red blood cells results in anemia, a lack of functional white
blood cells, particularly granulocytes, leads to granulocytopenia and an increased sus­
ceptibility to infections, and insufficient platelets cause thrombocytopenia, predisposing
the patient to bleeding complications[8].
On a global scale, leukemia in all its forms constituted approximately 2.5% of total new
cancer incidence and 3.1% of cancer­related mortality in the year 2020, with AML having
the highest incidence among adults older than 20 years[9]. A multinational study assess­
ing AML incidence reported age­standardized rates ranging from 0.70 to 3.23 cases per
100,000 persons across 27 countries, with projections indicating a significant increase in
the number of new AML diagnoses over the next two decades. Median overall survival
has increased from 4 to 11 months over the last 4 decades[10].
AML is recognized as the most common type of acute leukemia affecting adults and typ­
ically exhibits a rapid clinical progression if left untreated[9]. The disease arises from
acquired genetic variations within hematopoietic stem cell precursors of the myeloid lin­
eage, which instigate neoplastic transformation and drive clonal proliferation[11].
Under homeostasis, hematopoiesis is a tightly regulated system where stem cells give rise
to various progenitor cells that progressively differentiate into all mature blood lineages.
Hematopoietic stem cells can split into the lymphocytic lineage, which gives rise to T­ and
B­cells, or the myeloid lineage, which gives rise to monocytes and granulocytes but also
has the option to become erythrocytes and thrombocytes[12]. In AML, there is a failure at
an early stage of myeloid differentiation, coupled with uncontrolled proliferation of these
abnormal cells. This results not only in the accumulation of malignant, non­functional
blasts but also in the active suppression of normal blood cell development, explaining
the severity and rapid onset of clinical manifestations[13]. The alternative nomenclatures
for AML, such as ”acute myelogenous leukemia” or ”acute nonlymphocytic leukemia”,
reflect the historical evolution of leukemia classification. ”Nonlymphocytic” distinguishes
AML from acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), which arises from lymphoid precursors,
while ”myelogenous” or ”myeloid” specifically pinpoints its origin within the myeloid arm
of hematopoiesis[7].
AML is not a single well­defined condition, but like most other cancers, is an intra­ and
inter­patient heterogeneous disease, exhibiting considerable diversity in clinical presen­
tation, underlying genetic abnormalities, response to therapeutic interventions, and over­
all prognosis[14]. Several molecular factors are associated with this diversity, such as
the chromosomal translocation t(8;21)(q22;q22) resulting in the RUNX1­RUNX1T1 fusion
gene, inversion inv(16)(p13.1q22) or translocation t(16;16)(p13.1;q22) forming CBFB­
MYH11, and the t(15;17)(q22;q12) characteristic of Acute Promyelocytic Leukemia (APL)

2.1.1
AML Pathophysiology
Normal hematopoiesis, the continuous production of all blood cell lineages, is a meticu­
lously organized and hierarchical biological process. It originates from a relatively small
and rare population of hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) that primarily reside within the
specialized microenvironment of the bone marrow. HSCs are defined by the properties
multi­potency and self­renewal. Multi­potency refers to their capacity to differentiate into
all mature blood cell types, including the myeloid lineages (granulocytes, monocytes, ery­
throcytes, and platelets) and the lymphoid lineages (B cells, T cells, and NK cells)[17].
Self­renewal is the ability of HSCs to divide and produce daughter cells that retain the
stem cell identity and functional capacity, thereby maintaining the HSC pool throughout
an organism’s lifespan while also supplying cells for differentiation and blood system re­
plenishment[12].
Under homeostatic conditions, the majority of HSCs exist in a metabolically and prolifer­
atively inactive state, typically residing in the G0 phase of the cell cycle. This quiescence
serves as a protective mechanism, limiting replicative stress, reducing the accumulation
of DNA damage, and preserving the long­term functional integrity of the HSC compart­
ment[12]. The delicate balance between HSC self­renewal, commitment to differentiation,
and maintenance of quiescence is tightly regulated by intrinsic cellular programs like tran­
scriptional networks, epigenetic modifications and extrinsic signals emanating from the
bone marrow microenvironment, often referred to as the ”HSC niche”[18].
The quiescent nature of normal HSCs, while ensuring their long­term maintenance and
protection against genotoxicity, presents a major therapeutic challenge in AML, since
leukemic stem cells (LSCs), the malignant counterparts of HSCs, can also adopt a quies­
cent state[15]. Conventional chemotherapeutic agents often target rapidly dividing cells,
making quiescent LSCs less susceptible to their cytotoxic effects. This property allows
LSCs to evade therapy, persist as minimal residual disease, and subsequently drive dis­
ease relapse, making it an essential mechanism resulting in treatment failure[19].
2.1.2
Myeloid Lineage Development
The differentiation of HSCs into mature myeloid cells follows a hierarchical pathway in­
volving a series of progressively lineage­restricted progenitor cells. An initial split from
the HSC can lead to either a common myeloid progenitor (CMP) or a common lymphoid
progenitor (CLP), committing the cell to one of the two major hematopoietic cell classi­
fications. Within the myeloid lineage, the CMP is the multipotent progenitor that retains
the capacity to generate all myeloid cell types. Further differentiation of CMPs leads to
two more restricted progenitor populations, such as granulocyte­macrophage progenitors
(GMPs, or also myeloblasts) and megakaryocyte­erythroid progenitors (MEPs). GMPs
are the precursors for neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils, and monocytes, the former of
which can further mature into macrophages and dendritic cells. MEPs give rise to ery­
throcytes (red blood cells) and megakaryocytes, the precursors of platelets[20].

The heterogeneity observed among different AML subtypes, which can manifest with pre­
dominantly myeloblastic, monocytic, erythroid, or megakaryoblastic features, likely re­
flects the specific stage of myeloid differentiation at which the transformative oncogenic
events occur and lead to developmental arrest. For instance, AML with monocytic fea­
tures, such as that associated with t(9;11)(p21.3;q23.3), or megakaryoblastic AML, like
that with t(1;22)(p13.3;q13.3), points to a differentiation block at the GMP or MEP lineage
stage, respectively[21]. The newer continuum model of hematopoiesis, suggesting that
lineage biases might be imprinted even at the HSC level, implies that the initial transform­
ing mutations might arise in an HSC already predisposed towards a particular myeloid
fate, thereby influencing the phenotypic characteristics of the ensuing leukemia[22].

The development of AML does not come from a single event but rather a multi­step evo­
lutionary process. It is driven by the sequential accumulation of genetic mutations and
epigenetic alterations within the DNA of HSCs, their early committed progenitors or cells
in the bone marrow niche that regulate the behavior of HSCs. These acquired changes
disrupt cellular functions, including the regulation of cell proliferation, cell cycle control, dif­
ferentiation, apoptosis and survival. Historically, a hypothesis was proposed, suggesting
that one class of mutations typically confers a proliferative or survival advantage, while a
second class impairs normal myeloid differentiation, with the cooperation of these distinct
types of lesions leading to leukemia[23].
Once transformed, pre­LSCs and subsequently LSCs, in possession of genetic and epi­
genetic alterations, gain a survival and proliferative advantage over their normal coun­
terparts. This leads to their clonal expansion within the bone marrow, progressively out­
competing and suppressing normal HSCs and progenitor cells. The consequence of this
malignant clonal dominance is the characteristic bone marrow failure observed in AML,
manifesting as a deficiency of mature, functional blood cells[24].
These massively accumulating blasts often spill over into the peripheral blood, leading
to a high blast count in circulation. Beyond the bone marrow and blood, leukemic blasts
can also infiltrate extramedullary sites, such as the central nervous system (CNS), skin,
gums, lymph nodes, or other organs. These infiltrates can form solid tumors composed of
myeloid blasts, known as myeloid sarcomas (also called granulocytic sarcomas or chloro­
mas)[23].
While the physical ”crowding out” of normal elements by the expanding leukemic mass
plays a role, active mechanisms are also thought to be involved. Leukemic cells can alter
the bone marrow microenvironment by secreting inhibitory cytokines, disrupting normal
cell­cell interactions, or modifying niche cells/components to be less supportive of normal
HSC function and more conducive to leukemic cell survival[25].
2.1.3
Bone Marrow Microenvironment
AML originates in the bone marrow among many specialized cells that collectively make
up the bone marrow microenvironment. This niche is not just a passive scaffold but an
active regulator of HSC behavior, providing essential signals for HSC maintenance, self­
renewal, and differentiation[26]. This niche can be co­opted or remodeled by leukemic
cells to create an environment that supports their survival, quiescence, and resistance
to therapy, which becomes a factor in leukemogenesis and chemoresistance[25]. This
bidirectional interaction between leukemic cells and various cellular components of the
BM niche is crucial for AML initiation, maintenance, and relapse[27].
Mesenchymal stromal cells (MSCs), multipotent cells found within the bone marrow, play a
role in supporting AML. AML cells can induce MSCs to differentiate into osteoprogenitors,
that promote AML progression. AML­MSCs have been shown to exhibit lower apoptosis
and higher proliferation and potency compared to MSCs from healthy controls, suggesting
they can induce and maintain blast proliferation and contribute to a hostile microenviron­
ment. These AML­MSCs secrete factors like CXCL12, which enhances the survival and
proliferation of AML cells, transfer mitochondria to AML blasts, increasing AML cell sur­
vival, and provide a protective microenvironment that confers chemoresistance. There
is also evidence that mutations can occur within MSCs themselves, potentially initiating
leukemogenesis[28].
Osteoblasts, bone­forming cells derived from MSCs, are components of the endosteal
niche. Studies indicate that osteoblasts, particularly those in the process of differentiation,

can protect AML cells from apoptosis, including apoptosis induced by factors like SDF­1
(CXCL12) naturally present in the bone marrow. This protective effect can be mediated
through cell contact­independent mechanisms[29].
The vascular niche, composed of endothelial cells lining blood vessels, is also signifi­
cantly altered in AML. An increased micro­vessel density is often observed in the bone
marrow of AML patients, partly driven by factors like vascular endothelial growth factor
(VEGF). VEGF not only fosters angiogenesis but can also inhibit apoptotic signaling in
AML cells and support their proliferation. AML blasts interact closely with the endothe­
lium, and adhesion molecules such as E­selectin, upregulated on BM vasculature by AML
blasts, can protect leukemic stem cells from chemotherapy by inducing survival pathways.
Intriguingly, some studies suggest that primary AML cells can downregulate hematopoi­
etic markers and differentiate into endothelial­like cells, which are capable of giving rise to
AML upon transplantation, suggesting the vascular endothelium might serve as a reser­

AML blasts can impair T­cell cytotoxic activity, through the expression of inhibitory lig­
ands like PD­L1 on the surface of LSC’s or AML blasts, and promote the expansion of
regulatory T­cells (Tregs), which also suppress anti­tumor immune responses. AML cells
can downregulate surface molecules required for NK cell recognition and release fac­
tors that reduce NK cell cytotoxic activity. Leukemic cells can induce the polarization
of macrophages towards an M2 phenotype (tumor­associated macrophages or TAMs),
which are immunosuppressive and support angiogenesis. Myeloid­derived suppressor
cells, often more abundant in AML patients, contribute to T­cell inactivity and are asso­
ciated with disease progression. AML blasts also exhibit defective antigen presentation,
for example, by downregulating HLA molecules, further helping them hide from immune
surveillance[31].
Various proteins are known to play a role in this alteration of the niche. CXCL12 secreted
by MSCs helps retain AML cells within the protective niche, and active CXCR4 signaling
in AML cells is often associated with chemoresistance and poorer prognosis. Various
interleukins (ILs) are also involved; for instance, IL­1β can promote blast growth while
suppressing normal HSCs, and IL­10 secreted by MSCs or immune suppressor cells can
induce immune tolerance[32].
2.1.4
Markers and Features of Leukemic Stem Cells
Similar to the hierarchical organization of normal hematopoiesis, AML is also considered
a hierarchically structured disease, with a population of LSCs residing at the top of this
malignant cellular pyramid. LSCs represent a relatively rare subpopulation of leukemic
cells that are uniquely endowed with the ability to initiate, maintain, and propagate the
leukemia. This is in stark contrast to the bulk of leukemic blasts, which, although mor­
phologically malignant and capable of proliferation, possess limited long­term proliferative
potential and cannot typically re­initiate the disease upon transplantation[15].
A defining characteristic of LSCs is their extensive capacity for self­renewal, a property
they share with normal HSCs, but is dysregulated in the leukemic context[7]. While nor­
mal HSCs balance self­renewal with differentiation to ensure lifelong homeostatic blood
production[26], LSCs exhibit aberrant and often enhanced self­renewal coupled with a
block in differentiation, leading to the continuous production and accumulation of imma­
ture, non­functional blasts[23].
LSCs are frequently found to be more quiescent (less actively cycling) and inherently
more resistant to conventional chemotherapy compared to the rapidly dividing bulk blast

population[15]. These properties give them their ability to survive therapy, persist as min­
imal residual disease (MRD), and ultimately drive disease relapse. Leukemic blasts, the
progeny of LSCs, constitute the vast majority of the leukemic cell burden. They are char­
acterized by their immature myeloid morphology, expression of myeloid lineage markers,
and their fundamental failure to mature into functional end­stage blood cells[22].
The hierarchical model of AML, with LSCs at its root, means that eradicating the bulk of
leukemic blasts with chemotherapy may lead to a clinical state of complete remission,
where the disease is not detectable by standard morphological examination. However, if
the LSC population is not effectively eliminated, these cells will inevitably drive disease
recurrence[33]. A central mode of modern AML therapeutic strategy is the development
of approaches that can specifically target and eradicate LSCs. The differentiation block
observed in leukemic blasts is often an actively maintained state, driven by specific onco­
genic mutations[34], the classic example being the PML­RARA fusion gene in APL, which
directly causes a block at the promyelocyte stage of differentiation[35]. More recently,
other factors such as the transcription factor HIF2α have been implicated as regulators of
the AML differentiation block[36].
The operational definition of LSCs through xenotransplantation assays highlights the in­
herent difficulty in identifying and targeting these cells in patients based solely on currently
available surface markers. Many LSC­associated markers exhibit heterogeneous expres­
sion and can overlap with the immunophenotype of normal HSCs, complicating efforts to
develop LSC­specific therapies that spare the normal hematopoietic system[37]. The un­
derstanding that LSCs can evolve from earlier pre­LSC stages and that distinct subclones
within the LSC compartment can drive relapse, potentially with newly acquired mutations
and resistance mechanisms, indicates clonal heterogeneity and ongoing evolution within
the LSC population itself, and implies that LSCs present at the time of diagnosis may differ
biologically and genetically from those that drive relapse after therapy[15].
The identification and isolation of LSCs from the heterogeneous AML cell population rely
on a combination of cell surface immunophenotypic markers and distinct molecular char­
acteristics.
These features aim to distinguish LSCs from both normal HSCs and the
bulk leukemic blast population, although this distinction is often imperfect and context­
dependent[38].
One of the earliest described LSC­enriched populations in human AML is characterized by
the CD34+CD38­ immunophenotype[37]. While this marker combination indeed enriches
for LSCs in many AML cases, it is also a hallmark of some normal HSCs, necessitating
the use of additional markers for better discrimination[38].
Several other cell surface antigens have been identified that are preferentially or more
highly expressed on LSCs compared to normal HSCs:
CD123 (IL­3Rα): The alpha chain of the interleukin­3 receptor (also known as CD45RA)
is frequently overexpressed on AML LSCs and often correlates with a more aggressive
disease course and poorer prognosis[39].
CD90 (THY­1): CD90 is a transmembrane protein with an immunoglobulin V domain. The
protein is involved in tumor suppression and is expressed on healthy hematopoietic stem
cells, but under­expressed on LSCs[40].
TIM­3 (T­cell immunoglobulin and mucin domain­3): This immune checkpoint molecule is
expressed on LSCs in various AML subtypes but is largely absent on normal HSCs, mak­
ing it a promising marker for distinguishing LSCs and a potential therapeutic target[41][37].

2.1.5
AML Treatments
For several decades, the treatment for AML, particularly in younger and medically fit pa­
tients, has been intensive induction chemotherapy. The most widely adopted regimen is
commonly referred to as ”7+3,” which consists of a 7­day continuous intravenous infusion
of cytarabine (ara­C) combined with a 3­day course of an anthracycline antibiotic, such
as daunorubicin or idarubicin[24]. The primary objective of this intensive induction phase
is to achieve a complete remission (CR), typically defined by the restoration of normal pe­
ripheral blood counts and a bone marrow aspirate showing less than 5% leukemic blasts,
along with the resolution of any extramedullary disease. Using such regimens, CR rates
are generally in the range of 50­75% in younger adult populations, but these rates are
notably lower in older or less physiologically fit patients[42].
Achieving CR is a first step, but it is not curative in itself. Following successful induc­
tion, consolidation (or post­remission) therapy is essential to eradicate minimal residual
disease (MRD), the small number of leukemic cells that survive induction but are unde­
tectable by standard morphological examination, and thereby prevent relapse and im­
prove long­term survival. Consolidation therapy often involves one or more cycles of fur­
ther chemotherapy and, for younger patients with favorable­risk or intermediate­risk AML
who are not proceeding immediately to hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, consoli­
dation frequently includes high­dose cytarabine[43].
Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation
Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT), which involves the infusion of hematopoi­
etic stem cells from an immunologically matched donor after the patient has received
high­dose chemotherapy, is currently the most potent anti­leukemic therapeutic modality
available. For many AML patients, particularly those with intermediate­risk or adverse­risk
disease who achieve a first CR, or for patients who have relapsed, HSCT offers the best
prospect of long­term disease­free survival and potential cure. The efficacy of HSCT is
attributed to two main components: the intensive conditioning regimen designed to ablate
the patient’s bone marrow and leukemic cells, and an immunologic effect known as the
graft­versus­leukemia (GVL) effect, wherein immune cells from the donor recognize and
eliminate residual host leukemic cells[44].
Newer therapies, like tyrosine kinase inhibitors and antibody­drug conjugates, against
AML variants with specific mutations have been on the rise over the last 10 years, following
a period of considerable stagnation in the development of AML treatments[19].
2.2
The cancer microenvironment
The development of cancer from a small group of malignant cells into a clinically significant
disease is not just a consequence of the cancer cells’ intrinsic properties, but also involves
interactions with the surrounding tumor microenvironment (TME), which comprises a di­
verse set of immune cells, stromal cells, such as fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and various
acellular components like the extracellular matrix and signaling molecules[45][46].
Early neoplastic cell clusters face pressure from the host immune system, and their pro­
gression to cancer is contingent upon their ability to evade these surveillance mecha­
nisms. In this process, the TME is not just a passive bystander but is actively formed by

cancer cells from their inception, transforming into a supportive niche that supports tumor
growth, survival, and tissue invasion, making the TME important in all stages of cancer
pathogenesis. There is considerable heterogeneity observed in TME composition, both
between different tumor types and even within individual tumors[47].
Although AML is not a solid tumor cancer, the microenvironment of the bone marrow,
where the leukemic stem cells reside, is, as explained above, extremely important for
the development of the disease, and particularly the immune cell compartment of this
specialized niche[48].
2.2.1
T regulatory cells
Perhaps the most important immune cell in preventing the transformation of healthy cells
into malignant ones is the T cell, specifically the cytotoxic CD8 T cell. A counterpart to
this highly pro­inflammatory cell type is the regulatory T cell, that represents a distinct and
highly specialized subset of, primarily CD4+, T lymphocytes that maintain immunological
self­tolerance and prevent excessive or misdirected immune responses. They influence
immune homeostasis by suppressing the activation and effector functions of other immune
cells, and their dysregulation is implicated in a variety of pathological conditions, ranging
from autoimmune diseases to cancer[49][50].
Several different ways of classifying regulatory T cell subdivisions exist, but broadly speak­
ing, naturally occurring Tregs can at first be separated into two categories. Thymus­
derived Tregs (tTregs), also referred to as natural Tregs (nTregs), these cells differentiate
within the thymus from CD4 single­positive progenitor subsets, which can be character­
ized as CD25+ Foxp3– or CD25– Foxp3 low. tTregs establish central tolerance by elim­
inating or controlling self­reactive T cells that escape negative selection in the thymus.
Their development is driven by high­avidity interactions with self­antigens presented by
MHC class II molecules on thymic epithelial cells and dendritic cells[51].
Peripherally derived Tregs, sometimes referred to as inducible or adaptive Tregs, are
induced from naive conventional CD4+ T cells (Tconvs) in peripheral lymphoid tissues
and non­lymphoid sites. Their differentiation is typically driven by antigen exposure in the
presence of specific cytokines, most notably Transforming Growth Factor­β (TGF­β), and
sometimes Interleukin­2 (IL­2). pTregs play a significant role in maintaining tolerance to
non­self antigens, such as those derived from commensal microbiota in the gut, dietary
antigens, or fetal antigens during pregnancy[52].
Of unnaturally occurring Tregs, it is worth mentioning the existence of the iTreg, an in­vitro
generated Treg derived from the stimulation of naive T cells with TGF­β and IL­2[51].
That said, the Treg compartment is characterized by considerable heterogeneity in terms
of phenotype, tissue localization, and effector mechanisms, allowing Tregs to adapt their
suppressive functions to specific microenvironments and immunological challenges. For
example, Tregs destined to control immune responses in the gut may express the tran­
scription factors RORγt and the chemokine receptor CCR6, while those residing in visceral
adipose tissue express PPARγ[53].
A distinguishing factor between committed Tregs and conventional T cells, that might
transiently express Treg­associated markers, is their epigenetic signature. The FOXP3
gene locus contains a highly conserved region known as the Treg­Specific Demethylated
Region (TSDR), on which stable demethylation of CpG islands within the TSDR is a hall­
mark of both tTregs and pTregs, reflecting a committed regulatory lineage[54]. This re­
gion typically remains methylated in Tconvs, even if they transiently express Foxp3 upon

investigation, since if pTregs are less stable, they might lose their suppressive function or
even convert into pro­inflammatory effector cells under specific TME pressures[56].
Treg physiology
The fundamental physiological role of Tregs is to act as guardians of immune tolerance,
actively preventing the immune system from attacking self­tissues and thereby averting
autoimmune diseases. They achieve this by maintaining a state of peripheral tolerance,
suppressing the activation and effector functions of self­reactive lymphocytes that may
have escaped central tolerance mechanisms in the thymus. Beyond preventing autoim­
munity, Tregs also modulate the intensity and duration of immune responses to foreign
antigens, thereby preventing excessive inflammation that could damage host tissues[57].
The nature of Tregs in immune homeostasis is illustrated by the consequences of their
absence or dysfunction. Mutations in the FOXP3 gene, the master transcription factor for
Treg development and function, lead to a severe and often fatal autoimmune syndrome
in humans known as IPEX (Immune dysregulation, Polyendocrinopathy, Enteropathy, X­
linked) syndrome. Patients with IPEX suffer from widespread autoimmune attacks against
multiple organs, highlighting the role of Tregs in keeping autoreactive immune cells in
check[58].
This role in preventing autoimmunity is what can lead to challenges in the context of can­
cer. The same potent immunosuppressive mechanisms that protect the body from self­
attack are exploited by tumors to evade immune surveillance and destruction. This dual
nature of Tregs being essential for self­preservation but detrimental regarding malignancy
shows the delicate balance the immune system must maintain, and therapeutic strategies
aimed at enhancing anti­tumor immunity by inhibiting Treg function must be carefully de­
signed to avoid inadvertently triggering autoimmune side effects, a known complication
of several immunotherapies that disrupt immune tolerance[59].
Mechanisms of immunosuppression
Tregs employ a diverse arsenal of mechanisms to exert their suppressive influence on the
immune system. These mechanisms can be broadly categorized into cytokine­mediated
suppression, contact­dependent inhibition and metabolic disruption, often acting in con­
cert to effectively dampen immune responses[53].
Tregs are potent producers of several anti­inflammatory and immunosuppressive cytokines.
IL­10 is a pleiotropic cytokine with powerful anti­inflammatory effects. Tregs secrete IL­10,
which can directly inhibit the activation, proliferation, and cytokine production of effector
T cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells[60].
TGF­β is the major cytokine produced by Tregs that exerts immunosuppressive effects.
It inhibits T cell proliferation, differentiation into effector subtypes, and cytotoxic functions.
TGF­β also plays a role in promoting the differentiation of naive T cells into pTregs, thereby
amplifying the immunoregulation. Tregs can express membrane­bound TGF­β, allowing
for contact­dependent delivery of this suppressive signal to CD8+ T cells and DCs[61].
IL­35 is a more recently characterized inhibitory cytokine belonging to the IL­12 family. It is
selectively produced by Tregs and contributes to their suppressive capacity by inhibiting T
cell proliferation and promoting the conversion of conventional T cells into IL­35­producing
regulatory cells, further propagating immunosuppression[60].
Tregs can also suppress immune responses through direct cell­to­cell interactions, medi­
ated by various surface molecules. Cytotoxic T­Lymphocyte­Associated Protein 4 (CTLA­

LAG­3 is an inhibitory receptor expressed on activated T cells and Tregs. It binds to
MHC class II molecules with high affinity and can also interact with other ligands like L­

potentially by inhibiting DC maturation and function or directly dampening effector T cell

their function[63].
Many Tregs, particularly effector or activated Tregs, express the surface ectoenzymes
CD39 (ENTPD1) and CD73 (NT5E). CD39 hydrolyzes extracellular ATP and ADP, which
are pro­inflammatory danger signals, into AMP. CD73 then converts AMP into adenosine,
that adenosine, when found extracellularly, is a potent immunosuppressive molecule that
signals through A2A receptors expressed on various immune cells, including effector T
cells and DCs, leading to increased intracellular cAMP levels and subsequent inhibition
of their activation and effector functions[64].
Tregs can also influence immune responses by altering the metabolic landscape of the
local environment or by directly killing target cells. Tregs, particularly due to their high
expression of the IL­2 receptor alpha chain (CD25), can act as a ”sink” for IL­2, depriving
effector T cells of this growth and survival factor[65]. In a less specific manner of depriving
other cells of necessary growth components, Tregs can adapt their metabolism to thrive in
nutrient­poor or metabolite­rich environments, such as the TME, where they may prefer­
entially utilize fatty acids or tumor­derived metabolites, potentially outcompeting effector
cells for resources[66].
The multiplicity of these suppressive mechanisms shows the robustness and adaptability
of Treg­mediated immune regulation. This complexity is a challenge for therapeutic inter­
ventions aimed at neutralizing Treg activity. Targeting a single suppressive molecule or
pathway, such as blocking IL­10 or CTLA­4 alone, is insufficient to completely eliminate
Treg function due to actions by other mechanisms. This resilience requires the devel­
opment of more sophisticated therapeutic strategies, potentially involving combinatorial
approaches that can neutralize multiple suppressive arms simultaneously, or target mas­
ter regulators of Treg differentiation, stability, or function, to achieve more effective and
durable anti­tumor immunotherapy[50].
Treg Markers
Accurate identification and characterization of human Tregs are crucial for understanding
their biology and for developing targeted immunotherapies. This relies on a combina­
tion of molecular markers, including transcription factors, cell surface proteins, and their
corresponding gene expression patterns, which are increasingly being resolved at the
single­cell level through techniques like single­cell RNA sequencing (scRNA­seq)[67].

The FOXP3 gene encodes a transcription factor that is widely considered the master
regulator for the development, maintenance of lineage identity, and suppressive function
of Tregs. Its expression is predominantly localized to the nucleus. Continuous and stable
expression of FOXP3 is necessary for Tregs to maintain their characteristic suppressive
phenotype and functional capacity[68].
While FOXP3 is the most specific and indispensable marker for Tregs, its utility in defini­
tively identifying all functional Tregs, especially in humans, has some caveats. FOXP3
expression can be transiently induced at low levels in activated human conventional T
cells upon TCR stimulation and is also expressed by so­called ”furtive Tregs”. These
furtive Tregs, identified through scRNA­seq, express FOXP3, often at lower levels, but
share a significant transcriptional overlap with Tconvs, potentially representing a less sta­
ble or less suppressive Treg state[60]. This complicates the use of FOXP3 as an absolute,
standalone marker for committed Tregs, particularly when assessing populations from in­
flammatory environments or tumors[69].
This means that other markers must be used in combination with FOXP3 to confidently
identify Tregs. Tregs are predominantly found within the CD4+ T cell compartment, so
CD4 is another marker often used. This marker does not distinguish them from other
subsets of CD4 T cells[70].
As mentioned, one of the suppressive effects of Tregs is their ability to sequester IL­2,
and high and constitutive expression of CD25, the alpha chain of the high­affinity IL­2 re­
ceptor, is indeed a classical hallmark of Tregs. CD25 expression can also be upregulated
on activated conventional T cells, necessitating the use of further additional markers for
specificity[71].
Human Tregs are characterized by low to zero expression of CD127, the alpha chain of
the IL­7 receptor. This is in contrast to naive and memory Tconvs, which generally express
higher levels of CD127 for their IL­7­dependent homeostasis and survival. The phenotype
CD4+ CD25 high CD127 low/– is widely accepted and utilized for the enrichment of human
Tregs, as it helps to exclude activated CD25+ Tconvs that might express low levels of
FOXP3, but retain CD127 expression[72].
Several co­stimulatory and co­inhibitory receptors are characteristically expressed on
Tregs and play crucial roles in their function and regulation. Tregs constitutively express
high levels of CTLA­4 (Cytotoxic T­Lymphocyte­Associated Protein 4 CD152), both on the
cell surface and intracellularly[73].
While PD­1 is well­known for its role in T cell exhaustion, it is also expressed on a sig­
nificant fraction of Tregs, particularly those that are activated or reside within tissues and
tumor microenvironments. PD­1 signaling on Tregs can influence their suppressive func­
tion, proliferation, and stability, with some studies suggesting it enhances their activity
while others indicate it may contribute to Treg dysfunction in chronic inflammatory set­
tings[74].
TIGIT is expressed on Tregs, as well as activated T cells and NK cells, and is involved
in their suppressive mechanisms, often by competing with the co­stimulatory receptor
CD226 for ligands such as CD155[75].

Regulatory T cells in cancer environments
Within the complex environment of a developing tumor, Tregs act as architects in the con­
struction of an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. This environment actively
shields cancer cells from immune attack, thereby facilitating tumor growth, progression,
and metastasis. Tregs achieve this by actively migrating to and accumulating within tu­
mors, and once there, by deploying their diverse array of suppressive mechanisms to
neutralize anti­tumor immune responses[79].
The accumulation of Tregs within the TME is a process driven by signals coming from
the tumor and its associated stroma. Tumors actively recruit Tregs through the secretion
of specific chemokines. For instance, studies in pancreatic cancer models have shown
that tumor cells and associated myeloid­derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) can produce
CCL5, which attracts Tregs expressing its receptor, CCR5. Similarly, ovarian tumors have
been observed to secrete CCL28, which recruits CCR10­expressing nTregs, contributing
to their accumulation in the tumor and associated ascitic fluid. Hypoxic conditions, preva­
lent in many solid tumors, can also promote the homing of nTregs to the tumor site[80].
Beyond recruitment, the TME can also foster the in situ differentiation of naive CD4+ T
cells into pTregs. The abundance of cytokines like TGF­β within the TME, often produced
by cancer cells themselves or by other immune cells like TAMs, is a potent inducer of
Foxp3 expression and pTreg differentiation. It has also been reported that Th17 cells,
under certain TME conditions, can convert into Foxp3+ Tregs, further augmenting the
local suppression[56].
Once established within the TME, Tregs deploy their multifaceted suppressive mecha­
nisms to broadly inhibit anti­tumor immune effector cells. CTLs and NK cells are the
primary mediators of direct tumor cell killing, and Tregs potently suppress these through
the release of inhibitory cytokines such as IL­10 and TGF­β, which directly inhibit CTL
and NK proliferation, their production of effector cytokines like IFN­γ and TNF­α, and their
cytolytic capacity[81].
DCs are professional APCs responsible for priming naive T cells and initiating adaptive
anti­tumor immune responses. Tregs can severely compromise DC function by inhibiting
their maturation, leading to reduced expression of co­stimulatory molecules, like CD80
and CD86, necessary for optimal T cell activation. Tregs can reduce the capacity of DCs

to produce pro­inflammatory cytokines that support Th1 and CTL responses, while poten­
tially promoting the production of tolerogenic factors[82].
Tregs can inhibit the function of CD4+ T helper cells, which develop adaptive immunity
and provide help to CTLs and B cells[53]. They can also modulate the phenotype and
function of macrophages, often promoting a shift towards an M2­like, immunosuppressive,
and pro­tumoral polarization[83].
The cumulative impact of Treg­mediated suppression within the TME is the subversion of
anti­tumor immunity. This allows cancer cells to evade immune surveillance and destruc­
tion, leading to uncontrolled tumor growth, progression to more advanced and aggressive
stages, and an increased propensity for metastasis to distant sites[56]. In many human
cancers, a high density of intratumoral Tregs is correlated with poor prognosis, reduced
patient survival, and resistance to conventional therapies as well as immunotherapies[84].
Beyond their well­established roles in immune suppression, there is emerging evidence
suggesting that Tregs may also exert non­immunologic, direct pro­tumor effects, indicated
by the fact that Tregs can promote tumor angiogenesis and may even directly stimulate
tumor cell proliferation and survival through the secretion of certain growth factors[80].
Kynurenine is a metabolite produced from the essential amino acid tryptophan through the
action of enzymes like indoleamine 2,3­dioxygenase 1 (IDO1), tryptophan 2,3­dioxygenase
2 (TDO2), or interleukin­4­induced­1 (IL4I1), which can be expressed by tumor cells or
immune cells like DCs, MDSCs and macrophages[85]. Elevated Kyn levels in the TME
have multifaceted immunosuppressive effects. Kyn can directly inhibit effector T cell pro­
liferation and function. Kyn is a ligand for the Aryl Hydrocarbon Receptor (AhR). Activation
of AhR in T cells by Kyn has been shown to promote the differentiation and enhance the
suppressive function of Foxp3+ Tregs[86].
The depletion of L­arginine in the TME by arginase enzymes, classically expressed by
MDSCs or TAMs, can impair the function of tumor­infiltrating lymphocytes. Some evi­
dence suggests that Tregs may be more resistant to L­arginine deprivation or can even
utilize alternative metabolic pathways, giving them a survival and functional advantage in
such an environment[87].
In essence, the metabolic reprogramming of solid tumors creates a hostile environment for
effector immune cells while often favoring the survival and function of immunosuppressive
cells like Tregs and M2­polarized tumor­associated macrophages[88].
Tregs in AML
A consistent finding across numerous studies is the increased frequency of Tregs in both
the peripheral blood and, more significantly, the bone marrow of patients with newly di­
agnosed or relapsed AML when compared to healthy individuals or patients in remis­
sion[89]. These AML­associated Tregs often exhibit a highly activated phenotype and
possess potent inhibitory activity[90]. The preferential accumulation of Tregs within the
leukemic bone marrow niche implies their active recruitment and/or local expansion, cre­
ating an environment that is conducive to the survival and proliferation of leukemia cells.
Evidence indicates that leukemic cells and other pro­tumor immune cells actively orches­
trate the recruitment and functional modulation of Tregs to establish an immune­privileged
environment[91].
The upregulation of CD200 on the surface of AML cells, interacting with its receptor
CD200R, often found on myeloid cells and some T cells, has been linked to an increase
in Treg numbers and a decrease in memory T cell function[92]. Once enriched in the
AML niche, Tregs directly and indirectly suppress the activity of immune effector cells that

2.2.2
Tumor­associated and M2 macrophages
Macrophages are versatile innate immune cells known for their phagocytic activity and role
in maintaining tissue homeostasis. They originate from monocytes and populate virtually
all tissues as resident macrophages, where they clear pathogens and debris and secrete
signals that orchestrate inflammation and repair[93].
In the context of cancer, macrophages often infiltrate tumors as so­called tumor­associated
macrophages (TAMs), which can profoundly influence tumor progression. Tumor­derived
signals frequently skew TAMs toward an immunosuppressive phenotype that supports
tumor growth, angiogenesis, and metastasis[94]. High densities of macrophages with
pro­tumoral characteristics are often correlated with poorer clinical outcomes and poor
response to immunotherapy in many cancers, marking macrophages as important regu­
lators of the tumor microenvironment and potential therapeutic targets in oncology[95].
M1 vs M2 Macrophage Polarization
Macrophage activation is often described along a spectrum between two polarized phe­
notypes: M1 (classical) and M2 (alternative). M1 macrophages are induced by signals
associated with acute infection or Th1 responses and are characterized by strong pro­
inflammatory and microbicidal activity, secretion of high levels of inflammatory cytokines
and production reactive oxygen/nitrogen species, which gives them effective pathogen­
killing capabilities. They also express surface markers like CD80, CD86, and high MHC
class II, reflecting their role in antigen presentation and T­cell activation[96]. In contrast,
M2 macrophages arise in response to stimuli such as IL­4, IL­13, IL­10, TGF­β, immune
complexes, or helminth infection, conditions associated with Th2 or anti­inflammatory re­
sponses.
M2­polarized macrophages are immunoregulatory and tissue­remodeling in
function, and secrete anti­inflammatory cytokines like IL­10 and TGF­β, express scav­
enger and mannose receptors, and release factors that promote tissue repair and angio­
genesis[97]. Distinct surface markers are associated with each phenotype; for example,
CD86 and MHC­II are elevated on M1, whereas CD163 (hemoglobin scavenger receptor),
CD206 (mannose receptor), and arginase­1 are hallmarks of the M2 state[94].
Functionally, M1 macrophages are considered pro­inflammatory and often tumoricidal,
whereas M2 macrophages are anti­inflammatory, promote wound healing, and in tumors
tend to support tumor growth. It is important to note that these categories are not absolute
and macrophages can exhibit features of both phenotypes, existing on a spectrum rather
than in binary states[98].
Physiology and Functions of M2 Macrophages
M2 macrophages resolve inflammation and repair tissues. Upon activation by IL­4/IL­13
or other Th2­biased signals, M2 cells assume a wound­healing and immunomodulatory
program, giving them high phagocytic capacity for clearing apoptotic cells and the ability
to produce extracellular matrix components, pro­angiogenic factors to support tissue re­
modeling, IL­10 and other anti­inflammatory mediators that mitigate tissue damage and
encourage regeneration. An M2 response is essential during the later stages of inflam­
mation to dampen pro­inflammatory signals and promote repair[96].

In settings like parasite infection or allergic reactions, M2 polarization, sometimes termed
“alternative activation,” is dominant, and IL­4–driven M2 cells can protect against helminth

M2 macrophages are often labeled “pro­resolving” or “wound­healing” macrophages, since
they contribute to angiogenesis and fibrosis by releasing factors like VEGF, TGF­β, and
enzymes for matrix remodeling. While these functions are beneficial for normal heal­

immunosuppressive activities of M2 macrophages that aid in repair can also create a
tumor­friendly microenvironment in cancer[99].
M2 Macrophages in Acute Myeloid Leukemia
Macrophages are significant players in the immunosuppressive bone marrow microen­
vironment of acute myeloid leukemia. In AML patients, the bone marrow shows a shift
toward an M2­polarized macrophage landscape compared to healthy individuals[100].
Leukemia cells actively condition their microenvironment by secreting factors, namely IL­
10, IL­4/IL­13 analogs, or colony­stimulating factors, that polarize incoming or resident
macrophages toward an M2 phenotype. Studies have observed an increased frequency
of CD163+/CD206+ double­positive macrophages in the bone marrow of AML patients
relative to healthy donors. These leukemia­associated macrophages closely resemble
M2 macrophages, exhibiting high IL­10 production and other immunosuppressive fea­
tures[101].
The crosstalk between AML blasts and macrophages creates a feedback loop: AML­
derived cytokines drive macrophage M2­polarization, and in turn the M2 macrophages
secrete growth factors and cytokines that support leukemic cell survival and proliferation
in the marrow niche[102],a process also known to occur in other cancers[103].
Single­cell analyses show that AML­associated macrophages have impaired ability to en­
gulf malignant cells and exhibit metabolic reprogramming, through enhanced fatty­acid
oxidation and mitochondrial activity, typical of M2 cells[100] and likewise, co­culture ex­
periments demonstrate that exposure to M2­polarized macrophages makes AML blasts
more resistant to phagocytosis[101]. M2 macrophages can even transfer mitochondria
to leukemia cells, boosting the oxidative metabolism of the blasts and enhancing their
survival and growth[104].
M2 macrophages in AML have also been implicated in therapy resistance. Their produc­
tion of anti­inflammatory cytokines (IL­10, TGF­β) and enzymes like arginase can sup­
press T­cell function and inhibit effective anti­leukemic immune responses. AML TAMs
release factors that promote angiogenesis and tissue remodeling in the bone marrow,
which may hinder drug penetration or create safe spaces for leukemia stem cells[105].
Preclinical AML models support these observations. For example, in an MLL­AF9 AML
mouse model, macrophages adopted a predominantly M2 phenotype with diminished
killing and phagocytic function, and reprogramming these TAMs toward an M1 state im­
proved anti­leukemia immunity[106]. Blocking macrophage colony­stimulating factor or
related pathways,to prevent TAM recruitment/polarization, has shown reduced leukemia
burden in experimental systems[107].
There is also emerging evidence that AML cells can expand the M2 macrophage pool
via specific cytokines. A study found that AML cells overexpressing Interleukin­34, a
ligand of the M­CSF receptor, led to increased accumulation of M2­like macrophages in
the leukemia microenvironment. These IL­34­driven macrophages exhibited attenuated
phagocytic function and contributed to accelerated AML progression in vivo[108].

Clinically, the presence of M2­polarized macrophages in AML correlates with worse prog­

remission durations and reduced survival. For instance, analyses of AML cohorts have
shown that elevated CD163 expression in the bone marrow correlates with significantly
poorer overall survival, whereas total macrophage abundance, measured through the
pan­macrophage marker CD68, did not have prognostic value[109].
Similarly, CD206 has been identified as a novel prognostic indicator for AML, since pa­
tients with higher CD206+ macrophage infiltrates have markedly lower event­free survival
and overall survival than those with low CD206 levels[110].
M2 macrophages actively contribute to AML progression and resistance, making them an
attractive target for therapeutic intervention. Ongoing research is exploring strategies to
deplete or reprogram TAMs in AML, converting them from an M2 state to an M1 state, in
hopes of improving patient responses to treatment[111].
M1/M2 Dichotomy
While the M1/M2 classification provides a useful framework, it may represent an over­
simplification of macrophage biology. In vivo, macrophages exhibit remarkable plasticity
and a continuum of activation states rather than strictly binary forms. Tumor­associated
macrophages especially can co­express markers of both M1 and M2 or transition between
states depending on microenvironmental cues. The traditional view of “pro­inflammatory
M1 vs. anti­inflammatory M2” does not encompass the overlapping functional proper­
ties and hybrid phenotypes that macrophages can assume[98]. For example, chronic
tumor inflammation might induce macrophages that secrete both pro­inflammatory and
pro­angiogenic factors[112], blurring the M1/M2 distinction. M1 and M2 definitions largely
come from in vitro polarization extremes[113] and actual tissue macrophages often only
partially match these prototypes. Given this complexity, macrophages are often described
as “M1­like” or “M2­like” or classified by the dominant trait, instead of being divided into
two fixed types[98].
Another limitation of the dichotomy is that it doesn’t account for subclasses of M2. M2
macrophages have been subdivided (M2a, M2b, M2c) based on different stimuli and func­
tions[98]. In the context of cancer, the TAM population is quite heterogeneous and not all
TAMs are uniformly M2, and some may perform tumoricidal functions or present antigens
alongside immunosuppressive M2 traits[114].
Overall, the M1/M2 concept is a helpful starting point but fails to capture the full diversity
of macrophage activation states in vivo. Care must then be taken to interpret M1/M2 as
flexible descriptors and use multiple markers and gene signatures to define macrophage
phenotypes[94].
Markers of M2 Macrophages
A panel of surface and molecular markers is commonly used to identify M2­polarized
macrophages in tissues. CD163 is a scavenger receptor for hemoglobin–haptoglobin
complexes, often upregulated in alternatively activated macrophages[115]. High CD163
expression is associated with an anti­inflammatory, M2­like profile and macrophages co­
expressing CD163 and CD206 secrete IL­10 and other suppressive mediators[105]. CD163­
positive macrophages are enriched in tumor settings and have been used as an indicator
of M2­like TAM infiltration[116]. However, CD163 is not entirely specific to M2 and can
also be expressed basally by some tissue macrophages, and can even increase in certain
inflammatory conditions, so it should not be used as a sole marker of M2 polarization[117].
CD206 (mannose receptor, MRC1) is a C­type lectin receptor that binds glycosylated
ligands, involved in phagocytosis and clearance of glycoproteins and is a canonical M2

Arginase­1 (Arg1) is an enzyme of the urea cycle that catalyzes the conversion of L­

but has been suggested to play a role in immune­suppressive cancers by reducing CD8

2.2.3
Myeloid­Derived Suppressor Cells
Myeloid­derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) are a heterogeneous group of immature myeloid
cells characterized by potent immunosuppressive activity, that are observed in particularly
large numbers in settings of chronic inflammation and cancer[124]. These pathologically
activated myeloid cells are broadly subdivided into two major subtypes: monocytic MD­
SCs (mMDSCs) and granulocytic, or polymorphonuclear, MDSCs (gMDSCs)[125].
mMDSCs are phenotypically and morphologically akin to monocytes, whereas gMDSCs
resemble neutrophils, with segmented nuclei and cytoplasmic granules. These cells accu­
mulate in blood, bone marrow, and tumors under the influence of tumor­ and inflammation­
derived factors that derail normal myeloid development. Instead of differentiating into ma­
ture macrophages, dendritic cells, or neutrophils, the myeloid precursors are driven into
an “emergency” granulopoiesis/monocytopoiesis program that yields an excess of imma­
ture, immunosuppressive cells. This aberrant myelopoiesis is promoted by cytokines such
as GM­CSF, G­CSF, IL­6, and others commonly produced in tumors, which arrest myeloid
cells in a partially differentiated state with enhanced suppressor functions[126].
Monocytic MDSCs are CD11b+ CD33+ CD14+ HLA­DR low/– (distinguished from normal
monocytes by their characteristically low HLA­DR expression) and granulocytic MDSCs
are CD11b+ CD33+ CD14– CD15+ (or CD66b+) HLA­DR low/–[127].
gMDSCs tend to be slightly less mature than normal neutrophils. For example, patient­
derived gMDSCs often have fewer cytoplasmic granules and lower expression of certain
maturation markers, such as CD16 and CD62L, compared to neutrophils from healthy indi­

viduals[126]. In density gradients of peripheral blood processing, gMDSCs frequently co­
segregate with mononuclear cells as a population of low­density granulocytes, whereas
normal neutrophils are high­density and pellet separately[128].
A third subtype of MDSCs has been reported, namely the early myeloid­derived sup­
pressor cell (eMDSC). This cell is likely an earlier deviation in the myeloid differentiation
program, before any commitment to the monocytic or granulocytic lineage has been es­
tablished. They are extremely difficult to identify and annotate based on gene expression
alone, due to their high similarity to basophils[129].
Functions and Suppressive Mechanisms
By definition, all subtypes of MDSCs are immunosuppressive, but they deploy similar
mechanisms to dampen immune responses. They inhibit T cell activation and proliferation
through mechanisms that often involve the enzyme inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS),
leading to the production of large amounts of nitric oxide, which is a potent suppressor of
T cell function[130].
mMDSCs also secrete immunosuppressive cytokines like IL­10 and TGF­β, and they can
express immune checkpoint ligands such as PD­L1. gMDSCs, which are usually more
numerous in circulation and tumors, primarily suppress immune cells via the production
of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and Arg1. Arginase­1 depletes L­arginine needed for
T cell proliferation, while ROS, and their downstream product peroxynitrite, can cause T
cell receptor nitration and dysfunction[131].
gMDSCs tend to act in an antigen­specific manner, inducing T­cell tolerance especially
in the periphery[132]. gMDSCs are also a major source of prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) and
other inflammatory mediators, which can reinforce immunosuppression and support tumor
growth. By comparison, mMDSCs produce less ROS and arginase, but more NO and im­
munosuppressive cytokines. Despite these differences, both subtypes can upregulate en­
zymes like Arg1 and iNOS under appropriate stimuli, and both consume key nutrients (L­
arginine, L­cysteine) needed by lymphocytes[133]. mMDSCs rely on STAT1/STAT3 sig­
naling and iNOS activity for suppression, whereas gMDSCs engage STAT3 and are char­
acterized by increased Arg1, NADPH­oxidase­derived ROS, and PGE2 production[134].
Another key difference lies in the fate and localization of these cells.
mMDSCs are
somewhat plastic and can further differentiate once they infiltrate tissues, and within tu­
mor micro­environments, many mMDSCs mature into tumor­associated macrophages
(TAMs), which share many functional traits with suppressive M2­polarized macrophages.
gMDSCs, on the other hand, are closely related to the tumor­associated neutrophils
(TANs) found in cancers. gMDSCs and pro­tumor “N2” neutrophils are sometimes con­
sidered to be part of a continuum of neutrophil activation states, but it remains poorly
understood whether gMDSCs can eventually become fully mature neutrophils or if they
represent a terminal pathological state[135]. What is clear is that the tumor milieu skews
neutrophil development toward a suppressive phenotype. For example, neutrophils from
tumor­bearing hosts show transcriptomic differences from normal neutrophils and often
exhibit immunosuppressive functions[136].
gMDSCs are typically short­lived, just like neutrophils usually are, but they are produced
in large numbers and in many cancers, gMDSCs constitute the majority ( 80%) of the
MDSC pool in tumor tissues. mMDSCs are usually less abundant in situ, due to some
proportion having become macrophages, but they can persist longer in circulation. Both
subtypes express chemokine receptors that facilitate their recruitment to tumors or in­
flamed sites[135].

mMDSCs mirror monocytes that fail to fully mature and instead secrete suppressive fac­

than microbial killing.
Gene Expression Signatures of mMDSCs vs. gMDSCs
MDSCs can be distinguished from their normal myeloid counterparts at the transcrip­
tional level by gene expression patterns. Transcriptomic studies have shown that MD­
SCs possess unique transcriptional programs associated with inflammation and immune
suppression. For example, an analysis of >14,000 single­cell transcriptomes in a breast
cancer model defined an MDSC­specific gene signature that is largely shared between
the two MDSC subtypes but strongly diverges from the profiles of normal monocytes
or neutrophils. This signature reflects an “activated” state characterized by heightened
chemokine responsiveness and immunosuppressive effectors[137].
Both mMDSCs and gMDSCs highly express a core set of genes indicative of immature
myeloid cells under inflammatory stress, notably S100A8 and S100A9, which encode
calprotectin, a damage­associated molecular pattern protein complex. These S100 family
genes are commonly upregulated in MDSCs and often serve as markers for their presence
in tissues. Indeed, transcriptomic analyses of tumors frequently find myeloid clusters
with elevated S100A8/A9 along with ITGAM (CD11b integrin) and other myeloid genes,
corresponding to MDSC populations[138].
Despite a shared core signature, clear differences in gene expression distinguish mono­
cytic and granulocytic MDSCs. Monocytic MDSCs tend to upregulate genes associated
with macrophage/monocyte function and regulatory phenotype. They characteristically
express high levels of NOS2, consistent with their ability to produce NO, and often show
increased expression of anti­inflammatory cytokines such as IL10 and growth factors like
TGFB1. mMDSCs also maintain expression of many monocyte­lineage markers/tran­
scripts, like CD14, CSF1R (M­CSF receptor) and CCR2, while downregulating genes
involved in antigen presentation, for instance, pathologic mMDSCs typically have lower
HLA­DR expression than normal monocytes, which is evident not just at the protein level
but also in reduced HLA class II gene transcripts[126].
Several comparative transcriptome analyses in cancer patients have revealed pathway­
level differences between mMDSCs and gMDSCs.
In colorectal cancer, RNA­seq of
sorted blood MDSC subsets revealed that JAK/STAT signaling pathways were upregu­
lated in gMDSCs, whereas IL­6/STAT3, PI3K­Akt, and TGF­β signaling pathways were
more enriched in mMDSCs, compared to regular monocytes. gMDSCs showed higher
expression of genes related to DNA damage response, cell cycle arrest, and apopto­
sis regulation, aligning with the idea that they experience endoplasmic reticulum stress
and require survival pathways in the tumor environment. mMDSCs have upregulation
of inflammatory and metabolic regulators induced by IL­6 and NF­κB, that support their
immunosuppressive phenotype[139].
An in vitro study of human MDSCs induced by GM­CSF and IL­6 found that only a subset
of cells termed “resistant” MDSCs upregulated a network of stress­tolerance genes, that
included heat shock proteins and anti­apoptotic factors that likely help MDSCs survive
in tumor conditions. Such stress­resistant MDSCs expressed a distinct co­expression of
genes that clearly separated mMDSCs from normal monocytes in multiple datasets[140].
In summary, monocytic MDSCs are transcriptionally skewed toward an immunoregula­
tory monocyte/macrophage program with high NOS2, IL10, and checkpoint molecules,
whereas granulocytic MDSCs adopt a pathologically activated neutrophil program of high

ARG1, S100A8/A9, ROS­related enzymes, and often acute­phase inflammatory media­
tors. Both subtypes share a general “suppressive state” signature distinct from resting
myeloid cells.
MDSC Markers
Experimentally, MDSCs are identified by a combination of surface markers that distinguish
them from mature myeloid cells. As noted, human mMDSCs are defined as CD11b+
CD14+ CD15­ HLA­DR low/­, and human gMDSCs as CD11b+ CD14­ CD15+ CD66b+
HLA­DR low/­. Both subsets also typically express the myeloid marker CD33, but lack
lineage markers of lymphoid or erythroid cells. The HLA­DR low criterion is crucial for
identifying MDSCs, particularly for mMDSCs, because it distinguishes them from ordinary
monocytes, which are HLA­DR+[141].
One recent discovery was lectin­type oxidized LDL receptor 1 (LOX­1) as a specific marker
for human gMDSCs. LOX­1, encoded by OLR1, is virtually absent on neutrophils from
healthy donors, but is expressed on a subset of neutrophil­like cells in cancer patients,
typically 5–50% of circulating or tumor­infiltrating neutrophils, depending on the cancer.
Intriguingly, the LOX­1+ neutrophil fraction in cancer patients was found to be highly im­
munosuppressive, whereas the LOX­1­ neutrophils were not, and LOX­1 positivity ef­
fectively identified the gMDSCs within the broader neutrophil pool. Inducing LOX­1 ex­
pression on normal neutrophils via tumor­derived stress factors was sufficient to confer
suppressive ability, reinforcing that LOX­1 marks a pathological activation state[142].
MDSCs can then be annotated in single­cell workflows by a combination of canonical
myeloid markers (CD11b, CD33), low differentiation markers (HLA­DR), and the presence
of immunosuppressive genes (ARG1, NOS2, IL10, S100­family). The monocytic subset
is recognized by expression of CD14 and cytokines like IL­10, whereas the granulocytic
subset is recognized by CD15/CD66b and neutrophil­related genes like S100A8/A9 and
CXCL8[126].
MDSCs and their subtypes in AML
Patients with AML often exhibit elevated levels of both mMDSCs and gMDSCs in circu­
lation and in the bone marrow, which can impede anti­leukemia immune surveillance.
Several studies have characterized these cells in AML and linked them to disease out­
comes. In particular, high circulating mMDSC counts have been correlated with worse
prognosis[143]. One study noted that AML patients with increased CD14+ HLA­DR low
MDSCs had shorter overall survival and more advanced disease, highlighting this subset
as a negative prognostic indicator[144]. The total burden of MDSCs in the bone marrow
has been linked to residual disease: patients with higher MDSC accumulation in marrow
had higher minimal residual disease (MRD) levels after treatment, suggesting that MDSCs
may hinder complete leukemia eradication[145].
AML cells appear to actively recruit and generate MDSCs, to protect themselves from
immune attack, by secreting various cytokines that can mobilize myeloid progenitors and
skew them toward an MDSC phenotype[146]. AML­derived extracellular vesicles (exo­
somes) have been shown to convert bystander monocytes into suppressive MDSCs. Ex­
osomes shed by AML blasts, enriched in specific palmitoylated proteins, can be taken up
by conventional monocytes and trigger those monocytes to downregulate HLA­DR and
upregulate Arg1 and other suppressive mediators, effectively turning them into mMD­
SCs[147].
The malignant cells induce immunosuppressors which in turn allow them to expand unchecked.
Consistently, MDSCs in AML have been shown to inhibit T cell responses against leukemic
cells. They prevent the proliferation and cytotoxicity of T lymphocytes and NK cells, under­

mining graft­versus­leukemia effects and immune surveillance[144]. Adding MDSC­rich
fractions from AML patients to ex vivo T­cell/AML co­cultures reduces T cell­mediated

MDSCs in AML express high levels of checkpoint ligands such as PD­L1 and VISTA and

Higher MDSC levels have been associated with poorer responses to treatments. In pa­

with elevated peripheral mMDSC frequencies prior to transplant were found to have higher

The accumulation of MDSCs is one mechanism by which AML creates an immune­privileged
niche. By suppressing effector lymphocytes and promoting regulatory T cells, MDSCs
help AML blasts to escape immunosurveillance and even develop resistance to therapies.
Reducing the MDSC burden or inhibiting their suppressive pathways could enhance the
efficacy of AML vaccines, checkpoint inhibitors, and adoptive T­cell therapies by unleash­
ing the patient’s anti­leukemia immune response[151].
2.3
Siglec­15
Siglec­15, Sialic acid binding immunoglobulin­like lectin 15, is a type I transmembrane
protein belonging to the siglec family and the immunoglobulin superfamily. The protein
contains an extracellular domain, a transmembrane helix and a short cytoplasmic tail. The
siglec family of proteins is characterized by possessing an immunoglobulin variable­like
domain (IgV), responsible for the binding of sialic acid, and one or more immunoglobulin
type 2 constant (IgC2) domains. Several of the siglecs contain intracellular immunorecep­
tor tyrosine­based inhibitory motif (ITIM) or immunoreceptor tyrosine­based switch motif
(ITSM) domains that convey an inhibitory signal upon binding a target glycan[152].
Siglec­15 only has a short cytoplasmic tail and does not contain such motifs, and in­
stead pairs up with adapter protein DAP12 or DAP10, mediated by the presence of a
lysine residue on position 272 located in the transmembrane domain. DAP10/12 contains
instead an immunoreceptor tyrosine­based activatory motif (ITAM), giving Siglec­15 the
ability to activate immune cells[153].
The binding to sialic acid occurs in the IgV domain, where an arginine residue at position
143 forms an ionic bond with the carboxylate group of sialic acid. The IgV domain of
Siglec­15 contains an additional beta­strand that increases the surface area of the domain
dramatically[154]. Siglec­15 has only one IgC2 domain which contains an N­linked glycan
on position 172 that is necessary for its immunosuppressive function[155].
An Ig variable­like domain and a single type 2 constant­like domain is a type of struc­
ture common to the B7 family, with which siglec­15 has 20­30% homology. PD­L1, a
well­studied member of the B7 family, has 30% similarity with siglec­15 by sequence
alignment[156].
Orthologs of siglec­15 are found in almost all branches of vertebrates, and siglec­15 is the
only member of the siglec family to show such a level of conservation. This could indicate
that siglec­15 plays an important and conserved role in the regulation of the immune
system[157].

interestingly mostly expressed intracellularly, and on osteoclasts[158]. Osteoclasts are
cells of myeloid origin responsible for bone degradation, and siglec­15 plays a strong role
in the differentiation and maturation of these cells. Mice that have siglec­15 knockout
show mild osteopetrosis and decreased urinary deoxypyrodiniline[159]. Siglec­15 regu­

Siglec­15 has also been identified as a key immuno­regulatory protein in most cancers,

heavily upregulated by INF­γ. M­CSF, on the other hand, promotes the M2 differentiation

Other siglecs, including siglec­8, ­9 and ­14, have been found to be present in solution
either by alternate isoform expression or by ectodomain shedding[165][166][167] and at

2.3.1
Sialylation
Sialic acid is a class of alpha­keto acid sugars with a nine carbon backbone, the most
common of which in humans is N­acetylneuraminic acid (Neu5Ac). This sugar is found
as the terminal moiety connected to galactose through an α­2,3­, α­2,6 or α­2,8 linkage,
which can be found on short O­ or, more commonly, the branched N­linked glycoproteins.
Sialylation also occurs on glycolipids and even small glycosylated RNA’s[169]. Since
sialic acid is relatively exclusive to vertebrates, many cells of the immune system use
sialylation to distinguish between self and non­self, and hyper­sialylation has recently
become a hallmark of cancers[170]. Siglec­15 has been found to interact with sialyl Tn,
a Neu5AC­α2,6­GalNAc glycan structure that is mostly present as short O­linked glycans
on tumor cells. Importantly, siglec­15 recognition of tumor cell­surface glycans can occur
independently of this marker[171].
The binding of siglecs with their glycan ligand is relatively weak, having kD values in the
range of 1 ­ 0.1 mM. Siglecs instead rely on avidity to establish cell­cell connections[172].
Siglecs possess a unique role to bind in cis or trans configurations. A cis binding involves
the interaction with glycans on the surface of the same cell that the siglec is on, whereas
a trans binding is the binding of a glycan positioned on another cell. If a siglec binds to cis
ligands, it is said to be ”masked”, preventing it from interaction with trans ligands[163].

highly specific glycans, the presence of high density glycans, or the interaction of the
siglec framework with the protein context of the glycan[154].
2.3.2
Interaction partners of siglec­15
When siglec­15 was first patented as a potential target in cancer therapeutics, it was
determined by a receptor array assay that siglec­15 binds to LRRC4C and MAG[173].
However, Siglec­15 was first truly reported as a potential target for tumor normalization
immunotherapy by Wang et al. in 2019. Siglec­15 was identified as a direct immunomod­
ulator of T­cells by a T­cell Activation Array (TCAA). Siglec­15 supposedly displays its
immunomodulatory effect by acting as a ligand for an unknown T­cell receptor and deliv­
ering an inhibitory signal[174].
Siglec­15 binds to CD11b on T­cells in a glycan­dependent manner, meaning that removal
of the sialylation of CD11b abolishes the interaction. It is not clear how the engagement of
CD11b would lead to the transduction of an inhibitory signal [154]. Blocking of sialylated
TLR2 co­stimulatory signal by Siglec­15 has also been proposed [175].
CD44, which has a high density of sialylation, has also been shown to interact with Siglec­
15 in a manner that promotes metastasis in hepatocellular carcinoma[176]. CD44 is the
ligand recognized by siglec­15 expressing osteoclasts and might also be a ligand of osteo­
clasts in the microenvironment of osteosarcoma or bone metastases[177]. The bone mi­
croenvironment (BME) is particularly interesting regarding siglec­15, since bone is where
siglec­15 is natively found at its highest expression. It was demonstrated that osteoclast­
derived apoptotic bodies display siglec­15 and dampen anti­tumor T­cell responses of
metastatic bone lesions[178]. Siglec­15 has similarly been found on plasma­circulating
exosomes of radiation treated colorectal carcinoma patients[155].
Siglec­15 was found to bind to EGFR, in a manner dependent on α­2,6 sialylation, through
a pull­down assay. The interaction of siglec­15 with EGFR enhanced the half­life of the
former, resulting in increased migration of a thyroid carcinoma cell line in vitro[179].
Siglec­15 is regulated by the presence of the long non­coding RNA BACH1­IT2. This RNA
molecule acts as a sponge for the miRNA molecule MIR4786 that otherwise destabilizes
the siglec­15 mRNA [180].
Siglec­15 directly interacts with intra­cellular beclin­1, possibly through its short cytoplas­
mic tail, which promotes autophagy and facilitates proliferation of osteosarcoma cells[181].

The role of siglec­15 AML and other cancers

2.4
RNA sequencing
2.4.1
The Transcriptome
Overexpression analyses depend on the detection of a larger abundance of gene prod­
ucts. This can be done by measuring proteins, gene translations, but also RNA molecules,
gene transcripts. The transcriptome represents the complete set of RNA transcripts in a
cell or a population of cells at a specific point in time. It is highly dynamic, reflecting the
actively expressed portion of the genome and providing a snapshot of cellular states, re­
sponses to environmental stimuli, developmental processes, and disease conditions[197].
The central dogma of molecular biology describes the flow of genetic information from
DNA to RNA to protein. However, the transcriptome is a complex and highly regulated
layer of biological information, that besides mRNA, encompasses many non­coding RNAs
(ncRNAs), that include species such as microRNAs (miRNAs) and long non­coding RNAs
(lncRNAs), as well as pre­mRNAs and other RNA types, many of which have important
regulatory or structural roles within the cell[198]. The ability to comprehensively profile
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
28
==PAGE:36==
this diverse repertoire of RNA molecules is required for deciphering cellular function, reg­
ulation, and dysfunction[197].
Before the advent of RNA sequencing (RNA­Seq), transcriptomic analyses primarily re­
lied on methods such as Sanger sequencing of Expressed Sequence Tags (ESTs) and
RNA microarrays. Sanger sequencing is too low throughput to be practical, and microar­
rays require knowledge of the sequences to be interrogated, are susceptible to cross­
hybridization when analyzing highly similar sequences, and exhibit a limited dynamic
range, making it difficult to accurately quantify both lowly and very highly expressed
genes[197].
RNA­Seq, which leverages the power of next­generation sequencing (NGS) technologies,
has revolutionized the field of transcriptomics by offering significantly higher coverage and
greater resolution of the transcriptome’s dynamic nature. Advantages of RNA­Seq are
its ability to provide a more detailed and quantitative view of gene expression, discover
novel transcripts, identify alternatively spliced genes, and detect allele­specific expres­
sion, often without prior knowledge of the transcript sequences. The through­put of NGS
technologies are also vastly superior to its predecessors[199].
2.4.2
Principle of Sequencing
Next­generation sequencing encompasses a selection of technologies that enable the
rapid and massively parallel sequencing of millions to billions of DNA fragments simulta­
neously. This high­throughput capacity has dramatically increased the scale of genomic
studies while reducing the cost per sequenced base compared to traditional Sanger se­
quencing methods[200]. The general NGS workflow, irrespective of the specific platform,
typically involves four fundamental steps: nucleic acid extraction, library preparation, se­
quencing and data analysis[201].
DNA or RNA is isolated from the biological samples. The extracted nucleic acids are
fragmented into smaller pieces, and specialized DNA sequences known as adapters are
ligated to the ends of these fragments. These adapters are needed for amplification and
sequencing. The prepared library is loaded onto a sequencing instrument, for example,
the widely used Illumina sequencer employing sequencing­by­synthesis, or SBS, chem­
istry. During SBS, millions of fragments are sequenced in parallel on a flow cell. The
raw sequencing data is then processed to reconstruct sequences and perform various
downstream analyses[202].
In the context of RNA­Seq, the starting material is RNA. Because most NGS platforms are
designed to sequence DNA, the RNA is first converted into a more stable complemen­
tary DNA (cDNA) molecule through a process called reverse transcription. This cDNA
then serves as the input for the library preparation and subsequent sequencing steps.
The massively parallel nature of NGS is the core technological driver behind RNA­Seq’s
capacity to comprehensively analyze the transcriptome[200].
2.4.3
Bulk RNA Sequencing
Bulk RNA­Seq refers to the application of RNA­Seq to a population of cells, providing an
average transcriptomic profile of that population[197].
The success and reliability of an RNA­Seq experiment are heavily dependent on experi­
mental design. Several factors must be considered at the outset, as these choices can sig­
nificantly influence the quality, interpretability, and statistical power of the resulting data.
Considerations include the use of biological replicates, independently derived samples
from different biological units, and technical replicates, repeated processing of the same
biological sample. Biological replicates are essential for assessing inter­sample variability

29
==PAGE:37==
and ensuring that observed differences are genuinely biological rather than due to ran­
dom chance or technical artifacts. The number of replicates directly impacts the statistical
power to detect true differences in gene expression[203][197].
Another parameter of great importance is the sequencing depth, which refers to the total
number of reads generated per sample. Higher sequencing depth increases the likelihood
of detecting lowly expressed transcripts and provides more accurate quantification. The
required depth depends on the specific research objectives, for instance, detecting subtle
expression changes or discovering rare transcripts necessitates greater depth than ana­
lyzing highly abundant ones, and the desired coverage across the transcriptome, whether
the aim is to analyze only coding regions or also all untranslated regions, also influences
design choices. These parameters must match the biological question being addressed,
as insufficient replication or inadequate sequencing depth can lead to an inability to dis­
cern biologically meaningful changes[197].
RNA isolation
The initial step in any RNA­Seq experiment is the isolation of RNA from the chosen biolog­
ical material, which can range from cell cultures to complex tissues. RNA is an inherently
unstable molecule and degraded RNA can introduce significant biases[197].
During RNA extraction, this issue is further compounded by the pervasive presence of
RNases, enzymes that degrade RNA, which are present in nearly all living materials in­
cluding on human skin. This means that precautions must be taken to prevent RNase
contamination. This includes wearing gloves, using certified RNase­free plasticware and
reagents, treating work surfaces and equipment with RNase­inactivating solutions, and
incorporating RNase inhibitors into lysis buffers. Storing RNA samples appropriately, at
­80°C with RNA stabilization solutions, is also needed[204].
Several methods are commonly employed for RNA extraction, each with its own advan­
tages and disadvantages. The organic extraction method involves homogenization in a
­80°C phenol­containing solution followed by phase separation, where nucleic acids parti­
tion into the aqueous phase, while proteins are denatured and remain in the organic phase
or at the interface. This technique is historically considered a gold standard and can yield
high­purity RNA, but it is labor­intensive, involves the use of hazardous chemicals like
phenol and chloroform, and is not easily amenable to high­throughput processing[205].
The spin­column method utilizes silica­based membranes within spin columns that bind
RNA in the presence of chaotropic salts and RNase inhibitors and after washing steps
to remove contaminants, the purified RNA is eluted. Spin column kits are convenient,
relatively fast, and suitable for processing multiple samples. Using too much starting ma­
terial or incomplete sample homogenization can lead to membrane clogging and potential
contamination with proteins or genomic DNA[205].
In the magnetic bead approach, RNA binds to magnetic particles. These particles can
then be separated from the lysate using an external magnetic field, followed by washing
and elution. Magnetic bead methods are well­suited for automation and high­throughput
applications, are generally rapid, and avoid filter clogging issues. However, highly vis­
cous samples can impede bead migration, and there is a potential risk of magnetic bead
carryover into the final RNA sample[205].
Regardless of the extraction method chosen, a robust DNase treatment step is essen­
tial to eliminate any contaminating genomic DNA (gDNA). gDNA contamination can lead
to the erroneous detection of intronic sequences or an overestimation of gene expres­
sion if reads derived from gDNA are mistaken for cDNA. The quality of the input RNA

directly dictates the quality of the sequencing library and, by this, the reliability of the final
data[199][197].
Library Preparation
Once high­quality RNA is isolated, it must be converted into a library of cDNA fragments
that are compatible with the chosen NGS platform. As mentioned, this is a process de­
signed to transform fragile RNA molecules into more stable, sequenceable DNA. Total
RNA extracted from cells is predominantly composed of ribosomal RNA (rRNA), which
can constitute up to 90% or more of the total RNA mass. Since rRNA is typically not the
focus of most transcriptomic studies, its presence can overwhelm the sequencing results,
consuming sequencing capacity and reducing the detection sensitivity for less abundant
transcripts. Therefore, strategies are employed to either enrich for RNAs of interest or
deplete unwanted rRNA[206].
By utilizing oligo(dT) sequences (short stretches of thymine nucleotides) that are com­
plementary to the poly(A) tails found at the 3’ end of most eukaryotic mRNAs, you can
target only mature, polyadenylated messenger RNAs (mRNAs). The oligo(dT) probes are
typically attached to magnetic beads, allowing for the capture and enrichment of mRNA
molecules. This is a cost­effective approach for focusing on protein­coding gene expres­
sion. It will, however, not capture non­polyadenylated RNAs, such as many lncRNAs,
pre­mRNAs, or histone mRNAs[207].
Ribo­depletion involves the removal of rRNA molecules from the total RNA sample using
probes that are complementary to rRNA sequences. After hybridization, the rRNA­probe
complexes are typically removed, for instance, using enzymatic degradation of the RNA
in DNA:RNA hybrids or bead­based capture of the probes. This method allows for the
analysis of a broader spectrum of the transcriptome, including both polyadenylated and
non­polyadenylated RNAs, but can be more expensive and may not always achieve com­
plete removal of rRNA[208]. For studies focusing on specific RNA populations, such as
small RNAs, alternative strategies like size selection or targeted capture using specific
probes are employed[197].
Following RNA selection or depletion, the RNA is typically fragmented into smaller pieces,
usually a few hundred base pairs in length, which is optimal for most NGS platforms.
Fragmentation can be achieved by enzymatic digestion, chemical hydrolysis, or physical
methods like sonication. Achieving a consistent and appropriate fragment size distribution
is necessary for obtaining uniform coverage across transcripts and for sequencing. The
choice between poly(A) selection and ribo­depletion, and the method of fragmentation,
are decisions that shape the final representation of the transcriptome in the data[209].
Reverse Transcription
The selected and fragmented RNA is then converted into complementary DNA (cDNA)
through the process of reverse transcription. This step is necessary because most high­
throughput sequencing platforms are designed to sequence DNA, not RNA directly, and
RNA is too unstable to be useful. The enzyme reverse transcriptase is used to synthesize
a first strand of cDNA using the RNA molecules as templates[210].
Oligo(dT) primers that bind to the poly(A) tail of mRNAs are suitable if poly(A) selection
was performed or if the focus is solely on polyadenylated transcripts, but they often lead
to a 3’ bias in read coverage, especially if the RNA is partially degraded, as reverse tran­
scription will preferentially start from the 3’ end[204].
Random primers can also be used. These are short oligonucleotides, typically hexamers
or nonamers, with random sequences that can anneal at multiple points along the RNA
molecules. Random priming can provide more uniform coverage across the length of

==PAGE:39==
transcripts and can also prime non­polyadenylated RNAs if they are present. However,
they might also amplify any residual rRNA if depletion was incomplete[204]. Gene­specific
primers are used in targeted RNA­Seq approaches to enrich for specific transcripts of
interest[197].
After first­strand cDNA synthesis, the RNA template is often removed by RNase H diges­
tion, and a second strand of cDNA is synthesized to produce double­stranded cDNA (ds­
DNA) molecules. This dsDNA is more stable and serves as the input for the subsequent
adapter ligation steps. The choice of primers and the efficiency of the reverse transcrip­
tion and second­strand synthesis steps can influence the yield and representativeness of
the cDNA library[204].
Additional Steps
The dsDNA fragments undergo several modifications to prepare them for sequencing.
First, the ends of the dsDNA fragments, which may be overhangs or damaged from frag­
mentation, are ”repaired” to create blunt ends. This typically involves filling in 5’ overhangs
and removing 3’ overhangs using DNA polymerase activity, and then phosphorylating the
5’ ends using a kinase, which is necessary for efficient adapter ligation[201][200].
For some ligation strategies, an adenosine (A) base is added to the 3’ ends of the blunt­
ended fragments using a polymerase lacking proofreading activity. This creates a sin­
gle A­overhang[201]. Short, double­stranded DNA oligonucleotides called sequencing
adapters are then ligated to both ends of the end­repaired cDNA fragments.
These
adapters serve multiple functions: they contain sequences that allow the cDNA fragments
to bind to the surface of the sequencing flow cell, sequences that act as priming sites for
amplification both on the flow cell and during library PCR, and sequences that are used
as priming sites for the sequencing reactions themselves. Adapters often include unique
index sequences known as barcodes, that allow multiple samples to be pooled and se­
quenced in the same run, a method called multiplexing. These indices are later used to
demultiplex the reads, assigning them back to their original samples[201][200].
After adapter ligation, the cDNA fragments are typically size­selected to isolate a library
with a specific, narrow fragment size range. This is important for optimal performance on
the sequencing instrument, particularly for cluster generation on Illumina flow cells. Size
selection can be performed using agarose gel electrophoresis followed by gel extraction,
or more commonly, using magnetic beads that selectively bind DNA fragments of differ­
ent sizes depending on the concentration of polyethylene glycol (PEG) and salt in the
buffer[201][202].
The final step in library preparation is usually PCR amplification of the adapter­ligated,
size­selected cDNA fragments, which increases the quantity of library material to the lev­
els required for sequencing[200]. It is important to use a minimal number of PCR cycles to
avoid amplification bias, where certain fragments are preferentially amplified over others
due to sequence­specific differences in amplification efficiency. Inefficient reactions or
suboptimal purification can lead to low library yield, biases in fragment representation, or
the presence of contaminants, like adapter­ or primer­dimers, that can reduce sequencing
quality and usable data[201][202].
High­Throughput Sequencing
The constructed cDNA library is then loaded onto a high­throughput NGS platform. Illu­
mina sequencing systems are the most widely used for RNA­Seq and employ SBS tech­
nology[197]. In the Illumina workflow, the library fragments, which have adapters ligated
to their ends, bind to complementary oligonucleotides immobilized on the surface of a
glass slide called a flow cell, where each bound fragment is then amplified in situ through

bridge PCR to form a clonal cluster of identical DNA molecules[201]. During the sequenc­
ing reaction, fluorescently labeled reversible terminator nucleotides are iteratively added
to the growing complementary DNA strand, such that after the incorporation of each nu­
cleotide, a laser excites the fluorescent label, and a camera captures the emitted light.
The color of the fluorescence identifies the incorporated base, and the fluorescent label
and a terminator group are then cleaved, allowing the next cycle of nucleotide incorpora­
tion to occur. This process is performed in a massively parallel fashion, with millions to
billions of clusters being sequenced simultaneously across the flow cell[202].
The raw output from the sequencing instrument consists of image files, which are pro­
cessed by the instrument’s control software to perform base calling, converting the fluo­
rescence intensity signals into nucleotide sequences for each read in each cluster. Asso­
ciated with each called base is a quality score, known as a Phred score, which represents
an estimate of the probability that the base call is incorrect. The sequence read and its
corresponding Phred quality scores are then stored in a FASTQ file format. These raw
FASTQ files are the starting point for all subsequent bioinformatic analyses[211].
2.4.4
Data analysis
The raw sequencing reads generated by an NGS instrument are not immediately suitable
for biological interpretation. They must undergo a series of computational processing
steps to convert them into meaningful expression data[212].
Quality Control
As the first step, the raw sequencing reads in FASTQ format should be subjected to quality
control. This step identifies and mitigates potential issues such as poor base quality,
the presence of adapter sequences, PCR artifacts, and general sequencing errors, all of
which can adversely affect downstream analyses[211].
The average phred quality score for each read should be evaluated and reads with low
average scores can be filtered out. Phred quality scores are also examined across the
length of the reads, and typically, per­base sequence quality tends to decrease towards
the 3’ end of reads. Deviations in the proportion of A, C, G, and T bases at each position
can indicate biases or specificity issues[213][214].
The shape of the distribution of GC content across all reads should be consistent with the
organism being studied, where shifts or multiple peaks might indicate contamination[211].
The presence of specific short sequences (k­mers) that occur at unusually high frequen­
cies can indicate adapter contamination or other PCR artifacts[213]. A high percentage of
identical reads can suggest PCR amplification bias, although some level of duplication is
expected, especially with high sequencing depth or for highly expressed genes, and finally
the presence of the adapter sequences needed for sequencing within the reads[211].
FastQC is a very popular tool for generating comprehensive QC reports from FASTQ
files, providing graphical summaries of these and other metrics and allowing for a quick
assessment of data quality[213]. While acceptable thresholds for these metrics can be
specific to the experiment and organism, consistency across samples within the same
experiment is generally expected[213]. Significant outliers may need to be flagged for
further investigation or even discarded if the quality is too poor. Problems identified in this
initial quality control step, such as widespread low­quality bases or high adapter content,
can often be addressed by subsequent trimming steps, but severe quality issues might
point to fundamental problems in library preparation or the sequencing run itself, and the
experiment may have to be repeated[214][211].

Read trimming
Sequencing adapters, which are ligated to cDNA fragments during library preparation,
can be sequenced if the insert fragment is shorter than the read length. These adapter
sequences do not originate from the biological sample and must be removed, as their
presence would interfere with accurate alignment to the reference genome or transcrip­
tome[211][214].
Bases with low Phred quality scores, typically found at the 3’ ends of reads, should be
trimmed, since retaining low­quality bases can increase the number of mismatches during
alignment, leading to incorrect alignments, unmapped reads, or false positives in down­
stream analyses like variant calling or expression quantification[211][214].
Several software tools are commonly used for adapter trimming and quality filtering. Trim­
momatic is a flexible tool that can perform various trimming operations, including adapter
removal, quality­based trimming, and read length filtering[213]. Cutadapt is another ef­
ficient tool at finding and removing adapter sequences from reads[215]. Atria is a more
recent tool noted for its speed and accuracy. It integrates various trimming methods,
including adapter trimming, quality trimming, homopolymer trimming, and read complex­
ity filtration. Atria uses a fast byte­based matching algorithm for adapter detection and
employs decision rules to accurately trim even short adapter sequences while avoiding
over­trimming[216].
Some adapter trimming tools can also automatically detect adapter sequences from the
reads themselves if this information is not provided by the user. This is particularly useful
when working with publicly available datasets where adapter information might be missing
or be incorrectly specified[215].
Incomplete adapter removal can lead to a significant fraction of reads failing to map or
mapping incorrectly, while overly aggressive trimming can result in the loss of valuable
sequence data[211]. Some studies, however, point towards read trimming not being cru­
cial for RNA sequencing, specifically when mapping to gene level[217].
Alingment
After quality control and trimming, the high­quality reads are aligned to a reference se­
quence, which can be a reference genome or a reference transcriptome. The purpose of
alignment is to determine the genomic origin of each read[211]. The primary output of the
alignment process is typically a Sequence Alignment/Map (SAM) file, or its compressed
binary equivalent, the Binary Alignment/Map (BAM) file[214].
In eukaryotic organisms, genes are often interrupted by non­coding regions called in­
trons, which are removed from the pre­messenger RNA (pre­mRNA) during a process
called splicing. The remaining coding regions, called exons, are joined together to form
mature mRNA. A single RNA­Seq read derived from a mature mRNA can span an exon­
exon junction, meaning that parts of the read will map to exonic sequences that are non­
contiguous in the reference genome, potentially separated by thousands of base pairs of
intronic sequence[211].
Standard DNA alignment algorithms, which assume continuous mapping, are not suitable
for RNA­Seq data from eukaryotes because they would fail to map these spliced reads
correctly. Splice­aware aligners are specialized aligners designed to identify exon­exon
junctions and accurately map reads across these splice sites. The accurate detection of
splice junctions is required for correct quantification of gene and transcript expression,
but in particular also for the discovery of novel transcript isoforms and alternative splicing
events[218][211].

A variety of splice­aware alignment tools have been developed, each employing different
algorithms and strategies, but perhaps the two most common are STAR and HISAT2[219].
STAR (Spliced Transcripts Alignment to A Reference) is an RNA­Seq aligner that uses a
sequential maximum mappable seed search in uncompressed suffix arrays, followed by a
seed clustering and stitching procedure. It is known for its high speed, sensitivity, and its
ability to detect canonical and non­canonical splice junctions, as well as chimeric (fusion)
transcripts. Its two­step alignment process first aligns ”seeds” (short exact matches) and
then stitches them together, allowing for the identification of spliced alignments[220].
HISAT2 (Hierarchical Indexing for Spliced Alignment of Transcripts 2) is the successor
to HISAT and TopHat2. It uses a graph­based alignment strategy with a whole­genome
FM­index based on the Burrows­Wheeler Transform and Ferragina­Manzini index, for
anchoring alignments and numerous local FM­indexes for extending these alignments
across potential splice sites[221].
The choice of aligner can influence the results of an RNA­Seq study. Comparative studies
have shown differences in performance, with STAR often cited for its precision, particularly
with challenging samples or for novel junction discovery[219].
While reference­guided alignment is the most common approach, alignment­free methods
also exist. These methods typically quantify expression by counting k­mers shared be­
tween reads and a reference transcriptome, bypassing the explicit alignment step. How­
ever, alignment­free techniques generally underperform compared to reference­guided
methods, especially for quantifying lowly expressed genes, small RNAs, or for discover­
ing novel transcriptional events[214].
The SAM/BAM file format is the standard output for storing read alignments. Each record
in a SAM/BAM file corresponds to a single read or read pair, and contains a bunch of
information about its alignment to the reference sequence, including Identifiers, alignment
properties, read paired, read mapped in proper pair, read unmapped, strand of alignment,
and mapping quality score (probability that alignment is correct)[214].
The percentage of successfully mapped reads and the percentage of uniquely mapped
reads are important indicators of overall data quality and alignment success, because a
low mapping rate might suggest issues such as sample contamination, for example, with
DNA from another species, poor read quality that survived trimming, an inappropriate
or incomplete reference genome/transcriptome, or problems with the alignment parame­
ters[222][213].
Multi­mapping reads, reads that align equally well to multiple locations in the reference
genome or transcriptome, are a problem for most algorithms[211]. This can occur par­
ticularly due to repetitive sequences, but also gene families with highly similar members
or shared domains. How these multi­mapping reads are handled by downstream quan­
tification tools can significantly impact the expression estimates of the affected genes or
transcripts[214].
Quantification of Expression Levels
Following successful alignment, the next step is determining the expression level of ge­
nomic features by quantifying the number of reads that map to them. Expression can,
through sequencing, be quantified at different levels of genomic resolution: gene or tran­
script.
Gene­level quantification aims to produce a single expression value, a raw read count,
for each annotated gene. This is typically achieved by summarizing all reads that overlap
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
35
==PAGE:43==
with the exonic regions of a gene, according to a provided gene annotation file, usually in
GTF or GFF3 format[211].
Gene­level counts provide a general overview of gene activity and are commonly used as
input for standard differential gene expression (DGE) analysis. However, by collapsing
all isoforms of a gene into a single count, this approach inherently masks any changes
occurring at the transcript level[197].
Many eukaryotic genes produce multiple distinct transcript isoforms through mechanisms
like alternative splicing, alternative transcription start sites, or alternative polyadenylation
sites. Quantifying the expression of individual isoforms is often essential for a deeper
understanding of gene function and regulation, as different isoforms can have different,
and even opposing, functions[197].
Transcript­level quantification is considerably more complex than gene­level quantifica­
tion. The primary challenge arises because different isoforms of the same gene often
share a significant number of exons. Consequently, short RNA­Seq reads originating
from these shared exons cannot be uniquely assigned to a single isoform[211].
To address this ambiguity, specialized tools have been developed that employ statistical
models or computationally efficient pseudoalignment strategies. Here, the three tools
RSEM, Salmon and Kallisto will be quickly mentioned.
RSEM (RNA­Seq by Expectation­Maximization) uses an Expectation­Maximization (EM)
algorithm to probabilistically assign reads to isoforms and estimate their abundances
based on the alignment of reads to a reference transcriptome[223].
Salmon is a highly accurate and fast quantification tool. It can operate in either alignment­
based mode, taking SAM/BAM files as input, or, more commonly, in a ”quasi­mapping”
mode, which is significantly faster. Quasi­mapping rapidly determines the set of tran­
scripts each read is compatible with, without performing full base­to­base alignment. Salmon
then uses an EM algorithm or a variational Bayesian inference method to estimate tran­
script abundances. A feature of Salmon is its ability to model and correct for various
sources of technical bias, including fragment GC content bias, sequence­specific biases
at fragment ends, and positional biases, which can substantially improve the accuracy of
abundance estimates[224].
Kallisto is another fast quantification tool that utilizes ”pseudoalignment”. It constructs a
De Brujin graph of the reference transcriptome and uses this to determine the compatibility
of reads, or k­mers from reads, with transcripts. Like Salmon, it uses an EM algorithm to
estimate abundances[225].
The accuracy of transcript­level quantification is dependent on the completeness and cor­
rectness of the reference transcriptome annotation. If novel, unannotated isoforms are
expressed, these tools may misattribute reads or fail to quantify them accurately[211].
The inherent biases in RNA­Seq library preparation, such as GC content bias affecting
fragmentation or PCR efficiency, can also disproportionately affect the quantification of
certain transcripts and tools like Salmon, which explicitly model and correct for such bi­
ases, may offer a significant advantage in obtaining more reliable isoform abundance
estimates[224].
Normalization of Expression Counts
Raw read counts obtained from the quantification step are not directly comparable across
different samples or even between different genes within the same sample. This is due to
various technical factors that can systematically influence the number of reads obtained
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
36
==PAGE:44==
for a given gene or transcript. Normalization is the process of adjusting these raw counts
to account for such technical variability, thereby enabling more accurate and meaningful
comparisons of expression levels[211][214].
Samples sequenced to a greater depth will generally yield more reads for all genes, re­
gardless of their true expression levels. A gene in a deeply sequenced library (large
library size) might appear to be more highly expressed than the same gene in a shallowly
sequenced library, even if its biological expression is identical[226][227]. Longer genes
or transcripts naturally provide more template for fragmentation and sequencing and at
the same molar concentration of RNA, a longer transcript will tend to accumulate more
reads than a shorter one[226][227]. Changes in the expression of a few very highly ex­
pressed genes can affect the proportion of reads available for other genes, even if their
absolute expression levels remain unchanged, because sequencing is a relative sampling
process[226][227].
Normalization strategies are designed to mitigate these biases. Within­sample normal­
ization aims to adjust for differences in feature length when comparing the expression
of different genes within a single sample and between­sample normalization aims to ad­
just for differences in sequencing depth and library composition when comparing the ex­
pression of the same gene across multiple samples[226][227]. Without proper normaliza­
tion, downstream analyses such as differential gene expression detection can be severely
skewed, leading to a high rate of false positives or false negatives[228][229].
Several methods have been developed to normalize raw read counts, with RPKM, FPKM,
and TPM being among the most common for providing expression values that account for
both library size and feature length[226].
RPKM (Reads Per Kilobase of transcript, per Million mapped reads) is a metric developed
for single­end RNA­Seq. It normalizes the read count for a gene by its length (in kilobases)
and then by the total number of mapped reads in the library (in millions). The calculation
involves dividing the number of reads mapped to a gene by the length of the gene in
kilobases, and then dividing this value by the total number of mapped reads in the sample
(scaled to millions)[197][229].
FPKM (Fragments Per Kilobase of transcript, per Million mapped fragments) is analogous
to RPKM but is designed for paired­end RNA­Seq experiments. In paired­end sequenc­
ing, two reads are generated from each cDNA fragment. FPKM counts ”fragments”, a
pair of reads originating from the same cDNA molecule, rather than individual reads. For
single­end RNA­Seq data, FPKM is equivalent to RPKM. The calculation is similar to
RPKM, but uses the number of fragments mapped to a gene and the total number of
mapped fragments in the sample[197][229].
TPM (Transcripts Per Million) is another method that normalizes for both gene length and
sequencing depth. The main difference in its calculation compared to RPKM/FPKM is
the order of operations and the scaling factor. TPM first normalizes for gene length by
dividing read counts by gene length in kilobases to get ”reads per kilobase” or RPK and
then normalizes for sequencing depth by dividing each gene’s RPK value by the sum of
all RPK values in that sample, and then scaling this proportion by one million[229]. A
defining characteristic of TPM is that the sum of all TPM values in each sample is the
same (one million). This makes TPM values more directly comparable across samples in
terms of relative transcript abundance[229].
While RPKM and FPKM are useful for comparing the expression of different genes within a
single sample as they account for gene length, they can be problematic for direct compar­
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
37
==PAGE:45==
isons of a gene’s expression across different samples, because the sum of RPKM/FPKM
values can vary between samples, meaning that the proportion of the library dedicated to a
particular gene can be influenced by the expression levels of other genes[230][228]. TPM
addresses this issue because the sum of TPMs is constant across all samples. There­
fore, TPM is generally considered a more stable and comparable measure of the relative
abundance of transcripts across samples[229].
It is important to note, however, that for differential expression analysis, statistical meth­
ods that work directly with raw read counts (DESeq2, edgeR) are often preferred over
analyses performed on RPKM, FPKM, or TPM values. These count­based statistical
models incorporate normalization factors internally, typically based on library size and
composition, and are better equipped to handle the discrete nature and specific variance
properties of count data[231].
2.4.5
Deconvolution of Bulk RNA Sequencing Data
Reguar, or ”bulk”, RNA­seq provides an aggregate measure of gene expression from all
cells within a sample. While this approach is powerful for studying overall transcriptomic
changes, it by definition hides the contributions of individual cell types, which is required
for understanding complex biological systems like the tumor microenvironment or immune
responses[232]. Cell type deconvolution refers to computational methods that aim to infer
the relative proportions of different cell types within a bulk tissue sample based on its gene
expression profile[233].
Most reference­based deconvolution methods require two main inputs. The bulk gene ex­
pression matrix of the mixed samples and a signature matrix that defines the characteristic
gene expression profiles of the cell types of interest[233]. The accuracy of these methods
is highly dependent on the quality and completeness of this reference signature matrix,
and if cell types present in the bulk sample are missing from the reference, deconvolution
accuracy can be compromised[234].
The immunedeconv R package is a tool that provides various computational methods for
estimating immune cell fractions from bulk RNA­seq data. Developed to simplify com­
parative analyses, which can be complex due to differing dependencies and input/output
formats of individual tools, immunedeconv standardizes these aspects and offers a com­
mon nomenclature for quantified cell types[235].
One of the methods accessible through immunedeconv is quanTIseq, a deconvolution
algorithm specifically designed to quantify the fractions of ten major immune cell types
from bulk RNA­seq data in the context of cancer[236].
quanTIseq employs a constrained least squares regression approach to estimate cell frac­
tions. The method takes a gene expression matrix from bulk samples (M) and a signature
matrix (S) defining the expression profiles of target cell types as input. It solves the equa­
tion M≈S×F for F, the matrix of cell fractions, subject to constraints that cell fractions must
be non­negative and their sum must not exceed one, meaning that quanTIseq is allowed
to estimate the proportion of uncharacterized cells, such as those present in the mixture
but not represented in the signature matrix, which could very well be tumor cells due to
their inherently high heterogeneity[236].
QuanTIseq uses a signature matrix known as TIL10 (Tumor Infiltrating Leukocyte 10) that
was derived from RNA­seq data of sorted human immune cells and comprises signa­
ture genes for B cells, M1 and M2 macrophages, monocytes, neutrophils, NK cells, non­
regulatory CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, regulatory T cells (Tregs), and myeloid dendritic
cells. The derivation of TIL10 involved selecting genes with high cell­specific expression
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
38
==PAGE:46==
in immune cells and low expression in other cell types, filtering out genes highly expressed
in cancer cell lines to avoid confounding by tumor cells, and ensuring markers for one cell
type were not strongly associated with another using a compendium of gene sets[236].
For Treg cells, which are often in low abundance and whose signature can overlap with
other CD4+ T cells, quanTIseq employs a specific heuristic strategy involving two rounds
of estimation to improve accuracy. The estimated fractions are also normalized by cell­
type­specific scaling factors to account for differences in mRNA content per cell[236].
QuanTIseq’s approach of allowing the sum of the modeled immune cell fractions to be
less than one, with the remainder attributed to uncharacterized cells, provides more bio­
logically realistic estimates for the immune components and a useful proxy for tumor purity
or the proportion of other non­modeled cells. Despite this, some research suggests that
quanTIseq and deconvolution methods in general overestimate the proportion of immune
cells[237].
2.4.6
Single Sample Gene Set Enrichment Analysis
Single Sample Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (ssGSEA) is an extension of the widely
used Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) method[238], first described by Barbie et al.
2009[239]. ssGSEA calculates an enrichment score for each pairing of a sample and a
pre­defined gene set. For a given sample, genes are ranked based on their absolute ex­
pression values from highest to lowest, and the enrichment score for a specific gene set is
then calculated by integrating the difference between the empirical cumulative distribution
functions (ECDFs) of the genes within the gene set and the genes outside the gene set,
as they appear in the ranked list. This score reflects the degree to which the genes in
that particular gene set are coordinately up­ or down­regulated within that single sample,
essentially representing the activity level of the biological process or pathway defined by
the gene set[239].
An important feature of ssGSEA is its ability to analyze each sample individually, without
requiring comparisons across groups of samples, which is typical for standard GSEA[238].
The use of gene ranks makes the method relatively robust to data normalization strategies
and assumptions about data distribution[240].
While ssGSEA was initially developed for assessing pathway activity[239], its framework
can be adapted to estimate the relative abundance or activity of different cell types in bulk
RNA­seq samples. This is achieved by using cell­type­specific gene signatures, lists of
genes known to be predominantly or specifically expressed in a particular cell type, as the
input ”gene sets”. A higher ssGSEA enrichment score for a given cell type’s signature in
a sample suggests a greater prevalence or higher transcriptional activity of that cell type
within that sample[241]. Other studies have used ssGSEA to derive scores for various
immune cell subsets or for gene sets representing specific biological states, interpreting
these scores as indicators of enrichment or activity[242].
Unlike methods such as quanTIseq that employ regression models to explicitly estimate
the proportions of different cellular components that sum to a whole[236], ssGSEA’s cal­
culation is based on the rank distribution of signature genes within each sample’s overall
gene expression profile. It does not inherently model a mixture of cells or attempt to par­
tition the bulk signal into fractional components that must sum to one. Consequently, an
ssGSEA score for ”T­cells” indicates the strength of the T­cell signature in that sample rel­
ative to other genes, which can be a proxy for T­cell abundance or activity, but it doesn’t
directly state that ”X% of cells are T­cells”. This makes ssGSEA a tool for assessing the
relative presence or functional state of cell types on a sample basis[243].

2.5
Single­cell RNA sequencing
While bulk RNA­Seq provides valuable insights into the average transcriptomic state of a
cell population, when pooling all reads together, the diversity and heterogeneity that often
exists among individual cells within that population become masked. Single­cell RNA
sequencing (scRNA­Seq) has emerged to address this limitation[244].
Biological tissues and cell populations are rarely homogeneous, but instead composed
of diverse cell types and subtypes, each with distinct molecular profiles and functional
roles. Even cells of the same nominal type can exist in different states, such as different
phases of the cell cycle, varying levels of activation, or under transient responses to stim­
uli. Bulk RNA­Seq, by averaging the transcriptomes of all cells in a sample, obscures this
cell­to­cell variability and this averaging effect leads to the loss of information about cell
populations, transcriptional differences between cell states, or the dynamics of cellular
processes over time[244].
Single­cell RNA sequencing overcomes these limitations by enabling the transcriptional
profiling of individual cells, one cell at a time. By analyzing the transcriptome of each cell
independently, scRNA­Seq allows one to identify and characterize known and novel cell
types and subtypes within a heterogeneous population, map developmental trajectories
and differentiation pathways by ordering cells along a continuum of states, investigate
the stochastic nature of gene expression and its contribution to phenotypic variability and
deconstruct complex microenvironments such as those found in tumors, by identifying the
different cell types present and their interactions[245].
The core differences between bulk RNA­Seq and scRNA­Seq lie in their experimental
goals, the resolution of the data they generate, and consequently, the types of biological
questions they can address[246].
The goal of bulk RNA sequencing is to obtain an average expression profile from a pop­
ulation of cells, which can be used to identify general trends between conditions, and
typically yields a robust representation of the average transcriptome, with a relatively high
number of genes detected per sample and lower technical noise compared to scRNA­
Seq. Single­cell RNA sequencing identifies the expression profile for each individual cell
and is ideal for studying cellular heterogeneity, identifying rare cell populations, defining
cell states, plotting developmental lineages, and understanding cell­specific functions and
interactions within a mixed population[246][247].
scRNA­Seq data is characterized by high sparsity, meaning that for any given cell, many
genes will have zero detected transcripts, often referred to as ”dropouts”. This is due to
the very low starting amounts of RNA per cell (picograms) and the stochastic nature of
mRNA capture and reverse transcription at this scale. The data is also typically noisier
than bulk RNA­Seq data, with greater technical variability between cells and samples.
While scRNA­Seq can profile many individual cells, the number of genes detected per
cell is lower than the total number of genes detected in a bulk sample[248][247].
The choice between bulk and scRNA­Seq is therefore dictated by the specific research
question. If the primary interest lies in understanding overall changes in a relatively homo­
geneous cell population, bulk RNA­Seq may be sufficient and more cost­effective. How­
ever, if the goal is to dissect cellular heterogeneity, identify distinct cell types or states, or
study rare cells, scRNA­Seq is the choice, despite its higher per­sample cost and greater
data complexity[249][250][247].

2.5.1
Experimental Strategies for Single­cell RNA sequencing
A fundamental requirement for scRNA­Seq is the effective isolation of individual cells or
nuclei, followed by separate library preparation for each cell, or the barcoding of transcripts
from each cell, before sequencing[251].
One method is Fluorescence­activated cell sorting (FACS), where cells are labeled with
fluorescent antibodies against cell surface markers or with viability dyes and a FACS in­
strument then hydrodynamically focuses a stream of cells, shoots each cell with lasers,
and sorts individual cells into separate collection vessels based on their fluorescence
properties. FACS allows for the selection of specific cell populations of interest but can
be harsh on cells, potentially altering their transcriptomes, and is generally lower through­
put for scRNA­Seq library preparation compared to droplet methods[252].
Microfluidic droplet methods utilize microfluidic chips to capture single cells, or a cell and a
uniquely barcoded bead, within tiny aqueous droplets suspended in an oil emulsion. This
process allows for the high­throughput partitioning of thousands to tens of thousands of
cells into individual reaction compartments. Prominent examples include the 10x Ge­
nomics Chromium system, Drop­seq, and inDrop[232].
Microwell­based methods use chips containing thousands of nanowells, where cells are
loaded onto the chip at a limiting dilution such that most wells contain at most one cell[232].
The choice of isolation method depends on factors such as the starting material, the num­
ber of cells to be profiled, the need to select for specific cell types, and available resources.
Droplet­based methods, in particular, enables massively parallel single­cell analysis[248].
In plate­based scRNA­Seq protocols, individual cells are typically sorted by FACS or man­
ual picking into the wells of multi­well plates. Each well then serves as an individual re­
action chamber for cell lysis, reverse transcription, and cDNA amplification[253].
SMART­seq2 (Switching Mechanism At 5’ end of RNA Template 2) is one of the most
popular and well­established plate­based methods. SMART­seq2 is designed to produce
cDNA that covers the full length of the transcript. This is achieved through a template­
switching mechanism during reverse transcription. When the reverse transcriptase reaches
the 5’ end of the mRNA template, it adds a few non­templated nucleotides (preferen­
tially cytosines). A template­switching oligonucleotide (TSO), which has a complementary
oligo(G) sequence at its 3’ end, then anneals to these non­templated bases and serves
as a new template for the reverse transcriptase to continue synthesis[254][255].
Methods like SMART­seq2 generally exhibit high sensitivity, meaning they can detect a
larger number of genes per cell compared to many droplet­based methods[255]. The
full­length transcript information obtained from SMART­seq2 is particularly valuable for
studying alternative splicing, identifying single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in ex­
pressed transcripts, and analyzing allele­specific expression[248].
However, plate­based protocols also have limitations. They are typically limited to pro­
cessing hundreds of cells in parallel, which is significantly less than droplet­based meth­
ods, and the reagents, work and time involved in processing each cell individually in sep­
arate wells make these methods more expensive on a per­cell basis[255].
Droplet­based scRNA­Seq technologies have enabled a massive increase in the scale of
single­cell transcriptomic studies. These methods use microfluidic devices to co­encapsulate
single cells with uniquely barcoded beads and lysis/RT reagents within picoliter­volume
aqueous droplets in an oil emulsion. Each bead carries a large number of oligonucleotide
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
41
==PAGE:49==
primers that contain a cell barcode, a unique molecular identifier (UMI), a poly(T) se­
quence and a PCR handle[256].
The cell barcode is a unique sequence that identifies the droplet and thus the cell, as­
suming one cell per droplet. All primers on a given bead share the same cell barcode
and the UMI provides an additional layer of resolution through a short random sequence
that uniquely tags each individual mRNA molecule captured from that cell before PCR
amplification. The poly(T) sequence captures polyadenylated mRNAs by annealing and
the PCR handle ensures amplification[256][253].
Inside each droplet, the cell is lysed, releasing its mRNA. These mRNAs are captured by
the poly(T) sequences on the primers of the co­encapsulated bead and are reverse tran­
scribed into cDNA. During reverse transcription, the cell barcode and UMI from the primer
are incorporated into the first cDNA strand, meaning that all cDNA molecules originating
from the same cell will share the same cell barcode, and each original mRNA molecule
will have a unique UMI[257].
After the in­droplet reactions, the emulsion is broken, and the barcoded cDNAs from all
droplets, representing thousands of cells, are pooled together for subsequent steps, in­
cluding PCR amplification, library construction, and sequencing[258]. The cell barcodes
allow reads to be assigned back to their original cells, and the UMIs allow for the removal of
PCR duplicates, enabling more accurate quantification of transcript molecules[257][253].
Though capable of processing tens of thousands to millions of cells in a single experi­
ment and generally the most robust and cost­effective on a per­cell basis, droplet­based
protocols capture only one end of the transcripts, typically the 3’. This strongly limits the
ability to analyze full­length transcript structure, such as alternative splicing or SNPs out­
side the tagged region. Compared to highly sensitive plate­based methods like SMART­
seq2, droplet methods may detect fewer genes per individual cell, partly due to lower
RNA capture efficiency in droplets, which leads to the sparsity of the resulting count ma­
trices[257][248].
2.5.2
Single­Cell RNA­Seq Data Processing and Analysis
The minute amounts of starting material per cell and the high­throughput nature of the
experiments, necessitate specialized bioinformatics pipelines and statistical methods that
differ from those employed for bulk RNA­Seq[247].
The high proportion of zero counts for many genes within individual cells, means that
a gene may be detected in one cell but not in another, even if both cells express it at
low levels. These dropouts can arise from genuinely low mRNA abundance in a cell,
inefficiencies in mRNA capture during library preparation, for example if only a fraction
of a cell’s mRNA molecules are converted to cDNA, or the stochastic nature of gene
expression itself, particularly for lowly expressed genes[259]. This sparsity results in a
data matrix that is predominantly filled with zeros[247].
scRNA­Seq experiments measure the expression levels of thousands of genes across
thousands to millions of cells, leading to very high­dimensional datasets and due to the
extremely small amount of starting RNA, the technical noise in scRNA­Seq is generally
more pronounced than in bulk RNA­Seq. This noise can stem from variations in cell lysis
efficiency, mRNA capture rates, reverse transcription efficiency, and PCR amplification
biases across individual cells or droplets[259].
These data properties mean that applying analytical tools designed for bulk RNA­Seq to
scRNA­Seq data can often lead to inaccurate or misleading results and the high level of
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
42
==PAGE:50==
sparsity, in particular, violates the assumptions of many standard statistical methods[247].
After sequencing, the raw reads must be processed to identify the cell barcode (CB)
sequence associated with each read, allowing reads to be assigned (demultiplexed) to
their respective cells of origin. This process extracts the CB sequence from the read
and matches the observed CB sequence to a list of known, valid CBs that were used in
the experiment, helping to correct for sequencing errors that might occur within the CB
sequence itself[247].
Handling UMIs
The primary purpose of UMIs is to correct for PCR amplification bias. During the PCR am­
plification required to generate sufficient material for sequencing, some cDNA molecules
may be amplified more efficiently than others, leading to multiple identical copies (PCR du­
plicates) of the same original molecule in the final sequencing library. Without UMIs, these
PCR duplicates would be indistinguishable from reads originating from distinct mRNA
molecules that happen to have the same sequence, leading to an overestimation of gene
expression levels[247][259].
With UMIs, after sequencing and alignment, reads that share the same cell barcode, map
to the same gene (or transcript), AND possess the same UMI sequence are considered
to be PCR duplicates derived from a single original RNA molecule[232]. These reads are
then ”collapsed” so that they are counted as only a single molecule in the final expres­
sion matrix, providing a much more accurate estimate of the true number of unique RNA
molecules originally present in each cell for each gene[247].
Tools that process scRNA­Seq data, such as Cell Ranger, kallisto/bustools, STARsolo,
UMI­tools, and zUMIs, incorporate algorithms for UMI deduplication[260]. The use of
UMIs represents an innovation that significantly improved the quantitative accuracy of
scRNA­Seq, especially for high­throughput droplet­based methods[248].
The precise
method of UMI collapsing, where you can, for example, choose to allow tiny sequenc­
ing errors between collapsed UMIs, can also influence the final counts[256].
Quality Control
After demultiplexing and UMI counting, the resulting gene­cell matrix will often contain
”cells” (cell barcodes) that do not represent viable, intact single cells, because in droplet­
based methods, some droplets may not encapsulate a cell but might still capture ambient
RNA present in the cell suspension. These ”empty” droplets will still have a cell barcode
from the co­encapsulated bead and may yield some reads, but they do not represent true
single­cell transcriptomes[261].
Some droplets might contain cells that were damaged during processing, stressed, or
dying. These cells often show aberrant transcriptomic profiles, such as very few genes
or UMIs detected, or an unusually high percentage of reads mapping to mitochondrial
genes, which can be an indicator of apoptosis or cell membrane damage. Cells with a
high percentage of reads mapping to mitochondrial genes are often excluded[262]. Oc­
casionally, a droplet or well might also capture two or more cells and these can often be
identified by an unusually high number of detected genes or UMIs[263].
Filtering out these artifacts is needed to ensure that downstream analyses are performed
on data from live single cells. Cells with very few or very many detected RNA molecules
(UMIs) and very low total feature counts are typically filtered out[247][232].
Normalization
Normalization in scRNA­Seq is even more important than in bulk RNA­Seq due to the
higher levels of technical noise, sparsity, and variability in factors like RNA capture effi­
ciency, reverse transcription efficiency, and sequencing depth across individual cells. The
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
43
==PAGE:51==
goal of normalization is to remove these technical variations so that meaningful biological
comparisons of gene expression can be made between cells[247][259].
A widely used and relatively simple normalization method involves scaling the UMI counts
for each cell to a common library size, usually 10,000 total counts per cell, and then apply­
ing a log transformation, typically log(scaled_counts + 1). This approach is implemented
as ”LogNormalize” in the Seurat R package[264] and can be achieved using functions like
normalize_total followed by log1p in the Scanpy Python package[265].
The scaling step adjusts for differences in sequencing depth between cells. The ad­
dition of a pseudocount of 1 before log transformation prevents taking the logarithm of
zero for genes with zero counts, and it helps to stabilize the variance of lowly expressed
genes, compressing the dynamic range of expression values. While this log­normalization
method is computationally straightforward and performs well for many applications, it may
not fully account for the mean­variance relationship inherent in count data or the zero­
inflation characteristic of scRNA­Seq data[266].
To address the specific statistical properties of scRNA­Seq UMI counts more effectively,
advanced normalization methods have been developed. Implemented in the Seurat pack­
age, SCTransform uses regularized negative binomial regression to model UMI counts for
each gene, explicitly accounting for the relationship between gene mean and variance,
and also regressing out sequencing depth as a technical confounder[267]. The output of
SCTransform is Pearson residuals, which represent normalized and variance­stabilized
expression values. This method can also simultaneously regress out other unwanted
sources of variation, such as the percentage of mitochondrial reads or cell cycle effects,
during the normalization process[268].
Various other methods exist that attempt to explicitly model dropout events, technical
noise, and batch effects using more complex statistical frameworks, such as zero­inflated
negative binomial models or Bayesian approaches, but it should be mentioned that these
computationally complex methods rarely yield superior results compared to log normal­
ization[266].
2.6
Downstream analysis
Once the scRNA­Seq data has been preprocessed and normalized, it is ready for a variety
of downstream analyses aimed at extracting biological insights.
Given that scRNA­Seq datasets measure thousands of genes (dimensions) for each cell,
dimensionality reduction is an essential step in order to enable visualization of the high­
dimensional data in a lower­dimensional space, allowing for visual inspection of cell­to­cell
relationships and population structures and to reduce noise and computational burden for
subsequent analyses like clustering[269].
Three main modalities of dimensionality reduction are used for the purpose of single­cell
RNA sequencing; PCA, t­SNE and UMAP[259].
PCA (principal component analysis) is a linear dimensionality reduction method that iden­
tifies the principal components, understood as high­dimensional directions of greatest
variance, in the data. It is often used as an initial step to reduce the dimensionality to a
few dozen PCs, which capture most of the biological signal while discarding some noise.
These top PCs are then frequently used as input for non­linear dimensionality reduction
methods or for clustering algorithms[270].
t­SNE (t­distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding) is a non­linear dimensionality re­
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
44
==PAGE:52==
duction technique that is particularly effective at visualizing local structure and revealing
well­separated clusters of similar cells in a low­dimensional embedding. It aims to pre­
serve the neighborhood relationships between cells[270].
UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation Projection) is another non­linear dimensionality
reduction method that has gained popularity in scRNA­Seq analysis. Like t­SNE, it excels
at visualizing local structure, but it is often considered to better preserve the global struc­
ture of the data as well. UMAP is also generally faster to compute than t­SNE, especially
for large datasets, and has widely been accepted as the superior method for single­cell
RNA sequencing[270].
Cell Type Annotation
A primary goal of many scRNA­Seq studies is to identify and characterize the different
cell types or states present in a sample[248]. This can be achieved by clustering cells
based on their transcriptomic similarity, often using the reduced­dimensional representa­
tion obtained from PCA or the coordinates from UMAP[253].
Graph­based clustering algorithms are widely used in scRNA­Seq analysis and are imple­
mented in popular toolkits like Seurat[264][271] and Scanpy[265]. The general approach
involves building a K­nearest neighbor (KNN) graph, where each cell is a node, and edges
are drawn between a cell and its K most similar neighbors in the selected feature space
of principal components[272].
Clustering then involves partitioning this graph into groups using algorithms like the Lou­
vain algorithm or the Leiden algorithm. These algorithms aim to find clusters of cells that
are more densely connected to each other than to cells outside the group. The choice
of clustering parameters, particularly resolution, determines the granularity/size of the re­
sulting clusters[272][273].
Once clusters are defined, the next step is to determine their biological identity, to an­
notate them as specific cell types or states. This is typically done by identifying marker
genes for each cluster, defined as genes that are significantly more highly expressed,
or specifically expressed, in one cluster compared to all other clusters, or compared to a
specific subset of other clusters. Differential expression tests, such as Wilcoxon rank­sum
test, as implemented in Seurat’s FindAllMarkers[264] function, or t­tests, but also more
qualitative methods such as visualizing feature expression across clusters, are used to
identify these marker genes[274]. The identified marker genes are then compared against
known marker genes for various cell types from literature, existing cell atlases, specialized
databases, or personal expertise, introducing a degree of subjectivity[253].
Cell type annotation, when performed manually in this way, is an iterative process, often
involving re­clustering or sub­clustering of initial groups, with finer or broader resolutions
to refine cell type definitions and find populations that fit the canonical markers of your
cell type of interest. It often involves many cycles of examining marker genes, consulting
literature, re­clustering specific populations at different resolutions, re­identifying markers,
and slowly refining annotations until a consistent and biologically meaningful set of cell
identities is achieved[274][275].
The accuracy and depth of manual annotation can be heavily dependent on the annota­
tor’s expertise regarding cell type­specific markers, developmental hierarchies, and the
biology of the tissue being studied[213]. Very importantly, annotations can be subjective,
influenced by the individual researcher’s interpretation of marker gene expression and
literature, with a high risk of impacting reproducibility[276].
One of the primary strengths of manual annotation, despite its challenges, is its capac­
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
45
==PAGE:53==
ity as a discovery tool. Because it is not strictly constrained by pre­existing categories
found in reference atlases, manual investigation of data­driven clusters based on their
unique gene expression signatures can lead to the identification and characterization of
previously unknown cell types or cell states[277][276].
But, if a cluster represents a truly novel cell type for which no markers are known, or a very
rare population, its annotation becomes an exploratory research project in itself, requiring
careful characterization and validation beyond simple marker lookup[274].
Canonical marker genes established from bulk studies or other contexts may not always
be reliably specific or consistently expressed at the single­cell level, or across different
tissues, developmental stages, or disease states[276]. Marker genes identified as dif­
ferentiating a cluster are specific to the context of that particular dataset and its cellular
composition[275].
In many cases, cells exist along continuous spectra of differentiation or activation. Distin­
guishing discrete, stable cell types from transient cell states or closely related subtypes
based solely on transcriptomic snapshots can be meaningless. It may be more correct
to use the broader term ”cell identities” to encompass both types and states to acknowl­
edge this complexity, since clustering algorithmsdo not ”understand”, the meaning of the
features used to cluster different populations, and whether it distinguishes between two
distinctly different cell types such as a neuron and a hepatocyte or simply two different
activation states of, for example, a T cell, is unknown[276].
This trade­off between the depth of exploration and efficiency/objectivity is a central theme
in scRNA­seq annotation. To mitigate some of these burdens, tools are emerging that aim
to assist or semi­automate marker­based annotation, such as Sargent, which uses scoring
based on cell type­specific marker gene sets[278], or even leveraging large language
models like GPT­4 to interpret marker gene lists[279].
The process of cell type annotation, if successful, can identify both known and potentially
novel cell types or states, and thereby describe the cellular heterogeneity that underlies
biological functions and disease processes. Without it, scRNA­seq data remains a col­
lection of high­dimensional expression vectors with limited direct biological interpretation.
Annotation provides the necessary context for downstream analyses, such as differential
gene expression analysis between cell types, trajectory inference, and cell­cell commu­
nication studies[274][275].
Reference­Based Cell Type Annotation
One way to overcome the troubles of manual annotation is to use reference based meth­
ods.
Reference­based annotation methods automate the labeling of cells in a query
dataset by using existing, (manually) annotated scRNA­seq datasets, often referred to
as reference atlases. The core principle involves mapping the gene expression profiles
of query cells to those in the reference and then transferring the cell type labels from the
most similar reference cells to the query cells[280].
Common strategies for reference­based annotation include identifying marker genes for
clusters in the query dataset and comparing these to known marker gene lists for cell
types defined in the reference, calculating the similarity, for example Pearson correlation,
between the expression profile of a query cluster and the average profiles of each cell
type in the reference or training a classification model, support vector machine, neural
network, nearest neighbor classifier, on the annotated reference dataset[274][275].
Azimuth is a prominent tool and web application that implements a reference­based map­
ping pipeline for scRNA­seq data analysis. Developed as part of the NIH Human Biomolec­
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
46
==PAGE:54==
ular Atlas Project (HuBMAP), Azimuth automates several steps including normalization,
visualization (by projecting query cells onto the UMAP embedding of the reference), cell
annotation via label transfer, and differential expression analysis to identify biomarkers
for the predicted cell types. It utilizes the anchor­based integration and label transfer
functionalities within the Seurat R package[281][268].
A factor for the success of reference­based annotation tools like Azimuth is the quality,
comprehensiveness, and relevance of the reference atlas to the query dataset. If the refer­
ence is inaccurately annotated, incomplete, or derived from a different biological context,
the transferred labels are likely to be erroneous or misleading[277].
While automated tools like Azimuth offer significant advantages in terms of speed, scal­
ability, and reproducibility, they face the limitation of not accounting for novelty. They are
primarily designed to assign query cells to predefined categories present in the reference,
and if a query dataset contains a truly novel cell type or a rare cell state not captured by
the reference, automated methods might either misclassify it as the most similar known
type, assign it with low confidence, or fail to classify it altogether[282].
2.7
Peptide Vaccines
After transcriptomic tools have been applied to discover a potential target, a modality
utilizing that target needs to be used. Peptide vaccines represent a specialized form of
immunotherapy, a therapeutic strategy that aims to use the host’s own immune system to
combat malignancies[283]. Unlike other immunotherapies, such as monoclonal antibody
administration, peptide vaccines attempt to elicit a durable, antigen­specific immune re­
sponse capable of recognizing and eliminating tumor cells. This is achieved by introducing
defined peptide epitopes, short fragments of proteins, to the host’s immune system, and
eventually engaging an immune response that targets this fragment[284]. By focusing on
antigens that are preferentially or exclusively expressed by neoplastic cells, these vac­
cines aim to minimize the off­target effects and systemic toxicities commonly associated
with conventional cytotoxic broader immunotherapies[283].
The fundamental principle of peptide­based cancer vaccines is to activate the adaptive
immune system, particularly T lymphocytes, to identify and destroy cancer cells. These
vaccines deliver specific epitopes derived from certain antigens (proteins), to antigen­
presenting cells (APCs). These APCs, primarily dendritic cells (DCs), then process these
peptides and present them on their surface via Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC)
molecules, initiating T­cell activation[284].
This targeted approach distinguishes pep­
tide vaccines from other immunotherapies that might have broader mechanisms of ac­
tion[283].
Therapeutic cancer vaccines, including those based on peptides, have been an area of
intensive research for several decades, where early experimental models and Phase I
clinical trials often showed promising immunogenicity, demonstrating that these vaccines
could indeed induce detectable T­cell responses[285].
However, the translation of this immunogenicity into clinical efficacy in larger, randomized
Phase II and Phase III trials proved to be difficult, in contrast with the rapid successes ob­
served with other immunotherapeutic modalities, such as immune checkpoint inhibitors
(ICIs) and chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T­cell therapies[283]. Early vaccine designs
faced several challenges, such as the poor in vivo immunogenicity of simple peptides,
their rapid enzymatic degradation, inefficient delivery to professional APCs, the immuno­
suppressive nature of the tumor microenvironment[286] and the constraints imposed by
HLA restriction[287].

The field of peptide cancer vaccines has witnessed a resurgence in recent years. This is
driven by a more profound understanding of cancer immunology, breakthroughs in anti­
gen discovery, particularly the identification of patient­specific neoantigens through next­
generation sequencing, innovations in vaccine formulation and delivery technologies, and
a growing appreciation for rational combination therapies[287][283].
One of the most significant shifts has been the move from vaccines targeting broadly
expressed tumor­associated antigens (TAAs) towards highly personalized approaches
focusing on neoantigens, which make up the majority of tumor­specific antigen (TSAs).
Neoantigens, arising from somatic mutations unique to an individual’s tumor, are recog­
nized as truly ”foreign” by the immune system and are thus less likely to be subject to cen­
tral tolerance, offering the potential for more robust and specific immune responses[288].
This personalization trajectory, enabled by advancements in genomic sequencing and
bioinformatics, aligns peptide vaccines with the broader trend of precision oncology, while
the aspect of individualized manufacturing still presents logistical and economic chal­
lenges[289]. A way to enhance the low immunogenicity of the peptides in these vaccines
is to combine them with immune checkpoint inhibition therapy. Vaccines can increase
the frequency and repertoire of tumor­specific T cells, while ICIs can boost these T cells
against inhibitory signals from the TME[283].
2.7.1
Mechanism of Action
Peptide vaccines function by actively engaging the host’s adaptive immune system, pri­
marily T lymphocytes, to recognize and eliminate cancer cells. This process involves the
precise delivery of antigenic peptides derived from tumor cells to initiate a cascade of
immune events culminating in targeted cell killing[283].
These peptides can be derived from TAAs, which are overexpressed self­proteins, or the
more narrow TSAs, which include neoantigens arising from tumor­specific mutations and
are foreign to the host immune system[290].
Upon administration, typically alongside an adjuvant to enhance immunogenicity[291],
these peptides are internalized by APCs. The subsequent processing and presentation
pathway depends on the nature of the peptide and how it is encountered by the APC.
Exogenously delivered peptides, such as those in most vaccine formulations, are taken up
into endosomes, transferred to the endo­lysosomal compartment, processed and loaded
onto MHC class II molecules. These peptide­MHC class II complexes are then transported
to the APC surface for presentation to CD4+ T helper cells[283].
For the activation of CD8+ CTLs, that directly lyse tumor cells, peptides must be pre­
sented by MHC class I molecules. This can occur if the vaccine peptides are delivered
directly into the cytoplasm of APCs, or, for exogenous peptide vaccines, through a pro­
cess called cross­presentation. During cross­presentation, exogenous antigens taken up
by specialized dendritic cells are processed and shunted from the endosomal pathway
into the MHC class I pathway for loading onto MHC class I molecules[292].
The use of longer peptides (20­35 amino acids) is particularly advantageous for this, as
they require more internalization and intracellular processing by APCs, ensuring engage­
ment of both MHC class I and class II pathways, rather than directly binding to MHC
molecules on non­professional APCs which can lead to tolerance[293].
The activation of T cells is provided by two signales. The first signal is provided by the
interaction of the T­cell receptor (TCR) with the specific peptide­MHC complex on the
APC. The second, co­stimulatory signal, is delivered by the interaction of molecules like
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
48
==PAGE:56==
CD28 on the T cell with B7 molecules (CD80/CD86) on the APC. In physiological settings,
a third signal, mediated by secreted cytokines, is employed to direct the immune response
towards a specific modality (humoral, cytotoxic or neutrophil­based). Professional APCs,
like mature dendritic cells, can provide all three signals, but for DCs to express the co­
stimulatory signals they themselves must also be in an activated state, a process that is
highly repressed by the tumor microenvironment[294].
Upon recognizing peptide­MHC class II complexes, CD4+ T cells become activated and
provide help towards establishing the anti­tumor response, by engaging DCs, through
CD40­CD40L interactions, to enhance their capacity to prime CD8+ T cells effectively,
secreting cytokines, such as Interleukin­2 (IL­2), which promote the proliferation, survival,
and effector function of CD8+ T cells and support the activation and differentiation of B
cells for antibody production, although for most cancer peptide vaccines, the primary focus
is on cellular (cytotoxic) immunity[284].
Initial activation of CD8+ T cells happens in secondary lymphoid organs upon interaction
with peptide­MHC­I on activated APCs. These primed CTLs then proliferate and differen­
tiate into effector CTLs, which can migrate to the tumor site. Once at the tumor, effector
CTLs recognize the same peptide­MHC class I complexes displayed on the surface of
cancer cells This triggering the CTLs to kill the tumor cells directly through the release of
cytotoxic granules containing perforin and granzymes, that induce apoptosis in the target
cell, and the engagement of death receptors like Fas on tumor cells by Fas Ligand (FasL)
on CTLs[284]. Activated CTLs also produce cytokines such as Interferon­gamma (IFN­γ),
which has direct anti­proliferative effects on some tumor cells and can further modulate
the immune environment by, for example, upregulating MHC expression on tumor cells,
making them more visible to the immune system[287].
While CTLs are the direct mediators of tumor cell killing, historical and ongoing research
has revealed that CD8+ T cell responses can be weak, short­lived, or ineffective without
adequate CD4+ T cell help[295]. Long peptides can be used here because they are
naturally processed by APCs into epitopes that can be presented both on MHC class I
to CD8+ T cells via cross­presentation and on MHC class II to CD4+ T cells[292]. The
spatial arrangement of different epitopes within a single long peptide, or the use of specific
enzymatic cleavage sites or linkers between them, can influence how efficiently they are
processed and presented, adding another layer of complexity to vaccine design[296].
An important aspect of vaccine efficacy is the potential to amplify pre­existing anti­tumor
immunity. Clinical observations suggest that the strength of the immunological response
triggered by a vaccine, and the presence of pre­existing immune responses to the cancer
peptides included in the vaccine, determine clinical outcome[283]. This means that the
vaccine may function more effectively as a ”booster” to an already initiated, but likely weak
or suppressed, immune response, rather than inducing a novel response from a naive T­
cell repertoire. If no specific T­cell precursors are present, the vaccine may face a greater
challenge in mounting an effective response[297].
2.7.2
Tumor­associated Antigens
The choice of antigen type is also incredibly important. Here, three types of antigens,
relevant for peptide­based cancer vaccines, are mentioned; tumor­associated antigens,
tumor­specific antigens and specialized tumor microenvironment antigens (TMAs).
TAAs are proteins that show differential expression patterns between tumor cells and
normal healthy tissues. They may be overexpressed in cancer cells, aberrantly expressed
due to post­translational modifications unique to cancer, or represent proteins that are

typically expressed only during fetal development and are re­expressed in malignant cells
(oncofetal antigens)[292].
Examples of TAAs targeted in cancer vaccines include: Melanoma antigens like MART­1
(Melan­A), gp100, and tyrosinase[287], prostate­specific antigen (PSA) in prostate can­
cer[287], mucin 1 (MUC1), often aberrantly glycosylated in various carcinomas[298], sur­

Many TAAs are shared among patients with the same type of cancer, allowing for the de­

because TAAs may be expressed at low levels on normal cells, there is a risk of breaking
self­tolerance and inducing autoimmunity, although this is generally less common with
peptide vaccines compared to other immunotherapies[295]. Pre­existing immunological
tolerance to these self­proteins can weaken the immunogenicity of TAA­based vaccines,
leading to weaker T­cell responses and tumors can evade immune recognition by down­
regulating or losing the expression of targeted TAAs[283].
2.7.3
Tumor­specific Antigens
TSAs are antigens that are, in principle, exclusively expressed by tumor cells and are ab­
sent from normal healthy tissues. The most prominent category of TSAs is neoantigens,
which are novel protein sequences that arise from somatic mutations within the tumor’s
genome. These mutations can include point mutations, insertions and deletions lead­
ing to frameshifts, gene fusions, or aberrant splicing events[288]. Proteins derived from
oncogenic viruses (HPV E6/E7 proteins in cervical cancer) also fall under the umbrella of
TSAs[302].
Neoantigens are unique to the tumor, virtually eliminating the risk of on­target, off­tumor
autoimmunity and because they are not present in normal tissues, neoantigens are not
subject to central immunological tolerance in the thymus and the immune system can rec­
ognize them as ”foreign,” leading to potentially robust T­cell responses[288]. The iden­
tification of neoantigens through next­generation sequencing (NGS) of a patient’s tumor
and normal genome, coupled with bioinformatic prediction of immunogenic epitopes, is
the basis of personalized cancer vaccination strategies[288].
The vast majority of somatic mutations and resulting neoantigens are unique to each indi­
vidual patient’s tumor, which necessitates a personalized approach to vaccine design and
manufacturing, that is logistically complex, time­consuming and currently expensive[289].
Tumors with a low tumor mutational burden may yield few, if any, immunogenic neoanti­
gens, limiting the applicability of this approach for some patients[303].
Targeting clonal neoantigens arising from early mutations present in all tumor cells is gen­
erally preferred over subclonal neoantigens, present in only a subset of tumor cells, as this
reduces the risk of outgrowth and escape of antigen­negative variants. Targeting antigens
that are essential for the tumor’s survival or malignant phenotype, such as driver muta­
tions or proteins involved in oncogenic pathways, may exert greater selective pressure
on the tumor and reduce the likelihood of immune escape through antigen loss[303].

2.8
Tumor Microenvironment Antigens
Despite the conceptual elegance of targeting antigens associated with the malignant cells,
its clinical efficacy has been hampered by the tumor microenvironment. As mentioned
earlier, the TME is an active and complex ecosystem of non­malignant cells that tu­
mors abuse to support their growth, invasion, and evasion of immune surveillance. This
immunosuppressive network is populated by cells including regulatory T cells, tumor­
associated macrophages, myeloid­derived suppressor cells[47]. These cells deploy sup­
pressive molecules and changes in local metabolism to create a localized state of immune
dysfunction, shielding the tumor from anti­tumor T cells. This means that the goal is no
longer just how to generate T cells that can see the tumor, but how to destroy the shield
that prevents them from acting[304].
It is within this context that a new class of therapeutic targets has been proposed: Tumor
Microenvironment Antigens (TMAs). Unlike their traditional counterparts TAAs, TMAs are
defined by their expression not only on some tumor cells, but on the regulatory and stromal
cells that construct the TME. The therapeutic goal is not only to kill tumor cells directly but
also to change the immunological settings, converting the TME from a protective shelter
into an immunocompetent and tumor­hostile site[304].
The proteins that function as TMAs are functional components of the immunosuppressive
machinery within the TME, that can be systematically categorized based on their biological
roles. TMAs include metabolic enzymes, immune checkpoint ligands, immunosuppres­
sive cytokines and chemokines, regulatory transcription factors, and certain traditional
TAAs that exhibit dual functionality[304].
Metabolic enzymes like IDO and ARG1 create a nutritionally deprived site for effector T
cells by depleting essential amino acids, checkpoint ligands like PD­L1 act as a direct
”off switch,” engaging inhibitory receptors on T cells to induce anergy, and cytokines and
chemokines orchestrate the local immune landscape, with molecules like TGFβ acting
as a powerful inhibitor on cytotoxic cells and CCL22 serving as a specific recruiter of
Tregs[46]. Even some traditional TAAs like VEGF and survivin can be considered TMAs
when their expression on non­malignant stromal cells, such as the vascular endothelium,
contributes to support functions like angiogenesis[305][304].
Upon activation by a vaccine, CD8+ T cells recognize TMA­derived peptides presented
on HLA class I molecules and lyse the cells expressing them. This cytotoxic activity is not
limited to tumor cells that happen to express the TMA, but also extends to the cells con­
tributing to the immunosuppressive microenvironment[306]. For instance, IDO­specific
CD8+ T cells can eliminate IDO­expressing tolerogenic dendritic cells and MDSCs[307],
while ARG1­specific T cells can target suppressive TAMs[308]. In vivo evidence from
preclinical models supports this mechanism, showing that vaccination with IDO epitopes
leads to a reduction in IDO­expressing myeloid cells in the TME, and TGF­β vaccination
decreases TGF­β protein levels locally[309].
CD4+ T cells are producers of pro­inflammatory cytokines and when recognizing their
target TMA epitopes presented on HLA class II molecules by antigen­presenting cells
within the TME, they release cytokines, most notably interferon­gamma (IFN­γ) and tu­
mor necrosis factor­alpha (TNF­α)[306]. This localized cytokine expression can repolar­
ize regulatory cells, for example, by pushing immunosuppressive M2­like TAMs towards
a pro­inflammatory, tumoricidal M1 phenotype[116] and enhance antigen presentation
across the TME[304].

2.8.1
Antigen and Epitope Choice
Besides choice of antigen type, several other factors must be considered and weighed
when selecting specific epitopes for inclusion in a vaccine[283].
Tumors are heterogeneous, and they can evolve under immune pressure, leading to the
loss or downregulation of targeted antigens, the upregulation of alternative immunosup­
pressive pathways or alternative splicing of the antigen. If the antigen is found within
a specific exon, switching to an isoform of the protein that does not use this exon will
remove the immunogenic pressure. Strategies to counteract this include the use of multi­
antigen/multi­epitope vaccines, combination therapies that target multiple potential es­
cape mechanisms and usage of peptides found in an exon present in all isoforms of the
antigen[313].
The length and design of the peptides used in a vaccine determine their immunological
properties and efficacy. Short Peptides, typically 8­11 amino acids for MHC class I; 13­25
amino acids with a 9­mer core for MHC class II, correspond to the minimal epitope length
that binds to MHC molecules[287].
Short peptides can bind directly to MHC molecules expressed on the surface of various
cell types, including non­professional APCs that may lack the co­stimulatory signals, like
CD80/CD86 necessary for full T­cell activation, and presentation by such cells can lead
to T­cell anergy or tolerance rather than productive immunity[292]. Short peptides are
often highly specific to a single or very few HLA alleles, limiting their applicability across
a genetically diverse patient population. They are also susceptible to degradation by
exopeptidases and endopeptidases in vivo, resulting in a short half­life and potentially
insufficient antigen exposure for robust priming[292].
Long peptides are generally too large to bind directly to MHC molecules on the cell sur­
face and must be internalized and processed by professional APCs, which increases the
likelihood that peptide presentation occurs in the context of appropriate co­stimulation,
leading to effective T­cell activation[292]. A single long peptide can contain multiple dis­
tinct epitopes, including those that bind to MHC class I, arising after processing and cross­
presentation, and MHC class II molecules, allowing for the simultaneous activation of both
CD8+ CTLs and CD4+ T helper cells[292]. The inclusion of multiple potential epitopes
within a long peptide increases the likelihood that at least some of them will be able to bind
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
52
==PAGE:60==
to the specific HLA alleles expressed by an individual patient, thereby partially overcoming
the issue of HLA restriction and broadening population coverage[286].
The larger size of longer peptides and their potential for some tertiary structure may offer
increased protection against rapid degradation by exopeptidases, potentially improving
their in vivo stability and persistence compared to short peptides, but may also skew the
immune response towards an antibody­based response instead of the desired cytotoxic
response[286]. Preclinical studies and some clinical data suggest that long peptide vac­
cines can induce more durable T­cell memory and achieve better tumor control compared
to vaccines based on minimal short epitopes[296]. It is also possible to include several
peptides in the same vaccine, which may cover the HLA’s of a broader population and
decrease the risk of tumor immune escape, by terminating expression of the antigen[283].
The use of adjuvants should be considered, since peptides themselves are often poorly
immunogenic. While traditional adjuvants like Incomplete Freund’s Adjuvant have been
widely used, peptide vaccines have shifted towards modern adjuvants that activate spe­
cific innate immune pathways, including Toll­Like Receptor agonists like Poly­ICLC and
CpG oligonucleotides, STING pathway activators, or immune checkpoint inhibitors like
anti­PD­1 monoclonal antibodies[283].
2.8.2
MHC Binding
The peptide epitope must be capable of binding with sufficient affinity and stability to one
or more of the patient’s HLA class I or class II molecules to ensure effective presentation
to T cells, and this can become a problem since the HLA gene complex, located on chro­
mosome 6, is the most polymorphic region in the human genome[314]. Thousands of
different HLA alleles exist in the human population, and each individual inherits a unique
set of these alleles from their parents, composed of two alleles for each of the HLA­A, ­B,
­C, ­DR, ­DP, and ­DQ loci[287].
Each HLA allele encodes an MHC protein with a structurally distinct peptide­binding groove,
with specific preferences for the amino acid residues at certain positions (anchor residues)
within a peptide, meaning that a given peptide will bind with high affinity only to a limited
number of HLA alleles. This selective binding is known as HLA restriction, and for a pep­
tide vaccine to be effective, the peptides must be capable of binding to the HLA molecules
expressed by the patient. If a patient’s HLA type cannot bind and present the chosen pep­
tides, no T­cell response will be generated against those peptides, rendering the vaccine
ineffective for that individual, making the development of universal peptide vaccines diffi­
cult[315].
HLA typing of patients is needed for personalized vaccine approaches. Knowing a pa­
tient’s HLA genotype allows for the selection or in silico design of peptides that are pre­
dicted to bind to their specific HLA allotypes[316]. Beyond peptide selection, the HLA
phenotype itself can sometimes have prognostic implications in the context of cancer and
immunotherapy[317].
The diversity of the HLA system within the human population means that you must either
commit to full personalization, designing the vaccine to the individual’s HLA profile, or
attempt to achieve broader population coverage through the use of multi­epitope mixtures
or long peptides that have a greater chance of containing relevant epitopes for a wider
selection of HLA types[318].
2.8.3
Peptide­MHC binding Prediction
The interaction between a peptide and an MHC molecule is the first step for T­cell recogni­
tion of an antigen and given the number of potential peptide epitopes that can be derived

53
==PAGE:61==
from any specific protein and the extensive polymorphism of HLA molecules, experimen­
tal screening of all possible peptide­MHC interactions is practically impossible. Com­
putational prediction algorithms have become indispensable tools in the field of vaccine
development, particularly for identifying and prioritizing candidate peptide epitopes[318].
T­cell mediated immunity is initiated when a TCR on a T cell engages with a pMHC com­
plex displayed on the surface of an APC or a target cell[318]. In humans, MHC class I alle­
les are HLA­A, HLA­B, and HLA­C and bind peptides that are 8­11 amino acids in length.
These peptides are derived from intracellular proteins that are degraded by the protea­
some in the cytoplasm, where the resulting peptides are transported into the endoplas­
mic reticulum (ER) by the Transporter associated with Antigen Processing (TAP) protein,
where they are loaded onto newly synthesized MHC class I molecules, and the peptide­
MHC­I complex then traffics to the cell surface for presentation to CD8+ T cells[319].
The peptide binding cleft of MHC class I molecules is closed, meaning that the peptide
cannot ”spill” out of the binding cleft and is restricted in length. Depending on the HLA
allele, different residues of the peptide act as anchor residues and are necessary for
binding. For example, in HLA­A*01:01 an aspartic or glutamic acid is required at position
2 and a tyrosine at position 8[314].
MHC class II alleles are HLA­DR, HLA­DP, and HLA­DQ and generally present longer
peptides, typically 13­25 amino acids in length, although the core binding motif interacting
with the MHC groove is roughly 9 amino acids long. These peptides are derived from
extracellular proteins that are taken up by APCs through endocytosis or phagocytosis and
degraded into peptides within endo­lysosomal compartments, where they bind to MHC
class II molecules. The peptide­MHC­II complex is then transported to the cell surface for
presentation to CD4+ T cells[319]. The peptide­binding groove of MHC class II molecules
is open at both ends, which allows for the accommodation of peptides of varying lengths, in
some cases full­length proteins, with flanking regions extending beyond the core binding
site[320].
The binding of a peptide to a specific MHC allele is determined by the amino acid se­
quence of the peptide, particularly the residues at specific ”anchor” positions that fit into
corresponding pockets within the MHC binding groove, and by the polymorphic amino
acid residues that line this groove, which vary between different HLA alleles[287].
The primary goal of peptide­MHC binding prediction algorithms is to identify which pep­
tides from a given protein sequence are likely to bind to a specific MHC allele in silico so
that you can avoid having to go through the process of experimental testingBlass2021.
Different types of data have been used to train prediction models. Binding affinity data
are quantitative measurements, typically expressed as the concentration of peptide re­
quired to inhibit the binding of a standard radiolabeled probe peptide by 50% (IC50) or
as dissociation constants (kD). This data is generated from in vitro assays using puri­
fied MHC molecules and synthetic peptides and the method does not allow for collection
of very large amounts of data, but has the advantage of providing ”negative” data from
non­binders[316].
Mass spectrometry­eluted ligand data (immunopeptidomics) involves isolating pMHC com­
plexes from the surface of cells, eluting the bound peptides, and identifying their se­
quences using tandem mass spectrometry. MS data provides information on peptides
that are not only capable of binding to MHC but have also been successfully processed
and naturally presented by cells. This makes MS data particularly valuable as it reflects
more of the antigen presentation pathway[316].

54
==PAGE:62==
Prediction methods for peptide­MHC binding have evolved significantly from early ap­
proaches that relied on motif searching or simple scoring matrices based on amino acid
frequencies at anchor positions. Modern predictors now predominantly employ sophis­
ticated machine learning techniques. Among the most widely used are Artificial Neu­
ral Networks, which have demonstrated excellent performance, when trained on large
datasets[315]. More recently, Deep Learning models, such as convolutional and recur­
rent neural networks, are being applied to learn complex patterns from vast amounts of
sequence data[318].
The NetMHCpan family, with the newest version being 4.1, is a tool for pan­specific pre­
diction of MHC class I, capable of making predictions for almost any HLA­A, ­B, or ­C
allele, even those with limited experimental data. Similarly, the NetMHCIIpan family pro­
vides predictions for peptide binding to MHC class II molecules (HLA­DR, ­DP, ­DQ). Both
are trained on a combination of binding affinity and mass spectrometry data, and gener­
ate scores, for example, predicted IC50 binding affinity, percentile rank against random
peptides, or likelihood of being an eluted ligand[321].
The advent of high­throughput mass spectrometry enabled the direct identification of
the ”immunopeptidome”, the actual repertoire of peptides presented by cells. This MS­
derived data provides a more biologically relevant training set because it inherently re­
flects successful upstream processing. Consequently, tools that integrate MS data, like
the NetMHCpan family and MixMHC2pred, often show improved performance in identify­
ing naturally presented peptides[322].
Predicting peptide binding to MHC class II molecules remains inherently more challenging.
This difficulty arises from the open­ended nature of the MHC­II binding groove, which
accommodates peptides of variable lengths in different registers, alongside the greater
diversity of binding cores and high polymorphism in MHC class II genes[315].
A limitation of these predictions is that strong binding is necessary but not sufficient for im­
munogenicity and does not guarantee a T­cell response. Many high­affinity peptides are
not recognized by T cells, as immunogenicity also depends on the host’s T­cell receptor
repertoire, the peptide’s foreignness, and the stability of the peptide­MHC (pMHC) com­
plex[323]. It’s estimated that only about 2% of bioinformatically predicted neoantigens are
ultimately immunogenic[322].
2.9
R Shiny apps
The software application presented in this thesis works off of the R Shiny architecture.
The R programming language is a remarkably efficient system for statistical computa­
tion and data visualization, and it has become one of the most popular programming
languages in bioinformatics and data science. Translating the analytical power of R into
accessible, interactive tools for clinicians and bench scientists without programming ex­
pertise, has historically been a significant challenge[4][5]. The development of the Shiny
web application framework by RStudio, now Posit, in 2012 provided a direct pathway
to build web applications entirely within the R environment. By compiling R code into
the standard web technologies of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, Shiny abstracts the com­
plexities of web development, empowering data scientists and bioinformaticians to create
sophisticated, bespoke analytical tools[324].
At its core, every Shiny application is built upon a distinct two­component architecture: a
user interface object and a server function[325].
Investigating SIGLEC15 as a potential target for cancer peptide vacination
55
==PAGE:63==
The user interface defines the front­end of the application—the layout and appearance
presented to the user. It is constructed using R functions that generate the necessary
HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, and is composed of input controls (sliders, drop­down menus)
to collect user commands, and output elements (plot areas, data tables) that serve as
placeholders for results[325].
The Server function represents the computational engine and contains all the R code
responsible for data processing, analysis, and the generation of plots and tables. The
server function takes input and output objects as arguments, serving as the communica­
tion bridge to the ui. The input object holds the current values of all controls, while the
output object is where results are placed to be rendered in the ui[325].
This architecture is powered by reactivity. Instead of executing a linear sequence of com­
mands, a Shiny application is built around a graph that automatically links inputs to out­
puts, such that when a user changes an input, Shiny automatically traces this graph to
identify exactly which outputs depend on that input and re­executes only the specific por­
tions of R code necessary to update them. This process is governed by ”lazy evaluation,” a
principle that ensures the application performs the minimal amount of work required[325].
Shiny has emerged as a leading solution for enabling the exploration of complex ge­
nomic data, enabling the creation of user­friendly graphical interfaces for a wide array of
bioinformatics tasks. These applications allow users to perform complex analyses—from
differential expression to multi­omics integration—through intuitive controls. The power
of Shiny is magnified by its seamless integration with the extensive ecosystem of R pack­
ages developed for bioinformatics, allowing developers to build sophisticated applications
with minimal effort[324].

RNA sequencing gene expression data
Bulk RNA sequencing was downloaded from the Xena Browser[326]. For AML blood
samples, the TPM values from the TCGA LAML cohort were chosen and compared to
TPM from healthy peripheral blood samples in the GTEx[327] cohort subsetted to samples

the R package tximport to get gene TPM values. TPM for healthy bone marrow samples
were downloaded from the GTEX cohort by subsetting to samples with _primary_site as
”Bone Marrow”.
3.2
Celltype deconvolution
Cell type fractions for Tregs, M1 and M2 macrophages were derived from the bulk RNA
sequencing data by running the deconvolute function from the immunedeconv R pack­
age with the quantiseq method[329]. For MDSC’s sample­wise enrichment scores were
calculated by running single­sample gene set enrichment analysis from the GSVA pack­
age[241], using the gene set for MDSCs described by Wang et al[330] which is available
through the IOBR R package[331].
3.3
RNA sequencing isoform expression data
AML isoform expression data was downloaded for blood samples from the TCGA pan­
cancer cohort by subsetting samples to those where cancer type abbreviation was LAML.
Bone marrow isoform expression data in TPM was retrieved by the Kallisto pseudo­
aligned transcript abundances from the Leucegene cohort.
3.4
Single cell RNA sequencing data
Bone marrow single cell RNA sequencing samples were downloaded from three separate
GEO entries: GSE239721, GSE223844 and GSE116256.
Pre­processing
Raw counts were extracted and cells were filtered for having between 200 and 7500 fea­
tures (genes) expressed and a percentage of mitochondrial genes below 5%. Layers were
split using Seurat::SplitLayers and standard pre­processing was applied using the tools
from the Seurat R package; NormalizeData with log­normalize method, FindVariableFea­
tures, and ScaleData. Principal components were calculated by RunPCA and layers were
integrated with IntegrateLayers using the harmony method. A neighbor graph was con­
structed with FindNeighbors and clusters were initially determined with FindClusters, with
no defined resolution. Dimensionality reduction was performed with RunUMAP[271].
Reference­bases cell type annotation
Reference­based cell type annotation was performed with the RunAzimuth function from
the Azimuth R package[281] using the bone marrow reference[332][333].
Manual cell type annotation
Further manual celltype annotation was performed by taking a subset of a parent cell
group, using the Seurat::Subset function, from the reference­based annotation and re­
processing these cells with FindVariableFeatures, ScaleData and RunPCA. New neigh­
bors and clusters were found with FindNeighbors, inheriting the reduction generated by

Discussion
This thesis presents a software application enabling the swift visualization of transcrip­
tomic data intended for validation of a specific gene as a potential target for therapeutic
cancer peptide vaccination. The example gene, SIGLEC15, is over­expressed in AML
and is associated with an immunosuppressive M2 macrophage phenotype in the bone
marrow, while results from single­cell data are more inconclusive.
The analysis of bulk gene­mapped RNA sequencing data revealed a statistically signif­
icant over­expression of SIGLEC15 in AML patient samples compared to their healthy
tissue counterparts. Specifically, both blood samples from the TCGA­LAML cohort and
bone marrow samples from the Leucegene cohort exhibited markedly higher SIGLEC15
expression than corresponding healthy blood and bone marrow tissues from the GTEx
database. This observation is consistent with broader pan­cancer bioinformatic analy­
ses, which have similarly identified SIGLEC15 as being significantly over­expressed in
a wide range of malignancies[61][196], including AML[195]. This pattern of differential
expression provides preliminary evidence required to classify SIGLEC15 as a potential
tumor­associated antigen in the context of AML. Its virtually absent expression in most
other healthy tissues (with the exception of bladder) further positions it as a viable target,
since this may lead to fewer on­target off­tumor adverse effects[283].
A primary limitation, in the comparison of TPM in healthy vs diseased tissue, is the use
of unmatched control samples for the bulk RNA­seq data. The study compared tumor
data from the TCGA and Leucegene cohorts with healthy control data from the GTEx
consortium, which will inevitably add some additional genetic biological variation not con­
tributed to them being diseased or not[338]. The datasets were furthermore generated by
different research groups, at different times, likely using different protocols for sample pro­
curement, storage, RNA extraction, library preparation, and sequencing. These technical
differences will introduce batch effects in the form of non­biological variation[339].
It is well established that direct, uncorrected comparisons of expression data across such
disparate cohorts can be highly misleading, and multiple studies have demonstrated that
without a reprocessing pipeline that includes batch correction algorithms, apparent dif­
ferences between TCGA and GTEx may be driven more by technical artifacts than true
biology[340]. While the observed over­expression of SIGLEC15 in AML aligns with other
reports, it is impossible to exactly quantify the size of said over­expression or to be cer­
tain that it is not inflated by confounding batch effects. Methods that attempt to correct for
batch effects exist, but their use is risky, since they don’t ”understand” what variance is
caused by batch effects and what is caused by true biological difference, and in trying to
get rid of the former, you risk erasing the latter[338].
In the bone marrow, SIGLEC15 expression was strongly correlated with the presence of
M2­polarized macrophages. This finding aligns with the established biology of SIGLEC15,
which is known to be expressed on myeloid cells, including macrophages and dendritic
cells[153], and whose expression is promoted by macrophage colony­stimulating factor
(M­CSF), a cytokine driving the differentiation of macrophages toward the immunosup­
pressive M2 phenotype[103]. This relationship has been observed in other cancers; for
instance, in non­small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and glioma, SIGLEC15 expression on
tumor­associated macrophages (TAMs) is associated with M2­like polarization and may
contribute to an immunosuppressive TME[182][193]. The combination of over­expression

Different SIGLEC15 isoforms are dominant in blood versus bone marrow, and particularly
highly expressed in blood samples was the non­protein­coding, retained­intron transcript

While the analysis identified a conserved protein region shared by all protein­coding iso­

prevalence of the non­coding isoform is problematic. Its presence complicates the inter­
pretation of total gene­level expression values, as a significant portion of the SIGLEC15

vantage in treatment, since this pattern might represent an instance of isoform­switching,
where the cancer switches to the protein­coding transcript, to reap the benefits of the im­
munosuppressive function of the protein, while the protein is otherwise absent in healthy
tissue, where only non­coding transcripts are expressed[343]. This could further support
the idea that adverse effects may be minimized when targeting this protein, particularly
if the high expression seen at gene level in bladder, upon inspection at transcript level,
turns out to come from non­coding transcripts.
There is some contrast between the over­expression signal for SIGLEC15 identified in
bulk RNA­seq data and its far more ambiguous profile in the single­cell RNA­sequencing
(scRNA­seq) analysis. While bulk data pointed to SIGLEC15 as a significantly upregu­
lated gene in AML, the scRNA­seq data revealed a sparse and non­localized expression
pattern, and SIGLEC15 was not statistically significantly over­expressed in any of the cell
populations of interest (leukemic stem cells (LSCs), Tregs, M2 macrophages and MDSCs)
in any of the included datasets.
A significant portion of this discrepancy might be attributed to the inherent technical limita­
tions of the scRNA­seq technology itself. Droplet­based platforms, used to generate the
data analyzed, are known to have a lower per­cell mRNA capture efficiency compared
to the collective capture from thousands of cells in a bulk RNA­seq experiment, meaning
that for any given cell, only a fraction of its total mRNA content is successfully captured,
reverse transcribed, and sequenced. For genes that are expressed at low to moderate
levels, this can frequently result in a read count of zero in the final data matrix, even if the
gene was being actively transcribed in that cell[257]. This phenomenon leads to a high
degree of sparsity that can obscure the true expression patterns of many genes, making
them difficult to detect as statistically significant markers in differential expression analy­
ses. The lack of a strong, specific signal for SIGLEC15 in the single­cell data may be a
technical artifact of the method’s limited sensitivity for non­highly expressed genes[276].
Sparsity can also reflect transcriptional bursting.
Gene transcription is often a highly
stochastic process that occurs in sporadic, high­intensity ”bursts,” followed by variable
periods of transcriptional inactivity. These dynamics create significant cell­to­cell hetero­
geneity in mRNA levels, even within a genetically identical population[344]. A scRNA­seq
experiment captures a static ”snapshot” of each cell’s transcriptome at a single moment
in time. For a gene regulated by bursting, many cells will be caught in an off­state and
will register a zero mRNA count, even though protein from the gene might be present,
while a smaller subset of cells may be caught in an active on­state, exhibiting very high

expression[345]. The high average expression seen in the bulk RNA­seq data could be
the result of intense transcriptional bursting in a fraction of the cells, a signal that becomes
averaged and amplified across the entire population but appears sparse and variable at
the single­cell level[344].
The sparsity is particularly a problem when coupled with the low cell counts of some of
the annotations. For example, M2 macrophages were only identified a total number of
17 times across 18 samples in the dataset GSE223844. For the other two datasets, the
populations of macrophages were too small to allow sub­clustering (<50).
That said, this sparsity observed at the single­cell level is not necessarily contradictory to
the results found when examining bulk data. When compared to healthy single­cell bone
marrow samples, we might find that the aggregated expression is even lower and fits with
the difference seen at the bulk level. Since the bulk expression of SIGLEC15 in bone
marrow has a median of 0 TPM, we would expect to see virtually no SIGLEC15 counts
in healthy bone marrow single­cell data. The true problem for the analysis is that there
are not enough counts, or cells of a specific type, to definitively state anything regarding
differential expression[346].
The reason for the inconclusive single­cell data regarding SIGLEC15 may also lie in prob­
lems regarding the method used for cell type annotation. First, a reference­based anno­
tation method using Azimuth with its bone marrow reference was used. From this, the
cell types of interest were identified by choosing some of the automatically annotated cell
types and sub­clustering that group to find specific sub­populations. For Tregs and M2
macrophages, this approach may work well, since they are sub­populations of T cells and
macrophages, respectively, for which annotations in the reference exist[275].
Tregs were unidentifiable in the dataset GSE116256, where none of the sub­clusters
unanimously expressed all the markers associated with regulatory T cells. T regulatory
cells in the bone marrow are known to be different from regulatory T cells in other tis­
sues and, for example, express lower levels of FOXP3[347], which may be why the set
of markers used here failed to identify this cell type. Otherwise, the approach was gener­
ally successful, even for M2 macrophages, despite the very low number of macrophages
identified by the reference­based annotation.
With MDSC’s however, this method of cell type annotation is more difficult. The sub­
groups, monocytic MDSC’s and granulocytic MDSC’s, were found from CD14+ mono­
cytes, CD16+ monocytes, GMP (Granulocyte­Monocyte Progenitor) and BaEoMa (Basophil­
Eosinophil­Mast cell Progenitor), in order to cover all of the myeloid branch where this cell
type might have been placed. GMP and BaEoMa were also selected from the Azimuth
bone marrow reference as the parent population for both cell types for a couple of rea­
sons[332][333]. Monocytic MDSC’s share many features with regular monocytes and are
mainly distinguished by their lack of HLA­DR, so this cell type would likely be annotated as
a monocyte, even though they physiologically branch off from general myeloid progenitors
before the differentiation step towards monocytes[126]. Granulocytic MDSC’s are more
like neutrophils[126], a cell type not present in the reference, and the choice of parent
population here can only be justified in that they are likely found in some myeloid popu­
lation, making the inclusion of GMP and BaEoMa necessary. Clusters fitting the known
marker genes could in fact be identified from the parent populations, but by the above
reasoning, the identified cell type may not have actually been MDSC’s, but could also be
some state of regular monocytes, granulocytes, or myeloid cells[138]. The set of markers
used is considered applicable if you first filter away other lineages like lymphocytes and
erythrocytes[126].

With leukemic stem cells, the parent population was chosen as HSC (hematopoietic stem

just hematopoietic precursors, but specifically myeloid progenitors, in the case of AML.
Although populations fitting the chosen markers could be found in all datasets, it remains
unclear if all of them are true leukemic stem cells or merely blasts that have inherited the
characteristics of leukemic stem cells. This seems further plausible, since the popula­

from proliferation of the stem cells can be very high[23]. This might not be an issue for
development of treatments since both LSCs and blasts should be eliminated[24].
The parent populations also refer to a set of healthy cells, since the reference is de­
rived from healthy samples. Due to the high heterogeneity of malignant cells and since
”leukemic stem cell” does not exist in the reference, these cells may in fact be found
in other cell populations, such as monocytes, where they might further impede with the
annotation of MDSC’s[275], since both are immature myeloid cells[126][23].
Reference­based approaches are heavily reliant on the quality, comprehensiveness, and
relevance of their respective signature matrices or reference atlases. The ”missing cell
type” problem, where a cell type present in the sample is absent from the reference,
poses a significant challenge[234]. Particularly the relevance aspect is challenged here,
since there is clearly an issue with the use of a single­cell reference developed from
healthy bone marrow samples being applied to a diseased bone marrow, such as in
AML. Leukemia can cause an increase in the number of many different cell types, no­
tably leukemic blasts but also anti­inflammatory tumor­associated macrophages, MDSCs,
Tregs and fibroblasts, and also pro­inflammatory cells like CD8+ T cells that combat the
cancer[31]. The presence of these unusual cell populations can skew the accuracy of the
reference­based annotations and the ideal scenario would be to have a reference that is
built from AML samples and contains the desired immune regulatory cell types explored
in this thesis[275].
The robustness of marker genes is another challenge. Identifying gene sets that are truly
specific to a cell type and maintain that specificity across diverse biological contexts, dif­
ferent tissues, disease states, individuals, and even technological platforms, is non­trivial.
Markers identified in one study or dataset may not always generalize well to others[275].
Another fundamental challenge of cell type annotation is the very concept of cell type itself.
Biologically, cell types refer to the classification of cells based on their specific character­
istics such as structure, function and gene expression. Although these classifications are
discrete, the variables used to assign them are continuous. The structure, function and
gene expression patterns of a cell are highly variable across its lifetime[348]. For exam­
ple, where do you place the cut­off between a circulating monocyte and a fully mature
tissue­resident macrophage? The development process from the former to the latter is
continuous[349], and in reality, the concept of cell type should more be regarded as a
multi­dimensional spectrum between different differentiation states[348]. This idea reigns
particularly true for M2 macrophages, since these are in reality macrophages at one end
of a spectrum of functions available to macrophages (with M1 being at the opposite end
of this spectrum)[98].
The clustering algorithms employed in the processing of single­cell data are of course
not aware of this and a cluster that looks like a unique cell type might just be a mix of
cells under similar activation states, metabolic conditions or cell cycle states, all of which
greatly influence the gene expression patterns of cells[275].

Even if the amount of SIGLEC15 mRNA is over­expressed in AML conditions and is as­
sociated with immune regulatory cell types, the entire premise of evaluating a peptide
vaccine target based on transcriptomic data is somewhat faulty, since there are multi­
ple steps between the presence of an mRNA molecule and a successful T­cell­mediated
immune response[350].
RNA presence does not equal protein presence. Correlations between mRNA and protein
abundance for most genes have correlation coefficients typically falling in the range of 0.2
to 0.5[351]. This well­documented discordance arises from complex layers of biological
regulation, including variable mRNA stability, differential translation efficiency, and highly
variable protein degradation rates and half­lives. Some of this mis­match is explained
by the sensitivity of the method used for measuring the protein levels when making the
comparison[352], but we remain on the topic of RNA here.
If an mRNA’s half­life is so short that it prevents protein synthesis, it would almost certainly
fall below the detection limits of transcriptomic analysis. Consequently, such cases can­
not explain the discordance observed between quantifiable mRNA and their correspond­
ing proteins[353]. Protein degradation likely explains more of this discrepancy[353], but
for the purpose of peptide vaccines, degraded protein counts as present protein, since
all intracellular protein must first be degraded by the proteasome before it can even be
presented as peptides[283].
Translational efficiency, however, can be a problem when utilizing transcriptomics to val­
idate protein targets. Under certain conditions, the mRNA molecule can be present, but
yield no protein at all. Such examples include sequestering of mRNA molecules into
stress granules[354], regulation of translation by 5’ up­stream open reading frames[355]
and binding of the mRNA by RNA­binding proteins[356]. Consequently, observing high
SIGLEC15 mRNA levels doesn’t always guarantee that the SIGLEC15 protein is present
at correspondingly high levels[357].
Protein presence does not equal peptide presentation. For a peptide vaccine to be effec­
tive, the target protein must not only be synthesized but must also be properly processed
by the proteasome, and the resulting peptide fragments must be transported by the TAP
machinery into the endoplasmic reticulum, where they must successfully bind to the pa­
tient’s specific HLA molecules with sufficient affinity and stability to be presented on the
cell surface[358]. RNA sequencing provides no information about any of these down­
stream events.
Even if a peptide is bound by an HLA molecule, it is not guaranteed to be immunogenic,
and a response toward it can be suppressed by central or peripheral tolerance mecha­
nisms. This is where other aspects of vaccine design, like adjuvant usage, come into
play[359]
As seen from the in silico peptide binding prediction, only relatively weak binders, con­
cerning HLA­a*01:01, could be identified in the conserved region of siglec­15, indicating
that it is perhaps not a good target in populations where this allele is dominant. This anal­
ysis was limited to a single Class I allele, and the contribution of HLA­A, ­B, and ­C alleles
should not be neglected. The focus here was also exclusively on CD8+ T­cell epitopes,
but the activation of CD4+ helper T cells via MHC Class II presentation is also important
for a durable anti­tumor response[283].
In conclusion, this thesis represents a preliminary exploration of SIGLEC15 as a novel
immunotherapeutic target in AML and sets the stage for the developed software tool to
be used for the investigation of other genes that are of interest to the expert oncologist.
