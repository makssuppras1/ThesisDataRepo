Abstract
This MSc Thesis deals with mathematical modelling of strategies in harvesting
of renewable biological resources, in particular harvesting of fish populations.
Since too aggressive harvesting may have severe consequences, a cautious con-
trol of the process is called for.
The determination of optimal harvesting
based on the biomass size is made even more challenging since there is consid-
erable uncertainty in the estimation of the stock size, the so-called incomplete
information problem.
The fishery management is in this project established as an optimal control
problem for a model based on nonlinear stochastic differential equations, with
economic performance as objective. The so-called certainty equivalence princi-
ple, by which the estimate is used for the purposes of optimal feedback control
as if it were the certain value of the state variable, is adopted. Markov con-
trols are identified by solving the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation. State
estimates are obtained by a Hidden Markov Model filter, where the forward
Kolmogorov equation governs the temporal evolution of estimates with the
uncertain quantities.
Fishery profit over time as economic performance is
computed by Monte Carlo simulations, in order to compare the performances
of the strategies considering control rules or precautionary approach.
Two control rules in harvest policy – harvest control rule and effort control
rule – are compared, with respect to their robustness given the uncertainty.
The effort control rule produces a significantly higher cumulated profits, and
is less sensitive to the uncertainty in stock assessment present in the system.
Moreover, a precautionary approach taking the uncertainty associated with
biomass estimates into account, can be achieved via quantile estimators. This
approach produces an economic gain by making a more appropriately cautious
decision, and leads to sustainable harvesting of fish resources.

Dansk Abstrakt
Dette M.Sc. Projekt omhandler matematisk modellering af strategier for høst
af selvfornyende biologiske resourcer, specielt høst af fiskebestande. Da alt
for aggresiv høst kan have alvorlige konsekvenser, er det nødvendigt at pro-
cessen er omhyggeligt kontrolleret. Bestemmelsen af en optimal høstpolitik
baseret på bestandens størrelse er specielt udfordrende idet der foreligger
betydelig usikkerhed omkring bestandens størrelse, det såkaldte ukomplette
informationsproblem.

lem for en model baseret på ikke-lineære stokastiske differentialligninger, med
økonomisk udkomme som objektivfunktion. Det såkaldte visheds-ækvivalens
princip, ud fra hvilket estimater bruges i optimal feedback kontrol som om
de var sikre værdier af tilstandsvariable, benyttes. Markov kontroller identifi-
ceres fra løsninger til Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman ligningen. Tilstandsestimater
udregnes ved hjælp af et Skjult Markov Model filter, hvor den fremadrettede
Kolmogorov ligning styrer tidsudviklingen af de optimale estimater af de usikre
størrelser. Fiskeriindtjening hen over tid, som et mål for økonomisk ydeevne,
beregnes ved hjælp af Monte Carlo simuleringer, for at sammenligne strategier
med hensyn til kontrolregler eller forsigtighedsprincipper.
To kontrolregler for fangstpolitik – høstkontrol og aktivitetskontrol – bliver
sammenlignet med hensyn til deres robusthed, givet usikkerheden. Aktivitet-

giver en økonomisk fordel ved at tage passende forsigtige beslutninger, således
at fiskeriet forbliver bæredygtigt.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Optimal control is a very useful tool in the analysis of many real-world dy-
namical systems, such as aerospace, electrical and mechanical engineering. To
improve system performance requires more and more accurate modelling. If a
model is a good representation of the real system, it is often nonlinear. Many
control systems occurring in practice are subject to imperfectly known distur-
bances, and stochastic optimal control theory attempts to deal with models in
which random system disturbances are present. In describing a control model,
the kind of information available to the decision-maker at each instant time
plays an important role. A standard assumption is that they are observed
completely. However, this is rarely the case in real-world situations.
There is an growing demand for consumption of natural resources, such as
renewable resources, like fish or timber. As a consequence, optimal utilization
of natural resources is becoming increasingly important. Overfishing can lead
to economic collapse of fisheries and even biological collapse of the stock. The
collapse of Northwest Atlantic cod stock is an example, where mortality due
to fishing is a factor in the failure of the stock to recover [1]. Fishery resource
management is subject to a variety of unobservable and nonlinear processes,
which is a challenge for fisheries science, when aiming to maintain the stock
for sustained future harvesting.
State of art
Fish population dynamics is reasonably well described as a stochastic process.
Fisheries management is posed as a Markov control problem: how to choose
the catch rate. Such an optimization problem is solved by the Hamilton-Jacobi-
Bellman (HJB) equation, as presented in [2]. This is viewed as a stochastic
optimal control problem under complete information, where the state of the
system can be directly measured. In comparsion, its counterpart under incom-
plete information, where only noisy observations can be obtained, is substan-

optimal control, the separation principle [18] [19] is valid. For general cases,
the problem may in principle be turned into a complete information optimal

Two sources of uncertainty are important factors in the problem of over-
fishing. The first is variability in fish dynamics, which comes from the envi-
ronmental variations resulting in fluctuations of parameters. The second is
inaccurate stock size estimates, which means that the sampling error leads to
an incorrect catch quota. These sources of random variation in the fishery
are mentioned in [4]. Using dynamic programming as a tool, it is claimed
in [6] that uncertainty in measurements may have the greatest potential to
affect fishery policy. Also, fishery-dependent data is one of the factors that
contributes to the uncertainty in stock assessment results, as mentioned in [7].
In practice, the so-called precautionary approach1 (PA) [8] [9] strives to be
more cautious when information is less certain, and for the sake of precaution
a lower-than-optimum mortality is used. Therefore, the estimation of stock
status in a system is viewed as a key step.
Based on stochastic differential equations (SDE), which is a central mathe-
matical topic in this thesis, there are several estimation methods, each having
pros and cons. The popular method for handling nonlinearity is to approxi-
mate the solutions with Gaussians, for instance the Extended Kalman filter
(EKF) [12]. It is useful only if the system is close to being linear and if the
non-linearity is weak. The accuracy of this technique will however decrease
for large operating range of nonlinear systems. Higher-order approaches [13]
may give better results if the system nonlinearities are especially severe, but
it is more involved and requires more computational time. An alternative ap-
proach to dealing with the nonlinear filter problem is Template Model Builder
(TMB) [14]. Numerical optimization of likelihood functions is a powerful tech-
nique, but it is limited to situations where the posterior distribution is well
approximated by a Gaussian. In practical complex cases, the transition den-
sity used to derive the likelihood function does not have a closed form, and the

The certainty equivalence principle (CE) [16] [17] is adopted in this project.
It emphasizes the fact that the estimate is used for the purposes of optimal
feedback control as if it were the certain value of the state variable. The setup
for the control problem is depicted in figure 1.1. Here the HMM filter is an
approach to estimate the states in the nonlinear exploited fisheries models.
The optimal control strategy is identified by applying dynamic programming,
which reduces to solving the HJB equation.
Certainty equivalence means that the full information control law is ap-
plied with the certain state replaced by its estimate, due to different choices
of estimators. In this way, the control is influenced by the uncertainty on es-
timate and further on to the system performance. The expected fishery profit
obtained over an infinite time is considered as the objective, to be computed
by a Monte Carlo simulation. The relative performance of different control
rules to meet the fishery objective are affected differently by uncertainty in
the estimated stock sizes. The economic performance will be computed from
some specified control rules: harvest control rule (HCR) controlling the catch
rate or effort control rule (ECR) regulating fishing mortality rate. In addition,
precautionary approach (PA) has emphasis on attempting to react with cau-
tion to uncertainties, when deciding the optimal fishing. It can be achieved
by estimators, which is considered in this project as well. Mathematical ques-
tions like how uncertainty on estimates will affect the optimal harvesting, and
how precautionary an approach is optimal are of importance. Specifically, this
project will address:
• which control rule is more robust with respect to uncertainty;
• how various estimators can mitigate the loss due to incomplete informa-
tion by optimally incorporating the uncertainty into the control mecha-
nism.

CHAPTER 2
State estimation
One important application of stochastic differential equations (SDE) is the
estimation problem. In some cases only the observed time series Yt from a
system is available at a certain time t, and it gives some information about
the current state Xt. To obtain information about the true state, the aim
is to find the distribution of states, conditioned on the measurement. The
approach will be presented here using now-casting or on-line estimation. It
is about how to estimate a state from the previous measurements and based
on a Hidden Markov Model approach [10]. In a Hidden Markov Model, the
state process is discretized to constitute a discrete-time Markov process, and
the measurements are viewed as a known process. The states, as an unknown
process, are inferred from the measurements.
2.1
Process model and Observation model
The model needs to be specified. The process model and the observation model
are formulated as system equation and measurement equation, respectively.
Consider the diffusion process governed by an autonomous non linear
stochastic differential equation (SDE). The states of a system at time t are
described in the Itô interpretation [10] [18]
dXt = f(Xt) dt + g(Xt) dBt,
X0 = x, t ∈[0, ∞),
(2.1)
where Xt ∈Rn is state vector. The function f : Rn →Rn is the drift term,
and g : Rn →Rn×m is the diffusion term. They satisfy the conditions for
existence and uniqueness [10] of a solution to this SDE for a given initial
condition X0. Here, Bt denotes a m-dimensional Brownian motion. Together
with the diffusion term g(·), they constitute the system noise ηt.

CHAPTER 3
Dynamic optimization
Another application for stochastic differential equations (SDE) is decision mak-
ing. The aim here is to achieve some stated objective by affecting (controlling)
the system dynamics. The technique used here to solve the stochastic control
problem is dynamic programming. The dynamic programming theorem [10]
states that the Markov Decision Problem [10] is solved by iteratively breaking
the complex optimization problem into simpler sub-problems. The solution
progresses backward in time, starting from the terminal condition. During this
process both the optimal control strategy and the value function at each state
and time are identified. Some examples of discrete dynamic programming in
deterministic and stochastic cases are given in [20].
3.1
Statement of stochastic control problem
Now the structure of the Markov Decision Problem is applied to dynamic
optimization for diffusion problems.

7.1
Numerical methods and Algorithms
One stochastic differential equation (SDE) and two partial differential eq-
uitations (PDE) need to be solved.
The non-dimensional stochastic Pella-
Tomlinson (NDPT) model (6.9) governs the biomass process {Xt|t ≥0}, the
forward Kolmogorov equation (5.13) with the time-variant operator (5.14) gov-
erns the evolution of the predicted probability density function φt, and the
stationary Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation (5.7) decides the control
rule function α.
Numerical methods
For the NDPT model (6.9), there exist some numerical schemes to solve the
SDE, such as the Euler-Maruyama scheme, the Milstein scheme and the Heun
scheme. Other numerical schemes can be seen in [42]. Since the objective of
the control problem is formulated to maximize economic gain, not only the
individual sample path is of interest, but also the quantity of the expected
running payoff γ(α,β) (6.12).
Both the strong and week order of schemes
are relevant. The Euler-Maruyama scheme and the Milstein scheme have the
same convergence order [10] [15]. The Euler-Maruyama scheme is easily imple-
mented in general. Since the model is scalar, the multiple stochastic integrals
of Brownian motion are avoided, and this allows the Milstein scheme to be sim-
plified. The Heun scheme is consistent with the Stratonovich interpretation
[10] [18] of SDE. If the noise intensity is constant, the Itô and Stratonovich

7.2.1

Spatial grid sizes
There are two grid sizes, which are related to the numerical results. One is
involved in finding the generator to solve the forward Kolmogorov equation,
and the other one influences the solution of the stationary HJB equation.
First, to find the generator of the forward Kolmogorov equation, a non-
equidistant grid with N1 grid cells or grid points is used. They can be ex-
pressed as
x0 = 0,
xj = (jdx)2, j = 1, 2, . . . , N1 −2,
xN1−1 = L1.
Since the biomass process {Xt|t ≥0} is restricted to the positive subset of R,
the origin is a lower boundary point of the domain. For numerical solutions,
some conditions must be imposed on an artificial boundary, x = L1. This
upper boundary L1 must be sufficiently large in order to retain a precise solu-
tion in the state space. For computational efficiency, this value is considered
be to some extent larger than the maximal value of estimate ˆX(β)
t
. For the
various values of the shape parameter p, this maximum value is not necessary
the same: the upper boundary L1 depends on the growth function form. Fur-

Similar results in literature
Results similar to some of those presented in the previous chapter have also
been mentioned in the literature, even if other mathematical approaches were
applied. For instance, it is emphasized in the paper by Sethi et al. [6] that
imprecise stock estimation affects fishery policy significantly. A higher mea-
surement error lowers the level at which the fishery is shut down. Figure 8.2
has revealed a very similar causal relationship. As the measurement noise level
increases, the evaluated optimal control is less precise. It leads to a large loss
in the optimal reward rate, and it may even go to zero when the observation
error reaches a certain level. Also, the paper by Engen et al. [45] analyses
the effect of uncertainty in the population estimates. By adopting threshold
harvesting based on the estimated population size, the optimal threshold typ-
ically increases as the uncertainty increases. There is therefore a substantial
loss in the expected cumulative yield. A similar result is also seen in figure
8.2.
Furthermore, [45] applies a fixed fraction of the difference between the
population estimate and the threshold, is harvested. It is concluded that this
method is preferable to pure threshold harvesting, when the uncertainty in the
estimate exceeds some value. A similar result has been seen in section 8.3.2.
When the measurement noise level is relatively high, i.e. σY = 8, the economic

Population dynamic model
Combining the results under both complete and incomplete information, the
expected running payoff is impacted negatively by both system noise and
measurement noise. From the simulation it seems that both types of noise
have a large economic influence when the system has left skewed drift. For
different growth function forms, the explanation is not clear. This topic is
beyond the scope of this project and is left to be explored in future work.
In addtion, the population dynamic used is a single species model, where
the growth is a function of aggregate stock. Although oversimplified models
may outperform more realistic ones in implementing some closed-loop decision
rules, the estimates of exploitable biomass based on oversimplified models
may be too optimistic and should always not be used as a basis for policy
evaluation, as stated in [37]. More complex and realistic models, such as the
multispecies models could be pursued. Regarding high dimensional problems,
the estimation approach like TMB could be a powerful technique instead of
HMM filter where the forward Kolmogorov equation is involved. However, the
numerical solution of the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation will be difficult,
which is also mentioned in [2]. Future work may clarify this issue.
Control time step size
Let dt denote the simulation time step for the biomass. For simplicity, the
sampling time step for obervation and the control time step to determine catch
rate are set as equal, denoted ∆t. Either an instantaneous control (∆t = dt)
or a piecewise constant control (∆t > dt) could be reasonable. On the one
hand, the Markov strategy obtained by solving the HJB equation is based the
continuous time model. On the other hand, in fishery management the stock

assessment is based on data which is collected regularly each year. The total
allowable catch (TAC) in the following year will then be derived as a function
of the estimated stock size [7]. For mathematical interest of extending the
results from [2], instantaneous control has been adopted in this project.
Had piecewise constant control been used, the results seen in the previous
chapter would probably still be valid. In [32] a comparison of the performance
between the variable effort policy and the stepwise effort policy is conducted
under complete information.
This study concludes that the variable effort
policy is an expensive task and inapplicable from a practical point of view,
since it is not feasible to obtain information on population size all the time.
In contrast to this, the stepwise effort policy has the advantage of being ap-
plicable, but it is a sub-optimal policy. Under incomplete information, if a
piecewise constant control had been utilized in the system, it is not surprising
that it would cause deviation in the key performance indicator. This is rela-
vant for a stepwise non-anticipative modification [32], which means that the
decision is made without future values of the fish population size in the inter-
val, since they are unknown at that time. A further work could investigate
the behaviour of a decrease of the economic performance as the control time
step increases, i.e. the shape of decrease.
Alternative method of precautionary approach
Alternatively, the uncertainty from estimate can be incorporated into the con-
trol mechanism by using adjusted estimate.
This works because a smaller
value of the state estimate propagates less uncertainty in the evaluated opti-
mal control. It is indicated in [46] that the risk adjustment factor is important
in reducing the target fishing mortality because of errors in the estimate of
exploitable biomass.
Thus a more conservative control might be favoured, and this can be
achieved for example by the adjusted estimate ˆX(α,β)
t
exp(−λσt).
The ad-
justment parameter λ can be viewed as a measure of precaution, and σt is
the standard deviation of the state estimate at time t. The adjusted state
estimate is reduced to account for uncertainty. The aim of this method is to
select the optimal adjustment parameter λ∗, so as to maximize the expected
running payoff. Through simulation, it seems that the improvement from this
method is 3.22%, close to that by the quantile estimator found in this project.

Real-life fishery management
The results obtained so far are under specific noise parameter values.
In
order to put into perspective real-life fishery management problems from the
standpoint of the model used in this project, process noise and observation
error in the real-life problem should be quantified.
From the dataset, the

Uncertainty in parameters of the dynamics
The effect of imperfect information from stock size is examined in this project.
A fisheries management problem, based on population dynamics described by
a stochastic model, may face the challenge that the parameter values are not
always known a priori. This kind of problem might be serious for more com-
plicated models. The mathematical model is studied in [5] to find the optimal
harvesting policy, where the incompleteness of information is due to uncer-
tainties involved in the body growth rate of the fishery resource. Here, the
body growth rate is assumed to be regime switching, because of the temporal
dynamics of the main food source. The computational result suggests that
an accurate identification of the body growth is critical for efficient fisheries
management, based on the optimal harvesting policy. So studying the effect
of uncertainty in the dynamical parameters of the fishery resource could be
another further work.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
This project has described the basic framework for a stochastic optimal control
analysis under incomplete information, in the form of the uncertainty in stock
assessment. Pre-specified optimal strategy without uncertainty is applied by
using the estimates with error, and the cumulated profit is therefore affected
by the uncertainty in estimates.
First of all, the comparison between two
control rules in harvest policy was done to investigate their robustness with
respect to the uncertainty. These two control rule functions are: the harvest
control rule function which attempts to control the catch rate; and the effort
control rule function which through fishing effort regulates fish mortality rate.
Secondly, a precautionary approach has been achieved via various estimators,
to investigate the improvement by optimally incorporating the uncertainty
into the control mechanism. These comparisons were conducted in terms of
the key performance indicator for the available information.
The stochastic Pella-Tomlinson model was applied as the exploited biomass
model, and the observation model was formulated as a log-normal distribution
related to the population. The running reward was calculated as the difference
between the revenues formulated from price structure and costs of operating
fisheries. The expected running payoff was computed by a Monte Carlo sim-
ulation. During the simulation, the Heun scheme was used to simulate the
biomass according to the stochastic surplus production model. The optimal
catch rate as Markov control was determined by inserting the biomass esti-
mate into the feedback control. First, the biomass estimate was yielded from
the estimated probability density function using the estimators. Here, the
estimated density was obtained from the Hidden Markov Model filter, where
the predicted probability density function and the state likelihood function
are involved. In the HMM filter, the predicted density was from the control
associated forward Kolmogorov equation, solved by the implicit Euler method.
Second, the feedback control was governed by the control rule function, which
was derived from the solution to the stationary Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman

The key uncertainty source results in an imprecisely evaluated optimal
catch rate, which can cause large economic fluctuations, and even biological
collapse. As the measurement noise level increases, the lack of optimal reward
rate due to the cost of incomplete information becomes large.
The main
contributions from the work are summarized here:
• The effort control rule leads to a lower cost of incomplete information
in comparison with the harvest control rule. This implies that the ef-
fort control rule is less sensitive to the uncertainty in stock assessment
present in the system, and therefore is usable with the inaccurate data.
Using the effort control rule, the evaluated optimal catch rate is influ-
enced linearly by the uncertainty of the biomass estimate for the specific
growth model. For more general growth functions, the conditional vari-
ance of the evaluated catch rate for given biomass is smaller than that
caused by the harvest control rule. These features make the effort con-
trol rule produce a significantly higher cumulated profits. Selecting the
right control rule will facilitate effective management plans in fishery.
• The precautionary approach makes an economic gain by making a ap-
propriately conservative decision with respect to the uncertainty asso-
ciated with biomass estimates. If the biomass estimates are too high
which often results in an aggressive catch rate, this may lead to risk
of overexploitation and biological extinction. Conversely, when popula-
tion estimates are too low, which leads to a conservative catch rate, the
profit will decline and the fishery may be closed unnecessarily. Quantile
estimators are an effective mathematical method to achieve a precau-
tionary approach, since the level of caution is more precise which gives
the biomass estimate more accurately. For a given measurement noise
level, it seeks the optimal estimate which maximizes the expected run-
ning payoff over an infinite time. The improvement in estimation would
not only lead to sustainable harvesting of fish resources, but also help
to offset the expense used to obtain precise data.
In addition, to evaluate the performance in response to alternative descriptions
of the growth function, one lower and one higher shape parameters than the
original value have been considered. These alternative cases share the same
