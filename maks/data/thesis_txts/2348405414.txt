Abstract
The present thesis investigates numerically resonant properties of two- and three-dimensional active
coated nano-particles.
The two-dimensional particles posses cicularly cylindrical shape, while the
three-dimensional particles come in the form of spherical as well as truncated cylindrical geometries.
The coated nano-particles will be composed of silica (SiO2) dielectric and silver (Ag) plasmonic ma-
terials. When the core is made of silica and the shell is that of silver, the CNP is referring to SiO2-Ag
conﬁguration. In the other hand, when the core is made of silver and the shell is that of silica, the
CNP is called Ag-SiO2 conﬁguration. At the interface between a dielectric and plasmonic material, the
incident ﬁeld will give rise to surface plasmon polariton and hence the resonant properties. However,
there are losses in plasmonic materials, which can be overcome by a gain media. The gain is imple-
mented in the dielectic part of the particle and is that of canonical gain model. The two-dimensional
cases serve the purpose of initial validation of established numerical models in Comsol Multiphysics
as well as estimating the amount of gain and resonance frequency for novel three-dimension particles.
For spherical geometry, both concentric and eccentric designs will be investigated. For the truncated
cylindrical geometry, ﬁrst the eﬀect of truncation in the cylindrical nano-particles on the resonance
wavelength and the amount of gain will be adressed. Subsequently, the eﬀect of eccentricity on the po-
tential directive properties of these nano-antennas will be examined. Moreover, structural assymetries
will be introduced in selected three-dimensional nano-particles with the purpose of exciting higher
order modes which are necessary for the enhancement of their directivities. While the two-dimension
geometries will be excited by a plane wave as well as magnetic line source, the three-dimension ge-
ometries will be excited by a plane wave.
The resonant properties of all geometries will be thoroughly assessed through common parameters
such as radiated power, scattering width and cross section, as well as near-ﬁeld and power ﬂow dis-
tribution. Such a detailed axamination with reveal the potential of active coated nano-particles to
function as eﬀective nano-antennas.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nano-particles (NPs) are of interest for diﬀerent applications such of nano-sensors, , lasers, spasers,
lenses, invisible and nano-circuits [58]- [18] because of theirs sizes and optical properties. In addition
they can also be used as Nano-antennas that can be implemented to for instance cars. The criteria
for such antennas is that they might be light and the amount wireless communication will increase in
the future.
In previous work coated NPs (CNPs ) considered by a plasmonic material (such of silver and gold)
and silica have been investigated.
Desipite the potential applications, the nano-particles consisting of plasmonic material such of silver,
gold and copper are hindered by the ohmic loss. To overcome the losses a gain might be implemented.
This is done by combined the plasmonic material with a dielectric media; such NPs are called coated
nano-particles (CNPs).
Another advantage of CNPs is the ability of tunability meaning that the
resonance frequency can be changed by the core-to shell ratio. In the previous work two-dimension
cylindrical concentric active and passive coated nano-particles (CNPs) have been investigated [65].
They consist of a silica (SiO2)-core and Ag-shell. This thesis will take the starting point of the work.
The size-dependence of silver as well as frequency-dependence permittivity will be considered. The
gain model is that of canonical for simplicity. In reality the gain medium can either be electrically
injected or optically pumped. However, 2D structures can not be manufactured, so to make them
more realistic they will be implemented in 3D as truncated cylinders and thus the impact will be
investigated. In addition diﬀerent hights of truncation will be investigated to ﬁgure whether there
is a tendency for the resonance frequency and the amount of gain. However, the CNPs in the work
[65] radiate a dipole, which are not directive, so to investigate whether they are directive, the core
is displced. In addtion, there will alwasy be manufactoring error, so the eccentric CNPs does also
compromise with that. However, when the (SiO)2-core is displaced in the Ag-shell, the thickness will
be varying and it follows to doubtfull results. Therefore, the reverse conﬁguration will be considered
instead; that is a Ag-core and (SiO)2-shell. Another approach for achieving directivity is cutting the
shell and make the CNP assymetric. The thesis covers 5 chapters. Chapter 2 introduce shapes and
types of NPs and their optical properties. In addition diﬀerent NPs that give rise to directivity will
be discussed as well. Chapter 3 analyse the material parameters considered for these NPs that does
impact the optical properties. Chapter 4 show the results for optical properties in 2D models and
verify the implementation by comparing with previous results. Chapter 5 review the simulating result
3D achieving from Comsol Multiphysics.

Chapter 2
Nano-particles and theirs applications
This chapter will introduce some shapes of nanoparticles (NPs) and discuss some of their optical
properties related to the scattering, absorption and extinction eﬃciencies. These optical properties
make them candidate for diﬀerent applications which will be discussed in the following sections.
Plasmonic materials have a lot of loss, so this chapter will also show how gain implementations aﬀect
the mentioned eﬃciencies as well as the radiated ﬁelds for a type of NPs called coated nano-particle
(CNP). However, CNPs having the core in the origin of the shell are not directive since they radiate
as a dipole. Therefore, the last section will show some conﬁgurations to achieve directive CNPs.

2.2. Applications
Because of the optical properties discussed in the previous section, NPs can used in many applications
such of sensors, lasers, spasers, SERS, nantenna, displays etc. This report will discuss a few of these.
2.2.1. Drug delivery and imaging
In section 2.1.2 it was seen how proper adjustments of the core-to-shell ratio can make the CNPs
as greater scatterers or absorbers. This is an important knowledge when designing CNps for drug
delivery and imaging. As shown in Figure 2.9 the absorbers (thinner shells) are used in drug delivery,
while the scatterers (thicker shells) are used in imaging [8]. For the drug delivery (ﬁgure 2.9 a), the
CNPs can either encapsulate or adsorb drugs by a functional group or electrostatic stabilization and be
embedded in a polymer that has a melting temperature slightly above the body temperature. When
the CNPs are illuminated by a laser at theirs resonance frequency, the network will be heated up and
then collapsed, so the drug is released.

Several work have discussed the impedance of NPs and compared
these to the passive components such as resistors, inductors and
capacitors as seen in Figure 2.10 [17]-[19]. This means that NPs
can be used for nanocircuits. When an electric ﬁeld is applied
a voltage and current will be excited, these are given by V =
El and I = jωεES, respectively, where E is the electric ﬁeld
strength, l is the length of the NP, ω is the angular frequency,
ε is the permittivity of the material and S is the cross section
of the NPs. Thus, the impedance of the NPs are given by Z =
l/(jωεS). All the quantities, but ε will always be larger than
zero. However, the real part of the permittivity can be positive
for dielectric media and negative for plasmonic materials (gold,
silver and copper) at optical frequencies. The imaginary part of
the permittivity simply represent the loss and hence can be seen
as a resistor. For positive permittivity, the impedance of the NPs
looks similar to the impedance of a capacitor, ZC = 1/(jωC),
where C = εS/l is the capacitance.

2.2.3. Plasmonic solar cells
The aim of solar cells is to convert sun light energy to electricity by photovoltaic eﬀect. As illustrated in
Figure 2.11 (a) [20] there are three generations of solar cells. The ﬁrst generation consists of crystalline
semiconductor wafers that have a thickness of 200-300 µm. However, these solar cells were expensive
so the purpose of the second generation was to reduce the cost. These kind of solar cells are based on
thin-ﬁlm technology of cheap substrates such as glass, plastic or stainless steel and have a thickness
with 1-2 µm. However, the second generation is ineﬀective so the goal with the third generation is
increasing the ability to absorb light energy to electrical energy based on the cheap material used in
the second generation.
An example of improving the absorption is adding NPs on the solar cells as illustrated in Figure 2.11
(b) [21]. The semiconductor layer and the substrate are called the active layer or the reﬂector in other
publications [22]. The active layer is a mixing of two materials where one is conductive polymers that
acts as light absorber and electron donor, while the other is fullerene (derivative) that acts as electron
acceptor [23], [24]. The substrate has to reﬂect the light to the active layer so the light will be further
absorbed.

3.1. Dispersion models
According to Bohrs model, atoms consist of a nucleus and circulating electrons. For dielectric materials
the electrons are bounded to the atom and the phenomena is described by the Lorentz dispersion
model. In the other hand, for metals there is a single electron in the valence band that can break the
bound to the atom when an electric ﬁeld is applied an hence become a free electron; the dispersion
model that is used for these electrons is called the Drude dispersion model. However, metals have
also inner electrons which only will oscillate in their orbit and hence are bounded to the atom. So,
the full description of metals is a substitution of the Lorentz and the Drude dispersion model. In
addition, the free electrons will collide against the boundary to another material and in comparison
with bulk metals, the loss will diﬀer signiﬁcant for small varying of the nano-sized metals; therefore
the size-dependence is considered as well.

3.2. Gain model

loss in the plasmonic materials. The gain in this report is represent by the extinction coeﬃcient κ

permittivity is given by εr = (n −jκ)2 = n2 −κ2 −j2nκ and thus, a frequency independent material
is considered as canonical model.

along the x-axis. With the time factor exp(jωt), the electric ﬁeld will become:

electric conductor (PEC) as well as perfect magnetic conductor (PMC) are implemented.

This section will discuss the realistic gain, mentioned in section 3.2. As seen in Figure A.1 (a), three
diﬀerent sizes of QDs will radiate at three diﬀerent frequencies [42]. For instance, a QD consisting of

CdSe will illuminate the colors red, green and blue at the diameters of 7.5 nm, 2.5 nm and 2,1 nm,
respectively [43]. In addition to the ﬁgure A.1 (a), the energylevels for each of the three QDs are
included and unlike the bulk semiconductor, the energy levels are discreet and hence are characterized
as atomic like states [44]. If there is two energy levels, e.g. the bulk semiconductors that have a valence
and conducting band, four phenomenas called absorption, spontaneous emission, stimulated emission
and spontaneous saturation can occur [45]-[46]. Absorption means that an atom will be excited from
the valence band to the conducting band when a photon is absorbed, while the spontaneous emission is
the case, where the atom will decay to the valence band and emits a photon. The spontaneous emission
means that an incoming photon will stimulate the atom from the conducting band to the valence band
and two photons will be emitted. Saturation means that the absorption and stimulated emission does
compensate each other and the material will become transparent. However, such materials does not
produce gain since it requires the phenomena called population inversion, which can occur in materials
that has at least three levels [47]-[48]. Consider a materials that has a three energy levels, which are
named level 1, level 2 and level 3 with the energies E1, E2 and E3 as well as the number of atoms
N1, N2 and N3 as seen in Figure A.1 (b). Level 1 and 2 are also called the ground and metastable
state as,respectively [49]. Assume that the energy levels are E3 > E2 > E1 and N3 = N2 ≈0, while
the number of atoms at the ground state is N. When the system is irradiated by a photon of a
certain frequency, a large number of atoms at the ground level will be pumped to level 3 such that
N3 > 0. The atoms will not keep at this energy level for a long time, so they immediately decay to
the level 2 and keep there for a longer time than at level 3. This transition will not radiate photons
because of loss. Thus, the number of atoms at each levels are now N3 ≈0 and N2 > N1. This case is
called population inversion. For a number of atoms that decays to the ground state by spontaneous
emission, the same number of photons will be emitted and this phenomena will give rise to gain, which
in this thesis is denoted by the extinction coeﬃcient κ. Thus, to get a realistic gain the materials
for QDs that can achieve the the amount of needed gain might be found. Consequently, there is a
relation between the energy level and the frequency given by the Planck-Einstein relation, E = hf,
where h and f are the Planck constant and frequency, respectively. This might be considered, when
designing more realistic QDs, but the energy modelling is not that simple since the solution is based
on Schrodinger equation, where the dispersion model are considered as well; however, the gain has the
optimum about the same energy level, so canonical modelling is fairly description for the gain media
[50].
