Summary
Abstract
The main target of distribution operators is to reduce the customers’ interruptions
and energy losses for the purpose of achieving optimal network utilization. Network
reconfiguration proved to be an effective technique to accomplish this goal. It happens
by changing the status of switches that are either normally closed or normally open.
Many benefits can be potentially achieved by changing the open/close status of the
feeder switches in different operational conditions such as loss minimization, redirecting
the power flow to avoiding overloading, power outage restoration, improving the system
reliability, etc.
This project aims at assessing the potential value of network reconfiguration on a
selected 10 kV Danish distribution system in North Zealand. Different case studies were
composed and simulated through the software NEPLAN so as to define to what extent
the use of reconfiguration can be beneficial with respect to avoid critical grid loads, re-
duce outage time and grid losses. Influential factors that affect the potential value of
network reconfiguration in normal and fault operation were taken into account. Loss-
and cost- reducing potential by reconfiguration was investigated at different frequencies
over the year, using real data for the temporal variation of demand profile and energy
prices, while also taking forecasts into account. The results highlighted the benefits of
reconfiguration regarding the reduction of power losses in all the examined scenarios.
This reduction proved to be related to the frequency of reconfiguration and the load
distribution throughout the year. It is worth mentioning that voltage and line loading
performance was improved, although the objective of the analysis was the minimiza-
tion of power losses. Finally, it was noticed that the investment on automated switch
technology could be economically attractive in the future, taking into account current
forecasts.
Keywords: distribution network, network reconfiguration, loss reduction, NEPLAN

Summary
Resumé
Det vigtigste mål for distributions-operatører er at reducere kundernes strømafbry-
delser og energitab med det formål at opnå en optimal netværk udnyttelse. Det er be-
vist, at netværks-konfigurering er en effektiv teknik til at opnå dette mål. Det opnås ved
at ændre status af afbrydere, som enten normalt er lukkede eller åbne. Mange fordele
kan potentielt opnås ved at ændre åbn/luk status af tilførsels-afbrydere i forskellige
driftsforhold. Dette kan føre til tabsminimering ved at omdirigere strømløbet og derved
undgå overbelastning, strømsvigt restaurering og forbedre systemets pålidelighed, osv.
Dette projekt har til formål at vurdere den potentielle værdi af netværks-rekonfigurering
på et udvalgt 10 kV dansk el-distributionsnet i Nordsjælland. Forskellige casestudier er
behandlet ved brug af softwaren NEPLAN, for at definere i hvilket omfang anvendelsen
af rekonfigurering kan være gavnlig, med hensyn til at undgå kritiske belastninger af el-
distributionsnettet og reducere tiden på strømafbrydelser samt minimere energitab. Ind-
flydelsesrige faktorer, der kan påvirke den potentielle værdi af netværks-rekonfigurering
under normal og fejl drift blev taget i betragtning. Potentialet for minimering af ener-
gitab samt en omkostningseffektiv reduktion ved netværks-rekonfigurering blev under-
søgt ved forskellige frekvenser over året ved hjælp af reelle data for den tidsvariation i
efterspørgslens profil og energipriser. Samtidig er der taget højde for fremtidige prog-
noser af forbrugsprofilen for det givne netværk, samt fremtidige energipriser.
Resul-
taterne viser, at reducering af energitab blev opnået i alle undersøgte scenarier. Det
er blevet påvist, at denne reduktion er relateret til hyppigheden af rekonfigurering og
belastningsfordeling.
Det er værd at nævne, at spænding og linjebelastningen blev
forbedret, selvom formålet med analysen var minimering af energi tab. Til sidst skal
det bemærkes, at investeringen af automatiseret kontakt teknologi kan være økonomisk
attraktivt i fremtiden, med udgangspunkt i nuværende prognoser.
Nøgleord: el-distributionsnet, netværks-rekonfigurering, tabsminimering, NEPLAN

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In this era of smart grids, any concept that is beneficial with respect to the efficiency,
reliability, and cost effectiveness of the power grid is important [1]. The world average
power losses in the electrical network systems are 8.8% and around 7% in Europe [2].
Generally, the practical attainable power losses are around 3% to 6% in an electric power
system [3],[4]. Due to high flowing current, multiplicity of faults and radial structure of
distribution networks, the distribution network is estimated to have higher power loss
and lower reliability among generation and transmission grids [5], [6], [7]. Thus, loss
minimization in distribution systems is considered one of the most essential objectives
for researchers and engineers [5]. Moreover, a potential reduction of electrical losses in
distribution lines can lead toward minimum cost operation and can be accomplished
through a variety of methods [8].
Most distribution feeders are configured radially, to ease their protection coordination
[8].
They contain a number of switches that are normally closed and switches that
are normally open [9]. Those sectionalizing switches are used for both configuration
management, to reconfigure the network in normal operation and for protection, to
isolate a fault [4].
Network reconfiguration is the process of changing the topology
of distribution systems by altering the status of switches [10]. Under normal operation,
distribution engineers periodically reconfigure distribution feeders by opening and closing
of switches in order to increase network reliability and/or decrease losses. During a fault,
some of the switches would change status in order to isolate the faulted network branches
and to transfer part or all of the isolated branches to another feeder or to another
branches of the same feeder. The resulting feeders must remain radial and meet all load
requirements [9]. This technique has been applied in Portugal and experimentally in the
U.S. [11].
While network topology is changed, power losses are affected. Therefore, distribution
operators try to find the optimal configuration topology so as the losses are reduced
and the operational constraints are not violated (i.e., radial structure, voltage limits,
current limits, provided energy in all loads) [5]. Distribution network could be equipped
with remotely tie switches, preferably in every line in order to achieve better flexibility.
Although network reconfiguration can contribute to important savings, high investment
and operational costs appear so as to control the system automatically [12]. Thus, energy
demand and electricity price play a key role in the widespread application of network
reconfiguration.
The trend in Denmark is towards electrifying district heating, individual heating

1.2
Aim of the Research
As it was mentioned before, reconfiguration is essential because often altering the
structure of an electrical grid reduces operational losses according to [9], [16], [17] and
[12]. Voltage or line capacity violation could be avoided by reconfiguration [18]. It could
be also prevent system from high losses and voltage or current violations that caused
by Decentralized Generation (DG) [19]. Moreover, network reconfiguration can provide
service restoration after an outage, as it is proved in [20], [21] and [22].
From all the above, it becomes apparent that the establishment of network recon-
figuration in distribution networks is worth examining.
In this context, the specific
aim of this master thesis project is to explore the effects of network reconfiguration in
power distribution systems in terms of power system minimization, while gaining a gen-
eral understanding on the technical and economic value of network reconfiguration in
distribution networks.
In particular, a real Danish 10 kV distribution grid in North Zealand (DK2) will be
examined within the framework of this project in order to analyze the technical and
economic value of network reconfiguration. Specifically, the project process included:
• A detailed literature review on the topic of Network Reconfiguration.
• Familiarization with software NEPLAN.
• Formulation of the methodology followed for the analysis.
• Deep comprehension of the examined grid provided by DONG energy (DONG).
• Identification of different case studies for the provided grid in cooperation with
experts.
• Implementation of different case studies in software NEPLAN.
• Assessment of huge amount of outputs using Matlab and EXCEL.
• Investigation of the impact of network reconfiguration in respect of network per-
formance and cost over time.
It should be mentioned that all parameters used to assess the value of network recon-
figuration are based on real load and price data, for the specific area, the year 2013. A

2.3
Distribution network
Distribution network covers a large portion of current electrical power systems [26].
It serves the challenging task to distribute electricity efficiently, reliably and cost effec-
tively, while supplying voltage and quality of power within a specific range. Distribution
network is the most suitable system to meet the demand in the place of consumption
by fulfilling customer requirements such as uninterrupted power supply, reliability, and
power quality [26]. Thus, the process to achieve an optimal operation and planning of
an electricity power network could begin from distribution system [26].
In traditional power systems, the distribution networks were designed to be operated
in radial structure and power flows in a single direction [27]. The increasing integration
of DG units in the power grid transformed them from simple systems to complicated
networks with advanced needs for overview and protection. Typical protection problems

Approximately 30% to 40% of total investments in total electrical power systems are

passive to active circuits [29]. Advanced techniques are developed and computational

Reliable distribution of electric power system is of utmost importance for the develop-

2.5
Network Reconfiguration
One of the automation functions of Distribution System is Network Reconfiguration.
In reconfiguration process, the configuration of the distribution system is changing by
altering the status of switches in respect of system constraints [27]. These constraints are
related to active and reactive power satisfaction load demand, voltage limitations and
preserve a radial network structure [8]. The operation of switches affects the electrical
performance of the system by altering the topology of the feeders [36].
The majority of distribution systems functions with a radial topology for technical
reasons, such as, simplifying the grid management and protection, and decreasing current
appearing in case of short-circuit [25]. The main goal of reconfiguration is power loss
reduction, load balancing, optimal loadability and fast system restoration [27].
When a system component fails, some switches should be activated to restore power
to all possible customers. Moreover, as load varies during time, switch actions could
lead to reduced losses and thereby reduce cost of distribution in the power system. Both
operations are applications of reconfiguration [37]. Power outages reduce the service
quality and overstress the systems’ infrastructure. Thus, it is crucial to measure and
prevent such failures effectively [36].
The reduction of loss in distribution network can contribute to significantly less
losses in the whole power system [27]. The power loss in the distribution network is
higher than the one of transmission network since the available currents at distribution
phase are greater than the ones in the transmission. As it was mentioned in Chapter
1, approximately 75% of the electricity is lost in the distribution part [2]. Thus, the
minimization of electricity power loss is the objective in the distribution systems [38].
The configuration in most of the distribution networks is carried out radially, which
leads to a simplified overcurrent protection of feeders [26]. The radial topology is op-
erated although the power systems are physically constructed as interrelated meshed
networks in Europe [39]. Reconfiguration is a complex optimization problem consider-
ing the various operational constraints in large-scale distribution systems. Consequently,
many switches are installed on the distribution network [26].
The implementation of network’s restoration can be conducted through the manage-
ment of electrical switch pairs throughout the power system [40]. The larger the number
of switches, the greater the possibilities of reconfiguration and it is obvious that the
possible switching operations are also higher [26], [41]. For N switches in a network,
there are 2N possible switching combinations [3].
Two types of switches are used in distribution systems for protection; normally open
switches (tie switches) and normally closed switches (sectionalizing). Network recon-
figuration is achieved by closing normally open switches and opening normally closed

Circumstances for Network Reconfiguration
There are two different operating circumstances under which reconfiguration is acti-
vated; normal operation and fault condition. For these circumstances, network reconfig-
uration can be applied due to losses, due to voltage, due to loading, due to overload or
due to outage reduction [42]. Varied algorithms have been suggested to improve voltage
performance, reduce losses and increase power system efficiency and they are analyzed
in [28], [33], [43], [44], [45] and [46]. A comparison of different algorithms, according to
their historic development and evaluation of their results on power systems is outlined
in [4].
In the framework of this project, network reconfiguration will be applied to minimize
losses. Details about network reconfiguration due to voltage, line/component loading in
normal and fault conditions can be found in [42].

2.6.1
Reconfiguration under Normal Operation
Distribution system dimensioning is determined by load and voltage limits. Overload
and voltage as well as losses are the most critical parameters to address. Losses are
inherently related to load and voltage. Hence, loss curtailment in distribution system
will lead to better load and voltage behavior [42].
According to Zidan et al. (2013) reconfiguration has significant effect in reducing
the line losses which is translated to reduced system operational costs [5]. Furthermore,
Bouhouras et al.
(2012) proved that switching operations during a day can form a
realistic suggestion of active reconfiguration in order to reduce losses taking into account
load fluctuations [47].
Active network reconfiguration on a daily or hourly basis is unlikely to be used
today for normal operation [42].
On the other hand, static network reconfiguration
as optimization for longer time periods is an option that DSOs would likely use to
guarantee the optimal operation of the power grid. Moreover, static reconfiguration can
be implemented to avoid other costly measures to correct the grid, designed for dynamic
correction of voltages and loading. Static reconfiguration could also be used to optimize
periodically the grid and to deal with special circumstances (e.g. large social events)
that change the load profile [42].
2.6.2
Reconfiguration and Fault Conditions
Medium Voltage (MV) power networks are designed in terms of N-1 principle. When
a power system satisfies the N-1 criterion, it is called “N-1 secure” because it is able to
withstand in case any one of its N components is lost [48]. In a radial distribution system,
system operations after a failure of a line are interrupted for a short period equivalent
to the time needed to notice and isolate the fault and reconfigure the system to an
alternative radial configuration [11]. Thus, it is potential to resupply consumers with
electric power from backup connections, activated in case of faults. In fault conditions,
network reconfiguration is used to improve the grid reliability by decreasing the time
duration of outage and the number of affected consumers.
Outage reduction can be achieved via network reconfiguration. A component failure
usually causes outages and higher losses. When reconfiguration occurs, affected cus-
tomers can be resupplied with power from a backup connection until the failed circuit
element is repaired. This use of network reconfiguration is widespread today and has
been implemented for many years [42]. Nowadays, automation has started to improve
system response in case of outage.
All in all, network reconfiguration implementation in fault and post-fault conditions
is common today. The main goals of current use is to reduce outage time and number
of affected customers but it is also performed to avoid high power losses, violation of
loading and voltage limits during post-fault conditions where these factors are challenged
more than in normal operation.
Network reconfiguration is already implemented to resupply customers in N-1 con-
dition and an extension for load and voltage optimization conditions in the post-fault

grid would be more attractive. Though, remote control of switches and automation of
switching performance is indispensable if network reconfiguration deals with voltage and
overload. Moreover, it is essential to do more advanced network reconfiguration than
simply switching the faulted feeder to its nearest back-up connection. Such a switching
action in fault or post-fault conditions only assists the aim of outage reduction [42].
2.7
Types of Network Reconfiguration
Network reconfiguration can be implemented in various ways which can be separated
into automated and non-automated solutions. They are summarized below, according
to [42]. Non-automated solutions have more extensive use and contain manual recon-
figuration.
Manual reconfiguration is performed by a technician physically going to
a substation to change the switch status or by remote switch control from the DSOs
control center.
Automated reconfiguration can be local, decentralized or centralized.
Additional information related to costs and communication requirements is presented in
[49].
• Manual Control: It is implemented through manually changing the state of switches
by a technician to the substation where the state of a switch is urgent to be changed.
It is a slow form of switching and more useful for static changes.
• Remote control: The switching in this case can be controlled by distance from the
DSOs control center. An operator is still needed to be active in this process, but
the procedure is simplified by placing him at the DSO control center. The operator
can change fast the state of switches with remote control. This decreases radically
the time needed to start network reconfiguration, as it is done by just pushing of
a button.
• Local automation: It is the most simplified automation of the network recon-
figuration process. It makes possible a secondary substation to alter the switch
state depended on local information. The secondary substation is reconfigured
instantaneously without human interaction. The access to local information set a
limitation to its use on outage reduction only.
• Distributed automation: In case of distributed automation, secondary substations
which neighbor can inter-communicate. The ability to communicate with neigh-
boring secondary substations allows an overview of the grid around it. This better
understanding of the grid allows a better understanding of its role in the power
system, so operator can take more reliable decisions on network reconfiguration.
Secondary substations can interact with each other to promote cooperation during
network reconfiguration.
Distributed automation provides high reliability and adequacy, because the failure
of one secondary substation does not block the other secondary substations to
coordinate.
==PAGE:31==

• Centralized automation: During centralized automation, all secondary substations
share information with the DSO control center. The secondary substations do not
act independently, but it is relayed to the control center where the automation
system is located. The control center automation system concludes to the next grid
actions and conveys the related control indications to the secondary substations.
This form of automation provides state optimization of the whole distribution grid
because the control server has every time a holistic grid view. The drawback of this
method is that there is no redundancy. A problem in the control center automation
system causes the loss of the entire automation system and its ability for network
reconfiguration. Remote control by the operator would still be possible in case of
automation system failure [42].
2.8
Current Situation of Network Reconfiguration
The following section is based on information derived by [42]. According to this report,
network reconfiguration is currently used mostly to non-automated types of network
reconfiguration.
It was also stated that distributed automation is not currently used largely by DSOs.
Additionally, both local and centralized automation are quite novel procedures and they
are not widely implemented.
Remote control is performed for critical points in the
network, whereas the rest network uses manual switching.
In normal operation, network reconfiguration is used as a static optimization method.
Remote control and local automation are used for black-out reduction. Remote control
can be implemented to control loading and voltage limits in post-fault situations. How-
ever, it is applied for that use in a limited manner. Today, network optimization in
normal operation and post-fault conditions depends on DSOs’ decision. This is derived
from the inadequacy of efficient and available support tools for finding the optimal re-
configured topology. The only tools convenient to optimize the network condition are
basic calculations of load flow. Centralized automation is not yet performed in Danish
context [42].
2.9
Future Use of Network Reconfiguration
Information about the future situation of network reconfiguration and its application
is based on the report [42].
In the future, network reconfiguration will be used more actively as more support
tools and automation for switching actions would be commercially available. Though,
operators could continue to use network reconfiguration likely for static optimization in
normal operation and outage reduction in fault situations. The main difference com-
paring to current use would be the more extensive use of network reconfiguration for
post-fault situations in order voltage and load limitations to be respected and the overall
loading and voltage level in the post-fault network to be optimized.
==PAGE:32==
14
2 Literature Review
Post-fault network configuration would be optimized likely by distributed or central-
ized automation systems. Remote control might be also an option where operators will
rely on improved decision-making techniques and tools.
Manual and remote control systems will continue to be used for static optimization in
normal operation. Moreover, optimization algorithms applied on centralized automation
systems might be also used for static optimization.
While it is difficult to predict the network reconfiguration evolution in detail, it is
expected that its use will be greatly dependent on the development of communication
infrastructure.
To sum up, increased use of network reconfiguration is certainly a part of the smart
grid perspective. However, many features of smart grids, such as distributed generation
and demand response will affect the widespread use of network reconfiguration in the
future [50]. Other contributive aspects will be communication infrastructure and the
technological development of more flexible and dynamic products on the consumer and
network side.

in Denmark

CHAPTER 5
Methodology
5.1
Procedure of Analysis in Normal Operation
The objective of network reconfiguration in our study was to minimize the system
losses. Software NEPLAN was used to simulate the different case studies, through its
modules ”Load Flow”, ”Load flow with Load Profiles” and ”Optimal Separation Point”.
NEPLAN is a software that allows the user to create power system models and simulate
them with regard to different parameters [60]. It is a power system software applied
worldwide for network planning, modeling and analysis [61]. MATLAB and EXCEL
were used to evaluate the results and to encounter difficulties due to the huge amount
of data.
The first step of the analysis was to become familiar with NEPLAN. Thus, a ”toy grid
model” was built from scratch and examined under different applications. Furthermore,
a small part of the reference grid was investigated to get an insight of the grid perfor-
mance and to apply reconfiguration techniques to a smaller range. The aforementioned
introductory grid models are included in Appendix B.
The steps followed to analyze and evaluate the results of network reconfiguration in
the examined reference grid in normal operation, are described below. To begin with,
load flow with load profiles was calculated. Based on a specified network structure load
flow analysis solved the steady operation state with node voltages and branch power
flow in the power system [62]. Newton Raphson method was selected in this case, as
it was proposed in [63] for distribution networks, normally used due to its rapidness of
numerical convergency [64]. Load flow analysis was used to obtain results for the voltage,
loading, power and loss operational conditions. Next, the results were analyzed in order
to check whether the system could operate safely, i.e., if there were overloaded elements,
or too low or too high node voltages [62].
Furthermore, ”Optimal Separation Points” feature of NEPLAN was used to find the
optimal reconfigured structure when reconfiguration was requested. This feature offered
several objectives (minimize losses, minimize number of overloads, minimize loadings,
minimize voltage). The loss minimization was the one selected on this analysis. This
procedure eliminated network meshes by changing the network topology. Usually, there
was a considerable number of possible topology states. This procedure chose one topol-
ogy that satisfied the selected constrain without creating isolated sub-systems [65].
The procedure, followed in order to conclude to the reconfigured topology, is clearly
depicted in Figure 5.1.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
The scope of this project was to investigate network reconfiguration in a distribution
network and assess its technical and economic value. A part of the northern Zealand
distribution network was examined throughout the process of this project. The real
data, provided for the network by DONG, were for the year 2013. The total amount of
energy provided by the network throughout the year was approximately 331785 MWh
and the mean energy losses were between 2.7% and 3.2%, meaning that the grid is already
efficiently dimensioned and operated by DSOs. However, a further minimization of losses
by reconfiguration was examined.
In order to perform the investigation, simulation models were created in NEPLAN
software, describing different scenarios and evaluation of the results was conducted.
Scripts developed in MATLAB and EXCEL supported the set up of scenarios and the
management of the huge amount of data derived from simulations.
Network reconfiguration was selected to take place at critical moments, when seasonal
or yearly peak load appeared. Following that, four scenarios were created, including
normal and fault conditions for the year 2013 and 2040.
Regarding the effect of network reconfiguration on the reduction of power losses, it
was proved to be beneficial for all the study cases. In particular, during CS1 power
losses reduced between 9.78% to 14.10%. In CS2, the power losses during December
29th reduced about 11.6% by hourly reconfiguration, whereas on June 1st reduced about
15.6%. It was verified, comparing SC1, SC2, SC3 and SC4, that higher frequency of
reconfiguration led to higher reduction of power losses. In all the examined scenarios
of CS3, power losses in the reconfigured network are lower comparing to the network
in fault condition. However, the power losses in the reconfigured network are higher
in comparison to the pre-fault network. Further, CS4 followed the same trend with
improved loss performance during the different investigated future scenarios. Regarding
the number and the location of switching events, it was observed that they differed in
terms of load profile and distribution each time, as well as the fact that radial structure
should be kept after reconfiguration.
As it was mentioned before, the objective of network reconfiguration was the mini-
mization of power losses and it was achieved in all the examined case studies. However,
voltage and line loading performance were also improved. To be more specific, average
and minimum voltage of the network were moderately increased by reconfiguration in
all cases. Maximum voltage remained constant within the predefined limits by DSOs
[66]. As for the loading of distribution lines, in all cases, their maximum and average
value was decreased by reconfiguration. Thus, an enhanced performance was achieved

• The fuel prices are expected to increase in both Denmark and internationally.
• The Danish electricity price is mainly determined by neighboring price areas.
• Energinet.dk has only future international assumptions for the years 2020 and

the slope between 2020 and 2030 – which is an uncertain estimation.
• Available production capacity and technology distribution, interconnections, weather
effects, etc.
Thus, the future of a widespread application of network reconfiguration would be
determined from the actual future energy consumption and energy price.
In fact, it can be pointed out that the reconfigured topology in each case led to
energy reduction also during the rest of the day. Although this may be true, reconfigured
topology according to peak load can be applied to days with similar load profiles and
distribution but not for longer periods. This was verified in Table 6.15.
Finally, better and more precise results could be obtained with better optimization
algorithms. User cannot interfere and improve the algorithm used by NEPLAN, which
added some limitations in the current analysis. The used algorithm is not able to consider
more than one objective each time. Moreover, it is not possible to consider via “Optimal
Separation Points” feature more than one instance of time. Power loss reduction is based
on single instance of time whereas energy loss reduction was based over longer periods
(day-month- season).
To sum up, comparing the overall performance of the distribution network in different
case studies, it was verified that, both in respect of energy losses and potential cost
reduction, network reconfiguration is of great importance.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This project had as an ultimate target to study the application of network reconfig-
uration in a distribution system in North Zealand in order to ameliorate the network
reliability and reduce the line losses under normal operation or in response to a fault.
Due to daily and seasonal changes, for a specific moment (peak load), different case
studies were implemented and simulated through software NEPLAN for the year 2013
and 2040 and a power loss optimization method was used to define the reconfigured
topology.
A reconfigurable network can change its topology by changing the status
of switches on power lines.
As shown from the results, network reconfiguration, at
different frequencies over time, was proved beneficial to network performance without
jeopardizing network safety and power quality. Network efficiency increased as long as
energy losses decreased in all the examined cases. It can be concluded that a wide use
of reconfiguration for loss minimization might postpone the urgency for more generation
capacity by enhancing network efficiency instead.
As it should be noticed, a specific set of switching reduced the losses at a specific
time and it raised the losses at a later time. However, one should keep in mind that the
results obtained should not be generalized, since they derived from this specific grid, for
a specific load profile and distribution.
DSOs could reduce their costs due to the fact that energy losses were reduced. How-
ever, in the examined case studies, negative return was observed when considering just
the loss reduction and number of switchings for the year 2013. A potential increase of
electricity pricing led to scenarios with positive return. Nevertheless, there is always a
degree of uncertainty associated with forecast conditions, as it is difficult to predict the
pace of grid reinforcement and energy consumption and price. The larger the horizon of
the prediction, the higher the probability of an inaccurate forecast is.
To continue with, losses result in considerable financial and environmental costs
because more air pollutants emitted with the extra generation needed due to losses.
Hence, higher energy efficiency in distribution networks implies lower emissions during
electricity generation to provide the same energy to the customers.
All in all, the present project contributed to the field provided that it was an applica-
tion to a real operational network and cost of energy and switching operation were taken
into account. It was proved that network reconfiguration is a step towards the intelligent
utility of power network and smart grid vision. All in all, it seems that DSOs might
become able to make more efficient use of the existing grid and plan more ingeniously
the future. However, huge investment on distribution automation and decision support
tools is needed.

8 Conclusion
Indisputably, interesting and motivating directions of further research result from the
above work. More specifically:
As it has already been underlined, this thesis does not deal with setting up the
algorithm used to conclude to the reconfigured grid. To be more specific, NEPLAN
does not allow to modify the algorithm described in Section 5.1. Therefore, it would be
of great interest to formulate algorithms for the reconfiguration in order to reduce the
line losses and compare their outcomes.
A challenge encountered during the process of the thesis was the management of huge
amount of data. Hence, due to software limitations, MATLAB scripts were conducted
to analyze the large quantities of the derived data. They could be improved further to
make NEPLAN user-friendlier and more convenient for network reconfiguration analysis.
As a matter of fact, the assumption that future grid topology is perfectly known and
same as 2013 topology is not realistic. Thus, the grid topology should be reinforced
taking into account predictions for the future in order to obtain an even more accurate
model for 2040 analysis.
Unquestionably, even though Danish distribution networks are efficiently dimen-
sioned and operated, the value of network reconfiguration is still apparent. It goes with-
out saying that an interesting expansion would be to analyze the distribution network
of countries with higher losses than Denmark. A list of potential examined countries
could be found at the following references [72], [73], [74].
