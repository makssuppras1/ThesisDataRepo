Abstract
Seaweed is the largest aquaculture production globally, with an annual cultivation of 15.5
million ton wet-weight. As part of current seaweed research at P/F Fiskaaling this project
was done characterizing cultivated and natural population S. latissima. Saccharina latis-
sima was collected and cultivated at three sites with diﬀerent exposures. Samples were
taken regularly from the 29th of April until harvest the 19th of August, to examine sea-
sonal variations. S. latissima growth data was recorded and a selection of samples were
analysed for total Kjeldal Nitrogen, nitrate and amino acid composition.
Some morphological characteristics were found to be the same for natural and cultivated
individuals. Sheltered individuals had thin lamina and a high stipe-ratio, while exposed
individuals had thicker lamina and lower stipe-ratio.
Clear seasonal diﬀerences were seen in the total Kjeldahl nitrogen, nitrate, protein and
amino acid composition. The TKN ranged from 1.8-3.4 %DW in cultivated samples and
2.3-3.9 %DW in natural population. Protein ranged from 4-16 %DW in cultivated samples
and 4.7-10.9 %DW in natural population. The nitrate levels ranged from 5-60 µmol g−1
DW in cultivated samples and 5-380 µmol g−1 DW in natural population. Overall the
levels of TKN, protein and nitrate were higher in spring than in summer for both natural
and cultivated populations, with the exception of nitrate in the cultivated population
which was highest in the July samples. There were seasonal changes in the amino acid
composition, and these seasonal variations also resulted in diﬀerent EAA scores, varying
from 17.8% to 93.7%. There was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in EAA score between natural
and cultivated population.
Indications that S.latissima incorporates stored nitrate as organic nitrogen, when ni-
trate levels in the seawater are low, were found.
No diﬀerences related to exposure were found in chemical composition of the cultivated
S.latissima. However at the diﬀerent exposures the cultivation yields were signiﬁcantly
lower at the current exposed site compared to the sheltered and wave exposed sites (5.2
kg m−1 compared to 9.9 and 8.0 kg m−1).
Based on the high protein levels in the S. latissima and the high EAA score of the protein
in May, it is recommended that the biomass is harvested in May. In May, the biomass is
also non-fouled, and the result a high quality product for consumption.

Introduction
In Asian countries, seaweed has been a stable food source for centuries (McHugh, 2003).
In western countries the utilization of this resource is however mostly limited to use of
seaweed hydrocolloid extracts in the form of alginate, agar and carrageenan. Seaweed
hydrocolloids have a wide range of uses including e.g. in pharmacology, cosmetics, inks
and as food additives, where they are used as emulsiﬁers, stabilisers, thickeners and gelling
agents. According to FAO macroalgal cultivation is the largest aquaculture production
globally, with an annual cultivation of 15.5 million ton wet-weight (McHugh, 2003).
In the last years there has been an increasing interest in the western part of the world
to use macroalgae as food. There is an interest in using macroalgae as a culinary ingredient
(McHugh, 2003), a novel protein source in order to reduce the environmental impact of
meat production (van der Spiegel et al., 2013) and as an alternative to traditional cereals
due to the predicted increase in food demand caused by population growth (Mæ hre
et al., 2014). There has also been an increased focus on the diﬀerent bioactive compounds
from macroalgae that might have a beneﬁcial eﬀect on human health (e.g. fucoidans,
phycobiliproteins and peptides) (Holdt and Kraan, 2011).
Macroalgae have the potential to be used in integrated multi-trophic aquaculture
(IMTA), where they assimilate the excess nitrogen, produced by e.g salmon farming (Mar-
inho et al., 2015c). Furthermore macroalgae can be used in feed (McHugh, 2003), as an
alternative to conventional fertelizers (Michalak and Chojnacka, 2013) and they can be
bio-reﬁned for energy (Kerrison et al., 2015; Kraan, 2013) as well as other purposes .
In the Faroe Island there is currently one company, Sp/F Ocean Rainforest, which has
a small industrial production of Saccharina latissima with 245 kg harvested and sold in
January 2014 (Sp/f Ocean Rainforest, 2015), and a test production of Alaria esculenta
and Laminaria hyperborea. Another Faroese company Tari-Faroe Seaweed focuses on pro-
ducing products from diﬀerent seaweed species that have been harvested or cultivated
in a biologically sustainable manner. The Aquaculture Research Station of the Faroes
(P/F Fiskaaling) together with Tari-Faroe Seaweed is doing research on macroalgal cul-
tivation and quality of the cultivated biomass in the Faroe Islands in order to build up
knowledge that can be used industrially in the future. This study is a part of the current
research, and is the ﬁrst attempt to characterize the chemical content of Faroese seaweed
populations.

Introduction
1.1
Aim of Study
The aim of this study was to investigate:
 The morphological variations in S. latissima growing at diﬀerent exposures.
 The seasonal variations in total Kjeldal nitrogen, nitrate and protein content and
changes in the amino acid proﬁle.
 Changes in total Kjeldal nitrogen, nitrate and protein content and changes in the
amino acid proﬁle, related to exposure.
 The growth rate, growth data and yield at cultivation sites with diﬀerent exposures.
 Diﬀerences and similarities between natural and cultivated S. latissima populations,
with regards to morphology and chemically composition.
Based on the literature it was expected that there would be morphological diﬀerences
at diﬀerent exposures. It is known that there are seasonal variations in the chemical
composition of S. latissima, and since nitrate is the main nutrient in the seawater, it was
decided to look at several nitrogen compounds.
Exposure aﬀects most factors inﬂuencing the productivity of macroalgae. Growth rate,
growth data and yield for S. latissima cultivation were therefore related to exposure, as
well as it was investigated whether exposure aﬀected the chemical composition - looking
at the same nitrogen compounds as for the seasonal changes in chemical composition.
Finally, since most literature studies are based on natural populations and no studies
were known comparing cultivated and natural population, it was decided to examine
whether natural and cultivated populations were comparable.

Introduction
1.2
Saccharina latissima
The brown algal species Saccharina latissima (Linnaeus) C.E.Lane, C.Mayes, Druehl and
G.W. Saunders is common in the northern hemisphere and belongs to the family Lami-
nariaceae.
Saccharina latissima has a lamina, a ﬂexible stipe and a branched holdfast, which
can attach to multiple substrates e.g.
ropes, rock, pebbles.
Saccharina latissima is
characterised by the undivided frond with ruﬄed edges and no midrib. The lamina grows
from the stipe and most of the old lamina is shed after the initiation of the new blade in
the winter (Henley and Dunton, 1985). The holdfast and stipe are perennial, and the age
of S. latissima can be determined by annual growth rings in the stipe (Hymanson et al.,
1990). Saccharina latissima individuals usually have a lifespan of 2 to 5 years, and can
reach a length of up to 4 meters (White and Marshall, 2007).
Saccharina latissima has a hetromorphic diplo haplon life cycle. When the mature
diploid sporophyte individual becomes fertile sori appear as darkened, thickened portions
on the lamina (Redmond et al., 2014). The sori contain unilocular sporangia and meiosis
takes place in these unilocular sporangia and when they are mature, haploid meiospores
are released into the water column. The spores settle and develop into male and female
gametophytes. The male spermtozoid from the male gametophyte can fertilizes the egg
cell in the oogonium on the female gametophyte. The result is a diploid zygote, that
developes on the female gametophyte into a juvenile sporophyte. An illustration of the
life cycle of S. latissima can be seen in appendix A.
According to Kraan (2013) S. latissima is the fastest growing macroalgal-species in
the North Atlantic and gives cultivation yields of 3-28 kg wet-weight m−1 rope (Kraan,
2013). The ideal cultivation conditions are temperatures in the range of 5-15 °C and
moderate to high water motions above 0.1-0.25 m s−1 (Kerrison et al., 2015). The species
belongs to the same genus as Saccharina japonica that is one of the most used kelps in
China and Japan, and is cultivated at volumes of about 5-6 million tonnes per year in
Asia (Kraan, 2013).
Saccharina latissima is reported to be one of the ten most common macroalgaes in
the Faroe Islands. It is mainly found at sheltered sites down to 10-15 m, but is also
found at moderately exposed and exposed sites in smaller amounts (Bruntse et al., 1999).
Wegeberg et al. (2013) recommended S. latissima as one of the macroalgal species with
cultivation potential in the Faroe Islands.

1.3
Exposure and Morphology
The hydrodynamic forces at a macroalgal cultivation site aﬀect both directly and indi-
rectly most factors that inﬂuence the productivity of the macroalgae (Hurd, 2000; Peteiro
and Freire, 2013). The morphology of S. latissima is plastic and changes according to the
exposure to hydrodynamic forces (Bruntse et al., 1999; Buck and Buchholz, 2005; Gerard
et al., 1987; Koehl et al., 2008; Parke, 1948; Peteiro and Freire, 2013). The morphological
adaption, which is also observed in other species of macroalgae, has been suggested to
take place in order to reduce drag forces and to maximize photosynthesis (Gerard et al.,
1987; Koehl et al., 2008).
Saccharina latissima growing at sheltered sites has a wide, ruﬄed and thin lamina,
while S. latissima growing at exposed sites has a narrow, ﬂat and thick lamina (Bruntse
et al., 1999; Buck and Buchholz, 2005; Gerard et al., 1987; Parke, 1948; Peteiro and Freire,
2013).
Individuals with wide, ruﬄed and thin lamina, stay spread out even in slowly moving
water and have little self-shading, thus better utilising the light. The thin, ruﬄed mor-
phology allows the algae to ﬂap, which generates water motion and increases the nutrient
ﬂux to the lamina at sites with low water motion (Huang et al., 2011). According to
Gerard (1982) water velocities of 25 mm s−1 are suﬃcient to saturate nutrient uptake of
macroalgae.
At sites with high water motion ﬂapping will not increase the nutrient ﬂux (Huang
et al., 2011), and optimum utilization of light is of less importance for these individuals,
as the light availability can be expected to be higher in exposed environment due to
light ﬂashes (Peteiro and Freire, 2013). At these sites the most important feature is to
withstand the high drag forces from exposure. The morphology is therefore streamlined,
resulting in narrow, ﬂat and thick lamina.
Saccharina latissima has a ﬂexible stipe, making it capable of quickly re-orienting
and aligning with the direction of the hydrodynamic forces, which is an advantage for
oﬀshore cultivation (Buck and Buchholz, 2005). Buck and Buchholz (2005) found that
S. latissima can withstand harsh environmental conditions with wave heights of 6.4 m
and current velocities of 1,520 mm s−1.
A morphological variant of S. latissima in the Faroe Islands has a hollow stipe. The vari-
ant was previously considered to be a separate species, Laminaria faeroensis (Børgesen)
Børgesen - but was later found to be genetically conspeciﬁc with S. latissima (Erting
et al., 2004). The hollow-stipe variant is mostly found at shallow and sheltered sites,
where the hollow stipe increases the buoyancy of the algae (Bø rgesen, 1903).

1.4
Composition
The chemical composition of S. latissima is inﬂuenced by several factors such as; seasonal
variations (Fleurence, 1999; Henley and Dunton, 1985; Marinho et al., 2015b; Nielsen
et al., 2014; Schiener et al., 2015), gender and maturity (Schiener et al., 2015). Environ-
mental factors such as light, nutrient availability and hydrodynamic forces (which again
aﬀect light and nutrient availability (Hurd, 2000; Peteiro and Freire, 2013)) also contribute
to diﬀerences in composition (Marinho et al., 2015b; Nielsen et al., 2014). Furthermore
there is variation throughout the thallus. The composition of the stipe diﬀers from that of
the lamina and seasonal variations in the stipe are small and irregular compared to that
of the lamina (Haug and Jensen, 1954). There can even be diﬀerences in the chemical
composition across the lamina (Black, 1954; Henley and Dunton, 1985), and nutrients
can be translocated from the mature lamina to the meristem where new tissue develops
(Davidson and Stewart, 1983; Henley and Dunton, 1985). Finally there might also be ge-
netic diﬀerences within the same species, which aﬀect the chemical composition (Gerard,
1987). These variations and diﬀerences are important to bear in mind when comparing
compositional data.
Overall S. latissima has a high content of carbohydrates of around 73-90 % DW (Holdt
and Kraan, 2011); mainly polysaccharides (up to 61 % DW), mannitol (2-19 % DW)
and laminaran (0-33 % DW) (Holdt and Kraan, 2011). The lipid content is rather low
(< 3.5 % DW), but more than half of the fatty acids are polyunsaturated fatty acids,
including the long chain polyunsaturated n-3 fatty acids eicosapentaenoic (EPA; 20:5n-
3) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA; 22:6n-3) (Marinho et al., 2015a). Furthermore S.
latissima contains some minerals, where especially the iodine content is high (250-1,150
mg/100 g DW), and phenols and pigments, some of which have antioxidant properties.
Proteins and other nitrogen containing compounds are addressed in the following section.
1.5
Nitrogen Compounds
Macroalgae take up nitrogen, which is crucial for growth, from the surrounding seawater
(Bartsch et al., 2008). The macroalgae can utilize nitrogen in the form of nitrate (NO−
3 ),
nitrite (NO−
2 ), ammonium (NH+
4 ), urea (CO(NH2)2) and free amino acids (Lobban and
Harrison, 1994). Normally it is nitrate and possibly ammonium which are available in the
ambient seawater in concentrations of importance. Ammonium uptake is passive and the
ammonium assimilates rapidly into amino acids within the chloroplast. Nitrate uptake
on the other hand is thought to be active (Raven et al., 1992), which is why the uptake of
nitrate seems to be less eﬃcient than that of ammonium. Nitrate can either be stored in

the vacuole or it can be reduced to nitrite and then to ammonium, which is incorporated
into amino acids that are used in protein synthesis (Lobban and Harrison, 1994). The
stored nitrate can be used to maintain the growth of new tissue in nitrogen-limited waters
by internal transport of nitrogen from the mature lamina (Bartsch et al., 2008; Lobban
and Harrison, 1994). According to Lobban and Harrison (1994) storage of nitrate is not
widespread in brown seaweed, and internal nitrate levels never account for more than 5
% of total tissue nitrogen. The nitrogen in macroalgae is mainly found incorporated as
amino acids and protein.
There are several studies which to some degree investigate N-compounds in S. latissima.
In most studies natural populations are analysed, and studies on cultivated S. latissima
are vastly under-represented.
Some studies analyse S. latissima, either cultivated or
translocated, kept at controlled conditions in tanks, e.g.
Ahn et al. (1998); Gordillo
et al. (2006); Indergaard et al. (1990). Studies of this type are not included here.
Levels of nitrate in natural population of S. latissima from Iceland are recorded by Sjø tun
and Gunnarsson (1995). The highest levels were found in February-April (175 µmol g−1 DW),
with higher concentration in the new lamina tissue, than in the mature lamina. In sam-
ples from other months the diﬀerences in nitrate levels between old and new lamina, were
not as pronounced. The lowest levels were found in October (10 µmol g−1 DW), but the
levels in November, December and July were also low.
A comparison of total N and nitrate levels in new and mature S. latissima lamina
collected from natural population in April and July was made by Henley and Dunton
(1985). In April the levels in both mature and new lamina were the same, with total
N being 3 % DW and nitrate 250 µmol g−1 DW. In July the total N in the mature
lamina was 2 % DW compared to 1.25 % DW in new lamina. The nitrate levels were 50
µmol g−1 DW for mature lamina and 10 µmol g−1 DW for new lamina.
In an older study by Larsen and Jensen (1959) the seasonal variation in nitrate content
in natural population S. latissima from Norway was examined. They found the highest
levels to be 450 µmol g−1 DW in March. The levels decreaced throughout the spring
(200 µmol g−1 DW in April and 157 µmol g−1 DW in May). The lowest level of 29
µmol g−1 DW was found in June. In the same study no nitrite was found the analysed
in S. latissima.
Schiener et al. (2015) looked at the seasonal variations in protein content (analysed) and
organic N in natural population of S. latissima from a current exposed site. The lowest
protein contents were found in August and in the subsequent sample from October (5.3

Two older studies examine seasonal variations in crude protein (TKN x 6.25) in S. latissima
from natural populations. Haug and Jensen (1954) found that crude protein of S. latissima
lamina from a sheltered site amounted to a maximum of 20 % DW in April-May, and a
minimum of 5-6 % DW in August-November. The content was higher in young than in
old lamina. In the stipe the crude protein content was more constant throughout the
year (7-13 % DW). Black (1950) measured the seasonal variations in individuals from a
sheltered and an open-sea site. The crude protein reached a maximum of 13-14 % DW
in February-March. Minimum were 5-6 % DW in August-September for the sheltered
site, and 8 % DW in July-September for the open-sea site. In general the samples from
the sheltered site showed higher concentration of crude protein in the spring and a lower
concentration in the autumn, than the samples from the open-sea location.
The only study found on N-compounds in cultivated S. latissima was by Marinho et al.
(2015b), who looked into seasonal variations in the amino acid (AA) proﬁle and total pro-
tein content of S. latissima. According to the study, the protein concentrations (analysed
as ΣAA) showed a maximum of 10.8 % DW in November and a minimum of 1.3 % DW
in May. Overall the AA proﬁle showed that histidine was the ﬁrst limiting amino acid
(6-13 mg AA g−1 protein), and aspartic (169-202 mg AA g−1 protein) and glutamic acids
(185-366 mg AA g−1 protein) were the dominant amino acids - though with some seasonal
exceptions. The largest changes in the AA composition occurred during the summer.
Mai et al. (1994) and Murata and Nakazoe (2001) give a few informations on the amino
acids in S. latissima. Murata and Nakazoe (2001) report lysine to be the limiting AA.
Mai et al. (1994) provide a single amino acid proﬁle for S. latissima, in which methionine
is the essental AA present at the lowest concentration (1.9 % of total AA), while the
most abundant AA is glutamic acid (12.61 % of total AA). MacArtain et al. (2007)
and Fleurence (1999) state that for most seaweed aspartic and glutamic acids together
constitute a large part of the amino acid fraction.

1.6
Protein Nutrition
The nutritional value of proteins is determined by the amino acid composition and con-
centration and the protein digestibility. Since it is especially important to get the nine
essential amino acids through the diet, the methods for amino acid evaluation are based
on these. WHO/FAO (1991) recommended that the amino acid composition is compared
with a reference essential amino acid requirement pattern, giving the essential amino acid
score (EAA; %) of the protein.
It is evident that children and adults have diﬀerent essential amino acid requirement
patterns WHO/FAO/UNU (2007), and the EAA score for the protein will therefore diﬀer
depending on the target group. However using a reference pattern which is adequate
to meet the essential amino acid requirements of young children will also be adequate
for adults, while the opposite may not be true. Another option when evaluating protein
quality, is to compare the amino acid composition against the amino acid pattern of other
food, here e.g. egg are use commonly used as reference.
Marinho et al. (2015b) calculated the amino acid score for cultivated S. latissima
based on the WHO/FAO/UNU (2007) protein requirement pattern for children aged 1-2
years. The amino acid scores ranged from 16.7% to 68.9%, while Murata and Nakazoe
(2001) found the amino acid score to be 82 based on the old requirement pattern given
in 1973 by WHO/FAO.
FAO/WHO (1991); WHO et al. (2007)
The digestibility of a protein gives information on the body’s utilization of the protein
Misurcova (2012). It is very diﬃcult to accurately determine the protein digestibility, as
it is aﬀected by many factors. The digestibility of seaweed can be aﬀected by other com-
pounds present in the seaweed, such as polysaccharides and phenolic molecules (Fleurence,
1999; Misurcova, 2012), the amount of which might vary within and between species. The
digestibility value can also be highly inﬂuenced by e.g. age, gender and health conditions
of the consumer (Misurcova, 2012). Since in vivo studies are very expensive, there are not
many studies of this type available on protein digestibility of seaweed species, and those
studies available give inconclusive results (Fleurence, 1999; Misurcova et al., 2010). There
are more in vitro studies available, but also in these studies there are many variables which
can aﬀect the result (e.g. temperature, pH, type of enzyme and enzyme concentration).
No suggested digestibility factor was found for S. latissima, but for the related species
S. japonica a study giving an in vitro digestibility was found. Misurcova et al. (2010)
reported the digestibility of S. japonica to be 72.1 (expressed as 100% digestibility of ca-
sein), which was in the high end of the ﬁve investigated brown seaweed species, but lower
than the 87.3 for Palmaria palmata, having the highest digestibility of all investigated
species.

2.3
Analyses
Sampling of cultivated S. latissima was done regularly (depending on weather) from
April to August 2015. At each date of sampling three samples were taken at each site
(n = 3). Each sample consisted of ten S. latissima individuals from a single dropline.
Three droplines were randomly selected at each site at each sampling. The samples were
measured (total length, length of lamina and width of lamina) and weighed, and then
frozen (approx. -18 °C) in food approved plastic bags before further analysis.
Samples of natural S. latissima population were collected by diving in April and in July,
from sites corresponding to the cultivation sites in terms of exposure. Each sampling
consisted of three samples of ten randomly collected S. latissima individuals (n = 3).
Each sample was put into large plastic bags and stored outside until treatment the same
or the following day. The length of stipe and lamina of all individuals was recorded.
The length of the stipe was measured from the holdfast to the interception between stipe
and lamina. The length of the lamina was recorded as the length from the interception
between stipe and lamina to the tip of the lamina, as illustrated in ﬁgure 5.
A thin slice was cut from the stipe just above the holdfast, to determine the age of
the ten individuals in each sample. A circle with a diameter of 10.3 cm was cut above the
intersection between stipe and lamina . In individuals from which it was not possible to
cut a circle with the desired diameter, a piece with a length of 10.3 cm was cut above the
stipe or the entire lamina was used. The lamina pieces were weighed and frozen (approx.
-18 °C) in food approved plastic bags before further analysis.

2.3.1
Kjeldahl
The traditional Kjeldahl method was used to quantify the total Kjeldal nitrogen, which
is the organic N (e.g. in proteins) and ammonium N, content in the seaweed samples,
according to the manual by Fiskeindustriel Forskning (2006).
Concentrated sulphuric
acid and Kjeltabs (Thompson & Capper, Ltd, England; Bie & Berntsen) were added
to catalyse the extraction of nitrogen from the sample. The extraction took place in a
Kjeldahl Destruction unit from Gerhardt at 440 °C for approximately 1-1.5 hour. Water
and kjeldahl-base (35 % (W/W) NaOH) were added to the sample and placed in a 2100
Kjeltec distillation unit from Foss. During this step, the ammonia produced was distilled
and collected in hydrochloric acid, to which A.C.Andersen indicator was added.
The
excess hydrochloric acid was titrated with 0.1 M sodium hydroxide, using a Dosimat 665
from Metrohm, until the indicator colour changed from purple to green. The total N was
calculated from the titration volume.
2.3.2
Nitrate
Nitrate (NO−
3 ) was determined by ion chromatography. The method used was modiﬁed
from Berg and Granby (2013) (developed for meat).
Nitrate was extracted from the
seaweed in MilliQ-water by soniﬁcation for 15 min. The samples were centrifuged (3600
rpm, 10 min.) and the supernatant was cleansed in a C-18 SPE (solid phase extraction)
column (Chromabond, C18 EC 500 mg/6mL). The ﬁltrate was added in a HPLC vial

sample was run on the HPLC from Agilent, using 50 mM NaOH and MilliQ-water as the

UV absorbance. Standard curves were prepared from a natrium-nitrate stock solution

2.3.3
Amino Acids
The amino acid composition was analysed using a Phenomenex EZ:faastTM amino acid
analysis kit. The samples were ﬁrst hydrolysed in 6 M HCl in a microwave oven (Mi-
crowave 3000 SOLV, Anton Paar) for 60 minutes at 110 °C. Following the EZ:faastTM
user manual, a cleanup-step to remove matrix interferences and amino acids derivitiza-
tion were done. The amino acid composition was determined by liquid chromatography
with a mass spectrometry detector (Agilent 1100 series, LC/MSD Trap) using a Z:faastTM
4u AAA-MS column (250 x 3.0 mm, Phenomenex). The total protein concentration in
the samples was calculated by adding up all the amino acid masses and subtracting the
water incorporated during hydrolysis (18 g H2O mol−1 amino acid).
2.3.4
Nutrients
At each sampling of S. latissima water samples were taken at each site. Water samples
were collected in 200 mL plastic bottles, which were ﬁrst ﬂushed with the sample sea
water. The samples were collected about 0.5 m below the water surface. The samples
were preserved with 12 drops of chloroform, and stored in a cold dark shed until analysis.
The samples were analysed for nitrate content at Havstovan (Faroe Marine Research
Institute) on an autoanalyzer according to Grasshoﬀet al. (1999).
2.4
Statistical Analyses
Data collection and calculation from raw analytical data to values per g dry-weight (DW)
were done in Excel. Statistical analyses were done in SAS Enterprise Guide 7.1. Data was
checked for normality using QQ-plots. Two-way ANOVA analysis was used to analyse
the inﬂuence of season and the inﬂuence of cultivtion and harvesting sites on various
investigated factors. In a few cases only one factor was analysed, in which case a one-way
analysis was used. The ANOVA analysis was done on the arithmetic mean. When the

Discussion
Saccharina latissima was cultivated sucessfully at three sites with diﬀerent exposures.
A novel seeding method where the sporephyte culture was sprayed directly onto the
lines right before deployment was also proven sucessful.
Compared to the traditional
cultivation method of S. latissima, where the spores settle directly on lines, develop into
male and female gametophytes, male and female gametophytes become fertile, fertilisation
occurs and the new sporophyte developes on the female gametophyte, the entire life-
history developes in culture ﬂasks in a culture chamber. The seeding method used in
this work required very little laboratory space and the cultures were less time-consuming
to maintaine. Based on the experiences it can be concluded that the direct seeding of
sporophyte cultures can be used within the exposures degrees of the sites in this study.
According to Buck and Buchholz (2005) S. latissima can withstand current speeds
of up to 1,520 mm s−1.
Based on this it was not expected that the exposure would
be a limiting factor for the growth of S. latissima at the chosen cultivation sites, since
the current exposed site only rarely reached above 400 mm s−1. Gerard (1982) reported
that water motions of 25 mm s−1 were enough to saturate nutrient uptake of macroal-
gae, and therefore with a current speed of around 50 mm s−1 for about half of the time,
it was not expected that growth would be limited by lack of exposure at the sheltered site.
The yield per meter cultivation line corresponded to the values of 3-28 kg m−1 given in
the literature (Kraan, 2013). The signiﬁcantly lower yield of 5.2 kg m−1 at the current
exposed site compared to the 9.9 and 8.0 kg m−1 at the sheltered and wave exposed
sites, seems to contrast the weight observations where the current exposed site had higher
values than the the other two sites throughout the cultivation period.One possible reason
for the lower yield might have been due to more individuals being detached from the lines
during the cultivation period, because of the higher current levels at this site. Another
possibility is, that the higher current speed gave worse conditions for the sporephytes to
attach to the cultivation line after they were sprayed on, the current site thus being less
densely seeded than the two other sites from the beginning.
At the time of harvest an increase in bio-fouling was observed at the sheltered and
wave exposed site compared to the sampling at the 7th of August, while the same increase
in fouling was not observed at the current exposed site between these dates. The increased
fouling was most likely the reason for the increased growth rate between the 7th and 19th
of August at the sheltered and wave exposed sites, and might also explain a small part of
the higher yield at these sites.
According to Handaa et al. (2013) the preferred deployment time is in early February
for many brown seaweed species - allowing for the longest biomass production period
35

before the nutrients in the water were reduced in the summer. Our lines were deployed
on the 3th of March and even though the growth did not seem to be very nutrient limited
- even with nitrate levels in the seawater drastically decreasing, an earlier deployment
would have allowed an earlier harvest, possibly late June or early July. The fouling on
the cultivated S. latissima was ﬁrst observed in June at the current exposed site and in
July at the sheltered and wave exposed sites. Had the lines been deployed and harvested
one month earlier the produced biomass would have had better quality for consumption.
4.1
Morphology
The observed diﬀerences in morphology of the natural population were consistent with the
literature. The sheltered site had wide and thin lamina, while the lamina at the wave and
current exposed site were narrower and thicker. Between the current and wave exposed
sites there were also morphological diﬀerences; the lamina narrowed more down towards
the tip at the wave exposed site than at the current exposed site, and the wave exposed
individuals had thicker lamina than the current exposed individuals. According to the
literature these morphological diﬀerences indicate that the individuals at the wave exposed
collection site experienced higher water motions and drag forces than the individuals at
the current exposed sites.
Furthermore there were signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the stipe
ratio between the collection sites, with the current exposed individuals having the longest
stipe (% of thallus length) and the wave exposed individuals the shortest stipe (% of
thallus length). While a low stipe-ratio for individuals at wave exposed sites was expected
(L¨uning, 1990), the high stipe-ratio at current exposed site was not found described in the
literature. However the natural population at the current exposed collection site was the
hollow-stipe faroensis variant, which usually only grows at shallow and sheltered sites. It
therefore seems that, by mistake, the collected individuals are not representing a current
exposed site.
With the morphological features being quite pronounced in the natural population
individuals, most of which were second-year individuals, it was interesting to see whether
these were the same in the younger cultivated individuals.
From the weight/length ratio for the cultivated population, which gives an indication
on the lamina thickness, it was seen that the individuals at the current exposed culti-
vation site had thicker lamina than the sheltered site individuals, which corresponds to
the natural population morphology - and exposure theory. However the wave exposed
individuals was not diﬀerent from the sheltered individuals.
The stipe-ratio was signiﬁcantly higher at the sheltered site compared to the current
and wave exposed sites. The morphological features are somewhat comparable to the
natural population, since in both populations the stipe-ratio of sheltered individuals is

higher than that of wave exposed individuals, which as already mentioned is in agreement
with literature (L¨uning, 1990).
However the current site stipe-ratios were highest for
the natural population, while in the cultivated population they were not signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent from the wave exposed site. Possible explanations for his could be 1) the hollow-
stipe variant has a diﬀerent behaviour, see discussion above. 2) the higher stipe-ratio at

validated by cultivating for at longer period of time.
The lamina width/length ratio for cultivated individuals showed a clear diﬀerence be-
tween the three sites. The sheltered site had the lowest ratio, meaning that this site
had the narrowest lamina, and the current exposed site had the highest ratio, having
the widest lamina. This was not according to litterature Gerard (1987) or natural pop-
ulation morphology. Even though it was not measured, the tendency observed in the
natural population was that the wave exposed individuals had the narrowest lamina com-
pared to lamina length, and the sheltered individuals had the widest, with the current
exposed individuals being somewhere in-between. However, according to Sjø tun (1993)
the width/length ratio of new tissue produced increases with increasing age, which might
explain the diﬀerent tendencies, since the behaviour in young and old individuals might
not be the same.
4.2
Natural and Cultivated Populations
Comparing the chemical composition of natural and cultivated populations of S. latissima
had its challenges, and a few decisions had to be made. The cultivated populations are
more homogeneous than the natural populations; the cultivated populations are the same
age and have only new lamina, while the natural populations often have both new and
mature lamina and are not necessarily the same age. To minimize the variation between
natural population individuals it was decided to pool ten individuals into one sample
(n=3), the same was done on the cultivated population, but here it also served to obtain
enough biomass for analyses. On the other hand, for the amount of biomass from 10
natural population individuals not to be too large to handle in the laboratory, it was
necessary to choose a representative section of the individuals - in this study it was decided
to cut a 10.3 cm circle just above the stem, which included part of the meristematic tissue.
It is well known from literature that there are changes in chemical composition along
the lamina and between stipe and lamina, e.g. (Black, 1954; Sjø tun and Gunnarsson,
1995). The choice to cut a circle from the new lamina and include some meristematic
tissue, was based on the assumption that this would correspond best to the cultivated
individuals, since they were analysed whole; having new lamina and stem. This choice
can of course be debated and the ideal way of doing this would require separate research.

4.3
Chemical Composition
The chemical analyses done on the cultivated and natural population S. latissima is the
ﬁrst attempt to chemically characterise Faeroese seaweed populations.
The nitrogen levels in the natural population S. latissima were comparable to the values
given the literature, but with slightly higher maximum and lower minimum levels. The
highest levels found in this study were in the natural population from April with levels
of 3.6, 3.9 and 3.7 % DW for the sheltered, current and wave site respectively, while the
highest value found in the literature was 3.5 %DW in natural population from March
(Nielsen et al., 2014). The lowest nitrogen levels were found in the cultivated samples
from the 8th of July, 1.8 %DW at the sheltered site and 2.1 %DW at the wave exposed
site, while the the lowest reported levels were 0.8 %DW (Schiener et al., 2015) and 1
%DW (Haug and Jensen, 1954) also reported for July (np). The higher levels in this
study might be due to the fact that the Faroese fjords are not as nutrient limited as
waters in Scotland and Norway, and even though the levels reached 0 µmol/L it is quite
likely that the period is in which the waters are nutrient limited is shorter.
Overall the natural population had higher levels of nitrogen compared to the culti-
vated population, which had been expected, since according to the literature brown algae
can store nitrogen, which can be used for growth when nitrogen-limited (Lobban and
Harrison, 1994). A young population will not have had the possibility to store as much
nitrogen for future use, thus in theory having a lower nitrogen content.
The highest nitrate levels were found in the natural population collected in April, where
the levels were 380 µmol g−1 DW at the sheltered site, 103 µmol g−1 DW at the current
exposed site and 84 µmol g−1 DW at the wave exposed site. The highest level reported
in literature was 450 µmol g−1 DW in March (Larsen and Jensen, 1959). The levels for
the natural population are thus within the range in literature. It seems however slightly
strange that the level at the sheltered site is much higher than at the other two sites, one
possible explanation could be that there as been a nitrate source close to this collection
site.
Nitrate levels in the cultivated population increased signiﬁcantly between the 27th of
May and the 8th of July, and there was a signiﬁcant decrease between the 8th of July and
the 7th of August. The levels were within the low ranges reported in the literature, but
the peak in July was in contrast to Henley and Dunton (1985) who reported the lowest

The lower levels in the samples from the 27th of May are found even though the nitrate
levels in the seawater were higher than on the 8th of July. A possible explanation for this
might be the the age/size of the individuals. On the 27th of May the individuals still very

nitrate. It seems that their storage capability has increased from the 27th of May to the

The nitrate levels in the seawater are close to zero on the 8th of August. The decrease
between the 8th of July and the 7th of August is therefore most likely due to the seaweed
exploiting the stored nitrate reserves.
However the standard deviation on the nitrate analyses from the 8th of July are very
high (78% for the sheltered site and 66% for the wave exposed site) - making it possible
to question the reliability of the nitrate levels, thus the peak in nitrate levels is perhaps
not as much of a peak.
Assuming that the nitrate levels on the 8th of July are reliable, there is a possible co-
relation between the reduction in nitrate from the 27th of May to the 8th of July, and
the increase in nitrogen from the 27th of May to the 8th of July. It can be expected that
the seaweed has started to use the stored nitrate, which therefore decreases. Since the
nitrite has been incorporated into organic nitrogen the TKN increases. Relating these
factors to the protein content, it is quite possible that this incorporation of nitrate N is
into proteins, since the samples from the three cultivation sites have slightly higher values
for protein on the 7th of August compared to the 8th of July (even though the increase
was not signiﬁcant). As already discussed this re-mobilisation of Nitrogen is triggered by
drop in seawater nitrate levels.
Overall there were higher protein concentrations in the cultivated S. latissima compared
to the natural population. The concentrations were highest in the spring and signiﬁcantly
lower in the summer for both the natural and cultivated populations, which is in agreement
with the nutrient levels in the seawater.

(2015b) were found in May, at which time high levels were found in this study (up to 15.3

natural population S. latissima respectively. The EAA scores were signiﬁcantly lower
during the summer period ranging from 18-79 %. The EAA scores reported by Marinho

of 17 %, and did not seem to have the same seasonal tendencies. The limiting amino acid
in this study was mainly found to be histidine, which is in agreement with that reported
by Marinho et al. (2015b). In two samples from April lysine was the limiting amino acid,
which Murata and Nakazoe (2001) also report to be the limiting EAA in S. latissima.
Similar to what is reported in literature the largest amino acid fractions in the analysed
S. latissima in this study were glutamic and aspartic acid (Fleurence, 1999; MacArtain
et al., 2007; Marinho et al., 2015b).
Tryptophan is completely destroyed during the acid hydrolysis, and is therefore not used
when calculating the EAA score and ΣAA. However, since Kolb et al. (2004) found the
levels of tryptophan to be quite low in two species of brown algae, the eﬀect on protein
levels is most likely very low. Since tryptophan was found to be the limiting amino acid
in the algal species, it is quite plausible that the EAA score had been lower, had it been
possible to include in the calculations.
There were a few diﬀerences in chemical composition between natural population collected
at the three collection sites. However, since these changes were not observed between
S. latissima from the three cultivation sites, they were not considered interesting with
relation to a cultivation time-frame. It is of course possible that exposure cultivation
aﬀects other chemical constituents than the N-compounds investigated in this study.

Conclusion
5
Conclusion
Morphological diﬀerences in S. latissima related to exposure here found both in natural
and cultivated populations. In the natural population wave exposed individuals had long
narrow and thick lamina, short stipe and small ruﬄes along the edges. Sheltered individu-
als had short wide and thin lamina, longer stipe and large ruﬄes along the edges. Similar
to the natural population, in the cultivated population the current exposed individuals
had thicker lamina than the sheltered individuals, and the sheltered individuals had also
a higher stipe-ratio compared to wave and current exposed individuals. However in the
cultivated population, as opposed to the natural population, the sheltered individuals
had the narrowest lamin. It is likely that some morphological behaviour therefore is a
combination of exposure and age/size.
There were clear seasonal diﬀerences in the total Kjeldahl nitrogen, nitrate, protein and
amino acid composition. Overall the levels were higher in spring than in summer for
both natural and cultivated populations, with the exception of nitrate in the cultivated
population which was highest in the July samples. It is likely that this highest level was
related to the juvenile S. latissima from May, not being able to store much nitrate. In
August the nitrate decreased, this was thought to be related to the nitrate being limited
in the seawater. The S. latissima therefore had most of the stored nitrate from July. This
nitrate had been incorporated as organic nitrogen, which was observed as an increase
in TKN from July to August. The TKN was higher in natural population compared to
cultivated population, while the nitrate in the natural population samples from April was
much higher than in all other samples analysed.
There were seasonal changes in the amino acid composition, and these seasonal variations
also resulted in diﬀerent EAA scores, varying from 17.8% to 93.7%. There was no signiﬁ-
cant diﬀerence in EAA score between natural and cultivated population, but the protein
levels in cultivated S.latissima was overall higher than that of the natural population.
For the cultivated population no diﬀerences were found with regards to chemical com-
position that could be related to the exposure. There was a signiﬁcantly lower yield at
the current exposed site (5.2 kg m−1) compared to the sheltered (9.9 kg m−1) and wave
exposed sites (8.0 kg m−1). The growth rates were the same (0.068 day−1 for the shel-
tered and current exposed sites, and 0.069 day−1 for the wave exposed site). However the
individuals at the current exposed site had the highest weight throughout the cultivation
period, with the weight being signiﬁcantly higher than for individuals at the sheltered
site. The diﬀerence in ﬁnal yield is therefore thought to be either; due to individuals
41

being detached from the cultivation line by the high current, or because the high current
gave worse conditions for the sporephytes to attach to the cultivation line.
Based on the high protein levels in the S. latissima and the high EAA score of the protein
in May, it is recommended that the biomass is harvested in May. In may, the biomass is
also non-fouled, and the result a high quality product for consumption. However for the
yield to be high enough it will require an earlier cultivation start.

Detection of nitrate (and nitrite) in seaweed 
Summary  
Homogenized samples are extracted using water and sonification. The samples are centrifuged and 
cleansed on C18 SPE colomns. The extract is diluted with NaOH solution in HPLC vials. The 
detection is done based on ionchromatographic separation at 210/225 nM UV absorbance. 
The method is based on “Analysemetode AT451.1 Bestemmelse af nitrit og nitrat I fødevarer” and 
is modified to use on freeze-dried seaweed based on experiments done in June 2015.

Nitrate Analysis - Comments
The nitrate analysis using HPLC and UV detection was modiﬁed from Berg and Granby
(2013). The original method was developed for meat, in which nitrate was extracted from
the meat matrix using 1 mM NaOH and soniﬁcation. This provided some problems when
extracting from seaweed, since NaOH also extracted alginate which provided problems
in the C-18 SPE column clean-up. It was therefore decided to try extraction with water
and soniﬁcation, and instead adding the NaOH after the SPE column. Duplicates of the
following clean-up procedures on the same sample were compared.
 1mM NaOH added to seaweed sample, soniﬁcated for 15 minutes, centrifuged at 3600
rpm for 10 minutes, supernatant collected and ﬁltered through C-18 SPE column.
 Water added to seaweed sample, soniﬁcated for 15 minutes, centrifuged at 3600 rpm
for 10 minutes, supernatant collected and ﬁltered through C-18 SPE column, 1mM
NaOH added with ﬁltrate in HPLC vial.
 Water added to seaweed sample, heated at 95 °C for 30 minutes, soniﬁcated for 15
minutes, centrifuged at 3600 rpm for 10 minutes, supernatant collected and ﬁltered
through C-18 SPE column, 1mM NaOH added with ﬁltrate in HPLC vial.
Since the extraction with water gave higher values than extraction with NaOH, it was
assumed that some nitrate remained in the gel phase not pushed the C-18 SPE column,
and this method was not used.
The comparison between hot and cold extraction of nitrate showed no increase in
nitrate due to heating. Since the cold extraction was easier, it was decided to do this on
the analysis of the seaweed samples.
Output
Overall the output from the UV detector gave sharp signal peaks at the ex-
pected retention time for nitrate (4-6 min.) both for calibration standard e.g. 31, samples
with low nitrate concentration and high nitrate concentration e.g. ﬁgure 32. However
there were some outputs which did not give clean peaks, but e.g. signals as the one shown
in 33.
Recovery
During analysis one seaweed sample was spiked with a known amount of
nitrate stock solution (analysed in duplicates). The the recovery was then calculated as
the actual nitrate concentration divided with the theoretical nitrate concentration.
Recovery was calculated twice. On the ﬁrst run the recovery was 104% (average of
133% and 74%), while the recovery on the second run was 105% (average of 110% and
99%). So there might be a slight overestimation of nitrate.
