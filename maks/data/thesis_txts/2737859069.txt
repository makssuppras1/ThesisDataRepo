Abstract 
This paper presents a mechanical analysis of Francis turbine the vanes system, this system is a major 
element controlling the production of a hydroelectric power plant. The main objective is to design the 
mechanical part to prevent an eventual rupture of the sensible parts. The document presents the 
different existing mechanical solution and will focus on one used in the model. This study used an 
existing SolidWorks model to create a finite element analysis case in Code Aster. First, the necessary 
theory about mesh creation and the software SALOME is presented. Then the finite element method, 
and the method used to create the Code Aster analysis. Finally, a real-life model is instrumented with 
deformation gauges to conduct measurement and compare it to the simulation. 
The first results show a coherence between the theory, simulation, and experimental results. However, 
the numerical model struggles with high load due to contact non-linearity. To prevent this issue and 
work with the desired loads, an imposed displacement is used on the model instead of an imposed 
force. The study shows some discrepancies between the theory and the modelization results 
particularly when using a small shaft. The thesis concludes on the importance of a proper design of the 
mechanical part to ensure the safety of a hydroelectric plant.

1. Introduction 
Hydraulic energy is one of the most powerful sources of renewable energy. Indeed, a hydroelectric 
power plant has a production ratio of 80 %, which makes it the most profitable electrical plant. The 
company EDF owns most of the hydroelectric power plant in France, this thesis will focus on one of 
their hydroelectric power plants called “Lac Noir” that was located in the Vosges. This power plant is 
no longer used and so the vanes system has been extracted to be used as an experimental device. 
1.1. 
Context 
Technology behind a hydroelectric plant is an impressive field of engineering. In fact, depending on 
the conditions of operations the design of the plant can change a lot. However, the essential elements 
of a plant remain the same in all of them. A plant is always located along a stream with a difference of 
altitude, most of the time, upstream source is a lake and the downstream is a river. Then through a 
series of pipes, the upstream water is conducted to the turbine. The turbine is the part of the plant 
that converts water flow into electricity, it is composed by the vanes system that regulate the flow 
going onto the runner. Then, the runner driven by water energy is rotating, driving through the shaft 
the rotor that combined with the stator produces electricity. The water is afterwards ejected in the 
downstream water source [1]. The part that will be studied in this thesis is the vanes system of the 
turbine. Before going into the technical details, the different types of turbines need to be presented 
[2]: 
- 
The Pelton turbine is the oldest one, this turbine is robust and allows an important difference 
of level between the upstream and downstream but a small scope of flow rate. Figure 1 shows 
a sketch of a Pelton runner. 
- 
The Francis turbine allows medium water level difference of water but a large scope of flow 
rate. Figure 2 shows a Francis runner. 
- 
The Kaplan turbine allows only a small level difference but a high flow rate. Figure 3 shows an 
example of Kaplan runner.  
The plant studied in this thesis was generating electricity thanks to a Francis turbine. The thesis aims 
to study the vanes system of the turbine so the first theoretical section will aim to define a vanes 
system and describe its way to work.

1.2. 
Thesis organization and objective 
The objective of the thesis is to conduct mechanical computation on the vanes system of the “Lac Noir” 
turbine to determine the stress induced in the components depending on the constraint imposed on 
the ring. The CAD model of the system has already been designed by EDF and can be used to conduct 
the computations.

2. Theory 
This section will focus on the theory required to conduct this thesis. It will first describe the mechanical 
system studied, then the meshing tools and finally the methods used in finite elements software. 
2.1. 
The mechanical system 
First let explains how the water goes from the pipe to a Francis turbine. Figure 4 shows the important 
elements of a hydroelectric power plant and a diagram of a Francis turbine. The water arrives from the 
pipe in the turbine, it is led into the spiral body of the turbine. This spiral is concentric with the runner 
and aims to drive the water onto the runner. It can be noticed that the diameter of the body is 
decreasing as the water is passing through, this shape is mandatory to keep a constant flow rate as the 
water exits the body. Between the body and the runner stand first the guide vanes, the aim of these 
pieces is to hold the body but also to make a first orientation of the water flow in the direction of the 
runner. Finally, the last thing standing between the water and the runner is the vanes system. This 
system is designed to guide the water to the runner but also to regulate the flow rate and so the power 
generated by the plant. It also ensures the waterproofing of the runner when needed [4]. 
 
 
Figure 4 - On the LHS: a diagram of a hydroelectric installation, on the RHS: a diagram of a Francis turbine 
Figure 5 shows the design of the turbine of the “Lac Noir” power plant. The SolidWorks model is the 
perfect replica of the vanes system, in dark is the body where the water flows, in red and yellow stand 
the vanes system. In appendix A, more views of the model are shown.

Now let takes a closer look to the vanes system, the system is composed with many mechanical pieces. 
Figure 6 shows a nomenclature of the most important part of the “Lac Noir” vanes system model. First, 
two hydraulic actuators are mounted into each side of a ring, this is the ring (1). The two actuators 
make the ring rotate to operate the directors (6). Then the directors are the wings shape part that 
guide the water flow, these wings can rotate until they come all in contact to prevent the water from 
going on the runner. Between the circle and the directors stand some connecting rods and shaft, they 
aim to convert the circle rotation into a synchronized rotation of all the directors. For the “Lac Noir” 
they are 12 directors used to control the water flow [5].  
 
Figure 6 - Split view of the mechanical group of the vanes system: from the director to the ring 
The water can carry some unwanted objects such as trees or stones, so if something is blocked 
between two directors while a normal automatic closing, some directors would risk being damaged. 
Those are quite complicated to replace as it requires to disassemble the entire system, so it needs to 
be protected as much as possible. A first solution would be to stop the closing process if an abnormal 
resistance is detected. However, if the system is closing it should never be stopped as it could lead to 
==PAGE:8==
 
Page 8 of 55 
 
an over excitation of the turbine and so an overpower production, leading to damages worse than a 
broken director. Then, a solution protecting the directors while continuing to close the system needed 
to be designed. As the vanes system is not in the water it can be accessed easily and so most of the 
connecting rods and shaft can be changed without any big work [2]. From this conclusion, the choice 
of using some mechanical part with a predetermined breaking point has been proposed, these 
solutions will be presented next. For the “Lac Noir” turbine, the breakage shaft is the one used, 
therefore, the thesis will mainly focus on the breakage shaft solution. 
2.1.1. The spring rod 
This design is the only non-destructive design existing for the vanes. As seen in Figure 7, this rod would 
replace the one transmitting the effort of the circle to the shaft. This system works in traction when 
the vanes system is opening so the shaft will transmit the efforts as a classical rod, then when closing, 
the system will work in compression and so the spring will transmit the efforts. The spring is designed 
so that its stiffness is sufficient to function as a solid part when the force it must transmit is lower than 
a predetermined threshold. However, if the director is blocked the force will exceed the threshold and 
the spring will compress itself and so the other component will not endure this excessive load. This 
solution offers a great way to avoid changing parts in the system. Yet, it has some drawbacks, the 
spring has a limited lifetime as its stiffness will be affected by time and fatigue. Then the system would 
require more verification and maintenance than a destructive system that requires maintenance only 
in case of an issue [2]. 
 
Figure 7 - Illustration of the spring rod: on the left-hand side the position of the rod in the system, on the right-hand side the 
plan of the rod [2] 
2.1.2. The breakage bolt 
This solution aims to change the crank of the system that is linked directly to the director, as seen in 
the left-hand side of Figure 8. The design of this solution is slightly more complex than the other one, 
indeed, the crank is not only changed but divided into three pieces: the upper and lower cranks linked 
by a bolt, see Figure 8. The two rods have a vertical surface in contact such that when the vanes system 
is opening all the forces are going through this contact and the bolt is not enduring any stress. However, 
when the vanes system is closing, the movement will tend to separate the two rods and so the bolt 
will work in traction. As the bolt is enduring simple traction, the stress in the section can easily be 
calculated and so, with regard to the fracture threshold of the material the section of the bolt can be 
dimensioned. Moreover, the bolt will have a slightly more complex design, if the section of the bolt 
stays constant there will be no stress concentration and the fracture location will not be well known.

the fracture threshold of the pieces will not be the same and an empirical formula allows to compute 
the new threshold and so be able to pre-dimensioned the bolt. This formula is confidential and cannot 
be written down here [6]. The materials used for this system are low alloy steel.

2.2. 
Mesh theory and Salome 
The SolidWorks model of the vanes system shown in Figure 5 is a perfect replica of the real-life system. 
Therefore, it only needs to be simplified and meshed before using it in the FEM software. For that 
purpose, the meshing software is used SALOME [12]. SALOME is a project launched by the French 
Ministry of Research and the Ministry of Industry, the project is developed by EDF, the CEA and 
OpenCascade, three major French companies. This software offers a GUI capable of generating mesh, 
editing it and control its quality. The main three meshing tools included in SALOME are NETGEN, GMSH 
and MeshGems® [13]. Then to import the CAD model into SALOME, .step files can be used. As these 
files are written in ASCII format, it allows a communication between SolidWorks and Salome [14]. 
==PAGE:12==
 
Page 12 of 55 
 
First let remind that a mesh is composed by elements, that can take many shapes. The most common 
are for 2D meshes triangle and quadrangle and for 3D meshes tetrahedra and hexahedra. These 
elements can be linear or quadratic, a linear element has nodes in its summits whereas the quadratic 
elements include nodes in the middle of each segment too. An illustration of 2D elements is shown by 
Figure 12. For 3D elements they keep the same pattern as the 2D one: a tetrahedron is 4 triangles and 
a hexahedron is 6 rectangles [9]. 
 
Figure 12 - Representation of the 2D classical mesh elements 
Usually, meshes are built with tetrahedra elements as it is a more versatile way to fit any geometry. 
Hexahedra elements are more complex, and it can be difficult to fit the geometry, however it can offer 
a better efficiency of the computation. Finally, quadratic meshes are much better than linear one, it’s 
better to have quadratic elements than twice finer linear elements.  
When assembling these elements, a mesh is created. There are different mesh structures, that can be 
divided into three different groups: structured, unstructured and block-structured. A structured mesh 
follows a regular pattern, this type of mesh is suitable for simple geometry, allowing to make the 
computation easier. However, for complex geometry, block-structured or unstructured meshes are 
preferable. Block-structured mesh, as seen in figure 13, is a type of mesh where some parts follow a 
regular pattern, this allows to simplify the computation even when the geometry can remain complex. 
Then, the unstructured mesh is the most suitable for complex geometry as it is easier to create the 
mesh. The only drawback is that each element will have an inconsistent number of neighbors which 
makes the computation more complex. Then, the most used mesh structure is the block-structured 
one, this allows to create a structured mesh in the in some parts of the geometry that are “regular” 
and used an unstructured mesh when the geometry becomes more “irregular”. This mesh structure is 
the one offering the best computational performance and results as it offers the best mesh quality. 
 
Figure 13 - Different type of mesh, from the left to the right-hand side: structured, unstructured, and block-structured [10]

2.2.1. NETGEN 
The first tool offered by SALOME is the open source NETGEN. The J. Schöbert article [13] offers an 
overview of the NETGEN meshing algorithm. NETGEN uses an advancing front method, meaning that 
it will mesh the boundary of the geometry and fulfill it afterward beginning by the edges as it can be 
seen in Figure 18.  
 
Figure 18 - Example of an advancing front algorithm 
creating new elements after meshing the boundaries [13] 
 
Figure 19 - Illustration of the bisection algorithm [13] 
 
 
The first step of the NETGEN generator is to find the edges of the structure and create points on it. 
These points called special points are divided into two categories: 
- 
The points of intersection where at least three surfaces intersect. To find them, the bisection 
algorithm is used: a cube at first englobes the whole geometry then is divided in 8 and the 
operation is repeated. Each cube is evaluated to know if it needs to follow the bisection 
algorithm again: if the cube is completely outside or inside the geometry then it is removed, 
otherwise the algorithm is applied until the cube contains an edge and is small enough 
regarding a chosen criterion. Once this step is achieved the coordinates are stored, the 
algorithm is illustrated in 2D by Figure 19.  
- 
Points of extreme coordinates at the extremum of an edge. Finding the extremum on an edge 
is equivalent to looking for the maximum 𝑥 on the surface 𝑓1 and 𝑓2. The Langrangian problem 
can be written and so the KKT conditions. 
 
==PAGE:15==
 
Page 15 of 55 
 
Then, the second step aims to define the edges, an initial point is chosen then the edge is followed 
until the terminal point is reached, then the edge is divided into segments according to the required 
mesh size. The special points are divided in two sets:  
- 
Unconditional points 𝑆𝑢: must appear in the generated mesh for the point of intersection. 
- 
Conditional points 𝑆𝑐: the edge would not be found without them for the point of extreme 
coordinates.

2.3. 
Code Aster 
Code Aster is an open-source finite element software studying the mechanical behaviour of structures, 
moreover Aster offers to link many physical mechanisms that affects structures: mechanic of course 
but also thermal, acoustic, or fluid dynamics. At first glance Aster was designed to solve the structural 
issues concerning electricity transportation, that's why Aster focus mainly on metallic and concrete 
structures. Thus, only a few composite or elastomer studies have been conducted [17]. Aster 
interpreter and command system works with Python offering the possibility of using Python functions. 
Moreover, to facilitate the user work, many commands can be used to define the analysis: read a mesh, 
choose the study…  Aster separates the phenomena: mechanic, acoustic or thermic and the model: 
3D, plane stress, plane strain… The couple of phenomena and model allows to create the type of 
element with the right geometry, interpolations, and integrations.  
Aster can work with SALOME meshing tool, indeed the mesh from SALOME can be exported in a .med 
file. This type of files is binary and portable, offering an easy way to communicate between the two 
software. These files contain the geometrical information of the mesh that Aster needs to do a 
calculation, it especially describes: 
• 
The nodes: names and coordinates. 
• 
The cells: topologic figures, planar or volume, on which the boundary conditions and elements 
can be applied. 
• 
The nodes groups: called GROUP_NO. 
• 
The cells groups: called GROUP_MA.

A barrier to the international use of Code Aster stands as most of the commands are written in French.

2.3.2. Multi-Point Constraints 
First let define the Multi-Point Constraints (MPC) method, this method allows to link the degree of 
freedom of several nodes, the “slave nodes”, to a unique node, the “master node”.  There are two 
main ways to use this link: the RBE2 and RBE3 elements. The RBE2 elements also called kinematic 
coupling, is imposing a motion to the slave nodes as the one applied to the master node. This method 
adds stiffness to the slave surface. In Code Aster, only RBE3 elements are available, these elements do 
not add a stiffness to the model. Indeed, the displacement of the slave nodes is a weighted average of 
the master node motion. This allows the slave surface to deform and still have a motion coherent with 
the master one [20].

To use this method in Code Aster, a simple node cannot be used as the master node. Indeed, a node 
cannot be constrained, therefore once the node is created, it is transformed into a 0D element in the 
mesh and in a discrete element in the model. This allows the master node to receive an imposed 
displacement or load. 
2.3.3. Contact and non-linear computation 
In an assembly, contact is an important parameter, Code Aster includes this module with the function 
“DEFI_CONT”. This function allows to link two surfaces with or without friction. The first step in contact 
definition is to choose which surface will be the master one and which one will be the slave surface. 
There are four that four rules to choose the master surface, illustrated by Figure 26 : 
• 
One surface is rigid (case a) 
• 
One surface recovers the other one (case b) 
• 
One surface has a higher stiffness (case c) 
• 
One surface has a coarser mesh 
 
Figure 26 - Four main cases of pairing for two surfaces in contact [22] 
If any of these rules are applicable it becomes a partial choice leaving to the “art of engineering” [22]. 
Then the normal are usually chosen as the normal of the master surface, a useful tool of Code Aster is 
to offer the possibility to smooth the normal. This method is based on the average normal to the node 
interpolated from the shape function. This allows to ensure the continuity of the normal and is 
particularly useful in case of curved surfaces. 
Then, to solve contact a static nonlinear method needs to be used. The small deformation hypothesis 
will be used in the rest of the study. This hypothesis assumes that the deformed geometry is close to 
initial one. This allows to consider that the pairing of the two surfaces does not change during the 
loading and so the pairing can be done a single time at the beginning of the computation. 
Finally, the most stable algorithm in Aster to use contact is the penalization one, this algorithm does 
not try to solve a perfect contact. This method solves an approach relation allowing some 
interpenetration of the two surfaces. To define the condition of interpenetration a coefficient 𝐸𝑁 is 
used. As shown in Figure 27, the higher this coefficient is the lower the penetration will be. A first guess 
of this coefficient is usually the Young modulus times the characteristic length of the mesh elements 
in the contact surfaces. Then, the coefficient can be increased by 10 if the displacement and stress are 
not correct. 
==PAGE:21==
 
Page 21 of 55 
 
 
Figure 27 - On the LHS the contact pressure as a function of the clearance between the two surfaces for a perfect contact, on 
the RHS the same plot for a penalization method with a coefficient E_N [22] 
The solver used in Code Aster is the solver MUMPS and the resolution method of non-linearity is 
Newton’s method. 
One important thing about contact is that it does not block any degree of freedom in the stiffness 
matrix. This means that if a part is held only by a contact with another part it will be impossible to solve 
the case as the stiffness matrix will be singular. There is a way to solve this issue in Code Aster by using 
springs. Indeed, it is possible to damp the part to the body with weak springs applied in several nodes 
of the part. These springs will remove the zero on the diagonal of the stiffness matrix, replacing it by 
its own stiffness. This allows to inverse the matrix as it is not singular anymore. However, it is important 
to choose a spring stiffness low enough to not influence the behavior of the model but high enough to 
ensure the convergence of the computation. 
To validate the contact model, the displacement and stress results need to be checked, Code Aster 
also includes a field that helps to check the results. This result field is called “CONT_NOEU”, it includes 
many parameters, but the principal ones are: 
• 
CONT: this parameter shows the state of the contact in the surface it can take 3 values here: 
o 0: no contact between the two surfaces 
o 1: friction contact (not used here) 
o 2: frictionless contact 
• 
JEU: value of the clearance, if negative it shows the interpenetration of the surfaces 
These parameters allow to validate the consistence of the results with the mechanical logic behind the 
computation.

3.3. 
Definition of the joints 
If the assembly is considered as one mechanical piece the previous boundary conditions are sufficient 
to run the computation as all degrees of freedom are constrained and so the stiffness matrix is 
invertible. However, considering an assembly as a single mechanical piece is not consistent with reality. 
Therefore, each joint needs to be studied so it is defined as close to reality as possible. The kinematic 
diagram represented on Figure 29 will be used.  
1. The joint between the ring (1) and the excentric (2) 
This joint is sliding pivot; however, the adjustment of the shaft and the bushes is designed so that the 
joint is not rotating when the vanes system is moving. Therefore, there is no need to use a frictionless 
contact here. The two parts are so glued together and act as one single part in the simulation. 
2. The joint between the excentric (2) and the rod (3) 
Again, the joint is a sliding pivot; yet the two parts should not be glued together. Indeed, when moving, 
the rod is rotating around the excentric so gluing those two pieces together would generate a shear 
stress that should not occur. The solution is so to use frictionless contact, it would allow to transmit 
the torque of the ring to the rod without introducing a shear force as the excentric would rotate freely 
in the rod. 
3. The joint between the rod (3) and the shaft (5) and the joint between the shaft (5) and the 
crank (4) 
These two joints need to be considered together, indeed regarding the plan those two joints have the 
same adjustment and should act the same way. The adjustment is so that the shaft can rotate in the 
rod and crank when using the vanes. This allows to transmit a force through the rod to the crank 
without introducing shear stress in the joint. Saying that the two joints should be modeled by a 
frictionless contact. However, adding two contacts would add a lot of complexity to the model and two 
“flying” parts: the rod and the shaft. Thus, considering the goal of the contact only one frictionless 
contact can be used, and the other one can be glued. Indeed, blocking the rotation of one contact 
would not add shear stress as the other one would still release it. Then the choice has been made to 
glue the shaft and crank together, this choice was motivated by the will to have the simplest “flying” 
part as possible: with this model the rod will be the only one.

5. Discussion 
Regarding the previous results, a discussion can be conducted. First, the efficiency of quadratic mesh 
has been verified. Indeed, visually, the results of the stress in the shaft cross-section are much 
smoother in Figure 57 than in Figure 53 where linear elements have been used. Moreover, the error 
of the computed torque shown in Figure 55 is 7 times lower when using quadratic elements. 
The integration of the shear force in the shaft matches the theoretical values as the error plot in Figure 
54 is less than 5 %. Regarding the error of the integrated force in the rod, the results are less 
satisfactory: the error remains around 10 %. This error can be explained by the bending of the rod, the 
cross-section of the rod on Figure 53 shows that the stress is positive at the bottom of the section. 
Only a small part of the section is impacted by this behavior, but it can influence the results of the 
integration. This can explain why the theoretical force is not reached in the rod but is found as a shear 
stress in the shaft. This behavior of the rod appears correct as its section is not rectangular and thus 
the load could generate bending.  
Then, the contact introduction in the model created some computation difficulties. The fact that the 
model does not work under high load is an issue as it blocks the simulation before reaching the critical 
stress values. This difficulty can be explained as the more the load increases the smaller the contact 
area becomes. The appendix D shows this behavior through the CONT_NOEU field. A solution would 
have been to refine the mesh to improve the contact condition, but it did not work and so the 
computation could not converge for a load higher than 0.25 MPa. 
Therefore, imposing a displacement is an interesting way to reach the rupture load. However, it is 
more complicated and less precise to reach the required load as it requires several iterations to find 
the correct imposed displacement.  Nevertheless, once the displacement is set, the model could reach 
the right rupture load as seen in Table 7. In this table, the maximum shear stress in the cross-section 
is presented, as the load is the rupture one, this stress should be around 𝑅𝑚= 700 MPa. Table 8 
compares the results of the computation to the theory.  
The shear force imposed in the computation is consistent with the rupture force of the shaft for both 
diameters. However, for the 4 mm shaft, the stress in the cross-section is 23 % higher than the rupture 
threshold of the material. It means that the shaft would break for force lower than the theoretical one. 
This behavior is illustrated by the experimental measured that shows in Table 6 as the experimental 
==PAGE:41==

Moreover, the stress distribution in the shaft cross-section stays conform to the theory. Using the 
Saint-Venant principle, it is known that the shear stress should tend towards zero as it approaches the 
boundary. This behavior is confirmed by Figure 53 and Figure 58 where the shear stress 𝜎13 is 
decreasing on the side of the cross-section, parallel to the force direction. 
Finally, it was shown that the director is the only mechanical at risk of breaking in case of a poor design 
of the shaft. As the director material is a non-alloy steel, its elastic threshold is around 200 MPa, 
meaning that for higher stress there is a risk of plasticity. Figure 59 shows that in the case of the rupture 
of a 4 mm shaft, the Von-Mises stress can reach 200 MPa in the director. However, this result needs 
to be considered carefully as it does not correspond perfectly to the reality. Indeed, the director is 
blocked on only one side, so the stress should be more evenly distributed between the front and the 
tail. Moreover, the shaft shear stress is much higher than its elastic threshold, which should impact the 
stress in the director as it would deform more than it is with this elastic model. 
 
6. Conclusion 
To conclude, after many simplifications and modifications of the SolidWorks model, a mesh and Code 
Aster study have been created, allowing to compute the mechanical stress generated in the vanes 
system when using it. The use of MPC elements and contact in Code Aster has yielded satisfactory 
performances. 
This modeling and the experimental measurements have confirmed the validity of the theoretical 
formula used to calculate the rupture force of the shaft. Moreover, the simulation allows the selection 
of a maximum diameter of the shaft. As discussed, a 4 mm diameter is the maximum that should be 
allowed as it already generates a lot of stress in the director when breaking. Currently, the shaft 
diameter is 9.5 mm, which means this predetermined breaking point is useless and must be reduced 
to 4 mm. 
Finally, to continue this work, the model can be improved by introducing plasticity. This would 
introduce a second non-linearity in the model, which will make it quite challenging to run but the 
results would be more accurate. Moreover, the model can be modified to include more than one 
==PAGE:42==
 
Page 42 of 55 
 
director, it could show the impact of a bad set-up of the directors and the stress distribution while 
working. It could also be interesting to order more breakage axes with diameters between 3 and 4.5 
mm to measure the breaking force of more axes and create a better experimental analysis. Finally, 
adding a computational fluid dynamic modeling to the model would contribute to the make the model 
closer to reality.

                             PHENOMENE='MECANIQUE', 
                             TOUT='OUI'), 
                          _F(GROUP_MA=('0D_Dir', '0D_BCPetit', '0D_BCGros'), 
                             MODELISATION='DIS_T', 
                             PHENOMENE='MECANIQUE')), 
                    MAILLAGE=mesh_Coumpound) 
elemprop = AFFE_CARA_ELEM(DISCRET=_F(CARA='K_T_D_N', 
                                     GROUP_MA=('0D_Dir', '0D_BCPetit', '0D_BCGros'), 
                                     VALE=(1.0, 1.0, 1.0)), 
                          MODELE=model) 
X20Cr13 = DEFI_MATERIAU(ELAS=_F(E=215000000000.0, 
                                NU=0.28)) 
X3CrNiMo13 = DEFI_MATERIAU(ELAS=_F(E=200000000000.0, 
                                   NU=0.28)) 
SteelNonAlloy = DEFI_MATERIAU(ELAS=_F(E=210000000000.0, 
                                      NU=0.32)) 
CrMo4 = DEFI_MATERIAU(ELAS=_F(E=190000000000.0, 
                              NU=0.28)) 
fieldmat = AFFE_MATERIAU(AFFE=(_F(GROUP_MA=('DIR', 'ROD', 'MAN'), 
                                  MATER=(SteelNonAlloy, )), 
                               _F(GROUP_MA=('AXI', ), 
                                  MATER=(CrMo4, ))), 
                         MODELE=model) 
FUN = DEFI_FONCTION(NOM_PARA='INST', 
                    VALE=(0.0, 0.0, 20.0, 20.0)) 
LIS = DEFI_LIST_REEL(DEBUT=0.0, 
                     INTERVALLE=_F(JUSQU_A=0.075, 
                                   NOMBRE=1)) 
BC = AFFE_CHAR_MECA(DDL_IMPO=(_F(DX=0.0, 
                                 DY=0.0, 
                                 GROUP_MA=('0D_Dir', '0D_BCPetit')), 
                              _F(DZ=0.0, 
                                 GROUP_MA=('BC_Vert', ))), 
                    LIAISON_RBE3=(_F(DDL_ESCL=('DX-DY-DZ', ), 
                                     DDL_MAIT=('DX', 'DY', 'DZ'), 
                                     GROUP_NO_ESCL=('CONTACT_DIRPOT', ), 
                                     GROUP_NO_MAIT=('Node_Dir', )), 
                                  _F(DDL_ESCL=('DX-DY-DZ', ), 
                                     DDL_MAIT=('DX', 'DY', 'DZ'), 
                                     GROUP_NO_ESCL=('BC_Petit', ), 
                                     GROUP_NO_MAIT=('BC_NodePetit', ))), 
                    MODELE=model) 
contact = DEFI_CONTACT(ALGO_RESO_CONT='NEWTON', 
                            FORMULATION='CONTINUE', 
                            ITER_GEOM_MAXI=10, 
                            LISSAGE='OUI', 
                            MODELE=model,

                            REAC_GEOM='AUTOMATIQUE', 
                            RESI_GEOM=0.01, 
                            ZONE=_F(ALGO_CONT='PENALISATION', 
                                    COEF_PENA_CONT=8400000000.0, 
                                    GROUP_MA_ESCL=('CONTACT_AXIROD', ), 
                                    GROUP_MA_MAIT=('CONTACT_RODAXI', ))) 
#comment: Load_Rod = AFFE_CHAR_MECA(FORCE_FACE=_F(FX=-2500000.0,

                                      DY=-0.0291, 
                                      DZ=0.0, 
                                      GROUP_MA=('LOAD_Rod', )), 
                          MODELE=model) 
resnonl = STAT_NON_LINE(CARA_ELEM=elemprop, 
                        CHAM_MATER=fieldmat, 
                        CONTACT=contact, 
                        CONVERGENCE=_F(ITER_GLOB_MAXI=40), 
                        EXCIT=(_F(CHARGE=BC), 
                               _F(CHARGE=DPL_Rod,

                        MODELE=model, 
                        SOLVEUR=_F()) 
resnonl = CALC_CHAMP(reuse=resnonl, 
                     CONTRAINTE=('SIGM_ELGA', 'SIGM_ELNO', 'SIGM_NOEU'), 
                     CRITERES=('SIEQ_ELGA', 'SIEQ_ELNO', 'SIEQ_NOEU'), 
                     FORCE=('REAC_NODA', ), 
                     RESULTAT=resnonl) 
table = POST_RELEVE_T(ACTION=_F(GROUP_NO=('BC_Petit', ), 
                                INTITULE='Force', 
                                LIST_INST=LIS, 
                                NOM_CHAM='REAC_NODA', 
                                NOM_CMP=('DX', 'DY'), 
                                OPERATION=('EXTRACTION', ), 
                                RESULTAT=resnonl)) 
IMPR_RESU(RESU=_F(RESULTAT=resnonl),

                  RESULTAT=resnonl), 
          UNITE=4) 
IMPR_TABLE(TABLE=table, 
           UNITE=8)
