Abstract
This research presents the construction of a tunable Tm:YLF (Thulium-doped
Yttrium Lithium Fluoride) laser around 1.9 µm for mixing it with longer in-
frared wavelengths while implementing frequency up-conversion with the use of
the Silver Gallium Selenide (AGSe) non linear crystal. Two diﬀerent laser diodes
that serve this objective are used to pump the 1.9 µm laser cavity. The ﬁrst laser
diode consists of a more complex focusing procedure and maximum pump power
of 3W, while the second one is composed of a simpler focusing scheme with max-
imum output power in the order of 16W, bringing the 1.9 µm laser further above
threshold. In addition, two distinct prisms, Schott N-SF14 and CaF2, are uti-
lized with separate values of refractive index, dispersion and transmission rate
in order to compare the wavelength tuning range they can achieve. Tuning in
the above two circumstances is possible across two completely diﬀerent ranges.
The N-SF14 tuning range is found to be [1903nm −1932nm], while the CaF2
tuning range is equal to [1922nm−2080nm]. In the N-SF14 cavity, nitrogen gas
is attempted to purge the laser cavity from water vapor to investigate both the
displayed jumps between the wavelengths and the output power shape in the
above tuning range. With both the AGSe nonlinear crystal embedded inside
the CaF2 laser cavity and pump power equals to 4.56W, the tuning wavelength
range of the tunable laser is inspected to be [1907nm −1935nm]. Intracavity
frequency up-conversion is attempted in the CaF2 prism tuned cavity, without
being able to detect the up-converted signal via an InGaAs camera. To rem-
edy this, eventually, single-pass up-conversion is implemented mixing the pump
1.925 µm light with a 11 µm infrared light coming from a Globar to produce a
1.63 µm up-converted signal using a 10mm AGSe non linear crystal necessary
for the Sum Frequency Generation (SFG) process; a second-order non linear ef-
fect. Theoretical 0.0648% up-conversion eﬃciency of 11 µm light was measured
for about 1W output pump 1.925 µm ﬁeld.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at DTU Photonics in fulﬁllment of the requirements
for acquiring a MSc diploma in Telecommunication - Optical Communication
study line.
A tunable 1.9 µm Tm:YLF laser is built for mixing it with longer wavelengths
by implementing frequency up-conversion. At the same time, a better control
of the operating wavelengths is achievable via the tuning process and the use of
a prism inside the laser cavity. The N-SF14 prism tuning range is found to be
[1903nm −1932nm], while the CaF2 prism tuning is possible across the range
[1922nm −2080nm] . Theoretical 0.0648% single-pass up-conversion eﬃciency
of 11 µm light by using the AGSe crystal was measured for about 1W output
1.925 µm pump power.
The ﬁrst aim of this study is to describe the main principles as well as the
fundamental considerations needed for the design of such kind of a survey. Ad-
ditionally, and most importantly, this study intends to provide the experimental
work and the steps followed to build the tunable 1.9 µm laser ﬁrst, and then
apply the up-conversion stage. In the following, a brief overview of the contents
of the thesis is given.
Chapter 1 is an introduction to the concepts and the procedural steps followed
throughout this survey.
Chapter 2 describes the principles and the physics that are implemented in every
part of the experimental work of this survey. It starts with the basics of a laser,
how it operates and what are the fundamental optical components necessary
for the accomplishment of the project. It ends with the second-order non linear

theory that is an underlying basis for the implementation of the sum frequency
generation phenomenon.
Chapter 3 contains the detailed steps that were done to accomplish the build-
ing of the tunable laser, and ﬁnally the up-conversion second-order non linear
eﬀect. It starts with the familiarity and hands on experience with the optical
components needed for the design of the project, and it ends with the ﬁnal
tunable 1.9 µm laser built to pump long infrared light to generate 1.62 µm up-
converted signal through the intracavity SFG eﬀect. In addition, the single-pass
up-conversion stage is described where a straight cavity 1.925 µm pump is mixed
with IR light to produce 1.63 µm signal through SFG by using the AGSe non
linear crystal, which has a very broad transmission in longer IR wavelengths.
Chapter 4 contains the ﬁnal conclusions for this survey as well as recommenda-
tions for further improvement in future projects.
Appendix depicts some more pictures taken during the experimental process, as
well as, it provides a detailed description of all the materials used throughout
the survey.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Solid state lasers emitting around 1.9 - 2 µm play a major role in the ﬁelds
of medical diagnosis and surgery, laser radar and remote sensing [1, 2, 3]. The
propitious absorption in water makes such lasers also very useful for medical ap-
plications. Due to the strong absorption in water, which is the basic ingredient of
biological tissue, important heating of small areas is accomplished. This allows
for very precise cutting of biological tissue. Furthermore, the bleeding during
laser cutting is suppressed by coagulation, which makes the solid state lasers
emitting around 2 µm ideal for many surgical procedures. The 2 µm lasers that
operate in the so called "eye safe" region, have great market potential especially
in free space applications where eye safety is very important. One example of
their usage can be seen in LIDAR systems. Moreover, in the wavelength range
around 2 µm there are absorption lines of a number of atmospheric gasses (e.g.
H2O, CO2, N2O) which can be detected and analyzed in this spectral region.
One of the key technologies opened up by tunable laser sources around the
2 µm region is the sensitive detection of some lighter atmospheric gasses and
molecules. There are many methods of detection with varying complexity and
suitability for diﬀerent applications; many of these employ a tunable source to
allow the probe wavelength to scan through the wavelengths of interest. Remote
chemical sensing with compact laser sources in this wavelength range has good
potential in the chemical and petroleum industries in terms of safety and quality
control, as well as in medical and environmental applications.
==PAGE:24==
2
Introduction
The most common rare earth elements that have been used as active media in
solid state lasers to generate alternative wavelengths are the Tm (thulium) ions.
Thulium lasers have the great advantage that the Tm3+ ions can be directly
excited with commercially available laser diodes around 790 nm. Speciﬁcally,
high power 1.9 µm Tm lasers have been widely investigated in the past [4, 5].
Thulium lasers emitting in the spectral region of 1900 - 2010 nm have been de-
veloped to very high power levels for melting processes such as polymer welding
due to their higher volumetric absorption in unﬁlled polymers [6]. Tm:YLF has
attracted strong interest in the past decade due to its possibility to obtain high
eﬃciency output when operating in the mid-infrared region, especially around
1908 nm [7].
Non linear frequency conversion based on second order non linear susceptibilities
of a dielectric medium provides an attractive route to a widely tunable, eﬃcient
and powerful source of mid-infrared. Non linear interactions like Sum Frequency
Generation (SFG) are tunable and eﬃcient in the wavelength regions that are
not accessible with solid-state or gas lasers and are available with much higher
powers and superior beam properties compared to semiconductor diode lasers.
SFG process, which generates a new signal at a shorter wavelength, is also
known as frequency up-conversion. It can be utilized to translate the detectable
signal from longer to shorter wavelengths where high performance detectors are
available. Sum Frequency Generation can be done either as a single-pass of the
mixing laser through the active material or by placing the active material inside
the mixing laser cavity to achieve better conversion eﬃciency and experience
high eﬀective power gained with very little pump power.
Imaging and fast
spectroscopy in bulk material can be achieved, as diﬀerent wavelength will be
up-converted at diﬀerent input angles [8].
The Optical Sensor Technology group at DTU Photonics Department, which is
located at Risø campus, have previously published papers presenting eﬀective
intracavity up-conversion in bulk materials [9, 10, 11], and master students have
worked in mid-IR intracavity up-conversion detection, mixing 1.9 µm with 1064
nm laser [12]. The goal of this research is to build a 1.9 µm Tm:YLF laser
capable of both intracavity and single-pass up-conversion by using the Silver
Gallium Selenide (AGSe) non linear crystal.
Furthermore, another essential
aspect of this survey is to obtain hands on experience on working both with
the construction of 1.9 µm laser and the implementation of SFG employing the
AGSe crystal, as both are of primary interest in this group.
The motivation for introducing a 1.9 µm laser is twofold.
Firstly, by using a 1.9 µm laser as a pump laser, either intracavity or single-pass
up-conversion of long wavelength infrared light can be implemented. This re-
quires the non linear crystal used for that eﬀect to be transparent at the pump
wavelength, at the signal wavelength, and at the target wavelength. However, to
==PAGE:25==
3
our knowledge, there is a limited number of suitable non linear optical crystals
that are transparent in the 10 - 20 µm wavelength range, have good thermal
and mechanical properties, and also have high non linear coeﬃcient. Recent
improvements in mixture chalcopyrite non linear crystals like the Silver Gal-
lium Selenide (AgGaSe2 or AGSe), which is used in this project, have provided
a path for eﬃcient tunable frequency agile mid-IR sources. Silver Gallium Se-
lenide possess wide transparency, high non linear optical coeﬃcients, as well as
enough birefringence for phase-matching in mid-IR. Its transparency goes up to
approximately 18 µm. This makes it an attractive non linear optical material
for eﬃcient parametric devices in mid-IR. The challenge in this project is to
use the aforementioned AGSe non linear crystal for Sum Frequency Generation
(by mixing 11 µm and 1.9 µm pump from the laser is going to be constructed)
and not for Second Harmonic Generation, for instance, which has been widely
demonstrated in the past [13, 14].
Secondly, the 1.9 µm laser can be used as a pump laser in the quantum optics
area, and speciﬁcally for quantum measurements in the mid-IR using single-
photon detectors, which is something new and quite interesting ﬁeld for the
Optical Sensor Technology group at DTU Photonics. So far, 1064 nm lasers
have been mostly used for frequency conversion. However, mixing with wave-
length shorter than the signal wavelength adds noise to the signal, e.g. due
to up-converted spontaneous parametric down conversion. The single photon
wavelength (or shifting/generation) phenomenon is of the essence.
A down-
conversion quantum interface is able to be implemented, where the single pho-
tons emitted at 853 nm can be down-converted to the telecommunication wave-
length 1550 nm using a 1.9 µm pump laser. Moreover, the single photon de-
tection is under surveillance in this group. Especially, up-conversion detection
at the single photon level in the telecom wavelength spectral window of 1550
nm by mixing it with the 1.9 µm laser can be done, with the up-converted sig-
nal to be around 853 nm of interest. Low loss transmission is an intrinsic and
unique property for single photons at 1550 nm in optical ﬁber [15]. In quantum
networks over optical ﬁber, single photons at 1550 nm are used for virtually all
quantum information tasks, such as remote sensing, quantum metrology, quan-
tum computation and quantum cryptography [16]. The narrow-band photons
in the 780 - 950 nm region possess the most eﬃcient and contemporary single-
photon wavelength sources, and an ability of being easily detected by a low noise
silicon avalanche photodiode. Therefore, a coherent photonic interface is nec-
essary which is capable of spectrum shifting (e.g. with second-order nonlinear
crystals) and non linear frequency conversing (e.g. sum frequency generation)
in the telecom band simultaneously. Single photons coherent state at 1550 nm
is important in the development of future quantum communication tasks such
as quantum key distribution [17].

Theory
In this chapter, all the theory that should be taken into consideration for the
purpose of this survey is introduced. The following subsections are describing
the physics that are implemented behind every part of the experimental work.
2.1
What is a laser
A laser is an apparatus that emits light through a process of optical ampliﬁcation
based on the stimulated emission of electromagnetic radiation. It is basically an
acronym for "light ampliﬁcation by stimulated emission of radiation". A laser
usually comprises an optical cavity in which light can circulate. This can be done
in practice by using at least two mirrors. Within this cavity and between these
two mirrors a gain medium (e.g. a laser crystal) should be placed to amplify
the light. Without the gain medium, the circulating light would become weaker
and weaker in each resonator round trip, because it experiences some losses, e.g.
upon reﬂection at mirrors. The gain medium requires some "pumping", e.g. by
injecting light (optical pumping) or an electric current (electrical pumping) until
population inversion is reached. In other words, enough atoms must be located
at elevated energy states, so a photon that passes through the gain medium has a
higher probability of stimulating emission of another photon than of the photon
==PAGE:28==
6
Theory
being absorbed. The principle of laser ampliﬁcation is stimulated emission. A
laser can not operate if the laser ampliﬁcation is smaller than the resonator
losses; the device is then below the so-called laser threshold and only emits
some luminescence light. If the gain is larger than the losses, the power of the
light in the laser resonator rises quickly, starting for example with low levels of
light from ﬂuorescence. To support lasing, the two mirrors of the cavity must
have radii of curvature and be placed in such a way that the light mode is stable.
2.2
Population inversion in a 3-level laser
As is mentioned in section 2.1, the source of the ampliﬁcation is stimulated
emission that occurs from atoms in higher energy states in the gain medium.
Population inversion by optical pumping becomes possible when a three-level
system is used. Pump light with a shorter wavelength (higher photon energy)
can transfer atoms from the ground state (level 1) of energy E1 and atom pop-
ulation N1 to the highest level (level 3) of energy E3 and atom population
N3. From there, spontaneous emission or a nonradiative process (e.g. involving
phonons in a laser crystal) triggers atoms to quickly decay to an intermediate
metastable level (level 2) with energy E2 < E3 and E2 > E1, and atom pop-
ulation N2, called the upper laser level so that atoms can accumulate there.
From that level down to the ground state (level 1), the laser transition with
stimulated emission occurs. With suﬃciently high pump intensity, population
inversion for the laser transition can be reached as stimulated emission by the
pump radiation is prevented from the transfer to the intermediate metastable
level. The entire process is presented in Figure 2.1.

measured as gain g. In a simple model, the electric ﬁeld amplitude E will increase
exponentially over time with gain g:

2.3 Thulium-doped Yttrium Lithium Fluoride laser (Tm:YLF)
11
The gain medium used in this project is Tm:YLF 5% doped with Tm3+. Thulium-
doped Yttrium Lithium Fluoride (Tm:YLF) is a crystalline material composed
of Yttrium Lithium Fluoride (YLF) doped with thulium atoms. It is used widely
in optics and solid-state single crystal laser rods.
Tm3+-doped solid-state lasers operating in the 1.8 – 2 µm spectral range are
of great use in numerous applications, such as Lidar, medical operation, remote
sensing, and micro machining [22, 23, 24, 25, 26]. Tm:YLF crystals are one of the
most promising Tm3+-doped materials because of their negative temperature
coeﬃcient of the refractive index, strong natural birefringence, providing a linear
polarization generation, and a wide emission range.
However, in the study of Tm3+-doped solid state lasers, their inherent instabil-
ity is a common problem [27] that is mainly aroused by the relaxation oscillation
generated by the interaction between the intracavity radiation and the active
media. Many factors involving the mechanical vibration of the optical elements,
instability of the pump power, and thermal instability of the active media along
with the combination of the long lifetime of the upper laser level and relatively
low laser gain of the Tm3+-doped crystal, result in the intrinsic intensity insta-
bilities of this class of lasers [28]. These instabilities can cause damage to the
ﬁlms of optical elements and destabilize the operation of the entire laser setup
[29].
YLF is a naturally birefringent material, capable of producing linearly polarized
output with virtually no depolarization loss. Wavelength tuning of a Tm:YLF
laser from 1850 to 2050 nm has been demonstrated [4] and negative thermal lens
eﬀect of this crystal makes it suitable for acquiring high power laser output. This
is due to the fact that negative thermal lens eﬀects could be amended by the
positive lensing eﬀect caused by bulging of the end faces [30].
Thulium doped lasers can be pumped with ∼792 nm diode lasers and have the
advantage that Thulium exhibits a cross-relaxation process which creates two
ions in the upper laser level for each pump photon absorbed. YLF is an attrac-
tive host material since it has a relatively weak thermal lens. Thus, resonators
can be designed which have robust outputs over a wide range of pump powers.

Transverse modes
Many diﬀerent transverse distributions of light can be encountered in a laser
cavity, although something should be usually done to suppress unwanted trans-
==PAGE:36==

The diﬀerent transverse irradiance patterns are referred to as
Transverse Electric and Magnetic (TEM) modes. In an unconﬁned cavity with
modes having a rectangular geometry in a Cartesian coordinates system, trans-
verse modes are identiﬁed by their irradiance distributions and are designated
TEMmn, where the subscripts m and n refer to the number of nodes along
the two orthogonal axes x and y. The most optimal mode with the highest
brightness, and the goal of this project, is the fundamental mode TEM00, which
has a Gaussian irradiance distribution and it stays with this distribution while
passing through optical systems. This mode experiences the minimum possible
diﬀraction loss, has minimum divergence, and can be focused to the smallest
possible spot [19]. For these reasons, it is often imperative that the laser should
be restricted to operate at this mode.
Higher order modes have a larger spread and suﬀer higher diﬀraction losses.
The lower the order of the mode is, the smaller the beam diameter, the nar-
rower the far-ﬁeld divergence, and the lower the beam quality factor M2 value
[19]. In general, some lasers tend to operate at the highest-order possible mode,
because the larger beam diameter may allow them to extract more energy from
the lasing medium.

3.1
1064 nm laser alignment
In this very early stage of the project, the goal was to get familiar with optical
components that will be used in the following stages in order to build an op-

construct a simple laser cavity as a ﬁrst step, and make the laser operate at a
speciﬁc wavelength range.
Thus, the ﬁrst test was to build a simple straight cavity operating at 1064
nm, where it was easy to handle the beam inside the optical cavity and ensure
whether the laser was lasing or not, by using a common near-infrared detection
card. If the wavelength of a laser is longer (i.e. 1.9 µm, as is seen later in the
experimental work), then the only indication showing that the laser is lasing,
is the blinking and the reduction of the laser crystal ﬂuorescence. However, in
1064 nm systems, the laser can be easily led to a lasing mode by observing and
manipulating the light displayed at the near-infrared detection cards.
For this ﬁrst 1064 nm test, a Nd:YVO laser crystal had been used with the aid
of two mirrors, one highly reﬂective (HR) plane at 1064 nm and one curved with
150mm (R) radius of curvature. The latter was 95% partially reﬂective at 1064
nm. As a logical consequence, the output transmission of the curved mirror was
5%, so the output power of the system could be calculated at this point. In
order to pump this cavity with power, a laser diode (LD) with 808 nm pump
wavelength and maximum 1A of giving electrical current was introduced. The
808 nm light coming out of the laser diode was focused in the center of the laser
crystal with a beam radius about 75 µm, to excite its ions. The straight cavity
can be seen in Figure 3.1.

3.2.1
Laser Diode focusing
Due to the fact that the Tm:YLF gain medium of the laser can be pumped
by the 790 nm [42], 792 nm [35, 32] and 793 nm [34] wavelengths, the choice
of the appropriate laser diode should be taken according to that fact. Thus, a
laser diode was used that can pump the laser crystal at 792 nm with maximum
output power of 3W.
The output facet of the laser diode (LD) is a rectangle, which is wider in one
direction comparing to the other one. The output light diverges faster along
the direction where the facet is narrow (fast axis) than it does in the broader
direction (slow axis). In the following, the slow axis was aligned horizontally,
and the fast axis was aligned vertically.
For pumping the laser crystal, the beam waist should be smaller than the 1.9
µm mode radius, to suppress higher order transverse modes. The pump beam
radius and the laser beam radius should match for optimal overlap between
them. Furthermore, focusing the pump diode too hard may lead to damage on
the end facets of the laser crystal, as well as signiﬁcant thermal gradients.

3.2.3
Folded cavity
To accomplish the ultimate goal of the project, which is the implementation of
the up-conversion stage, especially for the intracavity case, the cavity should be
expanded from two mirrors to three to locate the non linear crystal inside this
cavity in an appropriate position. For instance, in such a position the beam
should have a quite small radius. In the ideal case, the non linear crystal should
be located at beam’s waist spot.
At this point of the project, a folded cavity was built by using one more mirror
as an end mirror. One of the reasons why a folded cavity had been built was
to check the stability of the optical cavity. Another aim was to determine the
correlation between any possible alteration of the length of each arm of the
folded cavity. In other words, if the length of one arm changes, how the length
of the other arm should be modiﬁed to ensure stability in the cavity. In addition,
an interesting observation would be the supervision of the beam spot size for
diﬀerent positions in the arms of the folded cavity. This is due to the position
of interest for the non linear crystal placement in the intracavity up-conversion
case. The ﬁrst wise move to be checked in order to align a folded cavity is if an
already formed straight cavity can assist as a beam guide. The M2 mirror with
output transmission was located between M3 and M1 by making this straight
cavity lasing (see subsection 3.2.2). The folded cavity consists of a third end
plane mirror (M4) which is highly reﬂective (HR) at the 1850 - 2100 nm region.
It was placed as end mirror to catch ﬁrst the beam from the M3, and then the
reﬂected light was traced back to M3 (see Figure 3.8). The rotation angle of
the M3 mirror should not be larger than around 10◦if the desired shape of the
beam is a rounded beam and not an elliptical one. M4 was then adjusted so
the outgoing and the returning beam were overlapping each other as close as
possible. This could be inspected by using the liquid crystal thermal card.

3.2.4
Prism cavity
By introducing a prism inside an optical cavity, tuning capabilities are being
provided. This gives better control of the wavelength, solving the wavelength
instability in principle and giving exact information about the operating wave-
length of the laser. If the coupling into the prism is not done exactly at the
Brewster’s angle, there will be two small surface reﬂections for each of the ac-
tive surface of the prism, providing an outcoupling from the cavity which can be
used to monitor power and wavelength. It is important to ensure that the light
is horizontally polarized in order to take advantage of the prism and achieve the
desirable tuning.
At this point of the project, a simple prism cavity was built by adding a Schott
N-SF14 prism (see Table 2.2) inside the straight cavity shown in Figure 3.5. The
design for the prism cavity is presented in Figure 3.10. The prism was located
60mm away from the M1 pump mirror and 38mm away from the M2 end mirror.

3.3.1
Pump ﬁber-coupled Laser Diode characterization
One usual way of increasing the 1.9 µm intracavity ﬁeld is by growing the power
used to pump the Tm doped laser. This power is injected into the system by a
laser diode. The laser diode has been used so far in the previous sections had
maximum power of 3W, and the beam coming out of it had a rectangle shape.
A new 793 nm Chinese ﬁber-coupled Laser Diode (LD2) was introduced with
maximum output of 16W. According to the manufacturer’s speciﬁcations, the
operation temperature of the LD2 ranges from 15◦C to 35◦C.
The optical ﬁber that leads the light to the system has a 105µm core diame-
ter and a 125µm cladding diameter. A minimum bend radius should be 300
times greater than the ﬁber cladding diameter, which corresponds to 37.5mm
of minimum static bending radius. In addition, the laser diode has a feedback
protection between 1900 and 2100 nm wavelength range, which prevents the
LD2 from unwanted damage. Next, a ﬁber patch cable collimator with SMA905
connectors was used to simplify the collimation of the light coming from the
SMA905-terminated ﬁber. This collimator contains one factory-aligned aspheric
lens with focal length of 11.07mm that has an AR coating, in the order of ∼0.2%
reﬂectance at 793 nm (the minimum possible percentage of reﬂectance in the
AR coating wavelength range of the item) on both sides to minimize as much
as possible surface reﬂections. The beam divergence at 793 nm is 0.0265 de-
grees, while the output collimated beam diameter is 2.1mm. As a next step in
focusing process of the LD2, an achromatic doublet lens of 20mm of focal length
was used to achieve a tighter focus and reduce further the beam waist radius
without damaging the laser crystal. These kinds of lenses seem to have a better
optical broadband and oﬀ-axis performance than aspheric lenses due to the fact
that dispersion in the ﬁrst (positive) element of the doublet is corrected by the
second (negative) element. They also have quite reduced sensitivity to centra-
tion on the beam axis when compared to spherical singlets and aspheric lenses.
Its transmission rate at 793 nm is 98.47%. The resulting beam is a circular one
comparing to the rectangle shaped of LD1. The whole focusing procedure is
way simpler than the one followed for the ﬁrst laser diode, and occupies less
space in the breadboard.

minimize loss to absorption in the non-pumped volume of the crystal.

3.3.5
Up-conversion stage
The initial purpose of building this 1.9 µm laser was to test it for intracav-
ity parametric up-conversion.
If this is not succeeded, then single-pass up-
conversion is going to be tried out. Sum Frequency Mixing was implemented
by combining the pump 1.9 µm light with the 11 µm signal light coming from
==PAGE:93==
3.3 Final 1.9 µm setup with a new ﬁber-coupled Laser Diode
71
a globar. From the energy conservation, the up-converted light was calculated
to be in the order of 1.62 µm. The generated signal was able to be detected
by an InGaAs camera with high sensitivity in the near infrared region from 950
nm to 1700 nm. The available and of primary interest non linear crystal that
was employed in the up-conversion stage was the AGSe crystal, with cut angle
of θ=49.4◦. The Sum Frequency Generation process requires phase matching
to be eﬃcient. The usual technique for achieving phase matching in non lin-
ear crystals is birefringent phase matching, where one exploits birefringence to
cancel the phase mismatch. In the experiment, for the implementation of the
SFG procedure, Type I collinear phase matching was chosen to achieve higher
eﬀective non linearity for this three wave non linear procedure. Both 1.9 µm
and 11 µm inputs have ordinary (o) polarization, whereas the product wave of
1.62 µm has extraordinary (e) polarization.
3.3.5.1
Intracavity up-conversion
Before starting locating all the involved components to build the ﬁnal up-
conversion setup, the ﬁrst requirement to consider about is the type of the
imaging device and its quantum eﬃciency, whether it is high enough for the
speciﬁc detectable up-converted signal or not. Secondly, the coating of the end
mirror should be also checked, and speciﬁcally the transmission rate of the mir-
ror in the generated signal wavelength. It is usually preferable the transmission
of the end mirror at this up-converted wavelength to be as high as possible.
Furthermore, it is better to use a highly reﬂective plane mirror as an end mirror
than using a partially reﬂective one, in order to obtain larger intracavity ﬁeld.
In other words, by inserting a non linear crystal inside the laser cavity, more
losses are introduced, so it is more suitable to make use of a HR end mirror to
have enough stable circulating power inside the cavity. Therefore, the output
coupler M5 was supplanted by a highly reﬂective plane mirror with around 80%
transmission at 1.62 µm. In theory, the circulating power density is proportional
to the output power and inversely proportional to the cross section area A and
to the transmission T of an output coupler. This is reduced by internal losses
described by the round trip loss L and by radiation coupled out of the resonator.
To achieve lasing with the AGSe crystal inside the cavity, it was necessary
to adjust even further the exact position of the plane end mirror (M6). Thus,
instead of placing the end mirror 143mm away from the prism, as was previously
discussed in subsection 3.3.4, it was now located at 149mm away from the CaF2
prism. This should be done due to the insertion of a 10mm long AGSe crystal
with 2.6 refractive index value. The power at the output of the M6 end mirror,
before inserting the AGSe crystal inside the cavity, was measured 4.8mW, and
the power at the output of the curved mirror was found to be 3.3mW, with 3.04W

Chapter 4
Conclusion
The main goals of the project were two. Firstly, the construction of a tunable
Tm:YLF laser operating at around 1.9 µm was the ultimate aim of this survey,
which laser would have been possible to take advantage of and use it as a pump
source for frequency up-conversion. This was done by mixing it with light at
the 10 - 20 µm wavelength range where there are not many non linear crystals
transparent at both the 1.9 µm and this long infrared wavelength range. One
useful non linear crystal, transparent at the above wavelength ranges, used in
this research was the 10 mm long AGSe crystal. Secondly, the entire project
has contributed to gaining practical laboratory experience, encountering prob-
lems, and coming up with interesting observations that can be used in further
applications.
Besides the ﬁrst part of the experimental work which was used as an introduction
to a basic alignment, all the rest diﬀerent conﬁgurations were tested to ﬁnd
a more suitable one for the building of the tunable laser and eventually for
implementing the up-conversion stage. All the above accomplished by taking
into consideration time constraints, various emerging problems and of course
breadboard’s space constraints. Two diﬀerent laser diodes were used to pump
the laser and excite the Tm ions at 792 nm and 793 nm respectively.
The
former was a C-mount laser diode with maximum output power of 3W, injecting
rectangle beam in the cavity. The latter was a ﬁber-coupled laser diode, easier to
focus it than the ﬁrst laser diode, injecting circular beam and infusing maximum
==PAGE:106==
84
Conclusion
power of 16W. Having high pump laser diode power constitutes a useful occasion
in the demanding case of intracavity up-conversion stage, where the intracavity
power, thus, the conversion eﬃciency, can be increased by raising the LD pump
power.
Furthermore, two diﬀerent cases of prism tuned laser cavities were examined
to provide tuning capabilities and acquire better control of the operating wave-
lengths.
The ﬁrst used was the Schott N-SF14 prism with almost 5% loss. With this
prism, the laser was fairly stable with wavelength tuning range around [1903
nm - 1933 nm]. The spectrum was nicely distributed with single modes repre-
senting one wavelength each time the end mirror’s horizontal angle was adjusted.
The intracavity power was measured to be 3.5W with laser diode pump power
of 3W and using a partially reﬂective mirror as an end mirror. The honestly
interesting experiment in that cavity was that one when nitrogen gas was em-
ployed to purge the moisture out of the laser cavity. Although the N2 injection
was utilized to remove the water vapor, the big jumps between the wavelengths,
sometimes in the order of 8 nm, could not be signiﬁcantly improved in order
to get a more continuous tuning. What happened was that two more wave-
lengths were introduced with maximum jump of 7 nm. In contrast, the curve
of the output power of the laser experienced a smoother behavior than without
applying the nitrogen gas in the cavity. In the latter, the output power was
ﬂuctuating when the laser was operating at a new wavelength. Plexiglass was
brought into operation to build a sealed chamber making the environment inside
it dry enough to guide the moisture out of it. But, due to the outcomes came
of, apparently this chamber was not so well enclosed, which can be enhanced
in future applications. The gas seemed to be quite dry, so the problem of not
obtaining a more continuous tuning could also originates from the duration of
the gas injection, apart from the completely sealed cabinet that should be used.
Due to the fact that it was something that have never been tried before in the
group, the behavior of the gas was not something that could be predicted be-
forehand. Maybe, the N2 injection should have been lasted for more minutes,
but again a well enclosed cabinet is primarily what it should be aimed for.
The second prism that was tested was the ultrafast dispersive CaF2 with max-
imum absorption of 1.6% around 1.9 µm. With this prism, the laser was not
so stable, but with a broad wavelength tuning range around [1922 nm - 2080
nm]. The circulating power was found to be 15W, using a partially reﬂective
mirror as an end mirror. When the PR end mirror switched to a HR plane
one, the intracavity power was calculated in the order of 24W. The spectrum
was single-moded until 1942 nm, representing one wavelength each time the end
mirror’s horizontal angle was adjusted. Albeit, a multimode spectra was noticed
in the 1942 - 2080 nm range, probably because the dispersion starts to aﬀect
the tuning more in longer wavelengths.
==PAGE:107==
85
When the AGSe nonlinear crystal was inserted in the cavity for the intracavity
up-conversion stage, the laser was tunable across [1907 nm - 1935 nm] with
a number of well deﬁned reproducible modes. These modes had a bandwidth
<1nm, which means that there is no signiﬁcant broadening of the up-conversion
spectrum from the width of the laser lines. In this project, the intracavity up-
conversion eﬃciency has only been measured for the CaF2 prism tuned system.
In that cavity, the circulating ﬁeld was calculated to be 24W. This gives a
theoretical maximum conversion eﬃciency of 1.4%.
An InGaAs camera was
used to evaluate the up-converted signal. However, a problem had arisen with
the display of the generated signal. Due to the overlap of the ﬂuorescence of the
laser crystal and the wavelength of the up-converted one, a bright white spot
was exposed from the camera that is inseparably linked with the ﬂuorescence
of the Tm:YLF. Even when the 1.9 µm laser was lasing, and simultaneously
the globar was on, it was impossible for the 1.62 µm up-converted signal to be
displayed due to this overlap at around this wavelength. The truly unexpected
was the fact that the white spot of the ﬂuorescence was so bright, reducing the
chances of detecting the up-converted signal.
Because of the fact that not much could be done intracavity to prevent the ﬂu-
orescence from being detected by the camera, a single-pass up-conversion has
been implemented by mixing 1W of output 1.9 µm pump power with infrared
light coming from a globar. This gives a theoretical maximum conversion eﬃ-
ciency of 0.0648%. As the conversion eﬃciency scales linearly with the pump
ﬁeld, the 1.9 µm ﬁeld can be increased to give larger conversion eﬃciency. This
can be easily done by raising the ﬁber-coupled laser diode pump power to bigger
values than 5.9W that were tested in this experiment. The conversion eﬃciency
is also possible to get increased by decreasing the size of the focus in the AGSe
crystal. By setting the exposure time of the camera to 199 msec, various tests
have been implemented to prove that the up-converted signal had been well
generated via the Sum Frequency Generation process.
The immediate next steps if the project is continued would be to use the 1.9
µm laser as a pump laser to a system that is intended for up or down conversion
of photons to or from the telecom band, like the one shown in Figure 4.1.
Long-wavelength pumped up-conversion opens for a number of exciting future
possible implementations. Pumping with a wavelength longer than the signal
wavelength will greatly improve the signal to noise ratio, as a result of the
removal of Spontaneous Parametric Downconversion (SPDC) noise. With this,
single photon counting at 1550 nm becomes a possibility. For instance, this
could be used for detection of quantum entangle photons. Another future work
could be to build a two cascaded SFG stage for spectroscopy, to avoid using
an InGaAs detector, and instead, make use of the widely used low noise silicon
detectors with no need of special cooling. In the ﬁrst stage of the process, long-
wavelength infrared light (e.g. 12 - 18 µm) can be mixed with the 1.9 µm laser
==PAGE:108==
86
Conclusion
through AGSe SFG producing a signal around 1.67 µm (detetion with InGaAs
camera). Then this signal can be mixed with the remaining 1.9 µm light from
the ﬁrst stage via Lithium Niobate SFG to generate a signal around 0.88 µm
for detection with Si cameras. Another possible future use could be detection
in the far-infrared region (e.g. around 25 µm) with silicon based detectors using
cascaded up-conversion.
In the slightly longer term, in the up-conversion stage discussed in Chapter 3, the
globar can be substituted by a Quantum Cascade Laser (QCL) to achieve a more
precise display of the up-converted signal. Instead of a spot, the whole generated
beam is expected to be demonstrated, implementing a more accurate alignment
though. Another future possibility would be to analytically estimate the up-
conversion eﬃciency by checking both the curve of the quantum eﬃciency of
the camera and the gain described in its speciﬁcations. Then, the up-converted
power can be assessed by integrating the entire area, for various wavelengths.
A more long term possibility would be to introduce a smooth continuous tuning
in the prism tuned cavity by constructing a completely sealed chamber and
injecting a substantially dry nitrogen gas into it for more minutes than tried in
this research.
