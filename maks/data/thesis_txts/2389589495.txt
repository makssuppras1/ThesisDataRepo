Summary
In this thesis, an active frequency multiplier chain was designed which consisted
of an active frequency multiplier used to double the input frequency of 162.5
GHz to 325 GHz and a power amplifier which would supply enough power to the
doubler for it’s operation. The frequency multiplier was designed and analyzed in
both single-ended as well as balanced topologies. The latter could theoretically
provide double power compared to it’s single-ended counterpart but demands a
180◦hybrid coupler to do so, requiring an additional 3 dBm input power, ideally.
The 180◦hybird coupler was selected to be a Marchand balun and was analyzed
and designed. The topology for the power amplifier meant to fulfill the input
power requirement for the doubler was selected to be 2-stacked configuration
which is also analyzed and designed. As a part of combining the power, 2-Way
Wilkinson Power Dividers/Combiners were used. All the mentioned components
were analyzed, designed and simulated on Keysight’s Advanced Design System
and Momentum or on ANSYS’s HFSS.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The Terahertz frequency range (300 GHz to 3 THz), shown in Fig.1.1 lies in
the middle of microwave and optical frequencies. The lowest THz frequency is
too high for the microwave electronics to operate in whereas, the highest THz
frequency is too low for any photonic circuit to work on. Making terahertz the
only remaining unexplored part of the EM spectrum in terms of low cost, portable
and accessible technologies. This phenomena is also referred to as THz Gap.
Figure 1.1: Electromagnetic Spectrum [FX03].
Many research institutes and universities are trying to fill up this gap. One
research group is tricking low-cost and lower fmax devices like CMOS to produce
near THz frequencies [MA11], [HA13], using doppler phenomena: [AM13], while
most others are focused on developing devices with higher fmax (in THz range)
by enhancing expensive technologies based on InP, SiGe and GaN. Whichever
path one might follow, exploring this pristine band will provide them with high
resolution data in security scanning and medical sensing, due to the high data
rates available in this unused band.
CELTA is the acronym for Convergence of Electronics and Photonics Tech-
nologies for Enabling Terahertz Applications, a European Union funded project
which is trying to fill this gap from both the microwave as well as the photonics
side. This work is focused on filling the gap from the side of microwave elec-
tronics. The thesis is an outcome of Project CELTA and my supervisor’s active
==PAGE:12==

involvement with Ferdinand-Braun-Institut (FBH), Germany.
The goal set was to produce an output power of at least 5 dBm (3.16 mW)
at 325 GHz. The equipments present at FBH could produce an output power
of 7 dBm (5 mW) @ 162.5 GHz. Therefore, it was thought to design an active

1.1
Architecture
Generally, an active frequency doubler has fairly poor Conversion Gain. Thus,
a Power Amplifier is needed at it’s front to supply it with enough input power.
A power amplifier can also be cascaded after the frequency doubler but the gain
would be very low as a transistor’s Maximum Stable Gain, decreases with increase
in the frequency. Thus, it was decided to have a power amplifier at the input
side of the doubler where the operation frequency was half of what it was at the
output. Shown in Fig.1.2, is a simplified block diagram for the architecture used
to design the frequency doubler.
Figure 1.2: Block Diagram Schematic of the Active Frequency Multiplication
Chain.
The frequency doubler can be synthesized in a single-ended or a balanced
topology. The balanced design will need an additional 180◦hybrid which would
ideally, divide the input into 2 equal outputs with a mutual 180◦phase difference
between them. The 180◦hybrid was chosen to be a Marchand Balun due to
it’s wideband performance.
Both the topologies are addressed and compared
and later, it was found that Balanced doubler is superior to it’s Single-Ended
counterpart in terms of harmonic rejection and stability. Thus, it was used in
the finalized design.
For a single-ended power amplifier, it is difficult to produce a fair amount of
output power at a high frequency like 162.5 GHz. Thus, it was decided to use 2
transistors in stacked topology. Later, it was found that still the power generated

1.2
Softwares
The softwares used in the entire thesis was ADS (2015.01) for Harmonic Balance
simulations whereas, Momentum and HFSS (15.0.3) for EM simulation.
1.2.1
Keysight’s ADS
ADS stands for Advanced Design System and is an electronic design automa-
tion software system. It supports every step of the schematic and layout design,
frequency-domain and time-domain circuit simulation and electromagnetic field
(EM) simulation. For EM simulation, Momentum is used. It is a 3-D planar
EM solver used for passive circuit analysis. Momentum is a partial differential
equation solver of Maxwell’s equations based on the Method of Moments. ADS
consists of physical models like transmission lines, tees, joints and curves, etc.,
along with tools for optimizing circuits like a multiplier, oscillator, mixer, power
or low-noise amplifier.

The MMIC technology used in this thesis takes an advantage of InP DHBT
devices transferred onto an Aluminium nitride (AlN) carrier wafer. This eases the
extraction of heat from the active region of the device and trims down the extrinsic
base-collector capacitance, Cbcx [Wei+16]. The 0.5µm Transferred Substrate InP

experimental technology based on, baseline technology of 0.8µm TS InP DHBT.
The improvements include emitter-width down-scaled to 0.5µm and higher fmax.
It constitutes of active InP DHBT (Double Heterojunction Bipolar Transistor)
devices and additional levels of BCB-encased gold interconnect wiring, capacitors,
resistors, RF pads and vias for inter-layer connections. The layer stack for this
technology can be seen in Fig.2.1
Figure 2.1: Layer Stack.

CHAPTER 3
Frequency Multiplier
Frequency Multiplier is an electronic circuit that produces an output which is a
harmonic multiple of the input frequency. As will be explained later, harmonics of
higher magnitude are generated when an excitation is applied across a nonlinear
element where it is most nonlinear. At the output, the frequency multiplier is
tasked to filter out all the unwanted harmonics and permit only the desired ones.
Thus, theoretically, a frequency multiplier for any harmonic can be built, if the
desired harmonic component of the excitation frequency can be extracted out
with success.
A multiplier can be designed as a passive or active device which would ei-
ther incorporate the use of a diode or a transistor, respectively since both are
nonlinear devices. But, active frequency multipliers are superior to their passive
counterparts in terms of broad bandwidth, conversion gain and efficiency.
3.1
Nonlinearity & Harmonic Generation
A nonlinear system can be defined as a system whose output varies non-linearly
with a linear variance in the input. A linear system is the one which satisfies
“superposition principle”. For example, if a system produces an output y1 and
y2 when 2 excitations x1 and x2 were applied to it, separately. Then, for an
excitation of ax1 + bx2, a linear system should produce an output of ay1 + by2,
where a and b can be real, complex, time-variant or time-invariant [Joh]. This
means that the response of a linear, time-invariant system will include only those
frequency components which were already present in the excitation. Whereas, in
a non-linear system, new frequencies will be generated. Most of the electronic
devices are linear only for a small range but a full range analysis can display
a nonlinear behaviour. A nonlinearity in a system can be weak or strong. In
electronic circuits, a strong nonlinearity is generally avoided while designing a
Power or a Low-Noise Amplifier as it downgrades it’s performance while the
same is beneficial in synthesizing mixers and frequency multipliers.

AC voltage source, Vs where the output I-V response of the nonlinear device is

Thus, it was verified theoretically that the generated current, I comprise of all
the harmonics of the excitation signal, Vs. As every electronic device is nonlinear
over a wide range and weakly nonlinear or linear over a comparative small range,
it is important to select proper operating points, either to stay in the linear region
or to exploit the nonlinear region depending on the performance expected from
the device. Therefore, it is important to visualize this concept from the point of
view of operating points of a practical nonlinear device.
3.2
Practical Nonlinearity
In this section, the aforementioned property of nonlinearity is been looked from
a practical device’s perspective. A non-linear passive device is a diode and an
active device is a transistor. Since, the aim of this thesis is to perform active
frequency multiplication and amplification, a transistor would be used to explain
the practical nonlinear behaviour.
For a BJT in Common-Emitter configuration, the Base-Emitter terminal I-V
response would be swept in order to find the optimum point of operation for
the desired application. This operation point would drive the device into linear,
weakly nonlinear or strongly nonlinear regions. This is done at the Base-Emitter
terminal because the Collector-Emitter terminal, is used to drive the device in
active region i.e. for amplification which is purely a linear operation.
For the sweep, a new parameter, Output Current Conduction Angle (ϕ) is
defined. ϕ is defined as the fraction of the RF signal period where a non-zero
current is flowing [CGL09]. Thus, when ϕ = π, the BJT conducts for zero to π
i.e. half of the excitation signal in time-domain, when ϕ = 2π, the BJT conducts
for zero to 2π i.e. entire excitation signal and so on.

3.3 Design Theory
17
It can be concluded from Fig.3.2 that for maximizing the fundamental har-
monic at the output, the CCA should be equal or greater than π, signifying that
half or more of the excitation signal should be conducted. Similarly, for maxi-
mizing the 2nd harmonic, the RF input signal should be conducted from zero to
2.5π/4, which is less that half of the signal (ϕ < π) and so on. Care should also be
taken that the unwanted harmonics should not appear at the output. For exam-
ple, if the desired signal at the output is only the 2nd harmonic, which would be
the case for a Frequency Doubler, then an operating point must be chosen such
that the all other harmonic components are at their lowest such as (ϕ = 3π/4),
which serves as a pure short circuit for 4th harmonic and a comparatively low
value for 3rd harmonic but a near maximum value for the 2nd harmonic. The
DC component can easily be managed by using DC blocking capacitors at both
input and output networks which would also help to direct the DC bias current
from the biasing circuit to the transistor. Nevertheless, the 1st harmonic will still
have the highest component, comparative to that other harmonics which would
interfere with the output of the desired Frequency Doubler thus, needs to be
short circuited before such high unwanted current reaches the output terminal.
Similarly for synthesizing a Power Amplifier, the 1st harmonic should be maxi-
mized while minimizing all other harmonics, which is only possible when all of
the input current is conducted i.e. (ϕ ≈2π). For maximizing higher harmonics,
lesser conduction angles are required. For frequency multipliers higher than 2nd
harmonic, a huge input power is needed to get a fair amount of output power
which translates to poor conversion gain. In such cases, the large input power
may drive the device into breakdown due to a very low conduction angle. Thus,
Frequency Doubler and Power Amplifier are the most common devices usually
published. The scope of this thesis is only limited to these two devices.
From this point onward, the theory of Power Amplifier and Frequency Dou-
bler diverge. A Frequency Doubler being a device exploiting nonlinear behaviour
of the transistor while the Power Amplifier exploiting the linear behaviour. Subse-
quently, several tricks and topologies will be discussed for maximizing the desired
harmonic and minimizing others in their individual sections.

The greater the VCE, the more the Conversion Gain, as more DC Power is con-

The amplification at the VCE is not selective to any harmonic but for all of

angle takes care of suppressing other unwanted harmonics. However, maximizing
a certain harmonic while minimizing all others just with the help of conduction
angle alone is next to impossible. To successfully do this, an external harmonic
rejection circuit is needed.
3.4
Harmonic Rejection
The shunt R-L-C resonator for the output harmonic rejection shown in the Single-
Ended Frequency Doubler representation in Fig.3.3 was an idealized depiction.
In practical circuits, especially at such higher frequencies, lumped elements and
resonant circuits like dielectric resonators don’t work as expected or due to the
technology limitations, can’t be designed. Leaving us only with using transmis-
sion line stubs or a clever use of topology like Balanced Frequency Multiplier
circuit.
The Balanced Doubler Topology, makes use of a Marchand Balun which is a
180◦hybrid to cancel out all odd harmonics at the output. Thus, only the strong
unwanted harmonic remained at the output is the 4th, near to which’s minima
lies the maxima for 2nd harmonic. A good output harmonic rejection can be
achieved but input harmonic rejection is still an issue in this topology. Thus, to
solve the problem of the presence of unwanted harmonics, at the input of the
balanced doubler and at both the input and the output of a single-ended doubler,
a reflector network is used.
A reflector network or a frequency/harmonic trap as referred in [Cam98],
is a filter for attenuating or short circuiting only one specific frequency.
As
discussed earlier, for designing a frequency doubler, it is important to get rid
of unwanted harmonics at the output and input terminals. A reflector network
therefore, can be synthesized to serve this need. As the name suggests, a reflector
network reflects the unwanted harmonic back into the transistor.
This serve
two advantages. The first advantage is that the strong unwanted harmonic no
longer interferes with the VCO at the input and the desired harmonic at output
load. The second advantage is that the transistor gets supplied with the reflected
power which contributes in the 2nd harmonic amplification consequently, needing

3.5.2
Balanced Design Topology
The most common and widely used topology for a frequency doubler is the Bal-
anced Topology. It constitutes of two transistors along with a 180◦hybrid coupler
at the input, biasing, matching networks and a power combining network at the
output. The 180◦hybrid coupler could be implemented with a Marchand Balun
or a Rat-Race Coupler and as the name suggests, it is used to divide the input
power equally with a mutual 180◦phase difference between the two outputs. Such
design causes the input signal of fo Hz to split equally out of phase at the input
and nullify each others effect at the output combining junction thus creating a
short circuit for all it’s odd harmonics [Maa03]. The 2nd harmonic is generated
at the collector of the transistor so adds up in-phase at the junction of output
combining network.
A reflector network is not required at the output as the
combining network junction is already a virtual-ground at odd harmonics due
to the Marchand Balun. Thus, the effectiveness of the virtual ground depends
on how well the Marchand Balun is made. For an effective virtual ground, a
perfect 180◦mutual phase difference between the 2 output ports is required. A
well designed Marchand Balun would eliminate the need of an output reflector

3.6
Marchand Balun
For the frequency doubler to work in the balanced topology, a 180◦hybrid is
required, as shown in Fig.3.9. In the literature, Rat-Race coupler is suggested
[Cam98]. But, due to the it’s narrow-band performance, it was decided to use
Marchand Balun instead.
A Marchand Balun is a single-mode to differential-mode transformer and vice
versa. As the former, a single-mode (unbalanced) signal arrives at the input port
of the Marchand Balun, divides equally and appears at output ports 2 and 3
with a mutual 180◦phase-shift which is the reason they are termed as differential
(balanced) signals. Thus, the name “Bal-Un”. The compensated Marchand Balun
is known for its ultrawide-bandwidth performance and with proper selection of
balun parameters, it can even achieve a bandwidth greater than 10:1 [Bah+07].
As shown in the Fig.3.10, the balun has it’s Port 1 connected to the half-wave
long open circuited line which is then coupled with two quarter-wave long shorted
lines and on their opposite sides are the Ports 2 and 3 where the balanced output
signal appears. The open quarter-wave stub acts as a series resonator and the 2
short quarter-wave stubs acts as a shunt resonator, as shown in Fig.3.11, which
maintains the phase variation across the bandwidth. The bandwidth is the ratio
of characteristic impedances of short-circuited and open-circuited stubs which
increases as the ratio increases.

CHAPTER 4
Power Amplifier
A Power Amplifier (PA) is the most important component in any electronic sys-
tem ranging from telecommunication, heating to bio-medical microwave imaging.
For a wireless system, it is important that the information signal at the transmit-
ter to get delivered to the receiver or in the case of a transceiver, to receive back
the original signal with additional back-scattered information. In either case, it
is required for the transmitter to generate enough power so that it reaches to
the receiver in adequate amount. To meet this requirement, a Power Amplifier
is used in the transmitting chain.
In this thesis, the power amplifier has the same role. A frequency doubler
is the final block in the transmitting chain, the power amplifier is cascaded in
front of the doubler to provide it with sufficient power at it’s input. It was seen
that the single-ended frequency doubler needed a power of 10 dBm to get the
desired output. A balanced frequency doubler, due to at most, 2 dB loss at each
branch of the Marchand Balun, requires at least 15 dBm input power to reach
one frequency doubler circuit. Thus, the expected output power from the Power
Amplifier was set to 15 dBm.
As mentioned previously, an input power of 7
dBm @ 162.5 GHz. can be obtained from the equipments available at FBH Labs
which sets the limit for the input power of the PA. Thus, a total Gain of 8 dB is
expected from the power amplifier arrangement. All the mentioned parameters
and more others which define power amplifier performance would be explained
below.

4.2
Classes
As it was seen from Fig.4.2, that as the transistor starts to operate in large
signal regime, the nonlinear effects increase, Gain decreases, the rate of increase
in Pout falls but the η increases. In this chapter, this phenomena is analyzed. As
mentioned previously, the increase in nonlinearity is because of the interaction
of the input signal near to the threshold voltage of the transistor. So, by setting
different points of operation on, higher than or lower than, the threshold voltage,
different operating classes of the Power Amplifier can be defined. Thus, assuming
constant input drive level, such operating classes are defined on the basis of
transistor’s CCA (ϕ), explained previously.
The transistor is loaded with optimum impedance for the fundamental fre-
quency while short circuiting all other harmonics. This optimum impedance is
called as, “Tuned Load” and it will change with the point of operation (ϕ) at
which the transistor is driven. Fig.4.3 shows η, Pout and Gain as a function of
operating conduction angle (ϕ).
From Fig.4.3, it can be observed that, going from Class A to Class C, the
Efficiency (η) increases. The maximum achievable output powers occurred at
Class A, Class AB and Class B are nearly the same. But, as the Gain being
comparatively lower for Class B and Class AB w.r.t Class A. For Classes B and
AB, the high output power comes at an expense of an increased input power.
Either a PA can be designed, for consuming less DC Power by using high RF
Power (Pin), as in Class AB and Class B or for more Gain with less Pin and
higher DC Power consumption as in Class A. In the present scenario, the RF
power is at 162.5 GHz and power at such high frequencies is a scare resource.
Thus, it was chosen to design a Class A Power Amplifier.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The minimum requirement of 5 dBm from the Active Frequency Multiplier Chain
was met as the output received was 5.5 dBm @ 325 GHz. Few suggestions which
can further improve the overall design are presented below.
6.1
Scope of Improvement
6.1.1
Frequency Doubler
The balanced topology works very well in terms of harmonic rejection. It’s per-
formance can even be further improved with an additional Gm-Boosted design
with Cascode [Joh+16b]. A Frequency Doubler in Traveling Wave topology can
also be implemented as it offers an effective odd harmonic rejection without the
need of a Marchand Balun which is in itself very lossy component. Also, in this
topology, 4 transistors use a common output matching network which is a very ef-
fective design strategy for ensuring less losses. One such Doubler using Travelling
Wave topology with Reflector networks even claims to have achieved a positive
Conversion Gain (dB) [YZS14].
6.1.2
Marchand Balun
The Marchand Balun designed for this thesis though offers probably the best in
class phase imbalance but that comes with huge losses ≈2 dB, at each branch.
To solve this problem, an Active Balun can be designed using a transistor. A
transistor as a balun may also offer some gain with the same, 180◦phase imbal-
ance.
6.1.3
Wilkinson Power Divider
The 2-Way Wilkinson Divider and Combiner designed in this thesis offer a total
loss of 1.88 dB at all 4 branches. Those losses were partly due to the quarter-wave
long transmission lines. The quarter-wave long lines can be shortened by using
==PAGE:102==

shunt capacitors at both sides. As shorter lines offers lesser losses, the losses will
be decreased.
6.1.4
Active Frequency Multiplier Chain
The active multiplier chain in this thesis was designed in such a way that the
components like PA and Doubler can be tested out individually. However, this
produced a redundancy as the Wilkinson Divider in the PA chain, as shown in
Fig.5.8, could have been replaced by a Marchand Balun from the Doubler circuit.
Such would be a more efficient design and can be implemented to save power and
reduce size.

APPENDIX A
Appendix
For testing any MMIC device on wafer, an RF pad is used. When a signal is
applied to a DUT (Device Under Test), 2 conductors are required, one for signal
and another for ground. Hence for symmetry purposes, most often Ground-Signal-
Ground RF pads are used. For testing the DUT, the central RF probe tip is kept
on the Signal pad whereas, the other 2 Ground probes are kept on the Ground
pads placed on both sides of the Signal pad as shown in Fig.A.1
Figure A.1: RF probe kept on RF pads for excitation [Kra+13].
Placing a probe on the RF pad changes the impedance for which the DUT
was designed for and hence it gives out false results. Thus, it is considered as a
good design practice to emulate the impedance effects of the external probe tip
beforehand and optimize the circuit accordingly. For this thesis, the behaviour
of the RF probe and pads from Fig.A.1 was emulated by Fig.A.2.
