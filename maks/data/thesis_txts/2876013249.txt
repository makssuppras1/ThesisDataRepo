Abstract
Projective Geometric Algebra (PGA) is a framework for representing Eu-
clidean geometry. Building on the recent Geometric Algebra Transformer
(GATr) architecture, this thesis introduces GATMA, which is a PGA based
transformer that is used for molecular analysis. The model encodes each
atom of a molecule as a 16-component multivector, where the trivector com-
ponents store Cartesian coordinates, and the scalar component stores the
atomic number.
Two datasets are used for the evaluation: (1) the QM9 data set with dipole
moment regression as the target, and (2) a synthetic set of 3-D points used
to prove equivariance and invariance. In QM9, GATMA achieves a mean
absolute error of 0.379, outperforming a naive baseline (MAE ≈1.17) by a
factor of three. In the artificial data set, the predictions of the model remain
stable under rotations, reflections, and translations, with a maximum MAE
increase of 5 · 10−9, which confirms strict equivariance of E(3).

Chapter 1
Introduction
Geometrical problems can be represented and solved using geometric alge-
bra. This type of problem often occurs in fields such as computer graphics,
robotics, and computer vision [Dorst et al. [2007]]. However, in mechanical
physics, geometrical algebra can also be used to describe motion in two- and
three dimensions. Even in more advanced physics topics like electromag-
netic field theory, quantum mechanics and the theory of relativity geometric
algebra have become useful [Doran and Lassenby [2009]].
There is a version of geometric algebra called Projective Geometric Al-
gebra or Plane-Based Geometric Algebra (PGA), which uses planes to con-
struct different kinds of objects from Euclidean geometry. PGA uses a 16-
dimensional vector space to represent geometric objects such as points, lines,
and planes, and the operations that can be performed on these.
It has been shown by [Brehmer et al. [2023]] that it is possible to build a
Geometric Algebra Transformer (GATr) neural network that is successful in
solving problems like n-body dynamics, wall-shear-stress estimation on large
meshes of human arteries, and robotic planning through invariant diffusion.
The aim of this master project has been to investigate the possibilities of
using a Plane-Based Geometric Algebra neural network for molecular analy-
sis. The writing of code for an PGA tool was the basis for building a neural
network transformer, which was tested on molecular data. In this thesis a Ge-
ometric Algebra Transformer for Molecular Analysis (GATMA) is presented,
and the GATMA is trained to calculate the dipole moment of a molecule.
The code is available at https://github.com/Amjed1234567/GATMA.

Chapter 2
Projective Geometric Algebra
The projective geometric Algebra (PGA ) is also called the plane-based ge-
ometric Algebra [Dorst [2020]]. This is because planes and hyperplanes play
a central role in this algebra compared to, for instance, linear algebra. In
PGA there are four basis vectors: e0, e1, e2 and e3. This is the reason why
PGA is referred to as G3,0,1. The subscript 3 means that there are 3 vectors,
e1, e2 and e3, for which the norm squared is equal to 1. And the subscript 1
means that there is one vector, e0, with norm squared equal to zero. Finally,
subscript zero means that in this algebra there are no vectors which have
norm squared equal to −1. In the following, the four basis vectors are always
orthogonal.
2.1

to the norm. The inner product of the basis vectors is shown in table 2.1.
The inner product is denoted with a dot, for example, e1 · e2 = 0. This is
why it is also called a dot product.
Second, there is the outer product. The symbol for the outer product is
∧, for example, e1 ∧e2. This is a plane made up of the two basis vectors e1

2.2
Blades and multivectors
The geometric product of k vectors is called a k-blade or just blade [Hestenes
[1999]]. The grade of the blade is the number of vectors it is made of [Dorst
et al. [2007]], which is k. In the following, only blades made of the four basis
vectors e0, e1, e2 and e3 will be considered.
If a blade has grade 0, it is a scalar. A vector has grade 1, a bivector grade
2, a trivector grade 3, and a blade with grade 4 is called a pseudoscalar.

2.3
Euclidean group E(3)
The group of transformations that preserve distance in the three-dimensional
space R3 is called the Euclidean group E(3), or simply E(3) [Doran and
Lassenby [2009]]. When performing these transformations, the shape and
size of a geometric object will not change. A geometric object is, for instance,
a point, a vector, or a rigid body.
A translation is a transformation that moves every point in the space by
the same vector. A rotation rotates the object around an axis. Reflection
is the mirror of the object across a plane. The combination of translations,
rotations, and reflections is also a part of E(3) [Macdonald [2025]], [Brehmer
et al. [2023]]. All the mentioned transformations are rigid motions, i.e, they
preserve distances, angles, and parallelism [Doran and Lassenby [2009]].
In contrast to GA, the multivectors in PGA represent points, lines, and
planes that do not have to pass through the origin. This makes it possible
to make arbitrary translations in R3. That is the reason why PGA is very
suitable for Euclidean geometry [Gunn [2020], Brehmer et al. [2023]].

Chapter 3
GATMA architecture
Now, let us take a closer look at the architecture of the Geometric Algebra
Transformer for Molecular Analysis (GATMA). In the following, we will go
through all the layers one by one. Later, we will also look at the differences
and similarities with GATr [Brehmer et al. [2023]]. The code of the GATMA
layers is found in the file gpu building blocks.py.
3.1
Shape of input tensor
Since there are more than 130,000 molecules, the data is divided into batches.
A batch contains a number of molecules that GATMA is trained on in each
step of the training. This number is called BATCH SIZE.
As mentioned above, different molecules may have different number of
atoms. The maximum number of atoms that a molecule can have is called
max atoms.
Each atom has a number of channels. These are the tokens, which in the
case of GATMA are multivectors. The number of channels for one atom is
called channels per atom.
From previous sections, we know that a multivector in PGA has 16 com-
ponents. So, now we see that the size of the input tensor X for the transformer
is [BATCH SIZE, max atoms, channels per atom, 16].

3.3
Bilinear layers
Linear layers can only be used for simple operations. However, these layers
are unable to compute distances, intersections, or transformations. Some-
thing that can mix grades and create new geometric features is needed. That
is why bilinear layers are necessary.
A bilinear layer is based on the two operations geometric product and join.
Please note that in this project join is the equivariant join.
First, the geometric product of the multivectors is found. Second, the equiv-
ariant join is found. The geometric product and the join are then concate-
nated across the feature dimensions.

3.6
Attention layers
The idea of attention goes back to the invention of the first transformer neu-
ral network [Vaswani et al. [2017]]. Originally, the transformer was used to,
for instance, translate from German to English. As an example, let us use
the sentence ”I have a car”. This sentence is tokenized, which means that it
is split into words. Then these words are embedded and positional encoded.
Embedding means that each token gets a vector representation, so the words
are converted into vectors made of numbers. Positional encoding is the or-
dering of the tokens, i.e. ”I” is first, ”have” is second, etc.
Each token is used to calculate three vectors q (query), k (key) and v
(value). All these vectors make up three matrices Q, K and V. The main
point of the transformer is to find how much attention each word should be
given. Attention is calculated in this way:

√dk · V )

habe ein Auto”. This is done on the basis of the different attention values
for each token. A token with a high attention value is more important in the
sentence.

3.7
Feed Forward Block
The Feed Forward Block (FFB) is made up of all the layers mentioned
above.
Please, see Figure 3.1.
It is an MLP-style block (linear layers
with non-linearity in between) for multivectors. First, the input tensor x
(shape[B,N,16]) is passed through an equivariant linear map (MVLinear)
and a scalar-gated GELU activation, producing a gated multivector x gated.
A reference multivector (either supplied or defaulting to x) is forwarded to-
gether with x and x gated to a bilinear layer (MVGeometricBilinear), which
computes the geometric product and the E(3)-equivariant join, concatenating
the two 16-component results into a 32-component tensor. This tensor is split
into gp part (geometric-product slice) and join part (join slice). Each slice
is processed by its own equivariant linear layer; their outputs are summed,
and then a dropout is applied.
The name Feed Forward Block has been chosen because information only
flows forward. There is no loop back.
Maybe you are wondering why there is a reference multivector. It is because
it is used by the join. Please, see the definition of join in the previous chapter.

3.9
Differences and similarities between GATMA
and GATr
3.9.1
Overall
Now, we will look at the ways in which GATr [Brehmer et al. [2023]] and
GATMA are different but still have similarities. First of all, both use PGA.
They are both built on the same projective geometric algebra. This is the
first similarity you would probably notice. All the components of the mul-
tivector (’1’, ’e0’, ’e1’, ... ’e0e1e2e3’), and all the tables with products and
dual relationships are mathematically very similar. The contents of the files
constants.py, multivector.py and batch operations.py from GATMA and the
GATr algebra definitions are functionally equivalent.
All important operations like geometric product, outer product, dual,
and join are defined in the same mathematical way. For instance, GATMA
join batch function and the similar GATr implementation would give the
same results for the same inputs because they are both defined as
dual(outer(dual(x), dual(y))).
(3.22)
But what are the differences between GATMA and GATr?
In the file multivector.py, there is an object-oriented approach with a
Multivector class.
Good for readability, learning, and testing operations.
Each operation creates new Multivector objects.
However, GATr has a more functional approach that is solely based on
tensors.
A multivector is represented only as a PyTorch tensor of shape
[..., 16], where ... represents batch and sequence dimensions. There is no
wrapper class. Please note that the GATMA file batch operations.py is also
tensor-based, but the core of the GATMA design is still the class-based file
multivector.py.
The idea behind GATMA was to create a general-purpose PGA library.
It has functions for rotation, translation, reflection, etc. It is designed to be
a toolbox for many operations.
It seems that GATr is a more specialized neural network. Probably, it is
optimized for the specific operations needed in a transformer, for instance,
attention-like operations.
There are no functions for transformations like
25
==PAGE:27==
rotate, translate, or reflect.
In the case of GATr, the network learns to
perform transformations through its equivariant features.
3.9.2
Architectural comparisson
GATMA and GATr architectures are both built to be E(3)-equivariant. This
is the most important similarity.
The biggest difference is the Feed Forwar Block. Both models compute a
geometric product and an equivariant join, concatenate the 32-dimensional
result, and then apply two separate linear maps (one for the GP part, one for
the Join part). GATr’s original description emphasizes the MLP-style gating
(GELU) before the bilinear step, whereas GATMA makes the split explicit
in the code.
GATMA inserts a dropout layer after the summed GP/Join linear projec-
tions, a regularisation step not present in the original GATr implementation.
In GATr the feedforward block is simpler and more standard. It con-
sists of two linear layers with a GatedGelu activation in between: FFN(x) =
Linear2(GatedGELU(Linear1(x))). This is the classic transformer [Vaswani
et al. [2017]] FFN pattern, just made equivariant.
GATr uses a fixed set of object types. For example, there is one multi-
vector for the position of an object, another for its velocity, etc. GATr input
embedding is more about encoding different types of geometric information
into the multivector, rather than expanding the number of tokens for a single
entity. This does not mean that GATr is not using more than one channel,
but it seems that encoding geometric information into the multivector has a
higher priority.
GATMA creates multiple tokens per atom by using a MVLinear Layer
to project a single input multivector into channel output multivectors. This
increases the capacity of the model.
This approach is well-suited for the
QM9 dataset. GATMA starts with a single feature vector per atom, and
then mapping into a multivector multiple representation.

When working with large datasets, a lot of memory is being used. To avoid
the ’RuntimeError: CUDA error: out of memory’ error some measures can
be taken. These are briefly described in the following.
30
==PAGE:32==
5.2.1
Automatic Mixed Precision
To save memory, Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) [PyTorch Docs] is used
during training. In mixed precision, some operations use the torch.float32
datatype and other operations use lower precision floating point datatype
(lower precision fp): torch.float16 (half) or torch.bfloat16. The basic idea
behind Automatic Mixed Precision is to match each operation to its appro-
priate datatype.
An important part of Automatic Mixed Precision is the gradient scal-
ing.
This is useful when the forward pass for a particular operation has
float16 inputs. In that case, the backward pass will produce float16 gra-
dients. However, if the magnitudes of the gradient values are small, they
cannot be represented in float16. Then the relevant parameters in the neural
network might not be updated properly, because the gradient values would
move towards zero. This is called underflow.
What happens in gradient scaling is that the network loss is multiplied
by a scale factor, and a backward pass on the scaled loss is invoked. This
scaling factor is also used for backward flowing gradients in the network. All
this prevents underflow.
5.2.2
Garbage collection
There is a garbage collector in Python [Python Docs] that automatically
frees memory when objects are no longer referenced. The problem is that it
works in cycles and that it may take some time before memory is reclaimed.
This is not good for objects that reference each other. That is why it is a
good idea to manually run a garbage collection cycle. Especially when using
PyTorch, because if the ’del’ command is used, then only the reference to
the object is deleted. If you want to release the actual CUDA memory right
now, manual garbage collection is needed. Or else you would have to wait for
the next garbage collection cycle. The manual garbage collection is achieved
using the command gc.collect().
Afterwards, the command torch.cuda.empty cache() can be used to clear
PyTorch’s internal caching allocator, making GPU memory available for
other tensors or jobs.

Chapter 7
Discussion
7.1
Key observations
The main goal of this thesis was to make a geometric algebra based trans-
former for molecular analysis. This has been achieved through the construc-
tion of the GATMA.
The main issue when training the GATMA on the QM9 data set has been
to get a low MAE, and at the same time avoid hitting the wall time of 24
h on the DTU HPC. An increase in the number of transformer blocks has
been observed to increase the training time. But the MAE fell. However,
a decrease in the number of transformer blocks decreased the training time,
but the MAE increased.
An increase in the number of channels in the Transformer has been shown
to slow down training, even though the MAE decreased.
A high learning rate and a low deacy rate produced results with the low-
est MAE.
Automated Mixed Precision (AMP) helped greatly in reducing memory
usage.
The key finding of this project is that it is possible to train a geomtric
algebra based transformer to predict the dipole moment of molecules. Sec-
ondly, GATMA is equivariant and invariant.

==PAGE:40==
The work in this thesis is pioneering. There is no doubt that there is
room for improvement. More could be done, but the duration of this project
was only 13 weeks, which turned out to be too little time for a project of this
magnitude.
7.2
Recommendation for future work
More time should be spent fine tuning the hyperparameters. Hopefully, this
will produce even better results.
The GATMA has only been used to predict the dipole moment of the molecules
in the QM9 data set. It is recommended to test GATMA on other targets;
Isotropic polarizability, Highest occupied molecular orbital energy, and Elec-
tronic spatial extent. Maybe this will produce new ideas about how to further
improve GATMA.
If possible, GATMA should be trained on computers even bigger than DTU
HPC. If these computers provide more training capabilities.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In this thesis a projective geometric algebra based transformer neural net-
work named GATMA has been introduced. It has been used successfully
to predict the dipole moment of molecules, and it has been experimentally
shown that GATMA is equivariant and invariant.
Compared to an earlier PGA based transformer neural network called GATr,
GATMA has been used for molecular analysis. There are architectural dif-
ferences and similarities between GATMA and GATr, but overall GATMA
has a simpler structure.
Although the results are promising, there are still some limitations. First,
GATMA has been tested on molecules only and specifically on the predic-
tion of their dipole moment. It would be a good idea to test GATMA on
other molecular targets and also on problems outside the field of chemistry
to better compare GATMA with, for instance, GATr.
Second, only trivectors have been used, since atomic positions are encoded
as trivectors. The architecture of GATMA allows all components of a multi-
vector to be used, but this has not been exploited in this thesis since it has
not been relevant for the dipole moment prediction.
It is encouraged that in future work, GATMA is tested on data sets requiring
more components of a multivector to be used, since it might show that this
type of neural network can be used to solve a more diverse set of problems
and not only dipole moment of molecules.

torch.einsum
In class MVLinear in the file gpu building blocks.py torch.einsum [PyTorch Docs]
is used a couple of times. It is used for tensor operations between two ten-
sors. Since the reader might not be familiar with this command, a small
explanation is provided here. There are two input tensors with some indicies
and one output tensor with some indicies:
torch . einsum ( ” input indices −>output indices ” ,
tensors )
Let us say that the same index appears in both inputs but not in the
output. In that case, we are summing that dimension. If an index appears
only in one input, then that index is preserved in the output. But when an
index appears in the output, it means that it is kept as a dimension. In the
code, torch.einsum is used, for instance, as follows:
torch . einsum ( ’ bnid , oi−>bnod ’ , xk [ : ,
: ,
: , k ,
: ] , Wk)
In this case, the indices are: b n i d, o i −> b n o d. In the first tensor
xk[:, :, :, k, :] the indices are b n i d. In the second tensor Wk the
indices are o i. The indices of the result are b n o d.
The index b is the batch index, n is the token index, i is the input feature
channel, d is the multivector component, and o is the output feature channel.

A.3
torch.bmm
In class MVAttentionHead in file gpu building blocks.py torch.bmm [Py-
Torch Docs] is used. Similarly to torch.einsum, it is used for tensor op-
erations between two tensors. The letters bmm stand for batched matrix
multiply. In plain words, it makes multiplication in a batch of 2-D matrices.
In general, if we have a tensor T1 with dimensions [B, M, K], a tensor T2
with dimensions [B, K, N], then the output dimensions are [B, M, N].
The reason why torch.bmm is used instead of torch.einsum is that torch.bmm
is faster when pure matrix multiplication is performed per batch.
In the same class, another version of torch.bmm called torch.baddbmm
is used. The difference is that in torch.baddbmm matrix multiplication and
addition is combined, meaning they are done in one call. Then extra memory
traffic and allocations are avoided. This is a cleaner and typically faster way
[PyTorch Docs].
