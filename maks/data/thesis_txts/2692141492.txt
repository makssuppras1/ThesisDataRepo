Abstract
Many startups face significant market navigation challenges. Most tools do not facilitate the
creation of a choice set of business opportunities and their rating. This study aims to determine
the most relevant factors for market navigation as well as evaluation and present a new model
that can be used as a supplemental step to established tools.
To investigate the difficulties of market navigation the case of the startup House in the Sky is
analysed to determine its market, ecosystem and innovation potential. Therefore, their busi­
ness opportunity choice set was created and narrowed down to its Gold Mines. For those,
holistic business cases were created and evaluated according to their ecosystem and innova­
tion potential. Further, the Gold Mines were rated and ranked based on an adjusted portfolio
management tool. Based on expert interviews and to compare the results a business opportu­
nity rating framework was developed and tested.
The final results suggest that House in the Sky should enter the outdoor camera market target­
ing outdoor events as it presents the most attractive market, architectural as well as disruptive
innovation and can be the centre of vast ecosystem innovation.

Introduction
Innovative business ideas and technological solutions emerge steadily. Their inventive nature
allows them to tackle surfacing challenges, especially in extreme situations. In 2020, the first
COVID­19 cases were detected in Denmark and people started to avoid public transport as
they did not want to get exposed to the virus. Further, people were almost confined to their
own houses ­ their stationary homes. Those new challenges also resulted in various new start­
ups helping people socialise and more. Though, citizens in other countries were even more
impacted by the pandemic and were heavily confined to their homes and could only see the
outside world through TV or their windows.
A high­tech solution to this challenge is House in the Sky. A manoeuvrable platform in the sky
where people could stay for extended periods and even travel with. This idea was developed
during the epidemic by Søren Riis, founder of the flourishing companies GoMore and Urban­
WineBox. He had the idea of using the established technology of airships and balloons to create
a new business idea. This concept was presented at the Technical University of Denmark to
curious and talented students who were keen on working on this project under his guidance
and advisorship. The proposed plan was to run this idea as a student­run startup.
The team consists of three students and two established businessman and founders.
• Julian Hoch Bruun, a General Engineering bachelor student at DTU with a great passion
for inflatable structures. He is responsible for the design of the balloon and payload
structure.
• Marian Petroc, a Wind Energy master student at DTU with immense interest in rapid
Rapid prototyping, 3D modelling and microcontroller programming. He is in charge of the
electronic aspects as well as 3D design and prototyping.
• Thomas Schosser, an Industrial Engineering and Management student at DTU with a
passion for entrepreneurship and a background in automotive engineering. He is leading
the project and responsible for organizing, project management and market navigation.
• Bo Benzon, founder and owner of Arkitekturministeriet, AMPD and Nova Tegra ApS, with
interest and experience in architecture and design. He assists with product design and
provides a space for the team to build small scale prototypes and to host their workshops.
• Søren Riis, Director of Studies, Philosophy and Science Studies at Roskilde University
and founder of GoMore and UrbanWineBox. He provides business inside and guidance
on how to structure the startup. Further, he is responsible for finances and funding.
As the project idea of a literal house in the sky is highly complex in terms of technology, legal
aspects, funding and many more Thomas proposed to keep the initial idea as a future goal
that can be aimed for and rather target a technical solution that appears more reachable in a
time frame of fewer than 3 years. Therefore, the group decided to focus on new markets that

Currently, no established tools exist that help early­stage startups narrow down market oppor­

• Does it create radical innovation and disrupt existing business ecosystems?
• Does this technology facilitate ecosystem innovation?

2
Delimitation
To keep the thesis coherent and brief it was consciously decided to not investigate and discuss
certain aspects in detail. Despite not focusing those aspect it has to be stated that those are all
relevant and have to be strongly considered for future work and research on the topic of market
navigation, especially for early stage startups.
Firstly, as the topic is purely management and entrepreneurship oriented the technical aspects
of the proposed technical solutions as well as the capabilities of the startup and its members
is assumed as possible and not discussed further. Those elements could heavily impact the
decision making progress but can be ignored for the sake of simplicity and focusing on the most
critical business aspects this thesis is related to. Vital discussion points could be the proposed
usage of helium, its capabilities, availability, risks, consumption and storage. Further, multiple
business opportunities are utilizing lengthy tethers to keep the objects in place. Here, the
materials and limits could impact solutions. The flying and maneuverability technologies itself
to control the in­air movement, the shape of the balloons, the usage of blimps and aerostats
are also not discussed. The same goes for the products’ power consumptions, the impact of
it on the weight, the travel time, the recharging via battery, cable or solar cells and how the
GPS­navigation or even the control software work are assumed as possible and therefore not
further discussed in detail. The last important technical aspect that is actively not thoroughly
analysed is the development time of the products as only rough estimates are presented. This
is done as the calculations for those require additional knowledge and time that the constraints
of this thesis do not present.
Next, the Danish aviation legislation on the foundation of the Danish Air Navigation Act limits the
tethered height of flying objects in Denmark to 100 meters. [1] This is the only legal limitation
that is stated in detail as other juridical aspects are not easily accessible and require legal
guidance for further understanding. Besides it is assumed that the limited height for tethered
balloons in Denmark is the biggest legal restriction that impacts the business opportunities.
Further, it is assumed that other European countries and even the global market share a similar
limit and, therefore, the proposed solutions would be applicable to markets besides the local
Danish and Nordic environments.
The last point directly relates to the geographical assumptions made in this thesis. The Dan­
ish market is not only representative for the local, but also for the global market accessible to
House in the Sky. The same goes for the assessment of the competitive environment. None
of the presented competitors are based in Denmark, though it is assumed that those also op­
erate in the local Danish market. The global market players from the United Kingdom and the
United States of America, China and more are seen as representative for the local and global
market environments. This simplification drastically reduces the assessment time of business
opportunities and speeds up the ranking and therefore the decision making process.
This streamlined evaluation process is a core part of the thesis as the research question also

Theoretical review
To provide a knowledge basis for the entrepreneurship, innovation and market navigation topics
current literature is analysed to further base the conducted research on it. Besides, this helps
to determine inconsistencies and gaps.
3.1
Market navigation
Before describing the selected models and how they build upon each other an overview of the
addressed innovation issue is given.
First, House in the Sky is neither the first early­stage startup nor business that has encountered
market navigation challenges. Gruber, MacMillan and Thompson encourage entrepreneurs
to thoroughly investigate possible market opportunities for technologies. This does not only
broaden the possible customer and market reach but rather helps to ”identify multiple market
opportunities prior to the first market entry”. Further, this allows them to choose out of a set of
previously developed market opportunities which can drastically increase the chances of the
products market acceptance and economic success. [3]
Besides, in another paper, the same authors add that more diversified founding teams are able
to establish not only a larger number but also more ”distant” business opportunities. Also, early
constraints in the market opportunity sets ”have lasting impact on the growth potential”. On the
other hand, greater diversification when developing and designing those opportunities ”has a
signiﬁcant effect on the likelihood of subsequent ﬁrm diversiﬁcation”. [4] The same statement
is underlined by Tal, Gruber and de Haan as the highlight that the way companies approach
their markets in very early stages can have drastic impact on if the business can adapt to new
market challenges and environments in the future. [5]
To better understand the difficulties and involved challenges of ”finding applications for tech­
nologies beyond the core business” Danneels and Frattini analysed multiple global companies.
There, they found that only a few businesses even attempt to find use cases and markets for
their current technology portfolio. Hence, they miss out on ”potential profits and avenues for
growth and renewal”. Another critical point brought up is that even society itself is indirectly
harmed as it can not ”reap the benefits of technological progress”. To better utilize the tech­
nologies potential and facilitate a better market fit they propose to follow four steps:[6]
1. Characterize the technology
2. Identify potential applications
3. Select from among the identified application
4. Choose the best entry mode
Another aspect limiting open innovation is the resource limitation that especially smaller com­
panies and startups encounter. Their concentrated ”business portfolio, specialized knowledge

rected to innovation­related actions. Hence, it is decisive to incorporate innovation activities as

3.2
Where To Play
The previously stated and reviewed literature provides a great insight into how to best struc­
ture and approach market navigation. To bring this knowledge to action the Where To Play
model is used to map and narrow down the possible market opportunities using helium­based
balloons. Even though, no tool is regraded as an industry standard for market navigation the
newly published Where To Play model is becoming more popular and gaining traction compared
to alternative tools.
It combines the theoretical knowledge of how to facilitate market navigation and presents it in
an easy to use tool that is optimally used in a workshop environment. The framework aims
to simplistically and also superficially design and evaluate business opportunities and is able
to narrow down the opportunity set to help the decision­making process of businesses and
startups. [8]
It is designed to be a three­step process. In the beginning, the market opportunity set has to be
created. Therefore, the core abilities or technological elements have to be described in detail
with their respective functions and properties. For this and for all following step the workshop
attendees are highly encouraged to stay unbiased and not focus on their maybe preferred
business opportunity. Next, all possible market opportunities have to be listed that can be build
based on the core abilities stated before. Besides, the possible target customers for the listed
business opportunities have to be stated. Step one of the model is completed once the market
opportunity set is created.
Next, the just created opportunity set has to be evaluated. To do this, all market ideas are rated
based on the same criteria. Those aspects are divided into two­axis ­ Potential and Challenge.
Everything is rated on a scale from low, mid, high to super high.
The criteria for Potential include:
• Compelling reason to buy
• Market volume
• Economic viability
The criteria for Challenge include:
• Implementation obstacles
• Time to revenue
• External risks
Once all opportunities are evaluated they can be systematically compared as they are mapped
on the attractiveness map as depicted in fig. 3.1. A Gold Mine is a business opportunity that is

At the end of the workshop, the team is presented with a set of primary business opportunities

The Where To Play model is a very interesting framework and the new method has been already
adopted by entrepreneurs and researchers. But using a critical point of view to judge it, there
are some shortcomings. The thesis is supposed to complement some shortcomings of the
model by adding an additional stage afterwards to rank the best opportunities as evaluated by
the Where To Play model. One of the decisive drawbacks of the Where to Play model is the
proposed choice set at the end. It only presents the analysed Gold Mines and their respective
Agile Focus Dartboard. Therefore, this tool those not facilitate the last step of the decision
making process as it lacks to rank the proposed opportunities and clearly declare which market
opportunity should be entered first.
3.3
GE/McKinsey model
Therefore, other tools will be creatively used to fill this gap and clearly declare which business
opportunity is most attractive and promising and should therefore be pursued by House in the
Sky.
The GE/McKinsey (nine­box) matrix is an advanced and extended version of the established
Boston Consulting Group (BCG) growth­share matrix. According to McKinsey, it can be de­
8

==PAGE:17==
scribed as the following. Similar to the BCG matrix, it also evaluates and maps products or
strategic business units on a two­axis grid – one for the industry and its position in said indus­
try.[9]
This framework was designed in the 1970s as a portfolio analysis for determining a corporate
strategy. The companies business units would be mapped and according to how they place
recommendations on how they should pursue with those could be made – invest further, invest
surplus or harvest now.
The two major differences between the two tools are that the nine­box matrix uses business
strength and market attractiveness as its axis. In comparison, the BCG matrix utilizes the mar­
ket growth rate and market share as its indicators. The business strength ­ also referred to as
competitive strength ­ and market attractiveness are determined by using specific parameters
for each, evaluating those, assigning weights to all criteria, and multiplying the criteria’s values
with the respective weight. Last all numbers for each axis are summed up and result in the final
value for the business strength or market attractiveness.
Afterwards, all data sets can be mapped on a 3x3 grid and their potential future movement in
the matrix. According to where they are located and where they might go different business
strategies can be generated as depicted in figure 3.3 stating where to invest or not. A diagonal
from the bottom left to the top right can be drawn.
• Everything above the diagonal should receive further investment.
• Everything on the diagonal should be held and considered for investing available surplus.
• Everything below the diagonal should not receive any further investment and be harvested
immediately.

The nine boxes and their respective strategical recommendations can be broken down even
further as seen in figure 3.4.
Figure 3.4: GE/McKinsey Portfolio Strategy Detailed [10]
• Protect Position: It should be invested to protect and grow the market position even fur­
ther.
• Invest to Build: It should be invested to bolster the market position by exploiting the
strengths and minimizing vulnerabilities.
• Build Selectively: It should be built upon strengths or drawn back from the market if ex­
ternal threats become too big.
• Manage for Earnings: It can be invested if the earnings are good and there is a low risk.

• Divest: It should be sold off as soon as possible.
All those changes and additions from the base growth­share matrix allow the GE/McKinsey ma­
trix to be a powerful and highly capable tool for portfolio management. If the discussed criteria
are adjusted it can also be used for market navigation and combines well with the previously
mentioned Where to Play model because it already shortens the list of potential business ideas
and only the Gold Mines have to be analysed and further evaluated according to the GE/McK­
insey model. Therefore, it was decided to use this framework for narrowing down the market
opportunities even further. Besides, similar to the initial challenge of market navigation there
are no established tools and, hence, purpose­build tools from other business aspects have to

Innovation
To further understand the proposed business opportunities their innovation potential has to be
investigated. One highly established tool for this is the Henderson­Clark model. It helps differ
between four kinds of innovation: architectural, radical incremental and modular innovation.
All those have different linkages between core concepts and components or core concepts as
seen in (fig. 3.6).
Figure 3.6: Henderson Clark Innovation model template [12]
The most common form of innovation is incremental innovation. Existing technology within a
company and market is used and improved so that it provides increased value to its customers.
Architectural innovation is when existing knowledge and technologies are applied to new mar­
kets resulting in new customers by providing them with a mature technology that was previously
not used in this market. If the product architecture stays the same and new components are
added to provide additional value to existing markets it is called modular innovation. Radi­
cal innovation is the most drastic kind of innovation. Those products either take over existing
business models as they are much superior or even destroy those. [12]
Not implemented in the original Henderson­Clark model from 1990 is disruptive innovation.

3.6
Ecosystem innovation
Staying competitive while also increasing their market share is the goal of any company op­
erating in the global market. As mentioned earlier, many small and medium­sized companies
struggle to sustain their innovation efforts due to limited resources. The COVID­19 pandemic
further pressures business into reallocating resources to sustain and even only survive in those
challenging times. According to Chung, Dietz, Rab and Townsend, this presents great oppor­
tunities for companies to flourish and even thrive by creating a new digital ecosystem their
customers can tap in. There, synergy effects of various products are used to create new on­
line services that create improved and new value. Even though the paper is primarily focusing
on digital ecosystems, it can be deducted that similar effects of better resource allocation and
ecosystem innovation also apply to producing companies in B2B and B2C markets. [14]
Another McKinsey article discusses the question of how companies can capture the value of
ecosystem strategies. They detected three archetypes. First, companies can expand their
existing core business via partnerships or create completely new ecosystems. Second, com­
panies can grow their networks and portfolios by introducing new products. Last, companies
can create new end­to­end solutions. As House in the Sky is a brand new entity they can not
tap into a preexisting ecosystem and will, therefore, create and shape a new one according to
theirs, suppliers’ and customers’ needs. [15]

The provided solution by House in the Sky for wildfire detection is all about providing a new,
convenient and more effective wildfire monitoring service. The balloon is able to fly at high
altitudes of about 50­200m and is anchored on the ground via an ultra­light and robust tether.
Due to its optimized shape, it can withstand high wind speeds and still collect valuable data and
detect fires. Further, it can even be flown in rain, snow, hail and other extreme weather condi­
tions. It is also usable in cold and hot climates. By using high­tech fire detection technologies it
is possible to cover large areas and detect fires up to 25 kilometres away. The balloon utilizes

longer. The solution also comes with software that monitors and sounds an alarm in case a fire
is detected. To provide a holistic service it is also important to provide repairs and maintenance
for the balloons. To detect wildfires three advanced sensor technologies are used to identify
fires at long range in different environments and reduce the false­positive detection rate. The
heat emitted by a fire is caught by thermal infrared region (TIR) imaging. This sensor technol­
ogy has the advantage of being able to detect heat even through heavy layers of smoke. Also,
near (NIR) and short­range (SWIR) infrared sensors improve the detection rate as they can de­
tect light and flicker. Further, due to their low light sensitivity allows night usage of the wildfire
detection solution. Last, visual detection for smokes and other wildfire signs is implemented.

5.5
Market opportunity evaluation based on expert data
The Business Model Canvas and the Where To Play model are good to evaluate business
models but do not facilitate decision. Further, the GE/McKinsey framework is not designed
for the purpose of market navigation but rather portfolio management. An adjusted version is
capable of providing an initial comparison but to further validate the results a new model is to
be developed based on expert data and insight.
5.5.1
Expert interviews
Evaluating market opportunities for companies and especially startups is not a new problem.
Even hundreds of years ago individuals, groups and communities had to decide where to invest
their coin. Despite the old age of the issue there is none fully established, universally accepted
method of narrowing down business ideas when presented with multiple alternatives. In con­
trast, the Business Model Canvas developed by Osterwalder is wildly regarded as the standard
for mapping a business. [11]
The Where To Play model is a modern approach to the proposed issue. It is promising but not
without flaws as discussed in 6.4. Further, the GE/McKinsey Portfolio Matrix is not designed
to compare market opportunities that have not been entered yet. It was designed as a more
detailed version of the generally well known BCG Matrix by the Boston Consulting group. [45]
Hence, it has to be adjusted to fit the need of evaluating unexplored business ventures.
To get and provide a better understanding of which factors are significant for making a busi­
ness decision under uncertainty, three business and entrepreneurship experts have been in­
terviewed. This data can facilitate the development of a new tool to narrow down market alter­
natives and decide which one to pursue. But it can also be used to adapt an already developed
and established model for another purpose to fit the needs and criteria of this decision­making
process.

One of the interviewees was Eric Rozario. A businessman with a lifetime of experiences and

When confronted with the challenge of finding the best market opportunity out of a lineup of
potential business ideas, Eric values numerous factors. One of the first aspects listed by him
was the topic of investment. How much capital is required to bring the project to life? Where
is the initial injection of funds coming from? Who are the stakeholders? Those questions are
crucial to inspect as without money nothing would get developed. Talking about the opportu­
nity attractiveness, it was stated that a high and a low financial investment requirement does
not indicate if a business opportunity is more compelling than alternatives. To make a better
statement multiple parameters have to be looked at amongst others the market potential. In­
vestment is not only about the financial situation as the investment of time and other resources
are often forgotten but equally relevant. He concluded, that an easier funding challenge (e.g.
accessible investors) makes a prospective idea more intriguing.
Secondly, the product development, production development and research were mentioned.
Making a business idea reality takes a lot of time. Only considering the product is a difficult
enough challenge by itself. The product has to fit customer needs and be better than current
alternatives. The production has to be set up so the predicted market demand can be supplied.
How long does the MVP development time take and how will the stakeholders be involved to
ensure a good market fit? Are the production and its strategy simple or complex? Where does
the product­related knowledge come from and what technologies are used? Those questions
are critical to answer and similar to the funding challenges it cannot be said that shorter re­
search, development and production development times make a better business case than a
longer time frame and vice versa.
Complementary to the two previously listed aspects the pricing strategy was mentioned. This,
the investment question and the product development are according to the interviewee closely
linked. The better the match between those three, the more enticing the business idea gets. If
a product has the wrong pricing strategy it will become increasingly complicated to find paying
customers for the product. This does not only apply to startup ideas but any good or service
that exists. High compatibility between the pricing strategy, the product and its market results
in a good business case.
Next, the ease of production including quality control was brought up by Mr Rozario. The mar­
ket demand has to be satisfied. But further, the quality of the product has to at least match if
not exceed the customers’ expectations. Depending on the sold good this can be rather chal­
lenging or even easily accomplished. Alike the production development the production, once

established, will bring challenges that have to be resolved to keep the company running. Espe­
cially early­stage businesses do not have the financial resources to make too many mistakes
early on. The smoother and simpler the production and quality control challenges the more
interesting the opportunity.
An important product­related factor is the safety risk. The product has to be safe to use for
the person that operates or uses it. What about bystanders, children, disabled people? Every
product has to be evaluated according to the hazards it poses. Many ambitious ideas come
with immense safety challenges that could become barriers. Mitigating those should be a top
priority to get a product to market.
Already considering the market and customers more, the product has to be adaptable. Ac­
cording to Eric, young companies have to be ambitious and create something that can be used
for various use cases close to the original intended purpose. The better the machine, device,
object or service fits this criterion the bigger the possible target audience. Further, it was noted
that many users do not fully use their products exactly according to the developers.
Besides the product­related factors, the market poses plenty of decisive aspects. First, the
market size was mentioned by Eric. Knowing and understanding the customer helps determine
the dimensions. Entering a niche market can be an attractive opportunity. But the risk of non­
adoption by the intended customer poses the risk that there might be a high chance the no
other market requires this product or service. Tremendous market size and potential could
prevent this case and help create a steady and positive revenue stream. The market has to
be thoroughly evaluated according to available data to get a good understanding of its volume.
This also helps determine what the market lacks to create business.
Additionally, the sales volume has to be considered. Only knowing the potential market size
and potential can create a misleading image. Examining the amount that can be sold and also
in what time frame hones that picture. For new ventures, the achieved market shares in the
first years is on average ranging between minuscule and low compared to the established and
mature competition. This should not be discouraging for upcoming businesses. Success and
settling into a market takes endurance and stamina in most cases. But being aware of and
realistic about those numbers helps evaluate ideas early on.
As just introduced the competitors have to be looked at. Most markets are already supplied by
one or multiple other business entities. But the chance of finding a blue ocean is real, exciting
and attractive. In red oceans, all market participants already have their market share and want
to grow, too. [47] But, startups should not necessarily shy away from competition as they bring
new innovative ideas to a market with mature and aged solutions. They can benefit and learn
from their potential rivals. Further, rivals could also be considered as a potential exit strategy
for the business. Understanding the business’ surroundings is crucial.
Understanding market development helps evaluate the attractiveness of this market. Eric men­
tions that roughly markets can be categorized in three different states. It can be shrinking,
static or growing. Being aware of the markets future potential is a great way to get a better
understanding of the business proposal. However, a market’s development can have various

Entering a market comes with many challenges. There are also entry barriers for companies
that are trying to venture into those markets. They can range from high development and set up
costs, brand recognition to switching costs for customers that want to change supplier. Further,
access to technology, licenses and patents can pose a barrier.
Similarly to the entry barrier, Eric Rozario mentioned legal limitations. In modern times, law­
makers do not leave much room for legal interpretations as most aspects of businesses, tech­
nologies and customer interactions have been clearly defined in the past. Some have been
updated but there are also heaps of outdated laws. When evaluating business opportunities
the current legal limitations, as well as upcoming regulations and changes, have to be strongly
considered.
Afterwards, the interviewee was asked to state which of those factors are more relevant when
evaluating prospective business ideas. Eric stated that all aspects are of utter importance as
most build upon each other. All listed points are the foundation for a business and startup.

the ease of production, market development and legal limitations were named as only slightly

MVP the quality control aspects are less essential as in those stages even unpolished products
can be used to gather data and attract customers. A static or shrinking market development
can still offer immense customer and development potential. When evaluating the reasons for
the development trajectory, Eric explains that it can often be due to outdated solutions that do
not fit the changed market needs anymore. The innovation and creativity that comes with a
young, dynamic startup can be exactly what the market needed and therefore offer gigantic
potential. The legal limitations were also ranked of marginally less critical importance as legal
aspects can change all the time to even facilitate new business and growth.

6
Discussion
The presented and analysed data in chapter 5 offers a good overview and discusses many
relevant points. Though, due to multiple factors e.g. time limitations and limited available
resources the thesis, its data and results can be improved upon to provide additional in­depth
value. Therefore, relevant points will be brought up in this chapter.
First, it has to be stated that due to the ongoing COVID­19 pandemic multiple processes related
to this thesis were delayed by multiple weeks or even months which further tightened the limited
available time. This resulted in postponing the Where To Play workshop at the beginning, which
was and is critical as a data basis for this thesis.
6.1
External environment analysis
The assessment of the external environment related to the three Gold Mines did not provide an
extensive overview. It can be understood that if additional tools would have been used there
aspects related to supply chain, industry life cycles and more could have been investigated.
This would have drastically strengthened the design and presentation of those three business
opportunities. Further, analysing the current and future competitors more in­depth could provide
a much better understanding of the corresponding business and the market. Besides, this helps
to get to know the customers much better before reaching out and interacting with them.
Two tools that could have been used and should be used for further research are Porter’s five
forces and the PESTEL analysis. Both are highly established models. Using both of those tools
further has the benefit of shedding light on the micro (Porter) and macro (PESTEL) environment
of the competitors and House in the Sky itself.
6.2
Competitors
Next, the number of competitors analysed for each of the three Gold Mines is rather limited,
ranging from three for the cellular network repeater business case up to seven for the wildfire
detection market opportunity. Even when further analysing them it can be seen that for some
of the competitors their core products are designed for completely different customer segments
and their product portfolio offers only a ”side”­product that fits the competitor comparisons.
Further, the competitors listed are predominately market­dominating on a global scale. Local
companies that supply the danish market might have been overlooked which would compete on
a much stricter geographical scale. Therefore, the competitors analysed for all business models
need to be expanded to provide a better overview of the market, as the current comparison
only offers a short and rough glimpse into the market that could mislead the decision­making
process.
6.3
Business model validation
Besides, all the discussed and designed business presented in this thesis including the ecosys­
tem presented for the outdoor camera station has to be validated. This does not only mean
88
Market opportunity navigation and innovation potential for a start­up
==PAGE:97==
value proposition validation which was already mentioned as a shared key activity for the Gold
Mines but, further, getting feedback on the proposed business models by stakeholders and
shareholders.
A good way this could be done even in the near future is having those business models dis­
cussed with and validated by startup coaches, experts and more similar to the people that were
addressed for the expert interviews. In addition, and providing crucial insights are the market
players that can be interviewed to further validate and optimize the designed business model
canvases.
6.4
Where To Play
The Where To Play model is very powerful and provides a strong overview for multiple business
opportunities and compares them based on simple parameters.
6.4.1
Parameter interpretation
During the conducted workshop, multiple participants stated that the interpretation of what ex­
actly certain parameters mean was not clearly defined in the Worksheet and literature provided
by the frameworks developers. Various business opportunities were defined in step one much
broader than others which resulted in discussions in step two about e.g. the corresponding
market volume. There, cases occurred where parts of the group saw only a very small market
volume while other members interpreted this parameter rather different resulting in a higher
market volume size. Therefore, for future Where To Play workshops it is highly recommended
to specify the business ideas in step one in greater detail to reduce the confusion in further
steps.
Another point brought up by the workshop attendees was, that the Challenge­axis of the Attrac­
tiveness Map, should be inverted. Some of the participants were confused that for the Potential
side Super High indicated an overwhelming positive evaluation while on the Challenge side Su­
per High indicated a dominantly strong negative score.
6.4.2
Workshop setup
The last relevant point about the Where To Play model and workshop that has to be discussed
is the setup of the workshop. It is important to bring multiple stakeholders into the discussion
so a broader spectrum of opinions is represented. Here, also external individuals e.g. en­
trepreneurship experts, technology providers or even market players and potential customers
for this technology or product direction could be invited. This would result in a longer workshop
with more discussion but also in a more thorough initial evaluation of all business cases. To
keep the workshop time­efficient the person who conducts the workshop should not be par­
ticipating and only be responsible for guiding the attendees and making sure they follow the
structure and answer all their framework related questions.
With smaller sizes like the workshop conducted by the author for House in the Sky it should be
tried and focused on finding an optimal workshop date where all three steps can be finished on
the same day. Splitting up the sessions reduces productivity as individuals forgot what a step
was about, or what was decided on in the previous meetings.

6.5
Selected criteria for the adjusted GE/McKinsey framework
The nine­box model as used with this selection of criteria presents multiple challenges and
issues. First, the nine aspects chosen to build the score for the two­axis might no be suited
best for assessing the situation as presented in the problem statement. They are supposed
to be aligned and selected for an early­stage startup that is presented with multiple market
opportunities and wants to assess those without spending too much time on them initially. The
original set of aspects as listed in section 5.4 do not optimally meet these criteria. Fifteen criteria
for the market attractiveness were listed but only 6 were deemed relevant to the presented
challenge. Even more difficult is the situation for the business strength criteria. Out of six­teen
elements only two were chosen. The product adaptability was a criterion chosen by the author
on his own to make the x­axis consist of at least three criteria compared to the six for the y­axis.
The other critic points do not only apply to the GE/McKinsey framework but also to the Business
opportunity rating model developed by Thomas presented in section 5.5 based on the expert
interviews’ data.
6.6
Data comparability
Besides, not all data is available for the same point in time. Comparing a market size or growth
rate for different years could be highly misleading and this impact has to be handled with caution.
Another critical point related to the data comparability is the selected data itself. Some markets
can be more specified and hence provide a smaller market size due to the better­detailed market
segmentation. It can be the case that a broad idea only addresses a niche market even though
because of its broad description the assessment shows a vast market size.
6.7
Criteria assessment
Multiple criteria ­ entry barriers, ability to differentiate, level of competition, knowledge require­
ments, value proposition uniqueness, available resources and product adaptability ­ are difficult
to assess as the scoring is not based on factual data but rather on the research and subjective
opinion of the person who assesses them. This makes the comparison rather hard. Only the
market size and market growth rate were quantifiable due to their chosen assessment elements.
6.8
Aspect scoring
Further, even if all assessment criteria could be quantified scoring those elements is challeng­
ing. Before rating the different market opportunities it the ranges for the scoring of one to five
have to be defined. Questions like ”what exactly makes this criterion be scored as a one?” have

6.9
Weights of criteria
Next, the weights of the criteria have to be based on quantitative and qualitative data. Further,
scientific validation of those weights would provide additional value.
6.10
Biases
Addressing all those aspects would also help remove or at least mitigate the assessing indi­
viduals’ biases. As the results are presented right now, they only hold limited merit. If only the
grading of weighting for one of the elements would be changed the final ranking of the business
opportunities could be drastically different. This could lead to a recommendation to pursue the
cellular network repeater business model even though the just conducted model indicates that
this is the least attractive case.
6.11
Motivation
The motivation scoring has to be improved for future comparisons. Rating a solution with the
highest average motivation best could be highly deceiving. Two out of five people might have
given the lowest possible score which indicates that they are not interested in pursuing and
working on this business opportunity in the future. Choosing this market idea might have a
detrimental impact. Therefore, it could be better to use the motivation’s standard deviation. As
a high discrepancy between team members can also indicate further issues that might arise in
the future related to the specific market opportunity or group dynamics.
Also, two of the participants stated that their motivation and passion lies rather with the market
opportunities located in the Moon Shot and Questionable quadrants a not with the three Gold
Mine solutions. This has to be discussed with all team members to make sure everyone is on
the same page to ensure future development of the product but also the team/startup itself.

Last, the technical difficulties between the market opportunities in the ecosystem evaluation
are not stated and could drastically shift the focus and ecosystem direction. This could change
the centre of the map from the outdoor camera station to something else, resulting in a different
proposed ecosystem starting point.

Conclusion
The conducted research aimed to identify if a student­run start­up project utilizing helium as a
platform based propellant offer market and innovation potential. Based on a quantitative and
qualitative analysis of business models utilizing balloons as the main carrier structure for inno­
vative products, it can be concluded that an outdoor camera station ­ a multi­axis controllable
camera that is attached to a tethered balloon or aerostat is the most promising market opportu­
nity and even functions as the centre for ecosystem innovation. The results indicate that House
in the Sky should focus on this business case.
Based on promising literature the thesis uses multiple models to answer its research question
and further bridge the gap for the missing tools designated to market navigation.
The Where To Play model is highly capable and well suited for this thesis’ core research ques­
tion as it provides a simplistic tool that helps to narrow down market opportunities based on
easily accessible criteria. Besides, it helped link business opportunities that are closely linked.
On the other hand, the results required additional tools to help determine which market ideas
are more promising and should therefore be pursued first.
To further understand the connection of the proposed technical solutions they were mapped to
investigate if any of the Gold Mines facilitates ecosystem innovation and analyse the business
model’s innovation potential.
Facilitating the design of business models, the established Business Model Canvas was used
to provide a more detailed overview of the most highly rated market opportunities according to
the Where To Play framework.
The GE/McKinsey framework has further proven that decision models designed for different
applications ­ portfolio management ­ can be easily adjusted to reflect the needs of market
navigation for early­stage star­ups. Though, it was also determined that to get better and more
objective results the parameters and their evaluation criteria have to be further adjusted than
expected.
Based on three entrepreneurship expert interviews a novel and promising model ­ Business
Opportunity Rating tool ­ was developed to help early­stage start­ups with their market naviga­
tion. It is designed to be used in addition to the Where To Play model. The most Gold Mines
determined by it can now be rated and directly compared based on the BOR.
It has to be further developed by interviewing more adepts from various fields and field­tested
with numerous start­ups to validate the provided value. This process will take many iterations.
Though, even this early version of the tool can profoundly help current and future start­ups that
need a model for simplistic market navigation as it closes a critical research gap.
To sum up, this thesis investigated the market opportunities of House in the Sky, showed that
in its current state it does not create radical but rather provides disruptive innovation to the

tool for market navigation based on expert data.

Many startups face significant market navigation challenges. Most tools do not facilitate the creation
of a choice set of business opportunities and their rating. This study aims to determine the most
relevant factors for market navigation as well as evaluation and present a new model that can be
used as a supplemental step to established tools.
To investigate the difficulties of market navigation the case of the startup House in the Sky is analysed
to determine its market, ecosystem and innovation potential. Therefore, their business opportunity
choice set was created and narrowed down to its Gold Mines. For those, holistic business cases
were created and evaluated according to their ecosystem and innovation potential. Further, the Gold
Mines were rated and ranked based on an adjusted portfolio management tool. Based on expert
interviews and to compare the results a business opportunity rating framework was developed and
tested.
The final results suggest that House in the Sky should enter the outdoor camera market targeting
outdoor events as it presents the most attractive market, architectural as well as disruptive innovation
and can be the centre of vast ecosystem innovation.
