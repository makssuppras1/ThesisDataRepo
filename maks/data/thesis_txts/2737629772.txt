ABSTRACT 
Quantum key distribution (QKD) establishes cryptographic keys between distant parties 
using the fundamental laws of physics. Paired with one-time pad encryption, it provides 
secures data communications against both current and emerging technologies. 
Continuous Variable (CV) QKD, which encodes quantum information in the continuous 
degrees of freedom of light—such as amplitude and phase quadratures—stands out for its 
compatibility with standard high-speed telecom components operable at room 
temperature. However, CVQKD’s performance, particularly its key rate and reach, is 
heavily affected by what is known as excess noise, predominantly stemming from 
residual phase noise (RPN). This is especially true in the most commonly deployed 
systems using the local local oscillator (LLO) scheme.  
This thesis introduces a novel use of machine learning to mitigate RPN within CV-QKD 
systems based on LLO, thereby boosting their overall performance. By optimizing the 
position of displaced coherent states in phase space—a method known in classic 
telecommunications as Geometric Constellation Shaping—we enhance the system's noise 
resistance. This approach is implemented via an end-to-end learning model that simulates 
the CV-QKD system with a linear channel affected by RPN and additive Gaussian noise. 
Extensive numerical simulations confirm that our optimized constellation reduces excess 
noise up to 6% compared to traditional square-shaped constellations. Consequently, our 
technique significantly extends the operational distance of CV-QKD systems, facilitating 
the development of a quantum access network that supports a larger number of users.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 
BACKGROUND 
In an era where technology permeates every facet of society, enhancing both life quality 
and process efficiency, it simultaneously introduces new vulnerabilities and cybersecurity 
threats. Cyberattacks are escalating in frequency and complexity, spurred by political and 
economic tensions. These attacks not only compromise information technology (IT) 
systems but also operational technology (OT) across critical sectors such as healthcare, 
energy, and government. Fig.1 shows the global data breach statistics over the world [1]. 
For example, significant cyber incidents have occurred, such as the widespread disruption 
caused by wiper attacks during the conflict in Ukraine in 2022 and the AcidRain attack 
affecting Viasat communications and European energy infrastructures [2]. 
Cyberattacks like ransomware, malware, and phishing are not just IT threats but cause 
real-world harm—disrupting medical devices, electricity supply, and industrial 
operations. The sophistication of these attacks enables extortion and significant data 
breaches, affecting millions globally and costing billions annually. Additionally, 
emerging technologies like quantum computing present new challenges; these powerful 
systems could potentially decrypt current encryption methods, posing a heightened risk 
to critical infrastructures.  
In response, Europe has initiated projects like the EuroQCI (Quantum Communication 
Infrastructure) to bolster cybersecurity through quantum-enhanced encryption, protecting 
sensitive data across various sectors [3]. Research advancements, particularly in 
continuous variable quantum key distribution (CV-QKD) supported by EU funding, have 
transitioned from lab-based setups to real-world trials [4], demonstrating significant 
potential in enhancing cybersecurity measures.

robustness against residual phase noise (RPN), particularly in systems using LLO.

explore machine learning strategies for optimizing the geometric constellation to mitigate

2. STATE OF THE ART 
2.1 
CLASSICAL CRYPTOGRAPHY 
Asymmetric and symmetric cryptography represent two fundamental encryption 
methodologies used today. Symmetric cryptography, which uses a single pre-shared key 
for both encryption and decryption, is favoured for its speed and minimal computational 
requirements. However, the necessity for key distribution at each endpoint introduces 
complexities, typically managed through key management systems. 
Conversely, asymmetric cryptography employs a pair of keys—a public key and a private 
key—for encrypting and decrypting messages. Each user has their own key pair; the 
public key is openly shared to encrypt messages, while the private key remains 
confidential to decrypt messages. This method, though computationally heavier, 
facilitates secure initial exchanges of symmetric keys, enabling subsequent symmetric 
encryption which underpins secure Internet communications [5]. 
The Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) system utilizes these principles to ensure secure, 
authenticated communication over the Internet. However, the security of such systems 
depends on the computational difficulty of certain mathematical problems, like those at 
the core of the RSA crypto-system, which relies on the challenge of factorings large 
numbers into primes [6]. 
With the advent of quantum computers, which can solve such problems in seconds [7], 
traditional encryption methods are at risk. The quantum advantage—a milestone achieved 
in 2019 where quantum computers began solving tasks impractical for classical 
computers—announces significant implications for cryptography. In response, initiatives 
like NIST's investment in post-quantum cryptography, aim to develop quantum-resistant 
algorithms [8]. This includes replacing vulnerable public-key algorithms well in advance 
of quantum computers being able to execute algorithms like Shor’s, which could factor 
large numbers swiftly using quantum mechanics. The urgency and priority of these 
replacements are critical considerations as the quantum era approaches [9]. 
2.2 
QUANTUM KEY DISTRIBUTION 
While current classical encryption methods are secure against today’s conventional 
computers, they are likely to falter with the advent of quantum computing [7]. In contrast, 
Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) is a quantum cryptographic protocol that provides 
theoretically secure key exchanges between two parties, employing quantum mechanics 
principles for robust security. QKD systems involve a sender (Alice) and a receiver (Bob) 
connected by two channels: a quantum channel, which is susceptible to eavesdropping by 
an adversary (Eve), and a securely authenticated public channel. 
The security of QKD is underpinned by several quantum principles [10]. The no-cloning 
theorem ensures that it is impossible to exactly copy an arbitrary quantum state, meaning

any attempt to intercept and replicate the quantum key would disturb the state of the 
photons, triggering an alert. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle complements this, 
stating that measuring one property of a particle, like a photon’s position, will 
unavoidably disturb another, such as its momentum, thereby preserving the privacy of the 
communication. 
Entanglement, another cornerstone of QKD, plays a critical role in QKD protocols [11]. 
It involves linking pairs of qubits such that the state of one qubit immediately influences 
its partner, regardless of the distance separating them. This property is exploited in QKD 
by keeping one entangled qubit at the sender's end while the other is sent to the receiver. 
At the reception, the entangled qubits are compared to confirm the integrity and security 
of the exchanged key. This method not only ensures the security of the key exchange but 
also the confidentiality of any subsequent communication encrypted with the key. 
Based on encoding and decoding techniques of the quantum information, QKD system 
are categorized into two main types: discrete variable (DV) and continuous variable (CV), 
each with distinct mechanisms and applications. The upcoming subsection will delve 
deeper into these QKD types, exploring their specific methodologies and benefits. 
2.2.1 DV-QKD 
In a discrete variable quantum key distribution system, Alice encodes the quantum 
information in discrete degree of freedom of the quantum system, such as phase or 
polarization of the single photon. This method differs from traditional digital 
communication, which relies on large pulses of photons or voltage changes to encode 
information. In DV-QKD, the information is uniquely encoded on individual photons [9].  
Figure 2 shows the traditional implementation of the BB84 protocol [12], a well-known 
example of DV-QKD. It involves Alice using four non-orthogonal quantum states, such 
as different polarizations of a photon, which are randomly selected from two bases: {H, 
V} (horizontal and vertical) and {D, A} (diagonal and anti-diagonal). Alice sends a 
sufficiently long sequence of these random states—more than twice the number of bits 
needed for the final key—over a quantum channel. 
Bob detects the incoming photons using one of the two bases chosen at random. Through 
a public channel, Alice and Bob compare the bases they used without revealing the actual 
results. They identify the instances where their chosen bases coincide, a process known 
as sifting, and estimate the quantum bit error rate (QBER) by comparing the detected 
values with the values Alice originally sent. 
Due to the no-cloning theorem, any eavesdropping attempt by Eve on the quantum 
channel will introduce errors, thereby increasing the QBER.

While DV-QKD offers many advantages, its implementation requires specialized 
components, such as single-photon detectors. Therefore, its integration into the exciting 
classical telecom network is quite challenging. Alternatively, CV-QKD is appealing 
because it can utilize standard classical telecommunication technologies.  
2.2.2 CV-QKD  
Continuous variables are quantum mechanical observables with a continuous spectrum. 
Examples include the amplitude and phase quadrature of the electromagnetic field of 
light. In CV-QKD, quantum information is encoded into these CV and decoded through 
standard technologies, such as quadrature modulation and coherent detection. This 
compatibility with standard component found in classical coherent system   facilitates the 
practical implementation of CV-QKD, leveraging its advantages for wider application. In 
general, the prepare-and-measure version of CV-QKD protocol involves several key steps 
[14]: 
1. State Preparation: Alice generates random data sequences to be encoded in 
modulated quantum states. These values typically have Gaussian distributions for 
Gaussian protocols or discrete modulation profiles for other protocols. She 
prepares a coherent signal state and applies displacement to the quadratures, 
verifying the modulation by monitoring the signal. 
2. State Distribution: The modulated quantum states are sent through a quantum 
channel to Bob. This channel is assumed to be under the control of an 
eavesdropper, Eve. 
3. State Measurement: Bob detects the incoming signal using homodyne or 
heterodyne detection, obtaining data sequences. For homodyne detection, Bob 
alternates between measuring different quadratures, necessitating a key sifting 
procedure to match Alice's data. 
4. Error Correction: Using a predefined protocol, Alice or Bob sends error 
correction information over an authenticated classical channel. The other party 
corrects their errors to ideally obtain an identical data sequence.

state to determine the upper bound on Eve's information leakage. 
6. Privacy Amplification: They apply a random universal hash function to their 
corrected data to minimize potential information leakage to Eve, resulting in 
identical secret keys.

CV-QKD TRANSMITTER 
CV-QKD transmitter comprises of three main components: the laser source, the 
modulation module, and the variable optical attenuator (VOA) [16]. 
SOURCE 
Firstly, the transmitter is characterized by a laser source capable of delivering phase and 
amplitude-coherent states, essential for system performance and stability. This laser can 
operate in either pulsed or continuous-wave modes [16]. High performance and stability 
are achieved through narrow-linewidth lasers with low relative intensity noise. For pulsed 
operations, a laser diode is typically paired with amplitude modulators, whereas 
continuous-wave operations generally utilize only a narrow-linewidth laser diode due to 
its simplicity and effectiveness.  
QUADRATURE MODULATION 
The light output from the laser source is modulated to encode quantum information and 
generate classical signals through two primary methods of quadrature modulation. One 
method combines a phase modulator with an amplitude modulator, while the other uses 
an in-phase quadrature (IQ) modulator, as shown in Fig.4. Both techniques involve 
displacing coherent states along the two quadratures of the phase space.

MODULATION FORMATS 
CV-QKD can be categorized into two main classes based on the modulation format: 
Gaussian-modulated and discrete-modulated CV-QKD protocols. 
In Gaussian-modulated CV-QKD protocols, the amplitudes of coherent states are 
generated by drawing two numbers from a sequence with a Gaussian distribution. 
Although this approach benefits from the most advanced security proofs, its practical 
implementation is challenging. This challenge arises from the need for a digital-to-analog 
converter (DAC) with high bit resolution to accurately approximate the continuous 
Gaussian distribution [17]. 
Alternatively, discrete-modulated CV-QKD protocols employ standard digital 
modulation formats, such as Quadrature Phase Shift Keying (QPSK) or higher-order 
Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM). These formats encode information in the 
amplitude of two carrier waves, which are out of phase by 90 degrees and known as the 
in-phase (I) and quadrature (Q) components. These amplitude and phase variations create 
different data points or symbols in the phase space, depicted on constellation diagrams. 
While the security proofs for these protocols are not as comprehensive as those for 
Gaussian modulation, they offer benefits in terms of higher transmission rates and easier 
implementation. This thesis will demonstrate how higher-order QAM can robustly handle 
high PN. Therefore, it improves the overall performance of CV-QKD system.   
VARIABLE OPTICAL ATTENUATOR 
The security of a CV-QKD system depends on the indistinguishability of displaced 
coherent states. These states are non-orthogonal to each other, which means that any 
interference by an eavesdropper (Eve) becomes noticeable through induced errors. 
To overlap the coherent states, we use a variable optical attenuator (VOA) to adjust the 
amplitude of the state. The variance of the state ensemble at the VOA's output, termed 
modulation variance (Vmod), is characterized in a manner just to the signal-to-noise ratio 
(SNR) used in telecommunications systems [18].

CV-QKD RECEIVER 
COHERENT DETECTION 
To decode quantum information at the CV-QKD receiver, coherent detection is 
employed, which involves mixing the quantum signal with a strong beam known as the 
local oscillator (LO), characterized by a predefined frequency and phase. The LO can be 
generated independently at the receiver using its own laser, known as the LLO scheme, 
or it can be generated at the transmitter and sent along with the quantum signal, referred 
to as the Transmitter Local Oscillator (TLO) scheme [19]. The LLO scheme, offering 
enhanced security, is more commonly adopted in CV-QKD systems. 
Coherent detection is divided into two methods: homodyne and heterodyne detection. 
Homodyne detection requires the quantum signal to be combined with the LO using a 
50:50 coupler, followed by a balanced detector. This setup measures only one quadrature 
at a time, depending on the phase of the LO, which is adjusted to be either 0 or π/2 relative 
to the quantum signal. By randomly switching the phase difference between the LO and 
the quantum signal between these two values, both quadratures can be statistically 
analyzed [17]. 
Heterodyne detection, on the other hand, allows for the simultaneous measurement of 
both quadratures of the quantum signal. This method utilizes a homodyne phase-diverse 
receiver, where the quantum signal and the LO are split, and each path is detected by a 
balanced detector. Introducing a π/2 phase shift to one path of the LO enables the 
simultaneous detection of both the I and Q quadratures. Additionally, RF heterodyne 
detection can be implemented, where the quantum signal and the LO have a frequency 
offset exceeding the quantum signal's bandwidth, facilitating efficient detection.   
MEASUREMENTS IN CV-QKD 
Measurements in CV-QKD can be categorized into two types: calibration measurements 
and quantum signal measurements. Calibration measurements include vacuum noise, 
electronic noise, and back-to-back (B2B) measurements. 
1. Electronic Noise Measurement: As shown in Figure 5(a), this involves opening 
both the signal and the LO ports to measure the noise contributed by electronic 
components alone. 
2. Vacuum Noise Measurement: For this measurement, the signal port is opened 
while the LO port is closed, allowing for the assessment of the vacuum noise, 
represented in Figure 5(b). 
3. B2B Measurement: Typically used to characterize the modulation variance of the 
generated thermal state, this setup connects the transmitter and receiver directly 
via a patch cord fiber. 
These measurement methods are crucial for calibrating the system and accurately 
interpreting the quantum signal.

DIGITAL SIGNAL PROCESSING 
The primary goal of employing Digital Signal Processing (DSP) in CV-QKD systems is 
to simplify the hardware requirements (making the system hardware agnostic) and 
enhance performance by mitigating various types of noise. 
On the transmitter side, a typical DSP chain includes a mapping function, an upsampling 
block, pulse shaping with a Root-Raised-Cosine (RRC) filter, and the multiplexing of 
auxiliary signals [16]. These auxiliary signals facilitate carrier phase recovery, clock 
synchronization, and time synchronization, which are essential for maintaining the 
integrity and timing of the transmitted signals [21]. 
At the receiver, the DSP chain comprises post-equalization, frequency estimation, carrier 
phase recovery, time synchronization, and finally, matched filtering and downsampling. 
These processes collectively ensure the accurate reconstruction and timing of the received 
signals. 
Following DSP processing, Alice and Bob perform post-processing to extract secret key 
bits from the correlations established during the transmission and reception. Post-
processing involves several critical steps: parameter estimation to assess the integrity of 
the transmission, information reconciliation to correct errors, and privacy amplification 
to enhance the security of the final key.

noisy signal in the frequency domain. This filtering technique effectively reduces the 
phase noise, yielding a more accurate estimate of the transmitted signal, particularly when 
the parameters of the noise signal are well-characterized.

2.3 
DEEP LEARNING 
Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that focuses on algorithms inspired by the 
structure and function of the brain's neural networks. Neural networks (NN) consist of 
interconnected layers of artificial neurons that process and transform input data to 
produce output predictions. The network learns by adjusting the weighs to minimize the 
cost function, related to the difference between the true and predicted results. Neurons 
are basic units that receive input, apply an activation function and pass the result to the 
next layer. The layers consist of input, hidden and output layers. There are several types 
of NN such as Feedforward NN which information flows in one direction, from input to 
output, without loops or cycles; or RNN, which are suitable for sequential data 
processing, with connections that form directed cycles, allowing them to retain memory. 
Deep learning brings several advantages in the communication sector, as it can be 
beneficial for learning noise parameters, end-to-end learning and adaptability. 
2.4 
PROBLEM STATEMENT 
In LLO-CV-QKD, the RPN is one of the main sources of noise in these systems. Poor 
phase compensation has a direct impact on excess noise, which in turn affects the SKR, 
reach distance and the cost of the system. Therefore, addressing RPN is critical to improve 
the performance CV-QKD and reduce the cost by allow the use of laser with low cost.

PROPOSED SOLUTION 
This work leverages the compatibility of CV-QKD with classical telecommunication 
technologies to minimize the variance of RPN. Among various strategies, geometric 
shaping enhanced by machine learning shows promise in significantly improving 
performance. This approach optimizes the shape of symbol constellations to adapt to 
specific noise conditions. 
This thesis employs machine learning to refine this optimization process. Specifically, 
autoencoders are utilized to harmonize the functions of encoders and modulators, while 
RNN are suited for estimating phase noise modeled as a Wiener process. This integration 
of neural networks promises to design better constellations that are more robust to RPN 
compared to the standard one. 
The proposed system will be tested in a controlled setup to validate the effectiveness of 
the approach. The training phase will minimize excess noise, while the testing phase will 
confirm the practical improvements in system performance. This project will specifically 
focus on the impact of RPN on 64QAM constellations, aiming to develop a noise-robust 
constellation shape that enhances overall system performance.

3. METHODOLOGY 
Machine learning offer potential benefits for optimising geometric constellation design, 
with reinforcement learning and deep learning being particularly effective [26]. Among 
the different machine learning approaches, supervised and unsupervised learning stand 
out, especially with the success of neural networks. Specifically, we used autoencoders, 
which combine neural networks with unsupervised learning, making them highly 
effective for tasks such as noise reduction. This approach enables more precise and 
adaptive optimisation of constellation shapes, improving the overall performance of the 
communications system. 
3.1 
GEOMETRIC CONSTELLATION SHAPING  
Geometric constellation shaping (GCS) optimizes high-order modulation formats to boost 
spectral efficiency and maximize mutual information (MI) specific to channel 
characteristics. Ensuring the robustness of GCS under varying channel conditions is 
critical for reliable performance in CV-QKD [27]. 
Geometric shaping focuses on the spatial arrangement of constellation points to enhance 
performance, whereas probabilistic shaping optimizes the probability distribution of 
symbols within the constellation to improve spectral efficiency and error performance, as 
seen in the Fig.8. In our case, the original data already follows a Gaussian distribution, 
probabilistic shaping is unnecessary, and only geometric shaping will be considered [18]. 
All constellations must adhere to proper normalization to align with the required average 
energy. 
 
Figure 8 Types of constellations shaping: a) geometric shaping b) probabilistic shaping  [28] 
An example of classical geometric constellation shaping is the Hexagonal Lattice-Based 
Hybrid Constellation Shaping technique aimed at improving the performance of 64QAM 
[29]. This approach uses a hexagonal lattice structure to shape the constellation, 
enhancing the nonlinear tolerance and overall performance of higher-order modulated 
signals, as shown in Fig.9. The process begins with a quantitative characterization of the 
constellation based on the hexagonal lattice. An objective function that maximizes the 
constellation figure of merits (CFM) determines the optimal distribution of the 
constellation points, resulting in geometric shaping-64QAM. The points are then divided

3.2 
END-TO-END LEARNING 
End-to-end learning of communications systems is a novel concept whose goal is to learn 
full transmitter and receiver implementations which are optimized for a specific 
performance metric and channel model [27]. This approach involves representing the 
transmitter and receiver as a unified deep NN, trainable as an autoencoder [30]. These 
networks can learn optimal solutions for complex channel models and offer faster 
execution and reduced energy consumption. Such intelligent communication system 
simulations are being applied in various areas, including waveform optimization for 
dispersive fiber, and are particularly useful for geometric constellation shaping. 
3.3 
AUTOENCODER 
 
Autoencoders are used in this project to geometrically optimise constellation shapes 
under varying channel conditions through unsupervised learning, focusing on discovering 
hidden patterns in unlabelled data [31]. The correlation of symbols is measured and 
controlled by mutual information due to their linear relationship.  
The autoencoder architecture consists of an encoder and a decoder linked by a channel 
model, represented schematically in Fig.10. The encoder adjusts the constellation point 
positions while the decoder learns the decision boundaries of distorted symbols [25]. The 
encoder transforms the noisy signal into a latent representation, and the decoder 
reconstructs the original input data from this representation, aiming to produce an output 
that closely resembles the input data.

3.4 
RECURRENT NEURAL NETWORK 
Despite learning a compressed representation of the transmitted signal in the presence of 
phase noise and AWGN channels, autoencoders struggle to accurately reconstruct noisy 
input data. Therefore, a RNN is added to handle phase noise more effectively. The RNN's 
ability to manage sequential data and adaptively learn stochastic processes makes it ideal 
for improving system performance by accurately estimating phase noise at each timestep 
based on previous values. 
RNNs are neural networks where the output from the previous step is fed as input to the 
current step, making them useful for sequential data where past information needs to be 
remembered. This characteristic makes them well-suited for estimating phase noise. They 
have hidden layers where hidden states retain past knowledge, as can be understood from 
Fig.12. To address vanishing and exploding gradient issues, a bidirectional RNN was 
developed [36], which takes the real-valued representation of the noisy signal as input 
and uses multiple Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) cells to learn temporal patterns in the 
noise. GRUs improve the RNN’s ability to handle long-term dependencies.

3.4.2 DEEP LEARNING FRAMEWORK 
There are different frameworks for developing deep learning programs, with PyTorch and 
TensorFlow being the most popular. PyTorch uses dynamic computational graphs built 
on-the-fly, while TensorFlow uses static computational graphs. In this case, TensorFlow 
is used. TensorFlow is a Python library designed for numerical calculations in machine 
learning and artificial intelligence. It defines parameters with placeholders, creating 
tensor objects with specific dimensions to be assigned later during the computational 
graph. These tensors are multidimensional matrices with defined range, shape, and type. 
Computational graphs, trained during sessions, consist of nodes (operations like 
activation functions) and edges (specifying tensor objects). Sessions in TensorFlow are 
used to assign values to tensors from feedDicts and for model predictions. Sessions are 
initialized with global initializers, run necessary operations, and are saved. During testing, 
sessions are restored and fed with testing data.

4.1.1 DATA TRANSFORMATION 
For the optimization step, we utilized a dataset of N samples with cardinality M, 
representing the symbols of the constellation.  In this case, we will be using a 64QAM 
simulation, thus the range will be between 0 and 63. A given gaussian-distribution dataset 
with 10 million samples was employed. This dataset required no preprocessing due to its 
specific numerical distribution and absence of outliers. The predefined dataset adopts a 
H5 format which improves data management efficiency.  
We divided the dataset into 75% for training (including 10% for validation) and 25% for 
testing. This split ensures that the model undergoes training, validation, and testing on 
distinct datasets to prevent overfitting. The training set adjusts the neural network weights 
based on the loss function and optimizer, while the validation set tunes the 
hyperparameters. The testing set evaluates the final model’s performance to confirm its 
generalization capabilities on new, unseen data. If necessary, regularization techniques 
like Dropout are implemented during training to enhance performance. 
Given the dataset's volume, we employed batch processing, dividing it into smaller 
batches for training in line with the batch gradient descent approach. For the RNN, time 
steps were used to organize the data sequentially.

4.1.2 ENCODER 
The encoder function takes an input tensor, structured as a one-hot encoded vector, and 
processes it using a specified set of autoencoder parameters in order to map it to a 
normalized complex constellation. It applies a fully connected layer (Dense) to the input 
layer, using a selected activation function and a kernel initializer. If the dropout flag is 
enabled, dropout regularization is applied to prevent overfitting by intermittently setting 
neuron outputs to zero during training.  
Additionally, a normalization factor is calculated to regulate the power of the signal based 
on the encoded constellation tensor. This factor scales the symbols and constellation 
tensors appropriately. Finally, the function converts and returns the encoded symbols and 
constellation tensors from real to complex numbers. The output is two-dimensional 
consisting of the real and the imaginary part of each symbol.

4.1.5 DECODER 
The decoder learns decision boundaries of distorted symbols as can be seen in the Fig.18. 
The output of the decoder is an approximation of the true posterior distribution equivalent 
to Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) theorem employed in classical communications. 
Therefore, the cross-entropy can be used to calculate the AE-based upper bound on the 
conditional entropy. This implies that minimizing the cross-entropy maximizes a lower 
bound on the mutual information which enables the decision boundaries [25].

4.1.6 COST FUNCTIONS 
Cost functions assess the accuracy of neural network predictions relative to actual data. 
With multiple machine learning components requiring optimization, the final cost 
function combines several loss measures. 
As mentioned, the RNN utilizes MSE to quantify and minimize the difference between 
estimated and actual phase noise, effectively guiding noise estimation. A hyperparameter, 
lambda_ph, dynamically adjusts the importance of these losses during training, allowing 
the RNN to focus initially on noise patterns before the autoencoder shifts to signal 
reconstruction. 
CROSS-ENTROPY 
Cross-entropy quantifies the difference between the probability distributions of 
reconstructed and original signals, guiding the autoencoder for accurate representation. 
TensorFlow's softmax_cross_entropy_with_logits_v2 function is suited for multi-class 
classification problems where classes are mutually exclusive [40]. This function measures 
the probability error in discrete classification tasks. 
In this context, labels and logits are matrices where rows represent training instances and 
columns represent classes. Logits are the raw predictions from a classification model 
before applying the activation function. These logits are then normalized by the SoftMax 
function. The normalized values are compared to the true labels using cross-entropy loss, 
which measures the error. The loss is then summed over different classes for each 
instance. The functions employed are shown in Fig.19.

5. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
Test simulations demonstrated that machine learning enhances CV-QKD system 
performance by reducing the variance of RPN through geometric shaping. During the 
optimization process, we made several adjustments to parameters and functions to 
develop a model that excels in mutual information and accuracy. Figure 27 compares the 
original and geometrically shaped constellations, showing how symbols are rotated and 
spread to maximize mutual information amid channel noises. The algorithm effectively 
compensates for phase noise using the RNN and manages RPN by distinctly positioning 
symbols based on their amplitude in a linear arrangement. This approach ensures that, 
despite rotation from phase noise, symbols remain distinguishable. It should be noted that 
optimizing cross-entropy also influences excess noise, as mutual information directly 
relates to covariance, as shown in equation (11).

6. CONCLUSIONS 
CV-QKD is gaining attention for its high secret key rate and compatibility with standard 
telecom components. LLO-CV-QKD, the predominant system, suffers significantly from 
excess noise due to RPN, which stems from the use of independent lasers. This thesis has 
successfully demonstrated that GCS can effectively reduce RPN in CV-QKD systems. 
Specifically, our results show that using end-to-end learning with autoencoders and RNNs 
enables GCS to create constellations that are more tolerant to RPN compared to the 
original. This improved tolerance minimizes RPN variance, resulting in 6% reduction in 
excess noise, enhancing overall system performance. 
The integration of autoencoder and RNN technologies provides a hybrid solution that 
leverages the robust feature learning of autoencoders with the dynamic adaptability of 
RNNs. This synergy not only increases the accuracy of geometric shaping and phase noise 
estimation but also strengthens the system's capacity to mitigate these effects. Thus, it 
offers a comprehensive and effective approach to enhancing quantum communication 
systems. 
 
6.1 
FURTHER WORK 
This approach sets a new standard for noise mitigation in CV-QKD systems and 
underscores the immense potential of integrating machine learning techniques with 
cutting-edge solutions that move beyond conventional signal processing methods. This 
strategy opens avenues for further research into sophisticated adaptive compensation for 
various channel impairments, particularly those arising from the coexistence of classical 
channels with QKD systems. The next phase will include experimental testing for GCS, 
solidifying the theoretical advancements with practical validation.
