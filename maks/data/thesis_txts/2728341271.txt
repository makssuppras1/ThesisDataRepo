Abstract 
This thesis focuses on the aerodynamic design and optimization of a UAV (unmanned aerial 
vehicle) for wind farm inspections. The objective is to develop a drone model that combines 
endurance for long distance travels to offshore wind farms with manoeuvrability and stability 
during hovering inspections. The selected drone model is a hybrid design with fixed wings 
and four rotors, including two front tilt-rotors for horizontal cruise. This design enables to ally 
VTOL, hovering and cruise flight capacities. The payload consists of the FLIR Vue Pro R 
camera for thermographic analysis to perform wind turbine inspection. A comprehensive 
literature review is conducted as a first step to understand the state of the art in drone design, 
optimization methods, wind farm inspection specificities and regulations. The research 
methodology involves studying site-specific factors, mission requirements, and aerodynamic 
principles. Calculations are performed to conceive and size the wings, tail, propeller surfaces 
and all other components, and to determine power requirements, motors and battery selection, 
and autonomy estimation. Additionally, considerations are made to implement solar-energy 
technologies on the drone surfaces to enhance the endurance. The findings of this study 
contribute to the field of drone design for wind farm inspection by offering a comprehensive 
approach that considers the specific mission requirements and aerodynamic principles. The 
proposed design and optimization methodology can serve as a basis for further research and 
development in the field. 
 
Keywords: UAV, aerodynamic design, wind farm inspection, hybrid quadcopter, design 
optimization

l'inspection des parcs éoliens offshore. L'objectif est de développer un modèle de drone alliant

d’optimisation, les spécificités des missions d'inspection des parcs éoliens offshore et 
règlementations. La méthodologie de recherche implique l'étude des facteurs spécifiques au 
site, des exigences de mission et des principes aérodynamiques. Les résultats obtenus 
permettent le dimensionnement des ailes, de la queue, des hélices et des autres éléments du 
drone ainsi que de déterminer la puissance nécessaire, la sélection des moteurs et batteries et 
l'estimation de l'autonomie de vol. De plus, des réflexions sont menées pour intégrer des 
technologies d’énergie solaire sur les surfaces du drone afin d'améliorer son endurance. Les 
résultats de cette étude contribuent aux travaux sur la conception de drones pour l'inspection

dans ce domaine.

I-1 Literature review 
I-1-1 Background and context of the project 
 
 
Renewable energy operations and especially offshore wind energy development have 
been a focus of attention in Europe for a decade now, especially with the Green European 
Deal [ 15 ] and the plan REPowerEU for producing clean energy. The execution of this plan 
through the construction of new offshore wind farms in European waters also brings a high 
demand for maintenance and overhaul of the wind turbines to remain sustainable and 
efficient. The objective of the project is to propose a new drone model, operable from shore, 
and capable of doing high-quality imaging inspection via non-destructive-testing (NDT) 
methods of offshore wind turbines (OWT). 
 
 
NDT methods are increasingly developed for infrastructure inspection. Shafiee et al. 
(2021) list especially ultrasonic testing (UT), acoustic emission (AE), fibre optics, 
thermographic testing (TT) and radiographic testing (RT) [ 33 ]. Both latter methods are 
particularly adapted for our inspection drone since they enable a short-time overview on 
surface (using TT) and on depth flaws (using RT). Combined with visual inspection, analysis 
and expertise, these tests are well adapted for wind turbines overhauls. The inspections of 
wind turbines last approximately between 20 to 130 minutes per turbine depending on the 
defaults detection and analyses needed to be performed. 
 
 
The utilization of multicopter unmanned aerial vehicles (UAV) for inspection missions 
is not something new and is used in numerous fields. Currently, drones are used to inspect 
bridges, buildings, powerplants, onshore and offshore turbines, powerlines, pipelines, 
railways, and other infrastructures. However, UAV used for most of these operations are 
multicopter or rotary wing designs often with VTOL capacities such as shown in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1 : Rotary-wing (or multicopter) design 
Providing the hovering capacity and enabling great manoeuvrability, multicopters are very 
well adapted to these missions. Nordin, et al. (2022) presented a general review of drone for 
inspection, maintenance, and repair (IMR) on OWT and highlighted the task complexity it 
represents [ 28 ]. However, visual inspections and NDT inspections are already efficiently 
performed by unmanned vehicles (Unmanned Aerial Vehicles – UAV, Unmanned 
Underwater Vehicles – UUV, and Unmanned Surface Vehicles – USV). Two main challenges 
are pointed out: endurance and communication issues due to far-from-land location of wind 
farms and the study proposes collaborative operations solutions. Several companies already 
propose UAV inspection of wind turbines using quadcopters such as Force Technology [ 17 ] 
and AUAV (Australian UAV) [ 7 ]. Yet, electrical quadcopters and currently used 
technologies do not meet endurance and range requirements to perform cruise flights and long 
operations. Thus, these missions require an entire team to be deployed by boat on the wind 
farm site, which is both time and money consuming. Kabbabe, et al. (2021) estimated the cost 
of the deployment of an inspection team in offshore wind farms reaches 18£ per hour for the 
boat workforce and the technician workforce and 600£ per day for the transport costs [ 23 ].

I-1-2 Aerodynamic methodology implementation for design and optimization of 
aircrafts 
Several research studies of design and optimization of aircrafts are being conducted, 
and a similar methodology stands out. The first factors considered are the mission 
specifications before choosing the appropriate targeted sketches and materials. Decision 
matrix can be a great way to underline the reasons of the choices [ 5 ][ 32 ]. For basic 
aerodynamic design of rotorcrafts, the blade element momentum theory (BEMT) is often 
applied, and due to its dependency to experimental data, the design process is extensively 
iterative [ 6 ][ 22 ]. To optimize of the aerofoil design, thorough tests and comparisons with 
several specialized softwares (such as XFLR, Xfoil or even Javafoil) are often conducted 
between the different options [ 20 ]. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is then commonly 
used to verify and optimize blade or wing designs [ 25 ][ 37 ].

I-1-4 Regulation 
The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) developed in 2020 a UAS 
Regulation Model and Advisory Circulars (AC) to help any country implement UAS 
regulations. These texts are mainly drawn from existing regulations of the member states of 
ICAO. They issued three regulation texts (Part 101, Part 102 and Part 149) and several 
complementary AC that can be roughly summarized as follows: 
- 
all UA should be registered; 
- 
UA weighing 25 kg or less and operating in Standard UA Operating Conditions 
require no additional operational review; 
- 
if the UA weighs more than 15 kg, the UA must be inspected and approved; 
- 
particular UA operations that do not comply to Part 101 requirements (mass 
superior to 25 kg, special operations, BVLOS flights…) are addressed in Part 102 
and need authorization and certification; 
- 
Part 102 enables a more expeditious review when manufacturers declare a type or 
model of UA as being sufficiently tested for a specific operational category or that 
has having received an approval from an Approved Aviation Organization.

I-1-4-1 European regulations 
 
 
The European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) implemented regulation 
measures concerning leisure or commercial civilian UAS in 2019 [ 14 ]. The UAS 
Regulations provides for three categories of drones depending on the risk of the missions: 
open, specific, and certified categories. The open category includes light UAS (with MTOW 
inferior to 25 kg) with low-risk operations. Divided into three subcategories (A1, A2, A3) 
depending on the proximity to people during operation, this category imposes: 
- registration of the operator (light UAS certificate – LUC); 
- safety distances to people and buildings. 
The specific category includes drone operations that exceed the open category limits such as 
BVLOS flights, flight exceeding 120 m AGL, material dropping missions, and operations of 
drones with MTOW heavier than 25 kg. To operate in this category, several documents are 
needed: 
- registration of the operator (LUC – required in the open category as well); 
- declaration based on a standard scenario (STS); 
- operational authorization with predefined risk assessment (PDRA). This authorization 
must be issued by the NAA (National Aviation Authority) of the registration country of 
the drone. Once issued, it covers every accorded flight operation  of the drone. If the 
operation is different from the ones covered by the authorization, a specific operation 
risk assessment (SORA) must be issued; 
- design verification report (DVR). This report is issued by the EASA once the design of 
the drone is verified and complies with the operational safety objectives. 
- once these documents are in order, the drone can be operated freely within the limits 
provided by the documents. 
The certified category plans the regulations for the operations with the highest level of risk 
such as passenger or cargo transportation, IFR flights, flight above populated area with a 
drone dimension superior to 3 m,  and future advanced UAS missions. 
 
I-1-4-2 United States regulations 
The Federal Aviation Administration is the US entity that issues regulations 
concerning air activities [ 16 ]. Small – UAS are defined as UAS with MTOW inferior to 25 
kg and horizontal speed inferior to 160 km/h. Certified UAS are ones that do not meet the 
previous requirements

II Methodology 
The methodology of this process is incremental and iterative allowing for gradual and 
responsive progress, evaluating results, and adjusting parameters accordingly. The iterative 
process is simplified and shown in the figure below (Figure 4): 
Figure 4 : Flowchart presenting the iterative process of the drone design 
 
The aerodynamic parameters are derived from first-principles using Blade Element 
Theory (BET), Blade Element Momentum Theory (BEMT), and a parameter fitting 
procedure. The book Principles of Helicopter Aerodynamics (Leishman, 2006) is used as the 
main source for the applied theory [ 26 ]. After application to the targeted mission 
requirements, a drone model is issued with aerodynamic and geometric parameters and 
performance evaluation. Different parameters of the drone are determined using COTS 
(commercial off-the-shelf) components specifications. The drone structure is designed in 
SolidWorks (wings, fuselage, rotor supports, landing gears, tail. A Matlab code is created to 
enable readers and researchers to modify the input parameters and obtain geometrical and 
aerodynamical values for a similar family-model drone for further studies. The code and the 
instructions are given in Appendix 7.

II-2 Description of the chosen aerodynamic design features 
II-2-1 Propeller blade aerofoil and dimensions

III-4 Solar panels addition 
To augment the autonomy of the batteries, we can use the sun as an additional source 
of energy. The drone’s mission requirements imposing great weather conditions for the flight, 
the use of solar energy seems a pertinent choice. The technology of thin-film solar cells is 
increasingly popular in aviation and battery-powered aircrafts (Abbe & Smith, 2016). Several 
factors are to take into account for the implementation of solar cells as a source of energy: the 
available sun-exposed surface, the energy flow in the aircraft and the conversion efficiency of 
the solar cells. 
Our drone has a total sun-exposed available surface (denoted Asc) of: 
Asc = A + AHT = 2.482 m2 
The current density of GaAs thin-film solar cells being approximately Jsc = 24 mA/cm2, an 
open circuit voltage Voc = 0.98 V and an efficiency of ηsc = 0.18 [ 27 ], we can calculate the 
usable current (denoted Iusable) and the power (denoted Psc) provided by the solar panels: 
Iusable = Jsc · Asc · ηsc = 107 A 
Psc = 105 W 
The provided power is very moderate compared to the mass added by the solar cell on the 
structure (even if GaAs thin film is a lightweight technology), and therefore the benefits of 
solar panel addition with those conditions are questionable. However, more efficient solutions 
of solar energy can prove to be a notable improvement for the drone autonomy and should be 
more thoroughly investigated.

IV Conclusion 
 
 
In the current work, a solution for BVLOS inspection operations on offshore wind 
turbines with a hybrid quadrotor UAV model has been investigated. The aim was to simplify 
wind turbine inspection through UAV-performed non-destructive testing while reducing costs 
and complexity of current quadrotor operations that require an entire ship crew to reach site. 
The establishment of the state-of-art in long-range, high-manoeuvrability UAV with hovering 
capacity enabled us to select a global model shape for our drone. Application of basic 
aerodynamics principles in consideration of the mission requirements allowed us to get a 
drone design with the following summarized results (Figure 28). 
 
Figure 28 : Simplified results of the drone design 
Performance calculations established a maximum hovering time of 35 minutes, an optimum 
(minimum consumption) cruise speed of 13 m/s and a cruise autonomy of 61 minutes, 
enabling the drone to travel a total of 47 km. The maximum range is 52 km and reached for a 
cruise speed of 16 m/s but augmenting the energy consumption. This means that the designed 
model does not meet the requirements for the hovering phase of the operation but respects the 
specifications concerning the range to reach and be able to come back from short-distance 
offshore sites. To improve the drone's performance, further studies should investigate more 
thoroughly the propeller blade optimization. Several options can be considered such as 
variable or controllable pitch of the blades, blade twist optimization, blade curvature, or even 
implementation of evolving aerofoil profile along the blade radius. Moreover, even though 
well-established, the blade element momentum theory uses a simplified aerodynamic model 
that does not take into account several impacting considerations for our model. That includes 
overall propeller shape (rectangular, elliptical, etc.), blade arrangement (twist, curvature, etc.), 
several dynamic effects (such as blade bending or blade interactions) and is very dependent 
on experimental data (for calibration of the model) and to flight condition parameters. 
Therefore, this study should be completed with computational fluid dynamics or wind-tunnel 
testing using a physical model of the drone to gather more accurate data and validate the 
==PAGE:50==
Final Year Thesis – L. GAZULL-KAZMIERCZAK 
 
34
findings. Furthermore, it is important to acknowledge that the airflow interactions between the 
wings and rotors or between two neighbouring rotors were not considered in this study. 
Investigating these interactions is crucial as they can have a significant impact on the overall 
aerodynamic performance and efficiency of the drone, especially in multi-rotor designs such 
as this model. Lastly, the performance and energy-related calculations presented in this study 
have certain limitations. One important aspect not considered were the non-stationary phases 
of flight, which can significantly impact the overall energy consumption and flight endurance. 
The discharge state of the battery during specific flight phases can also be tested. These 
factors introduce uncertainties in the calculated performances and energy efficiency of the 
drone. Future studies could focus on addressing these limitations to provide a more 
comprehensive understanding of the drone's performance. Solutions to improve the autonomy 
of the model can be found in energy-saving propeller design, augmented and optimized 
energy supply, and lightweight structural modifications for instance.
