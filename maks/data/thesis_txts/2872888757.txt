Abstract
This thesis presents a comprehensive study on the optimization of hydrofoils
using computational fluid dynamics and structural analysis. The work focuses
on developing efficient optimization methodologies for improving hydrofoil
performance while maintaining structural integrity.
The theoretical foundation encompasses incompressible fluid mechanics
governed by the Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes equations with Shear-
Stress Transport turbulence modeling, and linear elastic solid mechanics
for structural analysis. The numerical implementation employs the finite
element method for both fluid and solid domains, with particular attention
to proper discretization strategies and boundary layer resolution.
Two optimization approaches are investigated: shape optimization for
external geometry refinement and topology optimization for internal struc-
tural design. Both methodologies utilize the adjoint analytical method for
efficient sensitivity analysis, enabling gradient-based optimization with the
Globally Convergent Method of Moving Asymptotes algorithm. The adjoint
approach provides computational efficiency independent of the number of
design variables, making it particularly suitable for large-scale optimization
problems.
The optimization framework addresses multiple objectives including lift
maximization, drag minimization, and cavitation mitigation, while satisfying
structural constraints such as stress limitations and volume fractions. Special
consideration is given to cavitation phenomena, which significantly affect
hydrofoil performance in marine applications.
The developed methodology is implemented in COMSOL Multiphysics
6.3, leveraging its coupled multiphysics capabilities for fluid-structure inter-
action problems. The framework demonstrates the potential for systematic
hydrofoil design optimization, providing insights into the trade-offs between
hydrodynamic performance and structural requirements.
Results show that the combined approach of shape optimization can
significantly improve hydrofoil while the typology optimization was a failure.
A new approach is needed to complete the analysis.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The biggest innovation in sailing within the last decade is the use of hydrofoils.
By lifting the boats out of the water, the hydrodynamic drag can be drastically
reduced, creating a much more efficient platform, which turns into faster
boats.
This technology has been adopted in a growing number of boat classes.
From the America’s cup (AC), SailGP, International Monohull Open Classes
Association (IMOCA) to dinghies and even the Olympics with the introduction
of the Nacra 17. One of the first classes to adopt the use of hydrofoils was the
Moth dinghy class. The Moth dinghy is a one-person dinghy with a single sail.
It’s an open class with box rules, encouraging innovation and new thinking
within the class. Therefore this project is based on the design and function
of a Moth class dinghy, but can be useful for other applications.
1.1
Current Designs
Current hydrofoils fall into two major categories, single- and two-component
designs. The single component hydrofoils are a single stiff foil shape, where
the angle of attack is used to control the lift. The control is done at the base
of the foil, usually within the boat. This is seen on the F50 catamarans used
in SailGP. Some boats use a passive design, where no control is used. These
include the IMOCA 60 class, used for off-shore racing, and the Nacra 17. In
the case of Nacra 17, the weight of the crew is used for controlling the balance
and the angle of attack of the foils.
The two-component designs are similar to an airplane wing, with a flap on
the trailing edge used to control the lift. In this case, a control system exerts
a controlling force that moves the flap into the desired position. Usually the
foil is rigidly mounted on the boat with a fixed angle of attack. This type of

The Waszp class is a one-design boat based on the Moth class rules.
Created to simplify and consolidate the ideas of the previous decade of

to the bottom of a ’vertical’, attached to the boat. One foil is attached to the

1.1.1
Problem
At higher speeds a hydrofoil will begin to cavitate due to the low pressure
on the upper side of the hydrofoil. With a more ’streamlined’ hydrofoil it’s
possible to increase the speed where cavitation occurs. However, such a
’streamlined’ profile will also increase the take-off speed. As the hydrofoiling
boats have a substantial advantage when flying, it’s critical to have as wide a
foiling envelope as possible.
With the current design, the takeoff and cavitation speeds are limited by
the shape of the main body of the main foil. It would be beneficial to have
a foil that creates enough lift at a slower speed while minimizing drag and
mitigating cavitation at higher speeds.

1.2
Current methods
Optimization within this field is nothing new; shape optimization for airplane
and wind turbine profiles are an essential part of the design process. [7]
showed how an adjoint approach can speed up the shape optimization of
turbine blades within a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) domain. In
the America’s Cup, the absolute pinnacle of sailing, the teams utilize similar
shape optimization techniques for their foils with the inclusion of cavitation
mitigation. [8] showed the process and highlights the many compromises
necessary for an ’optimized’ design when using a two-component design. This
includes trading drag for a higher cavitation limit and balancing take-off speed
with high-speed performance. A difficult feat that leads to foils optimized for
a very specific working envelope. [9] showed how a more compliant trailing
edge can improve performance, in this case instead of a single rigid foil.
Using and optimizing the deformation of a solid mechanics body is also
not new. A so-called compliant mechanism is able to deform in a desired
manner. [10] showed how Topology Optimization (TO) can be used to create
such compliant mechanisms in a systematic way. TO for foils has also been
investigated, both for stiffness and morphing capabilities. [11] showed how
a morphing aileron can be implemented with the objective of reducing drag
from the imperfect connection between the wing and aileron. In this case
using a distributed prescribed deformation in the form of a linear-strain-
based actuator. Other examples include using a multi-layered compliant
mechanism to control the trailing edge flap [12] and the combination of shape-
and topology optimization to improve airplane performance, in this case the
leading edge of the wing [13].
1.3
Proposed design
Instead of using a two-component design, a single shape that can morph
from one shape to another is proposed. Functioning similarly to a compliant
mechanism. The outside of this shape, the shell, has to be able to withstand
water pressure, therefore it has to be solid. Two cross beams, one for carrying

2.2
Turbulence
Turbulence is perhaps best described in the famous experiment by Reynolds
(1883) where water is drawn through a glass tube along with a streak of highly
colored water. He describes the flow in 3 steps [15, p. 942]:
When the velocities were sufficiently low, the streak of colour
extended in a beautiful straight line through the tube.
As the velocity was increased by small stages, at some point in the
tube, always at a considerable distance from the trumpet or intake,
the colour band would all at once mix up with the surrounding
water, and fill the rest of the tube with a mass of coloured water.
On viewing the tube by the light of an electric spark, the mass
of colour resolved itself into a mass of more or less distinct curls,
showing eddies.
He included illustrations of the three descriptions seen in figure 2.1. This is
what we now call laminar and turbulent flow.
Reynolds (1883) also proposed a non-dimensional correlation for where
this laminar to turbulent transition happens. Later renamed the Reynolds
number by Sommerfeld (1909). The Reynolds number (Re) for a flat plate

Several different types of turbulent models exist. Ranging from the most
simple algebraic models to turbulence energy models and the more advanced
Large Eddy Simulation (LES) [14, p. 647]. The algebraic models express the
Reynolds stress as a product of turbulence viscosity and mean strain rate with

used due to the inaccuracy and reliance on the mixing length.

turbulence. Thus the computational time can be significantly reduced while
still being able to simulate higher Reynolds number flows. LES are especially
good for separation, such as flow around bluff bodies, as they can simulate
large eddies directly [17, p. 385].

2.5
Cavitation
With the low pressure on the upper side of the hydrofoil, the water can
reach its vapor pressure. This leads to the formation of vapor bubbles. This
phenomenon is known as cavitation. The formation of these bubbles can
==PAGE:32==

drag [23]. This disturbance can also cause loss of control and stability. When
the bubbles collapse, the violent pressure difference can create pressure spurts
or jets that can damage the surface of the foil and create pitting. See figure
2.3a.
For a hydrofoil, cavitation will usually happen on the suction side of
the foil along the leading edge, where the pressure is usually the lowest.
This is known as sheet cavitation. The formation of these bubbles can be
seen in figure 2.3b. In most applications, including hydrofoils, cavitation is
undesirable and efforts are made to mitigate its occurrence.

(FVM) usually used for solid and fluid mechanics problems respectively. In
this case, Comsol 6.3 is used which utilizes FEM for both fluid and solid
mechanics problems.

Chapter 5
Optimization
For both the shape and topology optimization, the Globally Convergent
Method of Moving Asymptotes (GCMMA) algorithm is used to solve the
optimization problem. It works at two levels [32]:
• The solid mechanics or CFD simulation is done. The objective function,
constraint and design variable gradients are computed. A subproblem
is constructed that is guaranteed to be convex and feasible is passed to
an inner iteration. The subproblem is constructed from the gradients
and moving asymptotes, based on the previous iteration.
• In the inner iteration, an optimum is found from the subproblem and
the objective function and constraints are evaluated at this point. If
the constraints and objective function are valid, this optimum is used
as the new position and the inner iteration is stopped. If not, a new,
more conservative optimum is found and tested.
Crucially, the evaluation of the gradients is only done once for each outer
iteration, with the gradients typically computationally expensive to find. This
is also the difference between the GCMMA and the single-loop Method of
Moving Asymptotes (MMA). One of the strengths is that the constraints are
integrated directly into the inner iteration of the solver, thus a step is rarely
taken outside of these constraints.
To help the algorithm, the values of the objective and constraints matter.
For both, it is desired for the values of these to be within 0 – 100 [32]. If the
objective or constraints fall out of this range, they should be modified before
going through the algorithm.

Sensitivity analysis
Sensitivity analysis is crucial for gradient-based optimization methods like
GCMMA. It provides the gradients of the objective function and constraints
with respect to the design variables, which guide the optimization algorithm
towards an optimal solution. As there are typically fewer constraints than
design variables, the adjoint analytical method is preferred over finite difference
methods [33, p. 100].

the forward problem once for each design variable. This approach becomes

A mesh convergence study of the CFD domain is done with the starting

Benchmark
The performance of the current foil is evaluated. As there is a constant lift
force, the coefficient of lift (Cl) should follow the analytical curve. Thus
the coefficient of drag (Cd) is the performance parameter and the minimum
pressure (pmin) defining the cavitation limit.
As the lift force is constant, the coefficient of lift is only dependent on the
flow speed and can be shown analytically in figure 9.1. The coefficient of lift
becomes an exponentially decaying function with very high gradients for low
flow speed and low gradients for high flow speed. As the geometry determines
the value of the coefficient of lift, this phenomenon tells us that the geometry
has to change quite radically at low speeds and very little at higher speeds.
It also indicates that a very low lift, nearly symmetrical, shape is needed for
higher speeds.
The benchmark foil achieves this lower lift by inverting the trailing edge
flap at higher speeds. Already at 5 m s−1 the flap is in its ’normal’ position.
This means that nearly the entire range of motion is used just for taking off.
Looking at the Cl
Cd would indicate that the foil is most efficient at 5 m s−1.
This is also where the flap is in its ’natural’ position with very little error in
the interface between the main body and the flap.
The minimum pressure of the simulation indicates that cavitation will
happen around 17 m s−1. This fits well with the maximum speed achieved in
the boat, giving the foil a critical cavitation number of cc = 0.663.
Looking at the local cavitation number distribution at 20 m s−1 (figure
9.2), three critical areas are seen: just below the leading edge, on the upper
surface, and at the interface between the main body and the flap. Some slight
fluctuations along the perimeter is also seen, this are most likely caused by
imperfect modeling of the geometry leading to small ’bumps’ on the boundary.
Interestingly both the front and the interface of the flap are critical areas
further exemplifying that both the shape and interface are suboptimal.

Chapter 12
Discussion
As can be seen from the results, it was not possible to create a morphing
structure that closely matches the desired shape, at least not with the limits
and methodology laid out. However, many lessons can be taken away from
the project.
12.1
Shape optimization
The shape optimization was able to produce more efficient foil shapes and
successfully limit the minimum pressure when needed. The accuracy of the
CFD simulations could be improved with a larger domain, more refined mesh
and lower y+ values. However, in this case the accuracy was deemed ’good
enough’ with the emphasis on faster simulation, which in turn enables more
SO iterations. Each complete SO took between 2 h – 4 h, which quickly adds
up when adjusting the proper constraint values and flow speed.
The interaction between the shape and topology optimization could be
improved. Currently only the perimeter constraint is directly influenced by
the presumed capabilities of the TO. The area constraint is a kind of pseudo
constraint, as the TO is not actually limited by the area, but rather by the
minimum thickness, change in shape, minimum feature size and so on. The
area constraint does however do a good job preventing such features from
developing. Several other constraints, like minimum thickness as seen in [8],
were implemented with small changes compared to only using the area. It
was also deemed positive to have as few constraints as possible, especially
when dealing with such a wide range of flow speed. Each new constraint adds
another level of complexity as the constraints have to be compatible with
each other, but they can also add an extra layer of control.
A more iterative process between the SO and TO is probably needed. One
57
==PAGE:74==
CHAPTER 12. DISCUSSION
58
possibility is to lock the front geometry to a ’fast’ shape, such that the TO
only needs to morph the rear of the geometry. However, such implementation
further constrains the SO, most likely leading to a less efficient shape.
12.2
Topology optimization
The TO was less successful in its execution. The implementation of the
TO seemed to be correct with the morphing geometry moving in the right
direction.
However, it was not possible for the optimizer to satisfy the
perimeter constraint even after 1000 iterations. This can either be because of
the implementation or the method/geometry. As the constraint is moving in
the correct direction, the implementation was deemed correct, indicating that
it is not possible for the solver to find a solution with the current method
and/or geometry.
12.2.1
Distributed load
One problem could be the use of a single point/body load. This requires
the TO to find a geometry that can transfer this force across the domain
and deform/morph where necessary. The control force is implemented this
way because of integration with the current control system on the boat. On
the current design a single point load is used to control the flap position.
However, it seems to be too tall an order for a morphing foil. Even with a
very large force, the front of the foil is still not morphing correctly.
In [11] a distributed force, implemented as a linear-strain-based actuator,
is used to control the trailing edge flap. The results clearly indicate the TO
placing the controlling force throughout the domain, further supporting the
difficulty with a single point force.
12.2.2
Fine balance between parameters
For the TO there is a fine balance between the volume fraction, magnitude of
control force and Young’s modulus. These are all values manually specified,
and thus difficult to be certain are correct. Perhaps some combination of the
three yields a better result; so far only qualified guesses are used based on
the output from previous studies. A full parameter study was not done.
==PAGE:75==
CHAPTER 12. DISCUSSION
59
12.2.3
More shapes
With this method only a fast and slow shape is used. This was decided based
on the shapes from the SO. As the shapes are changing in a somewhat linear
fashion, it could be assumed that the morphing between the two would follow
this trend. This would have to be verified. The shapes in between could also
be implemented; however, in this case the ’user’ will have to decide how large
a force is needed for each shape. This could be linear or something more
complex.
12.2.4
Materials
For this TO the linear-elastic material model is used, which does hinder
the solution space somewhat. One could imagine, especially with the large
deformations associated with morphing, that a non-linear material model
could be useful. When stepping into the plastic deformation range, the allowed
deformations can be much higher, of course dependent on the specific material.
However, it does come with some limitations. First of all, the control force
has to be bi-directional. As the geometry won’t spring back into shape, the
control force has to deform it in both directions. There could also be concerns
regarding longevity, fatigue and so forth.

Chapter 13
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the feasibility of developing a morphing hydrofoil for
a sailing application through the integration of shape optimization (SO) and
topology optimization (TO). The aim was to create a hydrofoil capable of
adapting its geometry across a range of speeds while maintaining the required
performance.
The methodology involved optimizing foil shapes for various speeds using
CFD simulations, followed by attempting to design a morphing structure
capable of transitioning between these optimized configurations using TO.
13.1
Key Findings
The shape optimization was successful in generating efficient foil geometries
that reduced drag while respecting cavitation constraints through minimum
pressure limitations. The optimization process revealed that hydrofoil geome-
try can vary dramatically across the velocity range, with low speeds requiring
high-lift configurations and high speeds demanding nearly symmetric, low-lift
profiles.
However, the primary objective of creating a viable morphing structure
was not achieved within the constraints of the current methodology. The TO
approach, while theoretically sound, failed to find a solution that satisfied the
morphing constraint, even after 1000 iterations. This indicates fundamental
limitations in either the optimization approach or the physical feasibility of
the proposed morphing concept.
13.2
Technical Limitations
Several critical limitations emerged during the study:
60
==PAGE:77==
CHAPTER 13. CONCLUSION
61
The use of a single-point control force proved inadequate for achieving
the required morphing behavior. The topology optimizer struggled to cor-
rectly morph the forward section of the foil into the desired shape, likely a
consequence of the single-point force being located in the rear part of the foil.
The integration between shape and topology optimization remained sub-
optimal. The current approach relies primarily on perimeter constraints to
link the two optimization phases, but this connection proved insufficient to
guide the TO toward feasible solutions. An iterative or feedback loop from
the TO to the SO is probably necessary.
13.3
Implications
Despite not achieving the primary goal, this study provides insights into the
challenges of morphing hydrofoil design. The work demonstrates that while
computational tools can effectively optimize individual aspects of the problem,
the integration between fluid and structural optimization remains challenging
and requires alternative approaches.
The findings suggest that distributed actuation systems, as shown in
[11], may be more suitable for hydrofoil morphing than single-point control
mechanisms. Additionally, the use of more sophisticated material models and
potentially non-linear structural analysis could be helpful to allow for larger
deformations.
13.4
Future work
A better or different integration between the SO and TO is required for a
similar method to be used. Either by ’freezing’ some part of the shape or
other methods.
To verify that a similar method is sound the intermediate steps of the
morphing have to be verified. For now the foil is assumed to morph somewhat
linearly between the ’fast’ and ’slow’ shape. However this is not tested.
For such a foil to work the intermediate shapes also have to be somewhat
’optimized’. Testing this can be quite challenging as the fluid forces and
control force are both in play with the control force unknown for a given
speed. Some sort of Fluid Structure Interaction (FSI) study is required to
verify wether or not more intermediate steps are required for the TO.
The solid mechanics performance is also not checked. A common problem
with high deformations is the creation of fatigue fractures, especially with a
cyclic loading like the control force. This however only makes sense to check

on a finished geometry. The fatigue is also very dependent on the specific
material.
