ABSTRACT  
In 2014, the Danish government made an agreement with the Red-Green Alliance and the Danish People’s 
Party to strengthen the railway infrastructure. With the aim of ensuring sustainable future transport, with 
the expected traffic growth to take place mainly in public transport, the Train Fund Denmark was 
established and DKK 28.5 billion was allocated. The “One Hour Model” and the electrification of the main 
lines are the two main projects established to reach this goal. In addition, several smaller projects shall 
increase the use of public transport. One of these projects is a new railway line to serve Billund Airport and 
the town of Billund. 
The overall objective of this thesis was to prepare a design proposal for the track alignment between Jelling 
and Billund with respect to design regulations, and to analyse the impact on the surrounding environment 
and existing rail traffic.  
The present work consists of two main parts; the technical data and the case study. The first part (chapter 2 
and 3), review the traffic flow to and from Billund and presents the forecast for the year of 2030, conducted 
by the Danish Transport Authority. The existing railway infrastructure and operations were evaluated 
through studies of technical data and existing timetables, and formed the basis for the operational plan 
with 30-minute services. Finally, the norms and regulations were reviewed to determine the requirements 
for the Billund-project. 
The case study (chapter 4, 5 and 6) deals with the open line design, the timetable scheduling and the 
capacity at the Jelling Station. Alignment proposals created with Bentley Rail Track Suite, with the 
surrounding environment evaluated through the Danish Environmental Portal, studies of regulations 
related to the airspace protection, and correspondence with staff at Billund Airport and Billund 
Municipality.  
The timetable scheduling evaluated the possibility to schedule new routes on the existing line. Assessments 
of the running time and the possibility to schedule new routes was evaluated through graphical timetable 
scheduling. The analysis of the Jelling Station capacity was assessed based on the complexity of the track 
layout, the probability of conflicts between train routes, and the capacity consumption of the platform 
tracks during the peak hour. 
The results indicate the majority of trips to and from Billund are related to the eastern part of Jutland, with 
most trips conducted using private transport, due to extended travel time offered by the public transport 
services. The forecasts specify a significant increase in the number of trips made into or out of the Billund 
Municipality in 2030. 
The existing railway line between Vejle and Jelling is designed as a single track line with low speed limits, 
which varies between 60 to 110 km/h. The alignment includes a crossing station at Grejsdal, which was 
decommissioned in the 1970. During the peak hour, four trains operate the line.  
The Railway Norms and the Track Engineering Rules applies to the Billund-project. Topics regulated by the 
Railway Norms shall at all times meet the requirements of the BN2-regulations, whereas railway subjects 
related to the Track Engineering Rules, shall fulfil the standard regulations. 
Evaluation of the surrounding environment showed the land is rich in terms of its nature and natural 
environment. The topography between Jelling and Billund include hilly terrain near the Vandel creek. 
Assessments indicated the need for a bridge structure to cross the creek towards the north, whereas the 
topography towards the south provides a smoother terrain profile, and a bridge structure is assessed not to 
==PAGE:5==

be needed. Directing the alignment south of the hilly terrain increases the length of the alignment and the 
minimum travel time.  
The airspace protection requires that the railway line is located on the west side of Båstlundvej to avoid 
conflicts with the unseen 3D boundaries, when the railway line is positioned in level with the terrain. 
Along the existing railway, several roads cross in level. Increasing the number of operations impact on road 
traffic. The road network between Jelling and Billund are generally with little traffic intensity. The majority 
of crossing roads are assessed to be closed. At Ballevej and Lufthavnsvej the railway line cross out of level, 
to limit the impact. At the Billund Airport and to the town of Billund, the railway line conflicts with the 
landside traffic terminal, road traffic and parking areas. Further assessments are necessary to ensure the 
safety of all users. 
The timetable scheduling indicated the need to cancel two Re-trains and to reschedule remaining 
operations, during a weekday between 6:30 and 9:30 a.m. Furthermore, there is a need to include the 
crossing station at Grejsdal, to allow scheduling of new trains.  
Based on the proposed timetable, the Jelling Station layout with two platform tracks are sufficient, as a 
maximum two trains operate at the Jelling Station simultaneously. The capacity is fully utilized during 6 
minutes throughout the peak hour. 
The conclusion of the project involves the recommendation of the north alignment with a top speed of 160 
km/h, to decrease the travel time of the public transport services. 
The Billund-line is recommended with 30-minute services with departures from Vejle Station at each hourly 
minutes of 26 and 56, whereas departures from the Billund Station are scheduled at each 09 and 39 
minutes. The travel time between Vejle and Billund corresponds to 26 minutes, whereas 29 minutes is 
needed in the opposite direction due to stops at the crossing stations. 
Jelling Station is recommended with its present layout. The recommendation is based on the assumption 
that trains are able to operate according to the proposed timetable. 
 
Key words: Railway track geometry – Norms and regulations – Operational plan – Timetable scheduling – 
Running time – Station capacity – Probability and complexity index

SAMMENFATNING 
I 2014 indgik den daværende regering en aftale med Enhedslisten og Dansk Folkeparti om at løfte 
jernbanenettet med henblik på at sikre en bæredygtig fremtidig transport, med forventningen om den 
primære trafikvækst skal ske i den offentlige transport. Dette udmøntede sig i Togfonden DK og 28,5 
milliarder blev afsat. Timemodellen og elektrificering af jernbanenettet er to primære projekter, etableret 
til at nå målet. Desuden skal flere mindre projekter øge brugen af den offentlig transport. Ét af disse 
projekter er en ny jernbane til Billund Lufthavn og Billund by. 
Det overordnede mål med dette speciale var at udarbejde et linjeføringsforslag mellem Jelling og Billund, 
som overholder gældende regler, samt at analysere påvirkningerne af det omkringliggende miljø og den 
eksisterende togdrift. 
Nærværende rapport består af to hoveddele; de tekniske data og et casestudie. Den første del (kapitel 2 og 
3) gennemgår rejsestrømme til og fra Billund og præsenterer den forventede fremtidige trafik for 2030, 
udarbejdet af Trafikstyrelsen. Det eksisterende jernbanenet og togdrift blev evalueret gennem studier af 
teknisk data og eksisterende køreplaner og danner grundlag for driftsoplægget med 30-minuters drift. 
Endelig bliver normer og regler gennemgået for at fastlægge kravene til Billund-projektet. 
Casestudiet (kapitel 4, 5 og 6) omhandler linjeføring, køreplansplanlægning og kapaciteten ved Jelling 
station. Linjeføringsforslag blev udarbejdet i Bentley Rail Track Suite med det omkringliggende miljø 
analyseret via Danmarks Miljøportal, studier af regelsæt omhandlende beskyttelse af luftrummet samt 
korrespondance med ansatte ved Billund Lufthavn og Billund Kommune. 
Køreplansplanlægningen vurderede muligheden for at planlægge nye ruter på den eksisterende bane. 
Vurderinger af køretiden samt muligheden for at planlægge nye ruter blev evalueret gennem udarbejdelse 
af grafiske køreplaner. Analyse af kapaciteten ved Jelling station blev vurderet på baggrund af 
kompleksiteten af sporudformningen, sandsynligheden for konflikter mellem togveje samt udnyttelse af 
perronsporskapaciteten i spidstimen. 
Resultaterne indikerer, at store dele af rejsestrømmene til og fra Billund er relateret til den østlige del af 
Jylland, med de fleste rejser foretaget med privat transport på grund af den øgede rejsetid med offentlig 
transport. Prognoserne angiver en betydelig stigning i antallet af rejser til og fra Billund Kommune i 2030. 
Den eksisterende bane mellem Vejle og Jelling er udlagt som en enkeltsporet bane med lav hastighed, som 
varierer mellem 60 og 110 km/t. Banen indeholder en krydsningsstation ved Grejsdal, som blev udfaset i 
1970. I spidstimen opererer fire tog på banen. 
Banenormer og sporregler er gældende for Billund-projektet. Emner, der reguleres af Banenormerne, skal 
til enhver tid opfylde kravene for BN2-regler, mens emner, relateret til sporreglerne, skal opfylde 
normalbestemmelserne. 
Evalueringen af det omkringliggende område viser at området er rigt på dets natur og miljø. Topografien 
mellem Jelling og Billund omfatter kuperet terræn nær Vandel Bæk. Vurderinger angiver et behov for en 
bro til at krydse bækken mod nord, mens topografien mod syd giver et mere jævnt terræn profil, og en bro 
vurderes ikke at være nødvendig. Føres linjeføringen syd om det kuperede terræn, øges længden af banen 
samt rejsetiden.

vi 
 
Beskyttelse af luftrummet ved Billund Lufthavn kræver, at jernbanen placeres på den vestlige side af 
Båstlundvej for at undgå konflikter med de usynlige 3D begrænsningsflader, når jernbanen placeres i niveau 
med terrænet. 
Langs den eksisterende jernbane krydser flere veje i niveau. Øges antallet af operationer på banen, vil det 
få konsekvenser for vejtrafikken. Vejnettet mellem Jelling og Billund er generelt med lille trafikintensitet. 
Det vurderes, at størstedelen af krydsende veje skal lukkes. Ved Ballevej og Lufthavnsvej krydser banen ude 
af niveau for at begrænse virkningen på vejtrafikken. Ved Billund Lufthavn og Billund by konflikter banen 
med forpladsen, vejtrafikken og parkeringsarealer. Yderligere vurderinger er nødvendige for at sikre 
sikkerheden for alle brugere. 
Køreplansplanlægningen viste et behov for at annullere to Re-tog og at omlægge de resterende operationer 
i hverdage mellem 6:30 og 9:30. Desuden er der behov for at anvende krydsningsstationen ved Grejsdal for 
at tillade planlægningen af nye tog på banen. 
Baseret på det anbefalede driftsoplæg er Jelling station med dets to perronspor tilstrækkeligt, da der 
maksimalt opererer to tog ved Jelling station på samme tid. Kapaciteten er fuldt udnyttet i 6 minutter i 
løbet af spidstimen. 
Konklusionen på projektet indebærer anbefalingen af den nordlige linjeføring med en tophastighed på 160 
km/t for at mindske rejsetiden med den offentlige transport.  
Billund-banen anbefales med 30-minuters drift med afgang fra Vejle station hvert 26 og 56 minuttal, mens 
afgang fra Billund er planlagt til hvert 09 og 39 minuttal. Rejsetiden mellem Vejle og Billund svarer til 26 
minutter, mens der er behov for 29 minutter i den modsatte retning på grund af stop ved 
krydsningsstationer. 
Jelling station anbefales med sin nuværende sporudformning. Anbefalingen er baseret på antagelsen af, at 
togene er i stand til at operere i henhold til det anbefalede driftsoplæg.

1 – INTRODUCTION  
 
This chapter presents the scope of the project. It introduces the project background and describes the 
overall aim and objectives of this master thesis. Due to the limited timeframe, it is necessary to define some 
key focus points to ensure comprehensive analyses and assessments. The focus points are specified in the 
delimitation, followed by a description of the methodology used to address the problems. Finally, an 
overview of the report structure is given.  
 
1.1 Background  
In 2014, the Danish Government announced their ambitious agreement, with the Red-Green Alliance1 and 
the Danish People’s Party2, to strengthen the railway infrastructure. With the aim to reduce congestions in 
cities and to ensure sustainable future transport, with the expected traffic growth to take place mainly in 
public transport, the Train Fund Denmark was established and DKK 28.5 billion was allocated [1].    
During the last decades, the Danish Governments have not given high priority to the rail network, which 
amongst others, results in outdated infrastructure and long travel time. The two major projects established 
to reach the goal, are electrification of the main lines and the “One Hour Model” to reduce the travel time 
to one hour between the main cities in Denmark. In addition, several smaller projects involving upgrades 
and building of new infrastructure shall increase the use of public transport [1; 2]. One of these projects is a 
new railway line to serve Billund Airport (BLL) and the town of Billund, which is expected to open in 2019 
[3].    
Prior to the agreement, preliminary investigations of possible rail connections to Billund were analysed. The 
Danish Ministry of Transport3 came with its recommendation to establish a railway with a top speed of 120 
km/h, with a connection to the existing railway infrastructure, at Gadbjerg or Jelling Station, which are 
located along the Holstebro – Vejle railway line [4; 5]. Figure 1.1 shows the two proposed alignments.

1.2 Problem statement 
Internal and external factors influence the design of a railway. Each railway project is unique as several 
project specific factors, such as, topographies, surrounding environment, train configuration and speed all 
have an impact on the design. 
The overall objective of this thesis is to prepare a design proposal for the track alignment with respect to 
design regulations, surrounding environment and existing rail traffic on the Holstebro – Vejle line.  
Several project objectives have been prepared and attached as appendix E, with the aim to answer the 
following questions: 
 
What impact does the railway have on the surrounding environment and the project cost, when the 
track alignment is designed for a speed of 120 km/h compared with a speed of 160 km/h? 
 
What is a suitable location for a crossing station in terms of timetable scheduling, network effect and 
surrounding environment? 
 
Does Jelling Station have the sufficient layout and capacity, and what effect does delays have on the 
network? 
 
 
1.3 Delimitation 
The complexity of this railway project makes it necessary to make several delimitations to ensure in-depth 
analyses and assessments. The following gives a description of the key focus points. 
This thesis deals with the Jelling – Billund alignment.  
Jelling Station is located at km 100.2 along the existing Holstebro – Vejle railway line [6, p. 484]. The single-
track line divides into two tracks prior to Jelling Station, and returns to a single-track line just beyond the 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
3 
 
station. The station layout and its capacity will be analysed ensuring Jelling Station is able to accommodate 
the future rail traffic without causing network effect due to minor delays. 
The open line design is another key element. This project focus on the rail-track geometry with respect to 
the surrounding environment and design regulations. Conventional trains will form the basis for the track 
design. Tilting trains are not part of this project, as they currently do not operate in Denmark. Due to the 
few cargo operations at the BLL Airport, freight trains are not part of this project. Distribution of cargo 
occurs to various destinations throughout Jutland [7]; therefore, it would not be profitable to transport 
cargo between BLL Airport by train.  
Electrification of the railway line is not part of this thesis. It is assumed that electrification of the Jelling – 
Billund line is conducted together with the electrification of the Holstebro – Vejle line, which is planned to 
be established between 2024-2026 [8]. However, the structure gauge is prepared for the future 
electrification of the railway line. 
Along the open track, it is analysed if there is a need for a crossing station to allow simultaneous operations 
in the opposite direction. A suitable location for a crossing station will be analysed, based on timetable 
scheduling, network effect and surrounding environment. On the existing line the timetable scheduling and 
network effect is limited to the stretch between Vejle and Jelling.

1.4 Methodology 
The following describes the methods used to address the key problems: 
 
1.4.1 Open line design 
The alignment design process involves the following stages: 
 
Download and validate technical CAD data  
 
Assess the natural surrounding environment through The Danish Natural Environment Portal4 
 
Evaluate the impact on the road traffic 
 
Analyse potential restriction based on the obstacle limitation surfaces protection the BLL aerodrome 


3 – TRACK GEOMETRY: NORMS AND REGULATIONS  
 
Norms and regulations play an essential role in any railway project as it sets restrictions to the design. This 
chapter describes the most important parameters of the track geometry, and presents the essential national 
legislations, which affect the design of the new Billund-line. It introduces the Track Engineering Rules and 
the Railway norms, and cover topics such as the horizontal and vertical alignment, the structure gauge, and 
the ballast track. 
 
3.1 Track engineering rules 
The Track alignment design standard applies to the horizontal and vertical elements of the track geometry. 
It describes three levels of threshold values, which all fulfil safety requirements, however differ in driving 
comfort and the wear on wheels and tracks. The three regulations are:   
 
Requested regulations9: should apply to new constructions and major conversions. Use of requested 
regulations often leads to increased driving comfort and minimum wear on wheels and tracks  
 
Standard regulations10: shall normally apply to all new constructions and conversions. Standard 
regulations accommodate a compromise between driving comfort and the wear on wheels and tracks 
 
Exceptional regulations11: may only apply in cases where standard regulations are impossible or very 
expensive to fulfil. Use of the exceptional regulations provides minimum driving comfort and maximum 
wear on wheels and tracks. Authorization is required to apply the exceptional regulations. The technical 
system administrators at the Rail Net Denmark12 have the authorization to approve dispensations

3.1.1 Horizontal alignment  
The horizontal alignment is a projection of the track centreline in the horizontal plane, and is comprised of 
straight lines, transition and circular curves. Transition curves can be omitted if large horizontal radii or low 
speed are used [17, p. 92]. The alignment design is an important aspect in terms of the railway efficiency 
due to its impact on the speed profile; hence the alignment design impacts the travel time. 
As a train travels in a horizontal curve, lateral centrifugal acceleration (a) acts on the train in an outward

3.1.1.1 Superelevation and cant 
Superelevation refers to the concept where the height of the outer rail is elevated while height of the inner 
rail remains level. Doing so creates a cant (Δh) with the top of the rails sloping inward towards the centre of 
the circles. Figure 3.1 illustrates a train running through a curve without (left side), and with (right side) 
superelevation. In both situations, the lateral centrifugal acceleration and the acceleration of gravity (g) 
acts on the train from the point of gravity. The resultant component (K) is the sum of the centrifugal 
acceleration and the acceleration of gravity. 
 
Figure 3.1: Forces on a train in curves without cant and with a balanced cant [17, pp. 84-85]

resulting in a > 0. The resultant component will therefore act towards the outer rail, causing an uneven

distribution of load, with greater wear on the outer wheel flanges and rail. This scenario is referred to as 
cant deficiency (I). 
The example on the right indicates the curve is created with a cant where the acceleration of gravity 
balances the centrifugal acceleration, resulting in a = 0. This scenario creates an even load distribution on 
the tracks, with the resultant component acting parallel to the trains centreline and perpendicular to the 
SO-plane; a plane located on top of the rails. It is referred to as the ideal cant (ha), and applies to an ideal 
speed only, for a curve with the radius R. 
In the case when a train travels faster than the ideal speed, it results in cant deficiency, whereas a lower 
speed, results in cant excess E. In such a case, the centrifugal acceleration is negative, a = < 0, and the 
resultant component acts towards the direction of the inner rail. This scenario transfers greater load to the 
inner rail, resulting in greater wear of the inner wheel flanges and rail.

3.1.1.3 Transition curves and ramps

has zero curvature. Therefore, the radius equals infinity, R = ∞. The curvature increases gradually along the 
length of the transition curve, and ends with a radius similar to the radius of the circular curve, at the point 
of tangency.

3.1.3 Switches and crossings 
Switches and crossings allow trains to move freely between tracks at the same level. At stations, the 
crossovers permits trains to operate between platform tracks, whereas a turnout is used to separate two 
tracks, e.g. where the Billund-line deviates from the existing railway.  
Switches and crossings should according to [17, p. 26, 41] be placed along the straight parts of the 
horizontal and vertical alignments. Locating switches and crossings in curves is unfortunate because of 
possible conflicts between cants. Furthermore, a crossover in curves increases the construction cost, due to 
the customized production.  
A crossover consists of two switches and crossings located opposite to one another, separated only by a 
short straight section. A train running through a crossover is exposed to lateral centrifugal acceleration, cf. 
section 3.1.1, which acts in one direction in the first curve, and opposite in the second, due to the contra 
flexure curve. The maximum speed through a standard crossover varies between 40 to 130 km/h, based on 
its type.

3.2 Railway norms  
The Danish Railway norms13 are divided into three regulations similar to the Track Engineering Rules. The 
Railway norms apply to elements related to the railway design, e.g. the structure gauge and cross section. 
The Rail Net Denmark are responsible for the norms, which applies only to their areas of responsibility [18]. 
The three regulations are: 
 
BN1-regulations: are subject to authorized approval by the Rail Net Denmark. Exceptions to deviate 
from BN1-regulations can only be granted by the Rail Net Denmark following an approval by the Danish 
Transport Authority 
 
BN2-regulations: are the minimum requirements a project manager must apply to a project. Deviations 
from BN2-regulations requires an authorized approval. The Rail Net Denmark have the authority to 
grant approval for deviations, as long as the deviations respect the BN1-regulations 
 
BN3-regulations: fulfil the demands from BN1 and BN2. A project manager can deviate from BN3-
regulations without further approval, as long as the deviations respects the requests from the BN2-
regulations  
 
3.2.1 Structure gauge 
The structure gauge defines the minimum clearance needed to ensure unrestricted operations. It defines 
the free space to the sides and above of the tracks where fixed objects; e.g. masts, bridges etc., cannot 
penetrate. The heights are measured perpendicular to the SO-plane, and widths are measured from the 
profile centreline and parallel to the SO-plane [19, p. 3].  
The requirements for the structure gauge differentiate in relation to the speed and electrification of the

200 km/h and with planned electrification, with new bridges and structures.

4.1.2 Airspace protection 
Obstacle limitation surfaces (OLS) surround airport runways (RWY). As unseen 3D boundaries, their purpose 
is to ensure the safety of aircrafts during take-offs and landings. The OLS defines the height limits of 
permanent and temporary objects. In regards to this project, it is necessary to define the surfaces to ensure 
no parts of the future railway penetrate the defined OLS area and thereby creates an obstacle for the 
aerodrome operations. 
Denmark is a member of the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) [24], which states the 
requirements to the OLS based on the RWY classification [25, pp. 79-90]. The RWY at the BLL Airport is 
classified as precision approach, category III [26], with take-offs and landings carried out from both ends of 
the RWY, due to changes in the wind direction. Therefore, the approach and take-off surfaces apply to the 
09-RWY threshold, which faces the railway stretch connecting the BLL Airport with Legoland. 
The surfaces of interest for this project are the approach, the take-off climb, and the balked landing 
surfaces. The surfaces have a starting point at the level of the RWY and extend outwards with various 
climbing gradients. Other surfaces protect the airspace around the BLL Airport; however, assessing the

4.2.1 North alignment 
The north alignment is, 18.37 km, the shortest alignment proposal. This alignment crosses the Vandel creek 
and the surrounding hilly terrain towards the northern part of the creek. It results in a terrain profile with 
an altitude difference of nearly 40 meters near the creek, cf. drawing P01.2015-T201. Due to the terrain 
profile, assessment indicates the need for a bridge structure to cross the creek. 
The horizontal alignment is designed with arcs, with the smallest radius measuring 2000 m, along the open 
line between Jelling and BLL Airport, cf. appendix G. Applying cant to the horizontal curves allows the train 
to travel through with nearly zero centrifugal acceleration acting on the train, as the ideal cant is achieved, 
cf. appendix H. 
Along the open line, transition curves are designed with a length of 200 m, which results in a rate of change 
in cant as the function of time (dh/dt) of maximum 14.17 mm/s when the speed is 120 km/h, which fulfils 
the requested regulations. If the speed is increased to 160 km/h, it is necessary to apply the maximum cant 
of 150 mm to achieve an ideal cant. The increased cant and speed results in the dh/dt rising to 33.3 mm/s, 
which fulfils standard regulations. 
Prior to the BLL Airport, the alignment turns into a straight line entering the BLL Airport area at the parking 
zone towards the east. The alignment runs along the existing pedestrian corridor with the new station 
expected to be located at the east end of the terminal building near the exit area. 
From the BLL Airport, the horizontal alignment turns with a radius of 500 m towards the north direction to 
avoid the hotel building located west of the passenger terminal building, cf. figure 4.7. As the railway turns 
from Båstlund and into Nordmarksvej, a radius of 310 m is used. Applying a larger radius conflicts with the 
take-off climb surface and the commercial buildings located near the roundabout at Nordmarksvej. The 
speed limit between BLL Airport and Billund is 80 km/h due to the size of the radius. 
Based on the north alignment, the minimum travel time needed to cover the distance between Jelling and 
BLL Airport is found to be 9 min. and 20 sec. when the maximum speed is 120 km/h. Increasing the 
maximum speed to 160 km/h, the minimum travel time is reduced to 7 min. and 36 sec., cf. appendix I. 
Seven properties, along with additional land needed for the construction of the railway, are expected to be 
expropriated. Furthermore, the areas with forest reserve are in conflict with the alignment proposal. Re-
establishment of the forest reserve is assumed to be necessary.

5.1.1.4 Running time according to the speed profile 
The speed profile of the railway line is important when calculating the running time. The speed restrictions 
shall be respected at all times and no part of a train must exceed the limit. At places where the speed limit 
decreases, decelerating of the train shall occur prior to the change to ensure the front of the train does not 
pass with a speed greater than the limit. Similarly, when the speed limit rises, no part of the train must 
travel with a speed greater than the limit of a given cross section. Hence, acceleration must not occur until 
the rear end of the train has passed the point where the speed limit changes.

5.2 Graphical timetable and crossing station 
A graphical timetable is a common tool used for planning of train operations. It consists of a co-ordinate 
system with the distance shown along one axis and the time along the other. In Denmark, the distance is 
displaced along the x-axis and the time along the y-axis, with the time progressing downwards [33, p. 21]. 
Each train scheduled to operate on the railway line is illustrated with its own line. A line with low gradient 
indicates a train planned with a high speed, whereas a steep gradient shows a train planned low speed. A 
vertical line indicates a planned stop, with the length of the vertical line describing the time of the planned 
stop. 
On a single track line, the graphical timetable is used to identify a suitable location for a crossing station. If 
two lines cross each other along the open line section, then a crossing station is needed or there is a need 
to reschedule the operations, to avoid collision.

supplements extend the travel time and take up more railway capacity.

6.6 Evaluation of the method used to analyse the station capacity 
The method used to evaluate the station capacity looks at the station in isolation and considers only the 
ideal conditions based on the proposed timetable.  
The probability index of conflicting routes is found based on the number of trains operating at the station 
during the peak hour. In principle, each operation could be scheduled to operate at the station by one at a 
time, which would result in no conflicts with other trains. Therefore, the probability of conflicting routes 
may be analysed during the peak period(s) of the peak hour, to give a more exact picture. However, with 
the few operations scheduled to operate at the Jelling Station, and the simple station layouts, the method 
to analyse the complexity of the track layout, and the probability of conflicting routes is found to be more 
relevant for complex stations with greater traffic intensity. 
Several other factors, outside the station, may influence the utilization of the station capacity; however 
these factors are not considered when evaluating the station. These factors are the railway infrastructure, 
the stability of the timetable, and external factors. The following gives a brief description on how these 
factors may impact the utilization of the station capacity. 
 
6.6.1 Railway infrastructure 
The railway infrastructure and how it is utilized is greatly linked with the available capacity. On a single-
track line, operations between two stations can only occur in one direction at a time. Dispatching of routes 
must be done carefully to avoid deadlocks; leaving the system locked where no train is able to move 
forward. An example is illustrated in the figure below: 
 
Figure 6.6: Capacity on a single line [31, p. 89] 
 
If train no. 1 continues to Station B and train no. 4 travels to Station C, it will leave the system locked, and 
no train will be able to continue. In such a case, at least one train must go in reverse to unlock the system. 
During the deadlock of the system, the capacity consumption of Station B and C is fully used. 
Breakdown of the infrastructure may affect the utilization of the station capacity. Wear of the 
infrastructure can lead to reduction in speed on part of the alignment. Figure 6.7 illustrates this principle, 
where the solid lines indicate the planned timetable, and the dotted line is the contingency operations due 
to reduction in speed between Station A and B.  
==PAGE:68==
 
 
57 
 
 
Figure 6.7: Lack of capacity due to speed reduction [31, p. 103] 
 
A result of the reduction in speed is an increased utilization of the station capacity as the layover time 
progresses (length of the vertical line increases), cf. figure 6.7. 
Another aspect related to the railway infrastructure, is the possibility for simultaneous movement of two 
trains in opposite directions. On a single-track line, the location of a crossing station is a key issue, in terms 
of reducing the layover time at stations.  
 
6.6.2 Stability of the timetable 
The robustness of a timetable is closely linked with the consumption of the capacity available. If a timetable 
is planned with little buffer time, and the timetable fully utilizes the capacity, the stability of the timetable 
decreases, due to the inability to recover from a delay. The effect from this scenario would propagate 
throughout the network, causing the stability of the timetable to decrease, cf. figure 6.7. On the other 
hand, including large buffer times in the timetable may prevent delays throughout the network, however 
each train takes up more of the capacity, allowing fewer trains to operate the line.  
Figure 6.7, illustrates how a planned timetable may increase the capacity consumption at a station if a 
crossing train is scheduled to depart shortly after an arriving train has arrived at the station. In this case, 
the arriving train is initially delayed at Station A, causing the crossing train to be delayed at Station B where 
it utilizes more of the station capacity.  
Other factors that affect the capacity utilization is the heterogeneity of trains and how they are scheduled 
to operate. Figure 6.8 illustrates a timetable with heterogeneous operations on a single-track line. The train 
running from Station A toward Station C (red line) is scheduled with long dwell time at Station B, as the two 
crossing trains (blue and green line) need to pass the station before the train can continue towards Station 
C. 
Due to the schedule of the heterogeneous operations, the train towards Station C takes up part of the 
station capacity for a period of time. If the two crossing trains were scheduled to travel at the same speed, 
the dwell time at Station B would decrease resulting in less capacity consumption of the station. 
 
 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
58 
 
 
Figure 6.8: Timetable for a single-track line with heterogeneous operations [31, p. 86] 
 
6.6.3 External factors 
External factors, such as weather conditions or persons on the tracks (e.g. due to an accident or suicide), 
may lead to reduction in speed or total stop in operations. External factors are difficult to predict and 
thereby difficult to include in the planning of a timetable. In such a case, a plan for contingency operations 
should apply.  
 
6.6.4 Conclusion 
Evaluation of the method used to determine the station capacity illustrates how some factors, outside the 
station, may cause the capacity consumption at the station to increase. However, these factors are not 
considered when evaluating the station capacity, as the method looks at the station in isolation and 
considers only the ideal conditions based on the proposed timetable.  
A key issue is the stability of the timetable, in particular on a single-track line, as a delay can spread in both 
directions throughout the network. A timetable should therefore include buffer time to allow the possibility 
to catch up from small delays, without affecting the network.  
Other factors, such as wear of the tracks, weather conditions and persons on the tracks may lead to 
extended capacity consumption at a station. The maintenance of the tracks is therefore of high importance, 
to avoid its impact on the capacity. The weather conditions and persons on the track are factors which are 
difficult to control, and in such cases, a contingency plan should apply.

7 – CONCLUSION 
This thesis analysed three horizontal alignments which deviates from the existing Holstebro – Vejle railway 
line, at approximately 1.5 km west of the Jelling Station. Prior to the BLL Airport, the alignments join and 
the geometry of the remaining stretch are similar for all proposals. 
The land between Jelling and Billund is rich in terms of its nature and its natural environment, and include 
large areas of forest reserve, several small lakes, creeks and wetlands, and a number of ancient 
monuments. The land is rural with few houses spread throughout the area.  
All alignments are designed not to conflict with the protection zones for ancient monuments. The forest 
reserve result in a horizontal alignment with a number of small curves and low speed limits, if the forest 
areas are to be avoided. To increase the speed limit, the alignments conflicts with forest reserve which 
requires a dispensation from the Danish Nature Agency. 
At approximately 10.5 km from the turn-out of the existing railway line, the Billund-line cross the Vandel 
creek. The Vandel creek is surrounded by hilly terrain towards the north, which indicate the need for a 
bridge structure to cross the creek, for the north and the central proposal. Towards the south, the 
topography provides a smoother terrain profile, and a bridge structure is assessed not to be needed. 
Directing the alignment south of the hilly terrain increase the length of the alignment and the travel time of 
the train. 
All alignments conflict with existing properties, with the south alignment having the highest score of 33 
conflicts. The north alignment conflicts with 7 buildings, whereas the central proposal requires for 14 
buildings to be demolished. 
Along the existing railway, several roads cross in level. Increasing the number of operations impacts road 
traffic as the traffic stops when a train passes by. The road network between Jelling and Billund are 
generally with little traffic. The majority of these roads are recommended to be closed, to limit the amount 
of crossings. The Ballevej and the Lufthavnsvej are two main roads which are recommended to cross out of 
level to limit the impact on the road traffic.  
At the BLL Airport, the railway line conflicts with the parking area, number of internal roads and the 
landside traffic terminal. Rearranging the area is necessary to ensure the safety of all users. To ensure the 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
60 
 
railway line does not conflict with the airspace protection, the railway runs on the west side of Båstlundvej 
at where it conflicts with two parking zones and an area with runway light. As it turns into Nordmarksvej, 
the railway runs along the north side of the road, where it crosses a number of access roads. The Rail Net 
Denmark have chosen to cross the Båstlundvej in level, due to financial issues. Crossings in level provides a 
safety risk, and road safety audits must be carried out to ensure the safety of all users. 
All alignments are designed with large horizontal curves. Applying cant to the horizontal curves allows the 
train to travel through with nearly zero centrifugal acceleration acting on the train, as the ideal cant is 
achieved for the north and central alignments, when the design speed is 120 km/h and 160 km/h. The ideal 
cant is achieved for the south alignment when the speed is 120 km/h, whereas a cant deficiency of nearly 
50 mm apply when the speed increases to 160 km/h. The cant deficiency results in the lateral centrifugal 
acceleration acting outwards, causing a greater wear of the outer rail and wheel flanges. 
 
Along the open line, transition curves are designed with a length of 200 m, which results in a rate of change 
in cant as function of time (dh/dt) of nearby 14 mm/s for the curve with the greatest cant of the north and 
central alignments when the speed is 120 km/h. The south alignment results in a maximum value of 19 
mm/s. Increasing the speed to 160 km/h results in a dh/dt of approximately 33 mm/s for all alignments, as 
a maximum cant of 150 mm is applied to the circular curves. Increase of the dh/dt result in decrease of 
passenger comfort.  
The large horizontal curves requires a change in cant if the speed limit is increased from 120 km/h to 160 
km/h. The size of the curve radius does not change. Therefore, the change in speed has little effect on the 
cost. The construction cost is estimated to be nearly DKK 878 million for the south alignment, DKK 913 
million for the central alignment and DKK 886 million for the north alignment. 
Based on the assessments, the north alignment is recommended with a top speed of 160 km/h, as the 
north alignment provides the shortest travel time. Considering the political aim to increase the use of 
public transport, and the low ridership today, due to extended travel time using public transport, the travel 
time is assessed to be a main factor.  
The operational plan recommended 30-minute services for the Billund-line with departures from Vejle 
Station at each hourly minutes of 26 and 56, whereas departures from Billund Station are scheduled at 
each 09 and 39 minutes. The travel time between Vejle and Billund corresponds to 26 minutes, whereas 29 
minutes is needed in the opposite direction due to stops at the crossing stations. The graphical timetable 
indicated the need of a crossing station at the km 13 of the new railway stretch.  
The departure times from Billund Station is chosen to allow crossing of trains to occur on a straight part of 
the horizontal and vertical alignment, to fulfil the requirements of the Track Engineering rules. 
Furthermore, it allows for the crossings to take place at a location outside of the bridge structures. 
Scheduling of new operations showed the need to include the crossing station at Grejsdal, and the need to 
cancel two existing operations during the peak hour. Several trains are scheduled to depart at Vejle, 
Grejsdal and Jelling Stations, shortly after the arrival of a crossing train. The tight timetable encourages the 
risk of delays spreading throughout the network. To strengthen the robustness of the timetable, it may be 
considered to upgrade the existing track to a double track line. 
Analysis of the capacity at Jelling Station indicated the existing layout have sufficient number of platform 
tracks, based on the proposed timetable. During the peak hour the station capacity is fully utilized during 6 
minutes, with available capacity through the remaining hour. Majority of operations are scheduled to cross 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
61 
 
a train traveling in the opposite direction at Jelling Station. If a train stops at the station for a greater 
period, e.g. due to technical problems, it will have an effect on the existing network, as two trains are no 
longer able to cross. The recommendation of keeping the existing track layout is based on the assumption 
that the trains are able to operate according to the schedule.

APPENDIX J: Cost estimation 
The cost is estimated based on the outline of the alignments, and includes several uncertainties, due to the 
preliminary stage. The following provides a description of the assumption made to estimate the cost of the 
three alignment proposals, which is followed by the cost estimation of each alignment.  
The cost of the railway structure is based on a unit price of DKK 37 mill. per km track, which were used to 
estimate the cost at the project “Ny bane Århus – Galten – Silkeborg” [36, p. 19]. The unit price is based on 
a single track line in rural area with some crossings. It includes an additional 50 % due to the early stage of 
the design phase. Hence, the unit price per km track is DKK 24.4 mill. without the safety margin. 
The unit price of the bridge structure crossing the Vandel creek is based on [5, p. 18], which estimate a cost 
of DKK 120 mill. for a bridge crossing the Vandel creek, which includes an additional 50 % due to the early 
stage of the design phase. The cost of DKK 20 mill. is assumed for each of the bridges crossing the Ballevej 
and the Lufthavnsvej. 
Expropriation of land is calculated as the length of the alignment times a width of 20 meters. An additional 
area, corresponding to twice the size of the forest reserve area affected by the railway, is included, 
assuming the Danish Nature Agency requires a replacement of the forest reserve. The Nature Agency often 
add a factor 2 to the replacement area. An average cost of the land, is estimated to DKK 150,000 per ha., 
based on the average agriculture land in Denmark were sold at DKK ≈132,000 per ha. in 2014 [37].  The cost 
is rounded to DKK 150,000, as not all land is agriculture. 
The expropriation of properties are counted as the properties places within a distance of 20 meters from 
the CL. The average value of the properties is assumed to be DKK 2 mill.
