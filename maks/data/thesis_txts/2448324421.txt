Summary
Robotic exploration in an unknown environment is still a very much open ended problem. Clas-
sical approaches are computationally costly and cannot take advantage of common features in
the environments to make intelligent decisions. Deep reinforcement learning has recently made
some incredible achievements however its use for robotic mapping is still not clear. As such,
this paper deals with the eﬃcacy of using deep reinforcement learning in the context of robotic
exploration. It is assumed that the agent has access to perfect pose knowledge however it is
equipped with noisy sensor and must eﬃciently explore the unknown environment. The map
used in this study is represented by an occupancy map and the performance metric is the
reduction of the map’s entropy at each step. A classical approach is chosen from literature
and compared to deep reinforcement learning algorithms developed in this paper. The devel-
oped deep reinforcement learning algorithm outperforms the classical approach in a controlled
environment setting but the scalability to a real world setting still needs to be researched.

Introduction
The Introduction chapter motivates the choice of topic and deﬁnes the problem statement.
1.1
Motivation
We have watched it on ﬁlms, read about it in books and day dreamed about it. The future
in which robots are an integral part of our daily lives is approaching. The main driving forces
behind what is being referred to as Industry 4.0 are: the decrease in computing units; massive
investment into emerging technologies and tech start ups; clouding computing accessibility; big
data and the rise of the Internet of Things. Industry 4.0 as a whole does not only represent the
revolution to manufacturing but also to in-house and service robotics. Already there are chef
robots, service robots and even a robot which can clean your room [7].
Robotic arms have long been used to automate manufacturing but these are often heavy and
a danger to humans. An area of soft robotics deals with safe robot human interaction to allow
robots to work alongside humans, for example, Cobots. Until around ten years ago drones
were just a word you would associate with the military but presently they have become heavily
commercialized for companies and consumers. Drones can inspect power lines, deliver products
to consumer’s homes but most notably can be used in search and rescue applications [17].
A key diﬀerence between robotic applications of the past and of the present/future is that of
leveraging big data and machine learning. In the past, robots were programmed and controlled
on the assumption that the dynamics are known or well approximated. However, through the
use of big data and machine learning it is possible to produce complicated models that better
represent the observations in the data. It is most noticeable in the computer vision area. Clas-
sically computer vision consisted of ﬁnding and applying the right ﬁlters to an image and then
based on those features a program could classify it. This changed in 2012 when a convolutional
neural network (CNN) known as AlexNet [32] won the ImageNet challenge. CNNs can learn
useful feature maps of an image based on the data provided through minimizing the error by
backpropogation. The breakthroughs in computer vision allow for robotic agents to increase
their perception and understanding of their surrounding environment and thus to increase their
autonomy.
One of the more challenging aspects of autonomous robotics is that of navigating in unknown
environments under uncertainty. In literature, this is commonly referred to as the ability of
an agent to map an unknown environment and simultaneously localise itself within that map,
or Simultaneous Localisation and Mapping (SLAM). SLAM can be thought of as two parts
namely, the front-end and back-end. The front-end abstracts sensor data into models that are
amenable for estimation, while the back-end performs inference on the abstracted data pro-
duced by the front-end. The front-end also performs short and long term data association.
Short term being temporary feature tracking and long term being loops closure. Depending on
the sensor type i.e. RGB, RGB-D or Laser, diﬀerent front-end approaches can be taken. The
back-end is often a set of algorithms for data inference. SLAM is well researched and there are

open source packages available in the robotics operating software (ROS), such as Gmapping or
Cartographer. However, a separate but related ﬁeld is that of autonomous mapping.
An agent who performs autonomous mapping must not only use SLAM to understand its
environment and localise within it but must also take actions which explore unknown areas.
The use of occupancy maps as part of the back-end of the SLAM system is quite common in
the ﬁeld of autonomous mapping. Occupancy maps can not only represent free and occupied
space but also unknown or unobserved space. Occupancy maps suﬀer from the issue of spatial
accuracy as they discretize the environmental space. The spatial resolution can be increased
at the cost of an increased amount of memory needed to store the map. Methods exist which
use diﬀerent layers of resolution to decrease map memory usage [24]. There are other types of
map representations [57] but occupancy maps are the most researched type as they are easy to
visualize and understand, and they are represented as a distribution.
Since the free, occupied and unknown cells in occupancy maps are represented by a binary
distribution, probabilistic approaches can be used. Information gain is a probabilistic approach
which seeks to reduce the entropy of the map by choosing cells that have a high expected in-
formation gain or entropy reduction on the map. A classical approach, called Frontier Search,
attempts to ﬁnd the border points between free and unknown space. It groups these borders or
frontiers and navigates to the closest one. The algorithm is simple as it is only a search through
occupancy cells with a set of rules to determine frontiers however, it often is outperformed by
the more computationally expensive information gain approaches [48].
An interesting perspective, especially with 2D occupancy grids, is that it is just an image
in which an agent moves from pixel to pixel. CNNs excel at operating on images, whether they
be classiﬁcation or object detection/localisation. However, in order to train such networks the
amount of data needed is signiﬁcant and in this case the high information gain cells would have
to be labeled in order to use supervised learning. If the goal was just to detect these cells this
would be possible but robotic mapping is concerned with outputting actions which decrease the
map entropy. As such the training data would rather need to be labelled with the actions that
decrease the map entropy. Learning from expert behaviour is the area of imitation learning.
Obtaining expert behaviour is often done by humans and thus is not only costly but also is
subject to human error.
Reinforcement learning (RL) on the other hand interacts with its environment and on the
basis of the received rewards attempts to improve its policy such that higher and higher re-
wards are received. The theoretical foundation of RL is mostly shown in a simplistic tabular
sense but incorporating deep learning 1 models is diﬃcult. The revival of deep RL came about
in 2013 when DeepMind published a paper known as DQN [36] in which an agent learnt to play
Atari games directly from the pixel input. Presently, through a number of insights and hacks it
is possible to train deep networks with RL. However, the convergence and stability are known
to be brittle at best.
In the context of robotic mapping using 2D occupancy grids, it would be possible for a hu-

1.2
Problem Deﬁnition
The goal of this thesis is to explore the eﬃcacy of using deep reinforcement learning compared
to a classical approach in the context of robotic mapping. The chosen classical robotic explo-
ration algorithm will be based on the information gain approach outlined in Robotic Mapping
and Exploration by Cyrill Stachniss [48]. The motivation for this choice is discussed in the
Background section. The basis of comparison will be both the metric reward based perfor-
mance as well as the computational performance.
It will be assumed that the agent has access to perfect pose knowledge however it is equipped
with noisy sensor and must eﬃciently explore using an occupancy map whilst decreasing the
map’s entropy.
As such the structure of the thesis is as follows:
• The background to classical robotic mapping is ﬁrst shown and the chosen information
gain baseline developed. The basics of RL are outlined so as to have a foundation for the
recent deep RL papers presented. Related work in the area of deep RL in exploration
and mapping is shown.
• Preliminary theory needed to understand the algorithms used in the thesis will be outlined.
• The training environment will be described and information gain baseline further devel-
oped.
• An initial design choice will be presented and investigated.
• Improvements to the original idea design will be made.
• The relative computational time complexity of the developed RL agent design and the
information gain baseline will be investigated.
• Finally conclusions will be drawn and recommendations made for future work.

Background
Firstly, the classical approaches to robotic mapping using occupancy grids will be presented
then an approach is chosen and justiﬁed. Finally, the basic foundation theory of RL is shown
so as to understand the following RL papers dealing with exploration.
2.1
Classical Robotic Exploration
Map representation is an important tool in the robotic mapping ﬁeld. Generally there are
two types of map representations being topological and metric. In the topological setup the
position of landmarks is estimated and by fusing sensors, an estimate of the robot pose and
landmark position can be deduced and thus it is represented as a graph. Metric representations
seek to represent the environment in a more detailed, spatially accurate way. Common metric
map representations are free space maps (Occupancy Maps [57]), object maps, and composite
maps [58]. Occupancy maps memory consumption can be reduced through hierarchical layers
of resolution [24]. Classical occupancy mapping uses an inverse sensor model which suﬀers
from inaccurate maps as each cell is estimated independently based on high dimensional data.
It is possible to use a forward sensor model, which produces a map through maximising the
distribution of the measurements given a map. A classic way do so is through expectation
maximization [56]. Robotic exploration concerns how an agent best explores its environment
based on the map provided by the SLAM system.
Frontier search and exploration is an algorithm that based on the current map, clusters the cells
which are on the border between free and unknown space, sorts the clusters based on distance
and attempts to navigate to the closest cluster. The original paper [65] uses two sweeps of
the breadth ﬁrst search, one for identifying the frontier cells and another for clustering. Wave
frontier search [59] also uses two nested breath ﬁrst searches but only traverses free cells not
the entire grid. Pereto frontier search [63] utilizes multi optimization techniques to solve the
frontier search algorithm. Artiﬁcial potential ﬁelds are a technique for path planning and by
modifying the attractor function the technique can be used for frontier exploration [60].
The original frontier search [65] algorithm was the pioneer for robotic exploration however
it assumes perfect pose knowledge. Information gain techniques seek to ﬁnd the goal or action
which not only decreases the map’s uncertainty but can also incorporate reducing the robot’s
pose uncertainty into the utility function. In [11] they use an entropy based utility function
with which to optimize and perform autonomous exploration. However, if the robot’s pose
uncertainty is high the updating of the occupancy map may clear a number of cells which are
actually occupied. As such they incorporated the robot’s pose uncertainty into the utility func-
tion [15]. The measure of uncertainty for the map and pose is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent therefore
a hyper parameter was used to linearly weight the pose uncertainty accordingly. In general the
Shannon entropy is the basis for the utility functions but in [13] they incorporate Renyi entropy
for the robot’s pose. In [48] they concisely outline how the basic information gain technique
outperforms the classical frontier search and as such the information gain approach will be the
baseline for comparison. They outline how to calculate the expected information gain for each
5
==PAGE:12==

cell in the set of reachable cells. The cell with the highest expected information gain is the
optimal cell to perform a sensor reading. The issue with the optimization technique used to
ﬁnd the optimal cell is that of high computational cost as to calculate the expected information
gain for each cell the sensor model needs to be simulated in the current occupancy map, the
details are explained in the Preliminary Theory section. It also does not take into account
the layout of the map; for example navigating down a corridor may lead to a local optima in
terms of entropy reduction as the agent must then return the same way it came. The high
computational cost and inability to reason about the map’s layout properties motivates the
choice of rather learning features of the occupancy map to decide on actions that decrease the
map’s entropy. As mentioned in the Introduction section CNNs excel at learning useful spatial
features of the input and have a ﬁxed computational time based on the depth.
2.2
Reinforcement Learning

making whilst interacting with an environment [49]. At each time step the agent receives the

If the MDP is known, meaning the state transition and reward function is known, then Dynamic
Programming can be used to perform policy evaluation and improvement. If the MDP is not
known the value functions Vπ and Qπ can be estimated from experience. For example if an
agent follows the policy π and records the average of the returns from each state, this average
will approach the Vπ(s) as the encounters of the state go to inﬁnity. Similarly, an average can
be maintained of the return from taking action a at state s. Estimating the value functions
through experience is the basis of Monte Carlo methods. The Bellman equation of the value
function expresses the value of state from its successors. The idea of backing up state value
information is the corner stone of reinforcement learning.
2.2.1
Q-Learning
Once the action-value function is estimated the optimal policy would be just one which chooses
the action which results in the largest return, which is the basis of Q-learning. Another way to
represent the policy is by parameterizing it which is the class of policy gradient methods. The
methods use in this thesis belong to policy gradient methods and are detailed in the Preliminary
Theory section.
When the state space is large it is intractable to maintain an average of the value functions
and thus function approximators are used to categorize the function with much fewer param-
eters than there are states. Neural networks are a very common function approximators and
deep networks are able to represent functions extremely well. However, a notorious problem
with using deep models as function approximators in RL is that the learning behaviour can
be unstable as the collected data at each step is highly correlated. The inspiration behind the
famous DQN [36] are two ideas which attempt to de-correlate the data so that the training
is stable. They use experience replay which stores the past experience and randomly samples
from it when performing updates. They also use a ﬁx target network, meaning the q value
function approximator is only updated in set intervals. This allows the policy to not prema-
turely converge to a sub optimal policy. Many hacks and implementations thus far in the area
of deep reinforcement learning is that of circumventing these issues of de-correlating the data
as well as suﬃcient exploration.
DQN often over estimated the value of an action thus it was proposed to use a double es-
timator method to estimate the maximum value of a set of stochastic values [22] in a tabular
setting and was later extended to deep double Q-network [61]. Experience replay is a powerful
biologically inspired technique which was improved to prioritize the experiences based on their
performance/reward, deemed prioritized reply [44].
2.2.2
Policy Gradient
The policy gradient class of RL algorithms do not consult a value function to decide which
action to choose, as in the case of Q-learning, but rather the policy parameterizes the action
space based on the state [49]. The classic policy gradient algorithm, REINFORCE [62], uses
the full episode to update the policy and thus is a Monte Carlo algorithm. Often a baseline is

used in order to reduce the variance of gradient estimation while keeping the bias unchanged.
A common baseline is the advantage which is the action-value subtracted by the state value.
Temporal diﬀerence, TD(0) [55], learning updates is the basis for the Q-learning and SARSA
algorithms [49]. It can be extended to TD(λ) which uses eligibility traces [49]. Actor-Critic
networks refer to a class of algorithms which use n-step TD learning and bootstrapping on
the output of the critic [31]. A critic refers to a network which outputs the expected value of
the current state. An advantage that eligibility traces have over n-step returns is that only a
single trace vector is required rather than a store of the last n feature vectors. An extension
to eligibility traces and the advantage baseline is that of the Generalized Advantage Estimator
[46]. One of the issues with training deep network via RL is that of brittle convergence due
to the data being highly correlated, experience reply is a method to deal with this, recently it
was shown that running diﬀerent actor-critic networks on the environment and asynchronously
updating the weights led to good convergence properties and quickly, A3C [34]. It was later
shown that it was the diversity of data not the asynchronous part which lead to a synchronous
update version, A2C.
Vanilla policy gradient methods have two major limitations, that of step size and sample ef-
ﬁciency. As the input data is non stationary, too large a step can cause the next batch of
data input to be collected under a bad policy and so the policy cannot recover. One way to
reduce sample size and ensure the policy does not deviate too much iteration is through the
use of natural gradient methods [27]. Natural gradients follow the steepest descent direction
that uses the Fisher metric as the underlying metric. However, computing the natural gradient
is intractable as it requires inverting the Fisher matrix. The trust region policy optimization
method [45] does not store and invert the Fisher matrix but rather uses the Fisher-vector prod-
ucts instead. The Kronecker-factored approximated curvature is a scalable approximation to
natural gradient [64]. The proximal policy optimization method [47] rather imposes the change
in Kullback–Leibler divergence as a soft constraint in the objective function.
2.2.3
Issues of RL
A major bottleneck in the world of RL is that of simulators to test or train agents on. Trans-
ferring a simulation trained agent to the real world is itself a research topic. One method is to
augment simulation data with real world data [49]. Another method is train in simulation and
perform transfer learning in the real world [28][67][26][38]. Training a agent end-to-end with a
CNN for feature extraction in simulation is a diﬃcult task due to the visual rendering/ depth
data in the simulator not being able to represent the real world suﬃciently. It is possible to
train a deep RL agent in a simpler simulator which can vary the lighting, textures etc. without
needing transfer learning to operate in the real world [43]. Another appealing aspect of training
in simulation is that of bench marking RL algorithms, a common environment benchmark is
that of OpenAI Gym’s environments [12].
Policy exploration is a major issue in reinforcement learning and is often referred to as the
exploitation vs exploration dilemma [49]. Policy exploration can be divided into two often in-
tegrated forms, that of parameter vs action perturbation. Action perturbation is performed by
modelling the output as a stochastic policy, such as soft max, Gaussian etc, and sampling from

it. Parameter perturbation is performed by altering the parameters of the model to produce
diﬀerent actions [42][16]. Evolutionary [37] approaches can be used to train RL agents but are
sample ineﬃcient and do not scale to large networks. Hybrids of evolutionary and standard
gradient approaches can trade oﬀthe beneﬁts of diverse training with sample eﬃcient gradi-
ent methods [29]. Exploration is key to ﬁnding good policies and for not allowing agents to
prematurely convergence to local optima. Architectures have been proposed to combat this
exploration issue such as, count-based exploration [54], intrinsic motivation [9] and intrinsic

2.2.4
Reinforcement learning in the context of robotic mapping
In [33] they train an agent to navigate to a goal location in 3D space and show that naviga-
tional abilities emerge as the by-product of an agent learning a policy that maximizes reward.
They augment the navigation task with auxiliary depth prediction and loop closure classiﬁca-

the training convergence properties. The policy was trained directly on pixels and thus was

In [51] they train a deep Q-network from pixels to navigate to a goal in an environment with

explores the highest depth node ﬁrst before back tracking up its search path.

3.3.2
Convolutional Neural Network
The description of what a convolutional neural network is in the name. It is a network which
applies a convolution to the input instead of matrix multiplication. The motivation is based
on the mathematical convolutional operator, please refer to [18] for an in depth motivation.
In general CNNs refer to dealing with images. However, the principles can be applied to time
series data, graphs etc. The image fed into the input is considered as volumetric, meaning the
diﬀerent channels of the image such as the RGB value of the 2D tensor form a volumetric tensor.
The network applies a kernal to extract features, it takes a square patch of the volumetric
input tensor and then follows the same logic as described before, whereby weights are multi-
plied by the pixels’ intensities, summed, put through an activation function to produce a single
output value. The square patch is moved in the image, which is determined by the stride, and
the process is repeated until the whole image is covered. This process will produce an "image"
of features from that kernal. The above process is then repeated to produce a certain number
of channels from diﬀerent valued kernals, please study ﬁgure 3.10 to understand the process
better. Max pooling is a technique which again applies a max pooling square patch operation
over the image which will decrease the dimensions of the image. The ﬁnal feature maps are
ﬂattened and then parsed through fully connected layers to reach the ﬁnal output.

error function is backwardly propagated through the network to update the weights. Gradient
descent minimizes an objective function by updating the parameters in the opposite direction

It performs one update to the parameters for the whole data set. It can be very slow and often
the data set will be too large for the memory capacity. Batch gradient descent is guaranteed
to converge to the global minimum for convex error surfaces and to a local minimum for non-
convex surfaces.
Stochastic Gradient Descent
θ = θ −η · ▽θJ(θ; xi, yi)
(30)
It performs an update for each entry in the data set. It makes the updates have high variance
but can be used in an online fashion.
Mini-batch Gradient Descent
θ = θ −η · ▽θJ(θ; xi:i+n, yi:i+n)
(31)
It merges together the two concepts above. It allows the variance of the updates to be reduced
but makes perform the update tractable.
In the supervised training setting the concepts above are important but for RL the size of
the batch of sampled data for each update is small so normal batch gradient descent is used.
Choosing the right learning rate, η, parameter is diﬃcult because too small a value can lead
to extremely slow convergence and too high a value can hinder convergence. In the setting of
RL the learning rate is even more challenging as, in the case of online learning, too large a step
along the gradient can result in a policy which can never recover from the bad update.

Environment
As mentioned in the literature review, due to the number of samples needed, it is often easier
to train an agent in simulation and transfer it to the real world. Currently, there is no open
source realistic simulator speciﬁcally designed for training RL agents. A number of companies
are developing simulators with the goal of training deep RL agents, especially for autonomous
cars. OpenAI’s gym packages provide the basis for bench marking Rl algorithms. However,
there are no robotic navigation environments as this would require a realistic simulator. Gazebo
is popular and well known simulator which started as a ROS package but now is a standalone
simulator. In [66] they extend the ROS interface for Gazebo into an OpenAI gym style envi-
ronment. They implement Q-learning on a Turtlebot with lidar data. The issues with such a
setup is that of customizable maps, robot types, reset fails, running headless on a GPU cluster
and that of simulation time [41]. As such an OpenAI gym style environment will be designed
with the following criteria:
• 2D grid environment
• Observation shall be the current occupancy map and location of agent within it
• Reward shall be related to the decrease in the occupancy maps’ cumulative entropy
• The sensor will be a noisy laser range sensor
• The environment can be run in headless but has rendering capabilities
• Environment properties are map size dependant
First the theory and design of the environment will be presented. Following this the overview of
the environment is presented and the IG baseline’s hyperparameter governing distance travelled
to expected entropy reduction will be optimized. The two chosen map sizes to train and test
on are shown in table 1. All metrics are units.

Design
The initial design choices will be outlined here and the results will be shown, following this
section improvements will be made to the design. Throughout this section, please refer back to
the Preliminary Theory section for more details.
The two algorithms chosen to investigate will be the REINFORCE with the advantage baseline,
referred to as REINFORCE-ADV, and A2C. In general, it is not common to train deep agents
with Monte Carlo reinforcement learning algorithms such as REINFORCE [62]. The main rea-
son is that it is incredibly slow as well as very high in sample ineﬃciency. As a comparison to
how A2C performs the REINFORCE-ADV algorithm will be implemented. Normally, REIN-
FORCE algorithm is trained in a sequential fashion meaning it performs a certain number of
episodes sequentially and then updates the model based on the sequential batch of episodes.
However, the implementation of REINFORCE-ADV in this paper, in a similar fashion as A2C,
uses parallel processes to gather the batch of experiences as this reduces training run time.
Initially, only Map1 of size 25x25 will be considered.
5.1
Policy Architecture
As mentioned in both the Environment and Introduction sections the information that the RL
agent will receive is the state of the current occupancy map, st, and the location, xt within in
the map. Based on the state the diﬀerentiable policy πθ(at|st, xt) must output an action. The
goal is to model the policy as function of the input state through approximating with a deep
neural network. In order to use REINFORCE with state-dependant baseline and A2C, both
the policy function πθ(at|st, xt) and value function Vθv(st, xt) must be learnt.
The two diﬀerent types of neural network architectures used for feature extraction will be
the multilayer perceptron and convolutional neural network. In both cases the features will be
parsed to another set of fully connected layers to output the ﬁnal action/value, see ﬁgure 5.1.
The whole network will be trained end to end. The output of the last action layer will be put
through a Softmax function and sampled to receive the action.
The location information will be represented by a one-hot vector and will be joined with the
ﬂattened features in the last fully connect layers as shown in ﬁgure 5.1. For example the x
coordinate location vector will be a vector of zeros of the length of the x dimension with a
single one presenting the position, the same is for the y position. Representing the coordinates
in this way was inspired by the A3C paper [34].
The value function will be regressed and the policy function will be trained using the pol-
icy gradient with the advantage estimate, please refer to the Preliminary Theory section. A
property to both algorithms, REINFORCE with baseline and A2C, is that of the shared pa-
rameters for the policy and value network. It is very common to share weights across both
networks as this is known to help training but it can also degrade performance if not carefully
implemented. As such a number of diﬀerent policy architectures will be investigated, please

Design Discussion
When looking at the training results the one CNN architecture that performs exceptionally
worse than the others is CNN0, please refer to the appendix for the architecture layout. The
major diﬀerence in architecture is that the ﬁrst convolutional layer reduces the dimentionality
of the input through the value of the stride and padding. As the input is the occupancy map
as an "image" by reducing the size of the image means that some spatial information is lost
and so the following layers cannot extract meaningful representations of the relative location
of obstacles etc. This will in turn not allow the agent to conﬁdently know whether or not an
action will cause a collision with an obstacle and thus end the episode.
CNN architectures 1 and 3 are designed so as to not reduce the size of the input with CNN3
having more layers. CNN architectures 2, 4 and 5 are designed such that the ﬁrst layer does
not reduce the size but subsequent layers do with CNN5 having the most layers. The top per-
forming architecture for both REINFORCE-ADV and A2C is CNN3 with CNN4 being second.
When the ﬁrst layer does not reduce the size of the input it is able to learn useful low level
spatial features with the following layers extracting higher level features based on the spatial
ones.

Separating the policy function πθ(at|st, xt) and value function Vθv(st, xt) into two neural net-

make the network’s memory requirement intractable on some GPUs.
Including the entropy map as an additional channel in the occupancy "image" input does show a
relatively good increase in performance (≈3%) on both training and testing except for the MLP
architectures. Since the reward is based on the reduction in entropy including the entropy map
as an input could possibly make the learning of features that not only avoid obstacles but also
navigate to areas of high entropy easier to learn. Including the entropy map could be thought
of as including an approximation to the reward map as part of the input. The most likely
reason as to why it does not relatively help the MLP architecture is again due to the inability
for an MLP to associate the entropy map with the occupancy map in spatial features based way.

forms that of the A2C cases, except that of CNN3. It is well known that introducing a value
estimation function reduces the variance of the policy gradient at a cost of increasing bias.
Even though high variance needs more samples, bias can be more of an issue as bias can cause
the algorithm to fail to converge or to converge to a solution which is not even a local optima.
Even further in the case of the A2C algorithm the number of n-steps again introduces more
bias as we only consider bootstrapping from the n-step return. The hyperparameters were ﬁne
tuned as best as possible. However, ﬁnding hyperparameters is diﬃcult especially the n-step
hyperparameter in A2C.
The reduced average reward and increase of the variance is expected when going from train-
ing to testing. The results of the trained models is dismal and can not even be compared to
that of the IG baseline which signiﬁcantly outperforms all models. Even with including the
entropy map, altering the feature extraction architecture and ﬁne tuning the hyperparameters,
the models are not even comparable with the baseline. The average number of steps in the test
results, ﬁgure 5, provides an insight as to the fundamental problem from which the algorithms
suﬀer. All models are not even coming close to running for the maximum episode step length
of 300 and are "crashing" before having explored the map enough. This indicates that the
models are unable to properly know the relative distances between the agent and the obstacles
and thus the fundamental problem must be to do with how the location of the agent in the
occupancy map is inputted into the general architecture model, shown in ﬁgure 5.1. Intuitively
the issue is that the feature extraction part attempts to extract features that are not based on
the location of the agent in the occupancy map but rather just on the occupancy map. The
location of the agent is joined to the ﬂattened feature vector from the feature extracting part
and parsed into an MLP to receive the ﬁnal action/value. So it is left up to the ﬁnal MLP part
to try integrate the one-hot location vector with that of the feature vector. This would result
in it not knowing if there is an obstacle next to the agent’s location that it must avoid. As such
a diﬀerent technique for inputting the location of the agent within the occupancy map must be

Improved Design
The improved design is outlined here and the ﬁnal results will be shown. Additional theory to
the Preliminary Section needed to understand the improvement to the two algorithms, A2C
and REINFORCE-ADV, is shown. Previously only the 25x25 map was considered however in
this section the best performing model on the 25x25 map will also be trained on the 100x100
map to analyse scalability.
The ﬁrst fundamental issue of inputting the agent’s location needs to be addressed. The issue
with using a one-hot vector as the location input is outlined in the previous section. In order
for the feature extraction part to learn features that are based on the location of the agent
alongside the occupancy map, the location of the agent will be an empty 2D image with a 2D
approximate discretized Gaussian centered on the location of the agent in the occupancy map.
The location image input in ﬁgure 6.1 is an example of this. The use of a 2D Gaussian not only
helps the convolutional layer keep information about the location of the agent but also if the
project needed to be extended to when the pose is uncertain this could be a method to take
into account pose uncertainty.
A well known method in reinforcement learning to reduce the bias introduced by a value es-
timator function is that of the λ-return. It essentially allows the mixing of diﬀerent n-step
returns in the policy gradient update. The mixing of the diﬀerent n-step returns is governed
by the parameters λ which implies a trade-oﬀbetween more bias (low lambda) and more vari-
ance (high lambda). In the case of using the advantage function in the policy gradient update,
a paper called the Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) which is based oﬀthe λ-return
from Sutton and Barto [49] will be implemented. The REINFORCE-ADV and A2C which
incorporates the GAE technique will be referred to as REINFORCE-ADV-GAE and A2C-GAE
respectively.
In the last design section it was shown how using deeper and deeper models increases per-
formance but to a point. Choosing the correct number of layers in the CNN model that does
not degrade performance is diﬃcult. An interesting idea was presented in a paper called Resid-
ual Networks [23] which addresses the issue of vanishing gradients in an elegant way. It allows
the function to rather approximate the residual of the input which allows for better gradient
ﬂow and as such will be implemented.
As the MLP model for the feature extraction does not generalize well it will not be implemented.
The best performing model of the previous design, CNN3, is chosen to be implemented with
the improvements so as to have a concrete comparison. Since adding entropy of the map as an
additional input increased performance, it will included in the design without further investi-
gation. The policy and value function will share parameters as it was shown not to increase
performance enough to warrant two separate networks.
The policy architecture will be similar to that of the previous design section except with the
location of the agents being parsed into the feature extraction part as a 2D image. As such the
inputs will be the occupancy map, entropy map and location map, see ﬁgure 6.1.

Residual Networks
The ImageNet challenge was the starting point for the revitalization of convolutional neural
networks, the trend was to make the network deeper and deeper. The Residual Network paper
36
==PAGE:43==
Francis Hamilton, s160959
Figure 6.4: Residual learning: a building block [23]
[23] answers the question of whether learning a better network is as easy as stacking more
layers. They show that by naively increasing the depth, the accuracy becomes saturated and
then rapidly degrades. As such networks with added layers are not similarly easy to train. An
intuitive example of this occurring is provided in the paper. They propose a network and the
same network with added layers and that there exists a solution by construction to the deeper
model whereby the added layers are just the identity mapping. It means that the deeper model
in theory should not behave worse than its shallow counter part. However, their experiments
prove otherwise in which current solvers are unable to obtain solutions which are close to the
shallower counterpart. They refer to the above problems with deeper networks as the degrada-
tion problem and propose a deep residual learning framework.
Instead of learning the underlying mapping H(x), the stacked layers rather ﬁt another map-
ping F(x) := H(x) −x. As such the mapping now will be F(x) + x, which can be realized by
feedforward neural networks with connections around the CNN block, shown in ﬁgure 6.4. The
architecture of the Residual Network now is deﬁned by the number and setting of the building
blocks. The network can be trained in the same fashion as the feedforward networks.

Design Discussion
Including the location of the agent as an image fed in alongside the occupancy map signiﬁcantly
improved the performance (≈2−3 times), as can be seen by comparing the results of the CNN3
architectures from table 4 and 10. In terms of the CNN3 architecture the A2C algorithm out-
performed the REINFORCE-ADV algorithm by ≈50%. The reason being is that the Monte
Carlo policy gradient algorithm, REINFORCE-ADV, requires signiﬁcantly more samples than
that of A2C and so in the given limited max samples A2C will reach higher rewards faster, this
can be seen in ﬁgure 6.5. However, comparing the test results for the CNN3 architecture for
both algorithms, REINFORCE-ADV and A2C, shows how the IG baseline outperforms them
by 97% and 30% in average reward respectively.
Implementing the feature extraction model as a residual network signiﬁcantly improved the
results across both algorithms. The diﬀerence between all models and algorithms from testing
to training is expected. Although using the residual network model improved the results the
REINFORCE-ADV and A2C agent are still outperformed by the IG baseline albeit with 14%
and 17% relative lower variance.
The use of the generalized advantage estimate allows the A2C-ResNet0 network to not only
reach levels of performance with lower variance, 2.78, compared to the IG baseline, 4.97, but
to slightly outperform it in testing. Comparing ﬁgures 6.5 and 6.6 not only shows how using
the residual network architecture improves performance early on in training but how using the
GAE further improves upon that and also decreases the variance.
In terms of training on the 100x100 map only the CNN3 and ResNet0 architectures with
A2C-GAE were chosen to investigate the scalability. It is well known in deep RL that train-
ing on large images, even in supervised learning, is extremely diﬃcult. As can be seen from
ﬁgure 9 this was exactly the case whereby the agents were even outperformed by the 25x25
map agents. All attempts at training would only result with performance slightly above that
of random behaviour. The IG baseline perfectly scales to the larger map with just an increase
in variance. The models trained on the 100x100 map were not tested as it would not be a
comparable measure.

8
Conclusion
The goal of this study was to investigate the eﬃcacy of using deep reinforcement learning in
the context of robotic exploration relative to a well known classical approach. The information
gain approach was chosen from literature and further developed to be used in the designed
environment. Reinforcement learning algorithms from literature were researched and two semi-
related algorithms were chosen to be further developed.
As there are no well founded RL training environments that deal with robotic exploration,
an environment was developed in an OpenAI gym style. It was well suited as a basis to com-
pare the diﬀerent approaches however as it was very simple the trained deep RL agent would
not be able to be transferred to the real world. Currently there is no general purpose RL
training environment that is realistic enough to train RL agents only in the simulation.
The extension of integrating path planning into the IG approach from [48] as the baseline
algorithm used as a comparison was necessary. It ensured that the output of IG baseline algo-
rithm was navigable and computational tractable. It also allowed it to be compatible with the
designed environment. It only required one hyperparameter to be optimized. It performed as
expected and the motivation for it over other approaches was well founded.
The initial algorithm design performed poorly and was not even comparable to the IG baseline.
However, a number of a key insights necessary to further improve the design in the following
section were found. The main insight found was that of the representation of the agent’s loca-
tion as a 2D grid image rather than a one-hot vector. It allowed the RL algorithms to perform
relatively well compared to the IG baseline using a CNN model for feature extraction. Including
the entropy of the occupancy map as an input further increased the performance. The issues
of using a CNN model were outlined in the initial design and lead to the implementation of the
residual network model instead. The use of the residual network model for feature extraction
lead the performance to be on par with that of the IG baseline. Incorporating the GAE into
the algorithms further improved the performance.
The scalability of the design was investigated by training on a larger sized map set.
The
results of the training were inconclusive as the algorithm would not converge and was only
slightly better than random behaviour. Training deep networks with large image sized inputs
is not common in deep RL and is notoriously diﬃcult even in the supervised setting. Occu-
pancy maps need to be large to cover more of the environment with higher resolution thus just
increasing the image input size to the agent is not a feasible approach for using deep RL.
The computational time complexity of the developed RL design and the IG baseline were tested
and compared. The IG algorithm needs to essentially simulate what will happen by moving to
a particular cell in order to calculate the expected information gain. The computational cost
of this algorithm is very high and would be signiﬁcantly higher if the search window was made
larger. The computational cost of the deep RL agent was 12% that of the IG baseline for the
small map and 30% for the larger one. The increase in computational cost with increase input
size for the model is expected due to how convolutional layers work.

==PAGE:49==

Over all the results from the paper achieved the goals set forth in the problem deﬁnition. Using
deep reinforcement learning is a feasible approach to robotic exploration. The performance of
the developed algorithms was on par or even slightly better than that of the IG baseline. The
computational cost is signiﬁcantly lower compared to the IG baseline. However, the problem
of scalablity to real world applications remains.
Key contributions and conclusions:
• A bench marking environment for robotic exploration was developed.
• The use of residual networks results in increased performance compared to CNNs.
• Inputting the location of the agent as an image alongside the occupancy image allowed
richer features to be learnt.
• The deep RL agents are more computationally eﬃcient compared to the IG baseline.
• The ﬁnal designed model’s performance is slightly better than the IG baseline.
• The current design is not scalable to a larger real world occupancy map.
Based on the above the following recommendations for the developed design in this paper are:
• Environment
As mentioned a major bottleneck in the adoption of deep RL is that of a realistic simula-
tor to train in. The development of such a simulator is a whole project in itself. However,
for future studies that investigate the eﬃcacy of deep RL for robotic mapping the envi-
ronment developed in this paper can be extended to a more complicated version. The
movement of the agent within the map should be continuous and not discrete. Once the
output is a continuous action the IG approach becomes even more complicated which
would further prove the feasibility of using deep RL.
• Auxiliary Tasks
Since the goal is to explore the map, the reduction in entropy between each step is the
ideal reward to measure the performance and to train on. However, there are a number
of other tasks which if learnt could help achieve the overall goal. Including the task of
locating the agent within the occupancy map based on the input, even if the location is
fed as an image, could decrease the number of samples needed to converge. Further tasks
that could be included to help the training of the agent should be investigated.
43
==PAGE:50==
Francis Hamilton, s160959
• Feature Extraction Model
Through using a more advanced model compared to the standard CNN architecture the
performance was increased. The residual network model is not the only advanced deep
network architecture currently available. Using techniques such as batch normalization
or dropout could further increase the performance.
• Optimizer
The optimizer used in the paper described in the Preliminary Theory section is a ﬁrst
order method. Although in the supervised setting ﬁrst order methods such as Adam are
preferred as they perform almost as well as the second order methods. However, RL
beneﬁts from using second order methods as it helps to prevent a policy from becoming
sub optimal and cannot recover. The common approach is to limit the change of the
policy between steps through bounding the KL divergence. Incorporating a second order
method into the design could not only improve performance but also decrease the amount
of samples needed.
• Scalability
The performance of the deep RL agent was improved the most by using a diﬀerent rep-
resentation of the agent’s location. The ﬁnal design incorporated the whole occupancy
map and the location within that map as the input. However, this was shown to scale to
large maps very poorly. As such the overall architecture of the design needs to be changed
so that it can deal with a larger occupancy map. Intuitively, the whole occupancy map,
especially in the beginning of the episode, is not needed to make a decision on where to
go next but rather a local window could suﬃce. If instead a local egocentric image of
the current occupancy map was the input to the agent then the size of the occupancy
map would not matter as the input would be set by the local window size. However, by
doing so the state is no longer observable so the system needs to be modelled as partially
observed MDP. The feasibility of this idea should be further researched.
9
Future Work
In line with the recommendations in the above section a possible idea to solving the main issue
of the scalability of the design is presented in this section.
The problem with using only a local egocentric window of the state of the current occupancy
map, is that the system would be partially observable. Whilst policy gradient algorithms can
be trained on partial observed systems the performance is very much related to the extent of
how observable the system is. In this case it would be the size of the window. For example,
44
==PAGE:51==
Francis Hamilton, s160959
if the window size was 1/4 the size of the global occupancy map then the algorithm might
converge to an optimal policy. Investigating the optimal size of the window that would produce
a good enough policy, is a potential solution for solving the scalability problem but it is not ideal.
If for example the agent had a recurrent network in its model it would be able to "remem-
ber" information from past states of the window and thus might be able to make more informed
decisions. Using a a recurrent network to solve the issue of partial observability is very common
in modern RL algorithms. However, it can of course only remember up to a certain point due
to the nature of recurrent networks. Also with the global occupancy map that is being updated
each iteration stores all of the past state information and so, by trying to remember past state
information through a recurrent network, is redundant.
A potential viable solution would be to draw from how human’s visual attention works. When
a human eye perceives an image it looks at diﬀerent parts of the image and not the whole
image at once. Attention in neural networks for classiﬁcation is currently a hot topic in image
classiﬁcation as it allows the network to be smaller and thus more computationally eﬃcient by
restricting the input to parts of the image. An interesting paper titled "Recurrent Attention
Module", (RAM) [35], classiﬁes images in the MNIST datatset through only allowing the net-
work to view a glimpse of the image but is able to choose another glimpse of the image in a
diﬀerent location before classifying it. Even though it only uses an MLP network for feature
extraction the RAM network is able to outperform state of the art convolutional based net-
works. The RAM network has to be trained with reinforcement learning using policy gradients
because the change of location of the glimpse sensor is non diﬀerentiable.
Again, as the occupancy map is just an image, with the agent located in it, the same principle
of using the RAM network can be used. The network would start with an egocentric glimpse of
the occupancy image and based on this can choose the next glimplse location before outputing
an action. This idea should be further developed to access the feasibility as a solution to the
scalability problem.
