Abstract
The ecological status of freshwaters is vital to the natural ecosystems of the world.
Assessing the water quality is essential in protecting and remediating surface water.
In this study, we explored the potential of hyperspectral optical remote sensing to
determine concentrations of water quality parameters.
The study was conducted
at Usserød stream (Denmark), where remote sensing from cameras mounted on un-
manned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and ﬁeld supporting data sets were collected in three
ﬁeld campaigns in February, March and May 2019. Data was collected along approx-
imately 8km of Usserød stream using a hyperspectral snapshot-imager, which has
138 discrete bands in the visible-near infrared spectrum. In-situ water samples and
in-situ ﬁeld spectroscopy measurements were collected at 11 measuring stations along
the stream.
Statistical models using partial least squares regression (PLSR) were developed to
explore the relationship between parameter concentrations from in situ water samples
with 1) Laboratory absorbance, 2) Field spectroscopy and 3) Hyperspectral imaging.
PLSR models were calibrated and validated using data across March and May. The
laboratory and ﬁeld spectroscopy models were developed to understand the feasibility
of the method under completely controlled conditions and when using sensors with
higher radiometric and spectral resolution respectively. In order to build models ﬁeld
data was pre-processed. Radiometric calibration was carried out on hyperspectral
images and reﬂectance was calculated. Hyperspectral images were masked to obtain
only pixels from stream water by testing diﬀerent unsupervised algorithms. . Lastly
hyperspectral images were used to create orthomosaic maps using Structure from Mo-
tion Photogrammetry. These maps were made with the intention of mapping water
quality, however this was not possible due to shortage of time.
The resulting PLSR models for laboratory spectroscopy showed moderate to high
calibration model accuracy of above 0.84 >R2 and rRMSE <20 %. This supported
the feasibility of building PLSR on ﬁeld spectroscopy and hyperspectral images. Sur-
prisingly, ﬁeld spectroscopy models and hyperspectral models had similar poor model
validation. The model validation R2 values were below 0.24 for most quality parame-
ters. Residual plots suggested homoscedasticity and un-robust models. Explanations
for poor model ﬁt include small sample size, changing cloud conditions, optical prop-
erties not accounted for and lastly optical complexity of the water. Summarized the

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The world’s surface water bodies are vital parts of the natural ecosystem because they
sustain life, contribute to the biochemical cycle and provide ecosystem services for
human beings (Mishra et al. 2017; Giardino et al. 2018). Unfortunately, human im-
pacts such as those derived from climate change, land use and land cover changes and
urbanisation have resulted in adverse eﬀects on the ecological status of surface water
environments. Some of these eﬀects include eutrophication (eg harmful algal blooms),
inorganic pollution, reduced water clarity and loss of biodiversty by reduction in nat-
ural vegetation, habitat and benthic organisms (Palmer et al. 2014; Li et al. 2015).
To address the issue EU implemented the water framework directive (WFD) in 2000
for the protection of EU’s surface- and groundwater bodies. The WFD deﬁnes good
ecological status of surface waters as having low chemical pollution, a healthy ecosys-
tem and being capable of maintaining high levels of biodiversity (EC 2008). The EU
WFD obliges member states to monitor, protect and restore their surface- and ground-
waters. The directive, which as mentioned was adopted in 2000, originally required
members states to achieve ’good ecological status’ in all waters by 2015 but it was
postponed until 2027 (EC 2009). For member states to achieve good ecological status,
monitoring programmes must be implemented to help identify freshwater ecosystems
which are polluted or in risk hereof.
Monitoring will also allow evaluation of the
eﬀectiveness of clean up solutions (EC 2009). Determining ecological status includes
addressing the biological (eg. communities of ﬂora and fauna), physico-chemical (nu-
trients and physical parameters) and morphological (eg.
water ﬂow) aspects of a
certain water body (Carvalho 2019). Diﬀerent scientiﬁc disciplines and methods are
required to assess these aspects of water quality. Traditionally the physico-chemical
water quality is determined by in situ water samples manually collected and analysed
on site and in laboratory. Water samples are examined for quality indicators such
as Chlorophyll-a (Chl-a), Colored Dissolved Organic Matter (CDOM), Secchi depth,
temperature, turbidity and Total Suspended Solids (TSS) (Gholizadeh et al. 2016).
Although in situ measurements are highly accurate they are point-based and therefore
do not inform about spatial or temporal variability in the water column (Giardino
et al. 2018). Temporal and spatial variability are vital to understand aspects such as
seasonal changes of pollution, detect point sources of pollution or predict the spread
of harmful algal blooms. Furthermore, manual labor associated with in situ ﬁeldwork
is expensive and time consuming. These aspects combined with the massive challenge
of assessing the ecological status of EU’s surface waters demands the development of

new monitoring methods. Traditional methods of monitoring could be replaced by
combining remote sensing from unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) with in situ sam-
pling methods. Sensors mounted on UAVs can provide spectral information in the
visible and near infrared wavelengths, that can capture light from the sun reﬂected in
the water column. Optically active water quality parameters scatter, emit or absorb
light which theoretically can give distinct features on the light reﬂectance spectra.
In order to retrieve these distinct feature or ’ﬁngerprints’ of water parameters, it is
desired to have narrow and contiguous spectral bands, or a high spectral resolution
(Gholizadeh et al. 2016). Water quality parameters could be retrieved by empirical,
semi-empirical. These models typically require calibration and validation with water
samples analyzed in the laboratory. Based on these models, water parameters in the
water surface could be mapped in detail (Mishra et al. 2017).
Hyperspectral imagery could provide the spectral detail necessary to retrieve qual-
ity parameters. It (see ﬁgure 1.1) commonly has hundreds or thousands of spectral
channels (also referred to as bands) compared to consumer grade Red-Green-Blue
(RGB) cameras which have 3 bands or multispectral cameras which generally have
5-12 bands.

Hyperspectral bands are also narrower compared to multispectral bands (Gholizadeh
et al. 2016). Hyperspectral imaging can be carried out from space-borne or airborne
vehicles. Satellite imagery however yields coarse pixel size which prevents it from
being used in most small surface water bodies, such as those prevalent in Denmark
(Mishra et al. 2017). In this sense, UAVs provide high spatial resolution while being
ﬂexible in terms of deployment for the data producer (Köppl et al. 2018; Giardino et
al. 2018). Nonetheless, using UAVs still presents challenges. For example, due to con-
straints in payload size and weight, the miniaturized sensors used typically have lower
signal to noise ratios than airborne or satellite sensors (Wang et al. 2019). Deploying

UAVs presents the added complexity that data user and data producer are often the
same. The workﬂow of image pre-processing, orthorectiﬁcation and calibrations is
also not trivial because it has to be developed speciﬁcally for each sensor.
The current state of the art of retrieving water parameter concentrations using
reﬂectance from hyperspectral imagery includes studies using both satellite and air-
borne derived data.
Retrieval algorithms encompass analytical bio-optical models
as well as empirical and semi-empirical models (Moses et al. 2011; Sugumaran et al.
2007; Hestir et al. 2015). One of the main challenges of remote sensing of water is
its’ low reﬂectivity with high absorption of the majority of light in regions beyond
the red wavelengths. This results in low signals measured in the sensor, that reduce
the signal to noise ratio. It also limits the penetration of light in the water column
which thereby limits monitoring to the top of the water column. Another challenge is
accounting for eﬀects of changing cloud cover, sun-glint and sensor and sun viewing
angles. Surface waters are optically complex because they contain water parameters
eﬀecting the scattering and absorption of light. Shallow waters also have the eﬀects
of the bottom sediment and bottom vegetation (Mishra et al. 2017). Furthermore,
water properties vary between and within each water body (Palmer et al. 2014). Some
water parameters (for instance CDOM) might interfere the presence of others (mask-
ing eﬀect) while highly-scattering parameters can cause saturation of reﬂectance for
increasing concentrations (Giardino et al. 2018). In studies, hyperspectral data is
frequently used to develop simple band ratio models to retrieve Chl-a (Moses et al.
2011; Fan 2014; Bhatti et al. 2008; Koponen et al. 2001; Olmanson et al. 2012). They
achieve good correlation in terms of R2 but do not utilize the full potential of the
many bands the hyperspectral imagery provides. Few studies including (Cao et al.
2018; Sugumaran et al. 2007; Fichot et al. 2015) use algorithms such as partial least
squares regression (PLSR) or principal component analysis (PCA) that utilize the full
spectrum. The most commonly modelled water parameters are Chl-a, CDOM and
TSS because they are optically active in terms of absorption and scattering of light
(Gholizadeh et al. 2016). Bio-optical models which are more theoretical in nature
utilize the inherent optical properties (IOP) which are the absorption and scattering
properties independent on the ambient light and apparent optical properties (AOP)
those that depend on the medium (IOP) and on the geometry of the radiance (He-
stir et al. 2015; DallOlmo et al. 2006). Empirical models include black box models
such as inversion of reﬂectance spectra, singular linear relationships and statistical
algorithms.
Most common singular linear relationships include band ratios which
relate the remote sensing reﬂectance of one or more bands to the concentration of
single water parameters. As mentioned band ratios do not utilize the full potential
of hyperspectral spectra but are shown to be very accurate (Giardino et al. 2018).
On the Danish island of Sjælland, the water quality of Usserød stream close to
Nivå is monitored as part of the Riverscapes project. The Riverscapes project is a
collaboration between the technical university of Denmark (DTU), the Danish Hydro-
logical Institute (DHI) and the companies Orbicon A/S and Drone Systems Aps. The
project investigates the potential of using UAVs to monitor the water quantity and

1.1
Objective
The aim of this study is to explore the potential of the multivariate statistical model
PLSR to retrieve water quality parameters from hyperspectral UAV sensors.
To
achieve that goal, three hyperspectral ﬂight campaigns were performed on Usserød
stream (Denmark) acquiring ground truth data for validation and model development.
Three sets of spectral data were acquired for model development; a) laboratory mea-
sured absorbance b) ﬁeld spectroscopy c) remote sensing hyperspectral data from a
UAV. The datasets were expected to have decreasing levels of accuracy to retrieve wa-
ter quality parameters. The examined water quality parameters were: Chlorophyll a
(Chl-a), non-volatile organic carbon (NVOC) (representing colored dissolved organic
matter (CDOM)), total suspended solids (TSS), dissolved oxygen (DO), Nitrite and
Phosphate.

2.1
Hyperspectral remote sensing of surface waters
Remote sensing imagery can be captured with either satellite, manned or unmanned
aerial vehicles (UAVs). Compared to manned vehicles or satellites, UAVs are more
ﬂexible for the data producer, cheaper and can capture images closer to the water
surface (Palmer et al. 2014). Imagery closer to the ground enables better spatial
resolution but can also eliminate the necessity for atmospheric correction (Giardino
et al. 2018). Hyperspectral sensors on board of UAVs typically acquire images in
either push-broom (line scanning) or as snapshots (or single shots). In the latter
all the pixels in the image detect the light in the same instant (Adão et al. 2017).
This has advantages for UAVs as the stability of the platforms is less than for larger
manned aircraft missions. From sensors mounted on UAVs light is detected at diﬀerent
wavelengths in contiguous and narrow (< 5nm) bands. The measured light intensity
in digital numbers (DN) at each bands in each image can be stored as a data cube (3D
matrix) of spatial and spectral information. The hyperspectral camera used in this
study creates a data-cube that consists of 138 spectral channels and 50 x 50 pixels.
Spectral reﬂectance in each pixel can be estimated from the radiance detected at
the sensor as radiation reﬂected from the water surface if the amount of downwelling
solar irradiance is known (Mishra et al. 2017). Radiance entering the water column
will be absorbed and scattered in diﬀerent wavelengths by the water itself and op-
tically active parameters. It then leaves the water column as reﬂected light which
can be captured by a sensor. Optically active parameters can have distinct signa-
tures on the reﬂectance spectrum based on their optical properties (Gholizadeh et al.
2016). Retrieving water quality parameters from remote sensing reﬂectance is how-
ever challenging. Surface waters are optically complex and contain many parameters
that eﬀect the reﬂect light. Figure 2.1 shows an overview on the eﬀect of the water
column on the double path of light from the sun to the sensor. In the detected signal,
there are also eﬀects from properties from the surrounding environment (eg AOP).
These include the reﬂection of the bottom surface, adjacency eﬀects, atmospheric
eﬀects, sun-glint and reﬂection on the water surface (Mishra et al. 2017).

2.1.1
Relevant water quality parameters
The following is a summary of the ecological and spectral signiﬁcance of relevant water
quality parameters examined in this study. Optically active parameters include Chl-
a, CDOM and TSS. In this study it is expected to ﬁnd correlations for optically
inactive parameters because they co-vary with optically active parameters like Chl-a
(Gholizadeh et al. 2016).
Chlorophyll a (Chl-a) is often used as a proxy for phytoplankton which is a biologi-
cal water quality parameter. Chl-a is an essential pigment to perform photosynthesis
and is found in algae and cyanobacteria. The presence of Chl-a has been directly
related to harmful algal blooms commonly caused by eutrophication by nutrients like
nitrogen and phosphorous (Gholizadeh et al. 2016; Mishra et al. 2017). Chl-a is op-
tically active and absorbs light in the blue and red wavelengths. It has prominent
absorption peaks around 665-670nm and 430-450nm (Gholizadeh et al. 2016).
Coloured dissolved organic matter (CDOM) also called gelb-stoﬀare naturally
occurring water-soluble yellow and brown substances. It is mostly comprised of de-
compositional products from animals and vegetation in the form of proteins and humic
and ﬂuvic acids (Gilmore et al. 2013). CDOM is optically active and mainly absorbs
in the blue-green region with exponentially decreasing absorption with increasing
==PAGE:17==
2.2 Study Site

wavelengths. CDOM is commonly linked with Non-volatile organic carbon (NVOC)
which is correlated with CDOM absorption in wavelengths 254nm (ultraviolet) and
440nm (violet-blue). Because of the overlap of Chl-a and CDOM absorption around
440nm, CDOM can mask the presence of Chl-a (Gholizadeh et al. 2016).
Total suspended solids (TSS) include biotic and abiotic particles that are not
dissolved in the water column and are therefore considered physical water quality
parameters. TSS is directly related to turbidity and transmissivity of light in the
water which is the opposite of water clarity. It is important for the status of surface
waters because it has inﬂuence on the penetration of light in the water column. TSS
scatters light over much of the visible spectrum and therefore complicates the retrieval
of other water quality parameters. TSS can also shift the peaks of reﬂectance of the
water column towards higher wavelengths (Gholizadeh et al. 2016).
Dissolved oxygen (DO) is vital for the ecological status of surface waters as it
essential for living organisms in the water. DO is commonly used to measure eu-
trophication because growth of algae and bacteria will decrease its’ concentration.
DO is optically inactive and relationships with remote sensing data are still poorly
understood and little research exists on it (Gholizadeh et al. 2016).
Nitrite (NO2-) is an intermediate oxidation state in the natural nitrogen cycle but
is eco-toxic towards living organisms. Nitrite is part of Total Nitrogen (TN) which
is an essential nutrient for biotic life and therefore associated with eutrophication.
Nitrite is optically inactive and little research exists on its’ retrieval from remote
sensing data. (Li et al. 2015).
Phosphate (PO4-3) is a nutrient to biotic life like nitrite and is associated with
algal growth. High loadings of phosphate can be an indicator of poor water quality
as eutrophication occurs. Phosphate is optically inactive but correlated with phyto-
plankton, TSS and turbidity (Gholizadeh et al. 2016).
2.2
Study Site
The study site in this project is the Usserød stream ﬂowing through Nivå on the island
of Sjælland in Denmark. Images of the stream can be seen in ﬁgures 2.3 a and b. The
Sjælsø lake feeds into Usserød stream which meets with Nivå stream in the north and
drains into Øresund, the sea between Denmark and Sweden. The stream is ca. 8 km
long (Iversen et al. 2011) and a map of the stream can be seen on ﬁgure 2.2. Sjælsø
lake has an area of appr. 3650 ha with an average depth of 3.2m. The area around

and military grass- ﬁelds (17%). Sjælsø lake experiences harmful algal blooms in the
summer caused by increased nutrient concentrations. Such nutrients and algae from
Sjælsø lake feed into Usserød stream which lowers oxygen concentrations decreasing
its water quality and ecological status (Usserød 2019). According to a report from
the Danish Environmental Protection Agency there are no public plans to remediate
Sjælsø lake because more knowledge is necessary to intervene (Usserød 2019). Along
Usserød stream there are three dams which were build in the 19th century. Also,

2.8
Statistical modelling
2.8.1
Descriptive analysis
Descriptive analysis of water quality parameter concentrations from in situ water
samples was carried out on March and May data. It was carried out in the soft-
ware R through the environment Rstudio.Min, Max, mean, median and standard
deviations were calculated for each water quality parameters to get an overview of
the dataset. An assumption for building the linear statistical model is normality of
data. Therefore, a Shapiro-Wilks test on a 5% signiﬁcance level was carried to test
if concentrations within months followed a normal distribution. It is expected that a
signiﬁcant diﬀerence in concentrations worsen the statistical model performance. For
that reason, it was tested whether there was signiﬁcantly diﬀerence between March
and May concentrations of parameters. For this purpose a two-sided paired ttest on
the diﬀerence of means was carried out. The H0 hypothesis states that the means are
not signiﬁcantly diﬀerent on a 5 % signiﬁcance level.
2.8.2
Partial Least Squares Regression (PLSR)
In order build a model based on the statistical relationship between the concentra-
tion of water quality parameters and their absorption/reﬂectance spectra PLSR was
performed. PLSR is a method that bears resemblance to PCA. In PLSR a multiple
linear regression model is built from an observed matrix of variables [X] to predict a
vector or matrix of variables [Y]. The general formula for the PLSR linear model is
seen in equation 2.10.
[Y ] = β · [X] + F
(2.10)
Where β is the slope of the linear model and F is the intersection on the y-axis.
According to PCA every dataset is a product of its scores and loadings (Nørgaard
et al. 2008; Siong 2013) this can be seen in equation 2.11 and 2.12.
[Y ] = U · Qy
′
(2.11)
[X] = T · Qx
′
(2.12)
Where U and T are the scores and Qy and Qx are the loadings of [Y] and [X].
PLS can predict [Y] from [X] by ﬁnding the scores of [Y] and [X] which have the
maximum co-variance (Dunn 2018). The model ﬁnds the multidimensional variables
in [X] which explain the highest variance in [Y]. PLSR also takes the co-variance
between the [X] variables into account which is an advantage over multiple linear
regression (MLR) because the reﬂectance of one wavelength is highly dependent on
the reﬂectance of adjacent wavelengths. In PLSR the scores of [X] and [Y] are ﬁt to
get highest possible linear correlation. A number of Principle Components (pc’s) are

calculated for each pc. The resulting model is a multiple linear regression model with
1 coeﬃcient term for each [X] variable (Collins 2010).

3.5.4
Discussion in relation to other studies
In order to place the results in the context of similar studies table 3.12 was compiled
containing a comprehensive literature review of models retrieving water quality from
remote sensing reﬂectance algorithms. Most studies use the AISA (Airborne Imaging
Spectrometer for Applications) hyperspectral imager combined with band ratio algo-
rithms. For Chl-a all studies show higher accuracy (0.62-0.937 R2) compared to this
study. This suggest that simple band ratio might be more appropriate to estimate
Chl-a concentrations. Common for most of the band ratio algorithms for Chl-a is
that they utilize the relationship between the reﬂectance dip around 670nm and the
reﬂectance peak around 700nm. The dip around 670nm is caused by Chl-a absorption
while the peak around 700nm is caused by phytoplankton back-scattering (Fan 2014).
When examining spectra from ﬁgure 3.4 for ﬁeld spectroscopy and endmember plot
in ﬁgure 2.9 for hyperspectral images it is apparent that these features are present
in the spectra. However model validations for Chl-a are still very poor. This might
be cause be low signal to noise ratio of measurements which would ﬂatten the peak
of the spectra. (Cao et al. 2018) used MDBPSO-PLSR with an MDBPSO algorithm
to select sensitive spectral variables for each water constituents and got a good cor-
relation (R2 of 0.97) but without MDBPSO only a R2 of 0.55. In literature CDOM
models have higher correlations than this study with (Fan 2014) of R2 of 0.83 using
the band ratio correlation between 700 and 450nm. For TSS studies the R2 values
are generally low except for the (Cao et al. 2018) study using PLSR. (Sudduth et al.
2015) similarly to this study used PLSR hyperspectral data and ﬁeld spectroscopy
data for water quality assessment of the Mississippi river basin. The similarly to
this study achieve moderate validation accuracy of 0.54 R2 for Chl-a and 0.47 R2 for
Nitrite. They suggest high variability in reﬂectance spectra for ﬁeld spectroscopy and
water parameter concentrations worsens the model-validation as was also seen in this
study.

3.7
Further research
Regarding further research of the retrieval of water quality parameters it would be
appropriate to carry out a power analysis to understand how many water samples are
necessary to ﬁnd signiﬁcant correlations and better model accuracy. Revealing the
amount of samples necessary would then give the opportunity to evaluate strategies
for future ﬁeld campaigns and weather other means of measuring water constituents
should be employed.
A signiﬁcance test of the existing models would clarify the
validity of the models, as model signiﬁcance should be prioritized equivalently to or
even higher than model accuracy (Aguirre-Urreta 2015). A cross-validation leaving
out one station at a time could also help build more robust models and to avoid model
over-ﬁtting (Cao et al. 2018). Better understanding of light conditions inﬂuence on
the captured spectra and the optical complexity would probably also improve models
if these were corrected for.
A better masking algorithm for hyperspectral images
would be expected to improve model ﬁts as more water is identiﬁed and thus more
heterogeneity captured in the data. Masking algorithms could be improved by using
supervised machine learning. It could then by carried out with hyperspectral images
(endmembers) from diﬀerent seasons to ensure algorithm application between months.
Regarding other modelling approaches simple band ratios could be tested for Chl-
a and CDOM. Furthermore bio-optical models which take into account Apparent
Optical Properties (AOP’s) such as bottom sediment could be created to understand
the inﬂuence of bottom sediment (Giardino et al. 2018).

CHAPTER 4
Conclusions
In this study we assessed the potential of using remote sensing from Unmanned Aerial
systems, in particular statistical PLSR models to map water quality parameters. We
used data from two ﬁeld campaigns in Usserød stream. The analysis was supported
by laboratory analysis and in situ spectroscopy from 3 ﬁeld campaigns.
Our results show that PLSR models for laboratory absorbance had high R2 for
calibration model ﬁts for TSS, CDOM(NVOC) and Chl-a. The good correlations of
the optically active parameters suggest it is possible to build a PLSR model based
on reﬂection with moderate or good ﬁt.
PLSR results for ﬁeld spectroscopy showed good calibration model ﬁt, however had
poor validation for most water quality parameters (R2 <0.20). It was expected that
ﬁeld spectroscopy model would perform well because of the high spectral resolution.
There are several possible explanations for poor model performance. First the models
are based on point measurements which means the measurements can capture a het-
erogeneity in the water column and bottom sediment. Second NVOC(CDOM) could
be subject to photo-bleaching. Third changing light conditions caused by changing
cloud conditions have large inﬂuence on the light intensity and thus the signal to noise.
PLSR on hyperspectral spectroscopy models had high R2 values for calibration
ﬁt for average pixel models and moderate for all pixel models.
The accuracy of
the validation was poor for both model structures for all water quality parameters
(negative and low R2). Residual analysis emphasized this with skewed histograms
and qq-plots (non-normality) and correlation in residual plots (homoscedasticity).
The best performing models were DO and Nitrite, which are both optically inactive.
Nitrite however had very large errors. It is not expected that the optically inactive
parameters have better model ﬁts, but this could be explained by them correlating
with optically active constituents. Model weights have a relatively ﬂat ﬁrst pc for most
water quality parameters. This suggests that majority of the variance in the imagery
is due to variation in light intensity. Varying cloud conditions and AOPs are not
accounted for and the bottom sediment and reﬂection in the water surface is present
in some images. Lastly it is expected that wrongly identiﬁed pixels in hyperspectral
image masking would seriously worsen the model performance, as models would then
be based on pixels other than water.
==PAGE:58==
48
4 Conclusions
General poor performance of ﬁeld spectroscopy and hyperspectral models could
be caused by limited available data. Small datasets decrease statistical power which
increases the risk of making a type II mistake (not ﬁnding correlation when there
is one).
Changing calibration- validation model combinations from 14 calibration
measurements and 5 validation measurements to 12 calibration measurements and 8
validation measurements did however not improve validation.
Next steps include understanding if larger datasets will improve PLSR accuracy.
It also includes understanding and accounting for eﬀects of light conditions. Alterna-
tively moving away from PLSR includes examining single band ratio and bio-optical
models.
