Abstract
Metabolism is a highly connected network of biochemical reactions, carried out by en-
zymes under a thermodynamic driving force defined by metabolite concentrations and
kint. One of the most important metabolic pathways in yeast is glycolysis - generating
energetic co-factors and shuttling carbon towards biosynthetic building blocks. Elucidat-
ing control in glycolysis will be beneficial for any strain engineering project due to the
widespread impact of glycolysis.
Previous studies to understand the impact of thermodynamics on yeast glycolysis required
detailed metabolomics or lumped reactions, reducing the results’ fidelity. In this thesis we
develop a kinetic model that fits the experimental measurements and use the trained
model to explore how flux is regulated. Kinetic models are mechanistic descriptions of
metabolism that attempt to replicate in vivo behaviour.
We sampled a kinetic model using Bayesian analysis, utilising kinetic priors from a previ-
ous kinetic model and omics data (metabolomics, proteomics, fluxomics) measured un-
der five carbon limited conditions. The model largely reproduced the in vivo metabolite
and enzyme concentrations, as well as the drain reactions. To validate our model, we
predicted the same five growth rates in nitrogen limited conditions. However, we hypoth-
esise that due to the lack of f26bp regulation on PFK we were unable to predict the in vivo
metabolite concentrations and fluxes.
Despite the poor external validation results, the trained model appears to be appropriate
for characterising thermodynamic regulation within the training data. We discovered that
our model agrees with the findings of Park et al. - that glycolytic reactions are largely
near equilibrium, with two thirds of the predicted Gibbs energies of reactions in the EMP
pathway satisfying |∆G| ≲4kJ/mol - yet requiring fewer absolute metabolomics. Further-
more, we noticed this trend remained along increasing dilution rates. Two of the reactions
that were far from equilibrium, PFK and CDC19, are catalyzed by allosteric enzymes that
are up regulated under increasing growth rates. This suggests that the entirety of glycol-
ysis is operating below capacity, which is advantageous for rapid glycolytic adaptation.
Moving forward, this study is an example of the use of kinetic models for strain design.
We were able to identify the sources of regulation in glycolysis which agreed with previ-
ously published findings, using incomplete measurements of multiomics data. This was
possible through Bayesian statistics, by which, biologically relevant priors regularised the
unmeasured enzyme and metabolite concentrations. Further refinement of this model is
required, specifically: including f26bp regulation of PFK, which is currently not possible
as we do not model enzyme phosphorylation.
The outlook for kinetic models is promising, identifying which enzymes are limiting and
also, which are in excess, will benefit rational strain design. However, the scope and gen-
erality of models must be improved by incorporating mechanisms such as phosphorylation
and through systematic model improvement such as the inductive-deductive spiral.

Introduction
Glycolysis in Saccharomyces ceravisiae (referred to as yeast from here on) is one of
the best studied microbial pathways. It degrades glucose to pyruvate, one of the funda-
mental building blocks of a cell, and generates energetic co-factors required for the cell’s
metabolism. Glycolysis is highly regulated and has a high variation of operating fluxes
due to the ’Crabtree’ effect. The Crabtree effect is characterized by a large up-shift in
glycolytic rates as growth rates are increased, where glucose is primarily converted to
ethanol (Frick and Wittmann 2005). At high growth rates, yeast uses the wasteful aero-
bic fermentation mechanism to produce ethanol from pyruvate rather than oxidising it to
CO2 in the TCA cycle. This is less efficient in terms of ATP production, one of the most
ubiquitous energy co-factors in a cell (De Deken 1966). Deciphering metabolic control of
reactions, i.e., the impact of each reaction in the cell metabolism, will improve our ability
to engineer yeast and understand its limitations. To study the metabolic control of reac-
tions, we can use kinetic models of metabolism. These approximate cellular behaviour
using functions, to predict the state of the cell in any condition. Mostly, kinetic models
are under-determined, relying on uncertain and insufficient measurements. By sampling
possible models where each model describes the experimental results equally well, we
hope to encapsulate cellular behaviour in silico. Using these models to probe the control
of glycolysis over increasing dilution rates will be invaluable for strain design and scientific
knowledge in general. In this thesis we will use kinetic models not to explore the Crabtree
effect, but rather to understand how control in glycolysis is distributed and can rapidly
adapt to metabolite changes.
1.1
Saccharomyces cerevisiae
Wild yeast has been used for millennia in bread and brewing. It is also the most widely
used cell factory for the production of heterologous products: from fatty acids to pharma-
ceuticals (Y. Liu et al. 2020; Q. Liu et al. 2020; J. Nielsen and Keasling 2016). Several
factors make yeast an ideal host organism: it is well studied with many curated metabolic
maps of metabolism (Lu et al. 2019; Sánchez et al. 2019); it has a strong industrial track
record with bioethanol, insulin, and artemisinic acid; and is a Generally Regarded As Safe
(GRAS) organism, which, lowers regulatory hurdles. Despite these benefits, other organ-
isms may be preferred bio-factories for different reasons, e.g. mammalian cells when spe-
cific glycosylation patterns are required, or E. coli when the specific production rate is not
high enough, or when secretion/inclusion mechanisms for a product make downstream
processing infeasible. In 2018, a pressure test was conducted where ten molecules had
to be synthesised in 90 days, of those ten, three were selected for production in yeast and
two were successful (Casini et al. 2018). The stress-test highlights how researchers are
capable of engineering yeast strains as well as their robustness to genetic modifications.
A current trend is the production of pharmaceuticals in yeast which take advantage of the
GRAS classification and the extensive knowledge of its metabolism (Walker and Pretorius
2018; Rahmat and Kang 2020; V. M. Liu et al. 2019).
Cannabinoids are a new target molecule for therapeutic use. Rather than production in
Cannabis sativa L., cannabinoids can now be produced in yeast by altering the meval-
onate pathway and introducing cannabis synthesis genes (Luo et al. 2019). Synthesis
in yeast could allow the augmentation of cannabinoids for improved therapeutic effects,
such as increasing receptor specificity or targeting different receptors (Luo et al. 2019).

Cannabinoid titres were initially in the low mg/L, however, these could improved by utilising
methods such as adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE) or, more recently, active-learning
(Borkowski et al. 2019).
Recent success for yeast as a bio-factory is seen in the industrialisation of the anti-malaria
drug Artemisin by producing the precursor molecule artemisinic acid in yeast (Paddon et
al. 2013; Ro et al. 2006; J. Nielsen and Keasling 2016). Artemisin is natively produced
in the plant Artemisia annua. However, the low yield and variability of production makes
it attractive for production in alternate microorganisms. Cytochrome P450 is required for
synthesis of artemisinic acid and must be expressed in the membrane, which, isn’t trivial
in E. coli. Thus yeast was chosen as the host organism (Hausjell, Halbwirth, and Spadiut
2018). Initial titres of Artemisinic acid were in the low hundreds of mg/L, this was improved
by tuning cytochrome P450 and its conjugate reductase: resulting in a titre of 25 g/L, which
is now used by Sanofi (Paddon et al. 2013; Ro et al. 2006).
Strain engineering in yeast as a host organism benefits from the large tool set and data
available. Many pathways contain allosteric regulation, a non-linear control of flux, where
effector metabolites inter-convert enzymes between active and inactive states. Positive
effectors stabilise the active state of the enzyme, so that equilibrium favours the active
state, whilst negative effectors stabilise the inactive state shifting the equilibrium to the
inactive enzyme. Increasing gene expression to improve flux through a pathway can
increase negative effector concentrations, reducing the amount of active enzyme and de-
creasing the pathway efficiency. An example of this is in aromatic amino acid biosynthesis,
where removing allosteric regulation of the ARO4 gene by replacing it with a feedback-
inhibition insensitive allele increased flux by 4.5-fold (Luttik et al. 2008). Engineering these
pathways is complicated. There could be several engineering solutions for this specific
example: increase expression of ARO4; increase expression of the enzyme consuming
the inhibitor metabolite; or the solution Luttik et al. chose, replacing the inhibited enzyme
with a product insensitive enzyme. Kinetic models that reproduce metabolic behaviour
can replicate experiments in silico, where large sets of possible experiments can be re-
duced to only those that improve flux through the pathway.

A kinetic model is a description of metabolism which is constrained by the rate laws of
the involved reactions. As an example, let’s observe a hypothetical metabolite A with its

a tightly coupled network. For example, if v2 leads to a metabolite B, the concentration of

kinetics which reproduce enzyme saturation behavior.
A recent use of kinetic models was by Christodoulou et al., with the purpose of under-
standing how flux is redistributed in E. coli under oxidative stress (Christodoulou et al.
2018).

Phosphate (PP) pathway while assuming allosteric interactions to be accountable. Their
method consisted of generating all possible single and pairwise allosteric interactions
within The Embden–Meyerhof–Parnas (EMP) pathway and the PP pathway. To evaluate
each model, parameter values were sampled from marginal distributions and the model
likelihood was evaluated against metabolomics and 13C labelling data, assuming they
were drawn from the predicted distribution. Christodoulou et al. concluded that NADPH
was a negative effector of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase; and oxidative stress,
which decreases NADPH levels, would increase the flux through the PP pathway.
A limitation of this method is the exponential increase in computation time with increasing
model complexity. Because a model is required for each hypothesis to be tested, i.e. if we
want to test ten different hypothesis, we need ten different model topologies. Linear scal-
ing would be reasonable for any fitting approach - yet the number of possible topologies
grows with O(n!), as the possible interactions are nCi, where n is the network size and i is
the number of expected interactions. Furthermore, only marginal parameter distributions
are considered, which is contrary to observed models of enzymes in which parameters
are highly correlated (Gutenkunst et al. 2007). This is illustrated in Fig. 1.3, each of the
contours represents equal model likelihoods, where the innermost boundary represents
the highest likelihood region and the x-axis and y-axis are the log of the parameters θ1
and θ2 values, respectively. When observing plot A, it is apparent that all of the parameter
combinations have equally high likelihood, yet they have a large variation in their estimate.
This is in contrast with plot C, where θ1 is measured with a high degree of certainty and
θ2 is measured poorly. However because the parameters in plot C are sampled only on
their measurements without considering how this impacts the model probability, there are
regions where the sampled parameters lead to poor model predictions, represented by
the points crossing over contour lines. Gutenkunst et al. suggest to not build a singular
perfect model but focus on models that can be tested and have predictive power.
Parameter identifiability is a common issue in kinetic models. An example of this is the
relationship between [E] and kcat, which is multiplicatively intractable: kcat[E] = Vmax.
Any combination of [E] and kcat that give the same Vmax value are indistinguishable.
To compensate for this unidentifiability, Tran et al. developed the ensemble modelling
framework which is used in several platforms (Tran, Rizk, and Liao 2008; P. Saa and
L. K. Nielsen 2015; Chowdhury, Zomorrodi, and C. D. Maranas 2014). In the ensemble
modeling framework, we decompose each reaction into its elementary steps (e.g. bind-
ing of substrate to enzyme) and use mass-action kinetics to model each of them. We
then sample the microscopic rate constants based on metabolite concentrations, fluxes,
and reaction Gibbs energies. The sampled parameters are guaranteed to generate a
model that reproduces the measured flux distributions, ensuring that all models are able
to reproduce the measured state.
Chowdhury et al. developed a platform called k-OptForce, which nests a kinetic model,
constructed using the ensemble modelling framework, within a stoichiometric model (Chowd-
hury, Zomorrodi, and C. D. Maranas 2014). Their method uses a Genetic Algorithm (GA)
to optimise reaction parameters and improve flux predictions. Comparing their approach
to the constraint based modelling technique OptForce, the kinetic model was more accu-
rate at predicting yield in the conditions that the data was trained on (Chowdhury, Zomor-
rodi, and C. D. Maranas 2014; Ranganathan, Suthers, and C. D. Maranas 2010). How-
ever, when predicting anaerobic conditions while trained only on aerobic data, the model
was less accurate.

This cycle is referred to as a bottom-up approach in (Westerhoff and Kell 2007), including
an iterative model refinement stage, and is quite applicable considering the importance of
network structure as discovered in (Eunen et al. 2012). It applies an inductive-deductive
loop: whereby, the kinetic parameters are updated to improve the fit to metabolite concen-
trations and reaction fluxes. This optimized model is evaluated, and after each iteration
the reaction which hasn’t been characterised and has the greatest control over glucose
uptake is replaced with a more complete rate law either measured in-house or from liter-
ature; for example, a simplified expression of the glycerol branch was updated to include
two-reactions (Smallbone et al. 2013). The paper by Messiha et al., extended this model
to the PP pathway using the same standardised conditions as in the previous study by
Smallbone et al., validating their model against redox and glucose pulses. Overall, the
detailed characterisation of the enzyme parameters makes this model one of the gold-
standards for yeast glycolysis and serves as a good foundation for prior knowledge in
Bayesian analysis.

2.3.4
Selected rate law
Based on the above discussions of rate laws and with the objective of characterising pa-
rameter uncertainty, the most appropriate rate law is the modular rate law. Parameter
uncertainty and by association model uncertainty is the most important output of a model-
ing framework. Metabolomics, proteomics, and fluxomics can all carry high uncertainties
leading to dispersed model likelihoods. The benefit of bottom-up approaches is that you
develop a model independent of the data, with the objective of constructing a general
model. The bottom-up approach can be captured by using previous measurements as
priors, which makes the modular rate law the ideal choice. Despite fitting multiple condi-
tions using the elementary mass action kinetics using K-FIT, which does provide a larger
scope, there is a strong dependence on the training set used, leading to over-fitting or
unidentified parameter regions.
The modular rate law offers reasonable approximations to ordered and ping-pong mecha-
nism, which are easily represented using elementary mass action kinetics (Liebermeister
and Klipp 2006). Information about the Michaelis-Menten constants allows integration of
priors, which, are typically unavailable for elementary mass action kinetics.

2.5
Thermodynamic Consistency
An often overlooked aspect of kinetic models is thermodynamic consistency. There are
two separate areas to introduce thermodynamic consistency: within reactions, ensuring
the forward and reverse rates at equilibrium result in the equilibrium constant, i.e. these
satisfy the Haldane relationship; and between reactions to ensure that the free energy
generated is independent of the path taken.
To satisfy the Haldane relationship a simple approach is to define one of the kinetic pa-
rameters in terms of the equilibrium constant, an example is shown in Eqn. 2.12. Defining
the reverse rate constant by using the Haldane relationship is beneficial, as many exper-
iments don’t measure the reverse rates. If reactions are assumed irreversible, then the
Haldane relationship is not satisfied as the equilibrium constant tends to infinity, which is
not possible. However, there are additional constraints on defining equilibrium constants,
where the standard Gibbs energy represented by the equilibrium constant (Eqn. 2.18)
must sum to zero around a cycle. If a reaction cycle has Gibbs energies of reactions that
do not sum to zero, then traversing that cycle becomes a method of generating energy
from nothing, which is impossible.

tent. A disadvantage of this method is the observability of the formation energies, which
aren’t measured for large sets of metabolites. In 2013 the entire formation energy mea-
surements covered only 10 % of E. coli metabolism. To overcome the lack of measured
formation energies, Noor, E., et al., created the component contribution framework, which
defines the set of formation energies that are consistent with Gibbs energies of reactions
and the measured formation energies (Noor et al. 2013).
2.6
Maud
Maud is the name of the kinetic modelling framework used in this thesis; named after
Maud Menten, the doctor and medical researcher who formulated Michaelis-Menten ki-
netics along with Leonor Michaelis. This framework was developed by Groves, ”Maud:
An application for Bayesian analysis of metabolic networks” [Paper in Progress]; with the
goal of creating an ensemble of models that capture the uncertainty of kinetic models.
Previous methods used mostly samplers that are likely to get caught in highly correlated
regions )Hackett et al. 2016), or did not capitalise on the extensive prior information of
kinetic parameters (P. A. Saa and L. K. Nielsen 2016).
The workflow for Maud is described in Fig. 2.3. There are two categories of information
required: multiomics data, which is experiment specific, and kinetic parameter estimates,
which are typically taken from literature.
2.6.1
Need for Bayesian inference
The correlations of parameters within enzymes and between isoenzymes in reactions,
generates correlated parameter combinations, where individual parameters are uniden-
tifiable as the combination of parameters determine the reaction behaviour. As this pa-
rameter space is highly dimensional with hundreds of dimensions, Bayesian samplers
become inefficient at generating meaningful samples (see section 2.2). One of the al-
gorithms that is suitable for MCMC analysis in higher dimensions is HMC. The Bayesian
analysis used in this thesis was constructed in Stan, which automates the probability cal-
culations and gradient evaluations required for HMC. Stan requires the prior distribution
specification, model likelihood, and the kinetic model topology? to generate the balanced
metabolites and fluxes.

by the prior distributions on the boundary conditions. Likelihood evaluations (the orange

3.1
Model Definition
The scope of the yeast model used for our analysis is defined in Figure 3.1. The network
contains two boundary categories, unbalanced metabolites and metabolite drains. Unbal-
anced metabolites remain constant during the simulation with the majority being co-factors
(see the list below). In addition to the unbalanced metabolites, there are biomass drains
on ribose-5-phosphate (r5p), pyruvate (pyr), and dihydroxyacetone phosphate (dhap),
which, are modelled as irreversible mass action so that at steady state they will always
represent a drain towards biomass. This reduces the scope of the model to regions where
the previous metabolites are diverted away from the network rather than towards. All of
the following metabolites are unbalanced metabolites:
• glucose-6-phosphate,
• nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide - reduced and oxidised (nad(h)),
• nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate - reduced and oxidised (nadp(h))
• adenosine triphosphate (atp),
• adenosine diphosphate (adp), and
• phosphate (pi).
If a metabolite is not mentioned in the above list they are a balanced metabolite, meaning
they are described by a system of ODEs.
3.1.1
Scope
The EMP and PP pathway are the targets of this thesis. Adequately describing the in-
teractions between these pathways requires glucose-6-phosphate (g6p) as a common
metabolite, and to decrease the model complexity it was determined that g6p be the start-
ing point of metabolism. This removes the previous reactions of hexokinase and glucose
transport. However, there appear to be no feed-forward interactions from glucose concen-
tration on the remaining glycolytic reactions suggesting that this removal will not influence
the remaining metabolism (Smallbone et al. 2013; Teusink et al. 2000; Eunen et al. 2012).
The lower boundary of glycolysis was set to pyruvate, with a drain representing the flux
towards biomass, the TCA cycle, and ethanol. This decision was a result of having no
measurement for acetaldehyde, alcohol dehydrogenase, or ethanol concentrations. In-
ference is still possible, yet the increased uncertainty would dramatically increase the
sampling time.
Using Maud currently, a network of approximately 20 reactions takes approximately 7
days to generate 500 samples per chain, with chain numbers depending solely on the
computer used. Increasing this to include ethanol fermentation and glucose transport,
which include isoenzymes will add five reactions

To describe perturbations in metabolism, metabolites are required to be balanced metabo-

There are multiple ways to define drain reactions: as a fixed exchange where over a
given period of time that concentration is lost; or as irreversible mass action, where a
constant is multiplied by the concentration of the drain metabolite to determine the rate of
metabolite removal. After simulating both approaches using an ODE solver to solve the
yeast kinetic model, using fixed exchange mechanisms may result in pathological solu-
tions, where the metabolite concentration tends to negative infinity. Whereas, irreversible
mass action kinetics produces more stable solutions.

cific, as the goal is not to model the drain under different conditions, but to provide a stable
way to solve the steady-state solution using an ODE solver. As the concentrations of all

3.3
Priors
3.3.1
Metabolites
There are two classes of metabolites: balanced and unbalanced. Only unbalanced metabo-
lites have prior distributions, which is defined as a log-normal distribution with a median
of 0.1 mM and a standard deviation of 4 on the log-scale. As mentioned in Section 2.6.1,
the priors on balanced metabolites are inferred by the priors on unbalanced metabolites,
enzyme concentrations, and kinetic parameters - hence their omission. The representa-
tion of the unbalanced metabolite prior is interpreted as the lower 5th percentile having a
concentration of 0.0001 mM and the upper 95th percentile being 72 mM.
3.3.2
Enzymes
As a state parameter of the system enzymes also have priors placed on them. These are
particularly important when enzyme concentrations are unmeasured, and the model can
be regularised to within a reasonable concentration range. For enzyme concentrations
a median value of 0.001 mM was set, with a standard deviation of 5 on the log-scale.
Resulting in a 5th percentile concentration of 2.7 × 10−7 mM and and a 95th percentile of
of 3.73 mM.
3.3.3
Kinetic Parameters
Smallbone et al. measured in vitro kinetic parameters and fit them to experimental data. In
Maud all kinetic parameters are assumed to be log-normally distributed as the parameter
values can never be negative. For binding constants and catalytic rates in Smallbone et
al. where the mechanism matches the modular rate law, a standard deviation of 10 %
of the mean value was assumed , with 95 % of the probability distribution falling within
±20 % of the estimated parameter value. If the mechanism deviates from the assumed
modular rate law mechanism then the binding constant standard deviations are increased
to ±130 %.
Allosteric parameters were treated with more uncertainty, as a slightly different mecha-
nism was assumed. Rather than a standard deviation of 10 % of the mean value, a much
larger 130 % was used.

Results
This chapter is divided into three main parts: ”Internal Model Validation”, where we ensure
that the posterior samples from Stan converge and that the measurements used to fit the
kinetic model are reproduced by the model; ”External Validation”, where we evaluate the
kinetic parameter sets when predicting an external yeast data set that was not used to train
the model; and ”Transition of Regulation”, where we analyse how the control of glycolysis
changes with increasing growth rates.
4.1
Internal Model Validation
There are three stages of model validation required: checking that the posterior samples
are unbiased, confirming that the training data set can be reproduced, and validating the
model against an external condition. This section focuses on the first two.
4.1.1
Sampling Performance
To assess the sampling performance we use the ˆR metric. The sampling statistics indicate
that ˆR ≤1.1 for all parameters, suggesting that all chains converged. However, there were
still divergences with approximately 5 % of transitions resulting in a divergence. Upon
reviewing the parameters, it did not appear that the divergences were concentrated in a
specific region. Decreasing the number of divergences could be improved by increasing
the adapt-delta variable in Stan which would lead to more steps per transition. However,
this would increase the sampling period, which would become infeasible for the current
study.
4.1.2
Experimental Fit
After sampling the posterior distribution, we need to assess how well the samples could
reproduce the measured parameters. This is indicated by the posterior distributions con-
taining the measured value.
Metabolite concentrations
To evaluate the within-sample performance we compare the simulated distributions of
metabolite concentrations with those measured experimentally and present the results in
Fig. 4.1. If the posterior metabolite distributions do not contain the measured metabolite
value, it is suggested that an interaction in the model is missing or the data is not repre-
sentative of the cellular state. Of the metabolites measured, essentially all metabolites
with the exception of fructose 1,6-bisphosphate (fdp) were contained within the posterior
distributions, this suggests that the model is capable of reproducing the training metabolite
conditions, although reactions involving fdp may be unreliable at higher growth rates.
Metabolites that were not measured against calibration curves to obtain absolute con-
centration values, but were measured in terms of relative changes to a reference state,
can still be assessed through the relative changes in the posterior distribution. By se-
lecting the median metabolite concentration value of the metabolites, and using a dilution
rate of 0.05 h−1 as the reference state, the relative metabolites changes in other growth

Fructose 2,6-bisphosphate (f26bp) acts as an allosteric activator for PFK, dramatically

of the current implementation.

4.4
General Discussion
Five of the enzymes in the modelled EMP pathway were primarily controlled by changes
in Gibbs energy, even as fluxes through the pathway increased. This suggests that these
enzymes are over-expressed relative to their biological demand. Out of the 19 enzymes
considered, 11 have a |∆G| ≲4kJ/mol, where at most 70 % of the sum of forward and
reverse fluxes contributes to the net flux, this is across conditions and did not decrease
as fluxes increased. This is supporting the rapid adaption to metabolic perturbations dis-
cussed in Park et al., and was reaffirmed in this study across an order of magnitude of
fluxes in multiple growth conditions.
The model presented in this thesis was capable of reproducing the training data for most
metabolites and fluxes. We were able to calculate the regulatory contribution of thermo-
dynamics and its impact on fluxes, concluding that many of the enzymes operate close
to equilibrium with excess flux capacity. Our findings align well with that of other stud-
ies of thermodynamic control, and supports the hypothesis that yeast glycolysis retains
excess flux capacity (Park, Rubin, et al. 2016; Park, Tanner, et al. 2019). What differ-
entiates this study from those previously conducted on thermodynamic driving forces in
yeast glycolysis, is the in silico approach to reach the conclusion in comparison to the
in vivo experiments in the previously mentioned studies. Furthermore, this kinetic model
was constructed using 14 out of 24 measured metabolites, and 17 out of 19 measured
enzyme concentrations.

5
Conclusions
A kinetic model of yeast glycolysis was trained on carbon limited fluxomics, proteomics,
and metabolomics to understand how metabolism changes as growth rates increase. The
data set used to train the model was incomplete, with only 14 of the 24 metabolites being
measured. This analysis would be infeasible to conduct in most modeling frameworks
using purely experimental results as reactions would have to be lumped.
The model was able to reproduce all fluxes used to fit the model, except for the pyru-
vate drain at a dilution rate of 0.30h−1 and fdp concentrations at dilution rates of 0.22 and
0.30h−1. Furthermore, the relative changes of metabolites that were not absolutely quan-
tified were largely reproduced, with only e4p displaying a trend contrary to the measured
relative differences.
The missing allostery hindered the generality of the model, which is apparent when pre-
dicting the nitrogen limited conditions. As the carbon limited training set compensated for
the missing allostery by varying enzyme concentrations, using the enzyme concentrations
from the nitrogen limited condition resulted in underestimated glycolytic fluxes.
5.1
Yeast’s ability to take advantage of available glucose
In agreement with Park et al., most of the glycolytic flux is regulated by Gibbs energy
changes. This is invariant with growth rate, and hence fluxes increase with increasing di-
lution rate (Park, Tanner, et al. 2019). This suggests that glycolysis has a large excess flux
capacity that is regulated by a few enzymes: PFK and CDC19. Since allosteric activation
of PFK by f26bp was absent, the allosteric activation characteristic of PFK was compen-
sated for by varying the enzyme concentration, this likely had an impact on CDC19, as
fdp is an allosteric activator for both (Smallbone et al. 2013; Dihazi, Kessler, and Eschrich
2004).
The insensitivity of many of the regulatory coefficients to dilution rates suggests an even
larger capacity for flux through glycolysis. This cannot be said for PFK or CDC19 as it is
unknown how, with proper allostery, the free state would interact, however, the question
arises: why are glycolytic enzymes constitutively expressed?
5.2
Are glycolytic enzymes a burden on cellular resources?
Enzymes that are constitutively expressed are a drain on metabolism, ideally, for bio-
engineering solutions the optimal amount of enzyme can be calculated for a pathway
of interest, and the benefit of rapid adaption exhibited in this study will not be required.
Using kinetic models as strain engineering tools, we can minimise the amount of excess
enzyme to divert resources to other pathways of interest. A possible approach would
be to optimise the kinetic model for a defined flux, with the cost of maximising enzyme
efficiency, thus reducing inefficient enzyme expression. The yeast model developed in
this thesis will be invaluable for strain engineering approaches to identify limiting enzymes
and, alternatively, wasteful enzymes.
5.3
Are kinetic models viable for in vivo predictions?
Kinetic models are a continually revitalising field, in the 90s, the promise of improved strain
design using Metabolic Control Analysis revitalised interest in kinetic modelling of cellular
pathways. The approaches used were primarily bottom up, constructing a model using

Future Directions
6.1
Continuation of yeast modelling
Naturally, the extension to remaining sections of metabolism - glucose transporters, hex-
okinase, fermentative pathways, TCA cycle - is the next step of model development. The
control of glycolysis cannot be fully understood without the inclusion of these pathways.
Such extension would also allow for boundary conditions to be specified at the extracel-
lular level. This is beneficial for identifiability as secretion/uptake rates tend to be more
accurate; and allows specification of media conditions, which can be directly altered.
As mentioned throughout the report there is a list of interactions required to improve the
current scope of our model:
1. Phosphorylation of PFK26 and f26bp regulation of PFK;
2. Conserved quantities for atp, adp, and amp;
3. Drains on e4p and pep to account for synthesis of aromatic aminoacids;
4. Drains on 3pg for serine synthesis.
6.2
State of kinetic models
The current direction of kinetic modelling frameworks has a strong focus on maximum
likelihood estimates with few models published about yeast metabolism considering al-
ternate parameter sets. Of the models that consider populations of solutions, there are
few that are implemented conditional on the model likelihood and prior distributions; and
the ones that do use inefficient sampling processes, which are unlikely to sufficiently ex-
plore the posterior distribution. Kinetic models typically rely on relatively few data-points,
which is a disadvantage for top down approaches.
For example, in this thesis the exploration of glycolysis control with varying levels of car-
bon limitation was explored; if the experiment stopped prior to the transition to the Crab-
tree effect the parameters pertinent to PFK activation would not be sufficiently explored.
By drawing on data across studies the predictive capabilities of kinetic models is bound
to increase. Bayesian statics provides an elegant solution to this incremental ”learning”,
whereby previously constructed models provide the prior for the next model. The goal
is to create many possible models, with only the relevant - possible - models being rep-
resented in the posterior distribution. (Gelman and Shalizi 2013) argued that Bayesian
statistics should be viewed as hypothetico-deductive, where model predictions are made
and falsified against experimental data. This corresponds with the principle that ”all mod-
els are wrong but some are useful”, and testing the limitations of the proposed model to
identify what interaction is missing. As an example, the oscillations of yeast glycolysis
are unable to be explored using the current scope, as atp would have to be a balanced
metabolite (Wolf et al. 2000).
Building upon previous scientific work isn’t without its caveats, the quality of third party
data must be evaluated, highlighting the need for proper data availability and accessibility
in easy to use formats. When possible, data should be available in a universal format such
as .csv and the authors should provide technical replicates in addition to their summary
statistics.

The question of how do you communicate the posterior distribution is partially answered
by a recent format called PEtab (Schmiester, Weindl, and Hasenauer 2019). PEtab was
developed for Bayesian models by appending the sampled parameter sets and measure-
ments to an SBML file. To complete the Bayesian analysis workflow, the posterior dis-
tribution should be described using a distribution rather than draws from the posterior,
this issue is not addressed by any kinetic modelling framework to date that accounts for
non-linear relations between parameters.
Even Maud fails to capture the posterior distribution adequately in its current state, to use
Bayes theorem as an iterative process the posterior must be used for the prior of the next
learning phase. This process is suitable for the experimental approach in strain engi-
neering, where potential strains are designed, grown in a bioreactor and sampled. Maud
outputs parameter samples rather than a distribution, which requires post-processing to
fit the posterior with multivariate correlated distribution, as the parameters are established
to be correlated. This problem would be solved by using Variational Bayesian methods,
where the posterior parameter distributions are approximated by a known distribution type
- such as a multivariate log-normal distribution - completing the design-build-test-learn cy-
cle.
