Summary
Sleep-disordered breathing (SDB) is a prevalent condition that significantly im-
pacts public health, yet current diagnostic methods such as full-night polysomnog-
raphy are cumbersome, expensive, and often inaccessible. This thesis presents an
investigation into the development and deployment of lightweight neural network
models for the real-time detection of sleep apnea using pulse oximetry data, with a
focus on implementation on resource-constrained embedded systems. By exploring
various model architectures, quantization strategies—particularly Quantization-
Aware Training (QAT)—and optimization techniques provided by CMSIS-NN li-
braries, our work demonstrates that it is feasible to achieve real-time sleep apnea
screening on microcontroller-based platforms such as the Raspberry Pi Pico.
Our experimental results reveal that quantization-aware training can be used
to achieve real-time inference, operating with inference times as low as 4.79ms and
consuming just 0.33mJ per inference. The model however lacks performance in
terms of accuracy, sensitivity, and specificty when compared to state-of-the-art
models. Analysis suggests that these limitations primarily stem from our prepro-
cessing pipeline and data handling strategies, which include fixed-length sliding
windows and oversampling techniques that may bias the model toward more sever
cases.
Despite these challenges, the substantial reduction in model size to 25.08 kB, in
its quantized form, demonstrates the potential for deploying such models in wear-
able or low-power medical devices. The findings of this work underscore the im-
portance of hardware-software cooptimization and set the stage for future research
to refine data preprocessing, address class imbalance with more sophisticated tech-
niques, and expand beyond single-channel pulse oximetry data. Ultimately, this
thesis contributes to the development of accessible, cost-effective screening tools
that have the potential to bridge the diagnostic gap for sleep-disordered breathing.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Sleep-disordered breathing (SDB) is a common condition that affects millions of
people worldwide and has been increasing in prevalence in recent years [Pep+13].
SDB includes a variety of disorders, such as obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), cen-
tral sleep apnea, and hypopnea. These conditions are characterized by irregular
breathing patterns during sleep, leading to interruptions in airflow.
Untreated
SDB can cause health complications such as cardiovascular disease, hypertension
[Jav+17; Cow+21], diabetes, cognitive impairment [ED04], and Alzheimer’s dis-
ease [Lig+21]. Additionally, it significantly impacts the quality of life, causing
fatigue during the day, reduced productivity, and an increased risk of accidents.
In current clinical practice, initial screening for sleep-disordered breathing of-
ten relies on self-reported questionnaires, such as the Epworth Sleepiness Scale.
While these tools are inexpensive and easy to implement, they depend heavily
on the patient’s subjective perception and memory of their sleep quality, snoring,
and daytime fatigue over several days or weeks. This introduces a high degree of
variability and may lead to underreporting or misclassification, especially in indi-
viduals who are unaware of their symptoms or have become accustomed to chronic
fatigue. As a result, many individuals may be missed or incorrectly assessed, which
can delay medical intervention. An objective, automated screening tool that pas-
sively collects physiological data, such as oxygen saturation, could provide a more
reliable and continuous alternative to questionnaire-based assessments, prompting
users to seek professional evaluation when irregular patterns are detected.
Given this context, there is a growing need for low-cost and accessible screen-
ing methods that can be deployed outside of clinical settings. Wearable devices
equipped with sensors such as photoplethysmographs (PPG) and microphones of-
fer a possible platform for continuous health monitoring.
However, one of the
key challenges in this domain is enabling accurate, real-time inference on resource-
constrained hardware. Most wearable devices rely on ultra-low-power System-on-
Chips (SoCs), such as the ARM Cortex-M0+ and M33, which have very limited
processing capacity and memory.
While previous studies have explored wearable-based detection of sleep-disordered
breathing, relatively few have addressed the challenges of optimizing models specif-
ically for deployment on ultra-low-power microcontrollers. Even fewer have demon-
strated actual deployment on embedded hardware. Most existing work relies on
higher sampling frequency rates (typically 8–16 Hz), which, while potentially im-
proving model accuracy, may also increase energy consumption due to increased
sensor usage and data transmission between the sensor and the SoC. This makes
them not ideal for long-term, battery-powered operation on compact devices like
smart rings or fitness bands. For resource-constrained systems, reducing the sam-

pling frequency is critical not only to lower power draw but also to minimize the
amount of data that needs to be transmitted or processed locally. However, this

This research specifically targets these trade-offs by focusing on low-frequency

The contributions of this work are the following:
• Assessment of the impact of segment length, sampling frequency, and data
overlap on model capabilities
• A lightweight, quantized convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture
optimized for real-time inference on embedded devices.
• An energy and latency benchmark using a microcontroller-class SoC, demon-
strating the feasibility of onboard inference for SDB detection.
By focusing on initial screening rather than diagnosis, this research aims to
bridge the gap between clinical-grade testing and consumer-level monitoring, mak-
ing early detection of SDB more accessible and actionable.

2.1
Medical Background
Sleep-disordered breathing refers to a group of conditions characterized by abnor-
mal respiratory patterns during sleep, which often lead to disrupted sleep cycles,
decreased blood oxygen saturation, and daytime fatigue. The two main groups of
SDBs are Apnea and Hypopnea conditions, where apnea refers to the absence of
airflow and hypopnea refers to a decrease in airflow. Both of them can be split
into three main conditions:
• Obstructive Sleep Apnea/Hypopnea occurs when the airway is blocked by
different degrees, e.g., the tongue falling backward, causing complete(apnea)
or partial(hypopnea) stoppage of breathing activity. The OSA syndrome is
the most common SDB and affects millions of people worldwide. It may be
often associated with hypertension, snoring, and daytime sleepiness.
• Central Sleep Apnea/Hypopnea (CSA) occurs due to impaired respiratory
control in the brainstem. The brain fails to send proper signals to the res-
piratory muscles, causing them to malfunction. CSA is more common in
patients with heart failure or neurological disorders.
• Lastly, there is the Complex or Mixed Sleep Apnea, a combination of OSA
and CSA.
In all cases, an abnormal breathing event must last for at least ten seconds
to be considered significant. It may be accompanied by electroencephalographic
arousal and a drop in blood oxygen saturation.
An example of readings can be seen in Figure 2.1. Oxygen levels are fairly
stable during normal breathing until apnea or hypopnea occurs. It is worth noting
that oxygen desaturation does not occur instantaneously but instead takes a few
seconds to reach its lowest point, even after the patient returns to normal breathing.

Patients are screened before undergoing expensive sleep studies using tools such
as questionnaires, physical examinations, and symptom-based assessments. One
example of such a tool is the Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS), a widely used ques-
tionnaire that assesses daytime sleepiness by asking patients about their likelihood
of falling asleep or dozing off in eight different situations, such as watching TV or
sitting in a car. Scores range from 0 to 24, with a score higher than 10 suggesting
excessive sleepiness and possible SDB.
Lastly, the Polysomnography exam is the golden standard for SDB diagnosis.
It is an overnight sleep study conducted in a sleep lab or in a hospital where
multiple physiological signals are recorded, including:
• Electroencephalography (EEG): Monitors brain waves to assess sleep stages.
• Electromyography (EMG): Measures muscle activity, including jaw move-
ment and leg twitching.
• Electrooculography (EOG): Tracks eye movements to differentiate REM and
non-REM sleep.
• Respiratory Sensors: Detect airflow obstructions and respiratory effort.
• Pulse Oximetry: Tracks oxygen desaturation events.
• Electrocardiography (ECG): Monitors heart rate variability, which can indi-
cate stress responses to apneic episodes.
Experts then analyze and annotate the data to generate a comprehensive anal-
ysis of sleep structure, breathing disturbances, and oxygen desaturation, leading
to an Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI) score. The AHI quantifies the severity based
on the number of abnormal events per hour of sleep.
A healthy individual’s normal score is below 5 AHI. Between 5 and 15 AHI,
the patient is considered to have mild OSA. Moderate OSA falls between 15 and
30 AHI, and severe OSA is defined as anything above 30 AHI.

2.2
Deep Learning Background
Deep Learning [LBH15] is a subfield of Machine Learning that utilizes Artificial
Neural Networks to learn patterns and features from data automatically. Unlike
many traditional machine learning approaches that require manual feature extrac-
tion, deep learning models can work directly on raw or minimally processed input.
This allows them to uncover complex and hierarchical representations that would
be diﬀicult to define explicitly.
A neural network consists of layers of interconnected nodes, each of which ap-
plies a mathematical transformation followed by a nonlinear activation function.
By composing many such layers, deep networks can model highly nonlinear rela-
tionships between input and output.
2.2.1
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are widely used types of neural networks
suitable for various applications, including image classification, video recognition,
and analyzing physiological signals. A typical CNN has three types of layers:
• Convolutional layers apply kernels with learnable weights to its input in
order to detect local features based on its receptive field.
• Pooling layers reduce spatial dimensions by summarizing outputs from the
previous layer. This downsampling operation helps achieve spatial invariance
while reducing computational complexity. The two most common pooling op-
erations are Max-pooling and Average-pooling, which extract the maximum
or average value, respectively, from local regions of the feature maps.
• Fully Connected layers connect every neuron in one layer to every neuron
in the subsequent layer, similar to traditional Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs).
These layers typically appear toward the end of the network architecture and
are responsible for high-level reasoning based on the features extracted by
earlier layers.
A key property of CNNs is their ability to achieve shift invariance, enabling the
network to recognize patterns regardless of their position within the input data.
This is accomplished by combining convolutional layers with pooling layers, which
is crucial for pattern recognition tasks.
2.2.2
Training and Optimization
Training a neural network involves finding a set of weights that minimize a loss
function, which measures the difference between predicted outputs and true labels.
This is achieved using optimization algorithms like Stochastic Gradient Descent
(SGD) or its variants, such as Adam, through a procedure called backpropagation.
During backpropagation, the gradients of the loss function concerning each weight
are computed using the chain rule of derivatives and propagated backward through

the network. These gradients guide how the weights should be updated to improve
the model’s accuracy.
In deep architectures, gradients can become extremely small as they are prop-
agated backward through many layers. This is known as the vanishing gradient
problem. When gradients are very small, weights in earlier layers receive minimal
updates, effectively stopping the network from learning.
To avoid overfitting, where the model performs well on training data but poorly
on unseen data, various regularization techniques are applied, such as dropout (ran-
domly deactivating neurons during training) and weight decay (penalizing large
weights). The model’s performance is typically evaluated using validation data,
and techniques such as early stopping can halt training when the validation per-
formance stops improving.
2.2.3
Activation Functions and Nonlinearity
Activation functions calculate a node’s output based on its inputs and the weights
associated with those inputs. They introduce nonlinearity into neural networks,
allowing them to approximate complex, real-world functions. Without nonlinearity,
stacking multiple layers would result in a model that behaves like a single linear
transformation, limiting its expressiveness.
Common activation functions include the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU), which
outputs the input if positive and zero otherwise, and the logistic sigmoid, which
squashes the input into a range between 0 and 1. ReLU is computationally eﬀicient
and has become the default choice in many deep-learning models due to its ability
to mitigate vanishing gradient problems during training. The sigmoid, on the other
hand, is often used in binary classification tasks where outputs must represent
probabilities. However, it can suffer from saturation at extreme values, leading to
vanishing gradients. Figure 2.2 shows a plot of both functions..

2.2.4
Loss Function and Evaluation Metrics
For binary classification tasks such as apnea detection, Binary Cross-Entropy
(BCE) is a commonly used loss function. It measures the distance between the
predicted probability ˆy ∈[0, 1] and the true class label y ∈{0, 1}, and is defined
as:
LBCE = −(y · log(ˆy) + (1 −y) · log(1 −ˆy))
This function penalizes incorrect predictions that are made with high confi-
dence and is particularly suitable for probabilistic, single output model.
Beyond minimizing loss, evaluating a model’s performance requires metrics
that better reflect classification quality, especially in the context of imbalanced
datasets. Common metrics include:
• Accuracy, the proportion of correct predictions over all predictions:
Accuracy =
TP + TN
TP + TN + FP + FN
where TP = true positives, TN = true negatives, FP = false positives, and
FN = false negatives.
• Precision, the proportion of true positive predictions among all predicted
positives:
Precision =
TP
TP + FP
• Recall (also known as sensitivity), the proportion of actual positives cor-
rectly identified:
Recall =
TP
TP + FN
• F1-Score, the harmonic mean of precision and recall:
F1 = 2 · Precision · Recall
Precision + Recall
• Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC), which measures the probability
that a randomly chosen positive instance is ranked higher than a randomly
chosen negative one. An AUC of 1.0 indicates perfect classification, while an
AUC of 0.5 corresponds to random guessing.
In this work, AUC, precision, and recall are prioritized over accuracy due
to the clinical importance of correctly identifying abnormal respiratory events.
High recall ensures that apneic events are rarely missed, while high precision helps
prevent excessive false alarms.

2.3
TinyML
Machine learning on embedded devices is challenging due to numerous constraints,
including latency, energy, and computational requirements. In 2018, the TinyML
concept began to take shape, and efforts have since increased to deploy optimized
machine learning models on low-powered and resource-constrained hardware. Ac-
cording to [Cap+24], the hardware requirements for TinyML models are a memory
footprint below 1MB, with most models falling between 64 and 256kB of memory.
Additionally, the SoCs and processors in these systems typically operate at clock
frequencies ranging from 40 to 400 MHz.
Currently, TinyML is being applied in various fields, including IoT and smart
cities, environmental challenges, predictive maintenance, anomaly detection, and
healthcare. For example, in [Tso+21], the authors conduct a review of tinyML
for the healthcare problem space. They discuss various applications, such as fall
detection and prediction for elderly people, and also examine the most commonly
used development boards within the healthcare ecosystem. Additionally, there are
many libraries and platforms available for use, such as TensorFlow-Lite Micro and
CMSIS-NN.
TinyML has three distinct workflow approaches. The traditional ML-oriented
approach, where experts train, test, optimize, and deploy the model to its target.
The hardware-oriented approach, where target hardware is developed to produce
highly optimized and very resource-eﬀicient models. This hardware includes ASICs
and FPGAs. Lastly, a newer co-design approach, where ML experts and hardware
engineers collaborate from the start to the end of the project to develop both the
software and hardware required for the problem domain. This research focuses on
the traditional ML-oriented approach for the healthcare problem domain.
2.4
Quantization
Quantization is an optimization step that reduces the precision of numerical values
in a model, often converting 32-bit floating-point weights and activations into lower-
bit formats, such as 16-bit floats or 8-bit integers. This compression significantly
reduces memory requirements and, in most cases, also reduces inference latency
and computational loads, enabling real-time execution on microcontrollers with
limited processing power.
However, quantization introduces trade-offs between
model size, speed, and prediction accuracy. In this project, we will evaluate the
following quantization techniques:
2.4.1
Post-Training Quantization (PTQ)
Full integer quantization converts both weights and activations( and even in-
puts/outputs if required) to 8-bit integers. This approach maximizes the model’s
eﬀiciency on integer-only hardware but can lead to a more substantial accuracy
drop if not properly calibrated. It requires a representative dataset to capture
the range and distribution of activations during quantization, ensuring that the
fixed-point representations accurately capture the dynamic range of the inputs.
==PAGE:19==
2.4 Quantization
9
This 8-bit quantization approximates floating-point values using the following
formula:
real_value = (int8_value −zero_point) × scale
In which the zero-point and the scale are determined using our representative
dataset.
Weights are symmetric, meaning that they have their zero-point = 0
and are represented by the int8 two’s complement value between [-127, 127]. In
contrast, activations and inputs are represented as an int8 two’s complement value
between [-128, 127] and are asymmetric, meaning the zero point can be anywhere
within the range of [-128, 127].
Dynamic Range Quantization, on the other hand, quantizes only the model
weights to 8 bits while leaving activations in floating-point, reducing the model size
without significantly impacting accuracy. It is simple to apply and often provides
a good balance between compression and accuracy. However, it can still involve
floating-point arithmetic, which may limit its effectiveness on devices without a
hardware floating-point unit. This option is considered the default quantization
option in the TensorFlow-Lite library, but it currently has limited support on
microcontrollers.
There are other variants, such as quantization for 16-bit floating-point numbers
to maintain more accuracy while still reducing size, as well as mixed-precision tech-
niques like int16 activations and int8 weights. These, however, still have limited
support on some microcontrollers as of today.
2.4.2
Quantization-Aware Training (QAT)
Quantization-aware training involves simulating quantization effects during model
training, allowing the model to adapt to the reduced precision. This approach
often achieves higher post-quantization accuracy compared to PTQ, as the model
learns to compensate for the reduced numerical precision during backpropagation.
QAT can be particularly beneficial for deeper neural networks, where the cumula-
tive effect of quantization error can be more pronounced. An example workflow
for tackling QAT on a library like TensorFlow involves first training the model nor-
mally and then applying all the required quantization annotations to the model.
Lastly, we fine-tune the model by quantizing it and re-training it for a few epochs
to achieve better metrics than a PTQ model.

Related Works
[Men+18] discusses various approaches for detecting Obstructive Sleep Apnea
(OSA), emphasizing traditional signal processing techniques and machine learning-
based methods. It highlights the use of signals such as ECG, PPG, airflow, and
SpO2, detailing feature extraction and classification techniques. Classical methods
include spectral and statistical analyses, while recent studies describe deep learn-
ing and other machine learning models. The review also explores challenges in
deploying OSA detection on wearable devices, including power constraints, data
variability, and the need for robust real-time performance. The work also high-
lights that models using ECG data are the most widely spread and the most
accurate.
[Xu+22] conducts a review of automated detection mechanisms and models for
different kinds of sleep disorders, including OSA and hypopnea. Their findings
show that most of the research conducted is for breathing disorders, and these are
still dominated by traditional machine learning techniques. The most common
method used for detections are SVMs, while for Deep Learning the most common
approaches are using CNNs. The best models are able to achieve more than 99%
accuracy.
[Kor+24] Discusses research perspectives and reviews on sleep-disordered breath-
ing, including symptoms, consequences, diagnosis, and technological solutions for
detection. It mentions how current wearable technology already tracks physiologi-
cal signals that can be of use to assist on SDB detection, including the use of PPG
signals. These things combined help on the initial assessment and monitoring of
the condition.
[RDK21] propose an automated approach for detecting obstructive sleep ap-
nea (OSA) using a single-lead ECG signal. The study employs discrete wavelet
transform (DWT) to extract statistical features from segmented ECG subbands,
followed by feature selection using particle swarm optimization (PSO). A random
forest classifier achieves high accuracy, with 89% and 90% using 50-50 hold-out
validation and 10-fold cross-validation, respectively. The study demonstrates that
wavelet-based statistical features enhance OSA detection performance, offering a
computationally eﬀicient and non-invasive alternative to traditional polysomnogra-
phy. These findings are relevant to this thesis, as they highlight feature extraction
and classification techniques suitable for low-power embedded systems.
[Kim+25] introduce SleepWatcher, a deep learning-based system designed for
detecting Sleep Apnea/Hypopnea Syndrome (SAHS) on wearable devices. The
study addresses key challenges such as class imbalance in medical datasets and re-
liance on handcrafted features. SleepWatcher employs heart rate variability (HRV)
and blood oxygen saturation (SpO￿) signals, converting them into two-dimensional

[Liu+24] introduce DeepApnea, a deep learning system to detect sleep apnea
by analyzing wrist movement data from smartwatches. It can identify three types
of apnea events: central apneas, obstructive apneas, and hypopneas. They intro-
duce signal preprocessing techniques to extract respiratory-related features from
accelerometer (ACC) data while mitigating noise. The model incorporates self-
attention and cross-axis correlation techniques to enhance feature extraction from
multi-axis ACC signals. Evaluation results from a clinical study demonstrate that
DeepApnea outperforms other existing methods, achieving high accuracy across
different apnea types.
[Pat+17] explore the feasibility of using single-sensor techniques for sleep apnea
detection by leveraging deep learning models. The study focuses on using either In-
stantaneous Heart Rate (IHR) or blood oxygen saturation (SpO￿) to classify apnea
and non-apnea events. The authors employ Long Short-Term Memory Recurrent
Neural Networks (LSTM-RNN), achieving an Area Under the Curve(AUC) of 0.98
using SpO2 and 0.99 using IHR.
[Laz+20] propose a method for detecting and classifying sleep apnea and hypop-
nea using photoplethysmography (PPG) and peripheral oxygen saturation (SpO￿)
signals. Their approach consists of two primary components: detecting reductions

4.1
Model Architecture
Our proposed architecture is similar to the convolutional neural network design in-
troduced in [Joh+21], with modifications to better suit binary classification. Specif-
ically, we replace the final softmax layer with a sigmoid activation in an attempt
to simplify the detection to a binary problem.
The input to the model consists solely of oxygen saturation (SpO2) signals,
represented as a single-channel time series. Input dimensions vary depending on
the dataset configuration. For instance, at a sampling rate of 1Hz and an 11-second
segment , the input shape is (11,1,1)
The network comprises three 1D convolutional layers with ReLU activations,
interleaved with max-pooling and dropout layers to reduce overfitting. The model
concludes with two dense layers followed by a sigmoid output neuron. A schematic
of the model architecture is shown in Figure 4.1.

The loss function is binary cross-entropy, reflecting the dichotomous nature
of our classification problem (typical vs. abnormal). In addition to the loss, we
monitor multiple performance metrics during training:
• Accuracy: Measures the overall proportion of correctly classified segments.
• AUC (Area Under the Curve): Evaluates the model’s ability to distinguish
between classes.
• Precision: The proportion of predicted abnormal segments that are actually
abnormal.
• Recall: The proportion of actual abnormal segments correctly identified.
• F1 Score: The harmonic mean of precision and recall, which balances the
impact of both false positives and false negatives—particularly relevant for
imbalanced datasets.
The model training also includes two key callbacks to improve generalization
and training eﬀiciency:
• EarlyStopping: Stops training when validation loss does not improve for 20
consecutive epochs. It also restores the model weights from the epoch with
the best validation performance.
• ReduceLROnPlateau: Reduces the learning rate by a factor of 0.5 if valida-
tion loss stagnates for 5 epochs, with a lower bound set at 1 × 10−5

4.4
Class Imbalance
All generated datasets are highly imbalanced, containing significantly more nor-
mal segments than abnormal ones. To mitigate this issue, we employed a static
oversampling strategy for simplicity and consistency across different dataset config-
urations in the ablation study. Each abnormal segment is duplicated three times,
and a small amount of Gaussian noise is added to the duplicates to introduce mi-
nor variability while maintaining physiological plausibility. This duplication factor
remains fixed regardless of the dataset’s class distribution, prioritizing implemen-
tation simplicity over optimal balancing.
To further counteract the imbalance during training, we apply custom class
weights, assigning a weight of 2.0 to abnormal segments and 1.0 to normal ones.
These values were selected arbitrarily to emphasize the importance of detecting
abnormal events, which are clinically more relevant. We acknowledge, however,
that these parameters may not be optimal, and further research should explore
dynamic or data-driven strategies for oversampling and class weighting to improve
model performance and generalization.

We explored five quantization strategies:
• Post-training dynamic range quantization
• Quantization-aware training
• Post-training full 8-bit int quantization
• Post-training int16 activations and int8 weights quantization
• Post-training float16 quantization
For the 16x8 and int8 quantized models, the entire training set was used as
the representative dataset for quantization. This approach was selected due to
the relatively small size of the training data, which mitigates the risk of memory
constraints during the quantization process.
Using the whole training set also
ensures a more comprehensive capture of the data distribution seen during model
training, potentially reducing quantization-induced accuracy loss.
Additionally,
this method simplifies the implementation, avoiding the need for complex sampling

inference run returns both the prediction and latency to the host machine for
evaluation. Given the sliding window approach used in our preprocessing, we im-
plemented a simple apnea-hypopnea index (AHI) calculation to reduce the risk of
double-counting overlapping events. This method tracks the minimum and maxi-
mum number of detected abnormal events as follows:
• Minimum Event Count: Incremented only when an abnormal event (1) fol-
lows a normal event (0), indicating the likely start of a new apnea or hypop-
nea event.
• Maximum Event Count: Incremented for every detected abnormal event (1),
providing an upper bound on the number of distinct apnea events.

For example, given a prediction sequence like:
010110
• The first 1 is counted as both a minimum and maximum event.
• The second 1 is also counted as both a minimum and maximum event, as it
is preceded by a 0.
• The third 1 is counted only as a maximum event, as it is part of a potentially
overlapping sequence with the previous 1.
While this approach is straightforward, it has some limitations. It assumes
that overlapping segments always correspond to the same event, and it lacks tem-
poral context. This approach lacks consideration of event duration and the space
between each event, which can lead to overestimation or underestimation if events
are closely spaced but distinct. We provide detailed explanations and comments
on the results in Chapter 5.

Additionally, to assess real-time power draw during inference, we utilized the

measurements. The device supports a Battery Emulator mode that mimics the

To perform the power measurements, we built a simple circuit in which the

terminals of the Keysight N6705C power analyzer. We included an external LED

active peripherals. Since the board lacks an external oscillator, we configured a

bouncing, which cannot be handled in software while the device is in sleep mode,
we included a 0.1 µF capacitor for hardware debouncing.

Results
The results presented in this chapter provide a comprehensive evaluation of the
proposed sleep apnea detection model across multiple quantization strategies and
hardware platforms. Metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and AUC are used
to assess model performance. At the same time, memory usage and latency are

5.1
Ablation Study Results
The complete set of experimental results is presented in Appendix B, while a
condensed summary is provided in Table 5.1 for clarity. Several key observations
can be drawn from these results.
First, increasing the sampling frequency does not consistently improve model
performance. This counterintuitive outcome may be attributed to the introduc-
tion of additional noise and complex, high-frequency features that are not neces-
sarily informative for detecting apnea events. In contrast, longer segment lengths
significantly enhance detection metrics. This is likely because blood oxygen desat-
uration does not occur instantaneously, together with an apnea event. Instead, it
often takes several seconds to reach a minimum level, even after normal breath-
ing resumes. Longer segments capture these delayed desaturation patterns more
effectively, providing the model with a more complete picture of the physiological
response to apnea.
It is also worth noting that some larger models failed to train successfully,
particularly those with the 60s @ 4Hz, 60s @ 8Hz, 20s @ 16Hz, and 60s @ 16Hz
configurations. This may be due to the aggressive class weighting applied dur-
ing training to counteract the significant class imbalance in the dataset. With
larger models and higher sampling rates, the increased complexity and noise may
overwhelm the training process, leading to poor convergence.
Given the heavily imbalanced nature of the dataset, traditional metrics like
overall accuracy are less informative for evaluating model performance. Instead,
we prioritize the area under the ROC curve (AUC) as our primary evaluation
metric, as it better captures the trade-off between true positive and false positive
rates. Additionally, we prioritize recall over precision, reflecting our design goal
of maximizing the detection of true apnea events, even at the expense of a higher
false-positive rate. This approach aligns with our intended use case, where the

of 1 Hz. This configuration offers one of the highest AUC values among all tested
models while maintaining relatively low memory requirements, making it more
suitable for deployment on embedded hardware. Despite this, the model’s recall
of 79.7% and precision of 44% remain below reliable standards for screening tools.
These limitations suggest that further improvements are necessary, particularly in
the preprocessing and data-balancing strategies used in this work.
The current preprocessing pipeline utilizes fixed-length sliding windows with
overlap and employs a straightforward min-max normalization. While effective
for basic feature extraction, this approach may fail to adequately capture subtle
temporal patterns or context across segments, which are essential for detecting
complex or borderline apnea events.
Additionally, the oversampling technique
employed may have failed to produce suﬀicient variability, limiting generalization
to unseen data.
Future work should consider more sophisticated preprocessing methods (e.g.,
contextual sequence modeling, learned feature encoders) and explore alternative
data balancing strategies, such as synthetic minority over-sampling (SMOTE) or
generative methods, to improve recall without severely compromising precision.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis has presented an investigation into the development and deployment of
lightweight neural network models for sleep apnea detection using pulse oximetry
data on resource-constrained embedded systems. Through experimentation across
model architecture, quantization strategies, and hardware platforms, we have
demonstrated the feasibility of real-time sleep apnea screening on microcontroller-
based devices while identifying key areas for future improvement.
6.1
Model Development and Optimization
Our ablation study revealed that increasing the sampling frequency does not con-
sistently enhance model performance. Higher frequencies may introduce additional
noise and non-informative features, which can degrade generalization. However,
using longer segment lengths proved more effective, as they better capture delayed
physiological responses such as oxygen desaturation, which are indicative of sleep
apnea events.
In addition, our quantization experiments confirmed the viability of model
compression for deployment on constrained hardware. By applying Quantization-
Aware Training (QAT), we were able to maintain predictive performance while
converting models to eﬀicient 8-bit integer representations. This approach yielded
a compact model achieving 73.2% accuracy and an AUC of 0.84, validating that
real-time inference is possible on microcontroller-class devices without a significant
trade-off in performance.
6.2
Clinical Relevance and Generalization
Our Patient-specific evaluation provided additional insight into the model’s behav-
ior across different demographics and apnea severity levels. The model consistently
performed better on patients with severe sleep apnea, possibly due to the more
distinct physiological patterns associated with these cases. In contrast, detection
was less reliable in milder cases, where signals may be subtler and more diﬀicult
to distinguish.
==PAGE:48==
38
6 Conclusion
6.3
Hardware Deployment and Performance
Deployment on both Raspberry Pi Pico platforms confirmed the practical viability
of our proposed solution for real-time, on-device detection of sleep apnea. Lever-
aging the CMSIS-NN optimization libraries resulted in substantial performance
gains, achieving up to a 14x speedup on the Pico 2. These optimizations enabled
inference times as low as 4.79 ms per segment, with energy consumption as low as
0.33 mJ per inference.
This ensures that the model can operate realistically without interfering with
other potential system functions, such as wireless data transmission, concurrent
sensor usage, or additional on-device inference tasks (e.g., sleep stage classification).
These results demonstrate that real-time, low-power sleep monitoring is feasible
on ultra-constrained embedded systems.
6.4
Limitations
While the chosen QAT model demonstrates the feasibility of deploying sleep ap-
nea detection on constrained hardware, its detection performance remains limited.
Specifically, the model achieves 73.5% accuracy, 80.7% recall, and only 41.9%
precision, which is well below state-of-the-art approaches(87-97% accuracy). Al-
though this level of recall may be acceptable for our intended purpose, it would
still be beneficial to have additional tools to evaluate each patient more effectively.
Also, the high false positive rate significantly limits its applicability as a standalone
diagnostic tool.
Several factors contribute to this performance gap.
First, the current pre-
processing pipeline, based on fixed-length sliding windows and simple min-max
normalization, may fail to capture the subtle temporal patterns and context re-
quired for identifying borderline or mild apnea events. Additionally, the oversam-
pling strategy used to address class imbalance, which involves duplicating apnea
segments with added noise, failed to introduce suﬀicient variability to enhance
generalization to unseen data.
The aggressive use of class weighting, while effective at boosting recall, intro-
duced a strong bias toward positive predictions. This led to elevated false positive
rates across all patients, reducing precision and overall accuracy. A more balanced
approach or more advanced augmentation strategies may be required to mitigate
this trade-off.
Demographic imbalance in the dataset also emerged as a notable limitation.
The model performed particularly poorly on female patients, very likely due to
their underrepresentation during the training process. The possible presence of
physiological variation between male and female SpO2 patterns means that our
model may have failed to capture it, emphasizing the need for a more demograph-
ically comprehensive study.
Additionally, the model underperformed in detecting mild cases of sleep apnea.
These cases may be more challenging to identify due to potentially subtler phys-
iological changes. Also, the oversampling of more severe apnea events may have
==PAGE:49==
6.5 Future Work
39
introduced an unintended bias during training, making the model less sensitive to
milder patterns and thereby reducing its clinical utility for detecting mild apnea.
Finally, this work does not address the challenges posed by faulty sensor read-
ings, noise caused by motion, or other sources of signal degradation common in
wearable devices.
However, we acknowledge that in real-world scenarios, such
factors are frequent and can significantly impact performance, warranting future
investigation into signal quality assessment and robust noise-handling strategies.
6.5
Future Work
Future research should focus on enhancing both the data preprocessing pipeline
and model generalization capabilities to address the limitations identified in this
work.
Generating apnea segments using variable-length windows rather than fixed-
length ones could introduce greater variability and better capture diverse apnea
patterns. This may improve the model’s ability to detect both mild and atypical
events.
Furthermore, more advanced strategies to combat class imbalance should be
investigated. For example, utilizing generative adversarial networks (GANs) to
synthesize new apnea segments could provide a more diverse training set, which
may help generalize unseen data.
Furthermore, expanding beyond single-channel pulse oximetry could signifi-
cantly boost detection performance. Possible directions include:
• Multi-sensor approach: Incorporating complementary physiological sig-
nals, such as heart rate variability (HRV) derived from PPG data, could
provide a more comprehensive view of sleep physiology.
• Personalized models: Introducing patient-specific calibration methods or
adaptive learning strategies may help account for individual physiological dif-
ferences, particularly across underrepresented groups such as female patients
or those with mild apnea.
Overall, combining improvements in data diversity, signal quality, and model
personalization is likely to yield more robust and clinically valuable screening tools
suitable for deployment in real-world wearable systems.
