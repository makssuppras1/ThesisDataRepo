Abstract
Estimation of reference points in data limited stocks is a diﬃcult task due to the lack of information, even
though it is of immediate concern for the sustainable exploitation of marine resources. This report examines
three stocks in the Mediterranean Sea which fall under the category of data-poor, by investigating methods
speciﬁcally designed to work under these limitations.
Length based indicators, length spawning potential per recruit and mean length estimator were the methods
explored with the length distributions and the respective biological parameters of Sardina pilchardus (sardine),
Engraulis encrasicolus (anchovy) and Parapenaeus longirostris (deep rose shrimp), located in diﬀerent sub-
areas in the Mediterranean Sea. The reference points derived from each method were examining the state
of the stock in respect to recruitment capabilities (length based spawning potential ratio), levels of ﬁshing
mortality (mean length estimator) and sustainability (length based indicators). The length distributions,
derived from the landings, were further manipulated by using the simple moving average method and in some
cases by removing noisy counterparts.
The stocks were assessed as overexploited in most cases, even though the results varied between methods
and stock. The uncertainty included in the required biological parameters and/or the quality of the data is
preventing the estimated reference points from use in management advice. Sensitivity analysis was conducted
on the various parameters that were used as inputs for each method. It was found that the life-history
parameter L∞was the most inﬂuential for all occasions, rendering the results unreliable.

Introduction
Fishing is an ancient activity with traces of it dated back to 8000 BC. For many coastal and riparian
civilizations ﬁshing was practiced daily as it would constitute their principal source of protein (Miller, Mann,
and Cordain 2009). With the passage of time, ﬁshing evolved by inventing new techniques and gears or
improving the existing ones. Entering the industrial revolution era, the technological advancements had a
signiﬁcant eﬀect on the ﬁsheries sector, by further expanding, among others, the capability of ﬁshing vessels
in terms of proximity and speed allowing an increased catch (FAO 2016b).
Nowadays capture ﬁsheries production amounts to 93.4 million tones globally in 2014, with marine production
representing approximately 87% of the total catch (State of World Fisheries and Aquaculture (FAO 2016a).
Globally, the importance of ﬁsheries can be translated in employment opportunities, food supplies, livelihood,
recreational activities etc. The signiﬁcance can be further ampliﬁed when taking into account developing
countries or countries in which ﬁshing signiﬁcantly contributes to their gross domestic product; ﬁsheries
provide the coastal populations with food and livelihood security.
Why manage ﬁsheries?
Natural resources aren’t inexhaustible and unfortunately ﬁsheries cannot be excluded. According to FAO
(FAO 2016a) up to 2005, 52% of the marine ﬁsh stocks were fully exploited, whereas 17% and 7% were
overexploited and depleted respectively . The European Commission addressed this issue by forging the
Common Fisheries Policy (CFP), ensuring the regulation of the ﬁshing activity within the European Union
(EU) waters, from the sea to the consumer (EC 2013). The overall objective of the CFP is to safeguard the
ﬁnancial, environmental and social sustainability of marine resources. Precisely, criteria are set to establish
a sustainable level of ﬁshing pressure and reproductive capacity of the stock, as well as to assure that the
unimpaired state of the stocks will be maintained or rebuilt at these levels (European Comission 2010). These
criteria, or best described as indicators, are subsequently used by experts to provide useful management
advice in regards to maintaining a good ecological status of the stock in concern while ensuring the welfare of
the stakeholders. Even though in many cases ﬁsheries management addressed the issue of overexploitation
through the control of the exploitation rates of ﬁsh stocks (Costello et al. 2012; Fernandes and Cook 2013;
Worm et al. 2009), while failures can be attributed, among others, in the availability of quantitative and
qualitative data required for a pertinent and analytical stock assessment and additionally methods that
consider the limitations of data-poor ﬁsheries. Analytical stock assessment is are mathematical models
utilizing biological characteristics from ﬁshery dependent and independent data resulting in the estimation of
reference points used for management purposes.
Data poor or data limited ﬁsheries can be deﬁned as the stocks that are lacking analytical stock assessments due
to uncertainty and (or) availability regarding the quantity and quality of the data. The management decisions
in this case are based on empirical indicators or correlation of these indicators to an established reference
point for management purposes (Dowling et al. 2015). Implications in the formulation of management plans,
arising from limited availability of information can be found around the world, constituting the majority of
stocks exploited globally (Costello et al. 2012). In Europe, the implementation of the CFP has had diﬀerent
results. In the North east Atlantic recovery of stocks previously exploited unsustainable has been observed
and associated with the 2002 CFP eﬀort control (Fernandes and Cook 2013). north cod The International
Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES) provides scientiﬁc advice on the allowable ﬁshing eﬀort, through
catches, by relating it to Spawning Stock Biomass and ensuring that the production of the stock falls under

the precautionary approach concept (ICES 2016). The southern counterpart, on the contrary, is experiencing
decline in the vast majority of stocks. In the Mediterranean 95% of the stocks with an oﬃcial assessment are
considered overexploited, whereas 75% are lacking assessments (Osio, Orio, and Millar 2015).
The Mediterranean Sea is characterized by a narrow continental shelf in most of the regions, low productivity
(excluding the Adriatic Sea, the Gulf of Lions and Gabes) and rich biodiversity ((EEA) 1999).The total
number of vessels approximated for the Mediterranean and the Black sea for all the adjacent countries
amounts to 92370 vessels, with 88% belonging only in the Mediterranean (FAO 2016b). The majority of
ﬁshing activity in the Mediterranean, in regards to EU countries is performed by vessels smaller than 12 m,
otherwise termed small-scale ﬁsheries (SSF), with Greece, Italy, Spain, France and Portugal accounting for
67% of SSFs in total (Macfadyen, Salz, and Cappell 2011).
Figure 1: Geographical subareas of the Black Sea and the Mediterranean.
Even though SSFs have a high socio-economic importance for the area, they are hard to monitor due to the
abundance of ports and ﬁshing vessels (Quetglas et al. 2016). SSFs are usually operated without a towed
gear but vessels using active gear such as sail trawling have been in force in the Mediterranean since 1700s
resulting in immense exploitation of the stocks (Osio 2012). The total amount of vessels reported ﬁshing in
the Mediterranean and the Black Sea are estimated at 92.700, although it is considered an underestimation
because several areas are undereported. SSFs in the region amount to 80%, trawlers to 8%, purse seiners
and pelagic trawlers 5% and others as 7% (FAO 2016b). Anchovy and sardine are dominating the catches
in this area (FAO 2016a). Regarding management measures in the Mediterranean, all ﬁsheries, with the
exception of the Bluﬁn tuna, swordﬁsh and small pelagic ﬁsheries (recently set catch limits) in the Adriatic
sea, are managed by input controls (i.e. restrictions on the number of ﬁshing units, the amount of time that
6
==PAGE:7==
can be spend in the sea and restrictions on the size of the vessels and/or gear), minimum conservation sizes,
closed areas and closed seasons. Moreover, regulating the time spend in the sea has proven ineﬀective; in
several areas around Italy, although ﬁshing is permitted only 5 days per week, the ﬂeet capacity has remained
unchanged in a level that is not sustainable. The input controls are attributed to the characteristics of the
Mediterranean Sea; its ﬁsheries are identiﬁed by a higher diversity of species and a narrower continental self,
as well as the importance of the SSFs (Hadjimichael, Edwards-Jones, and Kaiser 2010; Papaconstantinou
and Farrugio 2000). Primary production is also considered an important factor aﬀecting the dynamics of
ﬁsheries in the Mediterranean region with bottom up processes controlling the productivity (Piroddi et al.
2017). Nevertheless, shortfalls on the implementation of the CFP have spatial variation between the north
and the south, attributed to diverse sources. Political reasons as well as the nonessential sharing of the
EEZ (exclusive economic zone) in the Mediterranean, delayed the implementation of relevant legislations in
regards to conservation and management of the resources; the ﬁrst EU level regulation was introduced in 1994
approximately 10 years later than the implementation of the original CFP in the northern part of Europe.
Additionally, many ﬁsheries in the Mediterranean are jointly exploited by the Community and countries that
are not part of the EU. According to a review assessing the implementation of the management plans of
the EU states, no joint implementations of management plans has taken place in the Mediterranean up to
2013, although 23 countries (9 EU and 14 non-EU) are members of the General Fisheries Commission for
the Mediterranean, a regional ﬁsheries management organization for the Mediterranean and the Black Sea.
Turkey, Italy, Spain and Algeria are responsible for the majority of catches in these areas with the share
amounting to 31%, 16%, 7% and 7% respectively. On a national level, the slow progress in the implementation
of the management plans, as well as the constant amendments of laws over the past years has led to an increase
in infringements, further contributing to the lack of monitoring (Moutopoulos et al. 2016). Nevertheless, in
2013 a multiannual management plan for anchovy and sardine setting catch limits in respect to MSY is in
force and in 2016 further measures were implemented to ensure exploitation levels in respect to MSY by 2020
(GFCM 2013; GFCM 2016).
The aim of this report is to explore several available methods for data limited stocks, assess their credibility in
terms of estimation of reference points and compare them in regards to their ability to provide realistic results.
The derived reference points for each method were used to evaluate the state of the stock and determine
whether they are factual for utilization in management plans.
1.1
Review
The need to assess stocks that have limited information has led in the development of models designed for
this purpose. Reference points that correspond to the MSY and evaluate the state of the stock are important
for management advice to ensure the sustainable exploitation of the stocks in concern. Methods constructed,
such as per-recruit and length based models, utilizing minimum information on biological data are explored
in this breef review.
Beverton and Holt (1957) developed an equilibrium based per recruit model estimating the yield and spawning
biomass of the stock, adopting a Von Bertalanﬀy (1938) somatic growth curve and isometric length-weight
relationship. The main assumptions underlying this method are that mortality and recruitment are invariant
throughout time, as well as knife edge selectivity.
Gedamke and Hoening (2006) developed a method based on Beverton and Holt’s (1957) equilibrium estimator
7
==PAGE:8==
by incorporating changes in total mortality (Z) to account for the non-equilibrium state of the vast majority
of stocks regarding ﬁshing pressure. A time series of approximated mean lengths (¯L), estimated to be above
the length that individuals are available to the ﬁshery (length of full selectivity Lc), and selected values of
ﬁshing mortalities associated with the constant natural mortality of the respective stock are used to estimate
the total mortality using a maximum likelihood approach. By using the maximum likelihood approach the
diﬀerence between the observed and the estimated mean length is minimized with the Z optimized to account
for that ﬁtting. An assessment of two goose ﬁsh stocks in the north and south region of the US using 40 year
length frequency data to estimate mean length was conducted by the authors. Results for the southern region
showed that the initial Z (beginning of the time series) was 0.31 y−1 and a change in mortality occurred
in 1977 with Z = 0.6 y−1 validating the consistency of their ﬁndings with the landings data presented in
the 34th Stock Assessment Workshop (NEFSC 2002), whereas for the northern region the initial Z was
0.14 y−1, changing in 1978 to 0.29 y−1 and in 1987 to 0.55 y−1.Nevertheless, this method is restricting
selectivity by assuming that it can only be knife-edged; if the selectivity changes the recruited individuals will
gradually aﬀect the Lc. Another caveat of this method is the assumption of constant recruitment. An increase
in recruitment from a year class leads to overestimation of Z, because the mean length of the population
decreases and it’s added to the estimation of Z.
Then (2014) extended the Gedamke and Hoening non equilibrium extension by incorporating standardized
ﬁshing eﬀort (alternative is catch rates and catch) time-series, for the estimation of the catchability coeﬃcient
(q) and natural mortality M, which in turn is used to estimate year-speciﬁc ﬁshing and total mortality rates.
The reliability of M and q decreased with increased mean length variability. Nevertheless, the estimation of Z
was reliable even for unreliable estimates of M and q.
Beddington and Kirkwood (2005) utilized life-history invariants for the estimation of potential yield (Y)
and the stock status by addressing the properties of Fmax (as a proxy for Fmsy). They approached both
constant and varying recruitment by implementing the dimensionless parameter h in the latter. The equations
Y/ExB0 = 0.2k(1 – ln(0.67 – Lc)) (ExB0 being the unexploited biomass) and Fmax = 0.6k/(0.67 – Lc)
are constructed as a result of the proportionality expressed between them and Von Bertalanﬀy’s k and Lc
parameters (for a constant a(Lc)). Varying recruitment was estimated by assuming that the a(Lc) parameter
was dependent on both Lc and h (a(Lc,h)). According to the authors a caveat of this method is the diﬀerent
selection pattern of the gears on size and age, bypassing it in the case of a dominant gear. Furthermore the
model does not account for natural variations as well as varying natural mortality. Carruthers et al. (2014)
examined this method among other data limited approaches, using an operating model that simulates the
population with several management options and applies criteria for their evaluation (Management Strategy
Evaluation). Overall, Beddington’s and Kirkwood’s (2005) approach was assessed as average and prone to
“overﬁsh”. Furthermore, the use of Fmax, has been tested and found that under low productivity (decrease in
recruitment) periods it was highly variant in comparison with other reference points, aﬀecting its credibility
as a proxy for Fmsy (Morgan, Shelton, and Rideout 2014).
Hordyk et al. (2014) associated the Beverton Holt life-history invariants and the F/M ratio to length frequency
data, spawner per recruit (SPR) and selectivity at length under equilibrium conditions. The authors observed
that under equilibrium conditions M/K and F/M ratios govern the length composition. The analytical model
constructed was further examined by Hordyk et al.(2014) for data limited stocks to assess its performance
and sensitivity regarding the input parameters. F/M ratio is calculated from the input parameters, using
maximum likelihood approach to ﬁt the predicted to the observed length distribution, and it is used for
8
==PAGE:9==
the calculation of SPR. Results showed that the model was accurate for the four species they examined for
corresponding true values, but increasingly biased for M/k values < 0.53. Furthermore, CV Linf for length
at age is assumed to be normally distributed is required to be accurate for the estimation of SPR, which
highly expensive to test for data-limited stocks. Caveat of this method is primarily the equilibrium based
nature of the model. Multimodality observed in length distributions indicate violation of the recruitment
assumption, therefore estimates using this type of data will most likely provide unrealistic estimations.
Froese (2004) developed three simple ﬁsheries indicators to account for the sustainability of the catches, by
assuming that mature ﬁsh maximize the yield of the ﬁshery and using as an input proportions of length
distribution. The ﬁrst indicator aims in allowing all the ﬁsh in the stock to spawn at least once before
they are captured, while the second indicator describes the optimum length (typically larger than length
at maturity) as the target length for all the ﬁsh caught. The last indicator ideally would advocate in the
adoption a management plan to avoid exploitation of ﬁsh larger than plus 10% of the optimum length (0% of
the catch), whereas when it’s not possible, the megaspawners should reﬂect 30-40% of the catch. Justiﬁcation
for these indicators arise from the important role large individuals have in the survivability of the population:
1) Fecundity and egg size increases with length, 2) Larger individuals have “better genes” to contribute to
the stock and 3) A failure in recruitment can be combated when the stock has prolonged reproductive phase
as a result of a lengthened life-span. This method assumes therefore that the catch length distribution is
representative of the stock. Violation of this assumption might lead to a ﬂawed perception of the stock status.
Cope and Punt (2009) tested the indicators developed by Froese (2004) and constructed a new indicator
Pobj, as the sum of Pmat, Popt, Pmega. This indicator is used then to investigate the relations to ﬁshing
mortality and spawning biomass (speciﬁcally the target RP 0.4SB and limit RP 0.25B0) and associated with
a decision tree for the interpretation of stock status. The value of Pobj is used as an input in the constructed
decision tree to identify a link between a trigger point and stock status, using catch length composition data,
to inform catch recommendations through Harvest Control rules.

Sardine in Northern Spain (GSA 6)
Sardine (Sardina pilchardus) is a fast growing, small pelagic ﬁsh, amounting to 40% of the total landings in
the Mediterranean (Pertierra and Perrotta 1993). Spawning season of this species is autumn/winter with
12◦to 14◦C considered as the optimal temperature and salinity aproximated at 37 ppt; several spawning
peaks are observed due to the requirements of optimal conditions (Palomera et al. 2007). Sardine is assumed
to be restricted in the boundaries of GSA 6 due to absence of information concerning the stock structure
(STECF 2015). Although its commercial value is lower than anchovy, it is regarded as an important ﬁshery
in the area of Northern Spain in terms of biomass. Landings length distribution and total numbers were
provided for years 2002 to 2015 in GSA 6 from Spain. Landings ﬂuctuated between 17168 t in 2002 to 6450 t
in 2015 with a maximum peak of 27799 t in 2006 (ﬁgure 2). Since 2009 landings declined in a low of 7507 t
and 7627 in 2009 and 2010 respectivelly. A peak in 2011 of 12568 t is observed, whereas in 2015 landings are
reaching the lowest value of the time series. Pertierra et al. (1993), concluded that the stock up to 1991 was
underexploited (data coming from “confraries” of each port) and in Palomera et al. study (2007), the authors
observe an apparent decline in landings since the mid 1990s.

2.1.2
Anchovy in Adriatic Sea (GSA 17 & 18)
Anchovy (Engraulis encrasicolus) in Northern and Southern Adriatic Sea is a high value commercial species
for the region (Zorica et al. 2014). In this study, it is considered as an individual stock for both GSAs,
established by the information provided in STECF 16-22 (STECF 2016). According to Magoulas et al., (1996)
the frequency of a single clade is >85% in the Adriatic Sea, suggesting the presence of two diﬀerent clades
in the Mediterranean. Further studies have indicated the presence of two diﬀerent species of anchovy in
the Adriatic Sea based on bathymetry (Borsa, Collet, and Durand 2004) and allele frequency (Bembo et al.
1996). Nevertheless, a recent study has shown that the indication of bathymetry aﬀecting the distribution,
was not able to conﬁrm the presence of two diﬀerent species, whereas the genetic diﬀerences observed were
minimal and attributed to environmental and natural selection processes (Ruggeri et al. 2016).
Spawning season for anchovy is between April and October when the temperature is optimal (17◦to 23◦
C), highly correlated with the river run oﬀfrom the continental shelf and the stratiﬁcation in the water
mass (Palomera et al. 2007 Ruggeri et al. (2016)). Furthermore, anchovy can withstand a wide range of
salinity resulting in a spawning peak inﬂuenced only by the temperature and the productivity of the region,
considered as well deﬁned (Palomera et al. 2007).

Parapenaeus longirostris in Ligurian Sea and North Tyrrhenian Sea (GSA 9)
Deep sea rose shrimp (Parapenaeus longirostris) is an epibenthic, fast growing, short lived species with
increasing abundance in depths 100 to 400 m in sandy and/or muddy substrate and higher temperatures

in the ﬁshing pattern can be observed, attributed in the proximity of the port to the ﬁshery. Parapenaeus
longirostris is not the main species targeted by the ﬁshery, but the commercial importance has signiﬁcantly
increased in recent years.

2.3
Methods
2.3.1
Length based Indicators (LBI)
2.3.1.1
ICES approach
The cost-beneﬁt relationship observed in ﬁsh for a corresponding length has immense ecological importance.
For example large individuals exhibit higher fecundity and survival although the energy requirements are
bigger than their smaller counterparts (Enberg et al. 2012). Fishing pressure is directly inﬂuencing growth,
subsequently size at age for large time intervals so a trade oﬀbetween length and exploitation can be estimated.
ICES (ICES 2014) developed an approach based on a series of length based indicators to assess the stocks
with only catch (or landings) length distribution as available data, in terms of MSY, conservation of mature
and immature individuals as well as yield optimization. Data requirements are annual length distributions of
the stock in concern, speciﬁcaly, catches or landings in proportions and two biological parameters, length at
50% maturity (Lmat) and Von Bertalanﬀy’s asymptotic length (L∞). Furthermore, the length that provides
the maximum yield (Lmaxy) can be estimated with the length weight parameters a and b. The model requires
representative length distribution and estimates the length where the ﬁshing mortality equals the natural
mortality (LF =M) and the length that provides the optimal yield (or where the growth is maximum), (Lopt)
as reference points. In ﬁgure 9 shows the selected indicators and the corresponding reference points. These
indicators were selected based on the robustness and representative portayal of the stock in concern.
L25% and L95% are estimated by the cumulative frequency of the length distribution. They are compared
with Lmat and L∞for the estimation of the indicator ratios responsible for the conservation in terms of
immature and mature individuals respectivelly. Lmat is identiﬁed as an indicator by the European Comission
related to the eﬀects of ﬁshing pressure in a stock and L∞is a metric presenting the largest size an individual
of the stock can attain (European Comission 2010).
LF =M is calculated as:
LF =M = 0.75 · Lc + 0.25 · L∞
(1)
and is used as a length based proxy of MSY (derived from F=M), compared to Lmean (mean length of
individuals larger than length at ﬁrst capture). Lc, deﬁned as 50% of the individuals that are selected by
the ﬁshing gear and estimated from a length frequency curve (half of the maximum frequency on the ﬁrst
ascending part of the curve on the left side).
Lopt, estimated as:
Lopt = 2/3 · L∞
(2)
is used as the reference point for Lmean and Lmaxy, representing the optimization of the yield. Lmax5%
and Pmega, along with L95% mentioned before, are the metrics determining the conservation of mature
individuals in the catch. Lmax5% is estimated as the 5% of the largest individuals observed in the catch and
Pmega represents the megaspawners observed in the stock. The reference point for Pmega is considered the
value of 0.3, based on Froese’s ﬁndings (Froese 2004) stating that a healthy stock should have 30% to 40%

Approach of this report
During my thesis i had the opportunity to attend a working group on data-limited stocks conducted by

4
Discussion
In this report the examination of diﬀerent models was conducted in order to establish their eﬃcacy for
management advice. Reference points derived from each method were scrutinized and their utility was
assessed based on the performance of the models and their underlying assumptions, for the respective stocks.
Each model results in a diﬀerent reference point as a proxy for MSY, examining their recruitment capabilities,
the state of the stock from all aspects and estimating the ﬁshing mortality the stock is experiencing when
there is no prior knowledge.
4.1
Length Based Indicators
Length based indicators method was explored with sardine, anchovy and deep rose shrimp, resulting in
diﬀerent estimations of stock status for each. The overall state of the stock for sardine indicated that ﬁshing
is unsustainable, there are conservation issues regarding both the immature and mature portion of the stock
and the harvest is not optimized. Lmean/LF =M ratio is inﬂuenced by length at full capture, because both
metrics include L25% in their estimations, and the pattern of shifting selectivity from 2009 is subsequently
causing the MSY indicator to decrease corresponding to the decrease in length at ﬁrst capture with Lmean
being highly sensitive to changes in numbers of the respective length classes, as well. It is improbable that
the shift towards smaller length classes was caused due to ﬁshing because it’s very radical from year 2008 to
2009 and ﬁshing induced changes to the mean length of a stock require time to be observed. Additionally,
information on eﬀort by STECF is not showing an increasing trend (assuming that the catchability coeﬃcient
remained the same) and regulations in force are restricting the capture of indivivuals below 11 cm, although
the spatial distribution of the ﬂeet or the stock are not known. The indicator ratio of L25%/Lmat is describing
the regeneration capabilities of the stock and is below the reference value, nevertheless it remains close to it
and it is stable from 2009 to the end of the time series. Assuming that the length at maturity is correctly
estimated and not deviating from the true value, this indicator is showing us that the stock should be stable in
terms of recruitment and combined with the information that eﬀort is not increasing from 2009, other factors
than ﬁshing or recruitment are aﬀecting the mean length, although the model is able to detect the change.
Nevertheless, examining length at ﬁrst capture as an individual metric in respect to length at maturity,
in most cases it is estimated close or lower than length at maturity so females do not have the chance of
49
==PAGE:50==
contributing to the spawning before they are ﬁshed. Additionally, when length at ﬁrst capture is really close
to length at maturity, although theoritically females have reached the stage of maturation, their small size
(subsequently low fecundity) is hindering their full capability of contributing to the recruitment of the next
years. The mean length in the catch is decreasing from 2009 and onwards, corresponding to the shift towards
smaller length classes observed in the length distribution plots. Indicator ratios concerning the conservation
of mature individuals, as well as the proportion of mature individuals reaching the maximum age are also in
accordance with the observations derived from the length distribution plots, which is that less individuals
are observed in higher length classes from 2009 until the end of the time series. Optimal yield estimation
is inﬂuenced by the Lmean metric, which as mentioned before is estimated from the length at ﬁrst capture,
subsequently aﬀected by the change in selectivity. Metrics examining the presence of mature individuals,
such as Pmega, L95% and L75% are far from Linf which could be attributed to a false estimation of the latter.
Removing the OTB gear and using the simple moving average method resulted in insigniﬁcant divergence of
the results derived from the initial data.
Anchovy is ﬁshed unsustainably and the yield requires optimization, although the indicator ratio L25%/Lmat
concerning the immature portion of the stock is above the reference value, indicating that individuals are
contributing unhindered to the recruitment for all the years of this assessment. As mentioned before, L25% is
inﬂuencing the MSY and optimal yield indicator ratios, but in this case it’s stable estimation from 2011 to the
end of the time series results in rather stable estimations of current exploitation levels of the stock (expressed
by Lmean), also because for anchovy the numbers of the length classes remain more or less the same. It can
be seen that, although the length distributions are noisy maybe a recruitment trend in years 2003 to 2005 can
be observed, subsequently inﬂuencing the estimation of Lmean/LF =M, which is mistakenly translated by the
method as increase in ﬁshing pressure in 2004 when the recruited smaller length classes are more abundant.
Generally, no other trend is very apparent in the length distribution which could lead us in the deduction
that the indicator ratio for MSY is correctly estimated from 2007 till the end of the time series under the
assumption that the diﬀerent estimations observed are due to slight changes in the numbers observed in the
respective length classes. Length at ﬁrst capture is above length at maturity and close to the length that
provides the optimum yield after 2003 (with ﬂuctuations) meaning that the state of spawning is much better
in comparison with sardine. The presence of mature individuals in the stock is apparent and the indicator
ratio of Lmax5%/Linf is close to the reference value but slightly decreasing, although individuals reaching the
maximum age are not observed. This could be attributed to missspeciﬁcation of Linf, or truncuated length
distribution. Croatian length distribution of landings introduced in 2013 is inﬂuencing all indicator ratios
by decreasing the estimated values when compared with the length distribution of all countries combined,
although in 2015 they are increasing again. This means that Slovenian and Italian landings are composed
by smaller length classes and it’s directly aﬀecting metrics that are inﬂuenced by the input parameters and
the observed numbers of the respective length classes, as smaller length classes are diverging more from
Linf and Lmat. A diﬀerent pattern is observed with the moving average method. The smoothing increasing
the numbers of bigger individuals for the recent years and subsequently aﬀecting the results. The decrease
of bigger length classes in the Italian length distribution starts from 2012, subsequently the reference year
of 2012, calculated as the average of 2011 to 2013, in the moving average method including only Italy is
inﬂuenced by 2011 in which the bigger length classes are still observed.
Rose shrimp estimations of indicator ratios for the length frequency distributions derived from the surveys are
highly variant. Nevertheless examinatiom of the last years of the assessments show that the female portion of
50
==PAGE:51==
the stock is ﬁshed sustainably with mature individuals present in the length frequency distribution although
the indicator ratio is falling below the reference value for the last year. Additionally, there seems to be an
issue with the immature portion of the stock, the megaspawners and a need of optimization of the yield is
needed. For the male survey, it can be deducted that the male portion of the stock is ﬁshed sustainably,
even in the last year, because the estimation is quite close to the reference value. Conservation of mature
individuals is not an issue and the yield is optimized. Megaspawners are decreasing in the length distribution
from 2014, alhtough for 2013 they are above the reference of 30%. Lastly, the immature individuals of the
stock are below healthy levels. Combining the female and the male length frequency distribution resulted in
all estimations for all indicator ratios to fall below the reference values, suggesting overexploitation, absence of
mature and immature individuals, as well as the need to optimize the harvest. Length distribution of landings
had less variance in the estimations, although the length distribution seems noisy. A decrease in terms of
conservation for the mature and immature portion of the stock, as well as a need for optimizing the harvest
can be seen in the last years of the assessment, whereas up to 2014 the stock was ﬁshed sustainably. Although
the patterns observed in the length distribution of landings cannot be attributed even with uncertainty
to changes in ﬁshing pressure, environmental conditions or selectivity, as long as the length distribution
is representative of the stock and the uncertainty included in the biological parameters is minimized, this
method can approximate the state of the stock with accuracy. Nevertheless, it is certain that the stock is
increasingly exploited until the end of the time series in terms of abundance and the gear targetting it is ﬁshing
below the minimum landing size of 20 mm. Another problem with this species are the diﬀerent life-history
parameters observed between sexes. Females are indicative of sustainability due to their reproductive ability
and contribution to recruitment, but examining males and females individually would not lead to an accurate
status assessment for the stock; a signiﬁcant decrease of the male population would roughly have the same
eﬀect on the recruitment of the stock as a decrease in females.
4.2
Length Based Spawning Potential Ratio
The underlying assumptions of the length based spawning potential ratio model should be carefully examined
and estimations of SPR if they are violated should not be used for management purposes. Caveats of this
method are that it “reads” the absence of large individuals in the length distribution as heavy exploitation
resulting in overestimation of F/M and understimation of SPR, introduce bias in the estimations if the
selectivity of the ﬁshery is changed (and subsequently the length structure of the length distribution) and it
operates under the equilibrium assumption; constant ﬁshing mortality and recruitment. The model is not
able to account for time varying ﬁshing mortality and even though the estimations are independent from
one year to another, it is unable to detect changes in F. Furthermore, when recruitment is evident it is
possible that the F/M estimations would be highly overestimated or underestimated, resulting in the SPR
estimations that are deviating much from the true value. In the case of sardine, F/M estimations are within
a reasonable range of values for years 2004 and 2009 to 2013, whereas for the rest of the years are unrealistic.
Corresponding trends in the length frequency distribution show a possible recruitment trend or a rather noisy
length distribution, and from 2009 the individuals in bigger length classes are reduced. It is quite apparent
that the shift in smaller length classes in 2009 is not recognized by the model because SPR estimations
remain the same when it would have been expected that smaller individuals would have lower fecundity rates.
It seems that the “recruitment trend” is causing the selectivity to deviate from the asymptotic curve expected
from the model, which could justify the unrealistic value of 28 in 2003 for 50% selectivity, and in turn this is
51
==PAGE:52==
causing overestimation of F/M with the exception of year 2004. From 2009, even though according to the
length based indicators method and the length distribution plots the mature individuals in the stock show
great reduction, this method does not seem to recognize this up until 2014 to 2015 where the F/M ratio is
slightly overestimated. Overall, all of the SPR estimations for the years this assessment was conducted are
below the reference values indicating that the stock is heavily exploited. The moving average method has
SPR values above the reference points for years 2008 and 2009 (refence year of the average of 2007 to 2009
and 2008 to 2010 respectivelly), although F/M is highly underestimated so they are untrustworthy.
SPR estimations for anchovy are below the thresholds for all the years of this assessement, showing a stock
that is heavily exploited, with some years indicating that the potential for spawning is non existent. F/M
values are highly unrealistic, even though the respective estimation for selectivity at 50% and 95% length
seem to ﬂuctuate in normal levels. The unrealistic value of F/M for 2003 seems to be attributed to the
recruitment trend observed (again this trend is assumed), violating the equilibrium concept of the method,
subsequently showing the inability of the model to incorporate variance in recruitment by assuming that
selectivity is slightly doming. Additionally, in 2005 a change in selectivity, or a recruitment trend from 2003
moving on the higher length classes can be speculated, resulting in a value of 222 for F/M. It can be assumed
from the length distribution plots that even though it is not clear whether it’s an actual shift in selectivity
(towards smaller length classes) for these years, a not so obvious recruitment trend due to the fast growth of
small pelagic species, the model recognizes the decrease in bigger individuals (for example year 2012) or the
length distribution is noisy, the assumptions of the model are violated and the estimations should not be
considered accurate. Concerning the data set with the Croatian and Slovenia length distributions removed it
might be assumed that years 2013 and 2014 might be recognized as recruitment trend due to the double
peak, resulting in higher F/M values in comparison with the initial data. On the contrary, reducing the
bigger individuals coming from the Croatian landings resulted in lower estimation of F/M in 2015. The SPR
estimations from the moving average method are also indicating that the stock is overﬁshed with hindered
potential of spawing.
Female and male length frequency distributions when examined with the LBSPR method resulted in some years
having SPR estimations above the reference values. Nevertheless, selectivity at length and F/M estimations
gave unrealistic results, especially in the case of the female length frequency distribution. Additionally it
is hard to deduct whether or not the results are accurate enough. Observing the length distributions it
can be seen that it has double peaks in most years the model cannot ﬁt “multimodal” length compositions.
The total length frequency distribution has the same patterns in the length frequency distribution as the
female and male but aggregating them resulted in SPR estimations below the reference values. Landings
length distribution was less noisy, although year 2011 was recognized as doming selectivity and most probably
underestimated the SPR. Nevertheless, information on eﬀort shows that it has been steadily decreasing since
2004 although the abundance is ﬂuctuating indicating that ﬁshing mortality is not invariant.
4.3
Mean Length Estimator
Mean length estimator behaved diﬀerently for each stock. The observed mean length in the catch from
2006 to 2009 in the case of sardine peaked to approximatelly 16 cm, probably derived from the assumed
recruitment trend observed in the length distribution for these years and dropped signiﬁcantly for the rest of
the years. The strong recruitment year, starting from 2003 and moving on to the bigger length classes gives
52
==PAGE:53==
biased results in the estimation of the observed mean length whereas from 2009 the shift towards smaller
length classes decrease the estimates of the mean length, subsequently overestimating Z. The model was
able to ﬁt the predicted mean length to the observed both for the initial data set and the SMA method,
although it resulted in unrealist estimations of the the total mortality (Z < M) making impossible to trust the
results. It was more apparent that years 2003 to 2008 were problematic and were violating the recruitment
assumption when the data set was broken down to years 2003 to 2008 and 2009 to 2015, where the model
was able to ﬁt the data and give realistic values of Z. For years 2009 to 2015 F and F/FMSY are realistic but
for 2003 to 2008 the YPR estimation does not recognize the stock as exploited in terms of F, but it seems
that the total mortality is only attributed to M, and it’s approximating F to 0.007. The respective F/FMSY ,
is estimated at 0.008, a value that indicates that the stock is in near virgin levels in regards to exploitation,
deviating a lot from the estimatios with the initial data set and it’s manipulations.
The selected model for the initial data set for anchovy was better when only one Z was taken into account for
the whole time series. The other data sets, when comparing the AIC values of the models, performed better
when the breakpoints were introduced. Overall, the stock in all cases seem to be overexploited, with the
highest F/FMSY values observed when the Croatian and Slovenian length distributions were removed. The
spatial distribution of the ﬂeets targetting this stock is divided between GSA 17 and 18, although most of
the Italian vessels are ﬁshing in GSA 17. Nevertheless, it can be assumed that either the Croatian or the
Slovenian ﬂeet (although the change in the length distribution is more apparent when the Croatian ﬂeet is
removed) are ﬁshing in a location where larger individuals are observed, although without knowledge of the
coverage area of the stock or the ﬂeet this could only be speculated.
The male and landings length distribution set were ﬁtted with only the initial total mortality with no change
in the time series. The mean length ﬂuctuated a lot from year to year and estimations of the state of the
stock give diﬀerent results. The male survey is considered overexploited and the landings length distribution
is underexploited. The female and the total length frequency distributions are approximating F/FMSY quite
close, both indicating that the stock is underexploited. Additionally, the model was able to ﬁt changes in
total mortality for the respective years, but the predicted mean lengths did not ﬁt the observed during the
whole time series. Again, the survey length distributions were highly variant in terms of length at full capture
and estimated observed mean length.
4.4
Sensitivity analysis and further observations
Asymptotic length highly inﬂuences the ratios that are associated with it (Pmega, Lmax5%/Linf, Lmean/Lopt,
Lmean/LF =M). Underestimation of 15% in the value of Linf severelly overestimated all ratios, with the
proportion of megaspawners in the stock reaching an underestimation factored by 30. Pmega is highly sensitive
to Linf, due to it’s correlation with Lopt. On the contrary, overestimating the asymptotic length gave results
closer to the true value.
Sensitivity analysis of LBSPR showed that overestimation of M/k ratio overestimated SPR by a factor of
approximatelly 1.5, whereas it slightly underestimated F/M and vice versa. Linf was the parameter that
inﬂuenced the all the results, with an underestimation of 15% resulting in overestimation of SPR by a
factor of 5 and underestimation of F/M. Selectivity at 50% and 95% was also aﬀected, with underestimation
and overestimation appearing correlated with the decrease and increase in Linf. L50% and L95% were also
examined as they are considered uncertain due to exctraction from the LBI method and no prior information.
53
==PAGE:54==
Both inﬂuenced the SPR estimatios with L50% overestimating SPR when the value decreased and L95%
overestimating SPR when the value increased and vice versa. Sensitivity analysis of the life-history parameters
of Linf and k, in the case of the mean length indicator, showed that a change in these parameters led to
increased F/Fmsy values. Fishing mortality behaved in a similar way, increasing regardless of under or over
estimation of Linf. The value of Z in each case was inﬂuenced from both the asymptotic length and the
observed mean length estimated by the model, resulting in estimations that could not be characterized as
over or under estimated, but highly dependent on the observed mean length.
Length based indicators method is operating under the assumption of steady state, therefore changes in
the length distribution that can’t be attributed to a cause will have an impact on the results in terms of
uncertainty. Therefore, It is unlikely that this method will approximate the state of the stock without
uncertainty because the reference point Lmean/LF =M is highly inﬂuenced by the asymptotic length, that
includes uncertainty for these stocks in it’s estimation as well. If asymptotic length could be approximated
with accuracy and the length distribution is representative of the stock, then this method would be able to
provide trustworthy results. It should also be noted that ICES developed this method under the assumption
that all ratios should be considered for characterization of the stock as under or over exploited, operating
with a traﬃc light system (green when the ratio is above the reference value and red for the opposite). This
report tried to follow the same patern but estimates slightly below the reference value would be considered as
“not passing”. This implicates the decision making process because the uncertainty included in the estimations
could not provide a way to assess the credibility of the pass/not pass system when the ratios were close to
the reference values. Therefore, Lmean/LF =M is the main ratio considered to describe the state of the stock.
In almost all cases this method was unable to detect mature individuals reaching the maximum age. It is
hard to attribute the absence of mature individuals to a cause. Stocks are not restricted to the boundaries as
deﬁned by FAO, therefore a migration of the large individuals might give false estimations of stock status by
examining only the small length classes that are present. Fishing induced or environmental driven genetic
changes might also have been the cause of the absence of mature individuals and individuals reaching the
maximum age, therefore the mature individuals cannot be detected due to a misspeciﬁed Linf.
The LBSPR and mean length estimator methods operate under assumptions that are violated for all the
stocks in concern. All three species are highly inﬂuenced by environmental variables, either directly through
temperature or salinity, or indirectly by primary production. Changes in the length distribution could
have been caused by a change in spatial distribution of the stocks -subsequently aﬀecting the selectivity-,
ﬁshing pressure or as mentioned before environmental conditions. Examining these stocks under equilibrium
conditions might give accurate results when the annual length distribution falls under the assumptions
required for the operation of the models, at least for the LBSPR method which bases it’s estimations annually
without correlation among the years, but the inability of the models to assess the time variant dynamics of
the stocks would most likely deem them unable to provide reference points that could be considered accurate
for management advice. All the models are inﬂuenced by the input parameters in terms of the results, with
LBSPR requiring information (for example on CVLinf) that is not available for data limited stocks and it is
expensive to estimate. Additionally, LBSPR method is assuming that spawner abundance is proportional to
the egg production, but this is unlikely to hold. Changes in the length distribution, aﬀected by many factors,
will result in smaller individuals with lower fecundity rates. The mean length estimators main purpose is to
approximate the F in a stock where the ﬁshing mortality is not known. The assumption that the changes in
mean length happen due to ﬁshing mortality only is not very realistic with these stocks, whereas trends in the
54
==PAGE:55==
length distributions of data limited stocks are usually noisy and trends that could explain if the assumptions
are violated are not quite clear. The recruitment assumption, when violated, will lead in underestimation of
Z when recruitment is failing, whereas Z will be overestimated when the recruited individuals are added in
the length distribution, subsequently lowering the observed mean length.
An additional drawback of the mean length estimator method is the time consuming process of constantly
updating the estimated value of Z by hand in order to achieve the best ﬁtting. To avoid that, a loop was
constructed and run until the speciﬁed condition was false, i.e. the absolute diﬀerence of the estimated Zs
was over the speciﬁed value (|Zi - Zi−1| > 0.0001). Two values are requested as input, Z1 and X1, where Z1
is the “guess” of the operator, and the second value has to be ̸= Z1 and is acting as initiator of the loop (in
case the estimation of Z is urealistic or the model is not ﬁtted properly, diﬀerent initial guess is suggested).
The same logic was followed when introducing the breakpoints and assuming the years the change happened,
but the condition in this case should be false for all the Z values simultaneously. In this case, the operator
doesn’t have to input his guess as long as there is a previous estimation of the respective Z. The accuracy of
the condition value was reduced to 0.001 and 0.02 when the ﬁrst and second breakpoint were introduced for
the case of sardine, respectively, to avoid an extensive number of iterations or failure to converge altogether.
The condition value depends on the data provided; testing if it’s possible to ﬁnd the combination of Zs with
this condition is suggested, otherwise it would be best to relax the condition value to be more realistic. It was
previously tested that the values of respective AICs, as well as the Z estimations, for the comparison of the
models did not change signiﬁcantly when the ﬁtting was halted under this condition. Overall, it performed
better than the ﬁtting by hand and it was not time consuming, although closer examination on the eﬀect, if
any, on the results is suggested.
The simple moving average method was used to smooth the data sets of sardine and anchovy by “excluding”
the noise from annual ﬂuctuations of the length distributions. This technique has several drawbacks. It reﬂects
the past trends of the length distribution more than the recent ones and even though it is decreasing the
noise it could hide signiﬁcant environmental or ﬁshing induced trends experienced by the stock. Furthermore,
the time series of sardine and anchovy are relativelly short for proper smoothing, although it could be argued
that smoothing with short time series reﬂects better the initial data set. The “not suﬃcient smoothing trend”
is quite noticeable in the LBSPR method with sardine and achovy exhibiting high estimations of F/M when
the reference year was derived from an average that included noisy years forits estimation.
Several drawbacks are also included with the use of surveys. Although in this case the spatial distribution of
the Medits survey is considered in line with the commercial ﬂeet targeting the rose shrimp, the sampling is
conducted only once per year. Furthermore, the sampling gear is not restricted by management regulations,
therefore targetting individuals below the minimum landing size. The length frequency distribution of surveys
is likely to be imprecise relative to the landings length distribution. For example in the case of LBI, highly
invariant metrics will produce indicator ratios ﬂuctuating a lot from year to year, as in the case of rose shrimp,
and will be operating under assumptions that are biologically impossible unless extreme environmental
conditions are observed or extreme management measures are in force or exploitation is severe.

Conclusion
The uncertainty included in the estimations of reference points derived from the provided length distributions
with these methods is prohibiting their use for management purposes. It is quite clear that the data collection
framework of the Mediterranean Sea should improve, by enhancing the quality and quantity of the ﬁshery
dependent data and provide unbiased temporal ﬁshery independent data. The most important commercial
stocks in the Mediterranean Sea (for example sardine and anchovy) are lacking important information which
is directly aﬀecting their sustainable exploitation. Total catch, landings and eﬀort data, as well as length
and weight measurements should be collected through logbooks, ensuring that they are representative of the
stock. Biological data are the cornerstone of the methods examined in this report, with direct involvement of
the latter in the results. It is of outermost importance therefore that the estimated parameters would be as
accurate as possible.
New models should also be developed to account for all the restrains data-poor stocks exhibit and escape
assumptions that are biologically unrealistic. Nevertheless, this is not an easy task and a general primary
approach that would be useful, in my opinion, is the collection and categorization of the readily available
data of each data-limited stock. Classiﬁcation based on the quality and quantity of the availabe information
could provide a useful ﬁrst insight and enable the improvement of data collection systems. Speciﬁcally, this
categorization will allow the design of speciﬁc data collection processes based on the absent information of each
category. Furthermore, this could help assess the performance of models speciﬁcally designed for data-limited
stocks when they are explored with data falling under a speciﬁc category, subsequently manifesting already
expected characteristics (although the bias that might arise in this occasion needs to be examined). Matching
the data collection process with the existing assessment methods that are developed to estimate reference
points with little information, should also be considered in this endeavor. Lastly, the data collection systems
should be able to adapt and continuously improve to be in line with the required information, taking, of
course, into account the cost restrains.
