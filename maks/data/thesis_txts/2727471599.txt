Abstract
Offshore wind farms are gaining increasing significance as crucial sources of renewable energy world-
wide, prompting significant investments in their development by numerous countries. The electrical
infrastructure of these offshore wind farms plays a vital role in their design and operation, partic-
ularly the parallel export cables responsible for transmitting the generated power from the wind
turbines to the onshore grid.
However, the parallel connection of export cables in weakly connected offshore wind farms presents
several challenges, primarily the susceptibility to damage caused by fault currents. Therefore, the
development of effective protection strategies tailored specifically for these parallel export cables is
of utmost importance.
This thesis focuses on the investigation of selectivity strategies for distance protection of parallel
export cables in weakly connected offshore wind farms.
The central objective of this study is
to design and assess protection schemes that can deliver reliable and selective protection for the
parallel cables, thereby minimizing the risk of damage resulting from fault currents.
Initially, a simplified power system incorporating synchronous generator was modeled to validate the
distance protection performance. Subsequently, the model complexity was enhanced by substituting
the synchronous generator with an offshore wind farm, and further modifications were made to
certain parameters based on the observations derived from the validation phase. The resulting
model was then employed for the final case studies, focusing on the parallel cable protection.
The outcomes of the final case studies demonstrated the accurate functioning of the distance pro-
tection method in scenarios involving non-solid fault types. However, in the context of a weakly
connected wind farm, the presence of fault resistance led to maloperations in some protection zones.
These findings provide valuable additions to existing research in this field, affirming the influence of
grid strength on the performance of distance protection for offshore wind farms. The implications
of this study extend to future investigations concerning protection in the context of evolving grid
conditions.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The utilization of offshore wind farms has gained significant importance in meeting the growing
demand for renewable energy. These wind farms are typically located in remote and challenging
environments, necessitating the use of parallel export cables to connect multiple wind turbines to
a central collection point. However, ensuring the reliable operation and protection of these parallel
export cables presents considerable challenges due to the unique characteristics of offshore wind
farm grids.
The effective protection of parallel export cables is crucial to maintain uninterrupted power trans-
mission from offshore wind farms. Faults or abnormalities in the electrical system can result in
severe damage to the cables and disrupt power flow, leading to substantial financial losses and
downtime. Consequently, the development of robust protection strategies specifically tailored for
parallel export cables is of utmost importance.
One key aspect of protecting parallel export cables is achieving selectivity, which involves isolating
faults occurring on a specific cable segment while allowing continuous power flow in other segments.
This task becomes particularly complex in weakly connected offshore wind farms, where fault
currents are constrained due to cable impedance and suboptimal grid configurations. Traditional
protection methods may not offer adequate selectivity in such scenarios.
While addressing the challenges of protecting parallel export cables in weakly connected offshore
wind farms, this thesis will specifically focus on the application of distance protection techniques.
Distance protection is a widely used method for detecting and isolating faults in power transmission
systems, and its adaptation to the unique characteristics of offshore wind farm grids is of paramount
importance.
Distance protection relies on measuring the impedance between the protection location and the
fault point.
By comparing this impedance to predefined thresholds, the protection system can
determine the approximate distance to the fault and initiate appropriate corrective actions. How-
ever, implementing distance protection in weakly connected offshore wind farms presents additional
complexities due to factors such as cable impedance, grid configuration, and fault current limita-
tions.
The primary objective of this thesis is to investigate selectivity strategies for the protection of
parallel export cables in weakly connected offshore wind farms. Additionally, the research conducted
in this thesis will investigate the effectiveness of various distance protection schemes in accurately
detecting and isolating faults on parallel export cables. It will delve into the challenges of applying
distance protection in weakly connected grids, considering factors such as fault impedance, fault
resistance, and fault location accuracy.
The research will encompass theoretical analysis and computer simulations to evaluate the perfor-
mance of distance protection strategies. The selectivity, speed, accuracy, and reliability of these
strategies in detecting and isolating faults on parallel export cables will be thoroughly examined.
By gaining insights into the strengths and weaknesses of various protection methods, this study
aims to contribute valuable knowledge to the design and implementation of effective protection
systems for offshore wind farms, ultimately enhancing the stability and reliability of renewable

energy generation.
1.2
Motivation
The motivation to study selectivity strategies for the protection of parallel export cables in weakly
connected offshore wind farms, with a specific focus on distance protection, arises from the unique
challenges posed by such systems.
In weakly connected offshore wind farms, the distance between the wind farm and the onshore
grid is often considerable, resulting in long parallel export cables. These cables are crucial for
transmitting the generated power to the onshore grid. However, their extended length and the
associated impedance introduce complexities in fault detection, discrimination, and isolation.
Distance protection plays a vital role in ensuring the reliable and selective operation of paral-
lel export cables.
Traditional distance protection schemes measure the impedance of the cable
to determine the fault location.
However, in weakly connected offshore wind farms, the pres-
ence of distributed energy resources, varying fault conditions, and changing grid characteristics
can significantly impact the impedance calculation and compromise the effectiveness of distance
protection.
Therefore, there is a pressing need to explore and develop selectivity strategies specifically tailored
for distance protection of parallel export cables in weakly connected offshore wind farms. By ad-
dressing the challenges associated with fault detection accuracy, selectivity, and speed, this research
aims to investigate the performance and reliability of the protection system.
1.3
Methodology
The primary goal of this project is to evaluate the performance of protection systems under varying
grid conditions. To achieve this, a thorough review of the latest research literature on distance
protection is conducted to acquire latest advancements in these areas. This knowledge will then be
utilized to develop a protection model within the PSCAD software.
The designed protection algorithms will undergo testing in a simplified power system configuration.
Further, necessary modifications will be made based on the test results. Once the protection system
is deemed effective, it will be implemented for the final model with parallel cables outgoing from
an offshore wind farm (OWF).
Firstly, the protection system will be designed for a single transmission line (TL) outgoing from a
synchronous generator. After verification of correct performance of distance protection model, the
TL will be replaced with a cable line and the synchronous generator with the OWF. The OWF
model used for the case studies will be based on a model with a total capacity of 200 MW. The
Type IV wind turbine model from the PSCAD knowledge base will be employed as part of the
OWF model for simulation and analysis purposes.
The evaluation and testing of the protection model involve conducting assessments for single line
to ground, phase to phase, three-phase faults occurring at various locations within a designated
protection zone.
The emphasis of the protection system is specifically on distance protection,
excluding instantaneous overcurrent and differential protection.
Moreover, the introduction of POTT scheme can affect the overall response of the model during

stability by isolating the faulted section promptly.

Theory and Literature Review
Within this chapter, we present a concise overview of short circuits and distance protection fun-
damentals.
We delve into key concepts, theories, and previous research findings pertaining to
protection issues, establishing a robust theoretical framework for our study. Furthermore, we ex-
plore the specific impact of Type IV wind turbines on protection systems, considering their unique
characteristics and potential challenges they pose in ensuring effective and reliable protection.
2.1
Short Circuits
Several types of faults occur in power systems having various impacts on the grid. The single
line to ground fault (SLG) is the most common, which amounts to 70% of all the faults in a
power system [1]. The other types of faults in order of occurrence are phase to phase (Ph-Ph),
double line-to-ground (Ph-Ph-G), three phase (Ph-Ph-Ph) and three phase-to-ground (Ph-Ph-Ph-
G) faults. The three phase (Ph-Ph-Ph), (Ph-Ph-Ph-G) short circuits where current and voltage
have the same magnitude and are 120° phase shifted are called the symmetrical faults or balanced
faults. Whereas unbalanced faults (Ph-Ph), (SLG), (Ph-Ph-G) are faults where only one or two
phases are being affected. In these faults the magnitude and phase shift of current and voltage can
differ.
Figure 2.1: Types of short circuits a) Ph-Ph-Ph-G, b) Ph-Ph-Ph, c) SLG, d) Ph-Ph, e) Ph-Ph-G
In Figure 2.1 different types of fault are illustrated, where the balanced faults are shown in a, b
and the unbalanced are presented in c, d and e.
2.1.1
Symmetrical Components
The method of symmetrical components developed by Fortescue in 1918 aids in transforming a set
of three unbalanced phasors into three symmetrical sets of balanced phasors. These components
are known as positive, negative, and zero sequences.
4
==PAGE:14==
Assuming that we have been provided with a set of three-phase voltages denoted as Va, Vb, and
Vc and following Fortescue’s approach, the phase voltages are resolved into three sets of sequence
components as can be seen in Figure 2.3, which are as follows:
1. Zero-sequence components comprise of three phasors with identical magnitudes and zero
phase displacement.
2. Positive-sequence components include three phasors with equal magnitudes, 120° phase dis-
placement, and a positive sequence.
3. Negative-sequence components are represented by three phasors with equal magnitudes, 120°
phase displacement, and a negative sequence.

2.2
Distance Protection
Distance protection, commonly referred to as impedance protection, has a rich history that dates
back to the early days of electrical power systems. It has played a significant role in protection of
power transmission lines for several decades. The concept of distance protection originated from
the need to detect and isolate faults in power transmission lines efficiently.
In the early days,
protection schemes relied on overcurrent relays that measured the magnitude of current flowing
through the lines. However, these relays had limitations when it came to providing reliable and
selective protection, particularly in cases of high-impedance faults or faults located far from the
relays.
The development of distance protection introduced a new approach to power system protection.
Instead of solely relying on current magnitudes, distance protection considered the impedance seen
by the relay, which is the ratio of voltage to current.
2.2.1
Fundamentals
To accurately measure the distance to a fault on a transmission line, it’s necessary to use a relay
that can measure the line’s impedance up to a predetermined point (known as the reach point),
since the line’s impedance is proportional to its length. A distance relay is a specific type of relay
designed to operate only for faults that occur within a predetermined range or reach point from
its location.
It is engineered to provide selective protection by activating for faults within its
designated zone and not responding to faults outside of that zone. This selective operation ensures
that the relay accurately detects and isolates faults occurring within its reach, while disregarding
faults in other line zones. In essence, the distance relay acts as a protective boundary, ensuring
that fault detection and response are limited to the specified area of the power transmission line.
Distance relays are widely utilized in power systems. These relays offer good protection against
faults, selectivity and advanced operational security.
Distance protection basis involves dividing the voltage measured by the relay by the measured cur-
rent to calculate the apparent impedance, which is then compared with the reach point impedance.
If the measured impedance is less than the reach point impedance, a fault is assumed to exist on
the line between the relay and the reach point [2].
Distance relays employ multiple zones to provide a wide range of protection coverage, with each
zone covering a different part of the transmission line.
Zone 1 usually covers 80% of the line,
8
==PAGE:18==
while zone 2 covers 120% [2]. This difference in coverage is due to calculation and measurement
uncertainties that can cause faulty operations. Zone 1 is typically instantaneous, while zone 2 and
zone 3 are set with a time delay, typically ranging from 200 to 300 ms [3], to ensure selectivity
and prevent multiple relays from tripping simultaneously. Zone 3 or 4 can be used for backward
protection to increase the system’s selectivity. In a sample system shown in Figure 2.7, relay R1
protects 80% of the line between bus A and B, zone 2 covers 120% over bus B, and zone 3 covers
the remaining line between bus B and C.

The presence of fault resistance can significantly impact the fault impedance measured by a pro-

that the fault resistance can cause an increase in the R axis offset. This effect is most commonly
observed in phase-to-ground faults where high fault resistances tend to cause the offset to shift
along the R axis.

Overreaching occurs when a protective relay operates and trips for a fault that is outside of its
intended protection area. This means that the relay reaches farther than intended, which can lead
to unnecessary tripping of circuit breakers for faults outside the protected zone. Overreaching can
be problematic because it affects the selectivity of the protection system and potentially causes
widespread power failures or interruptions.
Underreaching, on the other hand, occurs when a protective relay fails to detect and trip for a fault
within its designated protection zone. It means that the relay does not reach far enough to protect
the desired portion of the power system. Underreaching can be equally problematic as it affects the
security and reliability of the power system, as faults within the protection zone can go undetected
and cause further damage or failures.
Both overreaching and underreaching can be caused by a variety of factors, including incorrect relay
settings, miscoordination between relays, inadequate fault detection algorithms, communication
errors, or improper application of protection systems. These problems must be carefully considered
during the design, commissioning and maintenance of distance protection systems to ensure effective
and reliable operation.

uniform coverage for fault resistance along the line.
2.2.4
Influence of Infeed
The infeed effect refers to the impact of power supply sources located within the protected zone
on the operation of distance relays. In traditional distance protection schemes, the relay measures
the impedance or apparent impedance of the protected line to determine if a fault has occurred.
However, when there are power supply sources within the protected zone, the fault currents can
flow in both directions, resulting in a complex fault current pattern.
The infeed effect poses challenges for distance relays in accurately detecting and precisely identifying
fault locations. Here are two key facets of the infeed effect:
Ambiguity in Fault Direction: When a power system has infeed from both ends, fault currents
can flow in both directions. This creates a challenge for relays in determining the direction of
the fault current. The relay needs to differentiate between fault currents flowing towards it and
12
==PAGE:22==
those flowing away from it. This ambiguity in fault direction detection can pose difficulties for the
relay in accurately identifying the correct fault location. As a result, the relay may face challenges
in making timely and accurate tripping decisions. If the fault direction is misinterpreted or not
properly determined, there is a risk of delayed or incorrect tripping actions. This can impact the
overall performance of the protection system and potentially lead to prolonged fault durations or
unnecessary power interruptions.
Estimation of Fault Impedance: When there are infeed sources in a power system, fault currents
encounter additional impedance elements along their path. These infeed sources, such as generators
or interconnected lines, contribute to the overall impedance observed by the relay during a fault
event. However, accurately estimating the fault impedance becomes challenging due to the complex
interaction of fault currents coming from different directions and the impedance contributions from
these infeed sources. The presence of infeed sources alters the apparent impedance seen by the relay,
making it difficult to precisely determine the exact location of the fault within the protected zone.
The relay relies on accurate fault impedance estimation to make informed decisions about fault
detection and tripping actions. However, the interaction of fault currents from various directions
and the impedance contributions from infeed sources introduce uncertainties and complexities that
can impact the relay’s ability to accurately estimate the fault impedance. This challenge in fault
impedance estimation can have implications for the overall performance of the protection system.
If the fault impedance is not accurately determined, it may lead to delays in fault detection or
incorrect identification of the fault location. This, in turn, can affect the selectivity and reliability
of the protection scheme, potentially leading to inefficient fault isolation or unnecessary tripping
actions.

2.2.6
Pilot Schemes
In practical terms, achieving full coverage of a transmission line with a single zone in distance
protection is not feasible due to the limited reach of these protection elements. It is not possible
to set the reach of these elements to cover the entire line length, typically 100 percent.
How-
ever, by positioning two distance relays at each end of the transmission line, full coverage can be
achieved.
Pilot schemes are employed to enhance the coverage and reliability of distance protection. These
schemes involve the simultaneous measurement and monitoring of system parameters at various
terminals of the transmission line, including both local and remote locations. By comparing the
conditions at both ends of the line simultaneously, pilot schemes accurately determine whether a
fault exists within the protected section or externally. This approach offers fast and comprehensive
protection coverage for the entire length of the line.
On the other hand, pilot schemes of protection are another approach that compares the conditions
at both ends of the line. This enables precise fault detection and localization within the protected
section. pilot schemes are effective in providing comprehensive protection coverage, but they have
a drawback. Unlike distance schemes, pilot schemes do not provide backup protection for adjacent
lines.
While pilot schemes offer localized protection for the specific line, distance schemes provide an
additional layer of protection by offering backup protection for adjacent lines.
This feature is
14
==PAGE:24==
beneficial in maintaining the reliability and stability of the overall power system.
In summary, while pilot schemes ensure accurate protection coverage for the protected section of
the line, distance schemes with pilot schemes can provide both comprehensive coverage for the
entire length of the line and backup protection for adjacent lines, enhancing the overall reliability
and effectiveness of the protection system.
The ideal solution combines the strengths of both approaches: almost instantaneous tripping along
the entire line length and backup protection for neighboring lines. This can be achieved by estab-
lishing communication channels that interconnect the distance protection relays at each end of the
protected line. This integrated scheme combines the benefits of both schemes, ensuring swift fault
detection and clearance across the entire line while also providing backup protection for adjacent
lines.
The primary objective of the communications channel is to facilitate the transmission of system
condition information between the two ends of the protected line. This includes conveying requests
to either initiate or prevent the tripping of the remote circuit breaker. The scheme in which tripping
signals are transmitted is commonly referred to as a ”transfer tripping scheme”. It’s important
to note that the specific terminology used for these schemes can vary significantly based on local
customs and practices.
Permissive Under-reach Transfer Tripping Scheme (PUTT): In this scheme, the underreaching
distance element (zone 1) initiates the transmission of a permissive bit (TX) to the remote terminal.
Tripping action is accelerated at the remote terminal when its overreaching zone (zone 2) is activated
and receives the permissive signal (RX) from the local terminal. PUTT is commonly used when the
local terminal has a higher level of authority or control. The concept of PUTT scheme is illustrated
on Figure 2.13.

Protection Issues of Cable Lines
Protecting AC cable circuits presents several distinctive challenges that are typically not experi-
enced while protecting overhead transmission lines [8]. The electrical properties of high-voltage
underground AC transmission cables differ substantially from those of overhead transmission lines.
It is crucial to understand the impact of cable grounding methods on the series sequence impedances
of underground cables, as this is fundamental to ensuring effective protection. Calculating the se-
ries sequence impedance of cable circuits requires accounting for magnetic coupling between phase
currents and, in certain cases, among currents in the cable sheaths[9].
Conducting short circuit studies for cables can be a complex, primarily due to the involvement of
various contributing factors[10],[11]:
1. The zero-sequence impedance is influenced by the characteristics of the return path, which,
in turn, are affected by factors such as the grounding method of the sheath, the presence of
parallel circuits, and the ground resistivity. It is important to consider the various grounding
techniques used and analyze how they impact the zero-sequence impedance. Additionally,
the ground parameters and the location of parallel circuits may exhibit variations along the
cable path, further emphasizing the need for careful analysis.
2. In pipe-type cables, which provide provide a combination of electrical conductivity and me-
chanical protection, the zero-sequence impedance is directly influenced by the permeability
of the steel enclosure, which, in turn, is contingent upon the zero-sequence current. Conse-
quently, the zero-sequence impedance exhibits a significant level of dependence on the current
flowing through it.
The Z0/Z1 ratio is not only dependent on current but can also differ significantly from average
values observed in overhead line applications. Therefore, careful consideration must be given to
the range, resolution, and accuracy of the zero-sequence compensating factor settings[12].
Mutual coupling between parallel cable lines can introduce coupling effects and affect the impedance
measurements. This can result in variations in the apparent impedance, potentially affecting the
accuracy of distance protection schemes.

Grid Code Compliance of Type IV Wind Turbines
In this section, we focus on the critical topic of grid code compliance, with a specific emphasis
on Type IV wind turbines. As the integration of renewable energy sources, like wind power, into
established electrical grids continues to expand, the adherence to grid codes becomes paramount.
17
==PAGE:27==
Grid codes encompass a collection of technical requirements and regulations designed to guarantee
the stability, reliability, and safety of power systems. They establish performance standards and
operational criteria that wind turbines must meet to enable their smooth integration into the grid.
Moreover, we will also examine the impact of Integrating Battery Resources (IBR) on system
protection. By exploring the influence of IBR on system protection, we can gain valuable insights
into the interaction between energy storage technologies and the overall protection scheme of the
power system.
2.3.1
Grid Code
The significant growth in wind power capacity integrated into transmission systems necessitates
the uninterrupted operation of wind generation during network disturbances. Consequently, recent
grid codes consistently require wind farms, particularly those connected to high-voltage grids, to
withstand voltage dips down to a specified percentage of the nominal voltage (which can even be
0% in some cases) for a specified duration. These requirements, known as Fault Ride Through
(FRT) or Low Voltage Ride Through (LVRT), are defined by voltage vs.
time characteristics
that specify the minimum level of immunity required for wind power stations. FRT requirements
also encompass the rapid restoration of active and reactive power to their pre-fault values once
the system voltage returns to normal levels. Some codes additionally mandate increased reactive
power generation by wind turbines during disturbances to provide voltage support, resembling the
behavior of conventional SGs operating in an over-excited state.
Furthermore, certain grid codes stipulate that wind farms should supply reactive power during a
network fault to support and expedite the restoration of grid voltage. For instance, the german code
from E.ON Nets requires wind farms to bolster the grid voltage by specifying the desired behavior
of connected generators during system disturbances. This includes voltage regulation, power factor
limits, reactive power supply, response to system faults (short-circuits), response to grid frequency
changes, and the ability to withstand brief interruptions in the connection. Additionally, wind
farms may be required to supply additional reactive current during voltage dips or increase their
reactive power consumption in the event of a voltage swell.
Voltage control actions should be
initiated within 20 ms of fault recognition by providing extra reactive current on the low voltage
side of the wind turbine transformer, equivalent to at least 2% of the rated current for each percent
of the voltage dip. If necessary, the wind turbines must be capable of delivering a reactive power
output of at least 100% of the rated current[15].

• Area A: The wind power plant is required to remain connected to the grid and continue

• Area B: The wind power plant must maintain grid connection while providing maximum
voltage support. This involves supplying controlled reactive current to ensure that the wind

current wind power plant technology.
• Area C: Disconnection of the wind power plant is permitted.
The majority of grid codes require wind power plants to curtail their active power output upon

ramp-down rates. These limitations are implemented to mitigate significant frequency fluctuations

Type IV Wind Turbines and Control Strategies
Type IV wind turbines refer to a specific classification of wind turbine technology. These turbines
are characterized by certain design features and operational capabilities that distinguish them from
other types of wind turbines.
WTG Type IV uses a pair of back-to-back converters that are directly connected to the stator of the
electrical machine in the WTG as can be seen on Figure 2.17. The wind turbine system comprises
various equipment, with the protection starting at the busbar. The MV switchgear incorporates
protective functions, including overcurrent protection, designed to detect internal faults within the
WTG system. After the MV switchgear, the MV/LV transformer steps down the voltage level to
match the rated value of the wind turbine converter.
Advancements in power electronics are enhancing the competitiveness of this concept in comparison
to Type III turbines, positioning it as the preferred option for offshore installations.
Type IV
turbines commonly utilize permanent magnet generators (PMGs) to convert the mechanical energy
from the rotor into electrical energy.
PMGs offer high efficiency, compact size, and improved
performance under varying wind conditions. This type of turbines are equipped with advanced
control systems that enable seamless integration with the electrical grid. These control systems
facilitate grid code compliance, voltage regulation, and support for grid stabilization measures,
enhancing the overall performance and stability of the wind power system.
Figure 2.17: Typical representation of a Type IV Wind Turbine [18]
A converter of Type IV wind turbines refers to the power electronic converter used in this specific
type of wind turbine system. The converter plays a crucial role in converting the variable AC power
generated by the wind turbine into usable electricity that can be fed into the grid or used locally.
Type IV wind turbines are known for their advanced converter technology, which enables efficient
power conversion and grid integration.
The converter of a Type IV wind turbine is designed to meet the specific requirements of the system,
20
==PAGE:30==
taking into account factors such as power capacity, voltage levels, and grid connection standards.
It typically consists of various components, including semiconductor devices, control circuits, and
filtering elements. The converter controls the operation of the wind turbine, regulating its output
power, voltage, and frequency to ensure optimal performance and compliance with grid codes and
standards.
Type IV wind turbine converters often incorporate advanced control algorithms, such as maximum
power point tracking (MPPT) to optimize energy capture from the wind, grid synchronization for
seamless integration with the electrical grid, and various protection mechanisms to ensure safe and
reliable operation.
The stator circuit of Type IV wind turbines is completely decoupled from the grid. As a result,
when a short circuit occurs, the stator does not experience the short circuit directly. Instead, the
contribution of the short circuit depends on the control action of the Grid-Side Converter (GSC).
Type IV turbines exhibit a very low short circuit contribution due to the decoupling of the stator.
However, this may pose challenges in detecting fault situations[19].
The fault ride-through capability of this WTG type is superior to others. When a fault occurs,
the excess active power produced by the electrical machine is dissipated through the chopper. The
active power output during the fault is dependent on the retained voltage in the grid, and the GSC
can function as a STATCOM by supplying reactive power. Once the fault ends, the chopper is
disconnected, and the active power is immediately transferred back to the grid through the GSC.
The reactive power output of the electrical machine and the reactive power output to the grid are
completely separate in this WTG type. The reactive power output to the grid is solely dependent
on the action of the GSC. Therefore, the range of reactive power output depends on the size of the
GSC. Additionally, the GSC must also deliver the active power, and any remaining capacity can
be utilized for reactive power output[20].
Type IV wind turbines can also provide reactive power support to the grid to regulate voltage
levels and improve grid stability. Reactive power control strategies involve adjusting the turbine’s
output reactive power by manipulating the converter’s control parameters, such as the voltage
and current phase angles, to maintain desired voltage profiles and power factor. In this particular
type of WTG, the production of reactive power by the electrical machine and its injection into
the grid are completely separate entities. The amount of reactive power delivered to the grid is
solely dependent on the operation of GSC, which controls this aspect. Consequently, the range of
reactive power output is determined by the size of the GSC. It should be noted that the WTG is
also responsible for supplying active power, and any remaining capacity can be allocated for the
generation of reactive power.
Nowadays most of the European Grid Codes do not require negative sequence injection in case
of unbalanced faults and they usually implement only positive sequence injection for any type of
fault, then short circuit current contribution from Full Converter will be balanced in that case [21].
Following the occurrence of a fault, there is a brief transitional period lasting approximately 40-60
ms during which the behavior of currents is unpredictable. During this transient phase, the current
waveforms deviate from pure sinusoidal shapes, which can introduce inaccuracies in impedance
measurements. However, once this transient period concludes, the converter control system operates
according to the implemented strategy, ensuring that the current stays within predefined limits set
by the control strategy. In the steady state that follows, impedance measurements are expected to
21
==PAGE:31==
be accurate, and the occurrence of impedance measurement errors is unlikely.
These control strategies are implemented through complex control algorithms and control systems
that utilize measurements from various sensors, communication interfaces, and advanced control
techniques. The goal is to optimize the wind turbine’s performance, maximize energy capture,
ensure grid compatibility, and support grid stability and reliability.
2.3.3
Impact of IBR on System Protection
Integrating Inverter-Based Resources (IBRs), such as solar and wind power plants, into the power
system has a significant impact on system protection. IBRs typically have limited fault current
contribution compared to conventional SGs. This can affect the coordination and operation of
protective devices, particularly during fault conditions.
Special attention is required to ensure
accurate fault current calculations and coordination with other protection devices. The presence
of IBRs can alter the fault current paths and fault impedance characteristics within the power
system. This can impact the coordination of protection devices, such as relays and circuit breakers.
Protective device settings and coordination schemes may need to be modified to ensure effective
fault detection, isolation, and system restoration.
Typically, there are several distinct fault response characteristics observed in Inverter-Based Re-
sources[22]:
• Fault current magnitude: In comparison to SGs, the fault current magnitude of Inverter-Based
Resources is typically lower.
• Fault sequence quantities: The fault current generated by the inverter does not include a zero
sequence component, and the presence of the negative sequence current component is either
fully or partially suppressed.
• Power factor / phase angle of fault current: The power factor and phase angle of the inverter
depend significantly on the inverter’s reactive voltage and power control mode. This, in turn,
has an impact on the angular relationship between fault voltages and currents, potentially
affecting the proper functioning of directional relays.
• Duration of fault current: During the initial transient response, the IBR may exhibit fault
currents that exceed the nominal values of the converter due to the reaction time of the
control system. However, after this brief period (typically up to a few cycles), the current
is regulated according to the control settings to prevent exceeding the thermal limits of the
switches.
During the LL fault, it was observed that a Type IV wind turbine with CSC (Coupled Sequence
Control) deviated from the expected phase angle difference mentioned earlier, resulting in a phase
angle difference of -20°. This deviation caused the detection zone to be inaccurately determined,
leading to relay maloperation.
It is important to note that communication-assisted protection
schemes relying on the proper operation of 50Q(overcurrent) and 67Q(directional) relays may also
experience malfunctions in such cases. Additionally, when a fault identification scheme is employed
in the protection of Inverter-Based Resources, there is a possibility of incorrectly identifying the
faulted phase, which can result in erroneous circuit breaker tripping[23].

A pipe-type cable was chosen for the project. A pipe-type cable, also known as a fluid-filled cable or
a filled pressure cable, is a type of power cable used for transmitting electrical energy. It consists of
a conductor, typically made of copper or aluminum, which is insulated and surrounded by a pressur-
ized fluid. Pipe-type cables are often used in high-voltage and extra-high-voltage applications, such
as for long-distance power transmission or subsea cables. They offer several advantages, including
high power carrying capacity, good thermal characteristics, and excellent dielectric properties. The
fluid insulation also provides additional protection against electrical discharges and helps reduce
the risk of cable failure. In addition to their electrical properties, pipe-type cables are also known
for their mechanical strength and durability. They are designed to withstand various environmental
conditions, including extreme temperatures, moisture, and mechanical stresses. Overall, pipe-type
cables are a reliable and efficient solution for transmitting electrical power over long distances or
in challenging environments, where high voltage and reliability are critical.
This cable model is essentially a collection of coaxial cables encompassed within a pipe conductor.
The inner coaxial cables are defined by a centre solid conductor, and can have up to three concentric
conductors about the centre. Each conductor is separated by an insulator. The pipe cable cross-
section is used to define the geometric position and the conductor/insulator properties of the cable
system.
The frequency dependent model is the most accurate model available at present for the mod-
elling of cables[28]. Cables, especially long and high-capacity ones, exhibit frequency-dependent
impedance and admittance. These variations can affect the transmission of power and introduce
impedance mismatches, leading to power losses, voltage drop, and other undesirable effects. By
using a frequency-dependent cable model, engineers can accurately capture these variations and

Mho relay settings or Mho characteristics, are parameters used to configure distance protection
relays in power systems. The Mho zone is a circular region in the impedance plane that represents
the operating characteristics of a distance relay. The Mho zone settings determine the size and
32
==PAGE:42==
location of this zone. The reach of the Mho zone determines the distance from the relay location to
the edge of the zone. It is typically set based on the length of the protected transmission line. Mho

and isolated, while faults outside the zones do not trigger unnecessary tripping. These settings
play a critical role in the overall performance and coordination of the protection scheme, ensuring
prompt fault detection, and minimizing the impact on system stability and reliability.

3.4.3
POTT scheme
Permissive overreach transfer trip is a protective scheme used in power systems. It allows a pro-
tective relay located at the remote end of a transmission line to send a trip signal to the relay at
the local end, based on the detection of a fault condition. This scheme operates by monitoring the
34
==PAGE:44==
fault current and voltage at both ends of the line. If the remote relay detects a fault within its
protected zone, it sends a permissive signal to the local relay, indicating that it should also trip to
isolate the fault.
The concept of overreach in this scheme refers to the intentional extension of the protected zone
beyond the physical boundaries of the transmission line. This is done to ensure that faults occurring
close to the remote end of the line are cleared quickly and effectively. By allowing the remote relay
to send a permissive signal for faults beyond its immediate coverage area, the scheme provides a
faster response to fault conditions, enhancing system reliability and reducing the risk of damage to
equipment.
The transfer trip aspect of the scheme involves the actual transmission of the trip signal from the
remote relay to the local relay. This can be achieved through various communication channels,
such as dedicated communication lines or telecommunication networks. The local relay receives
the permissive signal and verifies the fault condition before initiating a trip command to open the
circuit breaker, isolating the faulty section of the line.
The permissive overreach transfer trip scheme plays a crucial role in enhancing the coordination
and efficiency of protection systems in power networks. It allows for rapid fault detection, isolation,
and restoration of the system’s normal operation, thereby minimizing the impact of faults on the
overall power grid.
Figure 3.13 and Figure 3.14 show how the POTT scheme is implemented in the PSCAD model.
The transfer trip signal TT-1 outgoing from Zone 2 Tripping signals and used as input for distance
protection relay of circuit breaker 2 (CB2) which is placed at the other end of the transmission line.
Similarly, the TT-2 signal is used for circuit breaker 1. The logic of POTT is that if overeaching
occurs on the pick up of the zone and if both sides pick up zone 2 it means the fault is within the
protected line and POTT allows to trip faster not waiting for 0.2s of Zone 2 time delay.

5
Discussion
Conducting comprehensive tests on the distance protection of a power system that incorporated
synchronous generators and an overhead transmission line served as a valuable validation of the
implemented distance protection model.
However, the transition from an overhead line to a cable posed several challenges, resulting in
maloperations of the circuit breaker. These challenges, in addition to the ones discussed in previous
sections, were further exacerbated by the presence of fault resistance and the double in-feed, leading
to inaccuracies in impedance calculations. Moreover, an interesting observation was made regarding
the discrepancy in fault location detection between single line to ground faults and phase-to-phase
faults, highlighting the need for a re-evaluation of the zero compensation factor, a critical component
of distance protection.
The integration of the offshore wind farm model significantly impacted the performance of dis-
tance protection, particularly in non-solid fault scenarios. Notably, a majority of the calculated
impedances fell outside their designated zones, attributable to the weak in-feed from the wind farm
and the primary contribution of current flowing from the grid. However, the inclusion of a circuit
breaker on the grid side played a crucial role in improving the overall system performance by of-
fering selectivity for fault clearance, especially in cases where the distance relay on the wind farm
side failed to detect the fault.
The addition of a parallel cable to the system demonstrated an ”enhancement” in the performance
of distance protection during non-solid faults by reducing the amount of current from the grid.
Furthermore, extensive investigations were conducted to explore various parameters that could
potentially impact the impedance. However, it is worth emphasizing the importance of simultane-
ously examining the impact of fault resistance and SCR variation, as it can greatly contribute to
the investigations. The implementation of the POTT scheme yielded positive results by enhancing
selectivity in the operations of circuit breakers, further improving the overall performance of the
power system.
The issues highlighted in the investigation primarily focused on forward faults, leaving room to
explore the capabilities of distance protection of circuit breakers to operate effectively in the case of
reverse faults. The effectiveness of distance protection for reverse faults depends on several factors,
including the ability to accurately measure and analyze fault parameters, the sensitivity to detect
reverse fault currents, and the coordination with other protection elements in the system. These
aspects need to be thoroughly investigated to ensure reliable operation and timely fault clearance
in the case of reverse faults. Moreover, generally inverter based generators inject a small amount
of negative sequence components, which in turn affects the impedance calculation. The negative
sequence components generated by these inverters introduce asymmetry into the system, causing
deviations from the normal balanced operation. As a result, the apparent impedance observed by
the distance protection relays can be further influenced by the presence of these negative sequence
components. Another thing to mention, impedance-based distance protection may be susceptible to
power swings or large changes in system conditions. During power swings, rapid oscillations occur
in the voltage and current waveforms of a power system. These oscillations can have a significant
impact on the apparent impedance observed by distance protection schemes. The rapid changes
in the voltage and current during power swings can cause variations in the apparent impedance,
leading to potential issues such as false tripping or delayed fault detection.

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis has provided a comprehensive analysis of selectivity strategies employed
for parallel export cables in offshore wind farms. The research aimed to evaluate the effectiveness
of existing distance protection methods and address any associated challenges or limitations.
The literature review conducted in this study confirmed the crucial role played by distance protec-
tion schemes in ensuring the reliable operation of parallel export cables. The review underscored
the significance of various factors, including cable characteristics, system configuration, and fault
types, in determining the efficacy of these protection schemes.
The evaluation of different distance protection methods revealed both strengths and weaknesses in
impedance-based protection approaches. While impedance-based methods offer simplicity and ease
of implementation, they can encounter difficulties associated with double in-feed and grid strength.
These challenges highlight the importance of carefully reviewing and adjusting protection relays
to ensure accurate fault detection and selectivity. In addition to the aforementioned findings, the
investigation also shed light on the significance of the zero compensation factor for cables in distance
protection schemes. The zero compensation factor plays a critical role in accurately calculating the
impedance and detecting faults in cable systems. Proper evaluation and adjustment of this factor
are essential to ensure reliable fault detection and selectivity.
Furthermore, the investigation highlighted the impact of parallel operation on the performance
of distance protection schemes. Factors such as fault resistance necessitate thorough review and
adjustment of protection relays to ensure their ability to detect faults accurately and maintain
selectivity.
It is important to note that although the focus of the investigation primarily centered on forward
faults, further exploration of the capabilities of distance protection schemes in handling reverse
faults is needed. Understanding the behavior of circuit breakers and the coordination of protection
elements in reverse fault scenarios is crucial for ensuring the reliable operation of the protection
system.
In summary, this thesis provides valuable insights into the selectivity strategies of parallel export
cables in offshore wind farms. The findings underscore the importance of considering cable char-
acteristics and system configuration to enhance the reliability, selectivity, and speed of distance
protection schemes.
