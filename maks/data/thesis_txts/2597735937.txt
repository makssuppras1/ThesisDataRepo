Abstract
The increased level of greenhouse gasses in the atmosphere are causing global warming,
resulting in raised sea levels hurricanes etc. CO2 is considered to be the most dominant
greenhouse gas in affecting the climate, which means that CO2 capture is a top priority.
Many technologies exist for CO2 capture, but a new approach is to use amino acid-based
ionic liquids (AAIL). These are environmental friendly, simple to synthesize, non-toxic
etc, and the most commonly used are tetraalkylphosphonium, thetraalkylammonium and
imidazolium. Choline can be applied as cation in these AAILs, thus resulting in an even
cheaper and more biodegradable IL.
In this study, choline-prolinate IL [Ch][Pro] is synthesized and characterized using 1H-
NMR, 13C-NMR, and ATR-IR. Furhtermore, the working CO2 capacity of this IL at dif-
ferent temperatures and with different desorption methods is measured by running mul-
tiple cycles of adsorption/desorption. The adsorption enthalpy is calculated using van’t
Hoff equation and the thermal stability at 80°C of [Ch][Pro] is examined.
The working CO2 capacity of supported [Ch][Pro] was measured to be up to 7.5 g CO2/kg
SILP at 40 °C with adsorption by vacuum at 0.1 bar and 80 °C. This is comparable to
that of before mentioned commonly used AAILs, due to the low molecular weight of
[Ch][Pro].
The CO2 capacity of bulk [Ch][Pro] during ﬁrst adsorption cycle was measured to be ap-
proximately 1 mol CO2/mol IL, suggesting that the mechanism of adsorption follows the
1:1 mechanism. However the adsorption capacity decreases signiﬁcantly after the ﬁrst
cycle, indicating that the desorption requires high energy. The enthalpy of adsorption was
measured to be -16.78 kJ/mol, almost 30 times higher than that of [P4444][Pro] thus sup-
porting the hypothesis that the desorption process is inadequate.
The IL was shown to be long-term thermally stable at 67 °C, which means that it would
be suitable for CO2 capture from biogas at 40 °C with regeneration at 65 °C.
The ﬁndings of this study suggest that separating CO2 from biogas using [Ch][Pro] as a
non-toxic, cheap and biodegradable alternative to other ILs, may be possible.

Introduction
Since the mid-20th century, the level of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere has increased
signiﬁcantly, as a result of emission of greenhouse gasses [1]. The atmospheric CO2 level
the last 800,000 years is shown in Figure 1. In the 1860s John Tyndall demonstrated that
CO2 and other greenhouse gasses are heat absorbing, and he suggested that a change in
the composition of the atmosphere would affect the Earth’s temperature [2], which was
shown to be true. Global warming is measured by the change in global mean surface air
temperature, which has increased by 0.8 °C during the last decade, due to the increased
concentration of greenhouse gasses in the atmosphere, and CO2 is believed to be the most
dominant greenhouse gas in affecting the climate [3, 4].
Global warming has caused rising sea level, declining arctic sea ice, hurricanes, bushﬁres,
etc. and in 2017 a climate warning call for the public was signed by 15,000 scientists
[1, 5]. In 2015, Wu et al. published a study on the reversibility of CO2 induced cli-
mate changes, and they found no threshold beyond which changes in global mean surface
temperature, the global hydrological cycle, oceanic thermal expansion, Arctic sea ice,
the Atlantic meridional overturning circulation and the South Asia Monsoon becomes ir-
reversible [6]. This suggests that a reduction of CO2 in the atmosphere would reverse
global warming.
Figure 1: Level of atmospheric carbon dioxide in parts per million during the last 800,000
years [1].
Several approaches has been made to reduce the emission of greenhouse gases, such as
CO2 capture from ﬂue gas. CO2 capture from ﬂue gas is performed by three general
Page 2 of 55
==PAGE:8==
techniques: post-combustion capture, pre-compustion capture, and oxy-fuel combustion
capture, of which the former is the most widely use. These are described in Section 1.3.
Another approach is to replace coal with renewable energy, including wind and solar
energy as well as biogas. Biogas consists primarily of methane and CO2, and the produc-
tion involves anaerobic decomposition of organic material by microorganisms [7]. Even
though almost half of biogas is CO2, it is said to be CO2-neutral, because it is the same
amount of CO2 that is absorbed during growth of the organic material [7]. This means
that if the CO2 from biogas where to be captured, it would reduce the CO2 level in the
atmosphere, thus reversing global warming.
CO2 capture can be performed in several ways, but ionic liquids have shown to be effec-
tive in reversibly and selectively absorb CO2, and especially the amino acid-based ones
are attractive because of their non-toxicity and low-cost [8, 9, 10].
In this study, cholinium-amino acid-based ionic liquids will be synthesized, characterized
using NMR, ATR-IR, and TGA, and their ability to reversibly absorb CO2 will be ex-
amined. The purpose is to investigate the possibility of using this type of ionic liquid to
separate CO2 from biogas.

1.1
Biogas
1.1
Biogas
The composition of biogas is 50-75% methane, 25-50% CO2, 1-2% combined of hy-
drogen sulﬁde, hydrogen, and ammonia, and traces of carbon monoxide, nitrogen, and
oxygen [7, 11]. Since the caloriﬁc value of biogas is directly related to the content of
methane, it is desired to obtain a biogas consisting of as much methane as possible, which
is dependent on the material that is being decomposed. Biogas is the waste product from
anaerobic decomposition of organic matter, and this process is divided into four phases,
for which different groups of bacteria are responsible: hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetoge-
nesis, and methanogenesis [12, 13]. On Figure 2 is a simpliﬁed schematic illustration of
the process.
Figure 2: Simpliﬁed schematic illustration of the production of biogas [12, 13]
The ﬁrst phase, hydrolysis, is considered the rate-limiting step, and the yield of methane is
dependent on the extent of this step [11, 12]. In this extracellular process, carbohydrates,
proteins, and fats are decomposed to amino acids, sugars, and long-chain fatty acids, by
speciﬁc enzymes produced by different bacteria, e.g. cellulose and hemicellulose are eas-
ily hydrolysed by cellulase and hemicellulase respectively. Unfortunately cellulose and
hemicellulose is packed in lignin, which does not decompose under aerobic conditions,

which means that the process is not absolute [7].
During the second step, acidogenesis, sugars, amino acids and fatty acids are further bro-
ken down and the main product is carboxylic acids, but also hydrogen, alcohols, ammonia,
short-chain volatile fatty acids and CO2 are produced [12, 13]. The bacterial community
that are responsible for hydrolysis and acidogenesis include several thousand bacterial
genera and are called primary fermenters [13].
The products from acidogenesis are converted during the third step, acetogenesis, to form
mainly acetic acid, but also hydrogen and CO2 [12]. Homoacetogenesis do also occur in
this step, which is the synthesis of acetic acid from hydrogen and CO2 [13]. The bacteria
that are responsible acetogenesis and homoacetogenesis are called secondary fermenters
and homoacetogenic bacteria, respectively. The reverse reaction of homoacetogenesis,
is syntrophic acetate oxidation, which enables an alternative synthesis of methane from
acetic acid, and the diversity of the bacterial community responsible for this is much lower
than that of the fermenters [13].
The last phase of anaerobic digestion is methanogenesis, during which the products from
the earlier step is converted to methane and CO2. This happens by three different path-
ways: hydrogenotrophic, aceticlastic, and methylotrophic. In the hydrogenotrophic path-
way methane and water is produced by reduction of CO2 with hydrogen, see Equation (1).
In the aceticlastic pathway, acetic acid is cleaved to form methane and CO2, see Equa-
tion (2), while methane is formed from C1-molecules, such as methanol, in the methy-
lotrophic pathway, see Equation (3) [7, 13].

Ionic Liquids
1.2
Ionic Liquids
Ionic liquids are deﬁned as salts with melting point below 100 °C, and most of them are
composed of an organic cation and a polyatomic anion [14]. The low melting point of
ionic liquids is due to the lack of efﬁcient packing of the bulky cation with the smaller an-
ion [15]. These non-aqueous but polar solvents are an alternative to volatile and polluting
organic solvents, such as benzene and methylene chloride [16]. They are characterized by
high chemical and thermal stability, high conductivity, negligible vapor pressure, and high
decomposition temperature, which makes them applicable for various functions, includ-
ing as solvent in syntheses [17, 18, 19]. The major advantage of ILs is that their physical
and chemical properties depend on the composition of anion and cation, which means that
they can be designed for a speciﬁc application. The ions can even be chemically modiﬁed
with functional groups, called functionalized ILs.
The ﬁrst ionic liquid, ethylammonium nitrate, was discovered in 1914 by Paul Walden
[20]. However, the potential of ionic liquids were unnoticed, and it wasn’t until much
later that the ionic liquids got their breakthrough. Generally, ionic liquids can be divided
into three generations.
The ﬁrst generation took place in the 1970s and early 1980s, and included primarily ionic
liquids composed of dialkylimidazolium and alkylpyridinium derivatives as cations, and
chloroaluminate and other metal halides as anions [20]. These systems were studied by
electrochemists, to function as battery electrolytes [21]. Unfortunately, choloraluminate
can only be handled in inert-gas atmosphere, due to its sensitivity to water and oxygen,
which limited the development of these ionic liquids [20, 21].
The second generation appeared in the 1990s, where the anions were replaced by water-
and oxygen-stable halides or halogen-containing molecules. In spite of their disadvan-
tages, such as high cost and toxicity, ionic liquids have attracted much attention in this
generation due to their changeable properties, reliant on the cation-anion combination
[21].
The third generation is currently ongoing, and includes non-toxic, biodegradable ionic
liquids, with hydrophobic anions, such as amino acids, sugars, alkylsulfates, etc. [20].
Besides their non-toxicity and biodegradability, these ionic liquids have many advantages,
including low cost, simple synthesis, and no requirement of puriﬁcation.

1.2.1
Amino Acid-Based Ionic Liquids
Amino acid-based ionic liquids (AAILs) has, as the name indicates, an amino acid as
anion, and is a part of the third generation of ILs. Besides the previous mentioned ad-
vantages of third generation ILs, AAILs are beneﬁcial because it is easy to obtain large
amounts of pure amino acids, and because both the amino group and the carboxylic acid
group on the amino acid can be modiﬁed, to form functionalized ILs [18].
Some of the frequently used cations for AAILs are imidazolium, tetraalkylammonium,
and tetraalkylphosphonium, which are shown in Figure 3a-c [8, 9, 18, 22]. These com-
pounds, as cations in AAILs, were compared in a study performed by Ossowicz et al.
[22]. The study showed that the viscosity of the AAIL is dependent on the cation, and
that the distribution of the positive charge of the imidazolium cation, caused the imida-
zolium AAIL to have lowest viscosity. Results from the study implied that decreasing
molecular weight results in increasing thermal stability.
Figure 3: Frequently used cations for AAILs: a) imidazolium, b) tetraalkylammonium, c)
tetraalkylphosphonium. R1,2,3,4 = CH3(CH2)n, n ∈N. d) Choline.
Recently, choline-based AAILs ([Ch][AA]) have attracted attention, since choline is an
essential nutrient for cells, and its metabolism resemble that of an amino acid, which
makes it bio-compatible. The structure of choline is similar to that of tetraalkylammo-
nium, but with a hydroxyethyl group and three methyl groups, see Figure 3d. Another
advantage of choline is that it is much less expensive than the before mentioned cations.
In April 2020 the price of 25 g 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium chloride (≥99%), tetrabuty-
lammonium chloride (≥99%), and tetrabutylphosphonium chloride (96%) is 2100 DKK,
3810 DKK, and 4425 respectively. The same amount of choline chloride (≥99%) is 629
DKK [23].
Santis et al. [24] examined 14 different AAILs with choline as cation. They discovered
that the viscosity increases with increasing molecular weight, and that the viscosity of

AAILs composed of amino acids with a hetero atom on the side chain, is approximately
one order of magnitude higher than the viscosity of those containing only alkyl groups in
the side chain. This is explained by the increasing van der Waals interactions as a result
of longer chains and the ability of hetero atoms to make hydrogen bonds. Of the AAILs
considered in their study, [CH][Ala] has the lowest viscosity, followed by [Ch][Gly].
Bhattacharyya and Shah [25] have investigated the thermal stability of 13 different choline-
based AAILs but with modiﬁed choline, in form of etheriﬁcaion and extension of one of
the methyl groups to longer chains. Their study showed that longer chain and etheriﬁca-
tion reduced the thermal stability, and that all the AAILs with choline, short chain and no
etheriﬁcation were long-term (500 minutes) thermally stable at 100 °C in nitrogen.
The density of the AAIL is mainly affected by alkyl chain lengths on both the cation and
anion. Increasing alkyl chain length on the cation, results in decreasing density [26]. As
for the anion, the same trend applies for amino acids with only alkyl groups on the chain.
Functional groups on the chain of the amino acid increase the density of the AAIL due to
effects like hydrogen bonding [26].

of the ionic liquid depends on the number of primary and secondary amines on the anion.
They found that the greatest CO2 sorption capacity was found in AAILs with arginine
([Arg]) and lysine ([Lys]), which consists of two primary and two secondary amines and
one primary and one secondary amine respectively, as anions. Histidine ([His]), which has
one primary amine and one secondary amine in a ring, gave the third highest CO2 sorption

Supported Ionic Liquids
When ionic liquids are used as catalysts in their liquid form, there are some disadvantages
such as the relatively high viscosity of ionic liquids causing diffusion processes to be
limited, and the separation of products from the liquid phase causing small amounts of
ionic liquid and catalyst to be extracted as well [33]. These difﬁculties are overcome by
Figure 5: Illustration of the concept of the supported ionic liquid technology [34].
the ”supported ionic liquid” (SIL) technology, where the ionic liquid is immobilized on
a high-surface area porous solid support material [35]. The concept of this illustrated on
Page 11 of 55
==PAGE:17==
1.2
Ionic Liquids
Figure 5. This results in easy separation of the ionic liquid from the reaction mixture,
while the amount of ionic liquid required is reduced compared to the liquid-liquid bipha-
sic system [36].
There are multiple names and concepts of supporting ionic liquids, and an overview of
some of them were illustrated by Van Doorslaer et al. [37], shown in Figure 6.
In 2002 Mehnert et al. described the ﬁrst supported ionic liquid catalyst (SILC) [38]. A
monolayer of the ionic liquid was covalently bound to the surface of the support material,
followed by addition of more ionic liquid to form multiple free layers, in which a homo-
geneous catalyst was dissolved, as illustrated in Figure 6, type 1b. This enabled the solid
material to function as homogeneous catalyst due to the ﬂuid nature of the ionic liquid.
In 2003, Riisager et al. [39] described a similar concept; supported ionic liquid phase
(SILP). However, instead of anchoring the ionic liquid through a covalently bound mono-
layer, the ionic liquid was attached to the support by electrostatic and capillary forces, as
shown in Figure 6 type 1a.

1.3
CO2 Capture
1.3
CO2 Capture
For large-scale CO2 capture from use of fossil fuels and biomass, three basic systems ex-
ists: post-combustion capture, pre-combustion capture, and oxy-fuel combustion capture.
These systems are illustrated in Figure 7.
Figure 7: Illustration of CO2 capture systems [40]
Post-combustion capture is one of the most widely used technologies for CO2 capture.
The principle of this system is that the CO2 is separated from the ﬂue gas after combus-
tion in air of the fossil fuel or biomass, often by aqeous amine scrubbing, where CO2
is forced into the liquid phase of a counter-current ﬂow of aqueous monoethanolamine
[9, 40].
In pre-combustion capture carbon monoxide and hydrogen is produced by reacting the
fuel with oxygen, air and/or steam. Carbon monoxide is then converted to CO2 and more
hydrogen by reaction with steam, followed by separation of CO2 by physical or chemi-
cal absorption. The resulting hydrogen can then be applied in engines, fuel cells, etc. [40].
In oxy-fuel combustion capture the combustion is performed in pure oxygen instead of
air, which results in a ﬂue gas containing mainly CO2 and steam. High purity of oxygen is
ensured by separation of nitrogen from the combustion air prior to combustion. The CO2
is ready for storage after cooling, to condense thus remove water, compressing, drying,
and further puriﬁcation [40].
Page 14 of 55
==PAGE:20==
1.3
CO2 Capture
CO2 can be captured by three general methods: separation with solvent/sorbent, separa-
tion with membranes, and separation by distillation [40]. The ﬁrst mentioned technology
separates CO2 from the gas by capturing it on a liquid or solid sorbent, followed by heat-
ing to regenerate the sorbent material. In membrane separation, the ﬂow of gas through
a selective membrane is usually controlled by the difference in pressure over the mem-
brane, which makes this process favorable for high-pressure streams. When separating by
distillation, the gas needs to be liqueﬁed, which is achieved by compression, cooling and
expansion. The focus of this work is separation with solvent/sorbent, using ionic liquid
as liquid absorbent capturing CO2.
1.3.1
Mechanism of CO2 Absorption
The most commonly used technique for absorbing CO2 is aqueous amine scrubbing,
which follows the carbamate formation mechanism. CO2 substitutes a proton on the mo-
noethanolamine to form a carbamate, and the proton reacts with another monoethanolamine
to form ammonium, resulting in an ion pair [41]. This mechanism also applies for amino
acids. The amount of CO2 captured is n(CO2)/n(amine) = 0.5, and the mechanism is
shown in Figure 8.
Figure 8: Absorption of CO2 by monoethanolamine following the carbamate forming
mechanism.
Figure 9: Mechanism of absorption of CO2 by prolinate.
Absorption of CO2 by prolinate permits the more efﬁcient 1:1 mechanism (n(CO2)/n(amine)
= 1), because the amino acid is able to stabilize itself via a 7 membered ring upon reaction
with CO2, as shown in Figure 9 [42]. Glycinate, isoleucinate, lysinate, and asparaginate

is able to form the 7 membered ring as well, though this may not be their most stable
structure, as it is for prolinate [42].
1.3.2
Desorption of CO2
Generally, CO2 can be desorbed from amines in three ways: purge gas, heating, and vac-
uum, usually combined in one way or another.
The most widely studied technology for desorption of CO2 is temperature concentration
swing desorption (TCSD), in which the sorbent is purged with an inert gas while heated
[43]. The product of this is CO2 diluted in the purge gas, which can easily be separated
by condensation, when using steam in nitrogen instead of inert gas - a technology named
steam stripping.
The advantage of desorption with vacuum is that the product is pure CO2. Combining
vacuum with heating allows the applied vacuum to be milder, a technology called temper-
ature vacuum swing desorption [43].
Purge gas, heating, and vacuum is combined in the steam-assisted temperature vacuum
swing desorption process, resulting in decreased partial pressure of CO2, thus faster des-
orption [43].
For [AA]ILs, purge gas can not be applied due to decomposition of amino acid in oxy-
gen. Instead, inert gas can be applied following same methods. The focus of this work is
desorption of CO2 by vacuum and nitrogen gas ﬂow, both with and without heating.

1.4
Analytical Methods
1.4
Analytical Methods
For analysis of ionic liquids nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, infrared
(IR) spectroscopy, and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) have been applied in this work
as well as the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) analysis for measurement of pore volume
and surface area of support material. The principles of these analytical methods is de-
scribed in the following sections.
1.4.1
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy
The structure of a compound can be identiﬁed using nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)
spectroscopy. Atomic nuclei that possess odd atomic number and/or odd mass have a
quantized spin angular momentum and a magnetic moment and can therefore be analysed
using NMR spectroscopy, but the most commonly studied are hydrogen and carbon. Both
hydrogen and carbon have two allowed spin states, +½ when the magnetic moment of
the nucleus and the applied magnetic ﬁeld are aligned, and −½ when they are opposed.
The energy difference in the two spin states increases with the strength of the applied
magnetic ﬁeld, because the nucleus absorbs the energy and the change the orientation of
its spin state [44].
Figure 10: Schematic illustration of a NMR spectrometer [44]
The NMR spectrometer roughly consists of a magnet with great ﬁeld strength and a very
homogeneous ﬁeld, a radio frequency transmitter, a detector, and a computer, see Fig-
ure 10. The sample is placed in the magnetic ﬁeld and the radio frequency transmitter
Page 17 of 55
==PAGE:23==
1.4
Analytical Methods
emits a brief but powerful pulse of energy, causing the nuclei to excite. When the nuclei
relax, they emit electromagnetic radiation at different frequencies, which is measured by
the detector as a free-induction decay (FID) signal. The computer forms the NMR spec-
trum by deriving the individual frequencies from the FID signal using Fourier transform
[44].
The nucleus in a molecule is surrounded by electrons, which starts to circulate when a
magnetic ﬁeld is applied, thus forming an opposite magnetic ﬁeld that shields the nu-
cleus, an effect called local diamagnetic shielding. The nucleus is therefore deshielded by
electronegative substituents due to their electron-withdrawing effects. The measurements
are reported as chemical shift, which is the frequency difference between the measured
nucleus and a reference compound, whose nuclei are more shielded that those of most
other compounds [44].
1.4.2
Infrared Spectroscopy
Another method to obtain structural information about a molecule is infrared (IR) spec-
troscopy. The principle of this method is to excite molecules to higher energy states by
absorption of selected frequencies of infrared radiation. The stretching and bending vi-
brational frequencies of the covalent bonds in most molecules corresponds to the energy
changes (8-40 kJ/mole) that occur upon absorption of infrared radiation. The frequencies
absorbed, are the ones that match the natural vibrational frequencies of the molecule, and
the energy absorbed is causing the amplitude of the vibrational motions to increase. In or-
der for the energy to be transferred, the bond must have an electrical dipole that changes
at the same frequency as the radiation. The bonds can be determined due to the small
range of absorption of each type of bond [44].
The IR spectrometer roughly consists of an IR source, a beam splitter, two mirrors, a de-
tector and a computer, see Figure 11. The beam splitter is a mirror placed in a 45° angle,
separating the incoming radiation in two beams; one in a 90° angle, and one undeﬂected.
The former is reﬂected in a stationary mirror, and the latter is reﬂected in a moving mirror,
causing the wavelength to differ. This combined beam, containing all of the frequencies
in the IR spectrum, is called an interferogram, and is passed through the sample, to the
Page 18 of 55
==PAGE:24==
1.4
Analytical Methods
detector which measures the amount of energy at each wavelength. The computer com-
pares this to a reference laser beam, in order to calculate the absorbed frequencies, thus
forming the IR spectrum using Fourier transform [44].
Figure 11: Schematic illustration of Infrared Spectrometer [44]
1.4.3
Thermogravimetric Analysis
Thermogravitmetic analysis (TGA) is a method that treats the sample at different tem-
peratures and atmosphere while measuring the variation of weight of the sample. Due
to volatilization and decomposition, the sample will lose weight during the thermal treat-
ment [45].
Figure 12: Simpliﬁed schematic illustration of thermogravimetric analyzer [45]
In Figure 12 is a schematic illustration of a thermogravimetric analyzer. The sample is
placed in a cantilever on a microbalance and heated using an electrical furnace in either
inert or reactive atmosphere. The experiments can be performed at constant temperature,
linear or stepwise heating, and the result of the analysis is a plot of sample mass as a
function of time or temperature [45].

1.4.4
Brunauer–Emmett–Teller Analysis
The surface area and pore volume of porous material can be measured by N2-physisorption
followed by data analysis by BET theory. The BET analyzer consists of a degassing sta-
tion and an adsorption station. The quartz cell is evacuated under high vacuum at 300 °C
for 2 hours followed by one adsorption cycle. The sample is then loaded and degassed in
the degassing station to ensure that no water or volatiles are present before transferring to
the adsorption station, where the adsorbate gas is introduced. The surface area can thus
be calculated due to the correlation between the number of moles nitrogen adsorbed on
the surface and the pressure difference between the empty cell and the loaded cell [46].

The CO2 capacity of bulk [Ch][Pro] were measured at 20 °C, 40 °C, and 80 °C by bub-

IL decreases for each cycle, supporting the hypothesis that not all CO2 is desorbed during
regeneration. However, the limited regeneration time also inﬂuences this, as it is seen on
Figure 22 the weight does not become stable before adsorption is repeated.
Compared to that measured of SILP, the CO2 capacity of bulk IL is signiﬁcantly higher.
This may be due to the gas not being able to access the smallest pores of the support
material, hence not all IL in the SILP is utilized.
The CO2 capacity measured during ﬁrst adsorption cycle is 13.8 wt% which corresponds
to 0.69 mol CO2/mol IL. This indicates that the mechanism of adsorption follows both
mechanisms described in Section 1.3.1 (Figure 8 and Figure 9), thus resulting in a CO2
capacity between 0.5 mol CO2/mol IL and 1 mol CO2/mol IL.

6
Conclusion
In this work, [Ch][Pro] IL was synthesized and characterized by 1H-NMR,13C-NMR and
ATR-IR, from which SILPs were made with loadings varying from 18.33 wt% to 40.96
wt%. The CO2 capacities of these were measured at 20 °C to 60 °C, where the CO2
capacity increased with increasing loading of SILP, resulting in the 40.96 wt% loaded
SILP at 20 °C to adsorb 0.35 mol CO2/mol IL corresponding to 28 g CO2/kg SILP.
After the ﬁrst cycle of measurement of each SILP the CO2 capacity decreased signiﬁ-
cantly, leading to the hypothesis that much of the CO2 was not desorbed during regenera-
tion, particularly at 20 °C. This was supported by the CO2 capacity of the support material
at 20 °C being roughly equal to that of SILP at the same temperature. The enthalphy of
adsorption was calculated by the van’t Hoff equation to be -16.78 kJ/mol, which is sig-
niﬁcantly higher than that of [P4444][Pro] (-0.56 kJ/mol), afﬁrming that the desorption
process requires high energy, and explains the decrease in CO2 capacity after ﬁrst adsorp-
toin cycle.
The adsorption capacity of bulk [Ch][Pro] was measured, revealing that the mechanism
of adsorption 1:1 mechanism, since the bulk IL adsorbed 1.0 mol CO2/mol IL during the
ﬁrst cycle.
The stability of [Ch][Pro] was measured by long-term (60 hours) static TGA and long
term desorption by nitrogen gas ﬂow (24 and 48 hours) at 80 °C as well as vacuum at 80
°C for 2 hours. These experiments reveal that [Ch][Pro] is slowly decomposing at this
temperature.
The ﬁndings of this work, suggest that it is possible to apply [Ch][Pro] as a biodegradable,
cheap, and environmental friendly IL for separation of CO2 from biogas, although further
study of the effect of CO2/methane gas ﬂow instead of CO2/nitrogen gas ﬂow is required
and efﬁcient regeneration methods needs to be found.
6.1
Outlook
[Ch][Ala] ionic liquid was attempted synthesized in this work, but due to insolubility of
alanine in methanol, the amino acid and choline did not react to form IL. The viscosity

of this IL is lower than that of [Ch][Pro], suggesting that it may have higher adsorption
capacity, due to improved diffusion of CO2 in the liquid, thus making it interesting to
investigate. It may be possible to synthesize [Ch][Ala] by dissolving alanine in water
before mixing it with choline hydroxide in water.
To test the suitability of [Ch][Pro] for separation of CO2 from biogas, measurement of
CO2 capacity using biogas ﬂow instead of CO2/N2 ﬂow, should be done to ensure that
methane does not compete with CO2, thus lowering the amount of adsorbed CO2.
