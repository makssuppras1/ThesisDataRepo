Abstract
Light responsive liquid crystal elastomers (LCEs) have seen a surge in interest due to their prop-
erties of alignment and elasticity which give them actuation capabilities resulting in unidirectional
movement. Two-photon polymerization (2PP) can be used as a direct laser writing technique which
allows the fabrication of 3D structures at the microscale with many different shapes, making them
a useful candidate in the field of microrobotics.
In this thesis, a 2PP technique using NanoScribe GT+ was optimized on a novel LCE mixture to
fabricate high resolution microscopic block and petal structures designed using a 3D computer-aided
design (CAD) software. Polarized optical microscopy showed decent alignment of the structures
and was promising for unidirectional actuation. After a thorough optimization of the fabrication
step, the very same structures were characterized by light actuation followed by a topological char-
acterization with scanning electron microscopy. The topological properties of the 2PP fabrication
showed that block structures mimic the design very well in dimensions, except for the height which
was generally found to be around 50% lower than expected compared to the design. Actuation of
the LCE block structure showed a shrinkage of 7% when exposed to a laser of central wavelength
530 nm and 45 µW/µm2 power. Well-defined petal structures were not possible to fabricate due to
their more complex shape. However, low resolution petal structures showed some actuation. Further
work and statistical analysis is needed for both block and petal structures to determine their true
actuation potential in the new LCE mixture. This work could prove to be important in the field of
microrobotics. Controlling and manipulating the movements of a microrobot with LCE components
through light could be possible in the future.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The research for controlling and manipulating structures at the microscale has been growing in re-
cent years in fields such as actuation and sensing devices. Such devices can perform user-controllable
or autonomous tasks and are known as micro-robots. The field of micro-robotics has seen a surge
in interest due to the myriad of possible applications. Examples of applications include removal of
water pollutants, cargo transport through blood, sensing and other biomedical and environmental
applications[1]. Micro-robots which have the ability of moving in liquids are known as microswim-
mers. Microswimmers require a source of energy to move. Different sources can be used for this
purpose, e.g., thermal, chemical, electric or optical modalities to control and propulse the movement
of microswimmers. An increasing interest is in the subfield known as soft-robotics. Intense study is
being conducted in this field in which the usage of soft materials is important. Soft materials have
flexible properties making them more useful than their rigid counterparts, e.g., in manufacturing. Us-
ing soft materials, such as Liquid Crystal Elastomers (LCEs), have the additional properties of being
able to shrink when exposed to light or heat [2]. Specific techniques previously investigated in this
area include strategies for optical trapping in biological samples [3] and biomedical applications[4].
Because of this, the motivation in this project was to investigate a new LCE mixture for a potential
use in soft microrobotics movable when influenced by light.

Most matter in the world is known to exist in one of 3 phases: solids, liquids and gasses. Matter
can transition between these three states (figure 1(a)). A classical example is water, which in its
solid state (below freezing point) and is a crystal called ice. Increasing the temperature above its
freezing point, it goes into its liquid state, which is water. Finally, increasing the temperature above
its boiling point forms a gas, otherwise known as water vapor (figure 1(b)) [5].
Liquid crystals (LCs) represent a whole different class of matter falling outside the classical 3 phase
model of matter as described above. In addition to these 3 phases, they can be in a so-called Liquid
crystal phase (LC phase). Many LCs are typically depicted as rod-like structures inside a fluid.
They are technically liquids, but their stacking in layers give them crystal-like structural properties,
classifying them as ordered fluids. Their degree of orientational and positional order in the LC phase
can be highly dependent on the temperature. In such cases the LCs are known as thermotropic.
Figure 2 shows that thermotropic LC molecules only are in the liquid crystal phase for a limited
temperature range [6].
LCs are birefringent, meaning the refractive index depends on the polarization and propagation
direction of light. Furthermore, the LC molecules have dipoles. Because of this, applying a voltage
will orient the LCs in the direction of the electric field giving them their crystal structure. This
allows them to exhibit orientational order making them useful as electro-optic switches which are
used in e.g., liquid crystal displays (LCDs).[7]
Figure 2: A schematic depiction of change of nematic LC’s phases due to temperature
More details on the LCs are discussed in Chapter 2 (Theory).
1.3
Aim of the project
In this project, the focus is on using optical modalities for actuation and movement of microswim-
mers. Specifically, the goal was to fabricate microstructures based on a LCE mixture which had
not been tested before. These structures were fabricated with a 2-photon polymerization technique
using a NanoScribe GT+ 3D printer. Post-fabrication, the structures were actuated with an optical
modality and characerized structurally with scanning electron microscopy (SEM).
It has previously been demonstrated by Fusco et al. that Petal structures made of certain hydrogels
can be fabricated and actuated. One function of these Petal structures is the ability to grip certain
objects at the microscale, hence the term microgrippers. One of the long-term goal of these micro-
grippers is to control and guide them towards a targeted area in the body using an actuation method,
provide treatments and therapies for a certain time duration and then be removed from the system
without causing negative side-effects. More specifically, applications of Petal and other structures
include tissue engineering, drug and cell delivery, wound healing and micro object manipulation [8].
Taking inspiration from this, one desired outcome of the project was to fabricate Petal structures
with the never tested before LCE mixture. The hypothesis of this thesis was that post-fabrication
of the LCE Petal structures, it would be possible to actuate the structures with a light modality
causing the structures to open/close in a gripping movement, similar to [8]. Figure 3 shows the
structure by Fusco et al.

Workflow
The project workflow can be split into two phases with each phase containing the same 3 overall
parts Phase 1 is for fabrication and characterization of simple structures (blocks), while phase 2 has
the same steps but with complex petal structures.
The workflow of each phase can be divided into three main parts: pre-fabrication, fabrication and
characterization or correspondingly step A), B) and C) (Figure 5).
Figure 5: A schematic overview of the two phases in the workflow and their connection.
The three parts A), B) and C) in each phase can be further elaborated, since each part contains 3
individual steps. These steps are described in figure 6.
Figure 6: A schematic representation of the steps in each of the three parts of a phase. Each step in
each part has a letter and a number. E.g., the second step in part 3 is called C.2).
In step A.1), the LCE mixture is made. This is followed by the construction of a cell made of two
specially coated glass substrates in step A.2). The constructed cell is infiltrated with the LCE mixture
through capillary action in step A.2) as well. In step A.3) the mixture within the cell is characterized
in a polarized optical microscope. This concludes the pre-fabrication part of the workflow. In step
B.1), the structures to fabricate are designed in Solidworks and then these designed structures are
fabricated using a 2-photon polymerization technique with the Nanoscribe 3D printer in part B.2).
The fabricated structures are then developed in step B.3). In step C.1), after fabrication of the
structures, alignment of the LCE structures are verified using a polarized optical microscope. The
material response of the fabricated LCE structures are tested with light actuation in step C.2).

2
Liquid Crystals
LCs can be classified as being in one of three categories: thermotropic, lyotropic or amphotropic.
Thermotropic LCs are characterized by having phase transitions caused by change in temperature.
Lyotropic LCs have amphiphillic molecules in the mixture, which controls the liquid crystalinity. As
the concentration of amphiphillic molecules is increased, the liquid crystalinity of the mixture also in-
creases. In the case that the LCs are both thermotropic and lyotropic, it is known as amphotropic[9].
The LCs used in this project are thermotropic and mainly have two phases: the isotropic phase (I)
and the nematic phase (N). Intermediate phases between I and N phases are called mesophases and
are important during the temperature characterization of the LCs. The phases are highly dependent
on the temperature. The simplest state of orentiational order that the LCs can have is the nematic
state. In this state, the average orientational ordering direction, n, is unidirectional. On average,
however, these rods, also known as mesogens, are in the isotropic phase, where n is uniform on
average. When the LCs are cooled down beyond a transitional temperature, 𝑇IN, they will tend to
elongate and be in the nematic state giving them their distinct rod-like shape (figure 7). [10]. LCs
with the rod-shaped geometry are called calamitic.
Figure 7: A schematic representation of possible LC shape change. On average, polymers are in
the isotropic (I) state depicted by the spherical shape. When cooled beyond a certain transitional
temperature, 𝑇IN, the polymers elongate into ellipsoid shape in the nematic (N) state.
n is the
director which points along the principal axis of the shape spheriod [10].
Types of molecular organization within calamitics include nematic, cholesteric and smectic.
As
discussed earlier, nematic is only orientational ordering and no positional order, where the LC
molecules are lying almost parallel to each other. Cholesteric (or chiral nematic) occurs when the
molecules are slightly twisted relative to each other in layers. Thus, these have both orientational
and positional order. Finally, smectic is when the molecules are organized in layers. Together these
molecular organizations are known as mesophases. While other mesophases exist, these are the three
primary ones [11]. An overview of the types of molecular organizations of calamitic LCs can be seen
in figure 8.
Figure 8: A schematic representation of some of the possible molecular organizations that LCs can
take. From left to right the molecular organizations are: nematic, cholesteric, Smectic A and Smectic
B. In the representation of cholestreric organization, the blue disks represent the different planes.
The difference between smectic A and B is only that the latter has slightly tilted LCs, but both types
are standing upright in layers.

2.1
Liquid Crystal Elastomers
Elastomers are polymeric structures. As the name suggests, they have elastic properties similar to
that of elastic rubber bands or springs. Linking the polymer chains of the LCs in the nematic state
together into a gel network will fix their orientation. In other words, when you embed a LC inside an
elastomer, the result is a liquid crystal elastomer (LCE). The liquid crystalline structures gain slightly
crosslinked polymer networks by adding a crosslinker (CL) to the mixture. Thus the LCEs gain the
combined properties of the orientational order of LCs and the entropy elasticity from elastomers.
The polymeric elastomers furthermore provide weak intermolecular forces and exhibit viscoeleastic
properties (i.e., both viscous and elastic properties) when undergoing deformation. Such a LCE
can be combined with a photo-responsive material and a photo-initiator (IN). The photo-responsive
material is a azobenzene dye. Irridation of the dye with specific wavelengths induces the trans-cis
isomerization. This chemical reaction can cause a deformation over the entire span of the LCE [12].
LCEs can thus be reversibly deformed in response to external stimulation due to the isotropic phase
transition of the LCs [13]. A schematic example of this actuation is shown in figure 9.
Figure 9: A schematic representation of a possible actuation method of certain LCEs. LC’s are
embedded inside an elastomer/polymer. When laser light is shined on the LCE (left), the whole
structure moves in one way as indicated by the grey arrows. The result of the deformation is seen in
the figure on the right as a shrinkage.
With these properties LCE’s can act as actuators giving them the ability to shrink when exposed to
light. Removing the illumination light from the LCE’s allow them to return to their original shape.
These properties are particularly useful in artifical muscles, biomedical devices and soft robots [4].
It is possible to synthesize many different kinds of LCE’s. In the following, the components used for
the LCE mixtures in this project will be divided into two. (i) the general components used and (ii)
the specific mixtures used with weights of chemicals used.

i. General LCE mixture components
The type of LCE’s which were investigated in this project can be described by five general chemical
components Four of these general components are described by Nocentini et al. [13]. An overview
of these components can be seen below.
1. A mesogenic monomer, either 𝑀1 or 𝑀2.
The mesogenic monomer is responsible for the
alignment of the material.
2. A cross-linker (CL) which allows the formation of a Liquid Crystal Network (LCN). Changing
the ratio/percentage of the cross-linker will change the rigidity of the LCN.
3. An Azobenzene dye (an acrylate) which is a photo-switchable chemical containing a monomer.
The dye acts as a photo-responsive material, which is most sensitive to wavelengths around
550 nm corresponding to green light. When exposed to a power of at least 1 mW of this
wavelength, the dye light-activates the LCN. This process occurs by the dye absorbing the
light whereby the azobenzene undergoes photoisomerization causing reversible breaking of the
double nitrogen bonds between the two phenyl rings which the azobenzene is composed of.
4. A photo-initiator (IN1) added to achieve spatial control of the radical reaction during 2PP.
Irgacure 369 is commonly used which is also the case in this project.
In addition to the chemical components described by Nocentini et al., we add a fifth chemical
component, which is a liquid crystal solvent (LCS) used to further help with the alignment of the
LCE’s.
ii. Specific LCE mixtures used
An overview of the different specific chemicals used for the mixtures are shown in figure 10.

The LCS used is called 4-cyano-4’-pentylbiphenyl or 5CB for short. 5CB is a commonly used nematic
LC. Its usefulness comes from it being in LC phase at room temperature, making it very attractive

to use. 5CB by itself has a phase transition from crystal to nematic state at around 18◦C and from

common polymer to use is a polyimide (PI), but in laboratory settings Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) is
typically used. In our case, we have used both PI and PVA as coating.
Homeotropic alignment uses a surface agent added to the substrate.
The surface agent ensures
alignment of the LCE parallel to the chains of the surface agent. The surface agent creates long
alkyl chains which will tend to orient perpendicular to the glass plane. In our case, it is both PVA
and a type of PI which is used as the surface agent on each of their own glass.
As shown in figure 11, the splayed cell requires the combined use of a PI and a PVA coated glass
substrate, i.e., one coating which aligns homegeneously and one which aligns homeotropically.
One final way to align the LCE in the cell is by using two glass substrates, where both of them are
coated with PVA. This alignment is useful, because PVA is soluble in water. Its usefulness will be
discussed in the results (Chapter 6).

Polarized Optical Microscopy
Before understanding how polarized optical microscopy (POM) works, it is useful to explain how its
simpler counterpart, optical microscopy (OM) works.
Even though there are many diverse types of the microscope today, the simple principles behind
OM remains the same. The purpose of an OM is to visualize small objects. It uses glass lenses
to magnify a sample as an image. Light rays from a light source pass through one lens or a set of
lenses to focus the light onto the specimen, which typically is lying on a stage. Between the stage
and the light source there is usually a condenser whereby the amount of light passing through can
be controlled. The stage can be moved by course and fine focus knobs so that the light is optimally
focused on the specimen. The light then passes through an objective lens which magnifies the image,
usually between 4x - 100x its original size. Commonly, a commerical microscope also has a tube and
an eyepiece, which further magnifies the image by a factor of 10-15 times before the image reaches
the eyes of the observer. The total magnification of the OM equals the sum of all magnifications in
the microscope, i.e., the sum of the magnification from the objective lens and the occular/eyepiece.
Some microscopes have a total sum of up to 1000x magnification relative to the original size of the
specimen [16]. Figure 15 depicts the main components in a typical commercial OM.
Figure 15: A design of a typical components in a simple commercial OM. The light passes from the
light source through the sample plane on the stage and is focused and seen in the eye piece or through
a CCD camera to visualize the magnified specimen.

4.1
Two-photon polymerization
The equipment used for fabrication of the LCE structures is the NanoScribe GT+. This is a 3D
printer which utilizes a two-photon polymerization (2PP) technique and can use a liquid polymeric
resin, which is polymerized during printing. In this section, the theory behind 2PP is discussed.
2PP is based on 2-photon absorption (2PA) which is a non-linear optics phenomenon, in contrast
to one photon absorption (1PA), which is a linear optics phenomenon. Because the process for 2PA
is non-linear, the structures can be printed with a resolution smaller than the Abbe limit [22]. The
main physical differences between 1PA and 2PA can be explained by the bandgap theory in physics.
For 1PA to occur the energy of the single photon to be absorbed, 𝐸𝑝= ℎ𝜈, needs to be equal to or
greater than the bandgap of the material, 𝐸𝑔:
ℎ𝜈≥𝐸𝑔,
(4)
where ℎis Plancks constant and 𝜈is the frequency.
The energy of a single photon is given by:

𝜆𝑝

(5)
where 𝑐is the speed of light in vacuum and 𝜆𝑝is the wavelength of the photon. From equation
(5), it can be seen that a photon’s energy is inversly proportional to its wavelength. Therefore, a
high energy photon means short wavelength is required. For 1PA UV-light is typically used for the
polymerization process with 𝜆𝑝< 365 nm. The probability of a single photon absorption is very
high due to 1PA being a linear phenomenon. The effect is that polymerization occurs throughout
the entire volume of the resin material [23].
In 2PA, two photons are required to be absorped by the resin material at the same time for poly-
merization to occur. The total energy of the two photons needs to be greater than the band gap
energy for this to happen:
ℎ𝜈1 + ℎ𝜈2 ≥𝐸𝑔,
(6)
where 𝜈1 is the frequency of the first photon and 𝜈2 is the frequency of the second photon.
This is a non-linear process and the probability of two photons being absorbed closely after each
other (within the femtosecond scale) is very low. This probability is proportional to the square of
the light intensity[24], whereas the linear 1PA process has a probability of absorption proportional
to the light intensity[23]. Therefore, the polymerization in 2PP only occurs at the highest intensity,
which is at the focal point of the laser. Because polymerization only occurs at this small region, this
gives rise to the possibility of 3D fabrication. The smallest possible 3-dimensional region or volume
in which this polymerization can occur is called a voxel, appropriately named as its counterpart in
2D, a pixel [24] (see figure 30).
The phenomenon can be further elaborated using the idea of a virtual state. In order for 2PP to
occur, the laser source used is usually a femotsecond (FS) pulsed and in the near-infrared (nIR)
region. Polymerization happens through interaction between the photoinitiator in the resin and the
FS pulsed nIR laser. One photon is absorbed into a virtual state between the ground state and
excited state. The absorbed photon in the virtual state has a very short lifetime ( fs). Within this
lifetime another photon is absorbed. Photopolymerization is induced in a area of around 100 nm
through radical generation. A reaction occurs between the monomers in the resin and the light from
laser source, whereby the monomers form large networks which is promoted by the transfer of energy

Furthermore, Z-axis inversion can be changed in the job file. This is done by altering the value of
InvertZAxis to be either 0 or 1. A value of 0 means that the printing of the structures will occur in
immersion mode, whereas a value of 1 means that the printing will occur in DiLL mode. Figure 32
summarizes the differences between the two.
In immersion mode, the resin is placed on top of the substrate, which is desired to have the structures
printed on. On the bottom of the substrate is placed a drop of immersion oil which has a refractive
index (RI) matching the RI of the substrate. The laser beam is focused into the resin through the
oil and the substrate. The area that should be focused on when the printing starts is the interface
between the substrate and the resin. From here, the printing inside the resin happens in the upward
direction [22] (see figure 32(a)).
In contrast, DiLL mode (also known as dip-in) printing occurs in the opposite direction (downwards).
In the case of DiLL mode, it is the resin which acts as both the polymer to print on as well as the
immersion medium. Thus, no immersion oil is used in this setting. Instead, the resin is placed
directly on the bottom of the substrate. The laser beam is focused onto the resin directly, which the
objective is immersed inside (see figure 32(b)). When printing tall structures, the DiLL mode has
the advantage of printing with fewer abberations compared to the immersion mode. The trade-off,
however, is that DiLL mode has a lower resolution compared to immersion mode [26].
Generally, 190 µm tall structures is considered the limit with which immersion mode can be used with
63x objective with few abberations during printing. Any structures taller than 190 µm should be
printed using DiLL mode instead of immersion mode[22]. This has to do with the working distance
of this objective being 190 µm.

Printing method
Printing with the LCE cell is quite different than printing with the commercial resins on a single
substrate. With a single substrate the NanoWrite software can find the interface automatically on
which the printing should occur. However, since the LCE cell has two substrates, there is more than
one interface which could be found. This confuses the automatic interface finder, and therefore it is
not possible to use this method. Furthermore, the automatic interface finder requires an exact value
for the refractive index of the resin used, which is not the case for the LCE mixture.
The alternative method used to find the interface is known as manual interface finding. This requires
training to get used to and a few weeks were spent to become used to this process. The process is
explained below and related images can be found in Appendix B.
1. Once the LCE cell is mounted into the NanoScribe printer, Manual interface finding requires
the user to move the stage manually until the sample is in focus. Once this focal point is found,
it means that an interface is found.
2. It is important to be aware that there are in total 4 interfaces, since the cell consists of two
substrates. Each of the two substrates has a certain thickness, meaning each substrate has two
interfaces. Since the printing direction is upward (see figure 33), we are interested in using the
second interface as the printing starting point, where the printed structure will be attached.
3. The first interface is therefore ignored. It is obvious to the user when the second interface is in
focus, since it is possible to see both the lines from the rubbing during cell alignment as well
as LCE liquid and particles floating around, right above the second interface.
28
==PAGE:36==
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
Master Thesis F22
4. Due to experience, the focal plane of the second interface was usually found at the height of the
stage somewhere between 9.700 - 9.900 mm relative to the bottom position in the NanoScribe
GT+.
5. After finding this interface, it is important to also find the focal plane of the third interface,
since the difference in height between the second and third interface should be equal to the
height of the cell, which we know should be 18 µm due to spacers with 18 µm in diameter
placed in between the two substrates of the cell.
6. Once this height is verified, the stage is moved back to the focal plane of the second interface.
7. Because of the limitation of not being able to use the automatic interface finder, this means
that the interface has to be found manually every time a new print is to be conducted on the
same substrate. The exact same height of the stage could not be used for these different prints,
since the substrate could have been slightly tilted.
8. To be certain that the printing happens on the substrate, the printing is started slightly below
the second interface (0.5 µm below). This value is preset in the job-file.
9. Finally, just before starting the print another way to verify that we are close to the second
and third interface, is to use the ’manualcontrol’ command in the NanoWrite software. With
this command the laser is turned on with a specific manually chosen laser power. This laser
power should be chosen lower than 20% to avoid burning or overpolymerizing the sample. If
some bright light is seen during this procedure, this is due to the fluorescence occuring after
the 2PA process, where some excitation occurs.
10. Turning off the laser power again in the same ’manualcontrol’ window, it is often seen that
at least slight polymerization has happened. This is a good indicator, that the focal point is
close to either the second or third interface or in between the two, since it is the LCE being
polymerized. Images of this procedure are seen in Appendix B.
11. The ’manualcontrol’ window is then closed and the printing is started.
This method of printing is well described by the Nanoguide, which is a user-guide for NanoScribe
users [30].
Parameter sweep
There are several parameters which affect the quality of the printing. Among these, the laser power
(LP) and scan speed (SS) are most important [22]. Therefore, these two parameters were optimized
to get the best possible printing. It is a standard to optimize these two parameters, whenever a new
material is being used as resin. NanoScribe have their own commercial resin, wherin these parameters
are already optimized. Since the materials used in this project are home-made, the parameters were
needed to be optimized during the project.
The LP is represented by a percentage in terms of the absolute maximum laser power available.
This absolute value depends on several factors including which specific nanoscribe system is being
used. The laser configuration also depends highly on environmental conditions where fabrication is
ongoing, such as temperature and humidity.

Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a microscopy technique and as the name suggests, it involves
scanning the surface of a sample with electrons. The equipment used for this purpose was a Zeiss
Supra 40 VP SEM at DTU Nanolab.
Before describing what images can be produced with this technique, an explanation is needed for
how and why electrons is used for imaging, which is done in the following.
Just as light can be described as a wave and a particle due to the particle wave duality nature of
light, electrons can also be described in this way. The wave nature of matter is described by the de
Broglie wave given as:
𝜆= ℎ
𝑝,
(7)
where ℎis Planck’s constant and 𝑝is the momentum of a particle [31].
The reason why the wavelength of electrons is important is because if we want electrons to interact
with a sample, the wavelength of the electron must be comparable to the size of the particles in the
sample, which we wish to investigate. This makes SEM useful for high resolution imaging relative
to OM, since the wavelength of electrons is much shorter (0.037-0.859 Angstrom) [32].
The overall advantage of using electrons for imaging is the strong interaction that occurs between
electrons and matter in general. In SEM we primarily use the electron’s particle property to get
information. Generally, SEM is used to get information about the topography of a sample. The
reason for this can largely be explained by the signals we extract when using SEM and the elec-
tron scattering that occurs. The signals detected using SEM are namely two types: Backscattered
electrons (BSE) and Secondary Electrons (SE) [33].
BSE’s are higher energy electrons (greater than 50eV), which are scattered back from parts of the
sample below the surface, in the so-called interaction volume. These electrons are a result of elastic
scattering. From this we can get information about the density of the sample. BSE detection provide
information about the materials in the sample along with the topography, albeit the topographical
information is less in BSE than in SE. The BSEs are highly dependent upon the material weight,
32
==PAGE:40==
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
Master Thesis F22
or the atomic number Z, and the general trend is, that heavier materials exhibit greater brightness
due to an increase of the angle of deflection [34].
On the other hand, we have SE’s. These electrons are lower in energy (less than 50eV) and are
scattered back from the surface area of the sample. SE’s are a result of inelastic scattering due to
the interaction between the electrons from the beam and from the sample. Using a SE detector, it
is possible to visualize the surface of the sample and thereby get information about its topography
[33].
In the used SEM, there are two types of SE’s possible to detect, known as SE1 and SE2 with detectors
with the names InLens detector and SE2 detector, respectively. SE1’s are produced near the surface
of the sample by beam electrons, wheras SE2’s are produced by BSEs getting back to the surface
after scattering. In this project, only SE2s were used to image in SEM, because these provide a
higher topological contrast, depth of focus and a larger field of view. Furthermore, less charging is
experienced compared to using the InLens detector [34].
Even though there are other types of signals, such as X-Rays, Auger electrons and cathodolumines-
cence, in the used SEM only BSEs and SEs are detectable and therefore these are the focus here.
The different types of signals are given as an overview in figure 36.

Below the main components of SEM and its general layout is described.
The electron gun emits electrons. It consists of a thermionic cathode made with e.g., a tungsten
filament. It is heated up by an electric current which then emits electrons. This process occurs in
vacuum. An anode beneath the cathode accelerates the electrons downwards. These electrons are
called primary electrons.
By having electromagnetic lenses beneath the electron gun, the primary electrons are then focused
onto the sample.
Further components such as electromagnetic deflectors are needed.
By using
a raster scan generator and an electron detector, the surface of the sample is scanned using the
primary electrons. When the primary electrons interact with the surface of the sample, SE’s and
BSE’s are scattered and detected with the electron detector. The signal from these electrons are
amplified and shown on the screen of a computer. On the screen an image is built of the surface
of the sample using these electrons. Depending on how many electrons are detected when scanning
the surface of the sample, different colors are shown on the screen. Many electrons give a brighter
image, while few electrons give a grey image, and finally no electrons detected give a black image
[32].

Generally, the greater the chosen acceleration voltage is, the stronger the lens need to be to focus
the electron beam. The strength of the lens depends on the strength of the magnetic field, which
can be controlled in the SEM. Placing and adjusting apertures in the microscope can limit specific
scattering angles in the beam path[35].
The most important limitations with SEM are lens abberations, including spherical and chromatic
abberations. Other things to be aware of are phenomena such as astigmatism and aperture alignment
[34]. The electrons close to and far from the optical axis respond differently to the magnetic field of
the electromagnetic lens. This is true since the strength of the field is different and not homogenous.
As a result, we see that the image plane forms a disk, known as spherical aberration.
Because
energies of the primary electrons vary, the interaction between the electrons and the electromagnetic
lens also varies. This variation is known as chromatic aberration. Astigmatism is a property due
to lack of symmetry and distorts the image. This can, however, be corrected by the user of the
microscope [36].
The resolution which we see in SEM images are dependent on the probe size. Generally, we want a
smaller probe size to achieve a better resolution. Factors which define the smallest possible probe
size are:
• The mentioned aberrations
• The acceleration voltage or extra high tension (EHT) used (can be set between 20V - 30kV in
the used equipment)

• The probe size which decreases with increased acceleration voltage
• The working distance (which is the distance between the microscope and the sample)
It is possible to notice very bright spots during imaging. These spots are due to an effect called
charging.
Charging occurs when the sample is non-conductive and the secondary electrons get
stuck on the sample, without enough energy to escape. This can either be at the surface, or if the
acceleration voltage is too high, inside the sample where electrons lack the energy to escape after
an inelastic collision. Due to the nature of the charging effect, both decreasing or increasing the
acceleration voltage could solve the problem, depending on which of the two aforementioned effects
dominate. Another common solution is to coat the sample with thin conductive layer such as gold.
Another important factor to consider if the sample is charging and if coating with a conductive layer is
not a possibility, is whether to use the standard ultra-high vacuum (UHV) or variable pressure (VP)
during imaging. VP introduces some amount of air into the SEM which helps with discharging, since
the air particles can interact with the beam electrons. The trade-off, however, is that the variable
pressure also gives a lower resolution[34]. In this project, gold coating the sample was possible to do
as the last characterization step after actuation testing and therefore using VP was not necessary.
The optimal acceleration voltage to use during imaging with best resolution and least charging was
found to be between 0.8-1 kV without gold coating and between 1.5-2.5 kV with a 15 nm gold coating
using a Cressington 208HR Sputter coater.

6.3
III: Optimization of printing parameters
In this section the focus was on optimizing the printing parameters LP and SS during fabrication
with the LCE mixtures. An initial parameter sweep was conducted to find the optimal parameters.
The scaling of the LP could be assumed to be constant at constant temperature and other relevant
external conditions held constant, such as humidity. There was a trade-off between the LP and SS
It was therefore possible to achieve different combinations of LP and SS which could be optimal.
The parameter sweep was done manually, where the different LCE mixtures were tested with dif-
ferent parameters. As mentioned in Chapter 4 (Fabrication), it was generally to be considered near
impossible to find the interface automatically in NanoScribe when fabricating in cells. This was the
reason for having to make a manual parameter sweep, constantly ensuring that the printing began
at the interface or a little below, such that the print would be properly attached to the PI substrate.
Several different combinations of LP and SS were attempted. Examples of the different parameter
testing attempts mentioned below can be seen in Appendix B. The optimization process of the
printing parameters, LP and SS, were observed in real-time while printing using the NS camera.
The goal during parameter optimization was to achieve fast and efficient writing. Therefore the
printing was first tested with a maximum LP (100%) and a high SS of 30000 µm/s. These parameters
were not successful. Furthermore using a high LP with a relatively low SS polymerizes everything
and thus it was impossible to print structures. Based on these result, it was chosen to do a parameter
sweep between 80-100% LP and 20000-30000 µm/s in SS for the mixtures. This method was not
successful as over-polymerization was experienced when attempting to print block structures. The
overpolymerization was also experienced with LP as low as 60% (see figure 45).

An approach with lower LP was done to see whether this type of parameter sweep would help with
the overpolymerization issue. A parameter sweep of 40% LP and 10-20000 SS seemed promising for
the mixture without 5CB and a parameter sweep of 40% LP and SS of 1-5000 µm/s was promising
for the mixture with 30% 5CB. Unfortunately, it was too difficult to find optimal parameters for
the mixture with 50% 5CB, which either polymerized too much or too little when using a lower or
higher SS, respectively.
There seemed to be a correlation and trade-off between the percentage of 5CB used in the mixture
vs. the difficulty in both finding the optimal printing parameters and for the printed objects to stay
attached to the substrate post-development. The higher the concentration of 5CB in the mixture
the less adherence there was of the printed structures to the substrate. This was tested with all the
mixtures.
Because of these difficulties with 50% 5CB mixture, in the end this mixture was abandoned.
One attempt was made to improve the LCE1,50 mixture. Since the issue was the printing quality and
attachment of structures to the substrate, the mixture was improved by adding more photoinitiator
(IN1) to it. This new mixture was termed LCE1,50,10, indicating that the new mixture with monomer

6.4
IV: Resolution testing
In the following a comparison was made in the resolution between gold-coated samples of LCE1,50,10
and the commercial resin from NanoScribe known as IP-Dip. This comparison was done in SEM.
Note, however, that a direct comparison was not possible because IP-Dip is used in DiLL mode,
whereas the LCE mixtures were used in Immersion mode. DiLL mode is known for having the ability
to fabricate more accurate structures when taller. This is because DiLL mode allows polymerization
to occur inside the resin, whereas Immersion mode fabricates on the polymerized resin[37] (figure 32).
This means that when continously printing in DiLL mode, the refractive index in the resin medium
remains the same. In Immersion mode this is not the case, since printing on top of polymerized resin
will slightly alter the refractive index in the medium while printing. In hindsight, it would therefore
have been more appropriate to fabricate the LCE structures using DiLL mode, since these structures
were limited by the height of the cell, which was 18 µm, due to the size of the spacers between the
two substrates. According to previous studies, when printing tall structures, it is recommended to
use DiLL mode [22].
The design used for comparing the resolution consisted of tall and thin structures shaped like both
blocks and cylinders. Figures 49(a) and 49(b) show an overview comparison between the IP-Dip and
LCE1,50,10 fabricated resolution structures in SEM, respectively.

cantilever microstructures. Both have printed the microstructures correctly. However, as was ex-

that the printing started too much below the interface.

increased dimensions and is seen in figure 84 in the Appendix. The result showed that NanoScribe
was not able to print the structures correctly as the bigger cantilever was no longer standing upright,
but had fallen down compared to the small cantilever structures, most likely due to the increased
weight of the bigger cantilever.
Other complex microstructures included double cantilevers, which are generally the same as the
bigger cantilever but with another cantilever side attached to it (see figure 85 in the Appendix).
These also fell down compared to the small cantilever microstructures.
Finally, the Petal structure was attempted to be printed. This was, however, not successful with the
LCE1,50,10, but only with the LCE2,0 and the IP-Dip. The structure with LCE2,0 was sent to LENS
for actuation and consequently the SEM imaging was done prior to shipping without gold coating
and the imaging was therefore not optimal. The comparison between the SEM images for IP-Dip
and LCE2,0 is seen in figure 86 in the Appendix.
Many of the fabricated microstructures did not have actuation behaviour in a unidirectional manner
and the petal structure was no exception. As shown in figure 78, only the spherical part of the
petal structure was actuated properly, whereas the goal was to make the petal structures move as
exemplified with the schematic in figure 3 in the Appendix.
Unfortunately, further actuation tests of complex microstructures happened at a very late stage of

7
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to fabricate high resolution microstructures with a new and never
before tested LCE mixture and to characterize the topology and light response of the very same
microstructures.
The specific designs chosen for the microstructures in this project were block
structures and petal structures.
There were in total 7 different LCE mixtures made for fabrication. The developed mixtures con-
taining 50% 5CB were chosen as the primary mixtures to analyze and use for fabrication, since the
hypothesis was that 5CB improves the alignment of structures. Therefore, general comparisons were
made between results from these mixtures and the other mixtures with less than 50% 5CB. The
results in POM of the fabricated structures did not show an improvement in the alignment when
comparing the 50% 5CB structures with the structures without 5CB.
The LCE mixture with 50% 5CB and monomer M1 with added 10% photo-initiator proved to
be the most versatile in creating microstructures. The added photo-initiator solved the problem
of adherence to the substrate encountered during the duration of this project. The resolution of
this LCE mixture was comparable to the commercial IP-Dip resin for block structures but not for
cylindrical shapes.
It was impossible to compare actuation results directly, because the actuation is highly dependent on
the height of the actuated structures. The structures which were actuated in the different samples
(No 5CB, 30% 5CB and 50% 5CB) were all of different heights.
More research is needed in order to determine whether the actuation of the structures fabricated
with LCE1,50,10 is comparable or increased compared to mixtures with less or no 5CB. This can
be determined with statistical analysis by actuation of the same structures, but fabricated with
different mixtures for comparison. Further work also needs to be conducted to determine whether
the actuation of the 100x100x10 µm square block structures in this project is anamolous or not.
Since it was not possible to detach the structures with the micromanipulator system, testing the
same procedure with an increased laser power may help soften up the structures before they are
picked up.

A2. Substrate Coating
The details of the coating process of the substrates are given below:
In step 1. blank coverslips are first cleaned through sonication in water for 10 minutes followed by
sonication in acetone for 10 minutes. This cleaning procedure is followed by a drying step, where all
the cleaned coverslips are put in the oven at 60 C for 10 minutes.
Once the coverslips are dried they are cleaned and ready to be coated. For the coating process, the
spin-coater 6800 Spin Coater Series is used at LENS, Italy (figure 59).
64
==PAGE:72==
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
Master Thesis F22
Figure 59: Spin-coater used for coating substrates with PI and PVA, respectively. The equipment
was used in a semi-cleanroom setting.
The recipes used for coating with PVA and PI are different.
For the PVA coating, droplets of PVA are dispensed on one surface of the coverslip and put into
the spin-coater at 5000 RPM for 30 seconds. The coverslip itself is rectangularly-shaped as the PVA
coated substrate will act as the top of the cell. After the spin-coating is complete, the coverslip is
simply put in a petri-dish covered with lens-tissue to ensure that the coated coverslip stays clean.
To remember on which side the coating is, a letter which looks different when mirrored is written
on the uncoated side. Thus, the letter, e.g., R, is mirrored on the side where the coating is, just as
shown in figure 60.
Figure 60: Mirrored R means this is the correct side up,i.e., where the coating is.
For the PI coating several steps are needed. A single drop of PI is dispensed on a circular-shaped
coverslip glass. It is circular since it acts as the bottom of the cell and a circular plate is used during
fabrication.
The spincoater recipe has several steps: 5 seconds at 500 RPM, followed by 5 seconds of 800 RPM
and finally 40 seconds at 5000 RPM. After the coating is complete, the glass is left on a hotplate at
