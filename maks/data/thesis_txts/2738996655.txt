Abstract
Many studies have found plant leaves to be emitters of CO and CH4. Consensus indicates that CH4
emissions are stimulated by heat and UV, while CO release is additionally stimulated by visible light. The
mechanisms producing these emissions are yet to be discovered. To get closer to finding these mechanisms,
this study examined whether photosynthesis might influence CO and CH4 leaf emissions. Five plant
species of different photosynthesis pathways were analysed for their photosynthesis performance, as well
as CO and CH4 emissions under different temperatures and visible light intensities.
Findings reveal
CO release rates to be positively correlated with light intensity and temperature but suggest a separate
dark metabolism. CH4 rates were independent of light intensity and temperature. Much lower CH4
release from excised leaves compared to their connected counterparts, indicates that such is dependent
on stomatal opening, supporting the hypothesis that CH4 is dissolved in transpired water. CO release
rates are similar between attached and detached leaves, suggesting that CO is produced at epidermal
level. Photosynthesis appears to be unrelated to the release of either of these gases.
Keywords:
Plant Ecophysiology; Photosynthesis Pathways; Global CO budget; Plant CO Emissions; Plant CH4
Emissions

Chapter 1
Motivation
The plant kingdom dominates the Earth’s biological landscape. Approximately 81% of the world’s
biomass is stocked in plants (Bar-On et al. (2018)). This makes the study of plants essential for under-
standing the cycles and processes that orchestrate nature as we know it.
Unlike most animals, which can move around, plants are rooted to one location and must adapt more
effectively to the ever-changing environment that surrounds them. As a result, plants exhibit phenotypic
plasticity, continuously adjusting their behavior to the conditions they encounter.
Plant Ecophysiology is the science that investigates how plants’ physiological processes adapt to
changes in their ecosystem. This ancient discipline has served many purposes, from maximizing crop
yield to studying carbon sequestration.
A key area of interest is how environmental factors impact plants’ gas exchange processes. This is
particularly relevant as we try to forecast how nature will change in the Anthropocene. Beyond the main
gas exchange processes of respiration and photosynthesis, plants also emit carbon monoxide and methane,
a phenomenon identified in recent years. These gases are significant to the atmosphere, and the present
study will explore how their release from plant leaves is influenced by light intensity and temperature.
Ultimately, the goal is to determine if there is a connection between their release and photosynthesis.

Chapter 2
Theoretical Background
2.1
Leaf Physiology
Leaves are appendages of the stem of vascular plants and function as their main photosynthesis site.
The outermost layer of the leaf is the epidermis. It contains numerous stomata: pores through which
plants exchange gases. Each stoma is flanked by 2 guard cells, which regulate its opening.
Beneath the epidermis lies the mesophyll, a space hosting cells dedicated to photosynthesis, along
with vascular tissue. The latter consists of 2 types of vessels: xylem and phloem. The xylem transports
water and nutrients from the roots to the leaf, whereas the phloem conducts the photosynthesis products
from the leaf to the rest of the plant.
About 97% of the water leaves receive through the xylem is transpired.
The remaining 1-3% is
supplied to the photosynthetic apparatus and other biochemical reactions (Xing et al. (2022)).
2.2
Photosynthesis
2.2.1
Mechanism
Photosynthesis is the biological process through which most plants convert light energy into chemical
energy. It occurs in the chloroplasts of plant cells, which are composed of a network of membrane-bound
compartments (thylakoids) in a medium called stroma.
The thylakoid membranes house light-harvesting pigments, organized along with proteins into 2 com-
plexes - Photosystems I and II, each containing a special pair of Chlorophyll a pigments. When Photo-
system II absorbs photonic energy, it transfers this energy to its special pair, which releases an excited
electron. The positively charged pairs then pull electrons off of H2O, releasing oxygen(O2) and protons
(H+).
The electrons released by the special pair then travel an electron transport chain, providing energy
to pump additional H+ into the thylakoid, thereby creating a strong concentration gradient. The H+
stream out of the thylakoid though the enzyme ATP synthase, which harnesses the flow of H+ to convert
ADP into ATP.
3
==PAGE:16==
After navigating the electron transport chain, the electrons reach Photosystem I’s special pair of
pigments. The latter uses additional light energy to transfer the electrons to be used by an enzyme called
NADP+ reductase, which uses electrons to reduce NADP+ into NADPH.
Finally, the ATP and NADPH fuel the Calvin Cycle, a series of light-independent reactions which use
atmospheric CO2 to synthesize glucose. (Whitmarsh and Govindjee (1999))
Hence, the net equation of oxygenic photosynthesis is as follows:
6CO2 + 6H2O

The equation for aerobic respiration is equivalent to the photosynthesis equation but operates in
reverse and does not require light.
2.2.2
Wavelengths
Photosynthesis is constrained by the capacity of photosynthetic pigments to absorb light. The main
pigments used in photosynthesis are chlorophylls and carotenoids (Gonz´alez (2001)).
While chlorophylls efficiently absorb blue and red light, carotenoids, such as β-carotene, predominantly
absorb light in the blue and green wavelengths (Comar and Zscheile (1942), Ashenafi et al. (2023)).
Figure 2.1: Absorption spectra of diverse pigments in the visible light range. Adapted from Guidi et al.
(2017)
Photosynthetically active radiation refers to the spectrum of light, ranging from 400 to 700 nm, that
plants can execute photosynthesis with. The amount of photosinthetically active photons received by a
surface per unit of time is called Photosynthetic Photon Flux Density (PPFD).
2.2.3
Pathways
Different plant species have adaptations that allow them to maintain high production rates in envi-
ronments that may constrain photosynthesis. These are called photosynthetic pathways.
4
==PAGE:17==
The reason plants have evolved to use different photosynthesis pathways is to deal with photorespi-
ration. RubisCO, the same enzyme that initiates the Calvin Cycle, also commences a series of reactions,
known as photorespiration, that result in the loss of 3 fixed carbons.
Photorespiration may have evolved in photosynthetic organisms to prevent accumulation of toxic
levels of glycolate (Maurino and Peterhansel (2010)), acclimate to lower CO2 availability (Eisenhut et al.
(2017)) and protect Photosystem II from photoinhibition (Takahashi et al. (2007), Voss et al. (2013)).
Photorespiration is stimulated by higher temperatures and lower CO2:O2 concentration ratio (Greer
(2015)).
The classic photosynthesis pathway, used by about 85-95% of the Earth’s plant species (Yamori et al.
(2014), Boretti and Florentine (2019)), is known as C3. In arid conditions, C3 plants close their stomata
to reduce transpiration losses. But this also reduces CO2 intake and O2 outflow, thus decreasing the
CO2:O2 ratio inside the leaf and increasing photorespiration.
Photorespiration is consequently a big hindrance for C3 plants in dry and hot regions, where pho-
torespiration’s costs can vastly outweigh its benefits. These environments have driven the evolutionary
divergence into new photosynthesis pathways that can avoid high photorespiration rates.
Another pathway, known as C4, implements a physical separation between the light-dependent reac-
tions and the Calvin Cycle. In this pathway, CO2 is initially fixed by the enzyme PEP carboxylase into
a four-carbon compound, which is then transported to a bundle sheath cell. There, it is decarboxylated
to release CO2 for use in the Calvin Cycle (Osborne and Beerling (2006)). This whole process involves
a series of reactions that incur an extra energetic cost of 1-3 ATP per CO2 molecule transported (Nobel
(1991)).
Some plants use the crassulacean acid metabolism (CAM) pathway to deal with dry environments.
Unlike C4, it not only introduces a physical but a temporal separation as well, between the light-dependent
reactions and the Calvin Cycle. CAM plants open their stomata during the night, letting CO2 diffuse
in and be fixed by PEP carboxylase, following a series of reactions to malic acid, which is stored inside
vacuoles. During the day, CAM plants close their stomata to avoid losing water and CO2 is gradually
released from the vacuoles into the Calvin Cycle. The CAM pathway also comes at a cost of 2-4 additional
ATP for each CO2 molecule fixed (Nobel (1991)).
These physiological differences between plants can be seen at ecosystem level. C3 plants dominate
in cold and moist environments, while C4 and CAM plants are mostly found in hot and dry ecosystems
(Larcher (2003)), where their adaptations to reduce photorespiration are worth the added ATP costs.
2.3
Gases
2.3.1
CO2
Atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) is essential for life on Earth, regulating many environmental pa-
rameters such as temperature and pH. CO2 is uptaken by plants in photosynthesis to produce glucose.
Simultaneously, they also release CO2 into the atmosphere, due to the presence of mitochondria in their
5
==PAGE:18==
cells. This process, known as respiration, involves the breakdown of glucose and release of CO2. Thus,
glucose functions partly as a chemical energy battery for plant cells. While photosynthesis only occurs
in the presence of light, respiration is a continuous process, allowing plants to produce ATP at all times
of the day.
The current fraction of CO2 in the troposphere is about 423ppm as measured by NOAA (Thoning
et al. (1989)). Since CO2 mixes evenly in the atmosphere, this local measurement is representative of the
global background concentration of CO2. Research has shown an inverse correlation between the CO2
release from plant respiration and the CO2 concentration in the atmosphere, with an estimated 15-20%
reduction in CO2 output from respiration, following a doubling of its atmospheric concentration (Drake
et al. (1997), Curtis and Wang (1998)). Additionally, elevated CO2 concentrations also boost RuBisCO
activity, effectively enhancing CO2 fixation (Makino and Mae (1999)).
Canadell et al. (2021) estimated the global carbon budget, predicting that Earth’s plant biomass stocks
450PgC. Through photosynthesis, 113PgC are captured annually, while respiration and fires release 111.1
PgC. These values are predicted to be the status prior to industrial revolution. Human activity has
increased both the inflow and outflow of carbon in the flora, raising the net uptake from 1.9 to 5.3 PgC
per year. To put in perspective the annual release of carbon from fossil fuels is estimated by the authors
to be 9.4 PgC.
Given vegetation’s big role in the carbon budget, studying the release of organic compounds by plants
is cardinal for developing high accuracy climate models.
2.3.2
CO
Carbon monoxide (CO) is a trace gas in Earth’s troposphere that has garnered attention for its toxicity.
Global tropospheric CO concentrations have been measured by MOPITT, an astronomical instrument
aboard NASA’s Terra satellite (Atmospheric Chemistry Observations
Modeling Laboratory).
These
measurements reveal higher CO levels on land, closer to the equator line, decreasing towards the poles.
Since the beginning of MOPITT’s operation in 2000, a decline in worldwide CO concentrations has been
observed. This drop in CO concentrations is particularly visible in urban areas, thanks to a reduction in
the emissions from the transport sector, as technological advancements have allowed engines to perform
more complete combustions and the use of catalytic converters is now widespread (U.S. Environmental
Protection Agency).
CO emissions are both natural and anthropogenic. Anthropogenic emissions derive mostly from fuel
and biomass incomplete combustion, while the main natural sources are vegetation and the oxidation of
hydrocarbons in the atmosphere. The main sinks for tropospheric CO are oxidation by hydroxyl (OH)
radicals (85%), uptake by soils (10%) and diffusion into the stratosphere (Khalil and Rasmussen (1990)).
CO has a global average lifetime of about 2 months, but this value depends on latitude and temperature,
being able to surpass 1 year during the winter at high latitudes (Khalil and Rasmussen (1990)).
Studies have shown that plant leaves release CO in the presence of light and oxygen. Bruhn et al.
(2013) found a positive correlation between CO release and increases in temperature and UV intensity
across multiple plant species. Irradiation appears to be more significant than temperature in driving CO
6
==PAGE:19==
emissions (Schade et al. (1999)). Visible light accounts for up to 40% of the UV-induced CO emissions,
with lower wavelengths inducing a higher release of CO (Schade et al. (1999)). Another topic of interest is
where this CO comes from. Schade et al. (1999) suggested that it may stem from cleavage of the cellulose
chain. Wilks (1959) analysed chlorophyll extracts exposed to light, finding that the same component of
the light spectrum absorbed in photosynthesis (480 to 680 nm) results in release of CO.
Logan et al. (1981) estimated the global CO budget, counting plant emissions as 50-200Tg CO per
year, comprising 1.8-7.3% of total CO emissions. Earlier, Seiler et al. (1978) had estimated plant emissions
to be of 50-100Tg CO per year, based on experimental results of emission per unit area.
2.3.3
CH4
Methane (CH4) is the simplest organic matter found in nature. Despite participating in a myriad of
biological processes, it has attracted most attention for its role in greenhouse effect. Counting as about
30 CO2-equivalents over the course of 100 years (Balcombe et al. (2018)), CH4 is, ignoring water vapor,
the second most contributing gas to global warming.
Recent observations of methane release by eukaryotes under aerobic conditions have lead researchers to
try to identify the processes and conditions governing this release, and its contribution to the atmospheric
methane budget. Keppler et al. (2006) issued the first estimate of CH4 production in oxic environments by
plants, concluding that these may be responsible for 10-40% of the CH4 input into the atmosphere. The
authors’ findings were contradicted by further studies which estimated that aerobic emissions provide
1.7-11.5% of the CH4 released into the troposphere (Kirschbaum et al. (2006), Parsons et al. (2006),
Butenhoff and Khalil (2007)).
One major challenge in scaling up oxic CH4 release by plants is the uncertainty of results obtained
from studies at the ecosystem level, as an unknown amount of the CH4 being produced comes from
soil bacteria. Zeikus and Ward (1974) suggested that some observed CH4 release in attached leaves is
actually soil-produced, being transported via the transpirational stream. This hypothesis is corroborated
by Qaderi and Reid (2009), who found higher rates of CH4 release by attached pea leaves compared to
detached ones.
Bruhn et al. (2012) documented the 3 most generally accepted factors to stimulate plant CH4 release
to be increases in temperature, UV radiation and reactive oxygen species. The latter is a byproduct
of other stresses, such as heat, UV and wounds. This led the authors to call reactive oxygen species a
“unifying mechanism for plant CH4 production”.
Nisbet et al. (2009) tried to map the pathway that conducts the CH4 release. They found that the
same species of plant grown in different locations demonstrates notably different δ13C profiles in the
emitted CH4. The authors therefore raise doubts about a plant-specific process for CH4 production,
which they argue should result in a very similar isotopic composition. The paper concludes that the
observed CH4 release is dissolved in transpired water.

Chapter 3
Methodology
A series of experiments was conducted to try to uncover the yet-to-discover mechanisms underlying
the leaf-release of CO and CH4.
Given that photosynthesis involves many leaf-scale processes and organelles, it may be directly or
indirectly involved in the release of CO and/or CH4 from leaves.
To investigate the correlation between photosynthesis and the release of these gases, different plant
species’ photosynthesis rates were metered under various light levels. CO and CH4 release rates were also
calculated from the same plants.
For measuring photosynthesis a LI-COR® LI-6800 Portable Photosynthesis System was used. This
device pumps air and measures its parameters before and after crossing a leaf sample inside a chamber.
Reference air CO2 concentration ([CO2]), temperature (T) and relative humidity (RH) were controlled to
be constant for the entirety of a set of measurements, making the loop an open system. In other words,
for every circulation loop the air does, its parameters are reset before reaching the leaf, so the difference
between the measured parameters before and after the leaf represents plant gas exchange.
Each leaf was tested under the temperatures of 15, 25 and 35ºC. For each temperature, 13 different
levels of PPFD (0, 100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700, 800, 900, 1000, 1500, 2000 µmol · m−2 s−1) were
applied to the leaf. The light used consisted of a spectral blend of 10% 453nm + 90% 660nm, especially
targeting chlorophyll pigments.

After recording photosynthesis rates, the leaves were excised, washed with air depleted in CO and
CH4, and placed inside a WALZ® 3010-GWK1 Gas-Exchange Chamber connected to a Picarro® G2401
Gas Concentration Analyzer. Unlike the LI-6800, this setup is a closed-loop system, meaning that the
atmospheric parameters inside the setup continuously change. The advantage of a closed-loop is that it
makes it possible to see small changes in trace gases such as CO and CH4.
The G2401 continuously logs the air concentrations of CO and CH4. Emission rates are obtained
by calculating the linear regression slope of the gases’ concentrations over the 5-minute period following
closure of the chamber. Python was used to read the log files and calculate emission rates.
Each leaf sample was exposed to the chamber temperatures of 15, 25 and 35ºC. For each temperature,
6 different levels of PPFD (0, 100, 200, 600, 1000, 2000 µmol · m−2 s−1) were applied to the chamber,

4.1
Photosynthesis
4.1.1
C3 and C4
Photosynthetic rates can serve as indicators of plant adaptation to different environments. A par-
ticularly interesting case is comparing how C3 and C4 plants’ assimilation curves respond to increasing
temperatures.
As seen in section 2.2.3, C4 plants possess adaptations to minimize photorespiration, which can become
prominent in the C3 pathway under high temperatures. As a result, in higher temperatures C3 plants
may demonstrate lower assimilation rates as more RubisCO will bind to O2 instead of CO2 (Ghashghaie
and Cornic (1994)).
Indeed, a slight depression of photosynthesis was observed when temperature was increased from 25
to 35ºC in the C3 Brugmansia sanguinea. Such did not occur in the C4 plant studied:
(a)
(b)
Figure 4.1: CO2 uptake for different levels of PPFD for a) Brugmansia sanguinea, a C3 plant, and b)
Saccharum officinarum, a C4 plant.
These results aline with Yamori et al. (2014)’s claim that the optimal temperature for photosynthesis

==PAGE:26==
is higher in C4 than C3 plants.
Notably in all C3 and C4 plants tested, assimilation decreased past PPFD=1000 µmol·m−2 s−1 when
T=35ºC. In the case of Coffea arabica, a C3 plant, assimilation rates were highest at 35ºC and began to
decrease from PPFD levels as low as 500 µmol · m−2 s−1.
The negative assimilation values seen in darkness are exhibitions of respiration, as the absence of
light halts photosynthesis. In the majority of replicates, respiration in darkness was higher the higher
the temperature.
4.1.2
CAM
Overall, lower rates of photosynthesis were observed in CAM plants. In a few replicates, CAM leaves
produced a photosynthesis curve like that of a C3/C4 plant, exhibiting negative assimilation in darkness
and logarithmically increasing their assimilation with light. This may corroborate the hypothesis that
CAM plants can switch to a C3/C4-like style of photosynthetic metabolism when circumstances are
favorable (Osmond et al. (1973)).
Different replicates of both CAM plants exhibited very different photosynthesis profiles. This indi-
cates that the temporal duality of the photosynthetic process in CAM plants is governed by factors not
manipulated in this study. Figure 4.3 shows the photosynthesis rates of different replicates under the
same conditions. Turquoise represents the 1st, purple the 2nd, and pink the 3rd replicate.

Given that the relative humidity was kept constant at 50% for all measurements, the increases in
assimilation with temperature indicate that stomatal closure to prevent water loss is not triggered by
temperature changes. Instead, the stomatal closure and opening mechanism must be regulated by another
15
==PAGE:28==
factor. Dayanandan and Kaufman (1975) observed the stomata of Kalanchoe fedtschenkoi and other CAM
plants and reported that potassium ions migrate in and out of guard cells, creating turgor pressure that
opens and closes them.
However, recent research has shown that potassium ion migration isn’t enough to create turgor pres-
sure (Lee (2010)). During the night CAM plants produce malic acid which builds turgor pressure inside
guard cells, leading them to open (Kim and Lee (2007)). Through daytime, malic acid is broken down
to obtain CO2 for the Calvin cycle, dropping the turgor pressure and closing the stomata.
This way the stomatal aperture cycle follows the circadian rhythm and is controlled not only by the
intensity of the light, but also by potassium and malic acid concentrations inside guard cell vacuoles.
This explains why different replicates behaved differently in CAM plants, as intracellular factors were
not the controlled in this experiment.
4.2
CO
The graphs next page depict the CO2 assimilation and CO release curves for all the plants tested.
Results demonstrate plants release CO at all temperatures and light intensities.
In C3 and C4 species, the CO rate generally increases with light intensity, often in a linear fashion,
consistent with observations by Seiler et al. (1978) and Bauer et al. (1979) in tree leaves.
For some species, darkness induced more CO release than low light levels. This is particularly re-
markable in Vanilla planifolia which produced a U-shaped emission curve regardless of temperature.
Moreover, C3/C4 plants’ darkness measurements often produce rates outside the linear trend of light
measurements, indicating that there may be separate dark metabolic pathways producing CO. These
were most noticeable on Saccharum officinarum, the only C4 plant analysed. The observed CO release
in darkness disputes Wilks (1959)’s suggestion that such requires both oxygen and light.
In general, increases in temperature led to higher CO emissions from leaves, as well as wider emission
ranges depending on irradiation.
The relation between CO release and photosynthesis remains undisclosed. Like photosynthesis, CO
release is stimulated by visible light. But unlike photosynthesis it is not reliant on it, and CO rates didn’t
show signs of capping at high light intensities.
Moreover, the sensored CO emissions may not correspond exactly to the CO released from the leaves.
Logan et al. (1981) claim that an unknown, high share of the CO observed in experiments is actually
produced by the oxidation of hydrocarbons released by plants, such as isoprene (Zimmerman et al. (1978)).
This might be particularly noteworthy in closed-loop system measurements, such as this one.
Emission rates varied considerably from replicate to replicate, producing high errors for many of the
measurements. Standard deviation values for each datapoint can be found in appendix 6.2.

4.2.1
Upscaling
Seiler et al. (1978) and Bauer et al. (1979) conducted experiments on 4 vascular plants to measure
CO production. Their results, presented per unit area, are used to estimate the plant-based CO yearly
production. The temperatures were kept constant in the range 25-27ºC and different light levels are used.
The authors of both papers consider 5 ·104 erg cm−2 s−1 to be most representative of the world’s
average radiation.
In order to match results with those of Seiler et al. (1978) and Bauer et al. (1979) it was necessary
to convert light units from erg to µmol PPFD. For that purpose the energy of a photon was calculated
using the Planck-Einstein relation:
E = h · f
(4.1)
which is equivalent to
E = h · c
λ
(4.2)
Using equation 4.2, the photon energy (E) in Joules is calculated from the photon’s wavelength (λ),
Planck’s constant (h), and speed of light in vacuum (c). Through calculations, 5 ·104 erg cm−2 s−1 is
roughly equivalent to 250 µmol PPFD in the spectral mix used for this study.
For PPFD=250 µmol ·m−2 s−1 and T=25ºC, the inter-species average release of CO obtained in this
study was approximately 0.5 µg · dm−2 h−1.
To upscale plant CO emissions, the authors multiplied the emission rate per area by an estimate of
the average Earth’s surface leaf area.
Following the same calculations as Seiler et al. (1978) and Bauer et al. (1979), a global source of
roughly 200Tg is estimated from the results of this investigation. This figure is located in the high-end
estimate of Logan et al. (1981)’s CO budget.
However, in this study, dark CO rates were found to be outside the linear pattern of light-induced
emissions. Therefore, dark emission calculations should be done separately. To produce a better upscaling,
it is considered that all plants studied are subject to PPFD=0 for half the year and PPFD=500 for the
other half. To make the upscaling even more accurate, the 5 plant species in this study were combined
with the 7 plants species from Seiler et al. (1978) and Bauer et al. (1979).
Separating dark from light measurements of this study and combining the results for all 12 plant
species, a revised estimate of the scale of the global plant CO release is obtained: 138Tg.
138Tg correspond to 5.3% of the global annual source of CO, according to recent MOPITT data
(Zheng et al. (2019)).
However, these estimates must not be taken too seriously. First, they stem from incubation measure-
ments and may not be very representative at ecosystem level, and only a small group of plant species
were used in these calculations, meaning that the results may not be indicative of the bigger picture.
Additionally, estimating the average Earth’s leaf surface area and irradiation is over-simplistic.
19
==PAGE:32==
As Butenhoff and Khalil (2007) described: ”Extrapolation of the chamber studies to the global scale
is difficult due to the limited plant emission measurements and the spatial and temporal variability of
global vegetation”.
It is also worth noting Bruhn et al. (2013)’s finding of UV-induced CO plant release. This is expected
to be significant in nature but was not accounted for in this upscaling.
4.3
CH4
Results demonstrate plants release CH4 at all temperatures and light intensities.
Ch4 emission patterns are more chaotic than those of CO. Unlike CO, an increase in temperature
doesn’t translate into an increase in CH4 emissions for some of the plants tested. The range of emissions
seems independent from temperature as well.
Singularly, CH4 emissions didn’t show an exponential
correlation with temperature, as observed in previous studies (Keppler et al. (2006), Vigano et al. (2008)).
Among the CAM plants tested, a discernible pattern emerged across the different temperatures. For
Kalanchoe fedtschenkoi there is a logarithmic increase in emissions followed by a slight decrease beyond
PPFD=1000, in a fashion similar to that of photosynthesis curves of C3/C4 plants. For Vanilla planifolia
the U-shape is once again observed, as with CO, suggesting that both gases may originate in the same
biochemical process.
The magnitude of CH4 emissions observed in this study is way lower than that of previous studies
on attached leaves. Keppler et al. (2006) recorded CH4 emissions for attached leaves to be on the range
198-598 ng ·g−1 h−1. However, for excised leaves the authors found a much lower range of emissions,
1.6-15.8. Nisbet et al. (2009) found a similar range of 1-20 for detached leaves.
In this investigation, excised leaves were used to measure CH4 release and an emission range of 0.9-8.5
ng ·g−1 h−1 was obtained, matching the measurements of Keppler et al. (2006) and Nisbet et al. (2009).

4.4
Possible Origins of CO and CH4
Combining the findings from this study with existing research, it is possible to draw theories for the
potential origins of CO and CH4.
CH4
The most protruding feature about plant CH4 emission is the gap between connected and excised
leaves. The reason for this may be stomatal activity.
When leaves are excised, stomata close rapidly (McAdam and Brodribb (2012)). As a result, the CO
and CH4 measurements presented in this report represent the gas releases by leaves with partially to
totally closed stomata.
The remarkable difference between CH4 release from connected leaves and their excised counterparts
may stem from the difference in stomatal aperture. Given that connected leaves have CH4 release rates
of much higher magnitude, it is speculated that CH4 is mostly being released through the stomata.
The hypothesis that CH4 is being released through the stomata corroborates Zeikus and Ward (1974)
and Nisbet et al. (2009)’s suggestion that CH4 is produced in soil and transported through the xylem.
More research should be conducted to verify if there is a link between CH4 rates and transpiration or
stomatal conductance.
The findings of Vigano et al. (2008) conclude that UV incidence on excised leaves increases CH4
formation.
This may be explained by increased transpiration caused by UV light (Phyo and Chung
(2013)), or indicate towards an independent leaf CH4 production mechanism that is stimulated by UV
(or its by-products, such as free radicals).
CO
Compared to CH4, the difference in CO emissions from excised and connected leaves is less pronounced:
Bruhn et al. (2013) calculated CO rates of 2466 nmol m−2h−1 for a grass field and 1740 nmol m−2h−1
for freshly cut leaves (using sunlight, excluding most UV).
This indicates that CO release is independent from stomatal aperture. As such, CO may instead
originate from processes occurring at the leaf’s surface.
The leaf epidermis is covered by a film known as cuticle. The latter is enveloped by waxes, composed
of a mixture of alkanes, alcohols, ketones and esters (Kunst and Samuels (2009)). These compounds
may be subject to reactions with surrounding atmospheric gases and produce CO. The reactions causing
release of CO are expected to be stimulated by high temperatures and visible light, as was observed in
this study.
The reason why dark measurements sometimes demonstrated higher CO release than light mea-
surements remains unresolved. It points towards an unknown, CO-producing, dark metabolism that is
diminished by light. Additional research is needed to clarify dark CO release, as the positive rates found
in this study contradict Bruhn et al. (2013)’s findings of null CO release in darkness, despite the authors’
similar CO rates for light measurements.
23
==PAGE:36==
In summary, it is speculated that a high share of the CH4 measured in attached leaves is produced in
the soil, and may be complemented with independent leaf CH4 production. The observed CO is likely
being produced by reactions happening at the leaf’s surface.

Chapter 5
Conclusions and Future Work
The main findings of this study are hereby summarized:
• In C3/C4 plants, photosynthesis rates began to decline at high T (35ºC) past PPFD=1000 µmol ·
m−2 s−1
• CAM photosynthesis benefits from increases in T
• Stomatal opening in CAM plants appears to be controlled by turgor pressure inside guard cells
rather than light intensity or time of the day
• CO emissions increase linearly with PPFD, though darkness often results in higher emission rates
than low PPFD
• CO emissions increase with T
• CH4 emissions appear to be unrelated to PPFD and T
• CO emissions are suggested to be produced at epidermal level, given their low dependence on
stomatal opening
• CH4 is believed to dissolved in transpired water, but may also be produced by the plant
All the data found goes inside the consensus values for photosynthesis and detached leaf release of
CO and CH4.
One of the main observations from this study is that there is great variability between plant species.
Even within plants that follow the same photosynthesis pathways, different photosynthesis behaviors can
be found.
Equally important, despite the different replicates of most plants used being clones, they exhibited
different responses under the same conditions. This points towards the importance of environmental
stresses in the individual’s growth that modulate the future behavior of the plant.
This variability is important to take into consideration when producing upscalings from individual-
specific measurements, as it introduces wide margins of error in the final results.
25
==PAGE:38==
While the study provided conclusive findings, more research is required to develop finite theories.
As such, these additional experiments are recommended to refine our understanding of the origin of
plant-emitted CO and CH4:
• Compare stomatal conductance curve with CO and CH4 rate curve, in relation to PPFD and T
• Examine how CO and CH4 emissions evolve with changes in RH
• Use abscisic acid to force closing of stomata and observe changes in gas emissions
• Measure CO and CH4 release from isolated leaf extracts (namely the cuticle) under darkness, visible
and UV light
• Assess if atmospheric CO2 concentration affects CO and CH4 release
Further research should also aim to better understand the temporal cycle of CAM plants. It would
also be interesting to examine the photosynthesis curves of plants with different photosynthetic pathways
under constant PPFD and varying % RH.
Lastly, a link between leaf photosynthesis and plant release of CO and CH4 was not found.
In-
stead, alternative origins of these gases were suggested but will require supplementary investigation to
be confirmed.
