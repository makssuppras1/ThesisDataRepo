Abstract 
For over a decade now, the safety of nanomaterials has been continuously debated worldwide 
and despite the policy makers’ great efforts to find the best possible solution to regulate them, 
the subject is still controversial. One important aspect of regulation of nanomaterials that has 
been continuously discussed is the issue of labeling. A labeling scheme might be able to provide 
an increased ability of the researchers and governments to control and establish possible 
adverse effects of these products on the society. Some suggested that the public opinion in 
respect with the development of nanotechnologies should not be seen as an isolated process 
within a historical or geographical context but it should rather be linked to other debates 
regarding the new technologies, such as biotechnologies. However, the safety of nanomaterials 
is questionable and the regulatory authorities are under pressure to come up with the right 
piece of legislation that could deal with nanotechnology risks and in the same time allow 
industry to flourish while the public calls for transparency and assurance. The aim of this thesis 
was to: provide an overview of the existing legislation related to nanotechnology, highlight the 
challenges that regulatory authorities face when trying to regulate and label nanotechnology 
and nanomaterials, evaluate whether there were any lessons from the past labeling 
experiences that could apply for nanotechnology labeling and, not least, recommend the design 
of a labeling information and dialogue campaign.   In order to meet the objectives three trace 
analysis have been performed. The first analysis has presented the current situation and the 
challenges in respect to nanotechnology regulations. The second trace analysis related to the 
past labeling experiences (food labelling, GMO, ecolabelling) revealed important findings that 
might be proven successful in the case of nanotechnology and nanomaterials. The third trace of 
analysis was based on the survey that was performed in order to get updated information in 
respect to the consumers’ attitude towards nanotechnology and labeling of nanomaterials. 
Finally, all three trace analysis has been used in order to provide a recommendation on how a 
labeling information and dialogue campaign should be designed.

1. Introduction 
Nanotechnology plays an essential role towards all the scientific fields of study, such as - 
engineering, biology, chemistry, physics, material science, electronics and computer technology 
(Rajendran, 2009) - and has revealed vast commercial opportunities (Tolfree, 2006),  by offering 
huge profitable potential among marketers and investors(Bozeman et al., 2007). Today, the 
development of nanotechnology and its products- nanomaterials, have the prospective of 
revolutionizing the entire industry and market sector in Europe and worldwide (Hansen et al., 
2016). Moreover, the use of nanomaterials in novel technologies has lead to great 
environmental and economical improvements among all the industry sectors (Rajendran, 2009). 
All this are the reasons why nanotechnology is seen by scientists and experts as the fourth 
industrial revolution (Lo et al., 2012). According to Linstone (2011), nanotechnology offers an 
entire new generation of products, services and systems which have the capability to change 
the society and business market continuously. However, several studies have argued that the 
development of nanotechnology will depend on a great extend on the ability of the existing 
companies and industry to identify and take advantage of the commercial potential (Caerteling 
et al., 2008; Musso, 2009; Nikulainen and Palmberg, 2010; Chen and Guan, 2011).  
In the past few years many studies, addressing environmental and human health risks, were 
conducted and revealed that some of the nanomaterials could have negative impacts.(Linkov et 
al. 2009; Falken and Jaspers, 2012; Borm and Kreyling, 2004; Donaldson et al., 2004; Johnson et 
al., 2010; Albanese et al., 2012). For example, Shvedova et al. (2005) have found that carbon 
nanotubes can cause lung inflammations. As a response to these studies, discourses on 
regulatory approaches of nanotechnology emerged in all the Western industrialized nations 
(Kurath et al., 2014) and each of these nations come up with their own opinion on how to 
address the “nano-regulation” problem. The views on how to regulate nanomaterials, differ 
among nations trough a large extend, from a “laissez-faire” attitude to a whole moratorium 
regarding research, development and commercialization of nanomaterials (Franco et al., 2007).

In this context, on which the safety of nanomaterials is questionable and the regulatory 
authorities are under pressure to come up with the right piece of legislation that could deal 
with nanotechnology risks and in the same time allow  industry to flourish, the public calls for 
transparency and assurance. This thesis purpose is to explore these current subjects as follows. 
 
 
 
 
1.1 Objectives and outline 
 
 The driving problem of the thesis is related to the fact that currently, there are no or weak 
regulations when it comes to nanotechnology and labeling of nanoproducts. 
The purpose of this paper is to: 
  give an overview of the current situation of regulations regarding nanotechnology in 
the EU with a specific focus on labeling 
 highlight the challenges that regulators and policy makers face when trying to regulate 
and label nanomaterials  
 highlight  the importance of labeling and evaluate whether important lessons from past 
labeling experiences were learned 
 provide recommendations on how to label nanomaterials and on how to design a 
labeling information and dialogue campaign  
The first chapter of this thesis provides the reader with a brief introduction on the subject of 
nanotechnology. Chapter two starts by giving an overview of the continuously debated 
definitions of nanotechnology and nanomaterials, while the second part of this chapter 
presents the development of this industry by providing a short description of the most used 
nanomaterials and the fast expansion of nano related products on the market. In the chapter

three the existing regulations related to nanotechnology and labeling of nanotechnology are 
presented with an emphasis on the European Union approach. Chapter four highlights the 
importance of labeling and assesses whether there are any important lessons that can be

label nanotechnology and nanomaterials as well as the degree of knowledge of the general

information and dialogue campaign. In the last chapter the main conclusions will be presented.

2. Nanotechnology and Nanomaterials 
The first part of this chapter will provide an insight into the current existing definitions related 
to nanotechnology and nanomaterials followed by a second part where the development of 
this technology and its materials will be presented.  
2.1 Defining nanotechnology and nanomaterials 
In spite of the great importance that nanotechnology and its products have nowadays, there is 
currently no shared definition of the terminology. Moreover, scientific papers and policy 
documents confirm that terms as “nanomaterials”, “nanoparticles” and “nanotechnology” are 
defined in diverse ways (Bohnolm and Arvidsson, 2016). As a general statement, nanomaterials 
are “intentionally” produced in a synthetic or manufacturing process, by physical or chemical 
processes, resulting in materials that might have different characteristics from the bulk form 
(Alves et al., 2014). 
The first time when the term “nanotechnology” was mentioned in the context on engineered 
nanoparticles at the nanometer level, was in 1974, used by Taniguchi (Dowling et al. 2004). In 
2004, the Royal Society and Royal Academy of Engineering have come up with two different 
definitions regarding “nanoscience” and “nanotechnology” and in this separation between the 
two terms were made clear. Nanoscience has been defined as “…the study of phenomena and 
manipulation of materials at atomic, molecular and macromolecular scales, where properties 
differ significantly from those at a larger scale” while nanotechnology was referred to as “…the 
design, characterization, production and application of structures, devices and systems by 
controlling shape and size at nanometer scale” (Dowling et al. 2004). 
 
The definitions of nanomaterials have been revised several times since their first introduction in 
the middle of 1990s in order to go along with the development of the nanotechnology and also 
to facilitate the progress of the regulatory issued related to nanomaterials (Alves et al., 2014). 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
 
13 
 
Within the European Union, the definition for nanomaterials was first proposed in 2011 as 
follows: “Nanomaterial” as “a natural, incidental or manufactured materials containing 
particles, in an unbound state or to an aggregate or an agglomerate and where, for 50 % or 
more of the particles in the number size distribution, one or more external dimensions is in the 
size range 1–100 nm”. In 2012, this definition was revised in order to comply with REACH 
(Registration, Evaluation, Authorization and Restriction of Chemicals Regulation) (SCENIHR, 
2007). 
A recent study (Bohnolm and Arvidsson, 2015) has identified 36 different definitions related to 
nanotechnology and nanomaterials. Among the study conclusions, the authors claim that the 
lack of a clear shared definition might constitute a problem in the way of the scientific progress 
and regulations of nanotechnology. On the other hand, the study argues that a single definition 
might fail in capturing important aspects and even though it may serve different scientific 
purposes can be too restrictive in other contexts. However, The Scientific Committee on 
Emerging and Newly Identified Health Risks (SCENIHR), in a report named “Scientific Basis for 
the Definition of the Term ‘Nanomaterial’”, has come with some conclusions regarding the 
definitions on nanomaterials: “the definitions/descriptions of nanomaterials formulated so far 
have: (i) given a general size frame for nanomaterials in both external and internal dimensions; 
and (ii) some have referred to the unique physicochemical characteristics of the specific 
material under discussion. Whilst such broad definitions can be scientifically justified, they are 
not easy to apply within the context of a regulatory framework” (SCENIHR, 2010). 
According to Ruggiu (2015), the failure of finding a shared definition for all nanotechnologies 
prevents policymakers from adopting a simple solution in the case of regulation of 
nanomaterials, wheatear this solution might be a general ban or a general green light.  
2.2 Development of nanotechnology and nanoproducts 
The field of material research has been and continues to be one of the earliest applications of 
nanotechnology, ranging from high performance materials to everyday objects such as 
functionalized surface for glass (Cuenat et Winkless, 2010). The potential of nanotechnology 
industry was recognized in Europe in late 1990’s mainly as a reaction to the National 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
 
14 
 
Nanotechnology Initiatives in the US (Cuenat et Winkless 2010). After 2000, national initiatives 
and research studies were launched almost in all the countries of the world (Hansen, 2010). 
Nowadays, most European countries rely on Government-supported nanotechnology research 
and development known as” R&D” initiatives (Cuenat et Winkless 2010).  A summary of the 
main classes of nanomaterials are as follows: 
 Nanostructured materials: - have a dimension between 1 to 10 nm range and their 
proprieties depend on the size, arrangement and individual proprieties of their 
elements. 
 Nanoceramics: consisting in exceptionally fine grain structure ceramics relying on 
nanopowders as starting materials.  
 Nanocomposites: consist in materials constructed by introducing nanoscale objects (the 
filler) into a macroscopic sample material (the matrix). This materials are known to 
possess exceptional proprieties or combine different proprieties within a single material 
 Nanotubes: consist in a cylindrical structure based on hexagonal lattice of carbon atoms 
forming grapheme. This cylinder can have a diameter less than 5 nanometers and with a 
length of tens of micrometers.  
 Nanoparticles: are nano-objects with all three external dimensions within the nanoscale 
and their interesting proprieties are based on specific characteristics such as size, 
distribution, phase, etc  when compared with the same bulk material in a larger particle 
size.  
 Nanofibers for reinforcement: consist in nano objects with two similar external 
dimentions in the nanoscale and one significant larger extension. It is used most as 
reinforcement. 
 Nanoclays: consist in raw materials- nanoscale layered aluminum silicate platelets.  
 Fullerenes: are molecules formed only of an even number between 60 and 100 of 
carbon atoms  
 Electronic Nanowires: cosist in elongated structures with only one dimension in the 
nanoscale and which allow energy transmission 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
 
15 
 
 Quantum Dots: consisting in inorganic semiconductor nano scale crystals. Their special 
small size upshots new material proprieties and new quantum phenomena, arising from 
the arrangements of the electrons in the material 
 Dedrimers: are macromolecules with a very branched 3D structure which provides them 
a high degree of surface functionality. (Cuenat et Winkless 2010) 
 
 
According to Hansen et al. (2016), it is unclear which kind of products containing nanomaterials 
are available on the European markets and moreover there is no scientific evidence on how 
these goods have changed and developed over time. However, according to the same author, 
some inventories and databases with information on nanomaterials and nanotechnology 
related products are available and include:  
 Consumer Products Inventory (CPI) of the Project of Emerging Nanotechnologies, 
focusing on North America market, first on its kind, launched in 2006 
 The inventory established by the European Association for the co-ordination of 
Consumer Representation in Standardisation (ANEC)/The European Consumer 
Organisation (BEUC) containing products available on the EU market claiming to contain 
nanosilver particles, 2010 
 Nanoproduktdatenbank maintained by the Bund für Umwelt und Naturschutz 
Deutschland (BUND) focusing on products available on German market 2010 
 The NanoDatabase focusing on EU market available since 2012 
 French Nanomaterials compulsory reporting scheme launched in 2014 
 Danish Nanoproducts Registry  focusing on products available on Danish market 
available since 2014 
 Belgian Nanomaterials Registry  launched in 2016 
 
A number of limitations were found to be common among most of the above mentioned 
inventories, except for “The NanoDatabase”, and the most relevant have to do with the fact 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
 
16 
 
that: they are not continuously updated; sometimes products that are no longer available on 
the market are not removed from the database;  they are not openly available to the public 
impeding denying the right to the information for consumers; do not provide analysis tools that 
would facilitate independent analysis of the data and no health and safety information are 
provided (Hansen et al., 2016).   
However, “The NanoDatabase” was a project aimed to address all these limitations and it 
started in 2012.  The NanoDatabase is an online inventory, available at nanodb.dk, which 
consists in products based on nanotechnology or products, which manufacturers claim to have 
nanomaterials in composition. Currently, the NanoDatabase contains 2312 products 
(NanoDatabase, 2016). Along with the basic information of the product, such as name, the 
nanomaterials used in its production, location, category, etc., NanoDatabase facilitates access 
to NanoRiskCat framework and other analytical tools that allow the user to extract and class the 
information according to his own needs. According to an analysis performed by Hansen et al. 
(2016),   most products from the database are related to “personal care” and “clothing” 
category while the most common used nanomaterials are silver and titanium dioxide. The 
strengths of the NanoDatabase include the following:  it is daily updated, users are able to do 
their own analysis, it is publically available, includes hazard potential evaluation and evaluation 
of claims are performed regarding credibility.  The source of information relies on online 
research and reporting by users, while the limitations of the database have to do with the fact 
that the focus of finding products is mostly within European Union and the content of the 
nanomaterials in the specific product is based on claims of the manufacturers. (Hansen et al., 
2016)

3. Regulations of nanotechnology  
The purpose of this chapter is to give an overview of the current situation in respect to 
regulations of nanotechnology in the EU with a specific focus on labeling. The first part of this 
section will start with a short history of the regulations related to nanotechnology while the 
second part will provide a summary of the most important regulations within European Union 
that addresses the issue of nanotechnology. The last part of this chapter will highlight the 
challenges that regulators and policy makers face when trying to regulate and label 
nanomaterials. 
3.1 Regulating nanotechnology over time  
Regulatory discourses on the subject started in early 2000s, within all the countries that were 
involved at that time in research and development programs of nanotechnology and were 
characterized by intense efforts of assessment activities (Kurath et al., 2014). However, the 
question of whether to regulate nanotechnology, and if so to what extent, was first raised in 
1989 by David Forest (Pelley and Saner, 2009). In a paper that was written for a law course he 
stated: “The emergence of new technologies continually forces us to ask whether our laws 
provide the proper balance between protecting us from potentially harmful consequences of 
those technologies, and allowing us to reap the benefits. The development of nanotechnology, 
a molecular-precision manufacturing technology which is surprisingly close to realization, will 
seriously challenge the ability of our regulatory system to respond quickly and to maintain the 
critical balance between dangers and benefits” (Forrest, 1989) In his statement, Forrest 
predicted the problem that policy makers are facing today in a matter that is awe-inspiring, 
especially due to the fact that at the time he made this affirmation the nanotechnology was at a 
very early stage of development (Pelley and Saner, 2009).  
According to Kurath et al., 2014 the regulatory discourse concerning nanomaterials between 
2000-2013, has three distinct phases which at some times overlap, consisting in: an early phase 
that is being characterized by thematic discussions referring to nanotechnology with a rather 
small audience, a middle phase where intensive and heated debates took place with a broader 
number of participants and a late phase described by the author as a period where specific 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
 
18 
 
thematic importance was put on nanomaterials with an even higher number of participants 
(Kurath et al., 2014). In figure 2 bellow, which was obtained using “Google statistics” searching 
engine with the key words “regulations on nanotechnology” between the years 2000 and 2015, 
all the three phases can be observed as follows: from 2000 to 2004 there is almost a linear 
number of publications, followed by a constant increase between 2004 and 2008, whether from 
2008 an even greater increase in publication can be observed matching with Kurath et al.(2014)  
affirmation regarding the three distinct phases of the regulatory discourses.

cautious (Ruggiu, 2015).  However, according to the same author, the initial strategy that was 
adopted by the Commission at early 2000s was in agreement with the European Parliament and 
has followed the principle of “wait and see”. The main concerns were related to: whether there

nanotechnology and whether if there were any self regulations developed by the involved 
stakeholders (Ruggiu, 2015).   Behind this experimental strategy, there was a hope that the 
market and the industry will be able to develop and self regulate a solution for the problem of 
nanomaterials, at least until more scientific data will be available (Mandel, 2013).This approach 
can be characterized as “incremental approach” due to the fact that no specific regulations for 
nanomaterials were developed and instead, the existing legislative structures were used, and 
only when the case, these regulations were adapted to include nanotechnology (Franco et al.

In 2004, European Commission disclosed the strategy adopted towards nanotechnology and 
nanoscience in a Communication named: “Towards a European strategy for Nanotechnology” 
where authorities have start the basis for a more responsible approach. This strategy stated 
that the existing regulations should be revised and “a proactive approach should be taken” 
(European Commission, 2004). The aim was to differentiate the public and the private sector 
and to encourage a responsabilisation process of all the involved stakeholders that will 
furthermore lead to self regulatory attitude (Ruggiu, 2014).    
In 2005, the Commission has come forward to announce that the relevant existing regulations 
will be analyzed and an adaptation of them will be proposed with a special focus on toxicity, 
emissions, exposure paths and labeling requirements towards nanotechnology (Commission, 
2005). In the same Communication, the Commission requires that all Member States need to 
create inventories and have to review and adapt their national relevant legislation in order to 
include nanotechnology.  Moreover, a series of actions for a safe, integrated and responsible 
strategy for nanotechnologies have been proposed as follows: doubling the budget particularly 
for the nanoelectronics sector; increase toxicological and ecotoxicological studies; development 
of ethical, legal and sociological (ELSI) studies simultaneously in order to predict the possible 
impacts in early stage; encouraging the trading of best practices in industry together with an 
increase of its involvement in European Union R&D projects; specific guidelines and standards 
for risks assessment towards the whole life cycle of nanomaterials; increase dialog about 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
 
20 
 
nanotechnology at international level in order to create a code of conduct referring to the use, 
commercialization and development of nanomaterials; etc. (Ruggiu, 2015). 
To sum up, the foremost important aims were to boost the development and research studies, 
to include all the involved stakeholders in the development and regulation of nanotechnology 
and not least to apply the current regulations with a special focus on whether they are 
adequate or not (Ruggiu, 2015). 
The European Parliament, with its “2006 Parliament Resolution” has accepted the Commission 
approach with the intention to engage the Commission, Member states and industry in an 
effective dialogue that could lead to a sustainable path for nanotechnology. Moreover, the 
European Parliament underlines the fact that the focus should be put on industry, which needs 
to take into account all the risks related to nanomaterials, among their whole life cycle 
(European Parliament, 2006). 
Also, 2006 was the year when European Commission has launched the Seventh Framework 
Programme (FP7) dedicating €3,5 billions for research field, aimed to encourage 
nanotechnology industry, in order to build a “strong industrial base” and improve 
competiveness Within the programme, a wide ethic framework for research and innovation 
was developed with the aim to respect fundamental ethical principles and the Protocol for 
animal protection and welfare. (European Union, 2006) 
In 2007, the European Group on Ethics in Science and New Technologies (EGE) has published 
their opinion concerning nanomedicine arguing that there is a need for establishing procedures 
in order to verify the safety of nanomaterials and that these actions should happen on both, 
national and European level (EGE, 2007).  
In the same year (2007), the Commission has initiated the first proposal of a voluntary Code of 
Conduct for responsible nanoscience and nanotechnologies as a result of a consultation process 
and with the main aim of distributing the responsibility among the involved stakeholders and 
promote an integrated, responsible and safe research for the benefits of EU society (Ruggiu, 
2014). The Code of Conduct was meant to be seen as an instrument tool to insure a safe, ethical 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
 
21 
 
and sustainable development of nanoscience and nanotechnology. Moreover, it aims that 
Member States should encourage voluntary adoption of the Code of Conduct by all the involved 
stakeholders, from the regional and national authorities to employers, research founding 
bodies and any civil or individual organization involved in nanoscience and nanotechnology field 
(Dorbeck-Jung and Shelley-Egan, 2013). The Code of Conduct was adopted in 2008 and it was 
based on a set of principles that were identified by the stakeholders within the consultation 
process. The first principle -“Meaning”- was requiring that all the research projects related to 
nanotechnology should be comprehensible to the wide public. The second principle, 
“Sustainability”, call for a safe, ethical and sustainable development of nanotechnology and 
nanoscience without threatening human health, animals or environment in both, present and 
future. “Precaution” principle entail that research studies should follow the precautionary 
principle by anticipating the potential risks and by adopting specific measures for different 
protection levels. “Inclusiveness” and “Excellence” principles appeal to transparency among 
stakeholders and the use of the best scientific standards. The principle of “Innovation” was 
aimed to encourage creativity, growth, flexibility and planning ability for innovation. The last 
principle, “Accountability” asks for research organizations in the field to be hold accountable for 
social, environmental and human health impacts. (Dorbeck-Jung and Shelley-Egan, 2013)  
All the principles were generally well-accepted, except for the last principle (Accountability), 
which was contested in a number of surveys mainly regarding to the wide reference to future 
generations (Ruggiu, 2014; Ruggiu, 2015). Among the limitations of the Code of Conduct were 
mainly referring to the fact that there was a lack of correlation between the Code’s principles 
and its guidelines, and furthermore the last mentioned are not clearly formulated and might 
lead to misunderstanding or to an unintended consequence (Ruggiu, 2015; Meili et al., 2011). 
According to NanoCode survey, until 2011, only 21% of the NanoCode participants have 
adopted the Commission Code of Conduct leading to an unexpected disappointing result 
(Ruggiu, 2015).  
In 2008 the European Commission has launched a communication, consisting in two parts, on 
Regulatory aspects of Nanomaterials (European Commission, 2008). In the first part, the 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
 
22 
 
Communication evaluates whether the nanomaterials are covered within the existing 
regulations frameworks but also, more specific reviews the following fields of regulations: 
chemicals, health and safety at work, environmental and product legislations. The conclusion 
was that the nanomaterials are covered adequately by the existing legislation (European 
Commission, 2008).  In the second part, the Communication asks for the implementation of 
specific nano regulations, regardless of their previous conclusion, arguing that there are several 
steps that have been identified in order to improve the existing regulation in regards to 
nanomaterials. The steps that the Communication mention are: to address the existing 
knowledge gaps related to nanomaterials followed closely by the second step referring to an 
adoption of provisions that are specifically addressing nanomaterials, to provide better 
information to the consumers related to nanomaterials’ risks and the last step, a better 
implementation of the current legislation that should address the an increase in market 
surveillance and enforcement. (European Commission, 2008;  Gellert et al., 2015).    
If until 2009, European Commission was in agreement with The European Parliament, now the 
situation has started to shift, leading to a completely different attitude of the European 
Parliament towards nanotechnology.  With “European Parliament’s Resolution of 24 April 2009 
on Regulatory Aspects of Nanomaterials”, contrary to European Commission, states that there 
is a need for a clearer and more specific regulatory framework for nanomaterials in regards to 
potential issues, such as health, safety and environmental problems (European Parliament, 
2009). With this Resolution, the European Parliament mainly argues that the current legislation 
should be improved due to the fact that it does not cover the relevant risks concerning 
nanomaterials and moreover, calls for concrete legislative changes such as considering all 
nanomaterials as new substances. Moreover, the European Parliament asks for an inventory of 
all nanomaterials that are currently on the market and at the same time the safety of those 
should be reported.    
As a result, important frameworks concerning nanomaterials have been adopted or amended 
within the following areas to address nanotechnology and nanomaterials challenges: Chemicals,

Cosmetics, Food safety, Restrictions of the use of certain Hazardous Substances in Electrical and

 Regulation (EC) No 1907/2006 of the European Parliament and of the Council of the 
European Union called Registration, Evaluation and Authorization of Chemicals (REACH) 
In main lines REACH consists into four elements: Registration that refer to data collection on 
the chemical use and its toxicity; Evaluation referring to the examination performed by 
governments in regards to the need for additional testing and regulations of a chemical 
substance; Authorization of chemicals is basically the requirements for firms to request 
permission when using a high concern chemical substance; Restriction or complete ban of 
certain type of chemicals that cannot be used safely (Hansen et Baun, 2012). According to 
European Chemical Agency (2010), REACH does not specifically address nanomaterials even 
though there are a number of chemical substances that have been declared as nanomaterials, 
as for example nanosilver. According to Chaundry et al. (2006), one important limitation that 
REACH has towards nanomaterials is whether nanomaterials should be considered as to be 
equivalent or different from a bulk chemical. The same author (Chaundry et al., 2006) argues 
that according to REACH this limitation will have a great impact on the requirements that 
manufacturers have to fulfill prior to market and if the nano substance will be considered 
different from the bulk chemical, then hazard information will be required for the registration 
dossier if the substance production will exceed 1 tons per year. However if the nanosubstance 
is considered to be the same with the bulk substance, then whether the hazard information will 
be appropriate is a subject for an open discussion (Chaundry et al., 2006). Another significant 
limitation of REACH in addressing nanomaterials is related to the fact that “Substances 
manufactured or imported in volumes of less than 1 tons/year do not need to be registered” and 
according to Chaundry et al. (2006) and to Franco et al., (2007) this threshold is hardly to be 
reached by most producers of nanoparticles since the tonnage in this case is from gram to 
kilogram quantities. Moreover, according to the same authors, a number of nanomaterials 
could be excluded from REACH also due to the fact that nanoparticles come in small

concentration and REACH does not require registration when the concentration is lower than 
0.1% w/w.  
 
 Regulation (EC) No 1223/2009 on cosmetic products: 
The regulation calls for a notification requirement for all cosmetic products containing

safety data and exposure conditions. Moreover, the products containing nanomaterials should 
be labeled accordingly, by clearly indicating the ingredients list followed by the word “nano” in 
brackets (EP and CEU, 2009). The Cosmetic products regulation was the first framework in 
European Union that included provisions related to labeling of products containing 
nanomaterials as part of the information requirements (Brinch et al., 2015).

By taking into account the precautionary principle, the intention with this directive was to 
restrict hazardous substances, and here nanomaterials were also a main concern due to their 
proprieties related to their small size and structure that could be hazardous to environment 
and human health, and recommend some other alternatives that could be more environmental 
friendly and could bring safety to consumers (European Union, 2011).  
 Directive 2002/96/EC on waste electrical and electronic equipment (Recast) 
 
The idea behind the directive is to set new targets for collection of waste coming from electrical 
and electronic equipment, due to the possible risks related to nanomaterials components. 
(European Parliament, 2012)

 Regulations on Biocidal products May 2012

definition of Nanomaterials (Brinch et al., 2016). 
 
 Regulation (EC) No. 258/97 concerning novel food and Novel food ingredients 
 
The original legislation requires all food and food packaging to be safe for the consumers, 
implying that even food or food packaging that contains nanomaterials should go under the 
same circumstance. However many have criticized this legislation to be to loose when referring 
to nanomaterials therefore in January 2008, the European Commission adopted a new proposal 
on the Novel Food regulations to specifically address the nanomaterials problem and to define 
them as novel food (Falkner et al., 2009).

3.3 Regulating uncertainty 
For over a decade now, the safety of nanomaterials has been continuously debated worldwide 
and despite the policy makers’ great efforts to find the best possible solution to regulate them, 
the subject is still controversial. Currently, nanotechnology is developing and is being 
commercialized without a full scientific risk assessment (Maynard, 2011). One of the main 
impediments in regulating nanotechnology is the lack of data in regards to the implied risks of 
nanomaterials and the great challenge to overcome this problem (Ruggiu, 2015). 
 
Nanotechnology development can be characterized as breathtaking and it is intended to 
transform the whole industrial landscape (Falkner and Jaspers, 2012). Having such a wide level 
of extend it is likely to assume that nanomaterials come with unforeseen risks (Maynard 2011). 
In order for nanotechnology sector to prosper there is a need to understand the temporary 
risks as well as the long term risks of human and environmental exposure to this materials 
(McAlea et al., 2016). It is however quite difficult to cover the whole world of nanotechnology 
under the same set of regulations, and this is due to the fact that the research fields and at the 
same time the markets have very different needs (Ruggiu, 2013). Moreover, the same author 
(Ruggiu, 2013) argues that the developing of a complex framework for regulating 
nanotechnology would be a very hard task due to the fact that the legislator will have to create 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
 
27 
 
a toolkit able to address present and future challenges of nanotechnology and in the same time 
to comprise all the different nanotechnologies. 
 
Even though each nation has a different view over the matter, according to Hansen (2010), the 
challenges that they face when trying to implement regulatory measures are similar and consist 
in: “whether to adapt existing legislation or develop a new regulatory framework, how to 
define nanotechnology and nanomaterials, whether nanomaterials should be considered as 
different from bulk, and how to deal with the profound limitations of risk assessment when it 
comes to nanomaterials” (Hansen, 2010). 
Whether to adopt statutory law often referred to it as “hard law” or less binding modes of 
governance such as voluntary measures-“soft law” – is still a subject of debate among 
governments and the actors involved in the nanotechnology field (Kurath et al., 2014). 
 Another debate on the subject among policymakers worldwide is whether the existing 
regulations can be adapted to deal with the unique proprieties of the nanomaterials or a 
completely new framework specifically intended to deal with nanotechnology is need it. 
(Hansen, 2010). All these uncertainties and challenges are extended beyond the individual 
regulatory capacity of a state and require extensive international cooperation (Falkner and 
Jaspers, 2012).

4. Labeling experiences and the lessons from the past 
One important aspect of regulation of nanomaterials that has been continuously discussed is 
the issue of labeling. According to dictionary, a label consist in a small piece of paper, fabric, 
plastic or a similar material  that is attached to a specific object with the scope of providing 
information. In general, when intending to introduce a new label policy, there are two 
prerequisites that have to be fulfilled: first, there is a need of a clear definition of the targeted 
labeling attribute and second, a risk regulatory framework needs to be set up (Gruere, 2011).  
According to Zimmer et al., (2010), a labeling regulation that has no clear definition of the 
product that is intended to be label, will have difficulties in being adopted.  The author also 
specifies that usually, labeling regulations are set up in combination with a broader regulatory 
framework.  Even though one important reason of labeling is to inform consumers, often the 
framework is introduced as a complement of risk assessment and therefore usually labeling is 
refer to as a secondary regulatory approach, following risk assessment (Gruere, 2011).   
 
The aim of this chapter is to highlight the importance of labeling and to draw attention to the 
challenges that policy makers face when trying to proper label a product. In order to provide a 
better understanding of the labeling context, the first part of this chapter will provide a 
background related to the previously faced experiences concerning this matter. This will be 
done by looking into the product label history and more specific, by analyzing two labeling 
experiences: food labeling with an emphasis on genetically modified organisms (GMO), and 
ecolabelling as a more sustainable alternative. The reasoning behind these choice, was to 
present a glimpse on one of the oldest labeling experience- the food labeling- and in the same 
time to present the current context and challenge of a new type of label-ecolabelling. With the 
intention of understanding better the whole context in respect to the existing labeling schemes, 
US and Canada labeling initiatives will be presented in this chapter asides for the European 
Union approach.

4.1Product labeling  
In general, labeling, with the scope of informing the consumer about the containing ingredients 
of a product, is a long tradition, while the great variety of labels that are present today on the 
market is a result of the numerous initiatives conducted by consumers’ organizations and 
environmental groups (Throne-Holst and Rip, 2011). In a self service economy, a company that 
wants to sell needs to find a way to create attention, inform and promote its products to a 
specific target audience and in the same time to be able to separate itself from competitors 
(Tootelian and Ross, 2000). The the way that a product can be differentiating from other is 
resumed to its package and label and as a result, producers are using a lot of time resources 
and strategies to make sure that the packages and labels of their products are attracting the 
attention of their consumers (Tootelian and Ross, 2000). Studies have found that consumers 
have different expectations from a label and the differences between the opinions might be 
related to: reliability, easy identification, understanding and use, full information and freedom 
to choose (Grunert & Wills, 2007; Feunekes et al., 2008). Even though labels have proven to be 
successfully market tools, they also have the potential of misleading and confuse the consumer 
(Burke, Milberg, and Moe, 1997).  The labels are the main source to inform the consumer about 
the safety and the effective use of a product and in general, the legislation related to labeling is 
intended to protect public health and defend consumers from misleading information 
(European Commission, 2014). 
To overcome this problem and in order to protect the consumers, European Union has 
established the “Act 2006/2004 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 27 October 
2004 on cooperation between national authorities responsible for the enforcement of consumer 
protection laws (the regulation on consumer protection cooperation)” (European Commission, 
2004). The act implies that each of the Members States should establish a network of public 
authorities that will be held responsible for enforcing consumer protection laws and will enable 
national authorities to exchange information and cooperate between their counterparts within 
all the EU countries.  Another important regulation concerning the misleading and comparative 
advertising is “Directive 2006/114/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 12 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
 
 
30 
 
December 2006 concerning misleading and comparative advertising”. The main aim of this 
directive is to protect buyers and sellers against misleading advertising, which in this case is 
equivalent with unfair commercial practice but also to determine under which conditions 
comparative labeling is allowed (European Commission, 2006).  
Today specific labeling regulations have been developed for food, novel food, cosmetics, 
organic products, etc. As already mentioned, one of these chapter intentions will be to look into 
food labeling, GMO and ecolabelling experience and see to what extent these lessons can be 
applied to nanotechnology labeling.  
4.1.1 Food Labeling 
Consumer attitude regarding food and the new food technologies starts back in time and is 
linked with their traditions, believes and even with their whole identity. Food labeling can be 
categorized as the main way of communication between the producer and the consumer in 
respect to a certain product (Turner, 1995). In other words, food labeling can inform the 
consumers about the origin of their food and its nutritional content as well as can provide 
information about the practices used in the production stage. Moreover, the information stated 
on the labels can help consumers to make an educated choice by made them aware of the 
health, environmental and social impacts that a certain product might have (Howard, 2010). 
According to Turner (1995), the role of food labeling is very important because the label itself is 
a prime marketing tool and a key source of information for the purchaser. Moreover, the 
author claims that food labeling is an important tool to discipline the food chain with the help 
of regulations by protecting the consumer and insuring a fair trading (Turner, 1995). A label can 
furthermore help the consumer to differentiate between similar products (Golan et al., 2001), 
allowing them to make a choice that fits best with their needs. 
The attributes described on a food label can be numerous and are usually influenced by the 
stakeholders: the consumers rely on their purchasing power to determine the attributes that 
are to be printed on the labels; private firms try to identify the characteristics of products that 
are attractive for the consumers providing voluntary information on those specific 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
 
31 
 
characteristics and by doing so they attract more customers; Third-party entities, such as 
governments, private and international organizations play a key role on this matter because 
they mediate between consumers and producers by enhancing the transparency and  the 
credibility of the information through regulations, certifications and standard settings (Golan et 
al., 2001). 
Over time, food labeling has gone through different stages and regulations. Nowadays, all 
products that are present on markets come with an attached label of some sort. This was not 
the case in the past, when all food products were produced, sold and consumed locally and 
consumers were not relying on government controls and labeling to ensure the quality and 
safety of their goods (DASH, 2001; Turner, 1995).  In order to make sure that the products that 
were available on the markets had a high quality, consumers were testing the merchandise by 
looking at it, touching it, smelling it or poking at it (DASH, 2001). 
The legislative history of  food labeling started more than 100 years ago, in 1906 in US, when 
the Congress main aim with “The Food Drug Act of 1906” was to establish a basic standard for 
food labeling which will furthermore prevent the consumers to be misled (FDA, 2016). Here, an 
important milestone was the fact that the uses of preservatives was regulated and had to be 
indicated on the label. Also, 1906 is the year when additive labels first appear (Turner, 1995). 
Another important piece of legislation was “The Federal Food, Drug and Cosmetic Act of 1938” 
intended to cover a larger variety of products and to include labeling of cosmetics. Another 
intention of this act was to force producers to be more clear and specific about the ingredients 
stated on the labels and to clarify the vagueness of 1906 act. 
Today, regulations towards food labeling become very specific in many countries, in such extent 
as they become real means to educate the public about nutrition. The latest trends in labeling 
regulations are trying to consider the issues of the internationalization of food distribution and 
in the same time create new regulations that should not set boundaries among the 
international food distributors while trying to maintain as well individual regulations for each 
country. (DASH, 2001)

Within European Union, labeling of food products need to contain the following specifications: 
name under which the product is sold, the list of ingredients in descending order of weight, the 
quantity of ingredients/categories of ingredients expressed in percentage, the net quantity, 
date of minimum durability and a list o allergens (European Commission,2014).  
According to European Commission, starting with 13th of December 2014, consumers need to 
have better access to clear, comprehensive and reliable food information. The new act 
combined 2 Directives into one piece of legislation:  
 2000/13 EC- Labeling, presentation and advertising of foodstuffs 
 90/496/EEC- Nutrition labeling for foodstuffs

Nutritional labels and the consumers attitude towards food labeling 
A nutritional label is a great tool that can be used to promote healthy eating habits as well as it 
can contribute to a reduction of diet related diseases conditions of population (Gregori et al., 
2013). Furthermore, the nutritional labels are used to disclose essential information about the 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
 
 
33 
 
nutritional value and composition of certain food products to consumers. By taking into 
consideration this positive scenario, where consumers understand and take advantage of the 
information that was provided for them by labeling, one can assume that the result will lead to 
healthier choices and a significant reduction in disease related risk factors (Borgmeier and 
Westenhoefer, 2009). However, Rothman et al.(2006) suggested in their paper called “Patient 
understanding of food labels: The role of literacy and numeracy”, that even if it can be generally 
assumed that consumers can read and interpret the information stated on the labels, this is not 
always the case and moreover this assumption cannot be taken for granted. Moreover, Harper 
et al. (2007) revealed in a review by the Food Standards Agency, that 38% from the consumers 
find difficulties in understanding the information printed on the nutritional labels.  
The incapability of consumers to interpret the labeling is not news for the stakeholders. During 
the late 1980s, several research studies showed that “consumers find the current state of 
nutrition labeling complicated, confusing, incomplete and often misleading” (Schannon, 1997). 
Cowburn and Stockley (2004) have had also the same findings. Even though consumers 
reported that they do understand the terms: “fat”, “calories” “sugar”, “salt” and “vitamins” 
they still find nutrition labeling confusing, especially in regards to the technical and numerical 
information. But what does understanding of nutritional labeling by the consumer really mean? 
According to Cowburn and Stockley (2004), understanding of the content of labels equals with 
recognizing each ingredient and what the measurement unit means. Moreover, a consumer 
that is able to use the nutritional label in his favor will also be able to understand the role of the 
nutrients in the body and the relationships among them in terms of healthy eating.  
It has been demonstrated by several studies that age, gender, level of education, nutritional 
knowledge, the level of income, occupation and health status play a key role in understanding 
the nutritional labels and the usage of it (Mannell et al., 2006; Drichoutis et al., 2006; Coulson 
2000). Women, people with high income and educated people are most likely to check the 
nutritional labeling more often. Men are less likely to report an interest in reading the labels 
while for the elder people the label reading habits are reported unclear (Cowburn and Stockley, 
2004). 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
 
 
34 
 
Today, the gap between availability and actual use of nutritional labeling is object of a strong 
debate among the manufactures and stakeholders, and efforts are made in order to achieve 
more effective results (Gregori et al., 2013). The fact that nutrition labels do not meet 
consumer’s needs was a reality that has for long time been recognized. The reason for that 
might be because the content and format of nutritional labels are intended to meet legislative 
requirements rather than to be able to meet the needs of the costumers (Cowburn and 
Stockley, 2004). Also, there are no convincing evidence that nutritional labels can represent an 
effective impact on population level in regards to diet related disorders (Gregori et al., 2013), 
but definitely an improvement in nutrition labeling could make a difference among consumers 
by helping them make an informed choice (Cowburn and Stockley,2004). 
4.1.2 GMO labeling and the consumer’s right to know 
The consumer desires to have access to information in regards to the content of their food 
products has generate a concern in respect to biotechnology food issue that requires now a 
public policy concern (Raab and Grobe, 2003). Genetic engineering involves the manipulation of 
genes in the lab to create new varieties of crops with some new desired characteristics 
(Institute of Food Technologists, 2000). The genetically engineered food products started to 
appear on the market in mid-1990s (Raab and Grobe, 2003). Genetically modified food labeling 
relates to a statement printed on the label of a prepacked food product, or to a written 
declaration in case of non-packaged food, saying that the specific product contain or does not 
contain genetic engineered ingredients (Du, 2014). 
The first regulatory measure concerning  genetically modified organisms (GMO) labeling was 
adopted in 1997 by the European Union (Regulation (EC) No 258/97 of the European Parliament 
and of the Council of 27 January 1997 concerning novel foods and novel food ingredients) and 
today more than sixty countries promulgated laws in regards with this matter (Carter and 
Gruere, 2003). As a general classification GMO labeling schemes are classified in two categories: 
voluntary and mandatory. A mandatory scheme of labeling is a result of strict regulations, 
where the GMO products need to be clearly differentiated from non-GMO product, while 
voluntary labeling allows producers to decide if they want label or not (Du, 2014). 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
 
 
35 
 
 
In European Union the labeling system concerning GMO is mandatory and requires that 
products should be labeled as containing genetically engineered ingredients if the content of 
these ingredients is above 0.9% (Senauer, 2013; Du, 2014). The current EU legislation tries to 
address the consumer’s lack of confidence towards GMO food products with the use of risk 
assessment and risk management tools as well as by new labeling and monitoring requirements 
(Valetta, 2010).  Today, European Union is one of the biggest markets in the world regarding 
the numbers of granted authorization for GMO food products. However, studies have shown 
that the perception among consumers in relation to GMO in EU is still negative (Valetta, 2010; 
Du, 2014; Carter and Gruere, 2003).  
 
There are several explanations that can be taken into consideration when we refer at the 
negative perceptions and attitude towards GM food; sometimes the GMO are perceived by 
consumers as being unnatural and therefore risky even though the perception is not based on 
scientific evidence (Valletta, 2010). Another explanation might be the fact that many 
consumers have no or limited knowledge about the genetically modified food and its use in 
agriculture (Brown and Ping, 2003). On the other hand consumers are concerned that there is 
no evidence on the long term risks and that the GMO might be proven risky if more research 
will be done. This perception could be because the concern is that the GMO end up into the 
body and determine health and physical well-being (Valletta, 2010). Finally, consumers can 
argue that the risk analysis systems are opaque and also risk management and decision making 
practices are not clear in the eyes of the consumers (Carter and Gruere, 2003). An improved 
transparency and governance of the European Union GMO framework can contribute 
substantially in building consumer confidence, as well as bind a stronger relationship among the 
consumers, the stakeholders and academic scientists (Valetta, 2010). 
  
Contrary to the legislation from European Union of mandatory labeling, the US and Canada rely 
on voluntary labeling regimes fact that allows the food producers and retailers to choose 
whether to label or not their products as GMO or non GMO, or not label at all  (Du, 2014). 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
 
 
36 
 
According to Senauer (2013) most of US processed food products contain GMO ingredients. 
Food and Drug administration (FDA) found out that in 2012, a high percentage of corn and 
soybeans, 88% respectively 94%, were genetically modified in the US territory. Labeling of 
genetic engineered ingredients in the United States has been long debated and controversial 
(Korwek, 2000). 
 
The US legislation does not support “the consumer’s right to know” as a triggering tool to 
determine mandatory GMO labeling. The Canadian federal agency for food labeling and Health 
Canada state that the GM foods do not differ from non GM foods, therefore the problem 
should be addressed in the same way as for the conventional foods (Health Canada, 2006). 
According to Health Canada (2006) all foods that have been approved by this institute should 
be safe for the consumers even if they contain or do not contain GM ingredients. Moreover, 
Health Canada claims that GM food products should be labeled only if there are some changes 
in food that consumers need to be aware of, such as new allergens or change in composition. 
The proponents of mandatory labeling in US and Canada criticize the government by saying that 
voluntary labeling of GMO is not enough and also that consumers were kept ignorant in respect 
with GM foods, and linking it all to the consumer right to know movement ( Nep and 
O’Doherty, 2013). 
 
A study by Marks et al. (2002) among US citizens showed that a great majority are supportive or 
neutral regarding genetic engineered food. The same author suggested that this fact might be 
more due to ignorance than to acceptance. Moreover a survey showed that at least one quarter 
of the Americans do not know about the usage of biotechnology in food production (Raab and 
Grobe, 2003). 
 
The first important attempt to apply mandatory labeling within United States took place in 
state of Oregon in 2002. It was called Ballot Measure 27 and it was defeated due to possibility 
of very costly lawsuits for violations as well as for the possible economic losses in food industry 
(Senauer, 2013). Recently, there are more than 25 states in the US that take into consideration 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
 
37 
 
ballot initiatives and require labeling of genetically modified ingredients (Harman and Pollack, 
2012). In June 2013, State of Connecticut passed a mandatory law regarding labeling of GMO 
but the law cannot go into effect until at least another four states adopt similar regulations 
(Senauer, 2013).  According with the same author (Senauer, 2013) the main argument of the 
supporters of GMO labeling “conforms to the principle of the consumer’s right to know”.  
 
 Another similar act with Ballot Measure 27 happened in November 2013 in Washington and it 
was called I-522, “The People’s Right to know Genetically Engineered Food Act”. The bill require 
that raw agricultural products and prepacked products that contain GMO to display the words 
“genetically engineered” or “partially produced with genetic engineering” after the case 
(Senauer, 2013). A survey in the case of Ballet Measure 27 showed that the opinion among 
citizens are different but  main answers of the proponents of GMO labeling were: “the public 
has the right to know and the producers an obligation to let them know”, “People deserve to 
make an educated choice”, “I like to know what I am eating”, “we should be in control of what 
goes into our body”, ”they haven’t done enough studies on the effects” and “other countries 
are doing it to” (Raab and Grobe, 2003). On the other hand the opponents from the same 
survey claim that: it is not cost effective and that there are already enough regulations, and 
that the benefits will not outweigh the costs (Raab and Grobe, 2003). Other opponents bring 
more arguments against GE labeling. Caswell (1998) argues that once labeled, the products that 
contain GMO might be levied as being harmful by the consumers and an important technology 
that has been prove safe so far could suffer. Senauer (2013) believes that there is a difference 
between the consumer “right to know” and the consumer “need to know”, arguing that if a 
product does not pose a real risk to human health than the information is not necessary to be 
disclose.  
 
As already being seen, the consumers have different opinions from the experts. Their 
perception is influenced by different factors, such as media and their previous experiences with 
food products. However, as the above discussions reveal, in the US, the consumer’s right to 
know was not a sufficient reason to adopt mandatory labeling of GMO in foods.

4.1.3 Ecolabeling 
 
Ecolabels are voluntary market tools that are intended to bring benefits to environment, 
consumers and markets. The Ecolabel Index has listed more than 450 labels. Among them, 
many are not official and many are not real ecolabels. The International Organization for 
Standardization (ISO) has embark in attempting to standardize the principles, practices and the 
key characteristics to three types of voluntary environmental labels: Type I-environmental 
labeling, Type II-self declaration claims and Type III-environmental declarations. 
According to ISO standards, Type I  is “a voluntary, multiple-criteria based, third party program 
that awards a license which authorizes the use of environmental labels on products indicating 
overall environmental preferability of a product within a product category based on life cycle 
considerations” while Type II refers relies only on informative environmental self declaration 
claims. The third type of environmental labels (Type III) are similar to Type I and imply 
“voluntary programs that provide quantified environmental data of a product, under pre-set 
categories of parameters set by a qualified third party and based on life cycle assessment, and 
verified by that or another qualified third party.” These are not the only labeling systems in the 
world. Other third party labeling systems exist or are being developed now they have narrower 
focuses than the ISO standard program. Most of the times these labeling systems focus solitary 
on a single sector such as chemical industry, or only on a life cycle stage of the product such as 
the disposal, either addresses only one environmental problem.

The ISO standard program for labeling requires that companies, who want to be a part of an 
ecolabelling program, have to apply and submit their products for a third party verification. If 
granted, the companies will have to pay licensing fees in exchange for the ecolabel symbol for a 
specific time. (ILCD,2010) 
 
Among ecolabelling objectives, environmental conservation and protection is the major 
purpose. This can be achieved by: encouraging an efficient management of the renewable and

The meaning of ecolabels to consumers 
Today, the increase awareness regarding the environmental problems poses a pressure on 
consumers to make more environmental friendly choices in their everyday life. They are 
implored to use more the public transportation in the detriment of their private cars, to use less 
energy and water, as well as to reduce their carbon footprint.  Ecolabelling are a useful tool 
when it comes to addressing these challenges. Without having access to ecolabels, consumers 
are simply no able to make an educated choice, nor to compare products in environmental 
impact terms (Larson and Farkas, 2011). The same authors claims that being able to evaluate 
which products are better in environmental terms is not an easy task for consumers and require 
clear and specific information. Furthermore, Larson and Farkas (2011) believe that consumers 
need a simple, standardized label which can provide them with the information that they 
necessitate to gain environmental awareness and to include it in their purchasing decisions. 
These ecolabels will be proven successfully only if they will manage to transmit messages to the 
consumers and influence them in the acquisition of their goods (Daugbjerg et al., 2014).  
One might be thinking that if the consumers will be able to identify between labeled products 
they will be motivated to choose the more environmental friendly product. However, as studies 
have already proved, even if the consumers show interest in sustainability issues their 
purchases are disappointing for the stakeholders that tried to create “green” market choices 
(Peattie,2001). A reason for the lower interest to purchase  an ecolabeled products might be 
the premium price that consumers have to pay or even the fact that there is still not enough 
trust between the consumers and producers intentions and there is still a need for time to 
adapt.(Boer, 2003) Consumers might have mixed motives when choosing a product in the 
detriment of another and at the moment the purchase decision will depend very much on how 
they understand, value and trust a claim in relation to the desired product. However, 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
 
 
43 
 
consumers’ awareness towards green products is not an easy running process and it depends 
on the ability of stakeholders to make it clearer. 
Ecolabelling from the stakeholders point of view 
In regards to the sellers, labeling of products is an important market tool that can be used to 
improve their competitive position towards other companies. However, sometimes there are 
circumstances when collaboration towards companies is more advantageous (Boer, 2003). A 
decision of a company to use ecolabels is usually governed by tactical and political 
circumstances but also by technological innovations and public campaigns (Reinhardt, 1998).  
Policymakers play a very important role in environmental labeling of products. They make use 
of the regulating labeling schemes in order to address important aspects of ecolabelling. First, 
regulators have to establish a balance between the consumers and the sellers, making sure that 
there are no asymmetries in the information that is disclose to the public due to the fact that 
sellers have more information than buyers about the qualities of a certain product (Boer, 2003). 
Second, policymakers see the ecolabels as an important tool that can be used to achieve 
sustainability goals. From their perspective, labeling and certification schemes might be able to 
create motivation for business to adjust the market in a more sustainable path. According to 
Boer (2003), labels in general, are considered by regulators a first step on a government 
strategy that will put pressure on consumers and producers and making them choose a desired 
path. 
Environmental and social NGOs play a key role as well in the matter of ecolabelling. They put 
pressure as well on consumers and producers, but also on governments to choose a more 
sustainable path. NGO’s will probably support the ecolabelling system initiatives but they will 
also promote other things such as consuming less, using second hand products and consuming 
the same products for a much longer period of time.(Boer,2003) 
Professor Ralph Buckley Griffith University from Australia believes that there are three 
fundamental reasons why ecolabels have failed. First he thinks that consumers care much more 
about the labels that refer strictly to their personal benefits, and care much less about the 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
 
 
44 
 
ecolabels because their effect is spread among society and environment. Second, he believes 
companies tend to use false eco-labels in order to avoid regulation. His last argument is that 
there is no real way for consumers to be able to distinguish between a real ecolabel and a fake 
one and that usually, weak ecolabel tend to mimic the accurate ones. ( Buckley, 2013) 
However, all stakeholders involved in environmental labeling are still learning to cooperate 
together for sustainable goals in the context of marketplace. Consumers can learn but this is 
difficult process that usually takes time and producers need to find out as well, which kind of 
“greener” products the buyers really want (Larson and Farkas,2011). 
 
4.2 Lessons learned from previous experiences 
A lot can be learned from past experiences with labeling when it comes to implement a labeling 
scheme for nanotechnology and nanomaterials. Food labels started with the idea of preventing 
fraud and have ended up as a complex marketing tool that can now ease communication 
between consumers and producers, and moreover increases the awareness among consumers 
regarding health related problems. Three overall lessons learned revealed from the analysis of 
food labels: 
 
 Consumers are often not able to interpret labels:  
The case of nutritional labels described in the previous chapter indicates that consumers 
are often not able to interpret labels and even though information is made available to 
consumers, they might not really be able to understand it. This might be due to the fact 
that, more often, labels are designed to meet regulation requirements rather that the 
consumers’ needs by which the consumers often end up finding the information 
provided confusing and sometimes misleading. Providing information on labels to meet 
regulatory requirements is perfectly legit, but it just has to be clear that this is the 
purpose of the label and its design is to inform regulatory authorities about the 
presence of e.g. nanoingredients and materials and not consumers. 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
 
 
45 
 
 
 Just providing a label is not enough:  
When it comes to providing consumers with information, it is clear that “just” providing 
a label is not enough. A negative example that should be taken into consideration is the 
case of GMO food when a label was introduced without any follow up information and 
has led to a negative perception among public and a drastic drop in sales and one might 
argue that this could be also an undesired result in the nanotechnology case. Consumers 
can be lead by the principle “If you do not have enough information you might not buy.”  
To overcome this problems information campaigns might be a very good tool aiming at 
educating consumers to make more educated purchasing decisions and to care more 
about their health. 
In the case of food labeling, the new piece of legislation from 13th December 2014 
containing two directives (2000/13 EC- Labeling, presentation and advertising of 
foodstuffs and 90/496/EEC- Nutrition labeling for foodstuffs) can be also a source of 
inspiration when labeling nanotechnology. The following should be important in the 
case of nano containing products: 
 An improved legibility of the information is needed:  
Both the case of nutritional label and GMO show that consumers often find labels 
confusing. As the European Commission (European Commission,2014) has argued, this 
might be due to the fact that often labels are written with a small font, fact that might 
lead the consumers to think that the specific part of the label is not important. 
Therefore an improved legibility of the information needs to be taken into consideration 
in the case of labeling of nanoproducts as for example an increase in fond size or using a 
good contrast between the letters and the background.  
 
By looking into the ecolabelling experience, there are also some important lessons that can be 
taken into consideration and that might facilitate the introduction of nanoproducts labels. As in 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
 
 
46 
 
the case of food labels, the lack of sufficient information available makes consumers to have 
misled expectation from an ecolabeled product. However there are also some positive aspects 
that are worth look into: 
 An easily identifiable label should help 
The case of Endorsement Environmental Labels should be a positive example on how to 
introduce a label to the consumers. As already mentioned in the chapter 4, the 
endorsement labels have been proven very successful. A part of this success is due to the 
high level of awareness among consumers that was mainly gained from information 
campaigns and on the high rates of recognition of the label. However, if we take into 
consideration the Environmental Information-only labels, it is clear that a label that will only 
introduce technical terms without explaining them will not really work for the consumers.  
 Limit the use of the false labels  
In the case of environmental labels, there were often cases when producers were using fake 
environmental labels to influence consumers but also more often to skip regulations. This is 
definitely a possibility in the case of labeling nanomaterials since there is already evidence 
that some producers are using the word “nano” in brackets only as a way to promote their 
products even if sometimes these statements are only claims.

5. Labeling of nanoproducts  
One aspect of regulation of nanomaterials that has been continuously discussed is the issue of 
labeling. In this chapter the current situation related to the subject of labeling of nanoproducts 
will be discussed with an emphasis on the consumers’ perspectives in respect to this matter. 
The first part of this section will describe the current attempts to label nanoproducts while the 
second part will focus on the level of awareness and attitudes related to labeling of 
nanoproducts among consumers by making use of the existing literature. The third part will 
present the results of a survey that was conducted with the scope of gathering fresh 
information in respect with what is that the public is expecting from a label. The results of the 
survey will be furthermore used in chapter 5 to design a label information and dialogue 
campaign. 
5.1 Previous attempts to label nanoproducts 
The number of products containing nanomaterials is increasing daily on the European markets 
and worldwide (Nano Database; Gruere, 2011; Hansen et al, 2016). In this context, labeling of 
nanoproducts has become a demand within member states but also at European level 
(Eisenber et al., 2010). A labeling scheme will facilitate an easy tracking of the nanotechnology 
based products through society and moreover will permit an increased ability of the 
researchers and governments to control and establish possible adverse effects of these 
products on the society (D’Silva and Bowman, 2010; Gruere, 2011). However, labeling 
nanotechnology and nanomaterials is not a straight forward process (Throne-Holst and Rip, 
2011). Capon et al. (2015) argue that by supporting labeling of nano-based products with an 
emphasis on societal rights will lead to consumer acceptance of nanotechnology. The reasoning 
behind this argument is that once nanotechnology is labeled, the consumer will have the 
freedom of choice and an increased awareness related to products containing nanomaterials 
and moreover it will increase the transparency between stakeholders, which will furthermore 
lead to a larger acceptance of nanotechnology products (Capon et al., 2015).  Meanwhile there 
are also some arguments against labeling of nanomaterials and are mainly related to a possible 
misinterpretation of the meaning of the label and moreover mention the multitude of 
complications that one might be facing when trying to implement such a scheme (Capon et al., 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
 
 
48 
 
2015). More specifically, according to Capon et al., (2015), the arguments against labeling 
include a possible negative perception towards public, all the additional costs that labeling 
requires, the potential trade obstructions that might occur between a country that requires 
labeling and one that does not, a shift in responsibility between the regulatory authorities and  
consumers and not least, public apathy.  According to the same author (Capon et al., 2015) the 
complications in implementation of a labeling scheme include the lack of a clear definition of 
nanoproducts, the lack of accurate detection tools for enforcing the system, no clear guidance 
in regards to accidental or natural contamination with nanomaterials and the lack of a clear 
guidance regarding a products that does not contain nanoproducts but it has been produced 
using nanotechnology (D’Silva and Bowman, 2010; Gruere, 2011; Capon et al., 2015). 
According to von Schomberg (2010), the public opinion towards the development of 
nanotechnologies should not be seen as an isolated process within a historical or geographical 
context but it should rather be linked to other debates regarding the new technologies, such as 
biotechnologies. Moreover, the author states that the introduction of genetically modified 
organisms in particular is often used as a reference point within Europe when discussing the 
matter of nanotechnologies.  
Past experiences showed that the way that a new technology is introduced into the market is 
very important and requires caution. The case of genetically modified organisms (GMO) can 
serve as an example, showing that when the introduction is poorly done and the consumers 
have no access to clear information than the technology is rejected by the society (Vogel, 2012; 
Devos et al., 2006).  In order to avoid the fate of GMO, many have called for a greater 
engagement of the public and for an early consideration of regulatory tools such as labeling 
(D’Silva and Bowman, 2010).  At this extent, a number of deliberative processes regarding 
nanotechnology were organized by different organizations and the majority of them took place 
in Europe, but also in US (Stø et al., 2010).  These processes were varying in scope, number of 
participants, target groups but the intention was to engage the participants in debates 
regarding different aspects of nanotechnology (Throne-Holst and Rip, 2011). These deliberative 
processes revealed that one of the top requests of the participants that were invited to come 
==PAGE:49==
 
 
 
 
49 
 
with concerns, solutions or action plans, was to label the consumers products that contain 
nanomaterials (Stø et al., 2010). 
In general, labeling regulation characteristics depend on many aspects, such as: type, scope, 
message content and position, coverage, etc., but the greatest contrast between them refer to 
voluntary and mandatory labeling schemes (Gruere, 2011). Even though some voluntary 
schemes on labeling of nanoproducts have been proposed over time, according to Throne-Holst 
and Rip, (2011) the political initiatives for mandatory labeling seem to be most preferred 
approach, involving different actors, such as regulatory authorities, industry leaders, consumers 
and not last NGO’s.      
However, the views related to the purpose of labeling and its connection with risk assessment 
are in contrast among the involved stakeholders, as some of them see labeling as a way to 
provide information while others see it as a necessary precaution measure (Knebel and Maili, 
2010).  
In 2004 The Royal Society & The Royal Academy of Engineering in the UK has proposed that the 
nanoparticular materials should be included in the list of ingredients/components of consumer 
products and moreover, argue that the nanomaterials should be treated as a new chemical 
substance calling for transparency and better access to information for consumers (Royal 
Society, 2004).  
In 2006, the Canadian environmental protection organization Action Group on Erosion, 
Technology and Concentration (ETC-Group) has organized a competition for finding a 
“nanohazard symbol” after assessing the risks of synthetic nanomaterials as high, calling for a 
moratorium in 2003. The “nanohazard symbol” was aimed to warn the presence of engineered 
nanomaterials and was intended to be used for labeling of products, transporting containers 
and to be listed in laboratories and factories involved in manufacture and manipulation of this 
materials. The competition gathered 482 different designs from 24 different countries (ETC

5.2 The public knowledge related to nanotechnology 
The perception and the rate of knowledge of the public in regards to the new technologies play 
an essential role in the science-society interaction (Kim et al., 2014). The factors known to 
determine the perception of the public as well as the way that the information is being 
processed and used by the public is shaped to a large extent by that respective level of 
knowledge (Zimmer et al., 2009) and by the ability of the public to make an objective judgment 
(EBN, 2008). Whether the nanotechnology will be accepted or not will depend on a variety of 
different factors, such as mass media influence, scientific data available at the moment, 
impression that products containing nanomaterials have on the consumers and no least the 
==PAGE:53==
 
 
 
 
53 
 
public’s level of education (Takai and Wakamatsu, 2010).  The public perception in respect to 
science can be easily influential as in the case of genetically modified food, when an 
uninformed public lead to drastically drop in this products sales (Cobb and Macoubrie, 2004). 
Cobb (2005) has demonstrated that in absence of balanced information, the public’s only 
option is left to speculate about the possible impacts related to nanotechnology and it often 
relates with past technologies that might be misleading, such as asbestos, nuclear power or 
dioxin.  According to Scheufele and Lewenstein (2005) and Kohler and Som (2008), public 
opinion is a key factor deciding the success or failure of a new technology and its products. 
However the importance of public understanding of new technologies has been recognized in 
the beginning of 21st century with the UK Royal Society’s report on nanotechnologies, 
researchers from UK have developed and promote the term “upstream engagement” (Wilsdon 
and Willis, 2004; Royal Society, 2004). The upstream engagement refers to the fact that the 
general public should understand nanotechnology and be part of the decision making plan in 
regards to the development and applications of this field (Kim et al., 2014). According to Cobb 
and Macoubrie (2004), this type of engagement will allow citizens to perceive that their 
concerns are taken into account by scientists and policy makers, leading to an increase of the 
public trust in the involved actors and institutions.  The same authors point out that the public 
trust is also a key aspect in the general opinion on nanotechnology.  
 However, many studies suggest that the fast development of nanotechnology did not truly 
attracted much attention from the public and that only a small percentage of the population 
indicates some degree of comprehension about the term “nanotechnology”  (Cobb and 
Macoubrie, 2004; Gaskel et al., 2005; Rosenbladt et al., 2007; Kahan et al., 2007). For example, 
in 2004, Cobb and Macoubrie have carried out a national survey among US citizens, and found 
that the public have heard little or nothing at all about nanotechnology. In 2010, the 
Eurobaromater survey revealed that about 40% of the respondents indicate low level of 
knowledge related to nanotechnology while another 20% had a general negative perception 
about nanotechnology (Eurobarometer, 2010).  Another study in Europe, carried out in France 
was aimed to assess the public understanding regarding nanofood and nanotechnology food 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
 
 
54 
 
packaging (Vandermoere et al., 2011).  The results were similar with Cobb and Macoubrie study 
in 2004, showing that French public had little knowledge about nanotechnology. In the same 
year (2011), another study was carried out in Iran to examine the public awareness and 
attitudes towards nanotechnology. The results revealed that the majority of the questioners 
were not familiar with the term nanotechnology and that the public attitudes were driven by 
hopes and expectations (Farshchi et al., 2011). A more recent study conducted in South Korea 
(2014) aimed to examine the consumer awareness and concerns regarding nanotechnology 
(Kim et al., 2014). The study found out that about 80% of the respondents indicated either that 
they “did not know at all” or “did not know well” and only about 1.4% considered themselves 
well informed about nanotechnology.    
All this studies conducted worldwide have examined public perception on nanotechnology and 
revealed that even if more than a century has passed from the first attempt to assess the 
consumers perspectives in regards to nanomaterials, the level of knowledge among public is 
still very low. 
5.2.2 Consumers’ attitudes towards labeling of nanotechnology 
When it comes to consumers’ perception in regards to labeling of nanotechnology, studies 
showed that the public perceptions are in general supportive, regardless of the type of public 
being tested (Capon et al., 2015; Throne-Holst and Strandbakken, 2009) .  A study conducted in 
Norway in 2009, showed that the public concerns are related to the fact that the current 
regulatory frameworks might not cover completely the nanoproducts issue (Throne-Holst and 
Strandbakken, 2009). Another study that took place in US revealed that the American public is 
also concerned about whether the regulatory frameworks that apply to nanomaterials are 
sufficient or not but in the same time the study revealed that the public also believes that an 
involvement from their part will probably have a low influence on the regulation authorities 
when it comes to implement a labeling scheme of nanomaterials (Brown and Kuzma, 2013).  
The same study revealed that consumers believe that a label requires clear explanation in order 
to be used correctly and that the industry and the government should disclose this type of 
information and moreover that the public relies greatly on previous experiences on product 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
 
 
55 
 
labeling (Brown and Kuzma, 2013).   Capon et al., (2015) conclude that labeling of nanoproducts 
will have a significant influence in consumers’ purchasing behavior.  
Non Governmental Organizations, similar to consumers, seem to welcome a labeling scheme 
regarding nanomaterials (Throne-Holst and Rip, 2011).  In 2008, the European Commission 
conclusion was that the present regulations are sufficient to address nanomaterials, even 
though it admits that some modifications need to be made through amendments (European 
Commission, 2008). As a result, a number of organizations and groups have demanded the 
introduction of mandatory labeling schemes of nanomaterials in the consumer’s products 
(Falker et al, 2009).    
Based on the above, it could seem that no public surveys have been completed in Europe since 
2009, while the development and widespread use of nanomaterials, for instance in consumer 
products has risen dramatically. Hence, a need has been identified to conduct a survey on 
consumers attitude towards nanotechnology and labeling of nanomaterials in order to 
investigate whether the consumers have changed their attitude towards nanomaterials since 
2009 and more important, what they think in respect to the labeling concern of nanomaterials. 
The following part will present the results of a survey that was conducted in order to gather 
relevant information in regards to the consumers’ attitude towards nanotechnology and

5.3 Theory of the survey 
Nowadays, social surveys are one of the most preferred ways of gathering information in 
applied societal research (Kelley et al., 2003). According to the same author (Kelley et al., 2003) 
the term “survey” refers to a rather large sample of individuals from a pre determined 
population that is often referred to  as “the population of interest” and the data which is 
collected from these individuals in a standardize form. In this way the researchers use the 
information that they gather from a sample of individuals to draw conclusions about a wider 
population (Kelley et al., 2003) or in other words, “to obtain data for mapping” (Denscombe 
2010). 
Hoinville and Jovel (1985) argue that the design and the structure of a survey rely on three 
important factors that need to be clarified at the beginning of the study. These factors are: the 
purpose of the study, the target audience and the available resources (Hoinville and Jovel, 
1985).  Moreover, the same authors claim that a good survey has to be designed in a way to 
suit the aims of the study and the respondents’ nature. Usually, surveys are often used in 
descriptive studies but also to investigate different aspects of a situation or to look for 
explanations and test hypotheses (Kelley et al., 2003).  Denscombe (2010) - in his book entitled 
“The Good Research Guide” (For small scale social research projects) - state that there are 
many strategies that can be used when planning a survey and that there are always some other 
options and alternatives. Similar with Hinville and Jovel (1985), Denscombe (2010) argues that 
no matter what strategy a researcher will choose, the essential components will be the same: a 
research paradigm in which the researcher is using “the big picture” as a basis for approaching 
the research; a research design which is basically a plan of action to insure the project success 
and not last, the research problem consisting in fact in the target goal that should be clearly 
specified. Furthermore, the same author argues that the researchers should make a clear 
distinction between strategies and methods and be able to justify clearly the choice of their 
strategy. Moreover, factors like suitability- referring to the ability of the survey to collect 
appropriate data, feasibility- referring to the capability to successful collect data in a limit of 
time with a certain budget, ethics- in this case referring to the fact that participants should be 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
 
 
57 
 
ensure that their data is confidential and the nature of the research regarding their 
involvement, are all very important to consider when choosing a strategy (Denscombe, 2010). 
According to Denscombe (2010) there are many types of surveys from which a researcher can 
choose from such as postal surveys, internet surveys, telephone surveys, face to face surveys, 
etc., and each of these types have different strengths and weaknesses. For example, internet 
surveys are a cheap and fast alternative and can be attractive for researchers wanting a quick 
result on a restrictive budget. Even though one might argue that the data obtained online is not 
that reliable, studies suggested that there is actually no significant difference between Internet 
surveys compared to other more traditional methods (McCabe, 2004; Denscombe 2006; Lozar 
Manfreda et al., 2008). 
In regards to the questionnaire layout, according to Kelley et al., (2003), all the questions 
should be clear and well presented. The same author specifies that the researcher should be 
careful about the form of the question in a way to avoid doubled barreled questions (two or 
more questions in one), double negation or ambiguous and leading questions. Moreover, all the 
questions should be numbered and group warily by subject. 
Survey research should demand the same standards in research practice as any other research 
approach and it should be evaluated with the same strictness level as the previously mentioned 
(Kelley et al., 2003). According to the same author, the researchers need to take into 
consideration all the already mentioned steps that are required when performing a survey 
research and be organized and thoughtful when planning, executing and reporting the results 
of their project (Kelley et al., 2003).   
5.3 Theory and principles of public communication and dialogue campaigns 
The theory and the principles of public communications and dialogue campaign that will be 
presented in this section will be used as point of reference in chapter six “discussion” part of 
this thesis, when recommending the design of a labeling information and dialogue campaign. 
Public communication campaigns are defined according to  Rice and Atkin (2012), as “purposive 
attempts to inform or influence behaviors in large audiences within a specific time period using 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
 
 
58 
 
an organized set of communication activities and featuring an array of mediated messages in 
multiple channels generally to produce noncommercial benefits to individuals and society. “ 
According to the same author, a campaign designer should set clear specific objectives that 
ought to the development of a consistent set of strategies. Moreover, the designer should 
implement the campaign by using informational and convincing messages through the 
traditional media, new technologies but also through interpersonal networks.  
According to the same author, every communication campaign should start by identifying the 
audience. The strategic approaches should be chosen depending on the purpose of the 
campaign, with a focus on prevention or promotion.  In general prevention campaigns present 
fear and the negative consequences of a detrimental practice while promotion campaigns focus 
on presenting a desire of a positive alternative. Regarding the content of the message that is to 
be presented, according to Rice and Atkin (2012), the designer of the campaign should choose 
between an informational or persuasive message. The informational message usually refers to 
the ability of a communication to create awareness while persuasive message appeals to 
emphasizing reasons why the audience should adopt or avoid their behavior. 
A stakeholder dialogue is defined by Cuppen (2012) as “an organized meeting of stakeholders 
with different knowledge, values and backgrounds, who would otherwise not meet (or not all 
together), structured to a greater or lesser extent by means of specific methods, tools or 
techniques” (Cuppen, 2012). The same author states that a stakeholder dialogue is strongly 
associated with the concept of “learning”.  According to Kaptein and van Tulder (2003), 
contrary to a stakeholder debate where the “competition” ends with a winner and a looser, a 
stakeholder dialogue is based on cooperation where each of the involved is a winner. The same 
authors specify that there should be ten principles that will insure an effective stakeholder 
dialogue and are:  “to know and be understood, trust and reliability, clear rules for the 
dialogue, a coherent vision on the dialogue, dialogue skills, expertise in the subject matter, 
clear dialogue structure, valid information as basis, consecutive meetings, feedback of results 
(Kaptein and van Tulder, 2003).” Key performance indicators could be also developed from a 
stakeholder’s dialogue, which will furthermore help in reporting and monitoring the results 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
 
 
59 
 
(Kaptein and van Tulder, 2003). The correct identification and selection of the stakeholders 
plays a crucial part in designing the dialogues as well as guarantees the validity of it (Cuppen, 
2012).  
 
 
5.4 Survey on Consumers’ attitude towards nanotechnology and labeling of nanomaterials 
The main purpose of this study, as already being mentioned, was to gather relevant information 
from consumers with the scope of designing a potential labeling information and dialogue 
campaign in regards to nanotechnology. In other words, there was a need to know what 
consumers think about this matter, what is that they expect from regulation authorities, where 
do they look for information, and who would they trust best to inform them about 
nanotechnology. The degree of awareness towards nanotechnology among public, the public 
attitude in concern with labeling of products containing nanomaterials, the level of trust in 
public institutions as well as a risk benefits assessment were also subject of the present survey.  
As already mentioned in the previous chapter, in order to insure a clear strategy, some 
essential components need to be taken into consideration. The first was referring to the fact 
that a researcher should use the big picture as a basic approach in the survey. This study has 
considered this aspect, since the questions started to engage the respondent slowly in the 
topic. It started with a question as general as “Have you previously heard or read about 
nanotechnology and nanomaterials before?” up to asking specific questions related to 
regulations and labeling of nanomaterials such as “Do you think that consumer products that 
contain nanoparticles should be all labeled?”.   The second aspect considered in this study, also 
mentioned in the survey theory, refers on the ability to decide upon a research design that will 
insure the success of the survey. To do so, the internet survey type has been chosen especially 
due to its ability to gather the information fast since the time allocated for data collection was 
approximate 5 weeks. Suitability, feasibility and ethics were also important factors that have 
been taken into consideration for this study, since they represent insurance for a successful and 
reliable survey. The program used to collect data is one of the most used online software for 
==PAGE:60==

surveys, utilized by millions of companies, organizations and individuals worldwide ensuring a 
high suitability for this study. This survey can be also considered feasible since the data 
collection was gathered on time. In regards to ethics consideration, as the theory demands, the 
respondents were ensure at the beginning of the study about the confidentiality of their data 
but they were also informed in respect to their degree of involvement in this research.   
5.4.1 Methodology and limitations of the study 
For the present study 266 adults over 18 years old have been questioned between the period of 
May 2016 and June 2016. The sample size was relatively small and the data was collected, as 
already being motioned, via internet, by using the online survey program “Survey Monkey” as 
well as by distributing some hard paper samples. The survey target was men and women aged 
over 18, while the target sample size was at least 200 respondents. 
The survey was based on individual responses and approx half from the respondents were 
students or master students of Technical University of Denmark, while the rest of the 
respondents were collected with the help of social media. Half from the respondents were 
selected due to their grater interest and knowledge of science while the other half was 
representative for the regular type of consumers. No information about nanotechnology or 
nanomaterials was provided previous to the survey’s questions. In respect to demographics of 
the survey, three types of demographic data were collected: age, gender and country of 
residence. The survey items were classified into 7 different groups and consisted in 20 different 
questions besides demographics. The first group of questions was related to the general 
knowledge and familiarity in respect with nanotechnology, followed by questions related to the 
source of knowledge, attitudes concerning nanolabeling, interest in finding more information 
about nanotechnology, trust in institutions involved in nanotechnology industry, questions 
related to the potential risks compared with the benefits of nanotechnology and not least 
overall emotions related to nanotechnology filed.  
The limitations of this study were mainly referred to the relative small size of the participants. A 
more broadly representative survey related to the public perception on nanotechnology would 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
 
 
61 
 
be a better and more desirable option in order to be able to better apply the result for 
designing a potential labeling information and dialogue campaign. Another limitation is related 
to the fact that the demographics did not include questions related to the level of income or 
the level of education. The reasoning behind this choice was mainly due to ethic consideration, 
since sometimes people might feel offended when ask to reply such questions. However this 
type of demographics would have allowed this study to be subject for a better statistical 
analysis given that other studies showed that there is a correlation between the level of 
income, level of education and the level of awareness related to nanotechnology (Senokak, 
2013). Also, the sample was not carefully balanced in regards to the age of target population, 
the biggest percentage being represented by respondents aged 21-29, but this was due to the 
fact that the majority of the respondents were students.

 Low  level of awareness towards nanotechnology

nanoproducts. Moreover the need for clear information is also suggested by the fact that a 
majority of 68.3% would only buy nano labeled products if they would know for sure that these 
kinds of products are safe.

About 46% of the respondents indicate that the main source of information related to

6. Discussions 
This chapter will discuss the main findings of the thesis by taking into account all three trace 
analysis that have been previously mention. The first part of this section will provide 
recommendations by making use of the previous experiences related to the issue of labeling of 
nanoproducts as well as of the results of the survey. The second part of this section will 
propose a design for a labeling information and dialogue campaign. 
 
 6.1 Key findings from past experiences and the survey 
 
With the growing interest towards labeling of nanomaterials, the work described in this thesis 
aims to gather relevant information from consumers in order to design a potential labeling 
information and dialogue campaign. The main findings of the historical analysis of past 
experiences with different labeling requirements for food, GMO and ecolabelling can be 
summarized into five overall lessons learned. The first lesson relates to the fact that consumers 
are often not able to interpret the labels even though the information is available for them.  
This lesson drives to the next one, respectively that just providing a label is not enough. By 
taking into account these two experiences it is clear that information and dialogue campaign 
might be a very good tool aiming at educating and informing the public in respect to 
nanotechnology. The third lesson calls for an improved legibility of the information that is 
present on the label. In this case the analysis showed that the consumers find the labels 
confusing due to the fact that often labels are written with a small font, fact that might lead the 
consumers to think that the specific part of the label is not important. The fourth lesson refers 
to the fact that an easily identifiable label will probably guarantee a higher rate of success. The 
fifth lesson that was identified calls for limiting the false labels that are used by the companies 
to avoid regulations. As the analysis shows, the producers use fake labels also with the 
intention of influencing the consumers and this is where the relevant stakeholders, such as 
government, NGO’s could intervene and prevent this from happening.  
As already mentioned, one of the main lessons learned from the main experiences was that 
there is a need for a clear label. When it comes to designing a label there are a few good rules 
of thumb that need to be considered and, as previously stated, mainly referring to the 
importance of the message, the font size, contrast, etc. A good example of label that take into 
consideration all these aspects might be found in one of the winner labels of the “hazard 
symbol” contest  organized by the Canadian environmental protection organization Action 
Group on Erosion, Technology and Concentration (ETC-Group) from 2006 that was already 
discussed in chapter 5. The “nanohazard symbol” was aimed to warn the presence of 
==PAGE:73==
 
 
 
 
73 
 
engineered nanomaterials and was intended to be used for labeling of products, transporting 
containers and to be listed in laboratories and factories involved in manufacture and 
manipulation of this materials. As it can be seen in Fig. 24  the recommended label has a clear 
and simple message “nano hazard” that could facilitate the understanding even for an informed  
consumer in respect to nanotechnology, and this is due to the word “hazard” that has been 
intensively used as warning signal printed on different labels 
before. Apart from clearly drawing attention, the strong black and 
yellow contrast has been also widely used to warn in respect to 
dangerous situations. 
   
Current available information about consumer perception of 
nanotechnology and, specifically, labeling of nanoproducts is old 
and limited, but this type of information is vitally needed in order to reflect on, among other, 
how consumers feel about nanotechnology, in which way they should be approached in respect 
to a labeling campaign, who they would trust best to inform them about nanotechnology, what 
is that they expect from the regulatory authorities and where do they look for relevant 
information. The degree of awareness towards nanotechnology among public, the public 
attitude in concern with labeling of products containing nanomaterials, the level of trust in 
public institutions as well as a risk benefits assessment were also subject of the present survey 
that was aimed to provide fresh information in respect to this matter.  All this type of 
information data is really necessary when one want to plan for a potential labeling information 
and dialogue campaign.  
From the survey it seems clear that although the public has a general interest in 
nanotechnology, their level of awareness is still very low. Another important aspect that the 
survey revealed is that the internet is the main source used by the public to look for 
information in respect to nanotechnology and nanomaterials. When it comes specifically to the 
issue of labeling, the results of the survey indicates that the public is in general supportive in 
respect to this matter. Another important finding of the survey is that although public generally 
has a high level of trust in the government to protect them from nano related risk, they also 
seem to display a low level of trust in the same institution to be responsible for of informing 
them in regards to nanomaterials and nanotechnology. 
Another important aspect that one might want to take into consideration when implementing a 
labeling information and dialogue campaign scheme has to do with the availability of 
information that is accessible to the public trough the online media which often does not 
correspond with the existing information on the product. This consideration has been already

presented in the first chapter discussing nanoinventories, and there is definitely room for

6.2 Proposal for a potential labeling information and dialogue campaign on 
nanomaterials 
By taking into consideration the previous findings a recommendation for a set up campaign will 
be proposed. The main aim of the campaign and the dialogue is to provide information for the 
public regarding nanotechnology and labeling of nanomaterials and help them understand the 
labels. The campaign addresses to the general public as the main target group.  
As can be seen in fig 17, the proposed strategy of the campaign will follow five different steps, 
trying to comprise all the gathered information from this thesis analysis. 
==PAGE:75==
 
 
 
 
75 
 
 
Figure 25 Steps of labeling information campaign 
 
In the beginning a capable team will assembled, responsible with the research procedures, 
strategies, engagement of the stakeholders,  implementation of the campaign and not least, 
monitoring and reporting the results. As the results of the survey showed, the public has low 
level of trust in government to inform them in respect to nanotechnology. In this case, the 
government should consider funding independent entities, such as NGOs or Consumer’s 
Organizations to be responsible for the campaign, since these organizations have in general a 
high credibility among the public, fact also revealed in the survey. The industry leaders involved 
in nanotechnology production should be also invited by the government to contribute with 
financial support for the campaign, since they are also important stakeholders in this matter. 
The target audience could be then informed about this financial support coming from 
government and industry and in this way assure the public about the good intentions and 
support of these two important stakeholders towards a better informed population. As already 
mentioned, the public does not trust government and business leaders to inform them about 
nanotechnology matter and this opportunity might be used to increase trust and transparency.  
According to Cob and Macoubrie (2004), if citizens perceive that stakeholders take their 
==PAGE:76==
 
 
 
 
76 
 
concerns into account this will promote a higher trust in relevant actors and institutions leading 
to an enhanced democracy.     
In the second stage, preliminary research studies should be conducted as well as setting the 
goals. During the research stage, the team will look into the available literature to gather 
relevant data that will furthermore help to design the following steps. Also within this phase, 
the team will prepare for the stakeholder dialogue with the intention of involving all 
stakeholders. For example, the team will sketch a list with all the relevant stakeholders that 
need to be engaged in this dialogue.  
The next step of the campaign will be the strategy and tactical planning.  Since the survey 
results showed low level of awareness among consumers in respect to nanotechnology and 
labeling of nanotechnology, in this stage, the messages and the strategy that will be used in the 
informational campaign to increase awareness will be chosen and formulated. Also within this 
step the most effective way to spread information will be decided. The results of the survey 
revealed that the main source of information of the respondents was the internet, so the 
campaign might use this information and utilize different online public service promos, for 
example in the form of a banner ad message that will furthermore lead to a website with 
information about nanotechnology and labeling of nanomaterials. Blogs and social media like 
Facebook or Twiter, could be also used to inform public about nanotechnology. Commercials 
printed on public transportation might also be a good source of information for the public in 
regards to labeling of nanomaterials. For example a banner, showing a scientist giving a brief 
explain of nanotechnology and explaining what the word nano in brackets printed on the label 
represents. The scientist in the picture should be a well-known doctor or scientist since, 
according to the survey results, scientists and health institutions are by far the most trusted by 
consumers to get information from.  The banner should be exposed also in other public 
locations, such as supermarkets, libraries, bus and train stations, etc. Commercials on television 
will be also a great way to promote the campaign and inform the public. For example, a small 
advertising video spot in which the same person that was present in the banners is talking 
about nanotechnology and about what the word nano printed on the label means.   Postal 
service could be as well used to spread the information in the form of letters or flyers. Also 
during this stage the list of the stakeholders should be complete but also the timetable and the 
place of the dialogue meetings. 
The forth step is the implementation part, where all the strategies and tactical plans mentioned 
previously will be implemented but also the step where the stakeholder dialogue will take 
place. The proposed stakeholders for the dialogue are representative parties of government, 
general public, business leaders, NGO’s, consumer organizations and media. The media will be 
present with the intention of informing the general public about the subjects and the 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
 
 
77 
 
conclusions discussed in the dialogue. During the dialogue, the organizing team should ensure 
that all stakeholders speak but also listen to the other parties. There should be at least six 
different meetings within the dialogue since the subject of nanotechnology and labeling of 
nanotechnology showed in fact a very low level of awareness towards the general public.   
 In the last step monitoring and reporting actions will take place. Key performance indicators 
that have developed from the stakeholders’ dialogue and from the information campaign will 
help in reporting and monitoring the results. A good engagement and communication between 
the stakeholders during the dialogue will lead to a better informed public, therefore a good 
example of key performance indicator. A better informed public will furthermore facilitate the 
way towards an increase in trust, awareness and transparency.  
In respect to the information campaign, the monitoring could refer to an actual team member 
of the campaign that could go in the public places where the banners have been posted and 
perform face to face questionnaires to the people in the surroundings.  In order to assess 
whether the campaign has been proved successful, some general questionnaires could be 
carried out online or through the phone and investigate if there was an improvement in terms 
of awareness towards nanotechnology and labeling of nanomaterials.

7. Conclusions  
 Even though more than fifteen years have passed since the first regulatory discussions on the 
subject of nanomaterials has started, the regulatory authorities are still struggling to find the 
ideal piece of legislation that would deal with the risks posed by this industry to society. The 
main objectives of this thesis were to: provide an overview of the existing legislation related to 
nanotechnology, highlight the challenges that regulatory authorities face when trying to 
regulate and label nanotechnology and nanomaterials, evaluate whether there were any 
lessons from the past labeling experiences that could apply for nanotechnology labeling and, 
not least, recommend the design of a labeling information and dialogue campaign.    
In order to meet the objectives three trace analysis have been performed. The first analysis has 
presented the current situation and the challenges in respect to nanotechnology regulations. To 
summarize, it is reasonable to affirm that European Union does not have a consistent approach 
when trying to regulate nanotechnology, since there are still uncertainties on whether to adopt 
a new regulatory framework or to adjust the existing legislation. However, a full scientific risk 
assessment on nanomaterials is still not available fact that is making the mission of policy 
makers even harder.  Moreover, there is a call for a clear definition for nanotechnology and 
nanomaterials that might help to exclude vagueness and ease the path for regulators.  
 
The second trace analysis related to the past labeling experiences (food labelling, GMO, 
ecolabelling) revealed important findings that might be proven successful in the case of 
nanotechnology and nanomaterials. The findings from the food labeling case and GMO showed 
that consumers are not able to interpret the labels, therefore only providing a label will not be 
enough. Moreover, a need for an improved legibility of the labels was identified due to the fact 
that the consumers are often finding the labels confusing. In the case of environmental labels, 
there were often cases when producers were using fake environmental labels to influence 
consumers but also more often to skip regulations. This is definitely a possibility in the case of 
labeling nanomaterials since there is already evidence that some producers are using the word 
“nano” in brackets only as a way to promote their products even if sometimes these statements 
==PAGE:79==
 
 
 
 
79 
 
are only claims.  Consumers rely greatly on the producers to understand better the role of the 
labels so, perhaps producers need to learn better how to disclose this type of information in 
order to comprise also the ability of less educated people to understand it. Policy makers play 
also a key role in this matter of fact. They could intervene more between the sellers and 
consumers and furthermore find a way to differentiate the fake claimed labels from the 
accurate ones. The reality is that consumers, producers and policymakers still have to learn 
more about a better way to communicate to each other and this can be done only in time. 
The third trace of analysis was based on the survey that was performed in order to get updated 
information in respect to the consumers’ attitude towards nanotechnology and labeling of 
nanomaterials. From the study it appears to be clear that in spite of the fact that people are in 
general interest for nanotechnology, their level of awareness in respect to this subject is still 
low. Another significant finding was that the internet is the primary source utilized by the 
general population to search for data in respect to nanotechnology and nanomaterials. When it 
comes particularly to the issue of labeling, the results reveled that the general population is 
sustaining labeling of nanoproducts and trust the government in related to the safety issues.  
Finally, all three trace analysis have been used in order to provide a recommendation on how a 
labeling information and dialogue campaign should be design.
