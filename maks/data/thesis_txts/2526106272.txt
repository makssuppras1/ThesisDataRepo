Abstract
A novel variational approach to assess environmentally assisted fatigue in
structural steels has been proposed. The model builds upon a phase field formu-
lation for fatigue fracture, (ii) a coupled mechanical-diffusion response, driven
by chemical potential gradients and (iii) a hydrogen-dependent surface energy
degradation law grounded on Density Functional Theory. The modelling frame-
work is implemented in the commercial finite element (FE) package Abaqus
using user-defined subroutines. The coupled deformation-diffusion-phase field
FE equations are solved in an implicit time integration scheme by means of a
residual control-based staggered algorithm. Several case studies are addressed to
investigate the performance of the numerical implementation. Results demon-
strate the capability of the model to appropriately capture the main trends
observed in laboratory experiments and hydrogen-sensitive engineering applica-
tions: (i) the sensitivity of the hydrogen diffusion to the loading frequency/test-
ing time, (ii) the deleterious effect of hydrogen embrittlement on crack growth
resistance and Paris law parameters and (iii) the significant reduction in the
fatigue life due to hydrogen.
The present work could have important implications for lifetime assessment
of engineering components undergoing environmentally assisted fatigue.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
State of the art
Fracture of materials subjected to fatigue loading is arguably the main failure mecha-
nism of engineering components such as wind turbines and offshore structures. Among
the most complicated aspects of fatigue fracture is the prediction of crack nucleation
and its subsequent propagation when components are exposed to periodic and arbi-
trary loads. Due to the complexity of fatigue phenomena, numerical methods are of
great utility and fatigue life analysis has been a prevalent research field for several
decades.
The terminology fatigue failure/limit refers to repeatedly applied macroscopic
loads or displacements at stress levels whose maximum values are significantly lower
than the monotonic/quasi-static strength of the material. In general, the fatigue pro-
cess of a component can be divided into two phases; (i) crack initiation life and (ii)
crack propagation life. In the initial phase, irreversible microscopic degradation phe-
nomena such as micro-voids (at micro-heterogeneities such as pits or imperfections)
and, subsequently, micro-cracks are formed in the material [1]. These initial phases
are random in nature and ruled by the stochastic micro-structural arrangement of the
material. When the application of loads continues, damage is accumulated in the ma-
terial and the micro-cracks eventually coalesce, leading to the formation of a fatigue
(macro-) crack which initially starts to propagate stably. Subsequently, the fatigue
degradation process gradually lowers the material stiffness until the occurrence of
unstable crack propagation which finally leads to complete failure.
Commonly, the fatigue resistance is quantified in terms of the number of cycles
to failure which can be provided by statistical methods, fracture mechanics-based
approaches or constitutive material models accounting for fatigue. The statistical
methods are based on data fitting of a large number of experimental tests which, in
fact, can only characterize particular situations and barely allow for generalization [2].
In this regard, the well-known WГ¶hler or SN curve is the most prevalent correlation
relationship that relates the constant applied stress/strain amplitude of a cyclic load-
ing to the maximum number of cycles which a component can undergo until complete
failure, see Fig 1.1a. Here, the SN curve is divided into three regions corresponding
to oligocyclic (OC), low-cycle (LC) and high-cycle (HC) fatigue. The OC region is

related to high stress levels for which the material is significantly damaged already
after the initial cycles. In the HC region, damage and fatigue processes compete while
in the HC region, the fatigue life is dominated by the stable crack growth phase. For
materials featuring an endurance limit, the HC region asymptotically approaches an
infinite life.
Generally, the fracture mechanics-based approaches adopt the well-known Paris
law proposed by Paris and Erdogan [3] wherein a power function is introduced that
relates the stress intensity factor range to the fatigue crack growth rate in a bi-
logarithmic scale by means of experimentally calibrated parameters, which are meant
to be material parameters, see Fig 1.1b. However, this relationship is only valid in the
so-called Paris regime, i.e. in the stable crack propagation region (II) and away from
the transient regions, i.e. the crack nucleation (I) and the crack unstable propagation
(III) right before failure.
On the other hand, numerical methods based on the constitutive material mod-
els, accounting for fatigue, are among the most prevalent approaches in the context
of computational fracture mechanics. Among these approaches is the so-called Cohe-
sive Zone Models (CZM) for which the damage evolution under fatigue loading can be
determined by means of representative traction-separation laws [4,5]. However, CZM-
based analyses require an explicit resolution of the near-tip plastic and strain fields,
at each load cycle, to determine the actual accumulation of the crack opening. In
addition, CZM is limited in predicting complex crack trajectories due to the fact that
the crack propagation path has to be known in advance. Among other approaches is
the so-called Extended Finite Element Method (XFEM) proposed first by the group
of Belytschko [6, 7] that utilises a local enrichment of the standard finite element
shape functions. This approach can be employed to numerically determine the stress
intensity factor range values to be used in the Paris law formulation [8вҖ“10]. As the
crack propagation path has to be well traced, it is rather cumbersome to deal with
complex cracking conditions such as initiation, propagation, merging and branching
of cracks, specifically for non-planar crack surfaces in general 3D configurations.
In contrast to these techniques, the so-called Continuum Damage Mechanics
(CDM) has been identified as an alternative development in solid mechanics for charac-
terising the fatigue phenomena [11,12]. CDM is established based on thermodynamic
micro-mechanical models that address the creation and growth of micro-structural de-
fects, such as micro-voids or micro-cracks, in terms of macroscopic state variables to
describe the material degradation, causing crack initiation and growth, under mono-
tonic or cyclic loading.
There are a few damage initiation/evolution models that
simulate fatigue crack growth in the case of low-cycle fatigue loading [13] and high-
cycle fatigue loading [14]. One of the major difficulties with CDM-based models lies
on the choice of an appropriate material constitutive law to describe plastic behavior
of the material as well as initiation/evolution of the damage.

On the other hand, the so-called phase-field method for fracture has recently
emerged as a promising variational approach to overcome the above-mentioned lim-
itations of discontinuity-based methods in addressing complex cracking conditions.
The model builds upon GriffithвҖҷs thermodynamics framework where crack growth re-
quires an increase in the surface energy [15]. The method has gained increasing atten-
tion since the earlier work by Francfort and Marigo [16] and significant efforts have
been devoted to improve solution schemes [17вҖ“19], discretization strategies [20, 21]
and implementation aspects [22, 23]. The phase field method has not only been ef-
fectively applied to model brittle-type fracture but also to ductile fracture [24, 25],
composites delamination [26,27], hydraulic fracturing, [28,29], stress corrosion crack-
ing [30,31] and hydrogen assisted cracking [32вҖ“34]. The phase field method has very
recently been extended to fatigue [35, 36]. However, most failures often occur due
to the combination of environmental effects and mechanical fatigue loading, and the
use of phase field method for modeling environmentally assisted fatigue has not been
addressed yet. One of the most relevant environmental effects is the role of what
is generally referred to as hydrogen embrittlement. Hydrogen has been known, for

pressure vessels, pipelines and offshore structures that come into contact with hydro-

informed models of hydrogen embrittlement, the so-called Hydrogen-Enhanced Deco-
hesion (HEDE) mechanisms have proven to capture the major experimental trends
depicted by high-strength alloys in hydrogen-rich environments [38]. According to
the HEDE mechanisms, hydrogen atoms accumulated at the fracture process zone de-
grade the ductility and the fracture resistance of the material by lowering its cohesive
strength [39,40].
Modeling contributions in the hydrogen embrittlement community have been
mostly based on discrete numerical methods. Crack nucleation thresholds and growth
rates have been computed in the context of dislocation-based methods [41,42], weakest-
link approaches [43,44] and cohesive zone models [45вҖ“49], which can readily incorpo-
rate the cohesive strength reduction with hydrogen coverage. In the work by Jiang
and Carter [50], a first principles-based traction-separation law has been proposed,
demonstrating that the surface energy decreasing drastically as a result of increasing
hydrogen concentration. MartГӯnez-PaГұeda et al. [33] have recently developed a novel
approach to model hydrogen assisted cracking of brittle materials based on variational
phase field method for quasi-static fracture. Their framework enables to capture com-
plex crack propagation arising from defects intrinsic to hydrogen embrittlement in
industrial components. In similar work by Kristensen et al. [34], the so-call Strain
Gradient Plasticity (SGP) approch is adopted to model hydrogen assisted cracking
of elastic-plastic solids based on variational phase field method for fracture. Their
model predictions reveal the critical role of plastic strain gradients in rationalising
decohesion-based arguments and in capturing the transition from micro-void cracking
to brittle fracture observed by ductile steels exposed to hydrogen containing environ-
ments.
Despite these relevant achievements, there are only a few works dealing with the
modeling of environmentally assisted fatigue. In this regard, Moriconi et al. [47] have
proposed a CZM-based framework for investigating the role of hydrogen embrittle-
ment on a 15-5PH martensitic stainless steel, which is often intended for gaseous
hydrogen storage. Their model predictions demonstrate a very good agreement with
experimental results in the case of low hydrogen pressures but failed to capture the
detrimental effect of hydrogen on the fatigue crack growth at high pressures. In simi-
lar work by del Busto et al. [49], a hydrogen- and cycle-dependent CZM formulation is
presented for modeling hydrogen assisted fatigue. Their framework enables to demon-
strate the sensitivity of fatigue crack growth rates to the loading frequency and the
environment, in agreement with the experimental trends. However, their modelling
predictions reveal the need to incorporate some additional sources of stress eleva-
tion, such as gradient-enhanced dislocation hardening, to quantitatively reproduce
the effect of yield strength, work hardening and constraint conditions as observed in
experiments. As these studies are restricted to discontinuity-based methods, there
is an opportunity to combine the benefits of the phase field modelling of hydrogen

1.2
Objectives
Modeling environmentally assisted fatigue and developing robust predictive computa-
tional tools for fatigue life assessment is of great interest for engineering applications.
The aim of this Master Thesis is to contribute to the understanding, modeling and
design of advanced and sustainable engineering materials by identifying failure pro-
cesses in environmentally assisted fatigue using new numerical techniques. Hence,
the two main objectives of this work are,
(i) Implement the variational phase field framework of fatigue fracture in the context
of Finite Element Method (FEM). Study its robustness and its capability to accu-
rately capture the main features of fatigue phenomena; and,
(ii) Investigate fatigue life in aggressive environments by incorporating the embrittle-
ment effect of hydrogen in the aforementioned phase field formulation.
1.3
Outline of the dissertation
The content of this work is organized as follows. First (Chapter 1), the topic is intro-
duced and the objectives of the study outlined. Chapter 2 is devoted to theoretical
framework of the phase field model of fatigue fracture. Then, in Chapter 3, the effect
of hydrogen embrittlement is incorporated. Chapter 4 outlines the numerical scheme
and its implementation in the commercial finite element package Abaqus. In Chap-
ter 5, the performance and the robustness of the proposed numerical algorithms is
benchmarked through several representative numerical examples. Finally, concluding
remarks are given in Chapter 6.

2.1
Introduction
The variational phase field method for fracture has recently become a prevalent
methodology to study complex cracking conditions such as initiation, arbitrarily com-
plicated crack patterns, branching and merging of cracks. The model builds upon
the pioneering thermodynamics framework established by Griffith [15] where crack
growth will occur if a critical energy release rate of the material is reached. First,
Frankfort and Marigo [16] embedded GriffithвҖҷs framework into variational formula-
tions by including the surface energy dissipated by the formation of a crack in the
total energy potential of the material. Additional improvements were introduced by
Bourdin et al. [51, 52], inspired by the image segmentation work of Mumford and
Shah [53]. In [51, 52], a discrete crack surface is regularized into a solid body by
means of diffusive crack regions defined by a scalar damage variable, later termed as
crack phase field variable, which varies between the intact and fully broken states of
the material. In similar work by Hakim and Karma [54], a Ginzburg-Landau-type
evolution equation is adopted to formulate a phase field model for brittle fracture.
Kuhn and MГјller [55] developed a new finite element-based model using exponential
shape functions as an alternative to the standard Lagrange elements to assess the
exponential shape functions performance in cases where the crack growth path aligns
with the element edges.
Important contributions to the model have also been made by Miehe et al. [17,18]
wherein the thermodynamically consistent framework ensures the local irreversibility
of crack evolution which enables appropriate simulations of the material response
for both loading and unloading conditions. In [17], the coupled deformation-phase
field problem consisting of the macroscopic equilibrium and the evolution of crack
phase field are solved simultaneously by means of a fully coupled monolithic scheme.
Accordingly, the local irreversibility constraint of crack evolution is approximately
enforced by adding an artificial penalty term to the governing balance equation of
crack phase field. Alternatively, the coupled deformation-phase field problem can be

solved separately using sequentially coupled staggered scheme [18] wherein the local
irreversibility constraint of crack evolution is ensured via introducing a history vari-
able field. Due to its robustness, the proposed staggered solution scheme has become
very popular.
Nevertheless, numerical solutions obtained using the staggered scheme are not
unconditionally stable, and thus, sufficiently small time/loading increments must be
ensured, via time increment sensitivity analyses, to achieve an accurate equilibrium
solution. This issue has been addressed in a study by SeleЕЎ et al. [56] wherein a stop-
ping criterion of the iterative staggered scheme, based on the control of the residual
norm, is introduced. In similar work by Kristensen and MartГӯnez-PaГұeda [57], the
so-called Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno (BFGS) algorithm, presented in [58], is
adopted along with a new adaptive time stepping algorithm in order to model a quasi-
Newton monolithic solution scheme for phase field fracture.
Carrara et al. [36] have very recently proposed a novel approach to model fatigue
behavior of brittle materials based on variational phase field method for fracture.
Their framework enables to reproduce the main features of the fatigue behavior in-
cluding the crack nucleation, stable and unstable crack growth phases. The first 1D
implementation was conducted by Alessi et al. [35] and the existence of quasi-static
evolutions with a vanishing viscosity approach investigated in [59]. As reported in [35],
the fracture surface energy term of the monotonic case is modified, by introducing a
cumulated history variable and a fatigue degradation function, to account for fatigue
effects.
In this work, a novel variational framework is outlined for investigating the fatigue
effect on fracturing solids. The model builds upon the energetic principles of rate-
independent systems in the form of an energy balance and a dissipation inequality.
Isothermal conditions, negligible inertial effects, small strains and smooth loading
in time are assumed.

under quasi-static monotonic loading. Then, in Section 2.3, the effect of fatigue/cyclic
loading is incorporated.

3.1
Introduction
Hydrogen is broadly seen as a key enabler for development of the low-cost energy sys-
tems. It can be produced from a large variety of renewable energy resources, acting
as a substantial energy potential, providing low or zero emission energy to a large
number of energy consuming applications, such as heating, energy storage, power gen-
eration or even as a transport fuel. Despite these opportunities, hydrogen has been
known, for over a hundred years, to severely degrade the mechanical properties of
metallic alloys, restricting the application of modern steels in current energy sectors.
Catastrophic failure due to what is generally referred to as hydrogen embrittlement
has been constantly reported for a large number of industrial applications exposed
to gaseous hydrogen and aqueous solutions, such as pipelines, pressure vessels and
offshore structures. Thus, there is a strong need to understand the mechanisms be-
hind hydrogen embrittlement and to develop mechanistic-based models enabling to
characterise the microstructure-dependent mechanical response at scales applicable
to engineering practice.
Two main atomistic mechanisms of hydrogen embrittlement have been proposed;
Hydrogen Enhanced Decohesion (HEDE) and Hydrogen Enhanced Localised Plas-
ticity (HELP). According to the HEDE mechanism, hydrogen atoms accumulated
at the fracture process zone reduce the fracture resistance by lowering the mate-
rial cohesive strength [39, 40]. According to the HELP mechanism, hydrogen atoms
distributed around the dislocations soften the material by reducing the dislocations
Peierls stress 1 [63]. The HEDE is widely known as the dominant mechanism in high-
strength steels, enabling to demonstrate good agreement with the main experimental
trends in hydrogen-rich environments [38]. However, most cohesive zone models for
environmentally assisted fatigue have been largely restricted to discontinuity-based
methods [47, 49] and the application of phase field method for modeling hydrogen

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions and
future work
A phase field formulation for the long-standing problem of environmentally assisted
fatigue has been presented. A coupled mechanical-diffusion-phase field scheme has
been developed in the context of finite element method using Abaqus user-defined
subroutines. First, the phase field modelling of fatigue fracture in brittle materials is
outlined. The standard regularized free energy functional is modified by introducing
a fatigue degradation function that effectively governs the reduction of the fracture
toughness as a function of the fatigue history experienced by the material. The stored
bulk energy density is decomposed into active and inactive parts to avoid cracking in
compression. A stopping criterion based on the control of the residual norm is intro-
duced into the common staggered scheme such that the accuracy of the equilibrium
solution is no longer dependent on the careful selection of the size of time/loading
increments.
The effect of hydrogen embrittlement is subsequently incorporated by relating the
critical energy release rate of the material to the hydrogen content based on first prin-
ciples calculations. A penalty-based chemical boundary condition is also implemented
to enable the environment to immediately occupy the space created with crack ad-
vance. Several case studies have been addressed to investigate the performance of the
proposed modelling framework. First, the cracked square plate in an inert environ-
ment is modeled to verify the numerical implementation. Then, the crack tip fields
in the boundary layer problem are computed for a range of environmental and me-
chanical loading conditions. The numerical results demonstrate the capability of the
model to appropriately capture the main experimental observations: (i) the sensitiv-
ity of the hydrogen concentration to the loading frequency/testing time, and (ii) the
detrimental effect of hydrogen on crack growth resistance and Paris law parameters.
Finally, the SN curves of the notched cylindrical bar reveal a significant reduction
in the fatigue life due to the hydrogen embrittlement. However, simulating cycle-by-
cycle fatigue crack growth requires a very high computational effort, especially, in the
case of high-cycle fatigue loading. Hence, the future work will focus on developing an
efficient jump-in-cycles procedure to reduce the computational cost.
