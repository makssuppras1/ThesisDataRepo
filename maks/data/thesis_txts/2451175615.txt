Abstract
Since the 1960s the single crystal form Lithium Niobate was synthesized and characterized at
Bell Laboratories, Lithium Niobate has been widely used nowadays in various applications
by using its electric-optic, acoustic-optic, pyroelectric effect. The conventional bulk mate-
rial can’t meet the requirements of green sustainability, energy-saving and highly integrated.
However, for on-chip device fabrication, the challenge of this material is its highly etching
resistance. In this project, on-chip nano waveguides based on Lithium Niobate on insulator
was researched, which elaborated on the etching reactors, the optimized etching recipe and
processing for obtaining Lithium Niobate nano waveguides with smooth sidewall and low
loss.
Firstly, the Electron Beam Lithography pattern design by using Clewin and Matlab was
discussed. An optimized scheme with better pattern distribution, easy cutting and multi-
functional pattern design was given. The theoretic section based on bond dissociation en-
ergy and sputtering yields analyzed the IBE etching recipe, including the ion energy and gas
ﬂow, ﬁnalized the optimized scheme for further etching recipe development.
Secondly, the processing ﬂow of nano-structure based on Lithium Niobate on insula-
tor(LNOI) was developed. Instead of using multi-steps of resist and metallic mask as a hard
mask, HSQ was used directly as a hard mask, which simpliﬁed the processing. The effect
of HSQ mask layer thickness on the hare ears was demonstrated, which shows the obvious
reduction of hare ear as optimizing the ratio of resist thickness and the designed Lithium
Niobate etching depth. Besides, the line width reduction and sidewall angle of HSQ under
different E beam current and E beam aperture was discussed in this project.
Thirdly, the effect of IBE angle on etch rate and etch proﬁle was given in this project,
which elaborated on sidewall redeposition and hare ear in SEM imaging with the 15° and
10° IBE etching respectively. The possible solutions for removing the hare ears and reduc-
ing sidewall redeposition were discussed, which showed the details in SEM imaging after
those possible solutions, including ultrasonic treatment, plasma asher and thermal expan-
sion. Besides, the measurement accuracy was also discussed in this project, including the
SEM imaging, AFM and Dektak measurement, which discussed the difference of the mea-
surement tools and the reality measurement difference by those tools.
Finally, the fabricated nano waveguide with different width of 700nm, 800nm, 900nm,
1000nm were characterized using Fabry–Pérot method, which gave the relations of propaga-
tion loss, facet reﬂection, mode mismatch loss and the amplitude of the fringes. The optimal
propagation loss was obtained with 1.82dB/cm for 1000nm width waveguide with 8dB/facet
mode mismatch loss.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Optical ﬁber communication is widely used nowadays in our daily life that the data send-
ing or receiving by our laptop or telephone was ﬁnally transmitted in the form of the optical
signal. For an optical ﬁber communication system, Optical signal modulator is a key device
for keeping the ﬁdelity of the optical signal. Lithium Niobate is one of the most widely used
electro-optic materials with high linear electro-optic coefﬁcient in 34pm/V [1]. The con-
ventional Lithium Niobate electro-optic modulator was using bulk Lithium Niobate, which
can’t meet the requirements of highly integrated, energy-saving and green sustainability.
Therefore, the research of on chip device based on Lithium Niobate became the focus point.
The large bandgap of 4.2eV [2], superior nonlinear effects and broad transparency range
350nm∼5200nm of Lithium niobate made it widely researched for the potential application
in optical integration system. Besides, Lithium Niobate is also widely used in various ap-
plications for its good performance in terms of piezoelectric [3], electro-optic and acoustic-
optic [1].
The challenge of on chip device based on Lithium Niobate is its inert to any types of
chemicals. The earliest method was using diffusion and proton exchange for Lithium Nio-
bate to get refractive index contrast [4]. Out diffusion of Lithium oxide can realize ridge
waveguide with cross section of h×w = 7.5µm×19µm in low loss of 0.2dB/cm [5]. In diffu-
sion of metals, such as Mg, Ni, Zn, Fe, Co, Cr and Ti was used. The maximum refractive index
contrast ∆ne is less than 0.04 by Titanium diffusion [4]. Ion exchange of silver/Lithium [6]
and thallium/lithium [7] also has the limitation on refractive index contrast of only 0.13. The
same for proton exchange with only 0.12 refractive index contrast [8] can be realized. The low
refractive index contrast limited the optical conﬁnement thereby with limitation of optical
integration. Besides, the wet etching processing of Lithium Niobate using toxic chemicals at
high temperature and long time make this process unsustainable.
Etching directly of Lithium Niobate to get high refractive index contrast was researched
within decades. Hydroﬂuoric acid mixed with nitric acid with Ethanol smoothing, the pro-
cess can get low loss of 0.4dB/cm at 1550nm TE mode [9]. However, this method is limited
by the etch rate of 0.67µm/h with the structure scale in h×w = 8µm×5µm. The other com-
bining etching method has also been researched. For example, using laser micromachining
to deﬁne the pattern and removing the transformed Lithium Niobate by chemical mechani-
cal machine afterwards. This method also attained low loss with 0.027dB/cm, however, the
fabricated structure is in micrometre scale [10].

1.1. BACKGROUND
Comparing with wet etching, dry etching can get controllable etch proﬁle and etch rate.
The physical mechanism of dry etching process makes an anisotropic proﬁles and the chem-
ical react of dry etching process will make an isotropic proﬁle. Therefore, the combination
of the physical and chemical process can make a controllable structure. The etch rate of the
dry etching process is determined by the reactor type, chemistry, power level and substrate
temperature [11].
RIE is the commonly used tool for etching Lithium Niobate. The maximum etching rate
was reported with 187.5nm/min for z cut Lithium Niobate by 2000W ICP power and 120W
RIE power [12]. However, it didn’t show low loss with details of the fabricated structure. The
latest report of x cut Lithium Niobate can get 3dB/cm with cross-section 400nm×900nm by
ICP [15], which shows the low loss in nanoscale Lithium Niobate waveguide with acceptable
etch rate in 30nm/min. The optimized ICP etching with pure argon can get the lowest loss
of 3dB/m with cross-section h×w = 350nm×1200nm [16].
Mask material is critical for obtaining low loss structure. Ni [17], Cr [18], Ag [13] and NrCr
alloy [12] were used as hard mask with pattern deﬁned by liftoff technique. Those mask ma-
terial didn’t receive great attention because of the roughness sidewall or undeﬁned loss of
fabricated Lithium Niobate structure. In 2017, a novel method of HSQ as a hard mask was
researched with the result low loss of 3dB/m [16], that largely push the structured Lithium
Niobate technique forward.
Table 1.1 shows the development of etching Lithium Niobate structures in 2007∼2019.
The size of waveguide goes from micrometre to nanometer with optimized propagation loss
down to 3dB/m. The etching methods of Lithium Niobate changes from the earlier wet etch-
ing [9] to the later dry etching by RIE.
Figure 1.1 shows the fabricated Lithium Niobate structure by ICP. Figure 1.1a was using
C4F6 and He gas ICP [18] for fabricating waveguide with cross section of 5.8µm×8µm, that
the surface roughness is obvious. The etch rate and propagation loss was unknown for this
fabrication. Figure 1.1b was using SF6 and Ar gas ICP RIE with Gas cluster Ion beam for
smoothing the sidewall [13]. The etch rate was 57∼79nm/min, but the propagation loss
was unknown. Figure 1.1c and 1.1d was using pure argon plasma ICP RIE, which resulted in
smooth sidewall and low loss of minimum 3dB/m nano waveguide [16] [15].

calculation
This chapter gives the overview of processing in this project and the details of pattern design
by Clewin. The second section introduces the Lithium Niobate crystalline structure with the
discussion of etching recipe, which is based on the analysis of dissociation bond energies.
The theoretical calculation gives the analysis of argon gas volume for etching and the etch-
ing time for 300nm×10mm×10mm Lithium Niobate.
2.1
Processing Overview
The wafer in using is Lithium Niobate on insulator(LNOI). Figure 2.1 shows the structure of
LNOI. 600nm Lithium Niobate was bonding on top of 4.7µm silicon oxide with 500µm silicon
substrate. Lithium Niobate on insulator can be made by smart cut [20].
Figure 2.1: Lithium niobate on silicon oxide with silicon substrate. 600nm Lithium niobate,
4.7µm Silicon oxide and 500µm silicon substrate.
The type of hard mask and the dry etching recipe are the critical points in this project. It
was ﬁnally decided by using HSQ as a hard mask. The reason is that HSQ, which is mainly
composed of silicon and oxygen with the property similar to glass, can realize high resolution
within 10nm [21], make it possible to be used as a hard mask directly. Besides, E beam ex-
posure is well suited for processing development because it deﬁnes pattern directly without
additional deﬁned masking plate. The pure physical etching was chosen because Lithium
niobate is an inert material. Even though Niobium can react with ﬂuoride, the byproduct
LiF is also an issue to get rid of because of its high melting point of 845°C. Therefore, the
trade-off was made with the ending of pure argon ion beam etching.
Processing overview can be seen in ﬁgure 2.2. Details of the processing will be further dis-
cussed in chapter 4.

2.2
Pattern design
The software for drawing pattern is CLEWIN 5.0, offered by Nanolab of DTU. It is compati-
ble with scripts tools, such as Programming language C, Matlab, MaskEngineer Scripts, Lua
Scripts and Parameters, which make it easy to use for drawing large and complicated pat-
terns. In this Project, Clewin combining with Matlab was used for pattern design.
E beam exposure time should be taken into consideration that it depends on the de-
signed pattern and E beam parameters setting. Version-I pattern, which can be seen in ﬁg-
ure 2.3, was designed in the area of 20mm×14mm. The pattern was separated in four parts
at the four-quadrant with marks distributed at the edges for easy cutting. However, the large
and dense designed pattern takes 45 hours E beam exposure time by 6nA. After simplifying
the pattern and increasing the E beam exposure current and aperture, the E beam exposure
time was controlled within four hours.
Version-II is an optimized pattern with the pattern designed in three parallel sections for
easy cutting. The pattern area is 4mm×21mm. Based on the experience of Version I, that
precision of cutting by diamond scribe can’t be sure. Therefore, the version II pattern has the
waveguides connecting with each other. These waveguides are in mirror symmetry along
the designed cutting line. This design has tolerable cutting precision, which only needs to be
controlled within 3mm. Furthermore, wherever the cutting point could be, both of the two
sides waveguide facets will be exposed, that prevent the case that the edge of the waveguide
is far away from the edge of the sample, which blocked the coupling by lensed ﬁber. The
connected waveguide also can be used as marks for SEM imaging of cross-section.

2.3.1
Lithium Niobate chemical bonds and bond dissociation energies
X cut Lithium Niobate has a succession of −O9−Li6Nb6−O9−atomic layer with the surface
termination depend on the surface underlying relaxation [22]. The etching process would
break the chemical covalent bond of Li-O and Nb-O.
The calculated atomic model of LiNbO3 shows in ﬁgure 2.4. According to the calculated
model that for x cut LiNbO3 with terminated −Li, the surface is covering with lithium layer,
which further blocks the ﬂuoride gas dry etching because of non-volatile LiF byproducts.
Therefore, it further helps in deciding to not use ﬂuoride gas in dry etching of Lithium Nio-
bate.
The effect of different atomic distribution on pure argon IBE process hasn’t been re-
searched. Some researchers have veriﬁed the anisotropic wet etching rate [23] of Lithium
Niobate.

2.3. THEORY CALCULATION
The above calculation is based on the assumption that all of the argon atoms can be
ionized with sputtering yields of 1 atom/ion. However, in reality, the etch rate is limited by a
variety of different factors:
• Not all of the argon atoms are being ionized.
• The clustered argon ions make it work less efﬁciently.
• Argon ions will react with the silicon carrier, but not the target LN.
• Byproducts from physical etching process would redeposit on the surface, which will
consume the argon radials and ions.
From the above calculation, it can be seen that 10 Sccm Argon ﬂow rate is enough for
etching 300nm×10mm×10mm Lithium Niobate and 600eV is an acceptable voltage for the
sputtering yields of Argon [34].
Based on the above analysis, 10 Sccm argon ﬂow rate and 600eV ion energy were setting
as the Version-I IBE dry etching recipe. In the future, the argon ﬂow rate can be decreased
with lower IBE chamber pressure to help the etching byproducts get out of the narrow open-
ings, which would reduce redeposition.

3.3.1
Possible solutions for sidewall redeposition and hare ear
Solution-01 Thinner HSQ resist layer/ Over etching
The height of the hare ear depends on the top residue resist height. As the etching pro-
cess goes, the HSQ resist getting thinner at the same time as exposed lithium niobate being
etched away. The redeposition, which results from byproducts reﬂected to the sidewall, will
be deposited gradually from top to bottom. The top redeposition can be etched together
with HSQ simultaneously. As etching stop, etching of redeposition will also be stopped at
the same level as HSQ. Therefore, as striping HSQ resist, the redeposition on the sidewall
will be left because of unsolvable Lithium and niobium byproducts.
To get rid of hare ear in pure physical etching, a proper resist thickness is critical. In this
project, 220nm HSQ was left as etching stopped at 300nm Lithium Niobate etching depth.
The 220nm left HSQ left obvious tall hare ears as the ﬁgures show before. Therefore, the pro-
cessing needs to be optimized. One way is to spin thinner HSQ resist. Another way is to over
etch a little bit to see the height of hare ear.
In this project, over-etching by 30mins IBE at 15° was used to see its effect on hare ear.
Figure 3.11 shows the Lithium Niobate structures after striping the resist. Comparing with
the hare ear of 20mins 15° IBE in ﬁgure 3.8, ﬁgure 3.11 shows comparably lower hare ear.
Table 3.4 shows the comparison between the 20mins and 30mins 15° IBE etching. After
20mins IBE etching, 210nm HSQ was left, whereas, for 30mins, only little HSQ was left with

the size of several hundred micrometers in densely distributed all over the sample surface.

Characterization tools comparison
In this project, Dektak, SEM and AFM were used for characterizing the etch depth of IBE.
Dektak is a fast measurement tool for characterizing step height. However, the stylus will

the top layer is unknown because there are possible redeposition covering on the surface.
Besides, the hare ears as shown before in SEM imaging is erected thin layer that could be
pressed down/transformed by the scanning tip.
A normal cross-section of the structure needs to be obtained for characterization of the
etch depth by SEM. That is possible for a large sample. However, for small samples, such
as 1cm×1cm sample, it is difﬁcult to get the normal cross-section cutting using a diamond
pen. Therefore, laser micromachining needs to be used for precision cutting. In this case,
the sample needs to be coated in case of particles contamination from laser micromachin-
ing. Besides, according to the cleanroom rule, that any sample that has been outside of the
cleanroom should be cleaned thoroughly by ultrasonic followed by Piranha cleaning. There-
fore, laser micromachining cutting, which is located outside of the cleanroom, could be an
issue for the patterned layer with so many cleaning steps. The advantages of SEM imaging is

4.2
Theoretical calculation
Propagation loss is an indicator to evaluate the etching process of a waveguide, because the
scattering results from roughness sidewall would cause high loss. Many factors contribute
to the propagation loss, including material absorption from defects or impurity, scattering
from roughness sidewall and optical conﬁnement. All of those factors will be counted into
propagation loss in this project.
Propagation loss can be calculated by the Fabry–Pérot method and cut back method [69].
Cut back method is useful as characterizing long optical path propagation loss because the
propagation loss won’t be submerged in the mode mismatch loss or facet reﬂection loss.
However, for short waveguide, especially less than 10mm length waveguide, Fabry–Pérot
method is more reﬁning characterization by separating the mode mismatch loss and facet
reﬂection loss. The facet reﬂection loss can be read by the amplitude of interfered fringes.
The reﬂection light from facets would interfere with the forward light wave that makes the
output power in fringes [74]. The distributed backscattering results from material and rough-
ness sidewall make the output power in ﬂuctuation [75] [76] [77].

4.4
Troubleshooting
• Cleaving
The facets of waveguide need to be cleaved for characterization. Redeposition in dry
etching process will cover the input and output endings of the waveguide that increases
facet reﬂection loss. The cleaving was found to be hard because there is only 2mm length
left for cleaving. Using cleave pen and manual force won’t work for a small sample with only
5mm×8mm because of the law of the lever that the higher and more even force is needed
to break the smaller sample along crystal orientation. Therefore, laser micromachining was
ﬁnally used as the cleaving tool.
For protecting the sample in the laser cutting process, a layer of photoresist AZ5214
was coated on the sample by speed 2000r pm and accelerator 1000r pm/s. The sample was
treated with post-baking at 90°C for 2mins.
The sample was cut through with the cutting line set to be normal to waveguide. The
start point of the cutting is close to waveguide. After laser cutting, the manual force was
used to break the sample. It won’t take high manual force for breaking because the laser has
cut down almost 80%. However, the shifting of laser micromachining hasn’t been counted
precisely that the starting point shifted to the waveguide area, which can be seen in ﬁgure
4.2b. The ending of several waveguides was damaged by the laser. The second time cutting
was done at right bottom side around 500µm away from the ﬁrst starting point, which is
away from the straight waveguide. Thereby, the sample was broken by manual force along
this cutting line.

The coupling between lensed ﬁber and waveguide depends on the mode-matching and

ﬁber. After that, do the following characterization.
The facets of the waveguide, as can be seen in ﬁgure 4.2 that it can’t be sure in normal
with the waveguide. That cause the coupling angle which is away from the center point of
the waveguide. This issue can be solved by optimizing the coupling in XYZ direction repeat-
ably until it gets to the highest coupling efﬁciency.
The closer of ﬁber tip and waveguide, the smaller tolerance for deviation along XYZ.
Therefore, a little bit far away position could be a good starting point for coupling.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this project, the effect of different IBE etching angle of 15° and 10° was given. The high-
resolution SEM imaging shows us that the 15° IBE etching got a thinner redeposition layer
on the sidewall as compared with 10° IBE etching. The etch rate and selectivity of HSQ/LN
didn’t change much for the two different etching angle. For the etch proﬁle, the 10° etching
shows more vertical sidewall than the 15° etching.
Variety of possible solutions for removing the hare ear and sidewall redeposition were
discussed. A proper ratio of HSQ thickness and designed Lithium Niobate etching depth
can reduce the hare ears. The experiment results in SEM imaging shows that comparing
with 20mins IBE etching, over-etching with 30mins can effectively reduce the hare ear. For
the other solutions for removing the sidewall redeposition, the thermal expansion method
results with wave shape roughness sidewall in some speciﬁc gap area, the potential reason
could be the radiation heat transfer in the gap area. Plasma asher can’t remove the redeposi-
tion, but the ashing signs at the surface of the redeposition layer and the part of redeposition
that fell off shows the potential for removing the redeposition layer.
Line width reduction and sidewall angle of the HSQ layer is another focus point in E beam
exposure and development process. The two version exposure got a different sidewall angle
and linewidth reduction. It shows that low current and small aperture E beam exposure got
comparably more broaden gap and inclined sidewall.
The bubbles issue as etching HSQ/Silicon introduced another huge researching area of
the material surface property and HSQ transforming under E beam. The 1cm×1cm×660nm
HSQ degassing(without E beam) was calculated to release maximum 0.277cm3 gas, which
is enough for forming 2.73 × 105 round bubbles with 700µm diameter and 660nm depth.
However, the following etching of HSQ/LN(without E beam) shows no signs of forming large
volume bubbles except some small bubbles. There is no conclusion can be given here. But,
the degassing of HSQ, HSQ chemical property changes as in long time reservation, material
surface property and the cross-linking effect on the degassing of HSQ could be interesting
points for the further research.
The fabricated Lithium Niobate nanowaveguides with 2.5mm length, 290nm height and
width of 700nm, 800nm, 900nm, 1000nm were characterized, which result with ∼16dB
insertion loss. The insertion loss includes mode mismatch loss between lensed ﬁber and
waveguide, facet reﬂection loss and propagation loss. The propagation loss was calculated to
be 1.82dB/cm by Fabry-Perot method with 8dB/Facet mode mismatching loss and 0.56dB/facet
reﬂection loss for 1000nm width waveguide.

Further work
As doing this master project, it was found how plentiful of this topic. There were tons of
issues as researching, but thanks for those tons of issues that more new ﬁndings could be
obtained in the future.
Few tasks that could be done in the future as follows:
1. Redeposition components
Redeposition on the sidewall can be deﬁned by XPS(X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy) or
EDX(Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy) to see the composition.
2. Propagation loss reﬁning measurement.
In this project, the mode mismatch coupling loss 8dB/facet is an empirical data, which
would affect the accuracy of propagation loss measurement.
3. The relation of effective index and dispersion.
The relation of effective index and dispersion is not obvious in this project, that could be
optimized by designed long waveguide with a different cross-section.
4. E beam exposure and development process optimization.
As can be seen that E beam exposure and development process has an effect on the HSQ
resist line width and sidewall, thereby effect on the fabricated structure. The optimized HSQ
layer should be obtained combining with an optimized etching process to get a sharp etch
proﬁle with optimized linewidth reduction.
5. Precise selective ratio to get structures without hare ear.
Precise measurement of etch rate and depth for optimizing the ratio of resist and etching
depth, so that the resist would be etched all away as the etching process stops. That makes
the hare ear in minimal.
6. Quantity deﬁnition of sidewall smoothness.
The sidewall smoothness, even though can be seen in SEM imaging, it still hasn’t any quan-
titatively deﬁned method, which could be optimized by AFM for measuring the roughness
of sidewall.
7. The relation of sidewall smoothness and propagation loss.
As the sidewall roughness can be quantitatively deﬁned, the relation of sidewall smoothness
and propagation loss could be deﬁned.

8. Lower IBE chamber pressure and decrease Argon ﬂow rate.
As discussed before, that lower IBE chamber pressure and decrease Ar gas ﬂow would help

9. Where the etching byproducts go?
That’s a question. If the byproducts are nonvolatile, where it goes? Is it sticking on the sam-
