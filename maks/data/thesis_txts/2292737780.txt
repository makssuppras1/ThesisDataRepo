Abstract 
The arctic areas, including Greenland, experience rapid environmental alterations due to ongoing 
climate changes. These changes are expected to have consequences in all biotic ecosystems, 
including parasite-host-systems. As a top predator in a relatively simple and well-investigated 
predator-prey system, the arctic fox (Vulpes lagopus) can be useful for the monitoring of such 
consequences. 
 
In the general introduction of this thesis the detailed background for the study is described including 
the general biology of arctic foxes and a summary of previous research on parasites recovered from 
arctic foxes in the whole north arctic region. In addition, methods for recovery of parasite stages 
from faecal samples and preservation of faecal samples are summarised. In the last part of the 
introduction aims and delimitations of the project are described.  
 
This veterinary master project included a retrospective coprological study conducted to recover and 
detect endoparasitic helminths in arctic foxes from two localities in Northeastern Greenland, 
namely Zackenberg Valley and Karupelv Valley. A detailed description of the localities covered the 
most important biotic and abiotic features of Zackenberg Valley and Karupely Valley. The samples 
(N=298) were processed using a modified sieving method and identification of the recovered eggs 
was done using inverted microscopy.  
 
Parasite species detected in the present study comprised taeniid eggs and several nematode eggs, 
including Toxascaris leonina, other ascarididae, Eucoleus boehmi, Physaloptera spp., hookworm 
and larvae of Strongyloides stercoralis. The study demonstrated that old faecal samples stored in 
70% ethanol at -20°C may be used for isolation of nematode and cestode eggs using a modified 
sieving method.  
 
Zackenberg Valley  and Karupelv Valley were relatively similar regarding to occurrence and 
abundance of parasites in faeces, and  differences in prevalences between  years and fox dens were 
primarily driven by one parasite species, Toxocara leonina, which can be transmitted directly 
between foxes or through the intermediate host, the collared lemming (Dicrostonyx groenlandicus). 
 
==PAGE:4==

This study may serve as baseline for future research on changes in parasite fauna correlated to 
predicted climate changes. The results of this study confirmed the presence of parasites that 
potentially can harm the arctic foxes but also parasites of zoonotic importance. Thus, there is risk of 
transmission of parasites to other carnivores and also to scientists using research stations in these 
localities. Further conclusions and perspectives are given in the final part of the thesis. Extra data 
are given in an appendix in a separate CD.

PART I: Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Consequences of climate changes have in past years received much attention, especially in arctic 
areas including Greenland (e.g. Klein et al. 2008; Stendel et al. 2008; Gilg et al. 2009; Davidson et 
al. 2011; Gilg et al. 2012). The climate changes in Greenland comprise among other higher 
temperatures, changes in season duration, reduction of sea ice near the coast of Greenland, and 
more precipitation in terms of rain and snow (Klein et al. 2008; Stendel et al. 2008). These changes 
will have an impact on the reproduction, activity and growth of the biotic communities residing 
there (Klein et al. 2008) and therefore it is necessary to monitor ecosystems to get a better 
understanding of the impact of climate changes (Meltofte et al. 2008). Since viability and health of 
wildlife populations can be influenced by parasitic infections, this can ultimately lead to negative 
outcomes of ecosystems (Davidson et al. 2011) and the parasite-host systems should also be 
addressed. 
 
Researches on the relatively simple predator-prey relationship between the only rodent species in 
Greenland, the collared lemming (Dicrostonyx groenlandicus) and its predators: the arctic fox 
(Vulpes lagopus or Alopex lagopus), the stoat (Mustela erminea), the longtailed skua (Stercorarius 
longicaudus) and the snowy owl (Bubo scandiacus, former Nyctea scandiaca) have been conducted 
in Zackenberg Valley (ZV) since 1995 (Meltofte & Rasch 2008; Schmidt et al. 2008) and 250 km 
south of ZV in Karupelv Valley (KV) since 1988 (Sittler 1995; Gilg et al. 2009). 
 
The study areas ZV and KV are relatively anthropogenically undisturbed as they are part of the 
National Park of North and East Greenland (Meltofte & Rasch 2008). Between these two areas, 
large differences in relative predator densities exists, and the lemming population displays cyclic 
fluctuations with abundance every fourth-fifth year, as well as seasonal fluctuations with more 
lemmings in winter than summer, because of heavy predation pressure during the summer months 
(Schmidt et al. 2008) and lemming reproduction in the winter (Sittler 1995).  
Abnormal lemming population dynamics due to climate changes have already been seen in 
Northeastern Greenland that indirectly is expected to decrease population densities of the predators, 
possibly to the local extinction (Gilg et al. 2009). 
 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
 
 
The arctic fox is among the climate change flagship species picked by the International Union for 
Conservation of Nature (IUCN) to represent consequences of climate changes on the tundra 
ecosystem, because it is one of the top terrestrial predators in the arctic (IUCN 2009) and thus 
investigations on this species could be used to monitor ecosystems. 
Previous studies have investigated endoparasites in arctic foxes from arctic areas including 
Greenland (Rausch et al. 1983; Kapel & Nansen 1996), but not in ZV and KV.  
Age-related differences in occurrence and/or abundance of gastrointestinal helminths in arctic foxes 
have been described (Rausch et al. 1990; Kapel et al. 1996; Kapel & Nansen 1996; Stien et al. 
2010), which makes it interesting if a similar tendency exists in North East Greenland. 
Baseline knowledge of parasite species in a top predator can provide an understanding of parasite-
host-relationships in Northeastern Greenland, and therefore contribute to the research of climate 
changes and population dynamics. 
 
Another aspect is the health of the stationed scientists. A scientific leader has reported that curious 
arctic foxes in the area of ZV sometimes come near the camp and defecate on the belongings of the 
scientists (Niels Martin Schmidt, AU, personal communication, October 3, 2014) and therefore a 
risk of transmission of zoonotic infections may exist. 
 
1.2 Problem definition 
The arctic fox is one of the top predators in a relatively simple predator-prey relationship in 
Northeastern Greenland, and climate changes are predicted to alter the population dynamics there. 
The climate changes are also expected to influence the parasite-host systems and absence of 
baseline knowledge about parasite species in arctic foxes from Northeastern Greenland makes it 
difficult to evaluate any consequences of the on-going climate changes. 
 
The overall objective of this master’s thesis was to study the presence of endoparasitic helminths of 
a top predator, the arctic fox (Vulpes lagopus) in the northern parts of East Greenland, more 
precisely Zackenberg and Karupelv Valley to produce baseline information for future studies on 
consequences of climate changes and on the populations dynamics of wild life in the high arctic 
areas. 
 
 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
 
 
The specific aims were: 
1. to identify endoparasitic helminth species present in faeces of arctic foxes from Zackenberg 
and Karupelv Valley. 
2. to describe geographical variations between Zackenberg and Karupelv Valley as concerns 
the occurrence and abundance of endoparasitic helminths present in faeces of arctic foxes. 
3. to map year-to-year variations in occurrence and abundance of endoparasitic helminths 
present in faeces of arctic foxes from Zackenberg and Karupelv Valley in a two-three-year 
period, 2006-2008. 
4. to describe possible variation in occurrence and abundance of endoparasitic helminths 
present in faeces of arctic foxes from Zackenberg and Karupelv Valley between fox dens, 
including difference between dens with and without pups,  
 
During May-August, scats from arctic foxes were sampled from Zackenberg in 2006-2008 and from 
Karupelv Valley in 2006-2007. The faecal samples were preserved in 70% ethanol except for the 
faecal samples from Karupelv Valley collected in 2007 that were kept in a commercial DNA-
preservative. All samples were stored in individual tubes at -20°C until use. 
Parasitological investigations using a modified sieving method and inverted microscopy, as well as 
statistical analyses on parasite compositions were used to obtain the aims. 
 
1.3 Project Limitations 
This project was established as part of the Zackenberg Ecological Research Operations (ZERO) and 
Karupelv Valley Project and was conducted as a master’s thesis in veterinary medicine. 
Besides arctic foxes, scats of stoats, sledge dogs, wolves and birds were also sampled. Due to time 
limitations only scats of arctic foxes and stoats were analysed, however only results from arctic fox 
will be commented on in this thesis. Moreover, the sledge dogs had received anthelmintic treatment 
prior to sampling that would have limited the usefulness of the results. 
Arctic fox scats sampled from the same location on the same date were reduced so at least five 
samples/den/day were examined due to time limitations and the fact that it could have originated 
from the same fox. 
Scientists are only stationed in these deserted, high arctic and restricted areas during the summer 
months because of the weather conditions and transport/housing limitations and therefore sampling 
was only performed in a restricted area and time of year. The two areas are located in the National 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
 
 
Park of North and East Greenland and no hunting is allowed and consequently did not allow post 
mortem analyses of gastrointestinal parasites. 
As part of a field study on wild carnivores, it was not always possible to collect fresh faecal samples 
and not possible to collect information about the foxes, e.g. age and gender. 
Other limitations of this study were unequal sample sizes between years and the storage for 6-8 
years. 
 
1.4 Methods 
The literature review of this thesis serves as a framework to support the study and its results. In the 
literature review, primary sources and scientific articles from journals have been preferred, but 
general knowledge about parasite life cycle, transmission, hosts etc. and detections methods are 
largely based on textbooks, however complemented by articles. The literature research on the arctic 
fox and its parasitic species had some challenges, because some literature were so old that they 
were difficult or unable to find, and some literature were written in foreign languages, e.g. Russian, 
that the author did not speak. Another veterinary student who mastered the Russian language helped 
in translating some articles. 
Scientists stationed in ZV and KV performed the samplings in 2006-2008, and the author of this 
thesis primarily performed the parasitological detection and identification processes. More details 
about sampling and detection methods can be found in other sections. The choice of detection 
method was based on the fact that the sieving method can isolate a variety of parasite 
stages/species, including dead larvae and eggs of cestodes and nematodes (Mathis et al. 1996) and 
sometimes trematodes (Mohammad N. S. Al-Sabi, DTU Vet, personal communication, September 
2014).

PART II: Literature Review 
2.1 The Arctic Fox (Vulpes lagopus) and its Endoparasitic Helminths 
2.1.1 The arctic fox (Vulpes lagopus) 
This section will generally cover the biology and behaviour of arctic foxes in relation to 
distribution, habitat, denning, reproduction and feeding, which together with the next section about 
their helminth endoparasites, will be essential for a better understanding of the parasite-host-
relationship. 
 
2.1.1.1 General characteristics and distribution 
The arctic fox (Vulpes lagopus) is a small member of the Canidae-family, weighing from 1.4 to 5 
kg (Chesemore 1975; Garrott & Eberhardt 1987; Audet et al. 2002). 
The habitat of this species includes inland, coastal, marine and alpine landscapes (Garrott & 
Eberhardt 1987). The arctic fox is widespread in the arctic areas with a circumpolar distribution 
(Figure 1). With its thick coat, short rounded ears, short muzzle and reduced limb size the arctic fox 
is well adapted to the life in these cold regions (Chesemore 1975; Stroganov 1969; Garrott & 
Eberhardt 1987). Despite this the winter months are the time of year, where mortality is highest in 
foxes over six months (Macpherson 1969) and many arctic foxes can be found in poor condition 
(Wrigley & Hatch 1976). 
 
Figure 1 - Distribution of the arctic fox. Modified from Audet et al. 2002. 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
 
 
 
During summer arctic foxes are territorial (Anthony 1997; Audet et al. 2002; Gilg et al. 2006), 
whereas radio collaring of the arctic foxes have shown that the availability of food determines 
whether they live near their summer home range or migrate to areas with more food abundance 
during winter (Chesemore 1968; Macpherson 1969; Anthony 1997).  
Through radio collaring and tagging of arctic foxes it has been shown that they can migrate long 
distances, up to 2000km (Chesemore 1968; Wrigley & Hatch 1976; Eberhardt & Hanson 1978; 
Eberhardt et al. 1983b; Henttonen et al. 2001). Close genetic connection between arctic foxes from 
North America, Northern Greenland and Siberia has been documented indicating the use of sea ice 
to migrate (Norén et al. 2011). The long distance movements are not as well understood as winter 
movements, which were linked to food availability, because it seems that some arctic foxes migrate 
long distances even though food is abundant from where they come (Eberhardt & Hanson 1978). 
However, some arctic foxes follow polar bears in order to profit from their leftovers and when 
lemmings, preys of the arctic fox, are scarce in Northeast Greenland, more arctic foxes are trapped 
in the south of Greenland (Bræstrup 1941). In ZV and KV, the arctic fox is not residing in the 
valleys year-round (Schmidt et al. 2008). 
 
2.1.1.2 Reproduction and social behaviour 
Arctic foxes have complex and flexible social systems, sometimes they are monogamous and can 
mate for life (Stroganov 1969; Garrott & Eberhardt 1987; Angerbjörn et al. 1995; Strand et al. 
2000), other times they gather together and form groups of one mating pair and helper(s), juveniles 
- usually siblings, polygynous families with one male and more reproducing females, as well as 
other forms that are described elsewhere (Eberhardt et al. 1983a; Strand et al. 2000; Norén et al. 
2012). 
The breeding season starts in April-May and the three to 20 pups are then born in the dens in late 
May to early July (Stroganov 1969; Chesemore 1975; Eberhardt et al. 1983a). Several reports 
(Bræstrup 1941; Macpherson 1969; Stroganov 1969; Eberhardt et al. 1983a, Angerbjörn et al. 
1995; Strand et al. 2000) have shown that the litter size is largely determined by food - especially 
lemmings. 
Multiple dens can be used for raising the pups, but the use of dens is not limited to hosting pups, as 
dens can also be used for sheltering other time of year (Eberhardt et al. 1983a). The pups are raised 
and feed by both the male and the female and sometimes even helpers, if present, until August 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
 
 
(Strand et al. 2000). Important causes of pup mortality are food scarcity and sibling rivalry 
(Macpherson 1969). It has been observed that some arctic foxes have fidelity for some dens and 
will use them year after year to raise pups (Eberhardt et al. 1983a, Strand et al. 2000). 
 
2.1.1.3 Feeding behaviour 
Bræstrup (1941) has originally divided the arctic foxes according to their feeding behaviour into 
lemming foxes (also called inland foxes) and coast foxes. Lemming foxes have lemmings as main 
prey and coast foxes eat marine diet (Bræstrup 1941). Other reports (MacPherson 1969; Dalerum & 
Angerbjörn 2000; Norén et al. 2012) have described the members of the Arvicolinae-subfamily 
(lemmings, voles etc.) as preferable, when available in the habitat of arctic fox. Among these, the 
collared lemming is the only rodent living in North East Greenland. The collared lemming 
population has cyclic fluctuations every fourth to fifth year (Bræstrup 1941; Dalerum & Angerbjörn 
2000; Gilg et al. 2006) and is almost unavailable under the snow cover during winter (Schmidt et 
al. 2008). As previously described, these fluctuations in availability have impacts on the litter size, 
migrations and the feeding behavior of the arctic fox. 
Besides being a predator, the arctic fox has also been described as a scavenger and an omnivore. 
These behaviours are related to the variable food items, which includes rodents, miscellaneous 
mammalian herbivores (e.g. arctic hare (Lepus arcticus), musk ox (Ovibos moschatus), reindeer 
(Rangifer tarandus)), birds and their eggs, insects, various marine animals (e.g. seal, fish, shellfish), 
berries, human garbage and stored food caches (Bræstrup 1941; Macpherson 1969; Stroganov 1969; 
Eberhardt et al. 1983b; Kapel 1999; Dalerum & Angerbjörn 2000; Gilg et al. 2006; Schmidt et al. 
2008). It has indicated that they can be cannibalistic (Dalerum & Angerbjörn 2000). 
 
 
2.1.2 Endoparasitic helminths of the arctic fox (Vulpes lagopus) 
This section comprises a literature review of the endoparasitic helminths reported in arctic foxes in 
table-form (Table 1, Table 2,Table 3 and Table 4) and a presentation of the helminths, including 
hosts, location in host, life cycle and pathology. 
 
2.1.2.1 Acanthocephala (Table 1) 
Polymorphus spp. and Corynosoma spp. belong to the acanthocephalans. The members of this 
phylum require two hosts: a vertebrate definitive host, including fish, birds and mammals, and an 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
 
 
arthropod intermediate host. Few records of human infection with acanthocephalans are 
documented (Roberts & Janovy 2000). Adult worms live in the gastrointestinal system of the 
definitive host, wherein they attach to the wall with their spiny proboscis. Eggs containing acanthor 
larvae are excreted with the faeces of the definitive host. Arthropods get infected by ingesting the 
eggs, and the acanthor develops into a cystacanth, that is the infective larval stage to definitive hosts 
(Bowman et al. 2003). Sometimes a vertebrate paratenic host is involved in the life cycle, and the 
cystacanth will encyst in the viscera and rests until the paratenic host is consumed by the definitive 
host. The possible behavioural influences of the acanthocephalans on the intermediate hosts 
increase the likelihood of transmission to the definitive host. The attachment of the 
acanthocephalans on the gastrointestinal wall of the definitive hosts has a traumatic impact and can 
sometimes be fatal (Roberts & Janovy 2000). 
To the author’s knowledge, acanthocephalans have only been observed in arctic foxes after 
intestinal isolation, and only Kapel & Nansen (1996) has reported acanthocephalans in Greenland 
(Table 1).

2.1.2.2 Cestoida (Table 2) 
Most species in the class Cestoida are characterised by having an indirect life cycle with a definitive 
host and one or two intermediate hosts. Adult cestodes in the gastrointestinal system of the 
definitive hosts produce eggs, each of which contains a larva, the oncosphere, which can infect the 
first intermediate host. The oncosphere then develop into a second-stage larva and encyst in the 
tissue of the intermediate host. Depending on the cestode species, the second-stage larva can either 
infect a second intermediate host or the definitive host, wherein it develops into a third-stage larva 
or an adult worm with a scolex, respectively. The infective stage to the definitive host is called the 
metacestode and when ingested, it attaches to the intestinal wall with its scolex (Bowman et al. 
2003). 
 
The taenidae-family includes the genera Echinococcus and Taenia, and has been observed in both 
coprological and post mortem examinations (Table 2). Taenia sp. and Taenia ovis krabbei have 
been found in Greenland (Rausch et al. 1983; Kapel & Nansen 1996) (Table 2). 
The intermediate hosts of the carnivore parasites Echinococcus multilocularis and Echinococcus 
granolosus are microtine rodents and ungulates, respectively. These two parasites can cause 
echinococcosis in humans through ingestion of eggs from the faeces of definitive hosts (Eckert et 
al. 2001). In a study of red foxes (Vulpes vulpes), a significant difference between foxes over and 
foxes under 12 months of age was shown, in which the latter had higher worm burdens (Hofer et al. 
2000). Besides these two Echinococcus-species, Taenia crassiceps is also of public health 
importance because of the zoonotic potential. Rodents and lagomorphs are intermediate hosts in this 
species, Taenia polyacantha and Taenia pisiformis. The intermediate hosts of T. o. krabbei are 
ungulates (Jones & Pybus 2001). Infection with cestodes in the definitive hosts often cause little or 
no damage (Bowman et al. 2003), but the metacestodes can have a negative impact on the 
intermediate hosts, when infiltrated in the tissues (Jones & Pybus 2001).

The life cycle of the Mesocestoides spp. has not been completely identified, but various mammals, 
birds, rodents and reptiles have been shown to be involved (Roberts & Janovy 2000). 
Mesocestoides lineatus have been identified in Greenland (Kapel & Nansen 1996) (Table 2). 
 
Like other Diphyllobothrium species, Diphyllobothrium dendriticum has copepods as first 
intermediate hosts, fresh water fish as second and birds or mammals as definitive hosts in the life 
cycle. Eating raw or undercooked fish can induce illness in humans, but it is often with mild 
symptoms (Kuchta et al. 2013). D. dendriticum has been isolated in Greenland (Kapel & Nansen 
1996; Rausch et al. 1983) (Table 2).

2.1.2.3 Nematoda (Table 3) 
Members of the family ascarididae previously reported in arctic foxes include Toxascaris leonina 
and Toxocara canis, and only T. leonina has been found in arctic foxes from Greenland (Rausch et 
al. 1983; Kapel & Nansen 1996) (Table 3). 
The infection with T. leonina in the canid or felid definitive hosts is non-migratory and therefore is 
of relatively minor pathologic importance. Ingested eggs containing larvae hatch in the intestines, 
and the juvenile larvae penetrate the mucosa. After growth and moulting, they will enter the 
intestines again to mature into egg-producing worms (Roberts & Janovy 2000). Rodents can occur 
as optional intermediate hosts of this parasite (Okulewicz et al. 2012). Higher parasite burden of T. 
leonina have been demonstrated in arctic foxes less than one year of age (Kapel & Nansen 1996). 
T. canis has a more complicated life cycle than T. leonina. The adult worms live in the intestines of 
the definitive hosts, mostly canids, and produce a great number of eggs, which are passed on to the 
faeces. The embryo develops through several stages in the environment and become the infective 
third stage larva. From here two known routes of transmission can take place (Roberts and Janovy 
2000; Bowman et al. 2003). The first possibility is the ingestion and hatching of the eggs in the 
canid intestines and subsequently the larvae will penetrate the intestinal wall. In young puppies with 
no prior infection and due to age resistance, a hepatotracheal migration occurs and result in 
maturation of adult worms in the intestines. In a mature canid, a somatic migration will result in a 
developmental arrest of the third stage larva (Sprent 1958; Roberts and Janovy 2000; Bowman et al. 
2003). During the last trimester of the pregnancy, activation of these arrested larvae leads to 
maternal-foetal transmission and when the puppies are born, a transmammary transmission can 
occur. A hepatotracheal migration also occur in the foetus and puppies (Roberts and Janovy 2000; 
Bowman et al. 2003; Okulewicz et al. 2012,). Larval migration can cause tissue changes, and a 
large worm burden can have an impact on intestinal function and sometimes be fatal, especially for 
pups (Bowman et al. 2003). The second possible route of transmission requires a mammal paratenic 
host, e.g. a rodent or humans. The ingestion and hatching of the eggs by these hosts will cause 
visceral larvae migrans and larvae developmental arrest. Predation of the infected paratenic host can 
cause infection of the definitive host (Bowman et al. 2003, Roberts and Janovy 2000, Sprent 1958). 
The pathology of the visceral larvae migrans depends on the location site (Bowman et al. 2003, 
Roberts and Janovy 2000). 
Experimental studies on these two ascarids have shown that embryonated eggs of T. leonina are 
more resistant to freezing (-15°C) than T. canis in terms of viability (Okoshi & Osui 1968) and this 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
 
 
can indicate why T. leonina is more widespread than T. canis in arctic areas. Humidity is also an 
important factor in the environmental development of T. canis, as increased humidity will result in 
increased egg development (Gamboa 2005). 
 
Adult worms of Dioctophyme renale live in the kidney of the definitive host, and the eggs are shed 
with the urine (Roberts & Janovy 2000), thus eggs cannot be detected through coprological 
detection methods, thus no more comments will be made on this parasite. 
 
Trichinella spp. has been isolated in Greenland (Roth 1949; Kapel et al. 1996) (Table 3) and has a 
very unusual lifecycle: in the same animal, the adult worms live in the intestinal mucosa and the 
infective larvae in the skeletal musculature. The generally practiced diagnostic detection methods of 
this parasite rely on the demonstration of larvae in the muscles (Bowman et al. 2003, Roberts & 
Janovy 2000). Therefore no further comments on this parasite, as this study uses faeces to detect 
parasites. 
Capillaridae comprises several Capillaria-species and has been divided into smaller genera; e.g. 
Eucoleus, Pearsonema, Aonchotheca, which are species of the canids and felids found in the 
airways, lower urinary tract and intestines, respectively. Eucoleus (= Capillaria) aerophila is a lung 
parasite, which causes symptoms that are dependent on the worm burdens. The eggs of this parasite 
can be found in faeces or tracheal mucus (Bowman et al. 2003). 
Infective eggs of Trichuris spp., each of which contains a larva, are very resistant in the 
environment, and the ingestion of the eggs will cause them to hatch. All development in the host 
takes place in the epithelium of the large intestine, and the adult worms release the eggs in the 
intestinal lumen. Large worm burdens may cause intermittent diarrhoea, but often infections cause 
no symptoms (Bowman et al. 2003). 
The life cycle of species in the Spirurida-order is indirect, and definitive hosts have to ingest an 
arthropod intermediate host, or alternatively a paratenic host that have eaten an infected 
intermediate host. Adult worms of the Physaloptera species live in the stomach of canids and can 
cause symptoms such as vomitus (Bowman et al. 2003). Spirocerca spp. are parasites in the wall of 
the upper gastrointestinal tract. The tumours are formed as a consequence of the maturing and 
presence of the adult worms and are mostly located in the oesophagus and in the stomach (Petrow 
1927; Roberts & Janovy 2000). Other than the tumour formation, the larval migration of Spirocerca 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
 
 
lupi from the stomach to the aorta and back to the oesophagus can cause some serious disturbances 
like aneurisms, vomitus and haemorrhages (Roberts & Janovy 2000). 
 
Strongyloides stercoralis has a broad host range: humans, canids, felids and other mammals 
(Roberts & Janovy 2000) and have been reported in arctic foxes from Greenland (Kapel & Nansen 
1996) (Table 3). Infection with this parasite can cause diarrhoea, pneumonia and dermatitis (Foreyt 
2001). The life cycle consists of free-living and parasitic generations, but only females live as adults 
in the small intestines of the host (Roberts & Janovy 2000; Bowman et al. 2003). The female 
produces eggs that hatch in intestine and larvae pass with faeces. The larvae then develop into 
infective larvae in developmental arrest or free-living larvae that mature into free-living adults. The 
infective larva infects the host through ingestion or skin penetration and then undergoes visceral 
migration to end in the small intestines (Roberts & Janovy 2000). Autoinfection of this parasite 
species can also occur, in which larvae from hatched eggs will become infective within the 
intestine, migrate in the tissue and become an egg producing female worm in the intestines (Roberts 
& Janovy 2000, Viney & Lok 2007). Additionaly, transmammary transmission can occur (Bowman 
et al. 2003). 
 
Infection of the host with Uncinaria stenocephala occurs through ingestion of or skin penetration 
by the infective larvae. After larval migration and development, the adult worms attach to the wall 
of the small intestines and produces prodigious amounts of eggs. Hookworms suck blood and can 
cause anemia, but U. stenocephala is not as pathogenic as other hookworms, e.g. Ancylostoma 
caninum that also can cause cutaneous larva migrans in humans (Foreyt 2001). Furthermore, eggs 
and larvae of U. stenocephala are very resistant to cold temperatures and have been isolated in 
Greenland (Rausch et al. 1983) (Table 3), whereas freezing can destroy eggs of A. caninum 
(Bowman et al. 2003). 
 
The lungworm, Crenosoma vulpis has an indirect life cycle with canids as definitive hosts and 
molluscs as intermediate hosts. The adult female in the lungs of canid deposits first stage larvae, 
that then ascend the trachea, are swallowed and passed with faeces. The third stage larvae that 
develop in the intermediate host are the infective stage to the definitive host (Bowman et al. 2003).

2.1.2.4 Trematoda (Table 4) 
The development of digenean trematodes occurs in at least two hosts. A mollusc or rarely an 
annelid acts as the first intermediate hosts that get infected by a penetrating free-living miracidium. 
In the mollusc, the miracidium undergo asexual proliferation: from a sporocyst to rediae to 
cercariae. The cercariae are then released from the first intermediate host, and hereafter the 
development is species-dependent. In some species, the cercaricae are the infective stages to 
vertebrate definitive host and transmission occurs through skin penetration. In other species the 
cercaricae encyst on the vegetation or in an additional intermediate host to become infective as 
metacercariae. After ingestion of the metacercariae by the vertebral definitive host, the 
metacercariae excyst in the stomach and migrate to the final site, e.g. the liver, lungs or circulatory 
system (Roberts & Janovy 2000). Aguirre et al. (2000) was able to isolate trematode eggs from 
faeces, but could not identify the species (Table 4). Cryptocotyle sp. and Plagiorchis elegans, both 
recorded in Greenlandic arctic foxes (Rausch et al. 1983; Kapel & Nansen 1996), have fish and 
arthropods as second intermediate hosts, respectively (Stunkard 1930; Wooton 1957; Bock 1984).

2.1.2.5 Pseudoparasites and spurious parasites 
Faecal examination may reveal so-called pseudoparasites and/or spurious parasites that can be 
disturbing in the identification process and possibly give false-positive results. Pseudoparasites are 
objects that bear a resemblance to the morphology of the parasites, e.g. pollen grains, mite eggs, 
mites or free-living larvae. Parasites originating from a different host than the examined one are 
called spurious parasites. Spurious parasites can be found in the examined faeces due to 
coprophagia or predation of their original hosts (Zajac & Conboy 2012).

2.2 Endoparasitic Helminth Detection and Preservation Methods 
2.2.1 Detection of endoparasitic helminths 
A short presentation of six coprological diagnostic methods (direct smear, flotation, sedimentation, 
Baermann, sieving method, detection of parasitic coproantigens/-DNA) will be covered in this 
section. The precise procedure of these methods will not be discussed and can be found elsewhere 
(Foreyt 2001; Bowman et al. 2003; Zajac & Conboy 2012). 
Isolation of adult helminths from the whole gastrointestinal system is preferred over isolation of 
eggs and larvae from faeces, because faeces only contain a small quantity of parasites, and different 
studies support this (Wolfe et al. 2001; Saeed & Kapel 2006; Al-Sabi et al. 2010). Furthermore the 
excretion of eggs or larvae has been found to be varying (Oliveira et al. 2006; Al-Sabi et al. 2007) 
and can be partly due to non-patent infections (Wolfe et al. 2001).  
 
2.2.1.1 Direct smear 
Direct smear can be done with or without staining (e.g. Ziehl-Neelsen staining) and has a low 
sensitivity, because of the little amount of faeces used. This method can be used in the field and to 
identify protozoans, e.g. Giardia, Cryptosporidum, but it is not recommended for routine faecal 
examinations (Bowman et al. 2003; Zajac & Conboy 2012). 
 
2.2.1.2 Flotation 
The flotation techniques have the advantages of being easy, inexpensive and being able to separate 
light eggs, oocysts and larvae from heavy faecal debris (Zajac & Conboy 2012). This is due to the 
difference in densities between the faecal debris, which will sink, and the parasitic stages, which 
will float, when put in a medium with an intermediate density (Bowman et al. 2003). When 
increasing the specific gravity of the flotation fluid, the variety of floating parasite eggs will also 
increase, but so does the risk of distorting eggs and more debris will float as well (Foreyt 2001; 
Zajac & Conboy 2012). In solutions widely used (e.g. solution with salt, sodium chloride, 
magnesium sulphate), trematode eggs often fail to float and nematode larvae and protozoan 
trophozoites can be distorted. However, they are appropriate for the detection of protozoan oocysts, 
nematode and cestode eggs (Bowman et al. 2003). A helminth study using saturated sugar flotation 
fluid managed to detect trematode eggs in fox faeces (Wolfe et al. 2001).  Flotation fluid is added in 
the flotation process and this can also have impact on the ability to detect the eggs and/or larvae, as 
==PAGE:32==
31 
 
 
 
different physical characteristic of flotation fluids can result in sinking of larvae or reduce the speed 
of flotation (Scott 1972). Exposure of flotation solution has been shown to deform larvae (Conboy 
2000) and trematode eggs (Glinz et al. 2010). To increase the sensitivity of flotation procedures, 
centrifugation can be performed (Zajac & Conboy 2012). 
 
2.2.1.3 Sedimentation 
Trematode and acanthocephalan eggs can be identified using sedimentation techniques, because 
they are heavy and will sediment under centrifugation. Faecal debris can be removed, if the mixture 
of faeces and water is sieved through layers of gaze (Bowman et al. 2003).  
 
2.2.1.4 Baermann 
The Baermann test is based on a migration-sedimentation principle and can only be used to identify 
live motile larvae. Faeces are placed in gauze hanging in a water filled Baermann glass, and thus the 
procedure is dependent on the migration of larvae through the faeces and gauze. After the migration 
they will sediment due to gravity (Bowman et al. 2003).  
 
2.2.1.5 Sieving method 
The sieving method as described by Mathis et al. (1996) involves two sieve-steps with different 
mesh sizes (40μm and 20μm). According to studies, parasites that can be detected with sieving 
method are larvae in both fresh and frozen samples (Al-Sabi et al. 2010), cestode eggs (Mathis et 
al. 1996; Davidson et al. 2009) and eggs from nematodes (Mathis et al. 1996). Trematode eggs can 
also be recovered with using sieving method (Mohammad N. S. Al-Sabi, DTU Vet, personal 
communication, September 2014). The sieving method is time consuming, labour intensive (Øines 
et al. 2014) and requires an inverted microscope. Together these factors make it less suitable for use 
in clinical practice. The two-step procedure proposed by Mathis et al. (1996) has later been 
discussed by Øines et al. (2014), because during the first sieving-step eggs and/or larvae can 
potentially be blocked due to faecal debris and potentially be damaged so that they are washed 
through in the second step. Furthermore Øines et al. (2014) point out that any adult worms will be 
discarded during the first step and consequently the sensitivity of the method will get reduced. In a 
study regarding detection of Angiostrongylus vasorum-larvae in faeces by Al-Sabi et al. (2010), 
only one sieving-step is used (21μm). 
 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
 
 
2.2.1.6 Parasitic coproantigens/-DNA 
Detection of parasite coproantigens or -DNA is other methods that can be performed on parasites 
that morphologically cannot be distinguished from each other. This is especially valuable for 
parasites that have a public health concern like taeniid eggs. In this case it is intended to distinguish 
Echinococcus multilocularis and Echinococcus granulosus from Taenia spp. (Bowman et al. 2003; 
Trachsel et al. 2007). It can also be used in studies, where faecal samples have been frozen resulting 
in dead larvae and therefore cannot be detected by Baermann (Al-Sabi et al. 2010). This method 
faces limitations such as inhibitory substances in the faeces and therefore can be used in 
conjunction with isolation of eggs and larvae in the sieving method to increase sensitivity (Al-Sabi 
et al. 2007). 
 
Often a gold standard for detection of different parasites exists, but when performing a study on the 
general parasite fauna of an animal population, it is often not possible to execute them all due to 
time, economics etc. Therefore it is important to consider the possibilities and limitations of the 
detection methods. 
 
2.2.2 Preservation and storage 
The choice of storage methods, like media, temperature and time, are important considerations 
when performing parasitological investigations on preserved eggs and/or larvae in faecal samples. 
The available storage options depend on different factors, like the desired detection method, the 
availability of media in certain field conditions and the potential risk of zoonotic transmission 
(Schurer et al. 2014). Literature about preservation and storage of samples is mainly focusing on 
three topics: the viability of eggs and larvae, the ability to detect parasitic DNA and the induced 
changes on morphology of parasitic stages. 
The former two will not be discussed further in this section, but a comment on viability of 
Toxascaris leonina and Toxocara canis can be found in the section 2.1.2 Endoparasitic helminths 
of the arctic fox (Vulpes lagopus). 
 
E. multilocularis poses a zoonotic risk to humans and therefore the World Health Organization 
(WHO) and World Organization for Animal Health (OIE) recommend deep-freezing of faecal 
samples or intestines of carnivores at -70°C for at least 4 days or at -80°C for at least 2 days to 
inactivate the eggs of this species (Eckert et al. 2001). As a consequence of the freezing and 
==PAGE:34==
33 
 
 
 
subsequent thawing, the morphology of parasites can be altered (Schurer et al. 2014). Schurer et al. 
(2014) examined canine faeces after storage temperatures of -80 to -20°C for different storage times 
(3 days to 1 month + 3 days) and found that thin-shelled strongylide eggs were very fragile to this 
process and that 93% (102/110) of Uncinaria stenocephala-positive samples exhibited damage in 
egg structure, including rupture and decolourization. Furthermore, Schurer et al. (2014) observed 
that eggs of the ascarids, T. leonina and T. canis, were decolourized, but could still be recognized 
and that taeniid eggs did not show any changes in morphology. Other studies reports similar results 
on thin-shelled strongylide eggs: Foreyt (1986) showed that freezing (-20°C) of faeces from deer 
resulted in drastic lower recovery of trichostrongylidae, when compared to fresh faeces, and 
Cringoli et al. (2011) point out that freezing (-30°C for 10 days) could result in damaged 
Ancylostoma caninum-eggs and consequently in reduction of Faecal Egg Count (FEC) compared to 
5% formalin-preserved and fresh samples.  
 
Ethanol and formalin are commonly used storage media. However, 70% ethanol has been shown to 
destroy trichostrongylida eggs from deer after only one day of preservation (Foreyt et al. 1986). On 
the other hand, 70% ethanol effectively preserved Echinoccoccus-eggs for up to 7 months (Mathis 
et al. 1996) and in addition is an easy media to use in field studies. Foreyt (1986) and Cringoli et al. 
(2011) both demonstrated the effectiveness of formalin to preserve strongylide eggs. The 
disadvantage of formalin, however, is the classification as a carcinogenic chemical to humans 
(World Health Organization & International Agency of Research on Cancer 2004), and precautions 
must be taken during handling. 
 
The possible changes in morphology or physical properties of eggs and larvae trouble the 
identification process and can make the parasitological investigations less reliable, and therefore 
considerations regarding pros and cons of storage time, media and temperature must be made.

3.1.3 Sieving method and inverted microscopy 
Parasite eggs and larvae were isolated from the faecal samples using a sieving procedure modified 
from previously published protocols (Mathis et al. 1996; Davidson et al. 2009; Al-Sabi et al. 2010). 
See Appendix (Modified Sieving Procedure) for procedure. 
The tubes with samples were gently shaken so that precipitated eggs were suspended in the fluid 
preservative. The fluid was then immediately poured through a layer of gauze and then through a 
sieve with mesh sizes of 20 or 22 μm. The retained material with its corresponding sieve was kept 
for later steps involving collection of parasitic stages. 
Two grams of faecal sample were thoroughly homogenized with 36 mL deionized water and poured 
through one layer of gauze. The filtered material was transferred to four 10mL tubes and 
centrifuged at 178xRCF (Relative Centrifugal Force) for 10 minutes. Then, the supernatants were 
removed with suction, and the precipitates were thoroughly vortexed. Flotation fluid (sugar-salt, 
specific gravity 1.2) was then added until reaching 8 mL in each of the four tubes. Next, the four 
tubes were centrifuged at 53xRCF for 1 minute. The supernatants from the previous step were 
filtrated through the same sieve used earlier.  The sieve was washed 3-4 times with deionized water 
to remove flotation fluid and to clean retained particles, which then were backwashed into a 3.5 cm 
petri dish for examination using an inverted microscope (Leica Leitz DM IL) at 100x and 200x 
magnification. 
 
3.1.4 Parasite identification 
The parasitic eggs and larvae were identified according to morphology described by Zajac & 
Conboy (2012). If samples contained more than 100 taeniid eggs, a dilution was made to calculate 
an estimate of egg number. 
An ocular micrometer calibrated according to Zajac & Conboy (2012) was used to measure parasite 
stages when using the inverted microscope. If the level of details was insufficient to identify the 
parasites at the available magnifications on this microscope, the eggs/larvae were transferred to a 
glass slide using a pipette and examined by light microscopy (Leica DMR) at 400x and 630x 
magnifications. A camera (Leica DFC310 FX) supported by the software Leica Application Suite 
(LAS), v. 3.6.0, was used to obtain pictures of parasites on both microscopes and to measure 
dimensions of parasites when the light microscope was used. 
For species identification of taeniid eggs, samples were kept for molecular typing by transferring 
the examined to 2mL tubes and then centrifuged at 6000 RCF for 3 minutes. The sediment was 
==PAGE:42==
41 
 
 
 
stored at – 20oC. Molecular typing according to Trachsel et al. (2007) could not be completed 
within the timeframe of this project. 
 
3.1.5 Statistics 
Confidence interval fitted for binomial distribution were analysed using the online software 
http://statpages.org/confint.html. 
Range of intensity of infection, mean intensity and mean abundance were calculated using 
definitions giving by Bush et al. (1997). 
To test overall statistical variations between occurrences and abundances of parasites, a multivariate 
analysis, ANOSIM (Analysis Of Similarities), was used. If a significant difference was found, 
another statistical method combining SIMPER (Similarity Percentage) and Bray-Curtis similarity 
measure was performed to evaluate which data parameters were primarily responsible for the 
significant difference by comparing all possible options (Clarke 1993). All multivariate analysis 
was performed using the software PAST (PAleontological STatistics, version 3.04). 
Concerning the statistical test of the arctic fox dens in ZV, years were pooled and only samples 
originating from the arctic fox dens FOXD01, FOXD02, FOXD03, FOXD04, FOXD08, FOXD10 
and FOXD12 were included to ensure sufficient sample sizes (>10 samples/den). Furthermore, 
FOXD03 and FOXD04 were pooled, because they were connected and occupied by the same arctic 
fox family (Niels M. Schmidt, AU, personal communication, January 2015).

3.3 Discussion 
In this retrospective coprological study of endoparasitic helminths in arctic foxes from Northeastern 
Greenland 2006-2008, we found parasitic stages of a variety of nematodes and a cestode family 
previously reported in arctic foxes, and also a nematode, never described previously in arctic foxes, 
Eucoleus boehmi. 
 
==PAGE:49==
48 
 
 
 
This study has shown that ZV and KV are similar with respect to overall parasite composition of 
arctic foxes, even though large relative predator densities exist. This could be explained by the same 
animal species inhabiting the two areas (Schmidt et al. 2008) and thus contribute to the same food 
sources of the arctic foxes. Furthermore, KV is located on Traill Island, however in cold months, 
the sea ice connects the mainland and the island making it possible for the arctic foxes to migrate 
on. Comparative studies between mainland and islands in the Mediterranean Sea showed that 
parasites on islands had a higher level of host range (Nieberding et al. 2006) as well as the islands 
had a lower level of parasite diversity, especially parasites with indirect life cycles (Goüy de 
Bellocq et al. 2003). Thus, a reduction of sea ice along the coast of Greenland due to climate 
changes, may lead to a year-round isolation of Traill Island with resulting changes in parasite-host 
systems. Migration of arctic foxes is contingent on food availability (Chesemore 1968; Macpherson 
1969; Anthony 1997) and reduced lemming numbers may therefore results in dispersal of parasites 
to new localities. A direct life cycle may enable the parasites to complete a development on new 
localities if brought by the hosts. However, if parasites with indirect life cycles are brought to e.g. 
an island without suitable intermediate hosts, they cannot complete their life cycle there. On the 
contrary, sea ice is an important factor in the migration of arctic foxes (Norén et al. 2011) and a 
reduction could lead to less migration of the arctic foxes, thus decreasing the possibility of parasite 
dispersion by this host. 
 
Not surprisingly, eggs of the taenidae-family were found in both localities, because this family has 
been recorded previously in arctic foxes in many parts of the arctic (e.g. Shilyaeva 1968; Kapel & 
Nansen 1996; Stien et al. 2010; Mejler et al. 2011; Elmore et al. 2013), and moreover possible 
intermediate hosts resides here, including rodents, lagomorphes and ungulates (Dalerum & 
Angerbjörn 2000, Schmidt et al. 2008). Unfortunately a distinction between species was not 
completed within the timeframe of this project, but molecular identification is ongoing. 
As a consequence of the presence of the collared lemming in Northeastern Greenland as well as 
being the main prey for arctic fox when available (Bræstrup 1941), the found taeniid eggs could 
belong to the species Taenia polyacantha, Taenia crassiceps and/or Echinococcus multilocularis. 
These cestodes have rodents as intermediate hosts (Jones & Pybus 2001), but have never previously 
been identified in arctic foxes from Greenland (Rausch et al. 1983; Kapel & Nansen 1996). 
However, Kapel & Nansen (1996) emphasizes that the arctic foxes of East Greenland, where 
lemmings live, have a distinct type of Taenia sp. that was not similar to Taenia ovis krabbei found 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
 
 
in arctic foxes from West Greenland. Reindeers are intermediate hosts of the T. o. krabbei (Stien et 
al. 2010) and are common in West Greenland (Kapel & Nansen 1996). 
Furthermore, a tendency between the occurrence of taeniid eggs in faeces of arctic foxes and the 
occurrence of lemmings in KV existed: In 2006 with high lemming abundance, there was a high 
faecal occurrence of taeniid eggs and reverse in 2007. However this tendency was not observed in 
ZV, instead an unexplainable one-year delay occurred, i.e. no taeniid eggs were found in 2006, one 
year after a low number of lemmings, and more taeniid eggs were found in 2007 and 2008, which 
were years after medium to high numbers of lemmings. Raundrup et al. (2012) reported T. o. 
krabbei in musk oxen from West Greenland. The muskox is an inhabitant of ZV and KV and its 
carcass functions as a food source for arctic foxes (Schmidt et al. 2008). Five, 12 and 11 fresh musk 
oxen carcasses were found in ZV in 2006, 2007 and 2008, respectively (Jensen & Rasch 2009). 
Hence, in ZV a tendency similar to the proposed one in KV existed, but instead of lemmings the 
occurrence of taeniid eggs in faeces of arctic foxes in ZV followed the occurrence of musk oxen 
carcasses. Taken together, it is likely that the arctic foxes from the two localities harbour different 
species of taenidae. On Svalbard, a similar correlation between the abundance and distribution of 
intermediate hosts (reindeer or voles) and the presence of parasites, (T. o. krabbei or T. 
polyacantha, T. crassiceps and E. multilocularis) in the definitive host, the arctic fox, has been 
shown (Stien et al. 2010). 
The pathological impact of taenidae on the definitive hosts is mild (Bowman et al. 2003), but 
infection can have great negative impact on the intermediate hosts (Jones & Pybus 2001). Of the 
taenidae mentioned, only T. crassiceps and E. multilocularis are zoonotic. 
Warmer winters in Northeasten Greenland have probably already led to a collapse of the lemming 
cycles, i.e. a lower maximum number of the rodents and prolonged cycles (Gilg et al. 2009), 
thereby potentially limiting the predator-prey transmission of parasites with rodent intermediate 
hosts. On the contrary, increased mortality of musk ox is expected due to icecrust formation on soil, 
which means more musk ox carcasses for the arctic foxes to prey on (Gilg et al. 2009; IUCN 2009), 
consequently favouring the parasite transmission between ungulate intermediate hosts and the arctic 
fox. 
 
It was expected to find T. leonina as the most abundant parasite species in the faecal samples of the 
arctic foxes examined, because similar results were observed in other coprological studies (Aguirre 
et al. 2000; Mejler et al. 2011; Elmore et al. 2013) as well as in studies using gastrointestinal 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
 
 
isolation of adult worms (e.g. Shilyaeva 1968; Rausch et al. 1990; Kapel & Nansen 1996; Stien et 
al. 2010). 
The statistical results of the multivariate analysis of our data from ZV have shown that particularly 
T. leonina had a dominant role in the parasitic communities in the studied areas. 
In ZV, 2007 was a year with high lemming abundance and since a significant difference between 
2006 and 2007 was found, the collared lemming probably served as an intermediate host for T. 
leonina in ZV. Moreover, Rausch et al. (1990) suggest that dens could serve as a reservoir of 
embryonated eggs and this may explain that even though 2008 revealed a low number of lemmings, 
embryonated eggs from previous years in the dens could have maintained the infection levels in 
2008 and thereby a high faecal occurrence. These findings and the findings of different egg stages 
of T. leonina are congruent with investigations of embryonated eggs of T. leonine reporting that 
eggs can survive freezing (-15°C) and that egg development can occur in temperatures from 6 to 
37°C (Okushi & Usui 1968) that correlate with the summer temperatures in Greenland mentioned in 
3.1 Materials and methods. Another explanation of the higher faecal occurrence of T. leonina in 
2008, when lemmings were scarce in numbers, is the caching behaviour of arctic foxes when food 
is abundant (Bræstrup 1941). The arctic foxes in 2008 could have eaten the infected lemmings that 
were stored in 2007 and thereby maintaining infection levels this way. 
Our results indicate that both the direct and the indirect (with intermediate host) life cycle of T. 
leonina could have taken place in ZV, and that the direct life cycle probably was of most 
importance in the KV as no yearly significant differences were detected there.  
 
It appears that presence of pups in fox dens did not influenced the occurrence or the abundance of 
the found helminths, but instead significant differences driven by T. leonina were shown between 
dens in ZV and thus must be explained by underlying factors other than the presence of pups. These 
factors can be divided into abiotic and biotic factor, of which the former involves the environmental 
conditions that affect the development and survival of the eggs, e.g. local differences in humidity, 
soil composition and grass cover. Feeding behaviour of the host, genetic variations in the infectivity 
of the parasite or genetic variations in the susceptibility of the hosts could be biotic reasons. 
Another study has shown that only one arctic fox den, FOXD01, was significantly different from 
the other dens in relation to feeding behaviour that predominantly was of marine origin (Ehrich et 
al. 2015; Niels M. Schmidt, AU, personal communication, January 2015). However, this did not 
correlate with the higher occurrence of T. leonina in this particular den, as T. leonina is not 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
 
 
associated with marine diet. Variation between dens most likely relates to environmental factors or 
genetics of the host or parasite, as explained above. 
 
Eggs in the category of “Other ascarididae” could not be identified to a species level. Their roughed 
shell appearance and size range could indicate three things: 1) findings of  a new ascarid species, 2) 
shrunken Toxocara canis eggs as was seen with eggs of T. leonina or 3) maybe Baylisascaris 
procyonis, a racoon parasite that occasionally can infect dogs (Zajac & Conboy 2012). To the 
authors knowledge T. canis has only been reported a few times in arctic foxes from Iceland, 
Sweden and the Northeastern parts of Russia (Gubanov 1964; Skírnisson et al. 1993; Aguirre et al. 
2000; Mejler et al. 2011) The less occurrence of T. canis may be a result of the less resistance to 
freezing than eggs of T. leonina (Okoshi & Usui 1968; O’Lorcain 1995). 
 
Climate change can, as mentioned, influence the presence of lemmings and therefore potentially 
decrease the importance of an indirect cycle of T. leonina in ZV. However, global warming and 
increased precipitation as results of climate changes will probably favour the direct transmission of 
ascarid eggs, because increased temperatures improve survival and development of eggs (Okoshi & 
Usui 1968; Gamboa 2005) and occurrence of eggs in faeces have shown to be higher in areas with 
higher humidity (Dybing et al. 2013). A colonization of T. canis would have negative clinical 
impact on the arctic foxes, since visceral larval migrans of this parasite in young foxes can cause 
organ damage or death (Bowman et al. 2003). T. leonina and T. canis could cause chronic diarrhoea 
and unthriftiness in adult arctic foxes (Foreyt 2001). 
 
To the authors’ knowledge Eucoleus boehmi has never been reported in arctic foxes, but infections 
have been reported in dogs and wild canids in Europe, North and South America (Davidson et al. 
2006; Zajac & Conboy 2012). The location of adult worms in the host is the nasal mucosa, and 
diagnostic methods include detection of adult worms in nasal cavity and egg detection in nasal 
discharge or in faecal samples. Infections with this nematode are often asymptomatic, but sneezing 
and mucopurulent nasal discharge have also been described (Zajac & Conboy 2012). 
Arctic foxes can migrate up to 2000km (e.g. Chesemore 1968; Wrigley & Hatch 1976; Eberhardt & 
Hansson 1978; Eberhardt et al. 1983b; Henttonen et al. 2001) and thus the arctic fox positive for E. 
boehmi could have acquired the infection in an area inhabited by infected canids. It is also likely 
that no one has reported this parasite in arctic foxes if post mortem examinations of organs did not 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
 
 
included the nasal cavity or that the reported capillaridae eggs (Aguirre et al. 2000, Mejler et al. 
2011, Elmore et al. 2013) were in fact E. boehmi. An in-vitro study of E. boehmi has reported that 
temperature and humidity are important factors in development and viability of the eggs (Perrucci 
et al. 2014): exposure to low temperatures (5°C) stopped development, but subsequent transfer to 
more favourable conditions in relation to temperature (20°C) and relative humidity (85%) showed 
that the viability was not affected. Too low relative humidity (45%) was also shown to limit the egg 
survival independent of temperatures (Perrucci et al. 2014). Climate changes with warmer 
temperatures and more precipitation may therefore favour colonization of this parasite, and perhaps 
the finding was a consequence of climate changes. 
 
Only four samples were positive for Physaloptera spp. and similar low findings have been reported 
previously in post mortem investigations of arctic foxes (Gubanov 1964; Rausch et al. 1990) and 
Rausch et al. (1990) suggested that the low finding of Physaloptera in one fox was incidentally. 
However, underestimation of eggs was possible, because eggs are not always detected by flotation 
(Theisen et al. 1998), because Physaloptera spp. readily sediment and therefore sedimentation 
techniques are a more sensitive coprological method (Zajac & Conboy 2012). In dogs, Physaloptera 
spp. could infrequently cause intestinal inflammation resulting in vomitus (Bowman et al. 2003). 
The life cycle of Physaloptera species involves an arthropod intermediate host, and a changing 
climate may result in increased numbers of arthropods (Klein et al. 2008). 
 
Strongyloides stercoralis has previously been reported before in arctic foxes from Greenland and 
autoinfection has been assigned as transmission route (Kapel & Nansen 1996). The present 
observations of a broad size range of the larvae in this study that occasionally exceeded those given 
in Zajac & Conboy (2012) could indicate that larvae may be able to develop in the environment of 
Greenland, making the direct and free-living life cycles alternatives to autoinfection. Another 
option of larval development may have occurred in warmer and more humid surroundings, like the 
intestines of the arctic foxes due to coprophagia. However, the finding of several larval stages 
should also be seen in context of the sampling procedure, because caution should be made when 
interpreting larvae found in faecal samples that are not sampled immediately after defaecation due 
to risk of invasion of free-living larval S. stercoralis (Zajac & Conboy 2012). S. stercoralis can 
infect multiple hosts, including humans, causing e.g. diarrhoea, pneumonia and dermatitis (Foreyt 
2001). 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
 
 
 
The hookworm eggs found in this study were probably belonging to the genus Uncinaria, because 
they have already been reported in arctic foxes and the eggs are very resistant to cold temperatures 
unlike Ancylostoma caninum (Bowman et al. 2003). Emergence of Ancylostoma caninum can 
therefore be a consequence of warmer temperatures in a changing climate and this can affect the 
health of the arctic foxes, because A. caninum is more pathogenic (Bowman et al. 2003). The 
number of hookworm eggs may have been underestimated due the freezing and the fragility of their 
thin shells (Elmore et al. 2013; Schurer et al. 2014). 
 
Arctic foxes are renowned for being generalist predators (Bræstrup 1941; Macpherson 1969; 
Stroganov 1969; Dalerum & Angerbjörn 2000, Gilg et al. 2006) and the discovery of spurious 
parasite eggs suggest coprophagia or intestinal passing of eggs derived from ingestion of infected 
prey. Pseudoparasites like mites and mite eggs may be indicative of mite infections of the host itself 
or of the prey (Zajac & Conboy 2012). 
 
Some helminths that previously have been reported in arctic foxes were not isolated in this 
coprological study. These include acanthocephalans, trematodes and various cestodes and 
nematodes, e.g. Diphyllobothrium spp. and Crenosoma vulpis (Shilyaeva 1968; Rausch et al. 1983; 
Rausch et al. 1990; Skírnisson et al. 1993; Kapel & Nansen 1996; Aguirre et al. 2000). 
A low prevalence or absence of the parasite species in these areas may explain this, as well as 
absence of certain intermediate hosts of parasites, e.g. trematodes were not reported in arctic foxes 
from East Greenland, but was found in West Greenland (Kapel & Nansen et al. 1996). Furthermore, 
it is likely that the sieving method was not optimal to isolate the heavy eggs of acanthocephalans 
and trematodes, even though it has proven possible to detect trematode eggs with this method 
(Mohammad N. S. Al-Sabi DTU Vet, personal communication, September 2014) and with flotation 
(Wolfe et al. 2001). Perhaps a supplementary isolation method, e.g. sedimentation, could have 
detected heavy eggs in these samples. 
 
Despite up to eight years of storage, this study succeeded in isolating numerous parasite eggs, 
especially those of taenidae and ascarididae, in faecal samples of arctic foxes. The findings are 
important, because the storage and isolation methods have shown useful when examining old faecal 
samples and the results can be used to compare parasitism, population dynamic and climate changes 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
 
 
in these areas. Nevertheless, limitations should be considered in interpreting these findings, because 
as discussed above reasons for the lack of trematode and acanthocephalan eggs have not been 
investigated thoroughly. In addition, strongylid eggs have been shown to be fragile against freezing 
as storage method (Foreyt 1986, Schurer et al. 2014) and therefore underestimation of those 
parasites could have happened. Supplementary isolation method could have revealed more various 
endoparasitic species in the faecal samples. The mesh sizes of the used sieves were 20-22μm and 
some endoparasites that can be detected using flotation, e.g. Sarcocystis sp. (7-22x3-15μm), 
Toxoplasma gondii and Neospora caninum (11-14x9-11μm) (Zajac & Conboy 2012), are too small 
to be retained in the sieve. 
Another obstacle was the use of faecal samples instead of the more sensitive method of adult worm 
isolation from organs (Wolfe et al. 2001; Saeed & Kapel 2006; Al-Sabi et al. 2010). Adult worms 
found in the host indicate a real infection, whereas eggs and larvae found in faecal samples can be 
explained by either a real infection, invasion of free-living nematodes, coprophagia or even 
cannibalism (Zajac & Conboy 2012). Fluctuating egg/larvae shedding and the length of prepatent 
period could also influence the results of coprological methods (Wolfe et al. 2001; Oliveira et al. 
2006; Al-Sabi et al. 2007). Furthermore, some parasites, e.g. Trichinella spp. and Dioctophyme 
renale are not located in the gastrointestinal system of the hosts and thus cannot be isolated when 
examining faecal samples (Roberts & Janovy 2000). Therefore, the currently reported negative 
presence of parasites in faecal samples may be inconclusive. 
Limitations of the sampling and storage method should also be considered in the interpretation of 
the results, as the occurrence of parasites probably is accounted for more than once, because some 
of the faecal samples from the same den could originate from the same foxes. Faecal samples from 
KV in 2007 were stored with a different preservation type that could have influenced the results. 
Other limitations include differences in sample sizes between years and localities, sampling was 
only performed in May-August (seasonal variation in parasitism), the random exclusion of some 
faecal samples from the same den and date, and lastly the pooling of data in the statistical analyses. 
Pooling of data can be useful when dealing with small sample sizes, but one must consider that 
some differences may be disguised when pooling data. 
However, the strength of this study include that several other ecological investigations were 
performed in the same areas making it possible to gather multiple dataset in broader perspectives 
and in relation to other ecological issues. 
 
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
 
 
In this study, we found faecal stages of cestodes and nematodes that previously have been reported 
in other parasitological studies of arctic foxes, and moreover we discovered a nematode, Eucoleus 
boehmi, never described in arctic foxes before.  
ZV and KV were relatively similar regarding to occurrence and abundance of parasites in faeces, 
but yearly differences and differences between fox dens found in this study were primarily driven 
by one parasite species, T. leonina, which can be transmitted directly between foxes or through the 
intermediate host, the collared lemming. 
We have shown that old faecal samples stored in 70% ethanol at -20°C can be subjected for 
isolation of especially ascarididae and taenidae using a modified sieving method. This is functional 
and useful knowledge for field studies on wild life, where non-invasive coprological methods are 
the most convenient or perhaps the only possible choice. 
Climate changes in arctic are expected to induce invasion of new plants, herbivores and insects 
(Klein et al. 2008) together with a northward shift in distribution of marine mammals and sea birds 
(Gilg et al. 2012). Along with more alternative food sources comes less predation of current prey 
(Gilg et al. 2012) and this could eventually increase the occurrence of new and current parasites 
with indirect cycle. Endoparasites with direct cycle that have environmental stages are also 
expected to experience improvement of conditions in relation to egg/larvae survival and 
development.  
However, the dispersal of parasites by the arctic fox is expected to decrease due to less migration on 
sea ice. Studies have shown that response of the reproduction of arctic foxes is connected to 
lemming abundance in KV, but is not in ZV, probably due to alternative diet choices. In the case of 
ZV, the reproduction of arctic foxes has become stabile past years (Schmidt et al. 2012). Perhaps in 
future, the reproduction of the arctic foxes in ZV may increase due to the expected increases in food 
sources and thereby increasing transmission.  
Taken together, the climate changes in Northeastern Greenland are expected to affect the parasite 
composition of arctic foxes, and it will be dependent on several factors including the biology of the 
parasite species and the hosts, the local environmental changes, etc. 
In conclusion, this retrospective study provides an insight into some endoparasitic helminth species 
present in faeces of arctic foxes from Northeastern Greenland and thus can be used in future 
parasitological studies, especially in relation to climate change and population dynamics.

PART IV: Overall Conclusion and Perspectives 
4.1 Conclusion 
This master’s thesis has given an insight into the presence of some endoparasitic helminths in 
faeces of arctic foxes (Vulpes lagopus) from Northeastern Greenland, more precisely Zackenberg 
Valley in 2006-2008 and Karupelv Valley in 2006-2007. This was accomplished by performing a 
modified sieving method on samples preserved in 70% ethanol at -20°C. 
Larvae of Strongyloides stercoralis and eggs of taenidae, Toxascaris leonina, other ascarididae, 
Eucoleus boehmi, Physaloptera spp. and hookworm were found, and all but one, E. boehmi, have 
previously been reported in arctic foxes. No specific methods were used to isolate and identify 
protozoans, and the modified sieving method could have been insufficient in finding eggs of 
trematodes and acanthocephalans. 
Zackenberg and Karupelv Valley did not differ with respect to the occurrence and abundance of 
found endoparasites, probably due to the similar animal species present. The only year-to-year 
differences observed were between 2006 to both 2007 and 2008 in Zackenberg Valley and were 
contributed primarily by T. leonina. This could be linked to both intermediate host abundance and 
the biology of T. leonina eggs. 
The occurrence and abundance of the observed endoparasites were not found to be significantly 
different between arctic fox dens with pups and arctic fox dens without pups. However, differences 
were significant between some arctic fox dens and again T. leonina was a main contributor. The 
differences could be due to local environmental differences affecting viability and development of 
eggs or genetic variations in the infectivity of the parasites or genetic variations in the susceptibility 
of the hosts. 
Furthermore, possible consequences of the climate changes (reduced sea ice near coast of 
Greenland, warmer temperatures and more precipitation) were discussed with respect to the each 
parasitic species found, because expected changes in climate must be dependent on the parasitic 
species due to different life cycles, environmental parasitic stage survival, presence of intermediate 
hosts, if any, etc. Reduced sea ice could also have an impact on the difference between the two 
localities, because Karupelv Valley is located on Traill Island and may become isolated if sea ice 
disappears resulting in changes in parasitism. 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
 
 
The detection of parasites with zoonotic potential is important, because the scientists stationed in 
these localities then must take precautions where a risk of transmission exists, e.g. handling faecal 
samples or when arctic foxes defecate on the belongings of the scientists. 
 
4.2 Perspectives 
Trematodes and acanthocephalans were not recorded in this study, but the reason for this remains 
unclear: either the isolation method used was insufficient in isolating heavy eggs or no infection 
existed in the examined faecal samples of the arctic foxes. Consequently, supplementary isolation 
methods, e.g. sedimentation, should be applied on the remaining faecal samples of this study. 
Furthermore, a clarification of this is especially important in relation to climate changes due to a 
collapse of the regular fluctuations of the collared lemmings, so that the cycles have become 
prolonged as well as the maximum densities are reduced (Gilg et al. 2009; Schmidt et al. 2012). 
This reduced availability of lemmings as food source probably leads to a shift in feeding habits of 
the arctic fox (Bræstrup 1941), towards a diet with more fish, ungulates, etc. In addition, climate 
changes are expected to increase numbers of arthropods and marine mammals (Gilg et al. 2012), all 
of which could serve as alternative food sources. Arthropods are intermediate hosts in 
acanthocephalans and second intermediate hosts for some trematode species, and fish are second 
intermediate hosts in other trematode species (Stunkard 1930; Wooton 1957; Bock 1984; Roberts & 
Janovy 2000). In conclusion, the shift in diet could be reflected on the parasite composition, 
resulting in acanthocephalans, trematodes or newcomers becoming more significant in arctic foxes 
in the future. It is therefore essential to clarify the reasons for not finding them in this study.  
 
Eggs of taenidae were not identified to a species level. The results of the on-going molecular typing 
are interesting in relation to the intermediate hosts, because taeniid infection in these hosts can have 
a pathological significance (Jones & Pybus 2001). Could the collapse of the lemmings be partly 
caused by taenidae? Furthermore the zoonotic potential of some taenidae also justifies the 
identification of these eggs. 
 
This study did not pay attention to protozoans. Other studies have reported these in arctic foxes 
using different detection methods, including faecal flotation, immunofluorescent assays (IFA), 
serology etc. (Eaton & Secord 1979; Skírnisson et al. 1993; Sørensen et al. 2005; Aguirre et al. 
==PAGE:59==
58 
 
 
 
2000; Mejler et al. 2011; Elmore et al. 2013) (Appendix Protozoans). Detection of protozoans could 
thus be included in future endoparasitic surveys of arctic foxes from Northeastern Greenland. 
 
Health evaluations (haemogram, biochemistry, bacterial and parasitological examinations) of arctic 
fox pups have been performed in Sweden by trapping them, taking the necessary samples and then 
releasing them again (Aguirre et al. 2000). This could be interesting to do on arctic foxes in all ages 
in Greenland to obtain knowledge beyond parasitology, as impacts of climate changes should not be 
restrained to one field of science. Furthermore, samples would be fresh and we would know more 
about the individual fox, e.g. gender and age. 
 
It would also be interesting to investigate if the faecal samples of the sledge dogs from Daneborg 
contained parasites, even though they had anthelmintic treatment prior to sampling. Such an 
investigation would help to understand if they are part of the transmission and dispersal of parasites 
in these areas. 
 
In future parasitological investigations of arctic foxes or other animal species in such remote areas 
as the North and East National Park of Greenland, both the storage and isolation methods used in 
this study (i.e. 70% ethanol at -20°C and modified sieving method), supplemented by other 
coprological and molecular typing methods, could be applied.
