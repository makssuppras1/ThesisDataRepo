Abstract
As power systems worldwide increasingly integrate renewable energy sources and energy storage
technologies, system operators face significant challenges in ensuring a reliable and cost-effective
balance between supply and demand. A key aspect of this process involves determining appropriate
frequency restoration reserve (FRR) capacities to handle real-time imbalances caused by generator
outages, uncertainties of renewable production or fluctuations in demand. A common method to
approach this issue is by solving the Unit Commitment and Economic Dispatch (UC-ED) problem
from a day-ahead perspective, as well as for real-time adjustments.
Traditionally, FRR management has focused on conventional generation, but with the increasing
presence of limited energy reservoir (LER) units, there is a need to rethink how reserves are allo-
cated and utilized. The challenges lie in ensuring that reserves are optimally allocated to minimize
costs without sacrificing energy security, and in ensuring that storage resources are used efficiently
while taking their limited availability into consideration. Thus, the first part of this thesis builds
upon the foundational work of a UC-ED model from a day ahead perspective, extending it to in-
clude LER units such as battery energy storage systems (BESS), as well as aggregators and demand
response units. Taking a deterministic approach in solving the UC-ED problem, this section focuses
mainly on frequency reserve dimensioning of automatic and manual frequency restoration reserves
(aFRR and mFRR) under the consideration of a high grid share of renewable energy. This model is
designed for and adapted to the Danish power grid, incorporating the specific requirements set by
the Danish Transmission System Operator (TSO), Energinet.
The second part of this thesis focuses on the real-time power balancing aspect of the Danish en-
ergy market. In other words, given the optimal dispatch of the day-ahead market, the model now
addresses optimizing the real-time power adjustments within the ancillary services and FRR activa-
tions. As with the previous section, the emphasis is on the integration of LERs - BESS, aggregators
and demand response units - in response to renewable energy and demand fluctuations. However,
this model instead applies a stochastic approach to take into consideration the limited and uncertain
capacity and activation availability of these FRR effectively. Furthermore, given Denmark’s high
penetration of wind power and increasing share of solar power, this research integrates probabilistic
methods to handle uncertainties in wind and solar power, as well as demand.
The extended framework demonstrates how advanced FRR management strategies can optimize the
use of flexible and limited energy reservoir resources, reduce operational costs, and enhance system
reliability in systems with high shares of renewable energy and storage systems. The results of this
research are expected to offer practical insights for TSOs to better integrate renewable energy and
storage into their daily operations while maintaining a stable, cost-efficient, and reliable grid.

Section I: Introduction

Introduction
Climate change presents a pressing global challenge, driven primarily by greenhouse gas emissions
from fossil fuel use in energy production and industry, which together account for about two-thirds
of global emissions (Abidi and Nsaibi, 2024; Olabi and Abdelkareem, 2022; Russo et al., 2022). As
a result, current trajectories toward carbon neutrality foresee that by 2050, 90 percent of electricity
generation will need to come from renewable energy (RE) (Russo et al., 2022). However, the shift
to renewable energy introduces new challenges. These occur mainly due to the inherent variability
and uncertainty in power output from sources like wind and solar, due to their dependence on
weather and climatic conditions. This variability creates operational and financial uncertainties for
grid operators and investors, since forecasting renewable generation is complicated by factors such
as weather prediction errors and system reliability concerns (Abidi and Nsaibi, 2024; Olabi and
Abdelkareem, 2022).
Thus, as the share of RE sources increases, so does the strain on the power grid to provide storage
facilities and ancillary services to balance out the variable output of technologies such as wind and
solar power. This is particularly true for countries such as Denmark, which over the recent decades
have rapidly expanded the share of renewable energy in their power grid (International Renewable
Energy Agency (IRENA), 2024). This has had major effects on electricity markets, grid operations
and long-term planning. The increasing reliance on these variable RE sources has made capacity
markets, and frequency restoration reserves (FRR) even more important for energy flow management
and power plant revenues (Abidi and Nsaibi, 2024; Fusco et al., 2023; Roald et al., 2023).
As a result, optimizations of the short-term operations of dispatchable power plants (fossil-fueled,
RE with storage, aggregators, etc.) have also now increasingly taken a combined approach of con-
sidering not only one market structure such as the day-ahead energy market, but also grid bal-
ancing markets to effectively adapt to real-time operations (Fusco et al., 2023; Roald et al., 2023;
Tsaousoglou, 2023). Furthermore, research of the impact of grid-scale energy storage systems (ESS)
within these market structures and optimization models have also gained traction in literature and
the industry (Nourizadeh and Nazar, 2025; Fusco et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2019).
In terms of such ESSs, limited energy reservoirs (LER) such as battery energy storage systems (BESS)
are increasingly seen as essential to facilitate grid resilience in response to the higher share of RE
in the electricity grid (Abidi and Nsaibi, 2024; Olabi and Abdelkareem, 2022). As their capacities
are expected to continue to increase to hundreds of MWhs, their role in balancing peak loads and
system frequency in response to renewable power generation is expected to become more relevant
(Nourizadeh and Nazar, 2025; Roald et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2019).
Nevertheless, the effective integration of LERs in the energy markets and real-time dispatch re-
mains a challenge due to their limited capacity and availability across time once this limit reached
(Feng et al., 2024;Tsaousoglou, 2023). While optimization methods that account for uncertainty
have advanced quickly, with better models and algorithms making these techniques more practical

for real-world systems, significant shortcomings remain. These mainly revolve around the issue of
unpredictability of RE generation under their increasing share in the power grid and the limited
consideration of the role of LERs as grid-balancing and FRR units (Tsaousoglou, 2023; Roald et al.,
2023).
This thesis deems to address this issue through challenging the current conventions of storage in-
tegration and FRR commitment in optimization models by adopting a novel approach based on
Tsaousoglou (2023) and applying it to the Danish power grid. The outline of this thesis is thus as
follows: the continuation of Section 1 below will provide the background necessary to put the pro-
posed optimization approach into context, both in terms of a wider understanding of the real-world
structures and the wider scientific literature. Section 2 presents the mathematical model that forms
the backbone of this analysis, while Section 3 delves into the main set-up of the simulations, i.e.
the steps taken to implement and test the model. Section 4 presents the main results and findings,
while Section 5 summarizes the main limitations and conclusions that were reached throughout this
project.
1.1
Background
The following section is intended to give a brief overview over the most important concepts and
systems mentioned in this paper. First of all, the discussed markets. While several different markets
exist in the time span between unit commitment and real-time dispatch, the main focus of this
paper is the day-ahead market (DA) and real-time (RT) adjustment operations. The role of the DA
market is to balance the electricity supply and demand across the power system. Through a closed
auction format, it allows its participants to submit bids and offers for electricity delivery over the
upcoming day (Nordpool, 2025). Each trading day begins with the publication of available grid and
interconnector capacities. These capacities are provided by the Transmission System Operator (TSO)
which for the case of the Danish grid is Energinet. Market participants are then given a two-hour
window to place their final bids for the next day’s energy delivery (Nordpool, 2025). The submitted
orders are the input into the pan-European Single Day-Ahead Coupling system. This system uses an
algorithm, which is known as Euphemia (short for European Hybrid Electricity Market Integration
Algorithm) in order to match supply and demand across multiple bidding zones (Entsoe, 2023).
The objective of the matching process is to maximize overall social welfare while meeting the
physical network constraints. The market equilibrium is solved for each hour of the delivery day.
The intersection of aggregated supply and demand curves —constructed from submitted orders—
determines the market clearing price and accepted volumes in each bidding zone. The reasons as to
why there is the need for a day ahead market are numerous. Some of them are based on physical
considerations, for instance many conventional generation units such as coal power plants have var-
ious operational limits, thus their operational schedule needs to be known in advance. Additionally,
financial markets help improve market competitiveness and mitigate market power. Through such
a market, the influence of market participants is limited as to how they can manipulate the market
clearing outcomes to their own benefit.
2 of 57
==PAGE:16==
Optimization models that deal with the DA market usually define Unit Commitment and Economic
Dispatch (UC-ED) problems. UC-ED is a fundamental optimization challenge faced by the system
operators such as Energinet. It involves determining which units should be utilized and to what
extend, in order to cover the forecast demand and minimizing operational costs, while ensuring that
technical and regulatory constraints are still satisfied.
After the day-ahead market is closed, there are several other markets that serve to align forecasts
closer to real-time conditions, but usually, these still cannot fully eliminate all imbalances that may
occur during actual operation. The imbalances could happen for a variety of reasons, such as fore-
cast errors, unforeseen generator outages, or fluctuations in RE production. Therefore, to maintain
system stability and manage unforeseen events in real time, system operators rely on ancillary ser-
vices. This thesis’ focus is on the frequency supporting services, which enable the TSO to preserve
the grid frequency at 50 Hz. The main categories of the FRR are the the Frequency containment
reserve (FCR), Automatic frequency restoration reserve (aFRR) and Manual frequency restoration
reserve (mFRR) (Ramboll, 2019). FCR is the fastest of the available reserves with a reaction time
of a few seconds. It is also known as primary or regulating reserve. The aFRR or secondary reserve
has a reaction in a few seconds but full response within 5-10 minutes. Finally, the mFRR has a
full response within 30-60 minutes and is a comparatively slow frequency supporting reserve. These
activation times are also depicted in Figure 1, although notably, the reserve replacement process is
not implemented in Europe. The focus of this thesis will be the aFRR and mFRR type of reserves.
Figure 1: Graphical representation of the reserve types and their activation time (Ramboll, 2019).
In terms of FRRs, the aforementioned limited energy reservoirs have gained more importance in
recent years. Energinet (2025a) defines LERs as units and portfolios that due to their technical lim-
itations can have their energy reservoirs depleted over a short period of time. Depending on which
type of unit and as what kind of frequency reserve it is supposed to serve, a LER unit is designated
to be able to sustain a full frequency reserve activation over a fixed short number of concurrent hours
3 of 57
==PAGE:17==
without switching modes (e.g. switching between charging and discharging). It is capable of switch-
ing modes over longer periods of time, but cannot operate both modes simultaneously (i.e. cannot
charge and discharge at the same time) (Energinet, 2025a). For the relevant aFRR and mFRR of
this thesis, these designated consecutive hours range from 4 to 8 hours, respectively. As might be
gleaned from this description, while LERs is an umbrella term, it currently mainly encompassed
battery energy storage systems.
1.2
Literature Review
The concepts and optimization of the day-ahead markets, real-time operations, ESS units and FRR
have been widely explored in scientific literature. For instance, the importance of integration and
consideration of ESSs in the day-ahead unit commitment or real time dispatch problem has been
extensively investigated in the context of high grid shares of renewable energy (Roald et al., 2023).
The work that this thesis is primarily based on, the study by Tsaousoglou (2023) focused on the
integration of BESSs and effective resource commitment under a chance-constrained UC-ED problem
methodology. One of the issues that this paper focused on was the fact that if a BESS unit is needed
in order to cover the demand of upwards or downwards reserve in one time slot, that amount of
stored energy will be depleted and it might be impossible to cover the needs for the upcoming time
slots. To address this issue, a new concept of implementing reserve commitment across multiple time
slots was introduced to ensure that the demand for upward and downward reserves is met with a
certain threshold probability (Tsaousoglou, 2023). A similar approach is adopted in this thesis for
its first half, the DA model.
Moreira et al. (2024) also recognized the significant challenges that the increased uncertainty of
a high RE grid penetration can cause in terms of day-ahead commitment problems. The researchers
proposed a two-stage stochastic DA problem based on the Conditional Value-at-Risk methodology.
They effectively integrated BESSs and account for optimized cost, unit and reserve commitment
under a Column-and Constraint Generation algorithmic framework (Moreira et al., 2024).
Zhou et al. (2021) investigated a similar scenario, where the unit commitment in the DA multi-
stage stochastic problem had to effectively integrate energy storage with renewable uncertainty. Their
concern was the effective dispatch to avoid infeasibility and ensure non-anticipativity when the un-
certainties were revealed. As such, they proposed two mixed integer linear programming (MILP)
models, explicit, which assumed an affine relationship between variables and realizations; and im-
plicit, which instead focused on future feasibility through emphasizing unit operating ranges (Zhou
et al., 2021).
Apart from a single stochastic DA model approach, several researchers have also emphasized the
importance of multiple focal points when optimizing a multi-stage stochastic model for effective re-
newable and storage integration (Roald et al., 2023). Khaloie et al. (2020) presented a multi-objective
mixed-integer offering framework using lexicographic optimization and hybrid augmented-weighted
epsilon-constraint methods to determine optimal unit and reserve commitment, costs and expected
emission of thermal generators (Khaloie et al., 2020). Eslahi et al. (2021) similarly focused on a
4 of 57
==PAGE:18==
MILP bi-objective framework to effectively integrate BESSs in response to wind power uncertainty.
Applying the epsilon-constraint technique to find a Pareto frontier to minimize both costs and emis-
sions, their stochastic model addressed the necessity of optimal storage unit operation in the DA
market (Eslahi et al., 2021).
Nevertheless, in past studies, there has arguably been a lack of comprehensive optimal integration
of ESSs framework across market mechanisms. While a multitude of studies have investigated the
optimal UC under uncertainty in the separate energy markets, they rarely consider the interactions
between the markets, and how decision-making processes across them impact each other (Roald et al.,
2023). Conventional stochastic models also do not incorporate new information through updates of
uncertainties (Huang et al., 2023;Al-Lawati et al., 2021;Nasrolahpour et al., 2018). Recognizing this
issue, several researchers have decided to focus on optimal integration of renewable uncertainty in the
energy markets and/or the increased incorporation of BESSs. For instance, Wang et al. (2017) focused
on a look-ahead strategy for BESSs in the DA market mainly in terms of their State of Charge (SoC).
They presented bi-level optimization, later transformed into a single-level MILP model through
Karush–Kuhn–Tucker conditions, encompassing both a ramp-constrained multi-period market and
storage bid optimization (Wang et al., 2017). Being in the same frame of mind, Huang et al. (2023)
chose a multi-period look-ahead horizon approach to address this issue. They developed two bi-level
optimization models, strategic and integrated, to incorporate the intertemporal operation of ESSs
effectively (Huang et al., 2023).
Nasrolahpour et al. (2018) also emphasized the importance of considering market interactions
in terms of optimal ESS bidding strategies. They developed a bi-level complementarity method,
comprising the DA and balance markets to determine optimal trading actions for ESS in pool mar-
kets. For the DA and balancing settlements, the researchers chose to include ancillary services in
the form of reserve commitments and the real-time realization of uncertainties was modeled using
scenario generation from DA forecasts (Nasrolahpour et al., 2018). Similarly, Al-Lawati et al. (2021)
proposed two models for RE generators participating in all energy markets. One being a two-stage
stochastic optimization model that solves scenario in-/dependent decisions simultaneously, while the
other is an aggregation of four two-stage stochastic optimization models, feeding into the subsequent
models (Al-Lawati et al., 2021).
In regard to a combined approach, several researchers have also looked into an integrated approach
of both day-ahead and real-time models encompassing renewable and ESSs considerations. Feng
et al. (2024) proposed a consideration of a renewable-thermal-storage generation base through an
integrated three stage DA scheduling and RT dispatch methodology. Applying renewable interval
predictions, on–off statuses of thermal generators and a high voltage DC delivery plan in the day-
ahead, the intraday stage created flexible dispatch plans for non/-renewable units. Finally, in the
real-time, the researchers applied an online quantile policy to observe and transition from the realized
uncertainties to the real-time dispatch (Feng et al., 2024). Pozo et al. (2014), while in agreement with
the same problem statement, adopted a slightly different approach to effectively integrate storage
units to account for renewable uncertainty. They applied a stochastic unit commitment for ideal and
generic storage units in an integrated energy and reserves commitment and RT model (Pozo et al.,
2014).
5 of 57
==PAGE:19==
However, BESSs are not the only storage medium that has been investigated in terms of dealing
with the increased uncertainty of higher shares of renewables in both DA and RT considerations.
Khatami et al., 2020 have proposed Compressed air energy storage as a medium to provide large
scale grid flexibility. By developing a look-ahead horizon MILP optimization model for both day-
ahead and real-time energy and reserve markets, the researches attempted to effectively incorporate
and maximize usage of compressed air energy storage in response to renewable uncertainty, given
expected future profit (Khatami et al., 2020).
Furthermore, multiple studies emphasized the importance of integrating effective demand re-
sponse (DR) and flexible operation in the form of aggregators into DA unit commitment and real-
time models. Hermans et al. (2018) point out the importance of flexible generation and demand-side
management, as well as incorporation of storage systems to cope with large grid share of variable
and uncertain RE resources. While the researchers included many such components in their unit
commitment model and incorporate scenario generation to reflect wind and solar uncertainty, their
ultimate focus lay more singularly combined-cycle gas turbines as the main providers of conventional
flexible power, investigating the need for their fast start-up capabilities (Hermans et al., 2018).
Chen et al. (2021) underlined the importance of consumer-sited instead of grid-level ESSs to
effectively cope with intermittent supply. As such, the researchers developed and integrated power
generation and customer-sited BESSs expansion planning model for generator operation and load
dispatch optimization to explore the impact of this area on grid level operations and consumer level
demand (Chen et al., 2021). Yang et al. (2019) also focused ESSs in terms of demand response,
underlining economic feasibility and physical reliability of the grid. The researchers designated load
periods in an optimal period partitioning model, applying a PSO based optimization algorithm, while
also creating a demand management model that incorporates energy storage systems (Yang et al.,
2019).
In terms of the effective integration of aggregated units and systems and their benefits to grid oper-
ation under renewable uncertainty,Basu (2023) implemented a virtual power plant (VPP) model to
consider renewable uncertainties in terms of electrical, thermal and cooling demands. The researcher
applied several optimizers such bottlenose dolphin optimizer to effectively schedule conventional,
renewable and storage units as well as DR mechanisms to meet and flatten demand curves (Basu,
2023).
Fusco et al. (2023) also explored optimization of DA UC-ED and the ancillary markets
through conventional and VPPs that encompass both thermal units as well as BESSs and PV sys-
tems. The researchers focused on developing a multi-stage stochastic MILP with binary recourse
and uncertainty realization through scenario generation (Fusco et al., 2023).
Hu et al. (2024) approached this topic from a slightly different angle, investigating how aggregated
electric vehicle systems could be used to offset the increasing share of variable RE sources. Present-
ing decentralized clearing mechanism based on a multi-attribute evaluation model, the researchers
investigated the optimal bidding strategies in the DA market, while simultaneously accounting for
real time performance through an Entropy-Analytic Hierarchy Process (Hu et al., 2024). Nourizadeh
and Nazar (2025) studied an optimal DA UC-ED problem in terms of DR units and electric vehicle
aggregators. Their model presented the scheduling of active units and the competition among DR
units in terms of a three-level framework, investigating the impact of power exchange and energy
6 of 57
==PAGE:20==
security/sufficiency. Zhang et al. (2022) similarly performed a study in terms of DA and reserve
market flexible unit commitment of VPPs, aggregating conventional, renewable and storage units.
The researchers developed a stochastic adaptive robust optimization with a modified Benders dual
decomposition, in addition to distinguishing between exogenous uncertainties of renewable genera-
tion and market prices and endogenous uncertainties of real-time reserve activation (Zhang et al.,
2022).
As can be seen from this brief overview, a major emphasis in the scientific literature is the investi-
gation of a single type of market structure or LER unit. Even for a combined market approach or a
proposed method for integrating both DA and RT models, the focus is usually on one type of ESS,
neglecting the reality of the various LERs already incorporated into power grids and the individual
differing technical requirements and limitations of such units in terms of their potential as FRR and
role in coping with RE uncertainty.
1.3
Problem & Research Statement
In light of these investigations, it can thus be argued that there is a lack of studies that wholly com-
prise the various LER elements into a comprehensive model that not only deals with dispatch unit
commitment but also FRR commitment and the real-time effectiveness of these decisions. The real-
world applicability of these models can also be occasionally called into question. After discussions
with the Danish system operator Energinet, it was confirmed that the UC-ED problem is approached
by the TSO through inspecting the market needs and procuring reserves without the utilization of
a specific optimization model. This is common among system operators, who usually tend to rely
on well established, deterministic models and marginal prices for decision making. Even though
advanced techniques such as stochastic market clearing and chance constrained optimization have
been proposed as demonstrated in the previous section, the reality is that these approaches have not
yet been implemented in practice. The traditional approaches have been effective for conventional
generator dominated systems, but in the new landscape of high RE penetration, these face new
challenges. For instance, allowing storage units to offer their full capacity in one time slot without
taking into consideration the inter-temporal limitations can result in infeasible operations.
In other words, there is a necessity to effectively integrate the various LERs such as BESSs into a UC-
ED model and apply it to RT imbalances, while not only addressing renewable uncertainty, but also
the need to adequately designate sufficient frequency reserves using LERs. Thus, the main objective
of this thesis is to develop a comprehensive deterministic UC-ED model that incorporates BESSs,
aggregators and demand response as FRR and apply it to RT imbalances. These models serve as
an attempt to effectively represent the flexible units employed to cope with high shares of renewable
uncertainty, while simultaneously emphasizing an adequate dimensioning of the frequency reserves
needed to cope with the realization of these uncertainties. The multi-stage stochastic RT model is
addressed through a look-ahead framework with probabilistic methods, i.e. scenario generation, to
investigate the adequacy of the DA scheduling.
This model framework, tested through application to a real-world case study of the Danish
7 of 57
==PAGE:21==
electricity grid, aims to minimize the total operational cost of the power system while ensuring
reliable supply and effective integration of LERs as frequency reserves:
• Day-Ahead Model: In the first stage, a day-ahead unit commitment and economic dispatch
problem is solved.
This stage schedules the on/off status and output levels of generation
units and LERs based on forecasts of demand and RE generation. With the goal to minimize
operation costs, it also determines the allocation of LERs for FRR provision, subject to their
operational constraints and market requirements.
• Real-Time Model: In the second stage, a real-time dispatch model adjusts the set-points and
outputs of all resources to account for actual realizations of demand and renewable generation.
The RT model tests the effectiveness and necessity of the frequency reserve commitments,
ensuring system constraints are satisfied, and operational costs are minimized in the face of
uncertainties.
Mathematically, the problem is formulated first as a deterministic UC-ED for the the day-ahead
schedule and reserve allocations. The second stage performs real-time adjustments to minimize costs
and maintain system reliability, applying a multi-stage stochastic optimization. The objective func-
tion minimizes the start-up/shutdown, operational and maintenance costs and, later in a loss of load
analysis, potential penalty costs for deviations or reserve shortfalls. Constraints include generator
and LER operational limits, reserve requirements and power balance constraints.
In summary, this thesis aims to bridge the gap between theoretical breakthroughs and current op-
erational practices. As such, this integrated approach is supposed to demonstrate and leverages the
flexibility of LERs to provide both energy and FRR services, improving the economic and opera-
tional efficiency of the Danish power system across both day-ahead and real-time horizons. Hence,
this set-up intends to test the following research statement:
The advanced frequency restoration reserve optimization framework demonstrates the improved
performance of such strategies in terms of effective use of flexible resources, reducing operational
costs, and enhancing system reliability in systems with high shares of renewable energy and storage
systems.
1.3.1
System Model
The system under study is the Danish electricity network, which integrates conventional generation
units (such as thermal or gas-fired plants), renewable energy sources (wind, solar), and limited energy
reservoirs — primarily BESSs, aggregators and demand response units. These LER units are not
only used for energy arbitrage but are also considered as frequency restoration reserves, providing
ancillary services to the grid. The system operates in a market environment with both day-ahead
markets and real-time adjustment operations. The day-ahead market is used for unit commitment
and scheduling, while the real-time model addresses deviations from forecasts and ensures system
reliability. Key components of the system model include:
8 of 57
==PAGE:22==
• Generation units: Conventional and renewable sources, each with their own operational con-
straints (e.g., ramp rates, minimum up/down times, maximum/minimum output).
• LERs: Batteries, Aggregators and Demand Response Units. Characterized by power and en-
ergy limits, state-of-charge dynamics, efficiency losses, and constraints on charging/discharging
rates. In addition to the conventional units, these are also eligible to provide frequency reserve
services.
• Load and Renewable Uncertainty: Demand and wind and solar power profiles are forecast for
day-ahead scheduling and updated in real-time.
• Market structure: Participation in both day-ahead market and real-time operations, based on
marginal costs and reserve requirements.
• Network constraints: system reliability requirements, activation and ramping constraints.
This model captures the operational flexibility and constraints of all resources, the uncertainty in
renewable generation and load, and the requirements for frequency reserves and economic operation.
1.3.2
Scope
This thesis focuses on the day-ahead market from the system operator (Energinet) perspective. Af-
ter preliminary discussions with Energinet and data collection, the conditions and pre-qualifications
of the main units within the Danish electrical power grid as well as the scope of this project were
determined.
For a 24-hour time frame, the unit commitment and economic dispatch of conven-
tional, renewable and LER units is optimized, as well as the necessary frequency reserves that can
be provided from these units to cope with the uncertainty of RE production. While certain pre-
qualifications of units to participate in the day-ahead market were taken into consideration, such as
capacities, ramping rates, dis-/charge rates, it was not within the scope of this project to define such
pre-qualifications.
Furthermore, the system only refers to the Danish power grid, i.e. no cross-border trading of
energy is included in this model. Additionally, individual pricing and market zones within Denmark
are also not considered, instead simplifying the system into a single zone. The UC-ED is tested and
applied to a follow-up real-time model, where the system’s capability to deal with realized uncertain-
ties regarding demand, wind and solar production is investigated. Other market structures that exist
up to real-time operations are not within the scope of this thesis. Furthermore, as aforementioned,
not the entire range ancillary services is studied either, the main focus is on aFRR and mFRR.
1.4
Methodology
The following section briefly outlines the steps taken to develop the main models of this project (a
more detailed explanation of these procedures can be found in Section 3). As a first step, the math-
ematical model for the deterministic day-ahead model was developed. The LERs such as BESSs,
aggregators and DR units were researched based on other studies that investigated them and in-
corporated into the model. A rebound effect regarding the demand response was also integrated.
9 of 57
==PAGE:23==
Following the mathematical formulation, an optimization model was built in Python, applying the
Gurobi solver. The previously obtained datasets from Energinet (2025b) regarding demand, solar
and wind forecasts were clustered using the K-Medoids method to obtain a “typical day” forecast
that would then be used in the day-ahead model. This model was tested for several forecasts, wind
profiles and BESS capacities to test its response to different profiles and properly simulate a system
that represents today’s Danish electricity grid. Eventually, the capacities and shares of the incorpo-
rated BESSs and RE sources were gradually increased to simulate the behavior of the Danish grid
under high penetration of uncertain RE and storage units.
After attaining the unit commitment and dispatch decisions from the DA model, these results were
then used as a basis for the real-time model. The connection between the two models is depicted in
Figure 2.
Figure 2: Visual representation of the inputs and outputs of the two main optimization models.
As can be observed from the illustration, the main inputs for the DA model encompass the financial
data of the generation units, which includes fixed, variable, start-up, and shut-down costs. Another
important input is the capacity data of the grid for the corresponding units. The outputs of the DA
model are the dispatch decisions and the FRR allocations for the generation units. All these DA
decisions would then serve as the main basis of the real-time model.
Similar to the DA model, for the RT model, first the mathematical model was built, which was
then programmed in Python. Due to the RT model being a multi-stage stochastic model with a
10 of 57
==PAGE:24==
look-ahead horizon policy and scenarios, the first step in this stage of modeling was the scenario
generation. Based on the forecast data from Energinet, several scenario generation, reduction and
clustering methods were employed, such as Monte-Carlo and bootstrap, to determine the method
best suited for the data distributions of the forecasts. Eventually, for the demand and wind scenarios,
the bootstrap methodology with K-Metroids clustering was chosen, and similar for the solar forecasts,
although these needed to be adapted using a sine distribution to retain its typical bell curve shape.
A similar method, however, without look-ahead horizons, was used to generate the realizations of
the scenarios that would serve as the basis values for scenario generation throughout the modeling
stages. These realizations were then categorized into different RE realizations to simulate the impact
of time slots with a higher or lower RE output: the high and low RE realization cases were chosen
as the individual bootstrapped paths with the highest and lowest time-averaged values, respectively,
while a medium case was chosen based on the one whose average fell closest to the overall mean of
all scenarios.
The scenario trees generated from the bootstrap were then aggregated into scenarios used for the
optimization, i.e. each scenario contained values for wind, solar and demand, with normalized prob-
abilities. As the rolling horizon optimization was built, several policies, i.e. combinations between
number of scenarios, look-ahead horizons and clusters were tested to determine the combination that
would achieve the best objective value. The real-time model was run for 15-min intervals. As Figure
2 shows, the main output of the RT model was the adjustments of the day-ahead commitment and
dispatch in response to the realized renewable and demand uncertainties. Finally, an expected value
(EV) and loss of load (LOL) analysis were carried out.

After the DA model, the next step is the definition of the RT model. As aforementioned, this model
is a multi-stage stochastic model, that relies on probabilistic methods, i.e. generation of scenario

Section III: Simulation Set-up
This section describes the overall set-up of the optimization implementation, i.e. steps taken to solve
the optimization problem in Python (using the Gurobi solver).
3
Data Collection
As this paper encompasses and attempts to represent the entire Danish electrical grid, data collection
in regard to its main production units and production/consumption patterns was a main focus and
continuous process throughout the entire modeling phase. Main points of contact with both Energinet
and Energistyrelsen (DEA) were essential in gathering data on number of available units and their
capabilities. According to the DEA, there are two types of power plants that are utilized for the
production of electricity:
• Thermal electricity generation: Power plants producing electricity with thermal processes
such as steam cycle or internal combustion engines, including combined heat and power plants
(CHP).
• Non-thermal electricity generation:Technologies producing electricity without thermal
processes, such as wind power, solar power or hydroelectric power plants.
In terms of frequency reserves, information on both aFRR and mFRR was required for the
modeling to proceed.
Through direct correspondence with Energinet and several datasets from
Energinet (2025b), such as information on the aFRR and mFRR Capacity Market, as well as Storage
Available Capacity, the main reserve capacity necessities could be determined. To a certain extent,
through some inferences, especially when it came to the demand response units, this data could also
be used to gauge which units are capable of participating to which extent as which kind of reserve.
3.1
Conventional Generators
Conventional generators cover the majority of the available capacity of electricity in Denmark. After
extensive research online and utilizing the reports of Energistyrelsen (2025), it was possible to list
the majority of the different types of thermal generators across the country. Datasets regarding the
main installed power plants in Denmark were obtained based on DEA datasets, including fossil fuel
(coal, natural gas, oil), renewable (biomass, hydro) and combined cycle power plants, heat pumps
and smaller generators, among others, as long as they were capable of participating in and considered
part of the main power grid. The biggest share of thermal generators that contribute in the electricity
market include: Gas turbines and engines, Waste-to-Energy power plants and biomass CHP plants.
The generator dataset was corroborated and compared with datasets made publicly available by
Energinet (2025b), encompassing information on the current power system, production/capacity
installed per municipality and electricity suppliers by grid area. As for consumption data, this was
also obtained from Energinet (2025b) datasets, comprising consumption per grid area, declarations

==PAGE:38==
of the grid mix and the production and consumption settlements. Details on the properties and the
costs of the different types of generators were obtained from the DEA technology catalogs which are
available online publicly (Energistyrelsen, 2025).
3.2
DR-Electric Boilers
Following direct correspondence and discussions with representatives from Energinet, it was deter-
mined that electric boilers are considered the primary component of demand response from their
perspective. A dataset containing details of all electric boilers installed in the system, along with
a list of pre-qualified FRR capacities (which includes electric boilers), was made available to the
authors of this thesis. According to Energinet, electric boilers are active in providing both upwards
and downwards regulation in the aFRR and mFRR. However, while they are capable of delivering
upwards regulation, their potential in this regard is limited to periods of very low spot prices.
Still, the received FRR datasets only displayed overall capacities and were not detailed enough
to precisely determine which electric boilers were contributing to the reserves and to what capacity.
As such, the following approach was adopted: the largest capacity, which was identified to be the
mFRR upward capacity, was determined. Then the largest boilers were sequentially added until
this value was reached. It was assumed that all of these units selected for upwards regulation could
also provide downwards reserves. These selected boilers were considered to be available for demand
response reserves.
Cost data for electric boilers were also sourced from Energistyrelsen (2025)’s catalogues, which
include startup, shutdown, fixed, and variable costs.
3.3
Wind & Solar Power
Utilizing Energinet (2025b)’s datasets, which provides RE capacity data per municipality, it was
possible to access to information regarding the installed capacity for solar panels and wind turbines
(both onshore and offshore installations) in Denmark. The approach initially was to divide the overall
capacity for each municipality by the number of units within that municipality. This method allows
for estimating the mean capacity of each wind turbine and solar farm. However, for this specific
case study, the goal was optimizing the grid from the TSO’s perspective and not necessarily optimal
power flow. Furthermore, the operational costs, such as fixed and variable costs for the RE sources
can from this perspective be consider virtually 0 DKK. Due to this, the inclusion of the wind and
solar farms was simplified to overall onshore and offshore wind power capacities and total solar farm
capacities. As such, instead of modeling wind and solar units as single units, the wind set W and
the solar set Z represent the distributed capacities for onshore and offshore wind, as well as solar,
across all municipalities in Denmark.
Moreover, in order to comprehensively and accurately capture the uncertainty of renewable pro-
duction, the solar and wind (onshore and offshore) forecasts in 5-minute and 1-hour resolutions
provided by Energinet (2025b) were also obtained. Both consumption and renewable forecast data
was sampled for the entire month of February. In accordance to the intent of this thesis to investigate
the effect of a high grid penetration of storage and renewables, the share of solar power was increased

by a rate of 20 percent. Wind power was exempted from this due to its already considerable share
in the Danish power grid.

LER Units & Aggregators

The DA model was implemented for two different approaches: novel - which refers to the model with
reserves across time slots- and conservative - which is the model with the reserve commitment deci-
sions being made per time slot. The two approaches were also investigated for two time resolutions:
one with hourly intervals and one with fifteen-minute intervals. Since the mathematical formulation
remains the same regardless of the resolution, any reference to a time period T applies to both time
intervals. However, due to computational limitations, the DA model was eventually solved only using
the 1-hour resolution. The solution from the DA model then served as input for the RT adjustment
model. For the RT, the 15 minute interval was feasible and adopted for the stochastic, multi-scenario
investigations of renewable and demand uncertainty. To solve these models, several preparations in
regard to the input data had to be made. These are described below.

4.2
From DA to RT
Since the beginning of the year, the Nordpool market model has shifted to a 15 minute resolution. As
a result, the presented real-time model was mainly run for this resolution. Since, as aforementioned,
this fine granularity was not possible to achieve for the DA model due to computational resource
limitations, certain approximations had to be made to transform the 1-hour DA results into 15-min
resolution to be used as an input to the RT model. The main assumption was that the demand and
the power provided by main production units could be split into sections of 4. Nevertheless, in order
to achieve a high accuracy and compatibility with the RT model, a slightly more detailed approach
was adopted. For the renewables, the 5-min forecasts used as the basis for the 1-hour forecast in the
DA model was aggregated to 15 min and used instead of simply dividing their dispatch by 4. While
the forecast demand was assumed to be four quarters of the original hour, the rest of the production
units were divided by the fraction that was closest to 4 while ensuring that based on the changed
renewable dispatch, demand for the 15 min time slots could still be met.
4.3
RT-Scenario Generation & Reduction
In stochastic optimization problems, the probability distribution of specific parameters can be ap-
proximated using a variety of possible scenarios. These scenarios are generated sampling historical
data of the corresponding parameter. In order to solve the stochastic optimization problem numeri-
cally, a deterministic equivalent problem is build with a finite number of scenarios. In theory, a large
number of scenarios will always capture the distribution of the given parameter, but also increases
the complexity and the solving time of the model. Thus, the scenarios generated need to be clustered
into a smaller and representative set of possible realizations.
A wide range of algorithms and methods are available for the clustering of scenarios. One of
the most common used is K-means, which groups data by assigning observations to clusters based
on proximity to the cluster’s center.
However, according to Jin and Han (2010), the K-means
clustering method is sensitive to outliers, since the mean value can be heavily influenced by extreme
observations. K-medoids is a variation of the K-means, which is better at handling such outliers.
Unlike K-means which uses the average point as a cluster, K-Medoids utilizes an actual observation
27 of 57
==PAGE:41==
of the dataset, to serve as the representative center. This point, known as Medoid, is the one most
centrally located, minimizing the sum of distances to all other points in the cluster.
For the purpose of this thesis, the stochastic parameters for which scenarios were generated are the
offshore and onshore wind and solar power and the load. Based on the forecast data from Energinet,
several scenario generation, reduction and clustering methods were employed, such as Monte-Carlo
and bootstrap, to determine the method best suited for the data distributions of the forecasts. The
wind power and the demand were simulated similarly using the bootstrap for scenario generation
and the K-Medoids algorithm for clustering. As for the solar power, the scenario generation was
also simulated with the bootstrap method, but with a key difference in order to simulate a realistic
behavior of solar radiation.
4.3.1
Wind Power & Demand
As already mentioned, the wind power forecast for both onshore and offshore, and the demand were
generated using the bootstrap method, which generates scenarios by resampling historical data. This
enables simulation of multiple realizations of future scenarios by randomly selecting samples from
past observations. There are two ways of sampling using the bootstrap method, parametric and
non-parametric. For parametric bootstrap, it is necessary to fit the historical data into a known
distribution and then sample synthetic data from this generated distribution.
This approach is
useful, when there is strong knowledge about the distribution of the samples. On the other hand,
non-parametric distribution requires no assumption about the historical distribution. In this case,
the resampling is performed directly from the corresponding dataset and is optimal to use when the
distribution is unknown or hard to define. In this thesis, the non-parametric approach through block
sampling was implemented.
In time series forecasting, both methods of the bootstrap can be implemented. It can be applied
to provide a distribution of historical data rather than a single estimate by fitting ARIMA mod-
els and provide a variety of future realizations. In practice, non-parametric block bootstrapping is
commonly used, since it can provide similar results without the need of fitting the data into a distri-
bution and building a forecast model. The idea is to break the series into roughly equal-length blocks
of consecutive observations, to resample the block with replacement, and then to paste the blocks
together. A ”look-back” time step is defined and the model chooses randomly a block between the
maximum value of the look-back time step and the most current observation. This way it is certain
that the generated scenarios are sampled from a relatively relevant block.
For the purpose of this thesis, a scenario tree was created to simulate the possible future realizations
of the corresponding value. Applying the non-parametric bootstrap method, the process unfolded as
follows: firstly, the ”root” value was defined, as the latest sample in the dataset. Then a range of the
sample was chosen as look-back hours. The smaller the number of this range, the more recent obser-
vations were used for the sample. Then a random choice of a block of observations was performed,
whose time steps were included in the look-back hours. The first value of the block was assigned
as the first possible path of the tree. This procedure was then repeated for as many scenarios as
were defined to be generated. Once the large number of scenarios was generated, the clustering was
performed using the K-Medoids algorithm. This process was repeated for the number of look-ahead
28 of 57
==PAGE:42==
horizons, which is how far in the future the possible realizations were estimated.
A more specific example of this process is visually depicted in Figure 3. Starting with the ”root”
value and based on the historical data, 100 new scenarios are generated. These scenarios are then
clustered into 3 Medoids (or branches), with each cluster assigned a probability (πi) based on the
number of scenarios that fall within it. This process is repeated for the number of time steps defined
by the look-ahead horizon.

Solar Power
The solar power available for generation is highly dependent of the time of the day. It is inherently
periodic and it can be approximated by using a sinusoidal function. For this purpose, the block
sampling bootstrap is implemented with a key alteration in how the future states of the solar power
are generated.
The solar power timeseries can be observed in Figure 4. The plot shows a consistent daily pattern.
Solar power values are close to zero during night time and peaking in the middle of the day. The
primary difference is observed in the magnitude of the peak, which seems to vary depending on the
day. After plotting the solar power timeseries, it became clear that in order to generate realistic solar
scenarios it was essential to have zero values at both the beginning and the end of the 24 hours of the
day. To achieve this, a ”daylight window” was implemented, defined from 5:00 AM until 5:00 PM
local time. For the periods outside of this window, the bootstrap method was applied, but sampling
only occurred for time steps corresponding to the specific times of the day. This ensured that all
generated scenarios during these off-hours have values of zero or close to zero.

assumption that this incurs high penalty costs.

5.1
Proposed Intertemporal Reserve Commitment Approach
The Day Ahead Unit Commitment model was run for 1 typical day within the month of February. As
aforementioned, the main components included non-/renewable thermal power plants (termed here
as conventional generators for convenience), demand response units in the form of electrical boilers,
aggregated grid-connected systems, utility-scale BESSs and utility-scale solar farms connected to
BESSs that would serve as their frequency reserves as well as on- and offshore wind farms. These
were subject to several inter-temporal constraints, such as minimum up- and downtime constraints,
ramping constraints and curtailment constraints. Aggregators could act as either generators or loads
and the activation of DR units would result in a randomized rebound effect occurring that would
add onto the existing load. BESSs were constrained by a minimum/maximum length of charge and
discharge period. The optimization model was solved using the Gurobi solver in Python. Due to the
considerable size of the model, 1025424 continuous and 61896 binary variables, the optimization was
run for an hourly resolution for 521416 ms, and the unit commitment of both conventional dispatch
and frequency reserves was obtained.
As can be seen in Figure 9, renewable energy units-specifically wind and solar farms—are fully
committed in the schedule.
This is because these units enter the market with bids virtually at
zero cost (0 DKK), reflecting their extremely low marginal operating expenses, which makes them
the most cost-effective option and thus prioritized by the optimization algorithm.
Nevertheless,
conventional generation units, such as gas or biomass power plants, are still necessary to cover much
of the remaining load due to the intermittency and variability of renewables, as well as their essential
role in maintaining grid stability and reliability. However, these conventional units are dispatched far
below their available capacity, which is a clear indicator of the high penetration of renewable energy
in the grid and the reduced need for conventional baseload power. Meanwhile, demand response and
aggregator units are consistently utilized throughout the day but are generally not dispatched at
their full capacity, mainly because of their physical and operational limitations. Instead, they serve
primarily as balancing resources, helping to smooth out fluctuations and ensure system reliability.
As for the BESS units, they are only committed sporadically and at opportune hours, rather than
being used continuously or at full capacity. This selective use of BESS reflects their role in exploiting
price differences across hours in terms of their charging and discharging behavior, maximizing their
economic value while supporting the grid’s flexibility and reliability.

Scenario Policy Analysis
The effectiveness of the novel approach is further demonstrated by integrating the DA results into
a multi-stage stochastic optimization model, where real-time adjustments were made based on ac-
tual system conditions. This real-time stochastic framework captures the inherent uncertainties in
renewable generation and load, allowing for more robust and adaptive decision-making. Once the
1-h results of the DA model were subsequently converted to a 15-min resolution through the process
described in the previous section, the real-time model was successfully implemented and was sub-
jected to a comprehensive evaluation across a variety of configurations and operational scenarios. In
particular, the number of scenarios was varied in accordance with different look-ahead horizons and
cluster configurations, while the associated policies were consistently applied to three representative
cases: a low renewable production realization case, a medium realization case, and a high renewable
realization case.
The model simulated across a wide range of time steps extending to midday, thereby ensuring that the
full spectrum of renewable variability characteristic of daytime hours was captured. Figure 17 depict
40 of 57
==PAGE:54==
the average objective value across time slots achieved for different number of scenarios. As expected,
the high renewable realization scenario yielded the lowest objective costs, since renewables are essen-
tially treated as free units from a market bid perspective. Conversely, the scenario characterized by
low renewable output resulted in the highest operational costs. The lowest objective function values
for the three renewable cases were observed in the nine-scenario setup (a look-ahead horizon of 2 and
3 clusters). The next best objective values were found in the 27-scenario case (look-ahead of 3 and 3
branches), with only small differences compared to the 8- and 128-scenario set-ups. The differences in
the average objective values are not particularly significantly high, except in the case of 64 scenarios.
However, the 9-scenario policy performs noticeably better and was thus designated as the best policy.
This is representative of the nature of probabilistic methods in stochastic models. When using such
multi-stage stochastic models, having too few scenarios risks missing important possible futures and
can result in decisions that are not robust to rare but impactful events. Too many scenarios can
make the model computationally intractable and may not meaningfully improve solution quality if
the additional scenarios do not provide new information. This is reflected in the similarity between
the 27 and 125-scenario policies. Any more scenarios became to computationally taxing.
In other words, there is an optimal number of scenarios that balances computational effort with
solution quality—in this case, the 9-scenario set-up achieved the lowest average objective values,
indicating that this configuration successfully captured the most relevant uncertainties without over-
burdening the model with redundant information. This is likely also due to number of scenarios,
generated was limited due to computational tractability. Beyond this point, increasing the number
of scenarios led to only small improvements or, in the case of 64 scenarios, even slightly worse results,
likely due to the inclusion of less representative or noisy scenarios or increased computational insta-
bility. In this case, computational limits played a role in so far, as overly large scenario sets might
have introduced numerical issues or approximation errors, undermining the stability and quality of
the results.
Nevertheless, these results indicate that the scenario generation and reduction techniques employed
helped reduce dimensionality and maintain tractability while still reflecting key sources of uncertainty.
Ultimately, the quality of scenario generation and reduction mattered greatly, as good scenario
reduction can lead to better solutions with fewer scenarios, while the look-ahead horizon must be
matched with an appropriate number of scenarios to anticipate future risks effectively. As such, these
findings suggest that the generated scenarios functioned as intended and that the method effectively
captured the uncertainty of the stochastic variables.

On average, in every LOL instance, the novel approach would only need to commit 0.06 additional
units, while the conservative approach would have a mean value of 48.17 additional units activated.
This stark difference can be explained by the difference in FRR commitment strategies, with the con-
servative approach underestimating the requirements across time slots more significantly than the
novel approach. Furthermore, the approaches differ in the type of units the model activated. While
the novel model required minimal extra unit activation and achieved this by dispatching small scale
generators, the conventional model not only activated generators but also demand response units.
This has likely to do not only with physical limitations - as shown in the DR constraints, these units
have restrictions on both upper and lower capacities they can provide in relation to demand - but
also because there is a difference in the penalty cost of activating them compared to larger generators.
From this point of view, it can be said that the novel approach, by scheduling frequency reserves
inter-temporally, enables more flexible and efficient utilization of available resources. This flexibil-
ity allows the system to dynamically adjust reserve commitments based on evolving conditions and
anticipated needs over multiple time slots, which can be theoretically covered by dispatching ad-
ditional generators when LOL is detected. In contrast, the conservative approach, which commits
reserves only on a per-timestep basis, lacks this intertemporal optimization and is less able to adapt
to fluctuations in RE output or demand, leading to higher fractions of LOL events that would the-
oretically not be readily mitigated by conventional generators alone. As a result, the conservative
strategy often requires activating additional demand response units or other emergency measures to
maintain security, especially during periods of high renewable variability or unexpected system stress.
From a macro-perspective, it can still be argued that both approaches are quite robust to loss of
load scenarios. After all, despite the conservative approach necessitating comparatively significant
amounts of extra power to cope with such instances, the actual amount is less noticeable when
compared to the grid capacity. Nevertheless, under deterministic assumptions and averaged across
stages and multiple experiments, the two models tend to allocate different levels of backup generation

to hedge against forecast errors and renewable variability, thus resulting in different additional average
production needs.
In conclusion, by leveraging intertemporal reserve scheduling, the novel approach reduces the
severity of LOL events in real-time operations, as it can better anticipate and respond to deviations
from the DA forecast. Conversely, the conservative approach’s lack of intertemporal coordination
results in less efficient reserve deployment and higher reliance on costly or disruptive corrective
actions.
This difference highlights the value of advanced scheduling methodologies in enhancing
energy security and system resilience as RE grid penetration increases.

Discussion
While the impact on the real-time adjustments based on the novel versus the conservative approaches
are noticeable, the difference between the analyzed results is not too stark. Nevertheless, these out-
comes suggest that there are definite advantages to the novel approach of considering various time
slots when optimizing the commitment of day-ahead dispatch and frequency restoration reserves, as
well as carefully integrating LER units such as BESSs. The wider scientific community seems to agree
to the concept. While their investigations naturally differ from the model formulation proposed in
this thesis, the overall consensus seems to be that such an integrated method is usually determined
to be superior to an isolated and deterministic approach.
The results of this thesis indicate that there are benefits of effectively considering LER units. While
their overall impact might not be significant compared to conventional generation, apart from de-
mand response units, this can mainly be attributed by their still relatively limited presence in the
Danish grid, even after deliberately increasing their available capacity for the sake of this analysis.
Other researchers’ findings support this notion. For instance, in terms of BESS integration, Moreira
et al. (2024) found that for their a Contingency-Constrained Unit Commitment approach, the suc-
cessful leverage of BESSs resulted in reduced unit commitment and costs, increasing effectiveness in
both real-time resource usage and ramping needs (Moreira et al., 2024). Eslahi et al. (2021) adopted
the criterion of Fuzzy satisfying to obtain their optimal solution of BESSs integration in response
to wind power uncertainty. Their findings also indicate that effective operation of BESSs can signif-
icantly reduce both emissions and costs, when optimally dispatched for peak-shaving and -shifting
(Eslahi et al., 2021). Similar findings were reported by Pozo et al. (2014) in terms of their ideal and
generic model of BESS units.
As for the other LER units, demand response and aggregators, their importance in providing grid flex-
ibility under significant RE fluctuations such as were demonstrated in this thesis was also cemented
by other studies.Nourizadeh and Nazar (2025) concluded that the integration of aggregators and DR
units significantly reduced operational costs and improved optimal unit commitment and scheduling
in terms of energy sufficiency when compared to a conventional system (Nourizadeh & Nazar, 2025).
By the same token, for their robust optimization model, Nasrolahpour et al. (2018) conducted several
case studies, revealing the benefit of simultaneously considering aggregated, conventional and renew-
able units to cope with uncertainties in the DA and reserve markets (Nasrolahpour et al., 2018).
Hu et al. (2024) decentralized clearing mechanism for the DA bidding and RT balancing of aggrega-
tors also demonstrated the benefits of incorporating aggregators not only in terms of grid flexibility,
but also in overall profits, with their mechanism performing considerably better on comparison to
conventional clearing methodology (Hu et al., 2024).
Basu (2023) had similar findings for their VPP model, but furthermore emphasized the consider-
able impact of applying and integrating DR into the optimization process. The benefits of demand
response units are also demonstrated by Chen et al. (2021) study, which found that it could even
partially replace coal power plants to provide flexibility if incorporated into the grid structure effec-
tively. The researchers argue that this could be achieved when storage costs continue to decrease
47 of 57
==PAGE:61==
(Chen et al., 2021). This argument is supported by this thesis findings, as demand response units
already play a role in the Danish grid flexibility that is not to be underestimated and the importance
of BESS and aggregators is implied to further increase once their grid penetration increases. Lower
costs would undoubtedly accelerate this development even more than its current progression.
The scientific support of the findings of this thesis also extends to the effectiveness of stochastic mod-
els such as was chosen for this thesis’ RT model as an optimization approach over more simple models
that are currently commonly deployed by TSOs like Energinet. Through their explicit and implicit
MILP models, Zhou et al. (2021) demonstrated the robustness of the two different approaches in
terms of DA UC-ED. However, the researchers also emphasized the importance of ensuring future
feasibility under non-anticaptivity in order to appropriately incorporate ESSs and prepare for re-
newable uncertainty realizations (Zhou et al., 2021).Fusco et al. (2023)’s suggested stochastic MILP
model was also found to more effective in terms of dispatching and operating decisions regarding ag-
gregated units comprising of RE and energy storage compared to a deterministic model (Fusco et al.,
2023). In a similar manner Huang et al., 2023 validate their bi-level look-ahead horizon mechanisms
through case studies in Australia, demonstrating improved performance of this approach in terms of
effective ESS scheduling and operation and market costs for both its strategic and integrated models.
The importance of considering multiple market structures and models, as the day-ahead and real-time
sequences of this thesis, is also highlighted in scientific studies. This becomes even more apparent
when taking LERs and more generally ESSs into considerations. Comparable to this thesis main
findings, for their integrated DA and RT approach, Feng et al. (2024) also discovered that their
method not only achieved improved considerations of renewable uncertainty and bulk storage under
comparable optimality performance, but also significantly decreased the need of detailed renewable
forecasts. Nasrolahpour et al. (2018) found that their bi-level complementarity model across multi-
ple markets satisfactorily encompassed market interactions and the impact of incorporating energy
storage units. After validation through several numerical studies, the researchers also highlighted
that such models could provide valuable insights into enhanced ESS bidding strategies (Nasrolahpour
et al., 2018). Similarly, for their multi-objective hybrid framework, Khaloie et al. (2020) concluded
their combined approach of considering energy and reserve markets not only led to improved ex-
pected costs and unit commitment, but also an enhanced offering strategy of BESSs in the reserve
markets.
This thesis’ findings collectively indicate that the proposed frequency reserves optimization frame-
work, when coupled with appropriate scenario-based policies, enables efficient balancing of system
needs under varying renewable conditions. The advantages of over time slots scheduling consider-
ations for reserves as well as efficient LER integration were confirmed. Overall the aforementioned
studies support these main findings of the advantages of implementing new and integrated unit
commitment and dispatch strategies, especially in the context of effective LER integration into op-
timization models such as the one presented.
Nevertheless, as demonstrated by this analysis, the trade-off between computational complexity
and operational efficiency warrants further investigation, particularly in large-scale system imple-
mentations.

Main Findings
This thesis explored the integration and the impact of two frequency restoration reserve commit-
ment approaches for a deterministic day-ahead UC-ED and then a stochastic real-time model. The
case study investigated was the Danish power grid, which is characterized by high penetration of
renewable forms of energy, specifically wind power. This high renewable share, combined with an
artificially increased penetration of LER units, served as the basis to examine how these two models
react to and effectively incorporate large-scale distribution of RE and LER units, in terms of unit
dispatch and reserve commitment. The first approach, which was referred to as ’novel’, imposed
reserve commitments across time slots. The second, designated as ’ conservative’, considered reserve
commitment for each stage separately.
The results indicate that the novel approach was performing better out of the two, both in terms of
system costs, higher operational flexibility and energy security. For the DA model, the LER units
were both utilized in direct dispatch in the form of DR units and aggregators and frequency reserve
providers through BESS units.
In contrast, the conservative model, which had no foresight regarding into the future needs for
frequency restoration reserves, resulted in imprudent BESS discharging in the first time steps of the
simulated day. This resulted in LER units to be unavailable in later hours in the system. As a
result, it also designated far fewer BESS units and capacities as FRR and the grid is mainly relying
in conventional generators and wind curtailment for reserves. Apart from not utilizing BESSs across
time slots, this had an impact in the objective function value as well, since the conservative model
yielded higher costs compared to the novel approach.
These findings were further validated in the real-time stochastic framework, where multiple
scenario-based adjustments were evaluated across low, medium, and high RE realization cases. The
novel approach consistently resulted in lower optimal objective costs compared to the conservative
approach. This suggests that the imprudent dispatch and commitment of reserves in the day-ahead
of the conservative approach led to more adjustments to become necessary in real-time.
In terms of system reliability, both models maintained similar loss of load occurrences. However, the
severity of LOL events were notably different in terms of the amount of extra units that needed to
be dispatched in response. The novel approach enabled more flexible and anticipatory scheduling of
reserves, reducing the need for emergency measures like DR activations. In contrast, the conservative
model, constrained by its myopic reserve allocation, experienced more frequent and severe LOL
events.
The analysis continued by quantifying the Value of the Stochastic Solution, showing modest but
consistent benefits. The positive VSS confirmed that accounting for uncertainty through stochastic
policies leads to more cost-effective operation compared to expected-value approximations.

In conclusion, the thesis demonstrates that intertemporal frequency restoration reserve commitment,
when combined with real-time stochastic optimization, offers a robust framework for integrating lim-
ited energy reservoirs and managing uncertainty in power systems with high shares of renewable
energy. This approach enhances both economic effectiveness and operational resilience while effi-
ciently incorporating the potential of different kinds of LER units.

Limitations & Future Work
The modeling framework developed in this thesis aimed to replicate the Danish power system as re-
alistically as possible. Nevertheless, there were some limitations, which are primarily computational,
which should be acknowledged.
Firstly, it was not possible to solve the 15-minute resolution of the DA model due to the excessive
computational burden. As a result, the DA problem was solved for the 1-hour resolution and the
resulting dispatch was interpolated to produce 15-minute input data for the real time model. This
estimation made it possible to preserve the general trends of the model but the optimal approach
would be to solve the actual 15-minute resolution DA model.
Secondly the Danish power system’s geographic separation into DK1 (Western Denmark) and
DK2 (Eastern Denmark) was not modeled. Instead a single zonal model was implemented for sim-
plification. This omits potential congestion issues between the two zones and the potential impact
of pricing schemes on the actual system costs. Furthermore, all markets that exist between (and
before) the time frame of the day-ahead market and the real-time operations were ommitted in this
thesis, although the adjustments of commitment throughout these markets impact the final availabil-
ity during RT. As such, further investigations into these market interconnections in terms of LERs
and FRRs might be warranted.
Third, although the thesis examined multiple scenario configurations for the real-time stochastic
model, it was not possible to test a larger number of scenarios, based on longer look-ahead horizons
and more clusters due to computational constraints. In particular, larger scenario trees (e.g. more
than 128 scenarios) could not be explored, as even on DTU’s high-performance computing clusters,
memory limits were exceeded and jobs were terminated mid-run.
Additionally, another simplification was the fact that the cross border electricity exchange was
not taken into consideration. Although Denmark’s grid is interconnected with neighboring countries
such as Germany and Sweden, cross-border electricity trade was not included in this thesis. Modeling
such exchanges would require introducing additional market and modeling constraints, such as inter-
zonal transmission limits, which was beyond the scope of this thesis. Furthermore, since this thesis
was taking the perspective of TSO, optimal power flow was also not considered. Future research
would benefit from addressing the behavior of LER units and their potential as FRR under such
models as well.
Finally, the loss of load analysis was limited to 10 experiments again due to the large computa-
tional burden. Ideally, at least 100 experiments would be necessary to have a more stable statistical
analysis regarding the safety of the grid with the implementation of the two modeling approaches
for the real time model. Should sufficient computational resources allow for increasing the number
of simulations, future analysis would certainly become more robust.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated two approaches for frequency restoration reserve (FRR) commitment within
a deterministic day-ahead unit commitment-economic dispatch (UC-ED) model and a stochastic real-
time optimization model, using the Danish power grid as a case study. The system, characterized by
high renewable energy grid penetration and increased deployment of limited energy reservoir (LER)
units, provided a platform to assess how frequency restoration reserve strategies affect dispatch effi-
ciency and system reliability. The novel approach applied intertemporal reserve commitments, while
the conservative model treated each time slot independently. Results showed that the novel method
led to lower overall system costs, more effective use of LER technologies like BESS units, and im-
proved flexibility and energy security.
In both day-ahead and real-time scenarios, the novel approach consistently outperformed the conser-
vative model, reducing the need for costly real-time adjustments and avoiding imprudent dispatch
of LER units. While both models maintained similar levels of reliability in terms of loss of load
(LOL) events, the novel strategy resulted in less severe outcomes and fewer emergency measures.
Additionally, the value of the stochastic solution (VSS) analysis confirmed that incorporating un-
certainty through scenario-based modeling further improved system efficiency. Overall, the thesis
concludes that intertemporal FRR commitment, integrated with stochastic real-time adjustments,
offers a more resilient and economically sound framework for managing high-RE systems with LER
participation. These encouraging results suggest that the proposed approach could be of use to TSOs
and examined in actual power systems, since they can benefit from cost savings while maintaining a
reliable system.
