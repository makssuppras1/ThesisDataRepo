Summary
The still expanding Internet is giving access to endless amounts of content and
users are continuously demanding more and more tailored experiences that ac-
commodate their needs in a seamless manner which limits their interactions.
Information, apps, and media are expected to be delivered when and where
we need them and thus the Internet of Things and the context awareness of
products has become essential in development of new digital solutions. To fully
anticipate the preferences of the user, their current mental state is a key feature
to take into account. By knowing not just when and where, products can then
begin to assess and react to; concentration levels and even emotional states and
moods. In the present study we explore the capabilities of Aﬀdex facial analysis
software, developed by Aﬀectiva, in assessing facial expressions and emotions
and how it can be applied in future studies in automation of measuring mental
state of users. Through a series of experiment Aﬀdex’s measures, capabilities,
and limitations are explored, and it was found that the responses captured by
Aﬀdex is so unique in-between the users that the technology is best served in
analysing baseline responses and not for single trial purposes.

Resume
Internettet giver i dag adgang til uanede mængder af indhold, hvor brugerne
konstant sætter ﬂere og ﬂere krav til en skræddersyet oplevelse som afhjælper
deres behov uden nogen form for interaktion. Informationer, applikationer og

selv ved, vi havde brug for det. Derfor har udviklingen af digitale produkter
rettet sig mod kontekstuelt beviste produkter eller produkter som kan indgå i
et “Internet of Things”. For fuldt ud at kunne forudse brugeres præferencer og
behov, er det nødvendigt at skabe en bevidsthed om deres mentale tilstand. Ved
at udvide mængden af personlige information, vil produkter kunne begynde at
vurdere og reagere på brugeres koncentration, følelser og humør. Denne afhand-
ling vil udforske Aﬀdex ansigtsanalyseværktøj, udviklet af Aﬀectiva, og dets

vist af brugerne var så unikke fra person til person at det umuliggør brugen af

potentiale i vurderingen af et udgangspunkt for brugeres samlede vurdering af
speciﬁkt indhold.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This study was originally initiated to establish whether a connection between
facial expressions and eye movements could be made, especially within the men-
tal states that are conveyed through facial expressions and if these are equally
inferable from the movements of the eyes. The ﬁrst steps in working with fa-
cial expression data however showed to be challenging, as it was found that
even with few participants, this study showed vastly diﬀerent responses to the
same humorous content. In regards to this ﬁnding, the study pivoted to explore
the capabilities and limitations of Aﬀdex and how the incorporation of such
technology could be utilised in the future.
1.1
Motivation
1.1.1
Quantiﬁed Self
The last couple of years, we have seen a massive increase in the development
of Quantiﬁed Self (QS) and smart products. Users are investigating their own
behaviour, they want to know how long they spend doing this or that, they
want to know how far they have walked, run or biked, they want to monitor
that they are eating correctly and the list goes on.
With the emergence of

smart devices this collection of self-monitoring data is becoming more and more
integrated, out-of-the-box most smartphones can monitor how far you walk or
run. With the addition of wristbands, such as Apple Watch, users can now even
have their pulse regularly measured and more of these sensors are continuously
being integrated to this ecosystem. These sensors are providing great potential
in regards to healthcare having patients actively monitoring their own health
and be one step ahead. The self-monitoring opens a new era where users do
frequent measures on their health providing new insights in tendencies, which
are not possible to capture with the high-end equipment used in healthcare
today. We envision, that future products will aim to quantify the mental health
of users as well as the physical. Such products could suggest content based on
our current mood, foresee loss of concentration, or even keep fatigued users from
placing themselves behind the wheel. Our emotional state could even be used
for diagnostic purposes catching early signs of stress and depression or used in
the regulation of medicine in already diagnosed patients.
1.1.2
Big Data
A constant battle is being fought in the digital market, in order to draw the
attention of as many users as possible.
With the internet being a common
household item around the world, digital media’s reach is spreading so far that to
capture and retain the users it has to ﬁt the content to the unique preferences of
every user. Everyday massive amounts of data is collected about user activities
on the internet; our clicks, time spend on a site, most visited pages, return
visits, and so on. Our every move is being monitored in order to create the best
experience tailored to our individual needs. In marketing and user experience
design there is a constant need to get as good and insightful information about
users as possible. But to be truly attentive of a user’s needs and preferences,
we will need a glimpse into their emotional state.

Chapter 2
Research Objectives
The main focus of this study will be to implement a experimental framework that
obtains synchronised data from the Aﬀectiva Aﬀdex facial expression analysis
software and EyeTribe eye-tracking data. The framework should furthermore
be capable of record events in the facial and eye-tracking data in relation to the
content shown to the test subjects. With the framework, this study will explore
the capabilities of automated facial expression analysis and the possibilities of
incorporate facial expression data in other studies regarding digital solutions
for mental state assessments, hereunder especially eye-tracking. The study has
been run in an iterative process where hypothesises has continuously been put
forward and attempted to be proved or disproved.
2.0.1
Research Question
The primary research question of this thesis is:
"Can Aﬀdex facial expression analysis provide insights to a user’s mental state,
in such way that it can be used in the forward exploration of the inﬂuence of
mental state on eye movements?"
==PAGE:19==

This question will be evaluated through the following sub questions:
"What can be measured by the Aﬀdex software?"
"How does the responses extracted diﬀer from user to user?"
"Can Aﬀdex data be reliably synchronized with eye-tracking data for correla-
tion exploration?"

Chapter 3
Related Work
More than a hundred years ago, Darwin posed that emotions are an evolution-
ary trait, as he found similar expressions of emotions across races and cultures
[9], later the theory was reﬁned to also accommodate the cultural diﬀerences
observed. Especially P. Ekman argues for a set of basic emotions postulated
to be universal across cultures and also across most species and even seen as
a link in interspecies communication [12]. Ekman believe that emotions are a
response and preparation for an emerging interpersonal encounter, however sim-
ilarly argues that emotions can emerge without interpersonal stimuli, but social
connection pose as an ampliﬁer for the expressed emotion [16]. In especially
facial expression of emotions the general consensus lies on six basic emotions;
anger, disgust, fear, joy, sadness and surprise. Which also posed a crucial role
in Ekman and Friesen work on establishing the existence of cross-cultural facial
expressions [17].
3.1
Mental State and Emotions Inﬂuence on Phys-
iological Responses
When talking about emotional arousal, it has been shown that emotions do
inﬂuence the autonomic nervous system. Diﬀerent emotions have shown to in-

ﬂuence autonomic nervous system in diﬀerent ways, like blushing when feeling
shy or embarrassed. Also more evolutionary aﬃrming responses, such as in-
creased heart rate when feeling angry or scared are clearly linked to a ﬁght or
ﬂight response [29].
3.2
Emotions in Facial Expressions
Facial expression is a crucial part of non-verbal communication and has been
studied heavily the last century. Speciﬁcally because the facial gestures have
such integrated role in social behaviour it has been covered by psychologists,
anthropologists, neuroscientist, and lately also by computer scientists. For in-
stance, a smile is clearly associated with positive emotions, but the integrated
social behaviour in facial expression has evolved the use of it to vary vastly by
context. It has been found that a distinctions can be made between diﬀerent
smiles, such as amusement, polite, or embarrassed smiles [3]. Especially, the
inﬂuence of the Duchenne-marker has been heavily researched in the distinc-
tion of expressions of amusement. The Duchenne-marker is a small constriction
surrounding the eyes have been linked to strong positive emotions, and simi-
larly in strong negative emotions in infants [24]. It has been shown how even
mimicking facial expressions are correlated with emotional states and have a
direct inﬂuence on separate parts of the autonomic nervous system. A study
found that guiding test subjects to elicit facial muscles to express anger or fear
resulted in higher heart rates in the test subjects [22]. As highly social beings
these expressions of emotions are also subject to deception, people are capable
of inhibiting or masking the expressions showing the emotions they are feeling
and simulating expression is also a trait that most people use on a daily basis
to deceive others to believe an emotion is felt [13].
3.2.1
Facial Action Coding System
The broad spectrum of studies related to facial expression analysis, have resulted
in Facial Action Coding System (FACS) being adapted as the universal system
for describing facial expressions in humans. FACS, developed by Ekman and
Friesen [15], is based on a systematic mapping of the muscles in the face. The
implication of these muscles are described as Action Unites (AU). The adoption
of FACS gives consistent insight when working with a cross scientiﬁc subject as
is the case of facial expressions. With 44 unique AUs and more than 7000 com-
binations hereof, the extent of descriptive power is also why FACS has become
the go-to-tool for systematic approaches in facial expression analysis.

3.2.2
Cross Cultural Importance
An aspect of facial expressions making them an important measure for asserting
emotions in people is its cross cultural importance. Ekman and Friesen showed
in 1971 how a tribe in New Guinea, previously cut oﬀfrom modern society,
had good perception of basic facial gestures from modern society [17].
This
argues that one model can assert facial expressions and their meaning. However,
it should be noted that not all expressions are reliable in all instances and
contexts. A study on American and Japanese college students, showed that all
participant reacted similar to stressful video clips when being alone, presenting
with virtually the same expressions. When an interviewee was present in the
room the output changed, the Japanese test subjects were more likely to mask
their emotions. This has been linked to cultural rules in Japanese culture on
management of facial appearance in front of others [11].
3.2.3
Emotions in Eye-Movements
The motion of the eye plays a central role in non-verbal communication, this
includes joint attention, gaze direction, eye-contact, and also the absence of eye-
contact [19]. The eyes even poses as the most recognisable feature of the face
[26]. In the context of eyes in communication though it should be noted that
the face, as a whole, is essential, as the eyes are not processed separately from
the rest of the face. The use of the more subtle movements of the eye in relation
to mental state in test subjects, is a less explored ﬁeld. Studies have however
found eye-movement to correlate with fatigue [8, 30], arousal[5, 10], and valence
[4].

5.1
Facial Expression Measure
In order to use Aﬀectiva’s Aﬀdex facial expression analysis in a scientiﬁc context,
we have to establish the meaning of Aﬀdex’s extracted features. What is Aﬀdex
measuring? How does the measures correspond to the expected dynamics of
facial gestures?
5.2
Theory
When measuring faces, Ekman and Friesen argues that there are ﬁve aspects
[18]:
1. Classiﬁcation - the distinction between facial gestures by involved facial
muscles.
2. Intensity - the degree of elicitation of the muscles involved
3. Laterality - whether the involved muscles are present on both sides, one
side or has an unevenly distributed involvement.
==PAGE:27==
12
1st Iteration
4. Location - the time of start and end for a full expression.
5. Timing - the timings of Onset, Apex and Oﬀset.
Where Apex is the
duration wherein the intensity is at its utmost.
5.2.1
The Five Aspects in Aﬀdex v1.0.1
The features observed by Aﬀdex v1.0.1 are tracking six features: smile, brow
furrow, brow raise, lip corner depressor, valence and engagement. Excluding
valence and engagement the remaining features correspond to the classiﬁcation
aspect. The documentation for Aﬀdex only mentions smile as being a combina-
tion of AU’s, whereas the remaining expressions cover a lateral elicitation of a
single AU. Speciﬁcally the documentation deﬁnes smile as "lip corners pulling
outwards along with other cues from the face (e.g. eyes) combine to indicate
a true smile". This could suggest the Duchenne-marker is involved but it is
not conﬁrmed. Aﬀectiva provides little insight into the exact mechanics of their
algorithm and there is no precise description of the features measure or their
interrelationship. It is therefore unclear what the "cues" cover. The remaining
expressions, however, are described as you would expect. Brow furrow being
"both eyebrows moving lower and closer together" and likewise descriptions for
brow raise and lip corner depressor.
Aﬀectiva has released a public dataset for research of FACS labelled video con-
tent, from this we must assume that Aﬀectiva are basing their algorithm and
expression classiﬁcation on the FACS labelling system. FACS has an intensity
score categorized in ﬁve scores:
1. Trace (A)
2. Slight (B)
3. Marked/pronounced (C)
4. Severe/extreme/maximum (D)
5. Extreme/maximum (E)
However, in Aﬀdex all the expression features are output as a numeric value
between 0-100, giving no direct correlation to the FACS intensity scores. It is
therefore unclear whether thresholds in this scale would correspond to the FACS
intensities and also whether such thresholds would be consistent in-between
features or across distinct test subjects.

5.3 Hypothesis
13
The documentation for Aﬀdex provides no insights into the distinction of later-
ality of facial expressions.
With Aﬀdex providing a continues stream of data bound by speciﬁc frames in
the recording, the data will be highly linked with the time of capture. Thereby
all datapoints are timestamped in relation to the recorded video of the subjects
providing a parameter for which location and timings can be indicated. But as
with intensity the scale of 0-100 provides no insights into how the expressions
will map out. It is unclear what scores on this scale should be interpreted as
the given expression and when the apex is reached.
5.3
Hypothesis

6.2.1
The Five Aspects in Aﬀdex v2.0
6.2.1.1
Classiﬁcation
Aﬀdex are now splitting the extracted features into two categories, emotion met-
rics and facial expression metrics. The emotion metrics include: anger, sadness,
disgust, fear, joy, surprise and contempt. Furthermore there is two emotional
experience metrics; engagement and valence. The facial expression metrics in-
clude: attention, brow furrow, brow raise, inner brow raise, eye closure, nose
wrinkle, upper lip raise, lip suck, lip pucker, lip press, mouth open, lip corner
depressor, chin raise smirk, and smile [2]. As for the v1.0.1 there is a feature
for each classiﬁable expression, but now quite a few more speciﬁc expressions
can be detected. All features are of a single identiﬁable characteristic, except
smile and attention. Smile is as for v1.0.1 described as being a combination of
the lip corners pulled outwards as well as "other indicators". Attention is not
explicitly a facial expression, the feature is based on whether the user is facing
the camera and thereby focusing their attention on the stimuli shown.
6.2.1.2
Intensity
As with the v1.0.1, the documentation for Aﬀdex v2.0 is scarce. "Emotion and
expression metrics scores indicate when users make a speciﬁc emotion, or ex-
pression (e.g., a smile) along with the degree of conﬁdence. The metrics can be
thought of as detectors: as the emotion or facial expression occurs and intensi-
ﬁes, the score rises from 0 (no expression) to 100 (expression fully present)" [2].
The documentation ﬂuctuates between the measured metrics being a measure
of conﬁdence and a measure of intensity. For the purpose of our experiment the
latter would be preferable as intensity would be comparable with the model,
whereas conﬁdence would exclude the information of transition between inten-
sities.
6.2.1.3
Laterality
As for v1.0.1 Aﬀdex v2.0 still does not provide any information of which side
the expression or involved muscles are activated. The smirk feature however
is classiﬁed as "left or right lip corners pulled upwards and outwards", so the
underlying algorithm seems to be sensitive to the laterality but the information
is not outputted directly by Aﬀdex.

As Aﬀdex provides no direct measure of the intensity in the expressions classi-

7.2
Theory
7.2.1
All Smiles are not Created Equal
In order to increase insights gained from Aﬀdex, a qualitative exploration of
the relationship between the Aﬀdex features and the concurrent frames in the
recordings will be performed. Automated facial expression analysis is an intri-
cate process involving multiple separate steps for facial detection, tracking, reg-
istration, and feature extraction [31]. To fully understand the output of Aﬀdex
==PAGE:45==
30
3rd Iteration
an examination of what motions makes Aﬀdex react and how small diﬀerence
inﬂuence the output.
Multiple studies have been conducted in order to distinguish the diﬀerence be-
tween smiles. A feature covered in a multitude of these studies is the Duchenne-
marker, a small constriction of the muscles surrounding the eyes (AU6). The
Duchenne-marker has been correlated with a smile of enjoyment [14] and has
also been linked with strong positive and negative emotions in infants [24].
7.2.1.1
Smile Controls
Dacher Keltner introduced the term smile controls about a series of action units
potentially counteracting, masking or obscuring a smile.
The smile controls
were linked with distinction of embarrassed smiles and smiles of amusement
[20].
Keltner further more showed how gaze direction, head movement, and
touching of the face had inﬂuence on the diﬀerentiation of smiles.
Keltner worked with 17 action units, of these Aﬀdex supports ﬁve; lip corner
depressor (AU15), chin raise (AU17), lip pucker (AU18), lip press (AU24) and
lip suck (AU28). Keltner found that lip press and lip corner depressor were
most commonly found in embarrassed smiles and the remaining in smiles of
amusement.

10.1
Synchronization of Aﬀdex and Alternate Data
Sources
As the original motivation for this thesis was the opportunity to expand the
studies currently done at DTU in eye-tracking in the ﬁeld of other sciences.
Aﬀdex was seen as a viable candidate for gaining insights to the users mental
state as they were observing diﬀerent stimuli. Until now this thesis has primarily
covered the use of Aﬀdex and the analysis of facial expression data. This section
will cover the basis for combining the two data sources for future studies.
10.2
Hypothesis
It is hypothesised that the PsychoPy [28] interface provide a stable basis from
which a framework for combined eye-tracking and facial analysis can be per-
formed. Such a framework will be rated on the possibility to viably collect data
synchronously and the possibility to compare the data across diﬀerent sources.
For the synchronisation the blink of test subjects will be utilised, this approach
==PAGE:71==
56
6th Iteration
has previously been used in synchronisation of EEG and eye-tracking experi-
mentation [7].
10.3
Method
The synchronization of data has been an ongoing process through the exper-
iments performed in this study. Early low ﬁdelity solutions were to look and
hear for cues in the video recorded to ﬁnd the starting point of the video shown
to the subjects, these provided early insights into the relation between Aﬀdex
and the content shown. Later the experiments were done more structured by
setting up the precise timings of all stimuli in PsychoPy. The framework utilises
the epoch time of the computer to note timings of all recorded frames, extracted
eye-tracking points and the start and end points of the stimuli shown to the test
subjects.
10.4
Results
With updated Aﬀdex v2.0, a singular feature which could also be inferred from
the eye tracking data was introduced. The eyeClosureScore provided a measure
that makes it possible to spot the continuous blinking of the subject. Blinks
are also a brief and easy identiﬁable feature which can be spotted with high
accuracy in single frames. In ﬁgure 10.1 the corresponding frame to the local
maximum in a blink for each Subject is seen. In appendix B the same blinks
are shown side by side to the frame before and after, here its is clear that the
recordings and Aﬀdex are in synchronization.
To establish the synchronization of the eye-tracker and Aﬀdex, a blink needed
to be identiﬁed in the eye-tracking data as for Aﬀdex. The simplest way to
identify a blink with the eye tracker is to assume absence of signal as the eyes
being covered during a blink. This method will however by its simplicity also
classify sudden loss of signal as blinks, this can be due to the subject moving
or turning away from the tracker. For this analysis the pupil size has been used
in order to point out blinks, if either of the pupils read 0 it is interpreted as a
blink, this will account for asynchronous movement between the left and right
eyelid. In table 10.1 it is seen how the EyeTribe eye-tracker do have a higher
count of blinks, as anticipated due to the deﬁnition of blink from this source.
By computing the cross correlation between the two data sets it was found that
the maximum oﬀset in the experiment was 2. The average oﬀset delay for the
entire set was 0.03 seconds. Individual cross correlation plot for the subjects
can be found in appendix C.

Chapter 11
Conclusion
This study concluded in six iterations with various approaches to gain more
insight into the utilisation of facial expressions in future studies of mental state
inﬂuence on bodily functions. The study also explored the Aﬀdex features and
its output, this was deemed necessary due to poor documentation of the inner
workings of Aﬀdex.
The ﬁrst iteration sought to apply Ekman and Friesen’s ﬁve aspects of facial
measure to the data extracted with Aﬀdex. The results found that Aﬀdex where
capable of identifying speciﬁc facial expressions, however it was found that the
diﬀerent scores provided by Aﬀdex did not correspond to the intensities identi-
ﬁed in the recordings. It was concluded that the scores had poor capabilities for
comparing expressions among diﬀerent subjects, and even for a single subject.
The variability in the measures made designation of timings and location in the
expression troublesome, however patterns in the data was found to correspond
with expected behaviour.
Laterality of expressions was completely excluded
from the analysis as Aﬀdex provided no measure of such.
The second iteration reworked the results from the ﬁrst, as a major update
was released of Aﬀdex. Aﬀdex v2.0 went from six features to 28 and promised
dramatic improvements in accuracy. With Ekman and Friesen’s ﬁve aspects in
mind, it was found that the score previously thought as an intensity measure
was in fact a measure of conﬁdence. By running the same experiment recordings
==PAGE:77==
62
Conclusion
through the new version of Aﬀdex it was seen how the spikes previously seen as
intensity spikes was actually the more conservative estimate of conﬁdence in the
older version. Aﬀdex v2.0 now identiﬁed even moderate smiles with high conﬁ-
dence, which in some cases resulted in long periods of readings in the maximum
score 100. With the feature scores being established as conﬁdence and the much
more conﬁdent algorithm behind v2.0 the possibility of establishing timings of
the expressions was rejected. Location was in some case possible however due
to expressions overlapping it was not possible to pin-point all expressions start
and end. In an eﬀort to ﬁnd changes in intensity in the smileScore outputted
by Aﬀdex, the third iteration utilised multiple expression features which are
included in the expressions classiﬁed as smile controls by Keltner. The smile
controls did show promising indications of changes in intensity but no pattern
was identiﬁed regarding increase, decrease or magnitude of the change.
For the fourth and ﬁfth iteration a larger test group was recruited, the goal of
the experiment was to provide a static controlled set-up for the recording of test
subjects. In the experiment only a subset of participant was under the pres-
ence of an experimenter for the viewing, It was inferred from the results that
the social context of viewing the humorous content in the presence of others,
led to a signiﬁcant increase in the conﬁdence of smileScore compared to the
subjects whom watched the content alone. It was further seen how negative va-
lenceScore was almost exclusively measured in the subjects being alone during
the experiment, this was linked to the higher conﬁdence of browFurrowScore
in these subjects. There was in the experimenter/no experimenter set-up not
seen any signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the duration of the diﬀerent expressions, the
ﬁfth iteration therefore sought for a grouping of participants for which speciﬁc
patterns could be identiﬁed. A set of subjects were found to be more reactive to
the content shown than the remaining. These subjects all showed smileScores
of above 80% during the humorous video, whereas the remaining subjects were
dominated by the utter absence of expressions. These groups did show a sig-
niﬁcant diﬀerence in both conﬁdence and durations of smiles, however for the
second part of the experiment where the subjects were viewing images rather
than seeing video the diﬀerence ceased. The diﬀerence could therefore only be
seen in high arousal humorous content. The work with clustering and visuali-
sation of the dataset revealed an interesting characteristic of the smirkScore, it
was identiﬁed that in multiple subjects a smirk were indicative of an upcoming
smile. From the recordings it was seen how this was in some cases a result of
asynchronous lateral elicitation of the smile, whereas other cases showed that
the simultaneous presence of smirk and smile was an indication of Aﬀdex failing
to distinguish the two familiar expressions.
The sixth iteration explored the results of the experiment done in the fourth
and ﬁfth iteration for the assessment of synchronisation in the entire collec-
tion sources. Using blinks as reference there was shown single frame precision

between Aﬀdex and the recordings by assuming absence of signal as blinks in
eye-tracking data, an average delay of only 0.3 seconds between Aﬀdex and the
eye-tracker was shown to be achieved. Suggesting that the collection method
is a viable platform for future comparisons between Aﬀdex facial analysis data,
eye-tracking data, and potentially other measuring equipment down the line.

Conclusion
==PAGE:80==
Chapter 12
Discussion and Further
Work
The utilisation of facial expression analysis has proved to be bound by a complex
network of relations between distinct expressions, cultural diﬀerences, social as-
pects and the limitations of current technology which makes up for a domain
diﬃcult to tame but with foreseeable beneﬁts in future assessment of user be-
haviour and mental state.
This study found clear limitations in the Aﬀdex
system which complicates the usage of it, especially the absence of intensity
measure of an expression leaves the system without the possibility of directly
comparing singular expressions by a user or in between diﬀerent users. Also
without intensity the distinct starting and ending point of an expression diﬃ-
cult to separate from overlapping expressions and the identiﬁcation of timings
for onset, apex and oﬀset is unidentiﬁable without insights to intensity. But
what Aﬀdex do provide is points of interest in the content, in the 5th iteration
it was presented how the Aﬀdex data provided second-by-second evaluations of
the conﬁdence of smile in the humorous content shown. From the data a clear
connection between the user’s response and the story told in the small video
commercial was observed. Spikes and accumulated conﬁdence in the smileScore
builds up as the video progresses, these spikes and crevasses provides a basis
for analysing the content of the video, even from the limited amount of partic-
ipants in this study a set of points and sections of the video were pointed out
as important. As previously discussed there seems to be an apparent response
to even seemingly small things like the word "naked" being said, or the appear-
==PAGE:81==
66
Discussion and Further Work
ance of a familiar character like the red M&M. These minute responses seen
across multiple subjects creates an interesting dynamic which has not yet been
explored, the Aﬀdex technology might have its limitation due to the unique per-
sonal diﬀerences of every user but individual expressions might provide valuable
insights. What other words can provoke a response, does a word associated with
explicit nature like "naked" create other responses than words associated with
happiness, grief or hate? The same could be applied for the recall of a famil-
iar character, how will the responses diﬀer if we are seeing a commercial ﬁgure
contra an athlete we admire, a nostalgic reunion with a childhood hero or what
about an attractive celebrity? The comprehension of these responses and the
mental states associated could prove to be a valuable tool when editing movies
or commercials, it could possibly even be applied in user experience design, by
learning the triggers and the response they provoke.
This study’s original purpose was to investigate the possibility of measuring the
mental state or emotional response from the movements of users eyes using data
extracted using Aﬀectiva’s facial expression analysis tool, however the unique
and highly dynamic domain of facial expression showed to be so extensive that
the study focussed on seeking out the meaning of Aﬀdex’s features and how
they relate to the actual movements of users facial features.
It was shown
that there is a vast diﬀerence from user to user and even just the presence
of another person poses a measurable social impact. The utilisation of such
system in single trial experimentation or even as a tool in user experience still
seem doubtful as the results are too scattered and in some cases the users did not
even express anything with facial expressions. Especially, the complete absence
of facial expression in some user raises the question whether facial expression
analysis even has potential in the evaluation of mental state in users. However it
is important to note that it was possible to extract valuable information about
the interpretation of the video from the data measured with Aﬀdex and given the
composition of the video the data extracted lines up with what one could expect
from a commercial or joke in this case. But more interestingly it also made new
insights apparent, such as the inﬂuence of "naked" being uttered. This was
observed from only 15 test subjects and shows the same tendencies as Aﬀectiva
measured in their study with more than 4000 participants, there is therefore
seen great potential in using Aﬀdex for establishing baseline behaviour that can
be applied in other studies to ﬁnd similar behaviour in other bodily functions.
Given the communicative application of facial expressions it also plays a major
rule in social behaviour and has also been extensively research in relation to
deception, so utilising other sources for the interpretation of mental state in
users might provide a less noisy response that could be used even in single
trial. Eye-tracking could be such a feature to add to the equation with studies
already showing some indication of for instance pupil size being indicative of
arousal, also with the continued interest in wearable devices more and more
bodily functions are added to the list of parameters that could prove valuable

when asserting mental state, such as heart rate, EKG or even sleep.
