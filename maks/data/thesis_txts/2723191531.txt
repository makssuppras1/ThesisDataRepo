Abstract
This thesis examines the combination of blade turbine parameters to achieve the highest
power output/shaft bending moment ratio, for wind power machines producing 50 Watts
of power. Design Failure Mode and Effect Analysis (FMEA) is used to design the eleôÙ
ments of the turbine for optimal performance. The conceptualization process is based on
theoretical frameworks and research in the field of wind power, resulting in four different
concepts of Savonius type Vertical Axis Wind Turbines (VAWT) with varying structures and
efficiencies. A Mechanical Model is created from a Physical Model to optimize the width
and length of turbine wings for maximum power output under nominal conditions and minôÙ
imum bending moment under storm conditions at the bottom of the shaft. This model is
then converted into a Mathematical Model which is used to calculate and optimize the sysôÙ
tem through a multiôÙobjective optimization algorithm. Results showed that wider, shorter
turbines are more effective than tall, thin turbines commonly seen in commercial Savonius
VAWTS. After fixing the obtained optimal size and type of turbine, applying FMEA results,
and conducting design research, a final robust efficient turbine design was created that
can be mounted vertically or horizontally by simply altering the external support frame.

Introduction
In the continually growing field of sustainable energies, wind energy has numerous adôÙ
vantages such as costôÙeffectiveness, abundance or local production. Given the enormous
innovation potential in the area, this project seeks to design and build the mechanical
structure for a small drag based low power vertical axis wind turbine to use in remote
places. The key product requirements consequently are that the developed structure
is foldable, lightweight, easy to assemble while achieving a high level of durability and
longevity, which implies a number of tradeôÙoffs between the different mechanical characôÙ
teristics.
Off grid solar power installations are becoming popular all over the world. In Nordic counôÙ
tries though, specially during winter time, the sun is not a reliable power source. In this
context, wind turbines could be a good complementary or substitute sustainable solution.
Moreover, having a portable and easy to install device could not only be a solution in fixed
locations but also in non permanent events such as festivals, competitions or seasonal
restaurants (foodtrucks).
This project is going to be done together with DTU Department of Mechanical Engineering
and Energy Machines Wind, the company I am working for. The aim of this thesis is to
create a product that could be used to create a green source of electricity .

Theoretical Background
2.1
Wind Power
Wind power is an ancient source of energy that has been utilized by humans for centuries.
In the present day, it is the most efficient and widelyôÙused renewable energy source. As
Denmark faces a global energy crisis in 2022 due to shortages and rising prices of oil,
gas, and electricity caused by the COVIDôÙ19 pandemic and Russian invasion of Ukraine,
wind power has become the most viable source of electricity [1]. Additionally, it is a clean
energy source that can help to address the current Climate Crisis.
Wind power is an environmentally friendly and sustainable solution for electricity needs.
By converting the mechanical energy produced by wind turbine blades into electrical enôÙ
ergy, it does not produce any pollutants or waste, and does not contribute to global warmôÙ
ing. Additionally, wind energy is widely available around the world, eliminating the need
for costly transportation of electricity and making it more affordable. All in all, wind power
is a great choice for a clean and renewable source of energy.
Wind power is an ideal solution for producing energy sustainably, as it is environmentally
friendly, emits no pollutants, is limitless in supply, and is competitively priced compared
to other energy sources.
2.2
Types of Wind Turbines
There are basically two types of wind turbines:
ãÂ HorizontalôÙaxis wind turbines
ãÂ VerticalôÙaxis wind turbines
The size of these turbines can vary from 50 Watts (to charge your computer) to 15 Megawatts
(to power over 5000 houses [2]). The larger turbines are normally connected to the grid
but the smaller ones can be used offôÙgrid. They can be installed onshore or offshore with
floating platforms.
2.2.1
Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines
Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines also known as HAWT are the most used wind turbines.
HAWTs have good efficiency and have to face the wind to start spinning (yaw system).
Also they use a pitch system to change the angle of the blades, maximizing the power
output and also protecting the turbine from high wind speeds. Their basic components
are the tower, generator, brake, gear box and blades.
2.2.2
Vertical Axis Wind Turbines
Vertical Axis Wind Turbines also known as VAWT are another kind of wind turbines. They
are not as popular as the HAWTs because the efficiency is lower than the horizontal ones.
They are simpler as they do not need yaw or pitch system and also they do not need gear
box since the rotor is coupled to the shaft. There are two big groups, the lift and the drag
turbines.

2.2.4
Advantatges VAWT
1. No need of Gear Box: The usual reduced size of a VAWT, its low speed and the
vertical axis aligned between the mast, axis and generator makes this king of turôÙ
bines a perfect candidates for using a slow speed generator, which does not include
a gear box system.
2. Yaw System: One of the main advantages of the VAWTs is that these turbines are
equipped with rotor blades that can pick up wind coming from any direction. On
the other side, HAWTs needs of a yaw system that keeps orienting the rotor to the
direction of the wind. Also these vertical turbines can work with turbulent air flows,
since the direction of the wind is not that important. That makes this type ideal in
urban environments.
3. Pitch system: The pitch system is a mechanical system used in horizontal turbines
to get the turbine spinning at a lower starting wind speed and to brake or slow down
the blades. It changes the inclination of the blades. The use of a pitch system
in VAWTs is not commonly used but there is still in research since it could greatly
increase its efficiency.
4. Cut In Speed: The shape of the blades make the VAWTs, specially the ones using
drag system, start spinning at really low speeds (1,5 m/s) so the turbine can start
producing electricity with gentle breezes.
5. Environmental Harm: It is believed that VAWTs are much less dangerous for birds
than HAWTs [4]. The vertical axis rotation and the slower spin speed make the
blades more visible for birds so they do not approach.
6. Spacing : There are a lot of literature and simulations proving that setting various

VAWT at a close distance not only does not reduce its efficiency as it happens to
HAWTs (5 diameters apart across the wind, and about 10 diameters apart extending
downwind) but it increases. That could mean that with reduced space (rooftops,
balconies or parking stations), larger number of turbines could be installed [5].
7. Load Distribution: The forces distribution is more balanced since all the weight
falls exactly on the shaft. The generator can be placed at the ground since the
bladesã axis is shared with the generator one. This placement helps with lowering
the gravity point which translates into simpler and lighter structure. Also the fatigue

8. Mechanical Design: Similar to the Load Distribution, since the force distribution
of the loads it is simple, the mechanical fatigue, stresses and strains are not as
demanding as in VAWT systems.
9. Rotating Speed: The low rotating speed in VAWTs can reduce the noise and also
the vibration.
10. Maintenance: Another advantage thanks to the smaller size of vertical axis wind
turbines is that they are easier to transport, set up, and maintain. Maintenance do
not have to be done at the top of the turbines like HAWTs as the major components,
such as the generators (in some types of VAWTs), are built closer to the ground.
11. Blade rotation Area: The blade rotation are in VAWTs is lower than a HAWT, which

Types of VAWTs
Depending on the wind use, there are two basic types of vertical axis wind turbines: the
drag turbine, and the lift turbine [6].
Types of forces in flying bodies
Before analyzing the different vertical turbines technologies, the types of forces that flying
bodies can experiment are explained.
ãÂ Weight: Is the force of gravity. It acts into the center of the Earth, toward the center.
In wind turbines this force is take into account when designing the structure due to
the compression force and bending stress of the different parts.
ãÂ Thrust: Is the force that propels a flying machine in the direction of motion. Since
the wind turbines are made for the opposite, getting energy through the motion, this
force is not used to study turbines.
ãÂ Lift:
The lift forces acts by difference of pressure in a body caused by a fluid
flowing around this body. It is the component of the force that is perpendicular to
the oncoming flow direction [7].
ãÂ Drag:
Finally, the drag force is caused by friction and differences in air pressure.
Unlike the lift force, the drag acts opposite to the direction of motion. Taking a look
at fig. 2.1, the lift should balance the weight while the thrust should balance the drag
for the plane to keep itself in the air [8].

FMEA
FMEA, or failure mode and effects analysis, is a systematic, proactive method for assessôÙ
ing a process to determine where and how it might fail as well as the relative impact of
various failures in order to point out the areas of the process that need the most improveôÙ
ment.
To conduct an FMEA, a group of people (typically engineers, process analysts, and qualôÙ
ity personnel) must come together to identify potential failure modes in a process and
assess their potential impact. It is usually preceded by a brainstorming session in which
potential failure modes are identified and addressed. The team then scores each failure
mode based on its likelihood of occurrence, the severity of its impact if it occurs, and its
detectability.
Many different businesses, including the automotive, aerospace, medical, and software
development sectors, use FMEA. Organizations also utilize it for Six Sigma aimed at proôÙ
cess improvement. It is an excellent resource for preventing probable failure modes in a
process and locating the regions that require fixing action the most.
1. The first phase of an FMEA requires the identification of all potential issues, risks,
and failures with a process, a product, or a system. This includes looking at the
methods and procedures used as well as the supplies and components employed.
2. Analyze and Grade Risks: The second phase of FMEA is to assess and grade the
risks connected to each potential failure after it has been discovered. This entails
determining the likelihood of detection and the probability of occurrence for each
potential failure, as well as assigning a severity rating to each one.
3. Each probable failure mode in an FMEA is given a numerical rating called an RPN
(Risk Priority Number). The severity, occurrence, and detection numbers associated
with each failure mode are multiplied to arrive at the value. The generated RPN can
be used to rank corrective measures according to the relative importance of each
failure mode.

Discovery, Definition, Development, and Delivery are the four phases of this process and
together work as a map that designers can use to organize their thoughts in order to imôÙ
prove the creative process. The two diamonds represent divergent and convergent thinkôÙ
ingëƒ exploring an issue more widely or deeply, and then taking focused action. Of course,
this process is iterative and not linear so in this specific project the development/research
and delivery phase are repeated, as the following image shows (fig. 4.1).
Figure 4.1: Triple diamond shape design process.
The theoretical framework and problem statement have already been completed, conôÙ
cluding that a Savonius Wind turbine of around 50 Watts needs to be designed. The next
four stages are composed of two diamonds. The first diamond consists of the ConcepôÙ
tualisation phase, where various Savonius wind turbine concepts are developed, and
the MultiôÙobjective Optimization phase, where the developed concepts are evaluated
through an iterative algorithm to determine the optimal size of turbine blades and most efôÙ
ficient turbine concept. The second diamond includes the first stage of a DFMEA phase,
where all possible failure modes of the turbine are listed and research is conducted to
decide specific parameters of the turbine. Additionally, this diamond includes the Design
phase, where the second stage of DFMEA is done (taking action on failure modes) and
modelling of the turbine is carried out. Upon completion of this process, the final product
is the optimized turbine.
In order to gain an understanding of the optimization algorithm, fig. 4.2 provides an illusôÙ
trative representation of its functioning.
A randomized program is used to generate blades of varying sizes, which are then sent
to various calculation programs to obtain goal functions. These functions also utilize paôÙ
rameters derived from the chosen concept. The results of the goal functions are plotted
in a space design with certain size limitations, and the optimal blade size is identified
graphically.
For a more thorough understanding of this algorithm, please refer to the MultiôÙObjective
Optimization section.

6
MultiôÙObjective design optimization
This research seeks to optimize two parameters in the turbine design, width and length of
the wing, in order to maximize power output in nominal conditions (wind speed of 8m/s)
and minimize the bending moment in storm conditions (wind speed of 25m/s) at the bottom
of the shaft (the generator).
To proceed to the optimization process, the bending moment must be expressed numerôÙ
ically. This requires transforming the physical system into a Mechanical Model, which
includes making assumptions, hypotheses, and simplifications. Subsequently, this MeôÙ
chanical Model is converted into a Mathematical Model using the principles of physics
and mathematics. With this Mathematical Model, it is possible to carry out calculations
and optimize the system. It is not a one way ticket but a loop where the physical system
is constantly changing and improving.
The power output of the turbine follows the same steps as shown in fig. 6.1, but in a more
straightforward way. Through the use of fixed parameters and the size of the wings, it can
be easily calculated. As a result, compared to the bending moment process, it is easier
to analyze and calculate. The section 6.3 provides a more detailed explanation.
Figure 6.1: Mathematical modelling process

pressure depends on the diameter of the shaft, its length and the wind speed (that is

SCRIPT EXPLANATION
The randomize and plotting program are inside the same Matlab script. This Matlab
script is optimizing a vertical Axis Wind Turbine wing design based on power output and
generator bending moment.
1. The script starts by clearing the workspace and console. The number of samples
(N) is chosen. For the simulations, 5000 samples are used, taking a computational
time of approximately 40 minutes with the code optimized.
2. The two design variables, height and width, are provided with minimum and maxiôÙ
mum values to be processed. It should be noted that these are not limits, like those
MultiôÙObjective Design of a Portable Vertical Axis Wind Turbine
39
==PAGE:52==
explained in the section on Wing size restrictions, so there may be points that exôÙ
ceed these values.
3. 5000 Latin Hyper Cube samples are created for the design variables using lhsdesign,
and the samples are stored in an array V . Latin hypercube sampling (LHS) is a
statistical method used to create a nearôÙrandom sample of values from a multidiôÙ
mensional distribution, which is often employed in computer experiments [23].
4. The script sets the parameters for the turbine called in the last section as fixed
parameters and then calls the bending moment function and power output with the
randomized height and weight and all the fixed parameters.
5. The script first evaluates the objective and constraint functions. This information is
stored in the arrays O and g, respectively. The feasibility of each sample is stored in
the array Fs, which is used for calculating the overall feasibility of the design space.
Additionally, the arrays Fs2 and sn are used to calculate the number of feasible
solutions.
6. The design space is then plotted, showing the feasible and unfeasible samples for
each constraint within a heightôÙwidth plot.
7. The objective space is also evaluated and plotted, showing the feasible samples for
each objective within a bending momentôÙpower output plot.
8. Finally, the optimal point is graphically identified (max power output with min bending
moment). The index of this point in the O array is found, and this index is used to
find the corresponding wing dimensions in the V array. This allows the optimal H
and W to be determined.

Conclusion
The design of a Savonius type portable wind axis wind turbine has proven to be an efôÙ
fective and efficient way to generate renewable energy. The turbine is easy to install,
requires minimal maintenance, and is capable of producing a significant amount of enôÙ
ergy thanks to its robust design. Additionally it can be used in a variety of locations such
as remote cabins into the woods, rooftops in urban environments or even camping vans,
which makes it an ideal choice for those looking to reduce their carbon footprint and take
advantage of renewable energy sources.
To begin, the conceptualization process was conducted using theoretical frameworks and
research in the field of wind power. This resulted in the creation of four different concepts
with varying structures and efficiencies. Qualitative comparison of these concepts was
then conducted using knowledge gained from the research.
Subsequently, a Mechanical Model was created from the Physical Model to optimize the
width and length of turbine wings in order to maximize power output under nominal conôÙ
ditions and minimize bending moment under storm conditions at the bottom of the shaft.
This model was then converted into a Mathematical Model which was used to calculate
and optimize the system through a multiôÙobjective optimization algorithm.
The bottom of the shaft was analyzed for bending moment using the Elastic Curve EquaôÙ
tions and integrating them along a hyperstatic beam. As all the possible wing sizes studied
would affect the equilibrium forces and boundary conditions of the beam, a complex script
had to be developed to calculate and assign conditions based solely on the height and
width of the wing. The multiôÙobjective optimisation will call this function, along with the
power output, to calculate and graph the results.
Finally, with both qualitative comparison from conceptualization phase and quantitative
analysis from multiôÙobjective optimization, an optimal turbine concept with optimal wing
size was chosen.
The results of our optimization process have revealed that when the aspect ratio of the turôÙ
bine (the ratio between blade length and rotor radius) decreases, power output increases
while bending moment remains low. This suggests that wider, shorter turbines are more
effective than the tall, thin turbines commonly seen in commercial Savonius VAWTS. A
2014 article published in the International Journal of Energy and Environmental EngiôÙ
neering supports this fact, although this article is explaining that wider turbines are more
efficient due to an increase in Reynolds number which improves wind turbine performance
and does not imply the bending moment of the shaft in the study [37].
A Design Failure Mode and Effects Analysis was conducted on the fixed wing size and
chosen turbine concept. Over forty lines were identified, but only a few were selected to
be addressed during the design phase. Initially, the Risk Priority Numbers exceeded the
security thresholdëƒ however, after the design process, all of them had been significantly
reduced due to the analysis of all turbine parts. The next steps of this process would be
to continue working on all lines from highest RPN to lowest, in order to address the most
dangerous situations first.
In the design phase, the wing size was predeterminedëƒ however, the cross section, twist
angle and end plates were determined based on scientific studies that evaluated the effiôÙ
ciency of Savonius blades. Analytical, numerical and graphical methods were then used
MultiôÙObjective Design of a Portable Vertical Axis Wind Turbine
63
==PAGE:76==
to calculate the machine elements involved in the turbine, resulting in a robust design.
The turbine was assembled as a modular product for easy assembly and flexible instalôÙ
lation. It can be mounted vertically or horizontally by simply altering the external support
frame, making it suitable for various scenarios and requirements.

%THIS FUNCTION CALCULATES THE SHEAR FORCE, BENDING MOMENT
%ANGLE AND DEFLECTION DISTANCE OF A HIPERSTATIC 3 SECTIONS
%BEAM USING ELASTIC CURVE EQUATIONS. THE INPUTS ARE ONLY 
%THE WING HEIGHT AND WIDTH.
 
function bed_mom = bend(height,width) 
    % BEAM CHARACTERISTICS
    Beam_sections=3;
    v_emergency=25; %Nominal Conditions:8m/s 
                    %High winds:25m/s
                    %Storm:50m/s
    Rho=1.26;
    H=height;
    l_support=H;
    W=width;
    l_F1=H/5; %distance applied F1
    l_F2=4*H/5;%distance applied F2
    d=0.03; %shaft diameeter
    F1=0.5*Rho*v_emergency^2*W*H; %forece calculation F1
    F2=F1;%forece calculation F2
    F_Const=-0.5*Rho*v_emergency^2*d; %constant pressure P
    P=F_Const;
    E=200*10^9; %young modulus
    I=pi*d^4/64; %Inertia
        %ELASTIC EQUATIONS
        syms x Q(x) C1 C2 C3 C4 C5 C6 C7 C8 C9 C10 C11 C12 %Variables
    
 
 
%CREATION OF ELASTIC CURVE EQUATIONS USING INTEGRATION
 
%SECTION A
