ABSTRACT
Refugee camps are designed to provide “aid” to a community that has been displaced by conflict. They offer temporary shelter until 
the conflict ends, but what if a conflict that began in 1948 is still active and with no hope of an early end? Are refugee camps planned 
for a long-term response?
This uncertainty is currently lived by the thousands of Palestinian refugees who were forced to leave their land after the declaration 
of the State of Israel back in 1948. Many of them emigrated to Lebanon and were settled across the many refugee camps that were 
established to receive them.
Shatila, in Beirut, is an example of these refugee camps. It was planned and established in 1949 to receive the Palestinian community, 
but 70 years of uncontrolled and illegal development have transformed Shatila into a marginal, insecure and disconnected area of 
Beirut. Furthermore, Palestinians in Lebanon are not guaranteed with the citizenship status, which makes it impossible for them to 
leave the camp.
Integration of Palestinians in Lebanon will potentially entail the legalization of their citizenship status. Nevertheless, their situation will 
still not be the same as for the rest of the citizens, due to the conditions they live with in the refugee camps. If we want to achieve full 
integration, we must change the circumstances that surround them. This is accomplished through social and economical integration 
within the rest of the city. 
The following thesis proposes several sustainable urban development interventions within Shatila that will equal the refugees’ con-
ditions to those of the rest of the Lebanese citizens. In addition, we thereby propose, that upon a successful implementation of the 
following interventions and after the achievement of a legal citizen status for the refugees, the integration of the Palestinian citizens 
of Shatila in Beirut will be complete.

INTRODUCTION/HISTORY OF BEIRUT
Beirut is the capital of Lebanon. With a metropolitan population 
around 1.340.000 inhabitants, it hosts 1/3 of the population 
of the country. Within its municipal boundaries, it hosts a 
population around 400.000 inhabitants, 1/10 of the country´s 
population.
Lebanon, and therefore Beirut, has been governed or under 
the mandate of different empires during its history (Roman, 
Ottoman, French, etc) until it became independent in 1942.
The location of the city, as an important commercial port 
between Europe and Middle East resulted in an economic 
development that led to the arrival of both Lebanese rural 
migrants and Syrian workers in search of new opportunities and 
to improve their living conditions. 
The outbreak of the Palestinian-Israeli conflict, after the 
declaration of the State of Israel (1948), also caused the arrival of 
many refugees from the neighbouring country. These Palestinian 
refugees, along with rural migrants and Syrian workers settled 
in refugee camps and informal settlements respectively and 
began to form a kind of urban structure that Beirut continues 
to drag to this day.
The situation worsened after the Lebanese civil war (1975-1990). 
During this period many of the buildings and infrastructures of 
the city were destroyed, and the city was divided into two parts 
by what was called the green line. The western part was the 
Muslim zone, and the eastern part was mostly Christian. 
This social fracture caused the displacement of the population 
between both areas of the city and increased the number and 
quantity of inhabitants living in informal settlements.
The reconstruction of the city started in 1991, after the conflict 
ended. It was carried out not only by the state but also by private 
initiatives. The reconstruction projects carried out caused also 
the displacement of many families that were settled in those 
areas. 
The city has suffered since then periods of economic growth 
but also periods of economic recession, which has caused 
inequalities within the city to increase. Currently, 25% of the 
population lives below the poverty line, many of them in these 
illegal settlements or refugee camps, and many of them are 
Palestinians. The time-line with the most outstanding events for 
Beirut history is shown in Figure 1. For more information about 
the history of Lebanon and the history of Beirut please refer 
to Appendix 1: History of Lebanon and Appendix 2: History of 
Beirut.

INTRODUCTION/SLUMS OF BEIRUT
It is difficult to give a general definition of slum, although a 
description is necessary to set the scope of action of the present 
thesis in context. Slums can be defined as informal settlements 
within urban areas. Slums are densely populated, and the vast 
majority of its inhabitants live under precarious economic and 
political conditions. In this type of settlements, services are 
scarce and living conditions are worse than in other areas of the 
city. Due to all the before-mentioned, people living in this type of 
settlement are considered to be more vulnerable.
Under this definition, it is difficult to identify which areas in 
Beirut can be considered slums. On the one hand, territorial 
re-organizations according to religion were accompanied 
by human migration within the country, hence resulting in 
continuous illegalities in the property rights when i.e. families 
occupied empty and not self-owned houses. On the other 
hand, the widespread disobedience of building regulations, 
as well as the limited public data available about these areas, 
makes it difficult to distinguish between slums or informal urban 
settlements in Beirut. 
Nevertheless, the slums in Beirut can be classified according to 
their location in the city (within the limits of the city or in the 
southern and eastern suburbs) or according the time when 
they were established. Slums that were established at the same 
time, as well as slums established in the same area tend to have

1. SLUMS THAT EMERGED AS REFUGEE CAMPS FOR 
IMMIGRANTS (1920 - 1955).
These types of settlements were the first so-called slums of 
Beirut. They were established after the arrival of Armenian 
refugees together with Syrian workers to Beirut in 1920.
These settlements were established outside the municipal 
boundaries of the city.
The massive arrival of Palestinian refugees after the 
establishment of the Israel state on 1948 consolidated the 
founding of this type of temporary settlements in the city.
1.1. ARMENIAN CAMPS.
The first informal settlement established in Beirut, and 
considered a slum, was the camp of Medawar in Qarantina. 
It was established in 1922 by the Red Cross, French Mandate 
and the League of Nations after the arrival of 10.500 Armenians 
from Cilicia.
On a first attempt of improving the living conditions of the 
inhabitants of the camp, thousands of tents were established 
on an empty public land. This first attempt of accommodation 
was implemented in 1926 by Armenian associations and French 
authorities. Thereafter, and in order to find a more permanent 
solution to the informal settlement, the authorities gradually 
relocated the refugees outside the camp in neighbouring areas 
and other low-income neighbourhoods.
A few years later, a “new wave” of Armenian refugees from

Alexandrette and Syria occurred in 1939. They established 
themselves in areas belonging to Qarantina camp and in the 
north of the neighbourhood (as can be seen in Figure 3).  
Qarantina camp suffered different attacks during the Civil War 
by armed militias. It was attacked in 1975 and it was destroyed 
on January 18, 1977. Its dwellers fled to other areas within the 
city. Some of them established in the Southern Palestinian 
suburbs while others occupied the downtown areas that were 
left empty during the war.
1.2. SYRIAC CAMPS.
The wave of Syriac refugees (also known as Assyrian), ethnic 
group originating from Turkey, Syria and Iraq, arrived due to 
the deportations carried out by the Ottoman Empire in 1920 in 
areas belonging to the current Turkey. In order to accommodate 
them, the Catholic-Syriac associations set self-help structures in 
camps as a temporary housing in the first stages (which became 
known as the Syriac Camp), and later carrying out a housing 
project in the nearby camp Havy el Serya’an.
In the following years more Syriacs from Turkey, as well as from 
Iraq and Syria arrived. They were mostly low-income workers 
arriving to the two previously established camps (Syriac Camp 
and Havy el Serya’an) in an attempt to look for employment 
opportunities. 
Syriac Camp was located in the eastern sector of the city. It 
was established on Waqf land – which refers to land owned by 
religious entities - and housed members of the Syriac-Catholic 
community. In 1971 it had 464 inhabitants in a mix of 103 
temporary and more permanent constructions. Most of the 
inhabitants were Syriac, but of Lebanese nationality (94%). In 
addition, there were also Syrians living in the camp. The camp 
had a dimension of 0.54 hectares (ha). In 1995, the Catholic 
Syriac church authorities decided to replace the “temporary” 
settlement for a residential building in which the current 
camp residents could be accommodated. The camp almost 
disappeared from the area.
Havy el Serya´an camp was also established in the eastern 
sector of the city. It grew from a few Syriac families on its 
establishment to become a Syriac neighbourhood thanks to 
the housing project carried out by Syriac-Catholic associations. 
Muslims rural migrants also occupied the camp at later points.
During the Civil War, the non-Christians groups left the camp, 
due to the location of the camp in the Christian side of the city. 
Their houses were mostly rented, and the whole camp was 
deteriorated. Today, the neighbourhood hosts Catholic and 
Orthodox Syriac groups, Lebanese rural-urban migrants, few 
Assyrians, and foreign workers.
1.3. PALESTINIAN CAMPS.
In 1948, the state of Israel was established. This marked the 
beginning of the Palestinian exodus from the newly born Israeli 
state towards different cities including Beirut. 
During the first years, they established themselves in rented 
places or in the Armenian camps. In 1950, a especial UN body 
emerged, the UNRWA (United Nations Relief and Works Agency). 
This UN body was responsible for organizing the relocation of 
Palestinians in the neighbouring countries of Israel. In order 
to accommodate the Palestinians who arrived in Beirut, they 
established camps in rented lands or from private property 
owners. In these camps, Palestinians could first set tents and 
then houses. However, services and living conditions were poor 
inside the camp.
In 1952, there were six Palestinian Camps within the metropolitan 
boundaries of Beirut, and fifteen in the whole country. Mar Elias 
camp was the only one inside the municipal boundaries of 
Beirut. Shatila Camp and Bourj El Barajneh were settled in the 
southern suburbs. Three camps were established in the Eastern 
suburbs: Dbayyeh, Jisr El Bacha and Tell Ez-Zaatar.
2. SLUMS THAT BEGUN TO ACCOMMODATE RURAL-URBAN 
MIGRANTS.
The industrialization of the country and the greatest economic 
boom of Beirut with respect to the rural areas of the country 
caused the massive arrival of rural urban migrants. Many families 
left their original assets and fled to Beirut. These migrants were 
mainly coming from the poor Bekaá Valley and the poor and 
unsafe southern regions.
These rural-urban migrants were looking for the employment 
opportunities that Beirut could offer in factories, institutions 
(airport, port) or in the construction sector. 
Newcomers to the city were established in different types of 
settlements: (I) Extensions of the existing refugee camps or in 
the nearby lands; (II) Settlements established on agricultural 
lands in the far suburbs of the city, which were breaking the 
building regulations; (III) Squatter settlements in areas of rivalry 
for property ownership between different groups.

2.1. EXTENSIONS OF EXISTING CAMPS AND LOW-INCOME 
AREAS NEAR INDUSTRIAL AREAS.
The first rural migrants that arrived were first established in 
areas within or near the existing refugee camps. This caused 
the camps to grow and extend to neighbouring lands where 
these groups could settle. Thus, the already existing camps 
experienced the expansion due to the arrival of new inhabitants.
2.1.1. Areas in the Easter Industrial Zone (Extension of 
Armenian Camps)
The Armenian camps had already been extended in 1930, as 
previously shown in Figure 1, occupying nearby areas. Armenian 
refugees were known by their craft experience, so the firsts 
industrial zones were established around these extensions. 
These areas attracted other groups due to the employment 
opportunities, as well as rural workers that arrived to the city. The 
more people arrived to these areas, the more densely populated 
they became, leading to the creation of new extensions around 
the originally established Armenian core.
2.1.2. Palestinian Camps extensions.
Rural migrants also established themselves near the Palestinian 
Camps in the southern and eastern suburbs of Beirut. The camp 
that saw a greater extension was Tell ez-Zaatar, in the eastern 
suburbs, which multiplied by five its surface: from 56.646 m2 
in 1950 to over 300.000 m2 in 1971, accruing a total of 24.500 
inhabitants together with its extension of Ras Dekwaneh. 50% of 
its inhabitants were Palestinians and 34% were Lebanese.
In the southern suburbs, four new camps were established; 
Sabra Camp grew as an extension of Shatila Camp. This camp 
grew from 1960, when rural migrants’ families mainly coming 
from South Lebanon arrived and established first at the camp

The slums located in the far suburbs of the city (see Figure 5)
were established in low-value agricultural lands which were 
close to factories. These areas started to be illegally urbanized 
from the 1950s. 
In the eastern suburbs, three slums of this type were developed: 
Zaayttiyyeh, Roueissat and the small slum of Hayy el Ayn. The 
three of them are similar, being Zaaytriyyeh the one that 
developed the first. In the same way, the slum of Havy el Sellom 
was developed in the southern suburbs of the city.
All these slums were occupied by migrants from rural regions 
such as Beka´a Valley and the southern regions. Land was sold 
from owners in small plots to the rural-urban migrants through 
the mediation of land developers. In these small plots - which 
areas could be as small as 100-200 m2 - the newcomer migrants 
could establish themselves. In these settlements, people from 
the same area of origin or family tended to settle closely. The 
construction was carried out by local contractors or by the

2.3. SLUMS DEVELOPED AS SQUATTER SETTLEMENT IN 
CONTESTED PROPERTY RIGHTS ZONES
Illegal settlements were only established in few locations in 
Beirut: Raml, Ouzai, Horch el Qateel , Horsh Tabet, Jnah and 
Sabra in the southern suburbs, and Wata el Mousseibeh inside 
the municipal boundaries of the city.
These slums of the southern suburbs were located in areas facing 
property-ownership problems during the French Mandate, 
when they abolished the concept of a single communal land that 
prevailed during the Ottoman Empire. Upon its abolishment, 
the lands were first turned into public land and then sold to 
private owners. The increment of the prices in the neighbouring 
areas and political conflicts, marked the development of these 
southern slums during the mid- and late nineties. Raml and 
Ouzai neighbourhoods grew in the 1950s and especially in 
1967, following subsequent family and village settlements that 
defined patterns in the occupation or renting of lands. Raml 
extended by 1975, at the beginning of the Civil War, over 17.7 
ha and hosted around 8.000 inhabitants.
The only slum within the municipal boundaries of Beirut, 
Wata el Mousseibeh, was established around the 1940s, while 
conflicts with terrain property owners such as the municipality, 
Waqf or private owners arised). These conflicts together with 
the absence of the property owners made occupation the 
easiest way to settle in the area. In this way, the residents

INTRODUCTION/SHATILA
Shatila Refugee Camp was established in 1949, aiming to 
accommodate the great wave of Palestinian refugees arriving 
to Lebanon from the northern villages of Palestine after the war 
and declaration of the state of Israel in 1948. 3
It was set up by the International Committee of the Red Cross. 
The land where it was established was rented by UNRWA, on a 
lease for 99 years.
Originally, Shatila Camp was established in the southern 
suburbs of the city, where the surrounding areas were sparsely 
populated. Those surrounding areas correspond today to the 
suburbs of Ghobeiry, a Shiite neighbourhood, and Tariq el Jdide, 
a Sunni neighbourhood.
Box 4: About Palestine.
Back in the days, Palestine was one of the Provinces of the 
Ottoman Empire. It was under the Ottoman governance 
until 1920, when it became under the British government 
upon its formalization by the League of Nations in 1923.
In 1948, Israel state was established on Palestinian 
territory, claiming its belonging to the Jewish community. 
Therefore, only Jewish by religion and genealogy became 
the legitimate inhabitants of the newly named State of 
Israel – and the Palestinian, in turn – refugees in refugee 
camps alongside the West Bank of Gaza or in neighbouring 
countries such as Lebanon.  Around 700.000 – 800.000 
people fled the country. 
The day that the state of Israel was established is sadly 
remembered among Palestinians as the “Day of the 
Catastrophe”. This day not only ruled the beginning of the 
war period, dispossession and forced migration, but also 
the beginning of an era of rightlessness, dependence, 
humiliation and a new essentialist identity - Palestinian 
refugee -.4 
Shatila stands out for its high population density and the poor 
environmental and health conditions. By its creation, it was 
home to 3.000 Palestinian refugees although nowadays it hosts 
a population of approximately 40.000 residents, evidencing its 
growth and development.5 
In addition, since its establishment 40.000 Palestinian refugees 
have been born in Shatila. From the total Palestinian population 
of Shatila, only 6.000 achieved the Lebanese citizenship upon 
the end of the Civil War in 1990, thus positioning the Palestinian 
society as second class citizens. This ban prevents Palestinians 
from establishing boundaries with the local population, hence 
further depriving them from the basic social and economic 
rights that the Lebanese society enjoys. 6
Bans had also a negative impact on the reconstruction policies 
inside the camp, thus contributing to the general insecurity 
feeling of Palestinians in Lebanon. Banning and the strict control 
on the reconstruction forced them to live under poor conditions 
in destroyed buildings.
This situation is the result of the Lebanese sectarian structure, 
in which its citizens are divided between the Sunni, Shiite and 
Christian community. Palestinian refugees belong mostly to the 
Sunni Muslim community. Therefore, and taking into account 
the Sunni-Shiite conflict, the full integration of the Palestinians 
in the Lebanese society will threaten the delicate confessional 
balance.6 Furthermore, armed militias inside the camp are also 
one of the main governments concerns. As they threaten the 
national stability, they further difficult the integration of Shatila 
and its residents in Lebanon.
Since 2011, with the outbreak of the Syrian Civil War, more than 1 
million Syrian refugees have arrived at Lebanon, resulting in  the 
congestion of Shatila with Syrian refugees. The reason behind 
the notorious high flow of Syrian immigrants was facilitated by 
pre-existing family relationships between Syrian refugees and 
the newcomers.

The population of Shatila has increased more than two-folds

gradually turned into hostility when the Syrian conflict became

Box 5: Situation of Palestinians inside Lebanon
The declaration of the state of Israel caused a massive 
departure of Palestinians away from Israel to neighbouring 
countries. An extensive number of refugees, mostly from 
Northern Palestine and Galilee, fled to Lebanon. They have 
experienced the feeling of rejection since they arrived to the 
country. They have not only been deprived from nationality 
but also from civil rights
These Palestinians in Lebanon together with the Palestinians 
of Gaza are not entitled to passports, only been allowed to 
have travel documents. They are therefore considered as 
stateless Palestinians. They have found themselves as a 
nation in a transition and temporary state for 70 years now. 
They have remained in a permanent “state of waiting” due 
to the lack of interest from the Lebanese government to 
accommodate them within Lebanese territories. Lebanon 
believes that the resettle Palestinians, which are mostly a 
Sunni- Muslim population, threaten its sectarian balance 
and therefore, the peace of the country. 
During the first years of the massive arrival of Palestinians 
refugees to Lebanon - and in response to the economical 
and social struggle this caused in the country - Lebanon 
implemented strict regulations to difficult the settlement of 
Palestinians within its territory.
It was then when UNRWA was established, that refugees 
began to receive help from this organization, which objective 
is to support and provide them with institutional assistance. 
UNRWA describes Palestinians refugees as: “any person 
whose normal place of residence was Palestine during the period 
from 1st of June of 1946 to 15th of May of 1948, who lost both 
their home and their means of livelihood as a result of the 1948 
conflict”. 7 Palestinian refugees are under the governance of 
UNRWA, due to this fact they are excluded from the UNHCR 
(United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees) and from 
its international security. 8
According to the United Nations High Commissioner for 
Refugees (UNHCR), there are 14 million refugees worldwide. 
They estimate that 5 million of them are registered with 
UNRWA, from whom 455.000 live in Lebanon. Among 
these, 51% live within the twelve refugee camps that were 
established by UNRWA.
The exodus of Palestinians to Lebanon – also known 
as Nakbah - was first received with compassion by the 
Lebanese society and overseen by the intelligence services. 
They only received aids from UNRWA, who tried to keep the 
Palestinian exodus as apolitical as possible, prohibiting them 
to establish political organizations. In fact, one of the UNRWA 
policies in Lebanon was to end up with their Palestinian 
identity and their sense of belonging to Palestine.
Furthermore, while the Palestinians who fled to other 
countries were first allocated in tents within refugee camps 
that were later replaced by simple brick constructions; 
Palestinians in the Lebanese refugee camps were forced 
to remain living in tents for decades to remind them about 
their refugee and temporary situation. However, this 
situation changed during the “Golden Age of the Palestinians in 
Lebanon”. This period began when Israel and Lebanon defeat 
the Arabs in the Arab-Israeli war. At this point,  the Israeli 
government let Palestinian militias such as the Palestine 
Liberation Organization (PLO) join Israel and Lebanon in the 
fight against the Arabs. Thereupon, The Cairo Accords were 
signed between the Lebanese government and the PLO in 
1969, allowing the Palestinian refugees in Lebanon to be 
granted with social rights, permission to form autonomous 
institutions inside the camps, freedom to move in the Arqub 
district , and the possibility to have an armed resistance 
against Israel.4
When Jordan expelled the PLO from its territories, they 
established themselves in Beirut and the situation of 
Palestinians in Lebanon improved, especially in the south. 
This improvements in the living conditions of Palestinians in 
Lebanon resulted in what was considered as “a state within

a state” in Lebanon, being the “Palestinian State” under the 
protection of the PLO. Lebanon provided Palestine with 
infrastructure, training centres, and allowed them to migrate 
to other neighbouring Gulf countries as skilled labourers.
Nevertheless, the Lebanese fear to the sectarian imbalance 
and retaliation by the Israeli army, resulted in the outbreak 
of the Lebanese Civil War. This war, together with the 
Syrian invasion of Lebanon in 1976, marked the end of the 
Palestinians´ “Golden age in Lebanon”. PLO left Lebanon in

Israel invaded South Lebanon and Beirut, Christian forces 
invaded the Sabra and Shatila refugee camps, cutting

government created a war on the camps killing more of the 
Palestinian population. 4
Finally, the persecution of Palestinians finished with the 
peace of Ta’if, which was signed in 1989, and the end of 
the Lebanese Civil War in 1990. Nevertheless, Palestinians 
were not allowed to recover the privileges the had before 
1982. They had their civil rights comprising culture, society, 
economy and land ownership restricted, as well as their 
prior earned freedom of movement. In addition, neither 
the destroyed refugee camps were rebuilt. From this point, 
Palestinians were discriminated and assaulted, and the 
destroyed camps became again a symbol to remind them 
about their status of temporariness and control.
Furthermore, Yasser Arafat agreed to not include the 
resolution number 194 of the UN in the Oslo accord of 1993. 
This resolution states: “Palestinians who acquired citizenship 
from other countries, or who moved outside the camps, are 
stripped of their refugee entitlements and lose their right to 
return to their Palestinian towns”. Therefore, this resolution 
guaranteed the refugees the right to return to Lebanon at 
the same time as de-provided them of their refugee status 
and the option to go back home to Palestinians towns. Yasser 
Arafat’s negation, together with the willing of the Palestinians 
to have a Palestinian identity, forced them to remain refugee 
in other territories different from Lebanon.
In the following years, hosting countries established aids to 
support Palestinian communities. 
In 2000, Israel left from Lebanese territories. But even if 
this event improved the situation, the Palestinian conflict in 
Lebanon still remained unstable. Since 2003, the relations 
between Palestinians and Lebanese began to normalize, and 
the prohibition for Palestinians to work in certain sectors 
was abolished. 9
In 2005, Syria abandoned Lebanese territories.4 During 
this period, the Palestinian civil society joined forces to 
demand security, the right to vote and land ownership to 
the Lebanese government, showing their relationship with 
the Lebanese hosting society.  
In the last years, the situation of Palestinians inside Lebanon 
has become worst, especially after the outbreak of the 
Syrian war. This is due to the fact that many international 
organizations, including UNRWA, have focused their efforts 
in helping the Syrian community while leaving the Palestinian 
society on their own luck.
Overall, the sensitivity of the conflict relies on the feeling of 
belonging and their national identity, as the refugee status is 
linked to their home settlements. The previously mentioned 
194 resolution of the UN links the refugee status and their 
presence inside the camps to their Palestinian homeland 
and their possibility to return home. Therefore, providing 
Palestinians with a different nationality to guarantee their 
cultural, social and economical integration, is far from being 
the needed solution to a cultural and national identity 
conflict.

URBANIZATION PROCESS OF SHATILA
The initial Shatila Camp established by the Red Cross and 
UNRWA comprised several tents on a piece on land of 1.5 km2. 
It was first occupied by fifteen families who fled from northern 
Palestine in 1948, when the Arab-Israeli war started.
The development of the camp continued until 1952, growing 
rapidly. This is one of the reasons why there has never been an 
organized census of the people living in the camp. Upon arrival 
to Shatila, people was distributed in tents according to family 
ties and according the area they were coming from.
Refugees who first established in the camp lived for 20 years 
in tents. This comprised the first phase of the urbanization 
process of the camp.
The second phase started during the 1960s. Upon losing the 
hope of returning to their original territories, Palestinians 
decided to improve their tents by building house units of bricks 
and zinc, although the conditions inside the camp were still very 
poor.
However, the emergence of the PLO helped Palestinians to 
improve their living conditions. Palestinians started to have 
more freedom and began to build one to two story buildings, 
thanks to the money they received from relatives living abroad.
During the Lebanese Civil War, the situation in the whole country 
was uncontrolled and chaotic. This context of conflict enabled 
the uncontrolled development of the camp, in which everyone 
started to build their own houses illegally. At the same time, the 
UNRWA built houses for the poorest residents of the camp. 
Most of the illegal constructions of Shatila were chaotically 
built during the 1980s. These illegal constructions were mainly 
vertical extensions of the resident’s constructions consisting on 
the addition of an extra level to gain space to accommodate 
new family members. The quality of the construction was fully 
dependent on the resident’s skills and economical possibilities. 
However, most of the families’ income were spent in adding new 
levels to their houses or building new ones. 
Building construction regulations were in-existent in Shatila. 
The construction process was characterized by: 1) UNRWA 
built common bathrooms in the refugee camp, but as soon 
as the residents had enough money, they would rather build 
their private ones. The bathroom was always the first room to 
be fixed; 2) Construction of bedrooms; 3) Construction of living 
rooms, which were commonly shared between neighbours.

REFUGEE CAMP - INFORMAL SETTLEMENT/DEFINITION
The word refugee has its origin in the French word “refugié”, 
which referred to the protestants which left France in 1685.12 
The word was used to refer to anyone who was looking for 
asylum until 1924, when it started to be used to define those 
fleeing home during the World War I.13 It was in the Refugee 
Convention of Geneva, in 1951, when the current term refugee 
was described. This Convention also established the legal 
protection and assistant for the refugee, the legal obligations of 
the host governments and of the individuals non-qualified with 
the refugee status.14
The term refugee is sometimes used indistinctly with the term 
migrant, but UNHCR definition of both terms shows certain 
differences. UNCHR defines the term refugee as “persons fleeing 
armed conflict or persecution” 15, while their definition of migrant 
covers “individuals who choose to move not because of a direct 
threat of persecution or death, but mainly to improve their lives by 
finding work, education, family reunion, or other reasons”. 15
The definition of refugee includes people persecuted because 
of their political or religious beliefs, or people from specific 
ethnic groups. In the same way, the definition of the term does 
not include individuals fleeing due to unpleasant economic 
situations or victims of natural disasters, although they also 
need humanitarian assistant.16
The organism responsible from deciding whether an individual 
must be considered a refugee is the UNHCR. They interview 
immigrants finding asylum in refugee camps and decide if they 
fulfil the requirements to be granted with the refugee status. 
UNHCR also tries to provide long-time or durable solutions 
for refugees. There are three different possibilities of durable 
solutions: 1) Refugees are repatriated voluntary to their country; 
2) Integration in the country of asylum; or 3) Permanent 
settlement in another country. The time that elapses until a 
solution is granted to them is spent in the refugee camps. This 
temporary solution might in fact, turn to a long-term or even 
permanent solution.16
Nevertheless, even if there is consensus on the definition of 
refugee, it is difficult to find a clear and common description for 
refugee camp. Some of the questions arising may be: What is the 
difference between an informal settlement and refugee camp? Or 
what turns an informal settlement into a refugee camp?
The term refugee camp is strongly linked to the migratory process, 
which describes the movement of individuals from their original 
settlements under different circumstances. UNHCR describes 
the term refugee camp as  “temporary accommodation for people 
who have been forced to flee their home because of violence and 
persecution” 17. This definition of the term refugee camp makes 
them seem places of relief, rescue and well-being, and differs 
a lot from other definitions of the same term. For example, 
refugee camps are also defined as “places of reproduction of 
a massive population of undesirables, kept in existence in spaces 
remote from everything”.18 The term undesirables refers to the 
opposition that the arrival of a crowd of individuals generates 
in the local population, as the incomers are seen by the local 
population as a threat to keep their comfort zone. Therefore, the 
local population may encourage the establishment of refugee 
camps (as defined by them) to maintain the “order”. This is how 
Simon Turner describes the camp, as “an exceptional space that 
is put in place to deal with populations that disturb the national 
order of things” 19. This definition emphasizes the exceptionality 
of refugee camps, in order to show that the camps are not 
established to be permanent, but to face a humanitarian 
emergency together with provision of international aid and 
humanitarian governments. This vision of the refugee camp as 
a place of response to an emergency and exceptional situation 
remarks its temporariness (until the restoration of the normality 
in the conflict zones) and its uncertainty, as the end of the 
conflict is unknown. The longer the conflict lasts, the more 
permanent the camp becomes, starting its inhabitants to build 
new beginnings in a state of suspense.

Refugee camps can be fully described by three criteria, as it is 
shown in Figure 9: 
•	
Extraterritoriality: because normally, refugee camps do 
not appear as cities on maps. 
•	
Legal exception: because although the refugee camps 
and its inhabitants are not considered citizens of the host 
society, they have to follow its regulations and laws.
•	
Exclusion: due to the undesired feeling of the refugees by 
the host society, who does not consider the refugees as 
part of their society. 
However, a consensus in the refugee camp definition can be 
achieved by defining them and establishing its limits, considering 
its spatially and temporally. Spatially refers to the physical 
boundaries of the camps, which can be physically crossed. 
Temporally refers to the time frame within the transformation 
from a temporary to a permanent settlements. 20
Refugee camps are established as formal settlements, although 
the permanency of the camps its influenced by their urbanization 
process, in which refugees become more involve as the time 
passes in their will to settle-down. This results in an irregular 
and illegal development of the camps as a direct consequence.
 
They are considered irregular because constructions are built in 
public lands or in areas that are not suitable for constructions. 
Other characteristics of irregular settlements are the lack of 
a public-private land division, occupation of protected lands 
and irregular land acquisition or precarious constructions. 
Furthermore, the camps’ infrastructures are usually in lack of 
public services 21.
The term illegal is used to define the settlements established by 
occupation of lands without legal basis, in which its inhabitants 
have no citizenship and its constructions are built without 
following building regulations.22 Continuous irregularities and 
illegalities are what transform refugee camps into uncontrolled 
urban settlements, caused by the high demand for housing 
compared to that offered by humanitarian governments.

4. SHELTERS LAYOUT:
The layout chosen for the shelters will determinate the creation 
of communities inside the camp. Once the camp has been 
divided in sectors, each sector is divided into smaller sections 
in order to make the management of the camp easier. To do so, 
shelters are usually grouped following two possible ways: 
•	
Organize shelters according to communities. Shelters 
would then be placed close to shelter from common 
communities and far from different ones, thus creating 
personal and private spaces. In this way, facilities would 
also be distributed according to the communities.
•	
Organize the shelters linearly in rows and columns. This 
layout encourages future extensions of the camp and is 
suitable when there is the need of a fast implementation 
of the camp. However, it breaks the community feeling and 
does not provide any personal or cultural space.
As previously mentioned, in many occasions refugees are already 
established in the camp site before it is officially established. In 
these situations, the modus operandi must be adapted to the 
existing reality:
•	
The most common approach is to avoid moving the pre-
established shelters and integrate the new facilities within 
the area without major interventions. If there is the need, 
accesses will be, and the camp will be conditioned to be 
suitable for future extensions.
•	
Sometimes it will be needed to reorganize the shelters 
when the health or safety of the residents is in concern.
•	
Serious problems such as water shortage, or security issues 
may result in a total restructuring of the existing settlement 
or moving the camp to a new location.
5. SHELTER DESIGN:
Shelters are needed to protect refugees from the atmospheric 
conditions and to give them privacy. However, the type of shelter 
needed, its design and materials used, and by who and how will 
it be built depends on each situation. 
Being each situation unique, some common strategies can be 
used:24
•	
Already built structures must be analysed in order to prove 
that they are suitable to accommodate the right number of 
refugees.
•	
Refugees must help in the construction of their shelters 
when possible, providing them with the needed materials 
and techniques.
•	
Local materials and techniques must be used when 
possible. Prefabricated tents and shelters must be avoided 
and used only during the first weeks.
•	
Minimum space of 3,5m2 per person must be respected, 
but it can be adapted in case of different cultural needs.
6. POST-EMERGENCY PHASE:
Once the emergency phase is finished, temporary shelters must 
be improved using more term solution due to their expensive 
price and difficulties to manage because they require economic 
funds.23 
This can create issues, especially political, in cases where the 
development turns temporary into permanent camps, which 
can create reluctance in the host society.

1. SOCIOLOGICAL PERSPECTIVE:
Society, social structures and social systems are the responsible 
for transforming and shaping communities, as well as refugee 
camps. 
This perspective studies the development of a refugee camp 
from its spatiality, when its limits were established and the 
inside and outside areas were easily distinguishable. Refugee 
camps have first a humanitarian form of governance, but that 
turns into a self-reliant social organization as the camp becomes 
more permanent and independent from the surroundings. This 
independence tends to break the link between camps and the 
humanitarian governments, which result in a hyper politicization 
and arises the concept of citizenships, which is opposite to the

division is created in order to “prevent contamination”  of the host

an exclusion and seclusion mechanisms which keep refugees 
out of the society making difficult their integration within the 
local community or their access to labour market or education. 
Therefore, refugees are physically, economically, socially and

1.3. SELF - RELIANT ORGANIZATION.
The withdrawal of the humanitarian organizations after the end 
of the emergency program leaves the refugees unprotected, 
arising internal rivalries, and being able to transform the space 
according to their needs. Survival stops being a collective goal 
managed by the humanitarian organizations and becomes an 
individual and self-managed objective. This instinct of survival 
increases the illegal and irregular activities, also supporting the 
emergence of leaders, which can create conflicts of interest and 
troubles. 
Once the NGOs withdraw the camps, its once excluded economy 
starts to become more included within the local economy. 
Camps become city camps after the end of the emergency phase 
and evolve towards being considered self-reliant communities 
within the process of urbanization and the efficiency of the 
economy. These processes also create collaboration between 
the surrounding areas and the refugee community, enhancing 
urban economy growth in the surrounding areas and making 
the host community dependent on the refugee camp (mainly 
from an economical point of view). The refugee community 
takes advantage of this situation to adapt the camp to the new 
After the first emergency stage, the camps increase the 
number of inhabitants and grow, exceeding the control of the 
humanitarian organizations. This growth forces the camps 
to evolve into a governance of care and maintenance, where 
education, health, security and social development is provided, 
resulting in more stable and less temporary city camps.
Refugees get used to the provided protection, aid and care, what 
makes them reluctant to go back with the so-called “dependency 
syndrome” 26. This is followed by reluctance from the host 
society, when they fear the refugee community as soon as the 
camps’ economy starts expanding to the surrounding areas. To 
this extent, there is a human flow between the camps and the 
surrounding areas. Refugees start settling in areas around the 
camp, in order to benefit from the international help provided 
inside the camp. In the same way, the local community benefits 
from the better health care and relief services that the camp 
offers. NGO´S presence inside the camp hinder the transition 
of the camp into an autonomous life, becoming an informal 
settlement in the process of evolution towards a self-reliant 
community.

1.4. FROM DE-POLITICIZATION TO HYPER-POLITICIZATION.
The camp was intended to be an apolitical space when it was 
stabilised. It was created as an aid structure to provide physical 
help and basic needs, considering refugees as merely human 
beings and not social entities. The apolitical structure was 
intended to avoid the creation of any political identity in the 
refugees established inside the camp, further unrooting them 
from their origins. This was a neutralization mechanism, to 
preserve the stability inside the camp and therefore prevent any 
influence in the local community. This can be considered as a 
de-politicization strategy.
The change in the refugee camps from being apolitical to hyper-
politicized spaces occurs when the camps grow and become 
denser and a permanent solution. New identities and thoughts 
start to arise inside the camps, which will usually be contrary 
to the legislation of the humanitarian governments that comes 
imposed from above. This generates tensions between the 
camps and the humanitarian organizations which, in many 
cases will decide to leave the camps and withdraw their support, 
further creating extreme political thoughts and thus contributing 
to develop hyper-politicized spaces, for more details refer to 
Figure 15.
Furthermore, these political ideologies usually appear together 
with nationalisms and the desire of the refugees to return 
to their original places, as they need to reduce the feeling of 
uncertainty they live with in a socially excluded camp.
Humanitarian governments try to look for a solution to this 
hyper-politicization of the camps. They usually approach this 
problem in two different ways: Either they accept the new 
political beliefs emerged inside the camps and try to help 
refugees to choose their representants, including marginalized 
groups inside the camp, or they can also decide not to offer 
any support withdrawing its humanitarian help from the camps, 
leaving refugees alone fighting for their own survival.

Citizenship defines whether an individual is considered member 
of a society or not. When a person is not granted the citizenship 
of the country, he cannot be considered a full member of the 
society.19

Citizenship can be considered in an exclusive or inclusive way. 
On the one hand, exclusive citizenship restricts some social 
services to citizens with certain characteristics, such as language 
or religion. Therefore, if a refugee is granted citizenship in an 
exclusive way, he or she may be restricted from enjoying certain 
services if language or religion differ from the host society. 
In addition, even if the citizenship allows them the urban 
incorporation, an exclusive citizenship keeps them away from 
the host society, being considered as undesirable by them. This 
discrimination increases the violence rates within the society.27
In an inclusive way, citizenship grants the same rights to all 
individuals in a society, and the same possibilities regarding 
working options and housing.27
The citizenship concept causes an impact in the nation-state. 
The granted/non-granted citizenship depends on how refugees 
are perceived by the host society. Refugees are accepted when 
they are seen as individuals supporting the nation state and 
the host society, therefore enjoying an inclusive citizenship if 
granted. On the contrary, refugees are marginalized when they 
are considered as individuals who threaten the nation-state.19
These marginalized individuals are considered undesirable, are 
hardy granted with the citizenship and are usually grouped in 
the refugee camps, where normal order is facto-suspended.28 
Different laws are applied, making the camp to be considered a 
“no-ruled” city within the city, in which the links between nation, 
state and citizens are challenged by refugees.
However, the suspended rule inside the camps also allows for 
exceptions to happen. The same way some refugees are not 
granted with the citizenship and therefore are not considered 
members of the society, not all refugees are granted with the 
refugee status and therefore allowed to stay inside the camps.28
1.6. PERMANENT TEMPORARINESS.
The definition of refugee camp is link to the concept of 
temporality, but also with uncertainty. Temporality is what 
differences refugee camps from cities and hinders its camps’ 
citizens to define their future. Its structure makes it comparable 
with a city but impossible for its inhabitants to reach a normal 
urban life, because they see their permanence in the camp as 
temporal.
The more permanent the camp becomes, the more it evolves, 
and the more it differs from a city, because camps are shaped 
according to the residents’ needs without any control or 
regulation. The “hope of return” of its inhabitants makes it 
impossible for them to get rid of the temporary nature of the 
camps.29

2. ANTHROPOLOGICAL PERSPECTIVE.
The non-citizen status of refugees in the host society is the 
responsible for their exclusion, when ethnic differences become 
barriers when describing the refugee camp development from 
an anthropological perspective.
First, the ethnic differences between refugees and citizens result 
in ethnic-nationalism ideologies among the camp dwellers. 
The temporary and permanent nature at the same time of the 
refugee camp makes, its inhabitants, to define new identities, 
which become stronger the more permanent the camp 
becomes, and directly depending on their inclusion or exclusion 
within the host community.
2.1. ETHNIC-NATIONALISM OF REFUGEES.
When refugee camps are excluded from cities, they are merely 
considered as a group of individuals from different ethnic groups 
and backgrounds.18 This division promotes the construction of a 
common and national identity within the camps’ refugees, which 
is often different to the citizens nationality due to the ethnic-
segregation.  
Furthermore, refugee camps increase the link between refugees 
and their history, further hampering the refugees’ will to adapt 
to the hosting society. In this context, as refugees might hardly 
adapt to the city’s culture, the cosmopolitan life of the city is an 
inaccessible reality for the refugees, being thus forced to remain 
in the camps.26 
These opposite realities between camp dwellers and citizens 
result in the inclusion or exclusion mechanisms between camp 
and city. The isolation of the camp from the city’s daily life and the 
containment of the refugees within the camp’s limits becomes 
the normal and unchangeable reality of the city. Therefore, the 
freedom of the city inhabitants contrasts with the unfreedom of 
refugees within the camp.30
Furthermore, when the camp becomes a permanent living 
solution for the refugees, they tend to link their national identity 
to the camp’s way of living. Therefore, and in a hypothetical 
context, the further the refugees are from the presented 
camps’ living standards, the better they will integrate in the 
hosting society. This hypothesis will be further explored with the 
intervention described in the following Chapter 6 of the present 
thesis.

2.2. IDENTITY / POLITICAL IMAGE.
The temporary nature of refugee camps turns them into a 
place of disillusion. However, the more permanent the camp 
becomes, the more it is seen by the refugees as a place for new 
beginnings.19  It should be noted that a refugee has been pulled 
out from its history and background when being resettled in the 
new place of arrival. They build themselves a new identity but 
maintaining their refugee status, as they refuse to belong to the 
host society, aiming to go back to their countries of origin.
The camp thus turns into a moral community sharing the wish 
to return home, with an identity which is partly self-constructed. 
The non-belonging to the host society makes them to remain 
with their refugee status, in which their personal identity merges 
with their political image.
The camp is considered a device, becoming itself a city, to keep 
the national order of things.31 It is an instrument to care, contain 
and control refugees, forcing them to live in an apolitical space 
in a state of exception.32 Therefore, refugees become a new 
category, being considered as people out of place, excluded 
from legality and regulated and controlled by the law in the 
camp.33 Refugees are seen by society in two very different ways. 
They are either considered as a subject to help or a thief who 
threatens the stability of the host society. Citizens do not feel 
any moral obligation with refugees, and they become excluded 
from any ethical responsibility. 34
2.3. INDETERMINATE TEMPORARINESS. 
The temporary/permanent status of refugee camps creates a 
controversial situation. Refugees cannot settle permanently 
because they have the hope of returning to their homelands 
soon but, at the same time, they cannot remain in a temporary 
status because they might never be able to return. This situation 
forces them to live in a quasi-permanent situation, without 
knowing if it may end soon. They have to think about the camp 
as a present situation in order to face a future which remains 
uncertain.19
3. GEOPOLITICAL PERSPECTIVE
The refugee camp concept is closely connected to the 
geopolitical situation of every place. In a wide context, and as 
previously mentioned, refugee camps are spaces designed to 
contain and control refugees37. The refugee term defined as a 
mass displacement concept was first related to the World War 
I, when the Russian, Ottoman and Astro-Hungarian empires 
collapsed, and there was a complete reorganization of central 
and eastern Europe. This instability resulted in a wave of 
displaced people in search for shelter.
The term “refugee camp” started after the World War II, with the 
declaration of Human Rights in 1948. In the geopolitical context, 
it can be described as a technology of power, as a standard 
equipment to deal with mass displacement.31 
The Human Rights declaration in 1948, together with the United 
Nations Convention on Refugees in 1951, secure freedom of 
movement and asylum as fundamental rights for displaced 
communities. The application of these fundamental rights is 
relegated to the countries involved, which result in different 
geopolitical realities, according to different countries.31
The humanitarian organizations and governments are the ones 
that provide help to refugees, taking advantage of that help for 
controlling the stability of the hosting country, through political 
and social control in camps of the refugee community.26,38 By 
providing help to the camp dwellers holding the refugee status 
exert control and power over the refugees, as their well-being 
depends on the countries will. Refugee camps are, in this way, 
international technologies for controlling space and movement 
in a social and political context.31 
The problem may appear when camps are managed by military 
forces. Mass displacement of a large number of individuals 
is seen as a negative threat to the host community, to their 
political and social stability, and the massive consumption of 
resources.39

4. URBAN PERSPECTIVE.
From the urban perspective, the eternal question is whether 
refugee camps are included or excluded from cities. They have 
an important impact on the city planning, due to their sudden 
establishment as a fast response to an emergency. They are 
first conceived as efficient and punctual settlements whose 
construction process will be conditioned by the length of stay 
of the refugees.
They follow the models of urban planning, its principles of 
modular grids and grouped spaces, and its multiplicity of the 
functions and division of ethnics. All these principles led to 
the urbanization of the camps, in which other urban forms of 
defining citizenship cause the fight for their jurisdiction.10
4.1. EMERGENCY CONSTRUCTION PROCESS.
Refugee camps are built as a fast response to the arrival of large 
amount of people after an emergency.  Fast planning decisions, 
which can vary from military grid to isolated shelters, need to be 
taken to maintain the order and security of the society. These 
decisions need to consider efficiency of the camp and well-being 
of the refugees, different options on how to establish the camps 
and the cost of them. In addition, the costs of inhabitation of the 
camps also need to be considered. 35
This way of establishing refugee camps differs from the normal 
development of the city, especially in the way of thinking in 
solutions for urgent situations, compared with the long-term 
solutions that are deeply analysed when projecting the urban 
development of a city. Meanwhile city’s inhabitants want a 
customize home, the intention of humanitarian governments 
when establishing refugee camps is to keep the social order and 
security within the host country.36
4.2- MODELS OF URBAN PLANNING FOR ESTABLISHING REFUGEE 
CAMPS.
The so-called emergency models are created to minimize costs 
to the humanitarian organizations and governments as well as 
to create a social environment when an emergency situation 
needs a quick set up of a refugee camp.
The most expensive cost runs with setting up constructions and 
infrastructures. The emergency model covers two ways aiming 
to reduce costs:
•	
1) A first model proposes a modular grid layout. The 
advantage of these model is that under an increase in 
the population of the camp, it can be replicated, and the 
camp expanded according to the needs. The length of the 
roads is reduced, and all the available space is used.35 The 
design of the house units is made so as to provide comfort 
to the inhabitants. Usually, families who stay in the units 
extend their houses by adding floors. This modular grid 
layout creates a division between the camp and the local 
community and furthermore, its grid and solid structures 
prevents from cultural connection between its inhabitants. 
On the other hand, it is an easy model to establish and very 
practical to handle.36
•	
•	
2) On the other hand, another model that can be 
implemented is clustering. Contrary to the modular grid 
layout, clustering promotes the creation of a community. 
The models preserve the cultural value, because they aim 
to keep the connection between the camp dwellers and the 
surrounding city. There is a hierarchy in the public spaces 
and the transition between the public and open to the 
closed and private spaces is progressive. This is because 
the housing units follow a radial configuration, meaning 
that a central first unit expands radially in need of space 
upon arrival of new inhabitants to the camp. 35 This results 
in different levels of connection between private and public 
areas. The closed and private courtyards enhance the 
feeling of security, meanwhile the different open spaces 
when expanding radially result in different levels of privacy.
This configuration allows to use the area in an efficient and low-
density way. In this model, infrastructures and roads spread 
from the center of the camp, as well as the community and 
administrative buildings are centrally located, being the facilities, 
such as bathrooms and garbage systems decentralized.36
While the two models mentioned above aimed to reduce costs, 
other models can be used to create a more sustainable society. 
The first model that can be used is the multiplicity of functions. 
This model wants to encourage the creation of economic activity 
and defines the connective axes between the city and the camp 
as the best location for this activity. The model also establishes 
that high laboured building methods are more efficient as 
a housing option.  The multiplicity functions model propose 
mono-functional accommodation units that can be replicated.35 
There must also be a hierarchy between individual and public 
spaces.

4.3. COMPETING SOVEREIGNTIES.
In refugee camps, the contested citizenship, regulated and non-
regulated zones, exclusive and inclusive systems of governance 
and the internal competition between government states, 
religious institutions, international community and NGOs 
creates fragmentation and multiplicity. 
The individual rights ensured by the state create hostility with 
new citizenship forms,  that arises inside the camp. 
Finally, the ethnic division model can also be used to promote 
a more sustainable society. This model establishes a spatial 
organization of the camps, grouping refugees according to 
ethnic background, place of origin or clan.36
Overall, all models share that camps must be established away 
from borders and military centres, provide good services to 
its inhabitants and must be easily accessible from and to the 
city. They must be adaptable and let inhabitants to shape their 
private spaces.36

SUSTAINABLE URBAN DEVELOPMENT/DEFINITION
When the concept of sustainable development was first 
implemented, it was aimed to address a development process 
supporting the protection of the environment. It was described 
back in 1987 during the World Commission on Environment and 
Development as a form of development that meet the needs 
of the present generations without compromising the ability for 
future generations to meet their own needs. 40
It was later in 2005, during the World Summit, when the pillars 
of sustainable development were established. They described 
sustainable development as the satisfactory fulfilment of 
economic development, social development and environmental 
protection.41 These three concepts were first thought to be 
independent from each other, without any common link. 
On the one hand, economic development was perceived as 
the generation of employment. On the other hand, social 
development only concerned health and environmental 
protection aimed to reduce pollution. 
Nevertheless, upon its implementation, it was accepted 
that the three concepts were interconnected as social 
development implies economic activation and carries an 
intrinsic environmental impact. In addition, a deeper analysis 
of the concepts concluded that the three of them are not only 
interconnected, but also interdependent (please refer to Figure 
19 for a further description of the evolution of the sustainable 
urban development concept). 42 
In the urban context, sustainable urban development is referred 
to as the permanent adaptation, renovation and improvement 
of the physical structures and systems of an urban area. In 
addition, sustainable urban development also pursuits an 
economical progress in a way that it provides a satisfactory 
human environment with a minimal consumption of resources 
and a minimum impact on the environment. 43
Since the sustainable concept was first approached, it has 
not only been subjected to several discussions but also, it has 
entered the political agenda as a concept instead of a mere 
form of development.42
In order to achieve a sustainable development, the process 
begins in the planning phase of an urban development 
project. The way in which an urban area is conceived during 
its planning phase may affect its development, although it 
does not guarantee its accomplishment. In other words, a 
sustainable plan for an urban area does not ensure its further 
development in a sustainable way. There are some steps which 
must be followed in order to achieve a sustainable planned and 
sustainably developed area.

SUSTAINABLE URBAN DEVELOPMENT/SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT PROCESS
As mentioned above, sustainability is not a one-dimensional 
concept but a combination of social, economical and 
environmental factors that, in a long-term, will improve the 
quality of life of the current and future residents.
The three factors needed to be implemented in order to achieve 
a sustainable urban development are: 45
•	
Environmental: Improvements must be focused on 
achieving the same results but reducing the consumption 
and environmental impact.
•	
Economic: Upgrades must be oriented to increase the 
financial capacity of the most disadvantaged communities 
and promote equality.
•	
Social: There must be improvements towards promoting 
health and education systems and people cohabitation. 
The development considerations that must be accomplished in 
order to be considered sustainable include:
1. URBAN DENSITY:
This concept relates the number of citizens within the urbanized 
surface. It is directly related with:
•	
Built surface: The more occupied the surface is the lower 
efficiency in land use. The less built environment within a 
surface, the higher the needed maintenance of the urban 
infrastructures is.
•	
Use of public transportation: Scattered urban areas 
promote the private car use, but clustered urban areas 
congest the transportation systems.
•	
Complexity: The recommended density must be achieved 
by combining different urban and building typologies. 
Mono-functional urban areas become more vulnerable and 
less prepared to face internal and external setbacks.
•	
Endowments: Infrastructure and services for citizens 
become more expensive to manage, inefficient, less 
accessible in scattered urban areas, but become congested 
and insufficient in clustered urban areas if they are not 
lined with the number of inhabitants.
2. URBAN AND BUILDING TYPOLOGIES:
Sustainable urban development is linked with a mixture of 
urban and building typologies within the urban fabric:
•	
Different building typologies make it easier to organize 
the space, create singularity and make it possible to 
accommodate different types of citizens with different 
purchasing 
power, 
which 
promotes 
diversity 
and 
integration.
•	
A built environment must fit local conditions. For example, 
providing low-rise buildings in uneven surfaces or high-rise 
buildings in areas with wide roads.
3. MIXED-USED AREAS AND BUILDINGS:
Industrial, commercial and tertiary activities must be integrated 
within the urban fabric. Mono-functional areas, which only 
support a single activity create urban gaps. Buildings must 
integrate different commercial and tertiary activities in the same 
way that industrial activity must be integrated in urban areas 
if environmentally possible. This will promote integration and 
diversity.
4. GREEN AREAS AND PUBLIC SPACES:
Urban areas must integrate public spaces and green areas 
within its structure.  These spaces must be adapted to the 
needs of each area, establishing an equilibrium between built 
and public areas. Green areas must also be adapted to local 
and climate conditions, in order to have a keep maintenance to 
minimum.

cities with good infrastructures strengthening hygiene and 
safety and increase the attractiveness of an area.
6. FACILITIES:
Facilities must be provided according to the number of citizens 
and must be integrated within public spaces and green areas. In 
addition, they need to respond to the citizens demands and be 
appealing to all ages and ranges.
7. SUSTAINABLE MOBILITY MANAGEMENT:
Promote public transportation, pedestrian streets, and reduce 
the amount of traffic by reorganizing the size and number of 
traffic roads within the urban fabric.
8. REDUCE, REUSE AND RECYCLE:
Sustainable urban development strategies must encourage 
reduce, reuse and recycle habits, in order to avoid the 
generation of household and urban waste, inflicting the least 
possible impact to the environment and other citizens.
9. INTERACTION BETWEEN URBAN AREAS AND SURROUNDINGS:
Different urban areas should not develop independently of its 
surroundings, but instead, follow a parallel growth with adjacent 
zones. In the same way, different areas within the same city 
must develop closely to avoid a social and economical division.
10. SOCIAL COHESION:
Social coexistence, equality and citizen participation are 
instruments to measure level of achievement of the sustainable 
urban development. Urban areas must provide an optimal 
physical and mental health, thus generating social benefits.

FROM REFUGEE CAMPS TO SUST. URBAN DEVELOPMENTS/SUSTAINABLE PLANNING Vs. REFUGEE CAMPS
The planning process of a refugee camp (please refer to Section 
2.2, pages 41 - 42 for further details) differs widely from what 
it is considered a sustainable urban development process. The 
first difference - which is crucial to understand the planning 
process of refugee camps - is the time needed to carry out the 
project proposal.
•	
Refugee camps are intended to host a large number of 
individuals after a conflict forces people to flee their homes. 
They are established to provide a fast aid to an emergency 
situation; thus, the project needs to be performed and 
the infrastructure built as fast as possible. Therefore, time 
is the determinant factor pressing the planning process 
of refugee camps, what makes it different from what it is 
considered a sustainable urban planning process.
•	
A sustainable urban planning process aims to anticipate 
urban growth, hence designing urban areas capable of 
accommodating future new citizens. This type of urban 
development is dependent on a careful analysis of the 
potential growth, as the area must be flexible enough 
to adapt to the needs of the future citizens without 
compromising the present citizen’s needs.
Both of the planning processes differ in their phases in the 
following way:
PHASE 1: Goals & Principles: The planning process of both 
refugee camps and sustainable urban areas begins with defining 
clear principles. On the one hand, - in the case of the refugee 
camps - this phase follows a unique and common objective of 
providing fast accommodation to a large number of people. By 
contrast, - for sustainable urban areas - the goal may differ from 
project to project.
In addition, the principles also differ: On the one hand, the 
planning principles of refugee camps aim to develop a quick and 
cheap solution for housing according to the well-established 
refugee camps’ standards. On the other hand, the sustainable 
urban development principles are connected to various 
sustainable factors such as environmental, sociological and 
economical factors, all following a community-based thinking. 
PHASE 2: Analysis: Regarding refugee camps, the urgency with 
which the project needs to be designed makes it impossible 
to carry out any detailed analysis beforehand. In words of the 
UNHC, the only analysis performed during the site selection 
phase is a vulnerability assessment analysis. This is performed 
under the UNHC’s vulnerability screening tools, which are not so 
in-depth developed and present some limitations, mainly due to 
a lack of analysis of the before-mentioned sustainability factors 
46.
By contrast, the analysis phase is the core phase in the 
sustainable urban development planning process. This 
phase will determinate the crucial aspects of the project and 
anticipates potential complications. This is carried out through a 
deep economic, environmental and social analysis of the area to 
be developed together with its surrounding areas.
PHASE 3: Design: The design phase in a sustainable urban 
development consists on different stages that begin with 
covering the whole area that will be developed followed by 
focusing on each specific aspect needed to accomplish the 
set plan. In the same way, the design phase when planning a 
refugee camp is also divided in different stages (please refer to

urban development design phases.

congestion of these services.

Facilities inside the camp have also undergone a slower 
development than the buildings and the population. Primitive 
facilities such as low-quality public toilets, were built by the 
Lebanese government and UNRWA. During the development 
of the camp and the construction of private buildings, some 
facilities were also built by the camp dwellers and integrated 
within the buildings. However, the overpopulation of the camp 
and its buildings make these facilities insufficient and, in many 
cases, deficient.

Mobility is an important aspect of sustainable development, and 
it is closely linked to the term connect. In the example of Shatila, 
sustainable mobility has never been promoted by the fact that it 
was established as a place of exclusion, separated from the city. 
Its demarcation has caused Shatila to lack adequate mobility, 
with no expectation of improvement, due to its suspended state.
REDUCE, REUSE AND RECYCLE
In an area where primary needs are scarce, these have prevailed 
over the concept of sustainable development such as reduce, 
reuse and recycle.
INTERACTION BETWEEN URBAN AREAS AND ITS SURROUNDINGS
The connection, interaction, and parallel development of Shatila 
and its surroundings is tightly linked to the development of 
social, economic and legal aspects. 
The exclusion mechanisms in order to “prevent contamination” 
of the host society from the refugee community, together with 
the dependence that refugees have on external aids, creates a 
physical barrier which leads to inequalities and hinders social 
integration.
The non-granted citizenship status also hinders social integration, 
and makes it impossible to boost economic development, which 
in turn is reflected in the lack of any type of industry in the area, 
and therefore in the lack of economic integration.
These negative aspects that might try to be solved, are facing 
the psychological barrier that generates the suspended state 
in which Shatila is. On the one hand, the fact that the camp has 
remained in use for 70 years despite being constructed as a 
temporary settlement could make us think that it is a permanent 
solution. Nevertheless, the non-granted citizenship status of 
Shatila’s dwellers increases the temporary nature of the camp 
and the desire of its inhabitants to return to their original assets.
SOCIAL COHESION
Creating a good co-existence between Shatila’s inhabitants is 
the goal of a sustainable development. Due to its division in four 
different sectors, Shatila has also experienced the lack of social 
cohesion between its inhabitants. Although the camp, after its 
expansion, hosts many different nationalities, Palestinians are 
the only ones in lack of freedom of movement and a citizenship 
status. This creates differences within the camp inhabitants, 
making Palestinians feel even more isolated, thus making the 
Palestinian sector a further excluded area within an already 
excluded area.

FROM REFUGEE CAMPS TO SUST. URBAN DEVELOPMENTS/MISSING PHASES
The planning and development processes of a refugee camp 
differ from the ones of a sustainable urban development. Key 
differences during the planning process are detailed below:
Firstly, as can be seen in Figure 26, there is a lack of a deep 
economical, social and environmental analysis during the 
Analysis phase, not only in the area where the project will be 
implemented, but also on how the surroundings will be affected. 
The vulnerability assessment that is carried out, slightly analyses 
how the refugee camp will influence the surrounding areas 
economically or socially or how the surrounding areas will in 
turn influence the camp. However, the possibility of the refugee 
camp turning into a long-term settlement is rarely analysed 
throughout the planning phase, what is crucial to anticipate the 
impact of the camp in the neighbouring areas.  
On the other hand, within the design phase, the lack of designing 
public spaces leaves public areas meaningless for the public 
use, hence just been non-constructed soil. The consequent lack 
of public interactions within the slum´s area eases the loss of 
the feeling of belonging, causing the tendency to make a private 
use of the public spaces such as illegal building of self-owned 
constructions.  
As a consequence of building private extensions in the public 
area, the characteristic irregular and uncontrolled development 
of slums is a common trade of the development process of a 
refugee camp. 
The own nature of a refugee camp and its demarcation, together 
with the lack of a deep social, economic and environmental 
analysis, triggers a different pace between the development 
of the refugee camp and its surrounding areas. While the 
surrounding areas develop, the camp stays in a suspended 
state, fully dependent on external aids. The development of the 
camp only starts when its dwellers begin to understand that 
their settlement in the camp might not be a temporary, but   a 
permanent solution. Therefore, adapting the camp to their 
domestic needs becomes necessary. 
Nevertheless, the different rhythm in growth has prevented 
the camp dwellers and the host society from building social 
and economic relationships, thus breeding a vast social and 
economic division between the camp´s refugees and the urban 
citizens.
Another fundamental aspect to understand the differences 
in the development process between the refugee camp and 
a sustainable urban development is the banned citizenship 
that Palestinians have in Lebanon. Consequently, they are not 
considered legal citizens and therefore, cannot work legally or 
benefit from the freedom of movement within the country.
Taken all together, Shatila refugees are hold up in a social-, 
economical- and environmentally isolated area of Beirut, leaving 
its nearly 41.000 citizens in lack of a Lebanese citizenship and 
thus unable to benefit from the same services or enjoy the same 
amount of public areas as their host society.

Upon stating the shortcomings that Shatila shows as a 
permanent refugee camp, and after describing the impact that 
those missing phases in the planning and development process 
of the refugee camp have caused on its own evolution, the 
present work will further propose an intervention to revert the 
social and economical isolation of Shatila in Beirut.
Shatila has experienced two different processes:
•	
Firstly, a planning process in which the camp was designed 
as a fast response to an emergency situation took place 
prior its construction.
•	
Next, a development process, which began when the 
emergency situation finished and the situation of the camp 
dwellers turned from a temporary nature to a permanent

METHODOLOGY
In order to achieve the before-mentioned goals, the methodology 
must adapt to the two different scenarios of the intervention:
•	
A)  The social and economic integration of Shatila is based in 
the relation between the camp and the surrounding areas.
•	
B) The design of public spaces intends to increase the 
amount of public spaces within the boundaries of the camp.
Thus, meanwhile the first Aim comprises the relationship of 
Shatila with the surrounding areas, the second Aim focuses 
in the refugee camp itself, please refer to Figure 31 for more 
details.
A) SOCIAL AND ECONOMIC INTEGRATION:
To reach a social integration undergoing economical and legal 
union, physical interaction between the different communities 
is needed. Physical interactions will provide the refugees with 
the opportunity to express themselves. Nevertheless, different 
kinds of people mean different personalities, implying several 
and distinct  ways of expression and communication. Therefore, 
enhancing different ways of expression between the camp 
dwellers and the host society will promote the aimed physical 
interactions to achieve a social and economical integration.
To do so, the intervention will rest on the five different ways 
in which a message can be transmitted: by listening, reading, 
writing, speaking or looking. In this way, five independent but 
interconnected interventions will be carried out in five different 
areas of the camp.
Being these five interventions the responsible for promoting 
a physical interaction that will result in a social and economic 
integration, each intervention will thus  be built in  a way that 
both communities can be socially and economically integrated. 
B) PUBLIC SPACES
Reaching the same amount of public spaces within the camp 
than in the surrounding areas is a way of promoting equality 
between both communities.
The development of Shatila occurred both in surface and in 
height. The uncontrolled and unsustainable development 
occasioned that the public areas on the camp surface paved the 
way towards private spaces for the dwellers, making impossible 
the generation of public spaces at the ground level. Furthermore, 
the type of construction in Shatila is characterized by a medium 
size building with flat roof. These roofs are currently not publicly 
accessible, so residents cannot benefit from them.
Therefore, the present thesis suggests that by opening these 
spaces and generating public spaces in them, the residents 
of Shatila will relish the same amount of public-use spaces as 
the habitants of any other are of the city, thus promoting the 
equality between the different communities.

URBAN INTEGRATION/PURPOSE OF INTEGRATION
Integrating an area of the city consists on providing it with the 
same conditions or facilities than the rest of the city.
The stages that have not been carried out during the 
planning and development phases of Shatila have resulted 
in a lack of connection between Shatila and the surrounding 
neighbourhoods as well as in between its inhabitants.
Shatila is currently facing the need to open up to the city, to 
become one neighbourhood more, not an excluded and 
marginal area of Beirut. It is necessary that the inhabitants of 
the surrounding neighbourhoods see Shatila as an attractive 
area they want to go to, that offers something different and 
not found in their neighbourhoods. This will also transform the 
current vision of Shatila as a focus of insecurity and crime into 
an attractive space of Beirut.  
Besides the fact that Shatila must be transformed into an 
attractive area to integrate and open up to the rest of the city, 
it must also be transformed to improve the living conditions of 
the people living in Shatila, who suffer daily from the conditions 
of the camp.
For this reason, the proposed intervention in this thesis follows 
two objectives, which are hereby stated and summarized in 
Figure 32: 
•	
Aim 1: To open Shatila to the city and attract neighbouring 
communities to spend time within the camp, thus achieving 
social and economic integration by physically bringing both 
communities together. 
•	
Aim 2: To equal the living conditions of the inhabitants 
of Shatila to those of the neighbouring communities, by 
increasing the amount of public spaces. Public spaces were 
not designed during the planning phase and has been 
reduced due to its uncontrolled development.

Both areas are divided by a two-way road and two-three lanes 
per direction, creating a gap of more than 18 meters (m) 
characterized by a lot of traffic. 
The plan of bringing both communities closer together follows 
the “road dieting” technique, which consists on: 1) Reducing and 
narrowing the width of the lines from three to two lines of 3 
meters (m) each in this case; and 2) Creating a pedestrian refugee 
island in order to provide a safer pedestrian environment.48 
This can be seen in Figure 35:
It may seem that by reducing the number of lanes and their 
width can traffic congestion will be generated. However, 
the sociologist Lewis Mumford contradicts this theory and 
establishes a justification that supports the theory that traffic 
congestion and narrowing road lanes are unrelated.
“Increasing road width to reduce congestion is the same as loosening 
your belt to fight obesity.” 49
Lewis Mumford
In fact, according to the World Resources Institute, roads with 
3 m lanes can absorb up to the 93% of the traffic of 3.6 m lane 
roads, and also by decreasing the width of the lanes, the speed 
is also reduced, which increases safety and the chances of 
survival in the event of an accident. 48
The reduction of the width and the number of lanes will make 
it easier to cross the road and access Shatila from the northern 
area of Ghobeiry. The gap to cross, after the intervention, will 
consist of 2 lanes in each direction separated by a pedestrian 
refugee island. All in all, the overall distance of roadway to cross 
will be 6 m, as shown in Figure 36,A.

The area chosen to foster integration through visual 
communication is located at the eastern end of Shatila. 
The area to be intervened has an area of about 5000 square 
meters and is currently unoccupied, with empty lots and 
vegetation growing uncontrollably.
The adjacent neighbourhood is Chiyad. This neighbourhood 
developed during the 70s and 80s, and is now characterized 
by residential buildings with private common areas around 
them. The area has commercial activity, largely dedicated to 
restoration, and hosts middle class families.
Shatila and Chiyad are divided by the Jamal Abdu Nasser 
highway, an important connection between the south and the 
centre and eastern parts of the city. It comprises three lanes in 
each direction plus two access lanes on each side, thus creating 
a gap between the two neighbourhoods of about 40 meters, 
please refer to Figure 41 for details.

PHYSICAL INTEGRATION
The physical gap separating the two neighbourhoods will be 
eliminated by building an elevated pedestrian bridge connecting 
both communities. The highway is heavily trafficked and trying 
to create an access controlled by traffic lights would congest the 
traffic greatly. In addition, an elevated access is much safer than 
a zebra crossing at the same level as the car traffic, thus justifying 
the physical connection through an elevated pedestrian bridge. 
The location of the new pedestrian bridge access can be seen 
in Figure 41B.
Furthermore, with the present intervention we also aim to 
reduce car traffic in the area. To do so, and according to the

As for the Reading and Listening intervention, security will also

SOCIAL INTEGRATION
The area aims to promote the integration of Shatila with 
adjacent communities through sight. For this reason, and 
with the intention of attracting as many people as possible, an 
open-air cinema has been designed, which will also constitute a

After analysing both the neighbouring areas and Shatila, it is 
noticeable that there is no installation of this type. Furthermore, 
a cinema represents a powerful force that can, apart from 
entertaining, raise awareness and spark emotions. This can help 
both the inhabitants of Shatila and Lebanon to get to know the 
Lebanese and the refugees’ culture respectively, breaking down 
prejudices.
Its square shape and its different areas make it a multifunctional 
space including both seating areas and spaces for social activities, 
etc. The area located at the street level shown in Figure 41D, will 
be a covered space that can be used all the yearlong. There will

Another activity that can promote social integration through 
sight is photography. For this reason, two photo booths will be 
installed in another area adjacent to the open-air cinema. In 
addition, together with the booths, panels will also be installed 
as part of the urban furniture. The idea is that the taken photos, 
both from the inhabitants of Shatila and the rest of the citizens,

also be included as part of the urban furniture, thought to 
allow interaction between people who access the area and sits 
down in the benches. In addition, the intervention will also be 
completed with a planned planting of new trees that will replace

intervention will promote economic integration between Shatila 
and the surrounding areas.
Businesses in the area will also benefit from the intervention, 
since after making it more attractive, the number of people – 
and potential customers - visiting the area will increase. In turn, a 
wider economy will boost further economical development, as 
new needs will arise leading to the opening of new businesses. 
All the before mentioned will result in more employment options 
benefiting   the inhabitants of Shatila and reduce their high rate 
of inactivity.

The area chosen to promote integration through speaking 
does not constitute an area itself, but the main arterial road 
within Shatila responsible for the vertical division of the camp. 
The reason why this street was chosen is because it shows 
the potential to serve as a connection between the two 
divided neighbourhoods of Shatila. Therefore, Shatila itself 
will potentially also be united upon the implementation of the 
present intervention. In addition, and due to the location of this 
street, this street will conform a link between Shatila, Sabra (in 
the south) and Ghobeiry (in the north), please refer to Figure 44 
for details. 
This street is known as Sabra market and is characterized by 
being a street full of all kind of businesses, from repair shops to 
electronic retailers. It is characterized by a high amount of traffic 
and pollution, in addition to the large influx of inhabitants of 
Shatila who make their purchases here.
The physical, social and economic aspects of the intervention 
that will help this street to become a meeting and integration 
place for the inhabitants of the three before-mentioned 
neighbourhoods, are grouped in the following Table 11:
PHYSICAL INTEGRATION:
In the same way as for the previous spaces – and as stated in 
Box 7 in page 85–  car traffic will be reduced by converting the 
road from a two-way to one-way, with a final width of 3 m. This 
traffic reorganization will not only reduce the amount of transit 
in the road, but also reduce the speed at which vehicles can 
circulate on this street, therefore enhancing pedestrian safety 
in the area.
To further promote pedestrians’ security, vehicle and pedestrian 
traffic will be physically separated through a line of street-lights, 
street-furniture and trees. Furthermore, and as previously 
mentioned, increasing lightning of the streets will enhance the 
sensation of security during the night, in addition to making 
the area more attractive to visitors. Concretely, the planned 
street-lights will comprise vertical supports serving as holders 
for cable-hanged street-lamps. This kind of street-lamp is more 
effective, since it lights up both the central and lateral areas of 
its customizable installation site.
SOCIAL INTEGRATION:
Aiming to create spaces that promote integration through 
speaking, seven different interventions will be designed alongside 
this street. All interventions are focused on oral communication 
and adapted to all age ranges, so that everyone – from the 
youth to the elderly – can benefit from social interaction and 
therefore integration.
All interventions have been designed following a bi-directional 
“age gradient”, so that interventions located in the extremes of 
the streets have been planned for the younger, while the central 
ones for the adults and elders. This is shown in Figures 43 and 
44.
According to this, interventions 1 and 1´ will be aimed at 
children, interventions 2 and 2´ will be aimed at young people, 
interventions 3 and 3´ will be aimed at adult people, and 
intervention 4 will be aimed at families and old people. They 
have been designed this way so integration through speaking 
begins at an early age.
Parents from neighbouring communities will take their children 
so they can interact with children from Shatila. When these 
children	grow up, they will	also find a place to interact through	
speech in the following interventions. This will continue to 
happen until the new generations, born of these children, begin 
to use these areas in the same way as previous generations. 
This will create a chain reaction.

The solution to provide Shatila with elevated public spaces is 
theoretically implemented in the following case, in which an 
elevated urban playground for kids will be designed at the top 
of one of the buildings run by the Lebanese non-governmental 
organization Najdeh.
Najdeh works with the commitment of upholding the human 
rights of the Palestinian community in Lebanon. Specially, they 
aim to empower the Palestinian refugee women and children, 
who are considered the most vulnerable elements of the refugee 
community. Furthermore, the organization, stands up for 
providing the Palestinian society with the instruments needed 
to achieve a social, economic and political equality, further 
supporting them to contribute with a sustainable development 
to the most vulnerable sectors of the Palestinian society.57,58 
Najdeh runs twenty-nine centres across nine Palestinian camps 
in Lebanon, where the organization implements its programs 
geared for Palestinian women and children. The elevated 
playground that will be designed is planned to be located in one 
of the community centres that the organization runs in Shatila 
camp. The Shatila community centre receives a group of around 
120 refugee children aged from 6-15 years. The vast majority of

CONCLUSION
The planning and development of refugee camps have negative consequences on the people who live in them, especially when these 
places, conceived as a situation of temporary accommodation, become permanent. 
The fact of not being socially and economically integrated with the rest of the city and being in imbalance with them causes isolation, 
incomprehension and repudiation of its inhabitants.
Shatila is one of these cases It was conceived as a temporary aid solution until the resolution of the Palestinian-Israeli conflict, 
but the non-resolution of the conflict has made the camp still standing 70 years later. This place, not conceived as a permanent 
accommodation, and the different events and situations throughout its history have turned it one of the most vulnerable and 
depressed areas of Beirut, as well as its inhabitants. In addition, the Palestinians do not have the legal status of citizens in Lebanon.
To achieve a full integration of the Palestinians in Beirut, it is not enough to grant them their citizenship status, since they would 
continue living in the same isolation and inferiority conditions. The fracture between Palestinians and the Lebanese society is a 
combination of their legal situation and the factors surrounding Shatila.  It is necessary to integrate Shatila within Beirut to integrate 
its inhabitants, so that when their legal situation will change, they will be fully integrated.
The interventions carried out aim to achieve this integration and equilibrium between Shatila and the surrounding areas, and 
therefore between the inhabitants of both communities, changing the image of the Palestinians as the eternal refugees. This can 
be achieved because people do not make the place but the place makes the people. Palestinians do not turn Shatila into a refugee 
camp, but Shatila does turn them into refugees, so by carrying out these interventions, we will remove that imposed label that 
separates them from a society in which they have lived for more than 70 years.

APPENDIX 2/HISTORY OF BEIRUT
Beirut is nowadays a vibrant city full of contrasts. The name 
Beirut comes from the Canaanite-Phoenician word “bee-root” 
which means “wells”. It was first mentioned in the 15th century 
B.C. The city is located in the centre of the Lebanese coast and 
is built on a high area which descends towards north-west, west 
and east, being the most pronounced descent towards the 
north. 60
There are 3 elevated areas within the city; Moussaitbeh, Kraytem 
and Achrafieh, reaching a maximum high of 95 meters above 
sea level. These elevated areas suffered the most accused 
development during the first phase of the urbanisation. From 
then the city continued its growth westward (sandy dunes) and 
towards the plain East (agricultural fields), north (plain coast) 
and neighbouring mountains.62
The city has been continued its evolution till today, when it hosts 
a population of 2 million people divided in 12 different quarters.
Lebanon, and therefore Beirut was under the Ottoman empire 
till 1918. During this time, Beirut was not considered a major city 
within the Empire. Around 1840 the population of the city did no 
exceed 5.000 inhabitants.63

APPENDIX 2/HISTORY OF BEIRUT
The city began to grow exponentially in the last decades of the 
Ottoman Empire and in 1915 the city reached a population of 
130.000 inhabitants. By this time the city began to be considered 
an important coastal city of the Easter Mediterranean area and 
an important commercial port. Beirut was used as a connection 
Between Europe and the inner countries of the Middle East Such 
as Syria and Iraq. This location of the city in a strategic position 
for the transit of goods marked its growth, and the urban 
economy of Beirut has always been centred on commerce and 
service sector.
The importance that Beirut achieved due to trading favoured the 
enrichment and the construction of numerous infrastructures 
to unite the city with other near cities, such as the completion 
of Beirut-Damascus road (1863) and the railway line to Houran, 
extension of the port (1895).62
The French mandate was characterized by the economic growth 
and the modernising policies that were carried out, known as 
Tanzeemat.
It was during this period, in 1920, when Beirut became the 
capital city of Greater Lebanon. This meant the establishment 
in the city of all the administrative, political and economic 
structures of the country.63
The city accelerated its development and the old city district was 
destroyed and transformed into the central city district, paving 
and developing new roads.
The growth of the city was developed around this central district 
and along 3 axes: The Damascus road (East), Tripoli road (north) 
and Saida road (South)
Lebanon became independent in 1942. The first post-
independence years were characterized by a fast-economic 
growth, an extensive spatial development and a rural-urban 
migration.
Trading increased and so did the capital. Well educated class 
arrived from neighbouring countries, due to the fervent 
economy and the efficient banking sector.
Beirut received a wave of migrants from different areas; The 
economic growth caused an exodus of people looking for 
opportunities, which resulted in the arrival of low-income 
refugees from hinterlands (Bekaa Valley, and Northern and 
Southern areas of the country), as well as migrant workers from 
other countries (Syria). 
In 1948 was created the state of Israel, and the Palestinian-
Israeli-conflict erupted. Thousands of Palestinians refugees 
arrived to Beirut and they settled in the outskirts of the city 
creating new neighbourhoods, and increasing the population of 
the city to 300,000 inhabitants by 1950.
By 1960 the population of Beirut reached 400.000 inhabitants. 
The city became denser and grew in extension. Many nearby 
villages were incorporated within the city limits and many low-
income suburbs were established in the north-east and south 
of the city.60
There were attempts of trying to plan and control the 
development of Beirut and its recent new neighbourhoods but 
were unable to avoid the speculation and join forces to achieve 
a controlled development of the city. This caused that the rural 
migrants, Syrian low-income workers and Palestinian refugees 
were confined in what was called the “misery belt”, a series of 
low-income neighbourhoods around the city and surrounding 
it completely. 
These low-income suburbs grew remarkably specially from 
1960 and were characterize by a high density and poor living 
conditions. By the beginning of the Civil War, these types 
of neighbourhoods were hosting a population of 60.000 
inhabitants, most of them being non-Lebanese. (In all Lebanon 
1/4 of the population was non-Lebanese, and 2/3 of them were 
Palestinians at that time).
The large-scale expansion of Beirut continued till 1970, when 
the city reached 1.100.000 inhabitants. These new citizens 
changed the geography of the city, making the suburbs to grow 
faster and denser.62
By 1975, Beirut hosted 1/4 of the total population of the country, 
from which around 45% was composed by non-Lebanese. This 
non-Lebanese group was composed by foreign migrant workers 
and Palestine refugees, that in this period also fled from the 
rural refugee camps towards the city.
This migration of foreign workers is due to the growth of urban 
industries, and the number of Lebanese working in this sector 
reached 150.000 people, 43% of them in Beirut, and especially 
in the Suburbs.
40% of the population of the suburbs was working in this 
industrial sector by 1975. In 1980 60% of the population of 
Beirut was working on the industrial sector.61
