Abstract 
 
Elucidating the mechanisms by which cells acquire a specific fate is crucial to 
understanding how their disruption results in developmental disorders. The 
establishment of an in vitro cell culture system is critical for disease modelling, enabling 
investigations into the mechanisms underlying cell fate decision.  
Important transcription factors implicated in vertebrate organogenesis and 
mesoderm formation are encoded by the Pbx genes. The study of Pbx mutant 
embryos showed that Pbx1-KO/Pbx2-KO (hereinafter referred as Pbx1/2-/-) embryos die 
at embryonic day (E) 8.5 and exhibit distinct mesodermal defects like dilated heart, 
defective somite formation, short trunk, turned tail and limbs aplasia. These 
phenotypes suggested that the defective organogenesis observed in Pbx1/2-/- 
embryos could be the consequence of aberrations occurring at the time of mesoderm 
specification. In order to model these phenotypes and identify the aberrant molecular 
mechanisms, Pbx1/2-/- mouse epiblast stem cells (mEpiSCs) were generated and 
differentiated to mesoderm cells. Transcriptional profiling with mesoderm lineage 
markers revealed that these mutant cells could differentiate towards a middle 
primitive streak and lateral mesodermal fate. However, the downregulation of cardiac 
markers such as Foxf1, Hand1 and Mixl1 suggests a defect in the generation of cardiac 
progenitors. This observation is in agreement with the heart malformations observed in 
the Pbx1/2-/- embryos. Astonishingly, Pbx1/2-/- cells differentiated towards an anterior 
primitive streak and paraxial mesodermal fate faced a massive cell death. These 
results support the apoplastic somites formation observed in the Pbx1/2-/- embryo.  
Although the Pbx-dependent molecular mechanisms regulating the fate of these 
different mesodermal progenitors require further clarification, in my study I have 
successfully established an in vitro system to model mesoderm differentiation and the 
mesodermal phenotypes of the Pbx1/2-/- embryo.

1. Introduction 
 
Human development starts with the fertilization of an egg with a sperm to produce 
the zygote: the single cell that will give rise to a complete organism with over 200 
different cell types. How this unique single cell generates so many different tissues is 
one of the most challenging questions of developmental biology. Throughout 
centuries, the mechanisms behind these complex processes have mystified scientists. 
Although many aspects of these questions have been answered by advances in 
molecular biology technologies and computational analyses, understanding how the 
different organs originate from a single cell remains elusive.  
Mammals display properties of regulative development. Once the egg is fertilized 
with the sperm, the zygote undergoes several rounds of cell division resulting in 
identical daughter cells. These cells, termed blastomeres, are also totipotent as they 
can give rise to any type of cell. Then, the first difference comes breaking the 
totipotency, and two distinct populations of cells emerge. This is the very first decision 
point: the cells either become part of the inner cell mass (ICM) developing further into 
the embryo proper or contribute to the trophoblast supporting the life of the embryo 
by connecting it with the maternal tissue. This initial decision is followed by a series of 
binary choices that result in a fully functional organism with a myriad of cell types ( 
Figure 1) [Reviewed in (Arnold & Robertson, 2009)]. 
Since the cells in the embryo originate from one zygote and therefore contain the 
very same genetic information, how exactly does one cell acquire a completely 
different identity from another? How and when do the cells become different? 
A group of proteins called transcription factors are implicated in this decision-
making process that allows cells to acquire a specific cell fate. They selectively 
activate the expression of their specific target genes, hence leading to decision on 
one fate over another. Activation of genes encoding these different transcription 
factors at a given time are triggered by signaling cues secreted by the neighboring 
cells or by mechanical forces. Thus, besides temporal regulation, spatial differences 
among the cells and cellular communication are also important factors.

The embryo develops, creating successive molecular asymmetries, generating 
tissues and organs, until each organ has reached the correct cellular and tissue 
organization. All these events are referred as embryonic patterning. A critical event in 
embryonic patterning is the establishment of the embryonic axes, followed by 
gastrulation and the primitive streak formation where the three germ layers -
endoderm, mesoderm and ectoderm- emerge. To achieve their specific cell fates, 
cells must be present in the right space at the right time. Transcription factors 
conferring positional identity, like the Hox proteins and their cofactors Pbx, are 
important for attributing the required spatial and temporal coordinates enabling these

cells to acquire a specific identity in the embryo (Bürglin, 1997; Krumlauf, 1994; R S 
Mann & Chan, 1996). 
It is important to understand how these cell fate decisions occur in the embryo 
because this information can be used to implement stem cell-based clinical 
approaches. Knowing how pluripotent cells differentiate into specific cell types is 
critical for developing efficient and targeted in vitro differentiation protocols. 
Thus, efficient differentiation requires a detailed understanding of how cells 
choose one fate over another. To investigate this process, different in vitro protocols 
have been developed in laboratories to mimic the phases observed in vivo resulting 
in the formation of different cell types. To this purpose, stem cell studies use pluripotent 
cells from early-embryonic stages that have the potential to give rise to any cell type 
in vivo, isolate, and differentiate them in vitro with an appropriate cocktail of cytokines 
and inhibitors. There are specific molecular networks that are crucial for maintaining 
the cells in pluripotency or for differentiating them to acquire a specific fate. In this 
project, known key transcription factors marking pluripotency and cell lineages will be 
monitored to follow stage-dependent differences in mouse-derived cell cultures. 
Our research group focuses on dissecting the molecular networks controlling 
mesoderm specification and early pattering during mammalian development. My 
project specifically aims to investigate the function of the Pbx genes which play a 
crucial role in embryonic patterning and organogenesis. Pbx1 and Pbx2 (hereinafter, 
Pbx1/2-/-) loss-of-function studies showed that the primitive streak formation is the first 
process where Pbx1 and Pbx2 proteins (hereinafter, Pbx1/2) have a critical function. 
(Rauskolb, Peifer, & Wieschaus, 1993). In agreement, Pbx1-2 are expressed in the 
primitive streak and in the epiblast cells preceding gastrulation. 
In this thesis, I used an in vitro model, mouse-derived epiblast stem cells (mEpiSCs), 
to recapitulate the phenotype of the Pbx1/2-/- mutant embryos and identify the 
Pbx1/2-dependent molecular networks guiding mesoderm differentiation. I believe 
that the combination of in vitro differentiation systems and genetic studies in mice 
represents a powerful approach to understand the molecular mechanisms controlling 
mesoderm specification and patterning in mammals.

2. Project aim 
 
Loss of mesodermal progenitors (MPs) underlie a spectrum of developmental and 
degenerative diseases, e.g. congenital heart and kidney defects, and muscular dystrophies. 
However, the molecular bases of MPs specification and further differentiation into specific 
mesodermal lineages remain elusive. All MPs originate during gastrulation from cells that 
ingress into the primitive streak (PS), where they are spatio-temporally specified into distinct 
lineages.  
 Hox proteins and their cofactors Pbx are key transcription factors conferring positional 
identity and are implicated in the spatio-temporal specification of MPs. The Pbx proteins are 
specifically expressed in the epiblast cells prior to gastrulation and later in PS and mesoderm. 
The studies of Pbx1/2-/- mutant embryos revealed that mesoderm formation is the first process 
where Pbx proteins have a critical function. Thus, Pbx perturbation studies represent an 
outstanding model to dissect the mechanisms by which epiblast cells become competent 
after entering the PS leading to different mesodermal fates.  
Therefore, my project aims to model mesoderm differentiation in vitro and to obtain a 
comprehensive overview of Pbx-dependent gene regulatory networks in mesodermal 
lineage commitment.  
I will assess my hypotheses via the following steps: 
1) Generation of PbxF/F; Pbx2-/- mEpiSC lines  
2) Molecular characterization of the established cell lines  
3) Generation of Pbx1-/-; Pbx2-/- mEpiSC lines 
4) Analysis of the lineage markers. 
 
Elucidation of the Pbx-dependent regulatory code in mesoderm differentiation will be 
important for understanding its role in the embryo. In addition, a detailed characterization of 
the Pbx-dependent mechanisms driving mesoderm specification will enhance our 
understanding of mesoderm development and organogenesis. Thus, this study has substantial 
medical and financial relevance, as it will provide insights into the pathogenesis of 
developmental and degenerative diseases that could lead to cost-efficient treatments in the 
future.

3. Theory 
 
Development from zygote to a complete offspring is a very complex biological 
process involving numerous molecular mechanisms. Considering the level of 
complexity, investigation of these mechanisms requires arduous work. The requirement 
of genetic manipulation has set high ethical restrictions on the usage of human 
embryos. Therefore, easy-to-handle model organisms at relatively lower complexity 
are required to broaden the understanding of embryonic development and cell fate 
decision. Mouse is the most commonly used animal model mirroring human 
development at a very high similarity. More specifically, in human and mouse, both 
vertebrate mammals, embryonic development and differentiation of distinct 
progenitors are orchestrated by evolutionarily conserved sets of genes and molecular 
networks. Consequently, the mouse is a perfect platform to study the details of this 
complex process.  
The transcriptional networks that govern organogenesis and morphogenesis are 
poorly understood. Elucidation of these pathways remains one of the most fascinating 
questions 
of 
biology, 
since 
developmental 
programs 
that 
control 
organ 
morphogenesis are perturbed in congenital diseases. In my thesis, I established an in 
vitro system to model embryonic development with the aim of identifying key 
molecular networks guiding mesoderm differentiation. Specifically, I used a cell culture 
system to dissect the role of the Pbx1 and Pbx2 proteins in embryonic development, in 
order to identify the Pbx-dependent molecular networks driving the early stages of 
mesoderm formation. Therefore, the theory section of this thesis will firstly focus on early 
mouse development, then, in vitro cellular systems, and lastly, the role of Pbx in 
embryonic development and mesoderm specification. 
In the following paragraphs, I will describe the molecular mechanisms driving the 
initial decisions of the mouse embryos, the signals and the factors that initiate 
asymmetry leading to the formation of the three germ layers and the establishment of 
the body axes.

 Early development of mouse embryo 
 
Due to its intra-uterine development, the mammalian embryo has long been a big 
mystery for embryologists in comparison to the easily accessible chicken or frog 
embryos. Considerably smaller size of the mammalian egg and lower ovulation rate of 
mammals have delayed the collection of data until the end of 19th century, when Van 
Beneden conducted with detailed observations on rabbit embryos (Van Beneden, 
1880). Following his studies, in vitro culturing of mammalian embryos have been 
accelerated, and the 20th century witnessed the ‘domestication’ of mouse embryos 
(Alexandre, 2001). Since then, mice have become a model organism for mammalian 
development. 
Development of the mouse embryo starts with slow progression until its 
implantation into the uterine wall. After fertilization, the zygote undergoes three 
successive cell cleavages resulting in an 8-cell stage of which the cells are called 
blastomeres. Until this point, all blastomeres are totipotent and generally considered 
to have equal morphology, molecular content and developmental potency (Kelly, 
1977). This type of embryonic development is referred as regulative development and 
it is a characteristic of all mammals.  Indeed, other vertebrates like frogs or fishes, 
display mosaic development, wherein the fate of blastomeres is predestined by the 
differential deposition of specific determinants already in the egg (Roux, 1887; 
Spemann, 1987). Thus, mammalian embryos have developed a system to regulate the 
cell fate decisions. This aspect allows the re-adjustment and substitution of the initial 
blastomeres upon damage or destruction.  
The next round of division leads to the 16-cell morula stage at which point the 
blastomeres are no longer identical. Blastomeres located deep within the morula are 
smaller in comparison to cells positioned in the outer layer. Although morphologically 
dissimilar, blastomeres at this stage have the same developmental potency (Figure 2). 
Thus, at the 16-cell morula stage which corresponds to embryonic day E3.0 (3rd 
day post-copulation), the symmetry of embryos disappear and blastomeres get ready 
for their very first binary cell fate decision (Figure-2). (Fleming, 1987; Pedersen, Wu, & 
BaŁakier, 1986).

3.1.1. 
First decision point: Inner cell mass or 
trophectoderm 
 
 After the 16-cell morula stage, the equity among the blastomeres is broken. They 
either become inner cell mass (ICM) giving rise to embryonic tissue, allantois, amnion 
and yolk sac; or trophectoderm (TE) providing extraembryonic tissue and chorion, 
mediating uteral implementation. (Figure 2). The decision between the two distinct cell 
fates, ICM and TE is mainly mediated by two transcription factors: Oct3/4 (known as 
Pou5f1 or Oct4) and Cdx2 (Niwa et al., 2005). These cells are then regrouped in the 
blastocyst structure that is composed of an outer TE layer and ICM positioning on one 
pole of the blastocyst cavity. After this very first decision, ICM cells are no longer 
totipotent but instead pluripotent, being able to differentiate into any type of cell 
except TE cells and its derivatives. This first lineage segregation is anticipated by the 
mutual exclusive expression of two key transcription factors Oct3/4 and Cdx2 (Figure 
2) (Niwa et al, 2005). 
 
 
Figure 2 Lineage segregation in the blastocyst. At embryonic stage E2.5 the cells express both Cdx2 and Oct3/4 
(yellow). Once the equity among blastomeres is broken, Cdx2 and Oct3/4 are expressed in a mutually exclusive 
manner. The cells expressing Oct3/4 (red) differentiate into inner cell mass (ICM) and the ones expressing Cdx2 (blue) 
into trophectoderm (TE). At E3.5, these cells are arranged in a manner that TE forms the outer layer and ICM sits at one 
pole of the blastocyst structure. Second binary decision leads to Epiblast and primitive endoderm formation. Gata6

At E3.0, the morulae are composed of two cell types expressing either Oct3/4 or 
Cdx2. Cdx2 is highly expressed in outer cells due to asymmetric division after morula 
stage and to the position of the cells in blastocyst (Dietrich & Hiiragi, 2007; Toyooka, 
Oka, & Fujimori, 2016). High levels of Cdx2 in the outer layer cells are activated by 
polarity mechanisms and the autoregulatory feedback triggered by YAP/Hippo 
pathway that sustain Cdx2 and inhibit the expression of Oct4 and Nanog (Yagi et al.,

2007). In contrast, the inner cells, where Oct4 is expressed, repress Cdx2 expression and 
keep the cells in pluripotent state (Niwa et al., 2005).  
3.1.2. 
Second decision point: Epiblast or primitive 
endoderm 
 
At the blastocyst stage when the embryonic cavity is established, cells are 
segregated into TE and ICM, and some ICM cells face the blastocyst cavity. The 
embryo is ready for the second cell fate decision that will result in the segregation of 
two cell lineages, the primitive endoderm (PE) and the epiblast (Epi) (Figure 2). 
Distinction between Epi and PE is anticipated by the expression of the transcription 
factors Nanog and Gata6. 
Several studies have shown that already at early blastocyst, Nanog and Gata6 
expressing cells can be observed but they have a salt-and-pepper distribution. It has 
been hypothesized that random segregation and an asymmetric cell division/position 
based manner could be the reason for this mosaic pattern. (Krupa et al., 2013; Morris, 
Graham, Jedrusik, & Zernicka-Goetz, 2013). Furthermore, recent observations have 
shown that the position and the cell-to-cell variability of Fgf receptors (FgfR) are bias 
factors for the segregation of PE or Epi cell lineages (Bessonnard et al., 2014; Ohnishi 
et al., 2014; Schrode, Saiz, Di Talia, & Hadjantonakis, 2014). The detailed network by 
which Fgf/Erk signaling controls Nanog or Gata6 expression has not been completely 
elucidated. However, it has been reported that in the PE, Fgf/Erk reinforces Gata6, 
which in turn inhibits Nanog. On the contrary, in Epi this pathway remains inactive, 
keeping the high level of Nanog, hence maintaining pluripotency (Bessonnard et al., 
2014; Chazaud & Yamanaka, 2016). 
The late blastocyst stage embryo is finally composed of three lineage-restricted 
cell populations: TE eventually forming extraembryonic ectoderm (ExE) and 
ectoplancental cone, PE diversifying into parietal endoderm (ParE) and visceral 
endoderm (VE) further gaining in direct contact with maternal tissue and becoming 
yolk sac, and lastly Epi differentiating into the embryo proper. By day E4.5 of 
development, implantation occurs, and the mouse embryo is ready for the next step: 
the establishment of the axes (Figure 1).

3.1.3. 
 The formation of early axis 
 
Formation of axes in the embryo is a milestone during embryonic patterning. 
Before developing tissues and organs, the embryo must define the axis to proper 
locate the head and the tail (the anterior-posterior axis), the back and the belly (the 
dorsal-ventral axis), and finally the left-right asymmetry. 
Establishing the axis will provide the coordinate to sort and allocate the cells of the 
emerging cell lineages in the proper territory. At the time of implantation, E5.0, mouse 
embryo has two main axes: Proximal-Distal (P-D) and Anterior-Posterior (A-P). At the 
molecular level, formation of the axes is mainly regulated by three signaling pathways: 
Nodal/Activin, Bmp4 and Wnt3 (Brennan et al., 2001; Liu et al., 1999; Waldrip, Bikoff, 
Hoodless, Wrana, & Robertson, 1998). Gradient distribution of these signaling 
molecules and the integration of their signal activity will terminate the molecular 
homogeneity of the epiblast cells and provide them with the correct information 
about their fate. 
 
3.1.3.1. 
Proximal-Distal Axis 
 
P-D axis is the first one to be observed due to the fact and follows the 
extraembryonic-embryonic line. At E5.0-5.5, the mouse embryo is shaped into radially 
symmetric egg-like structure on its proximal-distal axis. TE cells maturate and 
differentiate into ExE and the ectoplacental cone. Meanwhile, the PE will give rise to 
the embryonic and extraembryonic VE. The region where Epi, ExE and embryonic VE 
come together is an important signaling region (Figure 3-A).  
The ExE, at the proximal end of the embryo, is the source of Furin, an enzyme that

3.1.4. 
Gastrulation, the most important event of life 
 
"It is not birth, marriage, or death, but gastrulation which is truly the most important 
time in your life." -Lewis Wolpert. 
 
Axis patterning is a prerequisite to a phenomenon called gastrulation. After the 
axis patterning, mono-layered blastocyst cells undergo gastrulation and become a tri-
layered embryo. The process of gastrulation will result in the formation of three distinct 
cell groups called germ layers originating from Epi: ectoderm, mesoderm and 
definitive endoderm. The ectoderm will form the nervous system and skin, the 
mesoderm will give rise to form the heart, spleen, kidneys, bone, muscle connective 
tissue and the hematopoietic system, whereas the definitive endoderm will form the 
digestive tract together with associated organs like the thyroid, thymus, lungs, 
pancreas and liver. 
In this section, differentiation of Epi into these layers will be briefly explained starting 
with primitive streak formation.  
The first step of gastrulation starts in the posterior proximal part of the embryo, 
where high levels of Bmp4, Nodal and Wnt3 signaling triggers the ingression of epiblast 
cells underneath the VE. As gastrulation progresses, the primitive streak elongates and 
extends to the distal tip of the embryo (Hashimoto & Nakatsuji, 1989). In order to ingress 
into the primitive streak, epiblast cells must abandon the epithelial features of the Epi 
layer and become mobile. Thus, epiblast cells lose their apical-basal polarity by 
downregulating junction proteins and cell-cell adhesion proteins, and acquiring 
mesenchyme characteristics through a process called epithelial-to-mesenchymal 
transition (EMT). Fgf and Tgf-β/Nodal signaling pathways promote EMT, via up-
regulation off Snai1, which downregulates E-cadherin, causing a loss of cell adhesion 
(Arnold, Hofmann, Bikoff, & Robertson, 2008).  
Epiblast cells ingress into the primitive streak and emerge between the Epi and VE 
forming a new intermediate layer called mesoderm. Nascent mesoderm moves in two 
directions forming mesodermal “wings”. The most posterior mesoderm moves towards 
the extraembryonic region, displacing the extraembryonic ectoderm, generating the 
extraembryonic mesoderm. Thus, the extraembryonic mesoderm arises from the most 
posterior end of the primitive streak, a feature observed in mouse embryos but not in 
==PAGE:25==
Pluripotent Stem Cell Studies to Elucidate Mechanisms of Mesoderm Specification 
15 
 
humans. These cells will eventually form the allantois, amnion and yolk sac vasculature. 
Meanwhile, cells that ingress from the middle and anterior part of the primitive streak 
move laterally around the circumference of the embryo and give rise to the cardiac, 
lateral plate and paraxial mesoderm (Garbutt, Chisholm, & Johnson, 1987; Tam & 
Beddington, 1987). 
At day E6.5, the anterior primitive streak (APS) cells intercalate into the outer VE 
layer to provide the first cohort of definitive endoderm (DE). APS do not only give rise 
to DE but also to other lineages and structures such as the prechordal plate, the 
notochord and the neuromesoderm (Kwon, Viotti, & Hadjantonakis, 2008). Epiblast 
cells that do not enter the primitive streak will generate ectoderm. After the 
gastrulation process has finished, the scene is set for further development leading to 
organogenesis: the formation of the organs.

3.2.1. 
Naïve and Primed states of pluripotency 
 
Rodent embryonic stem cells are grouped in two types accordingly to their states: 
‘naïve’ or ‘primed’ (Nichols & Smith, 2009). They are both pluripotent: capable of 
indefinite self-renewal and have high level of expression of the pluripotent transcription 
factors, Oct4, Sox2 and Nanog (Brons et al., 2007; Tesar et al., 2007). Furthermore, they 
have both the potential to differentiate into the three germ layers. The first category 
of rodent pluripotent stem cells includes mouse embryonic stem cells (mESCs) derived 
from pre-implantation stage embryos at E3.5-4.5 and induced pluripotent stem cells 
(iPSCs) derived by re-programming of adult somatic cells (Evans & Kaufman, 1981; 
Takahashi & Yamanaka, 2006). These cells are distinguished by their self-renewal in 
response to leukemia inhibitory factor (LIF) together with inhibitors of Erk and GSK-3β 
signaling (2i) (Kunath et al., 2007; Pease, Braghetta, Gearing, Grail, & Williams, 1990; 
Smith et al., 1988). Their naïve state pluripotency has also been demonstrated by their 
ability to efficiently colonize the blastocyst and consequently to form chimeras 
(Bradley, Evans, Kaufman, & Robertson, 1984; Okita, Ichisaka, & Yamanaka, 2007).  
In contrast to naïve mESCs, mouse Epiblast-derived stem cells (mEpiSCs) are at a 
state called primed. These cells are derived from post-implantation stage embryos, at 
E5.5-7.5. They display gene expression characteristics of pre-gastrulation embryo. 
Therefore, they already contain positional information based on gradient signaling 
molecules such as Wnt3, Bmp, Nodal/Activin etc. However, mEpiSCs are still 
pluripotent and retain the ability to generate in vitro the three germ layers. Their ability 
to form multiple cell types has been proven by teratoma assays with cell graft in 
immunocompromised mice. Although EpiSCs are pluripotent in vitro, they fail to form 
chimeras, unlike mESC (Brons et al., 2007). Their self-renewal is established in culture 
conditions containing FGF2 and Activin (Pauklin & Vallier, 2015; Tesar et al., 2007). 
Additionally, inhibition of Wnt signaling has shown to be an important factor for 
maintaining a homogeneous pluripotent state (Sumi, Oki, Kitajima, & Meno, 2013). 
Besides these differences in the culture conditions, mEpiSCs are epigenetically 
different compared to EpiSCs. They differ in their histone methylation patterns, with 
EpiSCs displaying increased DNA methylation and X-chromosome inactivation in XX 
cell lines (Surani, Hayashi, & Hajkova, 2007).  
Basically, mESCs and mEpiSCs are cell lines derived from two distinct 
developmental stages and kept by the media in two different states of pluripotency.

3.2.2. 
Comparison of human and murine embryonic 
stem cells 
 
These distinct states of pluripotency in terms of murine stem cell cultures have not 
been observed in non-rodent derived embryonic stem cells. In fact, mESCs possess 
different characteristics in comparison to human embryonic stem cells (hESCs) even 
though they are both derived from pre-implantation stage embryos. Comparative 
analysis of their transcriptomes highlighted that hESCs are not in naïve state but in 
primed state. Indeed, the expression of the pluripotency markers and epigenetic 
markers make them more similar to mEpiSCs (Tesar et al., 2007). Furthermore, their 
morphology and the fact that they grow in flat monolayer colonies rather than in 
dome-shaped manner reflects their mEpiSC-like epithelial characters. The culture 
conditions required to keep hESCs in undifferentiated state are also similar to the 
mEpiSC culture. As mentioned in the previous section, mESCs require LIF in culture 
media in combination with Erk and GSK-3β inhibitors (2i) to stay pluripotent. However, 
LIF is not required for self-renewal in hESCs but instead dependence on Activin/Nodal 
signaling and Fgf signaling has been shown to maintain an undifferentiated state 
(Brons et al., 2007; Dahéron et al., 2004; Greber et al., 2010; Pauklin & Vallier, 2015). 
Since Activin/Nodal (Tgf-β) is known to maintain Epi pluripotency in vivo, this finding 
directly demonstrated that hESCs are more similarly to mEpiSCs than mESCs, and 
therefore relates to primed state pluripotency. Besides, the genetic imprinting and 
epigenetic regulation levels play an essential role in distinguishing primed and naïve 
pluripotency. hESCs are derived from blastocyst stage human embryos where a high 
level of epigenetic activity is observed including DNA methylation, dynamic chromatin 
modeling and X-chromosome inactivation (Rugg-Gunn, Ferguson-Smith, & Pedersen, 
2005).  
Thus, mEpiSCs and hESCs share common features in terms of cellular responses to 
signaling cues, transcriptional regulations and their pluripotency state. Similar to hESCs, 
mEpiSCs can be differentiated in vitro into different mesodermal sub-types, and hence 
they are an excellent system for dissecting complex the transcriptional regulatory 
networks guiding mesoderm specification. Considering ethical issues regarding the 
usage of human-derived cells, mEpiSCs are a powerful tool to model also genetic 
aberrations, which can then be traced back to in vivo development in mice.

3.2.3. 
EpiSC derivation and differentiation in vitro  
 
Culturing of EpiSCs was developed mainly by applying both human and mouse 
ESC culturing techniques and monitoring the pluripotency maintenance under 
different media. The first versions of pluripotent EpiSC derivation was from the late 
epiblast layer of post-implantation mouse. Results showed that chemically defined, 
Activin containing culture medium was sufficient for long-term maintenance of hESCs 
encouraged the usage of a similar condition for derivation of pluripotent cells from 
pre- and post-implantation rodent embryos (Brons et al., 2007). Isolated cells have 
shown different response to the media: while early stage cells underwent rapid 
differentiation in this media, cells from later stage developed into colonies with high 
level of pluripotency markers. Latter were given the name EpiSCs as they were 
originated purely from epiblast cells dissected from E5.5-E6.5 embryos. These cells were 
then tested for differentiation potential in vitro by embryonic body formation. 
Generation of the three germ layers was confirmed by the expression of cell lineage 
markers (Brons et al., 2007; Tesar et al., 2007).  
The EpiSC lines, which Tesar and Brons used, are derived from the embryos by 
removing the extraembryonic ectoderm. The remaining part of the embryo contains 
both Epi and VE. Removal of the visceral endoderm requires enzymatic treatment and 
manual peeling of the layer resulting in loss of many Epi cells and contamination with 
extraembryonic tissue. With this method, the overall efficiency of EpiSC derivation was 
as low as 10-40% (Brons et al., 2007; Kojima et al., 2014; Sugimoto et al., 2015). 
Moreover, the observation of different levels of early mesoderm/ endoderm markers 
in different EpiSC lines or even the heterogeneous expression among the cells of the 
same culture emphasized the importance to establish a more robust system for mEpiSC 
derivation (Bernemann et al., 2011; Kojima et al., 2014; Sumi et al., 2013). The cells must 
be harvested in high efficiency from the embryos without a need for VE removal and 
they must be prevented from a spontaneous differentiation.   
Given that the inhibition of canonical Wnt signalling leads to the self-renewal of 
both EpiSC and hESCs, Wnt inhibition has been used as a method to derived and 
culture EpiSCs (Kim et al., 2013; Sugimoto et al., 2015). As a result, the mEpiSC derivation 
with the media containing Activin, Fgf and Wnt inhibitor has shown an efficiency as 
high as 90% and the heterogeneity of the germ layer lineage markers were diminished.

 In vitro differentiation protocols for EpiSCs were developed by adapting 
conditions established for hESCs based on in vivo mouse studies (Loh et al., 2016). As

procedure required for in vitro mesoderm differentiation. 
Primitive streak formation is the very first step to mesoderm development in vivo. 
The cells around the primitive streak are then differentiate into mesoderm and take 
different identities according to the signalling cues as explained in the previous 
section. In vitro differentiation of hESCs towards a mesodermal fate follows this pattern 
and the culturing media mimics the in vivo dynamic signalling gradients. Prior studies 
have shown that Activin/Nodal/Tgfβ, Bmp, Fgf, and Wnt signalling pathways broadly 
induce mesoderm differentiation from pluripotent cells. Alongside Fgf and Wnt 
activation, high concentrations of Activin promote anterior primitive streak cells (APS), 
whereas the addition of Bmp alongside the aforementioned factors promotes middle 
primitive streak cells (MPS) formation. Furthermore, Bmp4 inhibits paraxial mesoderm 
formation but induces lateral plate mesoderm and cardiac mesoderm (Cheung, 
Bernardo, Trotter, Pedersen, & Sinha, 2012; Umeda et al., 2012). Consequently, Bmp4 is 
the main difference in eliciting middle primitive streak and lateral mesoderm. Besides, 
inhibition of Wnt also enhanced the formation of lateral mesoderm (Loh et al., 2016). 
Following the sequential differentiation from Epi to primitive streak and then to 
mesoderm in vivo, differentiation in vitro is based on daily exposure to defined media 
containing the aforementioned morphogens. The fact that differentiated cells showed 
the enrichment expression of stage-specific lineage markers proves the efficiency of 
the technique (Loh et al., 2016).

 Role of Pbx gene family in mouse embryonic 
development 
 
Pbx proteins belong to the Three Amino acids Loop Extension (TALE) 
homeodomain protein family, and they are characterized by the presence of a 
conserved PBC motif at the N-terminus, adjacent to the TALE homeodomain (Bürglin, 
1997). The TALE homeodomain proteins are divided into three groups (reviewed in 
(Choe, Ladam, & Sagerström, 2014; Longobardi et al., 2014)): 
1) the PBC gene family, which includes Pbx1-4 in vertebrates, extradenticle (exd) 
in Drosophila, and ceh-20 in C. elegans;  
2) the MEINOX gene family (encoding hth/ Meis/Prep/Tgif proteins); 
3) the distantly related Iroquois (Iro) gene family  
The first member of the family to be identified was exd, the Drosophila ortholog of 
Pbx proteins. Exd is a maternal gene that is required for the proper development of 
the Drosophila embryo. Zygotic inactivation of exd resulted in several patterning 
defects such as disrupted head structure and altered thoracic segments, with the 
second thoracic segment adopting the characteristics of the first. Massive changes in 
segment identity have been observed in the null exd mutants and are termed 
homeotic transformations. These phenotypes recapitulated the transformation seen in 
the Hox mutants; arista and antenna are transformed to legs while the head capsule 
is altered to resemble the dorsal thorax or notum. Interestingly, in the exd mutants the 
expression domains of the Hox genes were not altered. This observation supported the 
initial idea that exd acts as a Hox cofactor and provides DNA-binding specificity to the 
Hox proteins (Rauskolb et al., 1993). Although each Hox protein exhibits a high target 
specificity in vivo, as demonstrated by both loss and gain of function experiments, in 
vitro they all bind similar target sequences with low affinities. This paradoxical result 
raises the question of how multiple proteins with similar DNA-binding specificity can 
activate or repress numerous and very different target genes in vivo. Several studies 
show that homeodomain proteins rely on protein-protein interactions to confer 
enhanced DNA binding affinity and specificity or enhanced transcriptional activity 
(Jürgens et al., 2009)(Richard S Mann, Lelli, & Joshi, 2009). The evidence supporting 
exd-Hox interaction has been demonstrated not just at the genetic level, but also via 
protein-protein interactions where it has been validated by in vitro binding studies (R S

3.3.1. 
Pbx protein family roles in development and 
diseases  
 
Pbx genes appear broadly conserved in vertebrates and also have been found in 
invertebrates as Drosophila (exd), in planarians (pbx), in C. elegans (ceh-20) (Bürglin, 
1997). The Pbx proteins in mice are expressed in different tissues both in fetal and adult 
body. Elevated Pbx1 expression has been observed in multiple tissues and organs 
during embryogenesis (Ferretti, Schulz, Talarico, Blasi, & Berthelsen, 1999; Selleri et al., 
2001).  Pbx2 expression is widespread throughout murine embryonic development and 
in the adult (Monica, Galili, Nourse, Saltman, & Cleary, 1991; Selleri et al., 2004). Pbx3 is 
present in the developing central and peripheral nervous system, particularly in the 
medulla oblongata (Toresson, Parmar, & Campbell, 2000). In the adult, Pbx1 is 
ubiquitously expressed, with the exception of lymphoid tissues (Monica, LeBrun, 
Dedera, Brown, & Cleary, 1994).  Similarly, Pbx2 and Pbx3 are also ubiquitously 
expressed in adult murine tissues. However, Pbx2 cannot be found in the heart, while 
Pbx3 is predominantly present in adult ovary (Monica et al., 1994). Pbx4 is specifically 
expressed in mouse testis and is not present in mouse embryos between E.7 and 10.5 
(Wagner, Mincheva, Korn, Lichter, & Pöpperl, 2001).  
 Therefore, the three Pbx genes (1-3) play fundamental roles in vertebrate 
organogenesis. As demonstrated by Pbx loss-of-function (LOF) studies in mice, Pbx 
genes have pleiotropic roles in the formation of multiple essential organs. Also, there is 
a strong association of PBX gene dysregulation with human pathologies such as cleft 
lip and palate (Welsh et al., 2018),  congenital asplenia (Koss et al., 2012), diabetes  
(Duesing et al., 2008), and cancer (Thiaville et al., 2012). Here, I will shortly describe the 
embryonic phenotypes and main properties of each member.

Pbx1 
 The PBX1 gene was originally identified upon cloning of the product chromosomal 
translocation (1:19), fusing the transactivation domain of the TF E2A to the PBX1 
homeodomain. This translocation is present in 25% of cases of pre-B-cell acute 
lymphoblastoid leukemia (pre-B-ALL), consequently the name pre-B cell homeobox 
(PBX) (Kamps, Look, & Baltimore, 1991; Kamps, Murre, Sun, & Baltimore, 1990; Monica 
et al., 1991) While E2A-PBX1 fusion protein has a strong transcriptional activation 
property, PBX1 alone does not carry a proper transactivation domain. Genetic 
inactivation of Pbx1 in mice has demonstrated it to be an essential gene, and its loss 
results in late gestational lethality. Pbx1 mutant embryos die in uterus at E15.5, 
accompanied by severe lungs, liver, stomach, gut, kidney and pancreas deficiencies, 
the presence of ectopic thymus and kidneys, and the aplasia of the spleen (Koss et 
al., 2012; Manley, Selleri, Brendolan, Gordon, & Cleary, 2004). Furthermore, Pbx1 has 
an important role in patterning the axial and proximal appendicular skeleton. Like its 
homolog exd in Drosophila, Pbx1 mutants exhibit homeotic transformation. Indeed, 
Pbx1-/- mice show a partial transformation of second branchial arch (BA2) cartilages 
to structures resembling to the first branchial arch (BA1) (Selleri et al., 2001). In other 
word, loss of Pbx1 is accompanied by a homeotic-like transformation.  
Pbx2 
Pbx2 knockout mice do not present any visible phenotype. The relation between 
Pbx1 and Pbx2 revealed that these two proteins work in a similar way in skeletal 
development whereas loss of Pbx2 does not display any discrepancy either in 
development or in survival (Selleri et al., 2004). Moreover, double deletion mutants 
show more severe dystrophy in comparison to  Pbx1 single mutants (Figure 7). These 
findings suggest that Pbx1 can possibly compensate for Pbx2 during embryonic 
patterning. 
Pbx3  
Pbx3-/- mice die a few hours after birth due to central respiratory failure. This death 
is dependent on the unusual activity of inspiratory neurons in the ventrolateral medulla 
(Rhee et al., 2004).

3.3.2. 
Role of Pbx proteins in ESC pluripotency and 
stem cell differentiation 
 
Besides being widely expressed during embryonic development, the Pbx genes 
are also expressed in both human and mouse ESCs but their function is such cellular 
context has been only partially addressed. The maintenance of pluripotency and self-
renewal is guaranteed by the action of the core transcription factors Nanog, Oct4, 
and Sox2. These factors promote the expression of pluripotency genes and repress 
genes that induce differentiation. Given its importance, the Oct4-Sox2-Nanog network 
needs to be fine-tuned. An additional step of regulation is offered by the synergistic 
activity of PBX1 and KLF4, which bind to the NANOG promoter and regulate NANOG 
expression in cooperation with OCT4 and SOX2 in hESCs (Chan et al., 2009). Thus, 
together with KLF4, PBX plays a key role in controlling the regulatory network required 
for maintaining the self-renewal state in hESCs.  
Experiments performed in Pbx1-mutant ESCs which were treated with retinoic acid 
(RA) suggested that Pbx1 is dispensable for neuronal differentiation of mESCs (Jürgens 
et al., 2009). Indeed, RA-treatment of ESCs carrying a deletion in the exons 6 and 7 of 
Pbx1 result in increased expression of neural specific genes.  These experiments implied 
that Pbx1 is important for maintaining pluripotency and restraining these ESCs from 
differentiation. Yet, no consequences have been reported related to Pbx1 loss-of-
function in mESCs.  
While much study has been devoted to the roles of Pbx in organogenesis and later 
development in connection to their function as a Hox selector gene, very little is known 
about roles of Pbx in early development when Hox genes are not yet expressed. 
Further experiments are indispensable to establish whether Pbx proteins have a role in 
lineage commitment in Hox-independent manner.

3.3.3. 
Pbx genes and mesoderm development 
 
 
 
Loss of Pbx1 function has been shown to result in abnormalities in mesoderm-
derived structures like the heart development and outflow tract formation, spleen, 
limb, kidney, vasculogenesis, hematopoietic progenitor maturation, and skeletal 
patterning as described above. These widespread phenotypes could be the result of 
the absence of specific mesoderm progenitors. Thus, the organogenesis defects 
observed in the Pbx mutants could be the consequence of aberrations occurring at 
the time of mesoderm specification. In my thesis I tried to address this hypothesis.

4. Experimental Strategy 
 
 
Figure 8 Flow Chart for Experimental Set-up. The project was run in six main steps. Once all cell lines were 
characterized, the selected line was transfected with recombinase. Resulting double-deletion mutant cell lines were 
first validated for the absence of Pbx1 and the differentiation protocol was performed on these lines. The observations 
from this step were compared to known in vivo and in vitro results from the literature to provide novel insights on the 
roles of PBX during mesoderm differentiation. 
 
This project aims to characterize Pbx1/2-/- mutant mEpiSCs regarding their in vitro 
mesoderm differentiation potential and to assess if their prospective progenitors 
recapitulate in vivo observations. 
Homozygous Pbx1 knock-out embryos fail to develop into healthy offspring making 
any embryonic stem cell derivation impossible. In order to by-pass the lethal effect of 
Pbx1 deletion and to be able to obtain EpiSC lines, conditional deletion mechanisms 
were addressed for Pbx1. Besides, as Pbx2 is known to compromise effects of Pbx1 
deletion, Pbx2 was also knocked-out.  This mutant genotype for mEpiSCs was 
accomplished by the derivation of EpiSCs from mutant embryos carrying Pbx2 deletion 
==PAGE:40==
Pluripotent Stem Cell Studies to Elucidate Mechanisms of Mesoderm Specification 
30 
 
and conditional Pbx1 gene of which the exon-3 placed between loxP Cre-
recombinase excision sites (referred as Pbx1Flox/Flox; Pbx2-/- EpiSCs).  
Pbx1Flox/Flox; Pbx2-/- EpiSCs are expected to have full differential potential and wild 
type-like state as Pbx2 deletion has no specific phenotype and Pbx1 is still active. 
However, EpiSC lines are a group of cells rather than a clonal population as mentioned 
before, they are not homogenous and for each EpiSC line, pluripotency level and 
response to differentiation protocol may vary among different cell lines. Therefore, 
Pbx1Flox/Flox; Pbx2-/- EpiSC cell lines derived from a TCF-mixed background were firstly 
scanned for pluripotency and cell lineage markers at transcriptional level with RT-qPCR 
in comparison to the 129 wild type EpiSC line, which is considered as the reference line 
for this project. After they were characterized, most similar lines were selected to study 
further the effects of Pbx1/2 deletion.  
Selected Pbx1Flox/Flox; Pbx2-/- EpiSCs cell lines were transfected with Cre 
recombinase carrying plasmid to knock-out Pbx1. Single clones of transfected EpiSCs 
were amplified in vitro and each clone line was validated for the deletion of Pbx1 at 
the DNA, mRNA and protein level. Later, obtained Pbx1-/-;2-/- double-KO EpiSC lines 
were differentiated into two branches in parallel: one branch to APS and Paraxial 
mesoderm, the other one to MPS and Lateral mesoderm. Finally, the change in the 
expression of transcription factors in mEpiSC lines before and after Pbx1 deletion were 
analyzed by RT-qPCR to conclude the effect of double deletion at molecular level 
and to relate to in vivo observed phenotype.

5. Materials and Methods 
 
 Generation of Pbx1Flox/Flox; Pbx2-/- mEpiSC lines 
 
mEpiSC lines were derived by Javier Martin Gonzalez from the Core Facility for 
Transgenic Mice (Faculty of Health and Medical Sciences, University of Copenhagen) 
from mixed background Pbx1Flox/Flox ; Pbx2-/-  mouse embryos as described in (Koss et 
al., 2012; Selleri et al., 2004). Additionally, Yfp (yellow fluorescent protein) cDNA was 
inserted at the Rosa26 locus preceded by a loxP-flanked stop sequence to generate 
Cre recombination reporter lines. After isolation, cell lines were cultured as described 
below and genotyped for Pbx1 and Pbx2 mutations. See supplementary material for 
genotyping primers and insert map. 
 
 mEpiSC culturing and maintenance 
 
mEpiSCs were cultured in 6-well plates pre-coated with human Fibronectin 
(FC01015, Merck Millipore, 15 µL/mL diluted in PBS). N2B27-based EpiMedium 
supplemented with Activin A, bFGF and XAV-939 was changed daily (see 
supplementary material for medium composition).  
 
5.2.1. 
Pre-coating of plates 
 
Non-treated tissue culture plates were pre-coated with human Fibronectin and 
incubated at 37˚C for at least 1-hour prior seeding of cells. Alternatively, plates were 
pre-coated overnight with the same coating solution at 4˚C. It is not recommended to 
use plates coated longer than overnight. Coating solution was removed right before 
seeding the cells. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:43==
Pluripotent Stem Cell Studies to Elucidate Mechanisms of Mesoderm Specification

5.2.2. 
Thawing of cell lines 
 
Frozen cell lines in CryoMedium (knock-out serum with 10% DMSO) were thawed 
in 37˚C water bath and immediately resuspended in pre-heated ten-fold excess of 
N2B27. Cells were centrifuged for 2 minutes at 0.4 RCF. After removal of the 
supernatant, cells were resuspended in pre-heated, freshly prepared EpiMedium and 
seeded in pre-coated 6-well plates. 
 
5.2.3. 
Passaging and maintenance of cells 
 
EpiMedium was changed daily for regular mEpiSC maintenance. Upon reaching 
80% confluence, cells were washed once with PBS, and digested with pre-heated 
Accutase (Thermo Fisher Scientific) for 3 minutes at 37˚C. The digestion reaction was 
stopped by the addition of ten-fold excess of pre-heated N2B27. Detached cells were 
collected in conical/eppendorf tubes, made into single-cell suspension by thoroughly 
pipetting, and then centrifuged for 2 minutes at 0.4 RCF. After removal of the 
supernatant, cells were resuspended in pre-heated, freshly prepared EpiMedium and 
seeded in a new pre-coated well of a 6-well plate (see Supplementary Material for 
details). 
 
5.2.4. 
Cryopreservation of cell lines 
 
Cells to be frozen were digested with Accutase, collected with ten-fold excess of 
pre-heated N2B27 and then centrifuged for 2 minutes at 0.4 RCF. After removal of the 
supernatant, cells were resuspended in 800 µL of CryoMedium (up to 4 million cells) 
and transferred in cryovials. Vials were stored at -80˚C for primary freezing and then 
transferred into liquid nitrogen (approximately -180˚C) for long-term preservation.

 Establishment of Pbx1-/-; Pbx2-/- cell lines by Cre-
treatment 
 
Pbx1 conditional mEpiSC lines were thawed and passaged two times before 
transfection. Transfection of these lines with Cre recombinase-expressing vector 
resulted in two genotypic changes: first, the exon-3 of Pbx1 was excised leading to 
gene knock-out; second, the STOP sequence in the Rosa26-YFP construct was 
removed leading to YFP expression that was used as a positive transfection marker. 
 
5.3.1. 
Transfection of selected lines 
 
For each conditional mutant line, three wells were seeded with 0.5 million cells/well 
on a 6-well plate 1 hour before transfection. Cells were seeded in 1.5 mL of pre-heated, 
freshly prepared EpiMedium per well and transfected as indicated: 
Well-1: pCAG-Cre (Cre recombinase-expressing plasmid under CAG promoter, a 
kind gift of Josh Brickman’s research group - DanStem) 
Well-2: no plasmid (negative control) 
Well-3: GFP-expressing plasmid (transfection positive control) 
For each transfection condition, 2 tubes were prepared:  
Tube-1: 250 µL of OptiMem (Thermo Fischer Scientific) + 3 µL of Lipofectamine 2000 
(Invitrogen) 
Tube-2: 250 µL of OptiMem + 200 ng of plasmid  
Lipofectamine were activated at room temperature for 5 minutes, then the 
content of tubes 1 and 2 was mixed and incubated for 30 minutes. The mixture was 
spread in each well drop-wise and the plate was gently rotated to equally distribute 
lipofectamines. 24 hours later, the transfection medium was replaced with 2 mL of pre-
heated, freshly prepared EpiMedium. The transfection efficiency was assessed by 
detection of the YFP signal in well 1 and the GFP signal in well 3.

5.3.2. 
Colony selection and expansion 
 
Cells were digested with Accutase and their number was determined by manual 
counting using the hematocytometer. For each transfected line, a cell suspension of 
10,000 cells/mL was prepared by serial dilution. 10 cm dishes pre-coated with human 
Fibronectin were used for colony selection with 10 mL of pre-heated, freshly prepared 
EpiMedium. For each transfected line, 6 dishes were seeded in duplicate with 200 
cells/dish, 500 cells/dish and 1,000 cells/dish. One dish was seeded with 500 
untransfected cells (negative control). Every second day the medium was gently 
changed with 10 mL of pre-heated, freshly prepared EpiMedium and the dishes were 
monitored on daily basis until cells developed single colonies. Once the colonies 
became visible to unaided eye, YFP-positive colonies were selected.  
In preparation for picking colonies, a V-bottom 96-well plate was filled with 20 µL 
PBS/well as a colony collection plate. EpiMedium was removed from the 10 cm dishes 
and they were washed with PBS to remove floating or dead cells. 4 mL of PBS were 
gently added to prevent drying while picking colonies. Selected YFP-positive colonies 
were picked with a 20 µL pipette. Each colony was gently washed with 20 µL PBS, 
scraped from the dish and immediately transferred into each well of the colony 
collection plate. Selected colonies were then digested with 25 µL of Accutase for 3 
minutes and the reaction was stopped with 225 µL of pre-heated, freshly prepared 
EpiMedium. Finally, 250 µL of the mixture were transferred into each well of a flat-
bottom 96-well plate pre-coated with human Fibronectin. Cells were monitored for cell 
growth and 225 µL of the medium was changed daily with pre-heated, freshly 
prepared EpiMedium. When confluent, cells were transferred and expanded into 
larger plates until reaching the volume of a 6-well plate. Upon expansion, cells were 
frozen and harvested for genotyping.

Differentiation to APS and Paraxial Mesoderm

After 24 hours, APSMedium was removed, and the wells were gently washed with 1 mL 
of N2B27. 2 mL of ParaxMedium were gently added to the wells and cells were 
incubated at 37˚C (day 2). On day 3, cells were digested with Accutase, washed with

Extraction of RNA 
 
For RNA extraction, QIAGEN RNA Extraction Mini kit (CAT.74104) was used. Cells

7. Discussion 
 
Pbx1 and Pbx2 proteins have widespread roles in the formation of lateral 
mesoderm-derived structures, such as blood, appendicular skeleton, heart, spleen, 
and body wall [reviewed in (Laurent, Bihan, Omilli, Deschamps, & Pellerin, 2008)]. 
Pbx1/2-/- mutants die at embryonic day E8.5 displaying dilated heart, defective somite 
formation, short trunk, turned tail and limbs aplasia (Figure 7A-D). Pbx1 is initially 
expressed in epiblast cells preceding gastrulation and becomes upregulated in the 
posterior PS and emerging mesodermal cells at E6.5 and E7.5 (Figure 7Q-V). 
Consistently, in Pbx1/2-/- mutants the PS is shorter and abnormally shaped, suggesting 
that some of the MPs could be impaired in the mutants. All above observations raise 
the possibility that Pbx1-2 could guide critical molecular mechanisms of mesoderm 
differentiation. In order to get a deeper understanding of such mechanisms, I 
developed an in vitro system to model the mesoderm development of the Pbx1/2 
mutant embryos. 
 Hereby I will discuss the findings of this project. 
The first observation from my results is the confirmation of the difference in terms of 
pluripotency markers expression between the naïve and primed pluripotency states. 
To this purpose, I compared mESCs and mEpiSC lines. High levels of Klf4, Sox2 and 
Nanog in mESCs highlight the earlier stage of pluripotency in accordance with the 
literature (Nichols & Smith, 2009). Furthermore, two mESC lines grown in two different 
conditions, representing naïve and early-primed pluripotency, respectively, showed 
that even though Oct4 levels are similar between the two conditions, Klf4, Nanog and 
Sox2 levels are higher in mESCs cultured in 2i-based medium. This confirmed published 
data that illustrated that 2i/LIF medium maintain the naïve state pluripotency better 
than serum/LIF medium (Ying et al., 2008). 
To assess my main aim and establish an in vitro system to model the Pbx1/2-/- 
mutant embryos, eight different Pbx1F/F: Pbx2-/- mEpiSC lines were derived from mice 
at two different embryonic stages (E5.5 or E6.5). Transcriptional analyses of these eight 
lines suggested that each individual mEpiSC line showed a specific mRNA expression 
profile. For instance, the lines #8 and #13 were considerably different than the other 
six lines especially with respect to Cdh2, Eomes and Sox2 expression. Specifically, the 
enrichment of Cdh2 and Sox2 suggests a neuronal bias in these two lines. This might 
==PAGE:67==
Pluripotent Stem Cell Studies to Elucidate Mechanisms of Mesoderm Specification 
57 
 
be due to either a specific characteristic of these cell lines that primed them towards 
a neuronal fate, or a spontaneous differentiation related to culture conditions. The 
other six lines share a similar trend in the expression of the lineage specific markers. 
The additional aim of my project was to compare the differentiation efficiency of 
the lines derived from embryos at different developmental stages. Although Line #1, 
which was derived from E5.5 embryo and lines #13 and 21A which were derived from 
E6.5 embryos showed variable mRNA levels in the EpiSCs stage, they all displayed 
potential to differentiate into the primitive streak and subsequent mesodermal 
progenitor state. However, they showed differential expression of some lineage 
markers, such as T, which is more abundant in the E6.5 lines. Given primitive steak 
formation starts at E6.5, this observation supports the hypothesis that while the lines 
derived from different embryo stages may have the same differentiation potential, the 
later E6.5 lines, appear to have better differentiation efficiency. 
After the characterization of Pbx1f/f; Pbx2-/-/ EpiSC lines, the #21A line was the 
selected to generate a Pbx1/2-/- line. Line #21A was transfected with Cre recombinase 
carrying plasmid. Recombination led to two changes in these cells, firstly it cut out the 
Pbx1 exon-3 placed between loxP sties and secondly, it cut out the transcription stop 
region from YFP insert. Therefore, YFP expression was used as an indicator to select 
efficiently transfected colonies, which have high probability to be Pbx1 mutants. 18 
colonies were picked, and the gene deletion was validated in three different manners: 
PCR for DNA level, qRT-PCR for mRNA level and Western blotting for protein level 
detection. At the end, two lines carrying Pbx1/2 deletions were established, #21A.5 
and #21A.9 were expanded and their mesodermal differentiation potential was 
assessed by in vitro assay. 
After the initial characterization of all of the Pbx1F/F; Pbx2-/- mEpiSC lines, the line 
#21A was selected to generate a Pbx1/2-/- line. Line #21A was transfected with a Cre 
recombinase-expression plasmid. Recombination led to two changes in these cells: 
firstly, it excised the exon3 of Pbx1 and secondly, it removed the STOP sequence from 
the YFP-coding region inserted in the Rosa26 locus. Therefore, YFP expression was used 
as an indicator to select efficiently transfected colonies, which have a high probability 
to be Pbx1 mutants. Eighteen colonies were selected, and the gene deletion was 
validated in three different manners: at the DNA level by PCR, at the mRNA level by 
RT-qPCR and at the protein level by Western Blotting. Ultimately, two lines carrying 
==PAGE:68==
Pluripotent Stem Cell Studies to Elucidate Mechanisms of Mesoderm Specification 
58 
 
Pbx1/2 deletions were established, (#21A.5 and #21A.9) and their mesodermal 
differentiation potential was assessed by in vitro assay. 
To this end, the two double mutant lines were differentiated in vitro into MPS-lateral 
mesoderm and APS-paraxial mesoderm. The differentiation into MPS and lateral 
mesoderm and the following profiling by RT-qPCR revealed that the lateral 
mesodermal markers Foxf1, Hand1 and Mixl1 were strongly downregulated in the 
Pbx1/2-/-  mutant lines compared to the parental control line. On the contrary, another 
mesoderm marker, Isl1, was slightly upregulated. Hand1 and Isl1 play important roles 
in heart development. They show mutually exclusive expression patterns, marking MPs 
of the first (FHF) and second heart field (SHF), respectively (Cai et al., 2003; McFadden 
et al., 2004). While FHF cells contribute to the left ventricle and parts of the atria, SHF 
cells generate the right ventricle and outflow tract, raising the possibility that Pbx1 
could specify the fate of FHF versus SHF. Interestingly, Pbx1/2-/- mutants exhibit 
defective heart development (Chang et al., 2008). Therefore, this in vitro model could 
be useful for addressing the phenotype of the Pbx1/2-/- mutant embryos and 
identifying the molecular mechanisms by which Pbx proteins control heart patterning. 
In addition to being important for heart development, Hand1 plays a critical role in the 
differentiation of the lateral plate-derived organs like spleen and limbs. Fate mapping 
experiments showed that the Hand1-expressing cell lineage contributes to structures 
that are abnormally patterned in the Pbx1/2-/- mutant embryos, like spleen and limbs, 
suggesting that some of the observed Pbx1/2-/- phenotypes could be the result of a 
failure in generating the Hand1+ MPs. As supporting evidence, loss of Hand1 has also 
been observed in Pbx1/2-/- mutant embryos (Ferretti laboratory, unpublished data).   
 Furthermore, Pbx1/2-/- mutant embryos showed an aberrant primitive streak 
formation, that could correlate with the downregulation of Mixl1 in MPS and lateral 
mesoderm. Indeed, Mixl1 inactivation resulted in defective primitive streak formation 
in vivo and consequently prevents healthy lateral mesoderm development (Pulina, 
Sahr, Nowotschin, Baron, & Hadjantonakis, 2014). Foxf1, Hand1 and Isl1 genes are all 
important not only for the cardiac morphogenesis, but also for the muscle 
development. On top of this, Isl1 is an important lineage marker for spleen formation. 
Therefore, some of the phenotypes observed in Pbx1/2-/- embryos could be linked to 
the aberrant expression of the lineage markers identified in our in vitro cell 
differentiation system. 
==PAGE:69==

59 
 
The differentiation of double mutant lines towards APS and paraxial mesoderm in 
vitro was not successful. In paraxial mesoderm medium, the biggest portion of the 
Pbx1/2-/- cells were detached from the plate. This is usually a sign of cell death. This 
phenotype was consistent and reproducible. Indeed, it was repeated twice. Even 
after increasing the number of seeded cells, there were few cells remaining after the 
paraxial differentiation (see Table 2).  
 These observations lead to two possible scenarios: either the Pbx1/2-/- cells die as 
they cannot undergo APS-paraxial mesoderm differentiation, or they survive but they 
lose adherence and detach from the plate. This finding correlates with the somite 
anomalies observed in Pbx1/2-/- mutant embryos and the skeletal malformations of the 
compound mutants Pbx1-/-:Pbx2+/- (Capellini et al., 2008). 
Overall, this study put forth several interesting findings: firstly, different mEpiSC lines 
diverge in terms of cell lineage markers content but these differences do not affect 
their potency to differentiate in vitro. Secondly, in vitro mEpiSC differentiation systems 
recapitulate the embryonic mesoderm development, hence they can be used for 
genetic manipulations to model diseases and dissect the related molecular 
mechanisms.  
As a result, throughout this project, mEpiSCs have been shown to be a robust in 
vitro model system to study complex gastrulation phenotypes like the Pbx1/2-/- 
mutants. Since genetic inactivation via CRISPR/Cas9 are easily accessible in this in vitro 
platform, this method will make the identification of molecular mechanisms and 
regulatory networks easily approachable. Hence, in this project we were able to 
recapitulate via in vitro modelling, the phenotypes observed in the Pbx1/2-/- mutant 
embryos and we identified possible Pbx-dependent molecular networks driving 
mesoderm differentiation.

8. Conclusion and Future Aspects 
 
The in vitro cell culture system developed in this study highlighted the importance 
of Pbx proteins in mesoderm development. In this project, I found that some 
transcription factors critical for the specification of lateral mesoderm progenitors were 
differentially expressed in the Pbx1/2-/- cell lines compared to the WT condition. 
Moreover, one of these factors, Hand1, was also found to be misregulated in the 
Pbx1/2-/- mutant embryos. This observation highlighted the reliability and efficiency of 
the developed cell culture system for modelling diseases. The next step will be to 
address the relationships among the differentially expressed transcription factors. This 
approach will help elucidate upon the mechanisms by which Pbx proteins control 
mesoderm development, and later, organogenesis. 
Furthermore, with this cell culture system I have shown that Pbx1/2-/- cells undergo 
an aberrant APS and paraxial mesoderm differentiation. It would be interesting to 
further analyse this phenotype and address if the mutant cells are dead or simply 
detached from the culture dish. 
Given that the mesoderm gives rise to many important organs like heart, blood 
and muscles, it is mandatory to study the mechanisms behind early mesoderm 
induction and the specification of distinct mesoderm progenitors. Our cell culture 
system will allow efficient modelling of these developmental stages, contributing to 
our overall understanding of developmental biology: how the entire body develops 
from a single cell. Knowledge about these molecular mechanisms will facilitate the 
development of innovative and efficient strategies for stem cell-based therapies.
