Abstract
The aim of this master thesis is to implement the Fable system to provide a detailed
lecture plan for teachers to follow. Furthermore it covers the wide area of teaching
theory and didactic, as much as it describes the modular robotic platform Fable. Fable
was developed as a playware product, from Shape Robotics, at Technical University of
Denmark (DTU). The education of children in an early stage is one of the many motivations
of Shape Robotics. Within this master thesis I provide a way and a solution in how to
integrate the Fable platform into the Danish school system. In particular the biology class
of the secondary school. In small projects and exercises, the children are encouraged to
work in groups with the Fable platform and learn about the many possibilities Fable has
to offer. My personal goal is to inﬂame the interest, and the motivation of robotics, and
engineering, of the children. In a world structured by project work, robotics provides a
interesting and unique opportunity in teaching subjects in a different way [1], [22].
The industry and other groups need highly educated engineers to tackle the upcoming
technological problems [13]. The Fable modular robotic platform aims to provide a product
which ﬁts into the transition period of beginner and expert user. On one side it provides,
with Blockly (a visual programming language), an easy approach to start programming
with robots, and is challenging enough to encourage advanced and expert users to use their
creativity in order to solve the task [25]. The end user can decide if he uses Blockly (a
visual programming language) or Python (a textual programming language) to do so. The
two options will be discussed throughout the thesis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
"We want our human capital to be knowledgeable, have skills that are
relevant to the times and be able to compete in a globalised world.
We want people with new ideas, who are critical and creative, adept
at problem-solving, able to create new opportunities and adaptable to
changes." (Datuk Seri Hishammuddin Hussein, Minister of Education,
The New Straits Times, 7/1/2007)
Over the last decades computers and IT have become a fundamental part of our daily
lives. The technology grew signiﬁcantly, not only at the working environment, but also
in our private life. Usage of smart phones, smart TV’s, computers, or home automation
control, just to name some. Everyone is surrounded by an enormous amount of technology
on a daily basis.
Therefore, it is increasingly important to ﬁnd a way where one can interact and use the
digital world. A general basic informatics education is necessary for everyone and not
only at a university level.
Robotics can be used as a tool for guiding school students toward an effective understanding
of programming and engineering principles [16]. Many available papers and research
studies such as, The Many Beneﬁts of Teaching Robotics in the Classroom [34] and Robots
in the classroom [19] show the positive effect on the learning outcomes with a robotic
system.
In this project an overview, and a possible solution in how to implement the Fable
modular robotic system into the biological education in Denmark, especially between
7th and 9th grade, is given. Due to the educational relevance this thesis not only covers
the robotic aspects, but also the educational theory and the impact of robotics on the system.
The ﬁrst section will provide a deﬁnition of robotics and modular robotic in general,
followed by the project objectives and in the end a description about the following chapters

1.1
State of the Art
In the following sections the main focus is set to modular robotics and therefore
the state of the art will cover mostly the modular robotics ﬁeld. However, ﬁrst a small
comparison between the classic robotic and the modular robotic is made.
Due to the further relevance, those two platforms will be shown in perspective to the
educational system of the Danish school system.

Fable
Fable is a modular robotic system which was developed as playwear technology at
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). This part is an introduction to, not only, the
modular robotics platform Fable, but also to the programming interface and the available
sets.
2.1
Design
The design of Fable and the modules allows to assemble and disassemble a robot very
fast, due to its unique connection method (which can be seen in Fig. 2.1). The design has a
radial symmetry which allows a connection pattern at 90◦intervals. Furthermore Fable,
with its different components, provides the user with numerous possibilities to create a
unique robot design. Due to the unique connection method many different robots can be
assembled in less than 1 minute [29].
The whole platform aims to enable non-expert users to develop robots, ranging from
advanced robotic toys to robotic solutions which encounter in every day lives. Furthermore
Fable is a cordless system which can be programmed wirelessly over a tablet or computer
system [26].
A key feature of the Fable design is its open system which supports several, from very basic
(Blockly) to advanced (Python), programming languages. Fable is therefore particularly
interesting for schools. The schools can use Fable to gradually develop programming
skills from simple visual programming to advanced textual programming. The system is
simple enough to be used from the second class, ﬂexible enough for secondary school and
sufﬁciently challenging for university education [25]. The ﬂexibility provided by the Fable
platform shows that the system is not limited to a speciﬁc age or class, which makes the
product particularly useful for schools[26].
Fable is an open system, which makes it possible to expand it with other modules,

2.1.3
Communication
With the help of the Dongle, which can be seen in Fig. 2.2, all active modules of Fable
are programmed wirelessly. The Dongle is connected via USB port to the desired device,
either PC or Tablet. The connection between computer and the active modules is a strict
master-slave topology. Due to a low lag radio communication link, the user can program
the distributed robots as if it was centralized and connected directly to the PC. In order
to establish a successful communication the user has to pair each active module with the
Dongle by choosing the right bandwidth. This is done by matching the colour, each of the
6 available bandwidths has its own colour code. That architecture prevents cross-compiling
and the difﬁculties of downloading the right software update to the robots. Furthermore
debugging is much simpler with the embedded platform due to the immediate feedback
[26]. Fable is a heterogeneous system.
The network architecture of the Fable system is shown in Fig. 2.5.

2.2
Programming Interface
The software platform is essential for a school, not only the wide support of the plat-
form but also the ﬂexibility that comes with it. In order to provide a suitable platform for
schools, the design of the programming tool, has to support both, an easily accessible visual
programming language, and also a more powerful textual language, for more ﬂexibility.
This is achieved by using Blockly, as visual programming language, due to its simplicity
and the open source platform which is provided by Google1. Blockly as a visual program-
ming language is particularly designed for non-expert users. For more advanced users
Fable also supports Python as a higher level programming language, with which one can
solve more complicated tasks, or in a more ﬂexible way. Furthermore the end user can use
Python to create his own blocks and functions, which can easily be used in Blockly. This
gives the teachers the possibility to constantly expand the Blockly library on their own, and
come up with completely new ideas and tasks.
Blockly is a block-based programming environment, developed by Google, where
the different blocks, which are stored in an open source library, can be put together via
drag and drop. Which means that it is a visual programming language, rather than a text
oriented programming language such as C/C++ or Java.
However it is possible to use Python to create new and more advanced blocks, that increases
the level of ﬂexibility enormous. Furthermore Blockly is completely open source and runs
in a web browser, which makes the use of it very comfortable. An example of Python and
Blockly can be seen in Fig. 2.6.

This chapter provides a detailed overview, to the impact and the importance of robots
in education.
The ﬁrst question one has to ask is, why are robots used in education in the ﬁrst place?
This is a valid question, especially since education worked without robots for most time
throughout history. So why are we trying to include robots into the education, and why do
we try to implement it even earlier.
"The constant breakthroughs in chemistry, medicine, materials and
physics reveal a new set of challenges and create an even greater oppor-
tunity for problem solving through technology. These problems are not
academic; the solutions could help save the world and those technology
problem solvers will be the ones to make it possible" [11].
Not only the basic technological understanding is of big importance, but also to awaken
the students general interest in a career in Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathe-
matics. Further on "Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics" will be shortened
by STEM, which is the standard. Between 2003 and 2008, many countries, also Denmark,
have seen declining numbers of students choosing to pursue the study of STEM related
subjects at university [24]. The percentage over the past years can be seen in Fig. 3.1 in
more detail.
One of many approaches has shown success in changing that, and encourage students to
reconsider studying a STEM related subject. This approach is the ﬁeld of Robotics, which
has been shown by papers like, Impact of robotics and geospatial technology interventions
on youth STEM learning and attitudes [23].
As a pedagogical tool for teaching STEM subjects, robotics has several interesting as-

4.1
Teaching Methods
The method of teaching is called teaching methods, which is the instruction and
guidance that is applied in classroom, the university and in educational offers of adult
education. Although there are many different learning methods and even more shades of
the different learning methods, one can make a distinction mainly between two learning
methods [40]:
1. Self-determined learning [41]. With the self-determined learning, learning objec-
tives are set by individual learning plans. This is done by the students himself, or in
cooperation with a learning assistant. The self-determined learning is used mainly
in adult education. The study plan is often subdivided into individual learning units
with intermediate learning objectives.
2. External-determined learning [2]. In the educational system the general learning
objectives are set by the Ministries of Education which are written down in a cur-
riculum for each grade. The detailed learning objectives can be seen in Chapter 5
in Section 5.2. The implementation of the curriculum is carried out by authorized
schools and teachers. For creating the separate teaching units, the teachers have to
deﬁne the intermediate learning objectives, in order to achieve the given learning
outcomes set by the Ministries of Education. Finding and setting meaningful inter-

4.2.1
Hierarchy of Learning Outcomes
A learning outcome is not, just fulﬁlled or not-fulﬁlled, in fact, in order to distinguish
different stages one has to introduce the hierarchy of learning outcomes.
To distinguish the hierarchy of learning outcomes, psychologist Dr. Benjamin Bloom
introduced the Bloom’s Taxonomy of Learning Domains in 1956, which can be seen in
Fig. 4.1. The Three Domains of Learning, according to Dr. Benjamin Bloom are [6]:
1. Cognitive: Mental skills (knowledge)
2. Affective: Growth in feelings or emotional areas (attitude or self)
3. Psychomotor: Manual or physical skills (skills)
Here, the focus is set solely to the cognitive aspect, because the aim in biology is to
increase the knowledge of the students, and not like for example in the sports class to
achieve a new psychomotor skill. One can see, in Fig. 4.1, the different stages of learning
a new subject, where remembering is the lowest possible learning outcome, and creating
is the highest possible learning outcome. This taxonomy of learning behaviours may be
thought of as "the goals of the learning process" [6].

Swarm Intelligence
To demonstrate swarm intelligence with the modular robotic platform Fable was the
next idea.
In order to demonstrate swarm intelligence with robots, some requirements are necessary,
like:
• All robots need to have the same or comparable layout/speciﬁcations
• They need to be able to communicate between each other
• The need to learn from the others experience and use it to their own advantage
5.7.1
Current status of the Fable platform
The current status of the Fable platform can be best described as strict Master-Slave
topology. That means the computer on which the program runs is sending the commands
in packages to all the robots, and only the robot with the matching address takes the order
and follows it, similar to a CAN-Bus architecture from a car.
In order to implement swarm intelligence behaviour, one needs to change the communica-
tion protocol of the robots and the computer.
5.7.2
Approach to change the communication protocol
To enable a communication between the different robots, the robots have to communi-
cate with each other. There are several ways of doing that, like:
• The robot is capable of broadcasting the message so that every other robot may read
it
• Virtual communication (send message to the pc, and the pc forwards it to the other
robots)
In this case the transmission protocol was changed so that the robots have a virtual
communication between them. Which means, the robots do not communicate directly
between each other but they cast their information back to the computer which then
broadcasts it to every robot. That technique has some advantages, ﬁrst the communication
protocol does not have to be changed completely and second the exchanged informations
can be easily seen and stored with a computer.
This kind of communications a triangular communication, is no real communication
between the robots. However the robots virtually communicate over the computer, this
topology forms a triangle, which can be seen in Fig. 5.9.
This triangular communication only simulates the robot-to-robot communication.

5.8.2
Braitenberg Vehicles
The Braitenberg vehicles are named after their creator, a Italian-Austrian scientist
named Valentin von Braitenberg. He was a brain researchers, cybernetics and writer. He
ﬁrst studied physics, and later medicine in Innsbruck, after that he was a specialist in
neurology and psychiatry in Rome. Valentin von Braitenberg was born 1926 in Bozen
(Italy) and died 2011 in Tübingen (Germany) 10.
A Braitenberg-vehicle is a hypothetical robotic vehicle, that started as a thought experiment.
This very simply constructed vehicle is equipped with either one or more sensors and can
react independently to environmental stimuli. The environmental stimuli are registered by
the sensors and directly forwarded to the vehicles actuators. Hereby the amazingly simple
mechanisms can result in surprisingly complex behaviour[8].
Principle
Out of that, a basic concept of every Braitenberg vehicle can be extracted:
1. The vehicle has two wheels at the rear each with its own drive engine and one or
more sensors.
2. A Braitenberg vehicle has integrated sensors, which are directly linked to the actua-
tors (motors).
3. The sensors report external stimuli to the motors that trigger certain reactions (so
called vehicles).
The sensors detect the different environmental stimuli, for example brightness, sound or
temperature. By coupling the actuators (the drive motors) and the sensors, the driving
wheels are controlled, and a variety of variations with different complexities is achieved,
such as:
• One sensor may affect multiple, or only one, drive motor
• The drive motor can be located on the same side of the vehicle as the sensor or on
the opposite
• The inﬂuence of the sensor may have a reinforcing or inhibitory effect
• The dependence of the engine, may be linear or monotonic, or follow any mathemat-
ical function
• It is possible to combine several sensors for different kinds of stimuli (such as
brightness, sound or temperature) which can differently affect the drive motors

One can see that depending on the coupling of the sensors and actuators, a more or less

vehicles can be represented by the behaviours of living beings such as wander around,
escape and aggression movement. There are 14 different Braitenberg vehicles which are

Vehicle 1: Wander around
The ﬁrst being is vehicle 1, which has one sensor directly
connected to one Motor as one can see in Fig. C.3.
The relationship between the two (sensor and actuator) is very simple: The output of the
sensor is linear to the motor speed. In other words the more the sensor detects the faster the
motor. Lets take a temperature sensor, and let the force applied by the motor rise precisely
proportional to the absolute temperature: The vehicle will always move in the direction in
which it is facing (always straight), because the absolute temperature is nowhere equal to
0. The vehicle will drive slower in cold areas and faster in warmer regions.
Figure 5.11: Vehicle 1: The speed of the motor is directly correlated to the sensor output

Vehicle 2: Fear and Aggression
Vehicle 2 is similar to the ﬁrst vehicle but it has two
sensors, one on each side of the vehicle, instead of only one.
Vehicle 2 is equipped with two drive wheels and two light sensors. The inﬂuence of
a sensor on its associated drive motor is monotone-reinforcing: the motor rotates faster
when brightness increases, and slower when brightness decreases. The vehicle exists in
two variations (see Fig. C.5).
• Vehicle 2a the sensor inﬂuences the motor on the same side
• Vehicle 2b the sensor inﬂuences the motor on the opposite side

The project ant trail provides a method to teach the students that, with the help of ob-

[32].

• learn how ants live and communicate.
• learn to abstract the behaviour of ants and transfer it to the robot world.

5.9.2
Implementation
The following robot task is very difﬁcult to realise in the current design stage of the
robotic platform. The sensor module is able to detect a distance to an object in front of it
but it cannot detect any direction. In order to surpass the direction problem, the approach
was to transmit the remote commands to the second (following) robot too. In other words,
the following robot would know when the ﬁrst robot turns and where it turns, therefore the
ultrasonic sensor in the sensor module would only be used to maintain a certain distance.
However the problem is that the friction between the wheel module and ground is insufﬁ-
cient, which leads to an unpredictable direction of the robot, even when just moving in a
straight line. This and the inability to detect a direction leads to the conclusion that it is
not possible to implement this project in its current design stage.
5.9.3
Closing Thought
In order to improve the entire robot conﬁguration and to make it more ﬂexible for
the projects, I suggest to implement an IR-Sensor into the sensor module. With an IR-
Sensor the ant trail exercise could be solved differently. For example the ant trail could be
represented by a black tape on the ﬂoor which can be recognised by the IR-Sensor in order
to ﬁnd the desired destination. All kind of shapes and patterns could be realised.
It would be important for the ant trail exercise that the IR-Sensor has a very short range

Dancing Bees
The honey bee performs different dances, depending on the distance and the location of
the food-sources to the beehive. The students develop their knowledge of the language of

but also improves the ability to describe certain phenomena. Both are very important to

with the Fable platform.

module this exercise would be very interesting to realise.

Introduction
Tuition and work is almost exclusively project-oriented. The tasks are provided in a
project work which is meant to solve in short groups of 2 to 4 people, depending on the
size of the class. The lecture plan is divided into two parts. First a part for the teachers
which includes the basic theory about the topic, the required time, learning outcomes, and
the dialectical methods. The second part is a collection of topic related questions and tasks
for the students. Furthermore it also contains useful hints in order to solve the given task.
That means that the students work independently at the practical implementation of a given
task, after a simple and kept short introduction of the subject.
The schedule of the lecture plan can be seen in Appendix E in more detail.

The lecture plan can be seen in the Appendix C, due to its length. The lecture plan for
the teachers was created to contain a theory background and a list of desired competences
reached by the students. It is not a fully structured lecture due to some feedback from
the teachers during numerous interviews. The majority of the teachers does not want a
ﬁnished product but rather a collection of ideas and ways to structure the lecture.

However the lecture plan and the quantity of ideas, to implement the Fable platform into

being improved and I am positive that with the new module even more possibilities will be
available.
==PAGE:79==
CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Throughout the construction and designing of the lecture plan several interesting ob-
servation were made. First of all the interest of the platform is enormous, not only the kids
but also the teachers are fascinated by the design and the appearance of the Fable platform.
Furthermore all the teachers I have spoken to were very eager to try it during their lectures.
However there is room for improvement, especially the wheel module is not quit ﬁtting
into the positive appearance. In fact the wheel module is redesigned in a parallel project
and I am certain that with this improvement made many more possibilities to implement
the platform into the lecture are available.
The designed lecture plan got very positive feedback throughout the interviews with
different teachers. However the real test will verify that. So far only pilot tests were made,
which only shown the general feasibility of the exercises and projects. The lecture plan
it its current state is useful and fulﬁls the purpose of following a 10 week course. An
additional tutorial which needs another 3-4 hours has been included in the thesis, but
excluded of the lecture plan.
With the improvement of the Fable platform, such as the wheel module and the sensor
module, regarding the IR-sensor, the usability of the Fable platform can be increased even
further.

APPENDIX C
Lecture Plan - Teacher
This chapter covers the lecture plan for the teachers. It covers the different exercises
and examples. Furthermore it provides a basic theory input in how to teach the different
subject.
C.1
Sense of sensors; Sensors for the senses
Feeling, smelling, tasting - what we can sense with our human senses, is also possible
in the technical world. For example, sensors detect smoke when it burns, or help mobile
robots to avoid obstacles.
C.1.1
Motivation
The schoolgirls and schoolboys get to know the deﬁnitions of senses and sensors
and acquire knowledge about the senses of people and animals. By the example of the
animals senses, the pupils transfer the learned knowledge to the robot world. Finally
the schoolgirls and schoolboys get to know the uses of sensors in the everyday life.
Thus develop a connections between the subjects computer science and biology, which
inspire thematically both girls and boys, especially the positive impact on girls can be
seen in Weinberg’s The impact of robot projects on girls’ attitudes toward science and
engineering[42].
C.1.2
Theory

C.2
Exercise on "Communication"
Every interaction between two or more individuals, same also applies for machines,
such as computer or robots, is based on communication. Therefore it is crucial to un-
derstand the concept of communication and why communication fails. Both, between
individuals such as animals or humans, and machines such as computer or robots.
C.2.1
Theory
That communication is possible and functions; it always needs a transmitter, a receiver
and a channel [37]. For example, if a male owl calls out its mating sound and a squirrel
sits in the proximity; that is still no communication, because the squirrel is not interested
at all in the mating call of the owl. However if an owl female hears the mating call, the
communication succeeds. It is not enough to just send a message; the message must also
arrive and be understood by the recipient. That can happen also very subtly. Thus, physical
features can already be sent and understood as signals in the animal kingdom. A sleeping
dog sends out a message; namely that it is asleep and therefore cannot hunt and chase a cat
which is running by. The transceiver model deﬁnes communication, in the information
theory, as transmission of a message from a transmitter to a receiver. In addition the
message is coded and transferred as signal over a channel to a receiver. However the
message can be falsiﬁed by disturbances. A condition for successful communication is
that transmitters and receivers use the same coding for the message. In order to recognize
disturbances, it is important that the receiver gives a feedback (coded feedback) to the
transmitter to what extent he understood the message. The information theory provides
additional tool, with which one can be computed how much redundancy a message must
contain, in order to adjust a given measure of disturbances. The term of the redundancy
indicates information that is expressed, to the receiver, more than once [37].

C.3
Exercise on "Ways to transmit messages"
Nothing works without communication in the nature. Animals need to communicate
with their environment and congeners in order to survive. This communication is critical,
whether it serves the kind recognition, the organization of the group, the partner choice,
the food search, the camouﬂage or the warning of dangers. Within millions of years the
nature developed a wide variety of communication channels and techniques, which often
remain hidden from us, because humans lack the sense to receive these signals [3].
C.3.1
Acoustic signals
The singing of male birds serves on the one hand the courtship display, on the other
hand it is used to keep strange male out of its territory. In addition, birds also communicate
by calls. A raven knows at least 34 calls, every sound of it has own meaning. Finally ravens
also like to imitate the calls of other animal species, e.g. calls of crows, the courtship
display song of the capercaillie, dog barking and a lot of other. Also herewith they try to
impress the other gender [7].
C.3.2
Optical signals
Facial play and gesture belong to the optical signals. Some monkey species have
the ability to communicate through different Facial expressions. A torn throat does not
indicate that the baboon yawns because it is tired, but that it is aggressive and threatens
other animals. If a baboon’s female shows her back to a dominant man during a conﬂict, it
shows respect and obsequiousness. Another example are honeybees, which indicate each
==PAGE:93==
C.3. EXERCISE ON "WAYS TO TRANSMIT MESSAGES"
75
other, by dances, the exact place of a feed source. These are only two examples of how
optical signals are used in the animal kingdom [7].
C.3.3
Ultrasound and click signals
Dolphins communicate on the one hand over different whistlers, on the other hand over,
for the human ear not perceptible, fast tone rows within the ultrasonic range. The animals
are capable of transmitting up to 2000 tones per second. These sounds of the dolphins,
the so-called "Click calls" serve the orientation and beyond that also the hunt with poor
visibility. Dolphins are not the only animals using ultrasonic sounds for orientation; also
bats use this system of the echo ranging [7].
C.3.4
Chemical signals
The insect larvae which gnaw at plant roots send chemical signals to the sheets. Other
insects receive this information, ﬁnd out that the plant is already occupied, and search
themselves for another plant. Also dogs or cats mark there territory by odoriferous
substances to separate it from others. Ants also communicate by means of chemical
materials, the so called pheromones. If a danger threatens, an ant sprays formic acid and
the close congeners ﬂee immediately in all directions. If an ant wants to attack a prey,
another chemical material is used which attracts other ants and makes them aggressive [7].
C.3.5
Deception
Butterﬂies often use visual effects of surprise to confuse and to deter enemies. The
local peacock butterﬂy has often folded the wings, which resembles a dried sheet. If an
enemy approaches, it quickly spreads its wings apart. Due to the big ocular spots on the
wings it mimicries the face of a bigger animal, and deceives with it its enemy [7].
C.3.6
Infrasonic
Elephants live in herds with up to 100 animals. However, individual elephants leave
the group from time to time, and wander alone. Since the elephant’s trumpets are not to be
heard about a wide distance, the elephants communicate by means of infra-sound. This
infra-sound is a very loud and deep noise between 14 and 25 hertz which people cannot
hear. The infra-sound transferences about the ground, therefore elephants cannot only hear
infra-sound but also feel it with their sensitive feet [7].

C.3.7
Touch/Contact
If a forest ant nation wants to move, ﬁrst scouts swarm out, in order to ﬁnd a new and
suitable place. If an ant ﬁnds a suitable place, it returns for the ant construction, bumps
there into a congener who rolls up. The rolled up ant is then carried to the new place. If
the carried ant is also persuaded by the new location of the new place, it also returns to the
ant nation and bumps into another ant. By this snowball system many ants get to know the
new place in short time, which ﬁnally leads to the move of the entire ant population [7].
C.3.8
Electric shocks
Electrical ﬁsh like for example the electric eel are able to produce current draws. These
can be used either for the hunt or defence, for orientation, for district demarcation and
for the dating. Whit these electrical signals, electrical ﬁsh can communicate with animals
of their same species - which gains in signiﬁcance by the fact that many of them are
night active, live in muddy waters and have no or stunted eyes. By their special receptors,
they can perceive small changes of the electric ﬁeld, as low as the areas of around 0.1
microvolts, and so get an exact spatial picture of their surroundings, even in complete
darkness [7].

C.4.4
Braitenberg Vehicles
The Braitenberg vehicles are named after their creator, a Italian-Austrian scientist
named Valentin von Braitenberg. He was a brain researchers, cybernetics and writer. He
ﬁrst studied physics, and later medicine in Innsbruck, after that he was a specialist in
neurology and psychiatry in Rome. Valentin von Braitenberg was born 1926 in Bozen
(Italy) and died 2011 in Tübingen (Germany) 4.
A Braitenberg-vehicle is a hypothetical robotic vehicle, that started as a thought
experiment. This very simply constructed vehicle is equipped with either one or more
sensors and can react independently to environmental stimuli. The environmental stimuli
are registered by the sensors and directly forwarded to the vehicles actuators. Hereby the
amazingly simple mechanisms can result in surprisingly complex behaviour[8].

Principle
Out of that, a basic concept of every Braitenberg vehicle can be extracted:
1. The vehicle has two wheels at the rear each with its own drive engine and one or
more sensors.
2. A Braitenberg vehicle has integrated sensors, which are directly linked to the actua-
tors (motors).
3. The sensors report external stimuli to the motors that trigger certain reactions (so
called vehicles).
The sensors detect the different environmental stimuli, for example brightness, sound
or temperature. By coupling the actuators (the drive motors) and the sensors, the driving
wheels are controlled, and a variety of variations with different complexities is achieved,
such as:
• One sensor may affect multiple, or only one, drive motor
• The drive motor can be located on the same side of the vehicle as the sensor or on
the opposite
• The inﬂuence of the sensor may have a reinforcing or inhibitory effect
• The dependence of the engine, may be linear or monotonic, or follow any mathemat-
ical function
• It is possible to combine several sensors for different kinds of stimuli (such as
brightness, sound or temperature) which can differently affect the drive motors
One can see that depending on the coupling of the sensors and actuators, a more or
less complex motion behaviour of the vehicle can be seen. The motion behaviour of the
vehicles can be represented by the behaviours of living beings such as wander around,
escape and aggression movement. There are 14 different Braitenberg vehicles which are
also described as "beings"[8].
