Abstract
This thesis investigates how oil price changes (WTI) and market uncertainty (VIX)
influence sector-specific U.S. stock returns across different market regimes. Focusing
on Information Technology, Consumer Discretionary, and Health Care, it examines how
sensitivity to these macroeconomic shocks varies across sectors with different economic
profiles and across shifting volatility environments. Using rolling Granger causality and
impulse response functions, the relationships are found to be highly time-varying and
predictive, especially in the period following the Global Financial Crisis of 2008. The
results also reveal asymmetric sectoral responses and evolving tail risks. Combined with
the increasing spillover of oil into financial markets, these findings point to a growing
role for oil not just as a commodity, but also in asset allocation strategies. This raises
important implications for both risk hedging and systemic stability.
Building on these insights, sector-specific XGBoost models are developed to test
whether oil and volatility signals can be used to improve return forecasts in practice.
When applied to actual market environments, the models perform especially well during
the turbulent phases of the testing period, namely COVID-19 and the 2025 tariff crisis,
where each sector-specific model offers downside protection and outperforms benchmark
strategies. A simple hybrid strategy, triggered by VIX levels, further shows how these
findings can be turned into an adaptive investment strategy. Overall, the results suggest
that, under the right conditions, these macroeconomic signals can be transformed into
useful tools for both forecasting and risk management. They also imply that portfolios
should be adjusted more actively during times of uncertainty, especially in more sensitive
sectors like Information Technology.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The interaction between oil prices and stock markets has long been the subject of eco-
nomic and financial research. As a fundamental input in the global economy, oil price
fluctuations have far-reaching consequences for corporate profitability, consumer behav-
ior, and overall investor sentiment. Especially in turbulent times, such as the Global
Financial Crisis in 2008, both oil prices and stock markets have historically experienced
increased volatility. Given that oil supply is frequently controlled or restricted for polit-
ical and strategic reasons, whether through OPEC’s production quotas, the expansion
of U.S. shale drilling, or geopolitical events like Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, researchers
are interested in investigating whether oil prices can be used to predict financial mar-
kets. Beyond establishing a relationship, a key question is also whether this link remains
stable across different market environments, or whether it changes during calm periods
versus times of crisis. Being able to anticipate how changes in oil price affect financial
markets could offer investors valuable risk management strategies, helping to construct
more resilient portfolios. Since systemic risk, by nature, cannot be eliminated through
diversification alone, identifying mechanisms to mitigate its effect, such as understand-
ing sector-specific sensitivities to oil price shocks, can be crucial for investors. These
insights could support decisions around asset allocation, hedging strategies and overall
portfolio construction, helping investors better manage risk tied to oil price movements
and market uncertainty.
Historical trends indicate that stock market reactions to oil price changes are neither
uniform nor consistent across economic cycles, geopolitical events, or industry sectors
(Chapter 2). Sectors like Energy and Industrials are directly affected by changes in oil
prices. Others, such as Information Technology and Health Care, respond in a more
nuanced way due to their lower dependence on oil. Moreover, the relationship between
oil prices and stock markets is not necessarily linear. Evidence suggests (Chapter 2)
that positive and negative oil price shocks may have asymmetric effects, which means
that their impact differs in magnitude and direction [1]. On top of that, their impact
can vary depending on how volatile the market is at the time, raising the question of
whether oil price fluctuations have a stronger influence during times of market stress
than in more stable periods.
In addition to examining historical relationships, this thesis incorporates machine
learning (ML) techniques for forecasting sector-specific stock performance under differ-

ent market conditions. Traditional econometric models such as Vector Autoregression
(VAR) and Granger causality tests have been widely applied to study these relation-
ships, but ML approaches, among them eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), offer the
potential to enhance predictive accuracy by capturing nonlinear and high-dimensional
dependencies in financial data.
By integrating econometric methods and ML-based forecasting techniques, this study
aims to investigate whether oil price fluctuations and market uncertainty have an in-
fluence on sector-specific returns and whether these relationships can be leveraged to
improve sector return forecasting. Furthermore, the findings will be analyzed from an
investment strategy perspective to determine how knowledge of these relationships can
enhance portfolio construction and risk management.
1.2
Research Objectives
This thesis investigates the relationship between historical oil prices, sector specific stock
performance, and market uncertainty, as measured by the CBOE Volatility Index (VIX).
By focusing on the Information Technology, Consumer Discretionary and Health Care
sectors, the study aims to analyze how these relationships evolve under different market
conditions: With a focus on comparing periods of high versus low volatility. Further-
more these relationships will be leveraged to assess their predictive power using machine
learning models. The findings will contribute to a deeper understanding of sectoral sen-
sitivities to oil price fluctuations and market uncertainty, providing practical insights
for risk management and investment strategies.
1.2.1
Research Question
The main objective of this thesis is:
How do oil price fluctuations and market uncertainty impact sector-specific stock
performance, and can these relationships be used to predict stock returns under different
volatility regimes?
To address this objective, the thesis will investigate the following key questions:
1. How does the relationship between oil prices an stock sectors evolve during periods
of high versus low market uncertainty, as indicated by the VIX?

1.3
Methodological Approach
To address these Research Questions, this study follows a methodological approach that
integrates econometric analysis with machine learning techniques. The first step is to
examine the relationship between oil price movements and market uncertainty, as mea-
sured by the VIX, and their combined effect on sectoral stock returns. This analysis is
conducted both at an aggregate level and across different time periods to account for
potential time-varying effects. Furthermore, the study explores whether the impact of
oil price fluctuations differs across volatility regimes and in response to various price
shocks.
To determine whether oil price changes and market uncertainty have a predictive in-
fluence on sector-specific stock returns, the study makes use of Granger-causality tests.
These tests assess whether past values of oil prices and the VIX contain valuable infor-
mation for forecasting future stock performance. Additionally, the relationship between
oil price shocks and VIX levels is analyzed both individually and in combination to
evaluate whether their interaction enhances predictive power. The findings from this
analysis will also be compared across different sectors to identify significant variations
in sensitivity to oil price movements and market uncertainty during periods of economic
turbulence.
Building on these insights, the study incorporates a machine learning approach by
leveraging an XGBoost model to determine whether oil price fluctuations and market
uncertainty can be used to forecast sectoral stock returns. The predictive performance
of the XGBoost model will be benchmarked against a baseline Buy & Hold approach.
This comparative analysis will determine whether machine learning predictions can be
leveraged into practical strategies for investing comparing them with market strategies,
offering practical implications for risk management and investment strategies.
By addressing these questions, this thesis aims to provide a more detailed and quanti-
tative understanding of how oil price movements impact financial markets beyond tradi-
tional broad-market analyses. Additionally, by incorporating machine learning models,
the study evaluates the predictive power of these relationships, potentially offering valu-
able insights for investors and risk managers.

this thesis, it is essential to place the study within the broader academic conversation.
Stock market forecasting and the investigation of cross-market linkages have long
been core concerns in financial economics, given their implications for risk management,
portfolio construction, and macro-financial stability. Within this context, the relation-
ship between oil price fluctuations and stock returns has attracted growing attention.
Several reviews [2, 3, 4] track the evolution of this literature, noting a significant rise in
research activity following the Global Financial Crisis in 2008, reflecting developments
such as the financialization of oil, increased geopolitical risk, and advances in empirical
methods. As shown in Figure 2.1, the number of publications on this topic has grown
steadily.

2.1
Findings on the Relationship between Oil
Prices and Stock Markets
2.1.1
Theoretical Framework for how Oil Price changes
can affect stock returns
The connection between oil price changes and stock returns has been studied from several
different theoretical angles linked to asset pricing and macroeconomic theory. At the
core of most frameworks is the idea that oil price fluctuations affect stock valuations by
influencing either expected future cash flows or the discount rate used in asset pricing.
Since both of these affect the present value of future earnings, changes in oil price can
have immediate effects on stock prices [5].
==PAGE:19==
2.1 Findings on the Relationship between Oil Prices and Stock Markets
7
From a firm-level perspective, oil price shocks influence input costs, profit margins,
and eventually earnings. For oil-producing companies, rising oil prices may boost rev-
enues and profits, while for oil-dependent or energy-intensive industries, input costs
increase, potentially decreasing margins [3, 9]. These asymmetries help explain why
sectoral responses to oil shocks are often heterogeneous.
Macroeconomic factors further contribute to this relationship. Oil price shocks of-
ten contribute to inflation, raising production costs and reducing consumer spending,
which then in turn affects corporate earnings [15]. Higher inflation typically leads to
higher interest rates, which increases borrowing costs and reduces stock market returns,
especially in sectors that are sensitive to the interest-rate [9, 16]. Additionally, oil price
volatility introduces economic uncertainty, which discourages business investments and
reduces overall household consumption [17, 18].
These theoretical mechanisms suggest that the oil-stock market relationship is un-
likely to be uniform. Instead, the nature and strength of the effect depends on macroe-
conomic conditions, sectoral characteristics, and the structure of the oil shock itself (e.g.,
demand-driven vs. supply-driven). This provides a compelling reason for exploring these
dynamics through models that allow for time variation and sector-specific sensitivity.
2.1.2
Empirical Findings Market Condition and
Uncertainty
Empirical research confirms that the relationship between oil prices and stock markets
is both complex and highly context-dependent. A number of studies highlight that the
magnitude and direction of oil’s impact on stock returns vary across economic environ-
ments, which calls for flexible, time-sensitive frameworks.
For example, Kilian et al. [1] show that oil price shocks have asymmetric effects
on financial markets depending on whether they are driven by supply disruptions or
demand surges. Similarly, Park et al. [11] and Sadorsky [19] find that oil price increases
tend to negatively affect stock returns in oil-importing countries, though this effect is
not stable over time.
Structural shifts in global energy dynamics, such as the U.S. shale revolution, have
also affected oil-stock linkages. As Nakhle [20] notes, the United States has transitioned
from a major oil importer to a more balanced actor in energy markets, complicating
previous assumptions about exposure.
Findings from other studies also highlight the time-varying nature of oil-stock market
connectedness. Here, Qin et al.[21] find that the correlation between oil prices and stock
markets depends on the changes in the interest rate, as monetary policy shifts have
an effect on both investor behavior and capital flows. During periods of rising interest
rates, stock market liquidity will lessen and thus also reduce the co-movements of oil
prices and stock prices [22]. The other way around, financial distress can strengthen this

2.1.3
Sectoral Differences in Oil-Stock Relationships
Beyond any aggregate market effects, sectoral sensitivity to oil price movements varies
significantly. Zhao et al. [10] find that the relationship between oil prices and stock
markets differs across industries, making it essential to analyze sector-specific trends
rather than treating the market as a single entity. This is supported by [2], who finds
that oil price sensitivity is heterogeneous across European sectors, with automobile,
energy, and industrial firms showing stronger linkages to oil price movements compared
to technology and financial stocks. These findings align with Mohanty et al. [9], who
note that oil-intensive industries experience more direct revenue shocks, whereas others
are affected more indirectly through inflation, interest rate changes, or shifts in consumer
demand. Hernandez et al. [25] highlight the heterogeneous behavior of the individual
stock sectors and that studying the spillovers in the stock market at a sectoral level
is essential for investors, who aim to create diversified portfolios or hedging strategies.
It also strengthens the case for integrating sector-level analysis into forecasting models,
particularly under varying market regimes.
Machine learning research shows this trend as well. Kumbure et al. [14] observe
that ML-based financial prediction studies increasingly focus on sectors like Information
Technology, Health Care, and Consumer Discretionary, which are industries that are
less directly oil-dependent but more sensitive to macro-financial variables and investor
sentiment. This mirrors the sectoral scope of the current thesis, which examines the
predictive impact of oil prices and uncertainty on these same industries.
Overall, empirical research confirms that beyond time-varying effects and market
conditions, the oil-stock dynamics are also highly sector-dependent. A one-size-fits-all
approach is not suﬀicient, and sector-specific analyses and models offer clearer insights
into market behavior.
This further supports the need for a detailed, sector-specific
analysis rather than broad market-wide conclusions.
2.2
Existing Methodologies and Gaps in the
Literature
2.2.1
Econometric Approaches
The methods commonly used for studying oil-stock relationships have evolved substan-
tially over the past two decades.
Early contributions primarily relied on traditional
econometric models, such as Vector Autoregression (VAR), cointegration techniques
and Granger causality tests, and GARCH-family models, to analyze risk spillovers and
volatility dynamics [12, 13]. While these models remain widely used, one major issue
is their inability to fully capture nonlinear, time-varying, and heterogeneous sectoral
behaviors [10]. For example, the linear specification of VAR models is often ill-suited for

capturing the nonlinear and evolving nature of oil-stock dynamics. Similarly, standard
Granger causality tests do not account for changes in the relationships over time.
In response, several modified models have been developed. Multivariate GARCH
variants like BEKK-GARCH and VAR-GARCH improve spillover analysis but come at
the cost of greater computational complexity and stronger distributional assumptions.
Time-varying parameter (TVP) VAR models and regime-switching frameworks offer
additional flexibility [21, 25].
However, they also require careful tuning and strong
model specifications.
While traditional econometric models remain widely used and can bring excellent
results as shown by several of the papers findings, their inherent limitations in capturing
complex, nonlinear, and time-varying relationships make a good case for exploring more
flexible, data-driven alternatives that can better adapt to the evolving nature of oil-stock
interactions.
2.2.2
Machine Learning Approaches (Emerging)
Meanwhile, the rise of machine learning (ML) has introduced powerful, data-driven alter-
natives. Unlike traditional econometrics, ML methods do not require prior assumptions
about the form of relationships. Instead, they are trained to uncover patterns directly
from data, making them well-suited for high-dimensional, nonlinear, and time-varying
systems.
As highlighted in Kumbure et al.
[14], ML approaches, such as ensemble
models1 like XGBoost, have increased in their application for financial forecasting tasks
due to their predictive accuracy and robustness against overfitting. As computational
capabilities have advanced, these strengths have become even more pronounced, which
has contributed to the rapid expansion of AI applications in finance. This growing adop-
tion is also shown in the increasing amount of literature exploring ML-driven financial
forecasting (Figure 2.3).

There is limited integration of macroeconomic variables such as oil prices or uncertainty

Even fewer papers link ML inputs to underlying economic theory or use

The structure and

The thesis builds forecasting inputs from time-varying Granger causal relationships
between oil prices, the VIX, and sectoral stock returns. It further segments data into
high- and low-volatility regimes based on VIX thresholds, allowing for regime-sensitive
modeling. These features are then used to train and evaluate XGBoost models, with per-
formance benchmarked against the overall market returns. In doing so, the thesis tests
whether theory, informed predictors and market conditions can enhance the application
of machine learning in sector-specific stock return prediction.
2.3
Contribution to the Literature
While the relationship between oil prices and stock markets has been studied extensively,
several gaps remain.
Existing research often overlooks the combined importance of
sectoral heterogeneity, market uncertainty, and time-varying dynamics. Studies that do
focus on sector-level behavior tend to rely on static relationships or assume linearity
across time, which can hide meaningful structural changes, especially during periods of
financial turbulence.
Additionally, although machine learning (ML) techniques are increasingly used in fi-
nancial forecasting, their application to oil-stock interactions remains limited. Most ML
models prioritize technical indicators and treat predictions as an end in themselves, with-
out considering the underlying economic mechanisms. Empirical insights from econo-
metrics, such as causality, asymmetric effects, and regime dependence are rarely used
to inform model structure or feature selection.
This thesis addresses these shortcomings by proposing a hybrid framework that com-
bines the strengths of both econometric and ML models. Specifically, it contributes to

CHAPTER 3
Data
This chapter presents the data used in the thesis, including the sources, key character-
istics, and preprocessing steps such as cleaning and feature engineering. The selection
process ensures that the data meaningfully reflects the relationship between oil price
fluctuations, market uncertainty, and sector-specific stock performance.
3.1
Data Selection
To investigate how oil price changes and market uncertainty influence stock market
sectors, three distinct sectors have been chosen for analysis: Health Care, Consumer
Discretionary, and Information Technology.
These sectors were chosen to capture a
range of varying economic characteristics and sensitivities to macroeconomic conditions.
Health Care is typically considered a defensive sector, meaning it tends to be more stable
during economic downturns due to the essential nature of its products and services. In
contrast, Consumer Discretionary represents a cyclical sector, which is more sensitive to
economic conditions, as consumer spending typically fluctuates with economic growth
and decline [29]. Finally, Information Technology was included due to its significance
in financial markets. It has been the fastest-growing sector in the S&P 500 for the past
decades and now accounts for 30.3% of the index, reflecting its importance in both U.S.
and global stock markets [30].
A key motivation for this sectoral focus is that while industries such as energy or
heavy industrials have a direct dependence on oil as an input resource, the relationship
between oil price movements and less directly exposed sectors remains less obvious and,
therefore, more intriguing to investigate. Understanding whether oil price shocks influ-
ence sectors such as Information Technology or Consumer Discretionary, even though
they do not depend directly on oil, helps uncover how broader market forces or investor
reactions connect commodity movements with equity performance. This makes it possi-
ble to identify financial linkages that are not immediately obvious from a production or
cost-based perspective.
The U.S. stock market was chosen as the primary focus of this study for several
reasons.
The United States has the world’s largest and most influential stock mar-
ket, representing approximately 53% of the total global stock market capitalization [20].
Additionally, U.S. markets have readily available, high-quality data, making them a
convenient and reliable option for this research. Furthermore, while many studies have

explored the relationship between oil prices and overall U.S. market performance, rela-
tively few have examined these effects on a sectoral level with a focus on market volatility
and uncertainty, making this study a valuable addition to the existing literature.
To represent oil price movements, this thesis makes use of West Texas Intermediate
(WTI) crude oil prices. WTI is one of the three primary global oil benchmarks, along
with Brent and Dubai Crude, and is particularly relevant for this research since it serves
as the referenced oil of the New York Mercantile Exchange (NYMEX) [31].
Given
that the analysis focuses on the impact of oil prices on U.S. stock sectors, WTI is an
appropriate benchmark to use.
This study specifically examines spot prices of WTI rather than futures prices. The
rationale behind this choice is that spot prices reflect immediate market conditions and
the direct impact of oil price fluctuations on financial markets. Futures prices, on the
other hand, incorporate a speculative component based on expectations of future supply
and demand, which could introduce additional noise unrelated to real-time market dy-
namics. By focusing on spot prices, the analysis provides a clearer picture of the direct
relationship between oil price movements and stock market performance.
Market uncertainty is captured using the Chicago Board Options Exchange Volatility
Index (CBOE VIX), also referred to as the ”Fear Index” of the stock market. The VIX
measures the 30-day expected volatility of the U.S. stock market, derived from real-time
mid-quote prices of S&P 500 call and put options. It is widely recognized as a key
indicator of investor sentiment and market stress, often cited in financial media and
used by traders, investors, and policymakers to assess overall market conditions [32].
The VIX was chosen as the primary measure of market turbulence because it effec-
tively captures investor fear and uncertainty, which are key factors influencing stock
price movements. Since this thesis examines how market conditions shape the relation-
ship between oil prices and stock performance, including the VIX makes it possible to
analyze how these dynamics change across different volatility environments.
By utilizing these sectoral stock return datasets, WTI spot prices, and the VIX, this
study aims to construct a comprehensive framework for understanding how oil price
shocks and market uncertainty interact to influence sector-specific stock performance.

For the forecasting task, which focuses on market dynamics during periods of height-

assign more recent market behavior, including relevant crisis periods, to the testing set.
This is problematic both in terms of model relevance and for practical applications,
where the goal is to leverage recent relationships for future forecasting.
To address this, the forecasting dataset is truncated to begin in 2000. This provides

data from multiple volatility regimes while still adhering to the 80/20 guideline.

80% of the data to training provides the best trade-off between parameter estimation
accuracy and predictive generalization.
To test the robustness of the forecasting framework, the test period starts in January
2020 to assess how well the model performs during the COVID-19 crisis and the years
that followed. This setup ensures that the model is evaluated in a highly turbulent
period.
The decision not to test on earlier crises also reflects the need to keep the
training data both as extensive and as relevant as possible, allowing the model to learn
from a broad range of pre-COVID market stress events while maintaining a test set that
covers approximately 20% of the total sample.

3.4.2
Input Features for Forecasting Model
To determine an appropriate lag length for the forecasting model, a Vector Autoregres-
sion (VAR) lag order selection procedure was conducted using Akaike Information Crite-
rion (AIC), Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) and Hannan-Quinn Criterion (HQIC).
The analysis was based on the dataset starting from January 2000, which aligns with
the forecasting period.
These criteria are designed to balance two competing goals. Achieving a good in-
sample fit and avoiding overfitting by penalizing model complexity. The AIC minimizes
the information loss associated with a model and is more lenient when it comes to
including additional lags, often favoring more complex models. BIC, on the other hand,
imposes a stronger penalty for adding parameters and tends to prefer simpler models with
fewer parameters. HQIC lies in between these two extremes and is generally considered
a reasonable compromise, especially when sample sizes are large [38].
As expected, the information criteria pointed to different optimal lag lengths. BIC
suggested a single lag, favoring model simplicity, while AIC continued to decline and
reached its minimum at lag 13. HQIC indicated an optimum at lag 2 but remained
relatively stable up to lag 5–6 before increasing more noticeably. Based on these obser-
vations and to balance the recommendations of all three criteria, lag 6 was selected as a
reasonable compromise. As shown in Figure 3.1, this choice strikes a balance between in-
corporating suﬀicient historical information and avoiding unnecessary model complexity
or overfitting.

forecasting model. A middle ground was selected using AIC, BIC, and HQIC.

CHAPTER 4
Methods
This thesis investigates the dynamic relationship between oil price changes (WTI), mar-
ket uncertainty (VIX), and sector-specific stock returns by analyzing their interaction
across different market conditions. The methodology is structured to address the follow-
ing key objectives:
• Examining the varying correlation between oil price changes and the VIX index un-
der different market regimes to identify how uncertainty influences the relationship
between commodity price fluctuations and investor sentiment.
• Investigating Granger causality to assess whether oil price changes and VIX move-
ments have predictive power over sector returns, both in aggregate and across
different time periods.
• Evaluating the sectoral sensitivity to oil price shocks by analyzing sector perfor-
mance and volatility under differing market uncertainty conditions.
• Assessing sector-specific risk exposure by measuring extreme losses and downside
risk under different market regimes and oil price conditions.
Non-parametric tests (Kruskal-Wallis and Mann-Whitney U) are used to assess statis-
tical significance, as financial return distributions often violate the assumptions required
for parametric methods.
Following the investigation into the nature of these relationships, predictive modeling
will be implemented to assess the practical applicability of the findings for forecasting
sector returns. The methodology employs a machine learning approach (XGBoost) to
capture both non-linear interactions and time-varying dependencies between oil price
changes, market uncertainty, and sector performance. The predictive accuracy of these
models will be assessed using conventional error metrics such as Mean Absolute Error
(MAE) and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE).
Finally, the models predictions will be tested for their practical implications. Sector
return forecasts derived from the models will be used to construct portfolio strategies,
which will be evaluated in terms of return and volatility over time. These model-driven
strategies will be compared against a traditional Buy & Hold approach, providing in-
sight into the real-world applicability of forecasting methods in asset allocation and risk
management.

time series.

4.2
Generalized Impulse Response Functions
To examine how sector returns dynamically respond to shocks in oil prices and market
volatility, Generalized Impulse Response Functions (GIRFs) are applied. Unlike orthog-
onal impulse responses, GIRFs do not depend on the ordering of variables in the VAR
system, making them well suited for settings where no clear theoretical ordering exists
between predictors (WTI and VIX).
The GIRF methodology, introduced by Pesaran and Shin [44], allows for more re-
alistic shock analysis by accounting for the contemporaneous correlations among the
residuals. This means that when a one-unit shock is applied to one variable (e.g., WTI),
the GIRF captures not only its direct effect on the sector return but also how other
variables (like the VIX) may simultaneously react due to historical co-movement. In
contrast to orthogonalized IRFs, which artificially hold other variables constant or re-
quire strong identification assumptions, GIRFs incorporate this endogenous interaction
structure directly into the response.

4.3
Assessing Risk Exposure
To evaluate sector-specific sensitivity to market turbulence , both from oil price shocks
and periods of heightened uncertainty, the tail behavior of sector returns across different
market regimes are examined. This is done by using Extreme Value Theory (EVT) to
model extreme losses and Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) to quantify the expected
shortfall in the negative tail of the return distribution. Together, these methods provide
a more targeted understanding of sector risk exposure during rare but impactful events.
4.3.1
Extreme Value Theory
Extreme Value Theory (EVT) is used to model the behavior of the extreme tail returns,
which are especially relevant for understanding downside risk during periods of financial
stress. Since financial markets frequently experience large, irregular fluctuations, EVT
offers a robust way to capture the magnitude and likelihood of rare sector losses.
Within EVT, the Peaks Over Threshold (POT) method is applied, which focuses
only on return observations that exceed a high threshold, rather than relying on block
maxima (the worst return in fixed intervals). The POT approach is preferred in financial
contexts because it makes more eﬀicient use of extreme observations and better handles
clustered tail events, which are common in financial time series [45].

4.4
Predicting Sector Returns
To build on the relationships identified between oil price changes, market uncertainty,
and sector returns, this section evaluates whether these variables can be used for return
forecasting under varying market conditions. The goal is to test whether the observed
relationships contain meaningful predictive information that can be leveraged to improve
return forecasts in practice.
Given the complexity and nonlinearity of financial markets, machine learning (ML)
methods offer a promising alternative to traditional econometric models. ML approaches
are capable of capturing nonlinear interactions, high-dimensional dependencies, and
time-varying dynamics that standard models may miss. Specifically, this thesis uses
eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), which is a scalable and eﬀicient implementation
of gradient boosting known for its strong performance in structured prediction tasks.
To test the practical value of the XGBoost forecasts, the predictions are translated
into a simple directional trading strategy and evaluated against a passive Buy & Hold
benchmark. This comparison provides insight into whether incorporating oil and volatil-
ity indicators leads not only to more accurate forecasts, but also to more profitable and
robust investment decisions.
Model performance is evaluated using standard error metrics such as Mean Absolute
Error (MAE) and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), along with return-based metrics
derived from the trading strategy. This framework helps assess both statistical accuracy
and economic relevance in a real-world forecasting context.
4.4.1
XGBoost
XGBoost is an advanced gradient boosting framework designed for speed and perfor-
mance, making it well suited for structured financial data. It is based on the gradient
boosting algorithm, where a sequence of weak learners (decision trees) is trained to
correct the residual errors of previous models.
The key idea behind gradient boosting is to fit new models to the residuals (also called
pseudo-residuals) of prior predictions, thereby improving performance by focusing on the
observations that the model previously handled poorly. This iterative correction process
allows the ensemble to progressively reduce prediction error.
The algorithm starts with a simple model, typically predicting the mean of the target
variable Y . In each iteration, it computes the residuals, which is the difference between
the true values and the current predictions, and fits a new decision tree to these residuals.

scaling controlled by a learning rate to prevent overfitting. This process is repeated until

4.4.1.2
Model Input Feature Selection
To reduce overfitting and improve interpretability, a two-stage approach is applied to
identify and evaluate the most informative features. This process combines Random
Forest feature importance scores for initial screening with SHapley Additive exPlanations
(SHAP) for post-training interpretation of the XGBoost model.
Before training the XGBoost models, feature selection is performed using a Random
Forest Regressor.
Feature importance in Random Forests is measured by the total
reduction in root mean squared error (RMSE) attributed to each feature across all
decision trees in the ensemble. Features that lead to greater reductions in prediction
error when used in splits are ranked more highly. The importance score for each feature
is computed as the total gain in accuracy across all trees and normalized to sum to
100%. This method provides a fast and intuitive way to identify which predictors carry
the most explanatory power
To avoid overfitting and reduce redundancy in highly correlated time series features,
only the top features accounting for 90% of cumulative importance are kept.
This
threshold balances model complexity and predictive performance by focusing on variables
that contribute most to reducing forecast error. In the context of time series data with
multiple lagged variables, this also helps mitigate the risk of feature dilution, where
similar predictors compete for importance and artificially expand the feature space. The
Random Forest is fit using 100 trees to ensure stability in the ranking process [50].
Once the XGBoost model is trained, SHAP values are used to interpret how each
selected feature influences the return forecasts. SHAP assigns an additive contribution
score to each feature for every individual prediction, indicating how much that feature
increased or decreased the forecast relative to the model’s expected output.
SHAP values are grounded in cooperative game theory, where the contribution of
each feature is distributed based on its marginal impact across all possible feature com-
binations. In addition to identifying average importance, SHAP also reveals how the
influence of a feature can vary depending on its value, observation, or market regime.
For example, a variable might raise predicted returns in one context and lower them in
another.
This level of interpretability is especially valuable in financial forecasting, where
relationships between variables and returns can shift significantly under different market
conditions. SHAP helps uncover which features matter most, how they affect predictions,
and whether their impact is stable or context-dependent [50].

where y is the value of the objective function and y∗is a performance threshold [51].
To ensure robust evaluation and avoid lookahead bias, a five-fold time-series cross-
validation is used during tuning.
Unlike standard cross-validation, which randomly
splits the data, time-series cross-validation preserves the chronological order by training
on earlier periods and validating on later ones.
At each fold, the model is trained
on historical data and tested on future data, closely reflecting a real-world forecasting
scenario.
This structure also prevents data leakage from future values entering the
training process [52]. For each trial, Optuna evaluates the average Root Mean Squared
Error (RMSE) across the validation folds. An illustration of this setup is provided in
Figure A.1 in Appendix A.

4.5
Forecast Evaluation and Strategy
Implementation
After tuning and training the final XGBoost models for each sector, their predictive
performance is evaluated using both statistical accuracy metrics and a simple trading
strategy to assess practical relevance.
Model accuracy is assessed using standard error metrics, Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE). MAE provides an average of the absolute dif-
ferences between the predicted and actual returns, offering an interpretable measure of
forecast deviation. RMSE, which penalizes larger errors more heavily due to squaring, is
used to capture how well the model handles volatility and large return swings. Together,
these metrics provide a balanced view of overall model performance.
Rather than applying traditional portfolio optimization frameworks such as mean-
variance analysis, this thesis adopts a model-driven trading strategy approach. Since
the forecasting models are trained separately for each sector and are designed to predict
daily directional returns, the focus is not on optimizing allocations across a diversified
portfolio. Instead, the objective is to evaluate whether the model’s predictions can be
translated into actionable investment decisions for each sector individually.
To test this, two simple trading strategies are constructed and benchmarked against
a passive Buy & Hold approach to evaluate whether the model adds value beyond re-
maining fully invested.
The first strategy follows a straightforward daily signal rule: if the model predicts
a positive log return for the following day, a long position is taken in the sector. If
the predicted return is negative or zero, the position is exited and the portfolio remains
uninvested. This binary rule reflects a tactical, signal-based strategy that reacts solely
to the model output, without introducing further complexity such as position sizing or
leverage.
==PAGE:45==
4.5 Forecast Evaluation and Strategy Implementation
33
The second strategy introduces a conditional overlay based on market uncertainty.
Recognizing that extreme volatility may affect the predictive relationships captured by
the model, the signal-following rule is only activated when the VIX exceeds a threshold
of 40, signaling heightened market stress. When the VIX is below this threshold, the
strategy defaults to a passive Buy & Hold position. This hybrid setup is designed to
limit unnecessary trading during stable periods, while preserving model responsiveness
during volatile regimes.
For both strategies, it is assumed that no transaction costs are incurred, and short
selling is not permitted for simplicity. These assumptions are made to isolate the per-
formance that is purely attributed to the forecasting signals and avoid introducing ad-
ditional factors that could introduce noise to the evaluation.
4.5.1
Evaluation of Strategy Performance
The returns of the three strategies are compared over a multi-year evaluation period. To
assess performance, several standard financial metrics are calculated for each strategy,
including total return, annualized return, and annualized standard deviation of returns.
Total return reflects cumulative performance over the full sample period, while an-
nualized return offers a normalized measure of yearly profitability.
The annualized
standard deviation captures the volatility of returns and serves as a proxy for risk, help-
ing assess whether the model-based strategies reduce return volatility compared to the
benchmark.

5.3
Stationarity Testing
One of the most critical tests employed in this thesis to examine the relationship between
oil price changes, VIX levels, and sector returns is the Granger causality test. This test
assumes that the time-series under investigation are stationary. A time-series is consid-
ered stationary if its mean, variance, and autocorrelation structure remain constant over
time [57].
In general, financial prices are non-stationary due to trends, seasonality, and struc-
tural shifts.
The average yearly return is typically positive, leading to exponential
changes over time. Additionally, financial crises and macroeconomic shocks introduce
seasonal effects and volatility clustering, especially in financial instruments and the VIX.
This non-stationarity can be observed in the time series of closing prices for the sectors
and oil prices, as shown in Figure A.3, in Appendix A.
Ensuring stationarity is essential for accurate modeling and inference, as it helps
prevent spurious relationships that may arise due to trends or structural changes rather
than genuine economic interactions. Financial data frequently exhibits trends, volatil-
ity clustering, and cyclical behavior, requiring methods such as differencing, seasonal
adjustment, or rolling window techniques to address non-stationarity [58].
For logarithmic returns, the exponential growth effect is removed, making stationar-
ity tests more applicable, as shown by the time-series in Figure A.4 in Appendix A. Sev-
eral unit root tests are employed, including the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF), Dickey-
Fuller GLS (DF-GLS), Kwiatkowski-Phillips-Schmidt-Shin (KPSS), Phillips-Perron (PP),
and Zivot-Andrews (ZA) tests. A detailed description of the statistical properties and
formulation of each stationarity test is provided in subsubsection A.3.1.2, in Appendix A.
To ensure robustness, multiple tests are applied as they differ in how they handle auto-
correlation, deterministic trends, and potential structural breaks in the data.
A unit root generally indicates non-stationarity, as it suggests that the mean and
variance of the series may change over time [59]. The results of these stationarity tests,
presented in Table 5.3, indicate that all tests suggest no unit root for each variable,
implying that differencing is not needed to achieve stationarity.
Given the nature of the data, additional verification is conducted using rolling mean
and variance plots, which provide further insight into time-varying behavior. The rolling
means and variances of the time series reveal noticeable fluctuations, with the VIX
level showing particularly significant variation over time due to its role as a volatility
measure. These findings are supported by autocorrelation functions (ACF) in Figure A.8,
Appendix A, where VIX exhibits a slow decay in autocorrelation, another indication of
non-stationarity [57].

they may not be suitable for this dataset. The time-varying nature of the relationships

6.3
Generalized Impulse Response Functions
To complement the Granger causality analysis, this section presents the sectoral reac-
tions to shocks in WTI and VIX using Generalized Impulse Response Functions (GIRFs).
While Granger causality addresses whether a predictive relationship exists, GIRFs pro-
vide insight into the magnitude and direction of that relationship over time following a
shock with a magnitude equal to 1 standard deviation.
Figure 6.5 shows the GIRFs for each sector. Following a shock to WTI, both the
Health Care and Consumer Discretionary sectors initially decline slightly, rebound after
a few days, and then gradually converge toward zero around day six. Information Tech-
nology, in contrast, responds with an initial increase, followed by minor dips around
days four and six, and then similarly converges toward baseline. As seen in the cumu-
lative responses (reported in Figure B.5 in Appendix B), the overall cumulative effect
remains negative for Health Care, while the other sectors show neutral to slightly positive
cumulative responses.
For VIX shocks, all sectors exhibit an immediate upward reaction, followed by a
reversal in the days after. However, the scale of the VIX responses is notably smaller
than that of the WTI shocks. Among the sectors, Information Technology shows the
largest initial reaction, approximately double the magnitude of the other two. Both
Health Care and Consumer Discretionary respond similarly.
Bootstrapped confidence intervals were constructed using 300 Monte Carlo replica-
tions. While the bands are generally wide, especially beyond the initial shock days, the
early responses for most sectors lie fully outside the confidence bounds of zero, which
provides directional insights. As the response horizon increases, the intervals gradually
tighten, which indicates that variation decreases and responses converge toward zero
with less fluctuation.

ξ, shown in Figure 6.8.
Information Technology consistently shows higher ξ values,
suggesting more extreme tail behavior.
Interestingly, the shape parameter decreases across all sectors during high-uncertainty
periods.
This might seem counterintuitive, since crises are typically associated with
greater downside risk. However, this decline reflects a flatter tail shape, which means
that losses become more dispersed across the tail, rather than tightly clustered near the
threshold.

visualized in Figure 6.9, where the empirical cumulative distribution functions (ECDFs)
under high uncertainty flatten, indicating wider and more dispersed tails compared to

6.6
Crisis Zoom-In
The previous sections demonstrated that sector sensitivities to oil price changes and
market volatility vary notably across different market regimes. Not only does the pre-
dictive relationship between WTI/VIX and sector returns shift under uncertainty, but
sector return behavior itself differs considerably. To further explore whether these ef-
fects are consistent across time or specific to certain types of crises, this section zooms
in on selected market crises. The goal is to examine whether the nature or timing of a
crisis affects both the predictability of sector returns and the sector-specific responses
to shocks.
6.6.1
Rolling Granger Causality
In the baseline analysis, a 252-day (one-year) rolling window was used to maintain a
balance between capturing time-varying relationships and preserving the assumption of
local stationarity. However, during periods of crisis, where market dynamics can shift
rapidly, a shorter window is more appropriate to better capture short-term structural
changes. For this reason, a 63-day (three-month) rolling Granger causality is employed
for the crisis zoom-ins, allowing a more precise view of evolving relationships with mini-
mal noise from unrelated market movements.

6.7
Forecasting Sector Log-Returns
Having established that oil price changes (WTI) and market uncertainty (VIX) exert
significant and time-varying influences on sector returns, and that this is especially
the case during crisis periods, it becomes evident that these variables carry predictive
information for future values.
The results from the Granger causality tests, the evolving risk structures identified
through EVT and CVaR, and the sector-specific sensitivities observed across different
regimes all suggest that sector returns are not only reactive but may also exhibit elements
of predictability if these signals are captured effectively.
This motivates the second core research question, whether these predictive insights
can be translated into forecasting models that account for changing market regimes and
improve predictive accuracy, thereby offering actionable information for investors and
policymakers.

Discussion
7.1
Interpreting Sectoral Sensitivities to
Macroeconomic Drivers
The analysis explored the dynamic relationship between oil price changes (WTI), market
uncertainty (VIX), and sector-specific stock returns, focusing on how these relationships
evolve across different market regimes and during major financial crises. The results
clearly show that these relationships are both time-varying and sector-dependent, in-
dicating that macroeconomic variables provide predictive power primarily in unstable
market conditions rather than consistently across time.
The negative relationship between the VIX and sector returns is persistent through-
out the sample and becomes particularly pronounced during periods of heightened uncer-
tainty. This reinforces the VIX’s role as a key indicator of market sentiment and risk aver-
sion. The strength of this relationship during crisis periods also reflects well-documented
investor behavior, where uncertainty leads to heightened sensitivity to macroeconomic
news and increased reliance on external signals when making investment decisions. Dur-
ing such times, even relatively small shocks, such as moderate oil price fluctuations, can
lead to outsized market reactions as investors search for any additional confirmation of
perceived market distress. As shown in the rolling correlation analysis, the VIX main-
tained a strong negative correlation with all three sectors, which deepened during crisis
periods like COVID-19 and the GFC.
The role of change in oil price in driving sector returns has evolved substantially
over the sample period. Before the Global Financial Crisis (GFC), WTI shocks showed
weak and often negative correlations with sector returns. This pattern reflected the tra-
ditional view of oil as a production input and cost factor, where rising oil prices signaled
increasing business costs and potential downward pressure on corporate profitability.
However, this relationship fundamentally shifted after the GFC, when rising oil prices
increasingly coincided with higher sector returns. This reversal is closely linked to the
financialization of oil markets. The results in the correlation and Granger causality sec-
tions highlight this change, where WTI only became a statistically significant predictor
of returns during and following the GFC, especially for Information Technology and
Consumer Discretionary.
This shift is often attributed to the financialization of oil markets. The launch of the
first commodity ETFs in 2003 allowed oil to be accessed as a financial asset and not just
==PAGE:83==
7.2 Regime Dependency and Breakdown of Stable Relationships
71
a physical commodity [65]. As oil became a component of diversified portfolios, its price
dynamics began to reflect not just supply and demand but also broader macroeconomic
sentiment. The financial crisis accelerated this integration. Equity market losses during
the GFC prompted investors to shift capital into commodities to hedge risk [66], while
the Federal Reserve’s Quantitative Easing (QE) programs injected liquidity and lowered
interest rates, driving further flows into assets such as oil [67, 68]. The U.S. Treasury’s
Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP) [69], launched alongside QE1, further stabilized
financial markets and reinforced confidence in financial instruments. The combined effect
of these policies coincided with a sharp drop in Granger p-values across all sectors, as
shown in the results chapter. This timing suggests that policy interventions not only
supported markets directly but also enhanced the predictive link between oil, volatility,
and returns.
The impulse responses suggest an asymmetry in how WTI and VIX shocks transmit
to sector returns. Shocks to oil prices tend to generate a larger and more persistent
return response compared to VIX shocks, which on first glance is contrary to all of the
other significance metrics conducted. One possible explanation is that WTI shocks may
indirectly trigger movements in the VIX, which amplifies the overall effect on returns
through a combined oil–volatility channel. On the other hand, VIX shocks appear to
have a more contained effect, potentially because they do not influence oil prices in the
same way. Additionally, a one-standard-deviation shock to the VIX may not reflect the
same magnitude of market stress as a one-standard-deviation shock to the WTI prices,
making its standalone impact appear less pronounced in the average response.
7.2
Regime Dependency and Breakdown of
Stable Relationships
The results from the Granger causality and forecasting analyses confirm that predic-
tive relationships between WTI, VIX, and sector returns are highly regime-dependent,
becoming statistically significant primarily during periods of market turmoil.
Crises
act as amplifiers of macroeconomic shocks, creating environments where market partic-
ipants rely heavily on macro-financial indicators and react disproportionately to new
information.
This pattern holds across a range of crises, not just COVID-19. During the Global
Financial Crisis, rolling Granger causality became significant for Consumer Discretionary
and Health Care as early as late 2007, suggesting early sensitivity to macroeconomic
disruption. Later during the COVID-19 pandemic, Granger causality reached its highest
and most persistent significance across all sectors, especially around March 15, 2020,
when large-scale lockdowns and fiscal interventions like the $2 trillion QE package were
introduced [70].
The GIRF results further reinforce the crisis-specific nature of market responses. Dur-
==PAGE:84==
7.3 Risk Asymmetries and Sectoral Tail Behavior
72
ing COVID-19, sector responses to WTI and VIX shocks displayed volatility rebounds
and oscillations not observed in other crises. These unique patterns point to the impact
of the rapidly shifting policies and unique uncertainty during the pandemic. In contrast,
other crises such as the Asian Financial Crisis or the China Market Crash triggered more
stable and uniform GIRF trajectories.
The 2025 tariff crisis presents a useful counterexample. When forecasting, the model-
based strategy was able to mitigate losses while the hybrid strategy, triggered only by
extreme VIX levels, failed. This suggests that regime shifts do not always coincide with
high VIX values. Instead, transitions in volatility regimes and short-lived events can be
missed by static thresholds. This underlines the value of predictive models that consider
broader volatility patterns, such as the volatility of the VIX itself, changes in its slope,
or rolling averages.
7.3
Risk Asymmetries and Sectoral Tail
Behavior
The Extreme Value Theory (EVT) and Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) analyses re-
vealed clear asymmetries in downside risk across sectors. Information Technology con-
sistently displayed the heaviest left-tail risk, especially during high-uncertainty regimes,
confirming its greater exposure to sharp market corrections. Health Care, on the other
hand, remained more resilient throughout the sample period, with thinner tails and
lower expected shortfall estimates, even under stressed market conditions.
During crisis periods, the EVT shape parameter ξ consistently declined, while the
scale parameter β increased. This indicates that although extreme losses become less
frequent relative to calm periods, the magnitude of these losses grows substantially
larger when they do occur. This behavior underlines why traditional risk management
approaches, which often focus solely on loss frequency, may underestimate the true sever-
ity of crisis-period risks. Instead, investors should place greater emphasis on managing
the potential magnitude of losses, particularly when market volatility is elevated.
The evolution of sectoral tail behavior also aligns with the broader financialization
of markets. As oil and other commodities became increasingly integrated into financial
asset allocations, cross-market spillovers intensified. Especially during crises, this created
more pronounced co-movements between commodities and financial markets, consistent
with the spillover effects described by [25]. These effects were most significant in growth-
oriented sectors such as Information Technology and Consumer Discretionary, which are
more sensitive to macroeconomic conditions and investor sentiment.

7.4
Forecasting Implications and Model

The predictive modeling results further highlight the regime-dependent nature of macro-
financial relationships. The XGBoost models performed well during crisis periods, ac-
curately predicting both the direction and magnitude of returns during the COVID-19
pandemic and recognized the most volatile days in the 2025 tariff crisis. However, in
more stable periods, the models tended to predict flatter returns, successfully capturing
the correct direction but struggling to forecast the magnitude of market movements.
This suggests that during calm market phases, returns become increasingly driven by
idiosyncratic factors rather than macroeconomic indicators, reducing the effectiveness
of predictive models based mainly on macro-financial variables. These dynamics were
especially evident for the Information Technology sector in 2024, when Buy & Hold
outperformed the model-based strategy due to an uptrend that lacked macroeconomic
volatility.
From a practical investment perspective, these findings suggest that such models
are better suited for crisis management rather than as continuous alternatives to pas-
sive investment strategies. Investors would likely benefit from applying these models
selectively during periods of rising uncertainty, allowing them to mitigate downside risk
during crises while still participating in long-term market recoveries during stable peri-
ods. Another practical consideration is transaction costs. The hybrid strategy, which
trades far less frequently than the daily ML model, is likely more robust once such costs
are introduced. Although the ML model showed superior loss mitigation in many crisis
windows, the hybrid’s more selective engagement may offer greater long-term sustain-
ability for real-world applications.
The hybrid strategy based on a simple VIX threshold demonstrated success during
the COVID-19 pandemic, but ultimately failed to protect against losses during the
2025 tariff crisis. The failure to prevent losses in 2025 emphasizes the need for more
sophisticated activation mechanisms. A more effective approach would incorporate not
only the absolute level of the VIX but also its volatility over recent periods, changes in its
slope, and the persistence of elevated uncertainty. These enhancements would improve
the model’s ability to identify regime shifts and provide more timely crisis response
signals.
Model performance also varied across sectors. Health Care achieved the lowest MAE
and RMSE values and the most stable return forecasts. Information Technology, despite
offering higher upside, had the largest errors, consistent with its greater volatility and
tail risk.
Consumer Discretionary fell in between, with moderate predictability and
strategy performance.

Conclusion
This thesis examined the relationship between oil price fluctuations (WTI), market un-
certainty (VIX), and sector-specific stock returns, with a focus on how these relation-
ships evolve under different market regimes and crisis conditions. Using a combination
of econometric techniques and machine learning, the analysis provided both theoretical
and empirical contributions toward understanding the macro-financial drivers of sectoral
return dynamics.
The findings demonstrated that the influence of oil and volatility on sector returns is
both time-varying and regime-dependent. During periods of elevated market stress, such
as the Global Financial Crisis (GFC) and the COVID-19 pandemic, WTI and VIX re-
vealed significant Granger-causal relationships with sector returns, answering Research
Questions 1 and 2. These effects were more pronounced post-GFC, consistent with the
broader financialization of oil as a macro-financial asset. Sector sensitivities were not
uniform, with Consumer Discretionary and Information Technology being more respon-
sive, while Health Care remained comparatively stable, addressing Research Question
2.
The rolling Granger causality and impulse response results supported the idea that
macroeconomic shocks, especially oil price movements, influence sectors asymmetrically.
WTI shocks often triggered larger and more persistent effects than VIX shocks, and
their predictive power increased in high-volatility regimes, confirming that extreme oil
movements and uncertainty jointly inform market dynamics thus answering Research
Question 4.
These results also offer insight into how perceptions of future uncertainty, captured
by the VIX, interact with oil-driven return dynamics, addressing Research Question
3. The impulse response analysis shows that the impact of oil price shocks becomes
stronger when market uncertainty is elevated, and Granger causality results reveal that
VIX often enhances the predictability of returns when combined with WTI, particularly
during crisis regimes. This interaction effect reinforces the idea that oil and volatility
jointly shape investor expectations, especially in turbulent markets. This was also seen
when comparing the return behavior during different types of oil changes for varying
uncertainty regimes.
Motivated by these empirical findings, the thesis proceeded to evaluate whether
such macro-financial signals could be leveraged to forecast sector returns. To this end,
XGBoost-based models were developed using a selected set of macroeconomic features.

These models performed especially well during volatile periods, offering both directional
accuracy and meaningful loss mitigation.
The COVID-19 crisis, in particular, high-

All three sector-specific models not only avoided

These results strongly support the thesis’ final Research Question 5, showing that oil

Altogether, this thesis answered each of its core Research Questions and demon-
strated that oil prices and market volatility influence sectoral return behavior in complex
and evolving ways. These relationships can be meaningfully captured and translated into
forecasting models, especially for periods of financial stress.
These findings also carry several practical implications. For investors and fund man-
agers, the sectoral differences in sensitivity to WTI and VIX, both across crisis types
and under varying volatility regimes, imply that portfolios should be monitored and ad-
justed dynamically. Static allocation or entirely passive strategies may fall short during
periods of rising macroeconomic risk. Regardless of the level of risk aversion, the hybrid
strategy introduced in the thesis offers a practical and flexible alternative. It provides a
practical way to combine predictive insights with market exposure and showed promise
for improving risk-adjusted returns across all three sectors of varying volatility.
Hedge funds with exposure to cyclical sectors may find value in using macroeconomic
financial indicators as early warning signals to rebalance or hedge positions more proac-
tively. The observed lag patterns between WTI, VIX and returns suggest that these
variables can be useful not only for forecasting returns but also for anticipating shifts in
market regimes. From a policy perspective, the heightened sensitivity of growth-oriented
sectors like Information Technology to macroeconomic shocks suggests that such sectors
may require targeted support during global crises, especially given their importance to
innovation and long-term economic growth. Additionally, the increasing role of oil as
a financial asset highlights the need to monitor commodity markets more closely when
assessing financial stability. Especially when designing policies that address systemic
risk, where oil is used for hedging during market stress.

Future Work
The findings open several routes for further research. First, the linear Granger causality
and impulse response methods used here may be too restrictive given the likely nonlinear
nature of macroeconomic relationships.
Applying nonlinear methods, such as those
based on Taylor approximations [25], could result in more accurate detection of predictive
dynamics. This is especially important for impulse response functions, where the current
linear framework cannot differentiate between positive and negative shocks.
For more detailed Granger causality, the rolling window approach could be refined.
Shorter windows may capture crisis dynamics more precisely, especially for identifying
short-lived but intense shifts in predictive relationships. Similarly, a deeper analysis of
the individual contributions of WTI and VIX to causality during crisis periods would
provide more detailed insight into their respective roles. Here, the Granger-Causality
models could also benefit from additional macroeconomic controlling variables. Incor-
porating interest rates or inflation expectations, for example, may help further isolating
the effects of the market uncertainty and oil price changes.
To better simulate real-world implementation, transaction costs should be integrated
into strategy evaluation. Since the ML-based model trades frequently, this is especially
important for comparing it against the more conservative hybrid strategy and the Buy
& Hold benchmark, which is completely passive. In this context, more sophisticated
activation rules could be explored. Rather than relying on a static VIX threshold, future
models could incorporate the trajectory, or volatility of the VIX to identify transition
periods more effectively.
Finally, while the forecasting model showed clear promise, more advanced methods,
such as neural networks, could be tested using the same input features to assess whether
higher returns can be achieved. Additionally, the model could be expanded to forecast
returns not just at the sector index level but for individual stocks within each sector.

A.3.1.2
Stationarity Testing: Unit Root Tests
To robustly assess stationarity, several unit root tests are employed, each with comple-
mentary strengths [71]:
• Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test: Tests the null hypothesis of a unit
root against the alternative of stationarity by estimating the following regression:
∆yt = α + βt + γyt−1 +
p
∑
i=1
δi∆yt−i + εt,
(A.2)
where ∆yt is the first difference of yt, t is a time trend, p is the number of lags and
ϵt is a white noise error term. ∑p
i=1 δi∆yt−i are the lagged differences. Rejection
of the null hypothesis (γ = 0) indicates stationarity [72].
==PAGE:95==
A.3 Preliminary Analysis
83
• Dickey-Fuller Generalized Least Squares (DF-GLS) Test: A modified ver-
sion of the ADF test that first applies a Generalized Least Squares (GLS) detrend-
ing procedure to improve power against stationary alternatives, particularly in the
presence of deterministic trends.
• Phillips-Perron (PP) Test: Also tests the null hypothesis of a unit root, but
corrects for serial correlation and heteroskedasticity in the error terms using non-
parametric methods, without adding lagged difference terms. The statistic adjusts
the standard ADF test to control for autocorrelation.
• Kwiatkowski-Phillips-Schmidt-Shin (KPSS) Test: Tests the null hypothesis
of stationarity against the alternative of a unit root. It decomposes the series
into a deterministic trend, a random walk, and a stationary error component: A
significant KPSS statistic suggests non-stationarity [73].
• Zivot-Andrews (ZA) Test: Extends the ADF framework by allowing for a
one-time structural break in either the intercept, the trend, or both.
This is
particularly relevant for financial data covering major crisis periods, such as the
Global Financial Crisis or COVID-19, where structural changes are expected.
