Summary
Agile development have proven to lower the project failure rate in software
development. However, some projects are not suitable for agile development
processes. These projects must follow other Software Development Life Cycle
(SDLC) models or even follow modiﬁed SDLC models. According to statistics
these projects are more likely to fail.
This thesis aims at reducing the project failure rate in projects not feasible
to follow agile development. This is achieved by introducing a hybrid SDLC
model, which combines principles from existing SDLC models. Furthermore,
this SDLC model is designed for complex projects developed in small-medium
development teams. The purpose of the developed SDLC model is to reduce the
Customer-Developer gap. The Customer-Developer gap is a concept introduced
in this thesis and refers to artifacts and participants of the development process
diverging from each other. A Customer-Developer gap is an indicator of issues
emerging in the development process that potentially leads to project failure.
This thesis introduces a SDLC model that combines principles from Scrum, the
Waterfall model, Prototyping model, Parallel Agile, Domain-Driven Design and
Model-Based Software Engineering. The developed SDLC model uses prototyp-
ing based on domain modelling and code generation from the domain models.
Furthermore, the developed SDLC model follows a design ﬁrst principle. This
is done by the behavioral domain being designed as control ﬂow and manually
written code covers speciﬁc behavior. Additionally, a software architecture pat-
tern is introduced to separate the source code in requirements, generated code
and manually written code. Finally, a proof of concept tool is introduced to
enable development processes to follow the developed SDLC model.
==PAGE:4==
ii
The developed SDLC model is validated through a case study and two tech-
shares. The case study and tech-shares are used to validate that the developed
SDLC model and software architecture pattern contribute to addressing the
seven most common issues leading to project failure.
Furthermore, the case
study also shows that it is feasible to build a tool that enable developments
team to follow the developed SDLC model. However, empirical evidence must
be collected to validate how projects perform when following the developed
SDLC model.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In IT projects, the project failure rate has decreased over the recent years. One
reason for this reduction of failure may be that agile development processes are
becoming more widely adopted. In the 9th global project management survey
PULSE of the PROFESSION made by the Project Management Institute (PMI)
[PMI17], 31% of the projects do not meet the goals of the project and 14%
end in failure. According to the CHAOS Report 2018 by the Standish Group,
projects utilising Agile models are two times more likely to succeed compared
to projects following the Waterfall model. However, following agile models is
not always feasible. The survey from PMI states that, 21% of the companies
uses agile models and 20% using hybrid agile models. Half of the respondents
that indicate they use agile methods are in fact using hybrid agile methods.
It indicates that even though respondents indicate they are agile, they may
in fact utilise other SDLC models. This fact indicates that agile models are
in fact not suitable for all types of projects and organisations. For instance,
for IT consultancy companies it is common that customers request an up-front
cost estimate of the development process. This implies that the development
process is not suited for agile development. Instead, the Waterfall models would
be better to accommodate such requests. This is also indicated in the survey
that shows that 37% of companies use the Waterfall model.
According to the survey, the seven main reasons for project failure are: inade-
quate communication, requirement misunderstanding, poor management, inac-

curate cost estimates, inadequate project vision and too many changes in both
organisation priorities and objectives. The inadequate communication relates to
both the internal communication among the development team and the com-
munication between development team and customers.
Often the customers
and the development team do not share the same idea of a requirement, which
leads to requirement misunderstandings.
Inadequate communication between
the customer and development team is often the reason for requirement misun-
derstandings.
These reasons for project failure are indicated by a gap between the customer’s
expectations of the software system and the software system actually delivered.
This gap is referred to as the Customer-Developer gap. In the development
process of a project, the Customer-Developer gap may increase, which may
ultimately lead to the project failing in the end of the development process.
An example of the Customer-Developer gap increasing during the development
includes that system models and implementations of a system diverge. This
might be an indication of inadequate communication and misunderstanding of
requirements. Hence, we must reduce the Customer-Developer gap throughout
the development process to reduce the risk of project failure.
To minimise the Customer-Developer gap, this thesis introduces a hybrid SDLC
model.
Furthermore, the hybrid SDLC model aims at projects that are not
suited to follow agile models. The developed SDLC model follows a principle
of tying requirements, domain modelling and implementation closer together.
This principle is followed to reduce the Customer-Developer gap. The devel-
oped SDLC model in this thesis combines principles from existing approaches
and methodologies. Additionally, the developed SDLC model combines princi-
ples from Scrum, the Waterfall model, the Prototyping model, Parallel Agile,
Domain-Driven Design (DDD) and Model-Based Software Engineering (MBSE).
To enforce that the developed SDLC model follows these principles, a Design
First approach is taken. This is done by modelling structural and behavioral
domains. In the structural modelling, the developed SDLC model follows DDD
principles. According to Eric Evans [Eva02], DDD principles makes it easier to
comprehend complex domains and understand the customer requirements. The
behavioral domain modelling in the developed SDLC model focuses on control
ﬂow, whereas speciﬁc functionality and integration with the structural domain
is handled in manually written code. Hence, the domain models are not only
illustrative but are used in the software system.
The developed SDLC model follows MBSE principles and states that the domain
models should be generated to code. Hence, the code that covers the behavior of
the system is a combination of generated code of the control ﬂow and manually
written code.
==PAGE:19==
3
The developed SDLC model is divided into phases. Each phase deﬁnes how the
development of a speciﬁc stage of the development process is to be carried out. In
each phase, the SDLC model deﬁnes the roles that are intended to participate in
the given phase. A role describes a speciﬁc participant’s responsibilities during a
phase, i.e a role in the SDLC model could be the project owner who is responsible
for conveying the expectations and needs of the customer or the developer that
is responsible for the implementation of the software system.
During each phase, artifacts are produced that contribute to the later phases
of the developed SDLC model. All artifacts produced by the developed SDLC
model are of virtual nature, so these can act as a source of truth throughout the
software development process. These artifacts are mutable but are encouraged
to change as the development process evolves.
It is not feasible to introduce a SDLC model that ﬁts every type of project.
The SDLC model developed in this thesis, is designed for small-medium size
development teams that develop large and complex systems.
In this thesis, a tool is developed to enable development teams to follow the
developed SDLC model. This tool combines existing technologies to ease the
development and improve the eﬃciency of some phases of the developed SDLC
model. These phases are the ones where domain modelling is carried out. The
tool focuses on enabling development teams to carry out structural and behav-
ioral modelling, including generating code from these models. As code genera-
tion is demanded, the source code of the project is aﬀected. To make a clear
separation between generated code and source code a software architecture pat-
tern is introduced. Furthermore, this pattern separates the implementation of
requirements to increase traceabillity of requirements throughout the develop-
ment process.
The subtitle of this thesis indicates that the developed SDLC model should
follow a combination of Behavior-Driven and Process-Driven Development to
bridge the Customer-Developer gap. However, during the development of the
SDLC model, Domain-Driven Design was determined to be better suited for
tying the requirements, domain modelling and implementation closer together.
The initial vision for this thesis was to use a combination of Behavior-Driven
and Process-Driven development in the developed SDLC. This way, the Process-
Driven development would cover the operational processes through modelling.
This modelling would describe the process with people, applications and data.
The Behavior-Driven development would focus on describing the behavior of
the applications involved in operational processes. However, understanding the
customer’s domain and designing the behavior in models provide more value
than describing the behavior of a system in narratives and acceptance criteria.
Behavior-Driven development is still used in the solution in the the sense that
the system’s behavior is formalised in a shared language.

1.1
Research Methodology
In this thesis, design science is used as a research methodology [JP14]. Design
science describes how research should be carried out. Design science expresses
that initially a knowledge base regarding the topic and problem should be col-
lected. Then the problem should be explicated. The explication should clarify
where and why the problem appears, give a precise description of the problem
and formulate the problem. A set of requirements is selected to deﬁne an appro-
priate solution for the problem. The requirements should be used to evaluate a
solution later. Next, an artefact that fulﬁll the requirements should be created,
which in this thesis is the developed SDLC model and software architecture
pattern. The artefact should be developed through iterative and incremental
steps that sketch, design, develop and verify the artefact. The artefact is then
demonstrated and evaluated from the requirements. In this thesis a running
example will be used for the sketch, design, development and veriﬁcation steps
of design science. For the demonstration and evaluation, the running example
and another case study is used. The other case study adds more complexity to
demonstrate that the artefacts works for real size projects as well.

The contribution of this thesis is divided into three parts. The ﬁrst part con-
tributes with an analysis of models, principles and properties of SDLC. The
analysis focuses on obtaining knowledge of the domain of SDLC. Furthermore,
the analysis contributes to identiﬁcation of principles and techniques usable in
the SDLC model developed.
The second part of the contribution is the SDLC model and software archi-
tecture pattern. The SDLC model elaborates on how the development process
should be carried out. The SDLC model do so by describing the phases, ar-

The last part is the proof-of-concept tool. This tool demonstrates that using a
tool based on domain modelling during the development process contributes to
the developed SDLC model.

2.1
Running example description
The running example is based on a charging-station company deciding to mi-
grate their core charging calculation software system from on-premise to a cloud
solution. The system is responsible for providing calculations for numerous pro-
cesses within the company. The code of the system has no dependencies that
restricts the code from being migrated to the cloud. However, the system was
previously running in a secure local environment. This setup only allowed de-
vices connected on the same local network to interact with the service. Migrat-
ing to the cloud eliminate this option. Because of this, the company requested
an authorisation process to secure their system information from unauthorised
access.
As a part of the initial idea, the company describes the context of the system,
their goals and the set of restrictions. In the following the initial idea is de-
scribed as it was provided by the customer. Note that this is a simulation of a
customer.
"With the migration of our core charging calculation software system our com-
pany’s information will become accessible by unauthorised external parties. In
the current setting it is unnecessary to have an authorisation process, as the
system is only accessible by devices within the local network.
We are forced
to integrate the system with an authorisation mechanism, which allows our em-
ployees to interact with the system. Furthermore, the authorisation should allow
arbitrary applications/services to consume the service.
We need the authorisation within a weeks’ time and have an available budget of
40 hours".
The charging calculation service is responsible for tracking the customer’s con-
sumption of electricity when vehicles are charged. The company uses both a
subscription and pay per use based payment strategy. Both payment strategies
require the system to calculate the customer’s monthly invoices based on their
consumption. Furthermore, the company installs wallboxes (home chargers) at
the customers’ homes. These wallboxes transmit data regarding consumption
and errors. The company uses the data of consumption to analyse the charging
patterns and optimise their revenue based on these patterns. The company uses
the error information for predicting when maintenance of wallboxes and charg-
ing stations are required.
The arbitrary application/services are potentially any application/service that
depends on the data stored in the charging calculation service. Application/ser-
vices like this could include enterprise resource planning (ERP) and customer
relationship management (CRM) systems.

Software engineering
In this section, the ﬁelds of software engineering are covered to build a founda-
tion of this thesis. In the following, the ﬁelds: requirements engineering, system
modelling, software development, software testing and software maintenance are

3.2.1
Requirements engineering
Requirements engineering relates to the process of understanding and deﬁning
requirements in a software project. requirements can be expressed as user re-
quirements or system requirements. A user requirement is a requirement that
describes an expected functionality of the software system from the user (cus-
tomers) point of view. Whereas the system requirements are a detailed descrip-
tion of expected behavior or property of the software system.
The software
development team uses system requirements during development.
The system requirements are often classiﬁed as functional or non-functional.
The functional requirements state how the system should behave in speciﬁc sit-
uation and with speciﬁc inputs. Non-functional requirements on the other hand
describes the constraints on the system. Such constraints could be throughput,
reliability etc.
The requirements engineering includes three steps: elicitation and analysis, spec-
iﬁcation and validation. The ﬁrst step is to understand the customers’ needs
which requires an understanding of how the customer work, the social and orga-
nizational environment of the customer and the customers expectation. There
are multiple techniques to obtain the information such as doing interviews with
the customer, doing workshops or through ethnography where the analyst (de-
velopment team) participates in the customer workday. Often during the elic-
==PAGE:42==
26
Concept and theory
itation and analysis step, the requirements are described through stories and
scenarios.
For the speciﬁcation step, the requirements are documented and prepared as an
artifact for the following SDLC phases. The requirements are documented in
multiple ways such as structured speciﬁcations, natural language speciﬁcations
and use cases.
The natural language speciﬁcations describe requirements in
natural language such as English. The speciﬁcation is vague but expressive and
intuitive for people. Specifying the requirements using a structured language is
structuring the requirement such that there is a common understanding of how
to interpret the requirement stated. Finally, the use case is graphical approach
to describe interaction between the system and users of the system. Along with
use cases, often detailed use cases are used to provide a structured speciﬁcation
of the requirements. Sometimes a software requirement speciﬁcation document
(SRS) is created to describe requirements and what should be implemented.
The SRS acts as an agreement between the customer and development team.
In an agile context, the SRS is often left out as requirements are expected to be
changed.
The veriﬁcation step involves testing and conﬁrming that the speciﬁed require-
ments provides the excepted behavior of the system. Some techniques exist to
validate the speciﬁcation; requirement review, prototyping and test-case gen-
eration. Requirements reviews are where the development team systematically
reviews the requirements. Prototyping is where development of a prototype to
match customer expectation is introduced. Test-case generation is where test
cases are established and tested for the requirements.
3.2.2
System modelling
System modelling is the process of developing models of a software system that
present the system from diﬀerent views. Often in software development, the
business processes of an organisation includes multiple software systems and
human activities, which causes the software systems to cover partial part of a
requirement. Therefore, it is important to determine the context of the software
system. The context can be described through context models to insinuate how
the entire landscape of the organisation software system and business processes
work.
Every software system has at least one type of interaction. That can be user
interaction or system-to-system interaction. To model these interactions, inter-
action models are used. Depending on the type of interaction, sequence diagram
and use case can be used to model the interactions. The use case models de-

interactions between actors and the objects within the system itself. Further-

When designing the system, structural models can be used to illustrate detailed
levels of components and their relation within the system. Class diagrams are
used to show the system’s classes and the relationship between them. Using the
class diagrams the complex system can be well documented to avoid misunder-

Another aspect of software systems are the behaviour of them.
Behavioural

An example of behavioral models are activity diagrams and state machines.

3.2.4
Software testing
In software development, testing is crucial to obtain validation and veriﬁcation
of the system. Testing includes the entire testing done during the development.
This includes manual testing as well as automated testing.
One way to do
testing is unit testing, which focuses on testing diﬀerent units of the system such
as methods, classes and features. Another approach is the component testing,
which tests that the speciﬁcations of the components behave as expected. User
testing is very important as the customer matches the system against their own
expectation.
As testing is a crucial step of software development, the test-driven development
has to be introduced. Test-driven development dictates that writing the test
should occur before implementation and that the implementation should written
code that pass the tests.

3.3
Software development Life Cycle (SDLC)
As software development has increased over the years, the question regarding
optimisation of development processes has arised. From this, the SDLC Method-
ologies emerged, which each try to improve the software development through
metrics like risk mitigation, productivity, eﬃciency etc.
Software Development is the process of building a software system. This process
is divided into multiple stages, which relates to the software engineering ﬁelds.
These stages contributes to the realisation of the software system. There are
seven SDLC stages: idea, requirement analysis, design, implementation, testing,
deployment and maintenance. These are shown in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: SDLC stages.
The idea stage is the stage where the context and goals of the software system
is established and described. The intention of the stage is to get an idea of what
the software system should solve or achieve. The idea stage is often completed
by the customer of the software system.

The requirement analysis is the stage where the idea of software system is anal-
ysed and transformed into requirements.
These requirements should be ad-

fulﬁls the purpose from the idea stage. Furthermore, the requirements are often
formulated in a high-level, structured language such as in the form of detailed
use case. This is done to allow software developers to better understand the
requirements and implement them.
The idea and requirements stage is also
referred to as the planning stage.
The design stage is part of the development team’s responsibility to map the

conducted in this stage. This includes external components (existing software
component) as well as internal components (components development by the
development team).
The implementation stage is the realisation stage of the SDLC. The implementa-

are transformed into source code that constitute the software system. This stage
is often the most time consuming of all the stages.

The deployment stage focuses on the source code being deployed and delivered

The hosting decide could for instance be in cloud or

code such that it can be delivered and executed.
The delivery of this stage

executing it.

delivery, the customer will ﬁnd a couple of desired changes or bugs which needs
to be sorted out. However, rather than preparing a new SDLC the development
team can often correct the issue directly.

3.4.1
Waterfall model
The model is one of the oldest SDLC models and was widely used by software
development teams. The Model belongs to the Plan-driven development pro-
cesses due to intensive planning early in the SDLC. The design of the model
follows the SDLC phases sequentially. Each phase depends on the previous ones
and phases do not overlap. The model does not allow to revisit previous phases.
The focus of the model is improving the code quality of the software system. It
is doing so, through intensive documentation and planning with only customer
involvement in the early phases. The model belongs to the Predictive Planning
since it values planning over development [AB15].
3.4.1.1
Advantages
The phases of the waterfall model are well deﬁned, which makes the model easy
to understand and follow. As a beneﬁt from the design of the model is that it
is simple to manage and provides clarity of the development process.
The model determines the end goal early and designs the whole system before

tributes to the reduction of refactoring costs. However, this requires experienced
developers and architects to be a beneﬁt in practise. [NON20].
3.4.1.2
Disadvantages
The model requires customer interaction only in a few stages namely, Require-
ment phase and testing phase, hence the model depends highly on the require-
ments being well deﬁned and the customer’s and development team’s vision of
software system being identical. As the model have no iterations, it relies on
getting the system right the ﬁrst time. The need of rework can be quite costly
as it might entail redoing the entire development process.
Since phases are completed sequentially and with no iterations the model only
produces working software at the end of the SDLC which entails a late time-to-
market. Meaning late validation of the software system [NON20].
Many practitioners and experts argue that the waterfall model is not a good ap-
proach for software development such as Martin Fowler. Martin Fowler objects
to use of the waterfall model saying that the model introduces a great deal of
risk within the development. This is due to diﬃculties in predicting elements
within the testing and implementation phases that lead to rework [Fow19].

Iterative Model

team continues to specify and implement improved versions of the software sys-

each iteration, all the SDLC phases are sequentially completed. The model fo-

on the design and implementation fulﬁlling requirements [T B13].
3.4.2.1
Advantages
The Iterative Model brings great advantages such as ﬁnding defects early in
the development process. Value is produced early in the development process as
quick and continuous release cycles. Furthermore, the model integrates customer
feedback into the iterations, which provides the opportunity to adapt to changes.

3.4.4
Agile Models
In fast-moving business environment, the Plan-driven software development pro-
cesses can cause problems. In such business environments the requirements are
likely to change during the development of the software system, leaving the de-
velopment team to rework the software system. Therefore, using for instance
the waterfall model in such a development process, might be costly. The need
for rapid software development and changeable requirements during the soft-
ware development gave rise to the Agile Development through the development
of agile models such as Scrum. The agile models follow the agile manifest that
describes the prioritised values of an agile model:
• Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
• Working software over comprehensive documentation
• Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
• Responding to change over following a plan
The agile model relies on customer communication and engagement rather than
documentation and planning [al01].
The agile models have proved to be particularly successful in software develop-
ment of small to medium sized software systems and in-house software develop-
ment of software systems [Som16].
In the following the agile model, Scrum is described.
3.4.4.1
Scrum
The scrum model addresses the scaling of agile development to be used in de-
velopment of complex software system.
The roles in scrum are the product owner, scrum master and development team
member [scr]. The intention of the roles is to have a clear segregation of respon-
sibilities within the software development.
The scrum master acts as the leader and facilitator by helping the team to
overcome decisions and keeping them on track. The product owner responsibility
is to convey the interest of the software system to the Scrum team. Lastly, the
scrum team members are responsible for development of each product backlog
[Drn08].

3.4 SDLC models
35
The artifacts in scrum are divided into product backlog, sprint backlog and
increment.
The Product Backlog is a list of requirements that needs to be
developed. It can be altered within each sprint. From the product backlog, a
subset of requirement is selected for each sprint which is the sprint backlog. The
sprint backlog is the outcome of the sprint planning event. Each sprint release
code at the end, which is the increment artifact that contributes to increment
the software system [Drn08].
Scrum consists of ﬁve phases: sprint, sprint planning, daily scrum, sprint re-
view and sprint retrospective. The model begins with an idea and the product
backlog being deﬁned. Then, the sprint planning phase starts where the scrum
team meets up and collaborates on the sprint backlog. With the sprint backlog
in place the sprint phase can begin. The phase is about designing and imple-
menting the requirements from the sprint backlog and do it within a speciﬁc
amount of time (usually 30 days). In parallel, a daily meeting with the scrum
team members and scrum master is done to ensure everything is on track. This
is the daily scrum phase.
The last two phases are executed after the sprint to review the software devel-
opment with the product owner (sprint review) and to revise the development
process of previous sprints to improve the upcoming sprints (sprint retrospec-
tive). Then, the entire process repeats all over again until the entire software
system is built [Drn08].
The key principles of scrum are to increase collaboration and quick deliveries
through intensive use of communication and milestones with time estimates.
3.4.4.2
Advantages
Scrum has multiple interesting advantages. The transparency and visibility are
increased because of the intensive communication among the participants of the
development. Scrum accommodates changes as the product backlog can change
after each sprint. Scrum forces quick development through deadlines for each
sprint and a selected set of requirements to be done after each sprint [Sam12].

Disadvantages
Scrum requires a lot of management and is highly depending on the knowl-
edge and competency of each participant. This brings the disadvantage of ev-

scrum process itself. Furthermore, it is diﬃcult to on-board participants in the

Prototype Model
The Prototype Model extends the waterfall Model by inserting prototype phases
into the waterfall Model. The Prototype Model distinguishes between diﬀerent
designs such as Evolutionary prototyping, Throwaway prototyping and more. In
the Throwaway prototyping, the analysis, design and implementation phases are
arranged in a loop. After each iteration of the loop, the system produces a
prototype that is either discarded or used in the ﬁnal delivered software system.
The Evolutionary prototyping is an iterative approach where the prototype is

discarded [Sam12].

Advantages

Disadvantages
Just like the waterfall model, the entire software system is considered in each
phase which bring a stable foundation of the software system. However, this
introduces a high risk in development processes with changing objective [Sam12].

Advantages
The Parallel Agile model introduces another agile model which provides the

The model is
adaptable to change due to iterative and incremental improvements. The model

3.5
Model-Based Software engineering (MBSE)
Model-based Software Engineering (MBSE) is a model-centric approach in soft-
ware engineering, which focus on coherent models of the system. The vision of
MBSE is a designed to tackle complex systems by describing the system through
models. Furthermore, MBSE states that models are the main artifact produced
during the SDLC. Hence, models are not just used as illustrations but serves
a purpose in software system. This typically result in the models dictating the
implementation, thus follows a model-centric approach. IBM express the model-
centric approach as "The model is the code" [Bro04]. For MBSE to express the
system, it relies on an extensive use of details in the modelling. Therefore, mod-
elling languages have be developed to support this, such as SysML. This uses
an extension of a subset of UML. Furthermore, MBSE relies heavily on tools to
support the modelling and the use of models in software systems. MBSE have
the advantages of being able to enhance communication and understanding, use
simulations and obtain validation of the software system. This allows the devel-
opment teams to mitigate risks and lower development costs. Due to the extent
of details in the modelling, generation of executable code from the models is

4.2
Design decisions
In this section the design decisions taken for the developed SDLC model are
described. From the previously presented SDLC models in Chapter 3, some
principles with valuable advantages have been found and are considered in the
following design of the developed SDLC model.
Initially, we consider which principles of the Waterfall model that contributes
to the developed SDLC model. The Waterfall model is often considered as an
ineﬃcient SDLC model due to the amount of risk that is introduces. The model
provides clarity of what and how the development process should be carried out.
Furthermore, the model simpliﬁes management process using documentation.
This is beneﬁcial for complex software development processes. The Waterfall
model introduces excessive planning as shown in Figure 3.3 and is therefore not
ideal for software development. Often in software development, the available
resources are limited. Hence, a budget impacts the development. This aﬀects
the development process remarkably as it sets a limit for how much of the
development is feasible to do within the given budget. The only way to precisely
==PAGE:65==
4.2 Design decisions
49
determine how much of the development is feasible to do, is to follow predictive
approach such as the Waterfall model.
The takeaway from this, is that the
Waterfall model brings great advantages for the early stages of the software
development process, which can be beneﬁcial in a hybrid SDLC model. However,
planning the entire system up-front is problematic.
Humans tend to have diﬃculties when processing large amount of information
at the same time, which often implies that we as humans are unable to fully
consider and plan a software system up-front. This is stated as following by
John Gall in his book Systemantics: How Systems Work and Especially How
They Fail [Gal78], page 52:
“A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved
from a simple system that worked.
The parallel proposition also
appears to be true: A complex system designed from scratch never
works and cannot be made to work. You have to start over, begin-
ning with a working simple system.”
Instead, we often take a divide and conquer approach as it is easier to consider
an small subset of the system. Furthermore, it is costly to ﬁnd bugs during the
implementation phase of the Waterfall model. Many SDLC models addresses
this situation by being iterative or incremental, such as: Incremental model,
Iterative model, Agile models, Scrum etc. These models are also known for their
ability to change throughout the development as the system is developed through
iterations. This has proven itself to be highly eﬃcient as the requirements and
the context in which the system is being developed is likely to change during
development. The context could for instance be the currently existing system
used by the customer, their business processes and a lot more. Besides becoming
adaptive to changes, these models improve the time-to-market as subsets of the
system can be validated iteratively. Therefore, these models rely intensively on
customer collaboration.
It has been established that the hybrid SDLC model needs to be adaptive to
change, have quick release cycles and high customer collaboration. This im-
plies relying on feedback. On the other hand, the model should enforce the
development team to plan the system up-front, which poses a contradiction as
the model cannot enforce the development team to plan everything ahead while
still plan iteratively. Hence, the hybrid SDLC model must ﬁnd a compromise
between the system planned up-front and planned iteratively. Martin Fowler
expresses that this compromise is achievable by requirement analysis and high
level design are done in a predictive manner and detailed design, code and test
are done in a iterative manner [Fow19]. Martin Fowler states state the com-
promise between predictive and iterative planning disables adaptive planning

i.e new features appearing during iterations. However, this claim is challenged
by introducing some overhead in the planning. Therefore, the hybrid SDLC
model should handle up-front plan the entire system minimalistically and de-
tailed planning handled iteratively where new features demand reﬁnement of the
predictive planning. This entails that the upfront planning only provides the
needed information to understand how the system is intended to work rather
than explicitly shows how the system should be implemented. This is a design
decision taken to reduce the total cost by introducing planning cost and lowering

Next, we cover how feedback should be obtained. This brings us to the consid-

mostly found during the prototyping. Prototyping is cheaper than implementa-
tion since prototyping is carried out quicker than implementation.
Next, we consider the contribution to the developed SDLC model from MBSE
and DDD. DDD is suited for complex systems.
The approach handles this
with the principles of conceptualising domains and establishing unambiguous
languages. The advantages of these principles are mainly enhancing communi-
cation and improving collaboration in the development team. Hence, this con-
tributes to multiple of the elicit requirements for the developed SDLC model.
However, a disadvantage of DDD is the eﬀort spent on modelling the domain
and maintaining the domain.
This brings us to the use of MBSE. MBSE highlights the use of system models in
the system itself. The disadvantage of large eﬀort spent of modelling when fol-

advantages such as quicker development, easier to understand the underlying
code and possible veriﬁcation through visualisation. To merge the use of DDD,
MBSE and prototyping in the developed SDLC model, prototyping should be
based on the domain. Hence, structural models and behavioral models should
be considered. This way, the domain modelled is validated and used in software
system itself. Furthermore, the developed SDLC model forces the domain to be
maintained.
The merge aims at tying the phases of the developed SDLC model closer to-
gether.
It is expected that tying the phases closer together, the Customer-
Developer gap will be reduced. This is main principle of the developed SDLC
model.

The most important phase to achieve a successful software project that fulﬁls

meant to obtain a shared understanding between the customer and development

of the use cases. Furthermore, the development team must model the structural
models during the workshop.

customer’s domain rather than the depth of it.

their contents contribute to dividing and grouping the tasks into sprints. This
reduce refactoring costs caused by sprints and tasks not being aligned properly.

two ﬁrst are elaborated on later in this section. Technological diﬃculties and
dependencies between tasks refer to unforeseen issues that often occurs during
software development. Therefore, the developed SDLC model allows the tasks
and sprints to be reﬁned after each iteration. For the change in customer re-

Coding
As mentioned, at this point the core of the system is built (structure and be-
havior is implemented and veriﬁed), but the platform speciﬁc code must be
implemented. In this phase, the developers should implement these platform
speciﬁc implementations. Initially, this section outlines what typical platform
speciﬁc code in a system might be. To do this, the section considers and ex-
tends an Architecture pattern presented by Martin Fowler. Finally, the section
provides an example.
One of the most common modularisation of a software application is the sep-
aration of Presentation Layer, Business Logic Layer and Data Access Layer
[Fow15].
The Data Access Layer handle Create, Read, Update and Delete
(CRUD) operations to the data store or data management through another
remote services. In the Presentation Layer the User Interface (UI) of the ap-
plication is responsible for giving a representation of the application. Lastly,
the Business Logic Layer (Domain Layer) contain validations, calculations and
behavior of the system. This modularization allows the developer to partially
focus on the system and provides the possibility to substitute or changes layers
without aﬀecting the remaining layers (of course assuming the layers are loosely
coupled).
There exists a vast variety of software architecture patterns that improve code
quality, such as the possibility to substitute layers. The thesis is not going into
details about software architecture patterns, but an example of such a pattern
is Command Query Responsibility Segregation (CQRS) pattern. Often software
architecture patterns must extend the three separated layers, like the CQRS pat-
tern. In web applications the Data Access Layer and Business Logic often is one
application and the Presentation Layer another. The CQRS pattern addresses
this by extending layers with a service layer that provides the Presentation Layer
with information from the system.
From the prototyping described in Subsection 4.6.2 the domain behavior of the

software system dictates the validations, calculations and of course the behav-
ior. Hence, the artifacts from the prototyping phase covers the Business Logic
Layer. According to the pattern presented by Martin Fowler, the remaining
layers should be the Presentation Layer and Data Access Layer. However, in
the following an extended version of the pattern is provided.

The provided software architecture pattern shown in Figure 4.3 contains an
Integration Layer and Data Access Layer. In this pattern, the Data Access Layer
is only responsible for managing the data store and mapping the information
from the data store to the Structured Domain. Whereas the handling of outgoing
communication to coexisting systems/application is instead carried out by the
Integration Layer.
The Structured Domain declares the data objects used within the application,
which reﬂect the entities of the domain. The Structured Domain is intended to
allow the layers of the application to communicate through a shared language.
The Business Logic Layer consists of the generated code and code snippets.
The generated code refers to the code generated from the behavioral models in
the prototyping and the code snippets to the manually written code snippets
from the prototyping. Furthermore, the Structured Domain Layer is entirely
generated from the structural models from the prototyping.
Like the CQRS design pattern, the design pattern described here is using a

4.7.3
Acceptance Testing
After the implementation of a task has been carried out, the customer needs
to validate that the implementation complies with the customer’s requirements.
Therefore, an acceptance testing phase is introduced. In this phase, the project
owner, stakeholder and occasionally testers check the acceptability. This is a
critical stage before the system can proceed to the delivery of the implemen-
tation. In case the customer rejects the implementation of the task, a second
iteration of the coding phase is conducted. Often the implementation is deliv-
ered to a testing environment that enables the customer to test. This is because
the customer is often not able the access the development environment.
Deploying to the test environment is required before the acceptance testing
phase, but relates to the deployment phase. Therefore, this is further described
in Section 4.8.
In the running example, the tasks Authenticate User and Authenticate Token
would be tested for acceptability together, as they depend on one another.
The customer should test the tasks through the User Interface implemented
to validate the behavior of the system. This would practically be carried out by
submitting a username and password and checking that access to the system is
only granted when a valid username and password is provided.

Deployment
The combination of development and operations (DevOps) has become a large
area of software development and considering DevOps would be to time consum-
ing for this thesis. Therefore, the deployment phase is considered out of scope for
this thesis. That being said, deployment phase is a large part of software devel-
opment, which provides unique values such as shorten the SDLC, continuously
delivery and reliability. In this report section some principles from DevOps are
described and expectation to what the developed SDLC model should include
from DevOps.
DevOps focuses on integrating development and operations, by automating the
integrating the developed code, delivery and deployment of the system. Con-

tinuous Integration and Continuous Delivery (CI/CD) are the main principles
from DevOps. Continuous Integration is about automating the integration of
code changes into version control. Furthermore, Continuous Integrations pro-
vide the ability to continuously test the code integrated, ensuring that the code
in version control is always correct.
Continuous Delivery is the second extension to Continuous Integration, which
is responsible for automatically packaging the code into deliverable artifacts,
which can be deployed and executed on hosting devices. This lowers the time
spend on the deployment phase and reduce issues through consistency in the
packaging. With less time spend on the deployment phase continuous releases
can be allowed, which introduce more frequently release cycle.
Continuous Deployment is the last part of DevOps, which relates to automati-
cally pushing the packaged code to the hosting devices. However, few companies
manage to implement Continuous Deployment as a deployment can have many
dependencies. For instance, handling data migrations related to a deployment.
Hence pulling oﬀContinuous Deployment would potentially require the use of
larger technology stack. This could technologies like application logging, net-
work logging, device logging, analytics software, rollback strategies etc.
As the developed SDLC model takes an iterative approach, it is beneﬁcial to
have CI/CD that increase the frequency of releases. CI should be implemented
to force the developer to ensure that the implementation delivered works as in-
tended. Hence, the testing carried out throughout the developed SDLC model
should be integrated in the Continuous Integration. The integration would then
ensure that the developers are not able to commit code to the source control
(like GitHub) if the unit tests, system tests or integration tests fails.
Implementing CI/CD contributes signiﬁcantly to the reliability of the code de-
ployed, which reduces bugs that potentially could be introduced. For instance,
while implementing the Authenticate Token task in the running example, the
Authenticate User task is being reﬁned as well. This implementation aﬀects
the behavior of the Authenticate User task, which could potentially introduce
unexpected behavior. Using CI this unexpected behavior could be caught and
prevented from being committed to source control (assuming the tests are well
written).
As mentioned in Subsection 4.7.3, the customer usually needs to conduct the
acceptance testing on a diﬀerent environment. Using the packaged code from
CD, the developers can push the code to the test environment and then the
acceptance testing can be conducted. Using the packaged code to deployment
to both the test environment and the production environment, the reliability is
improved even further.
The SDLC model does not require the use of CI/CD but highly recommending
it, as it contributes to multiple phases and reducing bugs. Therefore, the use of

Maintenance
Often software systems require upgrades or extension to the system is required
by the customer, such as integrations to other existing systems. This thesis is
not going into further details about maintenance. However, it is worth noticing
is that maintenance is only performed to the extent that developers can handle
it through minor changes to the system. If the upgrade, extension or repair

redundant.

Refactoring models
The tool needs to be able to handle changes of the modelling. Hence, the code
generation needs to be able to handle it as well. However, regenerating the code
that allow changes might potentially inﬂuence the custom code. The type of
changes allowed by regeneration of the code falls into the three categories listed
below.
• Addition:
Extending the modelling with new actions will not cause
breaking change in the custom code.
However, the developers need to
implement the snippets related to the actions added.
If the developer
does not implement the snippets the system would terminate with an ex-
ception during runtime. This exception would indicate that a snippet is
not registered.
• Deletion: Removing actions will cause breaking changes in some of the
snippets already written, which will force the developer to either remove
the snippets or ﬁx the breaking change. Fixing the breaking change would
include removing the extension of the abstract class that no longer exist.
In the Verify Credentials activity of the running example, removing the
action Authenticate user would be an example of this. The snippet be-
longing to the action would still appear in the source code, but the code
generation would have removed the abstract class that belongs to the ac-
tion. Hence, the class listed in Source Code 5.1 should be removed as the
abstract class AbstractAuthenticateUser would not exist.
• Altering: Changing actions will cause breaking changes as well. However,
the snippets need to be refactored accordingly. For instance, changing the
input of an action would break the existing implementation of the related
snippet and the code snippet needs to be refactored based of the new
input.
Currently the tool overrides the code generated every time code generation is
requested. Hence, if the developers could change the generated code then the
code generation would erase the changes done. Avoiding this issue, the devel-
opers are not allowed to change the generated code. Therefore, the Realisation
and Dependency relations are ignored by the code generation. The functionality
of the Dependency relation can be obtained by deﬁning an attribute with type
of the object that it is depended upon.

JetUML tool (prototyping tool) to insert objects and relations into the grid
view. For instance, the tool allows Classes, interfaces, packages, associations
etc. The packages are intended to deﬁne the bounded contexts, which contains
the domains of each applications. Therefore, the structural domain for all ap-
plications of the system must be represented in the same diagram. An example
of this can be seen in Figure 2.11 that shows the running example’s ﬁnal ver-
sion of the structural domain. Here the Authentication Service and Charging
Calculation Service are segregated and contains their own structural domain.
Each object and relation allow the user to enter the related properties of the
given relation or object. For instance, the class object allows the user to provide
a name, attributes (attributes of class) and methods of the object.
For the
generation of the structural domain both the attributes and methods must follow
a speciﬁc format:
access_specifier : name : type
For instance, an example of the format could be: + : firstname : string that
deﬁne an attribute of a domain object.
The access speciﬁer can either be + or -. + indicating public access speciﬁer and
- indicating private access speciﬁer. The name refers to the attribute or method
name. Lastly the type deﬁnes the type of the attribute or the return type of
the function. The generation requires the names to be precisely equivalent to
the names used throughout out the modelling. Currently the supported types
in the prototyping tool are: string, number, integer, boolean, object and classes
modelled. The type must be written exactly like listed and in lowercase, except
if domain objects modelled are referred to. Using a domain object, requires that
the type matches exactly the name of the domain object modelled. If the user
provides an unsupported type, the generation will default to the object type.
The properties of the Package domain object relate to the name of bounded
context and the directory path to JAVA project for bounded context. Hence,
the developers must create a JAVA project for each Package domain object.
The relations all allow to specify a name for it. The association, aggregation and
decomposition extend the properties by allow the user to specify the multiplicity
of the relation and the direction the relation is going. The generation is capable
of interpreting the following options for the multiplicity: 1..*, 0..*, *, 1 and
empty input. The empty input would be a provided as an empty string and will
be interpreted by code generator as an empty string as well. These multiplicities

5.3.1.2
Behavioral modelling
The behavioral modelling is based on activity diagrams. The use of these activity
diagram is described here. The tool allows actions to be named in both the
diagram and the named used in the generation of code. This way, the developers
can follow DDD principles in the generated code as well. In an Activity action
the user provides an output type. This type dictates the output of the code
snippet related to the Activity action. However, the user cannot provide the
output type in a Decision action. The code snippet related to a Decision action
is always returning an enum. The values of the enum are determined by the
outgoing transitions of the Decision action. Fork actions do not have a related
code snippet. Hence, they are completely handled in the modelling.
The transition only allows the user to provide a name for the action.
The
Decision action uses the names of its outgoing transitions to deﬁne the enum
values. If the user does not specify a name for the outgoing transitions, the
generation will generate one randomly. Therefore, it is highly recommended to
provide one.
The End action allows the type of output to be speciﬁed. The output of the
End action is the output of the entire behavior (the activity modelled).
Finally, the Start action requires the user to provide an activity name, type of
input to the activity and a directory path to the JAVA project. The activity
name distinguishes the requirements modelled from each other. Therefore, the
activity names must be unique between all the requirements that relates to the
same JAVA project. Hence, two activities with the same activity name and
directory path are not allowed.
The activity has only one input object and
providing an empty argument indicate that no inputs will be provided.
The Start action also specify a Run button in the properties. This button allows
the user to specify a concrete input to the activity. The input must reﬂect the
input type of the activity and be provided in a JSON format. After providing
the input the user can run the activity, which will run the ﬂow and trigger the
code snippets. An output will be provided in the tool as a result of this.

6.1
Introduction of the case study
An IT consultancy company delivering network security solutions must imple-
ment a Project and Portfolio Management system (PPM system). This system
is an information system that keeps track of company’s projects development.
The company delivers network and ﬁrewall conﬁgurations to its customers. Oc-
casionally, the company invest in internal projects. An example of such a project
could be learning technologies of cloud providers. This is a likely project of the
company as hosting through cloud service becomes more adopted by companies.
Hence, the need for network and ﬁrewall conﬁgurations fades away.
The system must deﬁne a representation of a project carried out by the company.
A project of the company progresses through multiple stages. The following
deﬁnes the stages a project goes through. Note that the following describes the
stages a company project progress through and not the development process of
the case study.
• Project vision: The project vision stage deﬁnes the creation of a project.
Any employee can create a project and must provide a title and description
of the project vision. After the creation, the project is set to be in the
project vision stage. While being in this stage any employee can edited
the title and description.
To move to the resource management stage,
the project manager (PM) must approve the project vision. Hence, the
employee should be able to request an approval.
• Resource management: The PM must deﬁne which employees should
participate in the project. Furthermore, the participation eﬀort expected
by each employee must be provided. Finally, materialistic resources such
as subscriptions, devices etc should be provided as well. The PM can at
any point during the stage move the project to the project proposal stage.
• Project proposal: When a project enters the project proposal stage, the
project remains idle until the Project Management Oﬃce (PMO) approves
the project. Internally, the company coordinates if the PMO should ap-
prove. This process includes the PM, PMO and Financial Oﬃce (FO).
==PAGE:99==
6.1 Introduction of the case study
83
The FO is responsible for evaluating the resources requested by compar-
ing them to the available budget. The PM is responsible for conveying the
project information to the FO and PMO, including adapting the project
information to the requests of the FO and PMO. Lastly, the PMO decides
which projects should be carried out.
The PMO has three options to handle the project, approve the project,
reject the project or leave the project in its current stage.
Approving
the project makes the project enter the ongoing project stage. Reject-
ing the project makes the project enter the closed project stage. Leaving
the project in its current stage implies that the PMO will reconsider the
project at a later point.
• Ongoing project: The ongoing project stage represent that the project
is currently being realised. During this stage, the assigned employees can
attach ﬁles to the project and communicate with each other. Those ﬁles
are documentation of the project such as models, white papers, guide etc.
The employees cannot change the remaining project information.
The
same is the case for the PM except that the PM can force the project re-
enter the project proposal stage. This allows the PM to edit the project
information. However, the PMO must approve the project once again.
• Completed project: When the project is ﬁnalised the PM can move
the project to the completed project stage. In this stage the project is
readable by all the employees, but no one can edit the information, attach
ﬁles or communication with each other. The PM has the option to re-
enter the project proposal stage, which requires the PMO to approve the
project again.
• Closed project: At any stage the PMO and PM have the option to
close a project, which indicate that the project is inactive. A project can
become closed due to the project being a failure, inadequate resources etc.
Like the completed project stage, the PM can make the project re-enter
the project proposal stage.
The portfolio aspect of the system focuses on tracking of the projects. This
includes obtaining an overview of project stages and resources. The PMO must
be able to follow the distribution of project stages to interpret the overall project
progress. The overview of resources should enable the PMO and FO to interpret
the overall investments in projects. This should be based on project progress
and resource committed to the development of projects. All these overviews
contribute to statistical analysis from which the company can evaluate the value
the projects bring. This value can then be compared to ﬁnancial investment and
eﬀort invested in projects.

What publications about hybrid SDLC models exist?
During Chapter 3 and related work in Chapter 1, the research about hybrid
SDLC models was investigated. The research shows that many hybrid SDLC
models have been developed that combines diﬀerent SDLC models to address
a large variety of concerns.
For instance, some of the hybrid SDLC models
focus on integrating security considerations, some on reducing a speciﬁc model’s

sources insinuate that many companies are using hybrid SDLC models and even
design hybrid SDLC models in the companies.
This research question has contributed to the thesis by expanding the knowledge
base.
What are the research lines for the hybrid SDLC models?
The research lines found are varying a lot, like insinuated previously. However,
a tendency regarding the need to optimise and extend existing SDLC models
appeared in many of the hybrid SDLC models. For instance, a combination of
the Waterfall model and Agile models were reoccurring. These SDLC models
particularly focused on the need to introduce more planning (like Parallel Ag-
ile). The research lines conﬁrm the need of early planning and Martin Fowler
expresses that the combination of the Waterfall model and an Iterative model
could be beneﬁcial in reducing risks of the SDLC [Fow19].

Conclusion
This thesis has motivated that agile development is not feasible in some soft-
ware development processes. However, there is a tendency in IT organisations
wanting to follow agile development. This probably has to do with the fact that
a lower project failure rate is experienced in software projects following agile
development. Hence, IT organisations seek to reduce software project failure
rates. This thesis has motivated that the reasons for project failures are related
to the Customer-Developer gap. Hence, gaps in a development process are an
indication of some of the reasons for project failures occurring as issues in the
development process.
In this thesis software engineering, SDLC models have been described. SDLC
models outline how software development should be carried out to cover the
SDLC phases.
Furthermore, the SDLC models outline which software engi-
neering concepts should be included in the software development. Many SDLC
models exist that each introduces principles to improve the development process.
This thesis has developed a SDLC model that combines principles of the Wa-
terfall model, Scrum (Agile models), Prototyping model, Parallel Agile, DDD
and MBSE. The developed SDLC model mainly follows the structure of Scrum.
Hence, the developed SDLC model iterates through sprints where a subset of
requirements is handled. From the Waterfall model, the principle of early plan-
ning of an entire system was introduced in the developed SDLC model. Ad-

developed SDLC model introduces a combination of behavioral modelling and
code snippets. The code snippets describe speciﬁc behavior of the behavioral
modelling, but the behavioral modelling dictates the control ﬂow.
To reduce the Customer-Developer gap, the developed SDLC model ties the
artefacts of phases together. To do this, code generation of domain models and
a software architecture pattern are introduced. The code generation ties the
requirements, modelling and implementation together as the modelling reﬂects
the requirements and the modelling is generated into source code. A software
architecture pattern was introduced to separate the source code produced in the
prototyping phase and implementation phase. However, the pattern also divides
the source code after requirements. Hence, the requirements are transparent in
the source code, which ties the custom source code and generated code to the
requirements. A consequence of this is increased redundancy in the source code.
This thesis has shown with a running example and case study that a develop-
ment process can follow the developed SDLC model. Furthermore, they have
contributed to demonstrate that the developed SDLC model contribute to bridg-
ing the Customer-Developer gap. This was demonstrated by describing how the
developed SDLC model addresses the seven reasons for project failure.
The
running example and case study also demonstrated that it is feasible to build a
tool that enables development teams to follow the developed SDLC model and
software architecture pattern. Additionally, external validation of the developed
SDLC model was obtained through two shares tech-shares. This external vali-
dation generally provided conﬁrmation of the developed SDLC model. However,
the ultimate validation would be to collect empirical evidence of the developed
SDLC model being used in practise.
The conclusion of this thesis is that the developed SDLC model contributes
to bridging the Customer-Developer gap.
However, further validation must
be obtained to say for certain. Furthermore, the thesis did not consider the
SDLC deployment stage and management process, which both are important in
development processes.
