Abstract 
Glycolysis, a common pathway across the tree of life, plays a central role in cellular growth and has 
been connected with diseases such as cancer and diabetes. Thorough understanding of the control 
points in glycolysis is therefore highly useful. Numerous studies have investigated the flux control 
of reactions in the pathway and found different results depending on the cell-type. In this work, 
glycolysis is examined in four different cell-types; L. lactis, yeast, E. coli, and human prostate 
cancer (PC3) cells. Cell-suspensions were injected hyperpolarised [1-13C,D] glucose or  [2-13C] 
fructose, and glycolytic metabolites were assayed using dDNP-NMR. A total of 6 glycolytic 
metabolites were quantified; F6P, FBP, DHAP, 3PG, PEP and Pyruvate. Balancing of metabolic 
intermediates was found to vary between cell-types. Different buffers and their effects on 
metabolism were also examined. Results showed that glycolytic metabolism was found to be 
significantly slower in the yeast cells in PB buffer compared with HEPES buffer. In addition to 
dDNP-NMR, L. lactis cells were grown on different sugars and examined using conventional 13C 
NMR in order to probe glycolysis on a longer timescale. It was found that L. lactis cells would 
adapt when grown on fructose medium resulting in increased rates of fructose metabolism. Lactate 
production in the L. lactis cells was also measured using 13C NMR. The lactate yield from 
injections of both glucose and fructose was found to be similar around 80-90%, indicating equal 
metabolism in both glucose and fructose.

1 Introduction 
Cellular metabolism is a general term encompassing a wide range of different reaction pathways in 
different cell types. These metabolic pathways are for example used to convert carbohydrates into 
useful energy or to synthesize molecular building blocks such as amino acids or fatty acids. 
Metabolic reactions are catalysed by enzymes and occur within the cell. Different metabolic 
pathways may run in parallel or even overlap with each other, while often containing many different 
intermediate compounds. Reaction intermediates and products in this context are termed 
“metabolites”. Due to the intricate nature of metabolic pathways and the amount of different 
metabolites involved, studying these pathways is often complicated. This project will focus on the 
glycolysis pathway, which follows the conversion of glucose (Glc) into two molecules of pyruvate.  
1.1 Importance of glycolysis 
Glycolysis is the main metabolic pathway in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes. It provides energy to 
the cell in the form of ATP and is entirely anaerobic as it requires no oxygen. In addition, glycolysis 
provides essential precursors for biomass synthesis. The entire glycolytic pathway produces net 2 
ATP, 2 NADH and 2 pyruvate molecules per glucose molecule. Pyruvate is then further 
metabolized into different end products such as lactate or ethanol, or used for biosynthesis, or enter 
the citric acid cycle where it is oxidized into CO2 providing additional energy and redox equivalents 
(ATP and NADH).  
Glycolysis is an ancient pathway that is conserved across the tree of life [Orlenko et al (2016)]. This 
implies that the enzymatic steps are identical, while the overall homeostasis (balancing of metabolic 
intermediates) differs between organisms. Understanding how different organisms are using the 
same pathway might provide inspiration for how glycolysis can be manipulated and exploited 
within the field of biotechnology and provide understanding of metabolic diseases such as cancer 
and diabetes.  
1.1.2 Glycolysis in biotechnology 
Understanding the kinetics of glycolysis is highly relevant for industrial applications and food 
fermentation, where for example glycolysis in lactic acid bacteria may ferment sugars into desired 
products such as lactic acid or ethanol [Martinussen et al (2013)]. Metabolic engineering in the form 
of gene manipulation of Lactococcus lactis has already been successfully performed to alter the 
metabolism of pyruvate, providing different end products [Neves et al (2005)]. The challenge 
==PAGE:8==
2 
 
remains in predicting how the cell will respond to altering the expression of its enzymes through 
genetic mutations, where it would be helpful to have thorough insight in the kinetics of glycolysis.  
1.1.3 Glycolysis in disease 
In addition to its biotechnological importance, glycolysis plays a central role in diseases such as 
diabetes and cancer [Tanner et al (2019)].  
Healthy mammalian cells will readily metabolize pyruvate via the citric acid cycle under aerobic 
conditions, using oxygen to efficiently convert glucose into energy. Cancer cells will instead 
predominantly metabolize pyruvate into lactic acid by fermentation even in the presence of oxygen. 
Glycolysis followed by lactic acid fermentation is less energy efficient per glucose molecule 
compared with metabolism from the citric acid cycle, but it is faster and provides more biomass for 
cellular growth. This inefficient metabolism in cancer cells is known as the Warburg Effect [Tanner 
et al (2019)]. One study found that different cancer cells would undergo different alterations, some 
being more oxidative and others more glycolytic, meaning the role of glycolysis in cancer seems to 
depend on the cancer cell type and it is not yet entirely understood [Wallace (2012)]. 
Diabetes is associated with high blood sugar concentration caused by lack of insulin or resistance to 
it. Normally, excess glucose in the blood is either converted to energy through glycolysis or 
metabolized in the liver through glycogenesis producing glycogen for storage. In diabetes, lack of 
insulin or resistance to insulin lowers the rate of glycolysis and fails to inhibit gluconeogenesis in 
liver cells. Gluconeogenesis is essentially reverse glycolysis, where metabolites are converted back 
into glucose through a different pathway than glycolysis [Park et al (2016)]. One study found that 
increasing the levels of fructose 2,6-biphosphate in type 2 diabetic mice, lead to an increase in the 
rate of glycolysis in the liver and subsequent lower levels of blood glucose concentration [Wu et al 
(2002)]. Therefore, it seems that thorough knowledge of glycolysis, its regulation and kinetic 
control is especially helpful for the complete understanding and treatment of both cancer and 
diabetes.

1.2 Background of glycolysis 
Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm and can be divided into 10 reaction steps. The first three 
steps are considered the investment phase as they consume ATP, while the remaining steps are the 
yield phase as they produce ATP and NADH. Figure 1 shows a simple overview of the glycolytic 
pathway.

Before glycolysis can proceed, Glucose has to enter the cell via transporter proteins on the cellular 
membrane. Then, in the first step, the enzyme hexokinase (HK) will catalyze the irreversible 
reaction of intracellular glucose into Glucose-6-phosphate (G6P), consuming one ATP molecule in 
the process. G6P now has a charge on the phosphate group and this charge ensures the G6P 
becomes trapped inside the cytoplasm, increasing its concentration. Hexokinase is known to be 
==PAGE:10==
4 
 
inhibited by its own product, G6P, which is likely due to direct competition between G6P and ATP 
for the active site of the enzyme [Xiaofeng et al (1999)]. Aside from glycolysis, here G6P can also 
take a different route and enter the pentose phosphate pathway.  
In the second step, G6P is converted into Fructose-6-phosphate (F6P) by the enzyme 
phosphoglucose isomerase (PGI). This reaction is reversible, but is driven forward by high 
concentration of G6P compared with F6P. Fructose may enter glycolysis through this step by being 
converted into F6P using hexokinase while consuming ATP. 
The third step converts F6P into fructose 1,6-bisphosphate (FBP) by the enzyme 
phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK). This step is irreversible and places an additional phosphate group on 
the fructose molecule.  
The fourth step converts FBP into one molecule of both glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (GADP) and 
dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) simultaneously by the enzyme aldolase. The two phosphate 
groups on FBP ensure both substrates remain charged preventing their diffusion out of the cell. This 
reaction is reversible and cleaves the fructose molecule through the middle, such that carbons 1,2 
and 3 become DHAP and carbons 4,5 and 6 become GADP.  
Only GADP is further metabolized in glycolysis, making DHAP essentially a waste product. 
Fortunately DHAP is readily converted into GADP by the enzyme triosephosphate isomerase (TPI) 
making the fifth step. Although this reaction is reversible, it is thermodynamically favoured towards 
DHAP. Furthermore, one study found the activities for both TPI and aldolase in yeast cells were 
relatively fast compared to the glycolytic flux [Hollander et al (1979)]. This means the reactions for 
both TPI and aldolase should operate close to equilibrium during glycolysis, at least for yeast cells. 
The fast activity of TPI is likewise expected as the enzyme is considered to be catalytically perfect 
[Proteopedia TPI]. One study estimated the equilibrium ratio between GADP and DHAP at room 
temperature in an enzyme solution to be around 1:24 respectively (4% GADP, 96% DHAP) owing 
to the higher thermodynamic stability of the C3 ketosephosphate as compared to the aldolase 
phosphate [Harris et al (1998)].  
The sixth step converts GADP + NAD+ + Pi into 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate (1,3BPG) + NADH + H+, 
where Pi is the hydrogen phosphate anion, HPO4

The seventh step simply converts 1,3BPG + ADP into 3-phosphoglycerate (3PG) + ATP by

The eighth step converts 3PG into 2-phosphoglycerate (2PG) by the enzyme phosphoglycerate

The tenth and final step follows the irreversible reaction of PEP + ADP + H+ into Pyruvate + ATP 
by the enzyme pyruvate kinase.

describes only the first 4 reactions, while the remaining 6 reactions constitute lower glycolysis 
[Tanner et al (2019)].

Having a highly negative ΔG usually, but not necessarily, means that the forward rate of reaction is 
much faster than the backward rate of reaction, making the backward rate negligible compared to 
the overall flux and the reaction approximately irreversible. The concept of irreversibility and ΔG in 
metabolic reactions is important in terms of the pathway kinetics and flux control [Orlenko et al 
(2016)].  
Flux control is a term describing how much a specific reaction controls the overall flux of 
the entire pathway. To put it simply, a reaction with a high flux control means the rate of this 
particular reaction directly affects the overall flux of the pathway. A low flux control means the 
pathway flux is largely independent of the rate of this reaction. In this sense, the reaction with the 
highest flux control could be considered the rate-limiting step in the metabolic pathway. It should 
be noted that flux control can be shared among different reactions, meaning there may be more than 
one rate-limiting step. One common assumption is that flux control is shared among reactions with 
large negative ΔG, or irreversible reactions [Orlenko et al (2016)]. Logically, it can be deduced that 
the first irreversible step in a pathway controls the flux of any following reactions downstream, 
under steady state conditions. This means the first irreversible step in a pathway must have some 
kind of flux-control. However, any subsequent irreversible reactions are not necessarily flux 
controlling if their rates are higher than the first. 
Rate-limitation may also lead a reversible reaction to becoming approximately irreversible 
under steady state conditions by pushing its equilibrium far forward until the reverse flux becomes 
negligible. Things become more complicated when you consider the possibility of metabolite 
inhibition such as the case with the first step in glycolysis, where the irreversible reaction by 
hexokinase is inhibited by its own product. From all this, it should be evident that while the ΔG of a 
reaction may be helpful in estimating flux control in glycolysis, it is certainly not sufficient. 
The free energies, ΔG, of each glycolytic step have been thoroughly investigated by Park et al in a 
study where they measured metabolite concentrations at steady state conditions using LC/MS. They 
investigated both Escherichia coli, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, and mammalian iBMK cells and 
combined the metabolite concentrations with formerly known standard Gibbs free energies to 
calculate ΔG. They were unable to measure the intracellular concentration of glucose and thus 
couldn’t determine ΔG of the very first step. Their results showed the formation of FBP using the 
PFK enzyme had ΔG ranging from -13 to -25 kJ/mol depending on cell-type in agreement with 
literature. However, the formation of Pyruvate using the PYK enzyme was found to have ΔG 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
between -4 to -10 kJ/mol, closer to zero, indicating the potential of some reversibility. Indeed, using 
[U-13C] isotope labelled pyruvate, Park et al measured the concentration of labelled 3PG and found 
the reaction has partial reversibility. They found the ΔG in most remaining reactions in glycolysis to 
be near zero, but still negative, therefore close to equilibrium and reversible [Park et al (2016)]. 
The reason why particularly steps one and three in glycolysis have such highly negative ΔG is in 
part due to their coupling with the hydrolysis of ATP into ADP. The phosphate bonds in ATP are 
high-energy bonds that are cleaved during hydrolysis providing a large negative ΔG°. In upper 
glycolysis, the coupling of ATP hydrolysis with steps one and three sufficiently decreases ΔG° such 
that reaction equilibrium is pushed far forward. In lower glycolysis however, ATP is reformed from 
ADP during steps seven and ten. In these reactions, the removal of phosphate groups from the 
metabolites cancels out the energy required to reform the phosphate bonds in ATP, making ΔG° 
still negative and the forward reaction favourable. 
There have been a number of studies in literature examining the kinetics of glycolysis using an 
approach where they inhibit or modulate the expression of a specific glycolytic enzyme in the cell 
and then measure the change in enzymatic activity compared with the change in glycolytic flux. 
This is used to determine how much flux control the enzyme has on the entire glycolytic pathway. It 
should be noted however, that the flux control depends itself on the enzyme activity, meaning any 
enzyme could become flux controlling if its activity is simply lowered enough. Poolman et al used 
this approach to investigate the flux control of the GADPH enzyme on glycolysis in lactic acid 
bacteria. The study found reduced activity of GADPH due to inhibition resulted in lower glycolytic 
flux, suggesting that GADPH is a flux controlling enzyme in glycolysis [Poolman et al (1987)]. 
Jensen et al published a more recent paper where they also investigated lactic acid bacteria, but in 
contrast found no flux control in GADPH on glycolysis at wild-type enzyme activity [Jensen et al 
(2002)]. Some explanations for the difference in results may be that Poolman et al only measured 
flux control below wild-type activity, or that they used different strains of lactic acid bacteria. 
Tanner et al used a similar approach to investigate the flux controlling steps in glycolysis of 
mammalian iBMK cells. Instead of investigating only one enzyme, Tanner took it one step further 
and systematically overexpressed each individual glycolytic enzyme and potential isozymes while 
simultaneously measuring glycolytic flux from lactate secretions. They evaluated the change in 
glycolytic flux to estimate any potential flux controlling steps and found that glycolysis, in iBMK 
cells, is controlled by four steps: (i) glucose import, (ii) glucose-6-phosphate production (HK), (iii) 
==PAGE:14==
8 
 
fructose-1,6-biphosphate production (PFK) and (iv) lactate export. Tanner also evaluated the 
expression of glycolytic enzymes in human tumor cells compared with normal healthy cells. They 
found an overall increase in the levels of some isozymes catalyzing glucose uptake and 
phosphofructokinase. Although the increase in enzyme levels was rather inconsistent between 
tumor types, it seems to support their findings for flux control in these steps [Tanner et al (2019)].  
One problem with determining glycolytic flux control from alterations in enzyme activity is the 
possibility that enzymes are multifunctional. GADPH in particular is known to have multiple roles 
outside glycolysis, which may indirectly affect the results [Tristan et al (2010)]. Another 
complication with this approach is that altering the activity of one enzyme may cause the cell to 
compensate by altering expressions of other enzymes through adaptation or even activate other 
enzymes, leading to changes in perceived flux control [Tanner et al (2019)].  
Orlenko et al used a more theoretical approach to estimate the flux controlling steps in glycolysis. 
They acquired Vmax for each enzyme from data of previously determined metabolic models of 
glycolysis in different cell-types. Vmax is a Michaelis-Menten parameter describing the enzymes 
maximum velocity at substrate saturation. They then used Vmax in combination with known values 
of ΔG° to be used in a metabolic pathway simulator, allowing them to simulate the expected rate-
limiting steps from the model. About half of the cell-types were found to have multiple rate-limiting 
steps. The proposed most rate-limiting steps are summarized in Table 1 below [Orlenko et al

The primary issue with this approach is that it is poorly described. No explanation is given as to 
how only Vmax and ΔG° for each enzyme provides enough information to estimate the kinetics of the 
entire glycolytic pathway, let alone what assumptions were made along the way. Another problem 
==PAGE:15==
9 
 
is that the Vmax acquired from some of the models were reported being only qualitatively correct, not 
useful for kinetic modelling. Finally, some of the models were simplified by combining few of the 
glycolytic steps into one step, making it impossible to assume correct kinetics in the combined 
steps. It should be no surprise that these results are viewed with scepticism in this work. 
Interestingly, a review by Liu et al summarized results from their earlier studies and observed that 
no individual enzyme appears to have flux control on glycolysis in L. lactis. They explain this 
paradox by proposing the idea that glycolysis is already running at its maximum possible rate at 
wild-type enzyme level in L. lactis [Liu et al (2019)]. This interpretation could make sense 
considering how glycolysis is so closely related to cellular growth. Evolution will naturally drive 
cells toward having the highest possible growth-rate thereby having the most optimal enzyme 
expression for maximizing glycolytic flux to generate the required energy and biosynthetic 
precursors. The optimal rate of glycolysis in L. lactis is supported by the fact that this cell-type is 
known to possess an especially high amount of glycolytic enzymes, accounting for about 20% of 
the total soluble protein [Guillot et al (2003)].  
It is even possible that the flux control of glycolysis lies outside the pathway itself. One study 
explored this by overexpressing ATPase in L. lactis, which lowered the ATP/ADP ratio. For 
growing cells, no change was observed, but in non-growing cells the increased activity for ATPase 
caused a significant increase in glycolytic flux, likely due to the higher demand for ATP caused by 
the overexpressed ATPase [Koebmann et al (2002)]. In another study, Neves et al used NMR to 
examine glycolysis in L. lactis. Similarly, they found glycolytic flux to be controlled by NAD+ 
limitation caused by ATP surplus [Neves et al (2005)].  
Considering all the mentioned studies, it seems that glycolysis cannot be thought of simply as a 
single metabolic pathway. The intricate relationship between different enzymes and metabolites 
coupled with the central role of glycolysis in cellular growth makes it exceedingly complicated to 
predict. The question of “Which steps exert flux control in glycolysis?” does not have a simple 
satisfying answer and likely depends on many different factors, some of which maybe haven’t been 
discovered yet.  
In this project, glycolytic flux control was investigated in four different cell-types, using DNP-
NMR and conventional NMR spectroscopy, in order to quantify metabolite concentrations after

injection of glucose. The determined metabolite concentrations would provide insight into potential 
metabolite accumulation, which might indicate some form of glycolytic flux control. 
1.3 NMR spectroscopy 
Because glycolysis takes place within the cytoplasm, it is difficult to directly measure the actual 
concentrations of metabolites. One method is to break the cellular membrane, causing the 
metabolites to spill out into solution, where they can be isolated and measured with HPLC/MS or 
NMR. This method is useful for measuring steady state concentrations but allows only one 
measurement due to the destructive nature of the extraction method. An alternative method is to use 
NMR, which is particularly useful because it can be done in vivo without affecting the cell. This 
non-invasiveness allows measurements of metabolites under dynamic conditions where 
concentrations change over time. One challenge with NMR is that the method is inherently 
insensitive, and concentrations of metabolites are often small, around mM to μM levels in steady 
state, making their signals difficult to detect and quantify. The signal-to-noise ratio is therefore a 
limiting factor with the use of NMR for this particular purpose. 
With NMR, all nuclei with a non-zero spin in the sample are polarized by an external magnetic 
field. A nuclear spin can be interpreted as a vector from the nucleus with a direction and a length. In 
the presence of a magnetic field the nuclei will orient their spins to be slightly favored towards 
facing the direction of the field. While the individual nuclear spin can face any direction, the sum of 
all nuclear spins or nuclear net magnetization, M, will point in the direction of the magnetic field, 
B0. In addition, the magnetic field will cause the nuclear spins to precess around the direction of the 
field,  , with the Larmor Frequency. This frequency depends on the magnetogyric ratio, which is an 
intrinsic property of each type of nucleus.

The direction of the nuclear net magnetization, M, can be perturbed by applying a magnetic pulse 
orthogonal to B0. When the frequency of the pulse equals the Larmor frequency of the nuclei, the

induction of electromagnetic current in a detector in the transverse plane. The transverse component 
of M will however decay over time due to spin-spin relaxation. The electromagnetic current is then 
detected as a free induction decay (FID), which is Fourier transformed from the time-domain to the 
frequency-domain to get the NMR spectrum [Robin].  
Nuclei of the same type may have slightly different Larmor frequencies due to shielding caused by 
the electron-cloud of nearby atoms. This tiny difference, called the chemical shift is measured in 
parts per million (ppm). It allows the signals from nuclei in different chemical environments to be 
separated and detected individually.

The important aspect of equilibrium polarization is that it is so incredibly small under normal

Whenever NMR is used for determining metabolic concentrations in vivo, the substrates must be 
labeled with 13C isotopes. This is necessary because the natural abundance of 13C is around one 
percent, while the majority (99%) of natural carbon exists as 12C which has zero spin making it 
NMR silent. The usage of isotope labeled substrates thereby increase the signal to noise level 
dramatically. The low natural abundance of 13C is even an advantage for 13C NMR because it 
ensures the relative metabolite signals are now significantly higher compared with the organic 
cellular background. This is one of the reasons, why 13C NMR is often preferred over 1H NMR 
when studying cellular metabolites.

Another way to increase the signal to noise level is by doing multiple consecutive scans of the 
sample and adding them together. Each scan will increase the signal proportionally, but the random 
noise only increases with the square root of scans. Thus, signal/noise ratio increases with the square 
root of the number of consecutive scans. The challenge with increasing the number of scans is that 
it takes time, because it is necessary to wait for T1 relaxation after each pulse. In other words, doing 
multiple scans will increase the signal to noise, but decrease the time-resolution of the experiment 
[Jensen (2021 lectures)].  
1.4 Liquid-state DNP-NMR 
One additional way to increase the NMR signal of the metabolites is to use a hyperpolarized 
substrate. Hyperpolarization can be achieved from different methods, but this project will focus on 
the dynamic nuclear polarization (DNP) method. DNP utilizes the ability to increase the 
polarization of nuclei beyond its equilibrium polarization by transferring polarization from highly 
polarized electrons to the nuclei in the substrate. The electron also has spin ½, but has a 
magnetogyric ratio more than 600 times larger than the 1H nucleus due to its much lower mass. 
Only free unpaired electrons are polarizable. Such free unpaired electrons may be found in trityl 
radicals commonly used for this purpose. The transfer of polarization takes place by placing the 
sample in low temperatures of around 1 kelvin in a medium strength magnetic field, allowing the 
electron polarization to reach approximately 100%. When using DNP, the sample is then irradiated 
with microwave radiation, which is absorbed by the electrons causing them to briefly flip to the 
higher spin state, while simultaneously flipping the spin-state of a nearby nucleus.  
This is achieved when the frequency of the microwave radiation is the Larmor frequency of the 
electron plus or minus the Larmor frequency of the nucleus in the applied field. Afterwards, the 
electron will quickly relax back to the lower spin-state, but the nucleus remains in the higher spin 
state. The simultaneous flipping of the nuclei takes place due to dipolar interaction, which is 
inversely proportional to the third power of the distance. This means the nuclei and radical must be 
mixed as closely as possible and any crystallization during the cooling of the sample is undesired. 
Over time, the microwave radiation causes the nuclei to become hyperpolarized, however the 
highest obtainable hyperpolarization is still limited by the ratio of the nuclear and electron T1 
relaxation [Ardenkjær-Larsen (2016)]. The T1 time in solid state is on the order of hours for the 
nuclei and milliseconds for the electron. A small amount of Gd complex is added to the substrate 
which further lowers the electron T1, increasing the polarization efficiency. Still, the time needed to 
==PAGE:20==
14 
 
achieve near-maximum substrate polarization is typically around one hour. When the 
hyperpolarization has reached near-maximum, the sample is dissolved into a liquid using hot buffer 
and injected into the spectrometer immediately afterwards. This dissolution process must be quick 
as the polarization now decays due to relaxation of the substrate in liquid state with the T1 in the 
order of seconds to minutes for 13C. 
The increased polarization from DNP-NMR can briefly enhance the signal strength by more than 
five orders of magnitude compared with conventional (thermal) NMR. Because of the increased 
signal to noise, this method allows real-time measurements of transient metabolites with a time-
resolution of less than a second. The fast decay of signal due to T1 relaxation, and the slow 
throughput of hyperpolarized substrate are the main limiting factors of DNP-NMR. It is well 
known, that T1 relaxation is generally larger for quaternary carbons, compared with protonated 
carbons such as methyl groups. The hydrogens shorten the T1 time of its parent carbon atom via 
cross-relaxation from the nuclear overhauser effect. To prevent this, the attached hydrogens may be 
replaced with deuterium, which is less magnetic due to its lower magnetogyric ratio, diminishing 
the nuclear overhauser effect and increasing the T1 time of the labelled carbon [Jensen (2021 
lectures)].

2 Methods and materials 
All experiments were performed in the laboratories at DTU Hypermag. Four different cell-types, 
Lactococcus lactis (MG1363), Saccharomyces cerevisiae (BY4743), Escherichia coli (MG1655), 
and human prostate cancer (PC3), were cultivated and injected with hyperpolarized glucose before 
being analysed with 13C NMR. Three different buffers were provided and used throughout the 
project, either PB (Phosphate Buffer, 40 mM, pH = 7.32), MES (2-(N-morpholino)ethanesulfonic 
acid, 30 mM, pH = 6.65) or HEPES (4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1-piperazineethanesulfonic acid, 40 mM, 
pH = 7.0).  
2.1 Preparation of cell suspensions 
The different cell suspensions used in the NMR experiments were generously cultivated and 
prepared by Alexandra Lauritsen Neergaard Zahid, Pernille Rose Jensen or Anne Egholm Høgh. 
Some of the L. lactis cell suspensions were cultivated and prepared by the author. Mammalian PC3 
cancer cells grow adherent to a surface in an atmosphere of 5% CO2 at 37 °C. They were cultivated 
to confluence and harvested by standard procedures. The comprehensive procedure for PC3 cells is 
described by Christensen et al [Christensen et al (2013)]. The procedures for E. coli and yeast are 
further described by Meier et al [Meier et al (2011Aug), Meier et al (2011Jun)]. For bacteria and 
yeast cells the same general procedure was followed for the preparation of cell suspensions, with 
the exception of the growth medium and temperature, which was GM17, 30 °C for L. lactis, TSB, 
37 °C for E. coli and YPD, 30 °C for BY4743. A diagram of the L. lactis cell preparation procedure 
is illustrated in Figure 2.

density at 600 nm (OD600) measurements using a spectrophotometer calibrated with pure growth 
medium. Dilution with growth medium was done whenever necessary to ensure OD was measured 
within the linear range of the spectrophotometer, below 0.3.  
On the day prior to the NMR experiments, an overnight culture was prepared in order to achieve 
adequate concentration and growth rate for the following experiments. The next day, OD was 
measured and used to calculate the required volume of overnight culture to reach a starting OD of

autoclaved 100 mL Blue Cap Flask. The blue cap was then filled with growth medium containing 1

incubator and cell growth resumed for 3-4 hours, while OD was measured every 30 minutes. When

OD = 0.5 was reached, after approximately 3-4 hours, the cells were harvested for the NMR 
experiment. During this stage, the cell growth was in the exponential phase.  
When harvesting, the OD was used to calculate the required volume of growing cell suspension to 
reach a final OD of 15, approximately 30 mL cell suspension. The calculated volume was transfered 
with a serological pipette into a 50 mL falcon tube and centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 5 min. The 
supernatant was discarded and 5 mL buffer added to wash the remaining pellet. The pellet 
containing the cells was re-suspended in the buffer and centrifuged again at 5000 rpm for 5 min. 
The supernatant was once again discarded, and any remaining buffer on the lip was removed with 
pipette or napkin to achieve constant volume of the pellet. Then, approximately 150 uL buffer was 
added and the pellet was re-suspended, giving a total suspension volume of around 200 uL. The cell 
suspension was then transferred to a 5 mm Shigemi tube, now ready for NMR.  
Because multiple runs were done during one day, additional cell cultures had to be incubated 
staggered apart. Harvesting had to be done around the time when the substrate was finished 
hyperpolarizing. Correct timing was usually achieved by starting a new cell culture from the 
exponentially growing culture when its OD reached 0.4. This was done by extracting the volume 
required to reach OD=0.1 in 100 mL, which was added to a fresh 100 mL blue cap and the 
remaining volume filled with growth medium + 1% sugar, before incubation.

2.3.1 DNP-NMR procedure 
During dissolution, the hot dissolved hyperpolarized substrate was automatically transferred to a 
receiver container. On the receiver, a 1 mL syringe was attached to be filled with exactly 400 uL 
liquid substrate, then manually transferred to the injection line. The dead-volume of the injection 
line was 200 uL and the remaining 200 uL was the actual injected volume of hyperpolarized 
substrate to the NMR tube. The whole transfer process from the hyperpolarizer took only around 10 
seconds, ensuring minimal signal loss from T1 relaxation. The initial sugar concentration after 
injection was 9.8 mM when 30 mg substrate was hyperpolarised. The pulse program was initiated at 
the same time as injection occurred. The program was set to perform a single pulse with a 90° flip 
angle after exactly 3 seconds. The 3 second delay was chosen from optimisation of signal intensity 
as explained in the results section.  
2.3.2 Conventional NMR procedure 
The hot water bath ensured the substrate temperature in the 1 mL syringe was around 310 K after 
travelling through the injection line and released into the Shigemi tube. The dead volume of the 
injection line was again 200 uL, giving an actual injection volume of 200 uL. The final suspension 
volume after injection was 460 uL, giving an initial sugar concentration of 10.9 mM. After 
injection, a time series of 1D 13C spectra was acquired. A 90° pulse was given once every 0.86 
seconds. A total of 70 pulses were summed into one spectrum. This was repeated 32 times, giving a 
time series of spectra spanning 32 minutes after injection.

3 Results 
dDNP-NMR experiments were performed on four different cell types; gram positive bacteria L. 
lactis, gram negative bacteria E. coli, yeast, and mammalian prostate cancer cells (PC3). The effect 
of different buffers was also investigated. One of three different buffers were used; PB, MES or 
HEPES. Phosphate buffer (PB) is a preferred buffer from an NMR perspective because it does not 
contain any carbon. This buffer nevertheless can impact cellular metabolism. Good buffers are 
biologically compatible buffers with low cell membrane permeability and appropriate pKa. Besides 
dDNP-NMR experiments, conventional 13C NMR experiments were also done on L. lactis cell-
suspensions in order to examine its metabolism over a longer timescale. 
The dDNP-NMR method has been used in previous studies for examining glycolysis in cellular 
systems such as yeast and mammalian cancer cells [Meier et al (2011Jun), Harris et al (2013)].  
These studies have used [U-13C,D] glucose as metabolic tracer, which allowed determination of 
glycolytic bottlenecks in the lower part of glycolysis. In the main body of literature about glycolytic 
flux control there seems to be consensus that the upper part of glycolysis exerts the most flux 
control [Tanner et al (2019)]. However, upper glycolytic metabolite peaks are difficult to quantify 
with the use of [U-13C,D] glucose due to spectral overlap between the metabolite peaks and the 
dominant glucose peak. The original [U-13C,D] glucose dDNP-NMR assay therefore had to be 
optimized with the aim of accessing metabolites in the upper part of glycolysis, which are all 
glucose and fructose derivatives.  
Hence, [1-13C,D] glucose was used as metabolic dDNP-NMR tracer as it liberates most metabolites 
of upper glycolysis from the dominant glucose signal. Metabolism could then be followed by 
applying 10° flip angles every 0.5 s, providing a time-series of 13C NMR spectra (Figure 4).  
==PAGE:27==

A recent study found that the maximum signal-to-noise in a dDNP-NMR experiment is obtained by 
doing a single 90° pulse [Jensen et al (2021)]. The main benefit of a 90° pulse is that it provides 5.8 
times higher transverse magnetization compared with a 10° pulse, which is directly translated into 
larger signal intensity, but the downside with 90° pulsing is that it quenches the entire polarisation 
in the direction of the magnetic field, meaning only a single useful spectrum can be obtained. All 
dDNP-NMR experiments in this project were performed utilizing the 90° pulse acquisition strategy.  
The timing of the 90° pulse should be carefully selected to maximize the acquired signal. The 
polarisation of metabolites decreases rapidly due to T1 relaxation, but their concentrations increase 
initially due to metabolic synthesis. There is an optimum time after injection, where the signal for 
the metabolites is highest, which was found to be around 3 seconds after injection, as determined 
from signal intensity over time for the metabolite with highest intensity in the 60-70 ppm region. 
(Figure 5)

This did not account for the loss of polarisation due to periodic 10° pulsing in the NMR acquisition. 
The actual optimal delay between injection and acquisition for a 90° pulse experiment is probably 
slightly longer than the determined 3 seconds. Therefore, a few experiments were also done using a 
6 second pulse delay. 
The dDNP-NMR experiments were used to quantify metabolite concentrations in order to examine 
potential metabolite accumulation, which could indicate some degree of glycolytic flux control. 
Before quantification could be done, the metabolite peaks had to be assigned correctly. This will be 
addressed in section 3.1. 
Besides [1-13C,D] glucose as metabolic tracer for detection of metabolites in upper glycolysis, [2-
13C] fructose was also considered. Fructose enters glycolysis via its conversion to F6P by the 
hexokinase enzyme, hydrolyzing an ATP molecule in the process. The primary advantage of [2-13C] 
fructose over [1-13C,D] glucose is that the C2 carbon in fructose is quaternary, not bound to any 
hydrogen atoms, giving the [2-13C] fructose substrate a longer T1 time. The same approach, as 
described for [1-13C,D] was used for all [2-13C] fructose experiments in L. lactis cells.  
Most dDNP-NR experiments were done with 30 mg substrate (and some with 20 mg), but a few of 
them were done with 38 mg. The higher substrate amount was initially used with the hope of

achieving faster glucose uptake rate, giving faster metabolism and larger metabolite signals. 
Because glucose uptake is achieved by transporter proteins, the uptake rate follows regular enzyme 
kinetics and becomes saturated at higher glucose concentrations. It was believed that 38 mg would 
provide slightly higher uptake rate than 30 mg, but this was never explicitly tested. However, the 
use of 38 mg substrate was quickly cancelled, as it was found to often cause overflow in the 
receiver of the spectrometer (ADC overflow) due to the incredibly high glucose signal. ADC 
overflow generated ripple artifacts in the resulting spectrum making it nearly impossible to quantify 
the metabolite peaks.

3.2 Metabolite peak quantification 
Appendix 3 shows the determined integrals in arbitrary units of all the quantifiable metabolites in 
all spectra. 
Metabolite peak quantification was done using MestReNova 14.1.2 by Mestrelab Research. All 
spectra were phase corrected and the baseline was manually defined using multipoint correction. In 
order to reduce artifacts and smoothen noise, the FID data was apodised with an exponential 
window function corresponding to a line broadening of 10 Hz. The metabolite peaks were fitted to a 
lorentzian function and the area under the curve of the functions determined the integrals of the 
peaks. Lorentzian peak fitting was necessary because some of the peaks were partially overlapping

The difference in the (C1 FBP + DHAP) / F6P ratio is important, because it most likely originates 
from a difference in glycolytic pathway kinetics. In the case, where the F6P → FBP reaction has 
high flux control, accumulation of F6P should occur, giving rise to a lower (C1 FBP + DHAP) / F6P

C6 FBP indicates the FBP ↔ DHAP reaction is highly reversible, therefore close to equilibrium, 
meaning the relative concentration between FBP/DHAP must be nearly constant. This indicates the 
glycolytic flux control of the irreversible F6P → FBP reaction is likely higher for L. lactis, lower 
for E. coli and lowest for PC3 cells.

4 Discussion 
One major issue with the assignment and quantification of metabolite peaks in the [1-13C,D] 
glucose spectra was the C-D splitting leading to wider peaks, causing signal overlap and lowering 
the signal-to-noise ratio. This could be solved by developing and using a pulse program able to 
reliably decouple both C-D and C-H coupling without introducing artifacts. The C-H coupling is 
still necessary as a hydrogen is provided to the labelled carbon during the formation of F6P, which 
remains throughout the pathway. The hydrogen on the labelled carbon in F6P and subsequent 
metabolites is expected to lower the T1 time, potentially affecting the estimated concentrations as 
determined in section 3.3. The origin of the newly attached hydrogen on the C1 carbon in F6P is 
unclear. It may simply originate from the solvent, but one mechanism indicated it could also be a 
hydrogen transferred from the C2 carbon in G6P [Proteopedia PGI]. In the latter case, using [1-13C, 
1,2-D2] glucose may be beneficial, as it would prevent the attachment of hydrogen to the labelled C1 
carbon through glycolysis. Still, without C-D decoupling the second deuterium would give rise to 
further C-D splitting and even lower signal to noise. 
A study by Mishkovsky et al mentioned that using D2O instead of H2O as solvent in the substrate 
would increase the T1 relaxation time of U-13C,D glucose by 20% on C3 and C4 [Mishkovsky et al 
(2017)]. The remaining carbons were not examined and it is unclear if the increase would likewise 
apply to the C1 carbon on the [1-13C,D] glucose substrate for this project. One potential problem 
with the usage of D2O as solvent is that it may affect the cellular metabolism through isotope 
effects; hence this idea was not further considered. Isotope effects of D2O are expected to be 
particularly significant since water is so abundant and a common participant in metabolic reactions. 
Due to a misunderstanding, the amount of OX063 trityl radical added to most of the [1-13C,D] 
glucose substrates (2.6 mg) was lower than intended (4.6 mg). This resulted in slower polarisation 
times due to the lower concentration of unpaired electrons in the substrate. It is unlikely to have 
affected the final polarisation during dissolution, but the throughput of hyperpolarised substrate 
would have been faster with higher amounts of trityl radical. 
Not all dDNP-NMR experiments were successful. One cause of failure was in the transfer process 
between the receiver container and the NMR tube. The 1 mL syringe was often wet and slippery, 
occasionally leading to faulty injections, where the syringe would slip out of the injection line 
during injection, spilling most of the substrate and resulting in little to no volume injection. An 
automatic transfer process between the dissolution chamber and NMR tube could have been useful 
==PAGE:46==
40 
 
for this project, with potential benefits such as providing a shorter delay between dissolution and 
injection, and a more consistent and precise volume injected into the NMR tube. A pre-designed 
automatic transfer system was actually briefly tested during the project, but found to be too 
unreliable and not useful.  
A significant source of variability between experiments was the injection volume, which seemed to 
have a standard deviation around 10%, occasionally more than that. The biggest source of 
variability is believed to originate from the cell-preparation procedure. In some early experiments 
with L. lactis, the cells were found to grow much slower than anticipated. Slower growing cells 
likely have slower metabolism giving rise to uncertainty in the metabolite concentrations. 
Numerous causes for variations in cellular growth have been considered such as; who performs the 
cell-preparation procedure, which agar plate was used for inoculation, the quality of the growth 
medium or even the presence of soap residues in the blue caps. The harvesting procedure is also a 
source of variation since the time it takes between harvest and injection easily varies by 5-10 
minutes. The actual cell densities in the NMR tubes also likely had some variation. Maybe the cell 
densities could have been measured after each experiment to account for this. 
Overall, the dDNP-NMR method seems well suited for quantifying metabolites of upper glycolysis. 
With both metabolic tracers, a total of 6 glycolytic metabolites could be observed with good signal 
to noise ratios. One major shortcoming with the [1-13C,D] glucose substrate is its inability to 
observe any signal for the G6P metabolite due to its chemical shift overlapping with glucose. The 
[2-13C] fructose substrate has no overlap with G6P, but in this case G6P is mostly irrelevant as it 
isn’t part of the fructose metabolism. Considering the chemical shifts from Appendix 2, there 
doesn’t seem to be any way to spectrally isolate G6P from both glucose and other metabolites with 
13C NMR.  
A source of concern with the use of labelled substrates is the possibility that they alter the 
metabolism by affecting the reaction mechanisms on the enzyme via isotope effects. The main 
function of enzymes in terms of catalysing reactions is their ability to lower its energy barrier, 
which is related to the energy required to cleave the bonds between the reacting nuclei. 13C nuclei 
are mostly similar in mass to the naturally abundant 12C nucleus, therefore not expected to have an 
isotopic effect. While deuterium (D) has twice the mass of hydrogen (H), experimental 
determinations of the bond dissociation energies in C-D and C-H bonds have been reported being 
nearly the same [Luo (2007)]. Isotope effect may still affect the reaction mechanism of metabolic 
==PAGE:47==
41 
 
reactions in other ways. One study investigated the kinetic isotope effect of U-13C glucose on its 
metabolism in E. coli. Their results found that kinetic isotope effects may have an impact in central 
metabolic enzymes, but can be neglected for glycolytic enzymes [Millard et al (2015)].  
One final detail to be discussed is the realization that metabolic flux control is itself a dynamic 
phenomenon. The results from this project suggested the difference in accumulation of F6P might 
originate from a difference in flux control of the F6P → FBP reaction. However, this can only be 
assumed in the scenario where the cells have been pulsed with a glucose (or fructose) injection at 
around 10 mM concentration. It should be emphasized that these results cannot necessarily be 
extrapolated to glycolysis in steady state conditions, where metabolite concentrations are different. 
One interesting observation related to this was made by the study from Tanner et al, mentioned 
earlier. They found an overexpression of the hexokinase isozyme HK2 lead to an initial increase in 
glycolytic flux 12 h after transfection, but would instead decrease glycolytic flux after 30 h. They 
suggested this apparent time-dependency of flux control was actually due to energy stress in the cell 
from the enzyme overexpression [Tanner et al (2019)]. In any case, more research should be done to 
investigate how glycolytic flux control might change between initial and steady state conditions.

5 Conclusion 
Cellular metabolism through glycolysis was followed in four different cell-types; L. lactis, yeast, E. 
coli and PC3. The different cells had been cultivated and cell-suspensions prepared to be examined 
with dDNP-NMR. The cell-suspensions were injected hyperpolarised [1-13C,D] glucose, and 
metabolite concentrations were determined from integrations of their respective signals in the 
acquired 13C NMR spectra. Additional experiments were performed on L. lactis cells injected 
hyperpolarised [2-13C] fructose. The 13C NMR spectra were acquired with a single 90° pulse exactly 
3 seconds after glucose injection. A total of 6 glycolytic metabolites were quantifiable using this 
method; F6P, FBP, DHAP, 3PG, PEP and Pyruvate. For both tracers, the metabolites were observed 
with mostly good signal to noise. Three different buffers, PB, HEPES or MES were used and 
compared. Results showed that glycolytic metabolism was found to be significantly slower in the 
yeast cells in PB buffer compared with HEPES buffer. A statistically significant difference in 
relative concentrations of (FBP+DHAP)/F6P was observed between most cell-types, which might 
be attributed to a relative difference in flux control of the F6P→FBP reaction for this circumstance. 
In addition to dDNP-NMR experiments, glycolysis in L. lactis cells was also examined with 
conventional 13C NMR. It was found that adaptation in L. lactis cells grown on fructose medium 
would lead to increased rates of fructose uptake and subsequent higher rates of glycolytic flux. 
Measurements of the lactate production in the L. lactis cells revealed the lactate yield from 
injections of both glucose and fructose was around 80-90%, in agreement with literature.

6 Perspective 
The dDNP-NMR method is most suited for substrates with high T1 times, such as pyruvate. But the 
results from this project demonstrate its usability for organic compounds with lower T1 times such 
as glucose, potentially leading it towards a wider range of applications. The method can still be 
further improved and optimized with a reliable automatic transfer system and decoupling of C-D 
splitting. It is the author’s belief that cellular metabolism will remain relevant far into the future, 
and a wide assortment of analytical methods, including dDNP-NMR, will be essential to further 
study and eventually understand its important nuances and details.

Appendix 7 - L. lactis growth curves 
The growth experiments were conducted the 12th April in parallel to the conventional NMR 
experiments. After harvesting for NMR experiments, cellular growth was continued with the 
remaining cell-suspension in the blue cap.  
 
Growth curve of L. Lactis grown on GM17 medium spiked with 1% of Fructose (■) or Glucose (▲). The 
cells were in exponential growth phase when OD was between 0.2 – 1.0. 
From the growth curves, it appears that the lag-phase (initial phase with little growth) lasts longer 
for L. lactis cells in fructose medium. However in later experiments with L. lactis cells in fructose 
medium (#10, 11 & 12 in Table 3), the lag-phase was significantly shorter. These observations are 
likely related to a high variation in cellular growth.

scale, and using the following formula:
