Abstract
Traditionally fuels have been characterized compared to primary reference fuels
under standardised condition. It is attempted to establish an altenative method of
testing based on autoignition temperature sensitivity to excess air ratio. The tests
are conducted in 2 cylinder marine diesel engine modiﬁed to operate as Homogeneus
Charge Compression Ignition (HCCI). To determine the temperature accurately a
comprehensive investigation of cylinder temperature has been conducted. Methods
for manufacturing and installation of ultra ﬁne thermocouples has been developed
with succes. Methods for reconstructing the cylinder temperature have been investi-
gated with promising results.
Excess air ratios of 0.1 - 10 have been investigated to determine the igntion temper-
ature. It is found that for excess air ratios larger than 0.2 the ignition temperature
independent of excess air ratio. At excess air ratios below 0.2 there seems to be a
strong dependency. It has not been determined what is the cause of trend.
There is no clear indication that the the temperature of autoignition, can be utilized
to characterize fuels more generally than is the case with traditional testing methods.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The scope of this report is to investigate a method to characterise a fuel generally, i.e. test-
ing any fuel the same way so that all fuels can be measured against each other. The need
for such a method arises from the fact that a series of new fuels are available, which diﬀers
from the earlier concepts of gasoline and diesel fuels. Also advances in engine technology
calls for a more general testing.
Traditionally SI fuels have been characterised by the octane index and CI fuels have
been characterised by the cetane index. These methods are described further in appendix
A. The two methods are both conducted using the Cooperate Fuel Research (CFR) engine.
This can be modiﬁed to be an SI or CI engine depending on the cylinderhead. The ratings
are highly dependent on the conditions. The test conditions are therefore highly standard-
ised in order to obtain a basis of comparison. This depency is clear when considering the
two diﬀerent octane ratings for SI-fuels. Research Octane Number (RON) and Motoring
Octane Number (MON). The two ratings are both obtained in the CFR-engine but under
diﬀerent conditions. Typically RON > MON, though the diﬀerence varies with fuels [1].
As the rating of a speciﬁc fuel changes due to combustion conditions, errors arise when
combusted in a real engine under real conditions. Previously the error has been tolerable
since the engine technology was very similar to the CFR engine. This picture though has
changed signiﬁcantly with direct injection of SI engines and pilot injection of CI engines.
Also the introduction of Homogeneus Charge Compression ignition (HCCI) and Partially
Premixed Compression igniton (PPCI) disturbs the picture. It is therefore interesting to
investigate whether fuels can be described more generally, prefeably without reference fuels.
The tests are conducted in a Homogeneus Charge Compression Ignition (HCCI) engine
which is an engine principle that can be seen as a combination of the otto and diesel cycles.
As the name implies the charge is homogeneous, i.e. fuel and oxidiser are mixed before
injection or very early in the cycle, as is the case in the otto cycle. The charge is then
ignited by compression which is a characteristic from the diesel cycle. The physical engine
resembles a diesel engine in that a high compression ratio is necesary in order to obtain
the high temperatures to facilitate autoignition. A low excess air ratio (λ) would lead to
servere knocking which would be harmfull to the engine. λ is therefore kept high in order
to decrease the amount of fuel in the cylinder. Alternatively exhaust gas recirculation
(EGR) can be used to reduce the tendency of servere knock.
The advantage of the engine principle is that the charge burns rapidly since it autoignites
in several spots in the cylinder. Furthermore the lean burn keeps the combustion tempera-
ture low. The formation of nitrous oxides, which is known to contribute to global warming
and acid rain[2], are thereby eﬃciently reduced. The disadvantages are limited operational
range and high temperature dependency of autoignition [3]. It is this depency that it is
attempted to utilise to characterize the fuel.
In previous studies performed with ethanol it has been observed that the fuel ignites
ealier with later injection timing. This condition is known as Partially Premixed Compres-
sion ignition (PPCI). Figure 1 shows how the fuel ignites earlier for later injection until a
certain point where the ignition is delayed signiﬁcantly.

The hypothesis is that the autoignition temperature of the fuel is dependent on excess air

be used to characterize the ignition quality of the fuel.
The fuel used to test the hypothesis is N-heptane due to the well known characteristica
and exothermic cold ﬂame, which eases the determination of initiation of fuel cracking.

Background
2.1
Fuel characterisation
The primary characterisation of fuels are the octane and cetane indexes used for gasoline
and diesel fuels respectively. Similar for the two characterizations is that they are both
based on Primary Reference Fuels (PRF), where the rating is given by the percentage of
one of the reference fuels. Test are conducted in a Cooperate Fuel Research (CFR) engine
that are a highly standardised single cylinder engine with variable compression ratio. Two
diﬀerent cylinderheads are available to accomodate gasoline and diesel combustion. The
reference fuels of the octane scale are isooctane (2,2,4-trimethyl pentane) and N-heptane.
The rating is then given by the mixture of the two PRF that has the same ignition behaviour
under the speciﬁed conditions. I.e. if a fuel ignites similar to a mixture of 95% isooctane
and 5% N-heptane the fuel has octaneindex 95. This number varies with the testconditions.
As described in section 1 RON and MON are typically diﬀerent, where the diﬀerence is
used as a measure of the sensitivity to conditions. A more robust characterisation is the
Octane Index (OI) which is a relation cotaining both RON and MON. OI is though also
sensitive to conditions [4]. The PRFs of the cetane index are the paraﬁns n-hexadecane
with the value 100 and heptamethylnonane (HMN) with the value 15. This reason the
odd base is that HMN replaced alpha-methylnaphtalene which had cetane number zero.
Generally a high octane number indicates that a fuel can resist autoignition well while a
large cetane number indicates that a fuel autoignites easily. The test methods for RON,
MON and cetane indexes are described in appendix A.
2.2
Ignition delay
Ignition delay (τign) is the time it takes for a given fuel to ignite under certain conditions.
The ignition delay is indicated by the octane number and cetane number for otto- and diesel
engines respectively. In an otto engine this is indentiﬁed by the gab between the spark and
ignition of the fuel. In a diesel engine it is given by the time between injection and ignition.
The matter is slightly more complex in an HCCI engine which is used for this project. The
fuel is not expected nor wanted to ignite at the point of injection and there is no spark.
The fuel is heated after injection and at some point these conditions exceed a critical
value and the fuel ignites. This can be decribed chemically but is very computationally
expensive. The Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (LLNL) meachnism, which is
one of the simpler models of combustion of two-component fuels, includes 1034 species and
4238 reactions[4]. Furthermore there is a limit to the accuracy of the input parameters of
the chemical model. The primary reference fuels are well known in composition and even
though actual fuels have an average composition, for example ∼C8H18 for gasoline the
exact composition can vary depending on the origin of the crude oil. In 1955 Livengood
and Wu introduced the integral method which is an empirical model. In equation 2.1 the
integral method is expressed. As the integral reaches unity the fuel ignites.

2.4
Knock
To some extend the limiting factor of an engine’s performance is given by the oﬀset of
knocking. Knock is an uncontrolled autoignition which in some cases can be harmfull to
the engine. In an ideal SI engine, i.e. no leakage loss, a high compression ratio is desired.
Thereby there will be a greater potential of expansion.
The compression ratio is then
limited by the oﬀset of knocking. If the compression ratio is too large the temperature
during the compression stroke exceeds the autoignition limit and the fuel burns rapidly
before the spark. More often though knocking occurs after the spark where the unburned

Knock in diesel engines are less critical than in otto engines. This is due to the nature
of the cycle where the fuel is injected into the hot cylinder gases and ignites upon injection.
Autoignition is thereby a necessity of the diesel cycle. After ignition the fuel burn as a
diﬀusion ﬂame where knock can not occur since the fuel and oxidiser are separated on
each side of the ﬂame. As decribed in section 2.2 there is a delay between injection and
ignition. Fuel and air therefore has time to mix before it is ignited. Eﬀectively a small
premixed charge thereby autoignites and can be classiﬁed as knock though usually not as
servere. This eﬀect is the cause of the classic ’diesel noise’ which is almost extinct after
the introduction of pilot injections in diesel engines.
The HCCI cycle is as mentioned earlier a combination of the diesel and otto cycles.
Autoignition is a key part of the cycle, but since the charge is premixed, uncontrolled
autoignition can cause servere damage to key internal components. Knock is therefore a
matter of deﬁnition in an HCCI engine as there is no generic diﬀerence between normal
operation and knock.
The method of controlling knock in the HCCI engine is to limit the amount of fuel. The
easiest way to do this is maintaining a large excess air ratio. In this work it is desired
to determine the charachteristics at low excess air ratios. EGR is a promising approach,
but is not ﬁtted to the test engine. It would therefore be a project in itself to install an
EGR system on the engine. Furthermore a problem would arise when testing equivalence
ratios below unity since the residual gas would contain unburned hydrocarbon which would
disturb the picture. It is therefore decided to dilute the intake air with nitrogen in order
to reduce the amount of oxygen. This is done through the intake air heater which under

2.5
Thermocouple
It is for this project desired to determine the temperature of autoignition of a fuel. A
thorough investigation of the temperature conditions within the cylinder is therefore carried
out. Thermocouples are used due to their inexpensive and well proven design.
The working principle of thermocouples are given by the Seebeck eﬀect. It was ﬁrst
discovered by Thomas Johann Seebeck in 1821. He discovered that a compas needle was
deﬂected by a pair of dissimilar metals with two junctions at diﬀerent temperatures. See-
beck is believed to think that there was a connection to the earth’s magnetic ﬁeld[6]. It
is though now known that the electric potential is caused by the diﬀusion of free electrons
at the interface of the two metal wires which make up a thermocouple. [7].
As it often is with measuring equipment a thermocouple does not measure the tem-
perature of the ﬂuid in which it is submerged but rather it’s own temperature. This does
not pose a problem at constant or slow changing temperature. In the case of the engine it
is desired to measure the temperature on the order of magnitude of crank angle degrees.
Considering that the charge under compression is heated to ∼1000 K within ∼25 ms re-
sponse time becomes essential. In relation to reaction time the junction type plays a key
role. In general there are three diﬀerent types of junctions; Grounded, ungrounded and
exposed. The ungrounded is the most resistant as the junction is completely isolated from
the environment. The grounded hot junction is physically attached to the sheating improv-
ing heat transfer. This type though is more sensitive to any electrical activity disturbing
the signal. The fastest response time is achieved with an exposed hot junction since heat
is transfered directly to the point of measuring. This type though is more fragile than the
others [8]. Since fast response is needed in this project an exposed thermocouple is needed.
A key concept when dealing with thermocouples is the timeconstant, often denoted
τ. The timeconstant is given by equation 2.3, where ρ is the density of the thermocouple
material, V is the hot junction volume, cp is speciﬁc heat, A is the heat transfer area og the
hot junction and h is the convective heat transfer coeﬃcient. The time constant expresses
the amount of time it takes for the thermocouple to register 1/e of an instant temperature
change, where e is the base of the natural logarithm. In the case of a linearly changing
ﬂuid temperature, τ describes the time delay from the point where the ﬂuid reaches a
temperature x until the thermocouple reaches that same temperature x[9].

Method
3.1
Engine
The engine used to perform experiments is a Bukh DV24. It is a 2 cylinder 4 stroke natu-
rally aspirated marine engine with speciﬁcations as seen in appendix B. One cylinder has
been modiﬁed to test HCCI operations. Figure 5 shows a sketch of the test rig. The main
modiﬁcations are exhanging the mechanical diesel injector nozzle with a Siemens-VDO
piezo electric gazoline nozzle and adding exchangeable piston tops. Thereby new piston

3.2
Approach
It was desired to make a sweep of excess air ratios from very rich to very lean. Under normal
operation, stochiometric combustion would lead to servere knocking and propable failure
of the engine. The intake air is therefore diluted with compressed nitrogen. This reduces
the oxygen in the charge, thereby reducing the amount of fuel. Eﬀectively this is the same
eﬀect as obtained using Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR). Actual EGR is not used for
several reasons. Mainly that the engine is not ﬁtted with an EGR system. Also the fact
that in this study fuel rich mixtures have been tested introduces diﬃculties with regard to
EGR since a signiﬁcant amount of unburned fuel will be present in the recirculated exhaust
gas. The actual air fuel ratio will therefore be hard to determine accurately. There will

5.1
Temperature Reconstruction
The temperature derived by the least squares method is a fairly good approximation though
not very accurate. Under both conditions the trend is that the derived temperature is too
small in the beginning of compression. Later in the compression the temperaturerise rate
of the the thermocouples are close to that of the adiabatic temperature as seen in ﬁgure 28.
This indicates that the thermocouples are reacting too slow in the beginning where turbu-
lence and temperature diﬀerence is low. later in the compression the turbulence increases
thus the thermocouples react faster. Due to the lower turbulence at the beginning of the
compression the time constant is larger. During the compression the turbulence increases,
which increases the convective heat transfer. This corelates well with the expected error
due to the assumed constant time constant. It is also seen that, as expected, the smaller
thermocouple reaches the highest rate of temperature rise before the larger, though only
slightly. This is also expected due to the relative small diﬀerence between the two hot
junctions.

5.2
Autoignition
The tendency is generally that the cold ﬂame autoignition temperature is unaﬀected by
excess air ratio. It though appears that very rich mixtures ignite at a lower temperature
than leaner mixtures. From ﬁgure 16 it is seen that if the measurements where λ < 0.2
is left out the tendency is roughly linear. Figure 17 also shows that these measurements
deviate from a clear tendency of larger ignition pressure for richer mixtures. The interest-
ing question is therefore whether these tendencies are actual or a result of an unconsidered
eﬀect. The system becomes very complex for rich mixtures. There will be a signiﬁcant
amount of unburned hydrocarbon and intermediate species in the residual gas. It is plausi-
ble that these species can interfere with the components of the fresh charge. The pressure
can also play a role in the low temperature ignition of rich mixtures. The ignition delay is
typically modelled with a linealy dependency to pressure and an exponential dependency
to temperature. Temperature is therefor considered the main factor. At very rich mixtures
the eﬀective heat capacity ratio is smaller which causes a slower temperaturerise with pres-
sure. As the pressure increases the ignition delay decreases at a lower temperature than is
the case for leaner mixtures.

DISCUSSION
5.2
Autoignition
The residual gas is a key factor when determining the temperature in the cylinder. It
calculated that the residual gas ratio is ∼20%, which is considered high. The sensitivity
analysis shows that the slight tendency of a higher ignition temperature under stochiomet-
ric conditions vanishes with a lower level of residual gas. The issue of residual gases could
be reduced by supercharging. The Exhaust system could furthermore be equipped with a
suction system, which could eﬀectively ﬂush the cylinder. The initial temperature could
thereby be determined to a higher degree of accuracy, resulting in a better estimate of the
temperature.
The hot ﬂame shows a greater sensitivity to excess air ratio as it for slightly lean to
rich mixtures approaches the cold ﬂame ignition temperature. It is though diﬃcult to
say anything conclusive about the hot ﬂame ignition behaviour. This comes down to the
deﬁnition of the hot ﬂame. A distinction between the hot and cold ﬂame can be done as
the cold ﬂame is cracking of the fuel, while the hot ﬂame is the actual oxidation of the
fuel. It is clear that the two approaches each other and starts overlapping when decreasing
excess air ratio. When they overlap it can though be diﬃcult to determine the point of
hot ﬂame ignition since the cracking of fuel is still releasing energy.
There is not a clear indication that the cold ﬂame ignition can be used to characterize
the fuel. It is though plausible that the relationship between the hot and cold ﬂames can
be utilized. There is though the diﬃculty of determining the ignition of the hot ﬂame.
There is therefore a non-neglicible risk that a potential altenative characterization method
will be inconstistent or overly complicated, plausibly both.
The eﬀect of early ignition at late injection in the PPCI engine can not be unam-
biguously explained by a lower ignition temperature at near stochiometric conditions. An
explanation could be that at with later injection the temperature at injection increases.
The fuel will thereby evaporate and cool the compressed charge. When injection is de-
layed further the piston approaches the injector nozzle. The fuel will therefore be injected
directly on to the piston which transfers heat signiﬁcantly better than gas. The energy
of vaporization is thereby taken from the piston and not the cylinder gas. The advance
in igniton timing will then not be an eﬀect of a λ sensitive ignition temperature but a
thermodynamic eﬀect.

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
A method for determining the temperature during a cycle in a 4 stroke diesel engine has
been investigated. A satisfactory result has not been obtained, but signiﬁcant issues have
been solved. Among these are manufacturing of ultra ﬁne thermocouples of 12.5 and 25
µm diameter wire and the installation of these in the engine. Various methods to recon-
struct the temperature has been investigated. They all require a number of assumptions.
The method with the least controversial assumption is the frequency domain reconstrution
which shows potential.
It has not been possible to unambigiously determine a sensitivity of ignition temper-
ature to excess air ratio. There is though indications that the fuel ignites at lower tem-
perature and pressure and with a shorter induction time at very rich mixtures (λ < 0.2).
Further experiments has to be made to determine whether this is a characteristic of the
fuel or an engine speciﬁc eﬀect, such as easy ignitable residual gases. At excess air ratios
larger than 0.2 there are strong indications that the ignition temperature is independent
of excess air ratio.
Knock control by nitrogen dilution of the intake charge has been used with good results.
The engine has been run at stochiometric conditions in HCCI mode, which without dilution
is a possible cause of failure. The increased nitrogen concentration does not aﬀect the
autoignition temperature.

PERSPECTIVES
7
Perspectives
For future work it would be interesting to investigate whether a pair of thermocouples with
a larger size diﬀerence would improve temperature reconstruction. The frequency domain
reconstruction is the most promising, as it reproduces the most convincing temperature
proﬁle. A reliable method to determine temperature would allow to investigate uniformity
of the gas temperature across the cylinder volume. The occurence of hot and cold spots
could thereby be identiﬁed and addressed.
There is no clear sign that the ignition temperature is a better mean of charachterizing
fuels than the conventional methods. Future work could though investigate the apparent
change in ignition behaviour for very rich mixtures.

C.2
Welding
The fast response bare thermocouples are welded on to a larger thermocouple to ease
the handling and strength. It is done with a Spempley Technical Products thermocouple
welding apparatus. It is a capacitance welding unit which means it stores a predeﬁned
charge in a series of capacitors which is discharged as the circuit is closed.
The larger thermocouples are desheathed with a grinder, the hot junction is removed and
the two wires and the sheathing are isolated from each other with a heat resistant cement.
Between each step it is controlled that the wires have not short- circuited.
C.2.1
Apparatus
The unit consits of a so called power pack, which contains the capacitors, adjustors and
outlet, a microscope mounted with two pairs of pliers to ﬁxate the thermocouple and an
electrode. The thermocouple is positively charged and as the electrode aproaches an elec-
tric arc melts the two components which thereby merges. Furthermore an electronic valve
is used to suply argon in order to create an inert atmosphere for the weld.
C.2.2
Procedure
In the instruction manual provided by the manufacturer it is informed that it is possible to
weld wires of similar and dissimilar materials and diameters. After some testing it though
turns out that a series of problems arises when the wires are on the order of magnitude of
10 µm and the diameters diﬀerentiate by a factor of ∼20.

In order to obtain a satisfactory weld the larger wire is supposed to melt and create a ball

is a sign of incomplete fusion which is sigﬁcantly weaker than a proper weld[22]. A sketch
of this is displayed in ﬁgure 30.
A problem arises when the small and large wire are subject to the same energy. In that
case the small wire burns and the larger is more or less unaﬀected. To avoid this problem
only the larger wire is charged. This improves the performance to some extend. Another
problem of the ﬁne wire welding, is that a normal force between the two wires a needed
in order to obtain a prober weld. If the two wires a just touching each other, the surface
tension of the molden wire is large enough to repel the smaller wire. Therefore in order to
create a normal force the smaller wire is pressed against a ceramic tile by the larger wire.
Besides providing a normal force this results in further cooling of the ﬁne wire. Using this
method the two highly dissimilar wires can be welded together.
It was also attempted to cool the wire further by putting a water soaked papertowel between
the tile and thermocouples. This increases the likelyhood of a good weld, but introduces
a lot of problems regarding handling of the thermocouples. On that basis this method is
discarded.
In ﬁgure 31 the ﬁnished thermocouples are seen. It is noted that the hot junction is
shifted towards one of the larger wires. This is the ﬁrst weld made. When the wire is
melted the surface tension creates the ball at the tip of the wire. The surface tension
of this molten sphere is great enough to consume the ﬁne wire. In the second weld the
ﬁne wire is better ﬁxed and the eﬀect is therefore smaller. Though it is not pretty this
asymmetry does not make a diﬀerence to the measurements. This problem can though be
solved by ﬁxating the ﬁne wire so that it can not move across the tile.
C.2.3
Practical experiences
When welding thermocouples of this nature the author has gained some practical expe-
riences. For geometric purposes it is diﬁcult to charge the wire at the end which is to
be welded. Instead the wire is to be charged at the connector end. This is additional
beneﬁcial since the loss is highly dependent on the wire length. This eﬀect makes to some
extend up for the fact that the aparatus was designed for larger diameter thermocouples
and therefore has, for this purpose, a rather crude voltage control.
Another consideration is that the electrode should be approached the larger wire axially.

DETERMINING POINT OF IGNITION
D
Determining Point of Ignition
Determining the point of ignition generally and accurately. Often the heat release curve
or apparent heat release curve are modiﬁed to make the start and end of the heat release
more distinct. This can be done by applying an artiﬁcially large value of the heat capacity
ratio, γ. This method works best for heat release curves which are relatively similar. Using
CA10 smoothens out some of the uncertainty in the determination of the point of ignition
and the result is generally satisfactory. In this work it is desired to know the temperature
at the point of ignition which is diﬀerent from the one at CA0. In combination with the
very wide range excess air ratios (0.1 ≤λ ≤10) it is considered unfeasible from a time
perspective to produce a code that can determine the point of hot and cold ﬂame ignition
accurately. The hot and cold ﬂame ignition are therefor determined manually from the
AHRR and HR curves for rich and lean conditions respectively.
