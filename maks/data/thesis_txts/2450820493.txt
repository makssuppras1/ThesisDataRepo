Preface
This M.Sc. thesis is a result of ﬁve-month study in the ﬁeld of the thermal engineering.
Purpose of the thesis is the investigation of the potential of high-temperature heat pumps in
combination with renewable sources in a techno-economic perspective. It was conducted
between 02-01-2019 and 17-06-2019 and it corresponds in 32.5 ECTS points. It was carried out
under the supervision of Professor Brian Elmegaard, PostDoc Fabian Bühler and Consultant
in Teknlogisk Institut Benjamin Zühlsdorf. This thesis was prepared at DTU Mekanik, Section
of Thermal Energy in fulﬁllment of the requirements for acquiring a Master of Science in
Engineering.
Lyngby, 17-June-2019

Abstract
Anthropogenic climate change is a global phenomenon which has recently become more
widely recognized. The EU’s climate policies and regulations are explicitly focused on the
reduction of greenhouse emissions and carbon-neutrality. Reﬂections of these, could not but
be found in process heat demand in industry. Around 30% of process heat is in the range of 100
◦C to 400 ◦C and is mainly fuelled by fossil fuel combustion. For that reason, an investigation of
high-temperature heat pumps operating in the stated temperature range provides promising
opportunities for a ’green’ transition of industry.
This study has, therefore, presented the concept of Reversed Brayton Cycle for a case study
concerning reﬁnery processes. Through waste heat recovery, the cycle could provide heat up
to 370 ◦C to the crude.
The heat pumps conﬁgurations were considered to be coupled with renewable electricity
sources for access to electricity generation with lower levelized costs. In this context, wind
turbines, photovoltaics and heat storage with increased capacity of heat pump were proven to
apply economic beneﬁts to such a project.
A renewable electricity based energy mix with Reversed Brayton Cycles outperformed biogas
and electric boiler based solutions. Despite the fact that it was found to be relatively inferior
to natural gas and biomass boilers, it was designated that they could be competitive to those
in possible future scenarios.
Concerning Reversed Brayton Cycle parameters, it was pointed out that the economic analysis
of a thermodynamically optimized heat pump does not deviate much from economic opti-
mum. Assumptions on the pinch point of heat exchangers of the cycle were found to be fair
and the isentropic efﬁciency of the compressor and turbine was inﬂuentiial on the economic
indicators. The feasibility of the project was mostly sensitive to electricity costs from the grid.
Due to fouling issues with heat exchanging with crude, the study could not be perceived
as a completely feasibility one but rather highlighted the potentials of high-temperature
heat pumps in the considered case study. However, the conclusions could be linked with
electricity-based heat supply for various industrial applications.

1 Introduction
This chapter presents, at ﬁrst, the motivation behind the presented project and
key aspects of high temperature heat demand in industry. Then follows a short
analysis of projects and studies in high temperature heat pumps and optimization
of micro-grids.
1.1
Background and Motivation
As greenhouse gas emissions are held mainly responsible for the effect of global warming, the
EU has imposed policies and regulations on climate and energy. In 2030’s climate and energy
framework the goal is set to 40% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions from the level of
those in 1990 [1]. In that context, Denmark has decided to put the stakes even higher. Energy
Strategy of 2050 contemplates a Denmark future completely independent from fossil fuels [2].
For that reason continuous research is ongoing for the removal and replacement of fossil fuels.
Many and different renewable technologies took place over the years in energy mix, mainly
in electricity production, and society is gradually moving towards a future with electriﬁed
systems based on renewable sources. Concerning heat production, heat pumps are a highly
attractive alternative based on electriﬁcation which could substitute boilers and furnaces.As
that, heat pumps are already adopted to a large extent in the residential sector – more than
one million units were sold in year 2018 . In the same year only 577 heat pumps were designed
for industrial purposes proving there is still a challenge for heat pump technology to inﬁltrate
in high-temperature applications [3].
The main reason could be traced on the fact, that high condensing temperatures will lead
to low Coefﬁcients Of Performance (COP) . With such COP values High-Temperature Heat
Pumps (HTHP) , characterized by sink temperature greater than 100◦C [4], could be viable and
feasible only if the electricity input price is low. For that to happen, heat pumps should be
considered in combination with large shares of renewable sources in electricity production, as
they are the only technologies taking the marginal price of electricity on lower levels.
A future like that, is already envisioned and captured by EU’s long-term strategy for 2050 for a
climate neutral economy, where HTHPs could play an important role and completely cover
for fossil fuels in industrial processes [5]. Considering the allurement of HTHPs, International
Energy Agency (IEA) have already conducted through Annex 35 an investigation in industrial
heat pump technologies and in a market overview to provide information on policy makers [6].

==PAGE:19==
Chapter 1. Introduction
Due to the attractiveness of Annex 35, the project continues on an ongoing campaign through
Annex 48 for large scale market introduction of industrial heat pumps which designates the
importance and the potential of HTHPs [7].
1.1.1
High Temperature Heat Demand in Industry
The potential of HTHPs integration in industry originates from the requirements of heat in
high temperatures. Various studies have been conducted that map the energy demand in
industry to each sector and also to temperature level intervals.
Naegler et al. [8] conducted a study through statistical analysis to determine the heat demand
in industry on the European level. For a temperature range of 100 ◦C to 400 ◦C in process heat,
they identiﬁed that there is an actual heat demand of around 2200 PJ in EU28 in 2012 while
mostly on that, contributed heavy industry related countries such as Germany, France, Italy
and UK. That amount reﬂects on the 27 % of total heat demand including not only process
heating but also space heating and hot water. In Denmark, the amount was 21.3 PJ which was
equivalent to 31 % of heat demand.
With focus on national level, Bühler et al. [9] showed a heat demand in danish industry of
around 11.3 PJ in the temperature interval of 155 ◦C to 265 ◦C. They divided the demand
between industry sectors and notable references on that interval would be industries with
subject as concrete and brick, gravel and stones, and dairies and other food industries. By
grouping industries into wider groups, they displayed that the largest share of heat demand
is above 150 ◦C mainly in building services followed by food, metal, chemical and pulp and
paper industries.
In that context, Bühler et al. [10] made an exergy analysis in danish industry sector and
designated the importance of electriﬁcation of industrial process through heat pumps and
organic rankine cycles for waste heat utilization, while Huang et al. [11], through mapping of
danish industrial energy, illustrated that although most of the waste heat is below 50 ◦C, there
is a considerable amount between 50 ◦C and 100 ◦C and a minor part between 160 ◦C and 250
◦C which could be utilized in HTHPs.
As such, Zühlsdorf et al. [12] highlighted the potentials of HTHPs above 150 ◦C, provided
two different thermodynamic cycles, while they identiﬁed candidate applications in different
kinds of industry.
This study is on a large extend built around [12] and aims to designate the importance of
integration of heat pumps on temperature levels higher than 150 ◦C.
1.2
State of the Art
1.2.1
High Temperature Heat Pumps
Studies and research in the ﬁeld of HTHPs, so far, have demonstrated technical feasibility for
supply temperatures up to 160◦C. Most of them focus on single-stage HTHPs with Internal
Heat eXchanger (IHX) . Helminger et al. [13] investigated a single-stage HTHP with IHX using
R1336mzz(Z) with a temperature at the sink close to 160◦C. In the same cycle and refrigerant,
Fleckl et al. [14] achieved a sink temperature at 150◦C. Noack [15] developed a laboratory
heat pump with sink temperature at 140◦C using a low GWP refrigerant. At the same sink
2

1.2. State of the Art
temperature, Peureux and Bobelin [16] designed a HTHP with a refrigerant mixture including
R245fa.
Steam compression systems have also been analyzed for high temperature applications. Kaida
et al. [17] investigated the performance on a system that steam is generated in a bottom
cycle and upgraded to higher temperature by turbochargers reaching a temperature of 165◦C.
Meroni et al. [18] compared different high-temperature cycles of a cascade heat pump for
generation of steam at 150◦C. They showed that direct compression of water in two-stages

Angelino and Invernizzi [20] showed that reversed Brayton cycle could be designed with

General Electrics (GE) [21] proposed a reversed Brayton cycle using R744 in supercritical
conditions.
In this study, HTHP is based on Reversed Brayton Cycle and it will be considered for application
greater than 150◦C.
1.2.2
Optimization in HTHPs with storage in Micro-grids
Philibert [22] made an analysis where it was shown that there is great potential for the energy
use in industry to be provided by renewable technologies through electricity generation. It is
designated that these technologies could replace fossil fuels and power up HTHP technologies
for production of heat. Zühlsdorf et al. [12] showed in a pre-feasibility study that such
combination of technologies, HTHP with renewable electricity sources of wind power and
photovoltaic, could be proven feasible and very attractive for implementation. In that aspect,
HTHPs are considered in this study to be optimized in combination with wind and photovoltaic
systems with the existence of thermal storage.
State of the art material and studies considering HTHP in optimization schemes in combina-
tion with storages and renewable electricity is limited. Wallerand et al. [23] presented a set of
multi-period Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) of equations for solar technologies,
solar thermal and photovoltaic, and superstructure for optimization of a heat pump cycle for
industrial application. A scenario they considered for optimization included an optimized
heat pump cycle conﬁguration coupled with photovoltaic electricity generation. Becker [24],
through multi-period and batch problem deﬁnition developed a methodology for optimizing
the integration of heat pumps in industrial non-continuous processes with the use of heat
storage.
On the contrary, in residential applications, Renaldi et al. [25] developed through MILP, an
optimization framework for integration of thermal energy storage in residential heat pump.
Facci et al. [26] constructed an optimization model for integration of heat pumps coupled with
photovoltaic production and thermal storage for residential application. Four energy power
plant solutions were assessed and compared with a reference one. As outcome, they showed
that with the use of heat pumps, energy costs are reduced by 41%, while CO2 emissions

1.2.3
Objectives and Approach
Based on the above, this project aims to analyse the energetic performance of HTHPs with
Reversed Brayton Cycle, over 150◦C. The HTHPs are considered then to be coupled with electric
renewable sources and heat storage formulating an optimization problem in terms of installed
capacity of each technology. The whole project will be evaluated from a techno-economic
perspective and be compared with alternative scenarios utilizing different technologies.
All the above will point in the main objective of the project. That would be, the evaluation
of the feasibility of HTHPs in combination with renewable electricity sources in comparison
with conventional technologies in practice, such as furnaces.
As such, the tasks necessary to reach the objective of the study, are as below:
• Thermodynamic modeling of Reversed Brayton Cycles and optimization of COP.
• Cost determination of Reversed Brayton Cycle.
• Build an economic model for a project involving. HTHPs, wind tubine, photovoltaics
and heat storage.
• Determination of optimum capacities of each technology in terms of feasibility of the
project.
• Comparison of the project with other scenarios considering different technologies.
• Analysis of the effect of key parameters and assumptions on the feasibility of the project.
In order to facilitate calculations and discussion, a case study has been formulated concerning
reﬁnery processes. Reversed Brayton Cycles are designed, so that crude oil is heated with the

2.1

2.1.1
Crude Preheat Train and Distillation
Crude oil preheat trains are designed to reduce energy consumption in terms of fuel combus-
tion. A simpliﬁed ﬂow sheet of a crude preheat train and distillation is given in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1 – Simpliﬁed ﬂow-sheet of Crude Preheat Train and Distillation.
Petroleum recovered from a reservoir contains many substances such as gases, water, chloride
salts and dirt which contain even more minerals. The effect of these on downstream processes
could be proven destructive as they are linked with scale formation, corrosion, and catalyst
deactivation. As such, they have to be removed by passing the crude oil through a desalting
process in so-called desalters [32].
After removing the salt content, crude oil is heated in the Preheat Heat Exchanger Network
(HEN) . In a series of heat exchangers, heat from distillation cuts is transferred to crude oil
with a dual positive effect. On one side crude is heated such that less fuel has to be burned in
6
==PAGE:24==
2.1. Case Study
the furnace in order to reach the desired temperature and, on the other side, the petroleum
fractions are further cooled to the desired temperature for storage.
The crude is then heated in the Atmospheric Distillation Unit(ADU) furnace to a temperature
range between 390-400◦C before entering to a fractionating column operating at close to
atmospheric pressure, wherein the gas fraction and several liquid fractions with different
boiling points are separated off [33]. The top and lighter products are extracted ﬁrst, followed
by increasingly heavier ones. Whatever is left is characterized as atmospheric residue.
The remnants of atmospheric distillation can provide further valuable distillates which would
otherwise be destroyed if further distillation was attempted in atmospheric pressure or above.
Thus, after the residue is heated again in a furnace to between 360◦C to 370◦C. It then enters
a column which operates in vacuum, called Vacuum Distillation Unit(VDU) , from which
Vacuum Gas Oil (VGO) is extracted. Whatever is left after the extraction forms the vacuum
residue which cannot be further processed. [33].
In this project the furnace before the ADU is to replaced with HTHPs leading to partwise
electriﬁcation of the crude preheat train process and the removal of its most polluting compo-
nents.

2.2.1
Crude Oil Selection - True Boiling Point (TBP) Curve
Ideally, reﬁneries for the purpose of producing the best-possible quality distillates have to use
light crude oils at their distillation sites. Nowadays, because of shifts in price of crude oils and
demand in distillation products, reﬁneries are forced to reduce the costs of their crude feed
stock. For the sake of reducing thus expenses, in terms of utilized crude, they typically employ
a mix between low and high quality crude to balance the associated costs [35].
Data presented in the above tables, considering crude and distillation fraction streams, all
refer to the distillation of a blended crude mixture. Because of the difﬁculties to trace back
the original crudes, which constitute each blend, it has to be assumed that the crude is purely
from one drilling site.
The crude oil selected for this project is of medium to high quality, with an API gravity of 52.5
and it is an assay used by the company ExxonMobil [36] by the name Aasgaard Blend. API
gravity is a measure of comparison of the weight of the crude oil with water. The higher the
value, the lower the weight of the crude and higher the quality is. Also, it has to be pointed out
that the choice of an assay is more or less made arbitrarily and ExxonMobil’s data are just used
because they provide TBP values as an addition for the generalized distillation fraction groups
considered as well. The latest assay report is provided in Appendix A and could be accessed
via [37].
TBP curves are the product of TBP distillation and they characterize the volatility of petroleum
fractions in crude oil. It consists a plot of Natural Boiling Point (NPB) , in other words boiling
temperature, and percentage of volume of sample distilled and they are used as basis for
characterization of crude oil or a petroleum cut for the purpose of design and analysis. The
procedure of TBP distillation is standardized and for information the reader should refer to
specialized references such as [38]. The respective TBP curve of Aasgaard Blend crude is given
in Figure 2.2.

2.3
Reversed Brayton Cycle
In contrast to Brayton Cycle, where the main purpose is to produce power, the Reversed
Brayton cycle aims to use power to move heat from a colder to a hotter body and works as a
heat pump. It stands out in comparison with conventional refrigeration cycles as, instead of
using an expansion valve, the working ﬂuid is expanded in a turbine. The turbine is mounted
in the same shaft as the compressor and expansion work is recovered so that less electricity is
imported in the compression process. In order that a turbine is utilized, the whole process
operates in supercritical conditions and CO2 is considered as a working ﬂuid. Because of the
high temperature application which involve a large temperature lift between source and sink,
the pressure ratios are relative high which indicate potential in the mounting of a turbine and
use of Reversed Brayton Cycle. A simpliﬁed schematic is presented in Figure 2.5.
The Internal Heat Exchanger exists so that the gas is cooled after the sink so that, after ex-
pansion, it is at the appropriate temperature levels, to receive heat from the source. Then
the gas exiting the gas heater re-enters the IHX and is heated so that, after compression, it
reaches appropriate temperature levels for it to deliver heat to the sink. From this, it follows
that, as more heat is exchanged internally the less power is consumed in the compression
process. More heat exchanged would also mean that less power is produced during expansion.
The power consumption in the compression process will always be greater than the power
production from expansion as it could also be observed in the isentrope curves at different
temperature levels. In Figure 2.6 there is a log(p)-h ﬁgure for a cycle as that one presented in
Figure 2.5. The further right the process is moved compared to the "bell" saturation region
the slope of the isentropes gets less steeper. Thus, for the same isentropic efﬁciency in the
expander and compressor, compression work is always larger and more effective. In Figure 2.6
in relevance with Figure 2.5, 1→2 stand for compression while 4→5 for expansion.

Sub-scenario A
The rationale behind the formulation of such a conﬁguration lies on the indirect application of
heat between the distillation fractions and crude. Through a Heat Exchanger Network (HEN) a

then heats the crude, through another HEN, to the desired temperature. The main advantage
of such a conﬁguration is that only one heat pump is needed, which substantially decreases
the investment costs. A schematic representation of the above can be observed in Figure 2.7.

Wind Turbines
Technical Characteristics
The energy output of wind turbine is mostly governed by the wind speed at the hub height. In
very low wind speeds, the wind can not be utilized to rotate the blades of the turbine and there
is no production of electricity. The wind speed that is referred to that limit, is called the cut-in
speed. After that point, with increase in wind speed more electricity is produced until a certain
point where the maximum power output is reached. The wind speed at that point, is called
rated wind speed. By controlling the angle of the blades the turbine continues to operate to
its maximum output. At very high wind speeds though, the turbine is shut off due to safety
reasons. The wind speed at which this happens is referred to as the cut-out wind speed.
The output of a wind turbine is approximated by the power curve. This curve designates the
percentage of power output of the turbine in terms of its rated capacity. A typical power curve
is presented in Figure 2.9.
Figure 2.9 – Power curve characteristics of a wind turbine
As previously mentioned, the driving force for wind turbine electricity production is the wind
speed. Monitoring of the wind speed is done globally on different sites and heights in order to
accurately predict wind power generation.
The wind speed, along with other weather data, is taken from MERRA-2 tool [59] which does an
atmospheric reanalysis through satellite imaging which was accessed via the database of [60].
Atmospheric reanalysis consists a mesoscale model which would mean that downscales form
global observations to regional [61]. Microscale modeling on the other side is used to transfer
from regional to local conditions [62]. As such, local weather conditions are not accounted for
as for example the effect of the wake of one turbine to the other. The location at which data
were extracted was Kalundborg, Denmark, where a reﬁnery resides owned by Equinor [63]
with a latitude of 55.683◦north and a longitude of 11.099◦east.

Photovoltaics
Technical Characteristics
Photovoltaic panels, unlike wind turbines, rely on solar energy to produce electricity. They
utilize the photovoltaic effect on semiconductor materials in order to convert light density
to electricity. That would mean that their production is prone to frequent discontuinities.
In the day period as there is sun, photovoltaics will produce electric energy while of course
that cannot happen at night. Thus, in countries like Denmark they consist a supplementary
renewable electricity technology to wind turbines where they will cover for sunny days with
not so much wind.
The physical quantity that mostly could describe the potential of a photovoltaic panel in
electricity production is the Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI). The solar beams can either
travel straight from the sun to a surface and be called ’direct normal radiation’, or the sunlight
can be scattered by particles in the atmosphere but still make it to earth’s surface as ’diffuse
radiation’. The sum of these quantities on a horizontal surface is equivalent to global horizontal
radiation. The units of global horizontal radiation are the rate of energy transfer per square
meter (W /m2).
In order though to take full advantage of the solar irradiance horizontal placement of photo-
voltaic panels could not be advised. They should be placed with a certain angle from earth
surface, ’tilt angle’, but also with a certain orientation, ’azimuth angle’, which is usually close
to south for the northern hemisphere and close to the north for the southern one. On top
of these, on a tilted surface, the radiation that is reﬂected of the earth’s ground could also
contribute is electricity production.
The procedure which was followed on this project was the optimization of total solar radiation
incident on tilted surface during a year with respect to the tilt and azimuth angles. The
methodology used is based on [68] and is also used by the commercial software HOMER [69].
For details on this aspect the reader should refer to the Appendix C section and in the above
references.
For the optimization as objective function was handled the summation of solar incident on
tilted surface of all time steps and goal was the maximization of it. The problem consists
non-linear both in the objective and constraint equations and Matlab was used along with it’s
built-in Genetic Algorithm solver from Global Optimization Toolbox [70].
The results indicate 37.2◦tilt angle and azimuth angle of 1.12◦from south to east. These are in
agreement with [71] which indicate a tilt angle of 36◦for Copenhagen area.

4 Discussion
This chapter presents a discussion on key subjects of the project.
4.1
Reversed Brayton cycle optimization
The Reversed Brayton Cycle optimization based on the COP was performed in respect to
three decision parameters; the high and low pressure of the cycle and degrees of superheat at
the outlet of the turbine. The constraints considering the optimization were formulated, so
that the low pressure could be from atmospheric to as high as the critical pressure of carbon
dioxide indicated by the peak of ’bell’ region in log(p)-h diagram and close to 70 bar. The high
pressure on the sink-side was considered to be higher than 70 bar and lower than 140 bar, due
to limitations in the compressor. Lastly, the degrees of superheat were just constrained to be
larger than 5 K to ensure gas state of carbon dioxide.
Within the ranges given for each decision parameter, there could be combinations which could
lead to infeasible solutions. For that reason, it was inevitable that algorithms highly sensitive
to local optima were used. Such algorithms for example, could be Conjugate Gradients or
Steepest Descent. The procedure, involved setting an initial guess of parameters, resetting to
another guess and optimizing the heat pump through trial and error methodology.
The issue of using algorithms, which are less sensitive to local optima, is that they have to
search in the solution space where infeasible solutions exist. For instance, Genetic Algorithms
will evaluate a combination of parameters within a generation which could be infeasible and
thus, they will not converge. The same could happen to Matlab’s ’fmincon’ function, which
would transfer to infeasible space in search of global optimum.
To streamline the procedure of Reversed Brayton optimization a mapping of the feasible
combinations of the three decision parameters should be done, so that the aforementioned
algorithms could function and probably lead to a global optima.
4.2
Isentropic efﬁciency on compressor and turbine and part load
The isentropic efﬁciency in both the compressor and the turbine was assumed, throughout
simulations, to be equal to 75 %. This would consist a conservative value but it could not be
compared with existing systems’ documentation to verify the fairness of this assumption.
Regarding heat storage implementation, the heat pump was assumed to be able to operate on
part load conditions along which the COP was assumed to be constant. In reality, the perfor-

mance of the compressor in part load may be different leading to different COP. Nevertheless,
due to lack of documentation at the conditions under which the system is studied and of linear

4.3
Mass presence of ﬂuids inside piping system
During the cost modeling of heat storage, it was estimated that an important part would be
the inventory of transfer ﬂuids in source- and sink-sides. For that reason, the cost of purchase
of the mass of heat transfer ﬂuids was approximated.
However, except for the heat transfer ﬂuid accumulation inside the tank, there would be heat
transfer ﬂuid presence inside the piping of the HENs but also working ﬂuid in the piping of
the Reversed Brayton Cycle. The cost of these, to be purchased for the operation of the whole
conﬁguration, was not accounted for. A more detailed design would be required, one which
also considers the volume of the piping system and the velocity of the ﬂuids inside of it.
4.4
Uncertainties in cost estimation
Cost estimation correlations for the capital cost of components were used in order to deter-
mine the cost of HTHPs and storage tanks costs. Although in many cases the scaling parameter
of correlations exceeded the suggested range, it was ensured that the correlations used fol-
lowed economies of scale. A more detailed analysis of correlations considered and used is
presented in Appendix D.
A more precise analysis on the economics of such conﬁgurations would require contact with
relevant manufacturers so that more accurate data could be used.
4.5
HENs with HTHPs
The HENs on both source and sink side were designed with 7.5 K pinch point temperature.
The mass ﬂow of the heat transfer ﬂuid on the source-side was selected as such, to provide the
highest-possible COP for the heat pump.
The pinch point choice of the HEN will affect the HEN cost, but also on the cost of HTHP
and COP of it. On the other side, the same would happen with the choice of mass ﬂow at
source-side, as it would also affect the inventory cost of the heat transfer ﬂuid for heat storage
applications.
The determination of the minimum pinch point temperature, usually takes into account the
cost of utilities but also the capital investment of heat exchangers. Utility costs are approxi-
mately proportional to ∆Tmin, while capital costs decrease more sharply. The optimum will be
deﬁned as in Figure 4.1 where the total costs, are minimized [94].
In reﬁnery processes with a targeting on Utility and Capital cost Linnhoff has presented
a suggested value of ∆Tmin around 30 ◦C [95]. This value, though could not be suggested
when HTHPs are integrated. The cost of HEN and utilities to cool down the distillation
fraction streams should be evaluated together with HTHPs integration to determine an optimal
minimum temperature difference in designing HENs for HTHPs application.

4.6
Fouling on HXs with crude oil
As mentioned in subsection 2.1.1, after the desalter, where inorganic salts are removed, crude
oil is preheated in a series of heat exchangers before it enters the furnace and be further heated.
Typically, the temperature range at the inlet of the furnace, named as coil inlet temperature
(CIT), is around 240◦C to 270◦C and at the outlet around to 365◦C [96]. The crude oil is
preheated before it enters the ADU, so that the viscosity is reduced and improves upstream
separation process.
In this industry, crude oil preheating furnaces are considered the most energy consuming
devices [97]. It is thus, essential that CIT is increased as far as possible, so that less fuel is
consumed in the furnace. Several studies in retroﬁtting and revamping the existing HEN are
conducted, so that CIT is further increased. Bulasara et al. [98] achieved a CIT of 260◦C, while
Aseeri et al. [99] 295◦C in their revamping studies. Those studies do not consider the fouling
mitigation problem in heat exchangers.
Fouling is a very complex phenomenon and is prone to various mechanisms. Models have
been produced to predict fouling, so that mitigation strategies can be developed. An overview
of existed models and description of phenomenon mechanisms is given in [100].
Ebert et al. [101] designated that fouling in industrial heat exchanger equipment is a result of
many factors and they identiﬁed that mainly the temperature and the velocity of crude in heat
exchangers are responsible. Thus, they produced the fouling threshold curve, as depicted in
Figure 4.2 The areas where fouling happens are highlighted.
61

In that context, further retroﬁtting studies including the fouling mitigation were conducted.
Wang et al. [102] with retroﬁt reached 243◦C CIT, Wilson et al. [103] 253◦C, while others 271◦C
[104]. Polley et al. addressed fouling limits in retroﬁtting HEN designs and showed a revamped

Effects of fouling have also been investigated in furnaces, such as in sources like [106] and
[107]. Unlike in heat exchangers, losses in heat transfer due to fouling are compensated for by
the large driving force produced by the radiant heat provided by the burners [96].
As fouling consists an important issue in HXs with crude oil, a more detailed cost analysis
should be conducted to determine the cleaning costs. This was not part of this project,
and should be included in a more detailed feasibility study in HTHPs integration in reﬁnery
industry.
4.7
State of the art HXs
Shell and Tube heat exchangers are the most common types used in reﬁnery applications
[108]. Consequently, the most fouling studies refer to these types of heat exchangers. Based
on the fouling issues, manufacturers have built a series of state-of-the-art heat exchangers in
order to address the associated issues. They are based mostly on compact heat exchangers
providing anti-fouling operation in heat exchanging.
Alfa Laval, for example, produced a series of such heat exhangers [109]. Compabloc consists
of a welded plate heat exchanger, which could work up to 350◦C, but only at 40 bar [110]. It
could be used in HENs to heat the crude oil, but not in the Reversed Brayton Cycle, where
pressure levels are higher. Another one is high pressure spiral heat exchanger (HPSHE),
which could work up to 400◦C and 100 bar. This could also operate as a condenser. It is a
heat exchanger designed for applications in reﬁneries and its geometry enables self-cleaning
without accumulation of fouling, leading to potential cleaning cost savings [111].
62
==PAGE:80==
4.8. Incentives of HTHPs
Different and more sophisticated designs also exist. Printed Circuit Heat Exchangers (PCHE)
got their name from the construction procedure, which is similar to printed circuit boards.
Heatric is a very large manufacturer of these kind of heat exchangers [112]. They are also part
of the compact heat exchanger family, with very large compactness and overall heat transfer
coefﬁcients [113]. The application of PCHE could, depending of constructed material, range
from cryogenic temperatures to 800 ◦C and the pinch point could reach as low as 1 K [114].
The large compactness and their potential to operate on low pinch temperatures could make
them ideal candidates as IHX in Reversed Brayton Cycle.
According to Ho et al. [115] the cost of such heat exchangers is lower for gas to gas operation
and there could be suitable in supercritical CO2 Brayton Cycles. On top of those, as stated
in Hesselgreaves et al. [116] the cost of piping, installation etc. (fBM) while in Shell and Tube
Heat Exchangers (STHE) could be ﬁve times the purchase cost, in compact heat exchangers it
is only two times.
All these point in the direction that these kind of heat exchangers are worth further investiga-
tion in Reversed Brayton Cycles during future research. In this study, they were not investigated
more thoroughly, due to the lack of cost correlations following economies of scale. Yoon et al.
[117] provided a cost correlation for purchase involving the calculation of the cost of material
for construction. It did not follow, though, economies of scale.
Despite the fact that, all these heat exchangers have anti-fouling operation, they are also
susceptible to fouling. The costs of their cleaning is lower making them potential candidates
for replacing STHEs.
4.8
Incentives of HTHPs
The subsidy included in the study for HTHPs was based on the energy savings scheme in
Denmark [86]. It was reported that such a scheme could almost cover half the investment
of industrial heat pumps, but due to low COP in the investigated scenarios the income from
the subsidy is rather limited. Via the energy agreement of 2018, the parties have agreed the
termination of this scheme in 2021, as it has proven to be very expensive for the government
and caused administrative burdens. Instead, a market-based scheme will be established
in the period 2021-2024, targeting savings in process energy in industry, but also in energy
consumption in buildings. The funds will be awarded through open tenders, where bidders
will offer a certain price for a speciﬁc volume of savings. The funds will be capped at DKK 500
million annually and 200 of them will be speciﬁcally for energy savings in buildings [118]. For
the same agreement, the government is committed to reducing the electricity tax for heating
purposes with the aim of supporting the conversion from combution fuel types to heat pump
technologies. Heat pump technologies are mentioned as a core component of a green heating
sector. In addition, as relevant to the study, the support for the transition to renewable energy
continues by distributing DKK 4.2 billion to technology-neutral procurement in renewable
electricity production between years 2020-2024.

5 Conclusions
This chapter presents the main conclusions extracted from the project
This work analyzed the techno-economic feasibility of high temperature pumps in the form
of Reversed Brayton Cycle, utilizing CO2 as the working ﬂuid and meeting their electricity
demand largely through renewable sources. The case study involved the replacement of the
furnace before the ADU in reﬁnery processes.
Due to limitations and fouling issues, two different scenarios were separated addressing differ-
ent levels of temperature glide in the crude oil. For each of those, three conﬁgurations were
analyzed introducing different numbers of heat pumps, but also thermodynamic performance.
The COP was found to be rather low in each scenario after optimization. In the high temper-
ature glide scenario COPs in the range of 1.397 to 1.46 were reported, while in the other the
corresponding range was 1.5 to 1.55.
It was found that in all scenarios and conﬁgurations the most costly component was the
compressor. Signiﬁcant costs were also observed for the turbine and the IHX. In case of heat
transfer ﬂuid utilization for indirect heat supply to the crude, the sink-side HEN also had large
impacts on the cost of HTHP.
Through the optimization algorithm built, the HTHPs were considered to be coupled with
wind turbines, photovoltaics and heat storage and the different conﬁgurations were compared
in terms of levelized cost of heat. Although they may have provided better thermodynamic
performance, conﬁgurations with larger amount of heat pumps introduced high investments
and resulted in worse economic performance in terms of economic feasibility.
As such, conﬁgurations with one only heat pump with secondary loops on both sink- and
source-side were further investigated. The energy technologies mixture optimization desig-
nated that wind turbines, photovoltaics and heat storage are eligible for application when
aiming to an economic optimization of a HTHP project. Wind turbines consist a permanent
choice of optimization in the mix, while the option to have increased capacity of the heat
pump together with heat storage was very much dependent on the COP, the heat demand
and how the HTHP costs scaled with capacity. Photovoltaics consisted mostly a ﬁller option
supplementary to wind turbines.
The scenarios formulated were, then, compared with conventional fuel-based technologies
and electric boilers. HTHPs were designated to be superior to electric boilers, but the analysis
64
==PAGE:82==
showed a negative NPV when HTHPs were compared with natural gas and biomass boilers.
Due to its high cost, biogas was deemed inferior to HTHPs.
Although HTHPs seemed not to be that competitive alternatives to boilers with natural gas
and biomass, an analysis performed on the cost projection of these fuels showed that HTHPs
would be more viable in the future.
An investigation on HTHPs parameters showed that the economic optimum may not be in
the pressure levels where COP is optimal, but rather at a lower value of low-pressure of the
cycle. Additionally, small improvements in NPV were observed for a lower pinch point limit
of the IHX. However, the economic beneﬁts were found to be limited. Thus an economic
optimization on an already optimized HTHP, in terms of COP, is judged to be fair.
Lastly, the isentropic efﬁciency assumption of both compressor and turbine was found to be
quite inﬂuential on the NPV value of the project. Regarding the economic parameters, the
feasibility was mostly sensitive to the electricity cost, but also to the investment of the heat
pump and wind turbines.
Assumptions and limitations, case-speciﬁc or not were analyzed and indicated that this study
should be perceived as pre-feasibility one, showing and highlighting the potentials of HTHPs
with Reversed Brayton Cycle.
