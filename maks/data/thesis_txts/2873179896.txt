Abstract
Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS) is a fast-progressing neurodegenerative disease tar-
geting motor neurons, causing muscle weakness, paralysis, and respiratory failure. Al-
though most cases are sporadic, some cases involve genetic mutations such as the mu-
tation in Superoxide Dismutase 1 (SOD1) gene. This mutation might result in toxic protein
aggregates and neuronal damage, causing oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunc-
tion, ultimately leading to neuron degeneration.
There is evidence of glymphatic system (GS) impairment in ALS. The GS plays a cru-
cial role in clearing brain waste during sleep, and its dysfunction may contribute to the
accumulation of toxic proteins and the progression of the disease. In addition, neuroin-
flammation, driven by glial cells such as microglia and astrocytes, shifts from protective
to neurotoxic over time, releasing inflammatory factors that accelerate neuron death.
Aquaporins (AQPs) are membrane channels that regulate water and small-molecule flow.
Specifically, AQP4 is the most important in the GS, as it is located in the end-feet of astro-
cytes, regulating the balance of water and the integrity of the blood-brain barrier. In ALS,
AQP4 is mislocalized and overexpressed, disrupting waste clearance and contributing to
neurotoxicity.
Finally, norepinephrine (NE), which controls attention and stress responses, could also
be involved in neurodegenerative diseases. Patients with ALS show an increase in NE,
which can contribute to the GS impairment.
The results of this study support the dysregulation of AQP4 in astrocytes and capillaries
within the CNS, and consequently within the glymphatic system. A neuroinflammation
in the microglia is also observed. Moreover, there is a decrease in the number of cells
and motor neurons due to the disease, as expected. Finally, although conclusive evidence
regarding the norepinephrine transporter is lacking, an observed trend supports the earlier
findings.

Introduction
1.1
Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis
Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS) is a rapidly progressive neurodegenerative disorder
characterised by the loss of upper and lower motor neurons in the cerebral cortex, brain-
stem, and spinal cord [1, 2]. As these neurons deteriorate, communication between the
brain and spinal cord becomes impaired, leading to muscle debilitation. This causes a
gradual loss of the ability to control voluntary muscles, which leads to paralysis and, ul-
timately, respiratory failure, which is typically the cause of death within 3 to 5 years [3].
ALS is a fatal disease with no known cure, and its exact cause remains unclear, making
it an idiopathic disease. This highlights the importance of researching it.
It is also referred to as motor neuron disease, and it is a uniquely human neurodegen-
erative complex disease having a variety of phenotypes. ALS is the 3rd most common
neurodegenerative disease after Alzheimer’s (AD) and Parkinson’s disease (PD). World-
wide, the number of people who develop ALS is estimated to be 1.9 people per 100,000
per year [4].
Symptoms are often subtle and vary depending on which part of the body is affected first.
During the clinical period of the disease, there may be a presymptomatic period charac-
terized by pathologic alterations involving motoneuron size and loss, as well as possible
corticospinal tract degeneration. Early symptoms of ALS impact different sections of the
body depending on which motor neurons are damaged first. This translates to the earli-
est symptoms, such as muscle weakness or muscle atrophy, typically on one side of the
body. Although the initial site of symptoms and subsequent rate of disability progression
vary from person to person, the initially affected body region is usually the most affected
over time, and symptoms usually spread to a neighbouring body region [5].
As ALS spreads to more regions of the body, symptoms become more widespread and
disabling. The development of symptoms in spinal cord levels occurs faster than the
accumulation of symptoms in the brain stem [6].
The final stage involves dysphagia and increases the risk of choking or aspirating food into
the lungs. Both aspiration pneumonia and maintaining a healthy weight are developed in
later phases and become a significant problem. As the diaphragm and intercostal muscles
of the rib cage weaken, measures of lung function such as vital capacity and inspiratory
pressure diminish, which is the main cause of death as previously stated [5].
1.1.1
Superoxide Dismutase 1 Gene
While the vast majority of ALS cases (90%) occur sporadically (sALS), around 10% are fa-
milial (fALS). In recent years, more than 120 genes have been implicated in ALS. Of these,
C9ORF72, FUS, TARDBP and superoxide dismutase 1 (SOD1) are the most common
causative genes [7]. Specifically, the SOD1 gene is considered the major gene involved in
ALS pathogenesis since it accounts for 20% of the fALS [8]. SOD1 is a Cu/Zn-dependent
metalloenzyme, requiring copper and zinc as cofactors for its enzymatic activity.
There are 3 types of human superoxide dismutase. SOD2 and SOD3 are located in the
mitochondria and extracellularly, respectively. On the other hand, SOD1 can be found
highly expressed in the cytoplasm, but it is also found in the intermembrane space of mi-
tochondria, apart from the nucleus, suggesting a broader role in cellular defence mecha-
nisms.

The primary role of SOD1 is as an antioxidant enzyme that protects the cell from the
toxicity of reactive oxygen species (ROS). It is a soluble homodimeric protein with ap-
proximately 32kDa of weight. SOD1 catalyzes the dismutation of superoxide anions into
less reactive molecules, specifically molecular oxygen and hydrogen peroxide (Figure 1)
[7]. Notably, it is believed that the pathogenicity of SOD1 mutations is not due to a lack
of functional protein but rather to the accumulation of its misfolded aggregates, probably
the sum of both [10]. It is still unclear whether all ALS-related SOD1 mutations are in fact
causative, co-causative, modifying or simply accompanying variants. Instead of simply
losing its normal function, mutant SOD1 (mSOD1) gains a toxic function that contributes
to motor neuron death. The exact mechanisms of the disease triggered due to mSOD1
are still being studied, but several major hypotheses try to explain how ALS could be
developed, which could be due to:
1. Oxidative stress. SOD1 normally protects cells from oxidative stress; mutated
versions can cause an increase in oxidative damage instead. This leads to cellular
stress, damaging DNA, proteins, and mitochondria (point 2), which ultimately leads
to neuron death [11].
2. Mitochondrial dysfunction. mSOD1 has been found inside mitochondria, espe-
cially in the spinal cord, where is more recruited. Studies suggest that the predis-
position of mSOD1 to incorrect crosslinking and aggregation (point 4), which would
suggest the disruption of mitochondrial function, leading to energy deficits and cell
death [11].
3. Disrupted cellular transport and toxicity to neighbouring cells. mSOD1 dis-
rupts the transport of essential proteins and molecules within neurons. It also harms
astrocytes and microglia, leading to neuroinflammation and increased toxicity [12].
4.
Protein misfolding and aggregation.
mSOD1 misfolds and clumps together,
forming toxic aggregates inside motor neurons. This disrupt the normal functioning
of cells, interfering with protein recycling and clearance carried out by the glymphatic
system [13].
1.2
Glymphatic System
The glymphatic system (GS) is a recently discovered macroscopic waste clearance sys-
tem that uses a unique system of perivascular channels created by astroglial cells. This
system enhances the clearance of soluble proteins and metabolic waste from the cen-
tral nervous system (CNS). In addition, the GS may also help distribute vital substances
2
==PAGE:13==
throughout the brain, including glucose, lipids, amino acids, and neurotransmitters in-
volved in volume transmission [14, 15, 16].
This system enables the rapid exchange of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) and interstitial fluid
(ISF). The mechanism initiates with the influx of CSF from the subarachnoid space into the
periarterial space (Figure 2), a movement propelled by forces including vascular pulsa-
tions and respiratory activity. From there, CSF enters the brain parenchyma through the
perivascular space surrounding the arteries [17]. The entrance of CSF into the interstitial
space is facilitated by aquaporin-4 (AQP4) channels located on the astrocytic endfeet.
This initiates a convective flow that pumps ISF toward the perivenous spaces, carrying
solutes such as metabolic waste products along. Ultimately, the convective influx of CSF
is balanced by the perivenous efflux of ISF, which drains into the CSF circulation [17],
completing the clearance process.
Intriguingly, the GS functions occur mainly during sleep when there is an exchange of inter-
stitial fluid and CSF, promoting clearance of toxic proteins from the brain parenchyma. On
the other hand, this clearance is largely disengaged during wakefulness [18, 14]. Sleep
disturbances significantly exacerbate the challenges faced by patients with ALS.
These individuals exhibit disturbances in CSF flow dynamics, indicating a possible alter-
ation in CSF circulation, which may contribute to the disease’s pathogenesis [16]. This
involves effects on the CNS’s microvascular units and may accelerate the motor neuron
damage. The observed changes in CSF flow dynamics in these patients may also sug-
gest disruptions in the GS, which correlates with preclinical data that has already shown.
The inhibition of the GS may contribute to disease processes and impact ALS progression
[17], agreeing with the hypothesis of mSOD1 misfolding and aggregation.

and respiration, drive cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) influx from the subarachnoid region into the periarterial area.
eventually, CSF flows into the interstitial space, aided by AQP4 channels located on the astrocyte endfeet. A
convective flow pushes interstitial fluid (ISF) towards the perivenous regions, transporting solutes, including
metabolic waste products. This highly structured system that allows for rapid CSF-ISF exchange. Image
taken from [17].

1.3
Neuroinflamation
1.3.1
Glial Cells
Glial cells make up the majority of cells in the nervous system. Glial cells are classified
according to their morphology, function, and location into microglia, the only immune-
derived glial cells that reach the brain through the bloodstream during early development;
astrocytes; and Schwann cells and oligodendrocytes, which form myelin sheaths around
axons in the peripheral and CNS, respectively.
Astrocytes are the most abundant glial cells, constituting about 25% of brain volume [19].
While the functions of microglia and oligodendrocytes are well known (local immune de-
fence and myelination, respectively), the role of astrocytes is more enigmatic.
However, astrocytes are known to be crucial for the integrity and function of the neu-
ronal network, as they form tripartite synapses with neurons and thereby ensure activity
modulation and regulation of neurotransmitters through factors such as glutamate uptake
[20]. In addition, astrocytes participate in monitoring and regulating the water flux and ion
and pH homeostasis and providing structural integrity to the extracellular matrix in order
to maintain an optimal environment. As a key component and regulator of the selective
permeable Blood-Brain Barrier BBB, astrocytes provide neurotrophic support, nutrients,
and waste removal, and together with microglia, they form the first line of defence against
harmful agents [21].
As knowledge of them has grown, astrocytes have become recognised as essential el-
ements for maintaining the microenvironment required for proper neural function. In the
past 20 years, a wide variety of specific roles have been attributed to them [20].
The molecular characterization of these cells has revealed that they play a fundamental
role in information transmission in the nervous system.

(ALS), there is an early anti-inflammatory or neuroprotective compensatory response of the surrounding glia and immune
cells. B. Cytotoxic phase of neuroinflammation. Late in the course of the disease as the motor neuron becomes damaged,

1.3.2
Neurotoxic astrocytic state
Studies have suggested that although ALS progression is the result of slow and gradual
dysfunction and loss of motor neurons, other non-neuronal cells in the CNS also play a
key role in the disease.
4
==PAGE:15==
The progression of ALS can be categorised into two phases. The initial stage is asymp-
tomatic and is defined by a neuroprotective immune environment, in which glial cells and
immune components as M2 microglia, Th2, and regulatory T cells (Tregs), secrete anti-
inflammatory factors such as cytokines or IL-4 and IL-10, that help sustain motor neuron
health. However, during the progression of ALS, this environment changes. The dis-
ease enters into a second rapidly progressing neurotoxic phase, in which M1 microglia
and proinflammatory T cells dominate. These cells release ROS and proinflammatory
cytokines like IL-1, IL-6, and TNF-α, while the production of neurotrophic factors declines,
accelerating motor neuron degeneration [2].
Figure 4: Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) primarily affects corticospinal motor neurons that control skeletal muscles.
Neurodegeneration in ALS involves multiple molecular and genetic mechanisms, including the accumulation of mutant
proteins such as SOD1, TARDBP, FUS, and C9orf72, which form toxic aggregates; mitochondrial dysfunction; impaired
axonal transport; and microglial activation leading to neuroinflammation. Other potential mechanisms not explored in this
study include glutamate-induced excitotoxicity causing excessive Na+ and Ca²+ influx and oxidative stress, dysfunction of
ion pumps and channels disrupting ionic balance, and defects in RNA processing and protein synthesis. Together, these
factors contribute to neuronal death and synaptic loss. Image taken from [8].
The mechanisms underlying motor neuron injury can be classified into ”cell autonomous”
processes, occurring in the motor neuron, and ”noncell autonomous” processes, which
involve non-neuronal cells participating in the disease progression [2, 10]. Therefore, the
5
==PAGE:16==
involvement of non-neuronal cells such as microglia or astrocytes seems essential for
rapid disease development and motor neuron degeneration.
In this later phase in SOD1 mutants, the impact of mSOD1 becomes particularly evident,
leading to microglial activation [12]. A significant part of the noncell-autonomous toxicity
can be attributed to neuroinflammation. Previously thought to be a consequence of motor
neuron death, neuroinflammation is now established as an important factor not only in the
pathogenesis of ALS, but also in many other neurodegenerative diseases, including PD
and AD [10]
1.4
The Aquaporins
Aquaporins (AQPs) are intrinsic membrane proteins that selectively allow water or other
small uncharged molecules to pass along the osmotic gradient. Moreover, recent ob-
servations have shown some aquaporins can also facilitate the transport of volatile sub-
stances, such as CO2 or NH3, across membranes [22].
The primary sequence of these proteins is six transmembrane helices (I-VI) connected by
five loops (loops A-E) ( Figure 5). Loops A, C and E are extracellular, while loops B and D
are intracellular. The protein comprises two internal tandem repeats, covering both amino-
and carboxy-terminal halves of the protein. Each repeat consists of three transmembrane
helices and a highly conserved loop following the second transmembrane helix (loops
B and E). This loop includes a conserved signature motif, (NPA). Loops B and E form
short α helices that fold back into the membrane. Finally, when AQPs fold, a seventh
transmembrane domain appears, created by the two NPA boxes, which are oriented 180
degrees to each other creating an aqueous pathway through the proteinaceous pore [23].
Figure 5: Topology of an aquaporin protein within the membrane. The protein consists of six transmem-
brane helices (I-VI) connected by five loops (A-E) and includes two internal tandem repeats (I-III and IV-VI,
respectively). Loops B and E, containing the conserved NPA motifs (in the single-letter amino-acid code),
form short α helices that fold back into the membrane from opposite sides. C, carboxyl terminus; N, amino
terminus. Image taken from [22].
Only 13 AQPs have been found to be expressed in humans (AQP0–12). They can be
roughly categorised into three main subgroups based on their selectivity and primary
structure [24]:
• Classical or orthodox AQPs: AQP0, 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 8. Primarily water channels, al-
though AQP6,8 can transport anions and ammonia as well respectively. Classical

AQPs are crutial regulators in brain homeostasis [25], besides to kidney water phys-
iology.
• Aquaglyceroporins: AQP3, 7, 9, 10. They also allow the transport of small non-
charged molecules such as urea and glycerol.
• Superaquaporins or unorthodox: AQP11 and 12.
Nevertheless, only 3 of them have a relevant role and presence in the CNS.
• AQP1 has been suggested to play an important role in CSF secretion. It is selec-
tively expressed in the apical surface of the choroid plexus epithelium, the producer
of CSF. Recently, it was directly demonstrated that AQP1 is a major pathway for
mediating water transport across the choroid plexus during CSF secretion. When
AQP1 was deleted in transgenic mice, the osmotic water permeability and CSF se-
cretion were significantly reduced [26].
• AQP9 can be found in multiple tissues around the body such as liver, inner ear
including also brain, spinal cord and astrocyes [24]. However, the role of AQP9 in the
CNS has been poorly studied; certain studies have proposed its altered expression
could lead to neuronal disorders and tumors [27].
• Finally, and most importantly, the focus of this study, AQP4.
1.4.1
Aquaporin 4
AQP4 is the most abundantly expressed water channel in the CNS, permitting passive
and bidirectional water diffusion [13], apart from being involved in various roles in neu-
rotransmission [28]. In addition, the integrity and permeability of the BBB could also be
affected by the role of AQP4 [13]. Its distribution is widespread throughout the brain, and
includes the cerebral cortex, corpus callosum, retina, cerebellum, magnocellular nuclei of
the hypothalamus, and brain stem. However, AQP4 is highly localised in the end-feet of
astrocytes, which allows it to control nervous system water homeostasis via its involve-
ment in water movement across the BBB. Due to its localisation and function, AQP4 is
critically involved in the operation of the GS as Figure 2 shows.
AQP4 overexpression has been observed in both the brain and spinal cord of SOD1-
G93A ALS models in rodents, suggesting that AQP4 may contribute to motor neuron
degeneration in ALS. Moreover, it has been seen that it is its mislocalization that dis-
rupts glymphatic clearance and BBB integrity, and therefore leads to an exacerbation of
astrocyte-mediated toxicity. This disruption may result in the accumulation of neurotoxic
proteins, such as mSOD1 and TDP-43, the main triggers of fALS, as previously men-
tioned. Thus, impaired AQP4 function might contribute to neuroinflammation, oxidative
stress, and neuronal excitotoxicity [13]. Consequently, AQP4 depolarization in astrocytes
would be one of the main triggers, if not the leading one, of motor neuron death. Finally,
GS dysfunction is closely related to the depolarisation of AQP4 [29], which suggest a main
role of this transmembrane transporter in the clearance of the CNS.
1.5
Norepinephrine
Norepinephrine (NE), also known as noradrenaline, is a key neurotransmitter in the mam-
malian brain that regulates attention, arousal, cognitive functions, and stress responses.
For example, in case of stressful situations, NE will be released in large quantities and
prepare the body for action by increasing the heart rate and energy mobilization.
The locus coeruleus (LC) provides the only source of NE to the cortex for modulation of
cortical synaptic activity in response to salient sensory information [30]. Hence, this hor-
7
==PAGE:18==
mone is released from the axon terminals of sympathetic postganglionic neurons through
the circulation to its effector cells [31]. Over the last few decades, much effort has been
dedicated to gaining information about the roles of NE in states of arousal and key brain
functions. The interest in NE is further justified by human cognitive brain pathologies
associated with its malfunction. LC neuronal loss is common and an early feature of
neurodegenerative diseases. Post-mortem studies have confirmed that LC neurons are
severely reduced in AD, PD, schizophrenia and progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP) to
a larger extent compared to neuronal loss in the nuclei commonly associated with these
disorders [32, 33].
A recent research [34] was the first to link sleep with GS function, showing that NE driven
slow vasomotion is the main force behind glymphatic clearance during non-REM (NREM)
sleep. Furthermore, patients with ALS have reported increased levels of NE in both blood
and CSF, along with depressed levels of GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid), an inhibitory
neurotransmitter, in the CSF, which may also affect the integrity of the GS [35, 29]. While
the mechanism behind this increase of NE remains unknown, one hypothesis relates to
the cortical impairment in ALS associated with cortical hyperexcitability, which has already
been documented in both sALS and fALS.
This fact has been shown to precede the appearance of motor symptoms in mSOD1 car-
riers as well as in sporadic cases of ALS, and it correlates with the severity and progres-
sion of the disease. Due to this information, a corticofugal disease trajectory has been
proposed, in which the pathology is initiated in the cortex and spreads along anatomi-
cal projections to subcortical regions [36]. Given the lack of information regarding the
role of NE in ALS despite the potential involvement in other neurodegenerative diseases,
investigating its implications opens a promising area of research.
The norepinephrine transporter (NET), present on the plasma membrane of noradrener-
gic neurons, limits the action of NE through reuptake into the cytoplasm. NET not only
regulates the longevity of NE in the synapse but also plays a very important role in presy-
naptic and postsynaptic homeostasis. Developing therapeutic strategies that support or
re-establish physiological sleep patterns, maintain the oscillatory activity of NE, and pro-
mote glymphatic function may be key to slowing or potentially reversing the progression
of neurodegenerative disorders [34]. Undeniably, this highlights the relevance of studying
NET within the GS, not only as an indicator of NE activity but also as a key component of
its regulation.

Aim of the project
Considering the previous work by Jaspreet Kaur and Maiken Nedergaard, among others;
in addition to the new findings in the development of ALS, the objectives of this research
can be described as follows:
• Characterise alterations in the neuroglial network in both the brain and the spinal
cord during the progression of ALS using the SOD1-G93A mouse model.
• Identify early and late-stage changes in NE levels in neurons and AQP4 channels
in glial cells, before and after symptom onset.
• Investigate the temporal progression of ALS-related pathology, particularly focusing
on the interaction between neurons and glial cells and transporters.

3.1
Animal Model
Mice used in this study were SOD1-G93A transgenic mice, descendants from a first gen-
eration, ordered from The Jackson Laboratory, which is a widely used hemizygous model
for ALS. These mice carry a high-copy transgene encoding a human SOD1 gene with
a glycine-to-alanine substitution at codon 93 (G93A). The animals are viable and fertile.
Hemizygous transgenic mice develop a progressive ALS-like phenotype, characterized
by motor neuron degeneration and subsequent paralysis, typically affecting one or more
limbs. The resulting neurodegeneration resembles major pathogenic characteristics of
ALS in humans. In particular, SOD1-G93A-activated microglia exhibit a distinctive tran-
scriptional profile, representing an ALS-specific inflammatory signature [37]. C57BL/6J
mice were used as wild-type controls, also from Jackson Laboratory, while transgenic

3.2
Behavioural Analysis
Behavioural data collection was conducted by the supervisor of the project, Jaspeer Kaur,
due to the lack of a FELASA certificate required to work with live animals. Mice have a
biphasic, nocturnal activity pattern, being active at night and mainly resting during the
day. The experiments were held in an open field (OF) box during the day. Movement of
the mice was observed are recorded both pre- and post onset of the disease. Data was
analysed by the software Anymaze, returning important parameters such as the distance
travelled, time mobile or frozen episode were considered.
3.3
Animal Manipulation
Same as the previous point, mice could not be directly manipulated. However, brains
and spinal cords were provided by the supervisor, Jaspreet Kaur. Both tissues were fixed
in 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) immediately after dissection. After 24 hours, the PFA
was replaced with 30% sucrose for cryoprotection and incubated for 2 hours. Finally, the
samples were stored either in 1% phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) at -4 °C or maintaned
dry at -80 °C.
3.4
Ex vivo tissue sectioning
Four different sections were prepared for this analysis: three from brain regions, motor
cortex (M1), hippocampus (H), and brainstem (BS) — and one from the spinal cord (SC),
specifically the lumbar region.
Spinal cords were embedded in 2% agarose (A5093, Sigma-Aldrich). Both brains and
spinal cords were diluted in PBS and sectioned using a vibratome (Leica VT1200S) into
slices of 30 µm and/or 60 µm thickness.

Disease-Associated Increase in AQP4-GFAP colocalisation
Colocalisation refers to the correlation of two molecules of interest, in this case, AQP4
and GFAP. The Pearson’s correlation coefficient (PCC) refers of the linear correlation be-
tween the intensity of both biomarkers. It is not related to the intensity, but the variation
23
==PAGE:34==
of it through the sample. PCC results can only go from -1, meaning total negative cor-
relation, to 1, total positive correlation. A value of 0 would suggest random overlap and
therefore no correlation. In the colocalisation analysis, Manders coefficients (M1 and M2)
are also tested. Manders shows the proportion of fluorophore’s signal that overlaps with
the other. However, it does not show the intensity but the presence relation between both
biomarkers. M1 (A/B) pertains to the overlapping of A (AQP4) over B (GFAP) while M2
(B/A) is the opposite. Manders values go from 0, no overlap, to 1, complete overlap.

Discussion and Conclusion
This study aimed to understand the mechanisms involved in the development of ALS,
especially through the depolarization of AQP4 at glial network level and changes in NE
through norepinephrine transporter levels, and further considering the alterations at glial
network level, such as neuroinflammation.
Starting with the behavioural analysis of the animals, it is clear that there is a hindlimb
motor dysfunction caused by ALS. The diseased mice could not move at all and their
rotation was also highly limited, highlighting the effect of this disease. Based not only
on the extracted data but also on the recordings and literature [38, 39], the mice were
immobile and suffered from tremors even in their tails.
However, in ALS mouse models, motor neurons do not die alone [2]. As described in the
Introduction cell death is a non-cell-autonomous process that involves complex interac-
tions between motor neurons and the surrounding glial cells. The results support this cell
degeneration mechanism. The total number of dead cells increases progressively dur-
ing disease development, which is consistent with SOD1 mutation. This mutated gene
would induce oxidative stress and lead to mitochondrial dysfunction [8], finally resulting in
general cell death.
Interestingly, NeuN staining data do not correlate with the current literature. No significant
decline has been observed. Nevertheless, there has been an rise in certain areas of
the CNS. The answer to this is probably due to the analysis. Images were divided into
several regions of interest, in which some areas have more dense neurons while others
are not as densely packed. Presumably, averaging these data might result in the results
we observed. Nonetheless, there is also a consistent reduction in the number of motor
neurons in the spinal cord. In general, if the NeuN results are excluded due to what
has already been mentioned, it can be stated that ALS induces the reduction of motor
neurons that cause symptoms such as muscle twitches and weakness [3]. such as muscle
fasciculations and progressive weakness [15].
Talking about AQP4, which is the main focus of this project, depolarisation can be con-
firmed considering the results. Despite having no change regarding AQP4-BSA colocal-
ization, results of AQP4-GFAP are clear. They indicate an upregulation of AQP4 due to
depolarisation. This upregulation leads to astrogliosis (astrocyte activation) and subse-
quently neuroinflammation, which altogether contribute to a dysfunction in the GS, result-
ing in the accumulation of waste products in the CNS and the progression of the disease.
The NE role in this disease was the other main topic. Despite the efforts made, no solid re-
sults have been obtained. Although a consistent decrease in NET density was observed,
the results related to its area and intensity were contradictory. Increasing the sample
size and possibly incorporating additional markers, such as NE itself, will be necessary to
better understand its role in the disease.
Finally, AQP1 was also analysed to support AQP4 results. However, different data were
obtained compared to AQP4. No definitive conclusion can be drawn for this protein, as
the significant decrease was observed in some samples conflicts with the lack of change
in others.
This study provides evidence supporting the multifactorial nature of ALS pathogenesis.
Behavioural analysis clearly demonstrated severe motor impairment in diseased mice,

consistent with the progression of ALS. Histological data, despite some inconsistencies
likely due to technical issues, align with the non-cell-autonomous model of motor neuron
death, in which interactions between neurons and reactive glial cells contribute to neu-
rodegeneration. Among the glial responses observed, the most consistent and relevant
findings involve AQP4. Its depolarization and upregulation appear to act as initial events
that trigger astrogliosis and subsequent neuroinflammation [13]. These processes may
lead to GS dysfunction and the accumulation of waste products in the CNS, ultimately
contributing to disease progression.
As a first approach into this area of research, this work sets the foundations for future
studies. Further investigation, especially regarding NET, and with an expanded sample
size could help clarify the preliminary observations made here.
