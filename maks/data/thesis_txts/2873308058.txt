Abstract
Agentic Entrepreneurial Experimentation: Framework and Case Study explores how
agentic workflows, powered by large language models (LLMs), can transform the speed,
cost, and quality dynamics of Digital Entrepreneurial Experimentation (DEE). Grounded
in Design Research Methodology, the thesis makes two connected contributions.
First, it introduces the Agentic Entrepreneurial Experimentation (AEE) framework, a dual-
layer conceptual model. This includes (i) a five-stage DEE categorization aligned to the
venture lifecycle, and (ii) the CARD model, a four-axis lens (Capability, Autonomy, Risk,
Data) for evaluating the technical and organizational feasibility of agentic processes.
Second, the thesis presents StoryGen, a working prototype that ingests swimlane dia-
grams and generates INVEST-compliant agile user stories. In collaboration with North
Consulting, StoryGen was benchmarked against the incumbent manual workflow across
24 real client tasks. The system reduced median lead time from 180 minutes to 20 sec-
onds (a 99.8% reduction), cut per-diagram cost from €600 to under €0.01, and produced
first-draft outputs rated at 73% of human-final quality. Experts estimate that this could
exceed 90% when compared to unpolished consultant drafts. Although syntactic parsing
was flawless, semantic anomalies were still observed, highlighting the need for down-
stream content validation.
These findings challenge the assumptions of the traditional Iron Triangle of project delivery.
With the careful integration of agentic systems, ventures may no longer need to trade off
between time, cost, and quality. The preliminary AEE framework provides a structured
lens for determining what kind of agentic system is feasible in a given context, helping
teams balance capability, autonomy, risk, and data readiness across the venture lifecycle.

Introduction
Chapter 1 lays the groundwork for this thesis by first explaining why accelerated Agentic-
driven experimentation is critical in today’s fast-moving digital economy. In Section 1.1,
we explore the challenges that businesses face when trying to validate ideas, streamline
processes, and scale ventures under time, cost, and quality pressures, and show how
recent advances in agentic systems offer a promising way forward.
Then, in Section 1.2, we introduce the core concepts that are going to be utilized in our
Digital Entrepreneurial Experimentation (DEE) proposal.
Together, these two subsections orient and prepare the reader to the problem context,
theoretical foundations, and practical scope of what follows.
1.1
Motivation
In today’s rapidly evolving digital economy, entrepreneurial ventures face growing pres-
sure to validate ideas, test business models, and scale operations at unprecedented
speed. Digital Entrepreneurial Experimentation (DEE) has emerged as a critical practice
for navigating uncertainty, enabling ventures to iteratively test hypotheses, refine products,
and optimize strategies (Kerr et al., 2014, p. 28-29). However, traditional DEE methods
remain constrained by human cognitive limits, resource intensity, and limited scalability,
posing challenges for ventures aiming to move quickly from ideation to execution, and for
established ventures aiming to scale operations and improve internal processes.
Recent advances in artificial intelligence (AI), particularly the emergence of large language
models (LLMs) and agentic systems, offer a new paradigm for automating and augment-
ing entrepreneurial processes (Patel, 2024; Wiesinger et al., 2024). Agentic systems
have shown potential to transform how businesses approach experimentation, especially
in data-rich, repeatable, and time-sensitive contexts, but also show potential for improve-
ments in other contexts.
Agentic systems can be broadly divided into two categories (Anthropic, 2024): workflows
and agents. Workflows are systems in which LLMs are embedded into predefined code
paths, executing structured sequences of actions in predictable and controlled environ-
ments. In contrast, agents are more autonomous, they dynamically direct their own pro-
cesses, make decisions in real-time, and choose which tools or actions to use in pursuit
of a goal. This self-directed behavior allows agentic systems to adapt fluidly to changing
contexts and perform complex tasks with minimal human intervention, making them highly
suitable for experimentation in dynamic business environments.
This thesis investigates how agentic systems,
defined as autonomous or semi-
autonomous frameworks, can be integrated into Digital Entrepreneurial Experimentation
(DEE) to accelerate common business processes, improving process efficiency, and
enhancing decision-making across different stages of venture development. Anchored
in the Design Research Methodology (DRM), this study makes a dual contribution: first,
it proposes the Agentic Entrepreneurial Experimentation (AEE) framework, a structured
method for designing, prioritizing, and assessing digital experiments supported by
agentic systems. Second, it proposes and evaluates StoryGen, a prototype agentic DEE
developed in the context of this thesis which is tested in a real-world setting.

At the core of the conceptual contribution, within the proposed AEE is the DEE Lifecy-
cle Categorization 4.2.1, that serves as the base for the CARD model, a four-dimensional
framework that assesses feasibility across Capability requirements, Autonomy, Risk level,
and Data richness 4.2.2. This model is used to map potential applications of agentic sys-

growth and optimization phases as the most promising context for empirical implementa-
tion, where speed, accuracy, and scalability are particularly critical.
To validate this framework, we design, implement, and evaluate StoryGen, an agentic sys-

providing a concrete demonstration of how agentic workflows can help to streamline and
scale complex business processes.
Through the research performed in this thesis, we aim to contribute both a conceptual
framework and a practical solution that demonstrates how agentic systems can redefine
entrepreneurial experimentation in the digital age.

Swimlane diagram
Swimlane diagrams are a business process modeling tool that organizes activities into
parallel lanes each representing a role, system, or department, allowing for clear visual-
ization of responsibilities and task flows across actors. These diagrams are commonly
used in operations and service design to identify process bottlenecks and interdependen-
cies.

User Stories
In agile software development, user stories are short, structured descriptions of features
or tasks told from the perspective of the end user.
They typically follow the format:
“As a [role], I want [feature], so that [benefit]”. These stories form the backbone of
agile backlogs and enable incremental, user-centered development. They are heavily
based on strict acceptance criteria predefined by the end user.

node includes a substantial amount of information, it may lead to the generation of multiple

Research Clarification
This chapter describes the application of the first stage of the DRM methodology, research
clarification, to our project. The problem statement and research objectives and ques-
tions are presented. Then a literature review is included, identifying research gaps and
presenting foundational definitions. The scope and limitations , as well as the expected

3.1
Problem Statement
Digital Entrepreneurial Experimentation (DEE) is essential for business process transfor-
mation, hypothesis testing, and product and process improvement, ensuring operational
efficiency, and sustainable growth. Traditional DEE methods allow for iterative product de-
velopment, optimization, and customer discovery but remain time-consuming, resource-
intensive, and constrained by human cognitive limits, therefore there is a potential for AI
application in Entrepreneurial Experimentation (EE), as has started to be done with the
Lean Startup Methodology (Patel, 2024). However, the majority of the methodologies
used, while proven effective, require a lot of time, resources, and still lack the integration
or use of AI or any other agentic solutions, that in recent years have proven effective in
other sectors.
Agentic based solutions have been evolving very rapidly in the recent past, and present
an opportunity to accelerate digital experimentation by accelerating hypothesis testing,
transforming or automating processes and experimentation cycles, reducing human bias
in decision-making, handling large-scale data analytics and data processing, and real-time
market adaptation by adjusting strategies based on live feedback.
Despite these opportunities, current research lacks a structured theoretical framework
for integrating agentic systems into entrepreneurial experimentation (Carayannis et al.,
2023; Kraus et al., 2018; Truong et al., 2023). The adoption of AI by entrepreneurial ven-
tures remains restricted by the absence of a guideline or framework for identifying agentic-
driven opportunities and evaluating organizational readiness, and the lack of structured
methodologies for the systematic deployment of the systems to enhance and optimize
core business processes. As stated in (Kraus et al., 2018), ”[...] research on how these
technologies influence digital business model innovation and entrepreneurial experimen-
tation remains limited and requires further investigation.”.
3.2
Research Objectives and Questions
Agentic systems (autonomous or semi autonomous) frameworks are reshaping how en-
trepreneurs validate, iterate, and execute business hypotheses. By embedding “co-pilot”
and “agent” capabilities into DEE, resource-constrained ventures can access sophisti-
cated testing methods once reserved for large organizations. This thesis explores how
such systems can transform and optimize core business processes, enabling intelligent
workflow automation, continuous adaptation, and enhanced decision support.
3.2.1
Research Objective
Guided by the DRM, our work unfolds in two complementary streams. The first stream de-
velops a conceptual framework for identifying where it is viable to deploy agentic systems
throughout the entrepreneurial lifecycle. This framework, combining a five-stage DEE cat-
egorization with the four-axis CARD model helps entrepreneurs assess the feasibility of
agentic interventions across diverse contexts.

The second stream delivers an empirical instantiation of that framework. We propose,
design, build, and evaluate an agentic prototype (StoryGen) that automates the translation
of swimlane diagrams into agile user stories for North Consulting. Our primary objective
here is to determine whether such an agentic workflow can produce consultant-quality
stories while reducing manual cycle time.
Together, these two efforts aim to advance theoretical understanding of AI-augmented
experimentation and offer entrepreneurs a practical roadmap for utilizing agentic systems
in process transformation and automation.
3.2.2
Research Questions
To achieve these objectives, we pose two overarching research questions, one for each
study stream:
RQ1. How can agentic systems be integrated into digital entrepreneurial experimenta-
tion?
Sub-questions:
1.1. What are the current limitations and challenges in digital entrepreneurial experimen-
tation that agentic systems can potentially address?
1.2. Which prerequisites ventures need to have in order to deploy agentic capabilities
in digital entrepreneurial experimentation, and what organizational and technical barriers
influence successful deployment of such an agentic systems?
1.3. To what extent do agentic systems accelerate processes and what related implica-
tions can it have?
1.4. How does the use of agentic systems in experimentation influence entrepreneurial
decision-making and venture outcomes?
RQ2: To what extent can an agentic system automate the swimlane-to-user-story pro-
cess?
Sub-questions:
2.1. How accurately do current VLMs extract the structure (lanes, nodes, flows) of swim-

3.3.2
Digital Entrepreneurial Experiments - Overview
DEE is a foundational concept that refers to the iterative process of testing business ideas
and hypotheses under uncertainty, simulating real-world environments. Using this pro-
cess, startups can iterate through different business models, products, and strategies,
identifying the best-performing ones and reducing the risk of failure.
According to (Zhao & Collier, 2016), ”Digital entrepreneurship refers to the creation of
new ventures and the transformation of existing businesses by developing new digital
technologies or experimenting with a novel usage of the same.”
While traditional methods involve manual experimentation and small-scale pilot testing,
DEE allows test scalability by leveraging digital tools to automate, structure, and validate
experiments. AI-driven experimentation enables startups and businesses to gather and
analyze large datasets of information, identifying patterns that inform business decisions
more efficiently than conventional methods. By using natural language processing (NLP)
and sentiment analysis, DEE systems can extract insights from user feedback and real-
time responses. Associating this with the use of AI to automate and scale experiments
reduces the need for manual testing and allows entrepreneurs to iterate and optimize pro-
cesses based on real-time experimental results, ensuring rapid validation, rejection and
optimization of hypotheses. DEE is a transformative approach as it improves experimen-
tal efficiency, lowers costs, and minimizes human biases (Carayannis et al., 2023).

Agentic Systems
The concept of agentic systems has emerged as a transformative paradigm within artificial
intelligence (AI), reflecting the rapid evolution of large language modes (LLMs) and their
capacity to transcend traditional, rigid computational frameworks. Unlike conventional AI
systems that operate within predefined boundaries, agentic systems herald a new era of
autonomy and adaptability, promising to redefine how intelligent machines interact with
complex, dynamic environments.
At the forefront of this discourse, Anthropic, a leading innovator in LLM development, pro-
vides a foundational distinction between two types of agentic systems: Workflows and
Agents (Anthropic, 2024). Workflows are characterized as “systems where LLMs and
tools are orchestrated through predefined code paths” (Anthropic, 2024), offering pre-
dictability and consistency for well-defined tasks.
Agents are defined as “systems where LLMs dynamically direct their own processes and
tool usage, maintaining control over how they accomplish tasks” (Anthropic, 2024). This
autonomy enables agentic systems to adapt fluidly to unforeseen challenges, leveraging
LLMs to make real-time decisions and orchestrate tools without rigid scripting.
Agentic systems distinguish themselves through their ability to function as self-contained,
decision-making entities. These systems perceive their environments, reason about avail-
able data, and execute actions through sophisticated computations, often without human
intervention. Their adaptability stems from the integration of natural language processing
(NLP), reasoning, planning, and interaction capabilities, underpinned by LLMs (Xi et al.,
2025). For instance, autonomous agentic systems can execute entrepreneurial exper-
iments, such as testing business hypotheses, by analyzing real-time data and refining
strategies iteratively, all while pursuing clearly defined goals (Wiesinger et al., 2024, p. 5).
This self-learning capacity positions agentic systems as powerful tools for dynamic, scal-
able automation.

Literature Review in Agentic Systems and Research Gaps
Agentic systems in the context of AI is still in its infancy, with the earliest mention in 2023
(Chan et al., 2023). Early efforts in agentic systems research were in governance prac-
tices (Shavit et al., 2023) and AI safety (Chan et al., 2023). In 2024, the scope of re-
search broadened to include design considerations (Abuelsaad et al., 2024), application
in healthcare (Qiu et al., 2024), and even using AI agents to automate the design of agen-
tic systems (Hu et al., 2024).
A recent study (Huang et al., 2025) introduces POPPER, a novel agentic system de-
signed to automate hypothesis validation through a process of sequential falsification
experiments. It relies on LLM-based agents to generate and conduct falsification tests
using real-world data, simulations, and statistical models. These falsification tests use
Karl Popper’s principle of falsification which states that a hypothesis must be testable and
refutable to be considered valid, so the tests try to disprove (falsify) a hypothesis and if
a hypothesis withstands, it becomes stronger, but it is never definitively ”proven”. This
ensures a thorough evaluation of hypotheses while strictly controlling Type-I errors. This
research was conducted in the context of biology, and it remains to be seen whether a
similar approach can be applied in other areas with positive results.
The need for agentic systems in business process settings is still very recent, so there
are not many documented implementations, representing a significant gap in the litera-
ture. This gap is beginning to be addressed. The recent contribution by (Hughes et al.,
2025) explore the enterprise-level implications of agentic systems. (Hughes et al., 2025)
outlines how AI agents can decentralize decision-making, enhance cross-functional col-
laboration, and reallocate tasks in sectors such as healthcare, supply chain, and business
operations. It identifies promising opportunities in adaptive automation and collaborative
intelligence, while simultaneously raising concerns about accountability, attribution, bias
in AI logic, and the integration challenges with legacy systems. Importantly, it calls for
further future research into transparent governance models, energy-efficient deployment,
and workforce reskilling strategies to enable ethical and scalable implementations.
These studies mark the emergence of a new research and innovation frontier, moving

3.4
Scope and Limitations
The study’s primary emphasis is on the integration of agents within DEE frameworks, pro-
viding a structured methodology that entrepreneurs can adopt to improve their business
validation process. Despite its broad scope, the study acknowledges some limitations:
• Industry-Specific Outcomes: AI experimentation outcomes may differ across and
also within the same industries due to variations in data availability, market struc-
tures, and AI adoption maturity. The findings from digital startups may not be directly
transferable to traditional industries with less digital infrastructure.
• Data Constraints: Early-stage ventures often struggle with limited access to high-
quality datasets. Many AI-driven models require large, diverse, and representa-
tive datasets to deliver accurate insights, but startups may lack the infrastructure to
gather and process such data effectively. On the other hand, while some established
ventures may have access to high-quality data, they may suffer from fragmented
data governance and insufficient organizational readiness, limiting their ability to
leverage those insights in a timely and scalable manner.
• Ethical and Regulatory Challenges: AI-driven decision-making introduces ethical
concerns related to bias, accountability, and fairness. Entrepreneurs in different
industries may face difficulties in ensuring AI models make transparent, unbiased
decisions according to the sector regulations, especially when relying on black-box
AI systems.
• Trust and Adoption Barriers: Entrepreneurs may be hesitant to fully delegate experi-
mentation and decision-making processes to AI agents due to concerns about trust-
worthiness (especially in cases of hallucinations), interpretability, and AI autonomy.
Resistance to AI adoption may limit the widespread implementation of AI-driven ex-
perimentation frameworks.

Ethical Considerations
AI integration raises critical ethical considerations that entrepreneurs must address to en-
sure responsible AI adoption. One of the primary concerns is data privacy and security,
as AI systems often rely on vast amounts of data, including sensitive personal informa-
tion. Entrepreneurs must ensure compliance with data protection regulations, such as
the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), to safeguard user privacy and prevent
unauthorized data access.
Another key issue is bias and fairness, as AI algorithms can inadvertently perpetuate and
accentuate existing biases present in their training data, leading to unfair or discriminatory
outcomes. Entrepreneurs are responsible for auditing their AI systems to identify and
mitigate biases, ensuring equitable treatment of all users.
Additionally, transparency and accountability pose challenges, since some AI models
make it difficult to understand how decisions are made. To ensure trust among users and
stakeholders, entrepreneurs should strive for transparency by making AI decision-making
processes interpretable and explainable.
Skills Gap are also a concern. It is essential to ensure that AI is well understood and used
responsibly as the misuse of AI, whether intentional or due to lack of understanding, can
lead to incorrect decision-making, ethical breaches, or even unintended societal harm.
Beyond legal compliance, entrepreneurs have a moral obligation to consider the broader
societal implications of AI applications. This includes ensuring that AI technologies are
used to enhance human well-being and do not cause harm or exacerbate social inequali-
ties (Woodhull-Smith, 2024).
Finally, entrepreneurs must also evaluate the impact of agentic AI on work itself. As sys-
tems increasingly automate routine cognitive tasks and decision steps, there is a real
risk of displacing roles or fundamentally shifting the nature of work, from hands-on ex-
ecution to oversight, curation, and exception handling. These workforce effects extend
beyond simple job loss: AI may hollow out middle￿skill positions and concentrate hu-
man effort in higher￿order problem￿solving, creating pressure for re-skilling and new
organizational models. Entrepreneurs should therefore plan proactively for worker tran-
sition and role redesign, investing in training, human–machine teaming practices, and
clear change￿management processes to ensure that automation augments rather than
replaces human contributions .

Gap Analysis and Framework Motivation
While the typologies proposed by Aagaard et al., Hsu et al., and Truong et al. each
offer valuable and complementary perspectives on entrepreneurial experimentation, they
reveal clear limitations when applied to the design and deployment of agentic systems
(Aagaard et al., 2021; Hsu et al., 2016; Truong et al., 2023).
Aagaard et al. present a typology derived from empirical studies of cleantech startups,
highlighting how experimentation supports learning, signaling, and stakeholder engage-
ment (Aagaard et al., 2021). Their categories capture the relational and strategic complex-
ity of real-world ventures, but remain inherently human-centered. The framework does not
consider whether experiments are automatable, decomposable into modular steps, or suit-
able for real-time decision-making. Experiment types such as Network Business Exper-
imentation and Sustainable Value-Driven Experimentation rely heavily on interpersonal
dynamics and tacit context, making them difficult to operationalize through autonomous
agents.
Hsu et al., in contrast, offer a methodological typology that distinguishes entrepreneurial
experiments along two axes: context realism and participant involvement (Hsu et al.,
2016). Their matrix is useful for evaluating the internal and external validity of academic
studies but does not address the executional or technical feasibility of agentic integration.
It lacks mechanisms for assessing automation potential, data availability, or autonomy
calibration.
Truong et al. offer the most relevant foundation for our purposes (Truong et al., 2023).
Their framework segments the entrepreneurial process into three phases, Discovery, Ex-
ploitation, and Operations, and aligns each phase with appropriate AI capabilities, such
as pattern recognition, prediction, and optimization. While this phasic logic resonates with
agentic system affordances, it remains conceptual. The framework does not differentiate
between types of experiments within each phase, nor does it provide criteria for assessing
feasibility, autonomy levels, or data requirements.

4.2.2
CARD Model
While the AEE strategic typology (Section 4.2.1) clarifies what kinds of experiments
matter at each venture phase, it does not determine whether those experiments can,
or should, be executed with an agentic system. To close this gap, the AEE’s second
pillar is the CARD model: a design-and-feasibility lens built around four orthogonal axes,
Capability, Autonomy, Risk, and Data richness. Because these CARD dimensions are
independent of venture maturity, the CARD model can flexibly overlay any phase of the
lifecycle.
Defining the CARD Axes
• Capability (C): What the agentic system must do (e.g. creative generation, predic-
tion, orchestration).
• Autonomy (A): How much decision-making is delegated to the system.
• Risk (R): The stakes if the system fails or produces unreliable outputs.

System Specification
The StoryGen tool developed in this thesis is a web-based application designed to auto-

ports analysts in minimizing the manual effort involved in interpreting swimlane diagrams,
extracting workflow components, and drafting structured story artifacts. The architecture
follows a modular design, combining a modern web front-end with a back-end pipeline
composed of AI-based services.
The front-end, built with React, Vite, and Tailwind CSS (Evan You and Vite Contributors,
2025; Meta, 2025; Tailwind Labs, 2025), enables a responsive user experience, allowing
users to upload diagrams and documents, inspect and refine extracted elements, and
review or export generated stories. On the back-end, the system integrates vision lan-
guage models (VLMs), large language models (LLMs), and vector databases. These
components are orchestrated across a multi-stage pipeline encompassing diagram in-
gestion, contextual enrichment, story generation, and structured export. Prompt behavior
is optimized using DSPy (Khattab et al., 2024), which enables declarative specification of
input–output mappings and recursive refinement based on representative examples.
To ensure maintainability and auditability, all core modules (LLMs, prompts, retrieval
databases, and UI elements) are version-controlled and decoupled. Outputs and prompt
templates are timestamped and stored, supporting reproducibility and enterprise-grade

traceability. The system is potentially extensible to adjacent tasks such as requirement
extraction, process audits, or test case drafting.
The following section details the pipeline components, tracing how input artifacts are trans-
formed into agile user stories through each stage of the system.
5.4.1
Pipeline Components
The proposed StoryGen agentic architecture (Figure 5.9) comprises five modular compo-
nents that transform swimlane diagrams into structured agile user stories.
The process begins when a user uploads a static image of a swimlane diagram. This
image is base64-encoded in-browser and sent via API to a vision language model, which
returns a structured JSON output. The output includes the process name, a list of swim-
lanes with unique IDs and labels, individual nodes (activities, decisions, start/end), and
the directed connections between them.
The extracted structure is rendered in an interactive editor, allowing users to inspect and
modify parsed nodes. They can rename or remove incorrect entries, add missing ones,
and selectively include or exclude nodes from the generation step. While parsing accuracy
is generally high, this interface provides a safeguard against edge cases or layout-specific
errors.
Once the node structure is finalized, the user initiates the story generation phase. For
each node, the system composes a context-aware prompt and generates a user story us-
ing a large language model. Prompts are dynamically constructed with injected examples
and cues from relevant uploaded documents. The resulting stories follow standard agile
syntax and include auto-generated acceptance criteria.
To support reliable output formatting, especially when interacting with vision language
models, the system integrates the DSPy framework. DSPy enables declarative input–
output specifications and applies recursive optimization to refine prompts. Fine-tuning
based on North Consulting historical backlog further stabilizes output quality, reducing
dependence on fragile prompt engineering.
Finally, the system incorporates retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) across two layers.
The first retrieves semantically similar user stories from a vector store, injecting exem-
plars into the prompt. The second retrieves relevant context snippets from embedded
documents, such as meeting notes and decisions, which enrich acceptance criteria and
improve semantic grounding.
5.4.2
System Output Format
Each selected node results in a single user story. Outputs include a concise title, a role–
action–goal description (“As a [role], I want to [action] so that [goal]”), a structured list
of acceptance criteria, and a traceability link to the originating node. Generated stories
can be reviewed in-browser and exported in CSV or Excel format, allowing seamless
integration with backlog systems such as Azure DevOps (Microsoft Corporation, 2025).

This chapter corresponds to DSII stage of the DRM methodology, presenting the results

The evaluation focuses on two technical stages. The first assesses parsing performance,

The second examines whether those structured representations can support the genera-

To simulate real-world use, twenty-four client tasks were processed using three different

Together, these experiments provide empirical evidence of the system’s feasibility, accu-

The objective of DS II is to empirically evaluate whether an agentic workflow can auto-
mate the conversion of swimlane diagrams into agile user stories with sufficient accuracy
and quality to be useful in a real-world consulting context. This includes assessing the
system’s performance across both the diagram parsing and story generation stages, and
benchmarking the results against expert consultant outputs.
The evaluation directly addresses the second research question introduced in Section
3.2.2, which asks to what extent an agentic system can automate the swimlane-to-user-

Finalized vs. draft outputs: Human-generated stories reflected finalized consultant de-

outputs reflect purely generative performance.
Single-rater scoring: Each user story was rated by a single expert, and no inter-rater

7.3
Theoretical Implications
This work contributes to and expands upon prior research in AI-based entrepreneurship
and agentic systems. It responds directly to gaps identified by Kraus et al. and Truong
et al., which highlight the lack of structured integration of AI into digital experimentation
frameworks (Kraus et al., 2018; Truong et al., 2023). While the academic discourse on AI
in entrepreneurship has matured considerably in recent years, most contributions remain
conceptual, focusing on theoretical potentials rather than tested, deployable systems. As
such, the majority of existing frameworks stop short of engaging with the technical, orga-
nizational, and operational dynamics involved in deploying agentic systems within real-
world ventures.
This thesis distinguishes itself by developing and empirically validating a domain-specific
agentic system designed to automate the conversion of swimlane diagrams into agile user
stories. Unlike earlier works that propose abstract roles for AI in entrepreneurial settings,
this study implements and tests a functioning prototype in an actual business context
(within North Consulting) and evaluates its performance relative to traditional human-only
methods. In doing so, it responds to recent calls in the literature for more practical and
grounded explorations of how AI can be integrated into innovation workflows.
Moreover, the empirical foundation of this study builds upon conceptual categorizations
proposed by Aagaard et al., Hsu et al., and Truong et al. particularly through its applica-
tion of the Capability-Autonomy-Risk-Data from the CARD model (Aagaard et al., 2021;
Hsu et al., 2016; Truong et al., 2023). While the initial theoretical target was to achieve
C2-C3 capabilities, A2-A3 autonomy, Low risk exposure, and D2 data availability, the ac-
tual deployment highlighted several mismatches. These deviations, particularly in data
maturity (observed D1-D2) and autonomy (A2 did not achieve A3), demonstrate the im-
portance of contextual adaptation when transitioning from theoretical models to practical
implementation.
This work also affirms findings from the broader literature on intelligent automation and
orchestration agents. Prior research has shown that AI systems can perform well in struc-
tured environments where schema alignment, repetitive logic, and rule-based validation
are possible.
The StoryGen agentic system developed operates within this paradigm, converting pro-
cess diagrams into user stories by leveraging structured input and validation criteria, such
as the INVEST rubric. However, unlike earlier implementations which often rely on syn-
thetic datasets or simulated business processes, this study grounds its evaluation in a live
deployment, offering rare insight into system behavior under real-world constraints.
Notably, this thesis extends earlier models by highlighting the limitations of existing as-
sumptions about data quality and accessibility. In many prior works, agentic performance
is implicitly conditioned on the presence of clean, centralized, and high-volume data
streams (D3 environments). In contrast, this project encountered a fragmented documen-
tation ecosystem marked by disperse data, inconsistent formats, and context-dependent
information scattered across multiple platforms. These challenges reveal critical gaps be-
tween theoretical data assumptions and enterprise data realities, and suggest that fully
autonomous (A3) agents may be difficult to deploy in the absence of deliberate data-
governance strategies.
Finally, this work contributes methodologically to the literature by combining structured
evaluation criteria (INVEST), practitioner feedback, and the CARD model framework to
assess the operational viability of the agentic system. This integrated approach provides

7.4
Practical Implications & Deployment Considerations
The empirical evaluation of StoryGen revealed not only where agentic systems can deliver
value, but also the conditions under which that value becomes accessible. Deploying
such systems within enterprise workflows is not a plug-and-play operation. It requires
careful alignment of organizational structures, technical pipelines, human workflows, and
governance mechanisms. Without these foundational enablers, system performance can
degrade rapidly or fail to deliver on its intended goals.
This section outlines the practical requirements and constraints that emerged during de-
ployment, structured around four core domains. Section 7.4.1 addresses the organiza-
tional preconditions for effective use, including documentation practices and workflow
adaptability. Section 7.4.2 details the technical architecture and its limits, especially re-
garding data flow, model behavior, and system modularity. Section 7.4.3 explores how
end users engage with the system, and the kinds of cognitive scaffolding or trust mech-
anisms required. Finally, Section 7.4.4 highlights the operational practices necessary to
sustain system performance over time.
The goal is not to present a formula for success, but to surface the infrastructure, human,
technical, and procedural, that must be in place to support responsible, scalable agentic
deployment in real-world settings.
7.4.1
Organizational Dynamics
The deployment of StoryGen revealed that organizational context plays a decisive role in
determining the viability and performance of agentic systems. Although North Consulting
had a well-established culture of process modeling, with swimlane diagrams used across
departments, the supporting documentation ecosystem lacked the structure necessary to
support full autonomy.
Key business data, such as stakeholder inputs, exception logic, and downstream depen-
dencies, were fragmented across SharePoint folders, email chains, slide decks, and team
wikis. This made it difficult for the information to be collected and accessed by StoryGen
to provide the full context required to generate complete and correct user stories without
manual intervention. As a result, human analysts remained essential not just for quality
control, but for contextual grounding. Rather than operating as a fully autonomous sys-
tem (A3), StoryGen functioned in a co-pilot mode (A2), producing draft outputs that were
subsequently reviewed and edited by domain experts.
Interestingly, this hybrid configuration was not viewed as a failure. On the contrary, con-
sultants found the division of labor well-aligned with their expectations. StoryGen handled
the repetitive structuring, while they applied higher-order judgment. The shift in workflow,
from original authorship to curation and refinement, was initially unfamiliar but ultimately
well-received. Analysts reported that this new role allowed them to focus more on stake-
holder needs, backlog planning, and cross-functional alignment.

The organizational shift requires careful change management so consultants using the
tool can understand their new way of contributing without feeling replaced. Targeted on-
boarding sessions, paired walkthroughs, and transparency around system limitations are
essential to re-frame expectations, improve adoption, and understand that the tool is not
meant to replace their expertise but to amplify it.
From a process standpoint, the biggest barrier to autonomy was not the complexity of the
task, but the inconsistency of the inputs. For future implementations, organizations should
prioritize centralized knowledge repositories, diagram-document linkages, and structured
tagging systems. Even lightweight metadata such as timestamps, owners, and swimlane-

In summary, organizational maturity in documentation and change-readiness proved to be
at least as important as technical capability in determining deployment success. Co-pilot
configurations may represent the most realistic and productive model for agentic systems
operating in enterprise environments that lack fully structured data infrastructures.

Technical Design Considerations
The deployment of StoryGen demonstrated that technical feasibility is closely tied to in-
put structure, system modularity, and model-task alignment. Rather than focusing purely
on algorithmic power, successful implementation depended on careful orchestration of
parsing reliability, structured data inputs, and well-scoped generation prompts.
Parsing accuracy was a critical factor in ensuring end-to-end system consistency. In this

extraction of swimlane and node structures in standardized diagram layouts. This finding
highlights a key precondition for agentic deployment: diagrams must conform to consis-
tent visual schemas and contain unambiguous role and task labels. In the absence of
such formatting standards, even high-performing vision language models may struggle
to produce usable outputs. For organizations aiming to adopt similar systems, diagram
governance, through modeling conventions or pre-processing normalization, emerges as
a foundational requirement.
Beyond parsing, StoryGen’s ability to generate structurally correct and contextually rele-

performed well on a per-node basis, it was not designed to infer deeper process logic or
resolve inter-task dependencies. This suggests that orchestration-level capabilities (C4-

links diagram elements to domain-specific intents, constraints, and edge cases. In prac-

documentation standards that extend beyond visual diagrams.

its effectiveness was limited by the quality and coverage of the example corpus. This
suggests that for RAG to be useful, the underlying story library must be richly tagged with
attributes like actor role, process type, and context domain. A sparse or noisy precedent
base offers little retrieval value and may introduce confusion rather than clarity. Future
deployments should treat precedent curation as a first-class engineering task, not an af-

7.4.3
Human-Tool Collaboration
The deployment of StoryGen reinforced a central theme in applied AI research: agentic
performance is inseparable from human interaction. Although the system was technically
capable of generating structured user stories, its adoption, perceived value, and opera-
tional impact were all mediated by how human users engaged with it.
Consultants consistently described StoryGen as a “first-pass accelerator”, a tool that gen-
erated structurally sound drafts which they could then review, refine, and contextualize.
This division of labor proved to be productive. Analysts reported time savings of 2-3 hours
per swimlane, and appreciated that the system handled repetitive formatting and boiler-
plate tasks. Importantly, users did not expect perfection. Instead, they evaluated the tool
based on whether it provided a meaningful head start on a cognitively intensive activity.
From this standpoint, even partially accurate outputs were considered valuable, provided
they could be efficiently validated and improved.
However, the system’s integration into existing workflows requires adjustment. The shift
from authorship to curation introduces uncertainty around task boundaries, responsibility,
and performance expectations. To address this, the deployment team must conduct tar-
geted onboarding sessions, clarify user roles, and implement lightweight feedback loops
to gather concerns and suggestions. These practices are instrumental in establishing
trust, not just in the system’s capabilities, but in its intended role.
The specialists also expressed specific preferences for how collaboration should be struc-
tured. They favored workflows that preserved human oversight while offering clear indica-
tors of output provenance, confidence, or potential gaps. This points to a broader design
implication: the effectiveness of agentic systems is not only a matter of what they gen-
erate, but how transparently they communicate their reasoning and limitations. Features
such as role attribution prompts, editable suggestion previews, and story-by-story ver-
sion histories were frequently requested as ways to maintain control without reverting to
manual work.
Beyond interface mechanics, cognitive trust plays a decisive role in adoption. Consultant
analysts are more likely to rely on the system when its outputs mirror their own mental
models, when phrasing, task framing, and role identification aligns with what they would
have written themselves. This highlights a crucial insight: LLM-generated content must
not only be correct, but familiar. Consistency with organizational idioms, documentation
tone, and stakeholder expectations is as important as semantic accuracy.
Taken together, these findings suggest that agentic systems should be designed around
collaboration, not substitution. Rather than aiming for end-to-end automation, systems
like StoryGen benefit from aligning with human judgment, providing legible outputs, and
offering interfaces that support selective refinement. In data-constrained or high-stakes
domains, this human-in-the-loop model is not a compromise, it is a design strength.

7.4.4
Toward Sustainable Deployment
Deploying an agentic system is not a one-time technical exercise, it is an ongoing op-
erational commitment. While StoryGen’s initial rollout demonstrated practical value in a
co-pilot configuration, sustaining that value over time requires investment in monitoring,
adaptation, and organizational alignment.
One critical dimension is performance monitoring. Even if initial outputs are acceptable,
the system’s relevance can drift as workflows evolve, team structures change, or process
documentation is updated. Without feedback loops to track usage quality, error patterns,
and post-edit effort, it becomes difficult to distinguish temporary anomalies from systemic
degradation. Metrics such as average time-to-acceptance, post-edit word count, rejection
rate, and prompt failure frequency should be tracked at regular intervals. These indicators
can inform both technical updates and product roadmap decisions.
Retraining and prompt iteration also become more important as organizational contexts
shift. The effectiveness of generation depends heavily on the clarity and consistency of
system prompts, which in turn reflect assumptions about roles, workflows, and formatting
norms. What works for one team or diagram type may degrade in a different use case.
Prompt engineering must be treated not as a static configuration, but as a living artifact,
versioned, annotated, and iteratively refined through real-world use.
Knowledge management is another long-term constraint. Systems like StoryGen rely on
precedent examples, standard terminology, and consistent formatting to produce relevant
outputs. Without structured processes for capturing new stories, curating high-quality
exemplars, and deprecating outdated artifacts, the system’s internal knowledge base risks
becoming stale or contradictory. As such, teams must define roles and responsibilities for
precedent library maintenance, and invest in tagging, metadata enrichment, and review
workflows that keep the system aligned with evolving domain practices.
Finally, governance and oversight must evolve alongside technical capabilities. Even
when deployed in co-pilot mode, agentic systems shape team dynamics, influence deliv-
erable content, and introduce new accountability questions. Who is responsible for veri-
fying a system-generated story? How should performance be evaluated when the source
of text is mixed human/machine? Addressing these questions requires more than tool
training, it demands clear norms around ownership, traceability, and escalation protocols.
In sum, sustaining agentic system performance is not simply a matter of technical upkeep.
It involves a layered approach to operational monitoring, feedback infrastructure, domain
knowledge curation, and collaborative governance. Without these supports in place, even
well-performing tools can drift into irrelevance, or worse, quietly produce unreliable out-
puts in ways that are difficult to detect or correct. For organizations adopting systems like
StoryGen, sustainability must be treated as a core design objective from the outset.
In summary, the deployment of StoryGen underscored that effective agentic integration is
not determined solely by model performance, but by a broader ecosystem of enablers:
standardized inputs, modular system design, aligned user workflows, and continuous
feedback mechanisms. Organizations seeking to implement similar tools must treat agen-
tic deployment as a cross-functional initiative, requiring investment not just in technology,
but in documentation practices, interface design, and operational governance. These in-
sights set the stage for the next section, which examines the specific limitations of the
present study and the boundaries of its generalizability.

7.5
Discussing the Limitations
While the findings presented in this thesis demonstrate the practical viability and archi-
tectural coherence of agentic systems like StoryGen, they must be interpreted within the
boundaries of the study’s design, scope, and assumptions. The evaluation was conducted
under real-world constraints, using a prototype system deployed in a single organizational
context. As such, the results offer directional insight rather than comprehensive validation.
This section outlines the most significant limitations affecting the interpretation and gen-
eralizability of the findings. These are grouped into four categories: methodological con-
straints related to scoring and model evaluation; contextual scope limitations tied to do-
main specificity; system-level assumptions that shaped design boundaries; and measure-
ment gaps that restrict claims about long-term or downstream impact. While these con-
straints do not invalidate the observed results, they do inform where caution is warranted,
and where future research must extend the empirical base.
7.5.1
Methodological Constraints
While the evaluation of StoryGen provided strong directional insights, several methodolog-
ical constraints limit the generalizability and precision of the findings.
First, the story evaluation protocol used a simplified scoring structure as each story re-
ceived a single holistic rating on a 5-point scale based on the INVEST rubric, and was
scored by one of two domain experts. Although this approach aligns with how consul-
tants often evaluate story utility in practice, it lacks the inter-rater redundancy needed
to assess scoring consistency. No inter-rater reliability statistics (e.g., Cohen’s κ) were
computed, and scoring variability across raters cannot be ruled out.
Second, there was an inherent asymmetry between the conditions under which human
and agentic outputs were evaluated. Human-authored stories were finalized consultant
deliverables, whereas agentic and baseline stories were rated in their first-draft form. This
difference may have introduced a quality bias in favor of the human benchmark and likely
underestimates the potential of LLM-generated drafts once refined through post-editing.
Third, the sample size, while sufficient to reveal performance trends, was relatively small.
The evaluation included 72 stories drawn from 7 business process diagrams, balanced
across three generation conditions (human, StoryGen, GPT-4o). Although care was taken
to select diverse tasks and control for confounding factors, the dataset was not large
enough to support statistically powered comparisons or subgroup analysis.
Finally, the diagram parsing evaluation was limited to three models (Gemini Flash 2.5,
GPT-4o, and Claude Sonnet 3.5) applied to a small set of diagrams. Gemini Flash 2.5
outperformed the others in terms of parsing accuracy, and was therefore used as the
production parser during story generation. However, this does not imply that Flash is
categorically superior for all diagram types or workflows. All models used a common
prompt template that was iteratively refined across several testing cycles. It is possible
that alternative prompt tuning strategies could yield better results for other models. As
such, the observed parsing advantage of Flash should be interpreted as a strong local
result, not a definitive benchmark.
Taken together, these methodological constraints suggest that while the evaluation de-
sign was appropriate for a mid-stage prototype assessment, broader or more conclusive
claims, especially regarding general model performance or scoring consistency, will re-
quire more rigorous and larger-scale study designs.

7.5.3
System Assumptions
Several design choices and environmental assumptions shaped the architecture and eval-

sults. Notably, the system was not tested under conditions that would allow full realization
of orchestration-level autonomy or end-to-end retrieval-augmented generation workflows.
At the outset of the project, it was assumed that North Consulting maintained a rich, well-
structured documentation ecosystem, with process artefacts sufficiently linked to support
semantic inference and grounding. However, this assumption did not hold in practice.
Within the first few weeks of deployment, it became clear that relevant data, such as prior
stories, templates, and acceptance criteria, were either missing, outdated, or distributed
across unstructured storage systems which StoryGen did not have access to. As a re-
sult, components that depend on data richness and linkage, such as retrieval-augmented
generation (RAG) or declarative programming layers like DSPy, could not be effectively
tested in this implementation. Their theoretical value remains strong, but could not be
empirically evaluated under the available conditions.
Additionally, StoryGen’s architecture was designed for modularity and controllability rather
than full autonomy. The system generates user stories on a per-node basis, without at-
tempting to infer interdependencies, simulate orchestration, or reconcile swimlane-level
flows. This design constraint served practical goals (traceability, transparency), but it also
limits the system’s coverage of the full problem space.
These observations have direct implications for the study’s alignment with the CARD
model, making the observed deployment reflect lower levels across all dimensions. These
deviations were not signs of system failure, but consequences of environmental con-
straints, particularly data maturity. As such, this study provides only a partial and context-

7.5.4
Open Measurement Gaps
In addition to the methodological and contextual boundaries outlined above, several im-
portant variables were not measured in this study, either due to scope limitations or prac-
tical constraints. As a result, some inferences about system value must be treated as
provisional.
Most notably, the evaluation did not capture the time or cognitive effort required to post-
edit StoryGen outputs into production-ready deliverables. While consultants qualitatively
described the outputs as “very close to what I’d write myself” or “90% there,” no quantita-
tive data was collected on how long post-processing actually took. Without this, it remains
unclear whether the observed time savings in generation translate into equivalent reduc-
tions in total time-to-value. In high-pressure delivery environments, even small friction in
refinement can erode headline efficiency gains.
Similarly, the study did not track downstream effects on team-level delivery metrics, such
as backlog velocity, sprint planning accuracy, or rework rates. These are critical indicators
of whether story quality holds up over time and contributes meaningfully to agile perfor-
mance. The absence of these measures means that claims about StoryGen’s strategic
value (beyond tactical acceleration) remain speculative.
These measurement gaps stem in part from practical constraints. Consultant availability,
project timelines, and access to downstream performance data made it infeasible to run
a longitudinal study within the scope of this thesis. While the short-term evaluation was
sufficient to demonstrate structural viability and perceived usefulness, future work will
need to capture longer-term outcomes to assess systemic impact.
Together, these gaps do not undermine the credibility of the results obtained, but they
do place boundaries on how far those results can be extrapolated. The findings should
be interpreted as proof-of-concept at the workflow level, not as evidence of sustained
performance improvements across the delivery lifecycle.
Taken together, these limitations define the interpretive boundaries of the study. While
StoryGen demonstrated strong performance under constrained conditions, its evaluation
was shaped by specific design choices, data availability, and organizational context. The
findings offer clear evidence of feasibility, but not yet of generalizability or long-term im-
pact. Addressing these gaps through broader deployments, richer instrumentation, and
extended observation windows will be essential to validating the full potential of agentic
systems, and to critically assessing the robustness of frameworks like CARD model in
guiding real-world adoption.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore how agentic systems (autonomous or semi autonomous
LLM-based workflows) can both structure and accelerate Digital Entrepreneurial Experi-
mentation. In pursuit of this goal, we first developed a dual conceptual framework, the
Agentic Entrepreneurial Experimentation Framework, composed by a strategic typology
of experimentation aligned with the entrepreneurial lifecycle, and the CARD model, a four
axis feasibility lens (Capability, Autonomy, Risk, Data) for designing and prioritizing AI
driven workflows. These contributions extend existing DEE and agentic theories by com-
bining a lifecycle typology with a practical feasibility assessment, guiding scholars and
practitioners on where and how to deploy agentic support.
To validate our framework, we partnered with North Consulting to build StoryGen, an
agentic pipeline that converts swimlane diagrams into INVEST compliant user stories us-
ing vision language parsing and LLM generation. Across 24 real world tasks, StoryGen
reduced median cycle time from 180 minutes to 20 seconds (a 99.8% reduction) and cut
per diagram cost from €600 to under €0.01, while producing first drafts that averaged 73%
of the quality of human finalized stories accordin to the evaluation of expert reviewers who
estimated the quality to be around 90% if compared to human generated first drafts.
Although our best parser (Gemini Flash 2.5) achieved perfect syntactic accuracy, deeper
semantic anomalies highlighted the need for content level validation beyond well formed
JSON.
Together, these results confirm that agentic systems can drastically improve speed and
cost-efficiency in DEE without sacrificing core quality.
Beyond empirical gains, our findings carry both theoretical and practical implications.
Theoretically, the integration of a lifecycle aligned framework with the CARD model lens
offers a structured methodology for agentic augmented experimentation, filling a gap in
prior literature.
Practically, StoryGen exemplifies how a co-pilot approach can offload repetitive, high la-
tency tasks, freeing analysts to focus on strategic work. Notably, the results challenge the
traditional Iron Triangle of project management, demonstrating that time, cost, and quality
can be optimized simultaneously under the right conditions.
At the same time, this study has important limitations. Our work was anchored in a single,
late stage DEE case at one firm, so generalization to ideation phases or other industries
remains to be tested. The evaluation depended on generation of first draft comparisons
against finalized human results, and no inter rater reliability metrics where applied. Addi-
tionally the sample of 24 tasks, while indicative, cannot capture the full diversity of process
complexities.
The retrieval augmentation yielded limited benefits with a sparse corpus, suggesting that
richer knowledge bases will be critical for future implementations.
Looking ahead, we recommend extending the CARD model to earlier DEE phases, such
as ideation and market validation, to understand low data, high risk contexts. Gover-
nance mechanisms and ethical oversight should be embedded as autonomy increases,
and longitudinal studies are needed to assess long term impacts on venture performance

and organizational learning. Iterative updates and testing under a richer environment will
likely drive first draft quality even closer to human parity and pave the way for near fully
autonomous workflows.
In closing, this thesis demonstrates that agentic systems are more than mere assistants.
They are active collaborators in entrepreneurial experimentation. By accelerating iteration,
reducing friction, and enabling intelligent automation, they herald a new paradigm in digital
entrepreneurship: Ventures can simultaneously optimize time, cost, and quality, unlocking
higher order strategic potential in dynamic business environments.
