Abstract
In 2015, in Paris, world leaders gathered at the UN climate change conference (COP21) where they
made an agreement on how to tackle climate change and the negative consequences that follows [55].
The most important goal for the nations, is to reduce global greenhouse gas emissions, to limit the global
temperature increase in this century to 2°C, while pursuing efforts to limit the increase to 1.5°C [55].
When the climate changes, there is a need for climate adaption. It costs materials and energy, which
emits greenhouse gases. This potentially contributes even more to climate change and there may in the
future, be an even bigger need for climate adaption.
In the Danish drainage industry there is a limited focus on emissions from activities not owned or con-
trolled by the company, as e.g. project related activities (embodied emissions (Scope 3)) [48]. Activities
related to handling of stormwater and wastewater belongs to the Danish drainage industry [18]. Even
though there is limited data on embodied emissions in the Danish drainage industry, there is still in-
centive to believe that they are high. It is known that 10% of the CO2 emissions from Denmark comes
from the construction and building processes, including the production of building materials [35]. In
order to work with reduction of CO2 emissions in projects in the Danish drainage industry, a baseline
of current CO2 emissions and guidance for lowering CO2 emissions from project-related activities are
needed. That is why the consulting engineering company WSP Denmark is commissioning a study in-
vestigating the carbon footprint of project-related Scope 3 emissions from the Danish drainage industry
and an investigation of the reduction possibilities.
Based on three selected projects, the combination of which is assumed representative for the entire
Danish drainage industry, a baseline of emissions in 2022 was calculated. The baseline was calculated
based on an average climate change intensity number, of the three projects, with the unit CO2/DKK.
Data on total investments made in the Danish drainage industry in 2021 was then used to calculate the
baseline, which was calculated to be 173,340 tons CO2eq. A “Near term Science Based Target mitigation
pathway” was furthermore defined, to investigate how much the industry must reduce from 2022 towards
2030, to be Paris aligned and stay below the limit of 1.5°C [44]. To succeed with staying below the 1.5°C
limit, the baseline of 2022 must be reduced with 42% to 100,537 tons CO2eq in 2030.
To achieve this, a methodology for reduction of CO2 in the Danish drainage industry is needed. It is
important that a common course and standard practice is developed for the industry. A tool called the
“Circle of actions”, developed by WSP Denmark, is a systematic approach for reducing carbon emissions
throughout all project stages [61]. This study examines the impact of using the tool and investigates the
reduction possibilities. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) will be used for quantification of CO2eq for the
baseline and two different case studies. LCA stages from cradle to the use stage are within the system
boundary for all LCA’s conducted in this report.
The "Circle of actions" was, for both case studies, used to analyse and investigate where action towards
a lower carbon footprint could be taken. The impact of using the "Circle of actions" was quantified.
The first case study analysis, of "Stenlængegård" (land site development project), showed many op-
II
==PAGE:5==
portunities for reducing project-related carbon emissions. Implementing some of the suggested actions,
using the "Circle of actions", showed a possible reduction of 42%. The second case study analysis, of
"Frederiksberg Hospital" (climate adaption project), showed a possible reduction of 70%. It was thus
concluded that the "Circle of actions" is a useful and concrete tool, when trying to reduce carbon emis-
sions in projects in the Danish drainage industry. It can be the tool making it possible for the industry
to reduce project-related Scope 3 emissions and comply with the Science Based Targets limit of 1.5°C.

Introduction
The world is in constant development and within the last 10 years a lot has happened especially concern-
ing climate change and the environment [18]. When the climate changes, a need for climate adaption
may arise. Climate adaption costs materials and energy, which emits greenhouse gases. This poten-
tially contributes even more to climate change and there may in the future, be an even bigger need for
climate adaption. Activities related to handling of stormwater and wastewater belongs to the Danish
drainage industry [18]. The road to a more climate friendly drainage industry might be challenging,
but not impossible. In the Danish drainage industry, the focus has primarily been on reducing direct
emissions controlled by the company and indirect emissions which arises from the company’s energy
consumption [48]. There is a limited focus on emissions from activities not owned or controlled by the
company, as e.g. project related activities (embodied emissions) . Even though there is limited data
on embodied emissions in the Danish drainage industry, there is still incentive to believe that they are
high. It is known that the building industry generates 37% of the annual global CO2 emissions where
10% is embodied carbon [42]. 10 % of the CO2 emissions from Denmark originate from the construction
and building processes, including the production of building materials [35].
In order to work with reduction of CO2 emissions in projects in the Danish drainage industry, a baseline
of current CO2 emissions is needed. WSP Denmark is commissioning a study investigating the carbon
footprint of the Danish drainage industry and an investigation of reduction possibilities. In this report
a baseline of project related emissions in the Danish drainage industry in 2022 will be calculated.
The baseline will be based on three selected drainage projects, the combination of which is assumed
representative for the entire Danish drainage industry. Furthermore, a “Near term Science Based Target
mitigation pathway” will be defined, to investigate how much the industry must reduce to be Paris
aligned and stay below the limit of 1.5°C towards 2030 [44]. To succeed with staying below the 1.5°C
limit, methodologies for reduction of CO2 in the drainage industry is needed. It is important that a
common course and standard practice is developed. A tool called the “Circle of actions”, developed by
WSP Denmark, may be the tool for this purpose [61]. This tool is a systematic approach for reducing
the carbon footprint throughout all project stages.
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) will be used for
quantification of CO2eq, as it accounts for all environmental impacts throughout the entire life cycle of
a project. LCA will be used for the calculation of CO2 footprint of the baseline and will furthermore
be used to calculate the environmental impact of two case studies. The "Circle of actions" will, for the
two case studies, be used to analyse and investigate where action towards a lower carbon footprint can
be taken. The impact of using the "Circle of actions" will then be quantified.
The report is made in collaboration with WSP Denmark who, among other things, has given me the
opportunity to work with different case studies. This has led to participation in several meetings with
customers. Furthermore, it has led to a submission of an abstract to the Danish Water Conference 2023
and I have been selected to present my thesis work at the WSP international web conference in autumn.
The abstract submitted for the conferences can be seen in Appendix J.

1.1
Climate change
The global temperature is rising, the sea level are rising, glaciers are shrinking, coral reefs are dying, and
more extreme weather is occurring. These events are just some of the consequences that climate change
causes. Climate change is long term shifts in temperature and weather patterns [56]. This change of
patterns can be natural, but since 1800, human activities have been the main driver [56]. Climate change
is primarily a result of the burning of fossil fuels, as this generates greenhouse gases [56]. Greenhouse
gases trap the heat from the sun, which raises the global temperature [56]. Examples of greenhouse
gases are carbon dioxide (CO2) and methane (CH4) [56].
In several UN reports, scientists and government reviewers has agreed that the temperature rise should
be less than 1.5°C [56].
This would help us maintain a livable climate and environment.
Current
predictions point towards a temperature rise of 2.8°C [56]. Action towards reducing greenhouse gases
globally must happen as fast as possible, before it is too late.
1.1.1
The Paris Agreement
Climate change is a global problem [55]. It requires international cooperation to coordinate goals and
solutions [55]. In 2015, in Paris, world leaders gathered at the UN climate change conference (COP21)
where they made an agreement on how to tackle climate change and the negative consequences that
follows [55]. 194 parties have joined the Paris Agreement which is legally binding [55].
The first and most important goal for the nations, is reducing global greenhouse gas emissions, to
limit the global temperature increase in this century to 2°C, while pursuing efforts to limit the increase
to 1.5°C [55]. The commitments made by the participating countries, is reviewed every five year to
make sure that they update their national climate action plan and keep what they promise [55]. The
participating countries furthermore work together to adapt to the impact of climate change [55]. With
the agreement comes assistance in the countries climate mitigation and adaptation [55].
1.1.2
Science Based Targets Initiative
The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) is a partnership between CDP, the United Nations Global
Compact, World Resources Institute (WRI) and the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF) and they
have one goal: To reduce global greenhouse gas emissions [44].
The purpose of science based targets (SBT) is to provide the companies that want to reduce their carbon
footprint, with guidance and a pathway to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions [44]. Furthermore
they help prevent the worst consequences of climate change. If a target complies, with what the most
recent climate change science believes is necessary to align with the Paris agreement, it is "Science
based" [44]. SBT defines concrete actions based on knowledge with a focus on the environment and the
climate, while at the same time making sense for business [44]. They work together with scientists and
climate and sustainability experts to develop Science Based Target (SBT) methodologies [44].

1.2
The Danish drainage industry
The Danish drainage industry consist of 104 wastewater (WW) companies. They are mainly owned by
the Danish municipalities. The drainage industry is a natural monopoly, which means that households
and companies cannot freely select which WW company they want. They are automatically connected
to their local WW treatment plant [14].
When it rains more than the sewer systems can handle, the sewers overflow. This result in the wastew-
ater, reaching the surface water.
Some of the water will flood basements and low-lying area.
The
remaining water will eventually run over the terrain until it finds its way to streams, lakes etc. This is a
very serious pollution source that can contaminate the surrounding environment and groundwater [49].
It is the WW supply companies’ responsibility to ensure that wastewater and stormwater are diverted
in a safe way [49]. The WW supply companies are often owned by the municipalities, and they are
typically also responsible for climate adaption and mitigation [49]. Stormwater is defined as the rainfall
that falls onto paved areas like roofs, streets, roads etc., then flows across and into the ground [22]. The
companies should prevent overflowing sewers during heavy rainfall and thereby prevent pollution [49].
Wastewater plan
The Danish environmental protection law requires all municipalities in Denmark to make a “Wastewater
plan” which describes the status and plans for the handling of WW and stormwater [47]. The plan also
describes existing and planned sewage areas and how wastewater and stormwater are handled outside
the sewage areas [47].
The wastewater plan is revisited regularly and corrected with new plans for maintenance, renewal
etc. [47]. Many municipalities in Denmark are currently in the process of separating the drainage of
wastewater and stormwater [47]. This means that stormwater is handled locally or diverted in separate
systems to e.g., stormwater basins. It can also be decided by the municipality, that roof and surface
water should be disconnected from the sewer network [47]. This is typically done in residential areas
with villa houses, where it is left to the landowners themselves to establish stormwater handling.
The WW plan is a very important tool for the municipalities when implementing climate adaptions
plans, but they are lacking with information on CO2 reduction of project related activities (Scope 3).
Why this is considered important will be elaborated in the following section. The WW plans are realised
by municipal WW companies, which through a change in the Danish water sector law, has been given
the opportunity to invest in climate adaption [47].

Scopes of emissions
The carbon footprint of the Danish drainage industry follows the classification of emissions, using the
international standard, the "Greenhouse Gas Protocol" [60]. This Standard divides emissions into three
categories: Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 emissions, see Figure 1. CO2eq stands for CO2 equivalent.
This means that all greenhouse gas emissions are calculated, so that they correspond to tons CO2 per
kg emission, as the effect from other emissions, like e.g. methane (CH4) and laughing gas (N2O) is much

higher than the effect from carbon dioxide (CO2).
Figure 1: Illustration of Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 emissions. The illustration originates from this source: [13]
In the drainage industry Scope 1 is all greenhouse gas emissions emitted from wastewater plants, biogas
plants and vehicles owned by the company. This is emissions that the company is directly responsible
for. Examples are laughing gas from wastewater treatment plants, methane from biogas production,
disposal of sludge, and emissions from fuel consumption in vehicles. [59]
Scope 2 is all greenhouse gas emissions that comes from purchased energy (Electricity and heat).
This is emissions the company is indirectly responsible for. Examples are transport of stormwater and
wastewater, wastewater treatment, and energy use from administration. [59]
Scope 3 is the remaining indirect emissions, emitted because of the company’s activities.
This is
emissions from sources that aren’t owned by the company, except for purchased energy, which is defined
as Scope 2 emissions. Scope 3 includes emissions from services, materials, and products bought by
the company. The main activity that contributes to Scope 3 emissions, in the drainage industry, is
construction projects. This includes material production, transport of materials, electricity, and fuel
consumption on the construction site. [59]
The organisation DANVA, the Danish Ministry of the Environment, Danish Industry, and the industry
association Danish Environmental Technology, held a joint conference in 2020, focusing, among other
things, on the pathway to an energy and climate-neutral water sector in 2030. With the Danish Climate
law, two things that will have an impact on the handling of wastewater has been implemented. A limit

for the discharge of laughing gas emissions has been set. Furthermore, a target for the recycling of
phosphorus from wastewater and sludge, has also been set. [4]
Water and wastewater companies in Denmark have informed the government that they expect to become
climate positive in 2030. This goal only focuses on the energy use and emissions in the operation phase.
This means that they focus on reducing energy consumption and reducing greenhouse gas emissions from
wastewater treatment plants. This can be categorised as Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions. There is, in the
objective of being energy and climate neutral in 2030, limited focus on reducing Scope 3 emissions. The
announcement saying that the water sector is well on its way to become climate positive, may therefore
be misleading, if Scope 3 emissions are taken into account. [4]
1.3.1
The Paris model agreement
The water sector has, in collaboration with the Danish Ministry of Environment, made a "Paris-model
agreement" called the "Paris model for the water sector in Denmark" [48]. It was agreed on, in June
2020. The report states that all drinking and wastewater companies, covered by the water sector law,
should report their ambitions regarding energy use, energy production, CO2 emissions, laughing gas
emissions and methane emissions, towards 2030 [48]. The ambitions were reported to the the Danish
Environmental Agency. The goal is that the water industry can contribute to the national climate goals
and ambitions. In this plan for the water industry, it is only Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions which are
accounted for [48].
This means that goals for reduction of Scope 3 emissions is not included in the agreement.
In the
report, "Paris model for the water sector in Denmark" the explanation is the following (Citation has
been translated but originates from [48], page 2.):
"Data regarding the water companies’ climate footprints from chemical consumption, con-
struction and transport activities are not included in the agreement, as it has been assessed
that the footprints are included elsewhere in the national climate account and that it will be
too administratively heavy for the companies to calculate it." [48]
This clearly shows that there is a need for a greater focus on Scope 3 emissions in the Danish drainage
industry. It is not about who is responsible for it, but where action towards a lower carbon footprint
can be taken.
The national climate account (calculated as CO2eq) presented in the "Paris-model agreement" report
is, as mentioned, only for Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions. The data available for Scope 1 and Scope 2
emissions, from the Danish water industry (Excluding drinking water), in 2019, can be seen i Figure 2.

National calculations of Scope 3 emissions in the drainage industry have not been carried out. Some

and Scope 2 emissions.

Here it can be seen that Scope 3 emissions are much higher than Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions. Scope
3 emissions are based on Novafos’ purchases and costs in 2021 [59]. Contractor services, or construction
projects, accounts for 50.000 tons CO2eq which equals to about 60 % of the Scope 3 emissions from

It should be noted that the calculations of Scope 3 emissions, made by Novafos, is
associated with high uncertainty, as they are calculated using EXIOBASE [59]. EXIOBASE is based on
economic input-output-based models, where the economic flows from many countries’ national accounts
are coupled with information on emissions from each sector [46]. For each industry a climate footprint
is calculated as kg CO2eq per DKK. As the current version of EXIOBASE is based on national accounts
from 2011, the results are affected by a high temporal and technological uncertainty, as there have been
significant technology development the last 12 years [59]. A second aspect which may also contribute
to the uncertainty of using EXIOBASE is that some of the sectors are very heterogeneous in terms of
CO2eq per DKK.
This means that the footprint of Scope 3 emissions might look different if other data sources were used.
But the results still give incentive to think that the carbon footprint of Scope 3 emissions from project
related activities in the Danish drainage industry are higher than Scope 1 and Scope 2.
The second example of a calculation of Scope 3 emissions is found in a report called
"The climate
footprint of public procurement, 2019" [25]. This report displays results of calculations of the CO2eq
footprint of different parts of the public sector. The calculations made for this report are also based on
EXIOBASE, and the results are therefore also affected by same uncertainty, as described earlier.
The report states that "Building and Construction work" is responsible for about 20 % of the entire
footprint of the public sector [25]. It should be noted that "Building and construction work" includes
all work done within this sector, not only related to drainage. As the building and construction sector
is responsible for about 1/5 of the total footprint of the public sector, the report gives good reason to
believe that the Scope 3 emissions from project-related activities in the drainage industry, are high.
These examples indicate that there is good reason to believe that the significant environmental impact,
in the Danish drainage industry, is found in Scope 3. This is especially for project related activities,
as you use a lot of CO2-heavy materials such as concrete, metal, plastic and diesel. It is therefore
important to gain focus on reducing emissions from project related activities, as the hypothesis is that
this footprint of Scope 3 is higher than Scope 1 and Scope 2. One consequence of climate change, that
will affect the drainage industry, is heavier rainfall and more frequent cloudbursts. This results in an
increased need for climate adaption. This costs materials and energy, which emits greenhouse gases. It
is therefore important that there is a gained focus on making projects, in the Danish drainage industry,
with the lowest possible CO2eq footprint.

The "Circle of actions" tool for CO2 reduction
Figure 4: The tool "Circle of actions" for reducing CO2 throughout all project stages, including examples of each action.
The tool is developed by Søren Gabriel from WSP Denmark [10].
The tool includes seven actions. Each action is elaborated below. They are written in the order in which
the actions must be considered. This meaning that the action in the top right corner, "Build less", is
the first one to consider when starting a new project.
• Build less: This action is about utilising the systems’ capacity and remaining lifetime to the
limit. This means that you should renovate rather than build new, upgrade the existing drainage
system rather than installing a completely new one. The goal is to build as little as possible, but
at the same time meet service goals and maintain safety. Furthermore, smaller and more simple
facilities must be planned. Instead of building new, temporary solutions can be established, which
can postpone larger installations. We face a future with new technologies and materials, and CO2
might therefore be saved by postponing projects. If we try to solve a problem, it often costs CO2
and it must therefore be investigated whether this makes sense with carbon footprint in mind, or
whether we can live with the problems that climate change brings with it.
• Build for the future: This action is about only building what is necessary right now. It is
important to remember that the world is constantly changing, and the long lifetimes of project
might not always be that appropriate when the goal is to achieve the lowest possible carbon
footprint. It would therefore be more beneficial with actions that are more flexible and with focus
on what is needed right know, rather than what might be needed in the future. It is about building
for today with the possibility of increasing the capacity in the future when the need arises.
• Build collaboratively: Involving citizens and landowners in projects can be an important part
of CO2 reduction of a project. It is important to involve them in solving the problems. It could
be beneficial to create a coherent narrative about what responsibility the citizens have and what
their own options for CO2 reduction are. The citizens should be made aware of the benefits of
using more visible solutions like permeable pavements, LAR (Local diversion of stormwater), local
storage and stormwater and cloudburst ventils etc.

• Build wisely: This action is about building with local conditions in mind. Instead of doing
projects based on national service goals, local service targets must be established. By doing this
the local need for climate protection can be addressed. The focus should be on solving the problem
where it is easiest and where the lowest carbon footprint can be achieved. This can e.g. be done
by utilising the local area for detention and delay of stormwater, build functional solutions, build
flexible, minimise dense impervious areas and minimise transport. An integrated thinking in the
industry today is that "Since we are building anyways, we might as well also...". This type of
thinking must be changed so that the residual life of the drainage systems is utilised to its limit.
• Build light: This action is about building simpler and smaller facilities with the possibility of
percolation, diversion and storage on terrain. This means that local protection against water on
terrain should be thought of instead of larger facilities.
• Build low carbon: This action is about doing the projects with the lowest possible carbon
footprint. This means using less CO2 heavy materials like metal, plastic and concrete. It about
finding the most optimal balance between the facility, operation and carbon footprint.
• Operate low carbon: This action is about operating the finished projects with lowest possible
carbon footprint. This could be done by using green energy sources, optimise machine hours and
maintain instead of replacing.
1.5
Quantification of CO2eq using Life Cycle Assessment
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a methodology used to analyse environmental impacts, during the
entire life cycle of either a product, process, service or in this report, a construction project. LCA covers
different impact categories, thus in this study the only focus is the impact category "Climate change"
with the unit tons CO2eq. The quality of the data used, determines the quality of the LCA results. [28]
A LCA can be divided into four different stages: Goal and scope definition, Life cycle Inventory Assess-
ment (LCI), Life cycle impact Assessment (LCIA) and interpretation [28]. In this report, the following
aspects are addressed.
• Goal definition: In this section the reasons for carrying out the study is defined as well as the
target audience of the LCA study [28].
• Scope definition: In this section the functional unit is defined, which describes the quantitative
and qualitative aspects of the function.
Furthermore, the system boundary is defined, which
states what is included and excluded in the conduction of the LCA. The geographical, temporal
and technological representativeness is also described in this section. [28]
• Life cycle inventory assessment: This section involves collection of data and quantification of
inputs and outputs of the system. [28]
• Life cycle impact assessment: In this section the data collected, and assumptions made are
translated into environmental impacts scores in characterised form, using a LCA tool [28]. In this
report, the LCA tool InfraLCA is used.

1.6
Current action taken in the Danish construction and building industry
As described in the previous sections, there is minimal action taken towards lowering Scope 3 emissions
in the Danish drainage industry. However, action is taken in other sectors.
1.6.1
The Danish climate partnership
In 2020, the climate partnership for the building and construction industry submitted a series of rec-
ommendations for reducing CO2 emissions in the entire industry [35]. Five general recommendations
have been made. Projects made in the drainage industry are a part of the construction industry. The
five general recommendation for CO2 reduction in the construction and building industry, made by the
climate partnership are : 1) Intelligent energy management and energy renovations, 2) From fossil fuels
to green heating, 3) CO2 accounts for buildings, 4) Fossil-free construction sites, and 5) Energy labelling
for all buildings [35]. The fourth recommendation, are the most relevant for CO2 reductions of project-
related activities in the drainage industry. Within the fourth recommendation, six main initiatives are
suggested by the climate partnership: [35]
1. Establishment of electricity and district heating on the construction site.
2. Use of green energy sources for heating and drying.
3. Fossil free building machines.
4. Demand for CO2 calculations of the construction site.
5. Less materials and less waste.
6. Better planning and process on the construction site.
These suggested initiatives are relevant for the Danish drainage industry. These initiative are important
to have in mind when trying to reduce the footprint of project-related activities in the Danish drainage
industry.
1.6.2
The national strategy for sustainable construction
The Danish government has developed a building regulation strategy that came into force on the 1. of
January 2023 [50]. This strategy is called "The national strategy for sustainable construction" [50]. It
follows up on the recommendations made by the climate partnership for the building and construction
industry. The strategy supports the national 70% reduction target [50].

Regarding reduction of CO2 emissions, it’s the first dimension, "Environmental quality", which is most

gases. During the construction phase there is often high energy consumption. If this is reduced, the
carbon footprint will also be reduced. Different initiatives have been proposed in the report [50] to
achieve the reduction. The main initiatives proposed in the report are a threshold limit values for CO2
emissions emitted when a new building is constructed, support the development of Life Cycle Assessment
tools, creating safe and healthy recycling and reuse systems, and promote fossil-free construction sites

of more than 1000 m2. The threshold limit value, valid until 2025, is 12 kg CO2eq/m2/year. There will
furthermore be a requirement of LCA calculations of all building sizes. From 2025 a threshold limit
value for buildings below 1000 m2 will be set. The requirements will help reduce the carbon footprint
of the building and construction industry. Furthermore, the industry will get more knowledge and ideas
on how to build sustainably. The implementation of the requirements for maximum CO2 emissions
are implemented gradually.

Baseline emissions in the Danish drainage industry
An estimation of the current carbon footprint of the Danish drainage industry is needed to set a baseline
which can be used for setting reduction goals. As mentioned earlier there has primarily been developed
focus on Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions in this industry. The goal in this part of the report is setting
a baseline for project-related Scope 3 emissions in the Danish drainage industry in 2022.
Different
approaches for setting a baseline can be taken. An example is Rambøll, who have calculated a baseline
for embodied carbon in buildings, using data from a large amount of LCA studies from all over Europe
[42]. Projects in the drainage industry vary a lot and it’s not common practice to conduct a LCA for
projects in the drainage industry. In this report the baseline will be based on LCA calculations of three
selected projects, the combination of which is assumed representative for the entire Danish drainage
industry. This approach will be further elaborated in the following sections.
2.1
Baseline data
In order to calculate a baseline of Scope 3 emissions in the Danish drainage industry, data was needed.
Three projects were selected and assumed representative for all construction projects in the Danish
drainage industry in 2022. This assumption is based on a guesstimate on which types of projects that
are most common in the industry. The projects are selected in collaboration with Søren Gabriel from
WSP and Lars Wilboe Pilman from FORS.
Lars Wilboe Pilman from FORS A/S, helped with the selection of the first two projects. Both projects
were carried out in 2022. Project 1 is a "Wastewater and stormwater sewage system project". Project
2 is a "Pumping station renovation project". Project 3, also from 2022, was selected in collaboration
with Søren Gabriel from WSP. This project is a "Climate adaption project". For each project a LCA
calculation was conducted. As the goal is to set a baseline of tons CO2eq, the only impact category of
focus is "Climate change" with the unit tons CO2eq.

Science Based Target mitigation pathway
3.1
Methodology
When defining a near term mitigation pathway, the necessary foundation must be established. Different
initiatives exist for setting budget-based target. In this report the Science based targets initiative (SBTi)
will be used. The "SBTi-Coporate manual" describes to set a SBT and what methods are available
[43]. Three of the main needs and considerations when setting a near term science based targets are the
following [43]:
• Choosing a base year: Here it is important that verifiable data exist for the selected base year.
Secondly it is important that the base year is representative of the typical GHG profile of, in this
study, the drainage industry. The base year should furthermore open for forward-looking reduction
ambitions. [43]
• Choosing a target year: The target year should cover a minimum of five years and a maximum
of 10 years. [43]
• Base year emissions: Measuring base year emissions is a very important step in the journey
of reducing CO2. Knowledge of current emissions makes it possible to set reduction targets and
develop a mitigation pathway that makes reduction of emissions possible. [43]
There are currently two main methodologies for SBT-setting. These are called the "Absolute contraction
approach (ACA)" and the "Sectorial Decarbonization Approach (SDA)" [43]. Both methodologies can
be used for Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions, whereas for Scope 3 emissions, thee "Absolute Contraction
approach" is, by SBTi, considered most suited [43].
The "Absolute Contraction approach" is a One-size-fits-all methodology that ensure that companies
setting SBT are delivering absolute emission reductions that align with the global decarbonization
pathways, either 1.5°C or WB2C [43]. WB2C stands for "well below 2°C". Using the ACA approach, all
companies reduce their absolute emissions at the same rate. As it is applicable to most sectors, 2/3 of
the goals approved by SBTi in 2020 used this approach [43]. The characteristics of the ACA, descrbed
in the "SBTi-Coporate manual" [43], can be seen in Table 9.

3.2
Defining the mitigation pathway
A mitigation pathway defines the level of emissions that over time will end up, where it is compatible
with the global carbon budget [42]. The pathway is also important to help keep track of reductions and
to make sure the emission levels stay within the science based target goals [42].
There exist different sharing principles for the global carbon budget.
As mentioned in Section 3.1
the most suitable approach for setting a mitigation pathway for Scope 3 emissions is the "Absolute
contraction approach".
The allocation principle this methodology is based on is "Grandfathering",
which means that the GHG budget is allocated and spread over time based on the current level of
emissions [42]. This currently means that high emitters have higher carbon budgets [42].
The baseline emissions in the Danish drainage industry were calculated to be 173,340 tons CO2eq. The
baseline year was selected to be 2022. The target year is set to be 2030 as this currently is a common
target year selected for setting near term science base targets.

3.2.1
Challenges in using the ACA approach
Selecting the ACA approach may entail some problems. By using this approach, all companies reduce
their emissions at the same rate. This can cause problems for the companies that already has reduced
their emissions. The usual approach when reducing emissions in a company is to start with the actions
that is easiest to implement. It might therefore be more difficult to reduce emissions using the ACA
approach, if the company already have reduced their carbon footprint. The companies that haven’t
done anything to reduce their emissions, get a greater leeway. For the Danish drainage industry, this
might not be an issue, as the timing is good. As mentioned earlier, the focus on reducing project-related
emissions in the Danish drainage industry is very limited. Using the ACA method might therefore be a
good approach for setting a science based target mitigation pathway for the Danish drainage industry,
as all companies within the industry will have the same starting point.
3.3
The road to reduced CO2 emissions
Ambitious reduction efforts need to be developed in order to stay below the 1.5°C limit. If the Danish
drainage industry keeps emitting what they are doing today, they will not comply with either of the
limits. If reduction efforts are taken, the reduction targets can potentially be met.

Action: Build wisely
Wise placement of pipes in the ground
Placing the pipes and wells wisely, where they are not exposed to heavy vehicles, could reduce the
carbon footprint. If a plastic well is placed where it is exposed to heavy traffic, it must be secured with
a concrete slab. A solution to this could be placing them were its most beneficial, with CO2 reduction
in mind. However, this could result in other challenges in relation to planting on the side of the road
etc., which must be taken into account.
Use of local pumping stations
As the construction site is located on a hilltop, it requires a lot of excavation work and some very deep
excavations, to get the right slope on the wastewater pipes. An alternative solution could be to install
2-3 local pumping stations instead. By doing this, the need for excavation work would be reduced.
However, it must be investigated whether the installation of the pumping station will result in a higher
carbon footprint. It costs almost no CO2 to pump wastewater [10], but building the facility and the
material use has an impact that must be taken into consideration.
Challenge government requirements
Requirements issued by the government can often become a challenge, when wanting to do things
differently. There are laws and service targets that must be complied with. It may be necessary to
challenge these requirements if we are to change the standard practice in the industry. An example
could be challenging requirements regarding the width of a road.

Build on the terrain surface

is developed [7]. This tool can be used to get an overview of the carbon footprint of different materials.
The tool is mainly made for use in the building industry but could also be used in the drainage industry
or the entire construction industry. If this tool gets further developed, and if the use of the tool gets
more common, it could be used to reduce the carbon footprint of projects.

transport, which results in the release of CO2 emissions. Instead of driving the soil away (25 km), it

making a small hill in a park or spreading the soil out across an area. The gain from doing this can
be seen in Figure 30. There is a reduction of about 90% of the carbon footprint [57]. This is a quite
beneficial action, and it might be one of the easiest to implement, depending on the location of the
project. If it is possible, it should be considered.

Reduce the bearing capacity of asphalt
The thickness of the asphalt is determined by the weight that it must be able to handle, called the
bearing capacity.
In Denmark it is divided into five classes, which indicates how heavy a load the
asphalt can handle [15]. It might be possible to reduce the thickness of the asphalt in smaller roads and
areas, where a lower bearing class is sufficient. However, this will probably be difficult to accomplish,
as its very difficult for an entrepreneur to say; "We will take the risk and reduce it". Here it would be
more beneficial to involve the client and get them to "take the risk".
Reuse dissembled components
By taking this action, the need for new products would be reduced. Today it is not common to reuse steel
and concrete elements, but it would reduce the carbon footprint. Reuse is often more beneficial than
recycling, as it is less energy demanding. If components become more standardised and demountable,
it will be easier to reuse the components 1:1. A study made by Rambøll [39] showed that 90% of the
materials from a building designed to be disassembled, can actually be reused. This action could not
only be beneficial in the building industry, but also in the construction and drainage industry. [39]
Using recycled or climate friendly materials
Using recycled materials, often result in a lower carbon footprint, as there is no need for extraction of raw
materials. Using products made of recycled plastic can sometimes be difficult, as there is restrictions
and laws that the products have to comply with. An innovative project made in collaboration with
several Danish companies, has investigated the benefits of using recycled plastic in the production of
plastic pipes for drainage. The study showed that using a pipe made of 70% recycled PVC plastic gives
a CO2 saving of 50% in the "Material production (A1-A3)" phase [1].
Using a more climate friendly type of cement could also be beneficial.
Modern cement types emit
significantly less CO2 during processing and the carbon footprint of cement can potentially be reduced
30%. [39]
Using electrical building machines
The CO2 footprint of "Stenlængegård" could also be reduced by changing from diesel buildings machines
to electrical building machines that uses green energy sources. Doing this could reduce the footprint
from machines hours in the "Construction installation process (A5)" by 2/3. This is based on a com-
parative LCA study, conducted by Icct [34], that compares vehicles types, see Appendix F.5 for a figure
showing the footprint of each vehicles type. Here it can be seen that an electric car running on renewable
energy has about 1/3 of the footprint of a diesel vehicle. Another possible reduction action is changing
all transportation vehicles to electrical vehicles.

Reduce paved area

example is roads. We are used to not having to look at patched asphalt. But if we repair rather than
remove the old asphalt and install new, it can extend the lifetime of the materials and thus provide a
lower carbon footprint over time.

This case study provides an example of the suggested approach for reducing the carbon
footprint of project in the Danish drainage industry. This case study could be relevant for all parts with
a connection to the Danish drainage industry. It could also be relevant for the entire Danish construction
industry, as the "Circle of actions" could potentially be a useful tool for CO2 reduction in this industry
as well. The outcome of the study could also be relevant for projects in countries with similar finances
and infrastructure as e.g. the nordic countries.
5.2
Scope definition
Before the "Circle of actions" can be used on the project, the functional unit must be defined. It must
enable mitigating actions to be implemented. The functional unit, for this project, is defined as:
Stormwater supply of a building plot on a total area of 14 ha., over a period of 50 years to a relevant
service level in 100 years.
5.2.1
System boundary
This LCA study includes processes from cradle to the use stage. Processes regarding EoL (end of life)
is excluded, see Section 2.2 for elaboration. The system boundary for this project is equivalent to what
was defined for the baseline, see Section 2.2. As for the previous LCA’s conducted in this report, the
only impact category of focus is "Climate change" with the unit tons CO2eq.

Discussion
6.1
Discussion of results
6.1.1
The baseline
The baseline of emissions from the Danish drainage industry in 2022 was calculated to be 173,340 tons
CO2eq. The three projects assumed representative for the baseline, had a climate intensity number
(tons CO2eq/DKK) which fluctuated between 2-13%. The baseline is therefore considered robust.
Even though the baseline is assessed to be robust, there are still uncertainties that must be considered.
The baseline is only based on three selected projects, which is a low data amount. There is a risk of the
selected projects giving misleading results, as projects vary in the drainage industry. The projects were
selected based on professional knowledge from WSP and are assumed to be representative of projects
typically done in the industry. A recommendation for further work, which could increase the robustness
of the baseline, is to increase the amount of data. It would furthermore increase the robustness if all
projects in the Danish drainage industry was divided into project types. Knowing the share of different
project types in the drainage industry, could give a more accurate baseline.
A second uncertainty regarding the baseline is, that the investments made in the drainage industry is
based on data from 2021 and not 2022. As the baseline is based on 2022 data, this might results in some
uncertainties. However, this uncertainty is not considered to be high, as it is expected that investments
made in 2021, are approximately the same as the investments made in 2022. Furthermore, it is assumed
that the intensity numbers would not have been different, had they been done for projects from 2021.
To further investigate the robustness of the calculated baseline, other sources were investigated. The
database EXIOBASE [46] was used to find an intensity number for the construction industry in Denmark.
This is not only activities related to the drainage industry, but the entire construction industry. Carbon
footprint of final consumption of 1 EUR in the sector "Construction in DK" is 2.71E-01 kg CO2eq [46].
This equals 3.62E-05 tons/DKK. As described in Section 1.3.2, EXIOBASE is associated with high
temporal and technological uncertainty. If EXIOBASE was used to calculate the baseline instead, the
baseline would have been about 1/3 higher. This gives confidence that we are in the correct order of
magnitude and it indicates that the calculated baseline gives a reliable status of the carbon footprint in
the Danish drainage industry in 2022.
6.1.2

for two different case studies. All calculations showed that the greatest environmental impact is seen in

knowledge for future work. It can be used to identify which hotspots, usually are seen in projects in
the drainage industry. The first case study conducted, called "Stenlængegård", was not only a drainage
project, but a land site development project. The following hotspots were identified for the baseline
projects and the two case studies.
• The baseline: For the three projects, used for the baseline, the main hotspots identified were

6.1.3
The "Circle of actions"
In this report, the "Circle of actions" were used on two different case studies. In both case studies, a
noteworthy reduction was seen. Especially for "Frederiksberg Hospital", where a reduction of 70% of
the carbon footprint was seen. For "Stenlængegård" a reduction of 42% was seen. With these results,
it is therefore assessed that the "Circle of actions" is an effective tool for CO2 reduction.
For all LCA’s conducted in this report, the only impact category of focus was "Climate change" with the
unit tons CO2eq. Whether CO2 is a good proxy for the remaining impact categories, usually considered
in a LCA study, will be discussed in a following section.
6.2
Qualitative discussion of uncertainties
6.2.1
General uncertainties regarding LCA
In general, there are uncertainties when conducting a LCA. LCA is one of the most widely used tools
for quantification of environmental impacts. One of the main uncertainties is the quality and variability
of the data used. This will determine the reliability and robustness of the results. The scope, system
boundary, data sources available and validation processes determine the data availability and quality.
The results of the LCA depend on these choices. [2]
When it comes to conducting a LCA in the drainage industry, there are several other uncertainties that
must considered.
68 of 126
==PAGE:84==
6.2
Qualitative discussion of uncertainties
The LCA tool InfraLCA, makes assumptions regarding the LCA phase "Replacement (B4)". When a
material is replaced in e.g. 20 years, InfraLCA sums the impact from "Material production (A1-A4)",
"Transport (A4)", and "Construction installation process (A5)" for that material, which is then equal
to the impact of "Replacement (B4)".
It is not taken into consideration that the carbon footprint
of "Replacement (B4)", hopefully will be reduced in the future.
It is difficult to predict what will
happen in the future, but it is expected that materials and energy with a lower carbon footprint will be
available. Therefore, it is important to have in mind, that there might be some uncertainties related to
"Replacement (B4)" and that the footprint from this phase might decrease when replacement is actually
needed. It is therefore recommended that "Replacement (B4)" is qualified with this uncertainty in mind.
A second aspect that is important to consider is the lifetime of materials. Today, it is considered to be a
beneficial thing if the materials you invest in, have a long lifetime. With carbon footprint in mind, it is
not always the materials with the longest lifetime that is the best choice, in particular if their production
is accompanied by a large carbon footprint. An example of this, is selecting concrete tiles over brick
tiles. Brick tiles have an average lifetime in excess of 100 years [51] and concrete tiles have a lifetime
of about 25 years [57]. Even though the brick tile is selected, because it has a much longer lifetime, it
also has a much higher footprint from "Material production (A1-A3)" (see Section 4.5). The emissions
from the production of brick tiles are released at year 0 (The construction year), no matter if it last
10 years or 100 years. If the brick tiles are paved in a place where they are exposed to heavy loads,
they probably won’t last 100 years. It may also be, that before the 100 years have passed, the area is
changed, and new pavement is installed. It is very important that, in the beginning of a project, it’s
considered if a long lifetime is necessary. Looking at Figure 44, the carbon footprint of "Stenlængegård"
distributed over the assumed lifetime (50 years), can be seen (Excluding the impact from houses and
their operation for 50 years).

A third aspect that might cause uncertainties is the Bill Of Quantity (BOQ). Most of the LCA studies
conducted in this report were based on data from the initial BOQ. The BOQ shows how many materials,
construction work etc. is needed in a project. However, the initial BOQ might be very different from
how project turns out.
The carbon footprint calculated using the initial BOQ, might be either an
overestimation or underestimation from the actual footprint of the project. Another uncertainty may
be, that waste of materials on the construction site, is not taken into account. It might be cheaper to
order more materials than needed in the beginning of a project, rather than ordering materials during
the construction process, if the need arises. It might be that the usual thought during a project process
is that "it is better to be on the safe side".
It could also be that building machines are idling or
they move around the construction site, more than predicted. There are many factors that must be
considered, which could potentially influence the outcome of the LCA. It is especially important to

6.2.2
Using LCA at different project stages
Timing is important when using LCA for reduction of CO2 in projects. The first case study analysis
represent a LCA conducted after the project was been defined ("Stenlængegård") and the second case
study analysis represents a LCA conducted before the project is started ("Frederiksberg Hospital"). A
different purpose and different uncertainties are connected to each type of study.
Conducting a LCA before a project begins can be used to calculate the carbon footprint of different
scenarios before a project is started. A recommendation for this purpose is to define a new project phase,
called the "Pre-project phase". This phase would relate to "Build less" and "Build for the future" in the
"Circle of actions". Doing it before could be helpful to define different scenarios and compare them, to
investigate which solution has the lowest carbon footprint, before the project is defined. When a LCA
is carried out in the early project phase, the data is based on estimates and assumptions. This causes
uncertainty, but it can be used to get some general ideas about which solutions and scenarios has the
lowest footprint. A LCA conducted in the early project phase has a lower degree of detail and thereby
a higher degree of uncertainty that must be taken into account.
Conducting a LCA after a project is defined has a different purpose. This is more about documenting
the carbon footprint of a project. It can also be used to identify hotspots that can provide knowledge
about where it is important to focus on reducing, in upcoming projects. When a LCA is carried out in
this phase, the data will often be based on the BOQ, which means conducting a LCA in this project
phase is associated with a higher degree of detail. This causes less uncertainty. But if the material
and energy use, stated in the initial BOQ, changes during the project process, it may be necessary to
conduct a LCA during several of the project phases to be aware of the current carbon footprint of the
project.
The earlier within the life cycle of a project, LCA calculations are conducted, the higher is the ability
to influence the reduction of CO2. The goal for the drainage industry, and potentially also the entire
construction industry, is that the “Circle of actions”, is used as early as possible, preferably in the
suggested "Pre-project phase". This can help define different scenarios, to which an investigation of
which scenario results in the lowest carbon footprint, can be made.
An existing approach used to estimate uncertainties when doing projects, is looking at economic un-
certainty. When planning a project, different uncertainties are defined at the different project levels.
This could potentially be useful to indicate where uncertainties are highest, when conducting a LCA
at different levels of a project. Figure 45, displays correction factors for construction price estimates,
based on project levels, commonly used in the industry [33].

6.2.4
Varying calculation approaches
As LCA calculations are currently not common in the Danish drainage industry, a common calculation
approach for carbon footprint doesn’t exist. This means, that when LCA calculations are conducted,
it is often executed in several different ways. This is a high uncertainty, as it makes it very difficult to
compare the footprint of different projects.
75 of 126
==PAGE:91==
6.3
CO2 as a Proxy
In order to make LCA calculations more robust in the Danish drainage industry and comparable, it
is important to make the calculation approach uniform and make sure that the system boundary is
equal as well. It is very important when comparing footprints of different projects, that the same LCA
phases are included in the calculation. Currently a lot is at stake. Every part of the industry might
conduct their LCA calculations differently. An example is the calculation of the carbon footprint of
"Køge Nord". Here, not all LCA phases for all materials and processes were included in the calculation.
This can cause problems if you want to compare this project’s footprint with a project where all LCA
phases for all materials and processes are included. It is therefore crucial to be aware of this issue, and
even more important that a common approach is defined for LCA calculations in the Danish drainage
industry.

6.4
Recommendation for a national strategy in the Danish drainage industry
From the 1st of January 2023 requirements for LCA calculation for new buildings came into force, see
Section 1.6.2 for elaboration. Furthermore, a threshold limit value corresponding to 12 kg CO2/ m2/year
was set for buildings of more than 1000 m2. At the end of 2023, the plan is to define a new threshold
limit for 2025 which will then apply to all building sizes. The phasing in of requirements for maximum
CO2 emissions is therefore happening gradually. To encourage further reduction, a low emission class
has been defined, which the companies and developers voluntarily can try to meet. [50]
The same approach could be used in the Danish drainage industry. In this report a baseline for the
Danish drainage industry was calculated based on the intensity unit tons CO2/DKK. When setting
national goals for the Danish drainage industry this intensity unit must be changed to allow for opti-
misation and reduction. In the national strategy for the building industry, they use the intensity unit
CO2/m2/yr. A similar intensity unit must be defined for the Danish drainage industry. In the building

industry, it is expected that there will be an additional cost when building low carbon [50]. CO2/DKK

7
Conclusions, Limitations and Recommendations
7.1
Conclusions
The following points can be concluded from the baseline study.
• An average climate change intensity number, for the three selected projects used for the baseline,
the combination of which is assumed representative for the entire Danish drainage industry, was
calculated to 2.61E-05 tons CO2/DKK.
• Total tons project-related Scope 3 emissions from the Danish drainage industry and the baseline
of 2022, was calculated to 173,340 tons CO2eq. EXIOBASE data confirmed that we are in the
right order of magnitude.
• As the three projects had an intensity number (tons CO2eq/DKK) which fluctuated between
2-13%, the average intensity number, and therefore also the baseline, is considered robust.
• If the Danish drainage industry decides to set a Science based target and comply with the 1.5°C
limit, emissions must be reduced with 42% to 100,537 tons CO2eq in 2030.
• The hypothesis that project-related emissions in the Danish drainage industry, are greater than
Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions has been confirmed. Project-related Scope 3 emissions (the baseline)
are 720% greater than Scope 1 emissions and 54% greater than Scope 2 emissions cf. the data
seen in Figure 2.
• The main hotspots identified for the three baseline projects, were plastic pipes and wells, concrete
pipes and wells, aluminium, and brick tiles.
It is "Material production (A1-A3)" that is the
primary contributor to the carbon footprints.
The following points can be concluded from the two case studies.
• The analysis of the case study "Stenlængegård", using the "Circle of actions", showed many
opportunities for reducing project-related CO2 emissions. Implementing some of the suggested
actions showed a possible reduction of 42%.
• The analysis of the case study "Frederiksberg Hospital", using the "Circle of Actions", showed a
total possible reduction of 70%.
• For both case studies, actions which especially showed reduction opportunities were identified.
Examples are detention of stormwater on terrain (minimises the need for pipes and wells), minimise
excavation work, minimise delivery of soil and gravel, less use of CO2 heavy materials, local
incorporation of excavated soil, and minimise installation of materials below existing asphalt.
• It can be concluded that the "Circle of actions" is a useful and concrete tool, when trying to
reduce the carbon footprint of a projects in the Danish drainage industry.

7.2
Limitations
The following points can be concluded from the discussion.
• Different uncertainties were discussed in this study. Phases where potential uncertainties were
especially identified were the "Construction installation process (A5)" and "Replacement (B4)"
phase.
• The investigation of whether "Climate change" is a good proxy for the remaining impact categories
showed that it is a good proxy for 11 out of 15 impact categories, using the R2 approach. The
analysis furthermore showed that it is important to pay special attention to the impact categories
"Human toxicity, cancer", "Land use", and "Water consumption" if there is an increased use of
PVDC, Aluminium, steel, cast iron, concrete, and round gravel.
7.2
Limitations
The main limitations of this study are the following.
• The approach for the LCA calculations made in this report causes some limitations, as the LCA’s
are based on the given data, as e.g. the "Bill of Quantity", but also assumptions and estimates.
It is thus not assessed a being a major limitation, as all LCA’s in this study are conducted using
the same approach.
• Using tons CO2/DKK as climate change intensity unit, cannot be used for setting a national
strategy in the Danish drainage industry, as it potentially limits the possibilities for optimisation
and reduction of CO2, as the implementation of sustainable solutions can potentially result in
additional costs.
7.3
Recommendations
The following recommendations for further work are given to the study commissioner.
• It is recommended that a new baseline is calculated for the Danish drainage industry. It should
be recalculated using a higher amount of data and should be calculated with knowledge on the
magnitude of the different project types in the Danish drainage industry. This will increase the
robustness of the baseline.
• For the Danish drainage industry, it is recommended to set threshold limit values, using the same
approach as they have done it in the Danish building industry. A new climate change intensity unit
must be defined, which should cover the main functions in the industry and allow for reduction.
• It is recommended that a common calculation approach of CO2 emissions in the Danish drainage
industry is defined. The recommended approach is including the LCA phases A1, A2, A3, A4, A5,
and B4, as these are the phases currently available for calculaton in InfraLCA. It is furthermore
recommended that B4 is refined to take future materials and energy sources into account. When
possible and enough data is available, it is also recommended that the phases C1-C4 (end of life)
is included.
• It is recommended that if an LCA is conducted for a project in the Danish drainage industry, it

Appendix: InfraLCA
In this report, the LCA tool InfraLCA (Version 2.11) developed by Vejdirektoratet and Banedanmark,
was used for the LCA calculations [57]. InfraLCA is a tool made to make assessments of the environ-
mental impacts of infrastructure and construction projects. The tool is an Excel file in which quantities
are entered, under the relevant items and processes. The tool then calculates the environmental impacts
of the "Material production (A1-A3)", "Transport (A4)", "Construction installation process (A5)" and
"Replacement (B4)" phase [57].
The emission factors behind the tool are based on an average of product specific EPDs for products and
materials used in the Danish market [57]. A basis emission factor is usually based on EPDs from three
sources [57]. The model is typically updated 1-2 times a year, to improve the quality of the data [57].
Currently there isn’t EPDs for all product groups. Other data sources is therefore used to calculate the
average emission factors.
InfraLCA uses the following data sources [57]:
• Representative product-specific EPDs based on EN15804*.
• Representative industry EPDs based on EN15804*.
• Representative data from recognised databases, such as Ecoinvent or GaBi.
*The EN 15804 is the EPD standard for the sustainability of construction works and services. This stan-
dard harmonises the structure for EPDs in the construction sector, making the information transparent
and comparable [20].
The basis transport distances represent average values for transport work in infrastructure projects.
Assessments of the expected transport distances of materials and products have been made. The emission
factors for construction machines, are currently based on average values [57].
The life cycle phases currently within the system boundary, cf. EN15804, in InfraLCA are the following
[57]:
• A1-A3: Emissions from production of materials and products
• A4: Emissions from transport using truck, dumpster, ship and train are calculated as tkm.
• A5: Emissions from energy use on the construction site.
This includes electricity, diesel and
gasoline use.
• B4: Replacement of products and materials of the material.
InfraLCA includes reporting on several different environmental parameters[57]. This report only includes
results on the parameter "Climate change" with the unit kg CO2eq.

Overall assumptions
The following assumptions are applicable to all LCA calculations. It does not apply that all assumptions
are relevant for all LCA’s conducted in this report.
• The lifetime is assumed to be 50 years, as this is a common timeframe used in the construction
industry. [10]
• Processes regarding EoL (End of Life) is excluded. This is because its not current practice to
recycle materials in the industry and there currently isn’t enough data to calculate this [10].
• Which LCA stages that, according to EN15804, are included in each LCA calculation, is described
for each of the projects’ system boundary.
• All items with a total price that is less than 10.000 DKK is not included in the calculations, unless
it has a clearly defined unit, which can easily be summed within one of the categories [10], seen
in Table 27.
• For all work done by "A man and a machine", it is assumed that it costs 1200 DKK/hour. From
the total sum of the relevant posts, the total hourly consumption is calculated. Based on hourly
consumption, the diesel consumption is calculated, with an assumption of 5 litres of Diesel/hour
[10]. Examples of this type of work is documentation, removal, clearing, operation and planting.
In the report this will be referred to as "Machine hours".
• All pipe lengths and number of wells, both plastic and concrete, have been adjusted according

Abstract submitted for two conferences
The WSP-DK approach to CO2 reduction in the drainage sector
The world is in constant development and within the last 10 years a lot has happened especially con-
cerning climate change and the environment. When the climate changes, there is a need for climate
adaption. It costs materials and energy, which costs carbon emissions. This contributes even more to
climate change and there will in the future be an even bigger need for climate adaption. Therefore,
it is important to reduce the CO2 footprint of projects in the industry as much as possible to avoid
being caught in this vicious circle. The road to become more climate friendly in the industry might
be challenging, but not impossible. Looking at the Danish drainage industry (Including rainwater and
125 of 126
==PAGE:141==
wastewater handling), the focus is primarily on reducing direct emissions controlled by the company
and indirect emissions which arises from the company’s energy consumption. There is a limited focus
on emissions from activities not owned or controlled by the company, as for example project related ac-
tivities (embodied emissions). Even though there is limited data on embodied emissions in the drainage
industry, there is still incentive to believe that they are high. It is known that the building industry gen-
erates 37% of the annual global carbon emissions where 10% is embodied carbon. In order to work with
CO2eq reduction of emissions in projects in the drainage industry, a baseline of current CO2 emissions
is needed. As a part of a master thesis, a baseline for project related emissions in the Danish drainage
industry in 2022 was estimated. This was done based on three selected drainage projects, which were
assumed representative for the entire industry. For the three projects, an average intensity number
with the unit tons CO2eq/DKK was calculated. The total investments made in the drainage industry
in 2021, estimated by DANVA, was then used to calculate the total carbon footprint of the Danish
drainage industry in 2022. Furthermore, a “Near term Science Based Target mitigation pathway” was
defined, to investigate how much the industry should reduce to be Paris aligned and within the carbon
budget, which determines the amount of GHG that can be emitted until the limit of 1.5°C is reached.
To stay below the 1.5°C limit, methodologies for CO2 reduction in the industry is needed. A tool called
“The circle of actions for CO2 reduction” is developed by Søren Gabriel from WSP Denmark. This tool
is a systematic approach for reducing CO2 throughout an entire project process. It links CO2 reduction
to the various project phases. The tool can be used during the strategy phase, planning phase, design
phase, construction phase and operation phase. This tool helps developing CO2 optimized projects
and it should be used by the entire industry. Examples are “Build only what is needed now”, “Postpone
projects”, “Think and source locally”, “Build in terrain”, “Use CO2 friendly materials and energy sources”.
This change of mindset and use of the tool, when doing projects, should become an obligatory part of
all project processes.
