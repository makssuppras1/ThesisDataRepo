Individual variation in round goby behavior and relations to 
catchability in fishing gear 
Carl Baden 
DTU Aqua: National Institute of Aquatic Resources, Technical University of Denmark, 2800 
Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark 
 
Abstract 
Variation in individual behavior traits are ecologically and evolutionary important as they 
may affect population dynamics and community stability, but selective fishery may reduce 
the variation in individual behavior traits. Maintaining variation in behaviors traits may thus 
be crucial for maintaining ecological resilience against invasive species and other threats, but 
could management deliberately reduce the trait variation among an invasive species through 
selective fishery? In the present study we used the invasive round goby (Neogobius 
melanostomus) from an established population in controlled experiments to examine the 
presence of behavior traits and its relations to catchability in passive fishing gears (fyke net). 
Five different measures of behavior traits (active-passive-1, active-passive-2, detection time, 
shelter, factor and avoidance-exploration) were measured in an experimental dispersal setup 
consisting of five consecutive tanks connected by tubes. We show that all measures of 
behavior traits, except for active-passive-2, were found to be significantly repeatable and we 
suggest that active-passive and avoidance-exploration BTs may be related to vulnerability to 
passive fishing gear (fyke-net). Furthermore, body mass was found to correlate significantly 
with all measures of behavior traits.

Introduction 
The global demand for fish is high. Fish provide the primary consistent source of 
protein to more than 35% of the world’s population and more than 50% in the least developed 
countries (FAO, 2016). Contrary to food production on land, most of the protein derived from 
the ocean is harvested from wild populations of fish (FAO, 2018). Each year, about 80 
million metric tons of fish are reportedly caught (FAO, 2018). Due to the high fishing 
pressure, fishing mortality can be several-fold higher than natural mortality among exploited 
fish stocks (Mertz & Myers, 1998). Additionally, even though the global maximum 
sustainable yield is estimated to be 98 million metric tons (Costella, 2016), approximately 30 
to 35% of fish populations are fished unsustainably, with an additional 60% fished at 
maximum capacity (FAO, 2016). Unsustainable fisheries may directly affect the status of fish 
populations, but the indirect implications of fisheries on marine ecosystems are much broader 
(Link, 2018). 
Fisheries often use selective gear where fish with certain traits are more likely to be 
caught (Diaz et al., 2017). If the traits under selection by fisheries are heritable, then 
evolutionary change in exploited fish populations may occur, a phenomenon known as 
fisheries-induced evolution (FIE). FIE may occur in many exploited fish stocks, particularly 
among traits related to life histories and reproduction (Engberg et al., 2012; Heino et al., 
2015). If fishing mortality among sexually immature fishes are high, fishing may select for 
fish that mature earlier and at a smaller size (Jørgensen et al., 2009). However, FIE may 
occur in many ways due to a great variety of fishing-induced selection and correlation of 
traits. For example, selective pressure from angling activities may cause declines in weight 
and length of Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar), if angling season coincides with the return of the 
largest sea-age class, as early returning fish are significantly larger than late returning fish 
(Sauroman 2010).

 Growth and size are often correlated (fast growing individuals are often bigger), but 
getting big may also be achieved through a long and slow-growing life with lower risk of 
predation (Biro et al., 2008). Fast growing individuals often have high resource acquisition 
(the processes involved in foraging, ingestion and digestion), which provides them with 
resources they can allocate to growth, but it may also increase the risk of predation (Biro et 
al., 2008; Dmitriew, 2011). Additionally, physiological traits such as growth rate and 
metabolism are often correlated with behavioral traits (BTs) (Biro et al., 2008; Metcalfe et al., 
2016; Behrens et al., 2020). BTs are usually consistent over time (i.e., repeatable) (Cote & 
Clobert, 2007; Bell et al., 2009; Cote et al., 2010) and in many cases with a heritability 
comparable to what has been observed for morphological and life history traits (Dingemanse 
et al., 2003; Korsten et al., 2013; Dochtermann et al., 2019). Most empirical studies of fish 
BTs are focused on axes of shyness–boldness, exploration–avoidance, activity, aggression 
and sociability (Conrad et al., 2011). Shyness–boldness characterizes how an individual 
reacts to danger, such as the presence of a predator (Réale et al., 2007), but in other studies, it 
has been used to describe an individual’s reaction to novel food items and environments 
(Fraser et al., 2001; Frost et al., 2007). Exploration–avoidance describes an individual’s 
disposition to explore novel environments, food items or objects, but generally differs from 
shyness–boldness by being assessed in the absence of a predator (Réale et al., 2007). The 
level of activity of an individual is considered a BT of its own, if it is observed in known 
environments in absence of dangers (Réale et al 2007), though it is often observed in 
combination with other BTs like shyness–boldness and exploration–avoidance (Conrad et al., 
2011). Aggressive behavior in fish is commonly observed in context of territory defense or 
competition for food, but, as for activity, aggression is often studied in combination with 
other BTs (Conrad et al., 2011). A behavioral syndrome is a cluster of correlated BTs that 
occurs consistently in individuals across variable circumstances (Dingemanse & Wolf, 2009; 
==PAGE:4==
4 
 
Sih et al., 2004; Wolf et al., 2009). Boldness, aggression and exploration are three commonly 
positively correlated BTs that together form a behavior syndrome (Sih et al., 2004). 
  
Effects of fishing 
BTs may be an important factor determining which fish are caught (Løkkeborg et al., 
2014) and hence are under strong human-induced selection (Uusi-Heikkilä et al., 2008). 
Furthermore, different methods of fishing may select for different BTs (Diaz et al., 2017). 
Bold and explorative fish appear to have a higher chance of escaping active fishing gear like 
trawl than non-explorative and shy fish (Diaz Pauli et al., 2015), though shy fishes might be 
more easily scared by approaching fishing vessels and active gears (Heino & Godø, 2002). 
Bold, explorative and active fish may also be more vulnerable to passive gears like gill nets 
and fyke nets, as active fish may encounter the trap more frequently and explorative fish may 
be more likely to enter it (Alós et al., 2012; Diaz Pauli et al., 2015; Moland et al., 2019). 
Individual variation in behavior may thus be an essential tool for predicting how a species 
will respond to human-induced selection (Sih et al., 2011). 
 
Behavioral traits at the front of invasions 
 
Globalization has led to intentional and unintentional transfers of organisms into new 
ecosystems, where some may become established beyond their native range (Keller et al 
2011). Some of these species may spread further, where some may have negative impacts on 
the ecosystem and are termed invasive (Kolar et al., 2001) and newly invaded areas of an 
invasive species may be considered as an invasion front (Thorlacius et al., 2018). BTs are 
commonly accepted to affect the likelihood of an invader to successfully establish in a new 
areas, and individuals of invasive species at the invasion front often have BTs that differ from 
==PAGE:5==
5 
 
individuals from established populations (Duckworth & Badyaev, 2007; Groen et al., 2012). 
Furthermore, BTs like aggression may co-vary with other BTs at the invasion front, such as 
explorativeness and boldness, and may together form a behavior syndrome that promotes 
invasion (Chucholl et al., 2008; Pintor et al., 2008).  For example, the highly invasive round 
goby (Neogobius melanostomus) may be more asocial, bold, and aggressive at the invasion 
front than in older source populations (Brownscombe et al., 2012; Thorlacius et al., 2018). 
Invasive crayfish at the invasion front may be more aggressive, active foragers and bolder to 
forage under predation than native populations (Pintor et al., 2018). This could potentially be 
useful knowledge for management of this invasive fish, as individuals at invasion fronts that 
are explorative, active and bold may be vulnerable to passive fishing gear (Alós, Palmer & 
Arlinghaus, 2012; Diaz Pauli et al., 2015; Moland et al., 2019). 
The invasive round goby 
The round goby is a demersal fish that can reach 20-25 cm in total length (Azour et 
al., 2015), native to the Ponto-Caspian region in Europe. It has through unintentional human 
transfer in the ballast water of shipping vessels been introduced to large parts of Europe, 
including the Baltic Sea, and to the Great Lakes in North America (Corkum et al., 2004). The 
round goby is considered to be a highly invasive species with negative impacts on ecosystems 
in both regions (Ojaveer et al., 2014, 2015). For example, it may compete with indigenous 
species for space and food (Balshine et al., 2005; Karlson et al., 2007) and eat their eggs and 
juveniles (French and Jude, 2001; Steinhart et al., 2004), which concerns fishermen as 
commercial fish species may be negatively affected by high densities of round gobies 
(fishingbaltic.eu, 2019). In established areas with high densities, round gobies may be 
smaller, slower growing and in poorer condition than in newly established areas (Azour et al., 
2015). Additionally, sex distributions are around 1:1 in these areas and populations consist of 
a wide range of ages, whereas the sex distribution tends to be male-biased and particularly 
==PAGE:6==
6 
 
consisting of intermediate ages closer to the invasion front (Skora & Rzeznik, 2001; 
Gutowsky et al., 2011; Azour et al., 2015). 
 
Study objectives  
The objectives of this study were to investigate if the invasive round goby has 
consistent individual behavioral traits and if this affects their likelihood of being caught in 
passive fishing gear (fyke net). For this purpose, we built an underwater dispersal setup 
(maze) equipped with electronic passive integrated transponder (PIT) antennae that could 
detect the movement of PIT-tagged fish over two separate trials with the same individuals. 
Fish personality traits were examined by testing for repeatability of five BTs measured in the 
two trials (Reale et al., 2007). To identify patterns in movement and behavior of round goby 
in the maze, we constructed five BTs for each individual, recorded in each of the two trials: 
active-passive-1, active-passive-2, detection time, shelter, factor and avoidance-exploration.  
 We predicted that the BTs would be repeatable over time (Reale et al. 2007. 
Furthermore, we examined whether a behavior syndrome was present by correlating all five 
BTs against one another (Sih et al., 2004). To test whether the likelihood of being caught in 
passive fishing gear was related to the BTs, we transferred the fish to a tank with a trap 
(miniature fyke net) after each trial in the maze, and recorded if or when the fish entered the 
fyke net. We predicted that more explorative and active fish would have a greater probability 
of being caught (Alós, Palmer & Arlinghaus, 2012; Diaz Pauli et al., 2015; Wiech et al., 
2015; Moland et al., 2019).

Materials and methods 
 
Study organisms and tagging procedure 
Round gobies used in this experiment were caught in the brackish estuary Karrebæk 
Fjord in Denmark (55°11'41.1"N 11°40'23.8"E), where they have been observed since 2011 
and hence considered to be a relatively established population (Azour et al., 2015; Carl, 
Azour & Møller, 2019). 100 fish were captured in spring 2019 by a local fishers using 
commercial fyke nets. The sex of individual fish was determined on location by examination 
of the urogenital papilla (Marsden et al., 1996) and only males (~200 in total) were collected 
to exclude any potential sex-mediated differences (Behrens et al., 2020). The fish were 
transported to the National Institute of Aquatic Resources (DTU Aqua) in Lyngby, Denmark, 
and kept in three 400 L holding tanks receiving recirculated, filtered, UV-treated and aerated 
water at 10 ± 0.5°C and a salinity of 10 ± 2 PSU. The light:dark cycle was set to 12:12 h, 
with lights turning gradually on from 7:00 to 7:30 and off from 18:30 to 19:00. 
58 round gobies with a body mass of 100.3 ± 24.0 g (mean ± SD) and total length of 
17.2 ± 1.4 cm (mean ± SD) were randomly selected on 20 March 2020 and transferred to a 
separate round tank (2 × 2 × 0.8 m) where they were fasted for 2-3 days in preparation for 
tagging. Tagging was done to allow individual recognition throughout the study, and fish 
were fasted to reduce any impact of digestive processes on fish recovery after implantation of 
tags (Norin & Clark, 2017; Tirsgaard et al., 2015). For tagging, fish were placed in a 3-L 
anesthetic bath (MS-222, 0.133 g L–1) for 3-4 min, after which passive integrated transponder 
(PIT) tags (12 × 2.1 mm, 0.1 g; Oregon RFID) were implanted into the fish’s abdominal 
cavity with disposable 12.2-gauge syringes. After implementation of tags, fish were 
monitored in temporary holdings tanks to confirm recovery.

Experimental dispersal (maze) setup 
Fish were assessed for the five BTs; active-passive-1, active-passive-2, detection 
time, shelter, factor and avoidance-exploration, in an experimental maze with five 570(w) × 
770(l) × 405(h) mm boxes connected by four pipes that were 900 mm long and 100 mm in 
diameter (Figure 1). Two PIT antennas were mounted on each of the four pipes (Figure 1). 
PIT antennas detected signals (PIT tags in the fish) at a frequency of 50 Hz and range of ~10 
cm horizontally from each antenna in either direction along the pipe. Ten artificial plants 
were made by attaching strips of green plastic (tarp) to a sinking ring of grey PVC pipe and 
provided as shelter. To provide heterogeneity in the maze and avoid each box looking the 
same, no plants were placed in the first box, one was placed in the second box, two in the 
third box and four in the fifth box (Figure 1). The maze had a steady flow-through supply of 
system water (same conditions as in the holding tanks), with entry in the first box and exit 
from the last box (Figure 1).  
To identify patterns in movement and behavior of round goby in the maze, we 
constructed five BTs for each individual, recorded in each of the two trials: (1) ‘Active-
passive-1’ is a basic measure of activity and is defined as the total number of movements 
between PIT antennas; (2) ‘Active-passive-2’ is a combined measure of activity and 
exploration and is based on the number of movements between antennas (NMBA) and the 
number of movements between pipes (NMBP), defined as NMBA × (NMBP / NMBA); (3) 
‘Detection time’ is a measure of how much time the fish spent inside a pipe  and is defined as 
the total detection time during a trial in milliseconds; (4) ‘Shelter-factor’ is a measure of how 
fast the fish moved past the PIT antenna when detected and is calculated by dividing 
detection time with number of detections; and (5) ‘Avoidance-exploration’ is a measure of 
how much of the maze the fish explored and is defined as the highest antenna number (1-8)  
the fish were detected at.

Experimental fyke net setup 
The likelihood of being caught in passive fishing gear (miniature fyke net) was tested 
in a round tank (2 × 2 × 0.8 m) with a fyke net (70 x 40 x 28 cm) with two openings in the 
middle (Figure 2). The fyke net was baited with food pellets in a cotton bag. Two artificial 
plants were placed on each side of the fyke net in order to avoid fish using the fyke net as 
their only option for shelter.  
 
Figure 2. Experimental fyke net setup. Fyke net with two entries (white rope) and a cotton bag with 
food pellets (black). Two green artificial plants were positioned on each side of the fyke net.  
 
Experimental protocol 
The fish were kept in two holding tanks throughout the experimental period and fed 
regularly until two days before starting a trial (i.e. before being introduced to the maze and 
subsequently the fyke net tank). The first trial was started on 6 April 2020, 14 days after the 
tagging procedure. Seven fish were caught at random from the first holding tank and were 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
then considered as group one. A total of nine groups of seven fish went through two 
behavioral trials in the maze and two capture trials in the fyke net tank. The fish’s PIT-IDs 
were registered by a hand-held PIT scanner and noted, and each group of seven fish were 
gently moved to the maze, where they remained for 24 h (11:00 to 11:00). After 24 h in the 
maze, the seven fish were gently caught by hand-netting and moved directly to the fyke net 
tank, where they were left until 16:00 to assess if and who would get caught. The fyke net 
was checked for captures once an hour from 11:00-16:00 and IDs of any caught fish were 
registered manually with a handheld PIT scanner mounted with an extension antenna that 
could reach into the fyke net. After the last check at 16:00, fish were caught and moved to a 
second holding tank. After each of the nine groups had been through their first trial (24 April 
2020) a second trial was started on 27 April 2020 following the same procedures and order of 
groups.  
 
Data analysis 
   
Statistical analyses were performed with the software R v. 4.0.2 (R Core Team, 2020). 
The presence and movement of round gobies within the maze were determined from 
detections at the eight PIT antennas through time. Due to zero-inflation in the data (e.g. 
several fish did not leave the first box in the maze and did not get registered on any 
antennas), we analyzed  two separate sets of data: data 1 included all data while data 2 
excluded zero-values.  
To estimate the individual consistency of each BT across the two trials, repeatability 
scores of each BT were estimated with the R package rptR (Stoffel, M. A., Nakagawa, S. and 
Schielzeth, H. (2017), rptR: repeatability estimation and variance decomposition by 
generalized linear mixed-effects models. Methods Ecol Evol, 8: 1639-1644. 
doi:10.1111/2041-210X.12797). The rpt function generates adjusted repeatability (Radj) 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
estimations from generalized linear mixed-effects models fitted by restricted maximum 
likelihood (REML). In previous studies (Lallias et al., 2017), BTs have been correlated with 
body mass and body mass was thus included as a covariate in our analyses to partition out its 
potential effect. Furthermore, trial number was also included as a second covariate in our 
analyses to account for its effect on behavior. The model formulation for Radj (model 1) in R 
syntax is as follows:  
BT ~ body mass + trial number + (1|fish-ID). 
 Confidence intervals and standard errors were estimated from 1000 parametric 
bootstraps. Adjusted repeatability scores were generated with the entire dataset (data 1) and 
without zero-values (data 2). 
The likelihood of being caught in the fyke net was examined by generalized linear 
mixed-effects model for each BT measure, body mass and specific growth rate (SGR), using 
the R package lme4 (Douglas Bates, Martin Maechler, Ben Bolker, Steve Walker (2015). 
Fitting Linear Mixed-Effects Models Using lme4. Journal of Statistical Software, 67(1), 1-
48.doi:10.18637/jss.v067.i01). We used the glmer function to fit a generalized linear mixed-
effects model for each parameter adjusted for body mass and trial number. The model 
formulation for our GLMM (model 2) in R syntax is as follows: 
 BT ~ body mass + trial number + (1|fish-ID).  
Estimates were generated both with data 1 and data 2. Logit-estimates (L)were transformed to 
odds as odds = exp(L) for an easier interpretation of the results. Odds can be interpreted as 
how much 1-unit in BT measure increases the odds of being caught.  
 
Correlation coefficients were generated through pairwise Pearson correlation analyses 
among all BTs, body mass and specific growth rate (SGR) using all BT values (data 1), but 
were restricted to fish where standard growth rate (SGR) was available, as not all fish were 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
alive when the second measure of weight were made. Specific growth rate (SGR; % day–1) 
for all fish were calculated from two measurements of body mass as (ln(start weight) − ln(end 
weight)) / days × 100. The first measurement (start) of body mass were recorded at the end of 
the experiment (15 May 2020) and the second measurements (end) of body mass were 
recorded 105 days later (28 august 2020).                      
 
Results  
 During the 24-hour trials, active-passive-1 ranged from 0-857, while the second measure of 
activity, active-passive-2, ranged from 0-295 (Figure 3). Detection time ranged from 0-
158154 milliseconds and shelter-factor ranged from 0-209 (Figure 3). Exploration-avoidance 
ranged from 0-8 PIT antennas explored (Figure 3). In both trial 1 and trial 2, 36 fish did not 
leave the first box, whereas a total of 40 fish did not leave the first box in one of two trials 
(Figure 3).  
  
Apart from active-passive-2, all BTs (data 1) tested were found to be significantly 
repeatable (P ≤ 0.05) over the 21 days period between the trials (Table 1) (Figure 4). With 
data 2, only exploration-avoidance was found to be repeatable (P < 0.05) and scored the 
highest Radj (0.88) (Appendix Table 1A).  
All measures of BTs (data 1) had a significant positive correlation when tested 
pairwise against each other (P < 0.001) (Table 2) (Figure 4). Weight had a significant 
correlation (P < 0.001) with active-passive-1 (r = 0.39), active-passive-2 (r = 0.32), detection 
time (r = 0.38) and SGR (r = 0.36) (Table 2). Avoidance-exploration and shelter-factor had a 
less significant (P < 0.05) and had lower correlation with weight (r = 0.24 and 0.22, 
respectively) (Table 2). With data 2; active-passive 1 and active-passive 2 had a significant 
positive correlation (P < 0.001) when tested against each other (r = 0.82) and both had a 
significant positive correlation (P < 0.001) with detection time (r = 0.94; r =0.71) (Table 2A).

Weight had a significant correlation with active-passive-1 (P < 0.01, r = 0.43) and detection

Being trapped in the fyke net (model 2, data 1) was significantly related with shelter-
factor (P < 0.05), detection time (P < 0.05) and active-passive-2 (P < 0.05) (Table 3). In 
(model 2, data 2), none of the BTs were significantly related with being trapped in the fyke

Discussion  
 
Our results indicate that round goby have consistent individual behavioral traits and 
the correlation between BT measures may suggest that active-passive and avoidance-
exploration are forming a behavior syndrome together. Furthermore, the likelihood of being 
caught in passive fishing gear was as expected affected by active-passive and avoidance-
exploration BTs. Hence, fish in our study showed individual variation in personality and a 
fish’ personality may affect its likelihood of getting caught in passive fishing gear.  
 
All BT measures, except for active-passive-2, were found to be significant repeatable 
in (model 1, data 1) (Table 1) and only avoidance-exploration was found to be repeatable in 
(model 1, data 2) (Table 1A). Our four significant repeatable BT measures had an average 
repeatability of 0,43 which corresponds to the 0,37 average of 759 estimates of behavior 
repeatability identified by Bell et al., (2009). All repeatable BTs may be interpreted as 
different measures of personality (Reale et al., 2007). However, only avoidance-explorative 
BT was found repeatable in both data sets. The difference in repeatability scores in data 1 and 
data 2 may indicate that results in (model 1, data 1) were affected by whether or not the fish 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
left the first box in the maze, as fish would not be detected if they remained in the first box. 
Thus, you cannot differentiate between the different BTs among fish that remained in the first 
box. However, the decision to leave the first box in the maze may be affected by the level of 
avoidance-exploration BT (Conrad et al., 2011). Hence, may the high repeatability of BTs in 
(model 1, data 1) partly be caused by a repeatable behavior to leave or stay in the first box in 
the maze.  
 
 
The probability of getting caught in the fyke net appeared to increase with an increase 
in all five BTs (model 2, data 1) (table 3), but the increase was only significant for active-
passive 2, detection time and shelter-factor, where only shelter-factor and detection time were 
found repeatable and hence considered as personality traits in this study. Detection time had 
the highest correlation (r = 0.96) with our measure of active-passive (active-passive-1) and 
may therefore be a good indicator of how active fish were in the maze (Table 2). 
Furthermore, shelter-factor measured how fast fish passed the PIT antennas and had the 
highest correlation (r = 0.79) with our measure of avoidance-exploration (avoidance-
exploration). Hence, more active and explorative fish may be more likely to get caught in 
passive fishing gear (fyke nets).  
 
 Our five BTs were significantly correlated (table 2). The strong correlations found 
between all BTs may indicate a behavior syndrome. However, the zero-inflated data may 
influence the results as staying in the first box results in completely identical BTs (zero).  
Nonetheless, the results of this study may support former literature in regards to avoidance-
explorative and active-passive BTs commonly forming a behavior syndrome (Sih et al., 
2004). For example, Cote et al. (2010) found that individuals of another invasive species, the 
mosquitofish (Gambusia affinis), displayed strong positive correlations between measures of 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
active-passive and avoidance-exploration BTs, which indicate that they may form a behavior 
syndrome. Furthermore, another behavioral study of round gobies showed that more active 
individuals from newly established populations had a lower latency to pass through tubes into 
a novel environment in a similar experimental setup (Thorlacius et al., 2015), which may be 
indicative of explorative behavior. Hence, it is possible that correlation between active-
passive and avoidance-explorative may be a common behavior syndrome in round goby.  
 
Body mass was found to correlate significantly with all measures of BTs and SGR 
was found to correlate significantly with shelter-factor (r = 0.19) (data1) (table 2). The 
relatively low correlations with SGR may be due to an imprecise and varying feeding 
schedule of the fish after the end of the experiment and the short period (105 days) between 
weight measurements. Hence, our measurements of SGR cannot be considered a precise 
indicator of growth, but we encourage similar future studies to include SGR based on longer 
periods under regular feeding, as it may correlate with BTs and metabolism (Biro et al., 2008; 
Metcalfe et al., 2016; Behrens et al., 2020). Individuals with high SGR often requires a higher 
resource acquisition and therefore often face higher risk of predation (Biro et al., 2008; 
Dmitriew, 2011).  
The growth–predation risk trade-off between acquiring food and predation risk is a 
well-known trade-off in behavioral ecology which effects the decision-making system 
employed by individuals under predation. Increased risk of predation may lead to decreased 
foraging effort of prey species (Randall et a., 2014). Hence, presence of a predator may affect 
prey foraging behavior with potential for cascading effects across the ecosystem (Randall et 
al., 2014). For example, the predation risk of round goby may affect how much it forages and 
thus competes with indigenous species for space and food and eat their eggs and juveniles, 
with lower predation risk leading to higher impact per round goby. However, higher 
population densities may lead to increased predation risk, as predators may feed more on high 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
density populations, a phenomenon known as density-dependent foraging (Mansour et al., 
1991). Round gobies in established areas with high densities may be smaller, slower growing 
and in poorer condition than in newly established areas (Azour et al., 2015), likely due to 
higher predation risk and intraspecific competition for food and space at higher densities. 
Whereas, round gobies at the invasion front may be bigger due to higher food availability and 
lower intraspecific competition (Brandner et al., 2013). However, the round goby is an 
aggressive species (Groen et al., 2012) and bigger individuals may have a competitive 
advantage over smaller individuals (Brandner et al., 2013; Hudina et al., 2014) as a size 
difference of only three percent is usually enough to determine the outcome of an aggressive 
encounter in favor of the bigger individual (Stammler and Corkum 2005). Thus, smaller 
individuals at high density areas may be more prone to disperse to the invasion fronts where 
density is lower and hence individuals may face less competition (Brownscombe and Fox, 
2012). This is supported by Brownscombe and Fox, (2012) and Thorlacius et al., (2018) that 
found round gobies in recently established invasive fronts were smaller, which is in contrast 
to the findings of Brandner et al., (2013) who found that round gobies at the invasive front 
were bigger. The development of alternative ontogenies in several invasive species may be 
related to the phase of the invasion and the local demographic, however, ecological factors 
may also affect the development of alternative ontogenies, which may explain the contrasting 
results of relative size at the invasion front (McGhee et al., 2013; Hôrková et al., 2015; 
Bekkevold et al 2020).  
 
Ontogenetic responses may influence how species interacts with its environment and 
other species with potential consequences for population dynamics and community stability 
(Mitchell 2009; Mougi and Kishida 2009; Cortez 2011; Flaxman et al. 2011; Mougi 2012). 
However, consistent variation in BT’s among individuals may result in different ontogenetic 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
responses to its environment and other species (Bolnick et al. 2011; Schreiber et al. 2011; 
Griffen et al. 2012; Pruitt et al. 2012; Sih et al. 2012; Wolf and Weissing 2012). Therefore, 
the dynamic development of predator-prey interactions may depend on underlying variation 
in BTs among individuals (McGhee et al., 2013). Furthermore, BTs may not only influence 
the outcome of predator-prey interactions, but also which predator-prey interactions are 
occurring. For example, sea stars (Pisaster ochraceus) that are more active may feed on 
sedentary snails than more passive sea stars which may feed more on more mobile snails 
(Pruitt et al. 2012). Variation in BTs may therefor influence how well indigenous species 
adapt to feed on an invasive species. Maintaining variation in BTs may thus be crucial for 
maintaining ecological resilience against invasive species and other threats. Furthermore, the 
presence of an invasive species may also alter the BTs of indigenous species (Whitlow et al., 
2009).   
 
Could management deliberately reduce the trait variation among an invasive species 
through selective fishery? It may be possible to deliberately change the traits among an 
invasive species population in order to inhibit the populations fitness. For example, in 
populations of invasive lionfish (Pterois volitans) through removal by spearfishing, 
individuals have shifted their behavior to become more crepuscular, thus likely increasing 
their encounter rates with indigenous fishes at dawn and dusk (Côté et al. 2014). Management 
of the invasive pumpkinseed sunfish population (Lepomis gibbosus) through removal by 
angling has led to changes in sunfish population size distribution and size at maturity 
(Evangelista et al., 2015). Our results indicate it may be possible to target more active and 
explorative round gobies through passive-fishing (Table 3), which adds to the growing 
evidence that fish with explorative, active and bold BTs may be more vulnerable to passive 
gears (Alós, Palmer & Arlinghaus, 2012; Diaz Pauli et al., 2015; Wiech et al., 2015; Moland 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
et al., 2019). This could be useful knowledge for management, as individuals at invasive 
fronts with explorative, active and bold BTs may be vulnerable to passive fishing gear (Alós, 
Palmer, & Arlinghaus, 2012; Diaz Pauli et al., 2015; Moland et al 2019). In addition, passive 
gears have already showed to be an efficient tool for catching round goby in Danish waters 
where most round gobies are caught in fyke nets (Carl, Azour & Møller, 2019), and catches 
may therefore consist of more explorative and active fish than the rest of the population if the 
same correlation between BTs and vulnerability to passive gears exist in the wild. 
 
 Removal of active and explorative BTs may decrease growth rate, as active and 
explorative fish tend to have higher growth rate. Decreased growth rate may lead to smaller 
body sizes, which may increase vulnerability to indigenous predators and thus increase 
natural mortality rates, which can further reduce population abundance (Audzijonyte et al. 
2014). Furthermore, active-passive and avoidance-exploration BTs may co-vary with other 
BTs at the invasion front and may together form a behavior syndrome that promotes invasion 
(Chucholl et al., 2008; Pintor et al., 2008; Brownscombe et al., 2012; Thorlacius et al., 2018). 
Decreasing active and explorative BTs within populations could therefore theoretically be 
used as a tool for controlling round goby, though there are still a lot of unknown factors and 
removal would probably have to be extensive.  However complete eradication of invasive 
round goby seems unlikely and therefore we suggest future studies to address the ecological 
effects and heredity of BTs in round gobies. Furthermore, the current management of round 
goby in Danish waters may already remove round gobies with more active and explorative 
BTs, as most round gobies are caught in fyke-nets (Carl, Azour & Møller, 2019).   
 
 
 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
Conclusion 
Our study supports the growing evidence of consistent variation in BT’s exist among 
fish and thus fish may have different personalities. In extension, our study suggests an 
existing behavior syndrome among the active-passive and avoidance-exploration BTs in 
round goby. Furthermore, our study suggest active-passive and avoidance-exploration BTs 
may be related to vulnerability to passive fishing gear (fyke-net), which is accordance with 
other studies. Variation in BTs may decrease over time as fisheries may be selective for 
certain BTs and thus may limit certain BTs under high fishing pressures. Decreased variation 
in BTs may have negative impact on fish populations which may have cascading effect 
throughout the ecosystem. We suggest future studies to investigate the possible effects of 
deliberately decreasing BTs of invasive populations in order to reduce their fitness.
