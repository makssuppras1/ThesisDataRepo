Abstract
The incidence rate of multiple sclerosis has been on the rise in the last few decades. The
disease is theorised to be caused by a mixture of genetic and environmental factors, how-
ever the precise aetiology is not known. In this thesis aim to locate areas and age intervals
where individuals in Denmark are more susceptible to the environmental factors.
A previous study on the same data set indicates that a spatio-temporal relationship be-
tween geographical location and age can be found. High-risk areas were found in the
eastern part of Jutland.
Multiple analysis are conducted throughout this thesis - spatio-temporal analyses in
SpaceStat and spatial analyses in SatScan. The results from SpaceStat point towards
many areas and ages of interest, while the results point towards a single area of inter-
est.
The area around Aarhus and Randers is found to be of high-risk along with the child-
hood years and teenage years. This corresponds with the results from a previous study
on the data.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Multiple sclerosis (MS) is the breakdown of the insulating covers of the nerves in the central
nervous system, thus resulting in degraded communication between the nerves in the eyes,
brain, and the spinal cord [25]. MS is deﬁned as a chronic inﬂammatory demyelinating dis-
ease. An illustration of the differences between a healthy nerve and a nerve affected by MS
can be found in ﬁgure 1.1. The number of cases of MS is on the rise, however this is likely
due to the new diagnostic techniques and heightened awareness of the disease [4, 1]. The
disease as it is known today was ﬁrst described by Charcot in 1868 [18]. The introduction of
the information age has enabled new analysis of the disease.
Figure 1.1: The illustration shows difference between a healthy and a MS affected nerve [3].
The most common symptoms of MS are fatigue, problems with balance, reduced mucsle
strength, or sensory disturbances [25]. Most individuals with MS also exprience mental and
cognitive inﬂuences. Some individuals with MS experience multiple symptoms daily, while
others experience symptom-free periods [25].
1
==PAGE:8==
1. INTRODUCTION
The progress of the disease is unique for each individual and age at diagnosis does not seem
to have an effect on the progress [25]. Both young and old can have severe and mild progress
of MS. Generally twenty years after diagnosis two thrids of the individuals with MS do not
have a need for a wheelchair, however, they migh have a need for a cane. MS in itself is not
a deadly disease, however the affected individuals have a lower life expectancy that not af-
fected individuals of about 5-10 years [25].
Throughout the progress of the disease relapses are common [8]. During a relapse the indi-
viduals existing symptoms become worse or new symptoms appear. In between relapses, the
affected individuals can sometimes have a symptom free period and the disease might be
dormant for months or years [11, 8].
MS is divided into four categories [11, 8]:
• Relapsing-remitting MS
The most common form of sclerosis. Around 85% of people diagnosed with MS are
initially diagnosed with relapsing-remitting MS.
Relapses are unpredictable in this type of MS. The relapses can last for days or month,
but there is partial or total recovery after the relapse.
• Primary progressive MS
Around 10% of people diagnosed with MS are initially diagnosed with primary progres-
sive MS. This type of MS is characterised by slow onset and steadily worsening symp-
toms.
• Secondary progressive MS
As symptoms of primary progressive MS worsen, individuals who develop a disability
later in life are diagnosed with secondary progressive MS.
• Progressive relapsing MS
The most uncommon type of MS. Around 5% of the people diagnosed with MS are
initially diagnosed with progressive relapsing MS. This type of MS is characterised by a
steady neurological decline and no remission.
Diagnosis is based on neurological examinations, disease history, and a series of other tests
that might include an MRI scan or blood tests [11].
The cause of MS is not entirely established, however it is theorised that a mixture of genetics
and environmental factors are the cause [4, 25, 24, 18]. The environmental factors are likely
affecting individuals during early life [2]. Relatives have an average increased risk of 20% [4],
where ﬁrst-degree relatives have an increased risk between 2% and 35% and second-degree
have an increased risk between 1% and 2%. The background risk is estimated to be close to
0% [4]. In ﬁgure 1.2 the lifetime risk of MS for relatives to a person with MS is shown [4].
2
==PAGE:9==
Figure 1.2: The illustration shows the age-adjusted risk for relatives of individuals with MS
and the degree of shared genetics between the relative and the person with MS [4].
A study on the Danish population conﬁrms the results on ﬁrst-degree relatives [20]. The study
by Nielsen et al. ﬁnds that ﬁrst-degree relatives have a risk of 2.5% with an additional spo-
radic risk of 0.3% for men and 0.5% for women in Denmark. In the study, no increased risk
for spouses of people with MS was found, which suggests that environmental factors do not
play a major role in expressing MS in adulthood.
The increased risk of expressing MS is likely due to environmental factors than genetic changes
in the Danish population [1]. Some of the environmental changes include use of oral con-
traceptives, women choosing to have children later in life, and changes in vitamin D levels.
Factors in lifestyle and environmental changes are likely to have an effect on the prevalence
of the disease in women [1]. Women have insufﬁcient vitamin D levels more often than men
[4, 1]; this could explain the skewed distribution of MS between the genders.
To be able to predict and determine the environmental factors that inﬂuence the expression
of MS, a known high-risk area of inﬂuence is needed. To be able to locate the areas of in-
ﬂuence geo-referenced data is needed to examine potential patterns in a population. The
increase in geo-referenced data over the last decade, has made the analysis of patterns pos-
sible [14, 22]. The problem is no longer acquiring the data, but the analysis of the massive
amounts of data.

1. INTRODUCTION
1.1
Aims
The occurrence of MS is most common in the regions populated by Northern Europeans [4]
and is more prevalent in women [18]. The disease usually has an onset at age 20-40 [11].
Denmark is a high-risk area for MS [1, 20]. A study on Danish data found a rise in the fe-
male to male ratio from 1.31:1 in 1950 to 2.02:1 in 2005 [1]. The incidence rate also increased
from 58.8 per 100,000 in 1950 to 154.5 per 100,000 in 2005. Due to the rise in the number of
individuals with MS, further exploration of the disease and risk factors is needed.
This thesis aims to look for spatio-temporal patterns in Danish MS cases and to determine
if there is a speciﬁc age range or location, where one is more susceptible to environmental
factors that induce MS.
1.1.1
Limitations
The data used in this thesis is conﬁdential and partially anomynised, thus the data cannot be
published or removed from secure storage. Therefore, external high performance computers
cannot be utilized for the analysis conducted in this thesis and utilisation of many big data
methods and machine learning algorithms using high performance will likely not be able to
run on a single desktop.
The data cannot be fully anomynised, as the analysis tools need the speciﬁc residential his-
tory of the individuals in the study. A way to elude this problem is to generate data from
patterns and weights found in the original data, which is not done in this thesis.

Chapter 2
Data
This section will explain spatial, temporal, and spatio-temporal data types along with an
overview of the data and the data puriﬁcation.
2.1
Spatial and temporal data
Spatial refers to a relation to space, thus spatial data describe the locations of geographical
relationships and features [5]. This type of information is usually stored as coordinates.
Temporal refers to a relation to time, thus temporal data is a record of a state in time [6].
An example of temporal data is the temperature at a certain location on a certain day. The
temporal dimension describes the evolution of the objects captured by the data [14]. If an
object does not evolve, then the data is a static snapshot.
2.2
Spatio-temporal data
Spatio-temporal data contains information about both space and time for an event [14]. The
data can for example contain information about the transmission route of a disease or the
moving patterns of a person. Spatio-temporal data is more sophisticated than spatial or tem-
poral data alone, due to the combination of the time and space factors [7].
Spatio-temporal data can be divided into ﬁve categories, with each having speciﬁc methods
for dealing with that type of data [14]. Table 2.1 gives an description of each data type. The
data is divided according to the amount of spatial and temporal data available and the con-
nection between the data.

2.2.1
ST events
Spatio-temporal events (ST events) are events that store static spatial and temporal informa-
tion [14]. The event can for example be an earth tremor. Usually the event is associated with
the location where it was recorded and the corresponding time stamp. The data is static as
no change over time is possible [14].
The ST events can be analysed using SatScan (see section 3.1) or other spatial scan statis-
tics such as DBSCAN [14]. Clusters among events points to the events being close in time and
space, as well as potentially sharing other non-spatial characteristics [14].
Depending on the type of data and event investigated during an analysis, different methods
and algorithms can be utilised. An illustration of ST event data can be found in ﬁgure 2.1 [14].

2.2.2
Geo-referenced variable and time series
Geo-referenced data is metrics about an event at a ﬁxed location [14, 7]. In the simplest case,
geo-referenced data is similar to ST events [14]. The difference is that the data pertains to the
same time instance and the non-spatial features are changing. The whole history of an object
is called a time series [14].
When analysing geo-referenced data, one needs to take the spatial and non-spatial features
into account and the clusters found at previous time stamps [14]. For time series, one needs
to compare the changes in the time series and relate the changes to the spatial location.

2.2.3
Moving objects and trajectories
Moving points and trajectories are observations of an event that moves in both space and
time [14]. In the simplest case of moving objects, it is only the most recent location and time-
stamp, while the trajectories contains the full history of movements. The objects often have
an ID number, so the movement history can be followed [7].
During analysis of this type of data, the focus points in the analysis of geo-referenced data
are also needed [14]. The aim of an analysis is often to detect changes in the movement of the
object. The trajectories can be analysed to detect groups of objects that behave in an identi-
cal way [14].

2.3
Study data set
The data set consists of the current and historical addresses for 13,968 Danes with an onset of
MS between 1971 and 2013 (called cases), along with 100 people per case (130,000 people in
total) randomly selected of the Danish population without MS (called controls) [2]. The data
has been extracted using incidence density sampling with replacement. This means that a
control is sampled at the time of diagnosis for the case [19, 27]. This also allows a control to
be a case or a control for multiple cases.
The data is provided by the Danish Multiple Sclerosis Registry (DMSR) and the Danish Civil
Registration System (DCRS). The registry is estimated to be more than 90% complete and
have a validity of 94% [1, 20]. Cases with no Danish address at the onset of the disease, cases
born outside Denmark, and immigrants and descendants were excluded from the data set [2].
In total 975 cases were excluded.
The 100 controls per case were matched on sex and age, and randomly selected by incidence
density sampling from the DCRS [2]. The controls are selected between 6 months before and
after the date of birth of the corresponding case. The controls were subjected to the same
exclusion criteria as the cases and should also be MS free and alive at the onset date of the
corresponding case.
During data reﬁnement, two cases were removed, as they were lacking address information.
The controls for the two cases are also excluded from the study along with controls with miss-
ing address data.
The data set consist of three ﬁles - diagnosis, case_kontrol, and adresser.
The diagnosis ﬁle contains information about the diagnosis of the cases. The diagnosis is an
integer value. If the value of the diagnosis ﬁeld is 3, then the case is correctly diagnosed.
The case_kontrol ﬁle contains information about the case and the corresponding controls.
The ﬁle contains the following data for both cases and controls: onset date, data of birth, year
of birth, gender, and an unique ID number. The IDs are assigned as the identiﬁer instead

of the personal identiﬁcation number from DCRS. This is done to anonymize the data. The
onset date is an estimated date at the point of diagnosis. An onset if often estimated between
5 and 10 years before the diagnosis, but since the disease is progressing slowly in some cases,
the onset date might be must earlier [25].
The adresser ﬁle contains the addresses of the cases and controls from birth to the index date.
The index date is the date where the data was collected from DCRS. The ﬁle contains the

Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) is a project that creates a grid around the world, thus
dividing the world into 60 zones [9]. The coordinates used in the UTM projection are called
easting and northing, and are measured in metres. The easting coordinate is measured from
the false origin 500,000 metres to the west of the central meridian of the UTM zone [9]. The
central meridian is deﬁned as the centre of each zone. The false origin is created to ensure
that the values of the easting coordinate do not become negative. This is done by assigning
the central meridian as the value 500,000. The northing coordinate is deﬁned as the number
of meters away from the equator the coordinate is located.
Denmark is located in UTM zones 32U, 32 V, 33U, and 33V, however the data has only been
encoded in UTM 32U.
From the above deﬁnitions of different types of spatio-temporal data I ﬁnd that the data of
this study are trajectories, since the full residential history of the individuals in the study is
availiable.

Chapter 3
Methods
In chapter 2 the data was described and found to be of spatio-temporal trajectory nature. The
data contained in trajectories is complex, but at the same time very promising for discovering
patterns [14]. Analysis of trajectories is usually done using one of seven approaches [14]:
• Descriptive and generative model-based clustering
Aims to create a global model adept at describing the whole data set. The methods can
rely on a deﬁnition of multivariate density distribution.
• Distance based clustering methods
The idea is to transform the complex object into feature vectors, that can then be clus-
tered using a generic cluster algorithm. With the reduced complexity the only problem
is ﬁnding a suitable generic clustering algorithm and a distance function. The cluster-
ing algorithm should be able to determine how the trajectories are joined and which
trajectories are candidates to be in the same cluster.
The method will also determine the shape of the resulting cluster - centre-based meth-
ods that are very sensitive to noise and will produce a compact spherical cluster around
a set of centroids, hierarchical clusters will organise the data objects into a multi-level
structure, while a density-based clustering method will form maximal, dense clusters.
• Density-based methods
A density threshold around each object is used to distinguish the interesting data ob-
jects from the noise.
The method relies strongly on the efﬁciency of the implementation of the neighbour-
hood query.
• Visual-aided approaches
Different tools have been developed for the different types of data and are able to work
with many types of movement data. An advantage of visual-aided approaches is that
the movement of the object is very clear. The programs are often designed in such a
way that the user can change the input parameters, interpret the results, and direct the
algorithm towards a solution that describes the underlying phenomena better.
13
==PAGE:20==
3. METHODS
• Micro clustering methods
Aims to determine a maximal time interval, where all the trajectories are pair-wise close
to each other.
• Flocks and convoys
This method is useful when working with migrating animals and convoys of vehicles,
and aims to discover a group of objects, that at a given time, move together.
• Important places
This method aims to ﬁnd the important places along a single trajectory. The method
works by ﬁnding places with many location measurements that have been clustered
together.
The method used to create the spatio-temporal analysis in this thesis is utilising the distance-
based method, by utilising a k nearest neighbour algorithm.
3.1
SatScan and spatial scan statistic
A spatial scan statistic method was developed by Martin Kulldorff to extend upon scan statis-
tics, as previous implementations were only able to test if a point ﬁeld is random or if a cluster
can be detected [15].
The following features are supported:
1. Detection of clusters in a multidimensional point ﬁeld
2. Varying size of the scan window
3. The baseline may be any inhomogeneous Poisson or Bernoulli process
The method implements both a Bernoulli model and a Poisson model [15]. The decision of
which process to use depends on the problem. If the data consist of binary counts, then the
Bernoulli model is optimal, and if the data has counts relating to a continuous risk factor,
then the Poisson model is best.
If the measure of all subsets, µ(G), is large compared to total number of points it does not
matter which processes is used, as the Bernoulli and Poisson process in this case closely ap-

The spatial scan statistics method is able to both do inference and detect the location of clus-

The spatial scan statistic method and the proposed extensions have been implemented by
Martin Kulldorff in the free software SatScan [17].
SatScan is only able to handle one address per individual and is thus only able to perform
a spatial analysis on my data set. The software tends to select clusters with large areas with
a large population at risk, since these areas have the highest levels of statistical power [22].
SatScan tends to overlook small areas with lower levels of signiﬁcance, but with higher preva-

SatScan is a well-tested method that is used to detect clusters in topics such as astrology,
war, and health.

Jacquez’ Q-statistics is a method used for detecting local and global clusters in the residential
history of the cases through the course of life, relative to the controls [26]. The method is well
known and often used for detecting clusters in case-control studies [21, 26]. Unlike SatScan,
Jacquez’ Q-statisctic is able to handle the entire residence history of a person, thus enabling
the method to perform a spatio-temporal analysis on a data set.

The implementation of Jacquez’ Q-statistics has four settings for the exposure period - con-
tinuous, retrospective, by age, and by cohort [10].
• The continuous exposure period generates a Qi,t each time an individual moves or its
neighbour arrangement changes [10].
• The retrospective exposure period allows the user to deﬁne a latency and exposure pe-
riod. The neighbours to an individual will only be examined when they are in the expo-
sure period of the individual [10].
• The by age exposure period compares the individuals to their neighbours at the same
age, even though they were not neighbours at that point in time. An age exposure pe-
riod can be set [10].
• The by cohort exposure period allows for restricting the analysis to an exposure time
that is deﬁned by a date. This is usually used for modelling discrete periods of exposure
to e.g. a pathogen [10].
When using Q-statistics, the user must set the number of nearest neighbours or the neighbour
distance. The neighbour distance should be indicated in metres, while the number of nearest
neighbours should be an integer. The choice of k is central for the analysis, as a k too small
can render the program unable to differentiate true and false positive, while a k too large
can result in failure to detect small clusters and over-smoothing [26]. Sloan et al [26] have
explored this issue and found 15 to be useful starting point for k. Their study was run using
two case-control data sets - one about mobility data from the United States and one about
mobility data from Denmark.

4.5. Results from SatScan analysis
4.5
Results from SatScan analysis
In the following section the results from the analysis with SatScan are displayed.
4.5.1
Initial analysis
A retrospective spatial analysis was done using SatScan version 9.5 [17]. The aim of the anal-
ysis was to see if there is an age where locations of cases are gathered into groups. Ages 0, 4,
8, 12, and 16 were chosen for the initial analysis. A data set was created for each of the ages
with 10 controls for each case.
A circular search window was utilised together with the Bernoulli model as the discrete scan
statistics. The circular window was utilised due to time constraints. The null hypothesis is
that the proportion of cases is the same inside and outside of the circular window, while the
alternative hypothesis is that the proportion of cases is larger within the circular window than
outside. A log likelihood ratio test is used to determine the signiﬁcance, while the p-value is
obtained using 999 Monte Carlo simulations.
During the analysis I was looking for both high and low rates. High rates are areas with higher
risk of MS, while low rates are areas with lower risk of MS. Each data set was run 10 times with
different values for the maximum cluster size. The values for the cluster size were in steps
of 5, starting at 5% and ending at 50% of the population at risk. Geographical overlap was
not permitted in the analysis. In ﬁgure 4.35 the results from the analysis in SatScan with a
maximum of 15% of the population at risk is displayed. Signiﬁcant clusters are deﬁned by a
p-value ≤0.05. In table 4.1 the result from the analysis at age 0 is shown. A similar table for
the other ages can found in appendix A.3.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In the following chapter the topics of discussion have been divided into sections, in order
to give a better overview. I will start by discussing the results of the analysis with SpaceStat
and SatScan and compare the results found in this thesis with each other and those found by
Bihrmann et al [2].
5.1
SpaceStat results
The SpaceStat results indicate clusters in childhood and teenage years in Denmark. These
clusters were found in Århus, Randers, Copenhagen, Hostebro, Svendborg, and Aabenraa.
Most of these areas were also found in the analysis by Bihrmann et al [2], however the cluster
in Copenhagen was found to be a low-risk area in the analysis, while the results from SpaceS-
tat indicates Copenhagen as a high-risk area.
The computational time for an analysis run on a single desktop was around 2 hours for the
largest data set (around 8,000 cases). For the smaller data sets, the computational time was
around 1 hour. This computational time is much better than what is reported by Sloan et al
[26]. In 2014 BioMedware updated SpaceStat to version 4.0 and introduced multithreading to
improve the performance.
5.1.1
The eﬀect of k in SpaceStat
Previous studies have proved that a k = 5 is less efﬁcient at differentiating false positives and
true positives, however larger values of k proved to reduce cluster signal and over-smooth the
data [26]. In this study the effect of changing the number of nearest neighbours located more
signiﬁcant clusters (Qit,p ≤0.05 and Qi,p ≤0.05). When using the p-values suggested by
Sloan et al [26] (Qit,p ≤0.05 and Qi,p ≤0.001), more individuals were found to be signiﬁcant
at k = 15 compared to k = 10 and k = 20.

5. DISCUSSION
5.1.2
The eﬀect of end age in SpaceStat
The end age parameter deﬁnes the age-exposure window. The more knowledge there is about
the age period prior to the analysis, the more focused the analysis will be on that age period.
The environmental factors, that play a role in MS, is hypothesised to play a role in the teens
and early twenties, thus our analysis was done with an end age of 30. In one of the analyses
the end age was changed to 40, because groups of signiﬁcant individuals were spotted in the
late twenties. However, the allowance of a higher age did not change the initial results of the
SpaceStat analysis.
When lowering the end age, the analysis resulted in more clusters than previously. This is
likely due to adding new individuals to the analysis. The analysis with one control and 20 as
the end age, show most of the areas found in previous analyses; both with one control and
ﬁve controls, while the analysis with four controls agree with the previous analyses with ﬁve
controls on an area of interest being present on Funen and in southern Jutland.
5.2
SatScan results on the entire data set
The initial investigation in SatScan on the data set containing all cases having a valid address
at ages 0, 4, 8, 12, and 16 showed Århus/Randers area to be signiﬁcant for all ages, except age
8. These results are in agreement with the initial analysis of the data done by Bihrmann et al
[2], where the same area was found to be a high-risk area of MS.
The computational time for an analysis run on a single desktop was around 8 hours for the
entire data set.
5.2.1
The eﬀect of the size of the search window in SatScan
The size of the maximum percentage of the population at risk does not seem to have much
of an inﬂuence on the results from the SatScan analyss. In a simulation study by Ribeiro and
Costa [23] the effects of the size of the search window is evaluated. A 50% search window is
suggested as a default, unless the user is investigating smaller areas or there are limitations to
the data available [23]. Smaller values for the search window size can also be a precursor for
ﬁnding irregular cluster shapes, without having to use the time consuming elliptical search
window [23].
In the study with the entire data set in this thesis, I was only able to ﬁnd a single cluster at
all the different values of the search window. Thus, I know there is no irregular shape in the
data set. The location of the cluster along with relative risk and p-value of the clusters all
seem to stabilise around 15% of the population at risk in the analysis done for this thesis.

5.3. Investigation of SpaceStat results in SatScan
5.2.2
The eﬀect of number of controls in SatScan
In the analyses conducted in SatScan I have run analysis with a different number of controls -
5, 10, and 25 controls. Of the three, only the entire data set with 10 controls was able to ﬁnd a
cluster in Denmark. When running the same data set with 25 controls, where 10 of them was
part of the original data set, I was unable to detect a signiﬁcant cluster. The signiﬁcant cluster
seems to be diluted by the 15 additional controls. However, I am not able to conﬁrm this, as I
was unable to ﬁnd related work about changing the number of controls in a SatScan analysis.
The analysis with 5 controls was speciﬁc to a certain case birth range, thus there might not be
a cluster at this point in time.
5.3
Investigation of SpaceStat results in SatScan
The results from the analysis in SpaceStat indicated that clusters might be present in the
teenage years in Denmark. To validate these results, the age 12 and 16 were investigated
for cases born between 1940 and 1979, and also in the time period 1950 to 1979. Neither of
the analysis with 10 controls yielded any signiﬁcant clusters.
The computational time for an analysis run on a single desktop was around 10 minutes for
the subsets of the data set.
5.4
SpaceStat results compared to SatScan results
Sloan et al. suggest using SpaceStat to search for time intervals and areas that might be of
potential interest and verify these results by analysing the SpaceStat results in SatScan [26].
Thus, I tried to verify the clusters I found in the analysis in SpaceStat with SatScan. None
of the clusters located in SpaceStat was found in the analysis with SatScan. This is not com-
pletely unexpected, as I needed to change the p-value for Qi from 0.001 to 0.05 to be able to
ﬁnd clusters in the analysis from SpaceStat. However, it was surprising as a cluster on the
entire data set was found in SatScan previous to the analysis in SpaceStat and I expected to
be able to ﬁnd the same region again in a subset of the data. A reason for the cluster to be
missing could be that the criteria that every person in the SpaceStat analysis should have
an address at age 0, 4, 8, 12, and 16 exclude the individuals that make the results from the
analysis in SatScan signiﬁcant.
5.5
Thesis results compared to previous results
The results from SatScan and SpaceStat are similar to the results by Birhmann et al [2] on the
same data set, but with a different analysis methods. There seems to be a consensus between
the three analysis methods that the Århus/Randers area is of high-risk. Some of the other
areas found during the SpaceStat analyses, were also found in the analysis by Bihrmann et al,
however they were not found to be signiﬁcant in SatScan.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this thesis I have investigated the spatial and spatio-temporal relationship between age
and geographical location between individuals with multiple sclerosis and individuals with-
out multiple sclerosis. Multiple spatial analyses were conducted in SatScan and multiple
spatio-temporal analyses were conducted in SpaceStat.
Subsets of the data set did not indicate any clusters in Denmark. However, the entire data
set indicates a cluster in the area around Århus and Randers, and indicates that the teenage
years and childhood are of high risk for the development of sclerosis later on in life.
The results from my analyses in SpaceStat do not point towards it being a robust program for
the detection of clusters in time and space.
The results from my analyses in SatScan point towards the number of controls having a large
role to play in the results. With a search window of 15% the results from the analysis are sta-
bilized, thus there is no need for running more time-consuming analyses that will yield the
same result.
6.1
Future work
Exploring the result from the open source implementation of Jacquez’ Q-statistics in pyJacqQ
might be interesting. If the open source implementation yield the same results as BioMed-
wares implementation in SpaceStat, then it would allow for a broader use of Jacquez’ Q-
statistics - not only for users with limited funds but also for users wanting to utilise an Unix
based operating system. Further development of the implementation is possible due to the
open-source nature.
Additional future work could utilise the family relationship between the cases and run the
analysis again. This would allow us to explore the relationship between the environmental
