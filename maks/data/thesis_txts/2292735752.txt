Introduction 
 
The field of metagenomics is in constant progression as continued improvements in next-generation 
sequencing techniques lead to higher capacity and lower cost of DNA sequencing. Metagenomics benefits not 
only because the sequencing of metagenomics data becomes cheaper, but even more so because the amount 
of genomic data available from central databases such as NCBI and EBI is constantly increasing as new species 
are identified and their genetic makeup charted, providing new references for the identification and study of 
future metagenomics samples. 
When studying metagenomics data the first problem encountered is most often determining what is in the 
sample; which species are present. MGmapper is a web based alignment tool capable of mapping large 
amounts of short DNA sequences to several large databases. It takes as input raw NGS reads and uses the bwa-
mem algorithm to align query sequences to a set of databases specified by the user and outputs alignment files 
as well statistics on references that have been aligned with queries. MGmapper has shown the most promising 
results when mapping against databases in so called chain-mode, that is, once a read has been successfully 
mapped to a database MGmapper will not attempt to map it against subsequent databases. Chain-mode 
mapping has been shown to outperform full-mode mapping (where every read is mapped against every 
database) (Petersen TN et.al.), but it also presents a problem as a read mapped to an incorrect species will not 
only result in a false positive, it will also prevent the read from being mapped to subsequent databases where 
it could potentially be correctly mapped. This study will examine the potential of MGmapper to properly 
classify the species of sequencing reads as well as the implementation of an additional output filter to reduce 
the rate of false positives.

Theory and background 
 
Metagenomics 
Metagenomics is the study of microorganisms by direct extraction of genetic material from environmental 
samples without the process of first isolating and cultivating individual organisms to have their DNA 
sequenced. Samples can contain genetic material from thousands of organisms or more, and when studying 
microbial diversity and complex ecosystems of microorganisms, metagenomics has several advantages over 
regular genomic approaches (Handelman J, 2004). The enormous species diversity found in these samples 
makes cultivating and sequencing individual species a considerable task, and many organisms are completely 
missed by regular cultivation-based approaches simply because they resist cultivation (Hugenholtz P, Goebel 
BM, Pace NR, 1998). Metagenomics has found use in agriculture, ecology and several other fields, but its most 
prominent use is possibly in medicine where microbial communities, the human microbiome, have been found 
to play a significant role in human health. 
 
Next-generation Sequencing 
Next-generation sequencing (NGS), also known as high-throughput sequencing, is a broad term that comprises 
several modern methods of DNA sequencing. These methods are characterized by being significantly cheaper 
and having much greater capacity than Sanger sequencing, which was the most widely used sequencing 
method for 25 years, but has now been almost entirely replaced. These improved methods have had a massive 
impact on bioinformatics as the amount of sequence data shared through central databases and made 
available to researchers has increased massively and continues to increase. It has enabled the use of whole-
genome sequencing on a much larger scale and revolutionized entire fields of study including metagenomics. 
The most popular NGS technologies include Roche/454, Ion Torrent, SOLiD and Illumina sequencing, the latter 
of which is the focus of this report as it is the technology used to sequence the metagenomics samples 
analyzed. 
 
The procedure for Illumina sequencing 
Library preparation 
As with most NGS technologies extensive preparation is required before the actual sequencing takes place. 
With a method dubbed "tagmentation" purified DNA samples are fragmented by transposomes, which 
simultaneously ligate adapter sequences onto both ends of the DNA fragments (Illumina, Inc., 2014). The 
motifs added to the end of the strands include binding sites for the sequencing primer, an index sequence and 
lastly a specific adapter sequence on each end, on one end one that is complementary to the first kind of oligo 
found in the cluster generation flow cells, and on the other end one that is identical to the second type of oligo 
found in the flow cells. 
==PAGE:5==
4 
 
Cluster generation and Clonal Bridge Amplification 
Amplification of fragments takes place when the ligated DNA fragments are washed through flow cells coated 
with 2 types of oligos (Illumina, Inc., 2010). The first type of oligos is complementary to the adapter region on 
the fragment strand, enabling hybridization between the two. After hybridization a polymerase creates a 
compliment of the rest of the strand. The resulting double strand is denatured and the original template 
washed away, leaving room for the remaining complementary strand to fold over as the adapter region on the 
other end of the strand hybridizes to the second type of oligo in the flow cell, creating the bridge structure that 
lends its name to the method. Once again polymerases generate the complementary strand, creating a double 
stranded bridge which is then denatured to create separate forward and reverse strands. These strands are 
now free to fold over and hybridize to other oligos and the process is repeated. After a number of amplification 
cycles the reverse strand is cleaved off and washed away, leaving several copies of the forward strand to be 
used for sequencing. The 3'-end of the strand as well as the cell-surface oligos are blocked to prevent any 
interference with the sequencing process. 
 
Sequencing by synthesis. 
Sequencing is initiated by adding fluorescently tagged nucleotides as well as primer complementary to the 
primer binding site on the 3'-end of the strand (Illumina, Inc., 2010). The primer will hybridize to the binding 
site after which single nucleotides are added to the strand in cycles. With all four nucleotides present the 
nucleotide that is incorporated will generally be complementary to the one on the template. After the addition 
of each fluorescently tagged nucleotide the cluster is excited by a light source, causing a fluorescent signal to 
be emitted. The type of nucleotide that has been added is interpreted based on the wave length and intensity 
of the signal. This cycle of adding a nucleotide and interpreting base type is repeated a number of times equal 
to the desired read length and is performed simultaneously for many copies of the same template. Once the 
read itself has been sequenced the read product is washed off and the first index primer is added, which allows 
the index sequence at the 5'-end of the strand to be sequenced in the same fashion. This concludes the 
sequencing of the first read, however for paired-end reads a similar process has to take place for the reverse 
strand. After the first index is sequenced the sequence product is once again washed off, and the 3'-end of the 
strand as well as the oligos on the cell-surface are deprotected, allowing the strand to fold over and bind to the 
second type of oligo. The index 2 sequence is read similar to index one, after which the oligo is once again 
extended by a polymerase, creating a double stranded bridge. The double strand is denatured and the forward 
strand is cleaved and washed off, leaving the reverse strand. A second type of sequencing primer 
complementary to the binding site on the 5'-end of the strand is added and sequencing takes place exactly like 
it did for the forward strand. 
 
MGmapper 
MGmapper is a web-based method for strain identification that can map large amounts of short low-divergent 
DNA sequences against several large reference databases. It takes as input sequence reads from next-
==PAGE:6==
5 
 
generation sequencing platforms and aligns those sequences to a number of available pre-indexed databases 
as specified by the user. Mapping against each database can be done in full-mode or chain-mode. In full mode 
MGmapper will attempt to map all input reads against the database while in chain-mode only reads that have 
not already been successfully mapped to a database will be tested. The alignment itself is based on bwa-mem, 
which is described more in-depth later in this chapter. 
The output produced by MGmapper includes overall statistics on the number of reads in input file(s); how 
many remain after trimming and how many where mapped to each of the selected databases, as well as a file 
for each selected database containing statistics on references in that database that had reads mapped to them. 
This file contains a line for each reference in the database that had one or more reads mapped to it and 
includes, among other things, a unique identifier, a description i.e.the fasta-file header of the reference which 
often contains a classification at genus or species level, the number of reads mapped to that reference, the size 
of the reference, and the total number of nucleotides mapped to that reference. MGmapper also outputs 
alignments in BAM-format and compressed fastq files containing the unmapped reads. 
 
Bwa-mem 
Bwa-mem is one of several alignment algorithms found in the bwa software package and is designed to 
efficiently map low-divergent sequences shorter than 1Mbp to a large reference genome (Li H, Durbin R, 2013). 
BWA stands for Burrows-wheeler Alignment Tool, so called because Burrows-Wheeler Transform is used to 
create an FM-index from the reference genome. Bwa-mem initially locates alignments in the index with 
Maximal Exact Matches (MEMs) above a certain threshold (default 19), as the name suggests these are exact 
matches between two strings, i.e. an exact alignment that cannot be extended in either direction without 
allowing for a mismatch. All alignments located this way are then extended with the Smith-Waterman 
algorithm allowing for mismatches and insertions/deletions, and the final alignments are evaluated with a 
specific scoring scheme to determine the single best alignment which is given as output. 
 
Burrows-Wheeler Transform 
The Burrows-Wheeler Transform (BWT) is a block sorting algorithm proposed by M. Burrows and D.J. Wheeler 
in 1994 (Burrows M, 1994). It rearranges the order of characters in a string with a tendency to group runs of 
similar characters, allowing for a greater degree of data compression, while still being fully reversible. For 
instance the Burrows-Wheeler Transform of the string 
“It_was_the_best_of_times_it_was_the_worst_of_times$” becomes 
“s$esttssfftteww_hhmmbootttt_ii__woeeaaressIi_______” which can be compressed much more efficiently 
due to the many character repeats.The BWT also has a second application besides compression; indexing. The 
Burrows-Wheeler Alignment Tool makes use of the so-called FM-index proposed by Paolo Ferragina and 
Giovanni Manzini in 2000 (Ferragina P, 2000), which based on BWT and some additional small data structures 
functions as a space-efficient index of a string.

In order to perform BWT on a string, an end-of-string character, $ is added to the sequence. The end of string 
marker should be lexicographically smaller value than any other character in the string so that a regular sort 
will put it first in the string. 
A matrix is created based on the cyclic shifts of T, also known as the rotations of T. I.e. the strings obtained by 
repeatedly removing a character from one end of the string and adding that character to the other end of the 
string, producing a number of strings equal to the length of T. For instance the rotations of the string 
T=atgactt$ would be, $atgactt, t$atgact, tt$atgc, etc. Listing these strings below each other in alphabetical 
order results in the Burrows-Wheeler Matrix, BWM(T) . BWM(atgactt$) would look like this:

In total the FM-index contains 4 data structures. The F and L columns from the Burrows-Wheeler Matrix, the 
latter of which is also the Burrows-Wheeler Transform of T, the rank-array containing the ranks of the 
characters in L, and the suffix-array. It is obvious that in their full state these structures combined take up more 
space than the original string; however the FM-index typically only include a fraction of the elements in the 
rank and suffix-arrays, referred to as checkpoints. For instance the FM-index might only contain the suffix-array 
for 1/32 rows in the matrix. To find the offset of a substring where the suffix-array has been discarded we must 
cycle through the string, repeatedly stepping one position left in the string using LF-mapping until landing on a 
row in the Matrix that has maintained its suffix array. The offset can then be calculated from the value given in 
the suffix-array for that row and the number of LF-mapping steps taken before reaching the row. 
The FM-index thus consist of checkpoints for the rank and suffix-arrays, which take up only a fraction of the 
disc space of the arrays, the L column which is sorted alphabetically and thus easily compressible, and the F 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
column, the Burrows-Wheeler Transform, which can be compressed significantly more efficiently than the 
original string. All together this makes for an index that takes up significantly less disk space than the original 
string. 
 
The Smith-Waterman algorithm 
Smith-Waterman (SW) is a dynamic programming algorithm that performs local alignment on two strings and is 
guaranteed to find the optimal alignment according to the specified scoring scheme (Smith TF, Waterman MS, 
1981). The scoring scheme will typically consist of a positive value for matches, a penalty for mismatches 
(either a fixed value for any mismatch or specified in a substitution matrix) and a gap-scoring scheme consisting 
of a penalty for gap-openings (insertions/deletions) and gap-extensions. In bwa-mem SW is used to extend the 
alignments identified by searching for MEMs in the FM-index.

= length(a)+1 and n = length(b)+1. The first row and first line in the matrix are filled out with zeros.

In essence each element in the scoring matrix is obtained from one of four paths depending on the alignment 
1. The alignment is extended by one nucleotide from each sequence, meaning a match or a mismatch. 
The score of the element in H is given by the score of the element above and to the right plus a match 
score or mismatch penalty. 
2. The alignment is extended by k nucleotides in string b but not in string a, deletion(s). The score of the 
element is given by the score of the element k spaces left of it plus a gap-penalty. 
3. The alignment is extended by l nucleotides in string a but not in string b, insertion(s). The score of the 
element is given by the score of the element l spaces above it plus a gap-penalty. 
4. All of the above result in a score less than or equal to 0 in which case the element will be given a score 
of 0.

Bwa-mem scoing and match+mismatch fraction 
Once the SW algorithm has found the optimal alignments bwa-mem performs a final evaluation of each 
alignment through its own scoring scheme consisting of a matching score (default 1), mismatch penalty 
(default 4), and gap penalty (default 6+1*k where k is the length of the gap). For each query the single 
alignment with the highest alignment score is given as output, unless no alignment achieves an alignment score 
above a specified threshold (default 30) in which case no output is given (Li H, Durbin R, 2013).  
Bwa outputs the alignment in SAM-format, an example of which can be seen in figure 2. 
 
 
 
Figure 2. Example alignments and the corresponding SAM-format (The SAM/BAM Format Specification Working Group, 2015). 
 
Central in the SAM-file is the CIGAR-string which contains the alignment in its most condensed form. The CIGAR 
string is found in the sixth column of the SAM-file and indicates when certain operations including 
matches/mismatches (M), insertions (I) and deletions are found in the alignment. The CIGAR-string in the file in 
figure 2 indicates that the alignment of r001 to the reference genome starts out with 8 matches/mismatches, 
followed by 2 insertions, followed by 4 matches/mismatches, followed by 1 deletion and finally 3 
matches/mismatches. The CIGAR-string can also contain several other operations such as hard clippings (H) and 
soft clippings (S). Clippings are areas at the end of reads that are cut from the alignment as they could not be 
aligned with the reference sequence, while other parts of the read aligns very well with it. These clippings 
present an issue for MGmapper since bwa-mem will occasionally output alignments where only a small fraction 
of the read actually aligns and the rest is ignored, leading to a large number of false positives. MGmapper 
attempts to resolve this by implementing an additional filter called the match+mismatch fraction, MMF, which 
simply refers to the fraction of a read that is listed as either matches or mismatches (M) in the CIGAR string.

GemSIM 
General Error-Model based Simulator, or GemSIM for short, is a software package that can generate models for 
errors in DNA sequencing, and apply those error models to genomic data in order to emulate sequencing reads 
obtained from NGS technologies. The program uses generic formats commonly used by sequencing 
technologies and tools for NGS analysis. Error models can be created based on a SAM-file containing 
alignments of real reads to a reference genome and reads can be simulated and obtained in fastq format based 
on a specified error model and input fasta file(s). Both single-end and paired-end reads of specified read 
lengths and insert sizes can be simulated. 
When applying the error model each nucleotide in a read is assigned an error rate based on 3 to 4 factors. 
1) The nucleotides position in the read. 
2) Overall insertion and deletion error rates. 
3) The nucleotides context, i.e. the makeup of the 5mer consisting of the three nucleotides preceding it, the 
nucleotide itself and the immediate succeeding nucleotide. 
4) For paired-end reads, whether it is the first or second read in a pair. 
As there are 4^5 = 1024 unique 5mers and 3 possible substitutions for each 5mer (the 4th nucleotide can be 
substituted for the 3 other bases) the error model includes substitution rates for 1024x3 = 3072 substitutions in 
their context. It also contains overall error rates for insertions and deletions and error rates for each position in 
the read. Error models for paired-end reads contain two sets of all this information, one for the first read and 
one for the second read in a pair. 
In addition to the option to generate error models the GemSIM package also includes three premade error 
models, 2 models for paired-end Illumina reads and a model for Roche/454 reads, see table 1 and figure 3. 
Several tendencies are well established when it comes to error rates of NGS-techniques. The 454 method has a 
greater tendency towards insertion and deletion errors and suffers from increased error rates in homopolymer 
regions, but has a lower substitution error rate than the Illumina methods overall and is less dependent on 
read length. The substitution error rates for Illumina reads are also found to be highly context-dependent 
(Abnizova I, et.al., 2012).

Methods 
 
The current version of MGmapper is able to map reads to 20 pre indexed databases of which nine were used in 
some capacity in this study.  Below is a short description of the used databases.  
 
Bacteria. A database of complete bacterial genomes from NCBI. 3017 entries classified at species level. 
 
MetaHitAssembly. Long (average around 21 kbp) DNA sequences from bacteria found in the human gut 
obtained from a European study.  41,126 entries, most classified at genus or species level. 
 
HumanMicrobiome.  Long (average around 63 kbp) DNA sequences from bacteria found in the human 
gut obtained from an American study.  42,928 entries, most classified at genus or species level. 
 
Bacteria_draft. Draft genomes from NCBI. 3,152,971 entries classified at species level. 
 
Human. Human chromosomes and mitochondrion from human genome assembly GRCh38. 25 entries. 
 
Virus. Viruses from NCBI. 5764 entries. 
 
Fungi. Fungi from Ensembl. 29,053 entries. 
 
Plasmid. Extracted from NCBI complete bacteria database. 2091 entries. 
 
Plant. Plant genomes from NCBI. 5,447,342 entries. 
 
The impact of the MMF filter on the mapping of real metagenomics data was studied using sequence data 
obtained from fecal samples from air toilets. The data set consisted of around 169 million reads total, removing 
adapter sequences with cutadapt and discarding reads mapped to the phiX genome left around 146 million 
reads that could be mapped with MGmapper. The remaining reads where mapped in chain mode against 6 
databases, Bacteria, MetaHitAssembly, Human, Virus, Fungi and Plasmid. Mapping was repeated five times 
using five different MMF thresholds, 0.0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6 and 0.8. 
The effect of the MMF filter was analyzed by looking at the number of reads mapped to each database in each 
of the five scenarios and examining taxonomy and classification of the entries that reads had been aligned 
with, and how this changed with increasing MMF-thresholds. 
 
While mapping reads from unknown organisms can give us an idea of how the MMF-filter impacts the output it 
cannot definitively tell us if the MMF-filter is successful at mainly targeting false positives. To more accurately 
measure the impact of the MMF threshold as well as read length on the rate of true and false positive 
predictions, 10 whole-genome sequences were picked out at random from the bacteria database and used to 
generate simulated reads with the program GemSIM. For each of the 10 entries two sets of paired-end reads 
were simulated using the Illumina v5 error-model supplied in the program package, one containing 2x 
1,000,000 reads with a read length of 50 bp and an insert size of 200 bp and one containing 2x 500,000 reads 
with a read length of 100 bp and an insert size of 300 bp. All reads were then mapped with MGmapper against 
the Bacteria database at five different MMF thresholds, 0.0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6 and 0.8. The taxonomy of the entries 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
that each read was aligned to was examined in order to find the exact number of correct and incorrect 
predictions at species and genus levels. 
 
Taxonomy analysis was carried out on two sets of metagenomics data obtained from fecal samples from two 
koalas, Hawkins and Kiewa. Around 22 million reads from the Hawkins sample and 116 million reads from the 
Kiewa sample were mapped separately against 7 databases, Bacteria, MetaHitAssembly, HumanMicrobiome, 
Bacteria_draft, Virus, Fungi and Plant with the application of an MMF threshold of 0.8. Species classification of 
references with 1 or more reads mapped to it was extracted to sum up the number of reads mapped to each 
species in each database. The most commonly found species where examined and compared between the two 
samples to find differences and similarities between the two microbial communities.

Results 
 
Impact of MMF threshold on the mapping of real metagenomics data 
Mapping reads from air toilet-samples in chain mode using different MMF thresholds demonstrated a 
significant impact of MMF filtering. When no MMF filter was applied 75% of reads where mapped to the 
Bacteria database. Increasing the MMF threshold drastically reduced this number to around 36% when MFF = 
0.4 and 28.2% when MMF = 0.8. A significant portion of the filtered reads were instead mapped to the 
MetaHitAssembly database, which accounted for around 12.5% of mapped reads when MMF = 0.0 and 31.5% 
when MMF = 0.4. Most of the filtered reads however remained unmapped, as the share of unmapped reads 
increased from around 11% when no MMF filter was applied to 40% using and MMF threshold of 0.8. Figure 4 
illustrates how the MMF filter impacted the number of reads mapped to each database.

Mapping of GemSIM-simulated reads 
 
Frequency of correct predictions 
The rate of true positives at species level, i.e the percentage of reads that were mapped to the correct species, 
varied greatly between the 10 strains, ranging from around 7% of reads from L. monocytogenes to around 97% 
of reads from A. saccharovans. Reads with a length of 100 bp outperformed the 50 bp reads for all 10 strains, 
however there was a significant difference as to how much better they performed. 50 bp L. pneumophila reads 
had a true positive rate of around 35% while 100 bp reads from the same strain had a true positive rate of 
around 57%. A similar large difference is seen among L. lactis reads while the TPR difference for several other 
strains was less than a percentage point.

Frequency of incorrect predictions 
Looking at the rate of false positives - the percentage of reads mapped to an incorrect species - the 100 bp 
reads again appear to outperform the 50 bp reads in general. For most of the tested strains however the rate 
of false positives is low enough to be considered largely insignificant. Reads from the B. cereus and L. 
pneumophila strains stand out as having a considerably higher rate of false positives due to a larger number of 
reads being mapped to highly similar strains within the same genus, however even for these strains the rate of 
false positives remain below 0.5%.

Impact of MMF threshold 
The MMF threshold was seen to have no impact on mapping of the simulated reads (exact number of correctly 
mapped reads at different MMF thresholds along with identifier of the strains can be found in appendix A), 
even using an MMF threshold of 0.8, an insignificant amount of reads where filtered. As such the MMF 
threshold did not prove effective at filtering out false positives from the set of simulated reads, however the 
number of false positives was already extremely low when using no MMF filtering, and from the mapping of air 
toilet data it has already been established that the MMF threshold does have a significant impact on real data. 
As such the most noteworthy conclusion is that the implementation of an MFF threshold did not filter out any 
significant amount of true positives from the simulated data. Since the strains used as the basis for the 
simulated reads were also found in the Bacteria database there is also the question of whether similar results 
could be expected when mapping reads from a strain that is not present in the database or if the genetic 
diversity within a species would lead to a lower rate of true positives. Several of the mapped strains however 
had a significant portion of reads mapped to other references from the same species, while some of the tested 
strains were the only representation of their species in the database, thus all correctly mapped reads were 
mapped to that reference.

Almost all mapped reads from the two samples were mapped to one of the four bacterial databases; Bacteria, 
MetaHitAssembly, HumanMicrobiome and Bacteria_draft, with less than 0.001% of mapped reads being 
mapped to the remaining three databases; Virus, Fungi and Plant. The distribution of reads mapped to the first 
four databases can be seen in figure 6. 88% of reads from the Hawkins sample and 86% of reads from the 
Kiewa sample remained unmapped.

Discussion 
 
The nature of chain-mode mapping with MGmapper makes minimizing false positives a high priority since false 
positives prevent the incorrectly mapped reads from being tested against subsequent databases, where they 
might be mapped to a correct species. When mapping air toilet reads while applying different MMF thresholds 
it was evident that the MMF filter had a significant impact on the output, filtering a large amount of reads 
mapped to the first database (Bacteria) of which a considerable share found better matches in the second 
database(MetaHitAssembly). While nothing definitive cold be concluded, there were several indications that 
the MMF-filter was at least moderately successful at targeting false positives over true positives, and some 
form of filtering is likely a necessity to prevent a large share of reads from incorrectly mapping to the first 
databases they are mapped against. 
The mapping of GemSIM simulated reads from known species supported the hypothesis that the MMF filter 
does not target true positives to any significant extent. The GemSIM experiment however had several issues, 
most prominently the large differences in the rate of true positives between reads from different strains, which 
is likely anchored in the error-model used to simulate the reads rather than in the properties of MGmapper 
itself. As such it would be beneficial to perform the experiment using actual NGS reads from known bacterial 
strains rather than simulated reads. Another issue was that the species used to create the simulated reads 
were all present in the database, while the main source of false positives when mapping real metagenomics 
reads is likely to be species that are not present in the database. Testing how the mapping of negative results is 
impacted by the MMF-filter thus seems equally important when evaluating its ability to filter false positives. 
Lastly MGmapper showed potential as a basis for taxonomy analysis and for comparing microbial communities 
from different metagenomics samples. Although MGmapper only mapped 12-14% of the reads extracted from 
the samples this number is likely to increase as databases expand and improve. The number is also likely to be 
higher when testing samples from more prominent areas of study such as human health, since environments 
such as the human gut has been studied much more thoroughly than koalas, and the databases reflect this.
