Abstract 
C-reactive protein (CRP) is an important acute-phase protein that is widely used as a sensitive 
indicator of inflammation and infection in the human body. The role of CRP is essential for the immune 
system as its interaction with Phosphocholine residues on pathogens or damaged self-antigens leads 
to the activation of complement cascade. It is interesting to find out how a multivalent Phosphocholine 
display towards CRP affects its bioactivity. Dendrimers are multivalent and have great potential for 
scaffold application because of their structure. This thesis examines the chemical synthesis of 
Phosphocholine-based dendrimer derivatives that mimics pathogens in different degrees. Several 
attempts of synthetic pathways were demonstrated and the most reliable approach was chosen. Lastly, 
the synthesised constructs were immunologically tested with various ELISA setups. The outcome of 
these experiments will give an overview on efficiency of the dendrimers that were applied.

1. Introduction 
1.1 The general concepts of the Immune system 
The immune system is a diversified complex that consists of many chemical and biological processes 
within an organism.  The most important fact about the immune system is its ability to identify self and 
thus recognise non-self-antigens [1]. Humans are constantly in contact with microorganisms, many of 
which cause disease. Despite this environmental exposure, we become ill only rarely and it is of great 
interest to understand the immune system at cellular and molecular levels. There are four categories 
of disease-causing microorganisms (Pathogens): Virus, Bacteria, Fungi, and Parasites (Figure 1) [2][3]. 
Pathogens can rapidly evolve and adapt, and thereby avoid detection and neutralisation by the 
immune system. However, multiple defence mechanisms have also evolved to recognise and neutralise 
pathogens more effectively. 
 
Figure 1: Different types of pathogens, their respective appearance and sizes [1, p.5]. 
Disorders of the immune system can result in several types of autoimmune diseases, inflammatory 
diseases and cancer [4]. Common autoimmune diseases including rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes 
mellitus type 1, multiple sclerosis, systemic lupus erythematosus etc. Host defence and host protection 
are achieved by two interdependent and closely interlinked types of immunity, i.e. the innate and 
adaptive immunity. The immune system protects its host against infection with a complex layered 
defence that increases in specificity. In other words, there are physical barriers preventing pathogens 
from entering the organism. If the pathogen breaks through these barriers and enters the organism, 
the innate immune system arranges an immediate, but non-specific, response. Though, if pathogens 
successfully evade the innate response, the adaptive immune system will be activated by the innate 
response. When receiving the initial response to a specific antigen, the adaptive immune system will 
improve its recognition of the specific pathogen and retain it after it is eliminated. This leads to 
immunological memory which constructs the immune system to act faster and stronger each time this 
specific pathogen is confronted [5][6]. In the following section, a more in-depth description of the innate 
immune system is presented to form an overall picture of the most important mechanisms, molecules 
and their relation to this project.

1.1.1 Innate immune system 
Innate (in-born) immune systems are found in all plants and animals [7]. In the human body, most 
microbial invaders are detected and destroyed within minutes or hours by the first line defence of 
innate immune system. The anatomical barriers of the innate immunity consist of three components, 
namely the physicochemical, the humoral, and the cellular barriers. The physical barriers include the 
skin and mucosae which are components that constantly restore and clean the mucosal surfaces (e.g. 
Cilia which supports the removal of debris and foreign remains from the body). The humoral barrier 
involves the production of antibodies, binding of these antibodies to the pathogen, and thereby 
elimination of the pathogen [1, p. 439]. Finally, the cellular components include different types of white 
blood cells (i.e. Neutrophils, Eosinophils, Mast cells) which have different functions and characteristics 
as well as Natural Killer cells (which belong to the innate lymphoid cell group but provide rapid 
responses to viral-infected cells) that play a major role in adaptive immune response [8][9]. Amongst the 
before mentioned barriers of innate immunity, the humoral immunity is of major significance. It is 
mediated by macromolecules found in extracellular fluids such as secreted antibodies, complement 
proteins, and certain antimicrobial peptides. 
Antibodies are glycoproteins that belong to the immunoglobulin (Ig) family with five different isotypes 
including IgA, IgD, IgE, IgG, and IgM [10]. Structurally, they appear in the monomer (IgD, IgE, IgG), dimer 
(IgA) and pentamer (IgM) shapes [11]. Their main function is neutralising pathogens such as viruses 
and bacteria through recognition and binding of an antigen [12]. The molecular forces that are involved 
in the antibody-antigen interaction are weak and non-covalent such as hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic 
interactions, electrostatic- and van der Waals forces [13][14][15]. This means that the antibody-antigen 
bond is reversible and their affinity towards each other is relative rather than absolute. Furthermore, 
different circumstances influence the antibody-antigen interaction such as temperature, pH, ionic 
strength and steric hindrance [16][1, p.149-151]. 
The complement system is a biochemical cascade that helps with the destruction and clearance of 
pathogens from an organism. It is derived from more than 30 different blood plasma proteins (i.e. 
Serum Albumins, Globulins, Fibrinogen, etc.), 12 of which are directly involved in the complement 
pathways, and are produced mainly by the liver [1, p. 49]. In the absence of infection, these plasma 
proteins circulate in an inactive matter, and in the presence of infection they become activated and 
initiate different pathways leading to the killing of pathogens. There are three different pathways that 
are associated with complement activation, namely the antibody-triggered, the alternative and the 
lectin pathway. Of these three known pathways, two are devoted to innate immunity (the lectin 
pathway and the alternative pathway). When the antibody-triggered pathway was discovered, it

became known as the classical pathway of complement activation [17]. In 1954, the alternative pathway 
was introduced and discussed by Pillemer et al. [18][19]. Twenty-five years later, the mannose-binding 
lectin (MBL) or the lectin pathway was discovered. Kawasaki et al. [20] found the MBL-protein in 1978, 
but its function remained unclear until 1989 when Super et al. [21] explained that part. The following 
paragraphs summarise the overall actions of these three pathways. 
The alternative pathway is activated by changes in the local physicochemical environment that are 
caused by the presence of bacteria, which is the earliest response during infection. The lectin pathway 
is activated by mannose-binding lectin proteins of plasma that recognise and bind carbohydrates on 
the surface of pathogens. In the innate immune response, the classical pathway is initiated by the 
binding of C-reactive protein (CRP) to bacterial surfaces, and in the adaptive immune response by the 
binding of antibodies [22]. This results in the elimination of bacteria in two ways, namely opsonization 
and bacteriolysis. Opsonization is the process where the binding of the antibody and complement 
molecules marks the microbe for ingestion by phagocytes. Bacteriolysis is the process where some 
complement system components assist antibodies in killing the bacterium directly [23].

1.1.2 Acute-phase proteins 
Acute-phase proteins (APP) are produced by hepatocyte cells in the liver [25]. In response to injury, 
local inflammatory cells such as neutrophils and macrophages secrete a number of cytokines (IL-6, IL-
1β, and TNF-α) that lead to the rise of body temperature, causing fever, thus stimulating synthesis of 
APPs by the liver [26]. APPs can be classified as positive and negative proteins whose blood plasma 
concentration increases (positive) and decreases (negative) in response to inflammation. The positive 
APPs include amongst others serum amyloid protein (SAP), C-reactive protein (CRP) and negative 
APPs include albumin, transferrin, retinol-binding protein [27][28]. During inflammatory disorders, 
negative APPs are downregulated by 25% [29], which gives the liver capacity to synthesise more of the 
induced positive APPs. Among APPs, CRP and SAP clearly stand out, because their concentrations rise 
several hundred folds. The effect is so strong and predictable that CRP protein concentration is used 
clinically as a diagnostic test for infection, inflammation, and tissue damage [30]. The following section 
is a general review on CRP, its chemistry, biology, function and its relation to this project. 
1.2 A study on C-reactive protein 
C-reactive protein (CRP) is a member of the pentraxin protein family, because of its structure being 
made of five identical subunits. It is a multipronged pathogen-recognition molecule that binds to 
Phosphocholine (PC) portions of bacterial and fungal cell wall lipopolysaccharides (LPS) [1, p. 120][31]. 
When CRP binds to a bacterium, it not only is able to opsonize the bacterium but can also activate the 
complement cascade by binding to C1q, the first component of the classical pathway of complement 
activation [1, p.121]. This interaction with C1q involves the collagen-like parts of C1q rather than the 
globular heads that make contact with pathogen surfaces, but the same cascade of reactions is 
initiated. The process of signalling, production of APP and their binding to pathogen surfaces is

1.2.1 Historical perspective 
Overall, there are a great amount of studies that focus on major acute-phase proteins as CRP and SAP. 
However, this section will give a chronological view into the history of CRP and thereby the most 
relevant studies that got ground-breaking attention at their respective time. 
In 1930, Tillet et al. discovered CRP from serum patients who were infected with pneumococcus 
bacteria [32]. Being the third component that was isolated, it was formerly named fraction C. It was 
initially thought that CRP might be a pathogenic secretion since its concentration was elevated in a 
variety of diseases, including inﬂammation connected with rheumatic fever, myocarditis or cancer [33]. 
However, later discoveries of hepatic synthesis in 1941 demonstrated that it is a native protein and 
came up with the term CRP to this serum protein [34].  
Different studies between the period of 1940s and 50s confirmed the practical relevance of CRP as a 
biomarker of inflammation. In 1966, Hurliman et al. [25] demonstrated that the liver is the site of 
synthesis of CRP. However, Dong and Wright [35] documented, in 1996, that CRP can be synthesised 
extrahepatic as well. From the mid-70s to mid-80s a lot of studies were carried out to verify the fact 
that CRP can act as an opsonin both directly and also by activating the classical complement pathway 
[36]-[41]. Moreover, S. Maudsley et al. [42] isolated and identified CRP and SAP from bovine serum in 1987. 
Other studies such as Schrive et al. [43] in the middle of 1990s revealed the three-dimensional structure 
of CRP as a pentraxin composed of five identical subunits arranged in pentameric symmetry.  
In 2003, Das et al. [44] reported 16 different pathological glycosylation conditions of human CRP, a 
finding that had never been reported earlier. This opened up a new pathway that was helpful for 
diagnosis and monitoring of different clinical samples. The first crystallisation of CRP was done by M.F. 
McCarty [45] in 2004. Later studies focused more on the biological and practical role of CRP and used 
that knowledge to understand more about the immunological aspects of the human body. For instance, 
Kushner et al. [46] studied the relationship between minor elevations of CRP in humans associated with 
developing diseases in healthy populations. With around 270 references, this study was of major 
importance in 2006 as it provided with statistics to show that small increases in serum concentration 
correlate with an increased risk of diseases. Today, a great amount of studies are still ongoing and a lot 
of new aspects are still to be discovered.

1.2.2 Structure, biology and recent discoveries 
Immunoelectron microscopic studies have shown that CRP is made up of five identical non-covalently 
bond polypeptide subunits, each carrying a calcium-dependent ligand binding site, which are all 
arranged on the same face of the pentamer [47][48]. This means that the CRP molecule has two faces, a 
‘’recognition’’ face exhibiting the five PC-binding sites and an ‘‘effector’’ face containing the C1q-binding 
sites, which is illustrated on the following Figure 5. 
  
Figure 5: CRP-PC complex with Ca2+-ions (yellow) and PC (green).  
CRP has 224 amino acid residues [49]. The molecular weight (MW) of a pentamer ranges between 110-
144 kDa. The subunit MW has been estimated by various methods varying between 20-30 kDa [50]-[52]. 
Monomeric CRP (mCRP) is naturally occurring tissue-based rather than plasma-based molecule [53]. 
Each subunit contains a single intramolecular disulphide bond that stabilises the folded form of the 
protein in several ways. The disulphide bond serves to destabilise the unfolded form of the structure 
by lowering its entropy. Furthermore, the overall stability of CRP is dependent upon temperature, 
protein concentration and pH. For instance, under low-pH conditions, CRP will tend to aggregate. 
One of the most interesting biological characteristics about CRP is that it is able to raise its serum 
concentration during acute-phase response approaching 1000-fold increase within 24-48h, and then 
quickly return to the very low normal concentration of a few µg/mL. Even though elevation of CRP 
levels can also be caused by lifestyle factors (i.e. alcohol [54], smoking[55], cholesterol[56] etc.), it has been 
==PAGE:15==
8 
 
extensively studied whether general plasma levels of CRP or other circulating APPs have predictive 
values in pathogenesis of cardiovascular diseases [57]-[60]. 
In human coronary artery smooth muscle cells (HCASMC), increased inflammatory CRP production 
has been observed. Calabro et al. [61] reported that local production of CRP in vessels may directly 
influence the development of atherosclerosis. In children with high rates of overweight and obesity, 
levels of IL-6, CRP and leptin have all been shown to increase [62]-[65]. Even at an early age, the level of 
CRP and other pro-inflammatory cytokines are elevated in pre-pubertal obese children, which have 
been associated with endothelial dysfunction, therefore potentially contributing to cardiovascular 
risk[66]-[68]. More recent studies reported that elevation in serum CRP levels is linked to an increased 
future risk of schizophrenia [69]. 
Henceforth, the focus will be on the chemistry of dendrimers, their application and relation to this 
project as well as the pathways used in to synthesise dendrimer-based PC for the binding of CRP. 
Thereafter, the use of these synthesised dendrimer-products in immunological assays and the related 
results will be presented. Finally, new ideas and future aspects of the study will be discussed. 
1.3 Aim of the study 
There is limited knowledge about the circumstances that determine whether C-reactive protein (CRP) 
react pro- or anti-inflammatory, though the availability of Phosphocholine (PC) in the damaged cell 
membrane is an important factor. This project demonstrates a brief presentation on CRP, its history 
and background and chemical properties. It is highly relevant to find out whether a multivalent ligand 
can make stronger bond to CRP than a monovalent ligand. If so, it will be interesting to discover how 
ligand multivalency (dendrimer-based) can be used to optimise the measurement of CRP in a sample. 
The project will examine the changes in CRP bioactivity by preparing and using dendrimer-based 
synthetic mimics of cell membranes that are damaged to different degrees. In the synthetic membrane 
imitations, we can accurately control the distance between the PC molecules, their flexibility and 
geometry. Binding properties and activity of CRP by these molecules will be investigated in cell and 
animal models. This will give us a deep insight into the molecular factors that influence CRP's chemical 
and immunological activity and its concentration in the body.

2. Dendrimer chemistry 
2.1 Overview 
Within the past 25 years, hyperbranched polymers have received an increasing amount of attention, 
due to their unique chemical and physical properties. These nanostructured macromolecules are 
potential applicants in different fields such as drug and gene delivery, immunotherapy, catalysis, and 
surface chemistry. Dendrimers are one group of the hyperbranched polymers that are known for their 
structural perfection, symmetry and dispersity (see Figure 6). They are biologically and chemically 
relevant due to their biocompatibility [70], solubility [71], polyvalency [72] and molecular weight [73]. These 
elements are among the most important characteristics making dendrimers suitable for drug delivery 
and targeting applications [74][75]. 
2.2 Structural properties and potential applications 
In addition to the above mentioned properties, dendrimers are also of great importance in practical 
purpose. They have been used as diagnostic reagents for tumour imaging by magnetic resonance 
imaging (MRI) [76]. Several types of contrast agents are dendrimer-based macromolecules that can be 
used for a range of specific imaging purposes [77]. 
 
Figure 6: Different types of polymers including dendrons and dendrimers. 
The first dendrimer-based pharmaceutical that received official approval for clinical testing from the 
American Food & Drug Administration (FDA) has been developed by an Australian company named 
Starpharma in 2008 [78]. VivaGel® (SPL7013 or astodrimer sodium) is an antiviral blocking bacteria for 
prevention of HIV, genital herpes and HPV, and causes neither irritation nor inflammation. Here, the 
polyvalency of the dendrimer is very crucial as it can act as a ‘’molecular adhesive tape’’ that attaches to 
the gp120 glycoprotein receptor on the virus surface preventing the infection. Normally, infection of 
HIV begins by the binding of gp120 protein to CD4-receptors on healthy cells. However, the polyionic 
structure of this drug based on fourth generation poly lysine-dendrimer can disturb this process [79]. 
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
Drug release from polymer-drug conjugates plays an important role in the efficacy of the drug and its 
action on the body. Dendrimers are good candidates in this field as they have a sterically crowded 
surface and many sites of interaction. In a study, Kurtoglu et al. [80] analysed drug release 
characteristics of dendrimer–drug conjugates with different linkers. Ester and amide-bonded 
Poly(amidoamine) dendrimers (PAMAM) in different solutions were examined. The study concluded 
that ester-linked PAMAM dendrimer conjugates were the most suitable drug carriers that could 
provide multiple controlled release of various drugs such as Ibuprofen, Methylprednisolone, and N-
acetyl cysteine [81]-[83]. Other applications of dendrimers include sensor technology, nanoparticles, 
coating, ink and other adhesives. For instance, third generation of poly(propylene imine) dendrimers 
(PPI) has been used in pH-sensor studies [84]. First and second generation of PPI-dendrimers have been 
used in the field of metal cation photo detection (metallodendrimers) [85]. Figure 7 and Figure 8 
illustrate the structural and specific differences between these dendrimers. In this project, PPI-
generations 1-5 and PAMAM-generations 0-4 are used.

2.3 Synthesis and characterisation  
Historically, the first synthesis of dendritic structures was done by Vögtle et al. [86] in 1978 using the 
divergent method known at the time. However, several chemists such as Denkewalter et al. [87][88] in 
1981, Tomalia et al. [89] in 1983 and 1985 [90], and Newkome in 1985 [91] succeeded in synthesising 
dendritic structures using the same divergent method. In 1990 another synthetic approach was 
introduced by Craig Hawker and Jean Fréchet [92] where they presented the convergent dendrimer 
synthesis. Dendrimer chemistry has since then been of major interest for a lot of chemists worldwide 
and the studies based on dendrimer chemistry and biology has been increased significantly [93]. 
 
Figure 9: Divergent and convergent synthesis of dendrimers. 
Characterisation of dendrimers is generally complex. However, based on the symmetry and the size of 
dendrimers, several analytical methods are normally used. Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) is one 
of the most important analytical tools in organic chemistry because of its flexibility and accuracy. NMR 
allows the study of intermolecular interactions as well as interactions with solvent molecules. In 
contrast to other kinds of spectroscopy methods (e.g. IR, UV) which generally give information about 
certain functional groups or molecules, NMR spectroscopy proposes the study of individual atoms, 
thus making it possible to predict the whole molecular structure of any compound. Besides NMR-
spectroscopy, Size-exclusion chromatography (SEC), Raman spectroscopy, X-ray crystallography, 
electrophoresis, fluorescence spectroscopy and chiroptical measuring methods are also attracting 
increasing attention as they make a useful contribution to dendrimer characterization [94]-[98].  
In this project, a combination of different methods was used to give an overall understanding of the 
final dendrimer-based product and its structure. High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) 
was used to obtain the molecular weight of the product. However, it was unclear whether the samples 
would run through the HPLC-column as the dendrimer alternates in size and hydrophilicity. Therefore, 
SEC was used to follow up and also to see whether there is any aggregation or impurity remains. IR 
was used to gain information about functional groups and to check for the reaction completion. Finally, 
when those different analysis techniques provide us with the relevant information, various NMR-
techniques (1H-NMR, 13C-NMR, COSY, HSQC, etc.) were used to predict the whole molecular structure.

3. Results and discussion 
In the following lines, every reaction-step and the theory will be presented. The results, which led to 
the choice of the most suitable procedure, will be discussed. Lastly, the most important analytical 
methods will be examined and the immunological binding assays and their results will be presented. 
3.1 The general procedure 
A great number of nucleophiles are known to undergo conjugate additions (1,4-addition). These 
additions occur only when the C=C double bond is immediately adjacent to the C=O group, which is 
mainly named α,β-unsaturated carbonyl compounds [99]. Generally, C=C double bonds are nucleophilic, 
but in a conjugated system where the double bonds are conjugated with carbonyl groups, they can be 
electrophilic. Conjugate addition includes several types of organic reactions such as Michael addition,  
in which there is a reaction between a nucleophilic Michael donor (i.e. enolate) and an electrophilic 
Michael acceptor with an electron withdrawing group (i.e. nitrile, keto or ester). The reaction is 
thermodynamically controlled. 
When looking at the procedure of the project in Scheme 1, the first step in the synthesis is a Michael 
addition with an acceptor (Methyl 3-mercaptopropionate) and a donor (MPC)1 using basic catalyst in 
Ethanol (EtOH).  The upcoming product (MPC-Methyl ester)2 is then cleaved with Lithium Iodide (LiI) 
for 48 h  to obtain the MPC-Lithium carboxylate3, which is finally coupled with the corresponding 
dendrimer types(PPI or PAMAM) using a coupling reagent (TFFH). In the following sections, every

3.3 The formation of MPC-Lithium carboxylate 
The following reaction is the stereo selective cleavage of the terminal methyl ester group by LiI. There 
are two ester groups and thereby two sites of attack in MPC-Methyl ester from the first reaction. 
Usually, ester cleavages are done via acidic or basic approaches, in which high yield percentages are 
obtained. Acidic approach includes Ester Hydrolysis [101]. Basic approaches include several types of 
hydroxides such as Lithium hydroxide (LiOH), Sodium hydroxide (NaOH), Potassium hydroxide (KOH) 
and TrimethylTin(II) Hydroxide (Me3SnOH) [102]-[106]. Despite their effectivity, these reactions are not 
useful in this case, because of their ability to cleave any ester group in a molecule. 
In a study from 1981, Tamura et al. [107] demonstrated the conversion of esters to carboxylic acids at 
elevated temperatures using lithium iodide. In 2015, Meyhoff et al. [108] confirmed that terminal methyl 
ester groups can be selectively transformed into the corresponding lithium carboxylates in good 
yields. Inspired by these studies, the cleavage was carried out with excesses of LiI (4 eq.) in 
tetrahydrofuran (THF) and refluxed for 48 hours evaporating oﬀ most of the solvent. The remaining of

the mixture was triturated three times in acetone for 30 min. to remove iodide and un-reacted starting 
materials. Product obtained as white crystalline resulted in 89.3% yield from a 200 mg starting 
material quantity. The reaction showed promising results from repeating investigations, yet, up-
scaling cases (3-5 g) have resulted in lower yields (63%) where remaining quantities of starting 
material in the product were still present. This was solved by dissolving the residue in Methanol 
(MeOH) then re-crystallise it dropwise in cold acetone (5 times MeOH volume) and the white 
crystalline will precipitate in a more pure form. It can be argued whether MPC-Methyl ester 
encapsulates LiI or not, and why the reaction behaves differently when up-scaling is done. Three 
important factors affect the reaction outcome; 1) equivalent of LiI used 2) temperature 3) purification. 
Up- and down-regulation of these factors might have an influence on up-scaling reactions, which has to 
be considered for future studies.

3.3.1. Purification step and removal of iodide 
Iodide was not fully removed after the reaction completion. This is caused by the fact that there are 
excesses of LiI (4 eq.) in the reaction mixture that leaves iodide stains in the product, which has to be 
removed. Furthermore, ionic interactions between iodide ions and the positively charged amine part 
of Phosphocholine are also important to take into account. At first, the removal of iodide was carried 
out alternatively by using Sodium thiosulfate pentahydrate (Na2S2O3·5H2O) which was inspired by 
Jessing et al. [109] but the method was not applicable because of the difficulty of removing thiosulphate 
by-product which was not miscible in THF. Thus, acetone was used as it reacts with the iodide stains 
and forms acetyl iodide, illustrated on Scheme 4. This acetone trituration resulted in the removal of 
yellow/red colour. 
Even though acetone trituration was applicable and successful in the removal of the red/orange

compound which can be seen in Figure 12. As explained before, this is caused by ionic interactions

3.4 The Dendrimer-PC coupling 
The next reaction-step is the coupling between MPC-Lithium carboxylate and the free amine residues 
on a dendrimer, i.e. the formation of an amide bond. The initial idea was to modify a PPI-G3 dendrimer 
and if this coupling would work, the method should be applied on other generations and even types of 
dendrimers. Inspired by different works, several coupling methods were tried with different outcomes 
which all will be presented in the following paragraphs. 
The formation of an amide bond (also known as peptide bond) is of great interest in organic chemistry 
as there are still new methods under development to expand this field of research. It is based on a 
nucleophilic substitution reaction, which involves an intermediate including carboxyl activation that is 
essential for the bond formation. This reaction can be done under different conditions. For instance, it 
is well known that amide bond formation can be obtained by converting the carboxylic acid into its 
corresponding acid chloride in the presence of a suitable amine base. This dates back to 1883 where 
two German chemists, Carl Schotten and Eugen Baumann, demonstrated this successful reaction that 
was later named after them as the Schotten–Baumann reaction [110]. 
The amine to amide reaction is done in a two-step manner. Starting with the carboxylic acid, Oxalyl 
chloride ((COCl)2) or Thionyl chloride (SOCl2) is applicable to make the acid chloride which is then 
reacted with the amine compound to obtain an amide bond [111][112]. Based on that, the coupling was 
carried out on a 30mg scale using 4 eq. (COCl)2 to activate the acyl chloride intermediate whereupon 
the dendrimer-amine was added to the solution and mixed for 20h at r.t.. The mixture was precipitated 
dropwise in cold Diethyl Ether (Et2O) removing NMP and other un-reacted starting materials. The 
precipitation was dissolved in Milli-Q water and isolated from the by-product using a SERVA dialysis 
membrane tube. The mechanism for a typical procedure is illustrated on Scheme 5.

Even though the acyl chloride method is a widely used approach, it wasn’t successful in the case of 
coupling with dendrimers, which can be caused by different factors. Oxalyl chloride reacts vigorously 
with water (exothermic reaction), decomposes and produces toxic and corrosive fumes which make it 
difficult to handle as a chemical compound [113]. Therefore, completely dry solvents and reaction 
conditions are of absolute necessity. Dialysis membranes were introduced to isolate the dendrimer 
product from by-products and avoid purification steps. All these steps were done in best possible way 
and the reaction still did not succeed more than 6% yield. Several peaks as is illustrated on the SEC 
chromatogram (Figure 13) shows that there is difference in intensity and placement of both 
unmodified PPI-G3 and acyl chloride reaction peak. Furthermore, Ninhydrin test showed positive 
demonstrating that there are still free amine residues, thus the dendrimer did not get fully derivatised. 
  
Figure 13: SEC of the acyl chloride coupling compared to an unmodified PPI-G3 denrimer 
As there were difficulties with the acyl chloride step, a new coupling pathway was introduced that 
showed promising results. This pathway involves uronium and phosphonium coupling reagents that 
are widely used for amide coupling reactions too. Phosphonium salts such as BOP4 were described in 
1969. Within its introduction in peptide chemistry, it has become a well-known reagent. Among 
disadvantages with BOP is the formation of its by-product, Hexamethylphosphorotriamide (HMPA), 
which has proven to be a human carcinogen and its link in leading to racemization [114][115]. Later 
studies led to the development of the substitute coupling reagent PyBOP5. The advantage of PyBOP is 
that it works similarly as BOP, but without producing toxic by-products [116].

3.6 The binding and activation of CRP by dendrimer-PC products 
When the PPI-PC constructs were successfully synthesised and characterised, they were tested for the 
binding of CRP. Binding assays were done by Kristoffer Gehring and Tobias Hegelund Olsen from the 
group of Professor Peter M. H. Heegaard9 from the National Veterinary Institute at DTU Frederiksberg. 
The students designed experiments to study PC-CRP-C1q complex formation. Solid phase and solution 
phase ELISA-setups were applied to investigate the C1q binding properties of CRP in complex with 
monovalent and multivalent PC-constructs. The results indicated that the binding between C1q and 
CRP was dependent on the binding between CRP and PC which itself depend on the multi numericity 
of the PC construct. Figure 32 below illustrates the C1q binding efficiency of multivalent dendrimer-
PC constructs, and especially PPI-G2-PC and PPI-G3-PC show a strong binding of C1q. Furthermore, 
monovalent PC-constructs based on MPC-Methyl ester showed lower PC-CRP binding.  
Figure 32: Comparison of CRP-PC-C1q binding activity tested on PPI-products measured by Optical Density (OD) 
Accordingly, multivalent PC-constructs that have multiple PC-molecules close to each other results in a 
stronger PC-CRP-C1q complex that possibly leads to the activation of complement cascade. However, 
too many PC-molecules will give a negative effect, as PPI-G4-PC and PPI-G5-PC dendrimers shows 
lower C1q  binding, which might be caused by steric hindrence and crowding. Based on that, PPI-G2-
PC and PPI-G3-PC were the most suitable PC-constructs for the studying PC-CRP-C1q complex.

4. Future aspects 
There are a great amount of future considerations connected to this project. Every reaction step 
should be looked further into to determine whether these steps can be improved or not. In case of 
MPC-Methyl ester reaction, there is not much left as the reaction provides an outcome of 98% yield. In 
the case of methyl ester cleavage reaction, the solvent properties should be studied. It could be 
interesting to examine if the reaction would succeed using less equivalents LiI than already used. 
Dendrimer coupling reactions should be repeated thoroughly and reaction conditions should be 
optimised. Especially the solubility of low generation dendrimers (gen. 1 in both PPI and PAMAM 
dendrimers that resulted in lower yields) should be studied.  It would be interesting to find out 
whether the reaction would succeed using other coupling reagents than TFFH, for instance COMU. 
Having an approach that is successful in derivatising both PPI and PAMAM dendrimers, it should be 
applied to other dendrimer types too such as poly-L-lysine dendrimers. Furthermore, it could still be 
desirable to find out whether the amide formation could be done via acyl chloride intermediate using 
alternative compounds such as thionyl chloride. Even though it did not work using Oxalyl chloride, it 
should be repeated again with more meticulousness because having chloride ions in a sample is less 
toxic than fluoride ions. Thus, the experiments can be done in vivo too. 
A relevant biological and chemical aspect could be trying to synthesise a biotinylated Phosphocholine 
to determine if it competes with the other modified PC dendrimers in the binding for C1q and the 
activation of CRP. However, this is not relevant to the current project, but chemically it is appropriate 
to carry out the synthesis via TFFH. Additionally, PAMAM-PC constructs would likewise be relevant to 
investigate. Despite PAMAM-dendrimers being less crowded that PPI-dendrimers, it would be 
interesting to study whether these constructs also behave in the same way that PPI-constructs did. 
Analytically, it would be optimal to study MALDI-TOF and HPLC-MS to further prove the structure and 
purity of the products.

5. Conclusion 
The project investigated the synthesis of dendrimer-based Phosphocholine (PC) derivatives that were 
developed via traditional solution-phase organic chemistry. A Phosphocholine methyl ester was 
synthesised via traditional Michael addition whereupon the methyl ester group was cleaved using 
lithium iodide. After that, two main strategies were investigated to generate an amide bond between 
PPI-dendrimers and the PC-compound. The first was via an acyl chloride intermediate which was 
challenging and did not succeed. The second strategy was by using coupling reagents such as PyBOP, 
BTFFH and TFFH which showed promising results. PyBOP and BTFFH managed to prepare the amide 
bond but they resulted in low yield percentages and after several purification steps, the by-product 
impurities still appeared in the product. On the other hand, TFFH showed reliable results after various 
repeats as it produced high yields and at the same time, its by-products were easily removed. 
Based on the abovementioned strategies, a general synthetic approach was developed and applied to 
derivatise 5 generations of PPI-dendrimers with the corresponding PC-compound. These products 
were analysed by different analytical tools including Ninhydrin test, size exclusion chromatography, 
HPLC-MS and IR. Additionally, various NMR techniques further proved that the amide coupling was 
successful. Thus, a reliable pathway based on TFFH was designed. Moreover, to examine the pathway 
flexibility, it was used to derivatise 5 generations of PAMAM-dendrimers which showed good results.  
Eventually, the PC-PPI-dendrimer products were tested immunologically for the binding of CRP by 
using different ELISA setups. The tests determined whether a monovalent or a multivalent PC-
construct would have the strongest binding affinity to C1q and thus activate CRP. The outcome of these 
tests concluded that a multivalent PC-dendrimer results in a stronger binding with C1q, because of its 
ability to present multiple PCs closely together. However, too many PCs together give the negative 
effect. Therefore, PC-PPI-G2 and PC-PPI-G3 showed to be the most suitable dendrimer products, and 
PC-PPI-G1, PC-PPI-G4 and PC-PPI-G5 resulted in lower binding affinity. 
In general, successful couplings were obtained, a reliable and flexible chemical approach was designed 
and the products were purified as well as being analysed by different methods. Finally, immunological 
binding assays contributed with new knowledge for a better understanding about changes in CRP 
bioactivity.

6. Experimentals 
6.1 Materials and Methods 
Commercially available starting materials such as 2-Methacryloyloxyethyl phosphorylcholine, Methyl 
3-mercaptopropionate, Lithium Iodide, Oxalyl chloride, PyBOP, BTFFH and TFFH were purchased from 
Sigma-Aldrich (Copenhagen, Denmark). PPI-dendrimers were purchased from SyMO-Chem BV 
(Eindhoven, The Netherlands), synthesised from a 1,4-diaminobutane (DAB) core and provided in 
generations 1-5. PAMAM-dendrimers were also purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, synthesised from an 
ethylenediamine core and provided in generations 0-4. Solvents were purchased from Fischer 
Scientiﬁc with HPLC grade purity. 
6.2 Characterisation tools 
NMR spectra were acquired on dendrimer samples without stable isotope enrichment (natural 
isotopic abundance), dissolved in 600 µl d6-DMSO (Sigma-Aldrich) and transferred to 5 mm NMR 
sample tubes. NMR spectra were acquired on 800 MHz Bruker Avance III HD spectrometer equipped 
with a TCI CryoProbe. DQF-COSY, TOCSY, NOESY, 1H-13C HMBC, 1H-13C standard and edited HSQC, 
1H-15N HSQC as well as 1H-13C HSQC-TOCSY and 1H-13C HSQC-NOESY spectra were employed for 
spectral assignment and compound characterization. NOESY and 1H-13C HSQC-NOESY spectra were 
acquired using mixing times of 150 milliseconds. 1H-31P HSQC spectra were acquired on a 600 MHz 
Bruker Avance III spectrometer  equipped with a BBO Smart probe by recording 1024(1H)x128(31P) 
complex data points, sampling 131 milliseconds in the 1H dimension and 18 milliseconds in the 31P 
dimension, respectively. One-dimensional 31P spectra were recorded by sampling 32k complex data 
points during an acquisition time of 340 milliseconds. A sweep width of 395 ppm was employed to 
observe residual PF6- impurities at a chemical shift of -144 ppm. Overall, a reporter-group concept was 
applied to resolve amide bond formation upon Phosphocholine attachment to the dendrimers and 
intact bonds (for instance phosphodiesters) in the Phosphocholine upon coupling. All spectra were 
processed with extensive zero filling in all dimensions and analysed using Bruker Topspin 3.5 pl5. 
HPLC-MS analysis was carried out using a Shimadzu Nexera X2 with a Bruker MicrOTOF-Q III, using a 
Phenomenex Kinetex C8 column (2.6µ, 100 ˚A, 50×2.1mm) and a 1mL/min. linear gradient using 0-2.7 
min. (0-20% buﬀer B), 2.7-4.5 min. (20-100% buﬀer B), 6-7 min. (100-0% buﬀer B), stop time 10 min. 
Buﬀer A: 0.025% TFA in 10% aqueous MeCN. Buﬀer B: 0.025% TFA in 90% aqueous MeCN.
