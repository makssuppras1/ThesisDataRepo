Introduction
1.1 Motivation
The information age took us from slim text eMails all the way to heavy online video
applications. The exponential growth of traﬃc demand has been met by the continuous
growth of technology, especially, optical communication systems. They are responsible for
the information exchange over long distances, for instance the transatlantic distance. Due
to the still growing demand of capacity, further progress in the technological evolution is
necessary [1].
In optical ﬁbers information is carried on waves. Linear and nonlinear optical distortion
eﬀects, occurring during transmission, limit the amount of carried information. Nonlin-
earities are of main concern in today’s optical communication systems. Higher signal
strengths are required for higher data-rates, but these provoke the nonlinearities. The
optical communication system is then prohibited introducing high order modulation for-
mats, since these require enhanced signal strengths. For this reason, the compensation of
nonlinearities is of signiﬁcant concern for further capacity growth [2].
We can distinguish between linear and nonlinear optical eﬀects. There are methods to
compensate for linear eﬀects, such as chromatic dispersion (CD) and polarization mode
dispersion (PMD). Nonlinear optical eﬀects are again distinguishable between intra- and
interchannel eﬀects. Self-phase modulation (SPM) is a intrachannel and cross-phase mod-
ulation (XPM), cross-polarization modulation (XPolM) and four-wave mixing (FWM) are
the interchannel eﬀects [3, 4]. Since linear eﬀects are well compensated for, the nonlinear
eﬀects remain as limiting factor.
In this work we focus on XPM and XPolM. In wavelength division multiplexing (WDM)
systems using polarization division multiplexing (PDM), the transmission performance is
limited by XPM induced cross-talk and XPolM induced phase-noise. Coherent detection
permits the use of digital signal processing (DSP) algorithms, which is the essence of this
work, the mitigation of optical nonlinearities using DSP algorithms.
This work builds on the ﬁber model proposed by Tao et. al. [5], describing XPolM and
XPM statistically with their autocorrelation function. Mitra and Stark [6] show that XPM
and XPolM are describable by multiplicative parameters in a linear channel. Together with

We ﬁrst consider a simpler channel model with only XPolM induced cross-talk, which
is modeled by ﬁrst-order as well as second-order autoregressive (AR) processes. With
the ﬁlters’ estimates, the nonlinear eﬀects are mitigated and the detection of the sent
information improved. It is shown that, with known channel parameters, the ﬁlters are
capable of tracking XPolM induced nonlinearities. Furthermore, we can demonstrate that
ﬁrst-order as well as second-order processes are trackable and thus mitigated. The DP-16-
QAM system performs worse than the DP-QPSK system. Layec et. al [7] propose a similar
approach. They use another DSP algorithm and obtain comparable results. Unlike Layec
et. al [7], we extend the model with XPM induced phase-noise. In this extended model,
we only consider ﬁrst-order AR processes. The aim is to investigate the inﬂuence of both
cross-talk and phase-noise on the transmission. The results show that the nonlinearities
can be mitigated to a certain extent. But with increasing intensities of the nonlinearities
the combination of the nonlinearities becomes rapidly untrackable. As before the DP-
QPSK system performs better than the DP-16-QAM system.
1.2 Related Work
The DSP algorithms have received increasing attention in the mitigation of nonlinear
eﬀects in optical communication systems. Most notably, digital back propagation (BP),
which permits to compensate linear and nonlinear eﬀects at the same time. BP solves
the nonlinear Schr¨oderinger equations numerically at the receiver end using the split
step Fourier method (SSFM) for every ﬁber span. With increasing step size the solution
becomes more accurate but also computational more expensive. Increasing the sampling
rate also improves the solution. These considerable drawbacks and the large number of
ﬁber spans make a BP implementation unfeasible in real-time applications [8]. However,
Li et. al. [9] propose a method reducing the necessary steps of the SSFM by 1/4. In [10],
low nonlinear eﬀects are further compensated for by shifting the phase of the received
signal corresponding to the amplitude variations of the signal. This works, due to the
fact, that the nonlinear phase variation is correlated with the amplitude variation.

In contrast to the post-compensation methods at the receiver end, Tao et. al. [11] propose
a pre-compensation method by determining the distortion beforehand. Consequently the
controlled distortion is subtracted from the signal, which leads to a clean signal after

This work chooses the approach of estimating the nonlinearities at the receiver end using

This work includes both eﬀects and proposes a joint compensation.
1.3 Outline
This work is structured as follows: Chapter 2, 3 and 4 describe the required theory related
to optical communication systems, DSP algorithms and stochastic processes, respectively.
In chapter 5, this theory is then applied and adapted to simulate the mitigation approach.
In the same chapter, we depict the results and discuss them. This work is concluded in
chapter 6 with a short outlook going beyond the scope of this work.

Fiber Optic Communication System
Optical communication systems were a major factor in the rise of the information age in
the 1970s. Every day electromagnetic waves carry a huge amount of information over long
distances. Wired lightwave systems outperform microwave systems due to their low loss
during transmission and higher capacity [13]. Thus, they are used to send information
through optical ﬁbers over long distances, e.g. the transatlantic distance at around 5330
km. Nowadays demands on these transmission links are increasing. Higher data rates and
more capacity is required.
In the past, mathematical methods, such as modulation or multiplexing operations,
have been invented to meet these demands. Despite physical limitations, they have been
adapted and improved. The importance of understanding these mathematical concepts –
and how they are limited by physics – is evident. In recent lightwave technology systems,
optical nonlinearities emerge due to the optical Kerr eﬀect, which poses a limitation to
the signal power used. Why higher signal power is necessary is described in section 2.1,
along with the concept of modulation. The ultimate goal is to mitigate or even compen-
sate the nonlinearities and then overcome the limitation on the signal power. Section 2.2
explains the optical noise ﬁelds distorting the ﬁber optical transmission. Section 2.3 and
2.4 both explain a multiplexing method to increase capacity. Section 2.5 covers the optical
nonlinearities where as section 2.6 explains why the nonlinearities emerge and analyzes
them statistically.
2.1 Digital Modulation Methods
Telecommunication uses electromagnetic waves to send information over long distances.
The actual input signal carrying the information is modulated onto a carrier wave of higher
frequency. Digital modulation methods, like QAM and 16-QAM, are formats combining
amplitude and phase modulation. Two orthogonal carriers are used with the same carrier
frequency. In this way, the combination of both carriers hold both amplitude and phase
of the signal.

2.2 Ampliﬁed Spontaneous Emission (ASE)
Over long distances (> 1000 km) signal qualities are limited by ﬁber losses. Ampliﬁcation
is required in order to repeat the signal frequently for its next destination. Unfortunately,
the ampliﬁcation produce spontaneous emissions. They disturb the signal in the optical
domain [15]. On the other hand they are capable of simultaneously amplifying all WDM
channels. In this way their integration is cheaper and less complex than optoelectronic
ampliﬁers. The optical ampliﬁers are characterized by their SNR degradation which is
forced upon the system.
Ampliﬁed spontaneous emissions have a constant/white spectrum density in a single
WDM channel’s bandwidth. Thus, mathematically they are modelled by AWGN, section
4.1. Further, Gordon et al. have shown that ASE has statistical properties as additive
Gaussian noise [2, 16] and also holds the same autocorrelation [2, 17].
As mentioned above, optical ampliﬁers are characterized by their SNR degradation. The
eﬃciency of an optical ampliﬁer is given by the noise ﬁgure Fn [15]:

2.3 Polarization Division Multiplexing (PDM)
The digital QAM use the orthogonal property of two waves in order to send information.
In a similar way, polarizations of light are used to double the symbol rate in an optical
communication system [4]. Polarized light can oscillate with one orientation. Unpolarized
light is able to oscillate with several orientations combined. Polarizing light with two
orthogonal orientations enables the optical system to send two waves at the same time, one
on each polarization. This approach is called polarization division multiplexing (PDM).
In return PDM also leads to a nonlinear optical eﬀect where one polarization interferes
with the other. QPSK and 16-QAM using PDM are called DP-QPSK and DP-16-QAM,
respectively.
2.4 Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM)
Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) is a multichannel optical transmission system
[18]. It is the optical equivalent to frequency division multiplexing in radio wave systems.
Both use several carrier frequencies – in optics carrier wavelengths – to transmits more
data simultaneously. Several transmitter outputs are multiplexed with each other, each of
them modulated onto their own carrier wavelength. Subsequently, all these channels com-
ponents are transmitted through the same optical ﬁber. At the receiver end the channels
are demultiplexed and passed to their respective receiver. The more channels used, the
higher data rates are achieved. Thus the maximum capacity depends on how many chan-
nels ﬁt next to each other in the available bandwidth. More channels results in a higher
spectral eﬃciency. WDM is typically employed with 80 channels and 50 GHz spacing
between them [4].
2.5 Linear Eﬀects in Optical Communication
2.5.1 Chromatic Dispersion
Light propagating through a optical medium, consists of several waves of diﬀerent wave-
lengths. Chromatic dispersion (CD) is a property of the individual waves, which de-
scribes the dependency of the group velocity on the wavelengths. Hence, waves with
diﬀerent wavelengths in the same wavepacket travel at diﬀerent velocities. In this way the
wavepacket disperses [19].

the pulse broadening is describable by the propagation constant β (and its derivatives)

2.6 Nonlinear Eﬀects in Optical Communication
Nonlinear inter-channel eﬀects, like XPolM and XPM, as well as the nonlinear intra-
channel eﬀect SPM are consequences of the optical Kerr eﬀect. It describes a shift of the
refractive index in a medium when exposed to an electrical ﬁeld. As given by equation
2.11 the Kerr eﬀect describes the power intensity dependency of the refractive index n
[18, 29]:
n = n0 + ¯n2 (P/Aeff) ,
(2.11)
where n0 is the refractive index, P is the optical power, Aeff is the eﬀective mode area and
¯n2 is called the nonlinear-index coeﬃcient, which describes too what extent the refractive
index increases along with the intensity of the optical ﬁeld.
Although this nonlinear eﬀect is very small it is still a major limiting factor in lightwave
systems due to the long ﬁber lengths and high power intensities.

Digital Signal Processing Methods
Digital signal processing (DSP) is used in telecommunication to transmit information,
process digital signals obtained from sensors and transform these signals. DSP is mathe-
matics put into algorithms such that computers can process signals [32]. Often DSP has
to separate or superimposed signals, e.g. the diﬀerent channels in WDM. Also QAM is
DSP, where the signal is pre-processed in order to sent it on radio or optical waves [33].
In this work DSP is used to reduce noise in a signal carrying information. Such algorithms
are called ﬁlters and are widely used in telecommunication.
3.1 Signal, Noise and Filters
Signals, data or time series are handled by digital computers and micro-controllers, there-
fore discrete signals are preferred and are only considered here. A ﬁlter is applied to a
signal in order to extract the relevant or required data it may contain. The signal is often
impaired by noise due to measurements from inaccurate sensors or physical eﬀects. These
eﬀects are described mathematically which enables better ﬁltering. If the ﬁlter takes the
physics of the system and bad measurements into consideration it becomes more ﬂexi-
ble and robust. The systems are described in the so called state-space models which are
used throughout this work. Especially, the Kalman ﬁlter but also the particle ﬁlter are
essentially based on the state-space model concept.
A ﬁlter is linear if it acts as a linear function when applied to a signal. For non-linear ﬁlters
this is not the case. For these cases the system is often linearized at the current time-step,
e.g. extended Kalman ﬁlter. This linearisation works thanks to the mathematical model
characterizing the system and signal. Often the signal is a superposition of true signal
and one or more noise processes. Successful ﬁlters (e.g. Wiener ﬁlter or Kalman ﬁlter)
use statistical properties of the noise and/or the signal to separate them, but are often
just capable of mitigating the unwanted noise. Often a noise signal is characterized by a
Gaussian distribution, since the noise signal is not known.
As mentioned earlier the measurements of noise and signals are all discrete. Thus the
data, signal or time series is always a sequence of samples. The same holds for noisy
signals or measurements of pure noise. Since these sequences are largely random, they are

characterized by statistical properties, such as their mean, variance or auto-correlation.
They are stochastic processes, a family of random variables. [34]

state. The state-space model along with the above mentioned ﬁlters can be derived from
a Bayesian viewpoint, where all information available is used to derive an understanding
of the state. [35, 36]

3.7 Summary
This chapter described the general DSP algorithms used in this work. In chapter 5 the
algorithms are adjusted to the optical communication system. Then, their performance
in estimating the nonlinear eﬀects is simulated and compared. In this way, it is possible
to mitigate the nonlinearities, with the ultimate goal to increase the signal power and
thus enhance the data-rate.
The execution speed of a particle ﬁlter is dependent on the number of particles used. Since,
often a high number of particles are necessary for excellent performance, the particle ﬁlter
is slower than the Kalman ﬁlter. It is unlikely that a slow particle ﬁlter is able to perform
in a real time telecommunication system. Thus, in this work the particle ﬁlter is applied
for the assessment of the EKF.

Emulation of Stochastic Processes
All the results in this work are solely obtained by simulations. In order to run simulation
all important parts of the communication system have to be emulated. In this work we
focus on the mitigation of nonlinear ﬁber eﬀects, XPM and XPolM. Since these two eﬀects
hold a certain randomness, they are emulated with stochastic processes. The paragon of
randomness in telecommunication is additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN). It is the
accumulated eﬀects of unforeseeable natural distortions added to the signal. In optical
communication systems ASEs, described in chapter 2.2, are responsible for AWGN [2].
The concept of random variables (RV) and collections of random variables, stochastic
processes, make it possible to imitate a naturally given random process by a determin-
istic digital computer. RVs follow a probability density function (PDF). It describes the
probability or likelihood of the RV to become a certain value. The Gaussian or normal
distribution is a very common PDF. A family of RVs and their progression over time
is called a stochastic process. A very important property of stochastic processes’ is the
auto-correlation function. If the stochastic process is interpreted as a discrete signal x(t)
then the auto-correlation function Rx(τ) determines the similarity of the signal x(t) with
a shifted version of itself x(t −τ). x(t) is identical to x(t −0) thus Rx(τ = 0) always
holds the peak value of the auto-correlation function [14]. A auto-correlation function
of a signal that holds values around zero τ = 0 suggests that successive observations of
that signal are correlated to each other. In this chapter, we use the very basic AWGN
process to create so-called autoregressive processes, section 4.1 and 4.2. The Yule Walker
equations enable us to create such a autoregressive process with certain characteristics –
with a certain autocorrelation function [34]. The autocorrelation functions of the random
processes we are trying to emulate, are given by the measurements from Tao et. al. [5],
section 2.6.4. In this way we emulate the nonlinear eﬀects of XPM and XPolM, section
4.3.

4.3.1 Emulation of XPolM induced cross-talk
XPolM induced cross-talk is solely occurring in optical communication links and comes
along with XPM. As described in section 2.6 it is a consequence of the optical Kerr eﬀect.
With the Yule Walker equations we obtain a process generator, emulating the nonlinear
eﬀect of XPolM induced cross-talk. A time-series of this generator is then used as the
XPolM parameters wxy(t) and wyz(t) of the Jones transition matrix W(t), described in
equation 2.21. The indexes and time dependence of wxy(t) and wyz(t) are now omitted
on behalf of compactness.
In ﬁgure 2.6(a) of chapter 2.6.4, Tao et. al. [5] show the autocorrelation function Rw(τ) of
such a process. We use a exponential decay eR ew(τ) = A · e−ατTs as approximation of this
autocorrelation function, where Ts is the symbol period. This is required to populate the
matrix of Yule Walker’s method with values. Such a exponential decay is fully determined
by two points, which are chosen from Rw(τ). We choose the ﬁrst point to be Rw(0) and
the second point NHW is where Rw(τ) is half way decayed, Rw(NHW = 15). This is very
convenient, since for diﬀerent ﬁber distances the autocorrelation function Rw(τ) changes
in magnitude, and thus in variance, but the characteristic time until it is half decayed
stays roughly the same.
The AR process producing the times-series ewk,xy/yx is given by:
ewk = −a ew · ewk−1 + qk,
(4.16)
where ewk ∈C has only one coeﬃcient a ew and its AWGN part qk ∼N(0, σ2
q) ∈C.
As mentioned above, for diﬀerent ﬁber distances the autocorrelation function Rw(τ) only
changes in variance. In the autoregressive process this variance is described by the variance
of its AWGN part σ2
q. Finally, we have a process emulating the XPolM induced cross-talk
describing the cross-talk parameters wxy(t) and wyz(t) in the ﬁber model of Tao et. al.
[5].

as the AWGN process in ﬁgure 4.1.

4.3.2 Emulation of XPM induced phase-noise
As XPolM, XPM induced phase-noise occurs in optical communication links, due to the
Kerr eﬀect. Especially in WDM systems with multiple carriers at diﬀerent wavelengths,
one carrier interacts with the phase of another carrier.
Just as XPolM induced crosstalk, Tao et al. [5] proposed a new ﬁber model able to
describe and determine the XPM induced phase-shift eﬀects. Analog to the previous
section 4.3.1, the Yule Walker equation will generate a autoregressive process with the
same autocorrelation function as the one determined by Tao et al. [5], see ﬁgure 2.7(a).
This time the process generator emulates the nonlinear eﬀect of XPM induced phase
noise. A time-series of this generator is then used as the XPM parameters φx(t) and φy(t)
of the Jones transition matrix W(t) described in equation 2.21. The indexes and time
dependence of φx(t) and φy(t) are now omitted on behalf of compactness.

System Simulations
In this chapter we specify the ﬁber model and then run simulations. The goal is to ascer-
tain whether the optical nonlinear eﬀects are trackable by a EKF or a particle ﬁlter and
thus mitigateable. In order to focus on the non-linear eﬀects in optical ﬁber, assumption
are made to simplify the system. We concentrate on the mitigation of ﬁrst XPolM only
and then XPolM and XPM together. For this purpose two diﬀerent state-space models
are used. We call the model which only considers XPolM ’simple model’ and the second
model which considers both XPolM and XPM ’extended model’. The performance of both
models is simulated using DP-QPSK and DP-16-QAM. The simulations are benchmarked
for diﬀerent SNR and diﬀerent variance of the stochastic processes emulating the XPM
and XPolM induced eﬀects. In this way, we determine how much these eﬀects penalize
the system and how much they can be compensated.
Finally, we also simulate the mitigation of second order AR processes for QPSK within
the simple model.
5.1 Model
5.1.1 Simulation Parameters
The ﬁber model is applied to either 112 Gb/s DP-QPSK or 224 Gb/s DP-16-QAM both
using WDM. Only one channel of the WDM system is simulated, but with nonlinear
cross interference from the other channels according to the setup of Tao et. al. [5]. In the
observed channel the symbol period Ts gives the sample frequency and at the same time
the symbol rate Rs = 1/Ts = 28GHz, thus with two polarization components and two
bits per symbol in QPSK we obtain a data-rate of 112 Gb/s. The data-rate is doubled for
DP-16-QAM since four bits per symbol are transmitted.
Linear distortions, see section 2.5, are assumed to be compensated. Further, SPM and
FWM are neglected. The XPM induced phase-shift and the XPolM induced polarization
cross-talk are included according to the ﬁber model discussed in section 2.6.4. They are
emulated as described in chapter 4.

5.1.2 Transmitter Modeling
No pulse shaping or modulation is performed in the transmitter. Since the proposed ﬁlters
only require the symbols, nothing beyond the symbol level is generated. The transmitter
randomly generates a multinomial distributed time-series of N = 2·105 symbols from the
used QAM at each OSNR step. Hence, the average power of the signal PS per polarization
is calculated by averaging over the 4 or 16 symbols. The simple and the extended model
use the same transmitter.

wxy[k] and wyx[k], the phase noise parameters φx[k] and φy[k] are omitted. In this case
the channel model includes parts traditionally considered part of the receiver – analog
front-end and standard DSP chain

5.1.4 Receiver Modelling
The receiver includes the DSP algorithms which are supposed to compensate the optical
nonlinear eﬀects. For this reason the receiver has to distinguish between the simple and
extended model as well. Especially because the state-space models’ measurement model is
highly dependent on the channel model in section 5.1.3. After the receiver has mitigated
the nonlinearity, it decides on a ﬁnal symbol and then counts the symbol errors.
5.1.4.1 State-Space Model Parameters
In this section we deﬁne the parameters of the state-space model, see section 3.2. The
unknown state of the state-space model is a vector consisting of the the nonlinear cross-
talk and phase noise parameters. If the state is accurately estimated by the ﬁlters, it
enables the mitigation of the nonlinear eﬀects. The general state-space model is given by
the following two equations:
xk = A · xk−1 + qk−1,

5.1.4.4 Genie Filter
Besides the two receiver with ﬁlters, we also use receivers without any compensation and
full compensation. The full compensation uses an unfeasible ideal ﬁlter, ’genie ﬁlter’. This
ﬁlter uses the nonlinear parameters generated by the AR processes and thus represents
the best possible solution in order to benchmark the performance of the feasible ﬁlters. As
mentioned earlier, the AR processes are a composition of a dynamic part and a AWGN
part. The dynamic part describes the recent states’ dependence of the previous state. The
AWGN part introduces the stochastic ﬂavor to the AR process. Since consecutive AWGN
samples are fully independent, there is no ﬁlter existing, which is capable of predicting
this part. For this reason the genie ﬁlter adds AWGN to the nonlinear parameters of the
AR process. In theory our genie ﬁlter is called a Rao-Blackwell estimator [34]. In the SER
to OSNR ﬁgures in the results sections, the performance of the genie ﬁlter is called ’lower
bound’.

5.2.5 Second Order Stochastic Processes
In section 4.3 we assume that the autocorrelation functions from Tao et. al [5] are ap-
proximately described by exponential decays. This ultimately leads to a AR process with
only one coeﬃcient, equation 4.16. In this section we assume two coeﬃcients. This means
that the AR process is of order 2. Since we cannot force the Yule Walker equations to
determine more than one coeﬃcient, we chose the two coeﬃcients such that the autocor-
relation function of the resulting AR process looks similar to the one proposed by Tao et.
al [5]. This section is a proof of concept rather than a real simulation, since we cannot
relate the AR process with two coeﬃcients to any ﬁber length. For this reason we only
consider the simple model within a DP-QPSK system.
A second order AR process is given by:
u(n) = −a1 · u(n −1) −a2 · u(n −2) + v(n),
(5.18)

k = 1, 2 being the coeﬃcients.

5.3. Discussion

5.3 Discussion
In this chapter the results are discussed. First, in general regarding the capability of
mitigation. Then compared to related work. Further, we will compare the performance
of the models and modulation formats, and ﬁnally a comparison of the ﬁlters used and
their parameterisation.
Emulation and Mitigation of the Optical Nonlinear Eﬀects
The AR process consists of its recursive and independent noise part. The state-space
model exactly describes the recursive part due to the known channel parameters. In
this sense, the EKF and particle ﬁlter are estimating the nonlinearities based on this
recursion and the measurement. It makes sense that the ﬁlters are capable of mitigating
these eﬀects. With increasing variance the AR process’ independent noise part prevails
and thus the ﬁlters have more diﬃculties of estimating the cross-talk and phase-noise
parameters. The results obtained support this argument.
Comparison to Related Work
The work of Tao et. al [5] suggests that the nonlinearities are trackable and thus mitigable.
Further, Li et. al. [12] and Layec et. al. [7] each implemented a method of compensation
for the XPolM induced cross-talk successfully. We approach this problem with the EKF
and the particle ﬁlter. Due to the knowledge of the channel parameters, the successful
outcome is no surprise, but again the extent of the mitigation is of importance. The results
show, that the used ﬁlters certainly perform better than the canceller of Li et. al. [12]. The
results of Layec et. al. [7] are very similar to ours, although in our method the channel
estimation is given. Especially, ﬁgure 5.2 looks very alike to its counterpart in Layec
et. al. [7]. A one-to-one comparison will become feasible after extending the model as
described in section 6.2. However, the concurrent mitigation of XPolM and XPM induced
nonlinearities is so far only comparable to the exhaustive digital BP method. In that
sense our approach promises a more eﬃcient solution.
Comparison of the Models and Modulation Schemes
The results show that the two ﬁlters are capable of tracking the XPolM induced cross-
talk in a DP-QPSK and DP-16-QAM system. Further, also XPM induced phase-noise
is trackable in both modulation formats. With increasing process variance both models
perform worse. Especially, the XPM induced phase-noise becomes rapidly untrackable
with increasing process variance, see ﬁgure 5.6 and 5.8. Whereas the XPolM induced
cross-talk is easier trackable, see ﬁgure 5.7 and 5.9. As expected QPSK outperforms 16-
QAM, in the simple model as well as in the extended model. Due to the lower phase
distance between symbols, the 16-QAM system becomes very sensitive to phase-noise.
For this reason we reran simulations within the extended model using DP-16-QAM at a
lower variance range of the phase-noise.
==PAGE:64==
60
Chapter 5. System Simulations
The results in section 5.2.5 show that also a second order AR process is trackable by the
two ﬁlters. Thus, by extending the state-space model, AR processes with any order are
implementable. This is important, since the ﬁrst order AR processes are obtained due
the assumption, that the autocorrelation functions used are approximately exponential
decays.
Filter Comparison and Parameterisation
The performance of the two ﬁlters is similar. The particle ﬁlter performs slightly better
in most cases, but then again the EKF performs better in some cases with low process
variances. Due to the high computational cost of the particle ﬁlter, it is not feasible
in a real-time application. Their performance is more dependent on the used model,
modulation scheme and channel parameters. As mentioned above, the two ﬁlters have
all information about the AR process’ behavior. For that reason the results we obtained
are under the assumption that the channel parameters are known. The next goal, section
6.2, is to determine the variance of how accurately our approach is capable of estimating
the channel parameters. It may even be possible that one of the two ﬁlters leads to
a better channel estimation. After the ﬁrst successful simulations we obtained unusual
results for the EKF at high SER and high OSNR conditions, e.g. see ﬁgure A.1. This
bad performance of the EKF is due to the chosen variance for zk. For high OSNR the
variance of the parameter zk becomes very low, because we choose it according to the
OSNR. We ﬁnd ourselves in the second case described in section 3.4. Thus, the EKF is
very sure about the measurement and neglects the prediction. The goal of the Kalman
ﬁlter is to solve the Bayesian ﬁlter equations, see section 3.3, and calculate the posterior
distribution p(xk|y1:k) of the state given all measurements. In this case, by neglecting
the prediction the EKF neglects all past measurements y1:k−1. We reran the simulations
with the variances of the parameter zk ﬁxed at high values and obtained improved results.
The new EKF with ﬁxed variance (FV) is annotated as EKFFV. The ﬁxed parameter
leads to less ﬂexibility but also more stability. It can be seen as a regularization. The
EKFFV improves the unusual results of the EKF, but then again performs worse where
more ﬂexibility is necessary. This shows how important the channel parameters are.

6
Conclusion and Prospects
6.1 Conclusion
The initial question of this work is, whether it is possible to compensate for the optical
nonlinearities with the proposed DSP algorithms. Since, if successful, this ultimately leads
to the implementation of higher order modulation formats and thus higher data-rates.
This is important to meet future traﬃc demands and increase the capacity in optical
communication systems.
The follow up question, to whether compensation is possible, is, to what extend are these
eﬀects compensatable. AR processes are used to emulate the nonlinearities in the optical
ﬁber channel. The proposed ﬁlters receive all the information about the channel, meaning
the dynamic behavior of the AR processes is used in the ﬁltering process. Thus, in theory,
if the variance of the AR processes is not too high, the ﬁlters are supposed to be able to
track them. This is also shown by the simulation results.
The new receiver side method of this work compensates nonlinear eﬀects in optical com-
munication systems. Solely XPolM induced cross-talk or along with XPM induced phase-
noise is/are mitigated successfully in either DP-QPSK or DP-16-QAM systems. This
compensation is necessary, since the nonlinearities prevent the implementation of sys-
tems with higher order QAM. Tao et. al. [5] ﬁndings suggest the possibility of mitigation,
due to their ﬁber model and aﬃrming experiments. With the ﬁber model, the nonlineari-
ties, namely the cross-talk and phase-noise, are emulated with AR processes of order one
or two and distort the signal in the channel. For this reason, we applied adapted versions
of the EKF as well as the particle ﬁlter on the nonlinearities. The methods estimate
the distortion and thereby improve the symbol detection. The more power the distortion
processes hold, the worse the performance obtained by the ﬁlters. All AR processes are
made out of two parts. A fully independent and a part dependent to the past. Thus the
results make sense, since the process power implies how much the independent process
part contributes to the overall process. Thus, if the AR process holds more independent
shares, the ﬁlters have more trouble tracking the dependent part. Further, at equal trans-
mission distance and its corresponding variance, the phase-noise causes worse distortions
than the cross-talk. Especially in 16-QAM, since this format is more sensitive to phase
shifts.
==PAGE:66==
62
Chapter 6. Conclusion and Prospects
6.2 Outlook
The aim behind compensating the optical nonlinearities in ﬁber transmission, is to over-
come another physical obstacle, the optical Kerr eﬀect. In order to determine whether the
nonlinear processes are trackable by the ﬁlters, we assumed that the ﬁlters hold all neces-
sary information required in order to operate. However, in fact they lack this information.
For this reason, the next step is to estimate the behavior of the AR processes, namely
their coeﬃcients and variance. Apparently, the ﬁlters rely on these channel parameters.
Whether it is possible to predict them within our method and if so, how accurately, is
still to be determined. This next step also includes investigating the obtained performance
loss due to error-prone channel parameter estimation. First concepts include brute force
maximum likelihood calculations and training sequences, which have not been used so
far.
Another assumption is that the autocorrelation functions of the AR processes are ap-
proximately exponential decays. This leads to the convenient ﬁrst order AR processes. In
this case, concerning the channel estimation, only one coeﬃcient has to be determined.
Neglecting the above assumption will result in higher order AR processes and thus a
more complex channel estimation. Further, the state and the dynamic model of the state-
space model will necessarily expand. However, we already proofed that second order AR
processes are trackable.
For those reasons, further research is necessary in order to state, how much the implemen-
tation beneﬁts from the assumptions we made. Hopefully, we will be capable of expanding
the method to a more complete and autonomous compensation method.
