Abstract
Wind energy has been developed to be the most popular of the renewable energy
technologies, due to the increased investment in renewable energy sources.
As
oﬀshore wind farms (OWF) have become a feasible solution for harvesting wind
energy, the Baltic Sea has become an area of interest for both Denmark and Ger-
many. A large scale OWF is planned to be located at the oﬀshore site Kriegers Flak
and will be connected to both Denmark and Germany. Having such a large OWF
requires high capacity transmission cables that allow power transmission across
borders. This project would be the ﬁrst oﬀshore node of a super transmission grid,
as a solution for distributing the renewable energy mix available across Europe.
The grid is proposed to be realized with high voltage direct current (HVDC) ca-
ble technology which is favourable compared to conventional alternating current
(AC) long distance cables. Multi-terminal HVDC transmission with voltage source
converters (VSC) is chosen as solution for the Kriegers Flak project. This thesis
investigates the impact of wind power production and energy markets on a cho-
sen multi-terminal HVDC solution.
The operation of VSCs and reactive power
capabilities have been assessed to evaluate the simulation model.
A simpliﬁed model of the multi-terminal HVDC connection is developed. The model
contains VSCs and the associated controllers for the power ﬂow. The model includes
power ﬂuctuation inputs from a simpliﬁed wind farm model. Wind speed measure-
ments are used to determine the representative power ﬂuctuations. The model of
the full test system is used for evaluating scenarios set by international standards
for reactive power capability and scenarios related to active power ﬂuctuations.
The results show that the multi-terminal HVDC solution meets the requirements
for reactive power control. It is however observed that step changes in voltages and
operation points at reactive power capability limits will result in one VSC aﬀect-
ing another. The study also shows that the time scale dynamics of the wind farm
power production as well as market movements are slower than the VSC controllers
dynamics. The multi-terminal HVDC solution is therefore capable of controlling
the power ﬂows of Kriegers Flaks wind power and cross border transmission based
on mentioned parameters.

Resumé
Vindenergi har gennem de sidste årtier gennemgået en omfattende udvikling og
er nu den mest udbredte vedvarende energiresource. Der er planlagt at bygge en
storskale havvindmøllepark i Østersøen mellem Danmark og Tyskland som skal stå
færdig i 2018. Denne havvindmøllepark, kaldet Kriegers Flak, skal samtidig være
en stærk transmissionsforbindelse mellem Danmark og Tyskland. Dette projekt vil
være den første realisering af et oﬀshore forbindelsespunkt mellem to lande, idet
en understation vil forbinde eﬀekt ﬂowet mellem havvindmølleparken, Danmark og
Tyskland. Sådanne kraftige transmissionsforbindelser ses som en løsning til at dis-
tribuere de forskellige vedvarende energiresourcer, der ﬁndes på tværs af Europa.
Det er foreslået, at forbindelsen realiseres med multiterminal HVDC teknologi,
eftersom AC transmission i kabler er ufordelagtigt over længere afstande.
De
forskellige udefrakommende påvirkninger fra energimarkeder samt variende vind-
energiproduktion formodes at kunne påvirke egenskaberne for de anvendte konvert-
ere og deres reaktive eﬀekt kompensering. En repræsentativ model af Kriegers Flak
projektet er udviklet. Denne model inkluderer modeller af konvertere og deres reg-
ulering, netværksekvivalenter samt havvindmøllerparken. Vindhastighedsmålinger
anvendes i havvindmølleparkemodellen til at evaluere energiproduktionen. Interna-
tionale netværksretningslinier anvendes til at bedømme reaktiv eﬀekt kontrol. Ud
fra modelanalyser observeres det, at HVDC-løsningen opfylder kravene til reaktiv
eﬀektkontrol. Samtidig ses det, at ved niveau skift af spænding i forbindelsespunk-
ter ved en konverter påvirkers en anden konverter. Dette ses også ved arbejdspunk-
ter ved grænseværdierne for den reaktive eﬀekt kompensering. Det konstateres at
dynamikken i energimarkeder og vindenergi er betydeligt langsommere end regu-
leringen af konverterne og derfor ikke er afgørende for deres funktion. Multiterminal
HVDC løsningen er derfor en løsning, der er i stand til at håndtere eﬀekt ﬂowet i
et teoretisk scenarie for Kriegers Flak projektet.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The global demand for renewable energy sources (RES) is rising due to the increased
concern of the environmental impact of the conventional fossil fuel energy resources.
Most recently the COP21 climate change conference in Paris 2015 conﬁrms the
global political concern for the environment. Wind energy is a fast growing RES and
many OWFs are planned as the onshore solutions will meet limitations of available
land and visual impact. One of the main issues of large RES is the location and
therefore the associated need for transmission of the power. Large RESs will often
be located far from the centre of the power grids load. The super grid has been
pointed out as a solution to the integration of a mix of RES (Van Hertem and
Ghandhari, 2010). In Europe a solution could be an improved transmission grid
which would enable harnessing and distribution of the large potential of diverse
and often remote RES. This improved transmission grid would be the super grid.
The diﬀerent types of RES will be able to compliment each other and thus balance
the total energy production across Europe. This super grid solution could be using
oﬀshore wind power and hydro power from Scandinavia with solar power and wind
power from Southern Europe.
The current power system is already heavily loaded and the need for very long
distance transmission would further stress the grid. This is due to the fact that
the current technology in use is AC. The AC transmission system is only useful for
bulk power transfer when using lines instead of cables. There is a great opposition
for more power lines because it has environmental impact as well as for political
reasons. The AC transmission as an underground cable has very limited capacity.
Underground or submarine cables are needed for connecting Scandinavia and Great
Britain on a larger scale. Also HVDC connections across the Baltic Sea would be
valuable. Thus focus has turned to HVDC as a transmission solution.
The OWF Kriegers Flak will have a generation capacity of 600 MW from oﬀshore
wind turbines (WTs) and will be a connection located in the Baltic Sea between

Denmark and Germany (Energinet.dk, 2015). The connection would also include

only Denmark out of the three countries has chosen to invest in the OWF resulting

political circumstances. The original studies envisioned the ﬁrst oﬀshore multi ter-
minal HVDC node to be commissioned and therefore a valuable research resource.
A realization of such a HVDC connection with larger scale of wind power production
will therefore be the base for this thesis instead of the now planned AC connec-
tion. The modern transmission HVDC system use voltage source converter(VSC)

technology which enables control if the current is leading or lagging the AC voltage
at its connection point. This makes a VSC able to supply or consume reactive
power at a connected network and thus replace the need of other reactive power
support components (Chaudhuri et al., 2014). The connection between Denmark

connections would be smaller and in the order of minutes.
1.2
Problem Description
The connection of the Kriegers Flak project would have been the ﬁrst oﬀshore
multi-terminal HVDC connection. Therefore it is unknown how the multi-terminal
HVDC technology will be able to control the power ﬂow when multiple external
forces aﬀect the power ﬂow. In this study only the most important parameters for
the analysis have been chosen. The power markets are operating at a ﬁxed time
scale of hourly changes caused by market movements. At the same time the OWF
farm has ﬂuctuating power production based on wind speed. All potential wind
power must be produced and transmitted and is thus uncontrolled. As the trans-
mission connection connects two transmission system operators (TSOs), guidelines
of regulatory capabilities for the connection points apply. These guidelines are de-
scribed in the grid codes deﬁned by the European Network of Transmission System
Operators (ENSTO-E). This leads to the possibility that the external forces of mar-
ket movements and ﬂuctuating wind power production could lead to bottlenecks in
power ﬂow or reactive power control capability problems. The scenario is incorpo-
rating these two aspects with the possibility of diﬀerent time scales in dynamics.
It is vital to investigate these external forces dynamics in relation to the dynamics
of the operation and control of the VSC technology. The problem is illustrated in
Fig. 1.2.
This thesis investigates the potential challenges of ﬂows of the multi-terminal HVDC
solution for Kriegers Flak, which could originate from ﬂuctuations in wind power
production together with the change in the surrounding energy markets. A simpli-
ﬁed model of a VSC and a OWF with required controllers will be developed and
veriﬁed. The sub model of the single VSC will be used for the three VSCs needed
for the full model. In the full model the WF model determines the input of the
WF power production based on wind speed data. The AC networks of the onshore
connections as well as the AC connection to the OWF will be modelled as network
equivalents. The full model will be evaluated by both the grid code requirements
but also the consequences of the dynamics of the OWF and energy markets. This
is also illustrated in Fig. 1.2.

1.4. Thesis Outline
1.4
Thesis Outline
Chapter 1, Introduction, describes background, the main tasks and the required
steps to accomplish the objectives.
Chapter 2, VSC-HVDC Converter Model Design, ﬁrst gives an overview of the
VSC technology used and the characteristics. The electrical layout of the model
of a single VSC with AC ﬁlter and network equivalents is presented. The control
mechanisms of the inner and outer controls are described. Multiple outer controls

2.1
Advantages and Proﬁle of the VSC
The HVDC technology has been used for half a century and it is currently under
promising development.
HVDC has several important qualiﬁcations.
It is very
eﬀective for high capacity transmission over longer distances. Latest concepts are
useful when connecting asynchronous power grids. The classical HVDC technol-
ogy is current source converter (CSC) and is based on thyristors as switches for
establishing a constant direct current (DC). By using thyristors as switching de-
vices the CSC becomes dependant on the AC voltage of the connected grid for
switching oﬀactions. With this concept the voltage is controlled to change the
power. A newer concept is the VSC that was developed in the mid-90s. For the
VSC the voltage is kept constant where the current is controlled to change the
power ﬂow (Van Hertem and Ghandhari, 2010) (Chaudhuri et al., 2014). VSC has
insulated-gate bipolar transistors (IGBTs) that can be controlled for both on- and
oﬀ-switching. The controllability of the IGBTs makes it possible to switch at any
7/77
==PAGE:30==
2.2. VSC Electric System Model
time during a cycle and that makes the VSC a self-commutated converter because it
is able to construct AC waveforms. The VSC is therefore able to feed power into a
passive network which would not be possible with CSC which are line-commutated
and therefore rely on external AC system for switching.
The ability to generate waveforms is very relevant for OWFs that are isolated and
need a reference frequency. Classic HVDC connections can transfer power from 100
to 10000 MW. The new HVDC light concept using VSCs, will have a transmission
capacity range from 50 to 2500 MW (ABB, 2013).
By maintaining stable voltage and frequency it is possible to control active and
reactive power. By controlling phase angle and amplitude of the converter output
voltage, the active and reactive power is set. This is again useful when supplying
weak grids that would need reactive power support for maintaining voltage levels
(Jiang-Häfner et al., 2002). Within the VSC technology there are two diﬀerent
concepts of control. The ﬁrst is the two-level or three-level diode clamped converters
creating a PWM signal. The other is the multilevel modular converter(MMC). The
technology of MMC has now been improved to surpass other multilevel converter
topologies that are in use for the HVDC applications. The MMC topologies are
now using lower switching frequencies and can thereby reduce the converter losses.
It is possible to scale up to higher voltage levels by increasing the number of sub
modules for each phase. The reliability of the converter has also been improved
recently. The number of sub modules for switching, determines the detail of the
models used later in this thesis. This thesis will only be using the 3-level VSC
for simpliﬁcation. Simpliﬁcation of sub-modules circuits for average value models
(AVM) will be included. This is assuming that IGBT components in a sub-module
can be represented by having a resistance of two states. Either conductive value
for ON state or large open-circuit value for OFF state. The AVM is an eﬀective
method for simplifying the simulation models calculation time and complexity for
studies of scopes of electromagnetic transient or lower frequency transients (Peralta
et al. (2012)).
2.1.1
PMW Concept for VSC
VSC-HVDC is based on the concept of using the switching of the IGBTs with PWM
to construct the desired voltage waveform for the converters AC output. There are
several methods for PWM of obtaining a wave form as close to a sine wave as
possible. This is done to match the AC grids sinusoidal waveform originating at
the rotating generators in the conventional power production. (Chaudhuri et al.,
2014) The theory of the SIMULINK block used for the test model is no further
explained in detail as this AVM has no need for conﬁguring the PWM.

The non-linear model constructed in MATLAB will need initial values for variables

the steady state conditions of the system, another model based on this non-linear
system, will be a linearised system that will be able to provide steady state condi-
tions for the non-linear system. The linearised system will be a state space model.
This description only covers the state space model of a single VSC setup connected
to the onshore grid.
A state space model is a mathematical representation of a physical model with at
set of input, output and state variables related by ﬁrst order diﬀerential equations.
The number of state variables are given by the minimum number of variables in the
system that are necessary to represent the system. Therefore the state variables
cannot be described by a linear combination of other state variables as they must be
linearly independent. The number of state variables can be found from the order of
the systems deﬁning diﬀerential equation. The system is of 18th order and will thus
have a state vector containing 18 state variables. The state space representation of
the system is represented by

3.2. Variable Speed Wind Turbines
Power Electronics
Depending on the type of wind turbine power electronics will be needed to feed the
generated power to a connected grid. The ﬁxed speed wind turbines are directly
connected to the grid and simple electric components are used to handle distur-
bances fed into the grid. For the variable speed wind turbines, the synchronous
generator speed will vary so much that power electronics, that can separate it from
the grid frequency, will be necessary. This could be achieved with an power elec-
tronic converter system that will connect the electrical system of the generator
with the grid system using independent frequencies. This solution is relevant for
this thesis as the model of the wind farm Kriegers Flak will consist of variable speed
wind turbines and the presence of a power electronic converter system will be an
argument for simpliﬁcation of the interconnection of the wind farm model and the
HVDC grid. The electrical power of the WT Pel is equal to the aerodynamic power
at the wind turbine rotor Pwtr when there is assumed zero losses in the system.
3.2
Variable Speed Wind Turbines
Based on the general structure of a WT shown in Fig.
3.1 several main types
of WTs exist. They are using diﬀerent classical and newer concepts for genera-
tors and power electronics and have diﬀerent advantages and drawbacks(Hansen,
2005a). This section will brieﬂy describe the type called variable speed wind tur-
bine which is chosen to represent the wind farm model and which obstacles that
are associated with this type of design concept.
The variable wind speed turbine is a very dominant type of WT among the most
popular types of designs. (Hansen, 2005a). The variable wind speed turbine is
designed with a characteristic where the WT is operating with variable rotational
speed. The variation in rotational speed results in variation in the electric frequency.
As the connected grid is operating at a ﬁxed electrical frequency a power electronic
interface is needed to decouple the two systems frequencies. By having the ability to
operate at varying rotational speed the WT can continuously adjust its rotational
speed to the present wind speed.
Thereby the WT can operate at the highest
level of aerodynamic eﬃciency at any wind speed. Other options of control is also
implemented in the design. To control rotational speed of the rotor the aerodynamic
proﬁle of the WT is changed by changing the angle of the rotor blades. This way
the blades can be turned into or out of the wind very fast.
The aerodynamic
characteristic of the blades are thus changed and will aﬀect the speed of the rotor.
This can be used to keep the rotational speed of the rotor at a rated value and thus
avoid stressing the components.

4.6
Short Circuit Ratio and System Robustness
In case of weak grid connections in comparison to the connected converter, large
changes in the power transfer can lead to voltage disturbances at the connection
point. Ideally the power capacity of the converter is matched by the grid models
Thévenin impedance. The SCR indicates the amount of power that can be trans-
ferred to a system without aﬀecting the power quality. A larger power transfer from
the VSC to the onshore grid will lead to over voltages in a weak grid. The SCR is
deﬁned from (Kundur, 1993). The wind farm power generation ﬂuctuations should
not lead to reactive power support need for the grid sides as the grid originally is
dimensioned to be a strong grid. If the grid was weaker the increased power pro-
duction could lead to over voltages that would need reactive power support. The
SCR, found for the system, can provide a base for evaluating simulations.
As a general guideline the SCR can be categorized as follows, according to (Kundur,
1993):
• Strong grid: SCR > 5
• Medium grid: SCR = 3 −5
• Weak grid: SCR < 3

following section. It is evaluated that the maximum power increase of the OWF
model is unable to inﬂuence the power quality of the power transmitted at the

multi terminal HVDC connection that could function with a large OWF connected
to two diﬀerent TSOs. Based on this realization, further tests focus on reactive
power support of the grid connections. The onshore VSCs implemented in the full
model are capable of operating within limits of the U −Q/Pmax proﬁle provided by
the grid codes for the Nordic region. If the limits applying to Continental Europe
were implemented the result would not be identical but as the limits are in similar
range, it is most likely that the full model would satisfy the grid codes. The reactive
power range is identical but the voltage range is 50 % larger. The simulation shows
that the change in operating point at one onshore VSC can inﬂuence the output
at the other onshore VSC because of the chosen voltage droop control.
It was

state.
The current limits of the control are set by the current limits according to the
grid codes.
The reactive power support is limited by current limits of the grid

equivalent that can be supported by reactive power is 11.4 %. The reactive power
support response to the maximum voltage drop is able to meet the time constant
requirements of the grid codes for voltage control mode. However it is observed
that the voltage drop will have an eﬀect on the active power of the other onshore
converter primarily because of the droop control at the onshore VSCs.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The base concept of this thesis has been to analyse the possibilities of realising
the Kreigers Flak project with multi terminal HVDC connections as solution for
power transmission.
The full model requires sub models of VSC, transmission
networks and OWFs. This leads to a test system of several time dynamics, as a
mechanical system in the WF model is connected to the electrical system of the
HVDC connection. From the analysis it is proven that the WF dynamics are of a
slower time scale than that of a VSC and the implemented control. The control
strategies implemented for the HVDC connection are therefore able to control the
external forces of the WF. This is of great signiﬁcance since it would be undesired
to have uncontrolled external forces from the WF to eﬀect the HVDC transmission
solution.
The market economics would be static as an external force since the
dynamics are of hourly scale. Ramp up and down are used and the change in active
power is therefore slower than the ramps found at the OWF. The intraday market
time scale of Denmark is hourly but the gate closure time is diﬀerent for other
countries. If the scope is expanded to future grids the dynamics in the markets
should be reconsidered as the gate closure times are decreasing. The fastest gate
closure is now in Germany at 15 minutes (Www.nordpoolspot.com, 2015). This
still leads to slow power ramps.
It was seen that if external forces have faster dynamics than the implemented
control, the external force on one terminal could aﬀect another terminal in the
model.
From the analysis of the the Kriegers Flak project realised with multi
terminal HVDC, it is clear that the implemented VSCs control dynamics are capable
of meeting the requirements set by the grid codes of ENTSO-E regarding reactive
power capabilities.
That the ﬂuctuations in the power generation of the WF was unable to cause any
complications conﬁrms the HVDC technology as a feasible solution for bulk trans-
mission of power.
This cater the vision of HVDC super grids as a solution for
distribution of renewable energy sources across continents. Besides purely econom-
ical, the reason for not using HVDC as a solution for the Kriegers Flak project is
not found through this project.
55/77
==PAGE:78==
Discussion
As this thesis is a simpliﬁed model of an theoretical setup, many complications
have been excluded. The economic feasibility of the solution has not been included.
Energinet.dk’s choice of realising the project with AC transmission would suggest
economic infeasibility of a HVDC solution. The cable distances of 50 km could
be the reason for AC transmission being economically more attractive. At longer
distances the HVDC transmission solution is expected to be a better economic
investment.
None of the analysed scenarios had multiple events at diﬀerent external forces. It
was seen that step changes and operation points could aﬀect other VSCs than the
one exposed. It is assumed that simultaneous events at diﬀerent terminals would
impact the evaluations of reactive power capabilities.
From a control perspective there is room for improvement. There has been a simple
tuning of the gain parameters in the control. The oscillations and lack of low pass
ﬁlters should be considered. For this study the lack of ﬁlters should not impact the
conclusions drawn, as the most important time dependant evaluation parameter is
approx. 500 times bigger than the measured time constant t1. The lack of ﬁlters
would mainly aﬀect the time constants of the controls response. Its implementation
would require more tuning of PI controllers. It should be implemented as a low
pass ﬁlter at each input for the control, that is a measurement. The ﬁlters would
protect the control against measuring faults that would be outside the scope. The
reactive power control could be redesigned as other control designs might improve
the dynamics at a voltage step.
No concrete components have been selected for the model and it is therefore a purely
theoretical study. Real life implementation would require extensive simulations.
The VSCs can momentarily operate beyond the limits given by the grid codes, but
the grid codes will be set as the parameters for the evaluation. The need for reactive
power support is only present as a result of the grid codes between TSOs. The grid
codes deﬁne the obligations from one TSO to another.
The measured wind speed data is input for the OWF model and therefore deﬁning
for the power ﬂuctuation calculations. The quality of the wind speed data could be
improved considerably by using oﬀshore measurements of same time scale. The end
results produced by the OWF model would most likely lead to the same conclusions
for the dynamics as the constructed extreme case that was used for evaluation,
without getting close to limits. If it was desired to calculate precise power output
data, radical improvements should be implemented in the wind farm model.
The thesis has evaluated certain aspects of the multi-terminal HVDC solution for
the Kriegers Flak project. The evaluations conclude that the solutions are feasible
with respect to reactive power control and integration of an OWF and its power
ﬂuctuations. Expanding conclusions to transmission losses or fault ride through
conditions would require improvement of the current model since it is now limited
to the scope of the thesis.
56/77
==PAGE:79==
Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this thesis a simpliﬁed model of Krigers Flak with multi-terminal HVDC connec-
tions was developed. A simpliﬁed model of a 1200 MW OWF with variable speed
WTs was implemented and analysed with wind speed data to determine the scale
of the OWF as an external force on the HVDC connection.
From this study of a multi-terminal HVDC connection in an oﬀshore grid with wind
power generation, it is possible to conclude on the impact of the external forces on
the connection’s reactive power control capabilities.
It is concluded that the implemented test models reactive power control is able to
meet the applying grid codes requirements. It was required to be able to switch
between types of reactive power control depending on the simulation scenario. It
is observed that step changes in grid equivalent voltages can aﬀect multiple VSCs
in the test model. The steady state operation points required by the grid codes for
one onshore VSC, will aﬀect active power ﬂow and DC voltages for both onshore
VSCs. This will be a consequence of using the same type of droop control for the
onshore VSCs. The power generation ﬂuctuations of the OWF is not aﬀecting the
power quality of the HVDC connections power transmission.
The simulation studies conclude that the two external forces, energy markets and
wind farm power production do not aﬀect the operation capabilities of the multi-
terminal HVDC solution.
Within the scope of the thesis, the simulations conducted on the test system proves
that the concept of using a multi-terminal HVDC connection for realising the
project of the Kriegers Flak connection, is a capable solution.

Chapter 8
Future Work
The simpliﬁed model of the Kriegers Flak with multi terminal HVDC connection
was developed. Suggested improvements that could be included in further work are
listed in this section.
• The tuning of the gains in the controllers could be improved as future work
since the tuning of the gains could lead to system instability and is currently
left out. Especially the reactive power control could be faster to minimize the
disturbances and oscillations that was observed at a voltage step change. If
the overall stability of the test model was to be improved, a dedicated state
space model should be created to analyse eigenvalues for better understanding
of instability problems. From a correct transfer function of the system, the
systems poles and zeros could be analysed. Such an analysis would be useful
when testing new control strategies as the state space model provides a good
tool for stability when a solution is implemented.
• If the WF representation was realized as a current source it would be possible
to analyse the dynamics of this WF AC system.
If the total model was
to be further developed it would be possible to connect SIMULINK model
of the WF directly to the electric system model. This would improve the
accessibility of the model if other dynamics in outputs of the WF model
should be included.
• Scenarios of simultaneous events of external forces and multiple VCS in the
full model should be considered as it is seen, that events at one AC network
equivalent can aﬀect an VSC at another AC network equivalent.
