Abstract 
Natural products with bioactive properties tested through the course of evolution, provide 
ecological advantages to their organism-producers and inspire novel drug leads used by the 
pharmaceutical and agriculture industry. The elucidation of the biosynthetic pathways of 
natural products and the identification of the pathway related genes are important for the 
heterologous expression of such pathways or the chemical synthesis of compounds and 
consequently their use for industrial purposes. Aspergillus nidulans, belonging to the class of 
filamentous fungi, serves as a eukaryotic model organism and is a well-known producer of 
such secondary metabolites.  
3-methylorsellinic acid is an aromatic fungal polyketide produced by A. nidulans, with 
cichorine, a phytotoxin, as the end product of its biosynthetic pathway. Sanchez and 
coworkers at 2012, identified pkbA or cicF, the polyketide synthase gene of the pathway and 
other seven genes (cicA-cicH) which all together form the gene cluster of the pathway. 
Following the identification of the genes and chemical analysis, they isolated three key 
pathway related metabolites, 3-methyl-orsellinic acid, nidulol and cichorine and proposed a 
biosynthetic pathway for 3-MOA/cichorine. However, several steps of the pathway remain 
unclear and without assigned enzymatic function. The aim of our project was to unravel and 
shed light upon these unclear steps of the pathway and also to confirm any possible relation 
of three previously discovered compounds with the pathway. 
To accomplish these objectives, we used two reference strains, NID3 resembling the wild type 
and NID762 in which the transcription factor of the pathway was overexpressed as well as  
eight deletion strains (NID952 – NID956, NID1010, NID1012, NID1015) each with a single gene 
deletion of the 3-MOA/cichorine gene cluster from a library of strains at DTU Bioengineering. 
After cultivating each strain in YES (Yeast extract sucrose) and minimal media for seven days, 
chemical extraction and UHPLC-TOF-MS analysis were performed. 
The results from this work, strongly indicate that the NRPS-like enzyme is the first enzyme 
acting on 3-MOA leading a likely aldehyde intermediate, compound 57, and the existence of 
another branch of the pathway leading to nidulol. Also, the analyses of two deletion strains, 
NID952 and NID954 provided evidence that CYP450 is a basic enzyme towards nidulol 
formation and also indicate a role of the NRPS-like enzyme in the lactone formation of nidulol. 
Furthermore, the findings show that O-methylation activity is promiscuous in the pathway. 
Another major finding of this work is the clarification of the relation of the three previously 
discovered compounds, a pyrone compound named 16,  cichoarginine and a ‘dimer’, with the 
pathway. The pyrone compound is a shunt 3-MOA product and related with the pathway 
while the results clearly suggest that cichoarginine and the ‘dimer’ are also part of the 3-MOA 
pathway. Except for cichoarginine, other ‘cicho amino acid derived’ compounds may exist as 
indicated by the presence of a likely compound 26, ‘cicholysine’. The findings of this work 
provide evidence and clarify steps of the 3-MOA pathway and also link specific steps with

defined enzymatic activity giving new direction at future research for the full elucidation of 
the 3-MOA pathway.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Natural products and their importance 
 
Cells, the basic units of life, accommodate the basic biochemical reactions of anabolism and 
catabolism, needed to build necessary molecules for their function or to break down 
molecules producing energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP). These reactions are 
organised in metabolic pathways and are strictly regulated according to the energy demands 
and the physiological state of the cell. The network of these metabolic pathways, necessary 
for the organisms’ growth and reproduction is referred to as primary metabolism and the 
molecules involved are termed primary metabolites. (Lehninger, Nelson, & Cox, 2005) 
 On the other hand, there are several biosynthetic pathways in different organisms, distinct 
from the primary metabolism, which result in end products that are not important for the 
normal function of the cell and the organism. Instead, they may provide an ecological role, 
such as defence or dominance against other surrounding species.  The structure of these 
products consists of primary metabolic intermediates put together and modified by 
enzymatic activities to form molecules which are termed ‘’secondary metabolites’’. While 
natural products may also include primary metabolites, they will refer only to secondary 
metabolites at this context.  (Dewick, 2009) 
Natural products, from living sources, have been used since ancient times to encounter 
human diseases and modern studies of these formulae could provide clues in topics such as 
the synergism of different drug combinations (Ji, Li, & Zhang, 2009). Pharmaceutical research 
and development during the last decades had a loss of interest in natural products as source 
of lead structures entering the development pipeline (Harvey, Edrada-Ebel, & Quinn, 2015), 
with the main reasons for that being the focus on chemically synthesised compounds and the 
exclusion of natural products from high-throughput screening methods used to test 
compounds against biological targets (Harvey et al., 2015). However, the key role that natural 
products continued to play in the drug discovery process is highlighted by the fact that during 
the years 2000-2010, half of all small molecules approved as new chemical entities were 
natural products or related (Newman & Cragg, 2012). Nowadays, there is an increasing 
interest of incorporating natural products in the industrial drug discovery process. 
The importance of natural products is also obvious in the agriculture industry. Natural 
products belonging in the class of neonicotinoids and pyrethroids (fig. 1) are used in insect 
management (Dayan, Cantrell, & Duke, 2009). Moreover, natural products are used in plant 
pathology with an example being Streptomyces spp. derivatives such as blasticidin-S used as 
fungicidal in rice crops (Dayan et al., 2009).

1.2 Filamentous fungi: Aspergillus nidulans 
 
1.2.1 Kingdom Fungi: 
Species belonging in the kingdom of Fungi are eukaryotic organisms with unique 
characteristics. They differ from bacteria, plants and animals in their cells’ structure, life cycle 
and their cell wall contains chitin, a substance which is only found in fungi and the exoskeleton 
of arthropods. Fungi are mostly aerobic and they reproduce both sexually and asexually. They 
are also heterotrophic, digesting and breaking down animal and plant tissues through the 
production and excretion of enzymes. They perform a significant ecological role for the 
biosphere as they recycle nutrients by decomposing organic matter. (Margulis & Chapman, 
2009) 
1.2.2 Filamentous fungi: 
Fungal species classified as filamentous are characterised by growing branched structures 
called hyphae (Meinhardt & Esser, 1993) which provide the organism with the ability to 
expand in its surrounding microenvironment growing on surfaces. Filamentous fungi are well-
known producers of proteins and secondary metabolites. Improvement of strains with 
genetic engineering methods such as gene cloning led to higher yields of these products and 
made commercial efforts viable (Punt et al., 2002), while development of new, sophisticated 
genetic engineering systems using the CRISPR-Cas9 method (Nødvig, Nielsen, Kogle, & 
Mortensen, 2015) will further strengthen the use of filamentous fungi for research and 
industrial purposes.

An important division of the fungal kingdom, the Ascomycota, includes many 
biotechnologically relevant genera such as Aspergillus, Neurospora and Saccharomyces. 
Aspergilli are important for humankind with both useful and harmful characteristics. The 
negative aspect involves the secretion of mycotoxins (fig. 2), including aflatoxins, ochratoxin 
A, sterigmatocystin and cyclopiazonic acid which can be harmful to living organisms (Sweeney 
& Dobson, 1998). On the positive side, species within genus Aspergillus, such as A. niger, A. 
oryzae and A. nidulans are used in the industry for the production of proteins such as 
amylases, proteases, lipases, cellulases and monoclonal antibodies (Lubertozzi & Keasling, 
2008). Furthermore, fungal metabolites such as aspyrones, brefeldins and echinocandins with 
antibiotic and antifungal properties are a few examples of bioactive compounds with 
pharmaceutical interest, produced by Aspergilli (Lubertozzi & Keasling, 2008).

1.2.3 Aspergillus nidulans 
A. nidulans, morphologically, consists of a vegetative (growth) and a reproductive form 
(Pontecorvo, Roper, Chemmons, Macdonald, & Bufton, 1953). There are three types of 
reproduction for A. nidulans, the sexual and the asexual, which can be present at the same 
type in the organism, and the parasexual which is a mitotic pathway with meiotic 
characteristics, not common in Aspergilli (Pontecorvo et al., 1953; Ward, Qin, Dhanjoon, Ye, 
& Singh, 2005). A. nidulans is a eukaryotic model organism and has been used as a system to 
understand, among others, the regulatory mechanisms related to carbon metabolism (Bailey

the DNA damage response (Goldman & Kafer, 2004). 
1.3 Fungal natural products and their biosynthesis 
The major categories of fungal natural products are: non-ribosomal peptides, polyketides, 
terpenes and indole alkaloids (Keller, Turner, & Bennett, 2005). At the following subsections 
the focus will be on the first two categories. 
1.3.1 Biosynthetic gene clusters 
The genes encoding for natural products in fungi are ,mostly, organised and grouped in gene 
clusters and their  study, although difficult because of the presence of introns, can be 
facilitated by sophisticated computational tools such as antismash (Blin et al., 2017; 
Schümann & Hertweck, 2006). In fungi, the clustered genes encode for RNA with the process 
of transcription. According to the central dogma of biology, the genetic information flows 
from DNA to RNA and then into proteins. Once in proteins, the information cannot go 
backwards into RNA and DNA. The formation of the mRNA after the removal of introns and 
the protein formation through translation are followed by the primary and secondary 
metabolic intermediates’ production which participate in the biosynthetic and metabolic 
pathways of the cell (fig. 4).  
 
Figure 4. Sequence of information from genes to metabolites. (Jørn Smedsgaard & Nielsen, 2005), Metabolite 
profiling of fungi and yeast: from phenotype to metabolome by MS and informatics. Journal of experimental 
botany, 56(410), 273-286.  Modified. 
 
A typical polyketide gene cluster controlled by a transcription factor of the pathway contains 
a gene encoding a polyketide synthase that forms the backbone of the compound through its 
different domains and tailoring enzymes such as oxygenases and methyltransferases that act 
on the PKS product.  
The assignment of secondary metabolites to their gene clusters is a challenging task but 
interdisciplinary research has made it possible. An example is the deletion of 32 polyketide 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
 
synthase genes in A. nidulans followed by assignment of nine genes to their products (M. L. 
Nielsen et al., 2011a). The expression of the biosynthetic gene clusters is regulated both 
globally and locally (Brakhage, 2012). In the first case, global regulators such as the nuclear 
protein LaeA, act on different biosynthetic pathways to control their expression. In Aspergillus 
spp., the metabolites sterigmatocystin and lovastatin are not produced after deletion of the 
laeA gene while in Penicillium chrysogenum, LaeA is also involved in sporulation and 
pigmentation of the fungi (Kosalková et al., 2009; Woo Bok & Keller, 2004). Secondly, each 
gene cluster responsible for the biosynthesis of a metabolite has, in most cases, also a gene(s) 
encoding for one or more transcription factors that act on the specific pathway. Finally, 
another regulatory mechanism functions through acetylation and methylation of the 
chromatin’s histone (Brakhage, 2012). DNA is coiled and packaged around the histone 
proteins in a complex known as chromatin. The modifications of histones can alter the surface 
of the complex, allowing interaction with other proteins and thus are involved in the 
regulation of gene expression (Strahl & Allis, 2000) The expression of silent gene clusters, such 
as the monodictyphenone gene cluster, was possible in A. nidulans after inactivation of the 
histone methylation related CclA gene (Bok et al., 2009). An increased number of natural 
product gene clusters, not expressed in laboratory conditions, was revealed after the progress 
made in the field of fungal genomics. The research focus on these cryptic gene clusters has 
shown a case of activation of their expression during co-cultivation with other organisms, 
such as the induction of the orsellinic acid, lecanoric acid and cathepsin K inhibitors’ pathways 
of A. nidulans after co-cultivation with Actinomycetes bacteria (Schroeckh et al., 2009). 
Another approach shown to activate cryptic gene clusters is the OSMAC (one strain, many 
compounds) method. This approach developed by Bode et al. at 2002 showed a production 
and isolation of different metabolites from organisms, including A. ochraceus, by changing 
aspects of the cultivation conditions such as aeration, type of media and addition of enzyme 
modifiers (Bode et al., 2002).  
 
1.3.2 Non-ribosomal peptides 
Non-ribosomal peptides consist of amino-acid building units linked through peptide bonds. 
They are formed by megaenzymes, termed non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS) which 
have several modules. Each module is responsible for the incorporation of one amino-acid 
unit and is comprised of several domains working to that purpose. These domains include the 
adenylation domain (A) which selects and carries the amino acid unit, the pantothenylation 
domain (P) which carries the forming peptide, the condensation domain (C) catalysing the 
bonding between the amino-acids and the thioesterase (T) domain involved in the release of 
the peptide from the synthetase. Examples of fungal non-ribosomal peptides include 
penicillin G, gliotoxin and cyclosporin (fig. 5). (Keller et al., 2005)

1.3.3 Polyketides 
 
1.3.3.1 Biosynthesis 
The usual building blocks for the formation of the polyketide backbone are acetyl-CoA, the 
starter unit, and malonyl-CoA, the extender unit, which after Claisen condensation result in 
the basic carbon chain of polyketides. Acetyl-CoA is derived from the primary metabolite 
pyruvic acid from the glycolytic pathway by decarboxylation and also from the β-oxidation of 
fatty acids (Dewick, 2009). Malonyl-CoA is derived from acetyl-CoA. Two basic reaction 
mechanisms related to polyketide biosynthesis will be presented below: the Claisen 
condensation and the conversion of acetyl-CoA into malonyl-CoA. 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
 
 
Figure 6. Claisen reaction mechanism. Based on fig. 2.7 p. 16,  by (Dewick, 2009) 
 
Claisen condensation is a basic carbon-carbon bond forming reaction. In a base-catalysed 
reaction, a hydrogen is taken from the (fig. 6.a) and an enolate ion is formed (fig. 6.b, 6.c). In 
fig. 6.d, the enolate ion performs a nucleophilic attack on the carbonyl of an ethyl acetate 
molecule. The product (e) is formed and the ethoxide which is a good leaving group leaves 
the molecule to form (f). The difference between aldol and Claisen condensation lies at the 
leaving group. An alcohol is formed when a hydrogen is present instead of, in our example, 
ethoxide which is the leaving group. (Dewick, 2009). In natural products, the Claisen 
condensation is performed between coenzyme A esters (thioesters) instead of ethyl acetate 
molecules.

The conversion of acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA is catalysed by the acetyl-CoA carboxylase, a 
megaenzyme with three component proteins: biotin carboxylase, carboxyltransferase and 
biotin carboxyl carrier protein (fig. 7). (Dewick, 2009)

Polyketide formation in fungi is done by the iterative action of type I polyketide synthases 
which are multidomain enzymes, with each domain acting repeatedly on the forming product. 
The basic structure of a fungal PKS consists of a ketoacyl CoA synthase (KS), an acyltransferase 
(AT) and an acyl carrier protein (ACP) domains (Keller et al., 2005). Depending on the 
existence of reducing domains- ketoreductase (KR), dehydratase (DH) and enoyl reductase 
(ER) which reduce the keto-group to an alcohol, remove a water molecule and reduce the 
double bond respectively (Dewick, 2009)- fungal type I polyketide synthases can be divided in 
three categories: highly reducing, partially reducing and nonreducing.  
Highly reducing PKSs can be further divided in two categories by having or not having ER 
domain activity (Cox, Skellam, & Williams, 2018). The cholesterol-lowering drug lovastatin is 
an example of a highly reduced polyketide produced by the combined action of two HR PKSs, 
the lovastatin nonaketide synthase (LNKS) and the lovastatin diketide synthase (LDKS) with 
LNKS to be the product of the lovB gene (Hutchinson et al., 2000). LNKS except for the 
domains mentioned before [KS, AT (malonyl), DH, KR, ACP] also contains a methyltransferase 
(MT), a condensation-like domain homologous to that of NRPSs and an inactive ER-like 
domain (Ma et al., 2009). 
In partially reducing polyketide synthases there is an incorporated, minimum reductase 
activity. 6-Methylsalicylic acid synthase, found in Penicillium patulum, is an example of this 
category with two reductive domains,  KR and DH, found in its structure performing a 
reduction of an oxygen to hydroxyl group and a further removal of water molecule by the 
dehydratase, both before the formation of the aromatic ring. (Dewick, 2009) 
Non-reducing polyketide synthases are characterised by the lack of reducing domains in their 
structures and the presence of aromatic rings in the formed molecules. Aromatic, non-
reduced polyketides, are found both in bacteria and fungi but the complex ones, having fusion 
rings, differ in the folding mode of their building units with streptomycetes bacteria having 
the S mode and fungi the F mode (Thomas, 2001).  
Aromatic polyketides produced by fungi include the orsellinic acid,  the norsolorinic acid and 
aflatoxin B1 (same pathway), naphthopyrone YWA1 and tetrahydroxynapththalene among 
others (Crawford & Townsend, 2010). A nr-PKS consists of SAT (starter unit acyltransferase), 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
 
KS, MAT (malonyl acyltransferase), PT (product template), ACP and TE-CLC (thioesterase - 
Claisen cyclase) domains (Crawford & Townsend, 2010). Studies in the norsolorinic acid 
synthase, encoded by the pksA gene in A. parasiticus, which forms the initial product in the 
aflatoxin B1 related pathway, have shown that PT domain is involved in aromatisation, 
cyclisation and release of the polyketide (Crawford et al., 2008). 
Orsellinic acid is a simple aromatic polyketide. There are four biosynthetic, orsellinic-acid-
related pathways from which the three of them will be described at this subsection. The 3-
methylorsellinic acid/cichorine pathway which is an important aspect of this report will 
described at the end of the section. 
1.3.3.2 Aromatic polyketides: Orsellinic-acid related biosynthetic pathways 
 
1.3.3.2.1 Orsellinic acid pathway 
Orsellinic acid is a simple aromatic polyketide which contains two phenol groups. It has been 
shown, that orsellinic acid is synthesised by the orsellinic acid synthase (OAS), a non-reducing 
polyketide synthase with SAT, KS, AT, PT, ACP and TE domains (Schroeckh et al., 2009). 
Another study by Sanchez et. al. (2010) showed that the synthesis of orsellinic acid can be 
performed without the action of tailoring enzymes and regulatory proteins while the genes 
of the orsellinic gene cluster include the identified AN7909.4 (orsA), AN7911.4 (orsB) and 
AN7912.4 (orsC) genes (Sanchez et al., 2010; Schroeckh et al., 2009). AN7009 (orsA) gene, 
which encodes for the OAS has also been linked to the biosynthesis of lecanoric acid, F-9775A, 
F9775B, gerfelin and diorcinol (Nielsen et al. 2011; Sanchez et al. 2010; Schroeckh et al. 2009) 
and two violaceols, violaceol I and II (Nielsen et al. 2011) (fig. 10, fig. 11)

1.3.3.2.2 Orsellinaldehyde related metabolites: Asperugins 
Another orsellinic-acid-related biosynthetic pathway from Aspergillus rugulosus has been 
proposed for asperugins by Ballantine et al. in 1971. Orsellinic acid is converted to 
orsellinaldehyde by reductive release, gets oxidized and the intermediate hydroxy-
orsellinaldehyde is formed. A prenylation and two oxidations follow to end up in asperugin B 
which is further methylated to form asperugin (fig. 12)

1.4. Analytical chemistry 
 
Analytical chemistry is the branch of chemistry with the aim of characterising chemical 
substances in a qualitative (identification) and quantitative way. Major analytical methods to 
achieve this aim include chromatography, spectroscopy and spectrometry with the 
combination of these methods to provide more accurate and in-depth analysis. Analytical 
chemistry is applied in many different sectors both in research and industry. Identifying and 
measuring disease biomarkers through analytical chemistry methods is an important 
application of chemical analysis in medicine (Zolotov, 2014). Analytic chemical methods are 
also employed in the agricultural industry such as in soil analysis. Research fields such as drug 
discovery and natural products chemistry use a wide variety of analytical chemistry tools. The 
pharmaceutical industry uses modern analytical methods to check drug impurities while in 
sports games, doping control is another application of chemical analysis (Zolotov, 2014). 
1.4.1 Chromatography 
For the analysis and detection of the compounds of a mixture, a preceding step is their 
separation. The separation of small molecules and in this case, fungal secondary metabolites, 
is achieved with the chromatographic process. Chromatography involves two phases, the 
mobile phase and the solid phase comprised of powdered particles. In a basic scheme, a 
sample mixture is added in a column which contains the solid phase, followed by addition of 
the mobile phase (liquid) which is let to flow by gravity through the stationary phase. The 
components of the sample are carried by the mobile phase and separate by moving in 
different speeds through the column. Depending on the state of the mobile phase, gas or 
liquid, chromatography can be divided in two types, gas chromatography and liquid 
chromatography, respectively. Another classification is based on the type of separation. 
Separations based on differences in size and charge, are made in size-exclusion and ion-
exchange chromatography. In normal and reversed phase chromatography, separations are 
based on polarity. (Rouessac & Rouessac, 2013) 
Modern liquid chromatography was invented at the beginning of the 20th century by Michael 
Tswett (Rouessac & Rouessac, 2013), is widely used since then and has been evolved through 
the years to the current state of the art UHPLC (ultra-high performance liquid 
chromatography) method. 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
 
 
Figure 18. Basic scheme of an HPLC system (p. 20, Snyder et al. 2010) 
In HPLC, the mobile phase is sent with pumps through the column, where it flows and leads 
the injected sample to separation of its components based on their chemical affinity for the 
stationary phase. As the mobile phase is constantly added, it washes off these separated 
components and leads to their exit of the column or elution. A detector at the exit of the 
column such as a UV or mass spectrometry detector records the signal of the separation and 
the plot of this signal with time is called a chromatogram. (Snyder, Kirkland, & Dolan, 2010).  
The basic components of an HPLC system, as shown in fig. 18 ,are: the reservoir of the mobile 
phase, the pump, the autosampler, the column enclosed in the column oven, the detector 
and the computer which is connected with the system (Snyder et al. 2010).  
Reversed phase chromatography (RPC) 
RPC separation is based on the polarity of the molecules to be analysed and is the most 
common chromatographic method used in chemical analysis as it can be adjusted and at the 
end detect a wide variety of molecules. In contrast to normal phase chromatography, RPC is 
characterised by a polar mobile phase and a non-polar stationary phase which is found inside 
the tube of the column. The mobile phase is usually composed of water mixed with an organic 
solvent such as methanol, acetonitrile or isopropanol (Rouessac & Rouessac 2013). The 
spherical particles of the column (1.5 to 5 μm diameter) are usually made of silica (Snyder et 
al. 2010).The hydrophobic stationary phase is attached to the silica support particles and can 
be octyl (C8), octyldecyl (C18), hexyl-phenyl, cyano and amino. If the molecules or solutes are 
polar, they are ‘’attracted’’ to the mobile phase and are leaving early the column and thus 
they have a shorter retention time. Less polar compounds are attached to the stationary 
phase and it is more difficult to be taken by the running mobile phase, and thus are present 
for more time inside the column and have a longer retention time.  
An important parameter for understanding the polarity of a compound is LogD. LogD is the 
logarithmic value of the solute concentration in 1-octanol divided by the solute concentration 
in water. The lower the LogD, the more hydrophilic the compound is while a higher LogD value 
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
 
indicates that the analyte is more hydrophobic. LogD refers to both the neutral and charged 
forms of the analyte found in both distribution systems. 
1.4.2 Spectroscopy 
 
Detectors 
UV/Vis detectors represent the most common types of detectors incorporated at the HPLC 
systems. The detection is based on spectrophotometry and apart from the detectors, there is 
a light source and a dispersive system or a monochromator. The light source for UV/Vis 
spectrophotometry is usually represented by a tungsten lamp (wavelength range: 320-1100 
nm) for the visible region and a deuterium lamp (wavelength range: 190-370 nm) for the 
ultraviolet region. There are three types of UV/Vis detectors: the fixed wavelength, the 
variable wavelength and the diode-array detectors (Snyder et al. 2010). In the diode array 
detector systems, the light passes through the sample, it is absorbed, dispersed and all the 
wavelengths can be measured at the same time by the detector (Rouessac & Rouessac 2013).  
 
 
Figure 19. Scheme of a diode-array UV detector. (p. 165, Snyder et al. 2010) 
Specifically, the diode array is set after the column and the mobile phase (sample) is passing 
through a flow cell where it accepts the light in the range of 190-1100 nm (fig. 19). The light 
then passes the diffraction grating, is dispersed and the numerous photodiodes accept the 
light, translate its intensity it into signals and finally an absorption spectrum is generated 
(Rouessac & Rouessac 2013) (Snyder et al. 2010). Optical detectors such as UV/Vis found in 
the HPLC system can be affected by temperature changes and bubbles present in the mobile 
phase. In those cases, technical solutions such as capillary heat exchangers and degassers of 
the mobile phase can ensure the normal function of the detectors (Snyder et al. 2010).   
The organic molecules that can be detected with UV/Vis spectroscopy are those with 
conjugated double bonds. Those molecules have alternating single and double bonds and can 
absorb light in the UV/Vis spectrum. The sites of the compounds with such conjugated 
==PAGE:28==
 
28 
 
systems giving the property of absorption are called chromophores (Rouessac & Rouessac 
2013) 
Mass spectrometry 
Mass spectrometry is a technique that measures the mass to charge ratio (m/z) of ions. 
Coupling liquid chromatography with mass spectrometry provides an important analytical 
tool for the identification of known and unknown compounds present in a sample. A mass 
spectrometer coupled with an HPLC system is comprised of an ionization source, one or more 
mass analysers, a detector for measuring the abundance of the ions and a data system 
(Hoffmann & Stroobant, 2007). A common ionization source is the electrospray ionization 
(ESI). ESI together with atmospheric pressure chemical ionization (APCI), atmospheric 
pressure photo ionization (APPI), desorption ESI (DESI) and direct analysis in real time (DART) 
fell into the category of atmospheric pressure ionization techniques (API) (Hoffmann & 
Stroobant, 2007). 
 
 
Electrospray Ionization (ESI) 
In ESI which is a soft ionization technique, the liquid sample coming from the chromatographic 
separation enters the apparatus and as it goes through a metal capillary, a current from a 
high-voltage power supply is applied and leads to formation of charged droplets that contain 
the analytes and the solvent. The potential difference between the two electrodes of the 
supply is 3-6 kV (Hoffmann & Stroobant, 2007). The spraying of the liquid from the capillary 
is done in atmospheric pressure conditions (Gross, 2017). Then, a release of hot nitrogen gas 
contributes to the evaporation of the solvent. As the solvent evaporates, the charge density 
increases and a coulomb explosion is generated giving gas phase charged ions. Depending on 
the potential applied, the gas ions are positively or negatively charged. An early ES interface 
is shown on fig. 20. 
 
Figure 20. Scheme of an early ES ion source interface (p. 567, Gross 2017) 
==PAGE:29==
 
29 
 
In both positive and negative ESI modes, the analytes can form ions with already present 
elements such as hydrogen, sodium and potassium. The formed ions are called adducts. 
Common positively charged adducts are [M+H]+  (also called protonated molecule), [M+Na]+  
, [M+K]+, [M+NH4]+, where M is one of the analytes. Dimer adducts can also be formed such 
as [2M+Na]+. By interpreting adduct formation and patterns in the MS spectra generated from 
the data system of the spectrometer, one can find the mass of the molecular ion and thus the 
mass of a molecule present in the sample. 
Quadrupole – Time of flight mass analyser (QTOF-MS) 
When the gas ions are produced, the next step of the spectrometric process is the mass 
analysis. The quadrupole mass analyser coupled with the time-of-flight mass spectrometer is 
a powerful tool for m/z identification of the charged ions. In the basic quadrupole structure, 
four metal rods, placed in parallel, accept two kinds of power: constant and radio-frequency 
voltage. By changing the voltage settings and consequently the electric field, it is possible for 
specific mass-to-charge ratio ions to be isolated. These ions can go through the empty space 
of the separator between the rods  towards the detector while the others collide at the metal 
rods. (Harris, 2010) 
In the time-of-flight (TOF) mass spectrometer, the inserted ions after accelerating through 
electricity, they reach an area where they all have the same kinetic energy (½mv2) since there 
is no field applied. At this point, the heavier ones move slower while the lighter move faster 
and reach the detector accordingly. In the meantime, a reflectron, which is an electrostatic 
mirror, stops the moving ions, reflects them and forces them to move again until they reach 
the detector. The final scope of the reflectron is to increase the spectrometer’s mass accuracy 
by ensuring that the ions’ difference in reaching the detector relies only on their mass 
differences. (Harris, 2010). QTOF-MS has also the capability of performing tandem mass 
spectrometry (MS/MS). In that case, specific precursor ions fragment through a collision cell 
into product ions (Chernushevich, Loboda, & Thomson, 2001). MS/MS spectra patterns can 
be useful in identifying molecules by comparing them with already obtained MS/MS spectra 
found in databases. 
 1.4.3 Data analysis 
The identification of known compounds in sample mixtures analysed in LC-UV-MS, termed 
dereplication, is a fast way of assigning the unknown chromatogram peaks with compounds 
already enrolled in UV-MS related databases while during the first steps of identification, 
positive mode ESI is the most reliable method for finding adduct patterns and identifying the 
molecular ion (K. F. Nielsen, Månsson, Rank, Frisvad, & Larsen, 2011). Searching for known 
compounds is done with extracting the extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) of the positive or 
negative molecular ion and comparing with that of the reference standard to see if it is the 
same, by comparing UV spectra or by extracting the MS/MS spectrum and then searching 
through MS/MS databases for the known compound and also comparing the spectra. When 
looking at unknown compounds, a common method is to extract the base peak 
chromatogram of the positive mode ESI, select specific peaks and then looking at the MS 
==PAGE:30==
 
30 
 
spectrum data to find adduct patterns in order to identify the [M+H]+ ion. The major mass 
spectrum peaks are then compared, using the process of deconvolution, in order to find if 
they belong in the same or in a different compound. Deconvolution is performed by 
comparing the extracted ion chromatograms of those peaks. Then, based on the mass of the 
molecular ion, or of the other adducts and different hypothetical formulas, a search in 
databases such as Antibase, is performed to find further information including UV data that 
will help with the identification of the compound and the conclusion whether it is an already 
known or unknown compound. The process of identifying a known compound, 3-MOA is 
presented below in fig. 21 and fig. 22. For a more detailed version and for the process of

2.3 Growth media 
Two distinct types of growth media were used for the cultivation of the strains, YES (Yeast 
extract sucrose agar) and Minimal Media (MM), both supplemented with arginine. After the 
inoculation of each strain in the plates, the strains were cultivated  in triplicates, at 37oC for 
7 days in the dark and the extraction was done on the seventh day. For the preparation of 
YES media, the following ingredients were mixed (for 1L of broth): 20 g of yeast extract, 150 
g of sucrose, 0.5 g of MgSO4 (7H2O), 20 g of agar, 1 ml of tracemetal solution and 885 ml of 
Milli-Q water. For the preparation of MM, the following ingredients were mixed for 1 L of 
broth: 10 g of glucose, 10 ml of NaNO3, 20 ml of mineral mix, 20 g of agar and 1000 mL of 
Milli-Q water. After that, the bottles were autoclaved at 121oC for 15 minutes, sterile 
arginine solution was supplemented. The liquid broth was then poured into plates for solid 
media preparation. 
 
2.4 Plug extraction 
At the seventh day of incubation, 6 mm plugs were cut from 5 spots across a colony of the 
plate and placed into 2 mL Eppendorf tubes. Then, 1.5 mL of ethylacetate:isopropanol 3:1 
v/v with 1% formic acid, solvent was added into the tubes. The tubes then were sonicated at 
an ultrasonication bath for 60 minutes. 1 ml of each Eppendorf tube was then transferred 
into a new vial and the liquid was let to evaporate with supply of nitrogen gas while heated 
at 35oC. Then, 200 μl of methanol were added to each vial which were then placed into the 
ultrasonication bath for 10 minutes. The vials were then centrifuged for 3 minutes at 13.400 
g and finally 150 μl of each supernatant was added to a clean HPLC vial. (Jorn Smedsgaard, 
1997) 
LC-MS

these three systems: a UHPLC Agilent 1290 Infinity system (Agilent Technologies, Santa

system with a Jet stream ionization source. The chromatographic column was an Agilent

done at temperature conditions of 60 οC. The solvents were: water with 0.1% formic acid 
and acetonitrile (ACN), both of HPLC grade. The flow rate of the solvents was 0.35 mL/min.

method with a duration of 20 minutes starting from 10% ACN to 100% ACN for 15 minutes, 
then 100% ACN was running for 2 minutes and finally 10% ACN was running for the last 2.9

4. Conclusions and Future Perspective: 
 
This project aimed at providing a more detailed description of the 3-MOA/cichorine 
biosynthetic pathway as known from the literature. The analysis of the metabolites of the 
gene deletion strains resulted to some major findings.  
First, the pyrone compound #16 is a pathway related shunt 3-MOA product and is relate with 
the overexpression of the pathway’s transcription factor while cichoarginine and the dimer 
compound 29, clearly seem to be related to the 3-MOA pathway.  
Second, the deletion of the NRPS-like gene led to the appearance of two major peaks the 
pyrone #16 and 3-MOA clearly indicating that the NRPS-like enzyme is the first enzyme acting 
on 3-MOA. At the same strain, the loss of nidulol is an indication for the role of the enzyme in 
lactone formation. However, a clear demethylnidulol peak (confirmed by MS/MS) is seen 
which is contradictory, so not a clear conclusion can be made.  
Third, the deletion of the CYP450 gene seems to an aldehyde major product #52. At the same 
time, neither nidulol nor demethylnidulol are produced, strongly indicating that the CYP450 
enzyme is a first step towards nidulol leading from the O-methylated-3-MOA compound 21 
to a likely aldehyde intermediate 55. At the same time the formation of the aldehyde product 
#52 confirms the initial action of the NRPS like enzyme since another study has shown such 
an enzyme to catalyse reduction of a carboxylic acid (Wang, Beissner, & Zhao, 2014). The 
NRPS-like enzyme thus seems to have an initial role in the pathway leading to a different 
branch than towards nidulol.  
Fourth, O-methylation seems to be more promiscuous, in the way that we detect a lot of both 
aldehyde intermediates, #56 and #57. 
Finally, in addition to cichoarginine, other ‘cicho-amino acid derived’ compounds such as 
‘cicholysine’ seem to be present while it is unclear which enzymes are involved at such 
integration of the amino-acids. 
The focus of future projects in relation with these findings could be on creating new deletion 
strains followed by their metabolites’ analysis. The gene deletions could involve also the other 
orsellinic acid related polyketide synthase genes such as the 3,5 dimethylorsellinic acid PKS 
and the orsellinic acid PKS, in order to confirm more 3-MOA pathway related compounds and 
clarify the role of the enzymes forming nidulol and cichorine.
