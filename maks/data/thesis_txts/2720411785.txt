Abstract 
 
Background: Health concerns regarding sugar and artificial sweeteners has put focus on 
finding alternatives in the form of natural sweeteners. Brazzein and monellin (MNEI) are sweet 
tasting proteins originally derived from the fruits of West African plants. They have the benefits 
of being several thousand times sweeter than sucrose on a weight basis and do not influence 
blood sugar levels. However, their applicability in food industry is limited because their 
sweetness depends on an intact tertiary structure which is degraded at high temperatures. 
Improved stability of the tertiary structure is therefore desired, and can be obtained by protein 
engineering. For heterologous expression of sweet proteins, Saccharomyces cerevisiae is a 
promising host because it is generally considered as safe (GRAS) and can therefore enable 
direct application of sweet proteins. In the present work, expression of sweet proteins and 
establishment of a pipeline for testing mutated sweet protein variants was comprised. 
Methods: Mutated variants of brazzein and MNEI were created by designing suitable primers 
for PCR mutagenesis. Sweet proteins with a C-terminal His-tag were cloned into the CPOTud 
expression vector by Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent (USER) cloning and fusion in 
competent E. coli cells. From here, plasmids were purified and transformed into S. cerevisiae 
strain CEN.PK 530‐1CK. The expression and purity were analysed by SDS-PAGE and western 
blot, and thermostability analyses were carried out by Differential Scanning Fluorometry 
(DSF). 
Results: The results verified the heterologous expression of Brazzein and MNEI in S. 
cerevisiae and successful purification with His-tag at C-terminal. Expression of brazzein was 
higher than the expression of MNEI. The expression and purity were significantly improved 
when the sweet proteins were cultivated in the minimal medium, Delft, in contrary to when 
they were expressed in the defined medium, SD-2xSCAA.  
Conclusion: Using S. cerevisiae as a host for the expression of sweet proteins opens new 
insights for providing new sweet yeasts that can be used as food additives.

1     Aim of Thesis  
 
This thesis aims to obtain heterologous expression of the sweet proteins brazzein and monellin 
in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, also called Baker’s yeast, and test the thermostability of 
engineered variants. Amino acid substitutions predicted to increase the thermostability of the 
sweet proteins were executed in silico by collaborators of this study. However, to figure out 
the actual behaviour of the protein it needs to be analysed ex vivo and part of this depends on 
whether the protein first can be expressed in vivo. Therefore, this study will establish an 
experimental pipeline for the production and analysis of sweet protein mutants. Finding 
thermostable variants of sweet proteins is desired to broaden their applications in the food 
industry. On top of that, a direct application of a sweet-protein-producing Baker’s yeast is 
desirable as it would abolish the need for downstream purification.  
 
 
2     Introduction  
 
2.1     Properties and Problematics of Commonly Used Sweeteners 
To achieve a better understanding of why research is put into finding and developing novel 
sweeteners such as sweet proteins, the following sections will explain the properties, 
physiological effects, and potential health concerns of sugars and artificial sweeteners, which 
are commonly used in the food industry. Lastly, presenting sweet proteins as a novel sweetener. 
 2.1.1     Sugars 
The increased availability of products with added sugars has paralleled an increase in sugar 
intake.1 World Health Organization (WHO) has acknowledged that excess sugar consumption 
has become a public health concern.2 Sugar molecules are simple carbohydrates that in nature 
are mostly present in an intact cellular/tissue matrix, such as inside fruits and vegetables.3 The 
problem with overconsumption occurs when the food industry refines sugar from these sources, 
such as sucrose from sugar beets and canes, and adds them to several food products and 
beverages. Intake of refined sugars influences the rate at which sugars are digested and 
absorbed, and further influences the glycaemic response and metabolic fate. The main 
metabolic fate of sugars is that they are converted into glucose - the main source of energy for 
the body’s cells and a significant driver of glycemia, which is the amount of glucose in the 
blood leading to insulin secretion. Insulin is the main anabolic hormone in the body that 
promotes the absorption of excess glucose from the body into glycogen in the liver and skeletal 
muscles, and excess is further stored as fat.4 The effects of refined sugars have been widely 
studied and research has shown that excessive intake of free sugars is associated with dental 
caries, obesity, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, abnormal levels of lipids in the blood, 
cardiovascular diseases, and fatty liver disease.5–7 In addition, overconsumption of sugars can 
lead to changes in neural systems, altered emotional processing, anxiety, and depression.8

2.1.2    Artificial Sweeteners 
With the increased awareness of health risks related to sugar, artificial sweeteners have been 
utilized for sugar substitution or reduction. The first artificial sweetener was marketed in 1879 
in the USA, called saccharin. Other examples include acesulfame, aspartame, and sucralose. 
The benefit of artificial sweeteners is that they provide a sweet flavour with few or no calories.9 
On top of this, they have a high potency of sweetness, which means they are more effective at 
sweetening than sucrose on a weight-for-weight basis, resulting in lower quantities needed to 
obtain the same sweetness. For example, aspartame is 200 times sweeter than sucrose on a 
weight basis. However, the physical and chemical properties of these compounds differ from 
that of sucrose, causing them to have different sensory characteristics.10,11  Because of this, 
aspartame is one of the most used artificial sweeteners nowadays due to its sweet taste without 
the bitter chemical or metallic aftertaste reported in other artificial sweeteners. However, it is 
not a perfect replacement for sugar, because it interacts with other food flavours, meaning that 
recipes need to be changed accordingly. Aspartame is made of two amino acids: aspartic acid 
and phenylalanine, of which the latter has been modified by adding a methyl group which gives 
aspartame its sweet taste.12,13 Several artificial sweeteners are generally considered as safe 
(GRAS) and have been approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA). Despite 
this, the safety of these compounds is a quite controversial topic. Recent studies have suggested 
that there might be a link between artificial sweeteners and the adverse health effects they are 
supposed to prevent, such as type 2 diabetes and obesity.14,15 On top of that, links between 
artificial sweeteners and certain types of cancer have been found.16 However, many of these 
studies have been criticized for having flaws in their design with unclear cause-effect 
relationships due to unreliable sample sizes and lack of follow-up studies.17  
 
2.1.3     Sweet Proteins as Novel Sweeteners 
Because of the health concerns regarding both sugar and artificial sweeteners in modern times, 
natural sweeteners, such as sweet proteins have gained much interest.18 Sweet proteins are a 
group of proteins that can elicit a sweet sensation in humans. To date, eight proteins have been 
found as sweet or sweet taste-modifying, these are monellin, thaumatin, brazzein, mabinlin, 
miraculin, neoculin, and lysozyme.19–25 They are naturally occurring in the berries of tropical 
plants found in Africa and Asia, apart from lysozyme which is found in egg white. None of 
these proteins share any structure or sequence homology.26 Thaumatin is the only sweet protein 
that has already been commercialized and approved by the FDA to date, as it has been 
extensively studied between 1975 and 1985 in regards to its safety, in which no health concerns 
have been found according to a review by Gibbs et al.27 A recent paper by Lynch et al. found 
that brazzein can be considered a safe sweetener based on in silico allergenicity tests, in vitro 
genotoxicity, and a 90-day dietary oral toxicity study in rats.28 The metabolic fate of sweet 
proteins has been found to be like that of other proteins in our diet; degraded into their 
constituent amino acids.29 On top of that, only small quantities are needed for a sweet taste, as 
thaumatin is 3000 times sweeter than sucrose on a weight basis.30 This thesis focuses on the 
sweet proteins brazzein and monellin because they are some of the most researched, thus 
enabling comparisons of this study to the literature. Like thaumatin, they are also very high-
potency sweeteners, with brazzein being 2000 times sweeter and monellin being 3000 times

sweeter than sucrose on a weight basis.18,31 In regards to their taste profiles, monellin has a 
slow onset of sweetness and a lingering aftertaste32, while the taste of brazzein is even similar 
to sucrose with no metallic aftertaste and it effectively reduces the aftertaste of other sugar 
substitutes when used in combination.33 
Sweet proteins are, however, limited in their use because the activity of the sweet proteins 
depends on their three-dimensional structure, and thus they are very sensitive to extreme 
physiochemical conditions during food-processing.34 Specifically, if sweet proteins should be 
utilized in baked food products, they would need to retain their sweet taste after being exposed 
to approximately 98 °C for 20-45 min. This thesis, therefore, investigates protein engineering 
approaches for improving the thermostability of sweet proteins and realizing this utilization.35 
 
2.2     Sweet Proteins – Structure, Stability, and Activity 
Prior to the framework of improving the thermostability of sweet proteins, it is crucial to 
understand the structure/activity relationship, as the structure of sweet proteins is what allows 
them to have their activity, which is eliciting a sweet taste.34 The following sections will present 
the correlation of structure, activity, and stability of the sweet proteins in this thesis. 
   
2.2.1     General Protein Structure and Stability 
When doing protein engineering, the primary structure is altered, which then might further 
affect the secondary and tertiary structure. The primary structure is defined by the order in 
which amino acids are bound together through covalent peptide bonds. Amino acids are 
molecules with a similar backbone structure consisting of an amino group and carboxyl group 
while they have unique side chains that can be divided into four categories: nonpolar, polar 
uncharged, negatively charged, and positively charged. The secondary structure of a protein is 
determined by hydrogen bonds between the backbones which form structures like α-helices, β-
pleated sheets, β-turns, and Ω-loops. Many regions of different secondary structures can be 
present in the same protein molecule. Hydrogen bonds are also present in the tertiary structure. 
The tertiary structure is defined by interactions that occur between the variable groups. Other 
interactions that make up this structure are ion bonds, disulphide bonds, hydrophilic 
interactions, hydrophobic interactions, and Van der Waals forces.36 If disulphide bonds are part 
of the tertiary structure, the proteins need to undergo a post-translational modification in the 
endoplasmic reticulum (ER) which involves the protein being in the presence of an oxidizing 
buffer in the ER.37 
The activity of proteins can be lost if the structure is denatured, which means the interactions 
in the protein are broken apart. High temperatures can denature the tertiary and secondary 
structure of a protein because it increases the kinetic energy, causing the molecules to vibrate 
rapidly and violently before disrupting the bonds. The thermostability of some proteins also 
depends on the pH of the environment, as it affects ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds. In detail, 
a protein will be less thermostable if either amino acid taking part of an ionic bond or a 
hydrogen bond loses its charge due to pH being higher or lower than its pKa. Changes in pH

have also been found to trigger precipitation, in which proteins misfold and associate into 
larger, insoluble structures. Other factors that can affect protein stability are proteases, 
mechanical forces, heavy metal ions, and whether the environment is oxidizing or reducing.36,38 
 
2.2.2    The Activity of Sweet Proteins  
The sweet sensation of sweet proteins is triggered as they interact with the sweet-tasting 
receptor on the tongue. The sweetness is perceived by humans, apes, and Old-World monkeys 
but is not perceived as sweet by other species including New World monkeys, mice, and 
rats.39,40 The human sweet-tasting receptor is a heterodimeric G-protein coupled receptor 
(GPCR) consisting of the two subunits T1R2 and T1R3. GPCRs are receptors only found in 
the membrane of eukaryotic cells where they allow for the detection of extracellular stimuli. 
When they are bound to external ligands a conformational change is induced which triggers 
internal signal pathways. The sweet-tasting receptor has like other GPCRs a seven alpha helices 
transmembrane domain (TMD) but in addition, it has a large extracellular domain, called the 
Venus Flytrap Domain (VFD), where ligands can bind.41 Different sweet substances are 
recognised by different regions of the VFD.42 The exact mechanism by which sweet proteins 
interact with T1R2-T1R3 has not yet been fully elucidated. However, it has been hypothesized 
that proteins binding to the sweet receptor can be described with the wedge model, as seen in 
Figure 1.42,43 This model suggests that wedge-shaped structures of the sweet proteins bind on 
a large surface overlapping the binding cleft of one subunit, with additional contacts on the 
other subunit.34,44 The wedge model has been built using a homology model of the T1R2-T1R3 
receptor, based on the structure of the metabotropic glutamate receptor mGluR1.45 The 
applicability of this model has been verified by successful docking studies of sweet protein 
variants that have proven to be sweet through sensory analyses.18 Meaning it allows the 
detection of contact points between the sweet protein and T1R2-T1R3. However, it is unable 
to individuate subtle differences in closely related proteins.18 A common correlation that has 
been found through mutations and analysing with both the wedge model and sensory analysis 
is that the surface charge of thaumatin, monellin, and brazzein is important for the binding to 
T1R2-T1R3. Mutations of residues increasing the negative charge would consistently decrease 
or even cancel sweetness, whereas the outcome of the introduction of a positive charge is not 
immediately predictable.46,47

2.2.3     Brazzein (WT)   
Brazzein is the smallest and most stable of the identified sweet proteins. In 1994 it was isolated 
and characterized as a 6.49 kDa monomer comprised of 54 amino acid residues with an 
isoelectric point of 5.4. Moreover, it is a highly soluble protein as it is soluble in concentrations 
of more than 50 g/L.21 The structure of brazzein has been characterized by X-ray 
crystallography made with a 1.8 Å resolution.49 This shows a secondary structure of two α-
helices at residues 13-17 and 21-30, and three β-sheets at residues 4-6, 34-39, and 45-50. 
Although, this differs from previous Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) studies of brazzein, 
as these do not report an α-helix at residues 13-17, indicating that the structure of brazzein 
might differ depending on whether it is in solid state or solution or depending on the pH in 
which these structural experiments were carried out.50,51 Regarding the tertiary structure, most 
notable is the eight cysteine residues that form four disulphide bonds, C4–C52, C16–C37, C22–
C47 and C26–C49 as can be seen in Figure 2. These disulphide bonds have been found to 
contribute to the stability of brazzein.52 Brazzein is stable in a pH range from 2.5-8 and 
thermostable at 80 °C for 4 hours and 100 °C for 30 min21. On top of stability, the disulphide 
bonds play a major role in the structure and activity, as mutations that alter de disulphide bonds 
lead to non-sweet protein variants.53 Other residues that have been found important for 
sweetness are loop residues 39-43 specifically the charge R43, C-terminal residues, β-sheet 
residue E36, loop residue R33, and loop residues 9-19. Mutations in these regions have been 
found to either decrease or increase the sweetness of brazzein.47,54–56

2.2.3     Monellin (MNEI)  
Wild-type monellin is not very stable as it is a heterodimer composed of two non-covalently 
linked chains, A and B, which dissociate when heated above 50 °C at acidic pH, leading to loss 
of sweetness.57 To circumvent the low melting temperature caused by the weak interactions of 
the two dimers, single-chain derivatives with higher thermostability have been designed, MNEI 
is one of them. MNEI was engineered in 1989 by fusing the two chains of wild-type monellin 
with a G-F dipeptide linker.58 This engineered variant has the same sweetness as native 
monellin but with a higher melting temperature. Different studies have found different melting 
temperatures. Lee et al. found a thermostability of about 70 °C at pH 8.0 and Leone found it to 
be 77 °C at pH 8.0.18,59 On top of that it has been found to renature easily after heating to 100 
°C at low pH.58 It is the MNEI variant of monellin that is focused upon in this thesis because 
it has been used as a starting point for many other studies that investigate the thermostability 
and sweetness of monellin. From here on, monellin is therefore going to be referred to as 
MNEI. MNEI is comprised of 96 amino acid residues as seen in Figure 3, has a molecular 
weight of 11.8 kDa, and displays a tendency to aggregate and form dimers and multimers, 
particularly at neutral to alkaline pHs.60,61 The structure of MNEI has been characterized by X-
Ray crystallography with a 1.15 Å resolution and NMR studies.61,62 Its secondary structure 
consists of an α-helix and a five-strand anti-parallel β-sheets62. MNEI has no disulphide bonds 
as it only contains one cysteine. Hydrophobic interactions, conformational entropy, and 
hydrogen bonding are believed to contribute most to monellin protein stability32. The flexibility 
of certain residues might play an important role for the sweetness of MNEI63. For instance, 
mutation G16A, involving a buried residue of MNEI, only modifies the protein flexibility but 
induces a nearly complete loss of the sweet taste64. On the other hand, a mutation that has 
improved sweetness is Y65R which introduced an extra positive surface18. Other residues that 
have been found to be important for sweetness are G1, E2, G16, R39, and C41.32,65,66

2.2.4     Thaumatin (WT) 
Thaumatin is not one of the sweet proteins that are the focus on this thesis, but because it is 
used as a control, some of its most relevant characteristics are mentioned here. It is a 22 kDa 
monomer with 207 amino acid residues and an isoelectric point of almost 1220. Thaumatin 
contains eight intramolecular disulphide bonds and no free cysteines67. It denatures upon 
heating above 70 °C at pH 7.0, whereupon its sweetness disappears. However, at a pH of 2 it 
remained sweet after heating at 80 °C for 4 h68. 
 
2.3     Protein Engineering for Improved Thermostability  
As explained in previous sections, sweet proteins retain their capability to interact with the 
sweet taste receptor only when correctly folded. Protein engineering is a valuable tool to make 
the tertiary structure of sweet proteins withhold the wide range of physio-chemical conditions, 
such as high temperatures they might encounter during food processing. The following sections 
aim to provide a state-of-the-art of what measures have been taken to improve thermostability 
in MNEI and brazzein and present other methods that have not yet been attempted to date.   
 
2.3.1     State of the Art - Testing and Improving Thermostability of Brazzein and MNEI 
This section will investigate the experimental approaches and findings that have been carried 
out for improving thermostability of brazzein and MNEI. However, no studies attempting to 
improve thermostability of brazzein was found. This is possibly because the wildtype of 
brazzein is already quite heat stable. On the other hand, improvement of the thermostability of 
MNEI has been investigated to a further extent. Advancements within this field are quite new: 
studies were mainly published in the span between year 2012 to 2022. The studies found are 
by Lee et al., Rega et al., Liu et al, Leone et al., Leone & Picone, Zheng et al., Zhao et al., 
Weiffert & Linse, and Delfi et al.18,32,59,60,69–73 . An overview of the mutations these studies 
have tested, and their findings, can be seen in Table 1. However, bear in mind that the 
thermostability values have been rounded for simplification, and the accurate values and 
belonging standard deviations can be found in the respective articles. In general, these studies 
found that one or more amino acid substitutions can have a huge impact on both thermostability 
and sweetness. Another interesting aspect is that these studies have quite a few things in 
common regarding their methods. They all expressed MNEI and MNEI-variants in E. coli 
except for Lee et al., who expressed MNEI in tobacco chloroplasts. Purifications were carried 
out in nondenaturing conditions either by nickel column affinity chromatography, ion exchange 
chromatography, or size exclusion chromatography. In most cases the purified proteins were 
followed by analysis on SDS-PAGE to show successful expression and purification. For 
thermostability analysis, circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy was widely utilized among the 
studies and all studies carried out a sensory analysis of their mutants. On top of these analyses, 
most of the studies provided different additional analyses, such as protein behaviour at different 
pH and structural analyses by X-ray crystallography.  The rationales for which mutations they 
chose to investigate were in most cases either based on predictions by in silico analyses or 
based on what had been found in previous studies in regards to either sweetness or stability. It 
can be said to be a combined effort which in 2021 led Delfi et al. to report the most thermostable

2.3.2   Other Potential Strategies for Finding Thermostable Sweet Mutants 
The ability to design thermostable proteins is practically useful. However, no single method 
has been found to be fully reliable.75 Therefore, it is relevant to try out different approaches. A 
recent in silico study by Tang et al. has proposed potentially thermostable mutations of brazzein 
and monellin, thaumatin, and curculin based on predicted free energy difference.76 They tested 
all possible combinations of single amino acid substitutions and expect the most promising 
mutants to be the ones with the most negative free energy difference values. For example, E23I 
was proposed to be a promising mutation for MNEI and D50Y for brazzein. These amino acid 
substitutions have not yet been tested in practice, but doing so could provide valuable 
information within the field. Another potential approach, which has not yet been attempted for 
improving thermostability of practice of sweet proteins, is disulphide engineering. The 
introduction of novel disulphide bonds into proteins has in other cases been used extensively 
to improve protein stability, modify functional characteristics, and to assist in the study of 
protein dynamics.77 Successful use of this technology is greatly enhanced by software which 
can predict pairs of residues that will likely form a disulphide bond if mutated to cysteines. The 
web-based program “Disulfide by Design” has been developed by Craig et al to predict 
stabilizing disulphide bridges that are both likely to form and improve thermal stability based 
on high B-factor values.77 
 
2.4     S. cerevisiae as a Host for Heterologous Sweet Protein Production 
2.4.1     General Advantages 
Investigation of potentially improved properties in the sweet protein variants relies on these 
being produced and purified first. The tropical fruits in which the sweet proteins are naturally 
expressed have the downsides of being unable to grow outside their natural habitat and yields 
in the fruit are very low e.g. 0.3-0.5% occurrence of monellin in the fruit.9 On top of this, 
growing the plant would be very time consuming. Besides that, engineering tools have not been 
developed for these plants that would enable production of variants. On the other hand, 
recombinant production of sweet proteins in S. cerevisiae, also known as Baker’s yeast, is 
favourable for many reasons. S. cerevisiae is a suitable host for food-grade products due to its 
status as a substance that the US FDA has designated “generally recognized as safe” 
(GRAS). Moreover, it exhibits a fast growth rate and possess the capability for post-
translational modifications and extracellular secretion.78,79 The capability of S. cerevisiae to 
properly fold proteins is accompanied by their ability to produce chaperonins that can help in 
the folding of certain proteins and can handle proteins rich in disulphide bridges.80 The 
mechanisms of S. cerevisiae can be utilized because genetic manipulation is relatively easy and 
widely accessible due to the available collections of genetic tools.81 Altogether, this organism 
is promising for producing mutated variants of sweet proteins and could in the long term allow 
for direct application, such as secreting sweet proteins during the fermentation of bread dough.

2.4.2     S. cerevisiae Strain Optimized for Protein Production 
In this thesis, an S. cerevisiae strain engineered by Huang et al., was utilized.82 They modified 
S. cerevisiae by single gene deletions of gene targets involved in the trafficking and secretory 
pathway and tested the synergistic effects of these mutations to produce fungal α-amylase. 
Their best strain contained 4 deletions (Δhda2, Δvps5, Δtda3, and Δgos1) and enhanced protein 
production in fed-batch more than fivefold with a yield of 2.5 g/L α-amylase and less than 10% 
retained within the cell. They saw similar improvements with another heterologous protein, 
1,4-α-glucosidase.82 Besides the modifications carried out in the study by Huang et al., they 
refer to articles by Liu et al. and Wittrup et al.83,84 that have investigated the most promising 
expression systems and media compositions for α-amylase production in S. cerevisiae, 
respectively. The expression of protein was done with the CPOTud expression system. 
CPOTud is a vector that use the POT1 gene from S. pombe as marker to complement 
the tpi1 deletion in the host. The Triosephosphate isomerase (TPI1) gene is a critical gene in 
both glycolysis and gluconeogenesis, which means a Δtpi1 strain does not grow on glucose as 
the sole carbon source and grows very slowly on other carbon sources.85 Therefore, a Δtpi1 
strain containing POT1 plasmid allows high plasmid stability. It has even been reported to yield 
a higher copy number than auxotrophic markers.83 Moreover, it is a 2µ plasmid, which allows 
for high copy numbers and has the TPI1 promoter that allows for constitutive expression of the 
desired recombinant protein.86,87 For ensuring secretion of the recombinant protein into the 
culture media, it has a glycosylated alpha factor leader in the N-terminal of the recombinant 
protein, carrying information for protein secretion.88 SD-2xSCAA medium was developed by 
Wittrup et al. who in the production of α-amylase achieved a 4-fold increase in saturated growth 
levels, a 70% increase in specific secretion rate, and an 8-fold increase in volumetric secretion 
productivity in comparison to SD minimal medium.84

3.2     Strain Library Construction 
3.2.1     Design of MNEI and brazzein variants   
Different in silico and bioinformatics approaches created suggestions for mutations that could 
potentially increase thermostability. These mutation suggestions came from Baker Lab institute 
for protein design (University of Washington), Gonzalo Nahuel Bidart Costoya (Novo Nordisk 
Foundation Center for Biosustainability), and a published article by Tang et al.76 Baker Lab 
utilized a bioinformatics approach of which all the resulting suggestions were chosen for this 
study, and Gonzalo utilized web-based tools such as “Disulphide by Design 2” and 
“Fireprot”.77,89 These suggestions were further narrowed down for this study, by favouring the 
mutants with the most negative free energy difference and/or highest B-factor. From the paper 
by Tang et al., this study only chose to only investigate the most promising mutations based on 
their lowest Predicted FoldX ΔΔG value.76 Which mutations that were based on what procedure 
can be seen in Appendix A1. 
Besides the choice of mutations within the protein sequences, the proteins were linked with a 
6xHis-tag in the C-terminal end for simplifying purification and visualizing protein expression 
in western blot. Upstreams of the His-tag a cleavage site for the TEV protease was included 
for making it possible to cleave off the His-tag if needed. The additions made to the expression 
vector CPOTud can be seen in Figure 4. The background, however, was designed differently: 
it encoded only the promoter and the terminator as well as the selective marker that would 
allow the yeast to grow. The total molecular mass for the recombinant proteins with the linker, 
TEV-site and his tag was calculated by Benchling to be about 13.3 kDa for MNEI and 8.5 kDa 
for brazzein.

3.2.2     PCR and Gel Electrophoresis 
Non-mutated brazzein and MNEI gene blocks were amplified to achieve sufficient template 
for all PCR reaction amplifying fragments of the respective sweet proteins. For this, the 2X 
Phusion High-Fidelity (HF) PCR Master Mix with HF Buffer (Thermo Scientific) was utilized. 
In PCRs that amplified BioBricks for Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent (USER) cloning, 2X 
Phusion U Hot Start PCR Master Mix (Thermo Scientific) was applied together with primers 
having USER-appropriate uracil-containing tails. Both of the described amplifications had 
PCR reactions in a 50 µL volume comprised of 2.5 µL of each primer (10 µM), 2 µL of template 
DNA (10 ng/µL), 25 µL Phusion PCR master mix, and the remainder of the volume being 
Milli-Q H2O. For analytical colony PCRs, RedTaq 2X Master Mix 1.5 mM MgCl2 (VWR) was 
applied. These reactions took place in a 25 µL volume, comprised of 0.5 µL of both forward 
and reverse primer (10 µM), 12.5 µL RedTaq Master Mix, a template in the form of E. coli or 
S. cerevisiae biomass, and Milli-Q H2O for the remaining volume. PCRs were conducted using 
the S1000TMThermal Cycler (Bio-Rad), with programs detailed in Appendix B. Elongation 
times depended on the applied polymerase, the RedTaq DNA Polymerase (VWR) has a 
polymerizing speed of 60 seconds per kb, and Phusion HF and Phusion U DNA Polymerases 
(Thermo Scientific) a speed of 30 seconds per kb. 
 
Standard gel electrophoresis for isolation of PCR products was conducted in 1% w/v agarose 
gels of Tris-acetate-EDTA (TAE) buffer (Thermo Scientific) and 10000X RedSafe nucleic acid 
staining reagent (iNtRON Biotechnology). USER-cloning fragments were prepared for the gel 
by adding TriTrack DNA 6X Loading Dye (Thermo Scientific), while colony PCR fragments 
were loaded directly onto the gel. GeneRuler 1 kb DNA Ladder (Thermo Scientific) used for 
scaling. The gel ran at either 110 V for 20 min. for big fragments (~8.5 kb) or 100 V for 20 
min. for small fragments (~0.1 kb). Gel imaging of colony PCR analyses was done with 
GelDocXR+ Imaging System (Bio-Rad). USER-cloning fragments were visualized by a blue 
light transilluminator (Invitrogen) and desired bands were cut out of the gel to be purified with 
NucleoSpin Gel and PCR Clean-up kit (Macherey-Nagel) following their protocol. Purified 
fragments were stored at -20 °C.

3.3     Protein Production, Purification, and Analyses 
3.3.1     Producing and Concentrating Recombinant Proteins from S. cerevisiae 
For producing and concentrating sweet proteins for subsequent analyses, the strains were pre-
cultured in 4 mL SD-2xSCAA or Delft media O/N at 250 rpm, 30 °C (Section 3.1.2). On the 
second day, the cultures were OD600 adjusted to 0.1 in a final volume of 50 mL and grown O/N 
in shake flasks. On the third day, the cultures were again OD600 adjusted to 0.1, this time to 
achieve a volume of 800 mL divided into two shake flasks. These were incubated for 96 h at 
200-250 rpm, 30 °C (Section 3.1.2). After the incubation time, the cultures were centrifuged 
(4500 x g, 15 min, 4 °C) so the supernatant could be harvested. The supernatant was filtered 
with 0.22 µm polyethylene glycol (PEG) membranes (BioFil) for the removal of remaining 
cells. Concentration of the proteins present in the supernatant was conducted by ultrafiltration 
where the supernatant was forced through the membrane by either centrifugation or air 
pressure. For concentration in the centrifuge, 80-100 mL supernatant was added to PierceTM 
Protein Concentrators, PES (Thermo Scientific) with a molecular weight cutoff (MWCO) of 
10 kDa or 5 kDa. The centrifuge was running at 4 °C and 2500 x g for 30 min until all 
supernatant had run through the filters. For concentration with air pressure, Amicon® stirred 
cell (Millipore) was utilized with Ultrafiltration discs (Millipore) with 3kDa or 5kDa MWCO, 
following the user guidelines from the manufacturer91,92. A schematic presentation of the 
workflow described here can be seen in Figure 6. 
Purified proteins (Section 3.3.2) were concentrated with Amicon® Ultra 15 mL Centrifugal 
Filter Units (Millipore) centrifuged at 4500 x g and 4 °C for either 10 or 30 min with sizes of 
either 10 kDa or 3 kDa MWCO. Finally, the samples were concentrated to a final volume of 
100 µL with Amicon® Ultra 0.5 mL Centrifugal Filter Units (Millipore), centrifuged at 13,300 
x g, 4 °C for 5 min or 10 min.

3.3.2     His-tag Affinity Chromatography for Protein Purification 
The concentrated proteins (Section 3.3.1) were purified with immobilized metal affinity 
chromatography (IMAC), which is a common method for purification of his-tagged proteins. 
For this procedure two different buffers were used. Binding/wash buffer consisted of 0.3 M 
NaCl, 0.2 M imidazole, and 0.01 M HEPES (pH 7.5) and elution buffer consisted of 0.3 M 
NaCl, 0.5 M imidazole, and 0.01 M HEPES (pH adjusted to 7.5). For samples to bind the resin, 
they were first buffer exchanged into the binding/wash buffer solution, as binding to the resin 
occurs best at near-neutral buffer conditions. Buffer exchange was done in extension of protein 
concentration (Section 3.3.1) in the centrifuge, running two cycles (40 min, 2500 x g, 4 °C) 
min where 80-100 mL binding/wash was added to PierceTM Protein Concentrators, PES 
(Thermo Scientific). For the stationary phase, 1 mL HisPur Ni-NTA Resin (Thermo Scientific) 
was added to disposable 10 mL polypropylene columns (Thermo Scientific). The resin was 
drained of its storage solution and equilibrated with 5 mL of binding/wash buffer. Subsequent 
addition of 4-10 mL concentrated protein in binding/wash buffer solution, wherein this step the 
his-tag should bind the nickel resin. For flowthrough of unspecific binding, the resin was 
washed 2 times with 10 mL binding/wash buffer. Lastly, the his-tagged proteins were recovered 
in the flow through after the addition of 2 times 2 mL elution buffer, as the high imidazole 
concentration would compete with binding of the resin. High amounts of imidazole can 
however have adverse effect on proteins over a prolonged time, the proteins were therefore 
immediately buffer exchanged into a storage buffer that consisted of 0.02 M Sodium phosphate 
tribasic dodecahydrate and 0.3 M NaCl (pH adjusted to 7.5). Buffer exchange was achieved

3.3.3     Assessing Protein Expression with Western Blot and Staining 
Sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS) polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) for separation of 
proteins according to their size was conducted in SDS-PAGE 4–20% Mini-PROTEAN® 
TGX™ Precast Protein Gels (Bio-Rad). 7.5 µL of the samples and a positive control were 
prepared with 2.5 µL of 4x Laemmi Sample Buffer (Bio-Rad) with 0.5 M of the reducing agent 
tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine (TCEP). Thereafter boiled at 95 °C for 5 min and loaded onto a 
gel together with PageRulerTM Plus 10-250 kDa Protein Ladder (Thermo Scientific), running 
at 200 V for 30 min. Hereafter, the gels were either subjected to western blotting protocol to 
confirm the protein of interest was present or stained with InstantBlue® Coomassie protein stain 
(abcam) to see how purified the protein solution was. For protein staining, the gel was 
incubated on a rotator at RT for 1 h with 20 mL of stain. For western blotting the gel was 
assembled in an iBlotTM NC regular stack (Life Technologies), which would transfer proteins 
and ladder onto a nitrocellulose membrane in the iBlotTM2 machine running at 20 V for 3 min. 
Thereafter incubated with blocking buffer consisting of 1x TBST buffer (200 mM Tris-HCl, 
1.5 M NaCl, 0.1% Tween® 20 (Sigma Aldrich) pH adjusted to 8.0) and 5% skim milk powder 
for 1 h at RT on a rotator. Afterward, immunodetection of His-tagged proteins by incubation 
O/N at 4 °C on a rotator with a primary antibody, Anti-His Tag clone HIS.H8 (Millipore), 
1:1000 times diluted in blocking buffer. The next day, the primary antibody was recovered and 
frozen at -20 °C for later use and the membrane was washed with 20 mL 1xTBST 3 x 5 s.

 4.1     Establishing a Pipeline for Expression of Sweet Proteins  
An overview of the pipeline and modifications that were made based on trial and error can be 
seen in Figure 7. Initially, this study followed the procedure for protein production as described 
by Huang et al., which included growing the production strains in the defined medium, SD-
2xSCAA. This medium was, however, found to have an adverse effect for the production and 
purification of sweet proteins, as strains in this medium seemed to yield less protein and be the 
cause for impurities that made measurements of thermostability non-conclusive. Protein

supernatant run through the filters for each 30 min centrifugation cycle. Production of brazzein

acquired and this concentration procedure could be attempted. At this point, the media change 
of MNEI had already proved successful, brazzein was therefore directly grown in Delft 
medium, so the effect of brazzein grown in SD-2xSCAA medium was not seen.

4.3     Thermostability Measurements  
Differential scanning fluorometry (DSF) analysis was carried out on thaumatin and MNEI 
from Figure 8 and the MNEI variants from Figure 9. DSF can find the melting temperature 
(Tm) of proteins by measuring fluorescence as a function of temperature as the fluorescent 
dye binds to hydrophobic amino acids that will become present as the protein denatures. The 
same concentrations of 1 g/L were analysed so the abundance of protein would not make a 
difference. All samples were run at pH 7.5 in sodium phosphate buffer and in technical 
triplicates when possible. Data analysis of the measurements were carried out on Protein 
Thermal ShiftTM Software that gave a Tm value based on a change in fluorescence. The raw 
data of the thermal denaturation profiles can be seen in Appendix C. A summary of the 
results from the DSF analysis can be seen in Table 2. A thaumatin standard is included to 
show how it is supposed to look with the fluorescence and peak. It shows that thaumatin has a 
thermostability of 66.2 ± 0.8 °C. Thaumatin produced from S. cerevisiae did not show any 
fluorescence, and the temperature measurement of this can therefore not be clearly 
interpreted. MNEI-WT showed a thermostability of about 50 °C. Measurements from the 
MNEI variants that showed a single peak were Mut6 (MNEI-G16A) and Mut7 (MNEI-Y29F) 
at 53.3 ± 0.4 °C and 50.0 ± 0.1 °C, respectively. Some of the variants showed a double peak – 
these were Mut2 (MNEI-E22C, L86C) and Mut4 (MNEI-V20C, A66C). In both cases, the 
first peaks were about 52.5 °C and the second peak was approximately 11 °C higher, at 
around 63.5 °C. Not all variants could be interpreted because they did not show enough 
fluorescence to provide a clear Tm value. These were the MNEI variants Mut1 (MNEI-A19C, 
A73C) and Mut5 (MNEI-E48C, K56C). On the other hand, peaks were seen in cases where 
the variants were not present in the western blot. These are Mut3 (MNEI-V20C, F34C) and 
Mut8 (MNEI-C41A) which showed a Tm of 51.5 ± 0.2 °C and 52.1 ± 0.2 °C, respectively.

5     Discussion 
 
5.1     The Findings  
5.1.1     Heterologous Protein Expression and Effect of Medium 
One of the main findings is that recombinant production and extracellular secretion of the sweet 
proteins brazzein, MNEI, and thaumatin with S. cerevisiae as a host proved successful. This 
means the engineered strain by Huang et al., together with the CPOTud expression system is 
applicable for sweet protein production besides producing α-amylase and 1,4-α-glucosidase.82 
Production of thaumatin and brazzein in S. cerevisiae has previously been reported in 
literature93,94 but no studies have reported use of this host for the expression of MNEI. Another 
main finding is that the use of SD-2xSCAA defined medium had an adverse effect on the purity 
and yield of sweet proteins. This media was initially used for the assays because it was found 
by Wittrup et al. to increase α-amylase production and was also the media utilized in the study 
by Huang et al. However, it is worth noting that these studies have not purified their proteins, 
because they could investigate the production yields with enzyme assay kits.82 A hypothesis 
for the adverse effect of the SD-2xSCAA medium is that impurities are caused because the 
medium produces a lot of foam. It is suspected that during incubation, the sweet proteins could 
be caught up in the foam and denatured. A study by Clarkson et al. acknowledges that protein 
denaturation in foam is a common problem and by studying it using Bovine Serum Albumin 
(BSA) they found that denaturation is likely to occur in the air/water interface.95 A factor that 
could add to the impurities is the fact that the SD-2xSCAA media itself contained BSA. Both 
BSA and sweet proteins could be denatured and then form random peptides of which the 
histidine tag also would bind and therefore they would be present after purification. The effect 
of BSA degradation could also explain the similar purification profile seen in the background 
and MNEI expressed in SD-2xSCAA medium. 
   
Switching to the Delft minimal medium showed a very pure profile on SDS-PAGE where the 
most prominent band was the size of the desired sweet proteins, and no bands were seen in the 
background. This finding is crucial for accurately determining thermostability of sweet protein 
variants. Moreover, the possibility of utilizing a minimal medium has practical benefits both in 
the laboratory where less labour time would be spent on media making, but also industrially 
minimal media is preferred for cultivation because it simplifies downstream purification.96 
Interestingly, however, an additional band of approximately double the size of MNEI was seen 
in the western blot when MNEI was expressed in Delft medium, but not in SD-2xSCAA 
medium. Studies investigating MNEI have found that it is common that MNEI tends to form 
SDS-resistant dimers and bigger size aggregates, especially at neutral pH.18,60 Rega et al. 
specifically investigated the aggregation propensity of MNEI and the mutant MNEI-Y65R by 
==PAGE:32==
                                                                                                     Improving the Thermostability of Sweet Proteins 
32 
 
centrifuging the samples to separate supernatant and potential precipitate, thereafter 
resuspending the pellet and loading same volumes of supernatant and pellet onto an SDS-
PAGE.60 With this method, they could find the conditions where aggregates are formed, as well 
as finding that the Y65R mutant possesses a lower tendency to aggregate than MNEI WT. 
Leone et al. represent a study where aggregates are not seen in their results, because they only 
analysed the supernatant of their samples18. The same procedures could be attempted for this 
study to see if similar results are obtained. A potential reason for why no aggregates were 
visualized when MNEI was expressed in SD-2xSCAA medium could be because yield was not 
high enough to be detected in Western Blot - meaning there might have been less than 0.5 µg 
of aggregate present due to the generally low yield of MNEI in this medium.97 On a side note, 
the western blot of the mutants expressed in Delft media seen in Figure 12 could have been 
relevant to repeat, in order to find if one of the mutants has lower aggregation propensity at pH 
7.5.  
 
The purity of samples expressed in Delft medium allows for determining the actual amount of 
protein presents when quantifying. Though the yields between batches are not directly 
comparable because measurements of these were not standardized, it is still interesting to note 
that significantly higher yields of brazzein was achieved in comparison of MNEI. This could 
be because brazzein (54 amino acids) is a smaller protein than MNEI (96 amino acids) and 
might therefore be synthesized faster during transcription and translation. A factor that could 
otherwise have thought to diminish brazzein secretion is the fact that it is a more complex 
protein than MNEI, because it has four disulphide bridges that need to be formed in the ER 
during the export process.37,52 Nonetheless, the relatively high yield of brazzein makes this 
sweet protein a great candidate for the development of a sweet Baker’s yeast and industrial 
processes in comparison to MNEI. On top of that, the high yield is beneficial for the 
experimental work to efficiently screen variants of brazzein with potentially improved 
thermostability. This means, volumes of culture smaller than 800 mL could obtain the 
minimum amount of yield required for samples, thereby reducing time spent on protein 
concentration.  
 
5.1.2     Effects of Mutations on Expression Levels 
For the MNEI mutants cultured in Delft medium, it is interesting to compare how the yields 
differ from each other (Table 3, Batch 4). The mutant which showed the lowest yield was 
Mut10 containing the mutations T12A, G16A, Y29F, C41A, M42Q, M47Q, and Y60F. A 
similar phenomenon was seen by Leone et al. who observed that one of their mutants (MNEI-
C41S, Y65R, S67K, D68N) had lower yields than their other mutants because it had a tendency 
to precipitate during purification.18 Another observation is that this mutant also proved to be 
the one with the lowest thermostability in their study. This correlates with a study by 
Szczepankiewicz et al., who found that there was an association between increased aggregation 
tendency and lower thermal stability.98 Based on this, hypotheses of the thermostability of the 
variants in this study can be based on yield alone. Accordingly, Mut11 (MNEI-E23I) would 
have the highest thermostability, Mut10 (MNEI-T12A, G16A, Y29F, C41A, M42Q, M47Q) 
would have the lowest thermostability, and Mut9 (MNEI-E48C, E54C) and Mut6 (MNEI-
G16A) would be somewhere in the middle. 
==PAGE:33==
                                                                                                     Improving the Thermostability of Sweet Proteins 
33 
 
 
For the MNEI mutants cultured in SD-2xSCAA medium, it does not make sense to compare 
the yields, because high yields were measured in samples where no band was seen in the 
western blot. However, it is interesting to consider why the mutants Mut3 (MNEI-V20C, F34C) 
and Mut8 (MNEI-C41A) were not seen in the western blot at all. There can be different reasons 
behind this. One explanation could be that they precipitated during purification, as described 
in the section above, thereby resulting in a protein yield that was too low to be detected in the 
western blot.18,97 Another reason could be that the variants were unable to be folded in vivo, 
because they did not have an energetically favourable conformation and thus misfold/unfold.99 
Alternatively, it might be the same case as the MNEI-A19E mutation described by Liu et al., 
which led to complete insolubility of MNEI.32 An unsuccessful expression can provide 
valuable information regarding residues which may play a critical role in the proper folding of 
the native protein. However, the variants would need to be repeated in Delft media to know 
whether this might be the case, or if quantities were too low for the western blot analysis. 
 
5.1.3     Thermostability Measurements with DSF 
While the machine required for the DSF analysis was still functional, only the proteins 
cultivated in SD-2xSCAA and the purchased thaumatin standard managed to be analysed. For 
the thaumatin standard a Tm of 66.2 ± 0.8 °C was measured at pH 7.5. In literature thaumatin 
has been reported to have a Tm of 70 °C at pH 7.0.20 The difference of melting temperature 
between this study and literature might be because of the difference in pH and different 
methods. It would however be interesting to test the standard at pH 7.0 to get an idea of how 
the DSF method compares to methods utilized by other studies. It was unfortunate that no clear 
peak was seen for thaumatin expressed in S. cerevisiae, as it could have provided valuable 
insight of both the applicability of the method and if proteins were correctly synthesized in the 
host. The absence of peak could either be due to degradation during storage or during other 
steps in the pipeline. Nonetheless, DSF measurements would need to be repeated for all of the 
sweet proteins, with the only change of them being expressed in Delft medium. 
 
5.2     Experimental Design Considerations and Further Investigation   
 
5.2.1     Additional Analyses 
Successful protein engineering depends on keeping the function intact, however this study did 
not put much emphasis on the activity of the sweet proteins. Additional in silico analyses with 
the wedge model could have been made when selecting the mutants to decrease library size 
and enhance chances of obtaining a variant which is both sweet and thermostable. Leone et al. 
reported that their results support the validity of the wedge model in predicting and explaining 
the physiological effects of mutated sweet proteins, confirming its applicability to drive in 
silico sweet proteins’ design.18  On top of including predictions of activity, analyses of 
sweetness could be added as a part of the experimental procedure as has been done by all the 
studies presented in the State of the Art, Section 2.3.1. Prior to thermostability and potential 
sensory analyses, the potential impact of the His-tag at the C-terminal of the recombinant 
==PAGE:34==
                                                                                                     Improving the Thermostability of Sweet Proteins 
34 
 
proteins in this study would need to be investigated. Liu et al. have previously found that a His-
tag in the N-terminal of MNEI did no difference in comparison to no-tagged MNEI in regards 
of thermostability or sweetness.32 However, since the His-tag is placed in the C-terminal and 
not the N-terminal in this work, the exact effect would need to be evaluated before keeping the 
His-tag for the analyses. If the presence of a His-tag is found be redundant, it means the 
experimental step for removal of the His-tag could be left out, thus simplifying screening of 
the mutant library. Lastly, in addition to the thermostability analysis, extra information 
regarding potential renaturation capabilities of the mutants could be provided if the DSF 
protocol had an extra step where samples are cooled to 25 °C after the heating to 99 °C.    
 
5.2.2     Optimization of Expression 
Screening of mutated sweet proteins would be more efficient if the host secreted higher yields 
of protein. This would mean that smaller volumes could be cultivated and therefore subsequent 
protein concentration and purification could be carried out more rapidly. This is important 
because it means limiting the time in which the sweet proteins are in the same solution as 
proteases that are naturally present in the culture supernatant.84 There are a plethora of potential 
modifications which could be attempted for this purpose. One of the simplest is making 
modifications to the expression system, such as changing promoter and leader sequences.83 For 
example Liu et al. compared a synthetic leader with no glycosylation with glycosylated alpha 
factor leader and found that the synthetic leader secreted insulin precursor most efficiently, 
while fungal α-amylase secretion was better with a glycosylated alpha factor leader.83 Another 
approach is further genome editing of the CEN.PK 530‐1CK By Huang et al.82 The ideal 
scenario would be to make one or more mutations that has synergistic effects with the already 
existing mutations. A potential candidate could be deleting the transcriptional regulator gene 
ROX1 which in oxidative conditions represses genes induced in hypoxia, as this deletion has 
been found by Liu et al. to increase yield of fungal α-amylase by 100%. 100  
 
5.2.3     Future Perspectives 
First and foremost, the variants created in this study would need to be tested for their 
thermostability by following the pipeline established in this study. Assuming that one or more 
thermostable variants are among them, the possibility of potential synergistic effects could be 
tested. Otherwise, some possibilities are further investigation such as X-Ray crystallography 
could provide valuable information to elucidate the mechanism of sweet proteins. In addition, 
studies of long-term storage in conditions relevant to food products could be conducted to 
evaluate its applicability. Alternatively, it could be interesting to have S. cerevisiae host the 
expression of the most thermostable MNEI mutant (MNEI-E23A, C41A, Y65R, S76Y) found 
to date by Delfi et al.73  Because this mutant is thermostable up to 96 °C the applicability of S. 
cerevisiae in baked goods might be realised in the near future.

6     Conclusion 
The main framework of this thesis shows how a change of media from a rich defined medium 
to the minimal medium, Delft, can affect the production and purification of sweet proteins. 
With this, a pipeline for how sweet protein variants can be tested in S. cerevisiae was 
established, which can be utilized for studies in the future. because this host is an eukaryote it 
can be used to test more complex variants of sweet proteins, such as sweet proteins with 
engineered disulphide bridges. On top of this, the successful expression of the sweet proteins 
brazzein and MNEI in S. cerevisiae opens new insights for the development of sweet yeasts 
that can be used as food additives.
