Abstract
This work concerns the use of a method known as super-resolution, which is a tech-
nique used in digital image processing and computer vision to improve an image’s
sharpness and quality beyond its original size or resolution.
The aim of super-
resolution is to reconstruct a high-resolution version of a low-resolution image by
inference of missing high-frequency details, resulting in a super-resolution image with
increased clarity and detail.
Super-resolution finds applications within many different domains, including for in-
stance surveillance and satellite imaging.
For the topic of this work, the use of
super-resolution will be examined from the perspective of medical imaging. In mod-
ern clinical settings, high-resolution image scans are crucial for accurate diagnosis and
planning of treatment. However, the quality of modern medical scans are limited by
factors such as scan-time, radiation limits and beam-artifacts. In such cases, super-
resolution may be used to improve the quality of medical imaging techniques, such
as MRI, CT, and ultrasound, providing physicians with more precise information.
With the aforementioned application in mind, this project details the design and im-
plementation of a 3D-compliant super-resolution framework capable of increasing the
visual quality of medical images.
To accomplish this, a type of deep neural network model known as a Generative Ad-
versarial Network (GAN) was implemented in this thesis. This model was trained
to infer the missing details of low-resolution medical images by comparing the low-
resolution image structures against a backlog of corresponding high-resolution images.
These image pairs were obtained by implementing a data preparation scheme designed
to generate training examples for the neural network, so it may learn a suitable map-
ping between low- and high-resolution. To guide and improve the training of the
super-resolution GAN model, a series of auxiliary loss functions were designed and
implemented. These loss functions, using different approaches, quantize how closely
the model’s output samples compare with the corresponding high-resolution samples.
Using a series of systematic tests, the performance impact of each auxiliary loss func-
tion was tested. To validate the performance of each trained model, both objective
and subjective evaluations were presented and discussed.
==PAGE:4==
ii
Abstract
Despite instability issues, a total of 12 super-resolution GAN models were successfully
trained using different loss functions. While the performance of these networks varied
greatly, all of them were able to drastically out-perform commonly used interpolation-
based super-resolution methods such as nearest-neighbour and 3D cubic B-spline. In
conclusion, this work proves that super-resolution GANs in conjunction with certain
loss functions have huge potential for improving the visual quality of medical images.

1.1
Background
In the current modern age of digital imaging, image acquisition techniques have de-
veloped to the point where low-cost, high-resolution cameras are now a common
commodity. As an example, modern day smartphones often feature multiple camera
sensors, each capable of capturing images in double-digit megapixel resolution. How-
ever, although recent years has seen rapid improvements in the hardware capabilities
of image devices, these improvements require that old devices are replaced with newly
manufactured hardware. In some situations, installing new camera sensors in for in-
stance an orbiting satellite or replacing an expensive CT-scanners in a hospital, may
not be the best option due to practical or economical circumstances. While some
devices such as smartphones are easily replaceable, the environmental cost of doing
so becomes an ever increasing priority in modern day society. For such applications,
image resolution enhancement, also known as super-resolution (SR), offers a more
flexible solution for increasing the visual quality of image acquisition devices.
Super-resolution refers to the process of reconstructing one or more high-resolution
(HR) images from low-resolution (LR) views of the same scene. The process of super-
resolution starts with the acquisition of a low-resolution image ILR, which can be
obtained in a multitude of ways. Usually, this image is generated synthetically by
degrading the quality of a high-resolution image IHR such that the LR image contains
the same scene as the corresponding HR image. The low-resolution image is then
enhanced using an upscaling function fs↑, producing the super-resolution image ISR.

The upscaling function is usually estimated based on the minimization of a sum
consisting of one or more so-called loss functions, denoted by L1, L2, . . . , Ln.

Obtaining a function which is successfully able to reconstruct realistic HR images

invasive measurements. As technology has evolved over the years, medical scanners
have become more and more advanced and today, several types of these devices exists

from an economical standpoint, it is usually not possible for most hospitals to have

Another aspect to consider is the amount of radiation being exposed to the patient.
In the case of Computed Tomography, for instance, each tomographic image is con-
structed via irradiating a section of a patient’s body using a moving x-ray source. The
intensity at each point in the image are the result of absorption measurements taken

correlated with the amount of exposed radiation. Conclusively, even if it would be
possible to capture a high-quality medical scan using a lower-quality scanning device,
this may not be advisable as the increase in radiation could potentially pose risks to
the patient’s health. Finally, the scan time is also of consideration in a clinical setting.

in increments of suitable lengths, usually millimeters, depending on the part of the

1.4.1
Image super-resolution
Super-resolution has been an active area of research for several decades and over the
years, several approaches have been developed to address the problem. Despite the
advancement of recent years research, the problem of super-resolution remains un-
solved. This largely stems from the fact that the problem of super-resolution is often
severely ill-posed.
Early super-resolution methods were largely based on interpolation techniques such
as bicubic interpolation or Lanczos resampling.
As opposed to nearest-neighbour
upsampling which generally produces very chunky and visually unappealing images,
these methods improve the visual quality by smoothing the pixels using an interpola-
tion function or filter. For simple black and white images as well as small upscaling
factors these techniques can produce convincing SR results with high computational
eﬀiciency. Although because they are unaware of the image content and cannot recon-
struct high-frequency information, they generally tend to produce scenes that appear
unrealistic. Despite the shortcomings of these methods, they are still widely used as
sub-operations for more advanced super-resolutions frameworks.
In recent years, the field of super-resolution has experienced a revolution thanks to
the advancements of deep learning-based methods. Deep learning is a branch of ma-
chine learning which uses deep artificial neural networks (ANNs) to learn abstract
representations of data, and are especially useful for approximating functions with
strong non-linearities. As opposed to relying on a domain expert to extract relevant
data features to learn from, the immediate advantage of deep learning algorithms
is that they automatically learn informative features in the data at multiple levels
of abstraction, and then combine these features to produce the desired output [59].
One of the earliest and most impactful deep neural networks for super-resolution was
SRCNN introduced by Dong et al. in 2016 [15]. This model consisted of a convo-
lutional neural network (CNN) that was trained to learn the mapping between LR
and HR images directly from data, resulting in highly realistic and sharp HR images.
Despite being simple in its design, SRCNN achieved state-of-the-art performance on
several benchmark datasets and paved the way for several high-performance CNN-
based super-resolution models.

Network design
One approach to improve SR performance is via the network design, also known as
the architecture. Dong et al. [15] were the first to use a linear network design with
great success. Despite the success of these models, linear networks become diﬀicult to
train as the depth is increased due to the gradient easily exploding or vanishing when
the network only contains a single data path. As a result, many alterations which
improve data flow, stability and feature extraction inside the network have been pro-
posed. Some of these include multi-branch connections [41], dense connections [51],
progressive reconstruction [67] and finally attention-based connections [65]. GANs
have also been incorporated to great effect within the field of super-resolution [25]
[53].
Degradation functions
Degradation functions are used to model the perceptual quality reduction associ-
==PAGE:25==
1.4 Literature Review
7
ated with a super-resolution problem. Commonly, interpolation schemes are used to
downsample the resolution of a HR image, producing a LR image depicting the same
scene. For real-world super-resolution tasks, however, several degradations may pre-
vail simultaneously. To better model these phenomenon, changes to the degradation
function have been proposed which include gaussian blurring and additive gaussian
noise [55]. Specifically for medical imaging, k-space truncation have been used to
model a lower-quality MRI scanner [6]. This technique involves the truncation of the
image’s Fourier space, resulting in an image containing fewer high-frequency details.
Loss functions
Pixel-wise losses such as L1 and L2 were used in SRCNN as a metric for minimizing
the difference between ISR and IHR. Since then, several other metrics have been
proposed which promote certain characteristics of the super-resolution output. These
include gradient loss [30], total variational loss [58], perceptual loss [25], texture/style
loss [42], cyclic consistency loss [60], prior-based loss [55] and adversarial GAN loss
[18].
Upsampling strategy
Early learning-based super-resolution frameworks, such as SRCNN, performed up-
sampling prior to convolution, also known as pre-upsampling. While this approach
enabled for easier training of the network, substantial computational resources can
be saved if upsampling is instead done in the later stages of the network. Larger
super-resolution models therefore usually employ post-upsampling or progressive up-
sampling which performs most of the convolutional operations in a lower resolution
space [55].
Upsampling operations
Apart from different upsampling strategies, several operations exist for performing
upsampling. SR models using a pre-upsampling strategy often utilize well-known in-
terpolation schemes for the upsampling. However, with the advent of post-upsampling
and progressive upsampling, new operations for upsampling have been suggested
which are able to learn the low-to-high resolution mapping in a end-to-end man-
ner. These include transposed convolutions, also known as deconvolutions [51] and
sub-pixel convolutions [45]. Both of these operations, while powerful and computa-
tionally eﬀicient, are prone to an undesirable phenomenon known as checkerboarding.
To circumvent this, approaches such as ICNR have been proposed by Ledig et al. [1].
Assessment methods
The standard metrics for assessing the performance of super-resolution models include
the Peak Signal To Noise Ratio (PSNR), Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) and the
Mean Squared Error (MSE). Over the years, other more advanced metrics have been
suggested which better model the quality observed by the human eye. Some of these
include the Multi-Scale SSIM [54] (MS-SSIM), Learned Perceptual Image Patch Sim-
ilarity [64] (LPIPS), Perceptual Index [68] (PI), Ma score [29] and Natural Image

Quality Evaluator [32] (NIQE). Furthermore, entirely subjective image quality assess-
ment methods have been proposed, such as the Mean Opinion Score (MOS) [25].
Other improvements
There have also been several suggestions to improve the robustness of super-resolution
networks via use of different activation functions. For instance activation functions
that make use of negative values have been suggested for SR problems handling images
normalized between -1 and 1 for better robustness. Some of these functions include
the Exponential Linear Unit [10] (ELU), Parametric ReLU (PReLU) and Leaky ReLU
(LReLU) [49]. Batch normalization [21] and layer normalization [4] have also been
suggested to increase the robustness and speed of convergence during training.
1.4.3
3D super-resolution frameworks
The field of 3D super-resolution has seen substantially less development than the field
of 2D super-resolution, which might be due to 3D image analysis being more niche.
Most of the works within 3D SR are fairly recent (often ≤5 years).
Thankfully,
many of the architectural improvements and developments seen in the field of 2D
super-resolution are able to be generalized to 3D.
The earliest attempts at super-resolution of 3D images involved decomposing the 3D
volume into a series of image slices. Slice-wise upscaling of each image was performed
using a 2D super-resulution scheme in order to obtain a series of high-resolution im-
ages. These were afterwards stacked again to obtain an upscaled 3D volume [28].
Although this approach was simple and eﬀicient, Pham et al. shifted this paradigm
using a 3D adapted version of SRCNN [38], which proved that end-to-end 3D super-
resolution was superior to 2D approaches for reconstruction of MRI brain images.
Since then, state-of-the-art performance have been achieved solely by models that
exploit all three dimensional components of volumetric images.
In the work done by Sanchez et al. [44], a GAN network model was used to directly
upsample 3D MRI brain scans using an architecture similar to SRGAN. This network
made use of residual network (ResNet) blocks [19] and skip-connections to enhance
the information flow within the network, and swapped out the 2D convolution lay-
ers with 3D convolutions to comply with the extra dimension of volumetric images.
They also compared different upsampling operations including Nearest-Neighbour,
sub-pixel convolution and convolution nearest neighbour resize.
It was concluded
that an eﬀicient and checkerboard artifact free upsampling method could be achieved
using a deconvolutional layer initialized according to the ICNR method proposed by
Ledig et al. [1]. While the GAN model of Sanchez et al. yielded visually convincing
results at upsampling factors of 4×, the resulting 3D images also appeared overly
smooth, likely due to the reliance on mean square error (MSE) as their primary loss
metric.

To enhance the texture of super-resolution 3D images, Zhang et al. [63] proposed
SOUP-GAN which featured a perceptual loss metric to guide the generator into re-

This was

network, in this case VGG19, was extracted by slicing the 3D volume along all three
dimensions, producing three stacks of 2D images. The feature maps from these three

to ResNet, concatenations were used to combine both high- and low-level features,
enabling for more effective extraction of hierarchical feature representations [47]. Ad-

requires parameters, making their model less computationally expensive in terms of

reducing the overall risk of overfitting [28].

2.1
Introduction
In this project, a super-resolution framework based on GANs was chosen due to their
ability to reconstruct highly realistic image textures from very little contextual infor-
mation. To lay the theoretical groundwork for this project, a general introduction to
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and their application for the task of super-
resolution, will be presented.
Generative Adversarial Networks are a type of deep neural network models which
have gained substantial popularity within recent years. The GAN concept was first
introduced in 2014 by Goodfellow et. al. [18] who presented the foundational theory
of the basic Generative Adversarial Framework. Since then GANs have been applied
in many fields of research to generate highly realistic and coherent data and several
alterations and improvements to the GAN framework have been made over the years.
At the core of GANs is a powerful and unique concept in which two neural networks, a
generator and a discriminator, compete and learn from each other in order to estimate
the data distribution of a given dataset. Given a set of data samples x1, x2, . . . , xn
from a certain distribution, the aim of the generator is to produce new synthetic
data samples resembling the real data samples. Meanwhile the discriminator aims
to differentiate the generated samples from the real ones. Through the prospect of
a two-player game, the generator and discriminator are trained to progressively im-
prove their respective roles with the generator learning to produce more convincing
data and the discriminator learning to better distinguish between real and synthetic
samples.
The typical GAN framework involves the generator producing synthetic data sam-
ples from a randomly generated noise prior. These samples are then parsed to the
discriminator which produces a classification output in the form of a probability in
the interval [0, 1], signifying the discriminator’s believed authenticity of the sample.
The classification output is then parsed back to the generator, providing feedback on
==PAGE:30==
12
2 Generative Adversarial Networks
the quality of its generated samples. During the training of GANs, this process of
generation and classification is carried out in an iterative fashion. This adversarial
framework drives the generator to improve over time, eventually leading to the gen-
eration of high-quality synthetic samples.
One of the key strengths of GANs is their ability to learn without relying on explicit
labels or annotations in the training data. Instead GANs exploit the modelling power
of deep neural networks to directly learn the complex distributions of real data. De-
spite the potential of GANs, they also have notable downsides. Training of GANs is
notoriously diﬀicult, as the convergence of these models require careful balancing of
the generator and discriminator. If one of these roles become too strong the training
can stagnate, resulting in a sub-optimal generative model.

2.4
Training of GANs
The typical training process of GANs, as presented in [18], can be seen in algorithm
1. Overall the training process comprises of two alternating steps. Step A: Generator
parameters are kept constant and the discriminator is trained to discriminate real
and synthetic samples. Step B: Discriminator parameters are kept constant and the
generator is trained to generate synthetic data [47]. These steps are repeated for a
number of iterations or until a certain criteria has been reached.
The training process of GANs poses several inherent challenges. The training of GANs
is a constant delicate balance of improving the generator and discriminator networks
simultaneously in order for them to progressively learn from one another. Failure to
achieve this can introduce a variety of issues. One common example is mode collapse,
wherein the generator is trapped in a certain local minimum where it only captures a
small portion of the target distribution [47]. Under these conditions, the generator is
stuck to produce very similar outputs and consequently fails to capture the variety in
the target distribution. One approach to increase the stability during training is to
repeat step A up to k times for each execution of step B. This puts the discriminator
always a bit ahead of the generator, ensuring its feedback is more likely to be to useful.
This approach comes with another issue of training GANs, the problem of vanishing
gradients, where the generator is prevented from learning anything as the discrimina-
tor becomes too successful [31] [47]. Another common issue is undamped/unstable

oscillatory loss behavior of the generator and discriminator during training, which
often prohibits the network from converging to a fixed point [31] and sometimes leads
to the losses diverging. Finally, GANs are highly sensitive to hyperparameters and

to perform.
A common strategy to increase the performance and reduce the instability of GANs

Ever since the introduction of SRGAN by Ledig et al.
[25] in 2017, GANs have
seen substantial use within the field of super-resolution. The working principle of
super-resolution GANs is very similar to the regular GAN scheme seen in figure 2.1
but instead of generating synthetic samples from noise vectors drawn from a known
distribution, super-resolution GANs generate synthetic high-resolution images from
low-resolution samples. Thus, instead of having to reconstruct realistic-looking sam-

the necessary high-frequency details of real a HR image. This introduces a sense of
diﬀiculty of the specific super-resolution problem, which can vary depending on the
severity of degradation of the LR image. Naturally, training a super-resolution GAN
model on a less diﬀicult SR problem usually enables better results and more stable
training.
As opposed to GANs, super-resolution GANs usually employ several loss functions
in addition to the adversarial loss to help guide the training process and to promote
specific characteristics in the output images. These losses are computed exclusively
using the image pair (IHR, ISR) or just using ISR, depending on the formulation.

drawn as illustrated in figure 2.3, where a 3D image super-resolution scheme has been

false information could lead to misdiagnosis. Consequently, it’s important to avoid
overfitting of GAN-based super-resolution models. This will be elaborated further in

3.1.2
2D vs. 3D super-resolution
With regards to super-resolution of three dimensional volumetric image, such as medi-
cal scans obtained using CT or MRI, both 2D and 3D super-resolution based methods
have seen extensive use throughout the years. As one can think of a volumetric im-
age being a stack of convectional 2D images, it seems very natural to upscale such
volumetric images by parsing each slice of the volume through a 2D super-resolution
model, then stacking the individual image slices back together in order to obtain an
upscaled version of the original volume. As opposed to direct 3D super-resolution
models, such 2D approaches have many upsides. For one, as the field of research

consumption and time-complexity.
It is not uncommon for a 3D super-resolution
model to require over 5x as many parameters as its 2D counterpart [7], making the
2D models more practical to apply.

be fully comprehended in 3D. To illustrate this point, consider the example shown in

As opposed to pre-upsampling, post-upsampling instead starts by employing convo-
lutional layers for extraction of features in the LR image. These features are then
upsampled directly into high-resolution images, resulting in a super-resolution model
consisting of end-to-end learnable layers. Because the process of feature extraction
is done in a low-dimensional space, these models are much more computationally
eﬀicient as opposed to models using pre-upsamling. For large upscaling factors of
4× and above, however, increasing the resolution in a single step can substantially
increase the learning diﬀiculty of these networks, making them harder to train [55].
The final common method is progressive upsampling, which can be thought of as a
combination of the two previous methods. The aim of the progressive upsampling
framework is to decompose the upsampling step into several smaller stages where at
each stage, the image is upsampled to a higher resolution. By breaking down the
problem of upscaling into simpler subproblems, the diﬀiculty of each stage is greatly
reduced, thus making these networks easier to train, especially for larger upscaling
factors [55]. In order to retain the benefits of post-upsampling, most of the compu-
tation is still performed in the low-dimensional space, with upsampling being done
as late as possible. As upscaling factors of up to 4× were considered in this work,
progressive upsampling was the most natural choice. This method was again deemed
the best choice from a computational perspective.
In addition to the different locations of the upsampling layers within the model, there
are many methods for carrying out the upsampling operation itself. The three most
common methods are interpolation schemes, sub-pixel convolutions and transposed-
convolutions, also known as deconvolutions.
For post-upsampling and progressive
upsampling, sub-pixel convolutions and deconvolutions have become the most pop-
ular upsampling operations because the weights of the upsampling process can be
learned in an end-to-end manner [55].
The process of deconvolution consists of an expansion of the input where zeros are
inserted around each value of the input. Then, a convolution with a certain kernel
size is performed over the upsampled feature space, filling out the zero-valued areas.
Deconvolution can be thought of as the opposite of a normal convolution, and the
upscaling factor is controlled by the stride, padding and kernel size [27]. In the case
==PAGE:46==
28
3 Learning-based image super-resolution
of deconvolutions, the kernel size determines the size of the region of the expanded
input that is used to create the output. This region, called the receptive field, thereby
affects how much of the surrounding image values’ contextual information is used to
generate the output. A larger receptive field generally enables generation of more
realistic details but increasing the kernel size also increases the computational cost
of the deconvolution.
Additionally, the stride affects the movement of the kernel
over the expanded input and the padding affects the size of the boundary around it.
Matching these parameters is crucial for circumventing checkerboarding.
Sub-pixel convolutions, first introduced in ESPCN [45], are an eﬀicient way of increas-

convolutions first involve a convolution which increases the depth of the feature space,
then a reshaping operation that shuffles elements from several feature maps to pro-
duce a feature space of higher resolution [27].

overlap each other in an uneven manner, resulting in a checkerboard-like pattern of

3.1.5
Performance evaluation metrics
To compare the reconstruction performance of different models, a large variety of
performance evaluation metrics have been developed and used within the field of
super-resolution.
The goal of these metrics is twofold: Firstly, they aim to pro-
vide a standard for quantifying and comparing the image quality of reconstructed
super-resolution images obtained using different models. Secondly, they provide an
indicator for the developer for evaluation of the model performance.
Image quality assessment (IQA) methods overall include two methods: Human-based
subjective methods and objective computational methods. The latter of these attempt
to estimate the image quality using a specific formulation, whereas the former relies
on the subjective judgement of humans to determine the image quality [27]. Of the
two methods, the subjective approach is usually the most accurate for determination
of image quality, due to the fact that human visual system is always involved. These
methods thus produce results which are directly inline with what is needed for the
task of evaluating the visual quality of super-resolution images. Unfortunately, these
methods are unreproducible, time-consuming and expensive.
In contrast, computational objective methods are simple, fair and easy to use, mak-
ing these metrics the de facto standard within the field. Accurately assessing image
quality via a quantitative formulation is a very diﬀicult task and therefore these meth-
ods aren’t always consistent with one another. Conclusively, several objective IQA
metrics are usually involved in order to give a more full fledged assessment of image

3.2.1
Generator design
The generator of the model performs upscaling of low-resolution input patches in two
steps. The first step is feature extraction, in which the input data is passed through
several densely-connected layers in order to extract as much information from the
input data as possible. The purpose of this step is to extract all the necessary contex-
tual information within the LR patch, allowing the network to grasp its contents, be
==PAGE:52==
34
3 Learning-based image super-resolution
they a piece of a specific section of bone, brain, etc. Once all the necessary features
of the LR patch have been extracted, the second step involves the reconstruction of
said features into HR image patches. This reconstruction phase consists of a series
of activational layers, upsampling layers as well as 3D convolutional layers in order
to reconstruct super-resolved image patches which ideally lie on a similar manifold as
that of HR image patches.
The feature extraction phase of the generator is primarily based on the architecture
proposed by Chen et. al [8] [7] [6] as well as the seminal work done by Huang et.
al. [20] and Tong et al. [51]. Characteristic for these works is the recurring use of
densely-connected convolutional blocks or Dense Blocks, for short. These blocks are
characterized by heavy use of skip-connections which serve to combine features from
several convolutional layers, resulting in a densely-connected network structure.
The foundational block on which the feature extraction is based, is the Dense Block,
the architecture of which is illustrated in figure 3.7.

the purpose of super-resolution. While it has been shown that deeper super-resolution
networks are superior for extraction and reconstruction of high-frequency details [23]

densely connected architecture incorporates short paths between the layers, each
convolutional layer is affected by the outputs of several other layers through back-
propagation. Thus, these paths effectively enhance the information flow within the
network, mitigating the vanishing-gradient problem and allowing for stable training
of much deeper models [51].

these densely connected convolutional layers also require relatively few network pa-
rameters, as the weight parameters of each convolutional layer are shared by multiple
data inputs throughout the network. Conclusively, these models are quite powerful
for their size [20].

consisting of 26k feature maps in total. Upsampling such a large number of feature
maps directly, would lead to a significant increase in computational cost and model
size. Therefore, to maintain a compact model and enhance computational eﬀiciency,
the output of the feature extraction step is first passed through a bottleneck layer in
order to decrease the number of input feature maps. Several studies have shown that
a convolution layer with a kernel size of 1x1x1 can be used as a bottleneck layer to
reduce the number of input feature maps [51] [6] [50]. This bottleneck layer, like the
compression layers, was implemented using a 3D convolution with a stride of 1, zero
padding, and a kernel size of 1 × 1 × 1, reducing the depth down to 8k.

each consisting of a 3D deconvolution that doubles the feature space’s height, width,
and depth. Following each deconvolution is a Parametric ReLU (PReLu) activation
layer, similar to the architecture of SRGAN [25].
After upsampling and activation, the feature space is parsed through a final recon-
struction layer, which consists of a 3D convolutional layer that reduces the number
of feature maps down to 1, resulting in a single-channel high-resolution output batch.

CHAPTER 4
Loss functions
4.1
Introduction
In chapter 2 it was discussed how the generator of the super-resolution GAN is op-
timized partly based on the output of the discriminator, and partly based on a set
of loss metrics computed directly using the SR output and its HR equivalent. Thus,
since a large part of the optimization of the network is based upon these loss metrics,
it was deemed paramount for this project to investigate the impact of these loss func-
tions.
While it may seem tempting to conclude that only adversarial loss is needed to achieve
a well performing generator, there are several advantages to employing additional loss
functions for the purpose of super-resolution. The addition of a pixel-wise MSE term
can for instance help guide the generator closer towards the distribution of the high-
resolution samples in the early stages of training, when the discriminator has not
yet learned the shape of the HR data manifold. Thus, apart from offering increased
stability during training, these loss function allow for faster convergence [66].
Another advantage of using auxiliary loss functions in addition to the standard adver-
sarial GAN loss is that the network designer is able control the optimization process
to a larger degree. By introducing loss functions which leverage specific desirable
qualities of the network output, the generator can be influenced to produce outputs
which, depending on the use case, achieve better performance. For example, assume
there is a high level of noise in the high-resolution dataset. Where a vanilla GAN will
attempt to mimic the noise to produce outputs which more closely resemble the HR
samples, a generator trained using a loss function which penalizes noise may produce
more visually appealing images.
The task of the network designer is therefore to define a suitable set of loss func-
tions which collectively quantize both how close the super-resolved output matches
the actual high-resolution data, and the performance of the outputs as seen from
the perspective of the specific use case. In this chapter, the different loss functions
considered in this project, will be presented.

ways, but the general approach is to compute the sum of squared differences between
neighbouring elements within an image [55]. Another popular choice in the literature
is the sum of absolute differences between adjacent elements.

Degradation function
In chapter 3 it was addressed how the design of the degradation function is critical
for modeling the super-resolution problem of interest. As the end goal of the project

the degradation function should ideally be able to model the degradation in quality

µ-CT scans which complicate the process of super-resolution, including differences in
intensity scales, scan artifacts and so on. It would have been preferable to model these
differences, but given that the brain scan dataset’s purpose is to serve as a benchmark
for the 3D super-resolution model, it was important not to make the super-resolution
problem too diﬀicult. For the sake of simplicity, it was therefore decided to model
the problem of upscaling brain scans using a simple interpolation function.

Because HCP 1200 samples have virtually no empty space, 3D patches may be sam-

5.2
Femur scans
To test the super-resolution model on a real-world problem, a collaboration with PhD
H. Martin Kjer from the department of Applied Mathematics and Computer Science
was agreed upon, wherein 3D data obtained from the 3D Imaging Center was made
available to the project. This data consisted of several CT-scans of femur bones from
deceased patients. These scans were originally collected as part of a PhD project
conducted by Sophia Elizabeth Bardenfleth, also at DTU Compute. The CT-scans
comprise of sets of low- and high-resolutions images taken from four different femur
bones.
The low-resolution scans were collected using a clinical scanner located at the Depart-
ment of Forensic Medicine at the University of Copenhagen, while the corresponding
high-resolution scans were captured locally at DTU in the 3D Imaging Center using
a µ-CT scanner. At the time, it was the intention that the LR scans should be com-
parable in quality to the clinical scans captured in regular hospital settings. These
scans were therefore taken using similar parameters as would be used when capturing
volumetric images of living patients. The quality limitations of these scans resulted
in the trabecular structure being captured with a high degree of blurring.
As previously discussed, there is great interest in being able to recover the micro-
structures of bone scans in greater detail currently possible with clinical CT-scanners.
The study of these micro-structures could be potentially groundbreaking in the esti-
mation and evaluation of bone health of patients suffering from bone deficiency related
diseases, such as osteoporosis. However, correctly recovering these micro-structures
while avoiding reconstruction of false details is a problem that is littered with diﬀicult
challenges as seen from a super-resolution perspective. These challenges will now be
briefly covered.

56
5 Data preparation
5.2.1
Challenges
The two machines used to capture the 3D femur scans differ from each other in sev-
eral ways. The stark disparity in resolution between the clinical scanner and µ-CT
scanner is one of the primary obstacles to the recovery of bone micro-structures. The
µ-CT scanner is able to capture each tomographic images at a much higher resolution
and the interval between each slice, which is usually ≥1mm for clinical scanners, can
instead be incremented in the scale of a few micrometres. This results in a larger
number of tomographic images over the same spatial domain, ultimately enabling
better resolution. As such, each individual voxel in the clinical scan covers a much
larger physical space.
To be able to comply with patients of different sizes, clinical scanners in hospitals are
required to have a certain physical distance between in the x-ray tube, and the digital
detector located directly opposite the beam source. When the distance between the
x-ray source and detector is large, the radiation is dispersed over a larger, more coni-
cally shaped area. As the x-rays are diffused over a larger area, the exact absorption
of a single point cannot be accurately determined, resulting in significant blurring
of finer details. Conversely, µ-CT scanners are usually smaller and have the x-ray
source located physically closer the media being scanned, which enables the beam
to be much narrower at the point of detection, enabling better and more detailed
absorption measurements.
Finally, the reconstruction algorithms used by the two machines are different, which
causes the intensity values in the two scans to be scaled differently. To illustrate the
diﬀiculty in reconstructing the bone structures, an ensemble of corresponding low- and
high-resolution bone patches are shown in figure 5.2. From just these samples, it can
be concluded that the femur scan dataset poses an extremely diﬀicult super-resolution
problem.
5.2.2

the process of tomographic reconstruction also introduces additional artifacts which
must be dealt with. The most significant of these is an artifact similar to what is ob-
served when the scanned object is unintentionally moved during the scanning process.
This artifact is most likely the bi-product a small misalignment between the physical
rotational axis of the femur, and the virtual rotational axis used by the reconstruction
algorithm to produce the output scan. When these two axes are misaligned, notice-
able blurring and overlapping of the bone structures occur, as can be seen in the left
subplot in figure 5.3.

ring artifacts which are present near the isocenter of the scan as well as in the upper

5.2.6
Reslicing
With the LR/HR scans aligned, if two cubes of n3 voxels are sampled at the same
relative location in each scan, the cubes will be guaranteed to contain at least some
of the same segments of bone. However, the cube in the LR scan will cover a larger
physical space than the cube in the HR scan as the two scans have different voxel
sizes. For the purpose of super-resolution this can be circumvented by sampling a
relatively larger cube in the HR scan containing for instance 2× or 4× the number
of voxels. Although in this case, there are several inconveniences to this approach.
Firstly, the resolution of the LR/HR scan pairs are at an odd multiple of one another,
making the division of the cube’s side length odd. Secondly, the different voxel-sizes
has to be taken into account when sampling.
Due to these implication the approach of sampling an equally dimensioned cube in
each scane was not implemented. Instead, the LR scans were resliced into the refer-
ence frame of the HR scans, ensuring voxel-correspondance between each scan pair.
Reslicing is the process of moving one 3D image V1, using a transformation matrix,
into another 3D image V2 and interpolating the anatomical data of V1 onto a new
voxel-grid that is aligned with the coordinate system of V2. This process also ensures
that the voxel-sizes of the two scans are the same. Within the field of volumetric
image analysis, reslicing is usually the preferred method of registration between im-
ages despite the interpolation process reducing the image quality. In this project,
the trade-off of convenience over quality was preferred and reslicing was performed

CHAPTER 6
Implementation
The main task in this project was the implementation of the 3D super-resolution
GAN model, the loss functions, and the data preparation scheme. In this chapter,
a short outline of the features and methodology behind the implementation will be
presented. The implementation in this project was made possible by several Python
libraries. As 3D super-resolution is a very computationally expensive process, several
performance optimizations were necessary in order to successfully train the model.
6.1
Libraries
All the necessary functions and scripts developed in this project were implemented in
Python using the PyTorch library. PyTorch is a free and open-source tensor library
designed for deep learning and features optimized functions for simplifying the devel-
opment of artificial neural networks.
Apart from PyTorch, several other libraries have been utilized. Some of the notable
Python libraries used in this project are:
• Torchvision: Used as a general image analysis toolbox.
• NiBabel: Used for creating, storing and reading of .nii files
• TorchIO: Used in the loading, sampling and interpolation of 3D image patches.
• Structure tensor library: Used as a temple for the development of tensor-based
functions for computing structure tensors and eigenvalues of 3D volumes.
One of the primary aspects to keep in mind when implementing new loss functions is
the preservation of the gradient. Not in the sense that the gradient is diﬀicult to com-
pute, as PyTorch does this automatically, but because the automatic differentiation
module in PyTorch does not support several numerical operations commonly used in
computer science. For instance, automatic differentiation does not support in-place
memory modification. Therefore it can be diﬀicult to eﬀiciently compute complex
loss functions in PyTorch.
==PAGE:82==
64
6 Implementation
6.2
Performance optimizations
Several performance improvements were implemented to reduce the computational
cost of training the network. The first improvement is the optimization of the bit
precision within some parts of the training loop. This process involved the casting
of variables to a lower precision in order to save computational time and space. The
standard precision used for floating point arithmetic in python is 32 bits per value,
also known as single precision format (FP32). While single precision allows for very
accurate computation, most of the precision is not necessary.
In most cases, 16
bits (also known as half precision) is enough for evaluating the convolutions of the
super-resolution model. This theoretically means the parameters of the network can
be represented using half the computational space. Although in practice this often
cannot be achieved as some computations still have to be done in single-precision or
higher in order to avoid numerical instabilities.
The bit precision optimization was implemented using the mixed-precision automatic
casting functionality of PyTorch. PyTorch achieves this by automatically casting of
computed values down to a user-specified precision, in this case 16-bit floating point.
To avoid complications with values of different precision, the lower precision values
are scaled using a scaling function which aims to reduce the probability of over/under-
flow errors. In this project, the inference process of the generator, the discriminator,
as well as the computation of loss functions was handled in half-precision. The only
exception to this was the computation of structural loss, which was handled in 64-bit
double-precision. In total, the addition of mixed-precision casting ended up reducing
the memory consumption by approximately 25%.
The second notable performance optimization lies within the data loader. The role of
the data loader was to fetch data samples and augment these before parsing them to
the network in the form of batches. In the field of machine learning, as image datasets
may contain tens of thousands of images, it is common practice to implement a data
loader which loads each file on-demand. This way the entire dataset does not need to
be loaded into memory. This approach was however not very practical for the femur
dataset as the entire dataset only contains four samples but the size of each sample is
several gigabytes. Thus, it was instead much more computationally eﬀicient to load
all four samples in the beginning of training rather than on-demand. To accomplish
this a custom data loader was implemented based on the Queue class within TorchIO.
The working principle behind this data loader is illustrated in figure 6.1.
After all bone scans and segmentation images are loaded into memory, a user-specified
number of processes sample pairs of low- and high-resolution patches inside the bone
regions. These image pairs are loaded onto a queue of a certain length. Once the
queue is full, the pairs are shuffled in order to ensure maximum variability within
each batch of image patches. Batches are then popped from the queue, after which
the LR patches are downsampled according to the specified upscaling factor.

6.4
Training details
As detailed in chapter 5, the MRI brain dataset consists of 183 samples, of which 168
were used for training and the remaining 15 were used for validation. Each super-
resolution GAN model was trained for 75 epochs, or 12600 training examples, which
took around 6-8 hours per session depending on the loss configuration. The batch
size was set to three, which was the highest permitted amount to avoid exceeding the
GPU memory limit. The weights were updated using the ADAM optimizer config-
ured with a learning rate of γ = 10−3 and β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999.
For the dataset of femur CT-scans, the same batch size and optimizer was used. Of
the four scans in total, three of the scans were used for training while the final scan
was used for validation. For each epoch, 150 randomly sampled patches were collected
for each scan in the training set. With a batch size of three, this resulted in 150 back-
ward passes per epoch. Each model was trained for 50 epochs, equating to 22500
training examples in total which took around 16-18 hours per model. For validation,
60 random samples were collected from the validation scan after each training step.
The size of the low-resolution image patches was set to 40×40×40 voxels for all train-
ing sessions. All models used a 4× upsampling scheme, resulting in super-resolution
patches of size 160 × 160 × 160.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
8.1
Loss impact
In this project, the adoption of L1 loss as opposed to L2 loss was based on the ob-
servation that the L2 loss tended to excessively smooth the super-resolution outputs.
This was discovered during the initial testing of different loss functions. Although
the addition of L2 loss yielded super-resolution outputs with minimal noise, it also
resulted in blurred edges and textures, which were visually unappealing in compar-
ison to L1 loss. This is not surprising as L2 often correlates poorly with perceived
image quality due to the metric emphasizing larger errors while being more tolerant
to smaller errors, regardless of the underlying structure in the image [66].
The inclusion of total variational loss enhanced the edges and reduced noise arti-
facts in the output, providing better performance than the model using only L1 loss.
Overall, the total variational loss behaved as expected, penalizing local fluctuations
to produce smoother outputs. To achieve sharp super-resolution outputs, the GAN
model is forced to increase LTV to better match the high-frequency textural details
of the high-resolution images, see figure 7.4. As such, to preserve sharpness in the SR
output, a small weight was deliberately chosen for the total variational loss. A higher
weight could have been chosen for a more pronounced loss impact but a lower weight
was preferred to demonstrate the benefits of the loss in conjunction with L1 loss.
The gradient and laplacian loss functions showed similar behavior which is unsurpris-
ing given the close relation between the two. The laplacian consists of second-order
derivatives, while the gradient consists of first-order derivatives, but both functions
put emphasis on edges. Given the second-order derivative’s higher sensitivity to in-
tensity changes, it is logical that the laplacian loss resulted in slightly sharper edges.
Remarkably, neither the gradient or the laplacian loss resulted in an improvement in
SSIM. One plausible explanation is that the employed weight values might have been
chosen too small to effectively impact the optimization process. It is also conceiv-
able that the SSIM might not be as responsive to structural variations as originally
assumed.
The perceptual loss was very effective at improving the visual appeal of super-resolution
outputs. While the perceptual loss provided very high sensitivity for finer edges and
==PAGE:108==
90
8 Discussion
textures, it often led to an increase in falsely fabricated details compared with other
auxiliary loss functions. However, this aligns well the objective of the perceptual loss
which emphasizes enhancing perceptual quality rather than penalizing edge differ-
ences, unlike the gradient and laplacian loss functions.
The structural loss showed no apparent advantages for 3D super-resolution compared
to previous loss functions. The inclusion of a structural loss term was expected to
enhance the edges of upscaled image patches, aligning them better with the struc-
ture of HR samples. The shortcomings of the structural loss could be due to the
Log-Euclidean distance metric or the back-propagation of the gradient. Due to the
complexity of the structural loss implementation, it is plausible that numerical insta-
bilities of the gradient during back-propagation prevented the generator from effec-
tively minimizing the structural loss. Another concern is the behavior of the distance
metric’s gradient when the SR and HR images are very dissimilar, which is often
the case in the initial stages of training. The logarithmic slope being small for large
arguments could explain the generator’s struggle to reduce the structural loss despite
large structural disparity.
Regarding the average discriminator classification output shown in figure 7.19, achiev-
ing D(ISR) = D(IHR) = 1
2 before the end of training is not equivalent to the gen-
erator having perfectly solved the super-resolution problem.
This is because the
discriminator improves in conjuction with the generator and therefore requires addi-
tional training to achieve optimality. Therefore, despite the classification difference
being close to zero for all models in the beginning of training, none of the generators
produce perfect examples since the discriminator hasn’t yet grasped the distribution
of the real data samples. Over the course of several tests, it was found that structural
loss and total variational loss often caused the discriminator classification difference
to increase earlier compared to other loss functions.
This observation was logical
given that both metrics were designed to restrict the generator into either producing
less noise or follow the structure of the LR input, rather than help it produce more
perceptually convincing examples.
8.2
Real-world application
Considering the results presented in chapter 7, the developed super-resolution model
managed to successfully fulfill its purpose of improving the visual quality of 3D image
patches extracted from either MRI- or CT-scans. Depending on the loss configura-
tion and the training set, the model showed different levels of performance. Although
overall, the super-resolution framework managed to drastically improve visual quality
over nearest-neighbour and 3D cubic B-spline interpolation. However, it is still up for
debate as to whether or not this framework could be applied in a real-world situation.
==PAGE:109==
8.3 Instabilities
91
While the GAN model was able to significantly improve the visual quality of medical
images, the model still fabricates some details in order to produce more perceptually
convincing super-resolution outputs. In a real-world scenario, producing such false
details could be very harmful as radiologists could be mislead into making a wrong
or otherwise inaccurate diagnosis. Not only that, but the GAN model also fills in
details based on the training examples it has previously seen. As such, since most of
the training examples come from normal/healthy individuals, there is a higher likeli-
hood of the GAN fabricating false details that more closely resemble healthy tissue.
Conclusively, abnormalities which would be of high importance to the radiologist
might get suppressed during the process of super-resolution. Therefore, despite the
large potential of GANs for improving the quality of medical images, the original
low-resolution scan should always be used as a cross-reference.
In order to reliably apply GAN-based super-resolution models for medical imaging,
functionalities that prevent the fabrication of false details are required.
However,
this is an extremely diﬀicult task as much of the high-frequency information has to
be inferred in order to produce a visually appealing super-resolution output. In this
project, a structural loss function was developed with the aim of solving the problem
of having the GAN model accurately replicate structural details. Unfortunately, the
performance of the models trained using this structural loss term weren’t very impres-
sive. However, loss metrics utilizing structure tensors is a very new field of research,
given further development, there might still be potential for the application of these
loss functions.
Despite the implications of applying the implemented super-resolution model in a real
clinical setting, this project has shown that GANs and densely-connected networks
show great potential for improving the quality of medical images.
8.3
Instabilities
As shown in chapter 7, instabilities were encountered during the testing of differ-
ent auxillary loss functions, which persisted when using both the MRI brain scan
dataset and the femur dataset.
While the exact reason behind these instabilities
is not known, several sources may be accountable for these. For one, because the
optimality conditions of G and D are interconnected via the adversarial loss term,
when the discriminator learns, it actually changes the optimization landscape of the
generator. As a result of this, if the discriminator improves in such a way that the
generator’s optimization landscape does not have a clear global convex shape, the
generator can easily get stuck in a local minimum, causing the generator to stop
improving.
Conversely, if the optimization landscape becomes very irregular, the
generator performance can be degraded as a result of the parameters overshooting
into a region with very high loss.
==PAGE:110==
92
8 Discussion
The spikes in loss could also be attributed to the back-propagation of numerically
unstable gradients. Some auxiliary loss functions involve long sequences of opera-
tions, and therefore, numerical instabilities might occur during back-propagation of
the gradients associated with these losses. This is especially true for the structural
loss, which is by far the most complex. During the implementation of this loss, it was
of high priority to formulate the computation using as few and as simple operations
as possible, exactly for the reason of conserving gradient stability. Despite this, it was
observed that the structural loss was not very well behaving during several training
sessions.
Another explanation for the spikes in loss, which is more likely in the case of the femur
dataset, could be attributed to a combination of bad patch correspondence and low
batch size. Within the field of machine learning, having a large batch size is usually
preferable to a small batch size. This is because when the batch size is relatively
large, each adjustment to the network parameters is computed based on a larger col-
lection of training samples. As a result, the estimated gradient direction that best
minimizes the loss within the optimization landscape is more stable. In turn, there
is a high probability of the network achieving better generalization performance with
each back-propagation.
In contrast, as the batch size is reduced, the gradient’s variability increases, resulting
in more jerky performance improvements. Beneficially, this makes the network more
sensitive to each training example, which helps improve performance on more atypical
training examples that would otherwise be drowned out by more commonly occurring
ones. Although because of the manual alignment of the LR and HR femur scans, the
generator may sometimes receive training examples where the LR/HR patches have
little correspondence. Similarly, the quality of some training examples could also be
low due to the presence of CT-related artifacts. In these situations, the generator
is left to completely guess the contents of the HR patch, which could result in large
discrepancies and therefore high losses. When the batch size is small, although still
unlikely, it’s possible that the generator could receive a batch containing only bad
examples. This would almost certainly result in a big increase in loss, potentially
pushing the generator parameters astray. If the gradient norm is large enough, the
generator could get stuck in a sub-optimal local minimum where it is unable to accu-
rately predict the contents of HR patches.
Instabilities could also be caused by the vanishing/exploding gradient problem. The
problem of exploding or vanishing gradients can itself be caused by several factors.
Generally, if the network is either very deep of very large, the gradient can become
unstable during back-propagation. During initial testing of different network hyper-
parameters, it was found that increasing the patch size of the network over a certain
point could result in unstable gradients. Because the size of network increases dras-
tically with the patch size, these instabilities may therefore have been caused by the
gradient exploding during back-propagation. Despite this, it was still of interest to
==PAGE:111==
8.4 Generator/Discriminator imbalance
93
keep the patch size as large as possible, since the performance of super-resolution
models tend to improve as the resolution of the input increases.
8.4
Generator/Discriminator imbalance
In chapter 2 it was discussed how balancing G and D is hugely important for achiev-
ing a well performing GAN model. For instance, the generator may stop improving
if the discriminator becomes too successful. This is due to the nature of the sigmoid
function in the discriminator, the gradient of which gets smaller and smaller as the
value of the sigmoid approaches either one or zero. If the discriminator produces
classification outputs close to perfect ones and zeros, the back-propagated gradients
associated with these outputs become very small, which can either cause the genera-
tor to stop learning or eventually create an uneven balance between the two.
Apart from introducing potential numerical instabilities, it can be speculated that
the addition of some auxiliary loss functions may unnecessarily induce an imbalance
between the generator and the discriminator. Generally, each auxiliary loss function
can be classified into one of two groups. Firstly, ones that restrict the generator in
some way into reconstructing truthful/clean structures and edges, and secondly, ones
that help the generator to produce more realistic samples by enforcing replication of
textures and local pixel variations. As a result, while some of these loss functions may
help enforce the SR and HR patches to have similar structures, they contribute less
with making the SR patches look perceptually convincing. The generator only cares
about minimizing its total loss so if these loss functions are weighted too strongly,
the generator becomes too concerned about images structures rather than being con-
cerned about fooling the discriminator, which in turn could lead to an imbalance
between G and D. This is especially worrying for the total variational loss, which pe-
nalizes the replication of high-frequency textures. The weighting of each loss function
therefore becomes a compromise between enforcing the generator to produce percep-
tually convincing SR patches, while still limiting the amount of falsely fabricated
details of each super-resolution output.
In continuation of the previous discussion point, due to the risk of potentially desta-
bilizing the training process, the weights for the auxiliary loss were chosen relatively
small compared with the L1 loss weight. While the exact point at which the auxiliary
losses start to have negative impact on the stability is unknown, it was observed that
increasing the weight of especially the structural loss and the laplacian loss functions
had a negative impact on the balance between D and G. This was rather unfortu-
nate, as being able to select a larger weight would make it easier to observe the exact
impact of each particular loss function.
Finally, imbalance could be caused by the reduction of bit precision in the training
loop. In chapter 6, it was mentioned how some calculations in the training loop were
==PAGE:112==
94
8 Discussion
done in 16-bit floating point (FP16) rather than 32-bit (FP32) in order to conserve
memory. In Pytorch when the precision is reduced, the gradients have to be scaled
to reduce the risk of underflow, which is a problem where gradients are equated
to zero due to precision limits. The scaling ensures that the gradients fall within
representable FP16 values but if the gradients are already huge, further scaling can
result in overflow.
Overflow occurs when the gradients, multiplied by the scaling
factor, exceed the maximum limit for FP16. In PyTorch, when this happens, the
back-propagation step is usually skipped automatically. However, each time back-
propagation for either G or D has too be skipped, one of the networks will get a
small advantage over the other.
8.5
Experimental strategy
In chapter 7, it was chosen to showcase each auxiliary loss function individually rather
than progressively adding each loss function to the total generator loss. The ratio-
nale behind this choice was the assumption that, in the absence of any other auxiliary
loss functions, the performance of each trained model would be more sensitive to the
addition of each loss. While a more thorough study would have investigated every
auxiliary loss combination, this was unfortunately not possible in this project. As six
loss functions were implemented in total, testing every combination would be imprac-
tical. Furthermore as the training of each model took several hours, depending on
the dataset and configuration, trying out all these different loss combinations would
be too time-consuming. Despite this, it would have been interesting to investigate
certain combinations of loss functions. For instance, perceptual loss and structural
loss or perceptual loss and gradient loss might have achieved superior performance
than any of the loss functions individually.
Another point for discussion is the choice of the weights for each auxiliary loss func-
tion. In this project, each weight was chosen based on experience acquired during
testing throughout the project. However, it is likely that these parameters could have
been selected in another way which would better highlight the performance impact of
each loss function. This is especially the case for total variational loss, the magnitude
of which was much smaller than the L1 loss term.
In several studies, in order to increase stability and performance, the generator and
discriminator are pre-trained independently before being coupled through the adver-
sarial loss. While this feature was actually incorporated into the final implementation,
it ended up not being used. The rationale for this decision was the notion that if each
model was trained from scratch, it would be easier to discern the influence of each
loss function. However, it is possible that better results would have been obtained if
each GAN had been pre-trained using an appropriate baseline model.

In this project, the same configuration of femur scans were used for training and
validation of all models. However, a more rigid approach for investigating the perfor-
mance of each trained loss model would have been to use some form of cross validation.
By recording results from each trained model using different configurations of femur
scans for training/validation, a more full-fledged analysis of how each loss impacts
performance could be made. Although, because of the number of loss functions and
the time required for training of each model, cross validation was deemed out of scope
for this project.
8.6
Network design
The massive size of the network was one of the primary disadvantages of the imple-
mented GAN model. The majority of the network parameters stemmed from the
reconstruction stage of the generator. Each deconvolution layer required a huge pa-
rameter space as a result of the 4× upsampling of the feature space’s height, width
and depth. Because the network operates in 3D, upscaling by a factor of four results
in a new feature space which has 64× the number of parameters. Considering only
the number of values required to represent the low-resolution batch, the resulting
number is 192000 voxels which is fairly manageable. However, after upsampling by a
factor 4×, this number jumps to an astounding 12288000 voxels just to represent to
batch itself. When the number of feature maps has to be added as an extra dimension
on top, the curse of dimensionality quickly causes the network’s size to explode.
In this work, the number of feature maps of each deconvolution was configured to re-
main constant in accordance with the SRDenseNet architecture [51]. In SRDenseNet,
the number of feature maps of the deconvolutional layers is set to 16k for both input
and output, while in this project, the number of feature maps was set to 8k. This
decision could have been challenged. For instance, a configuration where each de-
convolution would reduce the feature space depth upon upscaling could have been
investigated further. One possible choice would be to halve the number of feature
maps after each deconvolution. This would most likely reduce the required number
of parameters significantly.
Another design change could be to increase/decrease the number of dense blocks in
the generator. In the work by Chen et al. [6], eight dense blocks were used which
showed to have better performance as opposed to the four dense blocks. In this work,
as the memory consumption of the network was already high, four dense blocks was
deemed a good middle ground. The growth factor k, which controls the depth of each
dense block, could also have been changed. In this project, k = 12 was chosen as
suggested by Chen et al. [7]. A smaller k could have been chosen to free up memory
space, which could have been reallocated to increase the patch size.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
Returning to the project objectives stated in chapter 1, it can be concluded that the
overall goal of designing, implementing and testing a GAN-based 3D super-resolution
framework, has been achieved. A data preparation scheme was designed and imple-
mented in PyTorch and TorchIO, which enabled 3D medical images to be used inside
the developed super-resolution framework. Among these were MRI brain scans col-
lected online from the Human Connectome Project, as well as a CT-scans of femur
bones obtained through a collaboration with the 3D Imaging Center at DTU.
Based on the results presented in chapter 7, it was proven that the developed super-
resolution GAN model was able to accomplish its purpose of improving the image
quality of medical images when trained on the aforementioned datasets. With the
goal of improving the performance of the developed GAN model, six different auxil-
iary loss functions were implemented based on both well-known and novel approaches.
Over the course of 12 tests, the performance impact of the various loss functions was
evaluated objectively using PSNR and SSIM, as well as subjectively by examining up-
sampled patches from a dataset of validation samples. Depending on the dataset and
loss configuration, the super-resolution model displayed varying levels of performance.
For instance, the inclusion of a perceptual loss metric enhanced the model’s ability
to reconstruct image textures, yielding more visually appealing super-resolution re-
sults than standard L1 loss. Although the structural loss function did not illustrate
particularly great performance, there might still be potential for improving the recon-
struction of bone trabeculae using structure tensors.
In spite of the shortcomings of some of the implemented loss functions, all of the mod-
els tested were able to significantly improve the image quality over commonly used
3D interpolation schemes. This illustrates that GAN-based super-resolution models
have a huge potential for improving the quality of medical images. Given the perfor-
mance improvements offered by super-resolution GANs, however, super-resolution of
medical images still imposes ethical dilemmas due to the potential for reconstruction
of false details. This is especially the case with GANs, which have a tendency to
falsify images in order to provide more perceptually compelling outputs. But despite
these shortcomings, super-resolution technology will almost certainly enable doctors
and therapists to make better and more accurate diagnoses in the future.

CHAPTER 10
Future Work
Given more time, the following aspects could be studied and implemented in order
to increase the stability, eﬀiciency and performance of the developed super-resolution
model:
10.1
Improved GAN scheme
In order to improve the stability, chance of convergence and performance of each
trained model, a more stable GAN scheme could be implemented. Among different
types of GANs, Wasserstein GAN w. gradient penalty (WGAN-GP) is a popular
choice which instead minimizes Wasserstein distance (also known as Earth Mover’s
Distance) in order to achieve better stability than vanilla GAN. The downside is
that WGAN-GP is slower to converge and usually requires a large batch size. As a
faster but slightly less stable alternative, a relativistic GAN similar to the one used
by ESRGAN [53] could be implemented.
10.2
Dataset
A straightforward way of improving the super-resolution model would be to improve
the quality of the femur dataset. This could be achieved by better alignment of CT-
scans, reduction of artifacts, etc. Another approach to improve the femur dataset
quality would be to enhance the diversity of the data samples. A more extensive and
diverse dataset could help the model better generalize to various real-world scenarios
and improve its robustness.
Data augmentation could also be employed help the
model become more robust and capable of handling images with CT-related artifacts
or other types of noise.
10.3
Hyperparameter search
Within the field of machine learning, it is common practice to perform a random search
to get an estimate of an optimal set of hyperparameters. In relation to this project,
a random search could have been conducted to investigate how the weighting of each
auxiliary loss function impacts performance. An alternative search method is a sweep
==PAGE:118==
100
10 Future Work
over a user-specified range. The performance impact of the optimization parameters
γ, β1 and β2 could also have been investigated using one of these approaches. The
hyperparameters of the network architecture could be studied further. For instance,
how does the performance compare when reducing the growth factor k vs. increasing
the patch size and visa versa? How does reducing the depth of the feature space in
each deconvolution affect performance and does the reduction in depth have a large
or small impact on the network size? How is the performance of the model affected
by increasing/reducing the kernel size of the dense blocks and deconvolutions? These
questions could be investigated further through a randomized search or sweep.
10.4
Ablation study
Another aspect which could be explored further is how the super-resolution model’s
performance is impacted by ablation of different network components. Ablation stud-
ies involve systematically removing or disabling specific components or features of the
model to evaluate their individual contributions to the overall performance. In the
case of the model developed in this project, the number of dense blocks and dense
units in the network could for instance be varied. It would also be interesting to
see how decreasing the number of network blocks vs. increasing the growth factor k
affects super-resolution performance.
10.5
Image quality assessment
Although the PSNR and SSIM provide some information of the performance of each
model output, neither of these metrics are optimal for evaluating model performance.
In chapter 7, it was seen that all models trained using femur scans had somewhat
similar PSNR and SSIM despite large visible differences. Although PSNR is the most
often used performance metric, the results can vary drastically across nearly identical
images. Despite the fact that the SSIM evaluates brightness, contrast, and structure,
it is still not a very reliably metric for perceptual quality. Given more time, it would
be interesting to investigate other metrics to better quantify the performance of each
GAN model. It could for instance be beneficial to conduct a human evaluation study
to assess the visual quality and realism of the super-resolved images, similar to the
MOS used by Ledig et al. to test SRGAN [25]. Incorporating user feedback from real
doctors and therapist on the their preferences could also guide further refinements
of the model. To this end, it could be interesting to develop a cloud-based system
which could take a specific section of a MRI/CT scan as input and then enhance the
image using the model developed in this project. Users could then give feedback on
the super-resolution images to suggest how the model may be improved.

10.6 Loss functions

10.6
Loss functions
In future work, the number of loss functions could also be expanded upon. One in-
teresting loss function is the texture-based loss metric first used in EnhanceNet [42].
Like the perceptual loss introduced in SRGAN, this loss involves the use of a pre-
trained network to extract feature representations of HR/SR patches. The feature
maps from these patches are collected and transformed into Gram matrices, which
are then compared using a squared L2 distance metric. Depending on the size of the
patches extracted, this loss enables very realistic reconstruction of textures.
Another interesting loss metric could be implemented using the popular Canny-edge
detection scheme. A super-resolution scheme incorporating the canny-edge detector
was proposed by Nazeri et al. in their work on edge-informed super-resolution [34]
which was able to achieve impressive results despite very high upscaling factors (up to
8×). In this project, a loss function could be implemented which compares the canny-
edge maps of SR and HR image patches. However, one would need to generalize
the Canny-edge detection technique to 3D in order to use this method for medical
imaging.
10.7
Comparison with other super-resolution models
Comparisons with other super-resolution architectures would be useful to evaluate the
performance of the model developed in this project. For instance, a non-GAN version
of the network could be implemented to investigate the impact of the adversarial loss
term. By implementing several super-resolution models, it would similarly be possible
to benchmark different generator/discriminator architectures in order to investigate
which produce the best output while requiring the least amount of computational
resources.
10.8
Other points
Lastly, design changes to the discriminator could be made in order to make the archi-
tectures of the generator and discriminator resemble each other more. The hypothesis
is that if G and D are based on the same architectural design, the two networks might
learn and improve at a similar rate. Once again, since GANs rely on the balance be-
tween the generator and the discriminator, this could potentially improve the stability
and performance of the GAN as a whole. In SRGAN [25], both G and D make use
of residual network blocks (ResNet blocks) while in this work, only the discriminator
includes ResNet blocks. Therefore, a potential improvement could be to replace the
ResNet blocks in the discriminator with Dense Blocks.
