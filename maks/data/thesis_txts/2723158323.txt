Abstract
In this study, the aim was to explore and learn protein structure patterns from protein con­
tact maps. The objective was to predict protein structure patterns, including alpha helix,
beta strands, coils, anti­parallel beta sheets, and parallel beta sheets , from the contact
maps. This is expected to contribute to our understanding of protein 3D structure and
function. To achieve this, a U­Net model was trained on a dataset of the protein contact
maps, and the performance was assessed through cross­validation. The cross validation
performance yield a dice score of 0.827 on the test set by implementing ensemble which
improves the performance of a single model by combining the predictions of several mod­
els. The results showed that the average length of the protein sequences was around 200
amino acids long, with an average helix length of 12 amino acids and an average strand
length of 5 amino acids.Additionally we benchmarked the method against NetSurfP3 and
had a significantly higher AUC for alpha helix but lower for beta sheets with p­value less
than 0.001 that determined by bootstrapping. This study provides a promising approach
for predicting protein structure patterns from contact maps, and future work could include
exploring more complex protein structure patterns, such as motifs and domains, to study
the evolutionary relationship between proteins.

1.1
Protein background
1.1.1
Protein structure and function
Proteins are complex biomolecules that play a vital role in the structure and function of all
living cells. Protein structure and function are closely linked, with the shape and compo­
sition of a protein determining its ability to perform its specific biological role[1].Proteins
are composed of amino acids joined by bonds to form chains called polypeptides, which
influences the other structures of the protein, including the secondary structure with its
repeating patterns of alpha helices and beta sheets, motif which is ranging from a few
amino acids to a few dozen amino acids that has a specific function, domain which is
the functional folding and unit of a protein, tertiary structure, and finally quaternary struc­
ture[2].
Figure 1.1: An overview of aligning the 3CYT and 1CCR. After alignment, the root­mean­
square deviation of atomic positions between 3CYT and 1CCR is 0.415, and the percent­
age identity score of their seuqence is 57.28%.
Understanding the protein structure can help the protein function study because the func­
tion is essential for understanding the molecular basis of cellular processes and for the
development of drugs and therapies. The specific structure of a protein determines its
function because different regions of the protein can interact with other molecules. For
example, The protein 3CYT[3] and 1CCR[4] from RCSB PDB[5] are both cytochrome
protein , which is illustrated in Fig 1.1.
Techniques such as X­Ray crystallography and NMR spectroscopy are used to determine
the 3D structure of proteins, with X­Ray crystallography involving growing large crystals
of a protein and using X­rays to determine the positions of all atoms in the crystal, while
NMR spectroscopy uses the magnetic properties of atomic nuclei to determine distances
and angles between atoms in a protein and can study proteins in solution[2]. However,
only a small fraction of the known protein sequences have the determined protein struc­
tures by using the experiment methods such as X­Ray or NMR[1]. Nowadays, such as
AlphaFold[1] is developed to predict protein 3D structure accurately facilitating the under­
standing of protein functions.
1.1.2
Protein contact maps
Protein contact maps are graphical representations of the spatial arrangements of residues
in a protein structure. It is a useful tool for understanding the three­dimensional structure
of a protein. The protein contact map that is obtained from RCSB PDB[5] is created by

plotting the distances between the atoms of the amino acid residues, with closer distances
indicating stronger interactions. A contact is defined as a pair of residues that are within
a certain distance of each other. This map can be used to identify regions of the pro­
tein that are in close proximity to each other and are likely to be interacting. From the
Critical Assessment of Techniques for Protein Structure Prediction (CASP) competition,
the residue­residue contact is defined as having a Cβ (or Cα for glycine) distance less
than 8 Å. Cβ contact maps have more extensive information compared with Cα contact
maps, including protein fold topology and secondary structure, as well as intricate details
on protein side­chain packing patterns. These contacts are then represented in the map
using binary values, with a value of 1 indicating the presence of a contact and 0 indicating
its absence[6, 7].
Figure 1.2: A 2D protein contact map with its 3D representation [8]
Likewise, there are several computational tools available for generating protein contact
maps, including PconsC2 and Prospr. These tools use different algorithms to calculate the
distances or the number of contacts between residues, and can provide different levels
of accuracy and resolution. PconsC2 is a deep learning­based method for predicting
protein contact maps, and is an extension of the original Pcons method. Like Pcons,
PconsC2 uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to capture the local interactions
between residues in a protein sequence and make a prediction of the protein contact map.
However, PconsC2 has several improvements over the original Pcons method, including
the use of additional input features and a more complex network architecture[9]. Prospr
is another deep learning­based method for predicting protein contact maps. It uses a
combination of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Recurrent Neural Networks
(RNNs) to capture both the local interactions between residues in a protein sequence and
the sequential relationships between residues[10].
Overall, protein contact maps are a valuable tool for understanding the structure and
function of proteins. They can be used to identify potential binding sites, to predict protein­
protein interactions and to understand the structural basis of protein function.Thus, we
would like to develop a method to assist in the recognition of protein structures and the
understanding of the protein function from protein contact maps.

1.1.3
Previous work
There have been numerous works in the past on protein secondary structure prediction
and these methods are trained on large datasets of protein sequences and their cor­
responding secondary structure annotations. NetSurfP­2.0 predicts protein secondary
structure, solvent accessibility, disorder, and backbone geometry solely from a protein’s
primary sequence, using state­of­the­art methods[11]. NetSurfP 3.0 upgrades from ver­
sion 2.0 by incorporating the ESM­1b protein LM, which significantly reduces the tool’s
running time by over two orders of magnitude without sacrificing accuracy[12].
Deep learning has been utilized in processing protein contact maps, allowing for the iden­
tification of patterns and key features related to protein structure and function. Examples
of such models include FT­COMAR, a residual neural network approach[13], and Deep­
Cov, a CNN that predicts a protein’s 3D structure based on its contact map[14]. The
potential for further study in the field, such as pattern recognition for different protein folds
and functional classification of protein families, is promising with the use of protein contact
maps as a starting point[7].
In the field of protein structure research, much of the focus is on analyzing protein se­
quences to predict their secondary and 3D structures. However, protein contact maps
provide valuable information on both the secondary and 3D structures as well as the
structural units of a protein. With this in mind, we have developed a method that extracts
features from protein contact maps and compares the results with those obtained using
NetSurfP 3.0[12].

1.2.3
Minimum spanning tree
The Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) is a popular approach for clustering protein sequences
based on undirected weighted graphs.
The MST approach for clustering protein se­
quences involves the following steps: (i) The first step is to calculate the similarity score
between the protein sequences. (ii) The calculated similarity scores are used to create
a distance matrix. The distance matrix is a square matrix with the number of rows and
columns equal to the number of sequences. The elements of the matrix represent the
similarity score between the sequences. (iii) Once the matrix is created, the next step is
to build the MST. The MST is a tree­like graph where the edge represents the distance
between all the nodes (protein sequences) in the graph. (iv) After constructing the graph,
the next step is to cluster the sequences. The sequences are grouped into clusters based
on the edges in the MST. Under a given distance threshold, the sequences that are similar
to each other in the MST are grouped into the same cluster[18].

Convolutional layers are the building blocks of CNNs, which apply a set of filters (learnable

1.3.4
Cross­validation
Cross­validation is a technique used to evaluate the performance of a machine learning
model. It involves splitting the dataset into training, validating, and testing sets, where the
model is trained on the training set and evaluated on the validating set. However, in order
to get a more accurate estimate of the model’s performance, this process is repeated
multiple times with different random splits of the data into training sets and validating set.
The performance metrics obtained from each iteration are then averaged to give an overall
performance estimate for the model. Cross­validation is useful because it helps to reduce
overfitting and ensures that the model can generalize well to new data. It is also used for
hyperparameter tuning, which involves selecting the best hyperparameters for the model
to improve its performance.[28].

1.3.5
Early stopping
The concept of early stopping aims to interrupt the training process once the model’s
performance on the validation set starts to deteriorate, thereby avoiding overfitting. The
training stops at a designated ”stopping point”, which is defined as the epoch where the
performance on the validation set fails to improve over a set number of epochs. The op­
timal model is picked based on its performance on the validation set and can be utilized
to make predictions on new data. Early stopping is a straightforward and effective reg­
ularization method that can greatly enhance a machine learning model’s generalization
performance[29].

Bootstrapping
Bootstrapping is a statistical technique for estimating the distribution of a sample statistic
by randomly drawing samples with replacement from given samples. The idea behind
bootstrapping is to use the sample itself to estimate its own distribution, rather than relying
on assumptions about the underlying population. In bootstrapping, multiple resampling
of the original sample are drawn, and each one has the same sample size as the original
sample. For each resample, the desired statistic is calculated and a set of estimates
is obtained. The distribution of these estimates can then be used to make inferences
about the population from which the original sample was drawn. Bootstrapping is a useful
method for obtaining estimates of the variance of a sample statistic when the underlying
population is unknown or when the sample size is small. It is also useful for cases where
standard statistical methods may not be applicable, such as when the data is not normally
distributed or when the sample size is too small for conventional methods[33].

Aim
The aim of this project is to explore and learn protein structure patterns from protein con­
tact maps. The objective of this project is to be able to predict protein structure patterns
from the contact maps, which will potentially enable us to not only obtain a detailed and
accurate representation of proteins with unknown structures, but also to evaluate the sim­
ilarity of the 2D and 3D structures between proteins in the future.
The prediction of protein structure patterns in contact maps, including helix, strands, coils
on the diagonal, anti­parallel beta sheets, and parallel beta sheets on the off­diagonal,
is expected to contribute to our understanding of protein 3D structure. Additionally, our
research has the potential to expand to more complex protein structure patterns, such as
motifs and domains, to study the evolutionary relationship between proteins, paving the
way for future work in this field.

Materials and Methods
The upcoming chapter will cover the dataset preparation process, which involves the uti­
lization of original PDB files and filters to create protein contact maps. These maps are
subsequently auto­annotated with 5 classes (background, helix, strand, anti­parallel beta
sheets, and parallel beta sheets) to establish ground truth images. The chapter will also
discuss the model setup and the methods used to evaluate the model’s performance.
3.1
Dataset preparation
3.1.1
PDB protein dataset
The project involved downloading 5224 proteins from RCSB PDB[5], which were selected
to guarantee their precise structural determination and minimized redundancy with the fol­
lowing specific filtering criteria: (i) The resolution less than or equal to 1.5 Å, (ii) Sequence
identity no greater than 50%.
3.1.2
Dataset processing
Fig 3.1 illustrates how the information required for creating protein contact maps was
collected from PDB files. The process involved retrieving the sequence numbers and
identities of resolved or observed residues from a specific segment of the protein se­
quence. This was accomplished by utilizing the REMARK 465 protocol, which identifies
the residues present in the SEQRES records but not included in the coordinate sec­
tion(Fig 3.1(a)). The SEQRES records contain the primary sequence of the polymeric
molecules listed in the entry. Additionally, the DBREF protocol was utilized to establish
cross­reference links between the PDB sequences (listed in SEQRES record) and the
corresponding database sequence, while also providing the initial and ending sequence
numbers (Fig 3.1(b)). Subsequently, the atomic coordinates of the Cβ atom (or Cα for
glycine) for these residues (Fig 3.1(e)) were located and used to calculate the Euclidean
distance[7] among each pair of residues, and used for the generation of the protein contact
maps.
The PDB dataset was subjected to certain filtering processes. Many PDB annotation files
contained gaps of missing information in the amino acid sequence such as atom coordi­
nates, to overcome this limitation, we split the original protein sequence into fragments
and kept the longest one to generate the contact map.
The procedure for generating the protein contact maps is explained in detail as follows:

3.1.3
Data partition
After dataset filtering the remaining 4132 protein sequences were saved to a fasta file
and used to generate a BLAST database. Each sequence was then searched against
this database using BLAST, and similarity scores were obtained. An E­value less than
1e­10 was considered indicative of high similarity between the query sequence and the
sequences in the database, while values greater than 1e­10 were considered to indicate
low similarity.
To remove redundancy in the dataset, the Hobohm 1 algorithm was applied using a thresh­
old E­value of 1e­100. This ensured that only sequences with a high degree of similarity
were grouped together, and only one representative sequence(first hit) was selected from
each cluster.
Using the E­value as a distance metric, pairwise distances were calculated between the
non­redundant sequences. A minimum spanning tree (MST) was then constructed by
iteratively connecting the sequences with the shortest distance using edges until all se­
quences were connected into a single tree. The MST was used to identify clusters of
related sequences, with the tree being cut at a distance with an E­value of 1e­10. Each
resulting cluster represented a set of closely related sequences that were more distantly
related to sequences in other clusters.
Finally, the non­redundant dataset was partitioned into 5 folds by allocating the clusters
obtained from the MST.
3.2
Auto annotation
In order to train and assess the performance of a segmentation model. It is essential
to label each pixel in the contact maps to create the corresponding ground truth images
(target). In each contact map, each pixel was assigned one of five labels: 0(background),
1(alpha helix), 2(beta strand), 3(anti­parallel beta sheet), or 4(parallel beta sheet). These
labels indicate whether there is a residue­residue contact (<8Å) present in the correspond­
ing region of the protein, and what type of secondary structure it belongs to.
The annotations for alpha helix and beta strand can be obtained from the PDB files and
are shown in Fig 3.1(c) and Fig 3.1(d).The initial and ending sequence numbers of each
are provided, and the last column in Fig 3.1(d) indicates the sense of the strand with
respect to the previous strand in the sheet. A value of 0 indicates that it is the first strand,
1 indicates that it is parallel to the previous strand, and ­1 indicates that it is anti­parallel.
The process of annotation is described in detail as follows:
(i) The process depicted in Fig 3.2 is used to identify and label residue­residue contacts
in the diagonal of the protein contact map for each alpha helix and beta strand. This
involves iterating over the sequence numbers within the structure using the initial and
ending sequence numbers for the structure.
(ii) The process depicted in Fig 3.3 is used to identify and label residue­residue contacts in
the off­diagonal of the protein contact map for each anti­parallel and parallel beta sheets.
This involves iterating over the sequence numbers within those two beta strands.
Each pixel in the protein contact map was labeled or classified according to the residue­
residue contact indicated by each coordinate (i,j) from the Fig 3.2 and Fig 3.3.

3.3.1
The U­Net architecture
Fig 3.4 illustrates the U­Net architecture, which includes several features. A border of
zeros is added before the application of convolutional filters to maintain consistency in
spatial dimensions between the input and output feature maps. Batch normalization is
also incorporated to stabilize the model. The max pooling size is set to 2x2 with a stride
of 2, resulting in down­sampling of the input by a factor of 2 in each spatial dimension
using non­overlapping 2x2 regions. The decoder uses bilinear interpolation to increase
the spatial dimensions of the feature maps during up­sampling. At last, a 1x1 convolution
layer is introduced to combine and refine the learned features from the previous layers,
leading to an accurate classification result.

3.4
Performance measurements
3.4.1
Dice Score
Dice score (1.9) is used for evaluating the segmentation task in this project. It measures
the overlap between the output segmentation and ground truth with a value between 0
and 1. The protein contact maps have been labeled into 5 different classes, which are:
alpha helix, beta strand, anti­parallel beta sheets, parallel beta sheets, and background.
The background class represents residues that are not in contact with each other, with a
distance greater than 8Å. The Dice Score was calculated for each class in each protein
contact map by utilizing one­hot encoding, as described in Fig 1.9. The accumulated dice
scores were then averaged to obtain the final dice score, which served as an indicator of
the performance of the trained model.
3.4.2
Ensemble
With the 5 partitions of the data, we left one for independent evaluation, and trained 4
different models by combining 3 of the remaining partitions and using a 4th partition to
test the model and optimize hyper­parameters. After for the prediction of the indepen­
dent evaluation set we did an ensemblee model by combining the predictions of several
models.The models in an ensemble have the same architecture but trained on different
subsets of data. The final prediction is made by combining the outputs of individual mod­
els and taking an average on the score for each class. The final prediction for each pixel
on the protein contact map is the class with the highest score from the ensemble.

3.4.3
AUC value for Benchmark
In the benchmark task, we calculate the AUC for alpha helix, beta strands, and coils to
assess the performance for the model to classify.
We extended AUC to multi­class problems by applying the one­vs­all (OvA) strategy,
where the model is trained to distinguish each class from the others.
The protein contact maps we use for our predictions have diagonal labels that correspond
to different structural elements of proteins. On the diagonal, the different structural ele­
ments of proteins are labeled as follows: class 1 corresponds to alpha helices, class 2
corresponds to beta strands, and class 0 corresponds to coils.. The off­diagonal contains
information about anti­parallel beta sheets, which are classified as class 3, and paral­
lel beta sheets, which are classified as class 4. To predict beta strands, there are two
possible approaches we can take using our method. The first approach involves solely
extracting predictions for beta strands from the diagonal, which is classified as class 2. An
alternative method is to integrate predictions from both the diagonal and the off­diagonal,
but only when the off­diagonal predictions are classified as either class 3 or class 4. In
this approach, we project the off­diagonal predictions onto the diagonal to incorporate this
information.

Discussion
The results of this project demonstrates the viability of using deep learning models to
predict protein structure patterns in contact maps. The implementation of the U­Net model
and ensemble method was able to achieve high accuracy in its predictions, with a dice
score of 0.82756 for the test set. This represents a novel approach to extracting features
from protein contact maps and highlights the potential for further advances in this field.
One of the key findings of this study was the high prediction ability of our method for he­
lix structures, which was confirmed by the benchmark with NetSurfP 3.0. Our method
showed a higher AUC value of 0.978 compared to the benchmark value of 0.948, indicat­
ing that it is highly capable of detecting helix structures in protein contact maps.
However, the paired boxplot for AUC on the test set also showed that our method CMAP_D,
which only extracts secondary structure features from the diagonal of the protein contact
maps, had a lower prediction ability for strands compared to NetSurfP 3.0. This encour­
aged us to extract additional features from the off­diagonal of the contact maps to im­
prove the accuracy of strand predictions. By combining the off­diagonal predictions for
anti­parallel and parallel structures with the diagonal prediction for strands, our method
CMAP_D+A+P was able to achieve a prediction accuracy of 0.97, which is an improve­
ment over the results obtained with CMAP_D alone.
When benchmarking against NetSurfP 3.0, it is important to note that our model is trained
independently of the evaluation set. The evaluation set contains a set of proteins with low
overlap with the training sequences. On the other hand, NetSurfP 3.0 was trained using
the whole PDB data, which may lead to overestimated performance. Additionally, when
comparing the performance of our method and NetSurfP 3.0, it is possible to observe
some complementarity. Some proteins with low AUC value using our method have high
AUC value using NetSurfP 3.0, while some proteins with high AUC value using our method
have low AUC value using NetSurfP 3.0. For instance, the AUC value of 6TNQ_A using
CMAP_D+A+P is 0.49, whereas it is 0.99 when using NetSurfP3.0. The AUC value of
7KZW_A using CMAP_D+A+P is 0.95, while it is 0.72 when using NetSurfP3.0.
Despite these positive results, the study also revealed some limitations of our approach.
The prediction of strands still needs improvement, especially for proteins with short strands
that are a small fraction of the overall structure. This suggests that the features of the con­
tact maps may be limited in these cases and that our model may not be well­suited for
predicting fine targets. To address this issue, future work could focus on increasing the
size of the training dataset, or exploring alternative deep learning models, such as trans­
fer learning, which involve using a pre­trained model as a starting point and have been
shown to be effective in dealing with small targets.
In conclusion, this project has explored and learned protein structure patterns from protein
contact maps in a novel way, and the results demonstrates the potential for further ad­
vances in this field. The study could be expanded to include more complex protein struc­
tures, such as motifs and domains, which would further contribute to our understanding
of the 3D structure of proteins and the relationships between them.
