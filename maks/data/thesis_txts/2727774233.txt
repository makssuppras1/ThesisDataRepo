Abstract
This thesis presents a detailed comparative analysis of two distinct offshore platform
models: the UMaine semi-submersible platform and the Tension Leg Platform (TLP).
This work aims to offer a comprehensive assessment of the performance attributes, advan-
tages, and disadvantages of both models under identical operational and environmental
conditions. The controller tuning for the semi-submersible platform aims to optimize its
performance and stability is also presented.
The research commences with an exhaustive investigation into the structural and
functional characteristics of semi-submersible platforms. Moreover, the study includes a
system identification phase, utilizing HAWC2, with representative load cases to under-
stand the dynamic responses of the model under various conditions. Subsequent to this,
a series of load and response analyses under diverse simulated conditions are performed.
These simulations mirror real-world situations, encompassing various sea states, wind
conditions, and operational loads.
The results obtained from these analyses are utilized to contrast the performance
of the semi-submersible platform and the TLP. This comparison revolves around critical
performance indicators. The insights gleaned from this thesis offer valuable perspectives
on the relative strengths and shortcomings of semi-submersible platforms and TLPs.
This information can potentially aid in the selection, design, and operation of offshore
platforms for diverse applications. Moreover, the research pinpoints potential areas for
further investigation and enhancement in the design and operation of these platforms.

1.1.1
Development and significance of offshore wind energy
In light of the escalating concerns regarding climate change and carbon emissions in
recent decades, renewable resources have emerged as a reliable avenue towards sustainable
development, given their cost-free and limitless nature.
Among the diverse array of
renewable energy sources, wind energy stands out as a competitive and rapidly evolving
technology with significant potential. In the past, countries such as the United Kingdom,
Denmark, Germany, the United state, and China spearheaded the generation of wind
energy. Presently, an increasing number of nations are recognizing the advantages of
wind energy and are actively participating in the market. According to forecasts by the
Global Wind Energy Council (GWEC) Market Intelligence, it is projected that more than
205 GW of new offshore wind capacity will be installed globally by 2030[1]. Meanwhile,
offshore wind energy is perceived as a major contributor to the global economic recovery.
This is due to its resilience against the pandemic’s impact compared to other energy
sectors, coupled with its consistent growth as shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: New wind turbine installations outlook 2018-2022[1]

Offshore wind energy harnesses the power of the wind over the oceans to gener-
ate electricity. The offshore environment, characterized by its high and consistent wind
speeds, offers a significant advantage over onshore wind energy in terms of the poten-
tial for energy generation. Over the past ten years, offshore wind energy has not only
grown in importance but has also seen a rapid expansion in its capacity worldwide. In
fact, the world installed 6.1 GW of offshore wind in 2020, led by China, making it the
second-highest year for offshore wind installations. This growth has been facilitated by
several advancements in technology and infrastructure. The development of larger and
more efficient wind turbines has increased the energy yield per unit, making offshore
wind farms more economically viable. Innovations in turbine design have also allowed for
deeper water installations, thereby expanding the potential geographical range of offshore
wind farms. An example of this geographical expansion is the Rampion offshore wind
farm, which sits just 13km from the coast of southern England and has a capacity of
400MW[2].
Furthermore, improvements in the supporting infrastructure, including advance-
ments in power transmission and grid integration technologies, have enabled the efficient
distribution and utilization of the electricity generated offshore.
The development of
sophisticated forecasting models has also improved the reliability and predictability of
offshore wind energy, further enhancing its appeal as a viable source of renewable energy.
Looking ahead, offshore wind energy is expected to play an increasingly important role in
the global energy mix. The report[2] forecasts that 235 GW of new offshore wind capac-
ity will be installed over the next decade under current policies. This capacity is seven
times bigger than the current market size and represents a 15% increase on the previous
year’s forecasts. As such, offshore wind energy is poised to contribute significantly to the
transition towards a more sustainable and renewable energy future.
1.1.2
Floater concepts and types
In the realm of offshore wind energy, the choice of floater design is a critical aspect
that determines the efficiency and stability of the wind turbine system. There are primar-
ily three types of floaters used in offshore wind turbines: semi-submersible, tension-leg
platform (TLP), and spar-buoy.

==PAGE:8==

Figure 1.2: Illustration of three different floater types.
The spar-buoy design, characterized by a long cylindrical structure, offers excellent
stability due to its low centre of gravity. However, its deployment can be challenging due
to its large draft, which requires deep waters and makes transportation and installation
complex and costly. A notable example of the spar-buoy design is the Hywind project
by Equinor[3], located off the coast of Scotland.
The project consists of five 6 MW
turbines and has been operational since 2017. Despite the challenges associated with its
deployment, the project has shown high performance, with a capacity factor of around
56% in its first two years of operation.
The tension-leg platform (TLP), on the other hand, is a lightweight design that is
anchored to the seabed using tensioned mooring lines. While TLPs offer good stability
and require less draft compared to spar buoys, they are sensitive to wave frequencies and
require a robust anchoring system, which can be a challenge in certain seabed conditions.
The WindFloat project[4] by Principle Power, located off the coast of Portugal, is an
example of a TLP design. The project demonstrated the feasibility of the TLP design
for offshore wind energy, with a capacity factor of around 45%.
The semi-submersible floater is a type of platform that combines the advantages
of both spar buoys and TLPs. As one of the platforms with the most commercial po-
tential, it has a low draft, which makes transportation and installation easier, and it
offers good stability due to its wide platform and multiple buoyancy elements. The semi-
submersible design is less sensitive to wave frequencies compared to TLPs, making it a
preferred choice for regions with high wave activity. However, the design and operation
of semi-submersible floaters are not without challenges. The complexity of their design
can lead to higher manufacturing costs. Additionally, their stability is highly dependent
on the accurate distribution of buoyancy and weight, requiring precise engineering and
construction. Despite these challenges, semi-submersible floaters have gained popularity
in the offshore wind energy sector due to their versatility and performance. The Kincar-
dine Offshore Wind Farm[5], located off the coast of Scotland, is a good example of a
semi-submersible design. The project represents one of the largest floating wind farms in

1.1.3
Load analysis on a semi-submersible floating offshore wind turbine
Floating wind turbines are typically deployed in deep waters, often several kilometres
offshore. The primary forces exerted on these structures arise from the wind and the
marine environment, as depicted in Figure 1.3. Generally, these loads can be categorized
into:
Figure 1.3: Loads on a floating offshore wind turbine. [6]
Page 4 of 96
==PAGE:10==
Master Thesis
Introduction
• Aerodynamic loads: These are forces exerted on the wind turbine’s rotor, nacelle,
and tower due to wind flow.
• Hydrodynamic loads: These are forces acting on the semi-submersible floater and
mooring lines due to waves, currents, and tides.
• Gravitational loads: These are forces due to the weight of the structure and marine
growth on it. With the development of large-scale floating offshore wind turbines,
this load will become more and more important.
• Mooring loads: These are forces from the station-keeping system that maintains
the position of the floating structure.
• Structural loads: These are internal forces due to the flexibility of the structure.
• Turbine-induced loads: These are forces related to tower shadow, generator torque,
and controller effects.
• Environmental loads: These include forces due to icing, earthquakes, and lightning.
The design process for a semi-submersible floating wind turbine involves a thorough
assessment of the most critical loads. This includes structural response, aerodynamics,
hydrodynamics, and the mooring system. Both numerical simulations and experimental
tests are employed to gain valuable insights into the performance and feasibility of the
floating wind turbine under consideration. However, the engineering challenge lies not
just in conducting these tests and simulations, but in integrating the findings in a rational
manner. This is particularly relevant for semi-submersible floaters, where the interaction
between the hydrodynamic loads on the floater and the aerodynamic loads on the turbine
can lead to complex dynamic responses. Understanding these interactions is crucial for
optimizing the design and operation of semi-submersible floating wind turbines.

1.2.1
Structural dynamics
The structural dynamics of a floating offshore wind turbine, particularly one with a
semi-submersible floater, can be understood in terms of six degrees of freedom (DoFs).
These include three translational movements: surge, sway, and heave, and three rotational
movements: roll, pitch, and yaw, as depicted in Figure 1.4.

Newton’s second law and can be expressed in matrix form as:

1.2.2
Aerodynamics
The aerodynamics of a floating wind turbine are primarily influenced by the wind’s
interaction with the rotor, nacelle, and tower. These interactions generate aerodynamic
forces, which are typically calculated using the Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory.
This theory divides the rotor blade into small elements and calculates the forces on each
element based on the local flow conditions. The total aerodynamic force and moment
are then obtained by integrating the elemental forces and moments along the blade span.
The BEM theory is expressed as:
F = 1
2ρAV 2C
where F is the aerodynamic force, ρ is the air density, A is the area of the blade
element, V is the wind speed, and C is the aerodynamic coefficient which includes lift
and drag coefficients. The aerodynamic forces can cause significant dynamic responses
in the floating wind turbine system. For a semi-submersible floater, these responses are
further influenced by the hydrodynamic forces from waves, currents, and tides.
The
interaction between the aerodynamic and hydrodynamic forces can lead to the complex
motion behaviour of the semi-submersible floater.
The aerodynamic damping is another important factor that affects the dynamic
response of the floating wind turbine. The aerodynamic damping is related to the changes
in aerodynamic forces due to the motion of the turbine. It can have a significant impact
on the stability of the turbine, particularly in the pitch directions. The aerodynamic

Hydrodynamics
Hydrodynamics plays a crucial role in the design and operation of semi-submersible
floating wind turbines. The interaction between the water flow and the offshore structure
is primarily governed by potential flow due to the high Reynolds number. Linear or Airy
wave theory, which is based on potential flow and linearized boundary conditions, is often
used to describe the incident wave field. This theory assumes a flat bottom and small
wave amplitudes, and is represented by the Laplace equation for the velocity potential:
∇2Φ = 0
(1.3)

allows the radiation and diffraction problems to be solved separately. In the radiation
problem, the body is forced to oscillate with frequency ω in otherwise calm water. In the
diffraction problem, the body is fixed and exposed to regular waves with frequency ω and
unit amplitude.

Mooring dynamics play a pivotal role in the study of floating wind turbines. The
primary function of the mooring system is to maintain the position of the floating wind
turbine while allowing it to respond to environmental conditions. The dynamic behavior
of the mooring system is influenced by the environmental loads, the motion of the turbine,
and the properties of the mooring system itself.

1.3
Thesis Objectives
The primary objective of this thesis was to understand the advantages and disadvan-
tages of different types of floater substructures, specifically semi-submersible platforms
and TLPs, for FOWTs. The research aimed to provide a comprehensive comparison of
these two platforms in terms of their performance, load and dynamic characteristics, and
suitability for FOWTs.
The research was divided into four main parts: the FOWT system identification in
HAWC2, controller tuning, load and dynamic analysis of semi-submersible FOWTs, and a
comparison of semi-submersible platforms with TLPs. The findings from these parts were
intended to contribute to the design and operation of FOWTs, enhancing their efficiency
and reliability. The ultimate goal is to show the performance and characteristics of semi-
submersible platforms and TLPs for FOWTs and contribute to the design and operation
of FOWTs, enhancing their efficiency and reliability.

2.1
Description of IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine
This study is based on the International Energy Agency’s 15 MW reference wind
turbine (IEA-15-240-RWT), collaboratively designed by the National Renewable Energy
Laboratory (NREL), the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), and the University of
Maine (UMaine), under the auspices of the IEA Wind Task 37. The blade cross-sections
are crafted using the DTU FFA-W3 series of aerofoils, which ensure efficient aerodynamic
performance. The wind turbine tower, designed for submersion of 15 m above the Still
Water Level (SWL), results in a flexible tower length of approximately 129.50 m. This
tower features a varying diameter - starting from a wider 10 m at the base and tapering
to 6.50 m towards the top - designed for optimal strength and stability. The detailed
parameters of the turbine can be found in [8].
Created as an open design, the reference wind turbine provides a platform conducive
for advancing research and pedagogical purposes. Predominantly, the reference turbine
operates as an offshore turbine, stabilized with a bottom-fixed monopile foundation. It’s
worth noting, however, that for different floater designs, certain adaptations must be
made to the tower parameters. These alterations, which might involve modifying the
tower height, thickness, and mass, are essential to maintain the stability and operational
efficiency of the turbine in varying sea conditions.

2.2
UMaine VolturnUS-S semi-submersible platform
The VolturnUS-S is a steel semi-submersible floater, incorporating a single 10 m
diameter main column, onto which the wind turbine tower is mounted[9]. This main
column is structurally reinforced with three radially placed buoyant columns, each 12.5 m
in diameter, all connected by rectangular bottom pontoons and radial struts. In order to
achieve the required draft for stability and performance, a combination of fixed iron-ore-
concrete ballast (located at the bottom of the offset columns) and seawater ballast is used.
The seawater ballast, which floods the pontoons and the tower supporting column, helps
adjust the system’s buoyancy based on operational and environmental requirements. The
overall draft of the floater, with the ballasts in place, is designed to be 20 m below SWL.
This FOWT system is designed for deployment in water depths up to 200 m, where it’s
held in place by three catenary moorings, placed equidistantly at 120°. These mooring
lines are fastened to the fairlead of the outer columns at a depth of 14 m below the
SWL. The key parameters and a schematic of the UMaine VolturnUS-S semi-submersible
platform, its mooring design and floating tower are detailed in Table 2.2 and Figure 2.1.

2.3
Constructing the floating offshore wind turbine model in
HAWC2
Building a Floating Offshore Wind Turbine model in HAWC2 involves a systematic
process. The main file consists of nine blocks, each with a specific purpose. The first
block, the simulation block, contains information about the simulation’s duration, the
solver type, the convergence limits, the HAWC2 file, and the time step of the simulation.
The new_htc_structure block contains several sub-blocks, each representing a main
body of the structure.
These include the "interface floating body" (representing the
floater), the tower, the top of the tower, the connector between the tower top and the
shaft, the shaft, the three hubs connecting to the blades, and the three blades. Each
main body has its own structural parameters.
The "interface floating body" serves as an interface between HAWC2 and WAMIT
software. The hydrodynamic properties and inertia loads of the floater are transferred
from WAMIT to HAWC2 using the ESYSWamit interface.
The orientation block contains information about the change in orientation of the
coordinate system of one main body compared to the coordinate system of an adjacent
main body. The constraint block describes the relative motion situation between adjacent
main bodies.
The wind block contains information about the air density, the mean wind speed,
the turbulence intensity, and the orientation of the wind field. The aero drag block is
used for the simulation of aerodynamic drag force at a specified main body. The aero
block is used to provide the aerodynamic specification of the rotor. The hydro block is
used to specify the key water and wave properties.
The force block is introduced in case an external force is to be applied. The control
block contains the partial and full load control parameters that arose from the control

3
Controllers tuning
Wind turbine control systems are essential for efficient power generation and dura-
bility under diverse operational circumstances, with a primary objective of balancing
maximum power production, load reduction, and system stability. This chapter aims to
tune the controller for the IEA 15MW RWT on the UMaine semi-submersible platform,
based on DTUWEC (DTU Wind Energy Controller)[11], which has proven effective in
HAWC2 applications, with an emphasis on enhancing platform stability in above-rated
wind speed conditions to solve the notorious negative damping problem. Moreover, an
exploration into the feasibility and versatility of this controller tuning approach and gain
selection applied to generic floating wind turbines is envisaged.
In this section, a linear model of the FOWT is derived based on the comprehensive
aero-servo-hydro-elastic model HAWC2[12]. Subsequently, the chosen control strategy
is expanded, and a controller is designed predicated on the linear model. Ultimately,
simulations of one representative load case are performed to assess the effectiveness of
the controller in wind and wave conditions. Noted that this chapter only provides an
introduction to the whole tuning process of the UMaine semi-submersible FOWTs such
as only including the tuning of the nature frequencies and damping ratio of the pitch
controller and gain schedule of the torque compensator. The more detailed introduction of
DTUWEC and the verification of the numerical model was addressed by Zhang (2021)[13].
3.1
Linear model development
In order to efficiently estimate the response of floating wind turbines, a linear low-
order model is developed which is a necessary preliminary for control tuning. This model
incorporates four degrees of freedom and, through a series of simplifications and lin-
earization, a linear state-space model is formed using symbolic matrices. This approach
facilitates the model’s applicability to various FOWTs.
This section provides a brief
introduction to the model building process.

controller amalgamates rudimentary control mechanisms such as collective pitch and
generator torque proportional-integral-derivative (PID) controller, along with intricate
control capabilities [11].
A lower limit of 5 rpm is designated for the rotor speed to
mitigate the potential 3P (three times per revolution) resonance interaction with the
inherent tower frequency. Consequently, under the conditions of low wind velocities, the
turbine adjusts the blade pitches to capture maximum power, all while maintaining the
requisite minimum rotor speed. As the rotor speed increases with the wind speed, the
turbine operates at the optimal tip speed ratio at the optimal pitch angle above-rated
wind speeds, and the rotor speed is regulated at its rated value of 7.55 rpm, while the
proportional-integral (PI) controller keeps the rated power by pitching blades collectively.

Load and Dynamic Analysis of Semi-submersible FOWT
Utilizing the model of IEA 15MW RWT with the UMaine semi-submersible floater
in HAWC2, a multitude of simulations were executed for selected design load cases in this 
chapter. The following steps were undertaken to accomplish the comprehensive analysis. 
Time-domain simulations were conducted using the semi-submersible model, followed by
an examination of the loads exerted on the turbine.
4.1 
Simulation set up
The investigation encompasses power production, faults during power production, 
parked conditions, and faults during parked conditions, all in accordance with the IEC 
61400-3 (IEC 61400-3-1, 2019; IEC 61400-3-2, 2019) [18] design standard. A comprehensive 
summary of the Design Load Cases (DLCs) for this study is provided in Table 4.1. Note 
that DLC 1.2 will be presented in the next chapter which discussed the difference between 
the two platforms in detail and will not be covered in this chapter.
The wind shear for turbulent wind generation is modelled using the power law, em-
ploying a coefficient of 0.14 for power production conditions and 0.11 for parked condi-
tions. The generation of the turbulent wind field utilizes the IEC Kaimal spectra, based
on the Normal Turbulence Model (NTM) and the Extreme Turbulence Model (ETM) 
for power production conditions, and the Extreme Wind Model (EWM) is employed for 
parked conditions. The turbulent wind field is generated using the Mann turbulence for-
mat, with a grid size of 208 × 208 m and 1024 grid points (32 × 32). Turbulence intensity 
values for varying wind speeds are considered in line with the IEC(IEC 61400-1, 2019) 
design code. Stochastic waves are modeled using the JONSWAP spectrum for Normal 
Sea State (NSS), Severe Sea State (SSS), and Extreme Sea State (ESS) conditions. Each 
DLC’s simulation time is set according to the IEC 61400-3 design standard and will be 
introduced in each section, with an additional 200 seconds with the external damper
to eliminate the transient state. A consistent timestep of 0.01 seconds is maintained 
throughout all simulations.

4.3.6
DLC 6.1
In conditions where the FOWT is subjected to extreme wind and wave events with 1-
year and 50-year return periods, the rotor is maintained in a parked state. This approach
aims to mitigate the load on the wind turbine and circumvent potential damage to the
overall structure. The generator torque control is disabled to ensure the rotor remains
stationary, while the blades are pitched at 90 degrees, reducing their exposed surface area
to wind loads. The parked conditions persist for 800 seconds, excluding the initial 200
seconds in the post-analysis to disregard any transient effects. Recognized as an ultimate
load case, DLC 6.1 also entails the assessment of extreme loads. Furthermore, the yaw
error with respect to wind direction is accommodated at 8, 0, and -8 degrees. Each yaw
error encompasses six seeds, as recommended by the DLC. For the wind conditions, the
50-year extreme wind speed is considered with the wind shear exponent set at 0.11 and
turbulence intensity at 0.11. Applying the power law for the wind profile yields a wind
speed of 47.5 m/s at the hub height, which is 150 meters above sea level. The sea state
for this DLC incorporates an ESS with a 50-year return period, consisting of a significant
wave height (Hs,50) of 10.7 meters and a peak spectral period (Tp,50) of 14.2 seconds.

where:
• neq,L is the number of cycles to failure. This is a predefined parameter, typically 
set to 1 × 107 cycles.
• Req is the equivalent fatigue load for each wind speed. It is calculated as the mean 
of the load range for each wind speed bin. The load range is determined using the 
Rainflow Counting method, which simplifies a spectrum of varying stress into a set 
of simple stress reversals. This method allows the application of Miner’s rule to 
assess the fatigue life of the component.
• m is the material parameter, also known as the Wöhler exponent for the material. 
This parameter is specific to the material of the component and is typically 4 for 
steel and 10 for composite materials.
• P (V ) is the probability density function of the wind speed. It is calculated using 
the Weibull distribution, which is a common model for wind speed distributions. 
The Weibull distribution is a versatile distribution that can accurately represent the 
statistical properties of wind speed over time. In this work, the local Weibull distri-
bution as proposed by [19] is used, which takes into account the specific 
geographical and climatic conditions of the wind turbine location.
• nT is the total number of seconds in the lifetime of the wind turbine, typically 
calculated as the number of seconds in 20 years.

Conclusion
6.1
Conclusions and key findings
In this thesis, a comprehensive study was conducted on the performance and charac-
teristics of semi-submersible platforms and TLPs for FOWTs. The research was divided
into three main parts: controller tuning, load and dynamic analysis of semi-submersible
FOWTs, and a comparison of semi-submersible platforms with TLPs.
The controller tuning for the IEA 15MW RWT on the UMaine semi-submersible
platform was examined. The primary objective was to balance maximum power produc-
tion, load reduction, and system stability. An innovative control strategy was introduced,
which incorporated an additional loop into the generator torque. This strategy effectively
mitigated the non-minimum phase behaviour in the linear model. The tuning was based
on a 4-DOFs linear model, which enhanced the controller’s efficacy. The key finding in
this part was the successful implementation of the control strategy and its effectiveness
in improving system stability.
The load and dynamic analysis of semi-submersible FOWTs was performed. The
dynamic response of the IEA 15 MW semi-submersible FOWT with respect to wind
speeds was analyzed. The maximum, mean, and minimum values of the dynamic response
were recorded for different Design Load Cases (DLCs). The maximum and minimum load
of the wind turbine for various DLCs were also analyzed. The key finding in this part
was the understanding of the performance of the wind turbine under different conditions,
which could be crucial for the design and operation of FOWTs.
A comparison of semi-submersible platforms with TLPs was conducted. The ex-
treme displacement and fairlead tension of the two platforms were compared. Results
from SWL free-decay tests were included, which provided insights into the damping char-
acteristics of the two platforms. The 10-minute damage-equivalent fatigue with respect
to wind speed for different DLCs was also analyzed. The key finding in this part was
the comparative performance of the two platforms under various conditions, which could
guide the selection of platforms for FOWTs.
In conclusion, this thesis provides valuable insights into the performance and char-
acteristics of semi-submersible platforms and TLPs for FOWTs. The key findings from
this research could contribute to the design and operation of FOWTs, enhancing their
efficiency and reliability. Future research could further explore the implications of these
findings and investigate other types of platforms for FOWTs.

Future research opportunities
The findings from this thesis open up several avenues for future research. Here are
a few potential opportunities:
Exploration of Other Types of Floater Substructures: While this thesis focused on
semi-submersible platforms and TLPs, there are other types of floater substructures that
could be explored, such as spar buoys or barge platforms. Future research could compare
these platforms with semi-submersible platforms and TLPs, or investigate their suitability
for FOWTs.
Advanced Control Strategies: This thesis introduced an innovative control strategy
for the IEA 15MW RWT on the UMaine semi-submersible platform. Future research
could explore other control strategies or further optimize the current strategy to improve
the performance and stability of FOWTs.
Detailed Load and Dynamic Analysis: While this thesis provided a comprehensive
load and dynamic analysis of semi-submersible FOWTs, future research could delve deeper
into this area. For instance, research could focus on the impact of different environmental
conditions or operational scenarios on the load and dynamic characteristics of FOWTs.
Long-Term Performance Analysis: This thesis analyzed the performance of semi-
submersible platforms and TLPs under various conditions. Future research could investi-
gate the long-term performance and durability of these platforms, which would be crucial
for the practical implementation of FOWTs.
Cost-Benefit Analysis: Future research could also include a cost-benefit analysis of
different floater substructures. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding
of the economic feasibility of these platforms for FOWTs.
