Summary
To date million tonnes of pesticides are applied in the agricultural sector each year but
application data for quantifying impacts from pesticide usage is not readily available and
farmers lack tools for selecting proper pesticides. The aim of this study was to support a
more sustainable use of pesticides by developing a starting point for quantifying impacts
from pesticide usage and by aiding farmers to select a more sustainable pesticide given
resistance, environmental and health problems from using pesticides, authorisation, and
costs of pesticide use.
Pesticide application data was collected from 537 EFSA reports from 2009-2017, before
and within the time frame of this study. The application data was systematically set-up in
an application scenario database containing 8758 data points. Each data point contains
information on the pesticide used, the crop, the application method, toxicity factors, target
class etc.. Through this study, it has become clear that pesticides have individual application
patterns and generalising pesticides can be misleading. The 95% density interval of the
application dosage was between 0.01-2.62 kg a.i./ha and the most common number of
applications over the vegetation period was 2. Characterising the database reviled that
correlations between individual parameters of the database are difﬁcult to identify and that
extreme values occur for most parameters.
Three chemical substitution scenarios for lettuce were created and analysed. The scenar-
ios focused on fungicides targeting Downy Mildew, herbicides targeting Winter grass and
insecticides targeting Lettuce Aphids. The pesticides within each scenario were compared
based on their properties (risk of resistance, price, authorisation, ecotoxicity and human
toxicity), damage costs and total costs. The results from the chemical substitution scenarios
showed that by individualising and comparing pesticides sufﬁcient amount of savings can
be achieved, both for the farmer and society. Fosetyl was the most preferable fungicide with
regards to properties and damage costs but the large application amount required raised
the total cost above the two other analysed fungicides. During this study, it was found that
only one herbicide, diquat, is allowed for use on lettuce in Denmark and a substitution can
therefore not take place. Pymetrozine had the most attractive proﬁle out of the analysed
insecticides with regards to properties but acetamiprid had lower total costs and damage
costs from both human and ecotoxicity. Damage costs from ecotoxicity were generally much
lower than human toxicity damage costs.

ciders anvendelsesdata til kvantiﬁcering af deres påvirkninger ikke tilgængelige, og landmænd

at underbygge en mere bæredygtig anvendelse af pesticider. Dette er blevet opnået ved at
udvikle et udgangspunkt for at kvantiﬁcere effekter af pesticidanvendelse, og derved opfordre
landmænd til at vælge et passende pesticid i forhold til resistens-, miljø- og helbredsproblemer

og under tidsrammen for dette speciale. Anvendelsesdataene blev systematisk opstillet
i en anvendelsesscenariedatabase indeholdende 8758 datapunkter.
Hvert datapunkt
indeholder informationer om det anvendte pesticid, afgrøden, anvendelsesmetoden,
toksicitetsfaktorer mv..

omkostning overstiger to andre analyserede fungicider. I løbet af dette speciale blev det
konstateret, at kun ét herbicid, Diquat, et tilladt i Danmark til anvendelse på salat, hvorfor
et substitut ikke er muligt. Blandt de analyserede insekticider have Pymetrozine den mest

sicitetsskadeomkostninger samt total omkostning. Skadeomkostningerne fra økotoksicitet

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Pesticides have been used in massive amounts in the agriculture sector since world war II.
Pesticides are synthetic chemicals that kill pests or unwanted weeds on or around crops
and livestock. Pesticides can be divided into categories depending on their targeted pest.
The largest categories of pesticides are herbicides, insecticides, and fungicides (Matthews,
2015). The introduction of pesticides has beneﬁted the agriculture sector by improving the
crop yield, thus improving the economic aspects of agriculture and the production rate of
crops (Damalas and Eleftherohorinos, 2011). Pesticides are currently the most common
pest control management and since the introduction of pesticides to agricultural practices,
thousands of pesticides have been designed. Some pesticides are selective towards a spe-
ciﬁc pest while others are broad spectrum pesticides killing a variety of pests (Matthews, 2015).
Despite the beneﬁcial effects that pesticides have on crop yield these chemicals can have
negative effects on human health and the environment. Rachel Carson raised an important
aspect in her book Silent Spring from 1962, where she famously expressed her concerns about
the harmful impacts that pesticides create (Rachel, 1962). The interest in harmful side effects
of pesticides has been growing ever since and a large portion of the population is concerned
about the long-term effects of pesticides in food products and their local environment. Never-
theless, pesticide usage increased dramatically between 1960 and 2000 and is still increasing
in many countries (Blair et al., 2014).
"A Who’s Who of pesticides is therefore of concern to us all. If we are going to live so
intimately with these chemicals eating and drinking them, taking them into the very marrow
of our bones - we had better know something about their nature and their power."
Rachel Carlson, Silent Spring. 1962. Page 17.
Pesticides are intentionally sprayed on agricultural ﬁelds by farmers to kill pests, however,
pesticides also affect non-targeted areas. When pesticides are applied to the ﬁeld a part of the
pesticide is taken up by the plant and the surrounding soil, but the substantial part, up to 99%
(Zhang et al., 2011), is lost towards non-targeted areas due to wind drift and other processes.
The pesticides will thereby end up in the atmosphere, water bodies and soil outside of the
agricultural ﬁeld (Fantke et al., 2012), contaminating the environment.
1.1
Current pesticide practices and usage
The worldwide consumption of pesticide is around 3.5 million tons per year spanning a mar-
ket worth of 45 billion $ (Eyhorn et al., 2015). Currently, there are approximately 267 pesticides
authorised for use in the EU but around 1100 in the US (Bozzini, 2017). The pesticide is the
1
==PAGE:15==
speciﬁc chemical that kills or suppresses the pest, often also called the active substance (a.s.)
or the active ingredient (a.i.). The pesticide product that farmers and others can purchase
on the market is called a plant protection product and contains the pesticide among other
chemicals, such as water, solvents, formaldehyde, antifreeze agent etc. (De et al., 2014).
Pesticide application patterns depend greatly on crop, climate and geography (Mailly et al.,
2017). Limits and rules for pesticide usage is controlled by governing bodies but the farmers
affect the real application patterns within these limits as they select which pesticide is applied,
how much and when. Farmers buy pesticides in the form of a plant protection product from
retailers and apply on their ﬁelds by different application methods. The variety of pesticides
that a farmer can choose from depends on the farmer’s resident country and economic status.
The farmer’s socio-economic state, knowledge, perceptions and preferences all affect the
farmer’s selection of pesticides (Damalas and Eleftherohorinos, 2011). The most suitable
application method depends on the crop, the ﬁeld size, and regulations. Current application
methods of pesticides also differ globally, with more technically advanced methods used in
developed countries. Regulations on application methods also differ, e.g. aerial sprayers with
small planes or helicopters are banned in the EU but allowed in USA on big ﬁelds (European
Food Safety Authority, nd).
Governments, particularly within the EU, have for decades been promoting integrated pest
management (IPM) approaches to help reduce pesticide usage. The IPM practice is a pest
control with low chemical usage that includes rotational cropping, preventative measures,
cultural control, genetically modiﬁed plants and more (Sparks and Nauen, 2015). Replacing
conventional pesticide usage with an IPM system requires increased workload, costs and
education for farmers (Mailly et al., 2017). In spite of these efforts to implement IPM pest
control, pesticides remain the dominant pest control tool for farmers all over the world. It is a
complex process for a farmer to change pest control management, requiring information and
knowledge on various aspects. Farmers often have incomplete information on what methods
and techniques are available, which is one of the reasons for the failure of the IPM integration
(Damalas and Eleftherohorinos, 2011).
1.1.1 Approval of pesticides in the EU
The process of approval and registration of a pesticide in the EU is controlled by the European
Food Safety Authority (EFSA), the European Commission (EC) and the member states of the
EU. Currently, pesticides are regulated under the framework Regulation (EC) No 1107/2009
(Panizzi et al., 2016). The industry is responsible for applying for approval of a speciﬁc pes-
ticide. The application must contain scientiﬁc information on; the substance and studies
on biological efﬁcacy, toxicity in mammals, the substances metabolic pathway in crops and
livestock, possible risks for consumers, ecotoxicity proﬁle and the environmental fate and be-
haviour of the active substance (Storck et al., 2017). The application is received by a member
state which evaluates the information. The member states assessment of the pesticide is then
peer reviewed by the Pesticide Unit of EFSA (Panizzi et al., 2016). The conclusion of this peer
review is sent further on to the EC which decides in collaboration with the member state if
the pesticide should be approved.
2
==PAGE:16==
After approval of the pesticide, the industry can apply for authorisation of a plant protection
product containing the pesticide. This application describes the intended use of the pesticide,
which crops it can be used on and how much can be applied per hectare. The application is
assessed and approved by the national authority that receives it. The assessment includes a
proposal for maximum residue limits (MRLs), which is the highest level of pesticide residue
that is legally acceptable in food or feed when pesticides have been applied according to
guidelines. EFSA assesses the MRL and performs a risk assessment in a reasoned opinion that
is sent to the EC. The EC in collaboration with the member state then decides if the proposed
MRL is acceptable. If the MRL is accepted the member state can authorise and register the
plant protection product for sale and use. Member states are divided into North Europe and
South Europe zones in the EU and only if there is sufﬁcient information for the member state
zone it can register the plant protection product for use (European Food Safety Authority, nd).
The active substances are approved for a period of 10 years, but 15 years for low-risk sub-
stances (Storck et al., 2017). After 10 years a new application needs to be addressed. Moni-
toring of pesticide use is performed at member state level and is in Denmark conducted by
the Danish Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), miljøstyrelsen. Residues of pesticides in
food commodities are also monitored at member state level and evaluated on an annual basis
by EFSA (European Food Safety Authority, nd).
1.2
Current problems in selecting pesticides at the farm
level
Pesticides are a complex area due to the large amount of pesticides that exist, the mutli-criteria
reasoning behind their regulation and the many stakeholders that control or are affected by
pesticide usage.
Understanding the reasoning behind a farmer’s choice of pesticides is difﬁcult and depends
on many parameters. Few studies have been carried out with questionnaires to observe
what factors are important for farmers when selecting pesticides. These studies have shown
that the most important factor to farmers is efﬁcacy (Grieshop et al., 1992; Mengistie et al.,
2015; Damalas and Koutroubas, 2014), how effectively the pesticide kills the pest. Toxicity
is ranked low compared to other factors such as availability, personal experience, and user
friendliness (Grieshop et al., 1992). The ranking of pesticide cost is dependent on farmer’s
income and has more inﬂuence on low income farmers (Grieshop et al., 1992; Mengistie et al.,
2015; Damalas and Koutroubas, 2014). Studies however, do not completely agree that farmers
tend to buy pesticides at lower prices. A research by Goeb et al. (2016) from 2016 showed
that some farmers believe in a price-quality correlation and tend to buy more expensive
pesticides that are believed to perform better. Moreover, a study from China Wang et al. (2015)
observed that Chinese farmers mostly follow retailers advice when choosing pesticides. It
should be noted that these studies originate from different geographical areas (USA, Ethiopia,
China and Greece) and time frames. It is possible that the farmers have altered their mindsets
and perceptions since these studies were performed, as concerns about environmental and
human health effects of pesticides have increased (Hashemi and Damalas, 2010).
3
==PAGE:17==
Several studies show that farmers are using banned, restricted and unregistered pesticides
(Wang et al., 2015; Thuy et al., 2012). There are also examples of farmers not following the
set guidelines for speciﬁc pesticides. Due to lack of regulation, education, and information
on pesticides in many developing countries, farmers often choose more toxic pesticides that
effectively kill pests but can negatively affect human health and the environment (Thuy et al.,
2012). Another problem in current pesticide usage is the overuse of pesticides (Ntow et al.,
2006; Damalas and Koutroubas, 2014; Khan et al., 2015). Overuse can occur due to insufﬁcient
education and training of farmers but also as a consequence of pesticide resistance (Damalas
and Eleftherohorinos, 2011). Misuse of pesticides is extensive and can have an adverse effect
on ecosystems and human health, especially on the farmer’s health (Khan et al., 2015). Many
countries have no record of the amount of pesticides that have been applied (Khan et al.,
2015), making it difﬁcult to estimate the potential environmental and human health effects. It
is evident that many farmers make uninformed and unreasonable decisions when selecting
pesticides and pesticide guidelines.
1.2.1 Pesticides availability
In 2009 the European Union released a new directive with the aim to reduce the use of pes-
ticides (European Parliament, 2009). The directive resulted in withdrawal of 704 pesticides
and currently, 276 pesticides are approved for usage within the EU(Karabelas et al., 2009).
However, in other parts of the world, a much larger portion is authorised and approved for
use (Bozzini, 2017). This difference is also remarkable in the MRLs, were the MRLs set by EU
are lower compared to MRLs in USA and China. There are differences both in regulations and
restrictions on pesticides between countries, which sets a barrier for trade, as imported crops
to the EU are also required to follow the EU MRLs (Bozzini, 2017).
For many decades scientists designed new pesticides to replace the old ones but recently
the development of new pesticides is becoming more difﬁcult and expensive, resulting in
fewer alternatives for several pests (Borel, 2017). Restrictions in the availability of alterna-
tive pesticides with lower toxicity and alternative pest control measures can be a barrier for
the development of sustainable and economically competitive pest management approach
(Meissle et al., 2010; Simon et al., 2011). The focus is shifting towards genetically modiﬁed
crops (Borel, 2017), however, pesticide usage will remain the main pest control mechanism
for years to come making it an important research area.
1.2.2 Pest resistance
Pesticides are used to prevent destruction of crops by different pests or unwanted insects,
fungus, weeds etc.. Pest appearance depends on a complex system of ecological, climate and
socio-economic factors and pests have always been a natural part of agricultural practices
(Antle, 2015). Pesticide resistance is an expanding issue in the agricultural world (Georghiou,
2012). Resistance occurs when a pesticide is applied but does not manage to kill all members
of the pest, the remaining species become less sensitive to the pesticide and this becomes a
heritable change in the species (Sparks and Nauen, 2015). The ﬁrst case of pest resistance
was reported in 1947 towards DDT, which was developed by Paul Müller in 1939 to control
vector-borne diseases such as malaria. Resistance strategies and research is a big focus point
4
==PAGE:18==
in crop protection programs and resistance towards at least one pesticide has been found in
586 insects and mites, 235 fungi and 252 weeds according to CropLife International (Borel,
2017).
Pesticides kill pests by targeting speciﬁc processes within the pests organisms and the way
this is carried out is called the pesticides’ mode of action (MoA). Pesticides have various MoA
and some have only one MoA while others have several targeted MoA. Pests are more likely to
develop resistance towards pesticides with a single MoA than pesticides with multiple MoA.
Applying multiple pesticides with different MoA against one pest can also lower the likelihood
of the pest to develop resistance (Sparks and Nauen, 2015). The probability of resistance
depends on the MoA of the pesticide but factors such as the pests’ life cycle, the pests’ genet-
ics and operational approaches on the ﬁeld also have an inﬂuence on the development of
resistance (United States Environmental Protection Agency, 2001). Action committees that
are leading the research on resistance and classifying pesticides’ MoA exist for the three large
target classes, herbicide resistance action committee (HRAC), insecticide resistance action
committee (IRAC) and fungicide resistance action committee (FRAC).
Pesticide resistance is a major problem for many farmers that are in desperate need for new
alternatives or options (Borel, 2017). Many farmers end up using higher pesticide dosage
and/or applying more frequently than applicable for the ﬁeld due to decreasing efﬁcacy of
the pesticide (Van Den Bosch et al., 2011; Damalas and Eleftherohorinos, 2011). However, this
will not solve the resistance problem and can even increase the pest resistance in a long-term
perspective while contributing to enhanced toxicity to humans and the environment. As
pests develop resistance towards the most common pesticides, crop losses increase and
negatively affect farmers’ economy. The global population is expected to grow to 9 billion
in 2050 which will require enhanced food production (Popp et al., 2013). Crop losses from
resistance therefore propose a big threat to food security in coming years.
1.2.3 Environmental and human health impacts of pesticides
It has been scientiﬁcally proven that many pesticides have toxic effects on the environment
and human health. According to Zhang et al. (2011) only a fraction of the sprayed pesticide
reaches the target organism, the majority ends up in the soil or atmosphere and from there
the pesticide can diffuse to other environmental compartments. The pesticide undergoes
different pathways depending on its chemical structure, possible pathways including; wind
drift, runoff, leaching, degradation, volatilising or deposition (Fantke et al., 2012). The toxicity
effects are only calculated for the non-targeted area, as the targeted area is intentionally
intoxicated within a speciﬁc time frame (Rosenbaum et al., 2015).
Emission models that can estimate the processes a pesticide undergoes and calculate the frac-
tion that ends up in each compartment have been developed. In 2006 the ﬁrst PestLCI model
was developed that calculates the pesticide emissions from agricultural ﬁeld to environmental
compartments such as air, surface water, and groundwater (Birkved and Hauschild, 2006).
The model calculates emissions based on application amount, crop, application method,
climate conditions and other factors (Rosenbaum et al., 2015). A newer version was published
in 2012, with more precise fate calculations and a larger database (Dijkman et al., 2012a).
5
==PAGE:19==
This model is helpful for identifying the total emission fraction of individual pesticides. By
combining the emission with ecotoxicity and human health characterisation factors the im-
pact of each pesticide can be calculated. Characterisation models, such as USEtox, calculate
ecotoxicity and human health characterisation factors for pesticides on the basis of toxicity
tests and calculations of exposure (Rosenbaum et al., 2008). Ecotoxicity characterisation fac-
tors are reported in the unit potentially affected fraction of species integrated over time and
volume per kg pesticide emitted (PAF m3 day/kg emitted). Only by combining the ecotoxicity
characterisation factor and the emission fraction of a speciﬁc pesticide the real impact from
ecotoxicity is known.
Fantke et al. (2011) presented a model that calculates plant uptake and quantiﬁes human
health impacts from pesticide applications. The health impact score is measured in disability
adjusted life years (DALYs) per hectare and human health impacts are divided into non-cancer
and cancer toxicity effects (Fantke et al., 2012). Each health impact score is based on several
factors such as intake fraction, dose-response slope, severity factor and mass applied per
hectare. Health impact scores and damage costs of hundreds of pesticides have been calcu-
lated in Fantke et al. (2012); Fantke and Jolliet (2016).
1.2.4 Costs of pesticide use
The cost of individual pesticides differs between countries depending on taxes and availability.
In 2013 Denmark set a new strategy in which pesticides with high human health toxicity
and ecotoxicity have a higher tax level than less toxic pesticides. With this approach, the
government is trying to push farmers towards buying more sustainable pesticides (The Danish
Ecological Council, 2015). Other countries have tried to subsidise organic farming or low
pesticide farming (Eyhorn et al., 2015).
Pesticides also have a hidden cost on the society, both in form of increased pressure on
the health care system and in cleaning up polluted environment. These societal costs are
currently not fully included in the market price of pesticides (Bourguet and Guillemaud,
2016). Farmers are likely unaware of these damage costs and hence, they do not inﬂuence the
farmer’s decision when selecting pesticides. Damage costs from toxicity impacts depend on
the toxicity of individual pesticides which can vary several factors. Hence it is important not
to assume that damage costs are equal for all pesticides (Bourguet and Guillemaud, 2016).
Understanding monetary units due to environmental and health problems can be much
easier than understanding other widely used units for expressing environmental and health
problems. Incorporating damage costs as a criterion when selecting pesticides at the farm
level can enhance the sustainability of pesticide usage.
The health impacts from pesticides have been reported to cost 12 €/person over a lifetime
in Europe, with a conﬁdence interval from 0.03 to 5142 €/person (Fantke et al., 2012). The
damage cost was based on exposure to 133 pesticides in 24 EU countries. Damage costs
from ecotoxicity have also been published. Leach and Mumford (2008) reported on the basis
of Pretty et al. (2001) calculations that damage costs on the German society due to loss of
biodiversity/wildlife, pollution incidents, ﬁsh deaths, monitoring costs and bee colony losses
are 1.69 €/kg active substance, amounting up to 45 million €/year.

==PAGE:20==
The cost and value creation of pesticides is a complex relationship. To best serve the society
the economic beneﬁts of pesticide usage need to outweigh the cost of negative externalities
on the society.
1.3
State of the Art
The current way farmers select pesticides is not sufﬁcient to truly identify the most optimal
and sustainable pesticide. Improving this requires improvements of readily available data
and a set tools that allows for a simple pesticide comparison of multiple factors important to
farmers. Life Cycle Asessment (LCA) is a tool that is often used for quantifying environmental
and human health impacts of systems and products (Stranddorf et al., 2005). In order to
calculate impacts from agricultural systems or to compare different cropping systems the
impacts due to pesticide usage need to be included. Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) can
be used as a tool to combine emissions and characterisation factors to calculate the impacts
caused by pesticide use. Impacts from pesticides are however poorly represented in current
LCA approaches (Fantke et al., 2011; Dijkman et al., 2012b). This is mostly due to the fact
that application amounts are unknown and emission fractions in the life cycle inventory
approaches are inconsistent (Rosenbaum et al., 2015).
Risk assessments are popularly applied by governing bodies to estimate and compare risks
from pesticides by combining exposure and effects (Finizio and Villa, 2002). The end result of
most risk assessments are risk quotients calculated for each pesticide covered. Farmers are
rarely involved in these kinds of studies or informed which pesticides perform the worst and
should be avoided. Chemical risk assessment of pesticides is the approach used by EFSA to
ﬁnd an acceptable risk to both humans and the environment (European Food Safety Authority,
nd). The MRL marks the risk that is considered acceptable to consumers and is based on
analytic approaches and not actual environmental exposure (Fantke et al., 2012).
Many studies have been performed on different aspects of pesticide usage and application.
However these studies are mostly speciﬁc to one subject (e.g. human toxicity, resistance, pol-
icy, speciﬁc crops etc.) (Nowell et al., 2014; Mostafalou and Abdollahi, 2013; Fantke and Jolliet,
2016; Storck et al., 2017). Current substitution or comparison tools typically do not combine
environmental toxicity impacts with other factors such as resistance or costs. Methods such
as LCA and risk assessments also require sufﬁcient amount of time and knowledge that most
farmers do not have. Farmers need to be able to easily compare and choose the most suitable
and sustainable pesticide given the variety the farmer has considering authorisation and
targeted pest.
Models to calculate emission fractions and characterisation factors are available for use,
however, the application data that is necessary for using the models to quantify the impact
of speciﬁc pesticides is not accessible. In this context, identiﬁcation and quantiﬁcation of
pesticide applications and their impacts are the focal points in the development of sustainable
agriculture. Figure 1 illustrates how an application database can help to quantify impacts
from pesticides by two different approaches. The LCA approach focuses on the total envi-

Methods
This chapter describes the method behind the data collection and creation of the application
scenario database. Furthermore, the method and decisions behind the chemical substitution
scenarios and input values are given. Description of the method for calculating damage costs
and total costs of pesticides as part of the substitution scenario are covered in the last two

3.1
Pesticide application scenario database
EFSA reviews and publishes authorisations of new pesticides or alterations in the current
authorised usage of a pesticide. The published reports by EFSA contain information on rec-
ommended usage of pesticides, which are named Good Agricultural Practices (GAPs). Data
from the GAPs have been collected and mark the foundation for the pesticide application
scenario database. The collection of application data from the EFSA reports was begun before
this thesis started. Within the time frame of this study, additional EFSA reports were collected,
data cleaning was carried out, additional information was added to the database and the
database set-up was designed.
The goal was to collect application data for pesticides together with other relevant information
to document application patterns for a speciﬁc pesticide on a speciﬁc crop. The geographical
scope of the data is within EU, however, products imported to the EU from all over the globe
are also included. The database, therefore, contains global pesticide application data.
3.1.1 Data collection and data cleaning
The application data was collected from reasoned opinions and conclusions on pesticide
usage published by EFSA. The GAPs are given as appendix in the EFSA reports and include a
recommended usage of a speciﬁc pesticide with regards to crop, geographical zone and more.
The GAPs include how often the pesticide should be applied, the minimum and maximum
amount, how long interval is necessary between each application and the minimum pre-
harvest interval (minimum amount of days that need to be between application and harvest).
A total of 537 EFSA reports from 2009 to 2017 were collected for this study. These reports give
a good overview of pesticides registered and used within EU and allowances for pesticides on
imported crops. The pesticides included in the application database are solely based on the
substances that have been reviewed from 2009- 2017 by EFSA.
The information collected from the GAPs were; crop, member state country, zone, application
scenario, formulation type, formulation concentration, application method, growth stage,
application times (min-max), interval between applications, treatment concentration, treat-

ment rate and treatment dosage. The GAP template presented in the EFSA reports is available
in Appendix A, Figure 15 with remarks that explain what each parameter covers.
EFSA reports the same parameters in different units and therefore, all units needed to be
harmonised during the set-up of the database. The unit conversions used are presented in
Appendix A, Tables 13, 14 and 15. The crops and application methods given in the GAPs were
often inconsistently reported and clean up of these two parameters was necessary to unify the
data. The database was extended with information on the target class, chemical class, human
toxicity and ecotoxicity in order to complete the proﬁle of each substance. Furthermore, the
database was simpliﬁed by aggregating crops and application methods into classes.
3.1.2 Target classes and chemical classes of pesticides
All the pesticides covered in the application database were assigned to a speciﬁc target class
depending on the pests they are targeting. The pesticides are divided into the following six
target classes:
• Herbicide
• Fungicide
• Insecticide
• Acaricide
• Molluscicide
• Plant growth regulator
Herbicides, insecticides, and fungicides are the most commonly used pesticides. Herbicides
target weeds. Selective herbicides are mostly sprayed on crops as they target speciﬁc weeds
but not the crop itself. Broad spectrum herbicides can be sprayed before planting or pre-
emergence of the crop to clean the ﬁeld from weeds. Insecticides are used to kill unwanted
insects and fungicides inhibit fungal growth. Acaricides kill species belonging to the family
of acari, such as mites and ticks. Molluscicides are used in agriculture to kill snails. Plant
growth regulators are synthetic chemicals that are used to regulate the growth of plants to
make them grow at a faster pace. The target class of pesticides is an important parameter in
order to understand their different application patterns.
There are pesticides that can metabolise very quickly and therefore only appear as the metabo-
lite in the environment. These pesticides need to be modelled as the metabolite when mod-
elling environmental and human impacts. It should be noted that the data sets includes two
pesticides that should be treated as the metabolite when modelling and not as the pesticide
given. These metabolites are ﬂuazifop-P which is a metabolite of the herbicide ﬂuazifop-
P-butyl and trinexapac which is a metabolite of the plant growth regulator trinexapac-etyl.
These pesticides are given as the herbicide and plant growth regulator in the application
database but users of the database should be aware of their quickly formed metabolites.

of pesticides and their MoA against pests. The chemical classiﬁcation of the three different
action committees for insecticides, herbicides and insecticides have been followed. All pesti-
cides covered in the application database were assigned a chemical class, pesticides not yet
classiﬁed were marked as unclassiﬁed. The risk of pests developing a resistance to pesticides
is largely dependent on the chemical class of pesticide.

3.1.6 Toxicity
The aim of this study was to support a more sustainable use of pesticides and therefore toxicity
data, both for humans and the environment, was an important parameter to include in the
database. The purpose of the application database is to provide inputs to quantify environ-
mental and human toxicity impacts. The toxicity values are wanted as characterisation factors,
that describe the harm of a certain pesticide due to exposure from pesticide applications.
EFSA reports toxicity values as NOAEL which is the concentration of no observed adverse
effect level. This value can be found for all authorised substances within Europe as this
NOAEL is required to be evaluated before authorisation of cehmicals. The NOAEL values will,
however, not help to characterise the toxicity of chemicals as for this the concentration that
causes effect/harm needs to be known.
For this reason the toxicity data was gathered from the scientiﬁc consensus model USEtox,
which reports characterisation factors for chemicals (Rosenbaum et al., 2008). The toxicity
factors reported in the USEtox database are pre-calculated toxicity values gathered from
all around the world (Rosenbaum et al., 2011). The toxicity data therefore expands the
geographical scope of the data origin. The ecotoxicity data is based on aquatic toxicity tests of
three trophic levels, combined with exposure and fate factors (Rosenbaum et al., 2011).
14
==PAGE:28==
The human toxicity is calculated from effect factors, based on effect dosage tests on animals
or humans, and intake fractions of the chemical (Fantke et al., 2017).
The human toxicity is given in the unit DALY/kg emitted, where DALY stands for disability
adjusted life year. The ecotoxicity is given in the unit PDF m3 day/kg emitted, which translates
into potentially disappeared fraction of species over a certain volume (i.e. exposed water
volume for the fraction of pesticides that reach aquatic freshwater, where they affect aquatic
ecosystem species) and day per kg emitted of the pesticide. These types of units can be con-
verted into damage costs and be compared with other types of chemicals causing ecotoxicity
or human toxicity, as done for human health in (Fantke et al., 2012).
The USEtox database contains characterisation factors for human toxicity and ecotoxicity of
chemicals. The pesticides that have characterisation factors assigned in the USEtox database
were matched with the pesticides included in this study. Not all the pesticides included in
this study could be matched with the USEtox database and data gaps therefore appear in the
toxicity parameters in the application database.
The amount that is emitted of a pesticide is dependent on many variables (application method,
crop, pesticide properties etc.) and often models are required to calculate the emission. The
toxicity data therefore needs to be complemented with emission models to ﬁnd the net impact
of a pesticide.

3.2.1 Authorisation
A crucial factor in the chemical substitution scenario was whether or not the plant protection
product was registered for use in Denmark. The individual countries in Europe are the issuing
authorisation party for registering plant protection products and it therefore differs between
countries if a pesticide is authorised for use or not. Information on registered plant protection
products in Denmark are found on the website of the Danish EPA (Miljøstyrelsen, 2017).
3.2.2 Resistance
Pest resistance has become a big issue in the agricultural sector. Both genetics of the pests
and agricultural practices are factors that affect the development of resistance. To date ad-
equate information is available on resistance towards several common pesticides, however
at present stage the risk of resistance is mostly estimated with a low, moderate, or high criteria.
For herbicides the The International Survey of Herbicide Resistant Weeds reports the number
of resistance species for each chemical class. The chemical classes of the three herbicides
included in the substitution scenario have all reported species with resistant. Herbicides with
the MoA photosystem II inhibitors, including phenmedipham, have a total of 73 reported
resistance cases. Herbicides with the MoA PSI electron diverter, such as diquat, have 32
reported cases of resistance. Finally, microtubule inhibitors like pendimethalin, have 12
reported cases of resistance (Heap, 2017). Based on this phenmedipham has been assigned a
high risk of resistance while diquat and pendimethalin are assigned to the category moderate
as presented in Table 5.
For fungicides the deﬁnition of high, low and moderate risk of resistance together with a list
of which chemicals belong in which class is given by the FRAC (Brent and Hollomon, 1998).
Fungicides that are widespread and show a great decrease in efﬁcacy in one or more targeted
pests are categorised as high risk for resistance. Fungicides that show decreased efﬁcacy
in few cases are categorised with moderate risk. No detection of fungicide resistance or if
resistance is rare after many years of use the fungicide is categorised with low risk. All the
four analysed fungicides have been assigned a category. Azoxystrobin has been reported
with resistance in most of its targeted pathogens and is therefore classiﬁed as high risk of
resistance. The resistance risk category of each fungicide is presented in Table 5.
For insecticides, a list containing a high, moderate, low categorisation of the risk of resistance
is not available. The IRAC has though gathered a list of several common pests and what
chemical classes these pests have known resistance for. Based on this information the three
insecticides were categorised low, moderate and high. The action committee has documented
resistance in six pests relevant for lettuce. The insecticide pirimicarb, which is a carbamate,
had resistance known for ﬁve pests out of the six documented and therefore receives a scoring
of high risk. Acetamiprid was resistant to three out of the six pests and was scored with
a moderate risk. The insecticide Pymetrozine was not known to be resistant to any of the

resistance table can be found in Appendix B, Table 17.
3.2.3 Price
Price is normally an important parameter for individuals buying products and the price
can inﬂuence a farmer’s decision when selecting a pesticide. The price of the pesticides is
given for the plant protection product that contains the pesticide or the active ingredient.
Many different plant protection products exist for the same active substance. Only one plant
protection product was selected for each pesticide in the scenario. The aim was to choose a
plant protection product in the middle of the price range and which FC matched well with
the reported GAPs from the application database.
The price of pesticides is given in DKK/L or DKK/kg on the website of Middeldatabasen
(Middeldatabasen, 2017), which is a database containing information about the pesticides
that are or have been used in Denmark. The price was then converted to the unit DKK/ha

3.2.4 Human toxicity
Human toxicity should play an important role when farmers select pesticides for their crops,
as this can affect their health and impact the consumers of their products. The human toxicity
characterisation factors used in the substitution scenario have been published by Fantke and
Jolliet (2016). These factors are based on effect factors and intake fractions from oral exposure.
The reason for not using human toxicity characterisation factors from USEtox as in the pesti-
cide application database was due to data gaps. The USEtox database had human toxicity
characterisation factors for ﬁve out of the ten pesticides while Fantke and Jolliet (2016) had
eight out of ten pesticides with reported characterisation factors. The two missing human
toxicity characterisation factors were calculated from collected toxicity test on rats following
the USEtox guidelines (Fantke et al., 2017). The characterisation factor applied was based on
residue fractions as this fraction dominates the impact. The reported characterisation factors
in Fantke and Jolliet (2016) and the calculated ones are fully comparable once the USEtox
value has been converted from DALY/kg emitted to DALY/kg applied. The characterisation
factors of these two pesticides, azoxystrobin and pymetrozine, were added to the USEtox
database.

Toxicity to human health is measured in the unit of DALYs per kg applied. The DALYs are
calculated as the sum of the years of life lost due to premature death in the population and
the years lost due to disability for people living with a disease or its consequences.
3.2.5 Ecotoxicity
The ecotoxicity factors used in the substitution scenario are from the consensus model USE-
tox, speciﬁcally the endpoint characterisation factor from agricultural soil to freshwater. The
unit of ecotoxicity is given in PDF m3 day/kg emitted. Three pesticides had no ecotoxicity
characterisation factor reported in USEtox. These values were calculated based on effect
factors from collected aquatic toxicity tests, fate factors and exposure factors based on the
USEtox guidelines (Fantke et al., 2017) and added to the USEtox database.

3.3
Damage Costs of pesticides
Damage costs from pesticides are the externalities in monetary terms that a society is exposed
to due to pesticide usage. The characteristics of pesticides are important when evaluating
chemicals, but pesticides are applied in different amounts and the applied amount is an
inﬂuential factor with regards to toxicity impacts. Often toxicity impacts from chemical are
more easily communicated in monetary terms than other units. Giving damage costs in
monetary terms will help farmers to compare the price of a pesticide with the damage costs
caused by it, and eventually push farmers to select more sustainable pesticides. The average

4.1.4 Minimum pre-harvest interval
The minimum pre-harvest interval, the shortest time that can pass between pesticide appli-
cation and harvest, spans from 0 days up to 365 days in the application scenario database.
Figure 6 illustrates the minimum pre-harvest interval at several time frames and the number
of substances that belong to each time frame aggregated into target classes. Note that each
pesticide can be applied with different pre-harvest periods and therefore be included more
than once in the plot.
33
==PAGE:47==
Figure 6: Minimum pre-harvest intervals per number of substances dis-aggregated into target
classes. Others represent a sum of acaricides, molluscicides and plant growth regulators
Herbicides have relatively long minimum pre-harvest intervals, with the period 36-60 days
holding the most number of substances. Herbicides are often used before the crop is planted
or before pre-emergence of the crop in order to kill the pests that would compete with the
crop while growing. If some herbicides are applied to a crop directly the herbicide can kill the
crop and therefore herbicides tend to be applied with a longer minimum pre-harvest interval.
Fungicides and insecticides have mostly short minimum pre-harvest intervals. Both can be
applied to the crop itself to prevent damage to the crop and they can also be used after harvest
to prevent fungal effects or insect attacks while shipping and storing fruits and vegetables.
Figure 6 shows that the insecticides have slowly decreasing number of substances the longer
the minimum pre-harvest interval gets. Fungicides, however, have the highest number of data
points between 0-3 days and then 22-35 days. Herbicides show a trend of increasing amount
of substances with increasing minimum pre-harvest interval until 30-60 days and then the
number of substances declines again. Other pesticides, including acaricide, molluscicides
and plant growth regulators have a spread pre-harvest interval from 0-60 days.
Herbicides are sometimes used to make the harvest easier for the farmer or employees at the
farm. For example with potatoes, it is desired to kill the leafs above the soil but not affect
the potatoes in the ground, for this reason herbicides are applied to potatoes shortly before
harvest, explaining some of the short pre-harvest intervals of herbicides.

class is under 50 days for all crop classes. Paddy rice and panicoideae have the highest mean
values of 49 and 47 while vegetable fruit has the lowest mean value of 5.4, the pre-harvest
interval mean values for all crop classes are presented in Appendix A, Table 16. The box plot

Chemical substitution scenarios
The results from the three chemical substitution scenarios are presented. The properties of
the individual pesticides selected for each scenario were compared and the aggregated score
of each pesticide was calculated. The normalised values for each pesticide within each target
class category is presented in Figure 12. The factors were normalised to 100%, were the pesti-
cide with the highest score within each parameter got a value of 100% and the other values
where normalised according to that value. The lower the value is, the better the pesticide
performs. The raw data used for the chemical substitution analysis is presented in Table 5
and the calculated normalised values are presented in Appendix B, Table 18.
All four fungicides were authorised for use on lettuce in Denmark. Fosetyl has the highest
price but scores the lowest in all other categories. Azoxystrobin shows a less attractive proﬁle
with the highest ecotoxicity and resistance. Mandipropamid scores highest in human toxicity
and has a moderate risk of resistance. Propamocarb has a relatively high price together with
moderately high human toxicity and risk of resistance. It depends on the weighting factor
if the high price of fosetyl drags it down, but by judging from the normalised scores fosetyl
performs the best and azoxystrobin the worst in this scenario.
Diquat is the only herbicide registered for use on lettuce in Denmark according to "Bekæm-
pelsesdatabsen" which is published by the Danish EPA. Farmers have therefore no choice
and need to use Diquat if they want to apply herbicides on their ﬁelds. Diquat is though only
allowed to be used before planting or pre-emergence of the crop. Comparing the normalised
factors diquat has the highest ecotoxicity and human toxicity, medium high risk of resistance
and price. Looking into the two non registered herbicides, pendimethalin has the highest

normalised scores which of the two overall performs better, but certainly they both show a
less toxic, more preferable proﬁle than diquat.
Three insecticides, acetamiprid, pirimicarb and pymetrozine, were analysed in the insecticide
scenario. Acetamiprid and pymetrozine have similar prices. Acetamiprid has the highest
human toxicity but pymetrozine has the highest ecotoxicity. Overall pymetrozine has a better
performance due to the low risk of resistance and low human toxicity. Pirimicarb has the
highest price and risk of resistance and a moderately high ecotoxicity and human toxicity. For
many farmers risk of resistance and human toxicity would be considered more important
than ecotoxicity, making pymetrozine the most attractive pesticide to select.

4.3
Damage cost
Damage costs from the selected pesticides were calculated by combining toxicity factors with
the application dosages and valuation factors. The valuation factors represent the value that
people are willing to pay for these damages not to take place. Damage costs from both human
toxicity and ecotoxicity were calculated for the 4 fungicides, 3 herbicides and 3 insecticides.
Damage costs are expressed in €/ha for both toxicity categories and are presented in Figure
13. The detailed results are given in Appendix C, Tables 19 and 20. The trend was that damage
costs from ecotoxicity were much lower than from human toxicity, indicating that preventing
environmental toxicity is less important to people than preventing human toxicity. For better
visualisation of both toxicity factors in Figure 13, damage costs from human toxicity are shown
on the left axis (y-axis) and the ecotoxicity damage costs are shown on the right axis (z-axis).
Azoxystrobin has by far the highest damage costs from ecotoxicity, the other values can barely
be seen in Figure 13 a, but propamocarb has the lowest damage costs from ecotoxicity, which
was a factor six lower than for azoxystrobin. Propamocarb however had the highest damage
costs from human toxicity at 227 €/ha. Fosetyl had the lowest damage costs from human
toxicity of the fungicides, which was only 1% of the damage cost from propamocarb. Sub-
stituting propamocarb with fosetyl results in a total saving of 225 €/ha from human toxicity
damage costs. In a monetary perspective the low ecotoxicity damage cost of the fungicides will
have little inﬂuence on decision making compared with damage costs from human toxicity.
Focusing on damage costs the preferred fungicide would be fosetyl matching with the results
from the aggregated substitution scores.
The three herbicides all have damage costs from human toxicity under 1 €/ha, which is
low compared with the analysed insecticides and fungicides. The herbicides diquat and
pendimethalin have however high damage costs from ecotoxicity compared with the other
target classes. Emissions to soil from herbicides applied on lettuce are 80% compared with
11% for fungicide and insecticides. The high soil emission contributes to a higher ecotoxicity
proﬁle of applied herbicides. Phenmedipham has a factor six lower damage cost from eco-
toxicity than the other herbicides and also has the lowest damage cost from human toxicity.
Comparing damage costs of the three herbicides, phenmedipham is clearly the preferred
selection. The fact that both phenmedipham and pendimethalin are not registered for use
in Denmark can though not be neglected. This scenario can however identify the savings
that could be achieved in societal externalities by substituting diquat with less toxic herbicides.
Analysing the insecticide scenario, the damage cost from human toxicity is lowest for ac-
etamiprid, with 53 €/ha. Pirimicarb has the highest damage costs from human toxicity at 145
€/ha. Damage costs of the insecticides from ecotoxicity were generally low and do not vary
much between the three insecticides. Pymetrozine has the highest damage cost from ecotoxi-
city at 0.00025 €/ha. In this scenario acetamiprid has the lowest damage costs for both human
toxicity and ecotoxicity and is recommended as the insecticide to target Lettuce Aphids. A
total of 92 €/ha can be saved in damage costs by substituting pirimicarb with acetamiprid.
These results are contradicting to the aggregated scores, were pymetrozine performed the
best. Acetamiprid is applied in much lower dosages than the other two insecticides lowering
the damage costs per hectare.

damage costs from ecotoxicity. The calculated values are presented in Appendix D, Table 21.
Propamocarb and Fosetyl are within the same two equi-lines between 100 and 1000 €/ha and

the results of total costs. This emphasises the importance of including the application amount
when comparing pesticides. By substituting propamocarb with mandipropamid a saving of

CHAPTER5
Discussion
The application scenario database was developed and can now serve as an input parameter
to quantify impacts from pesticides with LCA or other tools. The database can though include
values that are inaccurate. During the work of this study, a few values were found to be
incorrectly written in the GAP Appendix of the EFSA reports. In most cases the values had
the wrong unit (e.g. g a.i./ha instead of kg a.i./ha) and were corrected for in the database. For
abnormal values appearing in the database, it is recommended to double check the EFSA
report and their references.
Hand-operated sprayers were the dominating application method by far, the large scale be-
tween hand-operated sprayers and other methods can be questioned since farmers in Europe
are using more and more tractors for pesticide applications. In the EFSA reports often the
device used for applying the pesticide was not reported but only how the pesticide should
be applied, such as foliar spray which can be several methods, the only condition is that
the pesticide is sprayed on the leaves of the crop. When designing the database all foliar
spray methods were assigned as hand-operated sprayers. Therefore, an overestimation in
the category of hand-operated sprayers is possible. The limitations of signing application
methods to a class was due to the way EFSA states the application methods in the GAPs.
More speciﬁc information from EFSA would be required to achieve a better aggregation into
application method classes.
In order to ﬁnd a clear trend in the pesticide application data, several parameters likely need
to be combined, as pesticide guidelines are based on a set of different parameters. Through
this study, it has become clear that pesticides have their individual patterns, extreme values
regularly appear and therefore generalising pesticides can be misleading. In the past studies
have generalised pesticides as one group, but this should be avoided and pesticides should
rather be individualised or grouped on the basis of the chemical class.
The database has limitations with regards to toxicity characterisation factors, no data was
available for over half of the pesticides included. The USEtox database is always expanding
and calculating characterisation factors of pesticides is a work in progress. Toxicity factors
can be calculated for all pesticides with missing factors using the USEtox guidelines if animal
and aquatic toxicity test exist, this will though require substantial amount of time. The char-
acterisation factors in the database can give a broad overview of toxicity scale of pesticides.
The data gaps in the toxicity characterisation factors can though give a misleading picture
of the correlation between the toxicity and application dosage and toxicity and minimum
pre-harvest intervals. A complete toxicity proﬁle is needed before any concrete conclusions
can be drawn.
49
==PAGE:63==
NOAEL and acceptable daily intake (ADI) values from EFSA could be added to complete the
pesticide proﬁles as these values are available for all authorised pesticides in EU. The GAPs
do not include the NOAEL or ADI values and these values are only reported at the initial
authorisation of the pesticide. These values are not found in a tabular form and it would
require a search and collection of one pesticide at a time. This was outside the scope of this
study and could not have been possible within the provided time frame. The NOAEL values
will also not help to quantify the real impacts from pesticides once applied but only to identify
the scale of toxicity based on analytic tests.
The human health characterisation factors are calculated on the assumption that animal
toxicity tests can be extrapolated to human toxicity. This is currently the most common way
to evaluate toxicity to humans as testing toxic effects directly on humans is often not possible.
This assumption can though result in over- or under-estimations of toxic effects, contributing
to uncertainty of the results.
Using the average application dosage per time or the total average application dosage applied
over several times depends on the purpose of the analysis. It also depends on the pesticide
itself, its half live and exposure. For pesticides with short half lives, the last application might
only be the applicable application to consider with regards to human toxicity. The half-live of
pesticides is incorporated when calculating toxicity characterisation factors, but half-lives
were not further considered in the substitution scenarios with regards to application times
and pre-harvest intervals.
Pesticides are not static, they are continuously being evaluated and between evaluations, pes-
ticides can be banned, or the authorised application guidelines can be altered. The pesticide
application scenario database contains data from 2009-2017 but new pesticides can have
appeared since the data collection was stopped and others may no longer be authorised. This
continuous change of pesticides complicates data handling and analysing.
Only one herbicide was registered for use out of the three herbicides appearing in the pesti-
cide/pest matrix. If only one pesticide can legally be used a substitution can not take place. By
further analysing the area of herbicide registration in Denmark loopholes were found in the
system. On the website of Middeldatabasen the controversial herbicide glyphosate is regis-
tered for use on lettuce after sowing and before pre-emergence. Glyphosate does however not
appear on the list of authorised substances given out by the Danish EPA because glyphosate
is allowed in general terms on soil containing pre-emergence crop. It is not speciﬁed which
kind of crops glyphosate is allowed on and therefore glyphosate does not appear on the
authorisation list for lettuce. Glyphosate does not either appear in the application scenario
database as applied on lettuce, likely due to the reason that the crop residues are expected
to be very low and not close to the MRL, and therefore no member states have applied for
authorisation from EFSA. These kinds of exceptions make it difﬁcult for external people with
little knowledge of pesticides to analyse and understand this complex system.
Diquat is a broad working herbicide, meaning that it is not speciﬁcally used against Winter
grass but against weeds in general and diquat therefore has beneﬁts that were not included in
the analysis of the substitution scenario. This kind of trade offs might be a big advantage for
50
==PAGE:64==
diquat and the reason why it is often a preferred herbicide for Danish farmers. Diquat can
though only be used before pre-emergence and therefore if problems with weeds occur later
on in the vegetation period of lettuce no herbicides can be used. It would also be interesting
to analyse if less toxic broad spectrum herbicides than diquat and glyphosate exist and push
for registration of these herbicides. Allowing other herbicides with lower human toxicity
impacts than diquat and glyphosate can lead to signiﬁcant savings for the Danish society
from damage costs. These savings will though only count if diquat is completely substituted
by pendimethalin or phenmedipham, which might not be the case.
Earlier on, many studies have generalised a speciﬁc damage cost for all pesticides, but this
should be avoided. Looking into the damage costs of these pesticides often a factor of ﬁve
was between the lowest and highest damage costs. By individualising pesticides and com-
paring the toxicity of each pesticide and linking them to application amount, possibilities
for sufﬁcient savings can be identiﬁed. Looking at the total costs of pesticide it should be
kept in mind that the ecotoxicity values are many orders of magnitude lower than human
toxicity damage costs and market prices of pesticides. Therefore, when calculating total costs,
damage costs due to ecotoxicity could in most cases be neglected. Preserving and protecting
ecosystems is though very important and should not be neglected. This reﬂects however
society’s perspective, that humans are much more valuable than the ecosystems surrounding
us. In order increase the importance of ecosystem protection it is recommended to weight
the ecotoxicity factor before calculating the total cost or eventually calculate damage costs
from ecotoxicity with other methods. The valuation factors can be doubted as humans can
quickly change perspectives or over or underestimate their willingness to pay. The valuation
factors are a big uncertainty factor with regards to the results of damage costs and total costs.
The fraction emitted from pesticides was calculated based on a general percentage, however
the emitted fraction depends on several factors and will be different for each pesticide, crop
and application method. The calculated emission fractions in the substitution scenarios
contribute further to the uncertainty of the results.
The chemical substitution contained three different methods for comparing pesticides, by
their properties, by damage costs and by total costs. Which method the farmer prefers de-
pends on the pest, the farmer’s income and his passion for the protection of consumers and
the surrounding ecosystems. From a societal perspective damage costs are important but for
individual farmers, total costs can appear more suitable.

CHAPTER6
Conclusion
The application database was successfully developed and can now function as the starting
point for quantifying environmental and human health impacts from selected pesticides.
The ﬁnal database submitted together with this thesis has 8758 data points. Each data point
includes a number of parameters such as; substance name, target class, application dosage,
minimum pre-harvest interval, crop, target application times, application interval, toxicity
factors.
The application dosages of pesticide vary between 0.00001-483 kg a.i./ha, with 95% of the
data within the limits of 0.01-2.62 kg a.i./ha. The 95% density interval of the application times
is between 1 and 5, with 2 being the most frequent number. Minimum pre-harvest intervals
differ between target classes, herbicides were used much earlier on in the vegetation period
than fungicides and insecticides. Correlations between human toxicity or ecotoxicity and min-
imum pre-harvest intervals could not be detected. No correlation was either found between
the toxicity factors and application dosage. The characterisation of the database has led to the
conclusion that generalising of pesticide applications should be avoided. Each pesticide is
authorised for use on a speciﬁc crop with a set of guidelines that should be carefully followed
by farmers.
One objective of this study was to design chemical substitution scenarios to demonstrate how
pesticides can be compared in order to ﬁnd the most suitable and sustainable pesticide. The
chemical substitution scenario with four fungicides applied on lettuce as a crop protection
against Downy Mildew was analysed. The results showed that fosetyl performed the best
when comparing their properties, both for the weighted and unweighted scores. Fosetyl also
had the lowest damage costs from human toxicity, and by substituting propamocarb with
fosetyl a total of 224 €/ha can be saved in damage costs. Looking into total costs of fosetyl
the large amount required to be applied causes the total cost of fosetyl to rise above both
mandipropamid and azoxystrobin.
In the herbicide scenario only one of the herbicides, diquat, was registered for use on lettuce
in Denmark and therefore a substitution is not possible. The scenario, however, showed
that substances with lower toxicity and lower risk of resistance are used on lettuce in other
countries and by registering a broader selection of herbicides farmers could select more
sustainable herbicide than diquat. Diquat is a broad working pesticide while the other two
are more selective and therefore, it depends on the weed problem at the farm which herbicide
is the most suitable.
52
==PAGE:66==
The insecticide substitution scenario showed that pymetrozine performed the best out of the
three insecticides when comparing their properties. Acetamiprid performed, however, better
with regards to total costs and damage cost from both human and ecotoxicity due to the low
application dosage required.
Generally, damage costs from ecotoxicity were much lower than damage costs from human
toxicity and had thereby a miniature impact on the total cost compared to pesticide prices
and human toxicity damage costs. It can furthermore be concluded that it is important not
only to consider the properties of pesticide but also the required application amount when
selecting or comparing pesticides. Sufﬁcient amount of savings can be achieved both for
farmers and the society by setting up a substitution scenario and comparing the applicable
pesticides for use.

CHAPTER7
Recommendations and
future research
Based on the knowledge acquired through this study a list of recommendations directed
towards farmers has been gathered with the hope to increase the sustainability of pesticide
usage.
1. Before selecting a pesticide study the risk of pests to develop resistance towards the
pesticide and what pests have been reported with resistance to that pesticide. Using
pesticides with a high risk of resistance should be avoided.
2. Follow guidelines on pesticide usage published by EFSA or other governing institution.
3. Systematically write down the application dosage, the application count and the con-
centration.
4. Keep track of the effect that each application has. If the effect decreases consider
changing pesticide instead of applying more of the pesticide.
5. Consider the externalities caused by pesticide usage and compare these with the market
price.
A few points were gathered to outline future research work needed within the ﬁeld of pesticide
applications and chemical substitution:
1. Close data gaps with regards to both for human toxicity and ecotoxicity characterisation
factors for pesticides.
2. Start the process of implementing the pesticide application data to databases used for
LCA.
3. Consider how to best manage and calculate toxicity factors from pesticide cocktails as
the toxicity effect is altered when pesticides are mixed together.
4. Generate a pesticide/pest matrix applicable for Denmark that farmers can use as a
starting point for creating substitution scenarios.

C.2 Ecotoxicity
Valuation factor is equal to 1.08E+11 €/species. The Characterisation factor for ecotoxicity for
the pesticide acetamiprid is 4.45 pdf m3 day/kg emitted.
The freshwater species density is 7.89E-10 species/m3, based on ReCiPe (Goedkoop et al.,
2009).
First we get rid off kg emitted in the unit
4.45 pdf m3 day/kg emitted·0.01328 = 5.91E −02 pdf m3 day
Freshwater covers 2.70E+05 km2 of Europe and the depth of freshwater in Europe is 15 m.
2.70E +05 km2 ·106 m2
km2 ·15m = 4.06E +13 m3 freshwater
5.91E −02 pdf m3 day
4.06E +13m3
·365 days/year = 5.32E −13 pdf year
This is the number of potentially disappeared species over one 1 year due to Acetamiprid
7.89E −10 species/m3 ·15m = 1.18E −08 species/m2 = 1.18E −04 species/ha
Now the potentially disappeared fraction of species lost due to acetamiprid is multiplied with
the total species per hectare.
5.32E −13 pdf year·1.18E −04 species/ha = 6.29E −17 species lost /ha
The valuation factor is then multiplied with the species lost/ha due to acetamiprid.
