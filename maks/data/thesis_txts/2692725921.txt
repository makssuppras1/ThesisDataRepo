Abstract 
The studies investigated in this master thesis is based on the principles from previous 
work conducted by S. Benthin. Investigation of growth energetics revealed that an 
additional ATP production is not associated with lactic acid production but originates in 
exploitation of certain compounds, most likely peptides and nucleotides. Growth 
energetics describes the relationship between generation and consumption of free energy, 
which can be used for improvement related to maximization of the biomass yield on the 
energy source. In addition, growth energetics is based on the consumption of substrate 
and the formation of biomass. The major energy generating process is the metabolism of 
sugars, which provides internal energy (ATP) to drive the biosynthetic processes. 
Moreover, growth energetics depends on the influence of substrate components, in 
particular the nitrogen, carbon and carbohydrate source (Benthin et al., 1994). 
Furthermore, LAB require an external source of essential amino acids and other 
nutrients, where the supply of amino acids is provided by a coordinated proteolytic 
system comprised of enzymes that degrade proteins and peptides, and transport systems 
for free amino acids and peptides. Energetics of such a proteolytic system was investigated 
with the use of native milk as a medium containing intact casein micelles. The growth 
energetics of L. lactis was studied in a pH-stat (continuous cultures) under different 
conditions by shift in pH setpoint along with the addition of 10 mM and 20 mM NaOH 
as a neutralizing agent in the feed medium, to increase pH for prolongation of growth in 
the presence of higher concentrations of lactate. The culture characteristics (dilution rate 
D, biomass formation (OD600 measurements), lactate production, glucose consumption, 
lactose concentration and yield coefficients Y [OD600/CLA]) was determined and used for 
comparative physiological studies of the isogenic strains (SK11 G1, SK11 G3, WG2 G1, 
WG2 G3 and control strain). Based on the experimental studies, quantitative analysis of 
the metabolism was successfully carried out with the use of native milk as growth medium. 
Statistical analysis showed minor differences among the used strains at different 
conditions.

Resume 
Det eksperimentelle arbejde i dette speciale er baseret på principperne fra tidligere 
studier udført af S. Benthin (Benthin et al., 1994). Studiet viste at en yderligere ATP 
produktion, som ikke er relateret til mælkesyre produktionen finder sted ved udnyttelse 
af sandsynligvis peptider og nukleotider. Energi metabolismen er baseret på forholdet 
mellem produktionen og forbrug af fri energi, som kan anvendes til at maksimere 
udbyttet af biomasse ved fuld udnyttelse af energikilden. Derudover er energi 
metabolismen baseret på forholdet mellem forbruget af substrat og dannelsen af 
biomasse. Produktionen af ATP finder sted i bakteriecellens cytoplasma, hvor sukker 
nedbrydes til pyruvat og danner intern energi (ATP) til at drive de biosyntetiske 
processer. Energi metabolisme afhænger af substrat komponenter som kvælstof, kulstof 
og kulhydrat kilder (Benthin et al., 1994). Desuden kræver LAB andre næringsstoffer og 
en ekstern kilde til essentielle aminosyre. Aminosyre overføres til LAB ved hjælp af den 
koordineret proteolytiske system, som består af enzymer der nedbryder proteiner til 
peptider og frie aminosyrer. Energien af et proteolytisk system blev undersøgt ved brug 
af mælk som medie, der indeholder kasein miceller. Energimetabolismen af L. lactis blev 
undersøgt i en pH-stat (kontinuerligt kulturer) under forskellige forhold ved skift i pH set 
punkt sammen med tilsætning af 10 mM og 20 mM NaOH, som er et neutraliserende 
middel, der tilsættes til vækst mediet for at øge pH og forlænge væksten ved høje 
koncentrationer af laktat. Vækst paramterne (fortyndingshastighed D, biomassedannelse 
(OD600-målinger), laktat produktion, glukose forbrug, laktose koncentration og udbytte 
koefficient Y [OD600/CLA]) blev bestemt og anvendt til at sammenligne fysiologiske 
undersøgelser af de isogene stammer (SK11 G1, SK11 G3, WG2 G1, WG2G3 og 
kontrolstamme). Baseret på de eksperimentelle undersøgelser blev kvantitative analyser 
af metabolismen udført ved brug af mælk som vækstmedie. Statistiske analyser viste at 
der var lidt forskel mellem de anvendte stammer under diverse forhold.

1 Introduction 
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) have an essential role in the dairy industry and have been used 
in the manufacture of fermented dairy products. The proteinases of LAB have been 
studied to improve speed of acidification and flavour formation. Growth energetics 
studies can be used to meet the economic importance (e.g. cost of substrate) of cultured 
dairy products, and manufacturers’ demands for starter cultures that will produce a 
consistent product (Mayo, 1993).  
The genetics and metabolism of several strains and species of LAB have been thoroughly 
investigated from various angles of applied and fundamental research. Although milk is 
frequently used in applied research, it is rarely used in fundamental research on energy 
metabolism. Milk is avoided for several reasons: it is complicated, difficult to standardize, 
turbid, it is a heterogeneous colloidal suspension, acidification cause coagulation, 
proteolysis can cause coagulation, etc. LAB metabolism is usually investigated using 
defined medium with soluble ingredients. Some milk components like lactose, casein, or 
casein hydrolysates are typically included in the medium to study metabolic routes of 
relevance for milk fermentation.  
The cell envelope proteinase (CEP) of L. lactis is a large extracellular protease covalently 
linked to the peptidoglycan of the cell wall. In order to grow to high cell densities in milk 
they need an extracellular protease since strains of L. lactis are typically auxotrophic for 
several amino acids. The purpose of this master thesis is based on recent research on cell 
envelope associated proteinases of L. lactis, which led to a proposal of a model (Fig. 1), 
where L. lactis adhere to the casein micelle in order to feed on caseins using a more 
coordinated proteolytic system than described previously (Hansen & Marcatili, 2020). 
The model organism used in this project is Lactococcus lactis subsp. cremoris MG1363, 
which contains a PrtP proteinase attached to the cell wall. This proteinase is responsible 
for the extracellular breakdown of proteins into peptides (Kevvai et al., 2014). 
In this project energetics of such a proteolytic system will be investigated with the use of 
native milk as a medium containing intact casein micelles. The yield coefficients (OD, 
concentration of glucose, lactose, lactic acid and acetic acid) under different growth 
conditions of the applied isogenic strains (differing only in the type of the CEP enzyme) 
will be determined and compared to investigate particular reactions. The process will be 
performed as continuous pH-stat cultivations incl. variations in medium composition.

2 Background 
2.1 Lactic acid bacteria 
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) are used in the production of a variety of dairy products such 
as cheese and yoghurts (Widyastuti et al., 2014). LAB refer to a non-taxonomic group of 
Gram-positive, non-respiring, non-spore-forming, cocci or rods, which produce lactic 
acid as the major catabolic end product during fermentation of carbohydrates. LAB are 
anaerobes, but most can grow in the presence of dioxygen and some strains carry out 
certain oxidative reactions. Generally, mesophilic bacteria grow rapidly and high 
populations develop within hours at temperature between 20-30°. An important factor is 
the acid tolerance of LAB strains since the sugar concentration in milk contributed to 
decreasing the pH below 4.5 (Adamberg et al., 2003). Acid production is slow or absent 
at temperature <20°. Temperature growth range is between 10-42°C, while growth is 
inhibited at temperature >39°C. The optimum growth range is between 20-35°C (Ahmed 
et al., 2006). LAB is comprised of genera of gram-positive bacteria such as Lactobacillus, 
Leuconostoc, Pediococcus, Lactococcus (formerly lactic Streptococcus) and 
Bifidobacterium. LAB can either be homofermentative by converting sugars to lactic acid 
as their final product or heterofermentative by converting sugars to a mixture of lactic 
acid and other products; carbon dioxide, acetic acid and ethanol. They are used in food 
==PAGE:10==
Page 10 of 64 
 
fermentations to enhance nutritional qualities, texture and flavour along with speed of 
acidification. In addition, undesirable bitter-tasting peptides, can lead to off-flavour. 
Traditionally, fermentation required LAB relied on natural contamination of the raw 
material with the appropriate bacteria, or addition of a small amount of previously 
fermented food (back slopping). Nowadays, defined starter cultures grown aseptically or 
bought ready-made frozen or freeze dried (Earnshaw, 1990).  
 
2.1.1 Lactococcus lactis 
L. lactis is a member of the mesophilic group of bacteria and cultures found in the dairy 
industry are classified as subspecies; cremoris, lactis and lactis biovar diacetylactis. 
Besides metabolism of carbohydrates to lactic acid they can convert milk proteins into 
flavour compounds. Lactococci have spherical or ovoid-shaped cells and occur singerly 
or in chains (Fig. 2). They are catalase-negative and nonmotile. They ferment glucose by 
the hexose diphosphate pathway producing L- (+)-lactic acid (Mills et al., 2011b). 
Furthermore, temperature controls the growth of the bacteria and affects the generation 
time according to phase of growth. L. lactis produces lactic acid quickly in comparison 
with other LAB strains, where the optimal growth temperature is 30°C. In addition, L. 
lactis subsp. cremoris is more salt sensitive (<4%). Moreover, the pH of the medium 
should be adjusted so as to fall in the range of 5.5 - 7.0 (Ahmed et al., 2006).

2.2 Fermentation 
Fermentation processes are used in the production of biopharmaceuticals, food, and feed 
supplements, biofuels and chemical building blocks (Yang & Sha, 2020). The main 
functions of the fermentation process using milk as medium are fermentation and 
depletion of the lactose (or added substrate e.g. glucose), reduction of the redox potential, 
citrate fermentation and degradation of casein (Savijoki et al., 2006). Fermentative 
microorganisms use carbohydrates to produce various acids, alcohols and gases under 
anaerobic or aerobic conditions. Other growth promoting factors are the source of 
nitrogen, carbon, nutrient salt and any excipients (vitamins or amino acids) (Dussap & 
Poughon, 2017), (Seborg et al., 2010). Important parameters that influence the process 
is agitation for homogenization of the medium, temperature, pH and pressure (Seborg 
et al., 2010).  
Continuous fermentation is a process with a constant flow of culture medium pumped 
into the culture vessel. The volume of the culture vessel in a continuous fermentation is 
typically constant. The concept of continuous fermentation processes can be related to 
chemostat, where nutrient source(s) is growth limiting and used to determine the growth 
rate. In addition, continuous fermentation can be controlled: through constant pH (pH-
auxostat), constant optical density (turbidostat), and constant substrate (nutristat) 
(Jagschies et al., 2018). Continuous fermentation starts as a batch process. At a certain 
point, where the desired conditions are reached e.g. culture reaches the exponential 
growth phase or when the culture becomes substrate limited, a feed with fresh growth 
medium is added, and an equal volume of culture broth is removed. Continuous 
fermentation processes is limited in the number of industrial application due to the 
increased risk for contamination, risk for genetic drift in the culture, and difficulties to 
control the process (Jagschies et al., 2018), (Yang & Sha, 2020). However, this process is 
an advantage due to achieving high cell densities, higher yield and productivity. High 
productivity is related to the decreased product inhibition, since lactic acid is constantly 
removed from the bioreactor (Olszewska-Widdrat et al., 2020). Continuous fermentation 
processes can run over an extended period of time (hours, days).

2.3 Glycolysis 
Glycolysis typically follows the Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP). The catabolic pathway 
where sugars are metabolised and taken up by the cells via either PEP-dependent 
phosphotransferase systems (PTS) involving coupled transport and phosphorylation of 
the sugar, or via permease systems in which sugar transport is followed by the kinase-
mediated phosphorylation of the free sugar within the cytosol. Once phosphorylated 
sugars are catabolised, many monosaccharides enter central metabolism via net gain of 2 
ATP per sugar (Cocaign-Bousquet et al., 1996). This enzyme catalysed glycolytic reaction 
supplies the cell with ATP for cell functions and growth  (Mills et al., 2011a). Since 
oxidative phosphorylation does not normally occur in L. lactis, ATP is only generated by 
glycolysis. The generated ATP by conversion of sugar to lactic acid is utilized in the 
anabolic processes for growth and the formation of product(s) (Benthin et al., 1994). 
Glycolysis takes place under anaerobic conditions e.g. fermentation processes. The two 
major pathways due to the metabolism of hexoses are illustrated in Fig. 3, where the 
glycolytic pathway is homofermentative and the phosphoketolase pathway is 
heterofermentative (Papagianni, 2012).

acetate and carbon dioxide (Romo-Buchelly et al., 2019).

2.3.1 Homofermentative and heterofermentative pathway 
The two fermentative patterns of LAB are homofermentative and heterofermentative. 
The difference between both pathways is that homofermentative LAB yields lactic acid 
as the fermentation product, where heterofermentative LAB yields mainly ethanol, 
acetate and CO2 in addition to lactate. Furthermore, the difference between both depends 
on the presence or absence of the enzyme aldolase, which is considered a key enzyme of 
glycolysis. Homofermentative LAB contain the enzyme and produce two molecules of 
lactate from glucose by the glycolytic pathway. Heterofermentative LAB lack aldolase and 
cannot break down fructose biphosphate to triose phosphate. Instead, they oxidize 
glucose 6-phosphate to 6-phosphogluconate and then decarboxylate this to pentose 
phosphate. The pentose phosphate is then converted to triose phosphate and acetyl 
phosphate by the key enzyme phosphoketolase. The early steps in catabolism by

Heterofermentative LAB convert triose phosphate to lactic acid with the production of 
ATP. Furthermore, to achieve redox balance the acetyl phosphate produced is used as 
an electron acceptor and is reduced by NADH (generated during the production of 
pentose phosphate) to ethanol. This occurs without ATP synthesis because the energy-
rich CoA bond is lost during ethanol formation. Because of this heterofermentative LAB 
produce only one ATP/glucose instead of the two ATP/glucose. In addition, because 
heterofermentative LAB decarboxylate 6-phosphogluconate, they produce 𝐶𝑂2 as a 
fermentation product unlike homofermentative LAB, which do not produce 𝐶𝑂2 
(Madigan, Michael T., 2012). The metabolic pathways of L. lactis are well known and a 
genome sequence is available. The free energy metabolism of L. lactis is relatively simple, 
where approximately 90% of the substrate is recovered as by-products, primarily lactate 
(Koebmann et al., 2002).  
 
2.3.2 Mixed-Acid fermentation 
Mixed acid fermentations occur when L. lactis utilize multiple pathways in the terminal 
steps of fermentation. The industrial importance of L. lactis is based on the ability to 
rapidly ferment sugars into lactic acid. In addition, metabolism of L. lactis is homolactic, 
where >90% conversion of sugars to lactic acid. This behaviour is typically seen during 
rapid growth in which significant amounts of glucose remained in the medium. However, 
under certain conditions, this behaviour is lost and leads to mixed-acid fermentation, 
==PAGE:14==
Page 14 of 64 
 
which yields an additional ATP along with the production of an increased number of 
other metabolites, such as formate or CO2, acetate and ethanol (Puri et al., 2014). Mixed-
acid fermentation occurs in glucose-limited chemostat cultures, at lower rates of growth, 
when fermenting sugars that are slowly metabolized e.g. galactose and maltose (Palmfeldt 
et al., 2004). carbon-limited chemostat steady-states or under carbon-excess conditions 
with certain sugars. Furthermore, rapid flux through the central pathways results in 
homolactic fermentation while diminished rates of sugar metabolism lead to mixed-acid 
fermentation (Garrigues et al., 1997). The metabolic pathway for anaerobic mixed-acid 
is shown in Fig. 4.  
 
Fig. 4. Illustration of metabolic pathway for anaerobic mixed-acid. Glucose is oxidized along the glycolytic 
pathway, and the product pyruvate is reduced to lactate via lactate dehydrogenase (LDH). Pyruvate is 
cleaved into acetyl-CoA and formate by pyruvate-formate lyase (PFL). Formate is exported via the channel 
FocA for periplasmic reduction by formate dehydrogenases (FDH), or it is disproportionated to H2 and

reduced in two steps by aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) and alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) to ethanol 
and remaining acetyl-CoA is converted to acetate via phosphotransacetylase (PTA) and acetate kinase 
(ACK). The products formate, lactate, acetate, and ethanol are exported from the cell (Lü et al., 2012). 
 
2.4 Proteolytic system of LAB 
The proteolytic system of LAB illustrated in Fig. 5 can be divided into three groups based 
on the function of the structural components: proteinases, peptidases and the transport 
system. The function of the proteinases is to breakdown caseins into peptides. Peptidases 
degrade the peptides and the transport system translocate the degraded products across 
the cytoplasmic membrane. The proteinases are present outside the cell wall and catalyse 
partial hydrolysis of one or more of the casein components to many different 
oligopeptides, where only large peptides are formed. To fulfil the needs for essential and 
==PAGE:15==
Page 15 of 64 
 
growth-stimulating amino acids further breakdown is performed by a series of peptidases 
located either outside or inside the cell membrane (Savijoki et al., 2006).  
 
Fig. 5. The proteolytic system of L. lactis. The first section A consist of a PrtP Cell-envelope proteinase, 
which contains transporters located in the cell wall; Opp oligopeptide permease, DtpT the ion-linked 
transporter for di- and tripeptides, and Dpp the ABC transporter for peptides containing 2 to 9 amino acid 
residues. In section B intracellural peptidases are illustrated; PepO and PepF are endopeptidases, 
PepN/PepC/PepP general aminopeptidases, PepX X-prolyl dipepti- dyl aminopeptidase, PepT 
tripeptidase, PepQ prolidase, PepR prolinase, PepI proline iminopeptidase, and PepD and PepV 
dipeptidases D and V. In section C illustrates the transcriptional repressor CodY, which senses the internal 
pool of branched-chain amino acids (isoleucin, leucine, and valine); using these residues as cofactors CodY 
represses the expression of genes comprising the proteolytic system in L. lactis (Savijoki et al., 2006). 
 
2.4.1 Cell envelope proteinases (CEP) of Lactococcus lactis 
There are two different types of CEP, where one type (PI) cleaves the β-casein 
components and a small amount of αs1-casein and the other type (PII) cleaves αs1-casein, 
β-casein and k-casein components (Pritchard, 1993), (Børsting et al., 2015). Between 
both types, an intermediate group is defined as PI/PII, which contribute caseins to amino 
acid supply, which have an influence on growth (Flambard et al., 1998) The sequences 
of the CEPs between different strains of L. lactis subspe. cremoris SK11 (CEP type PII) 
and L. Lactis spp. Cremoris Wg2 (CEP type PI) is about 98% homologic (Børsting et al., 
2015) The selection due to cleavage of αs1-casein and β-casein are based on the 
difference in amino acids of the A-domain. SK11 contains arginine and lysine in the A-
domain, while WG2 contains leucine and threonine. In addition, the proteinase of SK11 
==PAGE:16==
Page 16 of 64 
 
has a stronger positive charge, which is determined by the interaction with the casein. 
The CEP of L. lactis is a large extracellular protease covalently linked to the 
peptidoglycan of the cell wall. Strains of L. lactis are typically auxotrophic for several 
amino acids and need an extracellular protease in order to grow to high cell densities 
when using milk as medium (Hansen & Marcatili, 2020). The homological sequences of 
CEPs in specific LAB strains have been studied in a database, where the properties of 
the proteinases and their secondary structure have been found. In addition, different 
domains in CEP and their characteristics were described. The multi domain CEP is 
encoded by the genes PrtP of L. lactis, where these domains start in the N-terminal. PrtP 
proteinases require a maturation protein PrtM to be activated, these PrtM proteins are 
located directly upstream of the PrtP and oppositely transcribed in L. lactis (Wg2 and 
SK11). An overview of all the different domains in L. lactis are illustrated in Fig. 6. 
 
Fig. 6. Overview of the cell envelope proteinase in L. lactis incl. all domains: PrtP. PP (pre-pro domain) 
PR (protease domain), I (insert domain), A domain, B domain, H (helical domain), W (cell wall domain) 
and AN (anchor domain). Black dot indicates sorting signal (Siezen, 1999). 
 
The pre -pro domain (PP- domain) is translocated over the cell membrane via a signal 
peptide-dependent pathway and activated by cleavage of the pro-peptide activates the 
pathway. The pro-peptides of the proteinase PrtP is removed by autocatalytic activity. 
After the PP-domain, the catalytic protease domain (PR-domain) is located. The catalytic 
domain is coupled to the C-terminal extensions and the property of the sequence is 
repeated. The catalytic domain of CEP belongs to subtilisin-like serine proteases and 
contains an Insert domain (I-domain). Sequence identity of the I-domain is at least 10% 
significantly lower than rest of the PR domain and in L. lactis the I-domain of PrtP affect 
the substrate specificity, but not the protease activity or folding. The A-domain is located 
after the PR-domain, where the structure is globular and consist of β-sheet. The function 
of A-domain is unknown. However, based protein engineering studies A-domain is a part 
of regulation of the proteolytic activity and specificity of the PR-domain. Furthermore, 
the B-domain is only found in the CEPs of Lactococci and Lactobacilli. The B-domain 
also consist of β-sheet and form the secondary structure, these segments form 
==PAGE:17==
Page 17 of 64 
 
hydrophobic core of the domain.  The B-domain is not required for the proteolytic 
activity and specificity of the protease since it is absent in active CEPs. Studies revealed 
that the B-domain has an influence on the stabilization of the protease domain, since 
deletion of B-domain in lactocepin leads to autoproteolysis (Siezen, 1999). 
Autoproteolysis inhibit proteolytic activity and ultimately leads to inactivation of the 
proteinase (Laan & Konings, 1991). Autoproteolysis occur in high concentration of Ca+ 
ions and affect the conformation (Eijsink et al., 2011; Laan & Konings, 1991). The 
conformation is more rigid (unfolded) and leads to decreased interaction with β-casein 
and modify the specificity of autoproteolytic proteinase (Laan & Konings, 1991). The 
helix domain (H-domain) is located between B-domain and the cell wall. H-domain 
consist mostly of α-helixes secondary structure and therefore called Helix-domain. H-
domain is an additional domain in PrtP and the function is to position A- and B-domain 
away from the cell wall surface. The W-domain (Cell-wall spacer) is hydrophilic and 
located in cell wall. The cell-wall anchor (AN-domain) is present in L. lactis PrtP and 
located inside the cell wall. The AN-domain consist of α-helixes and has a sorting signal, 
which is cleaved and covalently linked to the peptidoglycan layer (Siezen, 1999).

Casein micelles (shown in Fig. 7) consist of casein proteins (dry basis 94%) along with 
calcium phosphate (6%). They have a supramolecular structure of colloidal (Phadungath, 
2004) and hydrophobic interactions (Huppertz et al., 2018). Casein micelles size varies

106 to 109 Da. Casein micelles has many genetic and phosphoryl residues, especially β -
casein. β -casein affects the structure of casein due to the large amount of propyl and 
proline residues that result in the formation of α-helical and β -sheets.  Casein proteins 
have different regions of hydrophobic and hydrophilic site along the protein chain, where 
β -casein is more hydrophobic than α-casein and k-casein (Phadungath, 2004). k-casein 
is hydrophilic and located at the surface of the micelles, which stabilise and makes the 
casein micelles soluble in water (Fehér et al., 2020). Caseins are heat resistant at 
temperatures below 120 °C, while sensitive to pH changes which can lead coagulation at 
pH < 4.6 (Phadungath, 2004), (Sinaga et al., 2017). Coagulation occurs during 
acidification, where the k-casein brush is lost when the negative charged residues become 
protonated (Huppertz et al., 2018).

2.5.1 Milk used as a growth medium 
The multiple auxotrophies in LAB have probably been accumulated as a consequence 
of the abundance of growth supplements in milk. This has made bacteria dependent on 
the correct release of all essential growth factors for optimal growth. However, L. lactis 
grown in milk will have to meet several challenges to survive in changing environments 
e.g. changing concentrations of amino acids, peptides, sugars, (an)organic compounds, 
decrease of pH and increasing cell density and ultimately, nutrient limitation (de Jong et

3 Analytical methods 
3.1 HPLC 
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is a chemical analytical method. The 
setup is illustrated in Fig. 8. HPLC technique provides an advantage for the chemical 
analytical area due to sensitivity, high power of separation, high speed and detectability. 
This method provides different aspect of molecule behaviour and interaction by 
detection and separation. The separation process is performed based on the 
concentration of the substances in the mixture (Kazakevich & Lobrutto, 2007)(Kennedy 
& Silva, 1991). 
 
Fig. 8. Illustration of HPLC system. 
High performance liquid chromatography works by separating the components of the 
liquid sample into a column depending on the affinity of the individual components for 
the mobile phase. Components with different polarities and mobile phases of a separate 
polarity will pass through the column, where one component will migrate faster than the 
other. Normally, the molecules of the same compound will move in groups, resulting in 
separation of compounds in separate bands inside the column. In relation to liquid 
chromatography, the mobile phase slowly filters down through the stationary phase at the 
same time as the separated components are brought along by the mobile phase. 
 
3.2 Flow cytometer 
Flow cytometry is a technology developed to rapidly analyse single cells or particles as 
they flow past single or multiple lasers while suspended in a buffered salt-based solution. 
Each particle is analysed for visible light scatter and is measured in two directions; the 
forward direction which indicate the relative size of the cell and at 90° which indicates the 
==PAGE:21==
Page 21 of 64 
 
internal complexity or granularity of the cell. In addition, the particle is analysed for one 
or multiple fluorescence parameter (McKinnon, 2018). Thousands of cells are forced 
one by one at high speed through the laser, where these measurements provide 
information related to cell counting, cell sorting, determination of cell characteristics and 
function, detection of microorganisms, detection of biomarkers etc. Flow cytometers 
require the cells to be in an evenly dispersed suspension (Wyllie, 1987). 
 
3.3 Optical density (OD) 
Optical density (OD) or turbidity measurements are applied to estimate biomass in 
microbial and mammalian cell processes and are typically measured at 600 nm. The light 
scattered by a culture sample depends on the concentration of cells, the species and the 
strain of microbe, and the growth-calibration must be performed for each type of cell 
line. Measurement of cell density is an important parameter to monitor during a 
fermentation process, which can be performed either by using a sterilizable probe 
inserted directly into the fermenter or off-line measurements in a spectrophotometer. 
Optical density methods will detect all particulates present in a sample (Hills, 2017).

4 Materials and methods 
4.1 Strains 
The model organism used to study the proteolytic system of LAB is L. lactis, where 
MG1363 pAK80-prtPSK11G1, MG1363 pAK80-prtPSK11G3, MG1363 pAK80-
prtPwg2G1, MG1363 pAK80-prtPwg2G3 and MG1363 pAK80 (control, PrtP-) were 
selected. The genome sequence of L. lactis subsp. cremoris MG1363 is the most 
intensively studied lactococcal strain worldwide. In addition, it is a plasmid-free 
Lactococcus strain. The set of strains used in this study was constructed by Ida Nynne 
Laforce in collaboration with the National Food Institute. The prtP gene from MS22337 
is inserted into MG1363. The strains were constructed by cloning the region carrying 
prtP and prtM from six different L. lactis strains into the plasmid vector pAK80, 
substituting the beta-galactosidase gene with the prt-genes. Two versions of all prtP genes 
were constructed, one carrying the entire prtP gene and a truncated version lacking the 
region coding for the domains, H, W, and Anchor. The strains are all lac negative as the 
lac plasmid is missing from MG1363. The strains are erythromycin resistant as pAK80 
carry the ery selectable marker. 
 
4.2 Stock solutions 
4.2.1 Glucose 
LAB initiate the mechanism of respiration by breaking down glucose into two molecules 
of pyruvate through glycolysis (EMP pathway) (Eiteman & Ramalingam, 2015). 
Conversion of glucose exclusively through the EMP pathway leads to a maximum 
theoretical yield of 2 mol lactate per mol glucose. Since the used strains in this study are 
lac-negative, glucose is added to the medium.  
A 5M glucose stock solution was made by dissolving 450 g of D-(+)-Glucose at 99.5% in 
500 mL of distilled water at 160 °C for approx. 30 minutes (on a magnetic stirrer) followed 
by sterilization in the autoclave.  
 
4.2.2 Sodium hydroxide 
The end product lactate is toxic and hinders the growth above a certain concentration. 
The toxic effect of lactate depends on its undissociated form. Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) 
is therefore added to the medium to increase the pH, which lead prolongation of growth 
==PAGE:23==
Page 23 of 64 
 
in the presence of higher concentrations of lactate. An increase of the fermentation pH 
to approx. 7.0 leads to prolonged exponential growth, which results in a higher final cell 
density. However, when increasing the pH to 7.5, a hindrance of the growth might occur 
and since NaOH is an alkaline neutralizing agent it could lead to cell damage at high 
concentrations. (Mierau et al., 2005).  
A 1M NaOH stock solution was prepared by dissolving 40 g NaOH in 1000 mL distilled 
water. The composition NaOH can be seen in appendix C, Table C 1 (MERCK). 
 
4.2.3 EDTA  
EDTA is alkaline and used to achieve a rapid clearance of the milky appearance 
indicating the destruction of micelle structures and aggregates (Morç et al., 2001). A 2 L 
stock solution of 0.2 % NaOH and 0.2 % EDTA was prepared to mix with the milk 
samples at a ratio 1:9 for clearing the samples before measuring OD600nm. The collected 
samples during each run of fermentation were diluted and incubated for 5 minutes on 
ice before measuring OD. EDTA is a hexaprotic ion (chelating agent), which means that 
each of the acid oxygen’s and each of the amine nitrogen’s can donate one electron pair 
by binding metal ions. A complex with calcium ions is formed, where EDTA will react 
with Ca in 1:1 molar ratio.

4.2.4 M17 
M17 agar medium is applied for the cultivation, detection and enumeration of e.g. LAB 
species in milk and dairy products. The M17 agar was prepared by dissolving 50 g M17 
agar powder in 950 mL of distilled water and sterilized by autoclaving at 121 °C for 15 
minutes. The composition of M17 agar medium is given in appendix C, Table C 2 
(Sigma-Aldrich). The used cultures were stored at -80 °C and maintained on M17 agar 
plates by incubating at 30 °C for 48 hours. 
After cultivating the strains on M17 agar plates a M17 broth is used for further cultivation 
ON. The used M17 broth is based on a recipe by Sigma-Aldrich and was prepared by 
dissolving 42 g of powder in 1 litre of distilled water and then divided into 5 x 200 mL 
and then sterilized by autoclaving at 121°C for 15 minutes. The 200 mL bottles were then 
divided into two 100 mL bottles to prevent contamination when opening the flask for 
each use. In addition, 2.5 mL glucose (0.5%) and 20 L Erythromycin (25 mg/mL) was 
==PAGE:24==
Page 24 of 64 
 
added. A colony of the applied strain was cultivated ON at 30°C in 10 mL broth along 
with a control sample of the broth to exclude any contamination risks. The procedure 
was performed for each experiment. The composition of M17 broth is given in appendix 
C, Table C 3 (Sigma-Aldrich). 
 
4.2.5 Inoculation culture 
The culture cultivated ON at 30°C in M17 medium (10 mL) was up concentrated by 
centrifugation for 5 minutes at 30°C followed by removal of 2 mL supernatant and 
vortexed for approx. 3 min for a homogenous suspension. 1 mL of the suspension was 
then added to 50 mL autoclaved skim milk with the addition of 1 mL 5M glucose and 
10 µL Erytomycine 25 mg/mL. This suspension was then cultivated ON at 30°C and used 
as inoculation culture for each experiment.    
  
4.2.6 Medium 
The standard medium used for all experiments is skim milk 0.1 % fat with the addition 
of 5 µg/mL erythromycin and 100 mM glucose. Each run between pH shift took approx. 
3-4 hours of continuous fermentation, where 4 L of medium is needed. The skim milk 
was divided into 2 L laboratory flasks and heated until approx. 95 °C. The milk was 
cooled in the fridge overnight and 40 mL of 5M glucose and 400 µL of 25 mg/mL 
erythromycin was then added. During fermentation, the medium was kept on ice to 
ensure low temperature and prevention of coagulation. The lid of the flask was connected 
with a 0.2 µm filter to avoid contamination and to release pressure during heat treatment, 
fermentation etc.  
 
4.3 Experimental setup  
The device applied for these experiments is a bioreactor BIOSTAT A (see Fig. 9). The 
temperature control is ensured by the cooling system with a heating blanket around the 
culture vessel. In addition, the culture vessel is designed with direct stirrer drive motor, 
6-blade disk impeller. Furthermore, three digital peristaltic pump modules WM 114 are 
installed in the device and convey the correction medium and nutrient medium through 
tubes into the culture vessel.      
==PAGE:25==
Page 25 of 64 
 
  
 
Fig. 9. Photo of the experimental setup. 
 
Each experiment is performed as a pH-stat analogous to a turbidostat, which is a 
continuous process where the medium is rich in nutrients and the growth rate is the 
maximal specific growth rate the organism is capable of under the given conditions. The 
feed is feedback controlled by the pH level in the culture vessel, which means that fresh 
medium is only applied when pH level exceeds a set point. The extracellular 
environment at steady state in the culture vessel deviates significantly from the feed 
medium composition since cell density secretion products accumulate, and nutrients are 
consumed. To ensure the accuracy of the performed L. lactis cultivations, the yields on 
glucose, lactose, lactic acid and acetic acid were determined for steady state conditions 
along with measurement of OD600. 
 
The most important parameter is the flow rate of the feed pump. Calibration of the feed 
pump is a very important step since the flow rate of the feed pump is equivalent to the 
dilution rate D (D = µ). The dilution rate is fixed by controlled pumping in of the inlet 
feed. The dilution rate equals the growth rate under these conditions. Calibration was 
performed manually, where the amount of feed pumped out (into the culture vessel) is 
measured to a specific time (e.g., 1 min). The measured amount (mL) was recorded in 
the software and the flow rate is determined automatically. The flow rate of the feed 
pump must be checked every time before running the experiments. Another important 
parameter is the pH probe, which was calibrated by using buffer 4 and 7. The pH probe 
includes a temperature sensor. Before each run the whole setup must be autoclaved. The 
==PAGE:26==
Page 26 of 64 
 
culture vessel and the feed flask with all the tubes, filters, probes etc. must be sterilised. 
It was important to cover the filters with foil to prevent them from getting wet during 
autoclaving. Sterilisation of the set-up is performed one day in advance.   
 
Before starting the experiment, all sensors were connected to the laptop. The level probe 
was placed at the surface of the medium in the culture vessel. The level probe ensures 
constant level (volume) in the vessel. Furthermore, the temperature was controlled by a 
cooling system along with a heating blanket covered around the culture vessel. The 
temperature was logged in the software and all experiments were performed at 30°C. The 
agitation was set to 100 rpm and the N2 source is set to 100 ccm and a pressure at 1 bar 
until oxygen level reaches 0%, where the setpoint for N2 was changed to 25 ccm and a 
pressure at 1 bar. During the whole experiment, the medium for continuous feeding was 
maintained max. 4 °C in a bucket of ice. It was important that all connected flasks in the 
setup had a special lid connected with an ”open tube” incl. a filter to release pressure and 
avoid contamination.   
   
4.3.1 Sampling  
All samples collected during each run was based on steady-state conditions. The flow rate 
of the feed pump corresponding to the dilution rate D, pH, temperature, O2 level, 
agitation speed and volume level were continuously recorded throughout the experiment. 
Furthermore, 3 mL samples were collected from the culture vessel approx. 5 minutes 
before and after inoculation. Afterwards, samples were collected every 30 minutes, when 
the pH setpoint was achieved and the feed pump starts to pump to maintain pH in the 
culture vessel. After samples were collected during steady-state conditions for the first pH 
setpoint, samples were again collected 30 minutes after the feed pump starts again when 
the next setpoint was achieved. From the 3 mL samples, 2 mL samples were used for 
HPLC analysis and 1 mL sample was used for OD600. 100 µL sample (from 1 mL OD600 
sample) was diluted in 900 µL 0.2 % NaOH, 0.2 % EDTA (clearing of the milk by 
alkaline EDTA with a 10-fold dilution, 100 mM pKa 8). OD600 sample with 10-fold 
dilution was kept on ice in 5 min before measured. At the same time 2 mL HPLC sample 
was heated at 95°C for 5 minutes and then the samples were kept on ice to inhibit growth 
and saved for further analysis (HPLC measurements of glucose, lactose, lactic acid and 
acetic acid concentration). Steady state is achieved when a steady cell density on OD600

with a 30-minute interval have been obtained, the conditions are changed to next setting 
by changing pH set point (pH 5.8) or medium with 20 mM NaOH and injection of 5 mL 
NaOH 1 M (if the fermentation medium contain 10 mM NaOH). 
 
4.3.2 HPLC analysis 
Liquid chromatography was performed on an Ultimate 3000 HPLC-system equipment 
consisting of a pump, vacuum degasser, UV detector and autosampler. The Aminex 
HPX-87H Column and instrument method named Smartstartup-HPX-87H+RI_purge 
was created for the equilibration and ramping of flow rate. The Aminex HPX-87H 
column size is 9 µm, 300 mm x 7.8 mm and the corresponding guard column were used.  
The corresponding mobile phase (A) is 5 mM Sulphuric acid and was used for sugar or 
organic acids analysis.  Each sample was measured with an automatic programmed 
method, where the measurement starts with “Smartstartup-Column” for a water sample with 
an injection volume of 5 µL and the following samples were measured with the instrument 
method “RI_30min_Lin2020” and the process method “New processing method” with 
an injection volume set to 20 µL. Each sample are running in 30 minutes with 0.5 mL/min 
flow rate at 5°C in approx. 31 bar. 
 
Eluent 
HPLC running phase buffer was prepared by adding 20 mL Sulphuric acid 5 M to 1000 
mL distilled water under vacuum filtration with a filter size of 0,22 µm. The final 
concentration of running buffer was 0.5 mM. 2 L running phase buffer runs approx. 100 
samples with the standard “New processing method”. 
 
Preparation of HPLC standard solutions

shaken until a homogeneous and clear solution was obtained. Five standard solutions of 
following concentrations; 1:10, 1:20, 1:100 and 1:200 was prepared by diluting from the 
stock solution in appropriate amounts (see Fig. 10). The five solutions were analysed and

6.1 Growth kinetics of L. lactis subps. cremoris SK11, WG2 and control 
strain 
Growth properties were measured based on spectrophotometric measurements of 
optical density (OD600) for continuous cultivations at four different conditions. The cell 
densities of the different strains are shown in Fig. 11, Fig. 12 and Fig. 13. For the strains 
SK11 G1, SK11 G3, WG2 G1 and WG2 G3 a similar pattern for growth was observed. 
The cell density was lowest at the first condition using standard medium and pH setpoint 
6.2. It was observed that the cell density is higher, when shifting the pH setpoint to 5.8. 
To achieve the desired pH level at 5.8, the bacteria must produce an increased amount 
of lactic acid (see Fig. 20, Fig. 21 and Fig. 22), which can only be possible by increasing 
the number of cells. Experiments including another pH setpoint at 5.4 was performed 
without success due to coagulation of the culture medium. Instead, addition of NaOH 
was introduced to the medium for further investigation of different conditions. NaOH 
was added to increase the pH, which lead to prolongation of growth in the presence of 
higher concentrations of lactate (Mierau et al., 2005). The cell density was higher, when 
using medium containing 10 mM NaOH and even higher when changing to medium 
with 20 mM NaOH.   
The cell density for the control strain is shown in Fig. 13. A visible difference was 
observed in comparison among the strains. The highest cell density was obtained when 
using medium containing 10 mM NaOH. In addition, a lower cell density was seen when 
shifting the pH setpoint to 5.8. A clear difference was observed in the medium containing 
10 mM NaOH and pH setpoint 6.2. This difference could be due the lack of a 
proteinase. Generally, LAB need 4 to 16 essential amino acids and peptides for growth. 
The amount of free amino acid and peptides in milk are limited, which lead to slow 
growth of the control strain. The rapid growth was observed for SK11 and WG2 strains, 
since the CEP degrade caseins in milk to peptides and amino acids and transport it into 
the cell. Furthermore, the cell density of SK11 G3 was higher than SK11 G1 when using 
standard medium pH 6.2. The OD600 measure was approx. 0.3 for SK11 G1 and 0.4 for

6.2 T-test  
In order to perform comparative studies of the used strains a two-sample t-test is used to 
test whether or not the means of two populations are equal. The t-tests are performed for 
OD600  measurements, dilution rates and yield coefficients Y [OD/CLA] for the used strains 
for each experimental condition. The tests are performed to determine whether or not 
the mean OD600  measurements, dilution rates or yield coefficients Y [OD/CLA] between 
two different species is equal e.g. SK11 G1 vs SK11 G3. However, there might be at least

to determine whether or not this difference is statistically significant.

is determined.

7 Discussion  
The purpose of this master thesis was to investigate the use native milk in fermentations 
designed to determine the yield coefficients under different growth conditions of isogenic 
strains. 
The use of milk as medium is often avoided for several reasons: it is complicated, difficult 
to standardize, turbid, it is a heterogeneous colloidal suspension, acidification cause 
coagulation and proteolysis can cause coagulation. However, the results of the present 
work demonstrate that metabolic quantitative measurements of L. lactis using native milk 
as medium were successfully carried out. All the experiments were performed as pH-stat 
continuous cultures. SK11 and WG2 strains containing two versions of the proteinase 
were studied, G1 has the entire proteinase and G3 lacks the region coding for H, W and 
anchor domains. Generally, SK11 and WG2 strains used for cheese production in the 
dairy industry contains all the domains.  
However, results obtained in this study revealed that there was no significant difference 
between the strains under certain conditions, even though there is a difference it might 
be so minor that it will be excluded due to large variations. The question is why nature 
have preserved three domains if there is no need for them. However, there might be a 
difference between the two versions of proteinases.  
Statistical analysis of the strains SK11 G1 and SK11 G3 showed significant differences 
for the conditions at pH 6.2 and pH 6.2 with 20 mM NaOH, where the yield for SK11 
G1 was Y = 4.8E-04 [OD600/CLA] and for SK11 G3 the yield is Y = 6.4E-04 [OD600/CLA] 
at pH 6.2. In addition, at pH 6.2 with 20 mM NaOH the yield for SK11 G1 was Y  = 
4.1E-04 [OD600/CLA] and for SK11 G3 the yield is Y = 5.0E-04 [OD600/CLA]. In addition, 
significant differences for the strain WG2 were observed for the conditions at pH 5.8. 
The yield for WG2 G1 was Y = 5.4E-04 [OD600/CLA] and for WG2 G3 the yield is Y = 
4.5E-04 [OD600/CLA].  
It may be the case, that the chosen conditions for these studies were not comprehensive 
enough and it is therefore necessary to investigate other conditions, where there is a 
possibility for observing a clear difference between the strains at same conditions. 
Though, due to limited time all the planned experiments were not carried out. A 
suggestion for other conditions to investigate could be running the experiments with a 
setpoint at approx. pH 6.4. A study about how the proteolytic system of LAB could be 
==PAGE:50==
Page 50 of 64 
 
optimized to improve acidification of camel milk conducted by Ida Nynne Laforce 
showed that difference of the slope values between the strains are clearer at approx. pH 
6.4 - 6.5 (see Fig. 31) in comparison with pH 6.2 and 5.8. The slope values correspond 
to the growth rate of the specific strains.  
 
Fig. 31. Acidification curves conducted by Ida Nynne Laforce of different L. lactis strains based on OD600 
measurements. The medium used is organic skim milk 0.1% fat from Arla A/S.  
 
In this study, native milk was successfully used for quantitative analysis of metabolism. 
However, the weakness with milk is when pH decreases below approx. 5.8 it starts to 
coagulate. Therefore, this kind of analysis is possible if the pH value is kept above 5.8. 
Normally, it will be difficult to reach high cell densities at such high pH. The problem 
was solved by the addition of NaOH to make the milk more alkaline and vary the cell 
density. There is a limit for pH tolerance of the bacteria and therefore medium with 10 
mM NaOH is used in the beginning of the experiments and later replaced by medium 
with 20 mM NaOH. This means that the bacteria are not in an environment, where they 
reach pH values at approx. 10. When using medium with 20 mM NaOH it contains 
sodium chloride and there is a possibility of changes of the milk proteins, but no changes 
in colour of the milk was observed.  As long as the milk is white and not transparent, the 
milk might still have micelle structure.  
Another suggestion to investigate differences in growth energetics between the strains 
could be variations in the feed medium e.g. 50% diluted milk, 25% diluted milk and 10% 
==PAGE:51==
Page 51 of 64 
 
diluted milk with the addition of 100 mM glucose and 5 µg/mL erythromycin. Diluted 
milk will be a poorer medium and it can be expected to see a lower growth rate and if 
the milk is diluted too much, the outgrown cell density might not reach the same cell 
count as in undiluted milk. Furthermore, when milk is diluted the biomass formation 
and lactate production might be lower and the buffer capacity might be affected, where 
there is a possibility for that pH level will be much higher or acidification does not reach 
the same level as for undiluted milk. However, diluted milk could be used to investigate 
the model, where the bacteria catch the entire casein micelle and it could be expected 
that G1 versions holds on to the micelle and grows well in diluted milk since it will have 
enough protein for a while. The G3 strain on the other hand would have a lower 
concentration of protein so peptides are released with a low concentration.  
Furthermore, the medium is heat-treated gently at approx. 90°C, which might have an 
impact on the growth kinetics of the bacteria. In the cheese production milk is not heated 
above 72°C since the proteins will be denatured (Singh & Waungana, 2001). Therefore, 
there might be a possibility of that the heat treatment applied in this study might have 
consequences for β-Lactoglobulins to react with the casein micelles. In addition, another 
study “Acidification kinetics in milk fermentations – influence of type and heat treatment 
milk, and type and attachment of the cell wall proteinase” conducted by Betül Yildrim 
revealed that there was clear difference in behaviour of the bacteria depending on 
whether the medium is raw milk or pasteurized milk. However, further studies are 
needed to investigate whether the native milk gets affected by the heat-treatment.         
Another challenge was fluctuations in glucose concentrations. From the same sample 
glucose, lactose and lactic acid were measured on HPLC, where values for lactose and 
lactic acid concentrations were reasonable. To investigate the cause of fluctuations, new 
standard curves were made and samples of medium with and without glucose were 
measured to investigate whether other organic compounds had the same retention time 
e.g. another peak on top of the glucose peak. When comparing the old and the new 
standard curves for glucose no difference was observed. The results of samples with and 
without glucose revealed that no other compounds had the same retention time (see 
appendix D, Figure D 1 and Figure D 2). This was not investigated further due to the 
limited time. However, an option could be to use other standard curves (dilution series) 
instead of diluting glucose in H2SO4. Instead, the dilution series could be glucose diluted 
in milk. In addition, the standard medium before inoculation or fermented culture 
==PAGE:52==
Page 52 of 64 
 
medium containing lactate could be used for the dilution series. Moreover, experiments 
with medium containing glucose could be performed and after steady-state conditions are 
obtained, the feed could be replaced with medium without glucose to study the growth 
behaviour of the strains. Growth is not expected since the strains are lac-negative, but it 
is still interesting to study.   
The strains MG1363 pAK80-prtPHPG1 and MG1363 pAK80-prtPHPG3 were not 
investigated due to limited time. However, it could be interesting to look further into it.  
According to the hypothesis it was expected to see a significant difference between the 
G1 and G3 strains at the same conditions, so the result could be plotted in a graph similar 
to the graph presented in figure 6 in S. Benthin studies (Benthin et al., 1994). In the 
graph based on S. Benthin’s studies, CLA /OD600 [(µg/mL)/OD] is plotted against 1/µ 
[min]. However, significant differences were not seen for the strains at same conditions 
and the variation of values for the dilution rate were large therefore the results from this 
study are presented as a yield coefficient Y [OD600/CLA]. 
 
8 Recommendation for future work 
Continuous cultivations using native milk as feed medium were carried out successfully. 
However, based on the obtained data further studies are needed. In the previous section, 
most suggestions for improvements for future work were described. However, some of 
the challenges that had an impact on the comparative studies of the growth energetics 
could be improved by small adjustments. Variations in the obtained results were observed 
and for future work it is important to consider other methods to avoid this.  
The two most important parameters are measure of biomass and the dilution rate D. 
Both parameters were affected by large uncertainties. It was very challenging to obtain 
uniform and accurate data from the OD600 measurements. OD are generally an acceptable 
tool to use for the determination of cell density. However, challenges due to accuracy of 
the measurement had occurred and some experiments were therefore excluded. A 
suggestion for similar future experiments is to use another instrument for measuring 
biomass e.g. a flow cytometer. A flow cytometer provides a more precise determination 
of the cell count. In standard flow cytometry the cells can be stained by addition of dye. 
Instead of using dye that must react with the bacteria, a fluorescence gene could be 
==PAGE:53==
Page 53 of 64 
 
inserted in the plasmid of the bacteria e.g. green or red fluorescence protein for detection. 
This would make it possible to cultivate multiple strains and study the growth kinetics.  
Furthermore, high uncertainties related to dilution rates were observed, which might have 
been caused by instability due to maintenance of a constant volume. A typical challenge 
related to fermentation processes is the formation of foam in the culture vessel, which 
was observed in most experiments. This can either be minimized by mechanical methods 
e.g. appropriate agitator and propellers, which is the best option recommended for not 
affecting the medium or by addition of anti-foaming agents.  
 
9 Conclusion  
L. lactis subsp. cremoris SK11 and WG2 are well characterized with respect to the major 
energy generating process, which is metabolism of sugars. In this study native milk was 
successfully used as medium for continuous cultivations to study the metabolism of the 
used isogenic strains. Comparative studies were conducted to investigate whether a 
significance difference between the strains could be seen. The statistical analysis revealed 
that a significance difference of the yield was seen between the SK11 G1 and SK11 G3 
at pH 6.2 and pH 6.2 with 20 mM NaOH. In addition, a significant difference of the 
yield was seen between WG2 G1 and WG2 G3 at pH 5.8. However, the significant 
difference between the strains were seen at different conditions, the uncertainty and 
variation observed for the results might have an impact. The overall conclusion is that no 
huge difference between SK11 G1, SK11 G3, WG2 G1 and WG3 is seen and requires 
further investigation, where other conditions might be introduced as mentioned in the 
discussion and recommendation section.
