Abstract
This thesis concerns modelling and optimizing periodic finite structures which are
strong against buckling including boundary effects. Using linear elastic theory, the
structures were optimized for increasing stiffness which is not directly linked to buck-
ling strength. Thus, geometrical perturbations were included in a multi-perturbation
strategy, where an topology optimization algorithm was used to find the worst case of
the stiffnesses of the perturbated structures and expecting the structures to be better
towards buckling if the perturbations are modelled by simple buckling mode shapes.
Two reference structure’s performance was calculated in terms of compliance as well as
the three first buckling modes and were used as the reference point for all other opti-
mized structures. In one case, including geometrical perturbations the performance of
the structure against buckling had an improvement of 177% of the first buckling load,
unfortunately, the structural compliance was 17% higher, but is still within acceptable
limits. Generally, it was found that most of the optimized structures including the
perturbated ones did not improve the design against buckling much more than the
standard linear optimized structure and hence other geometrical perturbations have to
be found to improve the structures further.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
With recent advancement in process technologies, manufacturers have the ability to
create structures with increasingly better topological resolution. As a result, a vast
amount of research in geometrically enhanced materials has been conducted, that is,
changes in the material layout to increase the performance of an arbitrary property.
Before the usage of topology optimization as a tool to explore the possibilities of creat-
ing materials with multi-physical properties, the design process of structures has been
based on experience, intuition and large parameter studies which is deemed to be very
time inefficient already for a low number of parameters. With the use of topology
optimization, the designer can ensure a better performing structure by letting math-
ematical algorithms optimize the structural performance. The advantage of topology
optimization lies in its versatility. Today, examples of fields of usage includes struc-
tural engineering, fluid mechanical problems and photonics and hence its applicability
is almost unlimited because of the very general optimization method.
Motivation
In recent years, studies on buckling instability using homogenization theory, linear sta-
bility theory and Floquet-Bloch theory have been conducted for arbitrary macroscopic
load conditions (Thomsen, Wang, & Sigmund, 2018; Neves, Sigmund, & Bendsøe,
2002). However, most of the studies do not take into account the non-linear effects
which inherently characterize buckling as well as the consequences of finite domain
boundaries. In addition, a robust design formulation including geometrical imperfec-
tions is implemented to ensure buckling strength improvements.
In this thesis, a finite periodic buckling structure is optimized using both linear and
geometrically non-linear physical modeling. The structure’s performance towards buck-
ling is here given by its stiffness. A larger stiffness is directly linked to postponing the
buckling load of the structure. The structure will also be investigated using a robust
formulation including a multiple of sinusoidal perturbations and hence optimizing for
the worst case of these perturbations.
Parallel computing
To decrease the computational cost of the optimization process, two computing libraries
PETSc and SLEPc for scalable parallel solutions of scientific applications in C++ has
been used to run the code on the HPC (High Performance Computing) cluster at the
Technical University of Denmark.
As the computational program is run in the high performance computing cluster, the
most efficient number of processors for the problem is wanted to cut computational

Report structure
The thesis is structured in the following way; In Chapter 2, the theoretical background
on structural instability is presented and physical models for both linear and geomet-
rically non-linear behaviour are considered and the topology optimization problem is
defined. Moreover, In Chapter 3, A robust design formulation including different geo-
metrical imperfections to increase the buckling strength of the structure is presented.
In Chapter 4, the general topology optimization theory is identified and utilized for
maximizing structural stiffness, in the shape of minimizing compliance. In this section,
the regularization and projection methods are included and lastly a computational
procedure overview is given. Lastly, in Chapter 5, the results are presented with a
thorough discussion on the validity of topology optimization solutions.

CHAPTER 3
Geometrical perturbation formulation
As stiffness does not guarentee that the structure will be strong against buckling and
many times the structure will easier buckle with increasing stiffness. For this reason,
geometric perturbations are included in the design process to increase the buckling
resistance by effectively increasing the width of the members in the structure and cre-
ating cross bars. This will ultimately also often decrease the stiffness of the structure
which is a compromise that is usually taken. However, by using the topology optimiza-
tion the decrease in stiffness is kept to a minimum as the structure is optimized for
maximum stiffness even with geometrical perturbations.
So how should one design the geometrical perturbations to increase buckling strength
of the structure? This is a very hard task and multiple tests have been made with
different perturbations. The simplest form of perturbation that comes to mind is some
kind of sine function. This is used due to the fact that the buckling modes of a simple
Euler beam is in the shape of a sine or cosine. As the first mode shape tend to a
shearing mode, the geometrical perturbations are only used in this thesis in the x-
direction. However, local buckling modes can occur if perturbations in the y-direction
were included. Although, it was found that using perturbations in the y-direction did
not change the design significantly and is only increasing the number of realizations
without giving much to the final design. The perturbation used was of form

CHAPTER 4
Topology Optimization
In this chapter, the key concepts of the topology optimization strategy are presented.
The topology optimization method will be used upon the material design problem
defined in the previous section for maximizing the stiffness of a periodic structure
and hence delaying the mechanical instabilities in the structure such as buckling and
snap-through. In addition, a design parametrization is elaborated which includes the
periodization using finite unit cells. Moreover, a sensitivity analysis using the adjoint
method is presented together with a regularization method and a projection technique.
Furthermore, an energy interpolation scheme is presented as numerical instabilities
in the geometrically non-linear optimization happens due to excessive distortion of
low stiffness elements.
Lastly, a computational procedure describing the numerical
implementation is presented.
4.1
Key concepts of topology optimization
Topology optimization is a general mathematical method to consistently design me-
chanical problems. More precisely, topology optimization concerns minimizing or max-
imizing a physical property while satisfying imposed constraints. The constraints exist
in two forms; equality and inequality functions which are used to describe the phys-

function could be the residual equation in non-linear mechanics or volume preservation
respectively. The general topology optimization model is given as:

4.2
Design parametrization
Since the problem is build on the density based approach, each finite element in the
design domain Ωp is given a continuous relative density µi ∈[0, 1]. As mentioned,
the material properties in each cell will be interpolated using the SIMP-scheme as it
has been generally proved versatile for a number of different problems, some of which
mentioned in the introduction. The modified version of the SIMP method will be used
as it includes finite elasticity modulus in the zero density regions. Another method
which could have been implemented is the Rational Approximation of Material Prop-
erties (RAMP) scheme proposed by Stolpe and Svanberg (2001) which has non-zero
sensitivities at zero densities. By the modified SIMP-approach, the element stiffnesses
are evaluated as
Ee = Emin + ρp
e(E0 −Emin)
where Emin is the void phase elasticity modulus to ensure that the void elements have
a minimum stiffness above zero to avoid singularity of the stiffness matrix, p is a
penalization factor and E0 is the Young’s modulus of the solid phase. The value of
the minimum stiffness is also ensured to be small enough to have no significant effect
on the total stiffness of the structure. A value of Emin = 10−9E0 is mostly used in
litterature. However, other values can be used for circumventing problems with e.g.
artificial modes in linear buckling analysis.

Computational Procedure
In Figure 4.4 a schematic of the computational procedure is shown. The routine starts
with initializing the design variables to a preset initial configuration, e.g. uniform, ran-
dom or structured distribution. The configuration can include passive elements. Next,
the design variables are periodized to impose periodic unit cells. Hereafter, the densities
are filtered using a density filter and projected using a smoothed heaviside projection
and then periodized again to periodize the filtered design variables. The next step
includes employing the finite element analysis including the geometrical perturbation
formulation and solve the compliances and linear buckling load factors. When this
step is completed, the sensitivities are mapped using the mapping matrix LT and then
the unfiltered sensitivities are calculated from the physical design variables using the
chain rule and again mapped back. Using the MMA solver, the design variables are
updated for the next solution step using the sensitivities and current state of the design
variables and if the solution has converged, i.e. the updated design variables are below
a tolerance limit, the routine terminates writing design variables and physical design
variables to postprocessesing files.

In this chapter, the results for a topology optimization of a rectangular domain under
compression applied to the top surface in form of a pressure load q is investigated as
defined in the problem formulation in Section 2.5. The default linear and non-linear
setup parameters are the following
Default linear setup parameters
For the linear case, the top surface is loaded with a total force of Q = 100 kN, this
is an arbitrary value and has no significance other than the scaling with the buckling
load factors as the system is linear. The domain is square with Lx = Ly = 1 m and
discretized into 800 × 800 elements. The design area is divided into nx × ny unit cells
with characteristic length scale d. A minimum volume fraction of 40% was imposed as a
constraint and the density is regularized using a penalization factor of p = 3 with a filter
kernel radius of r = 0.04d. The material considered has Young’s modulus E1 = 3 GPa,
and void Young’s modulus E0 = E1 · 10−9 = 3 Pa, Poisson’s ratio ν = 0.4 and is
modelled under plane strain assumption. The optimizer is modeled with maximum
change on the design variables of 0.1 for each iteration.
Furthermore, the passive elements are modelled with a height of 10% of the domain
height and the relative density is considered constant at ρ = 1. The smoothed heaviside
projection filter threshold was set to η = 0.5 and the intensity β = 0.5 initially. β was
updated using the continuation β →2β every 30 iterations until a maximum threshold
of β = 512 is reached. Lastly, the optimization is stopped when the design variables
µ change is within 0.1% and the beta value has reached its final value. These values
have been used in all designs if none otherwise specified.
Non-linear default setup parameters
For the non-linear case, the above should be used except the penalization factor is
updated using a scheme with p = 1 initially and p →p + 0.02 every 5 iterations until
p = 2 and hereafter following the updating scheme p →p + 0.05 until p = 3.
The non-linear solutions have not been finished and is thus not a part of this thesis,
while focus has been maintained on the geometrical perturbations instead.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to see whether it was possible to design structures that
are good in buckling while still keeping the stiffness of the structures. The foundation
of the work has been linear and non-linear continuum elastic theory combined with
topology optimization to enhance a periodic structure. As the objective for the topology
optimization was not buckling strength, but rather stiffness, measures had to be made
to try to increase the buckling strength of the structure. This was done by imposing
geometrical perturbations on the design domain and using a ”min-max” formulation to
optimize for the worst case scenario of the many stiffnesses of the perturbed structures.
It was found to be a very comprehensive task and the surface of the perturbation
strategy has just been touched.
It was found that the buckling load factors of most of the linear structures where not
significantly different from the standard linearly optimized structure. However, it was
found that in one case the buckling performance of the structure was 177% of the
reference structure with the cost of its stiffness resulting in an increase of compliance
by 17%. It was found that the tendency of the buckling loads increased with increasing
stiffness for these exact structures and could be a general trend for periodic structures,
but can not be verified by this work.
Due to the high complexity of the geometrical non-linear optimizations and their compu-
tational cost, they were not finished and the focus rested upon the linear optimizations
and especially trying to find a good perturbation strategy which is a far harder task than
initially thought.
6.1
Future Work
To the author’s knowledge, the topic of optimizing buckling strength for periodic struc-
tures with topology optimization is mostly an untouched area. Multiple directions to
improve design and performance of the structure is still available to investigate such
as
• Include other geometrical perturbations that will possible increase the buckling
strength better than the ones used without decreasing the stiffness of the struc-
tures.
• Non-linear prebuckling optimization of the structures are yet to be investigated
and it is assumed that with increasing non-linear stiffness in the prebuckling area
will increase the structures ability to withstand buckling.
• Another area of interest is the post-buckling behaviour of the optimized structures
and this field could be studied further using e.g. the arc length method.
