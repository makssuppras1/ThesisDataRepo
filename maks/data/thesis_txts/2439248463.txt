ABSTRACT

ABSTRACT 
The present Master Thesis project discusses the use of Machine Learning (ML) schemes in the field of 
Dynamic Vehicle Routing (DVR) with uncertainty, and more specifically in dynamic taxi sharing problems 
with stochastic travel times. The main goal is to investigate how different ML techniques could provide a 
better input in the routing algorithms and a more effective approach in order to capture the dynamic state 
of a transportation system. This approach is very interesting both from a ML and Operation Research 
perspective, since it combines the advanced input of traffic variables, such as speed, into optimization 
techniques. An important part of the present study is the evaluation of machine learning models in the 
context of time-series analysis. The evaluation is performed in terms of accuracy in short-term forecasting 
of speed values in a Copenhagen road network. These speeds are later transformed into travel times. 
Furthermore, a routing algorithm which aims to address the computationally expensive nature of the taxi 
ride-sharing problem is introduced. 
Within the context of this work an empirical research, in the form of a case study, was conducted, in order 
to integrate into the same framework both the routing algorithm and the machine learning inputs. The case 
study involved a Copenhagen area and real speed data, for the corresponding road network. Different day 
times of interest, for a particular day, were finally investigated in the case study. Given the historical speeds, 
short-term predictions on traffic speeds were made. Different scenarios, referring to different techniques 
of obtaining speed values and consequently travel times were tested for the times of interest. Namely these 
scenarios are: OpenStreetMap travel times, where no machine learning is used, Offline Machine Learning 
travel times, where median values of historical speeds (seasonality) are used, and Online Machine Learning, 
where predictions regarding speeds and travel times were made. Afterwards, the results of each scenario 
were compared to the true results, obtained using the true speeds and consequently true travel times about 
the time of interest. This way, conclusions about the accuracy of each scenario were drawn. As suggested, 
by the results, Online Machine Learning with Bayesian Linear Regression had the best performance among 
the tested models. 
This work was explicitly developed in Jupiter Notebook programming environment, in Python.

CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION 
1. Introduction 
1.1. General Description of the Topic 
Urban infrastructure, rapid urbanization and modern fast living pace have resulted in public transportation 
systems being overused and operating at the limit of their capacity (Samaranayake et al., 2017; Alonso-
Mora 2016; Taniguchi, 2014). Meanwhile, the crowdedness and frequent delays in public transportation 
means have made taxi services a key component of the urban transportation system, because of their 
provided flexibility and thereby, convenience (Sampaio et al., 2017; Gao, Jiang, & Xu, 2018). Citizens opt 
for ease and convenience in their everyday transportation. This need has prompted for a different type of 
urban mobility, namely mobility-on-demand (MoD) systems. MoD transportation systems provide customers 
with transportation services that match their preferences, in terms of pick-up and drop-off time and location 
(Alonso-Mora et al., 2017), while they reduce the travel times and caused distress. 
Whereas, no one can deny the contribution of urban mobility to the socioeconomic development of cities, 
concerns have been raised regarding the urban living quality, in respect to traffic congestion, energy 
consumption and environmental pollution (Gao et al., 2018; Masson R., 2017; Sampaio et al., 2017). 
According to Sampaio et al. (2017), this situation constitutes a challenge for City Logistics, which aims for 
environmentally friendly, yet cost-efficient ways to reduce the flows of people and freights (Sampaio et al., 
2017; Savelsbergh & Van Woensel, 2016; Crainic and Montreuil, 2016). 
Within this context, taxi-sharing seems to be a promising approach in order to bridge the gap between 
sustainable and convenient transportation. Similar to ride-sharing, taxi-sharing refers to individuals sharing 
the same taxi in order to commute to a common location, but rather than using a private car, a taxi is 
deployed instead (Ma, Zheng, & Wolfson, 2013). Taxi-sharing is regarded as an innovative solution that 
would reshape the traditional taxi service (Maciejewski, Bischoff, Nagel, & Berlin, 2016).

In an actual taxi-sharing setting, where customers’ requests are received in real time, the request 
combinations and taxi routes have to be computed dynamically and are not known in advance. The taxi 
routes are decided only after the requests are placed. Since information about the problem are revealed 
after the execution has started, this suggests that the taxi-sharing concept constitutes a dynamic vehicle 
routing problem (DVRP) (Alonso-Mora et al., 2017; Sopha et al., 2016). Additionally, in these types of 
problems, the time parameter, in terms of waiting time and travel delay, plays a significant role, since it is 
indissolubly connected with customer satisfaction. In other words, the estimation of realistic times, 
regarding customers’ waiting and travel times is of great importance, so that the proper routes are chosen, 
and customers are satisfied with the service. For this reason, in the present study stochastic travel times, 
based on historical data, are used for the prediction of future travel times. So, essentially this study 
discusses a dynamic and stochastic vehicle routing problem (DSVRP) (Ritzinger et al., 2015). 
In order to address properly this problem, the accurate prediction of travel times is crucial and also one of 
the basic aims of this work. In the process of predicting stochastic travel times, predictions regarding traffic 
speeds are made and different techniques can be used (Polson & Sokolov, 2017). In the present study, 
various ML models were examined in order to obtain a higher level of accuracy regarding the routing and 
scheduling results. Following, the model performing better is used in a real case study, and the relevant 
results are presented. 
1.2. Project Objectives 
Having discussed the general field of this study and its significance, the next step would be to explain the 
objectives of the present thesis.  
The main purpose of the project is the conduction of an empirical research, so as to obtain a better 
understanding regarding how Machine Learning (ML) techniques could facilitate and improve the routing 
and scheduling phase in dynamic vehicle routing problems (DVRP) with uncertainty. More specifically, the 
project is focused on a dynamic taxi-sharing problem with stochastic travel times. By studying this, we will 
be able to identify which Machine Learning (ML) models have better performance, and also how accurate, 
compared to the real-data, the predictions of these models are. Furthermore, the data used for the ML part 
are provided through crowdsourcing, therefore it will be possible to also evaluate how co-created 
information adds accuracy.

Apart from the main purpose, there are also some individual, distinct goals. These goals are the following: 
1. Since the topic of the study is a ride-sharing problem, our primary goal is to exploit the potentials 
of ride-sharing as much as possible, meaning that we wish to utilize at the maximum the capacity 
of each vehicle. This approach has previously been supported because of its sustainability (Alonso-
Mora et al., 2017; Wei et al. 2016). 
2. Since the type of this service is user centric, customers’ satisfaction is of great importance. For this 
reason, another goal is to create routes, and assign a fleet of taxis to requests in a way that the 
travel delay and the waiting time for the customers is minimized. The travel delay refers to the 
additional time that it takes for a request to be served due to taxi-sharing, while the waiting time 
refers to the time that customers have to wait to be picked-up by the taxi. 
3. Following, having studied historical data for our area of focus, we aim to identify the volatility of 
traffic behavior and how it affects travel times. Traffic congestion has a direct impact on the speeds 
of vehicles, which consequently affects the relevant travel times. 
4. Last, since we are dealing with a dynamic VRP it was considered of key importance to build a 
method that could follow-up the dynamism of the problem. Accordingly, another goal is to create 
a set of “heuristics” that would minimize the computational time, which is required for the system, 
in order to create the routes and assign requests to the vehicles.

2. Literature Review  
2.1. Vehicle Routing Problems  
2.1.1. Types of VRP 
Vehicle Routing Problems (VRPs), which tend to be complex problems that combine a lot of different 
components (Barkaoui, 2017), have been of great interest for the research community over the last years 
(Mayer et al., 2016). Based on various criteria, these problems can be divided in many different categories.  
Depending on the evolution and the quality of information within VRPs, they are divided to four (4) different 
categories. To elaborate more, based on the evolution of information a VRP can be categorized as 
dynamic, when information about the problem is revealed after the execution of the route has started. In 
this case, the evolution of information happens dynamically and therefore these types of VRP are considered 
dynamic (DVRP). An element of dynamism can be anything that includes a level of uncertainty, like 
unknown number of customers or demand level, possible traffic jams, unknown location of depots, 
unknown travel times, etc. (Bouchra et al., 2016; Cheng et al., 2017; Savuran & Karakaya, 2016; 
Swiechowski, 2017; Wei et al., 2016; Yousefi et al., 2017).  On the contrary, when all the required 
information about the routes and the planning is known to the planner before the beginning of the 
execution, then this suggests that the routing process will not change in the meantime (Sopha et al., 2016), 
instead it will remain static. The VRPs that incorporate such a concept are defined as static VRPs. 
Regarding the quality of information, VRPs are divided in two categories, namely stochastic and 
deterministic VRPs. In stochastic VRPs speculative information about the future is used in form of 
probabilities or probability distribution, in order to predict the parameters that are not known. For instance, 
these parameters could be the actual demand (Khaligh & Mirhassani, 2017; Pietrabissa & Pietrabissa, 2017; 
Sarasola et al., 2016; Sopha et al., 2016; Yousefi et al., 2017), the time that requests have occurred (Cheng 
et al., 2017; Hajibabai & Ouyang, 2016; Pöcher, 2017; M. Ulmer, 2015), the travel times (Felix, Ulmer, & 
Mattfeld, 2015; Perboli et al., 2017; Rodonaia, 2016; Swiechowski, 2017), the location of the requests (Al-

Kanj, Powell, & Bouzaiene-ayari, 2016; Hajibabai & Ouyang, 2016; M. Ulmer, 2015; W. Ulmer, Mattfeld, & 
Köster, 2017), etc. or a combination of them.  That being said, when no probabilities or probability 
distribution is used, but instead the information input is accurate and precise, then these VRPs are 
considered deterministic (Hajibabai & Ouyang, 2016; Psaraftis et al., 2015). 
Combining all this information, it comes naturally that there are four (4) main possible combinations of 
VRP, based on the categories mentioned above. These combinations are the following:  
▪ 
SD (static & deterministic) 
▪

▪

▪ 
DS (dynamic & stochastic) 
In comparison to static vehicle routing problems, the dynamic ones tend to be more complex and 
challenging, due to their dynamism, which allows more degrees of freedom (Pillac, Gendreau, Guéret, & 
Medaglia, 2013). However, allowing more degrees of freedom suggests that a considerably large decision 
space needs to be examined quite fast in order to provide the results in real-time (Alonso-Mora, 
Samaranayake, Wallar, Frazzoli, & Rus, 2017). Providing results in real-time and fast enough is necessary 
for DVRPs, since, at every given moment, the outcome of one step can significantly affect the following 
steps. Moreover, as Pillac et al. (2013) discussed in their paper, DVRP may differ from their static 
counterparts in terms of their objectives. For example, while in a typical static scenario the goal frequently 
is to minimize the routing costs, in a dynamic scenario the aim could be to maximize the service level, 
minimize the rejected requests or minimize the travel delay (Cao et al., 2016; Pillac et al., 2013). 
DVRPs with time windows (DVRPTW), meaning that they take into account time constraints, are NP-hard 
optimization problems, due to the complexity of obtaining optimal solutions (Rincon-Garcia, 2014). Such 
problems are discrete-time dynamic problems and can be perceived as a series of instances, where each 
instance can be regarded as a static problem that has a specific start time and needs to be solved within a 
determined period of time (Armas and Melian-Batista, 2014). NP-hard optimization problems require the 
use of techniques in order to produce a solution that will not be exact, but of high quality, while satisfying 
the time constraints. This way, an efficient solution can be achieved in a reasonable time and for a large 
number of instances. As Rincon-Garcia (2016) mentions, such optimization techniques include heuristics 
and metaheuristics in order to develop a solution within an acceptable time. Abdallah et al. (2017) highlights 
==PAGE:24==
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW 
7 
 
as well the use of metaheuristics for NP-hard problems and more specifically suggests the use of Ant Colony 
System (ACS) and Genetic Algorithm (GA), while in the same research GA was deployed. Santos and Xavier 
(2013) examined a DVRP, including taxi and ride-sharing, where requests arrive dynamically. According to 
their approach, the day was divided into time periods and each period was studied as an instance of a 
static problem, which was solved by a Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP). More 
specifically, in each GRASP iteration, first a greedy randomized solution is produced and then a local search 
is performed, aiming to improve the greedy randomized solution. The procedure ends when a specific 
number of iterations is completed, or within a maximum allowed time. Last, Alonso-Mora et la. (2017), who 
also studied a DVRP with ride-sharing, point out that obtaining an optimal solution for such an NP-hard 
problem can be computationally expensive. Likewise, they suggest the search of a sub-optimal solution 
within a predefined running time. Afterwards, this sub-optimal solution can be incrementally improved up 
to being optimal. 
Considering that in most real-world applications the element of uncertainty is inevitable, it comes as no 
surprise that dynamic and stochastic routing problems have become popular to study (Ritzinger, Puchinger, 
& Hartl, 2015; Saint-guillain, Deville, Solnon, & Louvain, 2015). Another reason for gaining such popularity 
could be the fact that in real-time, dynamic scenarios, predicting accurately the stochastic elements of the 
problem could become rather challenging. Cao et al. (2016), in their recent research, also support this 
opinion. According to them, it is rather trivial for a navigation system to define the shortest path, distance-
wise, from one location to another. However, the shortest path is not necessarily the fastest one because 
of parameters like traffic, roadwork or weather conditions. Therefore, dynamic stochastic VRPs have 
become an attractive subject for further investigation (Pillac et al., 2013).  
The literature has highlighted different ways of handling stochasticity, out of which the basic ones are the 
following: a) stochastic modeling, and b) sampling (Saint-guillain et al., 2015; Pillac et al., 2013). Stochastic 
modeling includes analytical integration of stochastic knowledge in order to fully capture the stochastic 
nature of the problem (Saint-guillain et al., 2015). Within this approach strong simplifying assumptions 
need to be made, while it is regarded as computationally expensive and its formulation requires special 
technical knowledge (Pillac et al., 2013). On the contrary, in the sampling method the stochasticity is 
captured by sampling scenarios. This approach is based on creation of various different scenarios, so as to 
gain a representative view of the random variables’ distribution. Once the different scenarios are generated, 
then each one is treated as a static deterministic problem that needs to be optimized (Pillac, Gendreau, 
==PAGE:25==
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW 
8 
 
Guéret, & Medaglia, 2013). While sampling offers flexibility regarding the distribution assumptions and is 
quite simple in contrast to stochastic modeling, its drawback is the huge number of generated scenarios, 
which is required in order to display accurately the true situation (Saint-guillain et al., 2015). 
2.1.2. Operational Types of DVRPs: People Transportation 
Based on their operational type, DVRPs can be divided to four (4) main different categories. These 
categories include service delivery, transportation of goods, transportation of people and simple visits (Pillac 
et al., 2013; Pietrabissa & Pietrabissa, 2017). Service delivery could be referring to maintenance visits, as 
Hajibabai & Ouyang (2016) described on their paper, or response to emergency scenarios, like power 
outages, as Al-kanj, Powell, & Bouzaiene-ayari (2016) discussed. Goods transportation, which is a pick-up 
and/or delivery (PDP) VRP, is also mentioned as city logistics (Pillac et al., 2013). PDP routing problems 
deal with picking-up goods from a specific location and delivering them to another one (Pelzer et al., 
2015a). In the context of city logistics, a high level of uncertainty lies in travel times, as subject to traffic 
congestion, road work and the time of the day (Köster, Ulmer, & Mattfeld, 2015; Pillac et al., 2013). Based 
on the operational type of the problems, other parameters like the time window, or capacity constraints 
might also be affected. For example, transportation of people at first glance looks similar to transportation 
of goods. However, the former one focuses on the human factor and therefore other parameters like 
customer convenience and satisfaction are of great importance. So, in such problems it is expected that 
attention will be placed on waiting and travel times and the respective constraints will be in place (Pillac et 
al., 2013). 
People Transportation 
As a matter of fact, people transportation problems, incorporates many different concepts and can be 
further classified, depending on the study perspective (Pillac et al. 2013). In the context of individual 
mobility, Wei et al. (2016) focused on a taxi pooling routing problem, where the objective was to maximize 
the benefits for taxis, while maximizing the load rate of the vehicle. Ramezani and Nourinejad (2017) also 
focused on taxi coordination problems, but their aim was to improve the service quality for the customers, 
while minimizing the operational costs for the taxis (Ramezani & Nourinejad, 2017). Within the same 
context, Billahard et al. (2017) centered upon minimizing the travel time for taxis and the waiting time for 
customers (Billhardt et al., 2017).

Last, both Pelzer et al. (2015) and Alonso-Mora et al. (2016) examined ridesharing systems, in terms of 
taxi-ridesharing. Pelzer et al. (2015) targeted in maximizing the benefits for the taxis, while minimizing 
the inconvenience for the customers. At the same time, Alonso-Mora et al. (2016) also focused on customer

2.1.3. Ride-Sharing Systems: Taxi-Sharing 
In modern society, urbanization and buoyancy of the economy have brought an upsurge in mobility 
demand. Consequently, urban transportation systems are operating overcrowded, while citizens’ demand, 
for a solution to be found cannot be ignored. This could suggest either improving the infrastructure and 
the road network, or seeking for smart solutions that would enable a more efficient use of the existing 
resources (Pelzer et al., 2015a). In this context, taxi-sharing has become an emerging trend and many 
prominent researchers highlight its significance (Alonso-Mora et al., 2017; Manna & Prestwich, 2014; Jung 
et al., 2013; Lin et al., 2012; Kuo & Tang, 2011; Agatz et al., 2010).  
It should also be highlighted that at the sight of the urban transportation challenges a new vision has been 
raised. Part of this vision is the development of self-driving vehicles that would be able to utilize more 
efficiently the current transportation infrastructure or even operate underground, using a new 
transportation network, similar to the subway. The extensive research and development in the field of 
Autonomous Vehicles (AV) and the constantly increasing transportation needs, especially in large 
metropolitan areas, are expecting to introduce these innovative AV applications in the near future. The 
majority of these AV applications are depending their operation on the concept of ride-sharing. Therefore, 
ride-sharing studies are expected to gain high popularity in respond to the new AV technologies. It can be 
argued that a solid background in ride-sharing algorithms will be necessary in order for these AV 
applications to be implemented successfully. In absence of ride-sharing algorithms, that make it possible 
for AV applications to operate in real time and scale up to the system’s demand, the AV technologies may 
get undermined due to poor ride-sharing approaches, and eventually, their adoption might arrive later than 
expected (Gurumurthy, K. M., & Kockelman, 2018; Hall, 2016; Zhao, Liang, & Chen, 2018).

Ride-Sharing 
Before elaborating more on taxi-sharing, it would be wise to understand how ride-sharing systems operate 
in general. According to Dong et al. (2018), “Ride-sharing is a means of transportation where more than 
one person with common origin and/or destination travel in a car to share (at least a part of) their journeys”. 
Essentially, ride-sharing constitutes a system of co-utility, since all parties involved have some potential 
benefits to gain (Sanchez et al., 2016). The drivers are independent entities, who drive their private vehicles 
and share the ride with other people, minimizing this way the operational costs. However, apart from 
minimizing costs, such a concept will maximize the vehicle occupancy and consequently the number of 
vehicles travelling on the city network will be reduced. In other words, this is a step towards tackling traffic 
congestion (Saadi et al., 2017; Morency, 2007). In a dynamic context, like  Wong et al.  (2017) mention, 
the procedure of finding a ride-sharing match would be initiated on a very short notice, or even en-route, 
and an automated system would be used for that purpose (Wong, 2017; Agatz et al., 2010).  
Considering different criteria, there are various categories of ride-sharing systems. Following Pelzer’s et al. 
(2015) work, based on the number of drivers and riders, there are four (4) different categories: 1) single 
rider – single driver, 2) single rider – multiple drivers, 3) multiple riders – single driver, and 4) multiple 
riders – multiple drivers. Moreover, based on the deviation of passengers’ routes the following four (4) 
models were identified: 1) many origins – one destination (MOOD), 2) one origin – many destinations 
(OOMD), 3) many origins – many destinations (MOMD), and 4) one origin – one destination (OOOD) (Pelzer 
et al., 2015). 
Whereas traditional ride-sharing seems to have a lot of potential, and many applications have been 
developed (Saadi et al., 2017), some concerns have been raised as well. To begin with, the security factor, 
or better, the lack of it, is a parameter that cannot be overlooked. Private ridesharing services are operated 
by private vehicles and drivers; therefore, it is anticipated that concerns regarding passenger security will 
be raised. Moreover, private information regarding passengers’ locations are shared with the private drivers, 
with no insurance about the existence of a fare-collection system. Additionally, in order to use such services, 
a vehicle must be available, and both schedules must be matching (Jung et al. 2013).  
Taxi-Sharing 
Taxi ride-sharing is a similar alternative, but a more effective one, that comes to bridge this gap. This 
concept is very similar to recurring ride-sharing (Lin et al., 2012), however, their differences have previously 
==PAGE:28==

been pointed out by several researchers (Manna & Prestwich, 2014; Jung et al., 2013; Lin et al., 2012; 
Agatz et al. 2010). Taxi-sharing can be perceived as an on-demand ride-sharing service, that includes a 
fleet of taxis instead of private vehicles, and licensed taxi drivers instead of private ones. The system 
receives individual transportation requests in real-time. An online dispatching center treats these requests 
and seeks among potential vehicles, to find the best match for each incoming request.  More specifically, 
customers that would like to use the service submit their ride queries in real-time via their cell phone. Each 
query includes information about the customer’s location, as origin, and desired destination. Additionally, 
the customer can indicate time windows, specifying this way the pick-up and drop-off time. Once a new 
request is received and processed, it can either be accepted, or rejected depending on whether or not a 
vehicle is available, in respect to the set of constraints. In case a vehicle is found, then the request is 
inserted to its schedule and the information regarding the new schedule are shared both to the driver and 
the passengers (Ma et al., 2013; Jung et al., 2013). 
Recently, various researches have been devoted to taxi ride-sharing problems. Lin et al. (2012) highlights 
the need for improvements in the transportation system, so as to tackle traffic congestion, and focus their 
study on routing optimization of ride-sharing taxis. Their objective is to minimize the operational costs, 
while maximizing customer satisfaction. Their results showed that the proposed model managed to save 
up to 19% and 66% respectively in regard to mileage and taxis available (Lin et al., 2012). A year later, 
Jung et al. (2013) examined how taxi sharing algorithms can be of benefit to taxi services and improve 
their performance. They applied Hybrid Simulated Annealing (HSA) and the simulation results proved that 
allowing ride-sharing for taxis increases the overall productivity of the service (Jung et al., 2013). At the 
same time, Ma et al. (2013) studied large-scale dynamic taxi ride-sharing. They proposed a scheduling 
algorithm and applied a lazy shortest path calculation strategy to speed up the algorithm. Moreover, an 
experimental platform was built to simulate real user behavior when using a taxi. Their findings showed 
that deploying ride-sharing on taxi services made it possible to serve 25% more users, and save 13% in 
travel distance, compared to traditional taxi services, where no ride-sharing takes place (Ma et al., 2013). 
Manna et al. (2014) also investigated on-line taxi-sharing, where additional requests arrive during the 
execution of the routes and new matches are made. Stochastic information regarding the arrival of requests 
was used and their focus was to maximize the shared trips, while minimizing the expected travel delay. 
Dynamic taxi-sharing is a DVRP that in most cases considers the element of stochasticity, in terms of travel 
times, time of requests occurrence, location of customers, etc. The main reason behind it is that in a real-

case scenario, decisions need to be made within a short period of time and they also have to be accurate. 
Within this context, the potential contribution of machine learning techniques for short-term forecasting is

in order to use such techniques, a high volume of information has to be available and this information has

2.2. Machine Learning 
2.2.1.  From Data to Decisions  
The continuous advances in the field of computer and natural sciences has unleashed abundant potential 
in evidence-based decision making (Horvitz & Mitchell, 2010). This potential is directly related with 
capabilities in the field of data science. The exponential increase in computing power and memory, the 
increased popularity of cloud services and the development of state-of-the-art data processing algorithms 
pave the way for intelligent information systems that are being introduced in a wide spectrum of 
applications. The high digitalization of services fosters the large-scale accumulation of data, which are then 
being fed into these intelligent systems to further be transformed into insights in decision support. This 
data transformation and creation of information has been greatly reinforced by machine learning algorithms 
that can automatically analyse a magnitude of historical data and evaluate several alternative outcomes. 
Often, the nature of those outcomes can be probabilistic and can provide a level of certainty on the decision-
making part.     
This data centric approach finds breeding ground in transportation applications where intelligent 
transportation systems are being deployed to provide solutions in a very dynamic and sensitive context. 
The success of contemporary innovations on autonomous vehicle technologies and car sharing are highly 
dependent on data that model travel patterns and traffic volumes. Low quality in these data and failure to 
accurately model the traffic speeds would most likely result in inappropriate routing of vehicles and poor 
system-wide optimization and coordination. It comes with no doubt that a poor evaluation of the travel 
behaviour in the network could slow down the adoption of those innovations, while undermine the efficiency 
of current applications (Rodrigues & Pereira, 2018).

In general, transportation is a field with substantial room for improvement within a wide range of modes, 
considering both the commute inside metropolitan areas and the worldwide commercial logistics. In the 
context of city logistics, data sets about traffic flows, speeds, road topology and public events are a 
necessary input to enhance the reliability of the machine learning algorithms and thus improve the efficiency 
of different modes of city transportation (Horvitz & Mitchell, 2010). The improvement of the transportation 
system is inherent in the ability of the operator to predict accurately future traffic flows and provide real-
time routing and fleet balancing services. However, the ultimate success of these implementations is 
explicitly dependent on the data quality and is subject to the operators understanding of critical factors in 
traffic behaviour. Unsupervised machine learning and deep learning are setting the background for 
intelligent information systems that can surpass human ability on pattern recognition and information 
extraction. Nevertheless, human judgement is still driving a big part of the success both from a data 
‘feeding’ and final decisions perspective. 
2.2.2. Data in Transportation: The value of co-creation 
The collection of data in a transportation context is certainly not trivial. The main traditional sources of data 
in transport studies are surveys, diaries, simulations or static traffic sensors and tracking devices. These 
conventional methods of retrieving data can be particularly time-consuming and quite often the quality is 
subject to participants judgment and memory, since they are asked to report their travel behavior (Assemi, 
Schlagwein, Safi, & Mesbah, 2015).  
The latter of the mentioned data sources, static traffic sensors, are being deployed for traditional speed 
modeling. The advantage of this approach is the high accuracy and reliability of the sensors. However, 
there are some inherent limitations in this method, such as the high deployment and maintenance cost.  
Consequently, in studies where a wider sensing coverage of an entire road network is needed, the static 
traffic sensors might be a prohibitive expensive alternative. 
The answer to the above limitation could come from simulation. Data collection through this method is 
cost-efficient and trajectory data has been generated in many studies as an alternative. Monte Carlo 
simulations, for example, could in some cases replace real-life data with acceptable accuracy (Pointer et al. 
2004). Nonetheless, these data are not always reflecting the true travelling behaviour of people and the 
occurrence of random events, such as driving culture, public events, weather conditions or accidents. In 
==PAGE:31==
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW 
14 
 
such a case, the quality of the trajectory data is biased on the assumptions supplied into the simulation 
model. 
Another data collection alternative that could face the shortcomings of the existing methods has emerged 
through the popularity of cloud services and mobile technology. The extensive use of GPS services in 
everyday life and their integration with personal mobile devices (smartphones, tablets, etc.), supported by 
very strong telecommunication networks (4G/5G), has provided a rich stream of online geosocial data. In 
this context, passengers who carry mobile computing devices in urban areas, are constantly transmitting 
location-based attributes (geographical coordinates, speeds, destinations, etc.) with a very high accuracy, 
due to the uninterrupted access to the Internet (Nunes, Galvão, & Cunha, 2014). Access to social data 
could also provide a leverage to transport operators from the perspective of being able to explain the 
seasonal variance of traffic behaviour and forecasting traffic congestion, in anticipation of public events 
(music concerts, holidays, football matches, etc.). 
The technology enablers described above are offering a novel approach that can improve the personalised 
public transport services and enhance the travel experience of urban residents. This approach is known as 
crowdsourcing and gains increasing popularity between researchers in the transportation community 
(Assemi et al., 2015; Nunes et al., 2014; Science, Wang, Zhu, Wang, & Liu, 2018). Crowdsourcing is 
inherent to the “co-creation” nature of the modern services. Nowadays, customers are increasingly getting 
involved in shaping their own experiences (Prahalad & Ramaswamy, 2004) and they are drastically engaged 
in knowledge creation. In a transportation context, passengers become engaged in co-creating value by 
providing spatio-temporal information in real-time (Nunes et al., 2014). This pool of structured information 
can provide valuable input in different aspects of any transportation application and enhance the 
performance of intelligent transportation systems. In a case like this, both sides experience a win-win 
situation, with the passengers receiving timely information on their transportation planning and an efficient 
urban network. It is worth mentioning that the passengers who exchange information should be able to 
keep their privacy intact for security reasons. 
2.2.3.  Floating Car Data  
Within the focus of intelligent traffic management system applications, the knowledge of the actual current 
state of the road traffic, and the capability to perform accurate estimates of its short-term evolution, for 
the entire road network, are two very significant components of their successful implementation  (De

Fabritiis, Ragona, & Valenti, 2008). Having discussed the importance of high quality data on traffic 
conditions and the inherent difficulties in traditional approaches for collecting these data, the need for a 
novel approach was identified in the literature. Crowdsourcing is filling this need by providing access to a 
virtually unlimited pool of trajectory data.  
In the traffic estimation and prediction of travel times literature, the data retrieved by crowdsourcing are 
discussed as Floating Car Data (FCD) (De Fabritiis et al., 2008; Gühnemann & Wagner, 2004; Rahmani &

Many studies, including this thesis, rely on these historical FCD in the effort to forecast short-term travel 
times and estimate traffic congestion (Nanthawichit et al., 2003; Sunderrajan et al., 2016). Machine 
learning techniques such as artificial neural networks, clustering and Pattern-Matching have been used for 
short-term predictions of road travel speeds, by using current and near-past speed observations (De 
Fabritiis et al., 2008; Sunderrajan et al., 2016). Such predictive models, combined with routing optimization 
could enhance the operation of a ridesharing application and possibly shift our transportation preferences 
towards collective transportation modes (Horvitz & Mitchell, 2010; Science et al., 2018). This potential 
would be of great significance from an energy, environmental and social welfare perspective. 
2.2.4. Machine Learning in Taxi Ride Sharing: Prediction of Travel Times 
Technology enablers, mathematical advances and urban transportation challenges have increased the 
interest in novel real-time ride-sharing solutions (Chen, Zahiri, & Zhang, 2017; Najmi, Rey, & Rashidi, 
2017). The dynamic taxi ride-sharing, which is the focus of this project, refers to a system where an 
automated process, deployed by a central operator, matches requests to a fleet of vehicles on a very short 
notice (Agatz et al., 2011). The ride-sharing concept has several classifications. For example, recurring 
ridesharing or carpooling (based on private vehicles) has been studied for years (Chan & Shaheen, 2012; 
Wolfler et al., 2004), while recently, the enormous imbalance between taxis demand and supply in cities 
has prompted the need for further research in taxi ride-sharing (Dong et al., 2018). 
==PAGE:33==
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW 
16 
 
In contrast to recurring ride-sharing, taxi ride-sharing is considered a more challenging problem since both 
queries and taxi vehicles occurrence are highly dynamic and difficult to predict. While dynamic ride-sharing 
has been addressed in recent studies (Buliung et al., 2009), little focus has been given in the development 
of optimization algorithms to match queries and vehicles in real time (Agatz et al., 2011). The significant 
computational requirements of these challenges and the need for real time applicability have introduced 
machine learning as a complimentary tool in the effort to address these transportation challenges (Chen et 
al., 2017). 
The role of machine learning in the taxi ride-sharing context is inherent in some very basic needs, such as 
short-term forecasting of customer demand, understanding urban travel behaviour, traffic estimation or 
speed prediction in road segments (Chen et al., 2017; Dong et al., 2018; Ke, Zheng, Yang, & Chen, 2017). 
These steps could be considered preliminary and crucial factors for the successful implementation of any 
further optimization approach. A taxi ride-sharing system is highly depended on time, due to strong 
periodicity of demand (rush hours), and traffic patterns, which affect the speeds and consequently the 
travel times. The results of a very good optimization algorithm could be over optimistic and eventually its 
reliability could be undermined if weak assumptions were supplied. Those weak assumptions are referring 
to speeds and travel patterns that are not reflecting the real state of a network at a given time of the day. 
Machine learning could address the shortcomings of simplified travel attributes, such as the assumption of 
constant speeds and traffic flows and provide a more accurate input in transportation models. This way, 
the high dynamism of taxi ride-sharing problems could be addressed more efficiently, providing a better 
setting for optimization techniques to be tested.   
In this project, machine learning models have been applied in order to model traffic speeds and use them 
as input in the routing algorithm of a taxi ride-sharing application. In terms of sources, the traffic speed 
data is usually obtained from either individual GPS devices or static traffic sensors. Regarding speed 
modelling techniques, there are three main groups: naïve, parametric and non-parametric methods 
(Vlahogianni et al., 2004; Hinsbergen et al., 2014).  
Naïve methods are widely used in practise because of their very low computational effort and easy 
implementation. An example of naïve methods is the historical averages. This method is averaging past 
speed observations and generates the historical average of speeds in a road network (Kamarianakis & 
Prastacos, 2003; Nikovski et al., 2005). These historical averages are typically conditioned on time-of-day 
==PAGE:34==
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW 
17 
 
and day-of-week, and sometimes even on holidays etc. However, their accuracy is very low. The parametric 
or non-parametric methods are outperforming the naïve methods in most of the times (Hinsbergen et al., 
2014). The performance of the historical average method has also been tested in this project and it will be 
presented in a following chapter. 
In the parametric approach, the parameters of the model need to be found using data and the model’s 
structure is predefined. Knowledge on the transportation processes can also be implemented. In general, 
the data needed for this approach is less compared to more advanced non-parametric ones (Hinsbergen 
et al., 2014). A very common parametric approach is linear regression. In this case it is assumed that the 
response has a linear relationship with its covariates. A linear model is simple, yet, in some occasions 
produce fast predictions with very high accuracy (Rice & Van Zwet, 2004). In the parametric time-series 
literature, statistical parametric models such as ARIMA (Auto Regressive Integrated Moving Average) is 
considered a more standard time-series approach for speed predictions (Kamarianakis & Prastacos, 2003; 
Zhang & Liu, 2009). Many variations of ARIMA have been further proposed, such as SARIMA (Seasonal 
Auto Regressive Integrated Moving Average), ARIMAX (Auto Regressive Integrated Moving Average with 
Explanatory Variable, Exponential Smoothing, etc. (Chang, 2012). These models could be considered as 
the baseline of time-series analysis (Lippi, Bertini, & Frasconi, 2013). The advantage of these techniques is 
their probabilistic treatment, which provides a better level of interpretability, their computational efficiency 
and their simplicity (Box et al., 2015).  In this project variations of linear regression and ARMA (Auto 
Regressive Moving Average) have been tested and compared.  
In the non-parametric models, there are no explicit parameters and no explicitly assumed relationship 
between the dependent and the independent variables. In such models, more data is typically required 
compared to the parametric models. The advantage of these models is their capabilities to model the very 
dynamic and non-linear processes found in traffic modelling (van Hinsbergen and  van Lint, 2014). Although 
these powerful non-linear models have been successfully applied for predicting speeds (Dia, 2001) and 
flows (Zheng et al., 2016), they present a major drawback. Their black-box nature and lack of probabilistic 
treatment often undermine the interpretability of the results. Non-parametric approaches in time-series are 
ranging from k-Nearest Neighbour applied to traffic flows (Kim et al., 2005; Oswald et al., 2001), to more 
sophisticated and widely applied methods like Neural Networks (NN)  or Support Vector Regression (SVR), 
for predicting the travel times (Wu et al., 2004) and traffic flows (Polson & Sokolov, 2017). In general, 
standard neural networks and their extensions perform very well in predicting traffic data (Dougherty & 
==PAGE:35==
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW 
18 
 
Cobbett, 1997; Kim et al., 2005). Methods like Decision Trees and Regression Trees are rarely used in the 
context of traffic predictions (Nikovski et al., 2005). More recently, literature has started adopting Gaussian 
Processes (GP) in traffic problems. The Bayesian non-parametric formulation of GPs fits better complex and 
noisy time-series (Rodrigues & Pereira, 2018). In addition, compared to other non-parametric methods, 
GPs outputs can be interpreted probabilistically and produce very good results for different transportation 
problems, such as traffic speed prediction (Min & Wynter, 2011) or traffic volume prediction (Ide & Kato, 
2009). However, most of the non-parametric methods above have focused more on predictions on single 
roads and less on road networks, because of lack of data on all roads (Hinsbergen, 2014). Furthermore, 
another drawback could be the fact that they are computationally expensive and possibly difficult to use 
them in a real-time scenario, where decisions needs to be taken on the fly.  
2.3. Contributions 
Taxi ride-sharing models have recently received an increasing attention in the literature. The proposed 
models differ in their approach to solve the optimization problem, as well as in the level of input data 
required (Najmi et al., 2017). One of the main challenges in taxi ride-sharing is to develop solutions that 
can scale up in respect to the number of customers and available vehicles. In a real-time application, there 
is the need to explore a very large decision space while simultaneously being able to calculate solutions 
fast enough to provide users the experience of real-time booking. However, the task of large-scale ride-
sharing in real time is not fully addressed by current mathematical models and algorithms (Alonso-Mora et 
al., 2017; Ma, Zheng, & Wolfson, 2013; Najmi et al., 2017; Pelzer et al., 2015). 
DVRPs with time windows and specific capacity, like the one examined in this work, are NP-hard problems. 
(Rincon-Garcia, 2016; Armas and Melian-Batista, 2015). The computational difficulty that is inherent in the 
taxi ride-sharing problems is the increased capacity of the vehicles and hence the increased complexity of 
the matching calculations, especially in the case that the matching between riders and vehicles happen en-
route (Xing et al., 2009). In the case of a single taxi with a capacity of two passengers, the decision on the 
pick-up and drop-off ordering is trivial and can be as simple as six permutations of origins and destinations. 
However, the computational burden increases exponentially, and it starts being a great challenge from 
capacity of four and above. More, specifically a capacity equal to four involves 2520 permutations between 
origins and destinations, while for capacity of six, this number is soaring to 7.484.400. Capacity greater 
than six is already computationally prohibitive in the absence of heuristics. Most of the routing studies on 
==PAGE:36==
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW 
19 
 
ridesharing are considering vehicle capacity, ranging from 2-4 available seats (Lin, Li, Qiu, & Xu, 2012; 
Santi et al., 2013; Stiglic, Agatz, Savelsbergh, & Gradisar, 2015; Teodorovic & Dell’ Orco, 2005; Teodorović 
& Dell’Orco, 2008). Furthermore, in a system with multiple taxis there is also the need to examine the 
different combinations of requests in trips of successive capacity until the maximum capacity is reached. 
Theoretically, this means that if global optimality is pursued, the exhaustive search between all the taxis 
and all requests in the system should be calculated and then decide the optimal sequence or origins and 
destinations in each trip of successive capacity. However, in practise global optimality is extremely difficult 
and for this reason heuristics that avoid this complete enumeration are introduced instead. 
Similar to recent studies, which aim to reduce the size of the above challenge (Shen et al., 2015; Pelzer et 
al.,2015; Nourinejad and Roorda 2016), this project is aiming to build on the described method by Ma et 
al., 2013 and introduce a partition based heuristic, which will be explained in detail in Chapter 3. In the 
proposed approach of Ma et al. (2013) the road network is conceptually partitioned using a grid. This idea 
is providing the capability to pre-compute the travel times between the centres of the grid cells in an offline 
mode and then use these travel calculations while the system is operating in real time. The origins and 
destinations of the requests, along with the locations of the available vehicles will be necessarily placed on 
the centres of the grid cells. Even though this approach has some inherent error, since it assumes that the 
origin and destinations of the requests are recurring in fixed positions (centres of grid cells), it speeds up 
the taxi searching process as well as the calculation of possible combinations between requests. 
The paper of Alonso-Mora et al. (2017) had also been extensively studied in the effort to build a model 
which would be capable, under further adjustments, to address large-scale taxi ride-sharing problems. The 
approach of Alonso-Mora et al. (2017) and Ma et al. (2013) had been integrated into a novel model which 
is also enhanced, as proposed by Ma et al., 2013, with advanced travel time prediction techniques.  
Finally, as suggested by Manna and Prestwich (2014), this project is examining a case study of a real city 
map. The model of this project has been applied in a Copenhagen region and real speed historical data 
had been incorporated in order to test the model under different scenarios of traffic. 
As presented above this project aims to contribute in the taxi ride-sharing problems from the following 
perspectives:   
▪ 
The project provides an approach to aleviate the high computational expense of taxi ride-sharing. 
This is achieved by partitioning the road network into road segments and these segments will be

system. The computation of the shortest paths in real time is computationally demanding, 
however, in this project a “database” of pre-calculated shortest paths has been created. When 
the system is “online” and it is necessary to calculate a huge number of paiwise calculations of 
shortest paths, between origins and destinations, these times are retrieved from the available 
“database” which has been created offline. This is the process, that speeds up the taxi ride-sharing 
and provides a partition heuristic that could be applied in a real application.     
▪ 
It creates a framework that could possibly scale up the parameters of the problem (number of 
requests and vehicles, vehicle capacity). As it was described above, the partition heuristic could 
speed up the process of routing. This is very important, in a case where the number of requests 
and vehicles is high, which is the actual case in most of the metropolitan areas. In such a case 
the number of shortest path calculations between individual requests and vehicles, and pairwise 
calculations between requests (ride-sharing pre-liminary step) can be prohibitevely high. For a 
single vehicle with capacity equal to 4, the different permutations of origins-destinations that need

could possibly address effectively a system with a large number of requests and vehicles.

It takes into account stochastic travel times. The parameter of travel time is of key value in any 
transportation system, especially when people transportation is considered, since it is closely 
related to customer satisfaction. In the context of an urban environment travel times can be 
characterized by a high level of uncertainty, therefore, the need to predict them, in order to 
provide services of high quality, becomes apparent. Within this work stochastic travel times are 
considered in terms of short-term forecasting, regarding travel times. Given that the distances are 
known beforehand, we aim to predict the speeds in the different road segments and, following to 
calculate the respective travel times. To do this, different techniques are deployed in order to 
identify the best performing one.

3. Problem Formulation & Modeling 
The purpose of the present study is to investigate the impact of machine learning techniques in ride-sharing 
challenges, and more specifically, how such techniques could be deployed for providing a more accurate 
prediction of travel times, and consequently more accurate routing and scheduling results. In the context 
of the study, the central aim is to maximize the number of served requests in a single ride, with the 
minimum cost, time-wise, while satisfying the time constraints.  
This chapter introduces the problem statement of the present work, along with the method that is used 
regarding the routing/scheduling part. Note that all examples used within this chapter are indicative and 
are not part of the real case study, which is presented in Chapter 6. The method explained in this chapter 
focuses only in the routing and scheduling part and will later be combined with the scenarios proposed in 
Chapter 5, and the machine learning schemes suggested in Chapter 4, in order to be applied to the real 
case study (Chapter 6).  
The proposed method was built and tested by the authors exclusively in Python programming environment. 
3.1. Problem Statement 
Consider a set of transportation requests 𝑹, placed by customers and a set of potential vehicles/taxis 𝑽, to 
serve these requests. For every request 𝒓 ∈𝑹, we denote 𝑰𝑫𝒓 the ID of the request, 𝒐𝒓 the origin and 𝒅𝒓 
the respective destination. For each request, the time 𝒕𝒓 that the request was placed is also known. For 
every vehicle 𝒗∈𝑽, we denote 𝑰𝑫𝒗 the vehicle ID, 𝒍𝒐𝒄𝒗 the vehicle location, 𝒑𝒂𝒔𝒔𝒗 the number of 
passengers that the vehicle carries, or is assigned to pick-up, and 𝒔𝒄𝒉𝒆𝒅𝒖𝒍𝒆𝒗, the schedule of the vehicle, 
in terms of request sequence.

All the received requests are saved in the list request_list, which includes all the information regarding them. 
Respectively, all the vehicles belonging to the system can be located in the list, vehicle_list, which includes 
the corresponding information regarding the taxis of the system. Example of both lists can be found in 
Table 1 and Table 2. 
In order for a request to be assigned to a taxi, and be a part of its trip, specific system constraints need to 
be satisfied. These are, the waiting time constraint, 𝑾𝑪, referring to the maximum time that a request can 
wait until it is picked-up, the travel delay constraint, 𝑫𝑪, referring to the maximum additional time that can 
be added to a request fulfillment, due to ride-sharing and the maximum capacity of each taxi, 𝒖. A trip 
consists of, a set of requests, or a single request to be served, and a vehicle that can fulfill it. 
Assuming that each request refers to the transportation of one person, and that the vehicle capacity is 
equal to 4, then a taxi can be assigned to a combination of maximum four (4) requests. So, every shared 
trip can have maximum 4 pick-up and 4 drop-off points, in case none of the requests share a common 
origin or destination, and minimum one pick-up and one drop-off point if all requests share the same origin 
and destination (Najmi, Rey, & Rashidi, 2017). Once a trip is assigned to a vehicle, the vehicle has to 
complete this trip, meaning that all requests have to be dropped-off, before a new trip is undertaken by 
this taxi. The number of passengers on a taxi will decrease over the duration of the trip, as destinations 
are reached, and people are dropped-off. However, the taxi won’t be able to take new requests, before 
dropping-off all the requests from a trip, even though capacity-wise that would be possible. 
Once a set of requests is defined as a feasible trip, this suggests that all constraints are satisfied. Moreover, 
within a trip, the sequence of the origins and destinations to be visited is locally optimal, meaning that the 
sequence with the lowest total travel delay is chosen. More specifically, this refers to the different 
permutations between the origins and destinations. For example, in a trip of capacity equal to four, there 
are 2.520 sequences of origin and destinations. Within these permutations the one with the lowest cost is 
chosen. However, this local optimality does not guarantee that the rest of the scheduling will result in global 
optimality (greedy approach).

3.2. Method 
3.2.1. Introduction to the system 
In the proposed method, we consider a system that consists of a central dispatcher, which holds the 
information regarding the positions of the vehicles and the received requests. Note that the positions of 
==PAGE:42==
CHAPTER 3: PROBLEM FORMULATION & MODELING 
25 
 
the vehicles are available to the system at any given moment. The system receives taxi requests over time 
and is updated every 3 minutes (collect requests in this interval). Once the system “opens” and is ready 
for the update, the received requests are assessed, and the routing and scheduling procedure begins. The 
moment that the system “opens” is denoted as 𝒕𝒔𝒚𝒔𝒕𝒆𝒎 , and even though the requests collected by that 
time were placed in different times, they are treated by the system as if they were received at time equal 
to 𝒕𝒔𝒚𝒔𝒕𝒆𝒎. The running (update) time for the system is approximately 1 minute, meaning that a solution 
needs to be found within this time (the implementation of the scheduling and routing algorithm). Note that 
the frequency that the update takes place, in this case every three (3) minutes, is flexible, meaning that it 
could be higher, less than every three (3) minutes, or lower, more than every three (3) minutes. After the 
running time is over, some of the received requests will be assigned to the available vehicles, while some 
other requests might be rejected, due to the constraints, and the use of a greedy algorithm, which is further 
explained in the following sections. Last, the schedule of each vehicle is updated in the system and the 
rejected requests are ignored completely. 
Even though we consider a dynamic system and requests occur in real-time, every time the system “opens” 
we are essentially looking at a snapshot of the current situation. Therefore, we can treat it as a static 
routing and scheduling problem. However, in the bigger picture, it is indeed a dynamic VRP, since the 
system updates every 3 minutes and the occurrence of requests is continuous, while dynamic information 
regarding the respective travel times is considered. 
For simplicity reasons, in the present study, we assume that at the moment we check the system, a number 
of requests has been received and that all vehicles are empty. Consequently, we need to create trips and 
assign the requests to the taxis, but no insertion feasibility approaches, for adding a request in a vehicle 
that is already traveling with a passenger, are considered. Furthermore, the assignment of requests to taxis 
takes place once, and is not a continuous procedure, as it would happen in a real application. Nevertheless, 
while building the method the aspects of a real-time application were considered, so that with a few 
modifications, the method will be appropriate for such a use. 
3.2.2. Road Segmentation – Grid 
In the endeavor to address the problem, a very obvious challenge arises. This is the need to calculate the 
shortest path, time-wise, between the current position of a taxi and the pick-up point of a new request. If 
the corresponding travel time is smaller than the predefined constraint of the waiting time (𝑾𝑪), then the 
==PAGE:43==
CHAPTER 3: PROBLEM FORMULATION & MODELING 
26 
 
taxi is considered as a prospect vehicle to serve the request. However, since we are studying a ride-sharing 
problem, a maximum value for detour time (𝑫𝑪), also needs to be defined. Assuming that the waiting time 
constraint is satisfied for all the selected requests, we also need to further calculate if that taxi is able to 
deliver the requests while satisfying the travel delay constraint, due to the ride-sharing. In a real-life service 
which includes hundreds of taxis and even more requests, this approach is very time-consuming because 
the shortest path computation is prohibitively expensive in real time for each vehicle (Ma et al., 2013), due 
to the large number of combinations, especially in the case of vehicles with high capacity. 
For the above reason, and with the incentive to contribute in the ridesharing problem from a real time 
perspective, we borrowed the idea of partitioning the road network from Ma et al. (2013). In their proposed 
method, Ma et al. (2013) segmented the road network into cells. The main advantage of this technique is 
that all the distances between every cell’s centroid in the grid is pre-computed. Since the generated 
requests and the location of the moving vehicles are essentially located in these centroids, we know in 
advance every shortest path at any moment. This reduces the required computational power while the 
system is operating, and consequently makes the system appropriate for real-time application. The idea of 
the grid has already been tested (Ma et al., 2013) and shows a good fit in our context without causing any 
prohibitive cost like other spatial indices. However, in the present study instead of partitioning the road 
network using a grid, we divided it into road segments, denoted as 𝑠𝑖, which we later treated as grid cells. 
Note that a road segment can be also referred to as 𝒑𝒍𝒂𝒄𝒆_𝒊𝒅. Each road segment has an anchor point, 
and conceptually, the whole road segment collapses on it. Whenever we mention a distance between two 
road segments, essentially, we refer to the distance between their anchors. 
Once the road segments are identified, both the geographical and the time distances from each segment 
to every other are calculated and saved in the memory. This way, we have created a database, which 
constitutes of the Geo-distance and Time-distance matrixes.

3.2.5. Routing Phases 
As acknowledged before, such a ride-sharing problem tends to be computationally expensive in order to 
find the best request combinations and routes to follow. Therefore, in order to facilitate the procedure and 
improve it, three (3) different routing phases are identified, as proposed by Alonso-Mora et al. (2017). 
These phases include the following: a) request pairing with virtual vehicle (RR), b) single request with real 
vehicle (RV), and c) trip-vehicle match (TV). The RR-phase examines which requests can be matched in 
pairs of 2, without considering the available vehicles, the RV-phase examines the matches between a single 
request and a vehicle, while the TV-phase combines the two pre-mentioned phases and examines the 
matches between trips and available vehicles, in order to identify the feasible ones. 
a. Request pairing with virtual vehicle (RR) 
The goal of the first phase (RR) is to exclude the pairs of requests that certainly aren’t feasible in the most 
optimistic scenario, so that they are not pointlessly checked again later. To do so, all possible pairs among 
the requests are made, and then they are assessed, regarding the satisfaction of the constraints set. 
However, this is a rather optimistic case, since real vehicles are not taken into account. Instead, it is 
assumed that a virtual vehicle is right at the starting point of the trip. For every pair of requests all possible 
==PAGE:48==
CHAPTER 3: PROBLEM FORMULATION & MODELING 
31 
 
permutations between origins and destinations are checked. Besides, every possible trip is subject to the 
𝑾𝑪 and 𝑫𝑪 constraints. If at least one trip can combine two requests, then these two requests are saved 
as a potential duo. However, if this is not the case, then it is obvious that the particular pair couldn’t be 
matched anyhow, since in reality it is more likely that a vehicle will not be located exactly at the origin of 
the trip. Algorithm 1 is used for this purpose, and function 𝒓𝒆𝒂𝒄𝒉 (Algorithm 2) is included for the 
identification of possible duos.  
This procedure constitutes an exhaustive search for pairs, since every request is tested with every other 
request, and is done offline. Note that even though we do not know the requests yet, we know all the 
possible origins and destinations, due to the partition heuristic. Nonetheless, 𝒂𝒄𝒄𝒆𝒑𝒕_𝒍𝒈𝒕 is already created 
for every road segment, therefore, the procedure is accelerated; the first condition is that at least one of 
the request origins is included in the 𝒂𝒄𝒄𝒆𝒑𝒕_𝒍𝒈𝒕 of the other request origin. This way, it is assessed if the 
two requests can be matched, while satisfying the 𝑾𝑪 constraint. For example, consider two requests with 
origins 𝒔𝟏, 𝒔𝟓. The origin 𝒔𝟓 is in 𝒂𝒄𝒄𝒆𝒑𝒕_𝒍𝒈𝒕[𝒌𝒆𝒚= 𝒔𝟐] so these two requests can be combined.  At this

therefore the duo is rejected.

b. Single request with real vehicle (RV) 
The second phase of the routing part is also a preliminary step in order to identify the feasible trips and 
the vehicles that can serve them. At this phase the aim is to determine which vehicles can serve a request, 
and this is assessed for every request included in the system.  
In order for a taxi to be able to serve a request, the origin of the request needs first to be reachable from 
the location of the taxi within the waiting time constraint (𝑾𝑪). Otherwise, it is indicated that the taxi won’t 
be able to pick-up the customer within the acceptable waiting time, and therefore this taxi won’t be 
considered for serving this request.  
At this point both the 𝒂𝒄𝒄𝒆𝒑𝒕_𝒍𝒈𝒕 list “heuristic” and the 𝒍𝒈𝒗 list are utilized for the development of 
𝑻𝒂𝒙𝒊_𝒅𝒊𝒄𝒕. To elaborate more, 𝑻𝒂𝒙𝒊_𝒅𝒊𝒄𝒕 is a dictionary, which includes all the requests, along with the 
vehicles that can serve them, while satisfying the 𝑾𝑪 constraint. In order to create 𝑻𝒂𝒙𝒊_𝒅𝒊𝒄𝒕, first, the 
road segments included in the 𝒂𝒄𝒄𝒆𝒑𝒕_𝒍𝒈𝒕 of all request origins are searched. The next step is, for each 
included road segment to enter the respective 𝒍𝒈𝒗 dictionary and seek for any available taxis. This way, 
we seek for taxis that can reach the origin of the request, without violating the waiting time constraint. The 
process of the development of RV-Graph is described in Algorithm 2, while an example of the 𝑻𝒂𝒙𝒊_𝒅𝒊𝒄𝒕 is

c. Trip-vehicle match (TV) 
To summarize, the information regarding the requests that can be paired (RR-Graph) along with the 
matches between single requests and vehicles (RV-Graph) are already in place. Following, the next step is 
to exploit this information, and create feasible trips with corresponding possible taxis to serve them. Each 
trip should include at least one request, while the number of people per trip should not exceed the capacity 
of the vehicle. Additionally, at least one vehicle must be able to serve a trip, in order for this trip to be 
regarded as a feasible one. At the same time, the time constraints need to be satisfied. In order to identify 
the feasible trips, function 𝒕𝒓𝒂𝒗𝒆𝒍 is used. 
Starting from trips of size one, suggesting that one request is served, the size of the trip scales up until the 
maximum capacity of the vehicle is reached. This way all the possible trips for each vehicle are created. 
The present study is considering common taxis, therefore the capacity of each vehicle is equal to 4. All the 
possible trips for each vehicle are saved in the 𝒄𝒂𝒑_𝒕𝒓𝒊𝒑𝒔 dictionary, categorized by capacity (number of 
passengers that they can carry). In total, there are four (4) trip sizes/categories per vehicle.  
Trips of size 1 
Regarding the trips of size one, this procedure is quite straight forward, since the RV-Graph provides all 
the required information, regarding which requests can each vehicle serve. If an edge exists between a 
vehicle and a request, then this request is regarded as a feasible trip for the respective vehicle. By the end 
of this step, all the single trips for each vehicle are identified and saved in the 𝒄𝒂𝒑_𝒕𝒓𝒊𝒑𝒔 dictionary. Table

Trips of size >1 
Regarding trips of capacity greater than one request, the proposed method includes the following steps for 
all vehicles: 
1. Create all possible requests combination, in each trip capacity (from capacity=2 until capacity=4) 
2. Identify the feasible combinations  
3. For the feasible combinations: create all possible permutations among origins and destinations 
4. For each feasible combination: identify the permutation/trip with the minimum cost  
5. Update 𝒄𝒂𝒑_𝒕𝒓𝒊𝒑𝒔 dictionary using function 𝒕𝒓𝒂𝒗𝒆𝒍. 
In step 1, all the possible request combinations, of the relevant trip size, are created. So, if the trip size is 
equal to 2, then these combinations would be pairs of requests, whereas if the size of the trip is equal to 
3, the combinations would include 3 requests each, and so on. Following, in step 2 the aim is to identify 
which of the request combinations created before are feasible ones. To achieve this, each combination is 
studied separately. More specifically, for a given combination we examine the requests included, in pairs

of two. If for all the pairs there are edges in the RR-Graph, implying possible connection, then it is confirmed 
that the respective combination is a feasible one, and only such combinations will be regarded for the 
following steps. Next, in step 3 the idea is, for any given feasible combination, to create all possible 
permutations of origins and destinations, of the included requests. These permutations essentially 
constitute a path to follow for serving the included requests. For example, if one feasible combination is 
(r1,r3), then the respective permutations/paths would be: (r1, r1’, r3, r3’), (r1, r3, r1’, r3’), (r3, r1, r3’, r1’), 
(r3, r3’, r1, r1’), (r1, r3, r3’, r1’), (r3, r1, r1’, r3’), where r denotes the origin of a request, and r’ denotes 
the destination. Notice, that within the algorithm, it is ensured that first the origins are visited and then the 
destinations, for each request. By the end of step 3, all the feasible combinations of requests (for a given 
trip size) are identified and all possible paths have been created. Afterwards, we need to determine which 
path to follow, for any feasible combination. To do this, in step 4, the costs, in terms of time, of all the 
identified paths is examined (complete enumeration of possible paths), and the path with the lowest cost 
is selected. The objective at this point is to choose the trip that minimizes the cost function. The relevant

3.2.6. Scheduling: Assignment of Trips to Taxis 
Finally, once all the possible trips for each vehicle are identified then the scheduling procedure begins. 
During this procedure the aim is to assign trips to vehicles, always bearing in mind that a request can only 
be assigned to one vehicle. Consequently, a trip should be assigned to one vehicle and that one vehicle 
must serve only one trip. 
As mentioned before, within the context of this work, the aim is to utilize the vehicles’ capacity at maximum, 
so as to reduce the number of rides. This would indicate lower costs for both the customers and the taxi 
drivers, while it could be environmentally sustainable as well. For this reason, in the scheduling part, priority 
is given to the trips of maximum size, meaning that the examination would begin with trips of maximum 
size (k) and degrade until trips of size one (1) are examined. So, the first objective is to maximize the size 
of the developed trips, while the second one is to minimize the cost of those trips, as elaborated on step 4 
of the Trip-vehicle match (TV) section, for trips of size more than one (Trips of size>1). 
Due to lack of resources, in terms of optimization software, and since the purpose of this study is not to 
optimize the routing/scheduling procedure, but rather to compare different speed scenarios, a greedy 
algorithm was deployed for the scheduling phase. The greedy algorithm in each iteration, examines trips 
sequence (origin-destinations) of same capacity, for all vehicles, and assigns the trip sequence with the 
minimum cost to the respective vehicle. Once this is done, the respective vehicle will no longer be 
considered as a potential vehicle for any other trips. Furthermore, once a request is assigned, then all trips 
that include this request are no longer considered as potential trips. The greedy algorithm used is described 
in Algorithm 4.

4. Machine Learning Schemes 
The ability to predict traffic variables such as speed, travel time or flow, based on real time and historic 
data is crucial for the Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS). ITS is gaining more popularity as its level 
of deployment increases and the reasons for that have been discussed in the chapter 2.2. The information 
which is integrated into the ITS can be classified into three distinct groups: historical, real-time and 
predictive. Historical information is based on aggregated data from the past while real time information is 
based on the current traffic values in the system. For pre-trip planning and decisions taken en-route, 
predicted information would be more useful than historic or real-time information (Vanajakshi & Rilett, 
2004). For this reason, short-term forecasting, usually referring to less or equal to one hour ahead (Li & 
Shahabi, 2016), of speed and travel time is of great importance.   
A preliminary step, before the short-term forecasting of the speed, is the pre-processing of the data which 
includes steps like the Explanatory Data Analysis (EDA), data cleaning, imputation of missing values, data 
transformation, etc. This part is necessary before the implementation of any machine learning model and 
is also the most time consuming one. For this reason, it is discussed extensively in the following sections. 
4.1. Introduction to traffic Time-series 
Time series prediction involves modeling a variable as a function of its past observations and an error term. 
Typically, each observation is depended on its own past observations. The problem of short-term 
forecasting of traffic variables, such as speed, is very challenging because of the complex spatial and 
temporal dependencies (Li & Shahabi, 2016). The advantage of traffic time series is that they demonstrate 
strong temporal dynamics that could model these dependencies (Li, Yu, Shahabi, & Liu, 2018). 
Nevertheless, traffic time series are characterized by their non-stationary nature which is inherent to the 
non-stationary traffic processes. This non-stationarity can be ascribed to recurring incidents, such as rush 
hours, weather patterns and accidents, rendering forecast even more challenging. Non-stationary traffic

time-series could undermine the accuracy of the forecasting model and for this reason it is necessary to 
model seasonality as well. A detailed approach on this challenge is discussed in the following pages. 
4.2. Description of the data 
The routing algorithms and the scheduling decisions in this project are based on two different types of data 
(Google, OpenStreetMaps). Depending on the data processing, the routing has been categorized into the 
following three (3) scenarios: 1) taxi ride-sharing based on static travel times, provided by the 
OpenStreetMap API, 2) taxi ride-sharing based on offline travel times (Offline ML), based on historical 
median values of speed and 3) taxi ride-sharing based on online travel times (Online ML), based on time 
depended speed predictions. The evaluation of these three main scenarios, in a Copenhagen region, is the 
main interest of this project and will be explained in further detail in Chapter 6. 
Data from OpenStreetMap  
Following the idea of space partitioning, explained in Chapter 3, there is a need to pre-compute the 
distances and travel times between predefined road segments in a Copenhagen region. More specifically, 
538 pre-defined road segments (Figure 4-2) are considered as possible origins and destinations of any 
future request and taxi locations. The data that serves this initial goal was collected from OpenStreetMap 
and consists of information regarding the road segment IDs and their pairwise distance and travel time. 
The distance between any two road segment IDs and the necessary travel time has been provided by the 
platform’s API, in respect to the shortest path time-wise. This shortest travel time reflects many different 
parameters, like speed limits, number of turns, traffic lights etc. For this reason, this project considers these 
assumptions sufficient and does not intend to examine any further algorithms to calculate the shortest 
paths between the road segments. These travel time data are static, since they are not considering the 
time of the day or time of the week and are used in the first taxi ride-sharing scenario. 
As discussed above, the OpenStreetMap provided the shortest path travel times and the respected distances 
between 538 pre-defined road segments in Copenhagen. For those 538 road segments there are 538 X 
538  possible combinations to be explored in order to account every single scenario of origin and 
destination. In Figure 4-1, there is a rough depiction of these data in a computational environment.

4.4. Handling missing data – Speed imputation 
EDA indicated that the quality of available speed data is sufficient for further analysis. However, there are 
still 1.1% of missing values and 1.8% outliers that need to be handled. From now on the missing and the 
outliers will be treated as missing values of speed. 
One possible solution would be to just drop the observations containing these missing values. However, 
this approach could result, in many cases, in a huge loss of data. In this case, even though the quantity of 
data lost could be acceptable, the nature of information lost is not allowing such an approach. It is so, 
because the data in time series is also depended on past observations and the loss of those can be 
detrimental for the success of any forecasting model, especially when the missing observation rate is high, 
as it is common with crowdsourced data (Rodrigues & Pereira, 2018). 
An alternative approach would be  to impute the missing speed data. The process of speed imputation can 
vary from very simple strategies, such as taking the median or most frequent value of a data neighborhood, 
to more advanced techniques, which include the use of machine learning models to infer these missing 
values through prediction. In this project linear interpolation was the strategy based on which the missing 
data had been imputed. 
4.5. Applying Stationarity in Time-series 
The ability to remain constant over time is expressed as stationarity. There are three basic requirements 
for classical time series models in order to be classified as stationary series. First, the mean of the series 
should not be a function of time and, second the variance and covariance of the 𝑖𝑡ℎ and the (𝑖+ 𝑚)𝑡ℎ term 
should be constant as well (M. G. Karlaftis & Vlahogianni, 2011).The existence of non-stationary variables 
can cause serious implications, such as falsely significant parameter estimates (Washington et al., 2011).  
In most transportation time series, the stationary criterion is violated, due to trends and seasonality. In this 
case, the first requisite becomes to test the time series against stationarity, and if non-stationary features 
exist, to turn the times series into stationary (Lavrenz, Vlahogianni, Gkritza, & Ke, 2018). Techniques, such 
as filtering, detrending, differencing or fractional differencing (M. G. Karlaftis & Vlahogianni, 2011; Matthew 
G. Karlaftis & Vlahogianni, 2009) should be applied prior to modelling. The step of stationarity is critical, 
==PAGE:67==
CHAPTER 4: MACHINE LEARNING SCHEMES 
50 
 
since existing machine learning models impose strong stationarity assumptions on the data (Li & Shahabi, 
2018).

Correlation of error terms 
An important assumption of the linear regression model is that the error terms, 𝜀1, 𝜀2, … , 𝜀𝑛, are 
uncorrelated. Time series are prone to such correlations, which consists of observations for which, 
measurements are obtained at discrete points in time. In many cases, observations that are obtained at 
adjacent time points will have positively correlated errors. To determine if this is the case for a given time 
series, the residuals can be plotted as a function of time. If the errors are uncorrelated, then there should 
be no discernible pattern. In contrast, if the error terms are positively correlated, then we may see tracking 
in the residuals. That is, adjacent residuals may have similar values. In Figure 4-16, no obvious pattern 
was identified, hence the assumption of uncorrelated terms for this time series is valid.

4. Bayesian Linear Regression 
In the Bayesian viewpoint, the linear regression is formulated using probability distributions rather than 
point estimates. The response, 𝑦, is not estimated as a single value, but is assumed to be drawn from a 
probability distribution. The model for Bayesian Linear Regression with the response sampled from a normal 
distribution is:  
𝑦 ~ 𝑁(𝛽𝛵𝛸, 𝜎2𝛪). 
The aim of the Bayesian Linear Regression is not to find the single “best” value of the model parameters, 
but rather to determine the posterior distribution for the model parameters. Not only is the response 
generated from a probability distribution, but the model parameters are assumed to be generated from a 
distribution as well. The posterior probability of the model parameters is conditional upon the training inputs 
and outputs:  
𝑃(𝛽|𝑦, 𝑋) = 𝑃(𝛽|𝑦, 𝑋) ∗ 𝑃(𝛽|𝑋)

Here, 𝑃(𝛽|𝑦, 𝑋) is the prosterior probability distribution of the model parameters, given the inputs and 
outputs. This is equal to the likelihood of the data 𝑃(𝛽|𝑦, 𝑋), multiplied by the prior probability of the 
parameters and divided by a normalization constant. This is a simple expression of Bayes Theorem: 
𝑃𝑜𝑠𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑖𝑜𝑟= 𝐿𝑖𝑘𝑒𝑙𝑖ℎ𝑜𝑜𝑑∗𝑃𝑟𝑖𝑜𝑟
𝑁𝑜𝑟𝑚𝑎𝑙𝑖𝑧𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 
In contrast to least squares in linear regression, we have a posterior distribution for the model parameters 
that is proportional to the likelihood of the data multiplied by the prior probability of the parameters. The 
advantage of the posterior prediction is that instead of getting a single estimate of speed, which would 
reflect a distinct travel time at a given time of day, now this speed prediction can be formulated in 
confidence intervals. Based on these confidence intervals, different predictions of speed, at a distinct 
timestamp, can be tested to calculate the travel times in the taxi ride-sharing problem. 
 
Figure 4-20: Speed confidence intervals (CI) 
Within this approach, different strategies could be applied in the taxi sharing context, in order to address 
the uncertainty that is inherent in the transportation problems. For example, in the case that we would like 
to approach a more risk averse scenario, where we consider pessimistic speed predictions (e.g. Bayes μ– 
1σ or μ-2σ), the travel times would eventually increase and thus, requests that would be initially served, 
based on the Bayes mean prediction, would be rejected. A slightly pessimistic strategy (prediction: μ-1σ) 
==PAGE:85==

could alleviate the risk of assigning vehicles to requests which are likely not to be fulfilled on time. A very 
optimistic strategy (prediction: μ+2σ) would result to false scheduling, since we assume high speeds, thus 
lower than the actual travel times. The possibility of testing different strategies, based on the Bayesian 
predictions is explored further in the next section and will be implemented in the context of a real case 
scenario, in Chapter 6.

5. Scenarios  
Chapter 5 is an introductory chapter, which is dedicated in explaining the different scenarios that will be 
implemented in the Copenhagen case study, in Chapter 6. The aim of this chapter is to explain the 
characteristics of each scenario, the strategies used and the limitations of them.  
The scenarios are created with regard to the travel times, suggesting that each one constitutes another 
strategy for getting the relevant travel times. Afterwards, these travel times are used in the proposed 
method of the routing/scheduling part (Chapter 4). In total 3 different scenarios were examined. These 
are: 1) OpenStreetMap travel times, 2) Offline Machine Learning, and 3) Online Machine Learning. Apart 
from the first scenario, the rest make use of the machine learning schemes described in Chapter 4. The 
reason behind this selection of scenarios was based on the limitation of many studies to use constant 
speeds and consequently static travel times for routing problems. On the other hand, literature is also 
suggesting the implementation of advanced methods to capture the dynamism of traffic variables, such as 
speeds. 
5.1. OpenStreetMap Travel Times 
In the first scenario, which is the simplest one, the data used was collected from OpenStreetMap. This data 
included information about the road segments/place IDs of a specific area in Copenhagen and their pairwise 
geographical and time distance. OpenStreetMap, provides the travel time between any two road segments, 
based on the shortest path, time-wise. Then, based on the path that was followed we can extract 
information regarding the geographical distance as well. Such information will be useful later, when the ML 
implementation takes place. However, the corresponding travel times provided by OpenStreetMap are not 
affected by the day or the time of the day. Moreover, even though parameters like speed limits, number 
of turns and traffic lights were considered, still this scenario is not able to grasp the dynamism of this taxi 
ride-sharing problem. Therefore, the provided travel times are regarded as static ones and this scenario is 
considered as the most optimistic one, since other parameters like traffic congestion in rush hours, road 
works etc. are not taken into consideration. This suggests that the travel times in this case will most 
==PAGE:89==
CHAPTER 5: SCENARIOS 
72 
 
probably be shorter than in the other scenarios, were more factors are considered and more accurate 
estimations regarding the stochastic travel times are made. 
 
Figure 5-1: Speed values provided by OSM: Nørre Allé 59 
Figure 5-1 displays the speed limit (60 km/h) in Nørre Allé, as provided by the OSM API. The speed value 
in this scenario is subject to the assumption that the speeds in the road segments remain constant without 
being affected by any disruptions in the system. This means that the traffic conditions are not changing 
throughout the day and thus, the daily patterns cannot be reflected in the values of speed. However, 
constant speeds will result in static travel times. In essence, the calculation of the time needed to traverse 
from one geographical point to another will always be the same. It should also be mentioned that the 
calculation of the travel time, based on the speed limit of 60 km/h, will be relatively low, since 60 km/h is 
very optimistic. There is no doubt that this assumption is way far from reality, since the trajectory variables, 
such as speed, are changing dynamically and have a tendency to follow patterns, due to people’s behavior. 
In such a case, the time needed to travel from one point to another will depend on values of speed at any 
given time.

In the best of our knowledge, based on the literature studied, all of the routing studies are using static 
values of speed (e.g. speed limits in road segments), since their focus mainly is to develop and innovate in 
the routing and scheduling part. In this project, we also aim to highlight the importance of a more complete 
approach that is addressing the different traffic states of a transportation system, with the use of machine 
learning. 
5.2. Offline Machine Learning 
Once again, the aim is to provide travel times that can be used in the routing/scheduling part in order to 
solve this taxi ride-sharing problem. The source of the data used in this scenario is Google, which made 
historical traffic statistics available. The data was extracted from a period of 6 months in year 2015. More 
specifically, Google provided information such as the traffic speeds and flows for a segmented road network 
in Copenhagen, where each road segment had a corresponding time-series of speed. In each time-series, 
speeds are provided in 5-minute bins.  
In this scenario, the median speed for each road segment, for each time of the day was created. So, by 
the end of this procedure we were able to identify a characteristic speed for each road segment, for any 
time within the “average” day in 2015. However, these speeds are not accurate day-wise, but only day-
time-wise, suggesting that for a given road segment, for a specific moment at any day, the value of the 
speed will be the same. Based on these speeds and on the distances provided by OpenStreetMap we were 
then able to calculate the travel time required in order to commute from one road segment to any other in 
the given road network. Afterwards, these travel times are supplied to the routing/scheduling algorithm for 
obtaining the taxi ride-sharing results.

5.3. Online Machine Learning 
The last scenario, namely the Online Machine Learning, is the most advanced of the ones studied in this 
work. In fact, it constitutes an improved version of the Offline Machine Learning, where more parameters 
are considered, so as to increase the accuracy of the results. In this case, like in the previous one, the data 
provide by Google is used and the aim here is to apply short-term forecasting of the relevant travel times. 
However, this scenario, is not limited to consider only the time of the day, for a given road segment. In 
contrast to Offline Machine Learning, which provides speeds only based on the time of an “average” day, 
Online Machine Learning also examines the day of interest. Additionally, apart from these two parameters, 
this scenario also takes into account the traffic condition, in terms of speed values, of the past twenty (20) 
minutes prior to the time of the prediction (lags). This way, not only the time and day are considered, but 
also the specific characteristics of each day. In other words, we can make date and time dependent 
predictions. 
Taking all this into consideration, different ML models were used in order to identify the best performing 
one, in terms of short-term forecasting of speeds, and consequently of travel times. Bayesian Linear 
Regression was proven to fit better the challenges of this transportation problem, and therefore this one is 
used in this scenario. This model is providing speed predictions in the form of a probability distribution. So, 
a strategy had to be developed in order to extract a specific set of speeds that could approach the ride-
sharing problem more effectively. In total, five (5) strategies were examined and the most suitable was 
finally used to make the speed predictions. Further information will follow in Chapter 6.

6.2. Criteria 
To benchmark the different cases against each other and examine which one was more accurate, in terms 
of having results closer to the true scenario, different criteria had to be considered. This decision is very 
important in order to evaluate the performance of the models objectively. The inherent difficulty in selecting 
effective metrics is that the scheduling part was based on a greedy algorithm and thus, metrics that reflect 
the “global” performance of the model are biased towards the locally optimal solutions. For this reason, 
criteria that are associated with the customer’s satisfaction, such as the individual delay or waiting time 
that each customer is experiencing, cannot provide strong evidence of good performance. However, since 
these are the criteria based on which the customers will judge the value of the system, it was decided to 
consider them as criteria which affect the satisfaction of the customers. Nevertheless, since these criteria 
are also directly affected by the greedy solutions, it was reasonable to assign lower weights in the decision 
model.    
==PAGE:105==
CHAPTER 6: COPENHAGEN CASE STUDY 
88 
 
Table 6-3 presents the criteria that were initially considered, while the ones marked with green were finally 
applied. 
Table 6-3: Criteria Considered 
 
The individual waiting time and delay for each particular request were considered as possible metrics in 
the beginning, however in many scenarios it was noticed that the requests served in each of them can vary 
due to the greedy assignment. Some requests were simply rejected, in the examined scenarios, not because 
of constrain violation, but because of a locally optimal solution. In such a case, there is lack of optimal 
scheduling consistency and the individual satisfaction of the customers does not indicate any kind of 
accuracy of the model.  
Another approach was to address the global waiting time and travel delay, in terms of total waiting time 
and total travel delay per scenario. However, this alternative was also rejected. In the case where more 
requests are getting rejected (pessimistic scenario: μ-2σ), the total delay is decreasing since less requests 
can participate in ride-sharing. In a similar context, small waiting times might occur due to very few 
requests being served, so even if they are close to the true waiting times, they wouldn’t indicate a good 
accuracy of the model. 
==PAGE:106==
CHAPTER 6: COPENHAGEN CASE STUDY 
89 
 
However, both the waiting time and the travel delay are parameters that can greatly affect customer’s 
satisfaction and since customer’s satisfaction is a key component in people transportation services, some 
criteria associated with it needed to be developed. For this reason, it was decided that the waiting time per 
assigned requests and the travel delay per assigned request are used as criteria. Still, these metrics tend 
to be biased because of the greedy algorithm, however this is mitigated by taking the average waiting time 
and travel delay. The difference between the individual delay and the waiting time per assigned requests 
is that in the first case the delay for each request ID is considered, while in the second we average the 
total delay to the number of requests served.  
Accuracy in rejected requests was another criterion that was considered in the beginning but was rejected 
later. This criterion refers to the ability of the scenarios to capture accurately the requests that were 
rejected in the true case. Once again, the greedy algorithm directly affects which requests are chosen to 
be served and which to be rejected. The rejected requests could simply vary in each scenario due to this 
approach and could not be trusted. For this reason, simply the number of the rejected requests was 
considered as a more objective metric. 
Total travel time has also been considered as a possible metric to compare the similarities between the 
scenarios and the true case, but this idea was abandoned at a quite early stage, and thus is not included 
in Table 6-3. In the beginning, it might seem as if this indicator is related to the customer’s satisfaction, 
however, it was noticed that this was not correct. This is because, travel time is the absolute essential time 
for a passenger to traverse towards his destination. A passenger is usually aware of the time needed to 
physically commute, having in mind also the traffic parameters, and he will not be dissatisfied if he knows 
that in order to reach the destination it takes minimum an 𝑥 amount of time. Since the scope of this project 
is the ride-sharing, the factors affecting the actual satisfaction would be the travel delay and waiting time.  
On the other hand, the metrics such as the number of edges RR and edges RV are solely dependent on 
the speeds predicted in each scenario and on the routing algorithm. Since the calculation of the edges in 
these two graphs are a preliminary step, prior to the implementation of the greedy algorithm, they are not 
affected by it. The edges are formed when the travel times, calculated based on the predicted speeds, are 
satisfying the time constraints. In this case these indicators are objective and are reflecting the state of the 
system based on the speeds in each scenario. The calculation of these metrics in the true case can be used 
as a baseline to compare with the rest of the scenarios. For example, the number of RV edges (number of 
connections between vehicles and requests) in the true case, it is expected to decrease in a pessimistic 
==PAGE:107==
CHAPTER 6: COPENHAGEN CASE STUDY 
90 
 
scenario where the speeds are lower and thus, the increase in travel times will probably violate the time 
constraints. This will eventually result in fewer RV connections since the vehicles were not able to serve 
the individual requests on time. 
In a similar context, the pairwise connection of requests (edges RR) could increase in an optimistic scenario, 
where the speeds are higher, and, in this case, more pairs of requests could be served. These two metrics 
are very important since they calibrate the performance in each scenario. If during this preliminary process, 
a higher number of vehicle-request edges (RV) is computed for a specific scenario, then there lies the risk 
of a taxi being assigned to a request which, based on the true case, it would not be able to serve it. This 
means that in the examined scenario, even if the scheduling is done properly, when a taxi is assigned to 
an individual request, in real conditions, it might exceed the waiting time and cause dissatisfaction to the 
customer. Similarly, if more pairwise requests have been identified (edges RR), then there is the risk of 
combining requests in trips that could not be served in a real case. This is because, the pairwise connection 
of requests (edges RR) is used to evaluate the possibility of combining different requests in trips of higher 
capacity (max 4 passengers).  
The above metrics observe the general behavior of the system in each scenario. Every change in the 
predicted values of speed is directly reflected in these indicators and for this reason it was decided that 
they are good measures of differentiation between the examined scenarios and the true case. Finally, the 
percentage of ride-sharing in each scenario was considered as another metric of differentiation to the true 
case. Since, ride-sharing is the scope of this project it was decided to include a metric to address this 
context. The greedy algorithm affects which individual request are being combined in trips, hence, it 
wouldn’t be reliable to evaluate the requests combined in each trip capacity individually. However, a 
percentage of ride-sharing would be more appropriate. This percentage is calculated based on the number 
of requests that are included in the trips of different sizes, excluding the trips with single requests (case 
with no ride-sharing). The greedy algorithm doesn’t have a great effect on the percentage of ride-sharing, 
since it always prioritizes the scheduling of higher capacity trips. For example, it may choose a different 
combination of requests in capacity of four, in the different scenarios, however the final number of trips in 
this capacity group will be the result of the predicted speeds and will reflect more objectively the global 
state of the system.

6.3.5. Summary of results for the true scenario  
Within the context of this work, the aim of implementing the concept of ride-sharing in taxi services is to 
reduce the number of rides and the taxis used, by utilizing more efficiently the capacity of each vehicle. 
Indeed, according to the results of the true scenario, by adopting this taxi-sharing approach the number 
of vehicles used was on average reduced by 53%. More specifically, during rush hours this reduction 
corresponds to 48,5%, while for non-rush hours it is estimated to be 57%. For instance, at 05:55, three 
(3) vehicles in total were scheduled to serve seven (7) requests, while if no taxi-sharing was in place, the 
number of taxis required to serve these requests would be larger, in specific seven (7) vehicles. Regarding 
rush hours, taking as an example the time 08:40, which is the morning rush hour, the use of taxi-sharing 
resulted in three (3) vehicles serving five (5) requests, while in a non-taxi-sharing situation five (5) vehicles 
would be required. This suggests a reduction of 40% in the number of vehicles used. 
At this point, it is important to mention that the waiting times, in the case of taxi ride-sharing, presented 
an acceptable increase, compared to the waiting times that would occur in case of no taxi-sharing. More 
specifically, the waiting time for customers was increased on average by 25.5%. Considering that when no 
taxi-sharing takes place, the average waiting time for a customer would be 2.5 minutes in no-rush hours, 
and 3 minutes during rush hours, this increase corresponds to a maximum of one (1) additional minute in 
waiting time per request. The maximum increase in waiting time was observed at 8:40, which is regarded 
as the morning rush hour. For that time, the taxi-sharing results suggest a 4 minutes waiting time per 
requests, while if the requests were individually served, the waiting time would be equal to 3 minutes, 
instead. In this case, the respective increase was 33%. In general, a raise of 28% in waiting times was 
observed during no-rush hours, while this percentage drops to 23% for rush hours. This can be explained 
by the fact that during rush hours the traffic congestion is intense and anyway a taxi needs more time to 
reach a customer. So, in case more customers are found on the way, and consequently are picked up, the 
average waiting time per requests would drop. On the contrary, during non-busy hours, typically, the time 
required for a taxi to reach a customer would be less. This suggests that making stops in order to pick-up 
or drop-off more requests would affect the waiting time, at a higher level, compared to the case of busy 
hours. This could possibly be an explanation why the waiting time is increased less during rush hours. 
Finally, the parameter of travel delay is added to the taxi-sharing concept. In a non-taxi sharing concept 
this parameter wouldn’t exist, however, in the case where taxis are shared, travel delay refers to the 
additional time that it takes for a request to be fulfilled, due to ride-sharing. On average, the travel delay 
==PAGE:120==
CHAPTER 6: COPENHAGEN CASE STUDY 
103 
 
was equal to 1.5 minutes. The maximum value of total delay, for the true scenario, is noticed at 8:40, 
during the morning rush hour. The travel delay at that time is equal to 2.2 minutes. The lowest travel delay 
per request is noticed at 22:00 and is equal to 0.7 minutes. In general, during rush hours the travel delay 
is estimated around 2 minutes per request, while during less busy hours the travel delay is estimated to be 
less than one minute, in particular 0.9 minutes. 
 Taking all these into consideration, we can conclude that taxi-sharing in fact is about a trade-off, in terms 
of minimizing the taxis used, but having additional times in regard to waiting and travel delay. However, 
the results for the true case suggests that this trade off works for the benefit of the ride-sharing concept, 
since the system operated efficiently, while the relevant times were slightly increased per request. In 
essence, this would be considered as a win-win situation for both customers, who would share the ride and 
therefore the fare would be lower, and for the system, symbolizing a taxi company, which would need less 
resources in order to cover customers demand. 
6.4. Evaluation of Scenarios and Results 
In this section all the different scenarios will be compared to the true case, on four selected times of the 
day: 05:55, 08:40, 16:40 and 22:00. More specifically, the values of the criteria in each scenario have been 
converted into a percentage of differentiation to the true case and then the WSM has been applied. In 
addition, the criteria Edges RR and Edges RV have been penalized in the case their number exceeds the 
value of the same criteria in the true case. Then a new value of WSM for each scenario has been calculated 
based on the penalty applied in these two criteria.  
To elaborate more, in scenarios where the values of Edges RR and Edges RV are higher than the ones in 
the true case, then these scenarios will receive a higher value in the new WSM, and thus their performance 
will deteriorate, in terms of increased differentiation to the true case. When the WSM value remains 
constant after the penalization, this means that in this scenario, the edges of the two criteria were not 
higher than the true case. In general, when the number of these edges is lower, but closer to the true 
values, this would be considered as a good indicator for a given scenario. In case, the number of the edges 
is too low compared to the true, the differentiation will be high enough because of the higher weights, but 
no penalty is assigned, since in such a case the satisfaction of the existing customers will not be 
==PAGE:121==
CHAPTER 6: COPENHAGEN CASE STUDY 
104 
 
compromised. In essence, the taxi company will probably lose some customers, because of the assumption 
of fewer RV connections, but at least the current customers will be served on time and without any risk.

especially in the case of the Edges RR and RV. Only, at 22:00 the results of this strategy are considered

results.

observations of speed (lags). This approach provides additional information and considers the daily 
seasonality in each day of the week, the time of the day and 20-minutes of past speed observations (5

percentage of differentiation. For example, at 22:00 the differentiation to the true case was only 9% both 
before and after the penalization. In this time scenario, this strategy didn’t overestimate the RR or RV 
edges and didn’t receive any penalty. However, in all the other time scenarios, Bayes (μ) is constantly 
overestimating the system’s connectivity and receives a high penalty resulting in a higher percentage of 
differentiation. More specifically, at 08:40 its initial differentiation (33%) increased to 66% which reflects 
the very high risk of failing to serve the assigned customers. This day time is considered critical since it 
reflects the morning rush hour, where the system is more sensitive to speed changes. In such a case, if 
the true system’s connectivity is overestimated it may result in a huge number of dissatisfied customers 
and this failure could not be compensated by an improved performance of this strategy in other time 
scenarios.

Finally, the Bayes (μ-1σ) strategy is another alternative with consistently presented low percentage of

and 5% differentiation respectively. This indicates that this strategy managed to capture very well the 
sensitivity during the morning rush hour, without compromising the satisfaction of the assigned requests. 
In the next time scenarios, 16:40 and 22:00, the differentiation increased basically because of the criteria: 
number of rejected requests and ride share, and indicated Bayes (μ) as a better approach. However, these 
two criteria might be subject, at some extent, to the greedy algorithm and could not be remarkably reliable. 
In general, Bayes (μ-1σ) estimated the system’s connectivity (RR and RV edges) very well, without taking

within an acceptable percentage of differentiation. It was decided that a more pessimistic strategy could 
address the inherent uncertainties of a transportation system more effectively, without compromising the 
satisfaction of the customers. It is obvious that if this approach is implemented in a company’s application, 
the taxi company might lose some customers during the scheduling, since it will assume that they cannot 
be served. However, it will mitigate the risk of assigning requests to taxis that will, eventually, not be 
fulfilled on the given time constrains.  
Based on the analysis and on the results of the different strategies compared to the true case, it is evident 
that the morning rush hour has the highest impact on the traffic speeds. In order to address the sensitivity 
of the system during the morning rush hour a more conservative approach (pessimistic strategy), such as 
Bayes (μ-1σ) could provide better results. On the other hand, OSM is assuming that the speeds at any 
given time is constant at 60 km/h (extremely optimistic), while the true speeds at 08:40 reached values as 
low as 17 km/h. This false assumption is reflected in the very low performance of OSM at this period. 
During the afternoon and evening, Bayes (μ) is performing better than the rest of the strategies, since 
there is normal traffic. Finally, at 5am, the quality of the crowdsourced data is questionable and very noisy, 
hence any strong conclusions might be questionable. 
For this reason, Bayes (μ-1σ) is considered to be the strategy that serves better the taxi ride-sharing

7. Conclusions 
7.1. Theoretical Conclusions 
Nowadays, the insufficiency of public transportation means, in terms of convenience and fast transition, 
has led to a raise in private transportation popularity (Samaranayake et al., 2017; Alonso-Mora 2016; 
Taniguchi, 2014). Citizens opt for ease and convenience in their everyday transportation and this has 
resulted in high use of mobility-on-demand (MoD) systems, including taxis, as well (Alonso-Mora et al., 
2017; Sampaio, Savelsbergh, Veelenturf, & Woensel, 2017; Gao, Jiang, & Xu, 2018). However, concerns 
have been raised regarding the increasing use of private transportation. These concerns are related to the 
degrade of living quality in urban areas, in terms of intense traffic congestion, environmental pollution and 
excess energy consumption.  
Taxi ride-sharing, similar to ride-sharing, has often been proposed as an alternative option in order to 
mitigate the negative effects of taxis in the urban environment. More and more researchers focus their 
work on that field, arguing that such an innovative solution could completely transform the traditional taxi 
services (Alonso-Mora et al. 2017; Sopha et al. 2016; Maciejewski, Bischoff, Nagel, & Berlin, 2016; Ma et 
al. 2013). Notwithstanding that, customer satisfaction is still a key parameter to consider when dealing with 
taxi ride-sharing systems. In transportation services, like taxis, customer satisfaction is closely related to 
the time it takes for a customer request to be fulfilled. Moreover, in the case of taxi ride-sharing that time 
will be increased, due to ride-sharing. Therefore, the need to accurately predict travel times and schedule 
the taxi dispatch system properly, so that customers are provided what they are promised, is apparent. 
The present study is focusing on short-term forecasting of traffic speeds within a taxi-ridesharing system, 
while a routing and scheduling algorithm were also developed in order to apply the forecasting results in a 
real taxi ride-sharing case study. Different machine learning models were examined, in order to compare 
the results and identify the model that outperformed the rest, in terms of providing credible results.

7.2. Research Conclusions 
The central purpose of this project was to conduct an empirical research, so as to examine how advanced 
forecasting methods, like Machine Learning techniques, can be deployed in order to improve the routing 
and scheduling procedure in DVRPs with uncertainty, and more specifically in dynamic taxi ride-sharing 
problems with stochastic travel times. In order to serve this purpose, a framework for addressing ride-
sharing problems was developed. This framework integrates a routing and a scheduling algorithm, both of 
which can scale up to an increased number of requests and vehicles in the system. Additionally, various 
ML models were tested in order to determine which one is performing better, in terms of providing 
predictions similar to reality. 
The proposed method was applied in a real case study, in Copenhagen city. In total 3 different scenarios, 
namely, OpenStreetMap travel times, Offline ML travel times and online ML travel times were examined, 
where the last one incorporated five (5) different strategies. So, in total 7 different cases were studied, in 
the context of the case study. The results of each case were later compared to the results obtained for the 
true travel time values, in order to determine which case was more accurate. 
As expected, the Online Machine Learning scenario, based on Bayesian Linear Regression, outperformed 
the rest in short-term forecasting. The online ML scenario considers the day of the week and the time of 
the day, in order to provide predictions, while lags corresponding up to 20 minutes before the time of 
interest are also taken into account. More specifically, two strategies of this model were proved to provide 
the most accurate results. These are the Bayes (μ-1σ) and the Bayes (μ). Based on the evaluation of six 
criteria (Edges RR, Edges RV, Number of rejected requests, Ride share(%), Waiting time per request, Delay 
per request), both of the Bayesian strategies performed almost as good. However, Bayes (μ) tends to be 
a more optimistic strategy, compared to Bayes (μ-1σ), suggesting that it considers higher speeds, and 
therefore shorter travel times. For this reason, even though performance-wise both models were equal, we 
suggest the deployment of Bayes (μ-1σ) for such transportation problems, especially in peak periods (rush 
hours). On the other hand, Bayes (μ) seemed to perform very well during the times of the day, when the 
traffic congestion is normal. In addition, the statistics (𝑅𝑀𝑆𝐸, 𝑅2), used to evaluate the predictions provided 
by the Bayes (μ), show that Bayes (μ) predictions follow better the true values of speed, compared to the 
predictions of Bayes (μ-1σ). For these reasons Bayes (μ) could be a better alternative at times of the day 
with normal traffic. 
==PAGE:137==

Our suggestion to a taxi-sharing company would be to experiment on a hybrid approach that would 
incorporate both Bayes (μ-1σ) and Bayes (μ) strategies in order to address more effectively the different 
observed traffic states of the system, in respect to the various times of the day. More specifically, the 
conservative Bayes (μ-1σ) strategy would fit better the traffic states during the peak hours, when there is 
a higher level of uncertainty, while Bayes (μ) would perform better in the rest of the day. 
7.3. Implementation Conclusions 
The proposed models of this study were tested in a real case study, were three (3) taxis and ten (10) 
requests were considered. The number of both entities is a rather small one, however, due to the nature 
of the data provided by Google, higher numbers of vehicles and requests wouldn’t be realistic. Nonetheless, 
the framework used in this work is able to scale up and be applied in real time for a larger number of 
requests and vehicles, and for larger vehicle capacity as well. Moreover, by using the proposed heuristics 
the real-time computational time is minimized (approximately one minute), suggesting that with a few 
modifications the implementation of the method in a real-time application would be possible. 
7.4. Limitations and Future Research 
7.4.1. Limitations 
In the context of this work, a considerable effort to examine the use of ML Schemes in DVRP was put in 
place, and the obtained results were more than satisfactory. Nonetheless, some limitations were also 
noticed. 
To begin with, regarding the routing and scheduling procedure, due to lack of data regarding taxi requests 
in the area of Copenhagen, a pattern for requests generation was not considered. Efforts to obtain data 
regarding real taxi requests were made, however, given the time this data was needed it was not possible 
to acquire them. This suggests that the origins and the destinations of the requests, along with the locations 
of the vehicles, were chosen randomly. Moreover, insertion feasibility, in terms of assigning a request to a 
taxi that already carries passengers, was not taken into account, while a greedy algorithm instead of an 
optimizing one was deployed. Even though our focus was not solely the routing part, we can’t ignore the 
fact that an optimizing algorithm would provide more accurate results regarding the scheduling procedure. 
==PAGE:138==
CHAPTER 7: CONCLUSIONS 
121 
 
However, due to lack of resources and the limited time available, it was decided to use a greedy scheduling 
algorithm. 
Another limitation noticed was that the area studied in the Copenhagen case study was a rather limited 
area, in terms of square meters representing. The data regarding the vehicle speeds, provided by Google, 
was referring to this area, and therefore a larger one couldn’t be considered, since no data was available. 
Consequently, the number of requests and vehicles considered in the case study was also affected, since 
a larger number of requests and taxis for an area of that size wouldn’t be realistic. Possibly, in specific 
occasions that could be the case, however, due to the fact that the requests were generated randomly, it 
was decided to consider a reasonable number of requests and vehicles. 
Last, our work was implemented in Python programming environment, and more specifically in Jupiter 
Notebook. Certainly, if we were about to scale up the problem, it would also have to be optimized 
programing-wise, suggesting that another environment should be chosen. 
7.4.2. Future Research 
Traditionally, prediction and optimization have been seen as two separate processes to be executed one 
after the other, often assuming that no interdependency exists between the two. However, especially for 
the transportation field, this is not valid, and even though this work didn’t focus on the optimization of the 
transportation part, this factor was not ignored. Therefore, further research of this combination is 
suggested. Besides, if an optimization algorithm was used in the scheduling part, then more accurate and 
representative evaluation criteria could have been used in the case study. 
Furthermore, the present work could be upgraded by including, in the system, vehicles of larger capacity, 
and also by considering a larger service area. Also, the proposed method could be further developed, with 
the use of insertion feasibility, regarding the placement of new passengers in already operating vehicles. 
Last, in this work the short-term forecasting was based on Linear Regression models, and alterations of 
them. However, it would be interesting to test the method with more advanced ML methods, like Support 
Vector Regression (SVR), Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) or Neural Network Regression (NNR).
