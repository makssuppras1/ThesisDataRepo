Abstract
This master’s thesis focuses on the implementation of metaheuristics, specifically Simu­
lated Annealing (SA), Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS), and Hybrid Adap­
tive Large Neighbourhood Search (HALNS), to optimise the allocation of rail drivers at
Danske Statsbaner (DSB). The goal is to reduce overall costs while understanding more
about how station drivers are employed. This study also provides DSB essential informa­
tion about the efficiency of station driver employment.
The application of the SA, ALNS, and HALNS algorithms is part of the research technique
used for solving the rail driver optimisation challenge. Adaptive diversification and inten­
sification strategies are introduced by ALNS, while SA efficiently explores the solution
space. HALNS combines ALNS’s strengths with an evolutionary algorithm.
Possibile solutions have been found by utilising the strengths of SA, ALNS, and HALNS,
offering hopeful signs for cost reduction and boosting operational effectiveness.
In conclusion, metaheuristics prove to be effective in tackling this complex problem, al­
though not achieving absolute optimality. However, they do provide high­quality solutions.
However, the ultimate decision lies with DSB, as they hold the final authority in making
such determinations.
Keywords: metaheuristics, Simulated Annealing, SA, Adaptive Large Neighbourhood
Search, ALNS, Hybrid Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search, HALNS, optimisation, CSP,
crew scheduling problem.

1
Introduction
This chapter is similar to the introduction for the MSc thesis of Vera Nijmeijer (s213011)
and Jan Thorissen (s212435). [1]
This thesis investigates the application of metaheuristics to improve railway scheduling
for DSB. By utilising intelligent search algorithms, this thesis aims to find near­optimal so­
lutions for enhanced duty scheduling, leading to more efficient and reliable transportation
services. This introduction provides a brief overview of DSB, the specific problem and the
relevant data involved.
1.1
DSB
DSB, short for ”Danske Statsbaner”, is the national railway company of Denmark, respon­
sible for operating passenger trains across the country. As a significant provider of trans­
portation services, DSB must effectively manage its personnel, especially the scheduling
of their drivers. Effective scheduling is essential to the efficient functioning of DSB’s train
services because it has an immediate impact on elements like timeliness, reliability and
overall customer satisfaction. An effective scheduling system for DSB’s drivers enables
optimal resource use and guarantees that the appropriate number of drivers are available
at the appropriate time and location.
The primary objective of developing efficient schedules is to ensure the allocation of all
tasks among a limited number of drivers at their respective depots. This, in turn, leads to
decreased personnel costs. DSB can successfully handle a number of issues that arise in
its operations by putting in place a strong scheduling system. Firstly, a well­organised duty
schedule reduces the likelihood of driver shortages and delays or cancellations caused
by insufficient staffing. This guarantees that the trains operated by DSB are sufficiently
manned, maximising productivity and avoiding service interruptions. Second, a thorough
scheduling system considers a range of variables, including driver availability, statutory
limitations, labour laws and individual preferences. As a result, DSB is able to develop
reasonable duty schedules that accommodate the need for their drivers to have a bal­
ance between work and personal lives and contractual requirements. Furthermore, by
minimising unneeded overtime and guaranteeing appropriate resource allocation, an ef­
ficient driver scheduling procedure helps DSB improve cost control.
Driver duty schedules can be coordinated with demand patterns to help DSB success­
fully control labour costs while upholding high levels of service. It is crucial to take into
account the compromise between punctuality and effective scheduling. Schedules that
are highly optimised for efficiency provide little room for delays and unforeseen circum­
stances. Without a buffer, even a slight delay can trigger a chain reaction of delays due
to the tightly packed schedule. Customers attach a high value on punctuality, for this
purpose this tradeoff needs to be carefully considered and handled.

1.2
Problem description
DSB is the largest Danish train operating company working in whole Denmark. This re­
search collaboration aims to assess a new approach to allocating train drivers to required
shifts in order to minimise the operating costs at DSB.
1.2.1
Main goal
The main goal of the research is to investigate the potential advantages of hiring afford­
able train drivers, often known as ”station drivers” or ”local drivers,” who can carry out a
particular set of duties for DSB. To do this, a dataset with stations and blocks will be used,
where each block corresponds to a series of subsequent tasks performed on a specific
train. The goal is to assign all drivers to relevant depots and optimise driver assignment,
ensuring a cost­effective solution while completing all assigned responsibilities and train
rides. However in order to do this task, one must also contend with numerous additional
constraints, such as those related to break times and shift lengths, which must be carefully
taken into account in order to find a feasible solution. Due to this scheduling problem’s
intricacy, operations research methodology may provide useful approaches for effectively
tackling it.
1.2.2
Description
The three main components of the problem are the blocks, the train drivers and the sta­
tions. The blocks are train trips or tasks that need to be executed. The three components
and their underlying constraints are explained in Paragraphs 1.2.2.1, 1.2.2.2 and 1.2.2.3.
1.2.2.1
Blocks
The first major component of this problem involves the blocks, denoted by the set J, which
need to be covered during the designated time horizon. For the scope of this thesis,
the time horizon extends slightly beyond one day, specifically covering blocks between
Wednesday 04:00 and Thursday 07:00. For each block, the departure and arrival time
and location are provided. It is also given whether the block can be handled by a station
driver, and which depot this station driver must be assigned to. A block can always be
handled by a regular driver if this driver meets the route qualifications, which is further
explained in Section 1.3.5. A more detailed description of the data set for the blocks is
given in Section 1.3.
1.2.2.2
Drivers
The next key element comprises the drivers, who hold the responsibility of operating trains
and carrying out various tasks. Primarily, two distinct types of drivers can be identified:
• Regular drivers: These drivers are the ones that DSB is currently employing to
cover the specified blocks. They have the necessary qualifications and appropriate
training, which enable them to successfully complete all blocks within their allocated
routes.

• Station drivers: The number of blocks that these drivers can travel is constrained.
Their jobs typically comprise localised tasks completed inside a particular depot.
They are not allowed to manage blocks that require movement between stations.
Because they do not need to meet the same standards that apply to regular drivers,
these drivers’ services are at a lower cost.
Train drivers follow a duty­based work schedule, wherein they are assigned different du­
ties throughout a week. Each duty has a maximum time limit that changes depending
on whether it is a regular shift or a night duty. Any task that partially occurs between the
hours of 1:31 and 4:29 on a weekday is categorised as a night task, while all other tasks
are day tasks. Weekend responsibilities, such as those that take place on a Saturday
or Sunday, are not covered by this thesis. The longest shift permitted is eight hours for
night shifts and 9.5 hours for day shifts. Drivers will be paid for a minimum of six hours for
whatever task they do, even though there is no minimum time for a duty. As a result of
this payment policy, an ideal schedule lasts longer than six hours. No duties may begin
between 0:00 and 03:00 in accordance with union agreements. A driver must check in
at the depot before each shift, which usually takes 20 minutes. Similar to that, the driver
must check out at the depot when their shift is over, which usually takes five minutes. Ev­
ery driver has a right to a specified break during work hours, thus it is important to follow
certain guidelines when defining responsibilities. Each duty must include either a single
30­minute break or two separate breaks – one lasting 20 minutes and the other 25 min­
utes. The break requirement will be streamlined for the sake of this thesis to guarantee
that every duty includes one 30­minute break. It is also crucial to keep in mind that a
driver is only permitted to work for a total of five hours without taking a break.
1.2.2.3
Stations
The final major component of the problem revolves around the stations or locations, with
a primary focus on west Denmark, even tough DSB works across the whole country.
Since they form the basis of the railroad network, knowledge of these stations is crucial.
This network gives important information about the connections between various stations,
which helps to comprehend how trains move through the network to get to their final des­
tinations. All the locations are consolidated within one comprehensive set. Main stations
are among these places; they are shown in Figure 1.2. While stations designated with a
blue circle exclusively serve as depots for regular drivers, those with a red circle act as
depots for both regular and station drivers. It is important to remember that there may be
changes to the arrangement shown in Figure 1.2. One change that might be made is to
limit access for station drivers to just the two most important stations, such Aarhus and
Fredericia. The locations dataset (L) consists of a number of subsets, each of which has
a particular set of stations.
• LS: Stations that are depots for station drivers
• LR: Stations that are depots for regular drivers
• LD: All stations that are depots, LS ∪LR
• LB: Station where drivers can take a break
In summary, not all of the given locations or stations can be utilised for breaks or serve
as depots. When a driver is assigned to a specific depot, their shift will consistently com­
mence and conclude at that particular depot. Additionally, each main location is associ­
ated with a subset of sublocations, which may share a common depot. For instance, the
main location Aarhus (AR) has a sublocation named Aarhus parking lot (ARp).

Parameters
The following parameters have been provided to represent either rules enforced by the
union or simplifications. Simplifications will be specified:
• α: fraction a station driver costs compared to a regular driver, this is a value between
0 and 1;
• βmin: minimum length of a shift for a driver;

• βbreak: duration of a break, 30 min if the shift has one break and 20 / 25 minutes if
the shift has two breaks. For simplification, only 30 minute breaks are considered.
• µ: maximum duration of work without break, set to 5 hours.

Literature study
Chapter 1 provided an overview of DSB’s problem, which falls under the category of rail­
way crew scheduling problem (CSP). The current chapter will offer insights into how this
particular problem is approached and tackled using metaheuristics.
Crew scheduling, where duties are created to cover all necessary tasks, is a crucial step
in the planning process. Following this, in a process known as crew rostering, actual
drivers are assigned to perform these tasks. Crew scheduling is the main subject of
the literature review because this thesis’s focus is on duty generation. However, due
to the large instances involved, which represent the trips a train company makes within
a particular time and area, solving crew scheduling can be quite difficult. Because train
trips are shorter and train drivers have the flexibility to switch between trains, there is
a significantly higher number of potential duties, making railway crew scheduling more
challenging than scheduling issues with airlines. Figure 2.1 shows all the steps of the
complete planning process (Heil, Hoffmann, and Buscher [2]).
Figure 2.1: Planning process in railway transportation
The prevailing method for addressing this issue involves modelling it as either a Set Cov­
ering Problem (SCP) or a Set Partitioning Problem (SPP). The SCP and SPP are two
essential optimisation problems in computer science and operations research, with simi­
lar but distinct objectives.
(a) SCP
(b) SPP
Figure 2.2: Formulations of the SCP and SPP
In the SCP, as shown in Figure 2.2a, given a finite set D of elements and a collection
T of subsets of D, the goal is to find the smallest number of subsets from T (i.e., the
smallest cover) that covers all the elements in D. The aim is to select a minimum number
of subsets such that every element in D is included in at least one of the selected subsets.
On the other hand, the SPP, as shown in Figure 2.2b, involves the same initial setup, but
the objective is different. Here, the goal is to partition D into disjoint subsets by selecting
a subset from T for each element in D such that every element belongs to exactly one
selected subset. The objective is to form a partition of D, where each element appears in

subsets.
In both formulations, cd represents the cost associated with selecting element d in the
cover, and At,d is a binary matrix where At,d = 1 if element d belongs to subset Tt, and
At,d = 0 otherwise.
Both problems are NP­hard, making it difficult to find optimal solutions for large instances.
The SPP aims to create exclusive groups of elements, whereas the SCP concentrates on
covering as many elements as possible with the fewest subsets.
Numerous real­world situations, such as resource allocation, facility location, crew schedul­
ing, production planning, and network design, include these problems. Effectively re­
solving these problems helps in various domains to improve decision­making processes,
reduce costs, and optimise resource usage (Karp [3]).
Nevertheless, it is important to note that each crew scheduling problem is unique to its
application, which means the approach to solving these problems will differ accordingly.
Among the commonly employed methods, two stand out: column generation and meta­
heuristics. Nevertheless, since exact models have a hard time handling extremely large
problems, these models have their limitations in real­world applications. Heuristic ap­
proaches should therefore be used in order to effectively address large­scale, real­world
problems (Silva and Reis [4]). This thesis and literature review will specifically focus on
the application of metaheuristics to crew scheduling problems.
Meta­heuristics utilise either a single­solution­based or population­based approach to be­
gin with feasible crew schedules as initial solutions. These solutions undergo iterative
improvements within a predefined search space, employing stochastic optimisation tech­
niques like random numbers, until a predetermined stopping criterion is met. The category
of meta­heuristics includes single­solution­based methods like tabu search (TS) and sim­
ulated annealing (SA), as well as population­based procedures such as genetic algorithms
(GA) and their hybrid variations (Heil, Hoffmann, and Buscher [2]).
2.1
Simulated Annealing
The concept of SA can be traced back to Metropolis et al. [5], who initially utilised a fixed
temperature scheme. However, it was not until the early 1980s that the technique was
applied to discrete optimisation problems in the seminal works of Kirkpatrick, Gelatt, and
Vecchi [6]. The approach works in a way which involves accepting moves that may worsen
the solution but in a controlled manner. The probability of accepting a neighbour that is
”worse” than the current solution decreases throughout the annealing process, controlled
by a parameter called the temperature.
2.2
Genetic Algorithms
GA’s were pioneered by Holland [7] and draw inspiration from natural selection and popu­
lation genetics. They are widely employed for approximating solutions to complex optimi­
sation problems. This study presents the application of a hybrid GA to address the train
CSP from DSB. This hybrid method combines a GA with a local search heuristic.
Several motivations for applying GA’s to the SCP or SPP exist. GA’s can provide flexi­
bility in handling various model variations, such as constraints on duty time, mandatory
rest periods or work allocation limits at specific depots, achieved through modifications to
the evaluation function. This adaptability is often challenging to achieve with traditional
10
Rail driver optimisation using metaheuristics at DSB
==PAGE:18==
methods when adding new constraints. Moreover, GA’s maintain a population of poten­
tial solutions at each iteration. As Manikas and Cain [8] noted,”the knowledge of a family
of good solutions outweighs the pursuit of an isolated optimum”. Lastly, the SPP proves
to be an ideal problem for testing the effectiveness of GA’s due to its NP­completeness
in finding feasible solutions in the general case , as well as the substantial size of the
problems encountered in current industrial applications (Garey and Johnson [9], Levine
[10]).
2.3
Local Search methods
Local search techniques have recently drawn more attention as efficient ways to solve
CSP problems that consistently produce pleasing results. In their groundbreaking work,
Silva and Cunha [11] combined the GRASP metaheuristic with the Very Large­Scale
Neighbourhood Search (VLNS) as a local search method to solve CSP’s. By reallocating
and/or changing tasks between multiple duties, this technique seeks neighbouring solu­
tions while potentially affecting all duties involved. Furthermore, Alves Silva and Silva [12]
employed the guided local search (GLS) metaheuristic for solving the CSP. GLS conducts
a local search within the current solution, differentiating itself from other metaheuristics by
penalising undesirable characteristics of the current solution to escape local optima. Upon
reaching a local optimum, GLS selects certain solution attributes to penalise, transforming
it into a non­local optimum and thus advancing the search to another local optimum. This
process iterates until specific stop criteria are met. By combining GLS with the local search
method variable neighbourhood descent (VND), Alves Silva and Silva [12] achieved re­
sults comparable to those found in the existing literature. Numerous approaches were
explored and implemented to tackle CSP’s, and the ones mentioned above represent just
a subset of these approaches.
To conclude, the Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS) heuristic was proposed
Pisinger and Ropke [13] to address the pickup and delivery problem with Time Windows.
It begins with a feasible solution and utilises destroy­reconstruct mechanisms to discover
better solutions. Three removal heuristics are employed: Shaw, random and worst, while
two insertion heuristics are used: greedy and regret. During each iteration, one removal
and one insertion heuristic are independently selected and then applied as a pair. The
selection of heuristics is influenced by individual weights dynamically adjusted based on
their success. The ALNS demonstrated substantial improvements over more than 50% of
the problems when compared to the best solutions known in the literature. This highlights
the advantages of using multiple removal and insertion heuristics concurrently instead of
relying on a single pair of heuristics (Carmo Martins and Silva [14]). Due to the similarities
between the CSP and the time window problem, Carmo Martins and Silva [14] proactively
modified the ALNS, tailoring it to address the CSP. The result of this adaptation demon­
strated the ALNS’ efficacy in resolving a class of problems falling under the domain of
CSP. Its superiority in solving these problems can be attributed to its adaptive nature,
making it more robust and adaptable to instances with diverse characteristics. Neverthe­
less, there exists scarce literature regarding the implementation of the HALNS method for
solving the CSP problem.

Metaheuristics
This chapter takes a closer look at the development and implementation of metaheuris­
tics. Metaheuristics are used as an alternative solution method to solve DSB’s problem.
However, these methods will not always succeed in finding the absolute optimum, but will
succeed in creating the best possible solution regardless of the size of the input. In this
section, 3 different options will be explored: SA, ALNS and Hybrid Adaptive Large Neigh­
bourhood Search (HALNS). The results of these methods are only described further in
chapter 5.
4.1
Simulated Annealing
SA, as presented by Kirkpatrick, Gelatt, and Vecchi [6], is a type of single­objective op­
timisation method inspired by the natural process of slowly cooling solids to achieve a
low­energy, crystalline state. In this physical process, as the temperature decreases,
the particles within the material have restricted movements, favouring configurations with
lower energy. The algorithm, mimicking this natural process in a computational model,
maintains a state and a computational temperature. Initially, the temperature is set high
and progressively reduced towards zero during the execution. The state represents a
solution to the optimisation problem, and at each iteration, the algorithm perturbs this so­
lution to generate a new one. The quality of a solution is associated with its energy level,
similar to real­world annealing. Comparing the qualities of both solutions using the ob­
jective function, a new state is chosen. If the new solution is not worse than the previous
one, it becomes the new state. However, if the new solution is of lower quality, it may still
be accepted based on the current computational temperature and the difference in qual­
ity. Solutions are more likely to be accepted if they are only slightly worse or when the
temperature is high. Significantly inferior solutions are unlikely to be accepted, and as the
temperature approaches zero, the probability of accepting any inferior solution becomes
extremely low (Smith [15]).
4.1.1
Components
4.1.1.1
Objective function
In SA, the objective function stands in for the evaluation of the effectiveness or suitability
of a specific optimisation problem solution or state. The objective function measures how
well a particular state performs in the given problem, similar to the energy function used
in the physical annealing process and directs the algorithm to find an optimal or nearly
optimal solution.
The objectives and constraints of the optimisation problem are used to define the ob­
jective function, which is problem­specific. Depending on whether it is a minimisation
or maximisation problem, the SA algorithm seeks to minimise or maximise this objective
function.
The objective function is repeatedly evaluated for various states as the SA algorithm ex­
plores the solution space during execution. The difference in the values of the objective
function between the current state and a newly generated state serves as the basis for
the SA acceptance criterion. It is always accepted as the new current state if the new
state enhances the objective function (i.e., reduces energy). To enable exploration of the
solution space and prevent getting caught in local optimums, the new state may still be
accepted probabilistically even if it is worse (i.e., increases the energy). SA can escape

21
==PAGE:29==
from suboptimal solutions and eventually converge towards an optimal or nearly optimal
solution thanks to this probabilistic acceptance mechanism (Kirkpatrick, Gelatt, and Vec­
chi [6]).
4.1.1.2
Perturbations
In SA, the process of making minor changes to the current state in order to produce a new
neighbouring state is referred to as perturbation. These modifications effectively enable
the algorithm to explore various areas of the solution space. The neighbourhood function
defines the set of possible perturbations that can be applied to the current state.
The nature and complexity of the perturbations depend on the specific problem being
solved. The solution representation’s elements may need to be switched around or mod­
ified in some situations, while parameters or variables may need to be changed in others.
The efficiency of these perturbations is a key factor in how well the algorithm explores the
solution space and finds a variety of solutions (Ingber [16]).
4.1.1.3
Stop criterion
In SA, the stop criterion defines when the algorithm should come to an end. The stop cri­
terion typically depends on predefined conditions or a predetermined number of iterations
because SA is a metaheuristic and cannot ensure finding the best solution.
Common stop criteria include completing a predetermined number of iterations, attaining
an acceptable level of solution quality, or running the algorithm for the allotted time. The
problem’s complexity, the desired level of solution quality, and the available computational
resources all influence the stop criterion selection.
4.1.1.4
Acceptence criterion
A crucial component of the SA algorithm is the acceptance criterion, which decides whether
a newly generated state should be accepted as the current state. This criterion makes
it easier to explore the solution space effectively and guards against becoming trapped
in local optima. It makes a decision by contrasting the current state’s and the recently
generated state’s objective function values.
It is always accepted as the new current state when the new state enhances the objective
function (i.e., results in less energy used or a better solution). There is a chance that
the new state will be accepted even if it produces a worse solution (i.e., more energy).
Because of this probabilistic acceptance, the algorithm is sometimes able to consider
subpar solutions in the early stages when the temperature is still high. The likelihood
of accepting suboptimal solutions declines as the algorithm evolves and the temperature
drops, causing the search to move closer to an almost optimal solution (Schneider and
Puchta [17]).
4.1.1.5
Cooling schedule
The temperature decrease during the SA process is controlled by the cooling schedule.
The algorithm’s initial temperature setting is high, allowing for the exploration of a wide
variety of solutions, including those with higher energies (less desirable solutions). The
temperature gradually drops as the algorithm develops, encouraging the use of better
solutions and guiding the search towards a local or global optimum.
Depending on the specific issue, the cooling schedule’s design can change and utilise a
variety of temperature­reduction techniques. Linear, geometrical and exponential cool­
ing schedules are frequently used. The quality of the algorithm’s solutions, the balance
between exploration and exploitation and the convergence rate are all impacted by the
cooling schedule selection (Schneider and Puchta [17]).

4.1.2
Intuition
SA is influenced by the metallurgical annealing procedure. On the basis of a probability
determined by a temperature parameter that gradually decreased throughout the algo­
rithm, it explores nearby solutions and accepts worse ones. It explores a variety of solu­
tion regions while avoiding local optimum conditions by accepting worse solutions early
on, especially when the temperature is high. The algorithm ramps up its search for bet­
ter answers as the temperature drops. The key intuition lies in the balanced strategy of
exploration and exploitation, skillfully negotiating difficult terrain to arrive at close to ideal
solutions. Its adaptability and ability to refine the search based on problem character­
istics make SA a powerful optimization technique widely used for challenging real­world
problems.
4.1.3
Advantages
SA is a reliable optimisation method with a number of strong benefits that make it a good
choice for tackling particular problem types.
Being able to handle issues with complex and challenging solution spaces is a key strength.
SA, on the other hand, excels at exploring a variety of regions, making it highly effective
for such scenarios. Conventional optimisation methods, on the other hand, may find it
difficult to navigate such complexities efficiently. SA is also useful for objective functions
that are difficult to evaluate analytically or that are non­differentiable. Due to the objective
function’s flexibility, it can be applied to a wide variety of real­world issues even when it
does not have an obvious mathematical form.
The algorithm’s probabilistic acceptance criterion, which enables it to avoid local optima
and seek out global or nearly optimal solutions, is another appealing feature. Its ability to
successfully navigate the solution space, even in the face of difficult problem landscapes,
is improved by this escape mechanism. SA is well known for its adaptability to different
optimisation issues. This is an efficient option for various scenarios and domains because
only the definition of an objective function and the generation of neighbouring states are
necessary.
Additionally, the SA’s cooling schedule adds to its appeal. By giving users the ability to
precisely control the tradeoff between exploration and exploitation, the algorithm’s con­
vergence behaviour is improved, leading to the achievement of preferable results. As
a result, SA is an invaluable tool for tackling challenging optimisation problems, espe­
cially in cases where other conventional approaches may be ineffective or complicated

Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search
Pisinger and Røpke [13] initially presented the ALNS framework for multiple variations
of the Vehicle Routing Problem, where they reported promising results. ALNS can be
regarded as a broad framework that can be used to solve a wide range of optimisation
issues. ALNS is a local search framework in which a number of simple neighbourhoods
compete to modify the current solution. The current solution is destroyed in each iteration,
and is repaired in the next phase. The new solution is accepted if it satisfies some criteria
defined by the local search framework applied at the master level. Neighbourhoods that
can access a significant part of the problem space are often the ones that are utilised.
Based on the performance score of the neighbourhoods in the past, an adaptive layer
stochastically determines which ones to select. A neighbourhood has a higher score and a
higher chance of being chosen the more it has contributed to the problem­solving process.

4.3.1
Components
4.3.1.1
Destroy and Repair methods
The ”destroy” and ”repair” methods are two key elements of the ALNS algorithm.
In
the process of exploration and optimization, these elements are essential. The destroy
method is responsible for perturbing or eliminating parts of the current solution, while the
repair method reconstructs a new solution based on the remaining components.
The destroy method divides the current solution into smaller elements or components in
order to diversify the search. Depending on the specific issue, these elements might be
routes, assignments or other useful structures. The destroy method adds randomness
and opens up new opportunities to explore uncharted territory in the solution space by
removing a portion of the solution.
The repair method, on the other hand, takes the fragmented solution produced by the
destroy method and makes an attempt to reassemble it into a feasible and improved
solution. Reassembling the remaining parts in a way that satisfies the constraints of the
problem and enhances the effectiveness of the solution defines the repair process.
The ALNS algorithm explores a wide range of solutions by iteratively applying the destroy
and repair methods, adapting its search based on the characteristics of the problem and
the effectiveness of the individual methods. Depending on how successfully the destroy
and repair methods find better solutions, the algorithm constantly changes the weights
given to them (Wu et al. [21]).

The intuition behind the ALNS algorithm lies in its ability to strike a balance between
exploration and exploitation for efficient optimization. Diversification and intensification,
two essential phases, are how ALNS accomplishes this. Diversification is the process
of exploring different areas of the solution space by perturbing or removing portions of
the current solution. In the intensification phase, a new solution is constructed using the
remaining elements in an effort to enhance the quality of the solution and take advan­
tage of regional structures. Through an adaptive learning mechanism, ALNS adapts its
search strategy by dynamically changing the weights of various components based on
their performance. Due to its adaptability and the incorporation of multiple optimization
techniques, ALNS is a strong and effective algorithm that can successfully solve a variety
of problems (Pisinger and Ropke [22]).
4.3.3
Advantages
The ALNS framework has many benefits.
The ALNS algorithm is built upon efficient
heuristics that are known to work well for a variety of optimization problems. The ALNS
algorithm systematically explores significant portions of the solution space by making use
of broad and varied neighbourhoods. As a result, it develops high resilience, the ability to
adjust to distinctive characteristics of individual instances and, a low probability of getting
stuck in local minima. ALNS excels at solving problems with tight constraints where small
neighbourhoods are unable to sufficiently escape from local minima or particular regions
of the solution space. In these cases, the large neighbourhood search enables significant
changes to a number of variables, aiding in the identification of fresh workable solutions.
Because each used neighbourhood’s influence is automatically adjusted by the adaptive
layer, the ALNS algorithm’s calibration process is fairly simple. It may still be necessary

it is possible to do so individually or by using the parameters from already­validated al­
gorithms. The ALNS approach allows for the simultaneous use of multiple (reasonable)
neighbourhoods, in contrast to conventional local search algorithms, which frequently re­
quire the researcher to select between different neighbourhoods. In fact, based on past
performance, the ALNS heuristic generally performs better the more diverse neighbour­
hoods it includes (Pisinger and Ropke [22]).

Repair
After decomposing the solution using a decomposition method, reassembling the list of
destroyed blocks plays a crucial role in the algorithm’s performance and solution quality.
There are two approaches available: choosing the best fit, where blocks are inserted at
their optimal positions, or utilising regret fit, which quantifies the difference between the
chosen solution’s fitness and the fitness of the best alternative solution.
4.4.1.3
Best Fit
The first repair strategy, as shown in Algorithm 17, used is typically considered the best fit.
This process adopts a simple methodology. For each block that has been removed from
the solution, a search takes place in the broken solution to find a suitable new position, i.e.
FindBestPlace(). Every potential place is taken into account, and if a feasible location is
found, the cost involved in that placement is calculated and temporarily stored in the list
BP. The same procedure is then used in case another feasible location is discovered,
with the cost only being reviewed and stored if it turns out to be better than the previously
stored cost. As a result, during the reassembly step, only the best position for each block is
remembered and taken into consideration. In contrast to the regret fit method, this strategy
remembers two locations as instead of one. The greedy algorithm then moves on to the
next block in the list of destroyed blocks after thoroughly examining every possible position
for each block. Once each block has been assessed, the iterative process repeats. The
blocks are then prioritised according to the extra cost they add to the solution. Priority is
provided and the block that incurs the least additional cost is positioned first in its preferred
position. The next blocks are then organised in decreasing order of the costs connected
with them. There are still other aspects and things to take into account when planning the
reconstruction.
• When a new block is added to a certain schedule, it gives the remaining blocks a
brand­new opportunity. Therefore, the succeeding calculation must be done before
allocating the next block. The remaining blocks are repeatedly and one at a time
added to this schedule. The cost is then contrasted with their current lowest cost. If
the new determined cost turns out to be superior, it surpasses the price that is now
the best. If it does not get better everything will remain. Due to the fact that each
allocation opens up new possibilities and options for later allocations, this phase is
essential and should not be missed.
• The possibility of numerous blocks having the lowest cost for the same position
in the solution during the initial calculation is another important consideration that
should not be ignored. But it is also possible that before the other blocks’ turn, a
block has already been assigned to that location. In order to add a block to the
solution, a new computation is ,therefore, required. After a block has been put in
place, all the other blocks are checked to see if their desired positions match the

The second major repair method, known as regret fit, operates based on the principle of
minimising the regret associated with block placements. This strategy considers the cost
difference between the selected solution and the best alternative solution. The regret fit
approach analyses various positions for each block, much like the best fit strategy does.

are kept, not just the lowest cost. The ranking of blocks is no longer entirely determined
by the cheapest alternative once each block has been examined. The focus is instead
shifted to the disparity between the lowest cost and the next­lowest cost. This difference,
also known as the regret, serves as an indicator of the potential gain on the first allocation
and the subsequent loss on the second allocation if the block is not placed in its preferred
position. As a result, the blocks are sorted according to their regrets, starting with the
block with the most regret and moving on to the block with the second biggest regret and
so forth. It is also important to recall that regret calculations are updated after each block
allocation. Additionally, it is checked to see if the regrets have been updated in case a

• If the proposed solution is technically possible but does not outperform the existing

HALNS is a powerful optimization algorithm that effectively handles challenging combi­

Components
4.5.1.1
Genetic algorithm
The GA is crucial to the HALNS algorithm’s enhancement of the exploration and diversi­
fication components of the search process. The concepts of natural selection in genetics
serve as the foundation for the GA. This works by keeping track of a population of po­
tential solutions, also known as individuals or chromosomes, and then repeatedly using
genetic operators to iterate, evolve, and improve the population over generations. These
genetic operators include crucial elements like mutation, crossover and selection.
The GA plays a crucial role in the HALNS framework for maintaining a diverse population
of solutions throughout the search process. It harmonises with the ALNS component,
encouraging exploration and preventing too early convergence to local maxima. A pop­
ulation of potential solutions is first generated by the algorithm, either randomly or using
heuristic techniques. Every member of the population stands for a potential answer to the
optimization problem.
Based on how well each member of the population performs in relation to the problem’s
objective function, their fitness is carefully evaluated. Higher fitness values are embodied

The crossover genetic operator involves combining genetic information from two parent

4.5.2
Intuition
The intuition behind HALNS lies in its ability to effectively explore large solution spaces

Through the use of a set of heuristics or operators to alter current solutions, the algorithm
uses ALNS to explore various areas of the solution space. HALNS can explore a wide

finding high­quality solutions. Based on how well the ALNS operators perform during
the search, the adaptive mechanism in HALNS dynamically modifies the weights and
selection probabilities of the operators. By doing this, HALNS can concentrate on more
promising operators and expand its search in regions with a high likelihood of producing
better solutions. With this flexibility, HALNS is able to modify its search strategy to fit
the unique requirements of the issue being solved, resulting in better exploration and
exploitation as the search moves forward.
Additionally, by using a population of solutions produced by ALNS and implementing ge­
netically inspired crossover and selection phases, HALNS’s hybridization with GAs in­
creases the diversity of solutions. With the help of this hybrid strategy, which combines
the advantages of both genetic and ALNS algorithms, it is possible to navigate complex
solution landscapes with ease. The intuition of HALNS, therefore, is to strike a balance
between exploration and exploitation, leveraging its adaptability and hybrid nature to effi­
ciently explore the solution space and converge to high­quality solutions. HALNS offers
a strong and adaptable framework for tackling difficult combinatorial optimization prob­
lems in a variety of domains by combining ALNS, adaptive mechanisms and genetically
inspired elements. HALNS is a potent tool for tackling practical optimization problems
with large solution spaces and complex constraints due to its adaptability and efficiency
(Voigt et al. [24]).

ALNS
The ALNS component of the HALNS technique is substantially the same as the standard
ALNS mentioned in Section 4.3. However, there are two significant differences. The
algorithm’s goal is the first difference. The ALNS in HALNS is performance­based rather
than time­based. A maximum number of iterations is given rather than a time limit for the
method. The algorithm will stop if it cannot find a better solution for this continuous amount
of iterations. Due to its adaptable design, the algorithm can run for varying lengths of time,
sometimes taking longer when improvements are discovered all the time and sometimes
finishing fast as it finds a local optima quickly. The second significant difference in HALNS
lies in the selection of candidates for destruction. All blocks were taken into account

are consistently updated. As a result, there is a larger likelihood that the approaches that

6
Discussion
Since it is impossible to find the best answer for the complete dataset, the problem at
hand has never been solved. The research of Nijmeijer and Thorissen [1] has clarified this
difficulty. Despite the complexity, progress can be achieved by employing metaheuristics
to approximate the ideal solution.
SA produces good outcomes when compared to other metaheuristics. However, ALNS
or HALNS are better options for a more precise approximation of the best solution. These
algorithms have demonstrated the ability to produce very positive results. In reality, with
smaller datasets, they can achieve up to 1% of perfection while SA can only get up to
10% of optimality. Although there is no assurance that these remarkable findings will hold
for larger datasets, the (H)ALNS algorithms demonstrate significant promise in producing
excellent results. Because of this, SA is not the ideal metaheuristic for this task. However,
picking between ALNS and HALNS is not an easy choice to make. The difference between
these two approaches is rather minimal when compared to SA. The choice ultimately
comes down to the particular situation where the algorithm will be used.
ALNS is a powerful method that yields good results and can operate within a time limit.
Nevertheless, there is no guarantee of obtaining the absolute top result. While the results
are likely to be of high quality, they may differ somewhat from the absolute best outcome
due to the inherent randomness in the algorithm. HALNS, on the other hand, deals with
this unpredictability problem and offers better assurance in its results. In terms of score,
the results of this method are more predictable and compact. The trade­off, however, is
that HALNS is not time­limited, making it difficult to predict when the algorithm will con­
verge. In light of these elements, the following proposal can be made: ALNS is advised
if speedy and effective outcomes are desired. On the contrary, if there is no strict time
constraint and reliability is of utmost importance, HALNS is the preferred choice. In the
end, the choice is made in light of the particular circumstances and needs at hand.
Despite the promising outcomes achieved so far, there are still opportunities for further
expansion and improvement in this project. To begin with, the SA approach can be sig­
nificantly enhanced. Currently, the SA implementation is relatively basic, but there is
sufficient room for adding several more perturbations. By incorporating additional pertur­
bations, the algorithm can explore a wider range of neighbourhoods, potentially uncov­
ering more diverse solutions within the solution space. Moreover, performance­wise, it
is worthwhile to explore the potential of parallelisation. Investigating the impact of paral­
lel processing on the algorithm’s performance could be fascinating to determine whether
it can lead to significant performance improvements. Overall, by expanding the SA ap­
proach and exploring parallelisation, the project stands to gain further insights and poten­
tially achieve even better results.
Furthermore, it appears that the (H)ALNS metaheuristics offer much potential for devel­
opment and have already shown significant improvements in results. The reward system
is one area that could use an improvement. Scores could be determined by the rate
of improvement per unit of time. A more thorough evaluation of the success of slower
procedures, like large destruction techniques, might be possible with the implementa­
tion of such a system. Although it requires much computer power, parallelisation offers
these metaheuristics a wealth of promise. Investigating how parallel processing affects
their performance may result in significant gains in problem­solving effectiveness. Mak­
Rail driver optimisation using metaheuristics at DSB
59
==PAGE:67==
ing destruction methods dynamic is an appealing possibility. The entire algorithm would
become more flexible as a result, maybe producing superior outcomes. If data shows that
greater destructions have different effective time periods than smaller ones, a tailored ap­
proach might be used. This variable might be handled by dynamically assigning destruc­
tion size, significantly enhancing the algorithm’s performance. Overall, these directions
offer promising opportunities for improving the (H)ALNS metaheuristics and getting even
better outcomes.
On the contrary, the SA framework holds potential for further improvements. Introduc­
ing flexibility into the approach could allow for more exploration­oriented acceptances at
the start and gradually shift towards exploitation­oriented acceptances as time advances.
This adaptive approach would create a better balance between exploration and exploita­
tion, potentially leading to enhanced performance. Additionally, an essential adjustment
involves dynamically configuring the parameters for the ALNS within the HALNS. Cur­
rently, these parameters are predetermined, but their performance could be significantly
improved by making them dynamic. Specifically, the iterations without improvement, serv­
ing as the stopping criterion for ALNS, need to be adjusted as time progresses. As the
search for small improvements often takes longer over time, dynamic parameters can
effectively address this issue and facilitate the search for diverse and optimal solutions.
The extension of all metaheuristics by including impractical solutions is a really intriguing
topic that deserves additional investigation. Rather than solely focusing on finding feasi­
ble solutions, the approach involves accepting infeasible solutions while penalising them
accordingly. Infeasible solutions face increasing penalties as time goes on, which even­
tually results in the convergence of a feasible solution. This strategy offers a great deal of
potential for improving all addressed metaheuristics’ exploring phase. These algorithms
are capable of efficiently navigating the solution space and perhaps finding more optimal
or nearly ideal solutions by accepting infeasible alternatives and dynamically adjusting
penalties.
The algorithm has the capability to accommodate expansions to the problem domain.
Incorporating additional constraints to enhance realism, such as introducing the option
for double breaks, would be a valuable and positive direction for future development.

Conclusion
In conclusion, metaheuristics prove to be effective in tackling this complex problem, al­
though not achieving absolute optimality. However, they do provide high­quality solutions.
SA performs reasonably well among the tested methods, but falls short when compared
to ALNS. ALNS delivers outstanding results over short and long time frames. For smaller
datasets, it makes promising progress, getting within 1% of the optimal value. However,
because there is not an absolute optimal solution, its performance for larger datasets is
less certain. Although it takes more time, a second high­quality approach called HALNS
consistently produces excellent solutions. This technique, which combines metaheuristics
and genetic algorithms in a sophisticated way, has exciting potential for future applications
and developments.
Based on the conducted analyses, drawing definitive conclusions becomes challenging.
The implementation of block restrictions for station drivers increases the number of station
drivers, but with a downside of a higher overall cost. As significant cost benefits are only
realised when the cost fraction falls below ”acceptable norms,” with a suggested value of
0.55, much lower than the current 0.89, adjusting the cost fraction is not a practical option.
Furthermore, examining the improved solutions generated by the metaheuristics raises
questions about the necessity of station drivers altogether, considering they account for
an average 3% of all drivers. However, the ultimate decision lies with DSB, as they hold
the final authority in making such determinations.
