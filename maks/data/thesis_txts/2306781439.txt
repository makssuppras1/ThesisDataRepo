Abstract
The 75,000 m3 water pond heat storage of Marstal has been investigated during the
present study.
Water pits thermal storage constitute a viable renewable technology,
spreading fast in Denmark as part of solar heating power plants. The aim of the study
was to assess thermal conditions, heat losses and eﬃciency over the period of October
2014 to September 2015 and suggest optimizations. Measurement data from the pond
have been analyzed in order to estimate energy ﬂows and heat losses. In addition, the
distribution of heat losses between the top lid surface, ground walls and transmission
piping was investigated.
Based on these results, CFD models have been developed
in ANSYS Fluent, simulating representative operating periods. Finally, an alternative
design was suggested. It consists of a large diameter inlet pipe at the top, which is
expected to provide low velocities. This design was accessed, through CFD calculations.
The heat losses have been estimated at 2950 MWh out of 8350 MWh, provided for the
studied year. The lid is evaluated as the most signiﬁcant heat loss source. The ground’s
contribution was estimated around 20% of the total losses, it is suggested to insulate
the top 2 m of ground since this would prevent most of the ground losses without the
large cost of an extended insulation. In terms of further recommendations, the lid heat
ﬂux sensor is non representative and the transmission piping losses should be taken into
consideration.
The CFD results were validated through measurements and proven accurate. Thermal
stratiﬁcation is found to be intact in most cases, except a late February case.
The
largest velocities were spotted near inlets/outlet but without disturbing stratiﬁcation
signiﬁcantly. Regarding the alternative design, various modiﬁcations were proven nec-
essary in order to be used in discharging. However, during charging the proposed large
inlet pipe behaves similarly to the real design, while it could be proven a much simpler
and cost eﬀective alternative. Finally, the presence of back ﬂow and mixing in the large
pipe should be faced, before proceeding to such an arrangement.
Keywords: Solar heating plants; Renewable energy systems; Solar pond; Thermal en-
ergy storage; Thermal stratiﬁcation; CFD

Chapter 1
Introduction
Nowadays, there is a growing concern regarding climate change and dependence on fossil
fuels. The world community and Europe are putting eﬀorts to design an environmental
friendly and sustainable development plan for the years to come. This eﬀort was re-
ﬂected in the recent United Nation Climate Change Conference in Paris, however many
countries, such as Denmark, have set much more ambitious goals for the next decades.
Denmark’s goal is to be completely fossil fuel free by 2050, while the energy demand
in buildings in terms of both electricity and heating, should be covered by renewable
sources by 2035 [8].
In terms of heating, the largest part of Domestic Hot Water and Space Heating in
Denmark is covered through the District Heating (DH) network [9]. The largest share of
the DH supply derives from combined heat and power plants, which usually are fueled
by fossil fuels. Nevertheless, new emerging renewable energy technologies cover larger
part of the heating market share every year. One of the most promising technologies are
large scale solar heating plants, which are increasing rapidly in Denmark. By the end
of 2014, it is expected that the 10 largest solar heating plants of Europe will be located
in Denmark, counting a total solar collector area of about 566,000 m2 [10].
Solar heating plants are meeting security of supply by adding ﬂexibility in their produc-
tion by installing thermal energy storage (TES). A fast developing solution during the
last years in Denmark are the Pit Thermal Energy Storage (PTES). The present study
investigates the 75,000 m3 water PTES of Marstals solar heating plant, constructed un-
der the SUNSTORE 4 project. The study focuses on energy system analysis, allocation
of heat losses and CFD modeling using ANSYS.

1.1
Background
One of the main drawbacks of the solar heating plants, is the mismatch between produc-
tion and consumption. The solar production ﬂuctuates through the year peaking during
summer and midday hours, however demand rises at winter and during morning and
evening hours [11], constituting heat storage a crucial parameter. Moreover, the recent
growth in renewables in electricity market and especially wind power, raise the demand
for energy storage applications. The integration of heat pumps in the energy system in
combination with heat storage technologies is proven beneﬁcial since it adds ﬂexibility to
the energy system [12]. Moreover, the heat pump and heat storage integration secures
connection between electricity and heat markets, which is crucial for the sustainable
shift of the energy system [13]. Therefor, there is a growing interest in adding ﬂexibility
in terms of seasonal heat storage and relevant research is ongoing [14].
At the moment, the most promising solutions in terms of seasonal heat storage seem
to be tank storage systems, water pit storage, aquifer thermal energy storage, borehole
storage and water-gravel pit storage [15] [16]. Moreover, cases of latent heat storage
using phase change materials are also investigated but there are still some barriers to
overcome in relevant research before applying them to large scale [17].
Large scale water pond thermal energy storage, consist a viable solution, as mentioned,
in both economical and environmental perspective since they are simple in construction
and relatively cheap. The aim is to allow solar fraction of the district heating to reach or
exceed 50% of the heat demand of the local areas [18]. Today, PTES are used as seasonal
heat storage solution in Denmark in Marstal (75,000 m3), Dronningland (60,000 m3),
Vojens (200,000 m3) and Gram (120,000 m3). It should be stated that larger storage
volumes lead to increased eﬃciency in practice, since the heat losses do not increase
with the volume proportionally.
Marstal’s PTES was developed and funded under the SUNSTORE 4 project, as part of
the ”EU ﬂag projects” [19]. Its aim is to increase the solar fraction of the plant up to 55%
of the thermal energy production, focusing on sustainability, increased eﬃciency and low
costs. Marstal’s 75,000 m3 PTES was commissioned in 2012 and it has a capacity of
6.96 GWh according to PlanEnrgi [20]. The operating temperatures vary depending on
season and depth, however the pond is designed based on a range of 10-90 °C. The heat
losses are estimated to be about 2.5 GWh per year, nevertheless by monitoring the pond
higher losses of about 25% occur [1] and their origin is not clear at the moment. The
pond’s operation is monitored by temperature measurements inside the water pit, on
the ground close to its sides and on the top insulation and by means of measurements
for energy quantities charged to and discharged from the heat storage. First results from
these measurements, were published by Solites in 2016 indicating a storage eﬃciency of
62-66% [1].

Respectively to smaller TES, such as domestic hot water tanks, thermal stratiﬁcation
is crucial in order to achieve high performances [21]. The water inside the storage is
stratiﬁed on diﬀerent temperature layers due to density diﬀerence. During charging or
discharging of the storage, the water is provided hot at top or cold at bottom respectively
in order for the thermal stratiﬁcation to remain intact. In this way, higher temperatures
can be maintained at the top of the storage for longer periods and lower in the lower
levels, instead of the pond cooling down uniformly. This pattern allows utilization of
larger parts of the stored heat increasing eﬃciency. An other point to consider is the fact
that a stratiﬁed water storage has decreased losses from the lower levels increasing also
the thermal eﬃciency and decreasing exergy destruction [21]. Moreover, high thermal
stratiﬁcation has a positive eﬀect on the solar ﬁeld’s eﬃciency, since it allows lower
return temperatures at the collectors [22].
Along these lines, emphasis is given in preserving stratiﬁcation and avoiding mixing
and high velocities inside the storage. Experience in smaller domestic tanks shows that
the main cause of distratiﬁcation are inlet jets [23]. As a result the avoidance of water
jets which may inﬂuence stratiﬁcation is a primary also for the larger thermal storage.
Generally, various designs are used in practice, the aim always in to secure low ﬂow
horizontal velocities. Regarding Marstal’s PTES, a steel tower stratiﬁer, which will be
discusses later, is used in practice, while measurement data indicate established thermal
stratiﬁcation through the year.
1.2
Hypothesis
In 2016, Solites presented the ﬁrst results from measurements concerning energy balance
and heat losses in the water pond [1]. However, there is a variety of phenomena con-
tributing in these heat losses and it is not clear how they are distributed at the moment.
For instance, there are measurements of heat ﬂuxes in the pit’s lid for the whole year,
which values do not take into consideration several facts such as thermal bridges in the
top insulation, ﬂaws or damages. Correspondingly, there are uncertainties regarding the
losses to the ground where the soil temperature close to the pond walls ﬂuctuates and
there is no clear indication of its pattern. An other point to consider is the importance
of the losses through transmission piping.
In the present study, the measurement data provided from the water pond are analyzed
in order to estimate heat losses and storage eﬃciency, then detailed calculations are
performed and models are developed in order to evaluate the contribution of the diﬀerent
heat loss sources. The results are compared to measurements in order to allocate losses
and evaluate the current estimations.
Another area of interest is the ﬂow and temperature distribution inside the water pond.
Right now the only available information from inside the pit are vertical temperature

1.3
Limitations and Uncertainties
The most important drawback in this study may be the fact that there are not a lot of
relevant studies and results to rely on. For instance, there are estimations available on
the scale of the heat losses, although there are no safe estimations on their distribution
on the diﬀerent sources. In addition, there are limited indications on how the ground
temperature close to the pit is distributed. A fact that is proven crucial, especially when
taken into account the fact that if the ground is hot enough after some years it could
heat up the pond, instead of contributing to the losses. This could take place in large
depths and bottom during winter. Thus, a lot of assumptions were made in order to
proceed with this study, some of which could be proven unsafe after further research.
An other limitation is the computational time demanded for the CFD investigation.
CFD models cover a large part of the current study. The models simulate a volume
of 75,000 m3 which demands a mesh of more than a million cells.
Additionally the
iterations timestep cannot exceed some seconds of simulation. All of these conclude to
an extensive computational cost. As a result the periods simulated correspond to some
hours of real operation and separate models, corresponding to diﬀerent periods of the
yearly operation, are developed. It would be interested to simulate longer time periods

Based on that, a ﬁrst approach was to set isotherms in the ground having the same slope
as the side walls. Their temperature for each month was set based on the corresponding
measurement from the censor at B. However, this assumption lead the isotherm lines
which touched the pond walls to have the temperature of the the B sensor at the top
surface, which was inﬂuenced strongly by the ambient conditions. As a results the ground
close to the water pond was much cooler, around 30-35 °C than in reality leading to large
heat ﬂuxes.
An other approach was to set a low uniform ground temperature and high stratiﬁed
temperature conditions inside the pond and simulate an extended period of time in order
to evaluate how the Tgr distributes after a large time period. The main drawback with
this approach was the computational cost, since 1 to 2 years of operation were needed
in order for the Tgr to increase suﬃciently and in a large distance from the pond. As
a result gravity and movement due to density diﬀerence were not considered and the
velocities inside the PTES were neglected including heat transfer from convection. As a
result the heat transfer coeﬃcients deriving from this simulation were not of great use.
However, the results on Tgr distribution were interesting, the concluded Tgr conﬁrmed
the hypothesis of parallel isotherms which consecrate at the surface.
As the ﬁnal approach it was decided to build a model simulating the heating of the
ground in more detail including the contribution of gravity, thus velocities and convective
heat losses. Time restrictions did not allow to simulate more than 4 months, however
the results on heat transfer coeﬃcients and ground distribution were combined with
measurement data providing a reliable approach on the heat losses to the ground.

The previous chapters outlined the operation of the PTES by describing Marstals en-

4.1.2
Periods Simulated
The large volume of the water pond comes along with an extended computational cost.
Due to time limitations an extended period of operation could not be simulated. The
numerical investigations thus focused in certain days of operation of the year.
The
selection of the investigated periods was based on choosing representative days of the
PTES operation for each season.
Conditions corresponding to four diﬀerent days were simulated, one for each season. The
aim was to depict how diﬀerent conditions, such as surrounding ground temperatures,
velocity magnitudes and diﬀerent use of inlets/outlet, inﬂuence the ponds conditions.
Moreover, in some of these days a diﬀerent stratiﬁcation design is also tested. Regarding
the simulation of the real pond design, the days simulated correspond to:
1. 7/October 2014, from 14:00 to 21:00.
2. 18/February 2015, from 02:00 to 09:00.
3. 22/April 2015, from 10:00 to 17:00.
4. 3/August 2015, from 10:00 to 20:00.
The cases of 7/10 and 22/4 were chosen because at that time the highest discharging
and charging were spotted correspondingly. In October the pond is discharged during
evening while in April the pond is charged at noon at times of high solar radiation. In
these cases the middle inlet is not in use while high volume ﬂow rates and velocities
are spotted.Since these are the cases were the largest heat is transferred to or from the
pond, the alternative stratiﬁcation design is also tested in these conditions.
During February the pond is cooled down, the thermal stratiﬁcation is destroyed in the
lower levels and the heat discharged from it during night time is relatively low. The
main reason for choosing a late February case, was to illustrate heat loss conditions on
that period and examine the thermal behavior of the ground. August is a hot summer
month, were thermal stratiﬁcation is established and both top and middle inlets are in
use. The initial approach was to simulate 7 hours of operation for August as for the
other months. However, temperature measurements inside the PTES indicated a much
lower heat gain, so the studied period was extended to 10 hours in order to obtain a
more accurate representation.

laminar ﬂow was assumed for the numerical solving. The contribution of gravity was

pressure velocity coupling.

increased computational time and accuracy. Nevertheless, in a preliminary study for
the pond with relatively close conditions, results with a time step of 2 and 5 seconds
were proven almost identical [31]. The maximum iterations per time step were set at
20.

Chapter 5
Discussion
During the present thesis project the water pond of Marstal’s solar heating plant was
studied. The examined period corresponds to a year of operation from 1st of October
2014 to 30th of September 2015. For this period the energy system of the PTES was
analyzed, concluding to a storage eﬃciency and heat losses from energy balances. The
heat losses have also been assessed in more detail and compared to the energy balance
results. Furthermore, CFD models investigated representative periods of the water pit’s
operation, illustrating ﬂow structures and evaluating thermal stratiﬁcation conditions.
Finally, an alternative stratiﬁcation arrangement has been suggested and compared to
the initial through CFD calculations.
Heat Losses
First of all, the energy ﬂows to and from the storage were evaluated through measure-
ments, for the corresponding period. The temperatures inside the pond ﬂuctuate from 20
to 84 oC. The temperature variation, the calculated charging/discharging of the storage
as well as the shift in the internal energy content of the pond are in good agreement with
Solites results [1], which suggested 2908 to 2945 MWh for 2014 and 2015 respectively.
The indicated heat losses of that year are 2950 MWh and the storage eﬃciency of the
PTES is estimated at 62%. This value is considered satisfactory, bearing in mind that
the advantage of such heat storage applications is not high eﬃciency but simplicity and
low costs.
The main heat loss sources taken into consideration were the losses from lid, ground
walls and transmission piping. The contribution of piping and ground was calculated,
while heat ﬂux measurements on the lid were used to estimate its contribution in the
losses. Regarding the piping, heat transfer calculations resulted in heat transfer rates
close to results of similar cases in the literature [28].
During preliminary studies of
the PTES, the contribution of piping was considered insigniﬁcant. However, it seems
that transmission piping accounts of about 5% of the heat losses calculated from energy
balance.
80
==PAGE:98==

81
In reference to the HL to the ground walls and bottom, it is known from measurements
and experience that the ground temperature close to the pond ﬂuctuates through the
year. The exact pattern close to the pond’s walls at large depths in not clear yet. Models
developed in ANSYS indicated the development of isotherms parallel to the pond’s
walls which concentrated at the top at lower temperatures. This pattern regarding the
temperature distribution as a function of the distance from the walls, except the top
level, is also conﬁrmed by the study of Hull et.al. [6]. Based on this pattern and some
correlations to real measured data, HL to ground were calculated with suitable ANSYS
models for each month. The result was higher losses close to the surface and restricted
heat losses at the lower levels and bottom.
The ground is estimated to account for about 600 MWh of losses, or 20%. It is crucial
to mention that the ground heats up during summer charging periods and acts as an
insulation during winter.
The discussed analysis indicates that in cases of February
and March, when the lowest water temperatures are spotted, the ground provides heat
back to the pond at the lower levels. Also, the years of operation are crucial regarding
ground temperature distribution. Measurement data suggests that its conditions begin
to stabilize after the third year of operation, maintaining the seasonal ﬂuctuations.
These patterns could be proven valuable when trying to predict the performance of the
storage at future times of operation.
While the ground accounts of about 20% of the yearly heat losses, these losses are not
distributed uniformly to the ground walls. The top 2 m of ground walls are responsible
for about 50% of the losses due to temperature drop of the ground near the top during
winter and the high water temperatures at the upper levels of the storage. The lower
levels and especially the bottom’s contribution is restricted. The bottom actually ap-
pears to heat up the water at the lower levels of the storage from December to March,
concluding to a neglectable HL sum throughout the year. It should be stated though
that the ground loss model developed in ANSYS was not accounting for charging/dis-
charging and it simulated a short time period. As a result these results depend also in
many assumptions and simpliﬁcations such as the assumption that the ground temper-
ature proﬁle follows the same distribution pattern through the year and that only its
magnitude is inﬂuenced. Another uncertainty is the way the T measurement at B 3 m
below the ground was used in order to correlate T proﬁle all over the ground. In reality
the ambient conditions could inﬂuence the measurements up to 4 m of ground depth at
some extreme winter cases.
The heat losses of the lid based on measured heat ﬂuxes are found to be 924 MWh, or
31% of these deriving from energy balance. This value is considered rather low, since 44%
of the expected heat losses remain unspeciﬁed. The heat ﬂux sensor in the lid is expected
to be the most unsafe parameter. There is a variety of phenomena concerning possible
losses from the top surface, including among others thermal bridges, the manhole, the
vacuum valves and ﬂaws in the insulation. It can be stated that the heat ﬂux sensor
==PAGE:99==

oﬀers a poor approximation of the heat losses from the top surface, this is also proven
by the CFD simulations and it will be discussed later on this chapter.
CFD Calculations
Regarding the CFD calculations, the models’ results have been validated with measure-
ment data and proven accurate. Thermal stratiﬁcation appears to be intact during the
cases of October, April and August, while during February it is destroyed at the lower
regions, as pointed out also in the measurements analysis. The higher velocities as well
as the most complex ﬂow structures appear close to the stratiﬁer. However the velocities’
magnitude is found to be relatively low, lower than 3 cm/s almost everywhere in the
pond volume. February case is an exception, since the provided stream in the bottom
is hotter than the lower water levels, causing buoyancy ﬂow and vortexes.
The heat losses for the CFD models have been simulated according to the previous
analysis, the lid boundary was set as the 250% of the average corresponding heat loss
sensor values. However, even with this adjustment the comparison between temperature
measurements and CFD results at the top water surface indicated underestimation of
the heat losses. The bottom’s contributions on the losses turned out neglectable again.
In regard to the energy balance in the CFD models, the internal energy change calculated
in CFD and measured is close in most cases but not in absolute agreement. Moreover,
the measured energy change is not in absolute agreement neither with the measured
charging.
The main reason behind this divergence is that the volume exchanged in
the simulated periods is relatively small, around 1 to 3%, having a negative eﬀect on
accuracy. Moreover, if the simulation was extended to times of no charging/discharging
perhaps the measurements would be in better agreement. This was done for August
and the results were improved, however in the rest of the cases charging/discharging
continued for many hours - even days- making any extend of the simulations really time
demanding. However the measured temperature proﬁle inside the pond was compared
to times of no operations and no signiﬁcant divergence was spotted. Moreover, the A2
sensor, which is placed at a corner of the pond, is rather unlikely to be inﬂuenced by
the inlets/outlet velocities. Overall, the main drawback of the CFD calculations was the
restricted periods of simulation due to increased computational cost.
With respect to the alternative stratiﬁcation designs, an arrangement of a large diameter
pipe on top side walls was suggested. The results were not conclusive, but it could be
argued that the large top inlet arrangement is suitable mostly for charging.
First of all, as stated in the previous chapter, the back ﬂow is the ﬁrst issue to be
confronted. CFD calculations showed that without a suitable arrangement, there would
be back ﬂow and mixing in the large pipe. As a result the velocities would increase
and the provided temperature would be reduced. A viable solution could be this of a
large pierced disc in the front, another approach could be for the pipe to be thinner and
adjust a wide nozzle in its end.
==PAGE:100==
Discussion
83
The alternative design with 2 m diameter was tested, in the October discharging period.
One of the main weaknesses of this design is that the bottom inlet jet is provided
directly vertically in the pond disturbing thermal stratiﬁcation. Another drawback of
this arrangement is that the large pipe acts as outlet at discharging periods. Since the
diameter of the pipe is 2 m, the absorbed water corresponds to wide range of temperature
layers, as a result the average outlet temperature is signiﬁcantly reduced as well as the
discharging of the pond.
Therefore, for the discharging periods various modiﬁcations could be suggested. The
bottom inlet vertical stream could be avoided, by installing a horizontal disc at the
bottom outlet, like the one existing in the initial steel stratiﬁer. Moreover, the large
pipe probably should not be avoided for discharging. Instead smaller and simpler pipes
could be placed horizontally on top and bottom of the large pipe in order to facilitate
discharging.
Regarding the charging case where the pipe would act as inlet, the velocities near the
inlet were reduced compared to the initial design, especially with the 4 m diameter pipe.
The developed temperatures on the top levels of the pond were reduced compared to
the initial design, accompanied with a reduction of heat losses from the lid due to lower
convective heat transfer. This pattern indicated that the provided hot stream did not
move to the top as fast and as hot as with the tower stratiﬁer, but it was distributed
smoothly.
Suggestions for Improvements
In terms of future consideration for heat losses, the lid is proven to be responsible for most
of the heat losses, as expected since the construction of the storage. Nonetheless, the
exact amount is diﬃcult to be determined since the heat ﬂux sensor does not describe the
actual heat transferred. Estimations of the heat losses from the lid should not be based
on data from the heat ﬂux sensor. The decision not to add insulation on the ground walls
is sound, since it would increase the costs signiﬁcantly without a corresponding beneﬁt
on the eﬃciency. However, installation of insulation only in the top 2 m of the ground
walls near the surface, where heat losses are intense, would reduce losses signiﬁcantly
while the cost would be much smaller than adding insulation all over the sides and
bottom. Moreover, in a relevant study of Berg at 1988 [30], extra insulation for the top
side walls is buried close to the ground. Burying 2 m depth of insulation around the pond
would certainly inﬂuence the ground temperature distribution positively. In terms of
geometry deeper ponds could be proven more eﬀective since the ratio of top surface area
to volume would drop. Nonetheless, these suggestions do not take into consideration
extra costs or diﬃculties in construction and they should be part of further studies.
The suggested stratiﬁcation arrangement could be proven a promising solution after
several modiﬁcations. These adjustments could be a pierced disc at the end of the pipe
to prevent mixing, a horizontal disc on top of the bottom outlet/inlet and some smaller
==PAGE:101==
Discussion
84
pipes at the top side to ease outﬂow from top levels. These modiﬁcations are relatively
simple and cheap, as the large horizontal pipe. The main diﬃculty and expense of that
design would be the connection of the HDPE liner to pipes in the side wall in order
to secure waterproof sealing. This is a challenging task, bearing in mind that the 2 m
diameter pipe would expand and subtract signiﬁcantly with temperature ﬂuctuations.
Nevertheless, the 15 m steel tower does not avoid the sealing complication. Moreover,
the welded steel tower is really expensive both in terms of manufacturing and installation
and it may come with maintenance and corrosion problems in the long term.
Future work
Pit thermal energy storages are a new emerging technology, it is clear that there is
large room for improvements and further research. One aspect, which the present study
did not investigate in detail, the ground temperature ﬂuctuations with time. It would
be really interesting to come up with a detailed pattern regarding the ground temper-
ature ﬂuctuations near the pond at an extensive time period and to validate it with
measurements. Instead of using the measurements as variables for the models.
In reference to CFD investigations, obviously the time of simulation is the most crucial
parameter. In the present model the thermal stratiﬁcation was set as an initial parameter
and it did not alter much during the simulation, due to the restricted volume exchange.
A future model could simulate the operation of the storage constantly for some weeks
or to a 100% volume exchange. In this way results on thermal stratiﬁcation, energy
balance and heat losses would be much more enlightening.
Further emphasis should also be given in the study of particular components. This could
be done by CFD models, closer to the stratiﬁcation components. These components
could be either the existing piping with the horizontal discs, either a future design such
as the large pipe inlet. In this way the prevention of mixing, backﬂow and turbulence
could be studied in detail.
To conclude, the present thesis highlights various aspects of the PTES operation. In
terms of heat losses it points out the importance of losses from transmission piping
which was neglected until now and evaluates the estimation of the lid’s heat ﬂux sensor
as very conservative. It also provides a glimpse at the temperature conditions of the
ground close to the pond’s walls which was not available, focusing on the heat losses
as a function of time period and position. Moreover, it illustrates the ﬂow structures
inside the water pond and the preservation of thermal stratiﬁcation. Finally, it suggests
improvements for future water pond heat storage applications, regarding insulation’s
inﬂuence and an alternative stratiﬁcation arrangement.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
The study explores the operation of Marstal’s heat storage water pond, both analytically
and in terms of CFD calculations and proposes optimizations for the pond including
a new stratiﬁer. The water pond heat storage solutions constitute a very promising
emerging sustainable technology, which faces great growth in Denmark. Nevertheless,
the relevant research is in an early stage at the moment. The study investigates the
origination of the heat losses, tries to determine the thermal stratiﬁcation of the pond
and the ﬂow structures at various occasions and ﬁnally seeks to answer whether a simpler
inlet stratiﬁer could be proven valuable.
With regard to heat losses, they reach about 2950 MWh per year, of the total 8350
MWh provided.
The contribution of transmission piping should be considered from
now on, since it accounts of about 5% of the losses. The ground walls contribute at
least 20% . The ground losses are mainly focused on summer months and about half
of it at the top 2 m of ground walls, while the bottom walls contribution was proven
negligible. This pattern indicates that a light insulation near the top ground walls could
prevent a large part of the ground losses. The lid is most likely to contribute the rest
heat losses; the exact amount is uncertain due to complexity of the relevant phenomena
at the top surface. It should be stated that the top surface heat ﬂux sensor does not
provide credible information on that matter.
The CFD models were validated with measurements as accurate.
They pointed out
preservation of thermal stratiﬁcation in times of intense operation and relatively sim-
ple ﬂow structures at most of the cases; except February, which was expected. The
comparison with measured data also implied underestimation of losses through the lid.
The energy balance comparison between temperature measurements, measured energy
ﬂows and CFD were in close but not absolute agreement, due to the limited volume
exchanged.
In reference to the suggested alternative large pipe inlet stratiﬁer, CFD calculations
showed that it is unsuitable for discharging, since it subtracts cooler water from the
86

higher levels. This could be confronted by thinner pipes at top to facilitate discharging.
Moreover, for both charging and discharging, a horizontal disc should be placed on top
of the bottom pipe to secure horizontal velocities. In terms of charging, the large inlet
pipe appears to be a viable, less complex and more cost eﬀective solution compared to
the large steel tower. The main issue still to be addressed, is the backﬂow and mixing in
the large pipe which was exposed by CFD. A feasible solution could be the placement
of a pierced vertical disc at the end of the large pipe, or a suitable nozzle arrangement.
This project oﬀered a valuable perspective of the ground temperature proﬁles, the water
thermal stratiﬁcation and velocities. However, it confronted various limitations regard-
ing computational time. Extended simulations of both ground temperature development
and pond’s operation need to be considered. Furthermore, CFD calculations focusing
on the alternative inlet stratiﬁer are proposed. In this way the inﬂuence of the large
pipe diameter as well as the arrangements for prevention of mixing could be examined.
To conclude, the largest advantage of pit thermal energy storage is simplicity.
The
present study enlightened various aspects of the Marstal’s water pond characteristics,
such as distribution of heat losses, thermal stratiﬁcation and ﬂow structures. Based
on that, noncomplex but critical modiﬁcations, including an alternative stratiﬁcation
arrangement, were suggested.
