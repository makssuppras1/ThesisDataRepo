Abstract 
Corporate venture capital (CVC) has grown in popularity over the last decades. While 
innovation is needed more than ever, balancing the risks and rewards of collaboration between 
start-ups and corporates during CVC investments remains an ongoing challenge. While 
research suggests that CVCs are mainly motivated by strategic benefits, there is a lack of 
understanding on how to achieve them. Especially the critical matter of collaboration between 
the CVC program and the start-up has not received major attention. While formal structures 
are more present in current research on how CVC programs interact with start-ups, there is a 
gap in understanding how CVC programs structure their interpersonal collaboration with start-
ups. Drawing on the current literature, this thesis explores both formal and informal types of 
collaboration from the perspective of CVC programs. By conducting interviews and surveys 
with CVC managers, I inductively examine various collaboration structures between CVC 
programs and start-ups. This study provides valuable insights for both CVC programs and start-
ups to foster fruitful collaboration.

1. Introduction 
Corporate venture capital (CVC) has gained in popularity and recognition as an effective tool 
for external innovation (Basu et al., 2016). Contrary to traditional independent venture capital 
firms (IVC), CVCs typically strive for strategic benefits, rather than financial return with the 
aim of gaining a window to new technologies and the creation of strategic awareness of 
disruptive innovation (Maula et al., 2013).  
The world is constantly changing, and the world’s economy changes too. A McKinsey study 
by Giordano et al. (2023) found that Europe has experienced slower economic growth than 
currently expected, and at the same time also lacks in key technologies. Furthermore, the study 
finds that the start-up ecosystem holds a massive potential, that mainly lacks in early-stage 
entrepreneurial activities. Taking Europe as one example, the potential of start-ups is not 
negligible and providing a relation to the current economic circumstances and the age of digital 
transformation (Steiber, 2020).  
While corporate innovation is historically driven by company internal research and 
development (R&D) departments (Kim & Choi, 2020), the first partnerships in the format of a 
CVC – start-up collaboration were seen in the late 1960s, corresponding with the rise in VC-
backed initial public offerings (IPOs) (Lerner, 2013). The ability of established companies to 
collaborate with start-ups and gain inside knowledge transfers (Dushnitsky & Shaver, 2009) 
provides a great benefit for the CVC and its parent company. Additionally, “[…], CVC may 
offer a valuable window on technology, as it provides an effective means of scanning the 
environment for novel technologies that either threaten or complement core businesses” 
(Dushnitsky & Lenox, 2006, p. 754), pointing out that the scouting process of the CVC for a 
potential collaboration is a benefit for the corporate parent in itself.  
Concluding, CVC activities of established corporations not only foster external innovation in 
collaboration with start-ups, but they are also benefiting the corporate parent firm in evaluating 
their competitiveness and enabling the future economy to grow. CVCs aim to bridge the gap 
between the corporate and start-up worlds by providing strategic benefits to the parent firm and 
giving start-ups funding and access to the complementary assets they need for further 
development (Ubarhande & Vanarse, 2024), providing benefits for both parties involved.

1.1 Problem statement and research question 
In current academic literature, structural configurations of CVCs are well documented and 
analyzed (e.g., Dushnitsky, 2009; Gutmann et al., 2019; Napp & Minshall, 2011; Rossi et al., 
2017; Szalavetz & Sauvage, 2024). CVCs have been categorized based on their integration 
level with their parent company to differentiate their operational setup (Dushnitsky, 2009). 
Pinkows and Iversens (2020) study suggests that the autonomy of the CVC with respect to the 
parent company influences the strategic outcome, but the relationship between the CVC and 
the start-up has not been broadly considered yet. Further, research highlights the importance of 
the internal collaboration between the CVC and internal R&D departments of the corporate 
parent (Birgl et al., 2018).  
Current academic findings on CVC – start-up collaboration, regarding contractual formal 
structures, are very limited and mainly focus on board representation (e.g. S. Basu et al., 2016). 
For the informal structures, Weber and Weber (2010), (2011) highlighted the importance of 
mutual trust, shared norms and the presence of a relational fit. While both formal and informal 
structures are individually partly acknowledged, in-depth analysis and the interdependency 
between both dimensions remain largely unexplored. Additionally, current academic literature 
is lacking in further insights on how CVC managers perceive and balance both formal and 
informal mechanisms to gain strategic insights from that collaboration for their corporate 
parent firm. Consequently, the following research question needs to be stated and answered in 
this study:  
- 
How do corporate venture capital firms perceive and structure formal and 
informal collaboration with start-ups to achieve strategic benefits?

2. Literature review  
The following section covers the literature review, providing an overview of current academic 
findings present and identifying a research gap. The chapter is separated by the explanation of 
key terminologies, the CVC – parent collaboration, the CVC and parent company internal R&D 
connection, the formal and informal ties between the CVC and the start-up, existing findings 
of the interdependency of both formal and informal ties and finishes with an interims 
conclusion on the literature review and revealing a research gap. 
2.1 Key terminologies 
To ensure clarity and consistency throughout this study, it is necessary to define key 
terminologies. The following section ensures the above: 
Definition Venture Capital :  
Typically, venture capital (VC) is defined as “[…] the investment by professional investors of 
long-term, unquoted, risk equity finance in new firms where the primary reward is an eventual 
capital gain, supplemented by dividend yield” (Wright & Robbie, 1998, p. 521). Being static 
and compact, the above-mentioned understanding of VC will be used as a basis in the 
following.  
Definition Corporate Venture Capital:  
Corporate venture capital (CVC) is an established term, known as an investment made by 
established firms in start-up companies (Dushnitsky, 2009; Ubarhande & Vanarse, 2024). 
Aiming for knowledge transfer and interorganizational learning, companies strive to access 
new technologies for continuous development and maintaining their competitiveness by these 
investments (Ireland et al., 2009; Schmidt et al., 2025). First CVC activity has been seen around 
the 1960s (X. Wu et al., 2020). Therefore, the idea of corporate business development by taking 
a minority equity stake in a start-up (Dushnitsky, 2009) is not a recent phenomenon. Driven by 
strategic benefits and internal renewal, CVCs value the strategic benefits of collaborating with 
start-ups to a higher degree than their financial returns (Dushnitsky & Lenox, 2006). In current 
research, not much variance in the definition of CVC is present. Thus, the definition of 
Ubarhande and Vanarse (2024) is used as a basis, with the additional focus on the strategic 
development of the parent company found by Dushnitsky and Lenox (2006). 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
4 
 
Definition Independent Venture Capital:  
Independent venture capital (IVC) firms are private investment entities that are operating 
independently of larger financial institutions or corporations (Gompers, 2007). These 
investment firms can follow different goals and therefore, contrary to CVCs, IVCs are mainly 
interested in the financial outcomes of their investments (LiPuma, 2006). Consequently, IVCs 
are trying to guide their portfolio companies into a different direction, aiming for the highest 
financial exit outcome, leading to an IPO (Cumming & Johan, 2008).  
IVCs themselves are financed by a limited partner structure, where limited partners provide the 
majority of capital and general partners manage the funds and make the investment decisions 
(Da Rin & Hellmann, 2020). Furthermore, IVCs have a fund horizon of 10 years, with the main 
goal of covering their operational expenses, and the incentive to maximize the final fund value 
(Barrot, 2017).  
Definition start-up: 
For the term start-up, no scientific standardized definition is present. Research provides a very 
broad range of definitions, with significant variations in the level of detail. The most 
generalized definition states start-ups are “[…] young firms with high growth ambitions” 
(Reypens et al., 2020 p.5), published in a study for the European Investment Bank. Other 
studies have indicated that start-ups are typically associated with technology-based business 
ideas (Fauziah et al., 2022), the creation of distinctive and unique products or services (Gera 
& Miglani, 2023), and the challenge of realizing innovative business ideas with limited 
resources (Yim, 2008). Furthermore, Skala (2019) found that the main characteristic of a start-
up is the possibility of a rapid increase of value and therefore its scalability. The most detailed 
definition of the term start-up is found in the work of Davila and Foster (2005), where a start-
up consists of minimum 50 and maximum 150 employees, has an existing age below 10 years, 
is independent and operates in a limited geographical area.  
This study takes the perspective of the CVC, and therefore considers companies the CVC gets 
a minority share in as start-ups. As CVC value innovation and knowledge transfer (Dushnitsky 
& Lenox, 2006), it is expected that the above mentioned innovation drivers are the main 
characteristics from a CVC perspective and thus define the term start-up for them. This is also 
seen as CVCs are especially active in technology-heavy industries (Park & Steensma, 2012). 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
5 
Definition pre-investment phase:  
The pre-investment phase describes the active deal sourcing of a CVC, solving the need of 
renewal and willingness to use a CVC as a corporate business vehicle opportunity (Röhm, 
2018). This phase usually includes the creation or activation of an existing network, by 
contacting IVC firms, to get an overview of the current market situation (Röhm, 2018; Weber 
& Weber, 2011). Simultaneously, start-ups seek opportunities, that enable themselves to 
continue their business idea (Park & Steensma, 2012). CVCs therefore display an attractive 
collaboration partner, that offers the unique opportunity to provide access to resources 
potentially more valuable than the monetary funding itself (Ubarhande & Vanarse, 2024). 
These resources include access to research, network, distribution and more (Ivanov & Xie, 
2010). Dushnitsky and Shaver (2009) found that an industry-overlap between the parent 
company and the targeted start-up, significantly influences the interest of the CVC 
Furthermore, they argue that an existing industry overlap raises the likelihood of a 
collaboration, as it allows for greater precision in the due diligence and investigation process 
of the potential collaboration.  
Definition post-investment phase:  
After the initial investment by the CVC, the post- investment phase starts. This phase is shaped 
by the active collaboration between the now portfolio company and the CVC. From an investor 
perspective, this phase is primarily focused on the transfer of existing knowledge and 
technology, as well as the creation of new knowledge and network resources (Lavie, 2006). 
According to the bilateral agreements, reporting, monitoring and control as well as 
collaboration types can be adjusted and enhanced.  
Definition exit-phase: 
The collaboration between the CVC and the start-up does not need to last forever, looking 
ahead investors practice strategies to cash in on their investments (Guo et al., 2015). Research 
defines exit paths as the withdrawal of the investors capital in one of the following ways: 1) 
acquisition, the sale to another business or independent entity, 2) buyout, sale to the companies 
employees, or management 3) going public through an IPO, 4) failure and the liquidation of 
the company (Birley & Westhead, 1993).  
As CVCs themselves have inside information and are therefore more qualified to screen their 
portfolio companies, identifying enhanced potential and are theoretically more qualified to 
==PAGE:13==

pursue an exit via acquisition (Weniger et al., 2023). Contrary to the theoretical possibility that 
CVCs might buy their portfolio companies, Guo et al. (2015) showed that only 5% of start-up 
acquisitions were formally part of the buyers CVC program. When discussing the general 
practice of exits, empirical studies have come to different results. While one side of research 
argues that the majority of CVC backed start-ups exit via acquisition (Chemmanur &

likely to exiting via IPO (Chemmanur & Loutskina, 2009; Gompers & Lerner, 2019). 
Furthermore, current scientific findings point out that the exit path is mainly dependent on 
external factors, e.g. IVC – CVC syndication, and not the participation of a CVC (Weniger et 
al., 2023). Keeping the beforehand scientific disagreement in mind, Weniger et al. (2023) 
found, that from the perspective of an entrepreneur it is less likely to exit via IPO, then via 
acquisition or buyout.

2.2 CVC – parent collaboration  
Based on the definitions above, the collaboration between start-ups and established firms 
mainly reasoned in the need of strategic renewal of the parent firm and the start-ups funding 
need or seek for complementary resources (Ubarhande & Vanarse, 2024). When defining the 
collaboration interest between the CVC and the start-up, the CVC’s strategic objectives are 
many times the following (Pinkow & Iversen, 2020): 
- 
Improve the core business 
- 
Enhancing the ecosystem  
- 
Gaining access to new technologies and markets 
To achieve these goals, companies have different philosophies when creating their CVC unit, 
responsible for the corporate start-up collaboration. The work of Dushnitsky (2009) defines 
three different CVC structures, which describe the way of collaborating, ranking from a loose 
connection to the parent company to a tight relationship:  
- 
Dedicated VC fund  
- 
Wholly owned subsidiary  
- 
Direct investment 
The weakest degree of affiliation with the parent company is found in a dedicated VC fund, 
this fund is represented by a CVC cooperating with an IVC fund (Dushnitsky, 2009). The 
second type of program employs a subsidiary that undertakes investment activities 
disconnected but in the interest of the parent company, while the subsidiary remains under full 
legal ownership of the parent (Danneels & Miller, 2023; Frey & Kanbach, 2024). The direct 
CVC investment strategy is characterized by its close alignment with the parent company. The 
responsibility for CVC investments is typically assigned to a specific business unit that is 
integrated within the existing operating business entity (Dushnitsky, 2009). These direct 
investments often have a long term capital commitment (Schroeder, 2021).  
The degree of autonomy of the CVC from the parent firm is broadly covered in research, 
especially when elaborating on the success of CVC programs. The autonomy of a CVC is 
defined by its independence from the parent firm, with a particular reference to its access to 
key resources, their ability to realize human resource decisions and to carry out investment 
actions (Gutmann et al., 2019). Pinkow and Iversen (2020) found in their study, that the degree 
of autonomy has a major impact, depending on the CVC’s main goal. They conclude that CVCs 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
8 
with low autonomy provide the best results in activities that strengthen the core business of the 
parent entity, while CVCs with a high degree of autonomy are fruitful when striving for new 
technology insights and markets for the parent to explore.  
With different programs and different degrees of autonomy, the market of CVCs is constantly 
changing and therefore often creating hybrid vehicles, not only striving for strategic goals but 
also valuing financial outcomes, as they are seen as a necessity to survive (Rossi et al., 2017; 
Szalavetz & Sauvage, 2024). While not only pursuing one goal, the degree of autonomy also 
needs to be adjusted to a middle ground between an exploitation oriented, and an exploration 
oriented CVC (Napp & Minshall, 2011; Pinkow & Iversen, 2020). 
The findings of Dushnitsky (2009) can therefore not be seen as a strict to follow framework, 
but can be assumed as a guideline while analyzing CVC structures. Similar to these findings, 
the work of Napp and Minshall (2011) concludes that there is not a one-size-fits-all solution 
and recommend defining the following, before setting up a CVC:  
1. Defining clear objectives, including prioritizing financial and strategic objectives. 
Determine, if the CVC focuses on explorational or exploitational value creation. 
2. Highlight the importance of the relationship between both the management of the parent 
company and the one of CVC as well as appropriate ties between the CVC and the 
portfolio company. These connections are important ties, strongly related to the ability 
of value creation for the parent company. 
3. Use a metrics system, tailored to the program objectives. This allows the CVC to 
evaluate the program’s outcomes and gives opportunity for adjustment.

2.3 Interfacing CVC with internal research and development activities 
In the context of internal collaboration, the relationship between the CVC and the R&D unit of 
the parent firm is of high importance. According to the Organization for Economic Co-
operation and Development (OECD), “Research and experimental development comprise 
creative and systematic work undertaken in order to increase the stock of knowledge – 
including knowledge of humankind, culture and society – and to devise new applications of 
available knowledge” (OECD, 2015. p. 44). For continuous business development, 
corporations have been operating such business units with the purpose of both producing 
innovation and fostering their ability to create absorptive capabilities to facilitate learning 
(Cohen & Levinthal, 1990). With the introduction of open innovation, the focus on external 
learning abilities becomes more important. Contrary to the previously closed innovation, in 
open innovation external ideas and external pathways reach the same level of importance that 
previously was reserved for in-house internal ideas and pathways (Chesbrough, 2003). 
Following the author’s work, achieving a competitive advantage does not need to exclusively 
be based on a newly discovered resource, it can also be achieved by combining knowledge 
from different sources to create a new value-adding strategy. When considering R&D and CVC 
as possible drivers of innovation Chesbrough and Tucci (2004) found that for technologies and 
markets close to the firms core business, internal R&D provides the best results. Consequently, 
if the innovation is in unknown territory, the use of a CVC may be advisable. These findings 
are in line with previous research, stating that small and new firms (start-ups) foster radical 
innovation, while internal R&D is better suited for incremental innovation within in large 
corporations (Yin & Zuscovitch, 1998).  
In academic literature a degree of consensus is seen, defining R&D as complementary to CVC 
activities, contrary to the intuitively expected rivalry (Cassiman & Veugelers, 2002; 
Dushnitsky & Lenox, 2005; Gompers & Lerner, 2001). When further uncovering the 
relationship between CVC and R&D departments, research finds that with rising R&D 
investments of corporations, their CVC activity and success rate in deal closure rise as well 
(Sahaym et al., 2010). In order to conduct successful R&D, individual openness to external 
knowledge has been seen as successful in developing value creating solutions (Sahaym et al., 
2010). Due to this awareness, R&D departments tend to have a keen sensitivity of current 
scientific development in the field of business. Furthermore, R&D departments tend to have 
good connections to high education facilities that are engaged in cutting edge research 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
10 
(Gompers & Lerner, 2001). By using their learning and absorptive capabilities, R&D units are 
enabled to discover opportunities for transformation (Zahra & George, 2002). This 
understanding of current research and development creates the ability to evaluate current 
technology development and makes R&D business units a major consultant of CVCs in their 
decision making (Sahaym et al., 2010). While operating both CVC and R&D activities, Birgl 
et al. (2018) found in a BCG global study that the collaboration between R&D and CVC needs 
to be embedded in an overall corporate innovation approach and furthermore requires reward 
for the R&D unit when leveraging their abilities to promote the CVC in their operations. 
Research also confirms, that R&D investments substantially increase the attractiveness of 
CVCs (Sahaym et al., 2010).

2.4 Corporate start-up collaboration (Portfolio relationship management)  
Moving from relationships inside the parent company, the link between the CVC and the start-
up has been underrepresented in current research. In the scientific field, the fruitful 
collaboration between a CVC and a start-up is not uncontroversial. While CVC’s are often part 
of an established corporation’s renewal and innovation strategy (Drover et al., 2017), other 
scientific findings counter that the goal of internal innovation can only be reached through 
acquisition instead of collaboration (Lu & Li, 2024). To underline the findings of Lu and Li 
(2024) the expected lifespan of a CVC is on average 6 years, and therefore unable to fulfill its 
strategic contribution (Ma, 2019), as the expected CVC investment horizon reaches between 7 
and 10 years (Barrot, 2017; Gompers & Lerner, 2000). Despite this disagreement in scientific 
analysis, the real-world development of active CVC units shown a compounded annual growth 
rate (CAGR) of 13% over the time period of 2014-2024 (Palmer, 2025). CVCs therefore 
demonstrated their resilience to survive, even in economically challenging circumstances, 
including the global COVID-19 pandemic. The presence of these steady growth rates are 
noteworthy, but they do not hide the structural and conceptual problems CVCs tend to have.  
To provide structure in the collaboration between the CVC and the portfolio company, the work 
of Gutmann et al. (2019) categorizes the structures of CVCs based on both their value creation 
services for their portfolio companies on one hand and the value capturing services on the other 
hand. Value creation services help the portfolio company to develop their core activities, 
including an operating business and the creation of a marketable service or product. Value 
capturing services help the venture to build direct revenue and establish important relations, 
both inside and outside the CVC, aiming for additional revenue streams. Consequently, CVCs 
tend to adopt one of the following service delivery orientations for the portfolio company, as 
illustrated in the following taxonomy of CVC unit types (Gutmann et al., 2019):

2.5 Formal and informal ties between CVC and their portfolio companies 
The collaboration between the CVC and the start-up is depending on both a formal and informal 
relationship. In the following, recent academic findings will be displayed:  
 
2.5.1 Formal requirements  
Formal requirements define the cooperation between the CVC and the start-up on a contractual 
and legal level. These contractual frameworks are commonly regulating monitoring and 
controlling activities of the CVC with respect to the portfolio company. The work of Cumming 
(2006) highlights the importance of contractual agreements as the agreement influences 1) the 
division of cash flow and therefore investor’s profits, 2) contracts directly influence the actions 
of both the entrepreneur and the investor and 3) contracts also clarify the exit process and 
related profits.  
 
CVC – IVC syndication  
Research defines the syndication of venture capital firms as the joint funding of a venture 
(Lerner, 1994). Historically, these syndications were based on different IVC firms, who wanted 
to mitigate the investment risk and improve their portfolio management (Dimov & Milanov, 
2010; Lockett & Wright, 2001). Although IVCs and CVCs tend to pursue similar goals, the 
IVCs primarily financial objectives are contrary to the strategic goals of CVCs and therefore 
tend to be viewed as conflicting (Kang, 2019). Similar results were found by Colombo and 
Murtinu (2017), stating that a syndication might provide benefits to the start-up in providing 
access to both capital and complementary resources, but resulting in conflicts of interest inside 
the syndication itself. Despite these differences, Dushnitsky and Shapira (2010) found that a 
majority of CVC investments were not done alone, but in a CVC - IVC syndication. This can 
be explained by the missing ability of CVCs to identify a suitable target, and therefore resulting 
in a dependency on their existing networks of IVCs (Sorenson & Stuart, 2008). In addition, 
Hallen et al. (2014) suggest that an IVC - CVC syndication has been preferably selected by 
entrepreneurs as a risk mitigation strategy to address concerns regarding technology 
misappropriation.  
==PAGE:23==
 
 
16 
The structural approach of a venture capital syndication, independent of the involvement of a 
CVC, includes one lead investor and the additional members of the syndicate are considered 
followers (J. Wu et al., 2021). The lead investor is defined as the initiator of the investment 
project (Li & Mahoney, 2011) and commonly has the largest equity stake in the start-up (Sufi, 
2007). Moreover, the authors find that the leading investor is responsible for the collaboration 
with the start-up as well as the monitoring of the investment and for continuously informing 
the remaining syndicate members on the current state of development. Consequently, the 
leading investor has an advantage, due to its direct contact and observer role as well as its 
decision-making abilities (Chakraborty & Ewens, 2018). 
The contractual framework between the syndicate and the external venture is also the 
responsibility of the leading investor (Basu et al., 2016; Sufi, 2007). Therefore, research does 
not provide extensive contractual knowledge of CVC – start-up connections. If a CVC is 
providing a contractual framework between itself and the start-up, the following clauses are in 
particular securing the interest of the CVC:  
 
CVC downside protection:  
As previously outlined, CVCs tend to invest in start-ups that are either closely related to or 
within the same field as their parent company. This limits the range of investment options 
available to them, thus also limiting their ability to diversify their portfolios. Consequently, 
CVCs tend to safeguard themselves against downside risks, by extending their control rights, 
focusing on drag along and redemption rights (Cumming, 2006). Drag along rights enable the 
majority owner of a company to force the minority shareholder to engage in the disposal of the 
company (Klonowski, 2018; Orien & Little, 2016). In the context of investor – start-up 
relationship, the drag along right is employed to ensure that the investor has the ability to 
withdraw from the collaboration with a portfolio company, in case that a portfolio company 
has not reached the previously agreed-upon goals or objectives. Thus, the investor can force an 
involuntarily exit and deliver the full venture to a willing buyer (Klonowski, 2018).  
As an additional layer of downside protection, investors also include redemption rights into 
their contractual agreements. Redemption rights describe the contractual ability of the investor 
to force the start-up to buy back its own shares after a predefined time period at a predefined 
price (Cumming & Johan, 2008). This downside risk protection serves the same purpose as the 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
17 
drag along rights described above. The investor protects themself by predefining an exit option, 
in case of unexpected underperformance of the portfolio company.  
 
Control and veto rights explanation:  
According to the findings of Cumming and Johan (2008), CVCs are more likely to take control 
rights from more experienced entrepreneurs. Wadhwa and Kotha (2006) suggest that CVCs, 
who monitor their investments through board memberships or observation rights are more 
efficient in achieving the jointly defined goals. Being part of the board of directors of the 
portfolio company enables the CVC to gain insights into the ventures development and to 
ensure alignment with the strategic interest of the parent firm (Basu et al., 2016). While the 
above shows the positively related outcomes for the CVC of a board membership, the findings 
of Strebulaev and Wang (2021) observe that CVC investors are far less likely to take a board 
seat compared to IVC firms. Furthermore, the authors discovered a contradiction in the 
landscape of CVCs: While some CVC justify their refusal of a board membership or observer 
role with the fear of exposing the parent to the risk of a shareholder lawsuit, other CVCs see 
the ability to join the board as a non-negotiable parameter for a successful collaboration. 
 
Collaborative blueprints  
Aside from legal frameworks, S. Basu et al. (2016) found that CVCs who minimize the 
complexity of deal negotiations and respect to the start-ups interests, they themselves gain in 
reputation among start-ups as well as IVCs for future syndication. Furthermore, the authors 
found that the creation of a collaborative blueprint leads to a gain in social contacts and 
improving the integration of the venture. Danneels & Miller (2023) found in their work, that 
these formal structures are often missing resulting in miscommunication and knowledge 
transfer that can be conducted in a limited way. Following these findings, the creation of a 
predefined blueprint can be considered as an item, rising the attractiveness of a CVC.

2.5.2 Informal requirements  
The collaboration between a CVC and the start-up is not only based on the contractual rules 
and agreements between the parties, it has an informal part, mainly based on interpersonal 
connection. Starting with the element of social capital, the further analysis of existing literature 
will be clustered by pre- and post-investment phases. 
Social capital: 
The term social capital is defined as “[…] an instantiated informal norm that promotes 
cooperation between two or more individuals” (Fukuyama, 1999, p. 1). A more elaborate 
definition of social capital is given by “[social capital is] the sum of the actual and potential 
resources embedded within, available through, and derived from the network of relationships 
possessed by an individual or social unit. Social capital thus comprises both the network and 
the assets that may be mobilized through that network” (Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998, p. 243). 
This concept of social capital can be added to the complexity of CVC – start-up collaboration. 
Next to the above shown contractual frameworks, the relationship between the CVC and the 
future portfolio company includes elements of interpersonal components. Specific components 
of social capital include trust, social networks, informal organization, and social support 
(Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998). Social networks are commonly understood as creating and 
holding social capital (Weber & Weber, 2011), therefore the definition of social capital by 
Nahapiet and Ghoshal (1998) provides an alternative, also pointing out that the creation of 
social capital may not occur. In this case, a collaboration can turn into a social liability 
(Sandefur et al., 1999).  
Social capital is frequently rated as a crucial element that enables knowledge transfer and 
therefore value creation among business partners (Maula et al., 2003). When describing social 
capital in the context of a CVC – start-up constellation, it refers to the intraorganizational 
network structure of the CVC and its parent company. This including a clear linkage of 
ownership and decision making, organizational structure as well as cultural differences and 
similarities between the headquarters and its subsidiaries, e.g., the CVC (Inkpen & Tsang, 
2005). Furthermore, there is the interorganizational connection between the CVC and the start-
up. Interorganizational connections are defined by their structural and procedural links, created 
by communication and information sharing utilizing their connection (Shumate et al., 2016).

The following is a review of recent research on the pre- and post-investment phases of the CVC 
- start-up relationship: 
 
Pre-investment phase:  
The pre-investment phase describes the active deal sourcing of a CVC, solving the need of 
renewal and willingness to use a CVC as a corporate business vehicle opportunity (Röhm, 
2018). Therefore, CVCs are following the interest of creating a new social network (Weber & 
Weber, 2011). Furthermore, the authors find that the creation of an interorganizational network 
requires the CVC manager to act as a connector, broker and facilitator, responsible for reaching 
out to possible portfolio companies.  
When following the investment process, right after the initial contact to possible portfolio 
firms, the due diligence process starts. When starting the conversation, the familiarity between 
the CVC and the start-up is very limited. This information asymmetry is common, as access to 
information is not equally distributed among all investors and investees (Stiglitz, 2000). 
Furthermore, the presence of information asymmetry establishes a boundary that limits access 
to knowledge, thereby rendering the investment riskier for the CVC (Zhang & Guo, 2023). The 
interviews conducted by Weber & Weber (2011) showed that in the pre-investment phase the 
relationship is more distanced and both parties are aiming for the maximization of their 
individual benefit. Moreover, the authors find that the CVC is interested in identifying a 
suitable potential portfolio company, that fosters collaboration and thereby generates social 
capital while avoiding the risk of becoming a potential social liability. As the information 
asymmetry is not only one sided, the attractiveness of a partnership with a CVC depends on its 
reputation (Anokhin et al., 2022). Moreover, the authors find that start-ups value experience 
higher than potentially unsuccessful collaboration with previous portfolio companies in the 
CVC’s history. Consequently, a good reputation in CVC – start-up collaboration can be seen 
as a competitive advantage for the CVC.  
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
20 
Post-investment phase:  
As described above, the pre-investment phase focuses on the initial connection between the 
CVC and the potential portfolio company. Following this initial stage in collaboration between 
the CVC and start-up collaboration, the post-investment phase starts. From the perspective of 
the CVC, the main goals of the post-investment phase consist of the transfer of knowledge and 
resources, leading to the creation of new knowledge as well as new network resources (Lavie, 
2006). In that context Weber and Weber (2011) found that the realization of these goals is 
dependent on initial social capital created. Furthermore, they state that the concrete 
collaboration between the start-up and CVC starts after this initial social capital has been 
created. Maula et al.(2009) highlight in their research the importance of clarifying the existence 
of complementary and relatedness of resources present in the newly created collaboration and 
using this information as a basis for evaluating how the future collaboration should be 
managed. The authors provide a definition of a complement of resources as follows: The 
presence of one resource directly enhances the effectiveness of another party’s resources. The 
relatedness of resources is defined as an overlap or even substitution of knowledge between 
the collaboration partners.  
The above mentioned findings are also confirmed by a framework from Weber & Weber (2010) 
that indicates that knowledge transfer depends on three main components: 1) social capital, 2) 
relational fit and 3) knowledge relatedness. The element of social capital, as described above 
in the social capital section, focuses on creating a social network and establishing contact with 
potential portfolio companies from a CVC perspective (Weber & Weber, 2010, 2011). To 
further follow the framework, the relational dimension between the CVC and the start-up is 
broken down in conative fit, affective fit, shared norms, and trust. The conative fit is a 
prerequisite for successful collaboration between two entities, ensuring the same intentions are 
shared, the commitment to the project is equally distributed and both parties reach similar or 
complementary goals (Gemünden et al., 1999). A good conative fit, driven by project 
commitment or any kind of cooperative motivation can also be seen as essential to solve 
internal conflicts (Ring & van de Ven, 1994). Transitioning from the aligned motivation and 
commitment, the affective fit describes the emotional and functional compatibility on the level 
of individuals or teams (Weber & Weber, 2010). Early CVC – start-up relationships display an 
untested connection, where emotions have a communicative relevance (Scherer & Tran, 2001). 
Furthermore, the authors state that individuals cannot behave rationally, and therefore 
underlines the importance for a good affective fit between the CVC and the start-up. Next up, 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
21 
when analyzing the element of a relational fit, is the common understanding of applicable 
norms. Social norms create a mutual understanding of accepted behavior within a group, 
providing a framework of accepted interpersonal behavior that social interactions need to 
follow (Van Kleef et al., 2019). The last element of relational fit between the CVC and the 
portfolio company deals with trust. Identifying and further analyzing the capabilities of both 
the CVC and the portfolio company requires the creation of trust to be initially present and 
further established in the post-investment phase (Weber & Weber, 2011). The general 
definition of trust contains the willingness of one party to expose itself to potential risk through 
the actions of another, based on the belief that the other will act in a way that supports the 
trusting party’s important interests, even without the ability to monitor or control those actions 
(Mayer et al., 1995). Following this definition, research finds trust as an element enhancing the 
overall organizational and partnership performance, reducing the cost of monitoring by limiting 
the risk of opportunism (Weber & Weber, 2010). Furthermore, research states that trust needs 
to be seen as a core component that affects the successful process of knowledge transfer, 
especially from a CVC perspective (Rutten et al., 2016).  
Continuing with knowledge transfer, the general goal of the CVC is to gain insights from their 
portfolio companies in valuable, rare and hard-to-get knowledge, focusing on newly developed 
technologies or markets (Barney, 1991). The knowledge transfer process is further divided into 
present fact-based hard knowledge (knowing what) and knowledge with enabling capabilities 
(knowing how). Weber and Weber (2010) further discussed in their paper to what degree 
complementary knowledge creates a basis for mutual understanding and to what degree a 
sufficient diversity of knowledge bases is required to foster continuous innovation and 
knowledge creation. Their research proves that an optimal collaboration is possible if both 
parties share a moderate amount of explicit knowledge enabling clear communication and a 
decent amount of common tacit knowledge to foster efficient learning. Following the research 
of Maula et al. (2009) the authors provide evidence that an enhanced level of social interaction 
between the CVC and portfolio company enhances the learning benefits to the portfolio 
company, while the two sided benefits are outcomes of complementary knowledge increased 
and realized through collaborative social interactions. Similar results are provided by Lee et al. 
(2015), stating that successful knowledge transfer core component is diversity in the knowledge 
base. Yang (2012) indicates in his research that knowledge transfer is also dependent on the 
CVC managers intrinsic and compensational related motivation. Furthermore, the author 
highlights the mediating role of the CVC and its underlying program structure, firstly indicating 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
22 
an interdependency between the formal and informal structures discussed and further identified 
later in this paper. Ghoshal and Bartlett (1988) found that strongly tied relationships among 
firms include regular communication creating a fruitful mechanism for knowledge transfer. 
Aligning with these findings, Yang (2012) found that especially CVCs have problems in 
absorbing gained knowledge from portfolio companies. In conclusion, current research leaves 
the “black box” of knowledge transfer strategies unopened.

2.6 Interdependencies in formal and informal structures  
In the following section, an overview of current literature findings, regarding the 
interdependencies between formal and informal structures and the effects on CVC – start-up 
collaboration will be presented.  
Like most collaborations, conflicts of interest (CoI) are existing and influential on future 
outcomes, also present in CVC – start-up collaboration. When analyzing CVC – start-up 
collaboration, the “swimming with the sharks” dilemma is very prominent. Katila et al. (2008) 
examine in their paper the way entrepreneurs navigate the tension between the need for 
complementary resources (by the CVC) and the existing disclosure risk when forming 
investment ties. The findings state that entrepreneurs tend to accept higher disclosure risks, 
depending on their resource needs, combined with the ability of the entrepreneur to defend 
their own assets through mechanisms like secrecy and timing. The work of Maula et al. (2009) 
further investigates the collaboration, analyzing how social interaction and the presence of 
safeguards influence the realized learning benefits and realized risks from the perspective of 
the portfolio company. The authors conclude that when successfully collaborating, both parties 
need to accept a series of dynamic trade-offs, accepting disclosure from the perspective of the 
portfolio company and the existence of safeguards that limit the access to portfolio internal 
information, from the CVC perspective. Furthermore, they are highlighting the individuality of 
the relationship and the dependency on risk-readiness of the entrepreneur and CVC. By 
combining formal safeguards and social ties a fist implication on an interdependency between 
formal and informal structures in the CVC – start-up relationship is given. To further narrow 
down the interaction between the CVC and the portfolio company, the CVC managers abilities 
come into focus. The work of Dokko and Gaba (2012) argues that CVC managers 
implementing new practices and modify them, based on prior personal experience and by 
adapting these practices to suit the CVCs environment. Furthermore, the authors find that if 
externally hired CVC managers understand the development and strategic goals of the CVC, 
these managers tend to adjust their operative behavior to align with the goals of the CVC. Keil 
et al. (2008) found that CVC managers also play a crucial role in knowledge transfer, pointing 
out that CVC managers can act as knowledge transfer brokers between the CVC and the start-
up. Consequently, it is reasonable to state that the direct touchpoint between the CVC manager 
and the start-up could also play an influential role, while balancing the social interaction 
between both parties. These findings show a first indication that the interplay between formal 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
24 
and informal structures play a crucial role in the CVC – start-up relationship. Taking the 
perspective of the CVC, current academic literature does not display further details on how 
formal and informal structures interplay, especially in the perspective of gaining strategic 
insides for the CVC and its parent firm.

2.7 Interims conclusion on literature review  
To recapitulate on the literature review, key terminologies and the importance of CVCs and 
their role in corporate business development were explained and provided. Furthermore, an 
analysis of the CVC – parent collaboration, CVC – R&D unit collaboration and the CVC – 
start-up collaboration was done. To further analyze the formal and informal structures and their 
interdependencies, a selection in pre-, and post-investment phase was executed. While the 
general structural development of a CVC was well provided in research (e.g., Dushnitsky, 
2009; Gutmann et al., 2019; Napp & Minshall, 2011; Rossi et al., 2017; Szalavetz & Sauvage, 
2024) further details on formal and contractual collaboration between the CVC and the start-
up were lacking in details. The interpersonal relationship between both the CVCs parent and 
between the CVC and the portfolio company is not provided in detail, while Weber and Weber 
(2010, 2011) covered the impact of social capital in CVC, no interpersonal or detailed practices 
were discussed. Moreover, the interdependency between formal and informal ties between the 
CVC and the start-up is not discussed in current research. Consequently, a research gap has 
been identified. It includes understanding how formal and informal structures are established 
between the CVC and the start-up, how they depend on each other, and how they help the CVC 
achieve its strategic goals.

snowballing method was applied, a systematic approach of identifying relevant academic

3.2.2 Interview description and process  
For convenience and logistical reasons of both the interviewee and the researcher, all 
interviews were conducted online, utilizing Microsoft Teams. To provide high transparency, 
all interview participants received the interview guideline, including all the questions, before 
the meeting for review. Furthermore, all participants were ensured that the gathered data will 
not be shared with any third party, nor an open publication of the raw data is planned. If 
specifically requested, the assurance was formalized into writing. Due to complete 
anonymization of the gathered data, backtracking of quotes by any CVC manager is not 
possible.  
To gain a deeper understanding and detailed data collection, semi-structured interviews with 
CVC managers were conducted. Semi-structured interviews are defined as a way of collecting 
qualitative data, where the researcher asks the interview partner predefined open questions, 
providing greater control over the topics of the interview (Given, 2012). Furthermore, semi-
==PAGE:38==
 
 
31 
structured interviews are designed to get a subjective response from the person of interest, 
regarding a specific matter (Lee et al., 2015).  
The prepared interview guideline was created in a three-part structure, to ensure the full 
understanding and thematical distance between the question paragraphs. The interview started 
with an introduction to the current research matter, a brief presentation of the researcher’s 
background and the use of the gathered data gained from the interview. Thereafter, the personal 
background of the CVC manager and its current position in the organization was the point of 
interest, including some basic information of the CVC itself. The third, main part, of the 
interview contained direct questions regarding the formal and informal relationship between 
the CVC and start-up and investigated how these interactions are currently structured. 
Therefore, the third interview section represented the main database for answering the research 
question. The complete interview guideline can be found in appendix A. 
The interview length itself was scheduled for 30-40 minutes, depending on the participants 
availability. Experience has shown that the suggested time was sufficient. 
 
3.2.3 Transcription  
Prior to the interview, all participants were asked for permission to transcribe the conversation. 
Once confirmed, the transcription feature of Microsoft Teams was utilized for a fully detailed 
transcription of the interview. Directly after the interview, all transcripts were reviewed and 
formalized based on additional notes manually taken during the interview. Furthermore, upon 
request, the finished transcription was sent to the interviewee for review before usage in any 
thereafter analysis.  
All the transcriptions were created in the same language as the interviews themselves. Three 
of the interviews were conducted in German, while the rest were conducted in English. For 
translating the German interviews into English, ChatGPT 4.0 was utilized, aiming for 
maximum objectivity in translation. The translated transcripts were carefully reviewed and 
compared with both the original German transcript and the manual notes taken, during the 
interview. In the case of misalignment, adaptations were done manually.

3.3 Data analysis 
The data analysis section outlines the applied methodology, helping to extract valuable insights 
from the gathered data. In order to facilitate the streamlining of the data analysis process, the 
software tool NVivo was utilized. The implementation of the software facilitated a more 
comprehensive examination and systematic organization of the data derived from the 
transcriptions of the semi-structured interviews. 
 
3.3.1 Approach to data analysis  
As previously outlined, the grounded theory approach was used for analyzing the interview 
based qualitative data. To further operationalize this approach, the Gioia method was applied.  
This method is considered especially suitable for inductive research and developing insights 
from unstructured qualitative data (Gioia et al., 2013). Furthermore, the authors elaborate that 
the data analysis is structured in three core steps: 
1. First-order analysis: The collected qualitative data is coded using terms and expressions 
close to the interviewee’s language, to generate descriptive categories reflecting the 
participants views and to observe similarities and overlaps in the gathered data. 
2. Second-order themes: The second stage of the Gioia method involves further analyzing 
the initial first-order codes and checking whether the emerging themes provide concepts 
that help describe the observed phenomenon, by bundling multiple first-order codes 
into one second-order theme.  
3. Aggregated dimensions: The creation of aggregated dimensions represents an abstract 
representation of the essence of the initial data collected, by bundling multiple second-
order themes into aggregated dimensions.  
Finishing the data analysis, the visualization follows in the form of a dynamic inductive model. 
Gioia et al. (2013) state that the core of the model is to show existing connections and display 
the dynamic properties in a transparent way among the themes and dimensions identified in the 
gathered (here: interview) data. Based on this, a final data-to-theory connection will be 
achieved. Overall, the application of the Gioia method allows for the transition from concrete 
empirical data to abstract theoretical insights, supporting qualitative research (Jiménez-
Partearroyo et al., 2024).

3.4 Validity and reliability  
Both validity and reliability are essential factors when evaluating the quality of presented 
research findings. Noble & Smith (2015) defined validity and reliability as follows:  
- 
Validity reflects the accuracy to which extent the findings accurately display the 
collected data.  
- 
Reliability describes the consistency of the analytical approach, taking both personal 
and research method-based biases into account, which may have an impact on the 
findings.  
To ensure validity and reliability in this study, different data sources have been used and 
triangulated to provide a coherent presentation of the findings. Additionally, the data collection 
and analysis processes are described in detail, ensuring full transparency. The application of 
the grounded theory approach, followed by the Gioia method, aims to minimize the 
researcher’s bias, ensuring all findings were based on the experiences of the interviewed CVC 
managers. Furthermore, the geographical restriction allows for the categorization of the 
presented findings, thus avoiding generalization to other CVC (geographical) ecosystems.  
All interviewed CVC managers participated voluntarily in the interviews. The use of semi-
structured interviews allowed all participants to individually express their opinions, while 
ensuring the core topics in the interview were addressed. To raise the interview participation 
rate, all CVC managers were assured access to an anonymized report, presenting the findings 
of the study once finished. Furthermore, no sponsorship was present at any time. 
No personal connection between the CVC managers and the researcher was present at any time.

4. Findings  
This findings chapter presents the empirical findings derived from the conducted interviews 
with CVC managers. The goal is to show the findings with regard to the research question and 
ensure transparency. Therefore, direct quotes from interview participants are used to support 
and illustrate the gained insights.  
All findings are presented in a descriptive manner, avoiding comparison with existing literature 
and interpretation, which will be addressed in the discussion chapter 5. 
The following findings chapter is structured into a data analysis section, circling back to the 
applied methodology and thereafter displaying the raw findings. All findings are displayed by 
their built aggregated dimensions and further displayed in figure 4.  
 
4.1 Data analysis  
The analysis process was initiated by systematically reviewing the transcripts of all conducted 
interviews with the CVC managers. The review was performed to understand how CVC firms 
perceive and structure formal and informal collaboration with start-ups to achieve strategic 
benefits. 
The gathered data was collected and analyzed through the coding process, described in the 
Gioia method and managed by utilizing the NVivo software. The application of the Gioia 
method, as described in Section 3.3.1, enables broader trends and overarching concepts to be 
identified by synthesizing findings and creating newly discovered, aggregated dimensions. 
This supports qualitative research in achieving a new understanding of connected topics, 
thereby enhancing the overall understanding of the underlying matter.

4.2 Aggregated dimensions  
Following the Gioia method, 4 aggregated dimensions were defined. In the following, all 
findings will be presented according to the defined aggregated dimensions: 
1. Adjustable but indispensable formal structures to foster strategic collaboration 
2. Establishing and maintaining interpersonal relationship as key enabler for strategic 
growth 
3. Strategic enablement of start-ups by CVC, without operational intervention, securing 
start-up autonomy 
4. Balancing strategic alignment with professional distance and parent firm loyalty 
 
Aggregated dimension one: Adjustable but indispensable formal structures to foster strategic 
collaboration 
In CVC – start-up collaboration the establishment of formal structures represents a major part 
of the collaboration between the two entities. The majority of interview participants mentioned 
board representation as a mandatory but flexible part of the formal collaboration (1.1 Board 
representation as mandatory but flexible requirement – second-order theme). Gaining inside 
information, while respecting board governance and the duties of the board member, aligns 
with the core reasoning for establishing a CVC in the first place. Participants commented the 
following on board representation as the primary mechanism for strategic access:  
I mean, a board seat is important because then you're actually part of the site and the faith of 
the company (Participant no. 9) 
You get access to all the information and you're there to kind of ensure that the team is good. 
(Participant no. 9)  
The primary formal structure is board representation, as a board director or board observer, 
depending on investment size and value. (Participant no. 5)  
Further, CVCs tend to be interested in board observer seats. Having similar access to 
information, without voting rights provides the CVC the benefit of remaining not liable for any 
decision making. Participants mentioned:  
We would rather have an observer seat because it means that we're not liable for any sort of 
decision making (Participant no. 1) 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
36 
So, most of the companies we invest in, we are on the board as an observer. So, we definitely 
have direct connection with the CEO of the company (Participant no. 6) 
While the board (observer) role was broadly seen as a mandatory part of the formal 
collaboration structure, further formal agreements were often seen as tailorable collaboration 
mechanisms that depend on the situation of both the CVC and the start-up. Interview 
participants highlighted the importance of formal structures, in addition to the board (observer) 
role:  
Formal links between portfolio companies’ and parent companies’ business units facilitate 
discussions around project implementation, pilots, and accelerating start-up growth 
(Participant no. 8) 
While not a fixed condition for investment (formal structures), CVC usually formally link 
portfolio companies with parent companies’ business areas to explore the viability of project 
implementation or pilots (Participant 8) 
So I think that is what you need (formal structures) […] for speed and structure and cost and 
all that (Participant no. 7) 
And then a key element (formal structures) of what we look to do, because it is a strategic 
investment (Participant no. 2)  
Additionally, the interviews showed that formal non-board collaboration structures play a 
present role in CVC – start-up collaboration, but the extent often depends on a variety of factors 
(1.2 Situation dependent tailoring of formal collaboration mechanisms – second-order theme):  
It really, really depends on the stage where the company is at. It also depends on where in the 
world the company is located (Participant no. 7) 
Formal engagements are assessed on a case-to-case basis, and there are no set-in-stone rules 
for every start-up (Participant no. 8) 
If we decide to invest, it turns into a longer-term relationship not a formal contract necessarily, 
but a commitment (Participant no. 6) 
Given the case of formal agreements between the CVC and the start-up, CVCs aim to foster 
mutual strategic intent due to standardized formal agreements (1.3 fostering mutual strategic 
intent due to standardized formal agreements – second-order theme). Only three of the 
interviewed CVC managers had established standardized formal process, shaped by 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
37 
collaborative blueprints, letters of intent (LOI) or the application of a memorandum of 
understanding (MOU). Participants stated:  
Yes. So in the investment phase, typically we will get you out and then co-sign a letter of intent 
or an memorandum of understanding, that will outline the main areas of collaboration that 
we'd like to explore more deeply together in a phased approach (Participant no. 2) 
That LOI will serve as the basis for a joint development agreement with testing technical and 
commercial tracks across the relevant functions in our business (Participant no. 2)  
Shareholder agreements establish channels for interaction and receiving updates, such as 
annual and financial reports (Participant no. 8) 
And when it comes to the formal one, I mean, we do have a, we always have these kinds of 
playbooks, like a playbook for doing our investments with the whole process with all the things 
we ask for and so on. I think that really makes sense for transparency (Participant no.7) 
It is noticeable that the establishment of playbooks was reasoned as a means of providing 
transparency for both the CVC and the start-up but was also implemented to ensure that the 
start-up follows the rules given by the parent company and meets the standards they have 
towards their stakeholders. The interviewee further elaborated:  
You need to have a lot of those things (formal requirements), and you need to have it in this 
kind of order and get it on a monthly basis or quarterly basis and so on. And because we, as 
large corporates, we have those requirements towards our stakeholders (Participant no. 7)  
The interviews showed that formal structure are indispensable from a CVC’s perspective, and 
that there is a preference in a board or board observer role. Additional formal structures are 
case dependent and standardized agreements are rarely implemented.

Aggregated dimension two: Establishing and maintaining interpersonal relationship as key 
enabler for strategic growth 
In further discovering the CVC – start-up relationship, the interpersonal relationship between 
these two parties was broadly unexplored in current research. Therefore, a core section of the 
interviews was dedicated to creating a mutual understanding of the importance of the 
interpersonal relationship, how it is shaped, and on what values these relationships are built 
(2.1 Interpersonal comparability is a requirement for collaboration – second-order theme). The 
first finding of the interviews is that creating and/or having a good relationship with the start-
up can be seen as a requirement for successful collaboration. Participants elaborated that a 
successful collaboration depends on the existence of interpersonal chemistry. While full 
alignment is not always necessary, a good personal relationship is seen as essential. Participants 
mentioned:  
While full alignment is not always necessary, a good personal relationship is essential 
(Participant no. 1) 
And of course, getting to know the key folks on an individual and personal basis is a key element 
of it (Participant no. 2)  
You want to, you know, I think you want to be mindful. I think you want to listen to them and 
hear what they're doing. So, I think it should be a two-way street (Participant no. 1) 
[…] going out to dinner afterwards, having some time to allow for organic conversation can 
be helpful (Participant no. 2)  
But then we realize that it is so beneficial to have that direct contact (Participant no. 7) 
Good personal connection significantly raises the overall potential of a successful 
collaboration (Participant no. 8) 
It also is about chemistry. Some companies you have a good chemistry with, and they are very 
good and kind in communicating with you (Participant no. 9) 
While having a generally good relationship, the personal fit between both entities is also an 
important measure for the success of the future collaboration. It is also noticeable that a good 
working relationship includes diverse points of view, according to the interviewed CVC 
managers. Furthermore, interviewees mentioned:  
==PAGE:46==
 
 
39 
You know, do you want to work with a person that's really similar to you, which is nice from a 
working perspective because you think alike, but you might miss certain things (Participant no. 
1) 
We always try to visit the company that we are going to invest in and try to spend enough time 
with so that they bring down their guard a bit and become what they usually are (Participant 
no. 4)  
For early-stage start-ups, the management team of the start-up has a heavy effect on the 
chances of success, making a good personal relationship important. Bad management is a 
huge, huge red flag, even if the technology is promising (Participant no. 8)  
While having this personal relationship, the real difficulty is to find out in the first place if there 
is a personal connection to build on. Therefore, first personal touchpoints between the CVC 
and the start-up are considered a fixed component of the extensive due diligence before the 
initial investment is made. In that matter, not only the personal first impression counts but 
CVCs also tend to activate their broad networks to find out what it is to work with the start-up 
and its team. Participants mentioned:  
I think that relationship start before we even invest in them, right? […] It’s more of a natural 
process—part of how we do due diligence and how you get to know someone (Participant no. 
6) 
No, but I think that before we do an investment, we always meet with the people and sit down 
with them for quite some time. And I think that, I mean, same as you when you meet friends, so 
you kind of have an indication quite early if you like them or not, and what kind of category 
they are in, so to speak (Participant no. 7) 
They establish personal connections with the management team during the investment process 
and maintaining this in post-investment stage. Start-ups are generally willing to engage due to 
trust and potential collaboration (Participant no. 8) 
It's a small world as well. So we always have people we jointly know. So, there are always 
references (if the start-up team is good to work with) as well (Participant no. 4) 
Getting into a collaboration with a CVC is also a conscious decision, as CVCs tend not to 
provide the biggest capital but rather provide complementary resources. CVC managers are 
==PAGE:47==
 
 
40 
aware of this and therefore mentioned that their interpersonal connection serves as an additional 
help beyond the capital provided:  
We coach and mentor founders, especially those lacking international/commercial experience. 
We focus on building the right team—coach individuals to grow (Participant no. 3) 
And when they choose to work with us, it’s definitely because they see us as a business partner 
or a potential customer—not just as a source of funding (Participant no. 6) 
But I think that the most important part is what you want to achieve. And usually then, if we 
want to do proof-of-concept pilots, the start-up really wants to work with us (Participant no. 
7) 
In the same context, the interpersonal relationship also depends on the start-up structure, the 
role and importance of the CVC for the start-up, and the current ownership distribution and 
additional shareholders present. In that matter, CVC managers commented:  
The relationship is important and of course it also depends on how big the (start-up) company 
is. If like for example, some of the (start-up) company we have invested in probably almost 10 
years now, and then they grow from a small company to a big company and then they have 
more and more shareholders (Participant no. 6) 
One more example: for seed round, the angel round investor—at the very beginning, the 
investors are very important. If they don’t have seed money, the company will die. But two 
years later, the company grows bigger and bigger. They have more influence by themselves

In the beginning, when it's a low technology readiness level (TRL), then it's more the 
skunkworks or the more R&D centric and testing and validation centric relationship naturally. 
And then as the start-up approaches a product offering in a solution, they can sell to XXX as 
customers or we can build into a XXX solution offering. Then the nature of the conversation 
changes to have a more and more commercial orientation (Participant no. 2) 
Building a relationship with the start-up, CVC managers are primarily interested in direct 
contact with the chief executive officer (CEO) or founding team. Consequently, a founder 
centric relationship is the primary goal to achieve (2.2. Founder centric relationship 
establishment – second-order theme). CVC managers mentioned the following:

Beyond that, I regularly speak with the CEOs – typically every four to six weeks, depending on

11)  
Typically, we would have monthly meetings with the CEO. So, I want to know the CEO update,

(Participant no. 2) 
I have regular meetings with the CEO just for the business update and for understanding 
(Participant no. 4) 
Furthermore, participants elaborated that the actual performance of the start-up also drives the

Informal supports are typically offered to well-performing start-ups that are actively raising

no. 8) 
And when the company is delayed or it goes into difficulties, you see that within the start-up,

or actually bad. And it's always easy when everything is going up, and it's very easy to have a 
good relationship. But when things go bad and you need to make hard decisions, then you go 
from having a very close relationship to kind of being more distant, because you need to make 
some tough decisions (Participant no. 9) 
Narrowing down the interpersonal relationship, it has become evident that a specific set of core 
communication values and behaviors form the core of any potential collaboration. It has 
become clear that sharing mutual values as a base layer of collaboration needs to be established 
(2.3 Mutual values and communication norms provide the base layer of collaboration – second-
order theme). Throughout most interviews, the person-to-person communication has been seen 
as a natural requirement to build any kind of connection or relationship with another person. 
Therefore, CVC managers highlight the importance of open and honest communication norms. 
The application of these communication norms provides value and set the foundation for any 
further development of relationship between the two entities. Participants mentioned:  
==PAGE:49==
 
 
42 
We talk to management early – also together with other investors – and set expectations. Then 
you can, for example, tell a research-oriented CEO that someone else might be more suitable 
for the next phase. If you do that transparently and respectfully, people usually understand

Personal coaching and close, ongoing contact with founders. Honest, direct feedback—helps 
founders find their blind spots (Participant no. 3)  
Be honest, open—especially during hard talks (Participant no. 3) 
Just straight dialogue. I think that it is more to be really transparent and be like this will work. 
This will not work, not because it's bad or right, but you just like openly say that this needs to 
be done (Participant no. 7) 
Furthermore, cross cultural differences also need to be considered. As humans are biased and 
shaped by their culture and established habits, being aware of this can help establish a better 
relationship and first impression. The interviewees mentioned:  
I try to make those (cultural differences) as transparent as possible to really, you know, to have

judged on, so to speak. And that I think helps people, especially if it's from completely different 
cultures or geographies and so on (Participant no. 7) 
I think it's really important to listen to different perspectives because we all have our own 
biases (Participant no. 6)

And there's transparency, which leads to trust being created (Participant no. 2)

But for us, it's we're not part of the business unit. We cannot kind of sell the company for them. 
So that's that we need to be very transparent. Otherwise, you will kind of raise expectations

In the same manner, CVC managers argue that this building of trust acts as a foundation for

And having them have confidence in you as a trusted partner and that they look for your advice

I think then you need to be there through the harsh time and answer on the strange hours and

Furthermore, the interviews showed that formal setups are enhanced by informal interpersonal

So as a principal at XXX Ventures, I would send in a lot of those meetings and orchestrate them

Like the formal, okay, there needs to be some kind of rules, but also you need to make sure that,

Based on the interviews with CVC managers, the interpersonal relationship between the CVC 
and the start-up emerges as the key enabler for strategic growth and a successful collaboration. 
The creation of mutual trust due to a founder centric cooperation approach, clear and honest 
communication norms and high transparency is one of the main goals the CVC tries to achieve. 
Furthermore, CVC managers state that interpersonal relationships, as an informal structure, 
enhance and strengthen the mutual commitment and the adapted formal structures, 
demonstrating an interdependent relationship between the two.

Aggregated dimension three: Strategic enablement of start-ups by CVC, without operational 
intervention, securing start-up autonomy  
A collaboration between two business partners needs to be driven by an intrinsic motivation. 
The CVC strives for inside knowledge from the start-up. Contrary the start-up requires funding 
and is moreover interested in the provision of complementary resources and support beyond 
the capital provided. In that matter, one of the core tasks of the CVC is to act as a facilitator 
into the parent company and to the outside networks (3.1 CVC facilitates inside the parent 
company and outside networks – second-order theme). In that matter, CVC enables the start-
up to navigate the network of the parent company acting as a facilitator. Participants mentioned:  
But we do speak with both sides – our colleagues and the start-up – to make sure things are 
going well (Participant no. 11) 
Correct. Yeah. I wouldn't describe it as, well, there's some mentoring to an extent, but that's 
adjacent to the main thing of really facilitating and helping them navigate the parent company 
(Participant no. 2) 
I would say we proactively promoted cooperation between the start-up and our mother 
company. […] So, I would say we proactively promote cooperation (Participant no. 5)  
While not only facilitating the contact between the start-up and the parent company, the CVC 
is also responsible for the way on how the start-up is seen inside the parent company. At the 
same time, the start-up gains leverage from the CVCs network inside the parent company. In 
that matter interviewees mentioned:  
CVC team members are the primary points of contact for the start-up, their input heavily 
influences the parent companies’ perception of the start-up (Participant no. 8) 
So, one thing that I think we've done well in XX Ventures is to make sure it's been anchored 
with the very top (management of the parent company) (Participant no. 2) 
The CVC connects portfolio start-ups to marketing events, conferences, and fairs to enhance 
their visibility (Participant no. 8) 
This access to visibility inside and outside the parent company, paired with access to 
complementary resources, provides the opportunity for the CVC to challenge the start-up ideas 
for validation and provide valuable help to the start-up (3.2 CVC provide advisory engagement 
==PAGE:52==
 
 
45 
with respect to the start-up’s autonomy – second-order theme). The CVC challenges start-up 
ideas for validation and provide operational insights, participants elaborated:  
So, then they, in their organization, they'll assign folks that are tech leads and or commercial 
leads, depending on the start-up's maturity, to foster that collaboration (Participant no. 2)  
And then it's the core business and the core commercial areas of the business that should be 
the one leading those discussions with the portfolio company. So it's more that we're a 
facilitator. And then when you get to the actual contract, it shouldn't be the CVC that's leading 
that negotiation. We step back and say, well, you two (parent company – start-up) are the two 
counterparties and then get to a yes (Participant no. 2)  
We are helping start-ups understand what the industry is looking for and what they need to do 
to scale up to be attractive to corporates like XXX (Participant no. 8) 
While interested in insights, CVCs are aware of the importance of start-up autonomy and do 
not interact in their operational day-to-day business, and do not participate in an active steering 
role inside the start-up company:  
We don't want to interfere with the operation of the company, but we want to comment from 
the investor perspective. For the roadmap, for the operational strategy, the company can define 
by itself and then report to the board members. If the board agrees, then they can share with 
the shareholders. But I think for some detailed work, like a technology roadmap, they have to 
ask first, they can definitely make the decision (Participant no. 5)  
Correct – the reason here is twofold: 1) if CVCs get involved in day-to-day operations, how 
will they be compensated? They have a contract with the CVC but spend a lot of time on one 
start-up. Ideally you want to cut back their CVC contract and introduce a start-up contract, 
both part time. This is however cumbersome and a lot of CVCs can’t work with this. Reason 2) 
– many CVCs goal is to create a portfolio and spending a lot of time with one start-up goes at 
the expense of building and maintaining a portfolio. It’s an asset management strategy – do I 
spend a lot of time building a couple of companies or invest in a lot but more hands off 
(Participant no. 2) 
The conducted interviews have shown that the CVC positions itself in a facilitation role 
between the start-up and the corporate parent firm. While offering advisory and providing 
visibility of the start-up inside and outside the corporate parent’s network, CVC tends to follow 
a hands-off approach and do not want to interfere in the start-ups day-to-day business.

Aggregated dimension four: Balancing strategic alignment with professional distance and 
parent firm loyalty 
While aiming for a very close connection with the start-up, the right balance between 
professional distance to maintain the start-up’s autonomy and close relation needs to be found. 
Particularly important is the professional distance in the connection of the above-mentioned 
aim of board representative of the CVC (4.1 Board representation and governance rules require 
professional distance – second-order theme). In that matter, especially from the start-up 
perspective, it is important to have clear boundaries in place regarding board-based overview 
and governance roles. CVC managers were consistently aware of this particularity and 
commented the following:  
When acting as a board member, the sole purpose I have is to act in the best interest of the 
(start-up) company. When acting as a board member and decides on a strategic commercial 
plan for a company, this will be in the interest of me as an owner since this will, hopefully, 
bring the company to a better position. And we set out Owners directive etc. to the boards, 
expressing our joint will as owners. It can rather be an opportunity for (the CVC) to use our 
companies in Industrial cooperation/offset in our larger campaigns. But as board members we 
would never act on such an activity if it not be commercially beneficial for the company, 
regardless if it would benefit the CVC or parent firm in the short term or not. So again, this is 
not a problem I believe as long as you can separate your different roles (Participant no. 3.) 
Correct – this is always the elephant in the room. Their legal/fiduciary responsibility as a 
board member is to act in the best interest of the company and its shareholders so not doing 
that, opens up room for litigation. That’s why sometimes business development teams don’t 
take board seats but observer seats: all the info without the legal liability but no official voting 
power. Over time, the ground rule is that once you’re in a transaction where there could be a 
potential conflict of interest (CoI), the respective board member needs to step out. For example 
– company X is negotiating with pharma A and B. Board member of CVC pharma C is on the 
board. As long as C is not bidding/part of the process, there’s no CoI. Once party C is involved 
somewhat, board member of CVC C needs to step out to prevent that offers from A or B are 
being used by C to manipulate the process. This is a fluid discussion though – sometimes 
they’re forced to step out when any sort of deal is discussed, CVCs will always argue that only 
when numbers are discussed and C is involved, they need to step out. The solution lies 
somewhere in the middle (Participant no. 1) 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
47 
Yes, that is actually a problem. But as a board member, first of all you have the duty to be loyal 
to the company where you are on board. That is the primary duty as a board member there. Of 
course, these board members are then in charge of CVC because we are the owners of the 
shares on the basis of which we can send a board member. That is simply the primary duty. 
And if there can be conflicts, then this board member is simply compromised and when it comes 
to deciding where there are conflicts of interest, he simply has to step back and cannot decide 
(Participant no. 10) 
Yes this can be a continuous challenge. This is where those informal, interpersonal 
relationships you mention in #1 become essential (boils down to "do you trust each other?" VC 
(at its best) is a trust based long-term relationship). Specifically e.g., in board meetings where 
there's a conflict of interest (e.g., pricing where the portfolio company could also sell to the 
CVC's parent company) the CVC representative is recuses themselves/it's aligned beforehand 
they step out for those part(s) (Participant no. 2) 
The difference between board decisions, where board members have a duty of loyalty to the 
firm they are on the board, and general interaction between the CVC and the start-up, where 
the CVC has an investor's perspective and is loyal to their corporate parent firm, needs to be 
clearly set out.  
Furthermore, CVC managers act intrinsically in the best interest of the parent company, as this 
remains their first priority to gain strategic insights. Consequently, the loyalty of the CVC 
belongs to the parent company. On these two topics, participants commented:  
But ultimately, as a fully owned subsidiary of a large company, the foremost loyalty is to the 
parent company (Participant no. 2) 
We want a good relationship, but we also need professional distance (Participant no. 11) 
You're not a part of the (start-up) company. So even if you need to think about the formal 
structures that you are a board member, you have your role as a board member. You need to 
understand that you're not an employee of the company (Participant no. 9) 
Overall, the interviews have shown that CVC managers need to carefully separate their 
professional duties as board members, and their investor perspective with an intrinsic interest 
to benefit their parent firm. This duality in interest requires clear boundaries and governance 
applied, supported by a well-maintained trust-based relationship with the start-up.

5. Discussion 
This chapter discusses the previously outlined findings according to the research question and 
puts them in relation to existing findings in published academic literature. The purpose of the 
discussion is to interpret the findings and further analyze how they contribute to an enhanced 
understanding of the underlying research matter of CVC – start-up collaboration, especially 
how formal and informal collaboration foster strategic growth from the CVC perspective, and 
the development of the parent firm.  
This chapter is structured around the four aggregated dimensions, resulting of the application 
of the Gioia method, drawing a connection to important theoretical frameworks and prior 
literature findings.  
 
5.1 Discussion by aggregated dimension 
Aggregated dimension one: Adjustable but indispensable formal structures to foster strategic 
collaboration 
The findings of the empirical study have shown that the CVC – start-up collaboration relies on 
both formal and informal forms of connection. The importance of a board or board observer 
seat is clearly shown in the findings of the conducted interviews. The favor of having a board 
or board observer seat can also be seen in existing academic literature, suggesting the board 
seat acting as investment protection and an opportunity to gain company insights (Wadhwa & 
Kotha, 2006). According to Anokhin et al. (2016) there is a positive relationship between the 
number of board mandates and the CVC activity of a company, implicitly confirming the 
likelihood of CVC investments and preference of board representation as found in this study 
as well. Yang (2012) found in his study that the board representation of the CVC hinders both 
the portfolio companies’ performance and the corporate investors’ innovativeness. The 
findings of this study therefore underline the preference of CVCs for board representation and 
their protective approach to investments, even if this may reduce the overall innovativeness of 
both parties.  
Next to the highly utilized board representation, the interview findings suggest that other 
formal agreements are seen as tailorable vehicles to further structure the collaboration, 
depending on many factors. As previously stated in the literature review, additional formal 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
54 
structures are highly individual and apart from collaborative blue prints (Basu et al., 2016) are 
not represented in current academic literature. The findings of the interviews confirm previous 
findings in academic literature and highlight, that the decision on additional formal structures 
is made on a case-by-case basis without generalized rules or predefined and standardized 
processes. When additional formal structures were applied, the application of playbooks as 
well as the adaptation of MOU or LOI agreements were seen and confirmed. As the application 
of playbooks or other generalized formal structures was only seen twice in the interviews, no 
pattern or consistency can be confirmed, again underlining previous findings on formal 
collaboration between CVC and the start-up, besides the preference of board (observer) 
representation. 
 
Aggregated dimension two: Establishing and maintaining interpersonal relationship as a key 
enabler for strategic growth 
Throughout all interviews, CVC managers were aware of the importance of a good 
interpersonal relationship between the CVC and the start-up. In that matter, participants 
emphasized that the existence of interpersonal chemistry, mainly based on personal 
compatibility and trust, is considered an important driver of successful collaboration. These 
findings are coherent with Zhang and Guo (2023), stating that the collaboration with a CVC is 
considered as a risky decision, also from a start-up perspective. The collaboration is full of 
uncertainty and requires the building of a trusting environment in order to be successful for 
both parties. The existence of shared values and the application of open and transparent 
communication were seen as an important base layer by interviewed CVC managers. 
Especially in the beginning of an upcoming collaboration, the application of enhanced 
transparency and showing presence in difficult times, can be seen as the preferred way of 
building trust and create a mutual working base. CVC mangers pointed out that real trust can 
only be built if you (the CVC) are always there, especially in hard times. In that matter, CVCs 
are trying to mitigate the “swimming with the sharks dilemma” investigated by Katila et 
al.(2008), which points out tensions in the trust building process between the CVC and the 
start-up. Furthermore, the findings of the interviews confirm Maula et al. (2003) findings of 
social capital, being a crucial element for value creation among business partners. Equally in 
line with this are Weber and Weber (2011) findings that trust develops and strengthens over 
time, as similar seen in interview participants statements for this study. Further, the fact of 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
55 
staying true to their word as a CVC, both on the personal and institutional level, and not 
overpromising can be seen as major drivers of trust in the CVC – start-up relationship building, 
according to the interviews conducted.  
The interview findings also showed that building relationships with the founder provides 
deeper insights into the importance of maintaining close connections with the main decision-
making entities in the portfolio company for the CVC. This enhances existing literature. 
Throughout the interviewed CVC managers, all were employed at CVCs investing in start-ups 
at various stages of maturity. The interview findings suggest that investing in an early-stage 
start-up provides more direct contact with the founding team which enables deeper relationship 
building than investing in a later-stage, or more mature start-ups. Paik and Woo (2017) found 
in their study that early-stage start-ups are primarily focus on innovation related activities, 
which align with most CVCs’ strategic objectives through an investment in that specific 
company. At the same time, investments in such early maturity start-ups do not provide 
comparable high revenues, nor are these start-ups able to utilize the CVCs complementary 
assets to their fullest (Di Lorenzo & Sabel, 2024). The findings of the interviews provide a 
different perspective and extend current literature, particularly in cases where CVCs aim for 
strategic insights. In such cases, relationships and connections are prioritized, making early-
stage investments more appealing, irrespective of the revenue potential at the start of the 
collaboration. Another explanation, given by one interview participant was that CVCs do not 
provide the biggest monetary package and CVCs are forced to invest early in high potential 
area start-ups, to be able to get on the ticket in the first way. While not further investigated, 
these findings are a contribution to existing literature and highlight the importance of 
interpersonal relationship between the CVC and the start-up. 
In all, creating an interpersonal relationship with the aim to work as key enabler for strategic 
growth, the conative fit described by Gemünden et al. (1999) was followed and findings were 
confirmed. CVCs tend to make great efforts to build such relationships, placing high 
importance on the interpersonal connection between the CVC and the start-up, particularly 
when forming relationships with founding teams. Nevertheless, no standardized actionable 
measures are in place. 
 
 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
56 
Aggregated dimension three: Strategic enablement of start-ups by CVC, without operational 
intervention securing start-up autonomy  
The findings of the third aggregated dimension have shown that the CVC strategically enables 
start-ups by facilitating access to the parent company’s internal and external resources. While 
doing so, interfering in day-to-day operations is avoided, to provide start-up autonomy to a 
high degree. Creating an environment, in which the start-up can develop independently is in 
the best interest of both collaborating parties, as Schückes et al. (2025) note, start-up autonomy 
is considered a necessity, because it protects the start-up from corporate roadblocks that could 
hinder its entrepreneurial creativity and development. The findings of the interviews confirmed 
that. Furthermore, autonomy is an important factor for attracting start-ups in the first place, and 
thereby fostering the interests of both the CVC and the collaborative start-up at the same time 
(Schückes et al., 2025).  
When further investigating the collaboration, the role of the CVC as a facilitator needs to be 
highlighted. The facilitation of the CVC enables the start-up to navigate the internal structures 
of the parent firm and thereby create connections to relevant stakeholders, deep inside the 
parent company. Consequently, this further expands the natural scope of the CVC as an 
innovation and development vehicle for the corporate parent, acting as a relationship broker 
between the start-up company and the corporate parent. These interview findings are extending 
current academic literature finings, where the CVC was already seen in a primarily knowledge 
broker role, mainly contributing to innovation in the parent firm (Bendig, Göttel, et al., 2024; 
Elfring, 2005; Henderson & Leleux, 2005).  
The collaboration between the start-up and the CVC needs to be a two-sided road for both 
partners. As commonly seen in academic literature, the CVC does not only provide the required 
capital or funding but also offers the utilization of complementary resources, provided by the 
parent firm (e.g., Bendig, Hensellek, et al., 2024; Schückes et al., 2025; Shuwaikh & Dubocage, 
2022). These findings are also confirmed by the findings of the conducted interviews in this 
study. Furthermore, the CVC managers interviewed pointed out their engagement in providing 
feedback, utilizing internal experts for consultation and generally sharing industry insights with 
the start-up and thereby contributing to both the start-up development and the overall goal of 
gaining strategic insights into new markets or technologies. 
 
==PAGE:64==

Aggregated dimension four: Balancing strategic alignment with professional distance and 
parent firm loyalty 
Throughout the conducted interviews it became increasingly clear, that CVCs aim to maintain 
a close cooperation with the start-up while also having a certain professional distance to ensure 
the start-up autonomy, as described in the previous aggregated dimension three discussion.  
The collaboration between a start-up and a CVC requires, in most of the cases, board 
representation of the CVC, as seen in aggregated dimension one. While being present at the 
board is seen as a fixed requirement, it is also one of the biggest sources of tension in CVC – 
start-up collaboration. Having the CVC on the board creates the fear of expropriation 
(Dushnitsky & Shaver, 2009) which is most prominently described by Katila et al. (2008) with 
the “swimming with the sharks” dilemma. The findings of the interviews have shown a great 
awareness of this potential tension, as the board acts as the main strategy and decision making 
entity of the start-up (Garg & Furr, 2017). This awareness leads to multiple measures being 
applied to mitigate that risk. According to the interviewed CVC managers, clear governance 
rules need to be established as an essential first step. This will help to develop trust, which is a 
major component of continuous successful collaboration. CVC managers themselves also 
know that they personally need to separate their function as board member from their function 
as a CVC employee that embodies the investor perspective, seeking some kind of return 
(strategic, financial, or both). The clear separation between board duties and investor interests 
is also set out in law and board members must fulfil their duty of loyalty by acting in the best 
interests of the organization they represent, regardless of any personal consequences that may 
result from their decisions for the CVC (Grant Thornton, 2023).  
As mitigation measures are not further analyzed in the conducted interviews, it is still valid to 
state that the tension get significant attention from CVC managers and is currently being 
mitigated by both strict in-place governance rules and the creation of trust over time, as seen 
in aggregated dimension two.

5.2 Discussion on interdependency of identified pillars  
The focus in this discussion section will be on the flexibility and heavily weighted interpersonal 
relationship between the CVC and the start-up to gain the strategic insights the CVC is aiming 
for.  
The findings of the interviews have shown that the interpersonal relationship between the CVC 
and the start-up is the most flexible part and can be developed and shaped over time. While all 
managers are aware of this, no in-place structures on how this development should be executed 
and monitored are in place. This is similar to the current academic literature status, where 
specific findings regarding the implementation and utilization of the interpersonal relationship 
for gaining strategic benefits are missing. The importance of an intact relationship therefore is 
non deniable and highlighted in multiple studies (Maula et al., 2009; Rutten et al., 2016; Weber 
& Weber, 2010, 2011). The interview findings therefore indicate that the topic of how to 
develop and utilize a good relationship to the start-up is not only overlooked in current 
academic literature but also insufficiently developed in real world applications. Taking Maula 
et al., 2009 considerations that an enhanced level of social interaction between the CVC and 
the portfolio company enhances the learning benefits to the portfolio company and Yang, 2012 
findings that especially CVCs have problems in absorbing gained knowledge from portfolio 
companies the extension of relational connection seems to be an actionable field, that could 
mitigate the above shown problems. 
As the findings provide evidence that the interpersonal relationship represents the biggest 
impact for gaining strategic insights for the CVC, the transition to actionable measures and 
outcome analysis needs to be investigated and challenged by future research.

6.1 Final conclusion  
This paper is set out to further investigate how CVCs achieve and perceive formal and informal 
collaboration structures with start-ups in the aim of achieving strategic benefits. In that matter, 
the central research question: “How do Corporate Venture Capital firms perceive and structure 
formal and informal collaboration with start-ups to achieve strategic benefits?” was addressed 
through a qualitative study. The study consequently takes the CVC perspective, focusing on 
the measures taken to receive strategic insights through the collaboration with start-ups. The 
geographical scope of this study is set in the Nordics and Germany. Utilizing the Gioia method, 
all conducted interviews with CVC managers were systematically coded, analyzed, and merged 
into aggregated dimensions, providing a structured understanding on how formal and informal 
collaboration types display key collaboration pillars.  
Through the analysis of the gained qualitative data, four aggregated dimensions were 
identified. These aggregated dimensions represent the mandatory key alignment areas between 
the CVC and the start-up and need to be maintained by the CVC to gain strategic insights:  
1. Adjustable but indispensable formal structures to foster strategic collaboration 
2. Establishing and maintaining interpersonal relationship as key enabler for strategic 
growth 
3. Strategic enablement of start-ups by CVC, without operational intervention, securing 
start-up autonomy 
4. Balancing strategic alignment with professional distance and parent firm loyalty 
The implementation of formal structures is seen as necessary by all interviewed CVC managers 
and board representation is considered mandatory. Thereafter, the empirical findings have 
shown that further formal collaborative agreements are made by a case-by-case decision. In a 
minor account of firms, the application of LOIs or MOUs and playbooks can be seen. This 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
60 
leads to the conclusion that besides the board seat, no standardized formal structures are in 
place, making the formal structures a static element for the CVC – start-up collaboration.  
The central finding of this study is the importance of an intact interpersonal relationship 
between the two collaborative parties. In that matter, CVCs strive for a close relationship with 
the founding team. Furthermore, CVC managers emphasized that interpersonal chemistry and 
shared values are seen as prerequisites for collaboration and can if absent lead to a dealbreaker. 
While a great awareness of the topic is present, active relationship building plans or the sharing 
of best practices are not displayed through mechanisms and heavily rely on personal incentive 
and experience. Among the four identified pillars, the interpersonal relationship is seen as the 
most flexible one and therefore provides the most development potential. CVCs should view 
the interpersonal relationship as a core strategic asset rather than just a way of communication.  
Another key finding is the facilitator role of the CVC and its avoidance of active operational 
involvement in the start-up’s business to ensure start-up autonomy. Furthermore, CVCs see 
themselves as enablers, providing strategic guidance, validation, and feedback. They extend 
their investor role to help the start-ups to help themselves, thereby gaining strategic insides for 
the corporate parent. While the strategic enablement is important, CVCs need to remain distant 
to a certain degree. Particularly in the dual role as an investor and board member at the same 
time, intact governance rules and strict separation, paired with trust and over-time relationship 
development, are aimed for mitigating the fear of misappropriation of information by the start-
up side. The ability to maintain an appropriate balance between a close relationship and 
professional distance is essential for long term alignment and a fruitful collaboration.  
Overall, the findings of this paper demonstrated an interdependency between formal and 
informal structures. While formal structures are providing an official connection to the start-
up, the informal interpersonal relationship ensuring the effectiveness of the collaboration and 
thereby are mainly responsible for the total success of the collaboration. To foster a fruitful 
collaboration, consensus on all 4 identified pillars needs to be found. CVCs ultimately need to 
balance both elements to secure long-term value creation and access to strategic insides for the 
parent firm.

6.2 Theoretical contribution  
This qualitative study addresses a broadly overlooked topic in current academic literature, regarding 
how formal and informal structures interplay in helping CVCs to gain strategic insights from their 
cooperation with start-ups.  
The four key pillars identified shape the collaborative approach from a CVC perspective and highlight 
the importance of interpersonal connection based on trust, personal chemistry, and founder-centric 
connection. These connections enhance the findings in current literature. The qualitative data analysis 
furthermore provides insights into how formal and informal structures are currently carried out in 
practice. This study shows that most CVC – start-up collaboration measures are not represented in fixed 
processes, revealing a lack of standardization in current case-driven decision making. This study reveals 
current blind-spots in the CVC – start-up collaboration structure, build by the CVC and shows possible 
room for enhanced value creation.  
This comprehensive study, based on the grounded theory-based framework, enhances the understanding 
of current CVC – start-up collaboration. The duality between formal and informal structures are 
analyzed, enhancing current literature findings, especially in the application of interpersonal 
relationship importance, while pointing out the need for advanced structured measures. Therefore, this 
study is not only enriching current academic findings but also provides solid groundwork for future 
research.

6.3 Practical contribution  
The identification of the four pillars, representing the key alignment areas in the CVC – start-
up collaboration are partly represented in the awareness of operating CVC managers, shown in 
the interviews. Furthermore, it should be noted that most collaborations generally lack 
structured frameworks and actionable processes that are continuously developed over time. In 
the following, actionable measures, resulting from the conducted interviews, are displayed for 
each pillar:  
1. Adjustable but formal structures: 
All CVC managers who participated in the interviews highlighted the importance of board 
(observer) representation with a small minority using additional frameworks like LOIs or 
MOUs. The application of playbooks was also seen in only one case.  
As current formal agreements in the CVC – start-up collaboration are made on a case-by-case 
basis, the following actionable recommendations should be considered:  
- 
Development of a modular toolkit, including a board seat and optional LOI and MOU 
templates 
- 
Utilize playbooks and roadmaps for transparency and expectation management 
regarding how the future collaboration could look like  
Formal structures should represent a contractual bond between the CVC and the start-up that 
can be enhanced as a clarification tool to set future development plans into writing. 
2. Interpersonal relationship: 
The interpersonal relationship should be seen as a strategic asset rather than a soft factor for 
communication and alignment. While interviewees continuously mentioned the importance of 
trust and shared values, no structured mechanisms are in place, to promote such factors in the 
CVC – start-up relationship. The following actionable items are recommended:  
- 
Create structured founder onboarding programs, to clarify mutual expectations for 
communication, share unwritten rules of the parent companies’ cultural environment, 
and provide an initial informal introduction to key parent company stakeholders for 
easier collaboration thereafter 
- 
Implementation of regular non-agenda driven check-ins, to foster the creation of 
organic trust  
==PAGE:70==
 
 
63 
- 
Assign a personal relationship owner in the CVC for coordination and monitoring the 
development of interpersonal relations between the CVC and the start-up. Furthermore, 
that person should be responsible for the collection and sharing of best practices in 
relationship building, based on CVC managers experience 
- 
Defining KPIs such as e.g., responsiveness of the start-up team, frequency, and quality 
of informal communication, and proactive sparring requests from the start-up can be 
implemented and monitored, enabling the quantification of the current relationship 
health status 
The enhancement of the interpersonal relationship represents the most dynamic and 
underutilized pillar identified through the conducted interviews. A further development of the 
pillar may lead to a deeper connection, enhanced communication, faster exchange of 
information and more detailed strategic access for the CVC and its parent company.  
3. Start-up autonomy: 
The interviews have shown that CVC managers see themselves as strategic enablers and 
facilitators inside the parent company while keeping a distance to ensure start-up autonomy. 
The following actionable measures are recommended:  
- 
Creation of a strategic enablement catalog, proactively displacing the ability of CVC 
facilitation and support inside the parent company 
- 
Clear definition of the CVC facilitation role, setting clear boundaries and ensuring an 
operational hands-off approach throughout the collaboration with the start-up 
CVCs need to transparently represent the facilitator and sparring partner role, while seen as a 
strategic enabler for the start-up. This can improve transparency and motivation to share more 
delicate insights in order to benefit from complementary resources and deepen the overall 
relationship. 
4. Professional distance / parent firm loyalty:  
The fear of misappropriation of information is driving the fourth pillar, calling for professional 
distance, especially in board member roles. Furthermore, it is obvious that parent firm loyalty 
of the CVC is set by the initial arrangement of the collaboration. In that matter, the following 
measures are recommended:  
- 
Definition of a conflict-of-interest protocol for board members 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
64 
- 
Emphasize on transparency and honesty in all formal and informal collaboration 
features, creating mutual trust to mitigate the misappropriation problem  
The creation of mutual trust and in-place governance roles are seen as the most effective way 
of addressing the problem of misappropriation of information. Any supportive measures are 
seen as an additional layer of problem mitigation, helping to ensure a professional distance, 
while closely cooperating.

6.4 Limitations and suggestions for future research  
Limitations: 
This qualitative study was based on conducted interviews with CVC managers and time 
restricted over 6 months. Consequently, a limited number of interviews were conducted, and 
the small sample size of 12 interviewed managers can be seen as the major limitation of this 
study. Furthermore, all interviewed managers were working in different industries, and the 
findings therefore display a rather general point of view with possible differentiation in specific 
industries present but not identified.  
Focusing on the geographical scope of the conducted interviews, it is noticeable that only 
European CVCs were represented, with an overhand of the Nordics (Denmark, Sweden, 
Norway, Finland) and Germany making the geographical scope even more narrow. The 
findings therefore might not represent current practices in other established markets like North 
America and Asia.  
This study presents the view of the CVC, aiming to understand and provide an answer on how 
CVC firms perceive and structure formal and informal collaboration with start-ups to achieve 
strategic benefits. In that matter, the perspective of the start-up is not represented.  
The application of the Gioia method, as a framework for analyzing the interview gathered data, 
represents only a snapshot in time. Therefore, the study does not represent a development, nor 
does it cover planned future measures regarding an enhanced collaboration between CVCs and 
start-ups.  
Combined, this study offers an insider view on which pillars the CVC – start-up collaboration 
is based, and which parts allow for optimization, grounded on the current state of interview 
results. These insights can bridge the gap in literature on the interplay between formal and 
informal structures aiming for strategic growth from a CVC perspective.  
 
Suggestions for future research:  
This study provides pioneering work in further identifying on how CVC firms perceive and 
structure formal and informal collaboration with start-ups to achieve strategic benefits. 
Advanced research can be done in all four identified pillars, with an extended focus on the 
interpersonal relationship as it proves to be the most promising for shaping the CVC – start-up

Introduction: 
My name is Benedikt Cohrs and I am enrolled in the master’s program of Technology Entrepreneurship 
at the Technical University of Denmark. My research aims to investigate how CVC firms design and 
balance formal and informal collaborations with startups to achieve their strategic goals. All the infor-
mation shared will be handled with the greatest care, used only for academic purposes, and will not be 
disclosed to any third parties. I am happy to sign any non-disclosure agreement your company may 
require. 
Personal background:  
1- Can you briefly describe your role in the CVC program and how long you’ve been involved in 
corporate venturing? 
2- How is your CVC program structured within the corporate organization?  
3- What mandate does your CVC program have? Is it focused on financial returns or strategic 
ones? If strategic, which goals do you pursue? 
Questions about corporate-startup interactions  
1- Do you interact with startups? If so, what do you aim to achieve through that interaction? Does 
this relate to strategic goals of your CVC program you mentioned, if any?  
2- When starting a collaboration with a startup, what are the main and most important formal 
structures do you use?  
3- Do you think formal structures help reach the goals you mentioned about corporate-startup 
interaction? If so, how?  
4- What informal approaches do you use to build relationships with startups in the post-investment 
phase?  
5- Do you think these informal interactions contribute to achieving the goals you mentioned for 
corporate-startup interaction? 
6- Have you been in situations where informal collaboration compensated for weaknesses in for-
mal structures — or the other way around? Would you say that there is an interdependency 
between formal and informal structures? Does this interdependency influence achieving strate-
gic returns?  
7- Do you have best practices in managing formal and informal structural relationships with 
startups?
