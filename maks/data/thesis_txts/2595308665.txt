Abstract
The goal of this project is to build an accurate 3D pose model using a markerless approach, so that it
can be used for either advanced analytics or broadcasting purposes. This thesis investigates diﬀerent
deep learning based methods for 3D pose estimation in collaboration with TrackMan.
In the thesis, we propose four diﬀerent models and evaluate all of them on the three standard 3D pose
datasets, Campus, Human3.6m, and Panoptic. The best performing model is an end-to-end learnable
volumetric triangulation that uses tagmaps obtained from associative embedding to threshold the
features of each actor. This model achieved a new state of the art average precision on the Campus
dataset and showed comparable PCP and MPJPE scores to the current state of the art. We discovered
that the model could not adapt to new domains, as it performs signiﬁcantly worse when transferred
to a new dataset.
From our studies, we believe that it would be feasible for TrackMan to implement an end-to-end based
3D pose detection network, and integrate it into their sports tracking pipeline. With suﬃcient data,
we have shown that the models are capable of achieving average precision of over 99%, and an MPJPE
below 3cm, which should be suﬃcient for broadcasting and analytical purposes.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Markerless motion capture is the art of extracting human pose information, without any physical
system attached to the person such as a motion capture suit. Instead, purely relying on camera sensor
output and then utilizing computer vision techniques to derive the pose of a human from video or
image data outputs.
Human pose estimation is often in computer vision used to refer to human pose skeleton estimation,
which is what we are referring to throughout the thesis. The human pose skeleton is constructed from
a set of keypoints, also called joints which, when connected in the right way, forms limbs such as an
arm, leg, etc. as illustrated in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Visualization of two diﬀerent sets of keypoints connected with a skeleton to form limbs.
COCO Keypoints (left) and MPII Keypoints (right).
Often the 2D human pose, as shown in Figure 1.1, is enough to derive useful information such as the
action the person is performing, but for a selection of problems it is required to have 3D information.
An example of this could be video animation, but also in sports 3D information introduces a whole

new area of analyzing athletes’ exact motion patterns. This information can be useful to practice
speciﬁc motion patterns to increase performance and prevent injuries of the athletes. To compute 3D
information accurately, a multi-view setup where multiple cameras are looking at the same subject is
needed.
Especially in sports, markerless is key to the success of a motion capture system, as it is less invasive
and would allow motion analysis for real-time games. One could imagine seeing such a system installed
in major baseball stadiums or golf tournaments. Such a system would allow the coaches to analyze
the athletes’ motion and also allow broadcasters to playback a 3D animation of the athletes to analyze
their swing.
Further such a system could in i.e., baseball be used to derive more advanced statistics of the players
automatically. One relevant baseball statistic that we believe would be relevant to derive from the
motion system automatically is when the batter commits to his swing relative to the time at which
the pitcher throws the ball.
Through this thesis, we will, in collaboration with TrackMan, investigate 3D markerless motion capture
from a deep learning perspective. The ultimate goal is to see if we can get an accurate enough estimate
of the 3D pose only based on cameras such that TrackMan can deploy a model for broadcasting and
analytical purposes.
1.1 | Project scope
Since pose estimation in itself is a massive scope with multiple possible directions to take, we have
decided to state the problems we wish to investigate and solve in this thesis below.
• Investigate diﬀerent deep learning based 3D pose estimation techniques and evaluate their per-
formance.
• The models should all be multi-view models with stationary cameras, where we assume that we
can obtain the intrinsic and extrinsic parameters of the cameras.
• The main metric we will evaluate the performance on is pure accuracy, while we let runtime play
a signiﬁcantly smaller role.
• An ideal model will be able to generate visually good 3D pose results on sports-related data
captured with a multi-view setup.
1.2 | Datasets
Throughout the thesis, we are mainly focusing on existing human pose datasets, both 2D and 3D.
We have decided to do so to be able to compare our solution to already existing solutions. We have
also captured a small dataset at TrackMan to test the pipelines. However, the data we captured has
not been annotated and can only be used to give a visual idea of the model accuracy, and to test
how well the model generalizes to a new domain. This section will include a brief description of all of
the existing data used for either training, validation, or testing of models and submodels used in the
project. In contrast, our captured data and the capturing process is described in section 4.2. For a
quick summary of the datasets, see Table 1.1.

1.2.1 | Singe-view 2D pose detection data
For 2D pose estimation, two major datasets exist, where the most well known is the COCO Keypoint
dataset [1]. With 250000 annotated persons in about 200000 frames, we have some frames with multiple
persons meaning that it can be used for multi-person pose detection. The images in the dataset are
“images in the wild”, meaning that the scene and activities are uncontrolled. Further, the images are
not from a video sequence but instead independent images, meaning that we can not use the dataset
to evaluate temporal performance. A few representative examples of scenes and activities from the
COCO dataset is illustrated in Figure 1.2. The dataset was released for the ImageNet, and COCO
Visual Recognition workshop at ECCV in 2016, and all of the images have been labeled by hand with
a total of 17 keypoints for each person where especially the facial features are densely annotated.
Figure 1.2: Representative example of scenes and actions in the COCO dataset. The image is taken
from http://cocodataset.org/#keypoints-2016
The other commonly used dataset for single-view 2D keypoint detection is the MPII Human Pose
dataset [2] with 40000 manually annotated persons in 25000 frames. The dataset is in many ways
similar to the COCO dataset with a varying number of persons in each frame and “images in the wild”
with varying scenes and activities. A random selection of the frames and annotations is illustrated
in Figure 1.3. Again the dataset consists of independent images and not sequences, so we can not
use it to test any temporal aspects of the model but purely the performance of multi-person keypoint
detection. Contrary to the COCO dataset, the MPII dataset only comprises 15 keypoints per person
with less emphasis on dense annotations in the facial area. As we do not care for the facial pose in this
project, but rather a general joint movement, MPII contains all of the needed keypoints. The MPII
dataset was released in 2014 for the IEEE conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition
(CVPR).

the head features might be quite relevant for some tasks where it is crucial to know the direction
of which the person is looking. We have decided to simplify the problem and discard these mainly

1.2.2 | Multiview 3D Human Pose data
Human3.6m
Human3.6m is the largest multi-view dataset counted on the number of frames and a common bench-
mark dataset for single-person pose detection. The dataset consists of 3.6 million annotated 3D poses
with corresponding camera calibration for 4 HD cameras such that they can be mapped down to the
corresponding 2D frames resulting in a total of 14.4 million annotated 2D poses. The 3D points are
obtained from a precise motion capture system and not annotated by hand.
Figure 1.5: Examples of images from the Human3.6m dataset. Illustration taken from the original
paper, “Human3.6M: Large Scale Datasets and Predictive Methods for 3D Human Sensing in Natural
Environments” [3].
One drawback of the Human3.6m dataset is that the scene is quite simple, and the dataset only contains
11 actors acting out 17 diﬀerent scenarios, some of which are illustrated in Figure 1.5. Further, only
one actor is in the scene at a time, so multi-person performance can not be evaluated on the dataset.
Human3.6m also uses 32 joints to parameterize the pose, which can be converted to either one of the
common MPII or COCO pose formats.
Panoptic
In many ways, the Panoptic dataset is similar to the Human3.6m dataset with a limited amount of
actors acting out sequences in a controlled environment. The panoptic dataset is, however, a multi-
person dataset and contains a total of 1.5 million ground truth 3D skeletons. The Panoptic data is
recorded in a huge dome where everything is controlled, and the scene is captured from 480 low res
cameras and 31 HD cameras placed around the dome illustrated in Figure 1.6.

2.1.2 | Random sample and consensus - RANSAC
The point triangulation discussed in subsection 2.1.1 gives equal weight to each point pair, which
means that it is not very robust towards outliers. If just one of the n image points is an outlier and
does not correspond to the same world point as the other n−1 image points, it will result in a degraded
triangulation.
Random sample consensus (RANSAC) is an iterative method that tries to estimate a model with the
most support in data. Given some model M with parameters φ, we randomly sample S data points
from our data set di, i ∈{1, ..., M}, and estimate M(φ) using those data points. To evaluate the
estimated model, we compute the consensus which is deﬁned as
dist(M(φ), di) < T.
(2.10)
Where dist is a distance function, and T is a threshold for determining if di is an inlier or an outlier.
We count the amount of our M data points that are inliers, which is deﬁned as the consensus. This
process is repeated N times, and the model parameters with the highest consensus is the resulting
model. In terms of point triangulation, we can use RANSAC to pick which cameras to use to calculate
a point in world coordinates. When using RANSAC, the sample size S, should always be minimal [6],
and from subsection 2.1.1 we know that we need at least two cameras to estimate a point Q, and thus
S = 2. For the distance function, we use the reprojection error which given the estimated point in

2.3 | Fiducial Markers
As discussed in section 2.2, a set of known points in world coordinates needs to be known to calibrate
the extrinsic parameters of a camera. Those points should be easily recognizable by the camera, and
thus there has been developed several diﬀerent ﬁducial marker systems. A good ﬁducial marker system
for our application should have the following properties:
• Be easily detectable by an automated algorithm.
• Have a unique ID embedded, such that we know which marker has been detected.
• Have several correcting bits, to increase the chance of correctly classifying the marker.
• Should be square-based, such that we can use the four prominent points, to obtain the pose of
the marker.
• Each generated marker should have a high inter-marker distance, such that the markers within
the dictionary are very diﬀerent from each other.

In deep learning and especially in very deep networks vanishing or exploding gradients often occurs.

The construction of the Residual block in Figure 2.4 allows the network to learn the underlying model
more easily due to the identity connection. Remember that with neural networks in general, the goal
is to learn a mapping of the input x to the desired output, i.e., learn the mapping M such that
M(x) = y. With the identity connection introduced in the Residual block we can reformulate the
mapping as M(x) = R(x) + x, where R(x) is the residual. This simpliﬁes the learning of the entire
mapping to a problem where we, instead of optimizing the full mapping, only optimize the residual.

3.1 | 2D object detection
In pose detection there are in general two diﬀerent approaches, top-down and bottom-up. Top-down
pose estimation relies on ﬁrst detecting the people in the scene, and then estimate the joints of those
people. The other approach is bottom-up, which detects all the joints in the scene, and then decide
which joints belong to which people. In general top-down approaches tend to achieve higher accuracy
at the expense of being more computationally demanding. The reason for the increased computational
complexity is the main problem being divided into multiple steps as also stated in [19], since they also
require an extra network to detect the bounding boxes of the people in the scene.
3.1.1 | EﬃcientDet
At the time of writing the state of the art network architecture for object detection on the well-known
object detection dataset COCO [1] is currently EﬃcientDet [20] which achieves a mean average preci-
sion at 51.0.
EﬃcientDet is built of three diﬀerent modules. The ﬁrst one is a feature extraction backbone called
EﬃcientNet [21], secondly they use a bi-directional feature pyramid network BiFPN and ﬁnally they
have a class and box prediction network. The three modules can be seen illustrated in Figure 3.1.

Bi-directional feature pyramid network
One of the main contributions of [20] is the bi-directional feature pyramid network as illustrated in
Figure 3.2. The module introduces an eﬃcient way to ﬁnd a transformation f that aggregates weights
through multiple resolutions and outputs a list of new features P out = f(P in). Unlike a traditional
feature pyramid network FPN that aggregates the features in a top-down approach, meaning only
having connections from higher resolutions to lower resolution layers, the BiFPN extends it with
bi-directional connections, that allows information to ﬂow between low resolution features to high
resolution features as illustrated in Figure 3.2.

features equally. They argue that an unweighted fusion will result in an unequal contribution from the

3.1.2 | YOLOv4
You Only Look Once (YOLO) models have always been known for both their high inference speed and
competitive accuracy. The most recent version at the time of writing is YOLOv4 [22], that achieves on
par performance to EﬀcientDet [20] at half the inference time. It can achieve this by making several
improvements compared to Yolov3: A new backbone, a new loss function, improved data augmentation,
and spatial pyramid pooling.
The new backbone that YOLOv4 employs is the CSPDarknet53 [23], which is more computationally
eﬃcient, as it prevents excessive amounts of duplicate gradient information by truncating the gradient
ﬂow.
Previous YOLO iterations used Mean Squared Error to regress the bounding box position and size.
The bounding box location was represented as an oﬀset of an anchor location, and the height and
width were regressed as the scaling of a given anchor box size. The disadvantage of this approach is
that it is not scale-invariant, and thus tends to give a higher loss to large objects compared to small
objects. Instead, Bochkovskiy et al. [22] opt for the IoU loss [24], which considers the coverage of the
predicted and ground truth bounding box and thus makes it scale-invariant.
To increase the amount of training data, the authors create mosaics of the training images, by con-
catenating four images into a single image, which allows for the detection of objects outside of their
usual context. The authors also propose a new data augmentation technique called Self-Adversarial
Training (SAT). SAT works by having the network make adversarial attacks on itself, by feeding in
an input image, and update the image itself, rather than the network weights, and then train on the
altered image.

Chapter 4
Implementation
This chapter will present the models we have designed to estimate multi-person 3D human poses and
discuss the pros and cons of the diﬀerent design choices. Our aim with the models is to create a model
that achieves high performance on 3D pose estimation ultimately deployed for sports tracking and
biomechanics analysis. The objective is to get the most accurate results on persons in the wild and
not to create a model that can produce real-time 3D positions.
4.1 | Models
Based on existing literature and the adaptation of single-person and 2D pipelines to multi-person
3D pipelines, we have a total of 4 models. Two of the models are reimplementation of [29] and [32]
respectively. The other two models are our proposals on how the two reimplementations could be
extended to improve performance.
4.1.1 | Algorithmic pipeline
This pipeline is, to a great extent, a reimplementation of [29], but with some slight alterations. We
use a pre-trained EﬃcientDet [20] for generating bounding boxes around each person. These bounding
boxes are used to crop the images and then fed into an HRNet [25]. The HRNet has been pretrained on
the COCO keypoints [1] dataset. Both the EﬃcientDet and HRNet handle the images independently,
both temporally and in terms of view. This means that the information, in one view, will not help
make another view better, and the prediction at time t, will not be provided information about t −1.
This can be seen in the gray box in Figure 4.1.

be suﬃcient, despite the ambiguities when going from 2D to 3D. If, however, the 2D skeletons are not
accurate enough, we might not match the persons correctly across diﬀerent views. In that case, we
rely on RANSAC to handle any mismatched persons.
The performance of this pipeline is not suitable for real-time applications. Since the 2D pose associa-
tion stage, as described in subsubsection 3.3.2, is a greedy search problem and might require multiple
RANSAC triangulations per frame, making it computationally heavy. Our non-optimized implemen-
tation takes around 3 seconds per frame depending on the number of actors and cameras, which means
that we would need around a factor 100 speed increase to achieve real-time performance.

4.1.3 | End-to-End Volumetric Pipeline
Halfway through this thesis Hanyue Tu et al. [32] released a paper that handles the problem of having

detections, thus providing heatmaps in a higher resolution than HRNet. This gives accurate joint

The CPN solves the biggest downside of the two previous pipelines, which is the multi-view matching.

to inference through the CPN to obtain 3D locations of people, rather than solving a greedy search
problem. Hanyue Tu et al. state that it takes around 300ms to estimate 3D poses in a ﬁve-camera
setup, which is signiﬁcantly faster than our two previous pipelines.
The disadvantage of this pipeline is that it requires one to set a predeﬁned action space, where the
network can detect poses. In most 3D pose estimation datasets, the action space is pretty small, so a
volume of size 8m x 8m x 2m is suﬃcient. In real-world applications such as sports, the action space
might be an entire football ﬁeld, which would become computationally expensive. The authors in [32]
propose making the feature volume sparse, thus making it able to scale better, but even then, the
inference speed would increase substantially.
4.1.4 | End-to-End Volumetric Pipeline with tagmap thresholding
As described in subsection 3.2.2 HigherHRNet also produces tagmaps, used to associate joints belonging
to the same person. As volumetric triangulation creates a volume of size 2m x 2m x 2m around a
person, we might have cases where multiple persons are standing within the same volume or cases
where another person is occluding the volume. This introduces noise in the feature volume when we
unproject the feature maps. Therefore, we propose using the tagmaps to threshold the feature maps
of each camera view, such that we only unproject the parts of the feature map that belong to the
same person.

distance between tags is low if they belong to the same person. As the CPN gives us a point in 3D
for each person in the scene, we can unproject these points onto the tagmaps of each camera view and
thus obtain the tagmap embedding of each person. As the tagmaps are not consistent across diﬀerent
views, we need to do this for each camera view. When unprojecting the feature map, Fc, into a volume,
Vk, we project the center of the cuboid proposal Ck onto the tagmap Tc, to obtain the associative

Chapter 5
Results
To evaluate our models’ performance, we will evaluate them on the three datasets, Campus, Panoptic,
and Human3.6m. The main metric we will focus on is the average precision with a threshold of 100mm,
and the mean per joint precision error (MPJPE). The MPJPE will be calculated both as the absolute
MPJPE and relative to the pelvis location. The relative MPJPE is primarily used for the Human3.6m
dataset, as single person pose detection methods often opt for this metric. For the Campus dataset,
we also calculate the Percentage of Correct Parts (PCP), as this is the default metric on this dataset,
and allows us to compare with other methods. We will also conduct an ablative study where we, for
the two end-to-end pipelines, will use the ground truth pelvis locations for creating the volumes. This
will allow us to investigate whether creating a more complex CPN would yield higher performance.
5.1 | Training strategy
All of our networks are trained with a ﬁxed learning rate of 10−4 using the AdamW optimizer. We do
not perform any data augmentation and train all the networks until convergence.
5.1.1 | Algorithmic pipeline
As this model mainly focusses on associating poses across frames, we do not further train any of the
backbones. We use the EﬃcientDet-d0 architecture for object detection of humans, pretrained on
the COCO2017 dataset. For 2D pose detections, we use HRNet pretrained on the COCO-keypoints
dataset. We use a threshold of 40 cm i.e. τC = 0.4 for Campus, τC = 40 for Panoptic and τC = 400
for Human3.6m, as the world coordinates in the three datasets are in m, cm and mm respectively.
5.1.2 | Algorithmic pipeline with volumetric triangulation
This pipeline is in many ways similar to the Algorithmic Pipeline, but we use volumetric triangulation
instead of linear triangulation with RANSAC. In order to do this, we need to train the V2V network. To
train the V2V network, we use the ground truth 3D poses, and project them to each of the cameras,
to obtain a bounding box for the person. We then crop the image for each actor according to the

bounding box, and feed cropped images of the same actor from diﬀerent views to the V2V-network
and backpropagate the weights according to the ground truth 3D pose. We make sure to update the
intrinsic camera parameters, such that it handles the crop.
During testing, we utilize the algorithmic pipeline to obtain the pelvis location and association of poses
across cameras. We then crop out each associated pose from the diﬀerent cameras and feed them into
the V2V network to obtain the ﬁnal prediction.
The backbone used for volumetric triangulation is HigherHRNet that is pretrained on the COCO
keypoints dataset. Similar to the Algorithmic pipeline, we do not further train this, nor the EﬃcientDet
and the HRNet from the algorithmic pipeline.
5.1.3 | End-to-End Volumetric Pipeline
This pipeline has two networks that we need to train, the cuboid proposal network (CPN) and the
pose regression network (PRN). To train the CPN, we follow [32] by creating a ground truth heatmap
volume according to the distance to the ground truth pelvis location. We compute the gaussian score
of each voxel according to the distance to the nearest pelvis, using a gaussian distribution with mean
0 and a standard deviation of 15cm.
During ﬁrst iterations of the CPN training, it will provide a lot of false positives. We would have issues
with memory if we were to create volumes around all the false positives. Instead, we opt to create
volumes around the ground truth pelvis locations and feed those into the PRN for training. We do
not use the ground truth pelvis locations during testing, but rather the predicted locations from the
CPN.
The size of each volume fed into the PRN has a size of 2m x 2m x 2m, and we discretize it into a voxel
grid of size 64 x 64 x 64.
5.1.4 | End-to-End Volumetric Pipeline with tagmap thresholding
This pipeline is almost identical to the Volumetric pipeline from subsection 5.1.3, except for the fact
that we perform thresholding on the feature maps, such that only the features corresponding to the
given person are non zero. Both the CPN and PRN are trained similarly to the previous pipeline, but
we need to train them both from scratch, as the feature maps will have a diﬀerent distribution than
without the tagmap thresholding. For training we use τT = 0.6 as the tagmap threshold value.

Chapter 6
Discussion
In this chapter, we will go through each of our four pipelines and discuss their performance. We will
explain why we saw the performance that we did and discuss what could improve the pipelines even
further.
6.1 | Algorithmic pipeline
The algorithmic pipeline is the most basic of our pipelines because it does not contain any learnable
parameters. The fact that it ﬁnds associations from ﬁxed, possibly erroneous, 2D poses, is a lot more
error-prone, as it solely relies on RANSAC for handling wrong joint detections. We see this lack of
performance from the average precision, which is signiﬁcantly lower than our other pipelines for all of
the datasets.
On the Panoptic and Human3.6m dataset, we see that the algorithmic pipeline has a signiﬁcantly
lower average precision compared to our other models.
This lack of performance is primarily due
to the EﬃcientDet producing a lot of false positives. We have set the threshold to 0.3, such that
the network only needs to be more than 30% sure about a bounding box for us to consider it as a
prediction. This threshold was chosen empirically on the Campus dataset and used for all datasets,
but in order to decrease the false-positive rate, it might be beneﬁcial to optimize it for each dataset.
We perform non-maximum suppression to reduce the number of false positives, with an intersection
over union threshold of 0.5. Nevertheless, still after the NMS, there are still a lot of false positives. We
have not reported the recall of our models in chapter 5, but we saw that we got a recall of 70.06% using
the algorithmic pipeline on the Panoptic dataset. This tells us that our model performed pretty well,
but it just produces too many false positives. It might be possible to boost the precision by increasing
the EﬃcientDet bounding box threshold.
The 2D pose association stage iteratively matches the 2D poses across the diﬀerent views, where the
geometrical cost is the lowest. This way of matching the poses scales poorly with the number of poses
and number of cameras, as the number of possible combinations increases exponentially. We especially

scene at once. Our non-optimized implementation takes up to around 30 seconds per frame on the

6.2 | Algorithmic pipeline with volumetric triangula-
tion
As seen in chapter 5, it does not seem that our algorithmic pipeline with volumetric triangulation
worked as well as expected. The idea was to use the algorithmic pipeline for matching the actors across
diﬀerent views and then utilize the power of volumetric triangulation to generate better triangulations.
On both the Campus and Human3.6m datasets, using volumetric triangulation resulted in a lower
average precision. This might be because we use HigherHRNet to generate feature maps of the cropped
images in the volumetric triangulation stage. HigherHRNet is a multi-person 2D pose detector, which
means that it might have a degraded single person performance compared to HRNet. As the images
fed to the HigherHRNet are already cropped, it might have been better to use HRNet to generate
the feature maps used for volumetric triangulation. On the Panoptic dataset, we see that volumetric
triangulation outperforms linear triangulation with RANSAC in the algorithmic pipeline, which might
be because the Panoptic dataset contains more clustered actors. Thus the cropped images might have
more than one person in them. In this case, HigherHRNet might be the better option, as it can handle
multiple persons in the same image.
If we look at the MPJPE for all of the datasets, we see that volumetric triangulation provides more
accurate predictions than linear triangulation with RANSAC. This is expected as by allowing the PRN
to interpret the uncertainty in the feature maps; we should be able to ﬁlter out some noisy predictions,
which is impossible with linear triangulation with RANSAC as it does not utilize the uncertainty.
6.3 | End-to-End Volumetric Pipeline
As seen from the previous chapter, we cannot achieve the same results as [32] despite trying to replicate
their exact architecture. The only dataset where we can compare ourselves to [32] one to one is on the
Campus dataset, as that is the only dataset where [32] do not train their backbone. On the Campus
dataset, we achieve an average precision of 97.5, which is signiﬁcantly higher than Tu et al.’s 91.4. This
might be attributed to the fact that we opted for HigherHRNet rather than HRNet as the backbone of
our network, due to the higher resolution feature maps. Despite the higher average precision, we were
not able to get a better PCP than the authors as the PCP is the percentage of correctly estimated
parts, where a part/limb is considered correct if the distance between the estimated limb and the
ground truth limb is less than half of the length of the ground truth limb. If we look at our MPJPE,
we can see that it is 0.0584m, which is signiﬁcantly lower than the average limb length. This suggests
that we have some joints with a higher error in some poses, and thus being deemed a wrong limb
regression. Unfortunately, Tu et al. [32] do not include the MPJPE metric on the Campus dataset.
Thus we can not compare whether their predictions are better despite having a lower average precision.

6.3.1 | Cuboid proposals versus ground truth pelvis
Tu et al. [32] conducted an ablative study on the Panoptic dataset, where they experimented with
providing the ground truth pelvis location and ground truth heatmaps. They found that providing
the ground truth pelvis location decreased the MPJPE from 17.68mm to 16.94mm while providing
GT heatmaps and GT pelvis location decreased the MPJPE to 11.32mm. In our experiments, we did
not see any signiﬁcant change in neither average precision nor MPJPE. This suggests that our CPN
is providing good enough proposals, that the PRN performs the same even with slight inaccuracies in
the cuboid proposal. We experience the same performance whether we use the CPN or ground truth
proposals, suggesting that our CPN performs better than the one trained by [32]. As both our CPN
share the same architecture, it seems that our superior cuboid proposals are attributed to the fact
that we use HigerHRNet rather than HRNet. HigherHRNet produces higher resolution feature maps,
which makes it easier for the CPN to make accurate proposals.
6.4 | End-to-end volumetric pipeline with tagmap thresh-
olding
In chapter 5 we saw that thresholding the feature maps according to the tagmap provided by High-
erHRNet yielded an increased precision in the Panoptic and Campus dataset. This makes sense as,
given that the associative embedding is properly trained, it allows us to remove noise from the feature
maps caused by other actors in the scene. This also explains why we do not get increased performance
by using the tagmaps on the Human3.6m dataset, as it only contains one actor, and thus we only risk
thresholding out something important.
If one were to extend this idea, it could be expanded to the CPN network. Currently, we do not
threshold the feature maps that are fed into the CPN network, as we want the features of all the
actors to be projected into the volume. However, utilizing the information from the tagmaps could
make the job of making cuboid proposals easier for the CPN. A possible way to achieve this would be
to concatenate the tagmaps onto the feature maps, such that feature map of joint j from camera c,
Vj,c is stacked onto tagmap Tj,c to create V ⋆
j,c = [Vj,c Tj,c]. By unprojecting V ⋆
j,c into the volume, we
would allow the CPN to learn which features correspond to the same person, which would make the
job easier for the CPN and thus give a possible increase in performance.

6.6 | Best model for sports tracking
Back in the introduction in chapter 1, we brieﬂy touched upon which criteria an ideal model to be
deployed in sports tracking should meet. If one were to select the best model entirely based on the
results on the datasets with labels i.e., Campus, Panoptic, and Human3.6m it is clear that the ideal
model would be the end-to-end volumetric pipeline with tag-map thresholding. However, we see a quite
poor performance from the end-to-end models when we try to transfer the models to a new domain,
which becomes clear in the visual inspection of the results on our data.
This means that if TrackMan were to deploy a model, they would have to decide between generating
a dataset in the domain the model should be deployed or just decide that the results from the algo-
rithmic pipeline are suﬃcient to derive the relevant knowledge about the biomechanics of the athlete.
Generating a speciﬁc domain dataset could be done in multiple ways, as we have seen with the Cam-
pus, Panoptic and Human3.6m datasets that all use diﬀerent ways to generate 3D labels. From the
Campus dataset with only 1780 training frames, we also see that the dataset does not need to be huge
to achieve decent performance.
Generating a dataset as they do in the Campus dataset would probably be the cheapest approach in
regards to equipment required both one of the most time-consuming ways since it would require a per-
son to manually annotate the 2D pose from all of the cameras in all of the frames and triangulate them.
The most accessible approach in terms of time used capturing the data but also most expensive in
terms of equipment needed would be to capture a dataset and label it as they do in the Panoptic
dataset. Since the Panoptic dataset only used estimated 2D poses in order to compute a ground truth
3D pose. This is not the most precise approach, which we also see in some of the Panoptic labels.
However, they rely on the power of many and argue that with a decent 2D pose estimator and more
than a hundred camera views, they will, in most cases, be able to create a good 3D pose estimate to
use for training. We believe that this approach will be the best ﬁt to capture a dataset to be used in
a large environment, such as a baseball ﬁeld.
In general, we are however, of the belief that a pose tracking system from a TrackMan perspective
has most uses in a golf simulator setup. An accurate dataset could here be captured by using the
Human3.6m approach where only a few camera views would be needed and then rely on a full motion-
capture suit for 3D pose labels.
If TrackMan generated such a dataset, we believe that the end-to-end volumetric pipeline with tagmap
thresholding would yield accurate enough results for a model to be deployed in a production setup.
Another beneﬁt of going with this model is the inference time. Even though it has not been a priority
in this project, it is safe to conclude that the inference time of the end-to-end models is signiﬁcantly
lower than that of the algorithmic pipelines.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, we have studied the subject of multi-person 3D pose estimation from a multi-
view camera setup. We have successfully implemented four diﬀerent deep learning pipelines that all
succeed with a varying performance at solving the task.
Our proposed models can be divided into two diﬀerent groups with the algorithmic pipelines taking a
more classic approach and heavily relying on old computer vision tools combined with deep learning
models to estimate the 3D poses. The other group consists of our end-to-end models that more or less
are completely deep learning based, except for the unprojection of the 2D heatmaps to a 3D volume,
such that we teach a network to solve the task of associating poses across diﬀerent views.
We see a general trend that these new full deep learning approaches are superior to the models using
classical approaches, as they are less prone to make faulty 3D predictions from an imprecise 2D pre-
diction. In comparison, the algorithmic models obtain signiﬁcantly ﬂawed 3D predictions with just a
small error in the 2D predictions from one of the views.
We further experience that an appreciated beneﬁt from the more or less full deep learning end-to-end
models is that they are less computationally heavy during inference time, which would be needed if
we were to extend the model to a real-time solution.
In the middle of this thesis Hunyue Tu et al. released their paper, End-to-End Estimation of Multi-
Person 3D Poses from Multiple Cameras, deﬁning a new state of the art on 3D pose estimation
precision on the Campus and Panoptic datasets. Our model design has been hugely inspired by their
approach. With the change of backbone from HRNet to HigherHRnet and the added tagmap thresh-
olding, we believe that our proposed model will show an improvement in results if we also were to
train the backbone as they do for their model. We conclude this based on our current implementation,
without the trained backbone, tangent or even beat the average precision of Hanyue tu et. al’s model,
but fails to achieve the same mean per joint precision error. With a backbone trained on the speciﬁc
data, we believe that the mean per joint precision error will decrease to perform as good or better than
63

the model presented.
Our model is however still not perfect, and we propose that it might be beneﬁcial to include the tagmap
information in the cuboid proposal network as we believe it, in multi-person scenarios, will aid the
cuboid proposal network in accurately predicting a cuboid around all of the persons.
In order to utilize the end-to-end models in a real-world sports scenario, it seems that we need to train
on a domain-speciﬁc dataset. We ran experiments with a model trained on the Human3.6m dataset
and evaluated it on the Campus dataset. Here we saw that the model was poor at transferring to a
new domain. We argue that this might be due to slight domain-speciﬁc features in the HigherHR-
Net heatmaps, that the volumetric network overﬁts to. One possible solution would be to gather a
domain-speciﬁc dataset and train a model on that. Another possible solution would be to gather multi-
ple 3D pose datasets and train a single model on all datasets, to create a model that is domain invariant.
Overall we believe that it would be feasible for TrackMan to implement an end-to-end based 3D pose
detection network and integrate it into their sports tracking pipeline. With suﬃcient data, we have
shown that the models are capable of achieving average precision of over 99%, and an MPJPE below
3cm, which should be suﬃcient for broadcasting and analytical purposes.
