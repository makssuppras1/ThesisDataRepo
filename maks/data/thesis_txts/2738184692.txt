Abstract 
  This work addresses the carbonation of minerals and cementitious materials with the aim 
of mitigating CO2 emissions. Initially, it presents a general overview of the issue of 
carbonation in the cement industry, including the phenomenon of natural carbonation and 
various aggregate methods. Subsequently, it highlights significant findings from other 
pertinent projects on carbonation. A detailed experimental procedure for the carbonation 
of different materials under varying conditions is then described, alongside an analysis of 
the impact of these different parameters on carbonation efficiency. Following this, a kinetic 
model is developed to provide insights into the reaction kinetics during the carbonation 
process. Finally, the study presents useful conclusions derived from both the experimental 
procedure and the existing literature.

Project Purpose 
  The cement industry is a significant contributor to global CO2 emissions, accounting for 
5-8% of the total. To address this, mineral carbonation has emerged as a promising method 
for capturing CO2. This process utilizes naturally occurring minerals and specific fractions 
of concrete demolition waste, which have shown potential for CO2 capture through 
reactions that can occur at low temperatures under wet conditions or in high-temperature 
reactors. This project will specifically investigate dry high-temperature carbonation 
processes using alkaline solid wastes from various industries, such as fly ash, steel slag, 
bypass dust, and construction waste, which possess high carbon sequestration capacities. 
Although materials like serpentine, olivine, and wollastonite are known for their carbon 
sequestration potential, this project will focus on cement-based materials from construction 
waste. Current knowledge gaps include understanding how high-temperature carbonation 
processes are influenced by material chemistry, particle size, temperature, and gas 
composition. 
The primary goal of this project is to develop a thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)-based 
method to analyze carbonation of various materials, quantifying the influence of sample 
properties on CO2 capture. Laboratory experiments will be conducted to quantify the 
effects of chemical composition, particle size, porosity, temperature, and residence time on 
the carbonation process of different minerals. Based on the experimental results, a 
heterogeneous model for the carbonation of specific materials will be developed. 
The learning objectives for this study include proposing an appropriate experimental plan 
for a laboratory study based on a comprehensive literature review, acquiring the capability 
to conduct laboratory TGA experiments and interpret the resulting data, and developing a 
chemical engineering particle model for specific materials, focusing on the calcination 
process. This project aims to contribute significantly to the reduction of CO2 emissions by 
optimizing the carbonation process, potentially offering a scalable solution for industrial 
applications.

1.Introduction 
 
  Concrete, one of the most popular elements in global usage, primarily consists of a cement 
binder known as Portland cement, along with sand, gravel, and water. When mixed with 
water, the binder undergoes a chemical reaction producing hydrate phases that lead to the 
setting and hardening of concrete, crucial for its structural properties [1]. Currently, there 
are no viable substitutes for cement and concrete capable of fulfilling the structural needs 
of infrastructure construction. In 2019, the production of cement reached 4.1 gigatons 
worldwide [1]. Cement production, which is on the rise [2], contributes approximately 8% 
to global anthropogenic CO2 emissions, the highest from any industrial process [3]. With 
the Paris Agreement aiming to cap the global temperature rise at 2 degrees Celsius above 
pre-industrial levels, it is imperative to cut CO2 emissions from cement production 
substantially [3]. Several strategies have been proposed to reduce these emissions, with the 
most effective being the use of non-fossil fuels and substituting cement clinker with 
supplementary cementitious materials like industrial slags, fly ash, limestone powder, or 
calcined clays [4,5]. However, these methods have plateaued, making further substantial 
reductions challenging [2,6,7]. Innovative solutions like alternative cement binders, new 
cement production technologies, or carbon capture and utilisation/storage face obstacles 
like raw material availability and high costs [8,9,10,11]. More research is essential to 
expand these innovative approaches. Additionally, recycling cement and concrete waste 
for CO2 storage through accelerated carbonation of demolished concrete and similar 
materials can reduce their CO2 footprint and potentially lead to the development of CO2-
negative construction materials. Next, we will explore recent research on CO2 storage in 
cement and concrete and other materials.

1.2 Natural carbonation of concrete 
  When water is mixed with Portland cement, a reaction known as "hydration" occurs, 
leading to the formation of calcium silicate hydrates, which constitute approximately 60% 
of the products formed upon full hydration of the cement. Another product, calcium 
hydroxide, forms about 20% of these hydrates and plays a crucial role in maintaining the 
pH of concrete above 12.5. This high pH is essential to prevent corrosion in the mild steel 
reinforcement embedded within the concrete. As concrete ages, it absorbs CO2 from the 
atmosphere. This process is influenced by factors such as the concentration of CO2, 
ambient temperature, relative humidity, and the concrete’s porosity, permeability, and its 
residual buffer capacity, which refers to the amount of lime (CaO) that is not yet combined 
with carbonates or sulfates [12]. Changes in the phases of hydrated cement pastes due to 
carbonation can be accurately predicted using thermodynamic modeling tools, such as the 
CemGEMS web-based application [13]. 
 Initially, calcium hydroxide is converted into calcium carbonate, which may take various 
forms like calcite, aragonite, vaterite, or amorphous CaCO3 [14]. This transformation 
lowers the pH of the pore solution to around 9. Subsequent carbonation affects other 
hydrates, resulting in partial decalcification of the calcium silicate hydrates [15] and 
decomposition of ettringite, a calcium aluminate sulphate hydrate. The end result is a fully 
carbonated paste mainly composed of calcite, silica gel, alumina gel, and gypsum.The 
progression of carbonation moves from the outer surface of the concrete towards its 
interior, reducing the pH value. Once the carbonation front reaches the steel reinforcement, 
it can no longer prevent corrosion, potentially leading to spalling of the concrete cover in 
moist environments. Hence, concrete mix designs typically aim to limit carbonation as 
much as possible. Studies from Scandinavia [16] have shown that approximately 25% of 
the CO2 theoretically capable of being chemically bound by concrete is actually absorbed 
within 100 years, assuming a life cycle that includes 70 years of service and 30 years of 
landfilling post-demolition. A similar pattern was observed in Switzerland [17].

Although concrete has a substantial capacity to sequester CO2, natural carbonation is a 
slow and incomplete process. Cembureau, the European Cement Association, has 
forecasted that in their scenario aiming for net zero emissions by 2050, naturally 
carbonated construction materials will contribute negatively by only 51 kg of CO2 per ton 
of cement. Additionally, they predict that technologies related to carbon capture and 
storage/use, including mineral carbonation, will help reduce CO2 emissions by 280 kg per 
ton of cement [1]. 
 
1.3 Accelerated carbonation methods- a general overview 
  Carbon capture utilization and storage technologies are gaining traction as effective 
methods to mitigate climate change impacts in the cement industry [18]. Among these, 
carbon-dioxide mineralization enables permanent CO2 storage through the formation of 
thermodynamically stable carbonation products [19]. Industrial waste residues, which are 
more easily carbonated compared to naturally occurring magnesium silicates [20-23], offer 
a practical and cost-effective solution near CO2 sources, albeit in limited volumes. 
Recycled concrete paste (RCP), derived from the recycling of demolished concrete 
alongside recycled aggregates and sand, consists primarily of hydrated cement paste and 
fine aggregate and sand fractions [24]. Studies have demonstrated that RCP carbonation is 
a swift process under normal pressure and temperature conditions, exhibiting significant 
CO2 sequestration potential [25-29]. Moreover, the carbonated RCP can be utilized as a 
supplementary cementitious material (SCM) for producing new composite cements [30, 
31]. Mineral carbonation processes are generally classified into two types: direct and 
indirect carbonation [3]. Indirect mineral carbonation involves multiple stages, while direct 
mineral carbonation is a one-step process where the solid material is directly treated with 
CO2-containing gas or CO2 in aqueous solution. The primary benefits of direct carbonation 
include lower costs and environmental impacts due to its simplicity and reduced chemical 
consumption compared to indirect carbonation  [32]. Direct carbonation at normal 
temperature and pressure can be categorized into semi-dry and wet direct carbonation. In

the semi-dry process, water is present as vapor, as an absorbed layer on particle surfaces, 
and in the pores between particles. Wet carbonation involves a suspension of liquid water 
or an alkaline aqueous solution, where water serves as a solvent for dissolving solids and 
CO2, and as a reacting medium for precipitating carbonates[33]. At higher temperatures, 
dry direct carbonation involving gas-solid reactions, such as in calcium looping for gas 
cleaning or energy storage, is also feasible[34]. Extensive research over the past few years 
on the direct wet carbonation process of RCP has shown that significant carbonation 
degrees for calcium-containing phases, ranging from 50 to 95%, can be achieved within a 
few hours of reaction [25-27].

1.4.2 Kinetics and mechanisms involved in the carbonation of calcium silicate (CS) 
particles under mild environmental conditions 
  The attached PDF describes an investigation into the carbonation mechanisms of calcium 
silicate (CS) particles under mild conditions. The study focuses on understanding the 
==PAGE:13==
13 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
kinetics and developing models to describe the carbonation process effectively. 
At room temperature (20°C), the carbonation of CS particles in a water-bearing system 
adheres to the unreacted-core shrinking mechanism, where the primary rate-limiting step 
is product layer diffusion. This model is suitable when there is minimal loss of system 
water [38]. However, at higher temperatures (40-80°C), the traditional USCM model 
becomes unsuitable due to significant water evaporation, which alters the carbonation 
kinetics. To address this, the Surface Water Coverage Model (SWCM) was developed to 
account for the changes in surface water coverage, which is critical for the ionic reactions 
necessary for carbonation [39]. The reaction rate Rs is expressed as Rs=kcsΦ, where kcs 
is the apparent rate constant of the chemical reaction, and Φ represents the water coverage 
degree. The model incorporates parameters to account for the reduction in water coverage 
over time, influenced by temperature and humidity [40]. The SWCM fitting results indicate 
that the carbonation process at elevated temperatures is primarily controlled by surface 
water coverage due to thermal evaporation. Parameters kcs and kcp (related to the reaction 
rate and water coverage reduction rate, respectively) are used to fit the experimental data, 
showing increased fitting goodness with temperature rise, confirming the model's accuracy. 
At higher temperatures, the intrinsic chemical reaction rate constant kcs increases, but the 
activation energy is also higher due to limited water, affecting the carbonation conversion 
rate [41]. Experimental CO2 uptake data validated the SWCM, showing that carbonation 
efficiency significantly depends on temperature-induced water evaporation. For instance, 
carbonation at 80°C shows enhanced CO2 uptake due to rehydration activation, improving 
conversion rates [42]. Microscopic analysis (XRD, SEM) revealed changes in crystal 
structure and microstructure of CS particles post-carbonation, indicating the formation of 
different calcium carbonate phases (calcite, aragonite) influenced by temperature and 
rehydration[43]. 
The study's findings on SWCM and rehydration activation provide insights into achieving 
efficient CO2 mineralization under mild conditions, potentially lowering the energy 
requirements for industrial applications like concrete curing [37]. By addressing both the 
unreacted-core shrinking mechanism and the surface water coverage model, the research

2.1 Semi-dry carbonation of recycled concrete paste 
 
  In experimental study conducted by Zając et al.’s  (2022) , a synthetic cement paste was 
prepared with a water-cement ratio of 0.5 and hydrated for three months at 40°C to achieve 
a high degree of hydration. The carbonation process was carried out using a semi-dry 
carbonation setup designed to mimic a gas fluidization reactor at the laboratory scale. This 
setup featured a sieving tower for particle separation. The semi-dry carbonation was 
performed by equilibrating samples at the targeted relative humidity (RH) for 
approximately 20 hours in a pure N2 gas stream, followed by exposure to CO2-containing 
gas at the same RH. The temperature during carbonation was maintained at 20°C. For semi-
dry carbonation, experiments were interrupted at various times: 0.1, 0.25, 2.0, 3.0, 5.0, 30, 
==PAGE:15==
15 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
60, 120, and 360 minutes. Wet carbonation experiments were stopped after 0.1, 0.25, 0.8, 
1.9, 2.2, 3.4, 5.6, 12.0, 19.3, 66.7, 120, and 360 minutes. The CO2 concentrations in the 
gas flow were set at 5% and 25%. The calcium carbonate content (CaCO3) and 
corresponding CO2 uptake values were measured under different carbonation conditions. 
In wet carbonation, the total CaCO3 content was found to be 90 g/100 g cement with a 
conversion degree close to 100% at 360 minutes and a CO2 uptake of approximately 40 g 
CO2/100 g cement. For semi-dry carbonation at 100% RH and 25% CO2, the total CaCO3 
content was 66 g/100 g cement, with a conversion degree of approximately 73% at 360 
minutes and a CO2 uptake of 29.04 g CO2/100 g cement. At 90% RH and 25% CO2, the 
total CaCO3 content was 51 g/100 g cement, with a conversion degree of approximately 
56.6% at 360 minutes and a CO2 uptake of 22.44 g CO2/100 g cement. At 80% RH and 
25% CO2, the total CaCO3 content was 35 g/100 g cement, with a conversion degree of 
approximately 38.8% at 360 minutes and a CO2 uptake of 15.4 g CO2/100 g cement. For 
semi-dry carbonation at 100% RH and 5% CO2, the total CaCO3 content was 61 g/100 g 
cement, with a conversion degree of approximately 67.7% at 360 minutes and a CO2 uptake 
of 26.62 g CO2/100 g cement. At 90% RH and 5% CO2, the total CaCO3 content was 50.7 
g/100 g cement, with a conversion degree of approximately 56.3% at 360 minutes and a 
CO2 uptake of 22.31 g CO2/100 g cement. These results demonstrate that higher relative 
humidity and CO2 concentration lead to higher conversion degrees and greater CO2 uptake 
during the carbonation process of recycled cement paste[44]. 
 
 
2.2 Investigation on the fast carbon dioxide sequestration speed of cement based 
materials at 300oC-700 oC 
 
In experimental study conducted by Wang et al.’s (2021), the carbonation efficiency of two 
sizes of dry samples of High Early Strength cement (JIS5210): 2 mm thickness blocks and 
powder samples was investigated. The experimental setup included a furnace, CO2 and N2 
flow meters, a CO2 monitor, and a waste gas collection system. Thermogravimetric 
==PAGE:16==
16 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
Analysis (TGA) was performed using the TAPS3000S system. The samples were exposed 
to CO2 at varying temperatures within a controlled environment without additional 
vapor.Carbonation experiments were conducted at temperatures of 300°C, 400°C, 500°C, 
600°C, and 700°C. The residence times for the carbonation process ranged from 1 to 120 
minutes, with a standard duration of 60 minutes used for certain experiments. The CO2 
concentration in the furnace was maintained at 25%. The TGA results revealed two 
significant mass loss peaks: one at 400°C–550°C due to the decomposition of portlandite 
and another at 600°C–850°C due to the decomposition of calcium carbonate. Smaller 
sample sizes, particularly powder samples, demonstrated higher carbonation efficiency due 
to their increased surface area available for reaction. For CO2 uptake, the powder samples 
showed significant results: Powder A achieved a carbonation degree of 27.6% in 5 minutes 
at 600°C. Powder B achieved a carbonation degree of 30.9% in 1 minute at 600°C. 
In contrast, block samples exhibited different results based on thickness: 2 mm thick blocks 
achieved a carbonation degree of 25.4% in 60 minutes at 600°C. 10 mm thick blocks 
showed higher carbonation efficiency on the surface compared to the inner part, with a CO2 
sequestration efficiency of 22.6%/h at a 5 mm depth. The conversion degree varied with 
temperature: At 300°C, dry samples exhibited almost zero carbonation, while saturated 
samples showed a carbon dioxide sequestration efficiency of about 5%/h. At 400°C, the 
carbonation degree was slightly higher than at 300°C, with dry samples showing lower 
efficiency than saturated samples. At 500°C, saturated samples had a residue CH content 
of 66.5%, whereas dry samples had a residue CH content of 58.4%. At 600°C, both 
saturated and dry samples displayed almost no residue CH, indicating complete 
carbonation. At 700°C, there was a slight decrease in the carbonation degree compared to 
600°C due to the decomposition of unstable calcium carbonate. These findings indicate 
that the optimum carbonation temperature for maximum CO2 uptake and conversion degree 
is 600°C, with smaller sample sizes, such as powder form, achieving higher efficiency in 
shorter time frames [45].

2.4 Experimental investigation of carbonation mechanism in calcium silicate (CS) 
particles 
  The calcium silicate (CS) used in experimental study conducted by T. Wang et al.’s 
(2021),   was purchased from Alfa Aesar company with 99% purity and a standard particle 
size of less than 20 μm. The CO2 mineralization reactor was a stainless autoclave (220 mL) 
equipped with pressure sensors (Omron company, precision of 0.1 bar) and inlet/outlet stop 
valves for injecting/ejecting gas. The reaction temperature was controlled using an 
enveloping heating jacket with a variation of ±0.2°C. The carbonation method involved 
mixing CS samples with deionized water in 2 mL centrifuge tubes, maintaining a water to 
solid (w/s) ratio of 0.4. CO2 gas (99.9% purity) was injected into the reactor at 40 bar 
pressure. The experiments were conducted at various temperatures: 20°C, 40°C, 60°C, and 
80°C. The reaction times varied between 48 hours and 114 hours, with specific conditions

19 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
detailed as follows: A1: 20°C & 48 hours, A2: 20°C & 114 hours, A3: 40°C & 48 hours, 
A4: 60°C & 48 hours, A5: 80°C & 48 hours, A6: 80°C & 24 hours followed by rehydration 
for an additional 24 hours. The concentration of CO2 used in the experiments was 
maintained at 40 bar throughout the experiments. Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA) was 
used to evaluate the CO2 uptake and the changes in mass related to the carbonation process 
of the CS particles. The initial CO2 uptake reached a relatively low level of approximately 
14.7 wt% at 40 bar and 80°C without rehydration. However, rehydration significantly 
enhanced the CO2 uptake. Specifically, the CO2 uptake increased by 58.16% with 
rehydration, demonstrating the critical role of surface water coverage in the carbonation 
reaction of CS particles. At 80°C, the CS sample lost over 80% of system water, resulting 
in a lower CO2 uptake of about 60% compared to the uptake at 20°C. The highest CO2 
uptake was observed at 40°C with a conversion rate indicating optimal reaction conditions, 
consistent with CO2 curing of concrete. 
 
 
The reaction time had a significant impact on the conversion degrees. At 20°C for 48-114 
hours, the unreacted reactants significantly decreased, indicating that longer reaction times 
improved the conversion of calcite at room temperature. A comparison of different 
conditions revealed that A1 (20°C, 48 h) represented the initial stage with limited 
conversion, while A2 (20°C, 114 h) showed extended time increased conversion 
significantly. A3 (40°C, 48 h) indicated the optimal temperature with the highest 
conversion rate observed. In contrast, A4 (60°C, 48 h) showed higher temperature with 
moderate conversion, and A5 (80°C, 48 h) indicated the highest temperature with 
significant water loss impacting conversion. Finally, A6 (80°C, 24 h reaction + 24 h 
rehydration) demonstrated that rehydration reactivated carbonation, leading to higher 
conversion rates. Quantitative values for the conversion degrees were as follows: 
− A1 (20°C, 48 h): Conversion degree of approximately 14.7 wt%.

in the CO2 uptake from the TGA results. The effect of reaction time was also evident as 
prolonging the reaction time from 24 h to 114 h at 20°C significantly increased the 
conversion rate of CS, as evidenced by the reduction in wollastonite characteristic peaks 
and the increase in calcium carbonate formation[36]. 
 
 
2.5 Increasing efficiency of carbon dioxide sequestration through high temperature 
carbonation of cement-based materials 
 
  The carbonation conditions and results of the TGA analysis for the experiment on 
increasing the efficiency of carbon dioxide sequestration through high-temperature 
carbonation of cement-based materials (Wang et al.’s 2019) are as follows: 
Cement paste samples were made from high early strength cement, which has low calcium 
carbonate content when compared to other types of cement, and cured in water for more 
than 90 days to ensure complete hydration after demolding. Two types of sample sizes with 
70% W/C were applied: 2mm x 10mm x 10mm blocks and particles. Powder samples were 
==PAGE:21==
21 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
ground from block ones in a glovebox filled with 100% nitrogen gas to avoid carbonation 
reaction and then sieved. The equipment used included a furnace with a broad temperature 
adjustment range from 20°C to 1500°C, a ring roll mill for grinding samples, and a Rigaku 
powder X-ray diffractometer. The carbonation process involved exposing samples to a 
mixed gas environment of CO2 and N2, with specific relative humidity (RH) values 
controlled by adding a salt solution. The carbonation temperatures tested ranged from 20°C 
to 300°C, with the optimum carbonation temperature found to be around 100°C. The total 
carbonation time was controlled up to 60 minutes, with intervals for adding liquid water. 
The CO2 concentration was maintained at 25% by controlling the flow rates of nitrogen 
and carbon dioxide. For the TGA process, samples were first ground in a ring roll mill, 
sieved at 315 µm, and then subjected to the analysis in a dry nitrogen atmosphere. The 
temperature was increased at a rate of 20°C per minute from normal temperature to 105°C, 
held for 20 minutes to release free water, and then increased again to 1000°C at the same 
rate. The TGA results indicated significant peaks corresponding to the decomposition of 
calcium hydroxide (CH) around 400-550°C and calcium carbonate around 550-800°C. The 
carbonation degree was defined as the total CO2 fixation with calcium ions in the samples. 
The maximum carbonation degree was achieved at 100°C with a rate of 16.4% per hour in 
the first hour. The DTG curves showed that higher carbonation temperatures led to larger 
peaks related to calcium carbonate decomposition and a broader temperature range of mass 
loss, indicating the presence of polymorphs of calcium carbonate such as vaterite. At 
100°C, both calcium carbonate and CH contents were lower than those at 20°C and 60°C, 
suggesting that other hydrated substances like calcium silicate hydrate (CSH) contribute 
more to calcium carbonate formation at higher temperatures. Adding liquid water at 
intervals during carbonation significantly improved the carbonation degree. The optimal 
interval was found to be 30 minutes, resulting in a maximum improvement of 34.1%. 
Regarding CO2 uptake, the CO2 uptake rates at various temperatures were as follows: at 
20°C, it was 0.98% per hour; at 40°C, it was 4.82% per hour; at 60°C, it was 6.41% per 
hour; at 80°C, it was 7.58% per hour; at 100°C, it was 8.05% per hour; at 120°C, it was 
5.72% per hour; at 140°C, it was 4.83% per hour; at 200°C, it was 3.55% per hour; and at 
300°C, it was 2.38% per hour. The CO2 uptake rate peaked at 100°C with 8.05% per hour, 
==PAGE:22==
22 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
showing significant improvement compared to other temperatures. The carbonation degree 
at various temperatures indicated that the highest carbonation degree occurred at 100°C, 
achieving 16.4%, while at 20°C, the carbonation degree was only 2.4%. The effect of water 
addition on the CO2 uptake was also significant. Without water addition, the CO2 uptake 
rate was 8.05% per hour. With water addition at a 15-minute interval, the rate improved to 
9.22% per hour (14.5% improvement). At a 20-minute interval, the rate was 10.40% per 
hour (29.2% improvement), and at a 30-minute interval, it was 10.81% per hour (34.3% 
improvement). The optimal interval for water addition to maximize CO2 absorption was 
found to be 30 minutes. These findings highlight the importance of temperature and water 
management in enhancing the carbonation process and CO2 sequestration efficiency in 
cement-based materials[47].

Further refinements and advanced techniques can enhance the model's predictive power, 
contributing to a deeper understanding of the reaction kinetics.This detailed approach 
ensures that the mixed model captures the complexities of the carbonation process under 
varying temperature conditions, making it a valuable tool for optimizing the process and 
improving efficiency. 
 
 
 
 
5. Discussion 
  The efficiency of carbonation processes varies significantly depending on the materials 
used, the operational conditions, and the specific methods applied. By comparing the 
aggregated experimental data from this project with findings from relevant literature, we 
can better understand these variations. Sorbacal, under conditions of 700°C for 1 hour with 
70% CO2 concentration, showed a CO2 uptake of 67,8% and a conversion degree of 
86,29%. This material exhibits high carbonation efficiency at elevated temperatures and 
higher CO2 concentrations, which aligns with the high-temperature carbonation processes 
documented in the literature. Calcium hydroxide samples (Ca(OH)2)  under conditions of 
600°C for 1 hour with 25% CO2 concentration demonstrated better CO2 uptake, achieving 
ranges between 28%-63% CO2 uptake and respective conversion degrees range among 
36%-81%. The literature suggests that Ca(OH)2 has strong carbonation potential due to its 
==PAGE:71==
71 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
reactive nature, particularly at these conditions. Calcium oxide samples (CaO), under the 
same conditions as Ca(OH)2 (600oC, 1h, 25%CO2), showed a CO2 uptake range between 
3,72% - 48,05% and respective conversion degrees range 4%-61%. Similar to Ca(OH)2, 
CaO benefits significantly from high-temperature carbonation, achieving substantial 
conversion degrees even with lower CO2 concentrations. Recycled concrete fines (RCF-
HC) tested at several different conditions of temperature, residence time and concentration 
exhibited moderate carbonation efficiency levels with a CO2 uptake range between 1%-4% 
and a respective conversion degree range of 5%-22%. This is consistent with findings in 
the literature, where RCF shows significant but varied uptake depending on specific 
conditions. Higher temperatures and longer residence times generally enhance its 
efficiency. Cement paste when tested under the same conditions with RCF showed 
relatively lower carbonation efficiency with a CO2 uptake of 3,16% and a conversion 
degree of 6,19%. This reflects its complex composition, which may inhibit CO2 diffusion 
and reaction compared to more reactive materials like CaO and Ca(OH)2. 
Wood fly ash and glacial rock flour, under similar conditions of 600°C for 1 hour with 25% 
CO2 concentration, demonstrated negligible CO2 uptake and conversion degrees, 
indicating limited potential for carbonation. Literature also points to the generally low 
reactivity of such materials for CO2 sequestration. The literature review provides further 
context and supports the experimental findings. For instance, Zhang et al. (2020) on RCF 
showed a CO2 uptake of 15% and a conversion degree of 56,69% under optimal conditions, 
which is significantly higher than the experimental results, possibly due to differences in 
material preparation and specific experimental setups. Studies by Xuan et al.s’ (2016) and 
Bertos et al.s’ (2004) report high efficiency for CaO and Ca(OH)2, respectively, which 
aligns with the experimental results showing high carbonation potential for these materials. 
Liu et al. (2021) discusses the performance of Ca(OH)2 under slightly varied conditions, 
confirming that higher residence times and optimal CO2 concentrations enhance uptake. 
Dananjayan et al. (2016) provides insights into CaO's efficiency, supporting the 
experimental finding that CaO performs well under high-temperature carbonation 
processes. 
  A notable observation from the literature is that semi-dry or wet carbonation methods 
==PAGE:72==

often yield higher conversion degrees (up to 100% under optimal conditions) compared to 
direct gas-solid carbonation findings from the experimental procedures in this project. 
Semi-dry carbonation, as described by studies such as those by Bertos et al. (2004), 
involves water vapor presence, which aids in the formation of a reactive layer on the 
material surfaces, enhancing CO2 diffusion and reaction kinetics. Wet carbonation, which 
fully immerses the materials in an aqueous CO2 solution, facilitates even greater reactivity 
by maintaining optimal moisture levels necessary for efficient carbonation. These methods, 
therefore, show higher conversion degrees and CO2 uptakes. The comparative analysis 
highlights that high-temperature carbonation materials like Sorbacal, CaO, and Ca(OH)2 
achieve higher carbonation efficiencies at elevated temperatures (600°C to 700°C) and 
longer residence times. These conditions optimize the reaction kinetics and enhance CO2 
uptake. Different materials respond uniquely to carbonation conditions; for instance, 
Ca(OH)2 and CaO show high reactivity and significant CO2 sequestration potential, while 
materials like wood fly ash and glacial rock flour exhibit limited carbonation efficiency. 
To maximize carbonation efficiency, it is crucial to optimize temperature, CO2 
concentration, and residence time based on the specific material properties. Future research 
should explore these parameters further to enhance the practical application of carbonation 
processes for CO2 sequestration.

6. Conclusions and suggestions for future research 
   The research’s aim is to investigate the carbonation potential of various materials, 
particularly focusing on cement-based materials from construction waste, under high-
temperature conditions. The experiments utilized thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) to 
assess the carbonation efficiency and CO2 uptake of these materials. This study contributes 
to the broader goal of developing efficient methods for CO2 sequestration, which is crucial 
for mitigating the environmental impact of industrial CO2 emissions.The key findings 
revealed that calcium hydroxide (Sorbacal) and calcium oxide demonstrated significant 
carbonation efficiencies at elevated temperatures (600-700°C). For instance, Sorbacal at 
700°C with 70% CO2 flow achieved a carbonation efficiency of 86,29%. The carbonation 
efficiency of materials was generally higher at increased temperatures and CO2 
==PAGE:74==
74 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
concentrations. This aligns with the theoretical understanding that higher thermal energy 
and CO2 availability enhance the carbonation reaction kinetics. Different materials 
exhibited varied responses to carbonation conditions. For example, recycled cement fines 
showed lower carbonation efficiencies compared to pure calcium hydroxide or calcium 
oxide. The actual CO2 uptake for recycled cement fines was notably lower, with some 
experiments recording  negligible CO2 uptake especially in lower temperatures.  
Wood fly ash and glacial rock flour displayed limited carbonation potential under the tested 
conditions, which indicates that not all industrial by-products are suitable for effective CO2 
sequestration through carbonation. The residence time, CO2 concentration, and carbonation 
temperature were critical factors influencing the carbonation efficiency. Longer residence 
times and higher CO2 concentrations generally improved the CO2 uptake. For instance, 
calcium hydroxide samples carbonated at 600°C for 180 minutes with 25% CO2 showed 
higher uptake compared to those with shorter residence times. A comparative analysis of 
different temperatures showed that Sorbacal's carbonation efficiency increased 
significantly with temperature, demonstrating a positive correlation between temperature 
and carbonation efficiency. There was a noticeable discrepancy between the theoretical 
CO2 uptake and the actual CO2 uptake in several experiments. This suggests that practical 
limitations, such as material surface area, porosity, and reaction kinetics, play a significant 
role in the efficiency of the carbonation process. The highest theoretical CO2 uptake 
recorded was 78,57%, but actual uptake varied significantly, often achieving only a fraction 
of the theoretical value. This highlights the need for optimizing material properties and 
experimental conditions to bridge this gap. The findings from this study provide valuable 
insights into the carbonation behavior of various materials under high-temperature 
conditions. The high carbonation efficiency of calcium-based materials suggests their 
potential for industrial-scale CO2 sequestration. However, the variability in results across 
different materials underscores the need for tailored approaches based on specific material 
properties. Future research should focus on optimizing material properties by enhancing 
the surface area, porosity, and chemical reactivity of materials to improve actual CO2 
uptake. Additionally, exploring semi-dry and wet carbonation methods, which have shown 
==PAGE:75==
75 
Technical University of Denmark    
 
higher conversion degrees in literature, can complement high-temperature carbonation. 
Assessing the long-term stability of carbonated products and their performance in real-
world applications is also essential. Comprehensive economic and environmental 
assessments are necessary to evaluate the feasibility, cost-effectiveness, and environmental 
impact of scaling up the carbonation process for industrial CO2 sequestration. In 
conclusion, while high-temperature carbonation presents a promising avenue for CO2 
capture, further research and optimization are essential to maximize its potential and 
practical applicability in mitigating global CO2 emissions. 
   Future experiments could be conducted at various high temperatures, CO2 concentrations 
and residence for in which the samples of the case project did not be tested, in order to have 
a more holistic dataset and more accurate outcomes could me extracted regarding their 
carbon dioxide sequestration potential. Further treatment of some materials (grinding, 
hydration) that did not appear efficiency in carbonation could also be applied as a possible 
improvement of their performance. Likewise, water vapor in our experimental procedure 
could also be a significant parameter in order to come up with more effective outcomes.
