Abstract 
It took millions of years to establish a chemical equilibrium between the crude-oil, brine and 
rock (CBR) including all the components of the reservoir. Oil has been the main source of en-
ergy for the past 100 years and is very essential for everyday life. It is used to produce, jet 
fuel, gasoline, synthetic materials, plastic, synthetic oils and asphalt. The use of oil has been 
constantly ongoing ever since the first oil spring was established in Canada in 18581. This 
field is still in production although not much is left. This makes oil essential and is therefore a 
necessity to optimize and enhance the production.  
Major part of the oil reservoirs today is waterflooded to improve the oil recovery. The bene-
fits for waterfloods are; 
• 
Give pressure support to the reservoir as its depleted during production, and to keep 
the pressure above the bubble point pressure Pres > Pb. 
• 
And taking advantage of the viscous forces and displacing the oil with water.  
Water injection has large beneficial values financially as well as environmentally. Water in-
jection has a huge potential with regards optimization and injection of optimal ionic concen-
trations and thereby enhancing the oil recovery and the life of the reservoir. This project in-
vestigates spontaneous imbibition with smart water. 5 different experiments are performed 
with different salinity concentrations and one where regular SW is diluted 10 times. Out of 
the 5 only the SW will be tested with constant salinity. All the following are provided by 
DHRTC; The crude oil from the Danish North Sea, formation water used during saturation 
and the 5 water compositions and the cores are from the Danish rock formation Stevns Klint 
(SK) as a substitute for the actual reservoir rock. All experiments are performed under reser-
voir temperature at 60°C but without any pressure.  
Out of the four brine compositions 3 showed medium to high oil recovery and the SW experi-
ment did not show any promising results due to core breakage. IR was performed and showed 
a significant decrease in carboxylic acids and amides after imbibition.

Oil recovery 
Recovering oil is the process of extracting crude oil from and oil field. The definition of oil 
recovery is partitioned into 3 different categories.  
1. Primary recovery – recovery by depletion 
2. Secondary recovery – recovery by water or gas flood, the process which is applied af-
ter primary reservoir development.2 
3. Tertiary recovery – recovery of the residual oil after the primary and secondary recov-
ery(chemical flooding etc.)2 
There are 2 different kinds of tertiary recovery, IOR and EOR.  
• IOR - Improved oil recovery, injection of fluids, which are normally pre-
sent in the reservoir (water, reservoir, gases) 
• EOR – Enhanced oil recovery, injection of fluids which are not normally 
present in the reservoir (surfactants, polymer etc.) 
The concepts of IOR and EOR are often mixed, and sometimes used as a synonym for tertiary 
recovery. Nowadays these methods can be used at any stage of reservoir development and are 
less commonly used.  
Primary recovery 
Primary recovery of oil is solely driven by the natural energy which is available to the reser-
voir. The natural energy can be pressurized fluids and rocks due to millions of years of com-
paction with high temperatures. By releasing the internal energy gradually in the reservoir, the 
oil can be produced. Through the use of natural energy, the adjacent aquifers which are pre-
sent and can also be used in specific reservoirs for oil production.3  
In conventional oil and gas reservoirs, the sources for natural energy can be many, including 
volatilization characteristics of petroleum fluids, expansion of the compressed gas, the effect 
of nearby aquifers and high reservoir pressure. All these mechanisms describe the primary 
reservoir performance to varying degrees. Many times, there is used a combination of the dif-
ferent mechanisms in order to increase recovery. Historical data have shown that recovery can 
be up to 80% for gas reservoirs with good porosity and permeability, but up to 10% for heavy 
oil reservoirs with unfavourable rock characteristics.3

If dealing with an unconventional oil reservoir the forces of nature are inadequate to produce 
the oil and gas. Here, techniques such as multistage hydraulic fracturing in horizontal wells    
(as in shale gas) or applying thermal energy (as in oil sands) to produce the unconventional 
accumulations of petroleum.3 
Secondary recovery  
This method involves the injection of an external fluid like, water or gas through the injection 
wells located in the rock, which will then displace the in-situ oil, forcing it to move ahead to-
wards the producing well and then to the surface. The purpose of this recovery method is to 
maintain the reservoir pressure and making it easier to move the hydrocarbons towards the 
producing wells. Most commonly water injection or gas injections are used. Usually this pro-
cess has already started during the primary recovery and is a form of enhanced recovery. This 
will continue until a specific amount of water or gas is produced and it’s no longer economi-
cally beneficial to keep injecting. Primary and secondary recovery produces about 15% to 
40% of original oil in place (OOIP) and is then considered a good recovery4. 
Tertiary recovery 
This is the 3rd stage of hydrocarbon recovery and follows the pressure maintenance and water-
flooding from the two previous stages. As mentioned earlier there are two different types of 
tertiary recovery IOR and EOR. Most commonly the tertiary recovery method is substituted 
with enhanced oil recovery since IOR is implemented along the way, and it will be a mix be-
tween primary and secondary recovery. EOR can potentially increase the recovery to 60% or 
more, while primary and secondary reach their limits around 40% of a reservoirs oil5.

Thermal flooding 
Introducing heat into the reservoir is a general form for injection process and beneficial for 
the mobility of the oil in the reservoir and helps with the production. The purpose of thermal 
recovery is to lower the viscosity of heavy/thick oils with API < 207. These oils are quite im-
mobile and cannot flow unless they are heated, and the viscosity is lowered sufficiently so 
they can move towards the producing well. When the oil is introduced to heat the crude oil 
undergoes both physical and chemical changes. Physical properties are such as viscosity, spe-
cific gravity and the interfacial tension (IFT) which are altered. Chemical changes will in-
volve cracking, which is the breakage C-C bonds, and thereby creating a compound with 
lower molecular weight. And dehydrogenation which is the rupture of C-H bonds. According

Cyclic steam injection 
The CSI method is commonly used in reservoirs with thickness greater than 30ft and depth 
less than 3000ft. Desirable properties are high porosity and oil saturation. Typically, the re-
covery factor (RF) is around 10-30%. Cyclic steam injection is done in 3 stages; injection, 
soaking and production. In the injection stage a slug of steam is injected into the well to heat 
the oil in the surrounding reservoir. The second stage is the soaking phase, requires that the 
well is shut down for some days to allow the steam to heat the reservoir around the well 
properly (soaking phase). In the final and 3rd stage the lowered viscosity oil is produced 
through the same injection well, which is now converted into production mode until the heat 
is dissipated with the production. It’s normal to let the production run a few of these Huff & 
Puff cycles before changing to a different kind of thermal EOR method i.e. steamflooding.7 
Steamflooding 
Unlike the CSI method the steamflooding has different wells for injection and different wells 
for production. High quality steam is injected into the reservoir with the heavy-oil, and the 
steam heats the oil and pushes it forward towards a producing wellbore. As with all the cases 
when using thermal flooding the steamflood has the mechanism of lowering the oil viscosity 
due to increase in temperature as seen on Figure 2, while the second mechanism is the steam 
and hot water physically pushing the oil forward. The RF for steamflood is normally higher 
than that of CSI and ranges from 40-60%.8

Steam-Assisted Gravity Drainage (SAGD) 
This technique can be considered as an advanced form of steam injection: here 2 horizontal 
wells are drilled into the oil. The distance between these wells are a few meters, where the up-
per well is the injector and the lower is the producer. Steam is then pumped down through the 
injector wellbore which is perforated in the end section which is inside the reservoir so that 
the steam is dispersed. This goes on for 2-4 months heating the surrounding oil and thus re-
ducing the oil viscosity. The heated oil and the condensed water from the steam then flows 
towards the production wellbore due to gravity. SAGD leads to about the same recovery as 
steamflooding 40-60%.9 
All these thermal processes described above require steam to be injected from the surface fa-
cilities to the reservoir. To minimize energy loss to surroundings and transportation time, it’s 
favourable to geographically place the facility at an economically beneficial site. In the reser-
voir, several optimizations can be done i.e. injection/production strategy.8,9 
Gas-Injection 
Gas injection is used for pressure maintenance to supplement the reservoir pressure, when it’s 
dropping due to production as a secondary recovery method. Gas injection also has enhanced 
recovery properties and many benefits as well as disadvantages:10,11 
Advantages 
• Pressure maintenance  
• Swelling of oil phase 
• Viscosity reduction 
• Reduction of interfacial tension (zero if miscible displacement) 
• Ecology (CO2 injection) 
Disadvantages 
• May be expensive (i.e. hydrocarbon injection) 
• May be instable (for immiscible displacement) 
• Requires an additional facility and installations 
Enhanced gas recovery when reached miscibility is very efficient since it reaches out in the 
formation and displaces all the oil components from each pore. It reduces the surface tension 
resulting in high mobilization of the dispersed oil phase. The mechanisms are different be-
tween gas injection and of the other EOR methods. Here the purpose is to make the injected 
==PAGE:12==
Page 12 of 52 
 
fluid become a part of the oil in the reservoir one way or the other. One way is to use the va-
porizing gas drive mechanism: Here the injected gas (CH4, CO2, N2, Flue gas) is injected in 
order to reach multiple-contact miscibility (MCM)11. When the gas gets into contact with the 
oil, some of the light and intermediate components vaporizes into the gas phase thus enriching 
the gas, if this is enough that the miscibility results with the oil, then there is multiple-contact 
miscibility. Another way of reaching the same result is the condensing gas drive: her the in-
jected gas is already rich in intermediate molecular weight components, so when the gas gets 
into contact with the oil it condenses some of these components into the oil phase. If the oil 
becomes sufficiently enriched in these components so that miscibility is reached, then the en-
riched oil and gas have multiple-contact miscibility11,12. 
 
Chemical, Polymer & Surfactant flooding 
Chemical flooding is an interesting way to explore carbonate reservoirs, this involves the in-
jection of a specific chemical i.e. surfactant. Polymer and surfactant flooding are the most ef-
ficient way, because of the synergy with IFT reduction and mobility control with small side 
effects. Alkaline, soap-like substances and micellar are used to reduce the interfacial tension 
between oil and water in the reservoir. 
Surfactant flooding 
A surfactant is a chemical compound which purpose is to stabilize the oil and water mixture, 
by reducing the IFT between the molecules. Normally these two liquids do not mix and there-
fore a surfactant is needed to keep them from separating into their respective 2 phase layers. 
Surfactants are used in everyday life to remove substances such as dirt from human skins, fab-
rics and other solid materials, but is also used as emulsifiers for soaps, shampoo etc.  
Surfactants are most commonly organic compounds which are 
amphiphilic meaning they have a hydrophobic group (tails) and 
hydrophilic group (heads).13 Which means they have a water 
soluble/insoluble or oil soluble and water soluble component. 
Figure 3 shows such a surfactant molecule. Surfactants must 
first reduce the interfacial tension enough so that the water and 
oil can create the emulsion which will help create a flow in con-
tinuous phases.14

Polymers are used to improve the sweep efficiency, by using polymers 
such as polyacrylamide or polysaccharide. These polymers have a high 
molecular weight and increase the viscosity of the water slug15. Surfac-
tants are used to dissolve 2 fluids which are immiscible, for this to hap-
pen surfactants create something called micelles spontaneously when the 
concentration or the amount of surfactant reaches a certain point. Mi-
celles can have one of three different shapes; cylindrical, bilayers or 
spherical as shown in Figure 4, where the blue hydrophobic tail is sus-
pended in the oil phase and the red hydrophilic is in water phase. The mi-
celles will act as a barrier between the 2 fluids, so they don’t come into 
contact with each other. The higher the concentration of the surfactant 
becomes the lower the surface tension will become until it reaches  
the critical micellar concentration (CMC). Here the maximum value is 
obtained and surface tension will then stay constant.13,15 
The purpose is to mobilize the capillary trapped residual oil after water 
flooding, by reduction in the interfacial tension between oil and water. 
Polymer flooding 
Polymer flooding is and EOR method where the polymer is added to the injected water and 
increases the viscosity and reduction in relative permeability to water. This means that poly-
mer flooding is favourable in high viscosity heterogeneous oil reservoirs with different per-
meabilities in the oil-bearing layers. Viscosity is increased until the mobility of the injectant is 
lesser than the oil phase and the mobility ratio is less than 113,15. This condition enhances the 
sweep efficiency, creating a smooth flood front, and prevents viscous fingering.   
Water based EOR 
All the components of the oil reservoir consist of crude oil, brine and rock (crude oil, brine 
and rock, CBR). Distribution of oil and formation water (FW) are fixed at specific saturations 
of oil and water. There exists a gradient in the oil and water composition in the oil reservoir 
and inhomogeneous rock properties which may change the relative distribution.  
Distribution of oil and water are related to the wetting properties of the CBR system. As a 
rough characterization the terminology of the reservoir rock, the terms water-wet, neutral-wet 
and oil-wet have been used16,17

According to Jadhunandan and Morrow (1995) the wetting properties of a given CBR system

• Displace the oil by water by taking advantage of the viscous forces.17 
Usually water flooding is characterized as a secondary recovery method, because no special 
addition has been made to the injection water like chemicals as in EOR. T. Austad (2013) ver-
ifies that injected water can disturb the equilibrium which is different in composition com-
pared to the initial water formation water17. This means, when a new chemical equilibrium is 
being established, the wetting properties will also be changed, which may result in improved 
oil recovery. In the case of the injection water being the same or very similar to the formation 
water the disturbance will minor, and the change in equilibrium will be small and only the sat-
urations will be affected2,16. It can be said the injection of formation water is a secondary pro-
cess, but if injection of water with a different composition than the formation water, it’s a ter-
tiary oil recovery process.  
Wettability  
Wettability is the ability of any liquid to attach to a solid surface, as a result of intermolecular 
forces when these two are brought into contact with each other. The wettability is determined 
by two forces respectively the adhesive forces (tendency of dissimilar particles to cling to-
gether) and cohesive forces (tendency of similar particles to cling to another). A system is 
said to be oil-wet if the oil molecules are attached to the rock surface, water-wet if the water 
molecules are attached to the rock surface and mixed wet if it’s alternating between these 
two13. Wettability can be described by the contact angle 𝜃 between the surface of the solid to 
the angle of the droplet.

Smart Water flooding 
 
It has been established that the wetting properties of an oil reservoir are related to the pres-
ence of a given CBR system. Examples have shown that limestone reservoirs which have very 
low AN but are still oil-wet. Fathi et al.(2010b) tried to modify and alter the wettability but 
was unsuccessful and showed that any kind of alteration was impossible.17,20

When injecting into a carbonate reservoir the oil recovery is dependent on the formation wa-
ter, which usually is different from the recovery obtained by injecting water with a different 
composition than that of FW16. Dependent on the CBR system which is in equilibrium with 
formation water, the recovery can both increase and decrease. If a “smart water” is used on 
the other hand the recovery can be significantly increased as illustrated in        Figure 7. The 
figure shows that injection was done at high temperatures and that changing from formation 
water to smart water drastically increased the recovery. Smart water can be made by changing 
the ion composition of the injected fluid so that the CBR system will modify the initial wet-
ting conditions17. When this happens, the oil is more mobile and easily displaced from the po-
rous network. This means that smart water is considered a tertiary oil recovery method since it 
is performed after secondary water flood with formation water, and more oil was also recov-
ered. This technique is cheap and environmentally friendly, and no chemicals are added.  
 
Smart water as an EOR fluid has been verified in the lab as well as in the field by several re-
search groups, and oil companies during the last 20 years17. In order to mobilize the oil in car-
bonate rocks the wettability needs to be changed from oil-wet to water-wet. By doing so it is 
believed that the oil be released from the carbonate surface and mobilize and henceforth be 
produced. T. Austad (2003) used a cationic surfactant of the type alkyl-trimethyl -ammonium

Cores permeabilities are measured in the laboratory by flowing a fluid of known viscosity 
though the core of known dimensions at a specific set rate, and measuring the pressure drop 
across the core, or by setting the fluid to a flow at a specific rate and set pressure difference 
and measuring the flow rate produced32. There is a difference when it comes to measuring the 
porosity and permeability with liquids contra gases. When using a liquid it’s straightforward 
as the requirement for laminar flow and incompressibility of the fluid, are always met at sur-
face geological conditions32,33. 
Gas is a compressible fluid, and this can cause an overestimation of permeability. When gas is 
flowing though the pore spaces it is compressed into smaller volume at the inlet where there is 
high pressure and then it expands at the outlet. Therefore, gases at low pressure can occupy 
very small pores which are not accessible by the liquid and hence the law of fluid flow 
through a porous medium not applicable anymore. This is known as the gas slippage effect or 
the Klingenberg effect. This problem mostly occurs at lower pressures since the gas is being 
less compressed, and the higher the pressure the more gas molecules per unit volume. This 
problem however does not occur with liquids since they are much denser than gasses. In order 
to compensate for this overestimation of the permeability gas flow though the core is meas-
ured at different pressure stages and a graph is plotted with the measured apparent permeabil-
ity against the reciprocal of the mean pressure.

Relative permeability  
As indicated permeability depends on several factors, one of them being to which degree the 
available pores space is saturated with the flowing fluid. If there are more than one fluid avail-
able, it might not get saturated with just one of the fluids32,35. Normally there is oil and water 
as minimum in the reservoir pores. Since they both have different properties such as viscosity 
they will not move at the same rates at the same time and will have different permeabilities 
and not the same permeability as of the rock if it only contained a single fluid. If this was the 
case it would be denoted as the absolute permeability and this would be the maximum perme-
ability of the rock. When there are two fluids present in the rock formation, the permeabilities 
of each fluid depend on its level of saturation. These are called effective permeabilities and 
==PAGE:30==
Page 30 of 52 
 
are both always less than the absolute permeability and their sums are always less as well. 
The effective permeabilities are mostly expressed as a fraction of the absolute permeability of 
the rock to either one of the fluids when present in 100% saturation, and denoted relative per-
meabilities32.  
In other words, if 100% oil was available in the reservoir rock then the permeability of the oil 
would be kao, an the same applies for 100% saturation of gas and water respectively denoted 
kaw, kag. In the case of any two fluids or all three present at the same time their partial satura-
tions Sw, So and Sg, their respective effective permeability’s kew, keo and keo can be measured 
and then used to calculate the relative permeabilities as a fraction of the absolute permeability 
of one of the fluids present for example kao.33

Conclusion 
The purpose of this project was to figure out if the electrolyte activity had an impact on the oil 
recovery during spontaneous imbibition. Even though one core broke, and another one 
showed unusually low recovery (4) with respect to the literature, it was a successful experi-
ment with 3 of them having medium to high recovery. It can be concluded that the active ions 
𝑆𝑂4
2−, 𝑀𝑔2+ and 𝐶𝑎2+have an impact on the oil recovery, is likely linked to the wettability in 
carbonates. A detailed chemical understanding is needed of the CBR system and can be ob-
tained from controlled and systematic laboratory conditions. Improved chemical knowledge 
of wettability alteration could be used for field behaviour observations and possibly identify 
water based EOR potential in the future. 
 
Perspective 
Effect of the ionic activities in the oil recovery with spontaneous imbibition showed some 
promising results. The experiment was successful and can be further enhanced. The oil recov-
ery for brine composition 3 showed the best results as was expected if 4 is not included. It is 
in accordance with the literature and the effect the active ions have on wettability and oil pro-
duction. In order to recover more oil, having constant salinity and injecting the optimum brine 
from the beginning can significantly increase the oil recovery. The setup for continues injec-
tion and constant salinity with SW10 showed that the setup can keep the water level constant 
and would be a good way to redo injection with all 4 brines to get a better recovery, and then 
choose the ones with the highest recoveries to do forced imbibition. The reason for brine com-
position 4 having so little recovery might be due to the temperature, so having a temperature 
of 70°C-80°C might show more promising results there. Having better analysis of the rock be-
fore injecting different brines might give some better understanding of the CBR system. It is 
definite that alternating the wettability from oil-wet to water-wet is important for mobilizing 
the oil, and this can be enhanced with the injection of optimum stochiometric concentration of 
the activity ions.
