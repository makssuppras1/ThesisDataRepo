Abstract
There are many different ways to protect a sensitive circuits against ESD and surge tran­
sients. This report looks at PCB spark gaps as protection for ESD and surge transients,
as well as TVS, BAV99, GDT for ESD protection, both theoretically and by testing us­
ing IEC61000­4 compliant test equipment. The component comparison is based on a
mock­up input circuit which represents an ADC input, which have also been put set up in
LTspice. Custom PCBAs are utilised for the testing. The testing of ESD and surge dis­
charges have been automated using Python, which reduces the time testing takes roughly
sixfold.

Introduction
The British new wave/synth­pop group the Buggles said famously in 1979 that ”Video
Killed the Radio Star” [1]. This controversial statement might well be true, but one thing
is for certain, people have not stopped enjoying listening to good music. For people to be
able to enjoy good music to the fullest, a good audio amplifier connected to good speakers
is a necessity in front of each couch in a living room.
In an ever­growing world of electronic equipment, the demand of reliable electronics is on
the rise. People want their electronics to last longer and be less fragile. Companies pro­
ducing reliable electronics stand out from the bunch since there has been years of planned
obsolescence in the industry, which is slowly becoming outlawed [2]. This demand is very
relevant for high­end ICEpower audio amplifiers.
ICEpower is constantly striving to push forwards in the audio industry, and making prod­
ucts that meet strict criteria from customers is what ICEpower does best. In a newly
ordered amplifier setup, a customer demands high ESD and surge transient protection on
low power audio inputs, such as microphone inputs. These kinds of inputs are usually not
protected against voltage transients such as ESD or surges, and to meet the demands of
the customer, this needs solving. When this customer will be mentioned in this report, it
will be noted as ’the customer’ from here forwards. The customer has a high regard for
satisfaction with their products, which applies both to end users and installation techni­
cians. The customer intends to use the new amplifier setup in harsh locations of the globe,
where the humidity and temperature can have a high range, and where lightning storms
are a regular occurrence. E.g., the customer wants to be able to route audio signals
outdoors where a lightning might hit a microphone left outside in a thunderstorm. These
harsh environments are the foundation for the high ESD and surge transient protection
criteria.
There are many protective components available on the market for protecting various
electronic circuits against ESD and/or surge voltage spikes. Not all of these components
are suitable for audio purposes as they can distort the low power sound above the allowed
distortion levels (THD) if not implemented carefully. By comparing multiple different pro­
tective strategies, a comparison matrix has been made which can note the pros and cons
behind each strategy. These components are for example TVS diodes, current shunting
diodes, spark gaps, and varistors. Each component has their own strength, but with every
strength comes some weakness.
By having a good solution which can protect delicate electronic circuits from ESD dis­
charges and voltage surges, a whole product becomes more reliable. This solution can
then be used in future ICEpower products which would benefit both the customers and
company. This solution is therefore not limited to this new customer with the strict criteria,
but is relevant for all current and future customers of ICEpower. The strategies and data
gathered in this report can then be extrapolated for different kinds of inputs and outputs,
which makes this report valuable for more than only low power audio inputs.

Literature Review
Before any testing is to be conducted, it is important to know the theory behind the tests.
In this chapter the theory behind ESD and surge discharges will be noted, alongside the
different protective circuitry that is widely used in the industry to protect various circuits
from voltage transients.
3.1
ESD
Figure 3.1: ESD warning label.
Electrostatic discharge (ESD) is a quick release of electric charge between two differently
charged objects in the form of a current flow. This type of discharge is most easily recog­
nised as atmospheric lightning bolts in everyday life. These lightning bolts are powerful
and obviously somewhat deadly, to both humans and electronics. Another non­deadly (to
humans) ESD event is the spark that forms on the tip of your finger after you have been
rubbing your shoes against a carpet and you touch someone or something. This activity
is looked at as a harmless prank or a fun afternoon activity, but this fun can quickly turn
to tragedy if anyone’s beloved electronic equipment is on the receiving end of the prank.
This seemingly harmless tickle on your finger can contain relatively high energy which can
cause damage in sensitive equipment, either by the high voltage or the instantaneous high
temperature that can burn sensitive components [3].
3.1.1
IEC61000­4­2
It should be impossible to mention ESD transients and discharges theoretically with­
out mentioning the IEC standard which is focused around ESD. This standard is the
IEC61000­4­2. This standard is titled Electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) ­ Part 4­2:
Testing and measurement techniques ­ Electrostatic discharge immunity test [4]. Con­
tained in this standard are definitions on range of test levels, typical waveform of the
discharge current, test equipment and setup, measurement uncertainty, and more. This
standard will not be noted in full detail, but the most relevant parts and sections will be
highlighted.
Figure 3.2 shows a simplified diagram of the internal workings of an ESD generator as
defined in IEC61000­4­2. The ESD generator consists in its main parts of a charging
resistor Rc, energy­storage capacitor Cs, distributed capacitance Cd, discharge resistor
Rd, voltage indicator, discharge switch, charge switch, interchangeable tips, discharge
return cable, and power supply unit.
The ESD generator should meet the contact discharge current waveform parameters
noted in table 3.1 and on figure 3.3. This current defines the frequency components in

3.1.2
Energy in an ESD event
It is difficult to know how resistant components are to ESD discharges, as the timescale
is short. E.g., usually in datasheets for resistors, the maximum electrical power ratings
are stated in maximum constant power dissipation in watts [W]. Some better datasheets
for chip resistors however state the surge performance of the resistor, an example of this
can be seen on figure 3.5. This is mostly the case for high power components that are
meant to be used in high power situations.
Using the wattage to quantify the amount of energy dissipated is an estimation which only
holds for relatively long time periods. For periods in the range of nano seconds, as is
the case in ESD discharges (see figure 3.3), watts are not representative for the energy
dissipated. Rather the energy, in joules [J], should be used, as that is the total dissipated
energy regardless of time. The relationship between watts and joules can be seen in
equation (3.1).

3.1.3
ESD test criteria
ESD test criteria defines which voltage levels the ESD generator should be set to when
testing circuits, as well as how many times each voltage level should be applied. IEC61000­
4­2 defines the maximum test voltage of air discharges for ESD immunity as ±15 kV, while
defining the maximum test voltage of contact discharges as ±8 kV. The customer requires
all audio input conductors to be tested up to ±15 kV using exposed wires, which makes
the use of contact discharge more suitable. The customers test voltage criteria is therefore
close to double the maximum contact discharge voltage mentioned in IEC61000­4­2.
Test levels for ESD discharges required by the customer at all inputs are 2, 4, 8, 10, 12,
and 15 kV. Each voltage level should be discharged 10 times at both negative and positive
polarity, so 20 times in total at each level. Between each discharge, the points of contact
should be grounded using a grounded banana plug. This is to ensure that all tests begin
at the same voltage potential.
The pass/fail criteria set by the customer is the equivalent to the criteria set in IEC61000­

3.2
Surge
Surge discharge is a common overstress factor in many power supply designs connected
to mains voltage. These surges are often caused by lightning transients somewhere on
the grid. The lightning quickly raises the voltage on the grid for a short period of time. This
high energy over­voltage transient can cause stress to or damage electrical equipment
which are not sufficiently protected.

Spark gap
In an ever going competition regarding cost in consumer electronics, saving a few cents
here and there per PCBA, a company can save a lot of money in the long run. ESD and
surge protective components are relatively expensive, and when the protection needs to

be applied to every I/O line, the cents quickly become dollars. A nearly free ESD and surge
protection method is using a PCB spark gap, sometimes called printed atmospheric spark
gap. A drawing of such a spark gap can be seen on figure 3.12. The parasitic capacitance
of an PCB spark gap is negligible as the area of cross section between the two copper
shapes is minuscule. The reason behind the cost effectiveness of the PCB spark gap is
that is only utilises the copper planes on the PCB which needs are used when producing
PCBs in the etching process [11].
Figure 3.12: Simple drawing showing a PCB spark gap. A spark gap is effectively
just two copper shapes close to one another with no soldermask covering them.
For PCB spark gaps, the atmosphere acts as a conductor, which gives it its name of
atmospheric spark gap. This means that for spark gap to conduct energy, the electrons
need to jump through air. This phenomenon is also called arcing.
When describing the discharge process in spark gaps, there are various models available.
These models depend on the air pressure P and airgap distance d. For Pd < 2.7 hPa·m,
the Townsend avalanche model can be used to describe the discharge [12]. This applies
to PCB spark gaps where the airgap distance d < 2.7 mm when P is normal atmospheric
pressure, 1 atm or 1013.25 hPa. These distances are quite higher than normal PCB spark
gaps which are usually on a range of less than 1 mm, and the Townsend discharge can
therefore describe the physics behind them. In this report all spark gaps mentioned will
have a distance of 0.5 mm or less.
For a Townsend discharge to be initiated, there needs to be some initial electrons available
in the airgap [13]. These electrons can for example be results of cosmic rays or ultra­violet
radiation. Without the initial electrons, a discharge can not happen, no matter how high
the voltage is over the airgap. When an electric field is applied over the airgap, by apply­
ing voltage to the poles, the free electrons can gain energy and collide with neighbouring
molecules. If the energy the electrons have gained is sufficiently high, the molecules can
ionise with a certain probability [14]. In this ionisation process, one or more electrons are
released, which also gain energy from the electric field, and collide with other molecules.
These electrons therefore make more new electrons, and an avalanche process has be­
gun. As well as making ionised molecules, the avalanche produces photons emitted by
the excited molecules of which have some sufficient energy to ionise other molecules at
a distance from the avalanche region, and effectively expanding the avalanche region.
As the avalanche increases, a conducting channel is created. This channel can carry
high magnitudes of electrons as the resistance through the ionised air is low. The voltage
needed for this process to persist, is called breakdown voltage, relating to the isolation

breakdown of the air. When an arc is happening, a voltage drop over the gap persists.
This voltage drop is called burn (or burning) voltage. This burning voltage is rather low,
below 100V, and for clean copper surfaces, the typical burning voltage is about 20V [15,
16]. Most of the burning voltage drops close to the the grounded potential (where the
ESD transient is not applied), or cathode, in what is called a plasma sheath, or cathode
fall. Details of this plasma sheath will not be noted here, but can be found in [15, 16].
This burn voltage can vary based on the surface contamination and material. The burn
voltage of a spark gap is important to note when choosing protective circuitry, as there
will be some voltage present which needs to be dissipated elsewhere.
With increased water vapour in the air between two poles of an airgap, the area that
photons can move before hitting a molecule (in this case water molecule) is shortened [13],
and thus the number of ionised particles will be reduced, which reduces the probability of
the arc making process and effectively increases the breakdown voltage.
It has been shown that the time it takes from a voltage is applied to the poles of a spark

increased over each air gap. That is due to increasing electric field which gives the initial

3.5.3
NMOS
An N­channel Mosfet, or NMOS, can be used as an over voltage protection. It works
similarly as the TVS diode previously mentioned. When the VDS voltage gets over the
breakdown voltage defined, it goes into avalanche mode and starts conducting between
the drain and source. The speed of this effect is quick for high enough voltages, as the
mosfet is working as a diode in reverse. This requires the gate of the NMOS to be short
circuited to the source. The NMOS chosen for comparison purposes is from ”International
Rectfier” with the part number ”IRLML6344TRPbF” [20]. Thousand units of this NMOS
costs 180 EUR as of June 2023.
The size and weight of IRLML6344TRPbF is the same as for the TVS diode as it also
comes in the SOT23 package.
The usage of an NMOS as a transient voltage suppressor in audio circuitry is limited as the
output capacitance Coss is quite high, on the range of 50­300pf. This capacitance changes
with changing VDS, and is higher on low voltages. As mentioned in the description of the

TVS diodes, this is an unwanted property on the audio inputs, and the sound distortion
would be rather high, or roughly 20 times worse than in the case of the TVS. The sound
distortion is therefore higher, and to quantify it, it is assumed to be 90% compared to the
30% in the TVS case.
Similarly as the TVS, the reliability of an NMOS over time is good. However, since NMOS
transistors are not made or tested against ESD or surge transients, the reliability of their
protection can not be guaranteed for all transients, and surge discharges would most likely
destroy the silicone substrate.
As the breakdown voltage (clamping voltage) of an NMOS is quite high compared its
TVS sibling, 30V for IRLML6344TRPbF. This voltage is quite high, and for sensitive logic
circuits, some other circuitry is needed to lower this voltage. The IRLML6344TRPbF can
therefore not be used as a standalone protection.
3.5.4
Gas filled spark gap
Gas filled spark gaps, or gas discharge tubes (GDT), works on the same principles as the
PCB spark gaps. In the gas tubes a lot of the variation effects of the PCB spark gaps have
been removed, such as humidity, free electron number, and geometrical defects. This can
be done by enclosing a low pressure inert gas and specialised electrodes in glass or ce­
ramic enclosure [19]. By building the component up in such an enclosure, the breakdown
voltage can be controlled and is very stable. With the simplicity of the construction of the
tube, having two electrodes apart with inert gas in between, the capacitance is kept very
low, or less than 0.6 pF. This capacitance does not change with voltage or other factors as
the TVS and NMOS mentioned above. Alongside the low capacitance comes a very high
insulation resistance of >1 GΩ. The gas filled spark gap has therefore little to no change
in sound quality or distortion.
The gas filled spark gap chosen for the comparison from ”YAGEO” and has the part num­
ber ”4532­075­LF” [21]. The price for thousand units of this product is 223 EUR as of June
2023. This solution is therefore more expensive than the TVS solution.
The absolute maximum size of the 4532­075­LF gas discharge tube is 4.8 mm x 3.5 mm =
16.8 mm2. This is therefore larger than the TVS and PCB spark gap. With this consider­
able larger form factor, the weight increases. It is stated in another datasheet for a similar
gas filled spark gap that the weight is ∼0.2 grams. As this is the only number found for
the weight criteria, it will be used in the comparison matrix.
As for the PCB spark gap, there is some time delay from when the transient is applied
until the gas filled spark gap gets short circuited. This time is way more constant than with
the airgaps, but on the range of 0.1 to 1 µs. Gas filled spark gaps do therefore allow some
of the transient voltage to pass past the component before the protection kicks in and it
short circuits. The component can therefore not be used as a standalone part. For this
same reason, if the total voltage is too high before the short circuiting happens, it does
not provide full protection. Therefore the reliability can be thought of as 95%.
Gas discharge tubes have a limited life, as high energy surges can burn the inner workings
of the tubes. The electrodes inside the enclosure can burn off after too many high energy
sparks. The time reliability is therefore not good, but also not bad, since without any high
energy discharges the degradation is minimal with time.

becomes forwarded biased at an event of transients. The diode therefore ”steers” the

reasoning behind why a designer should take extra caution when choosing components
for ESD protection. However, for the usage of BAV99 in the protection of audio inputs,
which is the focus of this report, it does its job well, which is protecting the input, even
though that might impact something else on the power planes. With the same reasoning,
it can be said that spark gaps also just ”steer” the current to the power planes when an
arc is formed. These considerations is therefore noted, but not further discussed.
As mentioned, BAV99 diodes have low capacitance, and therefore impact sound quality
at a low degree. In the case of the BAV99WQ­7­F the maximum capacitance is 2pF.
This is a relatively low capacitance, but it varies with voltage. As with the NMOS and
TVS, this is not a desired property of audio inputs, as it introduces some audio distortion.
This capacitance is 5 times less than the capacitance of the TVS diode, and the sound
distortion metric will be approximated as 5%.
For the same reasoning as why TVS diodes should only be used for ESD transients but
not for surge transients, the reliability of the BAV99 diodes is similar to TVS diodes, both
over a long time as well as under multiple test conditions.

Capacitor
When dealing with voltage/current ripple and voltage spikes, it is often solved with using
bulk capacitors. For transients such as ESD and voltage surges, bulk capacitors can be
used to slow down the rise of the voltage and absorb the energy while not increasing the
voltage over a certain limit on the capacitor.
For this to be a viable option as ESD and surge protection, the capacitor needs to be
around 50 µF, and one such has been selected. The capacitor is from ”Vishay” and with
the part number ”TVA1343”. This is a 50 µF, 100V leaded capacitor. It is both costly and

The 50 µF capacitance was chosen by simulating a surge generator as shown in sec­

Without a specified bleeding resistor, a capacitor can not be used as a protection, and
therefore it is not a standalone component. Without a parallel resistor, the capacitor would
hold the charge for a long period of time after the transient has passed.
3.5.7
Metal Oxide Varistor
The metal oxide varistor (MOV) in simple terms is a voltage dependant resistor. At low
voltages the resistance is high and at higher voltages the resistance lowers. A MOV works
similarly to a zener diode in both directions, see figure 3.18. One of the key parameters
of MOVs is the breakdown voltage, which is the transition point in which a small change
of voltage changes the resistance of the varistor drastically. The maximum peak current
through a MOV is a function of its cross sectional area, which means that MOVs can be
used for protecting circuits against surge transients as well as ESD transients [19]. How­
ever, the capacitance is a function of the cross sectional area as well, so when choosing
a MOV to use as a protective component, the pick lies between surge withstand and low
capacitance.

3.5.8
Surge resistor
In the case of audio inputs before ADCs, a simple RC low pass filter is often implemented.
To reduce the effect of capacitance on the audio line from transient suppressors such as
the TVS diode, they are often put in parallel with the capacitor in the low pass filter. This
minimises the effect that the variable capacitance has on the line as the low pass filter
capacitor is usually of many magnitudes higher than the capacitance of the suppression
device. However, by putting the current ”shunt” after the low pass filter, the whole current
of the transient is passed through the resistor of the low pass filter, see figure 3.19. This is
where the so called surge resistor come into play. These resistors work the same way as
regular resistors, except they can dissipate more energy in a short period of time. These
resistors are thick film resistors, and have a regular tolerance of 5%, which is standard for
E24 values. These resistors are manufactured to withstand surge transients as defined
in IEC61000­4­5 [6] mentioned in section 3.2.1.

Comparison matrix
The eight solutions described have been put into a comparison matrix, see table 3.6. This
matrix shows the pros and cons of the solutions while also colour coding them to weigh
every category against themselves. Table 3.6 shows that there is no single perfect solution
when choosing an ESD and surge transient protection of audio inputs. The best result
might be achieved by using a combination of multiple components. E.g., a gas discharge
tube and a BAV99 diode could be used together. The discharge tube would be placed
close to the input while the BAV99 diode would be closer to the circuit it is protecting.
In this kind of setup, the main part of the energy from the input transient will be directed
by the gas discharge tube while the quicker transients which the tube does not suppress
are redirected by the BAV99 diode. It is however important to note that when connecting
different protective components in parallel, the functionality of one of them might change.
This is usually the case with the slower components. E.g., if an PCB spark gap is placed
in parallel with a TVS diode, the spark gap would never arc as the TVS diode is quicker
at shorting the energy and lowering the voltage which would in turn never allow the air to
ionise on the PCB spark gap. To circumvent this issue, a component with high enough
impedance would have to be placed between the two protective components which would
allow the voltage over the slower component to be high.

voltage and time it takes for the spark to form and jump over the gap, if any. This time,
also called delay time, is the time from when the voltage over the spark gap rises, until
it quickly falls down again. With this data, some statistics can be noted which makes the
choice of spark gaps as ESD protection for design engineers more concrete and backed
up by data in the future.
4.2.2
ESD test setup and procedure
ESD transients can go up to 15 kV, and PCB spark gaps are often used to protect circuits
against these high voltage transients [12]. Figures 4.1 and 4.2 show the test setup used
for testing the PCB spark gap PCBA.
The PCB spark gap PCBA can be used to measure time delays from when voltage is
applied over a spark gap until an arc forms. The PCBA can also provide some statistics
about how well the spark gaps work at each voltage level. In the test setup a perfect sym­
metry is assumed and therefore only positive pulses are tested. With this assumption, the
functionality of a spark gap can not be fully derived as arcs are not perfectly symmetrical
for both negative and positive discharges [12]. This however reduces the dataset in half
without compromising integrity of the data. The voltage of the ESD discharge starts at 2
kV and is increased in 500V increments up to the voltage level which ensures 100% quick
sparking or 15 kV, whichever comes first. 100% quick sparking is when every discharge
applied to the circuit ensures that a spark happens in less than 10 ns from the time the

4.2.3
Surge test setup and procedure
Surge discharges hold more energy than the ESD discharges as mentioned in section 3.2.2.
These surges need to be redirected away from a sensitive circuit or component, and a
spark gap can be used as a redirection when an arc forms. Figure 4.3 shows a drawing
of the test setup when testing the PCB spark gap PCBA with surge inputs.
The surge discharge tests are all done with the coupling functionality of the CDN con­
nected to a 40 Ωseries resistance and 90 V gas arrestor, as is described in section 4.1.2.
The voltage of the surges tested starts at 1 kV and is increased in 500 V increments until
sparking happens over the spark gap or the voltage limit of the equipment is reached (6
kV), whichever comes first. The voltages higher than the voltage when arcing happens
are assumed to also make the spark gap spark, that is, if a spark happens at 4 kV, all
test voltages higher than 4 kV are assumed to also spark. As with the ESD test pro­
cedure, only positive pulses are considered, as it is assumed that the test setup is fully
symmetrical and the functionality will be equal both for positive and negative voltages.
Each voltage step is applied 10 times at each test as per the customers specifications
(see section 3.2.3) and enough time is passed between surges to make sure that all tests
start at the same voltage level. This time is kept 10 seconds for voltages less than or
equal to 4 kV, and 20 seconds for voltages higher than 4 kV. Before testing, solder is
applied to the spark gaps on the PCB to protect them from getting damaged due to the
high energy of the surge transient. This change in geometry might change the sparking
time and voltage, but since the tests need to be conducted multiple times, this protection

4.2.4
Data acquisition and considerations
Getting clean data measurements when doing ESD tests on the PCB spark gap PCBA
took some iterations. Multiple test setups were tried before arriving at the setup noted
previously in section 4.1.1.
In general, the oscilloscope is connected to the circuit via a oscilloscope ”tip clip” and a
ground clip connected to the side of the probe. The tip clip is connected to a small wire
loop that is soldered into the PCB in the plated hole next to the last resistor in the voltage
divider. These holes can be seen on the right most part of figure 4.5. The ground clip of
the probe is then connected to the return pin of the input connector (pin 2 on figure 4.5).
At first, an acrylic plastic ESD gun mount was used to hold the ESD gun in place, and a
short ∼15 cm wire connected the tip of the ESD gun to the PCB under test. This setup
introduced a lot of oscillations and therefore the data gathered needed a lot of harsh filter­
ing to be useful. Figure 4.8 shows the setup, a single raw measurement, the Fast­Fourier
Transform (FFT) spectrum, and the filtered data. The raw data on figure 4.8b shows that
the voltage tends to oscillate around some voltage trend. By using the FFT spectrum to
analyse these frequency oscillations it is clear that some frequency components in the
acquired data need to be filtered out to make the data more readable. These frequency
peaks are around 150MHz and 250MHz while the actual voltage trend is lower in fre­
quency. The filter used is a 5th order low­pass Butterworth filter with a cutoff frequency
of 100 MHz. This cutoff frequency and order removes most of the excessive oscillations.
Figure 4.8c shows the change in the FFT spectrum. Since the cutoff frequency is quite
low, the voltage trend is not as steep as it would otherwise be. This means that the exact
time from the rise time to the time when the voltage falls (also called delay time) has quite
high margin of error. A Butterworth filter has been chosen for it ease of use in Python as
well as its flat pass band region.
Another test setup using a similar layout as the acrylic setup was tried, but now without
the 15 cm wire between the PCB and the tip of the discharge gun. This was achieved by
turning the whole gun setup upside down, and placing the PCB on top of the tip of the
ESD gun. This topology reduced the main large oscillation at 100 MHz but introduced
other issues. Mainly that the setup was unstable and reliable measurements could not
be properly conducted as well as the grounding became more unstable since it needed
even longer wires, which in turn introduces more inductance in the ground path.
A better measurement setup was achieved when instead of connecting the spark gap PCB
to the tip of the gun with some jig, the ESD gun was brought to the PCB using a table top
vise. This eliminates the need of external wires, and keeps the ground wires and loop as
short as possible while also keeping the PCB on the test table. On figure 4.9 the setup, a
single raw measurement, the FFT spectrum, and the filtered data can be seen. The data
still needs some filtering but since the cutoff frequency of the filter can be kept higher, at
around 200 MHz compared to the 100 MHz before, the slope of the voltage rise and fall
is steeper than before. The raw data can even be used for more exact measurements if
needed, since the rise/fall time is of most importance.
In an attempt to locate the origin and reduce the oscillations even further, the reflections
in the probe connection to the oscilloscope were eliminated. This was done by changing
out the voltage divider so that the resistor the probe was connected over was 50 Ω. Then
a 50 ΩBNC connector was fitted right next to it, and a 50 Ωcable connected the circuit
to a 50 Ωterminated input on an oscilloscope. This change should, in theory, reduce

4.3.2
ESD test setup and procedure
Similarly to the PCB spark gap PCBA, the ADC input protection PCBA test setup for ESD
testing utilises the vise to hold the ESD discharge gun in place and the grounding is the
same (see figure 4.2). Testing the ADC input protection PCBA is more vulnerable to
ringings and voltage fluctuations, as the output voltage is on a lower voltage scale, and
the oscilloscope needs to be connected over the load resistor using a ground spring (pig
tail). This will be mentioned in more detail in section 4.3.4.
Some ADC chips have a built in 2 kV ESD protective diodes [36], and even though they
could potentially protect the inputs, it is assumed that the ADC inputs have no ESD pro­
tection.
In this test setup a perfect symmetry is assumed and therefore only positive pulses are
tested. The voltage of the ESD discharge starts at 1 kV and is increased in 500V incre­
ments up to the maximum voltage test level of 15 kV. Each voltage level is applied 10
times, and enough time is allowed to pass between discharges to ensure that all tests
start at the same voltage level. This time is kept around 5 seconds. After each voltage
level, the components need to be measured and measurements noted. Here only 10
discharges are applied, compared to the 20 discharges on the PCB spark gaps. This is
due to the fact that only positive pulses are considered and applying 20 discharges would
potentially overload the components. E.g., to protect a circuit from negative and positive
ESD and Surge Immunity for Audio Inputs

==PAGE:58==
pulses, two BAV99 diodes would need to be connected in opposite polarities and therefore
each diode would only shunt 10 discharges.
4.3.3
Surge test setup and procedure
As with the PCB spark gap PCBA, the ADC input protection PCBA utilises the coupling
functionality of a CDN connected to a 40 Ωseries resistance and 90 V gas arrestor, as
is described in section 4.1.2. In this test, as before, perfect symmetry is assumed, which
eliminates the need for negative voltage discharges. The voltage applied starts at 500
V and is incremented in 500 V increments until the surge voltage is 2 kV or something
violently breaks, whichever comes first. A violently broken component is a component
that is visibly or measurably broken and its functionality has deviated too much. This is a
case to case consideration and needs to be noted in the test results.
With 4 voltage discharge levels, ranging from 500 V to 2 kV, the stress of components
is kept at a minimum without compromising the value of the tests. Each voltage level is
applied 5 times, as per the customers criteria for positive discharges, and enough time
is allowed to pass between discharges to ensure that all tests start at the same voltage
level. This time is kept at 10 seconds. Before any test is conducted, solder needs to be
applied to the PCB spark gaps to protect the pads as mentioned in section 4.2.3.

4.4.2
Test setup and procedure
After each voltage level in both ESD and surge tests on the ADC input protection PCBA,
the status of the components needs to be checked. If the components have derated too
much or fails, the test is considered a fail. E.g., if a resistance value has changed more
than 5% from original value, or if a capacitor short circuits.
The ADC component measurement PCBA needs to be powered by a external 5 VDC
power supply. A suitable current source needs to be connected to one of the connectors
on the outputs of the multiplexers. A suitable current depends on the resistance value
range to measure. If it is assumed that the multiplexers have no internal resistance, and
a 5V maximum supply, to measure 220 Ωresistance as 4 V, a constant current supply
of 4/220 = 18 mA is needed. To increase the range of the resistance measurement, a
higher supply voltage would be needed or a lower amperage current source.
The ADC component measurement PCBA is connected to the ADC input protection PCBA
using the two 2 pin headers on the left most side of the PCBA. When the PCBA is powered
on and connected to a oscilloscope, with a single button press, all 16 measurements can
be gathered. After each measurement sequence, the PCBA needs to be disconnected
from the ADC input protection PCBA to safely do the next test level. If the PCBA is kept
connected to the ADC input protection PCBA, there is a high chance that the analogue

4.6.3
ADC component measurement PCBA autonomy
To manually measure multiple components can be time consuming. The component mea­
surement step utilises the PCBA noted in section 4.4.
The ADC component measurement PCBA has a small ATtiny MCU which needs manual
triggering by the press of a button. When this button has been pressed, the MCU sends 4
bit addresses to the multiplexers who connect the corresponding input to the output. The
MCU then waits a moment to make sure that the voltage measurement on the oscilloscope
connected over the outputs has had time to settle. After a predefined waiting time, next
addresses are sent to the multiplexers and it waits once again. This repeats until all
wanted measurements have been caught by the oscilloscope. The data is then fetched
from the oscilloscope and the process can continue. A future update to the PCB could
be done in a way that the PC (or oscilloscope) triggers the MCU to start sending the
addresses. That way one less human interaction would be needed.

4.6.4.2
ADC input protection measurements
When testing the ADC input protection PCBA the main goal is to measure how much
energy gets past the protective circuitry and gets to the load resistor.
The automation process follows the flowchart on figure 4.27.
The automation process is similar to the process mentioned for the PCB spark gap PCBA.
The main difference is that the voltage range on the oscilloscope should be lower. How­
ever, since this is a repeatable test, the voltage range on the oscilloscope needs to start
at a high range to be able to catch any voltage that might appear on the ADC input. If
the voltage is below a certain limit, then the test is run again, but now with the voltage
range lower. This is done to ensure that the voltage that might appear at the ADC input
is always caught in its full range. With the peak voltage measured at the ADC input, a
preliminary pass/fail criteria can be made. After each voltage step, the protection com­
ponents in the circuit need to be measured to verify that they have not broken down and
that the functionality of the full circuit is as expected. If all components are measured as
expected and there is little to no degradation, then the test is considered passed and the
next voltage level is tested. This is repeated until the maximum test voltage is reached.
4.6.5
Time comparison
Automating the testing process of ESD and surge discharge tests can save time, as well
as making the process easier and less prone to errors.
When utilising the automated test procedure, each ESD measurement on the PCB spark
gap PCBA takes close to 5 seconds, as that is the time allowed to pass between dis­
charges. This time can be reduced to make the process even quicker, verification of
voltage level would need to be conducted to ensure that all tests start at the same voltage
level. Doing a full test sequence of 20 ESD measurements at every 500 V increment from
1 kV to 15 kV would then take around 50 minutes ((5 ∗20 ∗29)/60 = 48.3).
For comparison, a fully manual test was conducted without any autonomy where 5 ESD
measurements were taken on the PCB spark gap PCBA. A computer connected to the
oscilloscope was used to download the measurements. All other functions was done
manually. Here the 5 measurements took a total of 150 seconds, so a single measurement
with the data download takes around 30 seconds. If this technique is used for a full test
sequence as before, the total time would be around 4 hours and 50 minutes. Here all
extra time needed for changing settings and voltage levels were not included, so the total
time would increase in the real world.
A similar comparison can be made for the surge discharges which would yield similar time
reduction.

ESD on spark gaps
ESD transients can get up to quite high voltages. As per the customers requirements,
the test voltages go up to 15 kV, as mentioned in section 3.1. This section addresses the
question of if spark gaps can be used as a standalone protective circuitry for high voltage
ESD discharges as well as at what voltages spark gaps become a reliable protection
(if any). The data gathered is with the use of the PCB spark gap PCBA mentioned in
section 4.2 and the procedure and setup mentioned in section 4.2.2.
A series of voltage measurements have been gathered using an oscilloscope connected
in parallel to the spark gap via a resistor divider with a division factor of ∼150. The mea­
surements gathered can be used to quantify whether a simple spark gap can protect the
following circuit on its own. The measurements can also be used to compare different
geometries and their effect on the voltage it takes for arcs to form with the same air gap.
At the time of testing the spark gap PCB with ESD discharges, the relative humidity was
37% ±5%, and the temperature 22°C ±1°C. This is well within the recommended climatic
conditions defined in IEC614000­4­2 and shown in table 3.2.
The measurement data displays the voltage over the spark gap from the point when the
voltage starts rising and until ∼130 ns have passed. This time does not encapsulate all
sparks that happen, see figure 5.1. Since the spark gap needs to protect some vulnerable
circuitry, the arc has to jump quickly after the voltage has started rising. If the spark does
not happen within the first 100 ns, it is assumed that the spark gap does not provide any
considerable protection as the energy has been dissipated at other places in the circuitry it
is supposed to protect. Therefore, the time division is sufficient to measure the protective
functionality of the spark gaps. The 100 ns is chosen to be representative of the time from
the ideal contact discharge current waveform shown on figure 3.3 defined in IEC614000­
4­2.
Another case of sparking that needs to be taken into account is when the arc forms before
the voltage threshold of the oscilloscope trigger is reached. This leads to the triggering to
happen later than at the initial voltage rise. In this case the arc forming time is assumed to
be less than the minimum tolerance in time, and therefore to be ∼0 ns, even though that
is not exactly correct, it is good enough for the testing purposes. Figure 5.2 shows this
phenomenon by ”zooming out” on the oscilloscope when such a measurement happens.

5.4.6
Conclusion
In sections 5.4.1 to 5.4.5, a few components for ESD protection have been tested and
described using the ADC input protection PCBA described in section 4.3. Due to timing
constraints in the project, more components could unfortunately not be tested.
From the results shown in sections 5.4.1 to 5.4.5 it can be seen why an ESD protection
is needed in sensitive circuits, since without it, the output voltage of the circuit tested can
get very high, or up to 1.1 kV when a 15 kV ESD discharge is on the input. If a circuit
only needs protection for ESD discharges, the best choice of the three tested would be
the TVS diode with a surge resistor. The TVS protection has the lowest measured output
voltage for the highest input voltage. However, if the protection needs to protect the circuit
against surges as well, the TVS might not be the best choice as the energy dissipation is
not high enough. This would need to be tested to ensure proper functionality or the lack
there of.
The data presented in sections 5.4.1 to 5.4.5 are as previously mentioned very limited
and should be read with care. However, the theory seems to match the data gathered
pretty well, so the data could potentially be used as a guide, while possibly not being the
holy truth.

6
Conclusion
Over the course of five months, the work done in this project has shown the capabilities
and limitations of ESD and surge testing.
The testing process of ESD and surge testing PCBAs has been automated using Python
which simplifies any and all possible future work. This includes communication to an
oscilloscope as well as the test transient generator. PCB spark gaps have been thor­
oughly tested and analysed for ESD and surge protection. A PCB spark gap, TVS diode,
BAV99 diode, and a gas discharge tube have been compared as ESD protective circuitry
in a mock­up ADC circuit setting. Furthermore, more protective circuitry has been theo­
retically compared and a comparison matrix has been set up. Finally, heat maps have
been generated which show how arcs move around the edge of PCB spark gaps when
increasing input voltage ESD discharges is applied over the air gap.
With increased air gap, the effective breakdown voltage of a spark gap increases propor­
tionally. Introducing edges to a PCB spark gap decreases the breakdown voltage, but it
reduces the spark gaps ability to handle multiple surge discharges as the edges are prone
to burning.
Arcs jumping over a flat edged spark gap seem to move around quite a bit at low voltages.
At higher voltages the sparks seem to accumulate from a single point with much higher
probability than at lower voltages. This opens up the question of if a wide spark gap is
required or if a single narrow one would be sufficient. This would need to be studied
further to be properly answered.
For ESD protection of a sensitive device, the overshooting voltage of a semiconductor
based component must be noticed, as well as the capacity of the circuit. For a higher
capacitive circuit, the slope of the rise and fall of the transient can be seen to be slower.
Having two TVS diodes in parallel increases the current shunting capabilities of the protec­
tive element, while increasing the overshoot. Placing extra protections therefore needs to
be approached with caution as the peak voltage persistent on the output might get higher
instead, which can be an unwanted property. Gas filled spark gaps have been noticed to
be poor ESD protection for low ESD voltages (<2 kV) but improves its protective features
with increased input voltage.
The data gathered in all sections of this project is limited, and can not be blindly trusted.
For better verification of the theories and solutions suggested throughout, more testing is
needed. Due to timing constraints, not all relevant tests could be done during the project
time span.
6.1
Further Work
While doing the testing the climatic conditions (relative humidity and temperature) were
relatively stable. Changes in humidity and temperature could be better analysed on PCB
spark gaps, and the laboratory could be fitted with a humidifier/dehumidifier to control the
conditions. This should introduce a more thorough testing setup. This was not considered
for this report since it introduces more variables in the setup and the data set gathered
would become massively extensive if the testing would be repeated for each temperature
and humidity levels.
